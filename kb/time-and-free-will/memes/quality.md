# Quality

Quality (*qualité*) is the **irreducible char[[action|act]]er** of conscious experience — what makes a state of [[consciousness]] what it is, as opposed to *how much* it is. Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is, at its core, an extended argument that psychic states are **qualitative through and through**, and that every attempt to treat them as quantities is a spatial projection that generates insoluble philosophical problems.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Qualitative Nature of Consciousness

Bergson's thesis is radical in its simplicity: every conscious state is a **pure quality**, and there is no such thing as a psychic quantity:

> [001-029] But that is a change of quality rather than of magnitude.

When we say a pain is "more intense" than before, we are not measuring it; we are noticing that it has **changed in kind** — it is a different quality of experience. The language of magnitude is a spatial metaphor that distorts the datum:

> [001-033] …some tinged with an indefinable quality, as with a kind of heat or light…

### Quality vs. Quantity: The Fundamental Confusion

Bergson identifies the con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of quality with quantity as the root error running through Western philosophy:

> [P02-001] …the confusion of quality into quantity has introduced…

> [P02-002] …the confusion of quality with quantity: this confusion is the source of the problem of free will.

When we treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as if they had **magnitudes**, we can then compare them, [[rhythm|measure]] them, and predict their outcomes — and [[free-will]] seems to disappear. But the comparison was illegitimate from the start: qualities do not have magnitudes.

### The Continuity of Qualitative Change

A quality, for Bergson, never remains the same. Every conscious state is continuously changing, taking on the color of the [[self|whole self]] in [[duration]]:

| Domain | Nature of [[change|Change]] | Logic |
|--------|-----------------|-------|
| [[space|Space]] (quantity) | Discontinuous, additive | A + B = C |
| Duration (quality) | Continuous, transformative | A becomes A', not A + B |

## Key Distinctions

### Quality vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]] is the **apparent** magnitude of a conscious state — how "strong" it seems. Bergson's analysis reveals that intensity is always either (a) the qualitative char[[action|act]]er of the state it[[ego|self]] or (b) the magnitude of the **physical cause** or **muscular accompaniment**, illegitimately projected onto the conscious state. There is no such thing as pure [[intensity|psychic intensity]] — only quality.

### Quality and the Problem of Measurement

The [[psychophysics]] of [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]] assumes that [[sensation]] qualities can be **ordered** by magnitude. Bergson's reply: ordering by magnitude already presupposes space. To say X > Y, you must place X and Y in a **[[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]** where they are juxtaposed and compared. But qualities interpenetrate; they cannot be juxtaposed.

### Quality and the Self

The [[ego|self]], for Bergson, is a **qualitative whole** — not a collection of states but an ongoing [[becoming|process]] of [[change|qualitative change]]. Each new experience **transforms** the whole quality of the [[ego|self]], so that the same stimulus never produces ex[[action|act]]ly the same response. This is why determinism fails: the [[ego|self]] is never the same from one moment to the next.
