# Voluntary

## Overview / Definition

In *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, the "voluntary" designates a mode of conscious [[action|act]]ivity that involves deliberate [[attention]] and innervation. Bergson distinguishes [[attention|voluntary attention]] from automatic or reflexive [[becoming|process]]es, arguing that "consciousness of the involuntary [[motion|movement]]s tending to follow" [002-011] underlies our experience of [[action|voluntary action]]. The voluntary is central to Bergson's argument for [[will|free will]] because it is the domain in which consciousness most clearly exercises its causal power.

## Bergson's Argument

**Voluntary [[attention]] as conscious effort.** Bergson describes the "[[motion|movement]]s which are char[[action|act]]eristic of [[attention|voluntary attention]]" [001-076], noting that a "f[[action|act]]or will always enter into [[attention|voluntary attention]], even if it be nothing more than the muscular [[sensation]]s accompanying it" [001-078]. This grounds [[attention|voluntary attention]] in bodily experience rather than pure [[intelligence|intellect]].

**Voluntary [[action|act]]ivity and [[dynamism]].** Bergson connects voluntary activity to [[dynamism]], which "derives from voluntary activity" [001-202]. Unlike [[mechanical|mechanism]], which explains [[action|act]]ion through external causes, [[dynamism]] starts from "the idea of voluntary activity, given by consciousness" [003-208]. This makes voluntary activity the experiential basis for [[mind|understanding]] [[free-will|freedom]].

**The geometry of [[action|voluntary action]].** Bergson critiques the "geometrical representation of voluntary activity" [001-271], arguing that representing [[action|voluntary action]] as a path through [[homogeneity|homogeneous space]] (Μ Ο) [003-299] misrepresents its temporal char[[action|act]]er. "This [[idea|conception]] of voluntary activity does not satisfy common sense" [003-293] because it spatializes what is essentially a temporal [[becoming|process]].

**Voluntary determination as distinct from intensity and duration.** In his concluding taxonomy, Bergson lists "intensity, duration, voluntary determination" [003-417] as the three fundamental aspects of psychic states. The "[[idea|ideas]] of duration and voluntary determination" [003-425] together constitute Bergson's positive account of [[free-will|freedom]] against determinism.
