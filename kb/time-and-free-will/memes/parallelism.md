# Parallelism (parallelisme)

Parallelism (or psychophysical parallelism) is the doctrine that [[consciousness|mental states]] and [[matter|physical states]] form two parallel series that correspond point-for-point without causal inter[[action|act]]ion between them. Bergson treats this position — historically associated with [[spinoza|Spinoza]] and developed in 19th-century [[psychophysics]] — as an inherently unstable compromise that collapses either into [[materialism]] or into a recognition of [[duration|real duration]].

## Definition

Parallelism attempts to reconcile the [[consciousness|mental]] and the [[matter|physical]] by denying that either causes the other: they simply run in **lockstep**, like two clocks keeping perfect time without influencing each other:

| Position | Mental–Physical Relation | Bergson's Objection |
|----------|-------------------------|-------------------|
| Materialism | Physical causes mental | Denies [[reality|the real]]ity of [[consciousness]] |
| Spiritualism | Mental causes physical | Unverifiable; projects inner experience outward |
| **Parallelism** | Neither causes the other — correspondence without [[causality|causation]] | Unstable compromise; eventually collapses |
| Bergson's view | [[Quality|Qualitative]] and [[quantity|quantitative]] are different orders, not parallel series | Parallelism's "correspondence" is the [[illusion]] of spatializing duration |

## Bergson's Usage

### The Psychophysical Context

Parallelism emerged from the [[psychophysics|psychophysical]] research program — the attempt to find ex[[action|act]] mathematical correlations between [[sensation|sensory intensity]] and physical stimulus magnitude:

> (003-223) …n, we observe a fairly strict parallelism between the physiological and…

For Bergson, the observed correlations are [[reality|real]] but misinterpreted. When we find a "parallel" between a physical stimulus and a conscious [[sensation]], we have not discovered two separate series but have rather spatialized one end of a single [[duration|durational process]] — translating the [[quality|qualitative]] into the language of [[quantity]].

### The Collapse Argument

Bergson's critique is that parallelism cannot maintain it[[ego|self]] as a stable position. If the two series are truly independent, why do they correspond? Three possible answers:

1. **Pre-established harmony** ([[leibniz|Leibniz]]): God arranged the correspondence — a metaphysical solution that abandons scientific explanation.
2. **Epiphenomenalism**: Consciousness is a by-product of physical [[becoming|process]]es — which collapses into materialism and denies [[reality|the real]]ity of [[free-will|freedom]].
3. **Identity theory**: Mental and physical are two aspects of one underlying [[reality|real]]ity ([[spinoza|Spinoza]]) — which requires a standpoint outside both series that we do not possess.

All three, Bergson implies, evade rather than resolve the problem. The correct move is to recognize that the supposed "parallelism" is an artif[[action|act]] of [[thought|reflective analysis]] — the [[ego|self]] grasped from without, spatialized, rather than lived from within as [[duration]].

### Parallelism and the Measurement Problem

Parallelism inherits the central difficulty of [[psychophysics]]: the attempt to [[quality|measure]] what cannot be [[rhythm|measure]]d. If [[consciousness|conscious states]] are [[quality|qualitative]] and interpenetrating, the very idea of a "parallel" physical series that maps onto them point-for-point presupposes that consciousness can be divided into discrete units — which is precisely what Bergson denies.

## Key Distinctions

### Parallelism vs. Interactionism

Inter[[action|act]]ionism holds that mind and [[matter|body]] causally influence each other. Parallelism denies causal inter[[action|act]]ion while affirming correspondence. Bergson's critique applies to both: inter[[action|act]]ionism cannot explain how [[quality|qualitative]] consciousness could causally affect [[quantity|quantitative]] matter, and parallelism cannot explain why the correspondence exists at all.

### Parallelism vs. Bergson's Duration

The deepest contrast. Parallelism treats the mental and physical as two *parallel lines*, presupposing that both are homogeneous series. [[Duration]] treats the mental as a [[quality|qualitative]] [[multiplicity]] — heterogeneous, interpenetrating — that cannot be lined up point-for-point with any physical series. The parallelism is an [[illusion]] produced by spatializing time.
