## Overview

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) occupies a pivotal position in the conceptual architecture of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*. For Bergson, Spinoza is not merely one philosopher among others but the endpoint of a specific trajectory of [[mind|thought]]: the "first [[idea|conception]] of causality" that begins with [[descartes|Descartes]]' reduction of matter to homogeneous [[extensity|extension]] and culminates in Spinoza's metaphysics of substance. Spinoza's doctrine—that the [[thought|modes of thought]] and the modes of [[extensity|extension]] correspond without ever influencing one another, each expressing in a different language the same eternal truth—is the most rigorous formulation of the deterministic worldview that Bergson's philosophy of duration sets out to challenge.

## Key Ideas

### The Parallelism of Thought and Extension

Spinoza's central metaphysical thesis, as Bergson presents it, is that [[mind|thought]] and [[extensity|extension]] are two attributes of a single substance, expressing the same [[reality|real]]ity in different ways. The modes of each attribute correspond perfectly, but there is no causal inter[[action|act]]ion between them:

> "Spinoza said that the modes of thought and the modes of extension correspond with but never influence one another: they only express in two different languages the same eternal truth." [003-224]

This [[parallelism]] eliminates any genuine inter[[action|act]]ion between mind and [[matter|body]], replacing it with a pre-established correspondence grounded in the unity of substance.

### Causality as Identity in the Absolute

Bergson identifies in Spinoza a specific transformation of the concept of causality. Whereas ordinary experience encounters causal relations as temporal sequences, Spinoza's metaphysics reconceives them as expressions of identity in the absolute:

> "Spinoza maintained that the indefinite series of phenomena, which takes for us the form of a succession in time, was equivalent, in the absolute, to the divine unity: he thus assumed, on the one hand, that the relation of apparent causality between phenomena melted away into a relation of identity in the absolute." [003-370]

What appears to us as a succession of cause and effect is, from the perspective of eternity (*sub specie aeternitatis*), the unfolding of a single timeless truth.

## Bergson's Critique

### The First Conception of Causality

Bergson structures his analysis of causality around a distinction between two [[idea|conception]]s, each leading to a different metaphysical endpoint:

> "This second conception of causality leads to Leibniz as the first led to Spinoza." [003-380]

The first [[idea|conception]] treats the causal relation as one of necessary determination: given the antecedent, the consequent follows with mathematical necessity. When this logic is pushed to its extreme—when the temporal gap between cause and effect is reduced to zero—causality becomes identity, and the world of successive phenomena collapses into Spinoza's single eternal substance. Bergson writes: "as with the first we reached Spinoza. And in both cases we merely push to their extreme limit or formulate with greater precision two half-hearted and confused [[idea|ideas]] of common sense" [003-383].

### The Neglect of Duration

The cost of Spinoza's metaphysical achievement, in Bergson's analysis, is the elimination of duration. By dissolving [[succession|temporal succession]] into eternal identity, Spinoza's system "cannot bind future to present without neglecting duration" [003-368]. The [[reality|real]] passage of time—the creative evolution in which genuine novelty emerges—has no place in a metaphysics where everything that happens is already contained, from the standpoint of eternity, in the divine substance.

### From Descartes to Spinoza

Bergson presents Spinoza as the necessary consequence of Cartesian premises. If matter is nothing but homogeneous extensity ([[descartes|Descartes]]), then the [[motion|movement]]s of its parts can be described by abstr[[action|act]] laws but cannot be represented concretely. The logical progression runs: "It thus leads to [[descartes|Descartes]]' physics and Spinoza's metaphysics, but cannot bind future to present without neglecting duration" [003-368]. Spinoza's metaphysics is, in this sense, the truth of [[descartes|Descartes]]' physics—the complete working-out of what is implicit in the mechanist worldview.

### Modes, Causality, and Apparent Succession

Bergson draws [[attention]] to Spinoza's treatment of the apparent succession of phenomena in time. The indefinite series of modes that we experience as temporal is, for Spinoza, equivalent to the eternal unity of substance [003-370]. The causal relation that seems to bind successive phenomena—that B follows A—is in [[reality|real]]ity a relation of identity seen from the limited perspective of a finite mode. Bergson's objection is not that this view is incoherent but that it extr[[action|act]]s from experience only what is spatial and logical, discarding precisely the temporal dimension that makes experience what it is.
