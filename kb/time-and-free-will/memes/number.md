# Number

Number, for Bergson, is not a pure concept of the [[mind|understanding]] but a **spatial construction**. Every number is a collection of units, and to count these units — to hold them together as a sum — we must place them side by side in a **[[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]**. That medium is [[space]]. The idea of number thus reveals, in miniature, Bergson's central thesis: that quantity, even in its most abstr[[action|act]] form, presupposes spatial [[intuition]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Number as a Synthesis of Units

> [001-005] When we assert that one number is greater than another number…

To form a number, we must first posit **distinct units** and then **synthesize** them into a whole. But distinctness presupposes juxtaposition — and juxtaposition is a spatial operation. Even when we count "in our head," we implicitly arrange the units in space:

> [001-009] …in the natural series of numbers, the later number exceeds the earlier…

Each successive number adds a unit to the preceding one, and this addition presupposes that the units are **external to one another**. Externality is the defining feature of space. Number, therefore, is spatial at its core.

### The Implicit Spatiality of Counting

The f[[action|act]] that we can count at all shows that we are already operating within a spatial framework. To say "five" is to hold five units together simultaneously — and simultaneity of distinct items is possible only in space:

> [001-015] …by the number and magnitude of the objective…

Bergson emphasizes that number applies to **objects** — things that can be separated and counted. In pure [[duration]], where states interpenetrate without mutual externality, there is no counting, because there are no separate items to count.

### Number and the Confusion of Quality with Quantity

Number is the paradigm case of what Bergson calls the **spatialization** of conscious life. We count [[sensation]]s, [[emotion|feeling]]s, and states as if they were objects in space. This is the error that generates the pseudo-problems of psychology — treating [[intensity]] as magnitude and [[multiplicity]] as numerical multiplicity.

> [001-025] …the larger or smaller number of simple states which make up…

When psychology talks about the "number" of psychic states composing an [[feeling|emotion]], it is already spatializing what is, in [[reality|real]]ity, a [[quality|qualitative]] [[interpenetration|fusion]]. The [[feeling|emotion]] is not made of parts — it is a whole that changes [[quality|qualitative]]ly, not [[quantity|quantitative]]ly.

## Key Distinctions

### Numerical Multiplicity vs. Qualitative Multiplicity

Bergson distinguishes between two kinds of multiplicity: **numerical** (spatial) and **[[quality|qualitative]]** (durational). Numerical multiplicity is what we find in number and in objects distributed in space. Qualitative multiplicity is what we find in [[consciousness|conscious states]] — distinct but not separate, many but not countable.

### Number vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]] is a quality that we mistakenly treat as a quantity. When we say a pain is "greater" than another, we are importing the schema of number into [[reality|the real]]m of quality. But intensity is not magnitude — it is [[change|qualitative change]].

## Historical Context

[[kant|Kant]] treated number as grounded in the *a priori* form of time (the successive addition of units). Bergson agrees that number involves succession but argues that the **simultaneous retention** of counted units — the synthesis that makes a sum possible — requires space, not time. Number is the clearest proof that our [[intelligence|intellect]]ual operations are spatial through and through.
