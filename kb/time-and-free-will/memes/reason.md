# Reason

Reason (*la raison*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the faculty that **spatializes** whatever it touches. It operates by breaking experience into discrete terms, setting them side by side in an ideal [[space]], and linking them by laws. This makes [[mind|reason]] supremely effective in the physical sciences, where its [[analysis|analyse]]s correspond to the mutually external char[[action|act]]er of material objects. But when [[mind|reason]] is applied to [[consciousness]] — to the interpenetrating states of [[duration]] — it systematically distorts its object.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Method of Reason

Bergson char[[action|act]]erizes [[mind|reason]] by its method: analysis, comparison, and the establishment of relations between **distinct terms**:

> [003-002] … seems to us, the flaw in his reasoning lies.

When Bergson identifies "the flaw in his reasoning," he is not critiquing a particular argument but a **mode of [[mind|thought]]**. The flaw is structural: reasoning about consciousness as if it were composed of distinct, mutually external states. The flaw "lies" not in the conclusion but in the very terms in which the problem is posed.

### Reason and Identity

> [003-004] …onymous with identity. But the reason is that every phenomenon, even…

Bergson notes that [[mind|reason]] tends to collapse causality into identity — the cause is said to "contain" the effect, or the effect is the cause "in another form." This is because [[mind|reason]] can only handle relations between **discrete terms** that it first separates and then reconnects. Identity is the simplest such relation; it reassures [[mind|reason]] by making change illusory. But in duration, change is [[reality|real]], and identity is the illusion.

### Reason and Quantity

> [003-013] … and S' alone were given; the reason is that the number S'—S, which…

Bergson dissects the mathematical representation of psychic states. "The [[mind|reason]] is that the number S'–S" — [[mind|reason]]'s explanation of a [[quality|qualitative]] transition in terms of a [[quantity|quantitative difference]] — is a substitution of the symbol for [[reality|the real]]ity. Reason can only handle quantities, so it transforms qualities into quantities before [[becoming|process]]ing them. This transformation is not neutral; it changes the nature of what is being studied.

### Reason and Psychophysics

> [003-018] …e or less confused process of reasoning interprets as such, we shall…

The [[psychophysics]] of [[Fechner]] is Bergson's prime example of [[mind|reason]] overreaching. The psychophysicist's "more or less confused [[becoming|process]] of reasoning" takes a subject's comparative judgment ("this [[sensation]] seems twice as intense") and treats it as a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of a psychic magnitude. But the judgment is not a [[rhythm|measure]]ment; it is a **construction of [[mind|reason]]** that spatializes what was originally [[quality|qualitative]].

### Practical Reason vs. Philosophical Reason

> [P01-008] …were to inquire of Nature the reason of her creative activity, and…

Bergson distinguishes between two uses of "[[mind|reason]]." In pr[[action|act]]ical life and natural science, [[mind|reason]]'s spatializing operations are legitimate and effective — they correspond to the nature of material objects. But when philosophy "inquires of Nature the [[mind|reason]] of her creative [[action|act]]ivity" — when it demands explanations of consciousness, [[free-will|freedom]], and duration in rational terms — it asks [[mind|reason]] to operate outside its proper domain. The result is the pseudo-problems that dominate metaphysics.

| Operation of Reason | What It Does | Problem for Philosophy |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| [[analysis|Analysis]] | Breaks wholes into parts | Duration is not composed of parts |
| Comparison | Sets terms side by side | Conscious states are not mutually external |
| Quantification | Transforms qualities into quantities | [[quality|Quality]] is the essence of consciousness |
| Causal explanation | Links distinct terms by laws | In duration, states interpenetrate, not link |
| Identification | Reduces change to identity | Duration is [[reality|real]] change |

### The Reason Why

Bergson frequently uses "the [[mind|reason]] is" as a transitional phrase — but he uses it to **undermine** rational explanations:

> [001-037] …nts are wanting in grace, the reason is that each of them is self-contained…

> [001-044] …han the sounds of nature, the reason is that nature confines itself to…

> [002-006] … the form of a magnitude, the reason probably is that it retains something…

> [002-054] This is the reason why the change in the sensation…

In each case, "the [[mind|reason]]" is not a rational deduction but a **phenomenological description** — an appeal to the [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er of experience, not to logical necessity. Bergson appropriates the language of [[mind|reason]] to demonstrate its limits.

## Key Distinctions

### Reason vs. Intuition

[[Intuition]] is the direct grasp of duration from within; [[mind|reason]] is the external analysis of spatialized representations. [[intuition|Intuition]] knows the whole before the parts; [[mind|reason]] knows the parts and constructs the whole. Bergson does not reject [[mind|reason]] — he **delimits** it. Reason is valid in its domain (the material world); it becomes misleading when it claims jurisdiction over consciousness.

### Reason vs. Instinct

[[Instinct]] operates within life, grasping its object immediately; [[mind|reason]] operates outside life, analyzing its object into discrete terms. The "[[instinct|confused instinct]]" Bergson describes at [003-175] is closer to the truth of duration than the clearest rational analysis — because instinct does not spatialize.

### Reason and Reflection

[[Reflection]], in Bergson's sense, is [[mind|reason]] **turned against its own habits**. It is [[mind|reason]] recognizing its limits and making the "strenuous effort" to suspend its spatializing operations. Philosophical reflection is the paradoxical [[action|act]] of using [[mind|reason]] to transcend [[mind|reason]].

## Historical Context

Bergson's critique of [[mind|reason]] places him in a line of French thinkers — from **Pascal** (*le cœur a ses raisons que la raison ne connaît point*) through **Rousseau** to **Maine de Biran** — who distinguished between rational analysis and a deeper mode of knowing. But Bergson is not an irrationalist: he does not reject [[mind|reason]] wholesale. He inherits the [[kant|Kant]]ian project of determining the **limits** of [[mind|reason]] while rejecting [[kant|Kant]]'s conclusion that these limits are the limits of knowledge it[[ego|self]]. For Bergson, what lies beyond [[mind|reason]] (duration, [[free-will|freedom]], the [[ego|self]]) is not unknowable — it is knowable by [[intuition]].
