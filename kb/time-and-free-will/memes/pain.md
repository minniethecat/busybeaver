# Pain

Pain (*douleur*) in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is not a [[magnitude|measurable quantity]] but a **[[quality|qualitative]] state of consciousness**. What we call the "intensity" of a pain is, upon analysis, not a magnitude that can be greater or less — it is the **extent of the [[body|organism]]** involved in the painful sensation. Bergson's analysis of pain is a crucial step in his broader argument that psychic [[intensity]] is never [[quantity|quantitative]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Pain as Qualitative, Not Quantitative

Bergson opens his analysis of intensity with the example of pain:

> [001-015] …we experience a more intense pain at the pulling out of a tooth than of a hair…

The common-sense view treats this as a [[quantity|quantitative difference]]: more pain vs. less pain. But Bergson argues that what we [[action|act]]ually experience is a **[[quality|qualitative difference]]** between two distinct [[sensation]]s, each involving different parts of the [[body|organism]] in different ways.

### Pain and the Extent of the Organism

The apparent intensity of a pain is determined by the **extent of the [[matter|body]] surface** affected:

> [002-014] Intensity of a pain estimated by extent of organism affected.

When a tooth is pulled, the [[sensation]] involves nerve endings, [[tension|muscular tension]], and anticipatory anxiety — a much larger portion of the [[body|organism]] than the plucking of a single hair. What we call "greater pain" is [[action|act]]ually the involvement of a **greater surface of the [[matter|body]]**, not a greater quantity of the same quality.

### Pain and Fatigue

Bergson connects pain to [[effort|muscular effort]] and fatigue:

> [001-072] …fatigue, and the fatigue pain. Yet the subject will imagine…

> [001-078] …becomes pressure, fatigue and pain.

Muscular fatigue produces a distinctive kind of pain, but this pain is not a separate [[sensation]] added to the fatigue. The fatigue **becomes** pain — a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation of the same conscious state, not the addition of a new magnitude.

### Pain and Pleasure as Signs

Bergson treats both pain and [[pleasure]] as **signals** rather than [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]s:

> [002-007] Pleasure and pain as signs of the future reaction…

> [002-008] …be asked whether pleasure and pain, instead of expressing only what has occurred…

Pain is not a subjective magnitude corresponding to a physical stimulus. It is a **signal** that the [[body|organism]] uses to anticipate and organize its future re[[action|act]]ions. The "intensity" of the signal is proportional to the extent of the [[body|organism]] that must be mobilized in response.

### The Affective Sensation

Bergson examines whether pain can be analyzed as a simple [[sensation]]:

> [002-003] …ve sensation, a pleasure or a pain, consist?

> [002-006] …the sensation of pleasure or pain, it remains unconscious as motor tendency…

The affective quality of pain is not a sensory content but a **motor tendency** — an [[tendency|inclination]] to [[action|act]], to withdraw, to protect. Consciousness of pain is consciousness of this tendency made explicit. The transition from unconscious motor tendency to conscious pain is a [[change|qualitative change]], not a [[quantity|quantitative]] increase.

| Aspect | Common View | Bergson's View |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| Nature of pain | Measurable magnitude | Qualitative state |
| "More" pain | Greater quantity | Larger [[body|organism]] surface affected |
| Pain and [[matter|body]] | [[body|Body]] reports pain | Pain signals future re[[action|act]]ion |
| Pain vs. pleasure | Opposites on a scale | Distinct [[quality|qualitative]] signals |

## Key Distinctions

### Pain vs. Intensity

Pain is often treated as the paradigmatic case of a [[sensation]] that varies in intensity. Bergson's analysis shows that even pain — seemingly the most [[quantity|quantitative]] of [[sensation]]s — is [[action|act]]ually a [[quality|qualitative difference]]. The "intensity" of pain dissolves into the **extent of the [[body|organism]]** involved and the **[[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er** of the associated motor tendencies.

### Pain and the Body

Pain is not "in" the [[matter|body]] as a property of tissues. It is a relation between the [[body|organism]] and its environment, a signal that mobilizes the whole [[matter|body]] for a response. When we locate pain "in" a [[matter|body]] part, we are projecting a conscious state onto a spatial location — another instance of the **spatialization of consciousness**.
