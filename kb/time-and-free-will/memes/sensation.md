# Sensation

Sensation is the primary datum of conscious experience and the starting point of Bergson's critique of **[[quantity|quantitative]] psychology** in Chapter I of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*. Against the [[psychophysics]] [[motion|movement]], Bergson argues that sensations are **[[quality|qualitative]]**, not [[quantity|quantitative]]: to say one sensation is "more intense" than another is to project a spatial comparison onto an experience that has no [[quantity|measurable magnitude]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Sensation as Qualitative, Not Quantitative

Bergson opens *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* by interrogating the common-sense notion that sensations can be "more" or "less" intense:

> [001-003] …there can be no harm in speaking of one sensation as being more intense than another…

But this seemingly innocent language, he argues, conceals a cognitive error. When we call a sensation "more intense," we are **spatializing** it — imagining it as occupying more [[space]], as if sensations were extended magnitudes like objects:

> [001-007] But how can a more intense sensation contain one of less intensity? …in both cases we allude to unequal spaces.

### The Psychophysical Confusion

The psychophysicists ([[Fechner]], [[Weber]]) claimed that sensation intensity could be **[[rhythm|measure]]d** — that a mathematical relationship ([[fechner|Fechner]]'s Law) connects physical stimulus magnitude to conscious sensation magnitude. Bergson's critique is that this assumes sensations have **quantities** in the first place:

| Approach | View of Sensation | Method |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| Psychophysics | Sensation has [[quantity|measurable magnitude]] | Stimulus-response [[rhythm|measure]]ment |
| Bergson | Sensation is purely [[quality|qualitative]] | Introspective analysis of consciousness |

The apparent "intensity" of a sensation is [[action|act]]ually a **[[change|qualitative change]]** — a different *kind* of experience, not a different *amount*:

> [001-029] But that is a change of quality rather than of magnitude.

## Key Distinctions

### Sensation vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]] is the apparent magnitude of a conscious state. Bergson's analysis reveals that what we call intensity is either (a) the **[[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er** of the sensation it[[ego|self]] (in the case of deep [[emotion|feeling]]s) or (b) the **[[effort|muscular effort]]** associated with the sensation (in the case of surface sensations tied to bodily [[action|act]]ion). There is no purely psychic magnitude.

### Sensation vs. Perception

[[perception|Perception]], for Bergson, involves the projection of sensation onto [[space]] — the interpretation of sensory data through spatial cat[[self|ego]]ries. Sensation is the raw [[quality|qualitative]] state; perception is sensation filtered through the pr[[action|act]]ical demands of [[action|act]]ion and the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s spatial habits.

### Muscular Sensation

A key element of Bergson's analysis is the role of **muscular sensation** — the bodily [[emotion|feeling]] of effort that accompanies many [[consciousness|conscious states]]. When we say a light is "more intense," we often mean that our eyes **strain more** to look at it. The [[quantity|quantitative]] language smuggles in a bodily, spatial referent.
