# Determinism

Determinism, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not simply false — it is **incoherent** when applied to conscious life. The determinist treats psychic states as if they were physical objects arranged in [[space]], each one causing the next according to fixed laws. But in [[duration]], [[consciousness|conscious states]] **interpenetrate**; they are not discrete items that could be linked by causal chains. Bergson's argument is not that determinism is empirically wrong, but that it is **badly posed**: it applies a spatial schema to what is essentially temporal.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Spatial Assumption Behind Determinism

For Bergson, every form of determinism — whether physical, psychological, or associationist — rests on the same unconscious spatialization of consciousness:

> [001-134] …of freedom, leads to determinism…

The determinist pictures the mind as a theater in which discrete states succeed one another like objects in [[space]]. Once conscious life is spatialized in this way, [[causality|causal connection]] between states seems inevitable — for in space, things are mutually external and can be linked by laws.

> [001-149] …determinism rests on inaccurate conception…

But this picture falsifies the very nature of conscious experience. In [[reality|real]] [[duration]], states do not follow one another as separate items; they melt into one another, each permeated by the whole of the past.

### Why Psychological Determinism Fails

The psychological determinist — represented in Bergson's time by the **associationist school** — holds that every conscious state is determined by preceding states through [[association|laws of association]]:

> [001-108] …associationist determinism…

Bergson replies that association it[[ego|self]] presupposes the spatialization he has already criticized. [[idea|Idea]]s can be "associated" only if they are first conceived as **distinct atoms** that can be externally linked. But in the flow of consciousness, [[idea|ideas]] are never atomically distinct; they form a continuous [[multiplicity]] in which each element bears the color of the whole.

| Form of Determinism | Spatial Assumption | Bergson's Reply |
|---------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Physical determinism | Mental states = physical states in [[space]] | Conscious states are [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]] |
| Psychological determinism | States linked by association laws | [[association|Association]] requires pre-spatialized atoms |
| Causal determinism | Every event has a sufficient cause in prior events | In [[duration]], past does not mechanically determine future |

### Determinism and the "Strongest Motive"

The common-sense version of determinism holds that we always [[action|act]] on our **strongest motive**. Bergson dismantles this by showing that motives do not have measurable intensities:

> [001-116] …this does not involve determinism…

To speak of a "stronger" motive is to treat motives as forces with magnitudes — a [[quantity|quantitative]] [[idea|conception]] that applies only in [[space]]. In consciousness, motives are **[[quality|qualitative]] states** that interpenetrate. One does not win by strength; the [[self|whole self]] [[action|act]]s, expressing its entire history.

## Key Distinctions

### Determinism vs. Causality in Duration

Bergson does not deny that conscious life has continuity and coherence. But the connection between states in [[duration]] is **[[quality|qualitative]]**, not causal in the mechanical sense. Each moment grows out of the whole past, but not in a way that could have been predicted or deduced from that past:

> [001-182] …successive phases in, leads to determinism…

The determinist demands that the connection be spatial — a link between separate items. Bergson shows that [[reality|the real]] connection is **organic**: the [[ego|self]] matures as a whole, and its [[action|act]]s are expressions of that whole, not mechanical results of prior partial states.

### Determinism vs. Free Will

The determinism–free-will debate is Bergson's central target in Chapter III. He argues that both sides share the same error:

> [001-214] …how determinism aided by…

The determinist says: "Every [[action|act]] is determined by prior states." The libertarian says: "No, the [[action|act]] could have been otherwise." Bergson says: **both spatialize the [[action|act]]**, treating it as a point on a line that either does or does not have a determinate predecessor. The [[reality|real]] solution is to abandon the spatial metaphor entirely and think in duration.

### Determinism and Psychophysics

The [[psychophysics]] [[motion|movement]] — led by [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]] — gave determinism a scientific veneer by claiming to **[[rhythm|measure]]** psychic states. If [[sensation]]s have measurable intensities, then the "stronger" [[sensation]] always wins, and [[will|free will]] is an illusion:

> [001-224] …molecular theory of, and determinism…

Bergson's entire analysis of [[intensity]] (Chapter I) undermines this premise, showing that [[psychophysics|psychophysical measurement]] confuses [[quality]] with [[quantity]].

## Historical Context

### The Nineteenth-Century Determinism Debate

Bergson wrote in the aftermath of a century-long debate about determinism. [[Kant]] had resolved the tension by relegating [[free-will|freedom]] to the noumenal [[reality|real]]m — we are free as things-in-ourselves, determined as appearances. But this made [[free-will|freedom]] unknowable.

Nineteenth-century science intensified the challenge. Advances in physics (Laplace's demon), biology ([[darwin|Darwin]]), and psychology ([[association|associationism]], [[psychophysics]]) seemed to close the space for [[free-will|freedom]]. Bergson's originality lay in challenging not the scientific data but the **metaphysical assumptions** — particularly the assumption that time is homogeneous and measurable — that made determinism seem inevitable.

### Lange and Materialism

> [001-213] Lange, on materialism and determinism…

F. A. Lange's *History of Materialism* (1866) documented how materialist determinism had become the default worldview of modern science. Bergson acknowledged the force of this tradition but argued it rested on a con[[interpenetration|fusion]] between **time and space**.
