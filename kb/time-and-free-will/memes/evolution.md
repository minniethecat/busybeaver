# Evolution

## Overview / Definition

Bergson uses "evolution" in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* not as a biological theory but as a **model for [[mind|understanding]] [[change|qualitative change]]** in consciousness. Unlike mechanical change (which is reversible and decomposable), evolution is "an organic evolution which is yet not an increasing" [003-427] — it is continuous, irreversible, and expresses the whole of what evolves. This concept is crucial to Bergson's account of [[will|free will]], where the [[ego|self]] "evolves" toward its [[choice|decision]]s through an organic, non-mechanical [[becoming|process]].

## Bergson's Argument

**Evolution as non-mechanical change.** Bergson distinguishes organic evolution from mechanical [[becoming|process]]es. Evolution "marks a certain phase in the evolution of certain sciences" [003-233] but its philosophical significance lies in providing an alternative to [[mechanical|mechanism]]. Where [[mechanical|mechanism]] explains change through external causes [[action|act]]ing on fixed elements, evolution describes change as the unfolding of an internal principle.

**The [[ego|self]] evolves through deliberation.** In his analysis of [[will|free will]], Bergson argues that "all the time that the deliberation is going on, the [[ego|self]] is changing" — a [[becoming|process]] that "will lead by a natural evolution to a free [[action|act]]" [003-278]. The [[ego|self]] does not calculate; it grows. The free [[action|act]] is the "continuous evolution of a free person" [003-437], not the output of a [[choice|decision]] procedure.

**Evolution and interpenetration.** Bergson connects evolution to the deeper metaphysical theme of "interpenetration" — the way [[consciousness|conscious states]] "permeate one another" rather than being externally related. "The continuous evolution of a free person" expresses the f[[action|act]] that "duration, the heterogeneous, is that which" [003-437] char[[action|act]]erizes genuine temporal being. Evolution is thus duration made manifest in [[action|act]]ion.

**Evolution as organic, not additive.** Bergson describes it as "an organic evolution which is yet not an increasing" [003-427] — it is not the mere accumulation of new parts. Like a living [[body|organism]], the evolving [[ego|self]] changes as a whole, each moment [[quality|qualitative]]ly transforming what came before rather than simply adding to it.
