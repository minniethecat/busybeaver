# Desire

Desire (*désir*) in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is a central case study in the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of psychic states. What we call the "growth" or "intensification" of a desire is, upon analysis, not the addition of more of the same but a **progressive transformation** of the whole psychic field. Desire develops by **changing in kind**, not by increasing in degree.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Progress of a Desire

Bergson opens his analysis of desire as an example of apparent [[quantity|quantitative]] growth:

> [001-026] …for example, the progress of a desire.

> [001-027] For example, an obscure desire gradually becomes a deep passion…

A desire begins as a faint [[tendency|inclination]] — barely distinguishable from other states. As it "grows," it reorganizes the entire psychic field, drawing more and more of [[consciousness]] into its orbit. But this "growth" is not the addition of homogeneous units. Each stage of the desire's development is a **[[quality|qualitative]]ly distinct state**, not a larger quantity of the same quality.

> [001-028] …remaining identical, such and such a desire has gone up a scale of magnitude…

The language of "going up a scale" is precisely what Bergson challenges. The desire does not climb a ladder of intensity — it **transforms**, and we retrospectively impose a spatial scale on a [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|process]].

### Desire and the Body

Bergson traces the bodily basis of desire's apparent intensity. Like other [[feeling|affective state]]s, desire engages the [[body|organism]]:

> [001-074] …s of joy, sorrow, passion and desire. Let us show briefly that the…

> [001-080] …sort of psychic tension: acute desire, uncontrolled anger, passionate love…

What we call the "intensity" of an acute desire is the **[[tension|muscular tension]]** that accompanies it — the bodily preparation for [[action|act]]ion. The desire that "burns" is the desire that mobilizes the whole [[matter|body]]. Its intensity is not a magnitude of the desire it[[ego|self]] but the extent of the [[body|organism]]'s involvement.

### Desire as Qualitative Transformation

Bergson insists that desire, like all [[feeling|emotion]]s, develops through [[quality|qualitative]] stages:

> [001-084] …degrees of joy and sorrow, of desire, aversion and even shame, the…

> [001-086] …To say that love, hatred, desire, increase in violence is to assert…

To say that a desire "increases in violence" is to use a spatial and [[quantity|quantitative]] metaphor. What [[action|act]]ually happens is that the desire passes through distinct [[quality|qualitative]] phases, each involving different elements of the psychic whole. The "violent" desire is not the "mild" desire amplified — it is a different state.

### Desire and the Will

Desire connects to the problem of [[free-will]] through the analysis of motives:

> [003-252] And a little further on: "His desire to do right and his aversion to doing wrong…"

The determinist treats desires as forces with measurable strengths — the "strongest" desire wins and determines the [[action|act]]ion. But Bergson argues that desires, being [[quality|qualitative]] states, cannot be compared in strength. The [[action|act]] that follows from a desire is not determined by the desire's "magnitude" but expresses the **whole [[quality|qualitative]] [[ego|self]]** at that moment. The free [[action|act]] is not the [[action|act]] that follows from the strongest desire; it is the [[action|act]] that expresses the entire personality.

### The Insatiable Desire

Bergson uses the image of insatiable desire to critique the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s drive to separate and analyze:

> [003-178] …goaded by an insatiable desire to separate, substitutes the…

The [[intelligence|intellect]] has an "insatiable desire" to divide, separate, and spatialize. This desire is never satisfied because it is directed at a task that has no end — the [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]] can always be further divided, but each division produces artif[[action|act]]s, not [[mind|understanding]]. The desire to separate is the desire of the superficial [[ego]], not the [[self|deep self]].

|--------|------------|----------------|
| Growth of desire | Quantitative increase | Qualitative transformation |
| "Violent" desire | More of the same desire | A different state involving more of the [[body|organism]] |
| Desire and [[action|act]]ion | Strongest desire wins | Whole [[ego|self]] expresses it[[ego|self]] |
| Bodily basis | Desire causes bodily changes | Bodily changes ARE the desire's "intensity" |

## Key Distinctions

### Desire vs. Emotion

Desire and [[emotion]] are closely related but distinct in Bergson's analysis. An [[feeling|emotion]] (joy, sorrow) is a [[quality|qualitative]] state that colors the whole psychic field. A desire has an additional **vector** — it is oriented toward a future object or state. But both share the same [[quality|qualitative]] nature: their apparent intensity is [[reality|real]]ly [[quality|qualitative]] transformation.

### Desire and Pleasure

Desire and [[pleasure]] are different moments of the same [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|process]]. [[pleasure|Pleasure]] is the consciousness of a tendency fulfilled; desire is the consciousness of a tendency in suspense. Neither is a quantity; both are [[quality|qualitative]] signals. The "intensity" of a desire is not proportional to the pleasure of its [[pleasure|satisfaction]] — this would be to compare two incommensurable qualities.

### Desire and the Deep Self

The [[self|superficial self]] experiences desires as discrete, competing forces — "I desire X more than Y." The [[self|deep self]] experiences desire as a continuous, [[quality|qualitative]] orientation that involves the [[personality|whole personality]]. The free [[action|act]] does not express the "strongest" desire but the **totality** of the [[self|deep self]], of which desire is one aspect among many.
