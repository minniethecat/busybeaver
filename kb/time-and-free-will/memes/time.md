# Time

Time, in Bergson's *Time and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not what we naively suppose it to be. What we ordinarily call "time" — the [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] [[rhythm|measure]]d by clocks, divided into equal minutes and hours — is, Bergson argues, a **hybrid concept**: a spatial representation imposed on the [[quality|qualitative]] flow of [[duration]]. Real time is not a container in which events occur; it is the very **texture of conscious life**, a continuous interpenetration in which the past lives on in the present and the future is genuinely open.

## Bergson's Argument

### Homogeneous Time as a Spatial Construct

Bergson's central claim about time is that measurable, homogeneous time is not a direct datum of consciousness but an [[intelligence|intellect]]ual construction:

> [P01-007] …a homogeneous and measurable time is shown to be an artificial…

When we think of time as a uniform medium divisible into equal parts, we are **projecting space** onto our inner experience. The clock does not [[rhythm|measure]] [[duration|real duration]]; it counts simultaneous positions of hands on a dial — that is, it counts **space**. We then interpret these spatial simultaneities as [[rhythm|measure]]s of time.

> [001-030] …appears to us at the same time under a multitude of forms…

Consciousness experiences time not as a series of discrete instants but as a [[quality|qualitative]] flow in which each moment is colored by the whole past. The [[intelligence|intellect]], shaped by [[space]], cannot grasp this flow directly; it substitutes a spatial schema.

### The Two Times

Bergson distinguishes between:

| Aspect | Homogeneous Time | Real Duration |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Nature | Quantitative, measurable | Qualitative, lived |
| Structure | Discrete instants in series | Continuous interpenetration |
| Relation to space | Projects spatial properties | Independent of space |
| Relation to [[ego|self]] | External framework | The very [[matter|stuff]] of the [[ego|self]] |
| Knowability | Intellectual (analysis) | Intuitive (immediate experience) |

This distinction runs through the entire book. Chapter I shows that [[intensity]] of [[consciousness|conscious states]] cannot be [[rhythm|measure]]d — which means they cannot be placed in homogeneous time. Chapter II develops the contrast between homogeneous time and duration directly. Chapter III applies the distinction to the problem of [[free-will]].

### Why We Confuse Time and Space

Bergson asks why we so habitually confuse time with space:

> [001-047] …of degrees of elevation. Sometimes the feeling which is suggested…

The answer lies in the **pr[[action|act]]ical orientation** of the human [[intelligence|intellect]]. We need to coordinate our [[action|act]]ions with others, to predict and control events, and for these purposes homogeneous time is indispensable. But what is pr[[action|act]]ically useful is not metaphysically true. The philosopher's task is to **recover** the immediate experience of duration beneath the spatial schema that pr[[action|act]]ical life has superimposed.

> [001-038] …sure of mastering the flow of time and of holding the future in…

### Time and the Self

The [[ego|self]], for Bergson, exists in duration, not in homogeneous time:

> [001-050] …down the barrier interposed by time and space between his consciousness…

When we locate our inner life in homogeneous time, we treat our own [[consciousness|conscious states]] as if they were **objects in space** — distinct, measurable, and externally related. But the [[ego|self]] in duration is a [[quality|qualitative]] [[multiplicity]]: its states interpenetrate, each reflecting the whole. The spatialized [[ego|self]] is a **shadow** of [[reality|the real]] [[ego|self]], distorted by the demands of social life and language.

## Key Distinctions

### Time vs. Duration

This is the master distinction of Bergson's philosophy. "Time" (in the ordinary sense) is a **mixed** concept that borrows from both duration and space. Duration is pure [[succession|qualitative succession]]; time is the spatial representation of that succession. To think clearly about [[free-will|freedom]], consciousness, and causality, we must **disentangle** the two.

### Time vs. Space

If time is merely space with an added dimension of succession, then geometry can, in principle, represent it completely. Bergson's entire argument is that this is false. The **[[quality|qualitative]]** aspect of temporal experience — the f[[action|act]] that a minute of boredom and a minute of excitement are not the same "length" — cannot be captured by any spatial representation.

### Time as Practical vs. Time as Real

> [001-060] …watch over this force and from time to time to open a passage for…

Bergson does not deny the pr[[action|act]]ical legitimacy of homogeneous time. Clocks, calendars, and train schedules are perfectly valid for organizing [[action|act]]ion. The error is to mistake this **pr[[action|act]]ical [[habit|convention]]** for a metaphysical description of [[reality|real]]ity. Science uses homogeneous time legitimately; philosophy must go deeper.

## Historical Context

### Newton and Absolute Time

Newton conceived time as an absolute, uniformly flowing medium — a "container" independent of events. Bergson's critique targets not Newton specifically but the deeper assumption that time can be treated as a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] at all. Even relational theories of time ([[leibniz|Leibniz]]) that deny Newton's absolute time still treat temporal relations as spatial relations between instants.

### Kant and Time as a Form of Intuition

[[Kant]] made time a **form of inner [[intuition]]** — a universal, necessary structure of human experience. Bergson agrees that time (as [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]) is contributed by the mind, but he denies that this homogeneous time is the original form of inner experience. For Bergson, [[kant|Kant]]'s "time" is already spatialized; [[kant|Kant]] mistook the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual representation of time for time it[[ego|self]].
