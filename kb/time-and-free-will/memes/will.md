# Will

The will (*la volonté*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not a distinct faculty of the mind that intervenes among motives to select one — that picture, Bergson argues, is a spatial fiction. The will is the **[[self|whole self]] [[action|act]]ing**. A free [[choice|act of will]] is one that expresses the entire history and char[[action|act]]er of the person, not a momentary victory of one motive over another. This redefinition of will is the positive core of Bergson's solution to the [[free-will]] problem.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Problem of Free Will

> [P01-007] …esults to the problem of free will, he shows that the difficulties…

Bergson announces from the Preface that the "problem of free will" is his central target. But his approach is not to argue for or against free will on the terms of the existing debate. Instead, he shows that the debate it[[ego|self]] is **badly posed** — both determinists and libertarians spatialize the [[choice|act of will]], treating it as a point-event to which causal relations either do or do not apply.

> [P02-002] …ychology, the problem of free will. What I attempt to prove is that…

Bergson's thesis: the difficulties surrounding free will arise from the con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of [[duration]] with [[space]]. Once we grasp [[duration|real duration]], the problem of free will dissolves — not because we have solved it on its own terms, but because we have recognized that those terms were incoherent.

### The Will as the Whole Self

The central move is to identify the will not with a **part** of the [[ego|self]] (the deliberative faculty, the rational power of choice) but with the **[[self|whole self]]** in its temporal depth:

> [001-040] …possession of our thought and will. Thus a kind of physical sympathy…

When a deep [[emotion|feeling]] takes "possession of our [[mind|thought]] and will," it is not one motive overpowering another — it is the **entire [[ego|self]] reorganizing it[[ego|self]]** around a new center. The will is not a muscle that can be strengthened; it is the name for the [[ego|self]]'s capacity to [[action|act]] as a unified whole.

### Will and the Spring Metaphor

Bergson's most vivid image of the free will is the **spring**:

> [001-013] …a spring which, in unwinding, will occupy a greater space. In the…

The spring, when released, does not "choose" a [[tendency|direction]] among several; it **unfolds** according to its own internal tension. Similarly, the free [[action|act]] is not a [[choice|selection]] among pre-existing options but the **unfolding** of the [[ego|self]]'s accumulated history. The spring metaphor captures Bergson's key insight: the will is dynamic, continuous, and undivided — not a point-[[choice|decision]] but a temporal [[becoming|process]].

> [003-433] …se, the dynamic and undivided tension of the spring. Thus, by a rea…

The "dynamic and undivided tension of the spring" is the will — not choosing, but [[action|act]]ing; not deliberating, but expressing.

### The Aesthetic Character of Will

> [001-047] …ery feeling experienced by us will assume an aesthetic character…

Bergson notes that [[emotion|feeling]]s, when detached from pr[[action|act]]ical urgency, "assume an aesthetic char[[action|act]]er." This is true of the will as well: a free [[choice|act of will]] has a kind of aesthetic unity. It is not "useful" in the pr[[action|act]]ical sense (predictable, controllable); it is **expressive**, like a work of art that manifests the creator's entire sensibility.

| Theory of Will | Model | What the Will Does |
|---------------|-------|-------------------|
| [[determinism|Determinism]] | Strongest motive wins | Nothing — motives determine outcome |
| Libertarianism | Faculty of choice among equals | Selects one option from several |
| Bergson | Whole [[ego|self]] expressing it[[ego|self]] | Unfolds organically in duration |

### Will and the Undivided Self

> [001-030] …of these becomes realized, it will be necessary to give up the other…

Bergson describes how, in deliberation, the [[ego|self]] oscillates between different possible selves. The [[choice|act of will]] is the moment when **one of these possible selves becomes [[reality|real]]** — not by winning a contest against others, but by the [[ego|self]] crystallizing into a determinate form. When this happens, "it will be necessary to give up the other" selves — not because they lost, but because the [[ego|self]] is now this and not that.

## Key Distinctions

### Will vs. Choice

[[Choice]] is the spatialized appearance of will — it pictures the will as selecting from a menu of options. Will, in Bergson's sense, is the **temporal [[reality|real]]ity** — the [[ego|self]]'s organic unfolding. [[choice|Choice]] is retrospective; will is prospective. We look back and say "I chose," but in the moment of [[action|act]]ion, there was only the [[ego|self]] [[action|act]]ing.

### Will vs. Desire

[[Desire]] is a partial state of the [[ego|self]] — a motive, a pull toward some object. Will is the **[[self|whole self]]** in [[action|act]]ion. A desire that does not express the [[self|whole self]] is not a free [[choice|act of will]]; it may be an impulse, a habit, or a compulsion. Bergson's distinction between free and unfree [[action|act]]s hinges on whether the [[action|act]] expresses the **entire person** or only a fragment.

### Will and Duration

The will is **temporal through and through**. It is not an instantaneous [[action|act]] but a [[becoming|process]] in [[duration]] — the [[ego|self]] ripening, the spring coiling and then releasing. The spatial picture of will (a point-[[choice|decision]] at a moment) freezes this [[becoming|process]] into a timeless instant, making [[free-will|freedom]] unintelligible.
