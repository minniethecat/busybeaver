## Overview

René Descartes (1596–1650) appears throughout *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* as a pivotal figure in the history of the relationship between physics and metaphysics. Bergson references Descartes not primarily as an epistemologist or the author of the *cogito*, but as the philosopher who most clearly articulated the [[idea|conception]] of matter as homogeneous [[extensity|extension]]—a view whose logical consequences Bergson traces through [[spinoza|Spinoza]] and [[leibniz|Leibniz]] to the problem of [[will|free will]]. Descartes' physics, his [[mind|thought]] experiment of the "mischievous genius," and his account of the conservation of motion form a conceptual lineage that Bergson uses to illuminate the tension between [[determinism|mechanistic determinism]] and [[reality|the real]]ity of duration.

## Key Ideas

### Matter as Homogeneous Extensity

Descartes' identification of matter with [[extensity|extension]]—the doctrine that the essence of [[matter|body]] is *[[extension|res extensa]]*—is, for Bergson, the philosophical origin of a persistent con[[interpenetration|fusion]]. By reducing matter to homogeneous extensity, Descartes made physical phenomena amenable to mathematical treatment but simultaneously eliminated the [[heterogeneity|qualitative heterogeneity]] that char[[action|act]]erizes [[duration|real duration]]:

> "For if matter is nothing, as Descartes claimed, but homogeneous extensity, the movements of the parts of this extensity can be conceived through the abstract law which governs them or through an algebraical equation between variable magnitudes, but cannot be represented under the concrete form of an image." [003-367]

This abstr[[action|act]]ion, Bergson argues, enables prediction but at the cost of losing the concrete temporal char[[action|act]]er of [[reality|real]]ity.

### Conservation of Motion

Descartes' principle of the conservation of the quantity of motion in the universe is treated by Bergson as a precursor to the modern [[idea|conception]] of physical determinism [001-138]. The idea that a fixed sum of motion is redistributed but never created or destroyed embodies the mechanistic view that Bergson subjects to critique. He notes that "it is not certain that all the physicists before [[leibniz|Leibniz]] believed, like Descartes, in the conservation of a fixed quantity of motion in the universe" [003-234], situating Descartes' conservation principle as a contingent historical development rather than a necessary truth.

## Bergson's Critique

### The Mischievous Genius and Duration

Bergson employs Descartes' famous "mischievous genius" [[mind|thought]] experiment to illuminate the nature of duration. In the *Meditations*, Descartes imagines an all-powerful deceiver; Bergson asks us to imagine a genius who "decreed that all the [[motion|movement]]s of the universe should go twice as fast" [003-331]. The point is that such an acceleration would leave all physical equations int[[action|act]]—the symbol *t* would still function—while radically altering the lived experience of duration. This demonstrates that physical time and conscious duration are not the same thing.

### The Path from Descartes to Spinoza

Bergson traces a direct conceptual lineage from Descartes' physics to [[spinoza|Spinoza]]'s metaphysics. Descartes' reduction of matter to homogeneous extensity, when consistently [[mind|thought]] through, "leads to Descartes' physics and [[spinoza|Spinoza]]'s metaphysics, but cannot bind future to present without neglecting duration" [003-368]. The problem is that a universe of homogeneous [[extensity|extension]], governed by necessary laws, leaves no room for the [[quality|qualitative]] novelty that char[[action|act]]erizes temporal experience. Bergson writes that Descartes "understood this so well" [003-370]—that identical antecedents produce identical consequents—precisely the deterministic principle that Bergson's philosophy of duration challenges.

### Providence and Instantaneous Time

In a brief but suggestive reference, Bergson notes that Descartes' [[idea|conception]] of divine providence involves an "instantaneous" view of time [001-277]. For Descartes, God's continuous creation of the world means that each moment exists independently—a [[idea|conception]] that Bergson sees as another expression of the tendency to spatialize time, breaking the continuous flow of duration into discrete instants.
