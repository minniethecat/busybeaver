# Pleasure

Pleasure (*plaisir*) in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is analyzed alongside [[pain]] as an affective quality that resists [[quantity|quantitative]] [[rhythm|measure]]ment. What we call the "intensity" of a pleasure is not a magnitude on a scale but a **[[quality|qualitative]] signal** indicating the [[body|organism]]'s orientation toward a future state. Bergson's analysis of pleasure is part of his broader dismantling of [[psychophysics]] and its attempt to treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as measurable quantities.

## Bergson's Argument

### Hope as Intense Pleasure

Bergson begins his analysis of pleasure with the distinctive case of hope:

> [001-030] …what makes hope such an intense pleasure is the fact that the future, which we dispose of at will, appears to us at the same time under a multitude of forms…

Hope is pleasurable not because it contains a large quantity of some "pleasure-substance," but because it presents the future as **open** — as a field of possibilities that we can arrange at will. The pleasure of hope is the pleasure of [[free-will|freedom]] contemplated in imagination. When the future narrows to a single outcome, hope ceases and the pleasure vanishes — not because a quantity has decreased, but because a quality has transformed.

### Bitter Pleasure and Mixed States

Pleasure is not always pure. Bergson notes that some pleasures carry an admixture of [[effort|struggle]] and even pain:

> [001-035] …struggle, causes us a bitter pleasure.

The "bitter pleasure" is not a compound of pleasure-at-intensity-X plus bitterness-at-intensity-Y. It is a **distinct [[quality|qualitative]] state** — a single, indivisible quality that cannot be decomposed into components without destroying its char[[action|act]]er. This is further evidence that [[feeling|affective state]]s are [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]].

### The Pleasure of Mastering Time

Bergson connects aesthetic pleasure to the mastery of temporal flow:

> [001-038] …the perception of ease in motion passes over into the pleasure of mastering the flow of time…

When we perceive graceful motion — a dancer's [[motion|movement]], a melodic line — the ease of perception becomes a pleasure. This is not a separate effect added to the perception; it is a **continuation** of the same [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]]. The pleasure is the perception of ease becoming conscious of it[[ego|self]].

### The Economy of Attention

Bergson offers a striking analysis of why aesthetic form produces pleasure:

> [001-042] …make out why it affords us such pleasure if it were nothing but a saving of attention…

The regularity of rhythm and rhyme saves us the **effort** of [[attention]]. In ordinary perception, we must constantly adjust and reorient; in aesthetic perception, the form carries us along. But this "saving" is not a [[quantity|quantitative]] economy — it is a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation of consciousness from effortful to effortless, from dispersed to unified.

### Pleasure and Pain as Signs

The most important claim Bergson makes about pleasure is that it is a **sign**, not a magnitude:

> [002-007] Pleasure and pain as signs of the future reaction…

> [002-008] But it might be asked whether pleasure and pain, instead of expressing only what has occurred…

Pleasure does not [[rhythm|measure]] a present state; it **anticipates** a future one. When we feel pleasure at the prospect of food, the pleasure is not a property of the food or of our present physiological condition — it is the [[body|organism]]'s **orientation** toward the [[action|act]] of eating. Pleasure is the consciousness of a motor tendency that inclines us toward a future [[action|act]]ion.

### The Motor Basis of Pleasure

Bergson traces pleasure to its bodily basis:

> [002-006] …translated into the sensation of pleasure or pain, it remains unconscious as motor tendency…

> [002-021] …means of comparing several pleasures with one another. What do we…

> [002-022] When confronted by several pleasures pictured by our mind, our body…

> [002-023] …the attraction of the pleasure is nothing but this movement…

When we "compare pleasures," we are [[action|act]]ually comparing the **motor tendencies** associated with each. The [[matter|body]] leans toward one pleasure and away from another; the attr[[action|act]]ion is not a calculated magnitude but an embodied [[tendency|inclination]]. The "stronger" pleasure is simply the one toward which the [[matter|body]] inclines more fully.

| Aspect | Psychophysical View | Bergson's View |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Nature | Measurable quantity | Qualitative signal |
| Comparison | Pleasure A > Pleasure B on a scale | Different motor [[tendency|inclination]]s |
| Relation to [[matter|body]] | [[body|Body]] reports pleasure magnitude | Pleasure IS the [[matter|body]]'s [[tendency|inclination]] |
| Function | Hedonic magnitude | Sign of future re[[action|act]]ion |

## Key Distinctions

### Pleasure vs. Intensity

Pleasure appears to vary in intensity — a great pleasure vs. a small one. But Bergson argues that what varies is not the "amount" of pleasure but the **extent of the [[body|organism]]** oriented toward the pleasurable object, the **number of faculties** engaged, and the **degree to which the pleasure monopolizes consciousness**. These are [[quality|qualitative difference]]s, not [[quantity|quantitative]] ones.

### Pleasure and the Aesthetic

The [[aesthetic]] pleasure is the clearest case of [[quality|qualitative]] transformation. When a work of art "increases" our pleasure, what it does is introduce new elements that alter the **nature** of the [[emotion|feeling]], not its magnitude. A deeper aesthetic pleasure is not an amplified version of a shallow one — it is a different state entirely.
