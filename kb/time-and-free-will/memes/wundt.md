## Overview

Wilhelm Wundt (1832–1920) is referenced by Bergson in several distinct contexts within *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, reflecting Wundt's broad influence across the nascent field of experimental psychology. As the founder of the first laboratory dedicated to psychological research at Leipzig, Wundt represents the attempt to subject conscious phenomena to experimental [[rhythm|measure]]ment—an enterprise that Bergson engages with critically but not dismissively. Wundt appears in Bergson's discussions of the [[sensation]] of effort, the perception of space, and the relationship between sensory physiology and the experience of intensity.

## Key Ideas

### The Sensation of Effort

Bergson discusses Wundt's observations on the [[sensation]] of [[effort|muscular effort]] in the context of his broader analysis of intensity. Wundt described a "[[sensation]], central in its origin, accompanying the voluntary innervation of the muscles," citing the case of a paralytic patient "who has a very distinct [[sensation]] of the force which he employs in the effort to raise his leg, although it remains motionless" [001-060]. This clinical observation challenges the view that the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is simply the perception of peripheral muscular [[sensation]], suggesting instead a central, neural component.

### Theory of Space Perception

Wundt's theory of space perception is discussed alongside that of [[bain|Alexander Bain]], as one of the major empirical-genetic accounts of how spatial awareness arises from non-spatial [[sensation]]s. Bergson writes:

> "Bain's theory, and the more comprehensive explanation suggested by Wundt, may seem at first quite independent of the Transcendental Aesthetic." [003-107]

Wundt attempted to reconcile competing theories of spatial perception, investigating "by what [[becoming|process]] our [[sensation]]s come to be situated in space and to be set, so to speak, alongside one another" [003-107]. His approach was to trace the genesis of spatial representation from originally unextended, [[quality|qualitative]] [[sensation]]s through a [[becoming|process]] of synthesis.

### Vocal-Auditory Connections

In discussing the intensity of auditory [[sensation]]s, Bergson references Wundt's neuroanatomical finding: "Wundt has drawn [[attention]] to the quite special connexions of vocal and auditory nervous filaments which are met with in the human brain" [002-031]. This observation supports Bergson's point that the perception of sound intensity involves not passive reception but an [[action|act]]ive, motor component—we internally "speak" the sounds we hear, and this motor participation contributes to the [[emotion|feeling]] of intensity.

## Bergson's Critique

### The Limits of Genetic Explanation

Wundt's theory of space perception, along with [[bain|Bain]]'s, is subjected to Bergson's critique of purely empirical accounts of spatial awareness. Bergson acknowledges the value of tracing the psychological and physiological [[becoming|process]]es by which spatial localization occurs, but he identifies a deeper question that these genetic theories leave unaddressed:

> "The conclusion to be drawn from the theories of Lotze and Bain, and from Wundt's attempt to reconcile them, is that the sensations by means of which we come to form the notion of space are themselves unextended and simply qualitative: extensity is supposed to result from their synthesis, as water from the combination of two gases." [003-107]

The problem, for Bergson, is that no synthesis of unextended elements can produce [[extensity|extension]] unless the mind already possesses a spatial frame in which to arrange them. The empirical-genetic approach explains the *content* of spatial perception but not its *form*—the [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] in which [[sensation]]s are situated and distinguished from one another.

### Sensation as Qualitative, Not Quantitative

In Bergson's analysis, Wundt's experimental work, for all its empirical sophistication, operates within a framework that implicitly treats [[sensation]]s as [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]s. The very project of psychophysics—determining the relationship between stimulus intensity and [[sensation]] intensity—presupposes that [[sensation]]s can be compared [[quantity|quantitative]]ly. Bergson's critique of this presupposition, developed at length in his analysis of [[fechner|Fechner]] and [[weber|Weber]], applies equally to Wundt's experimental program: [[sensation]]s are [[quality|qualitative]] states whose interpenetration resists [[rhythm|measure]]ment.

### The Central Sensation Problem

Wundt's appeal to a "central [[sensation]]" accompanying voluntary innervation represents, for Bergson, an acknowledgment that the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort cannot be reduced to afferent feedback from the muscles. However, the notion of a centrally originating [[sensation]] remains theoretically problematic within a strictly empiricist framework. Bergson uses this difficulty not to dismiss Wundt's observations but to motivate his own alternative account: the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is not a separate [[sensation]] at all but an aspect of the [[change|qualitative change]] in consciousness as it passes from one state to another.
