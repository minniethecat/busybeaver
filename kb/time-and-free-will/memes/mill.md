## Overview

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) appears in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* primarily in the third chapter on [[will|free will]], where Bergson engages with Mill's associationist account of moral [[choice|decision]]-making. Mill represents, for Bergson, the empiricist approach to psychic life: the analysis of [[consciousness|conscious states]] into discrete, externally related elements—desires, aversions, motives—whose inter[[action|act]]ions are supposed to explain [[action|act]]ion. Bergson quotes Mill's own [[language|words]] to expose a fundamental con[[interpenetration|fusion]] in the associationist picture: the treatment of [[quality|qualitative]]ly interpenetrating [[consciousness|conscious states]] as though they were distinct things that could be named, counted, and weighed against one another.

## Key Ideas

### Associationist Psychology

Mill's psychology inherits from his father [[james|James]] Mill and from David Hartley the view that complex mental states are built up through the association of simple elements. In the context of moral [[choice|decision]], this means that [[action|act]]ion results from the relative strength of competing desires and aversions. Mill presents these psychic forces as distinct, nameable entities whose conflict determines behavior. This atomistic picture of the mind is precisely what Bergson challenges: [[consciousness|conscious states]], in Bergson's view, are not external to one another but interpenetrate and mutually modify each other.

### The Analysis of Free Will

Mill's analysis of [[will|free will]], quoted by Bergson in Chapter III, is notable for its clarity in stating the associationist position:

> "I could have abstained from murder," says Stuart Mill, "if my aversion to the crime and my dread of its consequences had been weaker than the temptation which impelled me to commit it." [003-252]

Mill frames moral choice as a balance of forces: desire and aversion on one side, temptation on the other. If the balance had been different, the [[action|act]]ion would have been different. Bergson's critique targets not Mill's conclusion but his method—the very [[action|act]] of separating "aversion," "dread," and "temptation" into distinct items misrepresents how these states exist in consciousness.

## Bergson's Critique

### The Mosaic Self

Bergson identifies in Mill's writing a vivid illustration of what he calls the "mosaic" [[idea|conception]] of the [[ego|self]]—the treatment of psychic states as discrete, juxtaposed atoms. Mill writes:

> "His desire to do right and his aversion to doing wrong are strong enough to overcome ... any other desire or aversion which may conflict with them." [003-252]

For Bergson, this language presupposes that desire, aversion, and temptation exist as separate things with independent magnitudes, like weights on a scale. But in lived experience, these states do not merely coexist; they permeate one another. The "aversion to crime" is not a distinct item from the "desire to do right"—both are aspects of a single, indivisible moral [[tendency|disposition]] that cannot be decomposed without falsifying it.

### Hypothetical Action and Determinism

Bergson also quotes Mill's statement on the logic of hypothetical [[action|act]]ion:

> "When we think of ourselves hypothetically as having acted otherwise than we did, we always suppose a difference in the antecedents. We picture ourselves as having known something that we did not know, or not known something that we did know." [003-287]

Mill's point is that every "could have done otherwise" implicitly invokes a change in the antecedent conditions. Bergson acknowledges this as the core of the determinist position: "given certain antecedents, only one resultant [[action|act]]ion was possible" [003-287]. But he defers his full rebuttal to a later point concerning the [[self|ego]]'s [[ego|self]]-perception as a determining cause—a question of whether consciousness reveals it[[ego|self]] as determined or as free.

### The Role of Consciousness

Bergson notes that Mill assigns consciousness "the rôle of informing us about what is, not about what might be" [003-287]. This limitation of consciousness to the f[[action|act]]ual rather than the possible is, for Bergson, char[[action|act]]eristic of the empiricist limitation. Mill's empiricism, by restricting it[[ego|self]] to discrete observable states and their relations, cannot access the dimension of duration in which genuine [[free-will|freedom]] consists—the continuous, [[quality|qualitative]] unfolding in which the [[ego|self]] creates it[[ego|self]] anew at each moment.
