# Moral Feeling

Moral [[emotion|feeling]] (*[[feeling|sentiment]] moral*) in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is the affective dimension of ethical life. Bergson analyzes moral [[emotion|feeling]]s — particularly pity — using the same method he applies to [[aesthetic]] and [[feeling|emotion]]al states: their apparent intensity is not a [[quantity|measurable magnitude]] but a **[[quality|qualitative]] transformation** of the whole soul. The moral [[emotion|feeling]] changes in kind, not in degree.

## Bergson's Argument

### Pity as Paradigm

Bergson selects pity (*pitié*) as the paradigm case of moral [[emotion|feeling]]:

> [001-051] The moral feelings. Pity. Its increasing intensity is a qualitative progress.

> [001-052] The moral feelings might be studied in the same way…

Pity appears to admit of degrees: we can feel "more" or "less" pity. But Bergson argues that what increases is not a quantity of pity but the **extent to which the [[emotion|feeling]] transforms the whole of consciousness**. A mild pity is a distinct [[quality|qualitative]] state from an overwhelming compassion — they are different [[emotion|feeling]]s, not different amounts of the same [[emotion|feeling]].

### The Progress of Pity

Bergson traces the [[quality|qualitative]] stages of pity:

1. **Initial sympathy**: We imagine the other's [[pain]] and recoil from it — a faint echo of the other's [[pain|suffering]] in our own consciousness
2. **Identification**: We put ourselves in the other's place — the boundary between [[ego|self]] and other softens
3. **Aspiration to help**: The [[emotion|feeling]] transforms into an [[tendency|inclination]] to [[action|act]] — pity becomes an [[action|act]]ive orientation

> [001-053] …of others, in suffering their pain. But if it were nothing more…

> [001-054] This painful aspiration nevertheless has…

At each stage, new [[quality|qualitative]] elements enter the [[emotion|feeling]] and transform it. The "intense" pity is not the "mild" pity amplified — it is a [[emotion|feeling]] that has incorporated identification, aspiration, and the anticipation of [[action|act]]ion.

### Moral Feeling and Aesthetic Sympathy

Bergson connects moral [[emotion|feeling]] to [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]] through the concept of **sympathy**:

> [001-040] …through its affinity with moral sympathy, the idea of which is vaguely suggested…

The perception of grace in motion — a dancer's ease, a melodic line's flow — passes over into moral sympathy. The same capacity for immediate, non-calculative fellow-[[emotion|feeling]] that operates in [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]] also operates in moral experience. Both are [[quality|qualitative]], direct, and irreducible to analysis.

### Moral Feeling and the Body

Like other [[feeling|affective state]]s, moral [[emotion|feeling]]s have bodily accompaniments that are mistaken for their intensity:

> [001-084] …degrees of joy and sorrow, of desire, aversion and even shame, the…

The physical [[sensation]]s that accompany moral [[emotion|feeling]]s — the tightening of the throat in pity, the heat of indignation — are not [[rhythm|measure]]s of the [[emotion|feeling]]'s magnitude. They are the **extent of the [[body|organism]]** involved in the moral response. The "intensity" of a moral [[emotion|feeling]] is the degree to which it mobilizes the [[matter|body]].

### Moral Feelings and Free Will

The analysis of moral [[emotion|feeling]]s leads directly to the problem of [[free-will]]. If moral [[emotion|feeling]]s are [[quality|qualitative]] states that interpenetrate and form an [[organic|organic whole]], then moral [[action|act]]ion cannot be explained by the "strongest motive" winning:

> [002-023] …no longer a magnitude. In the moral as in the physical world, attraction…

A moral choice is not a calculation in which motives with different "weights" are compared. It is the expression of the **whole [[quality|qualitative]] [[ego|self]]** at a given moment. The free moral [[action|act]] is the [[action|act]] that expresses the entire personality, not the [[action|act]] determined by the strongest isolated motive.

## Key Distinctions

### Moral Feeling vs. Aesthetic Feeling

Both moral and [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]]s are [[quality|qualitative]], but they differ in their **orientation**. The [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]] is detached — it is contemplated, not [[action|act]]ed upon. The moral [[emotion|feeling]] is **pr[[action|act]]ical** — it inclines toward [[action|act]]ion, toward intervention in the world. But this [[tendency|inclination]] is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]]; it transforms the [[ego|self]] rather than adding a force to it.

### Moral Feeling vs. Moral Judgment

Bergson's analysis of moral [[emotion|feeling]] is not an analysis of moral **judgment** — of how we decide what is right. It is an analysis of the **affective texture** of moral life — of what it feels like to pity, to admire, to be indignant. This affective texture, Bergson argues, is [[quality|qualitative]] through and through.
