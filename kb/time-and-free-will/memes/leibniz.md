## Overview

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) occupies a crucial position in the philosophical architecture of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*. In Bergson's analysis of the relation between states of consciousness and [[extensity|extension]], Leibniz represents the second major form of deterministic metaphysics—the one that treats external qualities as inner, simple states. Where [[spinoza|Spinoza]] pushed the mechanistic [[idea|conception]] of causality to its extreme, Leibniz pushed the idealist [[idea|conception]] to its limit. Bergson's engagement with Leibniz focuses on three related doctrines: the preestablished harmony between mind and [[matter|body]], the monad as a center of [[quality|qualitative]] states, and the [[idea|conception]] of causality that follows from Leibniz's metaphysical commitments.

## Key Ideas

### Preestablished Harmony

Leibniz's doctrine of preestablished harmony holds that mental and physical events correspond to one another not through causal inter[[action|act]]ion but through a divinely ordained [[parallelism]]—like two clocks set to the same time. Bergson references this doctrine in the context of the mind-[[matter|body]] problem:

> "Leibniz ascribed it to a preestablished harmony, and would never have admitted that a motion could give rise to a perception as a cause produces an effect." [003-224]

For Leibniz, the correspondence between [[consciousness|conscious states]] and modes of [[extensity|extension]] is guaranteed by God's prior arrangement, not by any [[reality|real]] influence of one substance upon another.

### The Monad as Qualitative Center

Leibniz's monadology treats each fundamental substance as a simple, indivisible entity whose internal states are purely [[quality|qualitative]]. Bergson notes that Leibniz "builds up the universe with monads only" [003-452], each monad expressing the whole universe from its own perspective. This [[idea|conception]] is philosophically significant for Bergson because it recognizes the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of psychic states—a recognition Bergson shares—even while embedding it in a deterministic framework Bergson rejects.

### The Conservation of Vis Viva

Bergson notes Leibniz's contribution to physics: the substitution of the conservation of *vis viva* (kinetic energy) for [[descartes|Descartes]]' conservation of quantity of motion [003-234]. This scientific advance is cited by Bergson not for its intrinsic importance but as an illustration that even well-established physical laws may admit of exceptions—a point that serves Bergson's broader argument against applying deterministic physical models to conscious life.

## Bergson's Critique

### The Second Conception of Causality

Bergson presents Leibniz as the culmination of a particular way of [[thought|thinking]] about causality—one that interiorizes external qualities rather than exteriorizing internal states:

> "This second conception of causality leads to Leibniz as the first led to Spinoza." [003-450]

In Bergson's analysis, [[spinoza|Spinoza]] represents the extreme of [[determinism|mechanistic determinism]] (treating psychic states as though they were physical things), while Leibniz represents the extreme of idealist determinism (treating physical qualities as though they were inner states of monads). Both, however, share the same fundamental error: they deny [[reality|the real]]ity of duration and [[will|free will]] by constructing a universe in which everything is given in advance.

### The Problem of Determinism in the Monadology

Bergson's most incisive critique of Leibniz concerns the tension between his monadology and his determinism:

> "Nor do the perceptions of Leibniz's monad necessitate one another; God has to regulate their order in advance. In fact, Leibniz's determinism does not spring from his conception of the monad, but from the fact that he builds up the universe with monads only." [003-452]

The monad, as a purely [[quality|qualitative]] center of perception, does not in it[[ego|self]] imply determinism—its states could unfold freely. Leibniz's determinism enters only when he tries to construct a universe out of nothing but monads, requiring a divine harmonization that fixes every relation in advance. Bergson's insight is that the [[quality|qualitative]], temporal char[[action|act]]er of conscious life (which Leibniz's monadology at least partially acknowledges) is incompatible with the preestablished harmony that Leibniz superimposes upon it.

### Duration vs. Preestablished Order

Leibniz's system, for all its recognition of the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of psychic states, ultimately denies genuine temporal becoming. The entire history of every monad is fixed from creation, and time becomes merely the order in which these predetermined states are experienced. Bergson's philosophy of duration stands as the direct antithesis: time is not the unfurling of a prewritten script but a continuous creation in which genuine novelty emerges at every moment.
