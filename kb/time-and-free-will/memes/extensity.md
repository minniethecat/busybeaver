# Extensity (extensité)

Extensity (*extensité*) is Bergson's technical term for the quality of being **extended in space** — spread out, divisible, measurable. It is the defining property of the external world and, by a habitual projection, of our spatialized representation of [[consciousness]]. The con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of [[duration]] with extensity is, according to Bergson, the root error of both determinism and the psychophysical attempt to [[rhythm|measure]] [[consciousness|conscious states]].

## Definition

Extensity is the spatial counterpart to [[intensity]]. While intensity char[[action|act]]erizes the [[quality|qualitative]], indivisible states of consciousness, extensity char[[action|act]]erizes the [[quantity|quantitative]], divisible objects of [[perception|external perception]]:

| Property | Extensity | Intensity |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Domain | [[space|Space]] (external) | Consciousness (internal) |
| Nature | Quantitative, divisible | Qualitative, indivisible |
| Measurement | Possible (homogeneous parts) | Impossible (heterogeneous whole) |
| Relation to [[matter|body]] | Muscular [[sensation]], surface | Deep [[emotion|feeling]], [[feeling|emotion]] |
| Associated concept | [[magnitude|Magnitude]], quantity | [[quality|Quality]], duration |

> (P02-002) …confusion of duration with extensity, of succession with simultaneity, of quality with quantity…

This opening declaration from the Author's Preface establishes extensity as one pole of Bergson's fundamental distinction.

## Bergson's Usage

### Extensity and Intensity

Bergson's analysis in Chapter I shows that what we call the "intensity" of a [[sensation]] often reduces, upon examination, to the perception of **extensity** — the bodily surface affected:

> (001-057) …as if intensity were being developed into extensity, e.g. in the case of muscular effort

When we exert [[effort|muscular effort]], we feel a "greater intensity" — but this is [[action|act]]ually the [[sensation]] of more muscles, a larger surface of the [[matter|body]] involved. Intensity appears to transform into extensity as consciousness spatializes its own states.

### Extensity and Duration

The central philosophical error Bergson diagnoses is the **con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of duration with extensity**. Because we are ac[[habit|custom]]ed to [[thought|thinking]] in spatial terms, we project the properties of extensity — divisibility, homogeneity, measurability — onto duration:

> (001-214) …separation of duration and extensity…

> (001-294) …separates extensity and duration…

When we treat duration as if it were extended in space (as in the metaphor of time as a "line"), we lose the [[quality|qualitative]] interpenetration that defines [[duration|real duration]]. The result is a spatialized, [[time|homogeneous time]] — the time of clocks and physics, not the duration of consciousness.

### Extensity and Succession

The English empiricists attempted to derive extensity from succession — that is, to explain our idea of space as arising from the experience of temporal sequence:

> (001-159) English philosophers, on extensity and succession…

> (001-318) Succession, attempt to derive extensity from…

Bergson argues that this derivation gets things backwards. Extensity cannot arise from succession because succession it[[ego|self]], as we normally conceive it, already presupposes extensity — we imagine successive moments as points on a line.

### Extensity in Chapter III

In the final chapter, Bergson returns to extensity in the context of the [[will|free will]] problem. The spatialization of [[consciousness|conscious states]] — treating them as if they had extensity — is what makes determinism seem plausible:

> (003-102) …seen through the medium of extensity, this quality becomes…

> (003-125) …express duration in terms of extensity, and succession thus takes the form of a continuous line…

When [[consciousness|conscious states]] are seen "through the medium of extensity," they appear as separate objects with causal relations between them. The [[reality|real]]ity of duration — and the possibility of [[free-will]] — is recovered only when we set aside this spatializing lens.

### Extensity and Kant

Bergson engages with [[Kant]]'s theory of space in relation to extensity. [[kant|Kant]] argued that space is an *a priori* form of [[intuition]], not derived from experience. Bergson largely agrees that extensity is not derived from [[sensation]], but he argues that [[kant|Kant]] went too far in making space a necessary form of all experience:

> (003-106) …agree with Kant for extensity can not result from synthesis…

> (003-111) …distinguish between the perception of extensity and the conception of space…

Bergson distinguishes between the **perception of extensity** (the immediate experience of the [[matter|body]]'s spatial spread) and the **[[idea|conception]] of space** (the abstr[[action|act]], homogeneous framework we project). The former is [[reality|real]] and immediate; the latter is a pr[[action|act]]ical construction.

## Key Distinctions

### Extensity vs. Space

While related, extensity and space are not identical in Bergson's usage. Extensity is the concrete, [[quality|qualitative]] experience of spatial spread — primarily through the [[matter|body]]. [[space|Space]], by contrast, is the abstr[[action|act]], homogeneous framework of geometry and physics. Extensity is lived; space is constructed.

### Extensity vs. Extension

Bergson uses *extensité* (extensity) rather than the more common *[[space|étendue]]* (extension) to emphasize the **[[quality|qualitative]]** aspect of spatial spread. [[extension|Extension]] suggests pure geometry; extensity suggests the felt, bodily experience of being spread out in space.

### Extensity and the Body

The [[matter|body]] is the primary source of our experience of extensity. Muscular [[sensation]]s, the perception of our own bodily surface, and the localization of [[sensation]]s in the [[matter|body]] all contribute to our sense of extensity. This bodily extensity then serves as the model for our [[idea|conception]] of external space:

> (003-112) …these two parts of our own extensity do then appear to us as if they were external to each other…
