# Language

Language (*le langage*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not merely a tool for communication — it is a **structural force** that shapes how consciousness apprehends its own states. Bergson argues that language is inherently spatializing: it requires us to establish "the same sharp and precise distinctions, the same discontinuity, as between material objects" [[P02-001]], thereby transforming the fluid, interpenetrating [[reality|real]]ity of [[duration]] into a mosaic of fixed, separable terms. Language is thus both a pr[[action|act]]ical necessity and a primary epistemological obstacle — it enables social life but systematically obscures the nature of conscious [[free-will|freedom]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Language as Spatializing Force

Bergson's most fundamental claim about language is that it imposes spatial form on non-spatial [[reality|real]]ity:

> [P02-001] That is to say, language requires us to establish between our ideas the same sharp and precise distinctions, the same discontinuity, as between material objects.

Language "requires us to establish" spatial relations — the compulsion is structural, not optional. Because words are discrete, stable, and publicly shareable, they necessarily translate the continuous flow of consciousness into a series of **discontinuous terms**. This assimilation of [[mind|thought]] to things is not a failure of language but its very condition of operation.

> [001-042] …we like simple thoughts and because our language is ill-suited to render the subtleties of psychological analysis.

Language is "ill-suited to render the subtleties of [[analysis|psychological analysis]]" — this is a direct methodological warning. Any attempt to describe consciousness in language risks importing spatial assumptions disguised as neutral description. Bergson's own philosophical pr[[action|act]]ice — his use of metaphor, his insistence on temporal language — is an ongoing [[effort|struggle]] against this constraint.

### The Social Origin of Language

Language serves pr[[action|act]]ical and social ends, not metaphysical ones:

> [P01-006] …they further the interests of language and social life and are useful primarily for practical purposes. But they give us nothing of the life and movement of reality…

Concepts, expressed in language, "further the interests of language and social life" — they are instruments of **pr[[action|act]]ical coordination**, not organs of metaphysical insight. Language evolved to serve the needs of social existence: identifying objects, coordinating [[action|act]]ions, sharing useful generalizations. The life and [[motion|movement]] of [[reality|real]]ity — the [[quality|qualitative]] flow of duration — escapes this apparatus entirely.

> [003-178] …is much better adapted to the requirements of social life in general and language in particular, consciousness prefers it, and gradually loses sight of the fundamental self.

Consciousness "prefers" the refr[[action|act]]ed, spatialized version of experience because it is "much better adapted to the requirements of social life in general and language in particular." Language trains consciousness to attend to what is **public** and **repeatable** in experience, at the cost of losing cont[[action|act]] with the "fundamental [[ego|self]]" — the deep, durational [[ego|self]] whose states interpenetrate.

### Language and the Inexpressible

Bergson insists that what is most [[reality|real]] in consciousness resists linguistic formulation:

> [003-180] …the other confused, ever changing, and inexpressible, because language cannot get hold of it without arresting its mobility or fit it into its common-place forms without making it into public property.

Language "cannot get hold of it without arresting its mobility" — the verb "arresting" captures the violence of linguistic representation. To express a [[emotion|feeling]] in language is to **stop** its [[motion|movement]], to extr[[action|act]] it from the [[quality|qualitative]] flow and fix it as a public object. The [[emotion|feeling]] thereby loses both its **individuality** (it becomes "public property") and its **temporality** (it is immobilized).

### The Solidification of Feeling

Language does not merely describe [[emotion|feeling]]s — it transforms them:

> [003-181] One of which is due to the solidifying influence of external objects and language on our constantly changing feelings.

The "solidifying influence" of language operates alongside that of external objects. Our [[emotion|feeling]]s are "constantly changing" — in [[becoming|perpetual becoming]] — but language imposes **fixity**. We "[[instinct|instinctive]]ly tend to solidify our impressions in order to express them in language":

> [003-182] We instinctively tend to solidify our impressions in order to express them in language. Hence we confuse the feeling itself, which is in a perpetual state of becoming, with its permanent external object, and especially with the word which expresses this object.

This is Bergson's account of a fundamental cognitive error: we "confuse the [[emotion|feeling]] it[[ego|self]]" — a living, temporal [[reality|real]]ity — "with the word which expresses this object." The word, being stable and repeatable, substitutes it[[ego|self]] for the [[emotion|feeling]], and we come to believe that our inner life consists of **named states** rather than a continuous [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]].

### The Deception of Sensation

Language's influence reaches even to the level of simple [[sensation]]:

> [003-185] This influence of language on sensation is deeper than is usually thought. Not only does language make us believe in the unchangeableness of our sensations, but it will sometimes deceive us as to the nature of the sensation felt.

Language "deceives us as to the nature of the [[sensation]] felt" — it does not merely report differences but **creates** apparent identities. When we use the same word ("sweet," "blue," "pain") across different moments, we are led to believe that the [[sensation]] is the same each time, when in [[reality|real]]ity each occurrence is [[quality|qualitative]]ly unique, colored by the whole of our psychic history.

### Language, Society, and the Self

In a remarkable [[mind|thought]] experiment, Bergson asks what consciousness would be without language:

> [003-200] If each of us lived a purely individual life, if there were neither society nor language, would our consciousness grasp the series of inner states in this unbroken form?

His answer is nuanced: even without language, consciousness would not fully grasp the unbroken form of its states — because the [[matter|body]] it[[ego|self]] introduces [[space|spatiality]]. But language dramatically **amplifies** this tendency. "For the sake of language, the [[ego|self]] has everything to gain by reversing the natural order of its states" — language motivates the [[ego|self]] to spatialize its own experience for the sake of communicability.

| Aspect of Language | What It Does | Consequence for Philosophy |
|--------------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Spatialization | Imposes discontinuity on continuous experience | Duration is misrepresented as a series of states |
| Solidification | Fixes changing [[emotion|feeling]]s into stable terms | The [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]] becomes a mosaic of named items |
| Socialization | Makes inner experience publicly shareable | The "fundamental [[ego|self]]" is replaced by a public persona |
| Deception | Creates belief in unchanging [[sensation]]s | Qualitative uniqueness is erased by linguistic repetition |

## Key Distinctions

### Language vs. Duration

[[Duration]] is the continuous, [[quality|qualitative]], interpenetrating flow of [[consciousness|conscious states]]. Language is the system of discrete, stable, public terms that arrests this flow. The entire project of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* can be seen as an attempt to think duration **against** the grain of language — to use language in a way that gestures beyond its own spatializing logic.

### Language and Space

[[Space]] is the medium of externality — the [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] in which things are set side by side. Language operates by translating temporal relations into spatial ones: succession becomes juxtaposition, [[change|qualitative change]] becomes difference of magnitude. Language is, in effect, **space made verbal**.

### Language and Social Life

Language serves social life by creating a shared world of named objects and communicable [[emotion|feeling]]s. But this social world is a **constructed surface** over the deep, individual [[reality|real]]ity of duration. [[Consciousness]], trained by language and social life, comes to inhabit this surface world and "gradually loses sight of the fundamental [[ego|self]]" [[003-178]].
