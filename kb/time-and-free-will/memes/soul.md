# Soul

The soul (*âme*) in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is not a metaphysical substance but the **[[quality|qualitative]] whole of conscious life**. It is the name Bergson gives to the interpenetrating totality of states that constitutes the [[ego|self]] in [[duration]] — not a thing that has experiences, but the very texture of experience it[[ego|self]]. The soul is what the [[ego]] becomes when it "lets it[[ego|self]] live" and ceases to impose spatial cat[[self|ego]]ries on its own inner life.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Soul as Qualitative Whole

Bergson describes certain states as be[[desire|longing]] to the soul in a distinctive sense — they are "[[ego|self]]-sufficient," not tied to external objects, and they reveal the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of consciousness:

> [001-024] But certain states of the soul seem to us, rightly or wrongly, to be self-sufficient, such as deep joy or sorrow, a reflective passion or an aesthetic emotion.

These "states of the soul" are the purest expression of [[quality|qualitative]] consciousness. Unlike [[sensation]]s tied to physical stimuli, they seem to arise from within and to concern only the [[ego|self]]. They are therefore the ideal starting point for Bergson's analysis of [[intensity]].

### The Soul and Spatial Metaphors

Bergson shows how spatial metaphors mislead us about the soul:

> [001-028] …occupies a large space in the soul or even that it fills it entirely…

When we say an [[feeling|emotion]] "fills the soul," we are using a spatial metaphor. But the soul is not a container; it is the whole of conscious life. To say an [[feeling|emotion]] fills the soul is to say that it **transforms the whole** of consciousness, not that it occupies a larger volume. The spatial metaphor conceals a [[quality|qualitative]] truth.

### The Rhythm of the Soul

Bergson develops a striking image of the soul's rhythm in [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]]:

> [001-044] …the rhythm by which our soul is lulled into self-forgetfulness…

In [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]], the regular rhythm of art — poetry, music, dance — induces a state in which the soul ceases to be aware of it[[ego|self]] as a separate, individual entity. The soul "forgets it[[ego|self]]" and merges with the [[quality|qualitative]] flow. This is not a loss of consciousness but a **deepening** of it — a return to the [[duration|pure duration]] that underlies the spatialized [[ego|self]].

### The Soul and the Whole

The soul is char[[action|act]]erized by a distinctive property: the whole is reflected in each part:

> [003-100] …takes up the whole of the soul?—Yes, undoubtedly; but, just as…

> [003-118] …the simplest of them the whole soul can be reflected.

Each conscious state, however simple, reflects the **entire soul**. A momentary [[emotion|feeling]] of joy is not an isolated atom of experience — it is the whole soul at that instant, colored by the entire past and oriented toward the entire future. This is what Bergson means by [[interpenetration]]: the whole is present in each part.

### The Soul as Dynamic Process

Bergson insists that the soul is not a collection of states but a **[[dynamic|dynamic process]]**:

> [003-169] …going on in the depths of the soul, a wholly dynamic process, not…

> [003-185] …name them, and in the human soul there are only processes. What…

[[language|Language]] names "[[emotion|feeling]]s," "[[idea|ideas]]," and "motives" as if they were static things. But in the soul, there are no things — only **[[becoming|process]]es**. The soul is becoming, not being. Every attempt to freeze it into a set of discrete states is a falsification imposed by the [[intelligence|intellect]].

### The Inexpressibility of the Soul

The [[quality|qualitative]] richness of the soul exceeds all [[language|linguistic expression]]:

> [003-262] …a thousand emotions which stir the soul. We estimate the talent of a…

> [003-263] …translate completely what our soul experiences: there is no common measure…

The artist's talent lies in suggesting what cannot be directly expressed. The novelist does not describe [[feeling|emotion]]s — she creates a rhythm of language that **induces** in the reader a [[quality|qualitative]] state analogous to the char[[action|act]]er's experience. What cannot be said can be suggested; what cannot be analyzed can be lived.

## Key Distinctions

### Soul vs. Ego

The [[ego]] is the [[ego|self]] as it appears in reflective [[mind|thought]] and social life — divided, named, spatialized. The soul is the [[ego|self]] as it is **lived** — whole, dynamic, [[quality|qualitative]]. The [[self|ego]] is the soul's shadow in space; the soul is the [[self|ego]]'s substance in duration.

### Soul as Process vs. Soul as Substance

Traditional metaphysics treated the soul as a substance — a thing that persists through change. Bergson treats the soul as **[[becoming|process]]** — a continuous becoming that is change it[[ego|self]]. The soul does not have experiences; the soul **is** its experiences, in their [[quality|qualitative]] interpenetration.

## Historical Context

Bergson's concept of the soul engages with a long philosophical tradition. **Aristotle** defined the soul as the form of the [[matter|body]]; **[[descartes|Descartes]]** made it a [[thought|thinking]] substance distinct from extended matter; **[[kant|Kant]]** made it a regulative idea of [[mind|reason]], not an object of knowledge. Bergson rejects both the substance view (the soul is [[becoming|process]], not thing) and the [[kant|Kant]]ian limitation (the soul is directly lived, not merely postulated). His view is closer to **[[will|Will]]iam [[james|James]]'s** "stream of consciousness," but with a stronger emphasis on the unity and [[quality|qualitative]] richness of the whole.
