# Tendency

Tendency (*tendance*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a corrective concept: it describes the **directional quality** of conscious life without reducing it to a measurable force or a mechanical cause. A tendency is not a quantity that could be stronger or weaker than another — it is a **[[quality|qualitative]] direction** of the [[self|whole self]]. Whenever we mistake a tendency for a determinate cause, we spatialize consciousness and make [[free-will]] unintelligible.

## Bergson's Argument

### Tendency as Qualitative Direction

Bergson introduces "tendency" to describe how [[consciousness|conscious states]] orient themselves without being causally determined:

> [001-082] …of organic disturbance, all tendency towards muscular contraction…

What Bergson calls a "tendency towards muscular contr[[action|act]]ion" is not a measurable impulse but a **[[quality|qualitative]] leaning** of the whole [[body|organism]]. The error of [[psychophysics]] is to treat this [[quality|qualitative]] direction as if it were a [[quantity|quantitative]] force that could be [[rhythm|measure]]d and compared.

> [003-412] …from our own constitution. This tendency has become more and more marked…

The tendency Bergson describes is rooted in "our own constitution" — it arises from the nature of consciousness it[[ego|self]], not from external causes impinging on it. This is crucial for [[mind|understanding]] [[freedom]]: if our [[action|act]]s are expressions of our own constitutive tendencies, they are free, even if they are not "uncaused" in the libertarian sense.

### The Spatializing Tendency

One of Bergson's most important uses of "tendency" is to describe the **mind's inclination to spatialize** — to project the properties of [[space]] onto [[duration]]:

> [003-253] This erroneous tendency aided by language.

[[language|Language]], with its discrete [[language|words]] and spatial metaphors, reinforces the mind's natural tendency to think in spatial terms. Bergson is not merely describing a philosophical error; he is diagnosing a **constitutional tendency** of conscious beings — one that must be overcome by strenuous philosophical [[reflection]].

> [003-200] …of all conscious beings. Our tendency to form a clear picture of the…

The "tendency to form a clear picture" is the impulse to make everything distinct, separate, and external — ex[[action|act]]ly the operation that spatializes duration and generates the pseudo-problems of [[determinism]].

### Tendency and Anxiety

> [003-371] …this anxiety shows itself in a tendency to transform relations of succession…

Bergson makes a subtle observation: anxiety reveals it[[ego|self]] as a **tendency to spatialize time** — to transform "relations of succession" (temporal ordering) into relations of [[causality|causation]] and determinism. This is a psychological version of the spatializing tendency: when we are anxious, we seek to make the future predictable by treating it as if it were already determined.

| Aspect of Tendency | Bergson's Usage | Contrast |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| [[organic|Organic]] tendency | Qualitative direction of the [[body|organism]] | [[mechanical|Mechanical]] cause |
| Spatializing tendency | [[mind|Mind]]'s inclination to project space onto time | Genuine duration |
| Linguistic tendency | [[language|Language]]'s reinforcement of spatial habits | Immediate [[intuition]] |
| Anxious tendency | Transformation of succession into [[causality|causation]] | Free [[ego|self]]-expression |

### Tendency vs. Determinism

The determinist treats every state of consciousness as the **determinate effect** of prior states. Bergson replies that [[consciousness|conscious states]] express **tendencies**, not causal necessities:

> [003-085] …these mathematical points have a tendency to develop into lines in proportion…

Even mathematical points — the paradigm of discrete, spatial items — "have a tendency to develop into lines." Bergson's point is that even in mathematics, the discrete is not [[ego|self]]-sufficient; it has a tendency toward the continuous. In consciousness, this tendency is not a defect but the essential feature: states do not remain discrete; they tend toward interpenetration.

## Key Distinctions

### Tendency vs. Cause

A cause **determines** its effect; a tendency **inclines** without determining. When Bergson says consciousness has a tendency to spatialize, he is not saying it is causally determined to do so — the tendency can be resisted (as philosophical [[reflection]] demonstrates). This distinction between determination and inclination is at the core of Bergson's theory of [[free-will|freedom]].

### Tendency vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]], analyzed in Chapter I, is often confused with tendency. An intense [[sensation]] may seem to have a "tendency" to produce [[action|act]]ion, but Bergson's analysis shows that intensity is a **quality**, not a measurable force. Tendency, likewise, is a [[quality|qualitative]] direction, not a vector with magnitude.

### Tendency and Duration

In [[duration]], every state carries the whole past with it, and this carrying-forward is a **tendency** — the past tends toward the future without determining it. Duration is the medium in which tendencies operate; [[space]] is the medium that transforms tendencies into mechanical causes.

## Historical Context

Bergson's concept of tendency owes something to the Aristotelian-Scholastic notion of *inclinatio* (inclination) and to [[leibniz|Leibniz]]'s idea that every substance contains a tendency toward its future states. But Bergson radicalizes the concept: tendency is not a property added to a substance but the very mode in which conscious life unfolds. It is closer to what later phenomenologists would call **intentionality** — the directedness of consciousness toward its objects — though Bergson would resist the spatial connotations even of that term.
