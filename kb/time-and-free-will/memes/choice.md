# Choice

Choice, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the pivot on which the free-will debate turns — but Bergson's analysis shows that both sides of the debate **misunderstand what choice is**. The determinist treats choice as the inevitable result of the strongest motive; the libertarian treats it as an unmotivated selection among equally possible alternatives. Bergson argues that both pictures are spatial: they array "possible choices" before the mind as if they were external objects, then ask how one is selected. Real choice is the **organic [[action|act]] of the [[self|whole self]]** in [[duration]] — not a selection from a pre-existing menu.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Spatial Picture of Choice

The common-sense picture of choice presents it as deliberation among **distinct alternatives** arrayed before the mind:

> [003-293] …ent inert things awaiting our choice. But then we must certainly…

Bergson's critique is sharp: this picture treats possible [[action|act]]ions as "inert things" — objects in [[space]] that we inspect, compare, and select. But in consciousness, future [[action|act]]ions are not inert objects; they are **living possibilities** that grow out of the [[self|whole self]]. The spatial picture of choice makes the problem of [[freedom]] insoluble because it has already spatialized what it seeks to explain.

### How Does the Will Make Its Choice?

> [003-287] …by which the will makes its choice.

The "will" that makes a choice is not a separate faculty that intervenes among motives. For Bergson, the will is the [[self|whole self]] expressing it[[ego|self]]. The question "how does the will choose?" is badly posed because it treats the will as a **[[mechanical|mechanism]]** operating on pre-existing inputs. The [[reality|real]] question is: how does the [[ego|self]], as a unified duration, produce [[action|act]]s that express its entire history?

### The Impossibility of Foreseeing Choice

> [003-311] …to foretell with certainty the choice which Peter made.

Bergson's famous example: could anyone have foretold with certainty the choice Peter made? The determinist says yes — if we knew all of Peter's past states, we could predict his choice. Bergson replies: Peter's past states are not **discrete items** that could be summed to yield a prediction; they are an [[organic|organic whole]] that expresses it[[ego|self]] in the [[action|act]]. The [[action|act]] is unforeseeable not because of a defect in our knowledge but because it is genuinely **novel** — the [[self|whole self]] produces something that was not contained, as a determinate possibility, in any prior state.

> [003-302] …ossible, how have we made our choice? If only one of them was possible…

Bergson's analysis of possibility is radical: before the [[action|act]], the "possibilities" are not [[reality|real]] items at all. They are **retrospective projections** — we look back after choosing and say "I could have done otherwise," but this "could have" is a spatial fiction. In duration, the [[action|act]] was never "one among many" — it was the unique expression of the [[ego|self]] at that moment.

### Choice and Crisis

> [001-238] …choice without, shown at crisis…

Bergson acknowledges that choice becomes visible at moments of **crisis** — when the [[ego|self]] is divided, when deliberation is agonizing. But this visibility is deceptive: the crisis does not mean that the [[ego|self]] is choosing among external options; it means that the [[ego|self]] is **forming it[[ego|self]]** through the [[action|act]]. The choice is not the resolution of a deliberation; it is the maturation of a tendency.

### Choice and the Whole Self

The central thesis of Bergson's theory of [[free-will|freedom]] is that a free [[action|act]] is one that expresses the **[[self|whole self]]**:

> [003-309] …ct of our friend as to pass a judgment on his present character, tha…

When we "pass a judgment" on someone's char[[action|act]]er, we are [[intuition|intuitive]]ly grasping the connection between the [[ego|self]] and its [[action|act]]s — we know that the [[action|act]] expresses the person, not just a momentary motive. This is the model for [[mind|understanding]] free choice: the [[action|act]] is free when it carries the "colouring of the [[ego|self]]" (Bergson's phrase from [003-414]), expressing not a fragmentary motive but the **entirety of the person's history**.

## Key Distinctions

### Choice vs. Deliberation

[[deliberation|Deliberation]] is the spatialized **appearance** of choice; [[reality|real]] choice is the temporal **[[reality|real]]ity**. In deliberation, we seem to weigh options; in [[reality|real]]ity, the [[ego|self]] is maturing toward an [[action|act]]. The deliberation is not the cause of the choice; it is a symptom of the [[ego|self]]'s complexity.

### Choice vs. Tendency

[[Tendency]] is the [[quality|qualitative]] [[tendency|direction]] of the [[ego|self]]; choice is the [[action|act]] in which that [[tendency|direction]] crystallizes. A tendency inclines; a choice [[action|act]]ualizes. The determinist misses the distinction, treating tendency as if it were already a causal determination. The libertarian misses it too, treating choice as if it had no connection to the tendencies that preceded it.

### Choice and Duration

Choice is **temporal through and through**. It is not an instantaneous event but a [[becoming|process]] in [[duration]] — the [[ego|self]] ripening toward an [[action|act]]. The spatial picture of choice (alternatives arrayed in space, then one selected) freezes this [[becoming|process]] into a timeless instant, making it unintelligible.
