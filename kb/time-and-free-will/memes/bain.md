## Overview

Alexander Bain (1818–1903) appears repeatedly in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* as a representative of the 19th-century [[association|associationist psychology]] that Bergson subjects to sustained critique. Bain's work spans three domains that Bergson engages directly: the physiological theory of nervous energy and effort, the psychological theory of space perception, and the analysis of moral deliberation as a conflict of motives. In each case, Bain exemplifies the tendency to treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as discrete, externally related elements—a tendency Bergson's philosophy of duration is designed to overcome.

## Key Ideas

### The Outgoing Stream of Nervous Energy

Bain's account of the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort locates it in the outgoing (centrifugal) discharge of nervous energy from the brain to the muscles. Bergson quotes Bain's own formulation:

> "Bain, for example, declares that 'the sensibility accompanying muscular movement coincides with the *outgoing* stream of nervous energy.'" [001-060]

This centrifugal theory—that we are directly conscious of the motor impulse before any [[motion|movement]] occurs—is the view that [[james|William James]]'s experiments would later challenge. For Bergson, Bain's formulation exemplifies the shared assumption of 19th-century psychophysiology: that [[consciousness|conscious states]] can be mapped onto measurable physiological [[becoming|process]]es.

### The Theory of Space Perception

Bain contributed to the empirical theory of space perception, attempting to explain how the notion of space is built up from originally unextended [[sensation]]s. Bergson discusses Bain's theory alongside those of [[lotze|Lotze]], [[wundt|Wundt]], and Müller:

> "But Lotze's hypothesis of local signs, Bain's theory, and the more comprehensive explanation suggested by Wundt, may seem at first quite independent of the Transcendental Aesthetic." [003-107]

Bergson's analysis shows that, despite their apparent diversity, all these theories share a [[kant|Kant]]ian starting point: they treat [[sensation]]s as originally inextensive and then try to explain how [[extensity|extension]] is constructed from them.

### The Conflict of Motives

Bain's most detailed engagement with the problem of [[will|free will]] appears in his analysis of moral deliberation as a conflict between competing motives. Bergson notes the scope of Bain's treatment:

> "Bain, for his part, devotes a whole chapter to the 'Conflict of Motives.' In it he balances pleasures and pains as so many terms to which one might attribute, in abstraction from one another, a definite force." [003-252]

## Bergson's Critique

### The Illusion of Measurable Psychic Force

Bergson's critique of Bain centers on the assumption—shared by the entire associationist tradition—that motives, desires, and aversions exist as distinct quantifiable forces. When Bain "balances pleasures and pains as so many terms," he treats [[consciousness|conscious states]] as though they were physical weights on a scale. For Bergson, this is a cat[[self|ego]]ry mistake: psychic states do not exist side by side in a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] where they can be compared [[quantity|quantitative]]ly. They interpenetrate and [[quality|qualitative]]ly transform one another.

### The Kantian Assumption in Space Theory

Bergson's analysis of Bain's theory of space perception reveals a deeper philosophical commitment that Bain him[[ego|self]] may not have recognized. Despite claiming to offer a purely empirical account, Bain—like [[lotze|Lotze]] and [[wundt|Wundt]]—treats [[sensation]]s as originally inextensive, a premise derived from [[kant|Kant]]:

> "The conclusion to be drawn from the theories of Lotze and Bain, and from Wundt's attempt to reconcile them, is that the sensations by means of which we come to form the notion of space are themselves unextended and simply qualitative." [003-107]

For Bergson, this starting point is precisely the error. [[sensation|Sensation]]s are not originally inextensive; [[extensity|extension]] is given in perception from the start. The problem Bain and others set themselves—how to build [[extensity|extension]] from the inextensive—is a false problem generated by an unwarranted [[kant|Kant]]ian premise.

### The Mosaic Self in Moral Psychology

Bain's "Conflict of Motives" chapter, for Bergson, perfectly illustrates what he calls the "mosaic" [[idea|conception]] of the [[ego|self]]. The very [[action|act]] of naming and separating motives—this pleasure against that pain—falsifies how these states exist in consciousness. In lived experience, the [[ego|self]]'s various tendencies do not confront one another as external adversaries; they are aspects of a single, continuously developing psychic whole. Bain's analysis, by treating them as independent forces, "sets up clear-cut distinctions" where none exist in concrete experience [003-252].
