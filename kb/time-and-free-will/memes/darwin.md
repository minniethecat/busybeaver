## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) enters *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* not as an object of philosophical critique but as a source of empirical evidence. Bergson draws extensively on Darwin's *The Expression of the [[emotion|Emotion]]s in Man and Animals* (1872), quoting Darwin's detailed observations of the physiological manifestations of [[anger|rage]], love, and pain. Darwin's descriptions serve Bergson's argument by demonstrating the intimate connection between [[feeling|emotion]]al states and their bodily expression—a connection Bergson accepts while drawing a very different philosophical conclusion from it than the physiological reductionism that Darwin's work might seem to support.

## Key Ideas

### The Physiological Expression of Rage

Darwin's description of the bodily symptoms of [[anger|rage]] is among the most detailed Bergson cites. Bergson quotes Darwin at length to establish the range and intensity of physiological changes accompanying violent [[feeling|emotion]]:

> "Darwin has given a remarkable description of the physiological symptoms of rage. 'The action of the heart is much accelerated.... The face reddens or may turn deadly pale. The respiration is labored...'" [001-081]

For Bergson, this description establishes the undeniable f[[action|act]] that [[feeling|emotion]] involves the whole [[body|organism]]. The question is not whether [[feeling|emotion]] has physiological expression—it does—but what relation this expression bears to the conscious state it[[ego|self]].

### Love and the Physiology of Emotion

Darwin's observations extended beyond violent [[feeling|emotion]]s to tender ones. Bergson quotes Darwin's description of lovers meeting:

> "'When lovers meet,' says Darwin, 'we know that their hearts beat quickly, their breathing is hurried and their faces flushed.'" [001-085]

These physiological changes are not, for Bergson, mere external signs of an internal [[emotion|feeling]]. They are constitutive aspects of the [[feeling|emotion]]al experience—not its cause, but its embodied [[reality|real]]ity.

### Pain and Escalating Response

Darwin's observations on the behavioral escalation of pain provide Bergson with a model for [[mind|understanding]] [[feeling|emotion]]al intensity. Bergson summarizes Darwin's findings:

> "Darwin has drawn a striking picture of the reactions following a pain which becomes more and more acute. 'Great pain urges all animals ... to make the most violent and varied efforts to escape from the cause of suffering.'" [002-018]

The escalating physiological response to increasing pain illustrates Bergson's key insight about intensity: what we call "more intense" pain is not the same pain plus more of it, but a [[quality|qualitative]]ly transformed state involving progressively more of the [[body|organism]].

## Bergson's Engagement

### From Physiological Description to Philosophical Analysis

Bergson's use of Darwin is distinctive: he accepts Darwin's empirical observations in full while rejecting the reductionist conclusions others drew from them. Darwin him[[ego|self]] was cautious about philosophical implications, but the physiological tradition that followed him—represented by [[spencer|Spencer]], [[bain|Bain]], and others—tended to identify [[feeling|emotion]] with its bodily expression. Bergson resists this identification while retaining Darwin's data.

### The Irreducible Psychic Element

Even in the most physiologically overwhelming [[feeling|emotion]]s, Bergson insists on an irreducible psychic element. Discussing Darwin's account of [[anger|rage]], Bergson writes:

> "There will always be an irreducible psychic element in anger, if this be only the idea of striking or fighting, of which Darwin speaks, and which gives a common direction to so many diverse movements." [001-081]

This "idea" or "[[tendency|direction]]" is not it[[ego|self]] a physiological event—it is a conscious orientation that organizes the physiological responses into a unified [[feeling|emotion]]al state. The bodily changes are [[reality|real]] and important, but they are the embodiment of the [[feeling|emotion]], not the [[feeling|emotion]] it[[ego|self]].

### Intensity as Qualitative Transformation

Darwin's observations on the escalation of pain support Bergson's central thesis about [[intensity|intensive magnitude]]. When pain increases, the change is not like turning up a dial—it is a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation:

> "Great pain urges all animals ... to make the most violent and varied efforts to escape." [002-018]

The shift from moderate to severe pain involves a reorganization of the entire [[body|organism]]'s response. More muscles are recruited, more systems engaged, more of the [[matter|body]] involved. This is precisely Bergson's model of intensity: not the addition of homogeneous units but the progressive involvement of greater portions of the [[body|organism]], producing a [[change|qualitative change]] that we then retro[[action|act]]ively represent as a [[quantity|quantitative]] increase.
