# Substance

Substance, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a concept that belongs to the spatial world of material objects. A substance is something that **underlies** qualities — a permanent substrate in which properties inhere. When philosophy applies this cat[[self|ego]]ry to consciousness, it treats the [[ego|self]] as a **thing** — a substance that has [[consciousness|conscious states]] — rather than as the continuous flow of [[duration]] it[[ego|self]]. Bergson argues that the [[ego|self]] is not a substance; it is a **[[becoming|process]]**.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Cerebral Substance and Psychophysics

The [[psychophysics]] of Bergson's time located consciousness in the "cerebral substance" — the physical brain:

> [001-019] …atoms of the cerebral substance, and that the intensity of a…

Psychophysics assumed that the intensity of a [[sensation]] corresponds to the quantity of molecular motion in the cerebral substance. Bergson replies that this is a **cat[[self|ego]]ry mistake**: the "intensity" of a [[sensation]] is a quality, not a quantity, and it does not inhere in any substance, cerebral or otherwise.

> [003-341] …which gradually thickened its substance and altered its colour…

Bergson describes how the [[ego|self]] changes [[quality|qualitative]]ly over time, "thickening its substance" — but this is a metaphor, not a claim that the [[ego|self]] is literally a substance. The [[ego|self]] accumulates its past; it does not consist of an unchanging substrate.

### The Metaphysical Tradition of Substance

The concept of substance has a long history in philosophy, from Aristotle's *ousia* to [[Descartes]]' *res cogitans* to [[Spinoza]]'s single substance. Bergson's critique is that all these [[idea|conception]]s model the [[ego|self]] on the **material object**:

> [003-217] …atoms of the nervous substance…

The nervous substance — the physical brain — is undoubtedly material and substantial. But consciousness is not in the nervous substance the way a property inheres in a substance. The relation between consciousness and the brain is not one of inherence but of **correlation** — a topic Bergson develops more fully in *[[matter|Matter]] and [[memory|Memory]]*.

### Substance and the Self

The deepest error of the substance tradition is to treat the **[[ego|self]]** as a substance. If the [[ego|self]] is a substance, then its states are **accidents** that inhere in it. But in duration, there is no distinction between the [[ego|self]] and its states. The [[ego|self]] **is** the interpenetration of its states; it is not a separate thing that has them:

> [003-387] …all mechanical influence of substances on one another…

The mechanical worldview treats all relations as relations between substances — extended things [[action|act]]ing on one another in space. Bergson's argument is that consciousness is not a substance in this sense, and its "[[action|act]]s" are not mechanical influences.

| View of [[self|Self]] | Ontology | States | Freedom |
|-------------|----------|--------|---------|
| Substance ([[descartes|Descartes]]) | The [[ego|self]] is a thing (*res cogitans*) | States inhere in substance | Substance [[action|act]]s freely |
| Bundle (Hume) | The [[ego|self]] is a collection of states | States associated by laws | No [[ego|self]] → no [[free-will|freedom]] |
| **Duration (Bergson)** | The [[ego|self]] is continuous flow | States interpenetrate the whole | Act expresses the [[self|whole self]] |

## Key Distinctions

### Substance vs. Process

Bergson's philosophy replaces substance with **[[becoming|process]]**. The [[ego|self]] is not a thing that endures through time; it is the enduring it[[ego|self]]. This is why Bergson insists on the **[[reality|real]]ity** of time: if time is [[reality|real]], then being is becoming, and substance — which is what remains identical through change — has no place in the description of consciousness.

### Substance vs. Duration

[[Duration]] is the alternative to substance. Where substance is permanent, duration is [[becoming|continuous change]]. Where substance underlies qualities, duration is the [[quality|qualitative]] flow it[[ego|self]]. Where substance is spatial (a thing occupying space), duration is temporal (a being that is time).

## Historical Context

### Descartes and Spinoza

[[Descartes]] divided [[reality|real]]ity into two substances: [[thought|thinking]] substance (*res cogitans*) and extended substance (*[[extension|res extensa]]*). [[Spinoza]] unified them into a single substance with two attributes. Bergson's critique is that both thinkers remained within the substance paradigm, treating consciousness as a kind of thing rather than as a temporal [[becoming|process]].

### Leibniz and the Monad

[[Leibniz]]'s monads are non-extended substances — "metaphysical points" that mirror the universe. Bergson acknowledges [[leibniz|Leibniz]]'s insight that consciousness is not extended, but he argues that the monad is still a substance — a permanent unit — rather than a [[becoming|process]] in duration.
