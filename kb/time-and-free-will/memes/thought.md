# Thought

Thought, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not a neutral mirror of [[reality|real]]ity but a **pr[[action|act]]ical faculty** shaped by the demands of [[action|act]]ion. Thought operates by **spatializing** — breaking the continuous flow of [[duration]] into discrete, static concepts that can be manipulated like objects in [[space]]. This makes [[mind|thought]] enormously useful for pr[[action|act]]ical life and science, but when it is taken as a guide to metaphysical truth, it systematically distorts the nature of consciousness and time.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Practical Origin of Thought

Bergson insists that [[mind|thought]] is not designed for speculation but for [[action|act]]ion:

> [P02-001] …This assimilation of thought to things is useful in practice…

Thought assimilates it[[ego|self]] to **things** — to the solid, extended, measurable objects of the spatial world. This assimilation is pr[[action|act]]ically useful because it allows us to predict and control our environment. But it is philosophically misleading because it treats consciousness as if it were a thing.

> [P01-006] …any elaborate construction of thought: it is given in immediate experience…

The immediate data of consciousness are not constructions of [[mind|thought]]. Duration, [[quality]], [[heterogeneity]] — these are **given** in immediate experience, before [[mind|thought]] goes to work on them. Thought's constructions come afterward, substituting spatial schemas for the lived flow.

### Thought and Language

> [001-042] …because we like simple thoughts and because our language is…

Thought is inseparable from [[language]], and language is built for the spatial world. Words are discrete labels for discrete objects; they cannot capture the interpenetration of [[consciousness|conscious states]]. We "like simple [[mind|thought]]s" because language makes simple [[mind|thought]]s easy to form — but the simplicity is artificial, imposed by the structure of language it[[ego|self]].

> [001-044] …fixed for ever, absorbing our thought and our will in their own eternal…

Fixed concepts absorb [[mind|thought]] and will, making what is fluid appear static. The concepts of philosophy — substance, cause, [[ego|self]] — are "fixed for ever" in language, and [[mind|thought]] mistakes this linguistic fixity for metaphysical [[reality|real]]ity.

### The Two Kinds of Thought

| Kind of Thought | Object | Method | Result |
|----------------|--------|--------|--------|
| Spatializing (discursive) | Extended objects in [[space]] | [[analysis|Analysis]]: breaking into parts | Clear, distinct, pr[[action|act]]ical |
| Intuitive (philosophical) | Qualitative states in [[duration]] | Sympathy: grasping as whole | Difficult, fluid, metaphysical |

> [001-048] …the feelings and thoughts which the artist suggests to us…

The artist communicates [[emotion|feeling]]s and [[mind|thought]]s that are not reducible to discrete concepts. Art thus provides a model for a kind of thinking that does not spatialize — a thinking that respects the [[quality|qualitative]] continuity of experience.

> [001-314] …modes of thought and modes of extension…

Bergson repeatedly contrasts "modes of thought" with "modes of [[extensity|extension]]." The distinction between the two is [[reality|real]]: [[mind|thought]] and [[extensity|extension]] are different orders of being. But [[mind|thought]], by its nature, tends to model it[[ego|self]] on [[extensity|extension]] — to treat its own objects as if they were extended in space.

## Key Distinctions

### Thought vs. Intuition

Bergson contrasts discursive thought (analysis, which breaks things into parts) with **[[intuition]]** (sympathy, which grasps the whole). [[intuition|Intuition]] is not irrational; it is a different kind of thinking — one that enters into the object rather than standing outside it. *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* does not yet use the term "[[intuition]]" in its full Bergsonian sense (that comes later), but the distinction is already operative.

### Thought vs. Duration

Thought spatializes; duration temporalizes. Thought arrests the flow and makes it static; duration is the flow it[[ego|self]]. The philosopher's task is to use [[mind|thought]] to **point beyond** [[mind|thought]] — to lead the reader to an [[intuition]] of duration that [[mind|thought]] alone cannot provide.

## Historical Context

### The Cartesian Cogito

[[Descartes]]' *cogito* — "I think, therefore I am" — made [[mind|thought]] the foundation of philosophy. Bergson does not reject this but radicalizes it: the "I" that thinks is not a static substance but a being in duration. The Cartesian cogito, by treating the thinking [[ego|self]] as a **thing** (a substance), already spatializes it.

### Kant and the Limits of Thought

[[Kant]] showed that [[mind|thought]] cannot reach things-in-themselves. Bergson agrees but gives a different [[mind|reason]]: [[mind|thought]] cannot reach duration because it is constitutively spatial. [[kant|Kant]]'s "cat[[self|ego]]ries" are the forms of spatializing [[mind|thought]], not the forms of all possible experience.
