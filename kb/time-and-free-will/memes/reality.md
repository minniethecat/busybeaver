# Reality

Reality (*réalité*) for Bergson is **not a fixed structure** accessible through [[analysis|conceptual analysis]], but a **continuous becoming** (*[[becoming|devenir]]*) given directly in [[consciousness]].

Throughout *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, Bergson repeatedly exposes the gap between the spatialized abstr[[action|act]]ions of the [[intelligence|intellect]] and the [[quality|qualitative]] immediacy of real experience. What is psychologically real — the [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]] — is constantly mistaken for what is merely a spatial projection.

## Bergson's Argument

### Reality as Immediate Experience

Bergson's method begins from a radical premise: reality is not constructed but **given**:

> For him reality is not to be reached by any elaborate construction of thought: it is given in immediate experience as a flux, a continuous process of becoming, to be grasped by intuition, by sympathetic insight. [P01-006]

Concepts divide, freeze, and spatialize this [[becoming|flux]] — they are useful instruments but never capture reality it[[ego|self]]. The [[intelligence|intellect]] breaks duration into discrete instants and then wonders how they can be reconnected; the problem is of its own making.

### Psychological Reality vs. Physical Measurement

A central Bergsonian move is to insist that **psychological reality** does not correspond to physical [[rhythm|measure]]ment. What consciousness registers as real often differs systematically from what physics describes:

> But the very childishness of this distinction makes it a psychological reality. And not only do the heavy and the light impress our consciousness as generically different, but the various degrees of lightness and heaviness are so many species of these two genera. [002-045]

The difference between hot and cold, or light and heavy, is not a difference of degree on a single scale but a **[[quality|qualitative difference]]** — a difference in kind. Consciousness registers these as distinct realities, not positions on a thermometer or a scale.

### The Shadow Is a Reality

Bergson's analysis of photometric perception provides a striking example:

> This shadow is a reality to your consciousness, like the light itself. If you call the first surface in all its brilliancy white, you will have to give another name to what you now see, for it is a different thing: it is, if we may say so, a new shade of white. [002-052]

> …in fact, black has just as much reality for our consciousness as white… [002-053]

When a candle is extinguished, the resulting shadow is **not the absence of light** for consciousness — it is a positive [[quality|qualitative]] presence, a new shade with its own perceptual char[[action|act]]er. Physical theory teaches us to treat black as the absence of light, but consciousness experiences it as a distinct reality.

### The Illusion of Quantitative Reality

Bergson argues that when we treat transitions between [[consciousness|conscious states]] as quantities, we create fictions:

> …not only are you unable to explain in what sense this transition is a quantity, but reflection will show you that it is not even a reality; the only realities are the states S and S' through which I pass. [003-013]

The "difference" between two [[consciousness|conscious states]] is not a third thing — a quantity that can be [[rhythm|measure]]d. The only realities are the states themselves, and the passage from one to the other is a **[[quality|qualitative]] transformation**, not an arithmetical addition.

### Reality of Space and the Absolute

Bergson engages [[kant|Kant]]'s question about the absolute reality of space but treats it as a secondary issue:

> We shall not lay too much stress on the question of the absolute reality of space: perhaps we might as well ask whether space is or is not in space. [003-104]

[[space|Space]], for Bergson, is not a [[kant|Kant]]ian *a priori* form with absolute reality, nor a mere empirical abstr[[action|act]]ion. It is the **[[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]** that the [[intelligence|intellect]] projects in order to organize and quantify the [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]]. Its reality is pr[[action|act]]ical, not metaphysical.

## Key Distinctions

### Psychological Reality vs. Physical Reality

| Domain | What Is Real | Nature |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Psychological | Qualitative differences, interpenetrating states | Immediate, lived |
| Physical | Quantitative differences, discrete magnitudes | Constructed, spatialized |

The con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of these two domains is, for Bergson, the root of the [[free-will]] problem. [[determinism|Determinism]] treats psychological states as if they were physical magnitudes, and then finds necessity where there is only [[quality|qualitative]] transformation.

### Reality and Intuition

Concepts cannot reach reality because they operate by **division and immobilization** — they carve the continuous [[becoming|flux]] into static pieces. [[Intuition]] (*[[intuition]]*) is the faculty by which we grasp reality directly, without the intermediary of spatial symbols. It is "[[intuition|sympathetic insight]]" into the becoming it[[ego|self]].

### Reality and Sensation

Bergson consistently returns to [[sensation]] as the touchstone of the real. When we examine what we [[action|act]]ually experience — in the clenched fist, in the extinguished candle — we find [[quality|qualitative]] states, not quantities:

> In reality, what you experience in your hand remains the same, but the sensation which was at first localized there has affected your arm and ascended to the shoulder… [001-070]

The [[sensation]] changes in quality, not quantity. The "increase" we report is a spatial metaphor projected onto a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation.
