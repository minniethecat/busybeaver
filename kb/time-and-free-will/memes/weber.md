## Overview

Ernst Heinrich Weber (1795–1878) was a German physiologist and anatomist at the University of Leipzig, widely recognized as a founder of experimental psychophysics. His empirical investigations into the relationship between physical stimuli and sensory perception established the [[quantity|quantitative]] study of [[sensation]]. Weber's work on t[[action|act]]ile discrimination—particularly the two-point threshold and the just-noticeable difference—provided the experimental foundation upon which [[fechner|Gustav Fechner]] later built his psychophysical law [002-069]. In *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, Bergson engages with Weber's law not as a target of refutation but as the starting point of a broader critique: the illegitimate transition from a measurable stimulus differential to a [[quantity|quantitative]] [[idea|conception]] of [[sensation]] it[[ego|self]].

## Key Ideas

### Weber's Law

Weber's law states that the minimum increase in a physical stimulus required to produce a perceptible change in [[sensation]] bears a constant ratio to the original stimulus. Formally expressed as ΔE/E = const, where E is the stimulus magnitude and ΔE is the smallest detectable increment, the law captures a fundamental regularity in sensory physiology [003-061]. Bergson presents the law through [[fechner|Fechner]]'s formulation:

> "Fechner took as his starting-point a law discovered by Weber, according to which, given a certain stimulus which calls forth a certain sensation, the amount by which the stimulus must be increased for consciousness to become aware of any change bears a fixed relation to the original stimulus." [002-070]

### The Just-Noticeable Difference

Weber's experimental method involved determining, for each sensory modality, the precise increment at which a subject first reports a difference. This threshold—the *just-noticeable difference* (JND)—became the elementary unit of [[psychophysics|psychophysical measurement]]. Bergson notes that Weber's law, in its various modified forms, remained the basis of psychophysics: "it is for experiment to decide between the relation established by Weber and its substitutes" [002-072].

## Bergson's Critique

### The Transition Problem

Bergson's engagement with Weber's law is central to his critique of psychophysics. He does not dispute the empirical validity of the law it[[ego|self]]—he grants "the probable existence of a law of this nature"—but identifies a logical gap in how [[fechner|Fechner]] uses it. The law relates *stimulus* magnitudes; [[psychophysics|Fechner's psychophysics]] attempts to derive a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of *[[sensation]]* magnitudes from it. Bergson writes:

> "It is here really a question not of measuring a sensation but only of determining the exact moment at which an increase of stimulus produces a change in it." [002-072]

The critical transition from stimulus-[[sensation]] relationship to [[sensation]]-magnitude equation is, for Bergson, where psychophysics goes wrong: "The whole of psychophysics is involved in this transition, which is therefore worthy of our closest consideration" [002-072].

### Sensation as Quality, Not Quantity

Bergson's fundamental objection is that [[sensation]]s are [[quality|qualitative]] states, not [[quantity|quantitative]] magnitudes. Weber's law describes a threshold phenomenon—the *moment* at which a change becomes perceptible—not a continuous scale of [[sensation]] intensity. The ΔS values (minimal perceptible increments of [[sensation]]) are assumed by [[fechner|Fechner]] to be equal to one another, an assumption Bergson considers unwarranted. As he elaborates in Chapter III, "it is first of all agreed to consider our consciousness of an increase of stimulus as an increase of the [[sensation]] S" [003-002]—a conflation that treats what is [[quality|qualitative]]ly perceived as though it were [[quantity|quantitative]]ly additive.

### The Fechner Connection

Weber's law achieves its full significance in Bergson's text through its role in [[fechner|Fechner]]'s logarithmic law (S = C log E/Q). Bergson treats Weber's experimental discovery and [[fechner|Fechner]]'s mathematical elaboration as a single [[intelligence|intellect]]ual [[motion|movement]]: the attempt to express psychic states in the language of physical magnitudes [003-061]. By tracing this [[motion|movement]] back to its origin in Weber's experiments, Bergson identifies the moment where a legitimate physiological observation is transformed into an illegitimate psychological [[rhythm|measure]]ment.
