# Gustav Theodor Fechner

Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801–1887) was a German physicist, philosopher, and psychologist who founded **psychophysics** — the scientific attempt to [[rhythm|measure]] the relationship between physical stimuli and psychological [[sensation]]s.

His eponymous "Fechner's Law" claimed to establish a mathematical relationship between stimulus intensity and [[sensation]] magnitude. For Bergson, Fechner represents the paradigmatic case of the error he seeks to expose: treating the **[[quality|qualitative]]** states of [[consciousness]] as if they were **[[quantity|quantitative]]** magnitudes.

## Key Ideas

### Fechner's Law

Fechner's Law states that the intensity of a [[sensation]] is proportional to the logarithm of the stimulus intensity. Building on [[Weber]]'s earlier work on "just noticeable differences," Fechner claimed that [[sensation]]s could be [[rhythm|measure]]d indirectly through the summation of minimal perceivable increments:

> (001-142) Differentials, expressing Fechner's Law…

> (003-001) …the process by which Fechner's Law is reached.

The law assumes that [[sensation]]s are homogeneous magnitudes that can be added together — precisely the assumption Bergson disputes throughout Chapter I.

### Psychophysical Measurement

Fechner pioneered experimental methods for measuring the relationship between the physical and the psychical. His "method of minimum differences" involved presenting subjects with stimuli that differed by the smallest perceptible amount:

> (003-006) Fechner's method of minimum differences…

> (003-004) Fechner realized that measurement could…

These methods were genuinely innovative and laid the foundation for experimental psychology. But Bergson argues that they [[rhythm|measure]] only the **bodily accompaniments** of [[sensation]], not [[sensation]] it[[ego|self]].

### The Psychophysical Postulate

The fundamental assumption of Fechner's enterprise — what Bergson calls the "psychophysical postulate" — is that equal differences in stimulus correspond to equal differences in [[sensation]]:

> (002-068) …this is just the postulate of Fechner's psychophysics, which we are…

Bergson argues that this postulate is unwarranted. [[sensation|Sensation]]s are not additive; they are [[quality|qualitative]] wholes that change in kind, not merely in degree.

## Bergson's Critique

### The Error of Treating Sensations as Magnitudes

Bergson's central objection to Fechner is that he treats [[sensation]]s as if they were [[extension|extensive magnitude]]s:

> (001-012) …setting up a pure intensity as a magnitude, just as if it were extended…

When Fechner asks subjects to compare two [[sensation]]s and say which is "greater," he assumes that "greater" means the same thing for [[sensation]]s as for lengths. Bergson argues that the subject is [[action|act]]ually comparing **[[quality|qualitative]]** differences, and that the numerical response reflects a spatialized reconstruction, not the original experience.

### The Localization Problem

Bergson notes that Fechner's subjects must localize [[sensation]]s in the [[matter|body]] before they can compare them:

> (001-076) …Fechner had already reduced the effort…

The "[[rhythm|measure]]ment" of [[sensation]] intensity is [[action|act]]ually a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of the **bodily surface** affected — an extensive, not intensive, phenomenon. What Fechner calls "[[sensation]] intensity" is [[action|act]]ually **extensity**.

### Tannery's Critique

Bergson aligns him[[ego|self]] with the French mathematician Jules Tannery, who criticized Fechner on mathematical grounds:

> (003-016) …The most acute of Fechner's critics, Jules Tannery, has…

> (001-322) Tannery, J., as critic of Fechner…

Tannery showed that Fechner's method of summing "minimum differences" is mathematically invalid — you cannot add together psychological thresholds that are themselves variable and [[quality|qualitative]].

### The Chapter III Analysis

In Chapter III, Bergson returns to Fechner in the context of the [[will|free will]] problem. Psychophysics, by quantifying [[sensation]]s, contributes to the deterministic [[mechanism|worldview]]:

> (003-018) …The mistake which Fechner made, as we have just seen…

> (003-007) The novel feature in Fechner's treatment is that he did not…

Bergson argues that Fechner's approach — treating [[consciousness|conscious states]] as discrete, measurable units — is not just scientifically wrong but philosophically dangerous. It reinforces the spatialized view of consciousness that makes [[will|free will]] seem impossible.

## Key Works

- *Elemente der Psychophysik* (1860) — Fechner's magnum opus, establishing psychophysics as a discipline
- *Zend-Avesta* (1851) — Philosophical work on consciousness and nature
- *Vorschule der Ästhetik* (1876) — Application of psychophysical methods to aesthetics
