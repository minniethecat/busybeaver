# Extension

Extension, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the property of occupying [[space]] — of having parts that are **external to one another**. An extended object can be divided into homogeneous parts, each occupying a distinct position. This is the legitimate domain of [[rhythm|measure]]ment and [[quantity]]. But Bergson argues that we habitually **extend** the concept of [[extensity|extension]] beyond its proper domain, applying it to [[consciousness|conscious states]] that are not extended and thereby spatializing the [[ego|self]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Extension as the Basis of Measurement

Extension is what makes [[rhythm|measure]]ment possible. Because the parts of an extended object are mutually external, they can be compared, divided, and counted:

> [003-107] …even if we assume that extension is nothing but a relation…

Even if we adopt a relational view of [[extensity|extension]] (as [[leibniz|Leibniz]] did), [[extensity|extension]] remains fundamentally spatial — a relation between terms that are **external** to one another. This is the key: [[extensity|extension]] presupposes mutual externality.

### The Illegitimate Extension of Extension

> [001-158] …illegitimate extension of…

Bergson's critique targets the "illegitimate [[extensity|extension]]" of spatial concepts to consciousness. When we speak of a "greater" joy or a "larger" ambition, we are applying the language of [[extensity|extension]] to the non-extended. This is the error that underlies both [[psychophysics]] and [[determinism]].

> [001-314] …modes of thought and modes of extension…

The Cartesian dualism of [[mind|thought]] and [[extensity|extension]] (res cogitans and res extensa) correctly separates the two domains. Bergson's criticism is that subsequent philosophy, while paying lip service to this distinction, has **smuggled** [[extensity|extension]] into its descriptions of [[mind|thought]].

### Extension and Determinism

> [003-224] …of consciousness and modes of extension…

The application of extensive cat[[self|ego]]ries to consciousness is what makes determinism seem plausible. If [[consciousness|conscious states]] are treated as extended — as having distinct, measurable parts — then [[causality|causal law]]s between these parts appear inevitable:

> [003-231] …the very extension of the principle of the consequent…

The "[[extensity|extension]] of the principle" of sufficient [[mind|reason]] from the material world to consciousness is the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual move that generates the determinism problem. Bergson's solution is to retr[[action|act]] this [[extensity|extension]]: the principle applies to extended things, not to unextended [[consciousness|conscious states]].

| Domain | Extension | Parts | Relation |
|--------|----------|-------|----------|
| Material world | Extended in space | Mutually external | Measurable (quantity) |
| Consciousness | Unextended | Interpenetrating | Qualitative (duration) |
| Mixed (illegitimate) | Pseudo-extended | Semi-distinct | Deterministic appearance |

> [003-297] …ends, by a natural extension, in proving its absolute necessity…

The "natural [[extensity|extension]]" of mechanistic [[reason|reasoning]] from physics to psychology ends in the apparent proof of absolute necessity. But this proof is circular: it assumes the very spatialization of consciousness that it claims to demonstrate.

## Key Distinctions

### Extension vs. Extensity

Bergson distinguishes between [[extensity|extension]] (the spatial property of material objects) and [[extensity]] (the [[quality|qualitative]] [[emotion|feeling]] of [[space|spatiality]] that accompanies certain [[sensation]]s, like the perception of a surface). Extensity is a **[[sensation]]** — a [[quality|qualitative]] state in duration; [[extensity|extension]] is a **property** of objects in space. The two are often confused.

### Extension vs. Duration

Extension is the principle of space: parts are external, homogeneous, measurable. [[Duration]] is the principle of time: states interpenetrate, are heterogeneous, and cannot be [[rhythm|measure]]d. The core of Bergson's philosophy is the insistence on the **irreducibility** of duration to [[extensity|extension]].

## Historical Context

### Descartes and Res Extensa

[[Descartes]] defined matter as *res extensa* — extended substance — and mind as *res cogitans* — [[thought|thinking]] substance. Bergson accepts the distinction but argues that [[descartes|Descartes]], and the Cartesian tradition, failed to think duration as a genuine alternative to [[extensity|extension]]. They treated time as a fourth dimension of [[extensity|extension]] rather than as something cat[[self|ego]]rically different.

### Leibniz and Relational Extension

[[Leibniz]] denied that [[extensity|extension]] is a substance, treating it instead as a relation between monads. Bergson acknowledges this advance but argues that even relational [[extensity|extension]] presupposes the spatial schema of mutually external terms.
