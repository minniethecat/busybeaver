## Overview

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) appears in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* in two distinct contexts: Bergson's analysis of the [[emotion|feeling]] of grace and his examination of the physiological expression of [[feeling|emotion]]. In both cases, Spencer represents a tendency Bergson resists—the reduction of conscious experience to physiological [[mechanical|mechanism]]. Spencer's evolutionary philosophy, with its emphasis on adaptation and the conservation of energy, provides Bergson with a clear target for demonstrating that aesthetic and [[feeling|emotion]]al states resist purely mechanistic explanation.

## Key Ideas

### Gracefulness as Economy of Effort

Spencer maintained that the pleasure we take in graceful [[motion|movement]] derives from a saving of effort. According to this view, graceful motions appear pleasing because they suggest an economy of muscular energy, a principle consistent with Spencer's broader evolutionary framework in which [[body|organism]]s adapt toward greater efficiency. Bergson directly engages this claim:

> "We could hardly make out why it affords us such pleasure if it were nothing but a saving of effort, as Spencer maintains." [001-039]

### The Physiological Expression of Emotion

Spencer provided detailed physiological descriptions of [[feeling|emotion]]al states, treating the bodily manifestations—cries, palpitations, tremblings—as constitutive of the [[feeling|emotion]] it[[ego|self]]. Bergson quotes Spencer's own [[language|words]] on fear:

> "Fear, when strong," says Herbert Spencer, "expresses itself in cries, in efforts to escape, in palpitations, in tremblings." [001-084]

This identification of [[feeling|emotion]] with its physical expression exemplifies, for Bergson, a broader tendency in 19th-century psychology to conflate psychic states with their organic accompaniments.

## Bergson's Critique

### Beyond Economy: The Suggestion of Possibility

Bergson's analysis of grace challenges Spencer's economy-of-effort theory by introducing a dimension Spencer misses: the perception of future [[motion|movement]] within present posture. In graceful [[motion|movement]], Bergson argues, "we imagine ourselves able to detect, besides the lightness which is a sign of mobility, some suggestion of a possible [[motion|movement]]" [001-039]. Grace is not merely about saved effort—it is about the way a visible form suggests a virtual trajectory, a temporal unfolding that Spencer's static efficiency model cannot capture.

### The Irreducible Psychic Element

Against Spencer's physiological reduction of [[feeling|emotion]], Bergson insists that even the most violently expressed [[feeling|emotion]]s contain an irreducible psychic element. Bergson goes further than Spencer: "we go further, and maintain that these [[motion|movement]]s form part of the [[feeling|emotion]] it[[ego|self]]" [001-084]. However, Bergson's point is not simply to endorse Spencer's identification of [[feeling|emotion]] with [[motion|movement]]. Rather, he argues that the intensity of [[feeling|emotion]]—what differentiates violent fear from mild unease—involves not merely the quantity of physiological disturbance but a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation of conscious experience it[[ego|self]].

### The Limits of Evolutionary Explanation

Spencer's evolutionary framework treats [[consciousness|conscious states]] as adaptive functions shaped by natural [[choice|selection]]. Bergson's critique, implicit throughout *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is that this approach systematically misses the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of conscious life. The evolutionary lens sees states in terms of their utility, not their inner char[[action|act]]er. By treating grace as saved effort and fear as adaptive response, Spencer explains what psychic states *do* but not what they *are*—a distinction central to Bergson's entire project.
