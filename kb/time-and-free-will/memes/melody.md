# Melody (mélodie)

The musical [[melody]] is Bergson's most important concrete illustration of [[duration|real duration]]. When we listen to a melody, the successive notes do not stand as separate, discrete units laid out side by side in [[space]]; they **melt into one another**, forming an indivisible, [[quality|qualitative]] whole whose parts exist only as moments of a single [[consciousness|continuous act]]. The melody thus makes palpable the distinction between [[multiplicity|qualitative multiplicity]] (the interpenetration of [[consciousness|conscious states]]) and [[quantity|quantitative multiplicity]] (the juxtaposition of objects in space).

## Definition

A melody, for Bergson, is not a sequence of isolated notes but an **organic [[interpenetration|fusion]]** in which each note is colored by what precedes it and anticipates what follows. The experience of melody is the closest analogue in [[sensation|external perception]] to the inner experience of duration:

| Aspect | Melody as Heard | Melody as Analyzed |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Experience | Indivisible whole, [[quality|qualitative]] | Discrete notes, [[quantity|quantitative]] |
| Temporal mode | [[duration|Real duration]] — interpenetration | [[time|Homogeneous time]] — juxtaposition |
| Model for | [[consciousness]] as lived from within | Consciousness as observed from without |
| Philosophical import | [[free-will|Freedom]] is possible | [[determinism|Determinism]] seems necessary |

> (003-173) …he sounds form an indivisible melody in so far as they are pure se…

## Bergson's Usage

### The Melody of Inner Life

Bergson invokes melody to make vivid his central claim: [[consciousness|conscious states]] are not separable atoms (as [[associationism]] would have it) but form an indivisible continuity analogous to a musical phrase:

> (003-151) …embling that of a phrase in a melody.

Just as the [[quality|beauty]] of a melody disappears when we isolate individual notes for analysis, the [[free-will|freedom]] and [[duration|life]] of consciousness vanish when we spatialize it into discrete, measurable units.

### Melody and the Critique of Psychophysics

The melody analogy also serves as a critique of [[psychophysics]]:

> (003-224) …cular vibrations, just as the melody is on the rhythmical movement…

The [[psychophysics|psychophysicist]] attempts to reduce the melody of consciousness to the physical vibrations that accompany it — to substitute the [[quantity|quantitative]] for the [[quality|qualitative]]. But the melody *as heard* is not identical to the physical vibrations *as [[rhythm|measure]]d*; the former is an indivisible whole, the latter a divisible sequence. Psychophysics, by taking the [[rhythm|measure]]d vibrations as [[reality|the real]]ity and the heard melody as an [[illusion|epiphenomenon]], confuses the symbol for the thing symbolized.

## Key Distinctions

### Melody vs. Homogeneous Time

The crucial contrast. In [[time|homogeneous time]], moments are external to one another — t₁, t₂, t₃ stand side by side like points on a line. In a melody, by contrast, each note penetrates the others, forming a continuous, [[quality|qualitative]] whole. The melody IS duration made audible.

### Melody vs. Associationism

[[associationism|Associationist psychology]] would analyze a melody as a sequence of discrete [[sensation]]s linked by [[association|laws of association]]. Bergson's point is that no accumulation of discrete notes can account for the *quality* of the heard melody — the emergent whole that is more than (and different from) the sum of its parts. The same applies to [[consciousness]]: no accumulation of discrete mental states can account for the living unity of the [[ego|self]].

### Melody as Analogy, Not Identity

Bergson is careful: melody is an *analogy* for duration, not an identity. The notes of a melody are still external to one another in physical [[space]]; it is only in the [[action|act]] of [[perception|hearing]] them that they fuse. Duration proper — the inner life of [[consciousness]] — is an even deeper interpenetration, one that does not depend on an external sequence at all.
