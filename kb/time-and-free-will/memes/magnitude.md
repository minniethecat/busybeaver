# Magnitude

Magnitude, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, reveals a fundamental con[[interpenetration|fusion]] at the heart of psychophysics. We routinely speak of [[sensation]]s having **greater or lesser magnitude** — a louder sound, a brighter light, a more intense pain. But Bergson argues that "[[intensity|intensive magnitude]]" is a **contradiction in terms**: magnitude belongs to space, and intensity is purely [[quality|qualitative]]. The very idea of measuring intensity is the original error from which determinism and the denial of [[will|free will]] ultimately follow.

## Bergson's Argument

### Two Kinds of Magnitude

> [001-006] …such differences applicable to magnitudes but not to intensities.

Bergson opens Chapter I by distinguishing extensive and [[intensity|intensive magnitude]]. Extensive magnitude is legitimate: it [[rhythm|measure]]s objects in space (length, area, volume). But [[intensity|intensive magnitude]] is **illegitimate**: we speak of a greater pain, a more intense effort, a brighter [[sensation]] — as if these were measurable differences of degree rather than **differences of kind**.

> [001-008] This conception of intensive magnitude seems, indeed, to be that of…

Common sense and psychophysics both accept [[intensity|intensive magnitude]] as unproblematic. Bergson's entire first chapter is devoted to showing that this acceptance is the root of multiple philosophical errors.

### Intensive Magnitude as Confused Notion

> [001-010] …quantity: extensive and intensive magnitude.

The distinction between "extensive" and "intensive" magnitude already concedes the error: it assumes that both are species of a single genus — quantity. But if intensity is not quantity at all, then "[[intensity|intensive magnitude]]" is a **cat[[self|ego]]ry mistake**. What we call an increase in intensity is [[reality|real]]ly a [[change|qualitative change]]:

> [001-009] …intensity can be assimilated to a magnitude.

When we say a light is "brighter" or a sound is "louder," we are not measuring a pure increase in a single quality. We are perceiving a [[change|qualitative change]] in the whole of our conscious state — a change we then **spatialize** by converting it into the language of magnitude.

### Magnitude and the Psychophysical Law

The [[fechner|Fechner]]-[[weber|Weber]] law — that [[sensation]] increases as the logarithm of stimulus — assumes that [[sensation]]s have magnitudes that can be [[rhythm|measure]]d and compared. Bergson's critique of magnitude is thus also a critique of **psychophysics** as a science: if [[sensation]]s are not magnitudes, they cannot be [[rhythm|measure]]d, and psychophysics is measuring something else (the physical stimulus, or the muscular contr[[action|act]]ion accompanying the [[sensation]], not the [[sensation]] it[[ego|self]]).

## Key Distinctions

### Magnitude vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]] is not a magnitude. When we say a pain is "more intense" than another, we do not mean that it contains more units of pain — pain has no units. We mean that the pain has [[quality|qualitative]]ly changed, permeating more of our psychic life. "Greater" intensity is a metaphor borrowed from space.

### Magnitude vs. Number

[[Number]] presupposes the spatial juxtaposition of units. Magnitude presupposes the measurability of a continuous quantity. Both are spatial concepts illegitimately applied to [[consciousness|conscious states]].

### Extensive vs. Intensive Magnitude

Extensive magnitude (length, area) is the legitimate domain of [[rhythm|measure]]ment — it belongs to [[space]]. Intensive magnitude is a projection of spatial [[rhythm|measure]]ment onto **quality**, where it has no application. The entire project of [[quantity|quantitative]] psychology, Bergson argues, rests on this projection.

## Historical Context

Bergson's argument against [[intensity|intensive magnitude]] targets **[[fechner|Fechner]]** and the psychophysical tradition. [[fechner|Fechner]] claimed to have discovered a mathematical law relating physical stimulus to [[sensation]] magnitude. Bergson's response is that the very project is incoherent: you cannot [[rhythm|measure]] what is not a magnitude. Later philosophers ([[james|James]], Whitehead, Merleau-Ponty) developed similar critiques of [[psychophysics|psychophysical measurement]].
