# Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) is the philosopher whom Bergson engages with most extensively in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*. Kant's critical philosophy — particularly his theory of space as an *a priori* form of [[intuition]], his distinction between matter and form of knowledge, and his treatment of [[free-will|freedom]] — serves as both a foil and a foundation for Bergson's own arguments about [[duration]], [[consciousness]], and [[free-will]].

## Key Ideas

### Space as A Priori Form

Kant's most important contribution to the issues Bergson addresses is his theory of space. In the *Transcendental Aesthetic* of the *Critique of Pure [[reason|Reason]]*, Kant argued that space is not a property of things in themselves but an *a priori* form of [[intuition]] — a condition of the possibility of experience imposed by the mind:

> (001-095) …Aesthetic, Kant's Transcendental…

> (001-211) Kant, theory of space…

Bergson largely accepts Kant's char[[action|act]]erization of space as a form imposed by the mind, but he draws a different conclusion. For Bergson, this shows that space is **not** a form of things as they are in themselves but a pr[[action|act]]ical construction — and that duration, by contrast, is [[reality|the real]] form of conscious life.

### Matter and Form of Knowledge

Kant distinguished between the **matter** of [[sensation]] (the raw given) and the **form** (the organizing structures — space and time — imposed by the mind):

> (001-178) …Kant's distinction between matter …

> (001-146) …Kant's, between matter and form of consciousness…

Bergson reinterprets this distinction: the "matter" is the [[quality|qualitative]] content of [[consciousness|immediate consciousness]] (duration), and the "form" is the spatial framework we project onto it. Kant's error, in Bergson's view, was to treat time (as a form) on the same spatial model as space, missing the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of [[duration|real duration]].

### Freedom and the Practical Reason

Kant famously argued that [[free-will|freedom]] is possible because we can distinguish between the **phenomenal** [[ego|self]] (subject to [[causality|causal law]]s in space and time) and the **noumenal** [[ego|self]] (free, outside space and time):

> (003-449) Kant clung to freedom, but put the…

> (001-284) …Kant's doctrine of the Practical…

Kant "saved" [[free-will|freedom]] by placing it outside the phenomenal world — in the noumenal [[reality|real]]m of pr[[action|act]]ical [[mind|reason]]. Bergson agrees that [[free-will|freedom]] cannot be found in the spatialized, deterministic world of science, but he rejects Kant's two-world solution. For Bergson, [[free-will|freedom]] is not a property of a noumenal [[ego|self]] hidden behind appearances; it is a **direct f[[action|act]] of [[consciousness|immediate consciousness]]** when duration is grasped correctly.

> (003-450) Kant's great mistake was to take time as a homogeneous medium…

### The Self and Inner Sense

Kant's theory of inner sense treated time as the form of inner [[intuition]], just as space is the form of outer [[intuition]]. Bergson's critique is that Kant **[[time|spatialized time]]** — treating it as a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] like space:

> (001-149) …Kant put self outside…

> (001-182) …Kant on, [232]…

By treating time as a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]], Kant made the same error as the determinists — he placed [[consciousness|conscious states]] side by side in a spatialized temporal order. Bergson's [[duration]] is the corrective: time as [[action|act]]ually lived is [[quality|qualitative]] and heterogeneous.

## Bergson's Engagement

### Critique

Bergson's principal critique of Kant is that he **spatialized consciousness**. By treating time as a form parallel to space, Kant missed the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of duration:

> (003-104) …this latter conception to Kant: the theory which he works out…

> (003-107) …the solution given by Kant does not seem to have been seriously challenged…

Kant's framework, Bergson argues, makes it impossible to grasp [[free-will|freedom]] correctly. If inner sense is structured like outer sense (as a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]), then [[consciousness|conscious states]] appear as separate objects, and determinism follows.

### Appropriation

Despite his criticisms, Bergson appropriates several Kantian insights:

- The idea that **space is a form imposed by the mind** — Bergson radicalizes this by arguing that space is a pr[[action|act]]ical construction for [[action|act]]ion, not a condition of all possible experience.
- The distinction between **matter and form** — Bergson reinterprets this as the distinction between [[quality|qualitative]] duration (matter) and spatialized representation (form).
- The **primacy of the subject** — Bergson shares Kant's starting point in the analysis of conscious experience, even if he rejects Kant's conclusions.

## Key Works

- *Critique of Pure [[reason|Reason]]* (1781/1787) — The *Transcendental Aesthetic* is Bergson's primary target
- *Critique of Pr[[action|act]]ical [[reason|Reason]]* (1788) — Kant's treatment of [[free-will|freedom]] via the noumenal [[ego|self]]
- *Critique of [[judgment|Judgment]]* (1790) — Aesthetic judgment, which connects to Bergson's analysis of [[intensity]] and [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]]
