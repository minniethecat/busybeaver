# Feeling

Feeling, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the paradigm case of **[[quality|qualitative]] consciousness** — a state whose apparent intensity cannot be [[rhythm|measure]]d or quantified without falsifying its nature. The analysis of [[emotion|feeling]] serves as Bergson's primary argument against psychophysics: if the "intensity" of a [[emotion|feeling]] is not a magnitude, then the [[quantity|quantitative]] model of consciousness collapses. Feelings are not more or less of a single thing; they are **[[quality|qualitative]]ly different** states at each moment.

## Bergson's Argument

### Feeling and the Illusion of Intensity

> [001-002] …of consciousness, sensations, feelings, passions, efforts, are capable of growth and diminution…

Bergson begins by noting our common pr[[action|act]]ice: we speak of one [[emotion|feeling]] as being "stronger" than another, of a [[desire|passion]] "growing," of an effort being "greater." But this language, he argues, smuggles in a spatial metaphor. When we say a desire has "grown," we imagine it **extending** in space — as if it covered more surface, or occupied more volume. This spatialization is precisely what psychophysics relies on, and precisely what Bergson sets out to dismantle.

### Aesthetic Feelings and Grace

> [001-036] The aesthetic feelings. Their increasing intensities are here resolved into as many different feelings…

The aesthetic feelings offer a striking confirmation of Bergson's thesis. When we move from indifference to admiration, from admiration to rapture, we are not experiencing **more** of the same [[emotion|feeling]] — we are experiencing **different** [[emotion|feeling]]s, each filling the whole of consciousness at its moment.

> [001-040] …sympathy enters into the feeling of grace. Now, in analysing the feeling of grace, we find that it is not a simple sensation but involves the perception of a certain ease in movement…

The [[emotion|feeling]] of grace is not a [[magnitude|measurable quantity]]. It is a unique quality that arises from the sympathy between the perceiver and the rhythm of the work. Art does not deliver a larger dose of the same [[emotion|feeling]] — it changes the **quality** of our awareness:

> [001-045] Thus art aims at impressing feelings on us rather than expressing them…

| Conception of Feeling | Model | Problem |
|----------------------|-------|---------|
| Psychophysics | [[quantity|Quantity]] (more/less intense) | Treats quality as if spatial |
| Bergson | Qualitative multiplicity | Each [[emotion|feeling]] is a unique whole |

### The Body and Feeling

> [001-028] …increasing number of muscular contractions which take place on the surface of the body, thus converting them into one single feeling…

Bergson does not deny that [[emotion|feeling]]s have bodily accompaniments. But the bodily changes — quickened pulse, [[tension|muscular tension]], tears — are not the **cause** of the [[emotion|feeling]], nor are they what makes one [[emotion|feeling]] "more intense" than another. The [[matter|body]] is the **surface** on which the [[change|qualitative change]] of [[emotion|feeling]] registers; it is not the [[rhythm|measure]] of that change. The physiological changes that accompany a deep joy and a deep sorrow may be similar; what differs is the **quality** of the inner state.

## Key Distinctions

### Feeling vs. Sensation

A [[sensation]] (e.g., a pinprick, a flash of light) has a relatively localizable bodily correlate. A [[emotion|feeling]] (e.g., joy, melancholy) pervades the whole of consciousness and cannot be localized. [[sensation|Sensation]] tends toward the **extensive**; [[emotion|feeling]] toward the **intensive**.

### Feeling vs. Emotion

An [[emotion]] is a [[emotion|feeling]] that has been named, classified, and thereby socialized. Bergson's analysis focuses on the pre-social, [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er of [[emotion|feeling]] before it is captured by language. The word "anger" spatializes a [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|flux]] into a fixed cat[[self|ego]]ry.

### Feeling vs. Effort

[[Effort]] involves a felt tension between inner state and outer [[action|act]]ion — it is the experience of **transition**. Feeling, by contrast, is a state that fills consciousness without necessarily involving [[action|act]]ion. The [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is thus a hybrid: it combines the [[intensity|qualitative intensity]] of [[emotion|feeling]] with the bodily awareness of [[action|act]]ion.

## Historical Context

Bergson's theory of [[emotion|feeling]] engages with **[[spencer|Herbert Spencer]]'s** evolutionary psychology (which treated aesthetic feeling as a "saving of effort"), **[[wundt|Wilhelm Wundt]]'s** introspective psychology, and **[[fechner|Gustav Fechner]]'s** psychophysics. [[spencer|Spencer]]'s view was that aesthetic feeling is a surplus of nervous energy discharged without pr[[action|act]]ical purpose. Bergson's objection is that this reduces [[quality|qualitative]] experience to physiological quantity — precisely the error he diagnoses throughout Chapter 1. [[fechner|Fechner]]'s logarithmic law ([[sensation]] intensity ∝ log stimulus) assumes that [[emotion|feeling]]s are measurable; Bergson argues this assumption begs the question.
