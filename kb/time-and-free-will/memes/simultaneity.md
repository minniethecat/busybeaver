# Simultaneity

Simultaneity, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the **bridge** between [[time]] and [[space]]. When we [[rhythm|measure]] time — when we say an event lasted five minutes — we translate [[quality|qualitative]] [[succession]] into spatial simultaneity: we imagine all the elapsed moments coexisting side by side, like points on a line. Simultaneity is thus the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual operation that makes [[rhythm|measure]]ment possible, but it does so by **falsifying** the nature of duration, turning lived flow into a static array.

## Bergson's Argument

### Simultaneity as the Spatialization of Succession

Bergson's key insight is that to [[rhythm|measure]] time, we must first spatialize it:

> [003-125] …succession is converted into simultaneity and is projected into space.

When we look at a clock, we do not perceive time directly. We perceive the **simultaneous** positions of the hands on the dial — spatial positions that coexist at a single instant. We then interpret these spatial simultaneities as [[rhythm|measure]]s of a temporal interval. The entire edifice of [[rhythm|measure]]d time rests on this substitution.

> [P02-002] …of duration with extensity, of succession with simultaneity, of quality with quantity: this confusion once dispelled…

The con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of succession with simultaneity is one of the three fundamental con[[interpenetration|fusion]]s Bergson aims to dispel. When we treat successive moments as simultaneous points, we **destroy** the very nature of succession — the f[[action|act]] that in genuine duration, the past survives in the present and the future is genuinely open.

### The Connecting Link

> [001-311] …simultaneity as connecting link between time and space…

Simultaneity is the "connecting link" between time and space because it is the one point where the temporal and the spatial genuinely intersect. In the perception of an external event, consciousness and matter, duration and space, meet:

> [003-148] …space and duration, is simultaneity, which might be defined as the…

Simultaneity can be defined as the **intersection of time with space** — the point at which a duration (a conscious [[action|act]] of perception) coincides with a spatial configuration (the position of the clock hands).

| Operation | Input | Output | What Is Lost |
|-----------|-------|--------|-------------|
| Measuring time | Qualitative succession (duration) | Quantitative interval (seconds) | Interpenetration of past and present |
| Drawing a timeline | Lived flow of moments | Simultaneous points on a line | The openness of the future |
| Clock reading | Continuous becoming | Coexisting spatial positions | The [[quality|qualitative]] uniqueness of each moment |

### The Two Kinds of Simultaneity

Bergson distinguishes between two senses of simultaneity:

> [001-149] …two conceptions of, in causality…

The first is the simultaneity of **external events** — two things happening "at the same time" in the physical world. This is a legitimate spatial concept. The second is the simultaneity imposed on **[[consciousness|conscious states]]** — the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual operation by which we treat successive inner states as coexisting. This second sense is the source of philosophical error.

> [001-318] …attempt to represent by simultaneity…

The attempt to represent duration by simultaneity is doomed because duration is essentially **successive** — not in the spatial sense of "one after another" but in the [[quality|qualitative]] sense of continuous [[ego|self]]-creation. To represent it as simultaneity is to kill it.

## Key Distinctions

### Simultaneity vs. Succession

[[Succession]] is the temporal relation proper to duration: states interpenetrate in a continuous flow. Simultaneity is the spatial substitute for succession: states are arrayed side by side. The con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of the two is, for Bergson, the root of the determinism problem.

### Simultaneity vs. Duration

[[Duration]] is pure succession without mutual externality. Simultaneity is pure coexistence without succession. The concept of [[time|homogeneous time]] attempts to combine the two — to have succession within a framework of simultaneity — but this hybrid is incoherent: either states succeed one another [[quality|qualitative]]ly, or they coexist spatially; they cannot do both.

### Simultaneity and Measurement

> [001-134] …of simultaneity…

Measurement requires simultaneity. To say something is "five times as long" requires placing the five units side by side in [[mind|thought]]. This is why [[rhythm|measure]]ment, which is perfectly legitimate for space, distorts time: it replaces succession with coexistence.

## Historical Context

### Kant on Simultaneity

[[Kant]] treated simultaneity as one of the cat[[self|ego]]ries of the [[mind|understanding]], applied to the [[multiplicity|manifold]] of [[intuition]]. Bergson's critique is that [[kant|Kant]] took the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual representation of time (as a form of simultaneity) for time it[[ego|self]]. The [[reality|real]] temporal relation is succession, not simultaneity.

### Einstein and Bergson

Though *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* predates Einstein's relativity (the book was published in 1889), Bergson later engaged directly with Einstein's concept of time. Bergson argued that relativistic simultaneity — the relativity of "at the same time" across different reference frames — confirms rather than refutes his claim that simultaneity is fundamentally **spatial**, not temporal. The debate between Bergson and Einstein (1922) is a landmark in the philosophy of time.
