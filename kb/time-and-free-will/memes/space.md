# Space

Space (*l'espace*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the **[[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]** in which objects are juxtaposed side by side, distinct and measurable. It is the domain of **quantity**, division, and numeration. Bergson's central thesis is that we habitually project this spatial framework onto [[consciousness]] it[[ego|self]] — a projection that generates the illusions of determinism, measurable [[intensity]], and the denial of [[free-will]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Space as the Medium of Quantitative Distinction

Bergson opens his analysis with a fundamental observation: whenever we distinguish one thing from another as *more* or *less*, we are [[thought|thinking]] spatially:

> [001-007] …in both cases we allude to unequal spaces, as shall be shown in detail…

To say that one [[sensation]] is "more intense" than another is to think of it as **occupying more space** — as if [[sensation]]s were extended magnitudes. This is the root error that Chapter I systematically exposes.

### The Projection of Space onto Consciousness

[[language|Language]] it[[ego|self]], Bergson argues, compels us to spatialize:

> [P02-001] …we usually think in terms of space. That is to say, language requires us to establish between our concepts distinctions that are clear-cut, precise — in a word, spatial.

Because [[language|words]] are discrete tokens, they impose a **spatial logic** on the fluid, interpenetrating [[reality|real]]ity of [[duration]]. We break the continuous stream of consciousness into named states — "joy," "sadness," "anger" — and then treat these as if they were separate objects in space.

### Space and Number

The [[action|act]] of [[number|counting]], for Bergson, already presupposes space. To count items, we must retain each one as a distinct unit while adding the next — and this retention requires a **[[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]]** in which units are juxtaposed:

| Domain | Medium | Char[[action|act]]er |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| External world | Space | Homogeneous, [[quantity|quantitative]], divisible |
| Consciousness | Duration | Heterogeneous, [[quality|qualitative]], interpenetrating |
| **Projection error** | Space projected onto consciousness | Makes the [[quality|qualitative]] appear [[quantity|quantitative]] |

## Key Distinctions

### Space vs. Duration

[[Duration]] (*[[duration|durée]]*) is the [[succession|qualitative succession]] of [[consciousness|conscious states]] in which each moment **interpenetrates** the others. Space is the ex[[action|act]] opposite: a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] of mutual **externality** in which parts exist alongside one another without interpenetration. The con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of duration with space is, in Bergson's view, the fundamental error of Western philosophy.

### Space vs. Extensity

[[Extensity]] (*[[extensity|extensité]]*) is the concrete, [[quality|qualitative]] [[emotion|feeling]] of [[extension|spatial extension]] as experienced by the senses — the "spread" of a [[sensation]] across the [[matter|body]]. Space, by contrast, is the **abstr[[action|act]]**, [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] constructed by the [[intelligence|intellect]]. The psychophysicists ([[Fechner]], [[Weber]]) conflate extensity with measurable space, assuming that because [[sensation]] *feels* extended it can be *[[rhythm|measure]]d*.

## Historical Context

### Kant's Theory of Space

[[Kant]] made space an *a priori* form of [[intuition]] — a condition of possible experience rather than a property of things in themselves. Bergson acknowledges [[kant|Kant]]'s insight but argues that [[kant|Kant]] was wrong to treat space and time as parallel forms. [[time|Time]], for Bergson, is not the [[time|spatialized time]] of the scientist (a fourth dimension) but the [[quality|qualitative]] duration of consciousness.

### The Empiricist Theory of Space

[[Mill]] and [[Spencer]] treated space as a generalization from experience — a product of association. Bergson rejects both the empiricist and [[kant|Kant]]ian accounts: space is neither learned from experience nor a pure form of [[intuition]]; it is the **natural medium** of our pr[[action|act]]ical, [[action|act]]ion-oriented intelligence, which we illegitimately extend to conscious life.
