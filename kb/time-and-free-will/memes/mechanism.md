# Mechanism vs. Dynamism Debate

The debate between **[[mechanical|mechanism]]** and **[[dynamism]]** was one of the central philosophical controversies of 19th-century science. Mechanism held that all natural phenomena could be reduced to matter in motion governed by mathematical laws; [[dynamism]] countered that **forces** — not merely matter — were the fundamental [[reality|real]]ity. Bergson engages this debate in Chapter III of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, arguing that both sides share a deeper assumption that makes [[free-will]] unintelligible: the spatialization of [[duration]].

## Background

### The Rise of Mechanism

The mechanistic worldview achieved its greatest triumphs in the 17th and 18th centuries. [[Descartes]] reduced physics to matter and motion; [[Newton]] discovered universal laws of gravitation. By the early 19th century, **Laplace's demon** — the idea that a perfect intelligence knowing all positions and velocities could predict the entire future — expressed the mechanist ideal:

> (003-367) Nowhere has mechanism been carried further than in…

> (003-374) …less perhaps of a Spinozistic mechanism…

[[Spinoza]]'s philosophy represented the metaphysical culmination of [[mechanical|mechanism]] — a universe where everything follows with logical necessity from the nature of substance.

### The Dynamist Reaction

Dynamism arose as a re[[action|act]]ion against the perceived inadequacies of pure [[mechanical|mechanism]]. Thinkers like **[[leibniz|Leibniz]]** argued that force, energy, and [[action|act]]ivity — not passive matter — were the fundamental [[reality|real]]ities. [[matter|Matter]] it[[ego|self]] was a manifestation of force:

> (003-208) …two rival systems of nature, mechanism and dynamism. Dynamism starts from…

For the dynamist, laws are abstr[[action|act]] summaries of the behavior of [[reality|real]] forces; for the mechanist, forces are names for the regularities captured in laws.

## The Debate

### Competing Ontologies

The debate between [[mechanical|mechanism]] and [[dynamism]] was fundamentally about what is **most [[reality|real]]**:

| | Mechanism | Dynamism |
|---|-----------|----------|
| Fundamental [[reality|real]]ity | [[matter|Matter]] in motion | Force / [[action|act]]ivity |
| Status of laws | Primary (laws as fundamental) | Secondary (laws as summaries) |
| Status of matter | [[substance|Substance]] | Manifestation of force |
| Causation | Efficient (push-pull) | Teleological (purpose-directed) |
| [[determinism|Determinism]] | Strict | Qualified (forces can be spontaneous) |
| Representative | [[Spinoza]], Laplace | [[leibniz|Leibniz]], Maine de Biran |

> (003-209) …facts more real than laws: mechanism reverses this attitude…

### The Problem of Free Will

Both [[mechanical|mechanism]] and [[dynamism]] had implications for the [[will|free will]] problem. For the mechanist, [[will|free will]] is impossible — all events, including human [[action|act]]ions, follow necessarily from prior states:

> (001-245) …mechanism cannot escape…

For the dynamist, there appeared to be more room for [[free-will|freedom]] — if force is spontaneous [[action|act]]ivity, perhaps the will is a force that can initiate [[action|act]]ion. But Bergson argues that this is an illusion: [[dynamism]], no less than [[mechanical|mechanism]], spatializes duration and thus misses [[reality|the real]]ity of [[free-will|freedom]]:

> (003-293) …being essentially a devotee of mechanism, it loves clear-cut distinctions…

Even [[dynamism]], by treating consciousness as a force alongside other forces, imposes a spatial framework that makes genuine [[will|free will]] unintelligible.

## Bergson's Intervention

### Beyond Mechanism and Dynamism

Bergson's key move is to show that **both** [[mechanical|mechanism]] and [[dynamism]] share a common assumption — they spatialize time. For the mechanist, time is a fourth dimension of space where states succeed one another like points on a line. For the dynamist, time is the medium in which forces operate. Both treat duration as if it were homogeneous and measurable:

> (003-211) …mechanism and dynamism take the word simply…

Bergson's [[duration]] offers a way out of the debate. When we recover the [[quality|qualitative]], interpenetrating char[[action|act]]er of conscious time, the very terms of the mechanism-dynamism debate dissolve. The free [[action|act]] is not a force overcoming mechanical necessity; it is the expression of the [[self|whole self]] in duration.

### The Spinozistic Temptation

Bergson notes the enduring appeal of a "Spinozistic [[mechanical|mechanism]]" — the idea that mathematical necessity governs everything:

> (003-374) …less perhaps of a Spinozistic mechanism; but it will be seen that…

> (003-375) …the idea of a mathematical mechanism or unless some subtle metaphysics…

This remains tempting because it satisfies the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s demand for clear, distinct relations. But it does so at the cost of denying the immediate data of consciousness — the experience of duration and [[free-will|freedom]].

### The Practical Function of Mechanism

Bergson does not reject [[mechanical|mechanism]] outright. He acknowledges its **pr[[action|act]]ical** value — mechanistic [[thought|thinking]] is necessary for [[action|act]]ion in the physical world. The error is to mistake this pr[[action|act]]ical construction for metaphysical truth:

> (003-279) But this mechanism, to which we have condemned ourselves…

> (003-464) …the very mechanism by which we only meant at first…

Mechanism is a tool for manipulating the world; it becomes an error when it is taken as a complete description of [[reality|real]]ity.

## Consequences

Bergson's dissolution of the mechanism-dynamism debate is central to his argument for [[will|free will]]. If both [[mechanical|mechanism]] and [[dynamism]] spatialize duration, then the [[will|free will]] debate as traditionally conceived (determinism vs. indeterminism) is a **false problem**. The [[reality|real]] question is not whether the will can break causal chains but whether we can recover the experience of duration in which [[free-will|freedom]] is directly given.
