# Succession

Succession, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a term that conceals a fundamental ambiguity. There are **two kinds of succession**: the qualitative succession of [[consciousness|conscious states]] in [[duration]], where each moment interpenetrates the others without clear separation; and the [[quantity|quantitative]] succession of points on a spatial line, where terms are distinct, external, and countable. The failure to distinguish these two senses is, Bergson argues, the hidden source of many philosophical errors — including the belief that [[causality]] can be reduced to regular succession.

## Bergson's Argument

### Succession Without Distinction

Bergson's key insight is that conscious succession is not like the ticking of a clock. In [[duration]], states do not follow one another as separate items; they **interpenetrate**:

> [001-126] …succession without distinction in…

This "succession without distinction" is hard to grasp because our [[intelligence|intellect]], shaped by [[space]], demands clear boundaries between terms. We habitually project the spatial schema of **before-and-after-as-separate-places** onto our inner life. But in genuine duration, the past permeates the present; what we call "succession" is the continuous **thickening** of consciousness, not a sequence of discrete instants.

> [001-146] …succession without…

To experience succession without mutual externality is to experience time as it [[reality|real]]ly is — not as a container filled with events, but as the very **[[matter|stuff]]** of conscious life.

### The Confusion of Succession with Simultaneity

One of Bergson's most penetrating [[analysis|analyse]]s concerns the con[[interpenetration|fusion]] between succession and [[simultaneity]]:

> [P02-002] …of duration with extensity, of succession with simultaneity, of quality with quantity: this confusion once dispelled…

When we try to **[[rhythm|measure]]** succession — when we place events on a timeline — we convert genuine succession into simultaneity. We imagine all the moments spread out before us at once, like points on a line. This spatialization makes [[rhythm|measure]]ment possible but destroys the very nature of temporal succession.

| Kind of Succession | Terms | Relation | Domain |
|-------------------|-------|----------|--------|
| Qualitative (duration) | Interpenetrating | [[organic|Organic]] growth | Consciousness |
| Quantitative (space) | Mutually external | [[mechanical|Mechanical]] sequence | Physical world |
| Mixed (everyday time) | Partially distinct | Measurable before/after | Pr[[action|act]]ical life |

### Succession and Causality

The empiricist tradition — from [[Hume]] to [[Mill]] — had reduced [[causality]] to "regular succession": we say A causes B when we observe A regularly followed by B. Bergson replies that this reduction only works if we have **already spatialized** the terms:

> [001-116] …as regular succession…

In [[duration]], a conscious state does not "succeed" a previous one as a separate event; it grows out of it. The connection is not external (regularity) but internal (organic continuity). When the empiricist treats [[causality|causal connection]] as mere regular succession, he has already smuggled in the spatial assumption of distinct, countable terms.

> [001-182] …causality as regular succession…

## Key Distinctions

### Succession vs. Duration

Succession in the spatial sense is what we get when we **[[rhythm|measure]]** duration — when we break the continuous flow into discrete instants. But duration it[[ego|self]] is not a succession of instants; it is a **continuous interpenetration**. Bergson sometimes calls this "pure succession" to distinguish it from the spatialized succession of everyday language:

> [001-149] …order of succession in…

The "order of succession" in duration is not a linear order (first A, then B, then C) but a [[quality|qualitative]] order in which each phase **contains** the previous ones in a transformed state.

### Succession vs. Simultaneity

> [001-166] …endosmosis between succession and…

The [[intelligence|intellect]] constantly confuses succession and [[simultaneity]] because it wants to spatialize time. When we draw a timeline, we represent successive moments as simultaneous points on a page. This con[[interpenetration|fusion]] is not merely a theoretical nicety; it is the root of the determinism problem. If the future already exists as a determinate point on a timeline, then [[will|free will]] is impossible.

### The Compromise

> [001-156] …compromise:—between succession and externality…

Bergson notes that pr[[action|act]]ical life requires a compromise between pure qualitative succession and spatial externality. We need clocks and calendars to coordinate [[action|act]]ion. But philosophy must not mistake this pr[[action|act]]ical compromise for a metaphysical truth about time.

## Historical Context

### Hume and the Regular Succession Theory

[[Hume]]'s analysis of causality as "constant conjunction" — A is regularly followed by B, therefore A causes B — is the classical statement of the succession theory of causality. Bergson's critique targets not Hume's empiricism per se, but the spatial assumption that A and B are **separable items** whose connection can be observed externally.

### The Psychologists on Temporal Order

> [001-159] …philosophers, on extensity and succession…

Nineteenth-century psychologists debated whether our perception of temporal order was innate or learned. Bergson sidesteps this debate by arguing that the very idea of "temporal order" — as a linear sequence of distinct items — already presupposes spatialization. What is given in immediate experience is not order but **interpenetration**.
