# Free Will

Free will, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and Free Will*, is not a metaphysical postulate but a **concrete f[[action|act]] of conscious experience**. The free [[action|act]] is one that emanates from the whole of the [[ego|self]], expressing the unique [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er formed by the totality of one's lived [[duration]]. Bergson's contribution is not to prove that we are free — he takes freedom as a datum — but to show why the traditional debate between determinism and libertarianism is **badly posed**: both sides spatialize consciousness, treating it as if it were a chain of separable states in [[space]].

## Bergson's Argument

### The Error of Both Sides

Bergson argues that defenders and opponents of [[will|free will]] share a common error:

> [003-305] Defenders and opponents of free will alike forget this—the former when they conceive the free act as an effect without a cause, the latter when they represent it as inevitably determined by its antecedents.

Both sides treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as **juxtaposed in space** — as discrete, mutually external items that can be arranged in causal chains. But in [[duration]], [[consciousness|conscious states]] **interpenetrate**; they are not separate items that could be linked by deterministic laws, nor are they isolated events that could break free of [[causality|causation]] entirely.

### The Free Act as Expression of the Whole Self

For Bergson, a truly free [[action|act]] is one that expresses the **entire personality**:

> [003-287] "To be conscious of free will," says Stuart Mill, "must mean to be conscious, before I have decided, that I am able to decide either way."

Bergson replies that this very formulation — the ability to "decide either way" — spatializes the [[action|act]] of choosing, as if the [[ego|self]] stands before a fork in a path. The genuinely free [[action|act]] does not involve deliberating between pre-formed alternatives; it is the gradual maturation of a [[choice|decision]] that **grows out of** the [[self|whole self]], in the same way that a living [[body|organism]] develops according to its entire past.

| Conception | View of [[self|Self]] | View of [[choice|Choice]] |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| [[determinism|Determinism]] | [[self|Self]] = sum of discrete motives | Strongest motive wins (mechanical) |
| Libertarianism | [[self|Self]] = arbiter between equal options | Indifferent choice (abstr[[action|act]] freedom) |
| **Bergson** | [[self|Self]] = [[quality|qualitative]] whole in duration | Act expresses total personality (concrete freedom) |

## Key Distinctions

### Free Will vs. Indeterminism

Bergson's [[will|free will]] is **not indeterminism**. The libertarian pictures the [[ego|self]] as standing before two equally possible [[action|act]]s, choosing "either way." But this treats the [[ego|self]] as an abstr[[action|act]] point outside duration, and the options as pre-existing spatial paths. Bergson insists that the living [[ego|self]] in [[duration]] does not choose between ready-made alternatives; rather, **the [[action|act]] is the [[ego|self]]** at that moment.

### Free Will vs. Determinism

[[determinism|Determinism]], in Bergson's analysis, commits the same spatializing error. By treating psychic states as mutually external, capable of being compared in intensity (see [[psychophysics]]), the determinist can speak of the "strongest motive" winning — as if motives were forces with [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]s. But [[quality|qualitative]] [[consciousness|conscious states]] do not have magnitudes; they have **meanings**, and the way they combine is through [[interpenetration]], not addition.

### The Role of Duration

The problem of [[will|free will]] is ultimately the **problem of time**:

> [003-034] …hence the problem of free will. We shall insist rather on the real duration, of which the moments are not external to one another…

If time is [[reality|real]] — if the succession of [[consciousness|conscious states]] is [[quality|qualitative]] and interpenetrating, not a spatial line of instants — then the future is **not contained in the present** in any calculable form. The free [[action|act]] is genuinely creative, genuinely new, because the [[ego|self]] that [[action|act]]s at each moment is not reducible to its past states.

## Historical Context

### Kant's Solution: Freedom as Noumenal

[[Kant]] resolved the tension between determinism and freedom by placing freedom outside the phenomenal [[reality|real]]m. The [[ego|self]] as **noumenon** is free; the [[ego|self]] as **phenomenon** (appearing in space and time) is determined by [[causality|causal law]]:

> [001-211] …clung to freedom but made it noumenal…

Bergson's critique: [[kant|Kant]] preserved freedom only by making it **unknowable**. In [[kant|Kant]]'s system, we can never experience freedom concretely — it is a postulate of pr[[action|act]]ical [[mind|reason]], not a f[[action|act]] of consciousness.

### The Psychophysical Challenge

The [[psychophysics]] [[motion|movement]], led by [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]], threatened to extend [[quantity|quantitative]] determinism into [[reality|the real]]m of consciousness it[[ego|self]]. If [[sensation]]s have [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]s, then the "strongest" [[sensation]] always wins — and [[will|free will]] is an illusion. Bergson's entire analysis of [[intensity]] (Chapter I) is designed to dismantle this premise.

### Stuart Mill and British Empiricism

[[Mill]] and the associationist school treated the mind as a collection of discrete states linked by [[association|laws of association]]. Bergson engages [[mill|Mill]]'s definition of [[will|free will]] to show that even the empiricist formulation smuggles in spatial presuppositions.
