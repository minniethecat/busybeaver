# Homogeneity

Homogeneity, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the defining property of [[space]] — the [[quality|qualitative]] indifference of a medium in which every point is equivalent to every other, and where parts differ only in **position**, not in **nature**. Bergson's argument is that we habitually project this spatial homogeneity onto time and consciousness, thereby falsifying their essentially **heterogeneous** char[[action|act]]er.

## Bergson's Argument

### Homogeneity as the Essence of Space

For Bergson, space is not fundamentally a system of distances or dimensions; it is first and foremost a **homogeneous medium**:

> [001-149] …any homogeneity in, implies space…

Wherever we find homogeneity — a medium in which parts are [[quality|qualitative]]ly identical and distinguishable only by position — we are dealing with space or with something modeled on space. Homogeneity is what makes [[number]] and [[quantity]] possible: to count, we must treat the counted items as identical units in a homogeneous medium.

> [001-192] …interpreted as extensive homogeneity…

### The Illegitimate Projection onto Time

Homogeneous time is a contradiction in terms, Bergson argues, because the very nature of time is [[heterogeneity]] — the continuous production of the [[quality|qualitative]]ly new:

> [001-193] …is all homogeneity space?…

When we think of time as a uniform line of instants, each identical in quality and distinguished only by "position" (earlier/later), we are **spatializing** time. We are treating it as a homogeneous medium, when in [[reality|real]]ity each moment of [[duration]] is [[quality|qualitative]]ly unique, bearing within it the accumulated weight of the entire past.

> [003-130] …when you attribute the least homogeneity to duration, you surreptitiously…

The moment we admit any homogeneity into duration — the moment we treat any two instants of consciousness as [[quality|qualitative]]ly interchangeable — we have **smuggled space** into our [[idea|conception]] of time. And from this spatialization, Bergson argues, all the paradoxes of [[free-will|freedom]] and determinism follow.

### Homogeneity and the Problem of Difference

Homogeneity is the conceptual tool by which the [[intelligence|intellect]] **domesticates** difference:

> [003-111] …under the form of extensive homogeneity what is given it as qualitative heterogeneity…

The [[intelligence|intellect]], oriented toward [[action|act]]ion, needs a stable, predictable world. Homogeneity provides this: if all points in a medium are [[quality|qualitative]]ly identical, differences reduce to measurable distances. But the price of this domestication is that **[[reality|real]] [[quality|qualitative difference]]** — the kind that matters in conscious life — is erased.

> [003-116] For, homogeneity here consisting in the absence…

In a truly homogeneous medium, there are no [[quality|qualitative]] distinctions. Every part is like every other. But conscious experience is nothing but [[quality|qualitative]] distinctions — the taste of coffee, the sound of a voice, the ache of a memory — each irreducibly different from every other. The imposition of homogeneity on this field of [[quality|qualitative difference]]s is the **original philosophical error**.

| Aspect | Homogeneous ([[space|Space]]) | Heterogeneous (Duration) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Parts | Qualitatively identical | Qualitatively unique |
| Difference | Position only | [[quality|Quality]] (in kind) |
| Relation | External (juxtaposition) | Internal (interpenetration) |
| Measurement | Possible (additive) | Impossible (non-additive) |
| Domain | Material world | Conscious life |

### The Practical Origin of Homogeneity

Why do we project homogeneity onto time and consciousness? Bergson's answer is pr[[action|act]]ical:

> [003-118] …both exteriorities from the homogeneity of a medium which inserts into…

The demands of [[action|act]]ion require a stable, homogeneous world. We need to treat our experiences as **comparable** — to say "this pain is worse than that one," "this joy is greater" — and comparison requires a common scale, a homogeneous medium. [[language|Language]] it[[ego|self]], with its general terms, imposes homogeneity on the [[becoming|flux]] of experience.

### Homogeneity, Space, and Duration

The deepest claim Bergson makes about homogeneity is that it is the **common essence** of space and of [[time|homogeneous time]]:

> [003-433] …which is space in so far as it is homogeneity, and duration in so far as it…

[[space|Space]] is homogeneity as such. Duration is [[heterogeneity]] as such. The two are **opposed** principles, not complementary aspects of a single [[reality|real]]ity. When we try to combine them — as we do in the concept of [[time|homogeneous time]] — we produce a hybrid that falsifies both:

> [003-200] …externality of things and the homogeneity of their medium is the same…

The externality of things to one another and the homogeneity of the medium in which they are placed are, Bergson argues, **the same f[[action|act]]** seen from two angles. To place items in a homogeneous medium is to make them external to one another; to make them external is to place them in a homogeneous medium.

## Key Distinctions

### Homogeneity vs. Heterogeneity

Homogeneity is the principle of space, number, and quantity. [[Heterogeneity]] is the principle of duration, consciousness, and [[quality]]. Bergson's philosophy can be summarized as the attempt to **think heterogeneity** without reducing it to homogeneity — to think difference in kind without converting it to difference in degree.

### Homogeneity vs. Unity

Homogeneity should not be confused with unity. An [[body|organism]] is unified but not homogeneous; its parts are [[quality|qualitative]]ly distinct. A heap of sand is homogeneous but not unified; its parts are identical grains. Homogeneity is the specific kind of unity that belongs to space — the unity of interchangeable parts.

### Homogeneity and the Self

The spatialization of the [[ego|self]] — treating the mind as a theater of distinct, comparable states — is the application of homogeneity to consciousness. Bergson's alternative is the [[ego|self]] as a [[multiplicity]] of interpenetrating states, each [[quality|qualitative]]ly unique, forming a whole that is not the sum of homogeneous parts.

## Historical Context

### Kant and the Forms of Intuition

[[Kant]] treated space and time as **forms of [[intuition]]** — homogeneous media in which all experience must be organized. Bergson accepts that this describes the **[[intelligence|intellect]]ual representation** of space and time, but he denies that it describes their immediate [[reality|real]]ity. [[kant|Kant]]'s homogeneity, for Bergson, is a product of [[intelligence|intellect]], not a feature of experience it[[ego|self]].

### The Geometrical Tradition

The tradition from [[Descartes]] through [[Spinoza]] treated [[reality|real]]ity *more geometrico* — in the manner of geometry, which presupposes homogeneity. Bergson's philosophy is a sustained argument against this tradition: [[reality|the real]] is not geometrical, and the attempt to treat it as such generates insoluble problems (like the problem of [[will|free will]]).
