# Heyerdahl

Lensmand Heyerdahl is the district sheriff who succeeds [[geissler|Geissler]] in office. The son of a local magistrate, he lacked the means to attend university and instead spent fifteen years writing at a desk in the governor's office. His appointment as Lensmand represents a step up in life, and he approaches his duties with bureaucratic formality.

## Background and Appointment

Heyerdahl is the son of a local magistrate (bygdebyskriver), but his family lacked the means to send him to university. Instead, he spent fifteen years as a clerk in the governor's office, writing documents for others. His appointment as Lensmand is a belated career advancement, arriving when he is about forty years old (005-072):

> The son of a local magistrate, by name Heyerdahl. He had lacked the means to go to the university; he had sat for fifteen years writing at a desk in the governor's office, and had never got any farther. (005-072)

The Amtmand (county governor) sends him up to the village to replace Geissler. Heyerdahl arrives with his assistant [[brede-olsen|Brede]], and the two of them get thoroughly wet crossing the moors to survey Isak's land (005-080):

> Lensmand Heyerdahl came up to the farm, and brought with him his assistant, Brede. They had got thoroughly wet crossing the moors, and wetter still they were before they'd finished tramping the boundary lines through melting snow and slush up and down the hills. (005-080)

At the time of his appointment, Heyerdahl is unmarried—having never been able to afford a wife on a clerk's salary.

## Bureaucratic Style

Heyerdahl's approach to official matters is formal and elaborately phrased, in stark contrast to Geissler's directness. When handling Isak's land application, Geissler had written a straightforward statement; Heyerdahl recasts it in ornate official language, inserting formulas such as "humbly begs to submit" and elaborately numbering the purchase price (005-085):

> Lensmand Heyerdahl drew up his report in elegant official language. He now humbly begs to submit this application to the Department: that he be allowed to retain this land, upon which, albeit without right of possession, he has up to this present effected considerable improvements, for a purchase price of 50—fifty—Speciedaler... (005-085)

Despite the bureaucratic verbosity, Heyerdahl is not malicious—he promises Isak to do his best, and the narrator notes that he is fundamentally a decent man doing his job as he was trained to do it (005-086):

> Lensmand Heyerdahl promised Isak to do his best. (005-086)

His style contrasts with Geissler's throughout the novel: where Geissler acts with instinct and personal authority, Heyerdahl relies on procedure and formal correctness. Hamsun uses this juxtaposition to critique the creeping bureaucratisation of rural Norway.

## Inger's Case

Heyerdahl handles Inger's trial for infanticide with formal mercy. He questions her as he might speak to anyone, remarking only that it is "a great pity such things should happen at all" (007-052). His administration of the law is procedural rather than passionate—he follows the forms of justice without Geissler's personal engagement:

> The law was merciful. Lensmand Heyerdahl asked her questions just as he might speak to anyone, and remarked only that it was a great pity such things should happen at all. (007-052)

This episode illustrates the same bureaucratic temperament seen in his handling of Isak's land papers: the forms are observed, the language is correct, but human depth is absent.

## Marriage and Family

Heyerdahl marries the year before Inger returns from prison (007-105). His wife, Fru Heyerdahl, has no intention of ever being a mother—no children for her, thank you—and indeed she has none. Fru Heyerdahl becomes an influential figure in the district, taking up cases and wielding considerable social power.

The marriage transforms Heyerdahl's social standing. Eleseus later reflects that Heyerdahl himself is "but a former clerk in the governor's office," suggesting that the lensmand position alone does not command automatic respect in the new generation's eyes (019-043):

> "Why not?" said Eleseus. "What's Heyerdahl himself but a former clerk in the governor's office?" (019-043)

## Fru Heyerdahl and Social Power

Fru Lensmand Heyerdahl wields significant unofficial power in the district. She takes up Inger's case after Inger's return from prison, going about saying it was a disgraceful affair, and her words carry great weight (026-022, 012-072). She is also credited with finding a position for one of the local girls, demonstrating the patronage network that the lensmand's household controls (008-079):

> And it was Lensmand Heyerdahl's lady that had found that spot for her. (008-079)
