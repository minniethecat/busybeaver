# Telegraph Office

The government telegraph service that operated in the rural district, connecting the remote settlement to the outside world. The telegraph line was constructed across the hills and moors, and its operation involved several key characters in the novel, serving as both a symbol of modernization and a source of employment and conflict.

## Organization Form

The Telegraph Office was a government-run communications service, with a hierarchical structure typical of 19th-century Norwegian state institutions. An engineer and foreman supervised the construction of the line, while a local inspector — initially Brede Olsen, later Axel Ström — was responsible for maintaining the line and operating the apparatus (013-066).

The inspector received a small regular wage, described as "next to nothing wages" by Brede (020-034), but it provided a supplementary income that could be "good to reckon with as long as he's barely enough for his needs from the land" (029-147). The telegraph company exercised oversight from the town, sending "reprimands" when the local operator neglected his duties (015-070).

## Role in the Novel

### Construction and Arrival of the Telegraph

The telegraph first appeared in the district when surveyors and engineers arrived to mark out the line across the hills. They stopped at Sellanraa for food and lodging, asking Isak about the path across the moors (008-002). Later, an engineer with a foreman and workmen returned to continue the work (009-002). The construction crews — described as "burly telegraph men" — stayed at Sellanraa and interacted with the farm's inhabitants (013-031).

Isak supplied timber for the project, selling telegraph poles to the foreman (012-021). He also provided lodging for the workers when the foremost gang reached the farm (013-017). To Isak, the telegraph was a mysterious modern invention: "a wonderful thing, a string hung up on big poles, something altogether above the common earth" (010-035).

### Brede Olsen as Telegraph Operator

Brede Olsen, Geissler's assistant and a neighboring settler, became the local telegraph inspector. The engineer gifted him a telegraph apparatus — "a neat little machine set up on the wall, a curious little thing, a sort of telegraph in itself" (013-066). Brede took pride in his official position, but his commitment was inconsistent. He was frequently absent, claiming he had to "run away all times in the busy season, to work on the telegraph line" (009-018), yet he often neglected his duties.

The telegraph company had "already had occasion several times to reprimand him for neglect, and had again offered the post to Isak" (015-070). However, Isak declined, and Brede's true preoccupation was not with the telegraph but with the copper ore discovered in the hills — "it was his one idea now, a mania" (015-070).

### Transition to Axel Ström

In Book II, Brede announced he had "given up that telegraph" (020-032), complaining about the demands of maintaining the line while also working for the Lensmand and doctor (020-034). He had also quarreled with the telegraph office about his performance (020-034).

Eleseus, Isak's eldest son, briefly considered taking over the position, thinking he would "have to try a year at the telegraph to begin with" (020-076). Barbro, Brede's daughter, confronted him about it (021-070). However, Eleseus ultimately left the district.

The position passed to Axel Ström, who "took over the inspection of the telegraph line" at New Year (022-128). Axel approached the work diligently, following "the telegraph line all the way to the spot" and working to repair faults (022-026). Unlike Brede, he maintained the line, though he occasionally "rather wished he had left it alone" when autumn gales caused damage (024-087, 027-001).

### Conflict and Resolution

Brede did not accept the loss of his position gracefully. He threatened Axel: "I could make things awkward for you in many odd ways, seeing all I know about the telegraph and things; ay, 'twould be easy enough both to pull down poles and cut the line" (027-005). Barbro also resented Axel for taking the telegraph business from her father (029-061).

The conflict eventually resolved when Axel married Barbro. As Brede's son-in-law, Axel had "little to fear from Brede about the telegraph line" (029-147), and the position continued to provide a modest but useful income.

### Later Years

The telegraph roads transformed the landscape of the district. By the later chapters, "all things were changed, the land itself was different now, with broad telegraph roads up through the woods, that had not been there before" (028-059). In the final chapter, a new telegraph operator, Rolandsen, is mentioned in passing — "the telegraph operator in love with Elsie Mack" (031-128) — suggesting the service continued as a permanent fixture of the district.

## Thematic Significance

The Telegraph Office represents the encroachment of modernization and state institutions into the wilderness. The telegraph line literally cuts across the hills and moors that the pioneers have settled, bringing external communication — and external control — into their self-sufficient world.

The position of telegraph inspector serves as a narrative test of character. For Brede Olsen, the telegraph is a source of status and income that he squanders through neglect and distraction, reflecting his broader failure as a settler. For Axel Ström, the same position becomes a reliable supplement to his farming, reflecting his steadiness and diligence. Isak's refusal of the post — despite the company offering it to him — aligns with his commitment to the land above all else.

The telegraph also functions as a plot mechanism that connects the isolated district to the larger world. Geissler's remark that if there had been "a telegraph office in the village" he could have wired to Trondhjem about Inger's imprisonment (010-034) underscores how the absence and later presence of the telegraph marked the district's gradual integration into Norwegian society.
