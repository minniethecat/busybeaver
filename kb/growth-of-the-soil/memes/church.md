# Church

In Growth of the Soil, the church stands at the edge of the wilderness — a distant institution that the settlers visit only on special occasions. It represents organized religion, community ritual, and the civilizing forces that lie beyond the homestead, both alluring and alien to the pioneer's self-sufficient life.

## Definition

The church in Hamsun's novel is not a central presence but a remote marker of established society. It lies many miles from Sellanraa, accessible only by an arduous journey. For the pioneers, going to church is an event — an occasion to dress up, to see neighbors, to participate momentarily in the wider community. Yet the novel treats the church with characteristic ambiguity: Isak and Inger feel its pull, but the real faith of the novel resides in the soil and the seasons, not in the pews.

## The Church in the Novel

### A Distant Destination

Getting to church is itself an ordeal. When Isak and Inger first discuss it, the logistics are daunting: "come by it, getting down to a church and all. What do you say, Isak?" (003-059). Later, Inger prepares carefully: "she wanted to smell nice for going to church, the thing was to rub oneself" with scented herbs (009-060). The journey to church becomes a ritual of its own, a brief departure from the homestead's insular world.

### Community and Display

Church is where the settlement gathers. It is a social stage where status is performed: "he had also been to church there, and made a show" (018-033). When Isak notices Inger wearing a kerchief in church, the detail reveals how much observation and judgment passes between worshippers (016-097). Oline, ever the commentator, calls the experience "like church and organ music" (006-049), invoking the aesthetic and emotional power of organized worship.

### The Marble Church

The novel also contains a fleeting reference to a fantastical church — "that was a little church all of pure marble — the Danes" (014-065). This distant, idealized image contrasts sharply with the simple wooden churches of the Norwegian countryside, a reminder of the wider world of European Christianity beyond the fjords.

> "come by it, getting down to a church and all. What do you say, Isak?" (003-059)

> 'Tis like church and organ music, says Oline (006-049)

## Inger and the Church

Inger's relationship with the church is the most complex in the novel. Her disfigurement — the harelip that marks her face — becomes a source of shame in the church setting, where she is seen and judged by the community. The church is where her difference is most visible, where the eyes of the neighbours fall upon her with a scrutiny they would not apply at the homestead.

After her operation and return from prison, Inger's church attendance takes on new meaning. She now has a face that can be presented without shame, and she knows it. The church becomes a stage for her rehabilitation — not just spiritually but socially. When she wears her kerchief in church, it is an act of self-presentation that the novel registers without comment, trusting the reader to understand its weight.

## The Church as Moral Authority

The novel maintains an uneasy relationship with organized religion. Isak is not a churchgoing man by instinct — his faith, such as it is, is enacted in the daily work of the farm rather than professed in a pew. Yet the church exerts a gravitational pull on the settlement, and its minister speaks with an authority that even Geissler cannot fully counter. When moral questions arise — Inger's crime, the baptism of children, the burial of the dead — it is to the church that the community turns, however reluctantly.

Hamsun's treatment of this authority is characteristically ambiguous. The church is never mocked, but neither is it celebrated. It simply is — one of the institutions that encroach upon the wilderness as the settlement matures, neither wholly welcome nor wholly foreign.
