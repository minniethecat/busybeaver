# Telegraph Line Construction

The construction of the telegraph line through the Sellanraa district represents one of the most sustained infrastructure narratives in the novel, spanning from its initial surveying to its eventual operation and maintenance by Axel Ström. The line brings modern communication, wage labour, and social disruption to the isolated settlement.

## Initial Surveying

The first appearance of the telegraph project comes early, when a party of men stops at Sellanraa for food and rest:

> Once there came a number of men at once; they rested at Sellanraa, and had some food and a dish of milk; they asked Isak and Oline about the path across the hills; they were marking out the telegraph line, they said. (PN=008-002)

A second surveying visit followed, more formal in character:

> Once more there came visitors to Sellanraa; an engineer, with a foreman and a couple of workmen, marking out telegraph lines again over the hills. (PN=009-002)

The engineer identified Sellanraa as a strategic midpoint:

> "This place," said the engineer, "will be just about midway between the two stations." (PN=009-003)

## Construction

By early spring, the physical work began in earnest:

> There was a good average snowfall that winter, and early in the year, when the roads were passable, folk from the village began carting up telegraph poles over the moors, dropping their loads at regular intervals. (PN=013-011)

The construction crews advanced across the moors, eventually reaching Sellanraa:

> The telegraph workers had now reached so far up over the moors that the foremost gang came to the farm one evening and asked to be lodged for the night. (PN=013-017)

The presence of the telegraph workers brought social disruption. The labourers — burly men from outside the district — interacted freely with the local women:

> And those burly telegraph men — how they lifted her! (PN=013-031)

The young telegraph hand's attentions to Jensine created tension at Sellanraa, and Isak was relieved when the work concluded:

> And last, not least, the telegraph line was all but finished now; in a little while they would be left in peace. (PN=013-052)

## Brede Olsen as Inspector

After the construction gangs departed, Brede Olsen took on the role of line inspector. It was a position he clung to as other fortunes declined:

> And Brede was not altogether dejected when Isak met him; he consoled himself with the thought that he was still Inspector on the telegraph line; that was a regular income, anyway. (PN=019-010)

Brede's relationship with the line was marked by performance and anxiety. When inspectors appeared, he became theatrically busy:

> Brede looks away hurriedly, fully aware now of what is the matter; he glances up at the telegraph wires and seems to be whistling. (PN=022-038)

> But Brede is strangely full of zeal in his duty now, he keeps on looking at the telegraph wires, and whistling all the time. (PN=022-040)

## Axel Ström Takes Over

The inspection duties eventually passed to [[axel-strom]]:

> He was to fetch supplies of wire, a telegraph apparatus, tools and implements, from Brede Olsen, and take over inspection of the line from New Year's Day. (PN=022-128)

Axel quickly regretted accepting the responsibility, as it proved more trouble than it was worth:

> Axel further told how he had taken over the inspection of the telegraph line, but rather wished he had left it alone. (PN=024-087)

The autumn gales brought concrete problems:

> Axel had no long time to rest at home, as it turned out; the autumn gales led to fresh trouble and bothersome work that he had brought upon himself: the telegraph apparatus on his wall announced that the line was out of order. (PN=027-001)

The work interfered with the real business of farming:

> 'Twas none so pleasant to have to put aside all manner of necessary work to run and see after a telegraph line. (PN=027-006)

## Settlement and Resolution

When Axel married [[barbro]], the telegraph business became entangled with family obligations. Axel offered to return the work to Brede as a gesture of goodwill:

> he agreed that Barbro might have some reason to be angry with him in return for the way he had taken the telegraph business from her father. "But as for that," said he, "your father can have the telegraph business again for me; I'll have no more of it, 'tis but a waste of time." (PN=029-061)

But by then, with Barbro managing Maaneland, the telegraph income became more manageable, and Brede — as Axel's father-in-law — posed less of a threat:

> Now he's a woman of his own to look after the place, he can keep on the telegraph business for a bit. 'Tis a deal of money in the year, and good to reckon with as long as he's barely enough for his needs from the land, and little to sell. And little to fear from Brede about the telegraph line, seeing he's son-in-law to Brede now. (PN=029-147)

## Narrative Function

The telegraph line serves as a vehicle for several of the novel's central themes:

- **Modernisation and its costs**: The telegraph brings cash wages but also disruption — the workers unsettle the social order, and the inspection duties divert settlers from productive agricultural labour.
- **Brede Olsen's decline**: The inspection work tracks Brede's trajectory from a minor official's post to precarious dependence, and ultimately to a grudging family settlement.
- **Axel's character**: Axel's reluctant acceptance of the work, his frustration, and his eventual pragmatic accommodation reflect the settler's relationship with modern obligations.
