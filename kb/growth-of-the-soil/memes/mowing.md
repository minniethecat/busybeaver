# Mowing

Mowing — the cutting of grass for hay — is the central labor of summer in Growth of the Soil. The novel treats mowing with detailed attention, and the arrival of the first mechanical mowing-machine in the district marks a pivotal moment in the technological transformation of pioneer life.

## 定义

Mowing is the process of cutting grass on the hillsides and meadows to produce hay — the essential winter fodder for livestock. In the novel, mowing is portrayed as a form of labor that is simultaneously grueling and dignified. Isak mows by hand, with a scythe, covering vast areas day after day, turning and carting the hay to the barn (004-012):

> He mowed and mowed on the hillsides, farther and farther out; mowing and turning and carting home loads of hay, as if he would never tire — for he had a horse already, and a well-stocked farm.

## 在小说中的表现

### The Mowing-Machine

One of the novel's most vivid technological arrivals is Isak's purchase of a mowing-machine — a red-and-blue contraption of "steel springs and hooks and apparatus, and hundreds of screws" (019-061). It is the first such machine in the wilderness, a spectacle that draws neighbors to come and stare (019-070):

> It was the first mowing-machine in the wilds, the first in the village — red and blue, a thing of splendour to man's eyes.

The machine embodies the novel's ambivalent attitude toward technology. It multiplies Isak's productivity but also represents a departure from the pure hand-labor that defines his character. Notably, Hamsun treats the machine not as a corruption but as a legitimate tool — Isak masters it, and it becomes an extension of his work rather than a replacement for it.

### Mowing Alone

Axel Ström's haymaking is portrayed with particular sympathy. Working alone, he "had to do his mowing by day and haymaking by night, and all by himself" (015-077). The solitary mower, bent to his scythe in the long Nordic summer evening, is one of the novel's enduring images of pioneer self-reliance.

## 主题意义

Mowing represents the disciplined, rhythmic labor that the novel celebrates. Unlike plowing or sowing — which are acts of faith in the future — mowing is immediate, physical, and visibly productive. Grass becomes hay, and hay becomes winter survival. The mowing-machine episode also serves as the novel's meditation on technology: a tool is judged not by its novelty but by whether it serves the land and the life built upon it.
