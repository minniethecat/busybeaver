# Village Meeting on Mining Rights

A meeting held in the village to address the crisis caused by Geissler's refusal to sell his part of the mining tract. The gathering reveals how deeply the district had grown dependent on mining activity — and how a single man's personal grievance could paralyse an entire community.

> A meeting had been held in the village to consider the position which had arisen owing to Geissler's refusal to sell his part of the mining tract. (PN=027-042)

## Background and Economic Dependency

The copper mine on Isak's land had brought a wave of economic activity to the district. Surveyors, engineers, and mining experts arrived from Sweden; wages rose, and settlers grew accustomed to store-bought goods and finer living (PN=023-025). But after Geissler — the former Lensmand — was dismissed from his post, the mining operations stalled. Geissler still owned a key tract of land, and he was not inclined to sell it on terms favourable to the village (PN=027-044).

The meeting exposed how profoundly the district had grown dependent on mining money. The narrator describes the transformation with characteristic precision:

> They had grown accustomed to better food, finer bread, store-bought clothes and higher wages, general extravagance — ay, folk had learned to reckon with money more, that was the matter. And now the money was gone again, had slipped away like a shoal of herring out to sea. (PN=027-043)

The image of money vanishing "like a shoal of herring out to sea" captures a central anxiety of the novel: the difference between extractive wealth, which can disappear overnight, and the slow, cumulative wealth of the soil, which endures.

## The Meeting

The meeting was called to address a crisis: Geissler had refused an offer of twenty-five thousand Kroner from the Swedish mining interests, and without his tract, development of the entire district was blocked:

> The Swedes had offered him twenty-five thousand — Geissler had refused. But suppose the village here, the commune, were to make up the remainder, simply to get things going again? (PN=027-045)

There was no doubt among those gathered that Geissler was acting out of personal vengeance. By the simplest means — merely by demanding an unreasonable price for a mine — he had succeeded in checking the entire development of the district (PN=027-044). He had not simply disappeared after his dismissal; he had stayed and, from a position of apparent weakness, exercised absolute power over the community that had discarded him.

The meeting resolved to send a deputation to negotiate with Geissler. Both the trader at the shore station and Aronsen at [[storborg]] were willing to contribute privately and secretly to raise the purchase price, reasoning that the investment would be repaid in time (PN=027-045).

## The First Deputation

Two men were deputed to call on Geissler (PN=027-046). The choice of [[brede-olsen]] as one of the emissaries was, in retrospect, a serious mistake — he was chosen as the man who could best spare the time, not as the most capable negotiator:

> A week later the deputation returned home with a flat refusal. Oh, they had done the worst thing possible at the outset, in choosing Brede Olsen as one of the men they sent. (PN=027-048)

Geissler had received them, shaken his head, and laughed. "Go back home again," he had said. But he had paid for their journey back (PN=027-048). The gesture — paying for their return — is characteristic of Geissler: it reasserts his control over the situation while preserving an appearance of magnanimity.

## The Second Deputation

The village did not give up easily. After the first deputation failed, a second attempt was made with more trustworthy men (PN=028-013). But this second effort likewise failed. Geissler's position was immovable — he was not acting from economic calculation but from a deeper grievance against the community that had dismissed him. The quality of the envoys was irrelevant: the outcome had been predetermined by Geissler's implacable will.

## Aftermath

The failure of the deputation marked the effective end of the mining boom in the district:

- **Aronsen's desperation**: The trader Aronsen, who had hoped the deputation would succeed and had held out at [[storborg]] on the strength of that hope, now grew desperate. He soon after sold Storborg to [[eleseus]] for fifteen hundred Kroner — land, house, sheds, livestock and goods together (PN=027-050).

- **Eleseus's fate sealed**: By purchasing Storborg, [[eleseus]] abandoned his dreams of town life — of becoming a Lensmand or a gentleman in an office. He was to bury himself in the wilds (PN=027-051). His purchase was not a triumph but a surrender: the town had rejected him, and the farm was the only place left that would take him.

- **Economic regression**: The district was left to its fate. The miners departed, the money dried up, and settlers returned to subsistence. Aronsen's fury was the fury of a man who had bet on a boom that never came (PN=027-049).

## Narrative Function

The village meeting serves multiple structural roles in the novel:

- **The fragility of extractive wealth**: Hamsun dramatizes how mining prosperity — unlike agricultural labour — is contingent on forces beyond the settlers' control. A single landowner's refusal is enough to collapse the entire economy.

- **Geissler's power**: The meeting confirms Geissler's transformation from disgraced official to the man who holds the district's fate in his hand. He is shown to be a strong man — perhaps the strongest in the entire narrative (PN=027-044).

- **The decline of the boom**: The meeting marks the narrative pivot from the heady mining chapters (Ch. XXIII–XXVI) to the quieter, post-boom chapters that follow. The failure of both deputations signals that the mining era is definitively over.

- **Class and competence**: The first deputation's failure is attributed specifically to the poor choice of Brede Olsen — a man defined throughout the novel by his incompetence. The second deputation of "trusty men" fared no better, suggesting that the outcome was predetermined not by the quality of the envoys but by Geissler's implacable will.
