# Potato

The potato is the pioneer's staff of life in Growth of the Soil. More than any other crop, it marks the rhythm of the year — planting in spring, anxious watching in summer, the relief of harvest in autumn. Hamsun devotes some of his most lyrical prose to the potato's flowering, transforming a humble tuber into an object of wonder.

## 定义

In the novel's agricultural economy, the potato is the first crop Isak plants and the one he depends on most. Unlike hay, which is for animals, or timber, which is for sale, the potato is for eating — the foundation of subsistence. Its success or failure determines whether the pioneer family will have enough food for the winter.

## 在小说中的表现

### The First Planting

Isak plants potatoes almost as soon as he clears ground. In the very first chapter: "a patch of ground, and planted potatoes" (001-028). The act is understated — no drama, no description — just a fact. But this patch of potatoes is the first cultivation, the act that transforms Isak from a wanderer into a settler. Inger, too, takes to the potato field: "went out to the potato field and set to work hoeing" (001-057). When Isak sees that she has "hoed the potatoes after all he was not pleased" (001-061) — a small, telling moment of the pioneer's fierce pride in his own work.

### The Flowering and the Crisis

The novel's most sustained meditation on a single crop comes in Chapter IV, during a summer of drought. Hamsun breaks from narrative to ask: "What was that about potatoes? Were they just a thing from foreign parts?" (004-013). The passage that follows is remarkable — a philosophical digression on the potato's mysterious flowering, its improbable productivity, its silent defiance of the heat. "The potatoes stood all the time in flower" (004-016), and Isak watches helplessly as "the potato field flowering madly" (004-025) seems to mock the drought. When rain finally comes, "the potatoes were flowering still, worse than ever" (004-036) — but now the flowering is no longer a threat. The harvest that follows is "finished by Michaelmas, as it had always been" (004-040), a quiet return to the natural order.

### Counting as Knowledge

Isak counts his potato yield obsessively: "how many bushels of potatoes" (005-009) becomes a refrain. The number is never just a number — it is Isak's way of knowing his own competence. A low yield speaks not of bad luck but of failure: "so few bushels of potatoes, they'll say at once that it's because the soil's not properly worked" (005-061). In Isak's world, the potato is an honest witness.
