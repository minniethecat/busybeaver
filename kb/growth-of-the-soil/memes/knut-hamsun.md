# Knut Hamsun

Knut Hamsun (1859–1952) is the author of *Growth of the Soil*, the novel for which he received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1920. The novel's English edition includes an editor's introduction that situates the work within Hamsun's career and assesses his literary achievement.

## 角色定位

Hamsun does not appear as a character in the novel, but his presence is felt throughout — in the spare prose, the reverence for agricultural labour, and the suspicion of urban modernity. The editor's introduction to the English edition notes that "Knut Hamsun is now sixty. For years past he has been regarded as the greatest of living Norwegian writers" (031-094).

## 主要经历

### Early Career

Hamsun "attracted attention by the publication of his first book, *Hunger*, in 1890, at the age of thirty" (031-102). This early work was "subjective in the extreme" (031-100), focused on the inner life of a struggling writer in Christiania. His second novel, *Mysteries*, followed in 1893, and the next year he "astonished his critics with two books" (031-106), including *Pan* (1894), which the editor calls "probably Hamsun's best-known work" (031-109).

### Growth of the Soil

*Growth of the Soil* (*Markens Grøde*) is identified as "Hamsun's latest work" at the time of the English edition (031-095). The editor observes that it "is very far indeed from Hamsun's earliest beginnings" (031-097), marking a decisive shift from subjective, urban narratives to an objective, rural epic. The novel's reception — culminating in the Nobel Prize — confirmed Hamsun's status as a writer of international importance.

### Literary Method

The introduction offers a distinctive account of Hamsun's method. His characters possess a quality of "sensitive reticence" — "one of the greatest merits of Hamsun's work that he shows otherwise" (031-122). The editor also notes Hamsun's deliberate self-referentiality: "Hamsun lets a character in one book reappear in a later book" (031-117), suggesting a unified fictional world across his oeuvre. The Norwegian term *stemning* — a mood or atmosphere — is identified as the "subtle influence" (031-114) that pervades his writing.
