# Jensine

Jensine is the servant-girl at [[isak|Isak]]'s farm, [[sellanraa]]. Brought from the village by Isak to help with the household work, she becomes an indispensable part of the growing farm as it expands beyond what the family alone can manage.

## Arrival and Family Background

Isak makes a trip to the village and returns with a girl: "This is Jensine," he says simply. "Come to help" (PN=016-044). She becomes the household's first hired help, marking a new phase in Sellanraa's development from a pioneer homestead to an established farm. Isak's laconic announcement — no explanation, no introduction beyond a name and a function — is pure Isak. He sees what the farm needs and provides it, without ceremony:

> Some days passed, and Isak resolutely threw away a whole week-end — from Saturday evening till Monday morning — on a trip down to the village. He would not say what he was going for when he set out, but on his return, he brought with him a girl. "This is Jensine," he said. "Come to help." (PN=016-044)

Jensine is the daughter of the blacksmith in the village. Her father occasionally asks if she isn't coming home soon, but he is an easy-going man and does not press the matter (PN=023-015). The blacksmith may have his own reasons for letting her stay at the prosperous Sellanraa farm — her wages contribute to the family economy, and her position at the district's most successful homestead carries a certain prestige.

## Role in the Household

Jensine takes on domestic tasks — carding wool, helping with the children, and assisting Inger. She sits carding wool in the evenings while the family gathers (PN=020-092). Her presence at Sellanraa is quiet but constant; when others come and go, Jensine remains:

> Likewise Jensine the servant-maid, she sat carding wool. (PN=020-092)

As the farm grows, her presence becomes permanent. When Isak's daughter [[leopoldine]] leaves to go to service, Jensine stays on. The narrator notes that the farm has grown too large to manage without hired help (PN=023-015):

> The place is growing, getting bigger and bigger, a mighty big place at last. Impossible now to manage without a girl to help, and Jensine has to stay on. (PN=023-015)

Jensine accompanies the family on important trips, including acting as godmother when Inger's child Rebecca is taken across the hills to be christened (PN=016-091):

> Jensine, the servant-girl, was to go with them across the hills with the child. (PN=016-091)

She also looks after little Rebecca on church journeys later in the novel (PN=025-067):

> Rebecca had the maid Jensine to look after her on the way. (PN=025-067)

## Relationships with the Family

[[sivert]] sometimes interacts with her in his characteristic joking manner, and [[eleseus]], home from town, finds Jensine "a mere nothing, a worker and no more, rather suited to Sivert" (PN=018-033):

> Jensine, the servant-maid, was a mere nothing, a worker and no more, rather suited to Sivert. (PN=018-033)

Eleseus's dismissal of Jensine reflects his own pretensions after his city education — he sees himself as above manual labour and those who perform it. His condescension is the condescension of the town towards the farm, and it foreshadows his own eventual failure as a man who belongs to neither world.

When [[barbro]] briefly takes over household duties, Jensine is "out" — and the shift in domestic order is immediately felt (PN=025-080). Her absence, even temporary, reveals how deeply woven she has become into the fabric of daily life at Sellanraa.

## The Servant's Perspective

Jensine's role illuminates a rarely-seen dimension of Sellanraa: the household from the servant's point of view. While Isak and Inger are the farm's public face, Jensine is present for its most intimate moments — she sits with the family in the evenings, accompanies them on journeys, and watches the children grow (PN=019-016):

> And Sivert and Leopoldine, and Jensine the servant-maid, they were all at home. (PN=019-016)

One evening, seated with little Rebecca, Jensine reveals a sharp moral sensibility. When music begins playing, she speaks with uncharacteristic directness:

> ...gan music. After a long while Jensine says: "'Tis a shameful thing ... (PN=025-127)

The exact nature of her complaint is left ambiguous, but the scene captures Jensine's capacity for moral judgement — she is not merely a silent helper but an observer who forms her own opinions about the behaviour of those around her. The narrator adds, in response, that someone "took Jensine's part and patted her and made..." (PN=025-135), suggesting that her perspective carried weight within the household.

## Departure

Jensine's departure from Sellanraa is announced with the same brevity as her arrival:

> ...thing which happened was that Jensine left Sellanraa. And that made... (PN=028-018)

> Ay, it came to that: Jensine would leave, if you please; s... (PN=028-019)

The narrator's tone — "if you please" — carries a note of surprise, as if Jensine's decision to leave was both unexpected and, in its way, presumptuous. But the practical consequences were immediate:

> ...what did they want with this Jensine, fresh as could be and marria... (PN=028-020)

> ...t here's the spring come, and Jensine gone, and who's to manage the... (PN=028-023)

Isak, for his part, could not understand why she had left (PN=028-025). The departure exposed a fundamental truth about Sellanraa: the farm had grown so large that it could no longer function without hired help. Jensine's leaving was not merely an inconvenience — it was a crisis that revealed the dependency Isak's expanding operation had created. The pioneer homestead that once ran on the labour of two people now required a servant to manage its daily rhythms.

## Character Significance

In a novel dominated by the grand figures of Isak and Inger, Jensine represents the invisible labour that makes their world possible. She is the hands that card the wool, the feet that walk the children to church, the eyes that observe the household's private moments. Her quiet presence — and her eventual departure — reveal that Sellanraa is not only a family farm but a complex economic unit that depends on wage labour, for all Isak's devotion to the soil.
