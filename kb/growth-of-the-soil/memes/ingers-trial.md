# Inger's Trial

**Inger's trial** for the killing of her newborn child is one of the darkest and most consequential events in the early part of _Growth of the Soil_. The trial exposes the harsh realities of isolated settler life, the impersonal machinery of the law, and the psychological weight borne by a woman marked by a congenital deformity.

## Background

Inger, born with a harelip, had lived a life of exclusion before coming to Isak's homestead. After giving birth to her third child — a girl born with the same deformity — she killed the infant. The act was shaped by fear, isolation, and the knowledge that the child would face the same social rejection she herself had endured.

The killing occurred in secret while Isak was away. When the body was discovered, the law intervened — not out of local outrage but through the impersonal machinery of the state, represented by Geissler, the Lensmand.

## The Trial

The legal proceedings were handled by Geissler, who investigated and reported the case. At the time, the court considered Inger's mental state a significant factor:

> ... at the moment of killing the child she is simply not r... (PN=026-011)

The defence sought a revision of the case and a new examination, hoping for a more lenient outcome (PN=011-111). Inger was found guilty. The narrator frames the event with characteristic understatement — the law does not ask about motivation or circumstance:

> It was not the object of a trial to secure an addition to the ... (PN=026-070)

> There was nothing extraordinary in the killing of a child; she thought of it ... (PN=021-119)

The clinical language of the legal system stands in stark contrast to the emotional reality of the act: a mother, isolated and afraid, destroying what she saw as another life condemned to suffering.

## Imprisonment and Transformation

After the trial, Inger was sent to prison (PN=006-143). The sentence separated her from Isak and their two surviving children — Eleseus and Sivert — for several years. During her imprisonment in Trondhjem, she underwent a transformation: she was taught to read and write, learned domestic skills such as needlework, and was exposed to urban society. The harelip was surgically repaired during this period — an operation that would have been impossible in the wilderness.

These changes were deeply ambivalent. The prison gave Inger knowledge and skills, but it also detached her from the rhythms of farm life. She returned as a different person — more educated, more aware of the wider world, but also more alienated from the simple existence Isak had maintained in her absence.

### Long-Term Consequences

The trial set in motion several long-term consequences for the novel's characters:

- **Eleseus's trajectory**: Sent to town as a boy during his mother's imprisonment, Eleseus grew up in urban conditions, acquiring tastes and ambitions that would forever separate him from the soil. His later failure as a farmer can be traced back to this early displacement.

- **Inger's divided self**: After prison, Inger was suspended between two worlds — she had seen the city, learned its ways, and could never fully return to the simplicity of her former life. Her later discontentment at Sellanraa has its roots in the prison years.

- **Isak's stoicism**: The trial revealed Isak's deepest quality — his capacity to endure without complaint. He continued to work the land, expand the farm, and wait. The trial, in this sense, became the crucible that proved his character as the novel's moral centre.

## Return and Silence

When Inger returned to Sellanraa, neither she nor Isak spoke of what had happened:

> As to the dead child and the trial, neither said a word of that. (PN=029-080)

The silence between them became its own testament to the event's weight. The trial was never resolved in conversation — it was buried, like the infant, beneath the accumulating seasons of farm work. Isak's silence was not forgiveness but acceptance: he had waited for her, and now she was back, and that was enough.
