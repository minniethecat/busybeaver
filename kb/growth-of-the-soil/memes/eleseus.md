# Eleseus

The elder son of [[isak|Isak]] and [[inger|Inger]], whose trajectory from [[sellanraa|Sellanraa farm]] boy to city clerk embodies Hamsun's critique of urban life — a character who drifts away from the soil and, in the novel's moral economy, loses himself in the process.

## 角色概要

Eleseus is the first child born to [[isak|Isak]] and [[inger|Inger]] at [[sellanraa|Sellanraa]]. His mother names him — "she was so bent on calling him Eleseus" — establishing an early maternal influence that shapes his path away from his father's agricultural world. As a child, he is described as somewhat delicate, "inclined to be ailing sometimes," in contrast to his hardier younger brother [[sivert|Sivert]] (PN=004-003, PN=004-057).

## 叙事弧线

### 童年与命名

Eleseus is born into the growing prosperity of [[sellanraa|Sellanraa]]. His christening marks the family's connection to the wider community. [[isak|Isak]] accepts [[inger|Inger]]'s choice of name without objection, a small but telling moment of paternal distance (PN=004-003, PN=004-059).

### 农场童年

As a boy, Eleseus participates in the life of the farm. His father teaches him practical tasks: "Those cheeses, Eleseus," [[isak|Isak]] instructs, engaging him in the work of the household economy. Yet these early lessons in farm labor do not take root as they do in [[sivert|Sivert]] (PN=008-048, PN=008-049).

### 离开土地

Unlike [[sivert|Sivert]], who remains to work the farm, Eleseus is drawn to town life and clerical work. He becomes a clerk, representing the novel's ambivalence about education and urban employment — the "civilized" life that Hamsun portrays as thinner and less authentic than agricultural labor.

### 城市生活

Eleseus's trajectory takes him away from [[sellanraa|Sellanraa]] into the world of shops, offices, and towns. Through Eleseus, the novel explores the tension between the farm and the city, the manual and the clerical, the rooted and the rootless.

## 角色功能

Eleseus serves as a foil to both his father [[isak|Isak]] and his brother [[sivert|Sivert]]. Where [[isak|Isak]] embodies the pre-modern ideal of silent, productive labor on the land, and [[sivert|Sivert]] continues that tradition, Eleseus represents the gravitational pull of the modern — education, urban employment, and distance from the soil. Hamsun does not make him a villain but a figure of loss: what is gained in refinement is lost in substance.
