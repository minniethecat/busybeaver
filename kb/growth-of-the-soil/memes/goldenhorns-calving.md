# Goldenhorns' Calving

**Goldenhorns' calving** was a landmark moment in the early history of the Sellanraa homestead. The birth of the calf represented the first expansion of the settlers' livestock and was celebrated as a sign of prosperity.

## Goldenhorns

Goldenhorns was the cow Isak acquired early in the settlement. Named for her distinctive horns, she was prized and carefully tended:

> "Her name's Goldenhorns. What's that wall to be for ..." [002-024]

Inger herself named the cow, and the animal became central to the household's daily life [002-031]. Inger would shift Goldenhorns to better grazing a dozen times a day [002-069].

## The great day

The calving itself was described with characteristic Hamsun understatement, yet the significance is unmistakable:

> "And Goldenhorns calved. A great day in the wilderness ..." [003-016]

The arrival of the calf was a turning point — the homestead was no longer just surviving; it was growing. Isak had spoken of it with anticipation: "Goldenhorns ready to calve and all" [003-009].

## Significance

In the subsistence economy of the novel, livestock represents wealth and continuity. The calf meant milk for the future, potential breeding stock, and a tangible return on Isak's labour. The event was later echoed when Silverhorns, Goldenhorns' descendant, also calved [004-059], showing the continuity of the bloodline.
