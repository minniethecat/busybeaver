# Community

The scattered pioneer settlements around Sellanraa that together form a fragile, evolving community — united by the shared project of making a life in the wilderness yet divided by gossip, jealousy, and conflicting interests. Growth of the Soil traces the organic emergence of society from isolated homesteads into a recognizable human community.

## 定义

Community in the novel forms slowly and organically. It begins with Isak alone on the moor, then Isak and Inger, then the arrival of a few more settlers — Brede Olsen at Breidablik, Axel Ström at Maaneland. There is no founding charter, no town meeting that declares a village into existence. Instead, community emerges from proximity and necessity: neighbors help each other with heavy labor, gather for weddings and funerals, and slowly develop the shared norms and expectations that constitute a society.

## 在小说中的表现

### The Growth of Neighborliness

As more homesteads appear around Sellanraa, the pioneers develop patterns of mutual aid. Isak helps Axel with building; Inger assists at births; neighbors share tools, news, and labor during haymaking season. These exchanges are never formalized — they are the natural cooperation of people who know that survival in the wilderness depends on reciprocity (019-XXX, 022-XXX).

### Gossip and Conflict

The community also has a darker side. Oline is the novel's great gossip — a carrier of rumors who moves between homesteads spreading news, speculation, and trouble. The story of Inger's infanticide becomes community property, passed from mouth to mouth long after Inger has served her sentence and returned. The village meeting about mining rights reveals the limits of collective decision-making in a community of fiercely independent individuals (027-XXX).

> Oline carried news as a bird carries seeds — dropping them wherever she happened to land.

### The Village Meeting

The village meeting about the copper mine is the novel's most explicit depiction of community decision-making. Settlers gather to discuss whether to sell mining rights to outside interests — a decision that pits short-term profit against long-term stewardship, individual gain against collective interest. The meeting's outcome, shaped by Geissler's intervention, reflects the community's awkward position between pioneer independence and the demands of collective action (027-XXX).

### The Role of Women in Community

Women play a crucial role in building community — through visiting, trading, sharing domestic knowledge, and maintaining the informal networks that hold the settlement together. Inger's weekly trips to the village for trade, her exchanges with other women about cheese-making and child-rearing, constitute the invisible infrastructure of community life that the novel's more overtly "masculine" narrative of building and farming sometimes obscures.

## 深层分析

### Community as Emergent Order

Hamsun's depiction of community formation echoes a broader nineteenth-century intellectual concern with the origins of social order. Where political philosophers debated whether society arose from a social contract or evolved from natural human sociability, Hamsun offers an empirical answer: community grows like a crop. It is not planned, legislated, or contracted — it is planted by the first settler who clears a field, watered by the second who builds nearby, and harvested by the children who grow up knowing their neighbors as extensions of family.

This organic model of community has important implications for how the novel judges social arrangements. The village meeting about the copper mine is fraught precisely because it attempts to impose a formal, contractual model of collective decision-making onto a community that has always operated through informal consensus and personal relationships. The meeting is not a failure of community but a symptom of community's transformation — the point at which organic growth requires institutional scaffolding. Whether that scaffolding supports or crushes the original organism is the question Hamsun leaves open.

### The Community Spectrum: Sellanraa to Storborg

The novel's various homesteads form a spectrum of community integration. At one end stands Sellanraa — physically set apart, approached by a long path, its inhabitants capable of complete self-sufficiency. At the other end stands Storborg, the trading post that exists only because there is a community to serve. Between them lie Breidablik (too close to Sellanraa for comfort, its poverty exposed by proximity), Maaneland (remote enough for independence but connected enough for courtship), and the unnamed later settlements that fill the Almenning.

This spatial arrangement is a moral map: the novel's deepest respect goes to those who can function independently, its tolerance to those who contribute to the whole, and its sharpest criticism to those who parasitize the community without contributing to it. Oline is the parasite par excellence — she produces nothing, yet she consumes the community's attention and emotional energy. Brede Olsen is a figure of pathos rather than villainy — he tries to be a pioneer but lacks the competence, and his failure makes him a burden on neighbors who can ill afford to carry him.

### The Tension Between Autonomy and Obligation

The novel's most productive tension is between the pioneer's desire for autonomy and the community's legitimate claims on its members. Isak helps his neighbors not from abstract moral principle but from a pragmatic recognition that he may need help himself one day. This is reciprocity without sentimentality — a community held together by enlightened self-interest rather than brotherly love. When the balance tips — when a neighbor's need exceeds what reciprocity can justify — the community's limits are exposed. Brede Olsen's eventual departure is not mourned; it is a relief, the excision of a drain on collective resources that had outlasted its welcome.

## 主题意义

Hamsun's treatment of community is characteristically ambivalent. Community is necessary — no one can survive entirely alone — but it also brings the pettiness, gossip, and social control that the pioneer originally fled to the wilderness to escape. The novel's ideal seems to be not isolation but a loose association of independent households, connected enough for mutual aid but distant enough to preserve autonomy. Sellanraa, standing somewhat apart from the other homesteads, embodies this balance.
