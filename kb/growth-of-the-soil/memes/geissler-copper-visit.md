# Geissler's Visit About the Copper Mine

The visit of ex-Lensmand [[geissler]] to [[sellanraa]] in Chapter X is explicitly framed as "a great event" — a turning point that intertwines the copper mine's development with Inger's fate in prison. Geissler arrives unannounced, dressed with conspicuous elegance, and in a single day reshapes Isak's material prospects and family future.

## Arrival and Purpose

Geissler's appearance at Sellanraa is dramatic in its unexpectedness and in the figure he cuts against the wilderness setting:

> Next day was fated to bring a great event. There came a visitor to the farm — Geissler came. (PN=010-001)

> It was not yet summer on the moors, but Geissler paid no heed to the state of the ground; he came on foot, in rich high boots with broad, shiny tops; yellow gloves, too, he wore, and was elegant to see; a man from the village carried his things. (PN=010-001)

Before departing later that day, Geissler also inspected Isak's improvements — the new mill, the expanded fields — acknowledging the progress made since his last visit (PN=010-004).

Geissler's stated purpose was to acquire the copper tract on Isak's land, which had been discovered earlier by the mining prospectors who had taken stone samples from the hills:

> He had come, as a matter of fact, to buy a piece of Isak's land, up in the hills — a copper mine. And what about the price? (PN=010-002)

He also brought news of Inger, whom he had visited in Trondhjem:

> Also, by the way, he had a message from Inger — good girl, every one liked her; he had been in Trondhjem, and seen her. (PN=010-002)

## The Private Conference and News of Inger

Geissler and Isak withdrew into the little room, shutting the door — to Oline's evident displeasure. Once alone, Geissler assumed the dominant role:

> Geissler sat down, slapped his knee with a powerful hand, and there he was — master of Isak's fate. (PN=010-008)

His first question was about the copper:

> "You haven't sold that copper tract yet?" he asked. (PN=010-009)

Before discussing business, Geissler delivered his report on Inger, revealing that he had personally intervened with the prison authorities:

> "I went in to have a talk with your wife — they managed it for me, of course, no difficulty about that — and we had a long talk. 'Well, Inger, how are you getting on?' 'Why, I've no cause to complain.' 'Like to be home again?' 'Ay, I'll not say no.' 'And so you shall before very long,' said I." (PN=010-013)

Geissler described how Inger's disfigurement had been surgically corrected:

> "they've fixed up that trouble with her mouth, by the way — operation — sewed it up again." (PN=010-013)

He recounted his subsequent meeting with the Governor, in which he argued — on grounds that the original sentence had overlooked Inger's mental state after seeing a hare — that her case should be reconsidered. Geissler's rhetorical skill was on full display:

> "I went to the Governor — he saw me, of course, no difficulty about that. 'You've a woman here,' said I, 'that ought to be out of the place, and back in her home — Inger Sellanraa.'" (PN=010-014)

> "And I told him about the hare. 'A hare?' says the Governor. 'A hare,' says I. 'And the child was born with a hare-lip.' 'Oh,' says he, smiling, 'I see. And you think they ought to have made more allowance for that?'" (PN=010-014)

The Governor was not easily persuaded, but Geissler pressed on, raising procedural issues about Inger's original transport and her treatment during the first two months of imprisonment (PN=010-015). He secured permission to submit a formal statement on Isak's behalf.

## The Forged Statement and the Copper Survey

Geissler revealed that he had taken the liberty of writing and signing a statement in Isak's name — a forgery committed with complete self-assurance:

> Geissler went on: "I went straight back to the hotel and wrote out a statement; did the whole thing myself, you understand, and signed it 'Isak Sellanraa.'" (PN=010-017)

This casual assumption of authority — signing another man's name to an official document — is characteristic of Geissler's method: he acts, and others accommodate themselves to his actions.

With the business of Inger settled, Geissler turned to practical matters:

> "And now let's get to business." (PN=010-018)

He and Isak went up into the hills to examine the copper deposit:

> Then they went up into the hills; Geissler, his man, and Isak, and were out for some hours. In a very short time Geissler had followed the lie of the copper vein over a wide stretch of land and marked out the limits of the tract he wanted. (PN=010-020)

## Significance

Geissler's visit is one of the most consequential single events in the novel's first half:

- **The copper mine** passes into Geissler's control, setting up the mining boom — and bust — that will dominate the later chapters of Book I and Book II.
- **Inger's release** is secured through Geissler's intervention, reuniting the Sellanraa household.
- **Geissler's mode of operation** is established: he acts as an unpredictable, self-appointed agent who intervenes decisively in the lives of the settlers, then disappears for years at a time.

The visit also demonstrates Geissler's liminal position — he moves freely between the world of officials (the Governor, the prison authorities) and the world of settlers, manipulating both with equal confidence.
