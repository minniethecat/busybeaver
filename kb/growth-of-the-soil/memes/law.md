# Law

Law is a persistent though often invisible thread running through *Growth of the Soil*. From [[inger|Inger]]'s life sentence for infanticide, to the legal definition of land ownership, to contract negotiations over copper rights — the legal system, as an extension of modern state power, continuously shapes the destiny of every inhabitant of [[sellanraa|Sellanraa]]. Hamsun's attitude toward law is deeply ambivalent: he acknowledges its necessity for maintaining basic order, but maintains a profound critique of its mechanical application in the wilderness context.

## Definition

In the novel, law represents a normative system that comes from "outside" — formulated and interpreted by lawyers and judges in distant cities (PN=007-066), its operational logic fundamentally ruptured from the survival ethics of the wilderness. When [[isak|Isak]] says "the law must take its course" (PN=007-119), his tone contains both acceptance and resignation — law is a force he cannot alter, like the weather.

This externality is the defining feature of law in the novel. Unlike custom, which emerges organically from community practice, law arrives fully formed, carrying the assumptions and priorities of urban civilization into a context where they make little sense.

> "That is so. And can't be altered—the law must take its course. As a matter of fact, the sentence is extraordinarily light. There's one thing you must do now—that is, to set up clear boundaries between your land and the State's." (PN=007-119)

The seamless movement from a murder sentence to a land survey captures the dual nature of law in the novel: it punishes and it organizes, often in the same breath. Isak accepts both functions without protest because he has no alternative framework for understanding them.

## The Novel's Presentation

### Inger's Trial

[[inger|Inger]]'s trial is the novel's most concentrated display of legal power. By the letter of the law, she should receive life imprisonment (PN=007-066), but the human considerations in its execution — leniency, visitation rights, eventual pardon — reveal the tension between legal text and legal practice. The phrase "the law is merciful" (PN=007-052) is, in [[inger|Inger]]'s context, simultaneously ironic (she loses years of freedom) and factual (she could have lost more).

The sentencing scene crystallizes the novel's ambivalence. The magistrate, who himself has a club-foot, recognizes the inadequacy of the law's mechanical response:

> "As to the sentence," he said, "I hardly know. Really, it should be imprisonment for life, but … I can't say, perhaps we might get it commuted, second or third degree, fifteen to twelve years, or twelve to nine. There's a commission sitting to reform the criminal code, make it more humane, but the final decision won't be ready yet. Anyhow, we must hope for the best," said he. (PN=007-069)

The magistrate's hesitation — "I hardly know" — is the sound of a legal system confronting its own limits. He knows what the law requires; he also knows what justice demands. The gap between the two cannot be closed by the instruments at his disposal.

### Barbro's Trial and the Evolution of Law

Barbro's confrontation with the legal system in the novel's second half marks a historical shift. Her claim that "the law's not so cruel hard now as it used to be" (PN=021-118) registers the progress of Norwegian legal reform — but also reveals the continuing vulnerability of women within legal structures that they do not control.

> In time, too, she ceased to care at all for what he said about the death of the child. She simply sniffed and tossed her head. Not that she ever confessed anything, but only said: "Well, and suppose I had drowned it? You live here in the wilds and what do you know of things elsewhere?" (PN=021-118)

Barbro's worldliness — her knowledge of what happens in Bergen and Christiania — is her defense. She has learned that the law is variable, contingent, unequally applied. Her cynicism is not ignorance but a rational response to a system whose operation she has observed at close range.

> Barbro thought for a moment. "Well, I don't care," said she. "The law's all different now, and if you read the papers you'd know. There's heaps that have done it, and don't get anything to speak of." (PN=021-127)

The contrast between Inger and Barbro is instructive. Inger faced the law with fear and submission; Barbro faces it with strategic calculation. The law has not become more just — it has become more manipulable. Both women are caught in its machinery, but they cope with their captivity in different ways.

### The Barbro Trial: Society on Trial

Fru Heyerdahl's defense of Barbro before the court (Chapter XXVI) is the novel's most extended legal argument — and its most radical. She turns the court into a tribunal on society itself, arguing that the unmarried mother is society's victim, not its criminal.

> "Or at any rate, her punishment should be merely nominal," said Fru Heyerdahl. "We are all agreed, of course," she went on, "that infant life should be preserved, but is that to mean that no law of simple humanity is to apply to the unfortunate mother? Think, consider what she has been through during all the period of pregnancy, what suffering she has endured in striving to hide her condition... The child is at least killed in kindness. The mother tries to save herself and the child she loves from the misery of its life." (PN=026-011)

This is law turned against itself — using the court as a platform to indict the society whose laws the court exists to enforce. Fru Heyerdahl's argument is effective precisely because it appeals to values the legal system claims to uphold.

> It was impossible to consider the documents and depositions in the present case without feeling the deepest sympathy for this young girl in her forsaken situation. And yet there was no need to appeal to mercy on her behalf, only to justice and human understanding. (PN=026-062)

The phrase "only to justice and human understanding" is the novel's clearest statement of what law should be — and what, in its mechanical operation, it so often fails to become.

## Deep Analysis

### Law vs. Wilderness Ethics

The conflict between law and wilderness in the novel is not accidental — it is a collision between two fundamentally different normative systems. Wilderness ethics center on survival needs: a child with a harelip has no chance in the wilderness, and infanticide is a cruel but rational choice; a pioneer who occupies and improves unclaimed land earns his right to it through labor, not contract. Legal ethics center on abstract rules: killing is a crime regardless of motive; land rights derive from registration, not labor.

These two systems are irreconcilable because their starting points are opposite. Law cares about the universal applicability of rules — the same act must receive the same treatment. The wilderness cares about context — every decision occurs within specific material conditions, human relationships, and survival pressures. In Inger's trial, these two logics collide directly: law's logic says "she killed her child"; wilderness logic says "she did what a mother in desperation could only do." The novel does not pronounce which logic is correct — it simply records the pain of their collision.

### Geissler as Mediator

Geissler's unique function in the novel is as a translator between law and wilderness. He is fluent in legal language — he knows how to draft contracts, argue in court, exploit legal loopholes. But he also understands the logic of the wilderness. He helps Isak not because the law requires it but because he recognizes the value of the way of life Isak represents.

Geissler's tragedy is that he cannot fully belong to either world. He is too corrupt, too irregular, to be an acceptable member of the urban legal system; but he is too worldly, too knowledgeable about law, to immerse himself completely in the wilderness's simple life like Isak. He hangs between two worlds, a permanent mediator — indispensable to each, a stranger in both.

### The Historical Trajectory of Law

The changes in law across the novel reflect a larger historical process: from customary law to statutory law, from local adjudication to national justice, from "law must be feared to be obeyed" to "law must be known to be followed." Inger's trial occurs early in the novel, decided by a court in a distant city — law is to her an alien, incomprehensible force. By Barbro's time, law has become "different" — milder, but also more pervasive, penetrating more deeply into daily life.

This trajectory is modernization itself mapped at the micro level. The wilderness community initially operates through custom, personal credit, and informal agreement — the world of customary law. As state infrastructure extends (telegraph lines, regular postal service, formal land registration), statute law follows, replacing informal agreements with formal contracts, personal trust with legal documents. Hamsun's ambivalent attitude toward this process — acknowledging its necessity while mourning its cost — is his general attitude toward modernity.

## Thematic Significance

Law in the novel's thematic structure represents "civilization" at its most paradoxical: it is simultaneously an instrument for protecting the weak and a channel through which state power penetrates private life. Hamsun seems to suggest: the wilderness needs some minimum of legal order to prevent complete chaos, but when law attempts to impose urban standards on wilderness survival logic (as in [[inger|Inger]]'s case), it becomes a form of violence. The ideal law — if it exists — would resemble [[geissler|Geissler]]: understanding the particularities of the wilderness, seeking a wise middle path between text and reality.

> "But," said Fru Heyerdahl, "if this is the terrible fate that is meted out to the guilty—or, let us say, the more clearly guilty—unmarried mother who has killed her child, what of the innocent one who is merely suspected of the crime, and has not committed it? What reparation does society offer to her? None at all!" (PN=026-014)

This question — "what reparation does society offer?" — is the novel's legal heart. Law can punish; it can acquit; it cannot repair. The damage done by the legal process itself is permanent, regardless of outcome.
