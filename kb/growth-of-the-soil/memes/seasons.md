# Seasons

The changing **seasons** — spring, summer, autumn, winter — form the fundamental rhythm of life on the Sellanraa homestead. Each season brings its own tasks, from spring planting to autumn harvest, and each shapes the settlers' relationship with the land.

## Spring

Spring marks the beginning of the year's work. Isak plans his clearing and planting as the snow melts:

> "Ay, but just now with the spring coming on—and I've need of a ..." [003-032]

Spring was the time for manuring, preparing the soil, and looking ahead to the growing season [004-012]. The thawing earth released its promise, and Isak worked with an urgency that the novel presents as instinctive rather than calculated. When the frost left the ground, he was already there with spade and seed.

Spring also brought new life to the farm: lambs, kids, and calves arrived in their season. The novel's attention to these births is matter-of-fact — "the two she-goats had each had twins" (001-028) — but the accumulation of such details creates a quiet celebration of renewal that no single passage could convey.

## Summer

The light summer nights brought long working hours and a sense of possibility. Isak felled timber through the summer months [001-065]. The brief but intense Nordic summer drove the rhythm of outdoor labour, with haymaking and mowing occupying the long days.

Summer was the season of visible growth. The potato plants broke through the soil, the grass grew tall enough to cut, and the settlers' labour showed its results. It was also the season of visitors — Geissler, Oline, and other outsiders appeared more often in summer, when the roads were passable and the days long enough for travel. Summer brought the outside world to the homestead's door, for better and worse.

## Autumn

Autumn was the season of gathering and preparation. By his first autumn, Isak had already built a house for himself [001-014]. The autumn rains pressured settlers to finish covering buildings before the wet set in [002-069]. It was also the time for slaughter and storing winter fodder.

The novel treats autumn with particular gravity. It is the season of reckoning — the harvest measures the year's work, and a poor harvest means a hard winter. Isak's autumn potato harvest is described with almost ceremonial attention: "he dug up his potatoes again, a plentiful crop" (004-017). The repetition of "again" — year after year, the same act — captures the settler's relationship with seasonal time: not progress but persistence.

## Winter

Winter brought hardship but also craftsmanship. Isak made great wooden troughs during the winter months [001-022]. The frozen landscape forced a shift from outdoor labour to indoor tasks — repairing tools, working wood, and waiting for the thaw [001-024].

Winter in the novel is not merely an obstacle. It is the season of interior life — the time when the homestead turns inward, when characters talk by the fire, and when the slow work of mending and making occupies hands that in summer would hold a scythe. Isak's winter trough-making is not an idle pastime but an essential production: "he had made a number of them, and sold them" (004-063). Even in winter, the pioneer produces.

## The Seasonal Cycle and Human Life

The novel's chapter structure mirrors the agricultural year, but Hamsun goes further: he makes the seasonal cycle a metaphor for human existence. Births arrive with spring, marriages with the harvest season, and death — when it comes — tends toward winter. The cyclical time of the farm stands in deliberate contrast to the linear, progressive time of the city, where seasons are inconveniences rather than governing forces.

This contrast is never stated explicitly but is felt throughout. When Eleseus leaves for the city, he leaves seasonal time behind. When Sivert stays, he accepts a life governed by the same rhythms that governed his father. The novel's quiet preference for Sivert's choice is in part a preference for seasonal time — for a life in which returning, repeating, and renewing are the deepest forms of meaning.
