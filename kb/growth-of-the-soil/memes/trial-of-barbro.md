# Trial of Barbro

The court case against Barbro Bredesen for the death of her newborn child, held in the town during Book II of *Growth of the Soil*. The case mirrors Inger's trial in Book I — both women killed their infants, both were tried, and both were acquitted — but the circumstances and consequences differ, reflecting the changing moral landscape of the district.

## Background

Barbro, daughter of Brede Olsen, had been living with Axel Ström at Maaneland. She became pregnant and the evidence was visible to the community:

> "Barbro had been going about, evidently with child and plain to see, and she had left the place by herself all unencumbered and no child at all." (024-092)

The disappearance of the newborn raised suspicion. Oline, ever watchful for scandal, was the first to voice the question openly: "about Barbro — wasn't she far gone with child?" (022-097). The matter eventually reached the authorities, and a court case was convened.

## The Trial

The court proceedings took place in the town:

> "The court sat listening to what appeared to be its duty in regard to the case of a girl named Barbro, and the death of her child." (026-050)

The key question before the court was whether Barbro had intentionally killed the child or whether the death was natural. The prosecution examined whether Barbro had "attempted to conceal her condition" or "denied being with child" (026-058). Barbro defended herself capably — "Barbro was clever enough; she said a good word for Axel, and declared that he had known nothing of her having borne a child till after it was all over" (026-004). By exonerating Axel, she strengthened her own position.

The case proceeded favorably for the defendant. "The case was going well as far as Barbro herself was concerned, and if she were acquitted, then there could be no question of any complicity at all" (026-021). A small number of witnesses were heard: "the Lensmand, Axel himself, the experts, a couple of girls from the village" (026-022). Notably, Oline — who had done much to stir suspicion — "had not been summoned" (026-022).

## Verdict and Aftermath

Barbro was acquitted:

> "Brede's girl Barbro had had a lawsuit in the town, and come home acquitted; but Geissler, he had been there in court all the time." (027-044)

Geissler's presence at the trial — though his role is not specified — links this legal event to the broader network of power and influence he moves within.

The acquittal did not satisfy everyone. Oline was "displeased, ay, disappointed was Oline" (027-059). She continued to insist on Barbro's guilt:

> "Nay, 'twas a mean and wicked trick of Barbro to make away with the bit of a child and then run off herself." (027-061)

Despite the legal verdict, moral judgment lingered in the community.

## Parallels with the Trial of Inger

Barbro's trial deliberately echoes the [[trial-of-inger]] from Book I. Both women killed their newborn children — Inger because of the child's cleft lip, Barbro under circumstances that remain ambiguous. Both were acquitted. Yet the trials differ in tone: Inger's case was treated with gravity and led to her imprisonment and eventual transformation; Barbro's case is dispatched more briskly, suggesting a coarsening of the community's moral sensibilities or perhaps a growing tolerance shaped by experience.

Geissler's presence at both trials — he helped secure Inger's early release and attended Barbro's trial — links the two events through a recurring character who operates at the boundary between the district and the institutions of the state.
