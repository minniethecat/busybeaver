# Settler

The settler is the central human figure in Growth of the Soil. The novel follows Isak, the first settler, from his solitary arrival in the wilderness through the gradual accumulation of neighbours, buildings, and institutions that transforms the Almenning into a community. Each new settler who appears tests a different proposition about what it means to live on the land.

## 定义

In Hamsun's vocabulary, a settler ("nybygger" in the original Norwegian) is someone who clears uncultivated land and makes a home on it. The term implies more than mere residence — it means breaking soil, building shelter, and producing food where none was produced before. The novel distinguishes sharply between settlers and those who merely pass through: the Lensmand, the traders, the engineers from town. A settler stays.

## 在小说中的表现

### The First Settler

Isak is the archetype: "a settler in the wilds, nine hundred years old in type" (031-084). This phrase, appearing near the novel's end, collapses the distance between Isak and the first humans who cultivated land. He is not an individual so much as a recurring human form — the person who arrives, clears, plants, and stays. His competence is not intellectual but physical: "he didn't understand things like a farmer and a settler on new land" (019-009) — yet he does understand, in his bones, what the land requires.

### The Settler Community

New settlers appear gradually. Brede Olsen arrives with ambitions that exceed his competence. Axel Ström clears land at Maaneland and demonstrates a different kind of persistence. By Book II, there are multiple holdings: "the two new settlers had built their turf huts" (023-077). The settlers become a constituency, a collective that Geissler can address and whose interests he can champion. Hamsun tracks this transformation without sentimentality — the settlers are "healthy folk" (007-039), solid rather than heroic.

### The Settler's Wife

The novel gives sustained attention to the settler's wife as a distinct role. Inger, the first settler's wife, must manage the household while Isak works the land. Later, a new settler's wife is described as "earnest and thoughtful anew" (015-002) — the word "anew" suggesting that each settler couple must rediscover the same truths. The settler's wife is not simply a helper but the emotional and moral center of the homestead.
