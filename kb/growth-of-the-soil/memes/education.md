# Education

The formal schooling and book-learning that in Hamsun's novel represents the outside world's encroachment on pioneer life — offering advancement at the cost of estrangement from the soil.

## 定义

Education in Growth of the Soil is not a neutral good. It is the vehicle by which the values of the village and the city enter the pioneer homestead. For Isak, who cannot read, the land is his only teacher. For his son Eleseus, schooling opens a door to a different life — one of clerks and offices rather than spades and plows. The novel treats education as a double-edged gift: it enables but also alienates (010-030, 025-012).

## 在小说中的表现

### Eleseus's Schooling

Isak and Inger decide to send Eleseus to school in the village, hoping to give him opportunities they never had. He learns to read, write, and keep accounts — skills that set him apart from his illiterate father. But the education also changes him: he becomes "a gentleman," unfit for farm labor, preferring the pen to the spade (010-035).

### The Return of the Educated Son

When Eleseus returns to Sellanraa as a young man, the contrast with his brother Sivert is stark. Sivert, who never went to school, works the land with his father's strength and competence. Eleseus, the educated one, is "no hand at farming" — his knowledge is of a different world, and it has made him a stranger to his own home (025-015).

> He had learned to write, and was no hand at farming. (025-015)

### Sivert's Different Path

Sivert represents the alternative: education through labor rather than books. He grows up working alongside Isak, absorbing skills through direct experience — ditching, plowing, herding, building. Hamsun suggests that this tacit knowledge, embodied in competent hands rather than written words, is the truer form of education. The novel never criticizes Sivert for being unlearned; instead, it presents his competence as self-evident proof of a different kind of knowing.

### Inger's City Learning

Inger's case complicates the novel's apparent anti-education stance. During her years in Bergen, she learned to read and write, acquired sewing skills, and gained a broader perspective on the world. When she returns to Sellanraa, these new abilities do not destroy her connection to the farm — they enhance it. She becomes a more capable household manager, and her literacy proves useful in dealing with the outside world. Inger's story suggests that education is harmful only when it severs the learner from labor; when integrated with practical life, it becomes a tool rather than a trap.

### The Value of Practical Knowledge

Against formal education, the novel sets the value of practical knowledge: Isak's ingenuity with building and tools, Inger's skill with cheese-making, Sivert's instinctive understanding of animals and seasons. This knowledge, gained through labor rather than books, is portrayed as deeper and more valuable than school-learning (005-025).

> Isak had kept his eyes about him down in the village… he could build with timber himself. (004-012)

## 深层分析

### The Two Educations Framework

Hamsun's treatment of education rests on a fundamental distinction between two modes of knowing. The first is **embedded knowledge** — skills acquired through direct, sustained engagement with a material environment: Isak learning to build by watching and doing, Inger mastering cheese-making through trial and error, Sivert reading the seasons through years of physical labor. This knowledge is tacit, embodied, and inseparable from the person who possesses it. The second is **abstract knowledge** — skills acquired through formal instruction, detached from immediate material consequences: Eleseus learning accounts in a schoolroom, mastering a system of symbols that refers to money and goods he has never produced.

The novel's preference for embedded knowledge is not merely sentimental. Each instance of abstract education in the story carries a structural consequence: it pulls the learner away from the land by creating an alternative field of value — the ledger, the office, the city — where status is measured not in harvests but in credentials. Eleseus is Hamsun's case study in this displacement: his education does not fail him; it succeeds too well, equipping him perfectly for a life his father's world cannot offer.

### Education and the Colonial Context

Seen through the lens of colonial settlement, education takes on additional significance. The Norwegian government's push for schooling in the hinterlands was part of a broader civilizing mission — a way of extending state authority into remote regions by shaping the minds of the next generation. When Isak and Inger send Eleseus to school, they are not merely making a private family decision; they are participating in the incorporation of the wilderness into the national administrative state.

The irony is that this very incorporation threatens the pioneer ethos that Isak embodies. The settler who clears the land and builds from nothing represents a kind of radical independence from state structures. But once the schoolhouse appears on the frontier, that independence begins to erode — the pioneer's children become citizens, taxpayers, and clerks rather than builders and farmers. Hamsun registers this transformation with a mixture of regret and realism: the process is inevitable, but its cost is real.

### Comparative Perspective

Hamsun's suspicion of formal education places him in a nineteenth-century tradition of skepticism about book-learning — a tradition that includes Tolstoy's critique of urban sophistication in favor of peasant wisdom, and Thoreau's insistence that "we do not ride on the railroad; it rides upon us." But Hamsun adds a specifically Nordic inflection: his praise of practical competence is not a romanticization of ignorance but a recognition that the northern climate and terrain demand a particular kind of intelligence — one measured not by literacy but by survival through winter, by the ability to read weather and soil rather than text.

This framework helps explain why the novel treats Inger's Bergen education so differently from Eleseus's village schooling. Inger's learning was acquired under coercion (she was imprisoned), in an urban environment far from Sellanraa, and she returned with skills that she immediately put to use in the household. Her education was, in effect, recolonized by labor — she brought the abstract knowledge back to the material world and made it serve. Eleseus, by contrast, never reintegrates his learning with labor; his education remains an exit strategy, not a tool.

## 主题意义

Education embodies the novel's central critique of civilization. Book-learning separates people from the land by teaching them to value abstraction over experience, status over work, the city over the soil. Eleseus's tragedy is not that he is uneducated but that his education has made him unfit for the life that gives meaning.

But the novel is more nuanced than a simple condemnation. Inger's education in Bergen enriches her without alienating her. The difference is that Inger's learning was integrated into a life of labor, while Eleseus's education became a substitute for labor — he learned to be a clerk precisely to avoid being a farmer.

Hamsun's position is not anti-intellectual but anti-abstraction: knowledge that grows from engagement with the material world (Isak's building, Inger's cheese-making) is honored; knowledge that floats free of such engagement (Eleseus's office skills) is suspect.
