# Fru Heyerdahl

Fru Heyerdahl is the wife of [[heyerdahl|Lensmand Heyerdahl]] and a woman of considerable social standing in the district. She plays a pivotal role in [[trial-of-barbro|Barbro's trial]] for infanticide, using her position to advocate for leniency. Later, she takes Barbro into her household as a servant, where her mixture of kindness and strict control sets the stage for the final rupture between the two women.

## The Infanticide Trial

Fru Heyerdahl appears at Barbro's trial as a self-appointed advocate for women. She delivers an impassioned speech that proves decisive:

> "We women," said Fru Heyerdahl, "we are an unfortunate and oppressed moiety of humanity." ([026-008])

She argues that Barbro's punishment "should be merely nominal" and appeals to "simple humanity" toward "the unfortunate mother" ([026-011]). Her most effective rhetorical move is to contrast the clearly guilty unmarried mother with "the innocent one who is merely suspected of the crime" ([026-014]) — a framing that helps secure Barbro's acquittal.

Geissler dryly notes the alignment between the Crown prosecutor and Fru Heyerdahl, observing that her speech had "taken the wind out of his sails beforehand" by preemptively using several of his intended arguments ([026-056]).

After the acquittal, Fru Heyerdahl seeks out Axel Ström to smooth things over:

> But Fru Heyerdahl guessed, no doubt, that Axel was not over pleased with her speech of the day before, and tried to smooth it over, to make up for it somehow now. ([026-095])

She explains that she was "obliged to try and shift the blame a little" toward Axel, otherwise "Barbro would have been convicted, and you too" ([026-099]). Axel, touched, offers her a gift of meat in gratitude.

## Mistress and Guardian

Fru Heyerdahl takes Barbro into her household as a servant, initially treating her kindly — giving her "aprons and neat things to wear" ([029-003]). But she soon becomes increasingly watchful, particularly when Christmas festivities begin:

> But then Fru Heyerdahl had begun to be even more careful about her, and when the Christmas festivities began, she was strict. ([029-003])

She lectures Barbro and lends her books — a program of moral improvement that Barbro resents. When Fru Heyerdahl grows suspicious about Barbro's nighttime activities, she begins making surprise checks at her servants' room at three and four in the morning ([029-007], [029-014]).

## Confrontation

The relationship reaches a breaking point when Fru Heyerdahl discovers Barbro with a man at night. She screams and draws back in horror. Barbro, cornered, turns on her mistress:

> Fru Heyerdahl mightily indignant; she cares nothing about Cook, 'tis Barbro she is after, the girl whose character she has answered for. ([029-032])

Fru Heyerdahl is struck speechless, "only opening and closing her mouth" ([029-037]) — outmaneuvered by the very girl she had tried to save and reform.

## Significance

Fru Heyerdahl represents the district's nascent "society" class — educated, reform-minded, but ultimately ineffectual. Her advocacy at the trial is well-meaning but arguably misguided, and her attempt to control Barbro through surveillance and moral instruction backfires completely. She embodies the limitations of external moral reform when confronted with the stubborn realities of human nature — a theme that runs throughout the novel.
