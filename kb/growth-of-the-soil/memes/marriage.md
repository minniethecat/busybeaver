# Marriage

**Marriage** in _Growth of the Soil_ serves both as a personal bond and as a social institution that anchors the settler community. From Isak and Inger's informal union to the formal ceremonies that follow, marriage traces the arc from wilderness isolation to village society.

## Isak and Inger

Isak and Inger's relationship began without formal ceremony. When Inger arrived at the hut, she simply stayed:

> "Isak. And what's yours?" "Inger." ... She did not go away at all. [001-039, 001-047]

Their union was practical before it was legal — a partnership of labour and survival [001-049]. The question of formalizing the marriage arose only when practical circumstances demanded it:

> "If we put off the wedding business for a bit?" said Isak. [004-008]

They eventually remembered to have the wedding before the birth of their child [004-012], conforming to social expectation.

## Marriage as community event

As the settlement grew, marriage became a community concern. Inger thought about Jensine's marriage prospects [028-020], and the necessary ceremony was planned in advance:

> "Even the necessary marriage ceremony was to take place before ..." [029-079]

## The marriage theme

Beyond individual unions, marriage is treated as a theme that structures the novel's closing reflections:

> "... its main theme being the 'marriage' conflict touched upon in the ..." [031-138]

This suggests that Hamsun saw the marriage bond — its tensions, its practical necessities, and its place in the social order — as central to the novel's meaning.
