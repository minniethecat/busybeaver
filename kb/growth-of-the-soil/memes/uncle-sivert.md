# Uncle Sivert

Uncle Sivert is a relative of Inger's family — her uncle, after whom she names her second son, [[sivert|Sivert]]. He is an elderly man of considerable reputed wealth who lives over the hills. The subplot of his death and the dissolution of his fortune runs through Book I and into Book II, serving as a commentary on inheritance, family obligation, and the insubstantiality of wealth.

## A Reputation for Wealth

Uncle Sivert is first mentioned as a figure of substance, someone whose name carries weight. Inger asks after him particularly when visitors come ([006-064]). The family entertains hopes that young Sivert, having been named after his uncle, will one day inherit:

> And talking of money, Sivert ought to be none so badly off when his Uncle Sivert dies. ([014-008])

Uncle Sivert had heard of Eleseus and his grand doings in town, and the story did not please him. He insisted that his namesake "should not come to want" ([014-011]). Yet even then, there are doubts about the actual size of his fortune: "But what was this fortune Uncle Sivert was supposed to possess?" ([014-011]).

## The Deathbed Summons

When Uncle Sivert falls ill, Oline brings word that he is dying, and young Sivert is summoned to his bedside:

> Why, 'twas Oline had come over the hills one day with word from Uncle Sivert that he was dying; and, of course, young Sivert had to go. ([017-003])

But Uncle Sivert proves not to be on his deathbed at all — he is described as "incorrigible old knave," not confined to bed but simply an invalid over seventy, pottering about half-dressed ([017-011]). He keeps young Sivert waiting while he lingers.

## The Money-Chest

Uncle Sivert's wealth is embodied in his money-chest, a "famous bottle-case" containing his accounts ([018-021]). When Eleseus arrives, having learned bookkeeping in town, Uncle Sivert sets him to work on his accounts — "the weirdest book-keeping ever seen." Years-old tax payments in kind (a goat, a load of dried cod) are recorded nowhere, but the old man "searched his memory and said, 'He's paid!'" ([018-021]).

He is generous to Oline during his final days, promising: "If so be I shouldn't get better this time and get about again on earth I'll take care you're not left out" ([018-024]).

## Death and Disappointment

Uncle Sivert dies at last ([018-001]). But when his affairs are settled, the fortune proves a phantom:

> Old Sivert's affairs had been gone into, his accounts reckoned up, and the fortune remaining after him come to nothing. ([020-002])

The farm, the cow, the fishery, and nets are "barely enough to cover the deficit" ([020-012]). Oline, who had been promised a legacy, receives an empty chest — though she later blames not Uncle Sivert but the government men who settled the estate for cheating her.

## Significance

Uncle Sivert embodies a recurring theme in the novel: the unreliability of money and the superiority of land as true wealth. His fortune evaporates on death, while Isak's steady cultivation of Sellanraa produces real, lasting value. The naming of young Sivert after a man whose wealth turns to dust is an ironic touch — but young Sivert, raised on the land, proves to be the true heir to his father's values.
