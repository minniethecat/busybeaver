# Tillage of the Soil

The agricultural practice of breaking, turning, and cultivating the earth — presented in Hamsun's novel as the foundational human activity from which all genuine civilization springs.

## Definition

"Tillage of the soil" encompasses all forms of agricultural labor: clearing stones, plowing, sowing, harvesting, and the endless cycle of work that transforms wilderness into productive farmland. In the novel, it represents more than mere farming technique — it is a moral and spiritual practice, the source of authentic human value (PN=001-005, PN=003-001).

The concept is rooted in Isak's very first encounter with the land. Arriving at a nameless hillside, he tests the soil with an iron tool and finds what he is looking for — ground that can sustain life.

> The morning shows him a range of pasture and woodland. He moves down, and there is a green hillside; far below, a glimpse of the stream, and a hare bounding across. The man nods his head, as it were approvingly... Here and there he stops to dig with an iron tool, and finds good mould, or peaty soil, manured with the rotted wood and fallen leaves of a thousand years. He nods, to say that he has found himself a place to stay and live: ay, he will stay here and live. (PN=001-005)

This moment — the judgment of soil quality — is the novel's foundational act. Every subsequent event follows from this simple geological decision: Isak has chosen his ground.

## The Novel's Presentation

### The Rhythm of Labor

[[isak|Isak]]'s life follows the seasonal rhythm of agricultural work: spring sowing, summer growth, autumn harvest, winter preparation. This cycle provides the novel's narrative structure, and [[isak|Isak]]'s prosperity is directly proportional to his investment in the soil (PN=001-001, PN=002-039).

The spring sowing is presented not merely as an agricultural procedure but as a sacred ritual — an act connecting Isak to an unbroken chain of ancestors stretching "back into forgotten time."

> For generations back, into forgotten time, his fathers before him had sowed corn; solemnly, on a still, calm evening, best with a gentle fall of warm and misty rain, soon after the grey goose flight. Potatoes were a new thing, nothing mystic, nothing religious; women and children could plant them—earth-apples that came from foreign parts, like coffee; fine rich food, but much like swedes and mangolds. Corn was nothing less than bread; corn or no corn meant life or death. (PN=003-099)

The contrast between corn and potatoes is telling: corn is sacred, ancestral, tied to bread and survival; potatoes are useful but secular — foreign imports without ritual weight. Hamsun uses this distinction to measure the moral distance between different kinds of agricultural work.

The description of Isak sowing elevates labor into something approaching prayer. The sower becomes a universal figure — a speck in a cosmic pattern.

> Isak walked bareheaded, in Jesu name, a sower. Like a tree-stump with hands to look at, but in his heart like a child. Every cast was made with care, in a spirit of kindly resignation. Look! the tiny grains that are to take life and grow, shoot up into ears, and give more corn again; so it is throughout all the earth where corn is sown. Palestine, America, the valleys of Norway itself—a great wide world, and here is Isak, a tiny speck in the midst of it all, a sower. (PN=003-100)

Isak's daily labor is equally meticulous: the creation of cultivated land from raw earth requires unending physical attention.

> The days now were occupied in field work and more field work; he cleared new bits of ground, getting out roots and stones; ploughing, manuring, harrowing, working with pick and spade, breaking lumps of soil and crumbling them with hand and heel; a tiller of the ground always, laying out fields like velvet carpets. (PN=003-098)

### Soil vs. Other Endeavors

The novel systematically contrasts tillage with other forms of economic activity — mining, trading, and speculation. Each alternative is shown to be transient, morally suspect, or destructive. Only tillage creates lasting value (PN=008-002, PN=009-002).

When the telegraph line comes through Sellanraa, it marks the encroachment of the outside world. But the telegraph, unlike tillage, produces nothing — it merely transmits.

> Once more there came visitors to Sellanraa; an engineer, with a foreman and a couple of workmen, marking out telegraph lines again over the hills. By the route they were taking now, the line would be carried a little above the house, and a straight road cut through the forest. No harm in that. It would make the place less desolate, a glimpse of the world would make it brighter. (PN=009-002)

The ambiguous phrase "a glimpse of the world would make it brighter" captures Hamsun's ambivalence about modernity — the world outside offers light, but the light may not be an improvement over the darkness of the wilderness.

Isak himself is defined by the title "tiller of the soil" — a designation that recurs throughout the novel and functions almost as a formal identity.

> And Isak taught his sons many other things good and useful to know... but 'twas not for him, not for this born tiller of the soil, to give them any manner of higher education; the Catechism and Bible history lay quietly on the shelf with the cheeses. (PN=009-054)

### The Title's Meaning

The novel's title — *Growth of the Soil* — directly references tillage as its central metaphor. The "growth" is literal (crops, livestock) and metaphorical (human character, community, civilization). Everything of value in the novel grows from the soil (PN=001-001, PN=004-025).

The novel's closing portrait of Isak transforms him from a specific character into an archetype — the settler, the cultivator, the man who is what he does.

> A tiller of the ground, body and soul; a worker on the land without respite. A ghost risen out of the past to point the future, a man from the earliest days of cultivation, a settler in the wilds, nine hundred years old, and, withal, a man of the day. (PN=031-084)

This is the novel's final word on tillage: it is both ancient and contemporary, both the oldest human activity and the one most urgently needed. Isak is "nine hundred years old" — he belongs to no specific century, because what he does belongs to all centuries.

## Thematic Significance

### Tillage as Moral Foundation

Tillage of the soil is the novel's moral center. Hamsun presents agricultural labor as the activity that distinguishes genuine human achievement from hollow civilization. The farmer who works the soil participates in creation itself — transforming wilderness into a garden, chaos into order.

This moral privilege is not philosophical abstraction; it is earned through physical effort. When Isak works, his tools are simple — spade, pick, axe, sledge — but his results are lasting. The land he clears remains cleared. The soil he tills remains fertile.

> Isak—it was hard to say how he felt about it. Half as if he were not ill-pleased after all to find his land valued at a big price, after the work he had done... He made no further business about it; he could go on working as he had done hitherto, clearing and cultivating, fetching loads of timber from the untended woodlands. Isak was not a man to look about anxiously for what might come; he worked. (PN=005-037)

The sentence "he worked" — standing alone — is the novel's moral verdict. To work the soil is to be innocent of history, innocent of commerce, innocent of everything except the obligation to transform the earth.

### The Wilderness Transformed

The transformation from wilderness to farmland is the novel's primary narrative arc. When Isak arrives, the land is nameless — "this no-man's place." Through tillage, it becomes Sellanraa: a named place, a farm, a home, a legacy.

> The worst of his task had been to find the place; this no-man's place, but his. Now, there was work to fill his days... A born carrier of loads, a lumbering barge of a man in the forest—oh, as if he loved his calling, tramping long roads and carrying heavy burdens; as if life without a load upon one's shoulders were a miserable thing, no life for him. (PN=001-006)

Isak's "calling" is not chosen — it is discovered in the act of work. He loves labor not because it is easy but because it is meaningful. The load on his shoulders is the weight of civilization being built.
