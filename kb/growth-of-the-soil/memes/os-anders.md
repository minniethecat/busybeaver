# Os-Anders

Os-Anders is a Lapp (Sami) who appears in Book I of the novel, traveling between the mountain settlements and the village. He brings news, begs for food and shelter, and through his actions becomes entangled in the superstitions surrounding Inger's pregnancy.

## A Lapp Messenger

Os-Anders first appears coming over the hills with his dog, bringing practical news from the village:

> Os-Anders the Lapp, coming by with his dog, brought news that folk in the village had cut their corn already, for fodder. ([004-060])

He is a regular visitor to Sellanraa, and Inger provides him with milk and food for his dog. When he hears music inside the house — Inger playing a barrel organ Geissler had lent her — he lifts his head in surprise at the unfamiliar sound ([004-071]).

## The Custom of Begging

Os-Anders lives not by herding reindeer but by begging and staying with other Lapps. Inger observes that he always begs, as is customary among the Lapps whom she knows:

> Inger wondered why he did not beg for anything; Os-Anders always begged, as do all the Lapps. ([004-090])

Isak, however, is less charitable toward him, wondering aloud "what good Os-Anders has ever done to me and mine" ([008-055]).

## The Hare and the Hare-Lip

Os-Anders becomes significant to the plot through the folk belief surrounding Inger's pregnancy. He brings a hare to Sellanraa that summer, and when Inger's child is born with a hare-lip, the community attributes the deformity to this event. Inger herself acknowledges the connection:

> Inger—ay, she had told some Lapps about Os-Anders, how he came and brought a hare that summer, and gave her unborn child the hare-lip. ([007-053])

This superstition becomes one of the factors in Inger's decision to kill her deformed infant, a crime for which she is later imprisoned.

## Disappearance

Os-Anders visits Sellanraa twice in one summer during his travels, though he "had no business there at all, he should have passed on his way" ([008-044]). His visits cease after the infanticide becomes known. Oline later reports that Os-Anders no longer dares to come, and ultimately reveals that he has died:

> No, maybe you're not afraid of Os-Anders, but I've forbid him to come here all the same. 'Twas the least I could do for you. ([014-075])

Oline uses this knowledge — that Os-Anders the Lapp "had died the day before" ([014-079]) — as currency in her complex relationship with Inger, each woman now holding compromising knowledge about the other.
