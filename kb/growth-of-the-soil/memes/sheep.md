# Sheep

Sheep are among the earliest animals at Isak's homestead, and their care — or neglect — serves as an index of character throughout the novel. Unlike the grander cattle and horses, sheep are humble creatures, but in Hamsun's hands they become a quiet test of the pioneer's vigilance, responsibility, and relationship with the living world.

## Definition

In Growth of the Soil, sheep represent the first rung of pastoral prosperity. Before cattle, before the horse, Isak has sheep: "sheep with wool and lambs four new" (001-051). They are easier to keep than cattle, foraging on the mountainside, but they demand constant vigilance — against predators, against accidents, against their own wandering nature. A pioneer who cannot keep his sheep safe has failed at something fundamental.

## Sheep in the Novel

### Early Days at Sellanraa

Sheep appear in the novel's opening chapter, part of Isak's vision of abundance. By Chapter II, the farm already has "a farm with sheep, a farm with a cow already" (002-040) — sheep come first in the enumeration. They multiply rapidly: by Chapter IX, Isak can boast "I've twenty sheep and sixteen goats" (009-016), the numbers speaking louder than any description.

### The Accident and Its Lesson

One of the novel's most resonant episodes involves a sheep: "a sheep, roaming with her lamb, gets caught" (006-033). Isak discovers the injured animal and makes a resolution: "it shall break no more sheep's thighs!" (006-035). The scene is brief but telling — Isak's response is not sentiment but practical action. He builds better fences, clears dangerous terrain, and watches more carefully. The sheep's suffering is not dwelt upon; what matters is the improvement it prompts.

> "a sheep, roaming with her lamb, gets caught" (006-033)

### Counting as Ritual

Isak counts his sheep obsessively, a ritual of ownership and care: "I'd all but forgotten the sheep,' he says to himself; he counts them" (009-088). When he miscounts, he does it again: "He counts the sheep over again, putting his forefinger" on each one (009-089). This careful enumeration is not greed but diligence — the pioneer's constant audit of his own domain.

## Wool and Trade

Sheep provide more than meat. Their wool is one of Sellanraa's earliest trade goods — a product that requires no planting, no harvest, only the annual shearing and the patience to card and spin. Inger takes charge of this dimension of the farm's economy, transforming raw wool into goods that can be carried to the village and exchanged. The sheep thus bridge the gap between subsistence and commerce: they feed the family and, through their wool, connect the homestead to the wider world of buying and selling.

This dual function — subsistence and trade — is characteristic of Hamsun's economic vision. The pioneer does not choose between self-sufficiency and the market; he moves between them as circumstances require. The sheep, humble as they are, embody this flexibility more clearly than any of the larger livestock.

> "I've twenty sheep and sixteen goats. The beasts are doing well" (009-016)

## Sheep and Goats: A Comparison

The novel pairs sheep and goats throughout, but Hamsun draws a subtle distinction. Goats are the pioneers — hardy, curious, the first to arrive. Sheep are the settlers — more vulnerable, more productive, requiring more care but yielding more. Isak counts both together: "twenty sheep and sixteen goats" (009-016), but the sheep come first in the tally because they matter more. A lost goat is a misfortune; a lost sheep is a failure.

> "He counts the sheep over again, putting his forefinger" (009-089)
