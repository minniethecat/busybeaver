# Almenning

The Norwegian legal concept of common land — the vast untamed tracts belonging to no individual owner — that in Hamsun's novel provides both the literal landscape and the symbolic reservoir from which all pioneer settlement springs.

## 定义

Almenning (Norwegian: "common land" or "common tract") refers to the unowned wilderness — forests, moors, and mountains — that lies beyond the limits of private property. In the novel's world, the Almenning is the property of the State, open to any who would settle and work it. It is the primordial landscape from which pioneers carve their homesteads, and the inexhaustible source of new land for each successive wave of settlers (PN=001-001, PN=023-064).

## 在小说中的表现

### The First Path

The novel opens with the Almenning — "the great Almenning — the common tracts without an owner; no-man's-land." Through this wilderness the first path is trodden, first by a human, then by beasts, then by the Lapps, "wearing them deeper." The Almenning is the condition of the world before human settlement: trackless, ownerless, waiting (PN=001-001).

### The Settler's Source

When [[isak|Isak]] selects his hillside, he chooses a place within the Almenning. His act of settlement — clearing, building, planting — transforms a piece of the common land into private property. This transformation is the novel's foundational act: the Almenning is the raw material from which civilization is made (PN=001-005).

### The Limit of Settlement

Later, the Almenning reappears as a defined boundary: "its limit — and beyond that was Almenning, the property of the State." The surveyor establishes exactly where private land ends and common land begins, formalizing the boundary between the settled and the wild (PN=023-064).

### The Endless Reservoir

In the novel's final chapters, the Almenning is invoked as evidence that settlement can continue indefinitely. A path is trodden "across the waste Almenning; others again, and the path becomes a road." When asked how many settlers are in the Almenning now, the answer suggests an ongoing process — the common land remains, and "ten new settlers" take up where earlier pioneers left off (PN=028-096, PN=031-032, PN=031-085).

## 主题意义

The Almenning represents the novel's deepest conviction: that the earth is abundant, that there is always more land for those willing to work it. It symbolizes the generosity of nature and the openness of the Norwegian wilderness. Unlike the enclosed, purchased, and traded lands of civilization, the Almenning belongs to no one — and therefore to everyone who will settle and cultivate it. This makes it the antithesis of the novel's commercial and industrial forces: mining companies buy and sell, but the Almenning is given.

## 关键引文
