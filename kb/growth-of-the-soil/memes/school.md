# School

Education in Growth of the Soil is a double-edged force. The Sellanraa children are sent many miles to the village school, but the novel questions what formal schooling truly gives the pioneer. Eleseus returns from school and later the city with book-learning but no capacity for manual labor, while Sivert stays closer to the land. The school is the first portal through which the outside world enters the homestead — and the first step away from the soil.

## Definition

The school in Hamsun's novel is the village institution where the children of settlers learn reading, writing, and arithmetic. It lies many miles from Sellanraa, requiring the boys to board in the village for long terms. The schoolmaster and his wife are minor figures of village society, but the school itself functions as a gravitational force pulling the pioneer children away from the homestead — offering knowledge, but at a cost the novel measures carefully.

## Schooling in the Novel

### The Long Journey

From an early age, the Sellanraa sons must travel far to learn: "They were of an age to go to school, but the school was many miles away" (009-054). Inger herself accompanies them on the journey: "the boys on their way to school, nothing less, and Inger wearing her finest" (012-057). The departure is a ceremony — a proud moment for the family, but also a severance.

> "They were of an age to go to school, but the school was many miles away" (009-054)

### Eleseus and the School

Eleseus is the family's scholar. He attends school for multiple winters: "for several winters past" the boys had gone down to the village (013-001). But his education becomes a point of departure from farm life. He speaks differently, thinks differently, and ultimately cannot remain on the land. The school has given him access to another world — and made him unfit for this one.

### A School of Their Own

Later, the growing settlement gains a schoolhouse: "A school all to yourselves, down by Brede's" (015-018). This development marks the community's maturation — from scattered homesteads to an organized settlement with institutions. It is a practical solution to the distance problem, but also a sign that the wilderness is retreating before civilization.

> "and the boys with writing and schooling; they would not be altogether ignorant" (012-010)

## The Cost of Book-Learning

Hamsun's novel does not condemn education outright, but it weighs its costs with unusual care. The school gives Eleseus skills his father lacks — he can read contracts, write letters, and navigate the world of paper and law that increasingly impinges on the settlement. But these skills come at a price: he loses the physical competence that is Isak's defining trait, the ability to work with his hands from dawn to dusk without complaint or calculation.

The novel's most pointed critique of book-learning is indirect. Eleseus, for all his schooling, is not happier than Sivert. He is not more capable of building a life. He is not more respected by the community. The school has given him mobility — the ability to leave — but not the capacity to stay. In a novel that values staying above almost everything else, this is a damning verdict.

## Sivert's Education

Sivert, by contrast, receives his education from the land itself. He learns from his father's example, from the animals, from the rhythm of the seasons. He attends school too, but the novel makes clear that his real education happens elsewhere: in the fields, at the mill, on the mountainside with the sheep. When Sivert stays at Sellanraa while Eleseus departs, it is not because he lacks intelligence — it is because he has learned a different set of truths, ones the school cannot teach and does not value.

> "A school all to yourselves, down by Brede's" (015-018)
