# Rebecca

Rebecca is the youngest child of [[isak|Isak]] and [[inger|Inger]], born after Inger's return from prison. Her naming represents a small assertion of Isak's authority within the household, and throughout Book II she appears as the family's cherished youngest daughter, often called "little Rebecca."

## Naming

When Rebecca is born, Geissler hesitates between the names Jacobine and Rebecca, both being "sort of related to Isak." He asks Inger what she thinks of Rebecca, and Isak, unusually, asserts himself:

> And when Isak heard that, he grew suddenly independent and master in his own house. "If she's to have a name at all," he said sharply, "it shall be Rebecca!" ([016-094])

Isak insists on a proper christening with a "decent following" — a sign of the family's growing prosperity and social standing.

## Childhood at Sellanraa

Rebecca is a cherished presence at the farm. Inger carries her even after she has learned to walk. When Geissler visits, he picks her up and swings her in the air, and on another occasion gives her "a shining Krone" ([024-103]).

She is put to practical use early, set to guard the turnip fields alongside Leopoldine:

> Nothing for it but to set Leopoldine and little Rebecca to keep guard over the turnip fields, and little Rebecca walks about with a big stick in her hand and is a wonder at driving cows away. ([025-021])

The family considers her "the pearl and blessing of them all" ([025-067]).

## The Church Journey

Rebecca accompanies Sivert and Jensine to church, a significant outing for a young child. She sleeps through much of the journey and is carried indoors without waking. On the way home, she is "full of the wonder of going to church, the priest in his dress with a silver cross, and the lights and the organ music" ([025-127]).

During the journey she shows diplomatic skill, taking Jensine's side in a small family tension, patting her and making her smile. She even threatens to find "a big stick to beat" her brother Sivert, making him smile too ([025-135]).

## A Letter to Eleseus

Rebecca is mentioned as the author of a letter to her brother Eleseus, written on "a big sheet of grey paper scrawled all over" ([030-062]), showing she has learned to write — another marker of the family's progress.

## Womanhood

By the novel's end, Rebecca has grown enough to join the women of Sellanraa in their daily work:

> There go the women out with their milk-pails, carried on yokes from the shoulder: Leopoldine, Jensine, and little Rebecca. ([031-087])

The epithet "little" persists even as she takes on adult responsibilities, reflecting her enduring place as the baby of the family.
