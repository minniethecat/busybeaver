# Fredrik Ström

Fredrik Ström is the brother of [[brede-olsen|Brede Olsen]], who appears in the later chapters of Book II as a settler trying to establish himself at [[breidablik|Breidablik]]. Cheerful, proud of his modest achievements, and fond of teasing the pretentious, Fredrik embodies the resilient, good-natured spirit of the younger generation of settlers.

## A New Settler at Breidablik

Fredrik arrives at Breidablik, taking over where his brother Brede left off. He is quick to distinguish himself from Brede's indifferent farming, proudly cataloguing his improvements:

> "I've had a deal of things to hinder this year; the house to do up, being leaky and like to fall to pieces; hayloft to take down and put up again, and no sort of room in the turf hut for beasts, seeing I'd cow and heifer more than Brede he'd ever had in his time," says Fredrik proudly. ([025-037])

Even Isak, "the oldest among them, the giant himself," offers him praise, and "Fredrik might well be pleased" ([025-037]).

## Teasing Aronsen

Fredrik's most distinctive trait is his cheerful, teasing relationship with [[aronsen|Aronsen]] of [[storborg|Storborg]]. Their first encounter sets the tone:

> It began this way; Fredrik wanted a roll of tobacco. "I'll give you a roll of tobacco when I have one," said Aronsen. ([025-024])

Fredrik "smiles slyly" at Aronsen and makes fun of him, mocking the trader for not cultivating his land and having to buy feed for his beasts ([025-028]).

When asked whether he regrets not taking Aronsen's offered lease terms, Fredrik answers bravely: "'Tis like enough there'll be more to come" ([025-041]) — expressing the settler's eternal optimism.

## The Expedition

In the novel's final chapter, Fredrik joins [[andresen|Andresen]] and [[sivert|Sivert]] on an expedition, described as part of "his procession" ([031-001]). At the quay, Fredrik resumes his familiar banter with Aronsen:

> The caravan has stopped at the quay, and Fredrik Ström, cheerful as ever, is poking fun at Aronsen: "I'm clean out of tobacco; got any tobacco, what?" ([031-059])

When Aronsen grows distressed, Fredrik laughs and says comfortingly: "Nay, you've no call to take it all heavy-like and sad, Aronsen" ([031-061]).

Their running joke reaches its climax when Aronsen, despairing of ever getting rid of Fredrik, finally throws him out of the store — but "Fredrik didn't come out — no, he took his time, and talked a lot more" ([031-078]). The last thing heard from outside is "Fredrik trying to sell Aronsen a lot of rocking-horses" ([031-078]) — a perfectly absurd inversion of their earlier transactions.

## Significance

Fredrik Ström appears only briefly, but he represents something important: the possibility that the next generation might do better than the last. Where Brede Olsen was a failure as a farmer, Fredrik shows genuine pride and competence. His relentless good cheer, especially in the face of Aronsen's growing despair, aligns him with the novel's deeper values — those who work the land with good humor will endure, while those who only trade and scheme will falter.
