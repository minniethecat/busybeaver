# Sowing

Sowing is the act of planting seed in the spring earth — the foundational labor of the agricultural year in Growth of the Soil. More than any other farm task, sowing is portrayed as an act of faith: the sower entrusts seed to the ground, trusting that the land will return it multiplied.

## 定义

In the novel, sowing follows plowing in the spring cycle. Isak walks his fields bareheaded, scattering grain by hand — an image that Hamsun invests with almost religious solemnity. The sower does not see the harvest; he acts on trust. Sowing is the human contribution to a process whose completion depends on sun, rain, and soil — forces beyond human control (010-064):

> Isak ploughs one day, and a few days later he is sowing corn, planting potatoes.

## 在小说中的表现

### The Bareheaded Sower

The novel's most iconic image of sowing comes late in the book, when Isak — now an older man, a "stump of a man, a barge of a man to look at" — walks his fields bareheaded, sowing (031-082):

> Sowing — and he walks religiously bareheaded to that work; his head is bald just at the very top, but all the rest of him shamefully hairy; a fan, a wheel of hair and beard, stands out from his face.

The detail that Isak goes bareheaded only when sowing — treating it as a sacred act — distinguishes sowing from all other farm labor. It is the moment when the pioneer most directly collaborates with the soil.

### Spring Rhythm

Sowing marks the beginning of the active agricultural year. After the long winter, the sight of Isak walking his fields with seed is the novel's signal that the cycle has begun again. The repetition of this image across chapters — Isak sowing in spring, year after year — is one of the novel's key structural devices, anchoring time in the recurrence of agricultural labor (031-079, 031-086).

## 主题意义

Sowing embodies the theme of faith in the land. Unlike mowing (immediate, visible results) or harvest (culmination), sowing is an investment in an uncertain future. The sower gives without knowing what he will receive. This makes sowing the most morally significant of the farming tasks in Hamsun's vision: it is the act that most fully expresses the pioneer's trust in the relationship between human labor and the soil's response.
