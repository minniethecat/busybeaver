# Religion

Religion in Growth of the Soil is a quiet, almost invisible presence — not the doctrinal Christianity of sermons and catechisms but the ambient faith of people whose lives are already shaped by forces older than the church: the seasons, the soil, birth and death. Hamsun treats religion as part of the pioneer's cultural inheritance rather than a living moral force.

## 定义

The novel's characters are nominally Christian — they marry in church, baptize their children, and observe Christmas — but God is rarely invoked, and religious language is conspicuously absent from the narrative voice. The novel's true religion is the religion of the soil: the conviction that working the land, caring for animals, and building a home constitute a moral order more fundamental than any theological system.

## 在小说中的表现

### The Church as Social Institution

The church appears primarily as a social and administrative institution — a place for baptisms, weddings, and community gatherings rather than for spiritual transformation. When Isak and Inger marry or when their children are baptized, the religious ceremony is presented as a civic formality, not a sacred event. The church authenticates social facts (marriage, birth) but does not generate religious experience.

### Inger's Hare-Lip and Divine Judgment

The novel's most explicit engagement with religious interpretation comes through reactions to Inger's hare-lip and the death of her infant. Some characters interpret these events as divine punishment or signs — but the narrative voice never endorses such readings. Instead, the novel treats the hare-lip as a natural fact and the infant's death as a human tragedy, leaving theological interpretations to the characters who propose them.

### Oline's Piety

Oline is the novel's most ostentatiously religious character, frequently invoking God and moral judgment. But her piety is consistently presented as hollow — a performance that masks her gossiping, meddling nature. Oline's religion serves the novel's broader critique: formal piety, divorced from the honest labor that constitutes real morality, is worse than no religion at all.

### The Real Sacred

What is sacred in Growth of the Soil is not in church but in the homestead: the first calf born at Sellanraa, the first harvest, the land itself. When the novel describes Goldenhorns calving as "a great day in the wilderness, a joy and a delight" (003-016), it uses language that would be appropriate for a religious festival — but the occasion is agricultural, not theological. The novel has simply transferred the sacred from church to soil.

## 主题意义

Hamsun's treatment of religion reflects his broader suspicion of abstraction. Formal religion, like formal education, is an imported system that claims authority over aspects of human life that are better understood through direct experience. The pioneer does not need a priest to tell him that birth is sacred or that death is solemn — he knows these things because he lives them. The novel does not explicitly reject Christianity, but it renders it superfluous by relocating the sacred into the everyday work of pioneer life.
