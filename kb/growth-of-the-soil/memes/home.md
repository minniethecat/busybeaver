# Home

In Growth of the Soil, home is not a place you buy or inherit — it is a place you build with your own hands, season by season, until the walls absorb your labor and the soil remembers your footsteps. Hamsun's novel is one of literature's great meditations on what it means to make a home.

## 定义

Home for Isak is not a sentimental concept but a physical project. It begins with a turf hut — crude, temporary, barely more than a burrow. Over the years, it becomes a log cabin, then a house with multiple rooms, a barn, outbuildings, and fenced pastures. Each improvement is a layer of labor deposited in the land. The home grows as the soil grows; the two are inseparable. Isak does not "own" Sellanraa in the legal sense until much later — but from the moment he builds the first hut, it is his home because his labor is in every beam and stone.

## 在小说中的表现

### From Hut to Homestead

The physical evolution of Isak's dwelling mirrors the novel's arc. Chapter I: a turf hut. Chapter II: a proper log cabin. Later: a house with a real window, bought from the village store. Still later: additions for the growing family, a separate building for Eleseus's failed store, barns, sheds. The homestead grows organically, like the crops, in response to the family's changing needs.

### Inger's Touch

While Isak builds the structure, Inger makes it a home. Her cheese-making, her care of the children, her attention to domestic details — these transform the cabin from a shelter into a household. After her return from Bergen, she brings new domestic knowledge: better sewing, more organized storage, a sense of order learned in the city. The home absorbs her city experience without being overcome by it (015-XXX).

### Contrasts: Breidablik and Storborg

The novel offers counter-examples of failed homes. Brede Olsen's Breidablik is a place of decline — the land is never properly cleared, the animals are neglected, the family slowly disperses. Eleseus's Storborg is an even starker failure: a store with no customers, a building with no life in it, the empty shell of a home that was never really inhabited. Both stand as warnings: a home cannot be merely designated; it must be made, daily, through labor.

### Leaving Home

When Eleseus leaves Sellanraa for the city, he is leaving more than a house — he is leaving the accumulated meaning of his parents' lives. The novel treats this departure not as ambition or growth but as loss: he will never again be fully at home anywhere, because home for Hamsun is singularity, not a series of addresses (029-XXX). Sivert, who stays, inherits not just the farm but the entire project of making a home in the wilderness — the novel's ultimate blessing.

## 主题意义

Home is the novel's answer to the question that haunts modernity: where can a person belong? The answer, according to Growth of the Soil, is not "anywhere" but "where you have labored." Home is the product of invested time and effort — it cannot be inherited, purchased, or found. This is why Isak, who never reads a book or travels beyond the nearest village, is the novel's hero: he has made a home so thoroughly that the homestead and the man have become indistinguishable.
