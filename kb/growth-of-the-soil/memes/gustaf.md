# Gustaf

Gustaf is a young Swedish miner who comes to the district during the copper mining operations. He is described as "the wildest of them all" and is known for his mouth-organ, his reckless charm, and his way with children and women alike.

## The Wild Miner

Gustaf is the liveliest of the miners — a madcap with his hat over one ear and his lips ever ready with a merry word. He befriends [[aronsen|Aronsen]]'s children, playing music for them on his mouth-organ and carrying all three of them on his back while dancing (023-042):

> Young Gustaf came down, the wildest of them all, with his hat over one ear, and his lips ever ready with a merry word; ay, Gustaf it was that came and played with them for long at a time. (PN=023-042)

The narrator contrasts the copper mine's serious operations with Gustaf's playful spirit: instead of serious mining songs, the men play mouth-organs — and Gustaf is the one who brings this lighter energy to the worksite (PN=023-042).

He lets off rockets one evening at Sellanraa, and the wild fire of them reminds [[inger]] of the great world she had once seen (PN=023-046). When Gustaf finishes by playing his mouth-organ, the narrator notes that Inger "would have gone off along the road with him for sheer emotion."

## Among the Miners

Gustaf is a Swede himself, and he serves as a bridge between the Swedish mining crew and the local Norwegian settlers. When Geissler questions the miners about their work, it is Gustaf who answers directly (PN=023-044). His bilingual and bicultural position reflects the cross-border nature of the mining boom — the copper discovery draws labour, capital, and cultural energy from Sweden into the remote Norwegian district.

## Relationship with Inger

Gustaf develops a flirtation with Inger during the mining period. He comes up to Sellanraa, and Inger, though no longer young, is drawn to his vitality. The other miners warn him to be careful — Inger is a married woman, and Isak is not a man to be trifled with (PN=023-056):

> And the other men put in that Gustaf had better be careful not to play the fool with the wife there at Sellanraa. (PN=023-056)

But Gustaf is undeterred. He manages to get her gold ring on his little finger — quietly, without pressing himself forward. Their mutual attraction represents a moment of temptation and the lingering pull of the outside world on Inger's settled life (PN=023-057):

> But Gustaf was not going to be careful; he said a great deal more, and as for Inger, 'twas strange how he managed to win her for himself, for all that he never seemed to put himself forward that way. (PN=023-057)

The episode culminates with Gustaf bringing out the ring when pressed, and Inger silently assenting. The narrator captures the intensity of the moment: "she would not turn back as long as Gustaf was there; would not leave her place" (PN=023-058):

> And at that Gustaf had to bring it out; he was owning her gold ring. (PN=023-062)

### A Walk in the Woods

The relationship developed further in the woods beyond Sellanraa. The landscape provided "many sheltered spots" where the two could be alone (PN=024-010):

> ... the bushes, and Inger says: "Gustaf, you're mad to do it." And ho... (PN=024-009)

> ... by many sheltered spots, and Gustaf sees them, and Inger, she see... (PN=024-010)

The episode is narrated with an elliptical delicacy — Hamsun suggests far more than he states, leaving the reader to infer the nature of their encounters from the charged silences between passages.

## Work at Sellanraa

Gustaf offers to help with the building work at Sellanraa, though he is no hand at woodwork:

> "Well, Gustaf, and when are you coming to help me with the building?" Isak asks. "I might manage it, but can't be sure," Gustaf laughs in reply. (PN=023-053, 023-055)

He comes down from the mine and assists with construction, but his skills as a carpenter are limited. The work was to be "finished as soon as possible, but Gustaf is no hand at woodwork" (PN=025-003). This scene encapsulates Gustaf's role in the novel: he is energetic and willing, but his talents lie in charm and performance rather than the steady, earthbound labour that defines Isak.

Yet Gustaf is never at a loss. "But Gustaf is never at a loss, he has hi..." (PN=025-007) — he finds ways to contribute even when his skills fall short, and his presence lightens the atmosphere of the farm.

## Jealousy and Departure

As spring arrived, the dynamic began to shift. [[barbro]], observing the situation, tried to make Gustaf jealous — but Gustaf did not rise to the bait (PN=025-009):

> ...n't think much of him, anyway," says Gustaf. He does not seem jealous in ... (PN=025-009)

The exchange suggests that Gustaf's interest in Inger was genuine or at least good-natured — he was not playing a game of conquest to be won, and jealousy was not part of his temperament.

When the mining operations wound down, Gustaf departed with the others. He could not stay one more night (PN=025-011). The narrator's ambiguous assessment — "maybe Gustaf was getting tired of the game" (PN=025-012) — leaves open the question of whether his feelings for Inger had been genuine or merely a diversion. Either way, his departure was as sudden and complete as his arrival.

## Character Significance

Gustaf embodies the transient, destabilising energy of the mining boom: he arrives with the copper rush, disrupts the quiet rhythms of Sellanraa, and vanishes when the ore runs out. As a character, he is one of Hamsun's most vivid minor creations — a figure of reckless charm who illuminates, by contrast, the rooted stability of Isak.

His brief presence leaves a lasting impression on Inger — a reminder of the wider world she once knew during her prison years, and of the desires that farm life cannot fully satisfy. In a novel devoted to the virtues of the soil, Gustaf represents everything the soil is not: mobility, performance, transience, and the ambiguous freedoms of rootlessness.
