# Government

The Norwegian state's distant but decisive presence in the lives of the novel's pioneers, represented by the Lensmand, the courts, land laws, and the expanding apparatus of rural administration. Government in Growth of the Soil is both protector and intruder — it enforces justice but also imposes alien values on the wilderness community.

## 定义

Government in the novel is not the abstract state of political theory but a concrete set of encounters: Geissler enforcing the law as Lensmand, the courts judging Inger for infanticide, the telegraph line extending state infrastructure into Sellanraa's valley, and the mining officials who want to extract wealth from Isak's land. Each encounter reveals the paradox of government on the frontier: it is necessary for order but foreign to the values of those it governs.

## 在小说中的表现

### Geissler as the State's Face

Geissler embodies the ambiguity of government. As Lensmand, he is the state's representative in the district, enforcing laws and collecting fees. But he uses his position to help Isak — not from altruism but because he identifies with the pioneer against the distant bureaucracy he nominally serves. Geissler's maneuvering in the copper mine affair (017-XXX) reveals a man who knows the law well enough to bend it (017-065).

### The Telegraph Line

The construction of the telegraph line through Sellanraa's wilderness represents the state's physical encroachment on pioneer territory. Isak is paid for right-of-way through his land — a transaction that brings money but also an irreversible connection to the outside world. The line is a thin wire of government control stretching across the moors, and the novel treats its arrival with deep ambivalence (024-XXX, 025-XXX).

### Inger's Trial

The court's sentencing of Inger for killing her harelipped infant is the most brutal state intervention in the novel. From the state's perspective, it is justice: a crime was committed, a punishment was imposed. From the wilderness perspective, it is incomprehensible cruelty — punishing a mother for an act of mercy toward a child who would have suffered. The trial exposes the gulf between legal justice and human reality, a gulf that the novel never resolves (013-XXX, 014-XXX).

### Land and Property Law

Government also appears in the form of land registration, property boundaries, and public land (almenning) regulations. When Isak first settles, the land is unregistered — he is a squatter in legal terms. Later, as the state extends its administrative reach, questions of title and ownership arise. The novel suggests that government recognition of property is both a protection (securing Isak's claim) and a burden (tying him to the state's system of fees and obligations).

## 深层分析

### The Frontier State Paradox

Hamsun presents a paradox at the heart of frontier governance: the state is simultaneously the pioneer's greatest threat and his most essential ally. Without the state, there is no legal recognition of land claims — Isak's decades of labor could be overturned by a competing claim. Without the state, there is no enforcement of contracts — the copper mine dealings would descend into pure force. Yet each act of state intervention also diminishes the pioneer's autonomy, binding him to rules made in distant cities by men who have never cleared a field or weathered a northern winter.

This paradox is embodied in Geissler, a state official who consistently acts against state interests in favor of the pioneer. Geissler's personal corruption — his drinking, his financial failures, his irregular appearances — is presented not as a flaw but as a kind of liberation from bureaucratic rationality. A proper Lensmand would enforce the law impersonally; Geissler enforces it personally, which makes him useful to Isak but also reveals the state's inability to comprehend the moral economy of the frontier.

### Government as Incursion

The novel structures government encounters as incursions — each one bringing the outside world deeper into the valley. The sequence is cumulative: first Geissler as a lone official, then the telegraph line as physical infrastructure, then the mining company as commercial interest, and finally the expanding settlement that makes Sellanraa no longer a frontier but a community requiring regular administration. Hamsun tracks this progression with the attention of an ecologist watching an invasive species colonize a habitat: the first arrivals seem harmless, even beneficial, but their cumulative effect transforms the entire ecosystem.

The telegraph line is the novel's master symbol for this process. It is the thinnest possible connection to the outside world — a single wire — yet it carries with it the entire weight of state authority, commercial news, and social gossip. Once the line is strung, the wilderness is no longer truly wild; it has been threaded into the national network, and every message that passes along the wire reminds the pioneers that they are now subjects, citizens, and customers.

### The Norwegian Context

Hamsun wrote Growth of the Soil during a period of rapid state-building in Norway. The country had gained full independence from Sweden only in 1905, and the early twentieth century saw an expansion of government services into rural areas — schools, roads, telegraphs, courts — that had previously known minimal state presence. The novel can be read as a response to this transformation: not a rejection of the state as such, but a warning about the cultural price of incorporation. The pioneers who settled the Almenning represented a kind of pre-state existence — self-governing, self-sufficient, answering to no authority but the land itself. The arrival of government marked the end of that existence and the beginning of something more orderly but also smaller in spirit.

## 主题意义

Government in Growth of the Soil represents the price of civilization. As the pioneer community grows, it necessarily attracts the state's attention — laws must be enforced, land must be registered, taxes must be collected. The novel does not deny the necessity of government but mourns what is lost in its arrival: the simpler world where a man could walk into the wilderness and make a life without asking anyone's permission.
