# Charles II

**Charles II** (1630–1685) was King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from the Restoration of the monarchy in 1660 until his death. His passing provides the political catalyst for the events that ensnare [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]], and his reign furnishes a precedent for how the Crown might handle troublesome buccaneers.

## In the Novel

### The Succession Crisis

The novel opens in the immediate aftermath of Charles II's death. The proclamation posted at Bridgewater — which Peter Blood reads with sardonic amusement — sets forth the claim that upon the King's decease, the right of succession "did legally descend and devolve upon the most illustrious and high-born Prince James, Duke of Monmouth, son and heir apparent to the said King Charles the Second" [001-084, 001-087, 001-088].

Blood, ever the skeptic, finds the proclamation risible. The claim that Monmouth was Charles II's legitimate son and heir was specious at best — the King had acknowledged Monmouth as his natural son but never as his lawful successor. The proclamation's further allegation that "James Duke of York did first cause the said late King to be poysoned" [001-090] only deepens Blood's contempt for the enterprise.

Sabatini captures the political dynamics of 1685 with economy: Charles II's death without a legitimate Protestant heir created the vacuum that Monmouth attempted to fill, and the brutal suppression of that attempt — orchestrated by Charles's actual successor, [[james-ii|James II]] — is the machinery that ultimately transports Blood to the Caribbean.

### The Morgan Precedent

Charles II's reign provides a crucial political precedent later in the novel. When Lord Sunderland and the King's ministers consider how to deal with Captain Blood's depredations, they recall the case of [[morgan|Henry Morgan]], the notorious buccaneer who had been "enlisted into the King's service under Charles II" [018-279]. It occurs to them that a similar course — offering Blood a royal commission and pardon — might be similarly effective [018-279, 018-280].

> He turned to the consideration of extraordinary ones, and bethought him of the plan adopted with Morgan, who had been enlisted into the King's service under Charles II. It occurred to him that a similar course might be similarly effective with Captain Blood. [018-277, 018-279, 018-280]

This historical parallel — Charles II's pragmatic co-option of a dangerous buccaneer — provides the template for the novel's resolution, in which Blood is ultimately offered the King's commission and the governorship of Jamaica.

## Historical Basis

Charles II (1630–1685) was the eldest surviving son of Charles I, restored to the throne in 1660 after the collapse of the Cromwellian Protectorate. His reign saw the Great Plague (1665), the Great Fire of London (1666), and ongoing religious and political tensions between Crown and Parliament. He fathered numerous illegitimate children — including James Scott, Duke of Monmouth — but no legitimate heir, leaving the throne to his Catholic brother James, Duke of York. The historical Henry Morgan was indeed knighted and appointed Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica under Charles II, lending authenticity to the novel's resolution.
