# Don Esteban

## Overview

Don Esteban de Espinosa is the son of the Spanish Admiral Don Miguel de Espinosa. He accompanies his father aboard the *Cinco Llagas* when it is captured by Peter Blood. His subsequent captivity and release provide one of the novel's most intricate diplomatic exchanges — a scene that reveals Blood's capacity for gentlemanly courtesy even as he presses his tactical advantage.

## In the Novel

Don Esteban is introduced as accompanying his father, the Spanish Admiral, when Blood's buccaneers capture the *Cinco Llagas* ([009-218]). When the capture is "satisfactorily accomplished," Don Esteban and his companions are taken prisoner ([009-237]).

The most detailed portrait of Don Esteban comes during his parley with Blood in the stern sheets of the captured ship. Described as sitting opposite "Captain Peter Blood," Don Esteban "moistened his parched lips" as the negotiations commence ([012-130], [012-156]). He expresses his "last lingering hope" of favourable treatment, appealing to Blood's sense of honour ([012-167]).

Blood's handling of Don Esteban is masterfully diplomatic. When Don Esteban's father, the Admiral, is brought before Blood, the captain pointedly refers to his own prior "captivity by Don Esteban's most gallant father" — a reminder that the Spanish Admiral once held Blood prisoner ([012-186]). Don Esteban follows Blood closely during this exchange, and "for a moment Don Esteban's face was a mask of pallid fury" as the implications become clear ([012-210]). Blood sweeps aside his protest by noting that "was not Don Esteban there to confirm him?" — using the young Spaniard's own presence as witness to the Admiral's earlier conduct ([012-227]).

Uneasy with the direction of the negotiations, Don Esteban grows restless "on that account" ([012-260]). His frustration boils over when he "struck Hagthorpe across the face" — a rash act that reflects his aristocratic temperament and his sense of wounded pride ([012-309]). Blood responds with cold composure, a contrast that underscores his superior self-command. At daybreak, "Don Esteban and his followers were put ashore" — released rather than killed, a decision consistent with Blood's code of honour ([012-370]).
