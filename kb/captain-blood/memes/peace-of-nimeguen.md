# Peace of Nimeguen

The **Peace of Nimeguen** (also known as the Treaties of Nijmegen) was a series of peace treaties signed in 1678 and 1679 that ended the Franco-Dutch War (1672–1678). The treaties were negotiated in the Dutch city of Nijmegen and involved France, the Dutch Republic, Spain, the Holy Roman Empire, and other European powers. The peace serves as a crucial chronological waypoint in the biography of Peter Blood.[^1]

## Significance in the Narrative

In the biography of Peter Blood, the Peace of Nimeguen serves as a pivot point between Blood's European naval career and his subsequent adventures. The narrative uses the peace as a temporal anchor:

> "After the Peace of Nimeguen his movements are obscure."[^2]

This single sentence marks the transition in Blood's documented history. Before Nimeguen, Blood served under the famous Dutch admiral de Ruyter in the Mediterranean; after Nimeguen, the record becomes fragmented, and Blood's eventual path toward England, the Monmouth Rebellion, and transportation to Barbados begins.[^2]

## Blood's Service Under De Ruyter

Blood's naval credentials were established through service under Michiel de Ruyter, the most celebrated Dutch admiral of the seventeenth century. The narrative records that Blood "had served under de Ruyter" and "fought in the Mediterranean" — a detail that would later prove crucial when Blood's naval expertise was recognized by his Spanish captors and buccaneer allies alike.[^3]

> "That he had fought under de Ruyter" became a credential that even Blood's enemies acknowledged, lending his buccaneer career an aura of professional legitimacy unusual among the Brethren of the Coast.[^4]

The Peace of Nimeguen ended the conflict in which de Ruyter had been engaged, and with it, Blood's formal naval service.[^3]

## Impact on Caribbean Buccaneering

The peace reshaped European naval power in ways that directly affected the Caribbean. With the end of the Franco-Dutch War, many experienced naval officers and sailors found themselves without employment. This demobilization contributed a steady supply of skilled maritime labour to the privateer and buccaneer fleets operating in the West Indies — men who, like Blood, had been trained in European naval warfare and now sought fortune in the Spanish Main.[^5]

## Connection to Peter Blood

The timing of the Peace of Nimeguen is significant: it falls between Blood's Mediterranean service and his appearance in England as a physician. The "obscure" period that follows the peace encompasses Blood's transition from naval officer to settled doctor — a transition that would be violently disrupted by the Monmouth Rebellion and his subsequent transportation to Barbados.[^6]

Blood's service under de Ruyter gave him a level of naval competence far exceeding that of most buccaneer captains. This professional foundation, acquired in the years before the Peace of Nimeguen, explains Blood's tactical superiority over both his Spanish enemies and his French rivals throughout his Caribbean career.[^6]
