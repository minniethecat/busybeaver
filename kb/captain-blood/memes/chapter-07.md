[007-001] Finally he determined to go up to [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]]'s [[slavery|plantation]]. Probably

[007-002] Blood would be there. If he were not, Nuttall would find [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]], and leave

[007-003] a message with him. He was acquainted with Pitt and knew of Pitt's share

[007-004] in this business. His pretext for seeking Blood must still be that he

[007-005] needed medical assistance.

[007-006] And at the same time that he set out, insensitive in his anxiety to the

[007-007] broiling heat, to climb the heights to the north of the town, Blood

[007-008] was setting out from Government House at last, having so far eased the

[007-009] Governor's condition as to be permitted to depart. Being mounted, he

[007-010] would, but for an unexpected delay, have reached the stockade ahead of

[007-011] Nuttall, in which case several unhappy events might have been averted.

[007-012] The unexpected delay was occasioned by Miss [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]].

[007-013] They met at the gate of the luxuriant garden of Government House, and

[007-014] Miss Bishop, herself mounted, stared to see [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] on horseback.

[007-015] It happened that he was in good spirits. The fact that the Governor's

[007-016] condition had so far improved as to restore him his freedom of movement

[007-017] had sufficed to remove the depression under which he had been labouring

[007-018] for the past twelve hours and more. In its rebound the mercury of his

[007-019] mood had shot higher far than present circumstances warranted. He was

[007-020] disposed to be optimistic. What had failed last night would certainly

[007-021] not fail again to-night. What was a day, after all? The Secretary's

[007-022] office might be troublesome, but not really troublesome for another

[007-023] twenty-four hours at least; and by then they would be well away.

[007-024] This joyous confidence of his was his first misfortune. The next was

[007-025] that his good spirits were also shared by Miss Bishop, and that she

[007-026] bore no rancour. The two things conjoined to make the delay that in its

[007-027] consequences was so deplorable.

[007-028] “Good-morning, sir,” she hailed him pleasantly. “It's close upon a month

[007-029] since last I saw you.”

[007-030] “Twenty-one days to the hour,” said he. “I've counted them.”

[007-031] “I vow I was beginning to believe you dead.”

[007-032] “I have to thank you for the wreath.”

[007-033] “The wreath?”

[007-034] “To deck my grave,” he explained.

[007-035] “Must you ever be rallying?” she wondered, and looked at him gravely,

[007-036] remembering that it was his rallying on the last occasion had driven her

[007-037] away in dudgeon.

[007-038] “A man must sometimes laugh at himself or go mad,” said he. “Few realize

[007-039] it. That is why there are so many madmen in the world.”

[007-040] “You may laugh at yourself all you will, sir. But sometimes I think you

[007-041] laugh at me, which is not civil.”

[007-042] “Then, faith, you're wrong. I laugh only at the comic, and you are not

[007-043] comic at all.”

[007-044] “What am I, then?” she asked him, laughing.

[007-045] A moment he pondered her, so fair and fresh to behold, so entirely

[007-046] maidenly and yet so entirely frank and unabashed.

[007-047] “You are,” he said, “the niece of the man who owns me his slave.” But he

[007-048] spoke lightly. So lightly that she was encouraged to insistence.

[007-049] “Nay, sir, that is an evasion. You shall answer me truthfully this

[007-050] morning.”

[007-051] “Truthfully? To answer you at all is a labour. But to answer truthfully!

[007-052] Oh, well, now, I should say of you that he'll be lucky who counts you

[007-053] his friend.” It was in his mind to add more. But he left it there.

[007-054] “That's mighty civil,” said she. “You've a nice taste in compliments,

[007-055] Mr. Blood. Another in your place....”

[007-056] “Faith, now, don't I know what another would have said? Don't I know my

[007-057] fellow-man at all?”

[007-058] “Sometimes I think you do, and sometimes I think you don't. Anyway, you

[007-059] don't know your fellow-woman. There was that affair of the Spaniards.”

[007-060] “Will ye never forget it?”

[007-061] “Never.”

[007-062] “Bad cess to your memory. Is there no good in me at all that you could

[007-063] be dwelling on instead?”

[007-064] “Oh, several things.”

[007-065] “For instance, now?” He was almost eager.

[007-066] “You speak excellent Spanish.”

[007-067] “Is that all?” He sank back into dismay.

[007-068] “Where did you learn it? Have you been in Spain?”

[007-069] “That I have. I was two years in a Spanish prison.”

[007-070] “In prison?” Her tone suggested apprehensions in which he had no desire

[007-071] to leave her.

[007-072] “As a prisoner of war,” he explained. “I was taken fighting with the

[007-073] French--in French service, that is.”

[007-074] “But you're a doctor!” she cried.

[007-075] “That's merely a diversion, I think. By trade I am a soldier--at least,

[007-076] it's a trade I followed for ten years. It brought me no great gear,

[007-077] but it served me better than medicine, which, as you may observe, has

[007-078] brought me into slavery. I'm thinking it's more pleasing in the sight of

[007-079] Heaven to kill men than to heal them. Sure it must be.”

[007-080] “But how came you to be a soldier, and to serve the French?”

[007-081] “I am Irish, you see, and I studied medicine. Therefore--since it's a

[007-082] perverse nation we are--.... Oh, but it's a long story, and the Colonel

[007-083] will be expecting my return.” She was not in that way to be defrauded

[007-084] of her entertainment. If he would wait a moment they would ride back

[007-085] together. She had but come to enquire of the Governor's health at her

[007-086] uncle's request.

[007-087] So he waited, and so they rode back together to Colonel Bishop's house.

[007-088] They rode very slowly, at a walking pace, and some whom they passed

[007-089] marvelled to see the doctor-slave on such apparently intimate terms with

[007-090] his owner's niece. One or two may have promised themselves that they

[007-091] would drop a hint to the Colonel. But the two rode oblivious of all

[007-092] others in the world that morning. He was telling her the story of his

[007-093] early turbulent days, and at the end of it he dwelt more fully than

[007-094] hitherto upon the manner of his arrest and trial.

[007-095] The tale was barely done when they drew up at the Colonel's door, and

[007-096] dismounted, Peter Blood surrendering his nag to one of the negro grooms,

[007-097] who informed them that the Colonel was from home at the moment.

[007-098] Even then they lingered a moment, she detaining him.

[007-099] “I am sorry, Mr. Blood, that I did not know before,” she said, and there

[007-100] was a suspicion of moisture in those clear hazel eyes. With a compelling

[007-101] friendliness she held out her hand to him.

[007-102] “Why, what difference could it have made?” he asked.

[007-103] “Some, I think. You have been very hardly used by Fate.”

[007-104] “Och, now....” He paused. His keen sapphire eyes considered her steadily

[007-105] a moment from under his level black brows. “It might have been worse,”

[007-106] he said, with a significance which brought a tinge of colour to her

[007-107] cheeks and a flutter to her eyelids.

[007-108] He stooped to kiss her hand before releasing it, and she did not deny

[007-109] him. Then he turned and strode off towards the stockade a half-mile

[007-110] away, and a vision of her face went with him, tinted with a rising blush

[007-111] and a sudden unusual shyness. He forgot in that little moment that he

[007-112] was a rebel-convict with ten years of slavery before him; he forgot

[007-113] that he had planned an escape, which was to be carried into effect

[007-114] that night; forgot even the peril of discovery which as a result of the

[007-115] Governor's gout now overhung him.

[007-117] Mr. James Nuttall made all speed, regardless of the heat, in his journey

[007-118] from Bridgetown to Colonel Bishop's plantation, and if ever man was

[007-119] built for speed in a hot climate that man was Mr. James Nuttall, with

[007-120] his short, thin body, and his long, fleshless legs. So withered was

[007-121] he that it was hard to believe there were any juices left in him, yet

[007-122] juices there must have been, for he was sweating violently by the time

[007-123] he reached the stockade.

[007-124] At the entrance he almost ran into the overseer Kent, a squat,

[007-125] bow-legged animal with the arms of a Hercules and the jowl of a bulldog.

[007-126] “I am seeking Doctor Blood,” he announced breathlessly.

[007-127] “You are in a rare haste,” growled Kent. “What the devil is it? Twins?”

[007-128] “Eh? Oh! Nay, nay. I'm not married, sir. It's a cousin of mine, sir.”

[007-129] “What is?”

[007-130] “He is taken bad, sir,” Nuttall lied promptly upon the cue that Kent

[007-131] himself had afforded him. “Is the doctor here?”

[007-132] “That's his hut yonder.” Kent pointed carelessly. “If he's not there,

[007-133] he'll be somewhere else.” And he took himself off. He was a surly,

[007-134] ungracious beast at all times, readier with the lash of his whip than

[007-135] with his tongue.

[007-137] that he took. Then he plunged into the enclosure, to verify in

[007-138] mortification that Dr. Blood was not at home. A man of sense might have

[007-139] sat down and waited, judging that to be the quickest and surest way in

[007-140] the end. But Nuttall had no sense. He flung out of the stockade again,

[007-154] hut in the stockade.
