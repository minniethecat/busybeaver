[015-001] Then it was the turn of [[levasseur|Levasseur]], and black grew the brow of Captain

[015-002] Blood as the Frenchman's tale was unfolded. At the end he roundly

[015-003] expressed his disapproval. The Dutch were a friendly people whom it was

[015-004] a folly to alienate, particularly for so paltry a matter as these hides

[015-005] and tobacco, which at most would fetch a bare twenty thousand pieces.

[015-006] But Levasseur answered him, as he had answered Cahusac, that a ship was

[015-007] a ship, and it was ships they needed against their projected enterprise.

[015-008] Perhaps because things had gone well with him that day, Blood ended

[015-009] by shrugging the matter aside. Thereupon Levasseur proposed that the

[015-010] [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] and her prize should return to [[tortuga|Tortuga]] there to unload the

[015-011] cacao and enlist the further adventurers that could now be shipped.

[015-012] Levasseur meanwhile would effect certain necessary repairs, and

[015-013] then proceeding south, await his admiral at Saltatudos, an island

[015-014] conveniently situated--in the latitude of 11 deg. 11' N.--for their

[015-015] enterprise against Maracaybo.

[015-016] To Levasseur's relief, [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] not only agreed, but pronounced

[015-017] himself ready to set sail at once.

[015-018] No sooner had [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]] departed than Levasseur brought his ships

[015-019] into the lagoon, and set his crew to work upon the erection of temporary

[015-020] quarters ashore for himself, his men, and his enforced guests during the

[015-021] careening and repairing of La Foudre.

[015-022] At sunset that evening the wind freshened; it grew to a gale, and from

[015-023] that to such a hurricane that Levasseur was thankful to find himself

[015-024] ashore and his ships in safe shelter. He wondered a little how it might

[015-025] be faring with Captain Blood out there at the mercy of that terrific

[015-026] storm; but he did not permit concern to trouble him unduly.

[015-027] CHAPTER XV. THE RANSOM

[015-028] In the glory of the following morning, sparkling and clear after the

[015-029] storm, with an invigorating, briny tang in the air from the salt-ponds

[015-030] on the south of the island, a curious scene was played on the beach of

[015-031] the Virgen Magra, at the foot of a ridge of bleached dunes, beside the

[015-032] spread of sail from which Levasseur had improvised a tent.

[015-033] Enthroned upon an empty cask sat the French filibuster to transact

[015-034] important business: the business of making himself safe with the

[015-035] Governor of Tortuga.

[015-036] A guard of honour of a half-dozen officers hung about him; five of them

[015-037] were rude boucan-hunters, in stained jerkins and leather breeches; the

[015-038] sixth was Cahusac. Before him, guarded by two half-naked negroes, stood

[015-039] young d'Ogeron, in frilled shirt and satin small-clothes and fine shoes

[015-040] of Cordovan leather. He was stripped of doublet, and his hands were tied

[015-041] behind him. The young gentleman's comely face was haggard. Near at

[015-042] hand, and also under guard, but unpinioned, mademoiselle his sister sat

[015-043] hunched upon a hillock of sand. She was very pale, and it was in vain

[015-044] that she sought to veil in a mask of arrogance the fears by which she

[015-045] was assailed.

[015-046] Levasseur addressed himself to M. d'Ogeron. He spoke at long length. In

[015-047] the end--

[015-048] “I trust, monsieur,” said he, with mock suavity, “that I have made

[015-049] myself quite clear. So that there may be no misunderstandings, I will

[015-050] recapitulate. Your ransom is fixed at twenty thousand [[ransom|pieces of eight]],

[015-051] and you shall have liberty on parole to go to Tortuga to collect it.

[015-052] In fact, I shall provide the means to convey you thither, and you shall

[015-053] have a month in which to come and go. Meanwhile, your sister remains

[015-054] with me as a hostage. Your father should not consider such a sum

[015-055] excessive as the price of his son's liberty and to provide a dowry for

[015-056] his daughter. Indeed, if anything, I am too modest, pardi! M. d'Ogeron

[015-057] is reputed a wealthy man.”

[015-058] M. d'Ogeron the younger raised his head and looked the Captain boldly in

[015-059] the face.

[015-060] “I refuse--utterly and absolutely, do you understand? So do your worst,

[015-061] and be damned for a filthy pirate without decency and without honour.”

[015-062] “But what words!” laughed Levasseur. “What heat and what foolishness!

[015-063] You have not considered the alternative. When you do, you will not

[015-064] persist in your refusal. You will not do that in any case. We have spurs

[015-065] for the reluctant. And I warn you against giving me your parole under

[015-066] stress, and afterwards playing me false. I shall know how to find and

[015-067] punish you. Meanwhile, remember your sister's honour is in pawn to me.

[015-068] Should you forget to return with the dowry, you will not consider it

[015-069] unreasonable that I forget to marry her.”

[015-070] Levasseur's smiling eyes, intent upon the young man's face, saw the

[015-071] horror that crept into his glance. M. d'Ogeron cast a wild glance at

[015-072] mademoiselle, and observed the grey despair that had almost stamped the

[015-073] beauty from her face. Disgust and fury swept across his countenance.

[015-074] Then he braced himself and answered resolutely:

[015-075] “No, you dog! A thousand times, no!”

[015-076] “You are foolish to persist.” Levasseur spoke without anger, with a

[015-077] coldly mocking regret. His fingers had been busy tying knots in a length

[015-078] of whipcord. He held it up. “You know this? It is a rosary of pain that

[015-079] has wrought the conversion of many a stubborn heretic. It is capable

[015-080] of screwing the eyes out of a man's head by way of helping him to see

[015-081] reason. As you please.”

[015-082] He flung the length of knotted cord to one of the negroes, who in an

[015-083] instant made it fast about the prisoner's brows. Then between cord and

[015-084] cranium the black inserted a short length of metal, round and slender

[015-085] as a pipe-stem. That done he rolled his eyes towards Levasseur, awaiting

[015-086] the Captain's signal.

[015-087] Levasseur considered his victim, and beheld him tense and braced, his

[015-088] haggard face of a leaden hue, beads of perspiration glinting on his

[015-089] pallid brow just beneath the whipcord.

[015-090] Mademoiselle cried out, and would have risen: but her guards restrained

[015-091] her, and she sank down again, moaning.

[015-092] “I beg that you will spare yourself and your sister,” said the Captain,

[015-093] “by being reasonable. What, after all, is the sum I have named? To your

[015-094] wealthy father a bagatelle. I repeat, I have been too modest. But since

[015-095] I have said twenty thousand pieces of eight, twenty thousand pieces it

[015-096] shall be.”

[015-097] “And for what, if you please, have you said twenty thousand pieces of

[015-098] eight?”

[015-099] In execrable French, but in a voice that was crisp and pleasant, seeming

[015-100] to echo some of the mockery that had invested Levasseur's, that question

[015-101] floated over their heads.

[015-102] Startled, Levasseur and his officers looked up and round. On the crest

[015-103] of the dunes behind them, in sharp silhouette against the deep cobalt of

[015-104] the sky, they beheld a tall, lean figure scrupulously dressed in black

[015-105] with silver lace, a crimson ostrich plume curled about the broad brim of

[015-106] his hat affording the only touch of colour. Under that hat was the tawny

[015-107] face of Captain Blood.

[015-108] Levasseur gathered himself up with an oath of amazement. He had

[015-109] conceived Captain Blood by now well below the horizon, on his way to

[015-110] Tortuga, assuming him to have been so fortunate as to have weathered

[015-111] last night's storm.

[015-112] Launching himself upon the yielding sand, into which he sank to the

[015-113] level of the calves of his fine boots of Spanish leather, Captain Blood

[015-115] a dozen others. As he came to a standstill, he doffed his hat, with a

[015-116] flourish, to the lady. Then he turned to Levasseur.

[015-117] “Good-morning, my Captain,” said he, and proceeded to explain his

[015-118] presence. “It was last night's hurricane compelled our return. We had no

[015-122] of the island a couple of miles away, and we've walked across to

[015-124] designated the man and the woman.

[015-126] “Voila!” said he, pregnantly, to the firmament.

[015-129] “As you see, two prisoners.”

[015-130] “Ah! Washed ashore in last night's gale, eh?”

[015-131] “Not so.” Levasseur contained himself with difficulty before that irony.

[015-132] “They were in the Dutch brig.”

[015-133] “I don't remember that you mentioned them before.”

[015-134] “I did not. They are prisoners of my own--a personal matter. They are

[015-135] French.”

[015-144] “Yesterday you surprised me by making war upon the friendly Dutch. But

[015-145] now it seems that not even your own countrymen are safe from you.”

[015-146] “Have I not said that these... that this is a matter personal to me?”

[015-147] “Ah! And their names?”

[015-149] Levasseur's quick anger. The blood crept slowly back into his blenched

[015-150] face, and his glance grew in insolence, almost in menace. Meanwhile the

[015-151] prisoner answered for him.

[015-152] “I am Henri d'Ogeron, and this is my sister.”

[015-153] “D'Ogeron?” Captain Blood stared. “Are you related by chance to my good

[015-154] friend the Governor of Tortuga?”

[015-155] “He is my father.”

[015-159] molest the Dutch, who are our friends; next you take prisoners two

[015-163] Levasseur broke in angrily:

[015-164] “Must I tell you again that it is a matter personal to me? I make myself

[015-165] alone responsible to the Governor of Tortuga.”

[015-166] “And the twenty thousand pieces of eight? Is that also a matter personal

[015-167] to you?”

[015-168] “It is.”

[015-177] from me this part of the prizes taken on your last cruise, and for

[015-190] this couple at twenty thousand pieces, and, as I gather, the lady is

[015-197] “Buy her?”

[015-199] Levasseur contained his rage, that he might reason with the Irishman.

[015-200] “That is the ransom of the man. It is to be paid for him by the Governor

[015-210] “I quite agree with you,” said Captain Blood.

[015-241] had not Captain Blood intended otherwise.
