# Don Diego de Espinosa

## Overview

Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez is the Spanish admiral who becomes Peter Blood's most persistent adversary in the Caribbean. Commander of a Spanish warship "whose condition was commanded by Don Diego de Espinosa" ((008-090)), he first encounters Blood as his captor and repeatedly returns as his nemesis. Unlike the novel's other antagonists — Levasseur the brute, Rivarol the fraud, Don Miguel the strategist — Don Diego occupies a more complex position: he is an honourable enemy, a proud man repeatedly humiliated by a physician-turned-pirate, and ultimately a figure who earns Blood's grudging respect.

## In the Novel

### The First Capture

Blood's first victory over Spanish arms comes when he captures Don Diego's ship. The admiral is "informed by Don Diego, with an urbanity that was itself" a kind of weapon ((008-171)) — even in defeat, his aristocratic bearing remains intact. The ladder by which Don Diego had descended into captivity "still hung" as a symbol of reversed fortune ((009-129)). When "the boat with Don Diego and his" escort approached ((009-222)), the power dynamic of the Caribbean had already shifted: the Spanish admiral was now the Irish physician's prize.

### The Wounded Pride

The most intimate scene between the two men occurs when Blood, ever the physician, treats Don Diego's wounds. "Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez awoke" to find himself in Blood's care ((010-124)), a situation that baffles the proud Spaniard. Blood's "fingers touched the top of Don Diego's head, whereupon Don Diego winced and cried out in pain" ((010-166–167)) — a moment of vulnerability that humanizes the admiral even as it underscores his dependence on his captor. Blood takes "Don Diego's wrist between thumb and forefinger" ((010-168)), the physician's professional touch a reminder of the identity he has never truly abandoned.

### The Negotiation

The ransom negotiation reveals Don Diego's character. His protest — "'but there is a difference.' Don Diego sat up to argue the matter" ((011-037)) — shows a man who cannot accept his reduced status. Blood's calm treatment of his prisoner — "it proved soothing to Don Diego" ((011-004)) — establishes the paradoxical relationship that defines their arc: captor and captive, yet bound by a mutual recognition of honour. "Don Diego sank back on the couch, his gaze" fixed on his captor ((011-020)), and the conversation that follows is more negotiation between equals than demand from victor to vanquished.

### Repeated Defeats

Don Diego's arc is defined by repetition. Each encounter with Blood follows the same pattern — pursuit, defeat, capture — and each defeat compounds his humiliation. Blood's "war from Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez, who is" now personally invested in the vendetta ((010-052)), becomes more than a military campaign; it is a matter of family honour, eventually drawing his brother Don Miguel into the conflict.

### The Grudging Respect

What distinguishes Don Diego from the novel's lesser antagonists is the respect he earns. Blood never mocks him, never treats him with the contempt he shows Rivarol. Don Diego is a worthy adversary — defeated by superior wit and seamanship, not by moral deficiency. The novel grants him dignity even in humiliation, and his eventual acknowledgment of Blood's skill represents one of the novel's most satisfying character arcs.

## Character Analysis

Don Diego embodies the Spanish Empire's pride — magnificent, rigid, and ultimately brittle. His honour is genuine, not performative like Rivarol's, but it is also a prison: he cannot adapt, cannot learn from defeat, cannot see Blood as anything other than an upstart to be crushed. This makes him both sympathetic and pitiable. He is a good man serving a bad cause — Spanish imperial authority — and his personal virtues cannot save him from the institutional logic that demands his vengeance.

The doctor-patient scene between Blood and Don Diego is one of the novel's most revealing moments. Blood the physician heals the man he has defeated, and Don Diego the patient must accept care from the man who humiliated him. This inversion — the pirate as healer, the admiral as supplicant — encapsulates the novel's persistent subversion of social hierarchies.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — His captor and nemesis; their arc moves from enmity to grudging mutual respect
- **Don Miguel de Espinosa** — His brother, the Admiral of the West Indies, who continues the family vendetta with greater resources and strategic sophistication
