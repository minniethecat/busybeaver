# Don Francisco

## Overview

Don Francisco is the Spanish Deputy-Governor of Maracaybo, who enters the narrative when Captain Blood captures the city and threatens to burn it unless a ransom is paid. A slight, elderly man of noble lineage but limited physical vitality, Don Francisco displays unexpected courage in his negotiations with Blood — appealing not to the pirate's greed but to his sense of honour, a tactic that proves surprisingly effective. He stands in contrast to the intransigent Admiral Don Miguel de Espinosa, whose refusal to pay the ransom endangers the city.

## In the Novel

Don Francisco first appears as a messenger carrying the Admiral's answer to Blood's ransom demand. After delivering the unwelcome reply, he is dismissed with the grim parting words: "Ye may go, Don Francisco" ([017-186]). But Blood's threat to burn Maracaybo — "I have postponed the bonfire until nightfall. It will make a better show in the dark" — brings the Deputy-Governor back the following morning, seeking a private audience ([017-245]).

Sabatini describes him with characteristic economy: "Don Francisco, a slight, nervous, elderly man of high lineage and low vitality, came straight to business" ([017-247]). He offers to raise the ransom himself if Blood will grant him three days' leave on parole, leaving his son behind as a hostage ([017-256]). Blood, suspicious, challenges him: "By the Saints! Ye're a bold man, Don Francisco, to come to me with such a tale — to tell me that ye know where the ransom's to be raised, and yet to refuse to say" ([017-262]).

Don Francisco's reply cuts to the heart of Blood's character. When Blood threatens torture, the Deputy-Governor answers steadily: "That was the way of Morgan and L'Ollonais and other pirates. But it is not the way of Captain Blood" ([017-266]). Blood laughs, accusing him of playing on his vanity. "'Upon your honour, Captain,'" Don Francisco corrects him, then delivers the decisive line: "The honour of Captain Blood. You have the repute of making war like a gentleman" ([017-274], [017-282]).

This appeal succeeds where threats would have failed. Blood grants the three days, and Don Francisco departs on his errand — returning punctually with mules laden with plate and money to the full value demanded ([017-282]). His courage, his shrewd understanding of Blood's character, and his willingness to risk his own liberty for his city mark him as one of the more admirable Spanish figures in the novel.

## Relationships

- **Don Miguel de Espinosa** — The Spanish Admiral whose refusal to pay endangers Maracaybo; Don Francisco defies his intransigence
- **Captain Blood** — His captor and adversary, whom he persuades through an appeal to honour rather than fear
- **Cahusac** — The French buccaneer whose desertion with sixty men complicates Blood's position during the negotiations
