# Mutiny

## Overview

Mutiny — [[monmouth-rebellion|the rebellion]] of sailors against their officers — is the specter that haunts every ship in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. Blood's crew, composed of escaped slaves and rebels-convict, are bound to him not by law but by loyalty and shared interest. When that bond frays, the threat of [[treason|mutiny]] emerges. The novel distinguishes between justified resistance to tyranny (Blood's own "[[treason|mutiny]]" against the Stuart regime) and mere insubordination driven by fear or greed.

## Historical Background

Mutiny was the gravest crime at sea under 17th-century maritime law, punishable by death. The Articles of War prescribed severe penalties, and captains held near-absolute authority over their crews. Yet the conditions that produced [[treason|mutiny]] — harsh discipline, poor provisions, dangerous duty, and the proximity of escape in foreign ports — were endemic to Caribbean [[piracy|buccaneering]]. The line between a legitimate captain and a pirate chief was often thin, and crews voted with their feet (and occasionally their weapons).

## In the Novel

### Mutiny as Threat

When Blood's crew faces the prospect of capture by Spanish forces, "the sudden steely note in their Captain's voice" restrains them from "torn the Spaniard limb from limb" ((011-341)). The crew's discipline depends on Blood's personal authority, not on legal sanction. A false move, a perceived betrayal, and [[treason|mutiny]] would erupt.

### Mutiny as Treason

The novel's most explicit [[treason|mutiny]] reference conflates military and political [[treason|rebellion]]: "It's just [[treason|mutiny]]... treason! By God!" ((023-213)). This conflation is deliberate — the same act (defying authority) is called "[[treason|mutiny]]" at sea and "treason" on land. Blood, condemned for treason, now [[authority|command]]s men who might commit [[treason|mutiny]] against him. The irony is structural: the novel asks whether any authority that rules by force alone can legitimately call resistance a crime.
