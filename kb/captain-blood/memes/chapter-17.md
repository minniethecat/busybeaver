[017-001] The order came too late. [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]] had seen his six fellows drop

[017-002] overboard after the grapnels were fixed, and then had sped, himself,

[017-003] to the starboard gunwale. Thence he flung his flaming torch down the

[017-004] nearest gaping scuttle into the hold, and thereupon dived overboard in

[017-005] his turn, to be picked up presently by the longboat from [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]].

[017-006] But before that happened the sloop was a thing of fire, from which

[017-007] explosions were hurling blazing combustibles aboard the Encarnacion, and

[017-008] long tongues of flame were licking out to consume the galleon, beating

[017-009] back those daring Spaniards who, too late, strove desperately to cut her

[017-010] adrift.

[017-011] And whilst the most formidable vessel of the Spanish fleet was thus

[017-012] being put out of action at the outset, Blood had sailed in to open fire

[017-013] upon the Salvador. First athwart her hawse he had loosed a broadside

[017-014] that had swept her decks with terrific effect, then going on and about,

[017-015] he had put a second broadside into her hull at short range. Leaving her

[017-016] thus half-crippled, temporarily, at least, and keeping to his course,

[017-017] he had bewildered the crew of the Infanta by a couple of shots from the

[017-018] chasers on his beak-head, then crashed alongside to grapple and board

[017-019] her, whilst [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] was doing the like by the San Felipe.

[017-020] And in all this time not a single shot had the Spaniards contrived to

[017-021] fire, so completely had they been taken by surprise, and so swift and

[017-022] paralyzing had been Blood's stroke.

[017-023] Boarded now and faced by the cold steel of the buccaneers, neither the

[017-024] San Felipe nor the Infanta offered much resistance. The sight of their

[017-025] admiral in flames, and the Salvador drifting crippled from the action,

[017-026] had so utterly disheartened them that they accounted themselves

[017-027] vanquished, and laid down their arms.

[017-028] If by a resolute stand the Salvador had encouraged the other two

[017-029] undamaged vessels to resistance, the Spaniards might well have

[017-030] retrieved the fortunes of the day. But it happened that the Salvador was

[017-031] handicapped in true Spanish fashion by being the treasure-ship of the

[017-032] fleet, with plate on board to the value of some fifty thousand pieces.

[017-033] Intent above all upon saving this from falling into the hands of the

[017-034] pirates, [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]], who, with a remnant of his crew, had meanwhile

[017-035] transferred himself aboard her, headed her down towards Palomas and the

[017-036] fort that guarded the passage. This fort the Admiral, in those days of

[017-037] waiting, had taken the precaution secretly to garrison and rearm. For

[017-038] the purpose he had stripped the fort of Cojero, farther out on the gulf,

[017-039] of its entire armament, which included some cannon-royal of more than

[017-040] ordinary range and [[authority|power]].

[017-041] With no suspicion of this, [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] gave chase, accompanied by

[017-042] the Infanta, which was manned now by a prize-crew under the command of

[017-043] Yberville. The stern chasers of the Salvador desultorily returned the

[017-044] punishing fire of the pursuers; but such was the damage she, herself,

[017-045] sustained, that presently, coming under the guns of the fort, she began

[017-046] to sink, and finally settled down in the shallows with part of her hull

[017-047] above water. Thence, some in boats and some by swimming, the Admiral got

[017-048] his crew ashore on Palomas as best he could.

[017-049] And then, just as Captain Blood accounted the victory won, and that his

[017-050] way out of that trap to the open sea beyond lay clear, the fort suddenly

[017-051] revealed its formidable and utterly unsuspected strength. With a roar

[017-052] the cannons-royal proclaimed themselves, and the [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] staggered

[017-053] under a blow that smashed her bulwarks at the waist and scattered death

[017-054] and confusion among the seamen gathered there.

[017-055] Had not [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]], her master, himself seized the whipstaff and put the helm

[017-056] hard over to swing her sharply off to starboard, she must have suffered

[017-057] still worse from the second volley that followed fast upon the first.

[017-058] Meanwhile it had fared even worse with the frailer Infanta. Although

[017-059] hit by one shot only, this had crushed her larboard timbers on the

[017-060] waterline, starting a leak that must presently have filled her, but for

[017-061] the prompt action of the experienced Yberville in ordering her

[017-062] larboard guns to be flung overboard. Thus lightened, and listing now

[017-063] to starboard, he fetched her about, and went staggering after the

[017-064] retreating Arabella, followed by the fire of the fort, which did them,

[017-065] however, little further damage.

[017-066] Out of range, at last, they lay to, joined by the Elizabeth and the San

[017-067] Felipe, to consider their position.

[017-069] It was a crestfallen Captain Blood who presided over that hastily

[017-070] summoned council held on the poop-deck of the Arabella in the brilliant

[017-071] morning sunshine. It was, he declared afterwards, one of the bitterest

[017-072] moments in his career. He was compelled to digest the fact that having

[017-073] conducted the engagement with a skill of which he might justly be proud,

[017-074] having destroyed a force so superior in ships and guns and men that Don

[017-075] Miguel de Espinosa had justifiably deemed it overwhelming, his victory

[017-076] was rendered barren by three lucky shots from an unsuspected battery

[017-077] by which they had been surprised. And barren must their victory remain

[017-078] until they could reduce the fort that still remained to defend the

[017-079] passage.

[017-080] At first Captain Blood was for putting his ships in order and making the

[017-081] attempt there and then. But the others dissuaded him from betraying an

[017-082] impetuosity usually foreign to him, and born entirely of chagrin

[017-083] and mortification, emotions which will render unreasonable the most

[017-084] reasonable of men. With returning calm, he surveyed the situation. The

[017-085] Arabella was no longer in case to put to sea; the Infanta was merely

[017-086] kept afloat by artifice, and the San Felipe was almost as sorely damaged

[017-087] by the fire she had sustained from the buccaneers before surrendering.

[017-088] Clearly, then, he was compelled to admit in the end that nothing

[017-089] remained but to return to Maracaybo, there to refit the ships before

[017-090] attempting to force the passage.

[017-091] And so, back to Maracaybo came those defeated victors of that short,

[017-092] terrible fight. And if anything had been wanting further to exasperate

[017-093] their leader, he had it in the pessimism of which Cahusac did not

[017-094] economize expressions. Transported at first to heights of dizzy

[017-095] satisfaction by the swift and easy victory of their inferior force that

[017-096] morning, [[levasseur|the Frenchman]] was now plunged back and more deeply than ever

[017-097] into the abyss of hopelessness. And his mood infected at least the main

[017-098] body of his own followers.

[017-099] “It is the end,” he told Captain Blood. “This time we are checkmated.”

[017-100] “I'll take the liberty of reminding you that you said the same before,”

[017-101] Captain Blood answered him as patiently as he could. “Yet you've seen

[017-102] what you've seen, and you'll not deny that in ships and guns we are

[017-103] returning stronger than we went. Look at our present fleet, man.”

[017-104] “I am looking at it,” said Cahusac.

[017-105] “Pish! Ye're a white-livered cur when all is said.”

[017-106] “You call me a coward?”

[017-107] “I'll take that liberty.”

[017-108] The Breton glared at him, breathing hard. But he had no mind to ask

[017-109] satisfaction for the insult. He knew too well the kind of satisfaction

[017-110] that Captain Blood was likely to afford him. He remembered the fate of

[017-111] Levasseur. So he confined himself to words.

[017-112] “It is too much! You go too far!” he complained bitterly.

[017-113] “Look you, Cahusac: it's sick and tired I am of your perpetual whining

[017-114] and complaining when things are not as smooth as a convent dining-table.

[017-115] If ye wanted things smooth and easy, ye shouldn't have taken to the sea,

[017-116] and ye should never ha' sailed with me, for with me things are never

[017-117] smooth and easy. And that, I think, is all I have to say to you this

[017-118] morning.”

[017-122] ashore, his mind made up, and returned to the house of the Governor, to
