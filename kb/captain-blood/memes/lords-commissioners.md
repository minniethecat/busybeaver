# Lords Commissioners

The **Lords Commissioners** were the judicial tribunal that presided over the Bloody Assizes at Taunton Castle in September 1685. Composed of five judges in scarlet robes led by Lord Chief Justice George Jeffreys, this court tried hundreds of captives taken after the Battle of Sedgemoor, including Peter Blood, on charges of high treason against King James II.[^1]

## Composition

The tribunal consisted of five judges:

- **Lord Chief Justice George Jeffreys** — presiding judge, notorious for his severity and quick condemnation
- **Four other judges** — unnamed in the record, but vested with the full authority of the Crown to pass sentence

Jeffreys, whose terrible fame had preceded him from the earlier assizes at Dorchester, dominated the proceedings at Taunton with an iron will and a sharp tongue.[^2]

## The Trial of Peter Blood

Peter Blood was brought before the Lords Commissioners at Taunton Castle alongside other captured rebels. The court was "scarlet-hung," reflecting the judicial robes and the bloody reputation of the proceedings. Blood's composed demeanor during his trial contrasted sharply with the fear that gripped most defendants.[^3]

When Blood explained that he had been attending to a wounded friend and was not a combatant in the Monmouth Rebellion, the presiding judge dismissed his defense. Blood famously declared that he had "never was with that army" and pointed out the absurdity of a Catholic fighting for the Protestant Duke of Monmouth.[^4]

The Lords Commissioners sentenced Blood to death by hanging, a sentence which was subsequently commuted to transportation to the Caribbean plantations — a fate that initiated Blood's transformation from physician to slave to buccaneer.[^5]

## The Bloody Assizes

The court of the Lords Commissioners operated with brutal efficiency. Until September 18th, 1685, the sentences passed by the tribunal were "carried out literally and expeditiously." On September 19th, a courier arrived from London with orders that temporarily stayed further executions, saving Blood and several others.[^6]

The Bloody Assizes resulted in hundreds of executions and thousands of sentences of transportation, making it one of the most notorious judicial proceedings in English legal history.
