# Oath

## Overview

The oath — a formal, often sacred promise — appears throughout *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* as a marker of character and commitment. In the novel's 17th-century world, an oath carries legal and moral weight that transcends mere speech. [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s journey is shaped by oaths given and received: the oath of loyalty his followers swear to him, the oaths of office that govern [[colonial|colonial administration]], and the personal oaths that bind characters to courses of action from which they cannot retreat. The oath serves as a narrative device that crystallizes moments of decision, transforming private intention into public commitment.

## Historical Background

In Restoration England, oaths were instruments of both law and conscience. The Oath of Allegiance to [[crown|the Crown]], judicial oaths administered in court, and the oaths sworn by military officers upon receiving their [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s all carried the force of law — perjury and oath-breaking were crimes as well as sins. The period's religious character meant that an oath invoked God as witness, making its violation a matter of eternal consequence. In the colonies, oaths of office bound governors and magistrates to [[crown|the Crown]], while oaths of loyalty bound soldiers and sailors to their [[authority|command]]ers. The novel's setting in the aftermath of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] — in which men violated their oaths to James II by rising in [[treason|rebellion]] — highlights the tension between conflicting oaths to conscience and to [[crown|Crown]].

## In the Novel

### Oaths as Bonds of Loyalty

After escaping [[barbados|Barbados]], Blood's followers "swear to follow him loyally" (013-092), a voluntary oath that distinguishes their relationship from the coerced obedience of naval discipline. This oath creates a mutual [[duty|obligation]]: Blood must lead competently and justly, and his men must follow where he leads. The oath replaces the legal bonds that were severed by their [[treason|rebellion]] and transportation, creating a new political community founded on consent rather than compulsion.

### Oaths in Conflict

Characters throughout the novel invoke oaths to reinforce their positions or to bind others. [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] himself "rapped out a nasty oath" (003-107) in moments of frustration, while his opponents "screamed oaths at the ship" in their impotence (009-265). These exclamatory oaths — curses and blasphemies — reflect the emotional intensity of naval life, where men accustomed to violence express themselves through violent language. The frequency of oaths in the dialogue marks the social class and temperament of the speakers.

### Judicial and Political Oaths

The novel's opening scenes involve the judicial oaths of the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]], where witnesses are "bound by their oaths and consciences" (003-545) to testify. These oaths, administered by [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]]' court, are perverted into instruments of [[justice|injustice]] — witnesses are intimidated into swearing false testimony, and the oath becomes a tool of persecution rather than truth. This corruption of the oath at the novel's beginning establishes the theme of institutions twisted to unjust ends, against which Blood's subsequent [[honour|honor]] stands in contrast.

### The Unspoken Oath

Blood never formally swears an oath of vengeance or [[treason|rebellion]]. Instead, his conduct embodies an unspoken oath — to justice, to [[honour|honor]], and to his men. This implicit commitment is arguably more binding than any formal oath, because it arises from character rather than circumstance. When Blood refuses to attack English ships, he is [[honour|honor]]ing an unspoken oath to his country; when he treats prisoners with courtesy, he is fulfilling an unspoken oath to the code of the gentleman. The novel suggests that the truest oaths are those that need not be spoken.
