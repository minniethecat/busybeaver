## Overview

The Monmouth Rebels were the military force assembled by James Scott, Duke of Monmouth, during his ill-fated attempt to seize the English throne from his uncle, King James II, in the summer of 1685. Composed primarily of West Country artisans, farmers, and Dissenters, the rebel army fought and was destroyed at the Battle of Sedgemoor. Its surviving members were tried at the Bloody Assizes, with hundreds executed and hundreds more transported to the Caribbean — a fate that produced, among its unintended consequences, the transformation of Dr. Peter Blood into Captain Blood.

## Organization

The rebel army was not a professional military force but a hastily assembled popular uprising. Its composition reflected the social base of Monmouth's support — Protestant Dissenters, small farmers, artisans, and a scattering of gentlemen officers:

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Infantry** | Primarily pikemen and musketeers, many armed with agricultural implements converted to weapons |
| **Cavalry** | A small force of mounted gentlemen and farmers, poorly horsed |
| **Command** | Monmouth as Commander-in-Chief, assisted by Lord Grey of Warke (cavalry) and various mercenary officers |
| **Support** | Camp followers, physicians (including Peter Blood), chaplains |

## In the Novel

### A Physician's Service

Peter Blood's association with the Monmouth rebels is presented as a matter of professional duty rather than political zeal. As a physician, Blood was pressed into service to tend the sick and wounded. Sabatini portrays Blood as a man whose skills made him valuable to the rebel cause, regardless of his personal political convictions:

> "I make no concealment of my association with the Duke of Monmouth."
> ([001-00934])

Blood's candour about his service reflects his character — he will not dissemble to save himself, even when honesty means condemnation. His trial before Judge Jeffreys, where he gives an account of his actions without pleading or begging, establishes the dignity that will define him throughout the novel.

### The Rebels' Fate

The novel traces the rebels' fate from Sedgemoor to the Caribbean. Sabatini notes the grim statistics of the rebellion's aftermath:

> ...that we are concerned rather than with that of the Monmouth rebels.
> ([001-01288])

The defeated rebels become a commodity in the colonial labour market. Colonel Bishop, as a Barbados planter, acquires a substantial number:

> ...a score of survivors of the Monmouth men that Colonel Bishop had acquired...
> ([003-03752])

This trafficking in human beings, sanctioned by the English courts, is presented by Sabatini with understated moral clarity. The rebels are not criminals — they are political prisoners, sold into bondage by a vengeful government.

### The Rebel Identity

Throughout the novel, Blood's identity as a Monmouth rebel remains a defining characteristic — simultaneously a mark of honour and a social stigma. When Blood later rises to prominence as a buccaneer captain and, eventually, Governor of Jamaica, his rebel past becomes a point of political negotiation. In a world where loyalties shift with each change of monarch, yesterday's rebel can become tomorrow's governor.

The rebels' experience of transportation creates a bond among the survivors. Many of Blood's crew — including Jeremy Pitt, Hagthorpe, and Wolverstone — are fellow Monmouth men, united not only by shared circumstance but by shared history. The *Arabella's* crew is, in a sense, a floating community of Sedgemoor survivors.

### After Sedgemoor

The novel also touches on the psychological aftermath for the rebels. Rumours circulated that Monmouth had not truly died on the scaffold:

> ...began to circulate that a man resembling Monmouth had offered himself up in the Duke's stead, and that Monmouth survived to come again in glory.
> ([001-01334])

These rumours reflect the rebels' inability to accept the finality of their defeat. For men like Blood, however, there is no time for false hope — they must make their way in the new world to which they have been condemned.

## Significance

The Monmouth Rebels function in the novel on three levels:

1. **Origin Story**: The rebels provide the biographical backstory for Blood and his core crew. Without the rebellion, there is no transportation; without transportation, there is no Captain Blood.
2. **Moral Framework**: By establishing Blood as a political prisoner rather than a criminal, the rebel identity frames all of Blood's subsequent actions — even his buccaneering — as the acts of a man wronged by an unjust system.
3. **Community of Fate**: The shared experience of the rebellion, trial, and transportation creates the bonds of loyalty that sustain Blood's crew through every subsequent adventure. The *Arabella* is crewed by Monmouth men, and their loyalty to Blood is rooted in their shared history.
