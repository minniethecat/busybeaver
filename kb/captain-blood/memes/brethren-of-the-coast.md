# Brethren of the Coast

## Overview

The Brethren of the Coast — also called the Brotherhood of the Coast — were the loose confederation of buccaneers, freebooters, and pirates who dominated the Caribbean Sea in the latter half of the 17th century. In *Captain Blood*, they form the turbulent society into which Peter Blood is thrust after his escape from slavery in Barbados, and from which he must distinguish himself through a combination of strategic brilliance, personal honour, and iron discipline. The Brethren are not a formal organization with a single command structure but rather a shared identity and set of customs — the articles of association, the share system for prizes, and the code of the buccaneer life — that binds together adventurers of every nation operating out of Tortuga and other pirate havens ((013-089)).

## Historical Background

The historical Brethren of the Coast emerged in the early 17th century from the motley population of escaped indentured servants, shipwrecked sailors, deserters, and outcasts who settled on Tortuga and the western coast of Hispaniola. Originally hunters of wild cattle (the word "buccaneer" derives from the French *boucanier*, referring to the wooden grills on which they smoked meat), these men gradually took to the sea, preying on Spanish shipping in the Caribbean. By the 1680s — the period of the novel — the Brethren had evolved into a formidable maritime force operating under a customary legal code that governed the division of spoils, the election of officers, and the rights of the wounded. Their articles of association provided for each man to be paid by a share in the prizes captured, a system that Peter Blood adopted when recruiting his own crew ((013-121)). The Brethren's existence was a direct challenge to Spanish imperial claims in the New World, and their depredations had piled up "heavy debt" for the Spanish Crown ((008-087)).

## In the Novel

### A Society of Outlaws

When Blood and his fellow escapees from Barbados resolve to join "the great Brotherhood of the Coast, as those rovers called themselves," they are entering a world with its own rules and hierarchies ((013-089)). Tortuga, the Brethren's principal stronghold, is described as "that haven of the buccaneers" — a lawless port where they had "at least no danger of recapture to apprehend" ((013-053)–(013-055)). It is here that Blood recruits his crew, picking his men "with caution and discrimination" from among the adventurers of Tortuga and entering with them "into the articles usual among the Brethren of the Coast" ((013-121)).

### Blood Among the Brethren

Blood's relationship with the Brethren is complex. He is undeniably one of them — his successes against Spanish shipping depend on the same buccaneering skills that the Brethren value. But he also distinguishes himself sharply from the common run of pirates. Aboard the *Arabella*, he modifies the Brethren's customary articles to impose strict discipline: "Those who shipped with him undertook obedience and submission in all things to himself and to the officers appointed by election" ((013-123)–(013-125)). This departure from "the ruffianly indiscipline that normally prevailed in buccaneering vessels" marks Blood as an exceptional figure within the Brotherhood ((013-123)).

His reputation rises rapidly among them. Levasseur, a notorious French buccaneer, finds that his own standing "stood high among the wild Brethren of the Coast" — yet it is Blood who ultimately commands the loyalty of the buccaneer community ((014-022)). When Blood prepares his expedition against Maracaybo, he recruits five hundred adventurers from among the Brethren and could have had "as many thousands if he could have offered them accommodation" ((016-050)–(016-052)). His credit among them, protected by the powerful support of Governor d'Ogeron of Tortuga, "very rapidly reached its zenith" ((016-046)).

### The Darker Side

The Brethren are not romanticized. The novel is candid about the brutality that characterizes many of their number. Levasseur, Blood's rival, is described as a man of whom the narrator doubts "if in his day there was a greater scoundrel among the Brethren of the Coast" ((014-042)). The Brethren's greed is a constant force in the narrative — they are quick to anger when they suspect a captain of cheating them of prize money, as when they clamour for their shares after Levasseur's downfall ((018-166)). The mob of buccaneers that gathers at critical moments represents the volatile, dangerous energy of the Brethren, which Blood must constantly manage through a combination of oratory, reputation, and strategic generosity.

### The Brethren as Witness

The Brethren also serve as a Greek chorus to Blood's career. His standing among them rises and falls with his fortunes. At his height, his reputation "had stood so high among the Brethren of the Coast" that he could command fleets ((022-180)). Even in the novel's closing chapters, long after Blood has transitioned to legitimate service, the customs of the Brethren persist — treasure is "weighed in their presence, as is the custom among the Brethren of the Coast" ((028-205)), and Blood's enemies use his association with "the Brethren of the Coast" as a rhetorical weapon against him ((020-312)).

## Organization

The Brethren of the Coast operated under a customary constitution rather than a formal hierarchy. Key features of their organization, as depicted in the novel, include:

- **Articles of Association** — Each crew entered into written articles specifying the division of spoils, the election of officers, and the rules of conduct. Blood's articles with his crew followed "the articles usual among the Brethren of the Coast" in matters of prize-sharing, while departing from custom in matters of discipline ((013-121)).
- **The Share System** — Each man received a predetermined share of captured prizes, with extra compensation for those wounded in action. This system of mutual risk and reward was fundamental to the Brethren's cohesion.
- **Captain by Election** — Captains among the Brethren were elected and could be deposed by their crews. Blood's authority, though reinforced by his success and personal force, rested ultimately on the consent of his men.
- **Tortuga as Capital** — The island of Tortuga, under the governorship of d'Ogeron, served as the Brethren's unofficial headquarters — a place to refit, recruit, and divide spoils ((013-053)–(013-055)).
- **No National Allegiance** — The Brethren included French, English, and Dutch adventurers, united by their opposition to Spain rather than loyalty to any Crown. This made them politically useful to European powers who sought to harass Spanish shipping, but also unreliable as permanent allies.

## Key Members

- **Peter Blood** — The most successful and respected captain among the Brethren, whose honour and discipline set him apart from common buccaneers
- **Levasseur** — A French buccaneer whose cruelty represents the darkest element within the Brotherhood ((014-042))
- **Cahusac** — Levasseur's lieutenant, who succeeds to command of *La Foudre* and later joins Blood's fleet
- **Jeremy Pitt** — Blood's loyal first mate, a fellow escapee who rises within the buccaneer ranks
- **Hagthorpe** and **Wolverstone** — Blood's trusted officers, representing the English contingent within the Brethren
- **Governor d'Ogeron** — The French governor of Tortuga who advances Blood money and provides the political protection that enables his operations among the Brethren ((016-046))
