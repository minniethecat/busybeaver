# Don Miguel de Espinosa

## Overview

Don Miguel de Espinosa is the Spanish Admiral of the West Indies, "brother to the Spanish Admiral Don Miguel de" Don Diego ((008-091)), who emerges as Blood's most dangerous antagonist in the novel's second half. Unlike his brother — whom Blood repeatedly defeats and ransoms — Don Miguel commands greater resources and authority. His flagship anchors the Spanish naval presence in the Caribbean, and his pursuit of Blood transforms a personal vendetta into an imperial campaign. "Spain, through the mouth of Don Miguel, did not spare her threats" ((013-156)), and those threats carry the weight of the world's greatest maritime empire.

## In the Novel

### The Admiral of Spain

Don Miguel is introduced as the supreme Spanish naval authority in the region — "the flagship of Don Miguel de Espinosa, the Lord Admiral of the West Indies" ((011-330)). Where Don Diego commands individual ships, Don Miguel commands fleets. His arrival signals an escalation: Blood is no longer raiding isolated Spanish vessels but contending with the concentrated might of the Spanish Empire. When "[Cahusac] insisted. 'Don Miguel, the Spanish Admiral, is in the neighbourhood'" ((016-183)), the warning carries genuine menace.

### The Diplomat and Strategist

Don Miguel is more sophisticated than his brother. He greets his nephew with irony — "'Nay, nephew, nay,' Don Miguel protested with ironic repudiation" ((012-201)) — and employs diplomacy alongside force. His "laughter and stroked his pointed beard" ((012-243)) reveals a man who enjoys the game of power, not merely its fruits. He is patient where Don Diego is impetuous, strategic where Don Diego is merely proud.

### The Vendetta

The Espinosa family's honour becomes Don Miguel's obsession. Blood is "surrounded by his implacable enemy, Don Miguel de Espinosa" ((016-165)), creating a crisis that forces Blood into extraordinary measures — including his temporary alliance with the French. Don Miguel sends "a letter in which" he demands Blood's surrender ((016-223)), and his pursuit becomes so relentless that Cahusac advises Blood to send word "to Don Miguel that you have seceded from me" ((016-233)) — offering Blood up to save himself. This pressure drives much of the novel's third act.

### The Climax and Defeat

Don Miguel's campaign culminates in the novel's final naval engagement. Blood's escape from Don Miguel's tightening net — choosing "the surrender was not to Don Miguel, but to Peter Blood" ((016-245)) as Cahusac accurately predicts — represents the ultimate triumph of the individual over the institution. "They must strike before Don Miguel" ((016-299)) becomes Blood's imperative, and his victory against such odds cements his legend. Yet "Don Miguel was of stouter heart" ((017-157)) than his brother — even in defeat, he remains a figure of genuine authority, not a comic villain.

## Character Analysis

Don Miguel represents the impersonal, institutional face of Spanish power. Don Diego's motives are personal — pride, vengeance, the humiliation of capture and ransom. Don Miguel's are imperial. He prosecutes the vendetta not because Blood has insulted him personally but because Blood threatens Spanish authority in the Caribbean. This distinction makes him a more formidable antagonist: personal enemies can be outwitted or placated, but institutional enemies are relentless.

His sophistication — the ironic wit, the stroked beard, the patient strategy — also distinguishes him from the novel's other Spanish commanders. Don Miguel is not merely a bigger Don Diego; he is a different kind of adversary altogether, one who understands that power is exercised through diplomacy and patience as much as through cannon fire.

## Relationships

- **Don Diego de Espinosa** — His brother, whose repeated defeats at Blood's hands Don Miguel seeks to avenge
- **Peter Blood** — His "implacable enemy" ((016-165)); their conflict defines the novel's Spanish-English dimension
- **Cahusac** — French buccaneer who fears Don Miguel's power and advises Blood to surrender
