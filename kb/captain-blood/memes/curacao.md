## Overview

Curacao is a Dutch settlement in the southern Caribbean Sea that features prominently in the early chapters of *Captain Blood* as the escapees' intended destination. After seizing the *Cinco Llagas* and fleeing Barbados, Peter Blood and his crew aim to reach this neutral Dutch port where they can find shelter and plan their next move without fear of Spanish or English pursuit.

## In the Novel

### Escape Destination

After the dramatic escape from Barbados aboard the captured Spanish frigate *Cinco Llagas*, Peter Blood announces that the ship will sail for Curacao. The Dutch settlement represents safety—a neutral port beyond the reach of both Colonel Bishop's authority and Spanish retaliation. Blood describes it to the wounded Lord Gildoy as "the Dutch settlement of Curacao," noting that at that time of year the voyage "may safely be undertaken in a light craft" ([006-228]).

### The Deceptive Course

Blood's navigation toward Curacao becomes a central point of tension in Chapter XI. He informs his Spanish prisoners—Don Diego de Espinosa and his officers—that the ship is bound for the Dutch settlement, and requests Don Diego's parole. Don Diego agrees, and Blood announces that with the prevailing wind they "should reach Curacao inside three days" ([011-163]).

However, the crew grows suspicious when their course does not match the expected heading. Jeremy Pitt, observing the North Star's position, confronts the situation: "You told me—didn't you?—that we came west of the archipelago between Tobago and Grenada, steering for Curacao. If that were our present course, we should have the North Star abeam, out yonder" ([011-206]). Don Diego offers a technical explanation involving the quadrant's half-degree error, claiming that "Curacao is now almost due north" ([011-245]).

### The Landfall Revelation

The deception culminates when land is sighted ahead. Pitt, staring at the coastline, "had not conceived that Curacao was of such considerable dimensions. Indeed, this looked less like an island than the main itself" ([011-261]). The truth soon emerges—the land before them is not Curacao but the coast of Hispaniola, revealing that Blood has been steering a deliberate course away from the Dutch settlement all along to pursue other objectives ([011-287]).

### Beyond Reach

By the time Blood's buccaneer council meets to decide the fate of the Spanish prisoners, "Curacao now lay beyond their reach" ([012-336]). The ship's course has taken them too far from the Dutch settlement, and with dwindling water and provisions, the decision is made to release the prisoners on Hispaniola rather than attempt to reach Curacao.

## Historical Context

The historical Curaçao was a Dutch colonial possession seized from Spain in 1634. In the late 17th century—the period of the novel—it served as a major trading hub and centre of the Dutch West India Company's operations. The island's deep-water harbour at Willemstad made it a natural port of call for vessels traversing the Caribbean. Its neutrality in the Anglo-Spanish conflicts of the period made it a logical destination for fugitives like Blood's crew, who could expect neither English nor Spanish jurisdiction to reach them there.
