# Bridgewater

## Overview

Bridgewater (modern Bridgwater) is a market town in Somerset, England, on the River Parrett. In the novel, it is the primary setting for the opening events: the mustering of Monmouth's rebel army, Peter Blood's reluctant involvement, and the Battle of Sedgemoor's aftermath. The town represents the world of political turmoil and injustice that Blood must escape.

## In the Novel

### Arrival of Peter Blood

Peter Blood arrives in Bridgewater in January 1685, having left his ship at Bridgewater Bay due to worsening health. He takes lodgings above Water Lane, where he is regarded with suspicion by the townspeople for his aloof demeanor ([000-144]).

### Monmouth's Mustering

Bridgewater, "like Taunton, had yielded so generously of its manhood to the service of the bastard Duke" ([000-174]). The town is a key rallying point for Monmouth's rebel forces. On the night before the Battle of Sedgemoor, "all Bridgewater knew and had known now for some hours, that it was Monmouth's intention to deliver battle that same night" ([000-296]).

### Aftermath of Sedgemoor

Following Monmouth's defeat, Bridgewater becomes a scene of chaos as wounded rebels — including the yeoman Baynes — flee back through the town. Blood, Pitt, and their companions ride out of Bridgewater toward Oglethorpe's farm, passing "a vanguard of fugitives from the field of battle, weary, broken men" ([000-690]). Colonel Kirke's dragoons later occupy the area, hanging captured rebels along the road "from Weston to Bridgewater" ([000-844]).

## Historical Context

The historical Bridgwater was a significant Puritan center during the English Civil War and later a key location in the 1685 Monmouth Rebellion. The town's proximity to Sedgemoor made it Monmouth's headquarters before the decisive battle. After the rebellion's failure, Bridgwater — like Taunton — suffered severe reprisals during Judge Jeffreys' Bloody Assizes.
