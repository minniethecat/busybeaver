# Hanging

## Overview

Hanging is the most visible form of [[execution|capital punishment]] in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, appearing from the novel's opening pages at the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]] through to the summary executions carried out at sea. The threat of [[execution|hanging]] — whether by the King's justice or by a pirate's whim — hangs over every character in the novel. [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] himself narrowly escapes [[execution|hanging]] on multiple occasions: first at [[bloody-assizes|the Assizes]], where [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] sentences rebels to death "without trial" (003-001), and later at the hands of Spanish authorities who would gladly see the buccaneer "[[execution|hanging]] from the yardarm" (012-327). The novel's treatment of [[execution|hanging]] illuminates the distinction between judicial execution and murder, between the lawful gallows and the lawless noose.

## Historical Background

In 17th-century England, [[execution|hanging]] was the standard method of execution for felonies ranging from theft to [[treason|high treason]]. The [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]] of 1685, presided over by [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]], resulted in the [[execution|hanging]] of over 200 Monmouth rebels, with hundreds more transported to the colonies. At sea, [[execution|hanging]] from the yardarm was a summary punishment for [[treason|mutiny]] and piracy, administered by captains under naval articles of war. In the Caribbean colonies, slaves and indentured servants could be hanged for offenses that in England might warrant lesser punishments. Hanging was thus both an instrument of state [[authority|power]] and a tool of maritime discipline.

## In the Novel

### The Bloody Assizes

The novel opens with the threat of [[execution|hanging]] at the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]. Blood protests that the court is "[[execution|hanging]] men without trial" (003-001), challenging a procedure that treats execution as a foregone conclusion. The Assizes' "wholesale [[execution|hanging]]s" (004-069) function as state terror, designed to crush [[treason|rebellion]] through spectacle. Blood's own trial, conducted "so summary as to be no trial at all" (003-212), results not in [[execution|hanging]] but in transportation — a [[pardon|reprieve]] that sets the entire narrative in motion.

### Maritime Hanging

At sea, [[execution|hanging]] takes a more immediate form. The [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] execute prisoners and mutineers by [[execution|hanging]] them "from the yardarm" (012-327), a practice that requires no gallows and serves as a visible warning to crews. Cahusac, one of [[levasseur|Levasseur]]'s men, "had narrowly escaped [[execution|hanging]]" (018-147) for his crimes, reflecting the casual brutality with which buccaneer justice was administered. Blood himself threatens [[execution|hanging]] as a deterrent but rarely carries it out, preferring to be "making war like a gentleman" rather than a common executioner.

### The Threat as Power

The [[authority|power]] to hang — or to withhold [[execution|hanging]] — is political currency in the novel. [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]]'s determination to see "[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] [[execution|hanging]]" (021-010) drives his pursuit of the buccaneer across the Caribbean. Blood's enemies see [[execution|hanging]] as the proper fate for a pirate, while Blood's allies see it as the unjust end of an "unfortunate gentleman" (018-362). The novel consistently uses the threat of [[execution|hanging]] to measure the distance between justice and vengeance.
