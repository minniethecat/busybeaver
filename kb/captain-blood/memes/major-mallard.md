## Overview

Major Mallard is the military commandant stationed at Port Royal, Jamaica, who serves under Peter Blood during Blood's brief but eventful governorship. A professional soldier with crisp mustachios and a dry wit, Mallard is characterized by his efficient competence and unwavering loyalty to Governor Blood. Though a relatively minor character in terms of page time, Mallard plays a crucial role in the novel's climax: it is he who personally arrests Colonel Bishop on Blood's orders, executing one of the most dramatically satisfying moments in the narrative.

## In the Novel

### First Appearance — Waiting at the Mole

Major Mallard first appears at the mole (harbor) of Port Royal, standing alongside Lord Julian Wade as they await the arrival of a ship. Mallard is described waiting "in the shadow cast by the wall" ([020-15208]), a detail that subtly establishes his character as a man accustomed to the peripheries of power — always present, always observant, rarely the center of attention.

When the expected prisoner — Colonel Bishop — arrives, it is Major Mallard who, alongside Lord Julian, receives him ([020-16346]). Mallard's initial reaction to the awkward situation is apologetic, reflecting his discomfort with the political tensions surrounding the transfer of power in Jamaica:

> Major Mallard was disposed to be apologetic.
> ([020-16350])

His characteristic mustachios are noted by the narrator as a marker of his military bearing. When Bishop's bluster fails to impress, "Major Mallard's crisp mostachios" serve as a silent counterpoint to the Colonel's theatrical outrage ([020-16376]).

### The Governor's Man

When Colonel Bishop attempts to assert authority and question Blood's legitimacy as governor, Mallard responds with the dry deference of a soldier who knows precisely where power truly lies:

> "Oh, as your lordship says." Major Mallard's manner was tinged with a certain dryness that suggested he found the situation more amusing than troubling.
> ([020-16386])

Lord Julian, however, cuts off any further discussion with Mallard, making clear that the Commandant's role is to execute orders, not debate policy:

> "I do not propose to discuss the reasons with you, Major Mallard."
> ([020-16394])

This exchange establishes Mallard's position in the hierarchy: a military officer whose duty is obedience, not deliberation.

### Blood's Assessment

Later, when Peter Blood returns to Port Royal and reflects on his administration, he mentions Mallard with a characteristic blend of appreciation and irony:

> "...Mallard whom I found in Port Royal, apparently governing in this fool's paradise..."
> ([020-19836])

Blood's casual reference to Mallard as having been "apparently governing" underscores the Commandant's quiet competence — he kept the colony running in the governor's absence. When the need arises to move against Colonel Bishop's resistance, Blood trusts Mallard absolutely:

> "Mallard's resistance in the fort. Ho there, Jeremy!"
> ([020-20018])

### The Arrest of Colonel Bishop

Mallard's most significant moment comes in the novel's climactic scene: the arrest of Colonel Bishop. When Bishop comes to the Governor's office to confront Blood, it is Mallard who first brings word of Bishop's approach:

> ...when Major Mallard brought him word that Colonel Bishop had arrived and was demanding to see him.
> ([020-20566])

Mallard's crisp salute and departure after delivering this message mark him as a man who executes his duties with military precision:

> Major Mallard saluted and departed.
> ([020-20580])

When Bishop confronts Blood directly, Mallard becomes the instrument of justice. Blood orders the arrest, and Mallard steps forward:

> Major Mallard advanced. "Colonel Bishop, I have orders to arrest you."
> ([020-20758])

In a moment laden with dramatic irony, Colonel Bishop — who had once arrested and condemned Peter Blood — now finds himself arrested by Blood's own commandant. Bishop swings to face the officers behind Mallard, seeking support that will not come:

> Colonel Bishop, I have orders to arrest you. You are to be held pending trial for abuse of authority, corruption, and the unlawful imprisonment of His Majesty's subjects.
> ([020-20764])

### The Final Scene

In the novel's dénouement, Mallard reappears as the efficient facilitator of Blood's triumph. After Bishop has been removed, it is Mallard who escorts Miss Arabella Bishop into Blood's presence:

> ...whilst Major Mallard went ahead to announce her.
> ([020-20826])

When Mallard enters to find Blood and Miss Bishop together, he observes the scene with the discretion of a man who has seen everything and judges nothing:

> Major Mallard turned away his face that he might conceal his smile...
> ([020-20820])

This final glimpse of Mallard — discreetly smiling, turning away to hide his amusement at the governor's personal affairs — humanizes the military commandant and provides a note of warmth in the novel's closing pages.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — Mallard's commanding officer during Blood's governorship. Mallard serves Blood with unwavering loyalty, executing the arrest of Colonel Bishop without hesitation. Blood trusts Mallard implicitly as his military right hand.
- **Colonel Bishop** — Initially Bishop's nominal subordinate (as commandant under the previous governor), Mallard becomes Bishop's arresting officer when Blood orders the colonel taken into custody. The reversal of authority is one of the novel's most satisfying dramatic moments.
- **Lord Julian Wade** — Mallard works alongside Lord Julian in the official reception of arriving dignitaries and prisoners. Their relationship is professional, with Lord Julian clearly outranking Mallard in the civilian hierarchy.
- **Arabella Bishop** — Mallard shows gallantry toward Miss Bishop, escorting her to see Governor Blood in the final chapter and discreetly withdrawing to allow their reunion.
