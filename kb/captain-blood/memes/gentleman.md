# Gentleman

## Overview

The concept of the gentleman pervades *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* as both a social category and a moral ideal. [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]], though transported as a rebel-convict and forced into piracy, never ceases to consider himself a gentleman — and it is precisely this self-conception that distinguishes his conduct from that of common [[buccaneer|buccaneers]]. The novel draws a sharp line between the "gentleman adventurer" who fights with [[honour|honor]] and the mere pirate who fights for [[treasure|plunder]]. Blood's insistence on gentlemanly conduct — treating prisoners with courtesy, refusing to attack English ships, making "war like a gentleman" (017-275) — is the thread that ultimately leads him back to respectability and a [[crown|Crown]] [[letters-of-marque|commission]].

## Historical Background

In Restoration England (1660–1688), the term "gentleman" denoted a man of good birth, education, and manners — distinct from the nobility in rank but above the yeomanry and merchant classes. A gentleman was expected to display [[honour|honor]], courage, and courtesy, and to adhere to a code of conduct that governed dueling, oath-keeping, and the treatment of social inferiors. In the Caribbean colonies, the category was more fluid: men of obscure origin could acquire wealth and status through [[plantation-system|plantation]] ownership or military service. The figure of the "gentleman adventurer" — a man of good birth who sought fortune through risk and enterprise — was a recognized social type of the period.

## In the Novel

### Blood as the Reluctant Gentleman

Blood is introduced not as a buccaneer but as a physician — a profession compatible with gentlemanly status. His Irish birth and service under the Dutch give him a cosmopolitan education that sets him apart from the rough soldiers and sailors around him. Even in captivity on [[barbados|Barbados]], [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] recognizes "the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who advanced" (013-082) with a bearing that [[authority|command]]s respect despite his enslaved condition.

### Gentleman vs. Pirate

The novel repeatedly contrasts Blood's gentlemanly conduct with that of authentic pirates. The French buccaneer Jean [[levasseur|Levasseur]] lacks "gentlemanly fashion" (005-096) and behaves with brutality that Blood finds contemptible. Blood himself insists on his status: he makes "war like a gentleman" (017-275), a phrase that encapsulates his entire code. [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]], sent to assess the buccaneer threat, concludes that Blood is no common pirate but "an unfortunate gentleman" (018-362) driven to the sea by [[justice|injustice]].

### The Courtly Ideal

The Spanish nobles who encounter Blood are forced to acknowledge his gentlemanly bearing. [[don-diego|Don Diego de Espinosa]], Blood's prisoner, recognizes him as a "gentleman of about [[don-diego|Don Diego]]'s own height" (010-159) with a presence that belies his outlaw status. Even in defeat, Blood treats his [[hostage|captive]]s with the courtesy expected between gentlemen of opposing nations — a conduct that repeatedly surprises his enemies and wins him allies.

### Restoration of Status

Blood's ultimate vindication comes when he is recognized as a gentleman by [[crown|the Crown]]. His appointment as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] and his marriage to [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]] represent the restoration of the gentlemanly status that was stripped from him by the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]. The novel thus traces a complete arc: gentleman → rebel → slave → pirate → gentleman — a restoration achieved not through inheritance but through demonstrated worth.
