# Prison

## Overview

The novel opens in prison and closes with liberation — but the prison of *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* is as much psychological as physical. Bridgewater Gaol, where Blood awaits trial, is merely the first in a series of confinements: the prison of unjust law, the prison of slavery, the prison of the pirate's precarious existence. Blood's journey from prisoner to governor traces an arc from total confinement to ultimate freedom, with each stage representing a different form of imprisonment to be overcome.

## Historical Background

English prisons of the 17th century were not primarily institutions of punishment (that function was served by execution, transportation, and corporal penalties) but of detention — holding the accused before trial and convicts before sentence. Conditions were notoriously harsh: overcrowding, disease, and [[ransom|extortion]] by gaolers were endemic. Wealthy prisoners could purchase better conditions; the poor suffered and often died before trial. Bridgewater Gaol, a provincial prison in Somerset, would have been typical of the period.

## In the Novel

### Physical Confinement

The novel opens with Blood "a prisoner accused of treason" in Bridgewater Gaol ((003-402)). The prison is a waiting room for death or transportation — the condemned "languishing" ((006-243)) while the legal machinery grinds. The prison's physical reality — chains, guards, the threat of the [[hanging|gallows]] — establishes the baseline from which all subsequent freedoms are measured.

### Slavery as Prison

Transportation to [[barbados|Barbados]] extends the prison beyond English shores. The [[plantation-system|plantation]] is a "prison without walls" — Blood is not chained but cannot leave, not guarded but cannot escape. The "ten years of slavery" ((007-112)) represent a prison sentence in all but name. Even after his escape, Blood carries the psychological prison of his unjust condemnation.

### Freedom and Its Limits

Blood's eventual appointment as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] represents the final escape from prison — not just physical but social and political. The condemned rebel becomes the supreme authority. Yet the novel suggests that true freedom is never complete: the [[governor|Governor]] is still imprisoned by duty, by [[honour|reputation]], and by the memory of what was done to him.
