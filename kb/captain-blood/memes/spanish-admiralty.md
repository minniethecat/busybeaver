# Spanish Admiralty

The **Spanish Admiralty** was the naval command of the Spanish Empire in the Caribbean during the late 17th century. Under the command of Admiral Don Miguel de Espinosa, it was responsible for protecting Spanish shipping, combating English and French buccaneers, and safeguarding the treasure fleets that sailed between the Spanish Main and Europe.[^1]

## Command Structure

The Spanish Admiralty in the Caribbean operated under the authority of the Supreme Council of Castile and the Catholic King (Charles II of Spain). Don Miguel de Espinosa served as the senior naval commander, with his brother Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez commanding individual vessels within the fleet.[^2]

The Spanish fleet maintained a presence at key strategic points, including the bottleneck exit from the Caribbean Sea, where they sought to intercept pirate vessels attempting to prey upon Spanish merchant shipping.[^3]

## Conflict with Captain Blood

The Spanish Admiralty became directly involved in the pursuit of Captain Peter Blood after his escape from Barbados and subsequent career as a buccaneer. Don Miguel de Espinosa made a solemn vow to bring Blood to justice, declaring that the pirates had committed offenses against the subjects of the Catholic King.[^4]

Blood's tactical brilliance repeatedly frustrated the Spanish naval forces. On one notable occasion, the Spanish Admiral encountered Blood's squadron and was outmaneuvered — Don Miguel never guessed Blood's stratagem until it was too late.[^5]

## Diplomatic Role

Beyond military operations, the Spanish Admiralty served as a diplomatic channel for Spanish complaints against English and French buccaneers. The King of Spain lodged formal protests through diplomatic channels, demanding that the European powers restrain their nationals operating in the Caribbean.[^6]

## Legacy

The Spanish Admiralty's inability to suppress buccaneer activity in the Caribbean reflected the broader decline of Spanish naval power in the late 17th century. Despite possessing formidable vessels and experienced commanders, the vast territorial claims of the Spanish Empire stretched its naval resources thin, leaving merchant shipping vulnerable to the depredations of privateers and pirates.[^7]
