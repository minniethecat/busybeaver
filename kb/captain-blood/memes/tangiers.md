## Overview

Tangiers (Tangier) is a port city in North Africa, at the western entrance to the Strait of Gibraltar. In the 17th century, it was an English possession and garrison town, part of the dowry of Catherine of Braganza upon her marriage to Charles II. In *Captain Blood*, Tangiers is mentioned as the place where Peter Blood served as a soldier before the events of the novel ([003-002]).

## In the Novel

Peter Blood's military past at Tangiers is revealed during his arrest and trial after the Battle of Sedgemoor. When soldiers come to arrest the wounded Lord Gildoy at Blood's house, Blood dryly remarks to the troopers that they seem to think they are "in Tangiers" rather than England, mocking their rough treatment of a wounded man ([002-183]). The reference carries weight because Blood himself knew Tangiers well.

Colonel Kirk, who had served as Colonel of the Tangiers Regiment, recognized Blood from their time in the garrison: he "had known him in Tangiers some years ago" and acknowledged that Blood had been "an excellent soldier" before turning to medicine ([003-423]). This connection saves Blood from immediate execution — Kirk intervenes, allowing Blood to be tried rather than summarily hanged. The Tangiers Regiment itself is referenced as the military unit in which Kirk served before being dispatched to deal with the Monmouth Rebellion ([003-206]).

## Historical Context

English Tangier (1661–1684) was part of Catherine of Braganza's marriage dowry. The English maintained a substantial garrison there, including the Tangiers Regiment (later the Queen's Royal West Surrey Regiment). The garrison was abandoned in 1684 due to the high cost of defending it against Moroccan attacks. Blood's service there would have placed him among the professional soldiers who later fought in the Monmouth Rebellion on both sides.
