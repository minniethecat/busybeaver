# Privateering

## Overview

Privateering occupies the grey zone between naval warfare and criminal piracy. A privateer was a private ship captain who held a [[letters-of-marque|letter of marque]] — a government [[letters-of-marque|commission]] authorizing the capture of enemy vessels in exchange for a share of the prize money. In the Caribbean of the 1680s, where European [[authority|power]]s competed violently for trade routes and colonial possessions, [[piracy|privateering]] was an established instrument of state policy: it allowed governments to project naval [[authority|power]] without the expense of maintaining a standing fleet. In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the boundary between [[piracy|privateering]] and piracy is not merely a legal technicality but a moral fault line. The novel repeatedly invites Blood — and the reader — to consider whether a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] from a king makes the capture of ships and treasure any more honourable than doing the same without one.

## Historical Background

Privateering flourished in the Caribbean throughout the 17th century, driven by the persistent naval weakness of the European colonial [[authority|power]]s and the enormous wealth transported by the Spanish [[treasure|treasure fleet]]s. French [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] operating from [[tortuga|Tortuga]], English privateers sailing from [[port-royal|Port Royal]], and Dutch raiders from Curaçao all blurred the line between legitimate warfare and opportunistic predation. The French West India Company, under governors like M. d'Ogeron, actively recruited [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] as a form of cheap naval defence, offering semi-official [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s in exchange for a share of captured prizes.

The economic logic of [[piracy|privateering]] was straightforward: the privateer captain bore the cost of outfitting his vessel and crew, while the issuing government received a percentage of any prizes taken. For the captains themselves, the rewards could be enormous — a single successful cruise might yield more wealth than a lifetime of honest trade. But the risks were equally severe. A captured privateer who could not produce a valid [[letters-of-marque|commission]] would be treated as a pirate and hanged. And [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s were inherently temporary and political: a privateer authorized to attack Spanish ships under one treaty might find himself an outlaw when the treaty was renewed.

## In the Novel

Privateering is the profession that circles [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] throughout the narrative — sometimes as temptation, sometimes as threat, and finally as the vehicle of his ascent to respectability.

**M. d'Ogeron's proposal.** In [[tortuga|Tortuga]], Blood is approached by the representative of the French West India Company, who reframes [[piracy|buccaneering]] as a dignified commercial enterprise: "A trade that might have worn a repellent aspect when urged by greasy, half-drunken adventurers … became a dignified, almost official form of [[piracy|privateering]] when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France herself" ((013-079)). Blood, always alert to the gap between appearance and reality, notes the rhetorical sleight of hand: the same activities, endorsed by the right person, are suddenly respectable.

**[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]'s background.** The narrative reveals that Blood himself "[[authority|command]]ed a privateer of twenty guns" ((014-014)) before the loss of his ship, establishing that his maritime skill was forged not in piracy but in the quasi-legal world of commissioned raiding. This background is crucial to understanding his competence and his moral self-image: he does not see himself as a pirate but as a professional who has simply, temporarily, lost his [[letters-of-marque|commission]].

**Privateering as diplomacy.** When [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] arrives with blank [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s, the offer recasts [[piracy|privateering]] in explicitly political terms: Blood is not being asked to stop raiding, but to redirect his raiding toward England's enemies and away from England's friends. "Privateering, this [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]," ((014-154)) becomes shorthand for the man's ambiguous status — not quite a pirate, not yet a [[crown|Crown]] officer.

**The [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] of [[piracy|privateering]] lore.** The novel's narrator notes that Blood's exploits were recorded by [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]], who "[[authority|command]]ed a privateer of twenty guns that had … ((014-014))", and that the [[piracy|buccaneering]] enterprise was defined more by "the courtly, middle-aged gentleman" ((013-082)) who lent it official sanction than by the rough adventurers who carried it out. The passage encapsulates the novel's broader argument: that the only reliable distinction between [[piracy|privateering]] and piracy is political — a matter of whose signature appears on the [[letters-of-marque|commission]].

## Examples

- **[[tortuga|Tortuga]] recruitment**: M. d'Ogeron's elegant sales pitch transforms [[piracy|buccaneering]] into [[piracy|privateering]] through sheer rhetorical force ((013-079)–(013-082)).
- **Blood the privateer [[authority|command]]er**: The revelation that Blood [[authority|command]]ed a twenty-gun privateer before the loss of his ship establishes his professional credentials and moral self-image ((014-014)).
- **The blank [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s**: [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]]'s diplomatic mission offers Blood the chance to re-enter the world of legitimate [[piracy|privateering]] on England's behalf ((018-287)).
