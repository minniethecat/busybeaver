## Overview

The Battle of Sedgemoor, fought on 6 July 1685 near Bridgewater in Somerset, was the last pitched battle fought on English soil. The forces of King James II, commanded by Lord Feversham (Louis de Duras) and Colonel John Kirke, decisively defeated the rebel army of James Scott, Duke of Monmouth, ending the Monmouth Rebellion. The battle serves as the crucial backdrop to *Captain Blood*: Peter Blood, a physician who had joined Monmouth's cause, is captured in the aftermath, setting in motion the chain of events that will transport him to Barbados as a convicted rebel.

## In the Novel

### The Eve of Battle

The novel opens in the immediate aftermath of Monmouth's campaign, with Blood already a hunted man. Sabatini provides retrospective glimpses of the battle through dialogue and narration. The rebels' situation on the eve of Sedgemoor is portrayed as desperate:

> Mr. Blood assumed that Lord Feversham would deliver battle... Monmouth's intention to deliver battle that same night. The Duke was to attempt to surprise the Royalist army in its encampment on Sedgemoor.
> ([004-00302])

The decision to fight reflects Monmouth's recognition that his cause was failing — his army, ill-equipped and poorly trained, could not sustain a prolonged campaign against the King's professional forces.

### The Rout

The battle ended in disaster for the rebels. Sabatini describes the aftermath with characteristic economy:

> ...poor fugitives from that swift rout on Sedgemoor came flocking in scores to Bridgewater...
> ([007-00708])

The "swift rout" captures the military reality: the rebel army, attempting a night attack, became disoriented in the unfamiliar terrain of the Somerset Levels. The Royalist forces, alerted by a musket shot, counter-attacked with devastating effect. The rebel infantry broke and fled, pursued by Kirke's dragoons.

### The Aftermath

The days following Sedgemoor saw brutal reprisals. Colonel Kirke and Lord Feversham conducted summary executions and transported prisoners in inhumane conditions:

> Even so, in that first week after Sedgemoor, Kirke and Feversham carried out a series of executions that shocked even that brutal age.
> ([012-01272])

Blood himself is captured during this period and held for trial. The battle's outcome transforms him from a physician and political idealist into a convicted rebel facing transportation to the Caribbean.

### Blood's Reflection

Throughout the novel, Blood's association with Monmouth's cause is referenced as the original sin that set his extraordinary career in motion:

> "I make no concealment of my association with the Duke of Monmouth."
> ([009-00934])

Blood's participation is presented not as zealotry but as a matter of conscience — an honourable man making a catastrophic political misjudgment. The novel's interest is not in the battle itself but in its consequences: how a single military defeat can alter the trajectory of a man's life completely.

### Connection to Captain Blood

The Battle of Sedgemoor functions in the novel as the hinge between Blood's English life and his Caribbean destiny. Without Sedgemoor, there is no arrest, no trial before Judge Jeffreys, no transportation to Barbados, and no transformation of Dr. Peter Blood into the legendary buccaneer Captain Blood.

## Significance

In the structure of the novel, Sedgemoor represents:

1. **The Inciting Event**: Every subsequent action in the novel — Blood's enslavement, his escape, his career as a buccaneer — traces back to this single battle
2. **A Political Statement**: Sabatini uses Sedgemoor to establish the injustice of Blood's punishment — a man of conscience and skill, condemned for a political act rather than a criminal one
3. **Historical Authenticity**: By anchoring Blood's story in a real historical event, Sabatini gives his fictional hero a credible origin and places him in the company of actual historical figures
