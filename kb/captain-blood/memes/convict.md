# Convict

In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the term **convict** — often rendered "rebel-convict" or "rebels-convict" — denotes the political prisoners condemned for their part in the Monmouth Rebellion of 1685, sentenced to transportation to the Caribbean colonies and sold into indentured servitude. This legal status is the engine of the entire novel: without the conviction and transportation of Peter Blood, there is no story.

## In the Novel

### The Monmouth Rebels as Convicts

The novel opens in the aftermath of the [[sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]] and the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]. Those rebels not hanged or imprisoned are sentenced by Judge [[judge-jeffreys|Jeffreys]] to transportation — a fate that, in the novel's economy, is both cruelty and opportunity. [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]], a physician who tended the wounded of both sides, is "convicted of high treason" alongside men who actually bore arms.

Blood's response to the conviction is characteristic: "I will convict you," he tells the court — the wordplay revealing both his legal predicament and his determination to expose the injustice of the proceedings.

### The Rebels-Convict in Barbados

In [[barbados|Barbados]], the forty-two surviving rebels-convict are treated as valuable property. The term "rebel-convict" follows them everywhere — it is their identity, their brand, their social death. Blood is "a rebel-convict with ten years of slavery before him," a condition designed to break men of spirit.

Yet the rebels-convict form a distinct community. They work the [[plantation|plantations]] together, share quarters, and maintain the bonds forged at [[sedgemoor|Sedgemoor]]. When Blood plans their escape, he draws on "his fellow-convicts" — men whose loyalty was tested in battle and whose desperation makes them fearless.

### Escape and Transformation

The escape from [[barbados|Barbados]] transforms the rebels-convict from agricultural slaves into sea-raiders. Blood's "fellow-convicts" become his crew, manning the captured Spanish [[cinco-llagas|Cinco Llagas]] and forming the nucleus of what will become the most formidable buccaneer force in the Caribbean. The label "rebel-convict" is gradually replaced by "buccaneer" — but the shared experience of conviction and slavery remains the crew's binding agent.

The narrator tracks this transformation with care: "the rebels-convict surged" into battle against the Spanish, then "the rebels-convict refreshed themselves" after victory. At the novel's close, looking back, the narrator invokes the "rebels-convict who had left Barbados" — the original identity persisting as a reminder of how far they have come.

### Blood's Unique Position

Blood is distinguished from his fellow convicts by his gentility and profession. He is "his more gentlemanly fellow-convict," a physician rather than a soldier, educated at Trinity College rather than on a farm. This difference is both advantage and burden: it makes him a natural leader but also deepens his sense of injustice. "He was convicted for what I did, neither more nor less," Blood reflects — the conviction was legal but not moral, a distinction that sustains him through his years of servitude.

## Historical Context

The transportation of convicted rebels was a common practice in 17th- and 18th-century England. The Monmouth Rebellion of 1685 resulted in hundreds of executions (the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]) and the transportation of over 800 prisoners to the West Indies, where they were sold as indentured labourers to plantation owners. The legal distinction between indentured servitude and [[slavery|chattel slavery]] was real but, in practice, often imperceptible — convicts could be bought, sold, and worked to death with minimal legal recourse.
