# Letters of Marque

## Overview

A letter of marque (or "[[privateer|letters of marque]] and reprisal") was a formal commission issued by a sovereign government, authorizing a privately owned vessel — a [[privateering|privateer]] — to attack and seize ships belonging to an enemy [[authority|power]]. In the 17th-century Caribbean, where the distinction between lawful [[piracy|privateering]] and outright piracy was often paper-thin, the letter of marque served as the crucial legal boundary: a captain without one was a pirate; a captain with one was, in principle, a servant of [[crown|the Crown]]. In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the King's commission becomes a recurring instrument of political negotiation, character transformation, and ironic reversal — from the court that condemned [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] to slavery to the very government that later seeks to purchase his loyalty with the same document it once denied him.

## Historical Background

The practice of issuing [[privateer|letters of marque]] dates back to the medieval period, when European monarchs lacking standing navies would license private ships to wage maritime war on their behalf. By the late 17th century, the system was well established across the colonial [[authority|power]]s. England, France, Spain, and the Netherlands all used [[privateering|privateer]]s as force multipliers in their naval conflicts. A letter of marque typically specified the enemy nation whose vessels could be targeted, the duration of the commission, and the percentage of prize money due to [[crown|the Crown]]. In peacetime, [[privateer|letters of marque]] were revoked, and [[privateering|privateer]]s who continued their activities crossed the legal line into piracy.

The Caribbean was particularly shaped by this system. Spain's claim to a monopoly over American trade meant that English and French [[privateering|privateer]]s could legally harass Spanish shipping during periods of hostility, while Spanish guarda costas (coast guards) could legally seize foreign vessels suspected of smuggling. The resulting legal ambiguity created a world in which "the sea was a vast No Man's Land, where the only law was the law of the stronger vessel" — and where a piece of paper from a distant king could mean the difference between a hero's welcome and the [[hanging|gallows]].

## In the Novel

The King's commission functions in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* as a symbol of legitimacy that [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] both desires and distrusts. Its appearances mark key turning points in his trajectory from physician to slave to buccaneer to [[governor|colonial governor]].

**The commission as temptation.** When [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] arrives in the Caribbean bearing "commissions made out in blank" ((018-287)) from King James II, the offer is explicitly framed as a transaction: Blood's [[piracy|buccaneering]] skill in exchange for [[crown|the Crown]]'s seal of legitimacy. Wade presents the commission as an instrument of redemption — a way for an outlaw to re-enter the civil order on honourable terms. But Blood, scarred by his earlier encounter with royal justice at the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]], meets the offer with characteristic scepticism.

**The commission as weapon.** Blood eventually accepts the King's commission, but on his own terms. In a characteristic act of strategic foresight, he keeps the document in his pocket while navigating the treacherous politics of [[port-royal|Port Royal]] and [[tortuga|Tortuga]]. When challenged on his authority, Blood can produce the commission — "commission in my pocket" ((020-274)) — transforming an accusation of piracy into a declaration of royal service. The physical document becomes a kind of shield, its legal magic dependent on whether the person facing it chooses to recognise the authority of the king who issued it.

**The commission as irony.** The deepest irony of the commission plot is that Blood, the convicted rebel who was transported in chains by the justice of King James, now holds a commission from that same king. "He wouldn't take the King's commission; he wouldn't take my hand even," ((020-369)) observes a character who cannot understand why Blood hesitates to grasp the legitimacy he is offered. Blood's refusal to be easily bought — his insistence that a commission cannot erase the memory of unjust condemnation — is one of the novel's key moral statements: legitimacy, once corrupted, cannot be restored merely by issuing a piece of paper.

## Examples

- **[[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]]'s embassy**: Wade arrives with blank commissions, explicitly linking the offer of legitimacy to Blood's strategic value to England ((018-287)–(018-310)).
- **The pocket commission**: Blood's "commission in my pocket" ((020-274)) becomes a running motif, symbolising the provisional and contingent nature of his legal status.
- **The commission and Bishop**: "Will Bishop respect the commission when you hold it?" ((021-311)) — an unanswered question that underlines the fragility of any authority that depends on the goodwill of one's enemies.
