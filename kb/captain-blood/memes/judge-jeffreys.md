# Judge Jeffreys

## Overview

George Jeffreys, 1st Baron Jeffreys (1645–1689), was the Lord Chief Justice of England who conducted the "Bloody Assizes" following the Monmouth Rebellion. In *Captain Blood*, Jeffreys appears as the personification of corrupted justice — the "venal instrument of a brutally spiteful" regime who condemns Blood for the crime of mercy. Though a historical figure, the novel's portrayal is consistent with Jeffreys' reputation as one of the most brutal judges in English history.

## Historical Basis

The real Jeffreys was appointed Lord Chief Justice by James II in 1685 and immediately dispatched to the West Country to try Monmouth's followers. The "Bloody Assizes" resulted in approximately 320 executions and over 800 transportations. Jeffreys' courtroom conduct was notorious: he bullied defendants, denied counsel, and rushed trials. After the Glorious Revolution of 1688, Jeffreys was captured attempting to flee the country and died in the Tower of London in 1689.

## In the Novel

### The Trial

Jeffreys appears in Chapter 3, presiding over Blood's trial. The novel portrays him with devastating irony: a "wistful-eyed jack-pudding in scarlet" whose authority masks cruelty. When Blood invokes his "sacred duty" ((003-489)) as a physician, Jeffreys erupts: "Thy sacred duty, rogue, is to..." ((003-491)) — the implication being that duty belongs only to the Crown, never to conscience.

### The Symbol

Jeffreys functions primarily as a symbol rather than a rounded character. He represents the absolute corruption of authority — the point at which law becomes indistinguishable from violence. Blood's entire subsequent career can be read as a response to Jeffreys' judgment.

## Historical Basis

Jeffreys is a historical figure, accurately portrayed in the novel's courtroom scenes. His methods, rhetoric, and the outcome of the trial are consistent with historical accounts of the Bloody Assizes.
