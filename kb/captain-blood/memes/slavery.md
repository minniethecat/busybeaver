# Slavery

## Overview

Slavery in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* is the engine of the novel's plot: the penal transportation of Monmouth rebels to the West Indian [[plantation-system|plantation]]s, the brutal reality of [[plantation-system|plantation]] life, and the constant threat of being sold into Spanish slavery drive the central conflict and character motivations. The institution is portrayed as both a legal punishment administered by the Stuart regime and an economic system that treats human beings as "human merchandise" ((004-055)).

## Historical Background

The novel's system of slavery draws on real 17th-century practices of penal transportation and [[plantation-system|plantation]] slavery in the Caribbean colonies. After the failed [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] of 1685, the survivors of the Duke of [[monmouth-rebels|Monmouth's army]] were tried before [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] in the "[[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]" and many were sentenced not to death but to transportation — a form of exile into servitude in the southern [[plantation-system|plantation]]s ((004-064)). The West Indian colonies of [[jamaica|Jamaica]], [[barbados|Barbados]], and the Leeward Islands had a desperate demand for labor ((004-070)), with a healthy man valued at "ten to fifteen pounds a head" ((004-071)).

Unlike chattel slavery based on race that would later dominate the Caribbean, the system depicted in the novel is primarily *penal* — rebels-convict sentenced to "the space of ten years" ((004-082)) of forced labor before being "restored to liberty" ((004-083)). This system is administered by colonial officials such as [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]], who purchases convicts at auction and uses them to work his [[barbados|Barbados]] [[plantation-system|plantation]] ((005-085)).

## In the Novel

### Transportation and the "Human Merchandise"

The novel's fourth chapter, "Human Merchandise," establishes slavery as the fate that awaits Blood and his companions. Rather than [[execution|hanging]], the convicted rebels are transported to the colonies — a decision driven not by mercy but by economic calculation. The [[crown|Crown]] sells convicts to [[plantation-system|plantation]] owners, converting political justice into commercial transaction ((004-093)).

[[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] is purchased by [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]], the Deputy [[governor|Governor]] of [[barbados|Barbados]], who values him primarily for his medical skills rather than his [[plantation-system|plantation]] labor potential ((004-178)). Blood's status as a "slave" is absolute: "A slave is a slave, and has no [[authority|power]] to shape his fate" ((005-085)).

### The Psychological Weight

The novel repeatedly emphasizes the psychological toll of slavery. [[dr-whacker|Dr. Whacker]], seeking to manipulate Blood into participating in an escape plot, describes "this slavery in which you find yourself" as "singularly irksome to a man of parts such as yourself" ((006-209)). He paints a vision of what Blood could achieve if he could "escape from this hell of slavery" and practice medicine as a free man ((006-222)).

Blood himself, in a moment of bitter humor, reflects that medicine "brought me into slavery" — observing that "it's more pleasing in the sight of Heaven to kill men than to heal them" ((007-078)). Yet even in bondage, Blood remains defiant; in a moment of romantic reverie, he "forgot in that little moment that he was a rebel-convict with ten years of slavery before him" ((007-112)).

### From Slavery to Piracy

Blood's escape from slavery is not merely physical flight — it is a rejection of the entire political order that condemned him. When he captures Spanish ships and builds his pirate fleet, his crew of fellow escapees "were likely to exchange the slavery from which they had so lately escaped for a slavery still worse" if captured by the Spanish ((011-339)). The threat of being "sold into slavery" by the Spanish is a recurring source of terror ((020-247)).

Blood's final reckoning with [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]] articulates the [[justice|injustice]] most fully: "James Stuart afterwards sent me into slavery, because I had performed an act of mercy" ((020-325)). He was "sold into slavery" for dressing the wounds of a wounded man suspected of treason, under a law that punished [[mercy|compassion]] ((020-330)).
