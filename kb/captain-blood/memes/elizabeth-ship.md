## Overview

The *Elizabeth* is a twenty-six gun vessel in Captain Blood's buccaneer fleet, captured from the Spanish and rechristened in honour of Queen Elizabeth of England. Commanded by the loyal Hagthorpe, the *Elizabeth* serves as Blood's second-most-capable warship after the flagship *Arabella*, and plays a critical role in every major naval engagement of the novel's second half — from the escape at Maracaybo to the climactic battle against Captain Easterling's privateers.

## In the Novel

### Capture and Renaming

The *Elizabeth* originates as a Spanish prize, captured during one of Blood's early buccaneering operations. In a characteristic gesture that blends patriotism with piracy, Blood rechristens her:

> ...refitted and rechristened the Elizabeth, after that Queen of England whose memory he venerates.
> ([009-09968])

The name is a deliberate political statement — Blood, transported as a rebel against King James II, nevertheless honours the memory of England's great Protestant queen. This duality (rebel and patriot) defines Blood throughout the novel.

### The Fleet at Maracaybo

By the time of the Maracaybo expedition, the *Elizabeth* has become an integral part of Blood's naval force. She enters the great lake alongside the *Arabella* and the lesser vessels:

> ...with his own Arabella of forty guns, the Elizabeth of twenty-six, and two smaller vessels...
> ([010-10416])

When Blood divides his fleet, Hagthorpe in the *Elizabeth* is tasked with shadowing the Spanish forces at a cautious distance:

> ...watched at a distance by the Elizabeth, commanded by Hagthorpe...
> ([010-10498])

### The Escape from the Lake

The *Elizabeth* plays a crucial role in Blood's escape from the Maracaybo trap. Having slipped past the Spanish fort under cover of darkness, the *Arabella* rendezvous with her consorts:

> Out of range, at last, they lay to, joined by the Elizabeth...
> ([010-10716])

In the subsequent running fight, the *Elizabeth* proves her worth:

> The Elizabeth and the Infanta, leading side by side, were almost abreast of the Spanish flagship.
> ([011-11486])

As the Spanish soldiery masses on deck to repel boarders, the *Elizabeth* delivers a devastating broadside at close range:

> ...the Elizabeth emptied her larboard guns into the packed ranks of the Spanish infantry, and the carnage was terrible.
> ([011-11498])

### The Pursuit of Easterling

In the novel's later chapters, Blood's fleet — now sailing under the English flag — pursues the privateer Captain Easterling. The *Arabella* and *Elizabeth* again pair as the strike force:

> ...the Arabella and the Elizabeth put to sea upon that angry chase.
> ([019-19440])

The two ships plough northward in a stern chase:

> ...the Arabella and the Elizabeth ploughed briskly northward with a moderately favourable wind.
> ([019-19504])

The *Elizabeth* maintains close station with the flagship as they close on their quarry:

> ...the Arabella with the Elizabeth following closely raced nearer on their converging course.
> ([019-19566])

### The Final Battle

In the climactic engagement against Easterling's force, the *Elizabeth* demonstrates precise seamanship and discipline under fire. When Blood executes a daring manoeuvre, Hagthorpe matches it flawlessly:

> ...and in a moment they were moving, the Elizabeth following, ever in perfect station.
> ([020-20028])

The *Elizabeth* mirrors the *Arabella's* tactical movements with parade-ground precision:

> ...to the Elizabeth, which, following closely, executed the same manoeuvre.
> ([020-20074])

A second broadside from the *Elizabeth* crashes into the enemy:

> Came yet another broadside from the Elizabeth and then the Arabella's turn to answer.
> ([020-20084])

### Aftermath

The battle is costly. When the smoke clears, the *Elizabeth* has suffered severe damage:

> Even then the labours of Blood's men were not at an end. The Elizabeth had taken a severe pounding...
> ([020-20290])

By the final reckoning, both of Blood's principal ships have paid a heavy price for victory:

> ...the Elizabeth had suffered almost as badly, her casualties numbering more than half her crew.
> ([020-20352])

## Significance

The *Elizabeth* represents the professionalization of Blood's fleet. Where the *Arabella* serves as flagship and symbol of Blood's personal command, the *Elizabeth* is the reliable workhorse — always in station, always delivering her broadsides at the critical moment. Under Hagthorpe's steady command, she exemplifies the discipline that distinguishes Blood's buccaneers from common pirates.

The ship's name also carries thematic weight. By naming her after England's greatest Protestant queen, Blood asserts a continuing loyalty to his homeland, even as he operates outside its laws. The *Elizabeth* is, in a sense, Blood's reply to his own outlaw status — a declaration that he remains, in spirit, an Englishman.
