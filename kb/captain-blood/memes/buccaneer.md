# Buccaneer

## Overview

In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the term "buccaneer" denotes the free-roving maritime adventurers who inhabit [[tortuga|Tortuga]] and other Caribbean strongholds, operating in a legal grey zone between outright piracy and state-sanctioned [[piracy|privateering]]. Originally derived from the French *boucanier* — hunters who smoked meat on *boucans* (wooden grills) in the wilds of [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]] — these men were driven to sea by Spanish persecution and evolved into a formidable [[fleet|naval force]] that challenged Spain's Caribbean hegemony throughout the 17th century.

The novel presents buccaneers as a distinct social class with their own codes, customs, and internal hierarchies. They are not merely pirates: they operate under articles of agreement, elect their officers, share prizes by predetermined formulas, and maintain a rough-hewn democracy aboard their vessels. The buccaneers of [[tortuga|Tortuga]] serve as the pool of man[[authority|power]] from which [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] recruits his crew and, ultimately, the instrument through which he transforms from escaped slave to the most feared captain in the Caribbean.

Sabatini carefully distinguishes buccaneers from common pirates throughout the narrative. Blood himself, though called a "thief and pirate" by [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]], operates under strict discipline unknown among typical buccaneer crews. His unique approach to [[piracy|buccaneering]] — emphasizing military discipline over rapine, strategic judgment over wanton [[treasure|plunder]] — elevates him above his contemporaries and makes him, in the novel's estimation, "such as no buccaneer--not even Morgan--has ever boasted, before or since" ((018-161)).

## Historical Background

The historical buccaneers originated in the early 1600s when French, Dutch, and English adventurers settled in northwestern [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]] (modern Haiti), living by hunting wild cattle and selling smoked meat and hides to passing ships. The Spanish, who claimed exclusive dominion over the New World under the Treaty of Tordesillas, repeatedly attacked these settlements, driving the boucanhunters from their livelihood. In retaliation, the displaced hunters took to the sea, first in small canoes and later in captured vessels, preying upon Spanish shipping.

By the mid-17th century, the buccaneers had established [[tortuga|Tortuga]] as their principal stronghold, transforming the small island off the coast of [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]] into a bustling — if lawless — entrepot where prizes were divided, ships refitted, and crews recruited. The [[brethren-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]], as they called themselves, developed a sophisticated system of self-[[authority|governance]] based on written articles of agreement that specified the division of spoils, compensation for injuries, and rules of conduct at sea.

The legal ambiguity of [[piracy|buccaneering]] was central to its practice. Letters of marque — [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s authorizing private ships to attack enemy vessels — could transform a buccaneer into a [[privateering|privateer]] overnight. This ambiguity is exploited throughout the novel: Blood's [[letters-of-marque|commission]] from King Louis XIV of France and later from King William III of England both attempt to legitimize what is, in substance, [[piracy|buccaneering]] by a different name.

Historically, the buccaneer era peaked during the 1660s-1680s, with figures such as Henry Morgan, Francois l'Olonnais, and Michel de Grammont leading large-scale raids against Spanish ports including Portobelo, [[maracaybo|Maracaibo]], and Panama. The novel's fictional [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] is set amidst this golden age, with direct reference to Morgan as the benchmark against which [[piracy|buccaneering]] fame is measured.

## In the Novel

### The World of Tortuga

The buccaneers first enter the narrative in Chapter XIII when the escaped rebels-convict weigh their options. [[tortuga|Tortuga]] is a "lawless port" where buccaneers hold sway ((012-341)), and the natural destination for escaped slaves and political refugees with no place in the colonial order. When Blood's companions deliberate their future, the proposal is straightforward: "any of his companions in misfortune to join hands with the buccaneers" ((013-043)). Blood's information network "proceeded not only from adventurous [[piracy|buccaneering]] acquaintances in the taverns of [[tortuga|Tortuga]]" ((013-073)) but from a web of contacts spanning the Caribbean.

The [[piracy|buccaneering]] society of [[tortuga|Tortuga]] is cosmopolitan and multilingual. The port of Cayona is a melting pot of "traders of several nations--English, French, and Dutch--of planters and of seamen of various degrees, of buccaneers" ((025-083)), reflecting the diverse human material from which [[brethren-of-the-coast|the Brethren]] of the Coast drew their strength.

### The Brethren of the Coast and Articles of Agreement

When Blood forms his crew, he "entered into the articles usual among [[brethren-of-the-coast|the Brethren]] of the Coast under which each man was to be paid by a share in the prizes captured" ((013-121)). However, Blood's articles differ in a crucial respect: "Aboard [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]] there was to be none of the ruffianly indiscipline that normally prevailed in [[piracy|buccaneering]] vessels" ((013-125)). Those who shipped with him "undertook obedience and submission in all things to himself and to the officers appointed by election," and any to whom this was "distasteful might follow some other leader" ((013-121)).

This modification of traditional buccaneer articles is central to Blood's success. By imposing military discipline — "that stern condition of a discipline unknown before among buccaneers" ((020-047)) — he creates a fighting force superior to any other buccaneer crew in the Caribbean. The narrator emphasizes this point with cumulative force: "Never had buccaneers been so rigidly held in hand, never had they been so firmly restrained, never so debarred from the excesses of rapine and lust that were usual in their kind as those who sailed with [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]" ((020-040)).

### Blood as the Exceptional Buccaneer

The novel consistently presents Blood as an anomaly among buccaneers. His appearance alone marks the distinction: "There was in his appearance nothing of the buccaneer. He had much more the air of a lounger in the Mall or the Alameda" ((016-132)). He is a physician and a gentleman, educated and articulate, operating in a world of rough and often brutal men. [[levasseur|Levasseur]], by contrast, is described as a "hard-drinking, hard-gaming scoundrel, his [[honour|reputation]] as a buccaneer none too good" ((014-021)), representing the more typical type.

Blood's exceptional nature is most starkly drawn when the narrator observes: "He was not, and never would be, the complete pirate. There was not another buccaneer in all the Caribbean who would have denied himself the pleasure of stringing [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] from the yardarm" ((024-014)). His restraint — his refusal to become fully the thing he appears — is what distinguishes him from the buccaneer archetype.

### The Buccaneer as Political Instrument

The buccaneers function as a political and military instrument throughout the novel's later chapters. When Blood enters the service of King Louis XIV, he [[authority|command]]s a buccaneer fleet and states: "my buccaneers number eight hundred; your troops number six hundred" ((026-205)). He adds coldly that "any one buccaneer is equal in action to at least three soldiers of the line" ((026-207)), asserting the superior fighting quality of these irregular forces.

The tension between [[piracy|buccaneering]] and legitimate service reaches its peak in Blood's confrontation with Baron de [[de-rivarol|Rivarol]]. When the French general proposes a [[treasure|plunder]]ing raid on [[cartagena|Cartagena]], Blood turns the accusation of piracy back upon him: "You, M. le Baron, General of the King's Armies by Land and Sea in America, propose an enterprise of a purely [[piracy|buccaneering]] character; whilst I, the buccaneer, am urging one that is more concerned with upholding the honour of France" ((026-345)). The irony is devastating: "It is for me, a buccaneer, a sea-robber, to stand here and tell you what is in the interest of French honour and the French [[crown|Crown]]" ((026-358)). State-sanctioned warfare, the novel suggests, is often indistinguishable from [[piracy|buccaneering]] — except in whose flag flies at the masthead.

### The Buccaneering Spirit

Despite Blood's discipline, his men retain the essential buccaneer psychology. When [[de-rivarol|Rivarol]] attempts to swindle them after [[cartagena|Cartagena]], "Among the buccaneers that night there was hilarity over the sudden abatement of [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]]'s monstrous pride" ((028-236)). Their resilience and capacity for dark humor define them as a collective.

The novel's climax demonstrates the ultimate value of the buccaneer force. After all the swindles, betrayals, and political maneuvering, "of three hundred and twenty buccaneers who had left [[cartagena|Cartagena]] with [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]], a bare hundred remained sound and whole" ((031-006)). But the sacrifice is not in vain: "with a far inferior force and by sheer skill and desperate valour, Blood's buccaneers had saved [[jamaica|Jamaica]] from bombardment and pillage" ((031-012)). The buccaneers, operating at the margins of legitimate society, prove themselves its most effective defenders.

### The Cost of Buccaneering

The novel does not romanticize [[piracy|buccaneering]] without acknowledging its costs. Blood himself, in his moment of deepest despair, "had sworn that he had done with [[piracy|buccaneering]]" ((025-251)). His love for [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] and her scorn for him as a "thief and pirate" create a profound internal conflict. The buccaneer's life is one of violence, exile, and moral compromise.

Yet the transition away from [[piracy|buccaneering]] is itself the novel's resolution. When Blood "marshalled his buccaneers and surrendered to them" ((031-103)) the prizes of his final victory, and receives the governorship of [[jamaica|Jamaica]], the buccaneer gives way to the statesman. The buccaneer era ends, for Blood and for the Caribbean, with the consolidation of European colonial authority.
