# Rebellion

## Overview

Rebellion frames the entire narrative of *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. The novel opens in the aftermath of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] (1685), the failed Protestant uprising against King James II that resulted in the "[[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]" and the transportation of hundreds of rebels to the West Indies. But [[treason|rebellion]] is not merely the backstory — it becomes the model for Blood's entire career. His piracy is a form of [[treason|rebellion]] against the Stuart regime that condemned him, and his eventual service to William of Orange completes the circle: the rebel of one reign becomes the governor of the next.

## Historical Background

The [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] was the last major attempt to overthrow a Catholic monarch in England. James Scott, Duke of Monmouth (the illegitimate son of Charles II), landed at Lyme Regis in June 1685 with a small force, claiming the throne as the Protestant heir. His army, composed largely of Dissenters and artisans, was defeated at the [[battle-of-sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]] on July 6, 1685. Monmouth was captured and executed. The subsequent "[[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]" under [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] tried approximately 1,300 rebels, sentencing about 320 to death and over 800 to transportation.

## In the Novel

### The Failed Rebellion

The novel opens with the "frenzied fanatical heat of [[treason|rebellion]]" ((001-068)) still reverberating. Blood, a physician who "knew too much about this fellow Monmouth" ((001-078)), is swept up in the aftermath despite having "never conspired, never seditious in his life" ((001-104)). The [[treason|rebellion]]'s failure creates the conditions for the entire plot: the condemned rebels become "human merchandise" in the Caribbean.

### Rebellion at Sea

Blood's turn to piracy is itself a form of [[treason|rebellion]]. Having been "seduced into [[treason|rebellion]]" ((001-104)) on land, he now rebels against Spanish authority at sea. His crew of fellow "rebels-convict" ((005-002)) follow him because he offers the only alternative to [[plantation-system|plantation]] slavery. "The turbulence" of the original [[treason|rebellion]] ((001-068)) transfers to the Caribbean waters.

### Rebellion and Legitimacy

The novel's resolution involves the ultimate inversion of [[treason|rebellion]]. William of Orange's invasion of England in 1688 — itself arguably an act of [[treason|rebellion]] against James II — legitimizes Blood's position. The "rebel" Blood becomes [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] under the new regime. The novel thus suggests that the line between [[treason|rebellion]] and legitimate authority depends entirely on who wins.
