## Overview

Ferguson serves as chaplain to the [[monmouth|Duke of Monmouth]] and appears in the novel's opening chapter as the preacher whose inflammatory sermon at Castle Field in Bridgewater helped rouse the townspeople to join the Monmouth Rebellion. His single mention establishes the religious and political ferment that forms the backdrop to [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s arrest.

## In the Novel

Ferguson is referenced in Chapter I as having "preached a sermon containing more treason than divinity" earlier in the afternoon at Castle Field ([001-044]). The sermon draws a stream of townspeople — "men with green boughs in their hats and the most ludicrous of weapons in their hands" ([001-046]) — towards the rebel cause. This atmosphere of popular enthusiasm for the rebellion sets the stage for the events that will sweep up Peter Blood, an apolitical physician who wants nothing to do with the conflict.

## Historical Basis

The character is based on Robert Ferguson (c. 1637–1714), a Scottish clergyman and political conspirator known to history as "Ferguson the Plotter." A fervent Protestant, the historical Ferguson served as chaplain to the Duke of Monmouth and was deeply involved in the Whig opposition to Charles II and James II. He participated in the Rye House Plot (1683) and accompanied Monmouth during the doomed rebellion of 1685, preaching to the rebel troops. After Sedgemoor, Ferguson escaped to the Continent and continued his conspiratorial activities in support of William of Orange.
