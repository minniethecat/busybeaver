# Cartagena

## Overview

Cartagena de Indias was the most heavily fortified Spanish port on the Spanish Main, serving as the collection point for treasure fleets bound for Spain. In *Captain Blood*, Cartagena is the target of Blood's most ambitious military operation — a combined Franco-buccaneer assault that demonstrates his strategic genius and leads to his final break with French service.

## In the Novel

### The Assault

Blood's attack on Cartagena represents the peak of his military career. The city's formidable defenses — walls, forts, and a garrison — make it a prize worthy of a commander of Blood's abilities. The assault involves coordination between Blood's buccaneers and the French fleet under M. de Rivarol.

### The Victory Stolen

Blood's capture of Cartagena is the novel's great military set-piece. But the victory is immediately soured by Rivarol, who claims credit for Blood's achievement. "The Honour of M. de Rivarol" — the novel's most ironic chapter — details how the French commander appropriates Blood's triumph, setting the stage for Blood's departure from French service.

## Significance

- Site of Blood's greatest military achievement
- Setting for the conflict with M. de Rivarol
- Represents the Spanish imperial presence in the Caribbean
