[027-001] He stalked out, and his three captains--although they thought him

[027-002] mad--rolled after him in loyal silence.

[027-003] [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]] was gasping like a landed fish. The stark truth had robbed

[027-004] him of speech. When he recovered, it was to thank Heaven vigorously that

[027-005] the council was relieved by [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]'s own act of that gentleman's

[027-006] further participation in its deliberations. Inwardly M. de Rivarol

[027-007] burned with shame and rage. The mask had been plucked from him, and he

[027-008] had been held up to scorn--he, the General of the King's Armies by Sea

[027-009] and Land in America.

[027-010] Nevertheless, it was to [[cartagena|Cartagena]] that they sailed in the middle of

[027-011] March. Volunteers and negroes had brought up the forces directly under

[027-012] M. de Rivarol to twelve hundred men. With these he thought he could keep

[027-013] the buccaneer contingent in order and submissive.

[027-014] They made up an imposing fleet, led by M. de Rivarol's flagship, the

[027-015] Victorieuse, a mighty vessel of eighty guns. Each of the four other

[027-016] French ships was at least as powerful as Blood's [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]], which was

[027-017] of forty guns. Followed the lesser buccaneer vessels, the Elizabeth,

[027-018] Lachesis, and Atropos, and a dozen frigates laden with stores, besides

[027-019] canoes and small craft in tow.

[027-020] Narrowly they missed the [[jamaica|Jamaica]] fleet with [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]], which

[027-021] sailed north for [[tortuga|Tortuga]] two days after the Baron de Rivarol's southward

[027-022] passage.

[027-023] CHAPTER XXVII. CARTAGENA

[027-024] Having crossed the Caribbean in the teeth of contrary winds, it was not

[027-025] until the early days of April that the French fleet hove in sight of

[027-026] Cartagena, and M. de Rivarol summoned a council aboard his flagship to

[027-027] determine the method of assault.

[027-028] “It is of importance, messieurs,” he told them, “that we take the city

[027-029] by surprise, not only before it can put itself into a state of defence;

[027-030] but before it can remove its treasures inland. I propose to land a

[027-031] force sufficient to achieve this to the north of the city to-night after

[027-032] dark.” And he explained in detail the scheme upon which his wits had

[027-033] laboured.

[027-034] He was heard respectfully and approvingly by his officers, scornfully

[027-035] by Captain Blood, and indifferently by the other buccaneer captains

[027-036] present. For it must be understood that Blood's refusal to attend

[027-037] councils had related only to those concerned with determining the nature

[027-038] of the enterprise to be undertaken.

[027-039] Captain Blood was the only one amongst them who knew exactly what lay

[027-040] ahead. Two years ago he had himself considered a raid upon the place,

[027-041] and he had actually made a survey of it in circumstances which he was

[027-042] presently to disclose.

[027-043] The Baron's proposal was one to be expected from a commander whose

[027-044] knowledge of Cartagena was only such as might be derived from maps.

[027-045] Geographically and strategically considered, it is a curious place. It

[027-046] stands almost four-square, screened east and north by hills, and it

[027-047] may be said to face south upon the inner of two harbours by which it

[027-048] is normally approached. The entrance to the outer harbour, which is in

[027-049] reality a lagoon some three miles across, lies through a neck known as

[027-050] the Boca Chica--or Little Mouth--and defended by a fort. A long strip of

[027-051] densely wooded land to westward acts here as a natural breakwater, and

[027-052] as the inner harbour is approached, another strip of land thrusts across

[027-053] at right angles from the first, towards the mainland on the east. Just

[027-054] short of this it ceases, leaving a deep but very narrow channel, a

[027-055] veritable gateway, into the secure and sheltered inner harbour. Another

[027-056] fort defends this second passage. East and north of Cartagena lies

[027-057] the mainland, which may be left out of account. But to the west and

[027-058] northwest this city, so well guarded on every other side, lies directly

[027-059] open to the sea. It stands back beyond a half-mile of beach, and besides

[027-060] this and the stout Walls which fortify it, would appear to have no other

[027-061] defences. But those appearances are deceptive, and they had utterly

[027-062] deceived M. de Rivarol, when he devised his plan.

[027-063] It remained for Captain Blood to explain the difficulties when M. de

[027-064] Rivarol informed him that the honour of opening the assault in the

[027-065] manner which he prescribed was to be accorded to the buccaneers.

[027-066] Captain Blood smiled sardonic appreciation of the honour reserved

[027-067] for his men. It was precisely what he would have expected. For the

[027-068] buccaneers the dangers; for M. de Rivarol the honour, glory and profit

[027-069] of the enterprise.

[027-070] “It is an honour which I must decline,” said he quite coldly.

[027-071] [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]] grunted approval and [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] nodded. Yberville, who

[027-072] as much as any of them resented the superciliousness of his noble

[027-073] compatriot, never wavered in loyalty to Captain Blood. The French

[027-074] officers--there were six of them present--stared their haughty surprise

[027-075] at the buccaneer leader, whilst the Baron challengingly fired a question

[027-076] at him.

[027-077] “How? You decline it, 'sir? You decline to obey orders, do you say?”

[027-078] “I understood, M. le Baron, that you summoned us to deliberate upon the

[027-079] means to be adopted.”

[027-080] “Then you understood amiss, M. le Capitaine. You are here to receive

[027-081] my commands. I have already deliberated, and I have decided. I hope you

[027-082] understand.”

[027-083] “Oh, I understand,” laughed Blood. “But, I ask myself, do you?” And

[027-084] without giving the Baron time to set the angry question that was

[027-085] bubbling to his lips, he swept on: “You have deliberated, you say, and

[027-086] you have decided. But unless your decision rests upon a wish to destroy

[027-087] my buccaneers, you will alter it when I tell you something of which

[027-088] I have knowledge. This city of Cartagena looks very vulnerable on

[027-089] the northern side, all open to the sea as it apparently stands. Ask

[027-090] yourself, M. le Baron, how came the Spaniards who built it where it is

[027-091] to have been at such trouble to fortify it to the south, if from the

[027-092] north it is so easily assailable.”

[027-093] That gave M. de Rivarol pause.

[027-094] “The Spaniards,” Blood pursued, “are not quite the fools you are

[027-095] supposing them. Let me tell you, messieurs, that two years ago I made a

[027-096] survey of Cartagena as a preliminary to raiding it. I came hither with

[027-097] some friendly trading Indians, myself disguised as an Indian, and in

[027-098] that guise I spent a week in the city and studied carefully all its

[027-099] approaches. On the side of the sea where it looks so temptingly open to

[027-100] assault, there is shoal water for over half a mile out--far enough out,

[027-101] I assure you, to ensure that no ship shall come within bombarding range

[027-102] of it. It is not safe to venture nearer land than three quarters of a

[027-103] mile.”

[027-104] “But our landing will be effected in canoes and piraguas and open

[027-105] boats,” cried an officer impatiently.

[027-106] “In the calmest season of the year, the surf will hinder any such

[027-107] operation. And you will also bear in mind that if landing were possible

[027-108] as you are suggesting, that landing could not be covered by the ships'

[027-109] guns. In fact, it is the landing parties would be in danger from their

[027-110] own artillery.”

[027-111] “If the attack is made by night, as I propose, covering will be

[027-112] unnecessary. You should be ashore in force before the Spaniards are

[027-113] aware of the intent.”

[027-114] “You are assuming that Cartagena is a city of the blind, that at this

[027-115] very moment they are not conning our sails and asking themselves who we

[027-116] are and what we intend.”

[027-117] “But if they feel themselves secure from the north, as you suggest,”

[027-118] cried the Baron impatiently, “that very security will lull them.”

[027-119] “Perhaps. But, then, they are secure. Any attempt to land on this side

[027-120] is doomed to failure at the hands of Nature.”

[027-121] “Nevertheless, we make the attempt,” said the obstinate Baron, whose

[027-122] haughtiness would not allow him to yield before his officers.

[027-123] “If you still choose to do so after what I have said, you are, of

[027-124] course, the person to decide. But I do not lead my men into fruitless

[027-125] danger.”

[027-126] “If I [[authority|command]] you...” the Baron was beginning. But Blood unceremoniously

[027-127] interrupted him.

[027-128] “M. le Baron, when M. de Cussy engaged us on your behalf, it was as much

[027-129] on account of our knowledge and experience of this class of warfare

[027-130] as on account of our strength. I have placed my own knowledge and

[027-131] experience in this particular matter at your disposal. I will add that

[027-132] I abandoned my own project of raiding Cartagena, not being in sufficient

[027-133] strength at the time to force the entrance of the harbour, which is the

[027-134] only way into the city. The strength which you now command is ample for

[027-135] that purpose.”

[027-136] “But whilst we are doing that, the Spaniards will have time to

[027-137] remove great part of the wealth this city holds. We must take them by

[027-138] surprise.”

[027-139] Captain Blood shrugged. “If this is a mere pirating raid, that, of

[027-140] course, is a prime consideration. It was with me. But if you are

[027-141] concerned to abate the pride of Spain and plant the Lilies of France

[027-142] on the forts of this settlement, the loss of some treasure should not

[027-143] really weigh for much.”

[027-144] M. de Rivarol bit his lip in chagrin. His gloomy eye smouldered as it

[027-145] considered the self-contained buccaneer.

[027-146] “But if I command you to go--to make the attempt?” he asked. “Answer me,

[027-147] monsieur, let us know once for all where we stand, and who commands this

[027-148] expedition.”

[027-149] “Positively, I find you tiresome,” said Captain Blood, and he swung to

[027-150] M. de Cussy, who sat there gnawing his lip, intensely uncomfortable. “I

[027-151] appeal to you, monsieur, to justify me to the General.”

[027-152] M. de Cussy started out of his gloomy abstraction. He cleared his

[027-153] throat. He was extremely nervous.

[027-154] “In view of what Captain Blood has submitted....”

[027-155] “Oh, to the devil with that!” snapped Rivarol. “It seems that I am

[027-156] followed by poltroons. Look you, M. le Capitaine, since you are afraid

[027-157] to undertake this thing, I will myself undertake it. The weather is

[027-158] calm, and I count upon making good my landing. If I do so, I shall have

[027-159] proved you wrong, and I shall have a word to say to you to-morrow which

[027-160] you may not like. I am being very generous with you, sir.” He waved his

[027-161] hand regally. “You have leave to go.”

[027-162] It was sheer obstinacy and empty pride that drove him, and he received

[027-163] the lesson he deserved. The fleet stood in during the afternoon to

[027-164] within a mile of the coast, and under cover of darkness three hundred

[027-165] men, of whom two hundred were negroes--the whole of the negro contingent

[027-166] having been pressed into the undertaking--were pulled away for the shore

[027-167] in the canoes, piraguas, and ships' boats. Rivarol's pride compelled

[027-168] him, however much he may have disliked the venture, to lead them in

[027-169] person.

[027-170] The first six boats were caught in the surf, and pounded into fragments

[027-171] before their occupants could extricate themselves. The thunder of the

[027-172] breakers and the cries of the shipwrecked warned those who followed,

[027-173] and thereby saved them from sharing the same fate. By the Baron's urgent

[027-174] orders they pulled away again out of danger, and stood about to pick

[027-175] up such survivors as contrived to battle towards them. Close upon fifty

[027-176] lives were lost in the adventure, together with half-a-dozen boats

[027-177] stored with ammunition and light guns.

[027-178] The Baron went back to his flagship an infuriated, but by no means a

[027-179] wiser man. Wisdom--not even the pungent wisdom experience thrusts upon

[027-180] us--is not for such as M. de Rivarol. His anger embraced all things, but

[027-181] focussed chiefly upon Captain Blood. In some warped process of reasoning

[027-182] he held the buccaneer chiefly responsible for this misadventure. He went

[027-183] to bed considering furiously what he should say to Captain Blood upon

[027-184] the morrow.

[027-185] He was awakened at dawn by the rolling thunder of guns. Emerging upon

[027-186] the poop in nightcap and slippers, he beheld a sight that increased his

[027-187] unreasonable and unreasoning fury. The four buccaneer ships under canvas

[027-188] were going through extraordinary manoeuvre half a mile off the Boca

[027-189] Chica and little more than half a mile away from the remainder of the

[027-190] fleet, and from their flanks flame and smoke were belching each time

[027-191] they swung broadside to the great round fort that guarded that narrow

[027-192] entrance. The fort was returning the fire vigorously and viciously. But

[027-193] the buccaneers timed their broadsides with extraordinary judgment to

[027-194] catch the defending ordnance reloading; then as they drew the Spaniards'

[027-195] fire, they swung away again not only taking care to be ever moving

[027-196] targets, but, further, to present no more than bow or stern to the fort,

[027-197] their masts in line, when the heaviest cannonades were to be expected.
