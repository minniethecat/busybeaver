# Levasseur

## Overview

Levasseur is a French buccaneer captain whose methods and character provide the novel's darkest contrast to Blood's honourable piracy. Where Blood maintains a gentleman's code and treats prisoners with dignity, Levasseur embodies the brutality that gives buccaneering its fearsome reputation. "'It is Levasseur. You may have heard of me'" ((014-013)) — his self-introduction carries both pride and menace, the swagger of a man who has built his reputation on cruelty. His conflict with Blood tests whether honour can survive in the lawless world of the Caribbean, and the answer the novel provides is unambiguous: it cannot coexist with men like Levasseur.

## In the Novel

### The Brutal Buccaneer

Levasseur's career began young — "as a lad of barely twenty, Levasseur had sailed with" the most notorious pirates of the coast ((014-039)). His methods are defined by excess: "the climax of Levasseur's project was" violence so gratuitous that even hardened buccaneers found it "repulsive" ((014-047, 014-043)). He "had gone the length of audacity" that crossed the line from piracy into sadism ((014-030)). When faced with a terrified captive, "Levasseur took him by the throat, shook" him violently ((014-133)), and "dashed one hand against the other" in frustration at any restraint ((014-137)).

### The Treacherous Ally

The "articles drawn up between Levasseur" and Blood ((014-057)) represent an alliance of convenience that cannot last. The partnership is poisoned from the start by mutual distrust: Blood's "judgment of Levasseur filled him with misgivings" ((014-093)), and Levasseur's resentment of Blood's scruples festers beneath a veneer of cooperation. Levasseur's reply to Blood's ethical objection — "'The devil take you!' Levasseur had answered him. 'A ship's a ship'" ((014-177)) — crystallizes their irreconcilable worldviews.

### The Duel

Levasseur's treachery culminates in a direct confrontation with Blood. "Levasseur made clear his purpose" through actions rather than words ((014-143)), and the resulting conflict — the novel's Chapter 14, ironically titled "Levasseur's Heroics" — ends with Levasseur's defeat. His death at Blood's hands is both a personal victory and a symbolic one: honour has prevailed over brutality, the gentleman over the savage. "Levasseur narrowly escaped being shot" earlier in his career ((014-072)), but this time his luck runs out.

### The Moral Counterpoint

Levasseur's narrative function is to make Blood's virtue visible. Blood's code — no unnecessary cruelty, honour among enemies, mercy to prisoners — is merely asserted until Levasseur demonstrates what its absence looks like. When Levasseur asks "'who the devil will deny us?'" ((014-146)), he articulates the philosophy of absolute pirate freedom that Blood has been resisting all along. Levasseur is not just Blood's rival; he is the embodiment of what Blood refuses to become.

## Character Analysis

Levasseur is the novel's most unambiguous villain, but he is not a cartoon. His brutality is grounded in a coherent worldview: the Caribbean is a lawless space, and power is the only currency that matters. His bafflement at Blood's scruples — "'who the devil will deny us?'" — is genuine, not performative. He cannot understand why a man with power would choose not to use it absolutely.

This makes him a more effective antagonist than a mere thug would be. Levasseur challenges Blood's philosophy at its foundation: if the world has no law, why observe any law? Blood's answer — because honour is its own law — is the novel's central moral claim, and it is tested most severely in his conflict with Levasseur.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — Rival and moral counterpoint; their duel is the novel's most direct confrontation between honour and brutality
- **Cahusac** — Fellow French buccaneer and ally; "Levasseur entertained his admiral to dinner" ((014-085)), suggesting a relationship built on shared appetite rather than trust
