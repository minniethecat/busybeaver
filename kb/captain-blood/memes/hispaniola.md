## Overview

Hispaniola, the second-largest island in the Caribbean, plays a recurring role in *Captain Blood* as both a geographical waypoint and a political presence. Divided between French control in the west (centered on the port of St. Nicholas) and Spanish dominion in the east, Hispaniola represents the fractured colonial landscape of the late seventeenth-century Caribbean. The island's northern coast serves as a haven for French boucanhunters (buccaneers), who form a crucial pool of recruits and allies for Blood's expeditions.

## In the Novel

### A Landmark of Navigation

Hispaniola first appears as a navigational landmark when Captain Blood confronts his Spanish prisoners. Correcting Don Diego de Espinosa's deliberately misleading navigational information, Blood points toward the island:

> "...you treacherous, forsworn Spanish dog, is the island of Hispaniola."
> ([007-07066])

This moment establishes Blood's superior knowledge of Caribbean geography — a recurring theme throughout the novel — and his refusal to be deceived by Spanish duplicity. The island's massive silhouette on the horizon serves as an unmistakable reference point for any sailor in these waters:

> Hispaniola. But all that Don Diego had accomplished was to betray the truth that he had lied.
> ([007-07100])

### The French Buccaneers

The northern coast of Hispaniola harbors a community of French boucanhunters — men who hunt wild cattle and smoke the meat on *boucans* (wooden frames). These hardy frontiersmen form the nucleus of the Caribbean buccaneering community:

> ...of French boucanhunters from Northern Hispaniola, men who had good cause to hate the Spanish...
> ([008-08334])

Blood recruits these boucanhunters to augment his forces for the Maracaybo expedition. Their presence at his side underscores the multi-national character of Caribbean buccaneering, where French, English, and Dutch freebooters coalesced into a loose confederation of anti-Spanish privateers.

### St. Nicholas — A French Port

The port of St. Nicholas on the northwest coast of Hispaniola serves as a key rendezvous point:

> ...in the French port of St. Nicholas on the northwest coast of Hispaniola.
> ([011-11794])

It is here that Blood brings the captured Spanish prize ship *Milagrosa*, and where the French colonial authorities must decide how to handle this English buccaneer operating from their territory. The French Governor of Hispaniola recognizes the strategic value of buccaneers:

> ...felt that they were to be encouraged as a check against Spanish expansion...
> ([011-11818])

This calculated tolerance by the French authorities reflects the historical reality of colonial rivalry — buccaneers, however unsavory, served as an inexpensive proxy force against Spanish hegemony.

### A Cruising Ground

Later in the narrative, Hispaniola's waters become a hunting ground as Blood and his pursuers crisscross the Caribbean:

> The hunt was up. They cruised awhile off Hispaniola, watching the narrow seas...
> ([016-16938])

French Hispaniola also figures in diplomatic maneuvers. Rumors circulate that Blood's fleet intends to:

> ...raid French Hispaniola...
> ([017-17070])

This rumor, whether true or a feint, demonstrates Hispaniola's importance as both potential target and political chess piece.

### The Governor of Hispaniola

In a late diplomatic overture, the Governor of French Hispaniola himself appears, seeking to negotiate with Captain Blood:

> "...the Governor of French Hispaniola, who desires a word with you."
> ([017-17556])

The Governor proposes to Blood a formal commission under the French flag, offering legitimacy in exchange for Blood's services against the Spanish. This scene crystallizes the novel's central tension between piracy and privateering, lawlessness and legitimacy:

> ...the Governor of Hispaniola propounded his proposal...
> ([017-17588])

## Geography

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Location** | Second-largest Caribbean island, east of Cuba, west of Puerto Rico |
| **French Hispaniola** | Western third of the island (modern Haiti), centered at St. Nicholas |
| **Spanish Hispaniola** | Eastern two-thirds (modern Dominican Republic) |
| **Northern Coast** | Boucanhunter territory, rugged frontier settled by French hunters |
| **St. Nicholas** | Principal French port on the northwest coast, buccaneer haven |

## Significance

Hispaniola serves three distinct functions in the novel:

1. **Geographical Anchor**: The island's massive presence provides a constant reference point for the novel's complex Caribbean navigation, grounding the reader in real geography
2. **Political Stage**: The French-Spanish division of Hispaniola mirrors the broader European power struggle, with buccaneers like Blood exploiting the interstices between colonial jurisdictions
3. **Source of Manpower**: The French boucanhunters of northern Hispaniola constitute the human reservoir from which Caribbean buccaneering draws its strength — tough, independent men united by their hatred of Spain
