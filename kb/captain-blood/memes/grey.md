# Lord Grey

**Lord Grey** (Ford Grey, 1st Earl of Tankerville, also known as Lord Grey of Warke) was one of the principal leaders of the [[monmouth|Monmouth Rebellion]] of 1685. His fate after the rebellion's collapse serves as a pointed illustration of the double standard of Stuart justice: while common rebels like [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] were transported into slavery, Grey purchased his own pardon for an enormous sum.

## The Monmouth Rebellion

Lord Grey was, after the [[monmouth|Duke of Monmouth]] himself, perhaps the main leader of the rebellion [003-251]. A seasoned soldier and politician, Grey commanded the rebel cavalry at the [[battle-of-sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]], where the rebel army was decisively defeated by royal forces on 6 July 1685.

> Later they heard that Lord Grey, who after the Duke — indeed, perhaps, before him — was the main leader of the rebellion, had purchased his own pardon for forty thousand pounds. [003-251]

## The Price of Rank

Grey's fate stands in stark contrast to that of the common rebels captured at Sedgemoor. While men like Peter Blood — a physician who had merely attended a wounded rebel — were sentenced to death and then transported as slaves, Grey's wealth and rank enabled him to negotiate a different outcome [003-264].

The sum of forty thousand pounds was immense — a figure that underscores both Grey's personal wealth and the venality of [[james-ii|King James II]]'s regime. Blood's reaction to the news encapsulates the bitterness of the ordinary rebel:

> Peter Blood found this of a piece with the rest. His contempt for King James blazed out at last. "Why, here's a filthy mean creature to sit on a throne." [003-251]

Blood's rhetorical question — "And where will Lord Gildoy be, do you suppose?" — highlights the inequity that Grey's pardon represents [003-258]. Lord Gildoy, a lesser nobleman, had been the wounded man Blood was arrested for treating; his fate, too, would differ from Grey's.

## Grey's Case

The narrative explicitly invokes "Grey's case" as the explanation for why certain powerful figures were absent from the ranks of the condemned [003-264]. The implication is clear: justice in Stuart England was a commodity available to those who could afford it.

## Historical Significance

In the novel, Lord Grey functions less as a character than as a moral counterpoint. He never appears directly, but his story — relayed to Blood and his fellow prisoners — crystallizes the theme of class-based injustice that drives much of the novel's political critique. Grey's purchased freedom makes Blood's enslavement all the more galling, and it fuels the contempt for authority that later defines Blood's buccaneer career.
