# Captain Hobart

## Overview

Captain Hobart is an officer in Colonel Kirke's dragoons — the regiment known as "Kirke's Lambs" — who plays a decisive role in Peter Blood's arrest and conviction. Arriving at Oglethorpe's Farm in search of fugitive rebels, Hobart's confrontation with Blood is marked by sardonic amusement and bureaucratic rigidity: he is simply doing his duty, and the fact that Blood is a non-combatant surgeon tending a wounded man makes no difference to the legal machinery he represents.

## In the Novel

Captain Hobart introduces himself at Oglethorpe's Farm with the curt declaration: "I am Captain Hobart, of Colonel Kirke's dragoons. I have orders to search this house for fugitive rebels" ([002-171]). He is immediately characterised by his reaction to Blood's explanation: "Captain Hobart was amused" — finding dark humour in the surgeon's claim that he is merely a physician attending a wounded friend ([002-185]).

When Blood warns Hobart that Lord Gildoy has influential friends who might intercede, Hobart "swept the warning aside with a gesture of complete unconcern" ([003-057]). He is not moved by the social standing of the rebels, nor by the medical ethics of Blood's errand. Hobart operates within the remorseless logic of military justice, the kind that would erect "those gallows of which Captain Hobart had spoken" without hesitation ([003-098]).

The narrator notes in a moment of grim irony that being taken prisoner by Blood's men had a "beneficial operation" on Captain Hobart — that is, the experience of being overpowered taught him something ([003-136]). His physical description — "his sapphire eyes were bright" — suggests a man who takes a certain satisfaction in the exercise of authority ([003-161]).

Hobart's most consequential role comes at Blood's trial, where he is called as "the King's witness." As the record states: "Captain Hobart testified to what he knew" ([003-358]). His testimony establishes the crucial facts: that Blood was found at Oglethorpe's Farm, that Blood was treating the wounded rebel Lord Gildoy, and that Blood knew Gildoy had been "wounded in the battle" ([003-495]). Blood's lawyer later characterises the encounter as Hobart being "cozened... with your lies" — a charge Blood answers by noting that Hobart's testimony was factually accurate: Blood was indeed treating a rebel ([003-510]).
