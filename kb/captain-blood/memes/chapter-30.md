[030-001] CHAPTER XXX. THE LAST FIGHT OF THE ARABELLA

[030-002] “VHY do you vait, my friend?” growled van der Kuylen.

[030-003] “Aye--in God's name!” snapped Willoughby.

[030-004] It was the afternoon of that same day, and the two buccaneer ships

[030-005] rocked gently with idly flapping sails under the lee of the long spit

[030-006] of land forming the great natural harbour of [[port-royal|Port Royal]], and less than a

[030-007] mile from the straits leading into it, which the fort commanded. It was

[030-008] two hours and more since they had brought up thereabouts, having crept

[030-009] thither unobserved by the city and by [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]]'s ships, and all the

[030-010] time the air had been aquiver with the roar of guns from sea and land,

[030-011] announcing that battle was joined between the French and the defenders

[030-012] of Port Royal. That long, inactive waiting was straining the nerves of

[030-013] both Lord Willoughby and van der Kuylen.

[030-014] “You said you vould show us zome vine dings. Vhere are dese vine dings?”

[030-015] Blood faced them, smiling confidently. He was arrayed for battle, in

[030-016] back-and-breast of black steel. “I'll not be trying your patience much

[030-017] longer. Indeed, I notice already a slackening in the fire. But it's this

[030-018] way, now: there's nothing at all to be gained by precipitancy, and a

[030-019] deal to be gained by delaying, as I shall show you, I hope.”

[030-020] Lord Willoughby eyed him suspiciously. “Ye think that in the meantime

[030-021] Bishop may come back or Admiral van der Kuylen's fleet appear?”

[030-022] “Sure, now, I'm thinking nothing of the kind. What I'm thinking is that

[030-023] in this engagement with the fort M. de Rivarol, who's a lubberly fellow,

[030-024] as I've reason to know, will be taking some damage that may make the

[030-025] odds a trifle more even. Sure, it'll be time enough to go forward when

[030-026] the fort has shot its bolt.”

[030-027] “Aye, aye!” The sharp approval came like a cough from the little

[030-028] Governor-General. “I perceive your object, and I believe ye're entirely

[030-029] right. Ye have the qualities of a great commander, [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]. I beg

[030-030] your pardon for having misunderstood you.”

[030-031] “And that's very handsome of your lordship. Ye see, I have some

[030-032] experience of this kind of action, and whilst I'll take any risk that

[030-033] I must, I'll take none that I needn't. But....” He broke off to

[030-034] listen. “Aye, I was right. The fire's slackening. It'll mean the end of

[030-035] Mallard's resistance in the fort. Ho there, Jeremy!”

[030-036] He leaned on the carved rail and issued orders crisply. The bo'sun's

[030-037] pipe shrilled out, and in a moment the ship that had seemed to slumber

[030-038] there, awoke to life. Came the padding of feet along the decks, the

[030-039] creaking of blocks and the hoisting of sail. The helm was put over

[030-040] hard, and in a moment they were moving, the Elizabeth following, ever in

[030-041] obedience to the signals from [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]], whilst Ogle the gunner,

[030-042] whom he had summoned, was receiving Blood's final instructions before

[030-043] plunging down to his station on the main deck.

[030-044] Within a quarter of an hour they had rounded the head, and stood in to

[030-045] the harbour mouth, within saker shot of Rivarol's three ships, to which

[030-046] they now abruptly disclosed themselves.

[030-047] Where the fort had stood they now beheld a smoking rubbish heap, and the

[030-048] victorious Frenchman with the lily standard trailing from his mastheads

[030-049] was sweeping forward to snatch the rich prize whose defences he had

[030-050] shattered.

[030-051] Blood scanned the French ships, and chuckled. The Victorieuse and the

[030-052] Medusa appeared to have taken no more than a few scars; but the third

[030-053] ship, the Baleine, listing heavily to larboard so as to keep the great

[030-054] gash in her starboard well above water, was out of account.

[030-055] “You see!” he cried to van der Kuylen, and without waiting for the

[030-056] Dutchman's approving grunt, he shouted an order: “Helm, hard-a-port!”

[030-057] The sight of that great red ship with her gilt beak-head and open

[030-058] ports swinging broadside on must have given check to Rivarol's soaring

[030-059] exultation. Yet before he could move to give an order, before he could

[030-060] well resolve what order to give, a volcano of fire and metal burst

[030-061] upon him from the buccaneers, and his decks were swept by the murderous

[030-062] scythe of the broadside. The [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] held to her course, giving place

[030-063] to the Elizabeth, which, following closely, executed the same manoeuver.

[030-064] And then whilst still the Frenchmen were confused, panic-stricken by

[030-065] an attack that took them so utterly by surprise, the Arabella had gone

[030-066] about, and was returning in her tracks, presenting now her larboard

[030-067] guns, and loosing her second broadside in the wake of the first.

[030-068] Came yet another broadside from the Elizabeth and then the Arabella's

[030-069] trumpeter sent a call across the water, which [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] perfectly

[030-070] understood.

[030-071] “On, now, Jeremy!” cried Blood. “Straight into them before they recover

[030-072] their wits. Stand by, there! Prepare to board! Hayton ... the grapnels!

[030-073] And pass the word to the gunner in the prow to fire as fast as he can

[030-074] load.”

[030-075] He discarded his feathered hat, and covered himself with a steel

[030-076] head-piece, which a negro lad brought him. He meant to lead this

[030-077] boarding-party in person. Briskly he explained himself to his

[030-078] two guests. “Boarding is our only chance here. We are too heavily

[030-079] outgunned.”

[030-080] Of this the fullest demonstration followed quickly. The Frenchmen

[030-081] having recovered their wits at last, both ships swung broadside on, and

[030-082] concentrating upon the Arabella as the nearer and heavier and therefore

[030-083] more immediately dangerous of their two opponents, volleyed upon her

[030-084] jointly at almost the same moment.

[030-085] Unlike the buccaneers, who had fired high to cripple their enemies above

[030-086] decks, the French fifed low to smash the hull of their assailant. The

[030-087] Arabella rocked and staggered under that terrific hammering, although

[030-088] [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]] kept her headed towards the French so that she should offer the

[030-089] narrowest target. For a moment she seemed to hesitate, then she plunged

[030-090] forward again, her beak-head in splinters, her forecastle smashed, and a

[030-091] gaping hole forward, that was only just above the water-line. Indeed,

[030-092] to make her safe from bilging, Blood ordered a prompt jettisoning of the

[030-093] forward guns, anchors, and water-casks and whatever else was moveable.

[030-094] Meanwhile, the Frenchmen going about, gave the like reception to the

[030-095] Elizabeth. The Arabella, indifferently served by the wind, pressed

[030-096] forward to come to grips. But before she could accomplish her object,

[030-097] the Victorieuse had loaded her starboard guns again, and pounded her

[030-098] advancing enemy with a second broadside at close quarters. Amid the

[030-099] thunder of cannon, the rending of timbers, and the screams of maimed

[030-100] men, the half-necked Arabella plunged and reeled into the cloud of smoke

[030-101] that concealed her prey, and then from Hayton went up the cry that she

[030-102] was going down by the head.

[030-103] Blood's heart stood still. And then in that very moment of his despair,

[030-104] the blue and gold flank of the Victorieuse loomed through the smoke.

[030-105] But even as he caught that enheartening glimpse he perceived, too, how

[030-106] sluggish now was their advance, and how with every second it grew more

[030-107] sluggish. They must sink before they reached her.

[030-108] Thus, with an oath, opined the Dutch Admiral, and from Lord Willoughby

[030-109] there was a word of blame for Blood's seamanship in having risked all

[030-110] upon this gambler's throw of boarding.

[030-111] “There was no other chance!” cried Blood, in broken-hearted frenzy. “If

[030-112] ye say it was desperate and foolhardy, why, so it was; but the occasion

[030-113] and the means demanded nothing less. I fail within an ace of victory.”

[030-114] But they had not yet completely failed. Hayton himself, and a score of

[030-115] sturdy rogues whom his whistle had summoned, were crouching for shelter

[030-116] amid the wreckage of the forecastle with grapnels ready. Within seven or

[030-117] eight yards of the Victorieuse, when their way seemed spent, and their

[030-118] forward deck already awash under the eyes of the jeering, cheering

[030-119] Frenchmen, those men leapt up and forward, and hurled their grapnels

[030-120] across the chasm. Of the four they flung, two reached [[levasseur|the Frenchman]]'s

[030-121] decks, and fastened there. Swift as thought itself, was then the action

[030-122] of those sturdy, experienced buccaneers. Unhesitatingly all threw

[030-123] themselves upon the chain of one of those grapnels, neglecting the

[030-124] other, and heaved upon it with all their might to warp the ships

[030-125] together. Blood, watching from his own quarter-deck, sent out his voice

[030-126] in a clarion call:

[030-127] “Musketeers to the prow!”

[030-128] The musketeers, at their station at the waist, obeyed him with the speed

[030-129] of men who know that in obedience is the only hope of life. Fifty of

[030-130] them dashed forward instantly, and from the ruins of the forecastle they

[030-131] blazed over the heads of Hayton's men, mowing down the French soldiers

[030-132] who, unable to dislodge the irons, firmly held where they had deeply

[030-133] bitten into the timbers of the Victorieuse, were themselves preparing to

[030-134] fire upon the grapnel crew.

[030-135] Starboard to starboard the two ships swung against each other with a

[030-136] jarring thud. By then Blood was down in the waist, judging and acting

[030-137] with the hurricane speed the occasion demanded. Sail had been lowered

[030-138] by slashing away the ropes that held the yards. The advance guard of

[030-139] boarders, a hundred strong, was ordered to the poop, and his grapnel-men

[030-140] were posted, and prompt to obey his [[authority|command]] at the very moment of

[030-141] impact. As a result, the foundering Arabella was literally kept afloat

[030-142] by the half-dozen grapnels that in an instant moored her firmly to the

[030-143] Victorieuse.

[030-144] Willoughby and van der Kuylen on the poop had watched in breathless

[030-145] amazement the speed and precision with which Blood and his desperate

[030-146] crew had gone to work. And now he came racing up, his bugler sounding

[030-147] the charge, the main host of the buccaneers following him, whilst the

[030-148] vanguard, led by the gunner Ogle, who had been driven from his guns by

[030-149] water in the gun-deck, leapt shouting to the prow of the Victorieuse, to

[030-150] whose level the high poop of the water-logged Arabella had sunk. Led now

[030-151] by Blood himself, they launched themselves upon the French like hounds

[030-152] upon the stag they have brought to bay. After them went others, until

[030-153] all had gone, and none but Willoughby and the Dutchman were left to

[030-154] watch the fight from the quarter-deck of the abandoned Arabella.

[030-155] For fully half-an-hour that battle raged aboard the Frenchman. Beginning

[030-156] in the prow, it surged through the forecastle to the waist, where it

[030-157] reached a climax of fury. The French resisted stubbornly, and they had

[030-158] the advantage of numbers to encourage them. But for all their stubborn

[030-159] valour, they ended by being pressed back and back across the decks that

[030-160] were dangerously canted to starboard by the pull of the water-logged

[030-161] Arabella. The buccaneers fought with the desperate fury of men who know

[030-162] that retreat is impossible, for there was no ship to which they could

[030-163] retreat, and here they must prevail and make the Victorieuse their own,

[030-164] or perish.

[030-165] And their own they made her in the end, and at a cost of nearly half

[030-166] their numbers. Driven to the quarter-deck, the surviving defenders,

[030-167] urged on by the infuriated Rivarol, maintained awhile their desperate

[030-168] resistance. But in the end, Rivarol went down with a bullet in his head,

[030-169] and the French remnant, numbering scarcely a score of whole men, called

[030-170] for quarter.

[030-171] Even then the labours of Blood's men were not at an end. The Elizabeth

[030-172] and the Medusa were tight-locked, and Hagthorpe's followers were being

[030-173] driven back aboard their own ship for the second time. Prompt measures

[030-174] were demanded. Whilst Pitt and his seamen bore their part with the

[030-175] sails, and Ogle went below with a gun-crew, Blood ordered the grapnels

[030-176] to be loosed at once. Lord Willoughby and the Admiral were already

[030-177] aboard the Victorieuse. As they swung off to the rescue of Hagthorpe,

[030-178] Blood, from the quarter-deck of the conquered vessel, looked his last

[030-179] upon the ship that had served him so well, the ship that had become to

[030-180] him almost as a part of himself. A moment she rocked after her release,

[030-181] then slowly and gradually settled down, the water gurgling and eddying

[030-182] about her topmasts, all that remained visible to mark the spot where she

[030-183] had met her death.

[030-184] As he stood there, above the ghastly shambles in the waist of the

[030-185] Victorieuse, some one spoke behind him. “I think, Captain Blood, that it

[030-186] is necessary I should beg your pardon for the second time. Never before

[030-187] have I seen the impossible made possible by resource and valour, or

[030-188] victory so gallantly snatched from defeat.”

[030-189] He turned, and presented to Lord Willoughby a formidable front. His

[030-190] head-piece was gone, his breastplate dinted, his right sleeve a rag

[030-191] [[execution|hanging]] from his shoulder about a naked arm. He was splashed from head

[030-192] to foot with blood, and there was blood from a scalp-wound that he had

[030-193] taken matting his hair and mixing with the grime of powder on his face

[030-194] to render him unrecognizable.
