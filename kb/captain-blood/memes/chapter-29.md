[029-005] CHAPTER XXIX. THE SERVICE OF KING WILLIAM

[029-006] One of the boats bumped alongside [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]], and up the entrance

[029-007] ladder came first a slight, spruce little gentleman in a coat of

[029-008] mulberry satin laced with gold, whose wizened, yellow, rather peevish

[029-009] face was framed in a heavy black periwig. His modish and costly apparel

[029-010] had nowise suffered by the adventure through which he had passed, and

[029-011] he carried himself with the easy assurance of a man of rank. Here, quite

[029-012] clearly, was no buccaneer. He was closely followed by one who in every

[029-013] particular, save that of age, was his physical opposite, corpulent in

[029-014] a brawny, vigorous way, with a full, round, weather-beaten face whose

[029-015] mouth was humourous and whose eyes were blue and twinkling. He was

[029-016] well dressed without fripperies, and bore with him an air of vigorous

[029-017] authority.

[029-018] As the little man stepped from the ladder into the waist, whither

[029-019] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] had gone to receive him, his sharp, ferrety dark eyes

[029-020] swept the uncouth ranks of the assembled crew of the [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]].

[029-021] “And where the devil may I be now?” he demanded irritably. “Are you

[029-022] English, or what the devil are you?”

[029-023] “Myself, I have the honour to be Irish, sir. My name is Blood--Captain

[029-024] Peter Blood, and this is my ship the Arabella, all very much at your

[029-025] service.

[029-026] “Blood!” shrilled the little man. “O 'Sblood! A pirate!” He swung to

[029-027] the Colossus who followed him--“A damned pirate, van der Kuylen. Rend my

[029-028] vitals, but we're come from Scylla to Charybdis.”

[029-029] “So?” said the other gutturally, and again, “So?” Then the humour of it

[029-030] took him, and he yielded to it.

[029-031] “Damme! What's to laugh at, you porpoise?” spluttered mulberry-coat. “A

[029-032] fine tale this'll make at home! Admiral van der Kuylen first loses his

[029-033] fleet in the night, then has his flagship fired under him by a French

[029-034] squadron, and ends all by being captured by a pirate. I'm glad you find

[029-035] it matter for laughter. Since for my sins I happen to be with you, I'm

[029-036] damned if I do.”

[029-037] “There's a misapprehension, if I may make so bold as to point it out,”

[029-038] put in Blood quietly. “You are not captured, gentlemen; you are rescued.

[029-039] When you realize it, perhaps it will occur to you to acknowledge the

[029-040] hospitality I am offering you. It may be poor, but it is the best at my

[029-041] disposal.”

[029-042] The fierce little gentleman stared at him. “Damme! Do you permit

[029-043] yourself to be ironical?” he disapproved him, and possibly with a view

[029-044] to correcting any such tendency, proceeded to introduce himself. “I am

[029-045] Lord Willoughby, King William's Governor-General of the West Indies, and

[029-046] this is Admiral van der Kuylen, commander of His Majesty's West Indian

[029-047] fleet, at present mislaid somewhere in this damned Caribbean Sea.”

[029-048] “King William?” quoth Blood, and he was conscious that [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]] and Dyke,

[029-049] who were behind him, now came edging nearer, sharing his own wonder.

[029-050] “And who may be King William, and of what may he be King?”

[029-051] “What's that?” In a wonder greater than his own, Lord Willoughby

[029-052] stared back at him. At last: “I am alluding to His Majesty King William

[029-053] III--William of Orange--who, with Queen Mary, has been ruling England

[029-054] for two months and more.”

[029-055] There was a moment's silence, until Blood realized what he was being

[029-056] told.

[029-057] “D'ye mean, sir, that they've roused themselves at home, and kicked out

[029-058] that scoundrel James and his gang of ruffians?”

[029-059] Admiral van der Kuylen nudged his lordship, a humourous twinkle in his

[029-060] blue eyes.

[029-061] “His bolitics are fery sound, I dink,” he growled.

[029-062] His lordship's smile brought lines like gashes into his leathery cheeks.

[029-063] “'Slife! hadn't you heard? Where the devil have you been at all?”

[029-064] “Out of touch with the world for the last three months,” said Blood.

[029-065] “Stab me! You must have been. And in that three months the world has

[029-066] undergone some changes.” Briefly he added an account of them. King

[029-067] James was fled to France, and living under the protection of King Louis,

[029-068] wherefore, and for other reasons, England had joined the league against

[029-069] her, and was now at war with France. That was how it happened that the

[029-070] Dutch Admiral's flagship had been attacked by [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]]'s fleet

[029-071] that morning, from which it clearly followed that in his voyage from

[029-072] [[cartagena|Cartagena]], [[levasseur|the Frenchman]] must have spoken some ship that gave him the

[029-073] news.

[029-074] After that, with renewed assurances that aboard his ship they should

[029-075] be honourably entreated, Captain Blood led the Governor-General and the

[029-076] Admiral to his cabin, what time the work of rescue went on. The news

[029-077] he had received had set Blood's mind in a turmoil. If King James was

[029-078] dethroned and banished, there was an end to his own outlawry for his

[029-079] alleged share in an earlier attempt to drive out that tyrant. It became

[029-080] possible for him to return home and take up his life again at the point

[029-081] where it was so unfortunately interrupted four years ago. He was dazzled

[029-082] by the prospect so abruptly opened out to him. The thing so filled his

[029-083] mind, moved him so deeply, that he must afford it expression. In doing

[029-084] so, he revealed of himself more than he knew or intended to the astute

[029-085] little gentleman who watched him so keenly the while.

[029-086] “Go home, if you will,” said his lordship, when Blood paused. “You

[029-087] may be sure that none will harass you on the score of your piracy,

[029-088] considering what it was that drove you to it. But why be in haste? We

[029-089] have heard of you, to be sure, and we know of what you are capable upon

[029-090] the seas. Here is a great chance for you, since you declare yourself

[029-091] sick of piracy. Should you choose to serve King William out here during

[029-092] this war, your knowledge of the West Indies should render you a very

[029-093] valuable servant to His Majesty's Government, which you would not find

[029-094] ungrateful. You should consider it. Damme, sir, I repeat: it is a great

[029-095] chance you are given.

[029-096] “That your lordship gives me,” Blood amended, “I am very grateful. But

[029-097] at the moment, I confess, I can consider nothing but this great news. It

[029-098] alters the shape of the world. I must accustom myself to view it as it

[029-099] now is, before I can determine my own place in it.”

[029-100] Pitt came in to report that the work of rescue was at an end, and the

[029-101] men picked up--some forty-five in all--safe aboard the two buccaneer

[029-102] ships. He asked for orders. Blood rose.

[029-103] “I am negligent of your lordship's concerns in my consideration of my

[029-104] own. You'll be wishing me to land you at [[port-royal|Port Royal]].”

[029-105] “At Port Royal?” The little man squirmed wrathfully on his seat.

[029-107] Royal last evening to find its Deputy-Governor absent. “He had gone on

[029-108] some wild-goose chase to [[tortuga|Tortuga]] after buccaneers, taking the whole of

[029-109] the fleet with him.”

[029-110] Blood stared in surprise a moment; then yielded to laughter.

[029-111] “He went, I suppose, before news reached him of the change of government

[029-112] at home, and the war with France?”

[029-113] “He did not,” snapped Willoughby. “He was informed of both, and also of

[029-114] my coming before he set out.”

[029-115] “Oh, impossible!”

[029-116] “So I should have thought. But I have the information from a Major

[029-117] Mallard whom I found in Port Royal, apparently governing in this fool's

[029-118] absence.”

[029-119] “But is he mad, to leave his post at such a time?” Blood was amazed.

[029-120] “Taking the whole fleet with him, pray remember, and leaving the place

[029-121] open to French attack. That is the sort of Deputy-Governor that the late

[029-122] Government thought fit to appoint: an epitome of its misrule, damme!

[029-123] He leaves Port Royal unguarded save by a ramshackle fort that can be

[029-124] reduced to rubble in an hour. Stab me! It's unbelievable!”

[029-125] The lingering smile faded from Blood's face. “Is Rivarol aware of this?”

[029-126] he cried sharply.

[029-127] It was the Dutch Admiral who answered him. “Vould he go dere if he were

[029-128] not? M. de Rivarol he take some of our men prisoners. Berhabs dey dell

[029-129] him. Berhabs he make dem tell. Id is a great obbordunidy.”

[029-130] His lordship snarled like a mountain-cat. “That rascal Bishop shall

[029-131] answer for it with his head if there's any mischief done through this

[029-132] desertion of his post. What if it were deliberate, eh? What if he is

[029-133] more knave than fool? What if this is his way of serving King James,

[029-134] from whom he held his office?”

[029-135] Captain Blood was generous. “Hardly so much. It was just vindictiveness

[029-136] that urged him. It's myself he's hunting at Tortuga, my lord. But, I'm

[029-137] thinking that while he's about it, I'd best be looking after [[jamaica|Jamaica]] for

[029-138] King William.” He laughed, with more mirth than he had used in the last

[029-139] two months.

[029-140] “Set a course for Port Royal, Jeremy, and make all speed. We'll be

[029-141] level yet with M. de Rivarol, and wipe off some other scores at the same

[029-142] time.”

[029-143] Both Lord Willoughby and the Admiral were on their feet.

[029-144] “But you are not equal to it, damme!” cried his lordship. “Any one of

[029-145] the Frenchman's three ships is a match for both yours, my man.”

[029-146] “In guns--aye,” said Blood, and he smiled. “But there's more than guns

[029-147] that matter in these affairs. If your lordship would like to see

[029-148] an action fought at sea as an action should be fought, this is your

[029-149] opportunity.”

[029-150] Both stared at him. “But the odds!” his lordship insisted.

[029-151] “Id is imbossible,” said van der Kuylen, shaking his great head.

[029-152] “Seamanship is imbordand. Bud guns is guns.”

[029-153] “If I can't defeat him, I can sink my own ships in the channel, and

[029-154] block him in until Bishop gets back from his wild-goose chase with his

[029-155] squadron, or until your own fleet turns up.”

[029-156] “And what good will that be, pray?” demanded Willoughby.

[029-157] “I'll be after telling you. Rivarol is a fool to take this chance,

[029-159] plundered from Cartagena, amounting to forty million livres.” They

[029-160] jumped at the mention of that colossal sum. “He has gone into Port Royal

[029-161] with it. Whether he defeats me or not, he doesn't come out of Port Royal

[029-162] with it again, and sooner or later that treasure shall find its way into

[029-163] King William's coffers, after, say, one fifth share shall have been paid

[029-165] His lordship stood up, and shaking back the cloud of lace from his

[029-166] wrist, held out a delicate white hand.

[029-167] “Captain Blood, I discover greatness in you,” said he.

[029-168] “Sure it's your lordship has the fine sight to perceive it,” laughed the

[029-169] Captain.

[029-170] “Yes, yes! Bud how vill you do id?” growled van der Kuylen.

[029-171] “Come on deck, and it's a demonstration I'll be giving you before the

[029-173] CHAPTER XXX. THE LAST FIGHT OF THE ARABELLA

[029-174] “VHY do you vait, my friend?” growled van der Kuylen.

[029-175] “Aye--in God's name!” snapped Willoughby.

[029-176] It was the afternoon of that same day, and the two buccaneer ships

[029-177] rocked gently with idly flapping sails under the lee of the long spit

[029-178] of land forming the great natural harbour of Port Royal, and less than a

[029-179] mile from the straits leading into it, which the fort commanded. It was

[029-180] two hours and more since they had brought up thereabouts, having crept

[029-181] thither unobserved by the city and by M. de Rivarol's ships, and all the

[029-182] time the air had been aquiver with the roar of guns from sea and land,

[029-183] announcing that battle was joined between the French and the defenders

[029-184] of Port Royal. That long, inactive waiting was straining the nerves of

[029-185] both Lord Willoughby and van der Kuylen.

[029-186] “You said you vould show us zome vine dings. Vhere are dese vine dings?”

[029-187] Blood faced them, smiling confidently. He was arrayed for battle, in

[029-188] back-and-breast of black steel. “I'll not be trying your patience much

[029-189] longer. Indeed, I notice already a slackening in the fire. But it's this

[029-190] way, now: there's nothing at all to be gained by precipitancy, and a

[029-191] deal to be gained by delaying, as I shall show you, I hope.”

[029-192] Lord Willoughby eyed him suspiciously. “Ye think that in the meantime

[029-193] Bishop may come back or Admiral van der Kuylen's fleet appear?”

[029-194] “Sure, now, I'm thinking nothing of the kind. What I'm thinking is that

[029-195] in this engagement with the fort M. de Rivarol, who's a lubberly fellow,

[029-196] as I've reason to know, will be taking some damage that may make the

[029-197] odds a trifle more even. Sure, it'll be time enough to go forward when

[029-198] the fort has shot its bolt.”

[029-199] “Aye, aye!” The sharp approval came like a cough from the little

[029-200] Governor-General. “I perceive your object, and I believe ye're entirely

[029-201] right. Ye have the qualities of a great commander, Captain Blood. I beg

[029-202] your pardon for having misunderstood you.”

[029-203] “And that's very handsome of your lordship. Ye see, I have some

[029-204] experience of this kind of action, and whilst I'll take any risk that

[029-205] I must, I'll take none that I needn't. But....” He broke off to

[029-206] listen. “Aye, I was right. The fire's slackening. It'll mean the end of

[029-207] Mallard's resistance in the fort. Ho there, Jeremy!”

[029-208] He leaned on the carved rail and issued orders crisply. The bo'sun's

[029-209] pipe shrilled out, and in a moment the ship that had seemed to slumber

[029-210] there, awoke to life. Came the padding of feet along the decks, the

[029-211] creaking of blocks and the hoisting of sail. The helm was put over

[029-212] hard, and in a moment they were moving, the Elizabeth following, ever in

[029-213] obedience to the signals from the Arabella, whilst Ogle the gunner,

[029-214] whom he had summoned, was receiving Blood's final instructions before

[029-215] plunging down to his station on the main deck.

[029-216] Within a quarter of an hour they had rounded the head, and stood in to

[029-217] the harbour mouth, within saker shot of Rivarol's three ships, to which

[029-218] they now abruptly disclosed themselves.

[029-219] Where the fort had stood they now beheld a smoking rubbish heap, and the

[029-220] victorious Frenchman with the lily standard trailing from his mastheads

[029-221] was sweeping forward to snatch the rich prize whose defences he had

[029-222] shattered.

[029-223] Blood scanned the French ships, and chuckled. The Victorieuse and the

[029-224] Medusa appeared to have taken no more than a few scars; but the third

[029-225] ship, the Baleine, listing heavily to larboard so as to keep the great

[029-226] gash in her starboard well above water, was out of account.

[029-227] “You see!” he cried to van der Kuylen, and without waiting for the

[029-228] Dutchman's approving grunt, he shouted an order: “Helm, hard-a-port!”

[029-229] The sight of that great red ship with her gilt beak-head and open

[029-230] ports swinging broadside on must have given check to Rivarol's soaring

[029-231] exultation. Yet before he could move to give an order, before he could

[029-232] well resolve what order to give, a volcano of fire and metal burst

[029-233] upon him from the buccaneers, and his decks were swept by the murderous

[029-234] scythe of the broadside. The Arabella held to her course, giving place

[029-235] to the Elizabeth, which, following closely, executed the same manoeuver.

[029-236] And then whilst still the Frenchmen were confused, panic-stricken by

[029-237] an attack that took them so utterly by surprise, the Arabella had gone

[029-238] about, and was returning in her tracks, presenting now her larboard

[029-239] guns, and loosing her second broadside in the wake of the first.

[029-240] Came yet another broadside from the Elizabeth and then the Arabella's

[029-241] trumpeter sent a call across the water, which [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] perfectly

[029-242] understood.

[029-243] “On, now, Jeremy!” cried Blood. “Straight into them before they recover

[029-244] their wits. Stand by, there! Prepare to board! Hayton ... the grapnels!

[029-245] And pass the word to the gunner in the prow to fire as fast as he can

[029-246] load.”

[029-247] He discarded his feathered hat, and covered himself with a steel

[029-248] head-piece, which a negro lad brought him. He meant to lead this

[029-249] boarding-party in person. Briskly he explained himself to his

[029-250] two guests. “Boarding is our only chance here. We are too heavily

[029-251] outgunned.”

[029-252] Of this the fullest demonstration followed quickly. The Frenchmen

[029-253] having recovered their wits at last, both ships swung broadside on, and

[029-254] concentrating upon the Arabella as the nearer and heavier and therefore

[029-255] more immediately dangerous of their two opponents, volleyed upon her

[029-256] jointly at almost the same moment.

[029-257] Unlike the buccaneers, who had fired high to cripple their enemies above

[029-258] decks, the French fifed low to smash the hull of their assailant. The

[029-259] Arabella rocked and staggered under that terrific hammering, although

[029-260] Pitt kept her headed towards the French so that she should offer the

[029-261] narrowest target. For a moment she seemed to hesitate, then she plunged

[029-262] forward again, her beak-head in splinters, her forecastle smashed, and a

[029-263] gaping hole forward, that was only just above the water-line. Indeed,

[029-264] to make her safe from bilging, Blood ordered a prompt jettisoning of the

[029-265] forward guns, anchors, and water-casks and whatever else was moveable.

[029-266] Meanwhile, the Frenchmen going about, gave the like reception to the

[029-267] Elizabeth. The Arabella, indifferently served by the wind, pressed

[029-268] forward to come to grips. But before she could accomplish her object,

[029-269] the Victorieuse had loaded her starboard guns again, and pounded her

[029-270] advancing enemy with a second broadside at close quarters. Amid the

[029-275] Blood's heart stood still. And then in that very moment of his despair,

[029-276] the blue and gold flank of the Victorieuse loomed through the smoke.

[029-277] But even as he caught that enheartening glimpse he perceived, too, how

[029-278] sluggish now was their advance, and how with every second it grew more

[029-279] sluggish. They must sink before they reached her.

[029-295] themselves upon the chain of one of those grapnels, neglecting the

[029-296] other, and heaved upon it with all their might to warp the ships

[029-298] in a clarion call:

[029-299] “Musketeers to the prow!”

[029-300] The musketeers, at their station at the waist, obeyed him with the speed

[029-305] bitten into the timbers of the Victorieuse, were themselves preparing to

[029-307] Starboard to starboard the two ships swung against each other with a

[029-308] jarring thud. By then Blood was down in the waist, judging and acting

[029-311] boarders, a hundred strong, was ordered to the poop, and his grapnel-men

[029-313] impact. As a result, the foundering Arabella was literally kept afloat

[029-315] Victorieuse.

[029-316] Willoughby and van der Kuylen on the poop had watched in breathless

[029-327] For fully half-an-hour that battle raged aboard the Frenchman. Beginning

[029-328] in the prow, it surged through the forecastle to the waist, where it

[029-329] reached a climax of fury. The French resisted stubbornly, and they had

[029-332] were dangerously canted to starboard by the pull of the water-logged

[029-333] Arabella. The buccaneers fought with the desperate fury of men who know

[029-334] that retreat is impossible, for there was no ship to which they could

[029-335] retreat, and here they must prevail and make the Victorieuse their own,

[029-336] or perish.

[029-339] urged on by the infuriated Rivarol, maintained awhile their desperate

[029-340] resistance. But in the end, Rivarol went down with a bullet in his head,

[029-342] for quarter.

[029-343] Even then the labours of Blood's men were not at an end. The Elizabeth

[029-344] and the Medusa were tight-locked, and Hagthorpe's followers were being

[029-345] driven back aboard their own ship for the second time. Prompt measures

[029-346] were demanded. Whilst Pitt and his seamen bore their part with the

[029-347] sails, and Ogle went below with a gun-crew, Blood ordered the grapnels

[029-348] to be loosed at once. Lord Willoughby and the Admiral were already

[029-349] aboard the Victorieuse. As they swung off to the rescue of Hagthorpe,

[029-350] Blood, from the quarter-deck of the conquered vessel, looked his last

[029-351] upon the ship that had served him so well, the ship that had become to

[029-352] him almost as a part of himself. A moment she rocked after her release,

[029-353] then slowly and gradually settled down, the water gurgling and eddying

[029-354] about her topmasts, all that remained visible to mark the spot where she

[029-355] had met her death.

[029-356] As he stood there, above the ghastly shambles in the waist of the

[029-357] Victorieuse, some one spoke behind him. “I think, Captain Blood, that it

[029-358] is necessary I should beg your pardon for the second time. Never before

[029-359] have I seen the impossible made possible by resource and valour, or

[029-360] victory so gallantly snatched from defeat.”

[029-361] He turned, and presented to Lord Willoughby a formidable front. His

[029-362] head-piece was gone, his breastplate dinted, his right sleeve a rag

[029-363] [[execution|hanging]] from his shoulder about a naked arm. He was splashed from head
