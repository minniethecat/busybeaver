# Captain Gardner

**Captain Gardner** is the master of the *Jamaica Merchant*, the slave ship that transports [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] and the convicted Monmouth rebels from England to Barbados. A pragmatic merchant captain, Gardner appears in the early chapters as a figure who, while complicit in the brutal economics of the slave trade, displays occasional glimmers of professional regard for Blood's abilities.

## In the Novel

### The Voyage to Barbados

Gardner first encounters Peter Blood during the Atlantic crossing. As the *Jamaica Merchant* carries its cargo of convicted rebels to the West Indian plantations, the ship suffers the common affliction of slave transports: heavy losses among the human cargo from disease and harsh conditions. Gardner sees that he might face accountability for these losses, which prompts him to make a practical decision [004-109].

> Captain Gardner came to see that he might be brought to task for these too heavy losses of human merchandise, and because of this he was belatedly glad to avail himself of the skill of Peter Blood. [004-109, 004-111]

Recognizing Blood's medical expertise, Gardner permits him access to the medicine chest and grants him leave to minister to the sick prisoners [004-108]. This pragmatic concession — motivated by commercial rather than humanitarian concerns — proves crucial in preserving the lives of several rebels who would later form Blood's buccaneer crew.

### At the Bridgetown Auction

Upon arrival at Bridgetown, Gardner's role shifts from transporter to auction master. Colonel Bishop, the Deputy-Governor of Barbados, comes aboard to inspect the human merchandise. Bishop beckons Gardner forward and reviews a list of the prisoners with him for some minutes [004-181, 004-183].

> Then he beckoned forward Captain Gardner, the master of the Jamaica Merchant, and for some minutes stood in talk with him over a list which the latter produced at his request. [004-181, 004-183]

During the auction itself, Gardner serves as Bishop's intermediary. When Bishop inspects [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]], the Colonel speaks to Gardner over his shoulder [005-007], evaluating the young man's physical condition and offering a price. Gardner breaks into protestations when Bishop undervalues Pitt, arguing for his health, youth, and vigour [005-015]. His advocacy, however, is couched entirely in commercial terms — Pitt is discussed not as a man but as a beast of burden [005-016].

> Gardner broke into protestations of Pitt's health, youth, and vigour. It was not a man he was discussing; it was a beast of burden. [005-015, 005-016]

When Bishop dismisses Gardner's protests with finality, Gardner sighs and recognizes the futility of further argument. He moves on as Bishop proceeds down the line of prisoners [005-029, 005-030].

### Vouching for Blood

The most significant moment of Gardner's appearance comes when Arabella Bishop takes an interest in Peter Blood. After Colonel Bishop dismisses Blood as "a bag of bones" unworthy of purchase [005-063, 005-064], he is turning away when Gardner interposes:

> He was turning away when Gardner interposed. "He maybe lean, but he's tough; tough and healthy. When half of them was sick of a fever and the rest was sick of fear of the fever, this one carried on as brisk and confident as a surgeon at a levee." [005-065, 005-066]

This unexpected intervention — Gardner vouching for Blood's physical resilience and steady character — is one of the few moments in the auction scene where a prisoner is spoken of with something approaching respect. It is telling that Gardner's praise is earned: Blood's conduct during the voyage, tending the sick with professional competence, has impressed the pragmatic sea captain.

### Making the Announcement

After Bishop has selected his purchases, Gardner makes a loud announcement to the remaining buyers who had waited for the Deputy-Governor to take his first choice [005-048, 005-049]. He opens the auction to the general public, and the scene that follows — where Blood is ultimately purchased by Colonel Bishop at Arabella's insistence — is facilitated by Gardner's role as auctioneer.

## Character Assessment

Captain Gardner is a minor but carefully drawn figure who embodies the moral ambiguities of the slave trade. He is neither cruel nor compassionate — he is a practical man of commerce. His decision to let Blood practice medicine aboard the *Jamaica Merchant* is motivated by the desire to preserve valuable cargo, not by humanitarian impulse. Yet his unsolicited testimony to Blood's toughness at the auction goes beyond mere commercial interest and suggests that he has developed a genuine, if grudging, respect for the Irish physician.

Gardner's brief appearance serves several narrative functions: he establishes the grim economics of transportation that the rebels endure, he provides the mechanism by which Blood's medical skill first becomes known in the Caribbean, and his vouching for Blood's character lends an external validation that subtly counters Bishop's contemptuous dismissal.
