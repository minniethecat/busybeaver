[024-001] CHAPTER XXIV. WAR

[024-002] Five miles out at sea from [[port-royal|Port Royal]], whence the details of the coast

[024-003] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] were losing their sharpness, [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]] hove to, and the

[024-004] sloop she had been towing was warped alongside.

[024-005] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] escorted his compulsory guest to the head of the ladder.

[024-006] [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]], who for two hours and more had been in a state of mortal

[024-007] anxiety, breathed freely at last; and as the tide of his fears receded,

[024-008] so that of his deep-rooted hate of this audacious buccaneer resumed its

[024-009] normal flow. But he practised circumspection. If in his heart he vowed

[024-010] that once back in Port Royal there was no effort he would spare, no

[024-011] [[courage|nerve]] he would not strain, to bring Peter Blood to final moorings in

[024-012] [[execution|Execution]] Dock, at least he kept that vow strictly to himself.

[024-013] Peter Blood had no illusions. He was not, and never would be, the

[024-014] complete pirate. There was not another buccaneer in all the Caribbean

[024-015] who would have denied himself the pleasure of stringing Colonel Bishop

[024-016] from the yardarm, and by thus finally stifling the vindictive planter's

[024-017] hatred have increased his own security. But Blood was not of these.

[024-018] Moreover, in the case of Colonel Bishop there was a particular reason

[024-019] for restraint. Because he was [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]]'s uncle, his life must

[024-020] remain sacred to Captain Blood.

[024-021] And so the Captain smiled into the sallow, bloated face and the little

[024-022] eyes that fixed him with a malevolence not to be dissembled.

[024-023] “A safe voyage home to you, Colonel, darling,” said he in valediction,

[024-024] and from his easy, smiling manner you would never have dreamt of the

[024-025] pain he carried in his breast. “It's the second time ye've served me for

[024-026] a hostage. Ye'll be well advised to avoid a third. I'm not lucky to you,

[024-027] Colonel, as you should be perceiving.”

[024-028] [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]], the master, lounging at Blood's elbow, looked darkly upon

[024-029] the departure of the Deputy-Governor. Behind them a little mob of grim,

[024-030] stalwart, sun-tanned buccaneers were restrained from cracking Bishop

[024-031] like a flea only by their submission to the dominant will of their

[024-032] leader. They had learnt from Pitt while yet in Port Royal of their

[024-033] Captain's danger, and whilst as ready as he to throw over the King's

[024-034] service which had been thrust upon them, yet they resented the manner

[024-035] in which this had been rendered necessary, and they marvelled now at

[024-036] Blood's restraint where Bishop was concerned. The Deputy-Governor

[024-037] looked round and met the lowering hostile glances of those fierce eyes.

[024-038] Instinct warned him that his life at that moment was held precariously,

[024-039] that an injudicious word might precipitate an explosion of hatred from

[024-040] which no human [[authority|power]] could save him. Therefore he said nothing. He

[024-041] inclined his head in silence to the Captain, and went blundering and

[024-042] stumbling in his haste down that ladder to the sloop and its waiting

[024-043] negro crew.

[024-044] They pushed off the craft from the red hull of the Arabella, bent to

[024-045] their sweeps, then, hoisting sail, headed back for Port Royal, intent

[024-046] upon reaching it before darkness should come down upon them. And Bishop,

[024-047] the great bulk of him huddled in the stern sheets, sat silent, his black

[024-048] brows knitted, his coarse lips pursed, malevolence and vindictiveness

[024-049] so whelming now his recent panic that he forgot his near escape of the

[024-050] yardarm and the running noose.

[024-051] On the mole at Port Royal, under the low, embattled wall of the fort,

[024-052] Major Mallard and [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]] waited to receive him, and it was with

[024-053] infinite relief that they assisted him from the sloop.

[024-054] Major Mallard was disposed to be apologetic.

[024-055] “Glad to see you safe, sir,” said he. “I'd have sunk Blood's ship in

[024-056] spite of your excellency's being aboard but for your own orders by Lord

[024-057] Julian, and his lordship's assurance that he had Blood's word for

[024-058] it that no harm should come to you so that no harm came to him. I'll

[024-059] confess I thought it rash of his lordship to accept the word of a damned

[024-060] pirate....”

[024-061] “I have found it as good as another's,” said his lordship, cropping the

[024-062] Major's too eager eloquence. He spoke with an unusual degree of that

[024-063] frosty dignity he could assume upon occasion. The fact is that his

[024-064] lordship was in an exceedingly bad humour. Having written jubilantly

[024-065] home to the Secretary of State that his mission had succeeded, he was

[024-066] now faced with the necessity of writing again to confess that this

[024-067] success had been ephemeral. And because Major Mallard's crisp mostachios

[024-068] were lifted by a sneer at the notion of a buccaneer's word being

[024-069] acceptable, he added still more sharply: “My justification is here in

[024-070] the person of Colonel Bishop safely returned. As against that, sir, your

[024-071] opinion does not weigh for very much. You should realize it.”

[024-072] “Oh, as your lordship says.” Major Mallard's manner was tinged with

[024-073] irony. “To be sure, here is the Colonel safe and sound. And out yonder

[024-074] is Captain Blood, also safe and sound, to begin his piratical ravages

[024-075] all over again.”

[024-076] “I do not propose to discuss the reasons with you, Major Mallard.”

[024-077] “And, anyway, it's not for long,” growled the Colonel, finding speech at

[024-078] last. “No, by.....” He emphasized the assurance by an unprintable oath.

[024-079] “If I spend the last shilling of my fortune and the last ship of the

[024-080] Jamaica fleet, I'll have that rascal in a hempen necktie before I rest.

[024-081] And I'll not be long about it.” He had empurpled in his angry vehemence,

[024-082] and the veins of his forehead stood out like whipcord. Then he checked.

[024-083] “You did well to follow Lord Julian's instructions,” he commended the

[024-084] Major. With that he turned from him, and took his lordship by the arm.

[024-085] “Come, my lord. We must take order about this, you and I.”

[024-086] They went off together, skirting the redoubt, and so through courtyard

[024-087] and garden to the house where Arabella waited anxiously. The sight of

[024-088] her uncle brought her infinite relief, not only on his own account, but

[024-089] on account also of Captain Blood.

[024-090] “You took a great risk, sir,” she gravely told Lord Julian after the

[024-091] ordinary greetings had been exchanged.

[024-092] But Lord Julian answered her as he had answered Major Mallard. “There

[024-093] was no risk, ma'am.”

[024-094] She looked at him in some astonishment. His long, aristocratic face wore

[024-095] a more melancholy, pensive air than usual. He answered the enquiry in

[024-096] her glance:

[024-097] “So that Blood's ship were allowed to pass the fort, no harm could come

[024-098] to Colonel Bishop. Blood pledged me his word for that.”

[024-099] A faint smile broke the set of her lips, which hitherto had been

[024-100] wistful, and a little colour tinged her cheeks. She would have pursued

[024-101] the subject, but the Deputy-Governor's mood did not permit it. He

[024-102] sneered and snorted at the notion of Blood's word being good for

[024-103] anything, forgetting that he owed to it his own preservation at that

[024-104] moment.

[024-105] At supper, and for long thereafter he talked of nothing but Blood--of

[024-106] how he would lay him by the heels, and what hideous things he would

[024-107] perform upon his body. And as he drank heavily the while, his speech

[024-108] became increasingly gross and his threats increasingly horrible; until

[024-109] in the end Arabella withdrew, white-faced and almost on the verge of

[024-110] tears. It was not often that Bishop revealed himself to his niece. Oddly

[024-111] enough, this coarse, overbearing planter went in a certain awe of that

[024-112] slim girl. It was as if she had inherited from her father the respect in

[024-113] which he had always been held by his brother.

[024-114] Lord Julian, who began to find Bishop disgusting beyond endurance,

[024-115] excused himself soon after, and went in quest of the lady. He had yet to

[024-116] deliver the message from Captain Blood, and this, he thought, would be

[024-117] his opportunity. But Miss Bishop had retired for the night, and

[024-118] Lord Julian must curb his impatience--it amounted by now to nothing

[024-119] less--until the morrow.

[024-120] Very early next morning, before the heat of the day came to render the

[024-121] open intolerable to his lordship, he espied her from his window moving

[024-122] amid the azaleas in the garden. It was a fitting setting for one who was

[024-123] still as much a delightful novelty to him in womanhood as was the azalea

[024-124] among flowers. He hurried forth to join her, and when, aroused from

[024-125] her pensiveness, she had given him a good-morrow, smiling and frank, he

[024-126] explained himself by the announcement that he bore her a message from

[024-127] Captain Blood.

[024-128] He observed her little start and the slight quiver of her lips, and

[024-129] observed thereafter not only her pallor and the shadowy rings about her

[024-130] eyes, but also that unusually wistful air which last night had escaped

[024-131] his notice.

[024-132] They moved out of the open to one of the terraces, where a pergola of

[024-133] orange-trees provided a shaded sauntering space that was at once cool

[024-134] and fragrant. As they went, he considered her admiringly, and marvelled

[024-135] at himself that it should have taken him so long fully to realize

[024-136] her slim, unusual grace, and to find her, as he now did, so entirely

[024-137] desirable, a woman whose charm must irradiate all the life of a man, and

[024-138] touch its commonplaces into magic.

[024-139] He noted the sheen of her red-brown hair, and how gracefully one of its

[024-140] heavy ringlets coiled upon her slender, milk-white neck. She wore a gown

[024-141] of shimmering grey silk, and a scarlet rose, fresh-gathered, was pinned

[024-142] at her breast like a splash of blood. Always thereafter when he thought

[024-143] of her it was as he saw her at that moment, as never, I think, until

[024-144] that moment had he seen her.

[024-145] In silence they paced on a little way into the green shade. Then she

[024-146] paused and faced him.

[024-147] “You said something of a message, sir,” she reminded him, thus betraying

[024-148] some of her impatience.

[024-149] He fingered the ringlets of his periwig, a little embarrassed how to

[024-150] deliver himself, considering how he should begin. “He desired me,” he

[024-151] said at last, “to give you a message that should prove to you that there

[024-152] is still something left in him of the unfortunate gentleman that...

[024-153] that.., for which once you knew him.”

[024-154] “That is not now necessary,” said she very gravely. He misunderstood

[024-155] her, of course, knowing nothing of the enlightenment that yesterday had

[024-156] come to her.

[024-157] “I think..., nay, I know that you do him an [[justice|injustice]],” said he.

[024-158] Her hazel eyes continued to regard him.

[024-159] “If you will deliver the message, it may enable me to judge.”

[024-160] To him, this was confusing. He did not immediately answer. He found that

[024-161] he had not sufficiently considered the terms he should employ, and the

[024-162] matter, after all, was of an exceeding delicacy, demanding delicate

[024-163] handling. It was not so much that he was concerned to deliver a message

[024-164] as to render it a vehicle by which to plead his own cause. Lord Julian,

[024-165] well versed in the lore of womankind and usually at his ease with ladies

[024-166] of the beau-monde, found himself oddly constrained before this frank and

[024-167] unsophisticated niece of a colonial planter.

[024-168] They moved on in silence and as if by common consent towards the

[024-169] brilliant sunshine where the pergola was intersected by the avenue

[024-170] leading upwards to the house. Across this patch of light fluttered a

[024-171] gorgeous butterfly, that was like black and scarlet velvet and large

[024-172] as a man's hand. His lordship's brooding eyes followed it out of sight

[024-173] before he answered.

[024-174] “It is not easy. Stab me, it is not. He was a man who deserved well. And

[024-175] amongst us we have marred his chances: your uncle, because he could not

[024-176] forget his rancour; you, because... because having told him that in the

[024-177] King's service he would find his redemption of what was past, you would

[024-178] not afterwards admit to him that he was so redeemed. And this, although

[024-179] concern to rescue you was the chief motive of his embracing that same

[024-180] service.”

[024-181] She had turned her shoulder to him so that he should not see her face.

[024-182] “I know. I know now,” she said softly. Then after a pause she added the

[024-183] question: “And you? What part has your lordship had in this--that you

[024-184] should incriminate yourself with us?”

[024-185] “My part?” Again he hesitated, then plunged recklessly on, as men do

[024-186] when determined to perform a thing they fear. “If I understood him

[024-187] aright, if he understood aright, himself, my part, though entirely

[024-188] passive, was none the less effective. I implore you to observe that

[024-189] I but report his own words. I say nothing for myself.” His lordship's

[024-190] unusual nervousness was steadily increasing. “He thought, then--so

[024-191] he told me--that my presence here had contributed to his inability to

[024-192] redeem himself in your sight; and unless he were so redeemed, then was

[024-193] redemption nothing.”

[024-194] She faced him fully, a frown of perplexity bringing her brows together

[024-195] above her troubled eyes.

[024-196] “He thought that you had contributed?” she echoed. It was clear she

[024-197] asked for enlightenment. He plunged on to afford it her, his glance a

[024-198] little scared, his cheeks flushing.

[024-199] “Aye, and he said so in terms which told me something that I hope above

[024-200] all things, and yet dare not believe, for, God knows, I am no coxcomb,

[024-201] Arabella. He said... but first let me tell you how I was placed. I had

[024-202] gone aboard his ship to demand the instant surrender of your uncle whom

[024-203] he held [[hostage|captive]]. He laughed at me. Colonel Bishop should be a hostage

[024-204] for his safety. By rashly venturing aboard his ship, I afforded him

[024-205] in my own person yet another hostage as valuable at least as Colonel

[024-206] Bishop. Yet he bade me depart; not from the fear of consequences, for

[024-207] he is above fear, nor from any personal esteem for me whom he confessed

[024-208] that he had come to find detestable; and this for the very reason that

[024-209] made him concerned for my safety.”

[024-210] “I do not understand,” she said, as he paused. “Is not that a

[024-211] contradiction in itself?”

[024-212] “It seems so only. The fact is, Arabella, this unfortunate man has

[024-213] the... the temerity to love you.”

[024-214] She cried out at that, and clutched her breast whose calm was suddenly

[024-215] disturbed. Her eyes dilated as she stared at him.

[024-216] “I... I've startled you,” said he, with concern. “I feared I should. But

[024-217] it was necessary so that you may understand.”

[024-218] “Go on,” she bade him.

[024-219] “Well, then: he saw in me one who made it impossible that he should win

[024-220] you--so he said. Therefore he could with satisfaction have killed me.

[024-221] But because my death might cause you pain, because your happiness was

[024-222] the thing that above all things he desired, he surrendered that part of

[024-223] his guarantee of safety which my person afforded him. If his departure

[024-224] should be hindered, and I should lose my life in what might follow,

[024-225] there was the risk that... that you might mourn me. That risk he would

[024-226] not take. Him you deemed a thief and a pirate, he said, and added

[024-227] that--I am giving you his own words always--if in choosing between us

[024-228] two, your choice, as he believed, would fall on me, then were you in his

[024-229] opinion choosing wisely. Because of that he bade me leave his ship, and

[024-230] had me put ashore.”

[024-231] She looked at him with eyes that were aswim with tears. He took a step

[024-232] towards her, a catch in his breath, his hand held out.

[024-233] “Was he right, Arabella? My life's happiness hangs upon your answer.”

[024-234] But she continued silently to regard him with those tear-laden eyes,

[024-235] without speaking, and until she spoke he dared not advance farther.

[024-236] A doubt, a tormenting doubt beset him. When presently she spoke, he saw

[024-237] how true had been the instinct of which that doubt was born, for her

[024-238] words revealed the fact that of all that he had said the only thing

[024-239] that had touched her consciousness and absorbed it from all other

[024-240] considerations was Blood's conduct as it regarded herself.

[024-241] “He said that!” she cried. “He did that! Oh!” She turned away, and

[024-242] through the slender, clustering trunks of the bordering orange-trees

[024-243] she looked out across the glittering waters of the great harbour to

[024-244] the distant hills. Thus for a little while, my lord standing stiffly,

[024-245] fearfully, waiting for fuller revelation of her mind. At last it came,

[024-246] slowly, deliberately, in a voice that at moments was half suffocated.

[024-247] “Last night when my uncle displayed his rancour and his evil rage, it

[024-248] began to be borne in upon me that such vindictiveness can belong only to

[024-249] those who have wronged. It is the frenzy into which men whip themselves

[024-250] to justify an evil passion. I must have known then, if I had not already

[024-251] learnt it, that I had been too credulous of all the unspeakable things

[024-252] attributed to Peter Blood. Yesterday I had his own explanation of that

[024-253] tale of [[levasseur|Levasseur]] that you heard in St. Nicholas. And now this... this

[024-254] but gives me confirmation of his truth and worth. To a scoundrel such as

[024-255] I was too readily brought to believe him, the act of which you have just

[024-256] told me would have been impossible.”

[024-257] “That is my own opinion,” said his lordship gently.

[024-258] “It must be. But even if it were not, that would now weigh for nothing.

[024-259] What weighs--oh, so heavily and bitterly--is the thought that but for

[024-260] the words in which yesterday I repelled him, he might have been saved.

[024-261] If only I could have spoken to him again before he went! I waited for

[024-262] him; but my uncle was with him, and I had no suspicion that he was going

[024-263] away again. And now he is lost--back at his outlawry and piracy, in

[024-264] which ultimately he will be taken and destroyed. And the fault is

[024-265] mine--mine!”

[024-266] “What are you saying? The only agents were your uncle's hostility and

[024-267] his own obstinacy which would not study compromise. You must not blame

[024-268] yourself for anything.”

[024-269] She swung to him with some impatience, her eyes aswim in tears. “You can

[024-270] say that, and in spite of his message, which in itself tells how much

[024-271] I was to blame! It was my treatment of him, the epithets I cast at him

[024-272] that drove him. So much he has told you. I know it to be true.”

[024-273] “You have no cause for shame,” said he. “As for your sorrow--why, if it

[024-274] will afford you solace--you may still count on me to do what man can to

[024-275] rescue him from this position.”

[024-276] She caught her breath.

[024-277] “You will do that!” she cried with sudden eager hopefulness. “You

[024-278] promise?” She held out her hand to him impulsively. He took it in both

[024-279] his own.

[024-280] “I promise,” he answered her. And then, retaining still the hand she

[024-281] had surrendered to him--“Arabella,” he said very gently, “there is still

[024-282] this other matter upon which you have not answered me.”

[024-283] “This other matter?” Was he mad, she wondered.

[024-284] Could any other matter signify in such a moment.

[024-285] “This matter that concerns myself; and all my future, oh, so very

[024-286] closely. This thing that Blood believed, that prompted him..., that ...

[024-287] that you are not indifferent to me.” He saw the fair face change colour

[024-288] and grow troubled once more.

[024-289] “Indifferent to you?” said she. “Why, no. We have been good friends; we

[024-290] shall continue so, I hope, my lord.”

[024-291] “Friends! Good friends?” He was between dismay and bitterness. “It is

[024-292] not your friendship only that I ask, Arabella. You heard what I said,

[024-293] what I reported. You will not say that Peter Blood was wrong?”

[024-294] Gently she sought to disengage her hand, the trouble in her face

[024-295] increasing. A moment he resisted; then, realizing what he did, he set

[024-296] her free.

[024-297] “Arabella!” he cried on a note of sudden pain.

[024-298] “I have friendship for you, my lord. But only friendship.” His castle of

[024-299] hopes came clattering down about him, leaving him a little stunned. As

[024-300] he had said, he was no coxcomb. Yet there was something that he did

[024-301] not understand. She confessed to friendship, and it was in his power

[024-302] to offer her a great position, one to which she, a colonial planter's

[024-303] niece, however wealthy, could never have aspired even in her dreams.

[024-304] This she rejected, yet spoke of friendship. Peter Blood had been

[024-305] mistaken, then. How far had he been mistaken? Had he been as mistaken in

[024-306] her feelings towards himself as he obviously was in her feelings towards

[024-307] his lordship? In that case ... His reflections broke short. To speculate

[024-308] was to wound himself in vain. He must know. Therefore he asked her with

[024-309] grim frankness:

[024-310] “Is it Peter Blood?”

[024-311] “Peter Blood?” she echoed. At first she did not understand the purport

[024-312] of his question. When understanding came, a flush suffused her face.

[024-313] “I do not know,” she said, faltering a little.

[024-314] This was hardly a truthful answer. For, as if an obscuring veil had

[024-315] suddenly been rent that morning, she was permitted at last to see Peter

[024-316] Blood in his true relations to other men, and that sight, vouchsafed

[024-317] her twenty-four hours too late, filled her with [[mercy|pity]] and regret and

[024-318] yearning.

[024-319] Lord Julian knew enough of women to be left in no further doubt. He

[024-320] bowed his head so that she might not see the anger in his eyes, for as

[024-321] a man of honour he took shame in that anger which as a human being he

[024-322] could not repress.

[024-323] And because Nature in him was stronger--as it is in most of us--than

[024-324] training, Lord Julian from that moment began, almost in spite of

[024-325] himself, to practise something that was akin to villainy. I regret

[024-326] to chronicle it of one for whom--if I have done him any sort of

[024-327] justice--you should have been conceiving some esteem. But the truth

[024-328] is that the lingering remains of the regard in which he had held Peter

[024-329] Blood were choked by the desire to supplant and destroy a rival. He had

[024-330] passed his word to Arabella that he would use his powerful influence on

[024-331] Blood's behalf. I deplore to set it down that not only did he forget his

[024-332] pledge, but secretly set himself to aid and abet Arabella's uncle in the

[024-333] plans he laid for the trapping and undoing of the buccaneer. He might

[024-334] reasonably have urged--had he been taxed with it--that he conducted

[024-335] himself precisely as his duty demanded. But to that he might have been

[024-336] answered that duty with him was but the slave of jealousy in this.

[024-337] When the Jamaica fleet put to sea some few days later, Lord Julian

[024-338] sailed with Colonel Bishop in Vice-Admiral Craufurd's flagship. Not only

[024-339] was there no need for either of them to go, but the Deputy-Governor's

[024-340] duties actually demanded that he should remain ashore, whilst Lord

[024-341] Julian, as we know, was a useless man aboard a ship. Yet both set out

[024-342] to hunt Captain Blood, each making of his duty a pretext for the

[024-343] satisfaction of personal aims; and that common purpose became a link

[024-344] between them, binding them in a sort of friendship that must otherwise

[024-345] have been impossible between men so dissimilar in breeding and in

[024-346] aspirations.

[024-347] The hunt was up. They cruised awhile off Hispaniola, watching the

[024-348] Windward Passage, and suffering the discomforts of the rainy season

[024-349] which had now set in. But they cruised in vain, and after a month of

[024-350] it, returned empty-handed to Port Royal, there to find awaiting them the

[024-351] most disquieting news from the Old World.

[024-352] The megalomania of Louis XIV had set Europe in a blaze of war. The

[024-353] French legionaries were ravaging the Rhine provinces, and Spain had

[024-354] joined the nations leagued to defend themselves from the wild ambitions

[024-355] of the King of France. And there was worse than this: there were rumours

[024-356] of civil war in England, where the people had grown weary of the bigoted

[024-357] tyranny of King James. It was reported that William of Orange had been

[024-358] invited to come over.
