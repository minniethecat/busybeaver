# Rebel

The **rebel** — a participant in armed insurrection against the established authority — is the political identity that defines [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] at the novel's opening and haunts him throughout his career as buccaneer and [[privateer|privateer]]. The term carries both legal condemnation (rebellion is treason) and moral complexity (is rebellion against tyranny justified?), and the novel explores both dimensions through Blood's trajectory from condemned rebel to commissioned officer of the very [[crown|Crown]] he defied.

## In the Novel

### The Monmouth Rebellion

The novel's historical backdrop is the Monmouth Rebellion of 1685 — the attempt by James Scott, Duke of [[monmouth|Monmouth]], to seize the English throne from his uncle James II. The rebellion was crushed at the [[sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]], and its participants were hunted, tried, and executed or transported in the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]].

[[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s relationship to the rebellion is ambiguous. He is not a soldier but a physician, and he claims he joined the rebel cause only to tend the wounded: "I was not in rebellion." Yet the law makes no such distinction — to aid a rebel is to be a rebel, and Blood is convicted alongside men who bore arms.

### The Language of Rebellion

The novel's language around rebellion is precise and legalistic. The rebels are "Monmouth rebels" or simply "rebels," and their actions constitute "rebellion" — a capital offense under English law. Colonel [[colonel-kirke|Kirke]]'s dragoons hunt "rebels" through the countryside, and to "harbour and comfort rebels" is itself a crime punishable by death.

The narrator's irony is evident: the rebellion is described as "the late rebellion," "this plaguey rebellion," and "the turbulent spirit of rebellion" — phrases that echo the Crown's perspective while inviting the reader to question whether the Monmouth cause was, in fact, unjust.

### The Rebel Identity

Once branded a rebel, Blood cannot escape the label. In [[barbados|Barbados]], he is "a rebel-convict with ten years of slavery before him" — the compound term fusing his political crime with his legal punishment. The phrase "rebels-convict" is repeated throughout the novel's early chapters, a verbal brand as indelible as any physical mark.

The rebel identity also functions as a bond. The "forty-two surviving rebels-convict" form a community within the larger slave population, united by shared experience and shared grievance. When Blood leads their escape, it is the rebel bond — forged at [[sedgemoor|Sedgemoor]] and tempered in the [[plantation|plantation]] fields — that provides the cohesion necessary for collective action.

### From Rebel to Officer

The novel's central irony is that the condemned rebel becomes the Crown's commissioned officer. Blood's acceptance of the King's [[commission]] transforms his legal status overnight — from hunted outlaw to agent of royal authority. Yet Blood never repudiates his rebel past. He tells [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] that he accepted the [[letters-of-marque|commission]] to redeem himself in [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]]'s eyes, not out of any newfound loyalty to James II.

The crew's reaction underscores the contradiction: "this poxy commission" turns rebels into King's men, a transformation that some accept as survival and others resent as betrayal. The resolution — Blood's elevation to [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] under William III — completes the circle: the rebel against one king becomes the governor under another, the political identity shifting with the political winds.

## Historical Context

The Monmouth Rebellion (May–July 1685) was an attempt by the Protestant Duke of Monmouth to depose the Catholic James II. It attracted widespread support from Whigs, Dissenters, and others alarmed by James's religious policies. After Monmouth's defeat at Sedgemoor, Judge Jeffreys conducted the Bloody Assizes, sentencing approximately 320 rebels to death and over 800 to transportation. The transported rebels were sold as indentured labourers in the West Indies, a fate that, while brutal, was preferable to the gallows.
