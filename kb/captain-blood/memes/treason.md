# Treason

## Overview

Treason is the legal charge that destroys [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s life and sets the novel in motion. Accused of "high treason" for his involvement — however peripheral — in the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]], Blood is condemned and transported. The novel uses treason as a lens through which to examine the arbitrary nature of political justice: what is treason to one monarch may be loyalty to another, and the line between rebellion and patriotism depends entirely on who wears the crown.

## Historical Background

Under English law of the 17th century, "high treason" encompassed a range of offenses against the sovereign, including levying war against the King and adhering to the King's enemies. The Treason Act of 1351 (still in force) defined the crime broadly, and conviction carried the death penalty — [[execution|hanging]], drawing, and quartering. In practice, many convicted of treason during the turbulent Stuart period were transported rather than executed, as colonial labor demand made pardons-with-transportation economically attractive.

## In the Novel

### The Charge

The charge of "high treason" against Blood is legally dubious. The novel states that "he was not guilty" ((003-190)) — his involvement was limited to dressing the wounded. Yet the prosecution lumps him with the genuine rebels: "the wicked treason of these three rogues" ((003-378)) is presented as self-evident. The witnesses are described as men who, had they been "accused of treason" themselves, would have been equally condemned ((003-402)). Blood's situation illustrates how treason prosecutions after Monmouth were indiscriminate.

### Blood's "Treasonous" Speech

Throughout the novel, characters speak of "treason" in contexts that reveal its political nature. [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] warns [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] that her defense of Blood amounts to "talking treason" ((005-196)). Later, a French officer "committed what was construed into treason" ((018-371)), showing how the charge follows political convenience rather than actual disloyalty. Blood's own crew, when challenged, is accused of "just mutiny... treason!" ((023-213)) — a conflation of military [[mutiny|insubordination]] with political crime.

### Treason Inverted

Blood's journey inverts the treason narrative. Condemned as a traitor by James II, he ends his career as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] under William III — the king who himself could be called a usurper and traitor to James. The novel thus suggests that treason is often the victor's word for the loser's loyalty.
