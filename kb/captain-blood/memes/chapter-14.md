[014-001] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] looked up to consider the questioner before replying.

[014-002] The man was tall and built on lines of agile strength, with a swarthy,

[014-003] aquiline face that was brutally handsome. A diamond of great price

[014-004] flamed on the indifferently clean hand resting on the pummel of his long

[014-005] rapier, and there were gold rings in his ears, half-concealed by long

[014-006] ringlets of oily chestnut hair.

[014-007] Captain Blood took the pipe-stem from between his lips.

[014-008] “My name,” he said, “is Peter Blood. The Spaniards know me for Don Pedro

[014-009] Sangre and a Frenchman may call me Le Sang if he pleases.”

[014-010] “Good,” said the gaudy adventurer in English, and without further

[014-011] invitation he drew up a stool and sat down at that greasy table. “My

[014-012] name,” he informed the three men, two of whom at least were eyeing him

[014-013] askance, “it is [[levasseur|Levasseur]]. You may have heard of me.”

[014-014] They had, indeed. He commanded a privateer of twenty guns that had

[014-015] dropped anchor in the bay a week ago, manned by a crew mainly composed

[014-016] of French boucanhunters from Northern Hispaniola, men who had good cause

[014-017] to hate the Spaniard with an intensity exceeding that of the English.

[014-018] Levasseur had brought them back to [[tortuga|Tortuga]] from an indifferently

[014-019] successful cruise. It would need more, however, than lack of success

[014-020] to abate the fellow's monstrous vanity. A roaring, quarrelsome,

[014-021] hard-drinking, hard-gaming scoundrel, his [[honour|reputation]] as a buccaneer

[014-022] stood high among the wild Brethren of the Coast. He enjoyed also a

[014-023] reputation of another sort. There was about his gaudy, swaggering

[014-024] raffishness something that the women found singularly alluring. That

[014-025] he should boast openly of his bonnes fortunes did not seem strange to

[014-026] Captain Blood; what he might have found strange was that there appeared

[014-027] to be some measure of justification for these boasts.

[014-028] It was current gossip that even Mademoiselle d'Ogeron, the Governor's

[014-029] daughter, had been caught in the snare of his wild attractiveness, and

[014-030] that Levasseur had gone the length of audacity of asking her hand in

[014-031] marriage of her father. M. d'Ogeron had made him the only possible

[014-032] answer. He had shown him the door. Levasseur had departed in a rage,

[014-033] swearing that he would make mademoiselle his wife in the teeth of all

[014-034] the fathers in Christendom, and that M. d'Ogeron should bitterly rue the

[014-035] affront he had put upon him.

[014-036] This was the man who now thrust himself upon Captain Blood with a

[014-037] proposal of association, offering him not only his sword, but his ship

[014-038] and the men who sailed in her.

[014-039] A dozen years ago, as a lad of barely twenty, Levasseur had sailed with

[014-040] that monster of cruelty L'Ollonais, and his own subsequent exploits

[014-041] bore witness and did credit to the school in which he had been reared. I

[014-042] doubt if in his day there was a greater scoundrel among the Brethren of

[014-043] the Coast than this Levasseur. And yet, repulsive though he found him,

[014-044] Captain Blood could not deny that the fellow's proposals displayed

[014-045] [[courage|boldness]], imagination, and resource, and he was forced to admit that

[014-046] jointly they could undertake operations of a greater magnitude than was

[014-047] possible singly to either of them. The climax of Levasseur's project was

[014-048] to be a raid upon the wealthy mainland city of Maracaybo; but for this,

[014-049] he admitted, six hundred men at the very least would be required, and

[014-050] six hundred men were not to be conveyed in the two bottoms they now

[014-051] commanded. Preliminary cruises must take place, having for one of their

[014-052] objects the capture of further ships.

[014-053] Because he disliked the man, Captain Blood would not commit himself at

[014-054] once. But because he liked the proposal he consented to consider it.

[014-055] Being afterwards pressed by both [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] and [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]], who did not

[014-056] share his own personal dislike of the Frenchman, the end of the matter

[014-057] was that within a week articles were drawn up between Levasseur

[014-058] and Blood, and signed by them and--as was usual--by the chosen

[014-059] representatives of their followers.

[014-060] These articles contained, inter alia, the common provisions that, should

[014-061] the two vessels separate, a strict account must afterwards be rendered

[014-062] of all prizes severally taken, whilst the vessel taking a prize should

[014-063] retain three fifths of its value, surrendering two fifths to its

[014-064] associate. These shares were subsequently to be subdivided among the

[014-065] crew of each vessel, in accordance with the articles already obtaining

[014-066] between each captain and his own men. For the rest, the articles

[014-067] contained all the clauses that were usual, among which was the clause

[014-068] that any man found guilty of abstracting or concealing any part of a

[014-069] prize, be it of the value of no more than a peso, should be summarily

[014-070] hanged from the yardarm.

[014-071] All being now settled they made ready for sea, and on the very eve of

[014-072] sailing, Levasseur narrowly escaped being shot in a romantic attempt

[014-073] to scale the wall of the Governor's garden, with the object of taking

[014-074] passionate leave of the infatuated Mademoiselle d'Ogeron. He desisted

[014-075] after having been twice fired upon from a fragrant ambush of pimento

[014-076] trees where the Governor's guards were posted, and he departed vowing to

[014-077] take different and very definite measures on his return.

[014-078] That night he slept on board his ship, which with characteristic

[014-079] flamboyance he had named La Foudre, and there on the following day he

[014-080] received a visit from Captain Blood, whom he greeted half-mockingly as

[014-081] his admiral. The Irishman came to settle certain final details of which

[014-082] all that need concern us is an understanding that, in the event of the

[014-083] two vessels becoming separated by accident or design, they should rejoin

[014-084] each other as soon as might be at Tortuga.

[014-085] Thereafter Levasseur entertained his admiral to dinner, and jointly they

[014-086] drank success to the expedition, so copiously on the part of Levasseur

[014-087] that when the time came to separate he was as nearly drunk as it seemed

[014-088] possible for him to be and yet retain his understanding.

[014-089] Finally, towards evening, Captain Blood went over the side and was rowed

[014-090] back to his great ship with her red bulwarks and gilded ports, touched

[014-091] into a lovely thing of flame by the setting sun.

[014-092] He was a little heavy-hearted. I have said that he was a judge of men,

[014-093] and his judgment of Levasseur filled him with misgivings which were

[014-094] growing heavier in a measure as the hour of departure approached.

[014-095] He expressed it to Wolverstone, who met him as he stepped aboard the

[014-096] [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]]:

[014-097] “You over persuaded me into those articles, you blackguard; and it'll

[014-098] surprise me if any good comes of this association.”

[014-099] The giant rolled his single bloodthirsty eye, and sneered, thrusting out

[014-100] his heavy jaw. “We'll wring the dog's neck if there's any treachery.”

[014-101] “So we will--if we are there to wring it by then.” And on that,

[014-102] dismissing the matter: “We sail in the morning, on the first of the

[014-103] ebb,” he announced, and went off to his cabin.

[014-104] CHAPTER XIV. LEVASSEUR'S HEROICS

[014-105] It would be somewhere about ten o'clock on the following morning, a

[014-106] full hour before the time appointed for sailing, when a canoe brought up

[014-107] alongside La Foudre, and a half-caste Indian stepped out of her and went

[014-108] up the ladder. He was clad in drawers of hairy, untanned hide, and a red

[014-109] blanket served him for a cloak. He was the bearer of a folded scrap of

[014-110] paper for Captain Levasseur.

[014-111] The Captain unfolded the letter, sadly soiled and crumpled by contact

[014-112] with the half-caste's person. Its contents may be roughly translated

[014-113] thus:

[014-114] “My well-beloved--I am in the Dutch brig Jongvrow, which is about to

[014-115] sail. Resolved to separate us for ever, my cruel father is sending me to

[014-116] Europe in my brother's charge. I implore you, come to my rescue. Deliver

[014-117] me, my well-beloved hero!--Your desolated Madeleine, who loves you.”

[014-118] The well-beloved hero was moved to the soul of him by that passionate

[014-119] appeal. His scowling glance swept the bay for the Dutch brig, which

[014-120] he knew had been due to sail for Amsterdam with a cargo of hides and

[014-121] tobacco.

[014-122] She was nowhere to be seen among the shipping in that narrow, rock-bound

[014-123] harbour. He roared out the question in his mind.

[014-124] In answer the half-caste pointed out beyond the frothing surf that

[014-125] marked the position of the reef constituting one of the stronghold's

[014-126] main defences. Away beyond it, a mile or so distant, a sail was standing

[014-127] out to sea. “There she go,” he said.

[014-128] “There!” The Frenchman gazed and stared, his face growing white. The

[014-129] man's wicked temper awoke, and turned to vent itself upon the messenger.

[014-130] “And where have you been that you come here only now with this? Answer

[014-131] me!”

[014-132] The half-caste shrank terrified before his fury. His explanation, if he

[014-133] had one, was paralyzed by fear. Levasseur took him by the throat, shook

[014-134] him twice, snarling the while, then hurled him into the scuppers. The

[014-135] man's head struck the gunwale as he fell, and he lay there, quite still,

[014-136] a trickle of blood issuing from his mouth.

[014-137] Levasseur dashed one hand against the other, as if dusting them.

[014-138] “Heave that muck overboard,” he ordered some of those who stood idling

[014-139] in the waist. “Then up anchor, and let us after the Dutchman.”

[014-140] “Steady, Captain. What's that?” There was a restraining hand upon his

[014-141] shoulder, and the broad face of his lieutenant Cahusac, a burly, callous

[014-142] Breton scoundrel, was stolidly confronting him.

[014-143] Levasseur made clear his purpose with a deal of unnecessary obscenity.

[014-144] Cahusac shook his head. “A Dutch brig!” said he. “Impossible! We should

[014-145] never be allowed.”

[014-146] “And who the devil will deny us?” Levasseur was between amazement and

[014-147] fury.

[014-148] “For one thing, there's your own crew will be none too willing. For

[014-149] another there's Captain Blood.”

[014-150] “I care nothing for Captain Blood....”

[014-151] “But it is necessary that you should. He has the [[authority|power]], the weight of

[014-152] metal and of men, and if I know him at all he'll sink us before

[014-153] he'll suffer interference with the Dutch. He has his own views of

[014-154] [[piracy|privateering]], this Captain Blood, as I warned you.”

[014-155] “Ah!” said Levasseur, showing his teeth. But his eyes, riveted upon that

[014-156] distant sail, were gloomily thoughtful. Not for long. The imagination

[014-157] and resource which Captain Blood had detected in the fellow soon

[014-158] suggested a course.

[014-159] Cursing in his soul, and even before the anchor was weighed, the

[014-160] association into which he had entered, he was already studying ways

[014-161] of evasion. What Cahusac implied was true: Blood would never suffer

[014-162] violence to be done in his presence to a Dutchman; but it might be done

[014-163] in his absence; and, being done, Blood must perforce condone it, since

[014-164] it would then be too late to protest.

[014-167] Blood, nevertheless, accepted it, and weighed anchor before the

[014-168] appointed time upon perceiving his associate to do so.

[014-240] and then trussed him like a fowl.

[014-245] overcrowded, could yet contain them. Next, Cahusac having inspected the

[014-375] officers, and went to visit Captain Blood aboard the Arabella.

[014-376] “Our brief separation has been mighty profitable,” was Captain

[014-377] Blood's greeting. “It's a busy morning we've both had.” He was in high

[014-378] good-humour as he led the way to the great cabin for a rendering of

[014-379] accounts.

[014-380] The tall ship that accompanied the Arabella was a Spanish vessel of

[014-381] twenty-six guns, the Santiago from Puerto Rico with a hundred and twenty

[014-382] thousand weight of cacao, forty thousand [[ransom|pieces of eight]], and the value

[014-383] of ten thousand more in jewels. A rich capture of which two fifths under

[014-384] the articles went to Levasseur and his crew. Of the money and jewels a

[014-385] division was made on the spot. The cacao it was agreed should be taken

[014-386] to Tortuga to be sold.
