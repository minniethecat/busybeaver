# King William III

## Overview

King William III — William of Orange — is a historical figure whose accession to the English throne in 1688 frames the political resolution of the novel. Though he never appears directly, his ascension transforms the legal status of Peter Blood and his followers. Where they were once outlaws under James II, the new reign of William and Mary opens the door to royal commissions, pardons, and service to the Crown. The shift is conveyed through Lord Willoughby, King William's Governor-General of the West Indies, and through letters from the King's Secretary of State.

## In the Novel

Word of the Glorious Revolution reaches the Caribbean when Lord Willoughby, "King William's Governor-General of the West Indies," is rescued by Captain Blood from a Spanish attack ([029-045]). Blood — who has been operating outside the law — is initially bewildered by the name: "'King William?' quoth Blood, and he was conscious that Pitt and Dyke, who were behind him, now came edging nearer, sharing his own wonder. 'And who may be King William, and of what may he be King?'" ([029-048], [029-050]). Lord Willoughby explains: "I am alluding to His Majesty King William III — William of Orange — who, with Queen Mary, has been ruling England for two months and more" ([029-052]).

The political implications are immediate. Blood asks whether "they've roused themselves at home, and kicked out that scoundrel James and his gang of ruffians" ([029-138]). Lord Willoughby extends an offer for Blood to "serve King William out here" — a path away from piracy toward legitimacy ([029-091]).

The tangible result follows shortly: letters from "King William's Secretary of State" inform Lord Willoughby of commissions and instructions ([025-006]). By the novel's end, Blood and his men have placed themselves "in the service of King William," capturing a Spanish treasure ship for "the benefit of King William" and "King William's coffers" ([029-163], [031-014], [031-105]). The King thus becomes the silent architect of Blood's rehabilitation — his reign providing the legal and political framework that transforms an outlaw into a servant of the Crown.

## Relationships

- **Lord Willoughby** — His Governor-General of the West Indies, who delivers the news of the new reign to Blood
- **Lord Sunderland** — His Secretary of State, whose letters and commissions reach the Caribbean
- **James II** — His predecessor and father-in-law, deposed in the Glorious Revolution
- **Peter Blood** — The buccaneer who ultimately enters his service

## Historical Basis

William III was a real historical figure — William of Orange, Stadtholder of the Dutch Republic, who invaded England in 1688 at the invitation of the "Immortal Seven" and became joint sovereign with his wife Mary II. The novel accurately reflects the timeline: the Glorious Revolution occurred in late 1688, and news reached the West Indies in early 1689, which aligns with the novel's Caribbean setting. The offer of royal service to former outlaws willing to fight for the Crown was consistent with the period's practice of issuing letters of marque and privateering commissions.
