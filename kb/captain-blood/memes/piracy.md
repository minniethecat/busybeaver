# Piracy

## Overview

Piracy in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* is both a survival strategy and a form of political [[treason|rebellion]]. After escaping from slavery in [[barbados|Barbados]], [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] and his fellow rebels-convict turn to the sea, becoming the most feared [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] in the Caribbean. The novel explores the moral ambiguity of piracy — Blood operates under a self-imposed code of [[honour|honor]], refusing to attack English ships and treating his [[hostage|captive]]s with a chivalry that distinguishes him from common pirates.

## Historical Background

The 17th-century Caribbean was the golden age of buccaneering. The Spanish Main — the coastal regions of the Spanish Empire in the Americas — was contested by English, French, and Dutch [[privateering|privateer]]s operating under [[privateer|letters of marque]]. The line between [[privateering|privateer]] (legal, state-sanctioned) and pirate (criminal) was often thin and politically determined. Spain considered all foreign ships in "their" waters as pirates, while England and France viewed their own [[privateering|privateer]]s as legitimate naval auxiliaries. The novel's setting in the 1680s, during the transition from privateering to outright piracy, reflects this legal ambiguity.

## In the Novel

### Escape and Transformation

Blood's turn to piracy is gradual and reluctant. Initially a physician and a gentleman, he is driven to the sea by the [[justice|injustice]] of his transportation. His first capture of a Spanish ship is an act of survival, and each subsequent capture draws him deeper into the buccaneer life. The crew's escape from slavery directly enables their embrace of piracy ((011-339)).

### The Gentleman Pirate

Blood insists that he is not a common pirate. He maintains "such chivalry as is consistent with piracy" ((017-284)), refusing to prey on English vessels and treating Spanish prisoners with dignity. [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]], sent to suppress piracy in the Caribbean, observes that Blood has "quit piracy" by transitioning into service to [[crown|the Crown]] ((023-218)). This tension — between outlaw and patriot — defines Blood's character arc.

### Legal and Political Dimensions

The Spanish [[crown|Crown]], through [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]], accuses Blood of "piracy" ((019-015)), while the French are "right in desiring piracy to continue" as a means of harassing Spanish shipping ((019-080)). Later, Blood's service to King Louis of France brings its own complications when French privateering proves indistinguishable from "acts of piracy" ((025-337)).
