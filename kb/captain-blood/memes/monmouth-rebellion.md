# Monmouth Rebellion

## Overview

The Monmouth Rebellion of 1685 was the failed attempt by James Scott, Duke of Monmouth (the illegitimate Protestant son of Charles II), to seize the English throne from the Catholic King James II. The rebellion's defeat at the Battle of Sedgemoor and the subsequent "Bloody Assizes" under Judge Jeffreys resulted in hundreds of executions and over 800 transportations to the West Indies — providing the historical foundation for Peter Blood's story.

## Historical Context

Monmouth landed at Lyme Regis in June 1685 with a small force, gathering support from Protestant Dissenters, artisans, and farmers — the "pitchfork army" immortalized in the novel. His forces were decisively defeated at Sedgemoor on July 6, 1685. Monmouth was captured and executed on Tower Hill. The reprisals under Judge Jeffreys were exceptionally brutal even by 17th-century standards.

## In the Novel

### The Opening Context

The novel opens in the rebellion's aftermath. Blood, a physician who "knew too much about this fellow Monmouth" ((001-078)), is swept up despite his lack of political involvement. The "frenzied fanatical heat of rebellion" ((001-068)) has given way to the cold machinery of judicial vengeance.

### Blood's Involvement

Blood's role in the rebellion was minimal — he treated the wounded after Sedgemoor. But in Jeffreys' court, even this act of mercy is construed as sedition. Blood was "seduced into rebellion" ((001-104)) not by Monmouth's cause but by his own compassion — and this distinction means nothing to the law.

### The Legacy

The rebellion's defeat creates the entire premise: the condemned rebels, the transportation, the Caribbean setting. Without Monmouth's failed rebellion and Jeffreys' brutal response, there would be no Captain Blood.
