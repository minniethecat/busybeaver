# Lord Gildoy

## Overview

Lord Gildoy is a young nobleman who fought on the rebel side at the Battle of Sedgemoor during the Monmouth Rebellion. Severely wounded in the engagement, he is carried to Oglethorpe's Farm, where his desperate need for a surgeon sets in motion the chain of events that leads to Peter Blood's arrest. Though Gildoy appears only in the novel's early chapters, his role is pivotal: it is Blood's decision to tend Gildoy's wounds that places him at the wrong place at the wrong time when the King's dragoons arrive.

## In the Novel

Lord Gildoy is first mentioned when a messenger arrives breathlessly at Bridgewater to summon Dr. Peter Blood: "It is Lord Gildoy... He is sore wounded" at Oglethorpe's Farm ([002-062]). Blood, who knew Gildoy personally — "Gildoy had been a friend" — is distressed by the news ([002-068]).

Blood rides to Oglethorpe's Farm to tend Gildoy's wounds, performing surgery that saves his life. Gildoy is described as "a very tall and dark young gentleman" ([002-135]), a man who had "such bright hopes in life" before the calamity of Sedgemoor ([002-141]). When the King's dragoons under Captain Hobart arrive at the farm, Blood attempts to shield Gildoy, describing him simply as "the gentleman" who is "my Lord Gildoy" ([003-040]). Blood warns Hobart that "Lord Gildoy will have friends and influence" who might intercede on his behalf ([003-052]).

Gildoy makes "a feeble effort to put forth his interests" during the confrontation with Hobart ([003-066]). However, he is too weak from his wounds to affect the outcome. Blood's concern for Gildoy's safety is used against him — when Blood asks "where will Lord Gildoy be?" after Hobart's dragoons take control, the implicit threat to Gildoy's life becomes a lever of coercion ([003-258]). Gildoy is taken prisoner alongside the other rebels and later transported ([003-360]), with Blood's fate now irreversibly entwined with his.

At Blood's trial, his defence hinges partly on his medical errand: he had been "summoned that morning to succour Lord Gildoy" — a duty, not a political act ([003-466]). Captain Hobart confirms during his testimony that Blood was indeed treating "Lord Gildoy — yes" and that Blood "knew that Lord Gildoy had been wounded in the battle" ([003-494], [003-495]).
