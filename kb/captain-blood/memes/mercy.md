# Mercy

## Overview

Mercy is the virtue that destroys [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s life and ultimately redeems it. His "crime" — the act for which he is condemned as a traitor — was dressing the wounds of a dying rebel after the [[battle-of-sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]]. This act of medical compassion is reframed by [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] as treasonous sedition. Throughout the novel, Blood's relationship to mercy evolves: from victim of merciless justice, to pirate who shows mercy to [[hostage|captive]]s, to governor who can dispense mercy from a position of [[authority|power]].

## Historical Background

In the wake of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]], mercy was in short supply. [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]]' "[[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]]" were notorious for their brutality — defendants were denied counsel, trials lasted minutes, and those who pleaded guilty were sometimes executed anyway. The concept of "the King's mercy" was a legal fiction; in practice, James II used the treason trials to eliminate political opposition and generate revenue through transportation fines.

## In the Novel

### Condemned for Mercy

Blood's defining act of mercy occurs before the novel begins, but it is recounted in Chapter 3. He tells the court: "I had dressed the wounds of a man who was convicted of treason" ((020-328)). For this "act of mercy" ((020-325)) — performed "compassionately and without thought for creed or politics" ((020-327)) — he was "sold into slavery" ((020-330)). The novel thus establishes a world in which compassion is criminalized.

### Mercy on the Seas

As a pirate captain, Blood consistently shows mercy that distinguishes him from common [[buccaneer|buccaneers]]. He spares lives when "clemency full worthy of him" would suffice ((004-092)), converting [[execution|death sentence]]s into transportation — a grim echo of the mercy shown to him. The crew's treatment of prisoners reflects Blood's policy: "such chivalry as is consistent with piracy" ((017-284)).

### The Economy of Mercy

Blood's approach to [[hostage|captive]]s is pragmatic as well as principled. By showing mercy, he builds a [[honour|reputation]] that facilitates ransom negotiations. Spanish officials know that Blood will not kill prisoners, making them more willing to pay. Mercy thus becomes a strategic asset — a marked contrast to the self-defeating cruelty of Jeffreys' justice.
