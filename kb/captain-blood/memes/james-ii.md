# James II

## Overview

King James II (1633–1701) is the sovereign whose justice transforms Peter Blood from a physician into a slave, and whose political necessity later offers to transform him from a buccaneer into a colonial governor. He never appears directly in the novel, yet his authority — exercised through agents like Judge Jeffreys, Lord Sunderland, and Lord Julian Wade — is the gravitational centre around which Blood's legal status orbits. Condemned in James's name for serving Monmouth, Blood is later offered a commission "from the King of England" and the deputy-governorship of Jamaica in James's gift. The king is thus both the source of Blood's greatest injustice and the instrument of his ultimate rehabilitation — a paradox that Sabatini deploys to expose the arbitrary nature of sovereign power.

## In the Novel

James II functions in the narrative as an unseen but constantly invoked authority, his name and commission structuring the political choices that define Blood's trajectory.

**The king's justice.** The novel's opening chapters are dominated by the consequences of James's victory. Judge Jeffreys, operating under James's authority, conducts the Bloody Assizes, sentencing Monmouth's followers to death or transportation. Blood, who served as a physician in the rebel army, is among those swept up — his trial a foregone conclusion in a system where the king's justice and the king's vengeance are indistinguishable. The reference to "James Duke of York did first cause" ((001-090)) links James's earlier actions to the present misery of the transported rebels.

**The king's commission.** In a stroke of political irony, it is James II who later authorizes the offer of a commission to Blood. Lord Julian Wade arrives bearing "commissions made out in blank" ((018-287)) under the royal seal, seeking to recruit Blood's buccaneering skill to English service. The offer is framed as an act of royal grace: the same king who condemned Blood now offers him a place in the legitimate order.

**The king's governorship.** The offer escalates from commission to governorship: "you the governorship of Jamaica in His Majesty's" ((031-056)) gift. Blood's response to this offer — sceptical, deliberate, never servile — is one of the novel's key moral statements. He does not refuse, but neither does he forget that the hand offering the governorship is the same hand that signed his death warrant.

**The king's queen.** A small but telling detail: Arabella Bishop is described as having been "lady in waiting upon King James's queen" ((003-105)), Mary of Modena. The connection roots Arabella in the Stuart court and makes her relationship with Blood — a man condemned by the king she served — a complex negotiation between private feeling and public allegiance.

## Historical Basis

James II (1633–1701) was the second surviving son of Charles I and the younger brother of Charles II. As Duke of York, he served as Lord High Admiral and gave his name to the colony of New York (captured from the Dutch in 1664). His conversion to Catholicism around 1668–69 triggered the Exclusion Crisis, during which Parliament attempted to bar him from the succession. He succeeded his brother in February 1685 and immediately faced Monmouth's rebellion, which he crushed decisively. His reign lasted only three years: the Glorious Revolution of 1688 deposed him in favour of his Protestant daughter Mary and her husband William of Orange. His brief but consequential reign thus coincided almost exactly with the fictional timeline of Blood's Caribbean career.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — Condemned to death (and then transportation) in James's name; later offered a commission and governorship by the same king.
- **Lord Sunderland** — James's Secretary of State, who dispatches Lord Julian Wade to recruit Blood.
- **Judge Jeffreys** — The king's agent in the Bloody Assizes, who condemned Blood's fellow rebels.
- **Arabella Bishop** — Former lady-in-waiting to James's queen, Mary of Modena.

## Quotations

> "James Duke of York did first cause" ((001-090)) — the brief historical reference that links James's past actions to the novel's present, establishing the king as the ultimate author of Blood's misfortunes.
