# Trade

## Overview

In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the word "trade" carries a deliberate double meaning that captures the moral ambiguity of the novel's world. On the one hand, it denotes the honest crafts and professions — medicine, soldiering, sailoring — by which men earn their living. On the other, it refers to the [[piracy|buccaneering]] enterprise itself, euphemistically called a "trade" by those who practice it. This linguistic slippage, from peaceful commerce to predatory raiding, mirrors [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s own trajectory: a man trained in the healing trade who becomes, by circumstance, a master of the "desperate trade" of the sea ((013-107)). The novel's treatment of trade thus maps the economic geography of the 17th-century Caribbean, where sugar, tobacco, slaves, and [[treasure|Spanish gold]] all circulate through overlapping networks of legitimate commerce and organized predation.

## Historical Background

The Caribbean of the 1680s was a contested commercial zone. Spain claimed a monopoly over American trade under the Treaty of Tordesillas, but English, French, and Dutch merchants and [[privateering|privateer]]s challenged that monopoly at every opportunity. The [[navigation|Navigation]] Acts (1651 onward) required that English colonial trade be carried in English ships, fueling demand for experienced mariners and creating a legal framework in which the line between merchant and smuggler was often indistinct. [[tortuga|Tortuga]], the buccaneer stronghold north of [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]], operated under semi-official French protection, its governor M. d'Ogeron levying a ten-percent harbour due on all spoils brought into the bay ((013-079)). This arrangement — essentially taxing piracy as if it were legitimate commerce — illustrates how thoroughly trade and raiding were intertwined in the Caribbean economy.

The [[plantation-system|plantation]] system, producing sugar, tobacco, cacao, and logwood for European markets, depended on enslaved African labour and transported European convicts. The rebels of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]], including [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]], were swept into this system not as free traders but as tradeable goods themselves — sold at auction in Bridgetown to [[plantation-system|plantation]] owners who valued their skills as "trades of peace" ((001-109)): carpentry, masonry, smithing, and medicine.

## In the Novel

The novel uses "trade" in three distinct registers, each tied to a different phase of Blood's career.

**The trades of peace.** When the Monmouth rebels are transported to [[barbados|Barbados]], [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] assesses them not as political prisoners but as skilled labour: "carpenters, smiths, masons, bricklayers, cobblers, and representatives of every other of the trades of peace among these improvised men of war" ((001-109)). Blood himself is sold for his medical skill — "the trade for which he had qualified himself before he had begun to live" ((001-177)). His identity as a physician is repeatedly asserted through the language of trade: at Bridgetown he is told to "practise your trade on my patient here" ((003-033)), and even much later he will tell [[lord-willoughby|Lord Willoughby]] that he was "content to pursue a doctor's trade" ((005-193)) before circumstances forced him to sea.

**The piratical trade.** On [[tortuga|Tortuga]], [[piracy|buccaneering]] is described as a "merciless piratical trade" driven "chiefly at the expense of Spain" ((013-045)). When M. d'Ogeron, the governor's representative, proposes that Blood's crew join [[brethren-of-the-coast|the Brethren]] of the Coast, the language shifts consciously: "A trade that might have worn a repellent aspect when urged by greasy, half-drunken adventurers, boucan-hunters, lumbermen, beach-combers, English, French, and Dutch, became a dignified, almost official form of [[piracy|privateering]] when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France herself" ((013-079)). Blood's own inner conflict is framed in the same terms — he hopes "to help him keep his hands as clean as a man might in this desperate trade upon which he was embarking" ((013-107)).

**The trader's world.** In the final act, when Blood serves as a [[authority|command]]er under King Louis of France, the Caribbean is depicted as a bustling commercial zone: [[port-royal|Port Royal]] and Petit Goave are filled with "bustling traders of several nations — English, French, Dutch, and Spanish" ((025-088)), suggesting the return of legitimate commerce after the [[piracy|buccaneering]] interlude.

## Examples

- **The Bridgetown slave auction**: Blood and his fellow rebels are valued by their civilian trades — carpenters, smiths, masons — in a scene that reduces political prisoners to units of skilled labour ((001-109)–(001-148)).
- **M. d'Ogeron's proposal**: The French governor's agent reframes piracy as a dignified commercial venture, complete with taxation and bills of exchange upon France ((013-079)–(013-082)).
- **Blood's self-assessment**: Before [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]], Blood insists he was content with his doctor's trade ((005-193)), weaponizing the language of peaceful vocation against accusations of ambition or criminality.
