# Duke of Monmouth

## Overview

James Scott, Duke of Monmouth (1649–1685), is the historical figure whose failed rebellion provides the catalyst for *Captain Blood*. Though Monmouth never appears directly in the narrative — his fate is sealed before the novel's main action begins — his presence haunts the opening chapters, where his defeated followers, including Peter Blood, are swept up in the brutal judicial reprisals that follow the Battle of Sedgemoor. In a twist of historical irony that Sabatini exploits to the full, the "illustrious and high-born Prince James, Duke of Monmouth, son and heir" ((001-087)) of Charles II is also the reason that an obscure Irish physician finds himself transported in chains to the Caribbean, where he will reinvent himself as the most famous buccaneer of his age.

## In the Novel

Monmouth functions in the narrative as the absent cause — the figure whose ambition triggers the chain of events that transforms Peter Blood from a respectable doctor into a Caribbean buccaneer.

**The rebellion as backstory.** The novel opens with Blood already caught up in the aftermath of Monmouth's defeat. The rebellion is introduced through exposition and dialogue, particularly a conversation in which Blood is described as having been present at Sedgemoor. "King Monmouth's army. That Latin line, cont…" ((001-075)) — the reference to Blood's medical service in the rebel army establishes both his political sympathies and the legal vulnerability that will destroy his quiet life in Bridgewater.

**The contested succession.** Sabatini sketches the political context with economy: Monmouth "who now proclaimed himself King" ((001-099)), challenging his uncle James II's accession. The novel does not take sides in the constitutional question, but the fact that Blood served Monmouth rather than James is crucial to the plot — it is the reason Blood can later be offered a commission by the very king whose justice condemned him, creating the central irony of the novel's political architecture.

**The aftermath.** Monmouth's execution after Sedgemoor (July 1685) is not directly depicted, but its consequences dominate the early chapters: the transport of captured rebels to the Caribbean plantations, the Bloody Assizes conducted by Judge Jeffreys, and the transformation of English political prisoners into Caribbean slave labour. "Monmouth's intention to deliver battle" ((001-116)) is the decision that, more than any other single act in the novel, shapes Blood's destiny.

## Historical Basis

James Scott (1649–1685) was the eldest illegitimate son of Charles II by his mistress Lucy Walter. Created Duke of Monmouth in 1663, he was a popular Protestant figure during the Exclusion Crisis (1679–1681), when Parliament attempted to exclude the Catholic James, Duke of York, from the succession. After Charles II's death in February 1685, Monmouth landed at Lyme Regis in June, declared himself king, and raised an army primarily composed of West Country artisans and farmers — the "trades of peace" men who would later be transported alongside Blood. His force was decisively defeated at Sedgemoor on 6 July 1685, and Monmouth was executed on Tower Hill on 15 July. The judicial reprisals conducted by Judge Jeffreys in the "Bloody Assizes" followed in August–September 1685, resulting in approximately 320 executions and over 800 transportations to the Caribbean colonies.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — Served as a physician in Monmouth's army; his association with the rebellion leads to his arrest and transportation.
- **King James II** — Monmouth's uncle and rival for the throne; his victory leads to the judicial reprisals that ensnare Blood.
- **Judge Jeffreys** — The judge who conducted the Bloody Assizes, condemning Monmouth's followers to death or transportation.

## Quotations

> "The illustrious and high-born Prince James, Duke of Monmouth, son and heir" ((001-087)) — the ironic description of the man whose ambition, however legitimate in Protestant eyes, will destroy the lives of thousands of his followers.
