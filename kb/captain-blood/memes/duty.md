# Duty

## Overview

Duty is one of the central moral threads running through *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, appearing in multiple forms: medical duty (the physician's obligation to heal), military duty (the soldier's obligation to serve), and political duty (the subject's obligation to [[crown|the Crown]]). The novel repeatedly tests whether duty survives when institutions betray their servants — and whether a man who has been unjustly condemned owes anything to the system that condemned him.

## Historical Background

The concept of duty in 17th-century England was rooted in the hierarchical social order: duty to God, to King, and to one's station. A physician's "calling" was both a profession and a sacred trust. The [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] itself was framed by both sides as a conflict of duties — Monmouth's supporters claimed a higher duty to Protestant succession, while James II's government prosecuted them for violating their duty of loyalty.

## In the Novel

### Medical Duty

The novel's central [[justice|injustice]] arises directly from [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s adherence to medical duty. Captured while treating the wounded after the [[battle-of-sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]], Blood defends himself before [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] by invoking "the duty imposed upon me by my calling" ((003-467)). He had dressed the wounds of a rebel "as was my sacred duty" ((003-489)) as a physician. Jeffreys explodes in fury: "Thy sacred duty, rogue, is to..." — the expectation being loyalty to [[crown|the Crown]], not to suffering humanity ((003-491)). This collision between medical ethics and political obedience sets the entire plot in motion.

### Military Duty

Throughout the novel, military duty appears as both a constraint and a justification. [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] represents duty corrupted into cruelty; [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] represents duty tempered by [[honour|honor]]. When Blood encounters enemies who have "nobly done their duty" ((009-122)), he recognizes their professionalism even as he defeats them. [[don-diego|Don Diego de Espinosa]] is motivated by "his duty to his country" in addition to personal vengeance ((016-166)). The honest soldier's refrain — "I know my duty to my King" ((017-170)) — appears as both genuine commitment and self-serving rationalization.
