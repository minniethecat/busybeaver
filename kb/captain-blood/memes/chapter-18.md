[018-001] Sneeringly the Admiral laughed.

[018-002] “Aha! They hesitate, these English dogs! Por Dios, and well they may.”

[018-003] “They will be waiting for night,” suggested his nephew, who stood at his

[018-004] elbow quivering with excitement.

[018-005] [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] looked at him, smiling. “And what shall the night avail them

[018-006] in this narrow passage, under the very muzzles of my guns? Be sure,

[018-007] Esteban, that to-night your father will be paid for.”

[018-008] He raised his telescope to continue his observation of the buccaneers.

[018-009] He saw that the piraguas towed by each vessel were being warped

[018-010] alongside, and he wondered a little what this manoeuver might portend.

[018-011] Awhile those piraguas were hidden from view behind the hulls. Then one

[018-012] by one they reappeared, rowing round and away from the ships, and each

[018-013] boat, he observed, was crowded with armed men. Thus laden, they were

[018-014] headed for the shore, at a point where it was densely wooded to the

[018-015] water's edge. The eyes of the wondering Admiral followed them until the

[018-016] foliage screened them from his view.

[018-017] Then he lowered his telescope and looked at his officers.

[018-018] “What the devil does it mean?” he asked.

[018-019] None answered him, all being as puzzled as he was himself.

[018-020] After a little while, Esteban, who kept his eyes on the water, plucked

[018-021] at his uncle's sleeve. “There they go!” he cried, and pointed.

[018-022] And there, indeed, went the piraguas on their way back to the ships.

[018-023] But now it was observed that they were empty, save for the men who rowed

[018-024] them. Their armed cargo had been left ashore.

[018-025] Back to the ships they pulled, to return again presently with a fresh

[018-026] load of armed men, which similarly they conveyed to Palomas. And at last

[018-027] one of the Spanish officers ventured an explanation:

[018-028] “They are going to attack us by land--to attempt to storm the fort.”

[018-029] “Of course.” The Admiral smiled. “I had guessed it. Whom the gods would

[018-030] destroy they first make mad.”

[018-031] “Shall we make a sally?” urged Esteban, in his excitement.

[018-032] “A sally? Through that scrub? That would be to play into their hands.

[018-033] No, no, we will wait here to receive this attack. Whenever it comes, it

[018-034] is themselves will be destroyed, and utterly. Have no doubt of that.”

[018-035] But by evening the Admiral's equanimity was not quite so perfect. By

[018-036] then the piraguas had made a half-dozen journeys with their loads

[018-037] of men, and they had landed also--as Don Miguel had clearly observed

[018-038] through his telescope--at least a dozen guns.

[018-039] His countenance no longer smiled; it was a little wrathful and a little

[018-040] troubled now as he turned again to his officers.

[018-041] “Who was the fool who told me that they number but three hundred men in

[018-042] all? They have put at least twice that number ashore already.”

[018-043] Amazed as he was, his amazement would have been deeper had he been told

[018-044] the truth: that there was not a single buccaneer or a single gun ashore

[018-045] on Palomas. The deception had been complete. Don Miguel could not guess

[018-046] that the men he had beheld in those piraguas were always the same; that

[018-047] on the journeys to the shore they sat and stood upright in full view;

[018-048] and that on the journeys back to the ships, they lay invisible at the

[018-049] bottom of the boats, which were thus made to appear empty.

[018-050] The growing fears of the Spanish soldiery at the prospect of a night

[018-051] attack from the landward side by the entire buccaneer force--and a

[018-052] force twice as strong as they had suspected the pestilent Blood to

[018-053] [[authority|command]]--began to be communicated to the Admiral.

[018-054] In the last hours of fading daylight, the Spaniards did precisely what

[018-055] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] so confidently counted that they would do--precisely what

[018-056] they must do to meet the attack, preparations for which had been so

[018-057] thoroughly simulated. They set themselves to labour like the damned at

[018-058] those ponderous guns emplaced to command the narrow passage out to sea.

[018-059] Groaning and sweating, urged on by the curses and even the whips of

[018-060] their officers, they toiled in a frenzy of panic-stricken haste to shift

[018-061] the greater number and the more powerful of their guns across to the

[018-062] landward side, there to emplace them anew, so that they might be ready

[018-063] to receive the attack which at any moment now might burst upon them from

[018-064] the woods not half a mile away.

[018-065] Thus, when night fell, although in mortal anxiety of the onslaught of

[018-066] those wild devils whose reckless courage was a byword on the seas of the

[018-067] Main, at least the Spaniards were tolerably prepared for it. Waiting,

[018-068] they stood to their guns.

[018-069] And whilst they waited thus, under cover of the darkness and as the tide

[018-070] began to ebb, Captain Blood's fleet weighed anchor quietly; and, as once

[018-071] before, with no more canvas spread than that which their sprits could

[018-072] carry, so as to give them steering way--and even these having been

[018-073] painted black--the four vessels, without a light showing, groped their

[018-074] way by soundings to the channel which led to that narrow passage out to

[018-075] sea.

[018-076] The Elizabeth and the Infanta, leading side by side, were almost abreast

[018-077] of the fort before their shadowy bulks and the soft gurgle of water at

[018-078] their prows were detected by the Spaniards, whose attention until that

[018-079] moment had been all on the other side. And now there arose on the night

[018-080] air such a sound of human baffled fury as may have resounded about Babel

[018-081] at the confusion of tongues. To heighten that confusion, and to scatter

[018-082] disorder among the Spanish soldiery, the Elizabeth emptied her larboard

[018-083] guns into the fort as she was swept past on the swift ebb.

[018-084] At once realizing--though not yet how--he had been duped, and that his

[018-085] prey was in the very act of escaping after all, the Admiral frantically

[018-086] ordered the guns that had been so laboriously moved to be dragged back

[018-087] to their former emplacements, and commanded his gunners meanwhile to

[018-088] the slender batteries that of all his powerful, but now unavailable,

[018-089] armament still remained trained upon the channel. With these, after the

[018-090] loss of some precious moments, the fort at last made fire.

[018-091] It was answered by a terrific broadside from [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]], which had

[018-092] now drawn abreast, and was crowding canvas to her yards. The enraged

[018-093] and gibbering Spaniards had a brief vision of her as the line of flame

[018-094] spurted from her red flank, and the thunder of her broadside drowned

[018-095] the noise of the creaking halyards. After that they saw her no more.

[018-096] Assimilated by the friendly darkness which the lesser Spanish guns were

[018-097] speculatively stabbing, the escaping ships fired never another shot that

[018-098] might assist their baffled and bewildered enemies to locate them.

[018-099] Some slight damage was sustained by Blood's fleet. But by the time the

[018-100] Spaniards had resolved their confusion into some order of dangerous

[018-101] offence, that fleet, well served by a southerly breeze, was through the

[018-102] narrows and standing out to sea.

[018-103] Thus was Don Miguel de Espinosa left to chew the bitter cud of a lost

[018-104] opportunity, and to consider in what terms he would acquaint the

[018-105] Supreme Council of the Catholic King that Peter Blood had got away from

[018-106] Maracaybo, taking with him two twenty-gun frigates that were lately

[018-107] the property of Spain, to say nothing of two hundred and fifty thousand

[018-108] [[ransom|pieces of eight]] and other plunder. And all this in spite of Don Miguel's

[018-109] four galleons and his heavily armed fort that at one time had held the

[018-110] pirates so securely trapped.

[018-111] Heavy, indeed, grew the account of Peter Blood, which Don Miguel swore

[018-112] passionately to Heaven should at all costs to himself be paid in full.

[018-113] Nor were the losses already detailed the full total of those suffered

[018-114] on this occasion by the King of Spain. For on the following evening,

[018-115] off the coast of Oruba, at the mouth of the Gulf of Venezuela, Captain

[018-116] Blood's fleet came upon the belated Santo Nino, speeding under full sail

[018-117] to reenforce Don Miguel at Maracaybo.

[018-118] At first the Spaniard had conceived that she was meeting the victorious

[018-119] fleet of Don Miguel, returning from the destruction of the pirates. When

[018-120] at comparatively close quarters the pennon of St. George soared to the

[018-121] [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]]'s masthead to disillusion her, the Santo Nino chose the better

[018-122] part of valour, and struck her flag.

[018-123] Captain Blood ordered her crew to take to the boats, and land themselves

[018-124] at Oruba or wherever else they pleased. So considerate was he that to

[018-125] assist them he presented them with several of the piraguas which he

[018-126] still had in tow.

[018-127] “You will find,” said he to her captain, “that Don Miguel is in an

[018-128] extremely bad temper. Commend me to him, and say that I venture to

[018-129] remind him that he must blame himself for all the ills that have

[018-130] befallen him. The evil has recoiled upon him which he loosed when

[018-131] he sent his brother unofficially to make a raid upon the island of

[018-132] [[barbados|Barbados]]. Bid him think twice before he lets his devils loose upon an

[018-133] English settlement again.”

[018-134] With that he dismissed the Captain, who went over the side of the Santo

[018-135] Nino, and Captain Blood proceeded to investigate the value of this

[018-136] further prize. When her hatches were removed, a human cargo was

[018-137] disclosed in her hold.

[018-138] “Slaves,” said [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]], and persisted in that belief cursing Spanish

[018-139] devilry until Cahusac crawled up out of the dark bowels of the ship, and

[018-140] stood blinking in the sunlight.

[018-141] There was more than sunlight to make the Breton pirate blink. And

[018-142] those that crawled out after him--the remnants of his crew--cursed him

[018-143] horribly for the pusillanimity which had brought them into the ignominy

[018-144] of owing their deliverance to those whom they had deserted as lost

[018-145] beyond hope.

[018-146] Their sloop had encountered and had been sunk three days ago by the

[018-147] Santo Nino, and Cahusac had narrowly escaped [[execution|hanging]] merely that for

[018-148] some time he might be a mock among the Brethren of the Coast.

[018-149] For many a month thereafter he was to hear in [[tortuga|Tortuga]] the jeering taunt:

[018-150] “Where do you spend the gold that you brought back from Maracaybo?”

[018-151] CHAPTER XVIII. THE MILAGROSA

[018-152] The affair at Maracaybo is to be considered as Captain Blood's

[018-153] [[piracy|buccaneering]] masterpiece. Although there is scarcely one of the many

[018-154] actions that he fought--recorded in such particular detail by Jeremy

[018-155] [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]]--which does not afford some instance of his genius for naval

[018-156] tactics, yet in none is this more shiningly displayed than in those two

[018-157] engagements by which he won out of the trap which Don Miguel de Espinosa

[018-158] had sprung upon him.

[018-159] The fame which he had enjoyed before this, great as it already was, is

[018-160] dwarfed into insignificance by the fame that followed. It was a fame

[018-161] such as no buccaneer--not even Morgan--has ever boasted, before or

[018-162] since.

[018-163] In Tortuga, during the months he spent there refitting the three ships

[018-164] he had captured from the fleet that had gone out to destroy him, he

[018-165] found himself almost an object of worship in the eyes of the wild

[018-166] Brethren of the Coast, all of whom now clamoured for the honour of

[018-167] serving under him. It placed him in the rare position of being able

[018-168] to pick and choose the crews for his augmented fleet, and he chose

[018-169] fastidiously. When next he sailed away it was with a fleet of five fine

[018-170] ships in which went something over a thousand men. Thus you behold him

[018-171] not merely famous, but really formidable. The three captured Spanish

[018-172] vessels he had renamed with a certain scholarly humour the Clotho,

[018-173] Lachesis, and Atropos, a grimly jocular manner of conveying to the world

[018-174] that he made them the arbiters of the fate of any Spaniards he should

[018-175] henceforth encounter upon the seas.

[018-176] In Europe the news of this fleet, following upon the news of the Spanish

[018-177] Admiral's defeat at Maracaybo, produced something of a sensation. Spain

[018-178] and England were variously and unpleasantly exercised, and if you care

[018-179] to turn up the diplomatic correspondence exchanged on the subject, you

[018-180] will find that it is considerable and not always amiable.

[018-181] And meanwhile in the Caribbean, the Spanish Admiral Don Miguel de

[018-182] Espinosa might be said--to use a term not yet invented in his day--to

[018-183] have run amok. The disgrace into which he had fallen as a result of the

[018-184] disasters suffered at the hands of Captain Blood had driven the Admiral

[018-185] all but mad. It is impossible, if we impose our minds impartially,

[018-186] to withhold a certain sympathy from Don Miguel. Hate was now this

[018-187] unfortunate man's daily bread, and the hope of vengeance an obsession to

[018-188] his mind. As a madman he went raging up and down the Caribbean seeking

[018-189] his enemy, and in the meantime, as an hors d'oeuvre to his vindictive

[018-190] appetite, he fell upon any ship of England or of France that loomed

[018-191] above his horizon.

[018-192] I need say no more to convey the fact that this illustrious sea-captain

[018-193] and great gentleman of Castile had lost his head, and was become a

[018-194] pirate in his turn. The Supreme Council of Castile might anon condemn

[018-195] him for his practices. But how should that matter to one who already was

[018-196] condemned beyond redemption? On the contrary, if he should live to lay

[018-197] the audacious and ineffable Blood by the heels, it was possible that

[018-198] Spain might view his present irregularities and earlier losses with a

[018-199] more lenient eye.

[018-200] And so, reckless of the fact that Captain Blood was now in vastly

[018-201] superior strength, the Spaniard sought him up and down the trackless

[018-202] seas. But for a whole year he sought him vainly. The circumstances in

[018-203] which eventually they met are very curious.

[018-204] An intelligent observation of the facts of human existence will reveal

[018-205] to shallow-minded folk who sneer at the use of coincidence in the arts

[018-206] of fiction and drama that life itself is little more than a series of

[018-207] coincidences. Open the history of the past at whatsoever page you will,

[018-208] and there you shall find coincidence at work bringing about events that

[018-209] the merest chance might have averted. Indeed, coincidence may be defined

[018-210] as the very tool used by Fate to shape the destinies of men and nations.

[018-211] Observe it now at work in the affairs of Captain Blood and of some

[018-212] others.

[018-213] On the 15th September of the year 1688--a memorable year in the annals

[018-214] of England--three ships were afloat upon the Caribbean, which in their

[018-215] coming conjunctions were to work out the fortunes of several persons.

[018-216] The first of these was Captain Blood's flagship the Arabella, which had

[018-217] been separated from the buccaneer fleet in a hurricane off the Lesser

[018-218] Antilles. In somewhere about 17 deg. N. Lat., and 74 deg. Long., she

[018-219] was beating up for the Windward Passage, before the intermittent

[018-220] southeasterly breezes of that stifling season, homing for Tortuga, the

[018-221] natural rendezvous of the dispersed vessels.

[018-222] The second ship was the great Spanish galleon, [[milagrosa|the Milagrosa]], which,

[018-223] accompanied by the smaller frigate Hidalga, lurked off the Caymites,

[018-224] to the north of the long peninsula that thrusts out from the southwest

[018-225] corner of Hispaniola. Aboard the Milagrosa sailed the vindictive Don

[018-226] Miguel.

[018-227] The third and last of these ships with which we are at present concerned

[018-228] was an English man-of-war, which on the date I have given was at anchor

[018-229] in the French port of St. Nicholas on the northwest coast of Hispaniola.

[018-230] She was on her way from Plymouth to [[jamaica|Jamaica]], and carried on board a

[018-231] very distinguished passenger in the person of [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]], who

[018-232] came charged by his kinsman, my Lord Sunderland, with a mission of

[018-233] some consequence and delicacy, directly arising out of that vexatious

[018-234] correspondence between England and Spain.

[018-235] The French Government, like the English, excessively annoyed by the

[018-236] depredations of the buccaneers, and the constant straining of relations

[018-237] with Spain that ensued, had sought in vain to put them down by enjoining

[018-238] the utmost severity against them upon her various overseas governors.

[018-239] But these, either--like the Governor of Tortuga--throve out of a

[018-240] scarcely tacit partnership with the filibusters, or--like the Governor

[018-241] of French Hispaniola--felt that they were to be encouraged as a check

[018-242] upon the power and greed of Spain, which might otherwise be exerted to

[018-243] the disadvantage of the colonies of other nations. They looked, indeed,

[018-244] with apprehension upon recourse to any vigorous measures which must

[018-245] result in driving many of the buccaneers to seek new hunting-grounds in

[018-246] the South Sea.

[018-247] To satisfy King James's anxiety to conciliate Spain, and in response to

[018-248] the Spanish Ambassador's constant and grievous expostulations, my Lord

[018-249] Sunderland, the Secretary of State, had appointed a strong man to the

[018-250] deputy-governorship of Jamaica. This strong man was that Colonel

[018-251] Bishop who for some years now had been the most influential planter in

[018-252] Barbados.

[018-253] [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] had accepted the post, and departed from the plantations

[018-254] in which his great wealth was being amassed with an eagerness that had

[018-255] its roots in a desire to pay off a score of his own with Peter Blood.

[018-256] From his first coming to Jamaica, Colonel Bishop had made himself felt

[018-257] by the buccaneers. But do what he might, the one buccaneer whom he

[018-258] made his particular quarry--that Peter Blood who once had been his

[018-259] slave--eluded him ever, and continued undeterred and in great force

[018-260] to harass the Spaniards upon sea and land, and to keep the relations

[018-261] between England and Spain in a state of perpetual ferment, particularly

[018-262] dangerous in those days when the peace of Europe was precariously

[018-263] maintained.

[018-264] Exasperated not only by his own accumulated chagrin, but also by the

[018-265] reproaches for his failure which reached him from London, Colonel Bishop

[018-266] actually went so far as to consider hunting his quarry in Tortuga

[018-267] itself and making an attempt to clear the island of the buccaneers it

[018-268] sheltered. Fortunately for himself, he abandoned the notion of so insane

[018-269] an enterprise, deterred not only by the enormous natural strength of the

[018-270] place, but also by the reflection that a raid upon what was, nominally

[018-271] at least, a French settlement, must be attended by grave offence to

[018-272] France. Yet short of some such measure, it appeared to Colonel Bishop

[018-273] that he was baffled. He confessed as much in a letter to the Secretary

[018-274] of State.

[018-275] This letter and the state of things which it disclosed made my Lord

[018-276] Sunderland despair of solving this vexatious problem by ordinary means.

[018-277] He turned to the consideration of extraordinary ones, and bethought him

[018-278] of the plan adopted with Morgan, who had been enlisted into the King's

[018-279] service under Charles II. It occurred to him that a similar course might

[018-280] be similarly effective with Captain Blood. His lordship did not omit

[018-281] the consideration that Blood's present outlawry might well have been

[018-282] undertaken not from inclination, but under stress of sheer necessity;

[018-283] that he had been forced into it by the circumstances of his

[018-284] transportation, and that he would welcome the opportunity of emerging

[018-285] from it.

[018-286] Acting upon this conclusion, Sunderland sent out his kinsman, Lord

[018-287] Julian Wade, with some commissions made out in blank, and full

[018-288] directions as to the course which the Secretary considered it desirable

[018-289] to pursue and yet full discretion in the matter of pursuing them. The

[018-290] crafty Sunderland, master of all labyrinths of intrigue, advised his

[018-291] kinsman that in the event of his finding Blood intractable, or judging

[018-292] for other reasons that it was not desirable to enlist him in the King's

[018-293] service, he should turn his attention to the officers serving under him,

[018-294] and by seducing them away from him leave him so weakened that he must

[018-295] fall an easy victim to Colonel Bishop's fleet.

[018-296] The Royal Mary--the vessel bearing that ingenious, tolerably

[018-297] accomplished, mildly dissolute, entirely elegant envoy of my Lord

[018-298] Sunderland's--made a good passage to St. Nicholas, her last port of

[018-299] call before Jamaica. It was understood that as a preliminary Lord Julian

[018-300] should report himself to the Deputy-Governor at [[port-royal|Port Royal]], whence at

[018-301] need he might have himself conveyed to Tortuga. Now it happened that the

[018-302] Deputy-Governor's niece had come to St. Nicholas some months earlier on

[018-303] a visit to some relatives, and so that she might escape the insufferable

[018-304] heat of Jamaica in that season. The time for her return being now at

[018-305] hand, a passage was sought for her aboard the Royal Mary, and in view of

[018-306] her uncle's rank and position promptly accorded.

[018-307] Lord Julian hailed her advent with satisfaction. It gave a voyage that

[018-308] had been full of interest for him just the spice that it required

[018-309] to achieve perfection as an experience. His lordship was one of your

[018-310] gallants to whom existence that is not graced by womankind is more or

[018-311] less of a stagnation. Miss Arabella Bishop--this straight up and down

[018-312] slip of a girl with her rather boyish voice and her almost boyish ease

[018-314] much notice in my lord's discerning eyes. His very sophisticated,

[018-315] carefully educated tastes in such matters inclined him towards the

[018-316] plump, the languishing, and the quite helplessly feminine. Miss

[018-317] Bishop's charms were undeniable. But they were such that it would take a

[018-318] delicate-minded man to appreciate them; and my Lord Julian, whilst of a

[018-319] mind that was very far from gross, did not possess the necessary degree

[018-320] of delicacy. I must not by this be understood to imply anything against

[018-321] him.

[018-322] It remained, however, that Miss Bishop was a young woman and a lady; and

[018-323] in the latitude into which Lord Julian had strayed this was a phenomenon

[018-324] sufficiently rare to command attention. On his side, with his title and

[018-325] position, his personal grace and the charm of a practised courtier, he

[018-326] bore about him the atmosphere of the great world in which normally he

[018-327] had his being--a world that was little more than a name to her, who had

[018-328] spent most of her life in the Antilles. It is not therefore wonderful

[018-332] with stories of St. James's--in many of which he assigned himself a

[018-333] heroic, or at least a distinguished part--and she could enrich his mind

[018-334] with information concerning this new world to which he had come.

[018-335] Before they were out of sight of St. Nicholas they were good friends,

[018-336] and his lordship was beginning to correct his first impressions of her

[018-337] and to discover the charm of that frank, straightforward attitude of

[018-338] comradeship which made her treat every man as a brother. Considering

[018-339] how his mind was obsessed with the business of his mission, it is not

[018-340] wonderful that he should have come to talk to her of Captain Blood.

[018-341] Indeed, there was a circumstance that directly led to it.

[018-342] “I wonder now,” he said, as they were sauntering on the poop, “if

[018-343] you ever saw this fellow Blood, who was at one time on your uncle's

[018-344] plantations as a slave.”

[018-345] Miss Bishop halted. She leaned upon the taffrail, looking out towards

[018-346] the receding land, and it was a moment before she answered in a steady,

[018-347] level voice:

[018-348] “I saw him often. I knew him very well.”

[018-349] “Ye don't say!” His lordship was slightly moved out of an

[018-350] imperturbability that he had studiously cultivated. He was a young man

[018-351] of perhaps eight-and-twenty, well above the middle height in stature

[018-352] and appearing taller by virtue of his exceeding leanness. He had a thin,

[018-353] pale, rather pleasing hatchet-face, framed in the curls of a golden

[018-354] periwig, a sensitive mouth and pale blue eyes that lent his countenance

[018-355] a dreamy expression, a rather melancholy pensiveness. But they were

[018-356] alert, observant eyes notwithstanding, although they failed on this

[018-357] occasion to observe the slight change of colour which his question had

[018-358] brought to Miss Bishop's cheeks or the suspiciously excessive composure

[018-359] of her answer.

[018-360] “Ye don't say!” he repeated, and came to lean beside her. “And what

[018-361] manner of man did you find him?”

[018-362] “In those days I esteemed him for an unfortunate gentleman.”

[018-363] “You were acquainted with his story?”

[018-364] “He told it me. That is why I esteemed him--for the calm fortitude with

[018-365] which he bore adversity. Since then, considering what he has done, I

[018-366] have almost come to doubt if what he told me of himself was true.”

[018-367] “If you mean of the wrongs he suffered at the hands of the Royal

[018-368] Commission that tried the Monmouth rebels, there's little doubt that it

[018-369] would be true enough. He was never out with Monmouth; that is certain.

[018-370] He was convicted on a point of law of which he may well have been

[018-371] ignorant when he committed what was construed into treason. But, faith,

[018-372] he's had his revenge, after a fashion.”

[018-373] “That,” she said in a small voice, “is the unforgivable thing. It has

[018-374] destroyed him--deservedly.”

[018-375] “Destroyed him?” His lordship laughed a little. “Be none so sure of

[018-376] that. He has grown rich, I hear. He has translated, so it is said, his

[018-377] Spanish spoils into French gold, which is being treasured up for him in
