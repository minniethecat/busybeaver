# Lord Willoughby

## Overview

Lord Willoughby is King William III's Governor-General of the West Indies, dispatched to the Caribbean following the Glorious Revolution of 1688. A small, fierce man with leathery cheeks and a voice that "snaps" and "rasps," Willoughby is characterized by an impatient temper, a sharp tongue, and — most importantly — a shrewd capacity to recognize genuine merit when he sees it. Arriving late in the novel (Chapter 29), he serves as the agent of Blood's restoration: it is Willoughby who brings news of the revolution, who witnesses Blood's naval genius at first hand, and who ultimately appoints the former pirate and convicted traitor as Governor of Jamaica. His presence closes the novel's central arc of injustice and redemption — the man condemned by one king is elevated by another's representative, who judges him not by his past but by his demonstrated quality.

## In the Novel

### The Announcement of His Coming

Lord Willoughby is first mentioned indirectly in Chapter 24, when Colonel Bishop learns that "a Governor-General was coming out to the West Indies in the person of Lord Willoughby" ([024-736]), accompanied by a Dutch squadron under Admiral van der Kuylen. The news dismays Bishop, who realizes it will mean the end of his supreme authority as Deputy-Governor of Jamaica. This early mention establishes Willoughby as a distant but inexorable force of institutional change — the representative of the new Williamite order sweeping away the corrupt remnants of James II's administration.

### First Meeting with Captain Blood

Willoughby makes his actual entrance in Chapter 29, when Blood's ship encounters the Dutch Admiral's disabled flagship. Brought aboard the Arabella, Willoughby introduces himself with characteristic directness:

> "I am Lord Willoughby, King William's Governor-General of the West Indies, and this is Admiral van der Kuylen, commander of His Majesty's West Indian fleet, at present mislaid somewhere in this damned Caribbean Sea."
> ([029-080])

Blood's ignorance of the Glorious Revolution — he has been "out of touch with the world for the last three months" ([029-104]) — provokes Willoughby's astonishment and a brisk summary of the political transformation: James II has fled to France, William of Orange now rules jointly with Queen Mary, and England is at war with France ([029-106]). This exchange is the pivot on which the entire third act turns — Blood learns that he is no longer an outlaw but a potential servant of the Crown.

When Blood laughs at the irony of Bishop having sailed to hunt him "before news reached him of the change of government at home, and the war with France," Willoughby snaps back with characteristic force:

> "He did not," snapped Willoughby. "He was informed of both, and also of my coming before he set out."
> ([029-216])

Willoughby's anger at Bishop's desertion of his post is volcanic. He denounces the absent Deputy-Governor as "an epitome of misrule" ([029-242]) and threatens that Bishop "shall answer for it with his head if there's any mischief done" ([029-258]). He even speculates darkly that the desertion might be deliberate — that Bishop, having received his commission from James II, may be a Jacobite traitor rather than merely a fool ([029-264]).

### The Pact

When Blood proposes to engage de Rivarol's far superior force at Port Royal, Willoughby initially objects on tactical grounds:

> "But you are not equal to it, damme!" cried his lordship. "Any one of the Frenchman's three ships is a match for both yours, my man."
> ([029-276])

Blood's confident reply — "there's more than guns that matter in these affairs" — and his offer to give Willoughby a demonstration of how "an action should be fought at sea" ([029-280]) begins to shift the Governor-General's skepticism. When Blood reveals Rivarol's cargo of forty million livres in plunder from Cartagena and proposes that the Frenchman "doesn't come out of Port Royal with it again," Willoughby abruptly perceives the strategic genius before him. He stands, extends his hand, and delivers his verdict:

> "Captain Blood, I discover greatness in you," said he.
> ([029-318])

The self-deprecating Irishman's reply — "Sure it's your lordship has the fine sight to perceive it" ([029-320]) — completes the master-to-servant transformation: Blood acknowledges the compliment with his characteristic irony, while Willoughby proves himself able to see past the pirate to the commander.

### Witness to the Battle

Throughout the opening stages of Chapter 30, Willoughby shares the impatient suspense of Admiral van der Kuylen as Blood delays engagement:

> "Aye — in God's name!" snapped Willoughby.
> ([030-004])

When Blood explains his strategy — waiting until the fort has done damage to Rivarol's ships before committing his own — Willoughby's suspicious nature surfaces:

> Lord Willoughby eyed him suspiciously. "Ye think that in the meantime Bishop may come back or Admiral van der Kuylen's fleet appear?"
> ([030-040])

Blood's calm correction earns Willoughby's quick revision of judgment:

> "Aye, aye!" The sharp approval came like a cough from the little Governor-General. "I perceive your object, and I believe ye're entirely right. Ye have the qualities of a great commander, Captain Blood. I beg your pardon for having misunderstood you."
> ([030-058])

This is a telling moment: Willoughby is not too proud to admit error. His willingness to apologize to a man he met only hours before marks him as a superior judge of character — one who values truth over vanity.

During the desperate boarding action against the Victorieuse, Willoughby shares van der Kuylen's despair as Blood's flagship founders under them:

> Thus, with an oath, opined the Dutch Admiral, and from Lord Willoughby there was a word of blame for Blood's seamanship in having risked all upon this gambler's throw of boarding.
> ([030-216])

Yet when Blood's desperate tactics succeed, Willoughby watches from the poop "in breathless amazement the speed and precision with which Blood and his desperate crew had gone to work" ([030-288]). He and the Dutchman are left alone on the abandoned Arabella's quarter-deck as the buccaneer crew swarms over the French vessel — witnesses to an impossible victory ([030-306]).

### The Apology and the Offer

After the battle, when Blood stands bloodied and exhausted on the captured Victorieuse, Willoughby approaches him with his second apology of the day:

> "I think, Captain Blood, that it is necessary I should beg your pardon for the second time. Never before have I seen the impossible made possible by resource and valour, or victory so gallantly snatched from defeat."
> ([030-378])

This is the language of genuine admiration. Willoughby, who began the day dismissive of this pirate captain, ends it acknowledging that he has witnessed something unprecedented. The repetition of apology — twice in a single day — underscores Willoughby's fundamental decency and his capacity for intellectual honesty.

In Chapter 31, with Port Royal saved and Jamaica secure, Willoughby is eager to continue his tour of inspection but is delayed by the absence of Colonel Bishop:

> "And meanwhile," he complained to his Admiral, "I am detained here by the absence of this fool of a Deputy-Governor."
> ([031-036])

Van der Kuylen suggests Blood as Bishop's replacement, and Willoughby — who had already entertained the same thought — agrees instantly:

> "You think so, too, eh? Egad! I had thought of it; and, rip me, why not? He's a better man than Morgan, and Morgan was made Governor."
> ([031-066])

The comparison to Henry Morgan, the most famous buccaneer-turned-Governor in Caribbean history, is deliberate and significant. Willoughby is not merely rewarding a useful subordinate; he is making a historical judgment.

### The Governor-Appointment

Summoned before Willoughby, Blood is "a trifle dazzled by the honour proposed" ([031-070]) and demurs — he dreams of returning to England and the apple-blossoms of Somerset. Willoughby's response is explosive and unforgettable:

> "Apple-blossoms!" His lordship's voice shot up like a rocket, and cracked on the word. "What the devil...? Apple-blossoms!"
> ([031-088])

The Governor-General will have none of Blood's "damned sentimental nonsense" ([031-116]). He delivers a stern lecture on duty, pointing out that Blood has a past score to wipe clean and that the governorship represents the path to rehabilitation:

> "If you want your past forgotten, and your future assured, this is your chance. And you are not to treat it lightly on account of apple-blossoms or any other damned sentimental nonsense. Your duty lies here, at least for as long as the war lasts."
> ([031-112])

When Blood continues to hesitate — his thoughts on Arabella Bishop, unseen since his arrival — Willoughby's "rasping voice" cuts in again, "upbraiding him for his hesitation, pointing out to him his incredible stupidity in trifling with such a golden opportunity" ([031-134]). The dressing-down works: Blood accepts the commission.

Willoughby seals the appointment, leaving Colonel Bishop's fate to the new Governor's discretion:

> "He becomes your affair. You are now the Governor. You will deal with him as you think proper on his return. Hang him from his own yardarm. He deserves it."
> ([031-176])

After Blood marshals his buccaneers and distributes the captured treasure "with the sanction of Lord Willoughby" ([031-198]), the Governor-General prepares to depart, leaving behind a transformed Jamaica — and a transformed Blood.

### Final Appearance: The Arrest of Bishop

Willoughby's final direct intervention occurs when Colonel Bishop returns to Port Royal. Learning of Bishop's arrival, Blood sends a hurried note to Willoughby aboard van der Kuylen's flagship ([031-230]). When Bishop, defiant and ignorant of the changed circumstances, confronts the man who has displaced him, it is not Blood alone who humbles him — Willoughby is present and delivers the crushing identification:

> "I am Lord Willoughby, Governor General of His Majesty's colonies in the West Indies. You were informed, I think, of my coming."
> ([031-442])

Bishop's anger collapses into "a sweat of fear" ([031-452]). Willoughby, "on the point of sailing" and with no time for explanations, simply refers Bishop to the new Governor for justice ([031-458]). The reversal is complete: the man who once held supreme authority in Jamaica is now a prisoner of the man he had bought as a slave.

Willoughby's departure is marked by van der Kuylen's characteristic observation:

> "Id is fery boedigal!" he said, his blue eyes twinkling. "Cabdain Blood is fond of boedry — you remember de abble-blossoms. So? Ha, ha!"
> ([031-518])

The Dutch Admiral's joke — that Blood's elevation from pirate to Governor is "very poetical" — captures the fairy-tale quality of the ending. And Lord Willoughby, who set that ending in motion and departs satisfied that he has placed the right man in power, leaves the novel as he entered it: efficient, decisive, and vindicated in his judgment.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — The relationship between Willoughby and Blood evolves dramatically over less than two days. It begins in mutual ignorance (Willoughby does not know Blood, Blood does not know William III), proceeds through skepticism, and culminates in profound mutual respect. Willoughby is the instrument of Blood's restoration — the authority figure who judges him not by his past but by his demonstrated quality and elevates him accordingly.
- **Admiral van der Kuylen** — Willoughby's Dutch counterpart and companion throughout his West Indian mission. The two men function as a complementary pair: Willoughby fiery and impatient, van der Kuylen phlegmatic and humorously detached. Their shared experience of watching Blood's seamanship transforms both from skeptics to admirers.
- **Colonel Bishop** — Willoughby's contempt for Bishop is absolute. He sees the Deputy-Governor as a fool, a deserter of duty, and possibly a traitor to the new regime. Willoughby leaves Bishop's fate to Blood, departing with the casual suggestion that the new Governor "hang him from his own yardarm."
- **M. de Rivarol** — Though they never meet directly, Willoughby's mission — to secure the West Indies for William III — makes Rivarol his adversary. Willoughby witnesses Blood's defeat of the French commander and approves the seizure of Rivarol's treasure for the Crown.
