## Overview

Cayona is the main harbour settlement on the island of Tortuga, serving as the de facto capital of the Caribbean buccaneers in *Captain Blood*. A natural rock-bound bay "which Nature seemed to have designed for the stronghold of those who had appropriated it," Cayona is where Peter Blood first arrives after escaping Barbados, where he transacts business with French governor M. d'Ogeron, and from which he later departs for the Cartagena expedition ([013-002]).

## In the Novel

### First Arrival

Two days after releasing the Spanish prisoners on Hispaniola, the *Cinco Llagas* "sailed into the rock-bound bay of Cayona," the buccaneer stronghold on Tortuga. Blood's arrival marks his first entry into the world of the Brethren of the Coast, the buccaneer confederation that operates from this island base ([013-002]).

### D'Ogeron's Hospitality

On the eastern outskirts of Cayona, the French governor M. d'Ogeron had built himself a residence with "green-jalousied windows" set in "a spacious and luxuriant garden to the east of Cayona." Here Captain Blood becomes "a very welcome guest"—the governor being in Blood's debt for more than the twenty thousand pieces of eight that the Captain had provided from the *Cinco Llagas*'s cargo ([016-042]).

### The Quays of Cayona

By the later chapters of the novel, the quays of Cayona have become crowded with the human flotsam of the buccaneer world—adventurers, escaped bondsmen, and "dunghill-queans from the Old World" who collectively "converted the quays of Cayona into a disreputable image of Babel" ([025-092]). It is from this chaotic but vibrant port that Captain Blood eventually takes his leave, "winning clear at last, and after difficulties," to join the French expedition against Cartagena.

## Historical Context

Historically, Cayona was the main settlement on the island of Tortuga (Île de la Tortue) off the northern coast of Hispaniola. During the mid-to-late 17th century, it served as the principal base of operations for the Brethren of the Coast, the loose confederation of buccaneers, privateers, and filibusters who preyed upon Spanish shipping in the Caribbean. The French established formal control of Tortuga in the 1660s under Governor Bertrand d'Ogeron, who regularized the buccaneer presence by issuing French letters of marque. Cayona's natural harbour made it one of the most strategically important anchorages in the Caribbean during the buccaneering era.
