# Lord Feversham

**Lord Feversham** (Louis de Duras, 2nd Earl of Feversham) is the Royalist general who commanded the forces of [[james-ii|King James II]] against the [[monmouth|Duke of Monmouth]] at the Battle of Sedgemoor in 1685. Although he does not appear directly in the novel's dramatic scenes, Feversham's presence looms over the early chapters as the victorious commander whose actions set in motion the brutal aftermath that ensnares [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]].

## In the Novel

### The Eve of Battle

Feversham enters the narrative through Peter Blood's strategic assessment on the night before Sedgemoor. Monmouth, having learned of the Royalist army's position, resolves to deliver battle immediately by launching a surprise night attack. Blood, the seasoned soldier, evaluates the plan:

> Mr. Blood assumed that Lord Feversham would be equally well-informed, and if in this assumption he was wrong, at least he was justified of it. [001-118, 001-119, 001-120]

Blood's instinct proves correct. Feversham is indeed prepared for the attack, and Monmouth's gamble — counting on the element of surprise against a professional commander — fails catastrophically. The novel frames Blood's judgment here as the mark of a man who understands military realities: professional generals are not easily surprised.

### The Battle

The battle itself unfolds largely offstage, with Blood and his companions observing from a distance. The narrative's assessment is damning: Monmouth's advantages in surprise and numbers are squandered by poor leadership [002-032, 002-033]. The implication is clear — Feversham's steady generalship, contrasted with Monmouth's bungling, makes the outcome inevitable.

> The advantages he derived from falling by surprise upon an army that was more or less asleep were all lost to him by blundering and bad leadership before ever he was at grips with Feversham. [002-032, 002-033]

### The Aftermath

After the battle, as [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]] and Blood flee southward through the night, their direction ironically brings them closer to the source of danger. Pitt's chosen route, aiming to put distance between them and the pursuing dragoons, leads them near Feversham's headquarters [002-127]. The fugitives must navigate past the centre of Royalist power to reach safety.

> But as Pitt's direction was a southward one, bringing them ever nearer to Feversham's headquarters, they were presently clear of that human flotsam and jetsam of the battle. [002-126, 002-127, 002-128]

### The Brutal Suppression

Feversham's most consequential role is in the repression that follows Sedgemoor. In collaboration with [[colonel-kirke|Colonel Kirke]] — the notorious commander of "Kirke's Lambs" — Feversham oversees the summary execution of prisoners:

> Even so, in that first week after Sedgemoor, Kirke and Feversham contrived between them to put to death over a hundred men after a trial so summary as to be no trial at all. [003-210, 003-211, 003-212]

Only the intervention of Bishop Mews, the Bishop of Winchester, halts the drumhead courts-martial [003-208]. This bloodshed is the direct prelude to the arrival of [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] and the formal Bloody Assizes — and it is in the wake of this repression that Peter Blood is swept up, tried, and condemned to transportation.

## Historical Basis

Louis de Duras (c. 1641–1709) was a French-born nobleman who became a naturalized Englishman and a trusted military commander of King James II. Created Earl of Feversham in 1676, he commanded the royal forces that defeated Monmouth at Sedgemoor on 6 July 1685 — the last pitched battle fought on English soil. Following the Glorious Revolution of 1688, Feversham remained loyal to James II and his military career in England effectively ended.

Sabatini's portrayal of Feversham is historically accurate in outline: the general's professional competence, the decisive nature of his victory at Sedgemoor, and the harshness of the post-battle repression are all consistent with the historical record. The novel's sharpest criticism — that the trials following Sedgemoor were "so summary as to be no trial at all" — reflects the judgment of many contemporary observers and later historians.
