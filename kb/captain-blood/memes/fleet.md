# Fleet

## Overview

The fleet in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* functions as the primary instrument of naval [[authority|power]] and a symbol of [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s rising fortunes. Beginning with a single captured Spanish vessel, Blood's fleet grows through successive victories — the *[[cinco-llagas|Cinco Llagas]]* is the first acquisition, followed by prizes taken from [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]] and the Spanish [[treasure|treasure fleet]]. By the novel's climax, Blood [[authority|command]]s a fleet of five ships, a force so formidable that it attracts attention in the courts of Europe (018-176). The fleet is not merely a military asset but the embodiment of Blood's transition from fugitive slave to acknowledged [[admiral|naval commander]].

## Historical Background

In the late 17th century, the Caribbean was a contested maritime theater where naval [[authority|power]] was measured in squadrons. The Spanish [[treasure|treasure fleet]] (*flota de Indias*) sailed annually between the New World and Spain, carrying silver and goods — a tempting target for English, French, and Dutch [[privateering|privateer]]s. A buccaneer [[authority|command]]er's strength was reckoned by the number and quality of ships under his [[authority|command]]. The ability to man and provision a fleet required not only [[navigation|seamanship]] but the loyalty of crews, the maintenance of supply lines, and the tactical skill to coordinate multiple vessels in battle. Blood's fleet of five ships, crewed by former rebels-convict and supplemented by recruits from captured prizes, represented a significant private naval force in the region (018-168).

## In the Novel

### From One Ship to Five

Blood's first [[authority|command]] is the Spanish vessel captured off [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]], which he renames the *[[arabella-bishop|Arabella]]*. From this single ship, he builds his fleet through a series of daring captures. By the time of the [[maracaybo|Maracaybo]] campaign, Blood has "increased his fleet to twice its strength of ships" (016-055). The fleet grows from three to two — a temporary setback when "and so reduced our fleet from three ships to two, we shall be at a disadvantage" (016-157) — reflecting the constant attrition and replenishment that characterizes Caribbean naval warfare.

### The Maracaybo Gambit

At [[maracaybo|Maracaybo]], Blood's fleet faces the Spanish fleet guarding the narrow channel exit (016-162). The tactical challenge is severe: [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]]'s fleet blocks the bottleneck, and Blood must devise a way to break through. The engagement demonstrates Blood's tactical genius — he converts captured vessels into fireships, forcing the Spanish fleet to break formation and allowing his remaining ships to escape.

### Fleet Actions and Victories

Blood's fleet engages in several significant actions. It captures the vessels of the "great fleet" with [[treasure|plate]] on board (017-127), defeats [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]]'s fleet returning from a mission (018-119), and by the end sails away "with a fleet of five fine ships" (018-169). Each victory augments the fleet's strength, as Blood incorporates the best of the captured vessels and recruits crews from among prisoners willing to join the [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] (018-168).

### Political Significance

News of Blood's fleet reaches Europe, where it is discussed alongside "the news of the fleet that followed upon the news of the Spanish [[treasure|treasure fleet]]'s capture" (018-176). A squadron of this size, operating independently in the Caribbean, becomes a diplomatic concern — the English [[crown|Crown]] seeks to bring Blood into its service, the French court offers him a [[letters-of-marque|commission]], and the Spanish regard him as the most dangerous pirate in the New World.
