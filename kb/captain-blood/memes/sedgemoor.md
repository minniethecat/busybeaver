## Overview

Sedgemoor, a tract of marshy ground in Somerset, is where the Duke of Monmouth's rebellion met its bloody end on the night of 6 July 1685. For Peter Blood, the battle of Sedgemoor is the pivot upon which his entire life turns: though he takes no part in the fighting, his presence in the aftermath brands him a rebel and sets him on the path that leads from the courtroom of Judge Jeffreys to the Caribbean Sea.

## In the Novel

### The Eve of Battle

With Monmouth's rebel army encamped on Sedgemoor, Peter Blood — a physician who has been drawn into the rebel camp to treat a wounded friend — assesses the military situation with professional detachment. The Royalist army under Lord Feversham lies nearby, and Blood assumes the King's commanders are well-informed of the rebel position:

> ...now encamped on Sedgemoor. Mr. Blood assumed that Lord Feversham would be equally well-informed, and if in this assumption he was wrong, at least he was justified of it.
> ([001-118])

Blood does not participate in the battle. A physician, not a soldier, he considers the impending engagement no concern of his. Yet his presence at Sedgemoor will prove fatal to his fortunes.

### The Rout

The battle is a catastrophe for the rebel cause. Monmouth's ill-equipped peasant army, attempting a night attack, is discovered and cut to pieces by Feversham's regulars:

> ...months later, when the battle of Sedgemoor was fought.
> ([002-022])

For Peter Blood, watching from the periphery, the military disaster is a spectacle of incompetence and tragedy — one in which he bears no personal responsibility but for which he will be made to pay with his freedom.

### The Aftermath

In the week following the battle, Sedgemoor and the surrounding countryside become a hunting ground. Royal dragoons scour the lanes and hedgerows for fugitive rebels:

> ...poor fugitives from that swift rout on Sedgemoor came flocking in...
> ([002-120])

Blood, who took no part in the fighting, is nonetheless swept up in the dragnet. His capture near Bridgewater leads to his transportation before Judge Jeffreys at the Bloody Assizes:

> Even so, in that first week after Sedgemoor, Kirke and Feversham...
> ([003-210])

### The Human Toll

The aftermath of Sedgemoor is marked by the brutal reprisals of Colonel Kirke and the summary justice of the Bloody Assizes. Men who fought, men who merely followed Monmouth, and men like Blood who were simply in the wrong place at the wrong time — all are swept into a punitive machinery that makes no fine distinctions:

> ...who had lost an eye at Sedgemoor, drew his regard, and the haggling was...
> ([005-033])

Sedgemoor thus functions in the novel not merely as a geographical location but as the catalyst that transforms a respectable Somerset physician into a Caribbean buccaneer. The battlefield is the novel's point of departure — the moment when the ordered world of Restoration England gives way to the lawless freedom of the Spanish Main.
