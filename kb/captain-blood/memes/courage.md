# Courage

## Overview

Courage is the primary virtue of the adventure hero, and [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] possesses it in abundance. From his defiant laughter in the face of a [[execution|death sentence]] to his tactical brilliance against numerically superior Spanish fleets, Blood's courage defines his character. The novel distinguishes between physical courage (facing battle), moral courage (speaking truth to [[authority|power]]), and social courage (maintaining dignity in degradation).

## Historical Background

The 17th-century ideal of courage was closely tied to aristocratic [[honour|honor]] and military valor. A gentleman was expected to display courage in battle and composure under fire. The Caribbean [[buccaneer|buccaneers]], drawn from the lower ranks of society, developed their own code of courage based on survival against overwhelming odds — outnumbered, outgunned, and operating far from home ports.

## In the Novel

### Courtroom Defiance

Blood's first display of courage is not on the battlefield but in the courtroom. Facing [[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]] and a [[execution|death sentence]], "he laughed, and his laugh jarred uncannily upon the deathly stillness of the court" ((004-007)). This laughter in the face of death establishes Blood's fundamental character: he will not be intimidated.

### Naval Combat

Throughout the novel, Blood demonstrates tactical courage. He engages Spanish [[galleon|warship]]s with inferior forces, trusting in [[navigation|seamanship]] and strategy to overcome numerical disadvantage. His crew follows him because he leads from the front — "the steady muscles of the Irishman, tempered of late by the vicissitudes of slavery" ((011-309)) make him formidable in hand-to-hand combat.

### Moral Courage

Blood's courage is not merely physical. He speaks truth to [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]], confronting the hypocrisy of English justice even when doing so risks his fragile position. His insistence that "justice is justice in the New World as in the Old" ((026-199)) requires moral courage: the willingness to hold principles even when they are inconvenient.
