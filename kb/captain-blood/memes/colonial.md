# Colonial

## Overview

The colonial system of the 17th-century Caribbean provides the institutional framework of *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. The novel's world is structured by colonial hierarchies: governors appointed by [[crown|the Crown]], planters who own the land and the labor, indentured servants and slaves who work it, and the [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] who operate in the margins between colonial [[authority|jurisdiction]]s. Blood's trajectory takes him through every level of this hierarchy — from slave to buccaneer to governor — exposing the colonial system's [[justice|injustice]]s while ultimately finding a place within it.

## Historical Background

The English Caribbean colonies in the 1680s — [[barbados|Barbados]], [[jamaica|Jamaica]], and the Leeward Islands — were organized around sugar production using enslaved African and indentured European labor. The "colonial planter" was the dominant social type: a landowner whose wealth depended on the exploitation of unfree labor and who exercised considerable political influence over colonial [[authority|governance]]. The colonial system was characterized by rigid racial and class hierarchies, with a small white planter elite governing a large population of enslaved Africans and a middle stratum of indentured servants, merchants, and skilled tradesmen.

## In the Novel

The phrase "colonial planter" appears with contempt in the novel, as characters dismiss those who have made their fortunes in the colonies. The "damned colonial planter" (020-248) is a figure of derision in the eyes of English aristocrats, yet these planters wield real [[authority|power]] in the Caribbean. [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]] is the "unsophisticated niece of a colonial planter" (024-167), a description that reveals the social prejudice against colonials even as it acknowledges their centrality to the Caribbean economy. Blood's ultimate triumph is to master the colonial system — not to overthrow it but to rise to its highest office.
