# Prisoner

The condition of **prisoner** — captive, hostage, or prisoner of war — recurs throughout *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* as both a plot mechanism and a moral test. How characters behave when they hold prisoners, and how they behave when they are prisoners themselves, reveals their essential nature more reliably than any other situation in the novel.

## In the Novel

### Blood as Prisoner

The novel begins with [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] already a prisoner — captured after the [[sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]] and awaiting trial. In the courtroom scenes, Blood is "the prisoner Blood," addressed by the judge and questioned by witnesses while chained. His composure under these conditions — his refusal to plead, his ironic courtesy, his physician's detachment — establishes the character that will define him throughout the novel.

The prisoners "filed in under guard" and "filed out" again, reduced to cattle in the eyes of the law. Lord [[lord-gildoy|Gildoy]], one of the "prisoners now fast pinioned," receives Blood's medical attention even as both face death sentences. When the prisoners are transported to [[barbados|Barbados]], they become property — "a thousand prisoners were to be distributed" among colonial planters, a transaction that strips them of the last vestige of human dignity.

Blood's reflection "As a prisoner of war" captures his own self-conception: he was a combatant, not a criminal, and his imprisonment is a political act rather than a just punishment. This distinction sustains him through the years of slavery and, later, shapes his own conduct as a captor.

### Blood as Captor

Blood's treatment of his prisoners defines his character more clearly than any of his military triumphs. When he captures [[don-diego-de-espinosa|Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez]], he addresses him with the precise formula: "Don Diego, are my prisoner." But Blood does not chain, torture, or humiliate his Spanish captive. Instead, he treats him as a guest — a gentleman prisoner whose parole is accepted on honour.

This is not mere kindness; it is strategic. Blood understands that the Spanish code of honour binds Don Diego more tightly than any chain could. When Don Diego violates his parole by steering the ship toward Spanish waters, Blood's response is not violence but exposure — and the moral victory is more complete than any physical triumph could be.

Blood extends similar treatment to the Spanish prisoners captured with Don Diego. When his crew clamours for their blood, Blood interposes himself: "Here!" he shouts, protecting the prisoners from the mob. He orders them brought aboard and treated with dignity — a policy that distinguishes him from every other buccaneer captain in the Caribbean.

### Prisoners as Currency

In the novel's economy, prisoners function as currency. The Spanish prisoners are potential ransom, to be exchanged for money, favour, or strategic advantage. When [[levasseur|Levasseur]] captures two prisoners that Blood considers "a personal matter," the dispute over who owns the captives nearly leads to violence. Blood asserts his claim: "They are prisoners of my own" — and the tension between the two buccaneer captains crystallizes around the question of who has the right to hold whom.

The commodification of prisoners reaches its peak in the scene where a buccaneer suggests selling prisoners: "Then let some one buy the prisoners who has." The casual reduction of human beings to items of trade mirrors the earlier sale of the rebel-convicts in [[barbados|Barbados]] — the novel's quiet commentary on how the same systems of captivity and exchange operate under different names, whether in colonial markets or on pirate decks.

### Prisoner of War vs. Criminal

The novel consistently blurs the line between "prisoner of war" and "criminal." When Blood's men are captured by the Spanish, they are "prisoners of Spain" — but the Spanish would treat them as pirates rather than combatants, meaning execution rather than exchange. Blood's objective throughout is to secure his crew the protections of the "prisoner of war" status — which requires a commission and a flag, the very things he is pursuing.

## Moral Significance

The prisoner theme illuminates the novel's deepest moral concern: whether violence can be conducted with honour. Blood's answer is that it depends entirely on how one treats prisoners. A man who tortures captives is a pirate; a man who accepts parole and treats prisoners with dignity, whatever else he may be, is something else. This distinction — not the commission, not the flag, not the approval of kings — is what ultimately separates Blood from the [[levasseur|Levasseurs]] of the world.
