# M. de Cussy

**M. de Cussy** is the French governor of [[hispaniola|Hispaniola]] who acts as an intermediary between [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] and the French crown. A diplomat by temperament, de Cussy offers Blood a commission in French service but finds himself caught between the expectations of his superiors and the independent spirit of the buccaneer captain he seeks to recruit.

## Diplomatic Mission to Blood

### The French Offer

De Cussy enters the narrative as a mediator. When [[d-ogeron|M. d'Ogeron]], the governor of [[tortuga|Tortuga]], wishes to bring Blood into French service, de Cussy arrives bearing the formal offer [025-295]. He presents himself with polished courtesy, bowing and accepting Blood's greeting with diplomatic grace [025-299, 025-301].

> "I bring you M. de Cussy." [025-295]

His manner is that of a practiced courtier. He takes snuff delicately and exchanges glances with d'Ogeron that suggest a shared understanding of the delicacy of their mission [025-307, 025-310]. De Cussy personally lays out the terms of the French proposal before Blood [025-347], offering the service of France in a manner calculated to flatter and persuade [025-350].

### Negotiation with Blood

The negotiation reveals both de Cussy's diplomatic skill and its limits. When Blood and his captains raise objections — chief among them the buccaneers' reluctance to fight their own countrymen — de Cussy is distressed [025-356]. He has his instructions, and Blood's counter-demands push beyond what he is authorized to concede [025-353].

> M. de Cussy was distressed. He had his instructions. [025-356]

When the discussion reaches an impasse, de Cussy accepts the situation with dignified resignation [025-358]. He recognizes that further argument will not move Blood from his position.

### Confrontation with de Rivarol

De Cussy's position becomes acutely uncomfortable when [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]], the French general, arrives to take command of the expedition. Rivarol immediately undercuts de Cussy's authority, asserting that de Cussy has exceeded his instructions in the terms offered to Blood [026-061].

> M. de Cussy has exceeded his instructions. [026-061]

What follows is, for de Cussy, an extremely bad quarter of an hour [026-136, 026-137]. Rivarol treats him like a lackey, and de Cussy is forced to defend himself by arguing that he had been promised a free hand in negotiations [026-141, 026-146]. The scene exposes the precarious position of colonial governors caught between metropolitan authority and local realities.

De Cussy's relationship with Blood is further complicated when [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] arrives. Blood observes pointedly that de Cussy knows perfectly well that Blood and his men would fight against France under Spanish colours, a fact that de Cussy had conveniently ignored in his eagerness to secure their service [026-121].

## Character Assessment

De Cussy represents the French colonial administration at its most polished and, simultaneously, its most constrained. He is a competent diplomat who understands the value of courtesy and the art of persuasion. Yet he operates within narrow limits set by distant authorities who neither understand nor respect the complexities of Caribbean politics.

His treatment at de Rivarol's hands illustrates the contempt that military authority often held for civil administration in the colonial context. De Cussy's humiliation — a governor reduced to the status of a lackey — foreshadows the broader failure of French diplomacy with the buccaneers.

The dinner at de Cussy's table, interrupted by urgent news of Blood's situation, provides a final snapshot of the governor: a host whose hospitality cannot mask the political tensions that surround him [026-160, 026-177].
