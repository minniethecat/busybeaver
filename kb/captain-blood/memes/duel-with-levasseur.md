# Duel with Levasseur

## Overview

The Duel with Levasseur occurred on the shore of Tortuga when Captain Blood confronted the French buccaneer captain Levasseur over his violation of the Brethren of the Coast's articles ([014-013]). Levasseur had attempted to claim a female captive — Mademoiselle d'Ogeron, the niece of the Governor of Tortuga — as his personal prize, in direct contravention of buccaneer law ([014-030], [014-047]).

## In the Novel

### The Grievance

Levasseur, a French captain who had sailed with the Brethren since the age of twenty, had brought his ship back to Tortuga with a prize that included women captives ([014-039]). Among them was Mademoiselle d'Ogeron. When confronted by the Governor's representative, Levasseur refused to surrender the captive and departed in a rage after being shown the door ([014-032]).

### The Confrontation

Blood, learning of Levasseur's defiance, pursued him to the shore. There, on the sands of Tortuga, he called Levasseur to account for his breach of the articles. Levasseur, "repulsive though he was" in both appearance and manner, accepted the challenge ([014-043]).

### The Duel

The two captains fought with swords on the beach. Blood, the superior swordsman despite his medical background, ran Levasseur through. The French buccaneer fell dead on the sand, and the captive was released.

### Aftermath

The duel established Blood's authority among the Brethren of the Coast, demonstrating that he would enforce the articles without favour or fear. It also marked the beginning of Blood's ascendancy within the buccaneer community, as Levasseur's crew accepted Blood as their new captain.

## Key Participants

- **Captain Blood** — Victor of the duel, enforcer of the articles
- **Levasseur** — French buccaneer captain, killed in the duel
- **Mademoiselle d'Ogeron** — The captive whose detention triggered the confrontation

## Significance

The duel illustrates Blood's commitment to the rule of law even within the lawless world of buccaneering. His enforcement of the articles — risking his own life to protect a captive — distinguishes him from the true pirates among whom he sails.
