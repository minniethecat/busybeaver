## Overview

Bridgetown, the capital of Barbados, serves as the gateway through which Peter Blood enters the Caribbean world. It is here that the rebel-convicts are landed and sold, here that Blood establishes himself as a physician, and here that a Spanish raid — ironically — provides his path to freedom. The town functions as the novel's transition point between the Old World of English justice and the New World of Caribbean adventure.

## In the Novel

### Arrival of the Convicts

The Jamaica Merchant brings the condemned Monmouth rebels to Bridgetown, where they are to be sold as indentured labourers. Among the passengers is Arabella Bishop, niece of Colonel William Bishop, the planter who will purchase Peter Blood:

> The Jamaica Merchant at Bridgetown, Miss Arabella Bishop rode out from her uncle's plantation to observe the sale of the rebels.
> ([005-090])

Colonel Bishop quickly perceives the value of his purchase, recognizing Blood's abilities as a physician:

> ...Colonel Bishop had found that there was more profit to be derived from hiring out his slave as a doctor than from setting him to work in the sugar fields.
> ([005-153])

### Blood the Physician

Blood's medical skill earns him a reputation throughout Bridgetown, and he becomes known as one of the town's physicians. His practice flourishes despite his status:

> ...the medical skill of Bridgetown was represented by Dr. Whacker and Dr. Bronson, to whom was now added Dr. Blood.
> ([005-365])

His success breeds resentment among the established doctors. When Dr. Whacker confronts him, the retort is sharp:

> "You are not the only doctor in Bridgetown."
> ([006-178])

### The Spanish Raid

The defining event at Bridgetown occurs when a Spanish warship arrives and launches a surprise attack. Colonel Bishop's incompetent defence leaves the town vulnerable:

> All through the scorching afternoon the battle went on, the rattle and crack of musketry penetrating ever deeper into the town to show that the defenders were being driven steadily back. By sunset two hundred and fifty Spaniards were masters of Bridgetown, the islanders were disarmed...
> ([008-165])

The Spanish occupation of Bridgetown is brief but consequential. During the chaos, Peter Blood and his fellow convicts seize their opportunity to escape, capturing the Spanish ship and embarking upon their career of buccaneering ([008-168]).

### Aftermath

The raid leaves a lasting impression on the inhabitants of Bridgetown. The ease with which the Spaniards took the town exposes the weakness of colonial defences and the incompetence of its military leadership:

> ...the things that had happened in Bridgetown were not enough to teach them...
> ([011-139])

Years later, the memory of the Spanish raid at Bridgetown remains vivid for those who witnessed it, including Arabella Bishop, who recalls the events when she encounters Captain Blood again under vastly different circumstances ([012-061]).
