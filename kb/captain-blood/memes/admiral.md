# Admiral

## Overview

The rank of admiral appears in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]* as the ultimate naval title, conferred on those who [[authority|command]] fleets rather than individual ships. Blood, as the [[authority|command]]er of a growing buccaneer [[fleet|squadron]], is acknowledged as "the admiral" by his followers and adversaries alike. The title carries both military and political weight — an admiral speaks for his fleet in negotiations, makes strategic decisions that affect hundreds of men, and bears responsibility for victories and defeats. The novel's references to admirals — from the "great Dutch admiral" De Ruyter (001-200) to Blood's own assumption of the role — trace the arc of naval [[authority|command]] from formal [[crown|Crown]] appointments to the informal but effective authority of the buccaneer leader.

## Historical Background

The rank of admiral originated in the medieval Mediterranean and was fully institutionalized in the [[royal-navy|Royal Navy]] by the 17th century. Admirals [[authority|command]]ed [[fleet|squadron]]s and fleets, flew their flags from designated flagships, and were responsible for the strategic direction of naval campaigns. In the Caribbean, admirals of various European navies — English, Spanish, French, and Dutch — competed for control of sea lanes and colonial ports. The great Dutch admiral Michiel de Ruyter, who "lost his life" (001-200) in battle against the French in 1676, was the exemplar of the age: a [[authority|command]]er whose tactical genius and personal courage set the standard by which all subsequent admirals were measured. Outside the formal naval hierarchy, buccaneer and [[privateering|privateer]] leaders were often styled "admiral" by their followers, reflecting the functional equivalence between [[crown|Crown]]-appointed and self-made naval commanders in the contested waters of the Spanish Main.

## In the Novel

### Blood as Buccaneer Admiral

Blood's assumption of the admiral's role is a natural consequence of his fleet's growth. He acts as "his admiral" (014-081), making strategic decisions that affect the entire [[fleet|squadron]]. When [[levasseur|Levasseur]] "entertained his admiral to dinner" (014-085), the occasion is both diplomatic and tactical — two buccaneer leaders negotiating their relationship through the rituals of naval courtesy. Blood summons councils of war, receives enemy envoys, and negotiates with [[governor|colonial governor]]s — all functions of a flag officer.

### Adversaries and Equals

The Spanish "admiral" [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]] serves as Blood's principal naval adversary. When the Spanish flagship is destroyed, the text notes the "admiral in flames" (017-025), a dramatic image that marks the destruction not just of a ship but of Spanish naval authority in the engagement. The English regard Blood's status with ambivalence — "[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] and the rest are not admirals of England!" (019-016) — reflecting the tension between Blood's functional [[authority|command]] and [[crown|the Crown]]'s refusal to acknowledge it formally.

### Self-Recognition

By the novel's end, Blood can speak of "the admiral" and mean himself (023-241), a linguistic shift that marks his transition from fugitive to [[authority|command]]er. The "deliverance from the Spanish admiral" (023-381) that he engineers is the act of a flag officer protecting his fleet, not of a pirate seeking [[treasure|plunder]]. Blood's self-identification as admiral represents the internalization of the role — he has become what he once merely performed.
