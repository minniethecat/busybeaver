# Execution

## Overview

Execution — and the threat of it — opens the novel. [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] and his companions are sentenced to death for treason after the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]]. The sentence is commuted not to mercy but to transportation: a [[pardon|reprieve]] from immediate death in exchange for the slow death of [[plantation-system|plantation]] slavery. The novel repeatedly returns to the imagery and threat of execution, contrasting the "[[mercy|clemency]]" of [[crown|the Crown]] with the violence it inflicts on the innocent.

## Historical Background

The [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]] of 1685 resulted in approximately 320 executions. Methods included hanging, drawing, and quartering for treason — the most gruesome death the English legal system could devise. The public nature of executions (at markets, crossroads, and town squares) was intended to deter [[treason|rebellion]]. In practice, the scale and brutality of [[bloody-assizes|the Assizes]] generated revulsion that contributed to James II's unpopularity and eventual overthrow.

## In the Novel

### The Death Sentence

Blood's trial culminates in "sentence of death" for "[[treason|high treason]]" ((004-006)). The three prisoners — Blood, [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]], and Baynes — face "being hanged, drawn, and quartered as their sentences directed" ((004-096)). The commutation to transportation is framed as "[[mercy|clemency]] full worthy of him" — the King showing mercy ((004-092)). But the novel undercuts this: transportation is "to convert them into living deaths" ((004-092)).

### Execution Avoided

Blood's escape from execution creates the central tension of his character: a man who was supposed to die, now living on borrowed time. Every subsequent risk — piracy, naval combat, political intrigue — is measured against the death he already escaped. "I had dressed the wounds of a man who was convicted of treason" ((020-328)) — the original sin that led to the death sentence — becomes the moral foundation of his defiance.
