# Commission

A **commission** — also called a [[letters-of-marque|letter of marque]] — is a royal warrant issued by a sovereign authorizing a private vessel to capture enemy ships and claim prize money. In *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, the commission functions as the novel's central legal and moral mechanism: it is the piece of paper that separates the pirate from the [[privateer|privateer]], the outlaw from the officer, and ultimately the slave from the Governor.

## In the Novel

### The Commission as Legal Shield

The commission first appears as an absence: when the Spanish Ambassador protests [[peter-blood|Blood]]'s depredations, the English [[crown|Crown]] responds that Blood "held no commission from the King of England; that he was, in fact, a proscribed rebel, an escaped slave." Without a commission, Blood operates in a legal void — every navy in the Caribbean may attack him with impunity, and no court will recognize his claims.

Blood's crew includes men with legitimate naval experience, such as Nathaniel [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]], "whose sometime commission in the King's Navy gave him the best title to this office" of first mate. These men understand the value of the commission — and the precariousness of operating without one.

### Lord Julian Wade's Mission

The commission becomes the central plot device of the novel's middle act when [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] arrives in the Caribbean bearing "some commissions made out in blank, and full authority from the Secretary of State to fill in the names." King James II, facing war with France, hopes to co-opt the most dangerous buccaneer in the Caribbean into his service.

[[lord-julian-wade|Wade]] carries the commission "in his pocket" — a physical object whose possession confers authority. The irony is sharp: Blood, who has been hunted as a pirate, needs only accept this piece of paper to become an agent of the very [[crown|Crown]] that condemned him.

### Blood's Acceptance

Blood's decision to accept the commission is framed as a survival strategy rather than a moral conversion. Facing a superior Spanish force and likely destruction, he declares: "I am ready to take the way of Morgan: to accept the King's commission and shelter us all behind it."

When Blood accepts, the commission's authority is immediately tested. [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] attempts to arrest him, but is blocked: "Blood holds a commission in the King's service under the seal of my Lord Sunderland, His Majesty's Secretary of State." The question [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]]'s companion asks — "Will Bishop respect the commission when you hold it?" — is answered decisively: "It will go very hard with him if he attempts to flout the King's authority."

### The Commission's Moral Ambiguity

The commission does not settle moral questions — it highlights them. [[colonel-bishop|Bishop]] argues that "the mistake was... to grant Captain Blood a commission, the mistake is not so easily rectified now that Captain Blood holds it." The commission protects Blood from legal prosecution but cannot wash away the moral stain of his buccaneering past.

Blood himself is ambivalent. He tells [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]] that he accepted the commission "to redeem me in the eyes of Miss Bishop" — not out of loyalty to the Crown but out of love for [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]]. The commission is a means to rehabilitation, not an end in itself.

### The Commission as Plot Engine

The commission drives the novel's political dynamics. When Blood accepts it, his own crew grumbles — "this poxy commission" — fearing they will be turned into regular navy men. [[colonel-bishop|Bishop]] protests that granting such a commission to a former rebel is "unthinkable." And [[lord-julian-wade|Wade]] must defend his decision before the colonial authorities, arguing that Blood's military value outweighs his criminal past.

The novel's resolution reaffirms the commission's transformative power: "Thus it was settled. Blood's commission was made out and sealed in the presence of Mallard, the Commandant, and the other officers of the garrison." The man who began as a condemned traitor ends as the Crown's supreme representative in Jamaica — all legitimized by a signed and sealed piece of parchment.

## Historical Context

The letter of marque system was a cornerstone of 17th-century naval warfare. Nations unable to afford standing navies relied on private shipowners to harass enemy commerce, compensating them through prize courts that awarded captured ships and cargo. The system was codified in admiralty law but rife with abuse — many privateers continued their depredations after peace treaties were signed, becoming pirates by default.

The novel's setting in the 1680s captures a period of legal flux: the transition from Anglo-Spanish hostility to the War of the Grand Alliance, during which commissions changed hands rapidly and the line between [[privateer|privateer]] and pirate blurred almost to invisibility.
