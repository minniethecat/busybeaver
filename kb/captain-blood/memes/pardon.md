# Pardon

## Overview

The royal pardon is the most sought-after document in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. For the rebels-convict turned [[buccaneer|buccaneers]], a pardon from [[crown|the Crown]] represents the only path back to lawfulness — the absolution that would transform them from outlaws into subjects, from pirates into patriots. The novel treats the pardon as a political instrument rather than an act of mercy: pardons are "for forty thousand pounds" (003-253), bought and sold like any other commodity, their value determined by the wealth and influence of the petitioner. Blood's pursuit of a pardon — and his ultimate attainment of one through service to [[crown|the Crown]] — traces the novel's central arc from condemnation to redemption.

## Historical Background

The royal prerogative of mercy allowed the English monarch to pardon convicted criminals, either absolutely or conditionally. In the aftermath of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]] (1685), pardons were issued to some rebels who could pay for them, while others were transported or executed. The sale of pardons was a significant source of royal revenue, and the price reflected both the severity of the crime and the wealth of the petitioner. After the Glorious Revolution (1688), William III issued pardons to many former rebels and political prisoners as part of a broader policy of reconciliation. The conditional pardon — requiring military or naval service in exchange for [[mercy|clemency]] — was a common means of recruiting soldiers and sailors for colonial expeditions.

## In the Novel

### The Price of Mercy

The pardon is introduced as a financial transaction. King James, it is said, "intended to pardon" rebels if they could pay — but "for forty thousand pounds" (003-247–003-253). The sum is astronomical, far beyond the means of men like [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]. The pardon's commodification corrupts its moral purpose: mercy becomes a luxury good, available only to the wealthy, while the poor are hanged or transported. Blood's inability to purchase a pardon is the direct cause of his transportation to [[barbados|Barbados]] and his subsequent career as a buccaneer.

### The Pursuit

Blood's pursuit of a pardon drives much of the novel's middle section. He seeks "his pardon for all the evil brought upon" him by the [[justice|injustice]] of his conviction (015-355). The pardon is not merely a legal document but a moral acknowledgment — an admission by [[crown|the Crown]] that its treatment of him was wrongful. [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]]'s mission to the Caribbean includes the possibility of offering Blood a pardon in exchange for his service, transforming the buccaneer from a fugitive seeking mercy into a valued asset whom [[crown|the Crown]] must court.

### Social vs. Legal Pardon

The novel distinguishes between the legal pardon — the document issued by [[crown|the Crown]] — and the social pardon of acceptance by the community. Blood ultimately receives both: the legal pardon through his service to [[crown|the Crown]] and the social pardon through his marriage to [[arabella-bishop|Arabella Bishop]] and his appointment as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. The double pardon closes the novel's principal arc, restoring Blood to the station of gentleman that was stripped from him at the [[battle-of-sedgemoor|Battle of Sedgemoor]].
