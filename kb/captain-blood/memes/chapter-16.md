[016-037] CHAPTER XVI. THE TRAP

[016-038] That affair of Mademoiselle d'Ogeron bore as its natural fruit an

[016-039] improvement in the already cordial relations between Captain Blood

[016-040] and the Governor of [[tortuga|Tortuga]]. At the fine stone house, with its

[016-041] green-jalousied windows, which M. d'Ogeron had built himself in a

[016-042] spacious and luxuriant garden to the east of Cayona, the Captain became

[016-043] a very welcome guest. M. d'Ogeron was in the Captain's debt for more

[016-044] than the twenty thousand [[ransom|pieces of eight]] which he had provided for

[016-045] mademoiselle's ransom; and shrewd, hard bargain-driver though he might

[016-046] be, the Frenchman could be generous and understood the sentiment of

[016-047] gratitude. This he now proved in every possible way, and under his

[016-048] powerful protection the credit of Captain Blood among the buccaneers

[016-049] very rapidly reached its zenith.

[016-050] So when it came to fitting out his fleet for that enterprise against

[016-051] Maracaybo, which had originally been Levasseur's project, he did not

[016-052] want for either ships or men to follow him. He recruited five hundred

[016-053] adventurers in all, and he might have had as many thousands if he could

[016-054] have offered them accommodation. Similarly without difficulty he might

[016-055] have increased his fleet to twice its strength of ships but that he

[016-056] preferred to keep it what it was. The three vessels to which he confined

[016-057] it were [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]], the La Foudre, which Cahusac now commanded with a

[016-058] contingent of some sixscore Frenchmen, and the Santiago, which had been

[016-059] refitted and rechristened the Elizabeth, after that Queen of England

[016-060] whose seamen had humbled Spain as Captain Blood now hoped to humble it

[016-061] again. [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]], in virtue of his service in the navy, was appointed by

[016-062] Blood to [[authority|command]] her, and the appointment was confirmed by the men.

[016-063] It was some months after the rescue of Mademoiselle d'Ogeron--in August

[016-064] of that year 1687--that this little fleet, after some minor adventures

[016-065] which I pass over in silence, sailed into the great lake of Maracaybo

[016-066] and effected its raid upon that opulent city of the Main.

[016-067] The affair did not proceed exactly as was hoped, and Blood's force came

[016-068] to find itself in a precarious position. This is best explained in the

[016-069] words employed by Cahusac--which [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]] has carefully recorded--in the

[016-070] course of an altercation that broke out on the steps of the Church

[016-071] of Nuestra Senora del Carmen, which Captain Blood had impiously

[016-072] appropriated for the purpose of a corps-de-garde. I have said already

[016-073] that he was a papist only when it suited him.

[016-074] The dispute was being conducted by Hagthorpe, [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]], and Pitt on

[016-075] the one side, and Cahusac, out of whose uneasiness it all arose, on the

[016-076] other. Behind them in the sun-scorched, dusty square, sparsely fringed

[016-077] by palms, whose fronds drooped listlessly in the quivering heat, surged

[016-078] a couple of hundred wild fellows belonging to both parties, their own

[016-079] excitement momentarily quelled so that they might listen to what passed

[016-080] among their leaders.

[016-081] Cahusac appeared to be having it all his own way, and he raised

[016-082] his harsh, querulous voice so that all might hear his truculent

[016-083] denunciation. He spoke, Pitt tells us, a dreadful kind of English, which

[016-084] the shipmaster, however, makes little attempt to reproduce. His dress

[016-085] was as discordant as his speech. It was of a kind to advertise his

[016-086] trade, and ludicrously in contrast with the sober garb of Hagthorpe

[016-087] and the almost foppish daintiness of Jeremy Pitt. His soiled and

[016-088] blood-stained shirt of blue cotton was open in front, to cool his hairy

[016-089] breast, and the girdle about the waist of his leather breeches carried

[016-090] an arsenal of pistols and a knife, whilst a cutlass hung from a leather

[016-091] baldrick loosely slung about his body; above his countenance, broad and

[016-092] flat as a Mongolian's, a red scarf was swathed, turban-wise, about his

[016-093] head.

[016-094] “Is it that I have not warned you from the beginning that all was too

[016-095] easy?” he demanded between plaintiveness and fury. “I am no fool, my

[016-096] friends. I have eyes, me. And I see. I see an abandoned fort at the

[016-097] entrance of the lake, and nobody there to fire a gun at us when we came

[016-098] in. Then I suspect the trap. Who would not that had eyes and brain? Bah!

[016-099] we come on. What do we find? A city, abandoned like the fort; a city out

[016-100] of which the people have taken all things of value. Again I warn Captain

[016-101] Blood. It is a trap, I say. We are to come on; always to come on,

[016-102] without opposition, until we find that it is too late to go to sea

[016-103] again, that we cannot go back at all. But no one will listen to me. You

[016-104] all know so much more. Name of God! Captain Blood, he will go on, and we

[016-105] go on. We go to Gibraltar. True that at last, after long time, we catch

[016-106] the Deputy-Governor; true, we make him pay big ransom for Gibraltar;

[016-107] true between that ransom and the loot we return here with some two

[016-108] thousand pieces of eight. But what is it, in reality, will you tell me?

[016-109] Or shall I tell you? It is a piece of cheese--a piece of cheese in a

[016-110] mousetrap, and we are the little mice. Goddam! And the cats--oh, the

[016-111] cats they wait for us! The cats are those four Spanish ships of war that

[016-112] have come meantime. And they wait for us outside the bottle-neck of this

[016-113] lagoon. Mort de Dieu! That is what comes of the damned obstinacy of your

[016-114] fine Captain Blood.”

[016-115] Wolverstone laughed. Cahusac exploded in fury.

[016-116] “Ah, sangdieu! Tu ris, animal? You laugh! Tell me this: How do we get

[016-117] out again unless we accept the terms of Monsieur the Admiral of Spain?”

[016-118] From the buccaneers at the foot of the steps came an angry rumble of

[016-119] approval. The single eye of the gigantic Wolverstone rolled terribly,

[016-120] and he clenched his great fists as if to strike the Frenchman, who was

[016-121] exposing them to [[treason|mutiny]]. But Cahusac was not daunted. The mood of the

[016-122] men enheartened him.

[016-123] “You think, perhaps, this your Captain Blood is the good God. That he

[016-124] can make miracles, eh? He is ridiculous, you know, this Captain Blood;

[016-125] with his grand air and his....”

[016-126] He checked. Out of the church at that moment, grand air and all,

[016-127] sauntered Peter Blood. With him came a tough, long-legged French

[016-128] sea-wolf named Yberville, who, though still young, had already won fame

[016-129] as a privateer commander before the loss of his own ship had driven him

[016-130] to take service under Blood. The Captain advanced towards that disputing

[016-131] group, leaning lightly upon his long ebony cane, his face shaded by a

[016-132] broad-plumed hat. There was in his appearance nothing of the buccaneer.

[016-133] He had much more the air of a lounger in the Mall or the Alameda--the

[016-134] latter rather, since his elegant suit of violet taffetas with

[016-135] gold-embroidered button-holes was in the Spanish fashion. But the long,

[016-136] stout, serviceable rapier, thrust up behind by the left hand resting

[016-137] lightly on the pummel, corrected the impression. That and those steely

[016-138] eyes of his announced the adventurer.

[016-139] “You find me ridiculous, eh, Cahusac?” said he, as he came to a halt

[016-140] before the Breton, whose anger seemed already to have gone out of him.

[016-141] “What, then, must I find you?” He spoke quietly, almost wearily. “You

[016-142] will be telling them that we have delayed, and that it is the delay that

[016-143] has brought about our danger. But whose is the fault of that delay? We

[016-144] have been a month in doing what should have been done, and what but for

[016-145] your blundering would have been done, inside of a week.”

[016-146] “Ah ca! Nom de Dieu! Was it my fault that....”

[016-147] “Was it any one else's fault that you ran your ship La Foudre aground on

[016-148] the shoal in the middle of the lake? You would not be piloted. You knew

[016-149] your way. You took no soundings even. The result was that we lost three

[016-150] precious days in getting canoes to bring off your men and your gear.

[016-151] Those three days gave the folk at Gibraltar not only time to hear of our

[016-152] coming, but time in which to get away. After that, and because of it,

[016-153] we had to follow the Governor to his infernal island fortress, and a

[016-154] fortnight and best part of a hundred lives were lost in reducing it.

[016-155] That's how we come to have delayed until this Spanish fleet is fetched

[016-156] round from La Guayra by a guarda-costa; and if ye hadn't lost La Foudre,

[016-157] and so reduced our fleet from three ships to two, we should even now be

[016-158] able to fight our way through with a reasonable hope of succeeding.

[016-159] Yet you think it is for you to come hectoring here, upbraiding us for a

[016-160] situation that is just the result of your own ineptitude.”

[016-161] He spoke with a restraint which I trust you will agree was admirable

[016-162] when I tell you that the Spanish fleet guarding the bottle-neck exit

[016-163] of the great Lake of Maracaybo, and awaiting there the coming forth

[016-164] of Captain Blood with a calm confidence based upon its overwhelming

[016-165] strength, was commanded by his implacable enemy, [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]]

[016-166] y Valdez, the Admiral of Spain. In addition to his duty to his country,

[016-167] the Admiral had, as you know, a further personal incentive arising out

[016-168] of that business aboard the Encarnacion a year ago, and the death of

[016-169] his brother [[don-diego|Don Diego]]; and with him sailed his nephew Esteban, whose

[016-170] vindictive zeal exceeded the Admiral's own.

[016-171] Yet, knowing all this, Captain Blood could preserve his calm in

[016-172] reproving the cowardly frenzy of one for whom the situation had not half

[016-173] the peril with which it was fraught for himself. He turned from Cahusac

[016-174] to address the mob of buccaneers, who had surged nearer to hear him, for

[016-175] he had not troubled to raise his voice. “I hope that will correct some

[016-176] of the misapprehension that appears to have been disturbing you,” said

[016-177] he.

[016-178] “There's no good can come of talking of what's past and done,” cried

[016-179] Cahusac, more sullen now than truculent. Whereupon Wolverstone laughed,

[016-180] a laugh that was like the neighing of a horse. “The question is: what

[016-181] are we to do now?”

[016-182] “Sure, now, there's no question at all,” said Captain Blood.

[016-183] “Indeed, but there is,” Cahusac insisted. “Don Miguel, the Spanish

[016-184] Admiral, have offer us safe passage to sea if we will depart at once, do

[016-185] no damage to the town, release our prisoners, and surrender all that we

[016-186] took at Gibraltar.”

[016-187] Captain Blood smiled quietly, knowing precisely how much Don Miguel's

[016-188] word was worth. It was Yberville who replied, in manifest scorn of his

[016-189] compatriot:

[016-190] “Which argues that, even at this disadvantage as he has us, the Spanish

[016-191] Admiral is still afraid of us.”

[016-192] “That can be only because he not know our real weakness,” was the fierce

[016-193] retort. “And, anyway, we must accept these terms. We have no choice.

[016-194] That is my opinion.”

[016-195] “Well, it's not mine, now,” said Captain Blood. “So, I've refused them.”

[016-196] “Refuse'!” Cahusac's broad face grew purple. A muttering from the men

[016-197] behind enheartened him. “You have refuse'? You have refuse' already--and

[016-198] without consulting me?”

[016-199] “Your disagreement could have altered nothing. You'd have been outvoted,

[016-200] for Hagthorpe here was entirely of my own mind. Still,” he went on,

[016-201] “if you and your own French followers wish to avail yourselves of the

[016-202] Spaniard's terms, we shall not hinder you. Send one of your prisoners to

[016-203] announce it to the Admiral. Don Miguel will welcome your decision, you

[016-204] may be sure.”

[016-205] Cahusac glowered at him in silence for a moment. Then, having controlled

[016-206] himself, he asked in a concentrated voice:

[016-207] “Precisely what answer have you make to the Admiral?”

[016-208] A smile irradiated the face and eyes of Captain Blood. “I have answered

[016-209] him that unless within four-and-twenty hours we have his parole to stand

[016-210] out to sea, ceasing to dispute our passage or hinder our departure,

[016-211] and a ransom of fifty thousand pieces of eight for Maracaybo, we shall

[016-212] reduce this beautiful city to ashes, and thereafter go out and destroy

[016-213] his fleet.”

[016-214] The impudence of it left Cahusac speechless. But among the English

[016-215] buccaneers in the square there were many who savoured the audacious

[016-216] humour of the trapped dictating terms to the trappers. Laughter broke

[016-217] from them. It spread into a roar of acclamation; for bluff is a weapon

[016-218] dear to every adventurer. Presently, when they understood it, even

[016-219] Cahusac's French followers were carried off their feet by that wave of

[016-220] jocular enthusiasm, until in his truculent obstinacy Cahusac remained

[016-221] the only dissentient. He withdrew in mortification. Nor was he to be

[016-222] mollified until the following day brought him his revenge. This came

[016-223] in the shape of a messenger from Don Miguel with a letter in which

[016-224] the Spanish Admiral solemnly vowed to God that, since the pirates had

[016-225] refused his magnanimous offer to permit them to surrender with the

[016-226] honours of war, he would now await them at the mouth of the lake there

[016-227] to destroy them on their coming forth. He added that should they delay

[016-228] their departure, he would so soon as he was reenforced by a fifth ship,

[016-229] the Santo Nino, on its way to join him from La Guayra, himself come

[016-230] inside to seek them at Maracaybo.

[016-231] This time Captain Blood was put out of temper.

[016-232] “Trouble me no more,” he snapped at Cahusac, who came growling to him

[016-233] again. “Send word to Don Miguel that you have seceded from me. He'll

[016-234] give you safe conduct, devil a doubt. Then take one of the sloops, order

[016-235] your men aboard and put to sea, and the devil go with you.”

[016-236] Cahusac would certainly have adopted that course if only his men had

[016-237] been unanimous in the matter. They, however, were torn between greed and

[016-238] apprehension. If they went they must abandon their share of the plunder,

[016-239] which was considerable, as well as the slaves and other prisoners

[016-240] they had taken. If they did this, and Captain Blood should afterwards

[016-241] contrive to get away unscathed--and from their knowledge of his

[016-242] resourcefulness, the thing, however unlikely, need not be impossible--he

[016-243] must profit by that which they now relinquished. This was a contingency

[016-244] too bitter for contemplation. And so, in the end, despite all that

[016-245] Cahusac could say, the surrender was not to Don Miguel, but to Peter

[016-246] Blood. They had come into the venture with him, they asserted, and

[016-247] they would go out of it with him or not at all. That was the message

[016-248] he received from them that same evening by the sullen mouth of Cahusac

[016-249] himself.

[016-250] He welcomed it, and invited the Breton to sit down and join the council

[016-251] which was even then deliberating upon the means to be employed. This

[016-252] council occupied the spacious patio of the Governor's house--which

[016-253] Captain Blood had appropriated to his own uses--a cloistered stone

[016-254] quadrangle in the middle of which a fountain played coolly under a

[016-255] trellis of vine. Orange-trees grew on two sides of it, and the still,

[016-256] evening air was heavy with the scent of them. It was one of those

[016-257] pleasant exterior-interiors which Moorish architects had introduced to

[016-258] Spain and the Spaniards had carried with them to the New World.

[016-259] Here that council of war, composed of six men in all, deliberated until

[016-260] late that night upon the plan of action which Captain Blood put forward.

[016-261] The great freshwater lake of Maracaybo, nourished by a score of rivers

[016-262] from the snow-capped ranges that surround it on two sides, is some

[016-263] hundred and twenty miles in length and almost the same distance across

[016-264] at its widest. It is--as has been indicated--in the shape of a great

[016-265] bottle having its neck towards the sea at Maracaybo.

[016-266] Beyond this neck it widens again, and then the two long, narrow strips

[016-267] of land known as the islands of Vigilias and Palomas block the channel,

[016-268] standing lengthwise across it. The only passage out to sea for vessels

[016-269] of any draught lies in the narrow strait between these islands. Palomas,

[016-270] which is some ten miles in length, is unapproachable for half a mile

[016-271] on either side by any but the shallowest craft save at its eastern end,

[016-272] where, completely commanding the narrow passage out to sea, stands the

[016-273] massive fort which the buccaneers had found deserted upon their coming.

[016-274] In the broader water between this passage and the bar, the four Spanish

[016-275] ships were at anchor in mid-channel. The Admiral's Encarnacion, which we

[016-276] already know, was a mighty galleon of forty-eight great guns and eight

[016-277] small. Next in importance was the Salvador with thirty-six guns; the

[016-278] other two, the Infanta and the San Felipe, though smaller vessels, were

[016-279] still formidable enough with their twenty guns and a hundred and fifty

[016-280] men apiece.

[016-281] Such was the fleet of which the gauntlet was to be run by Captain Blood

[016-282] with his own Arabella of forty guns, the Elizabeth of twenty-six, and

[016-283] two sloops captured at Gibraltar, which they had indifferently armed

[016-284] with four culverins each. In men they had a bare four hundred survivors

[016-285] of the five hundred-odd that had left Tortuga, to oppose to fully a

[016-286] thousand Spaniards manning the galleons.

[016-287] The plan of action submitted by Captain Blood to that council was a

[016-288] desperate one, as Cahusac uncompromisingly pronounced it.

[016-289] “Why, so it is,” said the Captain. “But I've done things more

[016-290] desperate.” Complacently he pulled at a pipe that was loaded with that

[016-291] fragrant Sacerdotes tobacco for which Gibraltar was famous, and of

[016-292] which they had brought away some hogsheads. “And what is more, they've

[016-293] succeeded. Audaces fortuna juvat. Bedad, they knew their world, the old

[016-294] Romans.”

[016-295] He breathed into his companions and even into Cahusac some of his own

[016-296] spirit of confidence, and in confidence all went busily to work. For

[016-297] three days from sunrise to sunset, the buccaneers laboured and sweated

[016-298] to complete the preparations for the action that was to procure them

[016-299] their deliverance. Time pressed. They must strike before Don Miguel de

[016-300] Espinosa received the reenforcement of that fifth galleon, the Santo

[016-301] Nino, which was coming to join him from La Guayra.

[016-302] Their principal operations were on the larger of the two sloops captured

[016-303] at Gibraltar; to which vessel was assigned the leading part in Captain

[016-304] Blood's scheme. They began by tearing down all bulkheads, until they

[016-305] had reduced her to the merest shell, and in her sides they broke open

[016-306] so many ports that her gunwale was converted into the semblance of a

[016-307] grating. Next they increased by a half-dozen the scuttles in her deck,

[016-308] whilst into her hull they packed all the tar and pitch and brimstone

[016-309] that they could find in the town, to which they added six barrels of

[016-310] gunpowder, placed on end like guns at the open ports on her larboard

[016-311] side. On the evening of the fourth day, everything being now in

[016-312] readiness, all were got aboard, and the empty, pleasant city of

[016-313] Maracaybo was at last abandoned. But they did not weigh anchor until

[016-314] some two hours after midnight. Then, at last, on the first of the ebb,

[016-315] they drifted silently down towards the bar with all canvas furled save

[016-316] only their spiltsails, which, so as to give them steering way, were

[016-317] spread to the faint breeze that stirred through the purple darkness of

[016-318] the tropical night.

[016-319] The order of their going was as follows: Ahead went the improvised

[016-320] fire-ship in charge of Wolverstone, with a crew of six volunteers, each

[016-321] of whom was to have a hundred pieces of eight over and above his share

[016-322] of plunder as a special reward. Next came the Arabella. She was followed

[016-323] at a distance by the Elizabeth, commanded by Hagthorpe, with whom was

[016-324] the now shipless Cahusac and the bulk of his French followers. The rear

[016-325] was brought up by the second sloop and some eight canoes, aboard of

[016-326] which had been shipped the prisoners, the slaves, and most of the

[016-327] captured merchandise. The prisoners were all pinioned, and guarded by

[016-328] four buccaneers with musketoons who manned these boats in addition to

[016-329] the two fellows who were to sail them. Their place was to be in the rear

[016-330] and they were to take no part whatever in the coming fight.

[016-331] As the first glimmerings of opalescent dawn dissolved the darkness, the

[016-332] straining eyes of the buccaneers were able to make out the tall rigging

[016-333] of the Spanish vessels, riding at anchor less than a quarter of a mile

[016-334] ahead. Entirely without suspicion as the Spaniards were, and rendered

[016-335] confident by their own overwhelming strength, it is unlikely that they

[016-336] used a vigilance keener than their careless habit. Certain it is that

[016-337] they did not sight Blood's fleet in that dim light until some time

[016-338] after Blood's fleet had sighted them. By the time that they had actively

[016-339] roused themselves, Wolverstone's sloop was almost upon them, speeding

[016-340] under canvas which had been crowded to her yards the moment the galleons

[016-341] had loomed into view.

[016-342] Straight for the Admiral's great ship, the Encarnacion, did Wolverstone

[016-343] head the sloop; then, lashing down the helm, he kindled from a match

[016-344] that hung ready lighted beside him a great torch of thickly plaited

[016-345] straw that had been steeped in bitumen. First it glowed, then as he

[016-346] swung it round his head, it burst into flame, just as the slight vessel

[016-347] went crashing and bumping and scraping against the side of the flagship,

[016-348] whilst rigging became tangled with rigging, to the straining of yards

[016-349] and snapping of spars overhead. His six men stood at their posts on the

[016-350] larboard side, stark naked, each armed with a grapnel, four of them on

[016-351] the gunwale, two of them aloft. At the moment of impact these grapnels

[016-352] were slung to bind the Spaniard to them, those aloft being intended to

[016-353] complete and preserve the entanglement of the rigging.

[016-355] trumpeting, and shouting. At first there had been a desperately hurried

[016-356] attempt to get up the anchor; but this was abandoned as being already

[016-357] too late; and conceiving themselves on the point of being boarded,

[016-358] the Spaniards stood to arms to ward off the onslaught. Its slowness in

[016-359] coming intrigued them, being so different from the usual tactics of the

[016-360] buccaneers. Further intrigued were they by the sight of the gigantic

[016-361] Wolverstone speeding naked along his deck with a great flaming torch

[016-362] held high. Not until he had completed his work did they begin to suspect

[016-363] the truth--that he was lighting slow-matches--and then one of their

[016-364] officers rendered reckless by panic ordered a boarding-party on to the

[016-365] shop.
