[026-001] CHAPTER XXVI. M. de RIVAROL

[026-002] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] was still in that disgruntled mood when he sailed from

[026-003] [[tortuga|Tortuga]], and still in that mood when he came to his moorings in the bay

[026-004] of Petit Goave. In that same mood he greeted M. le Baron de [[de-rivarol|Rivarol]] when

[026-005] this nobleman with his fleet of five men-of-war at last dropped anchor

[026-006] alongside the buccaneer ships, in the middle of February. The Frenchman

[026-007] had been six weeks on the voyage, he announced, delayed by unfavourable

[026-008] weather.

[026-009] Summoned to wait on him, Captain Blood repaired to the Castle of

[026-010] Petit Goave, where the interview was to take place. The Baron, a tall,

[026-011] hawk-faced man of forty, very cold and distant of manner, measured

[026-012] Captain Blood with an eye of obvious disapproval. Of [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]],

[026-013] Yberville, and [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]] who stood ranged behind their captain, he

[026-014] took no heed whatever. M. de Cussy offered Captain Blood a chair.

[026-015] “A moment, M. de Cussy. I do not think M. le Baron has observed that

[026-016] I am not alone. Let me present to you, sir, my companions: Captain

[026-017] Hagthorpe of the Elizabeth, Captain Wolverstone of the Atropos, and

[026-018] Captain Yberville of the Lachesis.”

[026-019] The Baron stared hard and haughtily at Captain Blood, then very

[026-020] distantly and barely perceptibly inclined his head to each of the other

[026-021] three. His manner implied plainly that he despised them and that he

[026-022] desired them at once to understand it. It had a curious effect upon

[026-023] Captain Blood. It awoke the devil in him, and it awoke at the same time

[026-024] his self-respect which of late had been slumbering. A sudden shame of

[026-025] his disordered, ill-kempt appearance made him perhaps the more defiant.

[026-026] There was almost a significance in the way he hitched his sword-belt

[026-027] round, so that the wrought hilt of his very serviceable rapier was

[026-028] brought into fuller view. He waved his captains to the chairs that stood

[026-029] about.

[026-030] “Draw up to the table, lads. We are keeping the Baron waiting.”

[026-031] They obeyed him, Wolverstone with a grin that was full of understanding.

[026-032] Haughtier grew the stare of M. de Rivarol. To sit at table with these

[026-033] bandits placed him upon what he accounted a dishonouring equality.

[026-034] It had been his notion that--with the possible exception of Captain

[026-035] Blood--they should take his instructions standing, as became men of

[026-036] their quality in the presence of a man of his. He did the only thing

[026-037] remaining to mark a distinction between himself and them. He put on his

[026-038] hat.

[026-039] “Ye're very wise now,” said Blood amiably. “I feel the draught myself.”

[026-040] And he covered himself with his plumed castor.

[026-041] M. de Rivarol changed colour. He quivered visibly with anger, and was

[026-042] a moment controlling himself before venturing to speak. M. de Cussy was

[026-043] obviously very ill at ease.

[026-044] “Sir,” said the Baron frostily, “you compel me to remind you that the

[026-045] rank you hold is that of Capitaine de Vaisseau, and that you are in

[026-046] the presence of the General of the Armies of France by Sea and Land in

[026-047] America. You compel me to remind you further that there is a deference

[026-048] due from your rank to mine.”

[026-049] “I am happy to assure you,” said Captain Blood, “that the reminder is

[026-050] unnecessary. I am by way of accounting myself a gentleman, little though

[026-051] I may look like one at present; and I should not account myself that

[026-052] were I capable of anything but deference to those whom nature or fortune

[026-053] may have placed above me, or to those who being placed beneath me in

[026-054] rank may labour under a disability to resent my lack of it.” It was a

[026-055] neatly intangible rebuke. M. de Rivarol bit his lip. Captain Blood swept

[026-056] on without giving him time to reply: “Thus much being clear, shall we

[026-057] come to business?”

[026-058] M. de Rivarol's hard eyes considered him a moment. “Perhaps it will be

[026-059] best,” said he. He took up a paper. “I have here a copy of the articles

[026-060] into which you entered with M. de Cussy. Before going further, I have to

[026-061] observe that M. de Cussy has exceeded his instructions in admitting

[026-062] you to one fifth of the prizes taken. His authority did not warrant his

[026-063] going beyond one tenth.”

[026-064] “That is a matter between yourself and M. de Cussy, my General.”

[026-065] “Oh, no. It is a matter between myself and you.”

[026-066] “Your pardon, my General. The articles are signed. So far as we are

[026-067] concerned, the matter is closed. Also out of regard for M. de Cussy, we

[026-068] should not desire to be witnesses of the rebukes you may consider that

[026-069] he deserves.”

[026-070] “What I may have to say to M. de Cussy is no concern of yours.”

[026-071] “That is what I am telling you, my General.”

[026-072] “But--nom de Dieu!--it is your concern, I suppose, that we cannot

[026-073] award you more than one tenth share.” M. de Rivarol smote the table in

[026-074] exasperation. This pirate was too infernally skillful a fencer.

[026-075] “You are quite certain of that, M. le Baron--that you cannot?”

[026-076] “I am quite certain that I will not.”

[026-077] Captain Blood shrugged, and looked down his nose. “In that case,”

[026-078] said he, “it but remains for me to present my little account for our

[026-079] disbursement, and to fix the sum at which we should be compensated for

[026-080] our loss of time and derangement in coming hither. That settled, we can

[026-081] part friends, M. le Baron. No harm has been done.”

[026-082] “What the devil do you mean?” The Baron was on his feet, leaning forward

[026-083] across the table.

[026-084] “Is it possible that I am obscure? My French, perhaps, is not of the

[026-085] purest, but....”

[026-086] “Oh, your French is fluent enough; too fluent at moments, if I may

[026-087] permit myself the observation. Now, look you here, M. le filibustier, I

[026-088] am not a man with whom it is safe to play the fool, as you may very soon

[026-089] discover. You have accepted service of the King of France--you and your

[026-090] men; you hold the rank and draw the pay of a Capitaine de Vaisseau,

[026-091] and these your officers hold the rank of lieutenants. These ranks carry

[026-092] obligations which you would do well to study, and penalties for failing

[026-093] to discharge them which you might study at the same time. They are

[026-094] something severe. The first [[duty|obligation]] of an officer is obedience. I

[026-095] commend it to your attention. You are not to conceive yourselves, as you

[026-096] appear to be doing, my allies in the enterprises I have in view, but my

[026-097] subordinates. In me you behold a commander to lead you, not a companion

[026-098] or an equal. You understand me, I hope.”

[026-099] “Oh, be sure that I understand,” Captain Blood laughed. He was

[026-100] recovering his normal self amazingly under the inspiring stimulus of

[026-101] conflict. The only thing that marred his enjoyment was the reflection

[026-102] that he had not shaved. “I forget nothing, I assure you, my General.

[026-103] I do not forget, for instance, as you appear to be doing, that the

[026-104] articles we signed are the condition of our service; and the articles

[026-105] provide that we receive one-fifth share. Refuse us that, and you cancel

[026-106] the articles; cancel the articles, and you cancel our services with

[026-107] them. From that moment we cease to have the honour to hold rank in the

[026-108] navies of the King of France.”

[026-109] There was more than a murmur of approval from his three captains.

[026-110] Rivarol glared at them, checkmated.

[026-111] “In effect...” M. de Cussy was beginning timidly.

[026-112] “In effect, monsieur, this is your doing,” the Baron flashed on him,

[026-113] glad to have some one upon whom he could fasten the sharp fangs of his

[026-114] irritation. “You should be broke for it. You bring the King's service

[026-115] into disrepute; you force me, His Majesty's representative, into an

[026-116] impossible position.”

[026-117] “Is it impossible to award us the one-fifth share?” quoth Captain Blood

[026-118] silkily. “In that case, there is no need for beat or for injuries to

[026-119] M. de Cussy. M. de Cussy knows that we would not have come for less.

[026-120] We depart again upon your assurance that you cannot award us more. And

[026-121] things are as they would have been if M. de Cussy had adhered rigidly

[026-122] to his instructions. I have proved, I hope, to your satisfaction, M.

[026-123] le Baron, that if you repudiate the articles you can neither claim our

[026-124] services nor hinder our departure--not in honour.”

[026-125] “Not in honour, sir? To the devil with your insolence! Do you imply that

[026-126] any course that were not in honour would be possible to me?”

[026-127] “I do not imply it, because it would not be possible,” said Captain

[026-128] Blood. “We should see to that. It is, my General, for you to say whether

[026-129] the articles are repudiated.”

[026-130] The Baron sat down. “I will consider the matter,” he said sullenly. “You

[026-131] shall be advised of my resolve.”

[026-132] Captain Blood rose, his officers rose with him. Captain Blood bowed.

[026-133] “M. le Baron!” said he.

[026-134] Then he and his buccaneers removed themselves from the August and irate

[026-135] presence of the General of the King's Armies by Land and Sea in America.

[026-136] You conceive that there followed for M. de Cussy an extremely bad

[026-137] quarter of an hour. M. de Cussy, in fact, deserves your sympathy. His

[026-138] self-sufficiency was blown from him by the haughty M. de Rivarol, as

[026-139] down from a thistle by the winds of autumn. The General of the King's

[026-140] Armies abused him--this man who was Governor of Hispaniola--as if he

[026-141] were a lackey. M. de Cussy defended himself by urging the thing that

[026-142] Captain Blood had so admirably urged already on his behalf--that if the

[026-143] terms he had made with the buccaneers were not confirmed there was no

[026-144] harm done. M. de Rivarol bullied and browbeat him into silence.

[026-145] Having exhausted abuse, the Baron proceeded to indignities. Since he

[026-146] accounted that M. de Cussy had proved himself unworthy of the post he

[026-147] held, M. de Rivarol took over the responsibilities of that post for as

[026-148] long as he might remain in Hispaniola, and to give effect to this he

[026-149] began by bringing soldiers from his ships, and setting his own guard in

[026-150] M. de Cussy's castle.

[026-151] Out of this, trouble followed quickly. Wolverstone coming ashore next

[026-152] morning in the picturesque garb that he affected, his head swathed in a

[026-153] coloured handkerchief, was jeered at by an officer of the newly landed

[026-154] French troops. Not accustomed to derision, Wolverstone replied in kind

[026-155] and with interest. The officer passed to insult, and Wolverstone struck

[026-156] him a blow that felled him, and left him only the half of his poor

[026-157] senses. Within the hour the matter was reported to M. de Rivarol, and

[026-158] before noon, by M. de Rivarol's orders, Wolverstone was under arrest in

[026-159] the castle.

[026-160] The Baron had just sat down to dinner with M. de Cussy when the negro

[026-161] who waited on them announced Captain Blood. Peevishly M. de Rivarol bade

[026-162] him be admitted, and there entered now into his presence a spruce and

[026-163] modish gentleman, dressed with care and sombre richness in black and

[026-164] silver, his swarthy, clear-cut face scrupulously shaven, his long black

[026-165] hair in ringlets that fell to a collar of fine point. In his right hand

[026-166] the gentleman carried a broad black hat with a scarlet ostrich-plume,

[026-167] in his left hand an ebony cane. His stockings were of silk, a bunch of

[026-168] ribbons masked his garters, and the black rosettes on his shoes were

[026-169] finely edged with gold.

[026-170] For a moment M. de Rivarol did not recognize him. For Blood looked

[026-171] younger by ten years than yesterday. But the vivid blue eyes under their

[026-172] level black brows were not to be forgotten, and they proclaimed him for

[026-173] the man announced even before he had spoken. His resurrected pride had

[026-174] demanded that he should put himself on an equality with the baron and

[026-175] advertise that equality by his exterior.

[026-176] “I come inopportunely,” he courteously excused himself. “My apologies.

[026-177] My business could not wait. It concerns, M. de Cussy, Captain

[026-178] Wolverstone of the Lachesis, whom you have placed under arrest.”

[026-179] “It was I who placed him under arrest,” said M. de Rivarol.

[026-180] “Indeed! But I thought that M. de Cussy was Governor of Hispaniola.”

[026-181] “Whilst I am here, monsieur, I am the supreme authority. It is as well

[026-182] that you should understand it.”

[026-183] “Perfectly. But it is not possible that you are aware of the mistake

[026-184] that has been made.”

[026-185] “Mistake, do you say?”

[026-186] “I say mistake. On the whole, it is polite of me to use that word. Also

[026-187] it is expedient. It will save discussions. Your people have arrested the

[026-188] wrong man, M. de Rivarol. Instead of the French officer, who used the

[026-189] grossest provocation, they have arrested Captain Wolverstone. It is a

[026-190] matter which I beg you to reverse without delay.”

[026-191] M. de Rivarol's hawk-face flamed scarlet. His dark eyes bulged.

[026-192] “Sir, you... you are insolent! But of an insolence that is intolerable!”

[026-193] Normally a man of the utmost self-possession he was so rudely shaken now

[026-194] that he actually stammered.

[026-195] “M. le Baron, you waste words. This is the New World. It is not merely

[026-196] new; it is novel to one reared amid the superstitions of the Old. That

[026-197] novelty you have not yet had time, perhaps, to realize; therefore I

[026-198] overlook the offensive epithet you have used. But justice is justice in

[026-199] the New World as in the Old, and [[justice|injustice]] as intolerable here as there.

[026-200] Now justice demands the enlargement of my officer and the arrest and

[026-201] punishment of yours. That justice I invite you, with submission, to

[026-202] administer.”

[026-203] “With submission?” snorted the Baron in furious scorn.

[026-204] “With the utmost submission, monsieur. But at the same time I will

[026-205] remind M. le Baron that my buccaneers number eight hundred; your troops

[026-206] five hundred; and M. de Cussy will inform you of the interesting fact

[026-207] that any one buccaneer is equal in action to at least three soldiers of

[026-208] the line. I am perfectly frank with you, monsieur, to save time and hard

[026-209] words. Either Captain Wolverstone is instantly set at liberty, or we

[026-210] must take measures to set him at liberty ourselves. The consequences may

[026-211] be appalling. But it is as you please, M. le Baron. You are the supreme

[026-212] authority. It is for you to say.”

[026-213] M. de Rivarol was white to the lips. In all his life he had never been

[026-214] so bearded and defied. But he controlled himself.

[026-215] “You will do me the favour to wait in the ante-room, M. le Capitaine.

[026-216] I desire a word with M. de Cussy. You shall presently be informed of my

[026-217] decision.”

[026-218] When the door had closed, the baron loosed his fury upon the head of M.

[026-219] de Cussy.

[026-220] “So, these are the men you have enlisted in the King's service, the men

[026-221] who are to serve under me--men who do not serve, but dictate, and this

[026-222] before the enterprise that has brought me from France is even under way!

[026-223] What explanations do you offer me, M. de Cussy? I warn you that I am

[026-224] not pleased with you. I am, in fact, as you may perceive, exceedingly

[026-225] angry.”

[026-226] The Governor seemed to shed his chubbiness. He drew himself stiffly

[026-227] erect.

[026-228] “Your rank, monsieur, does not give you the right to rebuke me; nor

[026-229] do the facts. I have enlisted for you the men that you desired me to

[026-230] enlist. It is not my fault if you do not know how to handle them better.

[026-231] As Captain Blood has told you, this is the New World.”

[026-232] “So, so!” M. de Rivarol smiled malignantly. “Not only do you offer no

[026-233] explanation, but you venture to put me in the wrong. Almost I admire

[026-234] your temerity. But there!” he waved the matter aside. He was supremely

[026-235] sardonic. “It is, you tell me, the New World, and--new worlds, new

[026-236] manners, I suppose. In time I may conform my ideas to this new world, or

[026-237] I may conform this new world to my ideas.” He was menacing on that. “For

[026-238] the moment I must accept what I find. It remains for you, monsieur,

[026-239] who have experience of these savage by-ways, to advise me out of that

[026-240] experience how to act.”

[026-241] “M. le Baron, it was a folly to have arrested the buccaneer captain. It

[026-242] would be madness to persist. We have not the forces to meet force.”

[026-243] “In that case, monsieur, perhaps you will tell me what we are to do with

[026-244] regard to the future. Am I to submit at every turn to the dictates

[026-245] of this man Blood? Is the enterprise upon which we are embarked to be

[026-246] conducted as he decrees? Am I, in short, the King's representative in

[026-247] America, to be at the mercy of these rascals?”

[026-248] “Oh, by no means. I am enrolling volunteers here in Hispaniola, and I

[026-249] am raising a corps of negroes. I compute that when this is done we shall

[026-250] have a force of a thousand men, the buccaneers apart.”

[026-251] “But in that case why not dispense with them?”

[026-252] “Because they will always remain the sharp edge of any weapon that we

[026-253] forge. In the class of warfare that lies before us they are so skilled

[026-254] that what Captain Blood has just said is not an overstatement. A

[026-255] buccaneer is equal to three soldiers of the line. At the same time we

[026-256] shall have a sufficient force to keep them in control. For the rest,

[026-257] monsieur, they have certain notions of honour. They will stand by their

[026-258] articles, and so that we deal justly with them, they will deal justly

[026-259] with us, and give no trouble. I have experience of them, and I pledge

[026-260] you my word for that.”

[026-261] M. de Rivarol condescended to be mollified. It was necessary that he

[026-262] should save his face, and in a degree the Governor afforded him the

[026-263] means to do so, as well as a certain guarantee for the future in the

[026-264] further force he was raising.

[026-265] “Very well,” he said. “Be so good as to recall this Captain Blood.”

[026-266] The Captain came in, assured and very dignified. M. de Rivarol found him

[026-267] detestable; but dissembled it.

[026-268] “M. le Capitaine, I have taken counsel with M. le Gouverneur. From what

[026-269] he tells me, it is possible that a mistake has been committed. Justice,

[026-270] you may be sure, shall be done. To ensure it, I shall myself preside

[026-271] over a council to be composed of two of my senior officers, yourself

[026-272] and an officer of yours. This council shall hold at once an impartial

[026-273] investigation into the affair, and the offender, the man guilty of

[026-274] having given provocation, shall be punished.”

[026-275] Captain Blood bowed. It was not his wish to be extreme. “Perfectly, M.

[026-276] le Baron. And now, sir, you have had the night for reflection in this

[026-277] matter of the articles. Am I to understand that you confirm or that you

[026-278] repudiate them?”

[026-279] M. de Rivarol's eyes narrowed. His mind was full of what M. de Cussy had

[026-280] said--that these buccaneers must prove the sharp edge of any weapon he

[026-281] might forge. He could not dispense with them. He perceived that he

[026-282] had blundered tactically in attempting to reduce the agreed share.

[026-283] Withdrawal from a position of that kind is ever fraught with loss of

[026-284] dignity. But there were those volunteers that M. de Cussy was enrolling

[026-285] to strengthen the hand of the King's General. Their presence might admit

[026-286] anon of the reopening of this question. Meanwhile he must retire in the

[026-287] best order possible.

[026-288] “I have considered that, too,” he announced. “And whilst my opinion

[026-289] remains unaltered, I must confess that since M. de Cussy has pledged us,

[026-290] it is for us to fulfil the pledges. The articles are confirmed, sir.”

[026-291] Captain Blood bowed again. In vain M. de Rivarol looked searchingly

[026-292] for the least trace of a smile of triumph on those firm lips. The

[026-293] buccaneer's face remained of the utmost gravity.

[026-294] Wolverstone was set at liberty that afternoon, and his assailant

[026-295] sentenced to two months' detention. Thus harmony was restored. But it

[026-296] had been an unpromising beginning, and there was more to follow shortly

[026-297] of a similar discordant kind.

[026-298] Blood and his officers were summoned a week later to a council which sat

[026-299] to determine their operations against Spain. M. de Rivarol laid before

[026-300] them a project for a raid upon the wealthy Spanish town of [[cartagena|Cartagena]].

[026-301] Captain Blood professed astonishment. Sourly invited by M. de Rivarol to

[026-302] state his grounds for it, he did so with the utmost frankness.

[026-303] “Were I General of the King's Armies in America,” said he, “I should

[026-304] have no doubt or hesitation as to the best way in which to serve my

[026-305] Royal master and the French nation. That which I think will be obvious

[026-306] to M. de Cussy, as it is to me, is that we should at once invade Spanish

[026-307] Hispaniola and reduce the whole of this fruitful and splendid island

[026-308] into the possession of the King of France.”

[026-309] “That may follow,” said M. de Rivarol. “It is my wish that we begin with

[026-310] Cartagena.”

[026-311] “You mean, sir, that we are to sail across the Caribbean on an

[026-312] adventurous expedition, neglecting that which lies here at our very

[026-313] door. In our absence, a Spanish invasion of French Hispaniola is

[026-314] possible. If we begin by reducing the Spaniards here, that possibility

[026-315] will be removed. We shall have added to the Crown of France the

[026-316] most coveted possession in the West Indies. The enterprise offers

[026-317] no particular difficulty; it may be speedily accomplished, and once

[026-318] accomplished, it would be time to look farther afield. That would seem

[026-319] the logical order in which this campaign should proceed.”

[026-320] He ceased, and there was silence. M. de Rivarol sat back in his chair,

[026-321] the feathered end of a quill between his teeth. Presently he cleared his

[026-322] throat and asked a question.

[026-323] “Is there anybody else who shares Captain Blood's opinion?”

[026-324] None answered him. His own officers were overawed by him; Blood's

[026-325] followers naturally preferred Cartagena, because offering the greater

[026-326] chance of loot. Loyalty to their leader kept them silent.

[026-327] “You seem to be alone in your opinion,” said the Baron with his vinegary

[026-328] smile.

[026-329] Captain Blood laughed outright. He had suddenly read the Baron's mind.

[026-330] His airs and graces and haughtiness had so imposed upon Blood that

[026-331] it was only now that at last he saw through them, into the fellow's

[026-332] peddling spirit. Therefore he laughed; there was really nothing else to

[026-333] do. But his laughter was charged with more anger even than contempt. He

[026-334] had been deluding himself that he had done with piracy. The conviction

[026-335] that this French service was free of any taint of that was the only

[026-336] consideration that had induced him to accept it. Yet here was this

[026-337] haughty, supercilious gentleman, who dubbed himself General of the

[026-338] Armies of France, proposing a plundering, thieving raid which, when

[026-339] stripped of its mean, transparent mask of legitimate warfare, was

[026-340] revealed as piracy of the most flagrant.

[026-341] M. de Rivarol, intrigued by his mirth, scowled upon him disapprovingly.

[026-342] “Why do you laugh, monsieur?”

[026-343] “Because I discover here an irony that is supremely droll. You, M. le

[026-344] Baron, General of the King's Armies by Land and Sea in America,

[026-345] propose an enterprise of a purely [[piracy|buccaneering]] character; whilst I,

[026-346] the buccaneer, am urging one that is more concerned with upholding the

[026-347] honour of France. You perceive how droll it is.”

[026-348] M. de Rivarol perceived nothing of the kind. M. de Rivarol in fact was

[026-349] extremely angry. He bounded to his feet, and every man in the room rose

[026-350] with him--save only M. de Cussy, who sat on with a grim smile on his

[026-351] lips. He, too, now read the Baron like an open book, and reading him

[026-352] despised him.

[026-353] “M. le filibustier,” cried Rivarol in a thick voice, “it seems that I

[026-354] must again remind you that I am your superior officer.”

[026-355] “My superior officer! You! Lord of the World! Why, you are just a common

[026-356] pirate! But you shall hear the truth for once, and that before all these

[026-357] gentlemen who have the honour to serve the King of France. It is for

[026-358] me, a buccaneer, a sea-robber, to stand here and tell you what is in the

[026-359] interest of French honour and the French Crown. Whilst you, the French

[026-360] King's appointed General, neglecting this, are for spending the King's

[026-361] resources against an outlying settlement of no account, shedding French

[026-362] blood in seizing a place that cannot be held, only because it has been

[026-363] reported to you that there is much gold in Cartagena, and that the

[026-364] plunder of it will enrich you. It is worthy of the huckster who sought
