[002-001] After the Peace of Nimeguen his movements are obscure. But we know that

[002-002] he spent two years in a Spanish prison, though we do not know how he

[002-003] contrived to get there. It may be due to this that upon his release

[002-004] he took his sword to France, and saw service with the French in their

[002-005] warring upon the Spanish Netherlands. Having reached, at last, the age

[002-006] of thirty-two, his appetite for adventure surfeited, his health having

[002-007] grown indifferent as the result of a neglected wound, he was suddenly

[002-008] overwhelmed by homesickness. He took ship from Nantes with intent to

[002-009] cross to Ireland. But the vessel being driven by stress of weather

[002-010] into Bridgewater Bay, and Blood's health having grown worse during the

[002-011] voyage, he decided to go ashore there, additionally urged to it by the

[002-012] fact that it was his mother's native soil.

[002-013] Thus in January of that year 1685 he had come to Bridgewater, possessor

[002-014] of a fortune that was approximately the same as that with which he had

[002-015] originally set out from Dublin eleven years ago.

[002-016] Because he liked the place, in which his health was rapidly restored

[002-017] to him, and because he conceived that he had passed through adventures

[002-018] enough for a man's lifetime, he determined to settle there, and take

[002-019] up at last the profession of medicine from which he had, with so little

[002-020] profit, broken away.

[002-021] That is all his story, or so much of it as matters up to that night, six

[002-022] months later, when the battle of Sedgemoor was fought.

[002-023] Deeming the impending action no affair of his, as indeed it was not, and

[002-024] indifferent to the activity with which Bridgewater was that night agog,

[002-025] Mr. Blood closed his ears to the sounds of it, and went early to bed. He

[002-026] was peacefully asleep long before eleven o'clock, at which hour, as

[002-027] you know, Monmouth rode but with his rebel host along the Bristol Road,

[002-028] circuitously to avoid the marshland that lay directly between himself

[002-029] and the Royal Army. You also know that his numerical advantage--possibly

[002-030] counter-balanced by the greater steadiness of the regular troops on the

[002-031] other side--and the advantages he derived from falling by surprise upon

[002-032] an army that was more or less asleep, were all lost to him by blundering

[002-033] and bad leadership before ever he was at grips with Feversham.

[002-034] The armies came into collision in the neighbourhood of two o'clock in

[002-035] the morning. Mr. Blood slept undisturbed through the distant boom of

[002-036] cannon. Not until four o'clock, when the sun was rising to dispel the

[002-037] last wisps of mist over that stricken field of battle, did he awaken

[002-038] from his tranquil slumbers.

[002-039] He sat up in bed, rubbed the sleep from his eyes, and collected himself.

[002-040] Blows were thundering upon the door of his house, and a voice was

[002-041] [[duty|calling]] incoherently. This was the noise that had aroused him.

[002-042] Conceiving that he had to do with some urgent obstetrical case, he

[002-043] reached for bedgown and slippers, to go below. On the landing he almost

[002-044] collided with Mrs. Barlow, new-risen and unsightly, in a state of panic.

[002-045] He quieted her cluckings with a word of reassurance, and went himself to

[002-046] open.

[002-047] There in slanting golden light of the new-risen sun stood a breathless,

[002-048] wild-eyed man and a steaming horse. Smothered in dust and grime, his

[002-049] clothes in disarray, the left sleeve of his doublet [[execution|hanging]] in rags,

[002-050] this young man opened his lips to speak, yet for a long moment remained

[002-051] speechless.

[002-052] In that moment Mr. Blood recognized him for the young shipmaster,

[002-053] Jeremiah [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]], the nephew of the maiden ladies opposite, one who had

[002-054] been drawn by the general enthusiasm into the vortex of that [[treason|rebellion]].

[002-055] The street was rousing, awakened by the sailor's noisy advent; doors

[002-056] were opening, and lattices were being unlatched for the protrusion of

[002-057] anxious, inquisitive heads.

[002-058] “Take your time, now,” said Mr. Blood. “I never knew speed made by

[002-059] overhaste.”

[002-060] But the wild-eyed lad paid no heed to the admonition. He plunged,

[002-061] headlong, into speech, gasping, breathless.

[002-062] “It is Lord Gildoy,” he panted. “He is sore wounded... at Oglethorpe's

[002-063] Farm by the river. I bore him thither... and... and he sent me for you.

[002-064] Come away! Come away!”

[002-065] He would have clutched the doctor, and haled him forth by force in

[002-066] bedgown and slippers as he was. But the doctor eluded that too eager

[002-067] hand.

[002-068] “To be sure, I'll come,” said he. He was distressed. Gildoy had been a

[002-069] very friendly, generous patron to him since his settling in these parts.

[002-070] And Mr. Blood was eager enough to do what he now could to discharge

[002-071] the debt, grieved that the occasion should have arisen, and in such a

[002-072] manner--for he knew quite well that the rash young nobleman had been an

[002-073] active agent of the Duke's. “To be sure, I'll come. But first give me

[002-074] leave to get some clothes and other things that I may need.”

[002-075] “There's no time to lose.”

[002-076] “Be easy now. I'll lose none. I tell ye again, ye'll go quickest by

[002-077] going leisurely. Come in... take a chair...” He threw open the door of a

[002-078] parlour.

[002-079] Young Pitt waved aside the invitation.

[002-080] “I'll wait here. Make haste, in God's name.” Mr. Blood went off to dress

[002-081] and to fetch a case of instruments.

[002-082] Questions concerning the precise nature of Lord Gildoy's hurt could wait

[002-083] until they were on their way. Whilst he pulled on his boots, he gave

[002-084] Mrs. Barlow instructions for the day, which included the matter of a

[002-085] dinner he was not destined to eat.

[002-086] When at last he went forth again, Mrs. Barlow clucking after him like

[002-087] a disgruntled fowl, he found young Pitt smothered in a crowd of scared,

[002-088] half-dressed townsfolk--mostly women--who had come hastening for news

[002-089] of how the battle had sped. The news he gave them was to be read in the

[002-090] lamentations with which they disturbed the morning air.

[002-091] At sight of the doctor, dressed and booted, the case of instruments

[002-092] tucked under his arm, the messenger disengaged himself from those who

[002-093] pressed about, shook off his weariness and the two tearful aunts that

[002-094] clung most closely, and seizing the bridle of his horse, he climbed to

[002-095] the saddle.

[002-096] “Come along, sir,” he cried. “Mount behind me.”

[002-097] Mr. Blood, without wasting words, did as he was bidden. Pitt touched

[002-098] the horse with his spur. The little crowd gave way, and thus, upon

[002-099] the crupper of that doubly-laden horse, clinging to the belt of his

[002-100] companion, [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] set out upon his Odyssey. For this Pitt, in whom

[002-101] he beheld no more than the messenger of a wounded rebel gentleman, was

[002-102] indeed the very messenger of Fate.

[002-103] CHAPTER II. KIRKE'S DRAGOONS

[002-104] Oglethorpe's farm stood a mile or so to the south of Bridgewater on the

[002-105] right bank of the river. It was a straggling Tudor building showing grey

[002-106] above the ivy that clothed its lower parts. Approaching it now, through

[002-107] the fragrant orchards amid which it seemed to drowse in Arcadian peace

[002-108] beside the waters of the Parrett, sparkling in the morning sunlight,

[002-109] Mr. Blood might have had a difficulty in believing it part of a world

[002-110] tormented by strife and bloodshed.

[002-111] On the bridge, as they had been riding out of Bridgewater, they had met

[002-112] a vanguard of fugitives from the field of battle, weary, broken men,

[002-113] many of them wounded, all of them terror-stricken, staggering in

[002-114] speedless haste with the last remnants of their strength into the

[002-115] shelter which it was their vain illusion the town would afford them.

[002-116] Eyes glazed with lassitude and fear looked up piteously out of haggard

[002-117] faces at Mr. Blood and his companion as they rode forth; hoarse voices

[002-118] cried a warning that merciless pursuit was not far behind. Undeterred,

[002-119] however, young Pitt rode amain along the dusty road by which these

[002-120] poor fugitives from that swift rout on Sedgemoor came flocking in

[002-121] ever-increasing numbers. Presently he swung aside, and quitting the road

[002-122] took to a pathway that crossed the dewy meadowlands. Even here they

[002-123] met odd groups of these human derelicts, who were scattering in all

[002-124] directions, looking fearfully behind them as they came through the long

[002-125] grass, expecting at every moment to see the red coats of the dragoons.

[002-126] But as Pitt's direction was a southward one, bringing them ever nearer

[002-127] to Feversham's headquarters, they were presently clear of that human

[002-128] flotsam and jetsam of the battle, and riding through the peaceful

[002-129] orchards heavy with the ripening fruit that was soon to make its annual

[002-130] yield of cider.

[002-131] At last they alighted on the kidney stones of the courtyard, and Baynes,

[002-132] the master, of the homestead, grave of countenance and flustered of

[002-133] manner, gave them welcome.

[002-134] In the spacious, stone-flagged hall, the doctor found Lord

[002-135] Gildoy--a very tall and dark young gentleman, prominent of chin and

[002-136] nose--stretched on a cane day-bed under one of the tall mullioned

[002-137] windows, in the care of Mrs. Baynes and her comely daughter. His cheeks

[002-138] were leaden-hued, his eyes closed, and from his blue lips came with each

[002-139] laboured breath a faint, moaning noise.

[002-140] Mr. Blood stood for a moment silently considering his patient. He

[002-141] deplored that a youth with such bright hopes in life as Lord Gildoy's

[002-142] should have risked all, perhaps existence itself, to forward the

[002-143] ambition of a worthless adventurer. Because he had liked and honoured

[002-144] this brave lad he paid his case the tribute of a sigh. Then he knelt to

[002-145] his task, ripped away doublet and underwear to lay bare his lordship's

[002-146] mangled side, and called for water and linen and what else he needed for

[002-147] his work.

[002-148] He was still intent upon it a half-hour later when the dragoons invaded

[002-149] the homestead. The clatter of hooves and hoarse shouts that heralded

[002-150] their approach disturbed him not at all. For one thing, he was not

[002-151] easily disturbed; for another, his task absorbed him. But his lordship,

[002-152] who had now recovered consciousness, showed considerable alarm, and the

[002-153] battle-stained Jeremy Pitt sped to cover in a clothes-press. Baynes was

[002-154] uneasy, and his wife and daughter trembled. Mr. Blood reassured them.

[002-155] “Why, what's to fear?” he said. “It's a Christian country, this, and

[002-156] Christian men do not make war upon the wounded, nor upon those who

[002-157] harbour them.” He still had, you see, illusions about Christians.

[002-158] He held a glass of cordial, prepared under his directions, to his

[002-159] lordship's lips. “Give your mind peace, my lord. The worst is done.”

[002-160] And then they came rattling and clanking into the stone-flagged hall--a

[002-161] round dozen jack-booted, lobster-coated troopers of the Tangiers

[002-162] Regiment, led by a sturdy, black-browed fellow with a deal of gold lace

[002-163] about the breast of his coat.

[002-164] Baynes stood his ground, his attitude half-defiant, whilst his wife

[002-165] and daughter shrank away in renewed fear. Mr. Blood, at the head of the

[002-166] day-bed, looked over his shoulder to take stock of the invaders.

[002-167] The officer barked an order, which brought his men to an attentive halt,

[002-168] then swaggered forward, his gloved hand bearing down the pummel of

[002-169] his sword, his spurs jingling musically as he moved. He announced his

[002-170] authority to the yeoman.

[002-171] “I am Captain Hobart, of Colonel Kirke's dragoons. What rebels do you

[002-172] harbour?”

[002-173] The yeoman took alarm at that ferocious truculence. It expressed itself

[002-174] in his trembling voice.

[002-175] “I... I am no harbourer of rebels, sir. This wounded gentleman....”

[002-176] “I can see for myself.” The Captain stamped forward to the day-bed, and

[002-177] scowled down upon the grey-faced sufferer.

[002-178] “No need to ask how he came in this state and by his wounds. A damned

[002-179] rebel, and that's enough for me.” He flung a [[authority|command]] at his dragoons.

[002-180] “Out with him, my lads.”

[002-181] Mr. Blood got between the day-bed and the troopers.

[002-182] “In the name of humanity, sir!” said he, on a note of anger. “This is

[002-183] England, not Tangiers. The gentleman is in sore case. He may not be

[002-184] moved without peril to his life.”

[002-185] Captain Hobart was amused.

[002-186] “Oh, I am to be tender of the lives of these rebels! Odds blood! Do you

[002-187] think it's to benefit his health we're taking him? There's gallows being

[002-188] planted along the road from Weston to Bridgewater, and he'll serve

[002-189] for one of them as well as another. Colonel Kirke'll learn these

[002-190] nonconforming oafs something they'll not forget in generations.”
