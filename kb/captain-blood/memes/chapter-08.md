[008-001] An angry voice aroused him from his unhappy thoughts.

[008-002] “What the devil are you doing here?”

[008-003] The returning [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] came striding into the stockade, his

[008-004] negroes following ever.

[008-005] Mr. Blood turned to face him, and over that swarthy countenance--which,

[008-006] indeed, by now was tanned to the golden brown of a half-caste Indian--a

[008-007] mask descended.

[008-008] “Doing?” said he blandly. “Why, the duties of my office.”

[008-009] The Colonel, striding furiously forward, observed two things. The

[008-010] empty pannikin on the seat beside the prisoner, and the palmetto leaf

[008-011] protecting his back. “Have you dared to do this?” The veins on the

[008-012] planter's forehead stood out like cords.

[008-013] “Of course I have.” Mr. Blood's tone was one of faint surprise.

[008-014] “I said he was to have neither meat nor drink until I ordered it.”

[008-015] “Sure, now, I never heard ye.”

[008-016] “You never heard me? How should you have heard me when you weren't

[008-017] here?”

[008-018] “Then how did ye expect me to know what orders ye'd given?” Mr. Blood's

[008-019] tone was positively aggrieved. “All that I knew was that one of your

[008-020] slaves was being murthered by the sun and the flies. And I says to

[008-021] myself, this is one of the Colonel's slaves, and I'm the Colonel's

[008-022] doctor, and sure it's my duty to be looking after the Colonel's

[008-023] property. So I just gave the fellow a spoonful of water and covered his

[008-024] back from the sun. And wasn't I right now?”

[008-025] “Right?” The Colonel was almost speechless.

[008-026] “Be easy, now, be easy!” Mr. Blood implored him. “It's an apoplexy ye'll

[008-027] be contacting if ye give way to heat like this.”

[008-028] The planter thrust him aside with an imprecation, and stepping forward

[008-029] tore the palmetto leaf from the prisoner's back.

[008-030] “In the name of humanity, now....” Mr. Blood was beginning.

[008-031] The Colonel swung upon him furiously. “Out of this!” he commanded. “And

[008-032] don't come near him again until I send for you, unless you want to be

[008-033] served in the same way.”

[008-034] He was terrific in his menace, in his bulk, and in the [[authority|power]] of him. But

[008-035] Mr. Blood never flinched. It came to the Colonel, as he found himself

[008-036] steadily regarded by those light-blue eyes that looked so arrestingly

[008-037] odd in that tawny face--like pale sapphires set in copper--that this

[008-038] rogue had for some time now been growing presumptuous. It was a matter

[008-039] that he must presently correct. Meanwhile Mr. Blood was speaking again,

[008-040] his tone quietly insistent.

[008-041] “In the name of humanity,” he repeated, “ye'll allow me to do what I can

[008-042] to ease his sufferings, or I swear to you that I'll forsake at once the

[008-043] duties of a doctor, and that it's devil another patient will I attend in

[008-044] this unhealthy island at all.”

[008-045] For an instant the Colonel was too amazed to speak. Then--

[008-046] “By God!” he roared. “D'ye dare take that tone with me, you dog? D'ye

[008-047] dare to make terms with me?”

[008-048] “I do that.” The unflinching blue eyes looked squarely into the

[008-049] Colonel's, and there was a devil peeping out of them, the devil of

[008-050] recklessness that is born of despair.

[008-051] Colonel Bishop considered him for a long moment in silence. “I've been

[008-052] too soft with you,” he said at last. “But that's to be mended.” And he

[008-053] tightened his lips. “I'll have the rods to you, until there's not an

[008-054] inch of skin left on your dirty back.”

[008-055] “Will ye so? And what would Governor Steed do, then?”

[008-056] “Ye're not the only doctor on the island.”

[008-057] Mr. Blood actually laughed. “And will ye tell that to his excellency,

[008-058] him with the gout in his foot so bad that he can't stand? Ye know very

[008-059] well it's devil another doctor will he tolerate, being an intelligent

[008-060] man that knows what's good for him.”

[008-061] But the Colonel's brute passion thoroughly aroused was not so easily to

[008-062] be baulked. “If you're alive when my blacks have done with you, perhaps

[008-063] you'll come to your senses.”

[008-064] He swung to his negroes to issue an order. But it was never issued. At

[008-065] that moment a terrific rolling thunderclap drowned his voice and shook

[008-066] the very air. Colonel Bishop jumped, his negroes jumped with him, and so

[008-067] even did the apparently imperturbable Mr. Blood. Then the four of them

[008-068] stared together seawards.

[008-069] Down in the bay all that could be seen of the great ship, standing now

[008-070] within a cable's-length of the fort, were her topmasts thrusting above

[008-071] a cloud of smoke in which she was enveloped. From the cliffs a flight

[008-072] of startled seabirds had risen to circle in the blue, giving tongue to

[008-073] their alarm, the plaintive curlew noisiest of all.

[008-074] As those men stared from the eminence on which they stood, not yet

[008-075] understanding what had taken place, they saw the British Jack dip from

[008-076] the main truck and vanish into the rising cloud below. A moment more,

[008-077] and up through that cloud to replace the flag of England soared the gold

[008-078] and crimson banner of Castile. And then they understood.

[008-079] “Pirates!” roared the Colonel, and again, “Pirates!”

[008-080] Fear and incredulity were blent in his voice. He had paled under his tan

[008-081] until his face was the colour of clay, and there was a wild fury in his

[008-082] beady eyes. His negroes looked at him, grinning idiotically, all teeth

[008-083] and eyeballs.

[008-084] CHAPTER VIII. SPANIARDS

[008-085] The stately ship that had been allowed to sail so leisurely into

[008-086] Carlisle Bay under her false colours was a Spanish privateer, coming to

[008-087] pay off some of the heavy debt piled up by the predaceous Brethren of

[008-088] the Coast, and the recent defeat by the Pride of Devon of two treasure

[008-089] galleons bound for Cadiz. It happened that the galleon which escaped in

[008-090] a more or less crippled condition was commanded by [[don-diego|Don Diego de Espinosa]]

[008-091] y Valdez, who was own brother to the Spanish Admiral [[don-miguel|Don Miguel]] de

[008-092] Espinosa, and who was also a very hasty, proud, and hot-tempered

[008-093] gentleman.

[008-094] Galled by his defeat, and choosing to forget that his own conduct had

[008-095] invited it, he had sworn to teach the English a sharp lesson which they

[008-096] should remember. He would take a leaf out of the book of Morgan and

[008-097] those other robbers of the sea, and make a punitive raid upon an English

[008-098] settlement. Unfortunately for himself and for many others, his brother

[008-099] the Admiral was not at hand to restrain him when for this purpose he

[008-100] fitted out the Cinco Llagas at San Juan de Porto Rico. He chose for

[008-101] his objective the island of [[barbados|Barbados]], whose natural strength was apt to

[008-102] render her defenders careless. He chose it also because thither had the

[008-103] Pride of Devon been tracked by his scouts, and he desired a measure of

[008-104] [[justice|poetic justice]] to invest his vengeance. And he chose a moment when there

[008-105] were no ships of war at anchor in Carlisle Bay.

[008-106] He had succeeded so well in his intentions that he had aroused no

[008-107] suspicion until he saluted the fort at short range with a broadside of

[008-108] twenty guns.

[008-109] And now the four gaping watchers in the stockade on the headland beheld

[008-110] the great ship creep forward under the rising cloud of smoke,

[008-111] her mainsail unfurled to increase her steering way, and go about

[008-112] close-hauled to bring her larboard guns to bear upon the unready fort.

[008-113] With the crashing roar of that second broadside, Colonel Bishop awoke

[008-114] from stupefaction to a recollection of where his duty lay. In the town

[008-115] below drums were beating frantically, and a trumpet was bleating, as

[008-116] if the peril needed further advertising. As commander of the Barbados

[008-117] Militia, the place of Colonel Bishop was at the head of his scanty

[008-118] troops, in that fort which the Spanish guns were pounding into rubble.

[008-119] Remembering it, he went off at the double, despite his bulk and the

[008-120] heat, his negroes trotting after him.

[008-121] Mr. Blood turned to [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]]. He laughed grimly. “Now that,” said he,

[008-122] “is what I call a timely interruption. Though what'll come of it,” he

[008-123] added as an afterthought, “the devil himself knows.”

[008-124] As a third broadside was thundering forth, he picked up the palmetto

[008-125] leaf and carefully replaced it on the back of his fellow-slave.

[008-126] And then into the stockade, panting and sweating, came Kent followed by

[008-127] best part of a score of [[slavery|plantation]] workers, some of whom were black and

[008-128] all of whom were in a state of panic. He led them into the low white

[008-129] house, to bring them forth again, within a moment, as it seemed, armed

[008-130] now with muskets and hangers and some of them equipped with bandoleers.

[008-131] By this time the rebels-convict were coming in, in twos and threes,

[008-132] having abandoned their work upon finding themselves unguarded and upon

[008-133] scenting the general dismay.

[008-134] Kent paused a moment, as his hastily armed guard dashed forth, to fling

[008-135] an order to those slaves.

[008-136] “To the woods!” he bade them. “Take to the woods, and lie close there,

[008-137] until this is over, and we've gutted these Spanish swine.”

[008-138] On that he went off in haste after his men, who were to be added to

[008-139] those massing in the town, so as to oppose and overwhelm the Spanish

[008-140] landing parties.

[008-141] The slaves would have obeyed him on the instant but for Mr. Blood.

[008-142] “What need for haste, and in this heat?” quoth he. He was surprisingly

[008-143] cool, they thought. “Maybe there'll be no need to take to the woods at

[008-144] all, and, anyway, it will be time enough to do so when the Spaniards are

[008-145] masters of the town.”

[008-146] And so, joined now by the other stragglers, and numbering in all a

[008-147] round score--rebels-convict all--they stayed to watch from their

[008-148] vantage-ground the fortunes of the furious battle that was being waged

[008-149] below.

[008-150] The landing was contested by the militia and by every islander capable

[008-151] of bearing arms with the fierce resoluteness of men who knew that

[008-152] no quarter was to be expected in defeat. The ruthlessness of Spanish

[008-153] soldiery was a byword, and not at his worst had Morgan or L'Ollonais

[008-154] ever perpetrated such horrors as those of which these Castilian

[008-155] gentlemen were capable.

[008-156] But this Spanish commander knew his business, which was more than could

[008-157] truthfully be said for the [[barbados-militia|Barbados Militia]]. Having gained the advantage

[008-158] of a surprise blow, which had put the fort out of action, he soon showed

[008-159] them that he was master of the situation. His guns turned now upon the

[008-160] open space behind the mole, where the incompetent Bishop had marshalled

[008-161] his men, tore the militia into bloody rags, and covered the landing

[008-162] parties which were making the shore in their own boats and in several

[008-163] of those which had rashly gone out to the great ship before her identity

[008-164] was revealed.

[008-165] All through the scorching afternoon the battle went on, the rattle and

[008-166] crack of musketry penetrating ever deeper into the town to show that

[008-167] the defenders were being driven steadily back. By sunset two hundred and

[008-168] fifty Spaniards were masters of Bridgetown, the islanders were disarmed,

[008-169] and at Government House, Governor Steed--his gout forgotten in his

[008-170] panic--supported by Colonel Bishop and some lesser officers, was being

[008-171] informed by Don Diego, with an urbanity that was itself a mockery, of

[008-172] the sum that would be required in ransom.

[008-173] For a hundred thousand [[ransom|pieces of eight]] and fifty head of cattle, Don

[008-174] Diego would forbear from reducing the place to ashes. And what time

[008-175] that suave and courtly commander was settling these details with the

[008-176] apoplectic British Governor, the Spaniards were smashing and looting,

[008-177] feasting, drinking, and ravaging after the hideous manner of their kind.

[008-178] Mr. Blood, greatly daring, ventured down at dusk into the town. What

[008-179] he saw there is recorded by Jeremy Pitt to whom he subsequently related

[008-180] it--in that voluminous log from which the greater part of my narrative

[008-181] is derived. I have no intention of repeating any of it here. It is

[008-182] all too loathsome and nauseating, incredible, indeed, that men however

[008-183] abandoned could ever descend such an abyss of bestial cruelty and lust.
