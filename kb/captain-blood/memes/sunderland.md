# Lord Sunderland

## Overview

Robert Spencer, 2nd Earl of Sunderland (1641–1702), appears in *Captain Blood* only through the letters and instructions he dispatches from Whitehall, yet his influence on the plot is decisive. As James II's Secretary of State, it is Sunderland who authors the policy of recruiting Blood to English service, dispatching his kinsman Lord Julian Wade across the Atlantic with "commissions made out in blank" ((018-287)) and instructions to secure Blood's allegiance. Sunderland is the novel's representative of metropolitan political calculation — the man who, from the safety of London, makes the cost-benefit analysis that will determine Blood's fate. His letter "gives precise detail" ((004-077)) on matters of colonial labour supply, and his strategic vision drives the diplomatic subplot that ultimately transforms Blood's legal status.

## In the Novel

Sunderland's role is entirely epistolary and political — he acts through agents, never in person — but his decisions shape the novel's second half.

**The Secretary's letter.** Sunderland's first appearance in the narrative is through a letter concerning the supply of convict labour to the Caribbean plantations. "My Lord Sunderland's letter gives precise detail" ((004-077)) of the arrangements for transporting Monmouth's rebels, and the passage connects the distant machinery of state to the immediate reality of Blood's enslavement. Sunderland, in this early appearance, is the bureaucratic enabler of the plantation system — the man who, in Whitehall, signs the orders that will consign hundreds of men to "the sugar plantation under a pitiless sun" ((006-299)).

**The mission to Blood.** Later, Sunderland reappears as the author of a different policy: the recruitment of Blood to English service. "Sunderland, the Secretary of State, had" ((018-249)) concluded that Blood's buccaneering skill could be redirected against England's enemies, and his "kinsman, my Lord Sunderland" ((018-232)) — Lord Julian Wade — is dispatched as the emissary. The mission is characteristic Sunderland: a cold-eyed political calculation in which Blood's past crimes matter less than his present utility.

**The strategy of co-option.** Sunderland's policy toward Blood exemplifies the Restoration state's approach to the Caribbean buccaneers: co-opt where possible, hang where necessary. His willingness to offer a commission to a man condemned in the king's name reveals a pragmatism that borders on cynicism — and Sabatini, through Blood's wary response, invites the reader to recognize the moral emptiness of a system that condemns and rewards the same man depending on political convenience.

**Sunderland's despair.** At one point, the narrative notes that "Sunderland despair of solving this vexat…" ((018-276)) — the passage is truncated in surviving text, but it suggests that even the most powerful minister of state found the Caribbean's tangled politics resistant to Whitehall's remote control.

## Historical Basis

Robert Spencer, 2nd Earl of Sunderland (1641–1702), was one of the most skilled and unprincipled politicians of the Restoration period. He served as Secretary of State under Charles II and James II, converted to Catholicism to retain James's favour, and then switched allegiance to William of Orange during the Glorious Revolution — surviving each regime change through a combination of intelligence, flexibility, and the total absence of fixed principles. His historical reputation as a political survivor makes him an apt choice for the role Sabatini assigns him: the man who can, without apparent discomfort, draft the orders that transport rebels to slavery and then draft the commissions that recruit those same rebels to Crown service.

## Relationships

- **Lord Julian Wade** — Sunderland's kinsman and diplomatic agent, dispatched to the Caribbean to recruit Blood.
- **Peter Blood** — The object of Sunderland's policy: first a condemned rebel, then a strategic asset.
- **King James II** — The sovereign in whose name Sunderland acts, and whose commission he dispatches to Blood.
