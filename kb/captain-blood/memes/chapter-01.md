[001-036] CHAPTER I. THE MESSENGER

[001-037] [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]], bachelor of medicine and several other things besides,

[001-038] smoked a pipe and tended the geraniums boxed on the sill of his window

[001-039] above Water Lane in the town of Bridgewater.

[001-040] Sternly disapproving eyes considered him from a window opposite, but

[001-041] went disregarded. Mr. Blood's attention was divided between his task and

[001-042] the stream of humanity in the narrow street below; a stream which poured

[001-043] for the second time that day towards Castle Field, where earlier in

[001-044] the afternoon Ferguson, the Duke's chaplain, had preached a sermon

[001-045] containing more treason than divinity.

[001-046] These straggling, excited groups were mainly composed of men with green

[001-047] boughs in their hats and the most ludicrous of weapons in their hands.

[001-048] Some, it is true, shouldered fowling pieces, and here and there a sword

[001-049] was brandished; but more of them were armed with clubs, and most of them

[001-050] trailed the mammoth pikes fashioned out of scythes, as formidable to

[001-051] the eye as they were clumsy to the hand. There were weavers, brewers,

[001-052] carpenters, smiths, masons, bricklayers, cobblers, and representatives

[001-053] of every other of the trades of peace among these improvised men of war.

[001-054] Bridgewater, like Taunton, had yielded so generously of its manhood to

[001-055] the service of the bastard Duke that for any to abstain whose age and

[001-056] strength admitted of his bearing arms was to brand himself a coward or a

[001-057] papist.

[001-058] Yet Peter Blood, who was not only able to bear arms, but trained and

[001-059] skilled in their use, who was certainly no coward, and a papist only

[001-060] when it suited him, tended his geraniums and smoked his pipe on that

[001-061] warm July evening as indifferently as if nothing were afoot. One other

[001-062] thing he did. He flung after those war-fevered enthusiasts a line of

[001-063] Horace--a poet for whose work he had early conceived an inordinate

[001-064] affection:

[001-065] “Quo, quo, scelesti, ruitis?”

[001-066] And now perhaps you guess why the hot, intrepid blood inherited from the

[001-067] roving sires of his Somersetshire mother remained cool amidst all this

[001-068] frenzied fanatical heat of [[treason|rebellion]]; why the turbulent spirit which had

[001-069] forced him once from the sedate academical bonds his father would

[001-070] have imposed upon him, should now remain quiet in the very midst of

[001-071] turbulence. You realize how he regarded these men who were rallying to

[001-072] the banners of liberty--the banners woven by the virgins of Taunton, the

[001-073] girls from the seminaries of Miss Blake and Mrs. Musgrove, who--as the

[001-074] ballad runs--had ripped open their silk petticoats to make colours for

[001-075] King Monmouth's army. That Latin line, contemptuously flung after them

[001-076] as they clattered down the cobbled street, reveals his mind. To him they

[001-077] were fools rushing in wicked frenzy upon their ruin.

[001-078] You see, he knew too much about this fellow Monmouth and the pretty

[001-079] brown slut who had borne him, to be deceived by the legend of

[001-080] legitimacy, on the strength of which this standard of rebellion had

[001-081] been raised. He had read the absurd proclamation posted at the Cross

[001-082] at Bridgewater--as it had been posted also at Taunton and

[001-083] elsewhere--setting forth that “upon the decease of our Sovereign Lord

[001-084] Charles the Second, the right of succession to the Crown of England,

[001-085] Scotland, France, and Ireland, with the dominions and territories

[001-086] thereunto belonging, did legally descend and devolve upon the most

[001-087] illustrious and high-born Prince James, Duke of Monmouth, son and heir

[001-088] apparent to the said King Charles the Second.”

[001-089] It had moved him to laughter, as had the further announcement that

[001-090] “James Duke of York did first cause the said late King to be poysoned,

[001-091] and immediately thereupon did usurp and invade the Crown.”

[001-092] He knew not which was the greater lie. For Mr. Blood had spent a third

[001-093] of his life in the Netherlands, where this same James Scott--who now

[001-094] proclaimed himself James the Second, by the grace of God, King, et

[001-095] cetera--first saw the light some six-and-thirty years ago, and he was

[001-096] acquainted with the story current there of the fellow's real paternity.

[001-097] Far from being legitimate--by virtue of a pretended secret marriage

[001-098] between Charles Stuart and Lucy Walter--it was possible that this

[001-099] Monmouth who now proclaimed himself King of England was not even the

[001-100] illegitimate child of the late sovereign. What but ruin and disaster

[001-101] could be the end of this grotesque pretension? How could it be hoped

[001-102] that England would ever swallow such a Perkin? And it was on his behalf,

[001-103] to uphold his fantastic claim, that these West Country clods, led by a

[001-104] few armigerous Whigs, had been seduced into rebellion!

[001-105] “Quo, quo, scelesti, ruitis?”

[001-106] He laughed and sighed in one; but the laugh dominated the sigh, for Mr.

[001-107] Blood was unsympathetic, as are most self-sufficient men; and he

[001-108] was very self-sufficient; adversity had taught him so to be. A more

[001-109] tender-hearted man, possessing his vision and his knowledge, might have

[001-110] found cause for tears in the contemplation of these ardent, simple,

[001-111] Nonconformist sheep going forth to the shambles--escorted to the

[001-112] rallying ground on Castle Field by wives and daughters, sweethearts and

[001-113] mothers, sustained by the delusion that they were to take the field

[001-114] in defence of Right, of Liberty, and of Religion. For he knew, as

[001-115] all Bridgewater knew and had known now for some hours, that it was

[001-116] Monmouth's intention to deliver battle that same night. The Duke was to

[001-117] lead a surprise attack upon the Royalist army under Feversham that was

[001-118] now encamped on Sedgemoor. Mr. Blood assumed that Lord Feversham would

[001-119] be equally well-informed, and if in this assumption he was wrong,

[001-120] at least he was justified of it. He was not to suppose the Royalist

[001-121] commander so indifferently skilled in the trade he followed.

[001-122] Mr. Blood knocked the ashes from his pipe, and drew back to close his

[001-123] window. As he did so, his glance travelling straight across the street

[001-124] met at last the glance of those hostile eyes that watched him. There

[001-125] were two pairs, and they belonged to the Misses [[jeremy-pitt|Pitt]], two amiable,

[001-126] sentimental maiden ladies who yielded to none in Bridgewater in their

[001-127] worship of the handsome Monmouth.

[001-128] Mr. Blood smiled and inclined his head, for he was on friendly terms

[001-129] with these ladies, one of whom, indeed, had been for a little while his

[001-130] patient. But there was no response to his greeting. Instead, the eyes

[001-131] gave him back a stare of cold disdain. The smile on his thin lips grew a

[001-132] little broader, a little less pleasant. He understood the reason of that

[001-133] hostility, which had been daily growing in this past week since Monmouth

[001-134] had come to turn the brains of women of all ages. The Misses Pitt,

[001-135] he apprehended, contemned him that he, a young and vigorous man, of a

[001-136] military training which might now be valuable to the Cause, should stand

[001-137] aloof; that he should placidly smoke his pipe and tend his geraniums on

[001-138] this evening of all evenings, when men of spirit were rallying to the

[001-139] Protestant Champion, offering their blood to place him on the throne

[001-140] where he belonged.

[001-141] If Mr. Blood had condescended to debate the matter with these ladies, he

[001-142] might have urged that having had his fill of wandering and adventuring,

[001-143] he was now embarked upon the career for which he had been originally

[001-144] intended and for which his studies had equipped him; that he was a man

[001-145] of medicine and not of war; a healer, not a slayer. But they would have

[001-146] answered him, he knew, that in such a cause it behoved every man who

[001-147] deemed himself a man to take up arms. They would have pointed out that

[001-148] their own nephew Jeremiah, who was by trade a sailor, the master of a

[001-149] ship--which by an ill-chance for that young man had come to anchor at

[001-150] this season in Bridgewater Bay--had quitted the helm to snatch up a

[001-151] musket in defence of Right. But Mr. Blood was not of those who argue. As

[001-152] I have said, he was a self-sufficient man.

[001-153] He closed the window, drew the curtains, and turned to the pleasant,

[001-154] candle-lighted room, and the table on which Mrs. Barlow, his

[001-155] housekeeper, was in the very act of spreading supper. To her, however,

[001-156] he spoke aloud his thought.

[001-157] “It's out of favour I am with the vinegary virgins over the way.”

[001-158] He had a pleasant, vibrant voice, whose metallic ring was softened and

[001-159] muted by the Irish accent which in all his wanderings he had never lost.

[001-160] It was a voice that could woo seductively and caressingly, or [[authority|command]] in

[001-161] such a way as to compel obedience. Indeed, the man's whole nature was in

[001-162] that voice of his. For the rest of him, he was tall and spare, swarthy

[001-163] of tint as a gipsy, with eyes that were startlingly blue in that dark

[001-164] face and under those level black brows. In their glance those eyes,

[001-165] flanking a high-bridged, intrepid nose, were of singular penetration

[001-166] and of a steady haughtiness that went well with his firm lips. Though

[001-167] dressed in black as became his [[duty|calling]], yet it was with an elegance

[001-168] derived from the love of clothes that is peculiar to the adventurer he

[001-169] had been, rather than to the staid medicus he now was. His coat was of

[001-170] fine camlet, and it was laced with silver; there were ruffles of Mechlin

[001-171] at his wrists and a Mechlin cravat encased his throat. His great black

[001-172] periwig was as sedulously curled as any at Whitehall.

[001-173] Seeing him thus, and perceiving his real nature, which was plain upon

[001-174] him, you might have been tempted to speculate how long such a man would

[001-175] be content to lie by in this little backwater of the world into which

[001-176] chance had swept him some six months ago; how long he would continue to

[001-177] pursue the trade for which he had qualified himself before he had begun

[001-178] to live. Difficult of belief though it may be when you know his history,

[001-179] previous and subsequent, yet it is possible that but for the trick

[001-180] that Fate was about to play him, he might have continued this peaceful

[001-181] existence, settling down completely to the life of a doctor in this

[001-182] Somersetshire haven. It is possible, but not probable.

[001-183] He was the son of an Irish medicus, by a Somersetshire lady in whose

[001-184] veins ran the rover blood of the Frobishers, which may account for a

[001-185] certain wildness that had early manifested itself in his disposition.

[001-186] This wildness had profoundly alarmed his father, who for an Irishman was

[001-187] of a singularly peace-loving nature. He had early resolved that the

[001-188] boy should follow his own honourable profession, and Peter Blood, being

[001-189] quick to learn and oddly greedy of knowledge, had satisfied his parent

[001-190] by receiving at the age of twenty the degree of baccalaureus medicinae

[001-191] at Trinity College, Dublin. His father survived that satisfaction by

[001-192] three months only. His mother had then been dead some years already.

[001-193] Thus Peter Blood came into an inheritance of some few hundred pounds,

[001-194] with which he had set out to see the world and give for a season a free

[001-195] rein to that restless spirit by which he was imbued. A set of curious

[001-196] chances led him to take service with the Dutch, then at war with France;

[001-197] and a predilection for the sea made him elect that this service should

[001-198] be upon that element. He had the advantage of a commission under the

[001-199] famous de Ruyter, and fought in the Mediterranean engagement in which

[001-200] that great Dutch admiral lost his life.
