# Colonel Kirke

## Overview

Colonel Percy Kirke is a historical figure who appears in the novel through his dragoons — the regiment known infamously as "Kirke's Lambs" — rather than in person. His soldiers, led by Captain Hobart, are responsible for the arrest of the wounded Lord Gildoy and the subsequent capture of Peter Blood in the aftermath of the Battle of Sedgemoor. Kirke's name recurs as a byword for the brutal repression that followed the Monmouth Rebellion.

## In the Novel

Colonel Kirke's presence is first felt when Captain Hobart arrives at the yeoman's cottage in search of fugitive rebels. Hobart announces himself with the authority of rank: "'I am Captain Hobart, of Colonel Kirke's dragoons. What rebels do you harbour?'" ([002-171]). The name carries an immediate weight — Kirke's dragoons were notorious for their ferocity in hunting down the remnants of Monmouth's defeated army.

When Peter Blood attempts to intervene on behalf of the wounded Lord Gildoy, Hobart dismisses him with a chilling indifference that reflects his colonel's methods: "If he's alive, he can hang as well as any of 'em. If he's dead, he's saved us the trouble. It'll serve for one of them as well as another. Colonel Kirke'll learn these damesers to bear false witness" ([002-189]). The threat invokes Kirke's name as the ultimate sanction — the colonel whose discipline would punish any leniency toward rebels.

Later, during Blood's trial at Taunton, the narrator describes the public mood in Somerset, where "the market-place by the bloodthirsty Colonel Kirke" had become a place of dread and summary justice ([003-205]). The epithet "bloodthirsty" places Kirke within the gallery of Stuart enforcers — alongside Judge Jeffreys — whose harshness in suppressing the rebellion earned them lasting infamy.

Though Kirke never appears directly in the novel, his role as the commander of the dragoons who hunt down the rebels is structurally important: his men set in motion the chain of events that leads to Blood's arrest, trial, transportation, and eventual transformation into a buccaneer. Kirke thus belongs to the novel's opening tragedy — the mechanism of Stuart repression that creates the conditions for Blood's extraordinary reversal of fortune.

## Relationships

- **Captain Hobart** — His subordinate, who carries out the arrests in Kirke's name
- **Lord Gildoy** — The wounded rebel whom Kirke's dragoons discover and arrest
- **Peter Blood** — Captured by Kirke's men while attempting to aid the wounded Gildoy
- **Judge Jeffreys** — Fellow instrument of Stuart justice, presiding over the trials that follow Kirke's round-ups

## Historical Basis

Colonel Percy Kirke (c. 1646–1691) was a real historical figure who commanded the Queen's Regiment (later the 2nd Foot) during the Monmouth Rebellion. His troops earned the nickname "Kirke's Lambs" — an ironic reference to their lamb-like paschal emblem on their flag, which belied their brutal reputation. Kirke was made Governor of Tangier and later served under William III after the Glorious Revolution. The novel's portrayal of his dragoons as ruthless instruments of Stuart repression is consistent with contemporary accounts of the Bloody Assizes and their aftermath.
