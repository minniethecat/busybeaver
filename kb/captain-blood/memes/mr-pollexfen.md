# Mr. Pollexfen

## Overview

Mr. Pollexfen serves as Judge-Advocate at the Taunton Assizes, where Peter Blood is tried for treason following the Monmouth Rebellion. Though bound by his official position to prosecute, Pollexfen is described as "a Whig at heart" — a man whose private political sympathies conflict with his professional duties under the notoriously brutal Judge Jeffreys. His brief but vivid appearance in the trial scenes illuminates the moral compromises of the English legal system under James II.

## In the Novel

Pollexfen first appears when Peter Blood, standing trial for his involvement in the Monmouth Rebellion, hesitates over the formal plea. "It was Mr. Pollexfen, the Judge-Advocate," who snaps: "Are you guilty or not guilty? You must take the words" ([003-324]). Blood's witty rejoinder — "Words, is it? Oh — not guilty" — sets the tone for a trial in which the prisoner's sharp tongue will contrast with the grim machinery of Stuart justice.

After the preliminary pleadings of Blood's co-defendants, "Mr. Pollexfen got to his feet. With great prolixity he stated the general case against the three men, and the particular case against Peter Blood, whose indictment was to be taken first" ([003-354]). His thoroughness as a prosecutor is evident, yet Sabatini is careful to note that Pollexfen held no personal animus toward the accused.

Later, Lord Jeffreys — in his characteristically brutal manner — attempts to hurry proceedings along: "I take it, Mr. Pollexfen, that the wicked treason of these three rogues being established — indeed, admitted by them — there is no more to be said" ([003-378]). But Blood himself interrupts, insisting on his right to a full defence. The dramatic confrontation between Blood and Jeffreys that follows leaves the court stunned — and reveals Pollexfen's true sentiments.

Though he occupies the position of Judge-Advocate, "Mr. Pollexfen — a Whig at heart despite the position of Judge-Advocate which he occupied — was overheard by one of the jurors to mutter in the ear of a brother counsel: 'On my soul, that swarthy rascal has given his lordship a scare. It's a pity he must hang. For a man who can frighten Jeffreys should go far'" ([004-041]). Sabatini adds a characteristic authorial note: "Mr. Pollexfen was at one and the same time right and wrong — a condition much more common than is generally supposed" ([004-047]). Pollexfen was right that Blood had frightened Jeffreys; he was wrong only in his assumption that the prisoner must inevitably hang.

## Relationships

- **Lord Jeffreys** — The Lord Chief Justice under whom Pollexfen serves; the two men represent opposing temperaments — Jeffreys the brutal instrument of Stuart power, Pollexfen the reluctant professional
- **Peter Blood** — The prisoner he prosecutes, whose defiance earns Pollexfen's private admiration
- **Captain Hobart** — The prosecution's key witness, whose testimony Pollexfen presents to the court
