[013-002] Cayona, which Nature seemed to have designed for the stronghold of those

[013-003] who had appropriated it.

[013-004] CHAPTER XIII. TORTUGA

[013-005] It is time fully to disclose the fact that the survival of the story of

[013-006] [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]'s exploits is due entirely to the industry of [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]],

[013-007] the Somersetshire shipmaster. In addition to his ability as a navigator,

[013-008] this amiable young man appears to have wielded an indefatigable pen, and

[013-009] to have been inspired to indulge its fluency by the affection he very

[013-010] obviously bore to Peter Blood.

[013-011] He kept the log of the forty-gun frigate [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]], on which he served as

[013-012] master, or, as we should say to-day, navigating officer, as no log

[013-013] that I have seen was ever kept. It runs into some twenty-odd volumes of

[013-014] assorted sizes, some of which are missing altogether and others of which

[013-015] are so sadly depleted of leaves as to be of little use. But if at times

[013-016] in the laborious perusal of them--they are preserved in the library of

[013-017] Mr. James Speke of Comerton--I have inveighed against these lacunae, at

[013-018] others I have been equally troubled by the excessive prolixity of what

[013-019] remains and the difficulty of disintegrating from the confused whole the

[013-020] really essential parts.

[013-021] I have a suspicion that Esquemeling--though how or where I can make no

[013-022] surmise--must have obtained access to these records, and that he plucked

[013-023] from them the brilliant feathers of several exploits to stick them into

[013-024] the tail of his own hero, Captain Morgan. But that is by the way. I

[013-025] mention it chiefly as a warning, for when presently I come to relate the

[013-026] affair of Maracaybo, those of you who have read Esquemeling may be in

[013-027] danger of supposing that Henry Morgan really performed those things

[013-028] which here are veraciously attributed to Peter Blood. I think, however,

[013-029] that when you come to weigh the motives actuating both Blood and the

[013-030] Spanish Admiral, in that affair, and when you consider how integrally

[013-031] the event is a part of Blood's history--whilst merely a detached

[013-032] incident in Morgan's--you will reach my own conclusion as to which is

[013-033] the real plagiarist.

[013-034] The first of these logs of Pitt's is taken up almost entirely with a

[013-035] retrospective narrative of the events up to the time of Blood's first

[013-036] coming to [[tortuga|Tortuga]]. This and the Tannatt Collection of State Trials are

[013-037] the chief--though not the only--sources of my history so far.

[013-038] Pitt lays great stress upon the fact that it was the circumstances upon

[013-039] which I have dwelt, and these alone, that drove Peter Blood to seek

[013-040] an anchorage at Tortuga. He insists at considerable length, and with a

[013-041] vehemence which in itself makes it plain that an opposite opinion was

[013-042] held in some quarters, that it was no part of the design of Blood or of

[013-043] any of his companions in misfortune to join hands with the buccaneers

[013-044] who, under a semi-official French protection, made of Tortuga a lair

[013-045] whence they could sally out to drive their merciless piratical trade

[013-046] chiefly at the expense of Spain.

[013-047] It was, Pitt tells us, Blood's original intention to make his way to

[013-048] France or Holland. But in the long weeks of waiting for a ship to convey

[013-049] him to one or the other of these countries, his resources dwindled and

[013-050] finally vanished. Also, his chronicler thinks that he detected signs of

[013-051] some secret trouble in his friend, and he attributes to this the abuses

[013-052] of the potent West Indian spirit of which Blood became guilty in those

[013-053] days of inaction, thereby sinking to the level of the wild adventurers

[013-054] with whom ashore he associated.

[013-055] I do not think that Pitt is guilty in this merely of special pleading,

[013-056] that he is putting forward excuses for his hero. I think that in those

[013-057] days there was a good deal to oppress Peter Blood. There was the thought

[013-058] of Arabella Bishop--and that this thought loomed large in his mind we

[013-059] are not permitted to doubt. He was maddened by the tormenting lure of

[013-060] the unattainable. He desired Arabella, yet knew her beyond his reach

[013-061] irrevocably and for all time. Also, whilst he may have desired to go to

[013-062] France or Holland, he had no clear purpose to accomplish when he reached

[013-063] one or the other of these countries. He was, when all is said, an

[013-064] escaped slave, an outlaw in his own land and a homeless outcast in any

[013-065] other. There remained the sea, which is free to all, and particularly

[013-066] alluring to those who feel themselves at war with humanity. And so,

[013-067] considering the adventurous spirit that once already had sent him

[013-068] a-roving for the sheer love of it, considering that this spirit was

[013-069] heightened now by a recklessness begotten of his outlawry, that his

[013-070] training and skill in militant seamanship clamorously supported the

[013-071] temptations that were put before him, can you wonder, or dare you blame

[013-072] him, that in the end he succumbed? And remember that these temptations

[013-073] proceeded not only from adventurous [[piracy|buccaneering]] acquaintances in the

[013-074] taverns of that evil haven of Tortuga, but even from M. d'Ogeron, the

[013-075] governor of the island, who levied as his harbour dues a percentage of

[013-076] one tenth of all spoils brought into the bay, and who profited further

[013-077] by commissions upon money which he was desired to convert into bills of

[013-078] exchange upon France.

[013-079] A trade that might have worn a repellent aspect when urged by greasy,

[013-080] half-drunken adventurers, boucan-hunters, lumbermen, beach-combers,

[013-081] English, French, and Dutch, became a dignified, almost official form of

[013-082] privateering when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who

[013-083] in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France

[013-084] herself.

[013-085] Moreover, to a man--not excluding Jeremy Pitt himself, in whose blood

[013-086] the call of the sea was insistent and imperative--those who had escaped

[013-087] with Peter Blood from the [[barbados|Barbados]] plantations, and who, consequently,

[013-088] like himself, knew not whither to turn, were all resolved upon joining

[013-089] the great Brotherhood of the Coast, as those rovers called themselves.

[013-090] And they united theirs to the other voices that were persuading Blood,

[013-091] demanding that he should continue now in the leadership which he had

[013-092] enjoyed since they had left Barbados, and swearing to follow him loyally

[013-093] whithersoever he should lead them.

[013-094] And so, to condense all that Jeremy has recorded in the matter, Blood

[013-095] ended by yielding to external and internal pressure, abandoned himself

[013-096] to the stream of Destiny. “Fata viam invenerunt,” is his own expression

[013-097] of it.

[013-098] If he resisted so long, it was, I think, the thought of Arabella Bishop

[013-099] that restrained him. That they should be destined never to meet again

[013-100] did not weigh at first, or, indeed, ever. He conceived the scorn with

[013-101] which she would come to hear of his having turned pirate, and the scorn,

[013-102] though as yet no more than imagined, hurt him as if it were already a

[013-103] reality. And even when he conquered this, still the thought of her was

[013-104] ever present. He compromised with the conscience that her memory kept so

[013-105] disconcertingly active. He vowed that the thought of her should continue

[013-106] ever before him to help him keep his hands as clean as a man might in

[013-107] this desperate trade upon which he was embarking. And so, although he

[013-108] might entertain no delusive hope of ever winning her for his own, of

[013-109] ever even seeing her again, yet the memory of her was to abide in his

[013-110] soul as a bitter-sweet, purifying influence. The love that is never to

[013-111] be realized will often remain a man's guiding ideal. The resolve

[013-112] being taken, he went actively to work. Ogeron, most accommodating of

[013-113] governors, advanced him money for the proper equipment of his ship the

[013-114] Cinco Llagas, which he renamed [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]]. This after some little

[013-115] hesitation, fearful of thus setting his heart upon his sleeve. But his

[013-116] Barbados friends accounted it merely an expression of the ever-ready

[013-117] irony in which their leader dealt.

[013-118] To the score of followers he already possessed, he added threescore

[013-119] more, picking his men with caution and discrimination--and he was an

[013-120] exceptional judge of men--from amongst the adventurers of Tortuga. With

[013-121] them all he entered into the articles usual among the Brethren of the

[013-122] Coast under which each man was to be paid by a share in the prizes

[013-123] captured. In other respects, however, the articles were different.

[013-124] Aboard the Arabella there was to be none of the ruffianly indiscipline

[013-125] that normally prevailed in buccaneering vessels. Those who shipped with

[013-126] him undertook obedience and submission in all things to himself and

[013-127] to the officers appointed by election. Any to whom this clause in the

[013-128] articles was distasteful might follow some other leader.

[013-129] Towards the end of December, when the hurricane season had blown itself

[013-130] out, he put to sea in his well-found, well-manned ship, and before he

[013-131] returned in the following May from a protracted and adventurous cruise,

[013-132] the fame of Captain Peter Blood had run like ripples before the breeze

[013-133] across the face of the Caribbean Sea. There was a fight in the Windward

[013-134] Passage at the outset with a Spanish galleon, which had resulted in the

[013-135] gutting and finally the sinking of the Spaniard. There was a daring raid

[013-136] effected by means of several appropriated piraguas upon a Spanish pearl

[013-137] fleet in the Rio de la Hacha, from which they had taken a particularly

[013-138] rich haul of pearls. There was an overland expedition to the goldfields

[013-139] of Santa Maria, on the Main, the full tale of which is hardly credible,

[013-140] and there were lesser adventures through all of which the crew of the

[013-141] Arabella came with credit and profit if not entirely unscathed.

[013-142] And so it happened that before the Arabella came homing to Tortuga in

[013-143] the following May to refit and repair--for she was not without scars, as

[013-144] you conceive--the fame of her and of Peter Blood her captain had swept

[013-145] from the Bahamas to the Windward Isles, from New Providence to Trinidad.

[013-146] An echo of it had reached Europe, and at the Court of St. James's angry

[013-147] representations were made by the Ambassador of Spain, to whom it was

[013-148] answered that it must not be supposed that this Captain Blood held any

[013-149] commission from the King of England; that he was, in fact, a proscribed

[013-150] rebel, an escaped slave, and that any measures against him by His

[013-151] Catholic Majesty would receive the cordial approbation of King James II.

[013-152] [[don-miguel|Don Miguel de Espinosa]], the Admiral of Spain in the West Indies, and his

[013-153] nephew Don Esteban who sailed with him, did not lack the will to bring

[013-154] the adventurer to the yardarm. With them this business of capturing

[013-155] Blood, which was now an international affair, was also a family matter.

[013-156] Spain, through the mouth of Don Miguel, did not spare her threats.

[013-157] The report of them reached Tortuga, and with it the assurance that Don

[013-158] Miguel had behind him not only the authority of his own nation, but that

[013-159] of the English King as well.

[013-160] It was a brutum fulmen that inspired no terrors in Captain Blood. Nor

[013-161] was he likely, on account of it, to allow himself to run to rust in the

[013-162] security of Tortuga. For what he had suffered at the hands of Man he had

[013-163] chosen to make Spain the scapegoat. Thus he accounted that he served a

[013-164] twofold purpose: he took compensation and at the same time served, not

[013-165] indeed the Stuart King, whom he despised, but England and, for that

[013-166] matter, all the rest of civilized mankind which cruel, treacherous,

[013-167] greedy, bigoted Castile sought to exclude from intercourse with the New

[013-168] World.

[013-169] One day as he sat with [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]] and [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]] over a pipe and

[013-170] a bottle of rum in the stifling reek of tar and stale tobacco of a

[013-171] waterside tavern, he was accosted by a splendid ruffian in a gold-laced

[013-172] coat of dark-blue satin with a crimson sash, a foot wide, about the

[013-173] waist.

[013-174] “C'est vous qu'on appelle Le Sang?” the fellow hailed him.
