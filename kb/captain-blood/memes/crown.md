# Crown

## Overview

The Crown — the personification of royal authority — is the most [[authority|power]]ful force in the world of *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. It is the Crown that prosecutes the Monmouth rebels, the Crown whose justice condemns Blood to transportation, and the Crown whose pardon he ultimately receives. The novel traces Blood's relationship with the Crown from adversarial to cooperative: the same institution that nearly hanged him at the [[bloody-assizes|Bloody Assizes]] appoints him [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. This arc reflects the political reality of the late 17th century, in which the Glorious Revolution of 1688 replaced James II with William III, transforming rebels against James into loyal servants of William's Crown.

## Historical Background

The English Crown in the 1680s underwent its most dramatic constitutional crisis of the century. James II (r. 1685–1688) inherited the throne at the time of the [[monmouth-rebellion|Monmouth Rebellion]], prosecuted the rebels with extreme severity, and was himself deposed by the Glorious Revolution. His successor, William III (r. 1689–1702), pursued a policy of reconciliation with former rebels and political opponents. The Crown's authority in the colonies was exercised through governors, naval officers, and customs officials, but distance from London meant that royal authority was often more theoretical than practical. The distinction between the Crown as an institution (permanent, legitimate) and the king as a person (fallible, replaceable) was central to the political theory of the period and underpins the novel's treatment of allegiance.

## In the Novel

### The Crown as Adversary

Blood's trial turns on the question of whether the accused had "a better title to the crown" (003-554) — a reference to the Monmouth claim that directly challenges James II's legitimacy. The Crown that prosecutes Blood is James II's Crown, an institution whose justice has been corrupted by the king's determination to crush [[treason|rebellion]] at any cost. The trial scenes demonstrate how the Crown's authority, when wielded by a vindictive monarch, can pervert the very law it is meant to uphold.

### The Crown as Redeemer

Blood's acceptance of "the Crown" (025-003) under William III represents not merely a change in allegiance but a political judgment: he serves William's Crown because William's cause is just, just as he opposed James's Crown because James's justice was corrupt. The "crowning argument" (028-085) in Blood's diplomatic dealings with the French marks the rhetorical climax of this transformation — Blood now speaks with the authority of a Crown servant rather than the defiance of a Crown rebel.

### The Crown and the Colonies

The Crown's relationship with its Caribbean colonies is marked by distance and delegation. [[governor|Governor]]s like [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] exercise Crown authority with little oversight, allowing personal vendettas to masquerade as royal policy. Blood's appointment as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] represents the Crown's belated recognition that [[colonial|colonial administration]] requires competence, not just loyalty — and that the best governor of a buccaneer-infested island may be a reformed buccaneer himself.
