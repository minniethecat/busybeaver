## Overview

Mrs. Barlow is [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s housekeeper at his residence in Bridgewater. A minor but warmly drawn domestic figure, she appears in the novel's opening chapters tending to Blood's household and reacting with characteristic fussiness to the disruption brought by the Monmouth Rebellion.

## In the Novel

Mrs. Barlow is introduced in Chapter I as she prepares Blood's supper: "the table on which Mrs. Barlow, his housekeeper, was in the very act of spreading supper" ([001-154]). Her presence establishes the quiet domesticity of Blood's life before the rebellion overturns it.

In Chapter II, when [[jeremy-pitt|Jeremy Pitt]] arrives in the early hours with news of the rebellion's disastrous outcome at Sedgemoor, Mrs. Barlow appears "new-risen and unsightly, in a state of panic" on the landing ([002-044]). Blood must "quiet her cluckings with a word of reassurance" before attending to the urgent news ([002-045]).

As Blood prepares to ride to [[lord-gildoy|Lord Gildoy]]'s aid at Oglethorpe's Farm, he gives "Mrs. Barlow instructions for the day, which included the matter of a dinner he was not destined to eat" ([002-084]). When Blood departs, Mrs. Barlow is seen "clucking after him like a disgruntled fowl" ([002-086]) — a final domestic image before the narrative plunges into the violent events of arrest and trial.

## Relationships

- **[[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]** — Mrs. Barlow's employer. She manages his household and appears genuinely concerned for his welfare, her fussing over him suggesting a loyal, motherly attachment.

## Quotations

> "He quieted her cluckings with a word of reassurance, and went himself to open." — Blood calming Mrs. Barlow when Pitt arrives with news ([002-045])

> "When at last he went forth again, Mrs. Barlow clucking after him like a disgruntled fowl." — Blood's departure for Oglethorpe's Farm ([002-086])
