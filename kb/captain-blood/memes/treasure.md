# Treasure

## Overview

Treasure is the economic lifeblood of the [[piracy|buccaneering]] world depicted in *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*. It takes multiple forms: the treasure chests taken from captured Spanish ships (009-216), the "plate" (silver) carried by the Spanish treasure fleet (017-031), and the gold stored in colonial treasure-houses (028-134). For [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] and his crew, treasure is both the means of survival and the currency of political legitimacy — captured wealth funds their operations, rewards their followers, and ultimately buys their way back into the favor of European crowns. The novel traces the flow of treasure from [[galleon|Spanish galleon]]s to buccaneer holds to French and English coffers, mapping the economics of 17th-century Caribbean conflict.

## Historical Background

The Spanish Empire's American colonies produced enormous quantities of silver and gold, transported to Europe by the annual treasure fleet (*flota de Indias*). This fleet was the most coveted prize in the Atlantic world. English, French, and Dutch [[privateering|privateer]]s and pirates all sought to intercept Spanish treasure ships, and the Caribbean became a battleground where fortunes could be won or lost in a single engagement. [[buccaneer|Buccaneer]] settlements like [[tortuga|Tortuga]] functioned as clearinghouses for captured treasure, with goods fenced to colonial merchants who asked few questions. The distinction between "treasure" (legitimately captured under [[privateer|letters of marque]]) and "plunder" (taken by outright pirates) was often a matter of paperwork rather than conduct.

## In the Novel

### Early Captures

Blood's rise begins with the capture of Spanish treasure ships. The *[[cinco-llagas|Cinco Llagas]]* yields substantial treasure, distributed among the crew to maintain loyalty and fund further operations. After one successful raid, the men bring "four great treasure-chests, containing each" a fortune in Spanish gold and silver (009-216). Blood handles the distribution "effectively" (009-223), ensuring his [[authority|command]] is strengthened rather than undermined by the sudden wealth.

### The Spanish Treasure Fleet

The greatest prize in the novel is the Spanish treasure fleet that Blood's [[fleet|squadron]] intercepts. The fleet's "treasure-ship" carries plate "to the value of" an enormous sum (017-031–017-032). The capture of this fleet transforms Blood from a fugitive buccaneer into a figure of European significance. The treasure is "safely stowed" aboard his ships (017-353), providing the financial foundation for his subsequent operations.

### Cartagena and French Gold

The [[cartagena|Cartagena]] expedition involves the most ambitious treasure operation in the novel. The Spanish settlement's "treasure-house" contains wealth that the [[buccaneer|buccaneers]] aim to seize before it can be "removed inland" (027-030). The operation is compromised when the French [[authority|command]]er, [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]], absconds "with the treasure, taking with him the troops" (028-245), demonstrating that treasure can corrupt alliances as easily as it enriches purses. Blood's response — treating the betrayal as an opportunity rather than a defeat — reveals his strategic genius.

### Treasure as Political Currency

Captured treasure serves Blood's larger purpose. French gold is "treasured up for him in" anticipation of his service to King Louis (018-377), while Spanish treasure funds his independent operations. By the novel's end, Blood carries "in his hold the treasure" (029-158) that will secure his position as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] — wealth converted not into personal luxury but into political legitimacy.
