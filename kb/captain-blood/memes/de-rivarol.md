# M. de Rivarol

## Overview

M. le Baron (later Marquis) de Rivarol is the French naval commander under whom Blood serves during his period with the French fleet. Rivarol embodies everything Blood despises about aristocratic authority: he claims credit for others' victories, breaks his agreements, and values appearance over substance. The novel's most ironic chapter title — "The Honour of M. de Rivarol" (Chapter 28) — signals that Rivarol has none. His role in the narrative is to demonstrate that titled authority is no guarantee of moral worth, and that a physician-turned-pirate may possess more honour than a marquis.

## In the Novel

### The Aristocratic Commander

Blood is summoned to serve under "M. de Rivarol's flag" ((025-326)) as part of his commission from the French crown. From their first meeting, Rivarol's aristocratic hauteur is on display: "mightier grew the stare of M. de Rivarol" ((026-032)) as he surveys Blood — the social gulf between marquis and buccaneer is absolute in his eyes. Rivarol's discomfort at sharing a table with pirates is palpable: "M. de Rivarol changed colour. He quivered visibly" ((026-041)), revealing a man whose sense of status is easily threatened.

### The Honour Thief

Rivarol's defining sin is claiming credit for Blood's achievements. When Blood proposes a daring plan, Rivarol "smote the table in" indignation ((026-073)) — not at the strategic audacity, but at the suggestion that a pirate might lead where a marquis should command. Throughout the campaign, Rivarol "took over the responsibilities" ((026-147)) for successes while attributing failures to his subordinates. His theft of honour — "from him by the haughty M. de Rivarol" ((026-138)) — is, in the novel's moral universe, a graver crime than piracy.

### The Bully and the Coward

Rivarol's leadership style is defined by bullying: "M. de Rivarol bullied and browbeat him into" compliance ((026-144)), substituting force of rank for force of character. When Wolverstone offends his dignity, "by M. de Rivarol's orders, Wolverstone was under" arrest within hours ((026-158)). Yet beneath the bluster is cowardice — Rivarol "glared at them, checkmated" ((026-110)) when outmanoeuvred, his authority revealed as the empty performance it always was.

### The Confrontation with Blood

The novel's satisfaction comes when Blood confronts Rivarol directly. When Rivarol commands Blood's arrest — "'Put him under arrest,' said M. de Rivarol" ((026-179)) — Blood's response upends the power dynamic entirely. Blood points out that Rivarol has arrested "the wrong man, M. de Rivarol. Instead of the French officer" ((026-188)) — the marquis has mistaken his position, and Blood is not a man to be bullied. "For a moment M. de Rivarol did not recognize him" ((026-170)) — the aristocrat literally cannot see the man before him, blinded by his own prejudices.

## Character Analysis

Rivarol is the novel's most pointed satire of aristocratic pretension. Unlike Don Diego, who is a genuinely capable adversary, or Levasseur, who is a genuine threat, Rivarol is merely a fraud. His authority derives entirely from his title — he possesses neither the military competence to lead nor the moral character to inspire. He exists in the novel to prove Blood's central thesis: that a man's worth is measured by his actions, not his birth.

The escalating series of confrontations between Blood and Rivarol — from the first dinner to the final arrest — trace the collapse of aristocratic authority under the weight of its own pretensions. Rivarol begins as the haughty marquis who cannot bear to dine with pirates; he ends as the exposed fraud whose orders carry no weight.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — His subordinate and eventual nemesis; Blood's honour exposes Rivarol's complete lack of it
- **Wolverstone** — Crew member arrested on Rivarol's orders, whose punishment galvanizes Blood's resistance ((026-158))
