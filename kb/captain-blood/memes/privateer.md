# Privateer

## Overview

The [[privateering|privateer]] — a private vessel authorized by a government to attack enemy shipping — occupies the contested boundary between lawful warfare and criminal predation. In the world of *[[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]*, this distinction is both legally precise and politically fluid. A [[privateering|privateer]] carries a [[letters-of-marque|commission]], or "[[letters-of-marque|letter of marque]]," from a sovereign [[authority|power]]; a pirate carries none. Yet the same act — seizing Spanish[[galleon| treasure galleon]]s in Caribbean waters — could be termed [[piracy|privateering]] by the English [[crown|Crown]] and piracy by the Spanish [[admiral|Admiral]]ty. The novel traces [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]]'s progression across this boundary: from escaped slave and proscribed rebel with no [[letters-of-marque|commission]] at all ((013-149)–(013-150)), to buccaneer operating in a legal gray zone under the French West India Company ((013-082)), to [[letters-of-marque|commission]]ed officer of King James ((022-046)–(022-047)), to [[authority|command]]er under King Louis of France ((025-004)), and finally to [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] under King William III ((031-084)). [[privateering|Privateering]] is thus both the legal mechanism of Blood's rehabilitation and the central moral question of the novel — whether violence at sea can be legitimated by the flag under which it is conducted.

## Historical Background

The 17th-century Caribbean was the golden age of [[piracy|privateering]]. The Spanish Main — the coastal regions of Spain's American empire — was contested by English, French, and Dutch mariners operating under letters of marque issued by their respective crowns. Under the prevailing legal framework, a [[privateering|privateer]] was a private shipowner who received a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] from a sovereign to capture enemy vessels, with the sovereign taking a share of the prize money. This system allowed nations to project naval [[authority|power]] without the expense of maintaining a standing fleet.

The distinction between [[privateering|privateer]] and pirate was theoretically clear but practically porous. Spain considered all foreign vessels in "their" American waters to be pirates, regardless of [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s. England and France, meanwhile, issued letters of marque liberally as a cheap form of naval warfare against Spain. When peace treaties were signed, many [[privateering|privateer]]s simply continued their operations without legal sanction, becoming pirates by default. The novel's setting in the 1680s — during the transition from Anglo-Spanish hostility to the War of the Grand Alliance — captures this moment of legal flux.

The most famous model for Blood's career was Sir Henry Morgan (c. 1635–1688), the Welsh [[privateering|privateer]] who sacked Panama, was briefly arrested and sent to England, and ultimately returned to the Caribbean as Lieutenant [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]]. Blood explicitly invokes Morgan's example when he tells his crew: "I am ready to take the way of Morgan: to accept the [[letters-of-marque|King's commission]] and shelter us all behind it" ((021-304)).

## In the Novel

### The Legal Void: Blood the Proscribed Rebel

When [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] first takes to the sea, he occupies the most precarious legal position possible. The English [[crown|Crown]] has formally disclaimed him. When the Spanish Ambassador complains of Blood's depredations, "it was answered that it must not be supposed that this [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]] held any [[letters-of-marque|commission]] from the King of England; that he was, in fact, a proscribed rebel, an escaped slave" ((013-149)–(013-150)). The passage continues with the grim assurance that "any measures against him by His Catholic Majesty would receive the cordial approbation of King James II" ((013-151)). Blood is thus a man without a flag — fair game for every navy in the Caribbean, protected by none.

The irony is sharp: Blood was condemned as a traitor by the very king whose shipping he now threatens, yet that same king cannot even claim the right to prosecute him — he can only encourage Spain to do the dirty work. Blood's situation illustrates the paradox of the [[piracy|privateering]] system: without a [[letters-of-marque|commission]], a man of honour is indistinguishable from a common pirate.

### The French West India Company: A Quasi-Legal Cover

Blood's operations from [[tortuga|Tortuga]] provide the first veneer of legitimacy. M. d'Ogeron, the French governor of the island, levies harbour dues of one-tenth of all spoils and profits from [[letters-of-marque|commission]]s on financial transactions ((013-077)). Under d'Ogeron's patronage, "a trade that might have worn a repellent aspect when urged by greasy, half-drunken adventurers, boucan-hunters, lumbermen, beach-combers, English, French, and Dutch, became a dignified, almost official form of [[piracy|privateering]] when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France herself" ((013-082)).

This is [[piracy|privateering]] in all but name. The French West India Company provides a framework of quasi-governmental authority, but Blood holds no formal [[letters-of-marque|letter of marque]]. His crew, however, includes men like Nathaniel [[hagthorpe|Hagthorpe]], "whose sometime [[letters-of-marque|commission]] in the King's Navy gave him the best title to this office" ((009-099)), and the French [[privateering|privateer]] [[authority|command]]er Yberville, "who, though still young, had already won fame as a [[privateering|privateer]] [[authority|command]]er before the loss of his own ship had driven him to take service under Blood" ((016-129)). These men bring with them the residue of legitimate naval experience, lending Blood's enterprise a professional character that distinguishes it from common piracy.

### The Spanish Privateer: The Enemy's Mirror

The novel's first explicit reference to a [[privateering|privateer]] is Spanish. The ship that enters Carlisle Bay "under her false colours" and proceeds to sack Bridgetown is "a Spanish [[privateering|privateer]], coming to pay off some of the heavy debt piled up by the predaceous [[brethren-of-the-coast|Brethren of the Coast]]" ((008-086)). This is [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]'s first encounter with the [[piracy|privateering]] system, and it is instructive: the Spanish ship, sailing under a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] from its own [[crown|Crown]], is legally a [[privateering|privateer]] — yet to the English colonists, its actions are indistinguishable from piracy. The episode establishes the novel's recurring theme that the label "[[privateering|privateer]]" or "pirate" depends entirely on one's national perspective.

Later, [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]]'s journey to the Caribbean is disrupted when "the Royal Mary fell a victim to a Spanish [[privateering|privateer]]" ((022-070)), and he is rescued by Blood himself. The irony is layered: the King's envoy, travelling to offer Blood a [[letters-of-marque|commission]], is saved from a Spanish [[privateering|privateer]] by the very pirate he seeks to legitimize.

### The Commission: Blood Takes the Way of Morgan

The pivotal moment in Blood's legal transformation comes in Chapter 21, "The Service of King James." Facing a superior Spanish force and likely destruction, Blood makes his decision:

> "I am ready to take the way of Morgan: to accept the King's commission and shelter us all behind it." ((021-304)–(021-305))

The crew's reaction is telling: "The main body of them welcomed the announcement as only men who have been preparing to die can welcome a new lease of life" ((021-307)). The [[letters-of-marque|commission]] is not primarily a moral choice but a survival strategy — a piece of paper that transforms them from hunted outlaws into agents of [[crown|the Crown]].

[[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian Wade]] presents the [[letters-of-marque|commission]]: "Blood holds a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] in the King's service under the seal of my Lord Sunderland, His Majesty's Secretary of State" ((022-046)–(022-047)). The legal effect is immediate: when [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]] attempts to arrest Blood, he is blocked by the [[letters-of-marque|commission]]'s authority. "Will Bishop respect the [[letters-of-marque|commission]] when you hold it?" Ogle asks, and [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]] answers: "It will go very hard with him if he attempts to flout the [[crown|King's authority]]" ((021-309)–(021-310)).

Yet Blood's personal relationship with the [[letters-of-marque|commission]] is ambivalent. He tells [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]] that he accepted it "to redeem me in the eyes of [[arabella-bishop|Miss Bishop]]" ((023-551)) — the woman whose scorn for pirates drove him to seek legitimacy. The [[letters-of-marque|commission]] is a means, not an end.

### The Service of King Louis: Privateering Under the French Flag

When James II is deposed and Blood's English [[letters-of-marque|commission]] becomes worthless, he takes service under Louis XIV of France. Chapter 25, "The Service of King Louis," represents the nadir of Blood's [[piracy|privateering]] career. The French, as Blood's associate puts it, are "right in desiring piracy to continue" ((025-337)), and the line between [[privateering|privateer]] and pirate blurs almost to invisibility.

Blood's service to France culminates in the [[cartagena|Cartagena]] expedition, where he fights alongside the treacherous [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]]. The French [[letters-of-marque|commission]] proves to be a trap: [[de-rivarol|Rivarol]] cheats Blood of his share of the spoils, and Blood must seize what he is owed by force. The episode demonstrates that a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] is only as good as the honour of the [[authority|power]] that grants it — and French honour, in Blood's experience, is worthless.

### The Service of King William: Full Rehabilitation

The novel's resolution arrives with the Glorious Revolution and the arrival of [[lord-willoughby|Lord Willoughby]], King William III's [[governor|Governor]]-General of the West Indies ((029-045)). [[lord-willoughby|Willoughby]] offers Blood the ultimate prize: not just a [[letters-of-marque|commission]] but the governorship of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] itself. "I shall know how to earn His Majesty's approbation. You may depend upon my loyal service," Blood replies ((031-081)–(031-082)).

"Thus it was settled. Blood's [[letters-of-marque|commission]] was made out and sealed in the presence of [[major-mallard|Mallard]], the Commandant, and the other officers of the garrison, who looked on in round-eyed astonishment, but kept their thoughts to themselves" ((031-084)). The man who began the novel as a condemned traitor, who was denounced by England as a proscribed rebel, ends it as [[crown|the Crown]]'s supreme representative in the Caribbean. [[privateering|Privateering]] — the state-sanctioned violence that Blood practiced in the gray zone between legality and outlawry — has served as the bridge to full legitimacy.

### Levasseur: The Pirate in Contrast

The French captain [[levasseur|Levasseur]] serves as Blood's dark mirror throughout the [[piracy|privateering]] portion of the novel. He "[[authority|command]]ed a [[privateering|privateer]] of twenty guns that had dropped anchor in the bay a week ago" ((014-014)), and his [[honour|reputation]] as a buccaneer stands high among [[brethren-of-the-coast|the Brethren]] of the Coast. But [[levasseur|Levasseur]] has no scruples: when he proposes attacking a Dutch ship, Cahusac warns him that Blood "has his own views of [[piracy|privateering]]" ((014-154)) and "will sink us before he'll suffer interference with the Dutch." Where [[levasseur|Levasseur]] sees all ships as legitimate prey, Blood maintains distinctions — Dutch ships are off limits, Spanish ships are the enemy, and torture of prisoners is prohibited. Blood's "views of [[piracy|privateering]]" are a code of honour that [[levasseur|Levasseur]] cannot comprehend.

### Yberville: The Career Privateer

The young French [[authority|command]]er Yberville provides a counterexample of the successful [[privateering|privateer]]. He "had already won fame as a [[privateering|privateer]] [[authority|command]]er before the loss of his own ship had driven him to take service under Blood" ((016-129)). Unlike [[levasseur|Levasseur]], Yberville is a professional — skilled, disciplined, and loyal. His presence in Blood's fleet demonstrates that [[piracy|privateering]] can attract men of genuine quality, not merely the desperate and the criminal. Yberville's career trajectory — from independent [[authority|command]]er to subordinate officer — mirrors the broader arc of Caribbean [[piracy|privateering]] as it gave way to more organized naval operations.
