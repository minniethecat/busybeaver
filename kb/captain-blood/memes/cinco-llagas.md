# Cinco Llagas

## Overview

The *Cinco Llagas* (Spanish: "Five Wounds") is a Spanish warship of at least twenty guns, fitted out at San Juan de Porto Rico for a punitive expedition against Barbados ([008-100]). Originally the flagship of Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez, the vessel is captured by Peter Blood and his fellow rebels-convict in a daring night assault, becoming the platform from which Blood launches his career as the most successful buccaneer in the Caribbean. After sailing the *Cinco Llagas* to the pirate haven of Tortuga, Blood rechristens her the *Arabella* in honour of Arabella Bishop ([013-114]). The ship that began the novel as an instrument of Spanish vengeance ends it as the most feared vessel under English colours.

## In the Novel

### The Fitting-Out at San Juan

The *Cinco Llagas* first appears in the narrative as the vessel chosen by Don Esteban de Espinosa for a punitive raid against Barbados. Acting without the restraint of his brother the Admiral, Don Esteban:

> ...fitted out the Cinco Llagas at San Juan de Porto Rico. He chose for his objective the island of Barbados...
> ([008-100])

The ship arrives off Carlisle Bay and announces her hostile intent by saluting the fort with "a broadside of twenty guns" ([008-107]) — the first indication of the vessel's substantial firepower. The four watchers on the headland behold "the great ship creep forward under the rising cloud of smoke" ([008-110]) as she delivers a second broadside against the unready fortifications. The assault succeeds, and Barbados falls to the Spanish raiders.

### The Capture

Blood and his fellow slaves, having resolved to seize their freedom, strike on the very night of the Spanish victory. With the main body of Spaniards feasting ashore, not more than ten men remain on guard aboard the *Cinco Llagas* ([009-116]) — a fatal overconfidence. Under cover of darkness, Blood leads a boarding party that silently scales the ship's quarter gallery ladder, dispatches the sentries, and overpowers the celebrating gun crew.

The capture is confirmed at dawn, when the bewildered islanders ashore see:

> ...the flag of Spain come down from the mainmast of the Cinco Llagas, and the flag of England soar to its empty place.
> ([009-287])

The ship's guns, now under the command of the reformed gunner Ogle, proceed to destroy the Spanish boats attempting to retake the vessel. The mystery of the ship's sudden change of allegiance is probed, and it becomes clear "that the Cinco Llagas was now in friendly hands" ([009-308]).

When Colonel Bishop comes aboard to investigate, Blood greets him in full Spanish finery:

> "Be welcome aboard the Cinco Llagas, Colonel, darling," a voice vaguely familiar addressed the planter. "We've made the best of the Spaniards' wardrobe in honour of this visit..."
> ([009-335])

Blood identifies himself plainly: "Captain Blood, if you please, of this ship the Cinco Llagas, taken as a prize of war from Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez, who is my prisoner aboard" ([010-051]). The former slave has not only seized a ship but assumed command with all the authority of a naval captain.

### The Voyage and the Encarnacion Deception

Blood sets sail with the *Cinco Llagas* through the Caribbean, but Don Diego, still a prisoner in his own great cabin, deliberately misleads the course. The ship ploughs on for days with no landfall ([011-167]), and instead of reaching Curacao, finds herself closing with the Spanish admiral Don Miguel de Espinosa's flagship *Encarnacion*. The situation is desperate: "the Cinco Llagas will go belong to Spain" ([011-356]) if discovered.

Blood conceives a daring deception. Understanding that "just as your father recognized his brother's flagship, so will his brother have recognized the Cinco Llagas" ([012-087]), he sends Don Esteban aboard the *Encarnacion* to assure the Admiral "that all is well with the Cinco Llagas, that she is indeed still a ship of Spain" ([012-106]). Gold treasure chests, sealed documents with the Espinosa arms — all "manufactured in the cabin of the Cinco Llagas" ([012-164]) — complete the ruse. The *Encarnacion* dips her flag and fires a salute as the *Cinco Llagas* goes free.

### Cayona and the Renaming

Two days after the encounter, the *Cinco Llagas* sails into "the rock-bound bay of Cayona" ([013-001]), the pirate stronghold on Tortuga. Here Blood secures funding from Governor Ogeron to properly equip his vessel. It is at Tortuga that Blood makes the decision that transforms the ship's identity:

> ...the proper equipment of his ship the Cinco Llagas, which he renamed the Arabella. This after some little hesitation, fearful of thus setting his heart upon his sleeve.
> ([013-114])

The name Arabella honours Arabella Bishop, the woman Blood loves — though he tells his men it is merely an ironic jest. With this renaming, the *Cinco Llagas* ceases to exist as a Spanish warship and begins its legendary second life as Blood's flagship.

### The Ship's Appearance

The novel provides a detailed description when the vessel — now rechristened — reappears three years later in the final chapters. Miss Bishop, now a prisoner aboard a Spanish ship, sees it approaching:

> ...the big red ship that had once been the Cinco Llagas out of Cadiz. The vessel was bearing down upon them, her mountains of snowy canvas bellying forward, the long pennon with the cross of St. George fluttering from her main truck in the morning breeze, the gilded portholes in her red hull and the gilded beak-head aflash in the morning sun.
> ([019-107])–([019-110])

Miss Bishop fails to recognize "this for that same Cinco Llagas which she had seen once before — on a tragic day in Barbados three years ago" ([019-112]). The ship that once brought Spanish conquest to her island now sails to her rescue under English colours.

## Crew and Fate

### Key Personnel

- **Don Diego de Espinosa y Valdez** — Original commander, captured by Blood and held prisoner in the ship's great cabin; dies of "fear" in his bonds ([012-316])
- **Don Esteban de Espinosa** — Son of Don Diego, who fitted out the ship for the raid on Barbados
- **Captain Peter Blood** — Seizes command after the night assault; later renames the vessel *Arabella*
- **Nathaniel Hagthorpe** — Key member of Blood's crew, present during the capture and the Encarnacion deception
- **Ogle** — Reformed gunner who uses the ship's guns to destroy the Spanish boats attempting to retake the vessel ([009-287])

### Fate

The *Cinco Llagas* does not survive the novel under her original name. At Tortuga, Blood rechristens her the *Arabella* ([013-114]), and it is under this new name that the vessel achieves her greatest fame — becoming the forty-gun flagship of the most successful buccaneer fleet in the Caribbean, feared by Spaniards, respected by the English, and ultimately instrumental in Blood's redemption and appointment as Governor of Jamaica.

## Significance

The *Cinco Llagas* occupies a pivotal position in the novel's narrative architecture. She is the bridge between Blood's life as a slave and his career as a buccaneer captain — the vessel whose capture transforms him from a powerless convict into a commander with a warship at his disposal. The ship's dual identity (Spanish warship turned English pirate flagship) mirrors Blood's own duality (Irish rebel turned English gentleman). The renaming of the *Cinco Llagas* to *Arabella* is more than a romantic gesture: it represents Blood's self-transformation and his assertion of a new identity, one defined not by what he was forced to become but by what he chooses to be.
