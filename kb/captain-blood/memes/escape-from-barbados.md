# Escape from Barbados

The **Escape from Barbados** was the pivotal event in which Peter Blood and his fellow convict-slaves seized the Spanish frigate Cinco Llagas in Bridgetown harbour and sailed to freedom. The escape transformed Blood from a plantation slave into the captain of a buccaneer vessel, setting the stage for his subsequent career as the most formidable buccaneer in the Caribbean.[^1]

## Background

After being transported to Barbados as convicted rebels following the Monmouth Rebellion, Peter Blood and his companions were sold into slavery on the island's plantations. Blood's medical skills earned him preferential treatment from Colonel Bishop and Governor Steed, allowing him relative freedom of movement within Bridgetown. This comparative liberty provided the opportunity to observe harbour activities and plan an escape — advantages unavailable to the ordinary field slaves.[^2]

Blood's position as a physician placed him in a unique situation: trusted enough to move freely, yet still legally a slave. This duality defined his Barbados period, during which he tended to the ailments of the very planters who owned him while secretly nursing hopes of liberation.[^2]

## The Capture of the Cinco Llagas

The opportunity for escape arrived with the Spanish attack on Bridgetown. A Spanish squadron had been fitted out at San Juan de Porto Rico, and the Cinco Llagas — a powerful frigate — was among the vessels deployed in the raid. The ship carried a formidable armament, described in the narrative as "a formidable vessel of thirty-six guns, which had been armed at San Juan de Porto Rico."[^3]

When the Spanish attack damaged the Cinco Llagas and left her vulnerable in the harbour, Blood seized the moment. In the confusion following the raid, he organized his fellow convicts and led them aboard the Spanish vessel. A guard of ten men had been left on the ship, but Blood's desperate band overpowered them. The Spanish crew, already weakened by the engagement ashore, could offer little resistance.[^4]

> "Be welcome aboard the Cinco Llagas, Colonel, darling," a voice greeted Colonel Bishop when he arrived to investigate — a mocking invitation that underscored the completeness of Blood's triumph.[^5]

The capture was executed with remarkable speed and precision. Before the Barbados authorities could organize a response, the Cinco Llagas was under Blood's command, her Spanish colours struck and replaced.[^5]

## Transformation of the Ship

Having seized the Cinco Llagas, Blood renamed her the Arabella in honour of Arabella Bishop, the niece of Colonel Bishop who had shown him kindness during his captivity. The irony of naming his prize after the niece of his former master was not lost on Blood, and it established a pattern of audacious symbolism that would characterize his buccaneer career.[^6]

The transformation was more than nominal. The Spanish frigate, "taken as a prize of war," became the flagship of a growing buccaneer fleet, her guns now turned against Spanish shipping rather than in its service.[^6]

## Aftermath

Following their escape, Blood and his crew sailed to the French-held island of Tortuga, where they established themselves among the Brethren of the Coast. The escape permanently soured relations between Blood and Colonel Bishop, who swore vengeance against the man who had been both his slave and his medical officer.[^7]

The Cinco Llagas, now the Arabella, became legendary among Caribbean buccaneers. From this single captured frigate, Blood built a fleet that would challenge Spanish naval supremacy in the region for years to come.[^7]
