# Honour

## Overview

Honour is the moral compass that guides [[peter-blood|Peter Blood]] through the worst circumstances of his life. Stripped of rank, property, freedom, and country, Blood retains his honour — and it is this, more than his [[navigation|seamanship]] or medical skill, that defines him as a gentleman. The novel presents honour as an internal quality independent of social station: a slave can have more honour than a judge, a pirate more than a king.

## Historical Background

The 17th-century code of honour was rooted in aristocratic ideals of personal integrity, military [[courage|valor]], and reputation. A gentleman's "word of honour" was considered binding, and duels were fought over perceived slights. Yet honour was also class-bound: it was assumed that [[gentleman|gentlemen]] possessed honour while commoners did not. The novel systematically undermines this assumption by showing aristocratic characters ([[judge-jeffreys|Judge Jeffreys]], [[colonel-bishop|Colonel Bishop]]) behaving dishonourably while the condemned rebel Blood maintains his integrity.

## In the Novel

### Honour in Degradation

Blood's honour is most visible when he has least to gain from it. As a slave in [[barbados|Barbados]], he maintains his dignity and refuses to grovel. As a pirate, he develops "such honour as is consistent with a [[gentleman|gentleman of fortune]]" — a code that prohibits attacking English ships, mistreating prisoners, or breaking his word. [[lord-julian-wade|Lord Julian]], sent to "put down piracy" ((019-079)), finds himself confused by a pirate who behaves more honourably than many naval officers.

### The Honour of Enemies

[[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]], the French [[authority|command]]er, provides a counterpoint. His dishonourable conduct — claiming credit for Blood's victories, breaking agreements — demonstrates what Blood might have become without his internal code. "The Honour of [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]]" (Chapter 28) is the novel's most ironic chapter title: [[de-rivarol|Rivarol]] has none.

### Restored Honour

Blood's honour is ultimately recognized by [[crown|the Crown]]. His appointment as [[governor|Governor]] of [[jamaica|Jamaica]] represents society's acknowledgment that the condemned traitor was always the more honourable man. Honour, the novel suggests, is not conferred by institutions but demonstrated through conduct — and institutions eventually catch up.
