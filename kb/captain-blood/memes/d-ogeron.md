# M. d'Ogeron

## Overview

M. d'Ogeron is the French governor of Tortuga, the buccaneer stronghold north of Hispaniola, and one of the most subtly drawn authority figures in *Captain Blood*. Unlike the brutish Colonel Bishop of Barbados or the vain M. de Rivarol who later commands the French fleet, d'Ogeron operates through persuasion and institutional legitimacy rather than force. He is the man who reframes buccaneering as a form of state service — a rhetorical transformation that Sabatini captures in one of the novel's most revealing passages, when the "greasy, half-drunken adventurers" are recast as dignified privateers "when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France herself" ((013-079)). This ability to bestow legitimacy through the sheer force of civility makes d'Ogeron indispensable to the novel's economic and political architecture, even as his personal vulnerability — his daughter — becomes a plot point that threatens Blood's position on Tortuga.

## In the Novel

D'Ogeron appears primarily in the Tortuga chapters (Chapter XIII and surrounding sections), where he serves as the official face of French colonial authority.

**The governor as legitimizer.** When Blood and his crew first arrive at Tortuga, it is d'Ogeron who receives them — not as pirates but as potential assets. He levies a percentage on all prizes brought into Cayona's harbour, an arrangement that transforms buccaneering from an illicit activity into a taxed, quasi-official enterprise ((013-074)). "As the governor of the island, who levied as" ((013-075)) his due, d'Ogeron embodies the pragmatic accommodation between state power and maritime predation that defined the 17th-century Caribbean.

**The daughter complication.** D'Ogeron's daughter, Mademoiselle d'Ogeron, becomes infatuated with Levasseur, Blood's treacherous French partner. "But even from M. d'Ogeron, the Governor's" ((014-028)) own family, the scandal radiates outward. When Levasseur betrays Blood, the governor's daughter's involvement creates a web of obligation and resentment that constrains Blood's freedom of action on Tortuga. D'Ogeron himself "had made him the only possible" ((014-031)) offer of marriage — a detail that underscores how even the most powerful colonial official could be rendered vulnerable by the entanglements of family.

**The rueful father.** Sabatini notes that d'Ogeron "should bitterly rue the" ((014-034)) consequences of his daughter's choices, and the governor's predicament — caught between paternal affection, public authority, and the violent politics of the buccaneer community — humanizes a figure who might otherwise appear as merely a bureaucratic functionary. His "infatuated Mademoiselle d'Ogeron" ((014-074)) is a reminder that even in the mercenary world of Tortuga, personal passions can override political calculation.

## Historical Basis

The historical Bertrand d'Ogeron served as governor of Tortuga from 1665 to 1675 and is credited with formalizing the buccaneer economy under French protection. He recruited buccaneers as a form of cheap naval defence against Spanish interests and established the system of harbour dues that Sabatini depicts. His daughter's romantic involvements were indeed the subject of colonial gossip, though Sabatini compresses and dramatizes the timeline for narrative effect.

## Relationships

- **Peter Blood** — D'Ogeron treats Blood as a valuable asset to the French colonial enterprise, offering him the legitimacy of official sanction while extracting a share of his prizes.
- **Levasseur** — The French buccaneer who becomes entangled with d'Ogeron's daughter, creating a personal and political crisis for the governor.
- **Mademoiselle d'Ogeron** — The governor's daughter, whose romantic involvement with Levasseur complicates Blood's position on Tortuga and reveals the limits of d'Ogeron's authority.

## Quotations

> "A trade that might have worn a repellent aspect when urged by greasy, half-drunken adventurers, boucan-hunters, lumbermen, beach-combers, English, French, and Dutch, became a dignified, almost official form of privateering when advocated by the courtly, middle-aged gentleman who in representing the French West India Company seemed to represent France herself" ((013-079)).

This single sentence encapsulates d'Ogeron's function in the novel: he is the alchemist who turns piracy into policy through the authority of his person and office.
