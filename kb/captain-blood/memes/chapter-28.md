[028-001] Gibbering and cursing, [[de-rivarol|M. de Rivarol]] stood there and watched this

[028-002] action, so presumptuously undertaken by Blood on his own responsibility.

[028-003] The officers of the Victorieuse crowded round him, but it was not until

[028-004] M. de Cussy came to join the group that he opened the sluices of his

[028-005] rage. And M. de Cussy himself invited the deluge that now caught him.

[028-006] He had come up rubbing his hands and taking a proper satisfaction in the

[028-007] energy of the men whom he had enlisted.

[028-008] “Aha, M. de Rivarol!” he laughed. “He understands his business, eh,

[028-009] this [[peter-blood|Captain Blood]]. He'll plant the Lilies of France on that fort before

[028-010] breakfast.”

[028-011] The Baron swung upon him snarling. “He understands his business, eh? His

[028-012] business, let me tell you, M. de Cussy, is to obey my orders, and I have

[028-013] not ordered this. Par la Mordieu! When this is over I'll deal with him

[028-014] for his damned [[mutiny|insubordination]].”

[028-015] “Surely, M. le Baron, he will have justified it if he succeeds.”

[028-016] “Justified it! Ah, parbleu! Can a soldier ever justify acting without

[028-017] orders?” He raved on furiously, his officers supporting him out of their

[028-018] detestation of Captain Blood.

[028-019] Meanwhile the fight went merrily on. The fort was suffering badly. Yet

[028-020] for all their manoeuvring the buccaneers were not escaping punishment.

[028-021] The starboard gunwale of the Atropos had been hammered into splinters,

[028-022] and a shot had caught her astern in the coach. The Elizabeth was badly

[028-023] battered about the forecastle, and [[arabella-ship|the Arabella]]'s maintop had been

[028-024] shot away, whilst' towards the end of that engagement the Lachesis came

[028-025] reeling out of the fight with a shattered rudder, steering herself by

[028-026] sweeps.

[028-027] The absurd Baron's fierce eyes positively gleamed with satisfaction.

[028-028] “I pray Heaven they may sink all his infernal ships!” he cried in his

[028-029] frenzy.

[028-030] But Heaven didn't hear him. Scarcely had he spoken than there was a

[028-031] terrific explosion, and half the fort went up in fragments. A lucky shot

[028-032] from the buccaneers had found the powder magazine.

[028-033] It may have been a couple of hours later, when Captain Blood, as

[028-034] spruce and cool as if he had just come from a levee, stepped upon the

[028-035] quarter-deck of the Victoriense, to confront M. de Rivarol, still in

[028-036] bedgown and nightcap.

[028-037] “I have to report, M. le Baron, that we are in possession of the fort

[028-038] on Boca Chica. The standard of France is flying from what remains of its

[028-039] tower, and the way into the outer harbour is open to your fleet.”

[028-040] M. de Rivarol was compelled to swallow his fury, though it choked

[028-041] him. The jubilation among his officers had been such that he could not

[028-042] continue as he had begun. Yet his eyes were malevolent, his face pale

[028-043] with anger.

[028-044] “You are fortunate, M. Blood, that you succeeded,” he said. “It would

[028-045] have gone very ill with you had you failed. Another time be so good as

[028-046] to await my orders, lest you should afterwards lack the justification

[028-047] which your good fortune has procured you this morning.”

[028-048] Blood smiled with a flash of white teeth, and bowed. “I shall be glad of

[028-049] your orders now, General, for pursuing our advantage. You realize that

[028-050] speed in striking is the first essential.”

[028-051] Rivarol was left gaping a moment. Absorbed in his ridiculous anger, he

[028-052] had considered nothing. But he made a quick recovery. “To my cabin, if

[028-053] you please,” he commanded peremptorily, and was turning to lead the way,

[028-054] when Blood arrested him.

[028-055] “With submission, my General, we shall be better here. You behold there

[028-056] the scene of our coming action. It is spread before you like a map.”

[028-057] He waved his hand towards the lagoon, the country flanking it and

[028-058] the considerable city standing back from the beach. “If it is not a

[028-059] presumption in me to offer a suggestion....” He paused. M. de Rivarol

[028-060] looked at him sharply, suspecting irony. But the swarthy face was bland,

[028-061] the keen eyes steady.

[028-062] “Let us hear your suggestion,” he consented.

[028-063] Blood pointed out the fort at the mouth of the inner harbour, which was

[028-064] just barely visible above the waving palms on the intervening tongue of

[028-065] land. He announced that its armament was less formidable than that

[028-066] of the outer fort, which they had reduced; but on the other hand, the

[028-067] passage was very much narrower than the Boca Chica, and before they

[028-068] could attempt to make it in any case, they must dispose of those

[028-069] defences. He proposed that the French ships should enter the outer

[028-070] harbour, and proceed at once to bombardment. Meanwhile, he would land

[028-071] three hundred buccaneers and some artillery on the eastern side of the

[028-072] lagoon, beyond the fragrant garden islands dense with richly bearing

[028-073] fruit-trees, and proceed simultaneously to storm the fort in the rear.

[028-074] Thus beset on both sides at once, and demoralized by the fate of the

[028-075] much stronger outer fort, he did not think the Spaniards would offer a

[028-076] very long resistance. Then it would be for M. de Rivarol to garrison the

[028-077] fort, whilst Captain Blood would sweep on with his men, and seize

[028-078] the Church of Nuestra Senora de la Poupa, plainly visible on its hill

[028-079] immediately eastward of the town. Not only did that eminence afford them

[028-080] a valuable and obvious strategic advantage, but it commanded the only

[028-081] road that led from [[cartagena|Cartagena]] to the interior, and once it were held

[028-082] there would be no further question of the Spaniards attempting to remove

[028-083] the wealth of the city.

[028-084] That to M. de Rivarol was--as Captain Blood had judged that it would

[028-085] be--the crowning argument. Supercilious until that moment, and disposed

[028-086] for his own pride's sake to treat the buccaneer's suggestions with

[028-087] cavalier criticism, M. de Rivarol's manner suddenly changed. He became

[028-088] alert and brisk, went so far as tolerantly to commend Captain Blood's

[028-089] plan, and issued orders that action might be taken upon it at once.

[028-090] It is not necessary to follow that action step by step. Blunders on

[028-091] the part of the French marred its smooth execution, and the indifferent

[028-092] handling of their ships led to the sinking of two of them in the course

[028-093] of the afternoon by the fort's gunfire. But by evening, owing largely to

[028-094] the irresistible fury with which the buccaneers stormed the place from

[028-095] the landward side, the fort had surrendered, and before dusk Blood and

[028-096] his men with some ordnance hauled thither by mules dominated the city

[028-097] from the heights of Nuestra Senora de la Poupa.

[028-098] At noon on the morrow, shorn of defences and threatened with

[028-099] bombardment, Cartagena sent offers of surrender to M. de Rivarol.

[028-100] Swollen with pride by a victory for which he took the entire credit

[028-101] to himself, the Baron dictated his terms. He demanded that all public

[028-102] effects and office accounts be delivered up; that the merchants

[028-103] surrender all moneys and goods held by them for their correspondents;

[028-104] the inhabitants could choose whether they would remain in the city or

[028-105] depart; but those who went must first deliver up all their property, and

[028-106] those who elected to remain must surrender half, and become the subjects

[028-107] of France; religious houses and churches should be spared, but they must

[028-108] render accounts of all moneys and valuables in their possession.

[028-109] Cartagena agreed, having no choice in the matter, and on the next

[028-110] day, which was the 5th of April, M. de Rivarol entered the city and

[028-111] proclaimed it now a French colony, appointing M. de Cussy its Governor.

[028-112] Thereafter he proceeded to the Cathedral, where very properly a Te Deum

[028-113] was sung in honour of the conquest. This by way of grace, whereafter M.

[028-114] de Rivarol proceeded to devour the city. The only detail in which the

[028-115] French conquest of Cartagena differed from an ordinary [[piracy|buccaneering]] raid

[028-116] was that under the severest penalties no soldier was to enter the

[028-117] house of any inhabitant. But this apparent respect for the persons and

[028-118] property of the conquered was based in reality upon M. de Rivarol's

[028-119] anxiety lest a doubloon should be abstracted from all the wealth that

[028-120] was pouring into the treasury opened by the Baron in the name of the

[028-121] King of France. Once the golden stream had ceased, he removed all

[028-122] restrictions and left the city in prey to his men, who proceeded further

[028-123] to pillage it of that part of their property which the inhabitants who

[028-124] became French subjects had been assured should remain inviolate. The

[028-125] plunder was enormous. In the course of four days over a hundred mules

[028-126] laden with gold went out of the city and down to the boats waiting at

[028-127] the beach to convey the treasure aboard the ships.

[028-129] During the capitulation and for some time after, Captain Blood and the

[028-130] greater portion of his buccaneers had been at their post on the heights

[028-131] of Nuestra Senora de la Poupa, utterly in ignorance of what was taking

[028-132] place. Blood, although the man chiefly, if not solely, responsible

[028-133] for the swift reduction of the city, which was proving a veritable

[028-134] treasure-house, was not even shown the consideration of being called to

[028-135] the council of officers which with M. de Rivarol determined the terms of

[028-136] the capitulation.

[028-137] This was a slight that at another time Captain Blood would not have

[028-138] borne for a moment. But at present, in his odd frame of mind, and its

[028-139] divorcement from piracy, he was content to smile his utter contempt of

[028-140] the French General. Not so, however, his captains, and still less

[028-141] his men. Resentment smouldered amongst them for a while, to flame

[028-142] out violently at the end of that week in Cartagena. It was only by

[028-143] undertaking to voice their grievance to the Baron that their captain was

[028-144] able for the moment to pacify them. That done, he went at once in quest

[028-145] of M. de Rivarol.

[028-146] He found him in the offices which the Baron had set up in the town, with

[028-147] a staff of clerks to register the treasure brought in and to cast up the

[028-148] surrendered account-books, with a view to ascertaining precisely what

[028-149] were the sums yet to be delivered up. The Baron sat there scrutinizing

[028-150] ledgers, like a city merchant, and checking figures to make sure that

[028-151] all was correct to the last peso. A choice occupation this for the

[028-152] General of the King's Armies by Sea and Land. He looked up irritated by

[028-153] the interruption which Captain Blood's advent occasioned.

[028-154] “M. le Baron,” the latter greeted him. “I must speak frankly; and you

[028-155] must suffer it. My men are on the point of [[treason|mutiny]].”

[028-156] M. de Rivarol considered him with a faint lift of the eyebrows.

[028-157] “Captain Blood, I, too, will speak frankly; and you, too, must suffer

[028-158] it. If there is a mutiny, you and your captains shall be held personally

[028-159] responsible. The mistake you make is in assuming with me the tone of an

[028-160] ally, whereas I have given you clearly to understand from the first that

[028-161] you are simply in the position of having accepted service under me.

[028-162] Your proper apprehension of that fact will save the waste of a deal of

[028-163] words.”

[028-164] Blood contained himself with difficulty. One of these fine days, he

[028-165] felt, that for the sake of humanity he must slit the comb of this

[028-166] supercilious, arrogant cockerel.

[028-167] “You may define our positions as you please,” said he. “But I'll remind

[028-168] you that the nature of a thing is not changed by the name you give it.

[028-169] I am concerned with facts; chiefly with the fact that we entered

[028-170] into definite articles with you. Those articles provide for a certain

[028-171] distribution of the spoil. My men demand it. They are not satisfied.”

[028-172] “Of what are they not satisfied?” demanded the Baron.

[028-173] “Of your honesty, M. de Rivarol.”

[028-174] A blow in the face could scarcely have taken [[levasseur|the Frenchman]] more aback.

[028-175] He stiffened, and drew himself up, his eyes blazing, his face of a

[028-176] deathly pallor. The clerks at the tables laid down their pens, and

[028-177] awaited the explosion in a sort of terror.

[028-178] For a long moment there was silence. Then the great gentleman delivered

[028-179] himself in a voice of concentrated anger. “Do you really dare so much,

[028-180] you and the dirty thieves that follow you? God's Blood! You shall answer

[028-181] to me for that word, though it entail a yet worse dishonour to meet you.

[028-182] Faugh!”

[028-183] “I will remind you,” said Blood, “that I am speaking not for myself,

[028-184] but for my men. It is they who are not satisfied, they who threaten that

[028-185] unless satisfaction is afforded them, and promptly, they will take it.”

[028-186] “Take it?” said Rivarol, trembling in his rage. “Let them attempt it,

[028-187] and....”

[028-188] “Now don't be rash. My men are within their rights, as you are aware.

[028-189] They demand to know when this sharing of the spoil is to take place, and

[028-190] when they are to receive the fifth for which their articles provide.”

[028-191] “God give me patience! How can we share the spoil before it has been

[028-192] completely gathered?”

[028-193] “My men have reason to believe that it is gathered; and, anyway, they

[028-194] view with mistrust that it should all be housed aboard your ships, and

[028-195] remain in your possession. They say that hereafter there will be no

[028-196] ascertaining what the spoil really amounts to.”

[028-197] “But--name of Heaven!--I have kept books. They are there for all to

[028-198] see.”

[028-199] “They do not wish to see account-books. Few of them can read. They want

[028-200] to view the treasure itself. They know--you compel me to be blunt--that

[028-201] the accounts have been falsified. Your books show the spoil of Cartagena

[028-202] to amount to some ten million livres. The men know--and they are very

[028-203] skilled in these computations--that it exceeds the enormous total of

[028-204] forty millions. They insist that the treasure itself be produced and

[028-205] weighed in their presence, as is the custom among the Brethren of the

[028-206] Coast.”

[028-207] “I know nothing of filibuster customs.” The gentleman was disdainful.

[028-208] “But you are learning quickly.”

[028-209] “What do you mean, you rogue? I am a leader of armies, not of plundering

[028-210] thieves.”

[028-211] “Oh, but of course!” Blood's irony laughed in his eyes. “Yet, whatever

[028-212] you may be, I warn you that unless you yield to a demand that I consider

[028-213] just and therefore uphold, you may look for trouble, and it would not

[028-214] surprise me if you never leave Cartagena at all, nor convey a single

[028-215] gold piece home to France.”

[028-216] “Ah, pardieu! Am I to understand that you are threatening me?”

[028-217] “Come, come, M. le Baron! I warn you of the trouble that a little

[028-218] prudence may avert. You do not know on what a volcano you are sitting.

[028-219] You do not know the ways of buccaneers. If you persist, Cartagena will

[028-220] be drenched in blood, and whatever the outcome the King of France will

[028-221] not have been well served.”

[028-222] That shifted the basis of the argument to less hostile ground. Awhile

[028-223] yet it continued, to be concluded at last by an ungracious undertaking

[028-224] from M. de Rivarol to submit to the demands of the buccaneers. He gave

[028-225] it with an extreme ill-grace, and only because Blood made him realize at

[028-226] last that to withhold it longer would be dangerous. In an engagement,

[028-227] he might conceivably defeat Blood's followers. But conceivably he might

[028-228] not. And even if he succeeded, the effort would be so costly to him in

[028-229] men that he might not thereafter find himself in sufficient strength to

[028-230] maintain his hold of what he had seized.

[028-231] The end of it all was that he gave a promise at once to make the

[028-232] necessary preparations, and if Captain Blood and his officers would wait

[028-233] upon him on board the Victorieuse to-morrow morning, the treasure

[028-234] should be produced, weighed in their presence, and their fifth share

[028-235] surrendered there and then into their own keeping.

[028-236] Among the buccaneers that night there was hilarity over the sudden

[028-237] abatement of M. de Rivarol's monstrous pride. But when the next dawn

[028-238] broke over Cartagena, they had the explanation of it. The only ships

[028-239] to be seen in the harbour were the [[arabella-bishop|Arabella]] and the Elizabeth riding

[028-240] at anchor, and the Atropos and the Lachesis careened on the beach for

[028-241] repair of the damage sustained in the bombardment. The French ships were

[028-242] gone. They had been quietly and secretly warped out of the harbour under

[028-243] cover of night, and three sails, faint and small, on the horizon to

[028-244] westward was all that remained to be seen of them. The absconding M. de

[028-245] Rivarol had gone off with the treasure, taking with him the troops and

[028-246] mariners he had brought from France. He had left behind him at Cartagena

[028-247] not only the empty-handed buccaneers, whom he had swindled, but also

[028-248] M. de Cussy and the volunteers and negroes from Hispaniola, whom he had

[028-249] swindled no less.

[028-250] The two parties were fused into one by their common fury, and before the

[028-251] exhibition of it the inhabitants of that ill-fated town were stricken

[028-252] with deeper terror than they had yet known since the coming of this

[028-253] expedition.

[028-254] Captain Blood alone kept his head, setting a curb upon his deep chagrin.

[028-255] He had promised himself that before parting from M. de Rivarol he would

[028-256] present a reckoning for all the petty affronts and insults to which that

[028-257] unspeakable fellow--now proved a scoundrel--had subjected him.

[028-258] “We must follow,” he declared. “Follow and punish.”

[028-259] At first that was the general cry. Then came the consideration that

[028-260] only two of the buccaneer ships were seaworthy--and these could

[028-261] not accommodate the whole force, particularly being at the moment

[028-262] indifferently victualled for a long voyage. The crews of the Lachesis

[028-263] and Atropos and with them their captains, [[wolverstone|Wolverstone]] and Yberville,

[028-265] still hidden in Cartagena. They would remain behind to extort it whilst

[028-267] sailed with them do as they pleased.

[028-269] attempting to draw back he almost precipitated a battle between the two

[028-270] parties into which that same proposal had now divided the buccaneers.
