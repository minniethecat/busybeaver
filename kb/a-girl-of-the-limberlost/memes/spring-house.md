## Description

The spring house is a small outbuilding on the Comstock property, used for cold storage of perishable foods. Built over or near a spring, its naturally cool interior preserves butter, milk, strawberries, and other foods that would spoil in the summer heat of [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]]. (013-414) [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] refers to "that crock of strawberries in the spring house," indicating the building's routine role in the household's food economy. (021-116)

Above the spring house proper is a loft, accessible by ladder or stairs, where the Comstocks store old equipment — among them a lard oil lantern and an early-manufacture kerosene lantern, both cleaned and filled by [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] during a period of nocturnal activity. (013-426) The loft functions as a repository for the family's material history, holding objects from earlier, perhaps more prosperous, days.

## Location

The spring house stands on the Comstock property, near enough to [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] that family members can make quick trips to fetch items — a crock of strawberries, a pitcher of cold water — and return within moments. (024-164) Its proximity to [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] also makes it a waypoint in [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s late-night movements, when she crosses between [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] and the spring house carrying butter, the lamp lighting her path. (013-414)

The spring house is situated within the broader landscape of [[comstock-cabin|the Comstock home]]stead, which includes [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] itself, [[comstock-garden|the back garden]] with its gate and path, the grape arbour (at the nearby Sinton farm), and [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] swamp at the property's edge.

## Significance

The spring house represents the quiet domestic competence of the Comstock household. Unlike the dramatic scenes at [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], the school, or [[grape-arbour|the arbour]], the spring house serves a purely practical function — keeping food cool, storing old tools — and its presence signals the ordinary rhythms of rural life that continue beneath the novel's larger emotional currents.

The building also plays a small but significant role in [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s transformation. It is while carrying butter to the spring house at night that she encounters a winged creature — a moth — brushing close by her face, a moment of contact with the natural world that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] loves. (013-414) The spring house thus becomes an accidental site of [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s reconnection with [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]'s beauty, part of the slow erosion of her bitterness.

The loft over the spring house, with its old lanterns, connects to the novel's themes of light and darkness, both literal and metaphorical. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s retrieval and refilling of the lanterns precedes her secret night-time activities, illuminating her hidden compassion and her growing investment in her daughter's world.

## Key Scenes

**The Winged Creature** (013-414) — [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] carries butter to the spring house at night, and something brushes close by her face — a winged creature rising above [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]]. The brief encounter with a moth at the spring house door marks a quiet moment of wonder in [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s hardening life.

**The Lanterns in the Loft** (013-426–013-430) — [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] climbs to the loft over the spring house, hunting for old lanterns. She cleans and fills a lard oil lantern and an early kerosene lantern, preparing for her secret night excursions. The scene reveals her resourcefulness and the hidden stores of [[comstock-cabin|the Comstock home]]stead.

**Strawberries and Supper** (021-116) — [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] casually mentions the crock of strawberries in the spring house during a family meal. The reference, so ordinary, illustrates how the spring house is woven into the daily fabric of Comstock life — unremarkable, essential, taken for granted.

**A Pitcher from the Spring House** (024-164) — Near the novel's end, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] asks Philip to fetch a pitcher from the spring house as they prepare to gather in [[grape-arbour|the arbour]]. The request, simple and domestic, signals the return of normalcy and the integration of the spring house into the rhythms of a life now shared.
