## Summary

In the final chapter of the novel, [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] arrives on Mackinac Island having sent [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] a telegram that locates [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] reads the telegram, she understands its deeper meaning: Edith has acknowledged defeat. (025-084) "At last Philip was free," [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] realizes, her face radiant. (025-087) The telegram — and Edith's subsequent actions — represent the culmination of her character arc from entitled Chicago socialite to a woman capable of genuine [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]].

## Context

[[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s journey to this moment had been long and painful. She had lost Philip at the Lake Shore Club [[philip-ammon-engagement-ball|engagement ball]] when [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]] exposed her cruelty. She had pursued him to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], only to be confronted by [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s quiet moral authority beneath the willow tree. She had retreated to Mackinac Island with her family's summer cottage, nursing wounds she could not name.

Meanwhile, Philip had fallen seriously ill — a relapse brought on by his desperate return to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] after [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] left for Mackinac. He had "run away from the doctors" (025-075) and made his way to the O'Mores' cottage, where Freckles and the Angel took him in. Edith, [[education-as-liberation|learning]] of his condition, located [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and sent the telegram that reunited the lovers. The act of sending that telegram — of actively facilitating Philip's reunion with another woman — was Edith's first fully selfless act in the novel.

## Key Participants

**[[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]** — The telegram is the crucial artifact. By notifying Philip of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s location, Edith does what she has never done before: she puts Philip's happiness above her own claim on him. The narrator does not linger on Edith's interior state, but the reader understands the magnitude of the gesture. (025-084) [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], who once claimed Philip by "the right of a betrothed wife" (022-327), now concedes him to the woman who earned his love in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]].

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s reading of the telegram is the moment she knows — finally and without reservation — that Philip is hers. "At last Philip was free," she thinks, and the relief in that sentence carries the weight of months of uncertainty. (025-087) Her radiant face is the outward sign of an inward certainty: the contest is over, and she has won — not by defeating Edith, but by being who she is.

**[[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]** — Philip's role in this event is passive but essential. His illness — "brain fever" brought on by the strain of losing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and the desperation of seeking her — makes him the object rather than the agent of the [[forgiveness-and-redemption|reconciliation]]. (024-062) He cannot orchestrate the reunion; he can only be found. Edith's choice to help find him, and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s choice to accept him, are the active forces that resolve the novel's romantic plot.

## Aftermath

The resolution of the [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] thread opens the way for the novel's final scenes: Philip's proposal at Mackinac, the arrival of [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] and [[billy|Billy]], and the wedding that gathers the entire extended family — Sintons, O'Mores, and all — in celebration. Edith's telegram is the last obstacle removed, the final knot untied, before the happy ending can proceed.

The event also establishes Edith's future trajectory. Though the novel does not follow her further, her action suggests the possibility of growth: the woman who once demanded Philip as her due has learned to release him as a gift. The parallel with [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s own arc of [[education-as-liberation|learning]] to release — her daughter, her grief, her claim on the dead — is subtle but significant. Both women learn that love is not possession but liberation.

## Significance

[[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s gesture of [[forgiveness-and-redemption|reconciliation]] is the novel's final argument about the nature of love and maturity. Throughout the story, possession and ownership have been the vocabulary of romantic entitlement — Edith's "right of a betrothed wife," Philip's engagement ring slipped onto [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s finger as a "mute appeal." The telegram breaks that pattern. It is a communication that asks nothing, claims nothing, demands nothing. It simply tells the truth: she is there, go to her.

This is the novel's most radical redefinition of love — not as conquest or contract, but as the willingness to let the beloved go where love leads. [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], the character most defined by the desire to possess, becomes in her final significant act the character who most fully releases. Her transformation is not complete — she is still [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], proud and wounded — but it is real, and it provides the moral counterweight to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s triumph. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] wins Philip not because she fought harder for him but because, unlike Edith, she never tried to own him at all.
