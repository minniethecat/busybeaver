## Synopsis

Despite having been told as a child that the Limberlost is a place to be feared, especially at night (003-146), Elnora Comstock gradually learns to walk its dark trails alone in pursuit of the great night moths that emerge each June. These nocturnal expeditions mark her transition from a girl afraid of the swamp to a young woman who has mastered its secrets and made its creatures her livelihood.


## From the Text

> The swamp at night was a different world. The darkness pressed close,
> and every sound — the rustle of leaves, the splash of water, the call
> of a night bird — seemed magnified in the stillness. But it was here,
> in the deep night of June, that the great velvet-winged moths emerged,
> their colours unseen in the darkness yet vibrant to those who knew
> where to look. Elnora learned to walk the familiar trails by feel,
> her lantern a small island of light in the vast darkness of the
> Limberlost.
>
> (010-150)

## Key Participants

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Having conquered poverty and social rejection, Elnora returns to her deepest childhood terror — the dark swamp — and transforms it into a realm of wonder, income, and independence (003-146).

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — Elnora's mother, whose lifelong grief kept her from the swamp, begins to accompany Elnora on nocturnal walks. These shared excursions mark the beginning of Katharine's reconnection with the natural world and with her daughter (008-165).

**[[pete-corson|Pete Corson]]** — The swamp guide who explains to Elnora how the great night moths emerge in spring and can be sold, planting the seed of her nocturnal collecting expeditions (008-150).

**[[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]** — The young man from Chicago to whom Elnora later shares the splendor of the June swamp's night moths, bringing her intimate knowledge of the Limberlost to an appreciative audience (016-300).

## Key Moments

- As a young girl, Elnora is warned that everyone is "afraid at night" in the Limberlost, planting a fear she must later overcome through courage and necessity (003-146).
- Pete Corson explains to Elnora how the great night moths emerge in spring, revealing the economic and scientific value of the creatures she once feared (008-150).
- Elnora and her mother venture into the night together, a rare shared excursion that marks the beginning of Katharine's reconnection with the natural world (008-165).
- The birth of the "great, velvet-winged night moths" becomes central to Elnora's understanding of the June swamp's splendor, which she later shares with Philip Ammon as a form of intimate revelation (016-300).
- By the novel's later chapters, Elnora moves through the Limberlost at night with the confidence of long familiarity, no longer the frightened girl of her childhood but its undisputed mistress.

## Significance

The night walks represent Elnora's conquest of her last fear. Having overcome poverty, social rejection, and her mother's emotional distance, she returns to the primal childhood terror — the dark swamp — and transforms it into a source of wonder and income. The great night moths that draw her into the darkness become emblems of her transformation: fragile, luminous creatures that can only be found by those brave enough to enter the unknown. Her mastery of the night swamp completes the arc from frightened girl to self-possessed woman.
