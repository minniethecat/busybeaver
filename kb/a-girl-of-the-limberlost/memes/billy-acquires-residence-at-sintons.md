# Billy Acquires a Residence at the Sintons

## Overview

Billy Acquires a Residence at the Sintons is a central event in Chapter VII of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. After discovering that Billy's alcoholic father has died in a drunken sleep, Wesley Sinton brings the neglected street boy home to his farm. Despite Margaret Sinton's initial resistance, Billy gradually wins her heart, and a dramatic confrontation with Mrs. Comstock — who also wants the boy — forces Margaret to claim Billy as her own. The event embodies the novel's themes of community support, redemption through love, and the transformative power of a stable home.

## Key Participants

- **Billy** — A young street boy from the Corson gang's impoverished families, neglected and half-starved
- **Wesley Sinton** — The kind farmer who discovers Billy's situation and determines to give him a home
- **Margaret Sinton** — Wesley's wife, initially resistant to taking in a rough boy, but ultimately fiercely protective
- **Elnora Comstock** — Helps care for Billy and mediates between the adults
- **Katharine Comstock** — Elnora's mother, who also wants to adopt Billy, forcing Margaret to declare her claim

## Narrative

Wesley Sinton goes to investigate the Corson gang families living in squalor near the swamp. He finds Billy and his siblings in a filthy shack, their father passed out in a drunken stupor — only to discover the man is dead (007-115--121). Billy, practical and uncomplaining, matter-of-factly states the facts of his life. Sinton is moved by the boy's condition and offers: "If you'll come with me and be my boy, I'll see that they have plenty" (007-110).

Billy agrees, and Sinton first takes him to a lunch counter to eat (007-123--125), then buys him new clothes (007-129). Driving home, Billy sits on the front seat, already claiming his place beside his new guardian (007-210).

Margaret's initial reaction is horror and resistance. She has always disliked boys, and Billy immediately causes trouble — slashing the carriage upholstery and pulling out a turkey's tail feathers. Yet Wesley's patient care for the boy begins to move her. He bathes Billy, treats his sores with medicine, and dresses him in clean clothes. Billy looks at himself in the mirror and declares, "My, I'm purty good-looking, ain't I?" (007-323).

Wesley defends Billy passionately to Margaret, reminding her of Christian charity: "Billy is not a beast! He is a very dear little boy" (007-427--428). That night, Billy sleeps in a real bed for the first time, curled up in "rapturous content" (007-474).

A crisis comes when Elnora and Mrs. Comstock visit. Mrs. Comstock immediately bonds with Billy, declaring "If there is anything I love it is a boy" (007-642). She tries to take Billy home with her. This forces Margaret's hand: "You let go of our boy!" she orders (007-726). In the confrontation, Margaret finally claims Billy as her own.

Billy asks Margaret directly: "Do you love me now?" She answers: "I'll try to love you, if you are a good boy." Billy replies: "Then I guess I'll stay" (007-745--747) — sealing his place in the Sinton household.

## Significance

Billy's acquisition of a residence represents one of the novel's most powerful examples of community care in action. The neglected street boy finds not just food and shelter, but identity and belonging. The event also catalyzes Margaret Sinton's maternal awakening, paralleling Mrs. Comstock's own journey toward emotional re-engagement.
