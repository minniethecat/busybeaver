# CHAPTER XII

[012-001]WHEREIN MARGARET SINTON REVEALS A SECRET, AND MRS. COMSTOCK POSSESSES

[012-002]THE LIMBERLOST

[012-003]“[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], bring me the towel, quick!” cried Mrs Comstock.

[012-004]“In a minute, mother,” mumbled Elnora.

[012-005]She was standing before the kitchen mirror, tying the back part of her

[012-006]hair, while the front turned over her face.

[012-007]“Hurry! There’s a varmint of some kind!”

[012-008]Elnora ran into the sitting-room and thrust the heavy kitchen towel

[012-009]into her mother’s hand. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] swung open the screen door and

[012-010]struck at some object, Elnora tossed the hair from her face so that she

[012-011]could see past her mother. The girl screamed wildly.

[012-012]“Don’t! Mother, don’t!”

[012-013]Mrs. Comstock struck again. Elnora caught her arm. “It’s the one I

[012-014]want! It’s worth a lot of money! Don’t! Oh, you shall not!”

[012-015]“Shan’t, missy?” blazed Mrs. Comstock. “When did you get to bossing

[012-016]me?”

[012-017]The hand that held the screen swept a half-circle and stopped at

[012-018]Elnora’s cheek. She staggered with the blow, and across her face, paled

[012-019]with excitement, a red mark arose rapidly. The screen slammed shut,

[012-020]throwing the creature on the floor before them. Instantly Mrs. Comstock

[012-021]crushed it with her foot. Elnora stepped back. Excepting the red mark,

[012-022]her face was very white.

[012-023]“That was the last moth I needed,” she said, “to complete a collection

[012-024]worth three hundred dollars. You’ve ruined it before my eyes!”

[012-025]“Moth!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “You say that because you are mad. Moths

[012-026]have big wings. I know a moth!”

[012-027]“I’ve kept things from you,” said Elnora, “because I didn’t dare

[012-028]confide in you. You had no sympathy with me. But you know I never told

[012-029]you untruths in all my life.”

[012-030]“It’s no moth!” reiterated Mrs. Comstock.

[012-031]“It is!” cried Elnora. “It’s from a case in the ground. Its wings take

[012-032]two or three hours to expand and harden.”

[012-033]“If I had known it was a moth——” Mrs. Comstock wavered.

[012-034]“You did know! I told you! I begged you to stop! It meant just three

[012-035]hundred dollars to me.”

[012-036]“Bah! Three hundred fiddlesticks!”

[012-037]“They are what have paid for books, tuition, and clothes for the past

[012-038]four years. They are what I could have started on to college. You’ve

[012-039]ruined the very one I needed. You never made any pretence of loving me.

[012-040]At last I’ll be equally frank with you. I hate you! You are a selfish,

[012-041]wicked woman! I hate you!”

[012-042]Elnora turned, went through the kitchen and from the back door. She

[012-043]followed [[comstock-garden|the garden]] path to the gate and walked toward [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] a

[012-044]short distance when reaction overtook her. She dropped on the ground

[012-045]and leaned against a big log. When a little child, desperate as now,

[012-046]she had tried to die by holding her breath. She had thought in that way

[012-047]to make her mother sorry, but she had learned that life was a thing

[012-048]thrust upon her and she could not leave it at her wish.

[012-049]She was so stunned over the loss of that moth, which she had childishly

[012-050]named the Yellow Emperor, that she scarcely remembered the blow. She

[012-051]had thought no luck in all the world would be so rare as to complete

[012-052]her collection; now she had been forced to see a splendid Imperialis

[012-053]destroyed before her. There was a possibility that she could find

[012-054]another, but she was facing the certainty that the one she might have

[012-055]had and with which she undoubtedly could have attracted others, was

[012-056]spoiled by her mother. How long she sat there Elnora did not know or

[012-057]care. She simply suffered in dumb, abject misery, an occasional dry sob

[012-058]shaking her. [[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]] was right. Elnora felt that morning that her

[012-059]mother never would be any different. The girl had reached the place

[012-060]where she realized that she could endure it no longer.

[012-061]As Elnora left the room, Mrs. Comstock took one step after her.

[012-062]“You little huzzy!” she gasped.

[012-063]But Elnora was gone. Her mother stood staring.

[012-064]“She never did lie to me,” she muttered. “I guess it was a moth. And

[012-065]the only one she needed to get three hundred dollars, she said. I wish

[012-066]I hadn’t been so fast! I never saw anything like it. I thought it was

[012-067]some deadly, stinging, biting thing. A body does have to be mighty

[012-068]careful here. But likely I’ve spilt the milk now. Pshaw! She can find

[012-069]another! There’s no use to be foolish. Maybe moths are like snakes,

[012-070]where there’s one, there are two.”

[012-071]Mrs. Comstock took the broom and swept the moth out of the door. Then

[012-072]she got down on her knees and carefully examined the steps, logs and

[012-073]the earth of the flower beds at each side. She found the place where

[012-074]the creature had emerged from the ground, and the hard, dark-brown case

[012-075]which had enclosed it, still wet inside. Then she knew Elnora had been

[012-076]right. It was a moth. Its wings had been damp and not expanded. Mrs.

[012-077]Comstock never before had seen one in that state, and she did not know

[012-078]how they originated. She had thought all of them came from cases spun

[012-079]on trees or against walls or boards. She had seen only enough to know

[012-080]that there were such things; as a flash of white told her that an

[012-081]ermine was on her premises, or a sharp “buzzzzz” warned her of a

[012-082]rattler.

[012-083]So it was from creatures like that Elnora had secured her school money.

[012-084]In one sickening sweep there rushed into the heart of the woman a full

[012-085]realization of the width of the gulf that separated her from her child.

[012-086]Lately many things had pointed toward it, none more plainly than when

[012-087]Elnora, like a reincarnation of her father, had stood fearlessly before

[012-088]a large city audience and played with even greater skill than he, on

[012-089]what Mrs. Comstock felt very certain was his violin. But that little

[012-090]crawling creature of earth, crushed by her before its splendid yellow

[012-091]and lavender wings could spread and carry it into the mystery of night,

[012-092]had performed a miracle.

[012-093]“We are nearer strangers to each other than we are with any of the

[012-094]neighbours,” she muttered.

[012-095]So one of the Almighty’s most delicate and beautiful creations was

[012-096]sacrificed without fulfilling the law, yet none of its species ever

[012-097]served so glorious a cause, for at last Mrs. Comstock’s inner vision

[012-098]had cleared. She went through [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] mechanically. Every few minutes

[012-099]she glanced toward the back walk to see if Elnora were coming. She knew

[012-100]arrangements had been made with Margaret to go to the city some time

[012-101]that day, so she grew more nervous and uneasy every moment. She was

[012-102]haunted by the fear that the blow might discolour Elnora’s cheek; that

[012-103]she would tell Margaret. She went down the back walk, looking intently

[012-104]in all directions, left the garden and followed the swamp path. Her

[012-105]step was noiseless on the soft, black earth, and soon she came close

[012-106]enough to see Elnora. Mrs. Comstock stood looking at the girl in

[012-107]troubled uncertainty. Not knowing what to say, at last she turned and

[012-108]went back to the cabin.

[012-109]Noon came and she prepared dinner, calling, as she always did, when

[012-110]Elnora was in the garden, but she got no response, and the girl did not

[012-111]come. A little after one o’clock Margaret stopped at the gate.

[012-112]“Elnora has changed her mind. She is not going,” called Mrs. Comstock.

[012-113]She felt that she hated Margaret as she hitched her horse and came up

[012-114]the walk instead of driving on.

[012-115]“You must be mistaken,” said Margaret. “I was going on purpose for her.

[012-116]She asked me to take her. I had no errand. Where is she?”

[012-117]“I will call her,” said Mrs. Comstock.

[012-118]She followed the path again, and this time found Elnora sitting on the

[012-119]log. Her face was swollen and discoloured, and her eyes red with

[012-120]crying. She paid no attention to her mother.

[012-121]“Mag Sinton is here,” said Mrs. Comstock harshly. “I told her you had

[012-122]changed your mind, but she said you asked her to go with you, and she

[012-123]had nothing to go for herself.”

[012-124]Elnora arose, recklessly waded through the deep swamp grasses and so

[012-125]reached the path ahead of her mother. Mrs. Comstock followed as far as

[012-126]the garden, but she could not enter the cabin. She busied herself among

[012-127]the vegetables, barely looking up when the back-door screen slammed

[012-128]noisily. Margaret Sinton approached colourless, her eyes so angry that

[012-129]Mrs. Comstock shrank back.

[012-130]“What’s the matter with Elnora’s face?” demanded Margaret.

[012-131]Mrs. Comstock made no reply.

[012-132]“You struck her, did you?”

[012-133]“I thought you wasn’t blind!”

[012-134]“I have been, for twenty long years now, Kate Comstock,” said Margaret

[012-135]Sinton, “but my eyes are open at last. What I see is that I’ve done you

[012-136]no good and Elnora a big wrong. I had an idea that it would kill you to

[012-137]know, but I guess you are tough enough to stand anything. Kill or cure,

[012-138]you get it now!”

[012-139]“What are you frothing about?” coolly asked Mrs. Comstock.

[012-140]“You!” cried Margaret. “You! The woman who doesn’t pretend to love her

[012-141]only child. Who lets her grow to a woman, as you have let Elnora, and

[012-142]can’t be satisfied with every sort of neglect, but must add abuse yet;

[012-143]and all for a fool idea about a man who wasn’t worth his salt!”

[012-144]Mrs. Comstock picked up a hoe.

[012-145]“Go right on!” she said. “Empty yourself. It’s the last thing you’ll

[012-146]ever do!”

[012-147]“Then I’ll make a tidy job of it,” said Margaret. “You’ll not touch me.

[012-148]You’ll stand there and hear the truth at last, and because I dare face

[012-149]you and tell it, you will know in your soul it is truth. When Robert

[012-150]Comstock shaved that quagmire out there so close he went in, he wanted

[012-151]to keep you from knowing where he was coming from. He’d been to see

[012-152][[elvira-carney|Elvira Carney]]. They had plans to go to a dance that night——”

[012-153]“Close your lips!” said Mrs. Comstock in a voice of deadly quiet.

[012-154]“You know I wouldn’t dare open them if I wasn’t telling you the truth.

[012-155]I can prove what I say. I was coming from Reeds. It was hot in the

[012-156]woods and I stopped at Carney’s as I passed for a drink. Elvira’s

[012-157]bedridden old mother heard me, and she was so crazy for some one to

[012-158]talk with, I stepped in a minute. I saw Robert come down the path.

[012-159]Elvira saw him, too, so she ran out of the house to head him off. It

[012-160]looked funny, and I just deliberately moved where I could see and hear.

[012-161]He brought her his violin, and told her to get ready and meet him in

[012-162]the woods with it that night, and they would go to a dance. She took it

[012-163]and hid it in the loft to the well-house and promised she’d go.”

[012-164]“Are you done?” demanded Mrs. Comstock.

[012-165]“No. I am going to tell you the whole story. You don’t spare Elnora

[012-166]anything. I shan’t spare you. I hadn’t been here that day, but I can

[012-167]tell you just how he was dressed, which way he went and every word they

[012-168]said, though they thought I was busy with her mother and wouldn’t

[012-169]notice them. Put down your hoe, Kate. I went to Elvira, told her what I

[012-170]knew and made her give me Comstock’s violin for Elnora over three years

[012-171]ago. She’s been playing it ever since. I won’t see her slighted and

[012-172]abused another day on account of a man who would have broken your heart

[012-173]if he had lived. Six months more would have showed you what everybody

[012-174]else knew. He was one of those men who couldn’t trust himself, and so

[012-175]no woman was safe with him. Now, will you drop grieving over him, and

[012-176]do Elnora justice?”

[012-177]Mrs. Comstock grasped the hoe tighter and turning she went down the

[012-178]walk, and started across the woods to the home of Elvira Carney. With

[012-179]averted head she passed [[limberlost-pool|the pool]], steadily pursuing her way. Elvira

[012-180]Carney, hanging towels across the back fence, saw her coming and went

[012-181]toward the gate to meet her. Twenty years she had dreaded that visit.

[012-182]Since Margaret Sinton had compelled her to produce the violin she had

[012-183]hidden so long, because she was afraid to destroy it, she had come

[012-184]closer expectation than dread. The wages of sin are the hardest debts

[012-185]on earth to pay, and they are always collected at inconvenient times

[012-186]and unexpected places. Mrs. Comstock’s face and hair were so white,

[012-187]that her dark eyes seemed burned into their setting. Silently she

[012-188]stared at the woman before her a long time.

[012-189]“I might have saved myself the trouble of coming,” she said at last, “I

[012-190]see you are guilty as sin!”

[012-191]“What has Mag Sinton been telling you?” panted the miserable woman,

[012-192]gripping the fence.

[012-193]“The truth!” answered Mrs. Comstock succinctly. “Guilt is in every line

[012-194]of your face, in your eyes, all over your wretched body. If I’d taken a

[012-195]good look at you any time in all these past years, no doubt I could

[012-196]have seen it just as plain as I can now. No woman or man can do what

[012-197]you’ve done, and not get a mark set on them for every one to read.”

[012-198]“Mercy!” gasped weak little Elvira Carney. “Have mercy!”

[012-199]“Mercy?” scoffed Mrs. Comstock. “Mercy! That’s a nice word from you!

[012-200]How much mercy did you have on me? Where’s the mercy that sent Comstock

[012-201]to the slime of the bottomless quagmire, and left me to see it, and

[012-202]then struggle on in agony all these years? How about the mercy of

[012-203]letting me neglect my baby all the days of her life? Mercy! Do you

[012-204]really dare use the word to me?”

[012-205]“If you knew what I’ve suffered!”

[012-206]“Suffered?” jeered Mrs. Comstock. “That’s interesting. And pray, what

[012-207]have you suffered?”

[012-208]“All the neighbours have suspected and been down on me. I ain’t had a

[012-209]friend. I’ve always felt guilty of his death! I’ve seen him go down a

[012-210]thousand times, plain as ever you did. Many’s the night I’ve stood on

[012-211]the other bank of that pool and listened to you, and I tried to throw

[012-212]myself in to keep from hearing you, but I didn’t dare. I knew God would

[012-213]send me to burn forever, but I’d better done it; for now, He has set

[012-214]the burning on my body, and every hour it is slowly eating the life out

[012-215]of me. The doctor says it’s a cancer——”

[012-216]Mrs. Comstock exhaled a long breath. Her grip on the hoe relaxed and

[012-217]her stature lifted to towering height.

[012-218]“I didn’t know, or care, when I came here, just what I did,” she said.

[012-219]“But my way is beginning to clear. If the guilt of your soul has come

[012-220]to a head, in a cancer on your body, it looks as if the Almighty didn’t

[012-221]need any of my help in meting out His punishments. I really couldn’t

[012-222]fix up anything to come anywhere near that. If you are going to burn

[012-223]until your life goes out with that sort of fire, you don’t owe me

[012-224]anything!”

[012-225]“Oh, Katharine Comstock!” groaned Elvira Carney, clinging to the fence

[012-226]for support.

[012-227]“Looks as if the Bible is right when it says, ‘The wages of sin is

[012-228]death,’ doesn’t it?” asked Mrs. Comstock. “Instead of doing a woman’s

[012-229]work in life, you chose the smile of invitation, and the dress of

[012-230]unearned cloth. Now you tell me you are marked to burn to death with

[012-231]the unquenchable fire. And him! It was shorter with him, but let me

[012-232]tell you he got his share! He left me with an untruth on his lips, for

[012-233]he told me he was going to take his violin to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] for a new key,

[012-234]when he carried it to you. Every vow of love and constancy he ever made

[012-235]me was a lie, after he touched your lips, so when he tried the wrong

[012-236]side of the quagmire, to hide from me the direction in which he was

[012-237]coming, it reached out for him, and it got him. It didn’t hurry,

[012-238]either! It sucked him down, slow and deliberate.”

[012-239]“Mercy!” groaned Elvira Carney. “Mercy!”

[012-240]“I don’t know the word,” said Mrs. Comstock. “You took all that out of

[012-241]me long ago. The past twenty years haven’t been of the sort that taught

[012-242]mercy. I’ve never had any on myself and none on my child. Why in the

[012-243]name of justice, should I have mercy on you, or on him? You were both

[012-244]older than I, both strong, sane people, you deliberately chose your

[012-245]course when you lured him, and he, when he was unfaithful to me. When a

[012-246]Loose Man and a Light Woman face the end the Almighty ordained for

[012-247]them, why should they shout at me for mercy? What did I have to do with

[012-249]Elvira Carney sobbed in panting gasps.

[012-250]“You’ve got tears, have you?” marvelled Mrs. Comstock. “Mine all dried

[012-251]long ago. I’ve none left to shed over my wasted life, my disfigured

[012-252]face and hair, my years of struggle with a man’s work, my wreck of land

[012-253]among the tilled fields of my neighbours, or the final knowledge that

[012-254]the man I so gladly would have died to save, wasn’t worth the sacrifice

[012-256]be the thought of the awful injustice I always have done my girl. If

[012-257]I’d lay hand on you for anything, it would be for that.”

[012-258]“Kill me if you want to,” sobbed Elvira Carney. “I know that I deserve

[012-259]it, and I don’t care.”

[012-260]“You are getting your killing fast enough to suit me,” said Mrs.

[012-261]Comstock. “I wouldn’t touch you, any more than I would him, if I could.

[012-262]Once is all any man or woman deceives me about the holiest things of

[012-263]life. I wouldn’t touch you any more than I would the black plague. I am

[012-264]going back to my girl.”

[012-265]Mrs. Comstock turned and started swiftly through the woods, but she had

[012-266]gone only a few rods when she stopped, and leaning on the hoe, she

[012-267]stood thinking deeply. Then she turned back. Elvira still clung to the

[012-268]fence, sobbing bitterly.

[012-269]“I don’t know,” said Mrs. Comstock, “but I left a wrong impression with

[012-270]you. I don’t want you to think that I believe the Almighty set a cancer

[012-272]more of the Almighty. With a whole sky-full of worlds on His hands to

[012-282]She had gone only a few steps when she again turned back.

[012-283]“If you will gather a lot of red clover bloom, make a tea strong as lye

[012-284]of it, and drink quarts, I think likely it will help you, if you are

[012-288]neither could she sit outside and think. She attacked a bed of beets

[012-290]began on the potatoes. When she was too tired to take another stroke

[012-291]she bathed and put on dry [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]]. In securing her dress she noticed

[012-292]her husband’s carefully preserved clothing lining one wall. She

[012-293]gathered it in an armload and carried it to the swamp. Piece by piece

[012-294]she pitched into the green maw of the quagmire all those articles she

[012-295]had dusted carefully and fought moths from for years, and stood

[012-296]watching as it slowly sucked them down. She went back to her room and

[012-297]gathered every scrap that had in any way belonged to [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]],

[012-298]excepting his gun and revolver, and threw it into the swamp. Then for

[012-299]the first time she set her door wide open.

[012-301]wanted Elnora. It seemed to her she never could wait until the girl

[012-302]came and delivered her judgment. At last in an effort to get nearer to

[012-306]covered with cocoons; moths and dragonflies were pinned everywhere.

[012-308]out one and lifted the lid. The bottom was covered with a sheet of thin

[012-329]capturing moths with differing baits and bright lights.

[012-331]having nothing else to do she glanced at the clock and began preparing

[012-332]supper. The work dragged. A chicken was snatched up and dressed

[012-333]hurriedly. A spice cake sprang into being. Strawberries that had been

[012-334]intended for preserves went into shortcake. Delicious odours crept from

[012-335]the cabin. She put many extra touches on the table and then commenced

[012-336]watching the road. Everything was ready, but Elnora did not come. Then

[012-337]began the anxious process of trying to keep cooked food warm and not

[012-338]spoil it. The birds went to bed and dusk came. Mrs. Comstock gave up

[012-339]the fire and set the supper on the table. Then she went out and sat on

[012-340]the front-door step watching night creep around her. She started

[012-341]eagerly as the gate creaked, but it was only [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] coming.

[012-342]“Katharine, Margaret and Elnora passed where I was working this

[012-343]afternoon, and Margaret got out of the carriage and called me to the

[012-344]fence. She told me what she had done. I’ve come to say to you that I am

[012-345]sorry. She has heard me threaten to do it a good many times, but I

[012-346]never would have got it done. I’d give a good deal if I could undo it,

[012-347]but I can’t, so I’ve come to tell you how sorry I am.”

[012-348]“You’ve got something to be sorry for,” said Mrs. Comstock, “but likely

[012-350]truth, than to live in ignorance. If Mag had the sense of a pewee,

[012-351]she’d told me long ago. That’s what hurts me, to think that both of you
