# CHAPTER III

[003-001]WHEREIN ELNORA VISITS THE BIRD WOMAN, AND OPENS A BANK ACCOUNT

[003-002]Four o’clock the following morning [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] was shelling beans. At six

[003-003]she fed the chickens and pigs, swept two of the rooms of [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]],

[003-004]built a fire, and put on the kettle for breakfast. Then she climbed the

[003-005]narrow stairs to [[comstock-attic|the attic]] she had occupied since a very small child,

[003-006]and dressed in the hated shoes and brown calico, plastered down her

[003-007]crisp curls, ate what breakfast she could, and pinning on her hat

[003-008]started for town.

[003-009]“There is no sense in your going for an hour yet,” said her mother.

[003-010]“I must try to discover some way to earn those books,” replied Elnora.

[003-011]“I am perfectly positive I shall not find them lying beside the road

[003-012]wrapped in tissue paper, and tagged with my name.”

[003-013]She went toward the city as on yesterday. Her perplexity as to where

[003-014]tuition and books were to come from was worse but she did not feel

[003-015]quite so badly. She never again would have to face all of it for the

[003-016]first time. There had been times yesterday when she had prayed to be

[003-017]hidden, or to drop dead, and neither had happened. “I believe the best

[003-018]way to get an answer to prayer is to work for it,” muttered Elnora

[003-019]grimly.

[003-020]Again she followed the trail to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], rearranged her hair and left

[003-021]the tin pail. This time she folded a couple of sandwiches in the

[003-022]napkin, and tied them in a neat light paper parcel which she carried in

[003-023]her hand. Then she hurried along the road to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] and found a

[003-024]book-store. There she asked the prices of the list of books that she

[003-025]needed, and learned that six dollars would not quite supply them. She

[003-026]anxiously inquired for second-hand books, but was told that the only

[003-027]way to secure them was from the last year’s Freshmen. Just then Elnora

[003-028]felt that she positively could not approach any of those she supposed

[003-029]to be Sophomores and ask to buy their old books. The only balm the girl

[003-030]could see for the humiliation of yesterday was to appear that day with

[003-031]a set of new books.

[003-032]“Do you wish these?” asked the clerk hurriedly, for the store was

[003-033]rapidly filling with school children wanting anything from a dictionary

[003-034]to a pen.

[003-035]“Yes,” gasped Elnora, “Oh, yes! But I cannot pay for them just now.

[003-036]Please let me take them, and I will pay for them on Friday, or return

[003-037]them as perfect as they are. Please trust me for them a few days.”

[003-038]“I’ll ask the proprietor,” he said. When he came back Elnora knew the

[003-039]answer before he spoke.

[003-040]“I’m sorry,” he said, “but Mr. Hann doesn’t recognize your name. You

[003-041]are not a customer of ours, and he feels that he can’t take the risk.”

[003-042]Elnora clumped out of the store, the thump of her heavy shoes beating

[003-043]as a hammer on her brain. She tried two other dealers with the same

[003-044]result, and then in sick despair came into the street. What could she

[003-045]do? She was too frightened to think. Should she stay from school that

[003-046]day and canvass the homes appearing to belong to the wealthy, and try

[003-047]to sell beds of wild ferns, as she had suggested to [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]? What

[003-048]would she dare ask for bringing in and planting a clump of ferns? How

[003-049]could she carry them? Would people buy them? She slowly moved past the

[003-050]hotel and then glanced around to see if there were a clock anywhere,

[003-051]for she felt sure the young people passing her constantly were on their

[003-052]way to school.

[003-053]There it stood in a bank window in big black letters staring straight

[003-054]at her:

[003-055]WANTED: CATERPILLARS, COCOONS, CHRYSALIDES, PUPAE CASES, BUTTERFLIES,

[003-056]MOTHS, INDIAN RELICS OF ALL KINDS. HIGHEST SCALE OF PRICES PAID IN CASH

[003-057]Elnora caught the wicket at the cashier’s desk with both hands to brace

[003-058]herself against disappointment.

[003-059]“Who is it wants to buy cocoons, butterflies, and moths?” she panted.

[003-060]“[[bird-woman|The Bird Woman]],” answered the cashier. “Have you some for sale?”

[003-061]“I have some, I do not know if they are what she would want.”

[003-062]“Well, you had better see her,” said the cashier. “Do you know where

[003-063]she lives?”

[003-064]“Yes,” said Elnora. “Would you tell me the time?”

[003-065]“Twenty-one after eight,” was the answer.

[003-066]She had nine minutes to reach the auditorium or be late. Should she go

[003-067]to school, or to the Bird Woman? Several girls passed her walking

[003-068]swiftly and she remembered their faces. They were hurrying to school.

[003-069]Elnora caught the infection. She would see the Bird Woman at noon.

[003-070]Algebra came first, and that professor was kind. Perhaps she could slip

[003-071]to the superintendent and ask him for a book for the next lesson, and

[003-072]at noon—“Oh, dear Lord make it come true,” prayed Elnora, at noon

[003-073]possibly she could sell some of those wonderful shining-winged things

[003-074]she had been collecting all her life around the outskirts of the

[003-075]Limberlost.

[003-076]As she went down the long hall she noticed the professor of mathematics

[003-077]standing in the door of his recitation room. When she passed him he

[003-078]smiled and spoke to her.

[003-079]“I have been watching for you,” he said, and Elnora stopped bewildered.

[003-080]“For me?” she questioned.

[003-081]“Yes,” said [[professor-henley|Professor Henley]]. “Step inside.”

[003-082]Elnora followed him into the room and closed the door behind them.

[003-083]“At teachers’ meeting last evening, one of the professors mentioned

[003-084]that a pupil had betrayed in class that she had expected her books to

[003-085]be furnished by the city. I thought possibly it was you. Was it?”

[003-086]“Yes,” breathed Elnora.

[003-087]“That being the case,” said Professor Henley, “it just occurred to me

[003-088]as you had expected that, you might require a little time to secure

[003-089]them, and you are too fine a mathematician to fall behind for want of

[003-090]supplies. So I telephoned one of our Sophomores to bring her last

[003-091]year’s books this morning. I am sorry to say they are somewhat abused,

[003-092]but the text is all here. You can have them for two dollars, and pay

[003-093]when you are ready. Would you care to take them?”

[003-094]Elnora sat suddenly, because she could not stand another instant. She

[003-095]reached both hands for the books, and said never a word. The professor

[003-096]was silent also.

[003-097]At last Elnora arose, hugging those books to her heart as a mother

[003-098]clasps a baby.

[003-099]“One thing more,” said the professor. “You may pay your tuition

[003-100]quarterly. You need not bother about the first instalment this month.

[003-101]Any time in October will do.”

[003-102]It seemed as if Elnora’s gasp of relief must have reached the soles of

[003-103]her brogans.

[003-104]“Did any one ever tell you how beautiful you are!” she cried.

[003-105]As the professor was lank, tow-haired and so near-sighted, that he

[003-106]peered at his pupils through spectacles, no one ever had.

[003-107]“No,” said Professor Henley, “I’ve waited some time for that; for which

[003-108]reason I shall appreciate it all the more. Come now, or we shall be

[003-109]late for opening exercises.”

[003-110]So Elnora entered the auditorium a second time. Her face was like the

[003-111]brightest dawn that ever broke over [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. No matter about the

[003-112]lumbering shoes and skimpy dress. No matter about anything, she had the

[003-113]books. She could take them home. In her garret she could commit them to

[003-114]memory, if need be. She could prove that clothes were not all. If the

[003-115]Bird Woman did not want any of the many different kinds of specimens

[003-116]she had collected, she was quite sure now she could sell ferns, nuts,

[003-117]and a great many things. Then, too, a girl made a place for her that

[003-118]morning, and several smiled and bowed. Elnora forgot everything save

[003-119]her books, and that she was where she could use them

[003-120]intelligently—everything except one little thing away back in her head.

[003-121]Her mother had known about the books and the tuition, and had not told

[003-122]her when she agreed to her coming.

[003-123]At noon Elnora took her little parcel of lunch and started to the home

[003-124]of the Bird Woman. She must know about the specimens first and then she

[003-125]would walk to the suburbs somewhere and eat a few bites. She dropped

[003-126]the heavy iron knocker on the door of a big red log cabin, and her

[003-127]heart thumped at the resounding stroke.

[003-128]“Is the Bird Woman at home?” she asked of the maid.

[003-129]“She is at lunch,” was the answer.

[003-130]“Please ask her if she will see a girl from the Limberlost about some

[003-131]moths?” inquired Elnora.

[003-132]“I never need ask, if it’s moths,” laughed the girl. “Orders are to

[003-133]bring any one with specimens right in. Come this way.”

[003-134]Elnora followed down the hall and entered a long room with high

[003-135]panelled wainscoting, old English fireplace with an overmantel and

[003-136]closets of peculiar china filling the corners. At a bare table of oak,

[003-137]yellow as gold, sat a woman Elnora often had watched and followed

[003-138]covertly around the Limberlost. The Bird Woman was holding out a hand

[003-139]of welcome.

[003-140]“I heard!” she laughed. “A little pasteboard box, or just the mere word

[003-141]‘specimen,’ passes you at my door. If it is moths I hope you have

[003-142]hundreds. I’ve been very busy all summer and unable to collect, and I

[003-143]need so many. Sit down and lunch with me, while we talk it over. From

[003-144]the Limberlost, did you say?”

[003-145]“I live near the swamp,” replied Elnora. “Since it’s so cleared I dare

[003-146]go around the edge in daytime, though we are all afraid at night.”

[003-147]“What have you collected?” asked the Bird Woman, as she helped Elnora

[003-148]to sandwiches unlike any she ever before had tasted, salad that seemed

[003-149]to be made of many familiar things, and a cup of hot chocolate that

[003-150]would have delighted any hungry schoolgirl.

[003-151]“I am afraid I am bothering you for nothing, and imposing on you,” she

[003-152]said. “That ‘collected’ frightens me. I’ve only gathered. I always

[003-153]loved everything outdoors, so I made friends and playmates of them.

[003-154]When I learned that the moths die so soon, I saved them especially,

[003-155]because there seemed no wickedness in it.”

[003-156]“I have thought the same thing,” said the Bird Woman encouragingly.

[003-157]Then because the girl could not eat until she learned about the moths,

[003-158]the Bird Woman asked Elnora if she knew what kinds she had.

[003-159]“Not all of them,” answered Elnora. “Before Mr. [[duncan|Duncan]] moved away he

[003-160]often saw me near the edge of the swamp and he showed me the box he had

[003-161]fixed for [[freckles|Freckles]], and gave me the key. There were some books and

[003-162]things, so from that time on I studied and tried to take moths right,

[003-163]but I am afraid they are not what you want.”

[003-164]“Are they the big ones that fly mostly in June nights?” asked the Bird

[003-165]Woman.

[003-166]“Yes,” said Elnora. “Big gray ones with reddish markings, pale

[003-167]blue-green, yellow with lavender, and red and yellow.”

[003-168]“What do you mean by ‘red and yellow?’” asked the Bird Woman so quickly

[003-169]that the girl almost jumped.

[003-170]“Not exactly red,” explained Elnora, with tremulous voice. “A reddish,

[003-171]yellowish brown, with canary-coloured spots and gray lines on their

[003-172]wings.”

[003-173]“How many of them?” It was the same quick question.

[003-174]“I had over two hundred eggs,” said Elnora, “but some of them didn’t

[003-175]hatch, and some of the caterpillars died, but there must be at least a

[003-176]hundred perfect ones.”

[003-177]“Perfect! How perfect?” cried the Bird Woman.

[003-178]“I mean whole wings, no down gone, and all their legs and antennae,”

[003-179]faltered Elnora.

[003-180]“Young woman, that’s the rarest moth in America,” said the Bird Woman

[003-181]solemnly. “If you have a hundred of them, they are worth a hundred

[003-182]dollars according to my list. I can use all that are not damaged.”

[003-183]“What if they are not pinned right,” quavered Elnora.

[003-184]“If they are perfect, that does not make the slightest difference. I

[003-185]know how to soften them so that I can put them into any shape I choose.

[003-186]Where are they? When may I see them?”

[003-187]“They are in Freckles’s old case in the Limberlost,” said Elnora. “I

[003-188]couldn’t carry many for fear of breaking them, but I could bring a few

[003-189]after school.”

[003-190]“You come here at four,” said the Bird Woman, “and we will drive out

[003-191]with some specimen boxes, and a price list, and see what you have to

[003-192]sell. Are they your very own? Are you free to part with them?”

[003-193]“They are mine,” said Elnora. “No one but God knows I have them. Mr.

[003-194]Duncan gave me the books and the box. He told Freckles about me, and

[003-195]Freckles told him to give me all he left. He said for me to stick to

[003-196]the swamp and be brave, and my hour would come, and it has! I know most

[003-197]of them are all right, and oh, I do need the money!”

[003-198]“Could you tell me?” asked the Bird Woman softly.

[003-199]“You see the swamp and all the fields around it are so full,” explained

[003-200]Elnora. “Every day I felt smaller and smaller, and I wanted to know

[003-201]more and more, and pretty soon I grew desperate, just as Freckles did.

[003-202]But I am better off than he was, for I have his books, and I have a

[003-203]mother; even if she doesn’t care for me as other girls’ mothers do for

[003-204]them, it’s better than no one.”

[003-205]The Bird Woman’s glance fell, for the girl was not conscious of how

[003-206]much she was revealing. Her eyes were fixed on a black pitcher filled

[003-207]with goldenrod in the centre of the table and she was saying what she

[003-208]thought.

[003-209]“As long as I could go to the Brushwood school I was happy, but I

[003-210]couldn’t go further just when things were the most interesting, so I

[003-211]was determined I’d come to [[education-as-liberation|high school]] and mother wouldn’t consent. You

[003-212]see there’s plenty of land, but father was drowned when I was a baby,

[003-213]and mother and I can’t make money as men do. The taxes are higher every

[003-214]year, and she said it was too expensive. I wouldn’t give her any rest,

[003-215]until at last she bought me this dress, and these shoes and I came. It

[003-216]was awful!”

[003-217]“Do you live in that beautiful cabin at the northwest end of the

[003-218]swamp?” asked the Bird Woman.

[003-219]“Yes,” said Elnora.

[003-220]“I remember the place and a story about it, now. You entered the high

[003-221]school yesterday?”

[003-222]“Yes.”

[003-223]“It was rather bad?”

[003-224]“Rather bad!” echoed Elnora.

[003-225]The Bird Woman laughed.

[003-226]“You can’t tell me anything about that,” she said. “I once entered a

[003-227]city school straight from the country. My dress was brown calico, and

[003-228]my shoes were heavy.”

[003-229]The tears began to roll down Elnora’s cheeks.

[003-230]“Did they——?” she faltered.

[003-231]“They did!” said the Bird Woman. “All of it. I am sure they did not

[003-232]miss one least little thing.”

[003-233]Then she wiped away some tears that began coursing her cheeks, and

[003-234]laughed at the same time.

[003-235]“Where are they now?” asked Elnora suddenly.

[003-236]“They are widely scattered, but none of them have attained heights out

[003-237]of range. Some of the rich are poor, and some of the poor are rich.

[003-238]Some of the brightest died insane, and some of the dullest worked out

[003-239]high positions; some of the very worst to bear have gone out, and I

[003-240]frequently hear from others. Now I am here, able to remember it, and

[003-241]mingle laughter with what used to be all tears; for every day I have my

[003-242]beautiful work, and almost every day God sends some one like you to

[003-243]help me. What is your name, my girl?”

[003-244]“Elnora Comstock,” answered Elnora. “Yesterday on the board it changed

[003-245]to Cornstock, and for a minute I thought I’d die, but I can laugh over

[003-246]that already.”

[003-247]The Bird Woman arose and kissed her. “Finish your lunch,” she said,

[003-248]“and I will bring my price lists, and make a memorandum of what you

[003-249]think you have, so I will know how many boxes to prepare. And remember

[003-250]this: What you are lies with you. If you are lazy, and accept your lot,

[003-251]you may live in it. If you are willing to work, you can write your name

[003-252]anywhere you choose, among the only ones who live beyond the grave in

[003-253]this world, the people who write books that help, make exquisite music,

[003-254]carve statues, paint pictures, and work for others. Never mind the

[003-255]calico dress, and the coarse shoes. Work at your books, and before long

[003-256]you will hear yesterday’s tormentors boasting that they were once

[003-257]classmates of yours. ‘I could a tale unfold’——!”

[003-258]She laughingly left the room and Elnora sat thinking, until she

[003-259]remembered how hungry she was, so she ate the food, drank the hot

[003-260]chocolate and began to feel better.

[003-261]Then the Bird Woman came back and showed Elnora a long printed slip

[003-262]giving a list of graduated prices for moths, butterflies, and

[003-263]dragonflies.

[003-264]“Oh, do you want them!” exulted Elnora. “I have a few and I can get

[003-265]more by the thousand, with every colour in the world on their wings.”

[003-266]“Yes,” said the Bird Woman, “I will buy them, also the big moth

[003-267]caterpillars that are creeping everywhere now, and the cocoons that

[003-268]they will spin just about this time. I have a sneaking impression that

[003-269]the mystery, wonder, and the urge of their pure beauty, are going to

[003-270]force me to picture and paint our moths and put them into a book for

[003-271]all the world to see and know. We Limberlost people must not be selfish

[003-272]with the wonders God has given to us. We must share with those poor

[003-273]cooped-up city people the best we can. To send them a beautiful book,

[003-274]that is the way, is it not, little new friend of mine?”

[003-275]“Yes, oh yes!” cried Elnora. “And please God they find a way to earn

[003-276]the money to buy the books, as I have those I need so badly.”

[003-277]“I will pay good prices for all the moths you can find,” said the Bird

[003-278]Woman, “because you see I exchange them with foreign collectors. I want

[003-279]a complete series of the moths of America to trade with a German

[003-280]scientist, another with a man in India, and another in Brazil. Others I

[003-281]can exchange with home collectors for those of California and Canada,

[003-282]so you see I can use all you can raise, or find. The banker will buy

[003-283]stone axes, arrow points, and Indian pipes. There was a teacher from

[003-284]the city grade schools here to-day for specimens. There is a fund to

[003-285]supply the ward buildings. I’ll help you get in touch with that. They

[003-286]want leaves of different trees, flowers, grasses, moths, insects,

[003-287]birds’ nests and anything about birds.”

[003-288]Elnora’s eyes were blazing. “Had I better go back to school or open a

[003-289]bank account and begin being a millionaire? Uncle Wesley and I have a

[003-290]bushel of arrow points gathered, a stack of axes, pipes, skin-dressing

[003-291]tools, tubes and mortars. I don’t know how I ever shall wait three

[003-292]hours.”

[003-293]“You must go, or you will be late,” said the Bird Woman. “I will be

[003-294]ready at four.”

[003-295]After school closed Elnora, seated beside the Bird Woman, drove to

[003-296]Freckles’s room in the Limberlost. One at a time the beautiful big

[003-297]moths were taken from the interior of the old black case. Not a fourth

[003-298]of them could be moved that night and it was almost dark when the last

[003-299]box was closed, the list figured, and into Elnora’s trembling fingers

[003-300]were paid fifty-nine dollars and sixteen cents. Elnora clasped the

[003-301]money closely.

[003-302]“Oh you beautiful stuff!” she cried. “You are going to buy the books,

[003-303]pay the tuition, and take me to [[onabasha-high-school|high school]].”

[003-304]Then because she was a woman, she sat on a log and looked at her shoes.

[003-305]Long after the Bird Woman drove away Elnora remained. She had her

[003-306]problem, and it was a big one. If she told her mother, would she take

[003-307]the money to pay the taxes? If she did not tell her, how could she

[003-308]account for the books, and things for which she would spend it. At last

[003-309]she counted out what she needed for the next day, placed the remainder

[003-310]in the farthest corner of the case, and locked the door. She then

[003-311]filled the front of her skirt from a heap of arrow points beneath the

[003-312]case and started home.
