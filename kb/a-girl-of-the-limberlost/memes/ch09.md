# CHAPTER IX

[009-001]WHEREIN ELNORA DISCOVERS A VIOLIN, AND BILLY DISCIPLINES MARGARET

[009-002][[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] missed the little figure at [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]] the following morning.

[009-003]She slowly walked up the street and turned in at the wide entrance to

[009-004]the school grounds. She scarcely could comprehend that only a week ago

[009-005]she had gone there friendless, alone, and so sick at heart that she was

[009-006]physically ill. To-day she had decent [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], books, friends, and her

[009-007]mind was at ease to work on her studies.

[009-008]As she approached home that night the girl paused in amazement. Her

[009-009]mother had company, and she was laughing. Elnora entered the kitchen

[009-010]softly and peeped into the sitting-room. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] sat in her chair

[009-011]holding a book and every few seconds a soft chuckle broke into a real

[009-012]laugh. Mark Twain was doing his work; while Mrs. Comstock was not

[009-013]lacking in a sense of humour. Elnora entered the room before her mother

[009-014]saw her. Mrs. Comstock looked up with flushed face.

[009-015]“Where did you get this?” she demanded.

[009-016]“I bought it,” said Elnora.

[009-017]“Bought it! With all the taxes due!”

[009-018]“I paid for it out of my Indian money, mother,” said Elnora. “I

[009-019]couldn’t bear to spend so much on myself and nothing at all on you. I

[009-020]was afraid to buy the dress I should have liked to, and I thought the

[009-021]book would be company, while I was gone. I haven’t read it, but I do

[009-022]hope it’s good.”

[009-023]“Good! It’s the biggest piece of foolishness I have read in all my

[009-024]life. I’ve laughed all day, ever since I found it. I had a notion to go

[009-025]out and read some of it to the cows and see if they wouldn’t laugh.”

[009-026]“If it made you laugh, it’s a wise book,” said Elnora.

[009-027]“Wise!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “You can stake your life it’s a wise book.

[009-028]It takes the smartest man there is to do this kind of fooling,” and she

[009-029]began laughing again.

[009-030]Elnora, highly satisfied with her purchase, went to her room and put on

[009-031]her working clothes. Thereafter she made a point of bringing a book

[009-032]that she thought would interest her mother, from [[onabasha-library|the library]] every

[009-033]week, and leaving it on the sitting-room table. Each night she carried

[009-034]home at least two school books and studied until she had mastered the

[009-035]points of her lessons. She did her share of the work faithfully, and

[009-036]every available minute she was in the fields searching for cocoons, for

[009-037]the moths promised to become her largest source of income.

[009-038]She gathered baskets of nests, flowers, mosses, insects, and all sorts

[009-039]of [[nature-study|natural history]] specimens and sold them to the grade teachers. At

[009-040]first she tried to tell these instructors what to teach their pupils

[009-041]about the specimens; but recognizing how much more she knew than they,

[009-042]one after another begged her to study at home, and use her spare hours

[009-043]in school to exhibit and explain nature subjects to their pupils.

[009-044]Elnora loved the work, and she needed the money, for every few days

[009-045]some matter of expense arose that she had not expected.

[009-046]From the first week she had been received and invited with the crowd of

[009-047]girls in her class, and it was their custom in passing through the

[009-048]business part of the city to stop at the confectioners’ and take turns

[009-049]in treating to expensive candies, ice cream sodas, hot chocolate, or

[009-050]whatever they fancied. When first Elnora was asked she accepted without

[009-051]understanding. The second time she went because she seldom had tasted

[009-052]these things, and they were so delicious she could not resist. After

[009-053]that she went because she knew all about it, and had decided to go.

[009-054]She had spent half an hour on the log beside the trail in deep thought

[009-055]and had arrived at her conclusions. She worked harder than usual for

[009-056]the next week, but she seemed to thrive on work. It was October and the

[009-057]red leaves were falling when her first time came to treat. As the crowd

[009-058]flocked down the broad walk that night Elnora called, “Girls, it’s my

[009-059]treat to-night! Come on!”

[009-060]She led the way through the city to the grocery they patronized when

[009-061]they had a small spread, and entering came out with a basket, which she

[009-062]carried to the bridge on her home road. There she arranged the girls in

[009-063]two rows on the cement abutments and opening her basket she gravely

[009-064]offered each girl an exquisite little basket of bark, lined with red

[009-065]leaves, in one end of which nestled a juicy big red apple and in the

[009-066]other a spicy doughnut not an hour from [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]]’s frying

[009-067]basket.

[009-068]Another time she offered big balls of popped corn stuck together with

[009-069]maple sugar, and liberally sprinkled with beechnut kernels. Again it

[009-070]was hickory-nut kernels glazed with sugar, another time maple candy,

[009-071]and once a basket of warm pumpkin pies. She never made any apology, or

[009-072]offered any excuse. She simply gave what she could afford, and the

[009-073]change was as welcome to those city girls accustomed to sodas and

[009-074]French candy, as were these same things to Elnora surfeited on popcorn

[009-075]and pie. In her room was a little slip containing a record of the

[009-076]number of weeks in the school year, the times it would be her turn to

[009-077]treat and the dates on which such occasions would fall, with a number

[009-078]of suggestions beside each. Once the girls almost fought over a basket

[009-079]lined with yellow leaves, and filled with fat, very ripe red haws. In

[009-080]late October there was a riot over one which was lined with red leaves

[009-081]and contained big fragrant pawpaws frost-bitten to a perfect degree.

[009-082]Then hazel nuts were ripe, and once they served. One day Elnora at her

[009-083]wits’ end, explained to her mother that the girls had given her things

[009-084]and she wanted to treat them. Mrs. Comstock, with characteristic

[009-085]stubbornness, had said she would leave a basket at the grocery for her,

[009-086]but firmly declined to say what would be in it. All day Elnora

[009-087]struggled to keep her mind on her books. For hours she wavered in tense

[009-088]uncertainty. What would her mother do? Should she take the girls to the

[009-089]confectioner’s that night or risk the basket? Mrs. Comstock could make

[009-090]delicious things to eat, but would she?

[009-091]As they left the building Elnora made a final rapid mental calculation.

[009-092]She could not see her way clear to a decent treat for ten people for

[009-093]less than two dollars and if the basket proved to be nice, then the

[009-094]money would be wasted. She decided to risk it. As they went to the

[009-095]bridge the girls were betting on what the treat would be, and crowding

[009-096]near Elnora like spoiled small children. Elnora set down the basket.

[009-097]“Girls,” she said, “I don’t know what this is myself, so all of us are

[009-098]going to be surprised. Here goes!”

[009-099]She lifted the cover and perfumes from the land of spices rolled up. In

[009-100]one end of the basket lay ten enormous sugar cakes the tops of which

[009-101]had been liberally dotted with circles cut from stick candy. The candy

[009-102]had melted in baking and made small transparent wells of waxy sweetness

[009-103]and in the centre of each cake was a fat turtle made from a raisin with

[009-104]cloves for head and feet. The remainder of the basket was filled with

[009-105]big spiced pears that could be held by their stems while they were

[009-106]eaten. The girls shrieked and attacked the cookies, and of all the

[009-107]treats Elnora offered perhaps none was quite so long remembered as

[009-108]that.

[009-109]When Elnora took her basket, placed her books in it, and started home,

[009-110]all the girls went with her as far as the fence where she crossed the

[009-111]field to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]. At parting they kissed her good-bye. Elnora was a

[009-112]happy girl as she hurried home to thank her mother. She was happy over

[009-113]her books that night, and happy all the way to school the following

[009-114]morning.

[009-115]When the music swelled from the orchestra her heart almost broke with

[009-116]throbbing joy. For music always had affected her strangely, and since

[009-117]she had been comfortable enough in her surroundings to notice things,

[009-118]she had listened to every note to find what it was that literally hurt

[009-119]her heart, and at last she knew. It was the talking of the violins.

[009-120]They were human voices, and they spoke a language Elnora understood. It

[009-121]seemed to her that she must climb up on the stage, take the instruments

[009-122]from the fingers of the players and make them speak what was in her

[009-123]heart.

[009-124]That night she said to her mother, “I am perfectly crazy for a violin.

[009-125]I am sure I could play one, sure as I live. Did any one——” Elnora never

[009-126]completed that sentence.

[009-127]“Hush!” thundered Mrs. Comstock. “Be quiet! Never mention those things

[009-128]before me again—never as long as you live! I loathe them! They are a

[009-129]snare of the very devil himself! They were made to lure men and women

[009-130]from their homes and their honour. If ever I see you with one in your

[009-131]fingers I will smash it in pieces.”

[009-132]Naturally Elnora hushed, but she thought of nothing else after she had

[009-133]finished her lessons. At last there came a day when for some reason the

[009-134]leader of the orchestra left his violin on the grand piano. That

[009-135]morning Elnora made her first mistake in algebra. At noon, as soon as

[009-136]the building was empty, she slipped into the auditorium, found the side

[009-137]door which led to the stage, and going through the musicians’ entrance

[009-138]she took the violin. She carried it back into the little side room

[009-139]where the orchestra assembled, closed all the doors, opened the case

[009-140]and lifted out the instrument.

[009-141]She laid it on her breast, dropped her chin on it and drew the bow

[009-142]softly across the strings. One after another she tested the open notes.

[009-143]Gradually her stroke ceased to tremble and she drew the bow firmly.

[009-144]Then her fingers began to fall and softly, slowly she searched up and

[009-145]down those strings for sounds she knew. Standing in the middle of the

[009-146]floor, she tried over and over. It seemed scarcely a minute before the

[009-147]hall was filled with the sound of hurrying feet, and she was forced to

[009-148]put away the violin and go to her classes. The next day she prayed that

[009-149]the violin would be left again, but her petition was not answered. That

[009-150]night when she returned from the school she made an excuse to go down

[009-151]to see [[billy|Billy]]. He was engaged in hulling walnuts by driving them through

[009-152]holes in a board. His hands were protected by a pair of Margaret’s old

[009-153]gloves, but he had speckled his face generously. He appeared well, and

[009-154]greeted Elnora hilariously.

[009-155]“Me an’ the squirrels are laying up our winter stores,” he shouted.

[009-156]“Cos the cold is coming, an’ the snow an’ if we have any nuts we have

[009-157]to fix ’em now. But I’m ahead, cos [[wesley-sinton|Uncle Wesley]] made me this board, and

[009-158]I can hull a big pile while the old squirrel does only ist one with his

[009-159]teeth.”

[009-160]Elnora picked him up and kissed him. “Billy, are you happy?” she asked.

[009-161]“Yes, and so’s Snap,” answered Billy. “You ought to see him make the

[009-162]dirt fly when he gets after a chipmunk. I bet you he could dig up pa,

[009-163]if anybody wanted him to.”

[009-164]“Billy!” gasped Margaret as she came out to them.

[009-165]“Well, me and Snap don’t want him up, and I bet you [[jimmy|Jimmy]] and [[belle|Belle]]

[009-166]don’t, either. I ain’t been twisty inside once since I been here, and I

[009-167]don’t want to go away, and Snap don’t, either. He told me so.”

[009-168]“Billy! That is not true. Dogs can’t talk,” cautioned Margaret.

[009-169]“Then what makes you open the door when he asks you to?” demanded

[009-170]Billy.

[009-171]“Scratching and whining isn’t talking.”

[009-172]“Anyway, it’s the best Snap can talk, and you get up and do things he

[009-173]wants done. Chipmunks can talk too. You ought to hear them damn things

[009-174]holler when Snap gets them!”

[009-175]“Billy! When you want a cooky for supper and I don’t give it to you it

[009-176]is because you said a wrong word.”

[009-177]“Well, for——” Billy clapped his hand over his mouth and stained his

[009-178]face in swipes. “Well, for—anything! Did I go an’ forget again! The

[009-179]cookies will get all hard, won’t they? I bet you ten dollars I don’t

[009-180]say that any more.”

[009-181]He espied Wesley and ran to show him a walnut too big to go through the

[009-182]holes, and Elnora and Margaret entered the house.

[009-183]They talked of many things for a time and then Elnora said suddenly:

[009-184]“Aunt Margaret, I like music.”

[009-185]“I’ve noticed that in you all your life,” answered Margaret.

[009-186]“If dogs can’t talk, I can make a violin talk,” announced Elnora, and

[009-187]then in amazement watched the face of Margaret Sinton grow pale.

[009-188]“A violin!” she wavered. “Where did you get a violin?”

[009-189]“They fairly seemed to speak to me in the orchestra. One day the

[009-190]conductor left his in the auditorium, and I took it, and Aunt Margaret,

[009-191]I can make it do the wind in the swamp, the birds, and the animals. I

[009-192]can make any sound I ever heard on it. If I had a chance to practise a

[009-193]little, I could make it do the orchestra music, too. I don’t know how I

[009-194]know, but I do.”

[009-195]“Did—did you ever mention it to your mother?” faltered Margaret.

[009-196]“Yes, and she seems prejudiced against them. But oh, Aunt Margaret, I

[009-197]never felt so about anything, not even going to school. I just feel as

[009-198]if I’d die if I didn’t have one. I could keep it at school, and

[009-199]practise at noon a whole hour. Soon they’d ask me to play in the

[009-200]orchestra. I could keep it in the case and practise in the woods in

[009-201]summer. You’d let me play over here Sunday. Oh, Aunt Margaret, what

[009-202]does one cost? Would it be wicked for me to take of my money, and buy a

[009-203]very cheap one? I could play on the least expensive one made.”

[009-204]“Oh, no you couldn’t! A cheap machine makes cheap music. You got to

[009-205]have a fine fiddle to make it sing. But there’s no sense in your buying

[009-206]one. There isn’t a decent reason on earth why you shouldn’t have your

[009-207]fa——”

[009-208]“My father’s!” cried Elnora. She caught Margaret Sinton by the arm. “My

[009-209]father had a violin! He played it. That’s why I can! Where is it! Is it

[009-210]in our house? Is it in mother’s room?”

[009-211]“Elnora!” panted Margaret. “Your mother will kill me! She always hated

[009-212]it.”

[009-213]“Mother dearly loves music,” said Elnora.

[009-214]“Not when it took the man she loved away from her to make it!”

[009-215]“Where is my father’s violin?”

[009-216]“Elnora!”

[009-217]“I’ve never seen a picture of my father. I’ve never heard his name

[009-218]mentioned. I’ve never had a scrap that belonged to him. Was he my

[009-219]father, or am I a charity child like Billy, and so she hates me?”

[009-220]“She has good pictures of him. Seems she just can’t bear to hear him

[009-221]talked about. Of course, he was your father. They lived right there

[009-222]when you were born. She doesn’t dislike you; she merely tries to make

[009-223]herself think she does. There’s no sense in the world in you not having

[009-224]his violin. I’ve a great notion——”

[009-225]“Has mother got it?”

[009-226]“No. I’ve never heard her mention it. It was not at home when he—when

[009-227]he died.”

[009-228]“Do you know where it is?”

[009-229]“Yes. I’m the only person on earth who does, except the one who has

[009-230]it.”

[009-231]“Who is that?”

[009-232]“I can’t tell you, but I will see if they have it yet, and get it if I

[009-233]can. But if your mother finds it out she will never forgive me.”

[009-234]“I can’t help it,” said Elnora. “I want that violin.”

[009-235]“I’ll go to-morrow, and see if it has been destroyed.”

[009-236]“Destroyed! Oh, Aunt Margaret! Would any one dare?”

[009-237]“I hardly think so. It was a good instrument. He played it like a

[009-238]master.”

[009-239]“Tell me!” breathed Elnora.

[009-240]“His hair was red and curled more than yours, and his eyes were blue.

[009-241]He was tall, slim, and the very imp of mischief. He joked and teased

[009-242]all day until he picked up that violin. Then his head bent over it, and

[009-243]his eyes got big and earnest. He seemed to listen as if he first heard

[009-244]the notes, and then copied them. Sometimes he drew the bow trembly,

[009-245]like he wasn’t sure it was right, and he might have to try again. He

[009-246]could almost drive you crazy when he wanted to, and no man that ever

[009-247]lived could make you dance as he could. He made it all up as he went.

[009-248]He seemed to listen for his dancing music, too. It appeared to come to

[009-249]him; he’d begin to play and you had to keep time. You couldn’t be

[009-250]still; he loved to sweep a crowd around with that bow of his. I think

[009-251]it was the thing you call inspiration. I can see him now, his handsome

[009-252]head bent, his cheeks red, his eyes snapping, and that bow going across

[009-253]the strings, and driving us like sheep. He always kept his body

[009-254]swinging, and he loved to play. He often slighted his work shamefully,

[009-255]and sometimes her a little; that is why she hated it—Elnora, what are

[009-256]you making me do?”

[009-257]The tears were rolling down Elnora’s cheeks. “Oh, Aunt Margaret,” she

[009-258]sobbed. “Why haven’t you told me about him sooner? I feel as if you had

[009-259]given my father to me living, so that I could touch him. I can see him,

[009-260]too! Why didn’t you ever tell me before? Go on! Go on!”

[009-261]“I can’t, Elnora! I’m scared silly. I never meant to say anything. If I

[009-262]hadn’t promised her not to talk of him to you she wouldn’t have let you

[009-263]come here. She made me swear it.”

[009-264]“But why? Why? Was he a shame? Was he disgraced?”

[009-265]“Maybe it was that unjust feeling that took possession of her when she

[009-266]couldn’t help him from the swamp. She had to blame some one, or go

[009-267]crazy, so she took it out on you. At times, those first ten years, if I

[009-268]had talked to you, and you had repeated anything to her, she might have

[009-269]struck you too hard. She was not master of herself. You must be patient

[009-270]with her, Elnora. God only knows what she has gone through, but I think

[009-271]she is a little better, lately.”

[009-272]“So do I,” said Elnora. “She seems more interested in my clothes, and

[009-273]she fixes me such delicious lunches that the girls bring fine candies

[009-274]and cake and beg to trade. I gave half my lunch for a box of candy one

[009-275]day, brought it home to her, and told her. Since, she has wanted me to

[009-276]carry a market basket and treat the crowd every day, she was so

[009-277]pleased. Life has been too monotonous for her. I think she enjoys even

[009-278]the little change made by my going and coming. She sits up half the

[009-279]night to read the library books I bring, but she is so stubborn she

[009-280]won’t even admit that she touches them. Tell me more about my father.”

[009-281]“Wait until I see if I can find the violin.”

[009-282]So Elnora went home in suspense, and that night she added to her

[009-283]prayers: “Dear Lord, be merciful to my father, and oh, do help Aunt

[009-284]Margaret to get his violin.”

[009-285]Wesley and Billy came in to supper tired and hungry. Billy ate

[009-286]heartily, but his eyes often rested on a plate of tempting cookies, and

[009-287]when Wesley offered them to the boy he reached for one. Margaret was

[009-288]compelled to explain that cookies were forbidden that night.

[009-289]“What!” said Wesley. “Wrong words been coming again. Oh Billy, I do

[009-290]wish you could remember! I can’t sit and eat cookies before a little

[009-291]boy who has none. I’ll have to put mine back, too.” Billy’s face

[009-292]twisted in despair.

[009-293]“Aw go on!” he said gruffly, but his chin was jumping, for Wesley was

[009-294]his idol.

[009-295]“Can’t do it,” said Wesley. “It would choke me.”

[009-296]Billy turned to Margaret. “You make him,” he appealed.

[009-297]“He can’t, Billy,” said Margaret. “I know how he feels. You see, I

[009-298]can’t myself.”

[009-299]Then Billy slid from his chair, ran to the couch, buried his face in

[009-300]the pillow and cried heart-brokenly. Wesley hurried to [[sinton-barn|the barn]], and

[009-301]Margaret to the kitchen. When the dishes were washed Billy slipped from

[009-302]the back door.

[009-303]Wesley piling hay into the mangers heard a sound behind him and

[009-304]inquired, “That you, Billy?”

[009-305]“Yes,” answered Billy, “and it’s all so dark you can’t see me now,

[009-306]isn’t it?”

[009-307]“Well, mighty near,” answered Wesley.

[009-308]“Then you stoop down and open your mouth.”

[009-309]Sinton had shared bites of apple and nuts for weeks, for Billy had not

[009-310]learned how to eat anything without dividing with Jimmy and Belle.

[009-311]Since he had been separated from them, he shared with Wesley and

[009-312]Margaret. So he bent over the boy and received an instalment of cooky

[009-313]that almost choked him.

[009-314]“Now you can eat it!” shouted Billy in delight. “It’s all dark! I can’t

[009-315]see what you’re doing at all!”

[009-316]Wesley picked up the small figure and set the boy on the back of a

[009-317]horse to bring his face level so that they could talk as men. He never

[009-318]towered from his height above Billy, but always lifted the little soul

[009-319]when important matters were to be discussed.

[009-320]“Now what a dandy scheme,” he commented. “Did you and Aunt Margaret fix

[009-321]it up?”

[009-322]“No. She ain’t had hers yet. But I got one for her. Ist as soon as you

[009-323]eat yours, I am going to take hers, and feed her first time I find her

[009-324]in the dark.”

[009-325]“But Billy, where did you get the cookies? You know Aunt Margaret said

[009-326]you were not to have any.”

[009-327]“I ist took them,” said Billy, “I didn’t take them for me. I ist took

[009-328]them for you and her.”

[009-329]Wesley thought fast. In the warm darkness of the barn the horses

[009-330]crunched their corn, a rat gnawed at a corner of the granary, and among

[009-331]the rafters the white pigeon cooed a soft sleepy note to his dusky

[009-332]mate.

[009-333]“Did—did—I steal?” wavered Billy.

[009-334]Wesley’s big hands closed until he almost hurt the boy.

[009-335]“No!” he said vehemently. “That is too big a word. You made a mistake.

[009-336]You were trying to be a fine little man, but you went at it the wrong

[009-337]way. You only made a mistake. All of us do that, Billy. The world grows

[009-338]that way. When we make mistakes we can see them; that teaches us to be

[009-339]more careful the next time, and so we learn.”

[009-340]“How wouldn’t it be a mistake?”

[009-341]“If you had told Aunt Margaret what you wanted to do, and asked her for

[009-342]the cookies she would have given them to you.”

[009-343]“But I was ’fraid she wouldn’t, and you ist had to have it.”

[009-344]“Not if it was wrong for me to have it, Billy. I don’t want it that

[009-345]much.”

[009-346]“Must I take it back?”

[009-347]“You think hard, and decide yourself.”

[009-348]“Lift me down,” said Billy, after a silence, “I got to put this in the

[009-349]jar, and tell her.”

[009-350]Wesley set the boy on the floor, but as he did so he paused one second

[009-351]and strained him close to his breast.

[009-352]Margaret sat in her chair sewing; Billy slipped in and crept beside

[009-353]her. The little face was lined with tragedy.

[009-354]“Why Billy, whatever is the matter?” she cried as she dropped her

[009-355]sewing and held out her arms. Billy stood back. He gripped his little

[009-356]fists tight and squared his shoulders. “I got to be shut up in the

[009-357]closet,” he said.

[009-358]“Oh Billy! What an unlucky day! What have you done now?”

[009-359]“I stold!” gulped Billy. “He said it was ist a mistake, but it was

[009-360]worser ’an that. I took something you told me I wasn’t to have.”

[009-361]“Stole!” Margaret was in despair. “What, Billy?”

[009-362]“Cookies!” answered Billy in equal trouble.

[009-363]“Billy!” wailed Margaret. “How could you?”
