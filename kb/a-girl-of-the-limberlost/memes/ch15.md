# CHAPTER XV

[015-001]WHEREIN MRS. COMSTOCK FACES THE ALMIGHTY, AND PHILIP AMMON WRITES A

[015-002]LETTER

[015-003][[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] were finishing breakfast the following morning

[015-004]when they heard a cheery whistle down the road. Elnora with surprised

[015-005]eyes looked at her mother.

[015-006]“Could that be Mr. Ammon?” she questioned.

[015-007]“I did not expect him so soon,” commented Mrs. Comstock.

[015-008]It was sunrise, but the musician was [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. He appeared stronger

[015-009]than on yesterday.

[015-010]“I hope I am not too early,” he said. “I am consumed with anxiety to

[015-011]learn if we have made a catch. If we have, we should beat the birds to

[015-012]it. I promised [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]] to put on my waders and keep dry for a few

[015-013]days yet, when I go to the woods. Let’s hurry! I am afraid of crows.

[015-014]There might be a rare moth.”

[015-015]The sun was topping [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] when they started. As they neared

[015-016]the place Philip stopped.

[015-017]“Now we must use great caution,” he said. “The lights and the odours

[015-018]always attract numbers that don’t settle on the baited trees. Every

[015-019]bush, shrub, and limb may hide a specimen we want.”

[015-020]So they approached with much care.

[015-021]“There is something, anyway!” cried Philip.

[015-022]“There are moths! I can see them!” exulted Elnora.

[015-023]“Those you see are fast enough. It’s the ones for which you must search

[015-024]that will escape. The grasses are dripping, and I have boots, so you

[015-025]look beside the path while I take the outside,” suggested Ammon.

[015-026]Mrs. Comstock wanted to hunt moths, but she was timid about making a

[015-027]wrong movement, so she wisely sat on a log and watched Philip and

[015-028]Elnora to learn how they proceeded. Back in the deep woods a hermit

[015-029]thrush was singing his chant to the rising sun. Orioles were sowing the

[015-030]pure, sweet air with notes of gold, poured out while on wing. The

[015-031]robins were only chirping now, for their morning songs had awakened all

[015-032]the other birds an hour ago. Scolding red-wings tilted on half the

[015-033]bushes. Excepting late species of haws, tree bloom was almost gone, but

[015-034]wild flowers made the path border and all the wood floor a riot of

[015-035]colour. Elnora, born among such scenes, worked eagerly, but to the city

[015-036]man, recently from a hospital, they seemed too good to miss. He

[015-037]frequently stooped to examine a flower face, paused to listen intently

[015-038]to the thrush or lifted his head to see the gold flash which

[015-039]accompanied the oriole’s trailing notes. So Elnora uttered the first

[015-040]cry, as she softly lifted branches and peered among the grasses.

[015-041]“My find!” she called. “Bring the box, mother!”

[015-042]Philip came hurrying also. When they reached her she stood on the path

[015-043]holding a pair of moths. Her eyes were wide with excitement, her cheeks

[015-044]pink, her red lips parted, and on the hand she held out to them clung a

[015-045]pair of delicate blue-green moths, with white bodies, and touches of

[015-046]lavender and straw colour. All around her lay flower-brocaded grasses,

[015-047]behind the deep green background of the forest, while the sun slowly

[015-048]sifted gold from heaven to burnish her hair. Mrs. Comstock heard a

[015-049]sharp breath behind her.

[015-050]“Oh, what a picture!” exulted Philip at her shoulder. “She is

[015-051]absolutely and altogether lovely! I’d give a small fortune for that

[015-052]faithfully set on canvas!”

[015-053]He picked the box from Mrs. Comstock’s fingers and slowly advanced with

[015-054]it. Elnora held down her hand and transferred the moths. Philip closed

[015-055]the box carefully, but the watching mother saw that his eyes were

[015-056]following the girl’s face. He was not making the slightest attempt to

[015-057]conceal his admiration.

[015-058]“I wonder if a woman ever did anything lovelier than to find a pair of

[015-059]Luna moths on a forest path, early on a perfect June morning,” he said

[015-060]to Mrs. Comstock, when he returned the box.

[015-061]She glanced at Elnora who was intently searching the bushes.

[015-062]“Look here, young man,” said Mrs. Comstock. “You seem to find that girl

[015-063]of mine about right.”

[015-064]“I could suggest no improvement,” said Philip. “I never saw a more

[015-065]attractive girl anywhere. She seems absolutely perfect to me.”

[015-066]“Then suppose you don’t start any scheme calculated to spoil her!”

[015-067]proposed Mrs. Comstock dryly. “I don’t think you can, or that any man

[015-068]could, but I’m not taking any risks. You asked to come here to help in

[015-069]this work. We are both glad to have you, if you confine yourself to

[015-070]work; but it’s the least you can do to leave us as you find us.”

[015-071]“I beg your pardon!” said Philip. “I intended no offence. I admire her

[015-072]as I admire any perfect creation.”

[015-073]“And nothing in all this world spoils the average girl so quickly and

[015-074]so surely,” said Mrs. Comstock. She raised her voice. “Elnora, fasten

[015-075]up that tag of hair over your left ear. These bushes muss you so you

[015-076]remind me of a sheep poking its nose through a hedge fence.”

[015-077]Mrs. Comstock started down the path toward the log again, when she

[015-078]reached it she called sharply: “Elnora, come here! I believe I have

[015-079]found something myself.”

[015-080]The “something” was a Citheronia Regalis which had emerged from its

[015-081]case on the soft earth under the log. It climbed up the wood, its stout

[015-082]legs dragging a big pursy body, while it wildly flapped tiny wings the

[015-083]size of a man’s thumb-nail. Elnora gave one look and a cry which

[015-084]brought Philip.

[015-085]“That’s the rarest moth in America!” he announced. “Mrs. Comstock,

[015-086]you’ve gone up head. You can put that in a box with a screen cover

[015-087]to-night, and attract half a dozen, possibly.”

[015-088]“Is it rare, Elnora?” inquired Mrs. Comstock, as if no one else knew.

[015-089]“It surely is,” answered Elnora. “If we can find it a mate to-night, it

[015-090]will lay from two hundred and fifty to three hundred eggs to-morrow.

[015-091]With any luck at all I can raise two hundred caterpillars from them. I

[015-092]did once before. And they are worth a dollar apiece.”

[015-093]“Was the one I killed like that?”

[015-094]“No. That was a different moth, but its life processes were the same as

[015-095]this. [[bird-woman|The Bird Woman]] calls this the King of the Poets.”

[015-096]“Why does she?”

[015-097]“Because it is named for Citheron who was a poet, and regalis refers to

[015-098]a king. You mustn’t touch it or you may stunt wing development. You

[015-099]watch and don’t let that moth out of sight, or anything touch it. When

[015-100]the wings are expanded and hardened we will put it in a box.”

[015-101]“I am afraid it will race itself to death,” objected Mrs. Comstock.

[015-102]“That’s a part of the game,” said Philip. “It is starting circulation

[015-103]now. When the right moment comes, it will stop and expand its wings. If

[015-104]you watch closely you can see them expand.”

[015-105]Presently the moth found a rough projection of bark and clung with its

[015-106]feet, back down, its wings hanging. The body was an unusual orange red,

[015-107]the tiny wings were gray, striped with the red and splotched here and

[015-108]there with markings of canary yellow. Mrs. Comstock watched

[015-109]breathlessly. Presently she slipped from the log and knelt to secure a

[015-110]better view.

[015-111]“Are its wings developing?” called Elnora.

[015-112]“They are growing larger and the markings coming stronger every

[015-113]minute.”

[015-114]“Let’s watch, too,” said Elnora to Philip.

[015-115]They came and looked over Mrs. Comstock’s shoulder. Lower drooped the

[015-116]gay wings, wider they spread, brighter grew the markings as if laid off

[015-117]in geometrical patterns. They could hear Mrs. Comstock’s tense breath

[015-118]and see her absorbed expression.

[015-119]“Young people,” she said solemnly, “if your studying science and the

[015-120]elements has ever led you to feel that things just happen, kind of

[015-121]evolve by chance, as it were, this sight will be good for you. Maybe

[015-122]earth and air accumulate, but it takes the wisdom of the Almighty God

[015-123]to devise the wing of a moth. If there ever was a miracle, this whole

[015-124]process is one. Now, as I understand it, this creature is going to keep

[015-125]on spreading those wings, until they grow to size and harden to

[015-126]strength sufficient to bear its body. Then it flies away, mates with

[015-127]its kind, lays its eggs on the leaves of a certain tree, and the eggs

[015-128]hatch tiny caterpillars which eat just that kind of leaves, and the

[015-129]worms grow and grow, and take on different forms and colours until at

[015-130]last they are big caterpillars six inches long, with large horns. Then

[015-131]they burrow into the earth, build a water-proof house around themselves

[015-132]from material which is inside them, and lie through rain and freezing

[015-133]cold for months. A year from egg laying they come out like this, and

[015-134]begin the process all over again. They don’t eat, they don’t see

[015-135]distinctly, they live but a few days, and fly only at night; then they

[015-136]drop off easy, but the process goes on.”

[015-137]A shivering movement went over the moth. The wings drooped and spread

[015-138]wider. Mrs. Comstock sank into soft awed tones.

[015-139]“There never was a moment in my life,” she said, “when I felt so in the

[015-140]Presence, as I do now. I feel as if the Almighty were so real, and so

[015-141]near, that I could reach out and touch Him, as I could this wonderful

[015-142]work of His, if I dared. I feel like saying to Him: ‘To the extent of

[015-143]my brain power I realize Your presence, and all it is in me to

[015-144]comprehend of Your power. Help me to learn, even this late, the lessons

[015-145]of Your wonderful creations. Help me to unshackle and expand my soul to

[015-146]the fullest realization of Your wonders. Almighty God, make me bigger,

[015-147]make me broader!’”

[015-148]The moth climbed to the end of the projection, up it a little way, then

[015-149]suddenly reversed its wings, turned the hidden sides out and dropped

[015-150]them beside its abdomen, like a large fly. The upper side of the wings,

[015-151]thus exposed, was far richer colour, more exquisite texture than the

[015-152]under, and they slowly half lifted and drooped again. Mrs. Comstock

[015-153]turned her face to Philip.

[015-154]“Am I an old fool, or do you feel it, too?” she half whispered.

[015-155]“You are wiser than you ever have been before,” answered he. “I feel

[015-156]it, also.”

[015-157]“And I,” breathed Elnora.

[015-158]The moth spread its wings, shivered them tremulously, opening and

[015-159]closing them rapidly. Philip handed the box to Elnora.

[015-160]She shook her head.

[015-161]“I can’t take that one,” she said. “Give her freedom.”

[015-162]“But, Elnora,” protested Mrs. Comstock, “I don’t want to let her go.

[015-163]She’s mine. She’s the first one I ever found this way. Can’t you put

[015-164]her in a big box, and let her live, without hurting her? I can’t bear

[015-165]to let her go. I want to learn all about her.”

[015-166]“Then watch while we gather these on the trees,” said Elnora. “We will

[015-167]take her home until night and then decide what to do. She won’t fly for

[015-168]a long time yet.”

[015-169]Mrs. Comstock settled on the ground, gazing at the moth. Elnora and

[015-170]Philip went to the baited trees, placing several large moths and a

[015-171]number of smaller ones in the cyanide jar, and searching the bushes

[015-172]beyond where they found several paired specimens of differing families.

[015-173]When they returned Elnora showed her mother how to hold her hand before

[015-174]the moth so that it would climb upon her fingers. Then they started

[015-175]back to [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], Elnora and Philip leading the way; Mrs. Comstock

[015-176]followed slowly, stepping with great care lest she stumble and injure

[015-177]the moth. Her face wore a look of comprehension, in her eyes was an

[015-178]exalted light. On she came to the blue-bordered pool lying beside her

[015-179]path.

[015-180]A turtle scrambled from a log and splashed into the water, while a

[015-181]red-wing shouted, “O-ka-lee!” to her. Mrs. Comstock paused and looked

[015-182]intently at the slime-covered quagmire, framed in a flower riot and

[015-183]homed over by sweet-voiced birds. Then she gazed at the thing of

[015-184]incomparable beauty clinging to her fingers and said softly: “If you

[015-185]had known about wonders like these in the days of your youth, Robert

[015-186]Comstock, could you ever have done what you did?”

[015-187]Elnora missed her mother, and turning to look for her, saw her standing

[015-188]beside [[limberlost-pool|the pool]]. Would the old fascination return? A panic of fear

[015-189]seized the girl. She went back swiftly.

[015-190]“Are you afraid she is going?” Elnora asked. “If you are, cup your

[015-191]other hand over her for shelter. Carrying her through this air and in

[015-192]the hot sunshine will dry her wings and make them ready for flight very

[015-193]quickly. You can’t trust her in such air and light as you can in the

[015-194]cool dark woods.”

[015-195]While she talked she took hold of her mother’s sleeve, anxiously

[015-196]smiling a pitiful little smile that Mrs. Comstock understood. Philip

[015-197]set his load at the back door, returning to hold open [[comstock-garden|the garden]] gate

[015-198]for Elnora and Mrs. Comstock. He reached it in time to see them

[015-199]standing together beside the pool. The mother bent swiftly and kissed

[015-200]the girl on the lips. Philip turned and was busily hunting moths on the

[015-201]raspberry bushes when they reached the gate. And so excellent are the

[015-202]rewards of attending your own business, that he found a Promethea on a

[015-203]lilac in a corner; a moth of such rare wine-coloured, velvety shades

[015-204]that it almost sent Mrs. Comstock to her knees again. But this one was

[015-205]fully developed, able to fly, and had to be taken into the cabin

[015-206]hurriedly. Mrs. Comstock stood in the middle of the room holding up her

[015-207]Regalis.

[015-208]“Now what must I do?” she asked.

[015-209]Elnora glanced at Philip Ammon. Their eyes met and both of them smiled;

[015-210]he with amusement at the tall, spare figure, with dark eyes and white

[015-211]crown, asking the childish question so confidingly; and Elnora with

[015-212]pride. She was beginning to appreciate the character of her mother.

[015-213]“How would you like to sit and see her finish development? I’ll get

[015-214]dinner,” proposed the girl.

[015-215]After they had dined, [[love-and-courtship|Philip and Elnora]] carried the dishes to the

[015-216]kitchen, brought out boxes, sheets of cork, pins, ink, paper slips and

[015-217]everything necessary for mounting and classifying the moths they had

[015-218]taken. When the housework was finished Mrs. Comstock with her ruffle

[015-219]sat near, watching and listening. She remembered all they said that she

[015-220]understood, and when uncertain she asked questions. Occasionally she

[015-221]laid down her work to straighten some flower which needed attention or

[015-222]to search the garden for a bug for the grosbeak. In one of these

[015-223]absences Elnora said to Philip: “These replace quite a number of the

[015-224]moths I lost for the man of India. With a week of such luck, I could

[015-225]almost begin to talk college again.”

[015-226]“There is no reason why you should not have the week and the luck,”

[015-227]said he. “I have taken moths until the middle of August, though I

[015-228]suspect one is more likely to find late ones in the north where it is

[015-229]colder than here. The next week is hay-time, but we can count on a few

[015-230]double-brooders and strays, and by working the exchange method for all

[015-231]it is worth, I think we can complete the collection again.”

[015-232]“You almost make me hope,” said Elnora, “but I must not allow myself. I

[015-233]don’t truly think I can replace all I lost, not even with your help. If

[015-234]I could, I scarcely see my way clear to leave mother this winter. I

[015-235]have found her so recently, and she is so precious, I can’t risk losing

[015-236]her again. I am going to take the nature position in the [[onabasha|Onabasha]]

[015-237]schools, and I shall be most happy doing the work. Only, these are a

[015-238]temptation.”

[015-239]“I wish you might go to college this fall with the other girls,” said

[015-240]Philip. “I feel that if you don’t you never will. Isn’t there some

[015-241]way?”

[015-242]“I can’t see it if there is, and I really don’t want to leave mother.”

[015-243]“Well, mother is mighty glad to hear it,” said Mrs. Comstock, entering

[015-244][[grape-arbour|the arbour]].

[015-245]Philip noticed that her face was pale, her lips quivering, her voice

[015-246]cold.

[015-247]“I was telling your daughter that she should go to college this

[015-248]winter,” he explained, “but she says she doesn’t want to leave you.”

[015-249]“If she wants to go, I wish she could,” said Mrs. Comstock, a look of

[015-250]relief spreading over her face.

[015-251]“Oh, all girls want to go to college,” said Philip. “It’s the only

[015-252]proper place to learn bridge and embroidery; not to mention midnight

[015-253]lunches of mixed pickles and fruit cake, and all the delights of the

[015-254]sororities.”

[015-255]“I have thought for years of [[elnoras-college-dream|going to college]],” said Elnora, “but I

[015-256]never thought of any of those things.”

[015-257]“That is because your [[education-as-liberation|education]] in fudge and bridge has been sadly

[015-258]neglected,” said Philip. “You should hear my sister [[polly-ammon|Polly]]! This was her

[015-259]final year! Lunches and sororities were all I heard her mention, until

[015-260][[tom-levering|Tom Levering]] came on deck; now he is the leading subject. I can’t see

[015-261]from her daily conversation that she knows half as much really worth

[015-262]knowing as you do, but she’s ahead of you miles on fun.”

[015-263]“Oh, we had some good times in [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]],” said Elnora. “Life

[015-264]hasn’t been all work and study. Is [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] a college girl?”

[015-265]“No. She is the very selectest kind of a private boarding-school girl.”

[015-266]“Who is she?” asked Mrs. Comstock.

[015-267]Philip opened his lips.

[015-268]“She is a girl in Chicago, that Mr. Ammon knows very well,” said

[015-269]Elnora. “She is beautiful and rich, and a friend of his sister’s. Or,

[015-270]didn’t you say that?”

[015-271]“I don’t remember, but she is,” said Philip. “This moth needs an

[015-272]alcohol bath to remove the dope.”

[015-273]“Won’t the down come, too?” asked Elnora anxiously.

[015-274]“No. You watch and you will see it come out, as Polly would say, ‘a

[015-275]perfectly good’ moth.”

[015-276]“Is your sister younger than you?” inquired Elnora.

[015-277]“Yes,” said Philip, “but she is three years older than you. She is the

[015-278]dearest sister in all the world. I’d love to see her now.”

[015-279]“Why don’t you send for her,” suggested Elnora. “Perhaps she’d like to

[015-280]help us catch moths.”

[015-281]“Yes, I think Polly in a Virot hat, Picot embroidered frock and

[015-282]three-inch heels would take more moths than any one who ever tried the

[015-283]Limberlost,” laughed Philip.

[015-284]“Well, you find many of them, and you are her brother.”

[015-285]“Yes, but that is different. Father was reared in Onabasha, and he

[015-286]loved the country. He trained me his way and mother took charge of

[015-287]Polly. I don’t quite understand it. Mother is a great home body

[015-288]herself, but she did succeed in making Polly strictly ornamental.”

[015-289]“Does Tom Levering need a ‘strictly ornamental’ girl?”

[015-290]“You are too matter of fact! Too ‘strictly’ material. He needs a

[015-291]darling girl who will love him plenty, and Polly is that.”

[015-292]“Well, then, does [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] need a ‘strictly ornamental’ girl?”

[015-293]“No!” cried Philip. “You are ornament enough for the Limberlost. I have

[015-294]changed my mind. I don’t want Polly here. She would not enjoy catching

[015-295]moths, or anything we do.”

[015-296]“She might,” persisted Elnora. “You are her brother, and surely you

[015-297]care for these things.”

[015-298]“The argument does not hold,” said Philip. “Polly and I do not like the

[015-299]same things when we are at home, but we are very fond of each other.

[015-300]The member of my family who would go crazy about this is my father. I

[015-301]wish he could come, if only for a week. I’d send for him, but he is

[015-302]tied up in preparing some papers for a great corporation case this

[015-303]summer. He likes the country. It was his vote that brought me here.”

[015-304]Philip leaned back against the arbour, watching the grosbeak as it

[015-305]hunted food between a tomato vine and a day lily. Elnora set him to

[015-306]making labels, and when he finished them he asked permission to write a

[015-307]letter. He took no pains to conceal his page, and from where she sat

[015-308]opposite him, Elnora could not look his way without reading: “My

[015-309]dearest Edith.” He wrote busily for a time and then sat staring across

[015-310]the garden.

[015-311]“Have you run out of material so quickly?” asked Elnora.

[015-312]“That’s about it,” said Philip. “I have said that I am getting well as

[015-313]rapidly as possible, that the air is fine, the folks at Uncle Doc’s all

[015-314]well, and entirely too good to me; that I am spending most of my time

[015-315]in the country helping catch moths for a collection, which is splendid

[015-316]exercise; now I can’t think of another thing that will be interesting.”

[015-317]There was a burst of exquisite notes in the maple.

[015-318]“Put in the grosbeak,” suggested Elnora. “Tell her you are so friendly

[015-319]with him you feed him potato bugs.”

[015-320]Philip lowered the pen to the sheet, bent forward, then hesitated.

[015-321]“Blest if I do!” he cried. “She’d think a grosbeak was a depraved

[015-322]person with a large nose. She’d never dream that it was a black-robed

[015-323]lover, with a breast of snow and a crimson heart. She doesn’t care for

[015-324]hungry babies and potato bugs. I shall write that to father. He will

[015-325]find it delightful.”

[015-326]Elnora deftly picked up a moth, pinned it and placed its wings. She

[015-327]straightened the antennae, drew each leg into position and set it in

[015-328]perfectly lifelike manner. As she lifted her work to see if she had it

[015-329]right, she glanced at Philip. He was still frowning and hesitating over

[015-330]the paper.

[015-331]“I dare you to let me dictate a couple of paragraphs.”

[015-332]“Done!” cried Philip. “Go slowly enough that I can write it.”

[015-333]Elnora laughed gleefully.

[015-334]“I am writing this,” she began, “in an old grape arbour in the country,

[015-335]near a log cabin where I had my dinner. From where I sit I can see

[015-336]directly into the home of the next-door neighbour on the west. His name

[015-337]is R. B. Grosbeak. From all I have seen of him, he is a gentleman of

[015-338]the old school; the oldest school there is, no doubt. He always wears a

[015-339]black suit and cap and a white vest, decorated with one large red

[015-340]heart, which I think must be the emblem of some ancient order. I have

[015-341]been here a number of times, and I never have seen him wear anything

[015-342]else, or his wife appear in other than a brown dress with touches of

[015-343]white.

[015-344]“It has appealed to me at times that she was a shade neglectful of her

[015-345]home duties, but he does not seem to feel that way. He cheerfully stays

[015-346]in the sitting-room, while she is away having a good time, and sings

[015-347]while he cares for the four small children. I must tell you about his

[015-348]music. I am sure he never saw inside a conservatory. I think he merely

[015-349]picked up what he knows by ear and without vocal training, but there is

[015-350]a tenderness in his tones, a depth of pure melody, that I never have

[015-351]heard surpassed. It may be that I think more of his music than that of

[015-352]some other good vocalists hereabout, because I see more of him and

[015-353]appreciate his devotion to his home life.

[015-354]“I just had an encounter with him at the west fence, and induced him to

[015-355]carry a small gift to his children. When I see the perfect harmony in

[015-356]which he lives, and the depth of content he and the brown lady find in

[015-357]life, I am almost persuaded to— Now this is going to be poetry,” said

[015-358]Elnora. “Move your pen over here and begin with a quote and a cap.”

[015-359]Philip’s face had been an interesting study while he wrote her

[015-360]sentences. Now he gravely set the pen where she indicated, and Elnora
