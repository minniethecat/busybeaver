## Summary

One afternoon in the grape arbour of the Sinton farm, [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] asks permission to write a letter. He takes no pains to conceal the page, and from where she sits opposite him, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] reads the salutation: "My dearest Edith." (015-308) Philip writes to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] about his recovery and [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] summer, but when he runs out of material, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] playfully dares him to let her dictate a postscript. (015-331) What follows is a scene of exquisite tension — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] dictating vivid descriptions of the country cabin and grape arbour to the woman who claims Philip's future, and Philip writing her words in his own hand. (015-334)

## Context

By mid-summer, [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] had been in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] for weeks, regaining his health after typhoid fever and spending nearly every day with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]. Their relationship had deepened through shared moth hunts, violin music, and long conversations in [[comstock-garden|the garden]] and swamp. Yet Philip remained formally engaged to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] of Chicago — a fact that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] knew but that neither of them directly confronted.

The letter-writing scene takes place after dinner, with [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] sitting nearby working on her ruffle and listening. (015-218) [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and Philip have been mounting and classifying moths on sheets of cork, pinning specimens and writing labels. (015-216) When Philip finishes his labeling tasks, he asks to write a letter — and the quiet intimacy of the scene shifts into something more complicated.

## Key Participants

**[[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]** — Philip writes to Edith with a guilty openness, making no effort to hide his words from [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. His letter is factual — he reports on his health, the country, the moths — but he quickly runs out of things to say to the woman he is supposed to marry. (015-312) When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] offers to dictate a postscript, he agrees immediately: "Done! Go slowly enough that I can write it." (015-332) His face, as [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] dictates her witty description of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], is "an interesting study" (015-359) — a man caught between two worlds, two women, two versions of himself.

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s dictation is masterfully calibrated. She describes the setting — "an [[grape-arbour|old grape arbour]] in the country, near a log cabin where I had my dinner" (015-334) — in language designed to make [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] irresistible to a reader who has never seen it. Her postscript concludes with a challenge: "Buy a nice little home in the country" (015-362). It is a message sent through Philip's pen but unmistakably in [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s voice, and the reader understands that she is not merely dictating a letter — she is staking a claim.

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — Watching from the background with her sewing, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] absorbs everything that is said. Her presence — silent, attentive, newly engaged with the world after her own transformation — adds a layer of maternal witness to the romantic drama unfolding before her. (015-218)

## Aftermath

The letter scene crystallizes the romantic triangle at the heart of the novel. Philip sends his letter to Edith — his name signed beneath [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s words — and the asymmetry of the act is unmistakable. He writes to one woman while sitting inches from another; he describes a world that Edith has never seen in language she will never fully understand.

The bargain [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] strikes — that Philip must show her Edith's reply to the postscript (015-373) — establishes the terms of their unspoken competition. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is not passive in this [[love-and-courtship|courtship]]. She is actively working to make Philip see what she offers, and the letter scene is one of her most effective moves: she makes herself present in Edith's world through the very medium — Philip's handwriting — that should have kept her out.

## Significance

The letter-writing scene in Chapter XV is a small, domestic moment that carries the entire weight of the novel's romantic conflict. Philip writes to his fiancee while sitting across from the woman he is falling in love with, and the physical arrangement — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] opposite him, reading his words upside down — literalizes the impossible geometry of his situation.

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s dictation is a tour de force of indirect communication. She speaks to Edith through Philip, describing her own world in terms calculated to make it irresistible, and the effect is to make the reader — and Philip — acutely aware of the contrast between the two women in his life. The scene belongs to a tradition of epistolary triangulation in fiction, but its setting in a country grape arbour, with moths pinned to cork sheets and a woman with a ruffle watching from the corner, makes it unmistakably a Limberlost scene.
