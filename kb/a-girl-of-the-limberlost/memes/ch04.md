# CHAPTER IV

[004-001]WHEREIN THE SINTONS ARE DISAPPOINTED, AND MRS. COMSTOCK LEARNS THAT SHE

[004-002]CAN LAUGH

[004-003]With the first streak of red above [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] was

[004-004]busy with the gingham and the intricate paper pattern she had

[004-005]purchased. Wesley cooked the breakfast and worked until he thought

[004-006][[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] would be gone, then he started to bring her mother.

[004-007]“Now you be mighty careful,” cautioned Margaret. “I don’t know how she

[004-008]will take it.”

[004-009]“I don’t either,” said Wesley philosophically, “but she’s got to take

[004-010]it some way. That dress has to be finished by school time in the

[004-011]morning.”

[004-012]Wesley had not slept well that night. He had been so busy framing

[004-013]diplomatic speeches to make to [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] that sleep had little

[004-014]chance with him. Every step nearer to her he approached his position

[004-015]seemed less enviable. By the time he reached the front gate and started

[004-016]down the walk between the rows of asters and lady slippers he was

[004-017]perspiring, and every plausible and convincing speech had fled his

[004-018]brain. Mrs. Comstock helped him. She met him at the door.

[004-019]“Good morning,” she said. “Did Margaret send you for something?”

[004-020]“Yes,” said Wesley. “She’s got a job that’s too big for her, and she

[004-021]wants you to help.”

[004-022]“Of course I will,” said Mrs. Comstock. It was no one’s affair how

[004-023]lonely the previous day had been, or how the endless hours of the

[004-024]present would drag. “What is she doing in such a rush?”

[004-025]Now was his chance.

[004-026]“She’s making a dress for Elnora,” answered, Wesley. He saw Mrs.

[004-027]Comstock’s form straighten, and her face harden, so he continued

[004-028]hastily. “You see Elnora has been helping us at harvest time,

[004-029]butchering, and with unexpected visitors for years. We’ve made out that

[004-030]she’s saved us a considerable sum, and as she wouldn’t ever touch any

[004-031]pay for anything, we just went to town and got a few clothes we thought

[004-032]would fix her up a little for [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]]. We want to get a dress

[004-033]done to-day mighty bad, but Margaret is slow about sewing, and she

[004-034]never can finish alone, so I came after you.”

[004-035]“And it’s such a simple little matter, so dead easy; and all so between

[004-036]old friends like, that you can’t look above your boots while you

[004-037]explain it,” sneered Mrs. Comstock. “[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], what put the idea

[004-038]into your head that Elnora would take things bought with money, when

[004-039]she wouldn’t take the money?”

[004-040]Then Sinton’s eyes came up straightly.

[004-041]“Finding her on the trail last night sobbing as hard as I ever saw any

[004-042]one at a funeral. She wasn’t complaining at all, but she’s come to me

[004-043]all her life with her little hurts, and she couldn’t hide how she’d

[004-044]been laughed at, twitted, and run face to face against the fact that

[004-045]there were books and tuition, unexpected, and nothing will ever make me

[004-046]believe you didn’t know that, Kate Comstock.”

[004-047]“If any doubts are troubling you on that subject, sure I knew it! She

[004-048]was so anxious to try the world, I thought I’d just let her take a few

[004-049]knocks and see how she liked them.”

[004-050]“As if she’d ever taken anything but knocks all her life!” cried Wesley

[004-051]Sinton. “Kate Comstock, you are a heartless, selfish woman. You’ve

[004-052]never shown Elnora any real love in her life. If ever she finds out

[004-053]that thing you’ll lose her, and it will serve you right.”

[004-054]“She knows it now,” said Mrs. Comstock icily, “and she’ll be home

[004-055]to-night just as usual.”

[004-056]“Well, you are a brave woman if you dared put a girl of Elnora’s make

[004-057]through what she suffered yesterday, and will suffer again to-day, and

[004-058]let her know you did it on purpose. I admire your nerve. But I’ve

[004-059]watched this since Elnora was born, and I got enough. Things have come

[004-060]to a pass where they go better for her, or I interfere.”

[004-061]“As if you’d ever done anything but interfere all her life! Think I

[004-062]haven’t watched you? Think I, with my heart raw in my breast, and too

[004-063]numb to resent it openly, haven’t seen you and Mag Sinton trying to

[004-064]turn Elnora against me day after day? When did you ever tell her what

[004-065]her father meant to me? When did you ever try to make her see the wreck

[004-066]of my life, and what I’ve suffered? No indeed! Always it’s been poor

[004-067]little abused Elnora, and cakes, kissing, extra clothes, and

[004-068]encouraging her to run to you with a pitiful mouth every time I tried

[004-069]to make a woman of her.”

[004-070]“Kate Comstock, that’s unjust,” cried Sinton. “Only last night I tried

[004-071]to show her the picture I saw the day she was born. I begged her to

[004-072]come to you and tell you pleasant what she needed, and ask you for what

[004-073]I happen to know you can well afford to give her.”

[004-074]“I can’t!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “You know I can’t!”

[004-075]“Then get so you can!” said Wesley Sinton. “Any day you say the word

[004-076]you can sell six thousand worth of rare timber off this place easy.

[004-077]I’ll see to clearing and working the fields cheap as dirt, for Elnora’s

[004-078]sake. I’ll buy you more cattle to fatten. All you’ve got to do is sign

[004-079]a lease, to pull thousands from the ground in oil, as the rest of us

[004-080]are doing all around you!”

[004-081]“Cut down Robert’s trees!” shrieked Mrs. Comstock. “Tear up his land!

[004-082]Cover everything with horrid, greasy oil! I’ll die first.”

[004-083]“You mean you’ll let Elnora go like a beggar, and hurt and mortify her

[004-084]past bearing. I’ve got to the place where I tell you plain what I am

[004-085]going to do. Maggie and I went to town last night, and we bought what

[004-086]things Elnora needs most urgent to make her look a little like the rest

[004-087]of the [[education-as-liberation|high school]] girls. Now here it is in plain English. You can help

[004-088]get these things ready, and let us give them to her as we want——”

[004-089]“She won’t touch them!” cried Mrs. Comstock.

[004-090]“Then you can pay us, and she can take them as her right——”

[004-091]“I won’t!”

[004-092]“Then I will tell Elnora just what you are worth, what you can afford,

[004-093]and how much of this she owns. I’ll loan her the money to buy books and

[004-094]decent clothes, and when she is of age she can sell her share and pay

[004-095]me.”

[004-109]couldn’t either. You’ve got to love such a fine girl as she is; let it

[004-111]“You can hardly expect me to love her,” said Mrs. Comstock coldly. “But

[004-112]for her a man would stand back of me now, who would beat the breath out

[004-114]me. After all I’ve suffered you’d drag me to court and compel me to

[004-115]tear up Robert’s property. If I ever go they carry me. If they touch

[004-116]one tree, or put down one greasy old oil well, it will be over all I

[004-118]here!”

[004-119]“You won’t come and help Maggie with the dress?”

[004-122]haste consistent with dignity. But he did not go home. He crossed a

[004-123]field, and in an hour brought another neighbour who was skilful with

[004-124]her needle. With sinking heart Margaret saw them coming.

[004-125]“Kate is too busy to help to-day, she can’t sew before to-morrow,” said

[004-126]Wesley cheerfully as they entered.

[004-127]That quieted Margaret’s apprehension a little, though she had some

[004-128]doubts. Wesley prepared the lunch, and by four o’clock the dress was

[004-129]finished as far as it possibly could be until it was fitted on Elnora.

[004-130]If that did not entail too much work, it could be completed in two

[004-131]hours.

[004-132]Then Margaret packed their purchases into the big market basket. Wesley

[004-133]took the hat, umbrella, and raincoat, and they went to Mrs. Comstock’s.

[004-134]As they reached the step, Margaret spoke pleasantly to Mrs. Comstock,

[004-135]who sat reading just inside the door, but she did not answer and

[004-136]deliberately turned a leaf without looking up.

[004-137]Wesley Sinton opened the door and went in followed by Margaret.

[004-138]“Kate,” he said, “you needn’t take out your mad over our little racket

[004-139]on Maggie. I ain’t told her a word I said to you, or you said to me.

[004-140]She’s not so very strong, and she’s sewed since four o’clock this

[004-141]morning to get this dress ready for to-morrow. It’s done and we came

[004-142]down to try it on Elnora.”

[004-143]“Is that the truth, Mag Sinton?” demanded Mrs. Comstock.

[004-144]“You heard Wesley say so,” proudly affirmed Mrs. Sinton.

[004-145]“I want to make you a proposition,” said Wesley. “Wait till Elnora

[004-146]comes. Then we’ll show her the things and see what she says.”

[004-147]“How would it do to see what she says without bribing her,” sneered

[004-148]Mrs. Comstock.

[004-149]“If she can stand what she did yesterday, and will to-day, she can bear

[004-150]’most anything,” said Wesley. “Put away the clothes if you want to,

[004-151]till we tell her.”

[004-152]“Well, you don’t take this waist I’m working on,” said Margaret, “for I

[004-153]have to baste in the sleeves and set the collar. Put the rest out of

[004-154]sight if you like.”

[004-155]Mrs. Comstock picked up the basket and bundles, placed them inside her

[004-156]room and closed the door.

[004-164]his tilted chair and squared himself. Margaret gripped her sewing, and
