# CHAPTER XXII

[022-001]WHEREIN PHILIP AMMON KNEELS TO ELNORA, AND STRANGERS COME TO THE

[022-002]LIMBERLOST

[022-003]The month which followed was a reproduction of the previous June. There

[022-004]were long moth hunts, days of specimen gathering, wonderful hours with

[022-005]great books, big dinners all of them helped to prepare, and perfect

[022-006]nights filled with music. Everything was as it had been, with the

[022-007]difference that Philip was now an avowed suitor. He missed no

[022-008]opportunity to advance himself in [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]’s graces. At the end of the

[022-009]month he was no nearer any sort of understanding with her than he had

[022-010]been at the beginning. He revelled in the privilege of loving her, but

[022-011]he got no response. Elnora believed in his love, yet she hesitated to

[022-012]accept him, because she could not forget [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]].

[022-013]One afternoon early in July, Philip came across the fields, through the

[022-014]Comstock woods, and entered [[comstock-garden|the garden]]. He inquired for Elnora at the

[022-015]back door and was told that she was reading under the willow. He went

[022-016]around the west end of [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] to her. She sat on a rustic bench they

[022-017]had made and placed beneath a drooping branch. He had not seen her

[022-018]before in the dress she was wearing. It was clinging mull of pale

[022-019]green, trimmed with narrow ruffles and touched with knots of black

[022-020]velvet; a simple dress, but vastly becoming. Every tint of her bright

[022-021]hair, her luminous eyes, her red lips, and her rose-flushed face, neck,

[022-022]and arms grew a little more vivid with the delicate green setting.

[022-023]He stopped short. She was so near, so temptingly sweet, he lost

[022-024]control. He went to her with a half-smothered cry after that first long

[022-025]look, dropped on one knee beside her and reached an arm behind her to

[022-026]the bench back, so that he was very near. He caught her hands.

[022-027]“Elnora!” he cried tensely, “end it now! Say this strain is over. I

[022-028]pledge you that you will be happy. You don’t know! If you only would

[022-029]say the word, you would awake to new life and great joy! Won’t you

[022-030]promise me now, Elnora?”

[022-031]The girl sat staring into the west woods, while strong in her eyes was

[022-032]her father’s look of seeing something invisible to others. Philip’s arm

[022-033]slipped from the bench around her. His fingers closed firmly over hers.

[022-034]“Elnora,” he pleaded, “you know me well enough. You have had time in

[022-035]plenty. End it now. Say you will be mine!” He gathered her closer,

[022-036]pressing his face against hers, his breath on her cheek. “Can’t you

[022-037]quite promise yet, my girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]?”

[022-038]Elnora shook her head. Instantly he released her.

[022-039]“Forgive me,” he begged. “I had no intention of thrusting myself upon

[022-040]you, but, Elnora, you are the veriest Queen of Love this afternoon.

[022-041]From the tips of your toes to your shining crown, I worship you. I want

[022-042]no woman save you. You are so wonderful this afternoon, I couldn’t help

[022-043]urging. Forgive me. Perhaps it was something that came this morning for

[022-044]you. I wrote [[polly-ammon|Polly]] to send it. May we try if it fits? Will you tell me

[022-045]if you like it?”

[022-046]He drew a little white velvet box from his pocket and showed her a

[022-047]splendid emerald ring.

[022-048]“It may not be right,” he said. “The inside of a glove finger is not

[022-049]very accurate for a measure, but it was the best I could do. I wrote

[022-050]Polly to get it, because she and mother are home from the East this

[022-051]week, but next they will go on to our cottage in the north, and no one

[022-052]knows what is right quite so well as Polly.” He laid the ring in

[022-053]Elnora’s hand. “Dearest,” he said, “don’t slip that on your finger; put

[022-054]your arms around my neck and promise me, all at once and abruptly, or

[022-055]I’ll keel over and die of sheer joy.”

[022-056]Elnora smiled.

[022-057]“I won’t! Not all those venturesome things at once; but, Phil, I’m

[022-058]ashamed to confess that ring simply fascinates me. It is the most

[022-059]beautiful one I ever saw, and do you know that I never owned a ring of

[022-060]any kind in my life? Would you think me unwomanly if I slip it on for a

[022-061]second, before I can say for sure? Phil, you know I care! I care very

[022-062]much! You know I will tell you the instant I feel right about it.”

[022-063]“Certainly you will,” agreed Philip promptly. “It is your right to take

[022-064]all the time you choose. I can’t put that ring on you until it means a

[022-065]bond between us. I’ll shut my eyes and you try it on, so we can see if

[022-066]it fits.” Philip turned his face toward the west woods and tightly

[022-067]closed his eyes. It was a boyish thing to do, and it caught the

[022-068]hesitating girl in the depths of her heart as the boy element in a man

[022-069]ever appeals to a motherly woman. Before she quite realized what she

[022-070]was doing, the ring slid on her finger. With both arms she caught

[022-071]Philip and drew him to her breast, holding him closely. Her head

[022-072]drooped over his, her lips were on his hair. So an instant, then her

[022-073]arms dropped. He lifted a convulsed, white face.

[022-074]“Dear Lord!” he whispered. “You—you didn’t mean that, Elnora! You——

[022-075]What made you do it?”

[022-076]“You—you looked so boyish!” panted Elnora. “I didn’t mean it! I—I

[022-077]forgot that you were older than [[billy|Billy]]. Look—look at the ring!”

[022-078]“‘The Queen can do no wrong,’” quoted Philip between his set teeth.

[022-079]“But don’t you do that again, Elnora, unless you do mean it. Kings are

[022-080]not so good as queens, and there is a limit with all men. As you say,

[022-081]we will look at your ring. It seems very lovely to me. Suppose you

[022-082]leave it on until time for me to go. Please do! I have heard of mute

[022-083]appeals; perhaps it will plead for me. I am wild for your lips this

[022-084]afternoon. I am going to take your hands.”

[022-085]He caught both of them and covered them with kisses.

[022-086]“Elnora,” he said, “Will you be my wife?”

[022-087]“I must have a little more time,” she whispered. “I must be absolutely

[022-088]certain, for when I say yes, and give myself to you, only death shall

[022-089]part us. I would not give you up. So I want a little more time—but, I

[022-090]think I will.”

[022-091]“Thank you,” said Philip. “If at any time you feel that you have

[022-092]reached a decision, will you tell me? Will you promise me to tell me

[022-093]instantly, or shall I keep asking you until the time comes?”

[022-094]“You make it difficult,” said Elnora. “But I will promise you that.

[022-095]Whenever the last doubt vanishes, I will let you know instantly—if I

[022-096]can.”

[022-097]“Would it be difficult for you?” whispered Ammon.

[022-098]“I—I don’t know,” faltered Elnora.

[022-099]“It seems as if I can’t be man enough to put this thought aside and

[022-100]give up this afternoon,” said Philip. “I am ashamed of myself, but I

[022-101]can’t help it. I am going to ask God to make that last doubt vanish

[022-102]before I go this night. I am going to believe that ring will plead for

[022-103]me. I am going to hope that doubt will disappear suddenly. I will be

[022-104]watching. Every second I will be watching. If it happens and you can’t

[022-105]speak, give me your hand. Just the least movement toward me, I will

[022-106]understand. Would it help you to talk this over with your mother? Shall

[022-107]I call her? Shall I——?”

[022-108]Honk! Honk! Honk! [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]] set the horn of the big automobile

[022-109]going as it shot from behind the trees lining the Brushwood road. The

[022-110]picture of a vine-covered cabin, a large drooping tree, a green-clad

[022-111]girl and a man bending over her very closely flashed into view. Edith

[022-112]Carr caught her breath with a snap. Polly Ammon gave [[tom-levering|Tom Levering]] a

[022-113]quick touch and wickedly winked at him.

[022-114]Several days before, Edith had returned from Europe suddenly. She and

[022-115]Henderson had called at the Ammon residence saying that they were going

[022-116]to motor down to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] to see Philip a few hours, and urged

[022-117]that Polly and Tom accompany them. Mrs. Ammon knew that her husband

[022-118]would disapprove of the trip, but it was easy to see that Edith Carr

[022-119]had determined on going. So the mother thought it better to have Polly

[022-120]along to support Philip than to allow him to confront Edith

[022-121]unexpectedly and alone. Polly was full of spirit. She did not relish

[022-122]the thought of Edith as a sister. Always they had been in the same set,

[022-123]always Edith, because of greater beauty and wealth, had patronized

[022-124]Polly. Although it had rankled, she had borne it sweetly. But two days

[022-125]before, her father had extracted a promise of [[secrets-and-truth|secrecy]], given her

[022-126]Philip’s address and told her to send him the finest emerald ring she

[022-127]could select. Polly knew how that ring would be used. What she did not

[022-128]know was that the girl who accompanied her went back to the store

[022-129]afterward, made an excuse to the clerk that she had been sent to be

[022-130]absolutely sure that the address was right, and so secured it for Edith

[022-131]Carr.

[022-132]Two days later Edith had induced Hart Henderson to take her to

[022-133][[onabasha|Onabasha]]. By the aid of maps they located the Comstock land and passed

[022-134]it, merely to see the place. Henderson hated that trip, and implored

[022-135]Edith not to take it, but she made no effort to conceal from him what

[022-136]she suffered, and it was more than he could endure. He pointed out that

[022-137]Philip had gone away without leaving an address, because he did not

[022-138]wish to see her, or any of them. But Edith was so sure of her power,

[022-139]she felt certain Philip needed only to see her to succumb to her beauty

[022-140]as he always had done, while now she was ready to plead for

[022-141][[forgiveness-and-redemption|forgiveness]]. So they came down the Brushwood road, and Henderson had

[022-142]just said to Edith beside him: “This should be the Comstock land on our

[022-143]left.”

[022-144]A minute later the wood ended, while the sunlight, as always pitiless,

[022-145]etched with distinctness the scene at the west end of the cabin.

[022-146]Instinctively, to save Edith, Henderson set the horn blowing. He had

[022-147]thought to drive to the city, but Polly Ammon arose crying: “Phil!

[022-148]Phil!” Tom Levering was on his feet shouting and waving, while Edith in

[022-149]her most imperial manner ordered him to turn into the lane leading

[022-150]through the woods beside the cabin.

[022-151]“Find some way for me to have a minute alone with her,” she commanded

[022-152]as he stopped the car.

[022-153]“That is my sister Polly, her fiance Tom Levering, a friend of mine

[022-154]named Henderson, and——” began Philip,

[022-155]“—and Edith Carr,” volunteered Elnora.

[022-156]“And Edith Carr,” repeated [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. “Elnora, be brave, for my

[022-157]sake. Their coming can make no difference in any way. I won’t let them

[022-158]stay but a few minutes. Come with me!”

[022-159]“Do I seem scared?” inquired Elnora serenely. “This is why you haven’t

[022-160]had your answer. I have been waiting just six weeks for that motor. You

[022-161]may bring them to me at [[grape-arbour|the arbour]].”

[022-162]Philip glanced at her and broke into a laugh. She had not lost colour.

[022-163]Her self-possession was perfect. She deliberately turned and walked

[022-164]toward the grape arbour, while he sprang over the west fence and ran to

[022-165]the car.

[022-166]Elnora standing in the arbour entrance made a perfect picture, framed

[022-167]in green leaves and tendrils. No matter how her heart ached, it was

[022-168]good to her, for it pumped steadily, and kept her cheeks and lips

[022-169]suffused with colour. She saw Philip reach the car and gather his

[022-170]sister into his arms. Past her he reached a hand to Levering, then to

[022-171]Edith Carr and Henderson. He lifted his sister to the ground, and

[022-172]assisted Edith to alight. Instantly, she stepped beside him, and

[022-173]Elnora’s heart played its first trick.

[022-174]She could see that Miss Carr was splendidly beautiful, while she moved

[022-175]with the hauteur and grace supposed to be the prerogatives of royalty.

[022-176]And she had instantly taken possession of Philip. But he also had a

[022-177]brain which was working with rapidity. He knew Elnora was watching, so

[022-178]he turned to the others.

[022-179]“Give her up, Tom!” he cried. “I didn’t know I wanted to see the little

[022-180]nuisance so badly, but I do. How are father and mother? Polly, didn’t

[022-181]the mater send me something?”

[022-182]“She did!” said Polly Ammon, stopping on the path and lifting her chin

[022-183]as a little child, while she drew away her veil.

[022-184]Philip caught her in his arms and stooped for his mother’s kiss.

[022-185]“Be good to Elnora!” he whispered.

[022-186]“Umhu!” assented Polly. And aloud—“Look at that ripping green and gold

[022-187]symphony! I never saw such a beauty! Thomas Asquith Levering, you come

[022-188]straight here and take my hand!”

[022-189]Edith’s move to compel Philip to approach Elnora beside her had been

[022-190]easy to see; also its failure. Henderson stepped into Philip’s place as

[022-191]he turned to his sister. Instead of taking Polly’s hand Levering ran to

[022-192]open the gate. Edith passed through first, but Polly darted in front of

[022-193]her on the run, with Phil holding her arm, and swept up to Elnora.

[022-194]Polly looked for the ring and saw it. That settled matters with her.

[022-195]“You lovely, lovely, darling girl!” she cried, throwing her arms around

[022-196]Elnora and kissing her. With her lips close Elnora’s ear, Polly

[022-197]whispered, “Sister! Dear, dear sister!”

[022-198]Elnora drew back, staring at Polly in confused amazement. She was a

[022-199]beautiful girl, her eyes were sparkling and dancing, and as she turned

[022-200]to make way for the others, she kept one of Elnora’s hands in hers.

[022-201]Polly would have dropped dead in that instant if Edith Carr could have

[022-202]killed with a look, for not until then did she realize that Polly would

[022-203]even many a slight, and that it had been a great mistake to bring her.

[022-204]Edith bowed low, muttered something and touched Elnora’s fingers. Tom

[022-205]took his cue from Polly.

[022-206]“I always follow a good example,” he said, and before any one could

[022-207]divine his intention he kissed Elnora as he gripped her hand and cried:

[022-208]“Mighty glad to meet you! Like to meet you a dozen times a day, you

[022-209]know!”

[022-210]Elnora laughed and her heart pumped smoothly. They had accomplished

[022-211]their purpose. They had let her know they were there through

[022-212]compulsion, but on her side. In that instant only pity was in Elnora’s

[022-213]breast for the flashing dark beauty, standing with smiling face while

[022-214]her heart must have been filled with exceeding bitterness. Elnora

[022-215]stepped back from the entrance.

[022-216]“Come into the shade,” she urged. “You must have found it warm on these

[022-217]country roads. Won’t you lay aside your dust-coats and have a cool

[022-218]drink? Philip, would you ask mother to come, and bring that pitcher

[022-219]from [[spring-house|the spring house]]?”

[022-220]They entered the arbour exclaiming at the dim, green coolness. There

[022-221]was plenty of room and wide seats around the sides, a table in the

[022-222]centre, on which lay a piece of embroidery, magazines, books, the moth

[022-223]apparatus, and the cyanide jar containing several specimens. Polly

[022-224]rejoiced in the cooling shade, slipped off her duster, removed her hat,

[022-225]rumpled her pretty hair and seated herself to indulge in the delightful

[022-226]occupation of paying off old scores. Tom Levering followed her example.

[022-227]Edith took a seat but refused to remove her hat and coat, while

[022-228]Henderson stood in the entrance.

[022-229]“There goes something with wings! Should you have that?” cried

[022-230]Levering.

[022-231]He seized a net from the table and raced across the garden after a

[022-232]butterfly. He caught it and came back mightily pleased with himself. As

[022-233]the creature struggled in the net, Elnora noted a repulsed look on

[022-234]Edith Carr’s face. Levering helped the situation beautifully.

[022-235]“Now what have I got?” he demanded. “Is it just a common one that every

[022-236]one knows and you don’t keep, or is it the rarest bird off the perch?”

[022-237]“You must have had practice, you took that so perfectly,” said Elnora.

[022-238]“I am sorry, but it is quite common and not of a kind I keep. Suppose

[022-239]all of you see how beautiful it is and then it may go nectar hunting

[022-240]again.”

[022-241]She held the butterfly where all of them could see, showed its upper

[022-242]and under wing colours, answered Polly’s questions as to what it ate,

[022-243]how long it lived, and how it died. Then she put it into Polly’s hand

[022-244]saying: “Stand there in the light and loosen your hold slowly and

[022-245]easily.”

[022-246]Elnora caught a brush from the table and began softly stroking the

[022-247]creature’s sides and wings. Delighted with the sensation the butterfly

[022-248]opened and closed its wings, clinging to Polly’s soft little fingers,

[022-249]while every one cried out in surprise. Elnora laid aside the brush, and

[022-250]the butterfly sailed away.

[022-251]“Why, you are a wizard! You charm them!” marvelled Levering.

[022-252]“I learned that from the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]],” said Elnora. “She takes soft

[022-253]brushes and coaxes butterflies and moths into the positions she wants

[022-254]for the illustrations of a book she is writing. I have helped her

[022-255]often. Most of the rare ones I find go to her.”

[022-256]“Then you don’t keep all you take?” questioned Levering.

[022-257]“Oh, dear, no!” cried Elnora. “Not a tenth! For myself, a pair of each

[022-258]kind to use in illustrating the lectures I give in the city schools in

[022-259]the winter, and one pair for each collection I make. One might as well

[022-260]keep the big night moths of June, for they only live four or five days

[022-261]anyway. For the Bird Woman, I only save rare ones she has not yet

[022-262]secured. Sometimes I think it is cruel to take such creatures from

[022-263]freedom, even for an hour, but it is the only way to teach the masses

[022-264]of people how to distinguish the pests they should destroy, from the

[022-265]harmless ones of great beauty. Here comes mother with something cool to

[022-266]drink.”

[022-267][[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] came deliberately, talking to Philip as she approached.

[022-268]Elnora gave her one searching look, but could discover only an extreme

[022-269]brightness of eye to denote any unusual feeling. She wore one of her

[022-270]lavender dresses, while her snowy hair was high piled. She had taken

[022-271]care of her complexion, and her face had grown fuller during the

[022-272]winter. She might have been any one’s mother with pride, and she was

[022-273]perfectly at ease.

[022-274]Polly instantly went to her and held up her face to be kissed. Mrs.

[022-275]Comstock’s eyes twinkled and she made the greeting hearty.

[022-276]The drink was compounded of the juices of oranges and berries from the

[022-277]garden. It was cool enough to frost glasses and pitcher and delicious

[022-278]to dusty tired travellers. Soon the pitcher was empty, and Elnora

[022-279]picked it up and went to refill it. While she was gone Henderson asked

[022-280]Philip about some trouble he was having with his car. They went to the

[022-281]woods and began a minute examination to find a defect which did not

[022-282]exist. Polly and Levering were having an animated conversation with

[022-283]Mrs. Comstock. Henderson saw Edith arise, follow the garden path next

[022-284]the woods and stand waiting under the willow which Elnora would pass on

[022-285]her return. It was for that meeting he had made the trip. He got down

[022-286]on the ground, tore up the car, worked, asked for help, and kept Philip

[022-287]busy screwing bolts and applying the oil can. All the time Henderson

[022-288]kept an eye on Edith and Elnora under the willow. But he took pains to

[022-289]lay the work he asked Philip to do where that scene would be out of his

[022-290]sight. When Elnora came around the corner with the pitcher, she found

[022-291]herself facing Edith Carr.

[022-292]“I want a minute with you,” said Miss Carr.

[022-293]“Very well,” replied Elnora, walking on.

[022-294]“Set the pitcher on the bench there,” commanded Edith Carr, as if

[022-295]speaking to a servant.

[022-296]“I prefer not to offer my visitors a warm drink,” said Elnora. “I’ll

[022-297]come back if you really wish to speak with me.”

[022-298]“I came solely for that,” said Edith Carr.

[022-299]“It would be a pity to travel so far in this dust and heat for nothing.

[022-300]I’ll only be gone a second.”

[022-301]Elnora placed the pitcher before her mother. “Please serve this,” she

[022-302]said. “Miss Carr wishes to speak with me.”

[022-303]“Don’t you pay the least attention to anything she says,” cried Polly.

[022-304]“Tom and I didn’t come here because we wanted to. We only came to

[022-305]checkmate her. I hoped I’d get the opportunity to say a word to you,

[022-306]and now she has given it to me. I just want to tell you that she threw

[022-307]Phil over in perfectly horrid way. She hasn’t any right to lay the

[022-308]ghost of a claim to him, has she, Tom?”

[022-309]“Nary a claim,” said Tom Levering earnestly. “Why, even you, Polly,

[022-310]couldn’t serve me as she did Phil, and ever get me back again. If I

[022-311]were you, Miss Comstock, I’d send my mother to talk with her and I’d

[022-312]stay here.”

[022-313]Tom had gauged Mrs. Comstock rightly. Polly put her arms around Elnora.

[022-314]“Let me go with you, dear,” she begged.

[022-315]“I promised I would speak with her alone,” said Elnora, “and she must

[022-316]be considered. But thank you, very much.”

[022-317]“How I shall love you!” exulted Polly, giving Elnora a parting hug.

[022-318]The girl slowly and gravely walked back to the willow. She could not

[022-319]imagine what was coming, but she was promising herself that she would

[022-320]be very patient and control her temper.

[022-321]“Will you be seated?” she asked politely.

[022-322]Edith Carr glanced at the bench, while a shudder shook her.

[022-323]“No. I prefer to stand,” she said. “Did Mr. Ammon give you the ring you

[022-324]are wearing, and do you consider yourself engaged to him?”

[022-325]“By what right do you ask such personal questions as those?” inquired

[022-326]Elnora.

[022-327]“By the right of a betrothed wife. I have been promised to Philip Ammon

[022-328]ever since I wore short skirts. All our lives we have expected to

[022-329]marry. An agreement of years cannot be broken in one insane moment.

[022-330]Always he has loved me devotedly. Give me ten minutes with him and he

[022-331]will be mine for all time.”

[022-332]“I seriously doubt that,” said Elnora. “But I am willing that you

[022-333]should make the test. I will call him.”

[022-334]“Stop!” commanded Edith Carr. “I told you that it was you I came to

[022-335]see.”

[022-336]“I remember,” said Elnora.

[022-337]“Mr. Ammon is my betrothed,” continued Edith Carr. “I expect to take

[022-338]him back to Chicago with me.”

[022-339]“You expect considerable,” murmured Elnora. “I will raise no objection

[022-340]to your taking him, if you can—but, I tell you frankly, I don’t think

[022-341]it possible.”

[022-342]“You are so sure of yourself as that,” scoffed Edith Carr. “One hour in

[022-343]my presence will bring back the old spell, full force. We belong to

[022-344]each other. I will not give him up.”

[022-345]“Then it is untrue that you twice rejected his ring, repeatedly

[022-346]insulted him, and publicly renounced him?”

[022-347]“That was through you!” cried Edith Carr. “Phil and I never had been so

[022-348]near and so happy as we were on that night. It was your clinging to him

[022-349]for things that caused him to desert me among his guests, while he

[022-350]tried to make me await your pleasure. I realize the spell of this

[022-351]place, for a summer season. I understand what you and your mother have

[022-352]done to inveigle him. I know that your hold on him is quite real. I can

[022-353]see just how you have worked to ensnare him!”

[022-354]“Men would call that lying,” said Elnora calmly. “The second time I met

[022-355]Philip Ammon he told me of his engagement to you, and I respected it. I

[022-356]did by you as I would want you to do by me. He was here parts of each

[022-357]day, almost daily last summer. The Almighty is my witness that never

[022-358]once, by word or look, did I ever make the slightest attempt to

[022-359]interest him in my person or personality. He wrote you frequently in my
