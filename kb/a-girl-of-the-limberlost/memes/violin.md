# The Violin

## Overview

The violin in *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]* is far more than a musical instrument. It belonged to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s father, [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]], and after his death was hidden away by his grieving widow for sixteen years.

When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] discovers and reclaims it, the violin becomes a bridge between generations, a vehicle for her talent, and the instrument of her social triumph at the [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]] entertainment.

(009-119)

## The Father's Violin

Robert Comstock's violin was his most cherished possession. He played it with passion, and those who knew him remember the instrument as an extension of his personality:

> "All day until he picked up that violin. Then his head bent over it, and his eyes smiled." (009-242)

After Robert's death, [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] sealed the violin away along with his other belongings, creating a shrine to her grief. For sixteen years, the violin lay silent — a physical representation of the love and talent buried under [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]'s unresolved [[grief-and-loss|mourning]]. (009-215)

## Discovery and Reclamation

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] discovers her father's violin in a dramatic moment at school. Hearing the school orchestra, she is drawn to the instrument left on the grand piano:

> "I am perfectly crazy for a violin." (009-124)

She takes the violin from the piano and plays it instinctively. When she learns that her father also played, she demands access to his instrument:

> "Where is my father's violin?" (009-215)

> "I want that violin." (009-234)

With the help of Wesley and [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]], [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] recovers the violin from her father's sealed possessions. She has it repaired in [[onabasha|Onabasha]] and begins practicing with the same dedication she brings to her moth collecting. (009-284)

## The High School Entertainment

The violin's crowning moment comes at [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] entertainment, where [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] performs before the entire [[onabasha|Onabasha]] [[community-support|community]]. The same students who once mocked her country clothes sit in the audience as she takes the stage:

> Nature had grown still, the violin sobbed, sang, and carried the audience with it. (010-507)

> She bowed, and lifting the violin she began to play. (010-516)

The performance is a triumph — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s talent, inherited from her father and cultivated through years of secret practice, is publicly acknowledged. The girl from [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] commands the stage of [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] auditorium, and [[onabasha|Onabasha]] takes notice. (011-373)

## Musical Development

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] practices relentlessly. She carries the violin to school each day, plays during her lunch hours, and spends her evenings perfecting her technique:

> She could take the violin with her, practise, and prepare for the next entertainment. (010-101)

Her skill grows to the point where she leads [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] orchestra and is considered for professional opportunities. The violin opens doors that her academic achievements alone could not — connecting her to the cultural life of [[onabasha|Onabasha]] and [[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] her the respect of those who had dismissed her. (010-343)

## Thematic Significance

### Inheritance and Continuity

The violin represents the positive legacy [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] left his daughter — talent, passion, and beauty — in contrast to the legacy of grief and bitterness that [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] imposed. Through the violin, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] connects with the father she never knew. (010-040)

> "I only know that I played the violin last night, as he played it." (010-040)

### Nature and Art

The violin also bridges the novel's themes of nature and art. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s playing is described in natural terms — the violin "sobbed" and "sang" — suggesting that her musical expression, like her collecting, is a form of communion with the natural world. (010-507)

### Class and Worth

In the novel's social economy, the violin serves as a great equalizer. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] cannot compete with the town girls in [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]] or [[social-class-and-appearance|social status]], but her musical talent transcends class boundaries and earns her genuine respect. (011-373)
