## Summary

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] enters [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] swamp at night for the first time since her husband Robert's death there over a decade earlier. Accompanied by [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], she sets out with lanterns to help catch moths for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], who is recovering from illness. The night culminates in the capture of the rare Yellow Emperor moth, a specimen crucial to completing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s collection and securing the funds for her college [[education-as-liberation|education]]. (011-234)

## Context

For years after [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] drowned in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] quicksand, [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] refused to set foot within [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]. The Limberlost represented her deepest trauma — the place where she lost her husband and where her bitterness took root. (004-018) Her daughter [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], by contrast, had made [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] her sanctuary, [[moth-collecting|collecting moths]] and butterflies to fund her [[education-as-liberation|education]]. (001-042)

When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] fell ill and could not collect the moths needed for her [[elnoras-college-dream|college fund]], [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] faced a pivotal choice. Having already begun a gradual transformation — prompted by [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]'s confrontations (004-037) and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s persistent love — she resolved to venture into [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] herself. She enlisted [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], a local worker familiar with [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], to assist her. (011-190)

The immediate catalyst was a moment of fury and guilt: earlier that morning, Mrs. Comstock had killed a moth that Elnora needed, striking at it without understanding its value. Elnora's anguished cry — "It's the one I want! It's worth a lot of money!" — revealed the gravity of the loss. The moth was the last specimen needed to complete a collection that would fund Elnora's education. (012-014) Mrs. Comstock, heartsick at what she had done, resolved to replace it, no matter the cost to herself.

## Key Participants

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — The grieving widow who, for the first time, confronts the physical space of her trauma. Her entry into [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] marks the decisive turn in her character arc from bitterness toward healing. She proves unexpectedly fierce and capable in the night hunt, threatening intruders and coordinating the capture with determination. (011-218)

**[[pete-corson|Pete Corson]]** — A local man who assists [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] with the practical work of [[moth-collecting|moth hunting]]. He carries lanterns, manages the equipment, and provides physical support while marveling at [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s intensity and courage. (011-234)

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Though physically absent, recovering from illness at home, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is the motivating presence behind the entire expedition. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] acts out of love for her daughter and a desire to help secure her future. (011-200)

## Narrative

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] prepared for the expedition with grim determination. She put on heavy boots, gathered three lanterns, matches, and twine, and slipped from the cabin into the June night. The Limberlost's dark outline was forbidding, and she shuddered as she approached. The knowledge that her husband's body lay beneath its waters made the swamp a place of terror — yet the guilt of having destroyed the moth Elnora needed drove her forward past the garden, across a field, and onto the old trail into the Limberlost.

> Sitting on the back steps, she put on the boots, and then stood gazing into the perfumed June night, first in the direction of the woods on her land, then toward the Limberlost. Its outline was so dark and forbidding she shuddered and went down the garden, following the path toward the woods, but as she neared the pool her knees wavered and her courage fled.

Deep in the swamp, she lit the lanterns and hung them high. At first only bats came. Then, crouching in the damp grasses without thought of snakes or venomous insects, she waited. A large moth approached and she caught it — but it was brown, not the yellow Emperor she needed. The captured moth released its defensive spray, drenching her dress. By accident, she had become the most effective moth bait in the Limberlost: the Polyphemus spray attracted other moths, and soon creatures of the night flocked toward her from every direction. She snatched wildly, soon holding one moth in each hand with more approaching.

> Crouching in the damp swamp grasses, without a thought of snakes or venomous insects, she waited, her eyes roving from lantern to lantern. ... From that instant, she became the best moth bait ever invented. Every Polyphemus in range hastened to her, and other fluttering creatures of night followed. The influx came her way.

It was then she sensed another presence in the darkness — a crouching figure watching from the undergrowth. Rather than flee, she commanded the stranger to help her. The figure emerged: it was [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], an old acquaintance of the family. "Pretty fierce, ain't you!" he laughed, but he held out his hands to take the moths she had caught. When she explained that she had destroyed the last moth Elnora needed and must find another, Pete warned her it was unlikely — the Yellow Emperor was rare and not easily found.

Mrs. Comstock improvised a collecting bag from her calico skirt, using her apron strings and a hairpin as a bodkin. Together they caught several more moths, but not the Emperor. When a lurking man spied on them — one of the local men who would misinterpret the lanterns as distress signals — Mrs. Comstock drove him off with threats, brandishing Robert's revolver and declaring she would not be stopped by "the devil himself." Pete watched her, realizing she was past reason, sense, or fear. "She would stop at nothing, that was evident," and he began catching moths with renewed industry.

Then, at last, the Yellow Emperor appeared. Mrs. Comstock saw it descending toward the lantern light and prayed aloud: "This way, O Lord! Make it come this way!" Pete made two sweeps with his hat and missed. The moth darted away — then a second Emperor appeared in pursuit of the first. As the larger moth wavered back into the light, it flew straight toward Mrs. Comstock. She snatched off the remnant of her apron and held the calico before her. The moth struck full against it and clung. The Yellow Emperor was captured.

> Mrs. Comstock looked up and stepped out with a prayer on her lips. She could not tell the colour at that distance, but the moth appeared different from the others. On it came, dropping lower and darting from light to light. As it swept near her, "O Heavenly Father!" exulted Mrs. Comstock, "it's yellow! Careful Pete! Your hat, maybe!"

## Aftermath

The successful capture of the Yellow Emperor moth proved transformative for the Comstock family. The rare specimen was worth a significant sum, advancing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s [[elnoras-college-dream|college fund]] substantially. (012-038) More importantly, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s act of entering [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] shattered the psychological barrier that had defined her grief for over a decade. She returned from [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] changed — no longer a prisoner of her past but an active participant in her daughter's future. The event also deepened the bond between mother and daughter, as [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] recognized the magnitude of what her mother had done for her. (012-045)

## Significance

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s night in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] stands as the pivotal turning point of her character transformation throughout the novel. The event embodies several of the novel's central themes: the redemptive power of maternal love, the courage required to confront past trauma, and [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] itself as a space of both danger and healing. Where [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] had once taken her husband, it now gave back to [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] — through a rare moth and a rare moment of grace — the possibility of a renewed life and a restored relationship with her daughter. (012-050)

The scene also demonstrates the novel's pattern of characters finding salvation through action rather than passive suffering. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] does not merely decide to change — she physically enters the place of her greatest fear and works through the night, her prayerful determination rewarded by the capture of the Yellow Emperor. (011-320)
