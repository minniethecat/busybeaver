# Mrs. Comstock Dances in the Moonlight

## Summary

Mrs. Comstock Dances in the Moonlight is the transformative turning point in Chapter XVII of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. After two decades of bitter grief following her husband's death, Katharine Comstock hears Elnora playing the violin in the grape arbour under the moonlight, asks for the old dance tunes of her girlhood, and begins to dance. Philip Ammon joins her, and together they whirl until they collapse panting on the arbour bench. The event, witnessed by the boy Billy who races to report it to the Sintons, signals the long-awaited release of Mrs. Comstock's "penned-up force" and marks the beginning of her emotional rebirth.

## Context

For twenty years since Robert Comstock drowned in the Limberlost swamp on the night Elnora was born, Katharine Comstock had forbidden all music in her home. The violin — her husband's instrument — represented everything she had lost. She wore her grief like armor, bitter and withdrawn from the world. By Chapter XVII, however, Elnora has won her mother's consent to play the violin at home, a hard-earned concession that sets the stage for this event.

Billy, swinging in the hammock at dusk, hears the violin and follows the sound. He creeps through the woods and peeps into the grape arbour, where he sees Mrs. Comstock sitting in the moonlight, her face wearing "a look of perfect peace and contentment" (017-017--018).

## Narrative

Elnora had been carefully avoiding every note that might remind her mother of her father (017-022). But Mrs. Comstock interrupts the music: "That's all very melodious and sweet, but I do wish you could play Money Musk and some of the tunes I danced as a girl" (017-020--021). Elnora laughs softly and begins "Turkey in the Straw" (017-023).

"An instant later Mrs. Comstock was dancing in the moon light. Ammon sprang to her side, caught her in his arms, while to Elnora's laughter and the violin's impetus they danced until they dropped panting on the arbour bench" (017-024--027).

Billy, overwhelmed, races back to the Sinton house: "Aunt Margaret! Uncle Wesley! Elnora's playing her violin at home! And Aunt Kate is dancing like anything before the arbour! I saw her in the moonlight!" (017-031--033). Billy is so moved that he flees sobbing to Margaret's breast (017-035).

Wesley Sinton immediately grasps the significance: "You mark my word, once you get Kate Comstock started, you can't stop her. There's a wagon load of penned-up force in her. Dancing in the moonlight! Well, I'll be hanged!" (017-043--046).

The violin plays on until Elnora is too tired to lift the bow, and Philip goes home (017-065--067). In the weeks that follow, Mrs. Comstock asks for the violin every night — "her perfect hunger for music was sufficient evidence of how she had suffered without it" (017-235--237).

## Significance

The moonlight dance represents the breaking of a twenty-year emotional prison. Mrs. Comstock's willingness to hear music, to request the tunes of her youth, and to dance physically releases the vitality that Wesley Sinton recognized as a "wagon load of penned-up force." The moonlight — an intermediate state between darkness and daylight — symbolizes her movement out of grief and toward full emotional re-engagement with life. The community dimension is essential: Billy's witness and the Sintons' tearful relief confirm that Katharine's suffering has been a shared burden, and her healing is a shared joy.
