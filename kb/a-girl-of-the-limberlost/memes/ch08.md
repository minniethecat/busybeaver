# CHAPTER VIII

[008-001]WHEREIN THE LIMBERLOST TEMPTS ELNORA, AND BILLY BURIES HIS FATHER

[008-002]Immediately after dinner on Sunday [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] stopped at the

[008-003]Comstock gate to ask if [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] wanted to go to town with them. [[billy|Billy]]

[008-004]sat beside him and he did not appear as if he were on his way to a

[008-005]funeral. Elnora said she had to study and could not go, but she

[008-006]suggested that her mother take her place. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] put on her hat

[008-007]and went at once, which surprised Elnora. She did not know that her

[008-008]mother was anxious for an opportunity to speak with Sinton alone.

[008-009]Elnora knew why she was repeatedly cautioned not to leave their land,

[008-010]if she went specimen hunting.

[008-011]She studied two hours and was several lessons ahead of her classes.

[008-012]There was no use to go further. She would take a walk and see if she

[008-013]could gather any caterpillars or find any freshly spun cocoons. She

[008-014]searched the bushes and low trees behind [[comstock-garden|the garden]] and all around the

[008-015]edge of the woods on their land, and having little success, at last

[008-016]came to the road. Almost the first thorn bush she examined yielded a

[008-017]Polyphemus cocoon. Elnora lifted her head with the instinct of a hunter

[008-018]on the chase, and began work. She reached [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] before she knew it,

[008-019]carrying five fine cocoons of different species as her reward. She

[008-020]pushed back her hair and gazed around longingly. A few rods inside she

[008-021]thought she saw cocoons on a bush, to which she went, and found

[008-022]several. Sense of caution was rapidly vanishing; she was in a fair way

[008-023]to forget everything and plunge into the swamp when she thought she

[008-024]heard footsteps coming down the trail. She went back, and came out

[008-025]almost facing [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]].

[008-026]That ended her difficulty. She had known him since childhood. When she

[008-027]sat on the front bench of the [[brushwood-school|Brushwood schoolhouse]], Pete had been one

[008-028]of the big boys at the back of the room. He had been rough and wild,

[008-029]but she never had been afraid of him, and often he had given her pretty

[008-030]things from the swamp.

[008-031]“What luck!” she cried. “I promised mother I would not go inside the

[008-032]swamp alone, and will you look at the cocoons I’ve found! There are

[008-033]more just screaming for me to come get them, because the leaves will

[008-034]fall with the first frost, and then the jays and crows will begin to

[008-035]tear them open. I haven’t much time, since I’m going to school. You

[008-036]will go with me, Pete! Please say yes! Just a little way!”

[008-037]“What are those things?” asked the man, his keen black eyes staring at

[008-038]her.

[008-039]“They are the cases these big caterpillars spin for winter, and in the

[008-040]spring they come out great night moths, and I can sell them. Oh, Pete,

[008-041]I can sell them for enough to take me through [[education-as-liberation|high school]] and dress me

[008-042]so like the others that I don’t look different, and if I have very good

[008-043]luck I can save some for college. Pete, please go with me?”

[008-044]“Why don’t you go like you always have?”

[008-045]“Well, the truth is, I had a little scare,” said Elnora. “I never did

[008-046]mean to go alone; sometimes I sort of wandered inside farther than I

[008-047]intended, chasing things. You know [[duncan|Duncan]] gave me [[freckles|Freckles]]’s books, and

[008-048]I have been gathering moths like he did. Lately I found I could sell

[008-049]them. If I can make a complete collection, I can get three hundred

[008-050]dollars for it. Three such collections would take me almost through

[008-051]college, and I’ve four years in [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] yet. That’s a long

[008-052]time. I might collect them.”

[008-053]“Can every kind there is be found here?”

[008-054]“No, not all of them, but when I get more than I need of one kind, I

[008-055]can trade them with collectors farther north and west, so I can

[008-056]complete sets. It’s the only way I see to earn the money. Look what I

[008-057]have already. Big gray Cecropias come from this kind; brown Polyphemus

[008-058]from that, and green Lunas from these. You aren’t working on Sunday. Go

[008-059]with me only an hour, Pete!”

[008-060]The man looked at her narrowly. She was young, wholesome, and

[008-061]beautiful. She was innocent, intensely in earnest, and she needed the

[008-062]money, he knew that.

[008-063]“You didn’t tell me what scared you,” he said.

[008-064]“Oh, I thought I did! Why you know I had Freckles’s box packed full of

[008-065]moths and specimens, and one evening I sold some to the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]].

[008-066]Next morning I found a note telling me it wasn’t safe to go inside the

[008-067]swamp. That sort of scared me. I think I’ll go alone, rather than miss

[008-068]the chance, but I’d be so happy if you would take care of me. Then I

[008-069]could go anywhere I chose, because if I mired you could pull me out.

[008-070]You will take care of me, Pete?”

[008-071]“Yes, I’ll take care of you,” promised Pete Corson.

[008-072]“Goody!” said Elnora. “Let’s start quick! And Pete, you look at these

[008-073]closely, and when you are hunting or going along the road, if one

[008-074]dangles under your nose, you cut off the little twig and save it for

[008-075]me, will you?”

[008-076]“Yes, I’ll save you all I see,” promised Pete. He pushed back his hat

[008-077]and followed Elnora. She plunged fearlessly among bushes, over

[008-078]underbrush, and across dead logs. One minute she was crying wildly,

[008-079]that here was a big one, the next she was reaching for a limb above her

[008-080]head or on her knees overturning dead leaves under a hickory or oak

[008-081]tree, or working aside black muck with her bare hands as she searched

[008-082]for buried pupae cases. For the first hour Pete bent back bushes and

[008-083]followed, carrying what Elnora discovered. Then he found one.

[008-084]“Is this the kind of thing you are looking for?” he asked bashfully, as

[008-085]he presented a wild cherry twig.

[008-086]“Oh Pete, that’s a Promethea! I didn’t even hope to find one.”

[008-087]“What’s the bird like?” asked Pete.

[008-088]“Almost black wings,” said Elnora, “with clay-coloured edges, and the

[008-089]most wonderful wine-coloured flush over the under side if it’s a male,

[008-090]and stronger wine above and below if it’s a female. Oh, aren’t I

[008-091]happy!”

[008-092]“How would it do to make what you have into a bunch that we could leave

[008-093]here, and come back for them?”

[008-094]“That would be all right.”

[008-095]Relieved of his load Pete began work. First, he narrowly examined the

[008-096]cocoons Elnora had found. He questioned her as to what other kinds

[008-097]would be like. He began to use the eyes of a trained woodman and hunter

[008-098]in her behalf. He saw several so easily, and moved through the forest

[008-099]so softly, that Elnora forgot the moths in watching him. Presently she

[008-100]was carrying the specimens, and he was making the trips of

[008-101]investigation to see which was a cocoon and which a curled leaf, or he

[008-102]was on his knees digging around stumps. As he worked he kept asking

[008-103]questions. What kind of logs were best to look beside, what trees were

[008-104]pupae cases most likely to be under; on what bushes did caterpillars

[008-105]spin most frequently? Time passed, as it always does when one’s

[008-106]occupation is absorbing.

[008-107]When the Sintons took Mrs. Comstock home, they stopped to see Elnora.

[008-108]She was not there. Mrs. Comstock called at the edge of her woods and

[008-109]received no reply. Then Wesley turned and drove back to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]].

[008-110]He left Margaret and Mrs. Comstock holding the team and entertaining

[008-111]Billy, while he entered the swamp.

[008-112]Elnora and Pete had made a wide trail behind them. Before Sinton had

[008-113]thought of calling, he heard voices and approached with some caution.

[008-114]Soon he saw Elnora, her flushed face beaming as she bent with an

[008-115]armload of twigs and branches and talked to a kneeling man.

[008-116]“Now go cautiously!” she was saying. “I am just sure we will find an

[008-117]Imperialis here. It’s their very kind of a place. There! What did I

[008-118]tell you! Isn’t that splendid? Oh, I am so glad you came with me!”

[008-119]Wesley stood staring in speechless astonishment, for the man had

[008-120]arisen, brushed the dirt from his hands, and held out to Elnora a small

[008-121]shining dark pupa case. As his face came into view Sinton almost cried

[008-122]out, for he was the one man of all others Wesley knew with whom he most

[008-123]feared for Elnora’s safety. She had him on his knees digging pupae

[008-124]cases for her from the swamp.

[008-125]“Elnora!” called Sinton. “Elnora!”

[008-126]“Oh, Uncle Wesley!” cried the girl. “See what luck we’ve had! I know we

[008-127]have a dozen and a half cocoons and we have three pupae cases. It’s

[008-128]much harder to get the cases because you have to dig for them, and you

[008-129]can’t see where to look. But Pete is fine at it! He’s found three, and

[008-130]he says he will keep watch beside the roads, and through the woods

[008-131]while he hunts. Isn’t that splendid of him? Uncle Wesley, there is a

[008-132]college over there on the western edge of the swamp. Look closely, and

[008-133]you can see the great dome up among the clouds.”

[008-134]“I should say you have had luck,” said Wesley, striving to make his

[008-135]voice natural. “But I thought you were not coming to the swamp?”

[008-136]“Well, I wasn’t,” said Elnora, “but I couldn’t find many anywhere else,

[008-137]honest, I couldn’t, and just as soon as I came to the edge I began to

[008-138]see them here. I kept my promise. I didn’t come in alone. Pete came

[008-139]with me. He’s so strong, he isn’t afraid of anything, and he’s

[008-140]perfectly splendid to locate cocoons! He’s found half of these. Come

[008-141]on, Pete, it’s getting dark now, and we must go.”

[008-142]They started toward the trail, Pete carrying the cocoons. He left them

[008-143]at the case, while Elnora and Wesley went on to the carriage together.

[008-144]“Elnora Comstock, what does this mean?” demanded her mother.

[008-145]“It’s all right, one of the neighbours was with her, and she got

[008-146]several dollars’ worth of stuff,” interposed Wesley.

[008-147]“You oughter seen my pa,” shouted Billy. “He was ist all whited out,

[008-148]and he laid as still as anything. They put him away deep in the

[008-149]ground.”

[008-150]“Billy!” breathed Margaret in a prolonged groan.

[008-151]“[[jimmy|Jimmy]] and [[belle|Belle]] are going to be together in a nice place. They are

[008-152]coming to see me, and Snap is right down here by the wheel. Here, Snap!

[008-153]My, but he’ll be tickled to get something to eat! He’s ’most twisted as

[008-154]me. They get new clothes, and all they want to eat, too, but they’ll

[008-155]miss me. They couldn’t have got along without me. I took care of them.

[008-156]I had a lot of things give to me ’cause I was the littlest, and I

[008-157]always divided with them. But they won’t need me now.”

[008-158]When she left the carriage Mrs. Comstock gravely shook hands with

[008-159]Billy. “Remember,” she said to him, “I love boys, and I love dogs.

[008-160]Whenever you don’t have a good time up there, take your dog and come

[008-161]right down and be my little boy. We will just have loads of fun. You

[008-162]should hear the whistles I can make. If you aren’t treated right you

[008-163]come straight to me.”

[008-164]Billy wagged his head sagely. “You ist bet I will!” he said.

[008-165]“Mother, how could you?” asked Elnora as they walked up the path.

[008-166]“How could I, missy? You better ask how couldn’t I? I just couldn’t!

[008-167]Not for enough to pay my road tax! Not for enough to pay the road tax,

[008-168]and the dredge tax, too!”

[008-169]“[[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]] always has been lovely to me, and I don’t think it’s

[008-170]fair to worry her.”

[008-171]“I choose to be lovely to Billy, and let her sweat out her own worries

[008-172]just as she has me, these sixteen years. There is nothing in all this

[008-173]world so good for people as taking a dose of their own medicine. The

[008-174]difference is that I am honest. I just say in plain English, ‘if they

[008-175]don’t treat you right, come to me.’ They have only said it in actions

[008-176]and inferences. I want to teach Mag Sinton how her own doses taste, but

[008-177]she begins to sputter before I fairly get the spoon to her lips. Just

[008-178]you wait!”

[008-179]“When I think what I owe her——” began Elnora.

[008-180]“Well, thank goodness, I don’t owe her anything, and so I’m perfectly

[008-181]free to do what I choose. Come on, and help me get supper. I’m hungry

[008-182]as Billy!”

[008-183]Margaret Sinton rocked slowly back and forth in her chair. On her

[008-184]breast lay Billy’s red head, one hand clutched her dress front with

[008-185]spasmodic grip, even after he was unconscious.

[008-186]“You mustn’t begin that, Margaret,” said Sinton. “He’s too heavy. And

[008-187]it’s bad for him. He’s better off to lie down and go to sleep alone.”

[008-188]“He’s very light, Wesley. He jumps and quivers so. He has to be

[008-189]stronger than he is now, before he will sleep soundly.”
