# CHAPTER I

[001-001]WHEREIN ELNORA GOES TO HIGH SCHOOL AND LEARNS MANY LESSONS NOT FOUND IN

[001-002]HER BOOKS

[001-003]“[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]], have you lost your senses?” demanded the angry voice

[001-004]of [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] while she glared at her daughter.

[001-005]“Why mother!” faltered the girl.

[001-006]“Don’t you ‘why mother’ me!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “You know very well

[001-007]what I mean. You’ve given me no peace until you’ve had your way about

[001-008]this going to school business; I’ve fixed you good enough, and you’re

[001-009]ready to start. But no child of mine walks the streets of [[onabasha|Onabasha]]

[001-010]looking like a play-actress woman. You wet your hair and comb it down

[001-011]modest and decent and then be off, or you’ll have no time to find where

[001-012]you belong.”

[001-013]Elnora gave one despairing glance at the white face, framed in a most

[001-014]becoming riot of reddish-brown hair, which she saw in the little

[001-015]kitchen mirror. Then she untied the narrow black ribbon, wet the comb

[001-016]and plastered the waving curls close to her head, bound them fast,

[001-017]pinned on the skimpy black hat and opened the back door.

[001-018]“You’ve gone so plumb daffy you are forgetting your dinner,” jeered her

[001-019]mother.

[001-020]“I don’t want anything to eat,” replied Elnora.

[001-021]“You’ll take your dinner or you’ll not go one step. Are you crazy? Walk

[001-022]almost three miles and no food from six in the morning until six at

[001-023]night. A pretty figure you’d cut if you had your way! And after I’ve

[001-024]gone and bought you this nice new pail and filled it especial to start

[001-025]on!”

[001-026]Elnora came back with a face still whiter and picked up the lunch.

[001-027]“Thank you, mother! Good-bye!” she said. Mrs. Comstock did not reply.

[001-028]She watched the girl follow the long walk to the gate and go from sight

[001-029]on the road, in the bright sunshine of the first Monday of September.

[001-030]“I bet a dollar she gets enough of it by night!” commented Mrs.

[001-031]Comstock.

[001-032]Elnora walked by instinct, for her eyes were blinded with tears. She

[001-033]left the road where it turned south, at the corner of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]],

[001-034]climbed a snake fence and entered a path worn by her own feet. Dodging

[001-035]under willow and scrub oak branches she came at last to the faint

[001-036]outline of an old trail made in the days when the precious timber of

[001-037][[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] was guarded by armed men. This path she followed until she

[001-038]reached a thick clump of bushes. From the debris in the end of a hollow

[001-039]log she took a key that unlocked the padlock of a large weatherbeaten

[001-040]old box, inside of which lay several books, a butterfly apparatus, and

[001-041]a small cracked mirror. The walls were lined thickly with gaudy

[001-042]butterflies, dragonflies, and moths. She set up the mirror and once

[001-043]more pulling the ribbon from her hair, she shook the bright mass over

[001-044]her shoulders, tossing it dry in the sunshine. Then she straightened

[001-045]it, bound it loosely, and replaced her hat. She tugged vainly at the

[001-046]low brown calico collar and gazed despairingly at the generous length

[001-047]of the narrow skirt. She lifted it as she would have cut it if

[001-048]possible. That disclosed the heavy high leather shoes, at sight of

[001-049]which she seemed positively ill, and hastily dropped the skirt. She

[001-050]opened the pail, removed the lunch, wrapped it in the napkin, and

[001-051]placed it in a small pasteboard box. Locking the case again she hid the

[001-052]key and hurried down the trail.

[001-053]She followed it around the north end of the swamp and then entered a

[001-054]footpath crossing a farm leading in the direction of the spires of the

[001-055]city to the northeast. Again she climbed a fence and was on the open

[001-056]road. For an instant she leaned against the fence staring before her,

[001-057]then turned and looked back. Behind her lay the land on which she had

[001-058]been born to drudgery and a mother who made no pretence of loving her;

[001-059]before her lay the city through whose schools she hoped to find means

[001-060]of escape and the way to reach the things for which she cared. When she

[001-061]thought of how she appeared she leaned more heavily against the fence

[001-062]and groaned; when she thought of turning back and wearing such [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]]

[001-063]in ignorance all the days of her life she set her teeth firmly and went

[001-064]hastily toward Onabasha.

[001-065]On [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]] crossing a deep culvert at the suburbs she glanced

[001-066]around, and then kneeling she thrust the [[elnoras-lunch-box|lunch box]] between the

[001-067]foundation and the flooring. This left her empty-handed as she

[001-068]approached the big stone [[education-as-liberation|high school]] building. She entered bravely and

[001-069]inquired her way to the office of the superintendent. There she learned

[001-070]that she should have come the previous week and arranged about her

[001-071]classes. There were many things incident to the opening of school, and

[001-072]one man unable to cope with all of them.

[001-073]“Where have you been attending school?” he asked, while he advised the

[001-074]teacher of Domestic Science not to telephone for groceries until she

[001-075]knew how many she would have in her classes; wrote an order for

[001-076]chemicals for the students of science; and advised the leader of the

[001-077]orchestra to hire a professional to take the place of the bass violist,

[001-078]reported suddenly ill.

[001-079]“I finished last spring at Brushwood school, [[brushwood-school|district number nine]],”

[001-080]said Elnora. “I have been studying all summer. I am quite sure I can do

[001-081]the first year work, if I have a few days to get started.”

[001-082]“Of course, of course,” assented the superintendent. “Almost invariably

[001-083]country pupils do good work. You may enter first year, and if it is too

[001-084]difficult, we will find it out speedily. Your teachers will tell you

[001-085]the list of books you must have, and if you will come with me I will

[001-086]show you the way to the auditorium. It is now time for opening

[001-087]exercises. Take any seat you find vacant.”

[001-088]Elnora stood before the entrance and stared into the largest room she

[001-089]ever had seen. The floor sloped to a yawning stage on which a band of

[001-090]musicians, grouped around a grand piano, were tuning their instruments.

[001-091]She had two fleeting impressions. That it was all a mistake; this was

[001-092]no school, but a grand display of enormous ribbon bows; and the second,

[001-093]that she was sinking, and had forgotten how to walk. Then a burst from

[001-094]the orchestra nerved her while a bevy of daintily clad, sweet-smelling

[001-095]things that might have been birds, or flowers, or possibly gaily

[001-096]dressed, happy young girls, pushed her forward. She found herself

[001-097]plodding across the back of the auditorium, praying for guidance, to an

[001-098]empty seat.

[001-099]As the girls passed her, vacancies seemed to open to meet them. Their

[001-100]friends were moving over, beckoning and whispering invitations. Every

[001-101]one else was seated, but no one paid any attention to the white-faced

[001-102]girl stumbling half-blindly down the aisle next the farthest wall. So

[001-103]she went on to the very end facing the stage. No one moved, and she

[001-104]could not summon courage to crowd past others to several empty seats

[001-105]she saw. At the end of the aisle she paused in desperation, while she

[001-106]stared back at the whole forest of faces most of which were now turned

[001-107]upon her.

[001-108]In a flash came the full realization of her scanty dress, her pitiful

[001-109]little hat and ribbon, her big, heavy shoes, her ignorance of where to

[001-110]go or what to do; and from a sickening wave which crept over her, she

[001-111]felt she was going to become very ill. Then out of the mass she saw a

[001-112]pair of big, brown boy eyes, three seats from her, and there was a

[001-113]message in them. Without moving his body he reached forward and with a

[001-114]pencil touched the back of the seat before him. Instantly Elnora took

[001-115]another step which brought her to a row of vacant front seats.

[001-116]She heard laughter behind her; the knowledge that she wore the only hat

[001-117]in the room burned her; every matter of moment, and some of none at

[001-118]all, cut and stung. She had no books. Where should she go when this was

[001-119]over? What would she give to be on the trail going home! She was

[001-120]shaking with a nervous chill when the music ceased, and the

[001-121]superintendent arose, and coming down to the front of the flower-decked

[001-122]platform, opened a Bible and began to read. Elnora did not know what he

[001-123]was reading, and she felt that she did not care. Wildly she was racking

[001-124]her brain to decide whether she should sit still when the others left

[001-125]the room or follow, and ask some one where the Freshmen went first.

[001-126]In the midst of the struggle one sentence fell on her ear. “Hide me

[001-127]under the shadow of Thy wings.”

[001-128]Elnora began to pray frantically. “Hide me, O God, hide me, under the

[001-129]shadow of Thy wings.”

[001-130]Again and again she implored that prayer, and before she realized what

[001-131]was coming, every one had arisen and the room was emptying rapidly.

[001-132]Elnora hurried after the nearest girl and in the press at the door

[001-133]touched her sleeve timidly.

[001-134]“Will you please tell me where the Freshmen go?” she asked huskily.

[001-135]The girl gave her one surprised glance, and drew away.

[001-136]“Same place as the fresh women,” she answered, and those nearest her

[001-137]laughed.

[001-138]Elnora stopped praying suddenly and the colour crept into her face.

[001-139]“I’ll wager you are the first person I meet when I find it,” she said

[001-140]and stopped short. “Not that! Oh, I must not do that!” she thought in

[001-141]dismay. “Make an enemy the first thing I do. Oh, not that!”

[001-142]She followed with her eyes as the young people separated in the hall,

[001-143]some climbing stairs, some disappearing down side halls, some entering

[001-144]adjoining doors. She saw the girl overtake the brown-eyed boy and speak

[001-145]to him. He glanced back at Elnora with a scowl on his face. Then she

[001-146]stood alone in the hall.

[001-147]Presently a door opened and a young woman came out and entered another

[001-148]room. Elnora waited until she returned, and hurried to her. “Would you

[001-149]tell me where the Freshmen are?” she panted.

[001-150]“Straight down the hall, three doors to your left,” was the answer, as

[001-151]the girl passed.

[001-152]“One minute please, oh please,” begged Elnora: “Should I knock or just

[001-153]open the door?”

[001-154]“Go in and take a seat,” replied the teacher.

[001-155]“What if there aren’t any seats?” gasped Elnora.

[001-156]“Classrooms are never half-filled, there will be plenty,” was the

[001-157]answer.

[001-158]Elnora removed her hat. There was no place to put it, so she carried it

[001-159]in her hand. She looked infinitely better without it. After several

[001-160]efforts she at last opened the door and stepping inside faced a smaller

[001-161]and more concentrated battery of eyes.

[001-162]“The superintendent sent me. He thinks I belong here,” she said to the

[001-163]professor in charge of the class, but she never before heard the voice

[001-164]with which she spoke. As she stood waiting, the girl of the hall passed

[001-165]on her way to the blackboard, and suppressed laughter told Elnora that

[001-166]her thrust had been repeated.

[001-167]“Be seated,” said the professor, and then because he saw Elnora was

[001-168]desperately embarrassed he proceeded to lend her a book and to ask her

[001-169]if she had studied algebra. She said she had a little, but not the same

[001-170]book they were using. He asked her if she felt that she could do the

[001-171]work they were beginning, and she said she did.

[001-172]That was how it happened, that three minutes after entering the room

[001-173]she was told to take her place beside the girl who had gone last to the

[001-174]board, and whose flushed face and angry eyes avoided meeting Elnora’s.

[001-175]Being compelled to concentrate on her proposition she forgot herself.

[001-176]When the professor asked that all pupils sign their work she firmly

[001-177]wrote “Elnora Comstock” under her demonstration. Then she took her seat

[001-178]and waited with white lips and trembling limbs, as one after another

[001-179]professor called the names on the board, while their owners arose and

[001-180]explained their propositions, or “flunked” if they had not found a

[001-181]correct solution. She was so eager to catch their forms of expression

[001-182]and prepare herself for her recitation, that she never looked from the

[001-183]work on the board, until clearly and distinctly, “Elnora Cornstock,”

[001-184]called the professor.

[001-185]The dazed girl stared at the board. One tiny curl added to the top of

[001-186]the first curve of the m in her name, had transformed it from a good

[001-187]old English patronymic that any girl might bear proudly, to Cornstock.

[001-188]Elnora sat speechless. When and how did it happen? She could feel the

[001-189]wave of smothered laughter in the air around her. A rush of anger

[001-190]turned her face scarlet and her soul sick. The voice of the professor

[001-191]addressed her directly.

[001-192]“This proposition seems to be beautifully demonstrated, Miss

[001-193]Cornstalk,” he said. “Surely, you can tell us how you did it.”

[001-194]That word of praise saved her. She could do good work. They might wear

[001-195]their pretty clothes, have their friends and make life a greater misery

[001-196]than it ever before had been for her, but not one of them should do

[001-197]better work or be more womanly. That lay with her. She was tall,

[001-198]straight, and handsome as she arose.

[001-199]“Of course I can explain my work,” she said in natural tones. “What I

[001-200]can’t explain is how I happened to be so stupid as to make a mistake in

[001-201]writing my own name. I must have been a little nervous. Please excuse

[001-202]me.”

[001-203]She went to the board, swept off the signature with one stroke, then

[001-204]rewrote it plainly. “My name is Comstock,” she said distinctly. She

[001-205]returned to her seat and following the formula used by the others made

[001-206]her first [[onabasha-high-school|high school]] recitation.

[001-207]As Elnora resumed her seat [[professor-henley|Professor Henley]] looked at her steadily. “It

[001-208]puzzles me,” he said deliberately, “how you can write as beautiful a

[001-209]demonstration, and explain it as clearly as ever has been done in any

[001-210]of my classes and still be so disturbed as to make a mistake in your

[001-211]own name. Are you very sure you did that yourself, Miss Comstock?”

[001-212]“It is impossible that any one else should have done it,” answered

[001-213]Elnora.

[001-214]“I am very glad you think so,” said the professor. “Being Freshmen, all

[001-215]of you are strangers to me. I should dislike to begin the year with you

[001-216]feeling there was one among you small enough to do a trick like that.

[001-217]The next proposition, please.”

[001-218]When the hour had gone the class filed back to the study room and

[001-219]Elnora followed in desperation, because she did not know where else to

[001-220]go. She could not study as she had no books, and when the class again

[001-221]left the room to go to another professor for the next recitation, she

[001-222]went also. At least they could put her out if she did not belong there.

[001-223]Noon came at last, and she kept with the others until they dispersed on

[001-224]the sidewalk. She was so abnormally self-conscious she fancied all the

[001-225]hundreds of that laughing, throng saw and jested at her. When she

[001-226]passed the brown-eyed boy walking with the girl of her encounter, she

[001-227]knew, for she heard him say: “Did you really let that gawky piece of

[001-228]calico get ahead of you?” The answer was indistinct.

[001-229]Elnora hurried from the city. She intended to get her lunch, eat it in

[001-230]the shade of the first tree, and then decide whether she would go back

[001-231]or go home. She knelt on the bridge and reached for her box, but it was

[001-232]so very light that she was prepared for the fact that it was empty,

[001-233]before opening it. There was one thing for which to be thankful. The

[001-234]boy or tramp who had seen her hide it, had left the napkin. She would

[001-235]not have to face her mother and account for its loss. She put it in her

[001-236]pocket, and threw the box into the ditch. Then she sat on the bridge

[001-237]and tried to think, but her brain was confused.

[001-238]“Perhaps the worst is over,” she said at last. “I will go back. What

[001-239]would mother say to me if I came home now?”

[001-240]So she returned to the high school, followed some other pupils to the

[001-241]coat room, hung her hat, and found her way to the study where she had

[001-242]been in the morning. Twice that afternoon, with aching head and empty

[001-243]stomach, she faced strange professors, in different branches. Once she

[001-244]escaped notice; the second time the worst happened. She was asked a

[001-245]question she could not answer.

[001-246]“Have you not decided on your course, and secured your books?” inquired

[001-247]the professor.

[001-248]“I have decided on my course,” replied Elnora, “I do not know where to

[001-249]ask for my books.”

[001-250]“Ask?” the professor was bewildered.

[001-251]“I understood the books were furnished,” faltered Elnora.

[001-252]“Only to those bringing an order from the township trustee,” replied

[001-253]the Professor.

[001-254]“No! Oh no!” cried Elnora. “I will have them to-morrow,” and gripped

[001-255]her desk for support for she knew that was not true. Four books,

[001-256]ranging perhaps at a dollar and a half apiece; would her mother buy

[001-257]them? Of course she would not—could not.

[001-258]Did not Elnora know the story of old. There was enough land, but no one

[001-259]to do clearing and farm. Tax on all those acres, recently the new

[001-260]gravel road tax added, the expense of living and only the work of two

[001-261]women to meet all of it. She was insane to think she could come to the

[001-262]city to school. Her mother had been right. The girl decided that if

[001-263]only she lived to reach home, she would stay there and lead any sort of

[001-264]life to avoid more of this torture. Bad as what she wished to escape

[001-265]had been, it was nothing like this. She never could live down the

[001-266]movement that went through the class when she inadvertently revealed

[001-267]the fact that she had expected books to be furnished. Her mother would

[001-268]not secure them; that settled the question.

[001-269]But the end of misery is never in a hurry to come; before the day was

[001-270]over the superintendent entered the room and explained that pupils from

[001-271]the country were charged a tuition of twenty dollars a year. That

[001-272]really was the end. Previously Elnora had canvassed a dozen methods for

[001-273]securing the money for books, ranging all the way from offering to wash

[001-274]the superintendent’s dishes to breaking into the bank. This additional

[001-275]expense made her plans so wildly impossible, there was nothing to do

[001-276]but hold up her head until she was from sight.

[001-277]Down the long corridor alone among hundreds, down the long street alone

[001-278]among thousands, out into the country she came at last. Across the

[001-279]fence and field, along [[limberlost-trail|the old trail]] once trodden by a boy’s bitter

[001-280]agony, now stumbled a white-faced girl, sick at heart. She sat on a log

[001-281]and began to sob in spite of her efforts at self-control. At first it

[001-282]was physical breakdown, later, thought came crowding.

[001-283]Oh the shame, the mortification! Why had she not known of the tuition?

[001-284]How did she happen to think that in the city books were furnished?

[001-285]Perhaps it was because she had read they were in several states. But

[001-286]why did she not know? Why did not her mother go with her? Other

[001-287]mothers—but when had her mother ever been or done anything at all like

[001-288]other mothers? Because she never had been it was useless to blame her

[001-289]now. Elnora realized she should have gone to town the week before,

[001-290]called on some one and learned all these things herself. She should

[001-291]have remembered how her clothing would look, before she wore it in

[001-292]public places. Now she knew, and her dreams were over. She must go home

[001-293]to feed chickens, calves, and pigs, wear calico and coarse shoes, and

[001-294]with averted head, pass a library all her life. She sobbed again.

[001-295]“For pity’s sake, honey, what’s the matter?” asked the voice of the

[001-296]nearest neighbour, [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], as he seated himself beside Elnora.

[001-297]“There, there,” he continued, smearing tears all over her face in an

[001-298]effort to dry them. “Was it as bad as that, now? Maggie has been just

[001-299]wild over you all day. She’s got nervouser every minute. She said we

[001-300]were foolish to let you go. She said your clothes were not right, you

[001-301]ought not to carry that tin pail, and that they would laugh at you. By

[001-302]gum, I see they did!”

[001-303]“Oh, Uncle Wesley,” sobbed the girl, “why didn’t she tell me?”

[001-304]“Well, you see, Elnora, she didn’t like to. You got such a way of

[001-305]holding up your head, and going through with things. She thought some

[001-306]way that you’d make it, till you got started, and then she begun to see

[001-307]a hundred things we should have done. I reckon you hadn’t reached that

[001-308]building before she remembered that your skirt should have been pleated

[001-309]instead of gathered, your shoes been low, and lighter for hot September

[001-310]weather, and a new hat. Were your clothes right, Elnora?”

[001-311]The girl broke into hysterical laughter. “Right!” she cried. “Right!

[001-312]Uncle Wesley, you should have seen me among them! I was a picture!

[001-313]They’ll never forget me. No, they won’t get the chance, for they’ll see

[001-314]me again to-morrow!

[001-315]“Now that is what I call spunk, Elnora! Downright grit,” said Wesley

[001-316]Sinton. “Don’t you let them laugh you out. You’ve helped Margaret and

[001-317]me for years at harvest and busy times, what you’ve earned must amount

[001-318]to quite a sum. You can get yourself a good many clothes with it.”

[001-319]“Don’t mention clothes, Uncle Wesley,” sobbed Elnora, “I don’t care now

[001-320]how I look. If I don’t go back all of them will know it’s because I am

[001-321]so poor I can’t buy my books.”

[001-322]“Oh, I don’t know as you are so dratted poor,” said Sinton

[001-323]meditatively. “There are three hundred acres of good land, with fine

[001-324]timber as ever grew on it.”

[001-325]“It takes all we can earn to pay the tax, and mother wouldn’t cut a

[001-326]tree for her life.”

[001-327]“Well then, maybe, I’ll be compelled to cut one for her,” suggested

[001-328]Sinton. “Anyway, stop tearing yourself to pieces and tell me. If it

[001-329]isn’t clothes, what is it?”

[001-330]“It’s books and tuition. Over twenty dollars in all.”

[001-331]“Humph! First time I ever knew you to be stumped by twenty dollars,

[001-332]Elnora,” said Sinton, patting her hand.

[001-333]“It’s the first time you ever knew me to want money,” answered Elnora.

[001-334]“This is different from anything that ever happened to me. Oh, how can

[001-335]I get it, Uncle Wesley?”

[001-336]“Drive to town with me in the morning and I’ll draw it from the bank

[001-337]for you. I owe you every cent of it.”

[001-338]“You know you don’t owe me a penny, and I wouldn’t touch one from you,

[001-339]unless I really could earn it. For anything that’s past I owe you and

[001-340][[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]] for all the home life and love I’ve ever known. I know

[001-341]how you work, and I’ll not take your money.”

[001-342]“Just a loan, Elnora, just a loan for a little while until you can earn

[001-343]it. You can be proud with all the rest of the world, but there are no

[001-344]secrets between us, are there, Elnora?”

[001-345]“No,” said Elnora, “there are none. You and Aunt Margaret have given me

[001-346]all the love there has been in my life. That is the one reason above

[001-347]all others why you shall not give me charity. Hand me money because you

[001-348]find me crying for it! This isn’t the first time this old trail has

[001-349]known tears and heartache. All of us know that story. [[freckles|Freckles]] stuck to

[001-350]what he undertook and won out. I stick, too. When [[duncan|Duncan]] moved away he

[001-351]gave me all Freckles left in the swamp, and as I have inherited his

[001-352]property maybe his luck will come with it. I won’t touch your money,

[001-353]but I’ll win some way. First, I’m going home and try mother. It’s just

[001-354]possible I could find second-hand books, and perhaps all the tuition

[001-355]need not be paid at once. Maybe they would accept it quarterly. But oh,

[001-356]Uncle Wesley, you and Aunt Margaret keep on loving me! I’m so lonely,

[001-357]and no one else cares!”

[001-358]Wesley Sinton’s jaws met with a click. He swallowed hard on bitter

[001-359]words and changed what he would have liked to say three times before it

[001-360]became articulate.

[001-361]“Elnora,” he said at last, “if it hadn’t been for one thing I’d have

[001-362]tried to take legal steps to make you ours when you were three years

[001-363]old. Maggie said then it wasn’t any use, but I’ve always held on. You
