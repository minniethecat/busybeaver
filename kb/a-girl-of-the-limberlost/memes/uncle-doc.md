Uncle Doc is the town physician of Onabasha, known to the community by the affectionate nickname that suggests both his professional role and his place in the social fabric of the town. He appears in the novel's middle chapters, providing medical care and representing the practical, caring side of small-town life.

## Role in the Community

As a doctor serving a rural community, Uncle Doc occupies a respected position. His nickname — combining the familial "Uncle" with the professional "Doc" — captures the dual nature of his role: he is both a trained medical professional and a familiar, trusted figure in the lives of the people he treats.

## Appearances in the Story

Uncle Doc first enters the narrative when Mrs. Comstock's health concerns arise — "The doctor says it's a cancer" (012-215), revealing the gravity of her condition. Philip Ammon identifies Uncle Doc as his relative: "Possibly you know my people — Dr. Ammon's? The doctor is my uncle" (013-141). When Philip didn't regain strength properly after his illness, "Uncle Doc sent for" additional help (013-143). He is "Aunt Margaret's doctor" (013-146), serving the extended Sinton-Ammon family network.

Uncle Doc's care extends beyond the consult room. Philip arranges to "stay with Uncle Doc, so if I feel it coming again he can do something" (013-203), reflecting the trust placed in him. When Elnora visits, "Uncle Doc said I could come" (014-127), and she later notes she "promised Uncle Doc to put on my waders and keep dry" (015-012) — evidence of the doctor's practical, preventative care. His presence reinforces the novel's depiction of Onabasha as a community where people look after one another.
