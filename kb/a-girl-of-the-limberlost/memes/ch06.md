# CHAPTER VI

[006-001]WHEREIN MRS. COMSTOCK INDULGES IN “FRILLS,” AND BILLY REAPPEARS

[006-002]It was [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] who really wrestled with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]’s problem while he

[006-003]drove about his business. He was not forced to ask himself what it

[006-004]meant; he knew. The old Corson gang was still holding together. Elder

[006-005]members who had escaped the law had been joined by a younger brother of

[006-006]Jack’s, and they met in the thickest of the few remaining fast places

[006-007]of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] to drink, gamble, and loaf. Then suddenly, there would be

[006-008]a robbery in some country house where a farmer that day had sold his

[006-009]wheat or corn and not paid a visit to the bank; or in some neighbouring

[006-010]village.

[006-011]The home of [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] and Elnora adjoined the swamp. Sinton’s land

[006-012]lay next, and not another residence or man easy to reach in case of

[006-013]trouble. Whoever wrote that note had some human kindness in his breast,

[006-014]but the fact stood revealed that he feared his strength if Elnora were

[006-015]delivered into his hands. Where had he been the previous night when he

[006-016]heard that prayer? Was that the first time he had been in such

[006-017]proximity? Sinton drove fast, for he wished to reach the swamp before

[006-018]Elnora and the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] would go there.

[006-019]At almost four he came to the case, and dropping on his knees studied

[006-020]the ground, every sense alert. He found two or three little heel

[006-021]prints. Those were made by Elnora or the Bird Woman. What Sinton wanted

[006-022]to learn was whether all the remainder were the footprints of one man.

[006-023]It was easily seen, they were not. There were deep, even tracks made by

[006-024]fairly new shoes, and others where a well-worn heel cut deeper on the

[006-025]inside of the print than at the outer edge. Undoubtedly some of

[006-026]Corson’s old gang were watching the case, and the visits of the women

[006-027]to it. There was no danger that any one would attack the Bird Woman.

[006-028]She never went to the swamp at night, and on her trips in the daytime,

[006-029]every one knew that she carried a revolver, understood how to use it,

[006-030]and pursued her work in a fearless manner.

[006-031]Elnora, prowling around the swamp and lured into the interior by the

[006-032]flight of moths and butterflies; Elnora, without father, money, or

[006-033]friends save himself, to defend her—Elnora was a different proposition.

[006-034]For this to happen just when [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] was bringing the very

[006-035]desire of her heart to the girl, it was too bad.

[006-036]Sinton was afraid for her, yet he did not want to add the burden of

[006-037]fear to Katharine Comstock’s trouble, or to disturb the joy of Elnora

[006-038]in her work. He stopped at [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] and slowly went up the walk. Mrs.

[006-039]Comstock was sitting on the front steps with some sewing. The work

[006-040]seemed to Sinton as if she might be engaged in putting a tuck in a

[006-041]petticoat. He thought of how Margaret had shortened Elnora’s dress to

[006-042]the accepted length for girls of her age, and made a mental note of

[006-043]Mrs. Comstock’s occupation.

[006-044]She dropped her work on her lap, laid her hands on it and looked into

[006-045]his face with a sneer.

[006-046]“You didn’t let any grass grow under your feet,” she said.

[006-047]Sinton saw her white, drawn face and comprehended.

[006-048]“I went to pay a debt and see about this opening of the ditch, Kate.”

[006-049]“You said you were going to prosecute me.”

[006-050]“Good gracious, Kate!” cried Sinton. “Is that what you have been

[006-051]thinking all day? I told you before I left yesterday that I would not

[006-052]need do that. And I won’t! We can’t afford to quarrel over Elnora.

[006-053]She’s all we’ve got. Now that she has proved that if you don’t do just

[006-054]what I think you ought by way of clothes and [[education-as-liberation|schooling]], she can take

[006-055]care of herself, I put that out of my head. What I came to see you

[006-056]about is a kind of scare I’ve had to-day. I want to ask you if you ever

[006-057]see anything about the swamp that makes you think the old Corson gang

[006-058]is still at work?”

[006-059]“Can’t say that I do,” said Mrs. Comstock. “There’s kind of dancing

[006-060]lights there sometimes, but I supposed it was just people passing along

[006-061]the road with lanterns. Folks hereabout are none too fond of the swamp.

[006-062]I hate it like death. I’ve never stayed here a night in my life without

[006-063]Robert’s revolver, clean and loaded, under my pillow, and the shotgun,

[006-064]same condition, by the bed. I can’t say that I’m afraid here at home.

[006-065]I’m not. I can take care of myself. But none of the swamp for me!”

[006-066]“Well, I’m glad you are not afraid, Kate, because I must tell you

[006-067]something. Elnora stopped at the case this morning, and somebody had

[006-068]been into it in the night.”

[006-069]“Broke the lock?”

[006-070]“No. Used a duplicate key. To-day I heard there was a man here last

[006-071]night. I want to nose around a little.”

[006-072]Sinton went to the east end of the cabin and looked up at the window.

[006-073]There was no way any one could have reached it without a ladder, for

[006-074]the logs were hewed and mortar filled the cracks even. Then he went to

[006-075]the west end, the willow faced him as he turned the corner. He examined

[006-076]the trunk carefully. There was no mistake about small particles of

[006-077]black swamp muck adhering to the sides of the tree. He reached the low

[006-078]branches and climbed the willow. There was earth on the large limb

[006-079]crossing Elnora’s window. He stood on it, holding the branch as had

[006-080]been done the night before, and looked into the room. He could see very

[006-081]little, but he knew that if it had been dark outside and sufficiently

[006-082]light for Elnora to study inside he could have seen vividly. He brought

[006-083]his face close to the netting, and he could see the bed with its head

[006-084]to the east, at its foot the table with the candles and the chair

[006-085]before it, and then he knew where the man had been who had heard

[006-086]Elnora’s prayer.

[006-087]Mrs. Comstock had followed around the corner and stood watching him.

[006-088]“Do you think some slinking hulk was up there peekin’ in at Elnora?”

[006-089]she demanded indignantly.

[006-090]“There is muck on the trunk, and plenty on the limb,” said Sinton.

[006-091]“Hadn’t you better get a saw and let me take this branch off?”

[006-092]“No, I hadn’t,” said Mrs. Comstock. “First place, Elnora’s climbed from

[006-093]that window on that limb all her life, and it’s hers. Second place, no

[006-094]one gets ahead of me after I’ve had warning. Any crow that perches on

[006-095]that roost again will get its feathers somewhat scattered. Look along

[006-096]the fence, there, and see if you can find where he came in.”

[006-097]The place was easy to find as was a trail leading for some distance

[006-098]west of the cabin.

[006-099]“You just go home, and don’t fret yourself,” said Mrs. Comstock. “I’ll

[006-100]take care of this. If you should hear the dinner bell at any time in

[006-101]the night you come down. But I wouldn’t say anything to Elnora. She

[006-102]better keep her mind on her studies, if she’s going to school.”

[006-103]When the work was finished that night Elnora took her books and went to

[006-104]her room to prepare some lessons, but every few minutes she looked

[006-105]toward the swamp to see if there were lights near the case. Mrs.

[006-106]Comstock raked together the coals in the cooking stove, got out the

[006-107][[elnoras-lunch-box|lunch box]], and sitting down she studied it grimly. At last she arose.

[006-108]“Wonder how it would do to show [[margaret-sinton|Mag Sinton]] a frill or two,” she

[006-109]murmured.

[006-110]She went to her room, knelt before a big black-walnut chest and hunted

[006-111]through its contents until she found an old-fashioned cook book. She

[006-112]tended the fire as she read and presently was in action. She first

[006-113]sawed an end from a fragrant, juicy, sugar-cured ham and put it to

[006-114]cook. Then she set a couple of eggs boiling, and after long hesitation

[006-115]began creaming butter and sugar in a crock. An hour later the odour of

[006-116]the ham, mingled with some of the richest spices of “happy Araby,” in a

[006-117]combination that could mean nothing save spice cake, crept up to Elnora

[006-118]so strongly that she lifted her head and sniffed amazedly. She would

[006-119]have given all her precious money to have gone down and thrown her arms

[006-120]around her mother’s neck, but she did not dare move.

[006-121]Mrs. Comstock was up early, and without a word handed Elnora the case

[006-122]as she left the next morning.

[006-123]“Thank you, mother,” said Elnora, and went on her way.

[006-124]She walked down the road looking straight ahead until she came to the

[006-125]corner, where she usually entered the swamp. She paused, glanced that

[006-126]way and smiled. Then she turned and looked back. There was no one

[006-127]coming in any direction. She followed the road until well around the

[006-128]corner, then she stopped and sat on a grassy spot, laid her books

[006-129]beside her and opened the lunch box. Last night’s odours had in a

[006-130]measure prepared her for what she would see, but not quite. She

[006-131]scarcely could believe her senses. Half the bread compartment was

[006-132]filled with dainty sandwiches of bread and butter sprinkled with the

[006-133]yolk of egg and the remainder with three large slices of the most

[006-134]fragrant spice cake imaginable. The meat dish contained shaved cold

[006-135]ham, of which she knew the quality, the salad was tomatoes and celery,

[006-136]and the cup held preserved pear, clear as amber. There was milk in the

[006-137]bottle, two tissue-wrapped cucumber pickles in the folding

[006-138]drinking-cup, and a fresh napkin in the ring. No lunch was ever

[006-139]daintier or more palatable; of that Elnora was perfectly sure. And her

[006-140]mother had prepared it for her! “She does love me!” cried the happy

[006-141]girl. “Sure as you’re born she loves me; only she hasn’t found it out

[006-142]yet!”

[006-143]She touched the papers daintily, and smiled at the box as if it were a

[006-144]living thing. As she began closing it a breath of air swept by, lifting

[006-145]the covering of the cake. It was like an invitation, and breakfast was

[006-146]several hours away. Elnora picked up a piece and ate it. That cake

[006-147]tasted even better than it looked. Then she tried a sandwich. How did

[006-148]her mother come to think of making them that way. They never had any at

[006-149]home. She slipped out the fork, sampled the salad, and one-quarter of

[006-150]pear. Then she closed the box and started down the road nibbling one of

[006-151]the pickles and trying to decide exactly how happy she was, but she

[006-152]could find no standard high enough for a measure.

[006-153]She was to go to the Bird Woman’s after school for the last load from

[006-154]the case. Saturday she would take the arrow points and specimens to the

[006-155]bank. That would exhaust her present supplies and give her enough money

[006-156]ahead to pay for books, tuition, and clothes for at least two years.

[006-157]She would work early and late gathering nuts. In October she would sell

[006-158]all the ferns she could find. She must collect specimens of all tree

[006-159]leaves before they fell, gather nests and cocoons later, and keep her

[006-160]eyes wide open for anything the grades could use. She would see the

[006-161]superintendent that night about selling specimens to the ward

[006-162]buildings. She must be ahead of any one else if she wanted to furnish

[006-163]these things. So she approached [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]].

[006-167][[jimmy|Jimmy]] and [[belle|Belle]]—and we brought you a present.”
