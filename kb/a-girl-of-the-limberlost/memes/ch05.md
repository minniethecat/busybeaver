# CHAPTER V

[005-001]WHEREIN ELNORA RECEIVES A WARNING, AND BILLY APPEARS ON THE SCENE

[005-002]It would be difficult to describe how happy [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] was that morning as

[005-003]she hurried through her work, bathed and put on the neat, dainty

[005-004]gingham dress, and the tan shoes. She had a struggle with her hair. It

[005-005]crinkled, billowed, and shone, and she could not avoid seeing the

[005-006]becoming frame it made around her face. But in deference to her

[005-007]mother’s feelings the girl set her teeth, and bound her hair closely to

[005-008]her head with a shoe-string. “Not to be changed at the case,” she told

[005-009]herself.

[005-010]That her mother was watching she was unaware. Just as she picked up the

[005-011]beautiful brown ribbon [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] spoke.

[005-012]“You had better let me tie that. You can’t reach behind yourself and do

[005-013]it right.”

[005-014]Elnora gave a little gasp. Her mother never before had proposed to do

[005-015]anything for the girl that by any possibility she could do herself. Her

[005-016]heart quaked at the thought of how her mother would arrange that bow,

[005-017]but Elnora dared not refuse. The offer was too precious. It might never

[005-018]be made again.

[005-019]“Oh thank you!” said the girl, and sitting down she held out the

[005-020]ribbon.

[005-021]Her mother stood back and looked at her critically.

[005-022]“You haven’t got that like [[margaret-sinton|Mag Sinton]] had it last night,” she

[005-023]announced. “You little idiot! You’ve tried to plaster it down to suit

[005-024]me, and you missed it. I liked it away better as Mag fixed it, after I

[005-025]saw it. You didn’t look so peeled.”

[005-026]“Oh mother, mother!” laughed Elnora, with a half sob in her voice.

[005-027]“Hold still, will you?” cried Mrs. Comstock. “You’ll be late, and I

[005-028]haven’t packed your dinner yet.”

[005-029]She untied the string and shook out the hair. It rose with electricity

[005-030]and clung to her fingers and hands. Mrs. Comstock jumped back as if

[005-031]bitten. She knew that touch. Her face grew white, and her eyes angry.

[005-032]“Tie it yourself,” she said shortly, “and then I’ll put on the ribbon.

[005-033]But roll it back loose like Mag did. It looked so pretty that way.”

[005-034]Almost fainting Elnora stood before the glass, divided off the front

[005-035]parts of her hair, and rolled them as Mrs. Sinton had done; tied it at

[005-036]the nape of her neck, then sat while her mother arranged the ribbon.

[005-037]“If I pull it down till it comes tight in these creases where she had

[005-038]it, it will be just right, won’t it?” queried Mrs. Comstock, and the

[005-039]amazed Elnora stammered,

[005-040]“Yes.”

[005-041]When she looked in the glass the bow was perfectly tied, and how the

[005-042]gold tone of the brown did match the lustre of the shining hair!

[005-043]“That’s pretty,” commented Mrs. Comstock’s soul, but her stiff lips had

[005-044]said all that could be forced from them for once. Just then Wesley

[005-045]Sinton came to the door.

[005-046]“Good morning,” he cried heartily. “Elnora, you look a picture! My, but

[005-047]you’re sweet! If any of the city boys get sassy you tell your Uncle

[005-048]Wesley, and he’ll horsewhip them. Here’s your Christmas present from

[005-049]me.” He handed Elnora [[elnoras-lunch-box|the leather lunch box]], with her name carved

[005-050]across the strap in artistic lettering.

[005-051]“Oh [[wesley-sinton|Uncle Wesley]]!” was all Elnora could say.

[005-052]“Your Aunt Maggie filled it for me for a starter,” he said. “Now, if

[005-053]you are ready, I’m going to drive past your way and you can ride almost

[005-054]to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] with me, and save the new shoes that much.”

[005-055]Elnora was staring at the box. “Oh I hope it isn’t impolite to open it

[005-056]before you,” she said. “I just feel as if I must see inside.”

[005-057]“Don’t you stand on formality with the neighbours,” laughed Sinton.

[005-058]“Look in your box if you want to!”

[005-059]Elnora slipped the strap and turned back the lid.

[005-060]This disclosed the knife, fork, napkin, and spoon, the milk flask, and

[005-061]the interior packed with dainty sandwiches wrapped in tissue paper, and

[005-062]the little compartments for meat, salad, and the custard cup.

[005-063]“Oh mother!” cried Elnora. “Oh mother, isn’t it fine? What made you

[005-064]think of it, Uncle Wesley? How will I ever thank you? No one will have

[005-065]a finer lunch box than I. Oh I do thank you! That’s the nicest gift I

[005-066]ever had. How I love Christmas in September!”

[005-067]“It’s a mighty handy thing,” assented Mrs. Comstock, taking in every

[005-068]detail with sharp eyes. “I guess you are glad now you went and helped

[005-069]Mag and Wesley when you could, Elnora?”

[005-070]“Deedy, yes,” laughed Elnora, “and I’m going again first time they have

[005-071]a big day if I stay from school to do it.”

[005-072]“You’ll do no such thing!” said the delighted Sinton. “Come now, if

[005-073]you’re going!”

[005-074]“If I ride, can you spare me time to run into [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] to my box a

[005-075]minute?” asked Elnora.

[005-076]The light she had seen the previous night troubled her.

[005-077]“Sure,” said Wesley largely. So they drove away and left a white-faced

[005-078]woman watching them from the door, her heart a little sorer than usual.

[005-079]“I’d give a pretty to hear what he’ll say to her!” she commented

[005-080]bitterly. “Always sticking in, always doing things I can’t ever afford.

[005-081]Where on earth did he get that thing and what did it cost?”

[005-082]Then she entered [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] and began the day’s work, but mingled with

[005-083]the brooding bitterness of her soul was the vision of a sweet young

[005-084]face, glad with a gladness never before seen on it, and over and over

[005-085]she repeated: “I wonder what he’ll say to her!”

[005-086]What he said was that she looked as fresh and sweet as a posy, and to

[005-087]be careful not to step in the mud or scratch her shoes when she went to

[005-088]the case.

[005-089]Elnora found her key and opened the door. Not where she had placed it,

[005-090]but conspicuously in front lay her little heap of bills, and a crude

[005-091]scrawl of writing beside it. Elnora picked up the note in astonishment.

[005-092]DERE ELNORY,

[005-093]the lord amighty is hiding you all right done you ever dout it this

[005-094]money of yourn was took for some time las nite but it is returned with

[005-095]intres for god sake done ever come to the swamp at nite or late evnin

[005-096]or mornin or far in any time sompin worse an you know could git you

[005-097]A FREND.

[005-098]Elnora began to tremble. She hastily glanced around. The damp earth

[005-099]before the case had been trodden by large, roughly shod feet. She

[005-100]caught up the money and the note, thrust them into her guimpe, locked

[005-101]the case, and ran to the road.

[005-102]She was so breathless and her face so white Sinton noticed it.

[005-103]“What in the world’s the matter, Elnora?” he asked.

[005-104]“I am half afraid!” she panted.

[005-105]“Tut, tut, child!” said Wesley Sinton. “Nothing in the world to be

[005-106]afraid of. What happened?”

[005-107]“Uncle Wesley,” said Elnora, “I had more money than I brought home last

[005-108]night, and I put it in my case. Some one has been there. The ground is

[005-109]all trampled, and they left this note.”

[005-110]“And took your money, I’ll wager,” said Sinton angrily.

[005-111]“No,” answered Elnora. “Read the note, and oh Uncle Wesley, tell me

[005-112]what it means!”

[005-113]Sinton’s face was a study. “I don’t know what it means,” he said. “Only

[005-114]one thing is clear. It means some beast who doesn’t really want to harm

[005-115]you has got his eye on you, and he is telling you plain as he can, not

[005-116]to give him a chance. You got to keep along the roads, in the open, and

[005-117]not let the biggest moth that ever flew toll you out of hearing of us,

[005-118]or your mother. It means that, plain and distinct.”

[005-119]“Just when I can sell them! Just when everything is so lovely on

[005-120]account of them! I can’t! I can’t stay away from the swamp. The

[005-121]Limberlost is going to buy the books, the clothes, pay the tuition, and

[005-122]even start a [[elnoras-college-dream|college fund]]. I just can’t!”

[005-123]“You’ve got to,” said Sinton. “This is plain enough. You go far in the

[005-124]swamp at your own risk, even in daytime.”

[005-125]“Uncle Wesley,” said the girl, “last night before I went to bed, I was

[005-126]so happy I tried to pray, and I thanked God for hiding me ‘under the

[005-127]shadow of His wing.’ But how in the world could any one know it?”

[005-128]Wesley Sinton’s heart leaped in his breast. His face was whiter than

[005-129]the girl’s now.

[005-130]“Were you praying out loud, honey?” he almost whispered.

[005-131]“I might have said words,” answered Elnora. “I know I do sometimes.

[005-132]I’ve never had any one to talk with, and I’ve played with and talked to

[005-133]myself all my life. You’ve caught me at it often, but it always makes

[005-134]mother angry when she does. She says it’s silly. I forget and do it,

[005-135]when I’m alone. But Uncle Wesley, if I said anything last night, you

[005-136]know it was the merest whisper, because I’d have been so afraid of

[005-137]waking mother. Don’t you see? I sat up late, and studied two lessons.”

[005-138]Sinton was steadying himself “I’ll stop and examine the case as I come

[005-139]back,” he said. “Maybe I can find some clue. That other—that was just

[005-140]accidental. It’s a common expression. All the preachers use it. If I

[005-141]tried to pray, that would be the very first thing I’d say.”

[005-142]The colour returned to Elnora’s face.

[005-143]“Did you tell your mother about this money, Elnora?” he asked.

[005-144]“No, I didn’t,” said Elnora. “It’s dreadful not to, but I was afraid.

[005-145]You see they are clearing the swamp so fast. Every year it grows more

[005-146]difficult to find things, and Indian stuff becomes scarcer. I want to

[005-147]graduate, and that’s four years unless I can double on the course. That

[005-148]means twenty dollars tuition each year, and new books, and clothes.

[005-149]There won’t ever be so much at one time again, that I know. I just got

[005-150]to hang to my money. I was afraid to tell her, for fear she would want

[005-151]it for taxes, and she really must sell a tree or some cattle for that,

[005-152]mustn’t she, Uncle Wesley?”

[005-153]“On your life, she must!” said Wesley. “You put your little wad in the

[005-154]bank all safe, and never mention it to a living soul. It doesn’t seem

[005-155]right, but your case is peculiar. Every word you say is a true word.

[005-156]Each year you will find less in the swamp, and things everywhere will

[005-157]be scarcer. If you ever get a few dollars ahead, that can start your

[005-158]college fund. You know you are going to college, Elnora!”

[005-159]“Of course I am,” said Elnora. “I settled that as soon as I knew what a

[005-160]college was. I will put all my money in the bank, except what I owe

[005-161]you. I’ll pay that now.”

[005-162]“If your arrows are heavy,” said Wesley, “I’ll drive on to Onabasha

[005-163]with you.”

[005-164]“But they are not. Half of them were nicked, and this little box held

[005-165]all the good ones. It’s so surprising how many are spoiled when you

[005-166]wash them.”

[005-167]“What does he pay?”

[005-168]“Ten cents for any common perfect one, fifty for revolvers, a dollar

[005-169]for obsidian, and whatever is right for enormous big ones.”

[005-170]“Well, that sounds fair,” said Sinton. “You can come down Saturday and

[005-171]wash the stuff at our house, and I’ll take it in when we go marketing

[005-172]in the afternoon.”

[005-173]Elnora jumped from the carriage. She soon found that with her books,

[005-174]her lunch box, and the points she had a heavy load. She had almost

[005-175]reached [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]] crossing the culvert when she heard distressed

[005-176]screams of a child. Across an orchard of the suburbs came a small boy,

[005-177]after him a big dog, urged by a man in the background. Elnora’s heart

[005-178]was with the small fleeing figure in any event whatever. She dropped

[005-179]her load on the bridge, and with practised hand flung a stone at the

[005-180]dog. The beast curled double with a howl. The boy reached the fence,

[005-181]and Elnora was there to help him over. As he touched the top she swung

[005-182]him to the ground, but he clung to her, clasping her tightly, sobbing

[005-183]with fear. Elnora helped him to the bridge, and sat with him in her

[005-184]arms. For a time his replies to her questions were indistinct, but at

[005-185]last he became quieter and she could understand.

[005-186]He was a mite of a boy, nothing but skin-covered bones, his burned,

[005-187]freckled face in a mortar of tears and dust, his [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]] unspeakably

[005-188]dirty, one great toe in a festering mass from a broken nail, and sores

[005-189]all over the visible portions of the small body.

[005-190]“You won’t let the mean old thing make his dog get me!” he wailed.

[005-191]“Indeed no,” said Elnora, holding him closely.

[005-192]“You wouldn’t set a dog on a boy for just taking a few old apples when

[005-193]you fed ’em to pigs with a shovel every day, would you?”

[005-194]“No, I would not,” said Elnora hotly.

[005-195]“You’d give a boy all the apples he wanted, if he hadn’t any breakfast,

[005-196]and was so hungry he was all twisty inside, wouldn’t you?”

[005-197]“Yes, I would,” said Elnora.

[005-198]“If you had anything to eat you would give me something right now,

[005-199]wouldn’t you?”

[005-200]“Yes,” said Elnora. “There’s nothing but just stones in the package.

[005-201]But my dinner is in that case. I’ll gladly divide.”

[005-202]She opened the box. The famished child gave a little cry and reached

[005-203]both hands. Elnora caught them back.

[005-204]“Did you have any supper?”

[005-205]“No.”

[005-206]“Any dinner yesterday?”

[005-207]“An apple and some grapes I stole.”

[005-208]“Whose boy are you?”

[005-209]“Old [[tom-levering|Tom]] Billings’s.”

[005-210]“Why doesn’t your father get you something to eat?”

[005-211]“He does most days, but he’s drunk now.”

[005-212]“Hush, you must not!” said Elnora. “He’s your father!”

[005-213]“He’s spent all the money to get drunk, too,” said the boy, “and [[jimmy|Jimmy]]

[005-214]and [[belle|Belle]] are both crying for breakfast. I’d a got out all right with

[005-215]an apple for myself, but I tried to get some for them and the dog got

[005-216]too close. Say, you can throw, can’t you?”

[005-217]“Yes,” admitted Elnora. She poured half the milk into the cup. “Drink

[005-218]this,” she said, holding it to him.

[005-219]The boy gulped the milk and swore joyously, gripping the cup with

[005-220]shaking fingers.

[005-221]“Hush!” cried Elnora. “That’s dreadful!”

[005-222]“What’s dreadful?”

[005-223]“To say such awful words.”

[005-224]“Huh! pa says worser ’an that every breath he draws.”

[005-225]Elnora saw that the child was older than she had thought. He might have

[005-226]been forty judging by his hard, unchildish expression.

[005-227]“Do you want to be like your father?”

[005-228]“No, I want to be like you. Couldn’t a angel be prettier ’an you. Can I

[005-229]have more milk?”

[005-230]Elnora emptied the flask. The boy drained the cup. He drew a breath of

[005-231]satisfaction as he gazed into her face.

[005-232]“You wouldn’t go off and leave your little boy, would you?” he asked.

[005-233]“Did some one go away and leave you?”

[005-234]“Yes, my mother went off and left me, and left Jimmy and Belle, too,”

[005-235]said the boy. “You wouldn’t leave your little boy, would you?”

[005-236]“No.”

[005-237]The boy looked eagerly at the box. Elnora lifted a sandwich and

[005-238]uncovered the fried chicken. The boy gasped with delight.

[005-239]“Say, I could eat the stuff in the glass and the other box and carry

[005-240]the bread and the chicken to Jimmy and Belle,” he offered.

[005-241]Elnora silently uncovered the custard with preserved cherries on top

[005-242]and handed it and the spoon to the child. Never did food disappear

[005-243]faster. The salad went next, and a sandwich and half a chicken breast

[005-245]“I better leave the rest for Jimmy and Belle,” he said, “they’re ’ist

[005-246]fightin’ hungry.”

[005-247]Elnora gave him the remainder of the carefully prepared lunch. The boy

[005-248]clutched it and ran with a sidewise hop like a wild thing. She covered

[005-335]They are from a living bird. [[phoebe-simms|Phoebe Simms]] gathers them in her orchard

[005-338]there would have told that?

[005-339]“I haven’t a doubt but I can get you some,” she offered. “She gave Aunt

[005-340]Margaret a large bunch, and those are part of them. I am quite sure she

[005-341]has more, and would spare some.”

[005-342]Sadie Reed laughed shortly. “You needn’t trouble,” she said, “I was

[005-343]fooled. I thought they were expensive quills. I wanted them for a

[005-356]How those girls did laugh! One of them walked with Elnora to the

[005-357]auditorium, sat beside her during exercises, and tried to talk whenever

[005-358]she dared, to keep Elnora from seeing the curious and admiring looks

[005-359]bent upon her.

[005-360]For the brown-eyed boy whistled, and there was pantomime of all sorts

[005-361]going on behind Elnora’s back that day. Happy with her books, no one

[005-362]knew how much she saw, and from her absorption in her studies it was

[005-363]evident she cared too little to notice.

[005-364]After school she went again to the home of the Bird Woman, and together

[005-365]they visited the swamp and carried away more specimens. This time

[005-366]Elnora asked the Bird Woman to keep the money until noon of the next

[005-367]day, when she would call for it and have it added to her bank account.

[005-368]She slowly walked home, for the visit to the swamp had brought back

[005-369]full force the experience of the morning. Again and again she examined

[005-370]the crude little note, for she did not know what it meant, yet it bred

[005-371]vague fear. The only thing of which Elnora knew herself afraid was her

[005-372]mother; when with wild eyes and ears deaf to childish pleading, she

[005-373]sometimes lost control of herself in the night and visited [[limberlost-pool|the pool]]

[005-374]where her husband had sunk before her, calling his name in unearthly

[005-375]tones and begging of the swamp to give back its dead.
