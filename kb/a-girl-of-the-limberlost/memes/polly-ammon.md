# Polly Ammon

## Overview

Polly Ammon is [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]'s younger sister and a lively presence in the novel's later chapters. Engaged to [[tom-levering|Tom Levering]], Polly is sharp-eyed, playful, and acutely attuned to the social undercurrents among [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], Philip, and ultimately [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]. She serves as a bridge between the sophisticated Chicago social world and the simpler life Philip discovers in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. (019-071)

## Character

### The Sharp-Eyed Observer

Polly possesses a keen social intelligence. At the ball, she is the one who tells Edith about [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s moth-inspired dress, having done her own "sleuth hunt" to discover what it looked like:

> "Yes, Polly Ammon told me. I knew when I heard about it how you would look, so I started a sleuth hunt, to get the first peep." (019-071)

She reads social situations with precision, sensing Edith's anger before most others do and alerting [[tom-levering|Tom Levering]] to the brewing storm. (019-231)

### The Playful Sister

Polly's relationship with Philip is warm and affectionate. When she visits him at [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] cabin, she greets him with childlike openness:

> "She did!" said Polly Ammon, stopping on the path and lifting her chin as a little child, while she drew away her veil. Philip caught her in his arms and stooped for his mother's kiss. (022-182)

Her playful nature provides a counterbalance to the intense emotional drama surrounding Edith and Philip.

### The Loyal Friend

When [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s behavior threatens to disrupt the gathering at [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], Polly takes charge. She rises crying "Phil! Phil!" (022-147) and helps redirect the chaotic situation. Later, she joins [[tom-levering|Tom]] in the "delightful occupation of paying off old scores" (022-226) — a humorous, human moment that reveals her capacity for gentle mischief.

## Key Events

1. **The Ball Revelation** (019-071) — Polly tells Edith about [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s dress, setting in motion Edith's jealousy and the dramatic confrontation that follows.

2. **The Dance Floor Warning** (019-231) — Dancing with [[tom-levering|Tom Levering]], Polly spots Edith standing alone in the middle of the floor looking "awful mad" and alerts [[tom-levering|Tom]] to the coming trouble.

3. **The Visit to the Cabin** (022-147) — Polly and [[tom-levering|Tom]] accompany Edith and [[hart-henderson|Henderson]] to Philip's cabin. Polly cries out for Philip when the car arrives, and her warm greeting contrasts sharply with Edith's tense demeanor.

4. **Paying Off Old Scores** (022-226) — Inside [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], Polly and [[tom-levering|Tom]] engage in playful banter, lightening the heavy atmosphere created by Edith's presence.

## Significance

Polly Ammon represents the novel's ideal of wholesome, unpretentious [[womanhood-and-gender-roles|femininity]]. Unlike [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s dramatic, possessive love, Polly's relationship with [[tom-levering|Tom Levering]] is characterized by mutual understanding, playfulness, and loyalty. She is the "darling girl who will love him plenty" (015-289) — the kind of partner the novel endorses.

Her presence at [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] also symbolizes the bridging of two worlds: Philip's Chicago family and his new life connected to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. Polly's ready acceptance of the rustic setting — and her immediate warmth toward [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — signals the novel's conviction that genuine character transcends social station.
