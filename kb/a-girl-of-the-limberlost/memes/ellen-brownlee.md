# Ellen Brownlee

## Overview

Ellen Brownlee is one of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]'s earliest allies in [[onabasha|Onabasha]]. The daughter of a department store owner, Ellen first appears in Chapter II when she helps the country girl [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] purchase appropriate school clothes. She reappears throughout the early chapters as a steady, kind presence — running to catch [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] on the street, leading the celebrated "rescue" picnic, and facilitating [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s integration into [[onabasha|Onabasha]] society. (002-086)

## Character

### The Helpful Guide

Ellen's first action in the novel establishes her character immediately. Seeing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at her father's store, she takes charge with practical kindness:

> "I'll tell you," said Ellen Brownlee, "my father owns this store, I know all the clerks. I'll take you to Miss Hartley. You tell her just how much you want to spend, and what you want to buy, and she will know exactly what to select." (002-086)

Ellen does not mock [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s country appearance. Instead, she provides the practical help that bridges the gap between [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s rural upbringing and [[onabasha|Onabasha]]'s social expectations.

### The Compassionate Friend

When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] suffers social humiliation, Ellen is among the first to offer genuine comfort. After [[education-as-liberation|learning]] that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] has no father, Ellen speaks with heartfelt sincerity:

> "You have cause," said Ellen Brownlee. "A father is the very dearest person in the whole round world, except a mother, who is just a dear." (006-277)

Her words, though they inadvertently highlight [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s loss, are spoken with such warmth that they register as comfort rather than thoughtlessness.

### The Picnic Leader

Ellen's most vivid appearance comes in Chapter VI when she leads a group of [[onabasha|Onabasha]] girls in what becomes known as the "rescue" picnic:

> It was Ellen Brownlee and her crowd, and every girl of them carried a big parcel. (006-440)

When the group encounters chaos — a fleeing dog, a half-naked girl and boy — Ellen takes command: "We got to refill this box first! Who's got the butter?" (006-472) Her cheerful leadership transforms a chaotic scene into a festive breakfast, demonstrating the social ease that makes her a natural bridge between [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s two worlds.

### The Telephone Call

In Chapter XI, Ellen's role as [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s ally reaches a new level when the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] calls her directly before [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s entertainment:

> The Bird Woman ran to the telephone and called Ellen Brownlee. "Elnora has had an accident. She will be a little late," she said. "You have got to make them wait. Have them play extra music." (011-103)

That the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] trusts Ellen with this task — managing the audience before [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s performance — confirms Ellen's status as a reliable member of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s support network.

## Key Events

1. **The Store Encounter** (002-086) — Ellen meets [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at her father's department store and guides her to the right sales clerk, initiating [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s transformation from country girl to properly dressed student.

2. **The Freshman Report** (002-105) — Ellen reports to her friends about the "strange girl from the country" named [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — not with mockery, but with interest that sparks [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]]'s recognition.

3. **The Street Call** (006-234) — Ellen runs from her house to catch [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] on the street, delivering an urgent message. She is the go-between connecting [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to her [[onabasha|Onabasha]] friends.

4. **The Father Conversation** (006-277) — Ellen speaks movingly about the value of a father, inadvertently touching [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s deepest wound but doing so with genuine warmth.

5. **The Rescue Picnic** (006-440) — Ellen leads a group of girls in a chaotic but joyful picnic that involves chasing a dog and improvising a breakfast feast.

6. **The Entertainment Call** (011-103) — [[bird-woman|The Bird Woman]] entrusts Ellen with managing the audience at [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s school entertainment, confirming her place in [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s inner circle.

## Significance

Ellen Brownlee represents the novel's conviction that kindness and practical help — not wealth or [[social-class-and-appearance|social status]] — are what truly integrate a person into a [[community-support|community]]. She is not the wealthiest or most prominent girl in [[onabasha|Onabasha]], but her warm, competent nature makes her [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s natural ally. (002-086)

Ellen also embodies the novel's idealized vision of female friendship: supportive without being competitive, practical without being calculating. Like [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] in the domestic sphere and the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] in the intellectual sphere, Ellen Brownlee in the social sphere helps [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] navigate a world that was initially hostile to her.
