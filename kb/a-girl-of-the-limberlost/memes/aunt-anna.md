## Overview

Aunt Anna is Philip Ammon's aunt residing in Chicago. A minor but supportive character, she takes Philip to call on Miss Brownlee (Ellen Brownlee's family), expresses disapproval of Edith Carr, and ultimately supports Elnora as Philip's choice. Her role is to represent the family perspective that validates Elnora over the socially prominent Edith Carr.

## Role in the Novel

Aunt Anna appears primarily in the Chicago sections of the novel (Chapters 13, 17, and 21). She serves as a domestic anchor for Philip in the city and provides a contrast to the fashionable Lake Shore Club society represented by Edith Carr's circle.

### Taking Philip to Call on Miss Brownlee

In Chapter 13, Aunt Anna takes Philip to call on the Brownlee family, connecting the Chicago social circles with the Onabasha community (013-397). This visit demonstrates her awareness of Philip's growing attachment to the Limberlost region and its people.

### Unpacking Gifts with Philip

In Chapter 17, Aunt Anna helps Philip unpack items and shares in his enthusiasm about his time in the Limberlost (017-194). This moment shows her as a sympathetic confidante who understands Philip's emotional connection to Elnora before he fully acknowledges it himself.

### Disapproval of Edith Carr

Though not directly quoted, Aunt Anna's opinions are referenced in the narrative. She is said to have expressed reservations about Edith Carr (021-085), aligning her with the narrative's clear preference for Elnora's natural character over Edith's artificial sophistication.

## Character Significance

Aunt Anna functions as a narrative device: a respected family elder whose approval of Elnora and reservations about Edith Carr validate the novel's moral framework. She represents the sensible, mature perspective that sees through social pretension to recognize genuine worth — mirroring the novel's broader theme of natural goodness triumphing over artificial social status.
