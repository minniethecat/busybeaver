# Love and Courtship

## Definition

Love and courtship in *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]* refers to the romantic relationships that drive the novel's second half, most prominently the love triangle involving [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]], [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], and [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]. The novel presents a moral vision of love in which genuine affection is demonstrated through sacrifice, shared work, and mutual respect rather than passion or social advantage. Courtship is portrayed as a serious process of character testing, not a game of attraction. (014-204)(021-055)

The novel also explores love in its non-romantic forms — maternal love, neighborly love, and love of nature — positioning romantic love as one expression of a broader capacity for devotion and self-giving. (001-340)

## Role in the Story

The central courtship narrative begins when [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], a wealthy young man from Chicago, comes to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] to recover from an illness. He meets [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] while she is [[moth-collecting|collecting moths]], and their shared love of nature forms the foundation of their relationship. (011-213) Philip is initially engaged to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], a beautiful Chicago socialite, but his time with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] reveals the difference between superficial attraction and genuine compatibility.

The love triangle reaches its crisis when Edith visits [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] and confronts [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. Edith's beauty, wealth, and [[social-class-and-appearance|social status]] initially intimidate [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], but Philip's choice is ultimately clear: he values [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s character, intelligence, and kindness over Edith's glamour. "I am going to marry her when I am big enough," young [[billy|Billy]] declares prophetically about Philip's intentions toward [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. (011-328)

Philip must prove his worth before he can win [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. He works alongside [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] clearing the Comstock land, demonstrating that he is willing to labor — not just pay others — to earn his place. (004-077) When he finally proposes, he does so directly and respectfully: "[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]], I came to ask [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to marry me." (021-055) The novel's conclusion affirms that love built on shared values and mutual sacrifice is more enduring than love based on attraction alone.

## Themes

### Love as Choice and Sacrifice

The novel presents love not as a feeling but as a choice — one that requires sacrifice. Philip chooses [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] knowing it means [[sacrifice|giving up]] the easy life of Chicago society for the [[work-and-self-reliance|hard work]] of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] farm. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s willingness to release Philip rather than trap him in a marriage he might regret demonstrates that true love prioritizes the beloved's happiness above one's own. (022-433)(023-212)

### Nature as the Ground of True Love

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and Philip's love grows in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] — not in drawing rooms or ballrooms. Their shared appreciation for moths, birds, and the natural world forms the substance of their bond. The novel suggests that love rooted in nature and shared work is more authentic than love born of social convenience. (010-125)

### The Testing of Love

Both [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and Philip must pass tests before their love can be fulfilled. Philip must prove he can work, sacrifice, and choose wisely. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] must prove she can love without possessiveness, willing to let Philip go if it serves his happiness. The testing motif reflects the novel's broader moral framework: nothing valuable comes without effort. (019-091)
