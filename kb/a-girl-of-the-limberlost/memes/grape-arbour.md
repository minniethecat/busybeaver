## Description

The grape arbour is a circular structure at the Sinton farm, covered with grapevines that create dappled shade and a cool resting place. Its benches offer comfort after farm labour, with cold buttermilk served to visitors. (013-346) Sunlight filters through the leaves in "flickering patches" that soothe tired eyes, while bees produce "splendid music" around the structure. (013-349) A big Turnus butterfly once sailed through the arbour, pausing over the table where [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] offered it a lump of sugar. (013-380)

The arbour serves as an outdoor parlour — a place of rest, conversation, and contemplation. [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] stretched on its bench and fell asleep on his first visit, lulled by the shade and the hum of bees. (013-349) The space is described as an "old grape arbour in the country," rustic and unpretentious, set near a log cabin where meals are shared after farm work. (015-334)

## Location

The grape arbour stands on the Sinton farm, within sight of the Sinton house and the surrounding country fields. From its benches one can see tomato vines, day lilies, and the broader farm landscape that stretches toward [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. (015-304) The arbour is situated close enough to the farmhouse that [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] can enter it directly to deliver news, and the family gathers there in the evenings after moth-collecting expeditions. (016-293)

The arbour is enclosed enough to afford a sense of privacy — [[billy|Billy]] crept toward it from the west woods fence, satisfying his childhood curiosity about who was present — yet open enough to enjoy the moonlight and the night air heavy with the odours of damp vegetation. (017-014)

## Significance

The grape arbour serves as the novel's primary setting for the developing relationship between [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] and [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. It is here that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] writes her letters — including one that begins "in an old grape arbour in the country" — and where she and Philip share long conversations about ambitions, nature, and their futures. (015-334) Philip, a city man from Chicago, comes to love resting on the arbour bench; his vote to spend the summer in the country is vindicated by moments like these. (015-304)

The arbour also represents the hospitality of the Sinton household and the rural domesticity that gradually draws even [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] into its circle. Her growing openness to companionship is reflected in her willingness to sit in the arbour with the young people, a marked change from her earlier isolation in the Comstock cabin.

In the moonlight scenes, the arbour transforms into a place of music and emotional [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]]. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] plays her violin there, the strings "voicing the joy of a happy girl's heart," while [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] listens with her head leaning against the structure. (017-010) The damp night air, the intense moonlight under which print could be read, and the surrounding odours create an atmosphere of sensory richness that mirrors [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s inner awakening.

## Key Scenes

**Philip's First Visit** (013-346–013-380) — [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] arrives at the Sinton farm, splashes in the water trough, changes his boots, and falls asleep on the arbour bench after drinking buttermilk. A Turnus butterfly sails through the arbour, and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] feeds it a lump of sugar — a gesture that captures her deep affinity with the natural world and foreshadows their connection.

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] Writes a Letter** (015-334) — Seated in the arbour, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] composes a letter describing the log cabin, the country dinner, and the pastoral surroundings. The letter becomes evidence of her literary sensibility and her genuine contentment in the rural landscape, qualities that distinguish her from Philip's Chicago acquaintances.

**Moonlight Gathering** (016-293–017-018) — After an evening of moth collecting, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] sits in the arbour under moonlight so intense that a book could be read by it. The gathering marks [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s increasing integration into the social world [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] inhabits. [[billy|Billy]], creeping from the west woods, satisfies his curiosity about the assembly.

**Violin in the Arbour** (017-010–017-027) — [[billy|Billy]] hears [[violin|Elnora's violin]] pouring out "a perfect jumble of everything" in "an exultant tumult" from the grape arbour. The music expresses the joy of her heart. Philip enters the arbour shortly after, and the scene confirms the deepening emotional bond between the two young people.
