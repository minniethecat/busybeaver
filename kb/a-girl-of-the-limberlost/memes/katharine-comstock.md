# Katharine Comstock

## Overview

Katharine Comstock is [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s mother and one of the most complex characters in Gene Stratton-Porter's *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]*. For sixteen years she has lived in bitter grief over her husband [[grief-and-loss|Robert Comstock's death]], withholding affection from their daughter [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. Her transformation from cold, resentful widow to proud, loving mother forms the emotional backbone of the novel. (001-006)

The Sinton family observed that "Mrs. Comstock had a great trouble" and that this grief shaped her entire interaction with the world. (002-109)

## Character

### Grief and Bitterness

Katharine Comstock's defining trait in the early chapters is her unprocessed grief. She wears black, keeps Robert's personal effects in a closed room, and refuses to engage with the world beyond her cabin. Her response to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s desire for [[education-as-liberation|education]] is harsh and dismissive:

> "Don't you 'why mother' me!" cried Mrs. Comstock. "You know very well what I mean. You've given me no peace until you've had your way about this going to school business; I've fixed you good enough, and you're ready to start. But no child of mine walks the streets of Onabasha looking like a play-actress woman." (001-006)

When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] says goodbye, her mother's silence speaks volumes:

> "Good-bye, mother! Good-bye!" she said. Mrs. Comstock did not reply. (001-027)

### The Turning Point

Katharine's transformation begins when [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] confronts her about her treatment of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. Her defensive response reveals the depth of her pain:

> "I can't!" cried Mrs. Comstock. "You know I can't!" (004-074)

She is terrified of losing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] the way she lost Robert — to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] Swamp. When the topic of Robert's trees arises, her anguish erupts:

> "Robert's trees!" shrieked Mrs. Comstock. "Tear up his land!" (004-081)

Yet even in her coldness, glimpses of care emerge. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] returns from her [[elnora-first-day-of-school|first day at school]], Mrs. Comstock has ready the dinner she earlier claimed to deny:

> "I thought likely you would be," said Mrs. Comstock, "and so I got supper ready for you." (001-443)

### The Reformed Mother

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s success in school and her [[work-and-self-reliance|earning]]s from moth collecting gradually wear down Katharine's defenses. The mother who once sneered at every hope eventually becomes [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s supporter. (004-326)

When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] declares "Thank you, mother," the simple exchange marks how far their relationship has traveled from the silence of that first morning. (006-123)

## Key Events

1. **[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s First Day of School** (001-006) — Mrs. Comstock forces [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to wear unfashionable clothes and sends her off without lunch money, setting the stage for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s humiliation and resilience.

2. **The Sinton Intervention** (004-013) — [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], after spending a sleepless night rehearsing "diplomatic speeches," confronts Mrs. Comstock about her treatment of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]].

3. **L[[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s Earnings** (001-500) — When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] reveals she needs ten dollars for school books, Mrs. Comstock exclaims "Ten dollars!" in shock, setting up the financial struggle that drives [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to moth collecting.

4. **The Truth [[About]] Robert** (017) — L[[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] the true circumstances of her husband's death breaks through Katharine's emotional walls and allows [[forgiveness-and-redemption|reconciliation]] with her daughter.

5. **The Reconciliation** (006-123) — Katharine begins to show genuine maternal warmth, responding to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s gratitude with quiet care rather than sarcasm.

## Significance

Katharine Comstock serves as the novel's primary source of conflict in its opening chapters, yet she is never a villain. Her character arc illustrates the corrosive power of unresolved grief and the possibility of [[forgiveness-and-redemption|redemption]] through love and truth. (002-109)

Her relationship with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] mirrors the novel's broader theme of transformation through connection — whether to nature, to knowledge, or to other people.
