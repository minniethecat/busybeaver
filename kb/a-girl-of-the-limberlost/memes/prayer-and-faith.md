# Prayer and Faith

## Definition

Prayer and faith in *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]* refer to the deeply personal, often private religious sensibility that runs through the lives of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] and other characters. Unlike institutional or doctrinal religion, the faith depicted in the novel is practical and immediate — a direct appeal to God for help, protection, and guidance in moments of crisis. It is a faith rooted in the natural world of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] as much as in [[onabasha-church|the church at Onabasha]]. (001-128)

Prayer in the novel is not a ritual act but a spontaneous cry from the heart. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s first recorded prayer — "Hide me, O God, hide me, under the shadow of thy wing" (001-128) — is drawn directly from Psalm 17:8, establishing a biblical foundation for her personal spirituality. Throughout the novel, prayer marks the emotional peaks of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s journey: her humiliation, her hope, her gratitude, and her love.

## Role in the Story

### Elnora's Crisis Prayers

Prayer enters the narrative at [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s lowest moment. On her first day at [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]], mocked for her country clothes and misnamed on the blackboard, she finds herself pushed onto the auditorium stage during a morning assembly. As a man opens a Bible and reads, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] does not listen to the words — she prays frantically: "Hide me, O God, hide me, under the shadow of thy wing." She implores the prayer "again and again," and before she realizes what is happening, she is no longer on the platform but seated in the audience, invisible among the crowd. (001-128)(001-130)

This moment establishes a pattern: [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] prays when she is powerless, and the novel suggests that her prayers are answered — not through supernatural intervention but through the small mercies of circumstance and her own resilience. After the assembly, she stops praying and "the colour crept into her face" (001-138), marking a shift from desperate supplication to quiet determination.

### Prayer as Work Ethic

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s faith evolves over the course of the novel. After the crisis of her first day, she reflects: "The way to get an answer to prayer is to work for it" (003-018). This pragmatic theology — prayer matched by effort — becomes her guiding philosophy. She does not merely ask God for help; she walks the country roads, collects moths, studies late into the night, and sells specimens door to door.

At noon on her second day of school, she prays again: "Oh, dear Lord make it come true" (003-072) — a prayer for her moth-collecting enterprise to succeed. This prayer, unlike her desperate plea for concealment, is a prayer of hope and aspiration.

### Katharine's Prayer of Thanksgiving

The most transformative prayer in the novel belongs not to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] but to her mother. When [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] discovers the value of the moths [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] has collected — realizing that the very swamp she has hated holds the key to her daughter's future — she prays: "Oh, thank God, for my precious, precious money. Why, I didn't pray in vain after all! I thought when I asked the Lord to hide me, He wasn't doing it because I wasn't praying properly" (004-627, 004-632). This prayer marks the turning point in [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]'s character — from bitterness to gratitude — and mirrors [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s own first prayer, creating a spiritual bond between mother and daughter. (004-628)

### The Answered Prayer

Later, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] explicitly connects her success to divine providence. When she sells her first moths to the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]], she prays again: "I was so happy I tried to pray, and I thanked God for hiding me 'under the shadow of His wing'" (005-126). This echo of her first desperate prayer, now transformed into a prayer of gratitude, shows how faith has sustained her through her trials. [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], overhearing her, asks gently, "Were you praying out loud, honey?" (005-128) — a moment of tenderness that acknowledges the genuine depth of her spirituality.

### Faith in Others

Beyond explicit prayer, the novel depicts a broader faith — faith in other people, faith in the natural world, and faith that [[work-and-self-reliance|hard work]] will be rewarded. [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]'s unwavering support for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is an act of faith: he believes in her potential before she has proven it. [[bird-woman|The Bird Woman]] speaks of sharing "the wonders God has given to us" with "cooped-up city people" (003-272–273), framing her conservation work as a religious calling. Even [[billy|Billy]], the rough country boy, speaks of heaven with simple, unshakeable conviction: "Them things is in heaven!" (007-101).

## Themes

### Prayer as Refuge

Within the confines of their difficult circumstances, prayer offers [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] a private sanctuary. No one in [[onabasha|Onabasha]] knows that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is praying on the auditorium stage; no one in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] hears [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] pray in the night. Prayer is the one resource available to the powerless, the humiliated, and the grieving — and the novel suggests it is sufficient. (001-128)(004-632)

### Faith Without Institution

The novel's treatment of religion is notably non-institutional. While [[onabasha-church|the church at Onabasha]] appears as a social setting (011-035), and [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] is described as doing "the church-going and Bible reading" (007-428), the most powerful spiritual moments occur outdoors, in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], or in the privacy of a bedroom at night. Faith is personal, immediate, and deeply felt — not mediated by clergy or doctrine.

### Gratitude as Spiritual Maturity

The arc of prayer in the novel moves from petition to thanksgiving. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] begins by asking God to hide her; she ends by thanking God for having done so. This transformation mirrors her growth from a frightened girl to a confident young woman, and suggests that spiritual maturity lies not in having one's requests granted but in recognizing the gifts already given. (005-126)
