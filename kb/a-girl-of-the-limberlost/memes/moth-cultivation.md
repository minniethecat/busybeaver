## Overview

Moth cultivation — the indoor raising of caterpillars through their life cycle to adult moths — is a distinct practice from the wild collection of specimens in the Limberlost swamp. Elnora gathers caterpillars and cocoons, feeds them specific leaves, protects them through winter, and watches them transform. This patient, nurturing work represents a different dimension of her relationship with nature than the active hunting of moths in the swamp.

## Distinct from Wild Collection

While [[moth-collecting]] involves finding, capturing, and mounting adult specimens in the Limberlost, cultivation takes place at the Comstock cabin. Elnora creates a controlled environment where caterpillars can feed, spin cocoons, and eventually emerge as moths. This practice requires different skills: knowledge of host plants for each species, patience through the long winter months, and protective measures against predators.

## The Cultivation Process

### Gathering Caterpillars and Cocoons

Elnora searches the Limberlost for caterpillars and freshly spun cocoons (008-013). The Bird Woman's advertisement seeks "CATERPILLARS, COCOONS, CHRYSALIDES, PUPAE CASES" (003-055), recognizing that cultivated specimens are often in better condition than wild-caught adults. Elnora learns to identify the specific leaves each caterpillar species requires for food (004-244).

### Winter Care

Through the winter, Elnora maintains her growing collection of cocoons in the cabin (004-610). Her attic room becomes a miniature laboratory, with twigs and crevices stocked with dangling cocoons (004-609). This winter work is quiet and patient, contrasting with the active summer collecting season.

## The Emperor Moth Tragedy

The most dramatic cultivation episode involves Elnora's Yellow Emperor moths. She carefully raises the caterpillars and protects the cocoons, anticipating the emergence of the prized yellow specimens. But a mouse penetrates the case and eats the moths before they can be mounted (013-267). This devastating loss — the destruction of months of patient work — is one of the novel's most poignant moments, illustrating the fragility of Elnora's hopes and the cruelty of circumstance.

## Scientific and Economic Significance

Cultivation serves both scientific and economic purposes. Scientifically, it allows Elnora to observe the complete life cycle of moth species, deepening her knowledge as a naturalist. Economically, cultivated specimens often command higher prices than wild-caught ones, as the Bird Woman values undamaged, perfectly emerged specimens. The cultivation practice thus contributes to Elnora's growing income and independence.
