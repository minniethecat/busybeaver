# CHAPTER XIII

[013-001]WHEREIN MOTHER LOVE IS BESTOWED ON ELNORA, AND SHE FINDS AN ASSISTANT

[013-002]IN MOTH HUNTING

[013-003][[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] awoke at dawn and lay gazing around the unfamiliar room. She

[013-004]noticed that every vestige of masculine attire and belongings was gone,

[013-005]and knew, without any explanation, what that meant. For some reason

[013-006]every tangible evidence of her father was banished, and she was at last

[013-007]to be allowed to take his place. She turned to look at her mother. Mrs.

[013-008]Comstock’s face was white and haggard, but on it rested an expression

[013-009]of profound peace Elnora never before had seen. As she studied the

[013-010]features on the pillow beside her, the heart of the girl throbbed in

[013-011]tenderness. She realized as fully as any one else could what her mother

[013-012]had suffered. Thoughts of the night brought shuddering fear. She softly

[013-013]slipped from the bed, went to her room, dressed and entered the kitchen

[013-014]to attend the Emperors and prepare breakfast. The pair had been left

[013-015]clinging to the piece of calico. The calico was there and a few pieces

[013-016]of beautiful wing. A mouse had eaten the moths!

[013-017]“Well, of all the horrible luck!” gasped Elnora.

[013-018]With the first thought of her mother, she caught up the remnants of the

[013-019]moths, burying them in the ashes of the stove. She took the bag to her

[013-020]room, hurriedly releasing its contents, but there was not another

[013-021]yellow one. Her mother had said some had been confined in the case in

[013-022][[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. There was still a hope that an Emperor might be among

[013-023]them. She peeped at her mother, who still slept soundly.

[013-024]Elnora took a large piece of mosquito netting, and ran to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]].

[013-025]Throwing it over the top of the case, she unlocked the door. She

[013-026]reeled, faint with distress. The living moths that had been confined

[013-027]there in their fluttering to escape to night and the mates they sought

[013-028]not only had wrecked the other specimens of the case, but torn

[013-029]themselves to fringes on the pins. A third of the rarest moths of the

[013-030]collection for the man of India were antennaless, legless, wingless,

[013-031]and often headless. Elnora sobbed aloud.

[013-032]“This is overwhelming,” she said at last. “It is making a fatalist of

[013-033]me. I am beginning to think things happen as they are ordained from the

[013-034]beginning, this plainly indicating that there is to be no college, at

[013-035]least, this year, for me. My life is all mountain-top or canon. I wish

[013-036]some one would lead me into a few days of ‘green pastures.’ Last night

[013-037]I went to sleep on mother’s arm, the moths all secured, love and

[013-038]college, certainties. This morning I wake to find all my hopes wrecked.

[013-039]I simply don’t dare let mother know that instead of helping me, she has

[013-040]ruined my collection. Everything is gone—unless the love lasts. That

[013-041]actually seemed true. I believe I will go see.”

[013-042]The love remained. Indeed, in the overflow of the long-hardened,

[013-043]pent-up heart, the girl was almost suffocated with tempestuous caresses

[013-044]and generous offerings. Before the day was over, Elnora realized that

[013-045]she never had known her mother. The woman who now busily went through

[013-046][[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], her eyes bright, eager, alert, constantly planning, was a

[013-047]stranger. Her very face was different, while it did not seem possible

[013-048]that during one night the acid of twenty years could disappear from a

[013-049]voice and leave it sweet and pleasant.

[013-050]For the next few days Elnora worked at mounting the moths her mother

[013-051]had taken. She had to go to the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] and tell about the disaster,

[013-052]but [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] was allowed to think that Elnora delivered the moths

[013-053]when she made the trip. If she had told her what actually happened, the

[013-054]chances were that Mrs. Comstock again would have taken possession of

[013-055]the Limberlost, hunting there until she replaced all the moths that had

[013-056]been destroyed. But Elnora knew from experience what it meant to

[013-057]collect such a list in pairs. It would require steady work for at least

[013-058]two summers to replace the lost moths. When she left the Bird Woman she

[013-059]went to the president of the [[onabasha|Onabasha]] schools and asked him to do all

[013-060]in his power to secure her a room in one of the ward buildings.

[013-061]The next morning the last moth was mounted, and the housework finished.

[013-062]Elnora said to her mother, “If you don’t mind, I believe I will go into

[013-063]the woods pasture beside [[sleepy-snake-creek|Sleepy Snake Creek]] and see if I can catch some

[013-064]dragonflies or moths.”

[013-065]“Wait until I get a knife and a pail and I will go along,” answered

[013-066]Mrs. Comstock. “The dandelions are plenty tender for greens among the

[013-067]deep grasses, and I might just happen to see something myself. My eyes

[013-068]are pretty sharp.”

[013-069]“I wish you could realize how young you are,” said Elnora. “I know

[013-070]women in Onabasha who are ten years older than you, yet they look

[013-071]twenty years younger. So could you, if you would dress your hair

[013-072]becomingly, and wear appropriate clothes.”

[013-073]“I think my hair puts me in the old woman class permanently,” said Mrs.

[013-074]Comstock.

[013-075]“Well, it doesn’t!” cried Elnora. “There is a woman of twenty-eight who

[013-076]has hair as white as yours from sick headaches, but her face is young

[013-077]and beautiful. If your face would grow a little fuller and those lines

[013-078]would go away, you’d be lovely!”

[013-079]“You little pig!” laughed Mrs. Comstock. “Any one would think you would

[013-080]be satisfied with having a splinter new mother, without setting up a

[013-081]kick on her looks, first thing. Greedy!”

[013-082]“That is a good word,” said Elnora. “I admit the charge. I am greedy

[013-083]over every wasted year. I want you young, lovely, suitably dressed and

[013-084]enjoying life like the other girls’ mothers.”

[013-085]Mrs. Comstock laughed softly as she pushed back her sunbonnet so that

[013-086]shrubs and bushes beside the way could be scanned closely. Elnora

[013-087]walked ahead with a case over her shoulder, a net in her hand. Her head

[013-088]was bare, the rolling collar of her lavender gingham dress was cut in a

[013-089]V at the throat, the sleeves only reached the elbows. Every few steps

[013-090]she paused and examined the shrubbery carefully, while Mrs. Comstock

[013-091]was watching until her eyes ached, but there were no dandelions in the

[013-092]pail she carried.

[013-096]last they reached the creek, following it toward [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]]. Here Mrs.

[013-097]Comstock found a large bed of tender dandelions and stopped to fill her

[013-098]pail. Then she sat on the bank, picking over the greens, while she

[013-099]listened to the creek softly singing its June song.

[013-112]He was closer Elnora than her mother, but Mrs. Comstock thought

[013-115]sapling and leaned over the water to call Elnora. With her lips parted

[013-130]“Is that so?” cried the man. “Wait a minute. I’ve never seen one. I

[013-131]suppose it’s a Cecropia, from the location.”

[013-132]“Of course,” said Elnora. “It’s so cool here the moth hasn’t emerged.

[013-133]The cocoon is a big, baggy one, and it is as red as fox tail.”

[013-134]“What luck!” he cried. “Are you making a collection?”

[013-136]embankment to Elnora’s side, produced a knife and began the work of

[013-155]“Do you call that work?” Elnora indicated the creek.

[013-156]“I do, indeed! Nearly three miles, banks too soft to brag on and never

[013-158]“Yes,” laughed Elnora. “Work at which you might kill yourself and never

[013-159]get a fish. Did any one tell you there were trout in Sleepy Snake

[013-160]Creek?”

[013-161]“Uncle said I could try.”

[013-165]“But that isn’t exercise.”

[013-166]“Well, if you only want exercise, go right on fishing. You will have a
