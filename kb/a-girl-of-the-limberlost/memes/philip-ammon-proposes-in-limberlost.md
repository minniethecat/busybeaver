## Summary

On an early July afternoon in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] kneels before [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] and asks her to be his wife. Drawing a white velvet ring box from his pocket, he presents her with an engagement ring he had his sister [[polly-ammon|Polly]] purchase in Chicago, measured from a glove-finger tracing. (022-044) [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], though she believes in Philip's love, hesitates to accept immediately because she cannot forget [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] — the woman to whom Philip was previously betrothed. (022-011) Philip's direct question echoes through [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]: "[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], will you be my wife?" (022-086)

## Context

The proposal occurs one month into [[philip-ammon-returns-limberlost|Philip's return]] to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], during what the novel describes as "a reproduction of the previous June" — a season of renewed [[love-and-courtship|courtship]] following the dramatic events at the Lake Shore Club in Chicago where Philip's engagement to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] ended. (022-003) Despite spending every day with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], guiding her through [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] and sharing in her work with moths, Philip received no definitive answer to his repeated declarations of love. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] believed in his love, "yet she hesitated to accept him, because she could not forget [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]." (022-011)

Philip, determined to secure [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s commitment, wrote to his sister [[polly-ammon|Polly]] in Chicago to procure an engagement ring. Unable to measure [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s finger precisely, he traced the inside of her glove finger as a guide and sent it to [[polly-ammon|Polly]]. (022-048) The ring represented Philip's deliberate choice: he was severing his Chicago ties and staking his future on [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] and on [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]].

## Key Participants

**[[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]** — Arriving across the fields through [[comstock-garden|the garden]], Philip is passionately in love and determined to secure [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s promise. He tells her, "[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], you are the veriest Queen of Love this afternoon. From the tips of your toes to your shining crown, I worship you. I want no woman save you." (022-040) When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] hesitates, he negotiates a promise: she will tell him the instant she reaches a decision, rather than making him ask repeatedly. (022-092)

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Standing beneath the willow tree, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] receives Philip's proposal with a mixture of love and caution. She tells him she must be "absolutely certain, for when I say yes, and give myself to you, only death shall part us." (022-087) Her hesitation is not doubt of Philip's character but fear of being a consolation prize after [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]. She accepts the ring to wear while she deliberates, recognizing its symbolic power: "I have heard of mute appeals; perhaps it will plead for me." (022-082)

## Aftermath

The proposal scene is immediately followed by the arrival of two unexpected visitors: [[polly-ammon|Polly Ammon]] and [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] herself. [[polly-ammon|Polly]] embraces [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] as her sister, whispering "Sister! Dear, dear sister!" (022-195) But [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] confronts [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] directly, claiming Philip by "the right of a betrothed wife" and insisting, "An agreement of years cannot be broken in one insane moment." (022-327) [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s response — "I seriously doubt that" — marks her decisive refusal to defer to Chicago social convention. (022-332)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] then delivers the novel's central insight about love and cruelty: "There is a great poem, one line of which reads, 'For each man kills the thing he loves.' Let me tell you that a woman can do that also. He did love you — that I concede. But you killed his love." (022-376) This speech, spoken in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] rather than a Chicago drawing room, signals [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s moral authority and secures her claim on Philip against Edith's interference.

## Significance

Philip's proposal in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] is the romantic climax of the novel and a thematic counterpoint to the artificial world of Chicago society. Where Philip's prior engagement to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] was a matter of social arrangement — "an agreement of years" between families of equal standing (022-329) — his proposal to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] takes place in the natural world, under a willow tree, surrounded by [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] that shaped her character. (022-040) The setting affirms the novel's central argument: genuine love flourishes in authenticity, not in the ballrooms and country clubs of Chicago.

The proposal also establishes [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s agency within the [[love-and-courtship|courtship]] narrative. She does not immediately say yes. She demands time to be certain, asserting that her commitment, once given, is absolute and irrevocable. This refusal of impulsive romantic surrender aligns with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s broader character as someone who earns — rather than is given — everything she values: her [[education-as-liberation|education]], her [[moth-collecting|moth collection]], her financial independence, and ultimately, her choice of husband.
