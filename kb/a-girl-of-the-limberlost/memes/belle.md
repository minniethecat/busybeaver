Belle is one of the younger siblings of Billy, taken in by Wesley and Margaret Sinton along with her brother Jimmy. She is part of the group of children the Sintons rescue from a life of poverty and neglect, bringing them into their home at the edge of the Limberlost.

## Arrival at the Sinton Farm

Belle arrives at the Sinton household as a young child, one of several siblings discovered by Wesley Sinton during his visits to the impoverished family living near the swamp. Billy first mentions Belle when he explains that his mother left him and his siblings: "Yes, my mother went off and left me, and left Jimmy and Belle, too" (005-234). Margaret Sinton, whose own children had died, welcomed the children with open arms, providing them with food, clothing, and the stability of a proper home.

## Daily Life

As the only girl among the Sinton children, Belle occupies a distinct place in the household. She is described alongside her brother Jimmy during the early chapters that establish the Sinton family's role in the community. When Billy receives food from Elnora, he saves portions for "Jimmy and Belle" (005-240), showing the sibling bond. Later, Jimmy and Belle come with Billy to visit Elnora and bring her a present (006-167). The Sinton household, with its rescued children, serves as a moral counterpoint to the harsher elements of the novel's world — a place where generosity and care transform lives.

## Relationship with Billy

Belle is Billy's younger sister. Billy's question — "Must Belle be kind and polite to me, else she ain't a lady?" (006-214) — reveals both his growing awareness of social norms and his affection for his sister. While Billy's rebellious nature and eventual redemption arc take center stage in the narrative, Belle and Jimmy represent the quieter successes of the Sinton's intervention — children who are simply given a chance at a better life.
