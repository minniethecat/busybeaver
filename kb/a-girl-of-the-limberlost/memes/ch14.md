# CHAPTER XIV

[014-001]WHEREIN A NEW POSITION IS TENDERED ELNORA, AND PHILIP AMMON IS SHOWN

[014-002]LIMBERLOST VIOLETS

[014-003]The next morning [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] called to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], “The mail carrier

[014-004]stopped at our box.”

[014-005]Elnora ran down the walk and came back carrying an official letter. She

[014-006]tore it open and read:

[014-007]MY DEAR MISS COMSTOCK:

[014-008]At the weekly meeting of the [[onabasha|Onabasha]] School Board last night, it was

[014-009]decided to add the position of Lecturer on Natural History to our corps

[014-010]of city teachers. It will be the duty of this person to spend two hours

[014-011]a week in each of the grade schools exhibiting and explaining specimens

[014-012]of the most prominent objects in nature: animals, birds, insects,

[014-013]flowers, vines, shrubs, bushes, and trees. These specimens and lectures

[014-014]should be appropriate to the seasons and the comprehension of the

[014-015]grades. This position was unanimously voted to you. I think you will

[014-016]find the work delightful and much easier than the routine grind of the

[014-017]other teachers. It is my advice that you accept and begin to prepare

[014-018]yourself at once. Your salary will be $750 a year, and you will be

[014-019]allowed $200 for expenses in procuring specimens and books. Let us know

[014-020]at once if you want the position, as it is going to be difficult to

[014-021]fill satisfactorily if you do not.

[014-022]Very truly yours,

[014-023]DAVID THOMPSON, President, Onabasha Schools.

[014-024]“I hardly understand,” marvelled Mrs. Comstock.

[014-025]“It is a new position. They never have had anything like it before. I

[014-026]suspect it arose from the help I’ve been giving the grade teachers in

[014-027]their [[nature-study|nature work]]. They are trying to teach the children something, and

[014-028]half the instructors don’t know a blue jay from a king-fisher, a beech

[014-029]leaf from an elm, or a wasp from a hornet.”

[014-030]“Well, do you?” anxiously inquired Mrs. Comstock.

[014-031]“Indeed, I do!” laughed Elnora, “and several other things beside. When

[014-032][[freckles|Freckles]] bequeathed me [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], he gave me a bigger inheritance than

[014-033]he knew. While you have thought I was wandering aimlessly, I have been

[014-034]following a definite plan, studying hard, and storing up the stuff that

[014-035]will earn these seven hundred and fifty dollars. Mother dear, I am

[014-036]going to accept this, of course. The work will be a delight. I’d love

[014-037]it most of anything in teaching. You must help me. We must find nests,

[014-038]eggs, leaves, queer formations in plants and rare flowers. I must have

[014-039]flower boxes made for each of the rooms and filled with wild things. I

[014-040]should begin to gather specimens this very day.”

[014-041]Elnora’s face was flushed and her eyes bright.

[014-042]“Oh, what great work that will be!” she cried. “You must go with me so

[014-043]you can see the little faces when I tell them how the goldfinch builds

[014-044]its nest, and how the bees make honey.”

[014-045]So Elnora and her mother went into the woods behind [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] to study

[014-046]nature.

[014-047]“I think,” said Elnora, “the idea is to begin with fall things in the

[014-048]fall, keeping to the seasons throughout the year.”

[014-049]“What are fall things?” inquired Mrs. Comstock.

[014-050]“Oh, fringed gentians, asters, ironwort, every fall flower, leaves from

[014-051]every tree and vine, what makes them change colour, abandoned bird

[014-052]nests, winter quarters of caterpillars and insects, what becomes of the

[014-053]butterflies and grasshoppers—myriads of stuff. I shall have to be very

[014-054]wise to select the things it will be most beneficial for the children

[014-055]to learn.”

[014-056]“Can I really help you?” Mrs. Comstock’s strong face was pathetic.

[014-057]“Indeed, yes!” cried Elnora. “I never can get through it alone. There

[014-058]will be an immense amount of work connected with securing and preparing

[014-059]specimens.”

[014-060]Mrs. Comstock lifted her head proudly and began doing business at once.

[014-061]Her sharp eyes ranged from earth to heaven. She investigated

[014-062]everything, asking innumerable questions. At noon Mrs. Comstock took

[014-063]the specimens they had collected, and went to prepare dinner, while

[014-064]Elnora followed the woods down to the Sintons’ to show her letter.

[014-065]She had to explain what became of her moths, and why college would have

[014-066]to be abandoned for that year, but Margaret and Wesley vowed not to

[014-067]tell. Wesley waved the letter excitedly, explaining it to Margaret as

[014-068]if it were a personal possession. Margaret was deeply impressed, while

[014-069][[billy|Billy]] volunteered first aid in gathering material.

[014-070]“Now anything you want in the ground, Snap can dig it out,” he said.

[014-071]“[[wesley-sinton|Uncle Wesley]] and I found a hole three times as big as Snap, that he

[014-072]dug at the roots of a tree.”

[014-073]“We will train him to hunt pupae cases,” said Elnora.

[014-074]“Are you going to the woods this afternoon?” asked Billy.

[014-075]“Yes,” answered Elnora. “[[dr-ammon|Dr. Ammon]]’s nephew from Chicago is visiting in

[014-076]Onabasha. He is going to show me how men put some sort of compound on a

[014-077]tree, hang a light beside it, and take moths that way. It will be

[014-078]interesting to watch and learn.”

[014-079]“May I come?” asked Billy.

[014-080]“Of course you may come!” answered Elnora.

[014-081]“Is this nephew of Dr. Ammon a young man?” inquired Margaret.

[014-082]“[[About]] twenty-six, I should think,” said Elnora. “He said he had been

[014-083]out of college and at work in his father’s law office three years.”

[014-084]“Does he seem nice?” asked Margaret, and Wesley smiled.

[014-085]“Finest kind of a person,” said Elnora. “He can teach me so much. It is

[014-086]very interesting to hear him talk. He knows considerable about moths

[014-087]that will be a help to me. He had a fever and he has to stay outdoors

[014-088]until he grows strong again.”

[014-089]“Billy, I guess you better help me this afternoon,” said Margaret.

[014-090]“Maybe Elnora had rather not bother with you.”

[014-091]“There’s no reason on earth why Billy should not come!” cried Elnora,

[014-092]and Wesley smiled again.

[014-093]“I must hurry home or I won’t be ready,” she added.

[014-094]Hastening down the road she entered the cabin, her face glowing.

[014-095]“I thought you never would come,” said Mrs. Comstock. “If you don’t

[014-096]hurry Mr. Ammon will be here before you are dressed.”

[014-097]“I forgot about him until just now,” said Elnora. “I am not going to

[014-098]dress. He’s not coming to visit. We are only going to the woods for

[014-099]more specimens. I can’t wear anything that requires care. The limbs

[014-100]take the most dreadful liberties with hair and [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]].”

[014-101]Mrs. Comstock opened her lips, looked at Elnora and closed them. In her

[014-102]heart she was pleased that the girl was so interested in her work that

[014-103]she had forgotten [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]’s coming. But it did seem to her that

[014-104]such a pleasant young man should have been greeted by a girl in a fresh

[014-105]dress. “If she isn’t disposed to primp at the coming of a man, heaven

[014-106]forbid that I should be the one to start her,” thought Mrs. Comstock.

[014-107]Philip came whistling down the walk between the cinnamon pinks,

[014-108]pansies, and strawberries. He carried several packages, while his face

[014-109]flushed with more colour than on the previous day.

[014-110]“Only see what has happened to me!” cried Elnora, offering her letter.

[014-111]“I’ll wager I know!” answered Philip. “Isn’t it great! Every one in

[014-112]Onabasha is talking about it. At last there is something new under the

[014-113]sun. All of them are pleased. They think you’ll make a big success.

[014-114]This will give an incentive to work. In a few days more I’ll be myself

[014-115]again, and we’ll overturn the fields and woods around here.”

[014-116]He went on to congratulate Mrs. Comstock.

[014-117]“Aren’t you proud of her, though?” he asked. “You should hear what

[014-118]folks are saying! They say she created the necessity for the position,

[014-119]and every one seems to feel that it is a necessity. Now, if she

[014-120]succeeds, and she will, all of the other city schools will have such

[014-121]departments, and first thing you know she will have made the whole

[014-122]world a little better. Let me rest a few seconds; my feet are acting up

[014-123]again. Then we will cook the moth compound and put it to cool.”

[014-124]He laughed as he sat breathing shortly.

[014-125]“It doesn’t seem possible that a fellow could lose his strength like

[014-126]this. My knees are actually trembling, but I’ll be all right in a

[014-127]minute. [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]] said I could come. I told him how you took care of

[014-128]me, and he said I would be safe here.”

[014-129]Then he began unwrapping packages and explaining to Mrs. Comstock how

[014-130]to cook the compound to attract the moths. He followed her into the

[014-131]kitchen, kindled the fire, and stirred the preparation as he talked.

[014-132]While the mixture cooled, he and Elnora walked through the vegetable

[014-133]garden behind the cabin and strayed from there into the woods.

[014-134]“What about college?” he asked. “Miss [[mr-brownlee|Brownlee]] said you were going.”

[014-135]“I had hoped to,” replied Elnora, “but I had a streak of dreadful luck,

[014-136]so I’ll have to wait until next year. If you won’t speak of it, I’ll

[014-137]tell you.”

[014-138]Philip promised, so Elnora recited the history of the Yellow Emperor.

[014-139]She was so interested in doing the Emperor justice she did not notice

[014-140]how many personalities went into the story. A few pertinent questions

[014-141]told him the remainder. He looked at the girl in wonder. In face and

[014-142]form she was as lovely as any one of her age and type he ever had seen.

[014-143]Her school work far surpassed that of most girls of her age he knew.

[014-144]She differed in other ways. This vast store of [[education-as-liberation|learning]] she had

[014-145]gathered from field and forest was a wealth of attraction no other girl

[014-146]possessed. Her frank, matter-of-fact manner was an inheritance from her

[014-147]mother, but there was something more. Once, as they talked he thought

[014-148]“sympathy” was the word to describe it and again “comprehension.” She

[014-149]seemed to possess a large sense of brotherhood for all human and

[014-150]animate creatures. She spoke to him as if she had known him all her

[014-151]life. She talked to the grosbeak in exactly the same manner, as she

[014-152]laid strawberries and potato bugs on the fence for his family. She did

[014-153]not swerve an inch from her way when a snake slid past her, while the

[014-154]squirrels came down from the trees and took corn from her fingers. She

[014-155]might as well have been a boy, so lacking was she in any touch of

[014-156]feminine coquetry toward him. He studied her wonderingly. As they went

[014-157]along the path they reached a large slime-covered pool surrounded by

[014-158]decaying stumps and logs thickly covered with water hyacinths and blue

[014-159]flags. Philip stopped.

[014-160]“Is that the place?” he asked.

[014-161]Elnora assented. “The doctor told you?”

[014-162]“Yes. It was tragic. Is that pool really bottomless?”

[014-163]“So far as we ever have been able to discover.”

[014-164]Philip stood looking at the water, while the long, sweet grasses,

[014-165]thickly sprinkled with blue flag bloom, over which wild bees clambered,

[014-166]swayed around his feet. Then he turned to the girl. She had worked

[014-167]hard. The same lavender dress she had worn the previous day clung to

[014-168]her in limp condition. But she was as evenly coloured and of as fine

[014-169]grain as a wild rose petal, her hair was really brown, but never was

[014-170]such hair touched with a redder glory, while her heavy arching brows

[014-171]added a look of strength to her big gray-blue eyes.

[014-172]“And you were born here?”

[014-173]He had not intended to voice that thought.

[014-174]“Yes,” she said, looking into his eyes. “Just in time to prevent my

[014-175]mother from saving the life of my father. She came near never forgiving

[014-176]me.”

[014-177]“Ah, cruel!” cried Philip.

[014-178]“I find much in life that is cruel, from our standpoints,” said Elnora.

[014-179]“It takes the large wisdom of the Unfathomable, the philosophy of the

[014-180]Almighty, to endure some of it. But there is always right somewhere,

[014-181]and at last it seems to come.”

[014-182]“Will it come to you?” asked Philip, who found himself deeply affected.

[014-183]“It has come,” said the girl serenely. “It came a week ago. It came in

[014-184]fullest measure when my mother ceased to regret that I had been born.

[014-185]Now, work that I love has come—that should constitute happiness. A

[014-186]little farther along is my violet bed. I want you to see it.”

[014-187]As Philip Ammon followed he definitely settled upon the name of the

[014-188]unusual feature of Elnora’s face. It should be called “experience.” She

[014-189]had known bitter experiences early in life. Suffering had been her

[014-190]familiar more than joy. He watched her earnestly, his heart deeply

[014-199]“Can your city greenhouses surpass them?” she asked.

[014-201]“They are superb!” he said. “I never saw such length of stem or such

[014-202]rank leaves, while the flowers are the deepest blue, the truest violet

[014-204]girl I am going to marry.”

[014-205]Elnora handed him several others to add to those he held. “She must

[014-206]have wonderful eyes,” she commented.

[014-207]“No other blue eyes are quite so beautiful,” he said. “In fact, she is

[014-208]altogether lovely.”

[014-209]“Is it customary for a man to think the girl he is going to marry

[014-210]lovely? I wonder if I should find her so.”

[014-211]“You would,” said Philip. “No one ever fails to. She is tall as you,

[014-212]very slender, but perfectly rounded; you know about her eyes; her hair

[014-213]is black and wavy—while her complexion is clear and flushed with red.”

[014-214]“Why, she must be the most beautiful girl in the whole world!” she

[014-215]cried.

[014-216]“No, indeed!” he said. “She is not a particle better looking in her way

[014-217]than you are in yours. She is a type of dark beauty, but you are

[014-218]equally as perfect. She is unusual in her combination of black hair and

[014-219]violet eyes, although every one thinks them black at a little distance.

[014-220]You are quite as unusual with your fair face, black brows, and brown

[014-221]hair; indeed, I know many people who would prefer your bright head to

[014-222]her dark one. It’s all a question of taste—and being engaged to the

[014-223]girl,” he added.

[014-224]“That would be likely to prejudice one,” laughed Elnora.

[014-225]“Edith has a birthday soon; if these last will you let me have a box of

[014-226]them to send her?”

[014-227]“I will help gather and pack them for you, so they will carry nicely.

[014-228]Does she hunt moths with you?”

[014-229]Back went Philip Ammon’s head in a gale of laughter.

[014-230]“No!” he cried. “She says they are ‘creepy.’ She would go into a spasm

[014-231]if she were compelled to touch those caterpillars I saw you handling

[014-233]“Why would she?” marvelled Elnora. “Haven’t you told her that they are

[014-234]perfectly clean, helpless, and harmless as so much animate velvet?”

[014-235]“No, I have not told her. She wouldn’t care enough about caterpillars

[014-237]“In what is she interested?”

[014-239]pride in being a little handsomer and better dressed than any girl of

[014-240]her set. She is interested in having a beautiful home, fine

[014-243]“She likes to find new things which amuse her, and to always and in all

[014-246]How does she spend her time?”

[014-248]Her days are never long enough. There is endless shopping, to find the

[014-265]“You are angry,” faltered Elnora.

[014-323]“Billy, you are a jewel!” cried Elnora, throwing her arm across his

[014-324]shoulders as they came down the path.

[014-328]“Of course, you may!”

[014-329]“Is that man going to be your beau?”

[014-330]“Billy! No! What made you think such a thing?”

[014-331]“Aunt Margaret said likely he would fall in love with you, and you

[014-332]wouldn’t want me around any more. Oh, but I was scared! It isn’t so, is

[014-333]it?”

[014-334]“Indeed, no!”

[014-335]“I am your beau, ain’t I?”

[014-336]“Surely you are!” said Elnora, tightening her arm.

[014-337]“I do hope Aunt Kate has ginger cookies,” said Billy with a little skip

[014-338]of delight.
