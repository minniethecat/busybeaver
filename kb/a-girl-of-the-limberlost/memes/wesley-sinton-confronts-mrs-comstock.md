## Summary

[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], the Comstocks' nearest neighbor, confronts [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] over her failure to provide for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s [[education-as-liberation|education]]. (004-037) Having witnessed [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s humiliation on her first day of [[education-as-liberation|high school]] — wearing an outdated calico dress and carrying a meager lunch — Wesley and his wife Margaret take action. (001-296) Wesley delivers a sharp rebuke to [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]], telling her she must either begin supporting [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s [[education-as-liberation|schooling]] or allow the Sintons to do so. (004-075) The confrontation, though painful, catalyzes [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s slow journey toward re-engagement with her daughter's life. (004-121)

## Context

The confrontation had its roots in [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s disastrous first day at [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]]. Arriving in a homemade calico dress among students in fashionable [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] was mocked by her classmates, and she discovered that her mother's promise to pay for books was hollow — she would need to find the money herself. (001-267)

[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], who drove [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] home that day, witnessed her distress firsthand. (001-296) He and Margaret had already been providing discreet support to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — food, [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], and kindness — but they recognized that without her mother's cooperation, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s prospects were dim. (002-003) The morning after [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s difficult first day, Wesley resolved to speak plainly to [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]. (004-013)

## Key Participants

**[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]** — A farmer and the Comstocks' nearest neighbor, Wesley embodies practical kindness and moral courage. He delivers the difficult truth to [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] not out of anger but out of genuine concern for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s welfare. (004-037) His confrontation is direct: "Then get so you can! Any day you say the word you can sell enough of this timber to send her to college." (004-075) Wesley's willingness to risk his friendship with [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s sake marks him as one of the novel's most selfless figures. (004-121)

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — At the time of the confrontation, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] is still mired in grief over her husband's death. Wesley's words penetrate her defenses by linking her neglect of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to her larger failure to move past her loss. (004-075) Her initial reaction is defensive — she sneers at Wesley — but the seeds of change are planted. (004-037)

**[[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]]** — Wesley's wife, who works behind the scenes to prepare a new dress for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] while Wesley handles the difficult conversation with [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]. Her practical support complements Wesley's verbal confrontation. (004-022)

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Though not present during the confrontation, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is its cause and its beneficiary. The Sintons' intervention on her behalf represents the [[community-support|community]] support that sustains her throughout the novel. (004-100)

## Aftermath

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] did not transform overnight, but Wesley's words lodged in her conscience. She began, grudgingly at first, to permit [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s [[education-as-liberation|education]] to proceed without obstruction. (004-276) More significantly, she began the slow process of self-examination that would eventually lead to her entering [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] herself and reclaiming her role as [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s mother. (004-410)

The Sintons continued to support [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — Margaret's dress allowed [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to return to school with dignity, and Wesley's ongoing advocacy ensured that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] never faced her struggles entirely alone. (004-200)

## Significance

[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]'s confrontation represents the novel's argument that genuine love sometimes requires speaking uncomfortable truths. Wesley risks his relationship with [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] — and faces her considerable temper — because [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s welfare matters more than preserving social comfort. (004-121)

The event also establishes the pattern of [[community-support|community]] intervention that runs through the novel. Neither [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] nor [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] can heal alone; both require the active involvement of neighbors who refuse to look away from suffering. Wesley and [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] model the kind of engaged, practical compassion that the novel presents as the antidote to isolation and grief. (004-137)
