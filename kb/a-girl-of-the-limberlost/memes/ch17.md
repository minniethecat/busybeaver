# CHAPTER XVII

[017-001]WHEREIN MRS. COMSTOCK DANCES IN THE MOONLIGHT, AND ELNORA MAKES A

[017-002]CONFESSION

[017-003][[billy|Billy]] was swinging in the hammock, at peace with himself and all the

[017-004]world, when he thought he heard something. He sat bolt upright, his

[017-005]eyes staring. Once he opened his lips, then thought again and closed

[017-006]them. The sound persisted. Billy vaulted the fence, and ran down the

[017-007]road with his queer sidewise hop. When he neared the Comstock cabin, he

[017-008]left the warm dust of the highway and stepped softly at slower pace

[017-009]over the rank grasses of the roadside. He had heard aright. The violin

[017-010]was in the grape arbour, singing a perfect jumble of everything, poured

[017-011]out in an exultant tumult. The strings were voicing the joy of a happy

[017-012]girl heart.

[017-013]Billy climbed the fence enclosing the west woods and crept toward the

[017-014]arbour. He was not a spy and not a sneak. He merely wanted to satisfy

[017-015]his child-heart as to whether [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] was at home, and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at

[017-016]last playing her loved violin with her mother’s consent. One peep

[017-017]sufficed. Mrs. Comstock sat in the moonlight, her head leaning against

[017-018][[grape-arbour|the arbour]]; on her face was a look of perfect peace and contentment. As

[017-019]he stared at her the bow hesitated a second and Mrs. Comstock spoke:

[017-020]“That’s all very melodious and sweet,” she said, “but I do wish you

[017-021]could play Money Musk and some of the tunes I danced as a girl.”

[017-022]Elnora had been carefully avoiding every note that might be reminiscent

[017-023]of her father. At the words she laughed softly and began “Turkey in the

[017-024]Straw.” An instant later Mrs. Comstock was dancing in the moon light.

[017-025]Ammon sprang to her side, caught her in his arms, while to Elnora’s

[017-026]laughter and the violin’s impetus they danced until they dropped

[017-027]panting on the arbour bench.

[017-028]Billy scarcely knew when he reached the road. His light feet barely

[017-029]touched the soft way, so swiftly he flew. He vaulted the fence and

[017-030]burst into the house.

[017-031]“[[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]]! [[wesley-sinton|Uncle Wesley]]!” he screamed. “Listen! Listen! She’s

[017-032]playing it! Elnora’s playing her violin at home! And Aunt Kate is

[017-033]dancing like anything before the arbour! I saw her in the moonlight! I

[017-034]ran down! Oh, Aunt Margaret!”

[017-035]Billy fled sobbing to Margaret’s breast.

[017-036]“Why Billy!” she chided. “Don’t cry, you little dunce! That’s what

[017-037]we’ve all prayed for these many years; but you must be mistaken about

[017-038]Kate. I can’t believe it.”

[017-039]Billy lifted his head. “Well, you just have to!” he said. “When I say I

[017-040]saw anything, Uncle Wesley knows I did. The city man was dancing with

[017-041]her. They danced together and Elnora laughed. But it didn’t look funny

[017-042]to me; I was scared.”

[017-043]“Who was it said ‘wonders never cease,’” asked Wesley. “You mark my

[017-044]word, once you get Kate Comstock started, you can’t stop her. There’s a

[017-045]wagon load of penned-up force in her. Dancing in the moonlight! Well,

[017-046]I’ll be hanged!”

[017-047]Billy was at his side instantly. “Whoever does it will have to hang me,

[017-048]too,” he cried.

[017-049]Sinton threw his arm around Billy and drew him closely. “Tell us all

[017-050]about it, son,” he said. Billy told. “And when Elnora just stopped a

[017-051]breath, ‘Can’t you play some of the old things I knew when I was a

[017-052]girl?’ said her ma. Then Elnora began to do a thing that made you want

[017-053]to whirl round and round, and quicker ’an scat there was her ma

[017-054]a-whirling. The city man, he ups and grabs her and whirls, too, and

[017-055]back in the woods I was going just like they did. Elnora begins to

[017-056]laugh, and I ran to tell you, cos I knew you’d like to know. Now, all

[017-057]the world is right, ain’t it?” ended Billy in supreme satisfaction.

[017-058]“You just bet it is!” said Wesley.

[017-059]Billy looked steadily at Margaret. “Is it, Aunt Margaret?”

[017-060]Margaret Sinton smiled at him bravely.

[017-061]An hour later when Billy was ready to climb the stairs to his room, he

[017-062]went to Margaret to say good night. He leaned against her an instant,

[017-063]then brought his lips to her ear. “Wish I could get your little girls

[017-064]back for you!” he whispered and dashed toward the stairs.

[017-065]Down at the Comstock cabin the violin played on until Elnora was so

[017-066]tired she scarcely could lift the bow. Then Philip went home. The women

[017-067]walked to the gate with him, and stood watching him from sight.

[017-068]“That’s what I call one decent young man!” said Mrs. Comstock. “To see

[017-069]him fit in with us, you’d think he’d been brought up in a cabin; but

[017-070]it’s likely he’s always had the very cream o’ the pot.”

[017-071]“Yes, I think so,” laughed Elnora, “but it hasn’t hurt him. I’ve never

[017-072]seen anything I could criticise. He’s teaching me so much,

[017-073]unconsciously. You know he graduated from Harvard, and has several

[017-074]degrees in law. He’s coming in the morning, and we are going to put in

[017-075]a big day on Catocalæ.”

[017-076]“Which is——?”

[017-077]“Those gray moths with wings that fold back like big flies, and they

[017-078]appear as if they had been carved from old wood. Then, when they fly,

[017-079]the lower wings flash out and they are red and black, or gold and

[017-080]black, or pink and black, or dozens of bright, beautiful colours

[017-081]combined with black. No one ever has classified all of them and written

[017-082]their complete history, unless the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] is doing it now. She

[017-083]wants everything she can get about them.”

[017-084]“I remember,” said Mrs. Comstock. “They are mighty pretty things. I’ve

[017-085]started up slews of them from the vines covering the logs, all my life.

[017-086]I must be cautious and catch them after this, but they seem powerful

[017-087]spry. I might get hold of something rare.” She thought intently and

[017-088]added, “And wouldn’t know it if I did. It would just be my luck. I’ve

[017-089]had the rarest thing on earth in reach this many a day and only had the

[017-090]wit to cinch it just as it was going. I’ll bet I don’t let anything

[017-091]else escape me.”

[017-092]Next morning Philip came early, and he and Elnora went at once to the

[017-093]fields and woods. Mrs. Comstock had come to believe so implicitly in

[017-094]him that she now stayed at home to complete the work before she joined

[017-095]them, and when she did she often sat sewing, leaving them wandering

[017-096]hours at a time. It was noon before she finished, and then she packed a

[017-097]basket of lunch. She found Elnora and Philip near the violet patch,

[017-098]which was still in its prime. They all lunched together in the shade of

[017-099]a wild crab thicket, with flowers spread at their feet, and the gold

[017-100]orioles streaking the air with flashes of light and trailing ecstasy

[017-101]behind them, while the red-wings, as always, asked the most impertinent

[017-102]questions. Then Mrs. Comstock carried the basket back to [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], and

[017-103][[love-and-courtship|Philip and Elnora]] sat on a log, resting a few minutes. They had

[017-104]unexpected luck, and both were eager to continue the search.

[017-105]“Do you remember your promise about these violets?” asked he.

[017-106]“To-morrow is Edith’s birthday, and if I’d put them special delivery on

[017-107]the morning train, she’d get them in the late afternoon. They ought to

[017-108]keep that long. She leaves for the North next day.”

[017-109]“Of course, you may have them,” said Elnora. “We will quit long enough

[017-110]before supper to gather a large bunch. They can be packed so they will

[017-111]carry all right. They should be perfectly fresh, especially if we

[017-112]gather them this evening and let them drink all night.”

[017-113]Then they went back to hunt Catocalæ. It was a long and a happy search.

[017-114]It led them into new, unexplored nooks of the woods, past a red-poll

[017-115]nest, and where goldfinches prospected for thistledown for the cradles

[017-116]they would line a little later. It led them into real forest, where

[017-117]deep, dark pools lay, where the hermit thrush and the wood robin

[017-118]extracted the essence from all other bird melody, and poured it out in

[017-119]their pure bell-tone notes. It seemed as if every old gray tree-trunk,

[017-120]slab of loose bark, and prostrate log yielded the flashing gray

[017-121]treasures; while of all others they seemed to take alarm most easily,

[017-122]and be most difficult to capture.

[017-123]Philip came to Elnora at dusk, daintily holding one by the body, its

[017-124]dark wings showing and its long slender legs trying to clasp his

[017-125]fingers and creep from his hold.

[017-126]“Oh for mercy’s sake!” cried Elnora, staring at him.

[017-127]“I half believe it!” exulted Ammon.

[017-128]“Did you ever see one?”

[017-129]“Only in collections, and very seldom there.”

[017-130]Elnora studied the black wings intently. “I surely believe that’s

[017-131]Sappho,” she marvelled. “The Bird Woman will be overjoyed.”

[017-132]“We must get the cyanide jar quickly,” said Philip.

[017-133]“I wouldn’t lose her for anything. Such a chase as she led me!”

[017-134]Elnora brought the jar and began gathering up paraphernalia.

[017-135]“When you make a find like that,” she said, “it’s the right time to

[017-136]quit and feel glorious all the rest of that day. I tell you I’m proud!

[017-137]We will go now. We have barely time to carry out our plans before

[017-138]supper. Won’t mother be pleased to see that we have a rare one?”

[017-139]“I’d like to see any one more pleased than I am!” said [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. “I

[017-140]feel as if I’d earned my supper to-night. Let’s go.”

[017-141]He took the greater part of the load and stepped aside for Elnora to

[017-142]precede him. She followed the path, broken by the grazing cattle,

[017-143]toward the cabin and nearest the violet patch she stopped, laid down

[017-144]her net, and the things she carried. Philip passed her and hurried

[017-145]straight toward the back gate.

[017-146]“Aren’t you going to——?” began Elnora.

[017-147]“I’m going to get this moth home in a hurry,” he said. “This cyanide

[017-148]has lost its strength, and it’s not working well. We need some fresh in

[017-149]the jar.”

[017-150]He had forgotten the violets! Elnora stood looking after him, a curious

[017-151]expression on her face. One second so—then she picked up the net and

[017-152]followed. At the blue-bordered pool she paused and half turned back,

[017-153]then she closed her lips firmly and went on. It was nine o’clock when

[017-154]Philip said good-bye, and started to town. His gay whistle floated to

[017-155]them from the farthest corner of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. Elnora complained of

[017-156]being tired, so she went to her room and to bed. But sleep would not

[017-157]come. Thought was racing in her brain and the longer she lay the wider

[017-158]awake she grew. At last she softly slipped from bed, lighted her lamp

[017-159]and began opening boxes. Then she went to work. Two hours later a

[017-160]beautiful birch bark basket, strongly and artistically made, stood on

[017-161]her table. She set a tiny alarm clock at three, returned to bed and

[017-162]fell asleep instantly with a smile on her lips.

[017-163]She was on the floor with the first tinkle of the alarm, and hastily

[017-164]dressing, she picked up the basket and a box to fit it, crept down the

[017-165]stairs, and out to the violet patch. She was unafraid as it was growing

[017-166]light, and lining the basket with damp mosses she swiftly began

[017-167]picking, with practised hands, the best of the flowers. She scarcely

[017-168]could tell which were freshest at times, but day soon came creeping

[017-169]over [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] and peeped at her. The robins awoke all their

[017-170]neighbours, and a babel of bird notes filled the air. The dew was

[017-171]dripping, while the first strong rays of light fell on a world in which

[017-172]Elnora worshipped. When the basket was filled to overflowing, she set

[017-173]it in the stout pasteboard box, packed it solid with mosses, tied it

[017-174]firmly and slipped under the cord a note she had written the previous

[017-175]night.

[017-176]Then she took a short cut across the woods and walked swiftly to

[017-177][[onabasha|Onabasha]]. It was after six o’clock, but all of the city she wished to

[017-178]avoid were asleep. She had no trouble in finding a small boy out, and

[017-179]she stood at a distance waiting while he rang [[dr-ammon|Dr. Ammon]]’s bell and

[017-180]delivered the package for Philip to a maid, with the note which was to

[017-181]be given him at once.

[017-182]On the way home through the woods passing some baited trees she

[017-183]collected the captive moths. She entered the kitchen with them so

[017-184]naturally that Mrs. Comstock made no comment. After breakfast Elnora

[017-185]went to her room, cleared away all trace of the night’s work and was

[017-186]out in the arbour mounting moths when Philip came down the road. “I am

[017-187]tired sitting,” she said to her mother. “I think I will walk a few rods

[017-188]and meet him.”

[017-189]“Who’s a trump?” he called from afar.

[017-190]“Not you!” retorted Elnora. “Confess that you forgot!”

[017-191]“Completely!” said Philip. “But luckily it would not have been fatal. I

[017-192]wrote [[polly-ammon|Polly]] last week to send Edith something appropriate to-day, with

[017-193]my card. But that touch from the woods will be very effective. Thank

[017-194]you more than I can say. [[aunt-anna|Aunt Anna]] and I unpacked it to see the basket,

[017-195]and it was a beauty. She says you are always doing such things.”

[017-196]“Well, I hope not!” laughed Elnora. “If you’d seen me sneaking out

[017-197]before dawn, not to awaken mother and coming in with moths to make her

[017-198]think I’d been to the trees, you’d know it was a most especial

[017-199]occasion.”

[017-200]Then Philip understood two things: Elnora’s mother did not know of the

[017-201]early morning trip to the city, and the girl had come to meet him to

[017-202]tell him so.

[017-203]“You were a brick to do it!” he whispered as he closed the gate behind

[017-204]them. “I’ll never forget you for it. Thank you ever so much.”

[017-205]“I did not do that for you,” said Elnora tersely. “I did it mostly to

[017-206]preserve my own [[pride-and-dignity|self-respect]]. I saw you were forgetting. If I did it

[017-207]for anything besides that, I did it for her.”

[017-208]“Just look what I’ve brought!” said Philip, entering the arbour and

[017-209]greeting Mrs. Comstock. “Borrowed it of the Bird Woman. And it isn’t

[017-210]hers. A rare edition of Catocalæ with coloured plates. I told her the

[017-211]best I could, and she said to try for Sappho here. I suspect the Bird

[017-212]Woman will be out presently. She was all excitement.”

[017-213]Then they bent over the book together and with the mounted moth before

[017-214]them determined her family. The Bird Woman did come later, and carried

[017-215]the moth away, to put into a book and Elnora and Philip were freshly

[017-216]filled with enthusiasm.

[017-217]So these days were the beginning of the weeks that followed. Six of

[017-218]them flying on Time’s wings, each filled to the brim with interest.

[017-219]After June, the moth hunts grew less frequent; the fields and woods

[017-220]were searched for material for Elnora’s grade work. The most absorbing

[017-221]occupation they found was in carrying out Mrs. Comstock’s suggestion to

[017-222]learn the vital thing for which each month was distinctive, and make

[017-223]that the key to the [[nature-study|nature work]]. They wrote out a list of the months,

[017-224]opposite each the things all of them could suggest which seemed to

[017-225]pertain to that month alone, and then tried to sift until they found

[017-226]something typical. Mrs. Comstock was a great help. Her mother had been

[017-227]Dutch and had brought from Holland numerous quaint sayings and

[017-228]superstitions easily traceable to Pliny’s Natural History; and in Mrs.

[017-229]Comstock’s early years in Ohio she had heard much Indian talk among her

[017-230]elders, so she knew the signs of each season, and sometimes they

[017-231]helped. Always her practical thought and sterling common sense were

[017-232]useful. When they were afield until exhausted they came back to the

[017-233]cabin for food, to prepare specimens and classify them, and to talk

[017-234]over the day. Sometimes Philip brought books and read while Elnora and

[017-235]her mother worked, and every night Mrs. Comstock asked for the violin.

[017-236]Her perfect hunger for music was sufficient evidence of how she had

[017-237]suffered without it. So the days crept by, golden, filled with useful

[017-238]work and pure pleasure.

[017-239]The grosbeak had led the family in the maple abroad and a second brood,

[017-240]in a wild grape vine clambering over the well, was almost ready for

[017-241]flight. The dust lay thick on the country roads, the days grew warmer;

[017-242]summer was just poising to slip into fall, and Philip remained, coming

[017-243]each day as if he had belonged there always.

[017-244]One warm August afternoon Mrs. Comstock looked up from the ruffle on

[017-245]which she was engaged to see a blue-coated messenger enter the gate.

[017-246]“Is Philip Ammon here?” asked the boy.

[017-247]“He is,” said Mrs. Comstock.

[017-248]“I have a message for him.”

[017-249]“He is in the woods back of the cabin. I will ring the bell. Do you

[017-250]know if it is important?”

[017-251]“Urgent,” said the boy; “I rode hard.”

[017-252]Mrs. Comstock stepped to the back door and clanged the dinner bell

[017-253]sharply, paused a second, and rang again. In a short time Philip and

[017-254]Elnora ran down the path.

[017-255]“Are you ill, mother?” cried Elnora.

[017-256]Mrs. Comstock indicated the boy. “There is an important message for

[017-257]Philip,” she said.

[017-258]He muttered an excuse and tore open the telegram. His colour faded

[017-259]slightly. “I have to take the first train,” he said. “My father is ill

[017-260]and I am needed.”

[017-261]He handed the sheet to Elnora. “I have about two hours, as I remember

[017-262]the trains north, but my things are all over [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]’s house, so I

[017-263]must go at once.”

[017-264]“Certainly,” said Elnora, giving back the message. “Is there anything I

[017-265]can do to help? Mother, bring Philip a glass of buttermilk to start on.

[017-266]I will gather what you have here.”

[017-267]“Never mind. There is nothing of importance. I don’t want to be

[017-268]hampered. I’ll send for it if I miss anything I need.”

[017-269]Philip drank the milk, said good-bye to Mrs. Comstock; thanked her for

[017-270]all her kindness, and turned to Elnora.

[017-271]“Will you walk to the edge of the Limberlost with me?” he asked. Elnora

[017-272]assented. Mrs. Comstock followed to the gate, urged him to come again

[017-273]soon, and repeated her good-bye. Then she went back to the arbour to

[017-274]await Elnora’s return. As she watched down the road she smiled softly.

[017-275]“I had an idea he would speak to me first,” she thought, “but this may

[017-276]change things some. He hasn’t time. Elnora will come back a happy girl,

[017-277]and she has good reason. He is a model young man. Her lot will be very

[017-278]different from mine.”

[017-279]She picked up her embroidery and began setting dainty precise little

[017-280]stitches, possible only to certain women.

[017-281]On the road Elnora spoke first. “I do hope it is nothing serious,” she

[017-282]said. “Is he usually strong?”

[017-283]“Quite strong,” said Philip. “I am not at all alarmed but I am very

[017-284]much ashamed. I have been well enough for the past month to have gone

[017-285]home and helped him with some critical cases that were keeping him at

[017-286]work in this heat. I was enjoying myself so I wouldn’t offer to go, and

[017-287]he would not ask me to come, so long as he could help it. I have

[017-288]allowed him to overtax himself until he is down, and mother and Polly

[017-289]are north at our cottage. He’s never been sick before, and it’s

[017-291]“He intended you to stay this long when you came,” urged Elnora.

[017-292]“Yes, but it’s hot in Chicago. I should have remembered him. He is

[017-293]always thinking of me. Possibly he has needed me for days. I am ashamed

[017-294]to go to him in splendid condition and admit that I was having such a

[017-295]fine time I forgot to come home.”

[017-296]“You have had a fine time, then?” asked Elnora.

[017-297]They had reached the fence. Philip vaulted over to take a short cut

[017-298]across the fields. He turned and looked at her.

[017-299]“The best, the sweetest, and most wholesome time any man ever had in

[017-300]this world,” he said. “Elnora, if I talked hours I couldn’t make you

[017-301]understand what a girl I think you are. I never in all my life hated

[017-302]anything as I hate leaving you. It seems to me that I have not strength

[017-303]to do it.”

[017-304]“If you have learned anything worth while from me,” said Elnora, “that

[017-305]should be it. Just to have strength to go to your duty, and to go

[017-306]quickly.”

[017-307]He caught the hand she held out to him in both his. “Elnora, these days

[017-308]we have had together, have they been sweet to you?”

[017-309]“Beautiful days!” said Elnora. “Each like a perfect dream to be thought

[017-310]over and over all my life. Oh, they have been the only really happy

[017-311]days I’ve ever known; these days rich with mother’s love, and doing

[017-312]useful work with your help. Good-bye! You must hurry!”

[017-313]Philip gazed at her. He tried to drop her hand, only clutched it

[017-314]closer. Suddenly he drew her toward him. “Elnora,” he whispered, “will

[017-315]you kiss me good-bye?”

[017-316]Elnora drew back and stared at him with wide eyes. “I’d strike you

[017-317]sooner!” she said. “Have I ever said or done anything in your presence

[017-318]that made you feel free to ask that, Philip Ammon?”

[017-319]“No!” panted Philip. “No! I think so much of you I wanted to touch your

[017-320]lips once before I left you. You know, Elnora——”

[017-321]“Don’t distress yourself,” said Elnora calmly. “I am broad enough to

[017-322]judge you sanely. I know what you mean. It would be no harm to you. It

[017-323]would not matter to me, but here we will think of some one else. Edith

[017-324]Carr would not want your lips to-morrow if she knew they had touched

[017-325]mine to-day. I was wise to say: ‘Go quickly!’”

[017-326]Philip still clung to her. “Will you write me?” he begged.

[017-327]“No,” said Elnora. “There is nothing to say, save good-bye. We can do

[017-328]that now.”

[017-329]He held on. “Promise that you will write me only one letter,” he urged.

[017-330]“I want just one message from you to lock in my desk, and keep always.

[017-331]Promise you will write once, Elnora.”

[017-332]She looked into his eyes, and smiled serenely. “If the talking trees

[017-333]tell me this winter, the secret of how a man may grow perfect, I will

[017-334]write you what it is, Philip. In all the time I have known you, I never

[017-335]have liked you so little. Good-bye.”

[017-336]She drew away her hand and swiftly turned back to the road. Philip

[017-337]Ammon, wordless, started toward Onabasha on a run.

[017-338]Elnora crossed the road, climbed the fence and sought the shelter of

[017-339]their own woods. She chose a diagonal course and followed it until she

[017-340]came to the path leading past the violet patch. She went down this

[017-341]hurriedly. Her hands were clenched at her side, her eyes dry and

[017-342]bright, her cheeks red-flushed, and her breath coming fast. When she

[017-343]reached the patch she turned into it and stood looking around her.

[017-344]The mosses were dry, the flowers gone, weeds a foot high covered it.

[017-345]She turned away and went on down the path until she was almost in sight

[017-346]of the cabin.

[017-347]Mrs. Comstock smiled and waited in the arbour until it occurred to her

[017-348]that Elnora was a long time coming, so she went to the gate. The road

[017-349]stretched away toward the Limberlost empty and lonely. Then she knew

[017-350]that Elnora had gone into their own woods and would come in the back

[017-351]way. She could not understand why the girl did not hurry to her with

[017-352]what she would have to tell. She went out and wandered around the

[017-353]garden. Then she stepped into the path and started along the way

[017-354]leading to the woods, past [[limberlost-pool|the pool]] now framed in a thick setting of
