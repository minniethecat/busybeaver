# Philip Ammon Returns After the Ball

## Summary

Philip Ammon Returns After the Ball is the romantic climax of *A Girl of the Limberlost*, occurring in Chapter XXI. The day after Edith Carr publicly humiliates Philip at their engagement ball in Chicago — dropping his ring on the floor and insulting him before his entire family — Philip arrives at the Comstock cabin, exhausted and heartsick, to ask Elnora to marry him. Mrs. Comstock interrogates him fiercely before granting permission to stay. Elnora, however, refuses to hear his explanation immediately, imposing a one-week waiting period to give Edith time to reclaim him if the engagement is not truly broken. After the week passes and news arrives that Edith has departed for Europe, Philip makes his case: his love for Elnora is "a Limberlost product" — "as much bigger, cleaner, and more wholesome than any feeling I ever had for Edith Carr, as you are bigger than she" (021-411--414). Elnora, still cautious, murmurs her tacit acceptance: "Oh, what's the use to be extravagant?" (021-429).

## Context

Philip Ammon, a young Chicago lawyer, spent the previous summer in the Limberlost recovering from typhoid fever at the Comstock cabin. He fell in love with Elnora but was bound by a years-long engagement to the beautiful socialite Edith Carr. At the engagement ball (Chapter XIX), Edith erupted in "uncontrolled temper" when Philip caught a rare Yellow Emperor moth for Elnora. She dropped his ring, insulted him repeatedly, and humiliated him before his family and friends. Philip's father, Dr. Ammon, who "liked what he had told him of Elnora," advised his son to go directly to the Limberlost (021-074--079).

## Narrative

Philip arrives at the Comstock cabin sleepless and distraught. Seeing Elnora, he blurts out: "If you were engaged to me, and we were at a ball, among hundreds, where I offended you very much... would you drop my ring on the floor and insult me repeatedly?" (021-025--030). Elnora thinks the fever has returned. Mrs. Comstock intervenes, pulling Philip aside to split kindling while she interrogates him.

"Do you call yourself a man or a hound?" she demands (021-044). She accuses him of using Elnora to spite his quarreling fiancee. "I came to ask Elnora to marry me," Philip replies (021-055). As a lawyer, he appeals for a fair hearing: "It is beneath your ordinary sense of justice to decide a case without hearing the evidence" (021-065--067).

He explains the ball disaster, and Mrs. Comstock learns the Ammon family never approved of Edith Carr. She grants Philip permission to stay, with a warning: "If you bring tears and heartache to her, don't have the assurance to think I'll bear it tamely. I'll get right up and fight like a catamount!" (021-105--109).

Elnora takes a different approach. She refuses to hear Philip's explanation, imposing a one-week waiting period. She tells her mother privately: "There are three of us in this, and one must be left with a sore heart for life. If the decision rests with me I propose to be very sure that it is the one who deserves such hard luck" (021-142--144).

During the week, they resume their old summer routine — hunting moths, working together, with Philip growing ever more certain of his choice. News arrives: Edith Carr has sailed for Europe (021-153--159).

When Elnora finally consents to listen, she puts herself in Edith's place: "If I were in her place, I simply could not give you up" (021-329--330). Philip forcefully rejects any possibility of returning to Edith: "She killed all feeling in my heart for her so completely that I do not even dread meeting her" (021-340--341). He reframes the question as between two people only: "May I remain? May I love you? Will you give me the chance to prove what I think of you?" (021-350--351).

He contrasts the two women: Elnora standing before her classes explaining "the marvels of the Almighty" versus Edith on a ballroom floor giving way to "uncontrolled temper" (021-414--418). His love, he says, is "a Limberlost product" — bigger, cleaner, more wholesome (021-412--413).

Elnora's final murmur — "Oh, what's the use to be extravagant?" — is her tacit assent. She will let him stay.

## Significance

This event is the emotional climax of the novel's romance plot. Philip's return represents a conscious choice: he rejects the Chicago social world and its values (beauty, fashion, status) for the Limberlost and its values (authenticity, work, love of nature). Elnora's response demonstrates her emotional maturity — she does not rush, she empathizes even with her rival, and she protects herself with deliberate caution. Mrs. Comstock's "catamount" defense of her daughter confirms her complete transformation from the bitter, neglectful mother of the opening chapters. The event also resolves the novel's central thematic opposition: the artificial world of Chicago society versus the genuine life of the Limberlost.
