# Philip Ammon Is Shown the Limberlost Violets

## Summary

In Chapter XIV — subtitled "Wherein a New Position Is Tendered Elnora, and Philip Ammon Is Shown Limberlost Violets" — Elnora Comstock leads Philip Ammon to a hidden violet bed deep in the Limberlost swamp. (014-001) The entire forest floor is carpeted in purple: "a thick blanket of violets nodding from stems a foot in length." (014-195) Elnora kneels, gathers a handful, and gives them to Philip, asking, "Can your city greenhouses surpass them?" (014-197) (014-199) Philip is awed by their beauty, yet his response — comparing the violets to the eyes of "the girl I am going to marry" — reveals how blind he remains to the girl standing before him. (014-203)

## Context

Philip Ammon, recuperating from typhoid fever, has been staying with the Comstocks to regain his strength in the country air. Elnora serves as his guide to the Limberlost, showing him the swamp's wonders — moths, birds, and now violets. The morning after a letter from the Bird Woman arrives, Elnora takes Philip into the woods. She has just learned that the Onabasha school board wishes to appoint her as a natural history lecturer, but her thoughts are on the swamp and the man walking beside her. (014-003)

"A little farther along is my violet bed. I want you to see it." Elnora leads Philip deeper into the swamp. (014-186)

## Key Participants

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — The girl of the Limberlost, who knows every hidden treasure of the swamp. She shares her violet bed with Philip as an act of intimate trust — the violets are precious to her, a secret she has kept until now. Her face, Philip notices, bears the stamp of "experience." (014-187)

**[[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]** — A young Chicago man, recovering from illness and increasingly captivated by Elnora and her world. Yet he remains oblivious to the emotional significance of the moment. When he compares the violets to his fiancée Edith Carr's eyes, he unknowingly wounds Elnora. (014-203)

## Key Events

The violet bed is a breathtaking sight. Blue flags wave among the trees, clusters of cowslips nod gold heads, but the dominant color is purple — "the whole earth was purple with a thick blanket of violets." (014-194) Elnora kneels in the leaves and grasses, slipping her fingers to the roots to gather a bouquet. (014-196)

Philip is genuinely impressed. "They are superb! I never saw such length of stem or such rank leaves, while the flowers are the deepest blue, the truest violet I ever saw growing wild." (014-201) But then he adds: "They are coloured exactly like the eyes of the girl I am going to marry." (014-203) The comparison — meant as a compliment to the violets — lands as a quiet devastation for Elnora, whose own feelings for Philip are deepening.

## Aftermath

The violet bed scene encapsulates the central romantic tension of the novel: Elnora offers Philip the rarest beauty of her world, but he sees only a reflection of the woman waiting for him in Chicago. The image of the Limberlost violets — deep blue, long-stemmed, wild, and hidden — becomes a metaphor for Elnora herself. The moment foreshadows the long arc of Philip's awakening, in which he must learn to see what has been before him all along.
