## Synopsis

While eating her lunch alone beside the school building, Elnora Comstock meets Billy, a ragged boy who tells her of his younger siblings Jimmy and Belle crying for breakfast at home (005-213). Moved by their hunger, Elnora gives Billy her food to share with them, beginning a pattern of charitable giving that continues through the early chapters. Billy later brings Jimmy and Belle to meet Elnora, presenting her with a gift they have brought together (006-167).

## Key Participants

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Though she hides her own lunch in shame at school, Elnora does not hesitate to share her food with children who have even less. Her compassion reveals a character that transcends the social anxieties of the schoolyard (005-213).

**[[billy|Billy]]** — A ragged, swearing boy who became the caretaker for his younger siblings when their father abandoned the family. His desperate honesty — "Jimmy and Belle are both crying for breakfast" — pierces Elnora's own wall of shame (005-213) (005-234).

**Jimmy and Belle** — Billy's younger siblings, described as thin and hungry-looking. Belle is "most ten" years old (006-195). Their desperate hunger, and Billy's fierce devotion to them, become the catalyst for Elnora's charitable awakening.

## Key Moments

- Billy tells Elnora that Jimmy and Belle are "both crying for breakfast" at home, revealing the depth of the family's poverty with stark, unadorned honesty (005-213).
- When Billy's father "went off and left" the family, Billy became the de facto caretaker for his younger siblings, taking on adult responsibilities while still a child himself (005-234).
- Elnora gives Billy bread and chicken to take to Jimmy and Belle, receiving in return his solemn gratitude — a transaction of food for dignity (005-240).
- Billy and the children later appear with a small gift for Elnora, a gesture of reciprocation that moves her deeply and transforms the relationship from charity to friendship (006-167).
- Belle, "most ten" years old, is described as thin and hungry-looking, reinforcing the children's desperate circumstances and the urgency of Elnora's response (006-195).

## Significance

Elnora's encounter with Billy's starving siblings marks the beginning of her role as a provider for those even less fortunate than herself. Though she hides her own lunch box in shame at school, she freely gives her food to hungry children who have nothing at all. This contradiction — shame among equals, generosity toward those below — defines the complexity of her character and her emerging sense of moral purpose beyond personal advancement. The encounter also introduces Billy, whose arc from starving orphan to literate young man becomes one of the novel's most redemptive storylines.
