## Summary

During a visit to the Sinton farm, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] presses [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] for the truth about her father's death. Margaret, who was "next to the last person who saw him before he was drowned," describes the late June afternoon when [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] skirted the quagmire too close and sank into the bottomless pool. (010-046) He was bareheaded because he had found a quail's nest and gathered the eggs in his hat, leaving it in a fence corner — where it was found afterward. (010-048) The [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] opens a window into the family tragedy that had shaped [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s entire life and her mother's decades-long grief.

## Context

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] had grown up knowing only that her father died in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] swamp when she was a baby. Her mother [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] never spoke of Robert's death, and the silence around the event had calcified into a household rule — the drowned father was a ghost whose name could not be spoken. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s brief cryptic statement to the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] — "there's plenty of land, but father was drowned when I was a baby" (003-212) — was the sum of what she had been able to articulate about her origins.

[[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]], as the Comstocks' closest neighbor and [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]'s only real friend, had been present in the aftermath of Robert's death. She had helped the widowed [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] through the birth of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and the difficult years that followed. But the full story of what happened at the quagmire had never been told directly to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — until now.

## Key Participants

**[[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]]** — Margaret holds the memory of [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]]'s last hours with the clarity of a witness who has replayed the scene many times. Her description is precise and unflinching: Robert was dressed in trousers and a blue shirt, his head bare, his face beautiful. (010-043) "I could almost touch him when he sank," she tells [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], the intimacy of the detail underscoring how close she was — and how powerless to prevent what happened. (010-044) Margaret's account also reveals her suspicion that Robert was on the dangerous north side of the quagmire because he was trying to avoid being seen from [[comstock-garden|the garden]] — suggesting he was coming from somewhere he should not have been. (010-058)

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] receives the [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] not as a child [[education-as-liberation|learning]] of a tragedy but as a young woman piecing together the puzzle of her family. Her questions are analytical — she asks whether Robert was in a hurry, whether he was trying to hide his path from her mother (010-056) — suggesting she is already beginning to understand the darker currents beneath the surface of her parents' marriage. The [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] connects her father's death to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] itself, transforming [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] from a place of wonder and economic opportunity into a site of personal tragedy that she carries in her blood.

**[[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]** — Wesley, Margaret's husband and the man who had been [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s most consistent advocate since childhood, provides the steady, silent presence that allows the [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] to unfold. His role in this scene — protective but not intrusive — reflects his lifelong commitment to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s wellbeing.

## Aftermath

Margaret's [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] planted seeds that would bear fruit in later chapters. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s understanding of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] now encompassed both its beauty and its danger — the same swamp that yielded her beloved moths had taken her father. This dual nature of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] — nurturing and lethal, source of life and site of death — became a recurring motif in [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s relationship with her environment.

The [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] also prepared the ground for the more explosive truth that would later emerge: that [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] had been visiting [[elvira-carney|Elvira Carney]] on the day he drowned, and that the marriage [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] had mourned for nearly two decades had been shadowed by infidelity. Margaret's account of Robert skirting the quagmire on the north side — the side that could not be seen from the Comstock garden (010-058) — contained within it the seed of that darker story, though [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] would not fully understand it until later.

## Significance

[[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]]'s [[secrets-and-truth|revelation]] of [[grief-and-loss|Robert Comstock's death]] functions as the novel's moment of ancestral reckoning. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], who has spent her young life trying to escape the gravitational pull of her family's tragedy — the poverty, the grief-maddened mother, the dead father whose name cannot be spoken — finally confronts the story directly. In doing so, she begins the process of integrating her father's death into her understanding of herself rather than living in its shadow.

The event also establishes the Sinton farm as a space of truth-telling in a novel full of secrets. Where the Comstock cabin is a place of silence and repression, the Sintons' home is where hard truths are spoken. Margaret's willingness to tell [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] what her own mother would not — what [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] could not — underscores the Sinton family's role as the novel's moral compass and as the surrogate parents who filled the emotional void [[grief-and-loss|Katharine's grief]] had created.
