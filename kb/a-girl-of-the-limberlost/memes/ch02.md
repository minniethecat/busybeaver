# CHAPTER II

[002-001]WHEREIN WESLEY AND MARGARET GO SHOPPING, AND ELNORA’S WARDROBE IS

[002-002]REPLENISHED

[002-003][[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] walked down the road half a mile and turned at the lane

[002-004]leading to his home. His heart was hot and filled with indignation. He

[002-005]had told [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] he did not blame her mother, but he did. His wife met

[002-006]him at the door.

[002-007]“Did you see anything of Elnora?” she questioned.

[002-008]“Most too much, Maggie,” he answered. “What do you say to going to

[002-009]town? There’s a few things has to be got right away.”

[002-010]“Where did you see her, Wesley?”

[002-011]“Along the [[limberlost-trail|old Limberlost trail]], my girl, torn to pieces sobbing. Her

[002-012]courage always has been fine, but the thing she met to-day was too much

[002-013]for her. We ought to have known better than to let her go that way. It

[002-014]wasn’t only clothes; there were books, and entrance fees for

[002-015]out-of-town people, that she didn’t know about; while there must have

[002-016]been jeers, whispers, and laughing. Maggie, I feel as if I’d been a

[002-017]traitor to those girls of ours. I ought to have gone in and seen about

[002-018]this school business. Don’t cry, Maggie. Get me some supper, and I’ll

[002-019]hitch up and see what we can do now.”

[002-020]“What can we do, Wesley?

[002-021]“I don’t just know. But we’ve got to do something. Kate Comstock will

[002-022]be a handful, while Elnora will be two, but between us we must see that

[002-023]the girl is not too hard pressed about money, and that she is dressed

[002-024]so she is not ridiculous. She’s saved us the wages of a woman many a

[002-025]day, can’t you make her some decent dresses?”

[002-026]“Well, I’m not just what you call expert, but I could beat Kate

[002-027]Comstock all to pieces. I know that skirts should be pleated to the

[002-028]band instead of gathered, and full enough to sit in, and short enough

[002-029]to walk in. I could try. There are patterns for sale. Let’s go right

[002-030]away, Wesley.”

[002-031]“Set me a bit of supper, while I hitch up.”

[002-032]Margaret built a fire, made coffee, and fried ham and eggs. She set out

[002-033]pie and cake and had enough for a hungry man by the time the carriage

[002-034]was at the door, but she had no appetite. She dressed while Wesley ate,

[002-035]put away the food while he dressed, and then they drove toward the city

[002-036]through the beautiful September evening, and as they went they planned

[002-037]for Elnora. The trouble was, not whether they were generous enough to

[002-038]buy what she needed, but whether she would accept their purchases, and

[002-039]what her mother would say.

[002-040]They went to a drygoods store and when a clerk asked what they wanted

[002-041]to see neither of them knew, so they stepped aside and held a whispered

[002-042]consultation.

[002-043]“What had we better get, Wesley?”

[002-044]“Dresses,” said Wesley promptly,

[002-045]“But how many dresses, and what kind?”

[002-046]“Blest if I know!” exclaimed Wesley. “I thought you would manage that.

[002-047]I know about some things I’m going to get.”

[002-048]At that instant several [[education-as-liberation|high school]] girls came into the store and

[002-049]approached them.

[002-050]“There!” exclaimed Wesley breathlessly. “There, Maggie! Like them!

[002-051]That’s what she needs! Buy like they have!”

[002-052]Margaret stared. What did they wear? They were rapidly passing; they

[002-053]seemed to have so much, and she could not decide so quickly. Before she

[002-054]knew it she was among them.

[002-055]“I beg your pardon, but won’t you wait one minute?” she asked.

[002-056]The girls stopped with wondering faces.

[002-057]“It’s your clothes,” explained Mrs. Sinton. “You look just beautiful to

[002-058]me. You look exactly as I should have wanted to see my girls. They both

[002-059]died of diphtheria when they were little, but they had yellow hair,

[002-060]dark eyes and pink cheeks, and everybody thought they were lovely. If

[002-061]they had lived, they’d been near your age now, and I’d want them to

[002-062]look like you.”

[002-063]There was sympathy on every girl face.

[002-064]“Why thank you!” said one of them. “We are very sorry for you.”

[002-065]“Of course you are,” said Margaret. “Everybody always has been. And

[002-066]because I can’t ever have the joy of a mother in thinking for my girls

[002-067]and buying pretty things for them, there is nothing left for me, but to

[002-068]do what I can for some one who has no mother to care for her. I know a

[002-069]girl, who would be just as pretty as any of you, if she had the

[002-070]clothes, but her mother does not think about her, so I mother her some

[002-071]myself.”

[002-072]“She must be a lucky girl,” said another.

[002-073]“Oh, she loves me,” said Margaret, “and I love her. I want her to look

[002-074]just like you do. Please tell me about your clothes. Are these the

[002-075]dresses and hats you wear to school? What kind of goods are they, and

[002-076]where do you buy them?”

[002-077]The girls began to laugh and cluster around Margaret. Wesley strode

[002-078]down the store with his head high through pride in her, but his heart

[002-079]was sore over the memory of two little faces under Brushwood sod. He

[002-080]inquired his way to the shoe department.

[002-081]“Why, every one of us have on gingham or linen dresses,” they said,

[002-082]“and they are our school clothes.”

[002-083]For a few moments there was a babel of laughing voices explaining to

[002-084]the delighted Margaret that school dresses should be bright and pretty,

[002-085]but simple and plain, and until cold weather they should wash.

[002-086]“I’ll tell you,” said [[ellen-brownlee|Ellen Brownlee]], “my father owns this store, I

[002-087]know all the clerks. I’ll take you to Miss Hartley. You tell her just

[002-088]how much you want to spend, and what you want to buy, and she will know

[002-089]how to get the most for your money. I’ve heard papa say she was the

[002-090]best clerk in the store for people who didn’t know precisely what they

[002-091]wanted.”

[002-092]“That’s the very thing,” agreed Margaret. “But before you go, tell me

[002-093]about your hair. Elnora’s hair is bright and wavy, but yours is silky

[002-094]as hackled flax. How do you do it?”

[002-095]“Elnora?” asked four girls in concert.

[002-096]“Yes, Elnora is the name of the girl I want these things for.”

[002-097]“Did she come to [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] to-day?” questioned one of them.

[002-098]“Was she in your classes?” demanded Margaret without reply.

[002-099]Four girls stood silent and thought fast. Had there been a strange girl

[002-100]among them, and had she been overlooked and passed by with

[002-101]indifference, because she was so very shabby? If she had appeared as

[002-102]much better than they, as she had looked worse, would her reception

[002-103]have been the same?

[002-104]“There was a strange girl from the country in the Freshman class

[002-105]to-day,” said Ellen [[mr-brownlee|Brownlee]], “and her name was Elnora.”

[002-106]“That was the girl,” said Margaret.

[002-107]“Are her people so very poor?” questioned Ellen.

[002-108]“No, not poor at all, come to think of it,” answered Margaret. “It’s a

[002-109]peculiar case. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] had a great trouble and she let it change

[002-110]her whole life and make a different woman of her. She used to be

[002-111]lovely; now she is forever saving and scared to death for fear they

[002-112]will go to the poorhouse; but there is a big farm, covered with lots of

[002-113]good timber. The taxes are high for women who can’t manage to clear and

[002-114]work the land. There ought to be enough to keep two of them in good

[002-115]shape all their lives, if they only knew how to do it. But no one ever

[002-116]told Kate Comstock anything, and never will, for she won’t listen. All

[002-117]she does is droop all day, and walk the edge of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] half the

[002-118]night, and neglect Elnora. If you girls would make life just a little

[002-119]easier for her it would be the finest thing you ever did.”

[002-120]All of them promised they would.

[002-121]“Now tell me about your hair,” persisted [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]].

[002-122]So they took her to a toilet counter, and she bought the proper hair

[002-123]soap, also a nail file, and cold cream, for use after windy days. Then

[002-124]they left her with the experienced clerk, and when at last Wesley found

[002-125]her she was loaded with bundles and the light of other days was in her

[002-126]beautiful eyes. Wesley also carried some packages.

[002-127]“Did you get any stockings?” he whispered.

[002-128]“No, I didn’t,” she said. “I was so interested in dresses and hair

[002-129]ribbons and a—a hat——” she hesitated and glanced at Wesley. “Of course,

[002-130]a hat!” prompted Wesley. “That I forgot all about those horrible shoes.

[002-131]She’s got to have decent shoes, Wesley.”

[002-132]“Sure!” said Wesley. “She’s got decent shoes. But the man said some

[002-133]brown stockings ought to go with them. Take a peep, will you!”

[002-134]Wesley opened a box and displayed a pair of thick-soled, beautifully

[002-135]shaped brown walking shoes of low cut. Margaret cried out with

[002-136]pleasure.

[002-137]“But do you suppose they are the right size, Wesley? What did you get?”

[002-138]“I just said for a girl of sixteen with a slender foot.”

[002-139]“Well, that’s about as near as I could come. If they don’t fit when she

[002-140]tries them, we will drive straight in and change them. Come on now,

[002-141]let’s get home.”

[002-142]All the way they discussed how they should give Elnora their purchases

[002-143]and what Mrs. Comstock would say.

[002-144]“I am afraid she will be awful mad,” said Margaret.

[002-145]“She’ll just rip!” replied Wesley graphically. “But if she wants to

[002-146]leave the raising of her girl to the neighbours, she needn’t get

[002-147]fractious if they take some pride in doing a good job. From now on I

[002-148]calculate Elnora shall go to school; and she shall have all the clothes

[002-149]and books she needs, if I go around on the back of Kate Comstock’s land

[002-150]and cut a tree, or drive off a calf to pay for them. Why I know one

[002-151]tree she owns that would put Elnora in heaven for a year. Just think of

[002-152]it, Margaret! It’s not fair. One-third of what is there belongs to

[002-153]Elnora by law, and if Kate Comstock raises a row I’ll tell her so, and

[002-154]see that the girl gets it. You go to see Kate in the morning, and I’ll

[002-155]go with you. Tell her you want Elnora’s pattern, that you are going to

[002-156]make her a dress, for helping us. And sort of hint at a few more

[002-157]things. If Kate balks, I’ll take a hand and settle her. I’ll go to law

[002-158]for Elnora’s share of that land and sell enough to educate her.”

[002-159]“Why, Wesley Sinton, you’re perfectly wild.”

[002-160]“I’m not! Did you ever stop to think that such cases are so frequent

[002-161]there have been laws made to provide for them? I can bring it up in

[002-162]court and force Kate to educate Elnora, and board and clothe her till

[002-163]she’s of age, and then she can take her share.”

[002-164]“Wesley, Kate would go crazy!”

[002-165]“She’s crazy now. The idea of any mother living with as sweet a girl as

[002-166]Elnora and letting her suffer till I find her crying like a funeral. It

[002-167]makes me fighting mad. All uncalled for. Not a grain of sense in it.

[002-168]I’ve offered and offered to oversee clearing her land and working her

[002-169]fields. Let her sell a good tree, or a few acres. Something is going to

[002-170]be done, right now. Elnora’s been fairly happy up to this, but to spoil

[002-171]the school life she’s planned, is to ruin all her life. I won’t have

[002-172]it! If Elnora won’t take these things, so help me, I’ll tell her what

[002-173]she is worth, and loan her the money and she can pay me back when she

[002-174]comes of age. I am going to have it out with Kate Comstock in the

[002-175]morning. Here we are! You open up what you got while I put away the

[002-176]horses, and then I’ll show you.”

[002-177]When Wesley came from [[sinton-barn|the barn]] Margaret had four pieces of crisp

[002-178]gingham, a pale blue, a pink, a gray with green stripes and a rich

[002-179]brown and blue plaid. On each of them lay a yard and a half of wide

[002-180]ribbon to match. There were handkerchiefs and a brown leather belt. In

[002-181]her hands she held a wide-brimmed tan straw hat, having a high crown

[002-182]banded with velvet strips each of which fastened with a tiny gold

[002-183]buckle.

[002-184]“It looks kind of bare now,” she explained. “It had three quills on it

[002-185]here.”

[002-186]“Did you have them taken off?” asked Wesley.

[002-187]“Yes, I did. The price was two and a half for the hat, and those things

[002-188]were a dollar and a half apiece. I couldn’t pay that.”

[002-189]“It does seem considerable,” admitted Wesley, “but will it look right

[002-190]without them?”

[002-191]“No, it won’t!” said Margaret. “It’s going to have quills on it. Do you

[002-192]remember those beautiful peacock wing feathers that [[phoebe-simms|Phoebe Simms]] gave

[002-193]me? Three of them go on just where those came off, and nobody will ever

[002-194]know the difference. They match the hat to a moral, and they are just a

[002-195]little longer and richer than the ones that I had taken off. I was

[002-196]wondering whether I better sew them on to-night while I remember how

[002-197]they set, or wait till morning.”

[002-198]“Don’t risk it!” exclaimed Wesley anxiously. “Don’t you risk it! Sew

[002-199]them on right now!”

[002-200]“Open your bundles, while I get the thread,” said Margaret.

[002-201]Wesley unwrapped the shoes. Margaret took them up and pinched the

[002-202]leather and stroked them.

[002-203]“My, but they are fine!” she cried.

[002-204]Wesley picked up one and slowly turned it in his big hands. He glanced

[002-205]at his foot and back to the shoe.

[002-206]“It’s a little bit of a thing, Margaret,” he said softly. “Like as not

[002-207]I’ll have to take it back. It seems as if it couldn’t fit.”

[002-208]“It seems as if it didn’t dare do anything else,” said Margaret.

[002-209]“That’s a happy little shoe to get the chance to carry as fine a girl

[002-210]as Elnora to high school. Now what’s in the other box?”

[002-211]Wesley looked at Margaret doubtfully.

[002-212]“Why,” he said, “you know there’s going to be rainy days, and those

[002-213]things she has now ain’t fit for anything but to drive up the cows——”

[002-214]“Wesley, did you get high shoes, too?”

[002-215]“Well, she ought to have them! The man said he would make them cheaper

[002-216]if I took both pairs at once.”

[002-217]Margaret laughed aloud. “Those will do her past Christmas,” she

[002-218]exulted. “What else did you buy?”

[002-219]“Well sir,” said Wesley, “I saw something to-day. You told me about

[002-220]Kate getting that tin pail for Elnora to carry to high school and you

[002-221]said you told her it was a shame. I guess Elnora was ashamed all right,

[002-222]for to-night she stopped at the old case [[duncan|Duncan]] gave her, and took out

[002-223]that pail, where it had been all day, and put a napkin inside it.

[002-224]Coming home she confessed she was half starved because she hid her

[002-225]dinner under a culvert, and a tramp took it. She hadn’t had a bite to

[002-226]eat the whole day. But she never complained at all, she was pleased

[002-227]that she hadn’t lost the napkin. So I just inquired around till I found

[002-228]this, and I think it’s about the ticket.”

[002-229]Wesley opened the package and laid a brown leather [[elnoras-lunch-box|lunch box]] on the

[002-230]table. “Might be a couple of books, or drawing tools or most anything

[002-231]that’s neat and genteel. You see, it opens this way.”

[002-232]It did open, and inside was a space for sandwiches, a little porcelain

[002-233]box for cold meat or fried chicken, another for salad, a glass with a

[002-234]lid which screwed on, held by a ring in a corner, for custard or jelly,

[002-235]a flask for tea or milk, a beautiful little knife, fork, and spoon

[002-236]fastened in holders, and a place for a napkin.

[002-237]Margaret was almost crying over it.

[002-238]“How I’d love to fill it!” she exclaimed.

[002-239]“Do it the first time, just to show Kate Comstock what love is!” said

[002-240]Wesley. “Get up early in the morning and make one of those dresses

[002-241]to-morrow. Can’t you make a plain gingham dress in a day? I’ll pick a

[002-242]chicken, and you fry it and fix a little custard for the cup, and do it

[002-243]up brown. Go on, Maggie, you do it!”

[002-244]“I never can,” said Margaret. “I am slow as the itch about sewing, and

[002-245]these are not going to be plain dresses when it comes to making them.

[002-246]There are going to be edgings of plain green, pink, and brown to the

[002-247]bias strips, and tucks and pleats around the hips, fancy belts and

[002-248]collars, and all of it takes time.”

[002-249]“Then Kate Comstock’s got to help,” said Wesley. “Can the two of you

[002-250]make one, and get that lunch to-morrow?”

[002-251]“Easy, but she’ll never do it!”

[002-252]“You see if she doesn’t!” said Wesley. “You get up and cut it out, and

[002-253]soon as Elnora is gone I’ll go after Kate myself. She’ll take what I’ll

[002-254]say better alone. But she’ll come, and she’ll help make the dress.

[002-255]These other things are our Christmas gifts to Elnora. She’ll no doubt

[002-256]need them more now than she will then, and we can give them just as

[002-257]well. That’s yours, and this is mine, or whichever way you choose.”

[002-258]Wesley untied a good brown umbrella and shook out the folds of a long,

[002-259]brown raincoat. Margaret dropped the hat, arose and took the coat. She

[002-260]tried it on, felt it, cooed over it and matched it with the umbrella.

[002-261]“Did it look anything like rain to-night?” she inquired so anxiously

[002-262]that Wesley laughed.

[002-263]“And this last bundle?” she said, dropping back in her chair, the coat

[002-264]still over her shoulders.

[002-265]“I couldn’t buy this much stuff for any other woman and nothing for my

[002-266]own,” said Wesley. “It’s Christmas for you, too, Margaret!” He shook

[002-267]out fold after fold of soft gray satiny goods that would look lovely

[002-268]against Margaret’s pink cheeks and whitening hair.

[002-269]“Oh, you old darling!” she exclaimed, and fled sobbing into his arms.

[002-270]But she soon dried her eyes, raked together the coals in the cooking

[002-271]stove and boiled one of the dress patterns in salt water for half an

[002-272]hour. Wesley held the lamp while she hung the goods on the line to dry.

[002-273]Then she set the irons on the stove so they would be hot the first

[002-274]thing in the morning.
