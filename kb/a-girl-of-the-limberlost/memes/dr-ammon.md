Dr. Ammon is Philip Ammon's father, a respected physician from Chicago. Throughout the latter half of the novel, he serves as a voice of reason and paternal wisdom, particularly as Philip navigates his complicated feelings between Edith Carr and Elnora Comstock.

## Professional Life

Dr. Ammon is established as a physician in Chicago, part of the city's professional class. When Philip falls ill, he stays with Dr. Ammon in Onabasha, where the doctor provides both medical care and lodging for his son (013-141). When nothing else would stay on a patient's stomach, "Dr. Ammon keeps people alive with it," a testament to his medical skill.

## Fatherly Counsel

When Philip falls seriously ill with typhoid fever, Dr. Ammon's medical expertise becomes crucial. But his role extends beyond physical care. Philip, in his distress, asks, "Where is my father?" — and the elder Ammon arrives with a distressed expression, embodying the concerned parent (019-306). Dr. Ammon offers Philip thoughtful counsel about the nature of love, obligation, and integrity. He represents the measured, mature perspective that contrasts with the passionate impulsiveness of youth.

## Role in Philip's Decision

Dr. Ammon's influence is felt most strongly in the later chapters, when Philip must decide between honoring his engagement to Edith Carr and following his heart toward Elnora. Elnora encounters Ammon's father in the course of events (019-323), and the elder man's quiet dignity reinforces the values Philip ultimately embraces. Rather than dictating a course of action, Dr. Ammon guides Philip to examine his own feelings honestly, embodying the novel's broader theme that wisdom comes from self-knowledge and integrity.
