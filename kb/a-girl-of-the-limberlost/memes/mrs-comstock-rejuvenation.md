# Mrs. Comstock Experiments with Rejuvenation

## Summary

Mrs. Comstock Experiments with Rejuvenation is the physical transformation event in Chapter XVIII of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. While Elnora is away at a week-long Teachers' Institute, Katharine Comstock discovers at the bank that her farm income has been compounding interest for twenty years — all the bitter poverty was unnecessary. Liberated by this revelation, she embarks on a comprehensive self-transformation: ordering four new dresses, subjecting herself to a painful skin-rejuvenation regimen, having her snowy hair professionally styled, and buying elegant hats and a coat. She then rents a furnished house in Onabasha near the high school and stages a dramatic reveal: descending the stairs as a transformed woman in lavender gingham, unrecognizable to her own daughter. "You my mother!" Elnora cries. "I don't believe it!" (018-143).

## Context

By Chapter XVIII, Mrs. Comstock's emotional thaw — begun with the moonlight dance (Chapter XVII) — has prepared the ground for physical transformation. Her earlier grief had manifested externally: she went bareheaded in the sun, wore a sunbonnet she hated, and neglected her appearance entirely. Her dark, weathered skin and unkempt hair were the visible marks of two decades of frozen mourning. The catalyst is Elnora's departure for the Teachers' Institute: alone and heartsick, Mrs. Comstock finally faces practical realities.

## Narrative

After seeing Elnora off at the depot, Mrs. Comstock goes to the bank for the first time in twenty years. The cashier laughs: "We have been ready for you any time these twenty years" (018-059--060). Her account has been compounding interest. "Mrs. Comstock stared at the total with dazed and unbelieving eyes. Through her sick heart rushed the realization, that if she merely had stood before that wicket and asked one question, she would have known that all those bitter years of skimping for Elnora and herself had been unnecessary" (018-069--073).

She immediately telegraphs Elnora offering college: "Found money at bank didn't know about. If you want to go to college, come on first train and get ready" — and adds, for the first time in writing, "With love, Mother" (018-075--078). Elnora replies she will teach that winter.

Liberated, Mrs. Comstock goes to a dressmaker and orders four dresses: two plain everyday ones, a serviceable dark gray cloth suit, and "a soft light gray silk with touches of lavender and lace" (018-084--085). She makes extensive purchases at Brownlee's.

Then begins the physical transformation. She gathers tansy, boils it into a thick green tea, stirs in oatmeal, and bandages her face, neck, hands, and arms with the paste (018-090--095). The poultice chemically peels away decades of sun damage. "By the third morning she was a raw even red, the fourth she had faded to a brilliant pink" (018-099--100). Eventually, "she was a pale pink, later a delicate porcelain white" (018-109--110).

She visits a hairdresser who washes, dresses, and styles her "rope of snowy hair" with becoming pins and combs (018-110--112). At the milliner's, she buys a street hat and "a gray satin with lavender orchids to wear with the silk dress" (018-113--114). Her final purchase is a loose coat of soft gray broadcloth with white lining, lavender embroidery, and gray gloves (018-115--117).

Then she rents a furnished house three blocks from the high school, moves in, and arranges everything before Elnora knows. When summoned by a messenger boy, Elnora finds "a tall, dark-eyed woman with cheeks faintly pink and a crown of fluffy snow-white hair" wearing "a lavender gingham dress with white collar and cuffs" descending the stairs (018-141--142).

"You my mother!" Elnora cries. "I don't believe it!" (018-143).

"Well, you better!" said Mrs. Comstock, "because it's true! You said you wished I were like the other girls' mothers, and I've shot as close the mark as I could without any practice" (018-145). She concludes: "I've gone sane" (018-165).

## Significance

The rejuvenation is the physical counterpart to the emotional rebirth begun in the moonlight dance. Where Chapter XVII released Mrs. Comstock's "penned-up force" through music and movement, Chapter XVIII remakes her external self to match her newly liberated interior. The gray-and-lavender palette — muted, dignified, with a hint of soft femininity — defines her new persona. The bank revelation adds tragic dimension: her grief was so consuming that for two decades she did not even check her account. The transformation is earned through discomfort (the painful poultices) and practical effort (learning to style her own hair). Mrs. Comstock's purpose is explicitly maternal — "I saw I must put up an appearance that wouldn't disgrace you" — and the result is a mother Elnora can finally recognize and be proud of.
