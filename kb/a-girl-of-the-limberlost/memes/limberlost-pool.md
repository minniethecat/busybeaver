# The Bottomless Pool

## Description

The Bottomless Pool is a large, slime-covered pool deep within [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] Swamp, surrounded by dense woods and accessible only by winding swamp paths. It is the site where [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]], [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s father, drowned years before the novel begins. The pool is said to be bottomless — a local belief that contributes to its aura of mystery and dread. (014-157)

The pool is described as having a blue border of vegetation — a deceptive beauty that masks its dangerous depths. It lies in a secluded area of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], not visible from [[comstock-garden|the garden]] or [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], but close enough that [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] makes pilgrimages there. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], who knows the pool's tragic history, keeps her distance from it during her swamp expeditions. (015-178)

> It was tragic. Is that pool really bottomless? (014-162)

## Location

The pool is located within [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] Swamp, reachable from the Comstock cabin by following a swamp path past [[comstock-garden|the garden]]. It is deep enough into [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] to feel isolated, yet near enough that [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] can visit it when grief overwhelms her. The pool's location near the Comstock property means that the family lives in daily proximity to the site of their greatest tragedy. (011-226)

The path to the pool passes through woods and along [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]'s edge. [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], exploring [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], is drawn to the pool and its story, recognizing its significance in the Comstock family's history. (012-179)

## Significance

The Bottomless Pool is the physical embodiment of the Comstock family's trauma. [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]]'s drowning here — whether accident or suicide is never fully clarified — is the wound around which [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] has built her twenty years of grief and withdrawal. The pool represents everything [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] cannot let go of: her love for Robert, her anger at [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] that took him, and her fear of losing [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] to the same forces. (011-226)

> She kept far from the pool where Robert Comstock had sunk. (011-226)

For [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], the pool represents the tragedy she was born into but never witnessed. She knows the story, respects the grief it represents, but refuses to let it define her life as it has her mother's. Her decision to skirt the pool rather than fear it reflects her broader approach to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]: respect its dangers, but do not be paralyzed by them. (012-179)

The pool is also significant as the place where [[grief-and-loss|Katharine's grief]] is most raw. Her visits to the pool are private, ritualistic moments when she confronts the loss that has shaped her life. When Philip learns of the pool's history, he gains a deeper understanding of the forces that have shaped both [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and her mother. (014-157)

## Key Scenes

- **The pool's discovery by Philip**: [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], exploring [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], comes upon the pool and learns its tragic history. The moment deepens his understanding of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s family. (014-157)

- **[[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]'s pilgrimage**: [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] goes to the pool, drawn by grief and memory. The pool is where she feels closest to Robert and furthest from the living world. (012-665)

- **[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s avoidance**: [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] passes the pool with averted head, refusing to let its darkness claim her as it claimed her father. (012-179)

- **The night visits**: In her deepest grief, [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] visits the pool at night, a testament to the hold this place has on her soul. (005-373)
