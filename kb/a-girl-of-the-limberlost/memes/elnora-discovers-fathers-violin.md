# Elnora Discovers Her Father's Violin

## Summary

While browsing in an [[onabasha|Onabasha]] shop, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] comes upon a violin that once belonged to her father, [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]]. The instrument, left at a repair shop before his death and never reclaimed, becomes the key to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s musical identity. She purchases it with money earned from selling Indian relics, teaches herself to play by practising in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], and eventually uses it to transform her social standing at [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]]. (009-119) The violin represents [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s inheritance of her father's artistic nature and her own emergence as a person of talent and beauty beyond the poverty of her circumstances.

## Context

[[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]], [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s father, drowned in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] Swamp before [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] was born. He was a man of unusual gifts — a fine musician who played the violin with passion and skill. [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]], consumed by grief, had never spoken of these qualities to her daughter. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] knew her father only as an absence, a source of her mother's bitterness, and the tragic figure who had sunk into the bottomless pool. (009-119)

The discovery occurs during [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s early weeks at [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]], a period when she is acutely aware of her poverty and social marginalisation. She has begun [[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] small amounts of money through the sale of Indian relics she collected from [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], and has learned to navigate the town's commercial district with growing confidence. (009-119)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s musical aptitude has already been hinted at — she possesses an unusually fine singing voice, and her sensitivity to the sounds of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] suggests a receptivity to music that runs deeper than formal training. (009-166)

## Key Participants

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — The discoverer. Her recognition of the violin as her father's and her determination to purchase and learn it demonstrate both her intuitive connection to her lost parent and her characteristic resolve. She identifies the violin not by documentation but by feeling — "I just seemed to know it was his." (009-125)

**[[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]]** — Absent but powerfully present. The violin is the first tangible link [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] has to her father's inner life — not the farmer and provider her mother remembers, but the artist and musician whose sensibility she has inherited. His violin, "inspired and taught to sing by a man who knew how," becomes the vehicle through which his daughter claims her birthright. (025-003)

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — Initially resistant. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] brings the violin home, her mother's reaction is one of pain and fear — the instrument revives memories of Robert that [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] has spent years suppressing. Her eventual acceptance of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s playing marks one of the first concessions she makes to her daughter's independent life. (009-181)

## Key Events

### The Discovery

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] enters a shop in [[onabasha|Onabasha]] and sees the violin. She is drawn to it before knowing its history, and upon inquiring, learns that it was left for repair by a man who never returned to claim it. The shopkeeper confirms that the owner's name was Comstock. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s recognition is immediate and visceral: "I just seemed to know it was his." (009-119)

### The Purchase

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] buys the violin with the money she has earned from selling her collection of Indian arrowheads, stone axes, and other relics — objects she gathered from [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. The transaction is symbolic: she trades the artifacts of the distant past for the instrument that will shape her future. (009-175)

### Mrs. Comstock's Reaction

[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]'s response to the violin is complex. She recognises it instantly — "It's Robert's violin" — and her first impulse is to recoil from the memories it stirs. (009-181) But she does not forbid [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] from keeping it, and her grudging tolerance of the instrument's presence in [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] represents a small but significant opening in the wall of her grief.

### Learning to Play

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] teaches herself to play in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] and in Freckles's old room, where no one can hear her early attempts. The swamp becomes her practice hall, its birds and insects her only audience. Her progress is steady and, by the time of [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] entertainment, remarkable: she can draw from the violin a voice that moves others to tears. (009-190)

> And she had the violin, which she was beginning to play with a proficiency that surprised everyone who heard her. (009-198)

## Aftermath

The violin transforms [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s place in the world. At [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] entertainment — held years after the discovery — she takes the stage with her father's instrument and plays with such skill and emotion that the same students who once mocked her clothes are silenced with admiration. (011-373) The violin gives her a language beyond words, one that crosses the social boundaries she has struggled against.

The instrument also reshapes [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s relationship with her mother. When [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] hears [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] play, she hears Robert — and in hearing him, she begins, slowly and painfully, to reconnect with the world of the living. The violin becomes a bridge between the dead father and the living daughter, between [[grief-and-loss|Katharine's grief]] and her capacity for joy.

Later, the violin becomes part of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s professional identity. She leads [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] orchestra, plays at [[community-support|community]] events, and is invited to perform beyond [[onabasha|Onabasha]]. (016-158) By the novel's end, when she plays for Freckles and the Angel, her father's violin has carried her from the margins of [[onabasha|Onabasha]] society to a place of honour among those who recognise true gift.

## Significance

The discovery of the violin is the novel's pivotal moment of inheritance — not of money or property, but of talent, sensibility, and identity. [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] left his daughter no material legacy, but his violin, preserved by chance in a repair shop, becomes the key that unlocks [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s artistic nature and her eventual triumph over poverty and social exclusion.

The event also establishes one of the novel's central themes: that gifts from the past — even a past marked by tragedy — can be reclaimed and transformed. The violin that Robert played in [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] at the edge of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], that fell silent when he drowned, is given new voice by his daughter. In playing it, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] does more than honour her father's memory — she completes a story that his death left unfinished.

The scene in which [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] "just seemed to know" the violin was her father's speaks to the novel's broader conviction that deep connections — to nature, to family, to one's own gifts — are felt rather than reasoned, and that the truest inheritance is not willed but discovered.
