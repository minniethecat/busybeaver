# CHAPTER XVIII

[018-001]WHEREIN MRS. COMSTOCK EXPERIMENTS WITH REJUVENATION, AND ELNORA TEACHES

[018-002]NATURAL HISTORY

[018-003]For the following week [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] worked so hard there

[018-004]was no time to talk, and they were compelled to sleep from physical

[018-005]exhaustion. Neither of them made any pretence of eating, for they could

[018-006]not swallow without an effort, so they drank milk and worked. Elnora

[018-007]kept on setting bait for Catacolae and Sphinginae, which, unlike the

[018-008]big moths of June, live several months. She took all the dragonflies

[018-009]and butterflies she could, and when she went over the list for the man

[018-010]of India, she found, to her amazement, that with Philip’s help she once

[018-011]more had it complete save a pair of Yellow Emperors.

[018-012]This circumstance was so surprising she had a fleeting thought of

[018-013]writing Philip and asking him to see if he could not secure her a pair.

[018-014]She did tell the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]], who from every source at her command tried

[018-015]to complete the series with these moths, but could not find any for

[018-016]sale.

[018-017]“I think the mills of the Gods are grinding this grist,” said Elnora,

[018-018]“and we might as well wait patiently until they choose to send a Yellow

[018-019]Emperor.”

[018-020]Mrs. Comstock invented work. When she had nothing more to do, she hoed

[018-021]in [[comstock-garden|the garden]] although the earth was hard and dry and there were no

[018-022]plants that really needed attention. Then came a notification that

[018-023]Elnora would be compelled to attend a week’s session of the Teachers’

[018-024]Institute held at the county seat twenty miles north of [[onabasha|Onabasha]] the

[018-025]following week. That gave them something of which to think and real

[018-026]work to do. Elnora was requested to bring her violin. As she was on the

[018-027]programme of one of the most important sessions for a talk on nature

[018-028]work in grade schools, she was driven to prepare her speech, also to

[018-029]select and practise some music. Her mother turned her attention to

[018-030][[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]].

[018-031]They went to Onabasha together and purchased a simple and appropriate

[018-032]fall suit and hat, goods for a dainty little coloured frock, and a

[018-033]dress skirt and several fancy waists. [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] came down and the

[018-034]sewing began. When everything was finished and packed, Elnora kissed

[018-035]her mother good-bye at [[onabasha-station|the depot]], and entered the train. Mrs. Comstock

[018-036]went into the waiting-room and dropped into a seat to rest. Her heart

[018-037]was so sore her whole left side felt tender. She was half starved for

[018-038]the food she had no appetite to take. She had worked in dogged

[018-039]determination until she was exhausted. For a time she simply sat and

[018-040]rested. Then she began to think. She was glad Elnora had gone where she

[018-041]would be compelled to fix her mind on other matters for a few days. She

[018-042]remembered the girl had said she wanted to go.

[018-043]School would begin the following week. She thought over what Elnora

[018-044]would have to do to accomplish her work successfully. She would be

[018-045]compelled to arise at six o’clock, walk three miles through varying

[018-046]weather, lead [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] orchestra, and then put in the remainder

[018-047]of the day travelling from building to building over the city, teaching

[018-048]a specified length of time every week in each room. She must have her

[018-049]object lessons ready, and she must do a certain amount of practising

[018-050]with the orchestra. Then a cold lunch at noon, and a three-mile walk at

[018-051]night.

[018-052]“Humph!” said Mrs. Comstock, “to get through that the girl would have

[018-053]to be made of cast-iron. I wonder how I can help her best?”

[018-054]She thought deeply.

[018-055]“The less she sees of what she’s been having all summer, the sooner

[018-056]she’ll feel better about it,” she muttered.

[018-057]She arose, went to the bank and inquired for the cashier.

[018-058]“I want to know just how I am fixed here,” she said.

[018-059]The cashier laughed. “You haven’t been in a hurry,” he replied. “We

[018-060]have been ready for you any time these twenty years, but you didn’t

[018-061]seem to pay much attention. Your account is rather flourishing.

[018-062]Interest, when it gets to compounding, is quite a money breeder. Come

[018-063]back here to a table and I will show you your balances.”

[018-064]Mrs. Comstock sank into a chair and waited while the cashier read a

[018-065]jumble of figures to her. It meant that her deposits had exceeded her

[018-066]expenses from one to three hundred dollars a year, according to the

[018-067]cattle, sheep, hogs, poultry, butter, and eggs she had sold. The

[018-068]aggregate of these sums had been compounding interest throughout the

[018-069]years. Mrs. Comstock stared at the total with dazed and unbelieving

[018-070]eyes. Through her sick heart rushed the realization, that if she merely

[018-071]had stood before that wicket and asked one question, she would have

[018-072]known that all those bitter years of skimping for Elnora and herself

[018-073]had been unnecessary. She arose and went back to the depot.

[018-074]“I want to send a message,” she said. She picked up the pencil, and

[018-075]with rash extravagance, wrote, “Found money at bank didn’t know about.

[018-076]If you want to go to college, come on first train and get ready.” She

[018-077]hesitated a second and then she said to herself grimly, “Yes, I’ll pay

[018-078]for that, too,” and recklessly added, “With love, Mother.” Then she sat

[018-079]waiting for the answer. It came in less than an hour. “Will teach this

[018-080]winter. With dearest love, Elnora.”

[018-081]Mrs. Comstock held the message a long time. When she arose she was

[018-082]ravenously hungry, but the pain in her heart was a little easier. She

[018-083]went to a restaurant and ate some food, then to a dressmaker where she

[018-084]ordered four dresses: two very plain every-day ones, a serviceable dark

[018-085]gray cloth suit, and a soft light gray silk with touches of lavender

[018-086]and lace. She made a heavy list of purchases at [[mr-brownlee|Brownlee]]’s, and the

[018-087]remainder of the day she did business in her direct and spirited way.

[018-088]At night she was so tired she scarcely could walk home, but she built a

[018-089]fire and cooked and ate a hearty meal.

[018-090]Later she went out beside the west fence and gathered an armful of

[018-091]tansy which she boiled to a thick green tea. Then she stirred in

[018-092]oatmeal until it was a stiff paste. She spread a sheet over her bed and

[018-093]began tearing strips of old muslin. She bandaged each hand and arm with

[018-094]the mixture and plastered the soggy, evil-smelling stuff in a thick

[018-095]poultice over her face and neck. She was so tired she went to sleep,

[018-096]and when she awoke she was half skinned. She bathed her face and hands,

[018-097]did the work and went back to town, coming home at night to go through

[018-098]the same process.

[018-099]By the third morning she was a raw even red, the fourth she had faded

[018-100]to a brilliant pink under the soothing influence of a cream

[018-101]recommended. That day came a letter from Elnora saying that she would

[018-102]remain where she was until Saturday morning, and then come to [[ellen-brownlee|Ellen]]

[018-103]Brownlee’s at Onabasha and stay for the Saturday’s session of teachers

[018-104]to arrange their year’s work. Sunday was Ellen’s last day at home, and

[018-105]she wanted Elnora very much. She had to call together the orchestra and

[018-106]practise them Sunday; and could not come home until after school Monday

[018-107]night. Mrs. Comstock at once answered the letter saying those

[018-108]arrangements suited her.

[018-109]The following day she was a pale pink, later a delicate porcelain

[018-110]white. Then she went to a hairdresser and had the rope of snowy hair

[018-111]which covered her scalp washed, dressed, and fastened with such pins

[018-112]and combs as were decided to be most becoming. She took samples of her

[018-113]dresses, went to a milliner, and bought a street hat to match her suit,

[018-114]and a gray satin with lavender orchids to wear with the silk dress. Her

[018-115]last investment was a loose coat of soft gray broadcloth with white

[018-116]lining, and touches of lavender on the embroidered collar, and gray

[018-117]gloves to match.

[018-118]Then she went home, rested and worked by turns until Monday. When

[018-119]school closed on that evening, Elnora, so tired she almost trembled,

[018-120]came down the long walk after a late session of teachers’ meeting, to

[018-121]be stopped by a messenger boy.

[018-122]“There’s a lady wants to see you most important. I am to take you to

[018-123]the place,” he said.

[018-124]Elnora groaned. She could not imagine who wanted her, but there was

[018-125]nothing to do but find out; tired and anxious to see her mother as she

[018-126]was.

[018-127]“This is the place,” said the boy, and went his way whistling. Elnora

[018-128]was three blocks from the [[education-as-liberation|high school]] building on the same street. She

[018-129]was before a quaint old house, fresh with paint and covered with vines.

[018-130]There was a long wide lot, grass-covered, closely set with trees, and a

[018-131]barn and chicken park at the back that seemed to be occupied. Elnora

[018-132]stepped on the veranda which was furnished with straw rugs,

[018-133]bent-hickory chairs, hanging baskets, and a table with a work-box and

[018-134]magazines, and knocked at the screen door.

[018-135]Inside she could see polished floors, walls freshly papered in

[018-136]low-toned harmonious colours, straw rugs and madras curtains. It seemed

[018-137]to be a restful, homelike place to which she had come. A second later

[018-138]down an open stairway came a tall, dark-eyed woman with cheeks faintly

[018-139]pink and a crown of fluffy snow-white hair. She wore a lavender gingham

[018-140]dress with white collar and cuffs, and she called as she advanced:

[018-141]“That screen isn’t latched! Open it and come see your brand-new mother,

[018-142]my girl.”

[018-143]Elnora stepped inside the door. “Mother!” she cried. “You my mother! I

[018-144]don’t believe it!”

[018-145]“Well, you better!” said Mrs. Comstock, “because it’s true! You said

[018-146]you wished I were like the other girls’ mothers, and I’ve shot as close

[018-147]the mark as I could without any practice. I thought that walk would be

[018-148]too much for you this winter, so I just rented this house and moved in,

[018-149]to be near you, and help more in case I’m needed. I’ve only lived here

[018-150]a day, but I like it so well I’ve a mortal big notion to buy the

[018-151]place.”

[018-152]“But mother!” protested Elnora, clinging to her wonderingly. “You are

[018-153]perfectly beautiful, and this house is a little paradise, but how will

[018-154]we ever pay for it? We can’t afford it!”

[018-155]“Humph! Have you forgotten I telegraphed you I’d found some money I

[018-156]didn’t know about? All I’ve done is paid for, and plenty more to settle

[018-157]for all I propose to do.”

[018-158]Mrs. Comstock glanced around with satisfaction.

[018-159]“I may get homesick as a pup before spring,” she said, “but if I do I

[018-160]can go back. If I don’t, I’ll sell some timber and put a few oil wells

[018-161]where they don’t show much. I can have land enough cleared for a few

[018-162]fields and put a tenant on our farm, and we will buy this and settle

[018-163]here. It’s for sale.”

[018-164]“You don’t look it, but you’ve surely gone mad!”

[018-165]“Just the reverse, my girl,” said Mrs. Comstock, “I’ve gone sane. If

[018-166]you are going to undertake this work, you must be convenient to it. And

[018-167]your mother should be where she can see that you are properly dressed,

[018-168]fed, and cared for. This is our—let me think—reception-room. How do you

[018-169]like it? This door leads to your workroom and study. I didn’t do much

[018-170]there because I wasn’t sure of my way. But I knew you would want a rug,

[018-171]curtains, table, shelves for books, and a case for your specimens, so I

[018-172]had a carpenter shelve and enclose that end of it. Looks pretty neat to

[018-173]me. The dining-room and kitchen are back, one of the cows in [[sinton-barn|the barn]],

[018-174]and some chickens in the coop. I understand that none of the other

[018-175]girls’ mothers milk a cow, so a neighbour boy will tend to ours for a

[018-176]third of the milk. There are three bedrooms, and a bath upstairs. Go

[018-177]take one, put on some fresh clothes, and come to supper. You can find

[018-178]your room because your things are in it.”

[018-179]Elnora kissed her mother over and over, and hurried upstairs. She

[018-180]identified her room by the dressing-case. There were a pretty rug, and

[018-181]curtains, white iron bed, plain and rocking chairs to match her case, a

[018-182]shirtwaist chest, and the big closet was filled with her old clothing

[018-183]and several new dresses. She found the bathroom, bathed, dressed in

[018-184]fresh linen and went down to a supper that was an evidence of Mrs.

[018-185]Comstock’s highest art in cooking. Elnora was so hungry she ate her

[018-186]first real meal in two weeks. But the bites went down slowly because

[018-187]she forgot about them in watching her mother.

[018-188]“How on earth did you do it?” she asked at last. “I always thought you

[018-189]were naturally brown as a nut.”

[018-190]“Oh, that was tan and sunburn!” explained Mrs. Comstock. “I always knew

[018-191]I was white underneath it. I hated to shade my face because I hadn’t

[018-192]anything but a sunbonnet, and I couldn’t stand for it to touch my ears,

[018-193]so I went bareheaded and took all the colour I accumulated. But when I

[018-194]began to think of moving you in to your work, I saw I must put up an

[018-195]appearance that wouldn’t disgrace you, so I thought I’d best remove the

[018-196]crust. It took some time, and I hope I may die before I ever endure the

[018-197]feel and the smell of the stuff I used again, but it skinned me nicely.

[018-198]What you now see is my own with a little dust of rice powder, for

[018-199]protection. I’m sort of tender yet.”

[018-200]“And your lovely, lovely hair?” breathed Elnora.

[018-201]“Hairdresser did that!” said Mrs. Comstock. “It cost like smoke. But I

[018-202]watched her, and with a little help from you I can wash it alone next

[018-203]time, though it will be [[work-and-self-reliance|hard work]]. I let her monkey with it until she

[018-204]said she had found ‘my style.’ Then I tore it down and had her show me

[018-205]how to build it up again three times. I thought my arms would drop.

[018-206]When I paid the bill for her work, the time I’d taken, the pins, and

[018-207]combs she’d used, I nearly had heart failure, but I didn’t turn a hair

[018-208]before her. I just smiled at her sweetly and said, ‘How reasonable you

[018-209]are!’ Come to think of it, she was! She might have charged me ten

[018-210]dollars for what she did quite as well as nine seventy-five. I couldn’t

[018-211]have helped myself. I had made no bargain to begin on.”

[018-212]Then Elnora leaned back in her chair and shouted, in a gust of hearty

[018-213]laughter, so a little of the ache ceased in her breast. There was no

[018-214]time to think, the remainder of that evening, she was so tired she had

[018-215]to sleep, while her mother did not awaken her until she barely had time

[018-216]to dress, breakfast and reach school. There was nothing in the new life

[018-217]to remind her of the old. It seemed as if there never came a minute for

[018-218]retrospection, but her mother appeared on the scene with more work, or

[018-219]some entertaining thing to do.

[018-220]Mrs. Comstock invited Elnora’s friends to visit her, and proved herself

[018-221]a bright and interesting hostess. She digested a subject before she

[018-222]spoke; and when she advanced a view, her point was sure to be original

[018-223]and tersely expressed. Before three months people waited to hear what

[018-224]she had to say. She kept her appearance so in mind that she made a

[018-225]handsome and a distinguished figure.

[018-226]Elnora never mentioned [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], neither did Mrs. Comstock. Early

[018-227]in December came a note and a big box from him. It contained several

[018-228]books on nature subjects which would be of much help in school work, a

[018-229]number of conveniences Elnora could not afford, and a pair of

[018-230]glass-covered plaster casts, for each large moth she had. In these the

[018-231]upper and underwings of male and female showed. He explained that she

[018-232]would break her specimens easily, carrying them around in boxes. He had

[018-233]seen these and thought they would be of use. Elnora was delighted with

[018-234]them, and at once began the tedious process of softening the mounted

[018-235]moths and fitting them to the casts moulded to receive them. Her time

[018-236]was so taken in school, she progressed slowly, so her mother undertook

[018-237]this work. After trying one or two very common ones she learned to

[018-238]handle the most delicate with ease. She took keen pride in relaxing the

[018-239]tense moths, fitting them to the cases, polishing the glass covers to

[018-240]the last degree and sealing them. The results were beautiful to behold.

[018-241]Soon after Elnora wrote to Philip:

[018-242]DEAR FRIEND:

[018-243]I am writing to thank you for the books, and the box of conveniences

[018-244]sent me for my work. I can use everything with fine results. Hope I am

[018-245]giving good satisfaction in my position. You will be interested to

[018-246]learn that when the summer’s work was classified and pinned, I again

[018-247]had my complete collection for the man of India, save a Yellow Emperor.

[018-248]I have tried everywhere I know, so has the Bird Woman. We cannot find a

[018-249]pair for sale. Fate is against me, at least this season. I shall have

[018-250]to wait until next year and try again.

[018-251]Thank you very much for helping me with my collection and for the books

[018-252]and cases.

[018-253]Sincerely yours,

[018-254]ELNORA COMSTOCK.

[018-255]Philip was disappointed over that note and instead of keeping it he

[018-256]tore it into bits and dropped them into the waste basket.

[018-257]That was precisely what Elnora had intended he should do. Christmas

[018-258]brought beautiful cards of greeting to Mrs. Comstock and Elnora, Easter

[018-259]others, and the year ran rapidly toward spring. Elnora’s position had

[018-260]been intensely absorbing, while she had worked with all her power. She

[018-261]had made a wonderful success and won new friends. Mrs. Comstock had

[018-262]helped in every way she could, so she was very popular also.

[018-263]Throughout the winter they had enjoyed the city thoroughly, and the

[018-264]change of life it afforded, but signs of spring did wonderful things to

[018-265]the hearts of the country-bred women. A restlessness began on bright

[018-266]February days, calmed during March storms and attacked full force in

[018-267]April. When neither could bear it any longer they were forced to

[018-268]discuss the matter and admit they were growing ill with pure

[018-269]homesickness. They decided to keep the city house during the summer,

[018-270]but to return to the farm to live as soon as school closed.

[018-271]So Mrs. Comstock would prepare breakfast and lunch and then slip away

[018-272]to the farm to make up beds in her ploughed garden, plant seeds, trim

[018-273]and tend her flowers, and prepare [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] for occupancy. Then she

[018-274]would go home and make the evening as cheerful as possible for Elnora;

[018-275]in these days she lived only for the girl.

[018-276]Both of them were glad when the last of May came and the schools

[018-277]closed. They packed the books and clothing they wished to take into a

[018-278]wagon and walked across the fields to the old cabin. As they approached

[018-279]it, Mrs. Comstock said to Elnora: “You are sure you won’t be lonely

[018-280]here?”

[018-281]Elnora knew what she really meant.

[018-282]“Quite sure,” she said. “For a time last fall I was glad to be away,

[018-283]but that all wore out with the winter. Spring made me homesick as I

[018-284]could be. I can scarcely wait until we get back again.”

[018-285]So they began that summer as they had begun all others—with work. But

[018-286]both of them took a new joy in everything, and the violin sang by the

[018-287]hour in the twilight.
