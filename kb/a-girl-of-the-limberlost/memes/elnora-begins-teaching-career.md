## Summary

When school begins in the fall, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] rises at six o'clock each morning, walks three miles from the Comstock cabin to [[onabasha|Onabasha]], leads [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] orchestra, and then spends the remainder of the day "travelling from building to building over the city, teaching a specified length of time every week in each room." (018-045) She must have her object lessons ready, maintain her orchestra practice, eat a cold lunch at noon, and walk three miles home at night. (018-048) Her first day of teaching closes with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] "so tired she almost trembled" — but she has begun the career that will define her adult identity. (018-119)

## Context

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s appointment as Natural History Lecturer had been the triumphant culmination of her [[education-as-liberation|high school]] years — the School Board creating a position specifically for her expertise at seven hundred fifty dollars per year. (014-008) But the gap between appointment and execution was substantial. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] had to attend a week-long session of the Teachers' Institute to prepare her talk on [[nature-study|nature work]] in grade schools, select and practice music for the orchestra, and assemble object lessons for every grade level. (018-027)

Meanwhile, her mother [[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]] underwent her own transformation. Recognizing that three miles of daily walking and a cold lunch were unsustainable, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] went to the bank, inquired about her long-neglected account, and discovered — to her astonishment — that twenty years of compound interest had made her financially comfortable. (018-057) She used the money to rent a furnished house three blocks from [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]], surprising [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] with a home in town. (018-128)

## Key Participants

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — The physical demands of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s first teaching week are almost overwhelming: six o'clock rising, three-mile walks, orchestra leadership, multi-building travel, cold lunches, late-night preparation, and a Saturday teachers' session. (018-119) Yet her exhaustion is productive — it leaves no time for the romantic retrospection that would have pained her after [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]'s departure. As the narrator observes, "There was nothing in the new life to remind her of the old." (018-216) Teaching becomes not only a career but a form of healing.

**[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s support of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s teaching career represents the culmination of her character transformation. The woman who once resented her daughter's every success now actively removes obstacles from her path. She rents the town house, furnishes it, manages the household, and even takes over the delicate work of mounting moth specimens in glass-covered plaster casts that Philip sends from Chicago. (018-235) "She took keen pride in relaxing the most delicate with ease" (018-238) — a sentence that applies equally to her moth work and to her mending of the mother-daughter bond.

## Aftermath

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s teaching career transformed her relationship to [[onabasha|Onabasha]]. The girl who once entered [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] in shameful [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], mocked by classmates, now entered every grade school in the city as a respected professional. The position gave her financial independence, intellectual purpose, and a public identity separate from her family's history and her romantic entanglements.

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]]'s move to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] also reshaped the novel's geography. The Comstock women — who had been defined by their isolation at the edge of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] — became town residents, participants in [[community-support|community]] life rather than observers of it. The cabin remained, but it was no longer a prison of grief. Mother and daughter had both emerged into a wider world.

## Significance

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s first teaching day is the novel's most explicit statement about the redemptive power of work. After the emotional devastation of Philip's engagement and departure, teaching provides [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] with something that romantic love alone could not: a self-defined identity, earned through competence and effort. The exhaustion she feels at the end of her first day is not punishment but proof — evidence that she is building something that belongs to her alone.

The event also completes the novel's arc from exclusion to inclusion. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s first day of [[education-as-liberation|high school]] — the opening crisis of the novel — was defined by humiliation, poverty, and social rejection. Her first day of teaching is defined by professional respect, financial security, and [[community-support|community]] belonging. The symmetry is deliberate: the girl who was once the object of the town's mockery has become the teacher the town pays to educate its children. This is the novel's deepest argument about the American promise — not that talent rises effortlessly, but that sustained effort, supported by those who love you, can transform exclusion into belonging.
