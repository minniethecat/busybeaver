# Mrs. Comstock Makes New Clothes for Elnora

## Overview

Mrs. Comstock Makes New Clothes for Elnora is a pivotal character-development event in Chapter VI of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. After years of neglect following her husband's death, Katharine Comstock begins sewing proper, age-appropriate clothing for her daughter Elnora. This act of making "frills" represents the first tangible sign that Mrs. Comstock's long-frozen heart is beginning to thaw.

## Key Participants

- **Katharine Comstock** — Elnora's mother, who has been emotionally distant since her husband's death
- **Elnora Comstock** — The protagonist, who has been wearing ill-fitting, outdated clothes to school
- **Wesley Sinton** — Neighbor who first notices Mrs. Comstock's sewing and recognizes its significance
- **Margaret Sinton** — Wesley's wife, who brings a beautiful blue dress to replace Elnora's worn clothing
- **Ellen Brownlee** — Elnora's friend at school, daughter of the dry goods merchant

## Narrative

Wesley Sinton, driving about his business, stops at the Comstock cabin and finds Mrs. Comstock sitting on the front steps with some sewing. The work appears to be putting a tuck in a petticoat (006-039--042). Sinton, who has been pressing Mrs. Comstock to provide better clothing for Elnora, makes a mental note of this occupation — recognizing it as a significant change in her behavior.

Mrs. Comstock murmurs to herself about showing Margaret Sinton "a frill or two" (006-108--109), indicating a dawning sense of pride and competition regarding her daughter's appearance. The chapter title itself — "Wherein Mrs. Comstock Indulges in 'Frills'" — emphasizes the importance of this transformation.

At school, Ellen Brownlee reports that the other girls have been asking about Elnora's new dresses, though the talk has included unkind remarks about Mrs. Comstock's past neglect (006-296--298). Elnora tells Ellen she is "almost sorry I have these clothes," (006-315) puzzled by the social complexities that come with improved appearance.

Margaret Sinton arrives at the cabin that evening with a beautiful blue dress to replace a borrowed one (006-368--369). When Mrs. Comstock questions whether the dresses need washing every two days, Margaret explains that Elnora "can't keep a dress clean longer than that" because she works in the woods and fields (006-371--377).

When Elnora puts on the blue dress and ribbon, the effect is striking: she "was a picture" (006-382). Mrs. Comstock catches her breath with "a queer stirring around her heart" (006-383) — the first sign of maternal pride awakening after years of emotional numbness.

## Significance

This event marks a turning point in the mother-daughter relationship. Mrs. Comstock's willingness to sew proper clothes represents her first active step toward caring for Elnora's well-being. The "frills" symbolize not just fabric and thread, but the beginning of Mrs. Comstock's re-entry into the world of maternal responsibility from which she withdrew after her husband's drowning.
