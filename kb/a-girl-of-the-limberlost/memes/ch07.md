# CHAPTER VII

[007-001]WHEREIN MRS. COMSTOCK MANIPULATES MARGARET AND BILLY ACQUIRES A

[007-002]RESIDENCE

[007-003]Saturday morning [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] helped her mother with the work. When she had

[007-004]finished [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] told her to go to Sintons’ and wash her Indian

[007-005]relics, so that she would be ready to accompany Wesley to town in the

[007-006]afternoon. Elnora hurried down the road and was soon at the cistern

[007-007]with a tub busily washing arrow points, stone axes, tubes, pipes, and

[007-008]skin-cleaning implements.

[007-009]Then she went home, dressed and was waiting when the carriage reached

[007-010]the gate. She stopped at the bank with the box, and Sinton went to do

[007-011]his marketing and some shopping for his wife.

[007-012]At [[brownlee-store|the dry goods store]] [[mr-brownlee|Mr. Brownlee]] called to him, “Hello, Sinton! How

[007-013]do you like the fate of your [[elnoras-lunch-box|lunch box]]?” Then he began to laugh—

[007-014]“I always hate to see a man laughing alone,” said Sinton. “It looks so

[007-015]selfish! Tell me the fun, and let me help you.”

[007-016]Mr. Brownlee wiped his eyes.

[007-017]“I supposed you knew, but I see she hasn’t told.”

[007-018]Then the three days’ history of the lunch box was repeated with

[007-019]particulars which included the dog.

[007-020]“Now laugh!” concluded Mr. Brownlee.

[007-021]“Blest if I see anything funny!” replied [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]]. “And if you had

[007-022]bought that box and furnished one of those lunches yourself, you

[007-023]wouldn’t either. I call such a work a shame! I’ll have it stopped.”

[007-024]“Some one must see to that, all right. They are little leeches. Their

[007-025]father earns enough to support them, but they have no mother, and they

[007-026]run wild. I suppose they are crazy for cooked food. But it is funny,

[007-027]and when you think it over you will see it, if you don’t now.”

[007-028]“[[About]] where would a body find that father?” inquired Wesley Sinton

[007-029]grimly. Mr. Brownlee told him and he started, locating the house with

[007-030]little difficulty. House was the proper word, for of home there was no

[007-031]sign. Just a small empty house with three unkept little children racing

[007-032]through and around it. The girl and the elder boy hung back, but dirty

[007-033]little [[billy|Billy]] greeted Sinton with: “What you want here?”

[007-034]“I want to see your father,” said Sinton.

[007-035]“Well, he’s asleep,” said Billy.

[007-036]“Where?” asked Sinton.

[007-037]“In the house,” answered Billy, “and you can’t wake him.”

[007-038]“Well, I’ll try,” said Wesley.

[007-039]Billy led the way. “There he is!” he said. “He is drunk again.”

[007-040]On a dirty mattress in a corner lay a man who appeared to be strong and

[007-041]well. Billy was right. You could not awake him. He had gone the limit,

[007-042]and a little beyond.

[007-043]He was now facing eternity. Sinton went out and closed the door.

[007-044]“Your father is sick and needs help,” he said. “You stay here, and I

[007-045]will send a man to see him.”

[007-046]“If you just let him ’lone, he’ll sleep it off,” volunteered Billy.

[007-047]“He’s that way all the time, but he wakes up and gets us something to

[007-048]eat after awhile. Only waitin’ twists you up inside pretty bad.”

[007-049]The boy wore no air of complaint. He was merely stating facts.

[007-050]Wesley Sinton looked intently at Billy. “Are you twisted up inside

[007-051]now?” he asked.

[007-052]Billy laid a grimy hand on the region of his stomach and the filthy

[007-053]little waist sank close to the backbone. “Bet yer life, boss,” he said

[007-054]cheerfully.

[007-055]“How long have you been twisted?” asked Sinton.

[007-056]Billy appealed to the others. “When was it we had the stuff on the

[007-057]bridge?”

[007-058]“Yesterday morning,” said the girl.

[007-059]“Is that all gone?” asked Sinton.

[007-060]“She went and told us to take it home,” said Billy ruefully, “and ’cos

[007-061]she said to, we took it. Pa had come back, he was drinking some more,

[007-062]and he ate a lot of it—almost the whole thing, and it made him sick as

[007-063]a dog, and he went and wasted all of it. Then he got drunk some more,

[007-064]and now he’s asleep again. We didn’t get hardly none.”

[007-065]“You children sit on the steps until the man comes,” said Sinton. “I’ll

[007-066]send you some things to eat with him. What’s your name, sonny?”

[007-067]“Billy,” said the boy.

[007-068]“Well, Billy, I guess you better come with me. I’ll take care of him,”

[007-069]Sinton promised the others. He reached a hand to Billy.

[007-070]“I ain’t no baby, I’m a boy!” said Billy, as he shuffled along beside

[007-071]Sinton, taking a kick at every movable object without regard to his

[007-072]battered toes.

[007-073]Once they passed a Great Dane dog lolling after its master, and Billy

[007-074]ascended Sinton as if he were a tree, and clung to him with trembling

[007-075]hot hands.

[007-076]“I ain’t afraid of that dog,” scoffed Billy, as he was again placed on

[007-077]the walk, “but onc’t he took me for a rat or somepin’ and his teeth cut

[007-078]into my back. If I’d a done right, I’d a took the law on him.”

[007-079]Sinton looked down into the indignant little face. The child was bright

[007-080]enough, he had a good head, but oh, such a body!

[007-081]“I ’bout got enough of dogs,” said Billy. “I used to like ’em, but I’m

[007-082]getting pretty tired. You ought to seen the lickin’ [[jimmy|Jimmy]] and [[belle|Belle]] and

[007-083]me give our dog when we caught him, for taking a little bird she gave

[007-084]us. We waited ’till he was asleep ’nen laid a board on him and all of

[007-085]us jumped on it to onc’t. You could a heard him yell a mile. Belle said

[007-086]mebbe we could squeeze the bird out of him. But, squeeze nothing! He

[007-087]was holler as us, and that bird was lost long ’fore it got to his

[007-088]stummick. It was ist a little one, anyway. Belle said it wouldn’t ’a’

[007-089]made a bite apiece for three of us nohow, and the dog got one good

[007-090]swaller. We didn’t get much of the meat, either. Pa took most of that.

[007-091]Seems like pas and dogs gets everything.”

[007-092]Billy laughed dolefully. Involuntarily Wesley Sinton reached his hand.

[007-093]They were coming into the business part of [[onabasha|Onabasha]] and the streets

[007-094]were crowded. Billy understood it to mean that he might lose his

[007-095]companion and took a grip. That little hot hand clinging tight to his,

[007-096]the sore feet recklessly scouring the walk, the hungry child panting

[007-097]for breath as he tried to keep even, the brave soul jesting in the face

[007-098]of hard luck, caught Sinton in a tender, empty spot.

[007-099]“Say, son,” he said. “How would you like to be washed clean, and have

[007-100]all the supper your skin could hold, and sleep in a good bed?”

[007-101]“Aw, gee!” said Billy. “I ain’t dead yet! Them things is in heaven!

[007-102]Poor folks can’t have them. Pa said so.”

[007-103]“Well, you can have them if you want to go with me and get them,”

[007-104]promised Sinton.

[007-105]“Honest?”

[007-106]“Yes, honest.”

[007-107]“Crost yer heart?”

[007-108]“Yes,” said Sinton.

[007-109]“Kin I take some to Jimmy and Belle?”

[007-110]“If you’ll come with me and be my boy, I’ll see that they have plenty.”

[007-111]“What will pa say?”

[007-112]“Your pa is in that kind of sleep now where he won’t wake up, Billy,”

[007-113]said Sinton. “I am pretty sure the law will give you to me, if you want

[007-114]to come.”

[007-115]“When people don’t ever wake up they’re dead,” announced Billy. “Is my

[007-116]pa dead?”

[007-117]“Yes, he is,” answered Sinton.

[007-118]“And you’ll take care of Jimmy and Belle, too?”

[007-119]“I can’t adopt all three of you,” said Sinton. “I’ll take you, and see

[007-120]that they are well provided for. Will you come?”

[007-121]“Yep, I’ll come,” said Billy. “Let’s eat, first thing we do.”

[007-122]“All right,” agreed Sinton. “Come into this restaurant.” He lifted

[007-123]Billy to the lunch counter and ordered the clerk to give him as many

[007-124]glasses of milk as he wanted, and a biscuit. “I think there’s going to

[007-125]be fried chicken when we get home, Billy,” he said, “so you just take

[007-126]the edge off now, and fill up later.”

[007-127]While Billy lunched Sinton called up the different departments and

[007-128]notified the proper authorities ending with the Women’s Relief

[007-129]Association. He sent a basket of food to Belle and Jimmy, bought Billy

[007-130]a pair of trousers, and a shirt, and went to bring Elnora.

[007-131]“Why, Uncle Wesley!” cried the girl. “Where did you find Billy?”

[007-132]“I’ve adopted him for the time being, if not longer,” replied Wesley

[007-133]Sinton.

[007-134]“Where did you get him?”

[007-135]“Well, young woman,” said Wesley Sinton, “Mr. Brownlee told me the

[007-136]history of your lunch box. It didn’t seem so funny to me as it does to

[007-137]the rest of them; so I went to look up the father of Billy’s family,

[007-138]and make him take care of them, or allow the law to do it for him. It

[007-139]will have to be the law.”

[007-140]“He’s deader than anything!” broke in Billy. “He can’t ever take all

[007-141]the meat any more.”

[007-142]“Billy!” gasped Elnora.

[007-143]“Never you mind!” said Sinton. “A child doesn’t say such things about a

[007-144]father who loved and raised him right. When it happens, the father

[007-145]alone is to blame. You won’t hear Billy talk like that about me when I

[007-146]cross over.”

[007-147]“You don’t mean you are going to take him to keep!”

[007-148]“I’ll soon need help,” said Wesley. “Billy will come in just about

[007-149]right ten years from now, and if I raise him I’ll have him the way I

[007-150]want him.”

[007-151]“But [[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]] doesn’t like boys,” objected Elnora.

[007-152]“Well, she likes me, and I used to be a boy. Anyway, as I remember she

[007-153]has had her way about everything at our house ever since we were

[007-154]married. I am going to please myself about Billy. Hasn’t she always

[007-155]done just as she chose so far as you know? Honest, Elnora!”

[007-156]“Honest!” replied Elnora. “You are beautiful to all of us, Uncle

[007-157]Wesley; but Aunt Margaret won’t like Billy. She won’t want him in her

[007-158]home.”

[007-159]“In our home,” corrected Wesley.

[007-160]“What makes you want him?” marvelled Elnora.

[007-161]“God only knows,” said Sinton. “Billy ain’t so beautiful, and he ain’t

[007-162]so smart, I guess it’s because he’s so human. My heart goes out to

[007-163]him.”

[007-164]“So did mine,” said Elnora. “I love him. I’d rather see him eat my

[007-165]lunch than have it myself any time.”

[007-166]“What makes you like him?” asked Wesley.

[007-167]“Why, I don’t know,” pondered Elnora. “He’s so little, he needs so

[007-168]much, he’s got such splendid grit, and he’s perfectly unselfish with

[007-169]his brother and sister. But we must wash him before Aunt Margaret sees

[007-170]him. I wonder if mother——”

[007-171]“You needn’t bother. I’m going to take him home the way he is,” said

[007-172]Sinton. “I want Maggie to see the worst of it.”

[007-173]“I’m afraid——” began Elnora.

[007-174]“So am I,” said Wesley, “but I won’t give him up. He’s taken a sort of

[007-175]grip on my heart. I’ve always been crazy for a boy. Don’t let him hear

[007-176]us.”

[007-177]“Don’t let him be killed!” cried Elnora. During their talk Billy had

[007-178]wandered to the edge of the walk and barely escaped the wheels of a

[007-179]passing automobile in an effort to catch a stray kitten that seemed in

[007-180]danger.

[007-181]Wesley drew Billy back to the walk, and held his hand closely. “Are you

[007-182]ready, Elnora?”

[007-183]“Yes; you were gone a long time,” she said.

[007-184]Wesley glanced at a package she carried. “Have to have another book?”

[007-185]he asked.

[007-186]“No, I bought this for mother. I’ve had such splendid luck selling my

[007-187]specimens, I didn’t feel right about keeping all the money for myself,

[007-188]so I saved enough from the Indian relics to get a few things I wanted.

[007-189]I would have liked to have gotten her a dress, but I didn’t dare, so I

[007-190]compromised on a book.”

[007-191]“What did you select, Elnora?” asked Wesley wonderingly.

[007-192]“Well,” said she, “I have noticed mother always seemed interested in

[007-193]anything Mark Twain wrote in the newspapers, and I thought it would

[007-194]cheer her up a little, so I just got his ‘Innocents Abroad.’ I haven’t

[007-195]read it myself, but I’ve seen mention made of it all my life, and the

[007-196]critics say it’s genuine fun.”

[007-197]“Good!” cried Sinton. “Good! You’ve made a splendid choice. It will

[007-198]take her mind off herself a lot. But she will scold you.”

[007-199]“Of course,” assented Elnora. “But, possibly she will read it, and feel

[007-200]better. I’m going to serve her a trick. I am going to hide it until

[007-201]Monday, and set it on her little shelf of books the last thing before I

[007-202]go away. She must have all of them by heart. When she sees a new one

[007-203]she can’t help being glad, for she loves to read, and if she has all

[007-204]day to become interested, maybe she’ll like it so she won’t scold so

[007-205]much.”

[007-206]“We are both in for it, but I guess we are prepared. I don’t know what

[007-207]Margaret will say, but I’m going to take Billy home and see. Maybe he

[007-208]can win with her, as he did with us.”

[007-209]Elnora had doubts, but she did not say anything more. When they started

[007-210]home Billy sat on the front seat. He drove with the hitching strap tied

[007-211]to the railing of the dash-board, flourished the whip, and yelled with

[007-212]delight. At first Sinton laughed with him, but by the time he left

[007-213]Elnora with several packages at her gate, he was looking serious

[007-214]enough.

[007-215]Margaret was at the door as they drove up the lane. Wesley left Billy

[007-216]in the carriage, hitched the horses and went to explain to her. He had

[007-217]not reached her before she cried, “Look, Wesley, that child! You’ll

[007-218]have a runaway!”

[007-219]Wesley looked and ran. Billy was standing in the carriage slashing the

[007-220]mettlesome horses with the whip.

[007-221]“See me make ’em go!” he shouted as the whip fell a second time.

[007-222]He did make them go. They took the hitching post and a few fence

[007-223]palings, which scraped the paint from a wheel. Sinton missed the lines

[007-224]at the first effort, but the dragging post impeded the horses, and he

[007-225]soon caught them. He led them to [[sinton-barn|the barn]], and ordered Billy to remain

[007-226]in the carriage while he unhitched. Then leading Billy and carrying his

[007-227]packages he entered the yard.

[007-228]“You run play a few minutes, Billy,” he said. “I want to talk to the

[007-229]nice lady.”

[007-230]The nice lady was looking rather stupefied as Wesley approached her.

[007-231]“Where in the name of sense did you get that awful child?” she

[007-232]demanded.

[007-233]“He is a young gentleman who has been stopping Elnora and eating her

[007-234]lunch every day, part of the time with the assistance of his brother

[007-235]and sister, while our girl went hungry. Brownlee told me about it at

[007-236]the store. It’s happened three days running. The first time she went

[007-237]without anything, the second time Brownlee’s girl took her to lunch,

[007-238]and the third a crowd of [[education-as-liberation|high school]] girls bought a lot of stuff and

[007-239]met them at [[culvert-bridge|the bridge]]. The youngsters seemed to think they could rob

[007-240]her every day, so I went to see their father about having it stopped.”

[007-241]“Well, I should think so!” cried Margaret.

[007-242]“There were three of them, Margaret,” said Wesley, “that little

[007-243]fellow——”

[007-244]“Hyena, you mean,” interpolated Margaret.

[007-245]“Hyena,” corrected Wesley gravely, “and another boy and a girl, all

[007-246]equally dirty and hungry. The man was dead. They thought he was in a

[007-247]drunken sleep, but he was stone dead. I brought the little boy with me,

[007-248]and sent the officers and other help to the house. He’s half starved. I

[007-249]want to wash him, and put clean clothes on him, and give him some

[007-250]supper.”

[007-251]“Have you got anything to put on him?”

[007-252]“Yes.”

[007-253]“Where did you get it?”

[007-254]“Bought it. It ain’t much. All I got didn’t cost a dollar.”

[007-255]“A dollar is a good deal when you work and save for it the way we do.”

[007-256]“Well, I don’t know a better place to put it. Have you got any hot

[007-257]water? I’ll use this tub at the cistern. Please give me some soap and

[007-259]Instead Margaret pushed by him with a shriek. Billy had played by

[007-270]Billy threw himself on the grass and began to scream.

[007-271]“You said I could have fried chicken for supper,” he wailed. “You said

[007-278]“Oh, they might be from bruises made by fists or boot toes, or they

[007-281]“No, I won’t!” said Margaret.

[007-321]“I want to see me!” he demanded.

[007-328]“You are going to keep him for supper?” she asked

[007-329]“Sure!” said Wesley. “That’s what I brought him for. It’s likely he

[007-330]never had a good square meal of decent food in his life. He’s starved

[007-346]down his cheeks. “If I’d a knowed you’d have to get down in a hole, and
