# CHAPTER XX

[020-001]WHEREIN THE ELDER AMMON OFFERS ADVICE, AND EDITH CARR EXPERIENCES

[020-002]REGRETS

[020-003][[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] walked from among his friends a humiliated and a wounded

[020-004]man. Never before had [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] appeared quite so beautiful. All

[020-005]evening she had treated him with unusual consideration. Never had he

[020-006]loved her so deeply. Then in a few seconds everything was different.

[020-007]Seeing the change in her face, and hearing her meaningless accusations,

[020-008]killed something in his heart. Warmth went out and a cold weight took

[020-009]its place. But even after that, he had offered the ring to her again,

[020-010]and asked her before others to reconsider. The answer had been further

[020-011]insult.

[020-012]He walked, paying no heed to where he went. He had traversed many miles

[020-013]when he became aware that his feet had chosen familiar streets. He was

[020-014]passing his home. Dawn was near, but the first floor was lighted. He

[020-015]staggered up the steps and was instantly admitted. The library door

[020-016]stood open, while his father sat with a book pretending to read. At

[020-017]Philip’s entrance the father scarcely glanced up.

[020-018]“Come on!” he called. “I have just told Banks to bring me a cup of

[020-019]coffee before I turn in. Have one with me!”

[020-020]Philip sat beside the table and leaned his head on his hands, but he

[020-021]drank a cup of steaming coffee and felt better.

[020-022]“Father,” he said, “father, may I talk with you a little while?”

[020-023]“Of course,” answered Mr. Ammon. “I am not at all tired. I think I must

[020-024]have been waiting in the hope that you would come. I want no one’s

[020-025]version of this but yours. Tell me the straight of the thing, Phil.”

[020-026]Philip told all he knew, while his father sat in deep thought.

[020-027]“On my life I can’t see any occasion for such a display of temper,

[020-028]Phil. It passed all bounds of reason and breeding. Can’t you think of

[020-029]anything more?”

[020-030]“I cannot!”

[020-031]“[[polly-ammon|Polly]] says every one expected you to carry the moth you caught to

[020-032]Edith. Why didn’t you?”

[020-033]“She screams if a thing of that kind comes near her. She never has

[020-034]taken the slightest interest in them. I was in a big hurry. I didn’t

[020-035]want to miss one minute of my dance with her. The moth was not so

[020-036]uncommon, but by a combination of bad luck it had become the rarest in

[020-037]America for a friend of mine, who is making a collection to pay college

[020-038]expenses. For an instant last June the series was completed; when a

[020-039]woman’s uncontrolled temper ruined this specimen and the search for it

[020-040]began over. A few days later a pair was secured, and again the money

[020-041]was in sight for several hours. Then an accident wrecked one-fourth of

[020-042]the collection. I helped replace those last June, all but this Yellow

[020-043]Emperor which we could not secure, and we haven’t been able to find,

[020-044]buy or trade for one since. So my friend was compelled to teach this

[020-045]past winter instead of [[elnoras-college-dream|going to college]]. When that moth came flying in

[020-046]there to-night, it seemed to me like fate. All I thought of was, that

[020-047]to secure it would complete the collection and secure the money. So I

[020-048]caught the Emperor and started it to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. I declare to you that I

[020-049]was not out of the pavilion over three minutes at a liberal estimate.

[020-050]If I only had thought to speak to the orchestra! I was sure I would be

[020-051]back before enough couples gathered and formed for the dance.”

[020-052]The eyes of the father were very bright.

[020-053]“The friend for whom you wanted the moth is a girl?” he asked

[020-054]indifferently, as he ran the book leaves through his fingers.

[020-055]“The girl of whom I wrote you last summer, and told you about in the

[020-056]fall. I helped her all the time I was away.”

[020-057]“Did Edith know of her?”

[020-058]“I tried many times to tell her, to interest her, but she was so

[020-059]indifferent that it was insulting. She would not hear me.”

[020-060]“We are neither one in any condition to sleep. Why don’t you begin at

[020-061]the first and tell me about this girl? To think of other matters for a

[020-062]time may clear our vision for a sane solution of this. Who is she, just

[020-063]what is she doing, and what is she like? You know I was reared among

[020-064]those Limberlost people, I can understand readily. What is her name and

[020-065]where does she live?”

[020-066]Philip gave a man’s version of the previous summer, while his father

[020-067]played with the book industriously.

[020-068]“You are very sure as to her refinement and [[education-as-liberation|education]]?”

[020-069]“In almost two months’ daily association, could a man be mistaken? She

[020-070]can far and away surpass Polly, Edith, or any girl of our set on any

[020-071]common, [[onabasha-high-school|high school]], or supplementary branch, and you know high schools

[020-072]have French, German, and physics now. Besides, she is a graduate of two

[020-073]other institutions. All her life she has been in the school of Hard

[020-074]Knocks. She has the biggest, tenderest, most human heart I ever knew in

[020-075]a girl. She has known life in its most cruel phases, and instead of

[020-076]hardening her, it has set her trying to save other people suffering.

[020-077]Then this nature position of which I told you; she graduated in the

[020-078]School of the Woods, before she secured that. [[bird-woman|The Bird Woman]], whose

[020-079]work you know, helped her there. Elnora knows more interesting things

[020-080]in a minute than any other girl I ever met knew in an hour, provided

[020-081]you are a person who cares to understand plant and animal life.”

[020-082]The book leaves slid rapidly through his fingers as the father drawled:

[020-083]“What sort of looking girl is she?”

[020-084]“Tall as Edith, a little heavier, pink, even complexion, wide open

[020-085]blue-gray eyes with heavy black brows, and lashes so long they touch

[020-086]her cheeks. She has a rope of waving, shining hair that makes a real

[020-087]crown on her head, and it appears almost red in the light. She is as

[020-088]handsome as any fair woman I ever saw, but she doesn’t know it. Every

[020-089]time any one pays her a compliment, her mother, who is a caution,

[020-090]discovers that, for some reason, the girl is a fright, so she has no

[020-091]appreciation of her looks.”

[020-092]“And you were in daily association two months with a girl like that!

[020-093]How about it, Phil?”

[020-094]“If you mean, did I trifle with her, no!” cried Philip hotly. “I told

[020-095]her the second time I met her all about Edith. Almost every day I wrote

[020-096]to Edith in her presence. Elnora gathered violets and made a fancy

[020-097]basket to put them in for Edith’s birthday. I started to err in too

[020-098]open admiration for Elnora, but her mother brought me up with a whirl I

[020-099]never forgot. Fifty times a day in the swamps and forests Elnora made a

[020-100]perfect picture, but I neither looked nor said anything. I never met

[020-101]any girl so downright noble in bearing and actions. I never hated

[020-102]anything as I hated leaving her, for we were dear friends, like two

[020-103]wholly congenial men. Her mother was almost always with us. She knew

[020-104]how much I admired Elnora, but so long as I concealed it from the girl,

[020-105]the mother did not care.”

[020-106]“Yet you left such a girl and came back whole-hearted to Edith Carr!”

[020-107]“Surely! You know how it has been with me about Edith all my life.”

[020-108]“Yet the girl you picture is far her superior to an unprejudiced

[020-109]person, when thinking what a man would require in a wife to be happy.”

[020-110]“I never have thought what I would ‘require’ to be happy! I only

[020-111]thought whether I could make Edith happy. I have been an idiot! What

[020-112]I’ve borne you’ll never know! To-night is only one of many outbursts

[020-113]like that, in varying and lesser degrees.”

[020-114]“Phil, I love you, when you say you have thought only of Edith! I

[020-115]happen to know that it is true. You are my only son, and I have had a

[020-116]right to watch you closely. I believe you utterly. Any one who cares

[020-117]for you as I do, and has had my years of experience in this world over

[020-118]yours, knows that in some ways, to-night would be a blessed release, if

[020-119]you could take it; but you cannot! Go to bed now, and rest. To-morrow,

[020-120]go back to her and fix it up.”

[020-121]“You heard what I said when I left her! I said it because something in

[020-122]my heart died a minute before that, and I realized that it was my love

[020-123]for Edith Carr. Never again will I voluntarily face such a scene. If

[020-124]she can act like that at a ball, before hundreds, over a thing of which

[020-125]I thought nothing at all, she would go into actual physical fits and

[020-126]spasms, over some of the household crises I’ve seen the mater meet with

[020-127]a smile. Sir, it is truth that I have thought only of her up to the

[020-128]present. Now, I will admit I am thinking about myself. Father, did you

[020-129]see her? Life is too short, and it can be too sweet, to throw it away

[020-130]in a battle with an unrestrained woman. I am no fighter—where a girl is

[020-131]concerned, anyway. I respect and love her or I do nothing. Never again

[020-132]is either respect or love possible between me and Edith Carr. Whenever

[020-133]I think of her in the future, I will see her as she was to-night. But I

[020-134]can’t face the crowd just yet. Could you spare me a few days?”

[020-135]“It is only ten days until you were to go north for the summer, go

[020-136]now.”

[020-137]“I don’t want to go north. I don’t want to meet people I know. There,

[020-138]the story would precede me. I do not need pitying glances or rough

[020-139]condolences. I wonder if I could not hide at Uncle Ed’s in Wisconsin

[020-140]for awhile?”

[020-141]The book closed suddenly. The father leaned across the table and looked

[020-142]into the son’s eyes.

[020-143]“Phil, are you sure of what you just have said?”

[020-144]“Perfectly sure!”

[020-145]“Do you think you are in any condition to decide to-night?”

[020-146]“Death cannot return to life, father. My love for Edith Carr is dead. I

[020-147]hope never to see her again.”

[020-148]“If I thought you could be certain so soon! But, come to think of it,

[020-149]you are very like me in many ways. I am with you in this. Public scenes

[020-150]and disgraces I would not endure. It would be over with me, were I in

[020-151]your position, that I know.”

[020-152]“It is done for all time,” said Philip Ammon. “Let us not speak of it

[020-153]further.”

[020-154]“Then, Phil,” the father leaned closer and looked at the son tenderly,

[020-155]“Phil, why don’t you go to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]?”

[020-156]“Father!”

[020-157]“Why not? No one can comfort a hurt heart like a tender woman; and,

[020-158]Phil, have you ever stopped to think that you may have a duty in the

[020-159]Limberlost, if you are free? I don’t know! I only suggest it. But, for

[020-160]a country schoolgirl, unaccustomed to men, two months with a man like

[020-161]you might well awaken feelings of which you do not think. Because you

[020-162]were safe-guarded is no sign the girl was. She might care to see you.

[020-163]You can soon tell. With you, she comes next to Edith, and you have made

[020-164]it clear to me that you appreciate her in many ways above. So I repeat

[020-165]it, why not go to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]?”

[020-166]A long time Philip Ammon sat in deep thought. At last he raised his

[020-167]head.

[020-168]“Well, why not!” he said. “Years could make me no surer than I am now,

[020-169]and life is short. Please ask Banks to get me some coffee and toast,

[020-170]and I will bathe and dress so I can take the early train.”

[020-171]“Go to your bath. I will attend to your packing and everything. And

[020-172]Phil, if I were you, I would leave no addresses.”

[020-173]“Not an address!” said Philip. “Not even Polly.”

[020-174]When the train pulled out, the elder Ammon went home to find Hart

[020-175][[hart-henderson|Henderson]] waiting.

[020-176]“Where is Phil?” he demanded.

[020-177]“He did not feel like facing his friends at present, and I am just back

[020-178]from driving him to [[onabasha-station|the station]]. He said he might go to Siam, or

[020-179]Patagonia. He would leave no address.”

[020-180]Henderson almost staggered. “He’s not gone? And left no address? You

[020-181]don’t mean it! He’ll never forgive her!”

[020-182]“Never is a long time, Hart,” said Mr. Ammon. “And it seems even longer

[020-183]to those of us who are well acquainted with Phil. Last night was not

[020-184]the last straw. It was the whole straw-stack. It crushed Phil so far as

[020-185]she is concerned. He will not see her again voluntarily, and he will

[020-186]not forget if he does. You can take it from him, and from me, we have

[020-187]accepted the lady’s decision. Will you have a cup of coffee?”

[020-188]Twice Henderson opened his lips to speak of Edith Carr’s despair. Twice

[020-189]he looked into the stern, inflexible face of Mr. Ammon and could not

[020-190]betray her. He held out the ring.

[020-191]“I have no instructions as to that,” said the elder Ammon, drawing

[020-192]back. “Possibly Miss Carr would have it as a keepsake.”

[020-193]“I am sure not,” said Henderson curtly.

[020-194]“Then suppose you return it to Peacock. I will phone him. He will give

[020-195]you the price of it, and you might add it to the children’s Fresh Air

[020-196]Fund. We would be obliged if you would do that. No one here cares to

[020-197]handle the object.”

[020-198]“As you choose,” said Henderson. “Good morning!”

[020-199]Then he went to his home, but he could not think of sleep. He ordered

[020-200]breakfast, but he could not eat. He paced [[onabasha-library|the library]] for a time, but

[020-201]it was too small. Going on the streets he walked until exhausted, then

[020-202]he called a hansom and was driven to his club. He had thought himself

[020-203]familiar with every depth of suffering; that night had taught him that

[020-204]what he felt for himself was not to be compared with the anguish which

[020-205]wrung his heart over the agony of Edith Carr. He tried to blame Philip

[020-206]Ammon, but being an honest man, Henderson knew that was unjust. The

[020-207]fault lay wholly with her, but that only made it harder for him, as he

[020-208]realized it would in time for her.

[020-209]As he sauntered into the room an attendant hurried to him.

[020-210]“You are wanted most urgently at the ’phone, Mr. Henderson,” he said.

[020-211]“You have had three calls from Main 5770.”

[020-212]Henderson shivered as he picked down the receiver and gave the call.

[020-213]“Is that you, Hart?” came Edith’s voice.

[020-214]“Yes.”

[020-215]“Did you find Phil?”

[020-216]“No.”

[020-217]“Did you try?”

[020-218]“Yes. As soon as I left you I went straight there.”

[020-219]“Wasn’t he home yet?”

[020-220]“He has been home and gone again.”

[020-221]“Gone!”

[020-222]The cry tore Henderson’s heart.

[020-223]“Shall I come and tell you, Edith?”

[020-224]“No! Tell me now.”

[020-225]“When I reached the house Banks said Mr. Ammon and Phil were out in the

[020-226]motor, so I waited. Mr. Ammon came back soon. Edith, are you alone?”

[020-227]“Yes. Go on!”

[020-228]“Call your maid. I can’t tell you until some one is with you.”

[020-229]“Tell me instantly!”

[020-230]“Edith, he said he had been to the station. He said Phil had started to

[020-231]Siam or Patagonia, he didn’t know which, and left no address. He

[020-232]said——”

[020-233]Distinctly Henderson heard her fall. He set the buzzer ringing, and in

[020-234]a few seconds heard voices, so he knew she had been found. Then he

[020-235]crept into a private den and shook with a hard, nervous chill.

[020-236]The next day Edith Carr started on her trip to Europe. Henderson felt

[020-237]certain she hoped to meet Philip there. He was sure she would be

[020-238]disappointed, though he had no idea where Ammon could have gone. But

[020-239]after much thought he decided he would see Edith soonest by remaining

[020-240]at home, so he spent the summer in Chicago.
