# Hart Henderson Guards Edith Carr in Mackinac

## Overview

Hart Henderson Guards Edith Carr in Mackinac is a character-defining event in Chapter XXIV of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. Hart Henderson, who has long loved Edith Carr from a distance, follows her to Mackinac Island after the disastrous engagement ball. Over the course of a long day and night, he protects her from public scrutiny, confronts her about her past actions, and provides the steady emotional support she needs as she begins to reckon with the consequences of her behavior toward Philip Ammon and Elnora Comstock.

## Key Participants

- **Hart Henderson** — Philip Ammon's friend, who has silently loved Edith Carr for years
- **Edith Carr** — Philip's former fiancée, in Mackinac trying to come to terms with losing him
- **Terence O'More** — (offstage) Freckles, whose presence on the island adds pressure to Edith's situation

## Narrative

Edith Carr arrives in Mackinac, walking the picturesque main street in a clinging black gown and wide-brimmed hat. She is "the most distinguished figure of the old street" (024-010), but a close observer would note her unnaturally bright eyes and weary expression (024-016).

Hart Henderson is the first man off the boat, coming to her instantly (024-025--026). He brings devastating news: Philip Ammon is in the Lake Shore Hospital in Chicago, critically ill with typhoid fever and asking for Elnora Comstock (024-060--076). The doctors say "it will take careful nursing to save him" (024-072).

On the boat, Henderson confronts Edith directly: "Edith, what did you say to Miss Comstock that made her run away?" (024-092). After initial resistance, Edith breaks down. Taking both her hands, Henderson asks again, and she admits the truth: "I killed his love for me!" (024-120). She collapses, exhausted, and Henderson holds her, whispering comfort.

The confrontation proves cathartic. Edith asks Henderson to help her — and she proposes something unprecedented for her: to send a truthful telegram. She dictates a message to Philip: "Miss Comstock is with the Terence O'More's, on Mackinac Island. She is well and happy." She insists on signing it "Edith Carr" (024-270--272).

Through the long day and into the night, Henderson stays by her side. They spend hours on his boat, running "through shady coves" and "away from noisy parties" (024-286--289). He cooks for her, covers her with wraps against the chill, and ensures she eats and drinks.

At the end of the day, Edith asks about their future. Henderson, ever steady, tells her: "I want you where you will be loved, and good care taken of you" (024-312). Edith promises: "If peace ever comes and I want you, I won't wait to tell you" (024-332). They part with a handshake — Henderson having proven his devotion not through grand gestures, but through quiet, unwavering presence.

## Significance

Hart Henderson's steadfast guardianship of Edith Carr represents one of the novel's most poignant portraits of selfless love. Unlike Philip's passionate engagement or the romantic ideal of courtship, Henderson's love is characterized by patience, honesty, and service. His willingness to confront Edith with hard truths — and then stay to help her face the consequences — marks him as one of the novel's most morally grounded characters.
