# Social Class and Appearance

## Definition

Social class and appearance in *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]* refers to the ways in which clothing, material possessions, and social standing shape identity, opportunity, and interpersonal relationships. Set in early 20th-century rural Indiana, the novel examines the stark divide between the impoverished Comstock household and the prosperous townspeople of [[onabasha|Onabasha]]. External appearance — particularly dress — serves as a visible marker of class that determines how characters are perceived and treated. (001-108)

The novel critiques a society in which a young woman's worth is judged by her clothing rather than her character, while simultaneously affirming the dignity of honest labor and self-improvement. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s journey from "the girl from [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]" in her "scanty dress" to a respected, well-dressed scholar embodies this tension. (001-096)(005-262)

## Role in the Story

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s first day at [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]] is a devastating confrontation with class prejudice. Arriving in a faded calico dress and heavy lumbering shoes — the only clothes her mother would provide — she is immediately marked as an outsider. The other girls, in their bright, fashionable dresses, stare and whisper. (001-108)(003-006) This experience shapes [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s understanding of social hierarchy and her determination to earn her own way.

Clothing becomes a recurring motif of transformation. [[margaret-sinton|Margaret Sinton]] and Wesley intervene to provide [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] with proper school dresses, recognizing that appearance is a prerequisite for social acceptance. (002-025)(002-045) The making of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s new dresses — a [[community-support|community]] effort involving the Sintons and eventually [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] herself — symbolizes the bridging of the class divide through collective care. (004-026)(004-128)

The contrast between [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] is the novel's most pointed class commentary. Edith, beautiful and expensively dressed, represents inherited wealth and social entitlement. Yet her fine clothing masks a selfish character, while [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s humble origins produce genuine virtue. (005-281) By the novel's end, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s worth is recognized not because her clothing changed, but because her character proved superior — though the novel acknowledges that the clothing change was a necessary first step.

## Themes

### Clothing as Social Currency

In the world of the novel, clothing is not merely functional — it is a form of social currency that buys acceptance, respect, and opportunity. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s "skimpy dress" and "coarse shoes" mark her as poor and therefore unworthy. (003-255) When she later appears in well-made, appropriate clothing, the same peers who mocked her now accept her. The novel exposes this superficiality while acknowledging its social reality.

### The Dignity of Labor

Despite the class barriers she faces, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] never seeks charity. She insists on paying her own way — through moth collecting, farm work, and eventually through her academic achievements. (001-325)(003-018) The novel argues that work is more dignifying than inherited wealth, and that [[work-and-self-reliance|self-reliance]] is a higher virtue than social status.

### Inner Worth vs. Outer Appearance

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s teacher recognizes early that she "could do good work" regardless of her clothing. (001-194) The novel consistently separates inner worth from outer appearance: the best-dressed girls are not the best students, and the poorest girl proves the most virtuous. Yet it also realistically portrays a world where outer appearance must be managed before inner worth can be recognized.
