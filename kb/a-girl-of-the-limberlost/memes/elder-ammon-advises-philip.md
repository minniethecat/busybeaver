# Dr. Ammon Advises Philip After the Ball

## Overview

Dr. Ammon Advises Philip After the Ball is a pivotal turning-point event in Chapter XX of *A Girl of the Limberlost*. Following the disastrous engagement ball where Edith Carr publicly humiliated him, Philip Ammon returns home in the early morning hours. His father, Dr. Ammon, provides the steady counsel that helps Philip process the rupture of his engagement — and makes the fateful suggestion that Philip go to the Limberlost, setting the entire second half of the novel in motion.

## Key Participants

- **Philip Ammon** — Dr. Ammon's son, reeling from Edith Carr's public rejection at the engagement ball
- **Dr. Ammon** — Philip's wise father, who listens without judgment and gently redirects his son
- **Hart Henderson** — Philip's friend, who comes seeking Philip on Edith's behalf
- **Edith Carr** — Philip's former fiancée (offstage), whose collapse upon hearing of Philip's departure underscores the finality of the break

## Narrative

Philip walks aimlessly through the Chicago streets after the ball, a "humiliated and wounded man" (020-003). He eventually finds himself at his family home, where his father has waited up for him. Dr. Ammon, pretending to read, invites Philip to have coffee and talk (020-016--024).

Philip recounts the night's events. Dr. Ammon asks why Philip did not bring the Yellow Emperor moth to Edith, and Philip explains that Edith "screams if a thing of that kind comes near her" (020-033--034). He tells his father the full story of Elnora Comstock — the girl in the Limberlost who needs the moth to complete her collection and pay for college (020-045--047).

Dr. Ammon presses for details about Elnora. Philip describes her with striking warmth: "She has the biggest, tenderest, most human heart I ever knew in a girl" (020-074--076). He insists he never trifled with her: "I told her the second time I met her all about Edith" (020-094--095).

Dr. Ammon gently probes: "Yet you left such a girl and came back whole-hearted to Edith Carr!" Philip admits he never thought about what he would "require" to be happy — only whether he could make Edith happy (020-110--111).

The crucial moment comes when Philip declares: "My love for Edith Carr is dead. I hope never to see her again" (020-146--147). His father, convinced of his son's certainty, leans close and asks: "Phil, why don't you go to the Limberlost?" (020-155).

The father suggests that Philip may have a duty there, and that Elnora — "a country schoolgirl, unaccustomed to men" — might well have developed feelings for him during their months together (020-160--164). Philip sits in deep thought, then rises: "Well, why not! Years could make me no surer than I am now, and life is short" (020-168--169).

Philip leaves on the early train for the Limberlost, leaving no address. When Hart Henderson arrives seeking him on Edith's behalf, Dr. Ammon tells him plainly: "Last night was not the last straw. It was the whole straw-stack" (020-183--184). Edith, learning of Philip's departure, collapses (020-233). She leaves for Europe the next day.

## Significance

Dr. Ammon's advice is the narrative hinge between the Chicago world (Edith Carr, society, engagement ball) and the Limberlost world (Elnora, nature, authenticity). His suggestion that Philip return to the Limberlost sets in motion the novel's resolution. The father-son scene also models healthy masculine mentorship: Dr. Ammon listens without judgment, asks thoughtful questions, and only then offers guidance.
