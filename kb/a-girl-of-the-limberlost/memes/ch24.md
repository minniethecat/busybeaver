# CHAPTER XXIV

[024-001]WHEREIN EDITH CARR WAGES A BATTLE, AND HART HENDERSON STANDS GUARD

[024-002]Many people looked, a few followed, when [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] slowly came down

[024-003]the main street of Mackinac, pausing here and there to note the glow of

[024-004]colour in one small booth after another, overflowing with gay curios.

[024-005]That street of packed white sand, winding with the curves of the shore,

[024-006]outlined with brilliant shops, and thronged with laughing, bare-headed

[024-007]people in outing costumes was a picturesque and fascinating sight.

[024-008]Thousands annually made long journeys and paid exorbitant prices to

[024-009]take part in that pageant.

[024-010]As Edith Carr passed, she was the most distinguished figure of the old

[024-011]street. Her clinging black gown was sufficiently elaborate for a dinner

[024-012]dress. On her head was a large, wide, drooping-brimmed black hat, with

[024-013]immense floating black plumes, while on the brim, and among the laces

[024-014]on her breast glowed velvety, deep red roses. Some way these made up

[024-015]for the lack of colour in her cheeks and lips, and while her eyes

[024-016]seemed unnaturally bright, to a close observer they appeared weary.

[024-017]Despite the effort she made to move lightly she was very tired, and

[024-018]dragged her heavy feet with an effort.

[024-019]She turned at the little street leading to the dock, and went to meet

[024-020]the big lake steamer ploughing up the Straits from Chicago. Past the

[024-021]landing place, on to the very end of the pier she went, then sat down,

[024-022]leaned against a dock support and closed her tired eyes. When the

[024-023]steamer came very close she languidly watched the people lining the

[024-024]railing. Instantly she marked one lean anxious face turned toward hers,

[024-025]and with a throb of pity she lifted a hand and waved to [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]].

[024-026]He was the first man to leave the boat, coming to her instantly. She

[024-027]spread her trailing skirts and motioned him to sit beside her. Silently

[024-028]they looked across the softly lapping water. At last she forced herself

[024-029]to speak to him.

[024-030]“Did you have a successful trip?”

[024-031]“I accomplished my purpose.”

[024-032]“You didn’t lose any time getting back.”

[024-033]“I never do when I am coming to you.”

[024-034]“Do you want to go to the cottage for anything?”

[024-035]“No.”

[024-036]“Then let us sit here and wait until the Petoskey steamer comes in. I

[024-037]like to watch the boats. Sometimes I study the faces, if I am not too

[024-038]tired.”

[024-039]“Have you seen any new types to-day?”

[024-040]She shook her head. “This has not been an easy day, Hart.”

[024-041]“And it’s going to be worse,” said Henderson bitterly. “There’s no use

[024-042]putting it off. Edith, I saw some one to-day.”

[024-043]“You should have seen thousands,” she said lightly.

[024-044]“I did. But of them all, only one will be of interest to you.”

[024-045]“Man or woman?”

[024-046]“Man.”

[024-047]“Where?”

[024-048]“Lake Shore private hospital.”

[024-049]“An accident?”

[024-050]“No. Nervous and physical breakdown.”

[024-051]“Phil said he was going back to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]].”

[024-052]“He went. He was there three weeks, but the strain broke him. He has an

[024-053]old letter in his hands that he has handled until it is ragged. He held

[024-054]it up to me and said: ‘You can see for yourself that she says she will

[024-055]be well and happy, but we can’t know until we see her again, and that

[024-056]may never be. She may have gone too near that place her father went

[024-057]down, some of that Limberlost gang may have found her in the forest,

[024-058]she may lie dead in some city morgue this instant, waiting for me to

[024-059]find her body.’”

[024-060]“Hart! For pity sake stop!”

[024-061]“I can’t,” cried Henderson desperately. “I am forced to tell you. They

[024-062]are fighting brain fever. He did go back to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] and he prowled it

[024-063]night and day. The days down there are hot now, and the nights wet with

[024-064]dew and cold. He paid no attention and forgot his food. A fever started

[024-065]and his uncle brought him home. They’ve never had a word from her, or

[024-066]found a trace of her. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] thought she had gone to O’Mores’ at

[024-067]Great Rapids, so when Phil broke down she telegraphed there. They had

[024-068]been gone all summer, so her mother is as anxious as Phil.”

[024-069]“The O’Mores are here,” said Edith. “I haven’t seen any of them,

[024-070]because I haven’t gone out much in the few days since we came, but this

[024-071]is their summer home.”

[024-072]“Edith, they say at the hospital that it will take careful nursing to

[024-073]save Phil. He is surrounded by stacks of maps and railroad guides. He

[024-074]is trying to frame up a plan to set the entire detective agency of the

[024-075]country to work. He says he will stay there just two days longer. The

[024-076]doctors say he will kill himself when he goes. He is a sick man, Edith.

[024-077]His hands are burning and shaky and his breath was hot against my

[024-078]face.”

[024-079]“Why are you telling me?” It was a cry of acute anguish.

[024-080]“He thinks you know where she is.”

[024-081]“I do not! I haven’t an idea! I never dreamed she would go away when

[024-082]she had him in her hand! I should not have done it!”

[024-083]“He said it was something you said to her that made her go.”

[024-084]“That may be, but it doesn’t prove that I know where she went.”

[024-085]Henderson looked across the water and suffered keenly. At last he

[024-086]turned to Edith and laid a firm, strong hand over hers.

[024-087]“Edith,” he said, “do you realize how serious this is?”

[024-088]“I suppose I do.”

[024-089]“Do you want as fine a fellow as Philip driven any further? If he

[024-090]leaves that hospital now, and goes out to the exposure and anxiety of a

[024-091]search for her, there will be a tragedy that no after regrets can

[024-092]avert. Edith, what did you say to Miss Comstock that made her run away

[024-093]from Phil?”

[024-094]The girl turned her face from him and sat still, but the man gripping

[024-095]her hands and waiting in agony could see that she was shaken by the

[024-096]jolting of the heart in her breast.

[024-097]“Edith, what did you say?”

[024-098]“What difference can it make?”

[024-099]“It might furnish some clue to her action.”

[024-100]“It could not possibly.”

[024-101]“Phil thinks so. He has thought so until his brain is worn enough to

[024-102]give way. Tell me, Edith!”

[024-103]“I told her Phil was mine! That if he were away from her an hour and

[024-104]back in my presence, he would be to me as he always has been.”

[024-105]“Edith, did you believe that?”

[024-106]“I would have staked my life, my soul on it!”

[024-107]“Do you believe it now?”

[024-108]There was no answer. Henderson took her other hand and holding both of

[024-109]them firmly he said softly: “Don’t mind me, dear. I don’t count! I’m

[024-110]just old Hart! You can tell me anything. Do you still believe that?”

[024-111]The beautiful head barely moved in negation. Henderson gathered both

[024-112]her hands in one of his and stretched an arm across her shoulders to

[024-113]the post to support her. She dragged her hands from him and twisted

[024-114]them together.

[024-115]“Oh, Hart!” she cried. “It isn’t fair! There is a limit! I have

[024-116]suffered my share. Can’t you see? Can’t you understand?”

[024-117]“Yes,” he panted. “Yes, my girl! Tell me just this one thing yet, and

[024-118]I’ll cheerfully kill any one who annoys you further. Tell me, Edith!”

[024-119]Then she lifted her big, dull, pain-filled eyes to his and cried: “No!

[024-120]I do not believe it now! I know it is not true! I killed his love for

[024-121]me. It is dead and gone forever. Nothing will revive it! Nothing in all

[024-122]this world. And that is not all. I did not know how to touch the depths

[024-123]of his nature. I never developed in him those things he was made to

[024-124]enjoy. He admired me. He was proud to be with me. He thought, and I

[024-125]thought, that he worshipped me; but I know now that he never did care

[024-126]for me as he cares for her. Never! I can see it! I planned to lead

[024-127]society, to make his home a place sought for my beauty and popularity.

[024-128]She plans to advance his political ambitions, to make him comfortable

[024-129]physically, to stimulate his intellect, to bear him a brood of

[024-130]red-faced children. He likes her and her plans as he never did me and

[024-131]mine. Oh, my soul! Now, are you satisfied?”

[024-132]She dropped back against his arm exhausted. Henderson held her and

[024-133]learned what suffering truly means. He fanned her with his hat, rubbed

[024-134]her cold hands and murmured broken, incoherent things. By and by slow

[024-135]tears slipped from under her closed lids, but when she opened them her

[024-136]eyes were dull and hard.

[024-137]“What a rag one is when the last secret of the soul is torn out and

[024-138]laid bare!” she cried.

[024-139]Henderson thrust his handkerchief into her fingers and whispered,

[024-140]“Edith, the boat has been creeping up. It’s very close. Maybe some of

[024-141]our crowd are on it. Hadn’t we better slip away from here before it

[024-142]lands?”

[024-143]“If I can walk,” she said. “Oh, I am so dead tired, Hart!

[024-144]“Yes, dear,” said Henderson soothingly. “Just try to pass the landing

[024-145]before the boat anchors. If I only dared carry you!”

[024-146]They struggled through the waiting masses, but directly opposite the

[024-147]landing there was a backward movement in the happy, laughing crowd, the

[024-148]gang-plank came down with a slam, and people began hurrying from the

[024-149]boat. Crowded against the fish house on the dock, Henderson could only

[024-150]advance a few steps at a time. He was straining every nerve to protect

[024-151]and assist Edith. He saw no one he recognized near them, so he slipped

[024-152]his arm across her back to help support her. He felt her stiffen

[024-153]against him and catch her breath. At the same instant, the clearest,

[024-154]sweetest male voice he ever had heard called: “Be careful there, little

[024-155]men!”

[024-156]Henderson sent a swift glance toward the boat. Terence O’More had

[024-157]stepped from the gang-plank, leading a little daughter, so like him, it

[024-158]was comical. There followed a picture not easy to describe. The Angel

[024-159]in the full flower of her beauty, richly dressed, a laugh on her cameo

[024-160]face, the setting sun glinting on her gold hair, escorted by her eldest

[024-161]son, who held her hand tightly and carefully watched her steps. Next

[024-162]came [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], dressed with equal richness, a trifle taller and

[024-163]slenderer, almost the same type of colouring, but with different eyes

[024-164]and hair, facial lines and expression. She was led by the second O’More

[024-165]boy who convulsed the crowd by saying: “Tareful, Elnora! Don’t ’oo be

[024-166]’teppin’ in de water!”

[024-167]People surged around them, purposely closing them in.

[024-168]“What lovely women! Who are they? It’s the O’Mores. The lightest one is

[024-169]his wife. Is that her sister? No, it is his! They say he has a title in

[024-170]England.”

[024-171]Whispers ran fast and audible. As the crowd pressed around the party an

[024-172]opening was left beside the fish sheds. Edith ran down the dock.

[024-173]Henderson sprang after her, catching her arm and assisting her to the

[024-174]street.

[024-175]“Up the shore! This way!” she panted. “Every one will go to dinner the

[024-176]first thing they do.”

[024-177]They left the street and started around the beach, but Edith was

[024-178]breathless from running, while the yielding sand made difficult

[024-179]walking.

[024-180]“Help me!” she cried, clinging to Henderson. He put his arm around her,

[024-181]almost carrying her from sight into a little cove walled by high rocks

[024-182]at the back, while there was a clean floor of white sand, and logs

[024-183]washed from the lake for seats. He found one of these with a back rest,

[024-184]and hurrying down to the water he soaked his handkerchief and carried

[024-185]it to her. She passed it across her lips, over her eyes, and then

[024-186]pressed the palms of her hands upon it. Henderson removed the heavy

[024-187]hat, fanned her with his, and wet the handkerchief again.

[024-188]“Hart, what makes you?” she said wearily. “My mother doesn’t care. She

[024-189]says this is good for me. Do you think this is good for me, Hart?”

[024-190]“Edith, you know I would give my life if I could save you this,” he

[024-191]said, and could not speak further.

[024-192]She leaned against him, closed her eyes and lay silent so long the man

[024-193]fell into panic.

[024-194]“Edith, you are not unconscious?” he whispered, touching her.

[024-195]“No, just resting. Please don’t leave me.”

[024-196]He held her carefully, gently fanning her. She was suffering almost

[024-197]more than either of them could endure.

[024-198]“I wish you had your boat,” she said at last. “I want to sail with the

[024-199]wind in my face.”

[024-200]“There is no wind. I can bring my motor around in a few minutes.”

[024-201]“Then get it.”

[024-202]“Lie on the sand. I can ’phone from the first booth. It won’t take but

[024-203]a little while.”

[024-204]Edith lay on the white sand, and Henderson covered her face with her

[024-205]hat. Then he ran to the nearest booth and talked imperatively.

[024-206]Presently he was back bringing a hot drink that was stimulating.

[024-207]Shortly the motor ran close to the beach and stopped. Henderson’s

[024-208]servant brought a row-boat ashore and took them to the launch. It was

[024-209]filled with cushions and wraps. Henderson made a couch and soon, warmly

[024-210]covered, Edith sped out over the water in search of peace.

[024-211]Hour after hour the boat ran up and down the shore. The moon arose and

[024-212]the night air grew very chilly. Henderson put on an overcoat and piled

[024-213]more covers on Edith.

[024-214]“You must take me home,” she said at last. “The folks will be uneasy.”

[024-215]He was compelled to take her to the cottage with the battle still

[024-216]raging. He went back early the next morning, but already she had

[024-217]wandered out over the island. Instinctively Henderson felt that the

[024-218]shore would attract her. There was something in the tumult of rough

[024-219]little Huron’s waves that called to him. It was there he found her,

[024-220]crouching so close the water the foam was dampening her skirts.

[024-221]“May I stay?” he asked.

[024-222]“I have been hoping you would come,” she answered. “It’s bad enough

[024-223]when you are here, but it is a little easier than bearing it alone.”

[024-224]“Thank God for that!” said Henderson sitting beside her. “Shall I talk

[024-225]to you?”

[024-226]She shook her head. So they sat by the hour. At last she spoke: “Of

[024-227]course, you know there is something I have got to do, Hart!”

[024-228]“You have not!” cried Henderson, violently. “That’s all nonsense! Give

[024-229]me just one word of permission. That is all that is required of you.”

[024-230]“‘Required?’ You grant, then, that there is something ‘required?’”

[024-231]“One word. Nothing more.”

[024-232]“Did you ever know one word could be so big, so black, so desperately

[024-233]bitter? Oh, Hart!”

[024-234]“No.”

[024-235]“But you know it now, Hart!”

[024-236]“Yes.”

[024-237]“And still you say that it is ‘required?’”

[024-238]Henderson suffered unspeakably. At last he said: “If you had seen and

[024-239]heard him, Edith, you, too, would feel that it is ‘required.’

[024-240]Remember——”

[024-241]“No! No! No!” she cried. “Don’t ask me to remember even the least of my

[024-242]pride and folly. Let me forget!”

[024-243]She sat silent for a long time.

[024-244]“Will you go with me?” she whispered.

[024-245]“Of course.”

[024-246]At last she arose.

[024-247]“I might as well give up and have it over,” she faltered.

[024-248]That was the first time in her life that Edith Carr ever had proposed

[024-249]to give up anything she wanted.

[024-250]“Help me, Hart!”

[024-251]Henderson started around the beach assisting her all he could. Finally

[024-252]he stopped.

[024-253]“Edith, there is no sense in this! You are too tired to go. You know

[024-254]you can trust me. You wait in any of these lovely places and send me.

[024-255]You will be safe, and I’ll run. One word is all that is necessary.”

[024-256]“But I’ve got to say that word myself, Hart!”

[024-257]“Then write it, and let me carry it. The message is not going to prove

[024-258]who went to the office and sent it.”

[024-259]“That is quite true,” she said, dropping wearily, but she made no

[024-260]movement to take the pen and paper he offered.

[024-261]“Hart, you write it,” she said at last.

[024-262]Henderson turned away his face. He gripped the pen, while his breath

[024-263]sucked between his dry teeth.

[024-264]“Certainly!” he said when he could speak. “Mackinac, August 27, 1908.

[024-265][[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], Lake Shore Hospital, Chicago.” He paused with suspended

[024-266]pen and glanced at Edith. Her white lips were working, but no sound

[024-267]came. “Miss Comstock is with the Terence O’Mores, on Mackinac Island,”

[024-268]prompted Henderson.

[024-269]Edith nodded.

[024-270]“Signed, Henderson,” continued the big man.

[024-271]Edith shook her head.

[024-272]“Say, ‘She is well and happy,’ and sign, Edith Carr!” she panted.

[024-273]“Not on your life!” flashed Henderson.

[024-274]“For the love of mercy, Hart, don’t make this any harder! It is the

[024-275]least I can do, and it takes every ounce of strength in me to do it.”

[024-276]“Will you wait for me here?” he asked.

[024-277]She nodded, and, pulling his hat lower over his eyes, Henderson ran

[024-278]around the shore. In less than an hour he was back. He helped her a

[024-279]little farther to where the Devil’s Kitchen lay cut into the rocks; it

[024-280]furnished places to rest, and cool water. Before long his man came with

[024-281]the boat. From it they spread blankets on the sand for her, and made

[024-282]chafing-dish tea. She tried to refuse it, but the fragrance overcame

[024-283]her for she drank ravenously. Then Henderson cooked several dishes and

[024-284]spread an appetizing lunch. She was young, strong, and almost famished

[024-285]for food. She was forced to eat. That made her feel much better. Then

[024-286]Henderson helped her into the boat and ran it through shady coves of

[024-287]the shore, where there were refreshing breezes. When she fell asleep

[024-288]the girl did not know, but the man did. Sadly in need of rest himself,

[024-289]he ran that boat for five hours through quiet bays, away from noisy

[024-290]parties, and where the shade was cool and deep. When she awoke he took

[024-291]her home, and as they went she knew that she had been mistaken. She

[024-292]would not die. Her heart was not even broken. She had suffered

[024-293]horribly; she would suffer more; but eventually the pain must wear out.

[024-294]Into her head crept a few lines of an old opera:

[024-295]“Hearts do not break, they sting and ache,

[024-296]For old love’s sake, but do not die,

[024-297]As witnesseth the living I.”

[024-298]That evening they were sailing down the Straits before a stiff breeze

[024-299]and Henderson was busy with the tiller when she said to him: “Hart, I

[024-300]want you to do something more for me.”

[024-301]“You have only to tell me,” he said.

[024-302]“Have I only to tell you, Hart?” she asked softly.

[024-303]“Haven’t you learned that yet, Edith?”

[024-304]“I want you to go away.”

[024-305]“Very well,” he said quietly, but his face whitened visibly.

[024-306]“You say that as if you had been expecting it.”

[024-307]“I have. I knew from the beginning that when this was over you would

[024-308]dislike me for having seen you suffer. I have grown my Gethsemane in a

[024-309]full realization of what was coming, but I could not leave you, Edith,

[024-310]so long as it seemed to me that I was serving you. Does it make any

[024-311]difference to you where I go?”

[024-312]“I want you where you will be loved, and good care taken of you.”

[024-313]“Thank you!” said Henderson, smiling grimly. “Have you any idea where

[024-314]such a spot might be found?”

[024-315]“It should be with your sister at Los Angeles. She always has seemed

[024-316]very fond of you.”

[024-317]“That is quite true,” said Henderson, his eyes brightening a little. “I

[024-318]will go to her. When shall I start?”

[024-319]“At once.”

[024-320]Henderson began to tack for the landing, but his hands shook until he

[024-321]scarcely could manage the boat. Edith Carr sat watching him

[024-322]indifferently, but her heart was throbbing painfully. “Why is there so

[024-323]much suffering in the world?” she kept whispering to herself. Inside

[024-324]her door Henderson took her by the shoulders almost roughly.

[024-325]“For how long is this, Edith, and how are you going to say good-bye to

[024-326]me?”

[024-327]She raised tired, pain-filled eyes to his.

[024-328]“I don’t know for how long it is,” she said. “It seems now as if it had

[024-329]been a slow eternity. I wish to my soul that God would be merciful to

[024-330]me and make something ‘snap’ in my heart, as there did in Phil’s, that

[024-331]would give me rest. I don’t know for how long, but I’m perfectly

[024-332]shameless with you, Hart. If peace ever comes and I want you, I won’t

[024-333]wait for you to find it out yourself, I’ll cable, Marconigraph,

[024-334]anything. As for how I say good-bye; any way you please, I don’t care

[024-335]in the least what happens to me.”

[024-336]Henderson studied her intently.

[024-337]“In that case, we will shake hands,” he said. “Good-bye, Edith. Don’t

[024-338]forget that every hour I am thinking of you and hoping all good things

[024-339]will come to you soon.”
