# Catching the Yellow Emperor

## Summary

The catching of the Yellow Emperor is the pivotal event in [[mrs-comstock-transformation|Katharine Comstock's transformation]] from bitter, grief-frozen widow to loving mother. After [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s cruel first day of [[education-as-liberation|high school]], [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] impulsively crushes a rare Yellow Emperor moth that [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] needs to complete a collection worth three hundred dollars — money meant for college. Confronted with the truth of what she has done, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] ventures into [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] at night, aided by [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]-dweller [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], to replace what she destroyed. Her desperate, prayerful vigil beneath the lanterns is rewarded with not one but a pair of Yellow Emperors, and she returns home at dawn a changed woman. (012-050)

## Key Participants

- **[[katharine-comstock|Katharine Comstock]]** — The central figure of this event, undergoing the novel's most dramatic single transformation. She enters [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] as a woman consumed by grief and guilt — the same swamp that took her husband eighteen years before — and emerges at dawn with a pair of Yellow Emperors and a heart unlocked. Her actions — threatening intruders with a revolver, tearing her dress for a moth bag, praying aloud to God for a moth — reveal a capacity for fierce, primal love that she has suppressed since [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]]'s death. (012-064)
- **[[pete-corson|Pete Corson]]** — A rough swamp-dweller who has known [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] since childhood. He emerges from the darkness to help [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]], holding moths while she fashions a bag from her calico skirt, warning her of approaching poachers, and sharing her vigil through the long night. His blunt honesty — "There ain't a chance in a dozen the right one will come" — is tempered by a loyalty that proves more reliable than the respectable society that mocks [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. (012-687)
- **[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]** — Though absent during the night hunt itself, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is the event's emotional center. Her words — "I hate you! You are a selfish, wicked woman!" — deliver the shock that propels [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] into [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]. She waits through the night at [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]], and her stunned, tearful recognition of her mother's transformation at dawn completes the emotional arc. (012-064)

## Context

The event occurs against the backdrop of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]'s long struggle for [[education-as-liberation|education]] and her mother's eighteen-year refusal to love her. Since [[grief-and-loss|Robert Comstock's death]] in the quicksand of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]] had withdrawn from the world, neglecting the farm, refusing comfort, and withholding affection from her daughter. (004-001)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], meanwhile, had built a secret life in [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] — [[moth-collecting|collecting moths]], butterflies, and specimens for the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]], [[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] money for books, tuition, and [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]]. The Yellow Emperor (Saturnia imperialis) was the final specimen she needed to complete a collection that would bring three hundred dollars — enough to begin college. (010-173) She had named it childishly, as one might name a treasure, and had searched for it across four years. (012-050)

## The Moth Destroyed

The morning after a confrontation with her mother, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] is fixing her hair before the kitchen mirror when [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] cries out that there is "a varmint of some kind." She swings open the screen door and strikes:

> Elnora screamed wildly. "Don't! Mother, don't!" Mrs. Comstock struck again. Elnora caught her arm. "It's the one I want! It's worth a lot of money! Don't! Oh, you shall not!" (012-050)

[[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] strikes [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] across the face and crushes the moth beneath her foot. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] tells her it was worth three hundred dollars — the completion of her [[elnoras-college-dream|college fund]] — [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] wavers but refuses to admit fault. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s response is devastating:

> "You never made any pretence of loving me. At last I'll be equally frank with you. I hate you! You are a selfish, wicked woman! I hate you!" (012-064)

She flees to [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] and collapses against a log, sobbing in "dumb, abject misery." (012-064) Her mother, left standing in the kitchen, begins the slow work of recognition: "She never did lie to me. I guess it was a moth. And the only one she needed to get three hundred dollars, she said. I wish I hadn't been so fast!" (012-064)

## The Night Hunt

### Into the Limberlost

That evening, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] sets out with a determination born of guilt and dawning love. She carries Robert's revolver and a fierce resolve. She strings lanterns through [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] to attract night-flying moths — a method she has learned from watching [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], though she would never have admitted it. The Limberlost, the place that took her husband, becomes the arena of her atonement.

### Pete Corson's Aid

[[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], a rough swamp-dweller who has known [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] since childhood, emerges from the darkness. He recognises [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] and immediately understands the situation. When she tells him she trampled the moth [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] needed, he replies with characteristic bluntness: "Then I guess it's your funeral. There ain't a chance in a dozen the right one will come." (012-687)

But he stays. He helps her catch moths, holds them while she fashions a bag from her calico skirt, and warns her when [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]'s less savoury inhabitants — called by her lanterns, mistaken for poachers' signals — approach. When one man tries to interfere, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] raises the revolver:

> "You nasty, sneaking son of Satan!" she cried. "Why are you hiding there? You made me lose the one I wanted most of any I've had a chance at yet. Get out of here! Go this minute, or I'll fill your worthless carcass so full of holes you'll do to sift cornmeal." (011-431)

[[pete-corson|Pete]] "leaned against a tree, held his mouth shut and shook inwardly" — witnessing a woman transformed by desperation into a force of nature. (011-431)

### The Vigil

The night deepens. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]], her dress skirt torn away for a moth bag, her hair damp with swamp mist, her face "a ghastly green" in the lantern light, refuses to leave. She prays — not in words but in action, in persistence, in the refusal to abandon hope:

> "O Heavenly Father!" exulted Mrs. Comstock, "it's yellow! Careful Pete! Your hat, maybe!" (012-687)

The first Emperor eludes [[pete-corson|Pete]]'s hat. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] leans against a tree, covers her face, and cries: "That is my punishment! Oh, Lord, if you will give a moth like that into my possession, I'll always be a better woman!" (012-687)

### The Capture

Then a second Emperor appears in pursuit of the first. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] strips off her remaining apron and holds it before her:

> The big pale yellow creature clung firmly, lowering and raising its wings. The other came nearer. Mrs. Comstock held the cloth with rigid hands, while Pete could hear her breathing in short gusts. "Shall I try now?" he implored. "Wait!" whispered the woman. "Something seems to say wait!" (012-687)

She waits, trusting instincts she did not know she possessed, until both moths settle. Then:

> "They are mine. They are my salvation. God and the Limberlost gave them to me! They won't move for hours. The books all say so. O Heavenly Father, I am thankful to You, and you, too, Pete Corson! You are a good man to help me. Now, I can go home and face my girl." (011-431)

She drops to the ground, spreads the apron across her knees, and weeps — "the tears she had thought forever dried gushed forth, and she sobbed for pure joy." (011-431)

## The Return

At dawn, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] enters [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]] carrying a pair of Yellow Emperors. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], who has waited all night in fear, sees her mother transformed — dress torn, hair wild, eyes red from weeping, but triumphant. In her hands, "stiffly extended before her, on a wad of calico reposed a magnificent pair of Yellow Emperors." (012-687) [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] stares, "her lips parted" in disbelief.

[[pete-corson|Pete]] sets down the bag of other captured moths and vanishes into the morning. No thanks are needed; his part is done.

## Aftermath

The capture of the Yellow Emperors is the hinge on which the novel's emotional plot turns. Before this night, [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] was defined by her refusal to love; after it, she begins the slow, uneven journey toward becoming the mother [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] needs. She has not transformed overnight — she will still be prickly, stubborn, and sometimes cruel — but she has made the decisive movement. She has entered [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] not as the widow who lost her husband there, but as a mother fighting for her daughter's future.

The practical consequences are immediate: the completed collection brings the money [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] needs for college. But the emotional consequences are far greater. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] sees her mother holding the Emperors, she sees something she has never seen before: proof that her mother is capable of love, capable of sacrifice, capable of change.

## Significance

The Yellow Emperor hunt is the novel's most concentrated expression of its central theme: that love is not a feeling but an action, demonstrated through work, risk, and sacrifice. [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]] does not tell [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] she loves her — she spends a night in [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], threatens men with a revolver, and catches moths until dawn. The action speaks louder than any words could.

The scene also establishes [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] as a place of transformation, not merely danger. The swamp that killed [[robert-comstock|Robert Comstock]] becomes, in his widow's desperate vigil, the place where she reclaims her capacity for love. This reversal — the place of death becoming the place of rebirth — is the novel's deepest structural pattern.

Finally, the episode introduces [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]] as an unexpected ally — a man living on the margins of society who proves more loyal and more useful than the respectable citizens who mock [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]. His rough kindness anticipates the novel's broader argument that worth is found in unexpected places, and that [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] — for all its dangers — breeds a kind of integrity the town cannot match.
