# Hart Henderson

## Overview

Hart Henderson is a wealthy young man in [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s social circle, best known for his unwavering devotion to Edith and his ownership of a "big automobile." He appears primarily in the later chapters of the novel as part of the social drama surrounding [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]], and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]]. Despite Edith's dismissive treatment of him, Henderson remains steadfastly at her side — driving her to [[onabasha|Onabasha]], and later being the first to greet [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at the boat dock with genuine warmth. (019-078)

## Character

### The Devoted Admirer

Henderson's defining trait is his persistent loyalty to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], even when she openly prefers [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. At the grand ball in Chapter XIX, Edith dismisses him with a careless nod, choosing instead to take Philip's arm:

> Edith Carr nodded carelessly to Hart Henderson, and moved away. (019-157)

Yet Henderson does not abandon her. When Edith's engagement to Philip unravels, Henderson is the one who forces the ring back into her fingers and implores her to act: "Edith, quick. Come, quick! There's just time to catch him." (019-310)

### The Automobile Owner

Henderson's most distinctive material attribute is his "big automobile," which plays a key role in the plot:

> Honk! Honk! Honk! Hart Henderson set the horn of the big automobile going as it shot from behind the trees lining the Brushwood road. (022-108)

His car enables Edith to travel to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] in pursuit of Philip, setting the stage for the climactic confrontation at the Comstock cabin.

### The Silent Witness

By Chapter XXIV, Henderson has evolved from a background figure into someone capable of independent feeling. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] arrives by boat, Henderson is the first to greet her, and she marks "one lean anxious face turned toward hers" and waves to him "with a throb of pity." (024-025) This moment suggests a depth of character beyond mere devotion to Edith.

## Key Events

1. **The Ball** (019-078) — At the grand social event, Edith dismisses Henderson for [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]]. Henderson later forces the engagement ring back into Edith's hand, urging her to catch Philip before he leaves.

2. **The Drive to [[onabasha|Onabasha]]** (022-132) — Two days after the ball, Edith persuades Henderson to drive her to [[onabasha|Onabasha]] to find Philip. Henderson "hated that trip" but acquiesced to her wishes, locating the Comstock land by map.

3. **The Horn at Brushwood Road** (022-108) — Henderson sounds his automobile horn as they approach the Comstock cabin, inadvertently announcing Edith's arrival to [[love-and-courtship|Philip and Elnora]].

4. **The Boat Dock Reunion** (024-025) — Henderson is the first man off the boat, coming to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] instantly. She motions him to sit beside her, and they share a silent, understanding moment.

## Significance

Hart Henderson serves as a foil to [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] in the novel's romantic subplot. Where Philip is the object of Edith's obsessive love, Henderson is the steadfast admirer she takes for granted. His quiet, undemanding loyalty mirrors the novel's broader theme that true worth often goes unrecognized by those blinded by vanity. (024-025)

Henderson's evolution from Edith's doormat to an independent character capable of genuine feeling for [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] underscores the novel's moral arc: that proximity to authentic goodness — embodied by [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] — transforms everyone it touches.
