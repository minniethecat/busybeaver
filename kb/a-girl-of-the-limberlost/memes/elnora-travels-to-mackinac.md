# Elnora Travels to Mackinac

## Summary

After [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]'s manipulative visit to the Comstock cabin — a night spent under [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s own roof in what [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] correctly identifies as a scheme to reclaim [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] makes a characteristically decisive choice. Rather than compete for Philip's affections or wait passively for his decision, she removes herself entirely. She travels alone by trolley, train, and boat to Mackinac Island, seeking refuge with Freckles and Angel O'More, the former guardians of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. Her journey is both a physical crossing of the Midwest and an assertion of self-worth: she will not marry a man who might still love another woman. (023-055)

## Context

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s departure occurs at a moment of acute emotional pressure. [[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] has returned to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], declared his love, and begun [[education-as-liberation|learning]] [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] at her side. But [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] has not accepted her loss. She drives from Chicago to [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] in what [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] suspects is a staged automobile breakdown, spends the night at the Comstock cabin, and attempts to reassert her claim on Philip. (023-001)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] observes Edith with a mixture of pity and clear-eyed assessment. She recognizes Edith's sincerity — "Mother, the girl is honest! She is absolutely sincere!" (023-055) — but also her danger. Edith genuinely believes that if she can spend time alone with Philip, she can "rekindle his love for her and awaken in him every particle of the old devotion." (023-055)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s response is not jealousy but a kind of fierce integrity. She tells her mother:

> "But mother, if she won, it wouldn't make me half so miserable as to marry Phil myself, and then read hunger for her in his eyes! Some one has got to suffer over this. If it proves to be me, I'll bear it, and you'll never hear a whisper of complaint from me." (023-055)

Having spoken these words, she acts on them. She will give Edith the field, not from weakness, but from the conviction that Philip must choose freely — and that his choice, whatever it is, must be made without her presence complicating it.

## The Journey

### Departure

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] leaves two sealed notes on her desk, kisses her mother repeatedly, and slips away. She drops a small bundle and her hat from the window beside the willow, then crosses [[comstock-garden|the garden]] and enters the woods. She takes "a diagonal course" through the trees, emerging two miles west and one mile south of [[comstock-cabin|the cabin]]. (023-094) The route is deliberate — she avoids the roads, [[onabasha-station|the station]]s where she might be recognised, the people who might ask questions. Her departure is not a flight but a strategic withdrawal.

### By Trolley and Train

At the road, she straightens her [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], puts on her hat and a thin dark veil, and waits for the passing trolley. She rides to the first town, transfers to a train for Fort Wayne, and catches the evening train north to Grand Rapids. The journey is long and solitary — Indiana to Michigan, farmland to industrial city, the familiar landscape of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] receding mile by mile. (023-094)

She arrives in Grand Rapids after midnight and sleeps on a seat in the women's waiting-room of [[onabasha-station|the depot]], her bundle for a pillow. She is tired, alone, and uncertain of her welcome — but she is also free, in the particular way that comes from having made a hard choice and followed it through.

### Finding Freckles

At daylight, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] washes, rearranges her hair and [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]], and enters the general waiting-room. There she sees him — "the tall, lithe figure, the bright hair, the lean, brown-splotched face, the steady gray eyes" (023-094) — Terence O'More, the Freckles of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], dressed for travelling.

> "Oh, I was just starting to find you!" she cried. "Thank you!" he said. "You are going away?" she panted. "Not if I am needed." (023-094)

She identifies herself as "[[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] girl to whom your wife gave the dress for Commencement," and asks for shelter. Freckles's response is immediate and absolute:

> "You have!" answered Freckles. "Any boy or girl who ever suffered one pang in the Limberlost has a claim to the best drop of blood in my heart. You needn't be telling me anything more. The Angel is at our cottage on Mackinac. You shall tell her and play with the babies while you want shelter. This way!" (023-094)

### Crossing the Straits

They breakfast in a luxurious car, talking of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] and the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]]'s work. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] tells of her nature lectures in the schools. By evening, they leave the train at Mackinaw City and cross the Straits by boat:

> Sheets of white moonlight flooded the water and paved a molten path across the breast of it straight to the face of the moon. The island lay a dark spot on the silver surface, its tall trees sharply outlined on the summit, and a million lights blinked around the shore. (023-094)

The night guns boom from the white fort. A great tenor, summering in the north, stands on the upper deck and sings "Oh, the moon shines bright on my old Kentucky home." [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] thinks of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]], of Philip, and of her mother, and "almost choked with the sobs that would arise in her throat." (023-094)

On the dock, Angel O'More sweeps into her husband's arms: "Oh, Freckles! You've been gone a month!" "Four days, Angel, only four days by the clock," he replies. Freckles introduces [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] as "[[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] girl in whom the [[bird-woman|Bird Woman]] is interested," and the Angel welcomes her with characteristic warmth. (023-094)

## Aftermath

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s stay on Mackinac Island provides the setting for the novel's resolution. In the O'More cottage, surrounded by children and music and the easy affection of a happy marriage, she finds both sanctuary and perspective. She writes to her mother, asking her to come and bring the violin. She waits, with the patience she has learned from years of [[moth-collecting|moth hunting]], for Philip to make his choice.

The journey itself proves [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s competence and independence. She navigates a multi-leg route across three states alone, with minimal resources, and arrives safely at her destination. The girl who once could not find her way through the social labyrinth of [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]] has become a young woman capable of crossing the Midwest on her own terms.

[[edith-carr|Edith Carr]], meanwhile, follows her own journey to Mackinac — not to pursue Philip but to release him. Her parallel travel, undertaken with [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]], traces the same geography but with a different purpose: renunciation rather than pursuit. (024-264)

## Significance

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s journey to Mackinac is an act of radical self-possession. In a novel where women are often defined by their relationships to men — as wives, widows, fiancées, or rivals — [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] defines herself by her relationship to her own integrity. She will not compete for Philip because she refuses to see herself as a competitor. She is not an alternative to [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]]; she is a person with her own life, her own work, and her own standards for what love must be.

The journey also extends the novel's geography beyond [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]. The swamp is the novel's heart, but it is not its only world. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s ability to move through the wider landscape — to Grand Rapids, across the Straits, to the island — demonstrates that her competence is not limited to moth collecting and violin playing. She can navigate the world beyond [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] as surely as she navigates its paths and pools.

Freckles's welcome — "Any boy or girl who ever suffered one pang in [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] has a claim to the best drop of blood in my heart" (023-094) — establishes a [[community-support|community]] that transcends geography. The Limberlost is not merely a place; it is a bond, connecting those who have known its beauty and its danger. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] arrives at Mackinac a stranger and is received as family.
