## Description

Brushwood School was a rural district school — "district number nine" — where [[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] received her early [[education-as-liberation|education]]. (001-079) It was a small country schoolhouse with a front bench where younger children sat and a back of the room reserved for the older, rougher boys. [[pete-corson|Pete Corson]], one of the "big boys at the back of the room," had known [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] since childhood, their shared history rooted in those early school days. (008-027)

The school was accessible via the Brushwood road, a country thoroughfare that features in several pivotal moments of the novel. The road itself carried significance beyond mere geography: the Brushwood road tax, along with the redredging of the big Limberlost ditch, constituted a financial burden that pushed the Comstock family to the edge of solvency. (001-452)

## Location

Brushwood School served the rural [[community-support|community]] near [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]] swamp, drawing children from the scattered farms and cabins that dotted the countryside. The Comstock cabin lay within its district, and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] walked or was driven along the Brushwood road to attend classes. The school's rural location meant that most of its students came from farming families like the Comstocks, Sintons, and Corsons.

The Brushwood road itself ran past the Comstock land. In later chapters, [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]]'s automobile shot from behind the trees lining the Brushwood road as he and [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] approached the Comstock cabin — a moment that brought the wider world hurtling toward the rural isolation the road had long symbolized. (023-297)

## Significance

Brushwood School represents the limitations and opportunities of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s early life. "As long as I could go to the Brushwood school I was happy, but I couldn't go further just when things were the most interesting," she reflects, capturing the [[education-as-liberation|education]]al ceiling that rural children faced. (003-209) Her completion of the Brushwood curriculum marked both an achievement and a dead end until she resolved to attend the [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]].

The school also embodied the [[community-support|community]] of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s childhood. The faces of two children — likely siblings — lay "under Brushwood sod," a memory that [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]] carried with sorrow. (002-079) The Brushwood road, school, and graveyard together form a landscape of rural life, where [[education-as-liberation|education]], labour, and mortality intertwine.

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s transition from Brushwood School to [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]] is one of the novel's central journeys. She left Brushwood having secured a license to teach before making the leap to the city [[education-as-liberation|high school]], determined to "start to college when the other girls were going." (010-237)

## Key Scenes

**First Day at [[onabasha|Onabasha]]** (001-079) — On her first day at the city [[education-as-liberation|high school]], [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] introduces herself by stating, "I finished last spring at Brushwood school, district number nine." The declaration marks both her pride in her country [[education-as-liberation|education]] and the social gap she must bridge in the city.

**[[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s Reflection** (003-209) — Looking back, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] tells how she was happy at Brushwood but could not progress further — the country school could take her only so far. This moment crystallizes the [[education-as-liberation|education]]al limitations of rural life and [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s determination to overcome them.

**[[pete-corson|Pete Corson]]'s Memory** (008-027) — [[pete-corson|Pete]], one of the rough older boys who had sat on the front bench with [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at Brushwood, remembers their shared origins. His presence in the story connects the world of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] and the country school to [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s wider network of allies.

**The Brushwood Road at Sunset** (023-297) — Near the novel's end, the Brushwood road becomes the route of [[forgiveness-and-redemption|reconciliation]], as [[hart-henderson|Hart Henderson]]'s automobile carries [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] toward the Comstock cabin, past the trees lining the road [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] had walked so many times as a country schoolgirl.
