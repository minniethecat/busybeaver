# CHAPTER XXI

[021-001]WHEREIN PHILIP AMMON RETURNS TO THE LIMBERLOST, AND ELNORA STUDIES THE

[021-002]SITUATION

[021-003]“We must be thinking about supper, mother,” said [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], while she set

[021-004]the wings of a Cecropia with much care. “It seems as if I can’t get

[021-005]enough to eat, or enough of being at home. I enjoyed that city house. I

[021-006]don’t believe I could have done my work if I had been compelled to walk

[021-007]back and forth. I thought at first I never wanted to come here again.

[021-008]Now, I feel as if I could not live anywhere else.”

[021-009]“Elnora,” said [[katharine-comstock|Mrs. Comstock]], “there’s some one coming down the road.”

[021-010]“Coming here, do you think?”

[021-011]“Yes, coming here, I suspect.”

[021-012]Elnora glanced quickly at her mother and then turned to the road as

[021-013][[philip-ammon|Philip Ammon]] reached the gate.

[021-014]“Careful, mother!” the girl instantly warned. “If you change your

[021-015]treatment of him a hair’s breadth, he will suspect. Come with me to

[021-016]meet him.”

[021-017]She dropped her work and sprang up.

[021-018]“Well, of all the delightful surprises!” she cried.

[021-019]She was a trifle thinner than during the previous summer. On her face

[021-020]there was a more mature, patient look, but the sun struck her bare head

[021-021]with the same ray of red gold. She wore one of the old blue gingham

[021-022]dresses, open at the throat and rolled to the elbows. Mrs. Comstock did

[021-023]not appear at all the same woman, but Philip saw only Elnora; heard

[021-024]only her greeting. He caught both hands where she offered but one.

[021-025]“Elnora,” he cried, “if you were engaged to me, and we were at a ball,

[021-026]among hundreds, where I offended you very much, and didn’t even know I

[021-027]had done anything, and if I asked you before all of them to allow me to

[021-028]explain, to forgive me, to wait, would your face grow distorted and

[021-029]unfamiliar with anger? Would you drop my ring on the floor and insult

[021-030]me repeatedly? Oh Elnora, would you?”

[021-031]Elnora’s big eyes seemed to leap, while her face grew very white. She

[021-032]drew away her hands.

[021-033]“Hush, Phil! Hush!” she protested. “That fever has you again! You are

[021-034]dreadfully ill. You don’t know what you are saying.”

[021-035]“I am sleepless and exhausted; I’m heartsick; but I am well as I ever

[021-036]was. Answer me, Elnora, would you?”

[021-037]“Answer nothing!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “Answer nothing! Hang your coat

[021-038]there on your nail, Phil, and come split some kindling. Elnora, clean

[021-039]away that stuff, and set the table. Can’t you see the boy is starved

[021-040]and tired? He’s come home to rest and eat a decent meal. Come on,

[021-041]Phil!”

[021-042]Mrs. Comstock marched away, and Philip hung his coat in its old place

[021-043]and followed. Out of sight and hearing she turned on him.

[021-044]“Do you call yourself a man or a hound?” she flared.

[021-045]“I beg your pardon——” stammered Philip Ammon.

[021-046]“I should think you would!” she ejaculated. “I’ll admit you did the

[021-047]square thing and was a man last summer, though I’d liked it better if

[021-048]you’d faced up and told me you were promised; but to come back here

[021-049]babying, and take hold of Elnora like that, and talk that way because

[021-050]you have had a fuss with your girl, I don’t tolerate. Split that

[021-051]kindling and I’ll get your supper, and then you better go. I won’t have

[021-052]you working on Elnora’s big heart, because you have quarrelled with

[021-053]some one else. You’ll have it patched up in a week and be gone again,

[021-054]so you can go right away.”

[021-055]“Mrs. Comstock, I came to ask Elnora to marry me.”

[021-056]“The more fool you, then!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “This time yesterday

[021-057]you were engaged to another woman, no doubt. Now, for some little

[021-058]flare-up you come racing here to use Elnora as a tool to spite the

[021-059]other girl. A week of sane living, and you will be sorry and ready to

[021-060]go back to Chicago, or, if you really are man enough to be sure of

[021-061]yourself, she will come to claim you. She has her rights. An engagement

[021-062]of years is a serious matter, and not broken for a whim. If you don’t

[021-063]go, she’ll come. Then, when you patch up your affairs and go sailing

[021-064]away together, where does my girl come in?”

[021-065]“I am a lawyer, Mrs. Comstock,” said Philip. “It appeals to me as

[021-066]beneath your ordinary sense of justice to decide a case without hearing

[021-067]the evidence. It is due me that you hear me first.”

[021-068]“Hear your side!” flashed Mrs. Comstock. “I’d a heap sight rather hear

[021-069]the girl!”

[021-070]“I wish to my soul that you had heard and seen her last night, Mrs.

[021-071]Comstock,” said Ammon. “Then, my way would be clear. I never even

[021-072]thought of coming here to-day. I’ll admit I would have come in time,

[021-073]but not for many months. My father sent me.”

[021-074]“Your father sent you! Why?”

[021-075]“Father, mother, and [[polly-ammon|Polly]] were present last night. They, and all my

[021-076]friends, saw me insulted and disgraced in the worst exhibition of

[021-077]uncontrolled temper any of us ever witnessed. All of them knew it was

[021-078]the end. Father liked what I had told him of Elnora, and he advised me

[021-079]to come here, so I came. If she does not want me, I can leave

[021-080]instantly, but, oh I hoped she would understand!”

[021-081]“You people are not splitting wood,” called Elnora.

[021-082]“Oh yes we are!” answered Mrs. Comstock. “You set out the things for

[021-083]biscuit, and lay the table.” She turned again to Philip. “I know

[021-084]considerable about your father,” she said. “I have met your Uncle’s

[021-085]family frequently this winter. I’ve heard your [[aunt-anna|Aunt Anna]] say that she

[021-086]didn’t at all like [[edith-carr|Miss Carr]], and that she and all your family secretly

[021-087]hoped that something would happen to prevent your marrying her. That

[021-088]chimes right in with your saying that your father sent you here. I

[021-089]guess you better speak your piece.”

[021-090]Philip gave his version of the previous night.

[021-091]“Do you believe me?” he finished.

[021-092]“Yes,” said Mrs. Comstock.

[021-093]“May I stay?”

[021-094]“Oh, it looks all right for you, but what about her?”

[021-095]“Nothing, so far as I am concerned. Her plans were all made to start to

[021-096]Europe to-day. I suspect she is on the way by this time. Elnora is very

[021-097]sensible, Mrs. Comstock. Hadn’t you better let her decide this?”

[021-098]“The final decision rests with her, of course,” admitted Mrs. Comstock.

[021-099]“But look you one thing! She’s all I have. As Solomon says, ‘she is the

[021-100]one child, the only child of her mother.’ I’ve suffered enough in this

[021-101]world that I fight against any suffering which threatens her. So far as

[021-102]I know you’ve always been a man, and you may stay. But if you bring

[021-103]tears and heartache to her, don’t have the assurance to think I’ll bear

[021-104]it tamely. I’ll get right up and fight like a catamount, if things go

[021-105]wrong for Elnora!”

[021-106]“I have no doubt but you will,” replied Philip, “and I don’t blame you

[021-107]in the least if you do. I have the utmost devotion to offer Elnora, a

[021-108]good home, fair social position, and my family will love her dearly.

[021-109]Think it over. I know it is sudden, but my father advised it.”

[021-110]“Yes, I reckon he did!” said Mrs. Comstock dryly. “I guess instead of

[021-111]me being the catamount, you had the genuine article up in Chicago,

[021-112]masquerading in peacock feathers, and posing as a fine lady, until her

[021-113]time came to scratch. Human nature seems to be the same the world over.

[021-114]But I’d give a pretty to know that secret thing you say you don’t, that

[021-115]set her raving over your just catching a moth for Elnora. You might get

[021-116]that crock of strawberries in [[spring-house|the spring house]].”

[021-117]They prepared and ate supper. Afterward they sat in [[grape-arbour|the arbour]] and

[021-118]talked, or Elnora played until time for Philip to go.

[021-119]“Will you walk to the gate with me?” he asked Elnora as he arose.

[021-120]“Not to-night,” she answered lightly. “Come early in the morning if you

[021-121]like, and we will go over to [[sleepy-snake-creek|Sleepy Snake Creek]] and hunt moths and

[021-122]gather dandelions for dinner.”

[021-123]Philip leaned toward her. “May I tell you to-morrow why I came?” he

[021-124]asked.

[021-125]“I think not,” replied Elnora. “The fact is, I don’t care why you came.

[021-126]It is enough for me that we are your very good friends, and that in

[021-127]trouble, you have found us a refuge. I fancy we had better live a week

[021-128]or two before you say anything. There is a possibility that what you

[021-129]have to say may change in that length of time.

[021-130]“It will not change one iota!” cried Philip.

[021-131]“Then it will have the grace of that much age to give it some small

[021-132]touch of flavour,” said the girl. “Come early in the morning.”

[021-133]She lifted the violin and began to play.

[021-134]“Well bless my soul!” ejaculated the astounded Mrs. Comstock. “To think

[021-135]I was worrying for fear you couldn’t take care of yourself!”

[021-136]Elnora laughed while she played.

[021-137]“Shall I tell you what he said?”

[021-138]“Nope! I don’t want to hear it!” said Elnora. “He is only six hours

[021-139]from Chicago. I’ll give her a week to find him and fix it up, if he

[021-140]stays that long. If she doesn’t put in an appearance then, he can tell

[021-141]me what he wants to say, and I’ll take my time to think it over. Time

[021-142]in plenty, too! There are three of us in this, and one must be left

[021-143]with a sore heart for life. If the decision rests with me I propose to

[021-144]be very sure that it is the one who deserves such hard luck.”

[021-145]The next morning Philip came early, dressed in the outing [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]] he

[021-146]had worn the previous summer, and aside from a slight paleness seemed

[021-147]very much the same as when he left. Elnora met him on the old footing,

[021-148]and for a week life went on exactly as it had the previous summer. Mrs.

[021-149]Comstock made mental notes and watched in silence. She could see that

[021-150]Elnora was on a strain, though she hoped Philip would not. The girl

[021-151]grew restless as the week drew to a close. Once when the gate clicked

[021-152]she suddenly lost colour and moved nervously. [[billy|Billy]] came down the walk.

[021-153]Philip leaned toward Mrs. Comstock and said: “I am expressly forbidden

[021-154]to speak to Elnora as I would like. Would you mind telling her for me

[021-155]that I had a letter from my father this morning saying that Miss Carr

[021-156]is on her way to Europe for the summer?”

[021-157]“Elnora,” said Mrs. Comstock promptly, “I have just heard that Carr

[021-158]woman is on her way to Europe, and I wish to my gracious stars she’d

[021-159]stay there!”

[021-160]Philip Ammon shouted, but Elnora arose hastily and went to meet Billy.

[021-161]They came into the arbour together and after speaking to Mrs. Comstock

[021-162]and Philip, Billy said: “[[wesley-sinton|Uncle Wesley]] and I found something funny, and

[021-163]we thought you’d like to see.”

[021-164]“I don’t know what I should do without you and Uncle Wesley to help

[021-165]me,” said Elnora. “What have you found now?”

[021-166]“Something I couldn’t bring. You have to come to it. I tried to get one

[021-167]and I killed it. They are a kind of insecty things, and they got a long

[021-168]tail that is three fine hairs. They stick those hairs right into the

[021-169]hard bark of trees, and if you pull, the hairs stay fast and it kills

[021-170]the bug.”

[021-171]“We will come at once,” laughed Elnora. “I know what they are, and I

[021-172]can use some in my work.”

[021-173]“Billy, have you been crying?” inquired Mrs. Comstock.

[021-174]Billy lifted a chastened face. “Yes, ma’am,” he replied. “This has been

[021-175]the worst day.”

[021-176]“What’s the matter with the day?”

[021-177]“The day is all right,” admitted Billy. “I mean every single thing has

[021-178]gone wrong with me.”

[021-179]“Now that is too bad!” sympathized Mrs. Comstock.

[021-180]“Began early this morning,” said Billy. “All Snap’s fault, too.”

[021-181]“What has poor Snap been doing?” demanded Mrs. Comstock, her eyes

[021-182]beginning to twinkle.

[021-183]“Digging for woodchucks, like he always does. He gets up at two o’clock

[021-184]to dig for them. He was coming in from the woods all tired and covered

[021-185]thick with dirt. I was going to [[sinton-barn|the barn]] with the pail of water for

[021-186]Uncle Wesley to use in milking. I had to set down the pail to shut the

[021-187]gate so the chickens wouldn’t get into the flower beds, and old Snap

[021-188]stuck his dirty nose into the water and began to lap it down. I knew

[021-189]Uncle Wesley wouldn’t use that, so I had to go ‘way back to the cistern

[021-190]for more, and it pumps awful hard. Made me mad, so I threw the water on

[021-191]Snap.”

[021-192]“Well, what of it?”

[021-193]“Nothing, if he’d stood still. But it scared him awful, and when he’s

[021-194]afraid he goes a-humping for [[margaret-sinton|Aunt Margaret]]. When he got right up

[021-195]against her he stiffened out and gave a big shake. You oughter seen the

[021-196]nice blue dress she had put on to go to [[onabasha|Onabasha]]!”

[021-197]Mrs. Comstock and Philip laughed, but Elnora put her arms around the

[021-198]boy. “Oh Billy!” she cried. “That was too bad!”

[021-199]“She got up early and ironed that dress to wear because it was cool.

[021-200]Then, when it was all dirty, she wouldn’t go, and she wanted to real

[021-201]bad.” Billy wiped his eyes. “That ain’t all, either,” he added.

[021-202]“We’d like to know about it, Billy,” suggested Mrs. Comstock,

[021-203]struggling with her face.

[021-204]“Cos she couldn’t go to the city, she’s most worked herself to death.

[021-205]She’s done all the dirty, hard jobs she could find. She’s fixing her

[021-206]grape juice now.”

[021-207]“Sure!” cried Mrs. Comstock. “When a woman is disappointed she always

[021-208]works like a dog to gain sympathy!”

[021-209]“Well, Uncle Wesley and I are sympathizing all we know how, without her

[021-210]working so. I’ve squeezed until I almost busted to get the juice out

[021-215]Elnora glanced swiftly at her mother.

[021-216]“I worked so hard,” continued Billy, “that she said if I would throw

[021-219]“We will all go,” said Mrs. Comstock. “I am mightily interested in
