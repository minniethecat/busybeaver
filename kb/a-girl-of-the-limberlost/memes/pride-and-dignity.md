# Pride and Dignity

## Definition

Pride and dignity in *A Girl of [[limberlost-as-symbol|the Limberlost]]* are explored through a central tension: the difference between "false pride" — a stubborn refusal to accept help that harms oneself and others — and legitimate dignity — the self-respect that enables a poor country girl to hold her head high in a world that would shame her. The novel does not simply celebrate pride or condemn it; it anatomizes it, distinguishing between forms of pride that build character and forms that destroy relationships. (001-399)(004-517)

[[elnora-comstock|Elnora Comstock]] is the primary vehicle for this exploration. Her pride is her armor — it protects her from humiliation, drives her to work rather than beg, and fuels her academic and professional success. But it also isolates her, makes her refuse the help of people who love her, and threatens to cut her off from the very [[community-support|community]] that would sustain her. (001-414)

## Role in the Story

### The Charge of False Pride

The phrase "false pride" comes from [[wesley-sinton|Wesley Sinton]], who, in a moment of frustration, tells [[katharine-comstock|Katharine]]: "I guess we better all go to work to see if we can't chop a little of the thing you call 'false pride' in her" (004-517). Wesley loves [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] and wants to help her, but her pride prevents her from accepting money, clothes, or even advice that she has not earned.

This "false pride" is the pride that will not accept charity, that insists on paying one's own way even when the cost is suffering. When [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] refuses Wesley's offer of money for her tuition, he challenges her: "Swallow that little lump of pride in your throat" (001-399). He warns that "the thing you call pride is going to cut us out of all the pleasure we'd have in doing for you" (001-414).

### The Value of Pride

Yet the novel does not simply condemn Elnora's pride. Her refusal to accept charity is also her refusal to be pitied. In a social world where a "strange girl from the country" (002-104) is automatically positioned as inferior, pride is a form of resistance. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s insistence on [[work-and-self-reliance|earning]] her own way — through moth-collecting, through farm work, through study — is what enables her to walk into the [[onabasha-high-school|Onabasha High School]] with "her head high" despite her calico dress.

Wesley himself acknowledges this duality. After confronting [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] about her pride, he reflects that he walks "with his head high through pride in her" (002-078). The same quality that frustrates him — her fierce independence — is also what makes him proud of her. The novel thus refuses a simple moral judgment on pride, presenting it instead as a quality that must be calibrated: too little, and one becomes a dependent; too much, and one becomes isolated.

### Dignity as Achievement

The novel traces [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s movement from defensive pride to achieved dignity. Dignity, in the novel's terms, is not something one is born with or can purchase; it is something one earns through work, [[education-as-liberation|learning]], and moral conduct. By the novel's end, [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] no longer needs to refuse help because she no longer fears being seen as a charity case. Her dignity is secure — she has proven herself, and the [[community-support|community]] recognizes her worth.

This is captured in the description of [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] at [[onabasha-high-school|the high school]] [[elnora-graduates-high-school|commencement]]: she walks "in calm dignity" (021-415), explaining "the marvels of the" natural world. The confidence here is not defensive but generous — she has knowledge to share, and she shares it. This is the dignity that lies beyond pride.

### Pride and Love

The novel also explores how pride can interfere with love. Elnora's pride almost costs her Philip, as she refuses to compete with [[edith-carr|Edith Carr]] or to make her feelings known. Philip's own pride — his sense of obligation to Edith — nearly keeps him from pursuing his true feelings. The resolution requires both characters to set aside pride in favor of honesty and vulnerability.

## Themes

### Pride as a Poor Person's Resource

For [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]], pride is not a luxury or a vice — it is a survival strategy. In a world where poverty is a source of shame, pride is the assertion that one's worth is not measured by one's [[social-class-and-appearance|clothing]] or bank account. [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]]'s "I'll not take your money" (001-341) is not stubbornness; it is a declaration of personhood. The novel understands that for the poor, pride is often the only thing that cannot be taken away — and therefore the thing most fiercely defended.

### The Community's Claim on the Individual

Wesley's argument against Elnora's pride is a communal one: her refusal to accept help deprives others of the joy of giving. "The thing you call pride is going to cut us out of all the pleasure we'd have in doing for you" (001-414). This is a sophisticated moral argument — that excessive individualism, even when motivated by dignity, can damage the social fabric. The novel ultimately endorses a balance: accept help when it is offered in love, but never stop working to earn one's own way.

### From Pride to Pride

The novel traces a transformation in the meaning of pride itself. At the beginning, pride is defensive, brittle, isolating. By the end, the Sintons "take some pride in doing a good job" (002-147), [[elnora-comstock|Elnora]] takes "keen pride in relaxing" (018-238), and the entire [[community-support|community]] takes pride in her achievements. Pride has been transformed from a barrier into a bond — something shared rather than hoarded.
