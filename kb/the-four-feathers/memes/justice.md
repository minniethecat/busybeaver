# Justice

## Definition

Justice in [[The Four Feathers]] is poetic rather than legal. No court could adjudicate whether [[Harry Feversham]] was a coward — only the slow unfolding of events can revise the initial verdict. When Ethne asks "Were they justly sent?" and Harry answers "Yes" (004-051), the novel establishes that the accusation, however devastating, is not unjust. The novel's conception of justice is fundamentally narrative: truth emerges not through argument but through story.

## Narrative Role

Justice provides the novel's underlying moral structure. The feathers are unfair as a summary of Harry's character (they judge an act, not a person) but fair as a description of that act (he did resign on the eve of battle). As Sutch observes, Harry "ever incurred that charge of [[cowardice]]" (025-028) — the verb "incurred" suggests a debt entered into, a balance to be settled. The novel's work is to make the judgment fair on both levels through a counter-narrative of equal weight.

## Embodiment

The three accusers embody justice in its institutional form: impartial, impersonal, and severe. Harry embodies justice's subject — the man who accepts the sentence and works to overturn it through deeds rather than words. Ethne embodies justice's limit: her fourth feather goes beyond the institutional charge, adding personal pain that institutional mechanisms cannot remedy.

## Key Scenes

The return of the feathers (chapters 14–30) is justice as procedural drama — each return a step in a legal process conducted without courtroom or judge. When Willoughby brings "a tiny, soiled, white feather" (014-069) as proof, the word "soiled" carries the evidence of Harry's [[suffering]] — justice's physical record. The final reconciliation (Chapter 34) is justice completed: not merely the withdrawal of charges but the restoration of relationships, which is justice's deepest purpose.
