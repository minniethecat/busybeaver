# Trust

## Definition

Trust in *The Four Feathers* is the invisible medium in which honour, friendship, and love operate. When Harry resigns his commission, he breaks not a law but a trust — the trust of his fellow officers that he would stand with them in battle, the trust of [[ethne-eustace]] that he was the man she believed him to be, the trust of his father that the Feversham name meant courage. "It seemed that he could not trust himself to answer" (004-012) — the crisis of trust begins with Harry's loss of trust in himself.

## Narrative Role

Trust is what the white feathers destroy and what the returned feathers rebuild. Each returned feather is not just proof of a courageous act but the restoration of a trust that was broken. The novel's emotional arc is the slow reconstruction of trust — between Harry and Ethne, between Harry and his father, between Harry and his regiment. "He had failed in a sacred trust" (025-027) — trust, once broken, can only be rebuilt through sustained action, never through apology alone.

## Varieties

**Interpersonal Trust** is the trust between individuals. It is broken by Harry's resignation — "she could not trust her lips to utter it distinctly" (018-054) captures how the loss of trust makes even speech difficult — and rebuilt through years of secret action. Ethne's question to Durrance, "You distrust me?" (023-015), and his honest reply, "Have I no reason to distrust you?" (023-017), shows trust tested and partially broken even between those who love each other.

**Regimental Trust** is the trust among officers. It is impersonal, absolute, and unforgiving. The white feather is its withdrawal — trust revoked by the collective. Sutch's note to Harry, "When he comes trust him" (029-047), is the restoration of regimental trust in miniature: a single sentence that reverses a decade of suspicion.

**Self-Trust** is Harry's deepest crisis. He does not trust himself to be brave — "his tongue he could not trust" (015-017) — and this self-distrust becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. His redemption is the recovery of self-trust through action: learning, by doing, that he can be relied upon.

## Key Scenes

The white feather ball is trust's public breaking. Sutch's unwavering support embodies trust preserved — he trusts Harry when all evidence points the other way. The final reconciliation at [[glenalla]] is trust restored: "her pride was restored, and she had a fuller faith" (021-004) — faith being trust's other name.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Trust is the precondition of [[honour]] — honour is what trust protects. It is what [[cowardice]] breaks and [[courage]] repairs. And it is the emotional correlate of [[redemption]]: redemption is not complete until trust is restored.
