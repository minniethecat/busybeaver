# The White Feather

## Definition

The white feather is the material form of the novel's central accusation. Drawn from the Victorian folk tradition — in cock-fighting, a white feather in a gamecock's tail indicated inferior breeding — it was adopted by Mason as the symbol of cowardice made visible. Three white feathers arrive in a jeweller's box during a ball; a fourth is torn from Ethne's fan (004-088). Together they constitute the accusation that [[Harry Feversham]] spends the novel's remainder undoing.

## Narrative Role

The white feather is both inciting incident and structural device. The novel's plot can be mapped as a ledger of feathers: three sent in accusation (Trench, Castleton, Willoughby), one added in personal betrayal (Ethne), and each returned as Harry earns back his honour through secret acts of heroism in the Sudan. The feathers are the novel's moral accounting system — each return is a withdrawal from the accusation's balance.

## Embodiment

The three original feathers are tied to their senders: [[Captain Trench]], [[Mr. Castleton]], and [[Mr. Willoughby]] — each an officer of Harry's old regiment. As Harry saves each man in the Sudan, the corresponding feather is returned. Trench's feather returns first, carried by Willoughby from [[the House of Stone]]. Willoughby himself returns his own feather in [[Devonshire]], handing Ethne "a tiny, soiled, white feather" (014-069) as proof the charge is withdrawn. Castleton's feather is the last to return.

Ethne's fourth feather is unique: torn from her own ivory-and-feather fan, it represents not military judgment but personal betrayal — the lover's accusation layered upon the comrades'. Unlike the others, it is never formally "returned" — it must be earned back through the novel's concluding reconciliation.

## Key Scenes

The feather's arrival (Chapter 4) is Mason's most dramatic scene: "Three white feathers fluttered out of the box, swayed and rocked for a moment in the air, and then, one after another, settled gently down upon the floor. They lay like flakes of snow upon the dark polished boards" (004-038). Ethne's addition — "There are four" (004-087) — transforms the accusation from military to total.

In Chapter 6, Harry lays out [[the four feathers]] on the table before him (006-054), an act of private reckoning. In Chapter 14, Willoughby returns his feather in a scene that reverses the original accusation: "three separate accusations of cowardice, were sent to Feversham by three separate men. This was the answer to the first of them" (014-073).

## Symbolism

The feather's symbolic richness comes from its doubleness: it is at once fragile (it "swayed and rocked" on the air) and indelible (it brands Harry for years). It is white — the colour of surrender — yet functions as an act of war. Its return, "soiled" from [[the desert]], transforms it from an abstract symbol into a material witness to Harry's suffering.
