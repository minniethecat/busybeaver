# General Feversham

## Role in the Novel

General Feversham represents the old order of military honour — inflexible, inherited, and incapable of self-doubt. He is "that lonely iron man" (004-067) who sits on his terrace at [[Broad Place]] dreaming "of honours and distinctions worthy of the Fevershams to be gained immediately by his son in the Egyptian campaign" (004-067). When [[disgrace]] comes, he cannot process it — not from cruelty but from a constitutional inability to understand fear. His arc from paternal pride through frozen silence to the "joy at the reconquest of honour by his son" (026-034) traces the novel's larger movement from judgment to reconciliation.

## Biography

A veteran of the [[Crimean War]], General Feversham hosts annual "Crimean nights" at Broad Place where old comrades gather to tell war stories. It is during one such night that young Harry hears the stories of two men who "had both shirked" (004-068) — tales that become the seed of his obsessive fear. After Harry's disgrace, the General refuses to speak his son's name. Now "within a few months of his eightieth year" (026-015), he learns from Durrance that Harry never was a coward.

## Relationships

**[[Harry Feversham]]**: His only son. The General's inability to understand Harry's nature is the tragedy of Harry's childhood. Their reconciliation, mediated by Durrance, is incomplete — the General "will only be glad because the family honour is restored" (026-010) — yet deeply moving.

**[[Lieutenant Sutch]]**: An old comrade who loved the General's late wife Muriel. Sutch blames the General for his share in Harry's ruin.

**[[Muriel Feversham]] (deceased)**: Harry's mother, dead before the novel begins. Sutch's devotion to her memory drives his care for Harry.

## Key Scenes

[[The Crimean nights at Broad Place]], where young Harry internalizes the stories of [[cowardice]]. The General's silence after the disgrace — a refusal to speak Harry's name that lasts years. Durrance's visit (Chapter 26) to tell him "Harry never was a coward" (026-008), and the General's quiet joy when "the family honour is restored."
