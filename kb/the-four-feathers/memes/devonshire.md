# Devonshire

## Location

Devonshire is a county in the southwest of [[england|England]], bordered by Cornwall to the west and the English Channel to the south. In the novel, it provides [[memory|the past]]oral setting for the estates of [[broad-place|Broad Place]] and Guessens, the home of [[ethne-eustace|Ethne Eustace]]. The county's wooded creeks and meadows form the physical background to the novel's English sequences — a landscape of quiet continuity against which the drama of the [[four-feathers|white feathers]] unfolds.

## Estates

Two significant estates anchor the novel's Devonshire geography:

**[[broad-place|Broad Place]]** (Southpool): The [[harry-feversham|Feversham]] family seat, situated "upon a wooded creek of the Salcombe estuary" (003-023). It is here that [[harry-feversham]] grows up under the shadow of the ancestral portraits, and it is here that the [[four-feathers|white feathers]] are delivered — in the great hall whose windows look out upon the quiet Devonshire countryside.

**Guessens**: The Eustace family home, a neighbouring estate within walking distance of [[broad-place|Broad Place]]. [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]] describes herself as "your neighbour — a couple of fields away" (013-060), and the proximity of the two estates makes the relationship between [[harry-feversham|Harry]] and [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]] — and later between [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]] and [[jack-durrance|Durrance]] — geographically intimate.

## In the Novel

Devonshire is not merely a backdrop; it is the constant that the Sudan sequences measure themselves against. When characters are in danger in the desert, they think of Devonshire. When [[jack-durrance|Durrance]] lies blind at [[dongola|Dongola]], his mind returns to the Devonshire meadows:

> "The Devonshire meadows were about her, the swing of the sea, the calling of the gulls." [014-079]

This is [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]]'s world — the world she inhabits while the men she loves are in the Sudan. Devonshire is feminised space in the novel's imagination: it is where women wait, where news arrives, where the consequences of distant events are felt in drawing-rooms and on country walks.

[[captain-willoughby|Captain Willoughby]] "came to Devonshire six weeks ago" (025-026) bearing news of [[harry-feversham|Harry]]'s redemption — news that upends the quiet order of [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]]'s life. The arrival of truth from the desert into the Devonshire drawing-room is the novel's recurring structural pattern.

## Narrative Significance

Devonshire is the **pastoral counterpoint** to the Sudan. The novel's geography is structured around the contrast between the green, settled, ancestral landscapes of Devonshire and the harsh, barren, lawless expanses of the desert. [[harry-feversham|Harry]] must leave Devonshire to prove himself worthy of it; [[jack-durrance|Durrance]] must lose Devonshire (and [[ethne-eustace|Ethne]] within it) to find a different kind of peace.

> "He took the night mail into Devonshire the same evening, and reached his home in the early morning." [022-055]

The train journey from [[london|London]] to Devonshire is a recurring motif — the last leg of the return from empire to home. Every character who goes to the Sudan measures the distance back to the Devonshire meadows.
