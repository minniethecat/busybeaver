# Tokar

## Location

Tokar is located in eastern Sudan, inland from [[red-sea|the Red Sea]] coast, in the region between [[suakin]] and the Eritrean border. It sits among a cluster of battle sites — [[tamai]], [[tamanieb]] — that mark the eastern theatre of the [[the-mahdist-state|Mahdist]] campaigns, where Osman Digna's forces repeatedly engaged British and [[cairo|Egypt]]ian troops.

## Description

The novel does not describe Tokar as a physical location. Instead, it appears in catalogues of battle names that function as a shared vocabulary among the novel's military characters. "Tokar, [[tamai|Tamai]], [[tamanieb|Tamanieb]]" [007-052] is a triplet that recurs in the memories of [[jack-durrance]] and others — a metonym for the eastern Sudan campaign and the testing of courage under fire.

> "The rush down the Red Sea, Tokar, Tamai, Tamanieb — the crowded moments came vividly to his mind." [007-052]

## Narrative Significance

Tokar belongs to the novel's geography of memory and reputation. For the veteran [[the-british-officer-corps|officers]] — [[jack-durrance]], [[captain-trench]], and their peers — the eastern Sudan battles represent a shared experience that binds them and separates them from those, like [[harry-feversham]], who were not present. When [[jack-durrance|Durrance]] lies blinded in the [[the-house-of-stone|House of Stone]] in [[omdurman]], the memory of battles like Tokar becomes a sharp contrast to his present helplessness.

> "Perhaps, at Tokar, or Tamai, or Tamanieb, just as Trench had done!" [027-044]

The name carries no political or strategic weight in the novel; it carries psychological weight — the weight of having been there, of having faced the test that [[harry-feversham|Harry]] fears he has fled.

## Key Scenes

- **Chapter 7** (PN 007-052): Tokar appears in a flashback catalogue as [[lieutenant-sutch|Sutch]] reflects on the military life [[harry-feversham|Harry]] has abandoned.
- **Chapter 27** (PN 027-044): [[jack-durrance|Durrance]], blind and imprisoned, recalls Tokar as part of his military past — a memory that measures the distance between what he was and what he has become.
