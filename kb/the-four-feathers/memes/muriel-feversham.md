# Muriel Feversham

## Role in the Novel

Muriel Feversham never appears in the novel — she is dead before it begins — yet her absence is one of its most powerful presences. She is the lost mother whose [[death]] left Harry without anyone to whom he "could tell" his [[fear]] (004-067). She is the woman Sutch loved, and it is for her sake that he watches over Harry: "Since I was dead... surely you might have helped. Just for my sake you might have helped" (025-027) — the words Sutch imagines her speaking from beyond. Her ghost is the novel's silent measure of what Harry lost and what Sutch tried, imperfectly, to provide.

## Biography

Muriel Graham married [[General Feversham]] and bore one son, Harry. She died when Harry was a child. [[Lieutenant Sutch]], who loved her, has carried her [[memory]] for decades. Her absence shapes Harry's childhood — without a mother's understanding, his fear grew in isolation, unshared and unassuaged.

## Relationships

**[[Harry Feversham]]**: Her son. "My mother was dead, and my father —" Harry breaks off, unable to finish the sentence (004-067). Her death left him with only the General's inflexible expectations.

**Lieutenant Sutch**: The man who loved her and who, after her death, tried to watch over her son — a [[trust]] he believes he failed.

**General Feversham**: Her husband. The General's emotional inaccessibility after her death deepened Harry's isolation.

## Key Scenes

Sutch's vision of Muriel's reproach (Chapter 25) — "Muriel Feversham's eyes looking at him with reproach from the barrier of the skies" (025-027) — is the novel's most direct acknowledgment of her enduring significance. Harry's broken [[confession]]: "My mother was dead, and my father —" (004-067).
