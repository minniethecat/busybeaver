# Dermod Eustace

## Role in the Novel

Dermod Eustace provides the novel's comic relief and — in his bluff affection for [[Harry Feversham]] — one of its few instances of uncomplicated male warmth. He is "a sort of intoxicated Coriolanus" (004-031), a man "of another century, who looked out with an angry eye upon a topsy-turvy world, and would not be reconciled to it except after much alcohol" (004-031). Unlike the Feversham patriarchs with their crushing expectations, Dermod simply likes Harry — a contrast that highlights the different emotional climates of the Irish and English households.

## Biography

The master of [[Lennon House]] in [[Ramelton]], [[County Donegal]], Dermod Eustace is a widower who lives with his daughter Ethne. He "practised an ancient and tyrannous hospitality, keeping open house upon the road to Letterkenny, and forcing bed and board even upon strangers" (004-031). It is at his ball that the white feathers arrive. He is "rather afraid of his daughter, who wisely kept him doubtful whether she was displeased with him or not" (004-031), and "had conceived a great liking for Harry Feversham" (004-031).

## Relationships

**[[Ethne Eustace]]**: His daughter, who manages him with affectionate tact. Their relationship is the novel's healthiest parent-child bond.

**Harry Feversham**: Dermod likes Harry without reservation — a stark contrast to [[General Feversham]]'s conditional approval.

## Key Scenes

The ball at Lennon House (Chapter 4): Dermod's greeting to Harry — "If you want any whiskey, stamp twice on the floor with your foot. The servants understand" (004-030) — captures his character in two sentences. His legendary rides through Ramelton, "mounted on a big white mare of seventeen hands... swaying with drink, yet by a miracle keeping his saddle" (004-031).
