# Mrs. Adair

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Adair serves as the novel's most acute observer — a minor character whose social marginality (she is a widow, not a player in the military drama) gives her a clarity denied to the principals. She sees through Ethne's "boastings of [[courage]]" and "detractions" (023-033) with a [[patience]] that the principals, absorbed in their own dramas, cannot muster. She befriends Ethne during the long years of Harry's absence and Durrance's courtship, and her interventions — though small — are invariably shrewd. She represents a world outside the military [[honour]] code, where emotional intelligence matters more than martial virtue.

## Biography

A widow living in [[Devonshire]], Mrs. Adair enters Ethne's life during the period of Durrance's courtship. She intervenes (Chapter 19) in ways that gently nudge the emotional geometry of the triangle toward its resolution. As she later tells Ethne, "she had not courage to tell you she was afraid" (023-030) — speaking of Ethne's own unadmitted [[fear]] with a frankness that the novel's military men would never permit themselves. Her "apology" (Chapter 23) for speaking too frankly is, characteristically, not really an apology — she was right all along.

## Relationships

**[[Ethne Eustace]]**: Her friend and confidante. Mrs. Adair sees what Ethne cannot admit to herself about her feelings for Harry and her obligations to Durrance.

**[[Jack Durrance]]**: She respects him but recognizes that his engagement to Ethne is built on an incomplete foundation.

## Key Scenes

Her intervention in Chapter 19, when she speaks truths that the principals cannot utter. She perceives what Ethne herself barely allows into consciousness — that the engagement to Durrance is built on an incomplete foundation, and that Ethne "had not courage to tell you she was afraid" (023-030). Her "apology" in Chapter 23 serves to confirm rather than retract her earlier observations, a rhetorical move that reveals her shrewdness: by apologizing, she ensures her point is heard twice.
