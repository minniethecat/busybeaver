# Colonel Dawson

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Dawson is an officer of the [[Suakin]] garrison who appears in Chapter 12, during the tense period when [[jack-durrance|Jack Durrance]] is overdue from his desert reconnaissance. Dawson's role is functional but vivid: he voices the garrison's growing fears and serves as a foil to the more optimistic [[major-walters|Major Walters]].

## Character

Dawson is characterised by a certain gloominess and pragmatism. When Durrance is reported overdue, "Youth, however, in this instance had no optimism wherewith to challenge Colonel Dawson" (012-004). His pessimism is not baseless — he states plainly: "I am afraid" (012-004). Later, when the possibility of a lure or trap is raised, another officer echoes Dawson's judgment: "Like Colonel Dawson, I am afraid" (012-023), establishing Dawson as the garrison's voice of caution.

## Biography

Dawson serves in the Suakin garrison, where his duties include overseeing reconnaissance operations into [[the desert]]. His concern for Durrance is professional and personal: he knows the risks of desert travel and fears the worst when Durrance fails to return on schedule. In the mess-room, he is a steady if sombre presence: "Colonel Dawson was helping himself to cocoa" (012-029) during the tense breakfast that follows Durrance's disappearance, and his voice is heard "in the mess-room" as officers come and go with news (012-098).

## Relationships

**[[jack-durrance|Jack Durrance]]**: Dawson knows Durrance as a methodical officer whose overdue return is cause for genuine alarm. His respect for Durrance is evident in his acknowledgment that Durrance is "methodical after a fashion" (012-023), making his disappearance all the more ominous.

**[[major-walters|Major Walters]]**: The two senior officers form a complementary pair — Dawson the pessimist, Walters the incorrigible optimist. Their contrasting temperaments frame the garrison's response to crisis.

## Key Scenes

Dawson appears exclusively in Chapter 12, where his role is to articulate the garrison's worst fears and thereby heighten the dramatic tension surrounding Durrance's fate. His gloom, contrasted with Walters's cheerfulness, creates the emotional register against which the news of Durrance's [[blindness]] will later land with devastating effect.
