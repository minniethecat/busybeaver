# Colonel Trench

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Trench appears briefly in the novel's later chapters as the father of [[Captain Trench]], one of Harry's accusers. He represents the older generation of military families — men whose sons are tested in the Sudan, and who must process the news of their fates through the filter of regimental [[honour]]. His brief appearance underscores the novel's generational theme: the honour code passes from father to son, and its judgments ripple upward as well as downward.

## Biography

A retired colonel, he is the father of Captain Trench. His son's death in [[the House of Stone]] in [[Omdurman]] — and the circumstances of that death, when Trench asked Willoughby to return his feather as proof that "he withdrew his charge of [[cowardice]] against [[Harry Feversham]]" (025-026) — connect him to the novel's larger narrative of [[redemption]].

## Relationships

**Captain Trench**: His son, whose death in the Sudan is reported in the novel's later chapters. The elder Trench's reception of this news exemplifies the stoicism expected of military families.

**[[General Feversham]]**: A generational peer. Both are fathers of sons tested — and in different ways, broken — by the Sudan campaign. The parallel between the two fathers is underscored when Durrance tells the General "how Harry has redeemed his honour, how he is still redeeming it" (026-008) — words that Colonel Trench, in his own way, must also absorb.

## Key Scenes

His brief appearance in the later chapters, receiving news of his son's fate — a scene that echoes the novel's larger pattern of fathers learning what their sons have become. As Sutch reflects, the news that "Harry never was a coward" (026-008) would reach General Feversham through Durrance; Colonel Trench learns of his son's death and its meaning through a parallel chain of report and witness. The novel's intergenerational theme is most visible in these paired paternal reckonings.
