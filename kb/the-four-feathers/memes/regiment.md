# Regiment

## Definition

The regiment in *[[The Four Feathers]]* is more than a military unit — it is the institutional form of [[honour]] itself. When three officers send white feathers to a fourth, they act not as individuals but as representatives of the regiment's collective conscience. The regiment is the social body that judges, the community that expels, and ultimately the institution to which Harry must prove himself worthy of returning.

## Narrative Role

The regiment structures the novel's social world. Harry's crisis begins when "my regiment was ordered from India to Egypt" (004-008) and he resigns rather than face the test of battle. The three feathers come from three officers — "All three are officers of my old regiment" (004-048) — acting as its informal tribunal. Harry's [[redemption]] requires acts that serve the regiment's interests: recovering the Gordon letters, rescuing imprisoned officers. The regiment is both plaintiff and, eventually, vindicator.

## Embodiment

**[[Captain Trench]]** is the regiment's conscience in its sternest form — he sends the feather, and he is the first to return it, dying in [[the House of Stone]] with the request that his feather be carried back to [[England]].

**[[Jack Durrance]]** represents the regiment as career and identity. Blinded in the Sudan, he continues to serve, embodying the institutional continuity that Harry's resignation fractured.

**The Royal Mallows** (Harry's fictional regiment in the novel) function as a collective character. When Durrance returns "from his year's furlough from his regiment in India" (002-001), the novel establishes the regiment as the gravitational centre of its male characters' lives — it appears at the novel's beginning (the order to Egypt), haunts its middle (the feathers, the gossip), and provides its resolution (the regiment's honour restored with Harry's).

## Key Scenes

The telegram announcing the regiment's deployment (Chapter 2) is the novel's efficient cause — without it, Harry would never have been tested and never resigned. The three feathers, sent by three officers acting "for the credit of their regiment" (006-043), make the judgment collective and institutional.

When Willoughby speaks of "the honour of the regiment" in Chapter 30, the phrase carries the weight of the entire narrative: Harry's individual redemption is complete only when it is acknowledged by the institution that condemned him.

## Symbolism

The regiment's colours, its mess traditions, its collective reputation — these are the rituals through which institutional honour is maintained. The white feathers, sent collectively and returned individually, map the relationship between the regiment as a whole and the individual officers who constitute it.
