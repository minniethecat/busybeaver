# Loyalty

## Definition

Loyalty in [[The Four Feathers]] is the virtue that endures when all evidence argues against it. It is not blind faith (Ethne is not blind to Harry's [[confession]]) but a deeper knowledge that persists despite contrary facts. Sutch's loyalty to Harry's memory and Ethne's loyalty to 'the man she had known' represent two forms of the same virtue: one rooted in love, the other in paternal devotion.

## Narrative Role

Loyalty functions as the novel's counter-current to the [[honour]] code. Where honour demands public judgment, loyalty offers private fidelity. The tension between these two values drives much of the novel's emotional complexity: Ethne must choose between loyalty to Harry and loyalty to her own sense of right; Sutch must weigh loyalty to his promise against loyalty to the truth.

## Embodiment

[[Lieutenant Sutch]] embodies loyalty as sacred trust: 'I promised faithfully in [[the Criterion grillroom]]' (025-003). His five-year silence is loyalty's cost made visible. Ethne embodies loyalty in conflict: 'She was bound to him in honour' (016-054) — even after breaking the engagement, she is not free.

## Key Scenes

Sutch's vigil — years of waiting without news — is loyalty as [[endurance]]. When Durrance brings news of Harry's [[redemption]], Sutch reflects that Ethne "was bound to him in honour" (016-054), recognizing that loyalty, like honour, is a bond not easily broken. [[The reconciliation at Glenalla]] is loyalty's vindication: Ethne, who added the fourth feather, is the one who finally receives Harry back, her enduring loyalty outlasting her judgment.
