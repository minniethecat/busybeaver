# Fear

## Definition

Fear in *[[The Four Feathers]]* is distinguished from [[cowardice]]: it is the anticipation of failure, not the failure itself. [[Harry Feversham]]'s condition is not cowardice but "the fear of cowardice" (006-029) — a meta-fear, a dread of what he might become. This is a distinctively modern psychological insight. The novel's Victorian setting does not prevent Mason from rendering with precision a mind trapped in anxious anticipation: "I kept my fear to myself. There was no one to whom I could tell it" (004-067).

## Narrative Role

Fear is the novel's hidden engine. It drives Harry to resign his commission before the test ever arrives; it isolates him from his father, his comrades, and (nearly) from Ethne; it sends him to the Sudan to prove what he should never have needed to prove. The novel's central irony is that Harry's fear is well-founded as psychology but wrong as prediction — he *does* fear cowardice, but he is *not* a coward.

## Embodiment

Harry Feversham carries fear as a private inheritance. His father "when danger came his way... did not foresee" (004-068) — the General is constitutionally incapable of the anticipatory dread that consumes his son. This generational failure of understanding is one of the novel's tragedies: "I know him. When danger came his way, it found him ready, but he did not foresee. That was my trouble always — I foresaw" (004-068).

[[Lieutenant Sutch]], too, knows fear — not of battle but of failing his sacred trust. Sutch "had a fear that the lad meant to leap across" (001-041) — his fear is protective, outward-directed, the fear of a surrogate parent.

## Key Scenes

On the terrace at [[Broad Place]], during one of the General's "Crimean nights," two dreadful stories are told — "one of an officer, the other of a surgeon, who had both shirked" (004-068). Harry takes these stories to bed: "They never left my memory; they became a part of me." This is fear's origin scene — a child hearing what he most dreads to become and internalizing it as prophecy.

In [[the Criterion grillroom]], Harry tells Sutch of his resignation. Sutch's response captures fear's toll: "The nights you must have spent with that fear all alone with you. It makes me shiver to think of them" (006-028).

## Symbolism

Fear has no feather — which is precisely the point. It is invisible, internal, unshareable. The white feathers make visible what has been hidden; Harry's fear, by contrast, remains locked inside him until the [[confession]] at [[Lennon House]]. The novel's power comes from the gap between the external accusation (cowardice) and the internal reality (fear).
