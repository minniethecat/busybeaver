# Cowardice

## Definition

Cowardice in *[[The Four Feathers]]* is less a settled fact than a haunting possibility. For [[Harry Feversham]], it is not that he *is* a coward but that he "foresaw the possibility of cowardice" (004-068). The distinction is crucial: Harry's tragedy is that he is tormented by a failure he has not yet committed, and his [[redemption]] lies in proving that the accusation — however justly sent — was factually wrong. Durrance captures the paradox: "He believed himself a coward, and was not one."

## Narrative Role

The accusation of cowardice is the novel's inciting event and its central mystery. Three white feathers arrive during a ball, "three separate accusations of cowardice" (014-073), and the question they raise — *is Harry Feversham a coward?* — takes the entire novel to answer. The word "cowardice" haunts the text like it haunts Harry: "the [[fear]] of cowardice had sapped incessantly at his heart" (006-029). The novel's deep structure is a trial: accusation, evidence (hidden), verdict (reversed).

## Embodiment

**Harry Feversham** embodies cowardice as self-fulfilling prophecy. His anticipation of failure produces the failure itself — he resigns not because he *is* a coward but because he *fears* being one. This is the novel's most psychologically acute insight: "It was the possibility of cowardice from which he shrank, not the actuality" (015-032).

**The Three Accusers** — Trench, Castleton, Willoughby — embody cowardice as social judgment. They do not know Harry's inner torment; they see only the external fact: a man who resigned on the eve of active service. Their feathers are not cruelty but the [[regiment]]'s impersonal code applied without mercy or nuance.

**[[General Feversham]]** represents cowardice as inherited shame. For him, cowardice is worse than robbery or murder — a stain that dishonours the dead as well as the living.

## Key Scenes

[[The white feather]] delivery (Chapter 4) is cowardice made visible: "Three white feathers fluttered out of the box" (004-038). The return of Willoughby's feather (Chapter 14) is cowardice formally withdrawn: "a tiny, soiled, white feather" held out as proof that "the cowardice was atoned" (021-056). In Chapter 26, the General is told that "Harry never was a coward" — the accusation that began the novel is spoken and dissolved in a single sentence.

## Symbolism

The white feather, drawn from cock-fighting lore where a white feather in a gamecock's tail indicated inferior breeding, becomes the novel's most potent symbol. Three feathers for three accusers, a fourth from Ethne's fan — they are cowardice's material form. Their return, one by one, is cowardice unmade.
