# Major Walters

## Role in the Novel

Major Walters is a [[Suakin]] garrison officer who appears in Chapter 12 during the crisis over [[jack-durrance|Jack Durrance]]'s overdue return from [[the desert]]. His narrative function is to provide an optimistic counterpoint to [[colonel-dawson|Colonel Dawson]]'s pessimism, embodying the hope that Dawson and others struggle to maintain.

## Character

Walters's defining characteristic is his cheerfulness — described explicitly as "his unalterable cheerfulness" (012-024). Where Dawson fears the worst, Walters refuses to abandon hope. He points "out his arm to the white empty desert across the river" (012-010) as evidence that travel westward would have been the truly dangerous route, offering a reasoned basis for optimism. Even when Calder's story of a possible trap shakes the other officers, Walters is "the first to break" the ensuing silence, and "he offered no argument — he simply expressed again his unalterable cheerfulness" (012-024).

## Biography

Walters serves at the Suakin garrison, where he participates in the strategic discussions surrounding reconnaissance operations. When the decision is made to dispatch a search force, he contributes actively: "he at once proceeded to describe the force which he meant to despatch" (012-027). Yet even Walters's optimism is not immune to doubt — after a night spent "pondering over Calder's story," he "leaned across the table" at breakfast with renewed urgency (012-027). In quieter moments, his eyes are fixed "on his plate" (012-029), suggesting the effort required to maintain his hopeful demeanour.

## Relationships

**[[jack-durrance|Jack Durrance]]**: Walters's concern for Durrance is genuine, though expressed through action rather than overt worry. His determination to organise a search force reflects his belief that action is the proper response to [[fear]].

**[[colonel-dawson|Colonel Dawson]]**: The two officers form a complementary pair. Walters's hopefulness is the counterweight to Dawson's gloom, and their dynamic creates the emotional register of Chapter 12 — hope and fear in sustained tension.

## Key Scenes

Walters appears exclusively in Chapter 12, where his role is structural: he keeps hope alive in the garrison's mess-room, ensuring that the dramatic tension over Durrance's fate does not collapse prematurely into [[despair]]. His optimism makes the eventual revelation of Durrance's [[blindness]] — a blow for which no amount of cheerfulness could have prepared anyone — all the more affecting.
