# Mr. Castleton

## Role in the Novel

Castleton occupies a unique position among the three accusers: he is the least developed as a character but the most structurally consequential. It is his telegram — "sent by Castleton" (004-061) from a dinner with a man "likely to know" about the [[regiment]]'s deployment — that triggers Harry's decision to resign. Unlike Trench and Willoughby, Castleton has no deathbed scene and no dramatic feather-return moment; his significance is as the efficient cause of the novel's central crisis.

## Biography

An officer of the [[Royal Surrey Regiment]], Castleton dines with a War Office insider who reveals the regiment's imminent deployment to Egypt. He sends a telegram to Harry with the news, then departs for Scotland. His absence from [[London]] means he cannot correct the misunderstanding when Harry suppresses the news from Trench. He is the third to send a white feather.

## Relationships

**[[Harry Feversham]]**: The telegram Castleton sends, intended as a friendly warning, becomes the instrument of Harry's ruin. The irony is that Castleton meant no harm — he was merely sharing news.

**[[Captain Trench]] and Willoughby**: Fellow officers who join him in sending the feathers.

## Key Scenes

The dinner at which Castleton learns of the deployment and sends the telegram that Harry receives — "A telegram came. It was sent by Castleton" (004-061). The sending of the feather (implied). Castleton's role is structurally analogous to the other accusers: though absent in Scotland, his feather contributes to the judgment that, as the novel notes, was made "for the credit of their regiment" (006-043).
