# Pride

## Definition

Pride in *[[The Four Feathers]]* operates as the novel's emotional engine. It is the force that makes [[the white feather]] an unbearable wound — "Her pride, which had never recovered from the blow which [[Harry Feversham]] had dealt to it" (017-004) — and the force whose restoration marks the novel's resolution. Mason distinguishes between regimental pride (the collective honour of the officer corps), personal pride (the individual's sense of worth), and the pride of love (the pride Ethne feels in those she loves). These three forms intersect and conflict throughout the narrative.

## Narrative Role

Pride is the mechanism by which the accusation of [[cowardice]] achieves its devastating effect. The three white feathers wound because Harry Feversham possesses pride; without it, they would be mere feathers. Ethne's pride in Harry — "her face was tender with pride, and more than tender" (015-049) — is what she loses when he resigns, and what she slowly recovers as his [[redemption]] unfolds. The novel's emotional geography is mapped by pride's loss and return: from the "pride stricken as sorely as her heart" (032-034) to the final restoration where "her pride was restored, and she had a fuller faith" (021-004).

## Varieties

**Regimental Pride** is the collective honour binding British officers. It is impersonal, inherited, and absolute — the code that produces white feathers for anyone who shirks active service. [[General Feversham]] embodies this variety: his pride is indistinguishable from his regiment's honour, and his son's resignation strikes it mortally.

**Personal Pride** is Harry's internal conviction of worth. It is what his father's stories of "death, of hazardous exploits, of the devotion of men to their leaders" (001-015) instilled in him as a child, and what the accusation of cowardice shattered. His redemption is, at root, the reconstruction of personal pride through action rather than inheritance.

**The Pride of Love** is Ethne's pride in those she loves — "the new softness, the new pride, in your voice, the buoyancy" (019-005). It is the most fragile variety, wounded by Harry's resignation, and the last to return, requiring not only proof of courage but the restoration of trust.

## Key Scenes

[[Lieutenant Sutch]], "although he prided himself upon his impartial appreciation of the characters of his friends" (001-051), stands as pride's observer — the one character who sees all three varieties and tracks their fortunes. The General's face "aflush with pride" (001-069) watching his son listen to war stories prefigures the devastation when that pride is broken. Durrance, blind in the Sudan, finds that Harry "was overwhelmed with pride in that Harry Feversham, in spite of his own suffering" (025-064) — a moment where personal pride and the pride of [[friendship]] converge.

## Symbolism

The white feather's power derives entirely from pride. A white feather in a gamecock's tail signalled inferior breeding; to receive one is to be told one's blood is weak. The feather is pride's negative image — where pride is internal worth confirmed, the feather is external worthlessness imposed. Its return, feather by feather, is pride piece by piece recovered.
