# Remorse

## Definition

Remorse in *[[The Four Feathers]]* is the emotion that converts past error into present action. Unlike [[guilt]], which can be passive and paralysing, remorse in Mason's moral economy demands amendment. It is not self-pity but self-correction — "And his remorse was to grow" (027-059) — a growth that propels characters toward restitution rather than retreat.

## Narrative Role

Remorse is the novel's hidden engine during its second half. It operates beneath the surface of the adventure plot (the Sudan campaign, the escape from [[the-house-of-stone]]) and explains why characters act as they do. Ethne's remorse for sending the fourth feather drives her to keep it "as a reminder of her own wrong" (015-033). Durrance's remorse for his silence about his [[blindness]] shapes his treatment of Ethne. And Harry's remorse — "his remorse was to grow, for the night was not done" (027-059) — deepens his commitment to the redemption he has undertaken.

## Embodiment

**[[Ethne Eustace]]** embodies remorse as retrospective justice. "Not for the first time did remorse for that fourth feather which she had added to the three" (015-033) visit her — the fourth feather, taken from her fan and added impulsively, becomes her private burden. Unlike Harry's public shame, Ethne's remorse is entirely internal: "her remorse warned her very clearly" (015-042). She punishes herself with silence and waiting, a penance that mirrors Harry's in duration if not in danger.

**[[Jack Durrance]]** embodies remorse as silence's cost. He "would always have suffered remorse for his silence" (025-070) — the silence about his encroaching blindness, the silence about his love for Ethne. His remorse is the tax on concealment, and it makes him, in the novel's final movement, the agent of Harry's vindication rather than his rival.

**[[Harry Feversham]]** embodies remorse as action. His remorse for his resignation does not express itself in apology but in deeds — "on the contrary it taught him" (032-068). This is the novel's most distinctive moral claim: that remorse rightly understood is not feeling but doing.

## Key Scenes

The moment of Ethne's remorse in Chapter 15 — "remorse came to her because of that day" (018-006) — when she hears of Harry's [[suffering]], is the pivot on which the novel's romantic plot turns. Durrance's [[confession]] to Ethne in Chapter 19 is remorse spoken aloud: "in that outbreak and confession by Durrance" (019-027), remorse becomes language, and language becomes release. Harry's remorse, by contrast, is wordless — it manifests only in the risks he takes, the disguise he assumes, and the years he gives.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Remorse is the psychological bridge between [[cowardice]] and [[redemption]]. Cowardice is the accusation; remorse is the acknowledgment; redemption is the proof. Without remorse, the novel would be a simple adventure tale; with it, it becomes a study in moral causality — how an internal state (regret) produces external acts ([[courage]]), and how those acts, witnessed and recorded, rewrite a man's story.
