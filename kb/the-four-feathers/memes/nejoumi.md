# Wad El Nejoumi

## Role in the Novel

Wad El Nejoumi is the face of Mahdist power in the novel's Sudan chapters. As Emir of [[Omdurman]], he holds [[Harry Feversham]] captive and subjects him to a psychologically acute interrogation. "A white prisoner, captured at the [[Battle of Omdurman]], was brought into the house of the Emir Wad El Nejoumi" (020-009) — his entrance is the novel's most direct confrontation between British [[honour]] and Mahdist authority. Unlike the faceless Arab enemies of imperial adventure fiction, Nejoumi is given a personality: cunning, theatrical, capable of both cruelty and a kind of grudging respect.

## Biography

An emir in the army of the [[the-mahdi|Mahdi]], Nejoumi governs Omdurman with the authority of religious conviction and the pragmatism of a military commander. He interrogates Harry about his identity and intentions, offering alternately threats and inducements. "Nejoumi made a sign" (020-022) — a single gesture that can mean [[death]] or reprieve. His power is absolute within his domain, and Harry's survival depends on navigating Nejoumi's shifting moods.

## Key Scenes

The interrogation scene in Nejoumi's house is the novel's most sustained set-piece of the Sudan chapters. Nejoumi tests Harry's disguise, probes his story, and offers the choice between conversion to Islam and death. "Feversham, stubbornly, and Nejoumi took a different tone" (020-024) — the shift from threat to persuasion reveals Nejoumi as more than a simple villain: he is curious about this strange white prisoner, and that curiosity keeps Harry alive.

## Relationships

**Harry Feversham**: Captor and prisoner. Their relationship is adversarial but complex — Nejoumi recognizes something unusual in Harry's bearing and is intrigued despite himself. The power imbalance is total, yet Harry's composure under interrogation earns a measure of respect.

**The Mahdi**: Subordinate and leader. Nejoumi rules Omdurman in the Mahdi's name, his authority deriving from religious hierarchy. His zeal is the novel's reminder that the Mahdist movement was not merely military but spiritual — a war of [[faith]] as well as territory.
