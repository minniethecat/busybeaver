# Rank

## Overview

Rank — the system of social hierarchy that organizes Austen's world — is both omnipresent and rarely named directly in *Sense and Sensibility*. Characters constantly assess and are assessed by their rank, yet the word itself appears infrequently. Austen's treatment of rank is characteristically subtle: she accepts its reality while exposing its absurdities, and she measures her characters by how they navigate a hierarchy they did not create.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel opens by establishing the Dashwood family's rank through their estate: "The family of Dashwood had long been settled in Sussex. Their estate was large" (001-001). This is the language of established gentry — not the highest rank, but solid, respectable, and rooted in land. The Dashwood women's fall from Norland to Barton Cottage is a fall in rank as well as fortune.

Lady Middleton exemplifies rank consciousness. Her entire social life is organized around the maintenance of proper distance: she is civil to the Dashwoods because they are gently born, but she is not warm because they are poor. Her rank determines her hospitality but not her humanity. Sir John Middleton, by contrast, ignores rank in favor of sociability — he invites everyone to Barton Park because he likes company, not because he has calculated their social utility.

The novel's treatment of the Ferrars family exposes rank as a weapon. Mrs. Ferrars disinherits Edward for refusing to marry according to her ambitions; she uses rank (and the fortune that sustains it) to coerce her children's choices. Lucy Steele's campaign to marry into the Ferrars family is a study in rank aspiration — she navigates the hierarchy with cunning because she has no legitimate place within it.

## Key Passages

## Analysis

Austen neither endorses nor rejects rank — she treats it as a fact to be navigated. Her moral judgment falls not on the hierarchy itself but on how characters behave within it. The virtuous characters (Elinor, Colonel Brandon, Edward) treat those below them with respect; the vicious characters (Mrs. Ferrars, Fanny Dashwood) use rank as a club. The novel's happy ending does not abolish rank — Elinor becomes mistress of Delaford parsonage, Marianne mistress of Delaford itself — but it places virtuous characters in positions where their rank enables rather than constrains their goodness. In Austen, the right response to hierarchy is not to destroy it but to inhabit it humanely.
