## Overview

Mr. Palmer is one of Austen's most economically drawn characters — a man whose entire personality can be summarized in a single trait: determined disagreeableness. He is wealthy, well-bred, and not stupid, but he has decided that the world does not deserve his attention, and he treats everyone — his wife, his mother-in-law, his guests — with a studied rudeness that he seems to consider a form of superiority. His wife Charlotte's cheerful imperviousness to his contempt creates one of the novel's most darkly comic dynamics (008-001).

## Biography

Mr. Palmer is a gentleman of fortune who has married Charlotte Jennings, the younger daughter of Mrs. Jennings, apparently for her pretty face, which he has since ceased to notice. He spends his time reading newspapers, ignoring his wife, and being rude to visitors. His estate, Cleveland, serves as the setting for Marianne's near-fatal illness — and, ironically, this thoroughly disagreeable man proves an unexpectedly competent host when crisis strikes.

## Relationships

**Mrs. Palmer (Charlotte)** — The novel's most puzzling marriage. Mr. Palmer treats his wife with open contempt — ignoring her conversation, contradicting her statements, and generally behaving as if her existence is an inconvenience. Charlotte's response — to find all of this amusing and to report his rudeness to others as evidence of his wit — is either saintly forbearance or profound self-deception.

**Mrs. Jennings** — Mr. Palmer treats his mother-in-law with barely veiled disdain, which Mrs. Jennings, with characteristic insensibility, either does not notice or does not mind.

**The Dashwood Sisters** — During Marianne's illness at Cleveland, Mr. Palmer's behavior is unexpectedly decent. He arranges for medical care, provides the house, and shows a practical competence that his usual manner conceals. The crisis reveals that his rudeness is a style, not a substance — underneath it, he is capable of acting rightly.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Palmer represents a peculiarly English type of masculine dysfunction — the man who has decided that feeling is foolish and whose defense against emotion is a pose of ironic detachment. He is, in effect, the opposite of Colonel Brandon: where Brandon feels deeply and expresses quietly, Palmer may feel little and expresses nothing at all. His marriage to Charlotte is the novel's most unsettling portrait of what happens when neither party expects happiness.
