## Overview

Stanhill is a minor but telling location — the house where Mrs. Dashwood and her daughters lived before Henry Dashwood's inheritance of Norland Park. Its sale, which contributes to the ten thousand pounds that constitutes the Dashwood women's entire fortune, is one of the financial details that establishes their precarious situation. Stanhill represents the modest but independent life the Dashwood women enjoyed before their father's death made them dependent on others.

## Description

Stanhill is described only through its financial function — a property that, when sold, produces a portion of the capital that must sustain Mrs. Dashwood and her three daughters. Its very obscurity — Austen never describes it, never locates it precisely — reflects its narrative function as a marker of past stability rather than present reality.

## Significance

Stanhill is the novel's quietest place — barely mentioned, never visited, yet essential to the Dashwood women's economic identity. It represents the independence that Henry Dashwood's second marriage provided and that his death destroyed. The sale of Stanhill, like the removal from Norland, is another step in the Dashwood women's progressive economic diminishment — from independent household to dependent relations.
