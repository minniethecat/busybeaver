# Propriety

## Overview

Propriety — the quality of conforming to socially accepted standards of behavior — functions in *Sense and Sensibility* as both a moral compass and a social mask. Characters are constantly judged by their adherence to or deviation from what is "proper," yet Austen demonstrates that propriety can serve self-interest as readily as virtue. The question the novel poses is not whether to be proper, but what kind of propriety one practices.

## In Sense and Sensibility

John Dashwood is introduced as a man who "conducted himself with propriety in the discharge of his ordinary duties" (001-007) — yet this same propriety enables him to rationalize abandoning his sisters to genteel poverty. His propriety is the thinnest veneer over selfishness. Austen's irony is precise: the word praises while the narrative condemns.

In contrast, Elinor's propriety is genuine self-command in service of others. When Mrs. Dashwood wishes to leave Norland immediately after Mrs. John Dashwood's insulting arrival, "the entreaty of her eldest girl induced her first to reflect on the propriety of going" (001-010). Elinor invokes propriety not to suppress feeling but to prevent a rash action that would harm the family's interests.

The novel also explores the tyranny of propriety. Marianne's refusal to dissemble — her insistence that feeling should be expressed, not concealed — is presented as a critique of a social code that demands emotional dishonesty. Her eventual illness and recovery suggest that pure propriety (Elinor) and pure expressiveness (Marianne) are both incomplete; wisdom lies in integrating the two.

## Key Passages

> He was not an ill-disposed young man, unless to be rather cold hearted and rather selfish is to be ill-disposed: but he was, in general, well respected; for he conducted himself with propriety in the discharge of his ordinary duties. (001-007)

> So acutely did Mrs. Dashwood feel this ungracious behaviour, and so earnestly did she despise her daughter-in-law for it, that, on the arrival of the latter, she would have quitted the house for ever, had not the entreaty of her eldest girl induced her first to reflect on the propriety of going. (001-010)

## Analysis

Propriety in Austen is never merely about manners. It is the visible expression of a character's moral understanding. John Dashwood's propriety is hollow because it serves only himself; Elinor's propriety is substantive because it serves others. Lucy Steele presents the most troubling case — her propriety is flawless in form but entirely strategic in substance. She uses the language of proper feeling as a weapon. The novel thus distinguishes between propriety as moral substance and propriety as social performance, and it is the former alone that Austen endorses.
