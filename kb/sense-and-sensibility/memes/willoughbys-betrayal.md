## Overview

[[john-willoughby|John Willoughby's]] public rejection of [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne Dashwood]] at a [[london]] ball is the emotional catastrophe at the heart of *Sense and Sensibility*. After a whirlwind courtship in Devonshire — complete with shared literary tastes, musical duets, and his dramatic rescue of Marianne after she twisted her ankle — Willoughby departs abruptly for London without explanation. When Marianne follows to London as the guest of [[mrs-jennings]], she discovers that the man she considered her betrothed has attached himself to a wealthy heiress and treats her with cold, public indifference. The betrayal shatters Marianne's romantic worldview and sets in motion her near-fatal illness and eventual moral transformation.

## Background

Willoughby had entered Marianne's life like a figure from her beloved Romantic poetry: handsome, passionate, and seemingly attuned to every nuance of her sensibility. Their intimacy developed with a speed that shocked [[elinor-dashwood|Elinor]] — they exchanged locks of hair, toured [[allenham|Allenham]] together unchaperoned, and addressed each other by Christian names — behavior that suggested an engagement, though none had been formally declared (009-013)(009-023).

When Willoughby suddenly left Devonshire for London, Marianne assumed he would write. Her letters went unanswered. Her anxiety, then desperation, grew with each silent day. The revelation that awaited her in London was far worse than neglect — it was calculated rejection.

## Description

The confrontation occurred at a London ball. Marianne, having spent weeks waiting for any sign from Willoughby, finally encountered him in a public setting. Expecting the warm lover of Devonshire, she found instead a cold stranger:

> He approached her, but without any symptom of peculiar pleasure, and without speaking; merely inquired after her family, and looked round him, as if in search of some person who might relieve him from the trouble of talking to her. (028-014)

Marianne's reaction was everything her philosophy of [[sensibility]] demanded — immediate, passionate, and public:

> Starting up, she turned to Elinor, and with a look of the most acute distress, exclaimed, "Good God! he is there — he is there. Oh! why does he not look at me? why cannot I speak to him?" (028-004)

Willoughby's subsequent letter — cold, formal, and denying any attachment — completed the destruction. He returned her letters and the lock of hair she had given him, claiming he had never intended to create an impression of serious regard. The cruelty was compounded by what Marianne knew: that their intimacy had been real, that the understanding between them was mutual, and that Willoughby's sudden reversal was motivated by [[fortune]] — he had chosen Miss Grey's fifty thousand pounds over Marianne's five hundred a year.

## Consequences

The immediate consequence is Marianne's emotional devastation, expressed in the reckless behavior that leads to her illness at [[cleveland]] — walking in wet grass, neglecting her health, indulging the grief that her philosophy of sensibility demands must be felt fully.

The deeper consequence is philosophical. Marianne's entire worldview — that authentic feeling is the measure of a person's worth, that passionate souls recognize each other — fails the test of Willoughby's betrayal. The man she judged worthy of her love proved incapable of the sacrifice that love required. This forces Marianne to reconsider not just Willoughby but the very criteria by which she judges character — opening the path to her eventual [[marriage]] to [[colonel-brandon|Colonel Brandon]], a man whose quiet depth she had dismissed.

Willoughby's later confession to Elinor during Marianne's illness at Cleveland adds a final, unsettling layer: he truly loved Marianne. His choice of Miss Grey was not indifference but weakness — he could not bear the financial constraints that marrying Marianne would impose. This revelation, while not excusing his conduct, suggests that Marianne's romantic judgment was not entirely mistaken, and that the tragedy lies not in mistaken love but in the collision between genuine feeling and economic necessity (004-006).

> He had left the girl whose youth and innocence he had seduced, in a situation of the utmost distress, with no creditable home, no help, no friends! (031-012)
