# Fortune

## Overview

Fortune — meaning both wealth and the luck of circumstance — is the invisible hand that moves every character in *Sense and Sensibility*. From the opening pages, where the Dashwood women are disinherited by the rules of primogeniture, to the final resolutions where [[marriage|marriages]] secure (or fail to secure) financial stability, money is never absent from Austen's moral calculus. The novel insists that character cannot be understood apart from economic circumstance: [[elinor-dashwood|Elinor's]] [[sense]] is tested by poverty, [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne's]] [[sensibility]] is shattered by [[john-willoughby|Willoughby's]] mercenary choice, and even [[colonel-brandon|Brandon's]] generosity depends on the two thousand pounds a year that [[delaford]] provides.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel's central crisis is economic. Mr. Henry Dashwood, on his deathbed, extracts a promise from his son [[john-dashwood|John]] to "increase the fortunes of his sisters by the present of a thousand pounds a-piece" (001-008). John's subsequent retreat from this promise — abetted by his wife's relentless logic — reduces the Dashwood women from gentlefolk to dependents on five hundred pounds a year. The scene in which Mrs. John Dashwood talks her husband down from three thousand pounds to an occasional gift of fish is one of Austen's most devastating portrayals of how economic self-interest corrupts moral obligation.

Fortune shapes romantic prospects directly. [[edward-ferrars|Edward Ferrars's]] mother disinherits him for refusing to marry a wealthy woman; [[john-willoughby|Willoughby]] jilts [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne]] to marry Miss Grey and her fifty thousand pounds; [[colonel-brandon|Brandon's]] rival "had left the country" on discovering that his beloved's fortune was smaller than expected. Love without fortune is precarious; fortune without love is hollow.

## Analysis

Austen is unusually specific about money. Characters' incomes are stated precisely — five hundred pounds a year for the Dashwood women, two thousand for [[colonel-brandon]], fifty thousand for Miss Grey. These figures are not decorative; they are the material conditions that make certain choices possible and others impossible. [[elinor-dashwood|Elinor's]] [[sense]] and [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne's]] [[sensibility]] operate within sharply drawn economic constraints. The novel's moral vision is inseparable from its economic realism: virtue requires not just good intentions but the material means to act on them. The Dashwood sisters' reduced circumstances test their characters in ways that comfort never could — and [[colonel-brandon|Brandon's]] gift of the [[delaford]] living to [[edward-ferrars]] demonstrates that true generosity requires both a good heart and the fortune to express it.
