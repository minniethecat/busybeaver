## Overview

Lucy Steele's marriage to Robert Ferrars is one of the most consequential plot twists in *Sense and Sensibility*. For over four years, Lucy had been secretly engaged to Edward Ferrars, the elder son of Mrs. Ferrars. When Mrs. Ferrars discovered the engagement, she disinherited Edward and settled the family fortune on his younger brother Robert. In an audacious reversal, Lucy promptly transferred her affections — and her ambitions — to Robert, marrying him instead. This event simultaneously frees Edward from an entanglement he had long regretted and demonstrates Lucy's mercenary character in its fullest expression.

## Background

Lucy Steele's secret engagement to Edward Ferrars had been the hidden obstacle to any union between Edward and Elinor Dashwood. The engagement, formed when Edward was a young man under the tutelage of Lucy's uncle, had become a source of misery for Edward, who "had long ceased to love" Lucy (049-003) but felt bound by honor to maintain the commitment. When Mrs. Ferrars discovered the engagement, she demanded that Edward break it off; his refusal led to his disinheritance and the transfer of the Ferrars fortune to his younger brother Robert (037-041).

## Description

The news of Lucy's marriage reaches the Dashwood family at Barton Cottage through an unexpected messenger — Edward Ferrars himself. In a scene of masterfully controlled dramatic irony, Edward arrives, and the family inadvertently presses him about "Mrs. Ferrars," meaning his mother:

> "Perhaps you mean—my brother—you mean Mrs.—Mrs. Robert Ferrars." (048-021)

The revelation that Lucy had married Robert, not Edward, produces "unspeakable astonishment by all but Elinor, who sat with her head leaning over her work, in a state of such agitation as made her hardly know where she was" (048-024). Elinor's silent turmoil — the sudden release from years of concealed suffering — is the emotional core of the scene.

The marriage itself reveals Lucy's character in its most cynical light. Having secured Edward's engagement as a claim on the Ferrars family, she simply transferred that claim to the brother who now held the fortune. As the narrator later reflects, Lucy had "given a rout to the whole family" by securing "the son who had the fortune" (050-013). Her marriage to Robert Ferrars completed her social ascent from obscure Steele niece to the mistress of the Ferrars inheritance.

## Consequences

The marriage is the device that resolves the novel's central romantic obstacle. Edward, "released without any reproach to himself, from an entanglement which had long formed his misery, from a woman whom he had long ceased to love," is immediately "elevated at once to that security with another, which he must have thought of almost with despair" (049-003). Within hours of his arrival at Barton, he proposes to Elinor and is accepted.

Beyond the plot mechanics, the marriage provides a sharp moral contrast. Lucy's mercenary pragmatism — her willingness to trade one brother for another when the financial calculus shifted — stands in opposition to both Marianne's passionate sensibility and Elinor's principled self-government. Austen uses Lucy as the novel's darkest portrait of social ambition untempered by affection or honor.

The marriage also completes a pattern of ironic justice: Robert Ferrars, the vain and foolish younger son who had been set up as superior to Edward, becomes the husband of a woman whose character Edward had long recognized and despised. As the narrator notes with characteristic dryness, Lucy "persevered in her determination, and soon made her appearance in London as Mrs. Robert Ferrars" (050-013), the "Mrs. Ferrars" that everyone had expected Elinor to become.
