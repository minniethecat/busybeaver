# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in *Sense and Sensibility* is far more than a romantic conclusion — it is the central economic and social institution around which the novel's entire plot revolves. For the [[elinor-dashwood|Dashwood sisters]], marriage determines not only happiness but survival; without [[fortune|fortunes]] of their own, a favorable marriage is their only route to security. Austen examines marriage from multiple angles: as economic contract, as romantic ideal, as social performance, and as moral test. The novel's closing marriages — [[elinor-dashwood|Elinor]] to [[edward-ferrars]] at [[delaford]], [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne]] to [[colonel-brandon]] — represent the synthesis of [[sense]] and [[sensibility]] that authentic partnership requires.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel opens by foregrounding marriage as a legal-economic structure. The Dashwood sisters are impoverished precisely because of marriage law: "By a former marriage, Mr. Henry Dashwood had one son: by his present lady, three daughters" (001-002). The son inherits everything; the daughters are dependent on their half-brother's generosity — which proves disastrously inadequate. This opening establishes marriage not as a romantic ideal but as the mechanism of economic distribution that structures the entire social order.

[[mrs-jennings]] embodies the novel's satirical perspective on marriage as social sport. Having married off her own daughters, "she had now therefore nothing to do but to marry all the rest of the world" (008-001). Her relentless matchmaking — centered on her [[london]] establishment — provides comic relief while underscoring the serious economic pressures that make marriage a necessity rather than a choice for women without [[fortune]].

[[marianne-dashwood|Marianne]] articulates the romantic counter-argument — that marriage without love is degrading. She declares that a woman of twenty-seven "can never hope to feel or inspire affection again, and if her home be uncomfortable, or her fortune small, I can suppose that she might bring herself to submit to the offices of a nurse, for the sake of the provision and security of a wife" (008-010). Her contempt for mercenary marriage is genuine, yet the novel also shows the cost of her romantic absolutism when [[john-willoughby|Willoughby's]] desertion forces her to confront the economic realities she has disdained.

## Key Passages

> By a former marriage, Mr. Henry Dashwood had one son: by his present lady, three daughters. The son, a steady respectable young man, was amply provided for by the fortune of his mother, which had been large, and half of which devolved on him on his coming of age. (001-002)

> Mrs. Jennings was a widow with an ample jointure. She had only two daughters, both of whom she had lived to see respectably married, and she had now therefore nothing to do but to marry all the rest of the world. (008-001)

> No sooner did she perceive any symptom of love in his behaviour to Elinor, than she considered their serious attachment as certain, and looked forward to their marriage as rapidly approaching. (003-014)

> "He must have a pretty good income, I suppose." "About two thousand a year." "Two thousand a year!" — and then, for a moment, she was silenced. (008-005)

## Analysis

The novel presents a spectrum of marriages: the mercenary ([[lucy-steele|Lucy Steele's]] pursuit of [[robert-ferrars]]), the imprudent (Mr. Dashwood's first marriage that produced an ungenerous son), the pragmatic (Charlotte Palmer's cheerful endurance of a foolish husband), and the ideal ([[elinor-dashwood|Elinor]] and [[edward-ferrars]], [[marianne-dashwood|Marianne]] and [[colonel-brandon]] — unions of affection moderated by prudence). Austen's genius is to show that the romantic and the economic are never truly separable: every marriage in the novel is both a union of hearts and a transfer of [[fortune|property]]. The happy marriages are those in which the partners acknowledge both dimensions — building a life at [[delaford]] rather than pining for [[norland-park]], choosing steady affection over theatrical passion.
