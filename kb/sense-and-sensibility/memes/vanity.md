# Vanity

## Overview

Vanity — excessive pride in one's appearance, accomplishments, or status — appears throughout *Sense and Sensibility* as a minor vice that generates major consequences. From John Dashwood's vanity about his income to Robert Ferrars's vanity about his taste in toothpick-cases, Austen uses vanity to expose the moral emptiness that often hides behind social respectability.

## In Sense and Sensibility

John Dashwood's vanity is the novel's most consequential example. His self-image as a generous man is entirely at odds with his behavior — he gives his sisters nothing while congratulating himself on his good intentions. His vanity is the mechanism by which selfishness disguises itself as virtue: he believes he is generous, therefore he need not actually be generous.

Robert Ferrars represents vanity in its most comic form. His extended disquisition on the design of a toothpick-case is pure performance — the assertion of refined taste as social currency. Yet his vanity has real consequences: his arrogance makes him vulnerable to Lucy Steele's flattery, and he marries her unaware that she is merely transferring her ambitions from one brother to another.

Lady Middleton's vanity is maternal: her children are "the sweetest children in the world" (006-008) and her entire identity is absorbed in their cultivation. This vanity, while less harmful than John Dashwood's, produces a corresponding blindness — she cannot see her children as they are, only as reflections of herself.

## Key Passages

## Analysis

Austen treats vanity with more humor than condemnation. Unlike the great vices — cruelty, avarice, deceit — vanity is almost endearing in its transparency. The vain character is too busy admiring himself to do serious harm; his self-absorption acts as a kind of moral quarantine. Yet vanity has real costs. John Dashwood's vanity enables his neglect of his sisters; Robert Ferrars's vanity makes him a dupe. The novel suggests that vanity is not a serious sin but a serious limitation — it prevents the self-knowledge that makes moral growth possible.
