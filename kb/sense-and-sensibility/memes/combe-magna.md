## Overview

Combe Magna is Willoughby's estate, a property that should represent stability but instead, under Willoughby's management, represents dissolution. His inherited wealth, like his inherited charm, is squandered on pleasure rather than husbanded for the future. The estate's name — "Combe Magna," suggesting a great valley — carries a hint of the romantic landscape that suits Willoughby's sensibility, but the reality of his financial mismanagement undermines the picturesque surface.

## Description

Combe Magna is substantial enough to sustain a gentleman — Willoughby keeps horses, a carriage, and a hunting establishment — but his expenditure exceeds his income. The house itself is glimpsed only indirectly, through references to its stables, its grounds, and the lifestyle it supports. What matters is not its appearance but its economic insufficiency: Willoughby needs more money than Combe Magna provides, and this need drives his decision to marry the wealthy Miss Grey rather than the portionless Marianne.

## Residents

**[[john-willoughby]]** is the master of Combe Magna, and his character shapes the estate as much as the estate shapes his situation. His fondness for horses, hunting, and expensive tastes consumes the estate's income, creating a financial pressure that his aunt's conditional generosity partially relieves. When his aunt discovers his seduction of Eliza Williams and disinherits him, the financial imperative to marry money becomes absolute.

## Key Events

Combe Magna is never directly visited in the novel — it exists as a destination, a symbol of what Willoughby possesses and what his choices imperil. The estate's distance from Barton, combined with Willoughby's sudden departure, fuels the mystery of his intentions toward Marianne. The revelation that Mrs. Smith, Willoughby's aunt and benefactor, has the power to disinherit him if he marries imprudently connects Combe Magna directly to the novel's central romantic crisis.

Willoughby's confession to Elinor at Cleveland — in which he admits that his need to maintain Combe Magna drove him to marry Miss Grey — transforms the estate from a symbol of romantic possibility into one of moral failure.

## Significance

Combe Magna represents the intersection of wealth and character in Austen's moral landscape. The estate itself is not corrupting — it is the relationship between the owner and his property that determines its moral valence. Willoughby's failure to live within his means, to value love over comfort, and to subordinate his desires to his duties transforms Combe Magna from a country seat into a burden — the visible sign of his enslavement to money over love.
