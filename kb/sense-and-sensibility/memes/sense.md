# Sense

## Overview

Sense, in Jane Austen's *Sense and Sensibility*, denotes the faculty of sound judgment, rational self-command, and prudent conduct. It is the quality most fully embodied in Elinor Dashwood and stands as the thematic counterweight to the "sensibility" represented by her sister Marianne. The novel's title proposes a conjunction — *Sense and Sensibility* — suggesting that neither quality alone suffices for a well-lived life.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel opens by establishing the Dashwood family's circumstances, immediately introducing the tension between feeling and judgment. When Mrs. John Dashwood arrives at Norland without notice, Elinor's mother is ready to leave at once — but "the entreaty of her eldest girl induced her first to reflect on the propriety of going" (001-010). This early moment establishes Elinor as the voice of sense, tempering impulse with reflection.

Elinor's sense is not coldness but a disciplined restraint. When speaking of Edward Ferrars, she declares: "Of his sense and his goodness, no one can, I think, be in doubt, who has seen him often enough to engage him in unreserved conversation" (004-009). Her choice of "sense" as the first quality she praises reveals her own values — she prizes understanding and principle above charm or expressiveness.

The novel's youngest sister, Margaret, provides an early contrast: "she had already imbibed a good deal of Marianne's romance, without having much of her sense" (001-014). This juxtaposition frames sense as something distinct from romantic enthusiasm — a quality of mind rather than of feeling.

## Key Passages

> No sooner was his father's funeral over, than Mrs. John Dashwood, without sending any notice of her intention to her mother-in-law, arrived with her child and their attendants. No one could dispute her right to come; the house was her husband's from the moment of his father's decease; but the indelicacy of her conduct was so much the greater, and to a woman in Mrs. Dashwood's situation, with only common feelings, must have been highly unpleasing. (001-009)

> "Had he been in his right senses, he could not have thought of such a thing as begging you to give away half your fortune from your own child." (002-005)

## Analysis

Austen does not present sense as unambiguously superior to sensibility. Elinor's sense enables her to navigate social danger and financial precarity with dignity, but it also requires her to conceal her own suffering — most painfully when she must keep Lucy Steele's secret about her engagement to Edward. The novel's resolution rewards both sisters: Marianne learns to value sense, while Elinor's long-suppressed feelings are finally given their due. Sense, Austen suggests, is necessary but not sufficient — it must be animated by genuine feeling to constitute a complete moral life.
