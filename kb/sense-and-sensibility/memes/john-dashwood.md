## Overview

John Dashwood is a study in moral mediocrity — not actively cruel, but weak; not intentionally selfish, but easily persuaded to selfishness by his wife Fanny. His inheritance of Norland Park and his subsequent failure to provide for his stepmother and half-sisters, despite his deathbed promise to his father, illustrates Austen's insight that moral failure often takes the form of incremental compromise rather than dramatic villainy (001-006).

## Biography

As Henry Dashwood's son by his first marriage, John inherits the entirety of Norland Park, which is entailed in the male line. His father's dying wish is that John provide for his stepmother and three half-sisters. John initially resolves to give them three thousand pounds — a generous sum. But under Fanny's influence, this amount is reduced step by step: three thousand becomes "occasional assistance," then "an annuity," then "neighbourly presents of game and fruit," until it dwindles to nothing at all (002-018)(002-026).

## Relationships

**Fanny Dashwood** — John's wife, a woman of "a strong caricature" of his own character — more narrow-minded, more selfish, and far more effective at getting her way. John is thoroughly dominated by Fanny, whose arguments against providing for the Dashwood women he finds increasingly persuasive because they align with his own inclination toward ease (001-007).

**Elinor and Marianne** — His half-sisters, whom he genuinely likes but does not love enough to inconvenience himself for. His later satisfaction that Marianne's marriage to Colonel Brandon relieves him of any residual guilt about his failure to provide for her is characteristically self-serving (033-007).

## Key Scenes

The opening chapters' negotiation between John and Fanny Dashwood over how much to give the Dashwood women is one of Austen's most brilliantly satirical passages. John's initial generous impulse is eroded by Fanny's relentless logic of self-interest: "Why should they have more than enough? They will have ten thousand pounds divided amongst them. If they marry, they will be provided for" (002-026). The scene is a masterclass in how greed rationalizes itself.

## Character Analysis

John Dashwood is not a villain — he is something more realistic and, in Austen's view, more common: a man whose moral sense functions only when it costs him nothing. He feels genuine affection for his sisters and genuine guilt about his failure to help them. But feeling is not action, and his feeling changes nothing. He represents the comfortable conscience — the man who knows what is right and allows himself to be persuaded out of doing it.
