## Overview

The move to Barton Cottage marks the definitive break between the Dashwood women and their former life at Norland Park. After the death of Mr. Henry Dashwood, his widow and three daughters — Elinor, Marianne, and Margaret — find themselves increasingly unwelcome at Norland under the new ownership of John Dashwood and his wife Fanny. An unexpected offer from a distant cousin, Sir John Middleton, provides them with a cottage in Devonshire, setting the entire narrative in motion by relocating the heroines to a new social world.

## Background

The Dashwood family had lived at Norland Park for generations. Upon the old gentleman's death, the estate passed by entail to Mr. Henry Dashwood's son from his first marriage, John Dashwood (001-003). Although John Dashwood had promised his dying father he would assist his stepmother and half-sisters, his wife Fanny systematically persuaded him that any substantial help was unnecessary (002-024). Within months, the Dashwood women were reduced to living on an income of £500 per year while remaining as guests at Norland — a position that grew increasingly uncomfortable under Fanny's cold treatment.

## Description

The turning point came when Mrs. Dashwood received a letter from her cousin, Sir John Middleton of Barton Park in Devonshire. His letter offered a small house on generous terms:

> He understood that she was in need of a dwelling; and though the house he now offered her was merely a cottage, he assured her that everything should be done to it which she might think necessary, if the situation pleased her. He earnestly pressed her, after giving the particulars of the house and garden, to come with her daughters to Barton Park, the place of his own residence, from whence she might judge, herself, whether Barton Cottage, for the houses were in the same parish, could, by any alteration, be made comfortable to her. (004-020)

Mrs. Dashwood accepted immediately, her decision driven by her desire to escape Fanny's company:

> To quit the neighbourhood of Norland was no longer an evil; it was an object of desire; it was a blessing, in comparison of the misery of continuing her daughter-in-law's guest; and to remove for ever from that beloved place would be less painful than to inhabit or visit it while such a woman was its mistress. (004-020)

Elinor approved of the prudence of the move, noting that the cottage was "on so simple a scale, and the rent so uncommonly moderate, as to leave her no right of objection" (004-021). When Mrs. Dashwood announced the plan to John and Fanny Dashwood, Edward Ferrars, who happened to be visiting, was visibly distressed to hear they were moving so far — to Devonshire (005-001). Mrs. Dashwood pointedly invited Edward to visit them there, defying Fanny's disapproval of any attachment between Edward and Elinor (005-003).

Before departing, Mrs. Dashwood sold the carriage and horses that her late husband had left her, at Elinor's practical urging, and reduced the household to just three servants (005-005). John Dashwood, despite his earlier promises to his father, offered no financial assistance for the move, "his assistance extended no farther than their maintenance for six months at Norland" (005-006).

The journey to Devonshire was initially melancholy, but the family's spirits lifted as they entered Barton Valley:

> A view of Barton Valley as they entered it gave them cheerfulness. It was a pleasant fertile spot, well wooded, and rich in pasture. (006-001)

Barton Cottage itself, though modest, proved comfortable. Austen's narrator provides an exact description:

> As a house, Barton Cottage, though small, was comfortable and compact; but as a cottage it was defective, for the building was regular, the roof was tiled, the window shutters were not painted green, nor were the walls covered with honeysuckles. (006-002)

The cottage had four bedrooms, two garrets, and two sitting rooms flanking the entrance. Set against high hills and overlooking the valley, with the village of Barton on a nearby hill forming "a pleasant view from the cottage windows" (006-003), the Dashwood women made their new home.

## Consequences

The move to Barton Cottage is one of the most consequential events in the novel. It physically relocates the Dashwood sisters to a new social environment — the neighborhood of Barton Park, where they meet Colonel Brandon (007-006), Sir John Middleton's circle of acquaintances, and most critically, John Willoughby (009-010). Without this move, the central romantic entanglements of the plot — Marianne's passionate attachment to Willoughby and subsequent heartbreak, Elinor's quiet endurance of Edward's secret engagement, and the eventual settlement of both sisters at Delaford — could not have unfolded. The cottage's proximity to Allenham (Willoughby's temporary residence) and its distance from Norland (where Edward remained tied by family obligations) create the geographical tensions that drive the novel's emotional architecture.

The move also crystallizes the economic vulnerability of the Dashwood women. Their reduction from the estate class to mere cottage-dwellers illustrates the precarious position of widows and daughters in a system of primogeniture and entail — a theme that runs throughout the novel. As Mrs. Dashwood herself reflected, "though the house he now offered her was merely a cottage" (004-020), even this modest dwelling was dependent on the goodwill of a male relative.
