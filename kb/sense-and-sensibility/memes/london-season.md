## Overview

The London season is the emotional climax of *Sense and Sensibility*'s second volume. Elinor and Marianne travel to London in January as the guests of Mrs. Jennings. Marianne arrives full of expectation of seeing Willoughby, whom she secretly believes herself engaged to. Instead, she experiences a series of devastating disappointments — Willoughby's coldness at a party, his public snub, and finally the letter returning her correspondence and announcing his engagement to the wealthy Miss Grey. The London season transforms Marianne's romantic idealism into bitter disillusionment.

## Background

After settling at Barton Cottage, the Dashwood sisters formed an acquaintance with Mrs. Jennings, Sir John Middleton's mother-in-law. Mrs. Jennings was a wealthy widow who "had resided every winter in a house in one of the streets near Portman Square" (025-001). Her offer to bring the Miss Dashwoods to London was characteristically blunt:

> "I do beg you will favour me with your company, for I've quite set my heart upon it... if I don't get one of you at least well married before I have done with you, it shall not be my fault. I shall speak a good word for you to all the young men, you may depend upon it." (025-002)

Mrs. Dashwood, in a moment of characteristic optimism, encouraged her daughters to go, declaring she "would have every young woman of your condition in life acquainted with the manners and amusements of London" (025-008). Marianne, already deeply attached to Willoughby, saw the trip as an opportunity to be near him.

## Description

The journey to London took three days by carriage. Marianne's behaviour during the journey revealed her state of mind — she "sat in silence almost all the way, wrapt in her own meditations" (026-002) — while Elinor shouldered the burden of maintaining civility with their hostess.

The critical moment came on their first evening in London. Marianne wrote a note and sent it by the two-penny post; Elinor "thought she could distinguish a large W in the direction" (026-005) and concluded that Marianne and Willoughby must be secretly engaged. That evening, Marianne waited in an agony of anticipation:

> She could scarcely eat any dinner, and when they afterwards returned to the drawing room, seemed anxiously listening to the sound of every carriage. (026-006)

When a knock finally came, Marianne rushed to the door "in the ecstasy of her feelings" — only to find Colonel Brandon, not Willoughby (026-007).

Days passed without word from Willoughby, though Marianne "had seen all the particulars of the day, and had seen them with the advantage of a mind wholly unoccupied by any sensation of congenial pleasure, except in the present expectation of seeing Willoughby" (027-001). When they finally encountered him at a party, his behaviour was cold and distant. Marianne confronted him publicly, and his reply confirmed her worst fears: he was engaged to Miss Grey, a woman of fortune.

The aftermath was devastating. Marianne's collapse into grief — neglecting food, refusing company, and surrendering to the full intensity of her emotions — brought on the physical illness that would nearly kill her at Cleveland on the journey home.

## Consequences

The London season is the fulcrum on which the novel turns. Marianne's romantic worldview, built on sensibility and first impressions, collapses under the weight of Willoughby's betrayal. Her subsequent illness at Cleveland — brought on by "a nervous fever" aggravated by grief — brings her to the brink of death and serves as the novel's most explicit warning against ungoverned sensibility.

For Elinor, the London season proves equally painful, though she endures it silently. Lucy Steele's revelation of her secret engagement to Edward forces Elinor to conceal her own heartbreak while continuing to perform the role of composed elder sister. The contrast between the two sisters' modes of suffering — Marianne's open, dramatic grief and Elinor's quiet, self-contained endurance — constitutes the novel's central moral argument.

The London season also advances the plot mechanistically: Willoughby's engagement to Miss Grey is exposed, Colonel Brandon's character and history are deepened through his conversation with Elinor, and Lucy Steele's machinations set in motion the chain of events that will ultimately free Edward to marry Elinor. The season ends with the sisters returning to Devonshire, the novel's geography reversing the hopeful outward journey of the autumn.
