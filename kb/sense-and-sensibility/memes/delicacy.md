# Delicacy

## Overview

Delicacy — the quality of refined sensitivity, tact, and moral fastidiousness — occupies an ambivalent position in *Sense and Sensibility*. It is a term of praise when applied to characters of genuine refinement (Elinor, Colonel Brandon) and a term of critique when it shades into excessive scrupulosity or pretension. Austen uses delicacy to explore the boundary between genuine moral sensitivity and its social performance.

## In Sense and Sensibility

Elinor most fully embodies true delicacy. Her reluctance to probe Edward about his feelings, her careful management of her mother's expectations, and her tactful handling of Lucy Steele all demonstrate a delicacy that is moral rather than merely social. She feels acutely but does not demand that others accommodate her feelings.

The novel also critiques false delicacy. Mrs. John Dashwood's objections to the Dashwood women's continued residence at Norland are couched in the language of delicacy — it would be "improper" for them to stay — but her delicacy is transparently self-serving. The term is also applied ironically to characters whose sensitivity is performative: Fanny Dashwood's "agonies of sensibility" (049-041) are a parody of genuine delicacy.

Marianne's crisis turns partly on a reconsideration of delicacy. Her early conviction that true feeling must be openly expressed gives way to an appreciation for the delicacy that Elinor practices — a delicacy that conceals rather than displays, that protects others rather than asserting the self.

## Analysis

Delicacy in Austen is a class concept as much as a moral one. The ability to practice delicacy — to be tactful, to read social situations subtly, to express feeling indirectly — requires education and leisure. The Steele sisters' vulgarity is partly a failure of delicacy and partly a consequence of their social position. Austen is clear-eyed about this: delicacy is a genuine virtue, but it is also a marker of class privilege. The novel does not resolve this tension but holds it in view, allowing readers to see both the value of delicacy and its entanglement with social hierarchy.
