# Sensibility

## Overview

Sensibility, in the context of Jane Austen's *Sense and Sensibility*, denotes a heightened capacity for emotional feeling — an acute responsiveness to beauty, suffering, and the passions of the heart. It is the quality that defines Marianne Dashwood's character and stands in thematic tension with the "sense" represented by her sister Elinor. In the late eighteenth-century literary tradition that Austen both draws upon and critiques, sensibility was associated with the cult of feeling, Romantic expressiveness, and a moral philosophy that equated strong emotion with virtue.

## In Sense and Sensibility

Throughout the novel, sensibility appears as both a gift and a danger. Marianne's sensibility is first introduced in contrast to Elinor's composure: Elinor "saw, with concern, the excess of her sister's sensibility; but by Mrs. Dashwood it was valued and cherished" (001-013). This early judgment establishes the novel's central moral question — whether strong feeling, left unchecked by prudent judgment, leads to wisdom or ruin.

The novel repeatedly tests sensibility against real-world consequences. Marianne herself articulates the ideal of sensibility as a standard for romantic attachment, declaring that she could never love a man who read poetry "with so little sensibility" (003-020). Yet this very idealism blinds her to Colonel Brandon's quiet worth and Willoughby's superficial charm. Elinor later reflects on "the too great importance placed by her on the delicacies of a strong sensibility, and the graces of a polished manner" (031-004), identifying the danger of judging character by emotional performance rather than conduct.

Sensibility also proves to be a source of genuine moral action. When Marianne witnesses Mrs. Ferrars's cold treatment of Elinor, she is moved by "a strong impulse of affectionate sensibility" to comfort her sister (034-039). And in the novel's resolution, even the foolish Robert Ferrars is recalled from his self-regard "not by any reproof of hers, but by his own sensibility" (041-034) — suggesting that the capacity for feeling, properly directed, remains essential to moral life.

## Key Passages

> Elinor saw, with concern, the excess of her sister's sensibility; but by Mrs. Dashwood it was valued and cherished. (001-013)

> "Mama, the more I know of the world, the more am I convinced that I shall never see a man whom I can really love. I require so much!" (003-020)

> Like half the rest of the world, if more than half there be that are clever and good, Marianne, with excellent abilities and an excellent disposition, was neither reasonable nor candid. She expected from other people the same opinions and feelings as her own, and she judged of their motives by the immediate effect of their actions on herself. (031-004)

## Analysis

Austen's treatment of sensibility is neither simple endorsement nor wholesale rejection. The novel's title — *Sense and Sensibility* — proposes a conjunction rather than a dichotomy. Sensibility without sense, as Marianne discovers, leads to near-fatal illness and social ruin. Yet sense without sensibility, the novel implies, would be a diminished life. The resolution suggests a synthesis: Marianne learns to value restraint and judgment, while Elinor's own emotional life — long concealed behind composure — is finally acknowledged in her tearful reunion with Edward.

The word "sensibility" and its cognates appear at critical moments throughout the novel, from the opening description of the Dashwood family to the closing chapter. Fanny Dashwood's "agonies of sensibility" (049-041) at the end provide an ironic bookend — false sensibility, performed for social advantage, is exposed as self-serving theater, while true sensibility, tempered by experience, is vindicated.
