## Overview

Mrs. Jennings is one of Austen's great comic creations — a warm-hearted, tactless, prosperous widow whose chief pleasure in life is matchmaking. She is the mother of Lady Middleton and Mrs. Palmer, and through her connections provides the Dashwood sisters with their entrée into London society. Though Elinor and Marianne are often embarrassed by her indelicacy (particularly her relentless teasing about their romantic attachments), Mrs. Jennings demonstrates genuine generosity — nursing Marianne through her illness at Cleveland and offering practical kindness when the Dashwoods most need it (007-005)(008-001).

## Biography

Widowed with an ample jointure, Mrs. Jennings divides her time between her daughters' households and her own establishment in London. She has "only two thousand pounds a year" — a comfortable but not extravagant income — yet she lives with cheerful generosity. She brings the Dashwood sisters to London for the season, introducing them to society and providing them with opportunities they could not otherwise afford (008-001).

## Relationships

**Lady Middleton** — Mrs. Jennings's elder daughter, a cold, elegant woman who tolerates her mother with barely concealed disdain. Their relationship illustrates Austen's observation that refinement without warmth is poorer than warmth without refinement.

**Mrs. Palmer** — Her younger daughter, who shares her mother's good nature if not her boisterousness. Mrs. Palmer's cheerful acceptance of her husband's rudeness shows the same resilient good humor that characterizes Mrs. Jennings herself.

**Marianne Dashwood** — Mrs. Jennings takes particular interest in Marianne's romantic affairs, teasing her relentlessly about Colonel Brandon — "the Colonel" as she persistently calls him. Though Marianne finds this torment unbearable, Mrs. Jennings's instincts prove sound: Brandon is indeed the better man (012-015).

## Key Scenes

Mrs. Jennings's assembly of the Dashwoods, the Steeles, and the Middletons at her London house creates the social crucible in which the novel's romantic complications intensify. It is here that Marianne encounters Willoughby's coldness, that Lucy Steele confides her engagement to Elinor, and that the various romantic entanglements come to their crisis.

Her tending of Marianne at Cleveland — sitting with her through the fever, sending for the apothecary, and providing practical comfort — reveals the depth of her goodness beneath the vulgarity. "She was a great comfort to them all," Austen writes, "and her kindness was not confined to words" (012-016).

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Jennings embodies Austen's argument that moral worth is independent of social refinement. She is coarse, indiscreet, and sometimes ridiculous — but she is also kind, generous, and fundamentally decent. Her treatment of the Dashwood sisters, from inviting them to London to nursing Marianne through illness, puts to shame the cold politeness of characters like Fanny Dashwood and Lady Middleton.
