## Overview

Berkeley Street is the London home of Mrs. Jennings, where the Dashwood sisters reside during their visit to the capital. Unlike the cold elegance of Harley Street, Berkeley Street offers vulgar warmth — Mrs. Jennings's constant teasing, the Steele sisters' social climbing, and the domestic intimacy that makes the house both a refuge and a prison for Elinor during the most painful period of her romantic disappointment.

## Description

Mrs. Jennings's establishment on Berkeley Street is comfortable rather than fashionable — appropriate to a widow with two thousand a year who values company above display. The house's interior is defined by its social functions: the drawing room where Mrs. Jennings teases her guests about their romantic prospects, the private spaces where confidences are exchanged, the breakfast parlor where the day's social plans are arranged.

## Key Events

Berkeley Street is the setting for the most painful scene in Elinor's story: Lucy Steele's private interview, in which she reveals her four-year secret engagement to Edward Ferrars. The domestic intimacy of Mrs. Jennings's house — the very quality that should make it a refuge — becomes the condition for Lucy's calculated confidence. Elinor must receive this devastating news with composure, bound by Lucy's demand for secrecy and by the social impossibility of revealing the depth of her own feeling.

## Significance

Berkeley Street represents the novel's argument that physical comfort does not guarantee emotional safety. Mrs. Jennings's house is warm, sociable, and generous — yet within its walls, Elinor suffers her deepest anguish. The house's very domesticity — the ease with which Lucy Steele can draw Elinor aside for a "private word" — enables the intimate cruelty that the novel anatomizes.
