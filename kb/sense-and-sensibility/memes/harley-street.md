## Overview

Harley Street is the London residence of John Dashwood and his wife Fanny, and it functions in the novel as the urban counterpart to Norland Park — a place where wealth dictates behavior and family obligations are measured in guineas rather than affection. The Dashwood sisters' visits to Harley Street during their London sojourn dramatize the social distance between the fashionable world of John and Fanny and the reduced circumstances of their half-sisters (024-026).

## Description

Harley Street was, in Austen's time, a fashionable address in Marylebone — newly developed, expensive, and inhabited by the prosperous professional and mercantile classes rather than the aristocracy. John Dashwood's residence there signals his ambition rather than his lineage; he has the Dashwood name but not the Dashwood hospitality.

## Key Events

The Dashwood sisters' calls at Harley Street are exercises in social endurance. Fanny Dashwood's cold politeness, John's embarrassed ineffectuality, and the constant awareness that they are tolerated rather than welcomed make each visit an ordeal. The contrast between Harley Street's material comfort and its emotional poverty is one of the novel's sharpest social critiques.

## Significance

Harley Street represents the urbanization of the values that displaced the Dashwood women from Norland. In the country, John Dashwood's selfishness took the form of neglect — failing to provide for his sisters. In London, it takes the form of exclusion — maintaining the forms of family connection while denying the substance. Harley Street is the architecture of this refusal: elegant, correct, and cold.
