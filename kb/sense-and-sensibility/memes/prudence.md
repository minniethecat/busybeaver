# Prudence

## Overview

Prudence — the exercise of careful judgment in practical affairs — is a central but quiet virtue in *Sense and Sensibility*. Unlike the flashier qualities of sensibility or the rigidity of propriety, prudence operates in the background, shaping decisions about money, love, and social conduct. Austen presents prudence not as timidity but as the necessary complement to both feeling and principle.

## In Sense and Sensibility

Elinor Dashwood is the novel's exemplar of prudence. She is introduced as possessing "a strength of understanding, and coolness of judgment, which qualified her, though only nineteen, to be the counsellor of her mother" (001-011). Her prudence is not coldness but the capacity to act wisely under pressure — a quality her mother notably lacks.

Mrs. Dashwood's attitude toward Edward Ferrars's suit illustrates the tension between prudence and romantic hope. The narrator notes that "some might have repressed it from motives of prudence, for, except a trifling sum, the whole of his fortune depended on the will of his mother" (003-005). Mrs. Dashwood's refusal to be prudent about Edward's prospects is presented as both admirable (she values love over money) and dangerous (she ignores the real economic constraints her daughters face).

The novel's resolution vindicates prudence without glorifying it. Elinor's prudent concealment of Lucy Steele's secret causes her genuine suffering, yet it also preserves her dignity and ultimately enables her happiness. Prudence, Austen suggests, is a necessary but insufficient virtue — it must be warmed by feeling to be fully human.

## Key Passages

(003-005)

## Analysis

Prudence in Austen's world is a gendered virtue. Elinor must be prudent because she has no economic power; Edward Ferrars can afford to be imprudent (refusing his mother's demand that he marry wealth) precisely because he is a man who will eventually inherit. The novel's double standard is clear: a woman's imprudence leads to ruin; a man's imprudence leads to temporary inconvenience. This asymmetry gives Elinor's prudence a heroic quality — she exercises wisdom not as a luxury but as a survival strategy.
