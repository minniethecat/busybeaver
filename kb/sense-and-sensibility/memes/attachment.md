# Attachment

## Overview

Attachment — the formation of emotional bonds between characters — is the engine of *Sense and Sensibility*'s plot. Romantic attachments drive the central narrative, but attachments of family loyalty, friendship, and even self-interest shape every relationship in the novel. Austen examines how attachments form, how they are tested, and what distinguishes genuine attachment from its counterfeits.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel's first attachment is familial. The Dashwood women's bond to each other — and their forced separation from Norland, the estate where "for many generations, they had lived in so respectable a manner" (001-001) — establishes attachment to place as a form of emotional identity. Their removal to Barton Cottage is both physical displacement and emotional rupture.

Romantic attachment drives the plot. Edward Ferrars and Elinor's "growing attachment" (003-004) is established early but tested by secrecy, separation, and the revelation of Edward's prior engagement. Marianne and Willoughby's attachment is immediate, passionate, and publicly performed — and therefore, in Austen's moral scheme, suspect. The novel traces the arc from infatuation to disillusionment, and from quiet esteem to enduring love.

Mrs. Dashwood's eagerness to see attachments form — "No sooner did she perceive any symptom of love in his behaviour to Elinor, than she considered their serious attachment as certain, and looked forward to their marriage as rapidly approaching" (003-014) — shows how the romantic hopes of one generation are projected onto the next.

## Analysis

Austen distinguishes between attachments of feeling and attachments of judgment. Marianne's attachments are of the first kind — immediate, visceral, and expressed without reserve. Elinor's are of the second — gradual, considered, and expressed only when circumstances permit. The novel does not dismiss either form; rather, it shows that attachments of feeling without judgment lead to ruin (Marianne and Willoughby), while attachments of judgment without expression lead to suffering (Elinor concealing her knowledge of Edward's engagement). The happy resolution requires both: attachment must be felt deeply and judged wisely.
