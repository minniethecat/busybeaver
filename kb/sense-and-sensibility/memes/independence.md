# Independence

## Overview

Independence in *Sense and Sensibility* is both a cherished ideal and a practical impossibility for most characters. The Dashwood sisters desire independence — of judgment, of conduct, of affection — but their economic circumstances make them dependent on the goodwill of others. Austen explores the paradox of independence: it is most valued by those who can least afford it, and most easily claimed by those who already possess power.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The Dashwood women's removal from Norland to Barton Cottage is simultaneously a loss and a gain in independence. They lose the estate that defined their social position, but they gain freedom from Mrs. John Dashwood's insufferable presence. Barton Cottage, modest as it is, represents a form of independence — a household governed by their own choices rather than by the condescension of relatives.

Elinor's independence is internal. She thinks for herself, judges for herself, and acts according to her own principles — even when those principles require her to conceal her feelings from those she loves. Her independence is invisible to most observers, who see only her composure. Marianne's independence is external and dramatic — she refuses to dissemble, insists on the validity of her own feelings, and openly challenges social convention. Both forms of independence are tested and both are found wanting in their pure forms.

Edward Ferrars's choice to honor his engagement to Lucy Steele, despite no longer loving her, represents a perverse form of independence — he acts against his own interest and desire out of a rigid sense of honor. The novel ultimately endorses a moderated independence: the freedom to choose wisely, constrained by genuine obligations to others.

## Analysis

Austen is unusually honest about the material conditions of independence. A woman without fortune cannot be independent — this is not a moral failing but an economic fact. The novel's happy ending secures independence for the Dashwood sisters through marriage, which is both the solution to and the symptom of their dependence. Elinor and Marianne achieve independence not by rejecting the social order but by finding places within it that allow them scope for autonomous action. It is a compromise, and Austen presents it as such — the best available outcome in a world where full independence for women is structurally impossible.
