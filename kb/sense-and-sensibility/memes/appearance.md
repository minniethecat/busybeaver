# Appearance

## Overview

Appearance in *Sense and Sensibility* is both a surface to be read and a mask to be penetrated. Characters constantly judge and are judged by how things appear — physical beauty, social manner, the outward signs of wealth and status — yet Austen repeatedly demonstrates that appearances mislead. The novel's moral education consists largely in learning to see past surfaces.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel's first extended description of a character by appearance is of Lady Middleton: "the elegance of her appearance was favourable to their wishes. Lady Middleton was not more than six or seven and twenty; her face was handsome, her figure tall and striking, and her address was graceful" (006-008). The Dashwood sisters are initially impressed — and then gradually discover that Lady Middleton's elegant appearance conceals a cold, incurious character devoted entirely to her children.

Colonel Brandon presents the opposite case. His "appearance however was not unpleasing, in spite of his being in the opinion of Marianne and Margaret an absolute old bachelor" (007-006). His quiet, grave manner is a surface that conceals depth of feeling, generosity, and a tragic romantic history. Marianne, committed to reading appearance as character, dismisses him; Elinor, more cautious, reserves judgment and is rewarded.

Willoughby is the novel's supreme example of deceptive appearance. Handsome, charming, and passionate, he appears to be everything Marianne desires — and he is, in fact, none of it. His appearance is a performance calibrated to attract; his character is revealed only through his actions.

## Key Passages

## Analysis

Austen's treatment of appearance is sophisticated. She does not argue that appearances are irrelevant — on the contrary, characters who neglect appearance entirely (like the Steele sisters' vulgarity) are rightly judged for it. Rather, she argues that appearance is necessary but insufficient data for judgment. The wise character reads appearances carefully but does not stop at them; the foolish character treats appearance as self-evident truth. This epistemology of social life — how we know other people — is one of Austen's deepest subjects.
