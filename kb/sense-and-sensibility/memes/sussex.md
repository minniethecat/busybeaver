## Overview

Sussex is the novel's absent center — the place the Dashwood women come from and cannot return to. As the location of Norland Park, it represents everything they have lost: wealth, status, security, and the family home of their childhood. The county's function in the novel is largely elegiac — it is the measure of what has been taken from them.

## Description

Austen provides no direct description of Sussex beyond what can be inferred from Norland Park. It is a county of established landed estates, of old money and inherited property, where the legal mechanisms of entail and inheritance determine family fortunes with little regard for desert. The Dashwood women's removal from it is, in a sense, a liberation from its rigid structures — but a liberation they did not choose and would not have chosen.

## Key Events

Sussex is the setting of the novel's pre-history: Henry Dashwood's death, John Dashwood's inheritance, and Fanny Dashwood's campaign to minimize her husband's obligations to his stepmother and half-sisters. The county appears in the novel primarily as memory — Elinor and Marianne's recollections of Norland, the contrast between their former home and their present cottage, the knowledge that John Dashwood and Fanny now occupy the rooms where they grew up.

## Significance

Sussex represents the permanence of loss in the novel's world. Unlike the material losses that can be made good — an income restored through marriage, a home established through generosity — the loss of one's childhood home is final. The Dashwood sisters will never live at Norland again; Sussex will always be the place they left. This elegiac dimension underlies the novel's happy ending: even as Elinor and Marianne find love and security, something has been irrevocably lost.
