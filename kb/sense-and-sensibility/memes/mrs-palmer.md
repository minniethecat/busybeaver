## Overview

Mrs. Palmer (née Charlotte Jennings) is a minor but vivid character — a young woman whose unfailing cheerfulness in the face of her husband's studied rudeness is either admirable resilience or alarming insensibility, depending on one's perspective. She provides comic relief but also offers a genuine kindness to the Dashwood sisters, particularly in nursing Marianne through her near-fatal illness at Cleveland (008-001)(008-003).

## Biography

As Mrs. Jennings's younger daughter, Charlotte has made a materially successful marriage to Mr. Palmer, a man of fortune and consequence. Unlike her sister Lady Middleton, who values cold elegance, Charlotte values nothing much at all beyond the immediate pleasure of the moment. Her husband ignores her, her mother embarrasses her, and nothing disturbs her equanimity — she laughs at everything, including her own misfortunes.

## Relationships

**Mr. Palmer** — Charlotte's husband, a man who "seemed hardly to know what to say to her, after the first salutation." He spends his time reading the newspaper, ignoring his wife, and being rude to everyone. Charlotte's response to this treatment is to find it amusing — she reports his rudeness to others with delight, as if it were evidence of his wit rather than his contempt.

**Mrs. Jennings** — Charlotte shares her mother's good nature without inheriting her mother's vulgarity. She is genuinely fond of Mrs. Jennings and lacks Lady Middleton's condescension, though she is equally aware of her mother's social deficiencies.

**The Dashwood Sisters** — Mrs. Palmer's kindness to Elinor and Marianne at Cleveland — where she hosts them during Marianne's illness and provides whatever comfort she can — reveals a goodness of heart that her silly manner initially conceals.

## Key Scenes

Mrs. Palmer's arrival at Barton with her husband provides one of the novel's sharpest comic set-pieces. Mr. Palmer's determination to be disagreeable, and Charlotte's determination to find this charming, create a portrait of marital dysfunction that is simultaneously hilarious and appalling.

At Cleveland, Mrs. Palmer's childishness proves unexpectedly useful — her lack of gravity means she is not overwhelmed by the crisis, and her genuine kindness emerges in practical ways. When Marianne falls dangerously ill, it is Mrs. Palmer who insists that Mrs. Jennings and the Dashwoods remain at Cleveland as long as needed.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Palmer belongs to Austen's gallery of characters whose moral substance is obscured by manner. She appears foolish, and she is; but she is also kind, and her kindness matters more than her foolishness. Her resilience in the face of her husband's boorishness is either heroic or pathetic — Austen leaves the judgment to the reader — but it is certainly effective. Mrs. Palmer is never unhappy, because she has decided not to be.
