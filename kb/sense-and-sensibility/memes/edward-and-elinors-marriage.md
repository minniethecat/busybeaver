## Overview

Edward and Elinor's marriage is the culminating event of *Sense and Sensibility* — the quiet resolution toward which the entire novel has been tending. Released from his secret engagement to Lucy Steele, Edward travels to Barton Cottage, proposes to Elinor within three hours of his arrival, and marries her at Barton church in early autumn. Their union represents the triumph of sense: a love built on steady esteem, mutual understanding, and principled self-government, in contrast to the passionate sensibility that nearly destroyed Marianne.

## Background

The obstacles to Edward and Elinor's union had accumulated over the entire course of the novel: Edward's secret engagement to Lucy Steele, contracted four years earlier when he was a young man under the care of Lucy's uncle; Mrs. Ferrars's implacable opposition to any marriage beneath the family's station; and Elinor's own painful silence, required by her promise to Lucy to keep the engagement secret from her own family. Each of these obstacles was removed in turn — Lucy transferred her ambitions to the newly enriched Robert Ferrars, Edward was disinherited but freed, and Elinor's long endurance was finally rewarded.

## Description

The news of Lucy's marriage to Robert Ferrars transformed Edward's situation instantly. As the narrator observes with gentle irony:

> For after experiencing the blessings of one imprudent engagement, contracted without his mother's consent, as he had already done for more than four years, nothing less could be expected of him in the failure of that, than the immediate contraction of another. (049-001)

Edward's errand at Barton was simple: "It was only to ask Elinor to marry him" (049-002). But Austen characteristically refuses to dramatize the proposal scene itself:

> How soon he had walked himself into the proper resolution, however, how soon an opportunity of exercising it occurred, in what manner he expressed himself, and how he was received, need not be particularly told. (049-003)

What the narrator does record is Edward's emotional state — a happiness built on release as much as gain:

> He was released without any reproach to himself, from an entanglement which had long formed his misery, from a woman whom he had long ceased to love;—and elevated at once to that security with another, which he must have thought of almost with despair, as soon as he had learnt to consider it with desire. (049-003)

Colonel Brandon, whose quiet generosity runs through the novel, provided the practical foundation for their life together by offering Edward the living at Delaford — a modest but sufficient income. The ceremony "took place in Barton church early in the autumn" (050-006), after Elinor — in a rare departure from her characteristic patience — "broke through the first positive resolution of not marrying till every thing was ready" (050-006).

## Consequences

The marriage establishes the domestic resolution of the novel. The couple first lives at the Delaford Mansion-house while the Parsonage is being improved, "from whence they could superintend the progress of the Parsonage, and direct every thing as they liked on the spot;—could chuse papers, project shrubberies, and invent a sweep" (050-007). Mrs. Jennings visited them by Michaelmas and "found in Elinor and her husband, as she really believed, one of the happiest couples in the world" (050-007).

The closing sentence of this passage quietly captures the novel's moral vision. The couple "had in fact nothing to wish for, but the marriage of Colonel Brandon and Marianne, and rather better pasturage for their cows" (050-007). The juxtaposition of romantic resolution and practical concern — a friend's marriage and better grazing land — is quintessential Austen: happiness is grounded not in grand passions but in modest, well-ordered domestic life.

Edward and Elinor's marriage is the novel's answer to the question posed by its title. It represents the triumph of sense — not as cold calculation, but as the steady exercise of judgment, self-knowledge, and principled restraint that makes lasting happiness possible. Elinor's reward is not the ecstasy that Marianne sought and failed to find, but something more durable: a husband she respects, a home of her own, and the quiet satisfaction of a life built on mutual esteem.
