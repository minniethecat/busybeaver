## Overview

Allenham is the property of Willoughby's aunt, Mrs. Smith, and the source of Willoughby's expectations. His position as his aunt's heir — conditional on her continued approval — creates the financial dependency that ultimately drives his decision to abandon Marianne for a wealthy marriage. Allenham is thus the invisible economic force shaping the novel's most painful romantic crisis.

## Description

Allenham is never directly described, but its significance is established through what it represents: substantial wealth contingent on good behavior. Mrs. Smith's power to disinherit Willoughby — a power she exercises when she discovers his seduction of Eliza Williams — transforms Allenham from promise to threat. The estate's proximity to Barton enables Willoughby's constant presence in Marianne's company during their courtship.

## Key Events

Willoughby's unauthorized tour of Allenham with Marianne — showing her the house as if she will one day be its mistress, though no engagement exists — is the novel's most significant violation of propriety. The impropriety is both social (a single woman touring a single man's prospective property without chaperonage) and moral (Willoughby implies a commitment he has no intention of honoring).

Mrs. Smith's discovery of Eliza Williams's seduction and her consequent disinheritance of Willoughby converts Allenham from a symbol of romantic possibility into the mechanism of his betrayal — without Allenham's loss, he might have married Marianne; with it, he must marry Miss Grey.

## Significance

Allenham represents the power of money to shape romantic outcomes in Austen's world. It is not a character but a force — the promise of wealth that enables Willoughby's charm and the withdrawal of that promise that exposes its hollowness. Mrs. Smith, who never appears directly, exercises more power over the novel's romantic geometry than many of its visible characters.
