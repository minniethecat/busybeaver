# Expectation

## Overview

Expectation — the anticipation of future happiness, fortune, or social advancement — drives the plot of *Sense and Sensibility* as powerfully as any external event. Characters live in a state of hopeful waiting: for love to be declared, for inheritances to arrive, for marriages to be arranged. Austen shows that expectation is a double-edged sword: it sustains hope but also creates vulnerability to disappointment.

## In Sense and Sensibility

Mrs. Dashwood embodies the novel's most optimistic form of expectation. The narrator describes "that sanguine expectation of happiness which is happiness itself" (002-002) — a temperamental gift that enables her to weather adversity but also blinds her to danger. Her expectations for her daughters' romantic prospects are generous but often unrealistic.

The novel's central plot turns on disappointed expectations. The Dashwood sisters expect their half-brother to provide for them; he does not. Marianne expects Willoughby to propose; he abandons her. Elinor expects Edward to be free; he is secretly engaged. Each disappointment tests character differently: Marianne collapses, Elinor endures.

Mrs. John Dashwood's rhetoric of expectation reveals the selfishness that can hide behind the language of prudence: "we are not to think of their expectations: the question is, what you can afford to do" (002-013). By reframing moral obligation as a question of financial capacity, she transforms expectation from a claim on conscience into an unreasonable demand.

## Key Passages

> In seasons of cheerfulness, no temper could be more cheerful than hers, or possess, in a greater degree, that sanguine expectation of happiness which is happiness itself. (002-002)

> "We are not to think of their expectations," said the lady, "but we are not to think of their expectations: the question is, what you can afford to do." (002-013)

## Analysis

Austen uses expectation as a moral X-ray. How characters handle disappointed expectations reveals their deepest qualities. Elinor absorbs disappointment silently and continues to act with integrity; Marianne is shattered by it and must be rebuilt; Lucy Steele never allows herself to be vulnerable to expectation in the first place — she hedges every emotional bet. The novel suggests that the wise person expects with moderation, hopes without demanding, and is prepared for the gap between what is anticipated and what arrives.
