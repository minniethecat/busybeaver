## Overview

Margaret Dashwood, the youngest of the three sisters at thirteen, is a minor but significant presence. Too young to participate fully in the romantic dramas of her elder sisters, she serves as an observer and a reminder of what is at stake: the futures of all three Dashwood women depend on the prudence or imprudence of the marriages they make. Margaret's natural romantic enthusiasm — "she was already a sister in sensibility to Marianne" — suggests that the novel's argument about the dangers of unchecked sensibility extends across generations (001-011).

## Biography

Margaret is thirteen at the novel's opening, still young enough to be more interested in romantic stories than in the realities of courtship and marriage. She lives with her mother and sisters at Barton Cottage, largely in the background of the novel's major events. Her most notable moment comes when she inadvertently reveals to Mrs. Jennings that Elinor's love interest begins with "F" — a childish indiscretion that causes Elinor considerable embarrassment.

## Relationships

**Elinor Dashwood** — Margaret adores her eldest sister but, like Marianne, finds Elinor's emotional restraint puzzling. She is too young to understand that Elinor's composure masks deep feeling.

**Marianne Dashwood** — Margaret shares Marianne's romantic sensibility, and Austen suggests that, without the chastening experience that Marianne undergoes, Margaret might follow the same dangerous path of emotional excess.

**Mrs. Dashwood** — As the baby of the family, Margaret is sheltered by her mother, though Mrs. Dashwood's own romantic impulsiveness does little to prepare Margaret for the realities of the marriage market.

## Character Analysis

Margaret's function in the novel is largely structural: she completes the Dashwood family portrait and provides a third term in the spectrum of sensibility (Elinor's sense → Margaret's incipient sensibility → Marianne's full-blown sensibility). Her future — like that of all young women without fortune in Austen's world — depends on the marriage settlements of her elder sisters and on her own eventual ability to attract a suitable husband. She is the novel's silent question: what will become of the next generation?
