## Overview

Robert Ferrars is Austen's sharply drawn portrait of fashionable vacuity. As the younger son who becomes his mother's favorite after Edward's disgrace, he embodies everything the novel critiques: vanity, superficiality, and the triumph of style over substance. His extended comic scene — spending an unconscionable amount of time selecting a toothpick case — establishes his character with devastating economy (034-041).

## Biography

Robert is introduced through his brother Edward's account and through a memorable scene in a London shop, where Elinor observes him spending fifteen minutes choosing a toothpick case — a set-piece that captures his entire character: trivial, self-important, and blissfully unaware of his own absurdity.

When Edward is disinherited for refusing to break his engagement to Lucy Steele, Robert inherits both the family fortune and, eventually, Lucy herself. His marriage to Lucy is the novel's final satiric stroke — the woman who entrapped Edward through a secret engagement entraps Robert through flattery, and Robert is too vain to recognize that he has been managed.

## Relationships

**Edward Ferrars** — Robert treats his elder brother with condescension, lecturing him on the folly of refusing to break an engagement to a woman of inferior station — only to marry that same woman himself, having been flattered into believing it was his own idea.

**Lucy Steele** — Robert is Lucy's masterpiece of manipulation. She makes him believe that he — not she — is the prize, and his vanity prevents him from seeing that he is being played.

**Mrs. Ferrars** — Robert is his mother's favorite, a status he enjoys without earning. His elevation at Edward's expense illustrates the capriciousness of Mrs. Ferrars's favoritism.

## Key Scenes

The toothpick-case scene is one of Austen's most quoted comic passages. Robert's earnest deliberation over "the ivory, the gold, and the tortoiseshell" — each examined at length for its relative merits — transforms a trivial purchase into a revelation of character. Elinor's silent observation of the scene is a masterclass in narrative point of view.

## Character Analysis

Robert Ferrars is what Willoughby would be without the charm — a man of wealth and no substance, destined to be managed by a cleverer partner. His function in the plot is to solve Edward's problem — by taking Lucy off Edward's hands, he frees Edward to marry Elinor — but his function in the novel's argument is to demonstrate that wealth without merit produces nothing worth having.
