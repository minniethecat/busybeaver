## Overview

Barton Cottage is the Dashwood family's home after their displacement from Norland Park — a modest dwelling that Sir John Middleton offers his cousin Mrs. Dashwood at a nominal rent. Far smaller and less grand than Norland, the cottage represents the financial reality of the Dashwood women's situation: their combined fortune of ten thousand pounds yields an income of only five hundred pounds a year, and they must live accordingly. Yet the cottage also becomes a place of genuine domestic warmth, a contrast to the cold formality of Barton Park and the mercenary atmosphere of Norland under Fanny Dashwood (005-007).

## Description

Barton Cottage is pleasant but unpretentious — "as a cottage it was defective, for the building was regular, the roof was tiled, the window shutters were painted green, and the walls were not covered with jessamine and honeysuckle." Austen's description gently mocks the picturesque ideal of the cottage while acknowledging the reality of the Dashwoods' diminished circumstances. The cottage has four bedrooms, two sitting rooms, and a garden that Mrs. Dashwood immediately plans to improve — a domestic project that channels her restless energy (005-007).

## Residents

The cottage is home to **[[mrs-dashwood]]** and her three daughters: **[[elinor-dashwood]]**, **[[marianne-dashwood]]**, and Margaret. Their domestic life at the cottage — Elinor's drawings hung on the walls, Marianne's pianoforte in the sitting room, the fire around which Edward Ferrars sits during his visit — creates a warmth that the grander houses in the novel notably lack (006-005).

## Key Events

Edward Ferrars's awkward visit to the cottage — his first since the Dashwoods left Norland — is a crucial scene. His evident discomfort, which Elinor attributes to a cooling of his affection, is later explained by his entanglement with Lucy Steele. The cottage setting, intimate and domestic, intensifies the emotional stakes of this encounter.

Marianne's dramatic illness at Cleveland and her subsequent return to Barton Cottage to convalesce marks the novel's emotional resolution. The cottage becomes the place of healing — both physical and emotional — where Marianne reflects on her past conduct and where the novel's romantic threads are finally resolved.

## Significance

Barton Cottage embodies the novel's argument that domestic happiness depends on moral quality rather than material wealth. The Dashwood women have far less at the cottage than they had at Norland, but they are freer — free of Fanny Dashwood's cold condescension, free to create their own society, and free to make their own choices about love and marriage. The cottage is both a symbol of their loss and the setting of their eventual happiness (006-005).
