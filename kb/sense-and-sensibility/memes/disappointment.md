# Disappointment

## Overview

Disappointment is the emotional signature of *Sense and Sensibility* — the felt gap between what characters hope for and what they receive. From the opening pages, where the Dashwood women are disappointed of their inheritance, to the final chapters, where long-deferred hopes are finally fulfilled, disappointment structures the novel's emotional architecture. Austen treats disappointment not as mere misfortune but as a moral education.

## In Sense and Sensibility

The novel's first major event is a disappointment: "The old gentleman died: his will was read, and like almost every other will, gave as much disappointment as pleasure" (001-003). This sentence establishes disappointment as a universal experience — "like almost every other will" — while also introducing the specific economic disappointment that will shadow the Dashwood sisters throughout the novel.

Marianne's disappointment in love is the novel's most dramatic. Her expectation of Willoughby's proposal, her shock at his cold reception in London, and her eventual discovery of his mercenary marriage constitute a curriculum in disappointment that nearly destroys her. Elinor's disappointment is quieter but equally profound — she learns of Edward's secret engagement and must endure this knowledge alone, supporting others while concealing her own pain.

The novel's resolution does not eliminate disappointment but transforms it. Marianne learns to desire differently; Elinor's long disappointment is redeemed by Edward's return. Disappointment, Austen suggests, is not the opposite of happiness but its precondition — we learn what we truly want only by discovering what we cannot have.

## Analysis

Austen's treatment of disappointment distinguishes her from both sentimental novelists (who would wallow in it) and moralists (who would condemn the desires that lead to it). Disappointment in her hands is neither tragedy nor punishment but education. The characters who learn from disappointment — Elinor, Marianne, even the reformed Willoughby — emerge wiser; those who do not — John Dashwood, Lucy Steele — remain morally stunted. The novel suggests that the capacity to be disappointed, and to survive it, is a mark of moral seriousness.
