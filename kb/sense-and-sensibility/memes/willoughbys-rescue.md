## Overview

Willoughby's rescue of Marianne is one of the most pivotal events in *Sense and Sensibility*. While walking on the downs near Barton Cottage, Marianne twists her ankle in a fall. A passing gentleman — John Willoughby — carries her home in his arms. This theatrical rescue, which unfolds in Chapter 9, immediately captivates Marianne's romantic imagination and sets in motion the central emotional drama of the novel.

## Background

The Dashwood family had recently settled at Barton Cottage in Devonshire. Marianne, at seventeen, possessed a passionate sensibility and a romantic disposition that predisposed her to form attachments rapidly and intensely. The neighborhood of Barton Park brought them into contact with Sir John Middleton, his family, and Colonel Brandon — but no young man of striking appearance or sensibility had yet appeared to capture Marianne's imagination. The stage was set for a dramatic entrance.

## Description

The event occurs on a day when Marianne and her younger sister Margaret are walking on the downs above Barton Cottage. As Austen's narrator relates:

> A gentleman carrying a gun, with two pointers playing round him, was passing up the hill and within a few yards of Marianne, when her accident happened. He put down his gun and ran to her assistance. She had raised herself from the ground, but her foot had been twisted in her fall, and she was scarcely able to stand. The gentleman offered his services; and perceiving that her modesty declined what her situation rendered necessary, took her up in his arms without farther delay, and carried her down the hill. Then passing through the garden, the gate of which had been left open by Margaret, he bore her directly into the house, whither Margaret was just arrived, and quitted not his hold till he had seated her in a chair in the parlour. (009-008)

The effect on the Dashwood household was immediate. Elinor and Mrs. Dashwood were struck not only by his gallantry but by his person:

> Had he been even old, ugly, and vulgar, the gratitude and kindness of Mrs. Dashwood would have been secured by any act of attention to her child; but the influence of youth, beauty, and elegance, gave an interest to the action which came home to her feelings. (009-009)

Willoughby introduced himself, explaining that his home was at Allenham, a nearby estate, and departed in the rain after securing permission to call the next day (009-010). His exit — "to make himself still more interesting, in the midst of a heavy rain" — is a masterful piece of Romantic self-presentation.

Marianne's reaction reveals her character and foreshadows the danger of her sensibility:

> His person and air were equal to what her fancy had ever drawn for the hero of a favourite story; and in his carrying her into the house with so little previous formality, there was a rapidity of thought which particularly recommended the action to her. Every circumstance belonging to him was interesting. His name was good, his residence was in their favourite village, and she soon found out that of all manly dresses a shooting-jacket was the most becoming. Her imagination was busy, her reflections were pleasant, and the pain of a sprained ankle was disregarded. (009-011)

Sir John Middleton confirmed Willoughby's identity on his next visit, exclaiming with characteristic enthusiasm: "Willoughby! What, is he in the country? That is good news however; I will ride over tomorrow, and ask him to dinner on Thursday" (009-013).

## Consequences

This event is the catalyst for the novel's central romantic drama. Marianne's infatuation with Willoughby develops rapidly from this moment. The theatrical manner of the rescue — the swooning fall, the handsome stranger, the intimate carrying into the house — conforms perfectly to the romantic sensibilities that Marianne has cultivated through her reading. As the narrator notes, "the pain of a sprained ankle was disregarded" (009-011) — the physical injury is nothing compared to the emotional excitement.

The rescue establishes the pattern that defines Marianne's romantic trajectory: she interprets Willoughby's actions through the lens of sensibility, seeing a storybook hero rather than a man of uncertain character. When Willoughby later abandons her for a wealthy marriage, the fall from this initial height makes her suffering proportionally devastating. The event also introduces the contrast between Marianne's romanticism and Elinor's prudence — a contrast that structures the novel's moral argument.

In the broader architecture of the plot, the rescue brings Willoughby into the Dashwoods' circle, creating a rival for Marianne's affections against Colonel Brandon (whose "being quite smitten already" Sir John notes at 009-032) and setting up the chain of disclosures, secrets, and betrayals that will occupy the novel's second volume.
