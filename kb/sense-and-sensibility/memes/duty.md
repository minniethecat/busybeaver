# Duty

## Overview

Duty — the sense of moral obligation that binds individuals to others — operates in *Sense and Sensibility* as both a constraint and a compass. Characters invoke duty to justify restraint, generosity, and self-sacrifice, but Austen also exposes how the language of duty can mask selfishness or self-deception. The novel asks: to whom do we owe what, and how do we know?

## In Sense and Sensibility

The most sustained examination of duty occurs in Elinor's concealment of Lucy Steele's secret engagement to Edward. Elinor has promised Lucy to keep the secret, and she regards this promise as a binding duty — even though honoring it causes her profound suffering and prevents her from warning Edward's family. Marianne, by contrast, "was in all probability not merely giving way to as a relief, but feeding and encouraging as a duty" (015-020) her own grief over Willoughby — transforming emotional indulgence into a perverse moral obligation.

Elinor's refusal to probe Edward about his feelings reflects another dimension of duty: "I would not ask such a question for the world. Supposing it possible that they are not engaged, what distress would not such an enquiry inflict! At any rate it would be most ungenerous" (016-009). Her sense of duty to avoid causing pain to others trumps her own desperate need for clarity.

## Key Passages

> Her sister's affliction was indubitable; and she thought with the tenderest compassion of that violent sorrow which Marianne was in all probability not merely giving way to as a relief, but feeding and encouraging as a duty. (015-020)

> "At any rate it would be most ungenerous. I should never deserve her confidence again, after forcing from her a confession of what is meant at present to be unacknowledged to any one." (016-009)

## Analysis

Duty in Austen is never abstract — it is always duty to specific people in specific circumstances. Elinor's duty to Lucy (to keep her secret) conflicts with her duty to herself (to pursue her own happiness) and her duty to her family (to protect them from entanglement with an insincere woman). The novel does not resolve these conflicts with a rule; instead, it shows duty as a matter of judgment, not formula. The characters who err — John Dashwood rationalizing away his duty to his sisters, Willoughby ignoring his duty to Colonel Brandon's ward — do so not by rejecting duty but by redefining it to suit their convenience.
