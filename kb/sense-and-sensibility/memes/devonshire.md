## Overview

Devonshire is the novel's alternative England — the rural southwest where the Dashwood sisters, displaced from Sussex, find a new (if diminished) life. The county's distance from the fashionable world of London and the family associations of Norland represents a kind of exile, but it is an exile that proves unexpectedly fertile: in Devonshire, both Elinor and Marianne encounter the men they will eventually marry.

## Description

Devonshire is rendered through its social character rather than its landscape. It is a county of country gentry — Sir John Middleton's hearty hospitality, Barton Park's constant entertaining, the network of visits and dinners that constitute county society. The landscape appears only in glimpses — the high downs where Marianne walks and twists her ankle, the lanes around Barton Cottage, the autumn countryside that surrounds the Dashwoods' modest home.

## Key Events

The Dashwood family's arrival in Devonshire, their establishment at Barton Cottage, and their introduction to county society set the novel's romantic machinery in motion. Willoughby's dramatic rescue of Marianne — finding her injured on the downs and carrying her home — is a Devonshire event, grounded in the county's rural landscape.

The social world of Devonshire — Sir John's dinners, Lady Middleton's drawing room, the constant flow of visitors to Barton Park — provides the medium through which relationships develop. Colonel Brandon's quiet, constant presence in this world, contrasted with Willoughby's dramatic but fleeting appearances, encodes the novel's argument about substance over style.

## Significance

Devonshire represents the possibility of renewal after loss. The Dashwood women arrive diminished — financially reduced, socially displaced — and the county provides them not with restoration of their former status but with something more valuable: the conditions for genuine happiness. Austen's Devonshire is not a pastoral ideal (Sir John's vulgarity and Lady Middleton's coldness prevent that) but a real place where real relationships can form, tested by neither the corrupting wealth of Norland nor the fashionable emptiness of London.
