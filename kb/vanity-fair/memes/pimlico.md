# Pimlico

**Pimlico** is a district of central London, situated south of Victoria Station and north of the River Thames. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, it appears in two distinct contexts: as the name of a satirical minor character and as a landmark not yet constructed at the time of the novel's early scenes.

## John Pimlico

The narrator invokes "my friend, the fashionable John Pimlico" as a comic example in a passage about the emotional spectacle of society weddings:

> When my friend, the fashionable John Pimlico, married the lovely Lady Belgravia Green Parker, the excitement was so general that even the little snuffy old pew-opener who let me into the seat was in tears [016-006].

The name "John Pimlico" is a playful invention — his surname borrowed from the London district, his bride's name ("Lady Belgravia Green Parker") from Belgravia, another fashionable London neighbourhood. Thackeray uses these geographic names satirically, mocking the pretensions of the London marriage market.

## The Pimlico arch

In a later passage describing George Osborne and William Dobbin's carriage journey through London on the morning of George's wedding, the narrator notes an anachronism:

> The carriage drove on, taking the road down Piccadilly, where Apsley House and St. George's Hospital wore red jackets still; where there were oil-lamps; where Achilles was not yet born; nor the Pimlico arch raised; nor the hideous equestrian monster which pervades it and the neighbourhood [022-009].

The Pimlico arch — presumably a reference to a landmark erected after the novel's Napoleonic-era setting — had not yet been built. The passage is one of several in which the narrator, writing from the 1840s, reminds the reader of the distance between the story's time and the present day.
