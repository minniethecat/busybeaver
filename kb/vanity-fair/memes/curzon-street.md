## Overview

Curzon Street is a real London thoroughfare in the fashionable district of May Fair. In *Vanity Fair*, it serves as the primary London residence of Rawdon and Rebecca Crawley at No. 201 — a "very small comfortable house" [036-003] that becomes both the symbol of Becky's social ambitions and the scene of her most devastating exposure.

The street is described as the true social centre for the Crawleys: "it is at their house in Curzon Street, May Fair, that they really spent the greater part of their time" [036-022], despite nominally maintaining rooms at Gaunt House or elsewhere. The house at No. 201 previously belonged to Raggles, a former butler who becomes their landlord — and eventually a victim of their financial ruin.

## Description

The Crawley residence at No. 201 is modest by May Fair standards — a small comfortable house [036-003] rather than a grand mansion. Its most notable decoration is "a print of Queen's Crawley" [037-006], a nostalgic and ironic touch given Rawdon's estrangement from his family estate.

The house's physical arrangement is not described in great architectural detail, but it accommodated Rawdon, Rebecca, little Rawdon, and at various times Miss Briggs and a household staff. The presence of a blazing fire [041-045], the parade of carriages outside [051-014], and the comings and goings of visitors all establish it as a busy, aspirational household.

Thackeray notes the social geography of the street with characteristic irony: "So, probably, 200 and 202 in Curzon Street might know what was going on in the house between them, the one being Raggles's and the other Raggles's" [037-010] — the implication being that the entire street was in Raggles's hands, and by extension, the Crawleys' precarious finances were an open secret among the neighbours.

## Events

### Social Gatherings

The Curzon Street house is the setting for numerous social events as Becky climbs through London society:

> [051-014] Many scores of carriages, with vast numbers of guests, were setting down and going up the stairs of the little house in Curzon Street.

These gatherings are sustained by Becky's wit and charm rather than by genuine wealth — the refreshments and entertainments are often provided by Lord Steyne or other patrons, deepening the Crawleys' web of obligation.

### The Curzon Street Catastrophe

The most consequential event at Curzon Street occurs in Chapter 55, when Rawdon Crawley returns home unexpectedly, having been released from the sponging-house through Lady Jane's intervention. He finds Rebecca alone with Lord Steyne — the scene that destroys their marriage:

> [055-001] ...spirit until the bells of the Curzon Street Chapels were ringing for afternoon service...

Rawdon confronts Steyne, strikes him down, and discovers the bank-notes Steyne had given Rebecca — proof, in his mind, of her infidelity. The aftermath is swift and total:

> [055-003] ...had made her exit from Curzon Street.

> [055-004] ...vanished from the premises in Curzon Street together with Mademoiselle Fifine...

Rebecca leaves the house immediately, and the household dissolves. The "Curzon Street catastrophe" [064-002] becomes a turning point that echoes through the remainder of the novel.

### Lord Steyne's Visits

Lord Steyne is a frequent presence at Curzon Street, calling socially:

> [052-013] My lord dropped in upon the Curzon Street house — asked Briggs for a cup of coffee...

These visits, superficially innocent, are the foundation of the scandal that ultimately destroys the Crawley household.

Raggles, the former butler who "retired from the service of Miss Crawley" and invested his savings in the Curzon Street property, is the true financial victim of the Crawleys' residence: "Raggles' house in Curzon Street was to let when Rawdon and his wife returned to London" [037-007], and his fate is sealed when their debts collapse around him after the catastrophe.

## Historical Context

The real Curzon Street exists in May Fair, London, and was a fashionable residential address during the Regency period. The street runs parallel to Piccadilly, between Park Lane and Berkeley Square. During the period depicted in *Vanity Fair* (the 1810s–1820s), May Fair was transitioning from a semi-rural aristocratic enclave to the dense urban district of grand townhouses familiar from Thackeray's descriptions.

Thackeray's depiction captures the social nuance of the address: No. 201 Curzon Street sits at the boundary between genuine May Fair respectability and the more precarious social zone occupied by the Crawleys — close enough to the great houses to host their owners, but modest enough to hint at the financial strain beneath the surface.
