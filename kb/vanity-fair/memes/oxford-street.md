## Overview

Oxford Street appears in *Vanity Fair* in a single, vivid dawn scene: Becky Sharp, having slipped out of the Sedleys' house in Great Gaunt Street, makes her way into Oxford Street to summon a coach and begin her flight from the consequences of her scheming [007-041].

## Description

Thackeray's Oxford Street is glimpsed at an hour when the street's character is suspended between night and day. "At four o'clock, on such a roseate summer's morning as even made Great Gaunt Street look cheerful" [007-041], Becky emerges into the thoroughfare. The coachman she summons is "stationed thus early in the neighbourhood of Swallow Street, in hopes that some young buck, reeling homeward from the tavern, might need the aid of his vehicle" [007-041] — Oxford Street at dawn is a place of nocturnal departures and hungover returns, a stage for the transactions of the London night.

## Events

### Becky's Dawn Departure

The "faithful Tinker" unbolts the hall door and Becky "taking her way into Oxford Street, summoned a coach from a stand there" [007-041]. The scene is one of Thackeray's finest atmospheric passages: the clanging door, the sleeping echoes, the roseate summer dawn, and the coachman hoping for a drunken fare — all framing Becky's deliberate, composed escape from the house where she has spent the night consolidating her hold on the Sedley family.

## Historical Context

Oxford Street in the Regency period was undergoing its transformation from the Tyburn Road — the route along which condemned prisoners were taken to the gallows at Tyburn — into the West End's principal shopping street. By the 1810s, it was lined with drapers, milliners, and coaching inns. The coach stand from which Becky hailed her vehicle was one of many such stations along the street; the reference to Swallow Street (a small street running south toward Piccadilly) fixes the location near the western end of Oxford Street, close to the fashionable neighbourhoods Becky intended to exploit.
