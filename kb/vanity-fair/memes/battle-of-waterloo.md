# Battle of Waterloo

> (032-076) The cannon of Waterloo began to roar.

The Battle of Waterloo (18 June 1815) is the great historical event that divides *Vanity Fair* into before and after. Three days before the battle, the British army's officers are dancing at the Duchess of Richmond's ball in [[brussels|Brussels]]; three days after, many of them are dead. Among the fallen is [[george-osborne|George Osborne]], whose death on the battlefield transforms [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] into a widow and sets in motion the novel's second half.

## The Novel's Waterloo

Thackeray does not describe the battle itself — a notable omission in a novel otherwise so attentive to social spectacle. Instead, he shows us Waterloo through its civilian witnesses: the panic in Brussels as the wounded stream back from the front; [[amelia-sedley|Amelia's]] terrified vigil; the calculating self-interest of those who profit from others' misfortune. The battle is a distant roar, heard offstage — a deliberate choice that keeps the novel's focus on the social world the battle disrupts rather than the military action itself (032-076).

The choice is satirical: in *Vanity Fair*, even Waterloo is refracted through the lens of vanity. [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]], Amelia's brother, flees Brussels in panic, his courage evaporating at the first sound of cannon. [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]], by contrast, coolly calculates that Waterloo, whatever its outcome, will not harm her — and begins planning her next move before the guns fall silent (033-003).

## George Osborne's Death

George Osborne's death at Waterloo is the novel's most consequential event and its most anti-climactic. The handsome, vain officer whose marriage to Amelia the novel has spent chapters chronicling is killed off in a single sentence:

> (032-076) No more firing was heard at Brussels — the pursuit rolled miles away. Darkness came down on the field and city: and Amelia was praying for George, who was lying on his face, dead, with a bullet through his heart.

The abruptness is deliberate. George's death is not heroic; it is merely final. His vanity — the quality that defined him in life — offers no protection against a French bullet. The novel refuses to sentimentalise his death, just as it refuses to sentimentalise Amelia's grief, which it presents as genuine but also self-indulgent, a new form of vanity dressed in mourning.

## Historical Context

The historical Battle of Waterloo, fought on 18 June 1815 near the village of Waterloo in present-day Belgium, was the decisive engagement of the Napoleonic Wars. The allied forces under the Duke of Wellington and Gebhard von Blücher defeated Napoleon Bonaparte's French army, ending twenty-three years of European war and sending Napoleon into permanent exile on Saint Helena. The battle was one of the bloodiest single-day engagements in modern history, with approximately 47,000 casualties on both sides.

Thackeray's readers in 1848 remembered Waterloo as a defining national event — a victory, but one purchased at immense cost. The novel's treatment of the battle reflects a mid-Victorian ambivalence: pride in the victory, unease at the carnage, and a sense that the glory belonged to the dead, not to the civilians who claimed it afterward.
