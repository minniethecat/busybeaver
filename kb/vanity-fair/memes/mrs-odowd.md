# Mrs. O'Dowd

Mrs. Peggy O'Dowd, the wife of Major Michael O'Dowd of the —th Regiment, is one of Thackeray's most affectionate comic creations. She is loud, bossy, and magnificently free of the social anxiety that paralyses [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] and fuels [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]. Her Irish warmth and her conviction that she is the equal of any duchess make her, paradoxically, one of the novel's most genuinely dignified characters (027-005).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. O'Dowd appears primarily in the [[brussels|Brussels]] chapters surrounding the [[battle-of-waterloo|Waterloo]] campaign, where she presides over the regimental wives with an authority that no officer would dare challenge. She takes [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] under her wing, providing practical comfort during the terrifying days of the battle's aftermath when the wounded stream back from the front and the civilians panic.

Her comic function — the stage-Irish brogue, the preposterous anecdotes about her family, the feathered hat she wears to every occasion — masks a genuine moral function. Mrs. O'Dowd is what the novel's "respectable" characters are not: unpretentious, loyal, and incapable of hypocrisy. She does not perform goodness; she simply is good.

## Character Analysis

In a novel where nearly every character is performing for an audience, Mrs. O'Dowd's authenticity is radical. She does not care what [[society]] thinks of her because she does not recognise society's authority to judge. Her Irishness — a source of condescension from the English characters — is also the source of her moral freedom: she stands slightly outside the English class system, close enough to observe it, far enough to be unimpressed.
