# George Osborne's Funeral

> Dulce et decorum est pro patria mori. [035-010]

The funeral of [[george-osborne|George Osborne]], killed at the [[battle-of-waterloo|Battle of Waterloo]], is the event that transforms private grief into public ritual. George, vain and shallow in life, becomes in death an object of veneration — his faults forgotten, his virtues magnified, his memory enshrined by the very people who knew him least. The funeral exposes the machinery of social mourning: the black crepe, the solemn faces, the pious platitudes that substitute for genuine feeling.

## The Ritual

George's funeral is conducted with all the trappings of respectable grief. His father, the elder Mr. Osborne, commissions a grand monument and adopts his grandson [[georgy-osborne|Georgy Osborne]] as a living memorial. [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]], widowed at twenty, wraps herself in mourning and devotes her life to George's memory. The funeral begins her long widowhood — the identity that will define her for most of the novel.

The household's formal mourning is prescribed in exact detail. Mr. Osborne "gave orders that the whole establishment should be put into deep mourning; and desired that the male servants should be similarly attired in deep black. All parties and entertainments, of course, were to be put off" [035-001]. The machinery of grief is set in motion with the same efficiency that Mr. Osborne applies to his business affairs — mourning as a commercial transaction, measured in yards of black crepe.

The narrator observes these rituals with characteristic irony, noting that the same [[society]] that barely noticed George in life now competes to honour him in death. The funeral is, in the novel's terms, the ultimate vanity: a performance of grief for an audience of the living.

## The Monument

The physical memorial to George Osborne encapsulates the contradictions of his posthumous reputation. The monument, commissioned by his father, bore an inscription that converted a vain and careless young man into a classical hero:

> Sacred to the memory of George Osborne, Junior, Esq., late a Captain in his Majesty's —th regiment of foot, who fell on the 18th of June, 1815, aged 28 years, while fighting for his king and country in the glorious victory of Waterloo. Dulce et decorum est pro patria mori [035-010].

Thackeray's choice of Horace's famous line — "It is sweet and fitting to die for one's country" — carries the full weight of the novel's irony. George did not die charging the enemy lines but was struck down in the chaos of battle, and his life had been devoted not to king and country but to pleasure, vanity, and self-regard. The monument, like the funeral itself, is an exercise in what the narrator elsewhere calls "lying epitaphs" over "the corpse of love" [024-027].

## Aftermath

The funeral's consequences ripple through the remaining chapters of the novel. Amelia's widowhood becomes the central fact of her existence — a shrine she tends with the devotion of a true believer, keeping George's memory alive long after everyone else has moved on. When Dobbin finally brings her George's last letter, she responds: "George is my husband, here and in heaven. How could I love any other but him?" [035-010]. The funeral has frozen her in a posture of eternal mourning.

For Mr. Osborne, the funeral marks the beginning of his reconciliation with his son — too late to matter. His adoption of Georgy as heir is an act of posthumous forgiveness that George himself can never receive. The funeral thus becomes, paradoxically, both an ending and a beginning: it closes the chapter of George's life but opens the long narrative of its consequences for those who survive him.
