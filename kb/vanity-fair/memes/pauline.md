# Pauline

**Pauline** is Amelia Sedley's French cook and personal maid (*bonne*) in Brussels during the Waterloo campaign. A minor but emotionally significant character, she provides comfort to Amelia when George Osborne departs for battle, and her own romantic entanglement with a Belgian hussar mirrors the domestic anxieties of the women left behind.

## Role in the Narrative

Pauline appears during the Brussels chapters (XXIX–XXXII), serving as Amelia's attendant in the lodgings the Osbornes share with the Sedleys. Unlike the more assertive Mrs. O'Dowd, Pauline's comfort to Amelia is instinctive and emotional rather than practical.

### Consoling Amelia

On the morning George Osborne marches to war, Amelia sits by the window — "silent, motionless, and haggard" — watching the last bayonets of the column disappear. Pauline finds her there and offers the simplest of consolations:

> Tenez, Madame, est-ce qu'il n'est pas aussi a l'armee, mon homme a moi? [029-050]

The honest girl takes Amelia's hand and bursts into tears, whereupon Amelia falls into her arms and does likewise — "and so each pitied and soothed the other" [029-051]. This moment of shared female vulnerability makes Pauline the only character who consoles Amelia through pure identification rather than advice or remonstrance.

Pauline's words reveal her own stake in the battle: her lover is also among the soldiers who have marched out. The French phrasing Thackeray gives her — *mon homme a moi* ("my own man") — hints at a relationship both intimate and informal.

### Pauline's Hussar

Pauline's lover is a young Belgian hussar named Van Cutsum, whom Thackeray dubs "young Regulus" with characteristic irony. A native of Brussels, he spends nearly all his leisure hours in Pauline's kitchen, and when he departs for the campaign his pockets and holsters are "crammed full of good things from her larder" [032-011].

The Belgian troops, however, show "the greatest activity in retreating before the French" [032-012]. Regulus, "too good a soldier to disobey his Colonel's orders to run away," finds himself galloping miles from the field of action, entirely alone.

At ten o'clock that night, a knock at the kitchen door terrifies Pauline, who has just returned from church. Opening it, she finds her "haggard hussar" — pale, hungry, and having deserted the battle. She stifles her scream, conceals him in the kitchen, and feeds him with beer and the dinner Jos Sedley had been too frightened to eat:

> The hussar showed he was no ghost by the prodigious quantity of flesh and beer which he devoured — and during the mouthfuls he told his tale of disaster. [032-014]

When he declares his regiment was "cut in pieces," Pauline cries out for her mistress — *"O my mistress, ma bonne petite dame"* — and falls into hysterics that fill the house with her screams [032-015].

### A Humanitarian Counterpoint

Pauline's concealment of the deserter Regulus stands in counterpoint to the military values celebrated elsewhere in the Waterloo chapters. While George Osborne dies a heroic death at Waterloo, and Dobbin's courage is praised, Pauline's instinct is to shelter a frightened young man who has fled the field. Thackeray presents this without overt moralizing, allowing the reader to weigh the claims of martial honour against those of simple human compassion.

## Character Traits

Pauline is portrayed as honest, affectionate, and emotionally transparent. Unlike the calculating Becky Sharp or the socially ambitious Mrs. O'Dowd, she acts from pure feeling. Her sobbing with Amelia and her terror at her lover's return are rendered without satire — a notable exception in a novel that routinely skewers sentimentality.

Her bilingual speech (shifting between French and English) marks her as a working-class product of the Napoleonic borderlands, where national identities were fluid and loyalties personal rather than patriotic.
