# Social Class

Social class is the invisible force that governs every character's fate in *Vanity Fair*. Thackeray's novel is a sustained anatomy of the English class system, exposing its cruelties, absurdities, and immutable hierarchies through the intertwined stories of the Sedley, Osborne, and Crawley families. The narrator observes these hierarchies with a mixture of ironic detachment and moral seriousness, never allowing the reader to forget that class is both a social reality and a moral fiction.

## The Three Families as Class Map

Thackeray uses his three central families to map the class structure of Regency England. The Sedleys represent the prosperous merchant class — comfortable but vulnerable to financial ruin, as John Sedley's bankruptcy dramatically demonstrates. The Osbornes embody the rising commercial class, wealthy but lacking the social cachet of birth; Mr. Osborne's determination to marry his son into the aristocracy reflects the merchant's hunger for social legitimacy. The Crawleys (both the Queen's Crawley branch and the Gaunt/Steyne connection) represent the landed aristocracy — titled, encumbered, and increasingly dependent on marriages to mercantile money to sustain their estates and pretensions.

The novel's opening chapters establish this class map with precision. Miss Pinkerton's academy for young ladies [001-001] is itself a class institution: it trains the daughters of the commercial classes in the accomplishments that will secure them advantageous marriages. The difference between Amelia Sedley, who leaves the academy with the honours due to a paying pupil, and Becky Sharp, who leaves as a hired governess, is a difference not of merit but of birth and money.

## Becky Sharp: The Class Boundary-Crosser

Becky Sharp is the novel's great experiment in class mobility. Born to a painter father and a French opera-dancer mother, she possesses intelligence, charm, and beauty — but no birth. Her entire career is an attempt to climb the class ladder through marriage, intrigue, and social performance.

She nearly succeeds. Her conquest of Rawdon Crawley gives her entry to the Crawley family [015-009], and her later relationship with Lord Steyne brings her to the threshold of the highest aristocracy [047-002]. Her conduct in the great world is more polished than many who were born to it. But her origins cannot be escaped — when Rawdon discovers her with Lord Steyne [053-005], the class system reasserts itself with brutal finality. Becky falls, not because she is worse than her aristocratic counterparts, but because she lacks the protection of birth. Thackeray's point is not that Becky is innocent but that the same conduct that destroys her is tolerated — even expected — among women of rank.

## The Governess: Class Limbo

Becky's initial position as a governess at Queen's Crawley exemplifies the class ambiguity Thackeray explores throughout the novel. The governess is neither servant nor family member: educated enough to teach the children of the gentry but not considered their social equal. She eats apart from the family, is addressed without the respect due to a guest, and is perpetually suspended between the worlds of gentility and service.

This liminal position makes Becky's ambition both understandable and threatening to the established order. Her refusal to accept the governess's traditional fate — a life of obscure respectability — is what makes her dangerous. The novel's treatment of the governess theme, developed through Becky's experience at Queen's Crawley [010-012], reveals the class system's dependence on maintaining bright lines between categories that are, in practice, always blurring.

## Money and Class

*Vanity Fair* repeatedly demonstrates that class in Regency England was inextricable from money — but not reducible to it. The Osbornes have more money than many aristocrats but lack their social standing; Mr. Osborne's wealth cannot buy him entry to the world of Lord Steyne. Lord Steyne has a title but depends on the wealth generated by his estates and the deference of his social inferiors. Rawdon Crawley has noble blood but lives on gambling and credit.

The novel is unusually clear-eyed about the cash basis of social position. John Sedley's bankruptcy transforms him from a respected merchant to a figure of pity and contempt [035-001]. Miss Crawley's fortune makes her the object of sycophantic attention from every branch of her family [011-020]. Thackeray insists that what the world calls "birth" and "breeding" are, in large measure, simply money that has had time to grow respectable.

## Satire of Aristocracy

Thackeray's satire of the aristocracy is devastating. Lord Steyne's world is corrupt, cynical, and hollow. The great names of the peerage are attached to people who are often greedy, cruel, or ridiculous. The narrator's treatment of the Marquis of Steyne — his physical appearance, his marital history, his treatment of Becky — strips away every pretension to moral superiority that the aristocracy might claim [047-005].

Yet Thackeray also acknowledges the power of the aristocratic idea — the glamour that draws Becky and countless others to pursue social advancement at any cost. The aristocracy's hold on the imagination survives every exposure of its moral bankruptcy. This tension, between what the aristocracy is and what it represents, is one of the novel's deepest insights into the nature of social class.

## Thackeray's Narrative Stance

The narrator of *Vanity Fair* occupies a distinctive class position of his own: that of the worldly, ironic observer who sees through everyone's pretensions but claims no moral superiority. He is neither a reformer calling for the abolition of class nor an apologist for the existing order. His characteristic mode is to expose the mechanisms of class while acknowledging their power — to show us how the puppet-strings work, even as the puppets continue to dance. This stance, established in the novel's famous preface "Before the Curtain" [000-001], is central to Thackeray's treatment of class throughout.
