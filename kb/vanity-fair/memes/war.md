# War

War in *Vanity Fair* is not a heroic endeavor but a mechanism of fate — indifferent, arbitrary, and transformative. The Napoleonic Wars, culminating in the Battle of Waterloo, provide the novel's historical framework and its most consequential plot development.

## The Shadow of Napoleon

From the novel's opening, war looms in the background. Set during the Napoleonic Wars, the characters' lives are shaped by military service and the sudden reversals of fortune that war brings. The news of Napoleon's escape from Elba sends shockwaves through society:

> "Napoleon has landed at Cannes." Such news might create a panic at Vienna, and cause Russia to drop his cards, and take Prussia into a corner [018-002]

## Brussels and the Eve of Battle

The Brussels chapters depict the surreal gaiety of society on the brink of battle. The famous Duchess of Richmond's ball, with officers dancing while knowing they may die the next day, is one of Thackeray's most powerful set-pieces:

> All the great people in Brussels were at the ball, and the Duke of Wellington was there too [028-002]

The contrast between the glittering ballroom and the distant sound of cannon creates the novel's most dramatic irony:

> Rebecca performed her part in the Waterloo proceedings with her accustomed spirit [033-003]

## George Osborne's Death

Waterloo claims George Osborne, and his death is rendered with devastating economy. Weeks after the battle, the Gazette confirmed the casualties:

> Weeks after the famous fight of Waterloo, and after the Gazette had made its announcement [033-007]

Thackeray does not glorify the battlefield — George falls, and the narrative simply moves on, concerned with practical consequences:

> The great fights of Quatre Bras and Waterloo reached England at the same time [035-001]

His death transforms Amelia from wife to widow, and later enables Dobbin's patient courtship. War's most profound effect in the novel is not military glory but domestic rearrangement.

## Dobbin and the Military Life

Captain William Dobbin represents the unglamorous reality of military service. Unlike George, who swaggers in his uniform, Dobbin is awkward and unimpressive — yet he is the novel's most genuinely honorable character:

> The daring Peninsular and Waterloo officer. He has thick curling brown hair [028-022]

His military career spans decades, taking him to India and back, while his personal life remains in suspension. After the war, characters like Dobbin carry Waterloo as a defining identity:

> A veteran officer and Waterloo man, greatly liked by his regiment [054-024]

## Rawdon Crawley's Post-War Decline

Rawdon Crawley, a soldier by profession, finds himself adrift after Waterloo. His gambling and debts, tolerated during wartime, become ruinous in peace. The contrast between his wartime vigor and peacetime decline underscores the novel's ambivalent view of military life — it provides structure and purpose, but only temporarily:

> Rawdon Crawley, C.B., a distinguished Waterloo officer. We need not only men who can fight [055-051]

## Anti-Heroic Vision

Unlike many novels of its era, *Vanity Fair* refuses to romanticize war. Thackeray, writing decades after Waterloo, treats the battle not as a triumph of British arms but as a chaotic event that kills the undeserving and spares the unworthy in equal measure:

> They allow that he cheated at Waterloo, and was enabled to win the last rubber [036-008]

The novel's subtitle — "A Novel without a Hero" — is nowhere more apt than in its treatment of war. "Waterloo Sedley" becomes Joseph's mocking nickname [057-016], and the Waterloo medal on the Corporal's breast [037-053] is an object of both pride and childish curiosity — not a symbol of glory but a souvenir of survival.
