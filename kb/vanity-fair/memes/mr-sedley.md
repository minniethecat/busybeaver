# Mr. Sedley

John Sedley, Esquire, of [[russell-square|Russell Square]], is a prosperous London merchant whose rise and fall trace the economic volatility of the Napoleonic era. At the novel's opening, he is a man of substance — a member of the Stock Exchange, a fixture of respectable Bloomsbury society, the indulgent father of [[jos-sedley|Jos]] and [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] (004-043). By the novel's middle, he is ruined: his fortune lost to speculation and the disruption of European trade, his place in [[society]] forfeited, his character corroded by bitterness.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Sedley's trajectory is the novel's clearest illustration of the fragility of commercial prosperity. He is not wicked — merely complacent, trusting in the permanence of a world that can be overturned by a shift in the market or a battle in Belgium. His ruin is not deserved; it is simply the way the world works. [[money|Money]] that took decades to accumulate vanishes in months, and with it, his friends, his credit, and his sense of self.

His relationship with his daughter Amelia is one of the novel's few uncomplicated affections. Even in his decline, he loves her; even in her widowhood, she remains devoted to him. His relationship with his son Jos is more complicated — Jos's selfishness is a caricature of his own earlier complacency, and Mr. Sedley sees it but cannot change it.

## Relationships

- **[[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]]**: His beloved daughter, whose devotion never wavers
- **[[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]]**: His son, whose vanity mirrors his own former prosperity
- **[[mrs-sedley|Mrs. Sedley]]**: His wife, whose world collapses with his
- **[[george-osborne|George Osborne]]**: His daughter's husband (briefly), whose father despises him
