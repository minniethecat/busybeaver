# Mrs. Firkin

Mrs. Firkin is [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]'s lady's-maid — the senior servant in a household where proximity to the wealthy invalid is a form of currency. She dresses Miss Crawley, guards access to her bedchamber, and wages a quiet war of influence against her rival [[miss-briggs|Miss Briggs]], Miss Crawley's companion (011-059). In the economy of the Crawley household, Firkin represents the servant class's version of the same ambition that drives the novel's principals: she, too, schemes for advantage, but her arena is the dressing-room rather than the drawing-room.

## Role in the Novel

Firkin appears throughout the chapters set in Miss Crawley's household at [[park-lane|Park Lane]] and during her mistress's illness. She is the first to carry news of [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca]]'s secret marriage to [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon]] to Miss Briggs, understanding instinctively that anything damaging to the Sharp-Crawley interest benefits her own faction (014-018). Her rivalry with Briggs is one of the novel's minor comic threads: two dependents fighting over the right to be the most dependent.

## Relationships

- **[[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]**: Her mistress; Firkin's entire world is organised around her care and caprice
- **[[miss-briggs|Miss Briggs]]**: Her rival for Miss Crawley's favour
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: A threat to the established order of the household

## Character Analysis

Firkin is a minor figure, but she completes the novel's portrait of a social order in which everyone — from lord to lady's-maid — is jostling for position. Her world is small: a bedchamber, a dressing-table, the favour of a sick old woman. But within it, she strategises, schemes, and feels genuine emotion. Thackeray does not mock her; he simply notes that the passions of Park Lane are no less real for being enacted on a small stage.
