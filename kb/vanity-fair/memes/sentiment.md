# Sentiment

> (P01-003) ...some love-making for the sentimental, and some light comic business...

Sentiment — the cultivation and display of refined emotion — runs through *Vanity Fair* as both a narrative mode and an object of the narrator's sharpest irony. Thackeray introduces the theme in his preface, promising "some love-making for the sentimental" before immediately undercutting the expectation of romance (P01-003). Throughout the novel, sentimentality is shown to be not a virtue but a liability: it blinds [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] to reality, provides cover for [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]]'s manipulations, and is mocked relentlessly by a narrator who refuses to indulge the reader's taste for emotional excess (001-027). The concept is inextricably linked to prevailing ideas about [[marriage]], [[gentleman|gentlemanly conduct]], and feminine virtue.

## In the Novel

Thackeray systematically exposes sentimentality as a destructive force at every level of his narrative. Amelia Sedley, the novel's most consistently sentimental character, is introduced through Miss Pinkerton's scorn: "though she no more comprehended sensibility than she did Algebra," the teacher gave Amelia Samuel Johnson's dictionary, a gesture the narrator treats as empty ritual rather than meaningful judgement (001-026). The [[ch04-chapter-iv-the-green-silk-purse|green silk purse episode]] in Chapter IV exemplifies sentimental self-deception at its peak: Amelia gives George Osborne her carefully embroidered purse, imagining him treasuring it as a love token, yet he soon reveals he bought a similar one at Nathan's for a shilling (004-082). The gap between Amelia's emotional investment and George's casual indifference is the novel's first devastating exposure of sentiment's unreliability.

The [[ch12-chapter-xii-quite-a-sentimental-chapter|twelfth chapter]], whose very title announces its generic allegiance, plays out as a deliberate anti-climax. The narrator builds expectation for an affecting parting scene only to deflate it: "the fact is, peace was declared" before Amelia's sisterly tears could produce their full theatrical effect (012-019). What the "most sentimental reader" could not support is precisely the weight of unadorned reality, which Thackeray insists upon delivering (012-024). In a later passage, Rawdon Crawley himself curses his own "d—d sentimentality" as the cause of his misfortunes — a rare moment where a character acknowledges the destructive force of emotion overriding calculation (025-014).

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's narrator operates as a constant antidote to sentiment. Where a conventional Victorian novelist would pause for pathos, the narrative voice interrupts with deflationary wit. The narrator labels the story "trivial, twaddling, and ultra-sentimental" in the opening pages, establishing an ironic pact with the reader that no emotional display will pass without scrutiny (001-027). In the [[ch15-chapter-xv-in-which-rebecca-husband-appears-for-a-short-time|fifteenth chapter]], Thackeray addresses "every reader of a sentimental turn (and we desire no other)," a mock-ingratiating aside that actually warns the sentimental reader to expect disappointment (015-001).

The critique deepens when the narrator shows that sentimentality has real-world consequences. Dobbin's long and sentimental devotion to Amelia, far from being rewarded as in conventional romance, is portrayed as nearly pathetic — a waste of years on a woman who barely notices his existence (043-007). The sentimental Amelia whom Dobbin worships "was no more like the real Amelia than the Madame Tussaud image of the same person" — an almost cruel judgement on the sentimentalist's habit of loving an ideal rather than a person (043-007). Thackeray's satire thus operates on two levels: it mocks the conventions of sentimental fiction while simultaneously demonstrating that real sentimentality corrodes judgement, wastes lives, and enables exploitation.

## Historical Context

Thackeray wrote *Vanity Fair* in the long shadow of the eighteenth-century cult of sensibility and its Victorian inheritors. The novels of Samuel Richardson and the early works of Charles Dickens had established sentiment as a dominant literary mode, one that Thackeray — along with the broader reaction against Romanticism in the 1840s — found increasingly dishonest. Amelia Sedley can be read as a deliberate inversion of the sentimental heroine: instead of being rewarded for her tender feelings, she is punished by them, married to a man who does not love her and blind to the one who does.

The Regency period itself was marked by a growing split between public moralism and private behaviour, particularly among the upper classes. The Prince Regent's own extravagantly emotional public persona coexisted with notorious private dissipation, providing a real-world model for the gap between sentimental performance and actual conduct that Thackeray repeatedly exposes in his characters (014-024). Thackeray's narrator aligns himself with the worldly, ironic tradition of Fielding rather than the sentimental tradition of Richardson, and the novel's treatment of feeling reflects this allegiance.

## Related Characters

- **Amelia Sedley**: The novel's primary sentimentalist, whose excessive and misdirected feeling makes her vulnerable to George's indifference and blind to Dobbin's devotion (004-082) (043-007).
- **William Dobbin**: A sentimental man whose long, silent devotion to Amelia is portrayed as both noble and faintly ridiculous — Thackeray's most complex treatment of the sentiment theme (005-023) (043-007).
- **George Osborne**: The "sentimental youth" who performs feeling for Amelia when it suits him but whose affections are shallow and self-serving (004-082).
- **Rebecca Sharp**: The anti-sentimentalist par excellence, who exploits others' feelings with surgical precision, though even she is not immune: "some apothecary, or house-steward," the narrator hints, once made Becky "silly and sentimental too" (015-030).
