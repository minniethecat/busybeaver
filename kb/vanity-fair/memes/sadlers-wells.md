# Sadler's Wells

Sadler's Wells was a London theatre with origins dating to the 1680s, when Richard Sadler discovered a medicinal spring on the site and opened a "musick house." By the Regency period depicted in *Vanity Fair*, it had become established as a venue for popular entertainments, pantomime, and musical performances, occupying a distinct niche from the patent theatres of Drury Lane and Covent Garden.

## In *Vanity Fair*

Sadler's Wells appears in the novel as part of a catalogue of London's theatrical institutions, enlisted to convey the breadth of young George Osborne's metropolitan education under the tutelage of the Reverend Mr. Veal:

> In the company of this gentleman they visited all the principal theatres of the metropolis; knew the names of all the actors from Drury Lane to Sadler's Wells; and performed, indeed, many of the plays to the Todd family and their youthful friends, with West's famous characters, on their pasteboard theatre. [056-015]

The phrase "from Drury Lane to Sadler's Wells" functions as a cultural span: Drury Lane represented the patent theatre, the crown-sanctioned home of legitimate drama and Shakespeare; Sadler's Wells stood at the popular end of the spectrum, known for spectacle, pantomime, and musical entertainments. Thackeray uses the range to suggest that George's education, while broad, was superficial — he could name the actors but, the narrative implies, absorbed little of substance from the experience.

## The Theatrical World of Regency London

The London theatre scene in the early nineteenth century was strictly hierarchical. The patent theatres — Drury Lane and Covent Garden — held the royal monopoly on spoken drama. Other venues, including Sadler's Wells, the minor playhouses of the Strand, and the "illegitimate" theatres, operated under legal restrictions that limited them to musical entertainments, burlettas, and pantomime. Sadler's Wells, located in Clerkenwell north of the City, drew a mixed audience that included tradesmen, clerks, and holiday crowds, setting it apart from the fashionable West End houses frequented by characters like Becky Sharp and the Crawleys.

The theatre underwent a significant transformation in the early nineteenth century under the management of Charles Dibdin the Younger, who introduced aquatic drama — spectacular performances staged on a massive water tank installed in the pit. This innovation made Sadler's Wells one of London's most talked-about entertainment venues, though it remained firmly outside the pale of polite society.

## Thematic Significance

Thackeray's mention of Sadler's Wells serves a characteristic satirical purpose. George Osborne's familiarity with "all the actors from Drury Lane to Sadler's Wells" is presented not as evidence of cultivation but as a mark of the shallow, showy education provided by Mr. Veal. The novel's treatment of theatre more broadly reflects its social taxonomy: the charades at Gaunt House [051-027] represent aristocratic amateur theatricals, the professional stage is a source of social anxiety (Becky Sharp's own theatrical aspirations hover uneasily between talent and notoriety), and the "pasteboard theatre" of the Osborne children — on which they perform "West's famous characters" — miniaturises the entire theatrical world into a child's toy. Sadler's Wells, as the outermost point on the metropolitan theatrical compass, marks the boundary of what a boy of George Osborne's class could be expected to know.
