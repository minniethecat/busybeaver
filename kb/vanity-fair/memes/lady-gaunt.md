# Lady Gaunt

**Lady Gaunt** is the wife of Lord Gaunt (the Marquess of Steyne's elder son) and the highest-ranking lady in the aristocratic society of *Vanity Fair*. Tall, stately, and severe, she presides over the exclusive entertainments at Gaunt House and is a key figure in Becky Sharp's social ascent and subsequent disgrace.

## Position in Society

Lady Gaunt holds "the very highest rank in Vanity Fair" [047-015]. The exclusive dinners at Gaunt House — as opposed to the great crushes and grand balls "whither everybody went" — are "sacred, unapproachable, mysterious, delicious entertainments, to be admitted to one of which was a privilege, and an honour, and a blessing indeed" [047-014].

Her social power is such that the ambitious Mrs. Frederick Bullock "would have gone on her knees from May Fair to Lombard Street" for an invitation from Lady Steyne and Lady Gaunt [047-014].

## Relationship with Lord Steyne

Despite her exalted position, Lady Gaunt lives under the tyrannical authority of her father-in-law, Lord Steyne. His behaviour towards her — and the household's "Hareem" of women — is one of casual cruelty masked by polished manners. The distinguished courtesy with which Lord Steyne treats her charms observers into admitting "how perfect a gentleman he was," though Thackeray's irony is unmistakable [047-015].

When Steyne demands that the ladies of Gaunt House deliver invitation cards to Becky Sharp (then Mrs. Rawdon Crawley), Lady Gaunt refuses:

> "I will not write to that person," Lady Gaunt said, a tall and stately lady, who looked up for an instant and then down again after she had spoken. It was not good to meet Lord Steyne's eyes for those who had offended him. [047-011]

Her resistance collapses under Steyne's bullying. She writes the invitation and drives in person, "with bitter and humiliated hearts," to leave the cards on Becky [047-013].

## Family Dynamics

Lady Gaunt's marriage to Lord Gaunt has produced no children — "to my Lord Gaunt's dismay, and the chuckling delight of his natural enemy and father" [047-001]. This childlessness triggers the recall of Lord George Gaunt from Vienna to contract a matrimonial alliance with an heiress, ensuring the continuation of the family line.

The Gaunt household is a study in aristocratic dysfunction: Lord Steyne tyrannizes his wife and daughters-in-law; Lord Gaunt hates his father; and Lady Gaunt finds herself caught between pride of rank and personal humiliation. Steyne's cruelty is so calculated that "to see his wife and daughter suffering always put his Lordship into a good humour" [047-012].

## Appearance and Character

Thackeray describes Lady Gaunt as "severe, spotless, and beautiful" [047-015]. Her stateliness and hauteur are the outward signs of aristocratic breeding, but they also serve as armour against the degradations of her domestic life.

In public, she is imperious. At Becky's triumphant musical evening, Becky "patronized Lady Gaunt and her astonished and mortified sister-in-law" — a reversal of the natural social order that marks the height of Becky's ascendancy [051-009]. Later, the diplomat Tapeworm (a relative of Lady Gaunt) is the source who reveals Becky's full sordid history to the astonished Major Dobbin in Pumpernickel [063-015].

## Function in the Novel

Lady Gaunt embodies the paradox of aristocratic womanhood in *Vanity Fair*: she possesses the highest social rank, yet is powerless before the man who controls the family fortune. Her "spotless" reputation is a commodity that Steyne exploits and degrades at will. In this sense, she is a mirror to Becky Sharp — both women navigate male power, but from opposite positions on the social ladder.
