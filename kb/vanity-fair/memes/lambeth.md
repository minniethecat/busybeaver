# Lambeth

**Lambeth** is a district of south London, situated on the south bank of the River Thames. It is notable as the site of Lambeth Palace, the official London residence of the Archbishop of Canterbury. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, Lambeth appears in a single comic reference during the Vauxhall Gardens episode.

## In Vanity Fair

Lambeth is mentioned in the scene at Vauxhall Gardens, where a drunken Jos Sedley, having been rebuffed by Becky Sharp, confides to Captain Dobbin his intention to marry immediately:

> Jos kissed his hand to them as they retreated, and hiccupped out "Bless you! Bless you!" Then, seizing Captain Dobbin's hand, and weeping in the most pitiful way, he confided to that gentleman the secret of his loves. He adored that girl who had just gone out; he had broken her heart, he knew he had, by his conduct; he would marry her next morning at St. George's, Hanover Square; he'd knock up the Archbishop of Canterbury at Lambeth: he would, by Jove! and have him in readiness [006-031].

Dobbin uses this outburst to his advantage, persuading Jos that they must "hasten to Lambeth Palace" to secure the Archbishop — and once out of the Vauxhall gates, bundles the intoxicated Jos into a hackney-coach and sends him safely home. The reference to Lambeth thus becomes the pivot of a comic rescue: Jos's grandiose fantasy of an archiepiscopal wedding provides the excuse Dobbin needs to extract him from public humiliation.

## Lambeth Palace

Lambeth Palace, situated across the Thames from Westminster, was already a well-established ecclesiastical seat by the Regency period. By the early nineteenth century, the palace had served as the London residence of the Archbishops of Canterbury for over six hundred years. Its library, one of the finest ecclesiastical collections in England, and its great hall, rebuilt in the seventeenth century, made it a significant landmark on the south bank.

Jos's comic determination to "knock up" the Archbishop — that is, to rouse him from bed in the middle of the night — reflects both his drunken impulsiveness and the novel's satirical treatment of matrimonial panic. The idea that the Archbishop of Canterbury could be summoned at a moment's notice to perform a wedding for an intoxicated civilian is, of course, absurd — but the absurdity is precisely the point. Jos, in his cups, imagines that his personal romantic crisis warrants the immediate mobilization of the highest ecclesiastical authority in the land.

## The River Crossing

Lambeth's location across the Thames from the fashionable West End is significant. To reach Lambeth Palace from the Westminster side required crossing the river — either by the old Westminster Bridge or by one of the many watermen who plied their trade on the Thames. Jos's imagined journey to Lambeth is thus a crossing, both literal and metaphorical: from the pleasure-garden fantasy of Vauxhall to the ecclesiastical reality of Lambeth Palace, from drunken impulse to sober morning. The geography of London maps onto the geography of Jos's consciousness.
