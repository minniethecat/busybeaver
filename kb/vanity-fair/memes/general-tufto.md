# General Tufto

General Sir George Tufto is the commander of the cavalry division in which [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] and [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] serve during the Waterloo campaign. A middle-aged officer with a scandalous reputation and a wound from Talavera, he becomes one of the many men drawn into [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]'s orbit during the Brighton and Brussels sequences. Thackeray uses him to sketch the culture of the British officer corps — gallant, gossipy, and morally lax — in the last months before Waterloo.

## Role in the Novel

General Tufto first appears in the Brighton chapters, where the army is assembling before the campaign in the Low Countries. Rawdon Crawley mentions him as one of Rebecca's admirers [025-030], and his attentions contribute to the social whirl that makes Rawdon increasingly jealous. In Brussels, Tufto commands the cavalry division, and his identity is established with a telling anecdote: "It's General Tufto, who commands the —— cavalry division; he and I were both shot in the same leg at Talavera" [028-045]. The detail is characteristic of Thackeray's method — a single sentence conveys Tufto's competence as a soldier (he fought in the Peninsular War), his social world (he gossips about wounds with brother officers), and the casual intimacy of the regimental mess.

As the Brussels chapters accelerate toward Waterloo, Tufto recedes into the background — a figure of the military establishment that is about to be tested. His function in the narrative is less to advance the plot than to populate the world: he is one of the many officers whose presence conveys the texture of military life on campaign, with its balls and uniforms and flirtations conducted in the shadow of approaching battle.

## Character Analysis

General Tufto is a minor but telling figure in Thackeray's gallery. He belongs to the world of the regency military — a world of balls, uniforms, duels, and casual infidelity that the novelist depicts with forensic precision. He is competent enough as a soldier: Talavera vouches for that. But his private life is a different matter, and his attentions to a married woman are conducted with the same offhand gallantry he might display on the parade ground.

The novel does not judge Tufto harshly. He is simply another specimen in the Vanity Fair collection — no worse than most, no better than he should be. But the implication of his portrait is clear: these are the men who command armies, and their private morality is no more sterling than anyone else's. The Duke of Wellington may inspire "resolute faith," but the officers who serve under him are subject to the same vanities and appetites as the civilians they are supposed to protect.

## Thackeray's Satire of Military Society

Tufto's function in the novel is inseparable from Thackeray's broader treatment of the military. The British officer corps in *Vanity Fair* is a closed society with its own codes of honour and conduct — codes that are observed punctiliously in matters of form while being ignored in matters of substance. A man may be "shot in the same leg at Talavera" and yet conduct himself with complete moral laxity; the wound excuses, or at least distracts from, the conduct.

This is the satirical mode Thackeray perfects throughout the novel: exposing the gap between institutional prestige and individual behaviour without ever quite saying that the two are incompatible. The reader is left to draw the inference.
