# Genteel Society

> (006-002) We might have treated this subject in the genteel, or in the romantic, or in the facetious manner... But my readers must hope for no such romance, only a homely story.

Genteel society — the performance of respectability through manners, dress, and social connections — is one of the central objects of Thackeray's satire in *Vanity Fair*. Throughout the novel, the word "genteel" and its variants appear as an ironic marker, exposing the gap between social pretension and moral reality (001-005) (007-004). The concept is central to understanding Thackeray's social criticism across the novel's [[ch01-chapter-i-chiswick-mall|opening scenes]] through to the [[ch67-chapter-lxvii-which-contains-births-marriages-and-deaths|final chapter]].

## In the Novel

Thackeray employs "genteel" as a satirical scalpel. In the [[ch01-chapter-i-chiswick-mall|opening chapter]] at Miss Pinkerton's academy, the word is introduced through the snobbish Miss Jemima, who suggests that a bouquet is "more genteel" than a dictionary as a leaving gift — instantly establishing it as a signifier of shallow refinement (001-005). The narrator later explicitly refuses to write "in the genteel" manner, declaring that the reader will get "only a homely story" rather than fashionable romance (006-002).

The word recurs whenever characters attempt to assert social standing. Sir Pitt Crawley's family is introduced as having "very genteel connexions" through his first marriage to Lord Binkie's daughter (007-004). The irony is acute: Sir Pitt is coarse, dirty, and morally bankrupt despite his title, yet his social credentials are precisely what draw Rebecca Sharp into his orbit.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's satire operates through juxtaposition: the more "genteel" the setting, the more vulgar the human behaviour it masks. In [[ch07-chapter-vii-crawley-of-queens-crawley|Chapter VII]], the deserted London townhouse of genteel families is described as a mausoleum of shrouded furniture, exposing gentility as a hollow performance that vanishes when the audience departs (007-019). Similarly, the "genteel marriage season" at St. George's, Hanover Square, portrayed in [[ch16-chapter-xvi-the-letter-on-the-pincushion|Chapter XVI]], is a theatre of female hysterics where even "old ladies long past marrying" sob at the spectacle (016-006).

The narrative voice itself performs satirical self-awareness when it pauses mid-scene to note that "the passions of her mistresses occupy our genteeler muse" — a direct dig at the novel-of-manners tradition, which would gloss over the true emotional chaos unfolding below stairs (016-022).

## Historical Context

In Regency and early Victorian England, "genteel" denoted a precarious social category: families that aspired to gentility but lacked the land and lineage of the true aristocracy. The Sedleys — a stockbroker's family whose fortune can vanish overnight — epitomise this precariousness. Dobbin's hesitation about George Osborne's marriage to Amelia reflects the economic calculus behind genteel appearances: "the want of means to keep a nice carriage and horses... ought to operate as bars to the union" (020-007).

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's supreme performer of gentility, who weaponises genteel manners to climb socially despite lacking birth and fortune (007-004).
- **Miss Crawley**: The wealthy old lady who embodies genteel hypocrisy — demanding to be entertained by scandal while publicly upholding propriety (016-006).
- **Sir Pitt Crawley**: A titled baronet whose "genteel connexions" coexist with squalor, ignorance, and moral depravity (007-004) (007-019).
