# Boggley Wollah

> (003-028) Boggley Wollah is situated in a fine, lonely, marshy, jungly district, famous for snipe-shooting...

Boggley Wollah is the fictional Indian district where [[jos-sedley|Joseph Sedley]] holds the post of Collector in the East India Company's Civil Service. It is Thackeray's satirical name for a typical colonial administrative posting, the source of the wealth that allows Jos to live as a gentleman of leisure when he returns to England. The name "Boggley Wollah" is one of the novel's many comic inventions.

## In the Novel

Jos Sedley is introduced in the novel as the Collector of Boggley Wollah, "an honourable and lucrative post" as recorded in the East India Register (003-027). The district is described in mock-heroic terms as a remote jungle outpost, and Jos is comically out of his element when he returns to London: he finds himself "as lonely here as in his jungle at Boggley Wollah" in Russell Square society (003-030). The juxtaposition of the exotic colonial posting with the mundane setting of London drawing rooms is one of Thackeray's favorite comic devices.

Boggley Wollah represents the colonial dimension of Regency society, where young men went to India to make their fortunes in the service of the East India Company. Jos's income from his collectorship gives him a social standing that his personal qualities would never command, and [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]'s early attempt to catch him as a husband is motivated by the promise of this colonial wealth (003-027). [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] mocks Jos about his Indian experiences, asking if he "talked the language of flowers at Boggley Wollah" (004-081).

## Associated Characters

- [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] — The Collector of Boggley Wollah, whose colonial income funds his London life

## Historical Note

Boggley Wollah is a fictional place-name invented by Thackeray. It belongs to a tradition of comic Indian place-names in British literature, and it satirizes the real system of East India Company collectorship posts in Bengal and other Presidencies. The name may be a playful distortion of actual Bengali place-names.
