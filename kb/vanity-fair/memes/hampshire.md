# Hampshire

> (007-002) ...presented with some remarkably fine Hampshire beer which was then introduced...

Hampshire is the county that provides the rural setting for much of the Crawley family drama in *Vanity Fair*. As the location of [[queens-crawley|Queen's Crawley]] and the Crawley family rectory, Hampshire represents the world of the old English landed gentry — a world of shabby estates, village politics, and rustic manners that Thackeray contrasts sharply with the fashionable London society of Park Lane and May Fair.

## In the Novel

Hampshire is introduced through [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]'s connection to the county. The baronet speaks "in the coarsest and vulgarest Hampshire accent" (007-038), and the local Hampshire beer is served at his table (007-002). The county becomes the site of [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]'s first employment as governess, and her sojourn in Hampshire transforms her understanding of the English class system. She observes that the Hampshire gentry are not as wealthy as "you lucky folks of the City" (014-093), yet their social position commands a deference that money alone cannot buy.

The chapter "Between London and Hampshire" (Chapter 44) and its companion "Between Hampshire and London" (Chapter 45) explicitly use the county as a geographic and thematic counterpoint to the capital. Hampshire represents tradition, land, and the slow rhythms of country life, while London represents commerce, fashion, and social mobility. The county also features in the Crawley election episodes, where rural politics provide a canvas for Thackeray's satire of electoral corruption and the manipulation of the rural vote.

## Associated Characters

- [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]] — The Hampshire baronet, embodiment of the old county gentry
- [[mrs-bute-crawley|Mrs. Bute Crawley]] — The rector's wife, a force in Hampshire village society
- [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] — Learns the ways of the county as governess at Queen's Crawley

## Historical Note

Hampshire (historically also known as Hants or the County of Southampton) is a real English county on the south coast. In the Regency period it was predominantly agricultural, with several significant country estates belonging to old landed families. Thackeray's depiction of the contrast between Hampshire rusticity and London sophistication draws on the real social geography of the period.
