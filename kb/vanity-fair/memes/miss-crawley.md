# Miss Crawley

> (010-019) Old Miss Crawley was certainly one of the reprobate. She had a snug little house in Park Lane, and, as she ate and drank a great deal during the season in London, she was always glad to retire to the country for the summer…

Miss Crawley is one of the central figures in the novel's architecture of [[fortune]] and [[patronage]]. Possessing an independent income of seventy thousand pounds, she is simultaneously the object of universal adulation and Thackeray's sharpest instrument for exposing the venality of genteel society (011-028). Her vacillating favour — bestowing her fortune first on one relative, then on another, finally on none — drives a significant portion of the novel's plot and reveals the mercenary calculations beneath every polite gesture in the Crawley family circle.

## Background

Miss Matilda Crawley is the elderly sister of the late Sir Pitt Crawley Sr., making her the aunt of the current Sir Pitt and great-aunt to his sons, Pitt and Rawdon. She is a spinster of independent means — her fortune of seventy thousand pounds invested in the Funds gives her absolute autonomy over her life. Educated enough to appreciate a French novel and to profess liberal opinions, she is in practice a thoroughgoing snob whose "republican principles" extend no further than her dinner table. She lives between a townhouse in Park Lane and a country retreat, attended by a companion whom she treats with capricious tyranny.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Crawley functions as the novel's great prize — the wealthy patron whose inheritance could transform any recipient's fortunes. When [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] becomes her companion, Becky's intelligence and charm win her favour and raise hopes of a legacy. When Rawdon Crawley secretly marries Becky, Miss Crawley's fury at being deceived — and at her favourite nephew marrying a governess — causes her to disinherit him entirely (011-046). Later, she transfers her favour to the sanctimonious Pitt Crawley, only to see him inherit Sir Pitt's title and fortune independently. Her death, when it comes, leaves her fortune to "the survivor" of various schemes, a final satire on the futility of all the plotting she inspired.

## Relationships

- [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] — Her paid companion, whose intelligence and charm initially captivate Miss Crawley before the secret marriage destroys their relationship.
- Rawdon Crawley — Her favourite nephew, whose marriage to Becky causes her to cut him off completely (010-020).
- Sir Pitt Crawley — Her coarse, uneducated nephew the baronet, whose proposal of marriage to Becky appalls her.
- Pitt Crawley — The sanctimonious elder son who inherits her favour (and briefly, her fortune) after Rawdon's disgrace.
- Mrs. Bute Crawley — A clerical relation who schemes tirelessly for a share of the old lady's money.

## Character Analysis

Miss Crawley represents Thackeray's most sustained attack on the moral emptiness of wealth. She professes to be a freethinker and a "republican" who admires the French Revolution — yet her principles dissolve the moment they inconvenience her comfort or challenge her prerogatives as a wealthy woman. She adores Becky's wit and spirit while it entertains her, but recoils in horror when that same spirit asserts itself in a marriage across class lines. Her deathbed disinheritance of those who schemed for her money is the novel's most perfect comic closure — a reminder that the pursuit of inheritance, the engine of so much of the novel's action, is ultimately a gamble whose prize is as fickle as the dying whims of an old woman.

## Historical Context

Miss Crawley's independence — an unmarried woman controlling her own fortune — was a real but rare phenomenon in Regency England. The legal doctrine of coverture normally placed a wife's property under her husband's control, which is precisely why Miss Crawley never married: marriage would have meant surrendering the power that made her a force in her world. Her fortune in the "Funds" (government bonds) reflects the rise of the rentier class in early nineteenth-century England, where wealth held in paper investments, rather than land, created a new kind of social power that the novel anatomizes with merciless precision.
