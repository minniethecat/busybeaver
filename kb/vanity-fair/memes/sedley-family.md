# The Sedley Family

> (001-001) ...the Sedley family coach...

The Sedley family is the prosperous mercantile household at [[russell-square|Russell Square]] that forms the moral center of *Vanity Fair*'s early chapters. Through the Sedleys, Thackeray traces the full arc of fortune's wheel: prosperity, ruin, and partial recovery — a cyclical pattern that embodies the novel's theme of the vanity of worldly success.

## Role in the Novel

The Sedleys are introduced at the height of their prosperity. Mr. Sedley is a successful stockbroker, his household at Russell Square hums with hospitality, and his daughter [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] is the darling of her social circle. The Sedley family coach collects Amelia from [[miss-pinkertons-academy|Miss Pinkerton's academy]] in the novel's opening scene (001-002), and the family's generous welcome of [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] into their home sets the plot in motion. [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]], the son, has made his fortune in India as Collector of Boggley Wollah (003-027).

The Sedleys' fall is swift and devastating. Mr. Sedley's speculation on the Stock Exchange ends in bankruptcy, and the family is reduced to straightened circumstances. Amelia, once the most eligible young woman in her circle, becomes a poor widow dependent on her parents. The Sedley decline is contrasted with the rise of the [[osborne-family|Osborne family]], and Mr. Osborne's brutal refusal to honor his son's marriage to a now-penniless Amelia. Yet the wheel turns again: Jos's continued income from India supports the family, and young [[georgy-osborne|Georgy Osborne]], Amelia's son, is eventually recognized by his wealthy grandfather. The Sedley story is the novel's most sustained meditation on the fragility of worldly prosperity.

## Associated Characters

- [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] — The gentle, devoted daughter who endures the family's decline
- [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] — The son whose colonial income sustains the family through hard times

## Historical Note

The Sedley family is fictional but represents the vulnerabilities of the mercantile class in Regency London, where fortunes made on the Stock Exchange could be lost just as rapidly. The Sedleys' trajectory mirrors the real economic volatility of the Napoleonic era and its aftermath.
