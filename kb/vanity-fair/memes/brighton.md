# Brighton

> (022-024) ...the blue sea on the other, which Brighton affords to the traveller.

Brighton is the premier seaside resort of Regency England, a place of piers, promenades, and fashionable society. In *Vanity Fair*, Brighton serves as the principal setting for two important phases of the story: the honeymoon of [[george-osborne|George]] and [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Osborne]], and later Miss Crawley's convalescent retreat, where family members circle like vultures hoping to secure her inheritance.

## In the Novel

After their marriage, George and Amelia choose Brighton for their honeymoon, and the resort's glittering social scene provides the backdrop for the early days of their union (022-040). It is at Brighton that we see George's vanity in full display, as he parades his pretty wife before society while simultaneously beginning to neglect her. [[william-dobbin|Captain Dobbin]], ever loyal, is left alone in Brighton as the honeymooners enjoy their time, a pattern that prefigures his long, patient vigil over Amelia's widowhood (023-002).

Later in the novel, Brighton becomes the stage for a different drama when [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]] takes up residence there for her health. The resort becomes a magnet for Crawley family members, each hoping to inherit the old lady's fortune (022-041). The inn at Brighton provides "the best apartments" for Miss Crawley (022-043), and the scene is set for the complex maneuvers by which [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] and his aunt's entourage jockey for advantage. Brighton thus functions as a microcosm of Vanity Fair itself — a place where appearances are everything and the pursuit of fortune drives all social interactions.

## Historical Note

Brighton was transformed into a fashionable resort by the patronage of the Prince Regent (later George IV), who built the Royal Pavilion there. Its popularity during the Regency was enormous, and Thackeray's depiction reflects its genuine status as the premier destination for London society's seaside excursions.
