# Miss Pinkerton's Academy

> (001-001) ...there drove up to the great iron gate of Miss Pinkerton's academy for young ladies, on Chiswick Mall...

Miss Pinkerton's Academy for Young Ladies, also known as Minerva House, is the genteel educational establishment on [[chiswick-mall|Chiswick Mall]] where the novel opens. It is the institution that shapes — and in different ways, fails to shape — the two young women at the center of *Vanity Fair*: [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] and [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]].

## Role in the Novel

The academy is the novel's opening setting and serves as the launching point for both of its principal female characters. Under the stern governance of [[miss-pinkerton|Miss Pinkerton]], the seminary teaches young ladies the accomplishments thought proper for their station: deportment, music, a smattering of French, and above all, a reverence for Dr. Johnson (001-008). Amelia is its star pupil, the beloved daughter of a prosperous family, while Rebecca is the charity pupil — half-French, penniless, and treated with barely concealed condescension.

Thackeray uses the academy to satirize the shallowness of Regency female education. Miss Pinkerton's grandest gesture is to present Amelia with a copy of Johnson's *Dictionary* and to write a pompous letter extolling her virtues (001-009). Rebecca's response — flinging the Dictionary out of the carriage window — is the novel's first act of rebellion against the institutional hypocrisy that the academy represents. The academy's ethos of genteel accomplishment, with its emphasis on appearances and social conformity, is precisely what the novel's satire of Vanity Fair seeks to expose.
