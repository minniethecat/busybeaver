# Dumdum

Dumdum is a garrison town near Calcutta in Bengal, referenced in Vanity Fair as a significant location in the personal histories of several characters who spent time in British India. In the novel's social geography, Dumdum — like [[boggley-wollah|Boggley Wollah]] — represents the distant colonial world that provides the wealth underpinning comfortable London lives. It belongs to the Indian circuit of the East India Company's military and civil establishment, a world Thackeray treats with characteristic irony.

## Location and Character

Dumdum is mentioned exclusively in the characters' recollections and conversation, never described directly. It functions as a narrative marker of Indian experience: "There was a girl at Dumdum, a daughter of Cutler of the Artillery" (003-045). The name itself carries the associative freight of the East India Company's military presence — Dumdum is primarily a garrison town, where British officers and civil servants lived in a hybrid Anglo-Indian society far removed from the drawing rooms of Russell Square.

The Dumdum references are clustered around [[joseph-sedley|Joseph Sedley]]'s social circle. Sedley himself was Collector of Boggley Wollah, and his acquaintance with Cutler of the Artillery forms part of the web of colonial connections that links the novel's London characters to India. As [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] remarks, "Cutler that I used to meet at Dumdum, only fairer" (004-008), comparing [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] to a remembered colonial acquaintance.

## Key Events

No scenes are set at Dumdum, but the town features in the novel's crucial Indian backstory. It is the setting for Miss Cutler's reputation — "a devilish fine girl, at Dumdum" (029-007) — a phrase that reveals the novel's persistent theme of women's reputations travelling across imperial distances. "I felt just as I did at Dumdum with Miss Cutler" (004-022) is how [[joseph-sedley|Joseph Sedley]] characterises his feelings toward Rebecca Sharp, introducing a motif of colonial nostalgia that colours his subsequent romantic misadventures.

## Associated Characters

- [[joseph-sedley|Joseph Sedley]] — Boggley Wollah Collector whose social circle includes Dumdum acquaintances
- [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] — Recalls meeting Cutler at Dumdum during his army service
- Cutler of the Artillery — A Dumdum garrison officer whose daughter's beauty is legendary in colonial circles

## Historical Context

Dum Dum (Anglicised spelling) was a significant British military cantonment established near Calcutta in 1783. It housed the Bengal Artillery and later gave its name to the "dum-dum" expanding bullet, developed at the Dum Dum Arsenal in the 1890s — a posthumous irony Thackeray could not have known. During the Regency period in which Vanity Fair is set, Dum Dum was a key East India Company military station, part of the vast military-administrative network that sustained British rule in Bengal. Thackeray's use of it as a marker of colonial experience reflects how thoroughly India permeated the social world of early nineteenth-century Britain — even those who never travelled there knew someone who had.
