# British Army

> (005-055) ...the service had ordered his regiment, whilst so many of his gallant comrades were to be left at home...

The British Army of the Regency period is the institutional framework within which several of *Vanity Fair*'s major characters live, fight, and define their identities. The regiment — the unnamed "—th" to which [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] and [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]] belong — provides the novel with its military subculture of honor, hierarchy, and masculine camaraderie.

## Role in the Novel

The army structures the lives of the novel's male characters in fundamental ways. George Osborne's regiment is the center of his social identity, and his commission as an officer marks his gentlemanly status. Dobbin's career as a soldier earns him the respect that his humble origins and awkward manner would otherwise deny him (005-057). [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] serves in the cavalry, where his courage and physical prowess make him a respected figure despite his lack of intellect or wealth (011-070).

The regiment's movements drive much of the plot in the novel's middle section. The news that "the regiment was soon to be ordered abroad" (012-013) triggers George's hasty marriage to [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]], and the subsequent embarkation for the Waterloo campaign from [[chatham|Chatham]] brings the entire cast into the orbit of war (025-103). The regimental framework also shapes relationships: Dobbin's loyalty to his regiment is inseparable from his loyalty to George, and the codes of military honor govern conduct in ways that the novel both celebrates and satirizes. The army is also a route to social advancement, particularly for younger sons like Rawdon, who "could nothing beyond his horses, and his regiment, and his hunting" (011-070).

## Associated Characters

- [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] — Lieutenant in the —th regiment, killed at Waterloo
- [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]] — Captain and later Major, the conscience of the regiment
- [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] — Cavalry officer whose military courage contrasts with his social ineptitude

## Historical Note

The British Army of the Regency era was shaped by the long wars against Revolutionary and Napoleonic France (1793-1815). The purchase system for commissions was still in place, meaning that officers bought their ranks — a system that Thackeray indirectly critiques in his portrait of the army as a bastion of privilege and a proving ground for gentlemanly character.
