## Overview

Roehampton appears in *Vanity Fair* as the suburban retreat of City wealth — a place where banking families keep their villas "among the banking colony there" [042-006] and from which the Bullocks, the novel's exemplars of "twopenny gentility" [061-051], launch their social campaigns. Its proximity to Richmond links it to Amelia Sedley's later life, making Roehampton a minor but telling node in the novel's geography of class.

## Description

Thackeray situates Roehampton precisely within the social landscape of early-nineteenth-century London. The "young couple" Frederick and Maria Bullock "had a house near Berkeley Square and a small villa at Roehampton, among the banking colony there" [042-006] — the phrase "banking colony" capturing the clustering of City families who colonised the suburban villages southwest of London. The Bullocks' villa is a signifier of arrival: "Fred was considered to have made rather a *mesalliance*" [042-006] by marrying Maria Osborne, whose "superior pride and great care in the composition of her visiting-book" [042-006] was meant to compensate.

Mrs. Frederick Bullock drives "over constantly from Roehampton" [061-051] to visit Amelia at Richmond, "entertaining her friend with faint fashionable fiddle-faddle and feeble Court slip-slop" [061-051] — the repeated alliteration underlining Thackeray's contempt for this species of social performance. The Major "used to go off growling at the appearance of this woman, with her twopenny gentility" [061-051].

## Events

### The Bullocks' Visit to Richmond

"Roehampton is not far from Richmond" [061-045], and one day the Bullock chariot — "with the golden bullocks emblazoned on the panels" [061-045] — drives to Amelia's house at Richmond. The "flaccid children" [061-045] make an irruption into the garden, where Georgy Osborne "went over his head and bounded into the little advance of Bullocks" [061-045], whose "immense black bows" [061-045] and mourning sashes mark the recent death of old Mr. Osborne. The scene is one of Thackeray's small masterpieces of social comedy: children colliding, fortunes circling, grief rendered in dressmaker's trim.

### Fashionable Philanthropy

Mrs. Bullock "persisted in patronizing" [061-051] Amelia, "found Amelia's milliners for her and regulated her household and her manners" [061-051]. She "drove over constantly from Roehampton" [061-051] in a programme of social formation that Amelia, "entirely at a loss in the midst of their clavers" [061-051], endured with mute discomfort.

### The Donkey-Chaise at Roehampton

In an earlier episode, Mrs. Frederick Bullock visits her sister in Russell Square, "cackling" about her fine acquaintance and boasting that "her sweet Maria had been noticed by the Baroness as they were driving in their donkey-chaise at Roehampton" [046-006]. The donkey-chaise — that humble vehicle of suburban gentility — becomes, in Mrs. Bullock's telling, the stage on which her daughter's social prospects are displayed.

## Historical Context

Roehampton in the early nineteenth century was a rural village on the edge of Richmond Park, increasingly favoured by wealthy City families seeking country air within carriage-distance of London. Its development as a "banking colony" was part of the broader suburbanisation of southwest London that produced the villa landscapes of Putney, Wimbledon, and Richmond. The Bullocks' small villa typifies this pattern: a modest country seat for a family whose real wealth was earned in the City.

Thackeray's satire of Roehampton society draws on a well-established literary tradition — the mockery of suburban pretension that runs from Pope to Jane Austen — but adds his characteristic note of economic realism: the Bullocks' gentility is "twopenny" not because they lack money but because they lack the breeding that no amount of visiting-book management can manufacture.
