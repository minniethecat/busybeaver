# Gaunt House

> (047-019) ...behind the tall caned portals of Gaunt House with its smoky coronets and ciphers...

Gaunt House is the London palace of the Marquess of Steyne, occupying nearly an entire side of Gaunt Square. It is one of the grandest aristocratic residences in the novel, a place of immense wealth, power, and moral decay. It is at Gaunt House that [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] achieves her highest social triumph — and where her eventual ruin is set in motion.

## In the Novel

Gaunt House is introduced in detail in Chapter 47, which bears its name. The mansion is described as occupying "nearly a side of the square" and is furnished with the accumulated treasures of generations of aristocratic wealth (047-001). The house's "smoky coronets and ciphers" on its portals signal ancient lineage and the weight of tradition (047-019). Within these walls, [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]] exercises his immense political and social influence, presiding over the "very best of company" with regal hauteur.

Becky Sharp is introduced into this world through Lord Steyne's patronage. A wing of Gaunt House is assigned to her and Rawdon (047-011), and she becomes a fixture at the Steyne entertainments. Her performances at the charades and her ability to charm the highest society mark the peak of her social climbing. However, Gaunt House is also the scene of her downfall: it is here that her relationship with Lord Steyne becomes the subject of scandal, culminating in Rawdon's discovery of the situation and the violent confrontation that shatters Becky's social standing (053). The palace is thus both the site of Becky's greatest triumph and the instrument of her destruction.

## Associated Characters

- [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]] — The master of Gaunt House, the novel's embodiment of aristocratic corruption

## Historical Note

Gaunt House is fictional but is widely believed to be based on Hertford House (now the Wallace Collection) in Manchester Square, the London residence of the Marquess of Hertford. Thackeray's portrait of the Steyne household is one of the most savage satires of the aristocracy in English literature.
