# Money

> (002-020) "Give me money, and I'll go," said Miss Sharp.

Money is the invisible protagonist of *Vanity Fair*. Every character's fate — rise or fall, marriage or ruin, honour or disgrace — is ultimately determined by it. [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] pursues it openly; [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]] possesses it corruptly; [[john-sedley|John Sedley]] loses it through no fault of his own; [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] suffers from its absence and is rescued by its return. The novel's subtitle — "A Novel Without a Hero" — might as well read "A Novel in Which Money Is the Only Hero." Thackeray's satirical genius lies in refusing to pretend otherwise: in [[society|Vanity Fair]], as in the real world, wealth is the ultimate measure.

## Money as Moral Test

The novel's treatment of money is deliberately unsentimental. When John Sedley's fortune collapses after the Napoleonic Wars, his friends and creditors abandon him at once (005-001). When [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] is disinherited by his father for marrying Amelia, his social circle evaporates. The lesson is consistent: without money, one does not exist socially.

Yet the novel does not simply equate poverty with virtue or wealth with vice. [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]], the novel's most genuinely good character, is comfortably off. [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]], the most corrupt, is enormously wealthy. Money amplifies character; it does not create it. Rebecca Sharp would be ambitious on a shoestring; Miss Crawley would be tyrannical in a cottage. What money reveals is not character but the social system that rewards and punishes irrespective of character.

## The Governess Economy

Rebecca Sharp's career illuminates the constrained economics of women in Regency England. As a governess, she earns perhaps £30 a year — less than the price of a London season's ball gown. Marriage is the only available economic transaction, and Rebecca approaches it with the frankness of a merchant pricing goods. Her failure to secure [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] is a failed business deal; her marriage to [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] is a speculative investment that goes bad.

> (036-048) "I think I could be a good woman if I had five thousand a year."

This famous line — Rebecca's most quoted — is both self-serving and devastatingly accurate. In a society where virtue without income is invisible, the distinction between being good and being well-off collapses.

## Credit and Debt

The novel's economic universe includes not just earned wealth and inheritance but also credit — the shadow currency that allows characters to live beyond their means. Rebecca and Rawdon's London life is sustained entirely by credit, from their Curzon Street lodgings to their dinner parties. The debtors' prison hovers in the background as the inevitable reckoning for those who cannot pay. Thackeray, who knew financial ruin from his own family's experience, writes about debt with the precision of personal knowledge.

## Satire of the Wealthy

The wealthy characters of *Vanity Fair* are not admired for their wealth; they are dissected. Miss Crawley's fortune makes her the object of fawning attention from relatives who despise her. Lord Steyne's wealth purchases access to every door in London but cannot buy him dignity. The novel's satire is not directed at money itself but at a social order that treats it as the sole measure of human worth — and then pretends otherwise.
