# Sir Pitt Crawley

> (007-019) The turkey carpet has rolled itself up, and retired sulkily under the sideboard...

Sir Pitt Crawley, the baronet of Queen's Crawley, is Thackeray's most devastating portrait of the landed aristocracy. Titled, propertied, and secure in his social position, he is also dirty, ignorant, miserly, and coarse — a man whose every action contradicts the pretensions of his class. He talks like a farmer, lives like a pauper, and schemes like a criminal, all while enjoying the deference due to a baronet. The novel treats him with the savage comedy he deserves: a man whose title is the only gentlemanly thing about him (007-019).

## Background

Sir Pitt inherited Queen's Crawley, a substantial Hampshire estate, and has spent his life mismanaging it. He married twice: first to Grizzel, daughter of Lord Binkie, who bore him Pitt and Rawdon; second to Rosa Dawson, a tradesman's daughter whose fortune he promptly appropriated (009-018). His political career — he sits in Parliament for the pocket borough his estate controls — is conducted on the same principles as his personal finances: minimal investment, maximum return.

## Role in the Novel

Sir Pitt is primarily the novel's demonstration that a title does not make a gentleman. His household at Queen's Crawley is a chaos of squalor, his conversation is vulgar, his manners are offensive, and yet he commands the deference owed to a baronet. The paradox is Thackeray's point: the aristocracy's claim to moral and social superiority is a fiction maintained by collective agreement.

His proposal to Rebecca Sharp — after his wife's death, he comes to London and offers her marriage — is one of the novel's most richly comic and revealing scenes. That a baronet should propose to a governess is, in the novel's terms, both absurd and entirely characteristic: Sir Pitt has always married for money or convenience, and he assumes Rebecca will calculate as he does (015-035).

## Relationships

- **Rebecca Sharp**: His governess and, briefly, the object of his marital ambitions. His proposal is one of the novel's great comic set-pieces.
- **Rawdon Crawley**: His younger son, whom he despises. The fact that Rawdon has secretly married the governess his father proposed to is one of the novel's more piquant ironies.
- **Miss Crawley**: His wealthy half-sister, from whom he is perpetually borrowing money. Their relationship is a pure cash-nexus disguised as family affection.

## Character Analysis

Sir Pitt is Thackeray's answer to the romanticisation of the aristocracy in fiction. Where other novelists presented titled characters as naturally superior, Thackeray presents a baronet who is in every respect inferior to the merchants and tradesmen the aristocracy despises. The novel's democratic satire is nowhere more pointed than in its treatment of Sir Pitt: title and character have no correlation whatever.

## Historical Context

The Regency aristocracy was under unprecedented pressure from new commercial wealth and radical politics. Sir Pitt, who controls a pocket borough, represents the unreformed political system that the 1832 Reform Bill would partially dismantle. His combination of political power and personal incompetence is Thackeray's comment on a system that vested authority in birth rather than merit.
