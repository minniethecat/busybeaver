# London

> (017-001) There are very few London people, as I fancy, who have not been to Vauxhall.

London is the beating heart of *Vanity Fair* — not merely a setting but a character in its own right. It is the city where [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] climbs, where [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] waits, where merchants rise and aristocrats scheme. Thackeray's London is not the monumental city of Parliament and St. Paul's but the domestic and social city of drawing-rooms, clubs, parks, and debtors' prisons — a geography of [[money]] and [[society|social position]] mapped onto streets and squares.

## Geography of Status

London in *Vanity Fair* is a city stratified by address. [[russell-square|Russell Square]], where the Sedleys live, marks the respectable upper-middle class — prosperous merchants whose money buys comfort but not fashion (001-023). [[may-fair|May Fair]] and [[park-lane|Park Lane]], where Lord Steyne maintains his establishment, represent the pinnacle of aristocratic power. [[curzon-street|Curzon Street]], where Rebecca and Rawdon set up housekeeping, lies on the ambiguous border between gentility and pretence — close enough to May Fair to claim adjacency, far enough to betray the claim.

> (015-047) She had the handsomest carriage in London, and a box at the opera; and gave the most brilliant little parties that were to be seen.

The novel's precise attention to addresses is satirical: a character's street and square are reliable indicators of their social standing and, more importantly, their social aspirations. When characters move to a new address, their moral trajectory moves with them.

## The Season

London's social calendar — the Season — structures the novel's action. From spring to summer, the fashionable world converges on the West End for balls, opera, and Parliament. Miss Crawley comes up from Hampshire for the session (009-003); Rawdon and Rebecca launch their London careers "before the commencement of the London season" (014-029). The Season is the engine of social ambition: reputations are made in a single evening and destroyed in a single scandal.

## Commerce and Credit

Thackeray's London is also a city of commerce. John Sedley's counting-house on Thames Street represents the older mercantile world (005-001). The City — where fortunes are made on tallow, tea, and colonial trade — supplies the wealth that the West End spends. The novel's financial geography is precise: money flows east to west, from the counting-houses and docks to the drawing-rooms and clubs.

The shadow economy of credit and debt is similarly mapped onto London. Rebecca and Rawdon live entirely on credit, moving from lodging to lodging one step ahead of the bailiffs — a form of "social climbing" that is actually a spiral of mounting debt. The debtors' prison looms as the inevitable terminus for those who live beyond their means.

## London and Empire

The novel's London is intimately connected to the wider empire. Jos Sedley returns from India as the Collector of [[boggley-wollah|Boggley Wollah]] (003-029). The [[east-india-company|East India Company]] officers passing through London connect the metropolis to Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta. Colonial wealth — Indian diamonds, West Indian sugar, Chinese tea — flows into London's drawing-rooms, funding the display that the novel so mercilessly satirises.
