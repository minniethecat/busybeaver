# Raggles

Mr. Raggles is the former Crawley family butler who, having saved enough from a lifetime of service, purchases the house in [[curzon-street|Curzon Street]] and lets it to [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon]] and [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Crawley]] (037-002). He is born on the Crawley estate, raised in its shadow, and educated to venerate the family name — a reverence that proves his undoing. The Crawleys, with the casual carelessness of their class, never pay their rent, run up bills in his name, and eventually bankrupt him. His story occupies only a few paragraphs but is one of the novel's most damning: the great do not merely ignore the small; they consume them.

## Role in the Novel

Raggles represents the collateral damage of gentility. He is proud to have "the family" as his tenants — so proud that he extends credit beyond his means, unable to believe that persons of quality would ruin a faithful old servant (037-006). When the Crawleys' finances collapse and they flee Curzon Street, Raggles is left holding debts he cannot pay. He appears briefly in later chapters, pressing for money that will never come (040-038), a ghost of obligations the novel's protagonists have long forgotten.

## Relationships

- **[[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]]**: His tenant, who owes him rent and feels a passing twinge of conscience
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: His tenant, who regards his ruin as an inconvenience
- **[[curzon-street|Curzon Street]]**: The house he owns and loses

## Character Analysis

Raggles exists in the novel's margins, and that is precisely the point. The Crawleys' story is told in chapters; his ruin is disposed of in sentences. Thackeray does not give him a speech, a scene of pathos, or even a first name. He is a transaction that went wrong, a ledger entry in red ink, a life reduced to the economic function it served for others. The novel's silence about his interior life is its loudest comment on his fate.
