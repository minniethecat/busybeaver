# Mrs. Osborne

## Overview

Mrs. Osborne is the deceased mother of George Osborne and Maria Osborne. Though she never appears alive in the novel, her legacy — particularly the fortune she brought to the Osborne family — drives significant plot developments in the early chapters of *Vanity Fair*. Her marriage portion and the inheritance she left to her children become a point of financial contention between Mr. Osborne and his son George.

## Background

Little is said of Mrs. Osborne's origins, but she was the wife of the wealthy City merchant Mr. Osborne. The only physical trace of her in the novel is a family portrait: "George was on a pony, the elder sister holding him up a bunch of flowers; the younger led by her mother's hand; all with red cheeks and large red mouths, simpering on each other in the approved family-portrait manner" [024-031]. This is the sole visual record of Mrs. Osborne — frozen in the generic conventions of a family portrait, her individuality already erased by the conventions of representation.

Her fortune, described as having reverted to her children upon her death, was considerable. The legal letter that conveys Mr. Osborne's terms to George makes explicit reference to "the sum of two thousand pounds, 4 per cent. annuities, being the property of your poor mother, Mrs. Osborne, which reverted to you at her decease, and to Miss Jane Osborne and Miss Maria Frances Osborne" [026-018].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Osborne's role is entirely posthumous but economically pivotal. George stands to inherit his mother's fortune, and Mr. Osborne's threat to disinherit his son [025-002] centers on controlling access to this maternal inheritance. The trust arrangements surrounding her estate involved at least one outside trustee [026-021], suggesting formal legal structures governed the inheritance.

The irony of the situation is that the fortune Mrs. Osborne brought to the marriage — presumably meant to secure her children's future — becomes instead an instrument of patriarchal control. Mr. Osborne, having absorbed his wife's wealth into his own, now uses it as a weapon against the son who defied him by marrying Amelia. The mother's legacy, intended as a gift, is transformed into a threat.

## The Portrait and the Memory

The family portrait at the Osbornes' house is the only tangible memorial of Mrs. Osborne. It hangs in the front room where she died [024-031], and young George was seen "on a pony" outside the house after her death — an image of childhood innocence set against the domestic tragedy within. The portrait serves as a reminder of what the Osborne household has lost: not only a wife and mother, but the emotional centre that might have softened Mr. Osborne's tyrannical rule over his children.
