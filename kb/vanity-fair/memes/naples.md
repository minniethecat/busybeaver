# Naples

Naples is the southernmost point in Vanity Fair's continental geography, a city of sun, sea, and final reckonings. It is at Naples that [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]] meets his "melancholy end" after the French Revolution of 1830, and it is to Naples that [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] is rumoured to have fled in pursuit of him. The city serves as both refuge and terminus for the novel's most compromised characters.

## Lord Steyne's Death

The most significant event set in Naples is the death of the Marquis of Steyne. "Everybody knows the melancholy end of that nobleman, which befell at Naples two months after the French Revolution of 1830" [064-051]. The narrative catalogue of Steyne's titles and honours — "Most Honourable George Gustavus, Marquis of Steyne, Earl of Gaunt and of Gaunt Castle, ... Knight of the Most Noble Order of the Garter, of the Golden Fleece of Spain, of the Russian Order of Saint Nicholas" — is followed by the simple fact: "His body was buried at Naples" [064-052].

The posthumous eulogy, delivered through the voice of Mr. Wagg, claims Steyne "could not survive the misfortunes of his august kinsmen" (the Bourbon dynasty). His heart "was brought back to Castle Gaunt in a silver urn," a detail Thackeray deploys with full satirical force. The present Lord Steyne is said to prefer "the view of the Bay and Capri and Vesuvius to the dreary aspect of the wall in Gaunt Square" [047-001], a preference that links Naples to escape from English respectability.

## Becky in Naples

Naples is woven into the scandalous end of Becky's European career. After the exposure of her relationship with Lord Steyne, "some people said she had gone to Naples in pursuit of Lord Steyne, whilst others averred that his Lordship quitted that city and fled to Palermo on hearing of Becky's arrival" [055-101]. The rumours capture the uncertainty surrounding Becky's movements after her fall from London society.

Earlier, at Prince Polonia's ball in Rome, Becky appears "leaning on the arm of Major Loder, with whom she happened to be travelling at the time — (the same man who shot Prince Ravoli at Naples the next year)" [064-035]. The parenthetical reference to a Neapolitan duel, tossed off as casual gossip, illustrates how Naples functions in the novel as a byword for the louche and violent underside of cosmopolitan society.

## Brighton as "a Clean Naples"

Thackeray makes a revealing comparison when he describes [[brighton|Brighton]] as "a clean Naples with genteel lazzaroni" [022-024]. The Neapolitan lazzaroni — the urban poor who lived by their wits on the streets of Naples — were a well-known figure in Regency travel literature. By calling Brighton's pleasure-seekers "genteel lazzaroni," Thackeray collapses the moral distance between English seaside respectability and Mediterranean street life, a characteristic Vanity Fair manoeuvre.

## Historical Context

In the early nineteenth century, Naples was one of the largest cities in Europe and a major stop on the Grand Tour. Under Bourbon rule, it attracted wealthy British travellers and expatriates, drawn by the climate, the classical associations (Vesuvius, Pompeii), and the comparatively relaxed social code. The English community in Naples was substantial enough to support its own social calendar, and the city's reputation for intrigue and indulgence made it a natural setting for the novel's closing scandals.
