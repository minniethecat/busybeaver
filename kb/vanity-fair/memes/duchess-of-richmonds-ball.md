# Duchess of Richmond's Ball

> (028-002) As soon as he had agreed to go to the Duchess of Richmond's ball, he thought of nothing else.

The Duchess of Richmond's ball, held in Brussels on 15 June 1815, is the novel's supreme set-piece — a scene of glittering vanity poised on the edge of catastrophe. Three days before the [[battle-of-waterloo|Battle of Waterloo]], the officers of the allied army dance with the ladies of Brussels society while, miles away, Napoleon's army advances. The ball is both a real historical event and, in Thackeray's hands, the novel's most concentrated image of the vanity that gives the book its name.

## The Scene

The ball brings together nearly every major character then in [[brussels|Brussels]]: [[george-osborne|George Osborne]], dashing in his regimentals, flirting with [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] while his wife [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] watches; [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]], awkward and devoted, observing Amelia's unhappiness; [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]], magnificent in his civilian finery; and Rebecca herself, already calculating her next move (028-002). The narrator describes the scene with exquisite irony: the flowers, the music, the uniforms — and, beneath it all, the knowledge (available to the reader if not the characters) that many of these dancers will be dead within the week.

## Structure and Symbolism

The Duchess of Richmond's ball is the hinge on which the novel turns. Everything before it — the courtships, the rivalries, the social manoeuvring — is prologue; everything after — the deaths, the ruin, the slow aftermath — is consequence. The ball is the last moment in which the characters can believe in the permanence of their world. When news of the French advance reaches the ballroom, the music stops, the officers scramble for their regiments, and the civilian world of "society" gives way to the military world of war.

> (032-076) The cannon of Waterloo began to roar.

The structural symmetry is deliberate: the ball is the novel's most artificial scene (everyone performing, everyone on display), followed immediately by its most real event (the battle where performance is useless and display is death).

## Historical Note

The historical Duchess of Richmond's ball took place on 15 June 1815 at the Duke and Duchess of Richmond's residence in Brussels. The Duke of Wellington attended; many of the officers present marched directly from the ballroom to the battlefield of Quatre Bras the following day, and to Waterloo on 18 June. The ball became legendary in nineteenth-century British memory — a symbol of the gallant, doomed gaiety of the Regency era, immortalised in Byron's *Childe Harold* and later in Thackeray's novel. Thackeray's fictional account, weaving his invented characters into a real historical event, exemplifies the novel's technique of using history as a canvas for social satire.
