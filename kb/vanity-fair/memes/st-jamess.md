# St. James's

St. James's is the symbolic centre of aristocratic London in Vanity Fair. It is not a place where characters live in the domestic sense, but where they go to be seen, to advance, and to participate in the rituals of court society. From the gambling-houses about St. James's to the royal levees at St. James's Palace, the district defines the social summit toward which the novel's ambitious characters — [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] most spectacularly — direct their energies.

## St. James's Palace and Court

St. James's Palace was the official royal residence during the Regency, and Thackeray uses its name as shorthand for the world of court favour and aristocratic privilege. The palace procession is described as "making its way down Piccadilly and St. James's Street, towards the old brick palace where the Star of Brunswick was in waiting to receive his nobles and gentlefolks" [048-005].

The "St. James's assemblies" appear as the gathering place of "ladies of fashion," and the narrative voice places itself ironically among "we who wear stars and cordons and attend the St. James's assemblies, or we, who, in muddy boots, dawdle up and down Pall Mall and peep into the coaches as they drive up with the great folks in their feathers" [048-007]. The court presentation is the ultimate marker of social arrival — when [[rebecca-sharp|Mrs. Rawdon Crawley]] is presented, her appearance is described with the full apparatus of court journalism.

After [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] attends Court, he becomes "such a tremendous Tory and pillar of the State" that he insists [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] should attend a Drawing-room too, believing "the Sovereign would not be happy unless Jos Sedley and his family appeared to rally round him at St. James's" [060-015]. The St. James's ritual confers a spurious gravity upon even the novel's most comic characters.

## St. James's Park

St. James's Park appears in the novel as the setting for grand public spectacle. During the visit of the allied sovereigns to London after the defeat of Napoleon, the Park was transformed with elaborate displays: "we saw the Temple of Concord, and the fireworks, and the Chinese bridge in St. James's Park" [020-014]. The park, laid out between St. James's Palace and Whitehall, was redesigned by John Nash during the Regency and served as a fashionable promenade.

## Gambling and Society

Not all of St. James's is royal and respectable. The district was notorious for its gambling establishments, and it is in this context that [[william-dobbin|Captain Dobbin]] reproaches [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] for neglecting [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]]: "I've told you that when you go to town you ought to go to her, and not to the gambling-houses about St. James's" [013-016]. The juxtaposition of court refinement and gaming-house dissipation is characteristic of Vanity Fair's moral geography — St. James's contains both the highest and the most corrupt forms of London society.

The Congregation at St. James's — the fashionable church service — is mentioned alongside the Ring in [[hyde-park|Hyde Park]] as one of those institutions of polite society "which the most squeamish if not the most moral of societies is determined to ignore" [049-021].

## St. James's Street

St. James's Street runs from Piccadilly to St. James's Palace and was the location of gentlemen's clubs, including the Regent Club where [[rawdon-crawley|Colonel Crawley]] leaves his card during the crisis of his marriage [054-022]. After the morning's business, Rawdon and his friend Mac "passed down St. James's Street and entered into their Club" [055-036], the street serving as the artery of masculine club-land that Thackeray contrasts with the domestic sphere.

## Historical Context

St. James's Palace was built by Henry VIII on the site of a leper hospital dedicated to St. James the Less. By the Regency it had become the official seat of the monarchy, though George IV preferred Carlton House. St. James's Park was transformed by Charles II into a formal garden and opened to the public. John Nash's redesign of the park in the 1820s created the landscape Thackeray's characters would have known. St. James's Street, with its concentration of clubs — White's, Brooks's, Boodle's — was the centre of masculine aristocratic sociability.
