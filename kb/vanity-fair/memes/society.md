# Society

> (008-037) "It is the custom, sir, in decent society."

The word "society" appears throughout *Vanity Fair* with an irony that deepens on every repetition. What the characters call "society" — and what they mean by it: the fashionable world of birth, wealth, and appearance — is exposed by the novel as a collective delusion. The title itself, taken from Bunyan's *Pilgrim's Progress*, frames the entire social order as a fair — a temporary market where souls are bought and sold for trinkets. Thackeray's satire targets not individual hypocrisy but the systematic hypocrisy of a social order that calls itself "decent" while rewarding everything indecent.

## The Hierarchy of Vanity Fair

Society in the novel is a rigid pyramid. At the apex stands the aristocracy — [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]], the Marchioness, the hereditary peerage — whose power is absolute but whose morality is non-existent. Below them, the gentry — [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]], [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]], the country families — whose wealth is older than the aristocracy's titles but whose manners are coarser. The merchant class — the Sedleys, the Osbornes — occupy the middle tier: wealthy enough to aspire to gentility, vulnerable enough to lose everything. At the bottom, the governesses, servants, and poor relations who exist at the pleasure of their betters.

[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] moves through every level of this pyramid — from governess to baronet's daughter-in-law to the companion of a marquess — exposing each tier's pretensions as she climbs. That she is ultimately ejected from "society" for doing what society does covertly is the novel's sharpest satire.

> (004-045) She had never mingled in the society of women: her father, reprobate as he was, was a man of talent.

## Performance and Appearance

Society in *Vanity Fair* is a theatre. Every character performs a role: the virtuous wife, the honourable gentleman, the pious churchgoer. The novel's narrative voice, with its knowing asides to the reader, functions as a theatrical commentator, pointing out the gap between performance and reality. When [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] plays the grieving widow, when [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] plays the man of fashion, when [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca]] plays the grateful governess — the reader is invited to see both the performance and the performer behind it.

This theatricality extends to the novel's physical settings: drawing-rooms are stages, ballrooms are arenas, and [[vauxhall-gardens|Vauxhall Gardens]] — where the novel stages one of its most famous scenes — is the ultimate social theatre, a place where all classes mingle and all performances are on display (006-001). The narrator's voice is the ironic spectator, watching the fair with amusement and despair.

## Exclusion and Ruin

The novel's society is defined as much by whom it excludes as whom it includes. When John Sedley loses his fortune, he loses his place in society — his friends vanish, his credit evaporates, his name is no longer spoken in drawing-rooms. When Rebecca's relationship with Lord Steyne is exposed, she is cast out — not because her behaviour was exceptional (the novel makes clear it was not) but because her exposure was public. The mechanism of social ruin — rumour, scandal, the whispered judgment — operates with machine-like efficiency.
