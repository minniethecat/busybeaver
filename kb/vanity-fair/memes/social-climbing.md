# Social Climbing

Social climbing — the attempt to rise above one's birth — is the engine that drives *Vanity Fair*'s plot and the source of its most acute social observation. Becky Sharp is literature's greatest social climber, and her career is Thackeray's vehicle for exploring the possibilities and limits of social mobility in Regency England.

## Becky Sharp: The Climbing Artist

Becky Sharp is born to nothing — a painter father, a French dancer mother — and resolves to acquire everything. Her intelligence, charm, and beauty make her a formidable climber, but her lack of birth places a ceiling on her aspirations that no amount of talent can breach.

Her methods are varied: she attempts to marry Jos Sedley for his money, she charms Sir Pitt Crawley into a proposal, she cultivates Lord Steyne for access to the highest circles. Each attempt brings her closer to the summit of society, but each ultimately fails — not because of any deficiency in her performance, but because the system is rigged against those without birth.

## The Osborne Strategy

The Osbornes represent a different model of social climbing: accumulation through commerce. Old Mr. Osborne has made his fortune in trade and uses it to push his children toward aristocratic connections. His rage at George's marriage to Amelia — a merchant's daughter, and a ruined one at that — reflects the climber's terror of backsliding.

Unlike Becky, the Osbornes climb through money rather than charm. Their strategy is slower but more secure: wealth accumulates across generations, and each generation marries a little higher. George's sister Maria marries Frederick Bullock of Lombard Street — a lateral move in commercial terms but a consolidation of financial power.

## Rawdon Crawley's Descent

Rawdon Crawley is social climbing's victim. Born to the aristocracy, he marries beneath him — and is punished for it by his aunt's disinheritance. His descent from gentleman to gambler to colonial exile is the inverse of Becky's attempted ascent. The Crawley family's treatment of Rawdon illustrates the aristocracy's enforcement of class boundaries: those who marry down are cast out.

## The Price of Climbing

Thackeray is clear-eyed about the costs of social climbing. Becky's pursuit of status costs her her husband, her son, and (eventually) her reputation. The novel does not condemn her ambition — it condemns the society that makes such ambition necessary and self-destructive.

In the end, Becky survives. She does not reach the summit, but she avoids the abyss. This qualified success is Thackeray's final word on social climbing: it is a game you cannot win, but you can avoid losing completely.
