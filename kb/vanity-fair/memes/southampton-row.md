# Southampton Row

Southampton Row is a London thoroughfare running from Holborn northward toward Russell Square. In *Vanity Fair*, it appears as the neighborhood of the Sedley family mansion and a route frequently traversed by the novel's characters.

## The Sedley Mansion

The Sedley family residence is located on or near Southampton Row. George Osborne passes it on his way through the area:

> As George walked down Southampton Row, from Holborn, he laughed as he saw, at the Sedley Mansion, in two different stories two heads on the look-out. [006-049]

The two heads belong to Amelia and her mother, watching anxiously for George's arrival — a scene that captures the Sedleys' dependence on the young officer's attention.

## Young George's Portrait

Southampton Row reappears in the later chapters when George Osborne's son, young George, has his portrait painted. An artist whose works were displayed in a Southampton Row shop-window catches old Mr. Osborne's eye:

> His grandfather had wished to have a picture of him by an artist whose works, exhibited in a shop-window, in Southampton Row, had caught the old gentleman's eye [056-010]

## Local Transport

Early in the novel, George Osborne slips away from the Sedley house via a hackney-coach:

> there was a hackney-coach standing hard by in Southampton Row [003-048]

## Significance

Southampton Row represents the respectable but unfashionable Bloomsbury district where the Sedley family lives during their prosperous years. It stands in contrast to the grander West End squares — Russell Square, Cavendish Square — that the more ambitious characters aspire to. The street's association with the Sedleys marks their position in the social hierarchy: comfortable merchants, but not aristocrats.
