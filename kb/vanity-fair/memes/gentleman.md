# Gentleman

> (005-003) "My father's a gentleman, and keeps his carriage."

The figure of the gentleman — what defines him, who qualifies, and whether the category has any moral content — is one of the persistent questions of *Vanity Fair*. Thackeray systematically tests competing definitions: the gentleman by birth (George Osborne), the gentleman by conduct (William Dobbin), the gentleman by profession (Rawdon Crawley), and the gentleman by manners (Jos Sedley). None emerges unscathed. The novel's subtitle, "A Novel without a Hero," is also, implicitly, a novel without a true gentleman (002-002) (004-002). This theme is examined through the novel's treatment of [[honour|honour]] and [[genteel|genteel]] pretensions.

## In the Novel

The question "what is a gentleman?" surfaces in both social and moral registers. Young George Osborne's retort — "My father's a gentleman, and keeps his carriage" — offers the aristocratic definition: a gentleman is a man who is born one and who commands the visible trappings of rank (005-003). Captain Dobbin's quiet decency — refusing to read others' letters on principle that "no gentleman readth letterth" (005-012), as established in [[ch05-chapter-v-dobbin-of-ours|the chapter introducing Dobbin]] — offers the moral definition.

The novel stages this tension explicitly. George Osborne has birth, manners, and appearance — every outward sign of the gentleman — but his conduct is consistently selfish. Dobbin has no birth, no looks, no social graces, but his actions are consistently generous. The narrator refuses to resolve the tension, instead allowing the category of "gentleman" to remain productively ambiguous throughout.

Jos Sedley represents the comic version: a "gentleman" by wealth and office who is a figure of perpetual ridicule. The "old gentleman" who appears throughout the early chapters — a wag, a tease, a social observer — may be the closest the novel comes to an authorial surrogate on the subject (003-013) (003-044).

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's satire of the gentleman operates through accumulation rather than direct attack. No single character is exposed as a fraud; rather, every character who claims the title falls short in some dimension. The cumulative effect is to suggest that the "gentleman" is a social fiction maintained by collective agreement — much like the Vanity Fair itself.

The concept of "ungentlemanlike" conduct (005-023) operates as a social sanction rather than a moral one. What is deemed "ungentlemanlike" is whatever threatens the interests of the gentlemanly class.

## Historical Context

The early nineteenth century was the great age of debate about the gentleman in English culture. The old aristocratic definition — gentleman by birth — was challenged by the rising middle classes, who argued for a definition based on education and conduct. Thomas Arnold at Rugby and Cardinal Newman's *Idea of a University* both contributed to redefining the gentleman as a moral and intellectual type. Thackeray, writing for a middle-class readership, participates in this debate by dramatising its contradictions.

## Related Characters

- **William Dobbin**: The novel's candidate for gentleman by conduct — generous, loyal, self-effacing — yet presented as physically awkward and socially invisible, as if genuine virtue cannot coexist with worldly success.
- **George Osborne**: Gentleman by birth and appearance, whose selfishness and vanity undermine every claim to the title.
- **Jos Sedley**: The comic gentleman — a wealthy Collector who affects fashionable dress and manners while remaining a figure of ridicule (004-031).
