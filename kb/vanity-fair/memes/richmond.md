## Overview

Richmond, situated on the River Thames in Surrey, was in the Regency period a fashionable destination for Londoners seeking rural respite without the inconvenience of great distance. Its royal park, riverside setting, and the celebrated Star and Garter hotel made it a resort for those who could not afford — or did not require — the more distant watering-places. In *Vanity Fair*, Richmond appears as a place of temporary retreat, education, and suburban domesticity.

## Description

Thackeray's Richmond is a place of transit and temporary residence. Characters ride "on the omnibus from Richmond" [023-029], breakfast there [062-008], and take houses for short periods. The "Star and Garter at Richmond" [061-010] represents the town's capacity for genteel hospitality, while "Sugarcane House, Richmond" [005-038] — the address on a letter — evokes the suburban villas that housed the professional and merchant classes.

The town's proximity to London is essential to its character: far enough for a retreat, near enough for business. When Jos Sedley retreats to Richmond "after the tragedy" [061-010], he is withdrawing from the social battlefield while remaining within reach of it.

## Events

### A Seminary for Young Ladies

A ladies' seminary at Richmond [027-029] places the town within the geography of female education — like Chiswick, though presumably less formidable than Miss Pinkerton's establishment. The "bad luck" attached to it suggests that even Richmond's educational institutions are not exempt from the novel's satire of social pretension.

### The Retreat from Waterloo

In the aftermath of Waterloo, Jos Sedley retreats to the Star and Garter at Richmond [061-010], seeking comfort in its well-appointed rooms. Richmond here functions as a half-way house between the continental catastrophe and the resumption of London life — a place of convalescence for the nerves.

### Suburban Domesticity

The Sedley party "drove back to Richmond, where they had taken a temporary residence" [061-043], establishing the town as a site of provisional domestic arrangements. The proximity of Roehampton — "not far from Richmond" [061-045] — underscores the suburban character of the district, where families might settle for a season without committing to permanent establishment.

## Historical Context

Richmond's status as a fashionable resort dates from the Tudor period, when Henry VII built Richmond Palace. By the Regency, the palace had largely disappeared, but Richmond Park, the riverside walks, and the view from Richmond Hill remained celebrated attractions. The Star and Garter, opened in 1738 on Richmond Hill, was one of the most famous hotels in England, its terrace commanding a prospect praised by poets and painters alike.

The omnibus service connecting Richmond to London made the town accessible to the middle classes, contributing to its character as a place of suburban retreat rather than aristocratic exclusivity — a distinction Thackeray's references reflect.
