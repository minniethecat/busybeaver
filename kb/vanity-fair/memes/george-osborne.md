# George Osborne

> (004-056) George Osborne looked at the two young women with a touched curiosity; for there was a look of ineffable scorn in Miss Sharp's face, and a little gentle appeal in Miss Sedley's.

George Osborne is introduced as the handsome, fashionable bachelor of Russell Square — the natural "hero" of any conventional novel. Thackeray systematically dismantles this expectation, revealing him as vain, selfish, and morally hollow.

## Background

George is the only surviving son of a prosperous City merchant, brought up with every advantage that money can purchase. He is educated at a gentleman's school alongside William Dobbin, where his good looks and easy manners make him a natural leader among his peers. His father intends him for Amelia Sedley — a match that promises to unite two substantial mercantile fortunes — and George, though often indifferent to Amelia, complies because it costs him nothing.

## Role in the Novel

George is the false hero around whom much of the early action revolves. His clandestine marriage to Amelia, following her father's financial ruin, appears romantic but is driven less by love than by pressure from Dobbin and pique at his father's opposition. As a soldier at Waterloo, he acquits himself decently on the battlefield, but he dies owing a debt of honour — he had been on the verge of eloping with Rebecca Sharp, a betrayal Dobbin conceals from Amelia for years. His death at Waterloo canonises him in Amelia's memory, making him a more formidable rival dead than he ever was alive.

## Relationships

- [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] — His betrothed and then wife. George treats her with affectionate condescension at best, and at worst with casual infidelity.
- [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]] — His schoolfellow and unacknowledged benefactor. Dobbin's loyalty is the foundation on which George's comfort rests, yet George repays it with contempt disguised as friendship.
- Mr. Osborne — His father, a stern merchant whose tyranny George resents but whose values he fundamentally shares.
- [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] — He flirts with Becky and plans to elope with her on the eve of Waterloo, treating her as a conquest to be made rather than a person.
- Georgy Osborne — His son, who inherits his looks if not his character.

## Character Analysis

George Osborne is Thackeray's most sustained attack on the cult of the gentleman. He has every attribute that Regency society values: good birth, fine appearance, fashionable dress, military rank, and easy manners. He lacks every quality that might justify the esteem he receives — no honour, no loyalty, no generosity, no depth of feeling. His vanity is so complete that he cannot perceive the worth of those who genuinely love him. Thackeray uses George to demonstrate that the word "gentleman" — a term of immense social power — may describe nothing more than a well-tailored ego.

## Historical Context

George's commission in the army and his death at Waterloo situate him precisely in the Regency military establishment. The purchase of commissions, the culture of the officer corps, and the glamour surrounding the cavalry regiments are all part of the social machinery that produces men like George. His vanity is personal, but it is also systemic — the natural product of a society that equates surface polish with moral worth.
