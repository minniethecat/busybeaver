# Bute Crawley

The Reverend Bute Crawley is the rector of Crawley-cum-Snailby and the younger brother of [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]. A tall, stately, jolly man in a shovel hat, he is far more popular in the county than his boorish brother, known for his prowess at hunting, shooting, and dining rather than for any spiritual zeal. Thackeray presents him as a specimen of the worldly, comfortable rural clergy who treat the Church as a family living rather than a spiritual calling.

## Role in the Novel

Bute Crawley serves as the rector of the parish attached to the Crawley estate. While he himself is more interested in port and fox-hunting than parish duties, he is thoroughly managed by his energetic wife, [[mrs-bute-crawley|Mrs. Bute Crawley]], who directs the family's perpetual campaign to secure an inheritance from the wealthy [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]. Together they represent the grasping, worldly side of the provincial clergy — outwardly respectable, inwardly consumed by family ambition and material competition with the Hall.

> The Reverend Bute Crawley was a tall, stately, jolly, shovel-hatted man, far more popular in his county than the Baronet his brother. At college he pulled stroke-oar in the Christchurch boat, and had thrashed all the best bruisers of the "town." He carried his taste for boxing and athletic exercises into private life; there was not a fight within twenty miles at which he was not present, nor a race, nor a coursing match, nor a regatta, nor a ball, nor an election, nor a visitation dinner, nor indeed a dinner of any sort which he could avoid.

## Relationships

- **[[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]**: His elder brother, the Baronet, with whom he maintains a polite but competitive relationship
- **[[mrs-bute-crawley|Mrs. Bute Crawley]]**: His formidable wife, who manages the family's schemes for Miss Crawley's inheritance
- **[[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]**: His wealthy half-sister, whose inheritance both branches of the family scheme to secure
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: Views her with suspicion as a rival for Miss Crawley's favour

## Character Analysis

Thackeray uses Bute Crawley to satirize the worldly clergy of the Georgian era — men who obtained their livings through family patronage and devoted themselves to sport and sociability rather than pastoral care. Bute is not wicked; he is simply a man of his class and time, comfortably settled in a system that asks little of him beyond showing up. His wife does the thinking for both of them, and he is content to let her manage the family's eternal scheming while he attends to his dinners and his hunters.
