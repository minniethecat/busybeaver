# Mrs. Blenkinsop

Mrs. Blenkinsop is the housekeeper of the [[sedley-family|Sedley]] household on [[russell-square|Russell Square]], a comic minor character whose trajectory from loyal domestic to aloof banker's wife offers one of [[vanity|Vanity Fair]]'s sharpest miniature satires of social climbing. She first appears as an honest, plain-spoken servant who has served the family since before the birth of the Sedley children (018-012), but by the novel's close she has risen in the world — and uses her new station to snub the very people she once served.

## Background

Mrs. Blenkinsop is the longtime housekeeper of the Sedley household at Russell Square. She has been with the family for decades, having "seen the birth of Jos and Amelia, and the wooing of John Sedley and his wife" (018-012). Her speech patterns — marked by ungrammatical constructions and dropped h's — mark her as a member of the lower servant class, despite her position of responsibility within the household. Her name itself is a Thackerayan comic touch: "Blenkinsop" is a comically lower-class English surname, a detail that makes her eventual ascent to "banker's wife" all the more pointed.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Blenkinsop appears at several key moments in the novel's early chapters, functioning as a kind of Greek chorus from below stairs. When [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] arrives at the Sedley household in Chapter 4, Becky immediately sets about charming the domestic staff — she "interested Mrs. Blenkinsop by evincing the deepest sympathy in the raspberry-jam preserving, which operation was then going on in the Housekeeper's room" (004-002). The charm offensive works: by the end of the evening, "even Mrs. Blenkinsop, the housekeeper, condescended to smile" at Becky (004-070).

Mrs. Blenkinsop becomes enmeshed in the household gossip about Jos Sedley's supposed attachment to Becky, holding "long and intimate conversations with Miss Wirt, the governess" on the subject (006-006). Yet she also voices a plain-spoken democratic counsel when Mrs. Sedley objects that Jos would demean himself by marrying an artist's daughter:

> [006-007] "But, lor', Ma'am," ejaculated Mrs. Blenkinsop, "we was only grocers when we married Mr. S., who was a stock-broker's clerk, and we hadn't five hundred pounds among us, and we're rich enough now."

This is the same Mrs. Blenkinsop who, when Becky's true character begins to show, harbors deep suspicions. After Becky's departure from the Sedley household, Blenkinsop comforts the weeping Amelia: "Don't take on, Miss. I didn't like to tell you. But none of us in the house have liked her except at fust. I sor her with my own eyes reading your Ma's letters" (006-071). She shares her class-based distrust of governesses with the lady's maid Pinner: "I don't trust them governesses, Pinner. They give themselves the hairs and hupstarts of ladies, and their wages is no better than you nor me" (006-073).

When the Sedleys fall into poverty, Mrs. Blenkinsop proves her genuine loyalty. "Honest old Mrs. Blenkinsop indeed" — the only servant willing to stay without wages — "accompanied the fallen people into their new and humble place of refuge, where she tended them and grumbled against them for a while" (018-012). Her grumbling loyalty is the most complex note in her portrait: she is neither saint nor hypocrite, but something recognizably human.

Yet her story takes a remarkable turn in the novel's second half. By the time Becky Crawley returns to England after Waterloo, Mrs. Blenkinsop has married a banker and achieved gentility. And she uses it. "Even Mrs. Blenkinsop, the banker's wife, cut her at church" (037-013) — Becky, the former favourite of the servants' hall, is now beneath the notice of the housekeeper-turned-banker's-wife. The novel does not tell us whether Mrs. Blenkinsop also cut Amelia, but the phrase "Even Mrs. Blenkinsop" implies that her social scissors were applied broadly.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Blenkinsop is a miniature masterpiece of Thackerayan irony. Her character arc — loyal housekeeper becomes banker's wife who snubs her former mistress — mirrors in small the novel's great theme: in Vanity Fair, everyone climbs, and everyone kicks down. She is no hypocrite in the ordinary sense; her grumbling loyalty to the ruined Sedleys proves that she is capable of genuine attachment. But give her money and status, and she becomes precisely the kind of snob she once mocked.

Her plain-spoken class wisdom — "we was only grocers" — is the novel's own democratic truth, spoken by a servant. But when she herself rises, she forgets it. This is not villainy; it is Vanity Fair's universal law. Thackeray does not single out Mrs. Blenkinsop for special condemnation. She is simply human, and the fair grinds on.

Her name deserves attention. "Blenkinsop" is a surname of comically lower-class English resonance — the kind of name Thackeray deploys to signal social standing without a word of description. Her transformation from "Mrs. Blenkinsop, the housekeeper" to "Mrs. Blenkinsop, the banker's wife" — the same name, attached to a different social identifier — says everything about the fluidity and fraudulence of status in the world of the novel.
