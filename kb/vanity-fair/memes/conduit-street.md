# Conduit Street

Conduit Street, off Regent Street in London's West End, was in the Regency period a centre of bespoke tailoring, alongside Savile Row and Cork Street. In *Vanity Fair*, it is the domain of Mr. Woolsey, the military tailor who serves as the unlikely agent of [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]]'s romantic ambitions — proving that in Vanity Fair, a good tailor can be as important as a good character.

## The Tailor's Domain

Mr. Woolsey, "military tailor, of Conduit Street," appears at [[william-dobbin|Major Dobbin]]'s summons to transform him into a presentable figure (038-030). The military tailor was a specific institution in Regency London — not merely a clothier but a kind of social engineer, responsible for the magnificent uniforms that were the passport to society. Woolsey's Conduit Street premises would have been filled with scarlet cloth, gold lace, and the fittings that turned ordinary men into officers.

## Wax Dolls and Kid Gloves

Thackeray uses Conduit Street as a metonym for the artifice of masculine fashion. Young George Osborne wears "the little kid gloves which Lamb's Conduit Street could furnish" (056-007) — the gloves that make his hands look delicate and gentlemanly, concealing the coarseness beneath. Lamb's was a real glover's establishment in the street, and Thackeray's detail grounds the novel in the actual geography of Regency consumption.

It is in Conduit Street that "Mr. Woolsey... gave a loose to his imagination" (056-016), producing the extravagant uniforms that turn military men into peacocks. The tailor's imagination is a dangerous thing — it inflates not just the shoulders of Major Dobbin's coat but his hopes as well.

## Commerce and Character

The street embodies a recurring theme in the novel: that external appearance, manufactured by commerce, matters more than internal worth. Dobbin's new coat from Conduit Street cannot change who he is, but it can — for a moment — change how he is seen. This is the promise and the illusion that Conduit Street sells.

## Historical Context

Conduit Street was laid out in the early eighteenth century as part of the westwards expansion of London. By the Regency period, the street was known for high-end retail including tailors, glovers, and haberdashers. It ran from Regent Street to New Bond Street, placing it at the centre of fashionable London. Lamb's, the glover's mentioned by Thackeray, was indeed a well-known establishment. The street's name derives from a conduit that once carried water to the City, but by Thackeray's time the water had been replaced by a flow of fashionable commerce.
