# Thames Street

Thames Street is a commercial thoroughfare in the City of London, near the River Thames. In *Vanity Fair*, it is the site of both the Osborne family counting-house and John Sedley's coal business, making it a key location for the novel's commercial subplot.

## The Osborne Counting-House

Mr. Osborne's business premises are on Thames Street. When Captain Dobbin needs to report to George about old Mr. Osborne's reaction to George's marriage, he goes there:

> going into the City to the paternal counting-house in Thames Street, he despatched thence a note to Miss Osborne [024-001]

## Sedley's Coal Business

After John Sedley's financial ruin, his reduced circumstances are reflected in his new business address:

> Mr. Sedley, Sole Agent for the Black Diamond and Anti-Cinder Coal Association, Bunker's Wharf, Thames Street, and Anna-Maria Cottages, Fulham Road West. [037-060]

This grandiose title for a modest coal agency is a classic Thackerayan touch — the more elaborate the name, the humbler the reality.

## Dobbin's Early Associations

Thames Street is also connected to Dobbin's family. A schoolmate at Dr. Swishtail's spots the cart of "Dobbin & Rudge, Grocers and Oilmen, Thames Street" [005-001], and young Dobbin is mercilessly teased for his family's trade connections.

## Significance

Thames Street embodies the commercial foundation of Regency society in *Vanity Fair*. While the novel's aristocratic characters live in Mayfair and Gaunt Square, the money that sustains them often originates in the counting-houses and wharves of Thames Street. The Osbornes' wealth comes from this mercantile world, and their social aspirations represent the tension between the City and the West End.
