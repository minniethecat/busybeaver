# Fortune

> (006-008) This girl has no fortune; no more had Mrs. Sedley.

Fortune — the possession or pursuit of wealth — is the gravitational centre around which every character in *Vanity Fair* orbits. Thackeray presents a world in which financial standing determines social position, [[marriage|marriage]] prospects, and even moral reputation with a frankness that scandalised Victorian readers. The novel's relentless focus on money functions as both social realism and moral satire, exposing the cash-nexus beneath the veneer of polite society (001-016) (003-030).

## In the Novel

Fortune operates in the novel as both inheritance and aspiration. Miss Crawley's fortune — the great prize that motivates the schemes of the Crawley family — is the most explicit example: "the best part of Miss Crawley's fortune" is the object of speculation by every relation (011-038). Sir Pitt Crawley's second wife "inherited her mother's large fortune," which the baronet promptly proposed to manage for himself (009-018).

Rebecca Sharp, the novel's most acute analyst of fortune's operations, understands that wealth is never truly separate from character in this world. Her valet "made a fortune out of his wardrobe" (003-030), and her own trajectory is shaped by the pursuit of financial security at every turn. Jos Sedley's fortune as Collector of Boggley Wollah, established in [[ch04-chapter-iv-the-green-silk-purse|Chapter IV]], makes him a target; Amelia Sedley's lack of one makes her vulnerable (006-008).

The novel also charts the loss of fortune as moral test: Mr. Sedley's bankruptcy transforms him from paterfamilias to dependent, while George Osborne's fortune — inherited from a father who disowned him — arrives too late to benefit its intended recipient.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's narrator adopts a worldly, cynical tone on the subject of money, refusing the sentimental convention that love conquers all. The observation that Mr. Sedley "attributed his good fortune to the sole agency and benevolence of his own" cleverness (005-044) is typical irony — fortune in Vanity Fair is never solely one's own doing.

The novel's satire cuts both ways: it exposes those who scheme for fortune (the Crawleys, Rebecca) and those who pretend to be above it (Amelia's sentimentalism). The narrator treats the marriage market as a subspecies of the financial market, with young women as commodities "universally sold in [[ch12-chapter-xii-quite-a-sentimental-chapter|marriage]]" (012-020).

## Historical Context

The Regency period saw the rise of new fortunes from trade, colonial administration, and speculation, challenging the old landed aristocracy. Jos Sedley's "honourable and lucrative post" in India (003-027) represents the colonial source of much Regency wealth. The Nabob figure — the Englishman who returned from India with a fortune — was a stock character of period satire, and Jos embodies it. Meanwhile, the Crawley family's anxiety about Miss Crawley's fortune reflects the genuine fragility of aristocratic finances in an era when agricultural rents were declining.

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: Born without fortune, she pursues it through marriage, charm, and speculation — the novel's most resourceful fortune-hunter (003-030).
- **Miss Crawley**: The wealthy spinster whose fortune makes her the most courted character in the novel, demonstrating that money, not title, commands real power (011-038).
- **Jos Sedley**: "Collector of Boggley Wollah," whose Indian fortune makes him simultaneously a prize and a figure of fun (003-027).
- **Sir Pitt Crawley**: An aristocrat whose title cannot mask his perpetual need for ready money, remarrying for a fortune after his first wife's death (009-018).
