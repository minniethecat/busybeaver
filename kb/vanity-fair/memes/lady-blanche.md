# Lady Blanche Thistlewood

Lady Blanche Thistlewood is the daughter of the [[lady-bareacres|Earl and Countess of Bareacres]], a young aristocratic woman who personifies inherited disdain. At a general's party in Brussels she dances with [[george-osborne|George Osborne]], and at the dinner that follows she stares at [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] through her eye-glass.

Later, she avoids [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] as though the adventuress carried an infectious disease (032-033). Her marriage to Lord Gaunt places her inside [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]'s orbit at Gaunt House, where she endures her father-in-law's verbal cruelty and writes the melancholy poems that Lady Steyne sings at his gatherings.

## Background

Lady Blanche was born into the noble but declining House of Bareacres, whose name satirises the landed aristocracy's vast "barren acres" of unproductive pride. Her father is a genial bankrupt who will dine anywhere; her mother is a monument of frigid snobbery who gathers her daughters about as if Becky Sharp's touch would contaminate them (037-013).

Lady Blanche was raised in this atmosphere of titled insolvency and was married to Lord Gaunt — the heir to the Marquess of Steyne — in what appears to have been an arranged match between two great houses whose best days were behind them.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Blanche first appears in the Brussels sequence before [[duchess-of-richmonds-ball|Waterloo]], where she dances the newly imported waltz with George Osborne "for hours" at a general's party (028-016). Her response to George's dinner invitation sets the tone for her character:

> (028-018) "Gracious Heaven, Mamma—you don't suppose the man would bring his wife," shrieked Lady Blanche, who had been languishing in George's arms in the newly imported waltz for hours the night before. "The men are bearable, but their women—"

At the dinner itself, she stares at Amelia through her eye-glass, excludes her from the conversation, and leaves George's new bride in misery (028-021).

During the Brussels panic, she sits with her mother in the carriage in the porte-cochere of their hotel, waiting for horses while Rebecca Sharp — the woman they have cut — holds the keys to their escape (032-032).

In the novel's second half, Lady Blanche has become Lady Gaunt, married to Lord Steyne's son. She lives unhappily at Gaunt House, at odds with her husband and subject to her father-in-law's cutting abuse. Lord Steyne taunts her about her family's ruin — the Bareacres have lost everything to bailiffs — and mocks her pretensions to virtue (049-012).

She writes the poems that the fragile Lady Steyne performs at Lord Steyne's gatherings, hinting at a sensitivity beneath the haughty surface (049-003). At the Gaunt House confrontation with Becky, she and her mother exchange "terrified and angry looks" — the old guard disturbed to find the adventuress they thought they had crushed still standing in their drawing room (049-026).

## Relationships

- **[[lady-bareacres|Lady Bareacres]]**: Her mother, who models and reinforces her contempt for social climbers; they appear as a snobbish unit throughout the novel
- **Lord Bareacres**: Her father, the bankrupt Earl whose appetite for dinners overrides his pride
- **Lord Gaunt**: Her husband, from whom she is estranged; they live in separate quarters at Gaunt House, "disagreeing" with one another (047-011)
- **[[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]**: Her father-in-law, who verbally abuses her publicly, mocking her family's bankruptcy and her childlessness
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: The social climber she instinctively despises and shuns — and who triumphs over the Bareacres women in the Brussels carriage scene
- **[[george-osborne|George Osborne]]**: Her dancing partner at the Brussels ball; she is happy to use him for his money but despises his wife
- **[[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]]**: The "beggar's daughter" she stares at through her eye-glass — the target of aristocratic scorn

## Character Analysis

Lady Blanche functions as a secondary instrument in Thackeray's dismantling of aristocratic pretension. Her name — "Blanche," French for white — carries an irony the novel never states directly: the pure white of noble blood is, in her case, the pallor of sterility. She bears no children, her marriage is a failure, and "Bareacres" announces the barrenness of the life she represents.

She is "Thistlewood" by birth — thistles being prickly, ornamental, and fundamentally useless plants — before becoming "Gaunt" by marriage, a name that suggests the emaciation of the old order.

Thackeray does not make her a villain; she is too minor and too passive for villainy. She is instead a product: the output of an aristocratic system that teaches its daughters to value rank above all else, then marries them into dynastic alliances where they are resented by husbands and abused by fathers-in-law.

Her eye-glass — through which she stares at Amelia — is the perfect emblem of her existence: a lens for looking down on others, through which she herself is never truly seen.

The novel grants her one moment of poignancy: she writes poetry, and Lord Steyne — in the same breath he uses to humiliate her — reveals that "Blanche writes them," the verses Lady Steyne sings (049-003). It is the only hint that behind the eye-glass and the shrieking snobbery there might be an interior life, however cramped and unhappy.
