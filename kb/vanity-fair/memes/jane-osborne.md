# Jane Osborne

**Jane Osborne**, commonly called **Miss Osborne**, is the elder daughter of Mr. Osborne of Russell Square and sister to George and Maria Osborne. A "fine black-browed" young lady educated by the best governesses and milliners, she passes through the novel as one of its quietest tragedies: the spinster daughter who devotes her life to a tyrannical father and is left with nothing but a carriage, charitable committees, and regret.

## Early Life

Jane and her sister Maria are raised in comfort at Russell Square, under the supervision of Miss Wirt, their "raw-boned Vestal" of a governess [012-001]. Their lives are "pompous and orderly," their amusements "intolerably dull and decorous," and their self-respect "prodigious" [012-001]. Together with Maria, she patronizes Amelia Sedley insufferably, wondering aloud "What could George find in that creature?" [012-001].

## The Dobbin Interview

Jane's most significant scene comes when Captain Dobbin visits to enlist her support for George's secret marriage to Amelia. Dobbin's nervousness — "many twitchings of his face," the "rapid buttoning and unbuttoning of his frock-coat" — leads Jane to believe, with spinsterly hopefulness, that the Captain is about to propose to her [020-016]. The clock striking twelve becomes a "prolonged knell to the anxious spinster" when Dobbin instead reveals that he has come to talk about George [020-017].

Dobbin's adroit question — "Would a man, think you, give YOU up if you were poor?" — touches Jane's heart. "I don't know whether we poor girls ought to believe what you men say, Captain," she replies. "I'm afraid you are cruel, cruel deceivers" [020-020]. Dobbin feels "a pressure of the hand which Miss Osborne had extended to him" — a fleeting gesture of romantic possibility that leads nowhere [020-020].

## Life After George's Death

Following George's death at Waterloo and his father's estrangement from Amelia, Jane settles into the role of companion to the increasingly violent and moody old Mr. Osborne. She becomes, in Thackeray's devastating summary:

> a lonely, miserable, persecuted old maid. [047-025]

She drives through Hyde Park in her fine carriage, with her name on half the public charity lists of the town, and looks out day after day hoping to catch a glimpse of her nephew, little Georgy — the beautiful boy who is her brother's son and whom she has seen only once.

## Relationship with Maria

After Maria marries Frederick Bullock, Jane watches her sister's social climbing with clear-eyed realism. When Maria and Frederick quarrel with old Osborne over the marriage settlement, Jane consoles her sister with the bitter truth: "I always told you, Maria, that it was your money he loved and not you" [042-014].

Maria, now Mrs. Frederick Bullock, visits Russell Square occasionally in her chariot with its emblazoned bullocks, simpering about her fine acquaintance and urging Jane to extract more money from their father. Jane endures these visits with the resignation of a woman who long ago stopped expecting happiness.

## Character

Jane Osborne is neither villain nor heroine. She shares her family's snobbery and money-consciousness, yet she possesses a capacity for sympathy that distinguishes her from her father. Her brief moment of hope during Dobbin's interview — that this decent, awkward soldier might be about to change her life — is one of the novel's quietest heartbreaks. She is a prisoner of Russell Square, of her father's moods, and of the limited destinies available to unmarried women of her class.
