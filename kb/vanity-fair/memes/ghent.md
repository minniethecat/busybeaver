# Ghent

> (025-046) "Ghent! Brussels!" cried out Amelia with a sudden shock...

Ghent is the historic Belgian city that figures prominently in the Waterloo sequence of *Vanity Fair*. It is mentioned first as the seat of the exiled French king Louis XVIII's court during the Hundred Days, and then as the intended refuge of [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] when panic grips Brussels on the eve of battle.

## In the Novel

Ghent is introduced early in the Waterloo chapters as a place of significance: "many persons of note" were traveling "on their way to Brussels and Ghent" (026-018), and the French king's court had established itself there (025-045). Amelia and Jos travel "in canal boats to Bruges and Ghent, thence to march to Brussels" (028-006), establishing the city as a waypoint between England and the theatre of war.

When panic breaks out in Brussels after the Duchess of Richmond's ball, Jos Sedley's thoughts turn immediately to flight. "The King of France is at Ghent" (031-015), he observes with affected importance, rationalizing his plan to flee. As the exodus from Brussels begins, Jos declares "I'm off for Ghent" (032-011), joining the stream of civilians galloping "away by the Ghent barrier" (032-001). His retreat to Ghent — leaving his sister Amelia behind — becomes one of the novel's most damning moments of cowardice, and the city's association with Jos's flight is a permanent stain on his character. In the novel's moral geography, Ghent represents the safe option, the place of retreat and self-preservation, in contrast to the danger and duty of Brussels.

## Associated Characters

- [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] — Flees to Ghent in panic, abandoning his sister in Brussels

## Historical Note

Ghent is a real Belgian city with a rich medieval history. During the Hundred Days campaign of 1815, King Louis XVIII of France did indeed establish his court-in-exile at Ghent while Napoleon held Paris. Thackeray's depiction of Ghent as a refuge for those fleeing the approaching battle is historically accurate.
