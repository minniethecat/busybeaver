# Drury Lane

**Drury Lane** is a street in the Covent Garden area of London, most famous as the site of the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane — one of London's two patent theatres (along with Covent Garden) and the most celebrated stage of the Regency era. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, Drury Lane serves as a cultural reference point, signalling characters' familiarity with the London theatre world.

## In Vanity Fair

### The Narrator's Recollection

Thackeray's narrator, in one of the novel's direct addresses to the reader, recalls a boyhood trip to Drury Lane to see the King:

> Do you remember, dear M—, oh friend of my youth, how one blissful night five-and-twenty years since, the "Hypocrite" being acted, Elliston being manager, Dowton and Liston performers, two boys had leave from their loyal masters to go out from Slaughter-House School where they were educated and to appear on Drury Lane stage, amongst a crowd which assembled there to greet the king [048-002].

The reference is filled with period specificity: Robert William Elliston managed Drury Lane from 1819 to 1826; William Dowton and John Liston were celebrated comic actors of the day. The passage evokes the excitement of the patent theatres in their Regency heyday.

### The Todd Family's Theatrical Education

Drury Lane is also used metonymically to convey the breadth of London's theatrical world. Of the Todd family, the narrator notes:

> In the company of this gentleman they visited all the principal theatres of the metropolis; knew the names of all the actors from Drury Lane to Sadler's Wells [056-015].

The phrase "from Drury Lane to Sadler's Wells" spans the full spectrum of London theatre: Drury Lane, the prestigious patent theatre, to Sadler's Wells, a popular house in Islington known for spectacle and pantomime. Knowing the actors at both marks one as a true theatre enthusiast.

## Theatre Royal, Drury Lane

The Theatre Royal on Drury Lane was, along with Covent Garden, one of only two theatres in London licensed to perform spoken drama during the Regency period (all other theatres were restricted to musical entertainments under the Licensing Act of 1737). It was the home of many of the era's greatest actors, including Edmund Kean and Sarah Siddons.
