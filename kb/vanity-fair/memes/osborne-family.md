# The Osborne Family

> (042-001) ...which treats of the Osborne family.

The Osborne family is the prosperous mercantile household that represents the City side of the novel's social world — new money, commercial values, and the relentless pursuit of social advancement. Headed by the stern, unforgiving Mr. Osborne, the family's fortunes rise and fall in counterpoint to those of the [[sedley-family|Sedley family]], with whom they are intimately connected through the marriage prospects of their children.

## Role in the Novel

The Osbornes are introduced as a family of considerable wealth derived from trade. Mr. Osborne is a proud, hard man who has built his fortune through the City and expects his son [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] to advance the family's social standing through a strategic marriage. George is his father's favorite — spoiled, vain, and handsome — and Mr. Osborne's ambitions for him are boundless (004-046).

The family crisis comes when Mr. Sedley's business fails and the Osbornes' financial fortunes rise. Mr. Osborne, in an act of brutal pragmatism, forbids George's marriage to [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]], now the daughter of a bankrupt. When George defies him and marries Amelia anyway, Mr. Osborne disinherits his son — a decision that leads indirectly to George's death at Waterloo before any reconciliation can occur (024-042). The family's story continues in the next generation with [[georgy-osborne|Georgy Osborne]], George and Amelia's son, who becomes the object of his grandfather's belated and possessive affection. The Osborne family saga is a study in pride, materialism, and the destructive force of paternal authority.

## Associated Characters

- [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] — The spoiled, handsome son who defies his father and dies at Waterloo
- [[georgy-osborne|Georgy Osborne]] — George and Amelia's son, eventually claimed by his Osborne grandfather

## Historical Note

The Osborne family is fictional but represents a recognizable type of the Regency period: the wealthy merchant family whose money was made in trade and who aspired to gentility through advantageous marriages and the purchase of country estates.
