# Ludgate Hill

**Ludgate Hill** is a street in the City of London, running from Fleet Street up to St. Paul's Cathedral. In the early nineteenth century it was a thriving commercial thoroughfare, noted particularly for its "India shops" — retailers specializing in imported Eastern goods including shawls, muslins, and other textiles. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, Ludgate Hill appears in a brief but emotionally charged sequence involving Amelia Sedley.

## In Vanity Fair

After her marriage to George Osborne and his subsequent death at Waterloo, Amelia is left impoverished, living with her parents in reduced circumstances. Desperate for money, she decides to sell the cashmere shawls that had been a wedding gift from William Dobbin. The passage in which she journeys to Ludgate Hill is one of the novel's most quietly affecting:

> Packing up her shawl in a handkerchief (another of the gifts of the good Major), she hid them under her cloak and walked flushed and eager all the way to Ludgate Hill, tripping along by the park wall and running over the crossings, so that many a man turned as she hurried by him and looked after her rosy pretty face [046-020].

She is going to sell the shawls at "a fine India shop on Ludgate Hill" that she remembered visiting with her mother in better times:

> She remembered in former days going with her mother to a fine India shop on Ludgate Hill, where the ladies had all sorts of dealings and bargains in these articles [046-019].

The contrast is sharp: what was once a pleasure outing with her mother has become an errand of necessity. The India shop, once a scene of leisured shopping, is now the destination of a woman forced to sell her possessions.

## The India Shops

Ludgate Hill's India shops were a distinctive feature of Regency London commerce. They sold goods imported from the East — cashmere shawls from India, silks, muslins, ivory, and tea. For the fashionable ladies of *Vanity Fair*, these shops were both retail destinations and social spaces.
