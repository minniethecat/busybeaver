# Vanity Fair

*Vanity Fair* takes its title and its central metaphor from John Bunyan's *The Pilgrim's Progress*, where Vanity Fair is a perpetual fair held in the town of Vanity — a place where all that is worldly, corrupt, and transient is bought and sold. Thackeray extends this allegory: his novel is set not in one fair but in the world itself, which is nothing but Vanity Fair.

> Some people consider Fairs immoral altogether, and eschew such, with their servants and families. [P01-003]

## The World as Marketplace

Throughout the novel, Thackeray frames society as a marketplace where reputations, marriages, and fortunes are traded like commodities. Characters move through drawing rooms, ballrooms, and battlefields alike, all of which are booths in the great fair:

> Remember that this history has "Vanity Fair" for a title, and that Vanity Fair is a very vain, wicked, foolish place, full of all sorts of humbugs and falsenesses and pretensions. [008-056]

## Satire and Sentiment

Thackeray positions himself as the fair's showman, alternating between satire and sentiment as he presents his characters:

> What a spectacle is there in all Vanity Fair which Satire and Sentiment can visit by turns. [017-001]

The inhabitants of this world are "Vanity Fairians" [017-002], driven by self-interest even in their most sympathetic moments. No character escapes the fair's logic — even the virtuous Amelia participates, and even the cynical Becky Sharp is its product as much as its critic.

## Marriage as Commerce

The marriage market is Vanity Fair's most active trading floor. The narrator observes that "half the carriages of Vanity Fair" attend the wedding of George Osborne and Amelia Sedley [012-020], but the celebration masks the commercial transactions beneath. Characters marry for money, status, or security, and the narrator repeatedly reminds the reader to look past the ceremony to the bargain underneath.

## Universal Participation

Thackeray insists that no one is exempt from Vanity Fair. The narrator addresses the reader directly, collapsing the distance between the novel's world and the reader's own:

> I, for my part, have known a dozen men and women who were far more beautiful than the heroine of this tale. [011-004]

This rhetorical move — implicating the reader in the same vanities being satirized — is the novel's most radical gambit. You, reader, are also a Vanity Fairian.

## A Novel Without a Hero

The subtitle "A Novel without a Hero" is itself a Vanity Fair decree. In a world where everyone is complicit, no one can stand above it. The most admirable characters — Dobbin, Amelia — are also the most blinkered. The most dynamic — Becky — is also the most corrupt. Vanity Fair equalizes through universal imperfection.
