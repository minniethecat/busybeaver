# Frederick Bullock

**Frederick Bullock**, Esq., is a junior partner in the banking firm of Hulker, Bullock & Co. of Lombard Street and the eventual husband of Maria Osborne. A quintessential representative of the City mercantile class in *Vanity Fair*, Bullock approaches marriage with the same calculating spirit he brings to the ledger.

## Courtship of Maria Osborne

Bullock courts Maria Osborne over at least two London seasons, his interest calibrated precisely to her financial prospects. When George Osborne elopes with Amelia Sedley — thereby risking his inheritance — Bullock's reaction reveals his character:

> Mr. Bullock was a man of the world, and a junior partner of a wealthy firm. He knew what money was, and the value of it: and a delightful throb of expectation lighted up his little eyes, and caused him to smile on his Maria, as he thought that by this piece of folly of Mr. George's she might be worth thirty thousand pounds more than he had ever hoped to get with her. [021-005]

The sisters Osborne "had never thought of the money question up to that moment," but Bullock "bantered them with graceful gaiety" about it throughout their morning excursion [021-007].

## Role as Osborne's Informant

Bullock serves old Mr. Osborne as a source of financial intelligence. When the elder Osborne is scheming to marry George to the West Indian heiress Miss Swartz, "young Fred Bullock, one of his chief informants," investigates her fortune and reports back. Bullock himself "would have liked to make a bid for her himself" — his verb is characteristically commercial — "only he was booked to Maria Osborne" [020-009]. The disinterested Fred entirely approves of her as a sister-in-law, advising: "Let George cut in directly and win her. Strike while the iron's hot" [020-009].

When George brazenly withdraws money from the bank after his marriage, Bullock reports the incident to old Osborne with a memorable metaphor: "He came in as bold as brass" [028-012].

## Marriage Negotiations

After George's death at Waterloo and his disinheritance, Bullock's true colours emerge. He "insisted that the half of the old gentleman's property should be settled upon his Maria, and indeed, for a long time, refused 'to come to the scratch' (it was Mr. Frederick's own expression) on any other terms" [042-014]. Osborne retorts that Fred "had agreed to take his daughter with twenty thousand, and he should bind himself to no more."

The dispute escalates until Osborne withdraws his account from Bullock and Hulker's and threatens a horsewhipping "across the back of a certain scoundrel" [042-014]. Eventually Bullock capitulates, but the marriage is poisoned by his resentment.

## Life After Marriage

The Bullocks settle into a life of social climbing. Mrs. Frederick Bullock — the former Maria Osborne — "would have gone on her knees from May Fair to Lombard Street" for an invitation from Lady Steyne and Lady Gaunt [047-014]. Despite her pretensions, she presides over "third-rate parties" and commits the fatal error of calling Russell Square an "odious vulgar place" — offending her father, who still controls the purse strings [042-017].

In the Ring at Hyde Park, the Bullock chariot — "with bullocks emblazoned on the panels and harness, and three pasty-faced little Bullocks, covered with cockades and feathers, staring from the windows" — passes young Georgy Osborne on his pony. Mrs. Frederick Bullock flings "glances of the bitterest hatred at the little upstart" [056-005].

## Character

Frederick Bullock is one of Thackeray's most unsparing portraits of the money-marriage nexus. His face is described as "yellow" and "cadaverous" [028-011]; his approach to love is a balance-sheet calculation. Jane Osborne identifies the truth her sister cannot face: "I always told you, Maria, that it was your money he loved and not you" [042-014].

Yet Bullock is not a villain — he is simply the purest embodiment of Vanity Fair's governing logic. His values differ from those of the aristocratic Steynes only in being more honestly stated.
