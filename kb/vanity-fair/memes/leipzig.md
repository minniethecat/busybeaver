# Leipzig

Leipzig appears in Vanity Fair only in passing, a name in the itinerary of [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]]'s post-disgrace wanderings across the German states. It is less a setting than a trace — a place known only through the luggage Becky has left behind.

## Becky's German Interlude

The references to Leipzig are all connected to Becky's baggage: "when my trunks arrive from Leipzig, whence I came to this place" [066-010]. These trunks, delayed and "hagged-about boxes" [067-014], become a minor running joke in the novel's later chapters — material evidence of Becky's rootless existence. She "advertised one at Leipzig, and the Burschen took many towels, and also left many unpaid bills behind" [066-020], revealing that even her departure was not without its characteristic financial irregularities.

When the boxes finally arrive, they are described with sardonic precision: "three of them not by any means of the largest size" [067-014]. The gap between Becky's social pretensions and her actual possessions — three small boxes trailing behind her across Europe — is one of Thackeray's quieter satirical touches.

Leipzig in the novel's chronology belongs to the period after Becky's separation from [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]], when she drifts through the German courts and watering-places, living by her wits and charm. The city is never described directly; it exists only as a point of departure, one more station in a life of perpetual motion.

## Historical Note

In the post-Napoleonic period, Leipzig was a significant Saxon city within the German Confederation, known for its university, trade fairs, and its role in the 1813 Battle of the Nations. The "Burschen" Becky mentions were members of German university student fraternities (Burschenschaften), known in the period for their rowdy behaviour and liberal political sympathies.
