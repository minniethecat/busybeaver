# Napoleon Bonaparte

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) is the historical figure whose return from Elba in 1815 drives the military plot of *Vanity Fair*. Thackeray does not depict Napoleon directly — he never appears as a character — but his presence dominates the novel's central section. The "great roaring war tempest" of the Hundred Days sends [[george-osborne|George Osborne]], [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]], and [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] to Belgium, and his defeat at Waterloo reshapes the lives of every major character.

## Role in the Novel

Napoleon functions as an offstage force — the embodiment of history breaking into the private lives of the characters. In Chapter 18, Thackeray explicitly pauses to locate his domestic story within the sweep of European events: "Napoleon, humiliated as he was... had just reached Paris." The Brussels chapters (28–32) are saturated with the tension of his advancing army, culminating in the great battle. The novel's treatment of Napoleon is characteristically ironic: the great man's return matters not for its own sake but for how it disrupts the romantic and financial arrangements of a handful of English people.

> Our surprised story now finds itself for a moment among very famous events and personages, and hanging on to the skirts of history. When the eagles of Napoleon reached the coast of Provence, all the inhabitants of Europe trembled.

- **[[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]]**: Her life is devastated by George's death at Waterloo

## Historical Context

Thackeray wrote *Vanity Fair* in 1847–48, thirty years after Waterloo. His readers would have remembered Napoleon as a living figure, and the novel's Wellington-worship and anti-Napoleon sentiment reflect standard English attitudes of the 1840s. The novel treats Napoleon less as a historical actor than as a narrative device — the storm that tests all of Vanity Fair's pretensions against reality.
