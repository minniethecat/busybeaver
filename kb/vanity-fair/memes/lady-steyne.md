# Lady Steyne

Lady Steyne is the wife of the [[lord-steyne|Marquis of Steyne]] and the silent, suffering mistress of Gaunt House. Where her husband radiates worldly power and cynical amusement, Lady Steyne is described as "trembling, silent, superstitious, unhappy" — a woman whose spirit has been crushed by a lifetime of aristocratic marriage. She embodies the novel's darkest vision of what the marriage market does to women who are bought and sold for rank.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Steyne appears primarily in the Gaunt House chapters (Chapters 47-48), where she functions as a spectral presence — always in the background, never speaking much, but silently testifying to her husband's cruelty. Thackeray hints that she brought hereditary illness into the Steyne bloodline, a detail that Lord Steyne uses to justify his contempt for her. Her misery stands as a counterpoint to [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]'s apparent triumph in the same social circles — Lady Steyne shows what happens to women who lack Rebecca's weapons.

> All the world knew that Lord Steyne was a desperate profligate; and that his wife was a trembling, silent, superstitious, unhappy lady.

## Relationships

- **[[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]**: Her husband, who treats her with open contempt and flaunts his mistresses
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: The governess-turned-socialite who becomes Lord Steyne's protégée — and possibly more — under Lady Steyne's own roof

## Character Analysis

Lady Steyne is one of the novel's most quietly tragic figures. She has done everything the aristocratic system asked of her — married as directed, borne children, maintained appearances — and has been rewarded with a life of humiliation and fear. Her superstition and trembling are not presented as weakness but as the natural result of decades spent under Lord Steyne's contempt. She is what happens to women in Vanity Fair who play by the rules.
