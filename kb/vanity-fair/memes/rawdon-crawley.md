# Rawdon Crawley

> (010-020) ...the worthy old lady took a fancy to Rawdon Crawley when a boy, sent him to Cambridge, and proposed to bestow her fortune upon him...

Rawdon Crawley is the younger son of a baronet, a cavalry officer, and a gambler by profession — in outline, the classic Regency rake. What distinguishes him from that type is his capacity for genuine devotion: his love for Rebecca Sharp is the one unselfish passion of his life, and its destruction is the novel's most devastating arc.

## Background

Rawdon is the second son of Sir Pitt Crawley, the dissolute baronet of Queen's Crawley. As a younger son, he has no inheritance; his only asset is his aunt Miss Crawley's affection, which he is expected to convert into a legacy. He is educated at Cambridge (briefly) and then purchases a commission in the cavalry, where he acquires the habits — duelling, gambling, drinking — that define his social existence. To the world, he is simply a hard-living soldier; only beneath the surface does Thackeray reveal his capacity for authentic feeling.

## Role in the Novel

Rawdon's life is divided into two acts by his marriage to Rebecca Sharp. Before Becky, he is his aunt's favourite, living on expectations and charm. After the secret marriage is discovered, he loses Miss Crawley's fortune and enters a life of precarious credit, funded by his skill at cards and billiards and, unknowingly, by Becky's private arrangements. As a husband, Rawdon is uxorious to the point of blindness — he adores Becky and believes her to be an excellent wife. The discovery of her relationship with Lord Steyne, and the realization that she has used him as an instrument and screen, breaks him entirely. His final actions — striking Steyne, casting Becky off, accepting the colonial governorship of Coventry Island — have the dignity of a man who has lost everything worthwhile.

## Relationships

- [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] — His wife. Rawdon's love for Becky is the one genuine emotion of his life; her betrayal destroys the only part of him that was not merely conventional.
- Miss Crawley — His wealthy aunt, whose favour he forfeits by marrying without her approval.
- Sir Pitt Crawley — His father, a debauched baronet whose coarse manners Rawdon has inherited without his cunning.
- Lord Steyne — The powerful nobleman whose liaison with Becky ends Rawdon's marriage and nearly his life.
- Young Rawdon — His son, whom he loves with a rough tenderness that surprises even himself, and whose loss to Becky's indifference is a secondary tragedy.

## Character Analysis

Rawdon is Thackeray's most complex rendering of the gentleman-soldier type. He is not intelligent; he is not virtuous; he lives by his wits at the card table and by a code of honour that serves his class more than his conscience. And yet Thackeray grants him something that George Osborne lacks entirely — the capacity to love another person more than himself. Rawdon's honour is real, if limited, and his devastation at Becky's betrayal is the novel's most painful scene because it is deserved by neither party in the conventional moral sense: Becky has done what her circumstances required, and Rawdon has loved what he could not understand. The novel judges neither, but it mourns both.

## Historical Context

Rawdon's career as a cavalry officer and professional gambler reflects the Regency world of the London clubs, where fortunes rose and fell at whist tables and a gentleman's "honour" could be maintained or destroyed by a card. His colonial appointment to Coventry Island is Thackeray's satirical nod to the imperial safety valve — the distant posting that removes embarrassing gentlemen from polite society.
