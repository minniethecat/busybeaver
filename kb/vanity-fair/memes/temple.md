## Overview

The Temple is a historic area in the City of London, comprising the Inner Temple and Middle Temple — two of the four Inns of Court — as well as the Temple Church. In *Vanity Fair*, the Temple appears both as a residential quarter for legal gentlemen and, in the form of Temple Bar, as a landmark at the boundary of the City.

## Description

Thackeray's references to the Temple are split between its role as a residential address for a certain class of gentleman — "Deuceace, who lives in the Temple" [053-004] — and Temple Bar [051-078], the ceremonial gateway that marked the western entrance to the City of London. The Temple of Thackeray's novel is a masculine, professional world: chambers and coffee-houses rather than drawing-rooms.

## Events

### A Gentleman's Residence

The Honourable Mr. Deuceace, one of Thackeray's minor aristocratic scoundrels, "lives in the Temple" [053-004] — a detail that places him within the world of legal London, where younger sons of the nobility might maintain chambers without necessarily practising law.

### Passing Temple Bar

A cab drives "under Temple Bar" [051-078] with a cigar, a brief urban vignette that marks a transition from one part of London to another. Temple Bar, designed by Christopher Wren, stood at the junction of Fleet Street and the Strand until its removal in 1878.
