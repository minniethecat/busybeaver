# Greenwich

Greenwich, on the south bank of the Thames downstream from London, was in the Regency period a popular destination for day trips and riverside dining. In *Vanity Fair*, it represents the world of masculine hospitality and social obligation — the dinners that grease the wheels of society and the river that carries characters both toward pleasure and toward the wider world.

## Whitebait and Obligation

Greenwich is synonymous with the whitebait dinner, a quintessentially London ritual. Mr. Osborne, the wealthy City merchant, "pays their dinners at Greenwich, and they invite the company" (014-070) — a pithy summary of how social capital works in Vanity Fair. The Greenwich dinner is not merely a meal; it is a transaction. The host pays, and the guests repay with their presence, their conversation, and the reflected status they confer. This is the economy of sociability that Thackeray anatomises throughout the novel.

## The Riverside Retreat

For old soldiers and pensioners, Greenwich offered a different kind of retreat. The Royal Hospital for Seamen — the Greenwich Hospital, now the Old Royal Naval College — provided a home for retired sailors. Thackeray's narrator asks whether there is "no Chelsea or Greenwich for the old honest pimple-nosed" veteran (007-047), invoking the Hospital as a symbol of national gratitude, or its absence, for those who have served. The parallel between Chelsea Hospital (for soldiers) and Greenwich Hospital (for sailors) was well-established in the Regency imagination.

## The Passing Thames

The novel's geography repeatedly returns to the river, and Greenwich marks one of its key points. "By the time the boat had passed Greenwich," the narrator notes of one river journey (062-001), the passengers have entered a different world — the groom and loungers who populate the deck remind us that the Thames carried all classes, from the merchant princes at Greenwich dinners to the servants and idlers on the packet-boats.

## Historical Context

Greenwich in the early nineteenth century was a thriving riverside town, famous for its whitebait suppers at establishments like the Trafalgar Tavern and the Ship. The town was also home to Greenwich Hospital (chartered 1694) and the Royal Observatory (founded 1675), which gave Greenwich its association with maritime time-keeping and the Prime Meridian. The Thames packet-boats that passed Greenwich carried passengers between London and Gravesend, making the town a convenient stop for both river traffic and day-trippers. Thackeray's references to Greenwich tap into a shared cultural knowledge among his readers — the whitebait dinner was a well-known institution, and the Greenwich pensioners were a familiar sight.
