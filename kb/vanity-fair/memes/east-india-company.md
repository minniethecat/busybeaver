# East India Company

> (003-027) He was in the East India Company's Civil Service, and his name appeared in the East India Register, as collector of Boggley Wollah...

The East India Company was the chartered trading corporation that effectively governed large parts of India during the Regency period. In *Vanity Fair*, it serves as the institutional backdrop for [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]]'s colonial career and the source of the wealth that allows him to live as a gentleman of leisure in England.

## Role in the Novel

The Company's presence in the novel is primarily through the figure of Jos Sedley, who holds a senior post in the Company's Civil Service. His position as Collector of [[boggley-wollah|Boggley Wollah]] is "an honourable and lucrative post" (003-027), giving him an income sufficient to sustain a fashionable London life — and making him a target for [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]'s matrimonial ambitions. The Company's ships appear in the novel as part of the patriotic pageantry surrounding the Waterloo campaign, with "cheering from all the East India ships in the river" (028-001) as the troops embark.

The East India Company also appears in the novel's portrait of the wealthy mercantile class that sustains Regency society. Miss Crawley's investments include "three stars to her name in the East India stockholders' list" (020-028), and at her entertainments one meets "Goldmore, the East India Director" (020-035). The Company thus represents the colonial wealth that underpins the leisure and luxury of the novel's social world, as well as the career path for younger sons like Jos who cannot inherit the family business.

## Associated Characters

- [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]] — Holds East India Company stock as part of her investment portfolio

## Historical Note

The East India Company was founded in 1600 and by the Regency era had evolved from a trading enterprise into the de facto government of much of India. Its Civil Service provided well-paid careers for generations of British young men. The Company was dissolved in 1858 after the Indian Rebellion, and its functions were absorbed by the British Crown.
