# Jos Sedley

> (003-020) He was as vain as a girl; and perhaps his extreme shyness was one of the results of his extreme vanity.

Jos Sedley — "the Collector of Boggley Wollah" — is *Vanity Fair*'s great comic creation. Fat, vain, cowardly, and perpetually uncertain of his own dignity, he is the novel's representative of the Nabob: the Englishman who has made a fortune in India and returned to spend it on the social advancement that his money cannot quite buy. Thackeray treats Jos with a mixture of contempt and affection that is uniquely his own: he is ridiculous but never wicked, pretentious but never cruel, and his eventual fate — it is darkly implied — is to be hustled to his death by Rebecca Sharp for the insurance money.

## Background

Jos is the elder son of John Sedley, a prosperous London stockbroker, and the brother of Amelia Sedley. He has spent years in India as a civil servant of the East India Company, rising to the lucrative post of Collector at Boggley Wollah. He returns to England with a fortune, a liver complaint, and an acute consciousness of his own importance — a consciousness that is perpetually wounded by the indifference of the world.

His vanity is the engine of his comedy. He dresses in the height of fashion with disastrous results; he affects the manners of a man about town while being terrified of women; he consumes vast quantities of food and drink while complaining of his delicate health (003-020). The narrator's description of Jos preening before the mirror, sucking in his stomach and adjusting his collar, is one of the novel's most purely comic passages.

## Role in the Novel

Jos's primary narrative function is to be a target — first for Rebecca Sharp's matrimonial ambitions, later for her financial ones. In the novel's early chapters, Rebecca sets her cap at Jos with a determination that almost succeeds: he is flattered, flustered, and on the verge of proposing when George Osborne's warning and his own cowardice combine to drive him to flight (006-043).

He reappears throughout the novel as a supporting figure: escorting Amelia to Brussels before Waterloo, fleeing the battlefield in a panic, and finally, in the novel's closing chapters, becoming Rebecca's companion and victim. The narrator's final glimpse of Jos — ill, insured for a large sum, and entirely in Rebecca's power — is one of the darkest passages in the novel, all the more effective for being presented with the same comic tone as his earlier appearances.

## Relationships

- **Rebecca Sharp**: His would-be wife and eventual destroyer. Rebecca's pursuit of Jos is the novel's first demonstration of her strategic intelligence, and her final capture of him is its last demonstration of her ruthlessness.
- **Amelia Sedley**: His sister, whom he loves with a genuine but ineffectual affection. His flight from Brussels, abandoning Amelia, is his most shameful moment.
- **George Osborne**: His social superior and occasional tormentor. George's mockery of Jos is one of the novel's demonstrations that birth trumps wealth in the Regency social order.

## Character Analysis

Jos is Thackeray's study in the comedy of vanity. Unlike the novel's other vain characters — George Osborne, whose vanity is allied with cruelty, or Rebecca Sharp, whose vanity is allied with intelligence — Jos's vanity is entirely harmless. He wants to be admired, and he is not; he wants to be feared, and he is laughed at. The gap between his self-image and his reality is the source of his comedy.

But the novel's final treatment of Jos is unsettling. His death — hastened, it is strongly implied, by Rebecca's management — transforms him from comic figure to victim, and the reader's laughter at his expense is retrospectively shadowed. Thackeray refuses to let comedy insulate the reader from consequence: Jos is ridiculous, and Jos is dead, and the two facts are not unrelated.

## Historical Context

The Nabob — the Englishman returned from India with a fortune — was a stock figure of eighteenth- and early nineteenth-century satire. The East India Company's civil servants could accumulate vast wealth through salary, perquisites, and private trade, and their return to England was often treated as a social intrusion: new money attempting to buy its way into old society. Jos, with his Indian curries, his liver complaint, and his social pretensions, is a perfect specimen of the type.
