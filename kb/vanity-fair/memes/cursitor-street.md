# Cursitor Street

Cursitor Street is a real London street off Chancery Lane that appears in *Vanity Fair* as the location of Mr. Moss's "mansion" — a sponging-house where gentlemen arrested for debt were confined before being sent to prison.

## Mr. Moss's Establishment

The street's notoriety in the novel comes from Mr. Moss, assistant officer to the Sheriff of Middlesex, who operated a debtors' holding house there. He is introduced as:

> Mr. Moss, of Cursitor Street, Chancery Lane, and assistant officer to the Sheriff of Middlesex [051-075]

Mr. Moss acted on behalf of creditors, including Mr. Manasseh of Cursitor Street, who was one of Colonel Crawley's chief creditors [036-020].

## Rawdon Crawley's Arrest

The most significant scene on Cursitor Street occurs when Rawdon Crawley is taken there after his arrest for debt:

> Friend Rawdon drove on then to Mr. Moss's mansion in Cursitor Street, and was duly inducted into that dismal place of hospitality. [053-001]

Thackeray describes the grim interior of Moss's house with characteristic satirical detail:

> There were dirty trays, and wine-coolers en permanence on the sideboard, huge dirty gilt cornices, with dingy yellow satin hangings to the barred windows which looked into Cursitor Street [053-005]

## Rawdon's Letter

From his confinement on Cursitor Street, Rawdon writes a desperate letter to his wife Becky:

> I was NABBED by Moss of Cursitor Street—from whose GILT AND SPLENDID PARLER I write this—the same that had me this time two years. [053-009]

This moment is pivotal: Rawdon's arrest and Becky's cold response to his plea for help lead directly to the collapse of their marriage.

## Significance

Cursitor Street represents the harsh reality of Regency-era debt law, where gentlemen could be seized and confined at the behest of creditors. The sponging-house served as a half-way point between freedom and the Marshalsea or King's Bench prisons. Thackeray uses the squalid opulence of Moss's establishment — its gilded cornices and barred windows — to satirize the intersection of commerce and the law.
