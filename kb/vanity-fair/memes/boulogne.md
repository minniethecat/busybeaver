# Boulogne

Boulogne-sur-Mer is a Channel port that in Vanity Fair becomes "that refuge of so much exiled English innocence" [064-009]. It is the destination of characters who have been squeezed out of English society — by debt, scandal, or simply diminished fortune — and who reconstitute a shabby miniature of English social life on the French coast. For [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Crawley]], Boulogne is both a retreat and a new stage for the old performance.

## A Refuge for English Exiles

Thackeray sketches the English colony at Boulogne with satirical precision. The town is populated by those who "faded away to Boulogne, and will be heard of no more" [060-002], the genteel bankrupts and disgraced widows who cannot afford to live in England but cannot bear to live among the French. When Becky herself flees England after her separation from [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]], she makes directly for Boulogne: "she wrote him a neat letter from Boulogne, when she quitted England" [064-008].

The narrator's description of her arrival is at once comic and cutting: "when she touched upon the French coast at Boulogne, that refuge of so much exiled English innocence, the cliffs of Albion shone behind her" [064-009-010]. The phrase "exiled English innocence" is pure Thackerayan irony — Boulogne's English residents are anything but innocent, merely unlucky.

## Queen of British Boulogne

Becky's talent for social conquest does not desert her on foreign soil. Within a short time she has established herself at the apex of Boulogne's miniature society: she "was rather the Queen of British Boulogne and gave the most dinners and entertainments of any lady in that cheerful settlement" [064-017]. The social circuit she dominates is described with wearying sameness: "the same stupid Bois de Boulogne, the same company of an evening" [064-023]. Boulogne is a diminished stage for a performer of Becky's talents.

The [[bois-de-boulogne|Bois de Boulogne]] receives passing mention as the local promenade — "she rode in the Bois de Boulogne to the admiration of all" [034-072] — though this reference belongs to an earlier, more prosperous period in Becky's life.

## A Way Station

Boulogne is not a final destination but a way station. Becky's restless ambition cannot be contained by its "cheerful settlement" of English castaways, and when opportunity — or necessity — calls, she moves on: "From Boulogne to Dieppe, from Dieppe to Caen, from Caen to Tours" [064-020]. The French towns blur into an itinerary of evasion. Boulogne is merely the first step in a Continental wandering that will carry Becky across Germany and eventually back to England.

The town also serves as a transit point for other characters. When Mrs. Bute Crawley and her daughter pass through on their way to Switzerland, they encounter the Washingtons and "passed through Boulogne on their way to Switzerland" [064-014], a reminder that the English Channel crossing made Boulogne the gateway to the Continent for all classes of British traveller.

## Historical Context

Boulogne-sur-Mer in the post-Napoleonic period was a well-established destination for English expatriates. Its proximity to England — a short Channel crossing — made it accessible to those of modest means, and its cost of living was considerably lower than London's. The town had a substantial English population throughout the nineteenth century, with English churches, schools, and social institutions. Thackeray himself knew Boulogne well; he visited frequently and set portions of other works there. The "Bois de Boulogne" mentioned in the novel is the town's public park, not to be confused with the Parisian Bois of the same name.
