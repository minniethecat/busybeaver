# Honour

> (001-011) ...I have the honour and happiness of presenting Miss Amelia Sedley...

Honour — the code of [[gentleman|gentlemanly]] conduct, reputation, and social credit — functions in *Vanity Fair* as one of Thackeray's most persistent satirical targets. The word recurs throughout the novel in contexts that systematically undermine its pretensions: it is invoked in formal pleasantries (001-011), wielded as a weapon of social exclusion (002-027), and employed to justify conduct that is anything but honourable (004-086). The novel's subtitle, "A Novel without a Hero," as declared in the [[ch01-chapter-i-chiswick-mall|opening chapter]], is in part a declaration that true honour — in the chivalric sense — has no home in this world.

## In the Novel

The word "honour" appears most frequently in its hollowest form: the language of social ceremony. Miss Pinkerton's letter presenting Amelia Sedley employs the formula "I have the honour and happiness" (001-011), and her academy advertises itself as "honoured by the presence of THE GREAT" (001-013). The Dixonary presented to departing pupils is described as a "high honour" (001-023) — instant bathos, reducing honour to a school prize.

In the social world, honour is inseparable from status. The question of "whether she was to have that honour" — an invitation to the Lord Mayor's ball — shows how honour operates as a marker of inclusion and exclusion (002-027). George Osborne's casual "upon my honour" (004-086) exemplifies the phrase's degradation into mere conversational filler, while the "honourable and lucrative post" of Boggley Wollah (003-027) perfectly encapsulates the novel's conflation of moral and financial standing.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's treatment of honour is devastating because he rarely condemns it directly. Instead, he lets characters invoke honour in contexts that expose its hollowness. George Osborne, who speaks constantly of honour, abandons Amelia when her father is ruined — a plot development that unfolds across [[ch18-chapter-xviii-who-played-on-the-piano-captain-dobbin-bought|several chapters]] after the Sedley bankruptcy. Rawdon Crawley, the novel's closest approximation to a man of honour, is also a gambler and a duellist whose code is purely tribal.

The narrator's own relation to honour is ironic: "there are few things more honourable than to be a good woman" — a statement whose context makes clear that "honourable" women in this world are also the most exploited. Honour, like gentility, is a performance that the novel's clear-eyed observer sees through.

## Historical Context

The Regency code of honour — derived from aristocratic and military traditions — was under pressure from the rise of commercial values. The duel, honour's ultimate enforcement mechanism, features in the novel through the confrontation between Rawdon and Lord Steyne, but the duel is treated not as a noble ritual but as a sordid and dangerous anachronism. The novel registers the shift from honour-as-birthright to honour-as-reputation, a more democratic but also more precarious form of social credit that could be gained and lost through commerce and scandal.

## Related Characters

- **George Osborne**: The novel's supreme example of honour as performance — a man who speaks the language of gentlemanly conduct while acting from vanity and self-interest.
- **Rawdon Crawley**: A professional soldier and gambler whose personal code of honour is genuine but entirely tribal — he fights for his wife's reputation not from moral conviction but from the rules of his caste.
- **William Dobbin**: The one character whose conduct is consistently honourable, yet the novel treats his honour as a form of foolishness — he is rewarded with Amelia only after years of being ignored.
