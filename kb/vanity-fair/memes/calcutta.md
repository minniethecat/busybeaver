## Overview

Calcutta (present-day Kolkata) was the capital of British India and the administrative centre of the East India Company during the period of *Vanity Fair*. The city functions in the novel as a distant source of wealth, a career destination, and a social universe parallel to London — with its own hierarchies, scandals, and aspirations.

Jos Sedley's entire identity is shaped by his years in Calcutta: he "had been a great man in Calcutta" [059-001] and yearns for the recognition he enjoyed there. William Dobbin's regiment is posted to Calcutta, and his steady character thrives in the colonial administration while George Osborne's recklessness leads to his death at Waterloo.

## Description

Thackeray sketches Calcutta through a few evocative details: the "Course at Calcutta" is compared to Bond Street as a place for fashionable display [057-016]; the Chowringhee district is mentioned alongside Bath and Cheltenham as a social proving ground [022-039]; and "the most splendid vests that Calcutta could furnish" [059-006] represent the luxury goods available to wealthy colonials.

The city is depicted as hot, distant, and exotic — Jos brings back a "native servant" [057-016] and suffers from the "liver complaint" stereotypically associated with Europeans in the Indian climate. The social scene of Calcutta balls [004-075] mirrors London society in its snobbery and stratification.

## Events

### Jos Sedley in Calcutta

Jos Sedley serves as Collector of Boggley Wollah, a post in the Bengal Presidency administered from Calcutta. His years there define his character:

> [038-002] ...caused a tremendous sensation for some time at Calcutta, and was called Waterloo Sedley...

The "Waterloo Sedley" nickname — derived from Jos's exaggerated tales of his proximity to the battle — illustrates how reputations travel between the colonial and metropolitan worlds.

Jos's position at the Revenue Board is obtained through family influence, and he is "wild with rage" when his father's bankruptcy threatens to expose his modest origins [038-024]. Calcutta represents both his achievement and his imposture.

### Dobbin in Calcutta

William Dobbin spends years in Calcutta and Madras, rising in the Company's service. His letters to Amelia from Calcutta [059-001] are patient and devoted, contrasting with George Osborne's neglect. The "great Calcutta House of Fogle, Fake, and Cracksman" [060-001] represents the commercial network in which Dobbin's fortune is entangled, and his return to Calcutta to "wind up the affairs" [060-002] delays his reunion with Amelia.

### The Nabob

A "Nabob from Calcutta and Jamaica" [062-005] appears late in the novel — one of the wealthy colonials whose fortunes, made in the East and West Indies, dazzle English society. The nabob figure represents the ultimate colonial archetype: immense wealth, dubious origins, and social power that overrides English class distinctions.

## Historical Context

Calcutta in the early nineteenth century was the centre of British power in India, governed by the East India Company until 1858. The city was divided between the European "White Town" centred on Chowringhee and Fort William, and the Indian "Black Town" to the north.

The Company's civil servants — men like Jos Sedley — formed a distinct colonial elite, living in grand houses with numerous servants, their careers dependent on patronage networks that extended back to London. The "Revenue Board" where Jos served was one of the key administrative bodies governing British India.

The India connection was a major source of British wealth during the Regency, and returning "nabobs" were a recognizable social type — wealthy, sometimes ostentatious, and often the subject of satire for their attempts to buy their way into English society.
