# Baker Street

Baker Street is a real London street that appears in *Vanity Fair* as a symbol of past fashion and as part of Mr. Osborne's estate.

## Historical Prestige

Thackeray notes Baker Street's faded grandeur, once home to the great statesman William Pitt:

> Ladies, are you aware that the great Pitt lived in Baker Street? [051-002]

The narrator reflects on the transience of fashionable districts, predicting that Hyde Park Gardens and Belgrave Square will one day seem as remote as Baker Street had become by the time of the novel's narration:

> And some day or other (but it will be after our time, thank goodness) Hyde Park Gardens will be no better known than the celebrated horticultural outskirts of Babylon, and Belgrave Square will be as desolate as Baker Street, or Tadmor in the wilderness. [051-001]

Lady Hester Stanhope, Pitt's niece and housekeeper, is also associated with the street:

> Yes, Lady Hester once lived in Baker Street, and lies asleep in the wilderness. [051-002]

## Mr. Osborne's Bequest

Baker Street appears in a more practical context in Mr. Osborne's will:

> I leave my daughters with ten thousand pounds apiece—very good portions for girls; I bequeath my plate and furniture, my house in Baker Street, with a handsome jointure, to my widow for her life [061-011]

## Rebecca's Paris Salon

During Becky's triumphant Paris season, the narrator uses Baker Street as a point of satirical comparison:

> to have seen the stars and cordons in Rebecca's humble saloon would have made all Baker Street pale with envy. [034-075]

This suggests Baker Street represented solid but unglamorous middle-class respectability.

## Significance

Baker Street serves as Thackeray's touchstone for the changing fortunes of London neighborhoods. Once home to a prime minister, by the Regency era it had become a respectable but unfashionable address — suitable for a wealthy merchant's widow but not for the aristocratic circles Becky sought to infiltrate.
