# Long Acre

**Long Acre** is a street in central London, running from St. Martin's Lane to Drury Lane in the Covent Garden area. In the early nineteenth century it was known as a centre for coach-building, carriage-making, and heraldic services. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, Long Acre appears twice, each time in connection with the material pretensions of characters.

## In Vanity Fair

### The Osbornes' Coat of Arms

When George Osborne boasts to the Crawleys that his family "bear the same arms" as the aristocratic house of L———, the narrator reveals the decidedly un-aristocratic origin of the Osborne arms:

> "We bear the same arms," George said, as indeed was the fact; Mr. Osborne having consulted with a herald in Long Acre, and picked the L——— arms out of the peerage, when he set up his carriage fifteen years before [029-031].

The detail is typical of Thackeray's satire: the Osborne arms were not inherited but purchased, from a tradesman in a street of coach-makers. The herald in Long Acre is a commercial operator — not the College of Arms — underscoring the nouveau-riche quality of Mr. Osborne's gentility.

### Jos Sedley's Chariot

Later in the novel, Jos Sedley, returned from India and living comfortably in London, orders a carriage from the same district:

> It was a matter of great delight and occupation to Jos to superintend the building of a smart chariot which he and the Major ordered in the neighbouring Long Acre: and a pair of handsome horses were jobbed, with which Jos drove about in state in the park, or to call upon his Indian friends [059-018].

That Jos and Dobbin order their chariot "in the neighbouring Long Acre" — neighbouring, because Jos has taken lodgings near Covent Garden — confirms the street's role as London's carriage-making district.

## Historical Context

In the Regency period, Long Acre was at the centre of London's coach-building trade. Many of the most celebrated coach-makers of the era had their premises there or in the adjoining streets. The heraldic services mentioned in the novel were a natural complement to the carriage trade: a gentleman who had newly acquired a carriage typically wished to adorn its panels with a coat of arms — whether legitimately inherited or, like Mr. Osborne's, conveniently arranged.
