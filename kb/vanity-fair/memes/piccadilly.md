# Piccadilly

Piccadilly is one of London's great arteries, running westward from the Haymarket to Hyde Park Corner. In *Vanity Fair*, it serves as more than a mere street — it is the symbolic passageway between the worlds the novel traverses. Characters enter and exit along Piccadilly at moments of narrative significance, and the street's changing appearance reflects the changing fortunes of those who travel it.

## A Thoroughfare of Fortune

The novel uses Piccadilly as a measure of time and change. When [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] drives with [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] down Piccadilly after their wedding, "Apsley House and St. George's Hospital" mark their route (022-009) — the landmarks of a world still at peace. By the time [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]] returns to England years later, "a hundred changes had occurred" since he had last been in Piccadilly (058-015), and the street he walks along is both familiar and altered, much like the lives of the characters he is returning to.

[[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] makes her own dramatic passages along Piccadilly. After her triumph at court, she "whirled away into Piccadilly, nodding with the sweetest of smiles" (040-049), the street becoming the stage for her performed elegance. The carriage rolling into Piccadilly is one of the novel's recurring images of arrival and social self-presentation (041-045).

## The Road to Vanity Fair

Thackeray positions Piccadilly as a liminal space between domesticity and the larger world. The White Bear inn at Piccadilly is where journeys begin (007-047), and from Piccadilly the road leads westward toward Kensington Gardens and Brompton — the quieter domestic retreats where characters retreat from the bustle of Vanity Fair. To travel along Piccadilly is to move between the novel's poles of display and retreat, ambition and content.

## Historical Context

Piccadilly in the Regency period was already a major thoroughfare, lined with aristocratic townhouses, shops, and coaching inns. The street took its name from the "piccadill" — a stiff collar fashionable in the early seventeenth century — and by Thackeray's time it was one of the most travelled roads in London. Apsley House, the Duke of Wellington's London residence (mentioned at 022-009), stood at Hyde Park Corner at the street's western end, making the Piccadilly-to-Apsley House route a journey from the commercial West End to the summit of military and social prestige.
