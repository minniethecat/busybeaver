# Miss Horrocks

Miss Horrocks, the butler's daughter at [[queens-crawley|Queen's Crawley]], is [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] without the talent — a young woman who sees the social ladder and decides to climb it, but lacks the intelligence to do so subtly. Her ribbons become increasingly splendid as she gains influence over the decaying [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]] (033-013); her ambition to become Lady Crawley is transparent to everyone except the old man himself. When Sir Pitt dies and [[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]] inherits, she is turned out — her brief ascendancy collapsing into nothing.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Horrocks appears in the Queen's Crawley chapters as a minor but pointed satirical figure. While Rebecca Sharp schemes her way through the upper reaches of society, Miss Horrocks schemes in the servants' hall — the same drive, the same vanity, the same willingness to trade charm for advancement, just at a humbler address. Her fate — expulsion and disgrace — is what Rebecca's might have been with less intelligence and less luck.

## Relationships

- **[[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]**: Her elderly patron, whose decline she exploits
- **[[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]]**: The heir who dismisses her on inheriting
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: The successful version of what Miss Horrocks attempts

## Character Analysis

Miss Horrocks is a cautionary example in the novel's taxonomy of ambition. She possesses the desire but not the art; her ribbons announce what Rebecca's smiles conceal. Thackeray dispatches her with a paragraph — she is not important enough for tragedy — but her function is clear: to show that social climbing is not unique to the talented, merely more successful when practised by them.
