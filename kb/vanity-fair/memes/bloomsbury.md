# Bloomsbury

Bloomsbury is a residential district in central London where the Sedley family reside at Russell Square. In the early nineteenth century, Bloomsbury was a newly developed and fashionable neighbourhood for prosperous middle-class families — the exact social stratum occupied by [[john-sedley|John Sedley]], a successful stockbroker. The Sedley home on Russell Square serves as the London anchor for the novel's opening chapters, the setting from which [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] departs for Miss Pinkerton's academy at Chiswick and to which she returns as a young woman entering society.

## Location and Character

Thackeray places the Sedley residence with precision: "Russell Square, Bloomsbury" (012-019, 014-059). In the novel's social geography, Bloomsbury represents respectable upper-middle-class prosperity — comfortable but distinctly unfashionable compared to the aristocratic precincts of Mayfair and Park Lane. The narrator makes this class distinction explicit when noting that Osborne's rise in the world has "transported him to Park Lane from Russell Square" (014-082), marking the social distance between the merchant's Bloomsbury and the aristocrat's West End.

The Bloomsbury world is self-contained and inward-looking. The narrator describes "a stockbroker's family in Russell Square, who are taking walks, or lunching, or dining, or talking, or making love, as the case might be, just as entirely as if there were no such places as France or Germany" (006-001). This domestic insularity is both Bloomsbury's charm and its limitation — it is a world where Amelia can feel securely loved but also where news of the outside world, including [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s return, arrives as a distant disturbance.

## Key Events

The Sedley household on Russell Square is the stage for several pivotal early scenes. It is here that [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] stays after leaving Chiswick (001-015), where she first meets [[joseph-sedley|Joseph Sedley]] and begins her campaign to marry him, and where [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] comes courting. The evening parties and family dinners at Russell Square provide a domestic counterpoint to the novel's grander social scenes.

After John Sedley's financial ruin, the household declines precipitously. The family is forced to leave Russell Square for humbler lodgings at Brompton, and the Bloomsbury prosperity that defined the novel's opening chapters dissolves into the shabbier world of the Sedleys' reduced circumstances.

## Associated Characters

- [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] — Courting Amelia, transported from Bloomsbury respectability to Park Lane grandeur

## Historical Context

Bloomsbury in the Regency period was a relatively new residential development, laid out in the late eighteenth century on the Bedford Estate. Russell Square, designed by James Burton in 1800, was the centrepiece of this development. The squares and terraces of Bloomsbury attracted lawyers, merchants, and professional families — precisely the Sedleys' class. Thackeray himself lived in Bloomsbury as a young man, at 13 Great Coram Street, giving the district an autobiographical resonance. The social gradation between Bloomsbury and Mayfair that the novel satirises was a lived reality of Regency London.
