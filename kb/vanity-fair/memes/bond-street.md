# Bond Street

Bond Street is the premier shopping street of Regency London, where fashionable men and women acquire the costumes, accessories, and ornaments required for their social performances. In *Vanity Fair*, Bond Street serves as a site of transformation — the place where characters buy the appearances they wish to project.

## Fashion and Identity

The most telling Bond Street moment in the novel belongs to [[george-osborne|George Osborne]]. Newly commissioned in the army, "he went to Bond Street, and bought the best hat and spencer" (006-075) — the purchasing of clothes as the marking of a new social identity. This is Bond Street's function: it is where the raw material of money is converted into the polished appearance of gentility. When [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] and his fashionable set are seen "turning out for Bond Street" (057-016), they are not merely going shopping; they are participating in the endless labour of maintaining their place in the social order.

The novel also uses Bond Street as a measure of social arrival and departure. To be "cut" on Bond Street — as [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] fears being cut by her former acquaintances (028-020) — is to have one's social pretensions publicly denied. The street's pavement becomes a courtroom where reputations are silently adjudicated.

## Literary Commerce

Bond Street has a literary dimension as well. Becky Sharp, ever resourceful, sells "novels to a bookseller out of Bond Street" (037-018), converting her own life into saleable narrative for the consumption of the reading public. This detail collapses the distance between character and author — Becky, like Thackeray, is in the business of turning Vanity Fair into a commodity.

## Historical Context

Bond Street (formally Old Bond Street and New Bond Street) was already London's most exclusive shopping destination by the Regency period. Its shops catered to the luxury trades — tailors, milliners, jewellers, and booksellers. Beau Brummell, the era's supreme arbiter of male fashion, was a Bond Street habitué, and the street's association with dandyism was firmly established by the time Thackeray began writing. The novel's use of Bond Street as a metonym for consumer display would have been instantly recognisable to contemporary readers.
