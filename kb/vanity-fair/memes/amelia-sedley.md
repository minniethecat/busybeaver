# Amelia Sedley

> (001-024) Miss Amelia Sedley was a young lady of this singular species; and deserved not only all that Miss Pinkerton said in her praise, but had many charming qualities which that pompous old Minerva could not see.

Amelia Sedley, called Emmy by those who love her, is the sentimental heart of *Vanity Fair*. The daughter of a prosperous [[london|London]] merchant, she is educated at [[miss-pinkertons-academy|Miss Pinkerton's academy]] alongside [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]], whose cunning and ambition she can never quite perceive. Her trajectory — from adored daughter to impoverished widow to the eventual wife of [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]] — traces the fortunes and misfortunes of passive virtue in a world governed by [[money]], [[marriage]], and [[society|social appearance]].

## Background

Amelia is born into comfort as the daughter of John Sedley, a successful City merchant, and grows up at [[russell-square|Russell Square]] in the heart of respectable London (001-023). She is the darling of her family — her father indulges her, her mother adores her, and her brother [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] regards her with clumsy affection. At Miss Pinkerton's academy she is the prize pupil: "the most accomplished and the most virtuous young lady" ever to leave the establishment, presented with Dr. Johnson's *Dictionary* as the school's highest honour (001-011).

This idyllic beginning masks a fundamental fragility: Amelia's worth is defined entirely by others' affection. She has been praised for sweetness and submission, not for judgment or strength. When her father's fortune collapses — a casualty of the Napoleonic Wars — her social position evaporates, and her education in "accomplishments" proves useless for survival.

## Role in the Novel

Amelia's story is the counter-melody to Rebecca's aria.

**The Courtship (Chapters 1–15):** Amelia loves [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] with an unwavering devotion that the novel presents as both touching and foolish. George, vain and shallow, regards her as a "little milksop" and marries her largely because his father forbids it (013-045). The marriage is brief: George dies at [[waterloo|Waterloo]], leaving Amelia a widow with an infant son, [[georgy-osborne|Georgy Osborne]].

**The Widowhood (Chapters 16–45):** Amelia's grief for George becomes her defining identity. She devotes herself to his memory with an obsessive tenderness that blinds her to the living — particularly [[william-dobbin|William Dobbin]], who has loved her silently since childhood. She raises Georgy in impoverished gentility, refusing Dobbin's help and her father-in-law's offers, sustained by the "sacred" memory of a man who never deserved her devotion.

> (012-020) Now, love was Miss Amelia Sedley's last tutoress, and it was amazing what progress our young lady made under that popular teacher.

**The Awakening (Chapters 46–67):** The novel's final movement brings Amelia's long-delayed moral education. When she finally reads the letter revealing George's plan to abandon her for Rebecca, the idol shatters (048-051). She at last sees Dobbin — now a middle-aged man, weary from decades of unrequited devotion — not as a consolation prize but as the good man he has always been. Their marriage, in the novel's closing pages, is quiet and undramatic: the reward of persistence rather than passion.

## Character Analysis

Amelia Sedley is the novel's most difficult and most misunderstood character. Thackeray famously subtitled *Vanity Fair* "A Novel Without a Hero," and Amelia's status as the conventional "heroine" is deliberately undermined. She is gentle, faithful, and self-sacrificing — all the virtues Regency society claimed to value in women — yet her passivity causes real harm. Her obsessive mourning for George deprives her son of a living mother; her blindness to Dobbin's devotion wastes decades of two lives.

The satirical point is not that Amelia is a hypocrite (she is genuine in her feelings) but that conventional feminine virtue, as defined by her [[society]], is itself a form of moral incapacity. Amelia cannot see clearly because she has been taught only to feel, never to judge. [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]], for all her vices, sees the world as it is; Amelia, for all her virtues, sees it as she wishes it to be.

> (048-051) She took out the letter — the faded, years-old letter… and read it through. Her eyes were opened.

Thackeray's own illustrations depict Amelia as soft-featured and downward-gazing — the visual language of Victorian feminine submission. The contrast with his drawings of Rebecca — alert, direct, often laughing — is the novel's moral argument rendered in line and shade.

## Historical Context

Amelia embodies the "Angel in the House" ideal that dominated early nineteenth-century conceptions of respectable femininity — an ideal that mid-Victorian novelists (including Thackeray, Dickens, and Eliot) increasingly questioned. Her economic dependence on men — father, husband, father-in-law, eventual second husband — reflects the legal reality of coverture, under which married women could not own property or sign contracts. Amelia's "goodness" is, at bottom, the only survival strategy available to a woman with no independent means.
