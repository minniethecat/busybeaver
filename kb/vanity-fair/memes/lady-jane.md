# Lady Jane

Lady Jane, born Lady Jane Sheepshanks, daughter of Lord Southdown, becomes the wife of [[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]] and the mistress of [[queens-crawley|Queen's Crawley]] — a position she fills with a gentleness that startles in a novel so crowded with schemers. She is one of the few genuinely good characters in [[vanity|Vanity Fair]], and Thackeray treats her virtue not as satire but as a quiet, almost embarrassing anomaly: goodness that asks for nothing, performs for no audience, and is therefore invisible to the world the novel anatomises.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Jane first appears as the "booked" bride of Pitt Crawley — a match arranged between families of compatible rank and incompatible temperaments (014-041). Where Pitt is pompous, she is modest; where he sermonises, she acts. Her arrival at Queen's Crawley transforms the household: she nurses old Sir Pitt in his decline, treats the servants with decency, and extends genuine kindness to [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca]] — who repays her with condescension behind her back.

Her relationship with [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] is significant: she is one of the few who sees his genuine affection for his son and treats him accordingly. Her quiet influence is felt throughout the novel's second half — a reminder that goodness exists, even if it makes for less interesting reading than vice.

## Relationships

- **[[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]]**: Her husband, whose pomposity she softens
- **[[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]**: Her father-in-law, whom she nurses devotedly
- **[[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]]**: Her brother-in-law, treated with unexpected kindness
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: Her sister-in-law, who privately scorns her virtue
- **[[lady-southdown|Lady Southdown]]**: Her mother-in-law, whose religious tyranny she endures

## Character Analysis

Lady Jane is the novel's answer to the question "does goodness exist?" — and the answer, tellingly, is yes, but nobody much cares. Thackeray gives her no grand scenes, no witty dialogue, no dramatic victories. She is kind to servants, dutiful to a tedious husband, patient with a monstrous mother-in-law. Her goodness is domestic, habitual, untheatrical — precisely the kind that a novel like Vanity Fair, with its eye for spectacle and its taste for hypocrisy, is least equipped to celebrate. Her presence is a structural necessity: the still point against which the novel's frantic climbers can be measured.
