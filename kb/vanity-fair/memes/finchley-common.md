# Finchley Common

Finchley Common was a real area of common land north of London. In *Vanity Fair*, it appears almost exclusively as the titular subject of "The Washerwoman of Finchley Common," a satirical religious tract written by Lady Emily Hornblower (née Sheepshanks), daughter of Lord Southdown.

## The Washerwoman of Finchley Common

Lady Emily is introduced early in the novel as the author of pious works:

> Lady Emily, wrote those sweet tracts, "The Sailor's True Binnacle," and "The Applewoman of Finchley Common." [009-011]

The title evolved — or perhaps there were multiple works — as the "Washerwoman" became her most frequently cited publication:

> Lady Emily, the authoress of the "Washerwoman of Finchley Common" [033-026]

## Satirical Function

Throughout the novel, "The Washerwoman of Finchley Common" serves as Thackeray's running joke about Evangelical publishing. The tract is referenced by characters in contexts that highlight the gap between pious professions and worldly behavior:

> I won't read the Washerwoman of Finchley Common," vowed Violet; and so saying, and avoiding a passage at the end of which a certain coffin was placed with a couple of watchers, and lights perpetually burning in the closed room, these young women came away. [041-021]

The Dowager Lady Southdown uses the tract as a tool of moral instruction and social control, sending descriptions of her daughter's "worldly behaviour to the authoress of the Washerwoman of Finchley Common at the Cape" [045-001].

## Across the Empire

The tract's reach extends across the British Empire. Jos Sedley, during a storm at sea, retreats to his cabin to read it:

> He disappeared rather in a panic during a two-days' gale, in which he had the portholes of his cabin battened down, and remained in his cot reading the Washerwoman of Finchley Common, left on board the Ramchunder by the Right Honourable the Lady Emily Hornblower. [057-017]

## Significance

Finchley Common in *Vanity Fair* is less a geographical location than a satirical device. Through the absurd image of a washerwoman of Finchley Common dispensing religious wisdom, Thackeray mocks the Evangelical publishing industry, the Southdown family's aggressive piety, and the gap between religious profession and genuine virtue.
