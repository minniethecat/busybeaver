# Lady Southdown

Lady Emily Southdown, the mother of [[lady-crawley|Lady Jane Crawley]], is Thackeray's most devastating portrait of religious hypocrisy. She is an evangelical of the most aggressive sort — distributing tracts, bullying the sick with her "medical" theories, and wielding her piety as a weapon of domestic conquest. Her religion, the novel makes clear, is a form of power: it allows her to dominate her family, judge her neighbours, and congratulate herself on her own virtue (033-025).

## Role in the Novel

Lady Southdown appears in the novel's later chapters as a force of domestic chaos. She attempts to take over the treatment of the dying [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]], prescribing her own patent medicines and evangelical pamphlets in equal measure. She bullies her gentle son-in-law [[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]] and terrorises the household with her certainty of her own righteousness.

The narrator's treatment of Lady Southdown is merciless. Her piety is not questioned — she is genuinely convinced of her own virtue — but it is shown to be entirely compatible with cruelty, selfishness, and intellectual vacuity. She represents everything Thackeray detested about the alliance between evangelical enthusiasm and aristocratic privilege.

## Satirical Function

Lady Southdown is a satirical type: the religious bully. Her evangelicalism is not a source of humility but of pride; her good works are performances of dominance; her "concern" for others is a form of aggression. In a novel about [[society|social performance]], she is the performer who most thoroughly believes her own act — and is therefore the most dangerous.
