# Marriage

> (016-002) If people only made prudent marriages, what a stop to population there would be!

Marriage in *Vanity Fair* is never merely a romantic union — it is the novel's central economic transaction, the mechanism by which [[fortune|fortunes]] are made and lost, and the primary arena in which Thackeray's satire of social ambition operates. Throughout the novel, marriages are proposed, prevented, celebrated, and regretted not according to the dictates of love but according to the calculus of money, status, and convenience (002-014) (006-006).

## In the Novel

The marriage plot drives the novel's architecture. The central contrast — between Amelia Sedley's sentimental devotion and Rebecca Sharp's strategic ambition — is established through their respective marriage trajectories. Amelia's engagement to George Osborne is threatened by her father's financial ruin, revealing how quickly "love" evaporates when the dowry disappears — a theme that connects the marriage plot to the novel's larger treatment of [[gentleman|gentlemanly]] honour (006-008). Rebecca, who has no fortune, pursues marriage as a career move: first targeting Jos Sedley, the rich Collector of Boggley Wollah (006-006), then pivoting to Rawdon Crawley when Miss Crawley's fortune seems within reach (015-035).

The narrator's commentary on the marriage market is unsparing. The opening of [[ch16-chapter-xvi-the-letter-on-the-pincushion|Chapter XVI]] observes that prudent marriages would depopulate the earth, and declares Rawdon's marriage to Rebecca "one of the honestest actions which we shall have to record in any portion of that gentleman's biography" (016-002) (016-003).

## Satirical Function

Thackeray uses marriage to expose the hypocrisy of polite society. The same characters who declare that "no man would demean himself by a marriage with an artist's daughter" (006-007) eagerly pursue heiresses. Miss Crawley, who publicly champions romantic love, privately rages at Rawdon for marrying a governess with no fortune (015-049).

The novel's subtitle — "A Novel without a Hero" — finds its sharpest expression in its marriage plots. George Osborne marries Amelia after being prodded by Dobbin, not from conviction; Rawdon marries Rebecca believing she has expectations from Miss Crawley; Jos Sedley flees from marriage at every opportunity. No one marries well for the right reasons.

## Historical Context

In Regency England, marriage among the upper classes was explicitly a property transaction. The doctrine of coverture meant a wife's legal identity was subsumed into her husband's, and marriage settlements determined the financial terms of the union. Miss Crawley's fortune — the object of universal scheming — represents the period's reality that unmarried women of property controlled significant economic power, which they surrendered upon marriage (011-038). Sir Pitt Crawley's first marriage to the daughter of Lord Binkie was a straightforward aristocratic alliance (009-001), while his second marriage to a tradesman's daughter was "thought a great match" on her side (008-022).

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's supreme marriage strategist, who treats matrimony as a military campaign and succeeds in marrying above her station — only to discover that Rawdon's expectations are worthless without Miss Crawley's approval (015-035) (016-003).
- **Amelia Sedley**: Represents the sentimental ideal of marriage, which the novel systematically dismantles. Her devotion to George Osborne is portrayed as delusional, her second marriage to Dobbin as a reward he scarcely wanted by then.
- **George Osborne**: Marries Amelia out of a mixture of obstinacy and Dobbin's pressure, not conviction — a "hero" who is no hero at all (006-077).
