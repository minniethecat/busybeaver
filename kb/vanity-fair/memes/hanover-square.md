# Hanover Square

Hanover Square, in the parish of St. George's, was the most fashionable address for society weddings in Regency London. In Vanity Fair, it appears exclusively as the location of St. George's Church, where the marriages that drive the novel's social machinery are solemnised. The square itself, laid out in the early eighteenth century and named for the Hanoverian dynasty, was by Thackeray's time the epicentre of the London marriage market — a place where status was displayed, alliances were forged, and the rituals of gentility were performed with meticulous public observance.

## St. George's Church

St. George's, Hanover Square, was where the "genteel marriage season" played out each year. Thackeray's narrator observes the rituals with characteristic detachment:

> I have seen a woman weep in the most heart-rending manner in the church, just as she was entering the building, but I have never seen the bridegroom's male friends give way to tears, or the beadles and officiating clergy any way affected; yet it is not at all uncommon to see women who are not in the least concerned in the operations going on [016-006].

The church is mentioned as the place where the fictional Lord Methuselah "hobbled in after" a wedding [012-020], and it is where the business of one particular marriage "took place" [042-005]. The choice of St. George's carried automatic social cachet; to be married there was to participate in a ritual that signalled membership in the world of fashion.

In a moment of drunken enthusiasm, [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] vows he "would marry her next morning at St. George's, Hanover Square" and would "knock up the Archbishop of Canterbury at Lambeth" to have him in readiness [006-031]. The choice of St. George's — the default venue for a fashionable London wedding — underscores Jos's pretension even in his intoxicated state. It also reveals how deeply the church's name was embedded in the popular imagination as the natural destination for a respectable marriage.

## In Vanity Fair

Beyond the specific wedding references, St. George's serves as a symbolic backdrop to the novel's marriage plots. The church represents the public face of matrimony — the ceremonial performance that society demands — in contrast to the private realities of the marriages it sanctifies. [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] and [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]], [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] and [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] — each union, however troubled, would have been solemnised (or expected to be solemnised) in just such a church.

The narrator's description of the congregation at St. George's — the "women who are not in the least concerned in the operations going on" shedding tears — captures the novel's central insight about public ritual: that its emotional content is often performed rather than felt. The church becomes, in Thackeray's hands, another stage in Vanity Fair.
