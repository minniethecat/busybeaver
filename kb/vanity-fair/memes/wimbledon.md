# Wimbledon

In the Regency period, Wimbledon was a village in Surrey, about seven miles southwest of central London, bounded on one side by Wimbledon Common. Its elevated position, cleaner air, and relative proximity to the capital made it a favoured location for the country houses of London's prosperous merchant and professional classes — a step below the great aristocratic estates of the Home Counties but a clear marker of material success.

## In *Vanity Fair*

Wimbledon appears in the novel within a devastating satirical catalogue of the mercenary underpinnings of the Regency marriage market. Thackeray describes Miss Maria Osborne's "respectable attachment" to Frederick Augustus Bullock — an attachment, he explains, that would have just as readily fixed itself upon Bullock Senior had he been available:

> Miss Maria Osborne, it is true, was "attached" to Mr. Frederick Augustus Bullock, of the firm of Hulker, Bullock & Bullock; but hers was a most respectable attachment, and she would have taken Bullock Senior just the same, her mind being fixed — as that of a well-bred young woman should be — upon a house in Park Lane, a country house at Wimbledon, a handsome chariot, and two prodigious tall horses and footmen, and a fourth of the annual profits of the eminent firm of Hulker & Bullock, all of which advantages were represented in the person of Frederick Augustus. [012-020]

The passage meticulously lists the components of a desirable marriage settlement: the London townhouse (Park Lane), the country retreat (Wimbledon), the carriage and horses, and the share of partnership profits. Wimbledon functions in this inventory as the pastoral complement to Park Lane's urban prestige — together they constitute the complete material package that a "well-bred young woman" has been trained to fix her mind upon.

## The Geography of Vanity

Thackeray's placement of Wimbledon within a list of commodities is characteristic of the novel's treatment of place. Locations are repeatedly reduced to their exchange value — what they signify about the wealth and status of those who possess them. The young Maria Osborne does not love Frederick Bullock; she loves "a house in Park Lane, a country house at Wimbledon, a handsome chariot." The men are interchangeable ("she would have taken Bullock Senior just the same"); the properties are what matter. This reduction of marriage to a property transaction is one of the novel's most sustained satirical targets, and Wimbledon — the modest but respectable country house — serves as one of its recurring tokens.

The chapter title of Chapter 12, "Quite a Sentimental Chapter," is itself an ironic gesture: the chapter purports to deal in sentiment but systematically exposes the economic calculus beneath every "attachment" it describes. Wimbledon, Park Lane, and the rest of the Osborne-Bullock inventory are the real objects of affection.

## Historical Wimbledon

At the time of *Vanity Fair*'s Regency setting, Wimbledon was transitioning from a rural village to a residential retreat for the wealthy. Wimbledon Park House, the estate of the Spencer family (later Earls Spencer), occupied much of the area, and the Common was famous for its windmill and its highwaymen. The village's development accelerated after the enclosure of the Common in the early nineteenth century, when substantial villas began to line the ridges overlooking London. The Wimbledon that Thackeray invokes is this liminal space — close enough to the City for a merchant to commute, far enough to count as a country seat.
