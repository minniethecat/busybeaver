# Duke of Wellington

## Overview

Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington (1769–1852), is the British military commander whose presence dominates the Brussels chapters of *Vanity Fair*. Though he never appears as a speaking character in the novel, his reputation, decisions, and the confidence he inspires in the English community form the backdrop against which Thackeray portrays the Waterloo campaign. Wellington functions as an offstage symbol — a measure of genuine greatness against which the novel's civilian characters, with their petty vanities and self-deceptions, are implicitly judged.

## Role in the Novel

Wellington first enters the narrative when George Osborne's regiment receives orders to join "his Grace the Duke of Wellington" in the Low Countries [025-034], a development that causes great excitement in the Osborne household. The narrator notes that the Duke's presence inspires "resolute faith" in every Englishman serving under him [028-006], and observes with evident pride that English soldiers "paid their way" wherever they went — unlike the armies of other nations [028-004].

During the crisis of Waterloo, Wellington's movements are reported exclusively through the lens of civilian panic. Jos Sedley is told that "the Duke of Wellington had gone to try and rally his army" [031-012], and later that he was "retreating upon the capital" [032-068]. These fragmentary reports convey the confusion of the moment: even the Duke's strategy appears uncertain when filtered through the anxieties of non-combatants. The French themselves are said to acknowledge that Wellington "never suffered a defeat" [036-008], a reputation that, by the novel's Waterloo chapters, is about to be reaffirmed. Major O'Dowd burnishes his own credentials by noting he "carried despatches for the Duke" [038-002], illustrating how proximity to Wellington confers social capital even on minor military figures.

## Narrative Technique: The Offstage Hero

Thackeray's treatment of Wellington is a deliberate narrative choice. The most consequential figure in the novel's historical setting never speaks a line of dialogue, never appears directly in a scene. Every reference to Wellington is mediated — through newspaper reports, dinner-table gossip, the boasts of officers, or the fears of civilians.

This offstage technique serves two purposes. First, it preserves Wellington's mythic stature: by keeping him at a distance, Thackeray avoids the risk of diminishing the national hero through undignified fictional representation. Second, and more importantly, it sharpens the novel's central satirical point. *Vanity Fair* is set against the greatest military campaign of its age, but its real subject is the vanity, folly, and occasional courage of ordinary people — the Jos Sedleys, the George Osbornes, the Becky Sharps — who are caught up in events far larger than themselves. Wellington's silent, offstage greatness throws their noisy self-importance into ironic relief.

## The Civilian-Military Divide

Wellington also embodies the gulf between the military and civilian worlds in *Vanity Fair*. The professional soldiers — Major O'Dowd, General Tufto, even the hapless Rawdon Crawley — define themselves in relation to Wellington's command. The civilians, by contrast, experience Wellington only as a source of anxiety or reassurance. Mrs. O'Dowd, the major's indomitable wife, treats the entire campaign as an extension of regimental social life, and even the prospect of battle does not diminish her confidence in "the Duke." The narrator sustains this double vision throughout: Wellington is both the saviour of Europe and a name invoked to soothe nervous ladies in Brussels drawing-rooms.

## Historical Context

Wellington was one of the most famous living Englishmen at the time *Vanity Fair* was published (1847–48). His victory at Waterloo (1815) was still within living memory, and his later career as Prime Minister (1828–1830) and elder statesman kept him continuously in the public eye. Thackeray's original readers would have recognized Wellington immediately as a national hero, which makes the satirical contrast between his gravitas and Jos Sedley's panic all the more pointed. The novel assumes its audience's familiarity with Wellington's biography and reputation, using that shared knowledge as an unspoken benchmark against which the moral stature — or lack thereof — of every other character can be measured.
