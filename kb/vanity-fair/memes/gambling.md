# Gambling

Gambling in *Vanity Fair* operates on two levels: as a literal Regency pastime that shapes the fortunes of characters like Rawdon Crawley, and as a governing metaphor for the risks and uncertainties of social life. Thackeray presents a world where everyone is, in some sense, a gambler.

## Rawdon Crawley: The Professional Gambler

Rawdon Crawley is the novel's most conspicuous gambler. A skilled card player and billiards sharp, he makes his living — such as it is — at the gaming table. His reputation as a gambler is both his profession and his social identity:

> (014-082) Rawdon Crawley received George Osborne with great frankness and graciousness: praised his play at billiards: asked him when he would have his revenge: was interested about Osborne's regiment: and would have proposed piquet to him that very evening, but Miss Crawley absolutely forbade any gambling in her house.

Rawdon's dependence on gambling reflects the precarious position of younger sons in Regency society. Without an inheritance sufficient to support a gentleman's lifestyle, he turns to cards and billiards as a source of income — activities that are simultaneously disreputable and socially tolerated.

## George Osborne's Gaming

George Osborne, too, gambles — though as a gentleman amateur rather than a professional. His betting and card-playing are part of the military social life in Brussels before Waterloo.

> (013-016) I've told you that when you go to town you ought to go to her, and not to the gambling-houses about St. James's.

Thackeray treats George's gambling as a minor vice, less damaging than his vanity but indicative of his moral carelessness. His father's warning against gambling-houses proves prescient: George's carelessness with money and reputation follows the pattern of the gentlemanly gambler who never considers the stakes until they are lost.

## The Gambling Metaphor

Beyond literal gambling, Thackeray extends the metaphor to encompass all of social life. Marriage is a gamble — Becky bets on Rawdon's expectations, Amelia bets on George's love. Financial speculation is gambling (the novel's treatment of [[speculation]] explores this). Even reputation is a stake that can be lost in a single evening.

> (036-009) Though Frascati's and the Salon were open at that time in Paris, the mania for play was so widely spread that the public gambling-rooms did not suffice for the general ardour, and gambling went on in private houses as much as if there had been no public means for gratifying the passion.

Thackeray's description of the Paris gambling mania — where the public rooms cannot contain the passion for play — functions as a microcosm of the novel's broader argument: the appetite for risk is universal, and no institutional framework can fully contain it.

## The Waterloo Parallel

The Brussels gambling scenes, set on the eve of the Battle of Waterloo, create an implicit parallel between the card table and the battlefield. Men who gamble with money one night gamble with their lives the next. The randomness of cards mirrors the randomness of war — both distribute outcomes with no regard to desert.

## Becky's View

Becky Sharp, characteristically, has the clearest view of gambling. She neither plays nor moralizes — she understands that the game is rigged and seeks to manipulate the players rather than join them. Her entire career is a form of gambling conducted with superior information.
