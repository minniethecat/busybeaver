## Overview

Madras (present-day Chennai) was one of the three Presidencies of British India, alongside Calcutta and Bombay. In *Vanity Fair*, it serves as Major Dobbin's posting and the destination of the —th regiment after their service in Europe. The city represents duty, distance, and the colonial career that separates Dobbin from Amelia for years at a time.

Unlike Calcutta, which is depicted through the lens of Jos Sedley's social vanity, Madras appears as a place of soldiering and administration — less glamorous, more remote, but equally essential to the British imperial project.

## Description

Madras is depicted primarily through its function as a garrison town. The —th regiment is ordered to Madras after its European campaigns:

> [057-011] ...commandant to proceed to Madras, and thence probably to Europe...

The Madras Roads — the open anchorage off the coast — is where ships bound for Calcutta or Europe stop:

> [057-020] ...the three days she lay in the Madras Roads...

> [057-013] ...from Calcutta, touching at Madras...

The social world of Madras is glimpsed through Lady O'Dowd, the formidable wife of the regiment's senior officer: "Lady O'Dowd at Madras" [038-033] represents the transplanted Anglo-Irish gentry who formed the backbone of Company military society.

## Events

### Dobbin at Madras

Major Dobbin's years in Madras are characterized by patient, steady service — a counterpart to his patient, steady love for Amelia. His posting to Madras is the reason he cannot intervene when George Osborne neglects his wife:

> [038-024] ...the Major being in Madras at the time, had no particular...

Amelia writes him "letters to Madras, letters all about little George" [038-028], and Dobbin serves as her son's guardian from afar. The geographical distance between Madras and London mirrors the emotional distance in their relationship — Dobbin is always present in spirit but physically absent.

### The —th Regiment at Bundlegunge

The regiment is stationed at "the station of Bundlegunge, in the Madras division of our Indian empire" [043-001], a fictional outpost where the daily rhythms of colonial military life — parades, mess dinners, gossip — continue against the background of the vast Indian subcontinent.

### Jos Sedley's Passage

Jos Sedley, returning from India, sails from Calcutta "touching at Madras" [057-013] — the port serving as a waypoint on the long sea journey home. His weakness during the voyage underscores the physical toll of the India service.

## Historical Context

Madras was founded by the East India Company in 1639 at Fort St. George, and by the Regency period was a major commercial and military centre. The Madras Army was one of the three Presidency armies, and the city was a key node in the network of trade, administration, and military power that sustained British rule in India.

The sea voyage between England and India via the Cape of Good Hope could take four to six months, and Madras was a common port of call on the route to Calcutta. The "Madras Roads" were notorious for the difficulty of landing — ships anchored offshore and passengers and cargo were ferried to land by small boats.
