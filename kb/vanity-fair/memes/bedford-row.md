# Bedford Row

Bedford Row is a street in Holborn, London, historically associated with the legal profession. In *Vanity Fair*, it appears as the address of Higgs & Blatherwick, solicitors who handle Mr. Osborne's legal affairs.

## Higgs & Blatherwick, Solicitors

The firm is introduced when Mr. Osborne summons his solicitor to discuss George's disobedience:

> At twelve o'clock Mr. Higgs (of the firm of Higgs & Blatherwick, solicitors, Bedford Row) called by appointment, and was ushered into the governor's private room, and closeted there for more than an hour. [024-038]

A letter from the firm dated "Bedford Row, May 7, 1815" [025-007] formalizes old Mr. Osborne's rejection of his son.

## George Osborne's Visit

After being disinherited, George goes to Bedford Row with characteristic bravado:

> George meanwhile, with his hat on one side, his elbows squared, and his swaggering martial air, made for Bedford Row, and stalked into the attorney's offices as if he was lord of every pale-faced clerk who was scribbling there. [026-019]

George's swaggering confidence in the face of his financial ruin is both comic and poignant — he cannot quite believe his father's rejection is final.

## Legal Society

Bedford Row's legal character is reinforced by another attorney resident, Mr. Frowser:

> old Mr. Frowser, the attorney, from Bedford Row, a very great man, and from his business, hand-in-glove with the "nobs at the West End" [042-012]

## Significance

Bedford Row represents the legal machinery that executes the will of wealthy men like Mr. Osborne. The solicitors of Bedford Row are the intermediaries between the commercial City and the fashionable West End — handling wills, marriage settlements, and the disinheritance of rebellious sons with equal professional detachment.
