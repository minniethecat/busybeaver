# Death

Death in *Vanity Fair* is both a narrative device and a moral test. Characters die suddenly (George Osborne at Waterloo), slowly (old Mr. Sedley), or offstage (Sir Pitt Crawley's first wife). Each death reshapes the living world — redistributing money, altering marital prospects, and exposing the true nature of survivors.

## George Osborne: The Central Death

George Osborne's death at Waterloo is the novel's most consequential event. His fall transforms Amelia from a hopeful young wife into a widow eternally faithful to a husband who was never faithful to her. The irony is characteristically Thackerayan — Amelia canonizes a man who, had he lived, would almost certainly have disappointed her.

> He died the death of a hero, sir, fighting against the French [037-058]

George's death also liberates Dobbin, who might otherwise have spent his life watching Amelia married to his friend. And it destroys old Mr. Osborne, whose pride had led him to disown his son — a decision he cannot undo after George is killed.

## Old Mr. Sedley's Decline

John Sedley's death is different: slow, financial before physical, a gradual extinguishing of a once-prosperous man. His bankruptcy precedes his death, and his final years are spent in humbled dependence:

> Old Sedley made the last; and it was the best oration for his own funeral [061-011]

His deathbed scene is one of the novel's most genuinely moving passages, with Amelia's devotion undimmed by his fallen fortunes.

## Sir Pitt Crawley and Inheritance

Sir Pitt Crawley's death is a study in the gap between public mourning and private feeling. His heirs receive his fortune with varying degrees of desert. The funeral arrangements become occasions for social maneuvering:

> Pitt Crawley arranging the funeral ceremonial and other matters [041-033]

The death of the second Lady Crawley is noted almost in passing. Even the room where Lady Crawley died becomes a tool of intimidation:

> Lady Crawley died in it. Good night. [007-038]

The death of the Governor of Coventry Island ripples through the Crawley family's calculations [055-025], showing how even distant deaths reshape the inheritance landscape.

## Funerals and Performance

Thackeray is acutely attentive to the theatrical quality of mourning. Funerals in *Vanity Fair* are social performances:

> Mutes at funerals could not look more glum than these officials [024-027]

The pomp of Mr. Osborne's funeral contrasts with the modesty of Mr. Sedley's — and neither tells us anything reliable about the feelings of the mourners:

> He said, "and invite him to the funeral: it will be but becoming." [040-011]

## Death as Economic Event

Above all, death in *Vanity Fair* is an economic event. Wills are read, fortunes are redistributed, widows receive jointures, and heirs come into their own:

> Half down, and half at the death of Mr. Osborne, with the chances of survivorship [042-004]

Thackeray's materialism — his insistence on tracking the money — is never more clear-eyed than in his treatment of death. Grief may be real, but so is the transfer of property:

> The death of a child occasions a passion of grief [061-003]
