# Mr. Bowls

## Overview

Mr. Bowls is Miss Crawley's "large confidential man" [014-004], the butler at the Crawley establishment on Park Lane. He is a figure of pompous dignity, described by Thackeray as a person of considerable physical presence and self-importance. Along with Miss Briggs, he forms part of Miss Crawley's inner domestic circle and plays a role in the household politics surrounding Miss Crawley's fortune.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Bowls first appears at the very beginning of the novel's main action when he answers a knock at Miss Crawley's door:

> The parlour bell was rung briskly, and answered on that instant by Mr. Bowls, Miss Crawley's large confidential man [014-004].

When Miss Crawley takes ill, Bowls's authority in the household grows: he becomes the gatekeeper who can grant or deny access to Miss Crawley, famously shutting the door on Rawdon Crawley [017-019]. His name plate is symbolically "put by" when Miss Crawley's household temporarily closes [019-007].

Bowls's interactions with the Crawley family reveal his position as a trusted domestic. He produces champagne on order [034-046], answers inquiries about household matters [033-023], and delivers Miss Crawley's cards [033-041]. He treats the footmen as his "aide-de-camp" [016-023] and is not above a hearty guffaw at a joke at the expense of others:

> Mr. Bowls gave one abrupt guffaw, as a confidential servant of the family, but choked the rest of the volley; the diplomatist only smiled [034-034].

## Character

Bowls embodies the comic dignity of the senior servant in a great household. His sense of his own importance is absolute — he presides over Miss Crawley's house and cellar with the gravity of a man who knows himself indispensable. When the young James Crawley arrives and is asked which hotel to send for his luggage, Bowls advances "with a profound bow" to inquire [034-030], performing the deference expected of his station while quietly assessing the newcomer.

His loyalty to Miss Crawley is matched by a sharp eye for household economy. When James Crawley's gin bill arrives — "no less than eighteen glasses of that fluid at eightpence per glass" — it is Bowls who pays the bill and, crucially, "showed it on his return home to Mrs. Firkin" [034-038], thus ensuring that evidence of the young man's intemperance reaches the right ears. The pipe incident that follows cements James's disgrace: Bowls rushes upstairs "at three steps at a time" to cry "For Gawd's sake, sir, stop that 'ere pipe" [034-040], protecting his mistress's sensibilities with theatrical urgency.

## Relationship with Miss Crawley

Bowls's position depends entirely on Miss Crawley's favour, and he manages that dependency with professional skill. His predecessor, Mr. Raggles, had been born on the family estate at Queen's Crawley and maintained connections with the Crawley family even after leaving service — later becoming landlord to Rawdon and Rebecca [034-046]. Bowls, by contrast, is a pure London creation, owing his position to the urban household of Park Lane rather than to the country estate. This distinction marks him as part of the novel's geography of service: the country retainer with hereditary ties versus the metropolitan domestic whose loyalty is professional rather than sentimental.
