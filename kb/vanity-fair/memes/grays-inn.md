## Overview

Gray's Inn is one of the four ancient Inns of Court in London, situated in Holborn. In *Vanity Fair*, it appears exclusively as the location of legal business — a place where solicitors practise and where characters go to learn their professional and financial fates.

## Description

Thackeray references Gray's Inn solely in connection with the legal profession. The "lawyers in Gray's Inn Square" [025-097] are part of the professional apparatus that governs the characters' financial lives. The Inn is not described physically; its function as a seat of legal authority is its entire significance.

## Events

### The Lawyer's Errand

When a character's husband goes "off to Gray's Inn" [025-103] to learn his fate, the Inn becomes the site of a life-altering revelation — though Thackeray, characteristically, compresses the moment into a single line, leaving the reader to imagine the anxious return.

### Returning to Curzon Street

The journey "back from the lawyer's at Gray's Inn, to the blazing fire in Curzon Street" [044-011] contrasts the cold formality of legal chambers with the domestic comfort of home. The Inn represents the impersonal machinery of the law, distinct from the social world of drawing-rooms.
