## Overview

Ostend appears in *Vanity Fair* at two critical junctures: first as the port through which British forces and civilians pass en route to the Waterloo campaign, and later as the retirement refuge of Jos Sedley, where his life reaches its grim conclusion. The city's dual role — gateway and terminus — mirrors the arc of the novel itself, from the bustle of war to the quiet desperation of exile.

## Description

Thackeray's Ostend is a place of transit and terminal retreat. In 1815, "the packet made Ostend" [028-003] carrying soldiers and civilians toward the Belgian frontier. The sight of "the transports" [028-003] in the harbour signals the proximity of war. Years later, the same port city becomes Jos Sedley's "chief place of resort" [067-024] — no longer a point of departure for adventure, but a refuge from consequences.

The city is "much frequented by those peoples" [067-024] — the displaced, the fugitive, and the compromised — who find in Ostend a convenient distance from English justice and English society.

## Events

### The Channel Crossing

Jos Sedley, accompanying the army to Belgium, "proceeded under convoy to Ostend" [028-001]. The crossing is a moment of high anxiety for Jos, whose courage is of the drawing-room rather than the battlefield variety. "The gallant Jos" [028-001] finds his spirits reviving only when "the packet made Ostend" [028-003] and the prospect of solid ground restored his self-possession.

### The King's Destination

In the confused diplomacy of the Hundred Days, the King of France was reported "to join at Ostend" [031-014], and dispatches note that a royal personage "embarks today from Ostend" [031-016]. The port city becomes a node in the network of royalist communication and escape.

### Jos's Final Resort

In his later years, Jos Sedley's "chief place of resort was Ostend" [067-024]. Having fled England, he settled into the expatriate life of the Belgian coast. It is here that Becky Sharp, ever resourceful, tracks him down — and here that Jos meets his mysterious end, the "friends whom she should meet at Ostend" [067-025] perhaps including those who would not scruple at a quiet murder.

## Historical Context

Ostend in the early nineteenth century was a significant Channel port, connected to England by regular packet services. Its position on the Belgian coast made it the natural entry point for British travellers heading to Brussels and the Continental interior. During the Waterloo campaign, Ostend served as a supply port and transit hub for British forces.

The city's later nineteenth-century transformation into a fashionable seaside resort — complete with a casino and promenade — is prefigured in Thackeray's depiction of it as a place of expatriate leisure, though the Ostend of Jos Sedley's retirement is a shabbier, more desperate establishment than the glittering resort it would become.
