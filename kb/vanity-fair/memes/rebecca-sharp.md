# Rebecca Sharp

> (002-015) …though Miss Rebecca Sharp has twice had occasion to thank Heaven for what she considered signal good fortune, she possessed that indomitable resolution…which enabled her to bear poverty with a smiling face, and to plan, secretly, the means of rising superior to it.

Rebecca Sharp, known throughout the novel as Becky, is the most memorable creation of *Vanity Fair* and one of the great characters of nineteenth-century fiction. Born to a penniless artist father and a French opera-dancer mother, she arrives at [[miss-pinkertons-academy|Miss Pinkerton's academy]] as a charity pupil and departs determined never to be poor or powerless again (002-025). Her trajectory — from [[governess]] to the wife of a guards officer, from [[london|London]] social climber to Continental exile — provides the novel's primary plot and its sharpest satirical instrument. Thackeray uses her to embody the novel's central themes of [[ambition]], [[money]], and [[society|social pretension]].

## Background

Rebecca's origins are deliberately degraded by the standards of Regency society. Her father was "a painter, who had a great deal of cleverness…and a very bad character," and her mother "a Frenchwoman of the corps de ballet" who died when Rebecca was young (002-025). At [[miss-pinkertons-academy|Miss Pinkerton's academy]] she is educated alongside the daughters of merchants and gentry but treated as a servant — required to teach French in exchange for her tuition, snubbed by the headmistress, and sneered at by the pupils (002-016).

> (002-023) Rebecca laughed in her face, with a horrid sarcastic air… Miss Pinkerton retreated in consternation.

This early experience of social humiliation forms her: she learns that virtue is unrewarded, that [[money]] and position are the only currencies that matter, and that her intelligence and charm are the only weapons at her disposal. When she leaves [[chiswick-mall|Chiswick Mall]] at nineteen, she carries with her a "fierce resolution" to escape the governess's limbo by any means necessary.

## Role in the Novel

Rebecca's career in the novel follows a three-part arc: ascent, triumph, and exposure.

**The Ascent (Chapters 1–15):** Rebecca first attempts to secure [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]], the rich Collector of [[boggley-wollah|Boggley Wollah]], but her scheme is foiled by [[george-osborne|George Osborne's]] warning and Jos's own cowardice (006-043). Undeterred, she accepts a position as governess at [[queen's-crawley|Queen's Crawley]], the estate of [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]]. There she deploys every talent — languages, music, wit, and a calculated performance of humble gratitude — to win the favour of the household, particularly the wealthy [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]] (007-004) (007-019).

**The Triumph (Chapters 15–30):** When Sir Pitt proposes marriage (after his wife's death), Rebecca is forced to reveal that she is already secretly married to his younger son, [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] (015-035). The marriage is a gamble: Rawdon is a gambler and a spendthrift, but he is also Miss Crawley's favourite nephew and presumed heir. The gamble fails when Miss Crawley, enraged at the deception, disinherits Rawdon — but the couple are already launched on their career in [[london|London]] society.

> (016-003) Mr. Rawdon's marriage was one of the honestest actions which we shall have to record in this history. The young couple, without a rap of their own, except the lady's five hundred pounds, loved each other, — married, and went down to Brighton.

**The Exposure (Chapters 30–67):** Rebecca's London career takes her to the fringes of the highest society through her relationship with [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]. She lives on credit at [[curzon-street|Curzon Street]], entertains lavishly, and is rumoured to be Steyne's mistress (044-012). The bubble bursts when Rawdon discovers her with Steyne, precipitating a separation and Rebecca's exile to the Continent. She reappears in the novel's final chapters as a "demirep" — a woman of damaged reputation — but, true to form, she has survived. In the novel's closing pages, she becomes [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley's]] companion — and, it is darkly implied, hastens his death to inherit his life insurance (067-015).

## Character Analysis

Rebecca Sharp is Thackeray's most subversive creation. She is intelligent, charming, and clear-sighted in a world where virtue is systematically unrewarded. Her famous declaration — "I think I could be a good woman if I had five thousand a year" (036-048) — is both self-justification and genuine social criticism. The novel refuses to either condemn or exonerate her; instead, it shows that the [[society]] that punishes her for [[ambition]] is itself founded on the same values she merely pursues more openly.

Her satirical function is to expose the hypocrisy of a social order that pretends to value virtue while rewarding wealth, birth, and appearance. Rebecca is condemned for doing openly what every other character does covertly: pursuing self-interest. That she is punished more severely than the corrupt aristocrats she serves — notably [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]], who suffers no consequences — exposes the class and gender prejudices that underpin Regency morality.

Thackeray's narrative technique deepens the ambiguity: the narrator alternates between condemning Rebecca's "wickedness" and winking at her audacity. When she throws Dr. Johnson's *Dictionary* out of the carriage window — a gift from the detested Miss Pinkerton — the gesture is both shocking and liberating (002-013). The reader is never allowed to settle into simple moral judgment.

## Historical Context

Rebecca belongs to a recognizable nineteenth-century type — the adventuress — who appears in fiction from Defoe's *Moll Flanders* to Balzac's *Cousine Bette*. The figure of the woman who uses beauty and intelligence to climb socially was both a source of prurient fascination and genuine anxiety in a society where women's economic opportunities were severely constrained. The governess-to-adventuress trajectory was a recognized social peril: educated women without [[fortune]] had few respectable options and powerful incentives to cross moral boundaries.

Thackeray's own illustrations for the novel depict Rebecca at key moments — the vivacious young governess charming the Crawleys, the triumphant hostess at her London salon, the humiliated wife discovered by her husband. These visual records reinforce the novel's insistence on surfaces and performance.
