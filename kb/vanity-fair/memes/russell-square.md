# Russell Square

> (001-015) Miss Sharp's stay in Russell Square may not exceed ten days.

Russell Square, the Bloomsbury residence of the Sedley family, is the novel's emblem of prosperous middle-class respectability. It is here that [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] first encounters the world of London comfort — the comfortable rooms, the family dinners, the expectation that life will continue pleasantly — and it is from Russell Square that she launches her first, failed campaign to capture [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] (003-030). In the novel's moral geography, Russell Square represents the solid, unpretentious virtue of the commercial classes against which both aristocratic decadence and social climbing ambition are measured.

## The Sedley Household

John Sedley, Esquire, is a merchant whose success entitles him to a respectable address and a place on the Stock Exchange (004-043). The house is comfortable rather than fashionable: it speaks of prosperity earned, not inherited. Mrs. Sedley presides over a domestic routine of dinners, morning calls, and family affection. The household is a portrait of *embourgeoisement* — the transformation of commercial wealth into domestic gentility.

The arrival of [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] disrupts this placid world. Her wit and charm dazzle the family; her schemes expose their guilelessness. The Sedleys are good people, and the novel makes clear that goodness, in [[london|London]] society, is a form of vulnerability.

## Social Geography

Russell Square lies in Bloomsbury, north of the fashionable West End — a neighbourhood of professional and mercantile families rather than aristocrats. Its distance from [[may-fair|May Fair]] and [[park-lane|Park Lane]] measures the social distance between the Sedleys and the world of Lord Steyne. When [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] condescends to visit Russell Square, his air of fashionable superiority — "somehow" the honour of his presence confers distinction — reveals the gulf between old money, new money, and money aspiring to be old (004-076).

## Decline and Loss

The Sedley fortune, like John Sedley's health, declines through the novel. The family loses Russell Square — a material loss that mirrors their social fall. The house passes from the Sedleys to strangers, a quiet catastrophe that Thackeray records without melodrama: one more piece of the old world consumed by the new.
