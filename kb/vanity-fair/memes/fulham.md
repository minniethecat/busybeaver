## Overview

Fulham, in the early nineteenth century, was a semi-rural suburb on the western edge of London — a place of market gardens, modest cottages, and respectable retreat from the expense of London proper. In *Vanity Fair*, Fulham serves as the Sedley family's place of diminished circumstances after bankruptcy and as the quiet setting for George and Amelia's wedding.

Thackeray uses Fulham to mark the Sedleys' social descent: from Russell Square, one of London's most fashionable addresses, to "a street leading from the Fulham Road — one of those streets which have a kind of exhausted, sham-genteel look about them" [017-016]. The move to Fulham is the spatial expression of their financial ruin.

## Description

The Sedley residence in Fulham is a study in reduced gentility:

> [017-016] ...a street leading from the Fulham Road — one of those streets which have a kind of exhausted, sham-genteel look about them...

The house is cramped compared to Russell Square. Old Mrs. Sedley keeps a lodging-house at Fulham [038-018], the last resort of a woman who once entertained London's merchant elite. The "Anna-Maria Cottages, Fulham Road West" [037-060] evoke the suburban pretensions of the aspirational lower-middle class — a far cry from the Sedleys' former grandeur.

Despite its modesty, Fulham has its moments of brightness. George Osborne visits Amelia there during their engagement, driving "down to Fulham in his gig" [020-012]. The journey from Knightsbridge "towards Fulham" [038-001] is a recurring route, tracing the distance between George's fashionable world and Amelia's diminished circumstances.

## Events

### The Sedley Family's Retreat

After bankruptcy, the Sedleys relocate to Fulham, where Mr. Sedley sinks into despondency and Mrs. Sedley attempts to maintain appearances by keeping a lodging-house. The annual income from their children — Jos in India and Amelia married to George Osborne — becomes "the chief support of the old father and mother at Fulham" [038-004].

### George and Amelia's Wedding

The most significant event at Fulham is the marriage of George Osborne and Amelia Sedley. The wedding takes place not at a fashionable London church but at "a certain chapel near the Fulham Road" [022-009] — a quiet, almost furtive ceremony that reflects the Osborne family's disapproval of the match. George comes home from the wedding "to find his father" [021-015] waiting, and the confrontation that follows severs George from his family.

### Dobbin's Residence

Major Dobbin, the quiet hero of the novel, also has a connection to Fulham: "a certain quiet, comfortable house, in a street leading from the Fulham Road" [017-016] where he lodges. It is a characteristically modest residence for a man who shuns display.

## Historical Context

Regency Fulham was a transitional zone between the city and the countryside — close enough to London for daily business but rural enough to offer cheaper rents and a quieter pace. The Fulham Road was a major thoroughfare connecting Knightsbridge to the villages of Fulham and Putney.

The district's character in Thackeray's time was shaped by the presence of the Bishop of London's palace at Fulham and by the market gardens that supplied London's produce. The speculative building of cottages like "Anna-Maria Cottages" was characteristic of the period's suburban expansion, catering to those who aspired to gentility but could not afford central London addresses.
