# Hypocrisy

Hypocrisy is the central satirical target of *Vanity Fair*. From the opening chapter at Miss Pinkerton's academy to the final pages at the charity bazaar, Thackeray systematically exposes the gap between the professed values of Regency society and its actual behaviour. The novel's subtitle — "A Novel without a Hero" — signals this project from the outset: in a world where everyone is compromised, the very category of "hero" is itself a form of hypocrisy.

## Piety Without Virtue

Religious hypocrisy receives some of Thackeray's sharpest and funniest treatment. Lady Southdown distributes improving tracts like "The Washerwoman of Finchley Common" [033-005] while tyrannizing her family and scheming for worldly advantage. Mr. Osborne reads the Bible each morning but disowns his son George for marrying beneath him — an act of paternal cruelty dressed in the language of religious principle [024-012]. The Evangelical movement, with its emphasis on outward piety and inward assurance, provides rich material for Thackeray's satire of a society that has made religion another mode of social display.

The narrator's treatment of religious characters is consistently ironic. Those who profess the most piety — Lady Southdown, Mrs. Bute Crawley — are often the most ruthless in pursuing their worldly interests. Mrs. Bute Crawley manages her husband's parish with an eye to every possible advantage, combining religious zeal with a talent for intrigue [011-020]. Meanwhile, genuinely good characters like Amelia or Dobbin are rarely demonstrative about their faith; their goodness expresses itself in action rather than profession.

## The Marriage Market

The marriage market is the arena where hypocrisy operates most openly and systematically. Young women are trained to display accomplishments designed to attract husbands, while young men are evaluated primarily by their incomes and prospects. The language of love and sentiment — of hearts and darts and romantic devotion — disguises the commercial transaction at the heart of most marriages in the novel.

Becky Sharp is simply more honest about this transaction than her more "respectable" counterparts. When she pursues Jos Sedley for his money at the Vauxhall pleasure gardens [006-001], or charms Rawdon Crawley for his expectations as Sir Pitt's favourite son [015-009], she is doing openly what others do under the cover of romantic convention. Her honesty about her mercenary motives, paradoxically, makes her less hypocritical than the respectable young ladies who protest their disinterested love while calculating a suitor's income to the last shilling.

## Respectability as Performance

Thackeray presents respectability as a social performance rather than a moral quality. Characters are judged not by what they do but by what they appear to do — by the surface of their conduct rather than its substance. The appearance of propriety matters more than the reality, a truth that Becky understands and exploits throughout the novel with virtuosic skill.

When Lord Steyne's world of aristocratic corruption is contrasted with the Sedleys' merchant respectability, Thackeray refuses to moralize. The aristocracy is corrupt, but its corruption is at least not disguised by pretensions to virtue. The middle-class world, by contrast, demands that its failings be hidden beneath a veneer of respectability — a form of hypocrisy that is, in its way, more dishonest than the frank cynicism of a Lord Steyne [047-005].

## Self-Deception and the Hypocrisy of Sentiment

A deeper layer of Thackeray's anatomy of hypocrisy is self-deception. Characters do not merely deceive others; they deceive themselves. Amelia Sedley's worship of her dead husband George Osborne is the novel's most sustained example: she constructs a posthumous image of George as a faithful, devoted husband that bears no resemblance to the selfish, faithless man the reader has actually met [061-001]. Her sentiment is genuine in its intensity, but its content is a fiction. Thackeray treats this self-deception with a characteristic mixture of sympathy and irony: Amelia is a victim of her own capacity for illusion.

Jos Sedley's vanity about his appearance, his pretensions to fashion and military consequence, represent a more comic form of the same self-deception. He genuinely believes himself to be dashing and impressive — a belief that the narrator punctures at every opportunity [003-005].

## The Narrator's Irony

Thackeray's narrative voice is the novel's primary weapon against hypocrisy. The narrator's tone — worldly, ironic, never quite sincere — makes it impossible to take any character's self-presentation at face value. When the narrator praises a character's virtue, the reader learns to listen for the ironic undertone that almost invariably undercuts the apparent praise.

This narrative strategy places the reader in a position of apparent moral superiority — we see through the hypocrisies that the characters themselves cannot or will not acknowledge. But Thackeray's deeper game is to implicate the reader as well. The famous "Before the Curtain" preface [000-001] explicitly addresses the audience as participants in the same vanity fair they are about to watch. If we are honest, we recognize our own hypocrisies in his characters — and our own hunger for the performance of virtue in our appetite for novels that flatter our moral sensibilities.

## The Limits of Satire

Thackeray's treatment of hypocrisy is not without its own tensions. The narrator who exposes everyone else's pretensions must necessarily exempt himself from exposure — a position that is itself a form of the performance he satirizes. The novel's repeated insistence that it is telling the truth about human nature is, in its own way, a rhetorical performance designed to win the reader's trust. Whether Thackeray was conscious of this further layer of irony is one of the questions that makes *Vanity Fair* a more complex novel than its reputation as straightforward satire might suggest.
