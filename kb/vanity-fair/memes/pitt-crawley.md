# Pitt Crawley

> Blockquote from corpus

Pitt Crawley — the elder son and heir of Sir Pitt — is the novel's study in religious hypocrisy. A sanctimonious MP who speaks constantly of duty and principle while scheming as ruthlessly as any character in the novel, he represents the union of piety and self-interest that Thackeray found particularly nauseating.

## Background

Pitt is the elder son of Sir Pitt Crawley and the heir to Queen's Crawley. Educated at Eton and Oxford, he has acquired a manner of solemn piety that masks an ambition as cold as Rebecca Sharp's. He is his father's opposite in manner — polished where Sir Pitt is coarse, sanctimonious where Sir Pitt is frank — but identical in substance

## Role in the Novel

 both men are governed entirely by self-interest.

## Character Analysis

Pitt's role is to demonstrate that religion is no barrier to greed. He pursues Miss Crawley's favour with the same determination as the rest of the family, but he does it in the language of Christian duty. His triumph — inheriting the estate and the parliamentary seat — is the novel's final comment on the relationship between piety and success in Vanity Fair

## Historical Context

The character type represented by Pitt Crawley was a recognisable figure in Regency society. Thackeray's treatment draws on contemporary social observation and the satirical tradition.
