# House of Lords

> (047-001) Gaunt House occupies nearly a side of the square...

The House of Lords is the upper chamber of the British Parliament, composed of the hereditary peerage and the bishops of the Church of England. In *Vanity Fair*, it represents the institutional embodiment of the aristocratic power that [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]] and, later, the reformed [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]] wield — a power that the novel examines with satirical precision.

## Role in the Novel

The House of Lords is the political stage on which the novel's most powerful characters operate. [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]], as a marquess, sits in the Lords and exercises immense influence through the patronage and corruption that the unreformed Parliament made possible. His power radiates from [[gaunt-house|Gaunt House]] through the political and social world of Regency London, and his control of parliamentary seats is part of the system of aristocratic dominance that the novel exposes (047-020).

[[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]] also enters the Lords, though by a different route: the peerage he inherits from his father. The elder Sir Pitt had been a Tory of the old school, a borough-monger of the unreformed type. His son Pitt, by contrast, adopts reforming rhetoric and represents a new generation of aristocratic politics — though Thackeray implies that the substance of aristocratic power remains largely unchanged. The House of Lords thus serves as the institutional symbol of a social hierarchy that the novel's satire targets: a closed circle of hereditary privilege that dispenses patronage, controls elections, and maintains its hold on power regardless of political fashion.

- [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]] — Eventually enters the House of Lords as a peer

## Historical Note

The House of Lords in the Regency period was an entirely hereditary body (with the exception of the bishops). The Reform Act of 1832, which came after the novel's main action but within Thackeray's lifetime, began the long process of curtailing its powers. Thackeray's portrait of the Lords captures the institution at the height of its unreformed power.
