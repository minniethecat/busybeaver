# Rome

Rome enters Vanity Fair as one of the continental cities where [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] and [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] reside during their post-Waterloo European exile. It is a city of balls and bankers, of grand company and "unwholesome" air — a place where Becky's social ambitions play out on a European stage.

## The Crawleys in Rome

The Crawleys' time in Rome is narrated through the story of a ball given by a banker, the Prince Polonia. "It happened at Rome once that Mrs. de Rawdon's half-year's salary had just been paid into the principal banker's there, and, as everybody who had a balance of above five hundred scudi was invited to the balls which this prince of merchants gave during the winter, Becky had the honour of a card" [064-034]. The Prince, despite his grandiose title — the Princess was "of the family of Pompili, lineally descended from the second king of Rome" — was the grandson of a man who "sold wash-balls, essences, tobacco, and pocket-handkerchiefs" [064-034]. Thackeray's exposure of the gap between aristocratic pretension and commercial origin is characteristic of Vanity Fair's treatment of continental society.

"All the great company in Rome thronged to his saloons — Princes, Dukes, Ambassadors, artists, fiddlers, monsignori, young bears with their leaders — every rank and condition of man" [064-034]. The heterogeneous Roman society, mixing aristocracy with artists and clergy, offers a more fluid social landscape than London's rigid hierarchies.

## The Roman Fever

Rome is also a place of danger. Becky's valet warns her: "Rome is very unwholesome" [064-042]. He expands the warning: "There is always malaria for some people. That cursed marsh wind kills many at all seasons. ... Go away from Rome, I tell you — or you will be ill and die" [064-044]. The Roman fever (malaria, then endemic in the Campagna) serves as a practical threat and a narrative reminder that even the most glittering European cities have their mortal underside.
