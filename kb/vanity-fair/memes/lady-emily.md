# Lady Emily

Lady Emily, the daughter of Lord Southdown and sister of [[lady-jane|Lady Jane Crawley]], is the author of such improving works as "The Washerwoman of Finchley Common" and other evangelical tracts distributed to the rural poor (033-032). Thackeray uses her — and the Southdown family's religious enthusiasm more broadly — to satirise the marriage of aristocratic privilege and evangelical piety: a combination that allows the great to feel virtuous while changing nothing about the order that sustains them.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Emily appears in the Queen's Crawley chapters as a representative of the Southdown family's particular brand of piety. She presses tracts upon the household, recommends early religious education for the young, and embodies the alliance between the landed interest and the evangelical revival — two forces that, in Thackeray's view, reinforced each other at the expense of anyone outside the charmed circle. Her tracts are a running joke: well-meant, utterly ineffectual, and produced in industrial quantities.

## Relationships

- **[[lady-jane|Lady Jane Crawley]]**: Her sister, whose quiet goodness she lacks
- **[[lady-southdown|Lady Southdown]]**: Her mother, the matriarch of evangelical enthusiasm
- **[[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]]**: Her brother-in-law, who shares her taste for sermonising

## Character Analysis

Lady Emily is a minor figure, but she illuminates a major theme: the comfortable alliance between piety and property. Her tracts are not wicked; they are merely useless — literature designed to make the distributor feel virtuous without requiring anything of substance from her. In the novel's moral economy, she is not a villain but a symptom: religion as gentility, faith as furniture.
