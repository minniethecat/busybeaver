# Charity

> (051-024) Whereas, by a little charity and mutual forbearance, things would be so different!

Charity — in its theological sense of *caritas*, the love of God expressed through love of neighbour — is the moral standard against which *Vanity Fair* measures its characters, and almost all are found wanting. The novel is saturated with acts that call themselves charitable: Miss Crawley's "prodigious benevolence" (014-080), the charity bazaars furnished by Lady Southdown's daughters (039-003), the subscription lists that display donors' names as prominently as their donations (046-006). But Thackeray's treatment of charity is characteristically double-edged: genuine generosity exists in the novel — most notably in Dobbin's self-effacing devotion — yet it is perpetually shadowed by performances of benevolence that serve only the giver's vanity. The concept is intimately connected to [[honour]] and [[sentiment]]: true charity requires the first and is often confused with the second.

## In the Novel

The novel's most sustained treatment of charity appears in its portrait of Miss Crawley's benevolence. The wealthy spinster's "prodigious benevolence rather fatigued poor little Amelia" (014-080), and the narrator's choice of "fatigued" is precise: Miss Crawley's charity is exhausting because it is performative, a demonstration of power rather than a response to need. She "selected all sorts of objects for her benevolence" with "enlarged and rather capricious" taste (052-002), treating charitable giving as a form of consumption.

In [[ch05-chapter-v-dobbin-of-ours|Chapter V]], Dobbin's secret generosity provides the novel's counter-example. His anonymous gift of a piano to Amelia — an act that costs him financially and emotionally, and for which he expects and receives no credit — is genuine charity in the theological sense: love given without hope of return (005-044). The contrast with George Osborne, who "with the greatest generosity" offers money he never actually pays (013-021), is the novel's clearest moral distinction.

The [[ch01-chapter-i-chiswick-mall|opening chapter]] establishes the theme's satirical register. Miss Pinkerton's farewell to Amelia — the gift of a Johnson's Dictionary and a moral lecture — is charity as institutional ceremony, a transaction in which the giver's moral superiority is the real currency (001-008). Little Jemima's secret gift to Becky — "only two-and-ninepence, and poor Becky will be miserable if she doesn't get one" (001-021) — establishes the novel's moral economy: small, secret acts of kindness outweigh grand public gestures.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's satire of charity is directed not at the virtue itself but at its degradation into social currency. Charity in *Vanity Fair* is a vehicle for the very vices — [[reputation|self-regard]], status-display, moral one-upmanship — that genuine charity ought to oppose. Lady Southdown's charitable activities are explicitly linked to her religious fanaticism, a brand of evangelical Calvinism that enables her to despise the very people she purports to help (039-003). The charity bazaar — that quintessentially Victorian institution — is presented as a marketplace in which benevolence is bought and sold like any other commodity.

The novel's deepest satirical insight is that charity, like every other virtue, is subject to the corruptions of the fair. "A little charity and mutual forbearance" would change everything (051-024), the narrator observes — the conditional "would" carrying the weight of everything the novel has shown us about why it does not.

## Historical Context

The Regency-Victorian period witnessed a transformation in the organisation of charity. The Evangelical revival had made philanthropy a central expression of Christian piety, while the growth of voluntary associations — the Governesses' Benevolent Institution (1841), the Ragged Schools, the charity hospitals — created new arenas for public benevolence. The charity bazaar, the subscription list, and the philanthropic committee were inventions of this period, and Thackeray's depiction of them is a contemporary satire.

The novel registers the class dimension of Victorian charity: the "little charity-girls at the Foundling" (042-001) and the "charity-boys with shining faces" (054-022) are the objects of benevolence, never its agents. The charity school at Miss Pinkerton's — where the pupils are taught to be grateful for an education designed to keep them in their place — is the novel's emblem of institutionalised benevolence as social control.

## Related Characters

- **Amelia Sedley**: The novel's most naturally charitable character, whose genuine kindness is also her weakness — she cannot distinguish between those who deserve her charity and those who exploit it. Her benevolence is real but undiscerning (014-080).
- **William Dobbin**: The novel's only example of charity in its fullest sense — love given without hope of return, generosity without display, sacrifice without resentment (005-044).
- **Miss Crawley**: Charity as performance and power — her "prodigious benevolence" sustains her self-image while permitting cruelty to those who fail to perform gratitude (014-080) (052-002).
