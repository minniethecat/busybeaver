# Lord Steyne

> (047-015) He had done wife, children, love, ambition, vanity. But he remembered his old companions...

The Marquess of Steyne is the novel's most dangerous character — a man whose absolute power, immense wealth, and complete moral indifference make him the living embodiment of the corruption at the heart of the Regency aristocracy. His patronage of Rebecca Sharp is the novel's darkest transaction: in exchange for his favour, he expects everything, and what he cannot take by consent he is prepared to take by force. The novel treats him with an uncharacteristic seriousness: unlike the Crawleys or the Osbornes, Steyne is not comic. He is simply terrifying.

## Background

Lord Steyne is a marquess, a member of the House of Lords, and a fixture at court. His town residence, Gaunt House, is one of the great aristocratic palaces of London, and his influence extends into every corner of political and social life. His fortune is vast, his connections are impeccable, and his reputation — as a libertine, a gambler, and a man who has corrupted or destroyed everyone who has come within his orbit — is known to everyone and mentioned by no one.

## Role in the Novel

Steyne appears relatively late in the novel, and his entrance transforms its moral register. Where the earlier chapters treat social climbing as comedy, the Steyne chapters treat it as something closer to horror. His relationship with Rebecca — he provides money, entrée, and protection in exchange for her company, her charm, and, it is universally assumed, her sexual compliance — is the novel's most explicit demonstration of how power operates in this world.

The climax of the Steyne plot — Rawdon's discovery of Rebecca with Steyne, the confrontation, and Rawdon's departure to fight a duel — is the novel's most dramatic sequence. Steyne emerges from it damaged but intact; his power is such that even exposure cannot touch him. The novel's implication is clear: at the highest levels of society, morality is simply irrelevant.

## Relationships

- **Rebecca Sharp**: His protégée and, almost certainly, his mistress. Their relationship is the novel's study in the economics of patronage: he provides money and access; she provides entertainment, charm, and the performance of grateful submission.
- **Rawdon Crawley**: His rival and victim. Rawdon's discovery of Rebecca with Steyne destroys his marriage and his life, while Steyne walks away unscathed.
- **Lady Steyne**: His wife, a woman whom the narrator describes in terms that suggest she has been systematically destroyed by her marriage.

## Character Analysis

Steyne is Thackeray's most uncompromising portrait of the aristocracy. Unlike Sir Pitt, who is merely coarse and corrupt, Steyne is intelligent, cultivated, and completely amoral. He is what the aristocracy produces at its most powerful and least accountable: a man for whom other people are instruments and for whom morality is a form of naivety.

## Historical Context

The character is widely believed to be based on the third Marquess of Hertford, a notorious Regency libertine and art collector who was Thackeray's contemporary. The identification is plausible but not exact: Steyne is a composite of Regency aristocratic types rather than a portrait of any single individual.
