# Samuel Johnson

Dr. Samuel Johnson (1709–1784) is one of several historical figures invoked in *Vanity Fair* to establish the novel's temporal and cultural setting. Though he died decades before the novel's main action, his name — and his *Dictionary* — appear at key moments. [[miss-pinkerton|Miss Pinkerton]] claims him as a personal acquaintance, and the presentation of *Johnson's Dictionary* to departing students is the academy's most solemn ritual. Thackeray uses Johnson's cultural authority to underline Miss Pinkerton's pretensions, while the *Dictionary* itself becomes a quietly comic prop that appears at moments of linguistic struggle.

## Role in the Novel

Johnson appears in the novel in three ways: as a name Miss Pinkerton drops to assert her cultural standing, as the *Dictionary* she presents to [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] (and which [[jemima-pinkerton|Jemima Pinkerton]] hastily substitutes for [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]), and as a touchstone of English literary authority. The *Dictionary* reappears years later when [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]], illiterate in every practical sense, struggles to compose a letter with its help — a scene that wryly comments on the gap between Johnson's Augustan dignity and the grubby realities of Vanity Fair.

> Miss Pinkerton proceeded to write her own name, and Miss Sedley's, in the fly-leaf of a Johnson's Dictionary — the interesting work which she invariably presented to her scholars, on their departure from the Mall.

## Character Analysis

Thackeray's use of Johnson is characteristically double-edged. On one level, the *Dictionary* represents genuine learning and authority — Johnson really was a great man. On another, Miss Pinkerton's claim to have known him is exactly the kind of social pretension the novel exists to expose. And when Rawdon Crawley, the novel's most un-bookish character, must consult the *Dictionary* to write a letter, the distance between Johnson's world of moral seriousness and Vanity Fair's world of scheming and survival could hardly be wider.
