# The Crawley Family

> (007-003) ...male and female members of the Crawley family.

The Crawley family is the ancient but threadbare Hampshire gentry clan whose internal rivalries and inheritance struggles provide one of the major plot engines of *Vanity Fair*. Centered on the estate of [[queens-crawley|Queen's Crawley]], the family encompasses two main branches — Sir Pitt's household at the Hall and the Bute Crawleys at the rectory — united only in their shared ambition to secure the fortune of [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]], the family's wealthy spinster.

## Role in the Novel

The Crawley family is introduced as an old county family with a pedigree reaching back to Queen Elizabeth (007-002), but their circumstances are far from grand. [[sir-pitt-crawley|Sir Pitt Crawley]], the head of the family, is a coarse, miserly baronet who speaks "in the coarsest and vulgarest Hampshire accent" (007-038). His second wife [[lady-crawley|Lady Crawley]] is a silent, downtrodden figure. His elder son [[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]] is a priggish, reforming clergyman; his younger son [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] is a cavalry officer of courage but little intellect.

The family's internal dynamics are driven entirely by the question of [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]'s inheritance. The spinster's fortune of seventy thousand pounds is the prize, and every member of the family positions themselves to secure it (007-003). [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] enters this world as governess to Sir Pitt's daughters and quickly learns to navigate its treacherous currents. Her secret marriage to Rawdon disrupts the family's calculations, alienating Miss Crawley and setting off a chain of consequences that reverberate through the rest of the novel (014-082). The Crawley family drama is Thackeray's sustained satire of the greed, hypocrisy, and manipulation that the pursuit of inheritance generates within the English gentry.
