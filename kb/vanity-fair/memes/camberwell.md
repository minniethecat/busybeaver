# Camberwell

**Camberwell** is a district in South London, about three miles from the City. In *Vanity Fair*, it functions as far more than a residential address for the Dobbin family — it becomes a geographical emblem of the modest, steadfast, and unluxurious domestic virtue that the novel, in its complicated way, sets against the glittering vanities of the West End.

## Historical Character

In the early nineteenth century, Camberwell was a semi-rural village on the southern edge of London, surrounded by market gardens, open fields, and the occasional villa of a City merchant who had prospered enough to afford clean air and a garden but not enough — or not aspirationally enough — to aspire to Mayfair. The parish church of St. Giles, dating from the medieval period, anchored a village centre that had grown along the main road to the Surrey downs. By the Regency period, Camberwell was precisely the kind of place where a retired grocer (John Sedley's origins, perhaps not coincidentally) or a modestly successful tradesman's family like the Dobbins would settle — respectable, comfortable, and utterly without fashion.

## In the Novel

### The Homecoming

Camberwell first appears as the destination of Major Dobbin's headlong journey home from India. Thackeray describes the English countryside rolling past — "happy and green," "the charming friendly English landscape" — but Dobbin sees none of it:

> You see he was so eager to see his parents at Camberwell. [058-007]

The sentence is Thackeray at his most compressed. The entire English landscape, so lovingly described in the preceding sentences, dissolves into a single fixed point: Camberwell, where Dobbin's parents wait. For a character whose life has been defined by self-effacing loyalty — to George Osborne, to Amelia, to the regiment — this eagerness to reach his parental home is one of the few moments in the novel where Dobbin's own desires are stated directly. Camberwell represents what Dobbin himself wants, as distinct from what he is willing to give up for others. The modesty of the object — not an estate, not a title, but his parents' house in a South London suburb — is part of Thackeray's moral architecture: Dobbin is the novel's one genuinely good man, and his deepest wish is correspondingly simple.

### Miss Ann's Complaint

The second reference is in a very different key. Dobbin, returned to England, has become the *ami de la maison* of the Sedley household in Gillespie Street — always present for Emmy and Georgy and old Mr. Sedley, scarcely ever at his own family's Camberwell home:

> "He might almost as well be at Madras for anything WE see of him," Miss Ann Dobbin remarked at Camberwell. [060-008]

Ann Dobbin's complaint is the voice of the family left behind — the devoted sister whose love for her brother is no less real for being unromantic, predictable, and domestic. The detail that her remark is made "at Camberwell" is essential to its meaning: Camberwell is where the people who love Dobbin unconditionally and unglamorously are waiting for him — the same people he had been so eager to see when he stepped off the ship. That he now neglects them for the Sedleys' Gillespie Street household hints at the subtle cost of Dobbin's long devotion to Amelia: his own family has become secondary.

## The Moral Geography

Camberwell stands as one pole in the novel's moral geography of London. Against Mayfair and Park Lane — the glittering, heartless centres of fashion and intrigue — Thackeray sets the suburban villages of Camberwell, Fulham, and their like, where people live quietly, love steadily, and expect nothing more from life than modest comfort and family affection. The Dobbins of Camberwell are not glamorous (Miss Ann is, the narrator gently implies, a spinster of limited attractions), but they are *good* — and in the novel's final accounting, goodness counts for more than glamour. William Dobbin gets his hard-won happy ending; Camberwell, his origin and his moral compass, is part of why he deserves it.

Camberwell also serves as a quiet rebuke to the novel's more strident social climbers. Mr. Osborne, who made his fortune in the City, would be ashamed to live in Camberwell: he requires Russell Square, and later a house in the West End, to mark his ascent. The Sedleys, once prosperous, sink from Russell Square to a cramped house in a less fashionable neighbourhood. Only the Dobbins stay where they began — and they, uniquely among the novel's families, are never ruined, never humiliated, and never forced to perform a social identity that their income cannot support.
