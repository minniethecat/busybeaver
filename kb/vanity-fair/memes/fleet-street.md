# Fleet Street

**Fleet Street** is a historic street in the City of London, mentioned once in Thackeray's *Vanity Fair* as the setting for a small but revealing moment in George Osborne's courtship of Amelia Sedley.

## George Osborne's stop

On his way to visit Amelia at [[russell-square|Russell Square]], George Osborne gets off the coach in Fleet Street and is distracted by a jeweller's window:

> And I dare say he would have bought something very handsome for Amelia; only, getting off the coach in Fleet Street, he was attracted by a handsome shirt-pin in a jeweller's window, which he could not resist; and having paid for that, had very little money to spare for indulging in any further exercise of kindness [013-026].

The incident is characteristic of George Osborne: his vanity and self-indulgence take precedence over generosity towards Amelia. The shirt-pin he buys for himself consumes the money that might have bought her a gift.

## In the novel

Fleet Street appears only in this single passage, but its function is significant. As one of London's principal commercial thoroughfares — known for its shops, taverns, and later its newspapers — it represents the material temptations of the city that George, despite his pretensions to gentlemanly conduct, cannot resist.
