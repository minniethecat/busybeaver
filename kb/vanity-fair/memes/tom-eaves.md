# Tom Eaves

**Tom Eaves** is a minor but structurally important character in *Vanity Fair* — a London gossip who serves as a narrative device for revealing the private scandals and secrets of the great aristocratic families. Thackeray introduces him as "little Tom Eaves, who knows everything, and who showed me the place" [047-009].

## Function as Narrative Device

Tom Eaves appears in the chapters concerning Gaunt House and the Marquess of Steyne. He functions as an embodied footnote — a source of the kind of insider knowledge that no omniscient narrator could credibly claim without citing a human informant. Through Eaves, Thackeray can reveal the hidden architecture of aristocratic power: the private doors, the secret apartments, the financial entanglements, and the sexual humiliations that the great families conceal behind their stately facades.

## Knowledge of Gaunt House

Eaves reveals the geography of Lord Steyne's secret life at Gaunt House. He points out the "little modest back door" leading to Gaunt Mews and recounts:

> "The Prince and Perdita have been in and out of that door, sir. Marianne Clarke has entered it with the Duke of ———. It conducts to the famous petits appartements of Lord Steyne — one, sir, fitted up all in ivory and white satin, another in ebony and black velvet." [047-009]

He knows the financial history too — how Egalité Orleans roasted partridges in Steyne's silver kitchen on the night they won a hundred thousand pounds at ombre, and how "half of the money went to the French Revolution, half to purchase Lord Gaunt's Marquisate and Garter" [047-009].

## On the Steyne Marriage

Eaves provides the key intelligence on Lady Steyne's mysterious submissiveness to her husband:

> "The humiliations which that woman has been made to undergo, in her own house, have been frightful; Lord Steyne has made her sit down to table with women with whom I would rather die than allow Mrs. Eaves to associate." [047-010]

He hints at a dark secret: Lady Steyne's affair, years ago during the emigration, with the Abbé de la Marche — a former Colonel of Mousquetaires Gris who was later shot in Brittany. "People are not so unhappy unless they have something to repent of," Eaves concludes, "and depend on it, that woman would not be so submissive as she is if the Marquis had not some sword to hold over her" [047-010].

## Philosophy of Social Observation

Tom Eaves is not merely a gossip; he is a social theorist. He expounds a sardonic philosophy of aristocratic dysfunction:

> "Take it as a rule, the fathers and elder sons of all great families hate each other. The Crown Prince is always in opposition to the crown or hankering after it." [047-011]

On the subject of elder brothers and younger sons, he observes: "every elder brother looks upon the cadets of the house as his natural enemies, who deprive him of so much ready money which ought to be his by right. I tell you they are all Turks in their hearts" [047-011].

## Character and Circumstances

Eaves has "laid out every shilling of his fortune on an annuity" and can therefore "afford to bear no malice to his nephews and nieces, and to have no other feeling with regard to his betters but a constant and generous desire to dine with them" [047-012]. When a great man approaches, Tom Eaves's hat drops off his head and he rushes forward "with a bow and a grin, which showed that he knew the world too — in the Tomeavesian way, that is" [047-012].

This self-description captures the paradox of Eaves's position: he is both insider and outsider, a man who knows every secret of the great but must bow and scrape for his dinner. His gossip is both his currency and his revenge.

## Narrative Significance

Thackeray uses Tom Eaves to solve a technical problem in fiction: how to convey to the reader the full sordidness of aristocratic life without violating the decorum of the omniscient narrator. By attributing the most damning revelations to Eaves, Thackeray maintains plausible deniability — these are merely the words of a gossip — while still planting them firmly in the reader's mind. Eaves is, in this sense, Thackeray's mask: the novelist as scandal-monger, the historian of Vanity Fair as a man who simply "knows everybody's affairs."
