# Pumpernickel

> (062-022) ...the little comfortable Ducal town of Pumpernickel (that very place where Sir Pitt Crawley had been attache in younger days)...

Pumpernickel is a fictional German duchy that serves as the setting for the later chapters of *Vanity Fair*. It is a minor German state with its own court, theatre, and petty dignitaries, embodying the comic provincialism of small-state Germany in the post-Napoleonic era. Thackeray uses Pumpernickel as a stage for the novel's final act of exile, reconciliation, and social retreat.

## In the Novel

Pumpernickel appears in the novel's backstory as the diplomatic posting where the [[lord-steyne|Marquess of Steyne]] served as an attache in his youth (033-023). Young Pitt Crawley (later Sir Pitt) also served at the Pumpernickel legation, and his pretentious references to "H.H. the Duchess of Pumpernickel" are one of his affectations (033-023). The town is described as "the little comfortable Ducal town" where Sir Pitt had been attache (062-022).

In the novel's final chapters, [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] and [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] are discovered living in Pumpernickel, where Becky has carved out a modest position in local society. The town boasts a Royal Grand Ducal Pumpernickelisch Hof theatre (062-023) and a modest social scene that provides Becky with scope for her talents. In Pumpernickel, we see Becky in reduced but still resourceful circumstances, charming the local dignitaries and maintaining her hold over the hapless Jos. It is here that the novel's resolution begins to take shape, as [[william-dobbin|Dobbin]] finally reunites with [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] and Becky's long exile draws toward its ambiguous close.

## Historical Note

Pumpernickel is entirely fictional but draws on the real patchwork of small German states that existed before unification. Thackeray's portrait satirizes the pretensions and ceremonies of petty German courts, a familiar target of British humor in the nineteenth century. The name "Pumpernickel" is also the German word for a type of dark rye bread, adding a comic dimension for readers who recognized the pun.
