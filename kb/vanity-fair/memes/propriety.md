# Propriety

> (030-004) It was from a sense of female delicacy and propriety that she refrained from leading him into the room.

Propriety — the unwritten code governing correct female behaviour, speech, and appearance — is the standard by which the women of *Vanity Fair* are judged, and the performance that most of them cannot sustain. It encompasses everything from Miss Pinkerton's dictionary-bestowing ceremonies (001-008) to Amelia's anxious self-policing at Vauxhall, and it operates as the novel's sharpest instrument of social control: a standard that is absolute in its demands, arbitrary in its application, and impossible for any actual woman to meet. The concept is inseparable from [[genteel|gentility]] and [[reputation]]: propriety is the visible performance of the first and the brittle shell protecting the second.

## In the Novel

The novel opens in the temple of propriety: Miss Pinkerton's academy for young ladies, where every gesture is regulated, every word is measured, and the ultimate reward for correct behaviour is a Johnson's Dictionary — a book of rules for a life governed by rules (001-008). The academy's "frigid correctness" (002-017) is propriety stripped of warmth, humanity, or love: a system designed to produce women who will not embarrass their families.

Amelia Sedley is the novel's most devoted practitioner of propriety, and her devotion is her tragedy. At Vauxhall, her "sense of female delicacy and propriety" prevents her from leading Dobbin to her husband — a gesture of correctness that enables George's faithlessness (030-004). The scene is characteristic: propriety in the novel is always in tension with truth, always requiring the suppression of what a character actually feels in favour of what she ought to feel.

[[ch02-chapter-ii-in-which-miss-sharp-and-miss-sedley-prepare-to-open-the-campaign|Chapter II]] presents the novel's counter-example. Rebecca Sharp, leaving Miss Pinkerton's, performs propriety with such perfect technique that it becomes indistinguishable from parody. She is "humble, gentle, grateful" (004-044) — every adjective the propriety code requires — and every adjective is a lie. Becky understands what Amelia does not: that propriety is a performance, and that the performer who masters the code can exploit it.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's satire of propriety operates through a systematic demonstration that correct behaviour is no guarantee of correct character. Miss Pinkerton, the novel's high priestess of propriety, is a snob and a tyrant whose moral authority is entirely a function of her institutional position (001-008). Lady Crawley, who receives company "with great propriety and modesty" (063-020), is a cipher — propriety has so thoroughly replaced personality that there is nothing left.

The novel's deepest satirical insight is that propriety is a weapon wielded by women against other women. The code of correct behaviour is enforced not by men — who are generally indifferent to it — but by women who use it to police their rivals and secure their own [[reputation|standing]]. The respectability that propriety confers is a zero-sum commodity: one woman's propriety is established by another woman's disgrace.

## Historical Context

The Regency code of female propriety was rooted in the conduct-book tradition that flourished from the late eighteenth century — works like Hannah More's *Strictures on the Modern System of Female Education* (1799) and Dr. Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters* (1774), which prescribed minute regulations for female speech, dress, reading, and comportment. Miss Pinkerton's academy is a fictional representative of the hundreds of real seminaries that marketed propriety as a commodity to the middle classes.

The irony Thackeray exploits is that the Regency period — the age of Byron, the Prince Regent, and the *haut ton* — was notorious for its sexual licence. Propriety was the standard demanded of women while being systematically violated by the men who demanded it. The double standard is the novel's unstated subject: George Osborne's honour is publicly unimpeachable while his private behaviour is dishonourable; Becky Sharp's propriety is impeccable while her character is not.

## Related Characters

- **Amelia Sedley**: The novel's most perfect embodiment of propriety, and its most complete victim. Her "female delicacy and propriety" (030-004) is genuine but disabling — it prevents her from seeing what is before her eyes and acting on what she knows.
- **Miss Pinkerton**: Propriety institutionalised — a woman whose entire identity is a set of rules, and who uses those rules to dominate everyone in her power (001-008).
- **Lady Crawley**: Propriety without content — a woman who receives company "with great propriety and modesty" (063-020) because there is nothing else to receive them with. She is what Amelia might become if Amelia had no inner life at all.
