# Cavendish Square

**Cavendish Square** is a garden square in the Marylebone district of London, laid out in the early eighteenth century and by the Regency period one of the city's most fashionable residential addresses. In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, Cavendish Square is the setting of a brief but emotionally revealing episode in Amelia's marriage to George Osborne.

## In Vanity Fair

After their wedding in Brighton, George Osborne — ever concerned with appearances — brings his bride to London in style:

> On quitting Brighton, our friend George, as became a person of rank and fashion travelling in a barouche with four horses, drove in state to a fine hotel in Cavendish Square, where a suite of splendid rooms, and a table magnificently furnished with plate and surrounded by a half-dozen of black and silent waiters, was ready to receive the young gentleman and his bride [026-001].

The "fine hotel in Cavendish Square" is all display. But for Amelia, the grandeur is hollow. Alone in the hotel while George goes out to his club and his old haunts, she is overcome with homesickness:

> Then she thought with terror of the great funereal damask pavilion in the vast and dingy state bedroom, which was awaiting her at the grand hotel in Cavendish Square [026-013].

She longs instead for the little white bed she slept in at home, with her mother smiling over her in the morning. The Cavendish Square hotel, for all its splendour, is the first setting of Amelia's disillusionment in marriage.

## A Regency Wedding Trip

The choice of Cavendish Square for a wedding trip was socially significant. In the Regency period, a bride and groom who took rooms in a square hotel could expect to be seen — by the servants, by the square's residents, by visitors — and George Osborne, with his barouche and four horses, clearly intended to be noticed. The hotel's "black and silent waiters" (the silent, liveried servants) and "plate" (silver tableware) were the material markers of gentility that George valued above all else.
