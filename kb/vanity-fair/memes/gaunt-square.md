# Gaunt Square

Gaunt Square is a fictional London square in *Vanity Fair*, forming the aristocratic heart of the Gaunt family's London presence. Lord Steyne's town palace stands there, and Great Gaunt Street leads off from it.

## Location

Gaunt Square connects to Great Gaunt Street, forming the center of the Gaunt family's London domain. Thackeray describes the passage through the Square into the Street:

> Having passed through Gaunt Square into Great Gaunt Street, the carriage at length stopped at a tall gloomy house between two other tall gloomy houses, each with a hatchment over the middle drawing-room window; as is the custom of houses in Great Gaunt Street, in which gloomy locality death seems to reign perpetual. [007-009]

## Lord Steyne's Palace

The Square's defining feature is Lord Steyne's town palace, which forms one entire side of the square:

> All the world knows that Lord Steyne's town palace stands in Gaunt Square, out of which Great Gaunt Street leads, whither we first conducted Rebecca, in the time of the departed Sir Pitt Crawley. [047-001]

The connection between Great Gaunt Street and Gaunt Square is emphasized when Lord Steyne notes his proximity to Sir Pitt Crawley's London residence:

> as they were neighbours (for Great Gaunt Street leads into Gaunt Square, whereof Gaunt House, as everybody knows, forms one side) my lord hoped that as soon as Lady Steyne arrived in London she would have the honour of making the acquaintance of Lady Crawley. [045-026]

## Later Years

Lord Steyne eventually abandoned Gaunt Square, preferring the warmth of Italy to the gloom of London:

> For the present Lord Steyne lives at Naples, preferring the view of the Bay and Capri and Vesuvius to the dreary aspect of the wall in Gaunt Square. [047-001]

## Significance

Gaunt Square represents the pinnacle of London aristocratic society in the novel. Its gloomy, imposing architecture mirrors the moral decay of its inhabitants — the Steyne family, whose wealth and power conceal profound corruption. The Square is the setting for one of the novel's most dramatic moments, when Rawdon Crawley is arrested for debt immediately after leaving Gaunt House [051-071].
