# Bois de Boulogne

The **Bois de Boulogne** is a large public park on the western edge of Paris, which in Thackeray's *Vanity Fair* serves as the backdrop for Becky Sharp's triumphant social ascent during the winter of 1815, following Napoleon's defeat at Waterloo.

## Becky Sharp's Paris season

After the Battle of Waterloo, Becky and Rawdon Crawley spent the winter of 1815 in Paris, living in "great splendour and gaiety" funded by the proceeds of Jos Sedley's two horses [034-072]. Becky, ever the improviser, had a sable-lined cloak converted into a pelisse:

> Becky had it made into a pelisse for herself, in which she rode in the Bois de Boulogne to the admiration of all [034-072].

Her success in Paris society was immediate and conspicuous. "Famous warriors rode by her carriage in the Bois, or crowded her modest little box at the Opera" [034-075]. The park became the stage on which Becky displayed her newly acquired status, attracting the attention of generals and aristocrats alike.

## Later disillusionment

The glamour of the Bois de Boulogne did not last. By the time the Crawleys returned to Paris in later years, the novelty had worn thin. The narrator describes the tedium of Becky's respectable life, which included "the same drive over the same stupid Bois de Boulogne, the same company of an evening" [064-023]. The park that once symbolised her social triumph had become a symbol of her boredom.

## In the novel

The Bois de Boulogne appears exclusively in the Paris chapters of *Vanity Fair*, marking the brief period between Waterloo and the Crawleys' return to England when Becky's social ambitions seemed within reach. It functions as a [[london|London]] counterpart — the fashionable outdoor space where status is performed and observed.
