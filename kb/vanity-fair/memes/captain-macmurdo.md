# Captain Macmurdo

Captain Macmurdo of the Life Guards is a veteran of [[battle-of-waterloo|Waterloo]] and an old comrade of [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] (054-023). He appears late in the novel, when Rawdon — imprisoned for debt by [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]'s machinations and betrayed by his wife [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca]] — turns to his military friends for help. Macmurdo's role is brief but structurally crucial: he is the witness from the world of masculine honour whose presence exposes the corruption of the civilian sphere. He cannot comprehend the transaction Steyne is proposing; to him, an insult is settled by a challenge, not a cheque.

## Role in the Novel

Macmurdo appears in the novel's climactic sequence: Rawdon, released from the spunging-house by his sister-in-law [[lady-jane|Lady Jane]]'s kindness, returns to [[curzon-street|Curzon Street]] to find Rebecca dining intimately with Lord Steyne. The confrontation that follows sends Rawdon to Macmurdo — to arrange a duel. Macmurdo delivers the challenge to Steyne, who, with the smooth assistance of his man [[mr-wenham|Mr. Wenham]], evades it. Macmurdo's bluff, straightforward manner — "Be hanged to these civilians" (055-061) — captures the gulf between a world where honour still means something and one where it has been entirely monetised.

## Relationships

- **[[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]]**: Old comrade; serves as his second against Lord Steyne
- **[[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]**: The target of the challenge Macmurdo delivers; Steyne's evasion of the duel exposes his cowardice
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: Rawdon's wife; Macmurdo's involvement is a direct consequence of her betrayal

## Character Analysis

Macmurdo speaks perhaps fifty words in the entire novel, but they are among the most significant: he is the voice of a code that the novel's civilian world has abandoned. His incomprehension at Steyne's behaviour is not stupidity but the shock of a man who discovers that the rules he has lived by no longer apply. In a novel that anatomises moral decay, Macmurdo is a fossil — a survival from an earlier, simpler moral ecology.
