# Islington

**Islington** is a district immediately north of the City of London. In *Vanity Fair*, it appears in a single, sharply observed reference that encapsulates the novel's relentless exposure of social pretension.

## Historical Character

In the early nineteenth century, Islington was a transitional zone on the northern edge of London. The village's position on the Great North Road had long made it a stopping-point for travellers, with numerous inns and coaching houses (the famous *Angel* among them). By the Regency period, it was developing into a suburb of tradespeople, market gardens, and dairies serving the expanding metropolis. Its social character was respectable but modest — the kind of place where a widow might keep a small coal-and-potato shop without attracting notice. This ordinariness is precisely what Thackeray exploits.

## In the Novel

Islington appears in one of Mrs. O'Dowd's magnificent monologues to the newly arrived Amelia Sedley in Brussels, during the Waterloo campaign. Mrs. O'Dowd, wife of Major O'Dowd, is briefing Amelia on the other regimental wives — who is going with the regiment, who is staying behind — and arrives at Mrs. Magenis:

> "Fanny Magenis stops with her mother, who sells small coal and potatoes, most likely, in Islington-town, hard by London, though she's always bragging of her father's ships, and pointing them out to us as they go up the river." [027-029]

The passage is a masterclass in Thackeray's technique of social demolition through casual detail. Three elements work together:

**"Most likely"** — Mrs. O'Dowd does not actually know that Fanny's mother keeps a shop in Islington; she is inferring it from Fanny's vulgarity, and the inference is meant to wound. The qualifier makes the insult more devastating, not less: it suggests that Islington coal-and-potato shops are the natural habitat of people like the Magenises.

**"Though she's always bragging of her father's ships"** — Fanny Magenis claims maritime-mercantile gentility (ships on the river, a father in trade), but Mrs. O'Dowd's Islington attribution undercuts this claim. The juxtaposition of "ships" and "small coal and potatoes" collapses the distance between Fanny's pretensions and her probable origins.

**"Islington-town, hard by London"** — Thackeray's phrasing distances Islington from the capital proper; it is *hard by* London, not *of* London. This spatial marginality mirrors the social marginality of the coal-and-potato shop: close enough to the metropolis to supply it, but far enough from Mayfair to mark its keeper as unequivocally not-genteel.

The episode is part of the larger comedy of the regimental wives in the Brussels chapters, where Thackeray assembles a microcosm of British society in the shadow of Waterloo. Mrs. O'Dowd's gossip — candid, colourful, and casually cruel — enacts in miniature the social sorting that the novel performs at scale. Islington functions as an unanswerable social verdict: to be *of* Islington is to be outside the pale of the gentility that Fanny Magenis so strenuously claims.
