# Governess

> (008-023) "This is the new governess, Mr. Crawley," said Lady Crawley...

The governess occupies a uniquely revealing position in *Vanity Fair*: neither servant nor family member, neither working-class nor [[genteel|gentry]], she embodies the contradictions of a society that demanded gentility from women while denying them the economic independence to sustain it. Rebecca Sharp's career as a governess — and her determination to escape it — provides the novel with its primary plot engine and its sharpest lens on the precariousness of female respectability (002-023) (007-004).

## In the Novel

Rebecca Sharp arrives at Queen's Crawley as governess to Sir Pitt's two young daughters, having been recommended by her former schoolmate Amelia Sedley's family (002-023). The position is the only respectable employment available to a young woman of education without fortune, and Rebecca approaches it with frank contempt: "I am alone in the world... I have nothing to look for but what my own labour can bring me" (002-025).

The other characters' treatment of Rebecca-as-governess reveals their own social attitudes. Miss Crawley finds her "the most clever and amusing of women" — high praise that simultaneously marks the governess as entertainment rather than equal. The servants resent her ambiguous status: "I don't trust them governesses" (006-073). The men see her as prey: Sir Pitt proposes marriage to his daughters' governess, a transaction that would elevate her at a stroke from employee to mistress of the house (008-023).

Rebecca's escape from governessing — through [[marriage|marriage]] to Rawdon Crawley, chronicled in [[ch15-chapter-xv-in-which-rebeccas-husband-appears-for-a-short-time|Chapter XV]] — is treated by the novel as both triumph and tragedy, a transaction that exchanges one form of dependence for another.

## Satirical Function

The novel's treatment of the governess is doubly satirical: it exposes both the cruelty of a system that created a class of educated, genteel paupers, and the hypocrisy of a society that employed them while despising them. The "frigid correctness of the governesses" at Miss Pinkerton's (002-017) is the product of institutional cruelty, not natural disposition. Rebecca's own performance as governess — compliant, grateful, "humble, gentle" (004-044) — is a calculated act that masks furious ambition.

Thackeray, writing in the 1840s, was addressing a readership familiar with the "governess question" — a Victorian social debate about the treatment, pay, and prospects of the thousands of women employed as private teachers. The Brontës had made the governess a literary figure of pathos; Thackeray, characteristically, makes her a figure of satire who is also sympathetic.

## Historical Context

By the 1840s, the governess had become a recognised social problem. The 1841 census recorded approximately 25,000 governesses in England and Wales — educated women who could not inherit property and were barred from most professions. The Governesses' Benevolent Institution was founded in 1841, the same year *Vanity Fair* began serialisation. The governess's plight — too genteel for trade, too poor for marriage — was a standing indictment of the limitations placed on women's economic agency.

Rebecca Sharp's trajectory from governess to adventuress reflects a period anxiety: that educated, ambitious women without fortune would use their wits to escape their ordained station, threatening the social order.

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The governess who refuses to accept her lot, weaponising the very skills her education gave her — languages, music, charm — to climb out of the governess's limbo (002-023) (007-004).
- **Miss Crawley**: The wealthy woman whose own independence (she never remarried) makes her a cruel judge of other women's economic necessities (006-073).
- **Sir Pitt Crawley**: The employer who proposes to his governess — the ultimate expression of the position's sexual precariousness.
