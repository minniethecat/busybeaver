# Vauxhall Gardens

> (006-001) ...a stockbroker's family in Russell Square, who are taking walks, or lunching at the Gardens...

Vauxhall Gardens was the most famous of London's pleasure gardens in the Regency era, a place of illuminated walkways, music, entertainments, and social promenading. In *Vanity Fair*, the Gardens serve as the setting for one of the novel's earliest comic set-pieces: Jos Sedley's abortive courtship of [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]], in which the shy, vain colonial administrator becomes hopelessly drunk and humiliates himself before the woman he had intended to propose to.

## In the Novel

The Vauxhall expedition is planned by the Sedley household as an entertainment for the young ladies. [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca]], ever strategic, sees the outing as an opportunity to secure [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] as a husband (004-023). [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] arrives at Russell Square to join the party and cheerfully declares, "Gad, I'll pop the question at Vauxhall" (004-090), referring to Jos's intention to propose to Becky. The party sets off with Becky "singing like a lark, and as fresh as the morning" (005-060).

The actual evening at Vauxhall turns into farce. Jos, encouraged by George Osborne, drinks too much rack-punch and becomes incapacitated, his romantic pretensions dissolving into incoherence. The finest moment of the episode is Jos's intoxicated declaration to Becky, which ends not in a proposal but in a stupor. The chapter titled "Vauxhall" (Chapter 6) uses the gardens as a stage for Thackeray's satire of courtship rituals, male vanity, and the gap between social pretension and social reality (006-001).

## Historical Note

Vauxhall Gardens was a real and celebrated London institution, operating from the mid-17th century until 1859. In the Regency period it was at the height of its popularity, known for its illuminated groves, orchestral performances, fireworks, and the famous "dark walks" where assignations were conducted. Thackeray's depiction captures both the genuine charm of the gardens and their role as a venue for social display and romantic intrigue.
