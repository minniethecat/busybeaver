# Brussels

> (025-002) Many persons think so; and Brussels is full of fine people and ladies of fashion.

Brussels in the summer of 1815 is the glittering backdrop to one of the most dramatic sequences in *Vanity Fair*: the Duchess of Richmond's ball on the eve of the Battle of Waterloo. The city is thronged with British officers, their wives, and the fashionable society that followed the army to the Continent, creating a feverish atmosphere of gaiety and apprehension as Napoleon's forces advance.

## In the Novel

The British characters converge on Brussels as Napoleon returns from Elba and Europe mobilizes for war. [[george-osborne|George Osborne]]'s regiment is posted there, and [[amelia-sedley|Amelia]] accompanies her new husband, with [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] and [[william-dobbin|Captain Dobbin]] in attendance (028-014). The city is described as being "full of fine people and ladies of fashion" (025-002), and the great military balls provide the social highlight of the season (025-045). [[rebecca-sharp|Becky Sharp]] and [[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]] are also in Brussels, and the famous ball is the setting for George Osborne's brazen flirtation with Becky under the eyes of his wife.

The ball is interrupted by the news that Napoleon has crossed the frontier, and the officers must immediately join their regiments. The ensuing panic — Jos Sedley's hasty flight from the city, the chaos in the streets, and Amelia's terrified wait for news — is one of the novel's most powerful narrative sequences (032-001). Brussels thus serves as the emotional and dramatic pivot of the Waterloo chapters, a city of pleasure abruptly transformed into a city of fear.

## Historical Note

Brussels was indeed the social center for British society during the Hundred Days campaign of 1815. The Duchess of Richmond's ball on June 15, 1815, was a real and celebrated event, immortalized by Byron in *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*. Thackeray's depiction of the mix of gaiety and terror captures the historical reality of the Waterloo campaign as experienced by civilians.
