# Reputation

> (048-001) ...many a lady, whose reputation would be doubtful otherwise and who would have been hustled out of Vanity Fair long since, has been kept in countenance by the simple circumstance of her going to church...

Reputation — the social estimation of a person's character — functions in *Vanity Fair* as a parallel currency, as volatile and consequential as any financial asset. The narrator establishes this equivalence from the opening pages, observing that the dictionary Miss Pinkerton gave to Amelia "was the cause of her reputation and her fortune" — a sentence that binds social standing to material circumstance with startling concision (001-016). Throughout the novel, characters trade on their reputations, defend them with violence, deploy them as weapons, and watch them disintegrate through the ceaseless machinery of scandal and gossip. The theme connects directly to the novel's treatment of [[honour]] and [[genteel|genteel society]], forming a web of social valuation that governs every interaction in Vanity Fair.

## In the Novel

The architecture of reputation is built from the novel's first scene. At [[ch01-chapter-i-chiswick-mall|Miss Pinkerton's academy]], reputation is manufactured through ritual: the dictionary, the departing carriage, the carefully staged farewell — all are performances designed to project social credit onto the departing pupil (001-016). In [[ch02-chapter-ii-in-which-miss-sharp-and-miss-sedley-prepare-to-open-the-campaign|Chapter II]], Rebecca Sharp begins her lifelong campaign to construct a reputation from nothing, understanding instinctively that in a world where "she had no sort of character, nor talents, nor opinions, nor manners," the performance of virtue must substitute for its reality (009-004).

The novel traces a double arc of reputation: Rebecca's rise and catastrophic fall, and Amelia's slow, almost accidental rehabilitation. In [[ch51-chapter-li-in-which-a-charade-is-acted-which-may-or-may-not-puzzle-the-reader|Chapter LI]], the charade at Lord Steyne's house becomes the novel's most concentrated study of reputation as performance — every gesture is legible, every glance carries social meaning, and Becky's ambiguous relationship with Steyne is enacted before an audience that will later destroy her (051-001). When her reputation finally collapses, the narrator notes that it was "torn to shreds" in the drawing-rooms of Vanity Fair with the same casual cruelty that had once sustained it (054-030).

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's satire of reputation exposes the fundamental hypocrisy of a society that judges character by appearances. The narrator's observation about church attendance — that "many a lady, whose reputation would be doubtful otherwise" is saved by the "simple circumstance of her going to church" — strips religious observance of any spiritual content and reduces it to reputation management (048-001). The passage in Chapter XLVIII where women "take their degree in reputation" through social performance treats moral standing as something conferred by collective opinion rather than earned through conduct (048-002).

The novel's supreme satirical insight is that reputation is always a fiction, a story told by others over which the individual has limited control. George Osborne's "prodigious" reputation among the young officers of his regiment is based on glamour, not substance — he is admired for qualities he does not possess, while Dobbin, who possesses every virtue George lacks, is socially invisible (013-003). Lord Steyne, "of the very highest reputation in Vanity Fair," is in fact the novel's most morally corrupt character, his social standing a protection for his private vices (048-024). Reputation, Thackeray insists, measures social power, not moral worth.

## Historical Context

Regency society operated through an elaborate code of reputation that governed every aspect of public and private life. A woman's reputation was her only non-transferable asset: a sexual scandal could destroy not only her marriage prospects but her family's social standing across generations. The double standard was absolute — George Osborne's gambling debts and affairs damaged his reputation far less than Rebecca's rumoured relationship with Lord Steyne damaged hers, simply because she was a woman and his social inferior.

The novel was published in 1847-48, at a moment when Victorian domestic ideology was hardening the Regency's more fluid social codes into rigid doctrine. Thackeray's sceptical treatment of reputation can be read as a critique of this emerging moral absolutism, a reminder — drawn from the recent Regency past — that what passes for virtue is often merely successful performance. His narrator, who refuses to moralise while exposing every moral pretence, embodies this historically situated critical perspective.

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's great reputation-builder and reputation-destroyer, whose social credit rises to dazzling heights before collapsing into permanent disgrace (009-004) (054-030).
- **Amelia Sedley**: A woman whose spotless reputation is both her only asset and her prison — her very virtue prevents her from claiming the happiness Dobbin offers (001-016) (064-022).
- **Lord Steyne**: The supreme example of reputation as protection for vice: "of the very highest reputation in Vanity Fair" and its most corrupt inhabitant (048-024).
- **George Osborne**: A man whose "prodigious" reputation among his peers is a fiction built on surface glamour, masking his essential mediocrity and moral weakness (013-003).
