# Chiswick

Chiswick is a village on the Thames west of London, known in Vanity Fair almost exclusively as the location of Miss Pinkerton's academy for young ladies on Chiswick Mall. The novel opens at the great iron gate of this establishment, and the academy functions as the narrative's true starting point — the place from which [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]] and [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] are launched into the world.

## Miss Pinkerton's Academy

The academy dominates Chiswick in the novel's geography. Thackeray introduces it with precise, almost painterly detail: "there drove up to the great iron gate of Miss Pinkerton's academy for young ladies, on Chiswick Mall, a large family coach, with two fat horses in blazing harness, driven by a fat coachman in a three-cornered hat and wig" [001-001]. The letter-head of the establishment reads "The Mall, Chiswick" [001-010], anchoring it in the real topography of the Thames-side village.

Within its walls, the young ladies read [[samuel-johnson|Dr. Johnson]]'s *Cecilia* and receive instruction in dancing, drawing, and deportment. Rebecca Sharp arrived at Chiswick at age seventeen "and was bound over as an articled pupil" [002-013], her father having secured the position through his connection to "the Vicar of Chiswick, the Reverend Mr. Flowerdew" [002-014]. She taught French to the other pupils while receiving her own education — an arrangement that bred the resentment that would erupt in her famous act of rebellion.

The academy is presided over by the formidable Miss Pinkerton and her meek sister [[jemima-pinkerton|Jemima Pinkerton]], whose "little red nose" [001-001] peeps from the drawing-room window in the novel's opening paragraph.

## Becky's Farewell to Chiswick

Chiswick is the stage for one of the novel's most memorable early scenes: Rebecca's defiant rejection of the academy. When presented with Johnson's *Dixonary* as a parting gift — a book Miss Pinkerton intended as a mark of special favour — "the Dixonary, flying over the pavement of the little garden, fell at length at the feet of the astonished Miss Jemima" [002-001]. Becky's parting words, "thank God, I'm out of Chiswick" [002-001], establish her character in a single stroke: proud, resentful of condescension, and determined to leave behind a world that has slighted her.

Amelia's departure from Chiswick is, by contrast, sentimental and tearful. She parts from "her twelve dearest friends at Chiswick Mall" [004-076], leaving behind a world where she has been universally beloved. The contrast between the two girls' farewells — one bitter, one sweet — is the novel's first sketch of its central opposition.

## The Chiswick Letters

Even after the main action moves away, Chiswick maintains a ghostly presence through correspondence. Amelia writes to "Miss Pinkerton, The Mall, Chiswick" [011-009], and letters between former pupils circulate, carrying news and gossip. Sir Pitt Crawley's second wife, Rose Dawson, was herself educated at Chiswick — "we used to read Cecilia at Chiswick" [008-005], she recalls, though she finds Queen's Crawley "something like those at odious Chiswick" [008-008], suggesting the academy's gentility did not impress everyone.

## Historical Context

Chiswick in the Regency period was a fashionable Thames-side village, known for its villas, gardens, and fresh air — an attractive location for a boarding academy catering to the daughters of the professional and merchant classes. Chiswick Mall, a grand riverside terrace of eighteenth-century houses, still stands today. The real Chiswick was home to artists and writers, including William Hogarth (whose house still survives) and later Thackeray himself, who knew the area intimately.
