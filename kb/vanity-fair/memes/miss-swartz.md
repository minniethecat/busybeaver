# Miss Swartz

Miss Swartz is the wealthy "woolly-haired mulatto" heiress from St. Kitts who appears briefly but unforgettably in the novel's opening chapters. A parlour-boarder at [[miss-pinkerton|Miss Pinkerton]]'s academy alongside [[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]], she is introduced with the casual cruelty of Thackeray's racial language — "hysterical YOOPS" (001-033) — yet her fortune of "many, many thousands a year" makes her, in the logic of [[vanity|Vanity Fair]], a person of the highest consequence. The contradiction between her social treatment (as a racial curiosity) and her economic power (as an heiress) is one of the novel's sharpest satirical points.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Swartz appears in two key contexts: as Amelia's schoolfellow at Chiswick Mall, where her emotional, demonstrative nature is played for comedy (001-025), and later as a potential bride for [[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]] — a scheme promoted by [[george-osborne|George Osborne]] and his sisters, who see her fortune as the perfect solution to Jos's idle bachelorhood (021-001). The Sedley-Osborne circle fawns over her at dinner, plying her with champagne and compliments, while privately mocking her "woolly hair" and dark complexion. She is, in miniature, the novel's thesis: in Vanity Fair, [[money|money]] bleaches every stain but cannot quite erase the sneer.

## Relationships

- **[[amelia-sedley|Amelia Sedley]]**: Schoolfellow and friend; Amelia feels protective of her
- **[[jos-sedley|Jos Sedley]]**: Rumoured suitor, encouraged by George Osborne's matchmaking
- **[[george-osborne|George Osborne]]**: Eager promoter of the Swartz-Sedley match
- **[[miss-pinkerton|Miss Pinkerton]]**: Her schoolmistress, who values her fees if not her person

## Character Analysis

Thackeray's portrayal of Miss Swartz is uncomfortable for modern readers and was meant to be uncomfortable for his original audience too — though for different reasons. The narrator's racial language ("woolly-haired," "mulatto," the mockery of her accent) reflects the casual bigotry of Regency England, but the satire cuts both ways: the white characters who sneer at Miss Swartz are also the ones who court her for her money. She is presented sentimentally — generous, affectionate, weeping easily — but also as a figure whose fortune renders her race, in the market logic of the novel, almost irrelevant. She is what [[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] might have been with money and without cunning.
