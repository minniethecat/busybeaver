# Miss Pinkerton

> (001-011) I have the honour and happiness of presenting Miss Amelia Sedley to her parents...

Miss Barbara Pinkerton, the "Semiramis of Hammersmith," opens *Vanity Fair* and sets its satirical tone. Her academy for young ladies is the novel's first image of institutional hypocrisy — a place where the forms of gentility are taught while the substance is ignored, where social rank determines treatment, and where the headmistress's "frigid correctness" (002-017) masks a regime of petty tyranny. She appears only in the opening chapters, but her presence haunts everything that follows: the world she represents — snobbish, cruel, and self-deceiving — is the world the novel exists to expose.

## Background

Miss Pinkerton presides over an academy on Chiswick Mall, Hammersmith, where she educates the daughters of the gentry and merchant classes. Her establishment advertises itself under "the patronage of the admirable Mrs. Chapone" (001-013) — a detail that perfectly captures her pretensions. She is assisted by her younger sister Jemima, whom she bullies, and her curriculum consists of accomplishments (music, embroidery, deportment) rather than knowledge.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Pinkerton appears in the novel's opening chapters, where she presides over Amelia Sedley's departure from the academy. Her treatment of the two departing pupils — Amelia, the wealthy favourite, is showered with praise and a Johnson's Dictionary; Rebecca, the charity pupil, is sent off with barely concealed relief — establishes the novel's moral world in miniature. The academy is Vanity Fair in embryo: a place where money and status determine everything, dressed in the language of virtue and improvement.

## Relationships

- **Amelia Sedley**: Her star pupil, beloved because her father is wealthy and pays his bills on time. Miss Pinkerton's affection for Amelia is entirely a function of the Sedleys' social standing.
- **Rebecca Sharp**: Her charity pupil and nemesis. Miss Pinkerton despises Rebecca because Rebecca refuses to perform the gratitude expected of a charity case — and because Rebecca is cleverer than she is.
- **Jemima Pinkerton**: Her sister and assistant. Miss Pinkerton's treatment of Jemima is the novel's first demonstration of how power operates among women: the strong bully the weak, and the weak have no recourse.

## Character Analysis

Miss Pinkerton is the novel's introductory satire: a person whose entire identity is a set of rules, and who uses those rules to dominate everyone in her power. She is what Amelia might become if Amelia had no warmth, and what Rebecca might become if Rebecca had no intelligence. Her function is to establish the novel's satirical method — the exposure of hypocrisy through the juxtaposition of performance and reality — and she performs it with memorable efficiency.
