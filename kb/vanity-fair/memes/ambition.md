# Ambition

> (003-026) What causes respectable parents to take up their carpets, set their houses topsy-turvy, and to pay visits, and get into debt, but the noble ambition of matrimony?

Ambition is the engine of *Vanity Fair* — the restless, consuming desire for wealth, status, and social elevation that drives every major character's actions and determines every major plot turn. Thackeray presents ambition not as a personal vice but as the atmospheric condition of Regency society: a force so universal that even the novel's most sympathetic characters are shaped by it, and so destructive that even its apparent successes are failures. From the "noble ambition of matrimony" that sends respectable parents into debt (003-026) to the "balked ambition" that poisons Mr. Osborne's love for his son (024-035), ambition appears in the novel as both the motive force of social life and the cause of its moral ruin. The concept is inseparable from [[fortune]] and [[social-climbing|social climbing]]: ambition is the desire, fortune the object, and social climbing the method.

## In the Novel

Ambition in *Vanity Fair* is gendered. For women, it is channelled through marriage — the only legitimate avenue of advancement available to them. The narrator's catalogue of what "respectable parents" will do for "the noble ambition of matrimony" (003-026) is both comic and devastating: the word "noble" is the novel's signature ironic intensifier, transforming the sordid business of husband-hunting into a chivalric quest. Becky Sharp, who has no parents to arrange her advancement, must arrange it herself, and her entire career — the pursuit of Jos Sedley, the cultivation of Miss Crawley, the marriage to Rawdon, the campaign for Lord Steyne's patronage — is a single continuous expression of ambition.

For men, ambition is expressed through profession, [[speculation|speculation]], and display. George Osborne's ambition is the ambition of style: he wants to be seen as a gentleman of fashion, and his tragedy is that he succeeds — at the cost of everything else. His father's ambition is the ambition of accumulation: the "great dream of ambition" that enters old Mr. Osborne's mind (021-001) is the dream of dynastic wealth, of a fortune so large that it becomes a family name. Rawdon Crawley's ambition — which barely deserves the name — is the ambition of survival: to live well on credit, to maintain the appearance of a gentleman, to avoid the consequences of his own indolence.

[[ch15-chapter-xv-in-which-rebeccas-husband-appears-for-a-short-time|Chapter XV]] is the novel's pivotal study of ambition's costs. Becky marries Rawdon believing she has secured Miss Crawley's fortune; the revelation that the fortune is lost is the novel's first demonstration that ambition, however brilliantly executed, is always vulnerable to chance.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's treatment of ambition is distinctive in its refusal to moralise. Unlike the Victorian novelists who punish ambitious characters (and reward the contented), Thackeray presents ambition as a rational response to a society in which money and status determine everything. Becky Sharp is not wicked for wanting more than she was born with; she is clear-sighted. The novel's satire is directed not at ambition itself but at a social order that makes ambition both necessary and corrupting.

The narrator's catalogue of what ambition drives people to — "wife, children, love, ambition, vanity" (047-015) — is delivered in a tone of exhausted recognition rather than moral condemnation. The list places ambition among the universal human motives, a fact of life rather than a sin. The novel's true satirical target is the hypocrisy of those who pretend they have no ambition while scheming as ruthlessly as anyone: Mrs. Bute Crawley, Mr. Pitt Crawley, the entire tribe of legacy-hunters who disguise their ambition as [[family|family feeling]].

## Historical Context

The Regency period was an era of unprecedented social mobility. The Napoleonic Wars had created fortunes for contractors, speculators, and military men; the industrial revolution was generating new wealth that threatened the old aristocratic order; and the marriage market was the primary mechanism by which new money bought old names. Ambition in this context was not merely personal but structural — the entire society was organised around the pursuit of advancement.

Thackeray, writing in the 1840s, was the chronicler of a generation that had seen ambition's rewards and its costs. The railway speculation of the 1840s, the colonial fortunes made and lost in India, the political careers built and destroyed by patronage — all of these are reflected in the novel's treatment of ambition as both the energy of modern life and its moral danger. The Reform Bill of 1832, which expanded the franchise and redistributed parliamentary seats, was the political expression of the same forces the novel examines: the ambition of the middle classes to share in the power that had been the aristocracy's monopoly.

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's supreme study of ambition — a woman whose intelligence, charm, and ruthlessness would, in a just world, make her a success, and whose tragedy is that the world is not just (003-026). Her ambition is rational, her methods are cynical, and her ultimate fate is the novel's darkest comment on ambition's rewards.
- **George Osborne**: The ambition of appearance — a man who wants to be seen as a gentleman more than he wants to be one, and whose ambitions destroy his marriage before it begins (021-001).
- **Rawdon Crawley**: The ambition of indolence — a man who wants the rewards of status without the effort, and who discovers too late that even indolence requires a patron.
