# Patronage

> (013-009) Pity and patronage! I should rather have had an ounce of either when I was alone in the world!

Patronage is the invisible infrastructure of *Vanity Fair* — the system by which the powerful dispense favour, preferment, and protection to their dependents in exchange for loyalty, entertainment, and the performance of gratitude. From Miss Pinkerton's academy, where "the patronage of the admirable Mrs. Chapone" is invoked as an intellectual credential (001-013), to Lord Steyne's cynical sponsorship of Becky Sharp in the highest circles of London society (048-007), the novel traces the operations of a social economy in which nothing is given freely and every favour incurs a debt. The concept is inseparable from [[genteel|gentility]] and [[fortune]]: patronage is the mechanism by which the first is conferred and the second is pursued. Rebecca's trajectory from [[governess|governess]] to social climber is the novel's most vivid study of the system's operations and limits.

## In the Novel

The novel's architecture is built on patronage relationships. Miss Crawley is the central patron of the first half: her fortune is the object of universal scheming, and her favour — capricious, demanding, tyrannical — is the prize for which the entire Crawley family competes. Becky Sharp's career as Miss Crawley's companion is a study in the art of patronage: she amuses, she flatters, she performs the role of grateful dependent with such skill that she becomes indispensable (010-012).

Her reward — Miss Crawley's promise of social sponsorship — is the novel's sharpest depiction of patronage's double nature: the gift is real, but the condition of perpetual gratitude is the price. When Becky marries Rawdon without Miss Crawley's permission, the patronage is withdrawn in an instant, revealing the relationship's true character as control rather than affection (015-047).

In [[ch48-chapter-xlviii-in-which-the-reader-is-introduced-to-the-very-best-of-company|Chapter XLVIII]], the novel moves patronage to its highest level. Lord Steyne's "protection" of Becky Sharp (052-023) is the aristocratic version of Miss Crawley's middle-class patronage: more powerful, more dangerous, and ultimately more corrupting. The "great credit" enjoyed by those under Lord Steyne's patronage (055-005) is a euphemism for what the novel implies but cannot state directly — that patronage at this level is indistinguishable from procurement.

[[ch07-chapter-vii-crawley-of-queens-crawley|Chapter VII]] introduces the Crawley family's economy of patronage at Queen's Crawley, where Sir Pitt dispenses livings, places, and favour to his dependents with the casual brutality of a man who has never known what it is to need a patron himself (007-004). The scene's contrast with Becky's position — the governess who must accept "patronage" from everyone above her — defines the novel's social geometry.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's treatment of patronage is a sustained exposure of the fiction that British society was a meritocracy. Every character's advancement in the novel — professional, social, marital — depends on the favour of someone richer or more powerful. The "patronage" that Mrs. Bute Crawley extends to Becky is a parody of genuine kindness: it is the right to be condescended to by one's social superiors (015-047).

The novel's bitterest insight is that patronage corrupts both parties. The patron is corrupted by the flattery that sustains the relationship — Miss Crawley's vanity is fed by the very gratitude she demands. The dependent is corrupted by the performance of gratitude — Becky's entire social identity is a construction designed to please patrons. When Amelia experiences the "patronage" of her richer sister-in-law after her widowhood, the word carries its full weight of humiliation: to be patronised is to be reduced, not elevated (013-009).

## Historical Context

Patronage was the organising principle of Regency public life. Seats in Parliament, commissions in the Army, livings in the Church, places in the civil service — all were distributed through networks of personal connection rather than formal qualification. The system was defended as a guarantee of social stability and attacked as institutionalised corruption. The Reform agitation that culminated in the 1832 Reform Act was in part a campaign against patronage, and Thackeray's novel — written in the 1840s — records a society in which the old system was crumbling but not yet replaced.

The novel's treatment of Lord Steyne reflects the period's ambivalence about aristocratic patronage. Steyne is both a figure of immense worldly power — his influence reaches into the Cabinet, the Church, the diplomatic corps — and a figure of moral degradation. Patronage in *Vanity Fair* is always shadowed by the question of what, exactly, the patron expects in return.

## Related Characters

- **Miss Crawley**: The novel's archetypal patron — wealthy, capricious, and morally complacent, she dispenses favour as a form of self-entertainment and withdraws it as a form of punishment (010-012) (015-047).
- **Lord Steyne**: Patronage at its most powerful and most corrupt — his "protection" of Becky is the novel's darkest transaction (052-023) (055-005).
- **Sir Pitt Crawley**: The patriarchal patron whose favour is dispensed with feudal indifference — a relic of an older patronage system already being replaced by the cash nexus (007-004).
- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's supreme operator of patronage — she understands instinctively that the dependent's art is to make the patron feel not used but admired, and she practises this art on everyone from Sir Pitt to the King (010-012).
