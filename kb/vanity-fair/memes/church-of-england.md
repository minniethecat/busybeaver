# Church of England

> (011-050) ...the wife of a clergyman of the Church of England. By Jove!

The Church of England is the established national church, whose clergy and institutions appear throughout *Vanity Fair* as part of the novel's comprehensive satire of Regency society. From the ambitious rectors to the sycophantic domestic chaplains and the worldly bishops, the Church in Thackeray's hands is less a spiritual institution than another arena of Vanity Fair — a career path for younger sons, a source of social status, and a platform for worldly ambition.

## Role in the Novel

The Church of England appears in the novel primarily through its clerical characters and their families. [[mrs-bute-crawley|Mrs. Bute Crawley]], the rector's wife, is perhaps its most vivid representative: a formidable woman who uses her husband's clerical position as a base for family intrigue and social maneuvering (011-050). Her energy and cunning are deployed entirely in the service of securing [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley]]'s inheritance, and the fact that she is "the wife of a clergyman of the Church of England" is invoked as a badge of respectability even as her conduct belies any religious scruple.

The novel's other clerical figures reinforce this satirical portrait. The elder [[pitt-crawley|Pitt Crawley]] is a clergyman whose piety is inseparable from his priggishness and ambition. Domestic chaplains and tutors flatter the wealthy in hopes of preferment. In the background, the bishops sit in the House of Lords as part of the institutional alliance between Church and State. The novel's treatment of the Church reflects Thackeray's broader skepticism about institutional religion in an age when clerical livings were often treated as family property and religious vocation was indistinguishable from worldly advancement (032-029).

## Historical Note

The Church of England in the Regency period was intimately connected with the state and the landowning classes. Many livings were in the gift of local landowners, and the clergy were often drawn from the gentry. Pluralism (holding multiple livings simultaneously) and non-residence were common abuses. Thackeray's satirical portrait reflects the reality of a church that was more a pillar of the social order than a source of spiritual renewal.
