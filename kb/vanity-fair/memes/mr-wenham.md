# Mr. Wenham

Mr. Wenham is [[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]'s lawyer, wit, and confidential man of business — the man who handles the marquess's dirtiest work while maintaining the blandest of smiles. Where Steyne is terrifying, Wenham is merely unnerving: a man whose deferential politeness conceals an absolute readiness to do whatever his patron requires. He is not a villain in his own right but an instrument of aristocratic power, and the novel treats him with the particular contempt it reserves for those who serve corrupt masters with intelligence and without conscience.

> [055-069] "Dammy, indeed," said Mr. Wenham with the most agreeable smile; "still, look at the matter as a man of the world — as an honest man — and see if you have not been in the wrong."

## Background

Wenham is introduced as "the wit and lawyer, Lord Steyne's right-hand man" [051-007], a position that combines legal expertise, social charm, and utter dependability. He is a member of parliament and holds a seat at Lord Steyne's dinner table — a mark of his dual role as both political operative and social factotum. The novel briefly sketches his origins: "Mr. Wenham himself was a staunch old True Blue Tory, and his father a small coal-merchant in the north of England" [051-022]. This detail is characteristic of Thackeray's method: Wenham's humble origins make his devotion to the aristocratic Steyne neither admirable nor pitiable, but simply mercenary. He has climbed by making himself indispensable, and indispensability in Steyne's world means willingness to do what gentlemen cannot.

His wife, Mrs. Wenham, exists in the novel only as a convenient excuse — her "headaches" provide the alibi for why the Wenhams were not present at the supper party that triggered Rawdon's discovery [055-078]. Whether Mrs. Wenham's headaches are real or invented is left deliberately ambiguous.

## Role in the Novel

Wenham appears in a tight sequence of chapters — 51, 53, and 55 — that form the novel's dramatic climax: the unraveling of the Rawdon-Steyne-Becky triangle.

In chapter 51, he is first shown as a social operator, praising Becky Sharp around London after Lady Fitz-Willis has endorsed her [051-007]. Unlike the blundering Mr. Wagg, Wenham is "much more prudent in his behaviour and opinions" — he "never showed any sort of hostility to the new favourite, but pursued her with stealthy kindnesses and a sly and deferential politeness which somehow made Becky more uneasy than other people's overt hostilities" [051-022].

At the end of the same chapter, Wenham proposes walking Rawdon Crawley home from Gaunt House after the dinner party, offering him a cigar [051-070]. This apparently friendly gesture turns sinister: during the walk, bailiffs arrest Rawdon for debt. When Rawdon desperately asks, "Lend me a hundred, Wenham, for God's sake," Wenham replies, "I've not got ten pounds in the world" and walks away [051-076-077], leaving Rawdon to be taken to the spunging-house.

In chapter 53, Rawdon, imprisoned, connects the dots: "Who had put him there? Wenham had walked with him" [053-023]. The implication is clear — Wenham deliberately lured Rawdon away from the carriage so the bailiffs could seize him, likely on Lord Steyne's orders.

In chapter 55, Wenham arrives at Rawdon's club as Lord Steyne's emissary, charged with preventing a duel. His performance is a masterpiece of diplomatic manipulation. He smiles blandly, invokes the honour of both parties, insists on Becky's innocence ("as innocent as my wife, Mrs. Wenham" [055-071]), and offers Rawdon the governorship of Coventry Island as a bribe to go away quietly. When Rawdon calls him a liar, "Mr. Wenham turned deadly pale" [055-082], but he recovers with the help of [[captain-macmurdo|Captain Macmurdo]], who dismisses him with: "Mr. Wenham ain't a fighting man; and quite right, too" [055-086]. The chapter ends with Macmurdo accepting a thousand-pound note from Wenham — the price of peace — while refusing to let the money reach Lord Steyne directly [055-092].

## Relationships

- **[[lord-steyne|Lord Steyne]]**: His patron and master. Wenham is described as Steyne's "vizier and chief confidential servant" [051-022], handling the legal, political, and social dirty work that Steyne cannot perform himself.
- **[[rawdon-crawley|Rawdon Crawley]]**: The man Wenham betrays. Their cordial walk from Gaunt House ends with Rawdon in a spunging-house and Wenham walking away. Later, Wenham attempts to manipulate Rawdon into accepting a bribe and abandoning his grievances.
- **[[captain-macmurdo|Captain Macmurdo]]**: Rawdon's second, who treats Wenham with soldierly contempt but ultimately does business with him.
- **[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]]**: Wenham publicly praises Becky at Steyne's direction, but his "sly and deferential politeness" makes her uneasy [051-022] — she recognises a fellow performer.

## Character Analysis

Wenham is Thackeray's portrait of the professional servant of power: intelligent, articulate, and entirely without independence. His fluency in Parliament [055-080] is the same fluency he deploys to persuade Rawdon that his wife is innocent and his patron blameless — words are instruments, not vehicles of truth.

His name is characteristic of the novel's satirical method. "Wenham" is a homophone for "when" — he is the man who is always there *when* Lord Steyne needs something done, whether it is praising a new favourite, arranging an arrest, or buying off an inconvenient husband. He has no existence independent of his patron's requirements.

The novel distinguishes Wenham from the other members of Steyne's circle. Mr. Wagg is a buffoon; the other guests are openly disrespectful. Wenham alone maintains a prudent, diplomatic facade — and this makes him, in some ways, more dangerous. He is the one Steyne trusts with real work. As Macmurdo observes, he is "not a fighting man" [055-086]; his weapons are smiles, parliamentary oratory, and the thousand-pound note passed discreetly in an envelope.
