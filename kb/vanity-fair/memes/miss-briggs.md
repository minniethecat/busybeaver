# Miss Briggs

Miss Briggs, often called simply "Briggs," is [[miss-crawley|Miss Crawley's]] paid companion — a position that makes her at once a member of the household and a servant, a confidante and a target. She is timid, sentimental, and desperately aware that her livelihood depends on the whims of a tyrannical old woman. In the novel's taxonomy of female dependency, Miss Briggs occupies the narrowest niche: a gentlewoman without fortune, too refined for work, too poor for independence.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Briggs appears throughout the novel as a barometer of Miss Crawley's moods. When the old lady is pleased, Briggs is "dear Briggs" and "my good Briggs"; when displeased, she is "that fool Briggs" (011-026). Her function is to demonstrate the cost of dependency — the daily humiliation, the suppressed resentment, the performance of gratitude that the dependent must maintain.

[[rebecca-sharp|Rebecca Sharp]] sees through Briggs instantly and manipulates her with ease. Briggs is everything Rebecca refuses to become: a woman who has accepted her subordinate place and survived by making herself invisible. In the novel's moral economy, Briggs's goodness is real but powerless — a quality the novel respects without admiring.
