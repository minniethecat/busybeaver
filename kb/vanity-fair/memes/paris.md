## Overview

Paris in *Vanity Fair* is the stage for Rebecca Sharp's dazzling social ascent during the winter of 1815. Fresh from Waterloo, British society — soldiers, aristocrats, adventurers, and fortune-hunters — descended upon the French capital in a fever of celebration and consumption. For Rebecca and Rawdon Crawley, Paris offered a theatre in which to perform their most brilliant act before the debts closed in.

Thackeray's Paris is simultaneously a place of genuine cosmopolitan glamour and a gilded trap. The "gayest among the gay company" [034-009] cannot outrun creditors indefinitely, and the "idle and pleasant" [036-012] life proves, in the end, to be only an interlude.

## Description

The Paris of 1815 was the capital of the newly restored Bourbon monarchy, but Thackeray's interest lies not in its politics but in its society. "All the world was at Paris during this famous winter" [034-075] — English, French, and assorted Europeans mingled in a round of balls, theatres, and gambling houses. The "little Paris theatres" [008-058] and the "mania for play" [036-009] define the city's character as a place of pleasure and risk.

Rebecca's "success in Paris was remarkable" [034-073]. She spoke French with "purity and a Parisian accent" [002-013], a skill that set her apart from the provincial English. The French aristocracy received her: "all the French ladies voted her charming" [034-073]. Her salon became a gathering-place for the fashionable, and her reputation as a wit and beauty spread through the city.

## Events

### The Winter of 1815

After Waterloo, Rawdon and Rebecca established themselves in Paris "in great splendour and gaiety" [034-072]. The winter of 1815 was a unique moment: the wars were over, the allied armies occupied the city, and English money flooded into Parisian establishments. Rebecca, "flaunting at Paris" [034-009], became "the gayest among the gay company" that thronged the capital.

### Life in the Rue de Rivoli

The Crawleys "lived very happily and comfortably at Paris" [036-004] in rooms near the Rue de Rivoli. Rebecca "took a very smart and leading position in the fashion of the French capital" [036-005]. Rawdon, less at ease in French society, occupied himself with the gambling tables that "were open at that time in Paris" [036-009].

### The Creditors Close In

The idyll could not last. The "creditors of the Colonel at Paris" [036-013] began to press, and the Crawleys' financial recklessness caught up with them. The life "as idle and pleasant as their life at Paris was" [036-012] proved to be "after all only an idle and pleasant" interlude before the inevitable flight — a pattern that would repeat throughout their married life.

### The Army's March

Paris appears in the novel's military geography as well. The army was "marching to Paris" [033-007], and letters came "from Paris, whither the army was marching" [033-009]. For the soldiers, Paris represented the triumphant conclusion of the campaign — the destination toward which all the bloodshed of Waterloo had been directed.

## Historical Context

Paris in 1815 was a city in transition. The fall of Napoleon and the restoration of Louis XVIII brought a flood of British visitors — soldiers from Wellington's army, their wives and families, and the usual train of speculators and adventurers who follow in the wake of victory. The occupation of Paris by allied troops lasted until 1818, and during this period the English presence in the city was at its nineteenth-century peak.

Thackeray, who knew Paris well from his own youthful residence there, captures both the glamour and the seediness of the Anglo-French social scene. The gambling houses, the cheap theatres, the lodging-house keepers, and the creditors all form part of the Parisian ecosystem that the novel depicts with characteristic satirical precision.
