## Overview

Surrey appears in *Vanity Fair* in two sharply contrasted registers: as the location of the West India heiress Miss Swartz's "mansion in Surrey" [020-028], marking her as a person of substantial property; and as the destination of the "Surrey hounds" [046-011], offered as a treat to young Georgy Osborne by a family friend. Between these two references lies the full range of Vanity Fair society — from the new money of colonial trade to the old pursuits of the county gentry.

## Description

Thackeray does not describe Surrey directly; he invokes it as a social signifier. A mansion in Surrey, together with "a house in Portland Place" [020-028], signals Miss Swartz's wealth — land in the country, a townhouse in London, the classic formula of English prosperity. The name of the county does descriptive work: it places the owner among the respectable moneyed classes for whom Surrey, with its proximity to London and its pleasant landscape of downs and woodland, was the natural choice for a country seat.

The "Surrey hounds" [046-011] carry different associations — the traditional English sport of fox-hunting, the county as a landscape of field and hedgerow rather than villa and garden. The offer to "take him out with the Surrey hounds" [046-011] is a gesture toward young Georgy's future as a gentleman.

## Events

### The West India Heiress

George Osborne and his sisters meet the "rich West India heiress" [020-028] at old Hulker's house in Devonshire Place. Miss Rhoda Swartz "had a mansion in Surrey, and a house in Portland Place" [020-028], properties that establish her as a desirable match — her "I don't know how many plantations in the West Indies; a deal of money in the funds; and three stars to her name in the East India stockholders' list" [020-028] are announced with the same listing impulse. The Misses Osborne make "the most cordial advances to her" [020-028], the Surrey mansion looming large in their calculations.

### The Surrey Hounds

During Georgy Osborne's visits to his aunt Miss Dobbin, young George reports on the gentlemen who entertain him. "Mr. Dobbin, who arrived on the beautiful bay horse in the afternoon," promised "to show him the Tower of London and take him out with the Surrey hounds" [046-011]. The offer is one of many kindnesses shown to the boy by those who remember his father, and the county name lends the promise a flavour of authentic English country life — a world away from the "twopenny gentility" of suburban Roehampton.

## Historical Context

Surrey in the Regency period was a county of contrasts. Its northern edge, closest to London, was increasingly the site of City merchants' villas and country houses — the "Surrey mansion" pattern that Miss Swartz's property exemplifies. Further south, the county retained its agricultural character, with hunting country and established landed estates.

The Surrey Union Hunt, established in the early nineteenth century, hunted the county's southern downs. The reference to "Surrey hounds" reflects the real hunting culture of the period, though the specific pack Thackeray has in mind is less important than the social meaning of the invitation: hunting was the pursuit of gentlemen, and to offer a day with the hounds was to offer initiation into that world.
