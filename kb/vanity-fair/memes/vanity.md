# Vanity

> (051-003) It is all vanity to be sure, but who will not own to liking a little of it?

Vanity is the title concept of *Vanity Fair* itself — the "fair" being the great marketplace of human pretension where characters display themselves, compete for admiration, and measure their worth by the estimation of others. Derived from the biblical *vanitas* tradition (Ecclesiastes: "Vanity of vanities; all is vanity"), the term names both a moral failing and, in Thackeray's treatment, the universal condition of social life. The narrator introduces the theme through Miss Pinkerton's "pompous vanity" (002-017) and extends it across every social stratum: from the vanity of military glory (033-012) to the personal vanity that drives the novel's most intimate scenes of self-deception. The concept is inseparable from [[reputation]] and [[ambition]] — vanity is the engine, reputation the currency, ambition the direction.

## In the Novel

The novel announces its central theme before any character speaks, in the resonant title drawn from John Bunyan's *The Pilgrim's Progress*: "Vanity Fair" is the town where all human goods — "houses, lands, trades, places, honours, preferments, titles, countries, kingdoms, lusts, pleasures" — are bought and sold. Thackeray secularises Bunyan's allegory, relocating the fair from the spiritual journey to the drawing-rooms of Regency England. From Miss Pinkerton's academy — where "the pompous vanity of the old schoolmistress" sets the novel's satirical tone (002-017) — to the ballrooms of Brussels and the gaming-tables of the German courts, every scene is a stall in the great fair.

Vanity in the novel is democratic in its distribution: it afflicts the rich and the poor, the virtuous and the vicious, the self-aware and the self-deluded alike. Mr. Osborne's "extreme vanity" in his son George is at once paternal love and proprietorial pride — George is not a child to be loved but an asset to be displayed (003-030). Jos Sedley's vanity about his appearance at Vauxhall — his absurd self-regard in his new waistcoat — makes him simultaneously ridiculous and pitiable (006-015). Even Becky Sharp, the novel's most clear-eyed character, is subject to "a little more personal vanity" than she would admit (060-014). The [[ch06-chapter-vi-vauxhall|Vauxhall chapter]] is a study in collective vanity: every character performing for an audience that is itself performing.

The narrator's direct addresses to the reader extend the theme beyond the fiction: "How much vanity lurks in our warmest regard for others, and how selfish our love is" (035-018). The observation implicates not only the characters but the reader, who is no less a stallholder in Vanity Fair.

## Satirical Function

Thackeray's treatment of vanity is distinctive in its refusal of moral superiority. Unlike the satirist who stands above his targets, the *Vanity Fair* narrator repeatedly includes himself — and his reader — in the indictment. "It is all vanity to be sure," he concedes, "but who will not own to liking a little of it?" (051-003). This complicity is the novel's satirical signature: vanity is not a sin committed by the wicked against the virtuous but the atmospheric condition of social existence.

The [[ch10-chapter-x-miss-sharp-begins-to-make-friends|tenth chapter]] crystallises the satire. Miss Crawley's fondness for Becky Sharp is exposed as vanity of a refined sort — the pleasure of displaying a clever protégée — while Becky's performance of humility is vanity in its most calculating form (010-012). The scene is a closed economy of mutual flattery in which no one's motives are pure. Thackeray's point is not that vanity is avoidable but that honesty about it is the beginning of [[sentiment|wisdom]] — a wisdom the novel's most sympathetic characters (Dobbin, Amelia) conspicuously lack.

## Historical Context

Vanity as a literary theme descends from the biblical Ecclesiastes through medieval and Renaissance *contemptus mundi* traditions, but Thackeray's innovation was to merge this moral vocabulary with the novel of manners. The Regency period in which the novel is set was an era of conspicuous consumption, military display, and social mobility — conditions that made vanity both more visible and more democratically accessible. The industrial revolution had created new fortunes (the Osbornes); the Napoleonic Wars had created new heroes (the Dobbins, the O'Dowds); and the old aristocracy (the Crawleys, the Steynes) clung to the forms of pre-eminence even as its substance eroded.

Thackeray, writing in the 1840s, was addressing a Victorian readership that prided itself on moral seriousness — the very quality the novel's treatment of vanity calls into question. By making vanity universal rather than particular, the novel undermines the Victorian assumption that moral improvement is possible. The fair continues; only the stalls change hands.

## Related Characters

- **Rebecca Sharp**: The novel's most accomplished performer, whose vanity is the one weakness in her otherwise faultless social intelligence (060-014). Her ability to manipulate others' vanity is her great gift; her susceptibility to her own is her great vulnerability.
- **George Osborne**: Vanity personified — a man whose entire identity is constructed from the admiration of others. His "absurd vanity in fancying every woman whom he meets is in love with him" (036-012) is both comic and destructive.
- **Jos Sedley**: The novel's most purely comic study in vanity — a man whose self-regard about his appearance, his wealth, and his importance is perpetually at war with the evidence of his own cowardice and incompetence (006-015).
- **Miss Crawley**: The wealthy old woman whose vanity takes the form of intellectual pretension — she prides herself on her French novels, her free-thinking, and her discernment, all of which Becky Sharp exploits with surgical precision (010-012).
