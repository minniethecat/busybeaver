# Singing as Power

Singing in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* consistently functions as more than mere performance: it is a source of supernatural power, emotional influence, and narrative agency. Characters who sing shape their world — for good or ill — and the motif recurs across all seven tales.

## Overview

Across the collection, the act of singing serves three distinct narrative functions. First, it operates as **[[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] and counterspell** — [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s voice breaks the elves' curse in "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," while [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s dawn song lures [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]] to his [[fate-and-transformation|doom]]. Second, singing functions as **emotional [[truth-and-deception|truth]]** — characters reveal their inner state through song, from the baker's [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] to [[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]]'s longing for [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]]. Third, singing serves as **memory and identity** — [[arasmon|Arasmon]] recognizes his lost wife [[chrysea|Chrysea]] not by sight but by her voice, and the blackbird's song preserves the magic tune that once freed a village.

The motif is notably independent of instruments. While [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s [[music-as-art|lute]]-playing is often paired with [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s voice, singing alone — unaccompanied, direct from the body — carries the greater narrative weight in De Morgan's moral universe.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Singing as Liberation

The most fully developed treatment of singing occurs in "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]." [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s extraordinary voice is established at the outset:

> "When Arasmon played all who heard him were silent from wonder and admiration, but when Chrysea sang they could not refrain from weeping, for her voice was more beautiful than anything they had ever heard before."

The narrative's central conflict turns on [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s willingness to use her voice as a weapon against in[[punishment-and-reward|justice]]. The spellbound villagers are tormented by elves who renew their curse each night through song. Breaking the spell requires someone who has never accepted the elves' money to "sing through the tune just as they themselves do." [[chrysea|Chrysea]] volunteers:

> "But I have not taken their money. And there is no tune I cannot sing when I have heard it once. So I will go to the moor for you and break the spell."

Her success is abso[[music-as-art|lute]] — her voice not only breaks the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] but restores the land to fertility. Yet singing also brings her peril: the vengeful elves transform her into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]], condemning her to silence until someone plays the magic tune upon her strings. The resolution arrives decades later when the blackbird, singing the very melody [[chrysea|Chrysea]] first heard on [[the-moorland|the moor]], guides the aged [[arasmon|Arasmon]] to play the [[music-as-art|harp]] and restore her human form. Singing thus completes a full cycle: liberation → transformation → recognition → re[[marriage-and-union|union]].

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Singing as Seduction

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] weaponizes song for predatory purposes. Her dawn serenade beneath [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]]'s window is calculated entrapment:

> "She paused and began to sing a little song as sweet and joyous as a lark's. When Prince Hildebrandt heard it he got up and went to the window and looked out to see who sang, and when he saw Fiorimonde standing in the red sunrise-light, which made her hair look gold, and her complexion snow and roses, he thought he had never seen anything so lovely."

Where [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s singing liberates, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s singing ensnares — the beauty of the voice conceals deadly intent. De Morgan thus distinguishes between singing that empowers others and singing that serves only the singer.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Enchanted Songbirds

Singing appears in transformed form through the caged birds in the wizard's [[the-royal-castle|castle]], whose sweet voices mask imprisonment:

> "Hanging on the walls were cages which held what Michael thought were birds, which sang most sweetly."

These singing-birds, like the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] [[chrysea|Chrysea]] becomes, represent singing beings trapped in artificial forms — their beauty exploited for the captor's pleasure. The motif echoes the collection's broader concern with freedom and transformation.

### "The Wise Princess" — The Lark's Philosophy of Song

[[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]]'s encounter with the skylark offers the collection's most direct meditation on the meaning of singing:

> "Why do you sing?" she asked. "I sing because I am so happy," answered the lark.

The lark's inability to teach [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] — "I don't know how to teach" — underscores that authentic singing arises from an internal state that cannot be manufactured. This naturalistic vision of song contrasts s[[music-as-art|harp]]ly with [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s calculated performance.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's treatment of singing draws on two Victorian traditions: the Romantic idealization of music as the highest art (articulated by Walter Pater's dictum that "all art constantly aspires towards the condition of music"), and the fairy-tale convention of magical song found in the Brothers Grimm and Hans Christian Andersen. De Morgan's innovation is to make singing a moral index: how a character uses their voice reveals their ethical nature.
