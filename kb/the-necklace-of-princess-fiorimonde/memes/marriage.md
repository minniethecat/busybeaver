# Marriage

Marriage in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is a threshold, not a destination. It is the moment when identities transform — sometimes into salvation, sometimes into doom.

## Marriage as Trap

The title story presents marriage as the mechanism of disappearance. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] declares her willingness:

> "Next day Fiorimonde declared to her father the King, that she was quite willing to wed any prince he should fix upon as a husband for her." [0075-P1]

Her consent is a trap. Each suitor who arrives for marriage vanishes on the wedding morning, transformed into a bead on her necklace. Marriage, in Fiorimonde's hands, is not union but consumption.

## The Wedding Morning

The bridal preparations become increasingly hollow as suitors continue to disappear:

> "Next morning all was prepared for the marriage, and the Princess was dressed in white satin and pearls with a long white lace veil over her, and a bridal wreath on her head, and she stood waiting among her grandly dressed ladies." [0105-P1]

The detailed description of bridal finery — white satin, pearls, lace veil, wreath — creates a ritual frame that the narrative then empties. The bridegroom is nowhere to be found. The wedding dress becomes a costume for a ceremony that never happens.

## Marriage as Demand

Fiorimonde weaponizes the shame of being unwed:

> "'Sire, I pray that you will not let people say that when any bridegroom comes to marry me, as soon as he has seen me he flies rather than be his husband. I beg that suitors may be summoned from far and near that I may not be left alone unwed.'" [0145-P1]

The word "unwed" is her rhetorical weapon — it shames her father into continuing the cycle of suitors, ensuring a fresh supply of victims.

## Marriage as Reward

The collection's true marriages invert the Fiorimonde pattern. When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] frees [[yolande|Yolande]]:

> "And Gervaise married Yolande, and they went back with Prince Florestan to their home, and all lived happily to the end of their lives." [0347-P1]

This marriage is the collection's reward structure in its simplest form — mutual love, freedom from enchantment, and the fairy-tale ending. It is the standard against which Fiorimonde's false marriages are measured.

## Marriage Interrupted

The most dramatic marriage scene is the interruption of [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s wedding to [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]:

> "'I don't care if she bring trouble or happiness,' cried the Prince. 'But for certain she is the most beautiful creature in the world.'" [0651-P1]

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s declaration — that he does not care whether Joan brings trouble or happiness — is the collection's most radical statement about marriage: that love precedes and transcends the ritual, and that the right marriage is worth any cost.
