# Mary De Morgan

## Biography

Mary De Morgan (1850–1907) was a British writer of literary fairy tales, active during the Victorian era's revival of the fairy tale form. She was the youngest daughter of Augustus De Morgan (1806–1871), the renowned mathematician and logician who made foundational contributions to mathematical logic and Boolean algebra. Growing up in a household steeped in intellectual rigor and creative energy, De Morgan was exposed from an early age to the artistic and literary circles of Victorian London.

The De Morgan family was closely connected to the Pre-Raphaelite movement and the Arts and Crafts movement. Mary's brother, William De Morgan (1839–1917), became a celebrated ceramic artist and tile designer, collaborating with William Morris on decorative arts projects. This artistic environment — where craft, beauty, and moral seriousness intertwined — shaped Mary's literary sensibility, evident in the ornate yet precise prose of her fairy tales.

Despite her family's prominence, relatively little is known about De Morgan's personal life. She never married, and her literary career was overshadowed by the more famous Victorian fantasists — George MacDonald, Oscar Wilde, and Andrew Lang — whose fairy tales were published contemporaneously with hers. She died in 1907 in Cairo, Egypt, where she had lived for the later years of her life.

## Literary Works

### The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde and Other Stories (1880)

De Morgan's most enduring work is her 1880 collection of seven original fairy tales, published by Macmillan & Co. with illustrations by Walter Crane (1845–1915), a prominent Arts and Crafts illustrator. The collection includes:

1. The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde
2. The Heart of Princess Joan
3. The Wanderings of Arasmon
4. The Peddler's Pack
5. The Bread of Discontent
6. The Three Clever Kings
7. The Wise Princess

Each tale operates within the fairy-tale form but subverts expectations: princesses are cruel rather than kind, magic corrupts rather than redeems, and happy endings are earned through suffering rather than granted by fate.

### Other Works

- **On a Pincushion** (1877) — An earlier collection of original fairy tales, illustrated by William De Morgan.
- **A Choice of Chance** (1887) — A novel for adults, less well-known than her fairy tale collections.
- Short stories and articles published in Victorian periodicals.

## Literary Style and Themes

### Dark Moralism

De Morgan's fairy tales are notably darker than the contemporaneous tales of Andrew Lang or George MacDonald. Her stories do not shy away from cruelty, betrayal, and the corrupting influence of power. In "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde," the titular princess murders her suitors by transforming them into beads — a stark departure from the sanitized princess tales of the era. This darkness serves a moral purpose: evil is shown plainly so that its consequences are unmistakable.

### Gender and Power

De Morgan's female characters are unusually complex for Victorian children's literature. Princess Fiorimonde is a villain-protagonist — beautiful, intelligent, and utterly ruthless — whose evil is not excused by her gender. Princess Joan ("The Heart of Princess Joan") is tricked and victimized but ultimately triumphs through steadfastness. The Wise Princess Fernanda solves royal crises through intelligence rather than magic. Across the collection, De Morgan refuses the Victorian convention of passive, ornamental princesses.

### Economic Morality

Several tales explicitly link moral worth to material conditions. "The Bread of Discontent" explores how poverty and dissatisfaction can corrupt a community. "The Peddler's Pack" uses trade and exchange as metaphors for moral transaction. De Morgan's engagement with economic themes reflects the Arts and Crafts movement's critique of industrial capitalism and its effects on human dignity.

### Transformation as Narrative Engine

Physical transformation — humans becoming beads, harps, animals, or beggars — is De Morgan's signature narrative device. Transformation externalizes internal moral states: the vain become objects, the faithful are restored, and enchantment is always reversible through right action. This motif is explored systematically across the wiki's theme pages, particularly fate-and-transformation.

## Context in Victorian Fairy Tale Tradition

De Morgan wrote during a golden age of the British literary fairy tale, alongside:

- **George MacDonald** (1824–1905): _The Princess and the Goblin_ (1872), _At the Back of the North Wind_ (1871). MacDonald's tales blend Christian allegory with fantasy; De Morgan's are more secular and morally ambiguous.
- **Oscar Wilde** (1854–1900): _The Happy Prince and Other Tales_ (1888). Wilde's tales share De Morgan's dark moralism and aesthetic sensibility, though Wilde's are more overtly sentimental.
- **Andrew Lang** (1844–1912): The _Coloured Fairy Books_ (1889–1910). Lang collected and edited traditional fairy tales; De Morgan wrote original literary tales.
- **Juliana Horatia Ewing** (1841–1885): A contemporary whose original fairy tales similarly blended moral seriousness with imaginative power.

De Morgan's work has received renewed attention in recent scholarship on Victorian women writers and the fairy tale tradition, though she remains less widely read than her male contemporaries.
