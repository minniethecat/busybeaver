# Wealth and Poverty

Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* treats wealth with consistent suspicion. Gold, jewels, and material abundance appear not as [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]s but as tests — characters who pursue wealth are corrupted or deceived, while those who accept poverty often find unexpected forms of richness. De Morgan's moral economy inverts the values of the commercial Victorian society in which she wrote.

## Overview

Wealth in De Morgan's tales functions along two related axes. First, **wealth as temptation** — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s golden necklace, the pedlar's [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]d gold, and the wizard's cave of treasures all lure characters toward moral com[[the-sacred-vow|promise]]. Second, **wealth as false measure** — the collection repeatedly insists that material abundance and true value are unrelated. The soldiers who counsel surrender value "city and wealth" over honour; [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s recognition that his lost wife [[chrysea|Chrysea]] was his "only wealth" reframes prosperity in relational rather than material terms.

De Morgan's treatment of poverty is equally pointed. Characters who are materially poor — the spellbound villagers, the discontented baker before his rise — are not idealized as virtuous. Poverty, like wealth, is morally neutral; what matters is the character's disposition toward what they have and what they lack.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — The True Measure of Wealth

[[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s return to the transformed village provides the collection's most direct meditation on wealth's meaning:

> "Time has changed all things strangely; but it would be hard to say which is the more altered, this village or I, for then it was sunk in poverty and wretchedness, and now it has gained happiness and wealth, and I, who was so happy and glad, now am broken-down and worn. I have lost my only wealth, my wife Chrysea."

The contrast is devastating in its simplicity. The village has gained material prosperity; [[arasmon|Arasmon]], who was once happy and poor, is now wealthy in coin but destitute in meaning. His statement that [[chrysea|Chrysea]] was his "only wealth" reframes value entirely — the word "only" dignifies the singular treasure over the plural possessions.

The episode's earlier moment reinforces this inversion. When the city is threatened, the soldiers' counsel reveals wealth-prioritization as moral failure:

> "It would be better," said the soldiers, "to lay down our arms at once and let the enemy enter, for then we should not lose our lives as well as our city and our wealth."

De Morgan's narrative does not condemn this logic explicitly, but the story's resolution — in which [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s singing, not military might or material resources, breaks the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] — implicitly rejects the soldiers' calculus.

### "The Wise Princess" — Wealth Without Happiness

[[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]], the wise princess who understands "the languages of all animals" and could command "untold wealth," embodies the collection's most explicit statement on the insufficiency of material power:

> "She could have turned her enemies to stone, or given untold wealth to her friends. But for all that, when she smiled, her lips were very sad, and her eyes were always full of care."

The conjunction "but for all that" marks the disjunction between power and [[happiness-and-contentment|satisfaction]]. [[princess-fernanda|Fernanda]]'s sadness in the midst of capability is De Morgan's argument that wealth and power answer no essential human question. The wise princess's wisdom consists precisely in recognizing this — her quest is not for more power but for [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]], which material abundance cannot provide.

### "The Pedlar's Pack" — Gold as False Promise

The pedlar's opening [[truth-and-deception|deception]] — promising gold he does not possess — establishes gold as the currency of [[truth-and-deception|falsehood]] from the story's first scene. The pack itself, filled with magical and morally ambiguous objects, functions as a metaphor for material desire: what it contains [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s much but delivers transformation of uncertain value.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's suspicion of wealth aligns her with the Christian socialist tradition in Victorian letters — Charles Kingsley's *The Water-Babies* and John Ruskin's *The King of the Golden River* similarly treat wealth as a moral hazard. Her innovation is to embed this suspicion within fairy-tale structures that traditionally [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed protagonists with riches, thereby subverting the genre's conventional relationship to material [[punishment-and-reward|reward]].
