# The Moorland

## Location and Context

The Moorland lies beyond [[the-spellbound-village|the Spellbound Village]], separated from it by open ground. Before the wizard came, it was "covered with [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en gorse and heather," and the country folk held all their merrymakings there (003-031). The moor can be seen from the village, and the village woman pointed "towards the moor" when telling [[chrysea|Chrysea]] where the terrible old wizard dwelled (003-027).

## Description

Before the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]], the moorland was a place of celebration: [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en gorse and purple heather covered the ground, and the country people gathered there for festivals and dances. The villagers sold this land to the wizard, tempted by "a great deal of money" (003-031).

Under the wizard's spell, the moorland became a wasteland. [[chrysea|Chrysea]] found it "a large waste piece of land" that looked as though it had been burnt: the ground was charred and black, no grass or green plant grew anywhere upon it, and only blackened stumps of trees remained standing (003-039).

After the spell was broken, the moorland began to heal immediately. "Flowers and grass were already beginning to grow there" on the very first day of freedom, and the village children — who had never dared venture near it before — came to play upon it (003-063).

> "Over yonder dwells a terrible old wizard by whom we are bewitched, and he has a number of little dark elves who are his servants" (003-027).

## Key Events

### The Wizard's Arrival

Long ago, a terrible old magician came to the village and offered a great sum of money to dwell upon the moor. The villagers, tempted by [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]], sold what had been their common land for merrymaking. From that day, the elves began to torment them (003-031).

### The Nightly Spell Renewal

Each night between midnight and dawn, the dark elves rose from the ground in a lurid circle of light. [[chrysea|Chrysea]], hiding behind a charred tree stump, watched them dance around a miniature replica of the village and sing the spell-song that renewed the curse (003-039–042).

### Chrysea's Counter-Spell

After the elves finished singing, [[chrysea|Chrysea]] opened her lips and "sang the same tune through from beginning to end just as she had heard it" (003-043). Her voice rang out "loud and clear," and the little model village crumbled to dust. The enraged elves, led by their chief, transformed [[chrysea|Chrysea]] into [[the-golden-harp|a golden harp]] as [[punishment-and-reward|retribution]] before vanishing (003-044–054).

### Restoration

When [[arasmon|Arasmon]] returned to the moorland many years later, flowers and grass had regrown, and the village children played freely where once they had feared to tread — the moorland had become a place of life and [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] again (003-063, 003-094).

## Narrative Function

The Moorland is the physical manifestation of the story's central conflict: a place of communal [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] corrupted by greed (the villagers selling it for [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]) and restored by selfless courage ([[chrysea|Chrysea]] risking herself to break the spell). Its transformation from flowering heath to charred waste to renewed grassland mirrors the village's parallel journey from prosperity through [[despair-and-endurance|despair]] to liberation.

The moorland also serves as the arena where [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s heroic nature is fully revealed. Unlike the villagers — bound by their complicity in the wizard's bargain — [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is free to act because she "never took their money" (003-035). Her solitary journey onto the charred moor at one in the morning is an act of pure altruism, and the price she pays — transformation into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] — elevates her sacrifice to the mythic.
