# The Witch's Sabbath

The Witch's Sabbath is a vividly described supernatural event in "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]." It takes place in the underground chamber beneath [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s hut and re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] one of the most atmospheric passages in De Morgan's collection.

## Summoning

After giving [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread, [[the-old-witch|the witch]] declares: "since you are here, and there is yet time, we will have a dance, and I will summon the guests." She takes "from a corner a drum and a pair of drumsticks, and going to the door, began to beat upon it. It made a terrible rattling."

## The Guests

The drumming summons a grotesque assembly of supernatural beings:
- "little dark elves with long tails"
- "goblins who chattered and laughed"
- "other witches who rode on broomsticks"
- "one wicked fairy in the form of a large cat, with bright green eyes"
- Another fairy "came sliding in like a long shining viper"

## The Underground Chamber

The witch stamps on the floor, opening a trapdoor that leads "down a narrow dark passage, to a large underground chamber." Here the supernatural company "all danced and made merry in a terrible way." The dance serves as a ritual celebration of [[magic-and-witchcraft|dark magic]] — a contrast to the formal, ordered festivities of [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] above.

## The Ending

At "first sound of cockcrow all the guests disappeared with a whiff," and [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] hurries home on her giant bird. The cockcrow as a signal of the sabbath's end draws on European folklore traditions where supernatural beings must flee at dawn.

## Literary Significance

The Sabbath scene establishes the scale and reality of the magical world in De Morgan's universe. It demonstrates that [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[magic-and-witchcraft|witchcraft]] is not an isolated practice but part of a broader supernatural community. The vivid, grotesque imagery also marks De Morgan's prose at its most Gothic, reminiscent of the darker fairy tale traditions of the Brothers Grimm.
