# Marriage and Union

Marriage in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is not a beginning but an end — not the premise of a story but its hard-won conclusion. Characters marry only after proving themselves worthy through quest, [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]], or moral choice. Marriage is the collection's sign that order has been restored, that the right people have found each other, and that the narrative has reached its proper terminus.

## Overview

The collection's marriages fall into three patterns. First, **marriage as rescue** — the union that breaks an [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] and restores a character to wholeness, as in [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s marriage to [[princess-joan|Joan]] after he restores her heart. Second, **marriage as [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]** — the culmination of a long quest, as with [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] at the end of the title story. Third, **false marriage** — the threatened union that must be prevented, as with [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s arranged marriage to [[king-lambert|King Lambert]].

Across all three patterns, De Morgan consistently treats marriage as a moral institution. The right marriage blesses both partners and restores community; the wrong marriage threatens both individual and collective [[happiness-and-contentment|well-being]]. There is no neutral marriage in De Morgan's world — each union either affirms or violates the moral order.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — Marriage Earned Through Quest

The collection's most sustained treatment of marriage as narrative endpoint occurs in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]." The story's entire arc is structured around [[the-heros-journey|the quest]]ion of whether [[prince-michael|Michael]] and [[princess-joan|Joan]] will marry — a question deferred through seven years of quest and [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]].

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s arranged marriage to the aged [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] everything marriage should not be: a union motivated by [[greed-and-beauty|ambition]] rather than love, imposed by the Queen's exhausted consent after seven years of waiting:

> "Tomorrow she is to be married to old King Lambert, and the wedding will be very grand, but none of the country folk like it, for he is old and ugly, and they say he does not love her at all, but only marries her that he may be king of this country as well as his own."

This marriage is transparently political — [[king-lambert|Lambert]] seeks territory, not [[princess-joan|Joan]]. The country folk's disapproval registers the marriage's violation of deeper values. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s return transforms this false union into the true one, replacing coercion with love and reclaiming marriage as the proper [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] for his long ordeal.

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Marriage as Redemption

The title story's conclusion implies marriage between [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] — the only characters in the tale who have seen through [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]s and acted to break them:

> "And he drew off the twelfth bead, and there stood Yolande in her red dress; and when he saw her Gervaise flung away his dagger and took her in his arms."

The embrace is the prelude to marriage, the physical acknowledgment of the bond that the narrative has been building. Unlike the vanished suitors who sought [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s hand for her beauty and her throne, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]]'s union is earned through shared risk and mutual recognition.

### "The Wise Princess" — Marriage and Wisdom

In "[[the-wise-princess-story|The Wise Princess]]," marriage is the resolution that follows the princess's demonstration of wisdom. The narrative logic is clear: wisdom qualifies one for union; foolishness disqualifies. De Morgan's princesses earn marriage not through beauty or birth but through the exercise of intelligence and [[justice-and-injustice|judgment]] — a notably proto-feminist revision of the fairy-tale convention.

## Literary Context

Victorian fairy tales inherited a tradition in which marriage was the unexamined [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] for virtue — the princess marries the prince because she is beautiful and good. De Morgan maintains marriage as the narrative endpoint but alters its meaning: her characters marry not because the genre requires it but because they have proven themselves worthy of union through their actions. This aligns her with the moral seriousness of George MacDonald, for whom marriage was a spiritual institution, and distinguishes her from the more conventional resolutions of Andrew Lang's fairy collections.
