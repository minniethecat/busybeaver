# The Hero's Return

The hero's return in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is never a quiet homecoming. It arrives at the moment of greatest narrative tension — a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] about to be celebrated, a spell about to become permanent, a community about to lose [[hope-as-resilience|hope]] — and transforms the situation by the hero's mere reappearance. De Morgan treats return not as denouement but as intervention: the point at which the hero's long labour or absence is converted into action that resolves the plot.

## The Return in Disguise

### Michael Before the Wedding

The collection's most dramatic return occurs in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," when [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] arrives at [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] to [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] after seven years of absence. His appearance is deliberately unassuming:

> "I will be a guest at that wedding," cried Michael; "And I bring the best gift of all for the bride."

[[prince-michael|Michael]] enters not as a prince but as a beggar — sun-blackened, scarred, unrecognized. The servants mock him and threaten to punish him. But his gift — [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s restored heart, recovered through years of labour — transforms the situation. When [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart is returned and she recognizes [[prince-michael|Michael]], [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] to [[king-lambert|Lambert]] collapses and the true [[marriage-and-union|union]] is celebrated:

> "When the people heard her, they cried, 'It is Prince Michael, who went seven years ago, and who we all thought was dead, and he is returned in time to marry our Princess. Now will we indeed have a wedding.'"

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s return is structured as a three-part revelation: the arrival (unrecognized), the gift (the heart restored), and the recognition (identity claimed). De Morgan delays the recognition to maximize its dramatic force — the reader knows [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s identity long before the characters do, and the gap between knowledge and recognition generates the scene's tension.

### Gervaise Among the Courtiers

In the title story, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] — the twelfth suitor and the only one forewarned — is not a return after long absence but a strategic arrival. Unlike [[prince-michael|Michael]], [[gervaise|Gervaise]] knows the danger he faces, and his "return" is to a place he has never been — he arrives not to reclaim a lost love but to break an [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] that has consumed eleven men before him.

His success depends on knowledge rather than [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]]. Where [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s return is powered by love, [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s is powered by intelligence — he has learned the secret of [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] and approaches with a plan.

## The Return to the Village

In a lower key, "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" offers a collective return — the villagers' return to productive life after the spell of discontent is broken. The natural world signals the [[fate-and-transformation|change]] before the people fully understand it:

> "When morning dawned, and the villagers awoke, all felt that some great change had taken place. The heavy cloud which hung above the village had cleared away; the sun shone brightly, and the sky was blue."

This is return as restoration — not of a person but of a way of life. The villagers' "return" is not to a place but to a condition: meaningful work, natural abundance, communal purpose.

## The Return from the Sea

The framing narrative of "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" provides the return in miniature. The fishing boats that have been feared lost suddenly appear:

> "But as she stopped speaking, there rose a cry of 'Here they are,' and two or three fishing-boats were pushed on to the sand close by where they stood, and the women threw their arms round their husbands' necks, and all shouted for joy."

The return from the sea is the collection's most elemental version of the pattern — the physical return of those feared dead, greeted with embraces and shouts. [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s own decades-long search for [[chrysea|Chrysea]] and their eventual re[[marriage-and-union|union]] echoes this smaller return at a mythic scale.

## Narrative Function

The hero's return serves a specific narrative function in De Morgan's tales: it is the hinge between the story's long middle (quest, [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]], waiting) and its resolution. Without the return, [[the-heros-journey|the quest]] is incomplete; with it, the accumulated tension of absence is discharged, and the story can conclude. De Morgan understands that return is inherently dramatic — it forces a reckoning between what has [[fate-and-transformation|change]]d during the absence and what must be restored.
