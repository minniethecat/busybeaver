# The Iron Manacles

The Iron Manacles are enchanted restraints in Mary De Morgan's "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," placed upon [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] by the old wizard at the beginning of his seven-year trial. They serve as both physical limitation and magical test, designed to fall away only when [[prince-michael|Michael]] has completed his appointed labour — building a wall of heavy stones for a giant serpent's eggs.

## Origin

When [[prince-michael|Michael]] follows the crimson star to a desolate island where an old wizard tends a monstrous serpent, he accepts the task of building a protective wall around the serpent's eggs for seven years. Before setting him to work, the wizard produces the restraints:

> From a nail upon the wall he took a pair of manacles fastened together by a heavy iron chain. These he slipped over Michael's wrists, and stooping down over them, muttered a few words, and at once the manacles fastened together as if they had been locked, and Michael could not move them, or draw out his hands. Then the old man took down another heavy chain and passed it over the first and fastened it with more iron rings to his ankles, so that he could only move his arms and hands a little way, and could not raise them high, and could only walk with slow careful steps. [781-ST04]

The wizard explains the manacles' purpose by pointing to a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en sword hanging high on the wall — the key to the enchanted [[the-royal-castle|castle]] — which [[prince-michael|Michael]] cannot reach "while your hands are chained" [783-ST04].

## Physical Description

The restraints consist of two components: a pair of iron manacles joined by "a heavy iron chain" that binds the wrists together, and a second heavy chain that connects the wrist bonds to iron rings around the ankles [781-ST04]. The chains restrict movement severely: [[prince-michael|Michael]] can move his arms only "a little way," cannot raise them high, and can only walk with "slow careful steps" [781-ST04]. The wizard seals them with muttered [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]s, causing them to fasten "as if they had been locked" [781-ST04].

## Properties / Powers

The manacles possess a single magical property: they are bound to the completion of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s task. The wizard explicitly states the condition of their release: "when the serpent's eggs are hatched your iron rings will fall off, and you yourself may take the sword down from its place" [783-ST04].

This [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] proves true. When the seven years end and the serpent's eggs hatch — producing a hideous multi-coloured monster assembled from mismatched parts — "the manacles on [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s wrists burst asunder, and the chains fell to the ground" [825-ST04]. The release is instantaneous and automatic, requiring no action from [[prince-michael|Michael]]; the completion of the labour is itself the key.

The manacles thus function as both **prison and [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]**: they physically prevent [[prince-michael|Michael]] from seizing the sword prematurely, but their magical nature guarantees that faithful completion of the trial will be [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed with liberation.

## Role in the Story

The manacles define the physical reality of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s seven-year trial. They transform what might have been a simple waiting period into active [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] — every stone he lifts must be moved with bound hands, every step taken with chained ankles. Their weight and restriction make the labour genuinely punishing, testing [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s resolve.

The manacles also serve a narrative function as **visible markers of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s degradation**. When, near the end of the seven years, [[prince-michael|Michael]] sees his reflection in a pool of water, he beholds a man transformed: hair thin and grey, face furrowed and seamed, eyes dim, shoulders bowed — the manacles having shaped not only his movement but his entire physical being [813-ST04].

The moment of the manacles' breaking marks the story's turning point: from passive [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]] to active agency. Freed, [[prince-michael|Michael]] immediately seizes the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en sword and pushes his way into the [[the-royal-castle|castle]], the chains' fall signalling that the period of trial is over and the period of action has begun [825-ST04, 827-ST04].

## Symbolic Significance

The Iron Manacles embody the theme of **constrained agency** central to [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s character arc. As a prince who chose questing over comfort, [[prince-michael|Michael]] accepts limitations freely — the manacles represent the cost of his choice made tangible. Their weight literalizes the burden of commitment.

The chains also function as a **time-keeping device**. Unlike the seven years that pass invisibly, the manacles are a constant physical reminder of the trial's duration — every restricted movement a marker of time not yet completed. Their sudden bursting is the story's most dramatic physical transformation, more visceral than the magical illusions of the [[the-royal-castle|castle]] because it re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] earned, not gifted, liberation.

In the broader context of De Morgan's fairy tales, the manacles contrast with [[the-gold-necklace]] — both are binding objects placed upon the body, but where [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] transforms identity (princes into beads), the manacles preserve it ([[prince-michael|Michael]] remains [[prince-michael|Michael]], just constrained). One binding destroys selfhood; the other tests it.
