# Death

Death in Mary De Morgan's fairy tales is not a horror to be fled but a presence to be accepted — the quiet close of a story, the final proof of love, and the threshold at which transformation becomes permanent.

## Death as Union

The most poignant death in the collection belongs to [[arasmon|Arasmon]] and [[chrysea|Chrysea]], the wandering musicians. After years of separation, Arasmon finds his wife beneath the oak tree where she was enchanted:

> "'Chrysea!' cried he; 'I have found my wife Chrysea!' and he laid his head on her bosom and died. And when Chrysea saw it her heart broke, and she lay beside him and died without a word." [0581-P1]

Death here is not tragedy but reunion — the only possible completion of a love story that has already passed through enchantment and separation.

## Death as Public Fact

The villagers' discovery transforms private death into communal recognition:

> "In the morning when some of the villagers crossed the common they saw Arasmon and Chrysea lying beneath the oak-tree in each other's arms, and drew near them, thinking they were asleep, but when they saw their faces they knew they were dead." [0583-P1]

Death, in De Morgan's world, is the moment when a private story becomes public legend.

## Death Willingly Faced

The collection's deepest expressions of loyalty involve readiness to die. [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s devotion to [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]] is introduced with the ultimate measure:

> "Gervaise was tall, and broad, and stout of limb, and he loved Prince Florestan so well, that he would gladly have died to serve him." [0151-P1]

Willingness to die — not the act of dying itself — is the collection's gold standard of fidelity. Gervaise proves this willingness when he confronts Fiorimonde, risking the fate that consumed her other suitors.

## Death as Battle-Cry

In "The Wanderings of Arasmon," the acceptance of death transforms defeated soldiers into warriors:

> "When Arasmon heard this he sat upon the walls of the town, and began to play upon his harp, and this time its music was so loud and clear, that it could be heard far and wide, and its sound was so exultant and joyous, that when the soldiers heard it they raised their heads, and their fears vanished, and they started forward, shouting and calling that they would conquer or be killed." [0475-P1]

The phrase "conquer or be killed" is not despair — it is liberation. By accepting death as a possibility, the soldiers are freed from the fear that paralyzed them. Arasmon's music does not promise survival; it makes survival irrelevant.

## Death as Narrative Suspense

In "The Heart of Princess Joan," death hovers as a deadline rather than an ending. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] sets the terms of his quest:

> "'If, when they are past, I have not returned, and you have heard nothing of me, you must think that I am dead and gone.'" [0721-P1]

Death is the backup narrative — what will happen if the hero fails. It marks the boundary between quest and surrender.

## Death as Presumed Fate

The title story establishes the pattern of disappearance as presumed death:

> "So at last everyone gave him up for dead, and mourned for him, and wondered how he had met his fate." [0111-P1]

The phrase "gave him up for dead" recurs for each vanished suitor. Death by presumption — not witnessed but assumed — is the fate Fiorimonde inflicts on twelve princes. The community's mourning is always premature and always correct: the suitors are lost, even if they are not strictly dead but transformed into beads.

## The Princess Who Cannot Grieve

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heartlessness manifests as an inability to register death. When her horse dies:

> "'Dead is it?' cried the Princess. 'Then see quickly and get me another, that I may ride again tomorrow.'" [0687-P1]

And earlier, when her little dog dies: "she laughed outright" [0619-P1]. Her indifference to death is the collection's clearest sign of her spiritual deformity — the absence that the seven-year quest must fill. She cannot grieve because her heart, stolen by the fairy, cannot love.

## Death of Hope

The threat of starvation reduces [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] to the ultimate plea:

> "'Oh! Why have I toiled for seven years,' he cried aloud, 'and at last won my way into the castle, if now I am to be starved to death, and Joan will never know how I have laboured for her sake?'" [0855-P1]

Death at the threshold — so close to success — is the collection's most cruel irony. But De Morgan's pattern holds: the cry of despair is the immediate precursor to rescue. The voice that answers Michael's cry belongs to Joan herself, appearing with the ruby wine that will save him.
