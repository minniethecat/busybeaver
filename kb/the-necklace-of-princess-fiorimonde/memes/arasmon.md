# Arasmon

Arasmon is the wandering musician protagonist of "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," the second tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]*. His story is one of enduring love, artistic [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]], and the transformative power of music.

## Character Overview

Arasmon is a wandering [[love-and-loyalty|harper]] who travels the world with his wife [[chrysea|Chrysea]], who sings to his [[music-as-art|lute]]. Together their music is so beautiful "that people followed them in crowds and gave them as much money as they wanted." Both are described as "young and lovely, and were as happy as the day is long, for they loved each other dearly."

## Musical Gift

Arasmon's playing possesses an almost supernatural power over listeners:

> "When Arasmon played all who heard him were silent from wonder and admiration."

His music has the power to calm, inspire, and guide. Throughout his wanderings, his playing:
- Saves fishermen lost in a thick fog by guiding them safely to shore with his melody
- Rallies a demoralised army to victory when the soldiers hear his "exultant and [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]ous" playing
- Moves a King to make him chief musician of [[the-royal-castle|the court]]

## The Golden Harp

Arasmon's most treasured possession is a small [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] he discovers hanging from a tree on a moor. Unknown to him, the [[music-as-art|harp]] is his wife [[chrysea|Chrysea]], transformed by dark [[the-spellbound-village|elves]] as [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] for breaking their spell over a village:

> "Never before did harp give forth such music. It was like a woman's voice, and was most beautiful, but so sad that when Arasmon heard it he felt inclined to cry."

The [[music-as-art|harp]] cries "Arasmon, Arasmon, I am here! It is I, [[chrysea|Chrysea]]!" each time its strings are touched, but Arasmon never understands its words.

## The Long Search

After [[chrysea|Chrysea]] vanishes, Arasmon refuses to abandon [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]. He wanders "for days and months and years through countries and villages which he had never known before," always asking:

> "Have you seen my wife Chrysea? She is dressed in white and gold, and sings more sweetly than any of the birds of heaven."

The physical toll of his quest is devastating. By the time he reaches a foreign court, "his face had grown thin and pale, and there were gray locks in his hair." When he finally returns to the village where they parted, a girl does not recognise the "poor old man" as the beautiful young musician who once visited.

## Reunion and Death

Following a peasant woman's advice to return to where he last saw [[chrysea|Chrysea]], Arasmon goes back to [[the-moorland|the moor]]. There, beneath an oak tree where a [[the-royal-castle|blackbird]] sings the elves' magic song, he plays the tune on his [[music-as-art|harp]] with his last strength. The spell breaks, and [[chrysea|Chrysea]] stands before him:

> "'Arasmon!' she cried, 'see, it is I, Chrysea!' but Arasmon did not move... 'Chrysea!' cried he; 'I have found my wife Chrysea!' and he laid his head on her bosom and died. And when Chrysea saw it her heart broke, and she lay beside him and died without a word."

## Significance

Arasmon embodies the fairy-tale archetype of the [[love-and-loyalty|faithful love]]r whose [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]] is tested across time. Unlike the princes in "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]" who are victims of [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] born of [[greed-and-beauty|vanity and greed]], Arasmon's [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] comes from the very act of love itself. His story is a meditation on art (his music preserves the connection to his lost wife), [[love-and-loyalty|fidelity]] (he never wavers), and the bittersweet nature of re[[marriage-and-union|union]] after too long a parting.
