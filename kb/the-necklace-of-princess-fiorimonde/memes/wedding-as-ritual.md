# Wedding as Ritual

The wedding ceremony in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is rarely a celebration of love fulfilled. Instead, it functions as a charged liminal moment — a threshold across which characters pass into either [[marriage-and-union|union]] or destruction. De Morgan treats [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] not as an ending but as a crucible: a concentrated interval where hidden [[truth-and-deception|truth]]s surface, [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]s break, and narrative arcs reach their decisive turn.

## Overview

Weddings appear across multiple stories in De Morgan's collection, always bearing heavier narrative weight than the conventional "happily ever after" finale. Three patterns dominate. First, the **suspended wedding** — preparations made, guests gathered, but the bridegroom vanishes before the ceremony, as in the title story's serial disappearances. Second, the **thwarted wedding** — a last-minute intervention or revelation that prevents an unjust [[marriage-and-union|union]], most dramatically in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" where [[prince-michael|Michael]] returns after seven years. Third, the **symbolic wedding** — [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] as metaphor for transformation, as with the spellbound village's release into new life.

De Morgan's weddings are sites of exposure. The ritual's public nature means that when something goes wrong, the failure is witnessed — the community gathers to see the bridegroom's absence, the rival's return, or the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]'s breaking.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — The Wedding That Never Comes

The title story is structured around a series of failed weddings. Each groom arrives, preparations commence, and then the morning of the ceremony arrives — and the bridegroom is gone. The pattern repeats with [[king-pierrot|Pierrot]], [[prince-hildebrandt|Hildebrandt]], Adrian, Sigbert, Algar, and more, each vanishing into a bead on [[the-beads|Fiorimonde's necklace]].

> "So the wedding guests went away, and the Princess laid aside her bridal dress, and all waited anxiously for news of King Pierrot; and no news came."

The wedding becomes a site of ritualized disappearance, the ceremony replaced by absence. The guests assemble, the bridal dress is donned, but the central figure — the groom — is missing. De Morgan uses this pattern to build suspense across the narrative: each successive wedding heightens the reader's awareness that something is profoundly wrong.

> "And again great preparations were made for a wedding; and King Pierrot was quite forgotten."

The ease with which [[the-royal-castle|the court]] forgets each vanished suitor and prepares for the next wedding underscores a dark irony: [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] ritual has become a mechanism of erasure rather than [[marriage-and-union|union]].

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Wedding Interrupted

The collection's most dramatic wedding scene occurs in the final pages of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]." After seven years of waiting, [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] is to be married to the aged [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] — a match the Queen has resisted, but the seven-year deadline has expired.

> "It is for the marriage of the King's daughter Joan. Tomorrow she is to be married to old King Lambert, and the wedding will be very grand, but none of the country folk like it, for he is old and ugly, and they say he does not love her at all."

The wedding here is not a celebration but a threat — an unjust fate closing in. The country folk's disapproval signals [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]]'s illegitimacy before the plot resolves it.

[[prince-michael|Michael]], returning after his long quest, arrives at [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] as an unknown beggar. His return interrupts the ceremony at its decisive moment:

> "When the people heard her, they cried, 'It is Prince Michael, who went seven years ago, and who we all thought was dead, and he is returned in time to marry our Princess. Now will we indeed have a wedding.'"

The false wedding is replaced by the true one — [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] ritual itself is redeemed, shifting from coerced [[marriage-and-union|union]] to [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]ful re[[marriage-and-union|union]]. [[prince-michael|Michael]] declares:

> "I will be a guest at that wedding, and I bring the best gift of all for the bride."

His gift is [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s restored heart, and his presence transforms a travesty of [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] into its authentic celebration.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Wedding as Long-Deferred Hope

The wedding between [[arasmon|Arasmon]] and [[chrysea|Chrysea]] occurs only after decades of separation and transformation. It is barely described, occurring offstage after [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s restoration from [[music-as-art|harp]] to woman — but its very absence from detailed narration underscores that [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] is less the story's point than the re[[marriage-and-union|union]] that makes it possible.

## Literary Context

The Victorian fairy tale frequently used [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] as a narrative endpoint — the "happy ever after" that closes the tale and restores order. De Morgan's innovation lies in her refusal to treat [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] as a conclusion. Her weddings occur mid-story, are deferred, interrupted, or transformed. They are moments of narrative pressure rather than release. This aligns her with the darker stream of Victorian fantasy — Christina Rossetti's "Goblin Market," where [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] is likewise a site of rescue rather than generic convention — and distinguishes her from the Perrault tradition where [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] is the tale's unquestioned [[punishment-and-reward|reward]].
