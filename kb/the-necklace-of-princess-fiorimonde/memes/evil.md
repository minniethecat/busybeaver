# Evil

Evil in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is concrete and physical. It has a colour — yellow — and a texture. De Morgan's villains are not abstract moral forces but embodied corruptions whose evil is visible to those with the right lens.

## Evil Recognized

The collection rewards characters who can recognize evil before it strikes. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] warns [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]]:

> "'There is an evil fate about the Princess Fiorimonde,' quoth he; 'many have gone to marry her, but where are they now?'" [0155-P1]

Gervaise's ability to perceive evil from pattern — from precedent rather than direct evidence — is the wisdom that saves him. He reads the statistics of disappearance and draws the correct conclusion.

## Evil Self-Destroyed

De Morgan's moral structure ensures that evil defeats itself. When [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] seizes the false necklace Gervaise has offered:

> "No sooner had she taken it in her hands than they fell with a rattle to the earth, and Fiorimonde herself was nowhere to be seen. Gervaise bent down over the necklace as it lay upon the grass, and, with a smile, counted thirteen beads; and he knew that the thirteenth was the wicked Princess, who had herself met the evil fate she had prepared for so many others." [0321-P1]

The symmetry is exact: the instrument of evil becomes its own punishment. The thirteenth bead — Fiorimonde herself — completes the collection she built from her victims.

## The Yellow Hag

In "The Heart of Princess Joan," evil has a consistent visual signature. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] uses the magic glass to see through illusion:

> "Drawing it forth when she was not looking, gazed through it at her, and then he beheld no finely-dressed lady, but a wizened old woman, robed in yellow, with an evil yellow face and evil yellow eyes." [0835-P1]

The repetition of "yellow" — three times in a single sentence — fixes the colour of evil in De Morgan's palette. Yellow is the colour of sickness, age, and corruption.

## Evil Exposed

The magic glass — a gift of truth — reveals the same evil beneath every disguise:

> "Then Michael took from his bosom the magic glass and looked through it, and saw no lovely child, but the same yellow hag with shrivelled face and evil eyes." [0845-P1]

The phrase "the same yellow hag" confirms that De Morgan's evil is singular — not a variety of different evils but one corruption wearing different masks. The magic glass strips away the masks but cannot destroy what it reveals.
