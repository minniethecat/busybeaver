# Transformation in De Morgan's Tales

## Thesis

Physical transformation is the central narrative engine in Mary De Morgan's fairy tales. Unlike traditional fairy tales where transformation is a curse to be broken by external heroism, De Morgan's transformations externalize the moral state of the character: the vain become objects, the cruel become beasts, and restoration comes only through genuine internal change. This essay traces the transformation motif across all seven stories in the 1880 collection.

## Evidence

### The Beads: Transformation as Punishment

In "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde," Princess Fiorimonde uses a magical golden necklace to transform her unwanted suitors into colored beads. Each suitor — kings, princes, noblemen — is reduced to a decorative object, stripped of voice and agency. The transformation is both literal (human → bead) and symbolic: the suitors' willingness to be valued for their wealth and status rather than their character is made physically manifest. Their objectification is the logical endpoint of their superficiality.

The necklace itself, worn by Fiorimonde around her neck, serves as a trophy case of her cruelty. When Fiorimonde is eventually tricked into becoming a bead herself, the transformation achieves poetic justice: the one who turned others into objects becomes the final bead on her own string.

### The Harp: Transformation as Loss

In "The Wanderings of Arasmon," Chrysea is transformed into a golden harp. Unlike Fiorimonde's suitors, Chrysea is innocent — her transformation is an act of malevolent magic against her, not a punishment. The harp retains Chrysea's essence: it produces music of supernatural beauty, and those who hear it sense that something human resides within.

Arasmon's quest to find and restore Chrysea forms the narrative arc. His fidelity — years of wandering, never giving up hope — is the counter-force to the enchantment. Transformation here tests love through endurance rather than heroism.

### The Beggar King: Transformation as Revelation

In "The Heart of Princess Joan," King Pierrot is reduced from monarch to beggar. This transformation strips away the external markers of identity — crown, wealth, deference — and reveals the person beneath. Pierrot's worth, it turns out, depends on none of these things. His restoration is not a return to power but an elevation to a truer self.

### Animal Forms and Moral States

Across the collection, characters are transformed into animals — birds, beasts, creatures of the forest. These transformations consistently map to moral categories: the predatory become predators, the gentle become gentle creatures. Form follows nature.

## Analysis

### Transformation as Moral Externalization

De Morgan's transformations differ fundamentally from those in traditional fairy tales. In the Grimm brothers' tales, transformation is typically an external curse requiring an external solution (a kiss, a magic word, a heroic act). In De Morgan, transformation reveals rather than imposes. The bead-form of Fiorimonde's suitors is not a disguise but a distillation: they were always objects, valued for their titles and wealth. The transformation merely makes the metaphor literal.

### Restoration Through Internal Change

Consistent across all seven stories: transformations are never reversed by external magic alone. Restoration requires the transformed person (or their champion) to demonstrate moral growth — fidelity (Arasmon), humility (King Pierrot), or genuine love (Princess Joan). Magic in De Morgan's universe is morally reactive: it responds to, rather than overrides, the ethical state of the person.

### The Object/Subject Dialectic

A recurring pattern: characters who treat others as objects become objects themselves. Characters who insist on the subjectivity and dignity of others — who see past enchantment to the person within — are the agents of restoration. This dialectic reflects De Morgan's Arts and Crafts milieu, where the dehumanizing effects of industrial production were a central concern.

## Conclusion

Transformation in De Morgan's tales is not decorative fantasy but moral argument. The body's form mirrors the soul's condition. This makes De Morgan's fairy tales surprisingly modern: they insist that identity is not fixed but forged through moral choice, and that the distance between human and object, princess and bead, is measured not in magic but in character.
