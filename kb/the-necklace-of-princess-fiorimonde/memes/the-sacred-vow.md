# The Sacred Vow

The sacred vow in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is a promise that bends time around itself. A vow creates an obligation that extends into the future, and the keeping or breaking of that obligation is the narrative event that determines a character's moral standing. De Morgan treats the vow not as a legal contract but as a test of character — the person who keeps their word through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] is the person worthy of a happy ending.

## The Vow Across Seven Years

### Michael's Promise

The collection's most consequential vow is [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s pledge at the beginning of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]." Before departing on his quest, he extracts a promise from [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s mother:

> "But I pray, Queen, that Joan may wait for me for seven years, and if, when they are past, I have not returned, and you have heard nothing of me, you must think that I am dead and gone, and marry her to whom you will, for if I be alive, I will return before then. And till seven years are past remember that Joan is still mine."

This vow establishes the story's temporal structure. Seven years becomes the duration of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s quest and the limit of [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s obligation. The vow binds both parties: [[prince-michael|Michael]] must return within seven years, and [[princess-joan|Joan]] must wait for seven years. When [[prince-michael|Michael]] arrives on the final day of the seventh year, the vow is fulfilled at the last possible moment — De Morgan maximizes the dramatic tension by bringing the vow to the very edge of its breaking.

The Queen honours the vow reluctantly but faithfully, waiting until the seven-year term expires before consenting to [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] to [[king-lambert|Lambert]]. Her [[love-and-loyalty|fidelity]] to the vow's terms — even when she would prefer to break them — marks her as morally upright.

### Joan's Indifference

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s own relationship to the vow is complicated by her [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]. Without her heart, she cannot feel the emotional weight of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s promise:

> "And why do you go? I had thought there would be a grand wedding, and I should have all the gifts that are being prepared for me, and now I shall have nothing; but goodbye, if go you must."

[[princess-joan|Joan]] is not technically breaking the vow — she does not marry [[king-lambert|Lambert]] before the seven years expire — but her indifference to it reveals her enchanted state. The vow means nothing to her because she has been stripped of the capacity to feel loyalty or love. Her restoration makes the vow meaningful retroactively — once her heart is returned, she can feel the gratitude and love that the vow deserved all along.

### Arasmon's Search

"[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" [[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] a vow that extends across decades rather than years. His search is a vow enacted rather than declared, a promise that demonstrates itself through persistence rather than words.

### The Villagers' Vow to Work

In "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]," the villagers' return to productive life after the spell breaks is a kind of renewed vow — a commitment to meaningful work that the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] had dissolved. The vow is to themselves and to their community:

> "The people all felt strong, and able to work again."

The capacity for work is the capacity to keep a vow of purpose — to commit to something beyond immediate comfort.

## The Moral Weight of Promises

De Morgan's treatment of vows is notably egalitarian. Princes and villagers alike are bound by their word, and the narrative judges them by the same standard: did they keep their promise? The vow, in her collection, is the great equalizer — it binds the powerful and the humble with the same moral force, and its keeping or breaking is the event that determines a character's worth.
