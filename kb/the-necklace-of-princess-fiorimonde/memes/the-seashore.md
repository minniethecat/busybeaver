# The Seashore

The seashore appears as a significant setting across several of Mary De Morgan's fairy tales, functioning as a liminal boundary between land and sea, the familiar and the unknown. Characters arrive at the seashore at pivotal moments of transition or desperation.

## In "The Wanderings of Arasmon"

Arasmon and Chrysea, the wandering musicians, travel widely in their journeys, and the seashore features among the diverse places they visit:

> Both were young and lovely, and were as happy as the day was long, for they loved each other dearly, and liked wandering about seeing new countries and people and making sweet music. They went to all sorts of places, sometimes to big cities, sometimes to little villages, sometimes to lonely cottages by the seashore, and sometimes they strolled along the green lanes and fields. [353-P1]

## In "The Heart of Princess Joan"

The seashore serves as a critical crossing point for [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] in his quest to find [[the-black-castle]]. Reaching the water's edge, he must persuade reluctant fishermen to sell him a boat:

> At last he came to the seashore, and in front of him lay a great cold sea, and beyond it he saw no sign of land. But the star shone right over it, and he knew that he must cross, if he still would follow it. [761-P1]

The fishermen warn him that no boat has ever crossed the sea, but Michael persists, becoming the first to attempt the passage.

## In "The Wise Princess"

After leaving [[the-wizards-cave]], the Princess comes to the seashore in a state of spiritual searching. She has just asked the Wizard to teach her how to be happy, and he has confessed he cannot:

> Then the Princess left the cave and wandered down to the seashore. All that night she spent sitting on a rock that jutted out into the sea, watching the wild sky and the moon coming and going behind the clouds. The sea dashed up around her, and the wind blew, but she did not fear them. [1395-P1]

Later, the Princess returns to the seashore after leaving a church, where she witnesses a child struggling in the waves and intervenes to save them.

## Thematic Significance

Across these tales, the seashore represents a place of crossing — between safety and danger, between the known and the mysterious, and between despair and hope. It is at the shoreline that characters must make decisive choices: Arasmon and Chrysea find audiences for their music in seaside cottages; Prince Michael launches his boat into an unknown sea; and the Wise Princess confronts the vastness of nature in her search for happiness.
