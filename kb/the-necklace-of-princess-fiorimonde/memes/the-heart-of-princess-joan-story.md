# The Heart of Princess Joan

"The Heart of Princess Joan" is the third story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. It is a fairy-tale quest narrative structured around the recovery of a stolen heart and the transformation of [[princess-joan|its heroine]] from emotional void to loving woman.

## Plot Summary

The story opens with a proud Queen who "disliked everyone more powerful than herself" and "most of all, she hated the fairy folk." When her daughter [[princess-joan|Joan]] is born, a [[magic-and-witchcraft|fairy]] dressed in yellow appears at the cradle and warns the Queen to guard the child. Three nights later, as the clock strikes twelve, the fairy steals baby [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart — a "tiny bird, with pink, soft feathers" that flutters from the nursery window.

[[princess-joan|Joan]] grows up "the loveliest woman in the land" but utterly without feeling. Nothing troubles her. She never cries. When her dog dies, she laughs. She loves nobody. Her old nurse alone understands: "She is enchanted, but 'tis not her fault."

In a neighbouring kingdom, [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] sees [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s portrait in a wizard's enchanted book and falls in love. He visits her court disguised as a peasant and witnesses her heartlessness firsthand, yet resolves: "I will never marry my [[princess-joan|Joan]] till she loves me as I do her."

[[prince-michael|Michael]] learns from the wizard that [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart has been taken to a distant [[the-royal-castle|castle]] where all stolen hearts are kept. Following a crimson star with a magic glass, he sails across a trackless sea, labours for years as the slave of a gray gnome building a stone wall, waits for a serpent's eggs to hatch, and finally enters the Heart Castle.

Inside, the yellow fairy appears in three forms — a stately dame, a child, and a vision of [[princess-joan|Joan]] herself — each time [[the-exchange-of-gifts|offering]] food and drink to break his vow. [[prince-michael|Michael]] resists all three [[truth-and-deception|deception]]s, and the fairy yields the heart:

> "'Take it,' she cried, 'for well have you won it. Take it, and tell the Queen how many years of toil and labour her proud words and boasting have cost.'"

[[prince-michael|Michael]] returns, aged and in rags, on the very day [[princess-joan|Joan]] is to marry the elderly [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]. Imprisoned in a tower, he speaks the fairy's incantation, the pink heart flies to [[princess-joan|Joan]], and she is transformed. She declares her love for [[prince-michael|Michael]] before all [[the-royal-castle|the court]], and they marry.

## Structure and Form

The story follows a clear three-part structure:

| Part | Content | Duration |
|------|---------|----------|
| I — The Crime | Fairy's appearance, heart's theft, [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heartless childhood | ~16 years |
| II — The Quest | [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s journey, labour for the gnome, three temptations, recovery of the heart | ~7 years |
| III — The Return | [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s imprisonment, [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] procession, the heart's restoration, [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] | ~1 day |

This structure mirrors the classic fairy-tale quest pattern (departure → trials → return) while inverting the usual gender roles: the prince must quest not to kill a dragon but to restore the princess's capacity to love.

## Themes

The story weaves together several of the collection's central themes:

- **[[love-and-loyalty\|Love and Loyalty]]**: [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s seven-year [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]] is the story's moral centre. His love transforms from infatuation with a portrait to the willingness to sacrifice everything.
- **Pride and Humility**: The Queen's [[greed-and-beauty|pride]] — "I will ask no one's help" — is the original sin that costs [[princess-joan|Joan]] her heart. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s humility — labouring as a slave — is what recovers it.
- **Appearance and Reality**: The magic glass reveals what eyes alone cannot see, from the star's true colour to the fairy's disguises.
- **[[the-wedding-feast\|Wedding as Fulfilment]]**: Unlike the title story, where [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]s are traps, this tale ends with a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] that re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] genuine [[marriage-and-union|union]].

## Significance

"The Heart of Princess Joan" is the collection's most elaborate quest narrative. At roughly 6,000 words, it is one of the longer tales and features the [[wealth-and-poverty|riches]]t supernatural apparatus — a wizard's sunbeam lamp, a magic glass, a crimson-guiding star, a gnome, a serpent whose eggs hatch only when touched by tears, and a [[the-royal-castle|castle]] of illusions. The stolen-heart conceit literalises the collection's concern with the nature of [[love-and-loyalty|love]] itself: is love a feeling one has, or an action one performs? [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s answer — seven years of labour — suggests the latter.
