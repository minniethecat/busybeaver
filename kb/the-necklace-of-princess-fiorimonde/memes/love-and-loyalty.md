# Love and Loyalty

Love and loyalty form the moral backbone of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]*. Across all seven tales, De Morgan tests her characters' capacity for faithful devotion — and measures their worth by how they endure separation, [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]], and sacrifice.

## The Testing of Love

De Morgan's tales share a distinctive pattern: love is not celebrated as a [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] but tested as a trial. Three stories in particular embody this pattern:

| Story | Lovers | Trial | Duration | Outcome |
|-------|--------|-------|----------|---------|
| [[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]] | [[arasmon|Arasmon]] and [[chrysea|Chrysea]] | [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is transformed into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]]; [[arasmon|Arasmon]] searches for her without knowing she is beside him | Many years | They are reunited in [[death-and-mortality|death]] |
| [[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]] | [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] and [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] | [[prince-michael|Michael]] must find [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s stolen heart by labouring for a gnome and resisting the yellow fairy | Seven years | They marry |
| [[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] | [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] | [[yolande|Yolande]] is accidentally transformed into a bead; [[gervaise|Gervaise]] must outwit [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] to free her | One night | They marry and live happily |

## Arasmon: Love Beyond Recognition

[[arasmon|Arasmon]] re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the most extreme form of faithful love — devotion to someone whose presence is felt but never recognised. Though [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s voice speaks from the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]]'s strings, crying "[[arasmon|Arasmon]], [[arasmon|Arasmon]], I am here! It is I, [[chrysea|Chrysea]]," he never understands. Yet he never stops searching:

> "I am seeking the whole world round to find my wife Chrysea. It is many many years since I lost her... But now she is gone, and I care for nothing else."

When offered any [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] by the captains whose army he saved, [[arasmon|Arasmon]] asks only for news of [[chrysea|Chrysea]]. This single-minded devotion is both his nobility and his tragedy.

## Prince Michael: Love That Transforms

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s love is active and questing. He refuses to marry [[princess-joan|Joan]] until she can love him back: "I have sworn that I will not marry [[princess-joan|Joan]] till she loves me as I do her." His seven-year quest transforms him physically — his hair turns grey, his shoulders bow, his clothes become rags — but his purpose never wavers.

His loyalty is tested three times inside the Heart Castle, where the yellow fairy appears in three guises (a stately dame, a child, and a vision of [[princess-joan|Joan]] herself) to trick him into breaking his vow. Each time, the wizard's magic glass reveals the [[truth-and-deception|truth]], and [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s loyalty holds.

## Gervaise and Yolande: Love as Cleverness

The love between [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] shows loyalty in a different key — not [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]] across years, but quick-witted courage. [[yolande|Yolande]] risks her life to show [[gervaise|Gervaise]] [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]], and when she is accidentally transformed, [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s grief fuels his determination. His stratagem — using [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s own [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] against her — succeeds where force could not.

Their story ends most happily: "[[gervaise|Gervaise]] married [[yolande|Yolande]], and they went back with [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]] to their home, and all lived happily to the end of their lives."

## Loyalty's Dark Mirror

De Morgan contrasts true loyalty with its absence. [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] has no loyalty to anyone — not to her father the King, not to her suitors, not even to the [[the-spellbound-village|old witch]] who gave her power. Her only attachment is to her own beauty and [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] that preserves it. Her isolation at the story's end — a single bead "that glittered and gleamed in the sunlight" — is the visual emblem of loyalty's absence.

Similarly, [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] before her heart's return cannot feel loyalty because she cannot feel anything. Her mother the Queen's [[greed-and-beauty|pride]] — her refusal to seek anyone's help — is the original sin that costs [[princess-joan|Joan]] her heart and nearly costs her [[prince-michael|Michael]].

## Significance

De Morgan's treatment of love and loyalty reflects Victorian ideals of fidelity while subverting the passive-princess trope. In her tales, love is not a prize won by the strongest suitor but a quality demonstrated through sacrifice and [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]]. The heroes — [[arasmon|Arasmon]], [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]], and [[gervaise|Gervaise]] — succeed not through martial prowess but through constancy, cleverness, and the willingness to give years of life for the beloved.
