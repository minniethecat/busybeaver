# The Magic Water

The magic water in Mary De Morgan's *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]* is the quiet counterpart to the spectacular magic peas. It appears only once, in a single sentence, yet it completes the mechanism that enables Fiorimonde's entire secret life.

## The Return Ritual

After her journey to the witch's mountain, Fiorimonde must erase all evidence of her flight:

> "Then, when she had stepped in at her bedroom window, she poured into a cup from a small black bottle, a few drops of magic water, and gave it to the bird to drink, and as it sipped it grew smaller, and smaller, till at last it had quite regained its natural size, and hopped on to the roof as before." [0073-P1]

Every detail matters. The bottle is "small" and "black" — unobtrusive, easily hidden. The dose is "a few drops" — suggesting potency disproportionate to volume. The bird "sipped" — the verb is gentle, domestic, nothing like the violent growth that preceded it.

## The Rhythm of Transformation

The magic water and [[the-magic-peas|the magic peas]] form a complete cycle: expansion by peas, contraction by water. Fiorimonde's power depends on both. Without the peas, she cannot reach the witch; without the water, the giant bird would be discovered and her secret exposed.

## Concealment

The water's purpose is concealment, not creation. It does not transform the bird into anything new — it returns it to its "natural size." The water is the eraser of magic, the mechanism that allows Fiorimonde to "get into bed, and fell asleep, and no one knew of her strange journey, or where she had been." [0073-P1]

## The Significance of Water

Water in fairy tales is traditionally purifying — it washes away enchantment. But Fiorimonde's magic water inverts this: it hides enchantment, enabling further evil. It is not holy water that breaks a spell but dark water that preserves one. The black bottle is the visual marker of this inversion.
