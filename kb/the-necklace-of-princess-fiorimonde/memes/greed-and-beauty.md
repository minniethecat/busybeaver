# Greed and Beauty

Greed and beauty form the dark moral axis of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection, counterbalancing the redemptive power of [[love-and-loyalty|love and loyalty]]. Where love endures and sacrifices, greed consumes and destroys. Where beauty in the service of love uplifts, beauty cultivated through selfishness becomes a curse. De Morgan's tales systematically expose the hollow core of vanity.

## Fiorimonde: Beauty as Weapon

[[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] is the collection's supreme embodiment of greed allied to beauty. Her loveliness is not natural but purchased — "It was only [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s arts which had made [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] so beautiful that there was no one like her in the world." Her beauty is a tool for power, and she fears [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] because a husband "would stop her practising magic, and then she would lose her beauty."

This is the collection's s[[music-as-art|harp]]est diagnosis: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] does not fear losing love or freedom — she fears losing beauty. Her greed is for admiration, her vanity the engine of her [[evil-as-deception|wickedness]]:

> "She sat before her looking-glass and laughed till tears ran down her cheeks."

When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] tells her he has seen a woman fairer than she, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s reaction is not hurt but rage: "her breast began to heave and her eyes to sparkle with rage, for never before had she heard such a thing said." Her vanity is so extreme that it overrides the [[the-spellbound-village|witch]]'s warning, and she seizes the fatal cord herself.

## The Proud Queen

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s mother in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] a different form of pride — the arrogance of royalty that refuses to acknowledge any power above itself:

> "'I will ask no one's help,' said the Queen haughtily, 'for I am Queen of the land, and can have what I please.'"

This pride is not personal but positional. The Queen "disliked everyone more powerful than herself. And most of all, she hated the fairy folk." Her [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] is inflicted not on herself but on her innocent daughter — the fairy steals [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart not to harm the child but to humble the mother. The Queen's "red eyes from crying" for seven years are the penance pride exacts.

## The Witch's Economy

The [[the-spellbound-village|old witch]] who creates the [[the-beads|golden necklace]] operates on a [[the-fateful-bargain|transaction]]al logic that mirrors [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s own. She grants [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] beauty and power in ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] for secrecy and help with her "tricks." Their relationship is not mentorship but mutual exploitation — each uses the other for her own ends. The witch's warning about [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]]'s danger is not protective but contractual: she does her part; what happens next is [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s responsibility.

## The Village's Bargain

In "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," the spellbound village's misery traces back to a single act of communal greed:

> "One day this terrible old magician came and offered the villagers a great deal of money if they would let him dwell upon the moor; for before that it was covered with golden gorse and heather, and the country folk held all their merrymakings there, but they were tempted with the gold, and sold it."

The sale of common land — a betrayal of community for short-term gain — curses generations. The elves steal eggs, milk, poultry, and corn; houses crumble; no flowers grow. The moral is as plain as a parable: greed poisons what it touches.

## Beauty's Dual Nature

De Morgan does not condemn beauty itself — only beauty divorced from goodness. The collection's moral spectrum maps clearly:

| Character | Beauty | Heart | Outcome |
|-----------|--------|-------|---------|
| [[princess-fiorimonde\|Fiorimonde]] | Supernaturally beautiful | Wicked | Trapped as a bead forever |
| [[princess-joan\|Joan]] (enchanted) | "The loveliest woman in the land" | Stolen — cannot love | Nearly married to a man she could not love |
| [[princess-joan\|Joan]] (restored) | Still beautiful | Returned — now can love | Marries faithfully |
| [[chrysea|Chrysea]] | "Dressed in white and [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]" | Loving and brave | Dies with [[arasmon|Arasmon]], together at last |
| [[yolande\|Yolande]] | "A bright-faced girl with merry brown eyes, but she was not beautiful" | Loyal and courageous | Marries [[gervaise|Gervaise]], lives happily |

The pattern is clear: beauty without a loving heart is dangerous; a loving heart without exceptional beauty is [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed. The truly beautiful who also love — like the restored [[princess-joan|Joan]] — represent the ideal.

## The Gaze and the Mirror

Throughout the collection, mirrors and gazes serve as recurring motifs of vanity. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] "sat before her looking-glass and laughed till tears ran down her cheeks." She uses mirrors not to see herself but to admire herself — the difference between reflection and self-reflection. [[gervaise|Gervaise]], by contrast, sees his aged reflection in a pool and [[despair-and-endurance|despair]]s — his gaze is outward, concerned with how others will perceive him and whether [[princess-joan|Joan]] will recognise her deliverer.

## Significance

Greed and beauty in De Morgan's tales function as a warning against the Victorian cult of feminine beauty. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] is what happens when beauty becomes a woman's sole currency: she will do anything to preserve it, including destroying everyone who might threaten her monopoly. The antidote is not ugliness but [[love-and-loyalty|loyalty]] — the redirection of energy from the self to the beloved.
