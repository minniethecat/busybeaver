# The Golden Sword

The Golden Sword is an enchanted weapon and key in Mary De Morgan's "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]." Hung high upon the wall of the old wizard's hut, accessible only after seven years of faithful labour, the sword serves as both the means of entering the enchanted [[the-royal-castle|castle]] and [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s weapon against the [[truth-and-deception|deception]]s within it.

## Origin

The sword is first revealed to [[prince-michael|Michael]] when the wizard places the iron manacles upon him. Pointing to the wall, the old man indicates the sword and explains its dual nature:

> "That is the key of the castle, and you need only push the doors with its point and they will all fly open; but while your hands are chained you cannot reach it to lift it down, but when the serpent's eggs are hatched your iron rings will fall off, and you yourself may take the sword down from its place, and push your way into the castle." [783-ST04]

The sword's origin before the wizard is not explained; it exists as a fixed element of the trial, a [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] held in plain sight but out of reach.

## Physical Description

The sword is described as "a gleaming [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en sword, the handle of which was set with precious stones" [781-ST04]. It hangs on the wall of the wizard's hut, visible to [[prince-michael|Michael]] throughout his seven years of labour — a constant reminder of both his limitation and his goal. When [[prince-michael|Michael]] finally seizes it, he grasps it "firmly" and draws it "down from its place" [827-ST04], the verb "gleaming" and "shining" suggesting the sword's radiance is undimmed by the years of waiting [781-ST04, 827-ST04].

## Properties / Powers

The Golden Sword possesses two essential properties:

**Key to the Castle.** The sword's primary magical function is as a key. The wizard's instruction is explicit: "push the doors with its point and they will all fly open" [783-ST04]. This proves true when [[prince-michael|Michael]], freed from his chains, uses the sword to enter the [[the-royal-castle|castle]] and progress through its chambers, each door yielding to the sword's point.

**Oath-Bound Weapon.** Before entering the [[the-royal-castle|castle]], the sword becomes the instrument of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s [[the-sacred-vow|oath]]. The narrative emphasizes that [[prince-michael|Michael]] "felt the sword in his hand, and remembered his [[the-sacred-vow|oath]]" when tempted [835-ST04]. The sword thus functions not only as a physical key but as a **mnemonic anchor** — holding the sword reminds [[prince-michael|Michael]] of his vow to speak only his demand ("I seek the heart of [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]]"), to neither eat nor drink within the [[the-royal-castle|castle]], and to trust no illusion. When the final [[truth-and-deception|deception]] comes and the hag appears as [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] [[the-exchange-of-gifts|offering]] wine, [[prince-michael|Michael]] "grasped the sword and sat still, trembling all over" [867-ST04], the sword lending him the resolve to resist.

## Role in the Story

The Golden Sword structures [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s quest around the tension between **seeing and reaching**. For seven years, he can see the sword on the wall but cannot touch it — a physical allegory for his whole situation: [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart is somewhere in the [[the-royal-castle|castle]] he can see across the water, but he cannot reach it until the appointed time.

When the manacles fall and [[prince-michael|Michael]] seizes the sword, the narrative accelerates s[[music-as-art|harp]]ly. The sword grants him passage through the [[the-royal-castle|castle]]'s enchanted doors, and its weight in his hand becomes his anchor through the three temptations of the yellow hag. The sword does not fight for him — [[prince-michael|Michael]] must actively choose to use it, to remember his [[the-sacred-vow|oath]], to push through each door — but it enables every choice he makes.

After the final confrontation, when the hag is defeated and the [[the-royal-castle|castle]]'s illusions dissolve, the sword has served its purpose. The narrative focus shifts to [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s re[[marriage-and-union|union]] with [[princess-joan|Joan]], the sword fading from prominence once [[the-heros-journey|the quest]] it enabled is complete.

## Symbolic Significance

The Golden Sword re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] **deferred agency**. Unlike fairy-tale weapons granted at [[the-heros-journey|the quest]]'s beginning, [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s sword is visible but inaccessible — he must earn the right to wield it. Its position "high up" on the wall literalizes the gap between aspiration and capability, a gap closed only by time, labour, and faithfulness.

The sword's dual identity as both key and weapon is significant. It **opens** before it **fights** — passage comes before combat. This ordering reflects the story's moral architecture: [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s primary battle is not against the yellow hag (whom he never physically fights) but against [[truth-and-deception|deception]], and the sword's true power is keeping him grounded in [[truth-and-deception|truth]] rather than slaying enemies.

In the collection's wider symbolic vocabulary, the Golden Sword contrasts with [[the-gold-necklace]] — one object frees through earned access, the other imprisons through deceptive touch. Gold, in De Morgan's tales, is ambivalent: it can be the metal of liberation or of ensnarement, depending on how it is acquired.
