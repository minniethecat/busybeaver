# The Breaking of Enchantments

The breaking of [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]s in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is the pivotal event toward which each tale builds. It is the moment when magical oppression is reversed, the enchanted are restored, and narrative order is re-established. De Morgan treats disenchantment not as a mechanical reversal — say the right word, break the right object — but as a moral act that requires knowledge, courage, or love to succeed.

## The Necklace Broken

The collection's most elaborate disenchantment occurs in the title story. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en necklace has absorbed twelve victims — eleven suitors and her servant [[yolande|Yolande]] — each transformed into a bead on [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]]. [[gervaise|Gervaise]], the twelfth suitor, approaches with a plan:

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s method reverses [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s own: where she entwined the thread around victims' throats to enchant them, he cuts the thread with a dagger to free them. The breaking is physical and irreversible — once cut, the thread cannot be restrung, and [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s power dissolves with it.

> "'Now,' cried he, 'look, and see King Pierrot who was lost,' and as he spoke he drew from the cord a bead, and King Pierrot, in his royal clothes, with his sword at his side, stood before them."

Each bead restored is a miniature disenchantment, and De Morgan stages them sequentially — eleven restorations, one after another, each building the scene's cumulative power. The twelfth and final bead is [[yolande|Yolande]]:

> "And here is she who has helped to save you all — and he drew off the twelfth bead, and there stood Yolande in her red dress; and when he saw her Gervaise flung away his dagger and took her in his arms, and they wept for joy."

The restoration of [[yolande|Yolande]] is emotionally distinct from the restoration of the suitors — it carries the weight of recognition, gratitude, and love.

## The Heart Restored

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] is not an object to be broken but a vital organ to be restored. [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart has been stolen by a fairy, leaving her incapable of feeling love or [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s seven-year quest culminates in the heart's return, and the breaking of this [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] is instantaneous — the moment the heart is restored to [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s body, she is transformed:

The restoration of the heart is both a physical and an emotional event. [[princess-joan|Joan]] does not merely recover an organ; she recovers her capacity for feeling, and her first authentic emotion — love for [[prince-michael|Michael]] — confirms that the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] is truly broken.

## The Village Freed

"[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" offers a collective disenchantment. The spell that hangs over the village is broken not by a single hero but by the accumulation of awareness — the villagers recognize their discontent as externally imposed rather than internally generated:

> "When morning dawned, and the villagers awoke, all felt that some great change had taken place. The heavy cloud which hung above the village had cleared away; the sun shone brightly, and the sky was blue."

The breaking of this [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] manifests in the natural world — cleared clouds, flowing streams, birdsong — before it manifests in the villagers' psychology. De Morgan suggests that [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] and disenchantment are environmental as much as psychological events; the world itself [[fate-and-transformation|change]]s when a spell is broken.

## The Pattern of Breaking

Across the collection, [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]s are broken by action, not by waiting. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts the thread; [[prince-michael|Michael]] returns the heart; the villagers recognize their condition. Dis[[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] requires agency — someone must do something that reverses the magical harm. This is the event that distinguishes De Morgan's tales from the passive-rescue pattern of some fairy-tale traditions: her heroes earn disenchantment through knowledge, labour, and courage.
