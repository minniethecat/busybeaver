# Justice

Justice in Mary De Morgan's collection operates on two distinct planes: the flawed, self-interested justice of human institutions, and the inexorable poetic justice of the fairy-tale world.

## Satire of Human Justice

"The Bread of Discontent" offers the collection's most sustained satire of legal authority. The chief justice, the story's supposed arbiter of fairness, is the first to succumb to the enchanted bread's discontent:

> "The first person who tasted the bread was the chief justice. He came down to breakfast in high spirits, for he had just heard that an old aunt was dead, and had left him a great deal of money." [1081-P1]

The justice's greed undermines his authority — he is no impartial judge but a man pleased by inheritance, who later sentences the baker to flogging without evidence:

> "'Let him be taken to the marketplace, and I will order that he be publicly flogged by the soldiers.'" [1163-P1]

## The Mob as False Justice

The baker is dragged before the justice not through due process but through mob action:

> "So they seized the baker and dragged him away in spite of himself, and as they pulled him through the village the people thronged about them." [1155-P1]

De Morgan demonstrates how easily "justice" becomes collective persecution when discontent has poisoned the community.

## Poetic Justice

In contrast to the failures of human law, the collection's fairy-tale logic delivers poetic justice with precision. [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]], who enchants others into beads, becomes a bead herself:

> "He knew that the thirteenth was the wicked Princess, who had herself met the evil fate she had prepared for so many others." [0321-P1]

This is justice without courts — the villain is destroyed by her own mechanism.
