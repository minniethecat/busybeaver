# Evil as Deception

In Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*, evil consistently operates through the gap between appearance and reality. Witches wear the masks of beautiful women, villains speak fair words, and the most dangerous threat is not what is obviously monstrous but what appears lovely. De Morgan's moral universe insists that evil's truest form requires a second sight — a magic glass, a servant's clear eye, or the reader's growing suspicion.

## Overview

De Morgan's treatment of evil is notable for its psychological sophistication. Evil is not an external force that assails innocent victims from without; it is always mediated through [[truth-and-deception|deception]]. Three patterns recur across the collection. First, **concealed ugliness** — [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s "evil yellow face and evil yellow eyes" hide behind a "finely-dressed lady" ([[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]) and [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s radiant beauty conceals her [[magic-and-witchcraft|sorcery]]. Second, **self-destructive evil** — the wicked are ultimately undone by their own devices: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] becomes the thirteenth bead on her own necklace, [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s own chains bind her. Third, **revelation through [[truth-and-deception|truth]]-telling objects** — magic mirrors, glasses, and honest servants ([[yolande|Yolande]]) expose what appearances conceal.

The collection's moral architecture is unambiguous: evil is always discoverable, but only by those willing to look past surfaces.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Evil Wears a Crown

[[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] is the collection's most fully realized portrait of evil-as-beauty. She is introduced as "so beautiful that everyone thought she must be good as well, instead of which the Princess was really very wicked." The narrative's opening sentence establishes the gap between seeming and being that defines evil throughout the collection.

The witch's necklace embodies evil's seductive logic — it transforms princes into bright beads, making destruction beautiful. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s own end enacts the collection's moral of self-consumed evil:

> "Gervaise bent down over the necklace as it lay upon the grass, and, with a smile, counted thirteen beads; and he knew that the thirteenth was the wicked Princess, who had herself met the evil fate she had prepared for so many others."

The symmetry is exact: evil prepared for others returns upon the evildoer. De Morgan's [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] is poetic in its precision.

The story also encodes evil's claim to fearless invulnerability as its fatal weakness. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s arrogance is her undoing:

> "In truth I may sleep in peace, and fear nothing."

This declaration of fearlessness, occurring moments before [[gervaise|Gervaise]] tricks her into seizing her own necklace, reveals De Morgan's insight that evil's confidence in its own safety is precisely what makes it vulnerable.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Yellow Hag's Many Faces

[[prince-michael|Michael]] confronts evil in its most protean form through the yellow witch who shifts between appearances. He first encounters her as a "grand lady," then as a "seeming child." Only the magic glass reveals the [[truth-and-deception|truth]]:

> "Michael took from his bosom the magic glass and looked through it, and saw no lovely child, but the same yellow hag with shrivelled face and evil eyes."

The repetition of the phrase "evil yellow face and evil yellow eyes" across multiple scenes establishes [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s essential, unchanging nature beneath her changing masks. Evil in De Morgan's world is not protean in essence — only in appearance. The underlying reality is fixed and recognizable.

The yellow hag's persistence — she returns in three different guises — suggests evil's tirelessness. [[prince-michael|Michael]] must repeatedly deploy the magic glass; one revelation is never enough. Evil adapts, reappears, and must be recognized anew each time.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Dwarf's Moral Clarity

In contrast to [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s [[truth-and-deception|deception]], the misshapen dwarf who becomes [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s ally offers an inversion of the appearance-reality gap: what looks monstrous is actually good. De Morgan thus complicates her own pattern — the relationship between surface and substance runs both ways, and the reader who assumes ugliness signals evil makes the same error as those deceived by beauty.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's evil-as-[[truth-and-deception|deception]] motif draws on the fairy-tale tradition of disguised witches (the Grimm brothers' "Hansel and Gretel," Perrault's "Sleeping Beauty") while innovating through psychological interiority. Unlike the external witches of folklore, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] is a protagonist whose inner life the narrative invites us to understand even as we condemn her choices. This interiority aligns De Morgan's tales with the Victorian novel's interest in moral psychology, distinguishing her fairy tales from the flatter characterizations of her folkloric sources.
