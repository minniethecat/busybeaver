# Hope

Hope in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is not passive optimism but a force that propels characters through repeated disappointment. De Morgan treats hope as morally neutral — it can sustain the faithful or blind the foolish.

## Hope Deferred

The title story's central dramatic rhythm is hope raised and dashed. Each suitor arrives with hope, and each vanishes. The old King embodies this pattern:

> "The old King was sadly frightened, and would fain have given up all hope of finding a husband for the Princess, but now she implored him, with tears in her eyes, not to let her be disgraced in this way." [0147-P1]

Fiorimonde manipulates hope itself — she renews it in her father so that more suitors will come, knowing that each new hope will end in disappearance.

## Hope as Self-Deception

The court itself colludes in hopeful thinking. When Gervaise arrives:

> "When they reached the court, the old King received them and welcomed them warmly, and he said to his courtiers, 'Here is a fine young prince to whom we would gladly see our daughter wed. Let us hope that this time all will be well.'" [0161-P1]

The phrase "this time" reveals the pattern — hope persists not because evidence supports it but because the alternative is unthinkable. De Morgan shows community-wide hope as a form of collective denial.

## Hope Against Evidence

[[yolande|Yolande]]'s warning is the collection's clearest statement against hope founded on illusion:

> "'Then, I hope you have said goodbye to him well, for be assured you shall never see him again,' said Yolande nodding her head." [0191-P1]

Yolande uses "I hope" ironically — she knows the fate of the suitors and cannot share the court's willful optimism. Her hope is for honest acknowledgment, not false comfort.

## Hope Rewarded

The collection does not reject hope entirely. In "The Heart of Princess Joan," the King expresses parental hope:

> "'All is arranged for you to journey in state to her father's court and bring back your bride, so now I hope that you are happy and wish for nothing more.'" [0699-P1]

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s hope, unlike Fiorimonde's suitors', is built on sacrifice and perseverance. His hope is rewarded after seven years of trial — establishing De Morgan's pattern that hope tied to virtue succeeds where hope tied to vanity fails.

## The Pessimist's Paradox

In "The Bread of Discontent," De Morgan inverts the hope theme:

> "'The best thing we can hope for,' said the husband, sighing, 'is for some great misfortune to befall us; then we should be all right, for we should know then, that we knew the worst that could come.'" [1113-P1]

This is hope reduced to absurdity — the husband hopes for disaster because only then can he stop hoping. De Morgan suggests that the real burden is not misfortune but the suspense of waiting for it.
