# The Gold Thread

The Gold Thread is the fundamental magical component of [[the-beads|the enchanted necklace]] in "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]." It is introduced early in the story as [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s gift to [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]].

## Creation

The thread is produced when "[[the-old-witch|the witch]] dipped her hand into a black bag which stood on the ground beside her, and drew out a long [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread." The black bag re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s store of magical materials and serves as a classic fairy-tale motif of hidden magical power.

## Physical Properties

The [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread possesses remarkable physical characteristics:
- Its ends are "joined together, but no one could see the joins"
- It is unbreakable — "however much you pulled, it would not break"
- It is described as "light as a feather, but stronger than an iron chain"
- It fits easily over [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s head

## The Trigger Mechanism

The thread's magic is activated by a specific physical action: closing one's fingers around the string. Mere touch or wearing is safe — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] "caressed the bead with the tips of her soft, white fingers, but was careful that they did not close round the string." The witch's warning is explicit:

> "while this hangs here you are safe enough, but if once you join your fingers around the string you too will meet the fate of your lovers, and hang upon it yourself."

## Breaking the Spell

The spell can only be broken by cutting the thread while the wearer sleeps. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] accomplishes this in the story's climax, and as the thread is severed, all twelve trapped victims are restored to their human forms, while [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] herself becomes the final bead.

## Folklore Context

Unbreakable threads and cords appear across fairy tale traditions — from the Norse Gleipnir that bound Fenrir to the Greek thread of Ariadne. De Morgan adapts this motif with a distinctive twist: the thread does not bind physically but transforms, making it a tool not of restraint but of [[fate-and-transformation|metamorphosis]], imprisonment, and ultimately poetic [[punishment-and-reward|justice]].
