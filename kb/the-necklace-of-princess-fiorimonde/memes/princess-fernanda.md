# Princess Fernanda

Princess Fernanda is the protagonist of "[[the-wise-princess-story|The Wise Princess]]," the penultimate story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. Unlike the collection's more conventional fairy-tale heroines, Fernanda is defined not by a romantic quest but by an insatiable hunger for knowledge — and ultimately, for the secret of [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] itself.

## Character Overview

Princess Fernanda is the only daughter of a widowed King. From childhood she is distinguished by relentless curiosity:

> "Your Highness should not wish to know too much," they told her, whereat Princess Fernanda threw up her little head, and said, "I want to know everything."

As she grows, she masters "every language and every science" through conventional tutors, yet declares "It is not enough; I want to know more." Unlike the passive princesses typical of fairy tales, Fernanda actively seeks out knowledge on her own terms — sneaking out at night to study under a Wizard in a deep cave.

## Role in the Story

### The Wizard's Pupil

Fernanda seeks out the Wizard, an ancient sage whose face is "well-nigh black with wrinkles" and who has "nothing left for him to learn." She enters his cave alone at night, wearing a "blue and silver robe" with "bright hair unbound." When the Wizard asks why she wishes for knowledge, she replies:

> "I am not happy now. Teach me and you shall find me an apt pupil."

She studies with him every night for three years until he declares: "You are as wise as I." Her learning grants her power over nature — the language of animals, command of wind and sea, the ability to "turn her enemies to stone." Yet "when she smiled, her lips were very sad, and her eyes were always full of care."

### The Search for Happiness

A year after completing her studies, Fernanda returns to the Wizard and kneels before him:

> "You have taught me much, yet I am ignorant of one thing — teach me that also — *how to be happy*."

The Wizard confesses he cannot teach what he does not know himself. Fernanda's subsequent quest to learn [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] unfolds through a series of encounters, each reflecting De Morgan's moral philosophy that [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] cannot be taught but must be found through action and sacrifice.

She questions the skylark ("God is so good. The sky is so blue"), her lapdog ("I have plenty to eat, and a soft cushion"), and her maid Doris (who wants only a new hat) — none can teach her. At the village, a mother nursing her baby is "very happy" yet still worries about her husband at sea. In a church, Fernanda envies a dead soldier whose "peaceful" smile suggests he learned what she cannot.

### Death and Fulfillment

The story's climax is Fernanda's act of self-sacrifice. Seeing a child drowning in the sea, she plunges into the waves and saves him, though the currents overwhelm her. As she struggles, she sees Death approaching:

> "Now," said he, clasping her in his arms, "I will teach you all you want to know."

She dies smiling — the first genuine smile of her life. Her body is found "with her face white and her lips cold, but smiling as they had never smiled before." The Wizard, looking upon her dead face, reflects: "Yet I think now she must be wiser than I, and have learned that too. For see how she smiles."

- **The Wizard** — Her teacher, who knows all worldly wisdom but cannot teach [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]]
- **Her Father the King** — A widowed ruler who worries about his daughter's un[[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]]
- **[[princess-joan]]** — Another De Morgan princess-heroine, who finds fulfillment through love rather than knowledge
- **[[princess-fiorimonde]]** — The collection's antagonist princess, representing the dark inversion of female power that Fernanda embodies positively

## Significance

Princess Fernanda re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] one of De Morgan's most philosophically ambitious character studies. Her story inverts the conventional fairy-tale quest: instead of seeking a husband or a throne, she seeks knowledge and, failing that, [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]]. The story is a meditation on the limits of intellect — the Wizard possesses all knowledge yet remains unhappy, suggesting that wisdom alone is insufficient. Fernanda's [[death-and-mortality|death]] is not a [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] but a completion, the one lesson that could not be taught in a cave of books, requiring instead an act of selfless courage.

Her characterization also subverts the "curious woman" trope common in fairy tales (cf. Bluebeard's wife). Where curiosity typically leads to transgression and [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]], Fernanda's desire to "know everything" is portrayed as noble. Her tragedy is not that she sought knowledge but that knowledge alone could not fulfill her.
