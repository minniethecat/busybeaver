# The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde (Story)

"The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" is the title story and opening tale of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde and Other Stories*. It is the longest and most developed narrative in the collection.

## Plot Summary

[[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]], the beautiful but wicked daughter of a widowed King, has secretly learned [[magic-and-witchcraft|witchcraft]] from an old witch living on a lonely mountain. Her beauty is itself a product of magic. When her father decides she must marry, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] is enraged — a husband would discover her [[magic-and-witchcraft|witchcraft]] and she would lose her beauty.

The witch gives her a magical [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread that transforms anyone who grasps it into a bead. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] uses this to eliminate eleven suitors one by one, each [[fate-and-transformation|becoming]] a jewel on her ever-growing necklace. She performs elaborate public grief after each disappearance while privately laughing at her victims.

The twelfth suitor is [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]], who brings his loyal friend [[gervaise|Gervaise]]. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] learns the [[truth-and-deception|truth]] from [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s maid [[yolande|Yolande]], who has been watching [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] grow. Together they plan to expose [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]], but [[yolande|Yolande]] is accidentally transformed into the twelfth bead.

In the climax, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread while [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] sleeps. The twelve victims are restored to life, and [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] herself — who grasps the cord in her sleep — becomes the thirteenth and final bead.

- **[[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]]** — The first transformed suitor

## Themes

The story explores the danger of judging by appearances ([[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s beauty masks her [[evil-as-deception|evil]]), the corrupting nature of [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] and power, and the redemptive power of loyalty and courage (embodied by [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]]). It subverts the conventional fairy-tale trope of the beautiful, virtuous princess.

## Literary Context

Published in 1880, De Morgan's tale belongs to the Victorian fairy tale tradition alongside the works of George MacDonald, Oscar Wilde, and Juliana Horatia Ewing. It is notable for its dark, psychologically complex female antagonist — a significant departure from the passive princesses of traditional folklore.
