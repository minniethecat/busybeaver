# Prince Hildebrandt

Prince Hildebrandt is the second suitor — and second victim — of [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] in the title story of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection.

## Character Overview

Prince Hildebrandt is introduced as the successor to the vanished [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]]. He comes from "a country far north, of which one day he would be king." His physical description contrasts with the dark-haired [[king-pierrot|Pierrot]]:

> "He was tall, and fair, and strong, with flaxen hair and bright blue eyes."

When [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] sees his portrait, she is "much pleased" and eagerly agrees to the match, even putting off her mourning clothes. Unlike [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]] — whose disappearance prompted elaborate public grief — Hildebrandt's arrival marks the point where "[[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]] was quite forgotten."

## Role in the Story

Prince Hildebrandt arrives with "many fine gentlemen" bearing beautiful [[the-exchange-of-gifts|gifts]]. The evening of his arrival features a grand feast where [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] looks so beautiful that Hildebrandt "was [[joy-as-emotion|delight]]ed." This time, however, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] takes a different approach to her prey — she does not leave her father's side all evening, appearing the picture of propriety.

The trap is sprung at sunrise. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] rises early, dresses in a plain white gown, and goes to [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] gardens. Beneath Hildebrandt's window, she sings "a little song as sweet and [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]ous as a lark's":

> "When Prince Hildebrandt heard it he got up and went to the window and looked out to see who sang, and when he saw Fiorimonde standing in the red sunrise-light, which made her hair look gold, and her face rosy, he made haste to dress himself and go down to meet her."

This sunrise seduction scene is one of the story's most painterly passages — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] weaponizing not just her beauty but the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en light itself. Hildebrandt, like [[king-pierrot|Pierrot]] before him, vanishes, [[fate-and-transformation|becoming]] the second bead on [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]]. The King, now seeing a pattern, fears "a plot against us."

## Rescue

Hildebrandt is freed when [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]]. He is the second to be restored: "[[gervaise|Gervaise]] had drawn off another bead, and King Hildebrandt appeared." He joins the other rescued suitors in denouncing [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[evil-as-deception|wickedness]].

## Key Relationships

- **[[princess-fiorimonde]]** — His would-be bride who transforms him into a bead
- **[[king-pierrot]]** — The first suitor, whose fate Hildebrandt unknowingly repeats
- **[[gervaise]]** — The hero who eventually frees him
- **The Other Suitors** — Fellow victims including King Adrian, Prince Sigbert, and Prince Algar

## Significance

Prince Hildebrandt's capture establishes the pattern: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s suitors are not killed by violence but by their own hands — grasping [[the-gold-necklace|the gold cord]] at her invitation. His story also marks [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s evolving technique: the sunrise garden serenade is more calculated than her moonlit manipulation of [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]], showing her growing confidence in her powers. The rapid forgetting of [[king-pierrot|Pierrot]] — replaced by Hildebrandt within a month — underscores [[the-royal-castle|the court]]'s shallow relationship with [[truth-and-deception|truth]], a recurring theme in De Morgan's social critique.
