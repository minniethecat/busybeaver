# The Wise Princess

"The Wise Princess" is the eighth and final tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. It serves as the collection's philosophical capstone, telling the story of [[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]], who achieves perfect knowledge but finds that wisdom alone cannot confer [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]].

## Plot Summary

[[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]], daughter of a widowed King, vexes her ladies from childhood by asking questions about everything. "I want to know everything," she declares. She masters every language and science under tutors, but still says "it is not enough."

She secretly seeks out an ancient Wizard living in a deep cave, whose face is "well-nigh black with wrinkles" from a lifetime of study. For three years she visits him nightly, learning all his magic and knowledge — the languages of animals, control of winds and seas, the power to turn enemies to stone or bestow [[wealth-and-poverty|wealth]].

When the three years end, the Wizard tells her she knows as much as he. But the Princess finds herself weary and sad. A year later she returns to the cave and kneels before him: "Teach me also — _how to be happy_." The Wizard replies with a mournful smile: "I cannot teach you that, for I do not know it myself. Go and ask it of them who know and are wiser than I."

[[princess-fernanda|Fernanda]] wanders to the seashore and, understanding the language of birds, asks a skylark why it sings. "I sing because I am so happy," the lark replies. Through a series of encounters with creatures and simple folk, the Princess discovers that [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] comes not from knowledge or power but from love, simplicity, and connection with others.

## Key Characters

- **[[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]]** — The protagonist, whose pursuit of total knowledge leads to the discovery that wisdom has limits
- **The Wizard** — A figure of immense learning who confesses his own ignorance of [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]]
- **The Skylark** — The first creature to teach [[princess-fernanda|Fernanda]] about [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] beyond knowledge

## Themes

"The Wise Princess" functions as De Morgan's thematic conclusion to the collection. Where earlier stories warn against [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]], greed, and magical [[truth-and-deception|deception]], this final tale asks what, if anything, can make life worth living. [[princess-fernanda|Fernanda]] is De Morgan's most sympathetic protagonist — not wicked or foolish, but genuinely seeking the good, only to find that her methods (knowledge, magic, power) are the wrong tools.

The story inverts the traditional fairy-tale quest: instead of seeking a magical object or defeating a villain, the heroine seeks an answer that magic cannot provide. The Wizard's confession — "I cannot teach you that, for I do not know it myself" — is one of the most memorable lines in the collection, crystallizing the tale's central insight that wisdom and [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] are separate pursuits.

As the closing story, it leaves the reader with a gentle moral: knowledge without love is incomplete, and the simplest creatures may be wiser than the wisest wizards.
