# Despair and Endurance

Despair in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is not a failure of character but a station on the path to transformation. Characters who reach the point of abso[[music-as-art|lute]] hopelessness — [[prince-michael|Michael]] in chains, [[arasmon|Arasmon]] in his grief, the spellbound villagers in their cursed prosperity — face a choice that defines their narrative arc: to be consumed by despair or to endure through it. De Morgan's moral psychology treats despair as a crucible rather than a condemnation.

## Overview

Despair operates in three narrative patterns across the collection. First, **despair as the darkness before dawn** — characters reach a nadir of hopelessness immediately before the resolution of their trials ([[prince-michael|Michael]]'s "of what avail were all my years of toil" precedes his final victory). Second, **despair as a test of endurance** — the duration of suffering, not its intensity, is what threatens to break characters. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s despair accumulates across "more than a year" of labour; it is time, not pain, that erodes [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]. Third, **despair as a narrative threshold** — characters must pass through despair to reach transformation. Those who bypass it (the soldiers who counsel surrender rather than endure loss) are denied the [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]s that endurance brings.

De Morgan's treatment of despair is notably unsentimental. She does not minimize suffering or offer easy comfort; she insists that despair is real, that endurance is costly, and that the choice to continue is precisely what makes the subsequent transformation meaningful.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Anatomy of Despair

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s ordeal in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" provides the collection's most detailed anatomy of despair. The narrative traces his psychological deterioration across distinct stages — from [[hope-as-resilience|hope]] through fear, frustration, and finally despair:

> "But the days passed, and the serpent never moved from its eggs, and Michael's heart began to be oppressed with fear."

The fear grows into active despair across multiple scenes. The chains that bind him become symbols of psychological as well as physical entrapment:

> "He rose and went into the cottage, and looked longingly at the sword which hung high up, on the walls, and raised his arms to try and reach it, but the chains held him down, and as he turned from it in despair he saw the old man standing in the doorway watching him with his cold dull eyes."

The old man's "cold dull eyes" represent despair's external gaze — the world that watches suffering without responding. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s despair crystallizes into direct address:

> "And when will the serpent's eggs be hatched?" cried Michael in despair.

The question receives no answer. De Morgan refuses to provide comfort at this moment; the despair must be fully inhabited before it can be transcended.

The crisis arrives in the story's climactic cry:

> "Of what avail were all my years of toil, and for what am I gray and old before my time," he cried, "if after all, when I have earned that for which I worked so long, I may not give it to Joan, but must remain a prisoner and see her pass by to marry someone else?"

This is despair's fullest articulation — not merely suffering but the sense that suffering has been meaningless. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s "years of toil" and premature aging ("gray and old before my time") represent the accumulated investment that despair threatens to render worthless. The cry is not for relief from pain but for meaning — for the assurance that endurance has a purpose.

The structural position of this cry — immediately before the resolution — is De Morgan's narrative argument. Despair, fully articulated and fully inhabited, is the necessary prelude to transformation.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Quiet Despair

[[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s despair differs from [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s in tone but not in depth. His grief for [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is not shouted but carried:

> "I have lost my only wealth, my wife Chrysea. It was just here she stood and sang, and now I shall never see her again or hear her singing."

The quietness of this despair — its absorption into daily life rather than dramatic outburst — re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] a different mode of endurance. [[arasmon|Arasmon]] continues to wander, to search, to remember. His despair does not paralyze him; it becomes the weather of his existence, and his endurance consists not in overcoming it but in living through it.

### "The Bread of Discontent" — Comic Despair

The baker offers a comic counterpoint to the collection's more dramatic treatments of despair:

> "The best thing we can hope for," said the husband, sighing, "is for some great misfortune to befall us; then we should be all right, for we should know then, that we knew the worst that could come."

This is despair turned inside out — the wish for catastrophe as relief from the anxiety of uncertainty. De Morgan's comic register allows her to treat despair without melodrama, suggesting that the same psychological structure that produces [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s heroic endurance also produces the baker's absurd wish.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's treatment of despair as transformative crucible aligns her with the Romantic tradition that valued intensity of feeling over its pleasantness. Wordsworth's "the still, sad music of humanity" and Keats's "vale of soul-making" both inform the sensibility that treats suffering as formative rather than merely painful. De Morgan's innovation is to give this Romantic structure a fairy-tale form in which despair is both psychologically real and narratively functional — the emotional low point that the genre's logic of transformation requires.
