# Joy

Joy in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is not a steady state of happiness but a sudden, transformative eruption — a physical and emotional release that signals the breaking of an enchantment, the return of the lost, or the triumph of perseverance.

## Joy as Restoration

The collection's most concentrated expressions of joy occur when what was lost is restored. In the title story, when [[gervaise|Gervaise]] breaks the necklace and frees [[yolande|Yolande]]:

> "He drew off the twelfth bead, and there stood Yolande in her red dress; and when he saw her Gervaise flung away his dagger and took her in his arms, and they wept for joy." [0335-P1]

Weeping — not smiling — is De Morgan's primary marker of authentic joy. The emotion is so overwhelming it cannot be contained.

## Joy as Collective Awakening

When the spellbound village is freed in "The Wanderings of Arasmon," joy spreads through the entire community:

> "The heavy cloud which hung above the village had cleared away; the sun shone brightly, and the sky was blue; streams which had been dry for years, were running clear and fresh... The villagers looked from one to another and said, 'Surely the spell is broken;' and they wept for joy." [0413-P1]

Joy here is collective and elemental — tied to sunlight, water, and birdsong — as if the natural world itself participates in the celebration.

## Joy After Long Trial

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s seven-year quest climaxes in public vindication:

> "When the people heard her, they cried, 'It is Prince Michael, who went seven years ago, and who we all thought was dead, and he is returned in time to marry our Princess.' And King and Queen and people laughed for joy." [0961-P1]

This joy is earned through suffering — the seven years of wandering transform joy from a simple emotion into a hard-won reward.

## Joy as Natural Expression

De Morgan also presents joy as the instinctive response of uncorrupted beings. In "The Wise Princess," a skylark explains:

> "A skylark rose from the fields and flew straight up to heaven, singing as though his heart would burst with pure joy." [1395-P1]

The lark's joy is uncomplicated by human desire or ambition — it is the pure response to existence itself.
