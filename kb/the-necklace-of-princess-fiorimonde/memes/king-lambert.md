# King Lambert

King Lambert is the primary antagonist of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the third story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. He re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the threat of mercenary [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] that De Morgan critiques throughout the collection — an old king who seeks to wed a young princess not for love but to expand his kingdom.

## Character Overview

King Lambert is introduced late in the story as the foreign king to whom [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] is being forced to marry. He is described in unflattering terms by the country folk and by the narrator:

> "Tomorrow she is to be married to old King Lambert, and the wedding will be very grand, but none of the country folk like it, for he is old and ugly, and they say he does not love her at all, but only marries her that he may be king of this country as well as his own."

His physical description reinforces his villainy. When [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] sees him from the prison window:

> "On the other rode an ugly old man, whom Michael guessed to be King Lambert, and he smiled and bowed to the people, but they muttered and grumbled, when they looked at him, and saw how ugly and wicked he looked."

## Role in the Story

### The Forced Marriage

King Lambert's betrothal to [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] has been arranged against her will and that of the Queen, who "for seven years refused her consent." The [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] is politically motivated — Lambert already rules his own kingdom and seeks [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s hand to gain sovereignty over her father's realm as well. The Queen is described as "in sore distress," her eyes "red with crying" on [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] day.

### The Wedding Day Confrontation

The climactic confrontation occurs during [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] procession. [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]], hearing [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s voice from the prison, declares her [[love-and-loyalty|true love]] publicly, and the people rally behind [[prince-michael|Michael]]. King Lambert attempts to assert his claim:

> "'Twas in vain for King Lambert to rage, and cry that the Princess was betrothed to him."

The Queen delivers the decisive rebuttal:

> "Nay! She has been pledged to Prince Michael for seven years. We are grieved for your sake, King Lambert, but we cannot break our royal word."

### Defeat

King Lambert is defeated not through violence but through the assertion of a prior and more legitimate claim — [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s seven-year [[the-sacred-vow|pledge]], earned through toil and sacrifice, outweighs Lambert's political maneuvering. The people's [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] at his defeat ("King and Queen and people laughed for [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]") underscores the fairy tale's moral: love earned through [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]] triumphs over power secured through politics.

## Key Relationships

- **[[princess-joan]]** — The princess he attempts to marry for territorial gain
- **[[prince-michael]]** — His rival, whose return after seven years of labor defeats Lambert's claim
- **The Queen** — [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s mother, who "for seven years refused her consent" to the [[marriage-and-union|marriage]]
- **The People** — Who "muttered and grumbled" at the sight of him

## Significance

King Lambert functions as a stock fairy-tale villain — the unwanted suitor — but De Morgan gives the trope political weight. His motivation is explicitly territorial: he marries "that he may be king of this country as well as his own." This critique of dynastic [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] as a form of conquest reflects De Morgan's broader social commentary on the commodification of women in aristocratic society.

His defeat is significant for what it does not involve: no violence, no magic, no duel. [[prince-michael|Michael]] wins not by fighting Lambert but by having earned [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s love through sacrifice. This aligns with the collection's recurring theme that genuine bonds — whether of love, loyalty, or labor — are stronger than political arrangements.

Lambert's role also highlights the agency (or lack thereof) of [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]]. Though she is the object of contention between two men, [[princess-joan|Joan]] ultimately exercises her voice in the climactic scene, publicly declaring her love for [[prince-michael|Michael]], a moment that empowers her beyond the passive princess archetype.
