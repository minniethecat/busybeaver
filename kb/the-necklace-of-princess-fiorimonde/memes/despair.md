# Despair

Despair in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is the lowest point of the hero's journey — the moment when hope appears exhausted. But De Morgan treats despair as purifying rather than terminal. It strips away illusion and prepares the character for the final effort that succeeds.

## The Physical Toll of Despair

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s despair is measured in bodily deterioration:

> "His hands had grown hard and brown and cracked, with working at the heavy stones, and his face and neck were blistered and sunburnt with the fierce sun that beat upon them as he worked. His clothes were cut and torn and soiled, and yet he seemed to be no nearer entering the castle. Then he rose and went into the cottage, and looked longingly at the sword which hung high up, on the walls, and raised his arms to try and reach it, but the chains held him down, and as he turned from it in despair he saw the old man standing in the doorway watching him with his cold dull eyes." [0797-P1]

Despair is not just felt — it is visible. Michael's sunburnt face and cracked hands are the material evidence of his emotional state.

## Despair Cries Out

Michael's repeated cries of despair mark the rhythm of his trial:

> "'And when will the serpent's eggs be hatched?' cried Michael in despair." [0801-P1]

> "Time passed, but there came no change. Michael despaired in his heart, but he could not have escaped even if he would, because of the chains which hung from his arms." [0805-P1]

The chains are both literal and metaphorical — despair has the quality of imprisonment. Michael cannot escape his trial, but the fact that he persists through despair is what makes him worthy of Joan.

## Despair at the Threshold

The darkest moment comes just before deliverance:

> "Then at last he gave way to despair. 'Of what avail were all my years of toil, and for what am I gray and old before my time,' he cried, 'if after all, when I have earned that for which I worked so long, I may not give it to Joan?' and he threw himself on the ground and cried aloud." [0919-P1]

The phrase "at last he gave way" implies that despair is a barrier that Michael had been holding back through effort — and that surrender to it is the final stage before rescue.

## Despair's Hidden Purpose

De Morgan's narrative logic makes despair functional. It is the crucible that proves the hero's worth. Characters who never despair — like Fiorimonde's suitors, who vanish before they can suffer — are never transformed. Despair, in this moral economy, is the price of redemption.
