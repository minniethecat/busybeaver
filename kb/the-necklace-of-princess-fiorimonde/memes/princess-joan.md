# Princess Joan

Princess Joan is the heroine of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the third tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]*. She is a princess under a cruel fairy [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] — her heart has been stolen, rendering her incapable of feeling [[love-and-loyalty|love]], pity, or sorrow.

## Character Overview

Joan is described as "the loveliest woman in the land" with "yellow hair [that] fell to her waist, her face was like a blush rose, and her eyes were blue as forget-me-nots." Yet her outward beauty conceals an inward void: she has no heart with which to love. The [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] was cast when Joan was an infant, as revenge against her [[princess-fiorimonde|proud]] mother the Queen, who had scorned the [[the-spellbound-village|fairy folk]].

## The Stolen Heart

When Joan was a baby, a [[magic-and-witchcraft|fairy]] dressed in yellow appeared at her cradle and warned the haughty Queen to guard the child. On the third night, as the clock struck twelve:

> "'Twixt the strokes of the clock she heard a faint whistling outside the window... At last the little one gave one scream louder than all the others and then lay quite still, and at that moment the Queen saw something flutter across the room like a tiny bird, with pink, soft feathers."

The fairy had stolen Joan's heart, hiding it in a distant [[the-royal-castle|castle]] guarded by a gray gnome, where all stolen hearts were kept. The wizard later tells [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]: "A fairy has stolen her heart, and that is why she loves no one, and can feel no sorrow; she has no heart with which to love or pity, and till it is found and restored to her, she will be hard and cold as stone."

## Heartless Behaviour

Without her heart, Joan's actions reveal the chilling absence of empathy:

| Incident | Her Response |
|----------|-------------|
| Her little dog dies | She "laughed outright" |
| Her father goes to war | "It did not grieve her" |
| A maid's father breaks his leg | "Why should you cry? 'Tis your father and not you that is hurt" |
| Her horse dies from over-riding | "See quickly and get me another" |
| [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] leaves for seven years | "I had thought there would be a grand [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]... and now I shall have nothing" |

As the old nurse observes: "She is enchanted, but 'tis not her fault."

## Prince Michael's Quest

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]], having seen Joan's portrait in the wizard's book and then meeting her in person, refuses to marry her until she can love him. He embarks on a seven-year quest to recover her stolen heart, following a crimson star across land and sea to the Heart Castle. His journey requires:
- Years of slave labour building a wall for the gray gnome
- Waiting for a serpent's eggs to hatch
- Resisting the yellow fairy's three temptations in the [[the-royal-castle|castle]]
- Maintaining his vow to speak only of his quest and accept no food or drink

## The Heart's Return

When [[prince-michael|Michael]] finally returns, old and grey from his labours, he finds Joan about to marry the elderly [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]. From his prison window, he releases the pink heart with the fairy's incantation:

> "Heart of Joan / Lost and won / Fly back home, / Thy journey's done."

As the heart reaches Joan, she is instantly transformed. She cries out for [[prince-michael|Michael]], pushes her arms through the prison bars to embrace him, and declares: "How gray and worn you are now. How hard you must have laboured for me through these long years. Now, how shall I pay you, save by loving you all my life!"

## Significance

Princess Joan is a study in the nature of empathy itself. Her [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] literalises the metaphor of "heartlessness" — without her physical heart, she cannot access the emotional responses that define humanity. Her restoration raises [[the-heros-journey|the quest]]ion: is she morally responsible for her actions while enchanted? The narrative answers no — "she is enchanted, but 'tis not her fault" — making Joan a sympathetic victim rather than a [[princess-fiorimonde|willing villain]].
