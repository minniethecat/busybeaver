# Happiness and Contentment

Happiness in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* occupies a register distinct from [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]. Where [[joy-as-emotion|joy]] erupts at moments of liberation, happiness — or more precisely, contentment — is the steady state that characters achieve when they live in accordance with their true nature. De Morgan's stories suggest that happiness is not something to be pursued but something that settles upon those who have made the right choice about how to live.

## Overview

The collection's treatment of happiness follows two contrasting patterns. First, **happiness as simplicity** — the contentment of those who have abandoned [[greed-and-beauty|ambition]] for a life of modest satisfaction. Prince [[alderete|Alderete]], who leaves kingship for goose-herding, is the collection's purest example. Second, **happiness as relationship** — the contentment of [[marriage-and-union|union]] between those who love each other, as in the resolution of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]." Against these stands the counter-example of characters who pursue power, [[wealth-and-poverty|wealth]], or status and achieve none of the happiness they expected — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] most prominently.

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s father the King invokes happiness as an expected outcome of favorable circumstances:

> "And now, my son, all is arranged for you to journey in state to her father's court and bring back your bride, so now I hope that you are happy and wish for nothing more."

His formulation — "I [[hope-as-resilience|hope]] that you are happy" — treats happiness as the natural result of favorable arrangements. But the narrative demonstrates that happiness cannot be arranged for another person; it must be achieved through struggle and earned through right choice.

## The Wisdom of Prince Alderete

"[[the-three-clever-kings-story|The Three Clever Kings]]" provides the collection's most articulate philosophy of happiness. Prince [[alderete|Alderete]], having tried his cousins' unsatisfactory approaches to kingship, chooses a different path entirely:

> "Now, here I lie peacefully all day long and watch my geese, and it is much nicer than being King."

[[alderete|Alderete]]'s statement is radical in its rejection of the fairy-tale norm. The conventional narrative arc moves toward kingship as the ultimate [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] — but [[alderete|Alderete]] has held the crown and found it wanting. His contentment in simplicity is not resignation but wisdom: he has seen both options and chosen the better one.

The happiness [[alderete|Alderete]] embodies is self-knowledge. He knows he does not want to be king, and he does not pretend otherwise. His contentment flows from the alignment between his circumstances and his nature.

## Happiness and Its Absence

The collection's unhappy characters share a common trait: they have chosen wrongly about what will make them happy. The wizards in [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s quest pursue power through [[truth-and-deception|deception]] and remain miserable. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] seeks control over others and ends up destroyed. The discontented villagers in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" achieve material comfort only to discover that comfort without purpose yields complaint, not contentment.

De Morgan's moral psychology is pragmatic rather than punitive. Characters are not punished for seeking happiness in the wrong places; they simply fail to find it there. The collection's implicit argument is that happiness has a discoverable structure — it follows certain paths and eludes others — and the wise character learns which path leads where.

## Literary Context

Victorian fairy tales frequently concluded with the formula "and they lived happily ever after" — a phrase that collapses happiness into narrative closure. De Morgan, like George MacDonald and Juliana Horatia Ewing, resists this formula. Her stories suggest that happiness is not a permanent destination but an ongoing condition that requires the right choices and the right circumstances. The absence of "ever after" in her storytelling is itself a moral statement: happiness is fragile, contingent, and must be continually chosen.
