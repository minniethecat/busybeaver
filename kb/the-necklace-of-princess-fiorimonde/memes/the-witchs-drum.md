# The Witch's Drum

The witch's drum in Mary De Morgan's *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]* is the instrument of summoning — a terrible percussion that calls forth the dark assembly of the witch's mountain. It is the sound of Fiorimonde's initiation into the world of sorcery.

## The Summoning

The witch's drum is introduced in the scene of Fiorimonde's first visit:

> "'And now,' said the witch, 'since you are here, and there is yet time, we will have a dance, and I will summon the guests.' So saying, she took from a corner a drum and a pair of drumsticks, and going to the door, began to beat upon it. It made a terrible rattling." [0071-P1]

The word "terrible" is De Morgan's precise choice — the sound is not merely loud but morally awful, a noise that belongs to the world of dark magic. The drum is not described; only its sound matters, and that sound is the signal of evil approaching.

## The Guests

What the drum summons is a catalogue of nightmare creatures:

> "In a moment came flying through the air all sorts of forms. There were little dark elves with long tails, and goblins who chattered and laughed, and other witches who rode on broomsticks. There was one wicked fairy in the form of a large cat, with bright green eyes, and another came sliding in like a long shining viper." [0071-P1]

The drum's rhythm draws them from the air — they do not walk or ride but "came flying." The drum is a portal, its beat opening a channel between the witch's mountain and the dark places where such creatures dwell.

## The Underground Revel

After the summoning, the witch leads the assembly into darkness:

> "The old witch stepped through it, and led the way down a narrow dark passage, to a large underground chamber, and all her strange guests followed, and here they all danced and made merry in a terrible way." [0073-P1]

The drum's purpose is revelry — a dark inversion of the courtly dances Fiorimonde would attend above ground. The phrase "made merry in a terrible way" captures the collection's sense that evil has its own version of joy.
