# Fear and Power

Fear pervades Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection not as a passing emotion but as a structural force — a tool wielded by the powerful to control the weak, and a collective condition that communities must overcome to achieve liberation. The motif appears in every story, operating at personal, communal, and political scales.

## Overview

De Morgan [[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] fear in three interlocking modes. First, **fear as personal domination**: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] terrifies her servant [[yolande|Yolande]] into silence, ensuring her crimes go unreported. Second, **fear as collective paralysis**: the spellbound villagers in "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" live in "dread" of the elves' nightly visitations, too frightened to resist. Third, **fear as institutional violence**: the baker in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" is dragged before [[justice-and-injustice|the Justice]] and publicly flogged, a spectacle designed to intimidate the entire community into conformity.

The collection's moral arc consistently moves from fear to courage. Characters who overcome fear — [[chrysea|Chrysea]] going to [[the-moorland|the moor]], [[yolande|Yolande]] confessing to [[gervaise|Gervaise]], the baker protesting his unjust [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] — are [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed. Those who rule through fear, like [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]], meet destruction.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — The Reign of Terror

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s power rests almost entirely on fear. [[yolande|Yolande]], the only character who knows the Princess's secret, is paralyzed by terror:

> "Because I fear my mistress," said Yolande, speaking low and drawing near to him; "she is a sorceress."

This fear is rational — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] has murdered twelve suitors — but it also enables her crimes. The courtiers, too, live in fear: "The courtiers were afraid and whispered under their breath, 'It is not all right, it cannot be.'" The collective fear creates a conspiracy of silence that nearly costs [[gervaise|Gervaise]] his life. The story's resolution comes when [[gervaise|Gervaise]], armed with [[yolande|Yolande]]'s confession and [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]], confronts the Princess directly — an act of courage that breaks the cycle of fear.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Collective Dread

The villagers' condition is defined by fear. They explain to [[arasmon|Arasmon]] and [[chrysea|Chrysea]]:

> "Folks say we were spellbound. 'Tis so long ago that we do not now like to speak of that, for fear our troubles should return."

Even after liberation, the memory of fear persists. The villagers' reluctance to discuss the past reflects trauma — a psychological insight unusual in Victorian fairy tales. [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s willingness to face the elves, despite the danger, stands as the story's moral exemplar: fear is real, but it must be confronted.

### "The Bread of Discontent" — Fear as Social Control

The baker's persecution by [[justice-and-injustice|the Justice]] and the villagers is the collection's most explicit depiction of institutional fear. The baker is "frightened" at being dragged before [[justice-and-injustice|the Justice]]; the public flogging is designed to terrorize him and the onlookers into submission:

> "They seized the baker and dragged him off to the marketplace, and made a ring round him, so that he could not escape."

The story critiques how communities use fear to enforce conformity — the baker's "crime" is discontent, and the [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] is spectacular. Yet the baker's eventual resistance, when he protests "what has he done to be beaten?" prompts the crowd to question [[justice-and-injustice|the Justice]]'s [[justice-and-injustice|authority]], suggesting that collective fear can be broken by a single courageous voice.

### Beyond the Central Stories

Fear appears across the collection. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" "did not fear" the old magician — his fearlessness is a marker of virtue. The three clever kings are "frightened" by their people's resistance to reform, illustrating how fear of public opinion paralyzes governance. In "[[the-wise-princess-story|The Wise Princess]]," [[princess-fernanda|Princess Fernanda]] "did not fear" the storm and the sea, a sign of her exceptional character.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's treatment of fear as a social and political force distinguishes her from many Victorian fairy-tale writers. Where fear in traditional tales is often a test of individual bravery (the hero facing the dragon), De Morgan shows how fear operates systemically — through gossip, silence, institutional [[justice-and-injustice|authority]], and collective memory. This anticipates the psychological realism of later twentieth-century fantasy.
