# The Wedding Feast

Wedding feasts recur across Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection as pivotal events that reveal character, trigger transformation, and serve as the stage for both triumph and catastrophe. From the deadly betrothals of [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] to the [[joy-as-emotion|joy]]ous [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] of [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]], the wedding feast is De Morgan's most charged narrative set-piece.

## The Deadly Betrothals

In "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]," the wedding feast is a trap. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s father, eager to see his daughter married, summons suitors from across the world. Each follows the same fatal pattern:

> "A great feast was held, at which the Princess looked lovelier than ever... but she wore no jewels nor ornaments of any kind, save one shining gold string round her milk-white throat."

The feast — with its "[[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en chair," its "fine company," its "grandly dressed ladies" — is the public performance. The private moment follows: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] lures each suitor into [[the-palace-gardens|the garden]] or beneath a window, offers [[the-gold-necklace|the gold cord]] with a pretext, and watches them vanish.

By morning, the bridegroom is gone, and "where there is no bridegroom there can be no [[wedding-as-ritual|bridal]]." The [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] is perpetually deferred, the feast a prelude to disappearance.

### The Suitors' Wedding Mornings

| Suitor | Wedding Morning | Fate |
|--------|----------------|------|
| [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]] | [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] dressed "in white satin and pearls with a long white lace veil" | Vanished the night before |
| [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]] | [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] "dressed in gorgeous clothes" | Vanished at sunrise in [[the-palace-gardens|the garden]] |
| [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]] | [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] refuses to dress up — "let him come and find me as I am" | Vanished, then rescued by [[gervaise|Gervaise]] |

Each failed [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] is followed by the ritual of public mourning: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] "put on a black gown, and begged to be allowed to live in seclusion for one month in which to grieve" — a performance of sorrow that masks her private laughter.

## The Wedding as Comic Climax

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s triumph in the title story is staged as an anti-[[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]. Instead of a bridegroom disappearing, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] makes [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] herself vanish. The council chamber — not the church — becomes the scene of restoration, as [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]] and releases twelve prisoners one by one:

> "'Now,' cried he, 'look, and see King Pierrot who was lost,' and as he spoke he drew from the cord a bead, and King Pierrot, in his royal clothes, with his sword at his side, stood before them."

The public unmasking replaces the public [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]], [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] substituted for ceremony.

## The Wedding Earned

"[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" offers the collection's one genuine [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]. After [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s seven-year quest and the heart's restoration, the ceremony re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] true [[marriage-and-union|union]] rather than social arrangement:

> "They brought a fine white horse with a grand gold saddle, and jewelled bridle, and placed Michael upon it, and he rode to church beside the Princess, and married her, and the people threw flowers before them, and bells rang and trumpets sounded, and all were glad."

Crucially, this [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] is not the King's doing but the people's. When the crowd cries, "It is [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]], who went seven years ago... Now will we indeed have a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]," the [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] becomes a communal celebration rather than a dynastic arrangement.

## The Wedding Nearly Stolen

The tension of the collection's second [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] is that it almost does not happen. [[princess-joan|Joan]] is betrothed to the elderly [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] — "old and ugly, and they say he does not love her at all, but only marries her that he may be king of this country as well as his own." This [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] procession, with its "trumpets sounding, and clarions ringing," is a hollow ritual that the Queen attends with "red eyes" from crying.

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s intervention — releasing the heart from a prison window as the procession passes — transforms a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] of convenience into a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] of love. The false [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] and true [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] are juxtaposed within a single morning.

## Significance

In De Morgan's moral universe, the wedding feast is a test. Those who approach it with [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] or convenience ([[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]], [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]) are thwarted. Those who earn it through sacrifice and [[love-and-loyalty|fidelity]] ([[prince-michael|Michael]], [[gervaise|Gervaise]]) are [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed. The [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] is not a beginning but a culmination — the visible proof that the invisible work of [[love-and-loyalty|loyalty]] has been completed.
