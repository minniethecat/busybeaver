# Justice and Injustice

Mary De Morgan's collection offers a sustained and surprisingly s[[music-as-art|harp]] critique of institutional [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] while affirming a moral order in which the wicked are ultimately punished and the innocent restored. The tension between human legal systems — portrayed as arbitrary, corrupt, or absurd — and the natural [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] of the fairy-tale world is one of the collection's most distinctive thematic concerns.

## Overview

De Morgan [[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] on two levels. **Human [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]** is invariably flawed: the Justice in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" is a figure of satire, dispensing punishments based on personal irritation rather than law; [[the-royal-castle|the court]] in "[[the-peddlers-pack-story|The Pedlar's Pack]]" tries a donkey for speaking. **Cosmic or fairy-tale [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]**, by contrast, operates with precision: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] becomes the thirteenth bead on her own necklace; the elves' curse rebounds upon them when [[chrysea|Chrysea]] sings their song back.

The collection thus makes a radical argument for its time: institutional authority is not to be trusted, and true [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] comes not from judges and courts but from individual courage and the moral logic of the fairy-tale universe itself.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Bread of Discontent" — The Absurdity of Power

The most sustained satire of judicial authority appears in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]." The chief [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]'s introduction sets the tone:

> "The first person who tasted the bread was the chief justice. He came down to breakfast in high spirits, for he had just heard that an old aunt had died and left him a large fortune."

The [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]'s personal fortunes determine his mood, which in turn determines his judgments — a devastating critique of how actual [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] operates. When the baker is dragged before him for the "crime" of discontent, the [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]'s response is pure caprice:

> "Am I never to be left in peace? How hard is the life of a Justice!"

The punishment — public flogging — is both cruel and absurd, imposed without evidence or process. Yet the story's resolution is equally pointed: when the crowd turns on the [[punishment-and-reward|justice]], questioning "what has this man done to be beaten?", institutional authority collapses in the face of popular conscience.

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Poetic Justice

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s fate is the collection's clearest example of poetic [[punishment-and-reward|justice]]. Having transformed twelve suitors into beads on a necklace — a symbol of her [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] and greed — she herself becomes the thirteenth bead:

> "Everyone who saw it knew that it was the wicked Princess Fiorimonde who had justly met her fate."

The punishment fits the crime with fairy-tale precision. [[gervaise|Gervaise]], the one suitor who sought [[truth-and-deception|truth]] rather than beauty, becomes the agent of this [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] — not through violence but through cleverness and courage, armed with [[yolande|Yolande]]'s confession and [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en thread.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Restorative Justice

Unlike retributive [[punishment-and-reward|justice]], the resolution of "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" offers restoration: [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is freed from her transformation, the village is liberated, and [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s decades of faithful wandering are [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed. The elves, who punished [[chrysea|Chrysea]] for breaking their spell, are themselves undone — their power broken by a human voice. Justice here is not punishment but the restoration of a rightful order.

### "The Pedlar's Pack" — The Mockery of Courts

The beadle's "trial" of the pedlar, donkey, raven, and hedge-sparrow in the marketplace parodies judicial proceedings. The animals' ability to speak — a fairy-tale convention — exposes human legal processes as no more rational than a donkey's braying. The mayor sits in judgment, but his authority is undermined by the absurdity of the proceedings.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's critique of institutional [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] reflects the Victorian era's growing concern with legal reform — the period saw the Judicature Acts (1873–1875) restructuring the English court system. Her portrayal of the Justice as arbitrary and self-interested echoes critiques found in Charles Dickens and other social novelists, but her use of the fairy-tale form allows for a more radical resolution: true [[punishment-and-reward|justice]] comes not from reformed institutions but from overturning them entirely.
