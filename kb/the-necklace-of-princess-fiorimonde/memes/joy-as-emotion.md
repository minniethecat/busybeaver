# Joy as Emotion

Joy in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* arrives not as a steady disposition but as a seismic event — the sudden breach of an [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]], the unexpected return of the lost, the breaking of a long silence. It is always earned through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] and never given freely. De Morgan treats joy as the emotional signature of liberation, the affect that confirms that a transformation has truly occurred.

## Overview

Joy appears across the collection in three characteristic forms. First, **joy as recognition** — the shock of re[[marriage-and-union|union]] after long separation, as when [[gervaise|Gervaise]] restores [[yolande|Yolande]] from bead to woman or when the fishing villagers see their boats return. Second, **joy as collective awakening** — an emotion shared by an entire community when a spell breaks, as in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" when the village's darkness lifts. Third, **joy as vindication** — the [[happiness-and-contentment|satisfaction]] of [[hope-as-resilience|hope]] fulfilled against all expectation, most powerfully in [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s return to [[princess-joan|Joan]].

De Morgan distinguishes joy from mere pleasure or [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]]. Her characters experience joy at thresholds: the moment when [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] ends and a new state of being begins.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Joy as Restoration

The collection's most concentrated expressions of joy occur in the title story's climactic scene, when [[gervaise|Gervaise]] breaks [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] and restores its captives. His own reaction to seeing [[yolande|Yolande]] freed is described without reserve:

> "And now, here is she who has helped to save you all — and he drew off the twelfth bead, and there stood Yolande in her red dress; and when he saw her Gervaise flung away his dagger and took her in his arms, and they wept for joy."

The weeping here is significant — De Morgan's joy is not smiling [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] but an overwhelming release that manifests physically, spilling into tears. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] weep not from sorrow but from the sheer intensity of re[[marriage-and-union|union]] after the threat of permanent loss.

Earlier, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] herself uses the language of joy performatively, clapping her hands when she conceives the idea of [[the-beads|the enchanted necklace]]:

> "The necklace! the necklace! — cried the Princess, clapping her hands with joy. 'That will be best of all, to sling them upon a string and wear them around my throat.'"

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s joy is the collection's only example of joy in [[evil-as-deception|evil]] — her delight is genuine, but it is joy in power and destruction rather than in love and restoration. De Morgan uses this contrast to mark [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s moral distance from the other characters.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — Joy Deferred and Fulfilled

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s father, the King, expresses the [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]ful joy of a parent sending his son to claim his bride:

> "And now, my son, all is arranged for you to journey in state to her father's court and bring back your bride, so now I hope that you are happy and wish for nothing more."

But true joy in this story is deferred through seven years of [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]]. [[prince-michael|Michael]] experiences no joy during his quest — only determination and fear. Joy arrives only at the end, when he stands before [[princess-joan|Joan]] and her [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] guests recognize him. The people's cry — "Now will we indeed have a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]]!" — is joy as collective celebration, joy shared by an entire court.

### "The Bread of Discontent" — Joy as Collective Awakening

The most communal expression of joy in the collection occurs when the spellbound village is freed:

> "When morning dawned, and the villagers awoke, all felt that some great change had taken place. The heavy cloud which hung above the village had cleared away; the sun shone brightly, and the sky was blue; streams which had been dry for years, were running clear and fresh: and the people all felt strong, and able to work again; the trees were beginning to bud, and in their branches sang birds, whose voices had not been heard there for many a long year. The villagers looked from one to another and said, 'Surely the spell is broken; surely the elves must have fled;' and they wept for joy."

This passage is a compendium of joy's physical manifestations in De Morgan's world: the natural environment transforms (sun, streams, birdsong), the body feels renewed strength, and emotion overflows into weeping. Joy here is liberation made visible — not an internal feeling but a transformation of the entire world that the villagers inhabit.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Joy Beyond Expectation

The fishing village scene at the story's narrative frame provides a miniature of joy's pattern — fear yields to relief when the boats return:

> "But as she stopped speaking, there rose a cry of 'Here they are,' and two or three fishing-boats were pushed on to the sand close by where they stood, and the women threw their arms round their husbands' necks, and all shouted for joy."

The shout is joy's simplest and most instinctive expression — a sound that precedes language, the body's immediate response to relief.

## Literary Context

Victorian fairy tales often treated joy as a [[punishment-and-reward|reward]] for virtue — the good are happy, the wicked are not. De Morgan's treatment is more psychologically nuanced. Joy in her stories is not a moral dividend but a natural response to liberation. It can be felt by anyone — good or [[evil-as-deception|evil]] — when what they most desire comes to pass: [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] claps with joy at her necklace's power, just as the villagers weep with joy at their freedom. Joy, for De Morgan, reveals what a character truly values, making it a diagnostic emotion that exposes moral character rather than confirming it.
