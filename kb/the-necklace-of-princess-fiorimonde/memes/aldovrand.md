# Aldovrand

Aldovrand is one of the three cousins in "[[the-three-clever-kings-story|The Three Clever Kings]]," the seventh and final story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. He is the first of the three nephews to try his hand at kingship — and the first to reject it.

## Character Overview

Aldovrand is the eldest nephew of old King Roland, who on his [[death-and-mortality|death]]bed devises an unusual succession plan: rather than choosing one heir, he decrees that each of his three nephews — Aldovrand, [[aldebert|Aldebert]], and [[alderete|Alderete]] — should reign in turn, so that "you will know which is the best fitted to govern."

Aldovrand is crowned first, and the country celebrates with "great rejoicings everywhere."

## Role in the Story

### The Reluctant King

Aldovrand's reign lasts exactly one morning. On his first day as king, the Prime Minister and Chancellor arrive at [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] to conduct state business — only to be told "that his majesty was in bed and had given orders that no one should disturb him."

> "This is a bad beginning," sighed the Prime Minister.
> "Very bad," echoed the Chancellor.

When finally roused, Aldovrand is indignant: "A pretty thing, that I the King am to be sent for hither and thither as if I were a lackey. They must go away and come another time."

The next day brings even more officials — the Commander-in-Chief and the Lord High Admiral join the Prime Minister and Chancellor outside his bedroom door. Aldovrand's response is definitive:

> "Here, let one of my cousins be King, for I will not bear this longer. It is much more trouble than it is worth, so Aldebert or Alderete may try it and see how they like it, but as for me, I have had enough of it."

He throws down his crown and walks out of [[the-royal-castle|the palace]].

### The Goose-Herd

Aldovrand finds his true calling in the countryside. He approaches a farmer and asks for work, specifying: "I am not very fond of running about, but if you want anyone to mind your sheep, or keep the birds from your corn, I could do that nicely." He is given a flock of geese to mind.

> "This is capital," he cried, "and much better than being King at the palace. Here there is no Prime Minister or Chancellor to come worrying."

When the Prime Minister and Chancellor later find him and beg him to return, he laughs outright: "No indeed! I would not be King again for any man living."

## Significance

Aldovrand re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the temperament utterly unsuited to governance — not through [[evil-as-deception|malice]] or incompetence, but through a fundamental mismatch between personality and position. His [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] as a goose-herd is genuine, not feigned. De Morgan's portrait is gently satirical: Aldovrand is not lazy so much as allergic to bureaucracy. His story suggests that the best qualification for a ruler may simply be the willingness to do the work — a willingness Aldovrand conspicuously lacks.
