# Fear

Fear in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection is never merely felt — it is administered. Witches, sorceresses, and tyrants deploy fear as a tool of control, and the hero's task is to dissolve it through courage, music, or truth.

## Fear as the Instrument of Power

The witch's opening words to Fiorimonde establish fear as the collection's baseline of power relations:

> "'Nay, never fear,' said the Princess, 'I will be careful, only tell me what to do.'" [0061-P1]

The witch's response is to teach Fiorimonde the necklace spell — a transaction in which Fiorimonde trades fear for power. She becomes the one who inspires fear rather than the one who feels it.

## The Servant's Fear

[[yolande|Yolande]] embodies the fear of the powerless. Her relationship to Fiorimonde is defined by dread:

> "She was a bright-faced girl with merry brown eyes, but she was not beautiful like Fiorimonde, and she did not love her mistress, for she was afraid of her, and suspected her of her wicked ways." [0097-P1]

The phrase "afraid of her" comes before "suspected her" — fear precedes knowledge. Yolande's fear is rational, grounded in observation of the disappearing suitors and the growing necklace, but it also paralyzes her. She watches and trembles but cannot act alone.

## Fear Spread Through a Court

When suitor after suitor vanishes, fear becomes communal:

> "The courtiers were afraid and whispered under their breath, 'It is not all right, it cannot be;' but only Yolande noticed how the beads came upon the golden thread." [0147-P1]

The courtiers' fear is voiced only in whispers — public acknowledgment of the pattern is impossible because the King will not hear it. Fear, suppressed, becomes a shared secret that enables Fiorimonde's continued predation.

## Fear Confessed

Yolande's confession to Gervaise is the turning point of the title story:

> "'Because I fear my mistress,' said Yolande, speaking low and drawing near to him; 'she is a sorceress, and she wears the brave kings and princes who come to woo her, strung upon a cord round her neck.'" [0199-P1]

Speaking fear aloud — naming its source — is the first act of resistance. Yolande's whisper to Gervaise breaks the spell of silence that has protected Fiorimonde.

## Fear Before the Throne

When Gervaise exposes the necklace, the court's fear finds its proper object:

> "The King and all the courtiers sat pale and trembling, unable to speak for fear and shame." [0337-P1]

The phrase "fear and shame" is precise: the court fears what has been done in their midst, and is ashamed that they allowed it. The two emotions together mark the end of Fiorimonde's reign of terror.

## Fear Dissolved by Music

In "The Wanderings of Arasmon," fear is not defeated by arms but by art:

> "Arasmon sat upon the walls of the town, and began to play upon his harp, and this time its music was so loud and clear, that it could be heard far and wide, and its sound was so exultant and joyous, that when the soldiers heard it they raised their heads, and their fears vanished." [0475-P1]

The verb "vanished" is the same one used for Fiorimonde's suitors. Fear, like enchantment, can be made to disappear. Arasmon's music does what no weapon could — it replaces fear with exultation.

## The Hero Who Does Not Fear

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] is introduced as the exception to the rule of fear:

> "No one in all the country had ever ventured to climb the tower and see the old man at his work except Prince Michael, who knew the old sorcerer well and did not fear him at all." [0629-P1]

Michael's fearlessness is not bravado but familiarity — he "knew the old sorcerer well." Knowledge dispels fear, and Michael's immunity to the magician's dread is the first sign that he is the hero who can rescue Joan.
