# Chrysea

Chrysea is one of the two protagonists of "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," the third story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. She is a wandering musician whose extraordinary singing voice and courage drive the story's central conflict.

## Character Overview

Chrysea travels the world with her husband [[arasmon|Arasmon]], who plays the [[music-as-art|lute]] while she sings. Her voice is described as possessing exceptional beauty:

> "When Arasmon played all who heard him were silent from wonder and admiration, but when Chrysea sang they could not refrain from weeping, for her voice was more beautiful than anything they had ever heard before."

Both Chrysea and [[arasmon|Arasmon]] were young and lovely, deeply in love, and content with their wandering life — visiting cities, villages, cottages by the seashore, and green country lanes, bringing music wherever they went.

## Role in the Story

When Chrysea and [[arasmon|Arasmon]] arrive at a spellbound village, Chrysea is moved by the inhabitants' [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]]. The villagers are tormented by invisible elves who steal their food, destroy their houses, and keep them in perpetual misery — all because their ancestors sold [[the-moorland|the moor]]land to a wicked wizard.

Learning that the spell can be broken only by someone who has never taken the elves' money singing their enchanted song back to them, Chrysea volunteers despite the danger:

> "But I have not taken their money. And there is no tune I cannot sing when I have heard it once. So I will go to the moor for you and break the spell."

At midnight, she hides on the blackened moor and witnesses the elves' ritual: they dance around a miniature replica of the village and sing their binding song. Chrysea listens, memorizes the tune, and sings it back perfectly — shattering the miniature village and breaking the spell.

The enraged elf-chief then transforms Chrysea into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] as [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]]: "Let us [[fate-and-transformation|change]] her to no bird or beast, but to a bright [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]], and thus shall she remain, until upon her strings someone shall play our tune, which she has dared to sing."

## Key Relationships

- **[[arasmon]]** — Chrysea's beloved husband, a gifted [[music-as-art|lute]] player. His unwavering love drives him to find and restore her after her transformation.
- **The Elf-Chief** — The leader of the dark elves who transforms Chrysea into a [[music-as-art|harp]] as [[punishment-and-reward|retribution]] for breaking the village's spell.
- **[[the-spellbound-village|The Spellbound Village]]rs** — The [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] people Chrysea selflessly helps, despite their warnings of the danger.

## Significance

Chrysea embodies the theme of artistic courage — she uses her musical gift not merely for livelihood but as an instrument of liberation. Her transformation into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] is both a [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] and a metaphor: art itself becomes imprisoned when used against dark forces, yet it also holds the key to its own release, for only by playing the elves' tune upon her [[music-as-art|harp]]-strings can she be restored to human form.
