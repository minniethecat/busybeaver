# The Pedlar's Pack

"The Pedlar's Pack" is the fifth tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. Unlike the darker fairy tales in the collection, this story is a lighthearted fable about greed, dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]], and the circular folly of false [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s.

## Plot Summary

A pedlar trudges along a dusty road with his heavy pack and asks a donkey to carry his load, promising "two pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]" — though he knows he has no [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] to give. The donkey agrees, and as they travel, the donkey in turn hires a raven to drive away flies for "three pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]" — also a false [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]. The raven then engages a hedge-sparrow to bring worms for "four pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]," equally dishonest. This creates a chain: the pedlar owes the donkey, the donkey owes the raven, the raven owes the hedge-sparrow.

When they approach a town, the pedlar displays his wares. The hedge-sparrow spots a scarlet blanket and agrees to buy it for "four pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]" — money he does not have, assuming the raven will pay him. The hedge-sparrow presses the raven for payment, the raven presses the donkey, the donkey presses the pedlar, and the pedlar demands payment from the hedge-sparrow. The circular demands escalate into a noisy quarrel outside the town walls.

The beadle arrests them all and brings them before the mayor, who declares them all worthless vagabonds and punishes each: the pedlar is imprisoned, the donkey is beaten, and the raven and hedge-sparrow have their tail-feathers pulled out. The mayor confiscates the scarlet blanket for himself.

## Key Characters

- **The Pedlar** — A dishonest trader who begins the chain of false [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s, eventually imprisoned for his [[evil-as-deception|deceit]]
- **The Donkey** — Agrees to carry the pedlar's pack for [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] he knows does not exist, then makes equally false [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s to the raven
- **The Raven** — Promises [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] to the hedge-sparrow without any means of payment
- **The Hedge-sparrow** — The smallest link in the chain, who [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s to buy the blanket with money he does not have
- **The Mayor** — Sees through the mutual [[truth-and-deception|deception]] and punishes all parties, keeping the blanket for himself

## Themes

The story satirizes the folly of dishonest dealing through its circular structure: each character makes [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s they cannot keep, assuming the previous debtor will pay. When the chain collapses, all suffer — but each blames the next rather than recognizing their own dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]]. The mayor's self-serving confiscation of the blanket adds a final ironic layer: [[justice-and-injustice|authority]], too, operates on its own self-interest. The tale's moral structure echoes the traditional fable form, where animals and humans interact in a com[[the-fateful-bargain|pact]] narrative illustrating a clear lesson.
