# Gervaise

Gervaise is the true hero of the title story "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]," the first tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. A loyal and courageous friend, he succeeds where eleven princes and kings failed, defeating the sorceress through cleverness rather than combat.

## Character Overview

Gervaise is described as "tall, and broad, and stout of limb" — a physically imposing figure whose strength is matched by his [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]]. He is the dearest friend of [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]] and accompanies him to [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]]'s court when [[prince-florestan|Florestan]] decides to seek her hand in [[marriage-and-union|marriage]]:

> "He had a dear friend named Gervaise, whom he loved better than anyone in the world. Gervaise was tall, and broad, and stout of limb, and he loved Prince Florestan so well, that he would gladly have died to serve him."

Unlike the other suitors who are dazzled by [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s beauty, Gervaise remains suspicious from the start, his heart "heavy" with foreboding.

## Role in the Story

When [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]] becomes the eleventh suitor to vanish, Gervaise learns the [[truth-and-deception|truth]] from [[yolande|Yolande]], [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s maid: the Princess is a sorceress who transforms each suitor into a bead on her [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en necklace. [[yolande|Yolande]] shows him the sleeping Princess wearing [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] with its eleven beads, each containing a vanished king or prince.

Gervaise's plan is ingenious. Disguised as a [[wealth-and-poverty|wealth]]y suitor, he tells [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] he has seen a woman more beautiful than she. When she demands to see this rival, he offers to trade: her necklace for his. Enraged and forgetful of [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s warning, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] seizes her own string of beads — and instantly becomes the thirteenth bead herself.

> "No sooner had she taken it in her hands than they fell with a rattle to the earth, and Fiorimonde herself was nowhere to be seen."

Gervaise then cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]] with a dagger, releasing all the imprisoned suitors — [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]], [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]], King Adrian, Prince Sigbert, Prince Algar, and all the others — along with [[yolande|Yolande]], whom he marries.

## Key Relationships

- **[[prince-florestan]]** — Gervaise's beloved master and friend, whose disappearance drives Gervaise to confront [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]].
- **[[yolande]]** — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s maid who reveals the [[truth-and-deception|truth]] about [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]]; she and Gervaise fall in love and marry.
- **[[princess-fiorimonde]]** — The antagonist whom Gervaise outwits through psychological manipulation rather than force.
- **The Rescued Suitors** — [[king-pierrot]], [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]], and nine other kings and princes freed when Gervaise cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]].

## Significance

Gervaise is the archetypal loyal friend who becomes an unlikely hero. He defeats [[evil-as-deception|evil]] not through magic or combat, but through intelligence, patience, and psychological insight — understanding that [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] is her fatal weakness. His [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] to [[yolande|Yolande]] re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the story's moral center: [[love-and-loyalty|true love]] and loyalty triumph over beauty and [[truth-and-deception|deception]]. In De Morgan's moral universe, Gervaise embodies the virtue of steadfast [[love-and-loyalty|devotion]] [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed.
