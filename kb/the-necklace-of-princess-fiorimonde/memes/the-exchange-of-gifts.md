# The Exchange of Gifts

The ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] of gifts in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is never a neutral social ritual. A gift can be a trap, a test, or a token of genuine love — and the difference lies in the giver's intent. De Morgan uses the gift as a narrative device that reveals character: what someone gives, and how they give it, tells the reader everything they need to know about who that person is.

## The Gift as Trap

### Fiorimonde's Necklace as "Gift"

The collection's most consequential gift is [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s demand for the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en necklace. She frames it as a [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] present:

> "Tomorrow, then, my sweet Princess, you will be my Queen, and share all I possess. What gift would you wish me to give you on our wedding day?"

> "The necklace! the necklace!" cried the Princess, clapping her hands with joy. "That will be best of all, to sling them upon a string and wear them around my throat."

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] receives [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] as a gift from her suitor but transforms it into an instrument of destruction. The gift that was meant to adorn becomes a weapon that consumes. De Morgan subverts the gift-giving convention: the princess does not receive a gift graciously but seizes it instrumentally, converting love's token into power's tool.

Each suitor who offers the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread around his own throat is receiving [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s "gift" of [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] — a gift that is actually theft, taking the suitor's very existence and converting it into a bead.

### The Wedding Gifts Interrupted

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] preparations include gifts from all the guests:

> "The guests are already beginning to arrive at the palace, and each one brings some splendid gift."

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s complaint about missing these gifts reveals her enchanted state — without her heart, she values material presents over [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]d return:

> "I had thought there would be a grand wedding, and I should have all the gifts that are being prepared for me, and now I shall have nothing."

Her complaint is comically shallow, but it registers the enchanted [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s inability to distinguish between material gifts and the gift of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s love.

## The Gift as Restoration

### Michael's Gift of the Heart

The collection's truest gift is [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s restoration of [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart:

> "I will be a guest at that wedding, and I bring the best gift of all for the bride."

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s gift is not an object but a restoration — he returns what was stolen. The gift is [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s own heart, recovered through seven years of labour. Unlike [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s gift-as-trap, [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s gift is given freely and restores rather than diminishes the recipient. [[princess-joan|Joan]] receives not a thing but herself — her capacity to feel, to love, to be fully human.

### Gervaise's Gift of Liberation

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s gift to the twelve captives of [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] is liberation. He gives each restored person back their existence — [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]], [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]], the other suitors, and finally [[yolande|Yolande]]. His gift requires no reciprocation; the liberation is complete in itself.

## The Gift's Moral Economy

De Morgan's gift economy is governed by a simple principle: the gift that takes is [[evil-as-deception|evil]], the gift that restores is good. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s gifts consume the recipient; [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s and [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s gifts restore the recipient to wholeness. The true gift, in De Morgan's world, does not create obligation — it dissolves it.
