# Alderete

Alderete is the youngest and most ambitious of the three cousins in "[[the-three-clever-kings-story|The Three Clever Kings]]," the final story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. He is the last to attempt kingship — and his spectacular failure provides the story's s[[music-as-art|harp]]est political satire.

## Character Overview

Unlike his cousins, [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] (who [[fear-and-power|dread]]ed responsibility) and [[aldebert|Aldebert]] (who was overwhelmed by it), Alderete greets his turn at the crown with unbridled enthusiasm:

> "Now at last I will show what a king should really be like. My cousins were neither of them any good, but they shall now see how different I will be."

He is crowned with "great rejoicings all over the country" — the third such celebration in rapid succession.

## Role in the Story

### The Micromanager King

Alderete's vision of kingship is total control. On his first day he declares:

> "A good King ought to be like a father to his people, and this is what I mean to be. I mean to arrange everything for them myself, and if they will only obey me, and do as I direct, they are sure to be both prosperous and happy."

The Chancellor warns: "I fear, your Majesty, your people will not like to be too much meddled with." Alderete dismisses him angrily.

After a single drive through the city, Alderete issues his first decree — and it is characteristically absurd. Offended by the smell of cooking, he declares:

> "Nothing is so unwholesome as pea soup, and therefore it would not be right in me to allow the people to go on eating it. I command, therefore, that no one shall again make, or eat pea soup, within my realm on pain of death."

### The Revolt

The next morning, a vast mob gathers beneath his window, shouting: "The King, the King! Where is this King who would dictate to us what we shall eat and drink?" Terrified, Alderete immediately capitulates — telling the Prime Minister to let the people "eat what they like" — but it is too late. His [[justice-and-injustice|authority]] has evaporated.

### The Chimney Sweep

Like his cousins before him, Alderete flees [[the-royal-castle|the palace]]. He meets a chimney sweep on the country road and makes an unlikely request:

> "Of all things in the world I should like to be a sweep and learn how to sweep chimneys. May I go with you, and will you teach me your trade?"

The sweep agrees, and Alderete takes to the work with gusto. When the Prime Minister and Chancellor finally track him down — calling up the chimney after him — his response is definitive: "I'm not going to come down; I don't want to be King. I am going to be a sweep, and I like that much better." He then releases a shower of soot onto the Chancellor's head.

## Significance

Alderete is De Morgan's most explicit political satire. Of the three cousins, he is the only one who genuinely wants to rule — and he is by far the worst at it. His belief that a king should be "like a father" masks a tyrannical impulse to control every aspect of his subjects' lives, down to what they eat. The pea soup decree is absurd but recognisable: it parodies the paternalistic legislator who mistakes personal preference for universal [[truth-and-deception|truth]]. That all three cousins end up happier in humble trades — goose-herd, tinker, sweep — is De Morgan's quiet argument that good governance requires something none of them possess: a temperament that can neither be found nor taught by mere succession.
