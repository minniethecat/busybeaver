# Beauty and Deception

Beauty and Deception is one of the most prominent themes across Mary De Morgan's *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* (1880). De Morgan systematically subverts the traditional fairy-tale equation of physical beauty with moral goodness.

## Fiorimonde as the Central Example

[[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] embodies this theme in its most extreme form. She is described as "so beautiful that everyone thought she must be good as well, instead of which the Princess was really very wicked." Her beauty is itself a product of [[magic-and-witchcraft|witchcraft]]: "It was only [[the-old-witch|the witch]]'s arts which had made [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] so beautiful that there was no one like her in the world."

The story repeatedly emphasises the gap between [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s appearance and her nature. When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] enters her bedroom to see [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]], he is momentarily paralysed by her beauty: "Her face was calm and sweet as a baby's; her hair fell in ruddy waves on the pillow; her rosy lips smiled, and little dimples showed in her cheeks." [[yolande|Yolande]] must pull him back: "Do not look at her... since her beauty has cost dear already."

## Narrative Technique

De Morgan uses dramatic irony to highlight this theme. The courtiers and foreign princes see only [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s beauty; the reader, along with [[yolande|Yolande]], sees the laughter behind the mirror, the wicked sparkle in her eyes, the black gown that conceals the ever-growing necklace beneath.

## Contrasts in the Collection

Throughout the collection, De Morgan contrasts outward appearance with inner reality:
- The "hideous" old witch is openly what she is; [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] hides her [[evil-as-deception|wickedness]] beneath beauty
- [[yolande|Yolande]], "not beautiful like [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]," is brave and [[truth-and-deception|truth]]ful
- In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the beautiful [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] cannot feel love, while ordinary people around her feel deeply

## Victorian Context

De Morgan's subversion of the beauty-equals-goodness trope was striking for its time. Victorian fairy tales typically reinforced conventional morality, with beautiful heroines [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed and ugly villains punished. By making her most beautiful character also her most wicked, De Morgan offered a sophisticated moral vision that challenged readers to look beyond surfaces.
