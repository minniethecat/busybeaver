# Singing

Singing recurs throughout the fairy tales of Mary De Morgan as a motif of extraordinary power — capable of breaking enchantments, expressing profound emotion, and connecting the human world with the natural and supernatural.

## In "The Wanderings of Arasmon"

The most prominent instance of singing as power appears through [[chrysea|Chrysea]], whose voice surpasses even her husband [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s lute-playing. When they arrive at the spellbound village, it is Chrysea's singing that breaks the enchantment:

> Her voice rang out loud and clear, and at the sound the little village crumbled and fell away as though it had been made of dust. [0397-P1]

Her singing reverses the elves' dark magic, restoring sunlight, water, and life to the blighted village.

## As Joyful Expression

Singing also appears as the natural expression of pure joy. In "The Wise Princess," after the Princess asks the skylark why it sings:

> "I sing because I am so happy," answered the lark. [1401-P1]

This simple declaration contrasts with the Princess's own struggle to understand happiness, positioning singing as an instinctive expression of a state she has yet to attain.

## Connection to Nature

Throughout the tales, singing bridges human and natural worlds. The birds in the stories are repeatedly drawn to beautiful singing, flying down from trees to listen, suggesting that song is a universal language transcending species boundaries.
