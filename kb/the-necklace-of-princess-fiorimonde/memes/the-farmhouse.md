# The Farmhouse

## Location and Context

The Farmhouse lies in the countryside outside the royal city, reached by "the highroad into the country" that all three royal cousins — [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]], [[aldebert|Aldebert]], and [[alderete|Alderete]] — follow when they abandon their turns at the throne (971-1183). It is situated among fields and meadows, with a common nearby where geese graze. The farmhouse is identified as "the largest farm in all the country round" (971-57) and has belonged to the farmer's family for generations.

## Description

The farmhouse is a large, well-built structure standing amidst its fields. It has been passed down through the family — "it had belonged to his father, and his grandfather, and his great-grandfather, before him" (971-57). The grounds include agricultural fields where corn is harvested in autumn, a meadow where the farmer consults with his workers, and a common where livestock graze. The farmer who owns it is described as "a hale, bright man, with a good wife and pretty children" (971-57), suggesting a thriving, well-managed household. The farm re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] rootedness and productive labor — the antithesis of [[the-palace|the royal palace]] with its endless demands and quarrelling officials.

> When he had gone some way in the fields, he came to a farmhouse, and in a meadow near, the farmer stood talking to his men. Aldovrand went straight up to him, and, touching his hat, asked if he could give him any work. (971-1209)

## Key Events

The Farmhouse becomes [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]]'s refuge after his brief and unhappy reign as king. Overwhelmed by the demands of statecraft — the Prime Minister and Chancellor interrupting his sleep, the constant petitions — [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] slips out of [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] and walks into the countryside. At the Farmhouse he asks the farmer for work and is hired as a goose-boy.

The role suits him perfectly. Lying in the meadow watching geese, he reflects: "This is capital, and much better than being King at [[the-royal-castle|the palace]]. Here there is no Prime Minister or Chancellor to come worrying" (971-37). Later, when the Prime Minister and Chancellor come searching for the three cousins, they find [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] lying peacefully in his meadow, who greets them cheerfully: "Now, here I lie peacefully all day long and watch my geese, and it is much nicer than being King" (971-137).

The Farmhouse thus serves as the story's symbolic counterpoint to [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] — a place where honest labor replaces the burden of rule, and where [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] is found not in power but in simplicity.

## Narrative Function

The Farmhouse embodies the story's central thesis that fulfillment comes from matching one's nature to one's work, not from inherited status. All three cousins find their true callings in trades — [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] as goose-boy, [[aldebert|Aldebert]] as tinker, [[alderete|Alderete]] as chimney sweep — and the Farmhouse is the first and most vividly realized of these alternative spaces. Its generational continuity (father, grandfather, great-grandfather) contrasts with the instability of the throne, which none of the three cousins can bear to hold. The farmer's easy [[justice-and-injustice|authority]] over his domain stands in quiet rebuke to [[the-royal-castle|the palace]]'s fractious chain of command.
