# King Pierrot

King Pierrot is the first suitor — and first victim — of [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] in the title story of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*.

## Character Overview

King Pierrot is introduced as a young king who rules "a large rich country" across the sea. He is the first prince chosen by [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s father to be her husband. His physical description emphasizes his appeal:

> "He was strong and handsome, with black hair, and eyes like sloes."

The courtiers immediately approve of him, declaring him "a proper husband for our beautiful Princess." King Pierrot himself is "[[joy-as-emotion|delight]]ed with [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s beauty" and "happy as the day is long."

## Role in the Story

On the evening before their [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]], during a great feast at which [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] wears "a red gown, the colour of the inside of a rose" and a single [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] string around her throat, the Princess leads King Pierrot into [[the-palace-gardens|the garden]]. Under the elm tree in the moonlight, she challenges him to test the strength of her [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] cord:

> "It is as light as a feather, but stronger than an iron chain. Take it in both hands and try to break it, that you may see how strong it is."

The moment King Pierrot closes his fingers around the cord, he vanishes "like a puff of smoke," and [[the-transformation-of-king-pierrot|the first bead]] appears on [[the-beads|Fiorimonde's necklace]] — "bright and beautiful as was never bead before — clear as crystal, but shining with all colours — green, blue, and [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]."

His disappearance triggers a kingdom-wide search. Soldiers are sent "far and wide," but King Pierrot is never found. The [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] guests disperse, and everyone eventually gives him up for dead.

## Key Relationships

- **[[princess-fiorimonde]]** — His would-be bride who transforms him into [[the-transformation-of-king-pierrot|the first bead]] on her enchanted necklace.
- **[[gervaise]]** — The hero who eventually frees King Pierrot by cutting [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]] with a dagger.
- **[[yolande]]** — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s maid who first identifies the bead to [[gervaise|Gervaise]]: "This was Pierrot."

## Rescue and Aftermath

When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] with his dagger, King Pierrot is the first to be restored: "he drew from the cord a bead, and King Pierrot, in his royal clothes, with his sword at his side, stood before them." His first word upon reappearing is "Treachery!" — a cry of recognition at [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s betrayal. He is among the kings and princes who thank [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] and load them with [[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] before departing for his own land.

## Significance

King Pierrot re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the archetype of the innocent victim — handsome, trusting, and utterly unaware of the danger posed by [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s beauty. His transformation into [[the-transformation-of-king-pierrot|the first bead]] establishes the pattern that will claim ten more victims. As the inaugural bead, he is the foundation of [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s collection and the first evidence of her systematic cruelty.
