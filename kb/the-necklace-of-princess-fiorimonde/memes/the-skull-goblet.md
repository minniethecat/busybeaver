# The Skull Goblet

The skull goblet in Mary De Morgan's "The Heart of Princess Joan" is the collection's most macabre artifact — a drinking vessel formed from a human skull, offered by the yellow hag in the guise of [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] herself. It is the final temptation that [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] must resist.

## The Beautiful Disguise

The goblet appears at the climax of Michael's seven-year trial. The figure of Joan enters his prison chamber:

> "Into the room stepped the figure of the Princess Joan just as he had seen her last, dressed in white and gold, and in one hand bearing a golden goblet filled with clear ruby-coloured wine." [0857-P1]

The description is layered with beauty — "white and gold," "golden goblet," "ruby-coloured wine." Every detail invites trust. But the goblet is not what it appears.

## The Magic Glass Reveals

[[the-magic-glass|The magic glass]], Michael's gift of truth, exposes the horror beneath:

> "He drew it forth and looked at her, and he trembled with horror and disgust, for there he saw no lovely Princess Joan, but the same yellow hag, who held in one skinny hand a goblet, formed from a skull, from which she would have him drink." [0863-P1]

The transformation is complete: golden goblet → skull. The "skinny hand" holding a "skull" is death offering wine to the living. The goblet is a memento mori disguised as a loving gesture.

## The Final Test

The skull goblet represents the last barrier between Michael and Joan. All his physical trials — the sea crossing, the serpent's eggs, the heavy stones — lead to this moment of perception. If he drinks, he fails. If he sees truly, he wins. The goblet, like Fiorimonde's necklace, is an instrument that destroys those who cannot recognize what it really is.
