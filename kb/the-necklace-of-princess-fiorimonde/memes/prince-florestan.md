# Prince Florestan

Prince Florestan is the eleventh and final suitor-victim of [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] in the title story of Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. His disappearance prompts his loyal friend [[gervaise|Gervaise]] to confront the sorceress and ultimately break the [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]].

## Character Overview

Prince Florestan is a young prince from "a far off country" whose deepest bond is with his friend [[gervaise|Gervaise]]:

> "He had a dear friend named Gervaise, whom he loved better than anyone in the world."

When Florestan sees a portrait of [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]], he is immediately captivated and resolves to seek her hand in [[marriage-and-union|marriage]], despite [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s attempts to dissuade him.

## Role in the Story

Florestan's infatuation with [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s portrait is instantaneous and abso[[music-as-art|lute]]. He "at once swore he would go to her father's court, and beg that he might have her for his wife." [[gervaise|Gervaise]], sensing danger, accompanies him "with a heavy heart."

Upon meeting [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] at the fountain in [[the-royal-castle|the palace]] garden, Florestan is so overwhelmed by her beauty that he "scarcely knew how to answer her for bewilderment at her great loveliness." When [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] dismisses [[gervaise|Gervaise]] to be alone with Florestan, the prince obliges — and soon after, he becomes the eleventh bead on her necklace.

[[yolande|Yolande]] later points out his bead to [[gervaise|Gervaise]]: "last of all here is your own master Prince Florestan. Seek him now where you will and you will not find him, and you shall never see him again till the cord is cut and the charm broken."

## Rescue

When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts [[the-gold-necklace|the golden thread]] of [[the-beads|Fiorimonde's necklace]], Prince Florestan is among the last of the princes to be restored. He reappears in his royal attire and joins the other rescued kings and princes in denouncing "[[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] and her [[evil-as-deception|wickedness]]."

After the rescue, Florestan returns to his homeland with [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]], and "all lived happily to the end of their lives."

## Key Relationships

- **[[gervaise]]** — Florestan's dearest friend, whose loyalty ultimately saves him. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] loved Florestan "so well, that he would gladly have died to serve him."
- **[[princess-fiorimonde]]** — The beautiful sorceress who transforms Florestan into a bead.
- **[[yolande]]** — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s maid who identifies Florestan's bead to [[gervaise|Gervaise]].
- **The Other Suitors** — Fellow victims including [[king-pierrot]] and [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]], all freed together.

## Significance

Prince Florestan's role is twofold: he is both a victim of [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s predatory beauty and the catalyst for the story's resolution. His capture provides the emotional imperative that drives [[gervaise|Gervaise]] to action. Florestan re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the vulnerability of even good and beloved people to surface beauty — a cautionary figure whose near-tragedy illustrates the danger of judging by appearances.
