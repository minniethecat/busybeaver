# The Hero's Journey

The hero's journey in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is not the chivalric adventure of medieval romance. It is a descent into labour, obscurity, and [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] — a journey that strips the hero of rank and comfort before restoring both with new meaning. De Morgan's heroes depart as princes and return as workers, their royal identity submerged in the physical demands of their quests.

## The Journey as Labour

### Michael's Seven Years

"[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]" provides the collection's most sustained treatment of the hero's journey. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] departs to find the fairy who has stolen [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart, armed only with the knowledge that "there is no charm which cannot be broken if one does but know how." His journey strips him of everything:

> "But bid the people cease their preparations, Queen, and stop the wedding rejoicings, for there will be no wedding. No, not till I have found the fairy who has wronged my Joan, and made her set her free. Tomorrow I shall start at break of day, and journey to the farthest ends of the world."

"To the farthest ends of the world" — De Morgan invokes the geographical extreme to signal the journey's mythic scale, but the actual content of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s quest is not heroic travel but brutal labour. He works under the sun for a succession of deceptive wizards, enduring chains and impossible tasks. The serpent's eggs that refuse to hatch become a figure for [[hope-as-resilience|hope]] deferred — labour without visible progress.

The journey transforms [[prince-michael|Michael]] physically — when he returns to [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s court, he is unrecognizable, "his face burnt brown by the sun, his hands hard and blistered with toiling in the fields." The prince has become a labourer, and it is precisely this transformation that gives his return its power.

### Gervaise's Approach

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s journey in the title story follows a different pattern. Unlike [[prince-michael|Michael]], who departs in ignorance and learns through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]], [[gervaise|Gervaise]] departs already armed with knowledge:

> "There is an evil fate about the Princess Fiorimonde," quoth he; "many have gone to marry her, but where are they now?"

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s "journey" is as much investigative as physical. He approaches [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s court having studied the pattern of her suitors' disappearances, and his success depends on applying that knowledge rather than on enduring physical hardship.

### Arasmon's Wanderings

"[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" offers a journey measured in decades rather than years. [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s search for [[chrysea|Chrysea]], transformed into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] by a witch, extends across a lifetime:

His journey is one of [[love-and-loyalty|fidelity]] across time rather than distance — the transformation he seeks is not his own but [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s, and his role is simply to persist until the conditions for her restoration are met.

## The Journey's Structure

De Morgan's hero's journeys share a common structure: departure with a clear objective, a middle of deferred achievement, and a return that resolves the initial crisis. The departure is always voluntary — the hero chooses to go — and the middle is always longer than expected, testing the hero's resolve through delay rather than combat.

The journey never fails in De Morgan's collection — every hero who departs eventually succeeds. But the cost of success (seven years, a lifetime, scorched skin and blistered hands) ensures that the journey is felt as genuine ordeal rather than formulaic adventure.
