# The Three Clever Kings

"The Three Clever Kings" is the seventh tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. It is a political fable about three princes who each attempt to rule a kingdom in turn, discovering through experience that the best form of wisdom is self-knowledge.

## Plot Summary

Old King Roland lies [[death-and-mortality|dying]] and, having no son, summons his three nephews — [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]], [[aldebert|Aldebert]], and [[alderete|Alderete]] — to decide who will succeed him. Rather than choosing one, the King decrees that each shall "try it in turn": [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] first as the oldest, then [[aldebert|Aldebert]], then [[alderete|Alderete]], "and then you will know which is the best fitted to govern."

[[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] is crowned first and immediately indulges himself, staying in bed late and playing games rather than attending to affairs of state. When ministers crowd outside his bedroom demanding audiences, he throws down his crown in frustration, declaring "it is much more trouble than it is worth," and flees [[the-royal-castle|the palace]]. He becomes a contented goose-boy at a farm, where "there is no Prime Minister or Chancellor to come worrying."

[[aldebert|Aldebert]], by contrast, is eager to please everyone. He agrees to every minister's request — the Prime Minister wants to rebuild the city, the Commander-in-Chief wants to double soldiers' pay, the Chancellor demands severe austerity. When the contradictory orders collide and all three ministers confront him in fury, [[aldebert|Aldebert]] slips away as [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] did, declaring "I think I am scarcely fit to be a King."

[[alderete|Alderete]], the youngest, takes the crown last. Having observed both his cousins' failures, he approaches kingship with a blend of diligence and independence. He rises early, listens to all ministers but makes his own decisions, and earns the respect of [[the-royal-castle|the court]]. The kingdom prospers under his measured, thoughtful rule.

## Key Characters

- **King Roland** — The [[death-and-mortality|dying]] king who devises the trial-by-succession
- **[[aldovrand|Aldovrand]]** — The oldest cousin who treats kingship as a license for idleness and flees when responsibilities intrude
- **[[aldebert|Aldebert]]** — The middle cousin who tries to please everyone and is crushed by contradictory demands
- **[[alderete|Alderete]]** — The youngest cousin who learns from his predecessors' failures and finds the middle path

## Themes

The story is De Morgan's most explicit political allegory. Through the three cousins, she illustrates a taxonomy of bad and good governance: [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] the libertine ruler who neglects duty, [[aldebert|Aldebert]] the weak ruler who cannot say no, and [[alderete|Alderete]] the balanced ruler who combines listening with decisiveness. The framing device — a [[death-and-mortality|dying]] king who engineers a succession experiment — gives the tale the structure of a wisdom fable, while the comic episodes of [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] and [[aldebert|Aldebert]]'s failures provide a light satirical tone unusual in De Morgan's generally darker collection.

The story also explores the theme of self-knowledge as the prerequisite for leadership: both [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] and [[aldebert|Aldebert]] discover their own limitations through experience, while [[alderete|Alderete]]'s success comes from having watched and learned.
