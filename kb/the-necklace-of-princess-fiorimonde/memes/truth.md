# Truth

Truth in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* operates on multiple levels. It is at once a moral imperative, a strategic instrument, and a metaphysical force — the reality that sorcery can conceal but never abolish.

## Truth as Revelation

The hinge of the title story turns on [[yolande|Yolande]]'s decision to speak truth about the necklace:

> "'She is a sorceress,' she said, 'and it might be hard to kill her; besides, that might not break the spell, and bring back the princes to life. I wish I could show you the necklace, and you might count the beads, and see if I do not speak truth.'" [0203-P1]

Yolande's truth-telling is dangerous — Fiorimonde would kill her if discovered — but it is the first crack in the enchantment that has claimed so many suitors.

## Truth Strategically Withheld

[[gervaise|Gervaise]] demonstrates that sometimes the wise must withhold or invert truth to defeat evil:

> "Gervaise looked at her and in his heart he said, 'It is quite true, oh wicked Princess! There never was woman as beautiful as you, and never before did I hate a woman as I hate you now;' but aloud he said, 'No, Princess, that is not true; you are very beautiful, but I have seen a woman who is fairer than you.'" [0259-P1]

The passage divides truth into two registers — inward truth (what Gervaise thinks) and outward truth (what he says). De Morgan endorses strategic deception in the service of justice.

## Truth Demanded

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]], who lives by deception, cannot tolerate hearing truth that diminishes her:

> "'Who is she — where is she?' cried Fiorimonde, who could scarcely contain her anger. 'Bring her here at once that I may see if you speak the truth.'" [0265-P1]

Her fury at the suggestion that another woman is more beautiful reveals the fragility of her self-image. She demands truth but only to refute it.

## The Magic Glass: Truth as Vision

[[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s magic glass is the collection's emblem of truth as corrected perception. Given by his mother:

> "'The magician spoke truly,' he said to himself, 'and there will be nothing but sorrow for all those who love my poor Princess Joan.'" [0693-P1]

The glass reveals what is actually there beneath enchantment — truth not as proposition but as purified sight.

## Truth Too Late

In "The Wanderings of Arasmon," truth arrives as painful self-knowledge:

> "When she had gone Arasmon sat by the wayside and wept as though his heart would break. 'It is too true,' he said; 'I am so old and worn that when I find her she will not know me.'" [0565-P1]

This is truth as recognition — the moment when a character can no longer evade what he has become. The harp, which speaks truth through music, has tried to comfort him, but he must face the truth unaided.
