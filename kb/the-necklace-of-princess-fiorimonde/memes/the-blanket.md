# The Scarlet Blanket

The Scarlet Blanket is the central object in Mary De Morgan's fable "[[the-peddlers-pack-story|The Pedlar's Pack]]," one of the shorter, more comic tales in the collection "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories." A simple piece of merchandise — "a small scarlet blanket" — it becomes the linchpin in an escalating chain of broken [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s that ultimately brings four travellers before the town mayor for judgement.

## Origin

The blanket is part of a pedlar's pack of merchandise, displayed as he approaches a town in [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]s of making sales. As the pedlar unpacks his wares — "some shawls and stuffs" hung over the donkey's back — the scarlet blanket is placed "on the top of the other goods," where it catches the hedge-sparrow's eye [1001-ST05].

## Physical Description

The blanket is described only as "a small scarlet blanket" [1001-ST05] and "that little blanket" [1003-ST05]. Its defining physical quality is its colour — scarlet — which makes it conspicuous among the pedlar's other goods. The mayor later describes it as "the only good thing in the whole matter" [1047-ST05], suggesting it is of some quality despite its small size. The blanket's modest nature is emphasized by the hedge-sparrow's claim to be "badly in want of a blanket just now" [1003-ST05], framing it as a practical necessity rather than a luxury.

## Role in the Story

The blanket functions as the **catalyst object** in a chain-reaction fable about broken [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s and circular debt. Its role unfolds through a sequence of [[the-fateful-bargain|transaction]]s, each one dependent on the previous:

1. The hedge-sparrow desires the blanket and agrees to pay the pedlar four pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] for it [1007-ST05]
2. The pedlar agrees, planning to use the money to pay the donkey the two pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] he owes [1009-ST05]
3. The donkey needs this money to pay the raven two pieces for driving away flies [1017-ST05]
4. The raven needs his payment to satisfy the hedge-sparrow's demand for four pieces of [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] [997-ST05, 1021-ST05]

None of the four actually possesses any [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] — each has made [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s they cannot keep, and the blanket serves as the object that exposes this chain of bad faith. When the hedge-sparrow cannot pay, the pedlar cannot pay the donkey, who cannot pay the raven, who cannot pay the hedge-sparrow — a perfect circle of mutual indebtedness anchored to the blanket that none can afford.

The blanket's final role is as **confiscated goods**. When the four companions bring their dispute before the mayor, he dismisses them all as "a set of good-for-nothing fellows" and orders [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]]s for each — but claims the blanket for himself: "As for the blanket, it seems to me to be the only good thing in the whole matter, and as I cannot allow you to keep the cause of such a disturbance, I will take it for myself" [1047-ST05]. The blanket thus passes from merchandise to disputed property to spoils of office, its journey mirroring the tale's cynical view of how [[justice-and-injustice|authority]] resolves disputes — by taking what it can.

## Symbolic Significance

Unlike the magical objects that dominate De Morgan's longer tales — [[the-gold-necklace]], [[the-golden-harp]], [[the-magic-glass]] — the Scarlet Blanket is deliberately ordinary. It has no [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]], no transformative power, no supernatural origin. Its significance is entirely social: it reveals character through desire and [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]-making.

The blanket functions as a **Rorschach test for integrity**. Each character sees in it only what they want: the hedge-sparrow sees warmth, the pedlar sees profit, the mayor sees something worth taking. No one sees it as belonging rightfully to another. In this, the blanket exposes the central moral failing of the tale's world — that [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s are made casually and broken easily, with objects valued only for what they can extract from others.

The story's structure — four interlocking debts, each dependent on the settlement of another — makes the blanket a **narrative keystone**. Remove the blanket and the debt chain collapses into abstraction; with it, the tale becomes a concrete illustration of how desire for a physical object can expose systemic dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]]. The blanket's ordinariness is precisely the point: it takes no magic to reveal character, only a small scarlet blanket and four people willing to lie about paying for it.
