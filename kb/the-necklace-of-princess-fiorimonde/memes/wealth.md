# Wealth

Wealth in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is never merely material. De Morgan consistently contrasts the vulnerable wealth of gold and jewels with the lasting wealth of love, music, and communal flourishing.

## Material Wealth as Vulnerability

The collection opens with wealth presented as a target. In the title story:

> "He was king of a large rich country, and would take back his bride with him to his home." [0075-P1]

King Pierrot's wealth does not protect him — on the contrary, it makes him a desirable victim for Fiorimonde. In De Morgan's world, material wealth attracts danger rather than deflecting it.

## Wealth Lost and Found

The soldiers in "The Bread of Discontent" propose surrender as a strategy to preserve wealth:

> "'It would be better,' said the soldiers, 'to lay down our arms at once and let the enemy enter, for then we should not lose our lives as well as our city and our wealth.'" [0473-P1]

The soldiers value wealth above honor — a choice the narrative ultimately punishes. Wealth preserved by cowardice is not worth keeping.

## The True Wealth

[[arasmon|Arasmon]] learns the hardest lesson about wealth. Returning to the village he and [[chrysea|Chrysea]] had transformed:

> "'Time has changed all things strangely; but it would be hard to say which is the more altered, this village or I, for then it was sunk in poverty and wretchedness, and now it has gained happiness and wealth, and I, who was so happy and glad, now am broken-down and worn. I have lost my only wealth, my wife Chrysea.'" [0553-P1]

The passage turns on the word "only." The village's collective wealth — its neat gardens and cheerful voices — contrasts with Arasmon's personal poverty. His "only wealth" is Chrysea, and without her, all other abundance is meaningless.

## Wealth as Starting Point

De Morgan frequently opens her stories with characters who are "rich and happy":

> "Long ago, in the days of fairies, there lived a King and Queen, who were rich and happy." [0597-P1]

This formula — "rich and happy" — establishes wealth as the default condition of fairy-tale royalty. But the conjunction is deceptive: the stories that follow show that "rich" does not guarantee "happy," and that the two can be severed.
