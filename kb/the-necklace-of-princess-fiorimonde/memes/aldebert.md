# Aldebert

Aldebert is the middle cousin in "[[the-three-clever-kings-story|The Three Clever Kings]]," the final story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection. He is the second to attempt kingship under King Roland's rotational succession plan, and his failure illustrates the perils of trying to please everyone.

## Character Overview

When Aldebert learns that his cousin [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]] has fled the throne, his reaction is apprehension rather than [[greed-and-beauty|ambition]]:

> "I will do my best," quoth he; "but I really know very little about the matter. However, you must tell me, and I will do whatever you direct."

The Prime Minister and Chancellor are thrilled: "Now we have got the right sort of King," they declare, expecting a pliable ruler. Aldebert is crowned with "great rejoicings all over the country."

## Role in the Story

### The People-Pleaser King

Aldebert's fatal f[[justice-and-injustice|law]] is his inability to say no. In rapid succession on his first day:

- The **Prime Minister** requests funds to rebuild the crumbling city. Aldebert: "Pray let them begin building at once."
- The **Commander-in-Chief** requests doubled soldiers' pay to quell discontent. Aldebert: "By all means, that will certainly be the best way."
- The **Chancellor** reports the treasury is nearly empty and demands spending cuts and pay reductions. Aldebert: "I have no doubt you are quite right. You know best, let it be done as you wish."

The next morning, all three officials burst into his chamber in fury — each having been [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]d contradictory things. The Prime Minister's building project has been defunded; the Commander-in-Chief's pay raise has become a pay cut; the Chancellor's austerity has been undermined.

### The Tinker

Faced with three angry ministers, Aldebert's response mirrors his cousin's:

> "I am sure you are each of you quite right; but I think I am scarcely fit to be a King. Indeed I think you had better find my cousin Alderete, and let him be crowned, and I will seek my fortune elsewhere."

He slips away before anyone can stop him and encounters a travelling tinker mending tin cans by the roadside. Fascinated by the craft, he asks to become an apprentice: "Do you think I could learn how to do it if you would teach me?" The tinker agrees, and Aldebert takes to the trade with genuine enthusiasm.

When the Prime Minister and Chancellor later find him and beg him to return, he refuses, proudly showing off his work: "See how clever I have grown; I am learning to be a tinker, and I mended that hole all myself."

## Key Relationships

- **[[aldovrand]]** — His elder cousin, whose abdication forces Aldebert onto the throne
- **[[alderete]]** — His younger cousin, the third to attempt kingship
- **The Prime Minister and Chancellor** — The officials whose contradictory demands expose Aldebert's unsuitability

## Significance

Aldebert's failure is more sympathetic than [[aldovrand|Aldovrand]]'s. He genuinely tries — rising early, listening to all advice, attempting to accommodate every request. His tragedy is that good intentions without independent [[justice-and-injustice|judgment]] are worse than useless. De Morgan's satire targets not Aldebert's character but the fantasy that a ruler can satisfy everyone. His [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] as a tinker — a craft requiring actual skill and producing visible results — contrasts pointedly with the impossible abstractions of governance.
