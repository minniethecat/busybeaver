# Fate and Transformation

Physical transformation is the most visible form of magic in Mary De Morgan's collection, but it is never merely spectacular. Every metamorphosis — [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s suitors into beads, [[chrysea|Chrysea]] into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]], the pedlar's animals into speaking witnesses — encodes a moral [[justice-and-injustice|judgment]]. In De Morgan's universe, what you become reflects what you are, and fate is not arbitrary but the visible expression of character.

## Overview

The collection [[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] transformation as a form of **moral legibility**: the body is made to reflect the soul. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s suitors become beads — beautiful, silent, and strung together as ornaments of her [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] — because they pursued beauty without wisdom. [[chrysea|Chrysea]] becomes a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] — beautiful and silent — because her voice, which broke the elves' spell, must be preserved in a form that awaits the right player to restore it. The transformations are never meaningless; each is a [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]], a preservation, or a revelation.

The concept of **fate** in De Morgan operates through this logic of transformation. Characters do not suffer random misfortune; they encounter [[punishment-and-reward|consequences]] that are proportional, poetic, and inescapable. This distinguishes De Morgan's tales from the arbitrary cruelty found in some traditional fairy tales and aligns her with the moral clarity of the Victorian fairy-tale revival.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — The Bead-Metamorphosis

[[the-beads|Fiorimonde's necklace]] is the collection's central image of transformation as moral consequence. Each suitor, seduced by her beauty, is reduced to a bead — a beautiful object without agency:

> "If once you join your fingers around the string you too will meet the fate of your lovers, and hang upon it yourself."

The necklace thus literalizes [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s worldview: people are ornaments to be collected. Her own transformation into the thirteenth bead completes the pattern — the collector becomes the collected, and the predator meets her prey's fate. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] recognizes the poetic logic:

> "Oh, clever Princess!" cried he, laughing, "knew that the thirteenth was the wicked Princess, who had herself met the evil fate she had prepared for so many others."

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Transformation as Preservation

[[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s metamorphosis into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]] is more complex than simple [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]]. The elves transform her not to destroy but to neutralize — she who sang their secret song must be silenced. Yet the [[music-as-art|harp]] preserves her essence: it is [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en, beautiful, and produces music of extraordinary sweetness. Her transformation is a kind of suspended animation — she awaits the right player ([[arasmon|Arasmon]]) and the right tune (the blackbird's song) to restore her.

The motif of **waiting** is crucial: [[chrysea|Chrysea]] hangs on the tree for decades while [[arasmon|Arasmon]] wanders. Transformation here is not final but conditional — it lasts until the conditions for reversal are met. This makes De Morgan's metamorphoses more [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]ful than Kafka's or Ovid's: transformation is a state, not a sentence.

### "The Pedlar's Pack" — Transformation as Revelation

The pedlar's magical pack transforms a donkey, raven, and hedge-sparrow into speaking witnesses. Their speech reveals hidden [[truth-and-deception|truth]]s — the donkey's complaints about his master, the raven's account of his mistreatment. Here transformation serves [[truth-and-deception|truth]]-telling: the animals' changed state exposes in[[punishment-and-reward|justice]] that their silent forms concealed.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — The Serpent's Eggs

The wizard's serpent guarding its eggs re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] a different kind of transformation: gestation and waiting. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]] must wait seven years for the eggs to hatch, during which [[princess-joan|Joan]] nearly marries another. The transformation here is temporal — the passage of time itself becomes a metamorphosis, testing loyalty across years.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's treatment of transformation draws on Ovidian tradition but diverges in its moral framework. Ovid's metamorphoses often serve capricious gods; De Morgan's serve an intelligible moral order. The influence of George MacDonald — who argued that fairy tales should embody "Law" rather than "Caprice" — is evident. Each transformation in De Morgan reads as a natural consequence, not a deus ex machina.
