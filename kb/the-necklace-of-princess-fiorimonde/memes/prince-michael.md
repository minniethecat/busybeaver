# Prince Michael

Prince Michael is the protagonist of "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the fourth story in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. His seven-year quest to recover his beloved's stolen heart makes him one of De Morgan's most fully realized heroic figures.

## Character Overview

Prince Michael is the son of a King and Queen who live near a mysterious round tower inhabited by an ancient white wizard. Unlike everyone else in the kingdom — who fear the sorcerer — Michael visits him freely:

> "No one in all the country had ever ventured to climb the tower and see the old man at his work except Prince Michael, who knew the old sorcerer well and did not fear him at all, but went up and down the tower as he chose."

It is during one such visit that Michael sees [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]]'s living portrait in the wizard's magical book — a book containing "the portraits of all the men and women in the world" that "move, and look just like the originals." The wizard warns him: "she will bring nothing but trouble to all who know her." But Michael cannot forget her face.

## Role in the Story

### The Discovery

Michael arranges to marry [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]], but he first disguises himself as a poor man to observe her at her own court. He discovers a devastating [[truth-and-deception|truth]]: [[princess-joan|Joan]] feels nothing — no love, no pity, no sorrow. When she dismisses a starving beggar with cold indifference, Michael realizes the wizard's warning was literal.

The wizard reveals the cause: a fairy stole [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart at birth as revenge against her mother's [[greed-and-beauty|pride]]. [[princess-joan|Joan]] is "hard and cold as stone" because she literally has no heart. Michael makes a fateful vow:

> "I have sworn that I will not marry Joan till she loves me as I do her, neither can I return to my home and forget her."

### The Seven-Year Quest

Armed with a piece of magic glass from the wizard — through which he can track [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart, which appears as a crimson star — Michael sets out on a quest that lasts seven years. He journeys "over all the world, through many countries, and saw many strange things and had many adventures." Each night he gazes through the glass at the crimson star, measuring his progress.

### The Return

Michael returns on the very day [[princess-joan|Joan]] is to be married against her will to old [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]. Still in his travel-worn disguise, he waits by the roadside as [[the-wedding-feast|the wedding]] procession passes. When [[princess-joan|Joan]] sees him, the heart he has recovered stirs, and she recognizes him:

> "It is Prince Michael, who went seven years ago, and who we all thought was dead, and he is returned in time to marry our Princess."

The people rejoice, [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] is dismissed, and Michael — dressed in "purple and [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]" — finally marries [[princess-joan|Joan]]. He sends messengers to "his father and mother and the old wizard, that they might come and see how he had come home victorious."

## Key Relationships

- **[[princess-joan|Princess Joan]]** — Michael's beloved, whose stolen heart he quests to recover
- **The White Wizard** — Michael's mentor, who provides the magic glass and counsels him
- **[[king-lambert|King Lambert]]** — The old, ugly king who tries to marry [[princess-joan|Joan]]; Michael's rival
- **The Fairy** — The unseen antagonist who stole [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart

## Significance

Prince Michael is De Morgan's fullest portrait of devoted love. Unlike the story's other suitors — who are motivated by beauty or political advantage — Michael loves [[princess-joan|Joan]] despite her emotional emptiness. His seven-year quest makes him an active hero rather than a passive victim, setting him apart from the suitors in the title story. His use of the wizard's magic glass, and his patient tracking of the crimson star, represent the fairy tale's insistence that [[love-and-loyalty|true love]] requires work, not just feeling. The number seven — years of service, like Jacob for Rachel — gives his quest biblical resonance.
