# The Bread of Discontent

"The Bread of Discontent" is the sixth tale in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories*. It is a satirical fable that explores mass psychology through the device of magical bread that makes everyone who eats it wretchedly discontented.

## Plot Summary

A baker with a violent temper throws burnt bread around his kitchen in a rage. One loaf cracks open and a "tiny thin black man, no thicker than an eel" — an imp — emerges. The imp offers to live in the baker's oven and ensure his bread bakes perfectly, in ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] for a home. He warns the baker that the bread "would make them rather unhappy, but that will not hurt you, as you need not eat it yourself."

The imp's bread tastes excellent but transforms the disposition of everyone who eats it. The chief [[punishment-and-reward|justice]], who has just inherited twenty thousand pounds, laments that it should have been thirty thousand. The doctor, who saved a patient's life, wishes he had let the man die rather than face future misfortunes. A newlywed husband decides that [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] must end in sorrow and begins to [[fear-and-power|dread]] the inevitable. A prosperous farmer fears his solid farmhouse will collapse. Even the farmer's wife wishes her children had been born in a different order.

Soon the entire village is quarrelling, weeping, and complaining — except the baker, who remains cheerful as his trade flourishes. When the doctor and farmer hear the baker whistling happily, they are outraged and drag him before the [[punishment-and-reward|justice]], who orders him publicly flogged. The mob attacks the baker's house, smashing the oven. The imp escapes, and immediately the crowd's rage evaporates — everyone looks at each other in bewilderment, wondering what the trouble was.

The baker mends his oven and learns [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] from the experience: "now he knew that there were worse things than having his loaves burnt black."

## Key Characters

- **The Baker** — The protagonist, whose violent temper summons the imp but who ultimately learns gratitude
- **The Imp** — A "tiny thin black man" who bakes magical bread of discontent in the baker's oven
- **The Justice** — Receives a fortune yet finds fault with the amount
- **The Doctor** — Cures a patient but regrets the misery the man will face in future
- **The Newlyweds** — Turn from bliss to [[despair-and-endurance|despair]] over the inevitability of [[happiness-and-contentment|happiness]] ending
- **The Farmer and His Wife** — Prosperous and healthy, yet find reasons to complain

## Themes

The story is a masterful study of human ingratitude. The bread does not cause genuine misfortune — it merely transforms the eater's perception, making every blessing appear inadequate. De Morgan's satirical insight is that discontent can affect anyone regardless of circumstance: the rich, the loving, the healthy, and the successful all prove equally vulnerable. The story's resolution suggests that the capacity for [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] is a skill that must be learned, often through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]]: the baker only appreciates his lot after being flogged.

The tale also functions as a psychological allegory for mass hysteria: an external agent (the imp's bread) infects the village with shared misery, which escalates into mob violence before dissipating as mysteriously as it began.
