# The Fateful Bargain

The fateful bargain in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is a moment of apparent advantage that conceals a hidden cost. Characters agree to terms whose full implications they do not understand, and the bargain becomes the mechanism through which their true situation is revealed. De Morgan treats the bargain not as a neutral ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] but as a moral test: the terms a character accepts reveal the character's values, and the [[punishment-and-reward|consequences]] of a bad bargain are self-inflicted.

## Bargains with Magic

### The Necklace as Bargain

The title story's central device — the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en necklace — is itself a kind of bargain. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] makes an implicit deal with [[magic-and-witchcraft|dark magic]]: she gains the power to eliminate suitors who threaten her independence, but each use of [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] tightens its hold. The necklace's growth is the visible marker of the bargain's accumulating cost. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] believes she is using [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]], but [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] is also using her — the bargain transforms both parties, and only the destruction of the object can end the pact.

### The Wizards' False Promises

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," [[prince-michael|Michael]] encounters a series of wizards who offer him deals — labour in ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] for magical assistance. Each wizard [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s to help him find [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s heart, but each bargain proves hollow. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s bargains with the wizards are one-sided — he gives, they take, and no reciprocal value flows back to him. De Morgan uses these false bargains to illustrate the difference between a genuine ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] and exploitation disguised as one.

## Bargains and Their Consequences

### The Queen's Consent

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the Queen's consent to [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] to [[king-lambert|King Lambert]] is itself a bargain — the seven-year waiting period was a deal, and when it expires, the Queen believes she is bound by its terms:

> "The Queen is in sore distress about it, and for seven years refused her consent; but they will be over tomorrow, and so they will be wed."

The Queen treats the seven-year delay as a binding contract — once the term expires, she owes her consent. But [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s return reveals that the bargain was never truly binding; it was a hedge against [[hope-as-resilience|hope]], and [[hope-as-resilience|hope]]'s fulfillment dissolves the obligation.

In "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]," the villagers make an implicit bargain with the spell that afflicts them — they accept material comfort in ex[[fate-and-transformation|change]] for meaning and purpose. The baker's complaint illustrates the bargain's failure:
