# Wedding

The wedding in Mary De Morgan's fairy tales is never merely a romantic conclusion. It functions as a public arena where identities are tested, truths are revealed, and false claimants are exposed before the community.

## The Wedding as Trap

In "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde," the wedding is the mechanism by which [[princess-fiorimonde|the Princess]] ensnares her victims. Suitor after suitor arrives for the ceremony, only to vanish:

> "Come they did, kings and princes from south and north, east and west — King Adrian, Prince Sigbert, Prince Algar, and many more — but though all went well till the wedding morning, when it was time to go to church, no bridegroom was to be found." [0147-P1]

The wedding becomes a site of dread rather than celebration — a threshold that no groom survives to cross.

## The Rival Wedding

In "The Heart of Princess Joan," the wedding provides the climactic stage for [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s return after seven years. [[king-lambert|King Lambert]]'s wedding to [[princess-joan|Joan]] is interrupted by Michael's arrival:

> "When the people heard her, they cried, 'It is Prince Michael, who went seven years ago, and who we all thought was dead, and he is returned in time to marry our Princess. Now will we indeed have a wedding!'" [0961-P1]

The false wedding is replaced by the true one — the ritual serves as the mechanism of public vindication.

## The Deferred Wedding

Joan's childlike misunderstanding of the wedding's significance underscores De Morgan's moral framework:

> "'And why do you go?' said Joan; 'I had thought there would be a grand wedding, and I should have all the gifts that are being prepared for me, and now I shall have nothing.'" [0731-P1]

Joan's immaturity is revealed through her view of the wedding as a gift-giving occasion rather than a sacred commitment. Her transformation requires she first lose her heart entirely before she can truly participate in the ritual.
