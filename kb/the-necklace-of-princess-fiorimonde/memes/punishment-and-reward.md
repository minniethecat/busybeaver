# Punishment and Reward

Punishment and reward in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* are not arbitrary dispensations of authorial favour. They are the logical outcomes of each character's moral trajectory — the villain's destruction is implicit in the villain's methods, and the hero's reward is earned through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] and courage. De Morgan's tales are relentlessly moral without being moralistic: consequences follow choices with the inevitability of natural [[justice-and-injustice|law]].

## Punishment as Self-Destruction

### Fiorimonde's Fate

The collection's most spectacular [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] is [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s own. The [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en necklace that she used to enchant twelve victims becomes the instrument of her destruction. When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts the thread, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] vanishes — transformed not into a bead (which would preserve her for possible restoration) but into something far more final:

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] is not externally imposed but self-inflicted — she is destroyed by the very magic she wielded. De Morgan's moral logic is clear: [[evil-as-deception|evil]] contains the seeds of its own undoing. The necklace that gave [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] power over others becomes, in [[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s hands, the mechanism of her obliteration.

### The Wizards Deceived

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the wizards who exploit [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s labour — making him work under false [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]s of magical assistance — are not punished in any dramatic scene. Their [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] is structural: their [[truth-and-deception|deception]]s fail, [[prince-michael|Michael]] succeeds despite them, and their power is rendered irrelevant rather than destroyed. De Morgan's lesser villains are often punished by obsolescence rather than annihilation — their schemes simply stop mattering.

## Reward as Union

### Gervaise and Yolande

[[gervaise|Gervaise]]'s reward for breaking [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]] is [[yolande|Yolande]] — not a princess he has never met but the servant who risked her life to warn him. Their embrace at the story's climax is the reward for mutual courage:

De Morgan consistently rewards her heroes with the right partner — not the most beautiful or the highest-born, but the one who has demonstrated matching courage and loyalty. [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] earn each other through shared danger.

### Michael and Joan

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s reward is [[princess-joan|Joan]] — but only after seven years of labour. The reward is not merely the princess but the restored relationship: [[princess-joan|Joan]] with her heart back, capable of loving him as she could not when he departed. The reward is the full person, not the enchanted shell.

## The Economy of Consequence

De Morgan's moral economy is consistent across the collection. Punishment is self-generated — villains are destroyed by their own methods. Reward is earned — heroes receive not [[wealth-and-poverty|wealth]] or power but [[marriage-and-union|union]] and recognition. The narrative events of [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] and reward are not arbitrary endings but the working-out of principles established from the story's first pages.

This distinguishes De Morgan from the more punitive tradition of the Grimms, where villains are often subjected to grotesque [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]]s (dancing in red-hot shoes, being rolled downhill in a barrel of nails) that exceed their crimes. De Morgan's [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]]s are proportionate and self-executing — the villain's downfall follows from the villain's choices with no need for external vengeance.
