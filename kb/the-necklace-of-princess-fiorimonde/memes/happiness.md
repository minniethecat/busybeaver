# Happiness

Happiness in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* is a baseline, not a goal. Characters begin happy, lose their happiness through enchantment or separation, and recover it only after long trial. De Morgan treats sustained happiness as fragile and intermittent — something that can be recognized only by contrast with its absence.

## Happiness as Starting Point

The collection's heroes typically begin in happiness. [[prince-michael|Prince Michael]]'s parents are described in the archetypal terms:

> "Long ago, in the days of fairies, there lived a King and Queen, who were rich and happy." [0597-P1]

This is the fairy-tale opening — happiness as the unexamined condition. De Morgan uses it not as an ending but as a setup for loss.

## Happiness in Union

The strongest happiness in the collection is marital. When [[gervaise|Gervaise]] and [[yolande|Yolande]] are reunited:

> "And Gervaise married Yolande, and they went back with Prince Florestan to their home, and all lived happily to the end of their lives." [0347-P1]

This is De Morgan's most conventional fairy-tale ending — the happiness of union after peril. But it is hard-won, earned through Yolande's long imprisonment and Gervaise's dangerous confrontation with Fiorimonde.

## Happiness as Shared Art

[[arasmon|Arasmon]] and [[chrysea|Chrysea]] represent happiness as creative partnership:

> "Both were young and lovely, and were as happy as the day is long, for they loved each other dearly, and liked wandering about seeing new countries and people and making sweet music." [0353-P1]

The phrase "as happy as the day is long" recurs throughout the collection as De Morgan's formula for perfect contentment. Happiness here is active — it consists in movement, music, and mutual love.

## The Loss of Happiness

Happiness once lost defines the collection's central arc. Arasmon's lament after losing Chrysea:

> "'How happy you must be,' said Arasmon. 'I shall never see Chrysea again,' and as he spoke he struck a chord on the harp." [0541-P1]

The contrast between his present desolation and remembered happiness gives his grief its power.

## Happiness Restored

De Morgan's most complex treatment of happiness comes through restoration rather than new acquisition:

> "'It is many, many a long year since I was here,' he said. 'Time has changed all things strangely; but it would be hard to say which is the more altered, this village or I, for then it was sunk in poverty and wretchedness, and now it has gained happiness and wealth, and I, who was so happy and glad, now am broken-down and worn.'" [0553-P1]

The village has gained happiness while Arasmon has lost his — an inversion that captures De Morgan's sense that happiness is not portable. It belongs to places and seasons, not to individuals.

## The Happiness of New Life

The birth of [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] occasions communal happiness:

> "Time passed, and the Queen had a little baby — a daughter whom they called Joan — and the bells were rung, and there were great rejoicings all over the country, and the King and Queen were happy as the day is long." [0601-P1]

This "happy as the day is long" precedes the story's tragedy — the fairy's curse that steals Joan's heart. De Morgan repeatedly pairs the formula of happiness with impending loss, as if to remind readers that happiness in fairy tales is always conditional.
