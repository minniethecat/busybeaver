# Hope as Resilience

Hope in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* appears most often in moments of crisis, functioning as a lifeline that distinguishes the steadfast from the broken. Rather than a passive wish, hope is depicted as an active moral faculty — a determination to persist that characters either cultivate or lose. Across seven tales, De Morgan traces hope's fragility and its power.

## Overview

Hope in De Morgan's stories follows three distinct narrative arcs. First, it serves as **the engine of [[despair-and-endurance|perseverance]]** — characters who retain hope continue striving through seemingly impossible trials. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s years of labour in "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," the aged [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s search for [[chrysea|Chrysea]], and the spellbound villagers' [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]] all depend on hope's sustaining force. Second, hope's **fragility** is dramatized through the temptation toward [[despair-and-endurance|despair]] — the baker in "[[the-bread-and-discontents-story|The Bread of Discontent]]" illustrates how easily hope can curdle into complaint when material comfort is achieved. Third, hope functions as a **moral marker** — those who give up hope, like the soldiers who counsel surrender in "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," reveal a deficiency of character that De Morgan implicitly critiques.

De Morgan's treatment of hope is notably dialectical: it is presented not as simple optimism but as a capacity that must be tested by [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] and choosing to continue despite it.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Hope Deferred and Vindicated

The motif of hope appears throughout the title story in the context of [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s suitors. The old King's hope for his daughter's [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] is repeatedly defeated:

> "The old King was sadly frightened, and would fain have given up all hope of finding a husband for the Princess, but now she implored him, with tears in her eyes, not to let her be disgraced in this way."

The King's hope, sustained against mounting evidence, is portrayed as both admirable and blind — his persistence enables the cycle of disappearances to continue. It is [[yolande|Yolande]], the servant, who sees the [[truth-and-deception|truth]] that hope obscures. The resolution comes through [[gervaise|Gervaise]], who approaches not with naive hope but with knowledge and strategy, ultimately breaking [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]]'s [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]].

[[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]] embodies cautious hope tempered by foreknowledge of danger:

> "There is an evil fate about the Princess Fiorimonde," quoth he; "many have gone to marry her, but where are they now?"

His willingness to proceed despite this knowledge distinguishes his hope from the blind hope of earlier suitors — he hopes to succeed where others failed because he understands what he faces.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — Hope Sustained Across Years

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s quest for [[princess-joan|Princess Joan]] provides the collection's most sustained treatment of hope as persevering force. His father, the King, offers a paternal expression of hope at the outset:

> "And now, my son, all is arranged for you to journey in state to her father's court and bring back your bride, so now I hope that you are happy and wish for nothing more."

[[prince-michael|Michael]]'s own hope is tested through years of brutal labour — the chains on his arms, the scorching sun, the deceptive wizards. His hope survives not through optimism but through dogged refusal to abandon [[the-heros-journey|the quest]], even when the serpent's eggs refuse to hatch:

> "But the days passed, and the serpent never moved from its eggs, and Michael's heart began to be oppressed with fear."

The passage from hope to fear to [[despair-and-endurance|despair]] traces [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s psychological arc. Yet De Morgan ensures that hope ultimately prevails — [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s [[despair-and-endurance|endurance]] is [[punishment-and-reward|reward]]ed, and he wins [[princess-joan|Joan]]. The narrative logic is clear: hope sustained through [[despair-and-endurance|suffering]] yields transformation.

### "The Three Clever Kings" — Hope and Good Governance

The three kings turn to Prince [[alderete|Alderete]] with the refrain of hope:

> "This will never do," cried they; "we must go at once to Prince Alderete; and let us hope he may do better than his cousins."

[[alderete|Alderete]]'s response reframes hope as active [[happiness-and-contentment|contentment]] rather than passive waiting. Having chosen goose-herding over kingship, he embodies a hope that has already been fulfilled:

> "Now, here I lie peacefully all day long and watch my geese, and it is much nicer than being King."

His hope is not deferred to some future achievement but realized in present simplicity. This inversion — hope as [[happiness-and-contentment|satisfaction]] with what one has rather than desire for what one lacks — is De Morgan's most sophisticated treatment of the theme.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Hope Against Hope

[[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s decades-long search for [[chrysea|Chrysea]], after her transformation into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en [[music-as-art|harp]], re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] hope sustained beyond all rational expectation. The narrative acknowledges the implausibility of his quest while validating its ultimate success — De Morgan endorses hope that reason would dismiss as futile.

## Literary Context

Victorian fairy tales often used hope as a didactic virtue — Andersen's little mermaid hopes for an immortal soul, Kingsley's Tom hopes for moral transformation. De Morgan's treatment departs from the didactic tradition by rendering hope as existential rather than moralistic: characters do not hope because they are good; they are good because they continue to hope. This aligns her more closely with George MacDonald's theological fairy tales, where hope is a form of faith, than with the cautionary tradition.
