# The Gold Necklace

The Gold Necklace is the central magical object in Mary De Morgan's "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]." Created by the old witch of the mountain, it serves as both a tool of entrapment and a symbol of [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] and cruelty.

## Origin

The necklace was created by the old witch who lived in a hut on a lonely mountain. When [[princess-fiorimonde|Princess Fiorimonde]] complained that her father wanted her to marry, [[the-old-witch|the witch]] offered her a choice of transformations for her suitors — dogs, birds, or beads. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] chose the beads, crying: "The necklace! the necklace! That will be best of all, to sling them upon a string and wear them around my throat."

## Physical Description

The necklace is made of "a long [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread" whose ends were joined together invisibly — "no one could see the joins, and however much you pulled, it would not break." It could easily slip over [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s head but was unbreakable by force.

## Magical Properties

The necklace possesses two key magical properties:

1. **Transformation**: Anyone who closes their fingers around the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] string is instantly transformed into a bead — "strung upon it as bright hard beads." Each bead is unique, described as "clear as crystal, but shining with all colours — green, blue, and [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]."

2. **Self-protection**: The wearer is safe as long as they do not close their own fingers around the string. The witch explicitly warns: "if once you join your fingers around the string you too will meet the fate of your lovers."

## The Beads

Over the course of the story, twelve beads accumulate on the necklace:
1. [[king-pierrot|King Pierrot]]
2. [[prince-hildebrandt|Prince Hildebrandt]]
3. King Adrian
4. Prince Sigbert
5. Prince Algar
6. Cenred
7. Pharamond
8. Baldwyn
9. Leofric
10. Raoul
11. [[prince-florestan|Prince Florestan]]
12. [[yolande|Yolande]]

In the story's climax, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] cuts the [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]] thread while [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] sleeps. As the string is cut, "the twelve beads rolled on to the floor," and the victims are restored to life. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] herself, having also grasped the cord, becomes the thirteenth bead.

## Symbolic Significance

The necklace symbolizes the corrupting nature of [[greed-and-beauty|vanity]] and power. [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]] literally wears her victims as ornaments — a powerful metaphor for how [[greed-and-beauty|selfishness]] consumes others for personal adornment. The fact that she herself ultimately becomes a bead on her own necklace completes the moral arc: those who live by entrapment will themselves be trapped.
