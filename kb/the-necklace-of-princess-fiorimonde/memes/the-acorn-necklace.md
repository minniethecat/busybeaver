# The Acorn Necklace

The acorn necklace in Mary De Morgan's *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]* is the collection's most elegant symbol of the triumph of simplicity over artifice. While Fiorimonde's golden chain enslaves kings and princes, Gervaise's string of forest trinkets defeats her.

## A Necklace from the Woods

After witnessing Yolande's transformation into a bead, [[gervaise|Gervaise]] retreats to the forest at dawn:

> "Next morning when the sun rose, Gervaise started off into the woods, and there he plucked acorns and haws, and hips, and strung them on to a string to form a rude necklace. This he hid in his bosom, and then went back to the palace without telling anyone." [0295-P1]

The materials — acorns, haws, hips — are the opposite of Fiorimonde's gold. They are free, abundant, and living. Gervaise's "rude necklace" is the anti-necklace: made in minutes from the forest floor, carrying no enchantment but the maker's cleverness.

## The Bait

When Gervaise confronts [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]], he offers his simple creation as a counter to her arrogance:

> "'It is true I have never seen beads like yours, but I have a necklace here, which pleases me better;' and from his pocket he drew the haws and acorns, which he had strung together." [0311-P1]

The phrase "pleases me better" is the key. Fiorimonde cannot tolerate anything being preferred to her or her possessions. The acorn necklace is not valuable — but Gervaise's preference for it over her golden chain is an insult she cannot bear.

## The Exchange

Gervaise proposes a trade — his acorn necklace for hers. Driven by vanity and rage:

> "Fiorimonde herself was nowhere to be seen. Gervaise bent down over the necklace as it lay upon the grass, and, with a smile, counted thirteen beads; and he knew that the thirteenth was the wicked Princess." [0321-P1]

The acorn necklace was never the real weapon — it was bait. Fiorimonde's own greed causes her to touch her golden chain, triggering the transformation that traps her as its final bead.

## The Severing

With Fiorimonde imprisoned in her own creation, Gervaise completes the liberation:

> "Taking from his breast a sharp dagger, proceeded to cut the golden thread on which the beads were strung and as he clave it in two there came a mighty noise like a clap of thunder." [0329-P1]

The dagger cuts what Fiorimonde declared unbreakable. The "mighty noise like a clap of thunder" marks the cosmic scale of the liberation — twelve princes and Yolande restored, and a kingdom freed from its predator.
