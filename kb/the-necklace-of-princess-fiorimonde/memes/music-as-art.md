# Music as Art

While singing in Mary De Morgan's collection carries direct magical agency, instrumental music occupies a subtler but equally significant role: it is the art of preservation, memory, and emotional [[truth-and-deception|truth]]. The motif centers on [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s lute and harp in "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," the story that most fully explores music as a transformative human practice.

## Overview

De Morgan distinguishes between two modes of musical expression. **Singing** (treated in [[singing-as-power]]) directly alters reality — [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s voice breaks spells, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s song ensnares victims. **Instrumental music**, by contrast, operates through indirection: it moves listeners emotionally, preserves memory across decades, and ultimately restores what was lost. The lute re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] companionship and shared art; the harp re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] separation and the persistence of love through time.

The distinction maps onto a broader moral framework. Singing is immediate, embodied, and carries risk — [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is transformed into an instrument herself, a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en harp. Playing an instrument is mediate, disciplined, and requires patience — [[arasmon|Arasmon]] must wander for decades before the harp's music reunites him with his wife.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — The Lute and the Harp

[[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s lute-playing is established at the story's opening as equal and complementary to [[chrysea|Chrysea]]'s singing:

> "Arasmon played upon a lute to which Chrysea sang, and their music was so beautiful that people followed them in crowds and gave them as much money as they wanted."

The pairing of lute and voice re[[the-exchange-of-gifts|presents]] an ideal [[marriage-and-union|union]] — two art forms, two people, in perfect harmony. When [[chrysea|Chrysea]] is transformed into a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en harp by the vengeful elves, the separation of instrument and voice becomes the story's central tragedy. [[arasmon|Arasmon]], now alone, carries only his lute.

The [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en harp that appears decades later, hanging from the tree where [[chrysea|Chrysea]] once stood, is both a memorial and a [[the-sacred-vow|promise]]. The blackbird's song — the very magic tune [[chrysea|Chrysea]] first learned on [[the-moorland|the moor]] — bridges the years:

> "Dear blackbird," said Arasmon, looking up to it, "I wish your singing could tell me where to find my wife Chrysea."

The resolution comes when [[arasmon|Arasmon]] plays the blackbird's tune on the harp, restoring [[chrysea|Chrysea]] to human form. Instrumental music here functions as the key to a lock — a precise mechanism that, when correctly performed, undoes [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]]. Unlike singing's intuitive power, instrumental music requires skill, recognition, and the right instrument at the right moment.

### The Collection Beyond Arasmon

Outside "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]," instrumental music appears more fleetingly but consistently signals moments of emotional or social significance. In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," [[prince-michael|Michael]] hears "music and laughter" from the wizard's [[the-royal-castle|castle]] — sounds of merriment that mask imprisonment. In "[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]]," [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s "play" with a [[wealth-and-poverty|gold]]en ball and with her suitors' affections frames her as a performer who treats others as instruments.

The contrast is instructive: where [[arasmon|Arasmon]]'s playing is collaborative and generous, [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s games are solitary and predatory. De Morgan thus uses instrumental references to mark the moral character of her protagonists.

## Literary Context

De Morgan's elevation of music reflects the Victorian aesthetic movement's investment in music as the purest art form. Her specific emphasis on the **lute** — an instrument associated with troubadours and medieval romance — situates "[[the-wanderings-of-arasmon-story|The Wanderings of Arasmon]]" within a pre-modern tradition where the wandering musician embodies freedom, artistry, and the pursuit of love. The transformation of a singer into a **harp** draws on Celtic and Germanic folklore, where harps often serve as bridges between worlds.
