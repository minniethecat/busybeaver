# Death and Mortality

Death in Mary De Morgan's 1880 collection *[[the-necklace-of-princess-fiorimonde-story|The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde]] and Other Stories* occupies an ambiguous space between presence and absence. Characters are threatened with death, mourned as dead, and believed dead — yet death as a final, irreversible event is remarkably rare. Instead, De Morgan treats mortality as a boundary that can be blurred by [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]], a fate that hovers but rarely strikes, and a rhetorical figure deployed by characters to express the gravity of their circumstances.

## Overview

Death in De Morgan's stories takes three primary forms. First, **death as threat** — the possibility of death invoked to create narrative stakes, as when [[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s suitors vanish and are presumed dead. Second, **death as rhetorical frame** — characters speak of death to express extremity of emotion or consequence. Third, **death as transformation** — the boundary between "dead" and "enchanted" is deliberately blurred, with characters who are not dead but have been removed from the living world in ways that mimic death.

Significantly, true death — the cessation of life — almost never occurs in these stories. Characters vanish, are transformed, or are enchanted, but they rarely die in the definitive sense. De Morgan's fairy-tale world is one where mortality can be suspended, deferred, or reversed through magic.

## Manifestations in the Stories

### "The Necklace of Princess Fiorimonde" — Death as Disappearance

The title story's central device — [[the-beads|the enchanted necklace]] — collapses the distinction between death and disappearance. Each suitor who vanishes into a bead is, from the perspective of [[the-royal-castle|the court]], dead. The Princess herself exploits this ambiguity:

> "Then let it be put off, my father," cried the Princess, "and tomorrow we shall know if it is for a wedding, or a funeral, we must dress."

[[princess-fiorimonde|Fiorimonde]]'s rhetorical opposition of [[wedding-as-ritual|wedding]] and funeral positions death as the alternative to [[marriage-and-union|marriage]] — but it is a false binary, since her suitors are neither married nor dead in the ordinary sense. They are suspended, transformed into objects, awaiting restoration.

The courtiers and the old King treat the suitors' disappearances as deaths, mourning them as lost:

> "So at last everyone gave him up for dead, and mourned for him, and wondered how he had met his fate."

Yet the narrative's resolution reveals that none of the suitors died — they were all preserved within [[the-gold-necklace|the necklace]]. Death, in this story, is a misdiagnosis, a category error that confuses [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] with extinction.

### "The Heart of Princess Joan" — Death as Condition of the Quest

In "[[the-heart-of-princess-joan-story|The Heart of Princess Joan]]," death is invoked as the ultimate stake of the narrative. [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s declaration that [[princess-joan|Joan]] must wait seven years is hedged with a mortality clause:

> "And till seven years are past remember that Joan is still mine. But if, when they are past, I have not returned, and you have heard nothing of me, you must think that I am dead and gone."

[[prince-michael|Michael]] names death as the only acceptable reason for his failure to return — and even then, the possibility is hypothetical. [[princess-joan|Joan]]'s reply is strikingly unsentimental:

> "And why do you go? I had thought there would be a grand wedding, and I should have all the gifts that are being prepared for me, and now I shall have nothing; but goodbye, if go you must."

[[princess-joan|Joan]]'s apparent indifference to the possibility of [[prince-michael|Michael]]'s death marks her character — deprived of her heart, she cannot feel the depth of emotion that mortality should evoke. She experiences the news of possible death with the same flat affect she brings to everything else. Death's emotional meaning, De Morgan implies, depends on the capacity to feel — and when that capacity is removed, death becomes merely another fact.

### "The Wanderings of Arasmon" — Death and the Sea

In the framing narrative of the fishing village, death is a constant presence at the edges of life:

> "I will wait all night till morning, and all day next day, and next night, till I see some sign of the boats, and know if they be living or dead."

The fisherfolk live at the boundary of mortality — every voyage risks death, and every return defies it. The cry of "Here they are" that follows this anxious waiting is a reprieve from death, a momentary victory in an ongoing struggle with the sea.

## Literary Context

Victorian fairy tales inherited from the Grimms a willingness to confront death directly — witches are burned, wolves are killed, villains meet violent ends. De Morgan's treatment is notably gentler. Her stories share more with the French fairy-tale tradition of transformation and restoration than with the Germanic tradition of [[justice-and-injustice|punishment]] and death. This may reflect her sensibility as well as her readership: her tales were written for children yet eschew both the sentimental evasion of death and the punitive deployment of it found in some contemporaneous works.

The collection's treatment of death — as something threatened, feared, but almost never realized — aligns with its broader fairy-tale logic. Enchantment, not death, is the ultimate fate in De Morgan's world, and [[magic-and-witchcraft|enchantment]] can always be broken.
