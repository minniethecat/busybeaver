## Definition

Resentment in *The Painted Veil* is not the hot flash of anger but the cold, accumulating bitterness of a grievance that cannot be spoken. It is the emotion of the powerless — of Kitty trapped in a loveless marriage, of Walter humiliated by infidelity, of Mr. Garstin ground down by decades of his wife's contempt. Maugham's characters rarely explode; they simmer. Resentment is their default temperature, and the novel traces its corrosive effect on every relationship it touches.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's resentment of her mother is the novel's founding grievance. Mrs. Garstin's "bitter tongue" (011-032) and "calculating affection" (008-003) have taught Kitty to view family as a transaction — and she resents the terms. When she marries Walter to escape her mother, she merely exchanges one source of resentment for another: she resents Walter for being dull, for loving her too earnestly, for failing to be the man she imagined she deserved.

Walter's resentment, by contrast, is the novel's most dangerous — because it is silent. After discovering Kitty's affair, he does not rage; he smiles with "bitter mockery" (023-022) and devises the Mei-tan-fu posting as a form of exquisite punishment. His resentment is ice-cold, weaponised as strategy rather than vented as emotion. Kitty feels the weight of it in every interaction: "Her pain now was tinged with resentment" (025-032).

The novel's deepest insight into resentment is that it does not dissipate — it transforms. Kitty's resentment of Walter in life becomes, after his death, something more complex: "She did not hate him now, nor feel resentment of him, but fear rather and perplexity" (044-004). Resentment gives way to a stranger emotion — the discomfort of being unable to hate someone you once resented, because death has made them unknowable.

## Thematic Analysis

**Resentment as the price of powerlessness**: The novel's most resentful characters — Kitty, Mr. Garstin — are also its least powerful. Resentment is the emotion available to those who cannot act on their grievances. Mrs. Garstin dominates her household; she feels contempt, not resentment. Her husband, silenced for decades, feels only bitterness.

**Cold resentment versus hot anger**: Walter's resentment is terrifying precisely because it never becomes anger. He does not shout, accuse, or strike — he plans. His resentment is strategic, a chess game played with lives. The novel suggests that cold resentment is more dangerous than hot anger because it can be sustained indefinitely, its energy never exhausted by expression.

**The exhaustion of resentment**: By the novel's end, Kitty is "losing the vividness of her resentment" (078-002). This is not forgiveness but fatigue — the recognition that resentment, like any emotion, has a natural lifespan. What replaces it is not warmth but a kind of emotional flatness, a readiness to move on that is indistinguishable from indifference.

**Resentment as inheritance**: Kitty inherits her mother's capacity for resentment as surely as she inherits her social position. The novel traces a cycle: Mrs. Garstin resents her inadequate husband, Kitty resents her inadequate husband, and the pattern threatens to perpetuate itself. Breaking the cycle requires, in Kitty's case, the extraordinary intervention of cholera, death, and spiritual awakening — nothing less than the complete demolition of her old life.
