# The Causeway

The Causeway is the narrow raised road running through the interminable rice-fields of the Chinese countryside that connects the river port to the inland city of Mei-tan-fu. It serves as the primary artery of Kitty Fane's physical and psychological journey in *The Painted Veil*, appearing at key moments of arrival, inner torment, symbolic reflection, and ultimate liberation.

## Description

The causeway is a "narrow causeway" raised between rice-fields that stretch to the horizon (028-001). Along its course, groves of bamboo lean over the path, their leaves shivering even in windless air, creating an atmosphere of watchful unease (030-001). As it approaches the city, the causeway "wound down" past "bedraggled houses" and through the water-gate, where coolies carrying water from the river splashed the stones "so that it was as wet as though it had heavily rained" (040-001). The causeway "meandered sinuously among the green rice fields," offering vistas of the surrounding country and the hills dotted with burial mounds (038-016).

## Events at This Location

- **Journey to Mei-tan-fu** — Kitty travels along the causeway day after day, her mind consumed with Charlie Townsend's betrayal, too absorbed in her misery to notice the passing landscape (028-001)
- **First sight of the archway** — As her bearers point toward the memorial arch silhouetted against the setting sun, Kitty passes through the bamboo groves that "leaned over the causeway strangely as if they would detain her" (030-001)
- **Arrival in darkness** — The bearers pause to change poles at the archway; the causeway descends past houses as night falls, and peasants pass carrying an unpainted coffin (030-002)
- **View from the memorial arch** — Sitting with Waddington at the archway, Kitty looks down upon the causeway meandering through the green fields and the disordered burial mounds (038-016)
- **The water-gate** — Coolies hurry to and fro across the causeway at the water-gate, carrying huge buckets suspended from shoulder yokes, splashing water everywhere (040-001)
- **Journey to the Buddhist monastery** — Kitty travels along the causeway past comfortable farmhouses "nestling with friendly intimacy in a grove of bamboos" (053-002)
- **Last visit to the archway** — Kitty and Waddington "sauntered along the causeway" to the archway, whose sardonic irony she still cannot fully articulate (066-001)
- **Departure from Mei-tan-fu** — On her journey away from the plague-stricken city, Kitty passes an old woman on the causeway "in blue, and the blue in the sunshine was like lapis lazuli" (069-004)
- **Freedom** — Leaving the region behind, Kitty reflects that the bamboo copses leaning "with such an adorable grace across the causeway" have become beautiful to her, and the word "Freedom!" sings in her heart (070-004)

## Key Passage

> They were borne in chairs, day after day, along a narrow causeway between interminable rice-fields. They set out at dawn and travelled till the heat of the day forced them to take shelter in a wayside inn and then went on again till they reached the town where they had arranged to spend the night. (028-001)

## Symbolic Significance

The causeway functions as a threshold space in the novel, connecting the known world of Hong Kong and the river to the transformative crucible of Mei-tan-fu. On the inward journey, the causeway is the path of dread and torment — Kitty is too absorbed in Charlie's betrayal to see the landscape (028-001), and the leaning bamboos seem to menace her (030-001). On the outward journey after Walter's death, the same causeway becomes the path of liberation: the blue-clad old woman is beautiful (069-004), the bamboo groves appear graceful (070-004), and the word "Freedom!" beats in Kitty's heart.

The causeway also traces the novel's meditation on mortality. Along its course Kitty passes burial mounds, coffins borne by peasants, and the memorial arch — reminders that the causeway is not merely a route between towns but a passage through a landscape thick with the dead. Maugham uses the causeway's narrowness — a single raised path dividing flooded rice-fields — to mirror Kitty's own narrowing options and the tightening grip of fate.
