## Definition

Regret in *The Painted Veil* is not merely the fleeting discomfort of a mistake but a transformative psychological state that drives Kitty Fane's moral awakening. It begins as shame over being caught in adultery, deepens into genuine remorse for the harm inflicted on Walter, and matures into a comprehensive re-evaluation of the values by which she has lived. Maugham presents regret as painful but potentially generative: it is through regret that Kitty first glimpses the possibility of a different kind of life.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's regret first surfaces not after the affair itself but after its discovery — initially as wounded pride and fear of consequences. It deepens at Mei-tan-fu as she observes Walter's silent suffering and the selfless devotion of the French nuns. Her conversation with the Mother Superior about whether one can regret a life choice marks a turning point: the Mother Superior's frank admission that she has never regretted her vocation prompts Kitty to measure her own regrets against a life of genuine commitment. After Walter's death, Kitty's regret transforms again — no longer about what she has lost but about the person she has been. Her final conversation with her father, in which she asks his forgiveness for never having loved him properly, represents regret matured into moral clarity.

## Thematic Analysis

The novel carefully distinguishes regret from self-pity. Kitty's early regret is self-centered: she regrets being caught, being humiliated, being taken to Mei-tan-fu. Her later regret is other-oriented: she regrets the pain she caused Walter, the shallowness of her relationship with her parents, the waste of her own capacities. This progression from selfish to selfless regret mirrors the novel's broader thematic movement from appearance to reality, from the painted veil to the substance beneath. Maugham's treatment of regret is unsentimental: regret does not undo the past, and Kitty's final peace is not the peace of having atoned but the peace of having understood.
