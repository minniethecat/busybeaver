# Cruelty

Cruelty in *The Painted Veil* operates on multiple levels: domestic cruelty within the Garstin household, emotional cruelty between Kitty and Walter, and the ambient cruelty of colonial power structures.

## Mrs. Garstin's Domestic Cruelty

Mrs. Garstin is introduced as "a hard, cruel, managing, ambitious, parsimonious, and stupid woman" (007-001). Her cruelty toward her husband and daughters is foundational to Kitty's psychological development — Kitty marries Walter largely to escape her mother's dominion.

## Walter's Silent Cruelty

After discovering Kitty's affair, Walter deploys emotional cruelty as punishment. Kitty asks "Oh, how cruel! How can you be so heartless?" (025-045) when Walter presents his ultimatum. His cold, withdrawn treatment in Mei-tan-fu constitutes a sustained form of psychological cruelty. Kitty later reflects on "asking him why he had been so cruel" (033-001), recognizing that Walter's cruelty stemmed from his own wounded love.

## Self-Perception and Social Judgment

Kitty fears being perceived as "heartless and cruel" (070-001) for her emotional detachment after Walter's death. The novel suggests that cruelty is not only inflicted but also internalized — Kitty's awareness of her own capacity for cruelty becomes part of her moral awakening.

## Colonial Cruelty

The Manchu fortress is described as "the stronghold of a cruel and barbaric race" (033-004), reflecting the colonial gaze that Maugham both records and subtly critiques throughout the novel.

## Key Passage

> Mrs. Garstin was a hard, cruel, managing, ambitious, parsimonious and stupid woman. She was the daughter, one of five, of a solicitor in Liverpool and Bernard Garstin had met her when he was on the Northern Circuit. He had seemed then a young man of promise and her father said he would go far. He hadn't. He was painstaking, industrious and capable, but he had not the will to advance himself. Mrs. Garstin despised him.
>
> (007-001)
