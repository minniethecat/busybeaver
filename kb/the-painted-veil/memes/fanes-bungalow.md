# The Fanes' Bungalow

## Overview

The Fanes' bungalow in Hong Kong's Pleasant Vale is the novel's primary domestic space in its first half. It is here that Kitty conducts her affair — the white china knob of the bedroom door becoming a recurring motif of adulterous anticipation — and here that Walter confronts her. The bungalow is the stage for the collapse of Kitty's old life.

## Description

Situated in the Pleasant Vale rather than the more prestigious Peak, the bungalow reflects Walter's middling position in the colonial hierarchy. It is comfortable but not grand — a house that signals the social limitation that rankles Kitty.

## Events at This Location

- Kitty's affair with Townsend, conducted during Walter's absences (002-005)
- Walter's silent discovery and his cold confrontation of Kitty (017-024)
- Kitty's growing resentment of her domestic circumstances

## Associated Characters

- [[Kitty Fane]] — the mistress of a house she despises
- [[Walter Fane]] — the husband whose quiet domesticity drives Kitty to distraction
- [[Charles Townsend]] — the lover who visits when the husband is away

## Symbolic Significance

The bungalow represents the domestic trap — the marriage that Kitty entered for the wrong reasons and now chafes against. Its modesty (relative to the Peak) mirrors the social disappointment that fuels Kitty's restlessness.
