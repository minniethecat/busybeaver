## Definition

Desire in *The Painted Veil* is primarily sexual and romantic passion, presented with unusual directness for Maugham's era. It is the force that overwhelms Kitty's judgment, leading her into an affair with Charles Townsend, and it continues to exert power over her even after she intellectually recognizes Townsend's worthlessness. Maugham treats desire not as sin to be condemned but as a fact of human nature to be understood and, ultimately, transcended — the novel's moral arc moves from desire as compulsion toward desire as choice, culminating in Kitty's vision of a future shaped by integrity rather than appetite.

## Context in the Novel

Desire enters the novel with immediacy: Kitty's mouth is described as "a little open with desire" (002-025) in the opening scene of her tryst with Townsend. This physical frankness establishes desire as a force that operates below the level of reason. After Walter discovers the affair and forces Kitty to accompany him to Mei-tan-fu, desire appears to recede, replaced by the austerities of the cholera epidemic. Yet desire reasserts itself with humiliating force when Kitty returns to Hong Kong and again succumbs to Townsend's advances, despite knowing his character. This second fall is crucial to the novel's honesty about desire: moral insight does not automatically confer immunity. The novel's resolution comes not from the elimination of desire but from Kitty's ability to place it within a larger framework of purpose and self-respect.

## Thematic Analysis

Maugham presents a nuanced view of desire that resists both condemnation and celebration. The affair with Townsend is morally destructive, yet desire itself is not presented as evil — it is simply powerful. The novel's most sophisticated insight is that desire persists even after its object is revealed as unworthy: "He could not really desire her death. He had loved her so" (029-002) reflects Kitty's desperate hope that Walter's desire for her has survived his contempt. The French nuns offer an alternative to the world of desire — not repression but redirection toward service. The novel's Taoist-inflected wisdom, quoted by Waddington, suggests that freedom from desire is the ultimate peace: "Thou shalt be loved when thou hast ceased to desire it" (060-005).
