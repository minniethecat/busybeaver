## Definition

Indifference in *The Painted Veil* is the absence or suspension of emotional engagement — the state in which a person no longer cares, or cannot bring themselves to care, about what would once have seized their heart. Maugham treats indifference as a phenomenon with multiple valences: it can be a weapon (Walter's coldness toward Kitty), a wound (the indifference of the world to human suffering), a symptom of exhaustion (Kitty's numbness after Walter's death), and, ultimately, a form of liberation (Kitty's release from her passion for Townsend).

## Context in the Novel

Indifference pervades the novel's emotional landscape. Walter Fane weaponises it after discovering the affair, treating Kitty with an "indifference" that is "like the prick of a spear" (027-006) — a studied coldness more wounding than open anger. The colonial world itself is characterised by indifference: the Chinese coolies die of cholera while the British officials maintain their routines, and the narrator notes how "indifferent" the universe is to human affairs (066-018).

Kitty's relationship to indifference evolves across the novel. Initially wounded by Walter's coldness, she eventually discovers indifference as a form of healing: "She could think of him now with indifference. She loved him no longer. Oh, the relief and the sense of liberation!" (052-002). By the novel's end, she finds herself separated from her father by "a wall of indifference" built from their shared past (080-014), and she contemplates becoming "an indifferent spectator forever of the phantasmagoria of life" (070-002).

## Thematic Analysis

**Indifference as cruelty**: Walter's studied indifference toward Kitty after the affair is one of the novel's most psychologically acute portrayals of passive aggression. It is, the narrator notes, more wounding than any reproach would be: a refusal to engage that denies the other person the dignity of conflict. His "disdain" is all the more devastating for being expressed through silence.

**Indifference as liberation**: The novel's most surprising move is to redeem indifference as a form of healing. When Kitty realises she no longer cares about Charles Townsend — that she can think of him "with indifference" — it is presented as a moment of genuine liberation (052-002). The passion that once consumed her has simply burned itself out, and the resulting emotional neutrality is not emptiness but freedom.

**Cosmic indifference**: Maugham extends the theme beyond individual psychology to metaphysics. The world of the novel is one in which human suffering meets with "indifferent" air (066-018); the cholera epidemic kills without moral discrimination; and the universe offers no comfort for human grief. This cosmic indifference is the backdrop against which characters must construct their own meaning.

**Indifference and understanding**: In a striking passage, the Mother Superior regards Kitty with eyes that hold "detachment and yet with something that looked like a profound understanding" (043-018). This is indifference of a different kind — not coldness but the clear-sighted compassion that comes from having relinquished personal investment. The Mother Superior models a form of detachment that is not cruelty but wisdom.

## Key Passages

> His indifference was like the prick of a spear. (027-006)

> She could think of him now with indifference. She loved him no longer. Oh, the relief and the sense of liberation! (052-002)

> He is concerned only with the eternal. The material world...is but a shadow that a passing cloud casts on the earth; it passes and is gone. The great thoughts which, coming out of nothing, unroll themselves on the indifferent air. (066-018)

> She would like to wander forever through that smiling and friendly country in her rattan chair, and, an indifferent spectator forever of the phantasmagoria of life, pass each night under a different roof. (070-002)

> Their common past was a wall of indifference between them. (080-014)
