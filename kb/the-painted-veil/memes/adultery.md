## Definition

Adultery in *The Painted Veil* is the act of marital infidelity committed by the protagonist Kitty Fane with Charles Townsend, the Assistant Colonial Secretary in Hong Kong. It functions as the novel's inciting transgression — the moral breach that shatters Kitty's settled life, forces her into exile in cholera-stricken Mei-tan-fu, and initiates her漫长的 spiritual reckoning.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's affair with Charles Townsend occurs in the Hong Kong colonial society of the 1920s. Having married Walter Fane — a quiet, devoted bacteriologist — without love, Kitty finds herself drawn to Townsend's charm, social ease, and physical attractiveness. The affair is discovered by Walter, who issues his ultimatum: either Kitty accompanies him to the cholera-ridden interior city of Mei-tan-fu, or Townsend must divorce his wife and marry Kitty.

Townsend's refusal to leave his wife exposes the affair's superficiality and forces Kitty to confront the emptiness of her romantic illusions. The journey to Mei-tan-fu, precipitated by this discovery, becomes the crucible in which Kitty's character is transformed.

> "I knew that you were silly and frivolous and empty-headed. But I loved you. I knew that your aims and ideals were vulgar and commonplace. But I loved you." — Walter Fane confronting Kitty about the affair (047-011)

## Thematic Analysis

Adultery serves three intertwined thematic functions in the novel:

**As social critique**: Maugham uses Kitty's adultery to expose the hypocrisies of British colonial society. Townsend — publicly respected, privately philandering — embodies the gap between social appearance and moral reality. His wife Dorothy's strategic blindness to his affairs reflects the pragmatic accommodations that uphold respectable society.

**As moral awakening**: The affair and its consequence function as Kitty's *felix culpa* — a fortunate fall. Without the adultery and its exposure, Kitty would have remained trapped in her shallow, self-centered existence. The transgression is the necessary precondition for her moral and spiritual growth.

**As a study in asymmetry**: Walter's response to the adultery — his cold fury, his quiet revenge of taking Kitty to the cholera zone, and ultimately his refusal to forgive — reveals the gendered double standard of the era. Kitty's sin is publicly damning; Townsend's comparable sin barely touches his reputation.

## Key Passages

> Kitty's mother, suspecting her husband of adultery, represents the novel's broader treatment of marital suspicion — it pervades every level of the social order. [P03-012]

> "Supposing a silly woman had committed adultery and why should her husband, faced with the scandal, seek to make a fuss?" — the narrator reflects on the disproportionate consequences Kitty faces compared to the man involved. (047-011)

> "I had no illusions about you," Walter tells Kitty. "I knew you were silly and frivolous and empty-headed. But I loved you." — Walter's devastating confession reframes the adultery not as a surprise betrayal but as the inevitable expression of who Kitty was. (023-030)
