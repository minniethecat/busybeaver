## Definition

Social class in *The Painted Veil* denotes the stratified hierarchy of early twentieth-century British society, intensified by the artificial closeness of colonial life in Hong Kong. Social position determines marriage prospects, professional advancement, and personal identity. The novel traces how class anxiety — embodied most sharply in Mrs. Garstin's relentless ambition — drives characters into choices that prove morally and emotionally catastrophic.

## Context in the Novel

Class consciousness pervades the Garstin household from the opening chapters. Mrs. Garstin, the daughter of a Liverpool solicitor, has married beneath her ambitions and channels her frustrated aspirations into her daughters' marriages. Kitty's hurried engagement to Walter Fane — a man she does not love — is a direct response to her mother's disparagement and the threat of being outshone by her younger sister Doris. In Hong Kong, the colonial hierarchy further stratifies society: the Fanes are marginal within the official community, while the Townsends occupy its center. Walter's profession as a bacteriologist places him outside the conventional channels of colonial advancement.

## Thematic Analysis

Maugham uses class as a lens through which to examine the constraints on individual freedom — particularly women's freedom. Kitty is a product of her class: educated to be decorative, trained to marry well, and equipped with no resources beyond her beauty and charm. Her moral vacuity at the novel's opening is not presented as a personal failing alone but as the inevitable outcome of her social conditioning. The novel's critique of class is sharpest in its portrayal of Mrs. Garstin, whose deathbed revelation — that she had never loved her family, only her ambition — exposes the spiritual emptiness at the heart of social climbing. Kitty's final resolution to raise her daughter to be independent represents a conscious rejection of the class-bound values that shaped her own upbringing.
