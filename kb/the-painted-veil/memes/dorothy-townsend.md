# Dorothy Townsend

## Overview

Dorothy Townsend is the wife of Charles Townsend and, though she appears only briefly in the novel, her presence is felt throughout. Kitty thinks of her with a mixture of guilt and contempt — "How unfortunate to be called Dorothy!" (003-007) — but the novel ultimately treats Dorothy with more respect than Kitty's initial assessment suggests. She is competent, pleasant, and entirely unaware of (or chooses not to know about) her husband's infidelities.

## Background

Dorothy comes from the same British colonial society that produced Kitty and Townsend. She manages her husband's household efficiently and fulfills the social obligations of a senior colonial official's wife without drawing attention to herself. Her very ordinariness is, in the novel's value system, a form of quiet virtue.

## Role in the Novel

Dorothy functions primarily as an absence — the wife Townsend will not leave, the domestic stability he will not jeopardize. Kitty's initial dismissal of her ("How unfortunate to be called Dorothy!") reflects Kitty's own vanity: she assumes her youth and beauty make her superior. But the novel quietly suggests that Dorothy's unglamorous competence may be worth more than Kitty's dazzling superficiality.

## Key Passages

> How unfortunate to be called Dorothy! It dated you. (003-007)

## Relationships

- [[Charles Townsend]] — husband; the man whose infidelities she either ignores or accepts
- [[Kitty Fane]] — her husband's lover; Kitty's initial contempt for Dorothy gives way to something more complex

## Significance

Dorothy Townsend is a minor character who carries significant thematic weight. She represents the domestic world that Kitty's adultery threatens — and that Townsend's charm exploits. Her quiet competence is the novel's implicit rebuke to the glamour of adultery.
