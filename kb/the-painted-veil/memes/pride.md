# Pride

## Definition

Pride in *The Painted Veil* is the assertion of self-worth that can manifest as dignity or as arrogance, as moral integrity or as wounded vanity. Walter's pride is his defining characteristic — it sustains him through professional obscurity and marital humiliation, but it also prevents him from forgiving or connecting. Kitty's pride is social and superficial; it must be broken before she can develop a more authentic self-respect.

## Context in the Novel

Walter's pride is introduced as something almost physical — a coldness, a reserve, a refusal to engage on others' terms. His pride is the corollary of his intelligence: he knows he is cleverer than the people who slight him, and this knowledge sustains him. But his pride also imprisons him. He cannot forgive Kitty because to do so would be to surrender the one thing — his sense of moral superiority — that has protected him from despair.

Kitty's pride is of a different order: the pride of beauty, of being admired, of social success. This pride is shattered by her exposure, her exile to Mei-tan-fu, and her growing recognition of her own shallowness. The novel traces the replacement of her false pride with something quieter and more substantial.

## Thematic Analysis

**Pride as Armor**: For Walter, pride is protective — it insulates him from a world that does not value his qualities. But armor also prevents intimacy. His pride is both his strength and his tragedy.

**Pride as Blindness**: Kitty's pride prevents her from seeing herself clearly. Only when her pride is broken — by humiliation, by suffering, by the example of the nuns — can she begin to develop genuine self-knowledge.

**Pride Transformed**: The novel does not reject pride wholesale. Kitty's emerging sense of her own worth — based on her work, her choices, her growing integrity — represents pride transformed from vanity into dignity.
