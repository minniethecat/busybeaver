# The Memorial Arch

The Memorial Arch is a monumental stone gateway standing on the crest of a hill above Mei-tan-fu, erected in honor of a virtuous widow according to Chinese custom. Richly carved, "fantastic and ironical," it serves as the most potent visual symbol in *The Painted Veil* — a literal "painted" façade that marks the threshold between the outside world and the plague city, between life and death, between Kitty Fane's illusions and her awakening.

## Description

The archway stands silhouetted against the western sky on the hilltop, "more fantastic and beautiful than any she had seen" (030-001). It is "richly carved" with a "fantastic outline" and "capricious contours" that suggest the waving arms of a Hindu god (030-001). Waddington describes it as "flamboyant" (038-053). Below it spreads the river plain, and around it cluster the disordered green mounds of the Chinese burial ground, the hill "sown close with the little green mounds of the dead" (038-016).

Maugham presents the archway as "a landmark in the surrounding country" (038-016), visible from miles away across the rice-fields. It is the first thing Kitty sees on approaching Mei-tan-fu and the last thing she looks back on when leaving.

## Key Passage

> Then she came to the memorial arch and its fantastic outline seemed on a sudden to gain a monstrous life; its capricious contours were like the waving arms of a Hindu god, and, as she passed under it, she heard the echo of mocking laughter. (030-001)

## Events at This Location

- **First sighting** — On her journey to Mei-tan-fu, Kitty's bearers point to the archway on the hilltop. Though she has passed many such memorials along the river, this one unsettles her: "it had a significance which she felt but could not put into words: Was it a menace that she vaguely discerned or was it derision?" (030-001)
- **Passing through** — The chair-bearers pause to change poles as they pass through the archway, and Kitty sees peasants bearing an unpainted coffin in the gathering darkness (030-002)
- **Afternoon with Waddington** — On a restful afternoon, Kitty and Waddington walk to the archway and sit on its pedestal, facing the wide plain. Waddington tells her the arch commemorates a virtuous widow (038-016)
- **The flamboyant exit** — Later, they pass under the "flamboyant archway" and descend the hill (038-053)
- **Final visit** — Before leaving Mei-tan-fu, Kitty and Waddington "sauntered along the causeway till they came to the top of the hill" for a last look at the archway. She reflects that "it was a symbol, but of what she scarcely knew; she could not tell why it bore a note of so sardonic irony" (066-001)

## Symbolic Significance

The Memorial Arch is the novel's central visual metaphor, embodying the "painted veil" of the title. It is a monument to virtue — a widow honored for her faithfulness — yet Kitty passes under it as an adulteress on her way to what may be her death. The irony is "sardonic" (066-001): the archway celebrates an ideal of wifely virtue that Kitty has spectacularly failed to embody, yet its elaborate carvings are merely a painted surface over the reality of the burial mounds surrounding it.

The archway also functions as a *memento mori*. Kitty first sees it in the dying light of sunset, silhouetted against a westering sky; she passes through it in darkness alongside a coffin; and she contemplates it from a hillside covered with graves. The memorial's dedication to a dead woman, its location amid the burial mounds, and its role as the gateway to a plague-stricken city all reinforce the novel's preoccupation with death as the ultimate reality beneath the "painted veil" of social convention and self-deception.

The capricious, fantastic quality of the carving — "like the waving arms of a Hindu god" (030-001) — introduces an Eastern exoticism that Maugham uses to suggest the instability of Kitty's Western moral framework. In China, her familiar categories of propriety and shame are rendered strange, as strange as the memorial arch's "monstrous life."
