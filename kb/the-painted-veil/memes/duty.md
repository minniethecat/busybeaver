# Duty

## Definition

In *The Painted Veil*, duty represents the moral imperative that compels characters to act according to principle rather than inclination. It is most powerfully embodied in Walter Fane, whose sense of duty governs his professional commitment as a bacteriologist, his conduct as a husband, and ultimately his confrontation with the cholera epidemic in Mei-tan-fu.

## Context in the Novel

Duty operates as a counterforce to passion throughout the novel. Charles Townsend cynically exploits the concept when he suggests manipulating Walter "through his sense of duty" (024-045). But duty also redeems: Walter's devotion to his scientific work during the epidemic transforms him from a cuckolded husband into a figure of genuine moral weight. After his death, Townsend himself acknowledges Walter as "a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036).

The novel's most profound meditation on duty comes from the Mother Superior, who tells Kitty: "it is nothing to do your duty, that is demanded of you and is no more meritorious than to wash your hands when they are dirty; the only thing that counts is the love of duty; when love and duty are one, then grace is in you and you will enjoy a happiness which passes all understanding" (068-052).

## Thematic Analysis

**Duty as Constraint**: In the early chapters, duty appears as a form of social coercion. Kitty's mother feels duty-bound to arrange advantageous marriages; Walter feels duty-bound to remain in a loveless marriage. Duty is the velvet cage of Edwardian society.

**Duty as Redemption**: In Mei-tan-fu, duty transforms. Walter's commitment to combating cholera gives his life purpose beyond personal grievance. Kitty's growing sense of duty — to the convent children, to her unborn child — marks her moral awakening.

**The Limits of Duty**: The novel ultimately questions whether duty alone is sufficient. Walter's final surrender "to his sense of duty" (080-031) is described as pitiful — duty without love becomes a form of self-annihilation.

## Key Passages

> "He can bring pressure to bear on Walter. If he can't get at him through his ambition he'll get at him through his sense of duty." (024-045)

> "Walter died a martyr to science and to his duty." (066-036)

> "Remember that it is nothing to do your duty, that is demanded of you and is no more meritorious than to wash your hands when they are dirty; the only thing that counts is the love of duty; when love and duty are one, then grace is in you." (068-052)

> It was pitiful. The struggle had been short and he had surrendered to his sense of duty. With those few words he abandoned all his hopes. (080-031)
