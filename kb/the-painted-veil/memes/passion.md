## Definition

Passion in *The Painted Veil* is erotic and emotional intensity — the force of desire that overwhelms reason, shatters social convention, and reveals the deepest truths about those it possesses. Maugham presents passion as morally ambivalent: it is both the most alive a person can feel and the most destructive force a person can unleash. The novel's central dramatic question — whether passion can coexist with genuine love — is never answered definitively, only explored through the wreckage it leaves behind.

## Context in the Novel

Passion enters the novel through Kitty Fane's affair with Charles Townsend. Having married Walter without love, Kitty discovers in Townsend the intensity she has been missing. The narrator describes the affair in explicitly physical terms: Townsend is "passionate then, fierce, oddly hysterical" (012-001), and Kitty finds herself "conscious of the devastating passion which was in his heart" (011-024). Their encounters are electric: "the pressure of his upon them shot through her body like the flame of God. It was an ecstasy and she was burnt to a cinder" (075-065).

But the novel complicates passion at every turn. The very intensity that makes Townsend irresistible also makes him unreliable — he is "not a passionate man; he's only a vain one" (035-022), as Waddington shrewdly observes. And the passion Kitty feels for Townsend is mirrored, tragically, in the passion Walter feels for Kitty — a love so "passionate" that he is "prepared to accept any humiliation if sometimes she would let him love her" (020-044). The novel's cruel symmetry is that Kitty feels for Townsend exactly what Walter feels for her: a love that humiliates the lover and inspires contempt in the beloved.

## Thematic Analysis

**Passion versus love**: The novel draws a sustained contrast between passion and love, suggesting they are fundamentally different — even opposed — experiences. Passion is immediate, physical, and self-centered; love is quieter, more durable, and oriented toward the other. Walter loves Kitty; Townsend desires her. The tragedy is that Kitty, for most of the novel, cannot tell the difference.

**Passion as self-destruction**: The novel's most searing passage on passion comes after Kitty's relapse with Townsend in Hong Kong. "It had been ecstasy. Oh, hateful!" (076-015). The juxtaposition of "ecstasy" and "hateful" captures passion's double nature — the thing that feels most alive is also the thing that destroys self-respect. Kitty's passion for Townsend is not a source of joy but of shame and self-loathing.

**The passion of the unloved**: Walter Fane is the novel's most passionate character — and the one who most successfully conceals it. His "devastating passion" for Kitty (011-024) is never reciprocated, and his tragedy is that the intensity of his feeling only repels her: she feels "a faint sensation of contempt for a man who could love so slavishly" (020-044). The novel suggests that unrequited passion, when hidden behind a mask of coldness, can curdle into something lethal.

**Passion and the sublime**: Maugham occasionally allows passion a genuinely transcendent dimension. The description of Kitty's moment with Townsend — "like the flame of God" (075-065) — elevates physical desire into something approaching spiritual experience. But the novel immediately undercuts this with Kitty's subsequent shame. The sublime moment of passion proves to be an illusion, or at least a feeling that cannot survive the return of self-consciousness.
