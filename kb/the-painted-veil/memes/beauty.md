# Beauty

## Definition

In *The Painted Veil*, physical beauty functions as a double-edged inheritance. Kitty's beauty — her "large, dark eyes, liquid and vivacious, brown, curling hair in which there was a reddish tint, exquisite teeth and a lovely skin" (008-003) — is both her primary social asset and, the novel suggests, a liability that has prevented her from developing inner resources. Beauty attracts admiration but does not, in itself, confer wisdom or character.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's beauty is established from childhood as her defining characteristic. Her mother recognizes it as a marketable asset and invests accordingly: "her beauty depended a good deal on her youth, and Mrs. Garstin realised that she must marry in the first flush of her maidenhood" (008-003). The expectation that beauty will secure a "brilliant" marriage creates the pressure under which Kitty makes her catastrophic choice of Walter.

Walter, despite loving Kitty, is not swayed by her beauty in the conventional sense — he sees through it to her character. Townsend, by contrast, is a connoisseur of beauty without substance. The novel ultimately suggests that beauty, like the "painted veil" itself, is a surface that conceals rather than reveals.

## Thematic Analysis

**Beauty as Commodity**: In the marriage market of Edwardian England, beauty is a young woman's primary asset. Kitty has been taught to trade on her appearance, and she has never needed to develop other qualities.

**Beauty and Moral Myopia**: Kitty's beauty has protected her from consequences. People — especially men — have always treated her with indulgence. This has left her morally underdeveloped, unable to distinguish between flattery and genuine regard.

**Transcending Beauty**: Kitty's growth involves learning to value herself for something other than her appearance. Her work at the convent gives her a sense of worth independent of male admiration.
