# Science

## Definition

In *The Painted Veil*, science represents the rational, empirical approach to reality that Walter Fane embodies as a government bacteriologist. It is contrasted throughout the novel with the emotional and social domains in which Kitty excels and in which Walter so conspicuously fails. Science is presented as socially marginal — "from a social standpoint the man of science does not exist" (004-005) — yet morally essential when the cholera epidemic demands competence over charm.

## Context in the Novel

Walter's scientific identity is established early as the source of both his professional purpose and his social invisibility. Kitty quickly discovers that "as the wife of the Government bacteriologist she was of no particular consequence" (004-001). The very qualities that make Walter a good scientist — precision, detachment, reserve — make him a disappointing husband in Kitty's eyes.

But science redeems Walter in the novel's second half. His bacteriological expertise during the cholera epidemic saves lives and earns him the respect of the French nuns and the local community. Waddington observes: "the nuns at the convent swear by him. They think he's a hero" (038-035). After his death, even Townsend acknowledges him as "a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036).

## Thematic Analysis

**Science Versus Society**: The novel dramatizes the gulf between social value and actual value. Charles Townsend, a master of social performance, is morally worthless; Walter, socially awkward but scientifically competent, proves genuinely useful when cholera strikes.

**Science as Consolation**: Walter throws himself into his bacteriological work as an escape from personal misery. Science provides structure, purpose, and measurable results — everything his marriage lacks.

**The Limits of Science**: For all its practical value, science cannot resolve Walter's emotional torment. He can isolate the cholera bacillus but cannot cure his own despair.
