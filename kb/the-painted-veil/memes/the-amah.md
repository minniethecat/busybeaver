# The Amah

## Overview

The amah is Kitty Fane's Chinese maid, a quiet presence in the domestic spaces where the novel's most intimate scenes unfold. She says little, appears only at the margins of scenes, and yet her presence is a constant reminder of the Chinese world that surrounds but remains invisible to the British characters (001-033). She is the novel's most sustained portrait of the colonial dynamic at its most intimate: the servant who knows everything about her mistress and reveals nothing of herself.

## Background

The amah is one of countless Chinese servants employed by British households in colonial Hong Kong. She manages the Fane household with the competence born of long experience (027-009), and her discretion — she never comments on what she witnesses — is both professional and, the novel hints, a form of silent judgment.

## Role in the Novel

The amah appears in domestic scenes throughout the novel: preparing Kitty's bath, serving tea, witnessing Kitty's agitation without acknowledging it. Her presence during Kitty's affair, Walter's confrontation, and Kitty's subsequent distress is never remarked upon by the British characters — she is simply part of the furniture. But the novel's attention to her, however brief, registers the colonial reality that the British narrative would prefer to ignore.

## Key Passages

> [The amah] had been with them since their marriage and knew her habits. (003-001)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — employer; the woman whose secrets she keeps and whose world she observes from within

## Significance

The amah embodies the novel's peripheral awareness of the colonial power dynamic. She is the Chinese presence that the British narrative marginalizes — and her silence, the novel implies, may contain judgments the British characters are not equipped to hear.
