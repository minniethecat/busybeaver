# Walter's Ultimatum

## Overview

[[walter-fane|Walter]] discovers [[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s [[adultery|affair]] and presents her with a devastating choice: accompany him to cholera-stricken [[mei-tan-fu|Mei-tan-fu]], or face public divorce (017-004) — but only if [[charles-townsend|Townsend]] agrees to marry her. The ultimatum is both punishment and test. [[walter-fane|Walter]] knows [[charles-townsend|Townsend]] will refuse (023-020), and [[charles-townsend|Townsend]]'s refusal strips away [[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s romantic illusions.

## Significance

The ultimatum scene is the novel's first great moral crucible. [[walter-fane|Walter]]'s coldness is terrifying precisely because it is so controlled — he is not a man in the grip of [[desire|passion]] but a man executing a sentence he has already passed.
