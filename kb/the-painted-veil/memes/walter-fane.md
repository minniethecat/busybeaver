# Walter Fane

## Overview

Walter Fane is a government bacteriologist stationed in Hong Kong and the husband of Kitty Fane. Quiet, intellectually gifted, and socially awkward, Walter is a man whose inner life the novel treats as fundamentally unknowable. His love for Kitty — intense, silent, and tinged with contempt — is the novel's most complex emotional dynamic. His decision to take Kitty to cholera-stricken Mei-tan-fu after discovering her affair is simultaneously an act of punishment and, the novel suggests, a form of self-destruction. His death from cholera, and his enigmatic final words — "The dog it was that died" — remain among the novel's most debated elements.

## Background

Walter first meets Kitty at a dance in London, where he is visiting during leave from his post in China. He is a medical doctor who specialized in bacteriology — a quiet, studious man with no gift for social performance. "From a social standpoint the man of science does not exist," he tells Kitty with a self-deprecating smile (004-005). He falls deeply in love with Kitty, though he is clear-eyed about her limitations: he knows she marries him for convenience, not affection.

## Role in the Novel

Walter's discovery of Kitty's affair with Townsend is the novel's first turning point. His response — not rage but cold, methodical exposure — reveals a man whose emotional control masks profound injury. He offers Kitty a choice: accompany him to Mei-tan-fu, where a cholera epidemic rages, or face public divorce if Townsend will not marry her. The ultimatum is as much an act of despair as of revenge.

In Mei-tan-fu, Walter throws himself into his bacteriological work, earning the respect of the French nuns and the local community. Waddington observes: "They think he's a hero" (038-037). But his personal life remains frozen — he cannot forgive Kitty, and this incapacity becomes a form of self-imprisonment.

Walter's death from cholera is the novel's most significant event. His final words — "The dog it was that died" — echo Oliver Goldsmith's elegy and encapsulate his self-loathing. The Mother Superior later reframes his death as martyrdom: "Walter died a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036).

## Relationships

- [[Charles Townsend]] — rival; the man whose affair with Kitty catalyzes Walter's descent
- [[Mother Superior]] — admirer; she recognizes Walter's worth when others cannot
- [[Waddington]] — colleague; the one person in Mei-tan-fu who understands both Walter and Kitty

## Significance

Walter Fane is the novel's most enigmatic character — a man whose moral seriousness is both admirable and destructive. He embodies the collision between intelligence and emotion, duty and despair. His tragedy is not that he loved unwisely but that he could not forgive — not Kitty, and perhaps not himself.
