# The Hospital

## Overview

The hospital in Mei-tan-fu is the front line of the cholera epidemic. Crowded with the dying, staffed by the French nuns and supervised by Walter Fane, it is where the novel's central moral drama — the response to suffering — is enacted daily. The hospital is the place where Kitty first confronts the reality of death and discovers her own capacity for useful action. The French nuns converted their orphanage into a hospital, improvising a space of care from the materials of charity (022-022).

## Description

The hospital is described as a crowded, improvised space within the convent walls: "the infirmary was crowded with the victims of the epidemic" (041-013). Rows of patients, the smell of illness, the quiet efficiency of the nuns — it is a place stripped of comfort and pretense.

## Events at This Location

- Walter's medical supervision of the cholera patients
- Kitty's first exposure to the scale of the epidemic's human cost
- The nuns' tireless nursing of the dying

## Associated Characters

- [[Walter Fane]] — the doctor whose expertise directs the medical response
- [[Mother Superior]] — the administrator who manages the hospital's daily operations
- [[Kitty Fane]] — whose visits to the hospital begin her moral education

## Symbolic Significance

The hospital represents the novel's central moral question: how should one respond to suffering? The nuns answer with compassion; Walter answers with science; Kitty learns to answer with both.
