# Composure

Composure in *The Painted Veil* is the art of emotional concealment. Walter Fane is its master, Kitty its reluctant student, and Waddington its ironic commentator. The novel explores composure as both a survival mechanism and a form of emotional violence.

## Walter's Inhuman Self-Control

Walter's composure is first described as a shield: "His self-control was due to shyness or to long training" (012-002). After discovering Kitty's affair, his composure becomes a weapon — "His self-control was inhuman and she hated him" (023-039). Kitty finds it "dreadful that cold self-control of his which made the smallest outbreak of emotion portentous" (056-028).

## Kitty's Learning

Kitty gradually acquires composure as a survival tool. She learns to face Walter "calmly" (055-012), and under crisis "she was quite composed" (055-006). After Walter's death, "notwithstanding all there was to harass her, she was composed" (057-001). Composure becomes her armor against social judgment.

## British Restraint Critiqued

Waddington offers the novel's wryest commentary on composure: "Oh, this British self-control! There he is delighted beyond measure and he looks as though he were going to a funeral" (059-007). His observation exposes composure as a cultural performance rather than a natural virtue.

## Key Passage

> Kitty could more easily have coped with the situation if he had raved and stormed. She could have met violence with violence. His self-control was inhuman and she hated him now as she had never hated him before.
>
> (023-039)
