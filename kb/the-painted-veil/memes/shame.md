# Shame

## Definition

Shame in *The Painted Veil* is the emotional cost of seeing oneself clearly. It is distinct from guilt (regret for an action) — shame is regret for the kind of person one has been. Kitty's journey through the novel is a pilgrimage through shame: the shame of her affair exposed, the shame of being sent to a cholera zone as punishment, the shame of realizing Townsend never loved her, and finally the productive shame of recognizing her own superficiality.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's first experience of shame comes when Walter discovers her affair. But this is shame at being caught, not shame at having done wrong (023-041). Her true shame begins in Mei-tan-fu, where the nuns' selfless devotion shows her the poverty of her own values. The novel charts her progression from social shame (what others think of her) to moral shame (what she thinks of herself) (025-056).

The most devastating moment of shame comes when Kitty returns to Hong Kong after Walter's death and succumbs again to Townsend's advances. This relapse into her old self is more shaming than the original affair, because she now knows better, and her relapse fills her with self-loathing and humiliation.

## Thematic Analysis

**Social Shame vs. Moral Shame**: The novel distinguishes between the shame of social exposure — which Kitty feels when her affair is discovered — and the deeper shame of self-knowledge, which only develops through suffering and reflection.

**Shame as Catalyst**: Shame, in Maugham's treatment, can be productive. It is the pain that forces Kitty to change. Without shame, she would remain the shallow creature she was at the novel's opening.

**Shame and Gender**: The novel exposes the gendered asymmetry of shame. Kitty's adultery brings disgrace; Townsend's identical conduct carries no social cost. The "painted veil" of social convention protects men and punishes women.
