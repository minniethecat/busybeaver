# Mrs. Garstin

## Overview

Mrs. Garstin is Kitty's mother and the architect of the social ambitions that shape — and deform — her daughters' lives. A hard, competent woman chafing against the limitations of her husband's modest legal career, she channels all her frustrated ambition into securing "brilliant" marriages for Kitty and Doris. Her calculating approach to motherhood — treating her daughters as investments to be managed — creates the conditions for Kitty's disastrous marriage to Walter.

## Background

Mrs. Garstin married Bernard Garstin, a barrister, with the expectation that he would rise to the High Court. When his career stalled — "it was unlikely now that Bernard Garstin would ever be made a judge of the High Court" (008-002) — she transferred her ambitions to her daughters. Recognizing Kitty's exceptional beauty, she invested heavily in launching her into London society, expecting a spectacular return.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Garstin's presence drives the novel's opening situation even though she appears directly in only a few scenes. Her harsh, instrumental approach to motherhood — "a harsh, competent, calculating affection" (008-003) — has taught Kitty to value herself only as others value her. The pressure she exerts on Kitty to marry well creates the panic that leads to Walter's proposal.

Her death, reported late in the novel, is a narrative release: it frees Kitty from the judgment she has internalized and opens the possibility of a different relationship with her father.

## Key Passages

> Mrs. Garstin bestowed upon her all the affection, a harsh, competent, calculating affection, of which she was capable; she dreamed ambitious dreams; it was not a good marriage she aimed at for her daughter, but a brilliant one. (008-003)

> It was unlikely now that Bernard Garstin would ever be made a judge of the High Court, but he might still hope for a County Court judgeship or at the worst an appointment in the Colonies. (008-002)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — elder daughter; the primary object of her ambition and the principal victim of her values
- [[Mr. Garstin]] — husband; a man she regards as a professional disappointment
- [[Doris Dennison]] — younger daughter; the child who fulfilled the marital ambitions Kitty could not

## Significance

Mrs. Garstin represents the social system that the novel indicts — a world in which women's worth is measured by their marriages and mothers function as brokers. Her "harsh, competent, calculating affection" is the emotional inheritance Kitty must overcome to become a different kind of woman — and, the novel implies, a different kind of mother.
