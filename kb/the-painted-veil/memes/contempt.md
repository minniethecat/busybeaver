## Definition

Contempt in *The Painted Veil* operates on three intertwined levels: the personal contempt Walter Fane feels for his wife's betrayal and for her intellectual shallowness; the social contempt embedded in British colonial hierarchies; and the self-contempt Kitty experiences when she confronts her own moral inadequacy. Maugham treats contempt not merely as an emotion but as a structural principle — it organizes relationships, motivates actions, and ultimately must be overcome for genuine human connection to become possible.

## Context in the Novel

Walter's contempt is the novel's most devastating form of this emotion. His tone when speaking to Kitty after discovering her affair is described as "contemptuous" (023-019), and his decision to take her to Mei-tan-fu is framed as punishment born of contempt for her entire character. The colonial British society in Hong Kong expresses contempt for the Chinese population, attending to "the China in which they lived" with only "passing and somewhat contemptuous attention" (058-011). Kitty, in turn, feels contempt for herself after recognizing the depth of her folly — "She had only contempt for herself because once she had loved him" (044-004), referring to Townsend.

## Thematic Analysis

Contempt in the novel serves as the antithesis of the compassion and charity that the convent embodies. Walter's tragedy is that his contempt — however justified — imprisons him as much as it punishes Kitty. His inability to move past contempt toward forgiveness or understanding renders him unable to accept Kitty's genuine growth at Mei-tan-fu. Kitty's self-contempt proves more productive: by acknowledging her own contemptibility, she begins the painful work of moral reconstruction. The novel suggests that contempt, unlike hatred, carries an element of judgment that makes reconciliation particularly difficult.

## Key Passages

> His tone was so contemptuous that she flushed with anger. (023-019)

> She had only contempt for herself because once she had loved him. (044-004)

> It seemed to her faintly contemptible that when she lay in his arms, his desire appeased, he who was so timid and subdued in his everyday life should be so sure of himself. (012-002)

> The cathedrals and the temples, the mosques and the churches of the world were built by men who believed; but the factories and the prisons and the barracks and the palaces were built by men who did not. And Walter, she knew, had always held him in contempt. (036-001)
