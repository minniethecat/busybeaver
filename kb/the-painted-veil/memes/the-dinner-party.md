## Overview

The dinner party is the central social ritual of colonial [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] in [[The Painted Veil]]. It is at a dinner at [[government-house|Government House]] that [[kitty-fane|Kitty]] first meets [[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]], and it is through the dinner-party circuit that their [[adultery|affair]] develops and is sustained. The dinner party is where the novel's critique of colonial society is sharpest: the elaborate rituals of politeness that mask the operations of [[passion|desire]], ambition, and betrayal.

## Key Dinner Parties

### Government House

[[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s first significant dinner in [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] is at [[government-house|Government House]], where she meets [[charles-townsend|Charlie Townsend]] and his wife [[dorothy-townsend|Dorothy]]. [[charles-townsend|Townsend]]'s charm — his ability to make every woman feel she is the only person in the room — is displayed to maximum effect in this setting:

> Kitty, coming to Hong Kong on her marriage, had found it hard to reconcile herself to the fact that her social position was determined by her husband's official rank. (004-001)

### The Townsend Dinner

The dinner at the [[charles-townsend|Townsend]]s' house marks a turning point in [[kitty-fane|Kitty]] and Charlie's relationship. The domestic setting, with [[dorothy-townsend|Dorothy]] playing hostess, creates a charged triangle: the wife, the husband, and the guest who is already more than a guest. Maugham's handling of this scene is a master[[social-class|class]] in dramatic irony.

## Social Function

Dinner parties in the novel serve multiple functions:

1. **Status n[[vanity|ego]]tiation**: Seating arrangements, invitations, and conversation topics all encode and enforce the colonial hierarchy.
2. **Erotic theatre**: The dinner party is where [[passion|desire]] is performed under the guise of politeness — glances, asides, and the strategic arrangement of after-dinner groupings.
3. **Moral exposure**: The contrast between public performance and private reality is the dinner party's essential drama.

## Key Passages

> Kitty was lively; she was willing to talk and she was amused by the people she met. She found the parties as entertaining as she had expected. (012-005)

[[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s enjoyment of the dinner-party circuit reflects her initial immersion in the colonial social world, before the [[adultery|affair]] with [[charles-townsend|Townsend]] complicates her position.

## Significance

The dinner party is the novel's primary stage for the drama of colonial manners. It is where the "painted veil" of social propriety is most elaborately woven — and where it is most vulnerable to being torn. [[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s moral awakening requires her to see through the dinner party's illusions to the realities they conceal.
