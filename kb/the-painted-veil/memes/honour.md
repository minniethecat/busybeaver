# Honour

Honour in *The Painted Veil* functions as both a social obligation and a personal moral compass. The novel examines honour through the contrasting behaviors of Walter Fane (who lives by it), Charles Townsend (who violates it), and Kitty (who learns to understand it).

## Walter's Honour

Walter embodies honour through his professional dedication and personal conduct. Kitty recognizes "his great qualities, his unselfishness and honour, his intelligence and sensibility" (061-005) — but only after it is too late. Walter's honour is both his defining virtue and his tragedy: he "surrendered to his sense of duty" (080-031) even when it meant self-destruction.

## Honour as Duty

The novel repeatedly links honour to duty. Walter appeals to Townsend "through his sense of duty" (024-045) — a strategy that fails because Townsend lacks the honour to respond. Walter is later memorialized as "a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036). The Mother Superior articulates the moral framework: "it is nothing to do your duty, that is demanded of you" (068-052) — honour is the minimum, not the achievement.

## Townsend's Dishonour

Charles Townsend serves as honour's antithesis. He gives Kitty his "word of honour" (055-033) yet is incapable of sacrifice. His charm masks a fundamental dishonourableness that Kitty only fully grasps after returning from Mei-tan-fu.

## Key Passage

> It was a pity that with his great qualities, his unselfishness and honour, his intelligence and sensibility, he should be so unlovable. She was not in the least frightened of him now, but sorry for him, and at the same time she could not help thinking him slightly absurd. The depth of his emotion made him vulnerable and she had a feeling that somehow and at some time she could work upon it as to induce him to forgive her.
>
> (061-005)
