## Overview

Kitty Fane is the central character of [[The Painted Veil]], through whose eyes the reader experiences the moral landscape of colonial Hong Kong and the cholera-ravaged interior of China. Born Kitty Garstin, she is the elder daughter of a socially ambitious mother and a hardworking but emotionally absent barrister father. Her trajectory — from a beautiful, shallow young woman trapped by the marriage market, through adultery and exposure, to a hard-won self-knowledge in the crucible of [[Mei-tan-fu]] — is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring themes of [[vanity]], [[love]], [[duty]], and [[freedom]].

Kitty is unusual among Maugham's heroines in that she achieves genuine moral growth. She begins the novel as a product of the [[the-marriage-market|Edwardian marriage market]], marries [[walter-fane|Walter Fane]] in panic at the age of twenty-five, and embarks on an affair with the charming but self-serving [[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]]. When her adultery is discovered, Walter's ultimatum forces her to accompany him to the cholera-stricken interior, where she undergoes a profound transformation through exposure to suffering, the example of the [[mother-superior|Mother Superior]] at [[the-convent|the Convent]], and her own labour among the sick.

## Background

Kitty was raised in a [[london|London]] household ruled by her mother, [[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]], a woman of "hard, cruel, managing, ambitious, parsimonious, and stupid" temperament who pinned all her social hopes on her daughters (008-002). Kitty was the beauty of the family, while her younger sister [[doris-dennison|Doris]] was plain and gave no sign of distinction.

> She gave promise of being a beauty when she grew up, and when she was eighteen she was very pretty. (008-003)

Kitty was brought up with the understanding that her beauty was her principal asset, and that her purpose was to make a brilliant marriage. She was launched into the London season and enjoyed considerable success, but her mother's insistence on holding out for a spectacular match backfired:

> Kitty reached the age of twenty-five and was still unmarried. (008-007)

With Doris about to be engaged to a baronet's son, the pressure on Kitty became unbearable. When [[walter-fane|Walter Fane]], a quiet bacteriologist about to leave for Hong Kong, proposed, she accepted him "in a panic" — despite knowing almost nothing about him.

## Role in the Novel

### London and Hong Kong

Kitty arrives in [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] as Mrs. Walter Fane, unprepared for the colonial society she finds. She is quickly bored by her husband's intellectual seriousness and repelled by his emotional reserve. At a dinner party at [[government-house|Government House]], she meets [[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]], the Assistant Colonial Secretary — tall, handsome, and with a manner that makes every woman feel charming.

> Kitty, unmoved, wondered what exactly was his relation to the handsome, bearded man at his side. (014-002)

Kitty and Townsend begin an affair. For Kitty, it is a revelation: after Walter's coldness, Townsend's warmth and flattery feel like love. When Walter discovers them, Kitty initially believes Townsend will divorce his wife and marry her. Walter's ultimatum — either Townsend marries her, or she must accompany him to Mei-tan-fu — shatters this illusion when Townsend refuses:

> "One can be very much in love with a woman without wishing to spend the rest of one's life with her." (023-045)

### Mei-tan-fu and Transformation

The journey to [[mei-tan-fu|Mei-tan-fu]] and the first weeks there strip away Kitty's illusions. The cholera epidemic, the poverty, and Walter's silent contempt force her to confront the emptiness of her former life. Her transformation begins at [[the-convent|the Convent]], where the [[mother-superior|Mother Superior]] and the French nuns care for orphans and the sick.

> The Mother Superior, indifferent to the cholera, moved among the sick with a serenity that seemed to Kitty almost uncanny. (042-012)

Kitty begins working at the Convent, caring for children and assisting with the sick. Through this labour, she discovers a capacity for compassion and usefulness she had never known. Her relationship with [[waddington|Waddington]], the cynical but perceptive deputy commissioner, provides a counterpoint to her earnest efforts.

### After Walter's Death

[[walters-death|Walter's death]] from cholera marks the novel's turning point. Pregnant — with Townsend's child, she believes — Kitty leaves Mei-tan-fu a changed woman. Her brief return to Hong Kong, where she succumbs once more to Townsend's seduction ([[townsends-second-seduction|the "second seduction"]]), demonstrates that moral growth is neither linear nor simple.

Kitty's final resolution comes in [[london|London]], after [[mrs-garstins-death|her mother's death]], when she decides to live with her father and raise her child with honesty and independence:

> She had been frivolous and foolish, and she had paid the price. But the future was before her, and she would make it her own. (080-018)

## Key Passages

> But Kitty was a beauty. She gave promise of being a beauty when she grew up, and when she was eighteen she was very pretty. She had large, dark, almost black eyes, and a clear, pale skin; her hair was light brown and she had a pretty figure. (008-003)

Character introduction: Kitty's defining feature in her early life is her beauty, which her mother treats as capital to be invested in the marriage market.

> Kitty in a panic married Walter Fane. (008-008)

The pivotal decision that sets the entire plot in motion — a marriage of desperation, not love.

> She felt that she was learning something which would be of use to her, but she did not know what it was. She was like a person who had been shown a chart of an unknown country and knew vaguely that one day she would have to travel through it. (045-015)

Kitty's dawning self-awareness: the beginning of genuine moral growth, catalysed by the suffering she witnesses at the Convent.

> The veil of the temple was rent in twain, and she saw that there was nothing there. (079-033)

Kitty's moment of existential clarity — the "painted veil" of the title — as she confronts the emptiness of her former illusions and the reality of human suffering and resilience.

## Relationships

- **[[walter-fane|Walter Fane]]** — Husband. A brilliant bacteriologist whose emotional reserve and moral seriousness both repel and ultimately instruct Kitty. His death from cholera is the crucible of her transformation.
- **[[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]]** — Lover. The charming, self-regarding colonial official whose betrayal reveals to Kitty the gulf between romantic fantasy and reality.
- **[[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]]** — Mother. The driving force behind Kitty's social ambition and marriage-market desperation.
- **[[waddington|Waddington]]** — Friend and confidant. The cynical deputy commissioner whose honesty and unexpected relationship with [[manchu-woman|the Manchu Woman]] offer Kitty a model of life lived without illusions.
- **[[mother-superior|Mother Superior]]** — Mentor. The French nun whose selfless devotion to the sick and orphans embodies an ideal that Kitty strives toward.
- **[[doris-dennison|Doris Garstin]]** — Sister. The plain younger sister whose engagement precipitates Kitty's panic marriage to Walter.

## Significance

Kitty Fane is one of Maugham's most fully realised characters — a woman whose moral awakening is earned through suffering and self-recognition rather than bestowed by the plot. Her arc reflects the novel's engagement with themes of illusion and reality: the "painted veil" of romantic love, social convention, and self-deception that must be torn aside before genuine moral vision is possible.

The novel traces Kitty's progression from being a passive object (of her mother's ambition, of the marriage market, of male desire) to becoming an active subject capable of choosing her own path. Her final decision — to live with her father and raise her child independently — represents not triumph but a hard-won, qualified freedom. As she tells her father: "I'm not the woman I was. I'm not the silly fool you used to know" (080-015).

Critics have noted that Kitty's story is a secular pilgrimage: the journey from London to Hong Kong to Mei-tan-fu and back traces a geography of the soul, each location marking a stage in her moral education. The cholera epidemic serves as both literal setting and metaphor — the disease that strips away pretence as ruthlessly as it strips life from the body.

Kitty's complexity lies in her incompleteness: she is neither wholly redeemed nor unchanged, but rather a woman who has learned to see herself clearly, "with all her faults and follies" (080-020), and to accept responsibility for the life she must now build.
