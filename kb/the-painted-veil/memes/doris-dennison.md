# Doris Dennison

## Overview

Doris Dennison (née Garstin) is Kitty's younger sister. Less beautiful than Kitty but more pragmatic, Doris makes "quite a good marriage" (010-017) at eighteen to Geoffrey Dennison, a young man who will inherit a baronetcy. Her engagement is the event that precipitates Kitty's crisis: at twenty-five and still single, watching her younger sister marry first, Kitty panics and accepts Walter Fane's proposal.

## Background

Doris was always the less favored daughter. "Doris gave no sign of good looks, her nose was too long and her figure was lumpy" (008-002), and Mrs. Garstin invested her ambitions in Kitty instead. But Doris, free from the pressure of being the "beautiful one," made a sensible match while Kitty held out for brilliance.

## Role in the Novel

Doris appears primarily as a narrative catalyst rather than a developed character. Her engagement announcement triggers Kitty's fear of being left "on the shelf" and directly leads to her acceptance of Walter's proposal: "It wouldn't be very nice to be a bridesmaid at Doris's wedding. She would be glad to escape that" (011-032). Later in the novel, Kitty reflects that Doris "had done her duty and produced an heir to the new baronetcy" (061-004), contrasting Doris's conventional success with her own unconventional journey.

## Key Passages

> Then came the announcement of Doris's engagement to Geoffrey Dennison. Doris, at eighteen, was making quite a good marriage, and she was twenty-five and single. (010-017)

> She had done her duty and produced an heir to the new baronetcy; Kitty smiled at the thought. (061-004)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — elder sister; the sibling rivalry that drives Kitty's fateful decision
- [[Mrs. Garstin]] — mother; the daughter who fulfilled the marital ambitions Kitty could not
- [[Mr. Garstin]] — father; the parent Doris, like Kitty, barely knew

## Significance

Doris functions as the conventional alternative to Kitty's unconventional path. She did what was expected — made a good marriage, produced an heir — and the novel neither condemns nor celebrates her for it. She is simply the road not taken, and Kitty's ultimate wisdom includes accepting that both roads have their costs.
