# Happiness

Happiness in *The Painted Veil* is portrayed as something characters pursue but rarely sustain. Maugham traces its evolution from naive romantic expectation through disillusionment to a more complex, spiritually grounded form.

## Romantic Illusion

At the novel's opening, Kitty experiences happiness in her affair with Charles Townsend: "Her happiness, sometimes almost more than she could bear" (016-001). This happiness is built on illusion and collapses when Walter discovers the affair.

## Happiness in Service

At the convent, Kitty finds an unexpected form of happiness through caring for the children: "it gave her a peculiar happiness to discern their confidence" (049-002). The Mother Superior tells her to think "what a happiness for your husband" (055-039) that Kitty is working at the convent, reframing happiness as something earned through sacrifice rather than received through pleasure.

## Spiritual Redefinition

The Mother Superior offers Kitty a vision of happiness that "passes all understanding" (068-052), pointing toward spiritual contentment rather than worldly satisfaction. By the novel's end, Kitty reflects on the "mirage of happiness" (080-029) — the false promises of romantic and material satisfaction — and begins to conceive of a happiness grounded in autonomy and purpose.

## Key Passage

> Her happiness, sometimes almost more than she could bear, renewed her beauty. Just before she married, beginning to lose her first freshness, she had looked tired and drawn. The uncharitable said that she was going off. But there is all the difference between a girl of twenty-five and a married woman of that age. She was like a rosebud that is beginning to turn yellow at the edges of the petals, and then suddenly she was a rose in full bloom.
>
> (016-001)
