## Overview

Doris Garstin is the younger daughter of the Garstin family, and her role in [[The Painted Veil]] — though brief in page time — is structurally crucial. Where [[kitty-fane|Kitty]] is the family beauty, Doris is the plain, unremarkable sister whose conventional success in the marriage market precipitates Kitty's crisis. Doris's engagement to a baronet's son is the proximate cause of Kitty's panic marriage to [[walter-fane|Walter Fane]], setting the entire plot in motion.

Doris embodies the Edwardian ideal of feminine success: she makes the "right" match without drama or ambition. Her happiness is not explored but assumed — a mirror that reflects Kitty's more complicated, more interesting failure to fit the mould.

## Background

Doris was raised alongside Kitty in the [[the-garstin-household|Garstin household]] in [[london|London]], under the rule of their socially ambitious mother, [[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]].

> There were two, Kitty and Doris. Doris gave no sign of being anything but a commonplace girl. (008-002)

Unlike Kitty, Doris possessed no striking beauty and was never presented as a candidate for a dazzling marriage. Her mother regarded her with indifference, focusing all her social engineering on Kitty. This very ordinariness became Doris's advantage: without the burden of being "the beauty," she was free to pursue a conventional path without the pressure of expectation.

## Role in the Novel

Doris's engagement to Geoffrey Dennison — the only son of a baronet "with a good income" — is announced when Kitty is twenty-five and still unmarried:

> Doris gave no sign of being anything but a commonplace girl. She had never been able to get beyond a certain point in any of the subjects she studied. But she was not a fool, and she had a placid and amiable temper. (008-002)

For [[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]], Doris's engagement is both triumph and humiliation: triumph because one daughter has finally made a socially advantageous match; humiliation because it is the plain daughter, not the beauty, who has succeeded. The pressure on Kitty redoubles, and within months she accepts Walter Fane's proposal.

## Key Passages

> Doris gave no sign of being anything but a commonplace girl. She had never been able to get beyond a certain point in any of the subjects she studied. But she was not a fool, and she had a placid and amiable temper. (008-002)

The novel's definitive description of Doris: unremarkable but pleasant, a foil to Kitty's more vivid and complicated personality.

> Kitty reached the age of twenty-five and was still unmarried. Mrs. Garstin was exasperated. (008-007)

Doris's engagement is the catalyst for this crisis. Her conventional success illuminates Kitty's unconventional failure, and the contrast drives the novel's first act.

## Relationships

- **[[kitty-fane|Kitty Fane]]** — Sister. Doris is Kitty's foil: where Kitty is beautiful and restless, Doris is plain and content. Doris's engagement precipitates the crisis that sends Kitty to Hong Kong.
- **[[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]]** — Mother. Mrs. Garstin's favouritism toward Kitty and neglect of Doris reflects the instrumental logic of the Edwardian marriage market.
- **[[geoffrey-dennison|Geoffrey Dennison]]** — Husband. The baronet's son whose proposal marks Doris's quiet victory in the marriage stakes.

## Significance

Doris functions as a structural counterweight to Kitty. Her conventional path — marry well, produce heirs, never question the system — is the life Kitty was supposed to have and could not accept. By succeeding where Kitty fails, Doris reveals that the marriage market's logic works perfectly for those who fit its mould — and destroys those who do not.

Doris's very absence from the novel's later sections is telling: once she has served her function as catalyst, she disappears from the narrative entirely. She is, in a sense, a successful version of the woman Kitty was raised to be — and her success is presented not as triumph but as a kind of narrative irrelevance. The novel's interest lies with the woman who broke the mould, not the one who fit it.
