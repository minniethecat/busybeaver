# The Cemetery

## Overview

The cemetery in Mei-tan-fu is where the cholera dead are buried, and where Walter Fane is laid to rest (064-005). It represents the novel's most literal image of death as the great equalizer — the place where social status, beauty, and charm are rendered irrelevant.

## Description

The cemetery is described only briefly: a burying ground on the outskirts of the city, where the dead are interred with the hurried ceremonies that epidemic conditions demand (034-002). Walter's grave is a simple mound, unmarked by any monument (038-016).

> The hill was covered close with little green mounds, close, close to one another, so that the ground was ribbed like the sea-sand when the tide has gone out; and this she knew too, for she had passed just such a spot as they approached each populous city and left it. It was the graveyard.
>
> (030-001)

## Events at This Location

- The burial of cholera victims throughout the epidemic
- Walter Fane's interment after his death from cholera

## Associated Characters

- [[Walter Fane]] — buried here, his grave the physical marker of his sacrifice
- [[Kitty Fane]] — who must confront the finality of Walter's death at his graveside

## Symbolic Significance

The cemetery is where the "painted veil" is most fully stripped away. In death, Walter is neither the cuckolded husband nor the heroic scientist — he is simply dead, and Kitty's grief, belated and incomplete, must reckon with that irreducible fact (080-001).
