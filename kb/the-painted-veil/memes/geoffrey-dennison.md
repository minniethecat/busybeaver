# Geoffrey Dennison

## Overview

Geoffrey Dennison is a minor character with major narrative consequences. His engagement to Kitty's younger sister Doris — "Doris, at eighteen, was making quite a good marriage" (010-017) — is the event that precipitates Kitty's panic. At twenty-five and still single, watching her younger sister make a socially advantageous match, Kitty accepts Walter Fane's proposal in a desperate bid to escape humiliation.

## Background

Geoffrey is the son of a baronet, destined to inherit a title and estate. His engagement to Doris represents the kind of match Mrs. Garstin had hoped for Kitty — socially advantageous, financially secure, conventionally respectable.

## Role in the Novel

Geoffrey never appears directly; he is always offstage, mentioned only in connection with Doris. But his engagement announcement is one of the novel's most consequential events. Without the pressure it creates, Kitty might not have married Walter, and the entire chain of events — the affair, Mei-tan-fu, Walter's death — would not have occurred.

## Key Passages

> Then came the announcement of Doris's engagement to Geoffrey Dennison. Doris, at eighteen, was making quite a good marriage, and she was twenty-five and single. (010-017)

## Relationships

- [[Doris Dennison]] — wife; the younger sister whose early marriage catalyzes Kitty's fateful decision

## Significance

Geoffrey Dennison is a narrative catalyst rather than a developed character — the offstage figure whose very ordinariness (a young man making a conventional marriage to a suitable girl) precipitates the novel's extraordinary events.
