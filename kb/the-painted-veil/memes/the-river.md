# The River

## Overview

The river flowing through Mei-tan-fu provides the novel's most sustained natural imagery. Maugham describes it at length: "It was a strange, impressive scene. The river, broad here, flowed placidly between its low banks. On the far side mist hung over the hills" (029-001). From her window she could see the narrow river below and the city opposite (033-003). The river's beauty — indifferent to human suffering — frames the novel's meditation on the relationship between nature, mortality, and meaning.

## Description

The river is broad and placid, flowing through a landscape of hills and mist. It is described with the kind of attention to natural beauty that is rare in the novel, suggesting that the landscape of Mei-tan-fu offers a perspective unavailable in the social world of Hong Kong.

## Events at This Location

- Kitty's contemplative walks along the riverbank during her time in Mei-tan-fu
- Her conversations with Waddington, who shares his sardonic wisdom by the river

## Associated Characters

- [[Kitty Fane]] — the river is where she thinks, away from the convent and the hospital
- [[Waddington]] — her companion on riverside walks

## Symbolic Significance

The river represents the natural world that exists beyond human social dramas — beautiful, indifferent, eternal. It is the novel's visual reminder that the social values that consume the characters are, in the larger scheme of things, vanishingly small.
