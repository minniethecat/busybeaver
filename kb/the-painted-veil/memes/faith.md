# Faith

## Definition

In *The Painted Veil*, religious faith appears primarily through the French Catholic mission in Mei-tan-fu. The nuns' faith is practical rather than doctrinal — expressed through their tireless care for cholera orphans rather than theological discourse. It represents a value system entirely independent of the social calculations that govern the British colonial world, and it exerts a powerful gravitational pull on Kitty's developing moral consciousness.

## Context in the Novel

The convent is introduced as a counter-world to the colonial society of Hong Kong. Where British society is governed by status, appearances, and strategic marriages, the convent is governed by service, humility, and devotion. Kitty's first visit leaves her deeply moved: "Within the convent walls the infirmary was crowded with the victims of the epidemic" (041-013).

The Mother Superior becomes the novel's principal voice of spiritual wisdom. Her teaching — that "when love and duty are one, then grace is in you" (068-052) — offers Kitty a framework for understanding her own experience that transcends social convention.

## Thematic Analysis

**Faith as Action**: The nuns' faith is demonstrated through work, not declared through doctrine. They do not proselytize to Kitty; they simply care for the dying, and this example is more persuasive than any sermon.

**Faith and Doubt**: Walter, the scientist, exists outside the framework of faith. The novel does not resolve the tension between scientific rationalism and religious belief — it simply presents both as genuine human responses to suffering.

**Faith as Alternative to Society**: For Kitty, the convent represents a way of being in the world that does not depend on male admiration or social status. It suggests the possibility of a life organized around service rather than self.

## Key Passages

> Within the convent walls the infirmary was crowded with the victims of the epidemic. (041-013)
