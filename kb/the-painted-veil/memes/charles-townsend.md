# Charles Townsend

## Overview

Charles Townsend is the Assistant Colonial Secretary in Hong Kong and Kitty Fane's lover. Universally admired for his charm, good looks, and political skill, Townsend is revealed — under pressure — to be entirely self-serving. Waddington describes him with devastating accuracy: "He's made a science of popularity" (035-004). When Kitty's affair is discovered and Walter demands that Townsend marry her, Townsend refuses — not from malice but from an incapacity to sacrifice anything for anyone.

## Background

Townsend has risen through the colonial administration on the strength of his personal charm rather than any particular administrative talent. He is married to Dorothy Townsend, who is described as a competent, pleasant woman. Their marriage appears stable but is understood — by Townsend, at least — to permit extramarital affairs so long as they remain discreet.

## Role in the Novel

Townsend's affair with Kitty is the novel's inciting crisis. He pursues her with practiced seduction, and Kitty — starved for admiration in her marriage to Walter — falls completely. When exposed, Townsend's true character emerges: he will not jeopardize his career or his comfortable marriage for Kitty. His refusal to "stand by her" after promising he would constitutes Kitty's first genuine disillusion.

After Walter's death, Townsend reappears in Hong Kong and successfully seduces Kitty again — a relapse that fills her with self-loathing and precipitates her final decision to leave for England. Townsend functions as the novel's test: to resist him is to have genuinely changed.

## Key Passages

> "He's made a science of popularity. He has the gift of making everyone he meets feel that he is the one person in the world he wants to see." (035-004)

> "I can understand that it is something of a blow to your vanity," he returned mockingly. (054-012)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — lover; his affair with Kitty and subsequent abandonment catalyze her transformation
- [[Dorothy Townsend]] — wife; the woman Townsend will not leave, not from love but from convenience
- [[Walter Fane]] — cuckolded husband; Townsend's foil — charm versus substance
- [[Waddington]] — observer; the only character who sees through Townsend clearly

## Significance

Charles Townsend is the novel's supreme portrait of charm without substance. He is not a villain in any melodramatic sense — he is simply incapable of putting anyone's interests above his own. His function in the novel is to demonstrate that what passes for love in the social world is often mere vanity — and that recognizing this is the beginning of wisdom.
