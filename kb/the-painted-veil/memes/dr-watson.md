# Dr. Watson

## Overview

Dr. Watson was the missionary doctor stationed in Mei-tan-fu before Walter Fane's arrival. He died of cholera three days before the Fanes reached the city. Though he never appears directly in the novel, Watson is frequently invoked — by Waddington, by the nuns, by the Chinese — as a standard of selfless devotion against which Walter is compared and, ultimately, found worthy.

## Background

Watson was a medical missionary who served the people of Mei-tan-fu with evident devotion. "He loved the human race," Waddington tells Kitty, contrasting Watson's motivation with Walter's more ambiguous commitment (038-037). He lived simply, worked tirelessly, and died of the disease he was fighting.

## Role in the Novel

Watson functions as a ghostly presence — the man whose shoes Walter must fill. The comparison haunts Walter: Watson was loved for himself; Walter is respected for his competence. The question of whether Walter's motives are as pure as Watson's — or whether Watson's apparent purity is itself a simplification — is one the novel leaves open.

## Key Passages

> "Watson was different. He loved the human race." (038-037)

> "Watson was the missionary who was here. He died of cholera three days ago." (031-010)

## Relationships

- [[Walter Fane]] — successor; the man who replaces him and is measured against his example
- [[Mother Superior]] — colleague; she mourned Watson and comes to respect Walter equally
- [[Waddington]] — friend; his comparison of Watson and Walter provides crucial psychological insight

## Significance

Dr. Watson represents the ideal of selfless service that the novel both honors and complicates. His goodness is never questioned — but the novel also suggests that Walter's more complex, conflicted dedication may be equally valuable and more humanly true.
