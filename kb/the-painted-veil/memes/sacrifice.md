# Sacrifice

## Definition

Sacrifice in *The Painted Veil* is the act of surrendering personal desire in service of a higher claim — whether duty, love, faith, or simply the needs of others. It is embodied most dramatically in Walter Fane, who gives his life combating cholera, and in the French nuns, who have given up family, comfort, and country to serve the poor. The novel treats sacrifice with characteristic ambivalence: it can be noble, but it can also be a form of self-destruction.

## Context in the Novel

Walter's sacrifice is the novel's central instance. He goes to Mei-tan-fu knowing the danger, and his bacteriological work undoubtedly saves lives. His death is framed by Townsend as martyrdom: "Walter died a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036). But the novel complicates this reading. Walter's sacrifice is also, in part, a surrender to despair — his final words suggest a man who could not find a reason to live.

The nuns' sacrifice is presented more straightforwardly as genuine. They have given up everything the world values and gained, in the Mother Superior's account, "a happiness which passes all understanding" (068-052). Their sacrifice is not loss but exchange.

## Thematic Analysis

**Sacrifice as Redemption**: Kitty's moral growth involves learning to sacrifice — her comfort, her vanity, her sense of entitlement. The novel suggests that sacrifice is the mechanism by which character is built.

**The Ambiguity of Sacrifice**: Walter's case warns against simple valorization of sacrifice. Was his death heroic, or was it a form of suicide? The novel refuses to adjudicate definitively.

**Sacrifice and Gender**: The novel notes the asymmetry of sacrifice: women are expected to sacrifice for family and reputation; men sacrifice for duty and honor. Kitty's journey involves reclaiming sacrifice as a freely chosen act rather than a socially imposed obligation.
