# Vanity

## Definition

Vanity in *The Painted Veil* is the self-regarding impulse that prevents characters from seeing themselves or others clearly. It is the "painted veil" of the novel's title — the decorative surface that conceals the harder truths beneath. Each major character is ensnared by vanity in a different form: Kitty's vanity of beauty and social conquest, Townsend's vanity of charm and status, Walter's vanity of moral superiority.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty's vanity is established early: "accustomed to flattery all her life," she has built her identity on being admired (023-031). Her marriage to Walter wounds this vanity — he does not flatter her, and his quiet superiority makes her feel diminished. Her affair with Townsend restores her sense of being desirable and powerful.

Townsend explicitly names vanity when mocking Kitty's distress: "I can understand that it is something of a blow to your vanity" (054-012). Kitty's repetition of the word — "My vanity" (054-013) — marks the beginning of self-awareness. The novel's trajectory traces her gradual release from vanity's grip through the humbling experiences of the epidemic and the convent.

## Thematic Analysis

**Vanity as Social Currency**: Mrs. Garstin's ambition for her daughters is a form of vicarious vanity. Kitty is valued for her beauty, not for her character; her entire sense of worth is external and fragile.

**Vanity Disguised as Love**: Kitty's passion for Townsend is largely vanity — he makes her feel desirable. Walter's love for Kitty also contains vanity — he resents that she does not return his regard.

**The Cure for Vanity**: The novel suggests that vanity is cured not by moral instruction but by suffering and service. Kitty's work in the convent, her confrontation with death, and her growing care for others gradually dissolve the self-absorption that defined her.
