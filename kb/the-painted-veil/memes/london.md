# London

## Overview

London in *The Painted Veil* is the origin point of Kitty's values and the social system that entraps her. It is the world of London seasons, ambitious mothers, eligible bachelors, and the relentless pressure to marry well. The novel opens with Kitty already in Hong Kong, but London's influence — through Mrs. Garstin's letters, through Kitty's memories, through the values she has internalized — pervades the early chapters.

## Description

The London of the novel is the upper-middle-class world of barristers' families, debutante balls, and strategic social climbing. The Garstin household in South Kensington is the novel's primary London location: a house governed by Mrs. Garstin's ambition and Mr. Garstin's quiet withdrawal.

## Symbolic Significance

London represents the social conditioning Kitty must transcend. Its values — status, appearance, strategic marriage — are precisely what the novel's moral vision rejects. Kitty's return to London at the novel's end, and her decision to leave it for the Bahamas with her father, represents her liberation from its claims.

The distance between Mei-tan-fu and London — measured not in miles but in the gulf between two ways of living — is captured when Waddington, surrounded by cholera and death, turns the conversation to London's West End:

> But Waddington began to speak of London. He talked of the theatres. He knew everything that was being played at the moment and he told them what pieces he had seen when he was last home on leave. He laughed as he recollected the humour of this low comedian and sighed as he reflected on the beauty of that star of musical comedy.
>
> (032-011)
