# The Club

## Overview

The Club is the center of male social and political life in the colony. It is where Charles Townsend thrives — "He's made a science of popularity" (035-004) — and where Walter Fane, with his reserve and intellectual seriousness, is conspicuously out of place. The Club embodies the gendered asymmetry of colonial society: men gather, network, and decide; women are excluded.

## Description

The Club is described only in passing but its function is clear: it is the informal seat of colonial power, where careers are advanced and reputations made over drinks and conversation. Townsend's mastery of this environment is the source of his influence; Walter's discomfort in it is the source of his social invisibility.

Kitty, rationalizing her affair with Townsend and the damage it would cause, appeals to the Club as the refuge that would see any man through domestic upheaval:

> after all, he was absorbed in his work; and a man always had his club, he might be upset at first, but he would get over it; there was no reason why he should not marry somebody else.
>
> (017-002)

## Symbolic Significance

The Club represents the informal power structure that the novel critiques — a world where charm counts for more than competence and where women have no direct access to influence.
