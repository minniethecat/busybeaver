# Mr. Garstin

## Overview

Bernard Garstin is Kitty's father and one of the novel's most quietly poignant figures. A barrister who never achieved the professional eminence his wife expected, he has been treated as a disappointment for so long that he has retreated into emotional detachment. His reappearance at the novel's end — when Kitty, newly orphaned of her mother, proposes to accompany him to his new judicial post — provides the novel's most hopeful vision of familial relationship rebuilt on honesty rather than obligation.

## Background

Mr. Garstin married ambitiously and failed to deliver. His law career never reached the High Court, and his wife never forgave him for it. "He made no headway as a leader and his briefs were few. But he concealed any disappointment he may have felt, and if he reproached his wife it was in his heart" (007-005). Over the years, he withdrew from family life, finding refuge in his study and his routine.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Garstin appears only at the novel's margins until its final pages, when Kitty, returning from China after her mother's death, sees him clearly for the first time. Her recognition — that he has been as much a victim of Mrs. Garstin's ambition as she has — enables a reconciliation that is both moving and unsentimental. She asks to accompany him to his new post in the Bahamas, not from duty but from a genuine desire for connection.

## Key Passages

## Relationships

- [[Mrs. Garstin]] — wife; a woman whose disappointment has defined his adult life
- [[Doris Dennison]] — younger daughter; the child who followed the path Mrs. Garstin prescribed

## Significance

Mr. Garstin is the novel's quietest moral example. He has endured a life of disappointment without becoming bitter — a quieter achievement than it appears. His reconciliation with Kitty suggests that even the most damaged family relationships can be remade when honesty replaces obligation.
