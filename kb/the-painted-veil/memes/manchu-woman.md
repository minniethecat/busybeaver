# The Manchu Woman

## Overview

The Manchu woman is Waddington's companion in Mei-tan-fu. A member of the Manchu aristocracy displaced by the fall of the Qing dynasty, she lives with Waddington in an arrangement that scandalizes British colonial society but which Waddington treats with unapologetic affection. She says little, speaks no English, and represents — in her silent, dignified presence — a China that the British colonial characters cannot access or understand.

## Background

Waddington describes her origins elliptically: she is of Manchu nobility, her family ruined by the revolution (054-028). Her relationship with Waddington is one of mutual convenience that has deepened into genuine attachment. She remains culturally Chinese in a way that the British characters, for all their years in the East, never become.

## Role in the Novel

The Manchu woman appears only briefly but carries significant symbolic weight. She represents an alternative to the British social world that has entrapped Kitty — a life lived outside British conventions, on terms not dictated by marriage and status. Waddington's evident contentment with her challenges Kitty's assumptions about what constitutes a valid relationship.

## Key Passages

> [Waddington on his companion]: She has the most beautiful hands. (048-001)

## Relationships

- [[Waddington]] — companion; their relationship, unconventional by British standards, is the novel's quietest portrait of domestic contentment

## Significance

The Manchu woman embodies the novel's suggestion that genuine human connection can exist outside the institutions — marriage, church, colonial administration — that the British characters treat as universal. She is the novel's most radical figure: a woman who has found a life on her own terms.
