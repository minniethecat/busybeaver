## Definition

Ambition in *The Painted Veil* is portrayed through Mrs. Garstin's lifelong project of social climbing — a force as strong as parsimony, driving her to push her husband toward professional advancement and her daughters toward advantageous marriages. Maugham presents ambition not as admirable aspiration but as a corrosive compulsion that blinds its possessor to genuine human value and, in Mrs. Garstin's case, renders her incapable of love. The novel's irony is that Mrs. Garstin dies without knowing her ambition achieved its outward goal, its meaninglessness exposed by her death.

## Context in the Novel

Mrs. Garstin's ambition is introduced early in the novel as the organizing principle of the Garstin household. Her parsimony, which "balked her ambition" (007-003), prevents her from fully realizing her social schemes, creating a permanent tension between means and ends. Her ambition channels directly into her daughters: Doris is successfully married off to a baronet's son, while Kitty — the prettier, more promising daughter — disappoints by failing to secure a superior match in her first seasons. When Kitty marries Walter, a mere bacteriologist, Mrs. Garstin's contempt is barely concealed; it "accorded with her own desires" (008-004) when Kitty chose to accompany Walter to Hong Kong rather than remain a visible disappointment at home.

## Thematic Analysis

Mrs. Garstin's ambition represents the values of the society Kitty must ultimately reject. The novel sets up a contrast between social ambition and moral growth: Mrs. Garstin measures success in titles and dinner parties; the French nuns measure it in lives saved and children cared for. Kitty's journey takes her from her mother's value system toward the nuns'. The novel's closing irony — Mrs. Garstin dies "without knowing that her ambition, however modified by past disappointments, was at last achieved" (080-021) — drives home Maugham's judgment that ambition pursued for its own sake is hollow. The achievement means nothing because the person who desired it is gone, and the person who benefited from it (Kitty) no longer values it.

## Key Passages

> Her parsimony was as strong in her as ambition. She hated to spend money. She had the English housewife's soul, and her parsimony balked her ambition, and she could not bring herself to make the outlay that would enable her to cut the figure she desired. (007-003)

> She more than suspected her mother's ambition. It accorded with her own desires. She was launched upon the world, and the world was a very pleasant place. (008-004)

> She died without knowing that her ambition, however modified by past disappointments, was at last achieved. (080-021)
