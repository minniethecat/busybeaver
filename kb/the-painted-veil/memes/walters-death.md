# Walter's Death

## Overview

[[walter-fane|Walter]] contracts cholera, presumably in his laboratory, and dies after a brief illness. Colonel Yü sends word that collapse has set in (062-044). His final words — "The dog it was that died" — echo Oliver Goldsmith's elegy and have been debated ever since: are they an admission of self-contempt? A statement of futility? A final act of [[forgiveness|forgive]]ness? The novel leaves the question open. Colonel Yü attends the funeral, standing with bared head at the graveside as Waddington reads the burial service with an embarrassment unusual to him (065-001).

## Significance

[[death|Walter's death]] is the event that completes [[kitty-fane|Kitty]]'s transformation. In its aftermath, she must decide what kind of person she will be. The [[mother-superior|Mother Superior]]'s reframing — "[[walter-fane|Walter]] died a martyr to science and to his duty" (066-036) — offers one interpretation; [[walter-fane|Walter]]'s own words offer another. The novel holds both in tension.

## Key Passages

The novel's most famous exchange distills the central question — was Walter's death sacrifice, surrender, or both?

> "I thought it might mean something to you that Walter died a martyr to science and to his duty."
>
> Kitty shrugged her shoulders with a suspicion of impatience.
>
> "Walter died of a broken heart," she said.
>
> (066-036)
