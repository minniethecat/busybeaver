# Mei-tan-fu

## Overview

Mei-tan-fu is the remote Chinese city where the novel's central transformation occurs. Stricken by a cholera epidemic, it is a place of death, fear, and — paradoxically — spiritual renewal. Removed from the social world of Hong Kong, Kitty discovers capacities she did not know she had, and Walter finds in his scientific work a purpose that transcends his personal despair.

## Description

The city is described through contrast: the beauty of its natural setting against the horror of the epidemic. "It was a strange, impressive scene. The river, broad here, flowed placidly between its low banks. On the far side mist hung over the hills" (029-001). The convent, the hospital, Waddington's house, and the river landscape create a geography of illness, service, and quiet beauty.

## Symbolic Significance

Mei-tan-fu is the anti-Hong Kong — a place where social status is meaningless, where death strips away pretense, and where the only currency is what one can do for others. It is the novel's vision of a world stripped to its moral essentials.
