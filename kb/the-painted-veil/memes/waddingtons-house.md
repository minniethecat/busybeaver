# Waddington's House

## Overview

Waddington's house in Mei-tan-fu is where Kitty finds her closest thing to a friend during her exile. It is here that Waddington shares his sardonic wisdom over dinner, that Kitty meets the Manchu woman, and that the novel's sharpest psychological observations are delivered. On their first evening, Waddington invites them to dine, explaining that his cook has died and he would appreciate the company (031-026). The house, with its large Chinese dining-table, is furnished in a style that reflects Waddington's comfortable adaptation to local ways (032-002).

## Description

The house is modest — a colonial official's residence in a remote posting, comfortable but unpretentious. It is shared with Waddington's Manchu companion, whose presence scandalizes British visitors but whom Waddington treats with unapologetic affection.

## Events at This Location

- Kitty's first dinner with Waddington (031)
- Waddington's assessment of Townsend: "He's made a science of popularity" (035-004)
- Kitty's observation of Waddington's unconventional domestic arrangement

## Symbolic Significance

Waddington's house represents the possibility of living outside British conventions — a domestic arrangement that would scandalize Hong Kong society but that, in Mei-tan-fu, seems natural and contented.
