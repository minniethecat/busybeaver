## Definition

Courage in *The Painted Veil* is not the singular heroic virtue of adventure fiction but a quality that appears in multiple registers: Mrs. Garstin's hard ambition, Walter's silent endurance, the French nuns' daily self-sacrifice, and Kitty's gradual acquisition of moral resolve. Maugham presents courage as a capacity distributed unevenly across characters and circumstances — one may possess physical courage while lacking moral courage, and vice versa.

## Context in the Novel

The theme of courage is introduced early through Mrs. Garstin, whose "quality of courage" drives her relentless social ambition but is entirely self-serving. Walter Fane displays a different form of courage: his willingness to work in a cholera-stricken city is both professional dedication and, perhaps, a death wish born of despair. The French nuns at the convent represent a third register — religious courage expressed through daily, unglamorous service. Kitty's own courage develops gradually: from her initial panic at the prospect of death in Mei-tan-fu, through her decision to work in the convent, to her final resolve to face her future honestly and raise her child without the illusions that had crippled her own life.

## Thematic Analysis

The novel's treatment of courage is unusually nuanced. Courage is neither inherently virtuous (Mrs. Garstin's ambition is courageous but destructive) nor necessarily redemptive (Walter's courage does not save him). Instead, courage functions as an enabling condition: it creates the space in which moral growth may — or may not — occur. Kitty's courage is the most transformative because it is linked to growing self-knowledge. Her decision to return to Hong Kong after Walter's death, and later to leave it, requires a courage different from the physical bravery of facing an epidemic — it is the courage to confront one's own weakness and complicity.

## Key Passages

> But there was a quality of courage in Mrs. Garstin which in itself was admirable. She never complained. She never blamed her husband for his failure to become a leader at the bar. (008-001)

> "You've inspired me with courage. To go into the midst of a cholera epidemic — I could never have done that alone." (026-060)

> "That encourages me, ma mère," said Kitty. "I think not many wives would have had the courage to do what I did, and for the rest, how can I tell you how sorry I am?" (048-016)

> I'm beginning to feel a new courage. I feel like one of those old sailors who set out from their Spanish ports to discover a new world. (054-037)

> "I am glad to see you, my dear child. You show a fine courage in coming back here so soon after your great sorrow; and wisdom, for I am sure that a little work will keep you from brooding." (067-002)
