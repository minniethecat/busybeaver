# Mother

## Definition

Motherhood in *The Painted Veil* is portrayed across a moral spectrum: at one extreme, Mrs. Garstin's harsh, instrumental approach to her daughters' lives; at the other, the selfless maternal care of the French nuns tending cholera orphans. Kitty's own pregnancy — and her refusal to exploit it for social advantage — represents the novel's central statement on motherhood as a potential site of moral transformation.

## Context in the Novel

Mrs. Garstin's maternal ambition drives the novel's opening situation. She "bestowed upon her all the affection, a harsh, competent, calculating affection, of which she was capable" (008-003), treating her daughters' marriages as instruments of social advancement. This warped maternity creates the conditions for Kitty's disastrous marriage to Walter.

In Mei-tan-fu, Kitty encounters a radically different model of motherhood in the French nuns, who care for orphaned children with practical tenderness. Her own pregnancy — carrying a child whose paternity she cannot honestly confirm — becomes a test of her moral growth. She refuses to claim the child is Walter's to secure her social position, marking her break with her mother's values.

## Thematic Analysis

**Motherhood as Social Engineering**: Mrs. Garstin represents motherhood reduced to strategic calculation. Her "love" for Kitty is entirely conditional on social performance, creating in Kitty a hunger for admiration that fuels her later mistakes.

**Motherhood as Vocation**: The nuns' care for orphans offers an alternative model — motherhood as service rather than possession. They are not biological mothers but function as the novel's most nurturing maternal figures.

**Motherhood as Moral Catalyst**: Kitty's pregnancy forces her to think beyond herself. Her decision about the child's future — to raise her (Kitty hopes for a daughter) to be independent and fearless — represents her liberation from her own mother's legacy.

## Key Passages

> Mrs. Garstin bestowed upon her all the affection, a harsh, competent, calculating affection, of which she was capable; she dreamed ambitious dreams; it was not a good marriage she aimed at for her daughter, but a brilliant one. (008-003)
