## Overview

Streets in [[The Painted Veil]] mark the boundary between private life and public exposure. In [[london|London]], the streets of South Kensington are the stage for the [[the-marriage-market|marriage market]] where [[kitty-fane|Kitty]] is displayed. In [[mei-tan-fu|Mei-tan-fu]], the streets become sites of horror — lined with the dead and dying from [[cholera-epidemic|cholera]] — and of moral reckoning.

The contrast between London streets (sites of social performance) and Mei-tan-fu streets (sites of raw suffering) tracks Kitty's moral education: the same public space that once served vanity now demands compassion.

## London Streets

Kitty's young life is circumscribed by the fashionable streets of London, where she is displayed as a commodity in the marriage market. The street is where she is seen and assessed, where her beauty is her currency.

## Mei-tan-fu Streets

The streets of Mei-tan-fu are a shock to Kitty's sensibilities. [[waddington|Waddington]] guides her through them, and what she sees — beggars, corpses, the raw fact of suffering — begins to penetrate the "painted veil" of her self-absorption:

> The streets were narrow and tortuous, and the houses, with their overhanging upper stories, seemed to shut out the light. (028-015)

The Mei-tan-fu streets are the antithesis of the London drawing rooms Kitty was raised to navigate. They demand not performance but presence — not the display of beauty but the exercise of humanity.

## Key Passages

> The streets were narrow and tortuous, and the houses, with their overhanging upper stories, seemed to shut out the light. (028-015)

The Mei-tan-fu streets are described with a vividness that reflects their impact on Kitty's consciousness. Unlike London, where the street is merely a passage between social engagements, here the street itself is the lesson.

## Significance

The street is the novel's democratic space: in Mei-tan-fu, cholera makes no distinction between rich and poor, and the street displays suffering that cannot be screened by social convention. For Kitty, learning to walk these streets without flinching is part of her moral education — a lesson in looking directly at what she would prefer not to see.
