## Definition

Humiliation in *The Painted Veil* is the wound to self-regard — the experience of being made to feel small, worthless, or contemptible, whether by another person's actions, by social circumstance, or by one's own moral failure. Maugham treats humiliation as a near-universal condition: every major character in the novel endures it, and the narrative's dramatic arc is driven less by what characters do than by how they respond to being humiliated.

## Context in the Novel

Humiliation threads through the novel at multiple social levels. Walter Fane endures the "damned humiliating position" of the cuckold (020-027), his quiet dignity masking an inward devastation. Kitty, after the affair is exposed, is so "wretched that she welcomed the humiliation" of facing Walter (027-001) — as though punishment, even in the form of shame, is preferable to the suspended terror of waiting. Mrs. Garstin, the novel's most relentless social climber, spends her life enduring "humiliations" in pursuit of status, only to die before her ambition is realized (080-021).

The novel's deepest exploration of humiliation, however, occurs in Kitty's sexual relapse in Hong Kong. Having returned from Mei-tan-fu believing herself transformed, she yields to Charles Townsend's embrace and immediately afterward feels "absolutely degraded" (077-025). She stares at her own reflection expecting a visible "change of degradation" (076-013). This is humiliation not inflicted by others but self-inflicted — and therefore the most devastating kind.

## Thematic Analysis

**Humiliation as social structure**: The British colonial society Maugham depicts runs on a hidden economy of humiliation. Mrs. Garstin's social ambitions are driven by the desire to escape the humiliations of middle-class obscurity; Walter's work as a bacteriologist is undervalued by the fashionable set; Kitty is traded in marriage to avoid the humiliation of remaining unmarried while her younger sister is engaged. Humiliation is not an aberration in this world — it is the fuel that powers social ambition.

**The gender asymmetry of humiliation**: The novel draws a sharp contrast between how men and women experience sexual humiliation. Charles Townsend calls Walter's position "damned humiliating" (020-027) as a matter of masculine pride — a problem of how one *looks* to other men. Kitty's humiliation is existential: she feels "degraded" in her own eyes, regardless of what anyone else knows. The gendered double standard ensures that the same act — adultery — humiliates the woman far more deeply than the man.

**Self-humiliation as moral knowledge**: Kitty's most profound moment of humiliation — after her relapse with Townsend — is also her most profound moment of self-knowledge. "You can't possibly despise me as much as I despise myself," she tells him (077-025). The novel suggests that this kind of humiliation, unbearable as it is, is also the necessary precondition for genuine change. Without the burning clarity of self-contempt, Kitty might never have found the resolve to leave Hong Kong and return to England alone.

**The futility of social ambition**: Mrs. Garstin's death encapsulates the novel's bleak verdict on humiliation-driven striving. "After all her efforts, intrigues and humiliations, Mrs. Garstin had died without knowing that her ambition... was at last achieved" (080-021). The humiliation she endured in pursuit of status was, in the end, for nothing.

## Key Passages

> "I expect he was upset. It was naturally a shock. It's a damned humiliating position for any man. He always looks a fool." — Charles Townsend on Walter discovering the affair (020-027)

> She was so wretched that she welcomed the humiliation to which she must expose herself. She stopped and faced him. (027-001)

> She looked at herself with horror. It was the same face. She had expected in it she knew not what change of degradation. (076-013)

> "I feel absolutely degraded. You can't possibly despise me as much as I despise myself." — Kitty to Charles Townsend (077-025)

> The bitter irony of fate! After all her efforts, intrigues and humiliations, Mrs. Garstin had died without knowing that her ambition, however modified by past disappointments, was at last achieved. (080-021)
