## Definition

Self-respect in *The Painted Veil* is the quality Kitty has been denied from birth — and the quality she must earn through suffering. It is not pride (which compares oneself to others) nor vanity (which depends on the gaze of others), but the quiet conviction that one's own existence has value independent of external validation. The novel traces Kitty's movement from a woman whose entire sense of worth is borrowed — from her mother's social ambitions, from men's desire, from colonial status — to a woman who can say, at last, that she knows what she is worth.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty begins the novel without self-respect because she has never been given reason to develop it. Her mother has treated her as a marital asset; her society has valued her only for her beauty; and she has internalised these valuations so completely that she cannot imagine worth on any other terms. When Walter proposes, she accepts not because she respects herself enough to choose wisely but because she does not respect herself enough to refuse.

The affair with Charles Townsend is the novel's great self-respect crisis. Kitty mistakes Townsend's desire for valuation — she believes that being wanted by a charming man means she is worth something. The collapse of this belief, when Townsend abandons her, is the necessary precondition for genuine self-respect: she must learn that being wanted is not the same as being worthy.

Waddington offers the novel's clearest articulation of what is at stake. He tells Kitty of the nuns: "They've given up everything — home, family, security and self-respect" (054-022). The inclusion of self-respect in this list is startling — it suggests that self-respect is something one can voluntarily surrender, and that the nuns' renunciation is not just of material comfort but of the entire apparatus of secular self-worth. What they gain in return — spiritual purpose — is a form of self-respect so transformed that it no longer needs the name.

Kitty's own struggle to regain self-respect is the novel's final movement. "If she could only regain her self-respect at the cost of a bitter conflict, she must not shrink from it" (078-002). This is the novel's hardest lesson: self-respect cannot be given, borrowed, or inherited. It must be fought for, and the fight is always solitary.

## Thematic Analysis

**Self-respect versus social position**: The novel dismantles the equation of self-respect with rank. Mrs. Garstin has social position but no self-respect — her entire identity depends on comparison and display. The Mother Superior has no social position at all — she has surrendered everything — yet she possesses absolute self-possession. Self-respect, the novel insists, is orthogonal to status.

**The cost of self-respect**: Waddington's remark about the nuns giving up self-respect (054-022) points to the novel's most challenging insight: self-respect, in its worldly form, is a burden as much as an asset. To maintain self-respect is to maintain a conception of oneself that must be constantly defended. The nuns' renunciation is also a liberation — they no longer need to worry about what they are worth because they have placed their worth entirely in God.

**Self-respect and the gaze of others**: Kitty's entire life has been lived in the gaze of others — her mother, her suitors, her husband, her lover. She has no self-image independent of how she is seen. Regaining self-respect means learning to see herself without a mirror — to assess her own worth without reference to anyone else's judgment.

**Self-respect as achievement**: Unlike beauty or rank, self-respect cannot be inherited or bestowed. It must be earned. Kitty earns hers through the crucible of Mei-tan-fu: through work at the convent, through witnessing genuine goodness, through losing Walter and discovering that his death has changed her in ways she cannot yet articulate. Self-respect is the novel's hardest-won prize.

> "They've given up everything — home, family, security and self-respect. It's a good many years now since they gave up the desire for those things too." (054-022)

> If she could only regain her self-respect at the cost of a bitter conflict, she must not shrink from it. (078-002)

> But her self-respect obliged her to accept his offer. She could not very well refuse. (023-048)
