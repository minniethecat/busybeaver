# Mother Superior

## Overview

The Mother Superior is the head of the French Catholic convent in Mei-tan-fu and the novel's most authoritative moral voice. A Frenchwoman of aristocratic origin who gave up family and country to serve the poor in China, she embodies a form of spiritual authority entirely free of sanctimony. Her practical wisdom — delivered in brief, unadorned statements — provides the ethical framework against which the novel's other characters are measured. She is, in Kitty's estimation, the person who has most profoundly shaped her understanding of what a life can be.

## Background

The Mother Superior came from a distinguished French family — "her father was a general, and she had been brought up in the utmost refinement" (039-006). She left France decades ago, serving first in Hanoi and later in Mei-tan-fu, where she has built and sustained the convent's mission of caring for orphans and the sick. Her authority derives not from position but from evident goodness — she is respected by the Chinese, the British, and the Catholic community alike.

## Role in the Novel

The Mother Superior enters the novel in its second half, when Kitty begins visiting the convent. She becomes Kitty's most significant teacher — not through instruction but through example. Her most famous teaching, that "it is nothing to do your duty... the only thing that counts is the love of duty; when love and duty are one, then grace is in you" (068-052), crystallizes the novel's moral philosophy.

She also recognizes Walter's worth when others cannot: "the nuns at the convent swear by him" (038-035). Her acknowledgment of his heroism becomes, after his death, the definitive verdict on his character.

## Key Passages

> "Remember that it is nothing to do your duty, that is demanded of you and is no more meritorious than to wash your hands when they are dirty; the only thing that counts is the love of duty; when love and duty are one, then grace is in you and you will enjoy a happiness which passes all understanding." (068-052)

> "I thought it might mean something to you that Walter died a martyr to science and to his duty." (066-036)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — protégée; the young woman whose moral awakening she guides and witnesses
- [[Walter Fane]] — admirer; she recognizes his heroism and mourns his death
- [[Sister St. Joseph]] — colleague; her cheerful subordinate who embodies faith without solemnity
- [[Waddington]] — neighbor; the cynical Englishman who nonetheless respects her deeply

## Significance

The Mother Superior serves as the novel's moral touchstone. In a world of vanity, self-deception, and social calculation, she represents the possibility of a life organized around service and love. She is Maugham's answer to the question the novel poses: what would it mean to live well? Her presence — calm, practical, undemonstrative — suggests that the answer is simpler and harder than we imagine.
