# The Convent

## Overview

The French Catholic convent in Mei-tan-fu is the novel's most important symbolic space. Within its walls, the values that govern the outside world — status, appearance, self-interest — are replaced by service, compassion, and devotion. The convent houses an infirmary crowded with cholera victims, an orphanage filled with abandoned children, and a small community of French nuns whose practical faith transforms everyone who encounters them.

## Description

The convent is described as a compound of modest buildings: a chapel, an infirmary, dormitories for the orphans, quarters for the nuns. It is neither grand nor picturesque — its beauty is entirely in the work done within it. "Within the convent walls the infirmary was crowded with the victims of the epidemic" (041-013).

Kitty's first entry into the convent is rendered as a passage into a world apart, one that seems to exist outside ordinary time and space:

> But once within the convent it had seemed to her that she was transported into another world situated strangely neither in space nor time. Those bare rooms and the white corridors, austere and simple, seemed to possess the spirit of something remote and mystical.
>
> (044-002)

## Symbolic Significance

The convent is the novel's vision of a counter-world — a community organized around love rather than status, service rather than self. It is the place where Kitty learns that a different kind of life is possible.
