## Definition

Cholera in *The Painted Veil* is not merely a disease but a narrative device of radical simplification. The epidemic that ravages the inland Chinese city of Mei-tan-fu creates an environment where social conventions, personal vanity, and romantic illusions cannot survive. Against the backdrop of mass death, the characters' true natures are laid bare — Walter's devotion to scientific duty, Kitty's nascent capacity for selflessness, and the nuns' transcendent faith.

## Context in the Novel

When Walter Fane discovers Kitty's adultery, he gives her a choice: accompany him to Mei-tan-fu, where he has volunteered to combat a cholera epidemic, or face public divorce proceedings. The city — described as a "cholera-ridden place" (022-019) — represents both punishment and possibility.

The epidemic is devastating: it has already killed a missionary (022-020) and continues to claim thousands of Chinese lives (038-037). Walter, as a bacteriologist, works tirelessly to contain the outbreak. The French convent in Mei-tan-fu serves as both infirmary and orphanage, its nuns risking their lives daily.

Kitty's initial terror of the cholera — "I should be frightened out of my senses" (022-050) — gradually transforms into something approaching acceptance, and ultimately into purposeful action as she volunteers at the convent.

> "A cholera epidemic. I don't pretend to be very brave. I don't want to die." — Kitty's initial reaction to learning of the epidemic (022-052)

## Thematic Analysis

**Cholera as moral solvent**: The epidemic dissolves the social pretense that enabled Kitty's affair and Townsend's hypocrisy. In Mei-tan-fu, charm, status, and appearance are meaningless. Only action, duty, and compassion retain value.

**Cholera as equality**: The disease does not discriminate — it kills missionary and Chinese peasant alike. This democratic destructiveness undermines the colonial hierarchies that structured Hong Kong society and mirrors the novel's critique of imperialism.

**Cholera as spiritual catalyst**: As Kitty observes the nuns' selfless nursing of cholera victims, and as she herself begins to work among the sick, she experiences the first genuine moral awakening of her life. The epidemic that she feared becomes the vehicle of her redemption.

## Key Passages

> "But that's the place where the cholera is. They've got the worst epidemic that they've had there for fifty years." — Waddington describes Mei-tan-fu to Kitty (026-005)

> "To go into the midst of a cholera epidemic will be a unique experience. I wouldn't miss it for anything." — Waddington's characteristic detachment in the face of mortality (026-060)

> "He busied himself with the municipal officials in Mei-tan-fu and at the cholera which decimated the city. He worked late into the night." — the narrator on Walter's devotion to his scientific mission in Mei-tan-fu (037-003)

> "A hundred thousand Chinese die of cholera; he isn't here either in the long run." — the narrator's stark assessment of individual insignificance against the scale of the epidemic, echoing the novel's existential undertones (038-037)

> "Now, with the cholera, they are more than ever anxious to do something for the children." — the nuns' practical response to the epidemic's orphans (042-011)
