# Waddington

## Overview

Waddington is the Deputy Commissioner stationed in Mei-tan-fu and one of the novel's most distinctive minor characters. Cynical, witty, and unexpectedly kind, he serves as a bridge between the British colonial world and the Chinese setting, living with a Manchu woman and speaking fluent Chinese. His perceptive commentary on the other characters — particularly Townsend ("He's made a science of popularity") and Walter — provides the novel's sharpest psychological insights.

## Background

Waddington has been stationed in Mei-tan-fu long enough to acquire a deep familiarity with Chinese culture and a sardonic detachment from British colonial society. He lives with a Manchu woman in an arrangement that scandalizes the British community but which he treats with unapologetic good humor. His long service in a remote post has given him perspective that the Hong Kong characters lack.

## Role in the Novel

Waddington is Kitty's first friend in Mei-tan-fu and her most honest interlocutor. He sees through Townsend, respects Walter, and treats Kitty with a combination of amusement and genuine concern. His observation that Walter's colleagues "think he's a hero" (038-037) is one of the novel's key reassessments of Walter's character. He also provides the novel's most direct critique of British colonial attitudes toward the Chinese.

## Key Passages

> "He's made a science of popularity. He has the gift of making everyone he meets feel that he is the one person in the world he wants to see." (035-004)

> "After all this isn't his job, is it? He's a bacteriologist. There was no call for him to come here." (038-037)

## Relationships

- [[Kitty Fane]] — friend; he treats her with wry sympathy and helps her navigate Mei-tan-fu
- [[Walter Fane]] — colleague; he respects Walter's competence without romanticizing his character
- [[Charles Townsend]] — acquaintance; his assessment of Townsend is devastatingly accurate
- [[Mother Superior]] — neighbor; he respects her deeply despite his own lack of religious faith

## Significance

Waddington functions as the novel's chorus — the character who sees clearly and speaks honestly. His cynicism is not corrosive but clarifying; it strips away pretense without descending into bitterness. In a novel about self-deception, Waddington's clear sight is a quiet virtue.
