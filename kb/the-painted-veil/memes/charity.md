## Definition

Charity in *The Painted Veil* refers specifically to the Christian virtue of *caritas* — selfless love expressed through service to the suffering — as practiced by the French nuns at the convent in Mei-tan-fu. Unlike pity or compassion (which are emotional states), charity in the novel is an institutionalized way of life: the daily tending of cholera victims, the care of orphaned children, and the quiet acceptance of personal risk and sacrifice. The Mother Superior embodies "authority tempered by Christian charity" (041-014), a combination that impresses Kitty precisely because it has no counterpart in the British colonial society she knows.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty encounters charity as a lived reality when she begins working at the convent. The Mother Superior, described as possessing a "deep sense of charity" (060-003), becomes the novel's primary exemplar of the virtue. The nuns' charity operates without regard to nationality, religion, or personal safety — they care for Chinese orphans and cholera patients alike, expecting nothing in return. This stands in stark contrast to the transactional relationships that dominate Kitty's previous life: marriage as social advancement, love as romantic gratification. The nuns' charity is simultaneously inspiring and alienating to Kitty; she cannot fully share their faith, but she recognizes the moral grandeur of their commitment.

## Thematic Analysis

Maugham uses charity to pose the novel's deepest ethical question: can meaningful moral action exist without religious faith? The nuns' charity flows from their Catholic devotion; Kitty, who lacks faith, can imitate their actions but cannot access their motivation. Yet the novel suggests that the practice of charity — the work itself — has transformative power regardless of belief. Kitty's service at the convent changes her, even if she never converts. The novel also juxtaposes Christian charity against the "charity" of social politeness: the Mother Superior remarks that it is "a charity that you were doing me" to listen (048-018), inverting the expected relationship between giver and receiver.

## Key Passages

> You felt in her an authority tempered by Christian charity; you felt in her the habit of command, but you felt also that she had dedicated herself to the service of others. (041-014)

> The Mother Superior, with her deep sense of charity, should be content to leave Kitty to find her own way. (060-003)

> I know that what I say and it would be a charity that you were doing me if you would listen to me. (048-018)

> She seemed to look upon the world with a serenity which saw in everything compassion and charity, the Way and the Wayfarer, and all the striving and the pain. (080-061)
