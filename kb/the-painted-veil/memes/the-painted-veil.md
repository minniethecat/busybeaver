# The Painted Veil

## Publication

*The Painted Veil* is a novel by [[william-somerset-maugham|W. Somerset Maugham]], first serialised in *The Cosmopolitan* and *Nash's Magazine* in 1924 and published in book form in 1925 (P03-014). The title is drawn from a sonnet by Percy Bysshe Shelley: "Lift not the painted veil which those who live / Call Life" (P02-001).

The original characters were named Lane; Maugham changed the surname to Fane after residents of that name in Hong Kong brought a legal action (P03-014). The setting was subsequently changed from Hong Kong to the fictional colony of Tching-Yen after the Assistant Colonial Secretary threatened proceedings (P03-014). A small number of copies with the original Hong Kong setting were recalled and have become bibliophile collector's items.

## Origin

Maugham traced the novel's origin to his student travels in Italy. Reading Dante's *Purgatorio* in Florence, he encountered the story of Pia de' Tolomei — a Sienese noblewoman whose husband, suspecting adultery, imprisoned her in his castle in the malarial Maremma until she died (P03-001). Maugham writes that this is the only novel he wrote starting from a story rather than from a character: "the characters were chosen to fit the story I gradually evolved; they were constructed from persons I had long known in different circumstances" (P03-013).

## Plot Summary

The novel follows [[kitty-fane|Kitty Fane]], a beautiful but shallow young woman who, pressured by her ambitious mother [[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]] and alarmed by her younger sister's engagement, hastily marries [[walter-fane|Walter Fane]], a bacteriologist stationed in [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]]. In Hong Kong, Kitty begins an affair with the charming [[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]], a married Assistant Colonial Secretary. When Walter discovers the affair, he delivers an ultimatum: either Townsend divorces his wife and marries Kitty within a week, or Kitty must accompany Walter into the heart of a [[cholera|cholera epidemic]] in the remote inland town of [[mei-tan-fu|Mei-tan-fu]].

Townsend refuses to leave his wife, and Kitty accompanies Walter to Mei-tan-fu. There, through her work with French nuns at [[the-convent-org|the convent]], her encounter with [[waddington|Waddington]] (a cynical British official), and her growing respect for her husband's selfless [[walters-cholera-work|cholera work]], Kitty undergoes a profound moral transformation. Walter dies of cholera, and Kitty returns to Hong Kong, only to relapse with Townsend before finally fleeing to England. The novel ends with Kitty, pregnant and newly reconciled with [[mr-garstin|her father]], resolving to raise her child free from the social pressures that deformed her own life.

## Major Themes

- [[appearance-and-reality|Appearance and Reality]]: the "painted veil" of social convention and self-deception
- [[love|Love]] and [[desire|Desire]]: the distinction between passion and genuine love
- [[duty|Duty]] and [[self-sacrifice|Self-Sacrifice]]: Walter's work and the nuns' service
- [[freedom|Freedom]] and [[transformation|Transformation]]: Kitty's moral awakening
- [[shame|Shame]] and [[forgiveness|Forgiveness]]: the architecture of guilt and redemption
- [[marriage|Marriage]] as a social institution: critique of Edwardian marriage conventions

## Principal Characters

- [[kitty-fane|Kitty Fane]] (née Garstin): the protagonist
- [[walter-fane|Walter Fane]]: her husband, a bacteriologist
- [[charles-townsend|Charles Townsend]]: Assistant Colonial Secretary, Kitty's lover
- [[dorothy-townsend|Dorothy Townsend]]: his wife
- [[waddington|Waddington]]: a cynical British official in Mei-tan-fu
- [[mother-superior|The Mother Superior]]: head of the French convent
- [[mrs-garstin|Mrs. Garstin]]: Kitty's ambitious mother
- [[mr-garstin|Mr. Garstin]]: Kitty's long-suffering father

## Setting

The novel moves between three locations: [[london|London]] (the Edwardian marriage market), [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] (colonial society), and [[mei-tan-fu|Mei-tan-fu]] (a remote Chinese city ravaged by cholera). Each setting represents a different stage in Kitty's moral journey.

## Literary Significance

*The Painted Veil* occupies a distinctive place in Maugham's canon. Written at the height of his powers in the mid-1920s, it combines the narrative economy of his best short fiction with the moral seriousness of his major novels. Maugham remarked that this was the only novel he "started from a story rather than from a character" (P03-013). The novel has been adapted to film three times: in 1934 (with Greta Garbo), in 1957 (*The Seventh Sin*), and in 2006 (with Naomi Watts and Edward Norton).
