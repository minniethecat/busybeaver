# Marriage

## Definition

In *The Painted Veil*, marriage functions as both a social institution and a narrative engine. It is the structure through which Maugham examines the collision between personal desire and social expectation, particularly for women in Edwardian-era British society. Marriage in the novel is rarely about love; it is about survival, status, and the relentless pressure of convention.

## Context in the Novel

Kitty Fane's marriage to Walter Fane is the central axis of the plot. Driven by her mother's ambition and her own fear of being outdone by her younger sister Doris, Kitty accepts Walter's proposal as an escape from the humiliation of remaining single at twenty-five (011-032). She comes to Hong Kong as a bride but quickly discovers that her social standing is determined by her husband's unglamorous profession as Government bacteriologist — a fact that fuels her resentment and eventual affair with Charles Townsend (004-001).

Mrs. Garstin's calculating approach to her daughters' marriages sets the novel's opening context: "it was not a good marriage she aimed at for her daughter, but a brilliant one" (008-003). This maternal ambition, combined with Kitty's failure to secure a brilliant match, creates the conditions for her disastrous union with Walter.

## Thematic Analysis

Marriage in *The Painted Veil* serves as a lens through which Maugham explores several interconnected themes:

**Social Ambition and Class**: The Garstin family's social position — a barrister who will never become a High Court judge — drives Mrs. Garstin to treat her daughters' marriages as instruments of social advancement (008-002). Marriage is explicitly transactional.

**Duty Versus Love**: Walter marries Kitty knowing she does not love him; Kitty marries Walter to avoid social shame. The novel repeatedly contrasts the institution of marriage with genuine emotional connection. "Within three months of her marriage she knew that she had made a mistake; but it had been her mother's fault even more than hers" (006-001).

**Female Agency and Constraint**: The novel portrays marriage as one of the few paths available to women of Kitty's class. Her terror at the prospect of remaining single while her younger sister marries — "it would put her on the shelf" (011-032) — reveals the narrowness of women's options.

**Regret and Self-Knowledge**: Kitty's admission that "not a day had passed since their marriage without her regretting it" (017-008) marks the beginning of her journey toward self-awareness — a journey that ultimately transcends the institution that trapped her.

## Key Passages

> Kitty, coming to Hong Kong on her marriage, had found it hard to reconcile herself to the fact that her social position was determined by her husband's occupation. (004-001)

> Within three months of her marriage she knew that she had made a mistake; but it had been her mother's fault even more than hers. (006-001)

> Mrs. Garstin bestowed upon her all the affection, a harsh, competent, calculating affection, of which she was capable; she dreamed ambitious dreams; it was not a good marriage she aimed at for her daughter, but a brilliant one. (008-003)

> It wouldn't be a very good marriage for her, but it was a marriage, and the fact that she would live in China made it easier. (011-032)
