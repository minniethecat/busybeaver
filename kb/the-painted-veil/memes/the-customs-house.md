# The Customs House

## Overview

The customs house at the Mei-tan-fu river landing is Kitty's point of entry into the world that will reshape her. Waddington is waiting at the landing-stage to receive her — the first familiar face in an unfamiliar world (040-001). It is here that she first steps ashore, already afraid, already resentful, not yet knowing that this place — which she enters as punishment — will become the site of her liberation. When she and Walter step ashore they stand for a moment at the landing-place uncertainly, as though the ground itself were unfamiliar (048-001).

## Description

The customs house is a modest official building at the river landing, staffed by Chinese officials and the occasional British officer. It is the administrative threshold between the journey and the destination — the place where arrivals are processed and the reality of Mei-tan-fu first asserts itself.

Kitty's arrival at the landing-stage is rendered as a passage into an alien world:

> They crossed the river in a sampan. A chair was waiting for Kitty at the landing-stage and she was carried up the hill to the water-gate. It was through this that the coolies came to fetch water from the river and they hurried to and fro with huge buckets hanging from the yoke on their shoulder, splashing the causeway so that it was as wet as though it had heavily rained.
>
> (040-001)

## Symbolic Significance

The customs house is the novel's threshold — the point of passage between one world and another. Kitty arrives as a shallow, frightened adulteress; she will leave as someone who has confronted death and discovered her own capacity for moral seriousness.
