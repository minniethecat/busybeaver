# The Laboratory

## Overview

Walter Fane's laboratory in Mei-tan-fu is where he does the work that defines his professional identity and, ultimately, costs him his life. It is the space in which he is most fully himself — competent, focused, purposeful — and the space to which he retreats from the emotional wreckage of his marriage. The laboratory represents the world of science that Kitty cannot enter and that the novel presents as Walter's truest domain.

## Description

The laboratory is described only briefly, through Waddington's observations: a workspace equipped for bacteriological research, with microscopes, slides, and culture plates. It is a space of order and method — the antithesis of the emotional chaos that governs the Fanes' personal lives.

## Events at This Location

- Walter's work culturing and studying the cholera bacillus (035-038)
- His infection, presumably contracted during laboratory work (078-080)

## Associated Characters

- [[Walter Fane]] — the laboratory is his natural element

## Symbolic Significance

The laboratory represents the rational, empirical domain in which Walter excels — and the emotional domain from which he is exiled. It is both his redemption (his work saves lives) and his doom (his work kills him).
