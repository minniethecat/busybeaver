## Definition

Grief in *The Painted Veil* is the emotional response to irrecoverable loss — principally death, but also the loss of love, the loss of self-respect, and the loss of the life one thought one was living. Maugham treats grief not as a single emotion but as a psychological landscape in which different characters find themselves at different moments, each experiencing it — or failing to experience it — in ways that reveal their deepest natures.

## Context in the Novel

Grief appears at three crucial junctures in the narrative. The first is the death of Walter Fane from cholera in Mei-tan-fu, where Kitty finds herself unable to weep and is ashamed of her own emotional numbness (070-001). The second is her delayed, ambivalent grieving during the journey away from Mei-tan-fu, where sorrow for Walter shifts into pity for herself (075-060). The third is the death of Mrs. Garstin, where Kitty observes her sister Doris's extravagant, performative grief and feels herself strangely detached — having been through too much to feign a distress she did not feel (079-024).

Each of these episodes investigates a different angle on grief: the shame of not grieving properly, the self-pity that masquerades as sorrow, and the social performance of mourning that bears little relation to interior feeling.

> She could not speak any more. She wept without restraint. (025-049)

## Thematic Analysis

**Grief and shame intertwined**: Kitty's most profound encounter with grief is entangled with shame. After Walter's death, she is "ashamed" that she cannot weep, even as the Chinese officer Colonel Yü's eyes are wet with tears (070-001). Her inability to perform grief correctly becomes a source of self-reproach, as though her emotional failure confirms her moral worthlessness.

**Performative grief versus genuine sorrow**: Maugham draws a sharp distinction between authentic and performed grief. Doris Garstin's "extravagant grief" at their mother's death is suspect — Kitty wonders whether her sister "really felt" it, knowing that Doris had always been emotional and that their mother had neglected her (079-024). The novel suggests that the intensity of grief's display may be inversely proportional to its sincerity.

**Grief as self-pity**: In one of the novel's most psychologically acute passages, Kitty's sorrow for Walter "shifted into pity for herself" (075-060). Maugham refuses to sentimentalise grief; he shows how easily it curdles into self-absorption, how mourning for another can become mourning for oneself.

**Transmuted grief**: The grief that Kitty cannot express directly finds other outlets. Her tears finally come — not for Walter, but in a flood of shame after her relapse with Charles Townsend in Hong Kong. She weeps "bitterly," crying "Shame, shame!" (076-015). The grief that had been frozen since Walter's death finally breaks through, but it is grief for herself, for what she has done and what she has become.

## Key Passages

> At first because she had not wept when Walter died she was ashamed. It seemed dreadfully callous. Why, the eyes of the Chinese officer, Colonel Yü, had been wet with tears. She was dazed by her husband's death. (070-001)

> "Walter died of a broken heart," she said. (066-038)

> It seemed as though her bones were melting, and the sorrow she felt for Walter shifted into pity for herself. (075-060)

> Then, letting her face fall on her arms, she wept bitterly. Shame, shame! She did not know what had come over her. It was horrible. She hated him and she hated herself. (076-015)

> Bursting into tears, she flung herself into Kitty's arms. Kitty kissed her. She knew how her mother had neglected Doris in favour of her and how harsh she had been with her because she was plain and dull. She wondered whether Doris really felt the extravagant grief she showed. (079-024)
