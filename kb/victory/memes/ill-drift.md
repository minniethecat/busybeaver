## Context

After his father's death in London, the young Axel Heyst faced a choice: how to live in a world his father had taught him to despise. His answer, formulated as a deliberate existential strategy, was to refuse engagement entirely. The word "drift" becomes his motto, repeated throughout the novel as both self-description and self-justification — until Lena's suffering at Schomberg's hotel forces him to act, and his philosophy of non-engagement collapses.

## The Quote

> "I'll drift," Heyst had said to himself deliberately.
>
> He did not mean intellectually or sentimentally or morally. He meant to drift altogether and literally, body and soul, like a detached leaf drifting in the wind-currents under the immovable trees of a forest glade; to drift without ever catching on to anything.
>
> "This shall be my defence against life," he had said to himself with a sort of inward consciousness that for the son of his father there was no other worthy alternative.
>
> (010-004)

## Significance

The passage is the novel's most concentrated expression of Heyst's governing philosophy. The repetition of "deliberately" is crucial: Heyst's drift is not passive but willed — a conscious refusal shaped by his father's teaching. The image of the "detached leaf" suggests both freedom and helplessness; a leaf may drift but cannot steer. The final phrase — "this shall be my defence against life" — reveals the fear beneath the philosophy: Heyst drifts not from courage but from the conviction that any engagement with life courts disaster. The novel's tragic arc proves him right and wrong simultaneously: his engagement with Lena does lead to disaster, but it also gives him the only genuine experience of love and meaning he ever knows.
