## Overview

Morrison's death is one of *Victory*'s crucial offstage events. Having been rescued from financial ruin by Heyst, Morrison became his partner in the Tropical Belt Coal Company. After the company's failure, Morrison died — probably in England — leaving Heyst as the sole survivor of their shared venture. The death is never dramatised directly; it arrives as news, but its shadow falls across the entire novel. Morrison's absence makes Heyst vulnerable to the slander that he somehow caused or profited from his partner's death — a lie that Schomberg and later Mr. Jones exploit.

## Background

Morrison was a trader and shipowner operating in the Eastern Archipelago whose brig, the *Capricorn*, was impounded by Portuguese authorities in Delli. Facing financial ruin, he was rescued by Heyst, who lent him the money to redeem his vessel. This act of generosity — Heyst's first significant departure from his philosophy of detachment — created a bond of gratitude that led Morrison to propose the Tropical Belt Coal Company, with Heyst as his partner.

## The Death

Heyst learned of Morrison's death while in the Moluccas, through the Tesman brothers: "Heyst was immensely shocked. He got the news in the Moluccas through the Tesmans, and then disappeared for a time" (003-003). He stayed with a Dutch government doctor in Amboyna, who "looked after him for a bit" (003-003). When Heyst eventually reappeared in the world, there "still rested the shadow of Morrison's death" upon his eyes (004-026).

## Consequences

Morrison's death transforms Heyst's situation in several ways. It leaves him as the sole surviving partner of the failed coal company, making him the natural target for any suspicion about what happened to its assets. The absence of Morrison — "who was dead" and therefore "innocence itself" — means there is no one to defend Heyst's account of their partnership (019-057). When Heyst is accused of having "done a deadly wrong" to Morrison, his anguished response — "I! I! do a deadly wrong to my poor Morrison!" — reveals how deeply the death and its implied accusation have wounded him (019-109). Morrison's ghost becomes one of the weapons that Mr. Jones wields against Heyst in their final confrontation.
