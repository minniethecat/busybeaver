# Tropical Belt Coal Company

## Description

The Tropical Belt Coal Company (T.B.C. Company) is the commercial enterprise that established the No. 1 coaling-station on Samburan. Capitalized in London and Amsterdam and styled with the optimism of late-imperial commerce, the company built the infrastructure — the jetty, the barracks, the bungalow — that Heyst later inhabits alone (003-001):

> According to the unnatural mysteries of the financial world, the T.B.C. Company's capital having evaporated in the course of two years, the company went into liquidation — forced, I believe, not voluntary.
>
> (001-005)

The company's failure is typical of Conrad's colonial enterprises: grand in conception, undercapitalized in execution, and ultimately abandoned to the tropical vegetation that slowly reclaims its works.

## Role in the Novel

The T.B.C. Company shapes the novel's entire geography. Without its failed enterprise, there would be no Samburan — no bungalow for Heyst, no jetty for Mr. Jones to land upon, no abandoned infrastructure to serve as the novel's stage. The company is never seen in operation; it exists only as a ruin, a ghost of European ambition that makes the novel's events possible.

Heyst's role as "manager in the tropics" ties him to the company's failure (004-007). He is the last European standing amid the wreckage — a position that mirrors his philosophical stance as the last adherent of his father's discredited doctrine (004-007). The company's collapse and Heyst's persistence form a parallel: both represent European systems that cannot survive contact with tropical reality (003-020).
