## Overview

Wang's departure is one of the decisive turning points of *Victory*'s final act. Having served Heyst since the departure of the Tropical Belt Coal Company's coolies, the silent, efficient Chinaman quietly removes himself from Number One's household at the moment of greatest crisis, taking with him Heyst's revolver and barricading himself with the Alfuros. The departure is not emotional or confrontational — Wang simply says "Goodbye" (029-049) and vanishes — but its consequences are devastating: Heyst is left without ally, without servant, and without the one person on the island who understood its practical workings.

## Wang's Position

Wang was not an ordinary servant. "He had been a servant to white men before" (016-025) and had stayed on Samburan after "the last batch of the mine coolies was leaving" (016-025). He served Heyst with an almost supernatural efficiency — Conrad describes his "miraculous gift of materialising and vanishing, rather than coming and going" (026-021). He had acquired an Alfuro wife and established himself as a smallholder on the island. Crucially, he was the only other inhabitant of Samburan besides Heyst and Lena, and the only one with practical knowledge of the island's resources and geography.

## Motive: Self-Preservation

Wang's decision to leave was characteristically pragmatic. Pedro — "the wild man" — was the catalyst: "It was Pedro who had been the first cause of Wang's suspicion and fear. The Chinaman had seen wild men" (029-030). Calculating the odds as the siege developed, Wang decided the white men's conflict was not his fight: "He had made it up to withdraw himself and the Alfuro woman from the uncertainties of the relations which were going to establish themselves between those white men" (029-030). He took Heyst's revolver — the only firearm in the bungalow — not from malice but from a practical calculation of self-defence.

## The Goodbye

The departure scene is a masterpiece of understatement. Wang presents himself silently before Heyst, who is sitting with an unread book. The Chinaman delivers an inventory of the household goods — "cups, saucers, plates, forks, and knives" (029-024) — all "put away in their usual places" (029-024). This meticulous accounting is his valediction. When Heyst accuses him of taking the revolver, Wang feigns incomprehension: "Me no savee levolvel" (029-039). Then, with theatrical finality:

> Heyst had risen from his chair, but Wang kept his ground in the doorway for a little while longer. His almond-shaped eyes imparted to his face an expression of soft and sentimental melancholy. The muscles of his throat moved visibly while he uttered a distinct and guttural "Goodbye," and vanished from Number One's sight.
>
> (029-049)

## The Barricade

Wang's withdrawal is not passive retreat but active fortification. He disappears into the forest and joins the Alfuros behind a freshly reinforced barricade bristling with spears (033-008). When Heyst seeks him out, hoping to negotiate passage or shelter for Lena, he finds Wang transformed from servant to sentinel. Wang addresses him through the barricade with "horrible Chinese reasonableness" (033-026): he will not let Heyst and Lena pass because "we should be pursued" (033-026). Wang threatens to shoot Heyst with his own revolver "before it grows too dark to take aim" (033-028) if they do not leave. The reversal is complete — the former houseboy is now the gatekeeper, and Heyst is the supplicant.

## Consequences

Wang's desertion has cascading effects. Heyst, stripped of the revolver and his only ally, can no longer mount any credible defence against Jones and Ricardo. The loss of Wang also severs Heyst's connection to the practical operations of the island — cooking, water, the boat — leaving him and Lena in a state of helpless isolation. Symbolically, Wang's departure marks the final dissolution of Heyst's Samburan household: the coolies are long gone, Morrison is dead, the company has failed, and now even the last retainer has judged Number One's cause to be lost. Heyst is left with nothing but Lena — and the moral obligation to protect her that he can no longer fulfil.
