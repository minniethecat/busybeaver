# Schomberg

## Biography

Schomberg is a German hotel-keeper who runs the principal hotel in Surabaya, a gathering place for the colonial trading community of the Malay Archipelago. A former military man, he cultivates an image of masculine authority that masks a fundamentally timid and petty nature. He is married to a woman he treats with contempt and surrounds himself with the gossip and intrigue of colonial expatriate life.

Schomberg maintains an irrational and persistent hatred of Axel Heyst, whom he slanders at every opportunity:

> As a matter of fact, many of us did not hear of this death till months afterward — from Schomberg, who disliked Heyst gratuitously and made up a piece of sinister whispered gossip.
>
> (003-005)

## Role in the Novel

Schomberg functions as the novel's primary catalyst of conflict. His obsessive hatred of Heyst drives him to spread malicious rumors. More critically, his cowardice in the face of Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro — three "desperadoes" who take over his hotel — leads him to redirect their attention toward Samburan and Heyst, hoping to deflect danger from himself.

Schomberg represents the moral mediocrity of colonial society: loud, self-important, and ultimately hollow. His name echoes the German *Schönberg* (beautiful mountain), an ironic contrast with his ugly soul. Conrad uses him to expose the pettiness that lurks beneath the surface of imperial masculinity.

## Key Passages

> The offhand, hard manner and the contemptuous tone of "plain Mr. Jones" disconcerted Schomberg utterly. He had never been spoken to like this in his life. He shook his head in silence and withdrew, not exactly scared — though he was in reality of a timid disposition under his manly exterior — but distinctly mystified and impressed.
>
> (011-039)

> He said these things, not for Mrs. Schomberg's information, but simply thinking aloud, and trying to work his fury up to a point where it would give him courage enough to face "plain Mr. Jones."
>
> (012-007)

## Analysis

Schomberg is Conrad's most fully realized portrait of the colonial philistine. His combination of bluster and cowardice, his sexual jealousy (directed at both Heyst and Ricardo), and his casual cruelty toward his wife all mark him as the novel's moral antipode. Yet Schomberg is also a figure of dark comedy — his pomposity repeatedly undercut by his terror of the genuinely dangerous men he has unwittingly harbored. In the novel's moral economy, Schomberg's mediocrity is in some ways more damning than Mr. Jones's active evil.
