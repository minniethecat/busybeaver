# "An unholy fascination in systematic noise"

## Quotation

> But there is an unholy fascination in systematic noise (008-010). He did not flee from it incontinently, as one might have expected him to do. He remained, astonished at himself for remaining, since nothing could have been more repulsive to his tastes, more painful to his senses, and, so to speak, more contrary to his genius, than this rude exhibition of vigour. The Zangiacomo band was not making music; it was simply murdering silence with a vulgar, ferocious energy.
>
> (008-010)

## Context

Heyst returns to civilization after years on Samburan and finds himself at Schomberg's hotel in Surabaya. The Zangiacomo Ladies' Orchestra — a shabby all-female ensemble conducted by the "horrible" Zangiacomo — performs nightly in the hotel's barn-like concert room. The music is described in terms of physical assault: "screaming, grunting, whining, sobbing, scraping, squeaking." Heyst, a man of refined sensibility, is repulsed — and yet he stays.

## Significance

This passage is one of Conrad's most vivid set-pieces of sensory disgust. The music is not merely bad — it is violent. The verb "murdering" applied to silence elevates noise from an aesthetic failing to a moral violation. The passage anticipates the novel's larger concerns: the way civilization masks brutality beneath social forms, and the way human beings become inured to conditions that ought to be intolerable.

Heyst's self-surprise at remaining is crucial. He acknowledges that he should flee — his entire philosophy dictates withdrawal from unpleasantness. But something keeps him there. That "something" is, ultimately, Lena — one of the orchestra girls whose表演 has caught his attention. The noise that repels him is also the noise that brings him to love.
