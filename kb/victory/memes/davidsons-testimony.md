## Overview

Davidson's testimony to the Excellency forms the novel's closing frame — the moment when the fragmentary, secondhand narrative of *Victory* is assembled into an official account. Summoned because "the mystery of Samburan had caused such a sensation in the Archipelago that even those in the highest spheres were anxious to hear something at first hand" (039-002), Davidson delivers a report that is at once factual and profoundly inadequate to the tragedy it describes. His testimony preserves Heyst's last words, confirms the deaths of Lena and Heyst, and offers the novel's sole public accounting of what happened on the island.

## The Summons

Davidson — a placid, reliable trading captain who had been "keeping an eye on Heyst in a friendly way" (039-004) — was the only person who knew enough to tell the story. He had received Mrs. Schomberg's warning and turned his ship around mid-voyage to steam back to Samburan. "I arrived too late" (039-004) is the refrain of his testimony. His audience with the Excellency is an informal inquest, convened because the affair had become a sensation throughout the archipelago.

## The Account

Davidson explains to the Excellency how Mrs. Schomberg had overheard "two card-sharping rascals making inquiries from her husband as to the exact position of the island" (039-005) and, frightened that Lena might be brought back within reach of Schomberg, had conveyed a warning. Davidson recounts his stormy arrival at Diamond Bay, where "I had to grope my way dead slow" through "one of those silly thunderstorms that hang about the volcano" (039-009). He arrived to find the tragedy already consummated.

## Heyst's Last Words

Davidson preserves for the record the final statement of Axel Heyst — words that serve as the novel's epitaph:

> "Ah, Davidson, woe to the man whose heart has not learned while young to hope, to love — and to put its trust in life!"
>
> (039-013)
>
> (039-013)

The words are a confession and a self-indictment. Heyst, who had been taught by his father to trust nothing, to drift, to remain detached — acknowledges at the end that his disaster was not in engaging with the world but in never having learned how to do so. It is the novel's final judgment on the elder Heyst's philosophy.

## The Report of Death

Davidson's most difficult duty is to confirm the deaths. His statement is characteristically understated: "He is — ashes, your Excellency...he and the girl together. I suppose he couldn't stand his thoughts before her dead body — and fire purifies everything" (039-023). Wang, the Chinaman who had deserted Heyst before the final crisis, helped Davidson "investigate next day, when the embers got cooled a little. We found enough to be sure" (039-023). The bungalow, with its books, its silver candlesticks, and the elder Heyst's portrait, had become a funeral pyre.

## Significance

Davidson's testimony is the novel's final act of narration — the moment when private tragedy becomes public record. But it is also an act of interpretation: Davidson, the "plain man" of good sense and reliable sympathies, translates Heyst's strange, philosophical disaster into terms the colonial bureaucracy can receive. The Excellency hears what he needs to close the file. The reader, who has been privy to far more than Davidson can report, understands how much the official account must leave out. The gap between Davidson's placid summary and the experience the novel has conveyed is Conrad's final comment on the limits of testimony itself.
