# Heyst was not a hermit by temperament

## Quotation

> "But apparently Heyst was not a hermit by temperament. The sight of his kind was not invincibly odious to him. We must believe this, since for some reason or other he did come out from his retreat for a while."
>
> (004-025)

## Context

The narrator offers this reflection after describing Heyst's prolonged isolation on Samburan. Having spent over a year and a half alone on the island, Heyst unexpectedly reappears in the world of men — checking for mail at the Tesmans, drifting through ports, and eventually encountering Lena. The narrator frames this reappearance not as a failure of will but as evidence that Heyst's hermitage was never a genuine vocation: 'He had not the hermit's vocation! That was the trouble, it seems.'

## Significance

This passage is the narrator's clearest diagnosis of Heyst's fundamental contradiction. Heyst has adopted his father's philosophy of radical detachment, but he lacks the temperament to live it fully. He is a hermit in theory but not in practice — drawn back to human contact despite himself. The narrator's aphoristic conclusion — 'incompleteness of any sort leads to trouble (004-025)' — is one of the novel's thematic keystones. Heyst's tragedy is not that he is detached, but that his detachment is incomplete: he cares enough to rescue Lena, but not enough to trust her; enough to be hurt by betrayal, but not enough to defend himself.
