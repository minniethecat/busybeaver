# Heyst's Candlesticks

## Description

The candlesticks are silver objects from the study of Heyst's father in London. They are first seen in Heyst's recollection of his last evening with his father, where a five-branched candlestick stands beside the dying philosopher:

> A five-branched candlestick stood on a little table by the side of the easy chair. They had been talking a long time.
>
> (016-008)

When Heyst established the Tropical Belt Coal Company, he had many of his father's belongings shipped to Samburan, including "a lot of small objects, such as candlesticks, inkstands, and statuettes from his father's study" (016-022). These heavy silver pieces, described as "signs of opulence" (026-021), struck visitors as incongruous in the rough jungle bungalow.

In the novel's climax, Heyst seizes a four-branched candlestick to bring light to the dying Lena, tearing down the curtain in his haste — a moment where the object of inheritance becomes an instrument in the catastrophe (038-008).

## Symbolic Meaning

The candlesticks embody the weight of Heyst's paternal inheritance. Heavy, silver, and conspicuously out of place on a derelict tropical island, they represent the philosophy of detachment that Heyst has carried across the world — beautiful, valuable, yet incapable of providing the illumination he truly needs. When Heyst finally reaches for a candlestick in the climactic scene, it is too late: the light it brings only reveals the irreversibility of his failure.
