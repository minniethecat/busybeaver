## The Word That Sticks

In the peculiar economy of Conrad's *Victory*, certain words attach themselves to characters like epithets in Homer. Axel Heyst acquires two: "Hard Facts," from his youthful declaration to Tesman, and "Enchanted," from a moment of unguarded rapture in a Malacca billiard room. "I am enchanted with these islands!" he exclaims, chalking his cue (001-095). The narrator adds: "Perhaps it was some sort of enchantment. There are more spells than your commonplace magicians ever dreamed of" (001-097).

The word catches. "This is the origin of the name 'Enchanted Heyst' which some fellows fastened on our man" (001-101). But what begins as a nickname becomes a diagnosis.

## The Magic Circle

Conrad gives Heyst's enchantment a geographical shape: "Roughly speaking, a circle with a radius of eight hundred miles drawn round a point in North Borneo was in Heyst's case a magic circle" (001-099). Within this circle, Heyst drifts — Manila, Saigon, Delli, the Java Sea. The "enchanted circle" is both sanctuary and prison. Heyst's attempts to break out "were failures. The enchantment must have been an unbreakable one" (001-099).

The metaphor deepens when we learn that Morrison, the trader whom Heyst will fail to save, "was also an 'enchanted' man" (001-126). Morrison's enchantment is different — he is spellbound by the islands' human communities, the "Godforsaken villages up dark creeks and obscure bays" where he trades. But the shared language of enchantment links the two men in a common fate: both are held captive by a world they cannot leave.

## Disenchantment

By the novel's middle, the spell has broken. "He whom we used to refer to as the Enchanted Heyst was suffering from thorough disenchantment. Not with the islands, however... Heyst was disenchanted with life as a whole" (001-786). The failure of the Tropical Belt Coal Company, Morrison's death, the collapse of every venture — these have stripped away the illusion. "He was no longer enchanted, though he was still a captive of the islands" (001-790).

Yet the novel does not leave enchantment simply as a youthful error to be outgrown. Late in the narrative, at the moment of his greatest danger, Lena experiences a different kind of enchantment: "Out of the appeased enchantment of the senses she had found with him, like a sort of bewitched state, his danger brought a sensation of warmth to her breast" (009-870). This is enchantment transformed — no longer an illusion but a source of strength.

## Conclusion

The "enchanted" motif traces the novel's deepest arc: from Heyst's early rapture, through his middle disenchantment, to the final, tragic re-enchantment through love. Conrad's genius is to refuse any simple resolution. Enchantment is both what destroys Heyst and what, briefly, redeems him.
