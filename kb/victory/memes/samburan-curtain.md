# Samburan Curtain

## Description

The curtain that hangs in the doorway of Heyst's bungalow on Samburan is a recurring motif throughout the novel's second half. It is a simple, functional object — a calico or heavy fabric panel fitted across the doorway separating the living quarters from the verandah. Conrad returns to it repeatedly, using it as a theatrical device to frame entrances, exits, and moments of tension:

> In a moment she was at the curtain that hung in the doorway, and raised it with a steady hand.
>
> (024-002)

Lena moves through the curtain noiselessly with bare feet, the fabric falling behind her (024-002). The curtain separates the intimate space where Lena and Heyst exist from the threatening outside world represented by the verandah and, beyond it, the jungle. Its lightness paradoxically underscores the vulnerability of the bungalow's inhabitants — a barrier that offers no real protection (024-056).

The curtain is not limited to the bungalow. When Heyst first encounters the "concert-hall" at Schomberg's hotel in Part I, he "ascended three steps, lifted a calico curtain, and went in" (006-001), establishing the curtain as a threshold motif that follows Heyst from the corrupt world of Sourabaya to his island retreat.

## Symbolic Meaning

The curtain functions as a threshold between safety and danger, concealment and exposure. Throughout Part IV, it becomes the stage upon which the novel's violence is choreographed. The mysterious struggle between Lena and Ricardo takes place behind the curtain (027-002), its fabric muting the sounds of their fatal encounter. When Wang extends his hand toward it (027-001) or when Heyst lets it drop slowly (028-034), each gesture carries narrative weight. The curtain is both witness and veil — it conceals the truth until the moment of revelation.

## The Struggle Behind the Curtain

The curtain's most dramatic function is as the screen for Ricardo's assault on Lena. Conrad renders the scene with deliberate obscurity, the curtain both hiding and heightening the violence:

> The mysterious struggle was going on with confused thuds of bare feet, in a mute wrestling match, no human sound, hiss, groan, murmur, or exclamation coming through the curtain. A chair fell over, not with a crash but lightly, as if just grazed, and a faint metallic ring of the tin bath succeeded. Finally the tense silence, as of two adversaries locked in a deadly grip, was ended by the heavy, dull thump of a soft body flung against the inner partition of planks. It seemed to shake the whole bungalow.
>
> (027-002)

Wang stands paralyzed before the unstirring curtain, his hand extended but unable to reach it (027-002). The Chinaman's frozen posture — "walking backward, his eyes, his very throat, strained with fearful excitement, his extended arm still pointing at the curtain" — makes the fabric itself the focal point of the scene's horror. The curtain does not merely conceal the struggle; it becomes the surface upon which the imagination projects what cannot be seen.

## The Curtain and the Final Tragedy

In the novel's closing pages, the curtain becomes associated with the final tragedy. Lena, having dressed in black after her struggle with Ricardo, lifts the curtain with a sense of premonitory anguish:

> While lifting the curtain, she felt the anguish of her disobedience to her lover, which was soothed by a feeling she had known before — a gentle flood of penetrating sweetness.
>
> (038-001)

And in the novel's devastating final image, Heyst tears down the curtain in a gesture of desperate urgency as he carries the dying Lena:

> Laying her down on the bed, he ran out again, seized a four-branched candlestick on the table, and ran back, tearing down with a furious jerk the curtain that swung stupidly in his way.
>
> (039-022)

The "stupid" curtain, which has witnessed so much violence and concealment, is torn down in a moment when concealment no longer matters — when the truth Heyst has been shielded from is finally, irrevocably revealed.
