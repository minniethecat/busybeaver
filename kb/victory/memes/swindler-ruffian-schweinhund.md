# Vagabond, impostor, swindler, ruffian, schweinhund! (005-098)

## Quotation

> "Vagabond, impostor, swindler, ruffian, schweinhund! (005-098)"
>
> (005-098)

## Context

Schomberg delivers this tirade to Davidson, the ship captain, after Heyst has departed Surabaya with Lena, the woman Schomberg had effectively imprisoned in his hotel. The eruption of abuse is Schomberg's response to losing the girl who was the unwilling centerpiece of his 'ladies' orchestra.' Each epithet in the cascade reveals more about Schomberg than about Heyst: the hotel-keeper's obsessive hatred transforms a man he barely knows into a monster of his own invention.

## Significance

Schomberg's tirade is dangerous not because it is true but because it is vivid. When he later repeats these slanders to Ricardo and Mr. Jones, they become the justification for the trio's fatal expedition to Samburan. The passage demonstrates Conrad's understanding of how narrative drives action: Schomberg tells a story about Heyst — greedy, predatory, hiding stolen treasure — and that story, however false, becomes the engine of the novel's violence. The cascade of epithets is also a study in the psychology of resentment: each term escalates the accusation, ending with the German 'schweinhund' — the ultimate insult in Schomberg's vocabulary — that reveals a man utterly consumed by hatred.
