## Things With Souls

"There is a soul in things," Conrad writes in *Victory*, describing the furniture and books left behind by Heyst's father in London (001-094). This throwaway observation opens a door onto one of the novel's most sustained patterns: objects in *Victory* are never merely objects. They carry identity. They extend character. They speak.

Heyst himself understands this. When his father's London house is to be demolished, he is "surprisingly distressed" — not by the loss of the building, but by the fate of the objects inside. "It seemed as if in his conception of a world not worth touching... these objects familiar to his childhood and his youth and associated with the memory of an old man, were the only realities, something having an absolute existence" (001-094).

## The Portrait and the Books

The father's portrait dominates the bungalow on Samburan. It hangs "lonely in the middle of a wall" (001-196), "a wonderful presence in its heavy frame on the flimsy wall of mats, looking exiled and at home, out of place and masterful" (001-746). Every significant conversation in the bungalow takes place under the father's painted gaze. When Ricardo prowls the room, he gives "a prolonged stare to the portrait of Heyst's father, that severe profile ignoring the vanities of this earth" (009-658). The portrait judges; it watches; it represents the philosophical inheritance that Heyst cannot escape.

The books, too, are extensions of character. Heyst unpacks them from London and lines the walls of the bungalow with them — "the room now lined with the backs of books halfway up on its three sides" (001-196). He reads his father's works obsessively, "shrinking into himself, composing his face as if under the author's eye" (001-746). The books are not knowledge; they are a presence, a continuing conversation with the dead.

## The Weapons

If Heyst's objects represent inherited thought, the objects of the invaders represent pure agency. Mr. Jones's revolver is an extension of his will — elegant, lethal, aristocratic in its economy. Ricardo's knife is something else entirely: intimate, feral, "the very sting of death" as Conrad describes it. Jones theorizes violence; Ricardo lives inside it. Their weapons are their identities made tangible.

The contrast with Heyst is deliberate. Heyst owns no weapon. His objects — the portrait, the books, the heavy silver candlesticks that catch Ricardo's eye (009-658) — are objects of contemplation, not action. This is his tragedy in material form: he is equipped only for reflection in a world that demands response.

## The Curtain

The Samburan curtain — the fabric screen that divides the bungalow's living space — functions as the novel's most charged domestic object. It separates public from private, Heyst's world from Lena's. When Ricardo hides behind it, the curtain becomes a membrane between predator and prey, between the world of masculine violence and the world Lena has tried to create. "Nothing can break in on us here," Heyst tells Lena (012-???). The curtain is the fragile material of that illusion.

## Conclusion

In *Victory*, objects are not metaphors — they are identity extended into the physical world. Heyst's portrait and books embody a philosophical inheritance he cannot escape. Jones's revolver and Ricardo's knife embody modes of violence that Heyst cannot match. Reading the novel's objects is reading its characters from a different angle: not through what they say or do, but through what they keep close.
