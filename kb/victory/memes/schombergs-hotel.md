# Schomberg's Hotel

## Description

Schomberg's Hotel is the principal European-style establishment in Surabaya, serving as a gathering place for the colonial traders, sea captains, and drifters who populate Conrad's Malay Archipelago. The hotel features a billiard-room, a verandah overlooking the street, and a table d'hôte dining room where the novel's expatriate community assembles:

> Whenever three people came together in his hotel, he took good care that Heyst should be with them.
>
> (003-024)

The hotel's atmosphere is one of enforced conviviality masking underlying tensions. Schomberg patrols his domain with theatrical military bearing, his pacing audible from the billiard-room, while his wife — silent and terrified — serves the guests:

> Schomberg's moody pacing of the billiard-room could be heard on the verandah.
>
> (005-111)

## Role in the Novel

Schomberg's Hotel functions as the novel's first major setting — the social hub where Heyst's reputation is destroyed by gossip long before the physical violence begins. It is here that Davidson hears the rumors about Heyst, here that Lena is imprisoned as a virtual captive of the Zangiacomo orchestra (005-029), and here that Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro establish their temporary headquarters.

The hotel represents the "civilized" world that Heyst has fled — a world of petty malice, sexual predation, and moral cowardice. In a bitter irony, it is Schomberg's fear of the three desperadoes occupying his hotel that sets the entire tragic plot in motion: to rid himself of them, he directs their attention to Samburan and Heyst (012-023).
