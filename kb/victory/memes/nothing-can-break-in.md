# "Nothing can break in on us here"

## Quotation

> "Nothing can break in on us here," he went on and as if there had been an appeal or a provocation in her upward glance, he bent down and took her under the arms, raising her straight out of the chair into a sudden and close embrace.
>
> (020-051)

## Context

Heyst and Lena are alone on Samburan. Heyst has just told Lena to "dismiss all thought" of returning to the world. He declares his contentment with isolation — "there are no voices here to remind us (020-049)." When Lena looks up at him with what he perceives as appeal or provocation, he embraces her. Her response — "as light as a feather" — warms his heart. Moments later she exclaims "He's here!" and bolts away: Wang has arrived with news that will end their idyll.

## Significance

The quotation is structured around a devastating dramatic irony. Heyst's confident assertion — "Nothing can break in on us here" — is immediately undercut by the narrative, which has already set in motion the arrival of Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro. Samburan, far from being impenetrable, is about to become the stage for invasion, betrayal, and death.

The embrace that follows Heyst's words is one of the novel's few moments of genuine physical tenderness. But it is poisoned by dramatic irony: the reader knows what Heyst does not. His warmth toward Lena is real, but his philosophy of detachment has left him unable to foresee or prevent the catastrophe that is already on its way.
