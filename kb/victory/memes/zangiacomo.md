# Zangiacomo

## Description

Zangiacomo is the leader and namesake of Zangiacomo's Ladies' Orchestra,
a touring company of eighteen women performers that sets up at
Schomberg's Hotel in Sourabaya (008-001). He is a German who has
adopted an Italian-sounding business name: "He is a German really; only
he dyes his hair and beard black for business. Zangiacomo is his
business name" (005-038). He conducts the orchestra wearing "a white
mess-jacket" with "a great, pendulous dyed beard" (009-024).

As Davidson observes, Zangiacomo represents a class of touring
impresarios "who often were anything but musicians by profession"
(005-030). His enterprise is described by Conrad's narrator as less
respectable even than the sordid commercial ventures that populate the
novel's world (P01-011).

## Physical Appearance

Zangiacomo is described as having:

- Dyed black hair and beard, originally lighter (005-038)
- A "great, pendulous dyed beard" (009-024)
- A hook nose — Mrs. Zangiacomo, after an interval, goes "out to rejoin
  the hook-nosed Zangiacomo somewhere outside" (008-016)
- "An aspect of hangdog concentration" when following his wife's
  lead (009-024)

## Role in the Novel

### The Orchestra Enterprise

The Zangiacomo Ladies' Orchestra is established at Schomberg's Hotel
when Heyst arrives in Sourabaya (008-001). The ensemble is described in
terms of noise rather than music — "The Zangiacomo band was not making
music; it was simply murdering silence with a vulgar, ferocious
energy" (008-010). Zangiacomo himself conducts with "rude exhibition of
vigour" (008-010) rather than artistry.

> The Zangiacomo band was not making music; it was simply murdering silence
> with a vulgar, ferocious energy. One felt as if witnessing a deed of
> violence; and that impression was so strong that it seemed marvellous to
> see the people sitting so quietly on their chairs, drinking so calmly out
> of their glasses, and giving no signs of distress, anger or fear.
>
> (008-010)

### Conflict with Schomberg

Zangiacomo's temper is demonstrated in his physical altercation with
Schomberg. As one guest recounts: "Signor Zangiacomo ran amuck in the
morning, and went for our worthy friend. I tell you, they were rolling
on the floor together on this very verandah, after chasing each other
all over the house, doors slamming, women screaming" (005-106). The
fight began when the girl Lena went missing, and Zangiacomo blamed
Schomberg: "didn't he fly at you, when the girl was missed, because it
was you who insisted that the artists should go about the audience
during the interval?" (005-108).

### Relationship with Mrs. Zangiacomo

Zangiacomo is the subordinate partner in his own enterprise. Mrs.
Zangiacomo is described as "the haughty mate of Zangiacomo" (009-024),
and she is the one who drives the performers with physical menace.
Zangiacomo follows with "an aspect of hangdog concentration" (009-024),
suggesting his wife's dominance in both the business and personal
relationship.

### Heyst and Lena

Zangiacomo's significance in the novel is largely functional — his
enterprise provides the context in which Heyst encounters Lena. When
Heyst absconds with Lena, the Zangiacomos are initially blamed:
Schomberg "would have it, at first, that the Zangiacomos, for some
unfathomable reason, had secreted the girl" (010-012). Lena later
reveals to Heyst that Zangiacomo had conversations with Schomberg about
Morrison: "He talked to the boss — to Zangiacomo, I mean" (019-047).

Zangiacomo and his wife are the immediate agents of Lena's suffering at
Schomberg's hotel — they compel her to mingle with guests and subject
her to physical abuse (pinching, confinement). Their cruelty is the
catalyst for Heyst's compassion and intervention, setting the novel's
central plot in motion.

## Thematic Significance

Zangiacomo embodies the sordid commercialism that Conrad contrasts with
Heyst's idealism and detachment. Where Heyst's father preached
withdrawal from the world's corruptions, Zangiacomo represents the
world as it actually is — exploitative, vulgar, and indifferent to
human dignity. His "dyed" identity (as a German masquerading under an
Italian name) reinforces the theme of deception and false appearances
that runs through the novel, connecting him to the larger pattern of
disguise and misrepresentation embodied by characters like Mr. Jones
and his "secretary" Ricardo.
