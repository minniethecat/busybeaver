## Biography

Mrs. Schomberg is the wife of the hotel proprietor Schomberg, and together they run Schomberg's Hotel in Sourabaya. She is described as having a "sallow head" and a perpetually "unmoved physiognomy" (005-030), suggesting a woman worn down by years of subservience to her domineering husband. Her background is obscure; she appears in the narrative exclusively within the confines of the hotel, where she sits behind a raised counter "with a mirror and rows of bottles at her back" (005-030), a fixture as permanent as the furniture.

She is often seated behind this counter, immobile and silent, but her silence is not vacancy — it is watchfulness. Her immobility is described as giving her "an appearance of listening intently" (005-052), and she observes the comings and goings of the hotel's guests with a quiet, unnerving attention. When she does speak, her voice is described as a "low voice" (005-046), as if she has learned over the years to make herself as small and unobtrusive as possible.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Schomberg serves as a silent witness to the pivotal events that unfold in her husband's establishment. It is she who provides crucial information to Heyst about the young English girl, Lena, who is performing with the Zangiacomo Ladies' Orchestra. When Heyst inquires, Mrs. Schomberg reveals that the girl is "young enough" and English (005-045, 005-046), her low voice carrying the information that will set the entire plot in motion.

Her role is defined in opposition to her husband. Where Schomberg is "a big, manly, bearded creature" with "an ungovernable tongue" (002-055), Mrs. Schomberg is silent, small, and restrained. Her "immobility" (005-058) stands in stark contrast to Schomberg's restless, blustering energy. She never moves her head, and at times she does not even "seem to have heard him" (005-060). This domestic tableau — the tyrannical husband and the silenced wife — forms the backdrop against which Heyst's rescue of Lena takes on its full romantic and ethical dimensions.
