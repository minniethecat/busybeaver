# Martin Ricardo

## Biography

Martin Ricardo is introduced to the reader through [[Mr. Jones]], who registers them both at [[Schomberg's Hotel]]:

> "My name? Oh, plain Mr. Jones -- put that down -- a gentleman at large. And this is Ricardo." The pockmarked man, lying prostrate in another long chair, made a grimace, as if something had tickled the end of his nose, but did not come out of his supineness. "Martin Ricardo, secretary."
>
> (011-076)

His origins remain largely obscure. What is known comes from his own garrulous confession to [[Schomberg]] during an afternoon conversation at the hotel. Ricardo claims to have met Mr. Jones in the West India Docks, where Jones rescued him from a perilous situation. This chance encounter forged a bond of absolute loyalty in Ricardo, who became Jones's devoted follower for over four years, travelling the world as a professional gambler and criminal.

Conrad provides a real-world origin for the character in his Author's Note, recounting a voyage in the Gulf of Mexico where he encountered the man who would become "the physical Ricardo":

> His eyes were green and every cat I see to this day reminds me of the exact contour of his face.
>
> (Author's Note)

On that schooner, Ricardo served as the devoted attendant of a dying "rich man," cording trunks and tending to the invalid with a strange intensity that Conrad found simultaneously sinister and compelling.

## Character and Appearance

### Physical description

Ricardo is consistently described in animalistic, specifically feline, terms. His most striking features are his green eyes, which gleam phosphorescent in dim light, and his pockmarked face. Conrad reinforces this cat imagery throughout the novel:

* **Eyes**: Green, phosphorescent, with "oblique, coyly expectant yellow eyes, like a cat watching the preparation of a saucer of milk" (014-154)
* **Face**: Pockmarked, with a thin mouth that stretches "from ear to ear" in a grin
* **Moustache**: A "stiff little moustache" whose hairs "stirred by themselves in an odd, feline manner" (012-086)
* **Build**: Short and wiry, "short -- too short to get over without a noisy scramble" (027-067)
* **Mannerisms**: Shows his teeth in grins, passes his tongue over his lips "with an uncanny sort of gusto," moves with a cat's silent obliqueness

[[Schomberg]] memorably categorizes the three villains through Ricardo's animal nature:

> The morals of Mr. Ricardo seemed to him to be pretty much the morals of a cat.
>
> (014-154)

### Personality

| Trait | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| Fidelity | Absolute devotion to Mr. Jones, whom he calls "the governor" -- but this loyalty proves fragile when tested by his infatuation with Lena |
| Violence | Carries a knife strapped to his leg; his instinct is always for the "rip up" -- the sudden, lethal knife attack |
| Gambling | Passionate about cards, both as a professional skill and a philosophy of life |
| Vanity | Obsessed with his appearance, keeps a small looking-glass and examines himself in it with "silent absorption" |
| Volubility | Despite his feral nature, Ricardo is remarkably talkative, capable of long, meandering monologues |
| Class consciousness | Holds a quasi-religious reverence for "gentlemen" and distinguishes his service from wage slavery |

## Role in the Novel

Ricardo functions as the operational arm of Mr. Jones's criminal enterprise -- the "faithful retainer class," as Jones dismissively describes him (036-046). While Jones provides the eerie, spectral authority, Ricardo supplies the physical menace, the capacity for sudden violence, and the street-level cunning.

### At Schomberg's Hotel

Ricardo arrives at Schomberg's hotel with Mr. Jones and Pedro, taking up residence as long-term guests who terrorize the establishment. Together, they run a gambling operation in the hotel's concert-hall:

> In the middle, Mr. Jones, a starved spectre turned into a banker, faced Ricardo, a rather nasty, slow-moving cat turned into a croupier.
>
> (013-004)

During a long afternoon conversation, Ricardo reveals his life story to Schomberg, demonstrating his knife (014-024) and his peculiar moral code. His most significant contribution to the plot comes when he extracts from Schomberg the information about Heyst's supposed hidden wealth on [[Samburan]], providing the gang with a new target.

### On Samburan

Once on the island, Ricardo emerges as the most dynamic of the three invaders. He is the observer who studies Heyst's bungalow:

> The observer was Martin Ricardo. To him life was not a matter of passive renunciation, but of a particularly active warfare.
>
> (025-002)

Unable to sleep, Ricardo watches the clearing, impatient for action. His instinct is for the direct approach -- a "rip up" -- but Jones restrains him, preferring psychological manipulation. This tension between Ricardo's predatory impulses and Jones's more calculating approach becomes one of the novel's central dynamics.

### Infatuation with Lena

Ricardo's most consequential action is his assault on, and subsequent infatuation with, [[Lena]]. When he charges at her through the curtain:

> Ravish or kill -- it was all one to him, as long as by the act he liberated the suffering soul of savagery repressed for so long.
>
> (026-060)

Lena defeats him in the ensuing struggle, and in the aftermath, Ricardo undergoes a transformation. He becomes infatuated with her strength, seeing in her an ally and potential partner. This infatuation fundamentally destabilizes the gang, creating a division between Ricardo and Jones that proves fatal.

> An elation in which an extraordinary softness mingled with savage triumph would not allow it. ... He walked up and down the verandah far into the afternoon, eyeing the other bungalow at every turn. Once or twice he stopped dead short and looked down at his left slipper. Each time he chuckled audibly.
>
> (031-062)

Ricardo's fixation on Lena causes him to conceal her existence from Jones, to lie to his governor, and ultimately to contemplate open conflict with the man to whom he has sworn absolute loyalty. Jones, for his part, perceives the shift:

> He did not, could not know that Mr. Jones was quick-minded enough to see already the end of his reign over his excellent secretary's thoughts and feelings; the coming failure of Ricardo's fidelity. A woman had intervened!
>
> (036-188)

### Death

Ricardo's death occurs during the climactic storm scene. Having surrendered his knife to Lena, who has deliberately seduced him to obtain his weapon -- "the very sting of death was in her hands" (037-078) -- Ricardo is defenseless when Jones arrives:

> Suddenly Ricardo felt himself spurned by the foot he had been cherishing -- spurned with a push of such violence into the very hollow of his throat that it swung him back instantly into an upright position on his knees. He read his danger in the stony eyes of the girl; and in the very act of leaping to his feet he heard sharply, detached on the comminatory voice of the storm, the brief report of a shot which half stunned him, in the manner of a blow.
>
> (037-110)

Jones shoots Ricardo, presumably killing him. Ricardo's knife is later found by Davidson, and is referred to as "the spoil of vanquished death." The betrayal is complete: the loyal henchman, who once declared "I follow a gentleman" as his life's creed, is destroyed by the very man he served.

## Philosophy

Ricardo articulates a surprisingly coherent worldview during his conversations with Schomberg. His philosophy blends nihilism, class resentment, and a gambler's fatalism:

> "I follow a gentleman. That ain't the same thing as to serve an employer. They give you wages as they'd fling a bone to a dog, and they expect you to be grateful. It's worse than slavery. ... And if you sell your work -- what is it but selling your own self? You've got so many days to live and you sell them one after another."
>
> (014-128)

On gambling:

> "Gamble? That's nature. What's life itself? You never know what may turn up. The worst of it is that you never can tell exactly what sort of cards you are holding yourself."
>
> (014-144)

This philosophy -- that life is fundamentally a gamble, that wage labor is a form of self-sale, that only freely chosen service to a "gentleman" preserves one's integrity -- makes Ricardo one of Conrad's more intellectually interesting villains. He is not merely violent; he is violent with a rationale, however twisted.

## Analysis

Ricardo occupies a unique position in Conrad's gallery of villains. Unlike the spectral Mr. Jones, who embodies a kind of abstract, almost metaphysical evil, Ricardo is evil made flesh -- physical, visceral, and disturbingly human. His feline qualities invite comparison to the predator in nature, but his volubility and his capacity for infatuation mark him as more than a simple beast.

The Ricardo-Lena dynamic inverts the conventional predator-prey relationship. Ricardo initially sees Lena as prey and attacks her with feral directness. But in defeat, he becomes enthralled, transforming from predator to supplicant. Lena, in turn, uses his infatuation to disarm him -- both literally and figuratively -- extracting his knife and his loyalty. This subversion of gendered and predatory expectations is one of the novel's more sophisticated narrative achievements.

Ricardo's devotion to Mr. Jones is also worth examining. He insists on the distinction between freely chosen service to a gentleman and the degradation of wage labor, yet this philosophy crumbles at the first serious test. His infatuation with Lena proves stronger than four years of loyalty. Conrad seems to suggest that even the most fervently held codes of masculine allegiance are fragile constructions, easily undone by desire.

Finally, Ricardo's death embodies the novel's theme of ironic reversal. The man who lived by the knife is disarmed by a woman; the devoted servant is shot by his master; the predator becomes prey. In this, Ricardo's arc mirrors the broader pattern of *Victory*, in which all certainties dissolve and every character faces the consequences of their chosen mode of living.
