## Context

During an interval in the Zangiacomo concert at Schomberg's hotel, Lena — who has been telling Heyst about her impoverished, wandering childhood — suddenly becomes self-conscious about talking too much. She apologises for chattering. Heyst's response is one of the novel's few moments of unguarded tenderness — a declaration that bypasses his usual philosophical circumspection and speaks directly from the heart.

## The Quote

> She apologised for talking so much, for chattering away like this. Heyst was not looking at her. He had his face propped up on his hand, the other arm folded on his knee.
>
> "Don't let it trouble you," Heyst said. "Your voice is enough. I am in love with it, whatever it says."
>
> (009-066)

## Significance

This is the moment when Heyst's emotional engagement with Lena becomes explicit — both to her and to himself. The framing is characteristic: Heyst is "not looking at her," his face "propped up on his hand" — the posture of a man who cannot quite face the magnitude of what he is saying. The declaration is remarkable for its indirection: he does not say "I love you" but "I am in love with your voice, whatever it says." The qualification is pure Heyst — it makes room for his lifelong scepticism about words and meanings while nevertheless committing him to the person who speaks them. From this moment forward, Heyst's philosophy of detachment is in retreat, though he does not yet know it.
