## Overview

Loneliness in *Victory* is not merely the absence of company but a fundamental existential condition. Every major character is alone in a way that matters: Heyst, the philosophical orphan, cut off from both European society and the native community of Samburan; Lena, the working-class English girl adrift in a world of men who exploit her; Davidson, the reliable captain whose existence is "distinctly monotonous and, in a sense, lonely" (004-036); and even Schomberg, whose bluster conceals a profound isolation.

Conrad suggests that loneliness is the price of consciousness. Heyst's father recognized this and counseled withdrawal (016-014) — if you are alone anyway, why pretend otherwise? But Heyst discovers, through Lena, that the answer to loneliness is not philosophy but love. The tragedy is that this discovery comes too late, and under conditions that make genuine connection impossible.

## Role in the Novel

Loneliness drives the plot. It is loneliness that makes Heyst rescue Morrison in Timor (008-017) — a moment of spontaneous human connection that he will spend the rest of the novel trying to reconcile with his philosophy of detachment. It is loneliness that makes Lena vulnerable to Heyst's rescue, and it is loneliness that makes her cling to him even as danger closes in. Mr. Jones and his gang are themselves lonely figures — outcasts, criminals, men who belong nowhere — and their assault on Heyst's compound is, at some level, an assault on the possibility of human connection itself.
