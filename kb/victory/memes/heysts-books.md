# Heyst's Books

## Description

Heyst's books are a substantial collection of philosophical works written by and belonging to his father, a renowned philosopher of Scandinavian origin. After his father's death, Heyst had them shipped to Samburan:

> He sent instructions to have some of the things sent out to him at Samburan, just as any ordinary, credulous person would have done. They came, torn out from their long repose — a lot of books, some chairs and tables, his father's portrait in oils.
>
> (016-022)
>
> (016-022)

In the Samburan bungalow, the books line the walls, transforming the main room into a kind of private library:

> The room now lined with the backs of books halfway up on its three sides. Above the cases the fine matting met the ceiling of tightly stretched white calico.
>
> (018-004)
>
> (026-001)

The collection serves as Heyst's primary occupation during his years of isolation on the island. He spends long hours "plunged into an abyss of meditation over books" (016-033). Even in moments of crisis, Heyst reaches instinctively for a volume:

> He stirred impatiently in his chair, and raised the book to his eyes with both hands. It was one of his father's. He opened it haphazard, and his eyes fell on the middle of the page.
>
> (020-019)
>
> (026-020)

## Symbolic Meaning

The books represent the intellectual legacy of Heyst's father — a philosophy of universal contempt and detachment that Heyst has absorbed but cannot fully live by. They line the walls of the bungalow like a fortress of ideas, yet they offer no protection against the human entanglements that ultimately destroy him. In the novel's final scenes, the books become "shadowy, unsubstantial, the dumb accomplices of an amazing dream-plot" (038-002) — silent witnesses to the failure of philosophy in the face of lived experience.
