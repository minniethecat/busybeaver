## Biography

Tesman was the head of Tesman Brothers, a "tip-top" trading firm in Sourabaya, Java (001-013). Conrad introduces him as "a kindly, benevolent old gentleman" who received the young Heyst, bearing a letter of introduction, in the early days of the Swede's adventures in the Malay Archipelago (001-013). The firm was well-established, described as a "tip-top house" in Sourabaya, and Tesman himself seems to have been well-regarded in the European merchant community.

Tesman's relationship with Heyst was primarily financial but extended beyond mere business. It was through Tesman that Heyst acquired his ironic nickname "Hard Facts," after Heyst's philosophical pronouncements to the old merchant were repeated in the community: "Facts alone, Mr. Tesman" (001-015). The narrator notes sardonically that it is unknown whether "old Tesman agreed with him or not, but he must have spoken about it" (001-016). After this encounter, Heyst "mooned about the Java Sea in some of the Tesmans' trading schooners" before vanishing "on board an Arab ship, in the direction of New Guinea" (001-016), suggesting that Tesman provided not just capital but practical opportunities.

## Role in the Novel

The Tesman firm serves as Heyst's financial lifeline and connection to the civilized world of commerce. Before departing on his final cruise — the one that would lead him to Samburan and the tragic events of the novel — Heyst "left all his spare cash with the Tesmans, in Samarang, to meet certain bills which would fall due while he was away" (002-029). This act of trust underscores the long-standing relationship between the two.

The Tesmans also serve as unwitting heralds of Heyst's downfall. When they "appointed agents" and secured "a contract for government mail-boats," ushering in "the era of steam beginning for the islands," the narrator calls it "a great stride forward — Heyst's stride!" (003-001). Yet the very modernization the Tesmans represented would contribute to the collapse of the Tropical Belt Coal Company, Heyst's great venture. When Heyst learns of Morrison's death "in the Moluccas through the Tesmans" (003-003) and "disappeared for a time," the firm becomes the conduit of fate. Ultimately, "The Tesmans washed their hands of it" (003-020), marking the final severance of Heyst's ties to the commercial world.

The Tesmans' principal countinghouse remained in Sourabaya (004-043), and the narrator notes that "there had long existed a connection between Heyst and the Tesmans" (004-043). This oblique observation suggests that the relationship outlasted both the Tropical Belt Coal Company and, perhaps, Heyst himself.
