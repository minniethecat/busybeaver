# "Look on — make no sound"

## Quotation

> "Look on — make no sound," were the last words of the man who had spent his life in blowing blasts upon a terrible trumpet which had filled heaven and earth with ruins, while mankind went on its way unheeding.
>
> (016-014)

## Context

Heyst recalls his father's dying words during a moment of reflection on Samburan, after Lena's arrival. The elder Heyst was a Swedish philosopher whose pessimistic writings denounced all forms of engagement with the world. The phrase condenses a lifetime of thought into an imperative: observe, do not intervene. The father "had spent his life in blowing blasts upon a terrible trumpet" — writing works of radical pessimism — while humanity ignored him. His final advice to his son was to make no sound at all.

## Significance

This quotation is the philosophical keystone of *Victory*. It encapsulates the doctrine of detachment that Heyst attempts to live by, and which the novel systematically dismantles. The phrase carries a bitter irony: the father, who wrote furiously against engagement, tells his son to remain silent. The son follows this advice — and the silence leads to tragedy.

The opposition between "making a sound" (the father's writing, Lena's cry, the shot that kills Lena) and "silence" (Heyst's withdrawal) structures the novel's moral argument. By the end, Conrad has shown that silence in the face of evil is itself a form of moral failure, and that the only meaningful "victory" belongs to those who act.
