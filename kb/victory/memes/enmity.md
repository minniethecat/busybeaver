## Overview

Enmity — active, personal, obsessive hatred — is the motor that drives *Victory*'s plot from its earliest pages to its catastrophic conclusion. Unlike the abstract philosophical problems that preoccupy Heyst (detachment, the nature of reality, the cost of reflection), enmity is concrete, embodied, and devastatingly effective. Schomberg's hatred of Heyst, born of sexual jealousy and wounded vanity, becomes a force that reaches across the Java Sea to destroy a man who barely knew he was hated. Conrad's treatment of enmity suggests that hatred is more powerful than love — not because it is stronger, but because it acts while love hesitates.

## Schomberg's Obsession

Schomberg's enmity is the novel's most fully realised portrait of hatred. It begins in sexual humiliation — Heyst "snatched" Lena "from under his nose" (010-012) — but quickly expands into an all-consuming obsession. "Schomberg had Heyst on the brain" (010-016); he attributed every business failure, every disappointment, to "some subtly noxious influence of Heyst." The enmity is self-sustaining: Schomberg needs Heyst to be monstrous because his own identity as wronged avenger depends on it. Conrad diagnoses this as "the reality of Heyst as created by his own power of false inferences, of his hate, of his love of scandal" (015-081). The enmity becomes a closed system — impervious to fact, nourished by its own heat.

> Schomberg had Heyst on the brain. Even the unsatisfactory state of his affairs, which had never been so unpromising since he came out East directly after the Franco-Prussian War, he referred to some subtly noxious influence of Heyst.
>
> (010-016)

## Jones's Predatory Antagonism

Mr. Jones represents a different species of enmity: cold, predatory, curiously impersonal. Where Schomberg's hatred is hot and obsessive, Jones's antagonism is the languid cruelty of a man who has "refined everything away" — a dark mirror of Heyst's own detachment. Jones pursues Heyst not from personal grievance but from a kind of bored curiosity, a desire to exercise his power over another human being. His enmity is more frightening than Schomberg's precisely because it is less emotional: Jones destroys not because he hates but because he can.

## The Geography of Hatred

One of the novel's structural ironies is that enmity flourishes across distance while love requires proximity. Schomberg's hatred travels from Surabaya to Samburan in the form of Jones and Ricardo, while Heyst and Lena's love remains trapped on the island, unable to reach out for help. The novel's geography — the "enchanted" islands of the Eastern Archipelago — becomes a map of hostilities, with Samburan at the centre as the point where all the converging hatreds finally collide.
