## Overview

Slander — the deliberate spreading of false and damaging speech — is *Victory*'s central plot mechanism and one of its deepest thematic concerns. Conrad treats slander not merely as a character flaw but as a force with its own terrible efficacy: Schomberg's gossip directs Jones and Ricardo to Samburan; the whispered accusation about Morrison's death haunts Heyst; and the "abominable calumny" (020-019) becomes, in Heyst's own words, a thing that "can even destroy one's faith in oneself — dry-rot the soul" (034-040). The novel demonstrates that slander, unlike physical violence, leaves the perpetrator's hands clean — Schomberg never faces consequences — while destroying its target just as surely as any weapon.

## Schomberg: The Artist of Slander

Schomberg is the novel's virtuoso of malicious speech. His "genius ran to...the inventing, elaborating, and retailing of scandalous gossip with asinine unction and impudent delight" (010-017). After Heyst takes Lena, Schomberg's slander becomes a public performance: "It became a recognised entertainment to go and hear his abuse of Heyst, while sipping iced drinks on the verandah of the hotel" (010-013). But Schomberg's slander is not merely theatrical — it is strategically lethal. He "had only to let loose his natural gift for talking scandalously about his fellow lodger" (015-024) to send Jones and Ricardo against Heyst. Schomberg's particular skill is in making his fictions plausible: he believes in "the reality of Heyst as created by his own power of false inferences, of his hate, of his love of scandal" (015-081).

## The Morrison Calumny

The most damaging slander against Heyst is the accusation that he somehow caused Morrison's death to enrich himself. This "abominable calumny" (020-019) follows Heyst like a shadow, surfacing in the final confrontation when Jones wields it as a weapon. The accusation has power precisely because it cannot be disproven — Morrison is dead, the company records are in London and Amsterdam, and Heyst's reticence reads as guilt. Conrad's insight is that slander flourishes in the gap between what can be proven and what can be suspected. Heyst reflects bitterly that "the power of calumny grows with time. It's insidious and penetrating. It can even destroy one's faith in oneself — dry-rot the soul" (034-040).

## Slander as Plot Engine

Structurally, slander is the mechanism that connects the novel's two worlds: the colonial society of Surabaya and the isolated refuge of Samburan. Without Schomberg's campaign of denunciation, Jones and Ricardo would never have heard of Heyst or his island. The novel thus makes a dark argument about the relationship between speech and action: words, spoken with sufficient malice and in the right ear, become indistinguishable from deeds. Schomberg never fires a gun, but his words kill Heyst and Lena as surely as if he had.
