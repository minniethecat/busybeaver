# Fire at the Bungalow

## Summary

In the early hours of the morning following Lena's death, Heyst's principal bungalow on Samburan — the site of the novel's climactic tragedy — erupts in flames. Captain Davidson, anchored offshore, is alerted by his crew: "some of my calashes came running to me, yelling that there was a fire ashore" (039-020). He lands to find the main bungalow "blazing," the heat so intense it drives rescuers back (039-020). The fire spreads to the other two bungalows, which "caught one after another like kindling-wood" (039-020). By afternoon, when the embers have cooled, Davidson and Wang investigate the ruins and find enough evidence to confirm that Heyst died in the flames alongside Lena's body. The fire's origin is ambiguous: Heyst, unable to endure "his thoughts before her dead body" (039-023), likely set it himself — a final, purifying act that reduces everything to ashes.

## Context

The fire is the terminal event of the novel's extended climax. It follows the shooting of Lena by Mr. Jones (038-010), Jones's murder of Ricardo (039-018), and Jones's own death at the wharf (039-025). Heyst, having witnessed Lena's death and heard Jones's fatal shot, tells Davidson his father's philosophy had "upset his head when he was young" (039-012) and utters what are practically his last words: "Ah, Davidson, woe to the man whose heart has not learned while young to hope, to love — and to put its trust in life!" (039-013). He then insists Davidson return to his ship, saying he "wanted to be alone with his dead for a time" (039-014).

Earlier in the novel, Wang had already demonstrated a readiness to use fire as a tool. While Heyst was in Sourabaya, Wang "had busied himself with the ground immediately in front of the principal bungalow," clearing it with flame: "Emerging from the fringe of grass growing across the shore end of the coal-jetty, Heyst beheld a broad, clear space, black and level, with only one or two clumps of charred twigs, where the flame had swept from the front of his house to the nearest trees of the forest" (017-002). Heyst asked, "You took the risk of firing the grass?" and Wang simply nodded (017-003). This earlier, controlled burn prefigures the final, all-consuming blaze.

## Key Participants

- **Axel Heyst** — the man who almost certainly sets the fire, choosing immolation over living with the knowledge that his detachment caused Lena's death. The fire is his final, desperate act of agency.
- **Lena** — already dead, her body is consumed in the flames alongside Heyst. Davidson confirms "he and the girl together" were reduced to "ashes" (039-023).
- **Wang** — the Chinese servant who returns the next day to help Davidson investigate the embers. His pragmatic competence contrasts with Heyst's tragic end.
- **Captain Davidson** — the maritime trader whose belated return to Samburan makes him the witness to both Lena's death and the fire's aftermath. He narrates the entire sequence to the unnamed Excellency.
- **Mr. Jones** — already dead at the wharf when the fire begins; his body, unlike Heyst and Lena's, remains intact underwater — preserved as "a heap of bones in a blue silk bag" (039-025) rather than purified by flame.

## Key Passages

Davidson narrates the discovery of the fire to the Excellency:

> "I returned on board as he had insisted I should do. I didn't want to intrude on his grief. Later, about five in the morning, some of my calashes came running to me, yelling that there was a fire ashore. I landed at once, of course. The principal bungalow was blazing. The heat drove us back. The other two houses caught one after another like kindling-wood. There was no going beyond the shore end of the jetty till the afternoon." (039-020)

Davidson's conclusion about Heyst's death is stated with grim certainty:

> "He is — ashes, your Excellency," said Davidson, wheezing a little; "he and the girl together. I suppose he couldn't stand his thoughts before her dead body — and fire purifies everything. That Chinaman of whom I told your Excellency helped me to investigate next day, when the embers got cooled a little. We found enough to be sure." (039-023)

Davidson's final reflection on the fire's meaning closes the matter with characteristic placidity:

> "Let Heaven look after what has been purified. The wind and rain will take care of the ashes." (039-025)

## Consequences

The fire obliterates every physical trace of the tragedy on Samburan. The three bungalows — Heyst's principal dwelling, the empty guest house, and the one occupied by Jones and his gang — are reduced to ash. The conflagration is so total that Davidson cannot even approach the shore end of the jetty until the afternoon (039-020).

Thematically, the fire serves as an act of purification in the novel's moral economy. Davidson's phrase "fire purifies everything" (039-023) is not merely descriptive — it is the novel's closing judgment. Heyst, whose life was defined by withdrawal and refusal, finally acts: he burns himself, Lena, and the scene of her sacrifice in one annihilating gesture. The fire does not redeem him — nothing could — but it erases the evidence of his failure, leaving Samburan to the wind, the rain, and Wang's vegetable garden.

The only survivor who remains on the island is Wang, who "went at once over the hill to fetch his Alfuro woman back to the hut" (039-025). Life on Samburan continues, but on entirely different terms: the colonial enterprise of the Tropical Belt Coal Company is long dead, the European intruders are all dead, and Wang — the "original Adam" of the island — resumes his autonomous existence.

## Analysis

The bungalow fire is the novel's final and most devastating irony. Heyst's father taught him that life is not worth engaging — that detachment is the only rational response to a meaningless world. Heyst spends his adult life practicing this philosophy, drifting through the archipelago as "the Enemy" of conventional society. Yet when confronted with the consequences of his detachment — Lena's dead body, killed by men he allowed onto his island — Heyst cannot sustain the pose. His suicide by fire is the ultimate repudiation of his father's teaching: it is an act of profound, desperate engagement, undertaken in grief so overwhelming that only total annihilation will serve.

The fire also completes Conrad's pattern of symbolic reversal. Earlier, Heyst "envied the Chinaman's obedience to his instincts, the powerful simplicity of purpose which made his existence appear almost automatic" (016-043). By the novel's end, Wang has survived through precisely those instincts — shooting Pedro, casting off the boat, retreating to safety — while Heyst, the man of intellect and refinement, chooses self-immolation. The fire that Wang had used as a practical tool for clearing ground (017-002) becomes, in Heyst's hands, an instrument of absolute negation.

The juxtaposition of fire and water in the novel's final pages is also significant. Jones's body remains preserved underwater, "huddled up on the bottom between two piles" (039-025), while Heyst and Lena are consumed by flame. Water preserves the spectral villain in grotesque suspension; fire purifies the lovers into indistinguishable ash. The elements themselves seem to pass judgment.
