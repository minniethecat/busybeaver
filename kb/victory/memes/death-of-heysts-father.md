## Overview

The death of Heyst's father is the originating trauma of *Victory* — an offstage event that predates the novel's action by fifteen years but whose philosophical shadow falls across every subsequent page. The elder Heyst was "an expatriated Swede who died in London, dissatisfied with his country and angry with all the world, which had instinctively rejected his wisdom" (010-001). His death releases the young Axel Heyst into a life of wandering detachment; his books and portrait remain on Samburan as the tutelary spirits of Heyst's hermitage. Understanding the father's death is essential to understanding the son's life — and death.

## The Elder Heyst

Conrad sketches the father with devastating economy. He was a "Thinker, stylist, and man of the world in his time" who "had begun by coveting all the joys" but spent "more than sixty years" dragging "the most weary, the most uneasy soul that civilisation had ever fashioned to its ends of disillusion and regret" (010-002). His philosophy was one of radical negation — a "contemptuous, inflexible negation of all effort" (016-005) — which he bequeathed to his son during three years of intense companionship in the boy's formative adolescence. He published books with titles like *Storm and Dust*, filled with "broken text of reflections, maxims, short phrases, enigmatical sometimes and sometimes eloquent" (020-021).

## The Last Evening

The novel preserves a rare, intimate memory of the father's final hours. "He remembered always his last evening with his father. He remembered the thin features, the great mass of white hair, and the ivory complexion. A five-branched candlestick stood on a little table by the side of the easy chair" (016-008). The father, knowing he was dying, gave his son no comfort — only the pure, cold doctrine of negation. The younger Heyst was left with "a profound mistrust of life" (010-003), having learned that "reflection...is a destructive process, a reckoning of the cost" (010-003).

## Heyst's Grief

Heyst's response to his father's death is characteristically restrained but deeply felt. Alone in the bungalow on Samburan years later, contemplating his father's portrait, he found himself moved: "He became aware of his eyes being wet. It was not that the man was his father. For him it was purely a matter of hearsay which could not in itself cause this emotion. No! It was because he had looked at him so long that he missed him so much. The dead man had kept him on the bank by his side. And now Heyst felt acutely that he was alone on the bank" (016-019). The image is crucial: the father's philosophy was meant to keep Heyst safely on the riverbank of life, observing but never swimming. The father's death made that detachment possible — and, paradoxically, unbearable.

## Consequences

"Directly his father died he lit out into the wide world on his own, and had been on the move till he fetched up against this famous coal business" (004-037). The father's death sends Heyst into fifteen years of wandering — "invariably courteous and unapproachable" (010-001), "a waif and stray, austerely, from conviction" (010-007). The philosophy of "drift" that Heyst adopts is his father's legacy made literal: "'I'll drift,' Heyst had said to himself deliberately. He did not mean intellectually or sentimentally or morally. He meant to drift altogether and literally, body and soul, like a detached leaf" (010-005).

> The young man learned to reflect, which is a destructive process, a
> reckoning of the cost. It is not the clear-sighted who lead the world.
> Great achievements are accomplished in a blessed, warm mental fog, which
> the pitiless cold blasts of the father's analysis had blown away from
> the son.
>
> (010-003) But drift leads him, finally, to Lena — and to the fatal engagement with the world that his father's death had been meant to prevent. The portrait of the elder Heyst, hanging in the bungalow throughout the siege, watches his son's tragedy unfold — the silent, severe profile "ignoring the vanities of this earth" (026-021), even as those vanities claim his son's life.
