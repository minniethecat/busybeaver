## Overview

Schomberg's denunciation of Axel Heyst is one of *Victory*'s central engines of plot. Stung by sexual jealousy after Heyst "snatched" Lena from under his nose at Schomberg's own hotel, the hotel-keeper transforms his private humiliation into a public performance of slander. What begins as obsessive table-talk becomes a strategic weapon: Schomberg weaponises his "natural gift for talking scandalously" (015-024) to direct Mr. Jones and Ricardo against Heyst, setting in motion the novel's final tragedy.

## Origin: Sexual Jealousy

The root of Schomberg's hatred is wounded vanity. For weeks, he had pursued Lena — then performing with Zangiacomo's Ladies' Orchestra — with "attacks, his prayers, and his fiercest protestations" (010-012). He had even promised to send his wife back to Europe: "Hang her! I've never cared for her. The climate don't suit her; I shall tell her to go to her people in Europe" (010-010). When Heyst — "that Swede" — simply took Lena away, apparently without effort, Schomberg's humiliation was absolute. He refused at first to believe the fact, preferring to imagine that the Zangiacomos had hidden the girl "for some unfathomable reason" (010-012).

## The Public Performance

Schomberg channelled his fury into a relentless public campaign. Conrad frames it as entertainment:

> It became a recognised entertainment to go and hear his abuse of Heyst, while sipping iced drinks on the verandah of the hotel. It was, in a manner, a more successful draw than the Zangiacomo concerts had ever been — intervals and all. There was never any difficulty in starting the performer off. Anybody could do it, by almost any distant allusion. As likely as not he would start his endless denunciations, his invective against that swindler, that ruffian, that schweinhund.
>
> (010-013)

The breadth of Schomberg's accusations expanded over time: "Heyst's unfitness to be at large, Heyst's wickedness, his wiles, his astuteness, and his criminality" (010-014). He became a bore, unable to talk of anything else.

## Psychology of Obsession

Schomberg's denunciation was not merely performative — it became a psychological possession. "Schomberg had Heyst on the brain" (010-016). He attributed every business failure, every disappointment, to "some subtly noxious influence of Heyst" (010-016). Conrad diagnoses the condition precisely: Schomberg believed in "the reality of Heyst as created by his own power of false inferences, of his hate, of his love of scandal" (015-081). The denunciation had become self-sustaining — Schomberg needed Heyst to be monstrous because his own identity as wronged avenger depended on it.

## Weaponisation: Directing Jones and Ricardo

The denunciation crosses from speech to action when Schomberg sees an opportunity in his unwanted guests — Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro. "He had only to let loose his natural gift for talking scandalously about his fellow lodger" (015-024) to send these dangerous men against Heyst. Schomberg painted Heyst as the possessor of hidden wealth on the island of Samburan, goading Ricardo's greed and Jones's predatory curiosity. In a moment of bitter candour, Schomberg gave voice to his deepest belief: "Look how he bewitched that poor Morrison" (015-090) — the implication being that Heyst had somehow caused Morrison's death to enrich himself, the very slander that would later be wielded as a weapon by Mr. Jones in the final confrontation.

## Consequences

Schomberg's denunciation achieves its intended effect with devastating consequences. Jones and Ricardo sail for Samburan, laying siege to Heyst's bungalow. The invasion leads directly to the deaths of Lena and Mr. Jones, and leaves Heyst to burn in the bungalow he set ablaze. Schomberg himself never faces consequences for his actions — he remains behind at his hotel, his hands technically clean, having merely "talked scandalously" about a man he hated. The episode is Conrad's darkest study of how words, wielded with sufficient malice, can become indistinguishable from weapons.
