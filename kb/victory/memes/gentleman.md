# Gentleman

## Definition

The concept of the "gentleman" in *Victory* operates as a paradoxical moral and social marker that Conrad deploys with sustained irony. On the surface, the term denotes a man of honour, restraint, and good breeding — someone who observes the proper forms of conduct. But Conrad systematically destabilizes this meaning by applying the label to characters across the entire moral spectrum: the philosophically detached Heyst, the predatory Mr. Jones, the altruistic Morrison, and the criminal Ricardo all lay claim to the title or bestow it on others. The word appears 80 times in the novel, making it one of the most frequently invoked social concepts. As Ricardo declares to Schomberg: "I am an Englishman, and I know a gentleman at sight. I should know one drunk, in the gutter, in jail, under the gallows. There's a something — it isn't exactly the appearance, it's a — no use me trying to tell you" (P06-023). This "something" is precisely what Conrad puts under pressure: the idea that gentlemanliness is an essence perceptible beneath any surface, any degradation, any criminality.

In the colonial setting of the Malay Archipelago, the gentleman code is a vestige of European social order transplanted into a moral wilderness where its meanings become unmoored. Conrad uses the gentleman concept to explore the tension between the forms of civilization and the realities of human nature.

## Key Instances

### Heyst: The Perfect Gentleman

Heyst is repeatedly identified as a gentleman by other characters, and this identification defines how they treat him. Davidson, the novel's moral compass, insists: "He's a genuine gentleman" (P02-018). When Morrison encounters Heyst in his moment of desperation, Heyst treats him with "Polite attention, what's due from one gentleman listening to another" (P02-021) — a display of form that restores Morrison's composure. Even after Heyst elopes with Lena, Davidson refuses to withdraw the designation:

> Heyst had, no doubt, told her the truth. He was a gentleman. But no words could do justice to the conditions of life on Samburan.
>
> (P04-010)

Yet Conrad introduces an ironic twist through another character's observation: "Heyst being a gentleman only makes it worse" (P04-016). The notion is that Heyst's gentlemanly restraint — his inability to act decisively, his reluctance to assert himself — becomes a liability when real threats arrive. His detachment, which is the gentleman's philosophical armor, proves fatal when love and violence demand action rather than reflection. As the Author's Note states, "on the arrival of the gentlemanly Mr. Jones... Heyst, the man of universal detachment, loses his mental self-possession" (P01-004).

### Mr. Jones: The Gentleman at Large

The most subversive application of the term is to Mr. Jones. He repeatedly styles himself "a gentleman at large" (P06-001, P06-004), a phrase that brilliantly conflates liberty with menace — he is a gentleman without ties, without accountability, without limits. Even the worldly Schomberg, who instinctively distrusts Jones, cannot deny the label: "I know a gentleman at sight," Ricardo tells him, and Jones himself affirms, "Of course I am that; but Ricardo attaches too much importance to a social advantage" (P06-011).

Ricardo's lengthy narratives about Jones are exercises in gentleman-worship. He recounts how Jones calmly shot Antonio between the eyes at dinner: "See what it is to have to do with a gentleman. No confounded fuss, and things done out of hand" (P08-025). After the violence, "The governor hadn't turned a hair. That's where a gentleman has the pull of you. He don't get excited. No gentleman does — or hardly ever" (P08-030). The gentleman code here has been entirely detached from morality and redefined as pure style — competence, composure, efficiency — regardless of what one is being competent *at*.

### Morrison: Gentleman and Honest Fellow

Morrison, the tragic altruistic trader, is described as "besides being a gentleman, was also an honest fellow" (P03-006). The separation of "gentleman" from "honest" in this formulation is telling: they are distinct categories, and one does not guarantee the other. Morrison's gentlemanliness is bound up with his impractical generosity and his fatal trust in others — qualities that lead to his indebtedness and, ultimately, his dependence on Heyst.

### Schomberg: The Anti-Gentleman

Schomberg is the novel's designated non-gentleman, a status made explicit by Ricardo: "Fancy a mud-turtle like you trying to pass an opinion on a gentleman! A gentleman isn't to be sized up so easily" (P08-021). And again: "A dog knows a gentleman — any dog. It's only some foreigners that don't know; and nothing can teach them, either" (P08-031). Schomberg is a "foreigner" (German) in the Englishman Ricardo's eyes, and therefore constitutionally incapable of recognizing, let alone embodying, the gentleman code. This exclusion is simultaneously comic and sinister — Schomberg's inability to "know a gentleman" becomes the engine of the plot, as he fails to comprehend the danger Jones represents and instead becomes his unwitting instrument of vengeance against Heyst.

### Ricardo: The Servant Who Follows a Gentleman

Ricardo's self-definition hinges entirely on his relationship to Jones as a gentleman: "I follow a gentleman. That ain't the same thing as to serve an employer... I ain't a dog walking on its hind legs for a bone; I am a man who's following a gentleman. There's a difference which you will never understand, Mr. Tame Schomberg" (P08-032). This distinction between "following a gentleman" and "serving an employer" is Ricardo's claim to dignity — a perverse assertion of freedom through voluntary submission to a social superior. It is one of the novel's darkest jokes: the murderer and gambler Ricardo, who has participated in countless crimes, finds his moral self-justification in the gentleman code.

## Analysis

Conrad's deployment of "gentleman" in *Victory* functions as a sustained critique of the idea that social forms can contain or civilize human violence. The novel's three "gentlemen" — Heyst, Jones, and Morrison — are all destroyed, each in a way that reveals the inadequacy of the code they embody. Heyst's gentlemanly detachment makes him incapable of the decisive action that might have saved Lena; Jones's gentlemanly composure is the mask of a psychopath; Morrison's gentlemanly trust is exploited until it kills him.

The phrase "gentleman at large" captures the novel's central insight: in the colonial periphery, far from the institutional structures that give the gentleman code its meaning, the gentleman becomes "at large" — unmoored, adrift, answers to nothing but his own style. The code that was meant to restrain becomes a license; the form that was meant to signify virtue becomes a tool of predation. Conrad suggests that the gentleman ideal, exported from its native social ecology, does not civilize the frontier — the frontier unravels it.

The frequency of the word in Ricardo's speech (the majority of the novel's uses of "gentleman" occur in his monologues) underscores another irony: it is the criminal who is most invested in the gentleman mystique, most eager to define himself by proximity to it. The gentleman code, in Conrad's hands, is revealed as a fiction sustained by those who most need its validation — a shared illusion as fragile and as powerful as any other form of belief.
