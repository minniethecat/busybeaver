## Overview

Scepticism is the philosophical inheritance that shapes Axel Heyst's
character and drives the novel's central conflict. Taught by his father
as a doctrine of detachment from the world's illusions, Heyst's
scepticism is tested to destruction by his love for Lena and his
encounter with the predatory Mr. Jones.

## Heyst's Father

The elder Heyst is described as a philosopher who taught his son to
"look on — make no sound." His books, left to Axel as a material and
intellectual inheritance, embody a philosophy of radical detachment.
Heyst spends his years on Samburan reading these volumes, "plunged into
an abyss of meditation over books" (016-041).

The father's philosophy shapes Heyst's characteristic passivity. When
Morrison is in distress, Heyst acts — but the action is presented as
anomalous, a departure from his nature. His default mode is the
sceptical observer, the man who refuses to commit.

## Scepticism and Action

The novel dramatizes the tension between sceptical detachment and the
imperative to act. Heyst's rescue of Lena from Schomberg's hotel is one
of the few decisive actions he takes, and it sets in motion the events
that will destroy his carefully maintained isolation.

In the novel's climactic moments, Heyst experiences the collapse of his
sceptical reserve: "The sceptical carelessness which had accompanied
every one of his attempts at action, like a secret reserve of his soul,
fell away from him. He no longer belonged to himself" (023-003).

## Scepticism as Self-Defeating

The novel suggests that Heyst's scepticism, while intellectually
coherent, is existentially unsustainable. His detachment makes him
vulnerable — to Schomberg's gossip, to Ricardo's violence, to the
ultimate tragedy. The philosophy that was meant to protect him from the
world's "universal plotting" instead leaves him unable to defend what
he loves.

## The Gentlemanly Ideal

Heyst's scepticism is intertwined with his identity as a "gentleman" —
a category that in Conrad's hands becomes both an ethical standard and a
form of paralysis. Heyst's courteous manner, his "finished courtesy of
attitude and intonation" (004-008), is an expression of sceptical
detachment: politeness as a way of keeping the world at arm's length.
