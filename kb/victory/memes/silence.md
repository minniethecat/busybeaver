## Overview

Silence pervades *Victory* at every level. It is a philosophical principle, a social condition, a psychological state, and a narrative atmosphere. Heyst inherits from his father a philosophy that exalts silence as the only honest response to a meaningless world: the elder Heyst's books counsel withdrawal, detachment, and the refusal to engage (016-014) (016-005). His father's parting words to the young Heyst were:

> "Look on—make no sound," were the last words of the man who had spent his life in blowing blasts upon a terrible trumpet which had filled heaven and earth with ruins, while mankind went on its way unheeding.
>
> (016-014)

Silence becomes Heyst's natural habitat. As Conrad writes: "Heyst, the wanderer of the Archipelago, had a taste for silence which he had been able to gratify for years" (008-003).

But Conrad complicates this inheritance. The silence of Samburan, initially a refuge — "the silence around Samburan ... was dazzling" (001-004) — becomes oppressive. The silence between Heyst and Lena becomes a barrier: "she didn't know what his silences might mean" (019-005). The silence of the jungle conceals approaching danger: "A great silence brooded over Samburan — the silence of the great heat that seems pregnant with fatal issues, like the silence of ardent thought" (020-019). In the end, silence becomes the space in which tragedy unfolds — not a refuge from the world but the condition of its most terrible visitation (016-017).

## Role in the Novel

Heyst's father represents one form of silence: the philosophical withdrawal of the intellectual who sees through all illusions and refuses to participate (016-014). His advice to his son amounts to a doctrine of silence: do not engage, do not trust, do not hope — a "contemptuous, inflexible negation of all effort" (016-005). Heyst carries this legacy into the Malay Archipelago, where it makes him an object of suspicion and resentment among the European merchants who cannot understand a man who does not play their games (004-024).

The silence of Samburan is initially the realization of this philosophy. On the island, Heyst is free from the noise of human society — the gossip, the obligations, the "enchantment" of the isles. The "genius of the locality," Conrad tells us, "was certainly that of silence" (016-041). Till Heyst and Morrison had landed in Black Diamond Bay, "that side of Samburan had hardly ever heard the sound of human speech" (016-041). Heyst himself was not conscious of friends or enemies; his life was "a solitary achievement, accomplished not by hermit-like withdrawal with its silence and immobility, but by a system of restless wandering" (009-083).

But the arrival of Lena, and later of Mr. Jones and his gang, reveals the inadequacy of silence as a life philosophy. The Zangiacomo band at Schomberg's hotel offers the antithesis of Heyst's silence:

> The Zangiacomo band was not making music; it was simply murdering silence with a vulgar, ferocious energy. One felt as if witnessing a deed of violence; and that impression was so strong that it seemed marvellous to see the people sitting so quietly on their chairs, drinking so calmly out of their glasses, and giving no signs of distress, anger or fear.
>
> (008-010)

Silence cannot protect; it can only isolate. Heyst himself acknowledges his "untimely ... silences" (019-099) as a failure of human connection. In the novel's final pages, after Heyst buries his father — "the silenced destroyer of systems, of hopes, of beliefs" (016-016) — he meditates on the stream of humanity, "giving no sign of being aware that the voice on the bank had been suddenly silenced" (016-017). Silence and death converge in the novel's closing vision.
