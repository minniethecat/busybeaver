# "Woe to the man whose heart has not learned while young to hope"

## Quotation

> "Ah, Davidson, woe to the man whose heart has not learned while young (039-013) to hope, to love — and to put its trust in life!"
>
> (039-013)

## Context

These are the last words Axel Heyst speaks before his suicide on Samburan. Davidson, the trader who arrived too late to save Lena, reports them to the unnamed "Excellency" who frames the novel's outer narrative. Heyst reflects on his father's philosophy — the elder Heyst's injunction to "look on — make no sound" — and recognizes, at the end of his life, the tragedy of emotional detachment. The words echo the novel's epigraph from Comus: "That fatal and perfidious bark / Built in th' eclipse, and rigg'd with curses dark."

## Significance

This quotation is the moral verdict of the entire novel. Heyst, who spent his life observing rather than engaging, realizes too late that the capacity for hope, love, and trust is not a philosophical choice but a condition of being fully human. The structure of the sentence — "woe to the man" — deliberately invokes biblical cadences, framing Heyst's realization as a kind of secular last judgment.

The three infinitives — "to hope, to love, and to put its trust in life" — form a progression from abstract optimism to intimate connection to existential commitment. Heyst has failed at all three. His tragedy is not that he was wrong to be detached, but that detachment made him incapable of protecting what he finally learned to love.
