# Heyst's Leather Trunk

## Description

Heyst's leather trunk is an old, well-traveled piece of luggage that dates back to the period before his association with Morrison — the "pre-Morrison days" of his wandering existence. It is a flat leather trunk that accompanies Heyst on his journeys and ends up in the bungalow on Samburan:

> He had forgotten all about it till she came, and then had found it at the bottom of an old sandalwood trunk dating back to pre-Morrison days.
>
> (024-021)

The trunk contains the few material traces of Heyst's past — a dress or two of Lena's hanging on pegs nearby, and a small looking-glass that Ricardo later retrieves for his own purposes (032-030). When the boat carrying the three strangers arrives at Samburan, the visible cargo includes a flat leather trunk on which one of the men's long legs are tucked up (021-031). In the bungalow's bedroom, the chest and leather trunk serve as both furniture and concealment — Lena fears that Heyst's hand might discover her secret at any moment among these few possessions (028-034).

The trunk is not the only piece of luggage on Samburan. The criminal trio arrive with their own trunks — "a couple of large leather trunks browned by age and a few smaller packages" — which Schomberg notes with interest as promising "accounts" (012-010). Later, it is revealed that these trunks contain "knives and revolvers" (012-012), a detail that sharply distinguishes the contents of the intruders' luggage from Heyst's. Where their trunks hold instruments of violence, Heyst's holds only the remnants of an itinerant life.

## Symbolic Meaning

The leather trunk embodies the attenuated connection Heyst maintains with his own past. Unlike the trunks of the criminal trio, which are packed with weapons (012-010, 013-003), Heyst's trunk holds only the remnants of an itinerant life — a dress, a mirror, forgotten items. The trunk's age and the fact that it "dates back to pre-Morrison days" marks it as a vessel of memory, the sole surviving link to the period before Heyst's defining relationship with the man whose name he still bears. Its presence in the bungalow is a material reminder that Heyst, for all his detachment, cannot fully escape the accumulated weight of his own history.

## Role in the Narrative

The trunk plays a subtle but significant role in the novel's climactic scenes. When Lena must dispose of Ricardo's straw slipper — the crucial piece of evidence that would expose her struggle with the secretary — she searches the bedroom desperately:

> The chest, the leather trunk, a dress or two of hers hanging on pegs — there was no place where the merest hazard might not guide Heyst's hand at any moment.
>
> (028-034)

The trunk's inadequacy as a hiding-place underscores the vulnerability of the bungalow's inhabitants. There is no secure concealment in this stripped-down domestic space — every object, including the old trunk, is exposed to Heyst's potential discovery. Lena is forced to throw the slipper out the window instead. Later, Ricardo kneels before the trunk to retrieve a looking-glass:

> Ricardo walked about the room as if he had no care in the world. He hummed snatches of song. Mr. Jones raised his waspish eyebrows at the sound. The secretary got down on his knees before an old leather trunk, and, rummaging in there, brought out a small looking-glass. He fell to examining his physiognomy in it with silent absorption.
>
> (032-030)

This moment of grotesque vanity — the killer admiring himself in a mirror retrieved from Heyst's trunk — captures the violation of Heyst's private space. The trunk, a repository of personal memory, is rifled by the man who will attempt to assault Lena.
