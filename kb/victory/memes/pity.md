## Overview

Pity is the crack in Heyst's sceptical armour. His father's philosophy
counselled detachment, but pity — for Morrison, for Lena — repeatedly
draws Heyst into the human entanglements he has tried to avoid. The
novel treats pity as morally ambiguous: it is Heyst's finest quality and
the cause of his destruction.

## Pity for Morrison

Heyst's relationship with Morrison is founded on an act of financial
rescue that grows from pity. Morrison, "the victim of gratitude and his
native climate" (003-002), becomes the catalyst for the Tropical Belt
Coal Company. Heyst's pity sets in motion the entire chain of events
that will eventually strand him on Samburan.

## Pity for Lena

Heyst's decision to rescue Lena from Schomberg's hotel and Zangiacomo's
orchestra is described as an act of pity (033-092). Seeing her misery, Heyst
abandons his usual detachment: "He was not used to pitying anyone"
(referenced throughout the Sourabaya chapters).

The rescue, however, carries consequences Heyst cannot foresee. Pity (034-080)
leads to love, love to vulnerability, and vulnerability to tragedy. The
novel asks whether pity is a virtue or a trap — whether Heyst would have
been wiser to remain the detached observer his father taught him to be.

## Pity and the Gentleman

Heyst's pity is filtered through his identity as a gentleman. His
response to Lena's suffering is chivalric — the gentleman's obligation
to protect the vulnerable. This intertwining of pity with the
gentlemanly code creates a moral imperative that even Heyst's scepticism
cannot override.
