# Wang

## Biography

Wang was not a common coolie. He had been a servant to white men before arriving on Samburan with the Tropical Belt Coal Company's labour force. When the company collapsed and the Chinese coolies were evacuated, Wang chose to remain. On the day the last batch of workers departed, he approached Heyst on the verandah, raised his "yellow, thin face," and negotiated his continued service with words that captured his entire philosophy of existence:

> "Catchee one piecee wife," remarked Wang unemotionally, and marched off, turning his back on the wharf and the great world beyond, represented by the steamer waiting for her boats.
>
> (016-012)

Wang proceeded to do exactly that. He persuaded one of the women of the Alfuro village on the west shore of the island to come and live with him — the sole instance of a Chinese coolie crossing the ridge that separated the two communities. The Alfuro woman, though she never came near Heyst's bungalow, "anchored Wang to the spot" and formed the basis of his autonomous domestic world at the edge of the forest clearing.

Wang's relationship with Heyst was one of almost complete silence — they exchanged perhaps half a dozen words per day. He took control of the settlement's keys, annexed what he needed, and returned only those that could not be regarded as common property. He cultivated a vegetable garden behind a high fence, dismantling a company shed to obtain materials. Heyst observed all this from a distance and, looking at the silent Chinaman going about his work with "unhasty, steady" precision, "envied the Chinaman's obedience to his instincts, the powerful simplicity of purpose which made his existence appear almost automatic in the mysterious precision of its facts" (016-019).

Conrad frames Wang as a foil to the philosophically paralyzed Heyst: where Heyst thinks, Wang acts; where Heyst is paralyzed by detachment, Wang is propelled by instinct. When Wang took the revolver from Heyst's unlocked drawer — the night the three white strangers arrived on Samburan — he was not stealing a weapon for malice but securing control over his own fate. Unlike Heyst, who forgot he even owned a revolver until a sudden, novel fear of his own sleeping body's defencelessness prompted him to check the drawer, Wang had apparently been watching, waiting, and acting on a calculus of survival Heyst could never fully comprehend.

## Role in the Novel

Wang is the third pillar of Samburan's triangular society in the novel's second half — Heyst, Lena, and Wang — and his growing independence traces an arc that culminates in withdrawal and survival. He performs three crucial narrative functions.

First, Wang is the **practical counterweight to Heyst's detachment**. His automatic, efficient, pre-reflective mode of existence — gardening, maintaining the compound, vanishing at dusk to his Alfuro woman — stands in implicit contrast to Heyst's "abyss of meditation over books." While Heyst drifts in abstraction, Wang secures his ground: clearing brush, annexing keys, fencing off his vegetable patch, and keeping watch. This contrast is not hostile but structural; Wang does not resent Heyst, and Heyst does not command Wang. Theirs is a relationship of mutual, unspoken accommodation.

Second, Wang is the **harbinger of danger**. It is Wang who first spots the boat bringing Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro:

> "Round the point," said Wang, leaping into Malay unexpectedly, and in a loud voice. "White men — three."
>
> (021-004)

His report is the turning point at which the outside world breaches the fragile peace of Samburan. The accuracy of Wang's warning — dismissed briefly by Heyst as a hallucination — establishes his perceptual superiority over his master. Wang sees what Heyst, for all his philosophical training, cannot or will not see coming.

Third, and most decisively, Wang is the **agent of abandonment and survival**. On the morning of the crisis, having heard the sounds of Ricardo's struggle with Lena through the curtain, Wang makes his decision:

> It was at this time that Wang made up his mind to give up all connection with Number One, a man not only disarmed but already half vanquished. Till that morning he had had doubts as to his course of action, but this overheard scuffle decided the question. Number One was a doomed man — one of those beings whom it is unlucky to help.
>
> (027-003)

Wang does not stay to fight or to die. He walks out into the compound, "a self-possessed Chinaman idling there, with nothing but perhaps an unserved breakfast on his mind," concealing his decision behind an impenetrable facade of ordinariness. The judgement is as cold as it is final: Heyst is already lost, and Wang will not share his fate. He later tells Davidson that he followed Heyst and Lena through the forest "from pity, and partly out of curiosity," but his intervention is limited to securing the practical advantage — casting the boat adrift and shooting Pedro, the simian servant of Mr. Jones, when Pedro awoke growling in the boat.

Wang emerges from the catastrophe as Samburan's sole surviving inhabitant. He retrieves his Alfuro woman from over the ridge and resumes his gardening. In the final analysis, Davidson pronounces him "not a bad Chinaman" — a judgment that reflects Conrad's ambivalent respect for a character who, unlike the Europeans whose world collapses around him, understands exactly when to act and when to walk away.

## Analysis

Wang embodies what might be called **the victory of instinct over philosophy**. Conrad positions him as Heyst's antithesis: where Heyst is the product of European civilization at its most rarefied and self-defeatingly reflective, Wang is the product of nature — or more precisely, of a nature that civilization has not yet extinguished. Heyst himself intuits this when, watching Wang at work, he envies "the Chinaman's obedience to his instincts, the powerful simplicity of purpose which made his existence appear almost automatic."

The figure of Wang invites reading through the Edenic imagery that pervades the novel. Heyst identifies himself with "the original Adam" — the prelapsarian man who names the animals and acts without the burden of self-conscious thought. But it is Wang, not Heyst, who actually lives as that "original Adam." He cultivates the soil, takes a mate, builds a fence around his garden, and survives the serpent's arrival. Heyst may recognize the voice of "the oldest voice in the world," but it is Wang who obeys it.

Wang's racial positioning is equally significant. Conrad uses the figure of the "inscrutable Chinaman" but subverts the stereotype: Wang's inscrutability is not mystery but self-sufficiency. His silence is not evasion but power. When Wang takes the revolver, annexes the keys, and finally walks away, he acts from a clarity of purpose that the white characters — Heyst, Jones, Ricardo — all lack. His survival is not luck but the logical outcome of a worldview that subordinates everything to the calculus of practical existence.

The novel's final judgement of Wang, delivered through Davidson, is carefully ambivalent. "He's not a bad Chinaman" — neither hero nor villain, neither loyal retainer nor treacherous servant. Wang exists outside the moral categories that govern the European characters. He is, in the end, simply the one who remains.
