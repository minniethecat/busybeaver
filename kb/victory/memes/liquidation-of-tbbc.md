## Overview

The liquidation of the Tropical Belt Coal Company is the quiet catastrophe that shapes the entire world of *Victory*. The company, founded by Morrison with Heyst as his partner, was meant to exploit coal deposits on the island of Samburan. Instead, its capital "evaporated" and the firm "went into liquidation" (001-002), leaving behind rusting machinery, abandoned bungalows, and a single inhabitant — Axel Heyst, the "manager" who stayed behind. The failure is both a practical and symbolic event: it ends Heyst's one attempt at engagement with the world of commerce and marks his final retreat into the detachment his father had prescribed.

## The Rise and Fall

The Tropical Belt Coal Company was Morrison's brainchild, conceived in the flush of gratitude after Heyst rescued him from financial ruin. Heyst, touched by Morrison's enthusiasm, allowed himself to be drawn in: "He was not the same Heyst, who had been drifting about the Eastern Seas for years, a detached and courteous spectator" (001-004). The company established operations on Samburan, constructing bungalows, importing machinery, and employing a workforce of Chinese coolies under Wang's supervision. Photographs from this period show "a jetty, a tramway, a lot of schooners lying alongside" (016-021) — the visible signs of brief prosperity.

## The Evaporation

Conrad describes the failure in terms of unnatural physics that are really a satire of finance: "The world of finance is a mysterious world in which, incredible as the fact may appear, evaporation precedes liquidation. First the capital evaporates, and then the company goes into liquidation" (001-002). The liquidation itself was a languid affair, proceeding simultaneously "in London and Amsterdam" (001-005), far from the tropical island where its physical remnants rusted. By the time the legal process concluded, Morrison was dead and Heyst was the company's sole surviving representative — a ghost haunting the ruins of a dead enterprise.

> The world of finance is a mysterious world in which, incredible as the fact may appear, evaporation precedes liquidation. First the capital evaporates, and then the company goes into liquidation. These are very unnatural physics.
>
> (001-002)

## Heyst's Decision to Stay

When the mine coolies departed Samburan, Heyst did not leave with them. "Heyst, leaning over the balustrade of the verandah, was looking on, as calm in appearance as though he had never departed from his principles" (016-025). His reasons for remaining were complex: a sense of responsibility for the company's assets, a reluctance to re-enter a world that had disappointed him, and perhaps a perverse fidelity to the one place where he had attempted to live differently. The company's furniture, books, and his father's portrait — shipped out during the brief boom — anchored him to the island. "Whatever the decisive reason, Heyst remained" (016-023).

## Consequences

The liquidation transforms Heyst into "the Hermit of Samburan." The island becomes not a business venture but a refuge of defeat. The company's failure also becomes a weapon in the hands of Heyst's enemies: the empty bungalows, the rusting railway, and the abandoned jetty are taken as evidence that Heyst must have profited secretly from the collapse. Schomberg's slander — that Heyst "bewitched that poor Morrison" (015-090) and somehow caused his partner's death — draws its plausibility from the opaque circumstances of the liquidation. The company's failure, in short, leaves Heyst vulnerable to every accusation, with no corporate records or living partner to defend him.
