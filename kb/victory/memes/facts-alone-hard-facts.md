# Facts alone, Mr. Tesman

## Quotation

> "Facts," broke in Heyst in his courtly voice. "There's nothing worth knowing but facts. Hard facts! Facts alone, Mr. Tesman" (001-015).
>
> (001-015)

## Context

Heyst delivers this pronouncement during a conversation at the Malacca club, responding to a remark by Mr. Tesman of the Tesman Brothers trading company. The statement is received with enough attention that it becomes another of Heyst's portable epithets — for a time, he is known as 'Hard Facts.' The pronouncement is characteristic of Heyst's intellectual style: aphoristic, confidently universalizing, and delivered with the courtly manner that disarms potential objections.

## Significance

The 'Hard Facts' credo is the closest Heyst comes to articulating a positive philosophy, and its limitations map precisely onto the novel's tragic arc. Heyst's father taught him to distrust all systems of thought, all ideals, all emotional attachments — leaving 'facts' as the only reliable ground. But what happens when the facts themselves are inadequate? Lena's love, Mr. Jones's malevolence, and Ricardo's animal cunning are all 'facts,' but Heyst's commitment to detached observation leaves him unable to respond to them effectively. The credo is a defense mechanism disguised as epistemology: by reducing everything to 'hard facts,' Heyst avoids having to care about any of them.
