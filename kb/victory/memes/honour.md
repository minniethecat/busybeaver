## Overview

Honour in *Victory* functions as both a moral compass and a fatal liability. Heyst acts from a deeply ingrained sense of honour — the rescue of Morrison in Timor, the protection of Lena from Schomberg's establishment, the refusal to abandon Samburan even when danger threatens (002-051). These are not calculated acts but instinctive responses, the product of a gentleman's education that Heyst absorbed before his father's philosophy of detachment could fully take hold (002-017).

The irony is that Heyst's honour is invisible to the world that judges him. The European merchants of the archipelago see only the detached Swede, the "utopist," the man who does not play their games (003-031). His honourable acts — the loan to Morrison, the rescue of Lena — are interpreted through the lens of suspicion and resentment (003-020). Honour, in Conrad's world, does not protect; it isolates.

## Role in the Novel

Heyst's honour creates the central tension of his character: the conflict between his father's teaching (withdraw, do not engage) and his own instincts (protect, rescue, stand firm). The novel stages this conflict in a series of tests — Morrison, Lena, the siege — and in each case Heyst's honour compels him to act, overriding the philosophy that counsels withdrawal.
