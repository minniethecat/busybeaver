## Context

The narrator describes the three years the teenage Axel Heyst spent in his father's company in London. The elder Heyst — a disillusioned Swedish philosopher — subjected his son to a relentless education in pessimism. The result was not wisdom but paralysis: a "profound mistrust of life" that would shape every subsequent decision Heyst made. The aphorism about "the clear-sighted" is the novel's most concise diagnosis of what Heyst lost.

## The Quote

> The young man learned to reflect, which is a destructive process, a reckoning of the cost. It is not the clear-sighted who lead the world. Great achievements are accomplished in a blessed, warm mental fog, which the pitiless cold blasts of the father's analysis had blown away from the son's path.
>
> (010-003)

## Significance

This passage articulates one of *Victory*'s central themes: the cost of knowledge. The elder Heyst's gift to his son is clarity — the ability to see through illusions, to calculate costs, to anticipate disappointment. But this clarity is also a curse. Those who "lead the world" do so because they cannot see the futility of their efforts; they act within a "blessed, warm mental fog" of unexamined conviction. Heyst, stripped of that fog by his father's teaching, cannot act at all. The passage reframes Heyst's "drift" not as a choice but as the inevitable consequence of a mind that has been taught to see too clearly. The tragedy of the novel is that Heyst must learn — through Lena, through disaster — that some illusions are worth preserving.
