# Morrison

## Biography

Morrison was an English trader from Dorsetshire, owner and master of the *Capricorn*, a trading brig operating among the obscure villages of the Malay Archipelago. He belonged to the loose community of European traders "out there" and was known for a fatal flaw: an excess of altruism that made him beloved by the native populations he served but perpetually insolvent in his accounts.

Morrison's trading method was one of endless credit. He would sail through dangerous channels to impoverished settlements, find hungry populations without produce to trade, and land his rice anyway — entering the debt in a pocket-diary under the optimistic heading "Squeeze the So-and-So village at the first time of calling." He never actually squeezed any of them:

> Then he would stick the pencil back and snap the elastic on with inflexible finality; but he never began the squeezing. Some men grumbled at him. He was spoiling the trade.
>
> (002-008)

He was tall, lantern-jawed, clean-shaven, and wore a single eyeglass — a figure who "looked like a barrister who had thrown his wig to the dogs" (002-001). When he spoke of going home to England, he shuddered: he said it was like living with your head and shoulders in a moist gunny-bag.

Morrison's defining crisis came in Delli, Timor, where Portuguese authorities had seized his brig on a pretext and imposed a fine he could not pay. Destitute and despairing, he knelt in his cabin and prayed for help — the kind of prayer "like a child" makes. That same day, the wandering Axel Heyst happened upon him in the street, and in a scene that Morrison read as a direct answer to prayer, offered to lend him the exact sum needed. Morrison never recovered from the conviction that Heyst was an emissary of Providence.

Out of gratitude, Morrison insisted on Heyst's partnership and brought him into the coal-outcrop scheme that would become the Tropical Belt Coal Company. He sailed for London through the Suez Canal to push the venture, parted from his beloved brig, and — pining for the *Capricorn*, lonely and out of place in England — went to Dorsetshire, caught a cold, and died "with extraordinary precipitation in the bosom of his appalled family" (003-002). Conrad's epitaph is devastatingly concise: "Morrison, the victim of gratitude and his native climate, had gone to join his forefathers in a Dorsetshire churchyard" (003-002).

## Role in the Novel

Morrison is the **hidden catalyst** of the novel's entire plot. He appears only in flashback — the "I" narrator's retrospective account in Part I — and dies offstage before the main action begins. Yet without Morrison, there would be no Tropical Belt Coal Company, no Heyst stranded on Samburan, and no Victory.

His rescue by Heyst is the novel's foundational moral act. It demonstrates Heyst's capacity for generosity, the instinct that later compels him to save Lena from Schomberg's hotel — the same impulse that a later chapter calls "the same man who had plunged after the submerged Morrison whom he hardly knew otherwise than by sight" (009-001). Morrison's story thus establishes the pattern of Heyst's character: a man constitutionally unable to stand by while another suffers.

Morrison's death has profound consequences. Heyst is "immensely shocked" by the news and withdraws into guarded solitude, "as if afraid someone would reproach him with the death of Morrison" (003-003). The Tropical Belt Coal Company, born from Morrison's gratitude, proceeds without him — and without a living person to anchor it to reality. When it fails, Heyst remains on Samburan, trapped by the ruins of the venture Morrison's prayer set in motion.

Schomberg, the novel's consummate gossip, exploits Morrison's memory for malice: "That's what comes of having anything to do with that fellow. He squeezes you dry like a lemon, then chucks you out — sends you home to die. Take warning by Morrison" (003-006). The echo of Morrison's fate reverberates through every subsequent suspicion cast upon Heyst.

Morrison is also the novel's clearest instance of dramatic irony. He prays for rescue, receives it, and the rescue kills him — not through Heyst's fault but through the chain of consequences gratitude sets in motion. He is "the victim of gratitude" in the most literal sense: his thankfulness to Heyst drives him back to the climate he dreads, to the country that does for him.

## Key Passages

> Morrison, staring open-mouthed, groped over his shoulder for the cord of the eyeglass hanging down his back. When he found it, he stuck it in his eye hastily. It was as if he expected Heyst's usual white suit of the tropics to change into a shining garment flowing down to his toes, and a pair of great dazzling wings to sprout on the Swede's shoulders — and didn't want to miss a single detail of the transformation. But if Heyst was an angel from on high, sent in answer to prayer, he did not betray his heavenly origin by outward signs.
>
> (002-035)

> Ultimately we heard that Morrison had gone home through the Suez Canal in order to push the magnificent coal idea personally in London. He parted from his brig and disappeared from our ken; but we heard that he had written a letter or letters to Heyst, saying that London was cold and gloomy; that he did not like either the men or things, that he was "as lonely as a crow in a strange country." In truth, he pined after the *Capricorn* — I don't mean only the tropic; I mean the ship too. Finally he went into Dorsetshire to see his people, caught a bad cold, and died with extraordinary precipitation in the bosom of his appalled family.
>
> (003-002)
