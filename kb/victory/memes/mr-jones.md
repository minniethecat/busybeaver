## Biography

Mr. Jones is the primary antagonist of Joseph Conrad's *Victory*. His real name remains unknown — he registers at Schomberg's hotel simply as "plain Mr. Jones" and describes himself as "a gentleman at large" (PN 1137-1177). The narrator first encountered him in 1875 at a hotel on the island of St. Thomas in the West Indies, where Mr. Jones was "extended on three chairs, all alone in the loud buzzing of flies," his "immobility and cadaverous aspect" giving "a most gruesome significance" (PN 48). One of the narrator's companions identified him as "the most desperate gambler" he had ever met — "a terror; but I must say that up to a certain point he will play fair" (PN 48).

Little is known of his origins. He is a gambler, a sharp, and a man who "depended on himself, as if the world were still one great, wild jungle without law" (PN 1284). He travels with two accomplices: Martin Ricardo, his "secretary," and Pedro, a Colombian alligator-hunter he describes with chilling nonchalance — "Shall I tell you how I killed his brother in the wilds of Colombia? Well, perhaps some other time — it's a rather long story. What I shall always regret is that I didn't kill him, too" (PN 1177).

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Jones embodies what Conrad presents as pure "evil" in the novel — a spectral, calculated force that destroys the fragile world Heyst has built on Samburan. His arrival at Schomberg's hotel sets the plot in motion: he terrorizes the hotelkeeper into allowing him to run a gambling operation in the music-room, reduces Schomberg to a state of "moral decomposition" (PN 1230), and eventually learns of Heyst's hidden wealth and his possession of a woman he stole from Schomberg.

Jones is distinguished by a virulent misogyny. He declares, "I can't stand women near me. They give me the horrors. They are a perfect curse!" (PN 1167). When Schomberg mentions his wife's existence, Jones "let out an exclamation of dismay, and looked about as if the walls around him and the whole house had been infected with plague" (PN 1328). This pathological aversion to women becomes the fatal flaw that undoes his operations on Samburan, for when he discovers that Heyst is not alone but accompanied by Lena, he perceives her as the agent of Ricardo's wavering loyalty. "A woman had intervened! A woman, a girl, who apparently possessed the power to awaken men's disgusting folly" (PN 5164). He shoots Lena with his revolver, the bullet making "the little black hole . . . under the swelling breast of a dazzling and as it were sacred whiteness" (PN 5396).

After Ricardo is killed and his plans collapse, Mr. Jones's body is found "huddled up on the bottom between two piles, like a heap of bones in a blue silk bag, with only the head and the feet sticking out" (PN 5481). Davidson speculates he "tumbled into the water by accident — or perhaps not by accident. The boat and the man were gone, and the scoundrel saw himself all alone, his game clearly up, and fairly trapped" (PN 5481).
