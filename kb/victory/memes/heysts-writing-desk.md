# Heyst's Writing-Desk

## Description

Heyst's writing-desk is a tall stand-up desk located in the bungalow on Samburan. It is a solitary piece of furniture that carries the lingering atmosphere of the Tropical Belt Coal Company's failed enterprise. When the door of the long-abandoned bungalow is forced open after two years, the desk makes a dramatic first impression:

> From the dark slope of a tall stand-up writing-desk a forgotten, solitary sheet of paper flew up and settled gracefully on the floor.
>
> (022-077)

Wang later lights a candle and sticks it on the ledge of the stand-up desk (022-079), a small domestic gesture that transforms the abandoned office into a provisional living space. The desk thus bridges the bungalow's two identities — the failed commercial headquarters and the makeshift home Heyst has made.

The desk contains a drawer on the left where Heyst keeps his money — a small hoard of sovereigns. He reveals this to Lena when reassuring her of their material security: "Of course, there is some money in the house — there, in that writing-desk, the drawer on the left" (024-048). The hiding-place is more ingenious than it appears: "There is a recess, and the board at the back pivots; a very simple hiding-place, when you know the way to it. I discovered it by accident, and I keep our store of sovereigns in there" (024-048). This pivot-board mechanism — discovered by chance rather than designed — reflects the improvised nature of Heyst's entire existence on Samburan.

## Symbolic Meaning

The writing-desk functions as a symbol of the compromised sanctuary Heyst has created on Samburan. It holds both the administrative residue of the failed coal company and the secret store of money that sustains Heyst's retreat. The "forgotten, solitary sheet of paper" that flutters from it evokes the ghost of the commercial enterprise whose collapse left Heyst marooned.

## Role in the Narrative

The desk becomes an object of intense interest to Ricardo, whose criminal instincts are drawn to the promise of hidden wealth. In one of the novel's tensest confrontations, Heyst catches Ricardo in a moment of visible fixation:

> Heyst had caught Mr. Jones's secretary in the contemplation of his closed writing-desk. Ricardo might have been meditating how to break into it; but when he turned about suddenly, he showed so distorted a face that it made Heyst pause in wonder at the upturned whites of the eyes, which were blinking horribly, as if the man were inwardly convulsed.
>
> (035-056)

Ricardo's uncontrollable reaction — the "upturned whites of the eyes" and convulsive blinking — betrays the depth of his obsession with the desk's contents. The desk has become the focal point of his greed, the material object that embodies everything he has come to Samburan to take. Later that evening, the desk is the setting for the final confrontation between Heyst, Mr. Jones, and Ricardo:

> Two candles were burning on the stand-up desk. Mr. Jones, tightly enfolded in an old but gorgeous blue silk dressing-gown, kept his elbows close against his sides and his hands deeply plunged into the extraordinarily deep pockets of the garment. The costume accentuated his emaciation. He resembled a painted pole leaning against the edge of the desk, with a dried head of dubious distinction stuck on the top of it.
>
> (036-001)

Mr. Jones leaning against the desk "like a painted pole" creates a tableau that concentrates the novel's themes — decayed gentility, latent violence, and the corrupting power of money — around this single piece of furniture.
