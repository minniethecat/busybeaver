## Overview

Debt in *Victory* operates on multiple levels: as financial obligation,
as moral indebtedness, and as the gratitude that binds one person to
another. The novel's foundational event — Morrison's rescue by Heyst —
is a debt transaction that generates all subsequent plot developments.

## Morrison's Debt

Morrison, cornered by financial ruin, is saved by Heyst's intervention.
The debt creates an asymmetry that Morrison attempts to resolve through
gratitude — but gratitude, in Conrad's treatment, is itself a form of
obligation: "Morrison believed both in the efficacy of prayer and in the
infinite goodness of Heyst" (003-002).

Morrison's gratitude manifests as insistence on Heyst's partnership in
the coal venture — a gesture that transforms a personal debt into a
corporate enterprise. The Tropical Belt Coal Company is born from this
entanglement of financial and moral obligation.

## The Colonial Debt Economy

The novel's colonial setting operates within a broader debt economy.
Traders extend credit, ships carry cargoes on account, and the
"evaporation" of capital in the T.B.C. Co. represents debt on an
institutional scale: "First the capital evaporates, and then the company
goes into liquidation" (001-002).

## Debt and Freedom

Heyst's relationship to debt is paradoxical. By rescuing Morrison, he
creates an obligation — yet Heyst himself desires no repayment. His
detachment from money mirrors his detachment from human connection. The
irony is that Heyst, who seeks freedom from all entanglements, becomes
entangled precisely through his generosity.
