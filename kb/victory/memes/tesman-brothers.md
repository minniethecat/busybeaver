## Description

Tesman Brothers was a trading firm based in Sourabaya (now Surabaya, Indonesia), operating in the Java Sea region during the late 19th century. The firm maintained its principal countinghouse in Sourabaya and operated a fleet of trading schooners throughout the East Indies archipelago. (004-043)(001-016) Conrad describes it as a "tip-top house" — a reputable and well-established commercial enterprise. (001-013)

The firm was led by Mr. Tesman, described as "a kindly, benevolent old gentleman," (001-013) and by Julius Tesman, who was noted to be "unreasonably bitter about the Tropical Belt Coal fiasco." (004-043) The Tesmans occupied a position at the center of the colonial commercial network, connecting European capital (London and Amsterdam) with the day-to-day realities of island trade.

> The incongruity of a hermit having agents did not strike us, nor yet the
> absurdity of a forgotten cast-off, derelict manager of a wrecked,
> collapsed, vanished enterprise, having business to attend to.
>
> (004-043)

## Role in the Novel

Tesman Brothers serves as the invisible commercial infrastructure behind the novel's key events. Their role is largely offstage but pervasive — they are the agents, bankers, and facilitators without whom the characters' financial lives could not function.

When Axel Heyst first arrived in the islands, he presented a letter of introduction to Mr. Tesman, initiating a long-standing connection between the enigmatic Swede and the firm. (001-013) It was during this interview that Heyst delivered his famous pronouncement — "There's nothing worth knowing but facts. Hard facts! Facts alone, Mr. Tesman" — earning him the nickname "Hard Facts" after Mr. Tesman spoke of the encounter. (001-015)(001-016)

The Tesmans became Heyst's de facto agents, handling his correspondence and financial affairs. The narrator notes the incongruity: "The incongruity of a hermit having agents did not strike us, nor yet the absurdity of a forgotten cast-off, derelict manager of a wrecked, collapsed, vanished enterprise, having business to attend to." (004-043) During Heyst's eighteen-month retreat on Samburan, the thought that he "did come out from his retreat" was attributed to perhaps "seeing whether there were any letters for him at the Tesmans." (004-025)

Morrison, the captain of the *Capricorn*, also relied on the Tesmans, leaving "all his spare cash with the Tesmans, in Samarang, to meet certain bills which would fall due while he was away on his cruise." (002-029) When Morrison died in England, it was "through the Tesmans" that Heyst received the devastating news in the Moluccas. (003-003)

The firm played a critical role in the rise and fall of the Tropical Belt Coal Company. The Tesmans "appointed agents, a contract for government mail-boats secured" — a development the narrator calls "Heyst's stride." (003-001) When the TBBC collapsed, however, "the Tesmans washed their hands of it," and the Government cancelled the famous contracts. (003-020)

In the novel's climactic escape, Heyst and Lena fled Sourabaya "in the night on board one of the Tesman schooners bound to the eastward," with Javanese boatmen hired at three o'clock in the morning. (005-114) The schooner sailed at daylight, carrying them away from Schomberg's hotel and the pursuing gang of Mr. Jones.

Schomberg, interrogating Heyst's finances in conversation with Ricardo, reveals the prevailing view: Heyst "called on the Tesmans. Why did he call on the Tesmans two days running, eh?" (015-046) The answer, Schomberg concludes, was that Heyst's liquid wealth was only "the money he had with the Tesmans" — a finite resource that would soon be exhausted. (015-051)

The Tesmans' English clerk also makes a brief appearance in the narrative, remembered for noting that Lena had "a sallow face" during her brief stay in Sourabaya. (007-010)

## Key Members

- **Mr. Tesman** — Senior partner, "a kindly, benevolent old gentleman" who received Heyst's letter of introduction. (001-013)
- **Julius Tesman** — A partner noted to be "unreasonably bitter about the Tropical Belt Coal fiasco," suggesting he bore financial or reputational losses from the venture. (004-043)
- **English clerk** — An unnamed employee, described as "respectable and highly proper," who recalled Lena's appearance in Sourabaya. (007-010)

## Notable Passages
