# Heyst's Father

## Biography

Heyst's father was an expatriated Swedish philosopher who spent his final years in a quiet London suburb, writing prolifically and nurturing a worldview of radical negation. He was, by all accounts, a man of genuine intellectual stature — "Thinker, stylist, and man of the world in his time" — who began life coveting every form of human joy but ended it in disillusionment. For more than sixty years, Conrad writes, "he had dragged on this painful earth of ours the most weary, the most uneasy soul that civilisation had ever fashioned to its ends of disillusion and regret."

His estrangement from his native Sweden was complete; he had "quarrelled with his people" and lived among his books and furniture in London, producing a body of work that analysed "space and of time, of animals and of stars" as well as "the laughter and the frowns of men, and the grimaces of their agony." His final book advanced the unsettling claim that mankind deserved "the right to absolute moral and intellectual liberty of which he no longer believed them worthy." The title of one of his collections, "Storm and Dust," suggests the elemental fury of his intellect. He died quietly in his bed, as peacefully "as a child goes to sleep," without even straightening his legs — a gentle end for a man who had spent his life "blowing blasts upon a terrible trumpet which had filled heaven and earth with ruins, while mankind went on its way unheeding."

## Role in the Novel

The elder Heyst never appears directly in the narrative — yet his presence permeates the novel. His portrait hangs on the wall of Heyst's bungalow on Samburan, a "wonderful presence in its heavy frame on the flimsy wall of mats, looking exiled and at home, out of place and masterful." For three critical years, from the time the young Axel left school at eighteen, father and son lived together while the elder Heyst wrote his last book. This period, Conrad tells us, was formative and destructive in equal measure: "Three years of such companionship at that plastic and impressionable age were bound to leave in the boy a profound mistrust of life."

The father's philosophy of detachment — of observing life without participating in it — becomes the son's governing principle. Heyst resolves to "drift" through existence "like a detached leaf drifting in the wind-currents," reasoning that "for the son of his father there was no other worthy alternative." His father's dying words become his mantra:

> "Look on—make no sound," were the last words of the man who had spent his life in blowing blasts upon a terrible trumpet which had filled heaven and earth with ruins, while mankind went on its way unheeding.
>
> (016-014)

This inheritance is both Heyst's strength and his curse. The father's intellect equipped Heyst with a formidable capacity for detachment, but it also left him utterly unprepared for the demands of love, action, and moral engagement. Late in the novel, Heyst acknowledges the genetic and philosophical legacy with painful self-awareness: "I am not for nothing the son of my father, of that man in the painting. I am he, all but the genius."

The novel's tragedy unfolds as the philosophy of negation collides with the reality of human connection. The father's voice, preserved in his books, becomes a ghostly presence that Heyst consults in moments of crisis. When he reads "Storm and Dust" after the arrival of Mr. Jones's gang, he finds himself "hearing his father's voice, speaking and ceasing to speak again," drawn into "the half-belief that something of his father dwelt yet on earth."

## Key Passages

> "Is there no guidance?" ... "You still believe in something, then?" he said in a clear voice, which had been growing feeble of late. "You believe in flesh and blood, perhaps? A full and equable contempt would soon do away with that, too. But since you have not attained to it, I advise you to cultivate that form of contempt which is called pity. It is perhaps the least difficult — always remembering that you, too, if you are anything, are as pitiful as the rest, yet never expecting any pity for yourself."
>
> (016-012)

> "And I, the son of my father, have been caught too, like the silliest fish of them all," Heyst said to himself.
>
> (016-007)

> Of the stratagems of life the most cruel is the consolation of love — the most subtle, too; for the desire is the bed of dreams.
>
> *— from "Storm and Dust" (020-020)*

> Clairvoyance or no clairvoyance, men love their captivity. To the unknown force of negation they prefer the miserably tumbled bed of their servitude. Man alone can give one the disgust of pity; yet I find it easier to believe in the misfortune of mankind than in its wickedness.
>
> *— from "Storm and Dust" (020-022)*

> His son buried the silenced destroyer of systems, of hopes, of beliefs. He observed that the death of that bitter contemner of life did not trouble the flow of life's stream, where men and women go by thick as dust, revolving and jostling one another like figures cut out of cork and weighted with lead just sufficiently to keep them in their proudly upright posture.
>
> (016-016)
