## Overview

Colonialism in *Victory* is not a political argument but an atmospheric
condition — the saturated economic environment in which all characters
move. The novel's Southeast Asian setting is a world of European trading
posts, colonial hotels, and speculative capitalist ventures. The
Tropical Belt Coal Company, with its grandiose prospectus and eventual
liquidation, embodies colonialism as "the great stride forward" that
leaves only ruins behind.

## The Tropical Belt Coal Company

The T.B.C. Co. serves as the novel's central symbol of colonial
capitalism. Its formation is described in terms of mystification:
"The world of finance is a mysterious world in which, incredible as the
fact may appear, evaporation precedes liquidation. First the capital
evaporates, and then the company goes into liquidation" (001-002).

> The world of finance is a mysterious world in which, incredible as the
> fact may appear, evaporation precedes liquidation. First the capital
> evaporates, and then the company goes into liquidation.
>
> (001-002)

Heyst, styled "manager in the tropics," remains at his post long after
the company's collapse, surrounded by the physical evidence of its
failure: "the gigantic and funereal blackboard sign of the Tropical Belt
Coal Company, still emerging from a wild growth of bushes like an
inscription stuck above a grave" (005-062).

## Heyst's Colonial Enthusiasm

Heyst himself was an agent of colonial expansion. He located coal
outcrops across the tropical belt and "being a ready letter-writer had
written pages and pages about them to his friends in Europe" (001-006).
His rhetoric of the "great stride forward" was delivered with "the
convinced wave of the hand which accompanied the phrase" (001-007).
The narrative treats this enthusiasm with gentle irony — Heyst was
persuasive "for a time, at least" (001-007).

## Colonial Infrastructure

The physical remnants of colonialism litter Samburan: the black jetty,
the rickety wharf, the company bungalows, the coal gallery driven into
the hillside. These are described as already being reclaimed by the
jungle — "the living depths of the tropical vegetation, which is more
jealous of men's conquests than the ocean" (004-007).

## Economic Hierarchy

The colonial economy creates a stratified social world: European
managers and traders at the top, Chinese labourers (the "coolies"
imported to Samburan), Malay boatmen, and the indigenous Alfuros who
"having been frightened by the sudden invasion of Chinamen" retreat to
their side of the island (016-040). This racialized economic order
is presented as background fact, not polemic.

## Colonial Types

The novel populates its world with colonial archetypes: the company
promoter with his "romantic temperament" (003-008), the innkeeper
Schomberg who trades on colonial gossip, the trader Davidson who
navigates the archipelago's waters, and the "derelict manager" Heyst
presiding over ruins.

## The Colonial Gaze

The narrative voice itself participates in the colonial perspective,
referring to "we out there" and positioning the reader among the
European trading community. This insider perspective naturalizes the
colonial setting while simultaneously exposing its absurdities — the
"deplorable lack of concentration in coal" (001-001) and the "unnatural
mysteries of the financial world" (001-005).

## Critique Through Irony

Conrad's treatment of colonialism is characteristically indirect. The
failure of the T.B.C. Co. is not a moral judgment but an economic
absurdity. The novel's critique operates through descriptive
detail — the blackboard sign becoming funereal, the jungle reclaiming
the clearing, the "evaporation" of capital — rather than explicit
condemnation.
