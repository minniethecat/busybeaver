# Schomberg's Iron Safe

## Description

The iron safe in Schomberg's Hotel is a massive object that dominates the bedroom Schomberg shares with his wife. It is the repository of his commercial life — the cashbox into which the hotel's takings are deposited each day:

> Three weeks later, after putting his cashbox away in the safe which filled with its iron bulk a corner of their bedroom, Schomberg turned towards his wife, but without looking at her exactly, and said: "I must get rid of these two. It won't do!"
>
> (012-001)

The safe's physical properties — its iron bulk, its corner-filling mass — make it impossible to ignore. It is a constant reminder of the transaction that defines Schomberg's marriage: his wife's silent domestic labor in exchange for whatever security the safe represents.

## Symbolic Meaning

The iron safe embodies Schomberg's entire moral universe. It represents the commercial values he holds paramount, the avarice that makes him vulnerable to manipulation, and the domestic control he exercises over his wife. The safe is the only thing Schomberg truly serves — more than honor, more than decency, more than his marriage.

Conrad places the scene of Schomberg's decision — to redirect Mr. Jones toward Heyst — immediately after the safekeeping of the cashbox. The juxtaposition is damning: having secured his money, Schomberg sells out Heyst. The safe is not just an object but a moral indicator, marking the moment when commercial self-interest triumphs over any residual conscience (012-023).

In the novel's economy of symbols, the iron safe stands in opposition to the father's portrait on Samburan. Both are physical objects that define the spaces they occupy, but where the portrait symbolizes the failure of philosophy, the safe symbolizes the success of baseness — the petty calculations that survive while ideals collapse.
