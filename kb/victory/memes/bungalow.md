## Overview

The Bungalow is Heyst's dwelling on Samburan (Round Island), originally
constructed as part of the Tropical Belt Coal Company's infrastructure.
After the company's failure, it becomes Heyst's isolated home, shared
first with his Chinese servant Wang and later with Lena.

The bungalow consists of two rooms surrounded by a "profuse allowance of
verandah all round" (024-058). It is one of several identical structures
in the company's clearing, the others standing empty.

## Description

The bungalow faces the company's clearing, a broad blackened space kept
free of vegetation by Wang's "judicious system of periodical firing"
(022-067). Its high-pitched roof "towered up very close, eclipsing the
sky" (022-069). The building has a verandah from which Heyst habitually
observes the island, "spread his elbows on the railing, in an easy
attitude of proprietorship" (018-001).

The central ridge of Samburan cuts off the bungalow from direct
sunrises, so that "the dwellers therein were debarred from reading early
the fortune of the newborn day" (018-001). This geographical detail
contributes to the sense of isolation and delayed awareness that
pervades the novel.

Inside, the bungalow contains Heyst's books and furniture, including a
"tall stand-up writing-desk" from which "a forgotten, solitary sheet of
paper flew up and settled gracefully on the floor" when the door was
first opened after two years of repose (022-077).

## Role in the Novel

### Heyst's Solitude

After Morrison's death, Heyst retreats to the bungalow on Samburan,
beginning what the narrative terms his "hermit life" (016-041). He
plunges himself "into an abyss of meditation over books," emerging only
when Wang announces meals with the Malay word "makan" (016-041).

### Lena's Arrival

When Heyst brings Lena to Samburan, the bungalow acquires new emotional
significance. On the first morning, Heyst stands on the verandah
observing the dawn, the domestic scene marking a departure from his
previous solitude (018-001).

### The Siege

The bungalow becomes the site of the novel's central conflict when Mr.
Jones, Martin Ricardo, and Pedro arrive on the island. The three
strangers are quartered in one of the empty bungalows nearby (022-067).
The bungalow transforms from sanctuary to prison as Heyst and Lena
find themselves besieged.

The darkness of the bungalow during this period acquires symbolic
weight. Heyst notes with approval "the absence of light in his
bungalow," which "looked as uninhabited as the others" (022-067).

### Final Scenes

The bungalow witnesses Lena's death and Heyst's final despair. On the
verandah, Heyst experiences a moment where "with his hand on the back of
the girl's chair and within a foot of her person, he had lost the sense
of her existence, for the first time since he had brought her over"
(023-023).

## Other Bungalows

The company clearing contains several bungalows, all built to the same
design. After the company's abandonment, these stand empty and are used
by Wang for storage. When Mr. Jones's party arrives, Heyst offers them
"the choice of those empty bungalows for a temporary home" (022-064).

## Key Events at the Bungalow

- Heyst's hermit-like residence after Morrison's death (016-041)
- Wang's domestic arrangements with the Alfuro woman (016-042)
- Heyst's return with Lena from Surabaya (018-001)
- Arrival of Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro (022-064)
- The siege and its tragic conclusion (023-023)
