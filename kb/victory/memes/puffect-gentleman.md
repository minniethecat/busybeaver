# Heyst's a puffect g'n'lman

## Quotation

> "Heyst's a puffect g'n'lman. Puffect! But he's a ut-uto-utopist."
>
> (001-018)

## Context

This pronouncement is delivered by an unnamed "disreputable white man" in a place of public refreshment (001-018). Heyst has just paid for the man's drink and departed when the judgment is voiced. The narrator comments on the delivery: "with that deliberate sagacity which no mere water-drinker ever attained." The remark earns Heyst yet another nickname — "Utopist" — adding to his growing collection of epithets: "Enchanted Heyst," "Hard Facts," and now "Utopist." The narrator speculates that the "Utopist" label may have been prompted by Heyst's playful invitation to old McNab: "Come along and quench your thirst with us, Mr. McNab!" — a gesture of inclusive courtesy that, in the disreputable man's view, betrays an impractical, utopian cast of mind.

## Significance

The disreputable white man's drunken pronouncement is, paradoxically, one of the novel's most penetrating insights into Heyst's character. The juxtaposition — "puffect g'n'lman" and "utopist" — captures the two halves of Heyst's nature: the impeccable courtesy and form that make him universally liked, and the unworldly idealism that makes him fatally vulnerable. A "gentleman" in Conrad's world is defined by a code of conduct, but a "utopist" is defined by an impossible vision. Heyst's tragedy is that he is both: he behaves with perfect gentlemanly decorum while believing, or half-believing, in a vision of human relations purged of self-interest and violence. The drunken man's slurred insight is more accurate than any of the novel's more articulate judgments: Heyst is a gentleman AND a utopist, and the contradiction between the two will destroy him.
