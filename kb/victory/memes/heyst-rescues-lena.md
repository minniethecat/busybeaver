## Overview

The rescue of Lena (also called Alma or Magdalen) from Zangiacomo's Ladies' Orchestra is *Victory*'s inciting event. After years of wandering detachment following the failure of the Tropical Belt Coal Company, Axel Heyst encounters a young woman performing in a shabby touring orchestra at Schomberg's hotel in Surabaya. Moved by her visible suffering, he intervenes — an act of spontaneous compassion that shatters his carefully maintained isolation, earns Schomberg's undying hatred, and ultimately leads both Heyst and Lena to Samburan and to their deaths.

## Background

By the time Heyst arrives at Schomberg's hotel, Zangiacomo's Ladies' Orchestra has been performing there for some time (008-001). The orchestra is a squalid enterprise: the women are underfed, overworked, and bullied by Zangiacomo and his "haughty mate," who drives them "truculently" onto the platform (009-024). The concerts are described as "an instrumental uproar, screaming, grunting, whining, sobbing, scraping" (008-008). Schomberg himself has been pursuing Lena with "attacks, his prayers, and his fiercest protestations" — all of which she has resisted (010-012).

## The Encounter

Heyst, staying at Schomberg's hotel "in complete ignorance that his person was odious to that worthy" (008-001), attends the concerts. He is drawn despite himself: "there is an unholy fascination in systematic noise. He did not flee from it incontinently" (008-010). Watching the women file past his table, he notices Lena — "She had captured Heyst's awakened faculty of observation; he had the sensation of a new experience" (008-016). On a later evening, during an interval, the girl tells Heyst about herself: she is "almost a child of the streets," her father a musician in "the orchestras of small theatres" (009-004).

## The Rescue

The exact mechanics of the rescue are deliberately elided by Conrad — Davidson and the narrator only learn of it secondhand. Davidson's account frames it plainly: "This was in its essence the rescue of a distressed human being" (005-118). Heyst simply takes Lena away from the hotel, escaping Schomberg's predatory orbit and the orchestra's exploitation. Schomberg's reaction is volcanic: "It's easy to imagine Schomberg's humiliation, his shocked fury, when he discovered that the girl who had for weeks resisted his attacks...had been snatched from under his very nose" (010-012). Heyst's action — unpremeditated, compassionate, and oblivious to consequences — is both his finest moral moment and the seed of his destruction.

> This was in its essence the rescue of a distressed human being.
>
> (005-118)

## Aftermath

The rescue transforms Heyst from detached observer to committed actor. Schomberg's hatred becomes "a recognised entertainment" at the hotel, where guests gather to "go and hear his abuse of Heyst" — a more successful draw than the Zangiacomo concerts themselves (010-013). More significantly, the rescue binds Heyst and Lena together, sending them to Samburan where their isolation will eventually be invaded by Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro. Heyst's single act of engagement with the world brings him love — and, in the novel's tragic logic, death.
