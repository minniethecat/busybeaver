# Love and Passion

## Definition

Love and passion in *Victory* form the counter-force to the novel's governing principle of detachment. Where Heyst's philosophy counsels withdrawal, indifference, and the refusal of entanglement, love demands precisely the opposite: connection, vulnerability, and the surrender of self-possession. Conrad presents love not as a sentimental ideal but as an existential test — the one claim that Heyst's sceptical intellect cannot reason away. The novel tracks three distinct forms of passion: Heyst's awakening tenderness for Lena (genuine love struggling against philosophical habit), Schomberg's thwarted sexual obsession (predatory desire masquerading as affection), and Ricardo's fascinated submission to Mr. Jones (a homoerotic devotion expressed through the language of gentleman-worship). The word "love" appears 48 times in the novel, while "passion" and its variants occur 20 times. Together they trace the arc of Heyst's transformation from detached observer to engaged — and ultimately doomed — participant in human life.

The narrator's early observation frames love as fundamentally incompatible with Heyst's way of being: "No, I've never killed a man or loved a woman — not even in my thoughts, not even in my dreams" (P10-003). Heyst himself then declares: "To slay, to love — the greatest enterprises of life upon a man! And I have no experience of either" (P10-004). The pairing is deliberate and ominous: love and killing are the two acts that commit a man irrevocably to the world, and Heyst will experience both before the novel's end.

## In the Novel

### Heyst and Lena: Love Against Detachment

The central love story of *Victory* is the relationship between Heyst and Lena — a relationship that begins in compassion and ends in tragedy. Heyst's first impulse toward Lena is not romantic but something closer to pity: "He could not defend himself from compassion" (P05-010). When she demands that he act rather than merely advise, his response is characteristically detached yet transformative: "I am not rich enough to buy you out... but I can always steal you" (P05-012). This is Heyst's first assertion of will in the novel — his first movement from observation to action — and it is love, or the seed of love, that prompts it.

The narrator reflects on what this means for a man who had "no connection with earthly affairs and passions" (P03-031). Heyst's elopement with Lena strikes the colonial community as "the greatest wonder of all, astonishing and amusing" (P03-031) — precisely because it violates everything they thought they knew about him. Love has done what philosophy could not: it has made Heyst act.

Yet Heyst struggles to name what he feels. When Lena pleads, "You should try to love me!" his response reveals the chasm between his feeling and his expression: "Try! But it seems to me —" He breaks off, unable to complete the thought (P10-013). Conrad comments:

> That girl, seated in her chair in graceful quietude, was to him like a script in an unknown language, or even more simply mysterious: like any writing to the illiterate. As far as women went he was altogether uninstructed and he had not the gift of intuition... in a world in which love itself rests as much on antagonism as on attraction.
>
> (P10-019)

This passage is crucial: it reveals that Heyst's famous detachment is not merely chosen but also, in part, an incapacity. He cannot read Lena because he has never learned the language of intimacy. "Love itself rests as much on antagonism as on attraction" — Conrad suggests that the very qualities that make love vital (struggle, risk, vulnerability) are what Heyst has spent his life avoiding.

The novel's most devastating aphorism on love appears when Heyst reads his father's book:

> Of the stratagems of life the most cruel is the consolation of love — the most subtle, too; for the desire is the bed of dreams.
>
> (P10-001)

Love is a "stratagem" — a trick played by life on the living — because it promises consolation while delivering vulnerability. It makes Heyst care, and caring is what destroys him. The father's philosophy, which Heyst has inherited, views love as an illusion; the novel tests whether that philosophy can survive the reality of another person's need.

### Lena's Love: The Silent Devotion

Lena's love for Heyst is expressed not in philosophical reflection but in physical presence and, ultimately, in sacrifice. She "gave him a stealthy glance of passionate appreciation" (P09-018) when he takes her away from Schomberg, but her feelings run deeper than gratitude. Her love is protective — she shields Heyst from knowledge of the danger she perceives, and in the novel's climax she gives her life attempting to disarm Ricardo. Lena's love is the novel's only unambiguous moral force: it redeems nothing, since both lovers die, but it demonstrates a capacity for self-transcendence that Heyst's philosophy never anticipated.

Conrad makes clear that Lena's love is not naive. She understands the precariousness of her position, asking Heyst: "sometimes it seems to me that you can never love me for myself, only for myself, as people do love each other when it is to be forever" (P10-015). Her yearning is for a love that sees and values her intrinsic self, not her utility as companion or object of rescue. This is the demand that Heyst, for all his goodwill, cannot quite meet — and his failure to do so is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.

### Schomberg: Passion as Possession

Schomberg's "belated passion" (P09-018) for Lena is the novel's study in predatory desire. The narrator describes him as a "mature victim of belated passion" — the word "victim" is ironic, since Schomberg is the predator, but it captures the way his obsession consumes him from within. His pursuit of Lena is entirely possessive: "He had little doubt of his personal fascination, and still less of his power to get hold of the girl" (P05-027). The phrase "get hold of" recurs and reveals the nature of his feeling: Lena is an object to be acquired, not a person to be known.

When Lena escapes with Heyst, Schomberg's passion curdles into hatred: "his mind was perverted by the pangs of wounded vanity and of thwarted passion" (P05-031). This thwarted passion becomes the engine of the novel's second half — it drives Schomberg to conspire with Jones and Ricardo against Heyst, setting in motion the events that lead to the final catastrophe. Conrad thus traces a causal chain from predatory desire to murderous conspiracy: Schomberg's passion, denied its object, becomes a weapon.

### Ricardo's Infatuation: The Homoerotic Subtext

Ricardo's relationship to Mr. Jones, while not explicitly named as love, is described in the language of passionate devotion. Ricardo is "passionately" fond of cards (P06-022), but his true passion is for Jones himself. He follows Jones not for wages but out of a devotion that transcends economic calculation: "I follow a gentleman. That ain't the same thing as to serve an employer" (P08-032). His detailed, admiring narration of Jones's every gesture, his pride in Jones's composure under pressure, his insistence on Jones's unique quality among men — all register as the language of infatuation, displaced onto the social code of gentleman-worship.

Jones, for his part, "would give any woman a ten-mile berth. He can't stand them" (P06-026). This detail, coupled with Ricardo's intense homosocial bond, creates a current of implication that runs beneath the surface of their partnership. Conrad does not make this explicit — he rarely does — but the pattern of desire, submission, and violent possessiveness that characterizes their relationship mirrors and distorts the novel's central love story.

## Analysis

*Victory* presents love as the force that undoes philosophy. Heyst's entire worldview — the sceptical detachment inherited from his father, the refusal to take life seriously, the conviction that nothing matters — collapses when confronted with Lena's vulnerable, demanding presence. Love makes him care, and caring makes him mortal. The novel's bleak conclusion is not that love fails but that it succeeds too well: Heyst dies not because he couldn't love Lena but because he finally did, too late.

Conrad orchestrates a spectrum of passion to reveal love's moral ambiguity. Schomberg's desire and Heyst's tenderness share a common vocabulary — both men "want" Lena — but the difference lies in the direction of the impulse: Schomberg wants to consume, Heyst wants (however imperfectly) to protect. Yet both impulses lead to destruction. Schomberg's predatory passion destroys his business, his sanity, and ultimately others; Heyst's protective love exposes Lena to danger and costs her life. Conrad seems to suggest that passion, whatever its moral quality, is inherently dangerous — it is the price of being human, and it is always exacted in full.

The father's aphorism — "the most cruel is the consolation of love" — proves prophetic. Love consoles Heyst by breaking his isolation, but the consolation is cruel because it comes at the cost of everything. Heyst, who began the novel "like a feather floating lightly in the work-a-day atmosphere" (P03-031), ends it anchored to another person, and that anchor drags him down. Yet Conrad does not allow us to conclude that Heyst would have been better off without love. The alternative — the life of pure detachment — is revealed as a kind of death-in-life. Love may be a stratagem, a trap, a delusion, but it is also the only thing that makes Heyst fully alive, and the novel grants that transformation its full, terrible dignity.
