# The shades of death

## Quotation

> Exulting, she saw herself extended on the bed, in a black dress, and profoundly at peace; while, stooping over her with a kindly, playful smile, he was ready to lift her up in his firm arms and take her into the sanctuary of his innermost heart — forever! The flush of rapture flooding her whole being broke out in a smile of innocent, girlish happiness; and with that divine radiance on her lips she breathed her last, triumphant, seeking for his glance in the shades of death.
>
> (038-025)

## Context

This passage is the climax of the novel's closing scene (038-025). Lena has been shot by Mr. Jones — the bullet intended for Heyst has struck her instead. She dies believing she has saved Heyst, and this belief transforms her death into a victory. In her dying vision, she imagines Heyst lifting her into "the sanctuary of his innermost heart" — an image of perfect union that real life never quite permitted. Conrad gives Lena what he denies to most of his characters: a death that is genuinely triumphant, lit by "divine radiance" and sealed with "a smile of innocent, girlish happiness." The phrase "seeking for his glance in the shades of death" is the novel's final image of Lena — still reaching toward Heyst even as the darkness closes in.

## Significance

This is one of the most theologically charged passages in Conrad's fiction. The language of "divine radiance," "sanctuary," and "innermost heart" invests Lena's death with a religious weight that the novel's otherwise sceptical worldview seems to resist. Lena achieves in death what she could never quite achieve in life: complete possession of Heyst's heart. Her dying vision is arguably a fantasy — Heyst does not, in fact, lift her into any sanctuary; he is paralyzed by his "fastidious soul" and its "infernal mistrust of all life." But Conrad insists on the reality of her triumph regardless. The "shades of death" become, paradoxically, the medium of her apotheosis — she passes into darkness radiant with victory. The novel's title, *Victory*, finds its fullest meaning here: Lena's victory is not over Mr. Jones or Ricardo or the external threats to her life, but over death itself, through an act of love so complete that it transforms the moment of annihilation into a moment of triumph.
