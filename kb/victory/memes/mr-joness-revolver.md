# Mr. Jones's Revolver

## Description

Mr. Jones's revolver is a central plot device in the novel's final section. It is first mentioned in connection with the governor's six-shooter, carried by the criminal trio who arrive on Samburan. Ricardo recounts its history with a mixture of admiration and unease:

> At that time we had only one revolver between us two — the governor's six-shooter, but loaded only in five chambers; and we had no more cartridges. He had left the box behind in a drawer in his cabin.
>
> (013-003)

The revolver's lethality is demonstrated in Ricardo's tale of how Mr. Jones dispatched a man named Antonio with clinical efficiency: "snatches his revolver from under his jacket, and plugs a bullet plumb centre into Mr. Antonio's chest" (013-010). Ricardo's narration emphasizes Mr. Jones's gentlemanly economy of violence — "No confounded fuss, and things done out of hand" — and notes that after this killing, "Only four shots left, and ninety miles of wild coast to put behind us before coming to the first place where you could expect to buy revolver cartridges" (013-014).

The weapon's trajectory shifts when Heyst discovers his own revolver missing from the drawer where he kept it:

> Heyst said nothing to that naive and practical suggestion, for the object that he missed from the drawer was his revolver.
>
> (024-053)

Heyst comes to the conclusion that Wang, his Chinese servant, had taken possession of the revolver during his absence from Samburan (024-063). The weapon's disappearance leaves Heyst disarmed and defenseless — a state he confesses to Lena (027-025). Wang had purloined the revolver and the box of cartridges from the table drawer, motivated by his fear of Pedro and the strangers who had invaded Number One (029-030).

## Symbolic Meaning

The revolver represents the fundamental incompatibility between Heyst's philosophical worldview and the brutal reality he must confront. Where Heyst has lived by detachment and observation, the revolver demands action. Its theft by Wang signals the collapse of Heyst's authority — even his servant recognizes that the master is unprepared for the violence that has arrived. The weapon's trajectory through the novel traces the transfer of power from Heyst to the forces embodied by Mr. Jones and his companions.

## Wang's Theft and the Balance of Power

Wang's decision to steal the revolver is one of the novel's most consequential acts. His motive is revealed in his own words:

> It was Pedro who had been the first cause of Wang's suspicion and fear. The Chinaman had seen wild men. He had penetrated, in the train of a Chinese pedlar, up one or two of the Bornean rivers into the country of the Dyaks. He had also been in the interior of Mindanao, where there are people who live in trees — savages, no better than animals; but a hairy brute like Pedro, with his great fangs and ferocious growls, was altogether beyond his conception of anything that could be looked upon as human. The strong impression made on him by Pedro was the prime inducement which had led Wang to purloin the revolver.
>
> (029-030)

Wang's theft is thus a rational response to an irrational threat. He does not take the revolver to use against Heyst — "He did not suppose for a moment that Wang had the revolver anywhere about his person; and after having thought the matter over, he had arrived at the conclusion that the Chinaman never meant to use the weapon against him" (029-031) — but rather to protect himself from Pedro, the "hairy brute" whose very existence defies Wang's taxonomy of the human. When Heyst directly confronts Wang about the theft, the Chinaman responds with a characteristic denial and then delivers an enigmatic warning about Lena's danger, pointing at the bedroom curtain (029-042).
