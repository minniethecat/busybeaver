## Overview

New Guinea is the great island at the eastern edge of the Malay Archipelago, and in *Victory* it functions less as a geographical location than as a symbolic destination — the direction in which one vanishes. Heyst "vanished, on board an Arab ship, in the direction of New Guinea" (001-016) during his early wandering years, and the island becomes shorthand for his impulse to escape, to disappear, to break free from human entanglements.

The narrator captures the European colonial attitude toward New Guinea with an ironic aside: "A man who will go to New Guinea for fun — well!" (001-016). The island is conceived as a place beyond the pale, inhabited by "New Guinea cannibals" (009-025) and offering only "futile adventures" (009-025) for those who venture there. It is not a destination one reaches but a direction one flees toward.

## Role in the Novel

New Guinea appears in the novel as the vanishing point of Heyst's restless trajectory. When the "enchantment" of the Malay isles threatens to trap him, "he used to make a bolt clear out of sight in his attempts to break away from the enchantment of 'these isles,' either in the direction of New Guinea or in the direction of the Philippines" (003-020). The island is part of a pattern: Heyst's life is a series of escapes, and New Guinea is the furthest horizon of his flight.

The irony is that Heyst never truly reaches New Guinea. His bolt toward the primitive, the ungoverned, the "silent" forests (009-025) is always cut short — by Morrison's gratitude, by the Tropical Belt Coal Company, by Lena. The escape becomes the trap. Samburan, not New Guinea, becomes his final destination.
