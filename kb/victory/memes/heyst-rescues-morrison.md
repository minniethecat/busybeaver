# Heyst Rescues Morrison

## Summary

Before the main action of the novel, Axel Heyst encounters Morrison, a struggling British trader whose ship has been impounded by Portuguese authorities in the Dutch East Indies. Morrison faces financial ruin and is nearly "off his chump" with worry (002-010). Heyst, moved by pity, intervenes — lending or guaranteeing the funds needed to redeem the vessel (002-051). The rescue is a breach of his father's philosophy of non-involvement and becomes the defining act of his public identity.

## Context

The rescue occurs during Heyst's "aimless pilgrimage" through the Malay Archipelago after his father's death. It is described retrospectively by the narrator and by Davidson, who pieces together the story from fragments of colonial gossip. Morrison is a decent but impractical man, and Heyst's intervention is entirely gratuitous — he has nothing to gain and everything to lose by becoming entangled (002-054).

Morrison's reaction captures the ambiguity of the act:

> "Forgive me, Heyst. You must have been sent by God in answer to my prayer. But I have been nearly off my chump for three days with worry; and it suddenly struck me: 'What if it's the Devil who has sent him?'"
>
> (002-041)

## Key Participants

- **Axel Heyst** — the rescuer, whose compassion overrides his philosophical principles
- **Morrison** — the rescued trader, whose gratitude becomes a burden to Heyst's solitude

## Consequences

The rescue has far-reaching consequences. Morrison's lavish gratitude makes Heyst uncomfortable and brands him in the colonial imagination (002-054). After Morrison's death (during a visit to England), Schomberg spreads the rumor that Heyst murdered Morrison for his money — a slander that follows Heyst throughout the novel (003-020). The rescue also awakens in Heyst a capacity for pity that will later draw him to Lena, setting the entire tragic plot in motion.
