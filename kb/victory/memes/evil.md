# Evil

## Definition

Evil in *Victory* is not an abstract theological category but a concrete, personified force. It enters the novel in the form of three "desperadoes" — Mr. Jones, Martin Ricardo, and Pedro — whose arrival on Samburan transforms the island from a refuge into a death trap. Conrad presents evil not as supernatural but as a fact of human nature that cannot be avoided through withdrawal:

> "Forgive me, Heyst. You must have been sent by God in answer to my prayer. But I have been nearly off my chump for three days with worry; and it suddenly struck me: 'What if it's the Devil who has sent him?'"
>
> (002-041)

## In the Novel

### Mr. Jones as Embodied Evil

Mr. Jones is the novel's most explicit representation of evil. Described as a "spectre" with "hollow eyes," he exerts a cold, dispassionate malevolence that contrasts with Ricardo's animal violence. Jones is a gentleman by birth (011-038) who has chosen evil as his vocation — a perversion of the aristocratic ideal that parallels Heyst's perversion of the philosophical ideal:

> Being answered with derisive civility, he became confirmed in his sudden conviction of their desperate character. The way Mr. Jones turned his hollow eyes on one, like an incurious spectre, and the way the other, when addressed, suddenly retracted his lips and exhibited his teeth without looking round — here was evidence enough to settle that point.
>
> (012-019)

### The Test of Detachment

Evil in *Victory* functions primarily as the test that Heyst's detachment cannot pass. Heyst has constructed a life designed to avoid entanglement — but evil does not respect boundaries. When Jones and his gang arrive, Heyst can no longer "look on and make no sound" (016-014). The novel's tragedy unfolds precisely because Heyst's philosophical habits have left him unprepared for decisive action against genuine malevolence.

## Critical Perspectives

Conrad's treatment of evil in *Victory* draws on both the Gothic tradition (the spectral Jones, the bestial Pedro) and the philosophical tradition of Schopenhauer and Nietzsche. Evil is simultaneously real — it kills Lena — and banal — it arrives through the petty cowardice of Schomberg. This double nature makes it impossible to dismiss or escape.
