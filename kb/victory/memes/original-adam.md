# "There must be a lot of the original Adam in me, after all."

## Quotation

> "There must be a lot of the original Adam in me, after all."
>
> (016-004)

## Context

Heyst speaks this line to Lena after rescuing her from Schomberg's hotel and bringing her to Samburan. It is a moment of rare self-awareness for a character defined by his emotional distance. The "original Adam" is the prelapsarian man — the human being before the fall into civilization and philosophy. Heyst is admitting that beneath the layers of his father's teaching, beneath the studied detachment, there is something elemental and ungovernable: the capacity for love, for pity, for action.

## Significance

This is Heyst's most honest self-assessment in the novel. It acknowledges the failure of his philosophical program before the events of Parts III and IV have even tested it. The admission is gentle, almost humorous — a shrug at the persistence of his own humanity. But it carries the tragic irony of the entire novel: Heyst *knows* he is not really detached, yet he has structured his entire life around the pretense that he is.

The quotation also introduces the Edenic theme that runs through *Victory*. Samburan is an attempted Eden, Lena is an attempted Eve, and Mr. Jones is the serpent. Heyst's "original Adam" cannot stay in the garden — the fall is inevitable, not because of divine punishment but because human nature cannot be suppressed indefinitely.
