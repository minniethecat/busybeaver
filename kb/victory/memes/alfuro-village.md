## Overview

The Alfuro village is a native settlement on the island of Samburan, home to the indigenous Alfuro people who inhabit the island alongside Heyst's solitary European presence. The village exists as a separate, parallel world to Heyst's bungalow and the abandoned Tropical Belt Coal Company buildings — a reminder that Samburan, despite Heyst's isolation, is not an empty island but one with an existing human community (016-041).

The village's presence on Samburan establishes the layered social geography of the island: Heyst's European compound, the company's derelict infrastructure, and the native village all coexist in uneasy proximity. The Alfuro people remain largely in the background of the narrative, observed rather than engaged with, reflecting the colonial distance between Heyst and the island's original inhabitants.

## Role in the Novel

The Alfuro village functions primarily as setting and atmospheric context rather than as a direct participant in the plot. Its inhabitants are glimpsed but rarely heard, their presence contributing to the sense of Samburan as a place where civilizations brush against each other without truly meeting. The village underscores Heyst's position as an outsider — he is isolated not only from European society but also from the indigenous community that shares his island.
