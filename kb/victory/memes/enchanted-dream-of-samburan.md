# The enchanted dream of Samburan

## Quotation

> Duplicity — the refuge of the weak and the cowardly, but of the disarmed, too! Nothing stood between the enchanted dream of her existence and a cruel catastrophe but her duplicity.
>
> (027-062)

## Context

This passage occurs during Lena's confrontation with Ricardo in Part IV (027-062). The three invaders — Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro — have arrived on Samburan, and Ricardo has cornered Lena alone in the bungalow. He attempts to recruit her as an accomplice, suggesting she "stand in with us." Lena, summoning a courage she did not know she possessed, forces herself to smile and play along. The narrator captures her inner state in this extraordinary sentence, which transforms the morally loaded concept of "duplicity" into something heroic. Lena's deception is not the duplicity of the weak and cowardly — it is the duplicity of the "disarmed," the only weapon available to a woman who has no physical power but a fierce determination to protect the man she loves.

## Significance

This passage is a turning point in the novel's treatment of Lena. Until now, she has been largely passive — rescued by Heyst, grateful, devoted, but reactive. Here, she becomes the active defender of their shared life. Conrad redefines duplicity, stripping it of its conventional moral stigma and re-casting it as the resource of the powerless. The phrase "the enchanted dream of her existence" captures the fragile beauty of what she and Heyst have built together on Samburan — an idyll so precarious that only her cunning stands between it and annihilation. The passage also deepens the novel's central irony: Heyst, who prides himself on his philosophy of detachment and his world-weariness, is being protected by a woman whose "duplicity" he would, in his fastidiousness, probably despise — if he knew about it.
