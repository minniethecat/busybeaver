# Pedro

## Biography

Pedro is a Colombian alligator-hunter who becomes the third member of Mr. Jones's criminal trio. He and his brother Antonio lived in a jungle hut on the Colombian coast, where they encountered Jones and Ricardo hiding out after a failed treasure raid. When the brothers plotted to kill their guests for their possessions, Jones shot Antonio dead through the chest. Ricardo wrestled Pedro to the ground and strangled him, then Jones ordered him tied by the neck to a tree for two days. This brutal breaking — "Being tied up short by the neck like this would daunt the arch devil himself" — transformed Pedro from a hostile equal into a devoted follower, more dog than man in his loyalty.

Ricardo reflects on this transformation with a keeper's pride: "When I first saw him he was fit to frighten a grizzly bear, let alone a Chinaman. He's become civilised now to what he once was." Pedro had no known name beyond "Pedro" or "Peter" and was, as Jones introduced him, a "Citizen of Colombia" whose occupation was hunting alligators. The real Pedro, Conrad reveals in his Author's Note, was an actual person he encountered briefly in a hovel — an encounter that ended with Conrad fleeing "through the wall" from the man's inexplicable, terrifying rage.

> My contact with the faithful Pedro was much shorter and my observation of him was less complete but incomparably more anxious. It ended in a sudden inspiration to get out of his way. ... This bestial apparition and a certain enormous buck nigger encountered in Haiti only a couple of months afterwards, have fixed my conception of blind, furious, unreasoning rage, as manifested in the human animal, to the end of my days.
>
> (P01-009)

## Role in the Novel

Pedro functions as the physical embodiment of brute force within Jones's gang. Where Jones represents spectral, gentlemanly evil and Ricardo is the cunning, cat-like predator, Pedro is pure bestial violence — muscle without mind, ferocity without calculation. Conrad describes him with a consistent zoological vocabulary: his "fangs," his "tangled beard," his "little bear's eyes," his "great hairy paw," his movements like "a creature caught in the woods and taught to walk on its hind legs."

Schomberg, for all his fear of Jones and Ricardo, finds Pedro almost reassuring by comparison: "Pedro, at any rate, was just a simple, straightforward brute, if a murderous one. There was no mystery about him, nothing uncanny ... Pedro with his fangs, his tangled beard and queer stare of his little bear's eyes was, by comparison, delightfully natural."

On Samburan, Pedro is domesticated into a cook and porter, yet his animal nature remains barely contained. He is the first to discover Lena's presence on the island, glimpsing her and gaping "in gaping astonishment" before rushing off on his "bandy legs" to tell his masters. Wang, Heyst's Chinese servant, is terrified of him: "a hairy brute like Pedro, with his great fangs and ferocious growls, was altogether beyond his conception of anything that could be looked upon as human." It is Pedro's presence that drives Wang to steal Heyst's revolver — a small decision with enormous consequences for the novel's climax.

Pedro survives the events on Samburan and is last seen at the jetty, where Ricardo leaves him under threat of his "sudden-death air" to guard the boat. His final role is as a witness to the catastrophe, the last of Jones's gang left standing when the smoke clears.
