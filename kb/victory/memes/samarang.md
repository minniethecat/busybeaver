## Overview

Samarang (the colonial-era spelling of modern Semarang) is a port city on the north coast of Java. In *Victory*, it appears as a place of commerce and financial transactions — the location of the Tesman Brothers firm where Heyst conducted his business. It represents, in the novel's geography, the world of European mercantile order: ledgers, bills, and the careful management of "spare cash" (004-043).

The city is mentioned in connection with Heyst's dutiful financial arrangements before his final departure. "He had left all his spare cash with the Tesmans, in Samarang, to meet certain bills which would fall due while he was away on his cruise" (002-029). This detail — a man carefully settling his accounts before vanishing into the wilderness — captures the paradox at the heart of Heyst's character: the detached wanderer who nonetheless maintains the forms of commercial obligation.

## Role in the Novel

Samarang serves as a minor but significant node in the novel's mercantile geography. It is where Heyst's financial lifeline to the civilized world is anchored, where the Tesman firm operates, and where the practical structures that sustain his wandering are maintained. The city's brief appearance in the narrative — "At the very first call he made in Samarang he rolled the shawl as tightly as he could" (007-001) — connects it to the novel's subplot involving Lena's shawl, a gift from Heyst that becomes a symbolic token.

As a colonial port, Samarang belongs to the same world as Sourabaya: a place of European countinghouses, Chinese traders, Arab ships, and the ceaseless flow of commodities that drive the colonial economy. It is, in Conrad's vision, a place both necessary and stifling — the world Heyst must escape but cannot entirely abandon (004-007).
