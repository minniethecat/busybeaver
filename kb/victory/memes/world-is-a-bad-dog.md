# "The world is a bad dog. It will bite you if you give it a chance."

## Quotation

> "The world is a bad dog. It will bite you if you give it a chance; but I think that here we can safely defy the fates."
>
> (006-045)

## Context

Heyst speaks these words to Lena not long after bringing her to Samburan. They are in the bungalow, and Heyst is expressing a tentative optimism — perhaps on this remote island, cut off from the world of colonial commerce and gossip, they can escape the "bad dog" of human society. The metaphor is characteristically Heystian: sardonic, self-aware, and fundamentally wrong.

## Significance

The quotation carries the novel's deepest dramatic irony. Heyst believes Samburan is a sanctuary where the fates can be defied, but the island proves to be the very place where the world bites hardest. The "bad dog" of the world — in the form of Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro — follows Heyst to Samburan precisely because of his detachment. You cannot escape the world by withdrawing from it; the world finds you.

The phrase also reveals Heyst's pessimism as incomplete. He knows the world is dangerous, but he fails to recognize that passivity is not protection. The tragic arc of *Victory* demonstrates that the only way to "defy the fates" is to engage with them — to act, as Lena acts. Heyst's attempt to avoid the bite only ensures it will be fatal when it comes.
