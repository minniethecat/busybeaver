## Overview

Timor is an island in the eastern Malay Archipelago that plays a pivotal role in the early chapters of Heyst's story. Conrad introduces it with the memorable line: "One day Heyst turned up in Timor" (002-001), immediately establishing the island as a place of unexpected arrivals and fateful encounters. The narrator muses, "Why in Timor, of all places in the world, no one knows" (002-001), underscoring the apparent randomness of Heyst's wanderings — randomness that, in Conrad's world, conceals deeper patterns of destiny.

It is in Timor that Heyst encounters Morrison, the indebted captain of the Capricorn, and performs the act of generosity that will haunt the rest of his life: he lends Morrison the money to pay off his creditors. This single act — "the rescue of Morrison," as it becomes known — establishes the pattern of Heyst's relationship to humanity: detached benevolence followed by retreat.

## Role in the Novel

Timor represents the point of entry into Heyst's past. The novel's narrative structure circles back to Timor as the site of the "Morrison episode," the event that defines Heyst's reputation among the European merchants of the archipelago. The narrator reflects that "the fact of his turning up in Timor or anywhere else was no more wonderful than the settling of a sparrow on one's windowsill at any given moment" (002-036), yet the consequences of this seemingly chance arrival ripple through the entire novel.

The island functions as a threshold: it is where Heyst steps from his anonymous wandering into a web of obligations and resentments. Morrison's gratitude leads to the ill-fated Tropical Belt Coal Company, which leads to Samburan, which leads to Lena, Mr. Jones, and tragedy. Timor is the butterfly wing whose flap becomes a hurricane.
