# Black Diamond Bay

## Description

Black Diamond Bay is the bay on the northern shore of Samburan that served as the landing point and operational base of the Tropical Belt Coal Company. Conrad describes it as a quiet, sheltered sweep of water — a natural harbour rendered functional by a rickety wharf and a prominent blackboard bearing the company's initials. The bay's name is a wry allusion to coal, the "black diamonds" that Heyst dreamed would power a "great stride forward" for the region (001-001).

For a time the name carried official weight: the location was "known officially as Black Diamond Bay" (004-001). But the company's collapse reduced it to an abandoned outpost. Davidson, the merchant captain who kept a watchful eye on Heyst, reported that "the jungle has choked the very sheds in Black Diamond Bay" (004-020).

## Role in the Novel

Black Diamond Bay is the threshold of Heyst's isolation — the point where the outside world makes contact with his hermitage, and where the novel's violence finally erupts. It is here that Heyst and Morrison first landed and named the place, staking a claim that would outlast the enterprise itself (016-041). The bay's "quiet sweep" becomes the amphitheatre for the novel's most brutal moment: Ricardo's savage attack on Pedro, the crash of the blow resounding "all over the quiet sweep of Black Diamond Bay" (021-051).

In the novel's final passages, the bay's headland appears as a dark silhouette: "Beyond the headland of Diamond Bay, lying black on a purple sea, great masses of cloud stood piled up and bathed in a mist of blood" (033-101). The image fuses the bay with the novel's closing atmosphere of ruin and resignation.
