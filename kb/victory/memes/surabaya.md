# Surabaya

## Description

Surabaya (spelled "Sourabaya" in Conrad's text) is the bustling colonial port on the north coast of Java that serves as the commercial and social center of the novel's expatriate community. It is a town of traders, shipping agents, hotel-keepers, and drifters — the quintessential Conradian colonial setting where European commerce and Asian geography intersect:

> "Sourabaya," he would say distinctly, and would watch the discouragement at the outlandish sound coming into her eyes, which were fastened on his face.
>
> (009-012)

The town represents the "civilized" world from which Heyst withdraws (004-043). Its social life revolves around Schomberg's Hotel, the harbor, and the trading houses. For the European characters, Surabaya is a place of business, gossip, and temporary refuge — never a home.

## Role in the Novel

Surabaya functions as the novel's baseline of "normal" colonial life against which Samburan's isolation is measured. It is from Surabaya that Heyst rescues Morrison (002-009), from Surabaya that he takes Lena to Samburan, and to Surabaya that he briefly returns to buy supplies:

> "I never knew him even as that — not till this time, when you were so obliging as to take me to Sourabaya, I went to stay there from economy. The Netherlands House is very expensive, and they expect you to bring your own servant with you. It's a nuisance."
>
> (006-032)

The town embodies the gravitational pull of society that Heyst resists. Every character passes through Surabaya: Davidson the sea captain, Morrison the doomed trader, Schomberg the gossip, Lena the captive, and the three desperadoes who will ultimately destroy Heyst's refuge.
