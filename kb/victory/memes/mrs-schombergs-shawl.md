# Mrs. Schomberg's Shawl

## Description

Mrs. Schomberg's shawl is an Indian garment that appears at three crucial points in the narrative. It first serves as the wrapping for Lena's belongings when she flees the Schomberg hotel. Mrs. Schomberg, acting on a compassionate impulse that defies her husband's cruelty, gathers Lena's things and ties them in her own shawl:

> I got her things together, tied them up in my own shawl, and threw them into the compound out of a back window.
>
> (005-080)

Davidson later appears with the shawl hanging over his arm, identifying it to Heyst: "This is Mrs. Schomberg's shawl... An Indian thing, I believe" (006-027). The shawl becomes a token of connection between the isolated world of Samburan and the wider human community. Davidson undertakes to return it secretly to Mrs. Schomberg (006-040), a mission he accomplishes with surprising ease (007-001).

## Symbolic Meaning

The shawl represents one of the novel's few gestures of disinterested human kindness. Mrs. Schomberg's act — wrapping a stranger's belongings in her own possession — stands in quiet contrast to the predatory relationships that dominate the narrative. The shawl's journey from the hotel to the island and back again traces a thread of decency that survives despite the novel's accumulating darkness. It is a small, domestic object that carries outsized narrative significance as a token of solidarity between women and of Davidson's unobtrusive goodness.

## The Shawl's Journey

The shawl's physical journey across the narrative traces a geography of kindness. From Mrs. Schomberg's hands, it travels with Lena to the jetty, where Wang loads it onto the truck:

> The practical and automatic Wang had got hold of one of the little trucks that had been used for running baskets of coal alongside ships. He appeared pushing it before him, loaded lightly with Heyst's bag and the bundle of the girl's belongings, wrapped in Mrs. Schomberg's shawl.
>
> (018-001)

The shawl thus accompanies Lena across the threshold from the corrupt world of Sourabaya to the deceptive sanctuary of Samburan. On the wharf, Davidson presents it to Heyst with characteristic tact:

> "This is Mrs. Schomberg's shawl." He touched the stuff hanging over his arm. "An Indian thing, I believe," he added, glancing at his arm sideways.
>
> (006-027)

Heyst's concern for the shawl's return reveals an unexpected dimension of his character — a scrupulous attention to the social consequences that might befall Mrs. Schomberg if she cannot account for the missing garment. "He said that it might be very awkward for her if she were unable, if asked, to produce it" (006-030). This fastidious concern for a woman he has never met is characteristic of Heyst's peculiar code.

Davidson's return of the shawl is achieved with characteristic directness. Arriving at the hotel during Schomberg's siesta, he "rolled the shawl as tightly as he could into the smallest possible brown paper parcel" and left it for Mrs. Schomberg, an act of quiet decency that goes unremarked by the hotel's other inhabitants (007-001).
