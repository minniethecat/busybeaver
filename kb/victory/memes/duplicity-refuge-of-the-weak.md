# "Duplicity — the refuge of the weak"

## Quotation

> Duplicity — the refuge of the weak and the cowardly, but of the disarmed, too! Nothing stood between the enchanted dream of her existence and a cruel catastrophe but her duplicity.
>
> (027-062)

## Context

Lena faces Ricardo in the bungalow while Heyst is absent. Ricardo has proposed that she betray Heyst and join him and Mr. Jones. She has been "directed in the way of duplicity" and makes "a heroically conscious effort" to smile at her enemy (027-061). The narrative voice steps back to offer this extraordinary reflection: duplicity is the refuge of the weak — but also of those who have no other weapon. Lena, unarmed and alone, must use deception to protect the man she loves.

## Significance

This passage performs one of Conrad's characteristic moral reversals. The novel has already established duplicity as the defining trait of the "civilized" world (Schomberg's lies, the Tropical Belt Coal Company's fraud, the world's slander of Heyst). But here duplicity is reframed as the last defense of the powerless. Lena, who has never been taught moral philosophy, instinctively understands what Heyst's elaborate detachment cannot: that in a world of predators, the disarmed must sometimes fight with cunning.

The phrase "enchanted dream of her existence" captures both the beauty and fragility of Lena's life on Samburan. Her happiness is real, but it is a dream — and dreams end. The passage foreshadows the catastrophe to come while simultaneously honoring Lena's courage in facing it.
