## Context

Alone in the bungalow while Heyst is away, Lena confronts Mr. Jones. She has resolved to use her beauty and her "duplicity" — the one weapon available to her — to protect Heyst from the invaders. In this moment of solitary courage, she looks at Jones and understands him with an intuitive clarity that the philosophical Heyst cannot match. Jones is not merely a criminal; he is a metaphysical presence — evil made flesh.

## The Quote

> Nothing stood between the enchanted dream of her existence and a cruel catastrophe but her duplicity. It seemed to her that the man sitting there before her was an unavoidable presence, which had attended all her life. He was the embodied evil of the world. She was not ashamed of her duplicity. With a woman's frank courage, as soon as she saw that she must make a choice, she threw away all reluctance, everything — remembered all that she had heard, and had seen, and had guessed out of her short, hard experience in the world.
>
> (027-062)

## Significance

This passage is the moral centre of *Victory*'s final act. Where Heyst theorises about shadows and shades, Lena sees and acts. Her identification of Jones as "the embodied evil of the world" is not philosophical but experiential — it comes from "her short, hard experience in the world," the very experience Heyst has spent his life avoiding. The phrase "unavoidable presence, which had attended all her life" suggests that Lena has been waiting for this confrontation — that her hard-won knowledge of the world has prepared her for exactly this moment. Her "duplicity" — the deception she practises on Jones — is not a moral failing but a weapon of the disarmed. The passage inverts the novel's gender dynamics: the man philosophises; the woman acts. Lena's choice to face evil alone, using only her wits and her courage, makes her — not Heyst — the novel's true hero.
