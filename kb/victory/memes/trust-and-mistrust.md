## Overview

Trust, and its absence, defines the psychological landscape of *Victory*. Heyst's father's teaching instilled in him a "profound mistrust of life" (010-003) — a conviction that human relationships are inherently deceptive and that the wise man withdraws. This mistrust, forged during "three years of such companionship at that plastic and impressionable age" (010-003), becomes the filter through which Heyst views the world.

The novel traces Heyst's halting, painful movement toward trust — specifically, toward trusting Lena. Their relationship is the crucible in which his father's philosophy is tested. Can Heyst trust this young woman who has been passed from man to man, who has every reason to be calculating, who arrives in his life under circumstances that any cynic would call suspicious? The answer the novel gives is both yes and no: yes, Lena is worthy of trust; but no, trust cannot save them.

## Role in the Novel

The theme of mistrust pervades the novel's social world. The European merchants "in hatred and mistrust" (005-113) pursue their rivalries. Schomberg's gossip poisons Heyst's reputation. Mr. Jones and Ricardo operate in an atmosphere of mutual suspicion that is as dangerous to each other as to their victims. Against this background, the tentative trust between Heyst and Lena — incomplete, unspoken, never fully realized — becomes the novel's fragile moral center.
