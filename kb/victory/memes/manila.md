# Manila

## Description

Manila appears in *Victory* as a peripheral node in the geography of the novel's colonial underworld. It is never described in any physical detail — no streets, no buildings, no harbor scenes. Conrad mentions it only in passing, yet these mentions are freighted with narrative significance. Manila is the outer limit of Heyst's "magic circle" — "a circle with a radius of eight hundred miles drawn round a point in North Borneo" that "just touched Manila" (001-011). It marks the boundary of the archipelago world within which Heyst has chosen to drift.

The city's most consequential role is as the point of origin for the three desperadoes — Mr. Jones, Ricardo, and Pedro — who will ultimately destroy Heyst's refuge. They were "fired out from Manila" (032-015) before making their way to Sourabaya and Schomberg's hotel. Manila thus functions as a kind of staging ground for the novel's violence: an unseen place of criminal exile from which malevolent forces are launched into the Malay Archipelago.

## Role in the Novel

Manila's principal narrative function is to establish the credentials — and the menace — of Mr. Jones and his companions. When Schomberg challenges Jones about how he knows his name, Jones replies casually: "In Manila. From a man with whom I had a game of cards one evening in the Hotel Castille" (011-023). This reference to the Hotel Castille, unspecified but resonant, plants Jones in the world of colonial hotels, card games, and drifting expatriates — a world Schomberg himself inhabits.

The exchange also reveals the network of gossip and grievance that connects the novel's scattered locations. The unnamed man in Manila had called Schomberg "all the names he could think of" (011-023), referring to some old scandal "in Bangkok." This web of remembered slights and slanders — stretching from Bangkok to Manila to Sourabaya — is the social fabric of Conrad's colonial world.

For Heyst, Manila represents the furthest reach of his wanderings — a place he "had been seen" (001-011), suggesting a brief and perhaps failed attempt to escape the gravitational pull of the islands. The narrator speculates: "Perhaps these were his attempts to break out. If so, they were failures. The enchantment must have been an unbreakable one" (001-011). Manila, along with Saigon, is a test of the limits of Heyst's commitment to his rootless existence, and he does not pass beyond it.

Ricardo's later admission — "Haven't I heard you, sir, saying more than twenty times since we got fired out from Manila that we should want a lot of capital to work the East Coast with?" (032-015) — reveals Manila as the place where Jones and his gang's plans were first frustrated, setting in motion the desperate gambit that culminates in the tragedy on Samburan.

## Analysis

Manila in *Victory* exemplifies Conrad's technique of geographic suggestion — the art of making a place significant through minimal, precise reference. The city receives no more than a handful of sentences across the entire novel, yet it carries considerable structural weight. It functions as a narrative linchpin, connecting the novel's central characters and setting in motion the chain of events that leads to the final catastrophe.

The city occupies a distinct position in the novel's geography of civilization and wilderness. Unlike London — the distant metropolitan center of finance and philosophy — or Sourabaya — the bustling colonial port where the expatriate community gathers at Schomberg's hotel — Manila exists at the narrative periphery. It is mentioned only in recollection and hearsay. No scene takes place there. It is a place characters have left, or been expelled from, before the novel's present action begins.

This "past-tense" quality is thematically significant. The novel's world is one of aftermaths and consequences. Heyst's father has died in London before the story opens; the T.B.C. Company has already failed; Jones and Ricardo have already been run out of Manila. The present action is always shadowed by prior events that occurred elsewhere — and Manila is the key "elsewhere" for the novel's antagonists.

The designation of Manila as the edge of Heyst's "magic circle" also carries symbolic resonance. If the circle represents Heyst's self-imposed limitation — his decision to drift within the Malay Archipelago rather than return to Europe — then Manila and Saigon mark the furthest extent of his commitment to this life. He has "been seen" at these edge points, but he has never crossed beyond them. Manila is thus a kind of threshold: the last outpost of the colonial world before the open Pacific, and the limit of Heyst's willingness to engage with the wider world.

For Jones and Ricardo, Manila is something different: a site of failure from which they must recover. Being "fired out from Manila" (032-015) is the disgrace that fuels Ricardo's desperate ambition. His plan to extract money from Heyst is a direct response to this humiliation — an attempt to acquire the "capital to work the East Coast" that they could not obtain by legitimate (or semi-legitimate) means. The violence that erupts on Samburan thus has its origins in a Manila none of the other characters have ever seen.
