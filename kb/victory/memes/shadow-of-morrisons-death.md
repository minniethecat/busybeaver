# The shadow of Morrison's death

## Quotation

> Be this as it may, he suddenly reappeared in the world, broad chest, bald forehead, long moustaches, polite manner, and all — the complete Heyst, even to the kindly, sunken eyes on which there still rested the shadow of Morrison's death.
>
> (004-026)

## Context

The narrator describes Heyst's reappearance in the world after his first period of isolation on Samburan (004-026). Morrison, the captain of the brig with whom Heyst had formed his only genuine friendship, has died during Heyst's absence. Heyst's physical reappearance — broad-chested, bald, with his signature long moustaches — is the "complete Heyst" in external form, but the narrator notices something darker beneath the surface. The "shadow of Morrison's death" still rests in his "kindly, sunken eyes," suggesting that Heyst carries the moral weight of his friend's death as a permanent psychological burden. Davidson, who gives Heyst a lift out of his forsaken island, hears Heyst's insistence that his return to the world is only temporary — he means to go back to Samburan.

## Significance

This passage is one of the novel's most concentrated expressions of Heyst's moral predicament. Morrison is the one person for whom Heyst stepped out of his philosophy of detachment — he intervened to save Morrison financially, forming a partnership that was the closest thing to friendship Heyst had ever experienced. Morrison's subsequent death leaves Heyst with an unresolved guilt that, Conrad suggests, he will never fully escape. The image of the "shadow" resting in the eyes is a masterful piece of psychological portraiture: it externalizes an internal wound, making visible the invisible burden that Heyst carries. This shadow is the moral prehistory of the novel's central love story — Heyst's relationship with Lena is undertaken, in part, as an attempt to escape the haunted memory of the friend he could not save.
