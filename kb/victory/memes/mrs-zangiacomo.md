# Mrs. Zangiacomo

## Description

Mrs. Zangiacomo is the pianist and co-manager of the Zangiacomo Ladies'
Orchestra, a touring ensemble of eighteen women performers that sets up
at Schomberg's Hotel in Sourabaya (008-001). She is described in
unflattering physical terms — a "bony, red-faced woman with
bad-tempered nostrils" (008-008) whose white muslin dress conceals a
"rawboned frame" (008-013). She operates the grand piano, "raining hard
notes like hail through the tempest of fiddles" (008-008).

> The uproar in that small, barn-like structure was simply stunning. An
> instrumental uproar, screaming, grunting, whining, sobbing, scraping,
> squeaking some kind of lively air; while a grand piano, operated upon by
> a bony, red-faced woman with bad-tempered nostrils, rained hard notes
> like hail through the tempest of fiddles. The small platform was filled
> with white muslin dresses and crimson sashes slanting from shoulders
> provided with bare arms, which sawed away without respite.
>
> (008-008)

She is the dominant partner in the Zangiacomo enterprise. Her husband
follows her with "an aspect of hangdog concentration" (009-024), while
Mrs. Zangiacomo drives the performers with physical menace — "driven in
truculently by the haughty mate of Zangiacomo, who looked as though she
were restraining herself with difficulty from punching their backs"
(009-024).

## Role in the Novel

### Lena's Tormentor

Mrs. Zangiacomo is the immediate agent of Lena's suffering in Sourabaya.
She forces Lena to mingle with the hotel guests during the intervals,
"incited thereto by a pinch from the awful Zangiacomo woman" (009-007).
Lena later describes her as "that devil-woman" who "sometimes wouldn't
let me go away" (019-047). Heyst, understanding the dynamic, observes
that "she liked to torment you in a variety of ways" (019-048).

Her cruelty is the catalyst for Heyst's intervention. Seeing Lena's
misery under Mrs. Zangiacomo's regime awakens Heyst's compassion and
sets in motion the central event of the novel — his rescue of Lena from
Schomberg's hotel.

### The Zangiacomo Enterprise

The Zangiacomos are, as Davidson observes, representatives of a class of
touring impresarios "who often were anything but musicians by
profession" (005-030). Zangiacomo is "a German really; only he dyes his
hair and beard black for business. Zangiacomo is his business name"
(005-038). The couple run a sordid operation, exploiting eighteen women
performers under "sordid conditions and brutal incidents" (005-030).

The orchestra's presence at Schomberg's hotel creates the environment in
which Heyst meets Lena. The "unholy fascination in systematic noise"
(008-010) that draws Heyst into the concert hall is a product of the
Zangiacomos' enterprise, and Mrs. Zangiacomo's position at the piano
makes her the visible embodiment of the operation's coarseness.

## Relationship to Other Characters

Mrs. Zangiacomo serves as a foil to several characters. Her active
cruelty contrasts with Schomberg's blustering malevolence, her physical
domination of the orchestra women is the inverse of Heyst's courteous
detachment, and her marriage to the hangdog Zangiacomo mirrors — in
grotesque form — the transaction that defines the Schombergs' union.
