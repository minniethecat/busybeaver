## Overview

Gratitude, in Conrad's moral universe, is not a virtue but a trap. The central example is Morrison's response to Heyst's act of financial rescue in Timor: overwhelmed by gratitude (002-055), the captain insists on making Heyst a partner in the Tropical Belt Coal Company (003-002), an arrangement that will eventually strand Heyst on Samburan and set the stage for the novel's catastrophe. Morrison's gratitude is genuine, heartfelt, and utterly destructive — Conrad's narrator eulogizes him as "the victim of gratitude" (003-002).

> Morrison himself, not a perfect vessel by any means, was bursting with gratitude, and under the stress he must have let out something vague — enough to give the island gossip a chance.
>
> (002-055)

Heyst himself is uncomfortable with gratitude — not because he is ungrateful, but because his philosophy of detachment recoils from the obligations that gratitude creates. He lends Morrison the money as a free act, expecting nothing in return, but Morrison's gratitude insists on repayment, on connection, on partnership (003-002). The gift that was meant to be free becomes a chain. As Heyst later confesses to Lena:

> Already his gratitude was simply frightful. Funny position, wasn't it? The boredom came later, when we lived together on board his ship. I had, in a moment of inadvertence, created for myself a tie. How to define it precisely I don't know. One gets attached in a way to people one has done something for. But is that friendship? I am not sure what it was. I only know that he who forms a tie is lost. The germ of corruption has entered into his soul.
>
> (018-128)

## Role in the Novel

Gratitude is the mechanism by which Heyst's philosophy of detachment is defeated. His father taught him that the world is a place of illusion and noise, that the wise man withdraws. But Heyst cannot withdraw from Morrison's gratitude — it pursues him, creates obligations, builds a company, and ultimately delivers him to Samburan and to Lena (003-002). The irony is that Heyst's one truly generous act — the rescue of Morrison — produces consequences more entangled than any selfish action could have (019-109). Heyst himself, looking back, confesses that Morrison's thankfulness became an unbearable burden:

> He insisted on regarding me as his saviour; he was always restraining the eternal obligation on the tip of his tongue, till I was burning with shame at his gratitude. What could I do? He was going to repay me with this infernal coal, and I had to join him as one joins a child's game in a nursery.
>
> (019-109)

The novel also explores gratitude in the relationship between Heyst and Lena. Lena is grateful to Heyst for rescuing her from Schomberg's Hotel and the Zangiacomo orchestra, but her gratitude is complicated by love, fear, and her awareness of the social gulf between them. Unlike Morrison's gratitude, Lena's does not destroy — but the conditions under which it must be expressed (the siege, the threat of violence, Heyst's emotional inarticulacy) mean that it can never be fully realized.

In a darker variation of the theme, even the novel's villains are touched by gratitude. When Lena helps the trapped Ricardo escape through a window during the crisis on Samburan, the hardened secretary seizes her hand "with all the force of his dumb gratitude, as a man does to a chum when there is no time for words" (028-024). The moment is fleeting, and Ricardo's predatory nature quickly reasserts itself — but even here, gratitude proves to be an involuntary bond, a momentary humanizing flash across a character otherwise defined by reptilian self-interest.
