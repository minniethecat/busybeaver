## Definition & Scope

**Hypocrisy** is not merely one vice among many in *Anna Karenina* — it is the structural principle around which the social world is organized. The novel traces a society in which the gap between public performance and private conduct is not a failure of morality but its enabling condition. Those who maintain the performance ([[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]], [[Liza-Merkalova]], [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]]) survive and even flourish; those who refuse (Anna) are destroyed.

## The Mechanics of Social Hypocrisy

### The Adultery Double Standard

The novel's most focused critique targets the gendered asymmetry of sexual morality. Stiva Oblonsky's infidelity is treated as a minor domestic disturbance — his wife forgives him, his sister visits to reconcile them, and he continues his affairs without social consequence. Society does not judge him because he does not embarrass society.

Anna, by contrast, is judged not for her affair but for its visibility. As long as she and Vronsky conduct themselves with discretion, they are received — Princess Betsy's circle treats the affair as a source of amusement. But when Anna insists on honesty — when she appears at the opera in Part Five, publicly claiming the identity of Vronsky's companion — society closes ranks. The women who have conducted their own affairs for years now cut her; the men who have pursued her now avoid her gaze.

The narrator makes the mechanism explicit: "She was a woman who had put herself outside the pale" — and she had put herself there not by what she did but by refusing to pretend she had not done it.

### Official Morality

[[Alexei-Karenin]] embodies a related form of hypocrisy — the substitution of official rectitude for genuine moral engagement. When he discovers Anna's affair, his primary concern is not emotional but procedural: how to manage the situation so that his career does not suffer. His eventual "forgiveness" of Anna, urged on by [[Lydia-Ivanovna]], is a performance of Christian magnanimity that masks a refusal to grant her the divorce she needs to build a new life.

The bureaucracy that Karenin serves operates according to the same logic. Officials rise and fall not on merit but on the management of appearances. Stremov, Karenin's rival, flourishes precisely because he understands this: his connection with Liza Merkalova is discreet enough to be socially acceptable.

### The Country as Antidote

Against the hypocrisy of [[Petersburg-Society]] and the bureaucracy, Tolstoy sets the country, where authenticity is possible because social performance is less necessary. Levin's flight from the city is a flight from a world where "one had to be continually pretending." The peasants, unlike the aristocrats, do not dissemble — they may cheat Levin on the hay count, but they do not pretend to be something they are not.

## All-Encompassing or Commonplace?

The novel's critique of hypocrisy raises a question it does not fully resolve: is hypocrisy uniquely characteristic of Petersburg society, or is it the universal condition of social life? Stiva Oblonsky's cheerful dishonesty suggests the latter — he lies not out of malice but out of an almost biological incapacity for truth-telling, and the narrator treats him with amusement rather than condemnation. The novel reserves its harshest judgment for those, like Karenin and Lydia Ivanovna, who wrap their hypocrisy in the language of virtue.
