# Adultery in Anna Karenina

Adultery is the central moral problem of *Anna Karenina*, structuring the novel's plot and providing the lens through which Tolstoy examines marriage, society, gender double standards, and spiritual redemption. The novel opens with the aftermath of Stepan Arkadyevitch Oblonsky's infidelity and builds toward Anna Karenina's catastrophic affair with Count Vronsky, using these parallel cases to expose the hypocrisy of Russian high society.

## The Oblonsky Case: Tolerated Infidelity

The novel begins with the Oblonsky household in chaos after Dolly discovers her husband's affair with the French governess. Stepan Arkadyevitch reflects a pattern of male infidelity that society accepts: "Such men are unfaithful, but their home and wife are sacred to them" [^PN-027-047]. Despite the pain it causes his wife, Oblonsky's transgression is treated as a minor domestic inconvenience. Dolly, though deeply wounded, ultimately remains in the marriage, telling herself "this has not been an infidelity of the heart" [^PN-027-049]. Oblonsky himself, "like all unfaithful husbands," is "very solicitous for his wife's comfort" [^PN-084-001], maintaining the outward forms of domestic life while privately pursuing his pleasures.

The gendered logic of this arrangement is striking. Oblonsky's infidelity is understood by his social circle as a harmless male indulgence — the behavior of a man who loves his wife and family but reserves the right to private pleasures outside the home. His charm, his social ease, and his genuine affection for Dolly coexist without contradiction alongside his betrayals. The society that will later destroy Anna for the same conduct sees nothing remarkable in Oblonsky's behavior.

## Dolly's Experience: The Gendered Cost

Darya Alexandrovna Oblonskaya bears the full weight of her husband's infidelity without the luxury of revenge or escape. Her response to the discovery is not moral outrage alone but a desperate reckoning with her own position: she has six children, no independent income, and no social identity outside of marriage. When she contemplates leaving, she confronts the reality that the same society that forgives her husband's transgression offers her no viable path forward.

The cost to Dolly is not merely emotional but physical and existential. Her body, exhausted by repeated pregnancies and the demands of managing a large household, becomes a testament to the asymmetry of marriage: she has given everything to the domestic sphere while Oblonsky has reserved the right to seek pleasure elsewhere. When, later in the novel, Dolly visits Anna at Vronsky's estate, she confronts a woman who has made the opposite choice — and the encounter forces Dolly to reckon with what her own sacrifices have purchased.

## Anna's Affair: The Double Standard

Anna Karenina's affair with Vronsky reveals the brutal asymmetry of society's judgment. While Oblonsky's infidelity is absorbed without lasting social consequence, Anna's adultery destroys her. The masculine code that finds "something fine and grand" [^PN-046-014] in seducing a married woman has no equivalent for the woman herself. Karenin, confronting the situation, reduces it to legal categories, pronouncing the word "adultery" "with peculiar emphasis" [^PN-114-029] and formulating the problem as "adultery of one of the married parties" [^PN-114-031]. His legalistic mind even contemplates "the good sense of adultery by mutual consent" [^PN-114-032] — a concept that reduces moral catastrophe to a contractual arrangement.

Society's stance is crystallized in the observation that while "there were exemplary cases of husbands with unfaithful wives in the highest society," the treatment of those wives depended entirely on whether the husband chose to acknowledge the situation or not [^PN-090-008]. Some husbands "practically ceded or sold" their unfaithful wives [^PN-090-012] — a solution that preserved appearances while destroying the woman's position.

Anna's predicament exposes the fundamental hypocrisy of a moral code that is applied with radically different severity depending on the gender of the transgressor. The same society that treats Vronsky's conquest as a mark of masculine achievement treats Anna's surrender to love as an unforgivable breach. The double standard is not incidental to the social order — it is one of its foundational principles, a mechanism for controlling women's bodies and desires while granting men a sphere of unaccountable freedom.

## Karenin and the Question of Divorce

Alexey Alexandrovitch Karenin moves through several responses to his wife's infidelity. Initially he considers a duel, separation, or divorce — but rejects each as incompatible with his nature and position [^PN-090-013]. Divorce, the legal dissolution of a marriage tainted by adultery, exists as an option but one Karenin approaches with bureaucratic precision, seeking to "obtain a divorce and take away his son" [^PN-113-001] while minimizing public scandal.

The irony is that Anna herself desires a divorce — "it consists in refusing to give me a divorce!" [^PN-042-015] she exclaims bitterly — but Karenin withholds it as a form of control. The legal machinery of divorce becomes another weapon in the marital war, with Karenin instructing a lawyer to arrange matters so that his son is protected [^PN-114-020]. The law, which should exist to resolve marital breakdown, becomes another instrument of punishment — a way for the wronged husband to exact revenge while maintaining the appearance of legal rectitude.

Karenin's refusal is not merely vindictive — it reveals something essential about how adultery functions within the legal and social order. By refusing the divorce, Karenin keeps Anna in a state of legal limbo: she is neither wife nor free woman, neither inside nor outside the social structure. She is defined entirely by her transgression, and Karenin's power consists in his ability to keep her there indefinitely, suffering "a fictitious charge of adultery" [^PN-131-031] that is simultaneously real in its social consequences and fictive in its legal construction.

## The Religious Turn

After Anna's near-fatal illness following childbirth, Karenin undergoes a spiritual transformation and grants forgiveness. This moment of Christian grace, however, proves temporary. The society that condemned Anna's adultery cannot accommodate Karenin's forgiveness either — his position "became very precarious" [^PN-115-001] once he appeared to condone his wife's behavior. The narrative exposes how the social world uses adultery as both a moral weapon and a form of entertainment, caring less about the sin itself than about the drama it provides.

This episode is crucial to Tolstoy's moral vision. Karenin's forgiveness, however brief, represents a genuine Christian response to betrayal — the kind of response that the novel's epigraph from Romans gestures toward. But the social world has no category for such forgiveness. It can process condemnation and it can process scandal; what it cannot process is grace, which disrupts the entire economy of judgment on which social morality depends.

## Adultery and Tolstoy's Narrative Architecture

The novel's treatment of adultery is inseparable from its narrative form. By opening with the Oblonsky scandal — a case of adultery that is comic, manageable, and ultimately absorbed — and then progressing to Anna's tragedy, Tolstoy creates a diptych that refuses simple moralizing. If adultery were simply wrong, Oblonsky should suffer as Anna does. The fact that he does not — that he ends the novel essentially unchanged, still charming, still employed, still unfaithful — exposes the arbitrary nature of the social mechanisms that destroy Anna.

This structural irony is deepened by the novel's treatment of the would-be adulterers who do not act. Levin, tempted toward unfaithfulness in the early months of his marriage, recoils — not from superior virtue but from a visceral recognition of what the act would cost him. Kitty, who loved Vronsky before she loved Levin, navigates the memory of that near-betrayal within the frame of a marriage that succeeds precisely because it has been tested. The novel thus maps a spectrum of adultery: the committed (Oblonsky), the consummated (Anna), the contemplated (Levin), and the renounced (Kitty) — and shows that the consequences depend less on the act itself than on the gender and social position of the actor.

## Tolstoy's Moral Framework

Tolstoy does not treat adultery as a simple sin to be condemned. The epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — frames the question as one of divine rather than social judgment. Anna is destroyed, but not by a righteous society; she is destroyed by a hypocritical one. The novel's true adulterers, in Tolstoy's moral universe, are those who use the concept of adultery to punish, control, and entertain themselves while maintaining a façade of respectability.

The epigraph's meaning has been the subject of extensive critical discussion. It can be read as a warning against human judgment — an assertion that divine justice operates on a timescale and logic inaccessible to social tribunals. The society that condemns Anna does not understand that it too stands under judgment. The final word belongs not to the scandalized drawing rooms of Petersburg but to a God whose mercy and severity alike exceed human categories.
