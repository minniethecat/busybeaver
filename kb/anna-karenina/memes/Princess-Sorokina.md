## Identity & Role

Princess Sorokina is a young aristocrat who moves in Countess Vronskaya's social circle. She is referenced always in association with her mother, Madame Sorokina, and appears primarily in the novel's final sections. Her narrative function is almost entirely psychological: she is the rival whom Anna's jealousy constructs, the "imaginary girl whom he might want to marry, for whose sake he would break with her" [220-005]. Princess Sorokina exists in the text as a name, a social presence, and above all as a catalyst for Anna's deteriorating mental state.

## Biography / Arc

### Social Appearances

Princess Sorokina first appears in the opera-house scenes of Part Five, where she is part of Countess Vronskaya's entourage. She meets Vronsky in the corridor — "Varya with the young Princess Sorokina met him in the corridor" [165-037] — and is dispatched by Varya to summon Vronsky to his mother's box:

> "'Count, your maman is asking for you,' said the young Princess Sorokina, peeping out of the door of the box." [165-046]

These brief glimpses establish her as a conventional young society woman, pleasant and unremarkable, moving through the rituals of the Petersburg social season.

Later, Varya teases Vronsky about his absence from Anna's side, pointedly waiting until "Princess Sorokina had moved away" [165-050] before asking why he is not "faire la cour à Madame Karenina." The staging is significant: the conversation about Anna is bracketed by Princess Sorokina's entrance and exit, as though Sorokina represents the respectable world from which Anna is excluded.

### The Marriage Scheme

Countess Vronskaya's desire for her son to marry Princess Sorokina becomes known through Vronsky's own admission. In a moment of ill-judged frankness, he tells Anna:

> "His mother knew him so little that she had had the audacity to try and persuade him to marry the young Princess Sorokina." [220-005]

Vronsky intends this as reassurance — look how absurd my mother's schemes are — but for Anna, the information is toxic. It gives her jealousy a name and a face. From this moment forward, Princess Sorokina becomes the "object for her jealousy" that Anna had been searching for [220-005].

### The Object of Jealousy

Anna's fixation on Princess Sorokina intensifies rapidly. When Vronsky mentions he must visit his mother, Anna's mind immediately seizes on Sorokina:

> "It was now not the Queen of Sweden's swimming-mistress who filled Anna's imagination, but the young Princess Sorokina. She was staying in a village near Moscow with Countess Vronskaya." [221-019]

The progression of Anna's jealousy — from nameless "low women" to society women to "the imaginary girl" to this specific princess with a known location — traces the increasing precision of her torment. Each new object is more concrete, more socially plausible, and therefore more unbearable than the last.

On the day of her suicide, Anna's jealous imagination reaches its apogee:

> "She pictured him to herself as talking calmly to his mother and Princess Sorokina and rejoicing at her sufferings." [226-010]

The image is entirely a product of Anna's mind — Vronsky is not with Sorokina at that moment — but the imagined scene has the vividness of hallucination.

### Anna's Rational Knowledge

Anna is not delusional. Even in the depths of her jealous frenzy, she knows the truth:

> "Don't I know that he wouldn't deceive me, that he has no schemes about Princess Sorokina, that he's not in love with Kitty, that he won't desert me! I know all that, but it makes it no better for me." [227-004]

This is the tragedy of Anna's final state: she is lucid enough to recognize that her jealousy is irrational, but lucidity offers no relief. Princess Sorokina is not the cause of Anna's suffering; she is merely the screen onto which Anna projects a deeper truth — that Vronsky's love is waning, that the relationship is dying, and that no external rival is needed when the internal bond has already frayed.

## Relationships

### With Countess Vronskaya

Princess Sorokina is a protégée of Vronsky's mother, who sees in her the respectable, socially approved bride her son should have chosen instead of Anna. Countess Vronskaya's matchmaking is both conventional and cruel — an attempt to restore Vronsky to his proper social place that treats Anna as an aberration to be corrected.

### With Vronsky

There is no evidence in the text that Vronsky has any romantic interest in Princess Sorokina. He visits his mother's house — where Sorokina happens to be staying — out of filial obligation, not romantic pursuit. His "embarrassment" [221-019] when Anna's "intent, suspicious eyes" fix on him after he mentions his mother is the embarrassment of a man caught in a situation he cannot explain because there is nothing to explain.

### With Anna

Princess Sorokina and Anna never meet in the novel. Their relationship exists entirely in Anna's mind, where Sorokina becomes the embodiment of everything Anna fears: youth, social respectability, maternal approval, and the possibility of a legitimate future with Vronsky. She is less a character than a function — the necessary triangulation point in the geometry of Anna's jealousy.

## Themes & Significance

Princess Sorokina is one of Tolstoy's most economical creations — a character built almost entirely out of absence and projection. She speaks perhaps three lines in the entire novel, yet she exerts enormous gravitational force on Anna's consciousness. She represents the "normal" life that Anna cannot have: a young woman of good family, suitable for marriage, untainted by scandal, moving easily through the social world from which Anna is barred.

The gap between Sorokina's reality (a pleasant, unremarkable young aristocrat) and her significance in Anna's mind (the rival who will take everything) is the gap that defines Anna's final deterioration. As Anna's grasp on reality loosens, Sorokina grows from a name into an obsession, and the obsession becomes one of the forces propelling Anna toward the railway station.

## Key Passages

> "His mother knew him so little that she had had the audacity to try and persuade him to marry the young Princess Sorokina." [220-005]

Vronsky's careless admission — the seed from which Anna's obsession grows.

> "It was now not the Queen of Sweden's swimming-mistress who filled Anna's imagination, but the young Princess Sorokina." [221-019]

The moment jealousy finds its object, shifting from the absurd to the plausible.

> "She pictured him to herself as talking calmly to his mother and Princess Sorokina and rejoicing at her sufferings." [226-010]

Anna's jealous imagination at its most vivid — a scene entirely invented, yet experienced as real.

> "Don't I know that he wouldn't deceive me, that he has no schemes about Princess Sorokina... I know all that, but it makes it no better for me." [227-004]

The tragedy of lucid jealousy — knowing the truth and receiving no comfort from it.
