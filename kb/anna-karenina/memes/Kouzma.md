## Identity & Role

**Kouzma** is [[Konstantin-Levin]]'s longtime servant and valet, a figure of quiet reliability who appears throughout the novel from the early Moscow chapters to the final scenes at [[Pokrovskoe]]. He belongs to that class of old family retainers whose relationship with their masters transcends simple employment, carrying echoes of a pre-modern, patriarchal social order.

Kouzma's defining traits are his placid steadiness, his intuitive understanding of Levin's moods, and a gentle humor that surfaces in moments of domestic comedy. He is described as "his old servant" (135-012), a designation that signals both his age and the depth of his attachment to Levin.

## Biography / Arc

Kouzma first appears in Part One, roused from sleep in the middle of the night to help Levin prepare for an early departure:

> Kouzma, waked up by her, came sidling in. (034-002)

He is immediately established as Levin's man — the one who fetches the sheepskin, prepares the trap for travel, and shares in the unspoken rhythms of his master's life. When Levin sets off, Kouzma "already scented a big tip" (056-022), a detail that reveals his long experience with his master's impulsive generosity.

In Part Five, during the chaotic preparations for Levin's wedding, Kouzma becomes the center of a memorable comic episode:

> When Levin had asked for his evening suit, Kouzma, his old servant, had brought him a frock coat. (135-012)

The mishap escalates when Kouzma, with his characteristic "placid smile," fails to produce the right shirt:

> "You've got a shirt on," Kouzma answered, with a placid smile. (135-014)

The guilty Kouzma eventually "flew panting into the room with the shirt" (135-016), having discovered it had been packed into the van. This small domestic crisis, resolved by the old servant's breathless effort, captures the tender comedy that characterizes Levin and Kouzma's relationship.

In Part Seven, Kouzma appears as a quiet domestic presence during Levin's anxious wait for news of Kitty's labor (147-018). His role as the bringer of news — letters, telegrams, reports of visitors — positions him as a nexus of household communication.

In Part Eight, Kouzma appears in a more somber register, attending Levin during his spiritual crisis:

> He was stopped by his old servant Kouzma, who had been with him before his marriage. (199-042)

The servant's quiet question about "Katerina Alexandrovna" (199-046) grounds Levin in the ordinary human concerns that his metaphysical torment threatens to eclipse.

## Themes & Significance

- **The old servant and the vanishing world**: Kouzma represents a type of household retainer whose relationship to the master was personal rather than contractual, rooted in lifelong service rather than wage labor. His presence evokes a rural, pre-industrial social order that was already fading in Tolstoy's time.

- **Domestic comedy and human warmth**: The scene of Kouzma's shirt mishap on Levin's wedding morning is one of the novel's most purely comic moments. It humanizes both master and servant, showing the shared absurdity of formal occasions.

- **Stability amid crisis**: Kouzma's unchanging steadiness — his placid responses, his matter-of-fact announcements — provides a counterweight to Levin's oscillations between ecstasy and despair. He is, in a sense, the novel's quietest anchor.

## Key Passages

The wedding-morning scene is Kouzma's finest moment:

> "You've got a shirt on," Kouzma answered, with a placid smile. (135-014)

The imperturbable logic of the servant — why would the master need another shirt when he is already wearing one? — is both infuriating and endearing.

In the novel's final part, Kouzma's simple presence offers Levin a return to the ordinary:

> At home Kouzma told Levin that Katerina Alexandrovna was quite well. (208-006)
