# Matvey

## Identity & Role

Matvey is the valet of [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]] Oblonsky and one of the first characters to appear in the novel. He enters in Chapter 2 of Part One, carrying Stiva's clothes and boots into the study where his master has spent the night after being discovered in infidelity by his wife. From this opening scene, Matvey is established as more than a servant — he is an "old friend" [010-004], a confidant whose relationship with Stiva is built on years of mutual understanding communicated through glances, gestures, and shared humor.

His role is confined largely to the domestic sphere of the Oblonsky household, but within that sphere he is indispensable. He dresses Stiva, delivers messages, manages the household's rhythm, and — most importantly — provides the emotional support that Stiva, in his crisis, desperately needs. Matvey's steady, affectionate competence is a quiet counterweight to the chaos Stiva has created.

## Biography / Arc

Matvey's biographical arc is inseparable from Stiva's — he has been in Oblonsky's service for years and knows his master's character with the intimacy of long familiarity. The opening chapters show him at work: delivering the telegram announcing [[Anna-Karenina]]'s visit, helping Stiva dress, and offering terse but meaningful commentary on the domestic crisis.

When Stiva tells Matvey that his sister Anna is coming, Matvey responds "Thank God!" [010-012], "showing by this response that he understood the significance of the visit" — namely, that Anna might help reconcile the estranged couple. When Stiva asks whether his wife will "come round," Matvey answers with quiet confidence: "She will come round" [010-023].

Throughout these exchanges, much of the communication is wordless. Stiva glances at Matvey in the looking-glass [010-007], and Matvey understands. Matvey puts his hands in his pockets and "stepped back a little" [010-008], and Stiva knows he is preparing to joke. The two men share a private language built on years of companionship, and Tolstoy renders it with extraordinary economy.

Matvey's final significant appearance in the opening sequence is when he announces that "the carriage is ready" [011-021] and urges Stiva to "drink your coffee in peace, at least" [011-025] in an "affectionately gruff" tone — the tone of a servant who has earned the right to speak familiarly.

## Relationships

- **[[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]]**: The central relationship. Matvey is Stiva's valet, confidant, and in some sense his emotional anchor during the marriage crisis. Their communication — laconic, knowing, comfortable — is one of the novel's most economical portraits of intimacy across class lines.
- **[[Darya-Alexandrovna]]**: Matvey believes Dolly will "come round" [010-023], and his confidence is eventually vindicated. He understands both sides of the marriage with the clarity of an observer who sees everything and judges nothing.
- **[[Anna-Karenina]]**: Matvey greets the news of Anna's arrival with "Thank God!" [010-012], recognizing her potential role as peacemaker.

## Themes & Significance

Matvey is a masterclass in Tolstoy's characterization of servants — figures who are simultaneously essential to the narrative and invisible within the social world it depicts. Unlike many novelists who either ignore servants or sentimentalize them, Tolstoy gives Matvey a distinct personality, a history, and a form of dignity that does not depend on challenging the class hierarchy. Matvey is content in his role, and his contentment is part of what makes the Oblonsky household function.

The silent communication between Matvey and Stiva — the glances in the mirror, the understood jokes, the raised finger and the answering nod — embodies a form of human connection that the novel values deeply: intuitive, untheoretical, built on shared life rather than shared ideas. In a novel full of characters who talk brilliantly and understand nothing, Matvey says little and understands everything.

## Key Passages

> Stepan Arkadyevitch saw Matvey wanted to make a joke and attracted his attention. [010-010]

The entire master-servant relationship in miniature — Stiva perceives Matvey's intention without a word being spoken, and the verb "attracted his attention" captures the delicacy of their silent communication.

> "Thank God!" said Matvey, showing by this response that he understood the significance of the visit. [010-012]

Matvey's relief at Anna's arrival is genuine and revealing — he cares about the household's wellbeing and sees Anna as a force for healing.

> "She will come round," said Matvey. [010-023]

Three words that carry the weight of long experience and quiet wisdom. Matvey knows Dolly, knows marriage, and knows that time heals what words cannot.

> "Drink your coffee in peace, at least," said Matvey, in the affectionately gruff tone. [011-025]

The adverb "affectionately" and the qualifier "gruff" together capture the peculiar intimacy of the old servant — permitted to scold, trusted to care.
