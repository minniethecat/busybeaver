# Pyotr Dmitrievitch

**Pyotr Dmitrievitch** is the obstetrician called to attend [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya|Kitty Levin]]'s childbirth in the novel's final part. He is a figure of calm medical authority whose unhurried, methodical presence provides a counterweight to [[Konstantin-Levin]]'s anxious agitation during the delivery.

## Identity & Role

Pyotr Dmitrievitch is a St. Petersburg obstetrician — a "great and important personage" in the medical world, as Levin reflects with a mixture of awe and irony (211-004). He is summoned to the Levins' Moscow residence when Kitty goes into labor. His professional identity is entirely defined by his role at the birth: he arrives, assesses the situation, issues instructions, and manages the household's collective anxiety through sheer unhurried competence.

## Biography / Arc

Pyotr Dmitrievitch appears only in the childbirth chapters, but his presence dominates them. When Kitty's labor begins, the household debates whether to send for him immediately — "You should let Pyotr Dmitrievitch know, but don't hurry him," says the midwife, signaling both his importance and the expectation that he will not be rushed (210-028).

When Levin receives word that the doctor must be sent for, his mind fixates on the doctor's name with almost absurd intensity — "Pyotr Dmitrievitch, Pyotr Dmitrievitch!" he repeats, the name becoming a talisman of hope in the crisis (211-007, 211-012). His arrival is characterized by a deliberate, measured calm: he washes his hands, speaks quietly, and by his very slowness communicates that there is no emergency to panic over.

When Levin anxiously questions him about Kitty's condition, asking "Do you suppose it may go all right?", the doctor's response — unhurried and noncommittal — is itself the comfort: a man who has seen childbirth many times does not rush (211-018).

## Relationships

- **Kitty Levin**: His patient. He attends her professionally and with the calm of long experience.
- **Konstantin Levin**: The anxious husband whom the doctor's composure both calms and frustrates. Levin's fixation on the name "Pyotr Dmitrievitch" is a comic detail that captures his helplessness during the birth.
- **Marya-Vlasyevna**: The midwife who works alongside Pyotr Dmitrievitch during the delivery.

## Themes & Significance

Pyotr Dmitrievitch embodies the theme of professional competence as a form of moral authority. In a household gripped by the primal anxiety of childbirth, his calm — his refusal to be rushed — is not indifference but the practiced economy of someone who has presided over many births and knows that panic helps nothing. His presence is Tolstoy's quiet argument for expertise as a stabilizing force in moments of existential crisis.

## Key Passages

> "You should let Pyotr Dmitrievitch know, but don't hurry him. And get some opium from the chemist's." (210-028)

> "Pyotr Dmitrievitch, Pyotr Dmitrievitch!" he said to himself — and the name seemed to him now not merely familiar but somehow significant and reassuring. (211-007)

> "But what do you think, Pyotr Dmitrievitch? Do you suppose it may go all right?" (211-018)
