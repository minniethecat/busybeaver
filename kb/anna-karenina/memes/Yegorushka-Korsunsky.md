# Yegorushka Korsunsky

## Identity & Role

Yegorushka Korsunsky is the master of ceremonies at the Moscow ball in Part One of *Anna Karenina*. He is "extremely handsome and well-built" [030-004] and renowned as the "finest dancer, the best of partners" [030-008] — a reputation he has earned through years of dominating Petersburg and Moscow ballrooms.

His function in the novel is concentrated almost entirely within the ball scene (chapters 030–031). He is the animating force of the dancing — starting the waltz, directing the couples, ordering the orchestra, and creating the kinetic energy that makes the ball the novel's most celebrated set piece. Beyond this concentrated appearance, he is referenced only briefly in conversation afterward.

## Biography & Arc

Korsunsky enters the novel at full velocity: "He had only just left the Court ball, where he had been dancing" [030-004], arriving at the Moscow ball already in motion. He immediately begins the grand round: "Korsunsky began waltzing with measured tread straight toward the crowd in the corner" [030-013], selecting Kitty Shcherbatskaya as his first partner.

His manner with the ladies is gallant and self-assured. Addressing Anna Arkadyevna, he bows and declares himself one of her "most faithful supporters" [030-017]. When Anna protests that she and her husband know him, he answers smoothly: "everyone knows us" [030-019] — a line that captures his easy social confidence.

He waltzes with Anna, and despite her initial protest — "I thought it's impossible" [030-021] — he declares the waltz the one dance worth knowing. Anna "hastily put her hand on Korsunsky's shoulder" [030-023] and the dance begins.

Korsunsky's voice rings across the ballroom: "Pardon! Waltz! waltz!" [030-025], and "the peremptory shouts of Korsunsky starting them all into the grand round" [031-002] fill the air. His energy is so total that when Countess Nordston dances with him, "she had not to talk, because Korsunsky was all the time running about giving orders" [031-013] — dancing itself is secondary; direction is his real performance.

He orchestrates Anna's triumphant moment: "a figure, newly invented by Korsunsky, Anna came forward into the center of the circle" [031-018]. Drawing her "bare arm under his," [031-021] he presents her before the assembled company — the ball's visual climax.

After the ball, he appears only in passing conversation between Kitty and her mother about "the Korsunskys, husband and wife" [031-001], and his wife Lidi — "the beauty Lidi, Korsunsky's wife" [030-007], "unusually naked" — is noted in the crowd.

## Relationships

- **Lidi Korsunsky** — his wife, a famously beautiful woman described as "unusually naked" at the ball [030-007].
- **Kitty Shcherbatskaya** — his first waltz partner at the Moscow ball. He selects her with the easy confidence of a man who knows his social position.
- **Anna Karenina** — his most significant dance partner. His waltz with Anna and the figure he invents to display her are the ball's dramatic high point.
- **Countess Nordston** — dances with Korsunsky during the mazurka, though he is too busy directing to make conversation [031-012, 031-013].

## Themes & Significance

Korsunsky is the embodiment of the ball as a social institution. He is not a character in the psychological sense but a force — the animating energy that transforms a room full of people into a choreographed social spectacle. His shouts, his directions, his inventions of new figures, his pairing of partners — all of these make him the ball's director in a sense that is nearly literal.

His presentation of Anna at the climax of the ball scene — drawing her into the center of the circle — is the visual crystallization of Anna's social triumph. It is the last moment of pure, uncomplicated social success Anna will experience in the novel, and Korsunsky is its orchestrator.

Structurally, his concentrated appearance (essentially one extended scene) and disappearance from the rest of the novel is characteristic of Tolstoy's technique for minor characters: full vividness while present, no attempt to sustain them beyond their narrative function.

His physical description — "extremely handsome and well-built" [030-004], perpetually in motion, always "a little out of breath" [030-008] — makes him one of the novel's most visually memorable minor figures.

## Key Passages

> He was extremely handsome and well-built, Yegorushka Korsunsky. He had only just left the Court ball, where he had been dancing. [030-004]

Korsunsky's entrance establishes him as a man perpetually in motion, moving from one ball to the next.

> "A waltz, Anna Arkadyevna?" [030-019]

The directness of the invitation — and Anna's acceptance — sets in motion the ball's central dance.

> "Pardon! Waltz! waltz!" shouted Korsunsky from the other side of the room. [030-025]

The voice of authority — the ball's director commanding the orchestra and the dancers alike.

> Anna came forward into the midst of the circle, picking up her dress, to a figure newly invented by Korsunsky. [031-018]

The ball's climax, engineered by Korsunsky — Anna displayed like a jewel in the center of admiring society.
