# Divorce in Anna Karenina

Divorce is the legal and moral crux of Anna Karenina's tragedy. Throughout the novel, the possibility — and impossibility — of divorce structures the conflict between Anna, her husband Karenin, and her lover Vronsky. In nineteenth-century Russian society, divorce was legally possible but socially catastrophic, particularly for women. Tolstoy uses the divorce question to expose the hypocrisy of a society that permitted male infidelity while destroying women who sought legal release from unhappy marriages.

## Anna's Demand

Early in the novel, when Anna's affair with Vronsky has become known to Karenin but not yet to society, she expresses her central grievance with bitter clarity. "It consists in refusing to give me a divorce!" she exclaims [^PN-042-015]. For Anna, divorce represents freedom — the right to live openly with Vronsky and to legitimize their relationship. But Karenin's refusal transforms the divorce question from a legal procedure into a weapon of control.

## Karenin's Calculus

Alexey Alexandrovitch approaches the divorce question not as a husband but as a bureaucrat. "Alexey Alexandrovitch turned to divorce — another solution selected by him" [^PN-090-012], weighing the options as if processing a government memorandum. He considers several paths: a duel ("senseless and irrational"), a separation ("the very thing Alexey Alexandrovitch desired"), and divorce. But his analysis concludes that "an attempt at divorce could lead to nothing but a public scandal" [^PN-090-013].

The narrative exposes Karenin's cold calculation. "Apart from formal divorce," the text notes, "one might still do like Karibanov, Paskudin, and that good-natured Dram" — that is, live separately while maintaining the appearance of marriage [^PN-090-014]. Karenin is "obliged to weigh all the complications inseparable from a duel, a divorce, a separation, and once again rejected all" [^PN-090-015]. His refusal is a bureaucratic decision masquerading as a moral one.

## The Legal Machinery

When Karenin finally decides to pursue divorce, he does so with characteristic proceduralism. "He would carry out his threat — obtain a divorce and take away his son. He knew it was a difficult and complicated business, but he had made up his mind to go through with it" [^PN-113-001]. He instructs his lawyer: "My son is going to my sister" [^PN-113-030], and the lawyer elaborates: "You wish to be divorced by legal means — that is, to be divorced, but to do this so that my son did not remain with his mother" [^PN-114-020]. The divorce machinery is entirely oriented around the husband's interests.

When Stepan Arkadyevitch asks Karenin directly — "Is not a divorce possible?" — Karenin responds "feebly" [^PN-099-036], revealing that even he, the master of bureaucratic procedure, is paralyzed by the moral and social weight of the act. Divorce, in *Anna Karenina*, is never simply a legal formality; it is always entangled with questions of honor, property, children, and social standing.

## Society's Verdict

The novel documents a world in which divorce is a social death sentence. Princess Tverskaya, Princess Miagky, and other society women discuss Anna's case not with sympathy but with prurient interest. The judgment is gendered: while Oblonsky's infidelities are tolerated, a woman pursuing divorce is abandoned by her social world. The observation that some husbands "practically ceded or sold" their unfaithful wives captures the dehumanizing logic of a system in which women are property to be disposed of [^PN-090-012].

## The Unattainable Goal

Anna never receives her divorce. Karenin withholds it, then grants a kind of spiritual reprieve during Anna's illness, only to reassert control when she recovers. Vronsky, for all his passion, cannot navigate the legal system to secure Anna's freedom. And Anna, caught between two men and a society that condemns her, ultimately sees death as the only release. The divorce that might have saved her remains, like the train that appears throughout the novel, always approaching but never arriving on time.
