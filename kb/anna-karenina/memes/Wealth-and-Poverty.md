# Wealth and Poverty in Anna Karenina

Wealth and poverty operate as structural forces in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, determining characters' social positions, life choices, and moral outlooks. The novel examines economic inequality not merely as background but as a central moral and philosophical concern, particularly through Levin's struggles with the ethics of land ownership. Tolstoy, himself a wealthy landowner tormented by the injustice of his position, channels his own spiritual crisis into Levin's agricultural experiments, his arguments with his brother, and his persistent sense that inherited wealth is a problem to be solved rather than a blessing to be enjoyed.

## Wealth as Social Determinant

The novel's characters are defined in part by their economic standing. Vronsky is introduced as "very wealthy, clever, of aristocratic family" [^PN-020-004], and his wealth enables the social freedom that makes his pursuit of Anna possible — the leisure to attend balls, the means to maintain a separate establishment, the confidence that he need never calculate the cost of his choices. Conversely, Stiva Oblonsky, though "connected and not merely not wealthy, but always hopelessly in debt" [^PN-042-002], maintains his social position through charm and family connections, illustrating the complex relationship between wealth and status in Russian aristocratic society. Stiva's financial irresponsibility — selling Dolly's timber, deferring payments, borrowing from friends — is the behavior of a man for whom wealth has always been a background condition, something to be spent rather than earned.

## Levin's Crisis of Conscience

Levin experiences the most acute moral crisis regarding wealth. He "always felt the injustice of his own abundance in comparison with the poverty of the peasants" [^PN-034-001], a guilt that drives his agricultural experiments and his attempts to share profits with his workers. His discomfort with inherited wealth reflects Tolstoy's own philosophical struggles — the conviction that to live on unearned income while others labor is a form of theft, softened only by the sincerity of one's efforts to redistribute.

Levin's arguments about political economy are not abstract. He insists that "the wealth of the country has decreased" [^PN-104-020] under the current system, challenging the complacent assumption that existing economic arrangements benefit everyone. When others invoke abstract laws — that "the wealth of Europe had been developed" [^PN-106-011] through particular mechanisms — Levin responds with the concrete reality of his own estate: the peasants who distrust his innovations, the land that yields less than it should, the gap between economic theory and agricultural practice.

## The Economics of the Estate

Tolstoy renders the material world of the estate with extraordinary precision. Fields, harvests, livestock, wages, timber sales — these are not background details but the substance of Levin's moral life. When Stiva sells Dolly's forest at a discount to a merchant who exploits her ignorance of timber prices, the transaction is both a comic set piece and a damning portrait of how wealth circulates among the aristocracy: through inherited property, casual exploitation, and the labor of those who cannot refuse. Levin's own estate management is a continuous struggle against economic reality — his attempts at profit-sharing are met with peasant skepticism, and his innovations in machinery and crop rotation produce results that never quite match his theories.

The contrast between Levin's productive estate and Oblonsky's parasitic consumption is deliberate. Stiva extracts wealth from the land without ever touching the soil; Levin's wealth is tied to the rhythms of planting and harvest, and his moral seriousness is inseparable from his physical engagement with agricultural work.

## Urban Wealth and Rural Virtue

The novel organizes its economic geography around a moral axis: the city is the site of consumption, display, and financial opacity; the countryside is the site of production, simplicity, and honest accounting. Petersburg society runs on money whose sources are never examined — Vronsky's regimental lifestyle, Anna's fashionable dresses, the Oblonskys' dinner parties all presuppose a flow of wealth that characters receive but do not create. The countryside makes wealth visible: the hay that must be cut, the cattle that must be fed, the harvest that must be brought in before the rain.

This moral geography is not without its complications. Tolstoy's peasants are idealized — their poverty is described as simplicity, their material deprivation as spiritual richness — in ways that can read as sentimental. But the novel's economic argument is clear: wealth that is disconnected from labor is morally suspect, and a life lived close to the land offers a clarity that urban existence cannot match.

## Wealth, Marriage, and Moral Corruption

Dolly's reflections on marriage reveal how wealth shapes domestic life. She advises that one should not desire "only wealth, luxury, honor for your infant" [^PN-133-036] but rather moral goodness, suggesting that the pursuit of wealth can corrupt family values. The irony is that Dolly herself is trapped by economic dependence: she cannot leave her unfaithful husband because she has no independent means, and her children's futures depend on the estate her husband is systematically squandering.

The novel presents Anna's aunt, "a wealthy provincial lady" [^PN-153-009], as someone who uses her economic position to orchestrate social arrangements, including Anna's marriage to Karenin — a transaction in which a young woman is exchanged between guardians with only the thinnest veneer of consent. Wealth, in this world, is never merely wealth; it is power, and the power to arrange other people's lives is among the most consequential forms it takes.
