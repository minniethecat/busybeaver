## Identity & Role

Vassenka Veslovsky is a distant cousin of the Shcherbatskys — a fashionable young Petersburg gentleman who appears at Pokrovskoe during Levin and Kitty's newlywed summer (Part Six). He is described as a handsome, cheerful, and somewhat fatuous young man who arrives with Stepan Arkadyevitch, wearing a "Scotch bonnet with floating ribbons" [171-030]. His presence serves as the catalyst for one of the novel's most revealing comic episodes — Levin's irrational jealousy over Veslovsky's flirtation with Kitty, which exposes the fragility of Levin's happiness and his tendency toward possessiveness.

> "This was Vassenka Veslovsky, a distant cousin of the Shcherbatskys, a brilliant young gentleman in Petersburg society." [171-030]

## Biography & Arc

### Arrival at Pokrovskoe

Veslovsky arrives unexpectedly with Stiva, immediately establishing himself as a genial but somewhat clueless guest:

> "In place of the old prince, Veslovsky greeted Levin gaily, claiming acquaintance with him, and snatched up from him the game-bag and other things." [171-031]

Levin instantly dislikes him — not for any specific fault, but because Veslovsky represents everything Levin distrusts: the facile charm of Petersburg society, the easy intimacy that assumes familiarity without earning it:

> "The arrival of this Vassenka Veslovsky, a quite uncongenial and superfluous person, irritated Levin." [171-032]

### The Hunting Expedition

Veslovsky joins Levin and Stiva on a shooting expedition. His enthusiasm for the sport is genuine but his competence is nil — he falls asleep in the marsh, and Levin finds his presence disruptive to the serious business of hunting:

> "'Please, do let's go,' said Veslovsky, moving to another chair, where he sat down sideways." [172-005]

His attempts at camaraderie only irritate Levin further:

> "Levin maintained there maintained by Vassenka Veslovsky and Stepan Arkadyevitch." [172-003]

### Flirtation with Kitty

The breaking point comes when Veslovsky pays too much attention to Kitty. Levin, watching from his study, sees Veslovsky leaning close to Kitty as she serves tea, and his jealousy explodes:

> "And that was not all. He saw Kitty — he could see her in his mind's eye — smiling happily, gently, at this handsome, genial young man who was in such high spirits." [172-015]

Levin's response is characteristically extreme — he demands that Veslovsky leave immediately:

> "Do you know Veslovsky has been at Anna's, and he's going to see them again?" [172-012]

Stiva is astonished by Levin's outburst, and Kitty is hurt — but Veslovsky, to his credit, departs without rancor, apparently unaware of having caused offense.

## Relationships

### With Levin

The Veslovsky episode reveals Levin's deepest insecurities. He cannot tolerate another man paying attention to his wife, even innocently. The jealousy is irrational — Kitty is devoted to Levin and Veslovsky is harmless — but its very irrationality is the point: Levin's happiness, built so carefully after years of rejection, feels precarious to him, and any threat, however minor, triggers a disproportionate response.

### With Stepan Arkadyevitch

Stiva brings Veslovsky as a guest without asking, treating Pokrovskoe as an extension of his own social world — a casualness that Levin finds galling. The episode highlights the fundamental incompatibility between Stiva's social expansiveness and Levin's need for domestic sanctuary.

### With Kitty

Veslovsky's relationship with Kitty is entirely superficial — a few compliments, a shared laugh. But through Levin's eyes, it becomes a threat. Kitty's ease with Veslovsky, her ability to be charming without meaning anything by it, is precisely what Levin cannot understand or accept.

## Themes & Significance

The Veslovsky episode is a comic masterpiece that carries serious thematic weight. It dramatizes the tension between social life and domestic intimacy — between the open-door world of the Oblonskys and the closed, sacred space that Levin wants his marriage to be. Veslovsky himself is not a villain; he is merely a product of his social milieu, where flirtation is a pastime rather than a transgression. The conflict is not between Levin and Veslovsky but between two incompatible modes of living.

Tolstoy's genius in this episode is to make Veslovsky both genuinely annoying (to Levin) and genuinely harmless (to the reader). We sympathize with Levin's irritation while recognizing its excess — a balance that few novelists could sustain.
