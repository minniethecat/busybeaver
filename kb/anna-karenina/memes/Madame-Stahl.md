## Identity & Role

Madame Stahl is an invalid Russian lady of aristocratic background, living permanently at the German spa of Soden. Confined to a wheelchair, she is a figure of intense religiosity and moral authority, surrounded by an aura of saintly suffering that deeply impresses the vulnerable Kitty Shcherbatskaya. She is the center of a small circle that includes her adopted companion Varenka, and her example provides Kitty with an alternative model of womanhood after the humiliation of Vronsky's rejection.

> "The highest society there — the society of persons taking the cure — consisted of a German princeling who gave no one any trouble, and an invalid Russian lady, Madame Stahl, as everyone called her." [072-004]

She represents one of Tolstoy's most sustained ironic portraits of institutionalized piety — the unclearly motivated religiosity that the novel ultimately critiques through the skepticism of Prince Shcherbatsky.

## Biography & Arc

### Background

Madame Stahl is a Russian lady of good family, connected through her sister-in-law (belle-sœur) to the Shcherbatsky circle. The princess recalls having known her before:

> "I'll make acquaintance with Madame Stahl; I used to know her belle-sœur," said the princess, drawing herself up. [073-004]

There are multiple accounts of her past. Some say she had "made her husband's position through her own fault," while others maintained that she had suffered terribly and was now a living saint. Her exact history remains deliberately ambiguous, a tension Tolstoy sustains throughout Part Two.

### Life at Soden

At the spa, Madame Stahl maintains a court-like circle of admirers and dependents. She is wheeled about in a bath-chair, receives select visitors, and dispenses moral counsel. Her companion Varenka attends to her every need:

> "Madame Stahl belonged to the highest society, she was so much of an invalid that she could not walk, and every day at a certain hour a carriage drove up with her bath-chair..." [072-004]

She gave Kitty a French translation of the New Testament to read — a significant gift that marks Kitty's turn toward religious devotion [075-006].

### Encounter with Prince Shcherbatsky

The most revealing portrait of Madame Stahl comes through the eyes of Prince Shcherbatsky, who arrives at the spa in Chapter 76 and immediately sees through her pious façade:

> "The prince's eyes at the mention of Madame Stahl glittered with a gleam of irony." [076-018]

When he meets her, he performs elaborate courtesy while his internal skepticism is plain:

> "Alexander Shtcherbatsky," said Madame Stahl, lifting upon him her heavenly eyes. "I have a great liking for your daughter." [076-043]

The prince subtly exposes what Kitty's admiration had obscured — that Madame Stahl's saintliness may be a performance, and that her legs "are alive, only they will not work, the muscles have died" [076-048] — a physical detail that implies her condition may be psychosomatic rather than organic.

> "'It is not for us to judge,' said Madame Stahl, perceiving the shade of expression on the prince's face." [076-049]

## Relationships

### With Varenka

Madame Stahl essentially adopted Varenka, the orphaned daughter of a chef from the Imperial Household. Their relationship defines both characters: Madame Stahl provides Varenka a home and identity, while Varenka provides Madame Stahl the total devotion that confirms her saintly self-image:

> "Everyone who knew Madame Stahl knew and liked Mademoiselle Varenka, and called her by that name, just as Madame Stahl did." [074-003]

### With Kitty Shcherbatskaya

Madame Stahl takes an interest in the young, heartbroken Kitty, talking to her "as to a charming child that one admires as a work of art" [075-002]. The conversations and the gift of the French Testament catalyze Kitty's temporary religious awakening:

> "Kitty made the acquaintance of Madame Stahl too, and this acquaintance, together with her friendship with Varenka, did not merely exercise a great influence on her, it also comforted her in her mental distress." [075-001]

However, the infatuation proves fragile. When the prince's ironic gaze exposes Madame Stahl's feet, "the heavenly image of Madame Stahl, which she had carried for a whole month in her heart, vanished, never to return" [076-061].

### With the Princess

The princess, initially flattered by Madame Stahl's recognition of her daughter, feels slighted when Madame Stahl seems to avoid her:

> "The princess was offended that Madame Stahl had seemed to avoid making her acquaintance." [073-005]

### Late Reference

In Part Seven, Kitty recalls Madame Stahl as a negative example, resolving that her own life must be authentic rather than imitative: "Let him always be one than like Madame Stahl, or what I tried to be in those days abroad" [235-008].

## Themes & Significance

Madame Stahl embodies Tolstoy's critique of institutionalized, performative piety. Her religiosity — sincere or not — is inextricable from social performance and self-regard. She is the novel's most concentrated study of how spiritual authority can become a form of social power, and how the appearance of sanctity can mask the ordinary human vanity it claims to transcend.

The prince's deflation of Madame Stahl is one of the novel's most effective comic set-pieces, and it serves a serious purpose: it restores Kitty to reality, preparing her for the genuine (if imperfect) happiness of marriage to Levin rather than the false transcendence of religious imitation.

Tolstoy's irony is not cruel — Madame Stahl may well believe in her own goodness — but it is relentless. The physical detail of the "live" but useless legs is a classic Tolstoyan synecdoche: the body tells truths the spirit denies.
