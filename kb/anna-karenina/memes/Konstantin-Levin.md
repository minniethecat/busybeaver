# Konstantin Levin

Konstantin Dmitrievitch Levin is the co-protagonist of Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, serving as a moral and spiritual counterpoint to Anna's tragic story. A thirty-two-year-old landowner from the countryside, Levin is passionate about agricultural reform, deeply in love with Kitty Shtcherbatskaya, and engaged in a restless search for meaning that reflects Tolstoy's own spiritual struggles.

## Identity & Role

Levin is a country gentleman, landowner, and district councilor, though he holds the latter position with great skepticism. He is the brother of the celebrated author Sergey Ivanovitch Koznishev, a connection he finds burdensome — he insists on being known as Konstantin Levin, not merely "the brother of Koznishev" [013-032]. Unlike the urban aristocrats who populate St. Petersburg and Moscow society, Levin lives on his country estate, managing his land and working alongside his peasants.

Levin's first appearance is in Part One, Chapter 5, where he arrives in Moscow to propose to Kitty Shtcherbatskaya, the younger sister of his old friend Dolly Oblonskaya.

## Character Arc

### The Failed Proposal

Levin comes to Moscow specifically to ask for Kitty's hand in marriage [014-001]. But, consumed by self-doubt — he sees Kitty as "a creature far above everything earthly" and himself as "a creature so low and so earthly" [014-004] — he proposes at the wrong moment. Kitty, infatuated with Vronsky, refuses him. Humiliated, Levin retreats to his country estate.

### Life in the Country

Levin's chapters at his estate are among the novel's most celebrated passages. He throws himself into agricultural work, writing a treatise on farming, and grappling with the relationship between landowner and peasant. These sections are deeply autobiographical — Levin's theories on agricultural reform mirror Tolstoy's own.

> Levin arrives in Moscow always excited and in a hurry, rather ill at ease and irritated by his own want of ease, and for the most part with a perfectly new, unexpected view of things [013-026].

### Marriage to Kitty

After a period of despair and recovery, Levin and Kitty reconcile. Their marriage, depicted in rich domestic detail, becomes the emotional and moral center of the novel — a successful union built on mutual honesty, work, and family life, standing in deliberate contrast to Anna and Vronsky's doomed passion.

### Spiritual Crisis and Revelation

Levin undergoes a profound spiritual crisis, questioning the meaning of life despite his happiness in marriage and fatherhood. His eventual revelation — that one must live not for oneself but for "the good," for God, and that this truth is found not in reason but in lived experience — represents Tolstoy's own hard-won spiritual position.

## Key Traits

- **Physical vigor**: Oblonsky describes Levin as "a gymnast who lifts thirteen stone with one hand" [013-029]. His physicality connects him to the land and manual labor
- **Intellectual restlessness**: Levin is perpetually questioning — social institutions, agricultural methods, religious faith — and is never satisfied with easy answers
- **Social awkwardness**: In Moscow society Levin is uncomfortable, shy, and prone to saying the wrong thing — a stark contrast to the polished urban aristocrats
- **Moral seriousness**: Unlike Stiva, who laughs "complacently and good-humoredly," Levin "laughed without complacency and sometimes angrily" [013-026]

## Themes Embodied

Levin's story explores the meaning of work, the nature of faith, the possibility of authentic marriage, the dignity of rural life, and the relationship between the individual and society. As a partial self-portrait of Tolstoy, Levin is the novel's primary vehicle for philosophical and spiritual inquiry.
