# Marriage in Anna Karenina

Marriage is the central institution that Tolstoy examines, critiques, and — through Levin and Kitty — tentatively redeems in *Anna Karenina*. The novel opens with one marriage in crisis, traces the destruction of another, and builds a third from the ground up. Through these three marriages, Tolstoy asks what makes a union authentic, and whether the social institution of marriage can accommodate genuine love.

## The Novel's Opening: "Happy families are all alike..."

The novel's famous opening line — "Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way" [009-001] — immediately establishes marriage and family as the book's central themes. The scene that follows, in the Oblonsky household, shows the aftermath of infidelity: Dolly has discovered Stiva's affair with the French governess, and the household is in chaos.

## Three Marriages

### The Oblonskys: The Hollow Marriage

Stepan Arkadyevitch "Stiva" Oblonsky and his wife Dolly represent a marriage that persists outwardly while being hollow within. Stiva is serially unfaithful, yet his affection for Dolly is genuine in its way — he simply cannot understand why she makes "so much of it." Their marriage survives through Dolly's forbearance and Stiva's charm, but it offers no model of intimacy or mutual growth.

Dolly's position reveals the fundamental asymmetry of 19th-century marriage: Stiva's infidelity is a minor scandal, while a woman's infidelity would be social ruin. The Oblonsky household after the discovery is a microcosm — "the wife did not leave her own room, the husband had not been at home for three days" [009-002].

### The Karenins: The Cold Arrangement

Anna's marriage to Alexei Alexandrovitch Karenin is not unhappy because of infidelity — at least not initially — but because it was never a marriage of love. Karenin is twenty years older than Anna, a high-ranking government official who approaches everything, including his marriage, with bureaucratic correctness. Their union was arranged by Anna's aunt, and Anna realizes — once she has experienced passion with Vronsky — that her marriage has been a shell.

Karenin's response to Anna's affair is telling: his primary concern is not personal betrayal but social propriety and appearances. He is willing to tolerate the affair as long as it remains discreet. It is Anna's refusal to dissemble — her insistence on honesty — that forces the marriage to its breaking point.

### Levin and Kitty: The Authentic Marriage

Levin and Kitty's marriage, depicted in rich domestic detail, is Tolstoy's attempt to show what a genuine union looks like. It is built on honesty, shared work, and mutual respect. Their courtship arc — from Levin's initial rejected proposal [014-004] to their eventual reconciliation and marriage — follows the rhythm of genuine emotional growth rather than social convenience or romantic fantasy.

Crucially, their marriage is not idealized as perfect. Levin's spiritual crisis, his jealousy, and the mundane strains of domestic life are all shown. What distinguishes their union is not the absence of difficulty but the way they navigate it together — with honesty and commitment.

## Marriage as Institution vs. Marriage as Vocation

A key tension running through the novel is whether marriage is primarily a social institution — a contract governed by law, property, and convention — or a personal vocation — a lived commitment between two people. The novel's society treats marriage as the former; Levin and Kitty attempt to live it as the latter. Anna, trapped in an institutional marriage and seeking a vocational one, finds that society will not permit the transition.

## The Double Standard

The novel relentlessly exposes the gendered double standard in marriage. Stiva's affairs are winked at; Anna's affair destroys her. Vronsky's reputation is enhanced by the affair (he is seen as a romantic figure); Anna's is destroyed. Even Levin, the novel's moral center, is revealed to have given Kitty a diary of his past sexual experiences before their marriage — a scene that, while painful for Kitty, shows the asymmetry of what each gender is expected to bring to marriage.
