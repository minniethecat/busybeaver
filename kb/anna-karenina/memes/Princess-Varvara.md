## Identity & Role

**Princess Varvara** is introduced as [[Alexei-Karenin]]'s aunt, who takes up residence with [[Anna-Karenina]] and [[Alexei-Vronsky]] after Anna leaves her husband. Her official function is that of a chaperone — a respectable older female relative whose presence lends a semblance of propriety to Anna's unconventional living arrangement.

The reality is more ambiguous. Princess Varvara is described as comfortable, incurious, and easily satisfied:

> Princess Varvara was her husband's aunt, and she had long been living with Anna. (182-028)

She provides social cover without imposing moral judgment, making her the perfect companion for a woman whose position in society is precarious. But Anna herself acknowledges her limitations:

> Princess Varvara is no help, but a hindrance. (196-018)

## Biography / Arc

Princess Varvara first appears when Anna attends the opera with her:

> I'm not going alone. Princess Varvara has gone to dress, she is going with me. (164-019)

This plan provokes alarm in Vronsky, who understands better than Anna the social risk of appearing in public. The opera scene becomes a disaster — Anna is publicly humiliated — and Princess Varvara's presence as chaperone proves entirely ineffectual.

In Part Six, Princess Varvara joins the party at [[Vozdvizhenskoe]] during Dolly's visit. Dolly observes her with mixed feelings:

> What Dolly liked particularly was seeing Princess Varvara ready to overlook everything for the sake of the comforts of which she was now partaking. (185-010)

Princess Varvara's talent for overlooking uncomfortable truths is both her defining skill and her moral limitation. She enjoys the material comforts of Anna and Vronsky's household — the char-à-banc rides, the fine meals, the country air — without troubling herself about the ethical complexity of the situation.

During the drawing-room gathering, she is a fixture of quiet, undemanding presence:

> They went into the drawing-room and found Princess Varvara already there. (187-006)

When the conversation takes an uncomfortable turn, Princess Varvara's instinct is to smooth things over — to change the subject, to fill the silence, to maintain the surface of civility at all costs.

## Themes & Significance

- **Respectability as performance**: Princess Varvara's role is entirely performative — she provides the appearance of propriety without its substance. Her presence allows Anna and Vronsky to maintain the fiction that their household is socially acceptable.

- **Material comfort and moral complacency**: Dolly's observation that Princess Varvara is "ready to overlook everything for the sake of the comforts of which she was now partaking" (185-010) cuts to the heart of her character. She represents a type of moral accommodation that the novel treats with satirical sharpness.

- **The failed chaperone**: In a novel deeply concerned with the social regulation of female behavior, Princess Varvara is the chaperone who chaperones nothing. Her ineffectuality mirrors the broader collapse of the social codes she is meant to uphold.

## Key Passages

Dolly's assessment of Princess Varvara is the novel's most penetrating verdict on her:

> What Dolly liked particularly was seeing Princess Varvara ready to overlook everything for the sake of the comforts of which she was now partaking. (185-010)

Anna's blunt summation is equally damning:

> Princess Varvara is no help, but a hindrance. (196-018)

The juxtaposition of these two judgments — Dolly's ironic appreciation and Anna's weary dismissal — captures the character's tragicomic function in the novel.
