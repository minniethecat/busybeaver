## Definition & Scope

Religion in *Anna Karenina* serves multiple, often contradictory functions. It is a social institution that enforces conventional morality, a personal resource for those in crisis, a weapon of self-justification, and — in Levin's climactic spiritual awakening — a genuine answer to the question of how to live. Tolstoy's treatment of religion is characteristically divided: institutional religion is suspect, but authentic religious feeling is the novel's ultimate source of meaning.

Stiva Oblonsky's casual attitude toward religion in the opening pages sets the tone for much of Petersburg society: "religion is only a curb to keep in check the lower classes" (011-005). This instrumental view — religion as social control rather than spiritual truth — is the position the novel must work against to arrive at Levin's hard-won faith.

## Instances in the Text

### Karenin: Religion as Justification

After Anna leaves him, [[Alexei-Karenin]] turns to religion under the influence of Countess [[Lydia-Ivanovna]]. His refusal to grant Anna a divorce is framed in religious terms:

> "In accordance with the dictates of religion," he told himself. (090-015)

The novel is deeply skeptical of this religious posture. Karenin's piety gives him cover for what might otherwise be recognized as cruelty, transforming his wounded pride into moral principle. Yet Tolstoy does not dismiss Karenin's religious turn entirely — his forgiveness of Anna during her illness is a genuinely Christ-like act, suggesting that even compromised faith can produce real grace.

### Anna: Religion as Torment and Distance

For [[Anna-Karenina]], religion offers no comfort. After her social fall, she finds that "seeking help in her difficulty in religion was as remote from her as seeking help from Alexey Alexandrovitch himself" (092-007). Her exclusion from respectable religious society — from the church weddings, christenings, and funerals that mark the rhythms of social life — is part of her comprehensive isolation. Religion, far from being a refuge for the suffering, becomes another door closed against her.

### Levin: Religion as Existential Quest

[[Konstantin-Levin]]'s spiritual journey is the novel's most sustained engagement with religion. A man who "had never doubted the truths of religion" in his youth, he finds himself in adulthood unable to believe, yet unable to live without belief (125-030). His crisis is triggered by his brother Nikolai's death, which confronts him with mortality and meaninglessness.

Levin's eventual resolution — a conversion not to doctrinal Christianity but to a felt sense of the soul's relationship to the good — is crucial: "This life was disclosed in religion, but a religion having nothing in common with that which he had known from childhood" (075-001). Tolstoy presents Levin's faith as authentic precisely because it comes from experience rather than institution.

### The Peasants: Embodied Faith

The novel contrasts the troubled faith of the educated classes with the unreflective piety of the Russian peasantry. Levin observes that the peasants live by a moral code they do not articulate, and he envies their certainty. This romanticization of peasant faith is characteristic of Tolstoy's later religious thought, which would culminate in his rejection of institutional Orthodoxy in favor of a simplified, ethical Christianity.

## Development Across the Narrative

The treatment of religion in the novel moves from Stiva's cynical opening remark through Karenin's self-deceptive piety to Levin's climactic conversion. The arc is one of increasing sincerity: the novel strips away each false use of religion until only the essential question remains — how should a person live, and what makes that life meaningful?

Levin's answer — that "the law of good" is inscribed in the human soul and accessible to all — is the novel's final word on the subject. It is a religious conclusion that transcends any particular religion, aligning with Tolstoy's own evolving spiritual philosophy.

## Key Passages

Stiva's cynical instrumental view of religion:

> Religion is only a curb to keep in check the lower classes. (011-005)

Anna's spiritual isolation:

> Seeking help in her difficulty in religion was as remote from her as seeking help from Alexey Alexandrovitch. (092-007)

Karenin's self-justifying piety:

> "No honor, no heart, no religion; a corrupt woman." (090-006)

Levin's description of a new kind of religious knowledge:

> This life was disclosed in religion, but a religion having nothing in common with that which he had known from childhood. (075-001)

Levin's crisis of unbelief:

> He had never doubted the truths of religion, but his external unbelief did not in the least interfere with his inner certainty. (125-030)
