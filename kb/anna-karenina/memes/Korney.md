## Identity & Role

Korney is Alexei Alexandrovitch Karenin's valet and, after Anna's departure, the effective head of the Karenin household. He is referred to by his full patronymic — Korney Vassilievitch — by the other servants, a mark of the respect and seniority he commands within the domestic hierarchy [162-007]. A former soldier, Korney brings military directness to his management of the household, and his practical competence stands in quiet contrast to the abstract, ineffectual interventions of Countess Lydia Ivanovna, who nominally takes charge of Karenin's domestic affairs after Anna leaves [154-026].

## Biography / Arc

### Managing the Household After Anna's Departure

Korney's role expands dramatically after Anna abandons her husband and son. While Countess Lydia Ivanovna claims responsibility for reorganizing the Karenin household, the narrator reveals that "all her arrangements had to be modified because they could not be carried out, and they were modified by Korney, Alexey Alexandrovitch's valet, who, though no one was aware of the fact, now managed Karenin's household, and quietly and discreetly reported to his master while he was dressing all it was necessary for him to know" [154-026]. This passage encapsulates Korney's character — unobtrusive competence, discretion, and an instinct for what his master truly needs to hear.

### The Bill from the Draper's Shop

Two days after Anna's departure, Korney presents Karenin with an unpaid bill from a fashionable draper's shop. When Karenin struggles to maintain his composure — "several times attempted to speak and stopped short" — it is Korney who perceives his master's emotional state and acts decisively:

> "Korney, perceiving his master's emotion, asked the clerk to call another time." [153-004]

This small act of perception and initiative reveals Korney as more than a mechanical servant; he reads the emotional weather of the household and intervenes to protect his master's dignity. Karenin himself is aware that Korney has witnessed his collapse, and he feels "the weight of universal contempt and exasperation, which he had distinctly seen in the face of the clerk and of Korney" [153-005].

### Anna's Secret Return

When Anna secretly returns to the Karenin household to see her son Seryozha on his birthday, Korney plays a pivotal role. Discovering that the hall-porter Kapitonitch has admitted Anna without authorization, Korney takes charge of the crisis:

> "Korney, the valet, going down to the hall-porter's room, asked who had let her in, and how it was he had done so, and ascertaining that Kapitonitch had admitted her and shown her up, he gave the old man a talking-to." [162-004]

His contemptuous retort to Kapitonitch — "'Soldier!' said Korney contemptuously" [162-006] — reveals both his own military past (a former soldier dismissing a lesser one) and his hierarchical understanding of household order. He then coordinates with the nurse Marya Efimovna to prevent the catastrophic meeting between husband and wife:

> "'You, Korney Vassilievitch, you'd best keep him some way or other, the master, while I'll run and get her away somehow.'" [162-007]

### Korney in Anna's Nightmare

Korney appears in one of the novel's most haunting passages — Anna's recurring nightmare, which she recounts to Vronsky:

> "'And I began asking myself what it meant. And Korney said to me: "In childbirth you'll die, ma'am, you'll die...." And I woke up.'" [112-061]

The dream figure of Korney speaks in French-accented prophecy, a surreal intrusion of the domestic servant into the realm of fate. That it is Korney — not Karenin, not Vronsky, not any figure of romantic significance — who delivers the death prophecy in Anna's dream is one of Tolstoy's strangest and most suggestive choices, linking the mundane world of the household to the novel's deepest currents of doom.

### Announcing Oblonsky

Late in the novel, Korney continues his role as gatekeeper, announcing Stepan Arkadyevitch's arrival: "Stepan Arkadyevitch was about to go away when Korney came in to announce" [216-001]. Even in brief appearances, Korney's function is consistent — he is the membrane between the private world of the master and the public world of visitors, bills, and news.

## Relationships

### With Alexei Karenin

Korney's relationship with Karenin is that of the ideal servant in a Tolstoyan household — respectful, competent, and quietly indispensable. He manages Karenin's domestic life without ever appearing to manage it, and his reports, delivered during the intimate ritual of dressing, shape Karenin's understanding of his own home. The narrator notes that "no one was aware of the fact" [154-026] that Korney now ran the household, a testament to his skill at remaining invisible while exercising real authority.

### With Countess Lydia Ivanovna

The contrast between Korney and Lydia Ivanovna is structurally pointed. She offers Karenin spiritual guidance and Christian consolation; Korney offers practical management. Her arrangements "had to be modified because they could not be carried out" [154-026], and Korney is the one who modifies them. Tolstoy's preference for practical competence over abstract piety is quietly but unmistakably registered in this pairing.

### With the Servant Hierarchy

Korney occupies the top of the Karenin household's servant hierarchy. He gives orders to the hall-porter Kapitonitch, coordinates with the nurse Marya Efimovna, and is addressed by his patronymic — "Korney Vassilievitch" [162-007] — by those beneath him. His contemptuous "Soldier!" [162-006] to Kapitonitch signals his sense of superiority over the lower-ranking porter, even as both men share a military past.

## Themes & Significance

Korney belongs to the deep texture of Tolstoy's world — the servants, stewards, valets, and nurses who make the aristocratic household function while remaining largely invisible to its principals. His competence is the quiet infrastructure upon which Karenin's dignity depends, and the narrator's brief but precise attention to him — particularly the revelation that he "now managed Karenin's household" [154-026] — is characteristic of Tolstoy's determination to register the real distribution of power, as opposed to its official or social appearance.

Korney also participates in one of the novel's most symbolically charged moments. His appearance in Anna's nightmare [112-061] as the prophet of her death in childbirth is an instance of what the critic Boris Eikhenbaum identified as Tolstoy's technique of "defamiliarization" — the intrusion of the ordinary (a valet) into the extraordinary (a prophetic dream), rendered with such matter-of-fact precision that the effect is uncanny rather than absurd.
