# Grisha Oblonsky

## Identity & Role

Grisha Oblonsky is the youngest son of Stepan Arkadyevitch (Stiva) and Darya Alexandrovna (Dolly) Oblonsky. He appears throughout the novel in domestic scenes that illuminate the texture of family life in the Oblonsky household — its warmth, its chaos, and Dolly's devoted motherhood.

As a child character, Grisha has no independent arc, but his presence serves important narrative functions: he is one of the several Oblonsky children whose needs create both financial pressure and emotional meaning in Dolly's life. His interactions with his parents, his sister Tanya, and visitors to the household provide glimpses of childhood as Tolstoy renders it — vivid, unruly, and emotionally transparent.

## Biography & Arc

Grisha first appears in the novel's opening domestic scene. Stepan Arkadyevitch, just waking after the discovery of his infidelity, "recognized the voices of Grisha, his youngest boy, and Tanya, his eldest girl" [011-007] — the children's voices anchoring the household even as its foundations shake.

His little sister points to an item and says "For Grisha?" [011-019], one of the small, telling exchanges that establish the texture of sibling life.

When Anna Karenina visits the Oblonsky household, she encounters Grisha directly. Dolly tells him "Keep your hands still, Grisha" [027-002], and Anna exclaims "Is this Grisha? Heavens, how he's grown!" [027-009] — the standard observation of a visiting adult, but one that marks Grisha's place in the continuous life of the family.

Dolly's pride in her children and the effort they require find expression in a passage about teaching Grisha: "You wouldn't believe it, I was teaching Grisha just now: once this was a joy, now it's a torment" [027-038]. The ambivalence of motherhood — love mixed with exhaustion — surfaces here.

Grisha's mischievousness punctuates domestic scenes. He "poked his little face under his mother's arm" [028-009] and is chided "Grisha, don't pull my hair. It's untidy enough as it is" [028-024]. At one lunch, "Grisha began whistling, and, what was worse, refused to obey the governess and was left without dessert" [085-008] — a miniature domestic drama that prompts Dolly to reflect on the difficulty of consistent discipline.

Dolly's financial worries are crystallized in a practical moment: "I must get coats, you know, for Grisha and Tanya. Give me the money" [115-027]. The children's material needs drive her negotiations with her spendthrift husband.

A bright spot comes when "Dolly took Tanya and Grisha to a children's ball at the Sollogubovs'" [148-004] — a glimpse of the children entering the wider social world their parents inhabit.

Later, hearing "Alenka's contralto voice, calling Grisha, and a smile of delight passed over her face" [169-003] at the sound of the household's rhythms. Similarly, "to guard them from Grisha, she was calling little Masha" [170-002] shows Grisha as a force of childhood chaos that must be managed.

Grisha and Tanya's squabbling is noted with Dolly's characteristic mixture of exasperation and pride: "her pride in her children. Grisha and Tanya had been fighting over a ball" [087-049].

## Relationships

- **Darya Alexandrovna (Dolly)** — his mother, whose life is largely defined by caring for Grisha and his siblings. Dolly's love for Grisha is fierce but also exhausted.
- **Stepan Arkadyevitch (Stiva)** — his father, whose absence and financial irresponsibility create hardship for Grisha and the other children.
- **Tanya Oblonsky** — his older sister and frequent partner in childhood mischief and squabbles.
- **Anna Karenina** — his aunt, who visits the Oblonsky household and notes how Grisha has grown.

## Themes & Significance

Grisha represents the Oblonsky children as a collective — the innocent dependents whose welfare Dolly protects while Stiva evades responsibility. His childhood moments (fights over balls, pulling hair, whistling at lunch) are rendered with Tolstoy's characteristic realism about children: they are neither sentimentalized nor ignored, but presented as fully human beings whose inner lives matter.

The contrast between Dolly's constant attention to Grisha's needs and Stiva's cheerful neglect is one of the novel's quiet indictments of aristocratic male freedom. Grisha needs coats, food, discipline, and love — and his father provides money only when forced, while his mother provides everything else.

## Key Passages

> Stepan Arkadyevitch recognized the voices of Grisha, his youngest boy, and Tanya, his eldest girl. [011-007]

The children's voices open the novel's first domestic scene — life continuing regardless of adult dramas.

> "You wouldn't believe it, I was teaching Grisha just now: once this was a joy, now it's a torment." [027-038]

Dolly's confession to Anna captures the emotional cost of motherhood in a single line.

> Grisha began whistling, and, what was worse, refused to obey the governess and was left without dessert. [085-008]

A miniature moral drama in a child's world — the consequence of disobedience, and Dolly's internal wrestling with the pain of enforcing it.

> "I must get coats, you know, for Grisha and Tanya. Give me the money." [115-027]

The practical urgency of children's needs versus Stiva's financial carelessness, compressed into one demand.
