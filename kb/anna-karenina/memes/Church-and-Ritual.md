# Church and Ritual in Anna Karenina

The Orthodox Church occupies a paradoxical position in *Anna Karenina*: it is omnipresent in the rituals that structure Russian aristocratic life — weddings, confessions, fasts, services — yet it rarely provides the spiritual resources that characters actually need. Tolstoy renders church ritual with extraordinary sensory precision while exposing the gap between liturgical performance and genuine religious experience.

## The Wedding of Levin and Kitty

The novel's most sustained treatment of church ritual is the wedding of Konstantin Levin and Kitty Shcherbatskaya, which occupies most of Part 5, chapters 1–6. "In the church there was all Moscow" [^PN-137-001] — the ceremony is simultaneously a sacrament and a society event, and Tolstoy refuses to separate the two. The crowd "thronging round the church lighted up for the wedding" [^PN-135-001] is as much a part of the scene as the priest and the icons.

Levin, who has not confessed or taken communion for many years, is required to undergo confession before the wedding. The scene is treated with Tolstoy's characteristic blend of comedy and seriousness: the young priest asks Levin whether he believes in the teachings of the Church, and Levin's honest reply — that he doubts — creates an awkwardness that the priest, in pastoral wisdom, resolves by granting absolution anyway. The ritual accommodates doubt even as it demands belief.

The ceremony itself is rendered in meticulous physical detail: the "piece of pink silken stuff" on the lectern [^PN-138-001], the wax dripping from the candles, the "church became so still that the drops could be heard falling" [^PN-136-020]. This precision is Tolstoy's theological argument in narrative form — the sacred is not behind or beyond the physical world but within it.

## Confession and Fasts

The practice of confession appears throughout the novel as both social obligation and potential site of spiritual reckoning. Karenin, after Anna's confession of infidelity, goes "to church service" [^PN-117-001] and spends the morning in prayer — but his churchgoing is inseparable from his self-righteousness. Tolstoy suggests that Karenin uses the forms of religion to avoid its substance: confession becomes a performance of virtue rather than an act of contrition.

Levin's earlier encounter with the Church is described as a "period of fasts and monks and church services, when he was seeking" [^PN-032-002] — the past tense marking his subsequent departure from observance. For the young Levin, the Church was a place of seeking rather than finding, and his return to it for the wedding is an act of social conformity as much as faith.

## The Village Church

The novel's most authentic religious setting is not the Moscow cathedral but the village church. When Levin enters "the church where there was no one but the peasant" [^PN-085-006], the emptiness is itself a kind of presence. Here, religion is not spectacle but quiet practice — the old women at their prayers, the beggar soldier, the "idle time of standing in church" [^PN-133-013] that is both tedious and sacred.

Levin takes "special trouble to keep up the church in his village" [^PN-103-003], and the maintenance of the physical church becomes an analogue for the maintenance of spiritual life — both require regular attention, both deteriorate without care, and neither can be delegated to others. The peasant church embodies a faith that is woven into the rhythms of agricultural life rather than imposed upon it from above.

## Ritual Without Faith

The novel also depicts church ritual as empty form — social machinery operating without spiritual content. The "highest dignitaries of all the churches and sects" [^PN-074-002] who attend society functions represent an institutional religion that has merged with the state. Anna's inability to find solace in the Church after her fall reflects not only her personal spiritual condition but the failure of institutional religion to reach those who most need it.

The "unification of the churches" [^PN-040-015] is mentioned as a diplomatic project, not a spiritual one — the language of bureaucratic consolidation applied to matters of faith. Tolstoy's critique, developed more fully in his later theological writings, is already present here in compressed form: the Church has become an extension of the state, and its rituals, however beautiful, cannot substitute for the inward transformation that genuine religion demands.
