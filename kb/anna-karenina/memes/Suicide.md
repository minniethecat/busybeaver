# Suicide in Anna Karenina

Suicide is the catastrophic culmination of Anna Karenina's tragic arc and one of the novel's most powerful motifs. From the railway worker's death in the opening chapters through Anna's self-destruction under the train wheels, Tolstoy presents suicide as both a philosophical crisis and a narrative engine. The motif extends beyond Anna: both Vronsky and Levin contemplate taking their own lives, making suicide a lens through which the novel examines the existential condition of its characters.

## Anna's Suicide

Anna's death at the railway station in Part 7 is the novel's most harrowing set piece. After receiving Vronsky's casually written note — "I am very sorry your note did not reach me. I will be home at ten" [^PN-228-015] — she makes her way to the station in a state of mounting desperation. "A ticket to Obiralovka?" asks Pyotr; she has "utterly forgotten where and why she was going" [^PN-227-005] and only by effort understands the question. The station is the same liminal space where her affair with Vronsky began, and the symmetry is devastating: the train that first brought Vronsky into her life now takes her out of it.

As she waits on the platform, her mind circles through the grievances of her life. "And all at once she thought of the man crushed by the train the day she had first met Vronsky, and she knew what she had to do" [^PN-228-019]. She goes to the rails with "a rapid, light step," calculating the precise moment to throw herself between the wheels: "there, in the very middle, and I will punish him and escape from everyone and from myself" [^PN-228-021]. At the critical instant, a red bag delays her hand and she misses the first carriage; she must wait for the next. When it comes, she throws herself below its wheels, and the light by which she had read the book of her life "flared up brighter than ever, lighted up for her all that had been in darkness, flickered, began to grow dim, and was quenched forever."

## The Railway Worker's Foreshadowing

The novel's first death is a railway watchman crushed by a train at the Moscow station where Anna first meets Vronsky. A bystander remarks "What a horrible death!" [^PN-026-057], and Anna whispers, "It's an evil omen" [^PN-026-047]. This death structurally prefigures Anna's own end: the worker is killed by the machinery of progress, just as Anna will be destroyed by the society that the railway symbolizes. The repetition of the railway motif creates the sense of fate that pervades the novel.

## Vronsky's Suicide Attempt

After Anna nearly dies in childbirth and Karenin forgives her, Vronsky is overwhelmed by shame. "Pulling the revolver to the left side of his chest, and clutching it vigorously with his whole hand... he pulled the trigger" [^PN-gSTsTQ]. He survives but is wounded. Unlike Anna's carefully meditated suicide, Vronsky's attempt is impulsive and borne of humiliation — a response to being morally bested by the husband he has wronged. After Anna's actual death, Vronsky is shattered beyond even suicide: "my use as a man is that life's worth nothing to me" [^PN-233-009]. He volunteers for the Serbian war seeking death in battle rather than at his own hand.

## Levin's Suicidal Thoughts

Even Levin, the novel's moral center, is not immune. As a "happy father and husband, in perfect health," he is "several times so near suicide that he hid the cord that he might not be tempted to hang himself, and was afraid to go out with his gun for fear of shooting himself" [^PN-237-013]. His suicidal thoughts arise from the same existential terror that afflicts Anna: "He saw nothing but death or the advance towards death" in everything [^PN-H5tUZl]. The crucial difference is that Levin finds his way through the crisis through peasant faith, while Anna is offered no such redemption.

## The Epigraph and Moral Framework

The novel's epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — frames Anna's suicide within a theological context that Tolstoy refuses to simplify. The narrative does not moralize Anna's death; it simply records the terrible logic of her destruction. Her suicide is both punishment and release, a moment of horror that the society which condemned her absorbs without genuine reckoning.
