## Identity & Role

**Tushkevitch** is a fixture in the Petersburg social scene, closely associated with [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]'s salon. He is described as possessing a polished, almost eighteenth-century charm:

> "He always reminds me of something Louis Quinze about Tushkevitch," said one observer, glancing towards the handsome young man. (048-011)

He functions as a type of the idle aristocracy — always present at social gatherings, always impeccably dressed, always ready with a graceful remark — but without any discernible occupation or purpose beyond social performance.

## Biography / Arc

Tushkevitch appears throughout the novel as a recurring background figure in Petersburg society scenes. He is first noted at Princess Betsy's salon (048-011), where his relationship with the hostess is the subject of quiet speculation among the other guests.

He accompanies Betsy at her country estate in Part Three, where she suggests he partner with Masha for a game of croquet (094-010). His perpetual availability underscores his role as a social accessory.

In Part Five, Tushkevitch appears at a gathering where the conversation turns to the famous singer Patti. He offers to procure a box for the opera:

> "I can get one," Tushkevitch offered his services. (164-008)

During the visit to [[Vozdvizhenskoe]] in Part Six, Tushkevitch is among the party that includes [[Vassenka-Veslovsky]], [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]], and others. He participates in the outdoor amusements with the same easy grace he brings to Petersburg drawing rooms (185-009).

## Themes & Significance

- **The idle aristocracy**: Tushkevitch embodies the emptiness of Petersburg high society — a young man of charm and means who fills his days with calls, dinners, and country visits, contributing nothing beyond his agreeable presence.

- **Social performance**: His character illustrates how Petersburg society values surface over substance, style over sincerity. Tushkevitch is always appropriate, never authentic.

- **Ambiguous intimacies**: The whispered speculation about Tushkevitch's relationship with Princess Betsy (048-013) exemplifies the novel's interest in the blurred boundaries between social friendship and romantic entanglement.

## Key Passages

Tushkevitch's character is most vividly sketched through the impressions he makes on others:

> Of her secret connection with Tushkevitch it was all easy, while to her husband she could not speak of it. (064-021)

This passage, referring to Anna's thoughts about her own situation, uses Tushkevitch as a counterpoint — his liaison with Betsy is an open secret, managed with the smooth hypocrisy that Anna finds increasingly impossible.
