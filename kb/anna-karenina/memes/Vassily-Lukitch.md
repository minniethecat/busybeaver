## Identity & Role

**Vassily Lukitch** is the tutor employed by [[Alexei-Karenin]] to educate his son [[Seryozha-Karenin]]. He appears primarily in Part Five, during the period when Seryozha is separated from his mother [[Anna-Karenina]] and living under the rigid governance of his father's household.

Unlike the cold formalism that characterizes Karenin's approach to child-rearing, Vassily Lukitch shows genuine warmth towards his charge. He is described as capable of a rare smile:

> "You tell me," said Vassily Lukitch with a smile, which was rare with him. (159-023)

His position places him at the intersection of Seryozha's emotional world — the boy's longing for his mother, his prayers, his secret thoughts — and the institutional discipline of lessons and schedules imposed by Karenin.

## Biography / Arc

Vassily Lukitch appears in the chapters surrounding Anna's secret visit to her son. He is first seen calling Seryozha to his lessons:

> "Run along, run along, Vassily Lukitch is calling you," said the porter. (158-017)

When Seryozha delays, pleading for "a tiny minute," Vassily Lukitch waits with patient indulgence. During the lesson, Seryozha asks him an unrelated question about the Vladimir Cross, which the tutor answers straightforwardly — he is thinking "of nothing but the necessity of learning the lesson" (158-029), unaware of the deeper currents in the boy's mind.

On the morning of Anna's visit, Vassily Lukitch is in an unusually good humor and shows Seryozha a new way to do subtraction:

> It turned out happily for Seryozha. Vassily Lukitch was in a good humor, and showed him how to do it. (159-017)

Seryozha confides in his tutor about his nightly prayers, asking him to guess what he prays for. Vassily Lukitch's gentle response — "You tell me" — reveals a man who, though bound by his professional role, is capable of tenderness (159-023).

When Anna arrives, Vassily Lukitch initially fails to recognize her:

> Meanwhile Vassily Lukitch had not at first understood who this lady was. (162-001)

Once he realizes, he is caught between his duty to Karenin and the obvious intensity of the mother-son reunion. He retreats tactfully, understanding that some moments transcend the rules of the household.

## Themes & Significance

- **Institutional care vs. human warmth**: Vassily Lukitch occupies the liminal space between the cold, rule-bound world of Karenin and the emotional needs of a lonely child. His small kindnesses — a rare smile, a new way to do sums — take on outsized importance in Seryozha's constrained world.

- **The servant as witness**: As a tutor, Vassily Lukitch is both insider and outsider in the Karenin household. He observes the family's dysfunction from close range but has no power to address it. His silent witness to Anna's visit is one of the novel's most emotionally charged moments.

- **Ordinary decency**: Vassily Lukitch is not a heroic figure, but his simple decency — showing Seryozha kindness when no one else does — represents a quiet moral good in a novel filled with grand passions and grand failures.

## Key Passages

The most significant moment for Vassily Lukitch is his silent recognition of Anna:

> Meanwhile Vassily Lukitch had not at first understood who this lady was, and learned from their conversation that it was the mother who had left her husband, whom he did not know, as he had entered the house after she had gone. (162-001)

His decision to step aside rather than enforce protocol allows the novel's most poignant reunion scene to unfold.
