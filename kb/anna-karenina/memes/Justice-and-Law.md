# Justice and Law

Justice and law form a significant thematic undercurrent in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, particularly through the character of Alexei Karenin, a high-ranking government official whose professional life in the legal system contrasts with his inability to find personal justice. The novel critiques the gap between institutional justice and moral fairness.

## The Legal World of Karenin

Alexei Karenin inhabits a world of courts, wardships, and legal administration (013-035). His professional identity is defined by the legal structures of imperial Russia, yet Tolstoy uses Karenin's career to highlight the limitations of formal justice. The novel questions whether institutional justice can address the complexities of human relationships.

## Injustice and Social Inequality

Levin feels acutely "the injustice of his own abundance in comparison with the poverty of the peasants" (034-001), recognizing that economic inequality is a form of injustice that no court addresses. This sense of moral injustice drives Levin's agricultural reforms and his search for a more equitable relationship with the land and its workers.

Tolstoy extends this critique through discussions of property law. Characters "deny the justice of property, of capital, of inheritance" (109-005), reflecting the radical ideas circulating in Russian society during the period. The novel presents these critiques without fully endorsing them, leaving the question of economic justice unresolved.

## Personal Justice vs. Legal Justice

The novel contrasts formal legal proceedings with the characters' desire for personal justice. When Anna feels wronged by Karenin's refusal to grant a divorce, she seeks a justice the law cannot provide (090-007). Karenin, for his part, "weeping over the injustice of his punishment" (085-009), experiences suffering as an unjust infliction despite his own legalistic approach to life.

The local justices of the peace appear as figures of limited authority, handling minor disputes while the novel's deeper questions of justice remain beyond their jurisdiction (104-003). Anna reflects that she "felt all the justice of his words" (113-019) regarding Karenin's arguments, acknowledging that justice can be recognized even when it conflicts with personal desire.
