## Identity & Role

**Arseny Lvov** (referred to throughout the novel simply as "Lvov") is the husband of Natalia (Natalie) Shcherbatskaya, [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]]'s eldest sister. He is a diplomat by profession, a man of quiet dignity and understated intelligence.

He first enters the narrative indirectly, mentioned when [[Darya-Alexandrovna]] (Dolly) married [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]] — at that time, Natalia had already "married the diplomat Lvov" (014-003). His presence in the novel grows more substantial in Part Six, where Levin visits him in Moscow.

## Biography / Arc

Lvov's most significant appearance occurs in Part Six, when Levin — newly married and temporarily in Moscow — calls on him. Levin finds Lvov at home in a house coat, surrounded by his children, and the two men share a conversation that reveals Lvov's character with quiet precision.

Lvov has just returned from a meeting of a learned society, which he found "very interesting" (201-008). Levin is struck by Lvov's intellectual modesty — despite being clearly intelligent and well-educated, Lvov habitually underestimates himself:

> Levin, as he always did, felt touched at Lvov's low opinion of himself. (201-010)

The visit offers Levin a glimpse of a different model of married life — calm, intellectual, domestic — that contrasts with his own restless energy. Lvov's household, with its quiet order and intellectual contentment, represents an alternative ideal of family happiness.

At Kitty and Levin's wedding in Part Five, Lvov's wife Natalia (Madame Lvova) serves as Kitty's bridal-mother (135-004), underscoring the family connection.

## Themes & Significance

- **Models of marriage**: Lvov and Natalia's marriage offers one of the novel's several templates for conjugal life — dignified, affectionate, intellectually companionable — distinct from both the passionate turbulence of Anna and Vronsky and the hard-won harmony of Levin and Kitty.

- **Intellectual humility**: Lvov's self-deprecation stands in marked contrast to the performative brilliance of Petersburg salon culture. His quiet intelligence represents a Tolstoyan ideal of substance over display.

- **The diplomat as observer**: As a diplomat, Lvov inhabits a professional world that the novel largely leaves off-stage, yet his temperament — calm, observant, judicious — embodies the diplomatic virtues.

## Key Passages

Levin's visit to Lvov in Part Six (201-001 through 201-010) contains the novel's most sustained attention to this character:

> Lvov, the husband of Natalia, Kitty's sister, had spent all his life in foreign capitals, where he had been educated and had been in the diplomatic service. (201-001)

> Lvov was at home, and Levin went in to see him unannounced. (201-004)

> Lvov, in a house coat with a belt and in chamois leather shoes, was sitting in an armchair reading a book. (201-005)

The domestic image — Lvov at ease among his books and children — presents a quietly compelling picture of the intellectual life integrated into family happiness.
