## Overview

The Russian nobility (*dvoryanstvo*) depicted in *Anna Karenina* is the hereditary landowning and service class that dominated Russian society in the late 19th century. Tolstoy portrays the nobility not as a monolithic entity but as a class divided between the high aristocracy of Petersburg, the service nobility tied to government rank, and the landed gentry who lived on and worked their country estates.

> Besides the general election of the marshal of the nobility, the nobility of the Kashinsky province were occupied with questions relating to the finances of the nobility and the female high school. (191-010)

The novel's social critique is directed largely at the moral failures of this class: its hypocrisy, its economic parasitism, and its inability to adapt to a changing Russia. Yet Tolstoy also presents the nobility's country variant — embodied by [[Konstantin-Levin]] — as containing the seeds of moral regeneration through connection to the land.

## Role in the Narrative

### Petersburg High Society

The novel opens a window onto the highest circles of Petersburg aristocracy through the characters of [[Anna-Karenina]], [[Alexei-Karenin]], [[Alexei-Vronsky]], and Princess Betsy Tverskaya. This world is characterized by French conversation, elaborate social rituals, and a morality that values appearance over substance. Adultery is tolerated so long as it remains discreet; the sin is not the act but the scandal.

> The majority of the younger men envied Vronsky precisely for what was the most irksome part of his love affair — the exalted position of Karenin, and the consequent publicity of their liaison in society. (060-002)

### The Moscow Nobility

Moscow's aristocratic circles, represented by the Shcherbatsky family and [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]], are less formal than Petersburg's but no less concerned with status. The Moscow ball where Kitty expects a proposal from Vronsky is a concentrated portrait of the marriage market that structured noble society.

### The Country Gentry

Levin represents a different model of nobility: the landowner who lives on his estate, works alongside his peasants, and seeks meaning in productive labor rather than social display. His struggles with agricultural reform reflect the real economic pressures facing the Russian nobility in the post-Emancipation era:

> Besides the impoverishing of the nobility to which I belong, and, in spite of the universal impoverishment, the improving of my private affairs... (059-017)

### The Provincial Nobility

In Part 6, the novel depicts provincial noble assemblies in vivid detail. The election of the marshal of the nobility in Kashinsky province is a set piece of political theater — "men in uniforms of the nobility, buttoned up closely, with sword-belts" (192-003) — that reveals the class's attachment to ceremonial forms even as its economic foundations crumble.

## Social / Political Context

### Post-Emancipation Crisis

The novel is set in the 1870s, a decade after the Emancipation of the Serfs (1861) had fundamentally altered the economic basis of noble life. The nobility, deprived of serf labor, was caught between declining agricultural incomes and the need to maintain the lifestyle that defined their social position. Stiva's sale of his wife's forest and Levin's experiments with sharecropping both reflect this economic reality.

### The Question of Reform

The novel's noble characters divide along the fault line of reform. Levin and his half-brother [[Sergius-Ivanitch]] represent the reformist impulse — one practical, one intellectual — while the more conservative nobles resist change. The debates about the zemstvo (local government), agricultural methods, and education that run through the novel are not digressions but evidence of a class struggling to define its role in a modernizing nation.

### Morality and Hypocrisy

Tolstoy's deepest critique of the nobility is moral. The class that prides itself on honor and refinement is shown to be riddled with sexual hypocrisy, economic exploitation, and spiritual emptiness. A man like Stiva Oblonsky "would have received this post through the influence of his sister Anna's husband" (013-001) — the entire system runs on connections rather than merit, and the result is a class that has lost any genuine claim to leadership.

## Key Passages

The economic anxiety of the landed gentry:

> Besides the impoverishing of the nobility to which I belong, I must also think of improving my private affairs. (059-017)

The mechanics of noble patronage:

> Through his sister Anna's husband, Alexey Alexandrovitch Karenin, who held one of the most important posts in the ministry. (013-001)

The theatrical quality of provincial noble politics:

> Men in old uniforms of the nobility, buttoned up closely, with sword-belts. (192-003)

A reformist's disdain for noble privilege:

> He despised the nobility, and believed the mass of the nobility to be of no real use. (103-003)
