## Location & Setting

**Pokrovskoe** is [[Konstantin-Levin]]'s country estate, located approximately 200 miles from Moscow in central Russia. It is described as a working agricultural property with a manor house, farm buildings, fields, meadows, and forest. The estate is Levin's ancestral home, managed by him personally rather than by stewards, and it represents his commitment to the land and to a life of productive work.

> The big, old house at Ergushovo was near Pokrovskoe, and Dolly was staying there with her children. (084-001)

Pokrovskoe is contrasted throughout the novel with the artificiality of Petersburg and Moscow society. It is not merely a setting but a value system: the country represents authenticity, labor, and connection to the natural order, while the city represents pretense, idleness, and moral confusion.

## Physical Description

### The Manor House

The house at Pokrovskoe is an old country manor, comfortable but not grand — appropriate to a family of the lesser nobility who work their own land. It features a terrace overlooking the garden, a study where Levin does his accounts and reads his agricultural journals, and rooms that Kitty transforms after their marriage.

### The Farm Buildings

The estate includes barns, stables, a dairy, and quarters for the peasants who work the land. Levin's agricultural innovations — new machinery, different crop rotations, cooperative arrangements with the peasants — are centered here. The farm is not merely picturesque; it is a site of constant practical concern, and Levin's struggles with his peasants and his land are among the novel's most sustained subplots.

### The Landscape

The surrounding countryside includes "the meadows, the forests, the cornfields" (089-007) that Levin knows intimately. The estate's natural setting provides many of the novel's most lyrical passages, particularly during the mowing scene where Levin experiences a moment of transcendent unity with the land and the peasants who work it.

## Significance in the Narrative

### Levin's Moral Laboratory

Pokrovskoe is where Levin conducts his practical and spiritual experiments. His attempts to reform agricultural practice — introducing new machinery, negotiating new terms with the peasants — represent his broader search for right relationship between landowner and laborer, aristocrat and peasant. The estate is the material foundation for his philosophical questioning.

### The Setting of Married Life

After his wedding to Kitty, Pokrovskoe becomes the setting for their domestic life. Kitty transforms the house, bringing order and beauty to spaces that had been merely functional under Levin's bachelor management. The arrival of their first child at Pokrovskoe completes the estate's transformation from a man's retreat to a family's home.

> The summer with her children at Pokrovskoe, at her sister Kitty Levin's, was a time of great happiness for Dolly. (166-001)

### A Place of Visitors

Pokrovskoe receives many of the novel's characters: Dolly and her children, Levin's brother [[Sergius-Ivanitch]], and various neighbors and peasants. These visits allow Tolstoy to test the values of different characters against the standard of country life, and Pokrovskoe consistently emerges as the measure of authenticity against which city life is found wanting.

### The Contrast with City Life

The novel repeatedly juxtaposes the vitality of Pokrovskoe with the sterility of Petersburg and Moscow. When Levin visits the city, he feels contaminated by its artificiality; when he returns to Pokrovskoe, "driving from the railway station" to the estate, he feels restored (089-014). The estate is not merely a place but an argument — Tolstoy's argument for a life lived close to the land.

## Key Passages

The vitality of country life at Pokrovskoe:

> "Is the house standing still, and the birch trees, and our nursery?" Levin asks, homesick for his estate (033-048).

The estate as alternative to fashionable life:

> The necessity of work? Leave Pokrovskoe? Buy land? Become a member of some rural board? (089-007)

Dolly's happiness at the estate:

> The summer with her children at Pokrovskoe, at her sister Kitty Levin's, was a time of great happiness. (166-001)

The estate as touchstone of homecoming:

> Driving from the railway station to Pokrovskoe, everything seemed charming to Levin. (089-014)
