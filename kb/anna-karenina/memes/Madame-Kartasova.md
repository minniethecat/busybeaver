# Madame Kartasova

## Identity & Role

Madame Kartasova is a minor figure whose single dramatic action — a scene of public humiliation directed at [[Anna-Karenina]] at the Petersburg theater — proves to be one of the novel's most devastating social moments. She is described as "a thin little woman" [165-032] whose venom is disproportionate to her physical stature.

Her narrative function is precise: she is the instrument through which Petersburg society delivers its verdict on Anna. Where others have gossiped privately or distanced themselves discreetly, Madame Kartasova chooses the most public possible forum for her condemnation, and in doing so crystallizes the social death sentence that the novel's more refined characters have been constructing through indirection.

## Biography / Arc

Madame Kartasova appears in a single, searing episode. Anna, back in Petersburg and hoping to re-enter society, attends the theater with [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]. The event is freighted with tension from the start — Anna knows she is being watched, knows her presence will be judged.

The Kartasova incident unfolds with brutal economy. "Madame Kartasova, a thin little woman, was standing up" [165-032] in the box adjacent to Anna's. What begins as a social slight escalates rapidly. Kartasova "made a scene" [165-045], saying "something insulting" [165-045] to Anna across the boxes. The public nature of the attack — the theater, with its tiers of watching faces — is central to its cruelty.

Anna's companions are horrified. Princess Varvara Oblonsky declares it "mean and hateful" and insists that "Madame Kartasova had no right to do it" [165-039]. But the damage is done: the scene confirms to all of Petersburg society that Anna is no longer one of them. Anna leaves the theater in humiliation, and the episode marks the definitive end of her attempt to maintain her social position.

## Relationships

- **[[Anna-Karenina]]**: Kartasova is Anna's public accuser. The two have no prior relationship of note; Kartasova acts not from personal grievance but as an enforcer of social norms.
- **Princess Varvara Oblonsky**: Varvara, who accompanies Anna to the theater, is outraged by Kartasova's behavior and defends Anna verbally, calling the act "mean and hateful" [165-039].

## Themes & Significance

The Kartasova episode distills the novel's critique of social morality into a single, brutal scene. Kartasova is not a villain in any nuanced sense — she is simply the society woman willing to make explicit what everyone else thinks. Her "thin little" [165-032] frame emphasizes the disproportion between her physical insignificance and the damage she inflicts.

The theater setting is thematically charged: Anna is on display, watched by the same society that has been scrutinizing her since her return from Italy. The attack comes not from a rival lover or a wronged husband but from a woman whose only motivation is the enforcement of social codes. Kartasova's cruelty is the cruelty of the collective — impersonal, righteous, and final.

Tolstoy does not develop Kartasova as a character. He does not need to. Her anonymity — a "thin little woman" with no interior life — is precisely the point. The forces that destroy Anna are not great passions or tragic flaws alone; they are also the accumulated petty cruelties of people like Madame Kartasova, who enforce the rules not because they understand them but because they enjoy the power of enforcement.

## Key Passages

> Madame Kartasova, a thin little woman, was standing up in the box next to theirs, and, looking at Anna, was saying something insulting to her husband. [165-032, 165-045 combined]

The physical description ("thin little woman") and the setting (the adjoining box) compress the entire social dynamic into a single image: the small, the petty, standing in judgment over the magnificent.

> "It's mean and hateful, and Madame Kartasova had no right to do it. Madame Karenina..." [165-039]

Varvara's protest is the lone voice of defense in the theater. Her indignation is sincere, but it cannot undo the scene's effect.
