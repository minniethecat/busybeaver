# Kitty Shcherbatskaya

**Kitty Shcherbatskaya** (full name Ekaterina Alexandrovna Shcherbatskaya) is the younger daughter of Prince and Princess Shcherbatsky, sister of Dolly Oblonskaya, and ultimately the wife of [[Konstantin-Levin]]. She is one of the central characters in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, serving as a counterpoint to Anna's tragic arc through her own journey from romantic infatuation to mature, grounded love.

## Identity & Role

Kitty is introduced as the youngest daughter of the aristocratic Shcherbatsky family in Moscow. At the age of eighteen, she makes her debut in society and becomes the object of affection for both Konstantin Levin and Count [[Alexei-Vronsky]]. Levin, a longtime family friend, has known Kitty since childhood: "Kitty was still a child when Levin left the university" (014-003). Young, beautiful, and sheltered, Kitty initially embodies the romantic idealism of a girl on the brink of womanhood in Russian high society.

## Biography / Arc

Kitty's character arc unfolds across three distinct phases: romantic delusion, spiritual crisis, and mature fulfillment.

### Initial Rejection of Levin

At the novel's opening, Levin comes to Moscow to propose to Kitty. However, Kitty, infatuated with the dashing Count Vronsky, refuses Levin. She is certain Vronsky will propose to her, having been encouraged by her mother and society expectations. As Tolstoy writes, "it seemed to him that Kitty was so perfect in every respect" (014-004), and Levin, aware of his own perceived inadequacies, accepts her rejection with despair.

### Vronsky's Abandonment and Illness

Kitty's world collapses when Vronsky, after the ball where he meets Anna Karenina, abandons her without a formal proposal. The humiliation sends Kitty into a profound depression and physical illness. This crisis forces her family to take her abroad to German spas for recovery, where she encounters the devout Varenka and begins a spiritual awakening.

### Marriage to Levin

Upon returning to Moscow, Kitty meets Levin again. This time, mature and humbled by her experience, she accepts his proposal. Their courtship, wedding, and early married life at Pokrovskoe form the novel's central domestic counterpoint. The text captures the sweetness of their renewed connection at the skating rink: "When Levin darted up to Kitty her face was no longer stern" (017-034). Their marriage, though not without struggles, deepens into mutual understanding and love, culminating in the birth of their son.

## Themes & Significance

Kitty embodies Tolstoy's ideal of womanhood grounded in family, faith, and domestic devotion. Her arc functions as both a counterpoint to Anna's destructive passion and an illustration of how genuine love can flourish within the structures of marriage and family. Where Anna's story spirals toward isolation and death, Kitty's moves toward community, motherhood, and spiritual peace. Her willingness to nurse Levin's dying brother Nikolay in their home reveals the depth of her compassion and her embrace of the messy realities of family life.
