## Identity & Role

Mademoiselle Varenka is the adopted daughter and constant companion of Madame Stahl, the invalid Russian lady at the German spa of Soden. Her role is pivotal in Kitty Shcherbatskaya's moral and spiritual transformation during Part Two of the novel. Varenka embodies a selfless, duty-driven ideal of womanhood — one that stands in stark contrast to the social climbing and vanity of Russian high society. Kitty, wounded by Vronsky's rejection, takes Varenka as her model for a life of service and moral purpose.

> "Of Mademoiselle Varenka one would not say that she had passed her first youth, but she was, as it were, a creature without youth; she might have been taken for nineteen or for thirty." [072-005]

Her background is revealed in Chapter 74: she is the daughter of a chef to the Imperial Household, taken in by Madame Stahl after her father's disgrace and early death.

## Biography & Arc

### Origins

Varenka's father was a French chef employed in the Imperial Household. After his involvement in some scandal, he died young, leaving Varenka orphaned. Madame Stahl, who had learned of her situation while abroad, took her in, raised her, and treated her as her own daughter — though Varenka's role remained that of a devoted companion and nurse:

> "This was Varenka. Madame Stahl learned later on that Varenka was a girl with no relations of her own, and Varenka lived with her all the while abroad." [074-002–003]

### Life at the German Spa

At Soden, Varenka attends to Madame Stahl with unwavering dedication, always at her side, conducting herself with a serene dignity that captivates Kitty:

> "Varenka, in her dark dress, in a black hat with a turn-down brim, was walking up and down the whole length of the path with a blind Frenchwoman, and every time she met Kitty, their eyes exchanged friendly glances." [073-002]

She also cares for an old blind Frenchwoman and the children of the Petrov family who are also at the spa, demonstrating a life entirely given over to service:

> "Varenka, mamma's calling!" they cried from the Petrov children's side of the lawn. [073-038]

### Relationship with Kitty

Kitty becomes enchanted by Varenka's quiet, selfless nature. She sees in Varenka everything she wants to become:

> "What attraction of Mademoiselle Varenka? Kitty felt that in her, in her manner of life, she would find a model of what she was now so painfully seeking: interest in life, a dignity in life — apart from the worldly relations of girls with men, which so revolted Kitty..." [072-006]

Varenka treats Kitty with simple kindness, singing for the princess at her request, though "she obviously thought nothing of her singing" [074-013]:

> "'I'm very glad it gives you pleasure,' Varenka answered simply." [074-012]

### Background Revelation

The princess, initially suspicious of Varenka's intimacy with Kitty, investigates her background and learns the full story — that Varenka is of common birth, illegitimate perhaps, and that Madame Stahl had essentially adopted her. This only increases her respect for Varenka:

> "After getting to know Varenka, Kitty became more and more fascinated by her friend, and every day she discovered new virtues in her." [074-005]

## Relationships

### With Madame Stahl

Varenka is utterly devoted to Madame Stahl, whom she calls "maman" and serves as both nurse and companion. The relationship defines her existence, giving her a purpose and a social identity she would otherwise lack as the orphaned daughter of a disgraced chef:

> "Madame Stahl called her Varenka, and other people called her 'Mademoiselle Varenka.'" [072-004]

### With Kitty Shcherbatskaya

Varenka and Kitty's friendship is the emotional core of Part Two. Varenka unwittingly becomes Kitty's model for moral reformation, though Varenka herself seems unaware of the impression she makes:

> "Varenka flushed a little. 'I don't remember... I don't think she ever did anything particularly special...'" [073-023]

Kitty's admiration for Varenka represents her pendulum swing from vanity (the ball, Vronsky) toward a self-abnegating ideal of service — a swing that Tolstoy treats with characteristic irony, as Kitty's "vocation" for nursing proves short-lived.

### With the Spa Community

Varenka serves the blind Frenchwoman, the Petrov children, and other invalids at the spa. She is a universal caregiver, equally at ease with the high-born princess and the poorest patient — a quality that marks her, in Tolstoy's moral scheme, as genuinely good.

## Themes & Significance

Varenka represents Tolstoy's ideal of the selfless woman — one whose life is given entirely to others without the taint of ego or social ambition. She is the counterpoint to the worldly society ladies (Betsy Tverskaya, Countess Nordston) who populate Moscow and St. Petersburg.

However, Tolstoy's treatment is characteristically nuanced: Kitty's attempt to model herself on Varenka ultimately fails — not because Varenka's example is false, but because Kitty's own nature is different. The implication is that virtue cannot be imitated; it must arise from one's own authentic being.

Varenka's background — illegitimate daughter of a disgraced chef — also carries a subtle social critique: true worth has nothing to do with birth or title, and the saintliest character in the novel's German spa chapters comes from the humblest origins.
