## Location & Setting

**Vozdvizhenskoe** is [[Alexei-Vronsky]]'s country estate, where he retreats with [[Anna-Karenina]] after leaving Petersburg society. Unlike [[Pokrovskoe]], which Levin inherited and works with his peasants, Vozdvizhenskoe is a project of improvement — a model estate run with modern efficiency and hired labor.

The estate embodies Vronsky's approach to country life: rational, modernizing, and driven by principles of good management rather than emotional attachment. It is well-funded and well-appointed, with a new hospital Vronsky builds for the peasants (P02-795), a model farm, and imported English cattle. Yet for all its material excellence, it lacks the organic vitality of Levin's Pokrovskoe — the sense that the land and its people are part of a living whole rather than components of an efficient system.

## Vronsky's Hospital and Agricultural Projects

Vronsky pours considerable energy and money into estate improvements. The hospital he builds for the peasants is a concrete project that gives him a sense of purpose after leaving military service. He installs modern agricultural equipment, brings in an English steward, and attempts to run the estate on rational principles.

These projects serve a dual purpose: they give Vronsky an occupation when Petersburg society doors close to him after his affair with Anna, and they represent his attempt to build an alternative world — one where modern efficiency replaces aristocratic tradition. The hospital in particular is a point of pride, though local peasants view it with suspicion as another form of lordly interference.

## Contrast with Pokrovskoe

The contrast between Vozdvizhenskoe and Levin's Pokrovskoe is one of the novel's structural oppositions. Levin's estate is inherited, worked with personal involvement, and bound by tradition and affection; Vronsky's is acquired, managed at a distance through stewards, and driven by abstract principles. Where Levin feels the land's rhythms in his body, Vronsky sees it as a canvas for improvement.

This contrast maps onto the larger thematic opposition between organic and artificial life in the novel. Vozdvizhenskoe, for all its material comfort, remains a stage set — a place where Vronsky and Anna attempt to construct a life that society refuses to grant them. The estate cannot compensate for their social isolation.

## Life at Vozdvizhenskoe

During the period when Anna and Vronsky live there together, Vozdvizhenskoe becomes a gilded cage. Anna, cut off from society and from her son Serezha, finds the estate's luxuries hollow. Vronsky pursues his projects with the energy of a man trying to prove something — to himself, to Anna, and to the world that has rejected him.

The isolation of country life intensifies the tensions in their relationship. Without the distractions of Petersburg society, their mutual suspicions and resentments grow. Vozdvizhenskoe, meant to be their refuge, becomes the setting for their unraveling. The estate's very perfection — its well-run efficiency, its modern improvements — underscores the emotional sterility at its heart.
