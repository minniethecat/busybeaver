# The Moscow Ball

The Moscow ball is the novel's first major turning point — the event where the romantic geometry of Anna, Vronsky, and Kitty crystallizes into its tragic configuration. It is a masterful set-piece that encapsulates the novel's themes: the power and danger of romantic passion, the cruelty of social expectations, and the moment when lives change irrevocably.

## Overview

Kitty attends the ball expecting Vronsky to propose to her. She has refused Levin for him, and all of Moscow society expects the match. But when Anna appears at the ball — beautiful, married, and older — Vronsky's attention shifts entirely. Kitty watches as the man she expected to marry spends the evening dancing with Anna, utterly captivated.

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|

| Kitty Shtcherbatskaya | The evening's casualty — humiliated before society |
| Levin | Absent from the ball, having been rejected earlier |

## Narrative Significance

The ball scenes demonstrate Tolstoy's ability to render social rituals with psychological precision. Every glance, every dance, every unspoken expectation is mapped. Kitty's humiliation is rendered not through melodrama but through the accumulation of small observations — Vronsky's distractedness, Anna's glow, the whispers of the other guests.

For Kitty, the ball is the end of her girlhood illusions. For Anna, it is the beginning of a passion that will consume her. For Vronsky, it is the moment when he chooses — carelessly, as he has always lived — the woman who will become his fate.

## Thematic Resonance

The ball embodies one of the novel's central insights: that world-changing events do not announce themselves. Anna and Vronsky go to a dance, and by the end of the evening, three lives have been fundamentally altered. The social ritual of the ball — designed to contain and channel romantic feeling into acceptable forms (courtship, marriage) — becomes instead the mechanism of its explosion.
