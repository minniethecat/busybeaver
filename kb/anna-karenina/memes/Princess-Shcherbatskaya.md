# Princess Shcherbatskaya

**Princess Shcherbatskaya** (referred to in the narrative primarily as "the old princess") is the matriarch of the Shcherbatsky family in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. Mother of [[Darya-Alexandrovna]], [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]], and Natalia Lvova, she is a practical-minded aristocratic woman whose social ambitions for her youngest daughter nearly lead to disaster, before she ultimately embraces Levin as the right husband for Kitty.

## Identity & Role

The old princess is a product of Moscow aristocratic society, deeply invested in the marriage market as the primary mechanism for securing her daughters' futures. Unlike her husband, who trusts his instincts about character, she is guided by social calculation. Her initial preference for [[Alexei-Vronsky]] as a suitor for Kitty reflects the values of her world: rank, wealth, and brilliance. Only after Vronsky's abandonment of Kitty does she recognize the wisdom of her husband's preference for Levin.

## Biography / Arc

### The Marriage Campaign

At the novel's opening, the princess is consumed with Kitty's marriage prospects. She has managed successful matches for her two elder daughters and now turns her attention to the youngest. Her campaign to secure Vronsky for Kitty is methodical and socially astute—but it blinds her to Vronsky's true character and to Kitty's deeper compatibility with Levin.

### Crisis and Reassessment

Vronsky's abandonment of Kitty precipitates a family crisis. The princess, humiliated and worried for Kitty's health, takes her daughter abroad for a cure—a journey of physical and spiritual recovery. During this period, the princess's worldview shifts. She comes to value Levin's steadfastness and recognizes that her social ambition nearly cost Kitty her happiness.

### Acceptance of Levin

By the time of Kitty and Levin's marriage, the princess has fully accepted her son-in-law. She visits the young couple at Pokrovskoe: "the old princess too came to stay that summer" (166-001). In the novel's later scenes, she appears as a devoted grandmother, her earlier social scheming replaced by contentment with the domestic life her daughter has built. During Kitty's labor, "the old princess, hurriedly coming out at the door" (166-016) embodies maternal concern rather than social ambition.

## Themes & Significance

The old princess represents the practical, socially-minded maternal figure that Tolstoy both critiques and ultimately humanizes. Her journey from social schemer to contented grandmother mirrors the novel's broader argument that genuine human connection—family, love, children—matters more than social standing. Unlike Anna's society friends (such as [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]), the princess's fundamental morality is sound; it is merely her priorities that need correction.

## Key Passages

> The princess's persistent presence in the young couple's life: "the old princess too came to stay that summer" at Pokrovskoe (166-001)

> Counting her family at table: "every day it happened that the old princess, sitting down to table, counted them over" (166-002), a domestic ritual that underscores her devotion to family.

> During Kitty's labor, maternal instinct takes over: "the old princess came hurriedly out of Kitty's room" (166-016), her earlier social pretensions forgotten in genuine concern.
