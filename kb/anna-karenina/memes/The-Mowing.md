# The Mowing

## Overview

The mowing scene occurs in Part Three of *Anna Karenina*, during the early summer at Levin's country estate. Levin, frustrated by the abstract debates of his half-brother Sergey Ivanovitch and the ongoing tension with his estate management, decides to join his peasants in the physically demanding work of mowing the meadow. What begins as an impulsive experiment becomes one of the novel's most celebrated passages — a sustained depiction of physical labor as a path to spiritual clarity (079-003, 081-001).

## Participants

- **Konstantin Levin**: The landowner who joins the mowers. His participation is both a rejection of gentlemanly detachment and a search for authentic experience. Throughout the scene, he oscillates between awkward self-consciousness and moments of pure absorption in the rhythm of the work (081-002).
- **Tit**: A young peasant who mows ahead of Levin, setting the pace. Tit's effortless, economical movements serve as an implicit standard against which Levin measures his own labor. He is "a thin, small peasant" whose skill makes the work look easy (081-040).
- **Fomitch**: The estate steward whom Levin consults about when to begin the mowing (079-010).
- **The Peasant Mowers**: An anonymous collective of men and women who work the field, their physical competence and unselfconscious rhythm forming a contrast to Levin's self-aware effort (081-022).

## Narrative Sequence

### Lead-Up

The mowing is preceded by Levin's domestic restlessness. His half-brother's visit has unsettled him, and he is preoccupied with doubts about his agricultural reforms. The decision to mow is impulsive — he watches the peasants working and feels an overwhelming urge to participate: "he determined he would go mowing, however awkward he might feel" (081-003). The steward Fomitch supports the idea, though with a hint of amusement (079-010).

### The Mowing Itself

Levin takes a scythe and joins the line of mowers. The initial experience is humiliating — he cannot keep pace, his technique is clumsy, and he repeatedly fears he will collapse from exhaustion (081-030). The peasants, by contrast, move with a fluid efficiency that seems almost mechanical: "mowing slowly over the uneven, low-lying ground" (081-023). 

The turning point comes when Levin stops thinking about his performance and surrenders to the physical rhythm. He enters a state of complete absorption — his mind empties of everything except the motion of the scythe and the grass falling before him. In these moments, he experiences a profound happiness that eludes him in his intellectual and social life: "the hours passed without his noticing them" (081-043).

### Aftermath

The scene concludes with Levin physically exhausted but spiritually renewed. He returns home to find that Sergey Ivanovitch has been observing him, and the two share a meal. The mowing has shifted something in Levin — it has given him a felt connection to the peasant world that his theoretical sympathies could never achieve (081-049).

## Consequences & Significance

The mowing scene is one of Tolstoy's most direct expressions of his philosophy of physical labor as moral therapy. For Levin, intellectual effort — whether in agricultural theory, philosophy, or social reform — always leaves him divided and dissatisfied. Physical labor, by contrast, dissolves the barrier between self and world. The scene anticipates the novel's broader argument that authentic life is found not in reflection but in action, not in the drawing room but in the field.

Tolstoy also uses the mowing to dramatize the social distance between Levin and his peasants. Even as he mows alongside them, sharing their physical exertion, he remains an outsider — a gentleman playing at labor. The peasants are kind to him, but they do not mistake his experiment for genuine membership in their world. This tension between Levin's desire for solidarity and the reality of class difference runs throughout the novel.

The mowing also establishes a pattern that will recur in Levin's story: moments of perfect clarity and happiness that he cannot sustain or intellectualize. The happiness of mowing is fragile — it depends on not thinking about it. As soon as Levin tries to analyze or reproduce the experience, it slips away.

## Key Passages

> His thoughts passed to the mowing. He always felt something special, something that drew him, in this work.
> (079-005)

> He determined he would go mowing, however awkward he might feel, and however much his brother and the people might laugh at him.
> (081-003)

> They were mowing slowly over the uneven, low-lying ground, one behind another in a long line.
> (081-023)

> Before him Tit's upright figure mowing away, the crescent-shaped curve of the scythe flashing, the grass falling in a swath — and the sense that he could go on forever without tiring.
> (081-040)

> The peasants had been mowing no less than four hours without stopping, and Levin felt that he could go on forever.
> (081-043)
