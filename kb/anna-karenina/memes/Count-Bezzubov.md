# Count Bezzubov

**Count Bezzubov** is a French spiritualist and clairvoyant who operates under the assumed name **Landau** in the highest circles of St. Petersburg society. He is a key figure in Countess [[Lydia-Ivanovna]]'s religious-spiritual coterie and, through her influence, gains a hold over [[Alexei-Karenin]] at a pivotal moment in the narrative.

## Identity & Role

Count Bezzubov is introduced late in the novel as a figure wrapped in mystification. His real name is Bezzubov, but he is presented to St. Petersburg society as "Landau," a clairvoyant of French origin (218-009). The double name — Bezzubov (Russian: "toothless") and Landau (the French-sounding spiritualist persona) — is itself a source of mockery among the more skeptical characters (219-002). His role is that of a spiritual authority figure: he conducts mesmeric séances, gives counsel that is treated as divine revelation, and becomes a tool through which Lydia Ivanovna shapes Karenin's decisions regarding Anna.

## Biography / Arc

Bezzubov's public career in the novel is brief but consequential. It is reported that he "completely cured Countess Bezzubova" — presumably his wife or a relative — which earned Countess Lydia Ivanovna's devotion (218-010, 217-028). She in turn promoted him as a spiritual guide.

When Karenin is struggling with the question of whether to grant Anna a divorce, Lydia Ivanovna brings him to a séance with Landau. Karenin arrives with Countess Lydia Ivanovna and is announced together with "Count Bezzubov" to the porter (218-003). During the session, Landau falls into a trance and pronounces — in French — that the answer to Karenin's question has already been given, that his memory will return, and that the "struggle" was the decisive factor. This pronouncement, interpreted by Lydia Ivanovna, seals Karenin's refusal to grant Anna a divorce (219-002).

After this episode, Bezzubov disappears from the narrative. The skeptical characters, including Stepan Arkadyevitch, treat the whole business with contemptuous amusement — noting that "he is really Count Bezzubov" behind the Landau name (218-009).

## Relationships

- **Countess Lydia Ivanovna**: His chief patron in St. Petersburg. She promotes him as a spiritual authority and interprets his pronouncements to serve her own agenda regarding Karenin.
- **Alexei Karenin**: Bezzubov's most consequential "client." Through Lydia Ivanovna's mediation, Landau's mesmeric pronouncement becomes the divine warrant for Karenin's refusal to divorce Anna.

## Themes & Significance

Count Bezzubov / Landau represents the intersection of religious mysticism and social manipulation in Tolstoy's critique of St. Petersburg high society. His dual identity — Russian nobleman by birth, French clairvoyant by performance — embodies the inauthenticity that Tolstoy saw in the capital's spiritual fashions. The name "Bezzubov" (literally "toothless") is a satirical touch, undermining the grandeur of the Landau persona.
