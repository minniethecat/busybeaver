# Friendship

Friendship in *Anna Karenina* provides a counterpoint to the novel's dominant themes of romantic love and marriage. It serves as a social bond that reveals character, enables confidences, and in some cases, falters under the pressure of competing loyalties.

## Levin and Stiva: Unequal Friendship

The friendship between [[Konstantin-Levin]] and [[Stiva-Oblonsky]] is one of the novel's most sustained relationships. Though temperamentally opposite — Levin serious and introspective, Stiva carefree and sociable — their bond endures through shared history:

> In spite of his friendship with Oblonsky, Levin felt that he and all his family were infinitely beneath them. [014-003]

Levin's insecurity about class and worth manifests in his friendship with Stiva. Yet Stiva's easy warmth and refusal to judge make him an indispensable bridge between Levin and the social world, including his pivotal role in reuniting Levin with [[Kitty]].

## Anna and Dolly: Confidantes Across Crisis

[[Dolly]] serves as the novel's moral witness, and her friendship with [[Anna-Karenina|Anna]] is tested by Anna's adultery. Dolly initially defends Anna, understanding the loneliness of an unhappy marriage. But after visiting Anna and [[Alexei-Vronsky|Vronsky]] at their country estate, Dolly's sympathy wavers as she perceives the precariousness of Anna's position.

## Friendship and Social Boundaries

The novel depicts friendship as constrained by gender norms. Male friendships — Levin and Stiva, Vronsky and [[Serpuhovskoy]] — allow for open discussion of career, desire, and philosophy. Female friendships, such as between Kitty and [[Varenka]], operate in narrower domestic and spiritual spheres. Cross-gender friendships, like Anna's with various male acquaintances, are scrutinized for any hint of impropriety.
