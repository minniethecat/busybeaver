# Mademoiselle Linon

**Mademoiselle Linon** is the French governess in the [[Prince-Shcherbatsky|Shcherbatsky]] household, responsible for the education of the three Shcherbatsky sisters — including [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya|Kitty]], whom she regards as her favorite pupil. A background figure in the domestic scenes of Parts One and Five, she embodies the cosmopolitan, Francophone culture of the Russian aristocracy.

## Identity & Role

Mademoiselle Linon serves as governess to the Shcherbatsky household, a position typical of noble Russian families who employed French or Swiss tutors to educate their daughters in languages, music, and social graces. Her presence is noted in the daily rhythms of the household: the three young ladies speaking French one day and English the next, practicing piano at appointed hours, receiving instruction from visiting professors of French literature [014-002].

Kitty is her "favorite pupil" [125-023], and Mademoiselle Linon's affected but tender manner toward her suggests genuine affection beneath the professional formality.

## Biography / Arc

Mademoiselle Linon appears briefly but at telling moments. In Part One, she accompanies the three Shcherbatsky sisters on their coach ride to the Tverskoy boulevard — part of the social routine that defines their sheltered world [014-002].

Her most narratively significant appearance comes in Part Five, when [[Konstantin-Levin|Levin]] arrives at the Shcherbatsky house to see Kitty. Mademoiselle Linon is the first person he encounters, and her presence becomes an obstacle — she "vanished from Levin's eyes" only when Kitty approaches, the governess's departure signaling the transition from the public, chaperoned space to the private reunion of the lovers [124-011]. At the same visit, she interrupts their conversation "with an affected but tender smile" to congratulate Kitty, and her playful suggestion that "we shall have sweetmeats to eat" sends Levin off on a cheerful errand [125-024].

## Relationships

- **Kitty Shcherbatskaya**: Her "favorite pupil"; Mademoiselle Linon's affection for Kitty is warm but professionally contained.
- **Levin**: A courteous but distant relationship. Mademoiselle Linon serves as both gatekeeper and enabler — her departure clears the way for Levin's reunion with Kitty, and her sweetmeats request provides him a simple, happy task.
- **The Shcherbatsky family**: A loyal retainer in a household that values European cultivation.

## Themes & Significance

Mademoiselle Linon is a minor character who illustrates the **French cultural influence** in Russian aristocratic life. The governess, speaking French and teaching European accomplishments, is a fixture of the world Tolstoy depicts — a world whose cosmopolitanism would become increasingly contested in the later parts of the novel, as Levin's Slavophilic tendencies and the novel's moral seriousness push against the Francophone drawing-room culture.

Her role as a narrative device is equally telling: she is a benign obstacle, a figure whose presence marks the boundary between Kitty's public, family-governed life and her private relationship with Levin. Her exit from a room signals a shift from formality to intimacy.

## Key Passages
