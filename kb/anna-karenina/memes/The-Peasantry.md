## Definition & Scope

**The peasantry** (the *muzhiks*) functions in *Anna Karenina* as both a social reality and a symbolic resource. On one level, peasants are the laborers who work [[Konstantin-Levin]]'s land, the backdrop of an agricultural economy in the post-Emancipation era. On another level, they are the bearers of an authentic wisdom that the novel's aristocrats and intellectuals have lost — a wisdom grounded in physical labor, connection to the land, and an unreflective acceptance of life's rhythms.

## Levin and the Peasants

Levin's relationship with the peasantry is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring this theme. Unlike [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]], who sells his wife's forest to a merchant without a second thought, Levin works alongside his peasants, taking up the scythe during the hay harvest and experiencing the "long, exhausting, and yet blissful" labor that connects him to something larger than himself.

The mowing scene (Part Three, Chapters 4–5) is the novel's most extended treatment of peasant life, and it functions as a kind of secular communion. As Levin swings his scythe in rhythm with the peasants, the narrator describes a state of "unconsciousness" — a release from the self that Levin's intellectualizing mind cannot otherwise achieve:

> "The longer Levin mowed, the oftener he felt the moments of unconsciousness in which it seemed not his hands that swung the scythe, but the scythe swinging a body full of life and consciousness of its own."

## The Peasant as Moral Authority

Several peasant characters function as moral touchstones for Levin. **Ivan Parmenov**, the young peasant whose harmonious relationship with his wife moves Levin to envy, represents an ideal of natural domesticity. **Fyodor the peasant** (Part Eight, Chapter 12), who tells Levin that one man "lives for his belly" while another "lives for his soul, for God," triggers Levin's culminating spiritual insight. The teaching does not come from the intellectual Koznishev or the church, but from an illiterate peasant who articulates a truth Levin has been struggling toward through the entire novel.

## Post-Emancipation Agriculture

The novel also engages concretely with the economics of post-Emancipation agriculture. Levin's struggles with his peasants — their resistance to new methods, their suspicion of his motives, the difficulty of implementing rational farming on communal land — reflect the actual tensions of Russian agricultural reform in the 1870s. [[Sviazhsky]] represents the progressive landowner who has adopted European methods; Levin's own approach is more cautious, grounded in the recognition that reform must respect peasant custom.

## The Peasant as Symbol vs. Reality

Critics have noted a tension in Tolstoy's treatment of the peasantry. The peasants function symbolically as embodiments of authentic wisdom, yet as individuals they remain largely undifferentiated — types rather than characters. The novel's investment in individual psychology extends to its aristocrats and intellectuals but not to the peasants who serve as their moral instructors. This asymmetry reflects both the limitations of Tolstoy's own class position and the novel's thematic priorities: the peasant exists, in part, to teach the aristocrat something about himself.

## Key Passages
