# Turovtsin

## Identity & Role

Turovtsin is a minor but memorable character in *Anna Karenina* — a friend of Stepan Arkadyevitch Oblonsky whose defining trait is a magnificent, infectious laugh. He is described directly by the narrator as "good, simple man" [118-003], and this judgment holds throughout his appearances.

He moves in the Oblonsky social circle and appears at dinner parties, social gatherings, and in moments that illuminate the domestic lives of the central characters. His simplicity and good-heartedness serve as a quiet moral contrast to the sophisticated calculation of many other figures in the novel.

## Biography & Arc

Turovtsin first appears at a dinner party alongside the Shcherbatskys, Kitty, and Karenin [118-002]. His role in the conversation is modest — he speaks "simply to avoid being silent" [118-003] — but his presence is warmly received.

His most distinctive characteristic is his laugh. When someone tells a joke, "Turovtsin in especial roared loudly" [119-017], and he "exploded in a loud roar of laughter" [119-033] at another. His mirth is so physical that he once "in his mirth dropped his asparagus with the thick end in the sauce" [119-040], a comic detail that Tolstoy uses to render him vivid and endearing.

Levin admires him: "How nicely Turovtsin laughs!" [120-005]. Dolly too regards him with warmth, recalling a story about "Turovtsin in the scarlet fever" [120-013] — a hint of his history of kindness in the Oblonsky household.

His finest moment comes at a dinner when he breaks into a conversation about a disgraced man and declares simply: "Acted like a man, he did! Called him out and shot him!" [126-001]. Stiva recalls this as a phrase "of stupid, good-natured Turovtsin" that perfectly crystallized a moral judgment everyone else was dancing around.

Later, Dolly recalls him as "Stiva's good-hearted friend, Turovtsin, who had helped her nurse her children through scarlet fever" [181-018] — revealing the depth of his practical kindness during a family crisis.

In the novel's later sections, Turovtsin appears at a social venue with a young officer from Petersburg named Gagin, whom he introduces [204-009, 204-011].

## Relationships

- **Stepan Arkadyevitch Oblonsky (Stiva)** — close friend, part of Stiva's circle of bon vivants and social companions. Turovtsin's loyalty extends to helping Dolly during family illness.
- **Darya Alexandrovna (Dolly)** — Turovtsin helped nurse her children through scarlet fever, earning her lasting gratitude [181-018].
- **Konstantin Levin** — Levin admires Turovtsin's laugh and good nature [120-005], seeing in him a simple decency he values.
- **The Shcherbatsky circle** — Turovtsin moves among the social world of the Oblonskys and Shcherbatskys.

## Themes & Significance

Turovtsin represents a Tolstoyan ideal in miniature: the goodness of simple, unreflective decency. Unlike the sophisticated Stiva, who charms but ultimately fails those who depend on him, Turovtsin simply shows up and helps — nursing children through scarlet fever, speaking moral truth at a dinner party, laughing without self-consciousness.

His characterization is an example of Tolstoy's technique of revealing character through a single vivid trait (the laugh) and then deepening it with a single act of moral significance (the defense of the man who fought a duel). The contrast between "stupid, good-natured Turovtsin" and the clever, evasive talk of the other dinner guests makes a quiet philosophical point.

## Key Passages

> Turovtsin — good, simple man — felt unmistakably that he had been drawn into a gathering that was glad to see him. [118-003]

The narrator's direct judgment establishes Turovtsin's moral standing.

> Turovtsin exploded in a loud roar of laughter... and in his mirth dropped his asparagus with the thick end in the sauce. [119-033] [119-040]

The physical comedy of Turovtsin's laugh renders him one of the most vivid minor characters in the novel.

> "Acted like a man, he did! Called him out and shot him!" [126-001]

In a single phrase, Turovtsin cuts through social equivocation and delivers an unambiguous moral judgment that Levin and Stiva both recognize as just.

> Stiva's good-hearted friend, Turovtsin, who had helped her nurse her children through scarlet fever. [181-018]

This brief recollection reveals Turovtsin's history of practical, selfless kindness — the quality that distinguishes him from Stiva's more glamorous but less reliable companions.
