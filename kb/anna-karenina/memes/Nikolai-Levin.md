# Nikolai Levin

**Nikolai Dmitrievich Levin** (also rendered as Nikolay Levin or Nicholas Levin) is the elder brother of [[Konstantin-Levin]] in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. An intelligent but self-destructive radical, Nikolai embodies the failure of abstract intellectualism untethered from moral and spiritual grounding. His illness and death form a crucial turning point in Levin's own philosophical development.

## Identity & Role

Nikolai is introduced as Levin's estranged elder brother. The narrative describes him as having been a brilliant student who squandered his talents: "This brother Nikolay was the elder brother of Konstantin Levin, and had once been a promising student" (016-016). He has become a radical, living among the urban poor, espousing communist ideas, and drinking heavily. Levin's relationship with him is fraught with shame, pity, and guilt—emotions that push Levin toward deeper self-examination.

## Biography / Arc

### Early Promise and Decline

Before the novel's events, Nikolai showed great academic promise. However, he abandoned conventional society, became involved with radical political circles, and descended into alcoholism and dissipation. Levin recalls him with painful ambivalence: "He remembered his brother Nikolay, and felt ashamed and sore" (018-044).

### Life Among the Radicals

When Levin visits Nikolai in his squalid lodgings, he finds him living with Marya Nikolaevna, a former prostitute whom Nikolai treats with alternating tenderness and cruelty. Nikolai spends his days debating political economy and denouncing the exploitation of the working class: "I'll tell you what for! ... to save them out of their slavery" (033-011). His radicalism is passionate but incoherent—a symptom of his deeper spiritual unrest rather than a coherent political program.

### Illness and Death

Nikolai contracts tuberculosis, and his health rapidly deteriorates. Levin and his new wife [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]] travel to care for him in his final days. Kitty's quiet, practical compassion in the face of Nikolai's squalor and suffering profoundly moves Levin. Nikolai's death becomes the crucible through which Levin confronts the problem of mortality and begins his turn toward faith.

## Themes & Significance

Nikolai functions as a thematic foil to Levin. Where Levin seeks meaning through work, family, and faith, Nikolai seeks it through radical politics and sensual indulgence—and finds only despair. His deathbed scene is one of the novel's most harrowing passages, forcing Levin to recognize the inadequacy of purely rational approaches to the problem of death. As the text notes, Levin "recalled his brother Nikolay, and dwelt with pleasure on the thought that he would do everything possible to make up for his past shortcomings" (032-001).

## Key Passages

> "This brother Nikolay was the elder brother of Konstantin Levin, and had once been a promising student" (016-016)

> Nikolai's letter to his brothers reveals his self-awareness: "I humbly beg, and earnestly entreat you both ... to forgive me" (016-022)

> In his squalid apartment, Nikolai's intellectual passion burns alongside his physical decay: "Nikolay Levin went on talking" (033-004), arguing politics even as his health fails.
