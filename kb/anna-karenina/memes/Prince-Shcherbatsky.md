# Prince Alexander Shcherbatsky

**Prince Alexander Shcherbatsky** (referred to in the narrative as "the old prince") is the patriarch of the Shcherbatsky family in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. Father of [[Darya-Alexandrovna]] (Dolly), [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]], and Natalia (who marries the diplomat Lvov), the old prince is a representative of the old Russian aristocracy: proud, sharp-tongued, protective of his daughters, and possessed of a native wisdom that cuts through social pretension.

## Identity & Role

The old prince belongs to the generation of Russian nobility that predates the Westernized, cosmopolitan aristocracy of St. Petersburg. He is conservative in the best sense—rooted in tradition, family, and the land. Tolstoy presents him as a figure of earthy good sense, particularly in matters of the heart. "The old prince, like all fathers indeed, was peculiarly difficult about his daughters" (020-006), a watchful guardian who instinctively mistrusts the fashionable Vronsky.

## Biography / Arc

### Patriarch of the Shcherbatsky Household

The prince presides over a household in Moscow that includes his wife (the "old princess") and his unmarried daughter Kitty. His elder daughters Dolly and Natalia are already married, and the family's social standing is secure. The prince maintains a country estate and stud farm, embodying the traditional Russian landowner: "The old prince too had come in from his stud farm" (044-007).

### The Question of Kitty's Suitors

The prince's most significant role in the narrative concerns Kitty's marriage prospects. While the old princess schemes for a match with the brilliant Count Vronsky, the prince favors [[Konstantin-Levin]]—a preference rooted not in social calculation but in character judgment. His instinctive wariness of Vronsky proves prescient. When Vronsky arrives at the Shcherbatsky home, the prince observes him coolly: "'Come, let us play the ring game,' said the old prince, looking at Vronsky, and guessing his intentions" (022-068).

### Later Years

After Kitty's marriage to Levin, the old prince visits the young couple at Pokrovskoe and continues to offer his gruff wisdom. The country lodge on Levin's estate "had been pulled down long ago, and the old prince had had the lodge done up" (084-001), illustrating the ongoing connection between the families.

## Themes & Significance

The old prince represents an older, more authentic Russian aristocracy—one not yet corrupted by the superficiality of Petersburg society. His preference for Levin over Vronsky is more than personal taste; it embodies a moral judgment about what kind of man is worthy of his daughter. Through the prince, Tolstoy affirms the values of landed tradition, family loyalty, and instinctive wisdom over social calculation.

## Key Passages

> "The old prince, like all fathers indeed, was peculiarly difficult about his daughters" (020-006)

> The prince greets Levin with warmth: "'Very glad to see you.' The old prince embraced Levin, and talking to him, did not observe Vronsky" (022-065)

> At his stud farm, the prince embodies the traditional landowner: "The old prince too had come in from his stud farm" (044-007), stroking Kitty's hair with paternal affection.
