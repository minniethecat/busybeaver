# Liza Merkalova

## Identity & Role

Liza Merkalova is a young married woman who moves in the highest circles of Petersburg society. She is first mentioned in Part Three as a name in [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]'s social inventory, and she appears in person during Anna's return to Petersburg society in Part Five. She is described as possessing a "characteristic and intelligent face" [095-024] and is known for her unconventional personal arrangements.

Her role in the novel, though contained within a few chapters, is thematically significant: she embodies the sophisticated moral ambiguity of the Petersburg salon world, where adultery is tolerated so long as it is conducted with a certain style. Her presence sharpens the novel's critique of a society that embraces her while it condemns Anna — the difference being one of discretion, not of conduct.

## Biography / Arc

Liza Merkalova first enters the narrative indirectly, when Betsy reminds Anna of her social set: "Liza Merkalova and Baroness Shtoltz were coming" [092-004]. She is part of the glittering constellation around Betsy, whose salon is the epicenter of Petersburg's fashionable infidelity.

When Anna encounters Liza Merkalova in person at Betsy's, the young woman is accompanied by Stremov. The narrator notes that "Liza Merkalova was his wife's niece" [095-024] — a detail that adds a layer of familial impropriety to their public pairing. The elderly admirer of Liza Merkalova is identified as "Alexey Alexandrovitch's enemy in the political world" [094-001], linking personal and political rivalries in the dense web of Petersburg society.

Liza's marital arrangement is an open secret. Betsy remarks that "Liza Merkalova's husband carries her shawl, and is always ready to be of use" [094-033] — a wry acknowledgment of the arrangement in which the husband is reduced to an accessory while his wife enjoys the attentions of more prominent men.

During Anna's evening at Betsy's, Liza Merkalova displays "childlike affection" [095-034] toward Anna, and Anna is drawn to her charm and the "social atmosphere" [095-034] she radiates. Liza herself voices the ennui beneath the surface: "how dreary it all was!" [095-022] she exclaims, a confession that the glittering social round leaves her empty.

## Relationships

- **[[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]**: Liza is a fixture in Betsy's salon and part of her inner social circle [092-004].
- **[[Stremov]]**: Liza appears publicly with Stremov, and the two are described as "the cream of the Petersburg world" [094-017]. The nature of their relationship is left implicit but unmistakable.
- **[[Anna-Karenina]]**: Liza is drawn to Anna and shows her "childlike affection" [095-034]. Anna, for her part, finds the young woman's charm and frankness appealing, though the evening at Betsy's ultimately leaves her more isolated than before.
- **Merkalov (husband)**: An almost invisible figure — Betsy's remark about him "carrying her shawl" [094-033] speaks volumes about the power dynamics of their marriage.
- **[[Sappho-Shtoltz]]**: Mentioned alongside Liza as part of Betsy's set [092-004].

## Themes & Significance

Liza Merkalova functions as a case study in the moral economy of Petersburg high society. Her extramarital relationship with Stremov is well-known and tacitly accepted; her husband's role has been reduced to that of a compliant attendant. The narrator treats this arrangement not with outrage but with clinical interest — it is simply how things are done in this world.

The contrast with Anna's situation is carefully drawn. Liza Merkalova is not ostracized; she is celebrated. The society that calls her and Stremov "the cream of the Petersburg world" [094-017] will later humiliate Anna at the theater. The difference, Tolstoy implies, is not moral but social: Liza plays by the unwritten rules of discretion, while Anna's open departure from her husband breaches the code of appearances that Petersburg society values above all else.

Liza's own confession that "it was all dreary" [095-022] introduces a note of melancholy: even the woman who seems to have perfected the art of living freely within society's double standards finds the performance exhausting.

## Key Passages

> "Stremov and Liza Merkalova, why, they're the cream of the Petersburg world," said Betsy. [094-017]

Betsy's casual equation of social prestige with what is effectively an adulterous relationship encapsulates the novel's critique of high society's moral logic.

> "Liza Merkalova's husband carries her shawl, and is always ready to be of use," said Betsy. [094-033]

A single sentence that reduces a man to an accessory and a marriage to a performance — Tolstoy's compression at its sharpest.

> "Ah, how dreary it all was!" said Liza Merkalova. [095-022]

The honest admission beneath the glitter — a society woman's confession that her world of pleasure delivers no lasting satisfaction.
