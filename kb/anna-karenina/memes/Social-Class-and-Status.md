# Social Class and Status in Anna Karenina

Social class in Anna Karenina is both an inescapable structure and a contested terrain. Tolstoy depicts a Russian aristocracy in transition: the old nobility of land and lineage confronts new wealth, bureaucratic advancement, and the disruptive forces of modernization. Characters navigate the rigid hierarchies of Petersburg drawing rooms, Moscow society, provincial assemblies, and peasant villages, each space operating with its own class logic. The novel's treatment of class is fundamentally ambivalent — Tolstoy anatomizes the aristocracy's corruption and emptiness while recognizing that its codes provide the only social order his characters know.

## The Petersburg Elite

Petersburg society represents the apex of the class hierarchy: a closed world of drawing rooms, diplomatic receptions, and court connections. Vronsky, scanning a woman at a glance, immediately "classified her as belonging to the best society" [^PN-5YK8xO]. This instantaneous social sorting is the fundamental operation of the Petersburg world. Princess Betsy Tverskaya's salon is the epicenter of this elite, where the lines between legitimate and illegitimate desire are policed through gossip and selective invitation. Anna's tragedy begins precisely when she is excluded from this world — first subtly, then explicitly, in the devastating theater scene where she is publicly snubbed.

When Anna is humiliated at the opera, it is a class ritual as much as a moral judgment. The society that had tolerated and even encouraged her affair when it was discreet punishes her when she demands that it be acknowledged openly. The punishment is administered not through law but through the withdrawal of recognition — the averted gaze, the unreturned bow, the empty seat at the theater. These micro-gestures of exclusion are the Petersburg elite's primary instruments of social control.

## Moscow's Older Nobility

Moscow society, represented by the Shcherbatskys and their circle, operates on a different rhythm. It is older, more traditional, and more provincial than Petersburg — but also, in Tolstoy's rendering, more human. The Shcherbatsky household's concerns with marriage, family, and social standing reflect a class that traces its identity through lineage rather than through proximity to the court. "The families of the Levins and the Shtcherbatskys were old, noble Moscow families" [^PN-014-002], and this "old nobility" carries genuine prestige, though of a kind increasingly eclipsed by bureaucratic Petersburg.

Moscow society's rhythms are familial rather than political: births, marriages, and deaths structure social life more than promotions and court appointments. The Shcherbatskys' world is conservative in the literal sense — it conserves traditions, relationships, and properties across generations. Its limitations are obvious (Kitty's marriage-market anxieties are its direct product), but so is its stability. When the novel's Petersburg characters — Anna, Vronsky, Karenin — are destroyed, it is notable that they are destroyed in Petersburg, not Moscow.

## The Provincial Nobility

In Part 6, Tolstoy depicts provincial noble assemblies with anthropological precision. The election of the marshal of the nobility in Kashinsky province is a set piece of political theater: "men in uniforms of the most diverse types — military, naval, civil, court — some in old-fashioned uniforms, others in the latest fashion" [^PN-1WaVfE] assemble to perform the rituals of self-governance. The provincial nobility occupy an intermediate space: they possess land and titles but lack the cosmopolitan polish of the capitals. Levin's discomfort at the assembly — his inability to navigate its unwritten rules — reflects his broader alienation from aristocratic institutions.

The election scene is also a study in how political power operates within a class system. Votes are traded, alliances shift, and the outcome is determined less by principle than by personal loyalty and strategic maneuvering. Levin, who approaches the election expecting rational deliberation, is bewildered by its theatricality. The scene reveals class as performance: the uniforms, the titles, the elaborate courtesies are all part of a ritual that sustains aristocratic power by making it seem natural and inevitable.

## The Merchant Class: A Threat from Below

Ryabinin the merchant represents a new and unsettling presence in the novel's class landscape. When he purchases Darya Alexandrovna's forest from Stiva Oblonsky, the transaction is more than personal betrayal — it is class warfare conducted through commerce. Ryabinin's shrewd bargaining, his knowledge of timber prices, and his quiet satisfaction at outwitting a nobleman all signal the rise of a class that understands value in ways the aristocracy cannot.

Levin's reaction to Ryabinin is visceral disgust mixed with grudging respect. The merchant represents everything Levin despises about modern economic relations, yet he cannot deny that Ryabinin is more competent than the landowners he dispossesses. The sale of the forest for a fraction of its worth is emblematic: the old nobility, incapable of managing its own economic interests, is being quietly bought out by a class it considers beneath contempt.

## Levin's Class Ambivalence

Levin embodies the novel's deepest engagement with class contradiction. He is a wealthy landowner who prefers the company of peasants; an aristocrat who despises the aristocracy of birth; a man who argues that the nobility's privileges are unjustified while living comfortably on his estate. He works alongside his peasants in the fields, finding in physical labor a moral clarity that drawing-room conversation cannot provide. Yet Tolstoy never resolves this contradiction: Levin's utopian vision of class harmony through shared agricultural labor remains aspirational rather than achieved.

The tension in Levin's position is productive rather than disabling. His inability to fully inhabit any single class identity makes him the novel's most searching moral consciousness. Where Vronsky takes his status for granted and Karenin treats his as a bureaucratic entitlement, Levin earns his belonging through daily work — and in doing so, he exposes the emptiness of inherited status even as he recognizes that he cannot simply renounce what he was born into.

## Bureaucratic vs. Landed Status

The tension between bureaucratic Petersburg and the landed gentry runs throughout the novel. Karenin's status derives entirely from his position in the state apparatus, while Levin's comes from the soil. Stiva Oblonsky, who has sold off most of his wife's forest for cash and lives beyond his means, represents the decay of the landed class: a man who retains all the expectations of his station without the economic foundation to sustain them. His sale of Darya Alexandrovna's forest to the merchant Ryabinin is both a personal betrayal and a symbol of the old nobility's gradual dispossession.

This tension between bureaucratic and landed status reflects a real historical transformation in post-reform Russia. The emancipation of the serfs in 1861 had disrupted the economic basis of the landed gentry, and Tolstoy's novel, set in the 1870s, captures a class in the midst of an incomplete and painful adjustment. The novel's ultimate sympathies lie with the land — but it is honest enough to show that the landed life depends on economic arrangements that are themselves unjust.
