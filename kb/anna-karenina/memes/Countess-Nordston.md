## Identity & Role

Countess Nordston is a young society lady of St. Petersburg, a friend of Kitty Shcherbatskaya who married the preceding winter. She appears at the Shcherbatskys' gatherings and the great ball, serving as a foil to Konstantin Levin — the two maintain a relationship of "joking hostility," outwardly courteous yet mutually contemptuous. She is a spirited woman who enjoys verbal sparring and is a believer in spiritualism, particularly table-turning.

> "Five minutes later there came in a friend of Kitty's, married the preceding winter, Countess Nordston." [022-002]

She first appears in Part One, Chapter 22, when Levin visits the Shcherbatskys' house, and reappears at the Moscow ball (Part One, Chapter 31), at Kitty's wedding (Part Five, Chapters 136–137), and is mentioned later in connection with Anna Karenina.

## Biography & Arc

### First Appearance at the Shcherbatskys'

Countess Nordston enters during Levin's visit to the Shcherbatsky household, immediately establishing her dynamic with him:

> "The Countess Nordston and Levin got into that relation with one another not seldom seen in society, when two persons, who remain externally on friendly terms, despise each other to such a degree that they cannot even take each other seriously, and cannot even be offended by each other." [022-006]

She "pounced upon Levin at once" [022-007] and persistently attempted to draw him into arguments, though Levin refused to be provoked:

> "Noticing that Countess Nordston wanted to say something, he started a conversation with the old princess, not waiting for what Countess Nordston had to say." [022-034]

### At the Ball

At the great Moscow ball, Countess Nordston expressed concern for Kitty's feelings when Vronsky chose not to dance with her:

> "'Why is it you're not going to dance it with Princess Shcherbatskaya?'" [031-009]

She knew Kitty well enough to understand what Vronsky's apparent slight meant, and attempted to protect Kitty by diverting attention. She found Korsunsky to dance with herself instead.

### At Kitty's Wedding

Countess Nordston attended Kitty's wedding to Levin in Part Five. Her bantering tone with Levin continued even at this solemn occasion:

> "'You're a nice person! Well, has he arrived, the much-talked-of?' said Countess Nordston, coming up. 'You're a nice person!'" [136-013]

She also commented on Kitty's unchanged beauty:

> "'She hasn't lost her looks so,' Countess Nordston said to Madame Lvova. 'Still he's not worth her little finger, is he?'" [137-015]

### Later Mention

In Part Six, Countess Nordston is mentioned as one who "ventured to hint that she [Kitty] had done the same thing" as Anna — comparing Kitty's situation to Anna Karenina's, demonstrating her role as a sensitive observer of social dynamics [125-029].

## Relationships

### With Konstantin Levin

The defining relationship for Countess Nordston is her contentious dynamic with Levin. They maintain a façade of cordiality while engaging in intellectual combat:

> "They must certainly make a match here," Countess Nordston thought, referring to Levin's interest in Kitty. [022-009]

She provokes him on topics ranging from country vs. city life to spiritualism:

> "In the country you're not in a hurry always," said Countess Nordston. [022-031]

Levin, for his part, deflects her challenges with deliberate awkwardness, frustrating her attempts to display his "queerness" at Kitty's gatherings.

### With Kitty Shcherbatskaya

As Kitty's married friend, Countess Nordston appears in scenes significant to Kitty's romantic life — the ball where Vronsky's preference became clear, and Kitty's wedding to Levin. She seems genuinely concerned for Kitty's welfare, though her mode of expression is sardonic.

### With Madame Lvova

At Kitty's wedding, Countess Nordston converses with Madame Lvova (Kitty's sister Natalia) about the marriage, demonstrating her place within the Shcherbatsky social circle.

## Themes & Significance

Countess Nordston embodies the witty, sharp-tongued society woman who functions as both chorus and catalyst in the novel's social scenes. Her "joking hostility" with Levin highlights two contrasting temperaments — the earnest, intellectual country gentleman versus the clever, superficial urban society lady.

Her belief in spiritualism (table-turning) reflects the fashionable pseudo-intellectual pursuits of Russian high society in the 1870s:

> "Countess Nordston, who believed in spiritualism, began telling about the marvels she had seen." [022-037]

She serves Tolstoy's larger purpose of satirizing the empty rituals and conversation of the aristocracy, while also functioning as a plot device to externalize Kitty's emotional state and Levin's social awkwardness.
