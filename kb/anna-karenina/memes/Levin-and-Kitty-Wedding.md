## Overview

The wedding of [[Konstantin-Levin]] and Kitty Shcherbatskaya takes place in Moscow, following the customs of the Russian Orthodox Church. It occurs in Part 5 of the novel, serving as the structural counterweight to the dissolution of Anna's marriage. Where Anna's trajectory is toward isolation and despair, Levin's wedding represents entry into community, tradition, and shared meaning.

> On the day of the wedding, according to the Russian custom, Levin did not see his bride. (134-001)

The wedding is described with Tolstoy's characteristic accumulation of concrete detail — the lighting of candles, the chanting of the priest, the exchange of rings, the awkward moment when Levin cannot remember which hand to use. These details ground the spiritual significance of the ceremony in physical, sometimes comical, reality.

> A crowd of people, principally women, was thronging the church lighted up for the wedding. (135-001)

## Participants

### Konstantin Levin

Levin approaches his wedding day in a state of anxious exaltation. He is, as always, overthinking — worried about his unworthiness, his past, his confession of youthful sins to Kitty. His friends and brother make jokes at his expense, but beneath the comedy is genuine spiritual seriousness. Levin sees marriage as a transformation, not merely a social contract.

> Levin was in a continual state of embarrassment and discomfort during the whole time before his wedding. (125-023)

### Kitty Shcherbatskaya

Kitty, younger and more socially assured than Levin, moves through the ceremony with the composure of someone who has been preparing for this moment her whole life. Yet Tolstoy notes the subtle change the wedding day works on her: "She was not nearly so pretty on her wedding day as usual; but Levin did not think so" (136-003). This observation captures the difference between social display and spiritual significance — what matters is not how the wedding looks but what it means.

### The Priest and Congregation

The Orthodox priest performs the ceremony with ritual formality, and the gathered crowd — family, friends, servants — creates the communal witness that distinguishes this wedding from mere legal transaction. The presence of the community sanctifies the union; Levin and Kitty are being married not just to each other but into a social fabric.

## Narrative Sequence

### Lead-Up

The days before the wedding are filled with practical preparations and spiritual anxiety. Levin undergoes confession — a requirement of the Orthodox marriage rite — and is tormented by the requirement to confess his sins honestly to a priest he barely knows. His friend [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]] advises him on practicalities, including the suggestion to leave Moscow for the country immediately after the wedding.

### The Ceremony

The wedding itself takes place in the evening — an unusual choice that draws comment from the guests: "I wonder why they had the wedding in the evening? It's like shop-people" (137-004). Tolstoy uses this slight social irregularity to distinguish Levin's wedding from fashionable society events; it is, from the start, a ceremony oriented toward meaning rather than display.

The ritual proceeds through familiar Orthodox elements: the lighting of candles, the chanting of psalms, the joining of hands, the crowning of the couple — but Tolstoy renders each moment through Levin's heightened perception, so that even the most conventional gestures feel newly significant. The high point comes with the exchange of vows, where Levin's awkward earnestness contrasts with the ritual's ancient formality.

### Aftermath

Immediately after the ceremony, Levin and Kitty depart for his country estate — Pokrovskoe — where they will begin their married life. The transition from the sacred space of the church to the practical world of the estate is swift, and the novel follows them into the intimate challenges of early married life with the same attention to detail it brought to the ceremony.

## Consequences & Significance

### Structural Counterweight to Anna's Tragedy

Levin and Kitty's wedding is the novel's primary image of marriage as fulfillment rather than constraint. The ceremony's joy — earned through the earlier pain of Kitty's rejection of Levin and his subsequent despair — establishes the possibility of happiness that the novel needs to balance Anna's suffering. The wedding is not sentimental (Tolstoy's meticulous attention to awkward details prevents that), but it is deeply affirming.

### Tradition as Meaning

The Orthodox ceremony represents tradition not as empty form but as a vessel for genuine feeling. Levin, the habitual skeptic, finds himself moved by rituals he might intellectually question. The wedding suggests that meaning can be accessed through inherited forms, even by those who cannot fully believe in their theological foundations — a theme that anticipates Levin's later religious conversion.

### Community and Witness

The wedding draws the novel's social world together. Levin's family, Kitty's family, the servants, the priest, the gathered crowd — all participate in making the marriage real. This communal dimension is precisely what Anna and Vronsky lack; their union, conducted in secrecy and scandal, has no witnesses except hostile society.

## Key Passages

The opening of the wedding day, with its separation of bride and groom:

> On the day of the wedding, according to the Russian custom, Levin did not see his bride. (134-001)

The expectant crowd at the church:

> A crowd of people, principally women, was thronging the church lighted up for the wedding. Those who had not succeeded in gaining admittance were crowding round the windows. (135-001)

Levin's pre-wedding anxiety:

> Levin was in a continual state of embarrassment and discomfort during the whole time before his wedding. (125-023)

Kitty transfigured by the ceremony's meaning:

> She was not nearly so pretty on her wedding day as usual; but Levin did not think so. (136-003)

The social commentary on the evening ceremony:

> "I wonder why they had the wedding in the evening? It's like shop-people," said Madame Korsunskaya. (137-004)
