# The Cavalry Regiment

Count Vronsky's cavalry regiment is the social-military institution that forms his world. It provides his identity, his code of conduct, his friendships, and his ambitions — and it is the world he cannot fully leave, even for Anna. The regiment represents a particular kind of aristocratic masculinity: honorable within its own narrow terms, but fundamentally inadequate to the moral demands of genuine love.

## Role in the Narrative

Vronsky is introduced as an aide-de-camp and a brilliant young officer. His regiment is his social universe — it provides the structure of his days, the hierarchy of his ambitions, and the unwritten code by which he judges himself and others.

When Vronsky's affair with Anna becomes serious, the regiment represents the world he should leave but cannot. His fellow officers understand affairs — they are part of the code — but they do not understand sacrifice. Vronsky's inability to fully abandon his military identity for Anna is one of the central tensions of their relationship: she has sacrificed everything, and she demands that he sacrifice something in return.

## The Regimental Code

The regiment's code is a microcosm of aristocratic society's values: honor among men, discretion, physical courage, and the unwritten rules that govern everything from dueling to romance. This code gives Vronsky's life its shape and meaning — but Tolstoy shows that it is, finally, hollow. When Vronsky faces real moral crisis — Anna's escalating despair, her suicide — the regimental code has nothing to offer him.

## After Anna

Following Anna's death, Vronsky volunteers for military service in the Serbian war. The regiment, which defined his life before Anna, now offers only the prospect of a meaningful death — the final expression of a code that can honor sacrifice in battle but has no language for the moral demands of love.

## Themes

The regiment embodies the novel's critique of institutions. Like the government ministry that defines Karenin, the regiment defines Vronsky — and in defining him, it limits him. Both institutions are systems of rules and hierarchies that work perfectly within their own terms but fail catastrophically when confronted with the messy, passionate, ungovernable reality of human love.
