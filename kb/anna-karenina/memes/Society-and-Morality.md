# Society and Morality in Anna Karenina

*Anna Karenina* is in large part an anatomy of Russian aristocratic society in the 1870s — its codes, its hypocrisies, and the human cost of living within or outside its judgments. Tolstoy contrasts the morality of social convention with genuine ethical living, finding the former wanting at nearly every turn.

## The Code of Society

The aristocratic world of St. Petersburg and Moscow operates by an unwritten code: appearances matter more than truth, discretion more than honesty, and conformity more than conscience. Stiva Oblonsky is the perfect product of this world — charming, likable, and entirely without moral seriousness. He is unfaithful to his wife, irresponsible with money, and careless of others, yet he remains universally liked because he follows the one rule that matters: he is agreeable.

> Each of them — as is often the way with men who have selected careers of different kinds — though in discussion he would even justify the other's career, in his heart despised it. It seemed to each of them that the life he led himself was the only real life [013-026].

## The Double Standard

The novel's most devastating critique is of the sexual double standard. When Stiva has an affair, it is a minor domestic inconvenience — something his sister Anna can help smooth over. When Anna has an affair, she is destroyed.

This is not presented as the work of villains. Society's condemnation of Anna operates through a thousand small cuts — invitations not received, acquaintances who fail to recognize her, the quiet closing of doors. The people who shut Anna out are not evil; they are simply following the code they have always followed, and the code says that a woman who openly leaves her husband for a lover is beyond the pale.

## Karenin: The Bureaucrat as Moral Arbiter

Alexei Alexandrovitch Karenin embodies a particular kind of social morality — the morality of rules, procedures, and proper forms. He approaches Anna's affair as he would a bureaucratic problem: what is the correct procedure? What will people think? What does the law require?

Karenin is not a hypocrite in the ordinary sense — he genuinely believes in the rules he enforces. But Tolstoy shows that this rule-following morality, detached from compassion or genuine human feeling, is itself a form of moral failure. When Karenin forgives Vronsky at Anna's sickbed, it is the one moment where he acts from feeling rather than rule — and it is the only moment we feel genuine sympathy for him.

## The Country vs. The City

Tolstoy draws a sharp contrast between the morality of urban society and the morality of rural life. Levin's country estate is portrayed as a place where authentic moral life is possible — where work, land, and community provide a foundation that society's codes cannot. Moscow and St. Petersburg, by contrast, are places where morality dissolves into performance.

This is not simple pastoral nostalgia. Tolstoy shows the real tensions of rural life — Levin's struggles with his peasants, the economic pressures of farming, the intellectual isolation. But he argues that moral clarity is possible in the country in a way that it is not in the city, where social convention has replaced authentic ethical judgment.

## Authentic Morality

The novel's positive moral vision is embodied in Levin's trajectory. For Levin, morality is not a matter of social codes (which he despises) or intellectual systems (which he finds unconvincing) but of lived practice — honesty in relationships, responsibility in work, openness to spiritual truth. His revelation at the novel's end — that the good life is not justified by reason but lived by instinct and faith — is Tolstoy's answer to the moral emptiness of the society he has depicted.
