# Jealousy in Anna Karenina

Jealousy functions in *Anna Karenina* as both a destructive emotional force and a marker of the characters' moral and psychological conditions. Tolstoy traces jealousy through multiple characters — Karenin, Anna, Vronsky, Kitty, and even Levin — using it to reveal their understanding of love, honor, and self-worth.

## Karenin's Denial

Alexey Alexandrovitch Karenin presents the novel's most complex relationship with jealousy. He insists that he is not a jealous man: "Jealousy according to his notions was an insult to one's wife, and one ought to have confidence in one's wife" [^PN-050-004]. For Karenin, jealousy is a theoretical problem rather than an emotional one — "jealousy means lowering both myself and her" [^PN-050-005]. He "considers jealousy a humiliating and degrading feeling" [^PN-051-017] and refuses to indulge it, even as evidence of Anna's affair with Vronsky mounts.

This denial of jealousy is not strength but a form of emotional cowardice. Karenin intellectualizes what he cannot feel, treating his wife's infidelity as a "problem to be solved" rather than a betrayal to be grieved. The jealousy he refuses to acknowledge nevertheless "tortured him during the period of uncertainty" [^PN-090-015], surfacing in indirect ways — bureaucratic obstruction, withholding of their son, and a grim satisfaction in Anna's social ruin.

The irony is compounded by the fact that Karenin's refusal of jealousy is itself a form of moral vanity. He prides himself on being above a feeling that he considers beneath his dignity, but this pride makes him incapable of the honest emotional reckoning that might have saved his marriage. The jealousy he suppresses does not disappear; it transforms into a colder, more durable resentment — the bureaucratic cruelty of a man who has convinced himself that he is acting on principle.

## Vronsky's Possessive Jealousy

Unlike Karenin, Vronsky experiences jealousy directly and unapologetically. His "habitual feeling of jealousy" extends to "everything that drew his attention away from her" [^PN-076-003]. Vronsky's jealousy is possessive rather than moral — he resents any claim on Anna's time or attention, whether from her son Seryozha, her social acquaintances, or her independent thoughts.

As the affair progresses, "these fits of jealousy, which of late had been more and more frequent" [^PN-112-017] come to dominate their relationship. Anna and Vronsky develop a private language for it, giving "the name" to her jealousy — "the fiend" [^PN-112-018] — but naming it does not contain it. The jealousy feeds on itself, growing more intense as their social isolation deepens and their options narrow.

## Anna's Torment

Anna's jealousy is the most visceral in the novel. Having sacrificed everything for Vronsky — her social position, her son, her reputation — she becomes terrified of losing him too. She is "dreading the agonies of jealousy she had been through already" [^PN-044-004], and the dread itself feeds the jealousy in a self-reinforcing cycle.

The irony of Anna's position is that she recognizes the destructive nature of jealousy even as she succumbs to it. She knows that jealousy "means lowering both myself and him" [^PN-040-019], echoing Karenin's formulation but with genuine emotional stakes rather than abstract principle. Her jealousy becomes indistinguishable from despair — the despair of a woman who has bet everything on one man's love and sees no way back. When she tells Vronsky, "I'm not jealous: I believe you when you're here; but when you're away somewhere leading your life, so incomprehensible to me..." [^PN-112-019], she captures the particular torture of a love that has become her entire world while his remains larger.

In their final confrontations, Anna's jealousy escalates into a "stream of reproach, of senseless jealousy, of all that had been torturing her" [^PN-146-009]. The adjective "senseless" is Tolstoy's own — and it is devastating. Anna's jealousy is not senseless in the sense of being unfounded; Vronsky's independence is real, and her fear of abandonment is rational. It is senseless in the deeper sense that it accomplishes nothing except to drive Vronsky further away. Her jealousy, intended to hold him, becomes the very force that ensures his departure.

## Kitty's Jealousy: The Ball Scene

The novel's first extended treatment of jealousy centers on Kitty Shcherbatskaya at the Moscow ball. Kitty, expecting Vronsky to propose, watches him transfixed by Anna Karenina, and her world collapses in a single evening. Anna herself acknowledges her role: "Do you know why Kitty didn't come to dinner? She's jealous of me. I have spoiled ... I've been the cause of that ball being a torture to her instead of a pleasure" [^PN-040-020].

Kitty's jealousy is the jealousy of innocence betrayed — the raw discovery that the romantic narrative she had been raised to expect does not correspond to reality. Her subsequent illness and depression are as much a reaction to this disillusionment as to the loss of Vronsky himself. Unlike Anna's jealousy, which is entangled with guilt and despair, Kitty's jealousy burns clean: it wounds, but it also educates. Through it, she learns to see men — and herself — more clearly.

## Social Jealousy and Male Rivalry

Jealousy in the novel extends beyond romantic relationships into the domain of male professional and social rivalry. Mihailov the painter's hostility toward Vronsky and Golenishtchev is immediately recognized as jealousy: "Mihailov was simply jealous of Vronsky" [^PN-112-020]. This form of jealousy — the resentment of a working artist toward wealthy amateurs who can afford to play at art — connects the emotional dynamics of the drawing room to the economic structures of Russian society.

Levin, too, experiences a form of social jealousy in his relationship with the agricultural establishment. His innovations are met with the quiet contempt of traditional landowners, and his sense of being an outsider — of not quite belonging to the class he was born into — is a form of jealousy turned inward, a resentment of those who fit comfortably into a social world that excludes him. Before his marriage, Levin had viewed romantic rivalry with contempt: "petty cares, the squabbles, the jealousy — he had only smiled contemptuously" [^PN-146-002], certain that such emotions were beneath him. His subsequent experience teaches him otherwise.

## Levin's Contrast

Levin's jealousy, directed at Kitty's past relationship with Vronsky, is treated with greater sympathy by the narrative. His "irrational jealousy over his daughters, especially Kitty" [^PN-020-006] is portrayed as the natural protectiveness of a loving father. When Levin's jealousy of Vronsky resurfaces after marriage, it is resolved through honest conversation and mutual trust — the very resolution unavailable to Anna and Vronsky.

Yet Levin is not immune to the darker forms of jealousy. His reaction to Veslovsky's flirtatious attention to Kitty reveals a possessiveness that shocks even himself: "I'm not jealous, but I'm wounded, humiliated that anybody dare think, that anybody dare look at you with eyes like that" [^PN-112-021]. The distinction Levin draws — between jealousy and wounded pride — is subtle but important. His honesty about his own feelings, and Kitty's gentle response, model a way through jealousy that the novel's tragic lovers cannot find.

## Jealousy and the Moral Education of the Emotions

Tolstoy's treatment of jealousy is not merely psychological but moral. Each character's relationship to jealousy reveals their capacity for self-knowledge. Karenin, who denies jealousy, lacks self-knowledge entirely. Vronsky, who accepts it as a natural passion, lacks the will to master it. Anna, who is consumed by it, possesses self-knowledge without the power to act on it. Only Kitty and Levin, who acknowledge jealousy without being ruled by it, achieve the emotional equilibrium that Tolstoy presents as the foundation of a good life.

The novel suggests that jealousy is not in itself immoral — it is a natural response to a genuine threat. What matters is what one does with it. Dolly's advice to her younger sister — to "refuse things, not to show jealousy, not to be quarrelsome or taking offense" [^PN-013-002] — is a prescription for domestic survival that acknowledges the feeling while counseling against its expression. The wisdom is modest but real: emotions cannot be legislated out of existence, but they can be managed with honesty, restraint, and a commitment to something larger than the self.
