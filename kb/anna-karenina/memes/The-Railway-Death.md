# The Railway Worker's Death

The death of a railway worker at the Moscow station, occurring as Anna Karenina first arrives in the novel, is one of literature's most famous instances of foreshadowing. The incident is brief — a few paragraphs — but its structural significance is enormous: it prefigures Anna's own death under a train, and establishes the railway as the novel's central symbol of fate, modernity, and destruction.

## The Event

As Vronsky meets his mother's train from Petersburg at the Moscow station, a railway worker — drunk or careless — is run over by the train. The station-master reports the death to Vronsky, who silently gives money for the widow [026-051].

Anna's reaction is immediate and visceral: "Couldn't one do anything for her?" she whispers [026-047]. Later, in the carriage with her brother, she is visibly shaken — "her lips were quivering, and she was with difficulty restraining her tears" [026-060].

## Narrative Significance

The railway death operates on multiple levels:

- **Structural foreshadowing**: Anna will die in the same way, at a railway station — the novel's ending returns to its beginning
- **Character revelation**: The different reactions reveal character — Vronsky gives money and moves on ("Now let us be off" [026-051]); Anna is deeply troubled
- **Atmospheric omen**: The death casts a shadow over the "romantic" meeting of Anna and Vronsky, suggesting from the start that their story will end in destruction
- **Symbolic warning**: Anna herself calls it "an evil omen" — a warning she cannot heed

## The Railway as Symbol

The railway recurs throughout the novel as an image of modern, mechanical force — unstoppable, indifferent to human feeling, crushing what stands in its path. It represents the collision between Anna's passionate, living self and the impersonal machinery of society, fate, and modernity.
