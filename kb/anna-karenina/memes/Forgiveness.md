# Forgiveness in Anna Karenina

Forgiveness is the moral and spiritual center of *Anna Karenina*, surfacing at every critical juncture of the plot. Tolstoy tests each major character against the demand to forgive, and in doing so exposes the distance between Christian ideal and human frailty. The novel's epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — sets forgiveness as a divine prerogative that humans can aspire to but never fully claim.

## Oblonsky's Unearned Forgiveness

The novel opens with a plea for forgiveness. Stepan Arkadyevitch Oblonsky, discovered in an affair with the French governess, knows he has wronged his wife: "She won't forgive me, and she can't forgive me" [^PN-009-007]. His approach to obtaining Dolly's pardon is characteristically shallow — he asks the family servant Matvey for advice, and is told simply: "Beg her forgiveness, sir. There's no help for it!" [^PN-010-031]. Oblonsky's "idiotic smile" — which "he could not forgive himself" [^PN-009-013] — betrays his essential unseriousness: even in the moment of contrition, he cannot stop performing charm.

When Oblonsky does confront Dolly, he offers the most minimal of apologies — "forgive me" [^PN-012-017] — yet believes "she hates me. She will not forgive me" [^PN-012-024] even as she visibly wavers. Oblonsky's understanding of forgiveness is transactional: he wants absolution so that domestic life can resume. He does not truly repent, and Dolly's forgiveness, once granted, changes nothing fundamental in their marriage. His plea — "what can I say?... One thing: forgive.... Remember, cannot nine years..." [^PN-012-012] — appeals to shared history rather than genuine remorse, treating forgiveness as a debt that can be called in by invoking the past.

## Dolly's Christian Calculus

Dolly's struggle to forgive her husband is the novel's first sustained examination of the problem. She receives counsel from Anna, who frames the question in religious terms: "If one forgives, it must be completely, entirely" [^PN-027-054]. Yet Dolly's own feelings resist this ideal. "How am I to forgive him, how am I to be his wife after he has..." [^PN-027-034] — the sentence trails off, the injury too deep to articulate. Her question to Anna — "Yes, but could you forgive it?" [^PN-027-052] — is the novel's own deepest challenge to its Christian framework.

Anna's reply reflects her characteristic blend of moral seriousness and emotional generosity: "If there is enough for you to be able to forgive him. If there is, forgive him" [^PN-027-045]. The formulation is careful — Anna does not command forgiveness but makes it conditional on Dolly's own emotional capacity. The advice is wise but insufficient: it places the entire burden of reconciliation on the injured party, asking nothing of the one who caused the injury.

Tolstoy refuses to resolve this tension neatly. Dolly forgives Oblonsky in the sense that she remains in the marriage, but her forgiveness is never wholehearted. It is a forgiveness of exhaustion rather than grace — a practical accommodation to the constraints of her position as a mother of six with no independent means. The novel does not celebrate this outcome; it records it with sorrow.

## Karenin's Transformation

The novel's most dramatic act of forgiveness occurs at Anna's sickbed after the birth of her daughter by Vronsky. Believing Anna to be dying, Karenin experiences a spiritual awakening. At her bedside, "there was entreaty for forgiveness, and trust in him, and tender" feeling [^PN-118-040]. He forgives both Anna and Vronsky, in a moment of genuine Christian charity that transcends his earlier bureaucratic coldness. The transformation is radical: the man who had reduced his wife's adultery to a legal problem becomes, for a brief moment, something like a saint.

But Tolstoy is too honest a moralist to let this moment stand as resolution. Karenin's forgiveness, however sincere in the moment, proves unsustainable. Society's mockery and Anna's eventual recovery — and departure with Vronsky — erode the spiritual high ground. The forgiveness that had filled him with joy leaves "nothing but vexation" [^PN-126-001] in its wake, a painful reminder that human forgiveness is fragile and contingent. When Karenin later reads Nikolai Levin's deathbed letter — "I shall die easier with your forgiveness" [^PN-126-005] — he reacts with contemptuous dismissal rather than the compassion his own experience should have taught him.

Yet the memory of his transformative moment never entirely leaves him. "A glad feeling of love and forgiveness for his enemies filled his heart" [^PN-126-039] in the aftermath, and he recognizes that "I needed nothing but forgiveness, and I want nothing more" [^PN-126-042]. He even speaks of "the happiness of forgiveness" that "has revealed to me my duty" [^PN-126-055], and Tolstoy notes that "most of all, the joy of forgiveness, made him at once conscious" [^PN-128-001] of a spiritual dimension he had previously denied. The transformation is real; its impermanence does not make it false.

## The Performance of Forgiveness

Forgiveness in the novel is not only a private moral act but a public performance. When Oblonsky seeks Dolly's pardon, he is aware of the servants watching, the children listening, the social script that requires a certain choreography of contrition and absolution. The princess's instructions to Kitty — "to humiliate herself and beg forgiveness" [^PN-053-005] — treat forgiveness as a ritual of social repair rather than a transaction of the heart.

This performative dimension is clearest in the minor key of Oblonsky's business dealings. His letter to a country neighbor, "begging her forgiveness for not having thought of everything" [^PN-085-001], uses the language of moral reckoning for a trivial lapse in politeness. The debasement of forgiveness-language in social commerce mirrors the novel's larger concern: that the words of Christian morality have become so detached from their spiritual roots that they can be deployed without cost or consequence. When everyone begs forgiveness for everything, forgiveness itself becomes a hollow form.

## The Limits of Forgiveness

Tolstoy does not suggest that everything can or should be forgiven. Anna, in her final moments, does not seek forgiveness — she seeks oblivion. The novel's epigraph reserves vengeance for God precisely because human forgiveness is inadequate to certain injuries. Karenin's brief transcendence at Anna's sickbed is remarkable not because it succeeds but because it was attempted at all.

The novel's most radical suggestion is that the question of forgiveness may be less important than the question of judgment. The society that condemns Anna has no interest in forgiving her; it wants to punish her. But the epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — removes the right of punishment from human hands. In doing so, it does not guarantee forgiveness; it guarantees only that the final accounting belongs to God. Human beings are left with the more modest task of living with one another's failures, bearing the injuries that cannot be repaired, and extending what grace they can manage without claiming for it a permanence it does not possess.

## Levin's Spiritual Journey

Levin's relationship with forgiveness is less dramatic but more enduring. His initial inability to forgive Kitty for preferring Vronsky gives way to a mature love that does not require the language of pardon. When Levin's brother asks Kitty for forgiveness on his deathbed — "That's the only way she can forgive me" [^PN-018-087] — the scene connects the domestic drama of forgiveness to its ultimate horizon: death, judgment, and the hope of reconciliation beyond the grave.

Later, in his spiritual crisis at the novel's end, Levin arrives at a form of Christian faith that centers on the capacity to forgive — not as an occasional grand gesture but as a daily discipline of living rightly with others. His resolution is modest: he does not claim to have forgiven everyone or everything. He claims only to have found a way of living in which the impulse to forgive is stronger than the impulse to condemn. For Tolstoy, this is enough.
