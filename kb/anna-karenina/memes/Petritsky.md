## Identity & Role

Petritsky is a young lieutenant serving in the same cavalry regiment as Vronsky, and Vronsky's "friend and favorite comrade" [042-001]. He lives with Vronsky in the officers' quarters at camp (Krasnoe Selo) and is described as "not particularly well-born" [042-002]. Petritsky is a classic Tolstoyan comic officer — perpetually drunk, entangled with women, yet somehow endearing in his helplessness and good nature. His baroness calls him "Pierre" [042-009].

> "Petritsky was a young lieutenant, not particularly well-born, and not merely not wealthy, but up to his neck in debt. By evening he was always drunk, and he had more than once been locked up for all sorts of disgraceful larks, but he was a great favorite both with his comrades and his superior officers." [042-002]

## Biography & Arc

### The Baroness Scandal

Petritsky's most memorable story involves the Baroness Shilton, who has run away from her husband and taken refuge in the officers' quarters. When Vronsky arrives home after the ball, he finds Petritsky entertaining the baroness and her friend:

> "'Bravo! Vronsky!' shouted Petritsky, jumping up, scraping his chair." [042-003]

The baroness addresses him as Pierre and orders coffee with casual familiarity. Petritsky explains to Vronsky that he has "borrowed" her from a fellow officer, Kaluzhsky, and recounts the melodrama with relish:

> "'Kaluzhsky — simply lovely!' cried Petritsky. 'You know his weakness for balls; he never misses a single court ball.'" [042-025]

Petritsky then mimes the scene of discovery with theatrical flair:

> "Petritsky mimicked how he was standing there with his hat off, and the aide-de-camp, and the general." [042-027]

### Karenin's Visit

When Karenin comes to the officers' quarters in search of Vronsky (to confront him about Anna), Petritsky is entangled in the mess:

> "Petritsky, whom he liked, was implicated in it." [047-020]

The visit forces the officers to clean up their act — Petritsky nervously attempts damage control as Karenin, a man from an entirely different world, descends on their bohemian quarters:

> "'Petritsky's becoming impossible. Not a week without some scandal.'" [047-022]

Petritsky and his friend Kedrov are pushed forward to deal with the situation:

> "He maneuvered a retreat, shoving Petritsky out before him." [047-025]

### Camp Life

Petritsky shares Vronsky's hut at camp, where the two live in companionable disorder:

> "The hut, divided into two by a partition. Petritsky lived with him in camp too. Petritsky was asleep when Vronsky and Yashvin came into the hut." [062-001]

In the morning scenes after the steeplechase, Petritsky is woken by Yashvin, grouses, drinks pick-me-ups, and provides comic counterpoint to Vronsky's more serious concerns:

> "'Is it over? How did you come off?' queried Petritsky, blinking and rubbing his eyes." [062-006]

He was worried about his bet on Vronsky: "Here it is!" — he pulls out a letter confirming his wager [062-022]. Even nursing a hangover, Petritsky's first thought is for his friend's race result.

## Relationships

### With Vronsky

Petritsky is Vronsky's closest regimental companion — a friendship based not on shared intellect but on shared life. Vronsky genuinely likes Petritsky's good humor and forgives his debts and scandals. Their living arrangement at camp reflects the easy intimacy of military comradeship.

### With Yashvin

Petritsky and Yashvin are the two poles of Vronsky's regimental world. Yashvin is the dominant one — ordering Petritsky about, standing over him "like a mother making a child take medicine" [062-034] — while Petritsky is the clown, the dependent little brother who gets in trouble and needs rescuing.

### With the Baroness

The episode with the Baroness Shilton reveals Petritsky as less a seducer than a willing participant in chaos. He calls her his "wife" half-jokingly, and she treats the officers' hut as her temporary refuge from a bad marriage — a situation that captures the casual immorality of the military-aristocratic social circuit.

## Themes & Significance

Petritsky embodies the junior-officer type that Tolstoy knew intimately from his own military service. He is irresponsible, drunk, and in debt — yet also good-natured, loyal, and enviably free of hypocrisy. He belongs to the world of the regiment that Vronsky will increasingly leave behind as his affair with Anna deepens — and his presence in the early chapters of Part Two serves as a baseline of masculine camaraderie against which Vronsky's later isolation can be measured.

The Petritsky scenes also provide the novel's most sustained comic relief, their physical comedy (waking with ruffled hair, drinking brine, mimicking generals) a deliberate counterweight to the high drama unfolding in Petersburg drawing rooms.
