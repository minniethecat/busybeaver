# Princess Betsy Tverskaya

**Princess Betsy Tverskaya** (full name Princess Elizaveta Fyodorovna Tverskaya) is a prominent St. Petersburg society hostess in Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. As Anna Karenina's cousin's wife and the keeper of one of the capital's most fashionable salons, Betsy plays a pivotal role in enabling and normalizing Anna's affair with Count Vronsky. She represents the hypocrisy of high society: outwardly respectable, inwardly amoral.

## Identity & Role

Princess Betsy is introduced as a wealthy, well-connected member of the Petersburg aristocracy. Her social circle overlaps with the highest echelons of Russian society: "this circle was kept up through Princess Betsy Tverskaya, her cousin's wife" (046-003). She is married to a good-natured, corpulent husband who seems indifferent to her social machinations: "The husband of Princess Betsy, a good-natured fat man, an ardent collector of engravings" (048-019). Betsy's salon becomes the primary setting where Anna and Vronsky's relationship develops, shielded from public scrutiny by Betsy's social authority.

## Biography / Arc

### The Salon Hostess

Betsy's salon in St. Petersburg is the nexus of a particular set of society—one that prides itself on sophistication and worldliness while maintaining an outward decorum. She takes "a peculiar pleasure" (046-010) in managing the intrigues of her circle, and Anna's affair with Vronsky becomes her most captivating project. Betsy does not judge adultery; she facilitates it, provided it remains discreet.

### Facilitator of the Affair

Betsy is instrumental in bringing Anna and Vronsky together. She arranges meetings, manages social appearances, and provides cover for their rendezvous. After the theater one evening, "Princess Betsy drove home from the theater" (048-001) and immediately set about orchestrating the social dynamics of her salon to benefit Anna and Vronsky. When the ambassador's wife creates an awkward moment, "Princess Betsy tried to bring the whole party together" (048-029), demonstrating her skill at managing the delicate balance between scandal and respectability.

### Withdrawal When Things Go Wrong

When Anna's situation becomes openly scandalous—after she confesses to Karenin and society begins to ostracize her—Betsy distances herself. Her loyalty is to the social game, not to Anna personally. This abandonment mirrors the novel's broader critique of a society that encourages transgression in private while punishing it in public.

## Themes & Significance

Princess Betsy embodies the moral vacuum of high society. She is not actively malicious—she is merely indifferent to moral considerations that do not affect social standing. Her salon functions as a space where the rules of conventional morality are suspended and replaced by the rules of social performance. Through Betsy, Tolstoy critiques a society that creates the conditions for Anna's fall while reserving the right to condemn her for it.

## Key Passages

> Betsy takes "a peculiar pleasure" in her role as society's puppet-master (046-010)

> After the theater, Betsy's home becomes the stage for the evening's real drama: "Princess Betsy drove home from the theater, and had scarcely had time to go into her drawing-room" (048-001) before her guests arrived with news and intrigues.

> Betsy's social engineering: "Princess Betsy tried to bring the whole party together" (048-029), smoothing over tensions between rival society factions.
