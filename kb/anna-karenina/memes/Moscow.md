# Moscow

Moscow is the primary urban setting for the novel's early sections and a symbolic counterpoint to St. Petersburg throughout the narrative. The ancient capital represents traditional Russian life — more relaxed, more human, and less rigid than the bureaucratic world of Petersburg — yet it is also the site of the social codes and romantic complications that drive the plot.

## Role in the Narrative

The novel opens in Moscow, in the Oblonsky household. Moscow is where Levin arrives from the country to propose to Kitty, where Anna first meets Vronsky at the railway station, and where the fateful ball takes place. It is the city of family connections, social gossip, and the "half Moscow and Petersburg" that counts Stiva Oblonsky as a friend or relation [013-002].

## Moscow Society

Tolstoy depicts Moscow society as more intimate and less formal than Petersburg's. Stiva Oblonsky is the quintessential Moscow man — charming, well-connected, irresponsible, and universally liked. The Moscow of the novel is a city where everyone knows everyone, where social obligations are inescapable, and where the pleasures and pitfalls of high society are most visible.

Vronsky's observation about Moscow captures its distinctive character: "in all Moscow people ... there's something uncompromising. They are all on the defensive, lose their tempers, as though they all want to make one feel something" [025-026].

## Associated Characters

- **The Oblonskys**: Stiva, Dolly, and their children live in Moscow, and their household crisis opens the novel
- **The Shtcherbatskys**: Kitty's family, who Levin has loved since his student days [014-002]
- **Levin**: Arrives in Moscow to propose to Kitty; feels perpetually uncomfortable in the city
- **Anna**: Her arrival in Moscow by train (and the death of the railway worker) begins her tragic arc

## Symbolism

Moscow represents the heart of Russian social life — less artificial than Petersburg but still governed by codes that crush those who violate them. For Levin, Moscow is a place of social anxiety and emotional risk; for Anna, it is the site of her fateful encounter with Vronsky. The city embodies both the warmth of Russian tradition and the unforgiving scrutiny of aristocratic society.
