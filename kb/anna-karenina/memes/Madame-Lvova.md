## Identity & Role

Natalia Lvova (née Shcherbatskaya) is Kitty's eldest sister, married to the diplomat Lvov. She is mentioned as far back as Part One — "she married the diplomat Lvov" when Kitty was "still a child" [014-003] — but appears most prominently at Kitty's wedding in Part Five. A self-possessed beauty with "plump, handsome face" [136-015], she serves as Kitty's bridal-mother and represents the successful aristocratic marriage that stands as one model for Kitty's future.

> "Kitty's elder sister, who had taken her mother's place, the self-possessed beauty, Madame Lvova, who had just arrived from abroad." [137-002]

## Biography & Arc

### Early Reference

Natalia's marriage to Lvov is mentioned early in the novel as part of the Shcherbatsky family history. By the time the main action begins, she is already long married and living abroad:

> "She had been married when she married the diplomat Lvov. Kitty was still a child when she left home." [014-003]

### At Kitty's Wedding

Madame Lvova takes the role of bridal-mother at Kitty's wedding to Levin — a significant honor that reflects her status as the senior married sister:

> "Kitty stayed at her sister's house with her sister, Madame Lvova, who was her bridal-mother." [135-004]

She dresses Kitty for the ceremony with the care of an experienced married woman:

> "'It's a good thing you've thought of that,' said Kitty's sister, Madame Lvova, and with her plump, handsome face she smiled." [136-015]

During the wedding preparations and ceremony, she provides the calm, competent presence that the nervous Kitty needs:

> "The self-possessed beauty, Madame Lvova, who had just arrived from abroad." [137-002]

### Observations on Marriage

In conversation with Countess Nordston at the wedding, Madame Lvova offers a glimpse into the realities of aristocratic marriage:

> "'Still he's not worth her little finger, is he?' Countess Nordston said to Madame Lvova. 'Still he's not worth her little finger, is he?'" [137-015]

> "'We're all obedient wives; it's in our family,' answered Madame Lvova." [137-020]

This brief exchange suggests a resigned acceptance of the marital bargain — the acknowledgment that women like Kitty marry beneath their worth, but that this is simply how things are. The lightness of the remark masks a deeper melancholy about the institution that Kitty is about to enter.

### Later Appearance

Madame Lvova and her husband Lvov appear again in Part Seven, when Levin and Kitty visit them. Lvov is described as the husband of Natalia, and he and Levin discuss philosophical matters:

> "Lvov, the husband of Natalia, Kitty's sister, had spent his whole life in foreign capitals, where he had been educated and had served as a diplomat." [201-001]

## Relationships

### With Kitty

Natalia is Kitty's elder sister and, during the wedding, her substitute mother. The relationship seems warm but slightly distant — Natalia has been abroad for much of Kitty's young adulthood, and the age gap means they have never been close companions. Still, Natalia performs her duty as bridal-mother with grace and genuine affection.

### With Countess Nordston

At the wedding, Madame Lvova's conversation with Countess Nordston reveals the shared understanding of married women in their circle — an ironic, slightly weary recognition of what marriage actually entails, beneath the lace and ceremony.

### With Lvov

Her marriage to the diplomat Lvov appears stable and conventional — the sort of marriage that the Shcherbatsky parents hope for all their daughters. Lvov himself is a minor character, appearing mostly in philosophical conversations with Levin.

## Themes & Significance

Madame Lvova is a minor character who nevertheless serves an important structural function. She represents the "successful" outcome of the Shcherbatsky marriage market — a daughter married to a diplomat, living abroad, a credit to the family. Her presence at Kitty's wedding provides a benchmark against which Kitty's own marriage can be measured.

Her resigned remark about "obedient wives" is characteristic Tolstoyan irony — the recognition, never directly stated, that even the most successful aristocratic marriages involve a surrender of self that the wedding ceremony prettifies but cannot disguise.
