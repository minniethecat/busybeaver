## Identity & Role

Mihailov is a Russian painter living and working in Italy when Vronsky and Anna encounter him during their travels in Part Five. He is a genuine artist — poor, dedicated, and fiercely protective of his work — in contrast to Vronsky's dilettantish dabbling in painting. The Mihailov chapters (141–143) constitute one of Tolstoy's most concentrated statements on the nature of art, the psychology of the artist, and the gap between true creativity and aristocratic amateurism.

> "The artist Mihailov was, as always, at work when the countess's card and the visitors' cards were brought to him." [142-001]

He creates a painting of Christ before Pilate that becomes the focal point of the encounter — a work that Vronsky and Golenishtchev admire and critique, revealing more about themselves than about the painting.

## Biography & Arc

### The Struggling Artist

Mihailov lives in poverty, entirely devoted to his work. When Vronsky and Golenishtchev first mention him, the contrast with their own comfortable dilettantism is already established:

> "'Have you seen Mihailov's picture?' he said, handing him a Russian newspaper." [141-003]

> "'And is it true that this Mihailov is in such poverty?' asked Vronsky." [141-009]

Despite his poverty, Mihailov takes his work with absolute seriousness. The narrator enters his consciousness to reveal the artist's complex psychology — his hunger for praise, his contempt for the praise he receives, and his ultimate faith in his own vision:

> "Mihailov was, as always, at work when the countess's card was brought to him. In the morning he had been working at his large picture. When he came into the house he was furious with his wife for not having been able to manage the landlord..." [142-001]

### The Studio Visit

The studio scene is one of Tolstoy's most brilliant set-pieces. Vronsky, Anna, and Golenishtchev arrive to view Mihailov's Christ before Pilate. Mihailov is acutely conscious of their judgment even as he despises it:

> "Mihailov made an unpleasant impression on Vronsky at first sight... Anxiety to keep up his dignity made Mihailov assume an even haughtier manner." [142-012]

The visitors praise the painting's technique, which Mihailov knows is the easy compliment of amateurs. What he craves is recognition of the thing that matters — the inner truth of the work:

> "On entering the studio, Mihailov once more scanned his visitors and noted down in his imagination Vronsky's expression too, especially his jaws." [143-001]

But then Golenishtchev makes an observation about the figure of Pilate — a remark that happens to align with Mihailov's own deepest intention. In that moment, Mihailov's hostility dissolves:

> "Mihailov flushed with excitement... He had himself thought what Golenishtchev said." [143-004]

### Anna's Portrait

After the studio visit, Vronsky decides to paint Anna's portrait himself — and Mihailov, in a bitter irony, is asked to give lessons. He agrees, needing the money, but the result is that Vronsky produces a mediocre portrait while Mihailov, watching Anna sit, produces a small study that captures her essence:

> "Mihailov did not remember his surname though he recognized him as a Russian living here." [143-002]

The contrast between the two portraits — Vronsky's technically competent but lifeless image and Mihailov's deeply felt sketch — is Tolstoy's verdict on the difference between skill and art.

## Relationships

### With Vronsky

The encounter between Mihailov and Vronsky is one of the novel's most important minor relationships — not for any personal bond, but because it crystallizes Tolstoy's critique of aristocratic amateurism. Vronsky, who has taken up painting as a pastime, recognizes Mihailov's superiority without fully understanding it. The gap between them is not one of talent but of necessity: Mihailov must paint; Vronsky merely chooses to.

### With Anna Karenina

Anna is more perceptive than Vronsky about Mihailov's work. She sees something in the Christ painting that the men miss. Mihailov, in turn, is fascinated by Anna's beauty — and his portrait of her, done in a single sitting, captures something that Vronsky's more elaborate effort cannot.

### With Golenishtchev

Golenishtchev is the "intellectual" friend who provides the critical vocabulary for the visit. His observation about Pilate momentarily bridges the gap between artist and critic — but Tolstoy's irony is that Golenishtchev himself is pursuing a project (a book about "the principles of the new art") that is itself a form of dilettantism compared to Mihailov's actual making.

## Themes & Significance

The Mihailov episode is Tolstoy's most direct exploration of the nature of artistic creation within *Anna Karenina*. Through Mihailov, Tolstoy argues that true art comes not from leisure or education but from necessity — from the artist's inability NOT to create. The contrast between Mihailov's poverty and Vronsky's wealth is not incidental; it is central to Tolstoy's theory that art is a form of labor, not a form of consumption.

Mihailov's psychology — his oscillation between contempt for his audience and desperate hunger for their approval — is drawn from Tolstoy's own experience as an artist, and it rings with uncomfortable truth. The studio scene remains one of the most acute fictional renderings of what it feels like to have one's work judged by people who cannot understand it.
