# Dreams and Premonitions in Anna Karenina

Dreams in *Anna Karenina* function as a window into the subconscious, revealing fears and desires that characters suppress in waking life. Tolstoy uses dreams as structural devices to foreshadow tragedy, deepen psychological characterization, and expose the gap between what characters tell themselves and what they know. The novel's dream logic operates alongside its realist surface, providing a counter-narrative in which the truths that consciousness resists become unavoidable.

## Anna's Recurring Nightmare

The most significant dream in the novel is Anna's recurring nightmare, in which a peasant mutters in French while working on iron. This dream appears at critical moments — after her confession to Karenin, before her illness, and in the days leading to her death. The dream's power lies in its unspeakability: Anna cannot narrate it fully to herself, let alone to others. The narrator observes that "in dreams, when she had no control over her thoughts, her position presented itself to her in all its hideous nakedness" [^PN-053-014].

The peasant figure merges features of both Vronsky and Karenin, suggesting Anna's entrapment between the two men who define her existence. The dream's French muttering evokes the cosmopolitan artificiality of the society that judges her; the iron suggests the railway and the inexorable mechanical force of fate. Tolstoy's psychological insight is remarkable for its time: the dream is not a puzzle to be solved but a revelation of a divided self that cannot be made whole.

## Premonitions of Death

The novel is saturated with foreboding. The railway watchman's death at the Moscow station in Part 1 — the first significant event Anna and Vronsky witness together — foreshadows her own death on the tracks. "The station watchman fell under the train... A bad omen," Anna thinks [^PN-025-030], and the omen is fulfilled with terrible precision. Tolstoy structures these premonitions not as supernatural portents but as psychological facts: Anna sees the connection because, on some level, she already intuits her destination.

Levin experiences a different kind of premonition — a visceral fear of death after his brother Nikolai's passing. Where Anna's premonitions drive her toward destruction, Levin's mortality-awareness drives him toward meaning. The contrast is essential to the novel's moral architecture: the same awareness of death can propel one character toward the railway tracks and another toward faith.

## Vronsky's Dream

Vronsky, too, is haunted by dreams, though Tolstoy renders his with less psychological intensity than Anna's. After the steeplechase — in which his mare Frou-Frou is fatally injured through his own faulty riding — Vronsky dreams of the horse's shattered body. This dream is not a prophecy but a memory that refuses to be assimilated, a guilt that his waking consciousness cannot process. The parallel with Anna is deliberate: Vronsky's carelessness destroys the horse just as his pursuit destroys Anna, and in both cases his dreams register what his conscious mind evades.

Stiva Oblonsky, the novel's unreflective hedonist, is introduced through a dream — a comic fantasy of dinner parties and beautiful women that merges with his waking calculations about his salary and debts [^PN-009-004]. Stiva's dream is the novel's satire on the very concept of the unconscious: for a man without an inner life, even dreams are trivial. The contrast with Anna's torment could not be sharper. Stiva "must forget himself in the dream of daily life" [^PN-010-003] — a phrase that captures his strategy for avoiding the self-knowledge that dreams might otherwise force upon him.

## Dreams of Ideal Life

In contrast to the nightmare-visions that torment Anna, Levin and Kitty experience aspirational dreams — visions of a life they actively pursue. Levin drives home "in the pleasantest daydreams" [^PN-034-001], imagining the married life he hopes to build. His plans for agricultural reform, his visions of a partnership with the peasantry, his ideals of domestic happiness — these are all forms of wakeful dreaming that give shape and direction to his days. Unlike Anna's nightmare, which controls her, Levin's daydreams are instruments of his agency.

But Tolstoy is careful not to sentimentalize. Levin's dreams, too, can become traps — visions of perfection that prevent him from accepting the imperfect reality of marriage, farming, and human relationship. "He had dreamed of beginning with his wife" [^PN-035-001] a life of shared ideals, and the discovery that Kitty has her own desires and opinions is a shock. The novel's wisdom lies in its recognition that the healthiest dreams are those we can relinquish.

## Dreams as Moral Revelation

Tolstoy's use of dreams belongs to a long tradition — from biblical visions to Dostoevsky's fever-dreams — in which the unconscious is understood as a site of moral truth. Anna knows, in her dreams, what she cannot admit while awake: that her position is untenable, that the society she defied will destroy her, that Vronsky's love cannot sustain the weight she has placed upon it. The dreams do not cause her tragedy; they reveal it. The question the novel poses is whether such knowledge, once granted, can be acted upon — whether the self that dreams can save the self that wakes.
