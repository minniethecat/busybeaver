# Snetkov

## Identity & Role

Mihail Stepanovitch Snetkov is the marshal of the province — a nobleman "of the old school" [191-006] who has held the position for many years. He appears in Part Six during the provincial elections, where his re-election is challenged by a younger, more progressive faction backed by political ambition and social maneuvering.

Snetkov represents the old patriarchal nobility: men who held office by tradition and personal standing rather than through political organization. His defeat at the elections — despite the loyalty of old-guard supporters — marks a narrative turning point, signaling the erosion of the traditional social order that characters like [[Konstantin-Levin]] instinctively defend.

## Biography / Arc

Snetkov enters the narrative through Levin's sympathetic eyes. Levin sees him as "a nobleman of the old school, who had spent his whole life in the service of the nobility" [191-006]. The incumbent marshal is a man of "unimpeachable honor" [191-006], universally respected if not universally supported.

The election itself is a study in political mechanics. Sviazhsky's faction works to persuade district delegations "to ask Snetkov to stand" [192-005], creating the appearance of broad support while simultaneously organizing his defeat. The narrator notes the unspoken calculation: "if two refused to do so, Snetkov might decline to stand at all" [192-011] — a scenario the opposition hopes for.

Levin witnesses the campaign against Snetkov with growing unease. Snetkov's supporters resort to old-fashioned methods — getting opponents drunk [193-019] — while the opposing faction operates with a new kind of political sophistication. Levin, watching Snetkov at the balloting, feels "a little more respectfully" [194-023] toward the old marshal, whose quiet dignity contrasts with the maneuvering around him.

The vote itself is a set-piece of political theater. Snetkov's name is called: "Mihail Stepanovitch Snetkov, would now be balloted for as marshal" [195-034]. The nobles "thronged" [195-037] toward the balloting table, just as they had "followed Snetkov when he was elected" [195-058] in previous years. But this time, the numbers turn against him, and the old marshal falls to the organized opposition.

## Relationships

- **[[Konstantin-Levin]]**: Levin, who attends the elections almost as an anthropological observer, feels instinctive sympathy for Snetkov as a representative of the old order that Levin himself idealizes.
- **[[Sviazhsky]]**: Sviazhsky is among the leaders of the faction working to unseat Snetkov, representing the new political class that values organization over tradition.

## Themes & Significance

Snetkov's defeat is one of the novel's most understated political set-pieces. Without overt commentary, Tolstoy shows the machinery of modern politics at work: coalitions, whipping votes, the careful management of appearances. Snetkov, who governed by personal authority, is no match for men who govern by organization.

The episode also illuminates Levin's political philosophy. Levin supports Snetkov not because he has a program or a platform, but precisely because Snetkov embodies a pre-political ideal of noblesse oblige — rule by personal honor rather than by faction. His defeat is, for Levin, one more piece of evidence that the world he values is disappearing.

The description of Snetkov as a nobleman "of the old school" [191-006] resonates throughout the novel's treatment of social change. Like the old agrarian order Levin practices on his estate, Snetkov's world is being displaced by forces it cannot comprehend and has no tools to resist.

## Key Passages

> The marshal of the province, Snetkov, was a nobleman of the old school, who had spent his whole life in the service of the nobility, a man of unimpeachable honor in his own way. [191-006]

The narrator's introduction establishes both Snetkov's virtues and their limits — he is honorable "in his own way," a qualification that hints at the world's changing standards.

> Surely Snetkov was the enemy. As he went up, the nobles thronged about him. [195-036, 195-037]

The irony is pointed: Snetkov, the honorable incumbent, is now "the enemy," and the same nobles who once supported him now surround his opponent with equal enthusiasm.

> Just as they had followed Snetkov when he was elected. [195-058]

The closing image of the election sequence — the crowd shifts its allegiance, and the old marshal is left behind. The sentence's brevity is its power: no moralizing, just the fact of political mutability.
