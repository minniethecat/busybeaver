# St. Petersburg

St. Petersburg is the imperial capital — a city of government, high society, and rigid formality that stands in sharp contrast to both Moscow's warmth and the countryside's authenticity. It is Anna's home, the seat of her husband's bureaucratic power, and the arena where her social destruction plays out.

## Role in the Narrative

Anna lives in St. Petersburg with her husband Karenin and their son Seryozha. After meeting Vronsky in Moscow, she returns to Petersburg — but Vronsky follows her, and the affair begins in earnest in the capital's drawing rooms and society gatherings. It is in St. Petersburg that Anna experiences her progressive social exclusion, as the doors that once opened to her quietly close.

## Petersburg Society

Tolstoy portrays Petersburg society as colder, more formal, and more rigidly hierarchical than Moscow's. Where Stiva Oblonsky's Moscow charm forgives everything, Petersburg's judgment is final and unforgiving. The city's social world is organized around three concentric circles: Karenin's government colleagues, the fashionable set centered on Countess Lydia Ivanovna, and the genuine high society that Anna once belonged to.

Karenin embodies Petersburg at its most bureaucratic — his approach to life, including his marriage, is governed by procedures and proper forms. The city itself seems to share his character: efficient, correct, and without warmth.

## The Moscow–Petersburg Axis

Tolstoy uses the two cities to mark a fundamental division in Russian elite life. Moscow is traditional, familial, and Russian; Petersburg is modern, bureaucratic, and Europeanized. Stiva Oblonsky's observation that "half Moscow and Petersburg were friends and relations" of his [013-002] reveals both the interconnectedness of the aristocracy and the central role these two cities play in the novel's social geography.

## Associated Characters

- **Alexei Karenin**: A high-ranking government minister in Petersburg; his entire identity is tied to the city's bureaucratic world
- **Anna**: Lives in Petersburg as Madame Karenina; her social identity is defined by her position in Petersburg society — a position she loses
- **Countess Lydia Ivanovna**: A prominent figure in Petersburg's religious-social circles who turns against Anna
- **Countess Vronskaya**: Vronsky's mother, a Petersburg society fixture

## Symbolism

Petersburg represents the institutional power that Anna's passion challenges — and that ultimately crushes her. The city is a monument to rules, hierarchy, and appearances, the very forces that Anna's honest passion cannot survive within.
