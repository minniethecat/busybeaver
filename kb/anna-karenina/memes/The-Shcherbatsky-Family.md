# The Shcherbatsky Family

The Shcherbatsky family — [[Prince-Shcherbatsky|Prince Alexander Shcherbatsky]], [[Princess-Shcherbatskaya]], and their daughters [[Dolly]], [[Kitty]], and [[Natalie]] — represent the old Moscow nobility in *Anna Karenina*. They embody traditional values of familial loyalty, social respectability, and the marriage economy of aristocratic Russia.

## Family as Moral Anchor

Unlike the fractured [[Oblonsky-Family|Oblonsky household]], the Shcherbatskys maintain a stable, if sometimes fraught, domestic order. The Prince's bluff honesty and the Princess's anxious matchmaking reflect complementary approaches to family welfare:

> The families of the Levins and the Shtcherbatskys were old, noble Moscow families, and had always been on terms of intimacy. [014-002]

The Shcherbatskys serve as the novel's normative family — the standard against which other domestic arrangements are measured. Their stability makes [[Kitty]]'s marriage to [[Konstantin-Levin|Levin]] plausible, grounding it in shared class and cultural expectations.

## The Marriage Plot

The Princess's campaign to secure advantageous marriages for her daughters drives much of the early narrative. [[Kitty]]'s rejection of Levin in favor of [[Alexei-Vronsky|Vronsky]], followed by Vronsky's abandonment, precipitates the family's crisis. The Shcherbatskys' eventual acceptance of Levin — a socially awkward but morally sound suitor — signals the novel's preference for substance over style in marriage.

## The Shcherbatsky Sisters

[[Dolly]], [[Kitty]], and the absent [[Natalie]] represent three paths for aristocratic women. Dolly endures marital betrayal with stoic dignity; Kitty chooses a partner based on genuine connection; Natalie's marriage to a diplomat removes her from the family orbit. Through these sisters, Tolstoy examines the limited agency of women in a system that values them primarily as wives and mothers.
