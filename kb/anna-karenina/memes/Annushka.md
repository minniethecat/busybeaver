# Annushka

## Identity & Role

Annushka is Anna Karenina's maid and companion, one of the few characters who witnesses Anna in her unguarded moments. She appears throughout the novel as a quiet, loyal presence — dressing Anna, traveling with her, and providing the domestic continuity that contrasts with the turbulence of Anna's emotional life.

Unlike the society figures who judge Anna, Annushka's loyalty is uncomplicated. She serves without moralizing, and her steady presence at Anna's side across the novel's arc makes her a kind of silent witness to the tragedy.

She first appears during Anna's train journey back to Petersburg, where she sits beside her mistress in the compartment. Her role there is ambiguous enough that an outside observer mistakes her — the narrator notes Anna "could not have said for certain whether it were Annushka at her side or a stranger" [037-003], emphasizing the dreamlike quality of Anna's state of mind.

## Biography & Arc

Annushka's service to Anna spans the novel's geography. In Petersburg, she helps Anna dress for the ball: "with Annushka's assistance, pinning the last ribbon on her dress before the looking-glass" [069-001].

Her most significant scenes occur during the period of Anna's deepening crisis. When Anna prepares for her fateful meeting with Seryozha and Vronsky, Annushka is present: "I need nothing," Anna tells her as Annushka rearranges bottles and brushes on the dressing table [092-006, 092-007]. When Annushka returns to remind Anna that "Vronsky and Seryozha are waiting" [092-009], she smiles — one of the rare warm expressions in the increasingly dark atmosphere of Anna's inner world. Annushka's gentle observation — "Seryozha's been naughty, I think" [092-011] — is a delicate touch of domestic normality.

Annushka travels with Anna and Seryozha by the evening train to Moscow [092-025], and it is "when Annushka called her attention to the rattle of the carriage" [093-001] that Anna's attention is drawn to the outside world. When Anna abruptly changes plans, she tells Annushka "we are not going" [093-016].

In the novel's later sections, Annushka's return is marked by familiarity and relief. Anna "felt a great sense of relief when Annushka, whom she had known for years, came in to her" [184-003]. Annushka "was obviously much pleased at her mistress's arrival" [184-004], and her entrance "put a stop to Annushka's gossip" [184-008] with the hotel servants — a small vignette that shows Annushka not just as a servant but as a person with her own social world.

## Relationships

- **Anna Karenina** — her mistress. Annushka's loyalty to Anna is unwavering, and she is one of the few people Anna trusts unconditionally. Her presence offers Anna moments of comfort and normalcy.
- **Seryozha Karenin** — Anna's son, whom Annushka helps care for during Anna's brief reunion with him.

## Themes & Significance

Annushka represents the world of practical care that continues regardless of psychological turmoil. While Anna spirals toward destruction, Annushka pins ribbons, arranges bottles, calls attention to carriage arrivals, and provides the physical continuity of daily life. This contrast — between cosmic emotional catastrophe and the small rituals of dressing and travel — is characteristically Tolstoyan.

Her loyalty also serves as a structural counterpoint to the fickleness of society. Where Princess Betsy Tverskaya and her circle shift their judgments with the wind, Annushka's devotion to Anna is constant from the train journey to the final pages.

The narrator's description of Anna's "great sense of relief when Annushka, whom she had known for years, came in" [184-003] is significant: in a novel where Anna is progressively isolated, the comfort of a familiar face is a rare positive emotion.

## Key Passages

> She could not have said for certain whether it were Annushka at her side or a stranger. [037-003]

Anna's disoriented state during the train journey — the blurring of the familiar and the strange — captures her psychological condition at the start of her tragic arc.

> "I need nothing," she said to Annushka, who was rearranging the bottles and brushes on the dressing table. [092-006]

A moment of Anna dismissing even the small ministrations of care — a signal of her withdrawal from the world.

> She felt a great sense of relief when Annushka, whom she had known for years, came in to her. [184-003]

In the novel's darkening final act, the comfort of a long-known servant's presence is a rare bright note.
