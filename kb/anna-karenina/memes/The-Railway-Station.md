## Location & Setting

The railway station in *Anna Karenina* is not a single location but a recurring setting that appears at crucial moments throughout the novel. The most significant stations are the Moscow railway station (where Anna first meets Vronsky) and the Petersburg station (where she arrives with him on her return from Moscow, and where Karenin meets her). A smaller country station near Levin's estate also features, serving the rural railway line.

The station embodies the novel's ambivalence toward modernity. The railway is the great technological achievement of the age — a symbol of progress, speed, and the shrinking of distances. But it is also associated throughout the novel with danger, accident, and death. The railway is where the novel's two most famous deaths occur: the anonymous worker crushed under a train in Part 1, and Anna's suicide under a train in Part 7.

## Physical Description

The stations are described with Tolstoy's characteristic attention to the physical and social environment: the platforms, the waiting rooms, the buffet, the tracks stretching away into the winter darkness. At the Moscow station, the encounter between Anna and Vronsky takes place in the cold night air of the platform — an appropriately liminal space for a meeting that will dissolve the boundaries of Anna's orderly life.

> At the station of the Petersburg railway to meet his mother, Vronsky encountered Anna for the first time. (025-001)

The station is always a place of movement and transition, populated by travelers, porters, and officials. Its essential character is transience — people arrive and depart, lives intersect briefly, and the momentum of the journey carries everyone forward.

## Significance in the Narrative

### The First Meeting

Anna and [[Alexei-Vronsky]] first meet at the Moscow railway station, where both have come to meet the train from Petersburg — Anna to greet her brother Stiva, Vronsky to meet his mother. Their encounter on the platform, with its fateful prefiguration of the railway worker's death, establishes the motif that will run through the entire novel.

> "I met Vronsky at the railway station," Anna tells Dolly later (028-031), and even in this simple statement, the reader who knows how the novel ends hears the ominous undertone.

### The Omen

Immediately after Anna and Vronsky meet, a railway worker is crushed to death by a train. The incident is treated as an omen by Anna:

> "A bad omen," she said softly. (026-047)

The worker's death — a man crushed by the very machine of progress — is a structural prefiguration of Anna's own end. The repetition of the railway motif, from this first omen through to Anna's suicide, creates the sense of fate that pervades the novel.

### The Final Scene

Anna's suicide at the railway station in Part 7 is the novel's most harrowing set piece. The station — the same liminal, transitory space where her affair began — becomes the site of its ending. The train that first brought Vronsky into her life now takes her out of it. The symmetry is devastating: the railway, symbol of the modern world's promise, delivers only death.

### The Threshold

Throughout the novel, the railway station functions as a threshold between worlds: between Moscow and Petersburg, between city and country, between the safety of established life and the danger of passionate love. Characters pass through stations at moments of transition, and the station's character — neither here nor there, a place of waiting and departure — mirrors the uncertainty of those moments.
