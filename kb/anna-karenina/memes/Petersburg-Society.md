## Definition & Scope

**Petersburg society** in *Anna Karenina* is not merely a geographic designation — it is a moral and aesthetic category. The narrator consistently associates the imperial capital with coldness, formality, and a social order in which appearance takes precedence over substance. Where [[Moscow-Society]] is characterized by the warm disorder of the Oblonsky household and the youthful vitality of Kitty's ball, Petersburg represents the machinery of the state, the rigidity of court protocol, and a social circle whose judgments determine fates while operating by a code that rewards discretion over virtue.

Vronsky, on returning to Petersburg, "slipped his feet into old slippers" — the narrator's metaphor for the easy re-entry into a world where "all people were divided into utterly opposed classes" (042-016): the "ridiculous people" who believe in conventional morality, and the "real people" who live by elegance, pleasure, and the absence of shame.

## Social Geography

The novel maps Petersburg society through a series of interconnected circles:

- **The Bureaucratic Sphere**, centered on [[Alexei-Karenin]] and his ministry colleagues. This is the world of official rank, the Alexander Nevsky decoration, and the slow calcification of a career that has run its course. Karenin's horror at Anna's public affair is as much professional as moral — she has violated *both* codes.

- **Princess Betsy's Circle**, the fashionable salon where [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]] presides over a society that treats adultery as a conversational amusement. It is here that Anna and Vronsky's affair is tacitly sanctioned — until Anna insists on honesty, at which point she is ejected for violating the one rule that matters: discretion.

- **Countess Lydia Ivanovna's Pietistic Set**, a "set made up of elderly, ugly, benevolent, pious women, and clever, learned, ambitious men" (046-002). This circle absorbs Karenin after his humiliation and provides the religious language that transforms Anna from wronged wife into sinner.

## The Code of Discretion

The central paradox of Petersburg society is its tolerance of adultery provided it remains invisible. Princess Betsy's circle is populated by women with lovers and men with mistresses — but the *form* of the marriage must be preserved. Anna's crime is not her affair with Vronsky but her refusal to lie about it. By appearing at the opera in Part Five, she forces society to *see* what it prefers to ignore, and the punishment is swift and total.

[[Liza-Merkalova]] provides the counterexample: she conducts her liaison with [[Stremov]] with such perfect social grace that she remains unmarked. The difference is not moral — it is aesthetic, a matter of surfaces and timing, and Anna's tragedy is precisely that she cannot or will not play this game.

## Petersburg vs. Moscow vs. The Country

Tolstoy structures the novel around a geographic-moral axis:

| Realm | Values | Representative Characters |
|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Petersburg** | Formality, power, surfaces | Karenin, Lydia Ivanovna, Princess Betsy |
| **Moscow** | Warmth, family, authentic disorder | The Oblonskys, The Shcherbatskys |
| **The Country** | Labor, truth, natural order | Levin, the peasants |

Levin's flight from Petersburg society after one disastrous dinner crystallizes this opposition: "he felt that he had been caught, that he was being drawn into a world he did not understand and could not navigate" (Part Four). His redemption lies in returning to the land, to work, and to a life measured by seasons rather than seasons of society.
