# Pride and Humiliation

Pride and humiliation form a central emotional axis in *Anna Karenina*. Characters are driven by pride, undone by pride, and most devastatingly, subjected to public humiliation as a mechanism of social control.

## Anna's Pride and Fall

[[Anna-Karenina|Anna]] possesses an innate dignity that makes her social fall all the more tragic. After leaving her husband, she faces the humiliation of being barred from respectable society:

> "You've no pride, no dignity; you're disgracing yourself," [[Alexei-Karenin|Karenin]] tells her. [023-003]

Yet Anna's pride transforms. Initially a source of strength — her refusal to conduct a secret affair, her insistence on honesty — it becomes a brittle armor against social ostracism. At the opera, she endures public humiliation when [[Madame-Kartasova]] publicly snubs her. Each successive humiliation corrodes her self-worth, driving her toward isolation and despair.

## Levin's Humble Pride

[[Konstantin-Levin]] embodies a contrasting form of pride — one tempered by humility and grounded in authentic self-assessment. He takes pride in his agricultural work, his relationship with the peasantry, and his family, but his pride does not require social validation:

> If they had told me to do it as a result of some calculus, and I didn't, then pride would have come in. But in this case, one simply does what one must. [080-007]

## Pride as Social Marker

For characters like [[Stiva-Oblonsky|Stiva]] and [[Alexei-Vronsky|Vronsky]], pride is bound to social position. Stiva's pride in his liberal opinions and aristocratic connections masks a fundamental indifference to moral questions. Vronsky's pride — in his military career, his self-sufficiency, his conquest — is systematically dismantled by his love for Anna and its consequences.
