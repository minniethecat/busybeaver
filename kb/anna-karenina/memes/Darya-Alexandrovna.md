## Identity & Role

**Darya Alexandrovna Oblonsky**, universally called **Dolly**, is the wife of [[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]] Oblonsky and the eldest daughter of [[Prince-Shcherbatsky]] and [[Princess-Shcherbatskaya]]. She is the mother of six children (Grisha, Tanya, Lily, Masha, and two younger ones) and the sister of [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]] and Natalia Lvova ([[Madame-Lvova]]). Her discovery of Stiva's affair with the French governess opens the novel, and her journey from devastated wife to forgiving partner forms one of the novel's central domestic arcs.

The narrator introduces her as a woman worn down by years of childbearing and domestic management. At thirty-three, she is described as "thin, nervous, exhausted by her many children" — a stark contrast to her husband's perpetual buoyancy. Where Stiva floats through life on charm and social connections, Dolly grounds the Oblonsky household in the unglamorous labor of child-rearing, budgeting, and emotional endurance.

## Marriage and Family

### The Opening Crisis

Dolly's discovery of Stiva's infidelity sets the novel in motion. "Everything was topsy-turvy in the Oblonskys' house" (001-001): Dolly has found a note from the French governess and has locked herself in her room, refusing to see her husband or manage the household. The children run wild, the cook has quit, and Stiva wanders helplessly. This opening tableau establishes the fundamental asymmetry of their marriage: Stiva commits the sin, but Dolly bears the suffering.

[[Anna-Karenina]]'s visit to Moscow is specifically to reconcile the Oblonskys. Anna persuades Dolly to forgive Stiva — an irony that deepens as Anna's own marriage disintegrates. Dolly's forgiveness is not weakness but a pragmatic acceptance of her position: with six children and no independent means, she has little real choice. Yet she transforms this necessity into something approaching grace.

### The Visit to Vronsky's Estate

One of Dolly's most significant narrative functions is her visit to [[Anna-Karenina]] and [[Alexei-Vronsky]] at Vozdvizhenskoe in Part Six. This visit serves as Tolstoy's mechanism for contrasting two modes of female existence. Dolly, traveling in a peasant cart with children and bundles, arrives at an estate of almost theatrical luxury — and finds Anna performing a version of happiness that rings hollow.

During the carriage ride to Vozdvizhenskoe, Dolly reflects on her own life: "All her fifteen years of married life had been nothing but one long pregnancy, nursing, and sickness" — and yet she does not regret it. The visit crystallizes the moral architecture of the novel: Dolly's self-denying motherhood is set against Anna's self-asserting passion.

### Relationship with Stiva

Despite everything, Dolly loves her husband. When Stiva returns from selling the forest and arrives at [[Konstantin-Levin]]'s estate, Dolly's face lights up. She understands him — his irresponsibility, his charm, his essential good-heartedness — and chooses to live with rather than against his nature. This is not the grand passion of Anna and Vronsky, but it is a durable arrangement that keeps a family intact.

## Role as Moral Counterweight

Tolstoy uses Dolly as the implicit standard against which other female characters are measured. Her domestic virtue is not glamorous; the narrator does not aestheticize her exhaustion the way he does Anna's beauty. But Dolly's moral authority is quietly undeniable. When Levin, newly married, watches Dolly manage her household at Ergushovo, he is struck by how "she did everything herself, but it was just like a cheerful, easy game" — a description that captures both the labor and the grace of her domestic life.

Her key relationships extend across the novel's social world:
- **Kitty** — Dolly is her confidante and emotional anchor, particularly during the crisis over Vronsky and the eventual turn toward Levin
- **Anna** — Dolly admires Anna's brilliance but cannot fully endorse her choices; the Vozdvizhenskoe visit marks the emotional distance between them
- **Levin** — he respects Dolly deeply and sees in her the maternal ideal he hopes Kitty will embody

## Key Scenes

- The opening crisis: discovering Stiva's letter and collapsing into despair (Part One, Chapter 1–4)
- Anna's visit and Dolly's reluctant forgiveness (Part One, Chapters 19–20)
- Managing the household at Ergushovo with her children (Part Three, Chapters 6–9)
- The visit to Anna at Vozdvizhenskoe and the carriage-ride reflections on marriage (Part Six, Chapters 16–24)
- At Kitty's wedding, serving as her sister's emotional anchor (Part Five, Chapter 3–6)
- Hosting Levin and Kitty after their marriage, offering domestic wisdom (Part Six, Chapter 16)

Dolly is the novel's most fully realized portrait of maternal love — not idealized, but rendered in the gritty detail of soiled linen, childhood illnesses, and the endless arithmetic of making ends meet on an unfaithful husband's fluctuating income.
