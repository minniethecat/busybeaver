# Anna Karenina

Anna Arkadyevna Karenina is the central figure of Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*. A beautiful and passionate aristocrat married to the austere government official Alexei Alexandrovitch Karenin, her fateful encounter with the dashing Count Alexei Vronsky at a Moscow railway station sets in motion the novel's central tragedy.

## Identity & Role

Anna is the sister of Stepan "Stiva" Arkadyevitch Oblonsky and the wife of Alexei Alexandrovitch Karenin, a high-ranking government minister. At the start of the novel she is eight years married, with a young son, Seryozha. She moves in the highest circles of St. Petersburg society and is admired for her grace, intelligence, and charm.

Anna first appears in Part One, Chapter 17, arriving in Moscow by train to help reconcile her brother Stiva with his wife Dolly after Stiva's infidelity. Her arrival is preceded by the ominous death of a railway worker — a moment that foreshadows her own fate.

## Character Arc

### Arrival in Moscow

Anna travels from St. Petersburg to Moscow to mediate between her brother Stiva and his wife Dolly after Stiva's affair with the French governess is discovered [025-013]. On the journey she meets Countess Vronskaya, and upon arrival is introduced to her son, the young cavalry officer Count Alexei Vronsky. Their meeting at the station is overshadowed by a railway worker's accidental death — a moment that disturbs Anna deeply.

> "It's an evil omen," Madame Karenina said in an agitated whisper, as the station-master told Vronsky of the death [026-047].

### The Ball and the Affair

At a Moscow ball, Anna dances with Vronsky, beginning what will become the defining relationship of her life. Her return to St. Petersburg does not end the connection — Vronsky follows her, and the two begin a passionate affair that scandalizes society.

### Social Downfall

Unlike men in her circle, Anna bears the full brunt of social condemnation for the affair. While her husband Karenin is primarily concerned with appearances and legal standing, Anna finds herself gradually excluded from the society that once embraced her. Her refusal to dissemble — to conduct the affair discreetly while maintaining a public facade — distinguishes her from her peers and accelerates her isolation.

### Tragedy at the Station

Anna's story ends at a railway station — the same setting where she first appeared. Desperate, isolated, and convinced that Vronsky's love has cooled, she throws herself under a train. The symmetry of her death matching the worker's death at her first appearance is one of the novel's most famous structural devices.

## Key Traits

- **Beauty and vitality**: Tolstoy emphasizes Anna's "suppressed eagerness" and "light, resolute step" [026-017]. She is described as having a "fully-developed figure" that moves with "strange lightness" [026-029].
- **Honesty**: Anna refuses to live a lie, unlike many in her social circle who conduct affairs discreetly. This honesty is both her moral strength and her social undoing.
- **Emotional intensity**: From the railway worker's death that "oppresses" her [026-066] to her final despair, Anna feels deeply and acts on her feelings.

## Themes Embodied

Anna's story explores the constraints placed on women in 19th-century Russian aristocratic society, the double standard of sexual morality, the conflict between passion and duty, and the search for authentic life in a world built on appearances. Her fate serves as Tolstoy's indictment of a society that punishes women for the same transgressions it tacitly permits in men.
