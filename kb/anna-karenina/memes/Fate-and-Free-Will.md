# Fate and Free Will in Anna Karenina

The tension between fate and free will pervades Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, as characters grapple with whether their lives are governed by external destiny or personal choice. The novel operates on two levels simultaneously: characters experience themselves as making free choices, yet the narrative structure — with its ominous foreshadowing and recurring symbols — suggests an inexorable movement toward predetermined outcomes. Tolstoy does not resolve this tension; he presents it as the fundamental condition of moral life.

## The Language of Fate

Characters throughout the novel invoke fate to describe pivotal moments. Dolly speaks with a voice "submissive to fate and full of despair" [^PN-012-009], embodying a passive acceptance of circumstances beyond her control. When Levin visits the Shcherbatskys, he feels the visit will "decide his fate" [^PN-019-047], acknowledging the weight of a single moment. The language of fate is not merely rhetorical — it reflects a genuine cultural and psychological tendency to experience life's turning points as visited upon the self rather than chosen by it.

Kitty's illness prompts the princess to call the doctor to "decide our fate" [^PN-043-003], while the household anxiously awaits Kitty's "fate, which was to be decided that day" [^PN-044-001]. The repeated invocation of fate in domestic contexts — illness, marriage, family crisis — suggests that Tolstoy's characters inhabit a world in which agency is always already constrained by forces larger than the individual will. Anna, in her final moments, feels that "her fate was sealed" [^PN-099-026], accepting the inevitability of her tragic end with a clarity that is both terrifying and, in its own way, liberating.

The vocabulary of fate extends even to minor characters and subplots. Princess Varvara, contemplating her dependence, "looked to him to decide her fate, ready to submit to anything" [^PN-111-006], revealing how the language of fate masks structures of power and dependency. Dolly, reflecting on her children's future, worries that they will be "ill-fated children, who will have to bear the consequences" [^PN-188-040] of decisions made by adults who invoke fate precisely to evade the weight of those consequences.

## Anna's Fatalism

Anna's trajectory is the novel's most sustained meditation on the problem of fate. From her first appearance at the Moscow railway station — where a watchman is crushed by a train in what she immediately interprets as "a bad omen" [^PN-025-030] — she is shadowed by a sense of impending doom. Her recurring nightmare, in which a peasant mutters in French while working on iron, functions as a form of psychic fatalism: her unconscious mind has already pronounced the verdict that her conscious self resists.

Yet Tolstoy is careful to show that Anna's fatalism is also a form of self-deception. When she declares that her "fate is sealed" [^PN-064-032], she is also absolving herself of responsibility for choices she has made and continues to make. The novel's irony is that characters who most insistently invoke fate are often those most desperately avoiding the acknowledgment of their own agency. Anna chooses Vronsky, chooses to leave her husband, chooses to see her son in secret — and then describes the consequences of these choices as destiny.

## The Illusion of Choice

While characters speak of fate, the novel also emphasizes moments of genuine choice. Levin believes those who are free "make your own choice" and that it is "always clear whom you love" [^PN-087-028]. Yet the novel complicates this by showing how social pressures constrain individual agency. Anna reflects on how others spoke of "determining their fate and hers" [^PN-031-003], blurring the line between destiny and the consequences of others' decisions. Vronsky tells Anna that their "fate is sealed" [^PN-064-032], framing their illicit relationship as an inescapable outcome rather than a series of choices — a rhetorical move that serves his interest in not examining the alternatives.

The novel's treatment of choice is structurally reinforced by its parallel plots. Levin and Anna both face moments of existential crisis; both confront the inadequacy of the social roles assigned to them; both seek a form of transcendence. But where Anna surrenders to what she experiences as fate, Levin struggles — and the verb is essential — toward a form of agency grounded in moral clarity rather than mere self-assertion.

## The Railway as Fate's Symbol

The railway runs through the novel as an emblem of fate's mechanical indifference. Anna and Vronsky's first significant encounter occurs at a station where a man is killed; Anna's recurring nightmare features the railway worker; and her death occurs under the wheels of a train. The railway — that great nineteenth-century symbol of progress, speed, and technological mastery — becomes, in Tolstoy's hands, an image of the forces that human beings set in motion but cannot control. The iron tracks do not choose their victims; they simply continue, indifferent to the bodies that fall across them. In this, they are the novel's most perfect symbol of fate.

Vronsky's final scene with Anna before her death — their quarrel, his departure, her pursuit to the station — is saturated with the language of inevitability. "She felt that her fate was sealed" [^PN-099-026], and the feeling is both a surrender and, paradoxically, a form of action: she does not simply wait for fate to arrive; she walks toward it.

## Fate as Social Determination

Beyond the railway symbolism, Tolstoy presents social structures as a form of secular fate. Anna's destruction is not wrought by divine intervention or supernatural forces but by the implacable machinery of social judgment. The society that condemns her operates with the same mechanical indifference as the train: it has rules, it enforces them, and it does not care about the individual crushed beneath them. Karenin's legalistic approach to his wife's adultery — reducing it to "adultery of one of the married parties" [^PN-114-029] — is a bureaucratic version of the same fatal logic.

Levin's half-brother Koznyshev articulates the intellectual version of this determinism, arguing that human action "enters into conflict with fate" [^PN-202-004] in ways that philosophy can describe but not resolve. The novel repeatedly shows that social position — gender, class, wealth — functions as a kind of fate: a set of constraints so pervasive that characters experience them as natural rather than constructed. Dolly cannot leave her unfaithful husband not because she lacks will but because she has six children and no independent income; her "fate" is a product of the same social order that permits Oblonsky his freedoms.

When Oblonsky and Karenin maneuver to use Landau's spiritual influence, Dolly's sister Natalie observes that "your sister's fate is now in the hands of Landau" [^PN-217-030] — a statement that captures the absurdity of a world in which personal destiny is mediated by the whims of spiritualist charlatans and the men who deploy them.

## Tolstoy's Philosophical Position

Tolstoy uses the fate-versus-free-will dialectic to explore moral responsibility. The novel suggests that while characters may feel swept along by destiny, they remain accountable for their actions. The epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — introduces a third term beyond fate and free will: divine judgment. If human beings are neither fully free nor fully determined, then judgment belongs to God alone, and the novel's task is not to condemn or absolve but to understand.

Levin's eventual spiritual awakening represents a reconciliation of fate with personal agency, finding meaning not in resisting fate but in choosing how to respond to it. His discovery — that one can live rightly without resolving every philosophical question — is Tolstoy's answer to the problem of fate and free will as it presents itself in lived experience. The question is not whether we are free; it is whether we can love, work, and forgive without knowing the answer.
