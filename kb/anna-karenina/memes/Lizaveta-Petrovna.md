# Lizaveta Petrovna

**Lizaveta Petrovna** is the midwife who attends [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya|Kitty]]'s childbirth in [[Part-Seven-Chapter-13|Part Seven]] of *Anna Karenina*. A calm, professional figure, she is summoned to the Levins' household at the onset of labor and remains present throughout the entire delivery, becoming — in [[Konstantin-Levin|Levin]]'s words — "a new and important personage in [his] domestic life."

## Identity & Role

Lizaveta Petrovna is a professional midwife practicing in Moscow. She is referred to alternately as "Lizaveta Petrovna" (the form predominantly used in Constance Garnett's English translation) and "Marya Vlasyevna," the latter appearing when Levin first introduces her as a new fixture of the household. Her title and patronymic address mark her as a respected professional rather than a servant.

Her sole narrative function is the delivery of Kitty's child, but within that concentrated role she embodies medical competence and emotional steadiness. She issues quiet commands — directing Levin to light candles, summoning the doctor, managing the physical space of the bedroom — and her face becomes a barometer by which Levin reads the progress of the labor.

## Biography / Arc

Lizaveta Petrovna enters the narrative abruptly: at the first signs of Kitty's labor, the household sends for her, and she arrives "in the little sledge, wrapped in a velvet cloak, with a kerchief round her head" [210-027]. From that moment until the baby's first feeding, she does not leave the bedside.

Her presence anchors the long hours of labor. When the birth reaches its most difficult phase, her face — "stern and pale, and still as resolute, though her jaws were twitching" [212-010] — communicates the gravity of the situation to Levin without a word. Once the child is safely delivered, her professional reserve softens: she dandles the newborn "as though proud of her handiwork" and announces with evident satisfaction, "A splendid baby!" [213-017].

After ensuring the infant is properly cleaned and placed at Kitty's breast, she recedes from the narrative — her role complete, her competence having transformed a moment of existential terror into one of domestic triumph.

## Relationships

Lizaveta Petrovna's relationships are entirely professional and situational:

- **Kitty**: The midwife's primary patient. Lizaveta Petrovna addresses her with a blend of tenderness and firm authority — "Well, my dear Lizaveta Petrovna?" Kitty greets her, and the midwife responds with steady reassurance [211-030].
- **Levin**: The anxious husband whom Lizaveta Petrovna manages with quiet efficiency. She tasks him with practical errands (lighting candles) and, crucially, does not shield him from the intensity of the birth — she allows him to witness both the terror and the joy. When he panics at the baby's appearance, she laughs and tells him, "Don't be frightened, don't be frightened!" [213-011].
- **The doctor**: A professional counterpart; Lizaveta Petrovna works alongside him without tension, each respecting the other's domain.

## Themes & Significance

Lizaveta Petrovna is a minor character who nonetheless carries significant thematic weight. In a novel preoccupied with the extremes of passion and despair, she represents **professional competence** — the unglamorous, life-sustaining work that proceeds without drama. Her presence transforms childbirth from a metaphysical ordeal into a manageable medical event.

She also serves as a narrative foil to Levin's spiritual agonizing. While Levin is paralyzed by the incomprehensibility of birth — "he did not see and did not comprehend, and he had no wish to see or comprehend" [212-001] — Lizaveta Petrovna simply acts. Her deft hands and calm demeanor offer an implicit counter-argument to Levin's existential terror: meaning resides not in understanding but in competent, loving action.
