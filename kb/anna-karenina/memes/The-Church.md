## Overview

The Russian Orthodox Church in *Anna Karenina* is depicted as a powerful but ambiguous institution. It sanctifies marriages, administers sacraments, and provides the ritual framework for life's major transitions — birth, marriage, death. Yet Tolstoy's portrayal of the institutional Church is characteristically skeptical: its representatives range from the genuinely pious to the self-serving, and its doctrines are shown to be deployed as much for social control as for spiritual sustenance.

The Church's most significant narrative function is as the gatekeeper of marriage. The Orthodox understanding of marriage as indissoluble — divorce being nearly impossible without specific grounds — is the legal-religious reality that traps Anna between her loveless marriage to Karenin and her passion for Vronsky. The Church's refusal to recognize divorce creates the legal cage within which the novel's central tragedy unfolds.

## Role in the Narrative

### Marriage and Divorce

The Church's jurisdiction over marriage is the single most consequential fact of the novel's plot. Anna's inability to obtain a divorce from Karenin — first because he refuses on religious grounds, later because of intricate ecclesiastical requirements — condemns her to social limbo. She is neither wife nor widow, legally bound to a man she has left while living with another.

> "In accordance with the dictates of religion," Karenin tells himself, refusing to grant Anna a divorce. (090-015)

The Church thus becomes an instrument of punishment rather than mercy, its doctrines wielded by a wounded husband against his wife. Tolstoy's critique is implicit but unmistakable: the institution that should offer grace instead offers only law.

### Ritual and Ceremony

The Church's rituals provide some of the novel's most vivid set pieces. Levin's confession before his wedding — a requirement of Orthodox canon — becomes an occasion for his characteristic combination of earnestness and embarrassment. The wedding ceremony itself, described in rich detail, shows the Church at its most effective: channeling genuine emotion through inherited form.

> At this moment, in this idle time of standing in church, Levin felt the solemnity of what was happening. (133-013)

### The Clergy

The novel's clergymen range from the decent but ineffectual to the self-important. The priest who hears Levin's confession is an ordinary man doing his job, neither saint nor hypocrite — and Tolstoy's honest, unromanticized portrait of him is typical of the novel's treatment of institutional religion. The "highest dignitaries of all the churches and sects" (074-002) appear in the background of Petersburg social life, their spiritual authority inseparable from their social position.

### Village Piety

A counterpoint to the institutional Church is the unreflective piety of the Russian peasantry. The peasants at Levin's estate attend church as a matter of course, their faith woven into the fabric of daily life rather than a matter of intellectual choice. This embodied religion — "there was no one but the peasant in the church" (085-006) — represents a possibility of authentic faith that the educated characters struggle to access.

## Social / Political Context

### Church and State

The Russian Orthodox Church in the 1870s was, in effect, a department of the state. The Holy Synod, which governed the Church, was headed by a lay procurator appointed by the Tsar. The Church's entanglement with state power — its role in registering births, marriages, and deaths, its function in legitimizing autocracy — made it fundamentally a conservative institution, resistant to reform and aligned with the existing social order.

### Tolstoy's Religious Evolution

*Anna Karenina* was written during a period of intense religious searching for Tolstoy himself. His later works would explicitly reject institutional Orthodoxy in favor of a simplified, ethical Christianity based on the Sermon on the Mount. The novel does not go that far, but its treatment of the Church already shows the tension between institutional religion and authentic spiritual experience that would define Tolstoy's later life. Levin's climactic conversion — which owes nothing to church doctrine and everything to a peasant's simple statement about living for the soul — is Tolstoy's announcement that genuine faith lies beyond the Church's walls.

## Key Passages

The Church as social-theological authority over marriage:

> "In accordance with the dictates of religion," Karenin told himself. (090-015)

Levin's experience of church ceremony:

> At this moment, in this idle time of standing in church, Levin felt the solemnity of what was happening. (133-013)

The humbling simplicity of village piety:

> In the church there was no one but the peasant. (085-006)

The presence of high clergy in Petersburg society:

> The highest dignitaries of all the churches and sects. (074-002)

Levin's dissatisfaction with the institutional answers:

> He had never doubted the truths of religion, but his external unbelief did not in the least interfere with his inner certainty. (125-030)
