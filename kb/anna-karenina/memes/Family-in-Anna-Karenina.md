## Definition & Scope

Family in *Anna Karenina* is both the novel's explicit subject and the lens through which all other concerns — love, society, morality, faith — are refracted. The novel's opening sentence is one of literature's most famous declarations of theme:

> All happy families are alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way. (009-001)

This statement, which appears to offer a universal truth, immediately launches the reader into a specific unhappy family — the Oblonskys, whose household is "in confusion" after Dolly discovers Stiva's affair. The novel then traces this theme through three central domestic worlds: the Oblonskys (unhappy but enduring), the Karenins (unhappy and destroyed), and the Levins (striving toward happiness through shared purpose).

Family in Tolstoy's vision is not merely a social institution but a moral proving ground. "The whole framework of their family life" (027-003) is what Dolly sees threatened by Stiva's infidelity — not merely her personal hurt but the collapse of a structure that gives meaning to daily existence.

## Instances in the Text

### The Oblonskys: Unhappy and Enduring

The Oblonsky marriage survives infidelity through Dolly's forbearance and Stiva's charm. Their family life, though hollowed out by betrayal, continues to function — children are raised, social obligations met. The relationship demonstrates Tolstoy's belief that "family life certainly afforded Stepan Arkadyevitch little gratification" (011-005) when pursued without moral seriousness.

### The Karenins: Unhappy and Destroyed

Anna's family is the novel's tragic center. The Karenin marriage, already emotionally empty before Vronsky appears, is broken by Anna's affair. But Tolstoy's innovation is to show that the breakage does not end the family — it transforms it into a site of ongoing suffering. Karenin's refusal to grant a divorce, Anna's separation from her son Seryozha, and the social ostracism that follows all constitute the continuing violence done to a family that legally still exists.

### The Levins: Striving Toward Happiness

Konstantin Levin begins the novel "in love with the idea of family life" (014-002). His courtship of Kitty, their marriage, and their life on his country estate represent Tolstoy's ideal of family as a source of meaning through shared work, mutual devotion, and the raising of children. Levin's joy at the birth of his son is the novel's culminating affirmation:

> He pictured to himself first the family, and only secondarily the woman who would give him a family (035-003).

## Development Across the Narrative

The novel's treatment of family follows a dialectical pattern. The opening establishes the Oblonsky household as a model of dysfunction held together by inertia. The Karenin marriage is then destroyed by passion, demonstrating that love without family structure is destructive. Finally, the Levin marriage offers a synthesis: passionate love channeled into the disciplines of domestic life, work, and parenthood.

Anna's tragedy is that she cannot have both love and family. Society demands she choose — and her choice of Vronsky costs her her son, her social position, and ultimately her life. Levin's achievement is that he builds a family within which love can flourish, but his path requires the very things Anna lacks: a partner of shared values (Kitty), a meaningful occupation (his estate), and the social acceptance that the Karenin marriage never secured.

## Thematic Connections

Family intersects with every major theme in the novel:

- **[[Marriage-in-Anna-Karenina]]**: Family is the container within which marriage either thrives or decays
- **[[Society-and-Morality]]**: The social regulation of family life — divorce laws, inheritance, reputation — drives much of the plot
- **[[Love-and-Passion]]**: The tension between romantic passion and familial duty is the novel's core dramatic engine
- **[[Religion-in-Anna-Karenina]]**: Religious frameworks for understanding marriage and family (Karenin's eventual turn to faith, Levin's spiritual searching) inflect the family theme

## Key Passages

The novel's thesis statement:

> All happy families are alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way. (009-001)

Dolly's fear of losing the integral family structure:

> The whole framework of their family life. "But why should I not recognize it?" (027-003)

Levin's idealization of family as life's foundation:

> He had never been able to conceive of life without a family. (014-002)

The social cruelty Anna endures after breaking from her family is captured in Karenin's judgment:

> "No honor, no heart, no religion; a corrupt woman." (090-006)
