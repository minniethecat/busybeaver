## Identity & Role

**Marya Nikolaevna** is the common-law partner of [[Nikolai-Levin]], the brother of [[Konstantin-Levin]]. She is introduced in Part Three of the novel, where Levin meets her during a visit to his dying brother.

Marya Nikolaevna's background is humble and socially marginal. Nikolai describes her origins bluntly:

> "This," said Nikolay Levin, pointing with his thumb to her, "is the partner of my life, Marya Nikolaevna. I took her out of a bad house." (032-027)

Despite her past, Marya Nikolaevna is portrayed with notable sympathy by Tolstoy. She cares for Nikolai with genuine tenderness during his final illness, and her devotion complicates any simple moral judgment about her character.

## Biography / Arc

Marya Nikolaevna first appears when Levin visits his brother Nikolai in a provincial hotel. Levin is struck by the contrast between his brother's degraded circumstances and Marya's gentle presence. She moves quietly around the sickroom, attending to Nikolai's needs with uncomplaining patience.

When Levin is left alone with her, he attempts to understand her relationship with his brother. Her responses reveal simple, unpretentious devotion:

> Left alone with Marya Nikolaevna, Levin turned to her. "So you have been with my brother a long time?" he said to her. (033-028)

As Nikolai's health deteriorates, Marya Nikolaevna becomes increasingly anxious. She whispers her fears to Levin and Konstantin, her concern for Nikolai overriding any social awkwardness:

> "He is very ill, Nikolay Dmitrievitch," whispered Marya Nikolaevna, again going up to him. (033-038)

Later, in Part Five, she writes to Levin informing him of Nikolai's worsening condition (054-003), prompting Levin and [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]] to travel to see him. At Nikolai's deathbed, Marya Nikolaevna's grief is raw and genuine — Kitty observes her with compassion and recognizes the depth of her feeling.

Marya Nikolaevna occupies a liminal position in the novel's social world. She is neither a servant nor a wife, neither respectable nor outcast. Her relationship with Nikolai places her at the margins of the narrative, yet her presence illuminates Tolstoy's themes of moral judgment and human worth.

## Themes & Significance

- **Social marginality and moral worth**: Marya Nikolaevna's character challenges the equation of social respectability with moral goodness. Despite her "fallen" past, she displays more genuine love and selflessness than many of the novel's respectable characters.

- **Illness and care**: Her nursing of Nikolai during his decline represents one of the novel's most sustained portraits of caregiving. Tolstoy uses her devotion to explore what it means to love someone through suffering.

- **Redemption through love**: Marya Nikolaevna embodies a Tolstoyan ideal — the person of simple heart whose love transcends social stigma. Her name (Marya, evoking Mary Magdalene) reinforces this association.

## Key Passages

Marya Nikolaevna's most defining moments occur at Nikolai's bedside:

> Marya Nikolaevna smiled a sweet and timid smile. (033-061)

Her smile — sweet and timid — captures the essential gentleness that Tolstoy grants her, a quality that transcends her origins and makes her one of the novel's quietly affecting minor figures.

> "He is very ill, Nikolay Dmitrievitch!" said Marya Nikolaevna, stretching out her hand towards him. (033-059)
