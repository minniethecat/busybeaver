# Illness and Death in Anna Karenina

Illness and death punctuate *Anna Karenina* with the insistence of reality itself, breaking through the social performances and romantic obsessions that occupy most characters. Tolstoy uses illness to strip away pretense and death to force the fundamental questions that the living world evades. From the railway worker crushed at the Obiralovka station to Nikolay Levin's long decline, mortality provides the novel's deepest moral gravity.

## The Railway Death: Foreshadowing

The novel's first death occurs in its early chapters: a railway watchman is crushed by a train at the station where Anna first meets Vronsky. A bystander remarks, "What a horrible death!" [^PN-026-057], and the event is treated as a bad omen. This death foreshadows Anna's own suicide by train at the novel's end, creating a symbolic arc in which the railway — emblem of modernity, speed, and the artificial — becomes an agent of destruction. The railway death is meaningless, accidental, and impersonal, unlike the deaths that follow.

## Nikolay Levin's Decline

The death of Konstantin Levin's brother Nikolay is the novel's most sustained meditation on mortality. Nikolay, a radical intellectual who has wasted his talents and health, declines slowly from consumption. Levin's confrontation with his brother's approaching death forces him into a crisis of meaning.

"Death, the inevitable end of all, presented itself to him for the first time with irresistible force" [^PN-108-020]. Levin realizes he has been living as though death did not exist: "I had forgotten — death" [^PN-108-022]. The experience transforms him, making all his previous concerns — farming, his book, even his love for Kitty — seem trivial by comparison. Nikolay's deathbed scene, with its "dread of illness, the illnesses themselves" [^PN-084-007] that Dolly has learned to bear as a mother, presents physical decay without sentimentality.

## Kitty's Illness and Recovery

Kitty's illness after Vronsky's rejection is psychological in origin but physically real. The doctors at the German spa diagnose and treat her, but "all her illness and treatment struck her as a thing so absurd" [^PN-043-025] that she cannot take it seriously. Her recovery comes not from medicine but from confronting the vanity of her romantic ideals — a spiritual rather than physical healing.

The contrast between Kitty's recoverable illness and Nikolay's terminal decline structures the novel's treatment of mortality. Kitty's youth allows her to pass through illness into marriage and motherhood, while Nikolay's wasted life leaves him nothing to offer death but suffering.

## Anna's Death

Anna's suicide is the catastrophic culmination of the novel's meditation on death. Unlike Nikolay's gradual decline, Anna's death is sudden and self-willed. Her final moments — throwing herself under a train — recapitulate the railway worker's death from the novel's opening, closing the symbolic circle. Where Nikolay dies surrounded by family, Anna dies alone, her mind consumed by jealousy and despair.

The novel refuses to moralize Anna's suicide. The epigraph — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" — leaves judgment to God while the narrative simply records the terrible logic of Anna's destruction. Her death is both punishment and release, a moment of horror that the society that condemned her absorbs with the same callousness with which it received the railway worker's death.

## Levin's Existential Crisis

After Nikolay's death and Anna's suicide, Levin's own confrontation with mortality deepens into a full existential crisis. "He saw nothing but death or the advance towards death" [^PN-109-032] in everything around him. The question that had haunted him — "I'm afraid of death, awfully afraid of death" [^PN-033-068] — now dominates his consciousness, and he contemplates suicide.

Levin's resolution, achieved in the novel's final pages, is not philosophical but religious: a turn toward faith not as doctrine but as the simple capacity to live rightly in the face of death. His conclusion is that the meaning of life lies not in escaping death but in living with goodness toward others — a truth he discovers through the accumulated wisdom of the peasants who have always known it.
