# Matrona Philimonovna

**Matrona Philimonovna** is the nurse of the Oblonsky household in Moscow, a domestic servant of long standing whose stern, pockmarked face and unsentimental competence make her a memorable background presence in the novel's opening chapters. She is a figure of the old order of household service: long-serving, authoritative within her sphere, and wholly absorbed in the practical rhythms of child-rearing.

## Identity & Role

Matrona Philimonovna is the nurse (nyanya) of the Oblonsky children. Her full patronymic name — Matrona Philimonovna — carries a weight of formality unusual for a servant character, suggesting both her age and the respect she commands within the household. She is described as having a "stern, pockmarked face" that is "thrust in at the door" — a physical description that conveys both her intrusiveness into family life and her weathered, unglamorous reality (010-027).

## Biography

Matrona Philimonovna appears exclusively in the Oblonsky household scenes of the novel's earliest chapters, during the domestic crisis precipitated by Darya Alexandrovna's discovery of Stiva's infidelity. When Dolly resolves to leave the household, packing her things in a fury, Matrona Philimonovna — along with the English governess — manages to put several of Dolly's necessities back in place, quietly and without permission, acting on the household's behalf against its mistress's declared wishes (012-040). This small subversion — a servant undoing her mistress's packing — speaks to the nurse's sense of household order: she knows better than Dolly, in her view, what must stay.

Earlier, her advice had been offered and received with mixed feeling — "the satisfaction was embittered by Matrona Philimonovna's advice and the unsatisfactory state of things" (011-005), suggesting that she is not merely a silent servant but an active voice in household affairs, whether her counsel is welcome or not.

Her final appearance reinforces her role as a physical interloper in domestic drama: "she did not finish her thought, because Matrona Philimonovna put her head in at the door" (012-041). Throughout the novel's opening, the nurse's head at the door is a recurring image — a reminder that despite the emotional storms of the Oblonsky marriage, the practical work of the household continues, and Matrona Philimonovna is its embodiment.

## Relationships

- **Darya Alexandrovna (Dolly)**: The mistress of the household whom Matrona Philimonovna serves. Their relationship is professional but intimate — the nurse has been with the family long enough to offer advice and override Dolly's decisions when household order is at stake.
- **Stepan Arkadyevitch (Stiva)**: The master of the household. Matrona Philimonovna's loyalty is to the household as an institution, above either spouse individually.
- **The Oblonsky children**: Her primary charges, whom she has cared for across the years.

## Key Passages
