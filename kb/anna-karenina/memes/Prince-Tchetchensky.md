## Identity & Role

Prince Tchetchensky is a minor aristocrat who moves in the Petersburg club world that Stepan Arkadyevitch Oblonsky finds so alluring. He is introduced in a conversation between Levin and an old prince at the club, where he is identified as a "shlupik" — the club's own term for a man who has been coming so long that he has become, in effect, part of the furniture. The old prince explains the etymology: "'You know the game of rolling eggs: when one's rolled a long while it becomes a shlupik. So it is with us; one goes on coming and coming to the club, and ends by becoming a shlupik'" [205-006].

## Biography / Arc

### The Shlupik

Prince Tchetchensky's defining narrative moment is an anecdote told at his expense. "Only three years ago he was not a shlupik," the old prince relates, "and kept up his spirits and even used to call other people shlupiks." Then came the moment of reckoning. Tchetchensky arrived at the club and asked the porter — the famously witty Vassily — who was there:

> "'Come, Vassily, who's here? Any shlupiks here yet?' And he says, 'You're the third.' Yes, my dear boy, that he did!" [205-008]

The story is a miniature masterpiece of club-land social comedy: the man who once mocked the shlupiks has become one, and the servant's bon mot delivers the verdict with an authority no aristocrat can match. Levin's companion calls Tchetchensky "a well-known figure. No matter, though" [205-008] — the dismissive "no matter" suggesting that in this world, being a well-known figure and being a figure of fun are not far apart.

### The Two Families

Tchetchensky reappears in a more sobering context during one of Oblonsky's meditations on the superiority of Petersburg life over Moscow. Stiva, depressed by the accumulated burdens of family, debt, and official duties in Moscow, recalls with longing the "circle there in which he moved, where people lived — really lived — instead of vegetating as in Moscow" [217-002]. His exemplary case is Prince Tchetchensky:

> "Prince Tchetchensky had a wife and family, grown-up pages in the corps, ... and he had another illegitimate family of children also. Though the first family was very nice too, Prince Tchetchensky felt happier in his second family; and he used to take his eldest son with him to his second family, and told Stepan Arkadyevitch that he thought it good for his son, enlarging his ideas." [217-002]

The passage is layered with irony. Stiva presents Tchetchensky's double life as evidence of Petersburg's superior vitality — people there "really lived." But the reader, who has seen the devastation Stiva's own infidelities have wrought on Dolly, cannot share his admiration. Tchetchensky's belief that exposing his legitimate son to his illegitimate family "enlarges his ideas" is either monstrous or absurd, depending on how one reads it — and Tolstoy leaves the judgment to us.

The paragraph ends with a rhetorical question that cuts through Stiva's rationalizations: "What would have been said to that in Moscow?" [217-002]. The implied answer — that Moscow would condemn what Petersburg celebrates — is not necessarily Tolstoy's own conclusion, but the juxtaposition of Tchetchensky's unconventional domestic arrangements with Stiva's earlier meditations on his wife's "ill-humor and reproaches" [217-002] makes clear where the novelist's sympathies lie.

## Relationships

### With the Club World

Tchetchensky is defined by his relationship to the club — he is, in the most literal sense, a man who belongs there. The porter's joke — "'You're the third'" [205-008] — is both a humiliation and an identity. Tchetchensky is the shlupik, and there is no evidence in the text that he resists the designation.

### With His Families

Tchetchensky maintains two households: a legitimate family (wife, children, "grown-up pages in the corps") and an illegitimate one. The narrator notes, with characteristic dryness, that "though the first family was very nice too, Prince Tchetchensky felt happier in his second family" [217-002]. The "too" does a great deal of work — acknowledging that the first family is "nice," as though niceness were the only quality at stake, while registering that happiness, for Tchetchensky, lies elsewhere.

## Themes & Significance

Prince Tchetchensky is a walk-on character who serves two functions. At the club, he is comic relief — the shlupik whose own joke has been turned against him. In Stiva's reflections, he is something more troubling: an example of the moral casualness that Petersburg high society not only permits but celebrates. His two families, his son's "enlarged ideas," his greater happiness in the illegitimate household — all of this is presented by Stiva as evidence of Petersburg's superior vitality, and by the novel as evidence of the moral vacuum at the heart of that vitality.

Tchetchensky never speaks in his own voice. He exists entirely through the reports of others — the old prince's anecdote, Stiva's reflection — and this mediated existence is itself thematically significant. He is a character made of other people's stories, and what those stories reveal is less about Tchetchensky than about the tellers: the old prince's club-land snobbery, Stiva's desperate self-justification.

## Key Passages

> "'Come, Vassily, who's here? Any shlupiks here yet?' And he says, 'You're the third.'" [205-008]

The porter's bon mot — a servant's wit delivering a truth no aristocrat would speak aloud.

> "Prince Tchetchensky had a wife and family, grown-up pages in the corps, ... and he had another illegitimate family of children also." [217-002]

The double life, presented as unremarkable fact.

> "He used to take his eldest son with him to his second family, and told Stepan Arkadyevitch that he thought it good for his son, enlarging his ideas." [217-002]

A father's rationalization that is either enlightened or appalling — the novel refuses to adjudicate.
