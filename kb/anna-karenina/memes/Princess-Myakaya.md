## Identity & Role

**Princess Myakaya** — nicknamed "Bibish" by intimates — is one of the most distinctive voices in the Petersburg social world. She is introduced at [[Princess-Betsy-Tverskaya]]'s salon:

> This was Princess Myakaya, noted for her simplicity and the roughness of her manner, and nicknamed *l'enfant terrible*. (048-005)

Her signature quality is her refusal to observe the polite fictions that govern society conversation. She says what everyone else is thinking but would never voice, and in doing so, she periodically shatters the careful veneer of Petersburg drawing-room discourse:

> The sensation produced by Princess Myakaya's speeches was always unique, and the secret of the sensation lay in the fact that she said simple things that everyone thought but no one said. (048-028)

She wears an old silk dress amid the finery of Princess Betsy's salon — an emblem of her indifference to social performance.

## Biography / Arc

Princess Myakaya makes her first significant appearance in Part Two, at Princess Betsy's salon, where the conversation turns to [[Anna-Karenina]] and [[Alexei-Karenin]]. While others speak in careful circumlocutions, Princess Myakaya cuts through:

> "Bad luck to your tongue!" said Princess Myakaya suddenly. "Madame Karenina's a splendid woman. I don't like her husband, but I like her very much." (048-037)

When someone suggests Anna's situation is complicated, she dismisses the evasion:

> "If our husbands didn't talk, we should see things as they are," said Princess Myakaya. (048-039)

Her intervention changes the temperature of the room — everyone looks at her, then at each other — and the conversation shifts decisively.

In Part Three, she is invoked as a benchmark of outspokenness. When someone at the country estate makes a daring remark, the response is:

> "You're encroaching on Princess Myakaya's special domain now." (094-031)

This offhand reference confirms that her role as society's truth-teller is widely recognized.

In Part Four, she weighs in on a charitable question with characteristic independence of mind, disagreeing with Princess Betsy on the matter of posting-horses for the wounded (115-004).

In Part Seven and Eight, Princess Myakaya reappears in the drawing rooms of Moscow and Petersburg. At one gathering, her arrival provides welcome relief:

> Stepan Arkadyevitch was visibly relieved at the arrival of Princess Myakaya, which cut short their tête-à-tête. (217-021)

Her gleeful exclamation — "they're going to be married!" (217-026) — shows her undiminished appetite for the romantic dramas of her acquaintance.

## Themes & Significance

- **The truth-teller in a world of performance**: Princess Myakaya performs the essential social function of puncturing pretense. In a society where everyone is performing, her refusal to perform is itself a kind of performance — but one that serves a genuine moral purpose.

- **Age, gender, and license**: Her advanced age grants her a license to speak freely that younger women — and most men — do not possess. She illustrates how social constraints are calibrated differently across the life cycle.

- **Simplicity as moral clarity**: Her "simplicity and the roughness of her manner" are presented not as defects but as virtues — a form of honesty that the novel implicitly endorses against the elaborate deceptions of Petersburg society.

## Key Passages

Princess Myakaya's finest moment is her defense of Anna:

> "Bad luck to your tongue!" said Princess Myakaya suddenly. "Madame Karenina's a splendid woman. I don't like her husband, but I like her very much." (048-037)

This single speech, delivered into a room full of people who are thinking uncharitable thoughts about Anna but expressing them in elegant paraphrase, is an act of moral courage disguised as rudeness.

Her philosophy of clarity is encapsulated in another remark from the same scene:

> "If our husbands didn't talk, we should see things as they are." (048-039)
