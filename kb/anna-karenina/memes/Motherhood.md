## Definition & Scope

**Motherhood** is one of the novel's central thematic axes, explored not as an abstract ideal but through the embodied, often exhausting experience of three principal women: [[Darya-Alexandrovna]] (Dolly), [[Anna-Karenina]], and [[Kitty-Shcherbatskaya]]. Tolstoy uses their divergent maternal paths to construct an implicit argument about feminine virtue, in which Dolly's self-denying devotion to her children serves as the norm against which Anna's ambivalent motherhood is measured — and found wanting.

## Three Maternal Archetypes

### Dolly: The Self-Sacrificing Mother

Dolly Oblonsky is the novel's fullest portrait of maternal love in practice. Her life "had been nothing but one long pregnancy, nursing, and sickness" (Part Six) — a statement that captures both the physical cost and the narrative invisibility of her labor. She is mother to six children, managing their illnesses, their lessons, their clothes, and their moral formation while her husband pursues pleasure.

The narrator does not romanticize this: Dolly is "thin, nervous, exhausted." Her body bears the marks of serial childbearing in a way that contrasts sharply with Anna's still-youthful beauty. Yet she is not pitiable. When she visits Anna at Vozdvizhenskoe, the narrator uses her perspective to anatomize the hollowness of Anna's performance: Anna's child by Vronsky is tended by a nurse, and Anna's maternal feeling for her daughter is presented as cosmetic, a posture rather than a practice.

### Anna: The Divided Mother

Anna's motherhood is defined by division — between her legitimate son [[Seryozha-Karenin]] and her illegitimate daughter Annie, between her maternal love and her romantic passion, between the mother she wants to be and the mother society permits her to be. The novel's most wrenching maternal scene is Anna's secret visit to Seryozha on his birthday (Part Five, Chapter 29–33): she arrives before dawn, bribes the servants, and holds her sleeping son with a desperation that reads as both love and theft.

After this visit, Anna effectively loses her claim to Seryozha. [[Lydia-Ivanovna]] and [[Alexei-Karenin]] conspire to tell the boy his mother is dead — a spiritual death sentence that the narrative suggests Anna has earned through her choices. The daughter, Annie, receives little maternal attention from Anna, who is consumed by her deteriorating relationship with Vronsky. The novel's moral arithmetic is severe: Anna chose passion over motherhood, and the consequence is the loss of both.

### Kitty: The Idealized New Mother

Kitty's entry into motherhood at the novel's end represents the narrative's resolution of the maternal theme. Her pregnancy and the birth of her son (Part Seven) are rendered with a mixture of physical realism and spiritual elevation. Levin's experience of the birth — his terror, his revulsion at the "animal" quality of the event, his sudden overwhelming love for the child — parallels his spiritual awakening.

Kitty's motherhood is presented as natural rather than performative: she nurses her own child, manages the household, and provides the emotional anchor that enables Levin's philosophical struggles. She is Dolly's successor, fulfilling the maternal ideal without the bitterness that Stiva's infidelity has imposed on Dolly.

## The Moral Argument

Tolstoy's treatment of motherhood is inseparable from his broader critique of the "woman question" — the 19th-century debate about women's roles in society. The novel consistently associates authentic motherhood with self-denial, presence, and physical intimacy with children. Anna's tragedy is not simply that she had an affair but that she chose romantic passion over the daily, unglamorous labor of being a mother.

This argument has been contested by feminist critics, who note that Tolstoy stacks the deck: Dolly has no choice but to endure, Anna's passion is punished, and Kitty's happy motherhood depends on her having married a man wealthy enough to afford it. The novel's treatment of motherhood thus functions simultaneously as a moral position and as a social document — a record of what 19th-century Russian society demanded of women and how it punished those who refused.

> "All her fifteen years of married life had been nothing but one long pregnancy, nursing, and sickness." — Dolly's reflection on the carriage ride to Vozdvizhenskoe (Part Six)
