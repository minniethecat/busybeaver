# Childhood and Upbringing in Anna Karenina

Children are a central presence in *Anna Karenina*, functioning both as objects of parental love and as moral stakes in the novel's conflicts over marriage, fidelity, and responsibility. Tolstoy portrays childhood from multiple angles — the practical labor of child-rearing, the emotional bond between parent and child, and the ways in which children become arguments in adult disputes.

## Dolly's Children: The Labor of Motherhood

Darya Alexandrovna Oblonskaya is the novel's primary locus of childhood experience. Mother of five living and two dead children [^PN-010-001], Dolly's life is defined by the unrelenting demands of child-rearing. The novel opens with the household in chaos because "the children ran wild all over the house" [^PN-009-002] during the three days of marital crisis. The children are not decorative; they are disruptive, exhausting, and ceaselessly present.

Dolly's practical questions — "What were the children to put on for their walk?" [^PN-012-040] — form a counterpoint to the high drama of Anna's passion and Levin's philosophical searching. Tolstoy refuses to sentimentalize the work of raising children, instead rendering it as the novel's most honest form of labor. When Dolly thinks "there are the children, I am tied" [^PN-027-023], the statement is both a complaint and a commitment.

## The Moral Argument of Children

In the domestic confrontation between Stiva and Dolly, children become a moral weapon. Dolly deploys them against her husband's infidelity: "For mercy's sake, think of the children; they are not to blame!" [^PN-012-017]. Stiva's retort — "You remember the children, Stiva, to play with them; but I remember them, and know they are ruined" [^PN-012-019] — exposes the asymmetry of parental investment that the novel treats as a structural feature of marriage.

Matvey, the old servant, invokes the children when urging Stiva toward reconciliation: "You must have pity, sir, on the children. Beg her forgiveness, sir" [^PN-010-031]. The children serve as the unanswerable argument — they cannot be dismissed, cannot be reasoned away, and their welfare trumps adult grievances.

## Anna and Seryozha: The Lost Child

Anna's relationship with her son Seryozha is the novel's most painful treatment of childhood. Her love for him is genuine and fierce, yet it cannot compete with the force of her passion for Vronsky. When Anna visits Seryozha secretly after Karenin has forbidden contact, the scene is among the most devastating in the novel — a mother reduced to a fugitive in her own child's life.

Seryozha represents what Anna has sacrificed, and his absence from her daily existence hollows out her remaining joys. The child she bears with Vronsky, Annie, receives notably less narrative attention than Seryozha — a silence that registers Anna's diminished maternal capacity. The novel never suggests that Anna loves her children less; rather, it shows that her circumstances have made maternal love impossible to sustain.

## Upbringing and Social Reproduction

The novel also treats childhood as a matter of social reproduction. The parents' discussion of "arranging their children's future" [^PN-020-006] reveals the extent to which upbringing is shaped by class expectations. Marriage, education, inheritance — these are the coordinates within which aristocratic children's lives are plotted.

Levin and Kitty's approach to child-rearing, glimpsed in the novel's final chapters, suggests an alternative: a domestic life in which children are integrated into the rhythms of work and nature rather than managed at a distance. Levin's joy in fatherhood is inseparable from his broader reconciliation with life's meaning — the child is not a burden or an ornament but a reason for living.
