## Identity & Role

Marya Philimonovna — referred to as Matrona Philimonovna in the novel's opening chapters — is the Oblonsky family's nurse and housekeeper. She first appears as "the stern, pockmarked face of Matrona Philimonovna, the nurse" thrust in at the doorway [010-027], her voice "firm, pleasant" [010-027] — the voice of domestic authority, unimpressed by the dramas of her employers.

The narrator introduces her in full in Part Three with one of the novel's most striking thumbnail portraits: "in the Oblonskys' household, as in all families indeed, there was one inconspicuous but most valuable and useful person, Marya Philimonovna" [084-006]. She is the Tolstoyan ideal of practical competence — the person who, without fuss or self-importance, makes things work.

## Biography / Arc

### The Moscow Household

In the novel's opening, Matrona Philimonovna is part of the Oblonsky household in Moscow. She appears during the crisis over Stiva's infidelity, her advice — presumably practical and unsentimental — embittering Stiva's morning: "that satisfaction was embittered by Matrona Philimonovna's advice and the unsatisfactory state of the household" [011-005]. While Dolly is consumed by grief, Philimonovna and the English governess besiege her with questions that "did not admit of delay, and which only she could answer: 'What were the children to put on for their walk? Should they have any milk? Should not a new cook be sent for?'" [012-040]. The unrelenting demands of the household, mediated through Philimonovna, are both a burden and, implicitly, a lifeline — the ordinary rhythms that persist through catastrophe.

She interrupts Dolly's despair at a crucial moment: "she did not finish her thought, because Matrona Philimonovna put her head in at the door" [012-041]. The interruption is characteristic — Philimonovna's presence is a continual reminder that life, with its milk and walks and cooks, must go on.

### The Country House at Ergushovo

Philimonovna's finest hour comes when Dolly moves to the country estate at Ergushovo. The position is "hopeless" [084-006] — the house in disrepair, no servants, no supplies. But Philimonovna transforms chaos into order through a combination of social diplomacy and practical ingenuity.

She establishes what the narrator calls her "club" under the acacias — "consisting of the bailiff's wife, the village elder, and the counting-house clerk" [084-006] — and through this informal network, "the difficulties of existence were gradually smoothed away." In a single week, she orchestrates a minor miracle:

> "The roof was mended, a kitchen maid was found — a crony of the village elder's — hens were bought, the cows began giving milk, the garden hedge was stopped up with stakes, the carpenter made a mangle, hooks were put in the cupboards, and they ceased to burst open spontaneously, and an ironing-board covered with army cloth was placed across from the arm of a chair to the chest of drawers, and there was a smell of flatirons in the maids' room." [084-006]

The cumulative detail of this passage — the mangle, the hooks, the ironing-board — is Tolstoy at his most domestic and most celebratory. Philimonovna's achievement is to make the house habitable, and in doing so, to make Dolly's life bearable.

When the work is done, Philimonovna points to the ironing-board with quiet triumph: "'Just see, now, and you were quite in despair'" [084-007]. The gentle reproach of an old nurse to a former charge contains a lifetime of shared history.

### Dressing the Children for Church

Philimonovna's practical wisdom extends to every corner of domestic life. When the English governess ruins Tanya's dress — "it was so narrow on Tanya's shoulders that it was quite painful to look at her" — it is Philimonovna who saves the day: "Marya Philimonovna had the happy thought of putting in gussets, and adding a little shoulder-cape" [085-003]. When the carriage horse needs replacing, it is "thanks to the representations of Marya Philimonovna" that the bailiff's horse Brownie is harnessed instead of the restive Raven [085-004].

At the bathing-shed, when peasant women approach shyly, Philimonovna handles the social moment with characteristic ease — "called one of them and handed her a sheet and a shirt that had dropped into the water for her to dry them" [085-017] — turning potential awkwardness into neighborly exchange.

### Matchmaker's Instinct

When Levin visits Dolly at Ergushovo, Philimonovna's attention is immediately drawn to the romantic possibilities. Dolly introduces her: "'thanks to my old nurse,' she said, indicating Marya Philimonovna, who, seeing that they were speaking of her, smiled brightly and cordially to Levin. She knew him, and knew that he would be a good match for her young lady, and was very keen to see the matter settled" [086-010]. The old nurse's investment in Kitty's future, and her instant recognition of Levin as a suitable husband, reveal the depth of her emotional connection to the family she serves.

### Versus Theory

Philimonovna's worldview is, finally, a rebuke to abstract thinking. When Levin expounds his "general principles" about cows as "machines for the production of milk," Dolly remains skeptical:

> "It all seemed to her a far simpler matter: all that was needed, as Marya Philimonovna had explained, was to give Brindle and Whitebreast more food and drink, and not to let the cook carry all the kitchen slops to the laundry maid's cow. That was clear." [086-024]

In this small moment, Philimonovna's practical knowledge — specific, tested, embodied in named cows — triumphs over Levin's theories. It is a miniature of one of the novel's deepest convictions: that wisdom resides in practice, not in principle.

## Relationships

### With Dolly

Philimonovna is Dolly's "old nurse" [086-010] — a relationship that goes back to Dolly's own childhood. The bond is intimate and maternal; Philimonovna soothes, advises, and manages, filling the emotional vacuum left by Stiva's neglect. When she says "'you were quite in despair'" [084-007], she speaks with the privileged candor of someone who has known Dolly since she was a child.

### With the Household Staff

Philimonovna's "club" under the acacias [084-006] reveals her method: she builds networks, shares tea, and through informal relationships gets things done. She is a manager in the deepest sense — not through hierarchy but through connection, not through orders but through persuasion.

### With Levin

Philimonovna's "bright and cordial" smile at Levin [086-010] and her quick assessment of him as "a good match for her young lady" show her perspicacity in matters of the heart. She is, in this moment, aligned with the reader — we, too, know Levin is right for Kitty — and her approval functions as a kind of narrative endorsement.

## Themes & Significance

Marya Philimonovna belongs to a lineage of Tolstoyan servants — like Agafea Mihalovna at Levin's estate, like Korney at Karenin's — who embody practical wisdom and quiet competence. But Philimonovna has a particular warmth that sets her apart. Her "club" under the acacias, her gussets and shoulder-capes, her named cows and ironing-boards — all of it constitutes a philosophy of life that the novel endorses without ever stating explicitly: that the work of making a home is sacred, that competence is a form of love, and that the "inconspicuous" person who makes everything run is, in her way, as worthy of our attention as any countess or prince.

The name shift from Matrona to Marya across the novel may reflect Tolstoy's shifting conception of the character — from the stern, almost forbidding nurse of the opening to the warm, resourceful housekeeper of the country chapters — or it may simply be a variant in the narrative's address. Either way, the shift mirrors Philimonovna's own transformation in the reader's perception: from background figure to indispensable presence.

## Key Passages

> "There was one inconspicuous but most valuable and useful person, Marya Philimonovna." [084-006]

The narrator's introduction — a sentence that could serve as Tolstoy's epitaph for all the competent, invisible people who make the world run.

> "She soothed her mistress, assured her that everything would come round (it was her expression, and Matvey had borrowed it from her), and without fuss or hurry proceeded to set to work herself." [084-006]

Philimonovna's method in miniature: comfort, reassurance, action.

> "'Just see, now, and you were quite in despair,' said Marya Philimonovna, pointing to the ironing-board." [084-007]

The old nurse's quiet triumph — a lifetime of competence compressed into a gesture.

> "All that was needed, as Marya Philimonovna had explained, was to give Brindle and Whitebreast more food and drink, and not to let the cook carry all the kitchen slops to the laundry maid's cow." [086-024]

Philimonovna's philosophy of farming — specific, practical, and, Dolly is convinced, more useful than Levin's theories.
