# Jules Landau (Count Bezzubov)

## Identity & Role

Jules Landau — "le fameux Jules Landau, le clairvoyant" [217-028] — is a French medium who has insinuated himself into the highest circles of Petersburg society through his spiritualist pretensions. He now goes by the title Count Bezzubov, having been adopted, as it were, by the wealthy Countess [[Lydia-Ivanovna]] and her circle. Stiva Oblonsky learns about him with incredulity: "He's not Landau any more now, but Count Bezzubov" [217-030].

Landau functions in the novel as a satirical embodiment of the faux-spirituality that Tolstoy associated with Petersburg high society. He is a con man in all but name — or perhaps in exactly that name — using an aura of mystical authority to gain influence over wealthy, susceptible patrons. His presence in the narrative sharpens the novel's critique of religious hypocrisy and the spiritual emptiness that the fashionable turn to mysticism attempts to fill.

## Biography / Arc

Landau first enters the narrative by report, when Stiva Oblonsky learns that "Lidia Ivanovna and Landau" [217-024] are the influences behind [[Alexei-Karenin]]'s resistance to granting Anna a divorce. Stiva's friend warns him: "they're going to ask Landau what he's to say" [217-026] — meaning that Karenin's decisions are being guided by a medium's pronouncements. Stiva's response is characteristic: "Ask Landau? What for? Who or what's Landau?" [217-027] The worldly Oblonsky, who navigates society with instinctive ease, cannot fathom how a French charlatan has acquired such influence.

When Stiva encounters Landau in person, the scene is treated with Tolstoy's sharpest comic irony. The medium is at [[Lydia-Ivanovna]]'s home, and the countess addresses him with theatrical reverence: "Monsieur Landau!" [218-006]. Landau responds with studied affectation, "looked round hurriedly, came closer, and smiled" [218-007]. The countess explains her introduction "in a soft voice" [218-009], the hushed tone itself a marker of the performance.

The centerpiece of Landau's appearance is a mock-spiritual session in which he places his hands on people — or rather, has hands placed upon him. When Karenin approaches, the countess declares that "his heart is changed, a new heart has been given him" [218-018]. Landau sits "at the window, leaning his elbow on the back of the chair" [218-030], maintaining the pose of spiritual detachment while in fact observing everything.

Later, Landau approaches the group and fixes his "eyes" upon Stiva [219-001], a gaze that Stiva finds unnerving. But Stiva, ever the practical man, recovers his equanimity quickly. His internal commentary reduces the whole performance to absurdity: "Landau — Bezzubov — what's he Bezzubov for?" [219-002] — the meaningless title exposed as mere theatrical costume.

## Relationships

- **[[Lydia-Ivanovna]]**: Landau's principal patron and enabler. She introduces him as a spiritual authority, speaks of him in hushed tones, and is the engine behind his social acceptance. Their relationship is one of mutual exploitation — he gains access and influence, she gains spiritual validation.
- **[[Alexei-Karenin]]**: Landau is deployed as a spiritual advisor to Karenin, reinforcing the latter's refusal to grant Anna a divorce. Karenin's susceptibility to Landau's influence reveals his own spiritual desperation.
- **[[Stepan-Arkadyevitch]]**: Stiva encounters Landau as an obstacle to the divorce negotiations and treats him with worldly contempt disguised as bewildered politeness. Stiva's incomprehension — "Who or what's Landau?" [217-027] — is the novel's own verdict on the medium.

## Themes & Significance

Landau is one of Tolstoy's most economical satirical creations — a character who appears in only a handful of pages yet represents an entire social pathology. He embodies the intersection of religious fraud, social climbing, and the spiritual emptiness of Petersburg high society. His transformation from "Jules Landau" to "Count Bezzubov" is a miniature of the broader theme of false identity and the substitution of appearance for reality.

The Landau episode also serves a structural function in the novel's treatment of spirituality. Against the genuine religious searching of [[Konstantin-Levin]] — his anguished questions about meaning, his encounter with the peasant Fyodor's simple faith — Tolstoy sets Landau's theatrical mysticism. The contrast is absolute: Levin's faith emerges from lived experience and honest doubt; Landau's "clairvoyance" is a performance purchased by the wealthy and credulous. The novel's spiritual argument is made, in part, through this juxtaposition of the genuine and the fraudulent.

## Key Passages

> "What! you don't know Jules Landau, le fameux Jules Landau, le clairvoyant?" [217-028]

The introduction of Landau by reputation — the French phrases marking both his foreignness and the fashionable pretension that surrounds him.

> "He's not Landau any more now, but Count Bezzubov." [217-030]

A single sentence that captures the absurdity of Landau's social ascent — the medium has shed his identity and acquired a title, the ultimate Petersburg metamorphosis.

> "His heart is changed, a new heart has been given him," said the countess, and she got up and crossed over to Landau. [218-018]

The performance of spiritual transformation, complete with the countess's theatrical crossing of the room — piety as social theater.
