# Gender Roles in Anna Karenina

Gender in Anna Karenina is a system of asymmetrical constraint. Tolstoy renders with extraordinary precision the different worlds that men and women inhabit in nineteenth-century Russian aristocratic society: the boundaries they cannot cross, the freedoms they take for granted, and the punishments meted out for transgressions that are differently defined depending on who commits them. The novel does not explicitly argue for reform — it simply shows, with devastating clarity, what it costs to be a woman in a world designed by and for men.

## The Marriage Market

For aristocratic women, marriage is simultaneously destiny and commodity. Young women like Kitty Shcherbatskaya are raised for one purpose: to make a suitable match. The entire Moscow social season — the balls, the calls, the calculated invitations — is a machinery for converting eligible young women into married ones. Princess Shcherbatskaya "had herself been married thirty years ago, her aunt arranging the match" [^PN-020-006], and she now orchestrates her daughter's prospects with the same strategic calculation. A woman's value on this market is determined by beauty, family standing, and perceived purity; a man's by career prospects, wealth, and title.

Kitty's trajectory illustrates the emotional brutality of this system. Her rejection of Levin in favor of Vronsky, followed by Vronsky's abandonment of her for Anna, leaves her ill and humiliated. She has no agency except the right of refusal — and even that is circumscribed by parental expectations and the ticking clock of eligibility.

## The Double Standard

The asymmetry of sexual morality is the novel's most damning structural argument. Vronsky's affair with Anna, far from damaging his reputation, confirms his status as a desirable man. His friend Petritsky's various scandals — involving government clerks, debts, and women — are treated as amusing anecdotes within the regiment. The colonel tells Vronsky, "Petritsky's becoming impossible. Not a week goes by without some scandal" [^PN-047-022], but the consequences are minimal: a fine to be paid, an apology to be made, and life continues unchanged.

For Anna, the same behavior is catastrophic. After her affair becomes known, she is excluded from the society that formerly celebrated her. The theater scene, in which she is publicly humiliated while Vronsky moves freely, crystallizes the double standard. Even Karenin, who demands external propriety from his wife, operates in a world where "many look favorably on a man's infidelity while condemning the woman" [^PN-090-012].

## Motherhood as Identity and Constraint

Motherhood is the defining female experience in the novel, and Tolstoy portrays it in all its complexity. For Dolly Oblonskaya, motherhood is both joy and exhaustion — a relentless cycle of pregnancies, childhood illnesses, and domestic management that consumes her identity. When she visits Anna at Vronsky's estate, she sees a woman who has opted out of the maternal burden: Anna has left her son Seryozha with Karenin and cares only for her daughter by Vronsky. Dolly's response is a mixture of envy and moral horror.

For Anna, motherhood is the wound that will not heal. Her separation from Seryozha is the price of her freedom, and the novel makes clear that this price is exorbitant. When she visits Seryozha in secret on his birthday, the scene is almost unbearable in its tenderness and grief. The society that condemns Anna for leaving her husband permits him to keep her child — a punishment administered through the institutions of the law.

## Men's Freedom and Its Limits

Tolstoy does not present masculinity as uncomplicated privilege. Levin's agonized search for meaning, his social awkwardness, and his fear of death are all, in part, consequences of a masculinity that demands self-sufficiency and public confidence. Vronsky's inability to fully love Anna — his constant need to reassert his independence through social and military activity — is the tragic flaw of a man whose identity is constituted outside the domestic sphere. Even Stiva's cheerful hedonism masks a deeper emptiness: his financial irresponsibility and casual infidelity are the habits of a man who has never been required to grow up.

## The Peasant Alternative

Against the artificial constraints of aristocratic gender roles, Tolstoy holds up the peasants as an implied contrast. The peasant women at Levin's estate work alongside men in the fields; their marriages, however unromantic, are partnerships of necessity rather than performances of status. Levin's idealization of peasant life is partly a critique of aristocratic gender ideology — a suggestion that the "natural" relations between men and women have been distorted by class.
