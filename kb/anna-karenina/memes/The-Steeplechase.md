## Overview

The steeplechase takes place at the Krasnoe Selo officers' races, an event of the Petersburg social season. Alexei Vronsky, an accomplished cavalry officer, participates in the race on his mare Frou-Frou. Anna Karenina watches from the stands, her emotional investment in Vronsky's fate visible to all — including her husband, [[Alexei-Karenin]], who is also present.

The race occurs in Part 2 of the novel, at the height of Anna and Vronsky's courtship. The setting — a dangerous military competition observed by high society — concentrates the novel's themes of risk, display, and the collision between private passion and public scrutiny.

> That year races and a steeplechase had been arranged for the officers. (060-007)

The race features a series of obstacles including a water jump and ditches. "In a steeplechase it all depends on riding and on pluck" (063-012), a soldier tells the onlookers, establishing the event's ethos of courage and control.

## Participants

### Alexei Vronsky

Vronsky rides his own mare, **Frou-Frou**, a thoroughbred he has trained himself. His confidence before the race is absolute — he is an expert horseman, and the race represents his mastery of a world he understands. His fall is therefore both a professional humiliation and a symbolic collapse of masculine control.

### Anna Karenina

Anna watches from the grandstand, her emotions undisguised. When Vronsky falls, her reaction is public and unmistakable — she gasps, weeps, and cannot conceal her terror. This display of feeling before Petersburg society is a turning point in her social standing.

### Alexei Karenin

Karenin observes both the race and his wife's response to it. His presence in the stands with Anna creates a triangle of gazes: Anna watching Vronsky, Karenin watching Anna, society watching them all. Karenin's cold disapproval of Anna's emotional display precipitates their first confrontation about her relationship with Vronsky.

## Narrative Sequence

### Lead-Up

Vronsky's preparation for the race is meticulous. He has trained Frou-Frou personally, and the mare is in peak condition. His confidence is total. The morning of the race, he receives a note from Anna confirming her attendance, which heightens his determination.

### The Race

The steeplechase covers three miles with nine obstacles. Vronsky rides near the front of the field, his skill evident. At the water jump, he takes it cleanly. But at a crucial ditch — the most dangerous obstacle — he makes a fatal error. Coming down after the jump, he lands badly in the saddle, and:

> Vronsky made a blunder, one inexcusable in a rider: he dropped back in the saddle. (070-007)

The misplaced weight throws Frou-Frou off balance. The mare falls, her back broken. Vronsky is thrown but unhurt. Frou-Frou, thrashing on the ground, must be shot.

> When the three-mile steeplechase was beginning, she bent forward and did not take her eyes off Vronsky. (070-007)

### Aftermath

The immediate aftermath is Vronsky's humiliation and grief over Frou-Frou. But the deeper consequence is Anna's public display of emotion, which Karenin observes and which becomes the subject of Petersburg gossip. On the carriage ride home, Karenin confronts Anna about her behavior, and she confesses her love for Vronsky. The steeplechase, an event of aristocratic sport, has become the catalyst for the novel's central marital catastrophe.

## Consequences & Significance

### Foreshadowing

The steeplechase is one of literature's most famous examples of symbolic foreshadowing. Vronsky's blunder — his inexplicable, "inexcusable" error in the saddle — prefigures his inability to sustain his relationship with Anna. Just as he breaks Frou-Frou's back through a moment's carelessness, he will, over the course of the novel, destroy Anna through a combination of negligence, social pressure, and his own limitations. The beautiful thoroughbred mare shot in the mud is an image of what Anna will become: a magnificent creature destroyed by the man who loved her.

### Public vs. Private

The race forces the private drama of Anna, Vronsky, and Karenin into public view. Anna's visible terror at Vronsky's fall is the first breach in the facade of her marriage. Petersburg society, watching from the stands, sees what Karenin has avoided acknowledging. The steeplechase is the moment when the affair ceases to be a secret and becomes a scandal.

### The Masculine Ideal

Vronsky's identity is built on military prowess, physical courage, and self-control. His failure at the steeplechase cracks this masculine self-image, and he never fully recovers the assurance that defined him before the race.
