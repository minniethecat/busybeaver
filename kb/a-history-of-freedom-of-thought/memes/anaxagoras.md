## Life and Background

Anaxagoras (c. 500–428 BCE) was a Greek philosopher who came from Ionia to teach at Athens. He was part of the Ionian tradition of natural philosophy that sought to explain the world through rational principles rather than myth. In Athens, he became especially intimate with the statesman Pericles and was connected with the "subversive speculations of the day" (002-118).

## Major Contributions

Anaxagoras proposed that the cosmos was governed by *Nous* (Mind), an ordering principle that set the primordial mixture into motion. More provocatively, he taught that the heavenly bodies were physical objects rather than deities. The sun, to which "the ordinary Athenian said prayers morning and evening," was in Anaxagoras's view "a mass of flaming matter" (002-126).

This represented a direct challenge to popular Greek religion. In regard to the popular gods, Bury describes Anaxagoras as "a thorough-going unbeliever" (002-120). His naturalistic cosmology marked a key step in the Greek transition from mythological to rational modes of explanation.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Anaxagoras became one of the earliest recorded victims of religious persecution in Athens. The political enemies of Pericles, seeking to strike at the statesman, "attacked his friend" by introducing and carrying a "blasphemy law" under which "unbelievers and those who taught theories about the celestial world might be impeached" (002-122).

The prosecution was straightforward: "It was easy to prove that Anaxagoras was a blasphemer who taught that the gods were abstractions" (002-124). The influence of Pericles saved him from death; he was heavily fined and left Athens for Lampsacus (002-128).

Significantly, the suppression was incomplete. Bury notes that while copies of Protagoras's work were burned, "the book of Anaxagoras setting forth the views for which he had been condemned was for sale on the Athenian book-stalls at a popular price" (002-140). This inconsistency reveals that Athens lacked a systematic policy of suppressing free thought, even when individual thinkers were targeted.

## Legacy and Influence

Anaxagoras's case established an early precedent: the conflict between naturalistic philosophy and religious orthodoxy could lead to legal persecution, but the ideas themselves could not be easily suppressed. His book remained available, and his rationalist approach to cosmology influenced later Greek philosophers including Socrates and Aristotle.

Bury uses the Anaxagoras case to illustrate a broader point about the history of freethought: persecution was often political in motivation (striking at Pericles through his friend) rather than purely religious, and enforcement was frequently inconsistent. The fact that a condemned book could remain on sale demonstrated the limits of censorship even in an ostensibly pious society.
