# Baruch Spinoza

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677) was a Jewish philosopher of Holland whose system represented "a far wider and more open breach with orthodox opinion than either of his masters had ventured on" (006-076). His pantheistic conception of God as an impersonal substance, his rejection of free will and final causes, and his pioneering work in biblical criticism made him one of the most significant — and at the time, most reviled — philosophers of the early modern period.

## Life and Background

Spinoza was born in Amsterdam to a family of Portuguese-Jewish descent who had fled the Inquisition. Educated in the Jewish tradition, he also studied Latin and was deeply influenced by the philosophy of [[Descartes]]. In 1656, at the age of 23, he was excommunicated from the Amsterdam synagogue for his "abominable heresies" and "monstrous deeds."

After his excommunication, Spinoza lived a quiet, modest life in various Dutch towns, earning his living as a lens-grinder while developing his philosophical system. His masterwork, the *Ethics*, was published posthumously in 1677.

Spinoza was not the only philosopher to make a profound impact on the development of rationalism. His contemporary [[Bayle]] worked in the same direction by the investigation of history, while Spinoza approached the question through metaphysics (006-144).

## Major Contributions

Spinoza conceived ultimate reality, which he called God, as an absolutely perfect, impersonal Being — a substance whose nature is constituted by two "attributes," thought and spatial extension (006-082).

> When Spinoza speaks of love of God, in which he considered happiness to consist, he means knowledge and contemplation of the order of nature, including human nature, which is subject to fixed, invariable laws.
> (006-082–006-085)

His philosophy has often been described as atheism or pantheism. As Bury observes: "If atheism means, as I suppose in ordinary use it is generally taken to mean, rejection of a personal God, Spinoza was an atheist" (006-090). However, Bury adds the important nuance that in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, "atheist was used in the wildest way as a term of abuse for freethinkers" (006-091), and those so stigmatized were generally deists rather than true atheists.

Spinoza's *Theological Political Treatise* dealt with the principles of biblical criticism, arguing that Scripture must be interpreted like any other historical document. He laid down the principle that Scripture must be interpreted according to its own terms and historical context, rather than through the lens of dogmatic theology (006-210).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Spinoza's contribution to [[freedom of thought]] was profound but indirect. His biblical criticism undermined the authority of revealed religion, and his metaphysical system provided an alternative to the personal God of the Judeo-Christian tradition.

His philosophy exerted influence on the English deists, the French freethinkers, and later German idealists. Lessing and the German Romantic tradition, for instance, were "profoundly influenced by Spinoza" (006-898, 006-914). His ideas about the relationship between mind and matter anticipated modern monistic philosophies — indeed, later thinkers such as [[Ernst Haeckel]] explicitly identified their philosophy with Spinoza's (007-0220).

In the nineteenth century, rationalists looked back to Spinoza as a forerunner. Bury notes him alongside thinkers such as Simon as an example of early rationalist systematic philosophy (007-0302).

| Aspect | Spinoza's Position |
|--------|-------------------|
| Metaphysics | Pantheism — God = Nature, an impersonal substance |
| Free Will | Rejected — human actions follow invariable laws |
| Biblical Criticism | Scripture to be interpreted historically, not dogmatically |
| Ethics | Happiness = knowledge and contemplation of Nature's order |

## Legacy and Influence

"Spinoza's daring philosophy was not in harmony with the general trend of speculation at the time, and did not exert any profound influence on thought till a much later period" (006-096). His ideas only gained widespread traction in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, when the German Romantics and Idealists rediscovered his work and recognized him as a foundational figure in modern philosophy.

Later thinkers like [[Haeckel]] identified with Spinoza's monistic vision: "he identifies his philosophy with that of Spinoza" (007-0220). Spinoza's influence is indexed at pages 131 and following, 138, and 191 in Bury's history (999-189).
