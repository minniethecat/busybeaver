## Definition and Origins

Heresy, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes the holding of religious beliefs that deviate from established church doctrine. The term derives from the Greek *hairesis*, meaning "choice" or "school of thought," but in Christian Europe it acquired a profoundly negative connotation as the gravest spiritual crime. Bury documents how the charge of heresy became the primary legal mechanism for suppressing intellectual liberty from the late Roman Empire through the Enlightenment.

The systematic persecution of heresy began when Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire. Bury notes that the punishment of heresy by death was established early: the Church and State cooperated to punish the crime "of heresy by death" (003-062). This set a precedent that would shape European law for over a millennium.

## Historical Development

In the early centuries of Christianity, heresy was primarily a theological concern dealt with by church councils and excommunication. However, after the Emperor Theodosius established orthodox Christianity as the state religion, heresy became a civil crime. Bury records that "in the pursuit of heresy the Church was mainly guided by" the conviction that erroneous belief endangered not only the individual soul but the entire social order (003-080).

The medieval Inquisition was established specifically to combat heresy. Bury describes how "in order to root out heresy it was necessary to discover" those who held secret unorthodox beliefs, leading to the development of elaborate systems of surveillance, denunciation, and interrogation (003-110). Both ecclesiastical and secular authorities "inaugurated independently a merciless legislation against heresy" (003-125), creating a comprehensive legal apparatus for thought control.

During the Reformation, the persecution of heresy intensified as both Catholic and Protestant authorities sought to enforce doctrinal conformity. Bury documents how under English monarchs "Henry IV and Henry V the government repressed heresy by the stake" (003-149), demonstrating that the suppression of religious dissent was a bipartisan political project. The notorious case of Michael Servetus, burned at the stake in Calvin's Geneva, exemplifies Bury's account "of Calvin's warfare against heresy" (004-144).

The gradual decline of heresy prosecutions in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries marked a major advance for freedom of thought. Bury traces this development through the growth of toleration arguments, culminating in the legal recognition that religious belief could not be coerced.

## Key Proponents and Victims

- **Michael Servetus** was executed for heresy in Geneva (1553) after challenging the doctrine of the Trinity. His death became a rallying point for advocates of religious toleration (004-144).
- **Giordano Bruno** was burned at the stake in Rome (1600) for heretical cosmological and theological views, becoming a martyr for free thought.
- **Martin Luther**, though himself accused of heresy by the Catholic Church, established his own system of doctrinal enforcement that punished dissenters.
- **Sebastian Castellio** argued against the execution of heretics, insisting that religious differences should be resolved through persuasion rather than violence.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The concept of heresy was arguably the single greatest obstacle to freedom of thought in European history. By criminalizing religious disagreement, the heresy laws established the principle that the state had the right and duty to police belief. This set a precedent that extended beyond religion to philosophical and scientific questions whenever they touched on doctrinal matters.

Bury's narrative demonstrates that "hatred of heresy was a sort of infectious germ" that permeated all levels of society, from monarchs to common people (003-223). This social dimension meant that even when official persecution relaxed, the stigma of heresy continued to inhibit free inquiry and expression.

The eventual triumph over heresy prosecutions required both legal reform and a fundamental shift in attitudes. The arguments of toleration advocates gradually prevailed: that sincere belief cannot be compelled, that persecution corrupts religion itself, and that free inquiry ultimately strengthens rather than weakens truth. The abolition of heresy as a crime represents one of the most significant achievements in the history of intellectual liberty.
