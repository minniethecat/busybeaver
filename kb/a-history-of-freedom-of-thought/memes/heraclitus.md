## Life and Background

Heraclitus was born in Ephesus, an Ionian Greek city on the coast of Asia Minor, around 535 BCE. He came from an aristocratic family but lived a solitary, contemplative life. According to ancient sources, he deposited his philosophical work in the temple of Artemis at Ephesus. Only fragments of his writings survive, preserved in quotations by later authors.

## Major Contributions

Heraclitus belongs to the tradition of early Greek philosophers who, in Bury's account, laid the foundations of rationalism. These early thinkers — most of them materialists — began the long work of training reason to look upon the universe without recourse to myth or supernatural explanation (002-066, 002-067).

Bury selects Heraclitus and [[democritus]] as the two great names of this tradition, because they did more perhaps than any of the others, by sheer hard thinking, to train reason to look upon the universe in new ways and to shock the unreasoned conceptions of common sense (002-068, 002-069, 002-070, 002-071).

Heraclitus taught, for the first time, that the appearance of stability and permanence which material things present to our senses is a false appearance, and that the world and everything in it are changing every instant (002-072, 002-073, 002-074). This doctrine of universal flux would become one of the enduring insights of Western philosophy.

## Intellectual Context

The early Ionian philosophers — including [[thales]], [[anaximander]], and [[anaxagoras]] — created a tradition of explaining natural phenomena through impersonal causes rather than divine will. Heraclitus contributed the idea that change itself, rather than any static substance, is the fundamental reality. Unlike the mythological cosmogonies that preceded them, these thinkers offered accounts of the universe that could be debated, criticised, and refined — establishing the method of rational inquiry that Bury traces throughout his history.

In Bury's narrative, the early Greek philosophers are the first heroes of freedom of thought: no fantastic tales of creation, imposed by sacred authority, hampered these powerful brains (002-079). All this philosophical speculation prepared the way for the educationalists who were known as the [[sophists]] (002-080, 002-081).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Heraclitus is significant in the history of freedom of thought not for any political advocacy of liberty — that was still centuries away — but as an exemplar of the rationalist impulse itself. His willingness to challenge the evidence of the senses and the assumptions of common sense exemplifies the spirit of critical inquiry that is the intellectual precondition of all later freethought.

## Key Ideas

| Idea | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Universal Flux (*Panta Rhei*) | Everything is in constant change; stability and permanence are illusions |
| Unity of Opposites | Contradictory forces are in tension yet form a coherent whole |
| *Logos* | The rational principle ordering the cosmos, accessible to human reason |
