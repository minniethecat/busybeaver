## Life and Background

Denis Diderot was born in Langres, France, on 5 October 1713, the son of a master cutler. Educated by the Jesuits in Langres and later at the Collège d'Harcourt in Paris, he received a thorough classical and philosophical education. By the early 1740s, Diderot had abandoned his early intention of entering the clergy and was earning a precarious living as a writer and translator in Paris.

Diderot's first significant original work, the *Pensées philosophiques* (1746), argued for a rational deism and was condemned by the Parlement of Paris. His *Lettre sur les aveugles* (1749), which proposed a materialist account of cognition, resulted in his imprisonment at Vincennes for three months. This experience taught Diderot caution: much of his most radical work was circulated privately or published posthumously.

In 1747, Diderot became the chief editor of the *Encyclopédie, ou Dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers*, a project that would consume more than two decades of his life. Working with contributors including [[Voltaire]], [[Rousseau]], [[Montesquieu]], and [[Holbach]], Diderot transformed the *Encyclopédie* into far more than a reference work — it became the intellectual engine of the French Enlightenment. He died in Paris on 31 July 1784, having lived to see the *Encyclopédie* completed but not the Revolution it helped inspire.

## Major Contributions

All the leading ideas in the revolt against the Church had a place in Diderot's great work, the *Encyclopedia*, in which a number of leading thinkers collaborated with him (006-0591). It was not merely a scientific book of reference — it was representative of the whole movement of the enemies of faith (006-0593). The *Encyclopédie* was intended to lead men from Christianity, with its doctrine of original sin, to a new conception of the world as a place which can be made agreeable and in which the actual evils are due not to radical faults of human nature but to defective social organization (006-0595).

Diderot himself moved from deism to a thoroughgoing materialism. He was a friend of [[Holbach]], who had also come to reject deism, and together they represented the atheistic wing of the French Enlightenment (006-0589). Through the *Encyclopédie*, Diderot and his collaborators disseminated a comprehensive alternative to the Christian worldview — one grounded in reason, science, and faith in human improvement.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Diderot advanced freedom of thought through two channels: the content of the *Encyclopédie* and the ideal of social transformation it promoted. To teach men that man's felicity depends not on Revelation but on social transformation — this was what Diderot and [[Rousseau]] in their different ways did so much to effect (006-0601). Their work influenced even those who did not abandon orthodoxy; it affected the spirit of the Church itself.

Bury, quoting Lord [[Morley]], asked whether the Catholic Church in France would have reformed itself "without the work of Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, and their fellow-combatants" (006-0605). The question is rhetorical: the mere fact that the Church felt compelled to reform was testimony to the power of the ideas Diderot and his collaborators had set in motion.

Diderot's teaching that man's energies should be devoted to making the earth pleasant substituted a new ideal for the old ideal based on theological propositions (007-0953). This reorientation of human purpose — from preparing for the afterlife to improving this life — was one of the most consequential shifts in the intellectual history of the West. It inspired the English Utilitarian philosophers — [[Bentham]], James Mill, [[John Stuart Mill]], and [[Grote]] — who preached the greatest happiness of the greatest number (007-0956).

## Legacy and Influence

Diderot's *Encyclopédie* was a landmark in the history of publishing and the dissemination of knowledge. Its twenty-eight volumes (seventeen of text, eleven of plates, published 1751–1772) reached subscribers across Europe and were widely pirated in cheaper editions. The work brought advanced scientific and philosophical ideas within reach of the educated public, eroding the Church's monopoly on intellectual authority.

In the nineteenth century, Diderot was rediscovered and celebrated by rationalist historians. [[John Morley]] published his study *Diderot and the Encyclopaedists* in 1878, part of a sympathetic trilogy on the French freethinkers that also included studies of Voltaire (1872) and Rousseau (1873) (007-0850). Bury himself drew on Morley's work and recognized Diderot as one of the central figures of the Enlightenment's war against theological authority.
