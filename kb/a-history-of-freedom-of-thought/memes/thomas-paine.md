# Thomas Paine

**Thomas Paine** (1737–1809) was a British-born political writer and revolutionary who played a leading part in the [[American Revolution]] before returning to England to publish *Rights of Man* (1791–1792) (006-773). An ardent deist, Paine made the case against established religion accessible to ordinary people — and was prosecuted, outlawed, and vilified for doing so (005-377).

## Life and Background

A Norfolk man by birth, Paine migrated to America in 1774 and played a leading part in the Revolution (006-773). His pamphlet *Common Sense* (1776) made the case for American independence in plain language that reached a mass audience. Despite his eminent service in the War of Independence, he later received appalling ingratitude from Americans after publishing his religious views (005-648).

Returning to England, Paine published *Rights of Man* in 1791. At that period it was as dangerous to publish revolutionary opinions in politics as in theology (006-779). When a cheap edition made the book accessible to the poorer classes, the government decided to prosecute (006-785). Paine escaped to France, where he received a brilliant ovation at Calais (006-786).

## Major Works

Paine was an enthusiastic admirer of the American Constitution and a supporter of the French Revolution, in which he was awarded citizenship (006-779). His key works include:

| Work | Year | Impact |
|------|------|--------|
| *Common Sense* | 1776 | Catalyzed American independence |
| *Rights of Man* | 1791–1792 | Defended the French Revolution and natural rights |
| *The Age of Reason* | 1794–1796 | Deist critique of organized religion |

## Religious Views and Prosecution

Paine was an ardent deist, who regarded nature as God's revelation (006-835). Unlike earlier deists, Paine is the first to present with force the incongruity of the Christian scheme with the conception of a benevolent deity (006-821). He wrote:

> Were a bill brought into any parliament, entitled 'An Act to tolerate or grant liberty to the conscience of man,' it would be received as a mark of intolerance.
> (005-377)

Paine held that the people at large had the right of access to all new ideas, and he wrote so as to reach the people (006-860). This was precisely what made him dangerous to the authorities.

The trial was a landmark in the history of free expression. Erskine was Paine's counsel, and he made a fine oration in defence of freedom of speech (006-798). Nevertheless, Paine was found guilty and outlawed (006-813).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Paine's contribution to [[freedom of thought]] was twofold. First, he transformed the abstract arguments of Enlightenment philosophy into language ordinary people could understand and act upon. His protest that toleration is not the opposite of intolerance but its counterfeit — the one assuming the right of withholding liberty, the other of granting it (005-373) — reframed the debate in terms of natural rights rather than magisterial concession.

Second, he paid the price for his convictions. This was not the end of Paine prosecutions. In 1811 a Third Part of the *Age of Reason* appeared, and Eaton the publisher was condemned to eighteen months' imprisonment (006-865). If Paine felt the injustice of religious prosecution a hundred years ago, the principle he fought for — that belief should be free from state coercion — has become a cornerstone of modern liberty (007-0276).

## Legacy and Influence

Paine's writings reached audiences that [[Voltaire]] and [[Rousseau]] never touched. Several of his criticisms of Scripture extracted admissions from defenders that were calculated to damage the doctrine of biblical infallibility (006-848). His brand of popular deism, combined with democratic politics, influenced working-class radical movements throughout the nineteenth century.

Though vilified in his lifetime — speaking of Paine, it is borne out by the persistence of religious prejudice even in the modern world (007-0889) — his insistence that religious and political liberty are inseparable has been vindicated by history.
