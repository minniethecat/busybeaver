# Christopher Marlowe

**Christopher Marlowe** (1564–1593) was an Elizabethan poet and dramatist who was accused of atheism. His case is notable as an instance of religious persecution cut short by violence: he was killed in a sordid quarrel in a tavern before the prosecution against him could be concluded (004-267).

## Accusation of Atheism

Marlowe was a heretic of comparable stature to [[giordano-bruno|Giordano Bruno]]. He was formally accused of atheism, and the prosecution was pending over him when, in 1593, he was killed in a tavern brawl (004-267).

His death meant that the English authorities were deprived of the opportunity to make an example of him, and posterity was left to speculate on what his trial might have revealed about unorthodox opinion in Elizabethan England.

## Significance for Freedom of Thought

Marlowe's case illustrates the continued danger faced by freethinkers in the Reformation era. The charge of atheism remained a capital offence, as the contemporaneous execution of [[giordano-bruno|Bruno]] in Rome (1600) demonstrated (004-267).

The fact that a prominent literary figure could be accused of outright atheism — rather than mere heresy — testifies to the penetration of radical ideas into Elizabethan intellectual culture, even if such ideas could not yet be openly avowed (004-267).
