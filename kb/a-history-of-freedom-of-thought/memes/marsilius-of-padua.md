## Life and Background

Marsilius of Padua (Marsiglio dei Mainardini) was born in Padua, Italy, around 1275. He studied medicine and philosophy at the University of Paris, where he later served as rector. His most famous work, *Defensor Pacis* (The Defender of Peace, 1324), was a sustained argument against papal temporal power. When his authorship became known, he was condemned as a heretic and fled to the court of Louis IV, the Holy Roman Emperor.

## Major Contributions

In the history of religious toleration, Marsilius is significant for having considered the question of coercion from the standpoint of law and the legal relations between State and Church (005-508, 005-509, 005-510). He maintained that the Church had no power to employ physical coercion, and that if the lay authority punished heretics, the punishment was inflicted for the violation not of divine ordinances but of the law of the State, which excluded heretics from its territory (005-511, 005-512, 005-513, 005-514, 005-515).

This argument was revolutionary for its time, because it deprived ecclesiastical persecution of its theological justification. By relocating the punishment of heresy from the spiritual to the civil sphere, Marsilius opened the door to the eventual recognition that the State, too, might have no legitimate interest in coercing religious belief.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Marsilius' separation of spiritual authority from coercive power was an early forerunner of the jurisdictional theory of toleration — the idea that religious liberty can be secured through a proper understanding of the legal relationship between Church and State. This line of argument would later be taken up by [[christian-thomasius]], who laid down in a series of pamphlets (1693–1697) that the prince alone has the power of coercion and has no right to interfere in spiritual matters (005-516, 005-517, 005-518, 005-519).

The Marsilian approach is one of the two principal intellectual routes to toleration identified by Bury — the jurisdictional or legal route, as distinct from the theological route taken by the [[socinianism|Socinians]] and other dissenting sects. It would eventually feed into the broader Enlightenment case for religious liberty and the separation of Church and State.
