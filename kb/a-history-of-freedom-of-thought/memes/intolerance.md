## Definition and Origins

Religious intolerance is the systematic refusal to permit the holding, expression, or practice of religious beliefs that deviate from an established orthodoxy. In Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, intolerance is not merely a moral failing but the central institutional mechanism by which freedom of thought was suppressed for over fifteen centuries of European history.

The origins of religious intolerance in the West lie in the transformation of Christianity from a persecuted minority sect into the official religion of the Roman Empire. Once the Church acquired the power of the state, it deployed that power to suppress dissent. As Bury documents, "the intolerance arising from such views could" be enforced with the full apparatus of law, from fines and exile to imprisonment and death (003-029).

## Historical Development

Intolerance was present even in the ostensibly freer atmosphere of classical Athens, where "the intolerance which prevailed at Athens" (002-260) led to the trial and execution of Socrates. Bury acknowledges that ancient society was "never entirely free" from intolerance, "and that the mass of intolerance which existed was" considerable even in relatively open societies (002-263). The Athenian case demonstrated that democratic societies could be as intolerant as tyrannies when fundamental religious sensibilities were challenged.

With the triumph of Christianity, intolerance became systematized. Bury describes the medieval Church's position in stark terms: its "exclusiveness and intolerance" (002-373) and its "intolerance and in hostility towards" non-Christians and heretics alike (002-392) created a comprehensive framework of thought control. "So far as intolerance went," Bury observes, "there was no distinction between Catholic and Protestant states" after the Reformation (004-120).

The Reformation intensified rather than relieved religious intolerance. Where there had been one orthodoxy, there were now competing orthodoxies, each demanding the right to persecute dissenters. Bury notes that "Calvin's fame for intolerance is blackest. He did not, like" other reformers merely inherit an intolerant system — he actively perfected it (004-137). Protestant reformers quickly adopted "the principle of ecclesiastical intolerance" (004-181) that they had once denounced when applied against themselves.

In England, "Puritan intolerance, indeed, passed an" ordinance "of death for unbelief" (004-288), demonstrating that the impulse to coerce belief survived every change of regime. Europe was trapped "amid the competing intolerances of Catholic and Protestant powers" (004-387). The gradual decline of official intolerance in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was the result of sustained campaigning by rationalists and freethinkers.

## Key Proponents and Opponents

The most significant voices opposing intolerance included:

- **Sebastian Castellio** — Argued that religious differences should be resolved through persuasion, not persecution. He and others "were forced by the intolerance of Calvin to flee" (005-027) from Geneva, becoming witnesses to the cruelty of theocratic enforcement.
- **John Locke** — His *Letter Concerning Toleration* provided the philosophical case against intolerance, arguing that belief cannot be coerced by the state. Locke was among those who "fled from the intolerance of the English Church and State" (005-071).
- **Voltaire** — The most famous critic of intolerance in the eighteenth century. Bury notes that Voltaire's sustained attack on "intolerance" was central to his larger campaign for rational reform, though his tactics were sometimes "suitable to the circumstances" of his time (005-163) (005-310).

Among the principal architects of intolerance was [[john-calvin]], whose Geneva regime represented the purest fusion of theological certainty and state power. The persecution of [[michael-servetus]] for anti-Trinitarian views demonstrated that Protestants could be as ruthless in suppressing heresy as the Inquisition they condemned.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Intolerance was the most effective practical obstacle to freedom of thought because it raised the cost of dissent to the level of life and death. The intellectual advances of rationalism and science could only benefit society when the legal and social apparatus of intolerance had been dismantled — otherwise, thinkers risked persecution for expressing conclusions that challenged orthodoxy.

By the later eighteenth century, many European governments "were proud of abolishing the intolerance of the government" (005-387), but this official tolerance did not extend to all beliefs. "Intolerance" persisted in subtler forms; for much of the period, "atheism was regarded as a vice" rather than a legitimate philosophical position, and this social intolerance was often "meant intolerance" in practice even where law had relaxed (005-408).

Bury's narrative shows that "much practical intolerance" continued to exist alongside formal legal guarantees, "and liberty before the law is" not sufficient on its own to produce a culture of genuine toleration (005-634). Even in the nineteenth century, freethinkers "complained of the general intolerance which" persisted in society, while "the same facts which testify to intolerance testify also" to the ongoing struggle for intellectual freedom (006-263) (006-264).
