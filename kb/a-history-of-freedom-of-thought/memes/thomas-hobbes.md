## Life and Background

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) was born in Westport, Wiltshire, England, during the Spanish Armada crisis. Educated at Oxford, he later served as tutor and secretary to the Cavendish family, which gave him access to intellectual circles across Europe. His travels brought him into contact with Galileo, Gassendi, and other leading figures of the Scientific Revolution.

Hobbes lived through the English Civil War (1642–1651), an experience that profoundly shaped his political philosophy. His support for absolute sovereignty made him controversial in both royalist and parliamentarian circles, and he spent years in self-imposed exile in Paris.

## Major Contributions

Hobbes is best known for *Leviathan* (1651), a foundational text of modern political philosophy. In it, he argued that human beings in a "state of nature" live in continual fear and conflict — a "war of all against all." To escape this condition, individuals contract to submit to a sovereign authority with absolute power to maintain peace.

Beyond politics, Hobbes developed a comprehensive materialist philosophy. He rejected the existence of incorporeal substances, including immaterial souls, and argued that all phenomena — including thought — could be explained in terms of matter in motion. His nominalism and empiricism anticipated later developments in British philosophy.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Hobbes was, in Bury's assessment, "perhaps the most brilliant English thinker of the seventeenth century" (006-058). His significance for freedom of thought lies in his uncompromising rationalism — his insistence that all claims, including religious ones, must be justified by reasoned argument from empirical premises.

The principles on which Hobbes built his theory were "rationalistic rather than theological" (006-068). His application of deductive reasoning to political and religious questions implicitly challenged the authority of revelation and ecclesiastical institutions. This approach made him deeply suspect to orthodox opinion.

During the Restoration, the consequences of Hobbes's intellectual courage became clear: he was "silenced and his books were burned" under Charles II (006-075). His influence nevertheless persisted, and later thinkers in the rationalist tradition continued to draw on his political speculations (006-077).

## Legacy and Influence

Hobbes's impact on subsequent thought was immense, even when thinkers disagreed with him. His social contract theory influenced Locke and Rousseau, who modified his framework for more liberal purposes. His materialism and nominalism anticipated Hume's empiricism. His challenge to ecclesiastical authority set a precedent for later Enlightenment critiques of religion.

The reaction against Hobbes also proved productive: attempts to refute him generated important works in moral philosophy, political theory, and theology throughout the late seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

## Key Works

- *Elements of Law* (1640) — Early treatment of natural law and sovereignty
- *De Cive* (1642) — Systematic political theory, published in Latin
- *Leviathan* (1651) — Masterpiece of political philosophy and materialism
- *De Corpore* (1655) — Materialist metaphysics and physics
- *De Homine* (1658) — Human nature and psychology
