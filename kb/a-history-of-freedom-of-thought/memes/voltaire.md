# Voltaire

**Voltaire** (François-Marie Arouet, 1694–1778) was a French Enlightenment writer, philosopher, and historian. He was the most energetic and forceful leader in the campaign against religious [[intolerance]] in eighteenth-century Europe (005-310). His literary genius converted the work of English freethinkers into a world-force that reshaped European attitudes toward [[religion]] and authority (006-490).

## Life and Background

Born in Paris to a middle-class family, Voltaire was educated by Jesuits at the Collège Louis-le-Grand. His early career was marked by sharp satirical writing that twice landed him in the Bastille. From 1726 to 1729 he lived in England, where he encountered the ideas of [[John Locke]], [[Isaac Newton]], and the English deists — an experience that fundamentally shaped his intellectual outlook.

After returning to France, Voltaire spent much of his later life at Ferney, near Geneva, from where he conducted a vast correspondence and published works that reached every corner of Europe. When he visited Paris in 1778, shortly before his death, he was acclaimed by crowds as the "defender of Calas and the destroyer of fanaticism" (005-334).

## Major Contributions

Voltaire's literary output was enormous — encompassing plays, poetry, histories, philosophical tales, and thousands of letters. His most enduring works include *Candide* (1759), a satirical attack on philosophical optimism; the *Philosophical Dictionary* (1764), which presented deist and rationalist arguments in accessible alphabetical form; and his *Letters on the English* (1734), which introduced English empiricism and political liberty to a French audience.

His historical writings, particularly *The Age of Louis XIV* (1751) and the *Essay on Manners* (1756), represented a new approach to historiography that emphasized cultural and intellectual developments rather than military and dynastic history.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Voltaire's most direct contribution to [[freedom of thought]] was his campaign against specific cases of religious persecution. The most famous was the Calas affair:

> Through the activity of Voltaire, then living near Geneva, the widow was induced to go to Paris, where she was kindly received, and assisted by lawyers of distinction. The case was brought before the courts, and the sentence was eventually reversed at Toulouse.
> (005-320, 005-334)

Jean Calas, a Protestant merchant of Toulouse, had been broken on the wheel in 1762 on the false charge of murdering his son to prevent his conversion to Catholicism. Voltaire's three-year campaign publicized the injustice across Europe, leading to the posthumous exoneration of Calas and the compensation of his family.

Voltaire's approach was not abstract philosophizing but concrete exposure of injustice. As he himself observed, "at Paris, fanaticism, powerful though it may be, is always controlled by reason" (005-325). His method of shaming authorities through publicity proved remarkably effective.

However, his system of toleration had limits. He did not advocate for complete religious liberty in the modern sense but rather for the supremacy of the state over ecclesiastical power and the elimination of cruel persecution (005-342).

## Religious Views

Voltaire was a convinced deist. He considered that the nature of the universe proved that it was made by a conscious creator, but he rejected the supernatural claims of organized Christianity:

> We are now in the age of Voltaire. He was a convinced deist. He considered that the nature of the universe proved that it was made by a conscious creator.
> (006-481)

His deism, unlike that of [[Rousseau]], was not religious and emotional. It was the "natural religion" of the philosophers — anterior and superior to any particular revelation (005-428). The clergy perhaps dreaded Rousseau's theories more than "the scoffs and negations of Voltaire" (006-558), yet Voltaire's attacks were so tremendously effective that he was looked on as a sort of anti-Christ (006-538).

| Aspect | Voltaire's Position |
|--------|-------------------|
| Metaphysics | Deist — universe proves a conscious creator |
| Christianity | Rejected supernatural elements; Christ was a wise teacher |
| Religious toleration | Supported for all peaceful sects; state supremacy over church |
| Method | Satire, exposure of injustice, public opinion campaigns |

## Legacy and Influence

Voltaire's influence on the subsequent development of [[freedom of thought]] is difficult to overstate:

> Without the work of Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, and their fellow-combatants, would it have been reformed?
> (006-605)

His friend [[Frederick the Great]] of Prussia, the friend of the great "blasphemer" Voltaire (005-554), implemented policies of religious toleration that made Prussia a model for enlightened governance.

Later commentators debated whether his artillery of wit produced more lasting effect than more systematic philosophical works (006-736), but there is little doubt that Voltaire's popularization of rationalist ideas prepared the ground for the [[French Revolution]] and the nineteenth-century advances in [[liberty of thought]].
