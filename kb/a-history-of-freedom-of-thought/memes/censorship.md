## Definition and Origins

Censorship, as examined in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to the institutionalized control of ideas through the suppression of speech, writing, and publication. Bury traces censorship as one of the principal mechanisms by which religious and political authorities sought to constrain the liberty of thought. The censorship of the Press, exemplified by the Index of Forbidden Books, was the expression of ecclesiastical authority over intellectual life (004-230).

Censorship in the Western tradition originated with the Church's concern over heretical ideas. As printing technology spread in the fifteenth century, the potential for dissenting views to reach wide audiences alarmed authorities. The logic of censorship rested on the premise that certain ideas were intrinsically dangerous to souls and social order, and that preventing their circulation was an act of protection rather than oppression.

## Historical Development

The institutionalization of censorship began in earnest during the Renaissance. Pope Alexander VI inaugurated censorship of the Press by his Bull against unlicensed printing, establishing a precedent that would be followed across Catholic Europe (004-375). The requirement that all publications receive official permission before printing carried severe penalties: printing without official permission was punishable by death (004-377).

In Germany, censorship was enforced through both ecclesiastical and secular channels. The fragmentation of the Holy Roman Empire meant that censorship practices varied among the German states, though the principle of pre-publication review was widely adopted (004-377).

In England, censorship of the Press had long ago disappeared by the time Bury wrote in 1913 (007-0913). The English experience illustrated a broader pattern: as political liberalism advanced, pre-publication censorship gave way to post-publication prosecution through libel and blasphemy laws. However, Bury noted that where the censorship is severe, it does not suggest that it is useful for promoting genuine intellectual inquiry (005-146).

By the early twentieth century, Russia retained its notorious censorship, standing as a prominent exception to the liberal trend in Western Europe (008-347). Bury observed that the censorship will conduce "to the discouragement of all learning" when applied rigorously (005-136).

## Key Proponents / Opponents

- **John Milton** wrote *Areopagitica* (1644), one of the most influential arguments against pre-publication censorship, defending the principle that truth would prevail in a free marketplace of ideas.
- **John Locke** argued for religious toleration and against the government's authority to regulate belief, laying philosophical groundwork for opposition to censorship.
- **The Inquisition** served as the Church's primary instrument for censorship, maintaining the Index of Forbidden Books and prosecuting authors and printers of prohibited works.
- **Pope Alexander VI** established the papal system of pre-publication censorship, making Church approval a prerequisite for printing throughout Catholic Christendom.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Censorship directly impeded the development of freedom of thought by creating a climate of fear around the expression of unorthodox ideas. The requirement of official permission before publication meant that ideas challenging religious or political orthodoxy could be suppressed before they reached readers. This prior restraint was more effective than post-publication prosecution because it prevented ideas from circulating at all.

Bury's narrative demonstrates that the struggle against censorship was central to the freethought movement. The abolition of pre-publication censorship in England was a landmark achievement, though it was replaced by other forms of control such as blasphemy prosecution. The persistence of censorship in Russia at the time of Bury's writing illustrated that the battle for free expression was far from universal.

The principle that emerged from centuries of resistance to censorship was that open debate, rather than suppression, best serves the pursuit of truth and the health of society.
