# Constantine the Great

**Constantine the Great** (c. 272–337 CE) was a Roman Emperor whose reign marked a decisive turning point in the history of [[religion]] and [[toleration]]. About ten years after the Edict of Toleration issued by Galerius, Constantine the Great extended and solidified the policy of religious liberty, bringing an end to the systematic persecution of Christians and establishing a new relationship between the imperial state and religious institutions (003-001).

## Life and Background

Born in Naissus (modern Niš, Serbia), Constantine was the son of Constantius Chlorus, a Roman officer who became one of the four tetrarchs governing the empire. After his father's death in 306, Constantine was proclaimed emperor by his troops in York. He spent the next eighteen years consolidating power through a series of civil wars, culminating in his victory over Maxentius at the Battle of the Milvian Bridge in 312 — an event later mythologized as the moment of his conversion to Christianity.

Constantine became sole ruler of the Western Empire in 312 and of the entire Roman Empire in 324, establishing a new imperial capital at Byzantium (renamed Constantinople) in 330.

## Major Contributions

Constantine's most significant contribution was his transformation of Christianity from a persecuted minority faith into an imperially favored religion. The Edict of Milan (313 CE), issued jointly with Licinius, granted religious freedom to all citizens of the empire:

> The second edict, of which Constantine was the author, known as the Edict of Milan, proclaimed full liberty of conscience for all subjects of the empire.
> (002-481)

This was a landmark in the history of [[freedom of thought]] — not because it established religious liberty in the modern sense, but because it recognized that the state should not coerce religious belief through violence.

Constantine also convened the First Council of Nicaea (325 CE), which produced the Nicene Creed and established a framework for doctrinal orthodoxy within the Christian church. He built major churches including the original St. Peter's Basilica in Rome and the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Constantine's legacy for freedom of thought is profoundly ambiguous. On one hand, he ended the brutal persecution of Christians that had periodically terrorized the faithful for three centuries. On the other hand:

> Under Constantine the Great and his successors, edict after edict was issued against paganism and heresy, gradually transforming the empire into a persecuting Christian state.
> (003-050)

The toleration Constantine established was one-sided: it protected Christians from persecution but did not extend equal protection to pagans, Jews, or Christian heretics in the long term. The trajectory he set in motion ultimately replaced one form of religious coercion with another.

Later historians would examine the development of the faith up to the time of Constantine in such a way as clearly to demonstrate how the church, once it gained imperial favor, adopted the very methods of coercion it had once suffered (006-677).

## Legacy and Influence

Constantine's reign established the paradigm of church-state relations that would dominate European history for more than a millennium. The idea that the emperor had the authority to convene church councils and enforce doctrinal conformity set a precedent for state involvement in religious matters that persisted through the [[Reformation]] and beyond.

His conversion also accelerated the intellectual shift from classical rationalism to Christian theology. The philosophical schools of antiquity, already in decline, found themselves increasingly marginalized in a culture that now privileged revealed truth over rational inquiry.
