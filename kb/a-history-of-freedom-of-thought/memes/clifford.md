# W. K. Clifford

**William Kingdon Clifford** (1845–1879) was a British mathematician and philosopher. Bury lists Clifford's *Body and Mind* among the key works of late Victorian freethought, alongside [[Huxley]]'s *Animal Automatism* and Tyndall's Address to the British Association at Belfast (007-1067).

## Life and Background

Born in Exeter, Clifford showed exceptional mathematical ability from an early age. He studied at King's College London and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he was elected a fellow. In 1871, he was appointed Professor of Applied Mathematics at University College London, where he remained until his early death from tuberculosis at the age of 33.

Despite his short life, Clifford made significant contributions to geometry and mathematical physics, anticipating aspects of Einstein's general theory of relativity. He was also a gifted popular lecturer who brought scientific ideas to a wide audience.

## Major Contributions

In mathematics, Clifford developed a theory of geometric algebras (now known as Clifford algebras) that unified Hamilton's quaternions with Grassmann's exterior algebra. He was among the first to suggest that space might be curved, anticipating the geometric framework of relativity.

In philosophy, Clifford was a leading proponent of what he called the "scientific habit of mind." His most famous philosophical essay, "The Ethics of Belief" (1877), argued that "it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence" — a principle that became a rallying cry for rationalist and agnostic movements.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Clifford's *Body and Mind* (007-1067) was part of a cluster of late-Victorian works that advanced naturalistic explanations of consciousness against traditional dualist and theological accounts.

This literature — including [[Huxley]]'s work on animal automatism and Tyndall's Belfast Address — represented the extension of scientific naturalism into the territory previously reserved to religion and philosophy.

Clifford's work belongs to a period when [[rationalism]] was gaining confidence in its ability to explain phenomena previously ascribed to supernatural causes.

Bury mentions his *Body and Mind* alongside [[John Stuart Mill]]'s *Three Essays on Religion*, Winwood Reade's *Martyrdom of Man*, and Cassels's *Supernatural Religion* — works that collectively challenged the intellectual foundations of Christianity (007-1064 to 007-1067).

Clifford's career was cut short by his early death, but his insistence on the sufficiency of evidence and [[reason]] embodied the rationalist spirit that Bury traces from ancient Greece to the modern era.

## Legacy and Influence

Clifford's "Ethics of Belief" continues to be debated in epistemology and philosophy of religion. His argument that belief without sufficient evidence is morally wrong — not merely intellectually mistaken — provided an ethical foundation for the rationalist and freethought movements.

In mathematics, Clifford algebras have become fundamental tools in theoretical physics, used in quantum mechanics, string theory, and general relativity. This posthumous vindication of his mathematical vision is one of the more remarkable stories in the history of science.
