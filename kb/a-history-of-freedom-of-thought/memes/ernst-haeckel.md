# Ernst Haeckel

**Ernst Haeckel** (1834–1919) was a German zoologist and professor at the University of Jena, who may be called the "prophet" of [[evolution]] in Germany (007-0208). His popularization of Darwinian theory and his development of Monism as a scientific-materialist philosophy made him a central figure in the late nineteenth-century conflict between [[science]] and [[religion]].

## Life and Background

Born in Potsdam, Prussia, Haeckel studied medicine at Berlin and Würzburg before turning to zoology. In 1865 he was appointed professor of zoology at the University of Jena, where he remained for the bulk of his career. His early encounter with [[Charles Darwin|Darwin]]'s *On the Origin of Species* (1859) proved decisive: Haeckel became the most energetic advocate of evolutionary theory in the German-speaking world.

His academic position at Jena was not without controversy. An attempt was made to chase Haeckel from Jena due to the perceived anti-religious implications of his teachings (007-0430). He nevertheless retained his professorship and continued to publish extensively on evolutionary biology and its philosophical implications.

## Major Contributions

Haeckel's scientific work spanned marine biology, comparative anatomy, and embryology. He discovered and named thousands of new species and coined key biological terms including "ecology," "phylum," and "ontogeny." His artistic representations of radiolarians and other marine organisms influenced the Art Nouveau movement.

His most significant contribution to the history of ideas, however, was his formulation of **Monism** — a philosophical system that held that mind and matter are two aspects of a single underlying substance, eliminating the need for a transcendent creator. Haeckel, evolution as the guiding principle in the history of living things, built Monism on a foundation of materialist science (007-0990).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Haeckel's Monism became the basis of an organized movement dedicated to replacing religious belief with a scientific worldview:

> This movement is based on the ideas of Haeckel, who regarded the universe as a unity and rejected the dualism of spirit and matter.
> (007-0981)

The Monist League (*Deutscher Monistenbund*), founded in 1906, advocated for secular education, church-state separation, and the application of evolutionary thinking to ethics and social policy. The movement was part of a broader wave of free thought in Germany from 1910 to 1912 (007-0981).

Haeckel's popular book *The Riddle of the Universe* (1899) sold hundreds of thousands of copies and was translated into many languages, bringing scientific rationalism to a mass audience. However, his Monist philosophy attracted criticism both from traditional religious authorities and from scientists who felt he had overstepped the boundaries of empirical science.

## Legacy and Influence

While Haeckel's specific biological theories — such as the "biogenetic law" that ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny — have been largely discredited, his broader impact on the secularization of European thought was substantial (007-0223). His insistence that science could provide a complete worldview without recourse to supernatural explanations anticipated the secular humanism of the twentieth century.

The Monist movement he inspired underwent a significant transition in its later years: under the influence of [[Wilhelm Ostwald|Ostwald]], the new leader, the movement shifted its emphasis while remaining grounded in Haeckel's original vision (007-0983).
