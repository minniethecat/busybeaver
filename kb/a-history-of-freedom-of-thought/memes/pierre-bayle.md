# Pierre Bayle

**Pierre Bayle** (1647–1706) was a French Protestant philosopher and scholar who, living in exile in Holland, produced works that supplied weapons to assailants of Christianity throughout Europe (006-185). His *Philosophical Commentary* on toleration and his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* made him one of the most influential figures in the development of early modern rationalism and religious [[toleration]].

## Life and Background

Born in Carla-le-Comte in southern France, Bayle was the son of a Calvinist minister. He briefly converted to Catholicism as a young man before returning to Protestantism — an experience that exposed him to the coercive methods of religious authorities on both sides. This personal encounter with religious persecution shaped his lifelong commitment to freedom of conscience.

Driven from France by the persecution of Protestants under Louis XIV, Bayle took refuge in Holland, where he lived and worked in Amsterdam. There he published his major works, including the *Philosophical Commentary on the Text "Compel Them to Come In"* (1686) and his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697).

## Major Contributions

Bayle's most important contributions were in two areas: the defense of religious toleration and the development of skeptical historical criticism.

His *Philosophical Commentary*, written in response to the French clergy's justification of the persecution of Protestants, "stands beside Locke's work which was being composed at the same time" (005-288). The treatise argued against the use of force in matters of religion. The most characteristic thing in Bayle's treatise was his skeptical argument that, "even if it were a right principle to suppress error by force, no truth is certain enough to justify us in applying the theory" (005-294).

His *Historical and Critical Dictionary* was a massive work of scholarship in which Bayle marshalled objections to established dogmas:

> Bayle's work, marked by scholarship and extraordinary learning, had a great influence in England as well as in France. It supplied weapons to assailants of Christianity in both countries.
> (006-185–006-187)

Though really a freethinker, "he never dropped the disguise of orthodoxy, and this lends a particular piquancy to his work" (006-147). He exposed without mercy the crimes and brutalities of biblical figures, showing that David — "this favourite of the Almighty" — was a person with whom one would refuse to shake hands (006-152).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Bayle worked in the same direction as [[John Locke]], contributing to [[freedom of thought]] through historical investigation rather than metaphysical philosophy. While Locke derived all knowledge from experience, setting authority in its place, Bayle advanced rationalism "by the investigation of history" (006-144).

His most effective rhetorical strategy was to feign defense of orthodoxy while systematically undermining it. When criticized for the "unedifying candour" with which he exposed the moral failings of biblical figures, Bayle adopted the attitude of Montaigne and Pascal, opposing faith to reason. He argued that "the merit of faith becomes greater, in proportion as the revealed truth surpasses all the powers of our mind; the more incomprehensible the truth and the more repugnant to reason, the greater is the sacrifice we make in accepting it" (006-153–006-162).

This was, of course, an ironic defense. "The upshot was, under the thin veil of serving faith, to show that the Christian dogmas were essentially unreasonable" (006-184).

Bayle also defended the moral character of atheists. When challenged for doing justice to "the moral excellencies of persons who denied the existence of God," he replied that if he had been able to find any atheistical thinkers who lived bad lives, he would have been delighted to dwell on their vices, but he knew of none such (006-166). He wickedly added that most criminals in history believed in a Divinity — a natural consequence of the theological doctrine that the Devil, incapable of atheism, is the instigator of all sins (006-171–006-175).

## Legacy and Influence

The influence of Bayle's work was extensive. The English deist [[Anthony Collins]] wrote *A Discourse of Free-thinking* in which Bayle's influence is evident (006-261). [[Voltaire]], too, owed a significant debt to Bayle's methods of ironic criticism — in works such as the *Questions of Zapata*, "we can see what he owed to Bayle" (006-507). Bayle's skeptical rationalism, along with the work of the English deists, supplied a powerful arsenal for the [[the-enlightenment|Enlightenment]] assault on revealed religion (006-478).
