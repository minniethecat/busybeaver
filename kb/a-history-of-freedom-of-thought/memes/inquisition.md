## Definition and Origins

The Inquisition was an organized system of ecclesiastical tribunals established by the Roman Catholic Church for the discovery and punishment of heretics. J.B. Bury describes it as a "powerful engine for the suppression of the freedom of men's religious opinions" that is "unique in history" (003-113). The system was founded by Pope Gregory IX about A.D. 1233, and fully established by a Bull of Pope Innocent IV in 1252, which Bury notes "regulated the machinery of persecution as an integral part of the social edifice in every city and every State" (003-113).

The Inquisition emerged in the aftermath of the Albigensian Crusade against the Cathars in southern France. Although the Albigensians had been crushed, Bury observes that "the poison of their doctrine was not yet destroyed," prompting the Church to create a permanent mechanism for rooting out dissent (003-113).

## Historical Development

The medieval Inquisition expanded rapidly across Western Christendom. Bury records that "when the Inquisition was fully developed it covered Western Christendom" and was most effective "in its task of imposing unity of belief" (003-144, 003-152). The institution would not be abolished until the nineteenth century (003-157).

The Inquisition's methods were systematic and severe. It enlisted the laity in its work through organizations such as the "Militia of Faith," which Bury notes "enlisted the people in the service of the Inquisition" (003-167). The institution operated on the principle that heresy was a contagious disease requiring extirpation, and it possessed "unlimited authority" with inquisitors "subject to no supervision and responsible to no man" (003-113).

Lea, the great historian of the Inquisition whom Bury cites, observed: "Of all the curses which the Inquisition brought in its train, this perhaps was the greatest"—referring to the way it corrupted social bonds by encouraging denunciation of neighbors and family members (003-206–207).

The Roman Inquisition was established in the sixteenth century as part of the Counter-Reformation, alongside "the Council of Trent" and other measures to combat Protestantism (004-229). It famously tried Galileo Galilei, who was brought before the "Office of the Inquisition by two Dominican monks" and threatened with proceedings if he did not recant his Copernican views (004-312, 004-321). Earlier, Giordano Bruno had been "finally condemned in Rome" and "burned (1600) in the Campo dei Fiori" (004-251).

## Key Proponents and Agents

- **Pope Gregory IX** (r. 1227–1241), who founded the Inquisition around 1233 as a centralized papal institution for combating heresy.
- **Pope Innocent IV** (r. 1243–1254), whose bull *Ad extirpanda* (1252) authorized the use of torture and established the Inquisition's legal framework.
- **Emperor Frederick II**, who, though "himself undoubtedly a freethinker," enacted merciless secular legislation against heresy between 1220 and 1235, "consecrating the stake as the proper punishment for heresy" (003-124, 003-157).
- The **Dominican Order**, which provided the majority of inquisitors and became closely identified with the institution's work.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The Inquisition represented the most systematic assault on freedom of thought in Western history. By creating an institutional mechanism for detecting and punishing dissent, it cast a long shadow over intellectual life for more than six centuries.

Bury emphasizes that the Inquisition's effects extended far beyond its direct victims. The system corroded the social trust necessary for free inquiry by incentivizing denunciation and making private belief a matter of public policing. It "enlisted the people" in persecution, turning communities against their own members (003-167).

The Inquisition also established a precedent for ideological surveillance that persisted long after the institution itself declined. Bury groups "Inquisition, Censorship of the Press, Blasphemy laws" together as interconnected mechanisms for "the suppression of the freedom of men's religious opinions" (008-412, 003-113). The long struggle against inquisitorial methods was central to the development of modern concepts of toleration, free speech, and liberty of conscience.
