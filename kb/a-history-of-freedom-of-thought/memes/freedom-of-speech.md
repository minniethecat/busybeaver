## Definition and Origins

Freedom of speech, as Bury uses the term, encompasses the right to express thoughts and opinions publicly without fear of legal penalty or social persecution. In its fullest and most valuable sense, freedom of thought "includes freedom of speech" — for thought that cannot be expressed is thought that remains incomplete (001-018). The distinction between inner belief and outward expression is crucial: a person may think freely in private, but without the liberty to speak and publish, those thoughts cannot contribute to the progress of knowledge or the improvement of society.

Bury opens his history by noting that in "the most civilized countries, freedom of speech is taken" as a matter of course, yet this was a hard-won achievement (001-019). The concept emerged gradually through centuries of struggle against censorship, blasphemy laws, and ecclesiastical control of publishing.

## Historical Development

The ancient Greeks enjoyed a degree of free speech unknown in later ages. In Athens, political discussion was "perfectly free" and the comic poets could mock the gods on the public stage (002-115). However, this liberty was bounded: Socrates was executed in part for his speech, demonstrating that even democratic Athens placed limits on expression deemed dangerous to the state religion.

During the Middle Ages, freedom of speech was virtually extinguished. The Church's control over education and publishing meant that any expression contradicting orthodoxy could be punished as heresy. The invention of the printing press in the 15th century created new possibilities for the spread of ideas, but also prompted new mechanisms of control — ecclesiastical censorship and the Index of Prohibited Books.

The Enlightenment transformed freedom of speech from a practical accommodation into a matter of principle. Thinkers began arguing that "freedom of speech is a natural and inalienable birthright of man" rather than a privilege granted by rulers (001-101). The abolition of pre-publication censorship in England (1695) marked a turning point, creating a public sphere in which ideas could compete freely.

In the 19th century, freedom of speech became a central political demand. John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) provided the most influential philosophical defense, arguing that even false opinions contribute to the vitality of truth by forcing it to be continually re-examined. Mill argued that liberty of speech should be a privileged form of liberty because it harms no one and benefits all (008-021). By the end of the century, freedom of speech was widely recognized as a hallmark of civilized government, even as its practical boundaries remained contested.

## Key Proponents

- **Socrates**, though executed for his speech, established the model of the philosopher who speaks truth to power regardless of the consequences. His declaration that he would "obey God rather than you" redefined the relationship between individual conscience and state authority.
- **John Milton** defended freedom of the press in his *Areopagitica* (1644), arguing that truth would prevail in open contest with falsehood and that licensing of the press was an affront to human dignity.
- **Voltaire** embodied the Enlightenment commitment to free expression, famously declaring that he would defend to the death another's right to speak even if he disagreed with every word. His campaigns against judicial murders for blasphemy made freedom of speech a European cause.
- **John Stuart Mill** provided the definitive 19th-century defense of free speech in *On Liberty*. He argued that silencing an opinion is "an assumption of infallibility" and that liberty of speech is discoverable as a natural right (008-021). His harm principle — that speech may only be restricted when it directly harms others — remains the philosophical foundation of modern free speech doctrine.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Freedom of speech is both a component and a consequence of freedom of thought. Bury's narrative demonstrates that each expansion of free expression — the end of pre-publication censorship, the abolition of blasphemy laws, the recognition of political dissent as legitimate — directly advanced the broader cause of intellectual liberty. Conversely, each restriction on speech reinforced the power of orthodoxy and retarded the progress of knowledge.

The relationship between free speech and democracy is particularly significant. Bury notes that political discussion was an essential feature of Athenian democracy (002-115), and the modern revival of free speech coincided with the rise of representative government. By the 19th century, some argued for representative democracy precisely because it provided the best institutional framework for protecting freedom of speech (006-782).
