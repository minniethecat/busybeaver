# Socrates

**Socrates** (c. 470–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher whose method of relentless questioning — the [[Socratic method]] — and his execution by the Athenian democracy for "corrupting the youth and introducing new gods" established him as one of the earliest and most enduring symbols of [[freethought]] and intellectual courage. His trial, as recorded in Plato's *Apology*, represents one of the first documented clashes between individual [[reason]] and state-enforced orthodoxy.

## Life and Method

Socrates left no writings of his own. What is known of him comes primarily through the dialogues of his student [[Plato]] and the historical accounts of Xenophon. He spent his days in the agora of Athens, engaging citizens — politicians, poets, artisans — in dialectical questioning that stripped away unexamined assumptions. This "Socratic method" of inquiry proceeded by asking definitions and testing them against counterexamples, exposing the limits of conventional knowledge.

He described himself as a "gadfly" sent by the gods to stir the sluggish horse of the Athenian state. Unlike the [[Sophists]], who charged fees for instruction in rhetoric, Socrates took no payment and claimed no positive doctrine — only an awareness of his own ignorance.

## Trial and Execution

In 399 BCE, Socrates was brought to trial on two charges: impiety (not recognizing the gods recognized by the state) and corrupting the youth. The indictment was politically motivated — among his associates were [[Alcibiades]], who had betrayed Athens to Sparta, and [[Critias]], one of the brutal Thirty Tyrants. Yet the underlying offense was intellectual: Socrates taught young men to question received authority.

> [002-187] …if the Athenians had had a daily press, Socrates would have been

> [002-206] …and of these disloyal persons Socrates was

The *Apology* — Plato's reconstruction of Socrates' defense speech — shows him refusing to compromise. Rather than propose exile or silence as an alternative to death, he argued that "the unexamined life is not worth living" and suggested that Athens should reward him with free meals at the Prytaneum for his service. Condemned by a narrow margin, he drank hemlock in the company of his followers, discoursing on the immortality of the soul until the end.

> [002-193] Socrates reached old age, pursuing the… kind, of life he had chosen, and was then put to death by the Athenian state for the same reasons that had made him unpopular as a young man.

## Significance for Freedom of Thought

Socrates occupies a foundational position in the history of [[freedom of thought]] for three reasons:

1. **The principle of intellectual autonomy**: Socrates insisted that each person must reason independently and follow the argument wherever it leads — a direct challenge to appeals to tradition, authority, or majority opinion.
2. **The social value of criticism**: He demonstrated that a society benefits from having its fundamental beliefs subjected to scrutiny, anticipating [[John Stuart Mill]]'s arguments in *On Liberty* by over two millennia.
3. **Martyrdom for conscience**: His willingness to die rather than renounce his philosophical mission created a powerful template of intellectual martyrdom that would inspire later [[freethinkers]] from the [[Renaissance]] to the [[Enlightenment]].

> [008-043] We saw how Socrates indicated the social value of the individual mind in free discussion, and that is the principle on which the justification of liberty of thought rests.

## Socratic Legacy in the Enlightenment

Eighteenth-century thinkers repeatedly invoked Socrates as a precursor. [[Voltaire]] praised his critique of superstition; [[Diderot]] contributed the entry on "Socrates" to the *Encyclopédie*. The comparison was strategic — if pagan Athens could produce a thinker of Socrates' moral seriousness without Christian revelation, then morality did not depend on revealed religion, a key thesis of [[rationalism]].
