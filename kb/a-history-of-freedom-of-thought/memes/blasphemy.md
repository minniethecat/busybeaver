## Definition and Origins

Blasphemy, in the context of J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to speech or writing deemed disrespectful toward God, religious doctrines, or sacred things. Bury treats blasphemy as a legal and social mechanism for suppressing religious dissent, distinct from but related to heresy. The charge of blasphemy was a powerful weapon against freethinkers throughout history (002-267).

The concept of blasphemy originated in ancient religious communities where the sacred was protected by taboo. In Greek antiquity, charges of impiety—functionally equivalent to blasphemy—were used against philosophers who questioned traditional religion. The Athenians introduced and carried a blasphemy law to prosecute those who denied the gods or introduced new divinities (002-122).

## Historical Development

In classical Athens, a charge of blasphemy was lodged against prominent thinkers who challenged religious orthodoxy (002-137). The most famous case was that of Socrates, executed for allegedly corrupting the youth and introducing new gods. Bury notes that the real evil of the blasphemy law was that it could be used as a pretext for political persecution, rather than a genuine defense of religious sentiment (002-152).

During the Christian era, blasphemy laws were strengthened and systematized. The Inquisition prosecuted blasphemers alongside heretics, and secular authorities adopted similar statutes. In England, the common law of blasphemy developed as an offense against the Christian religion, punishable by fines and imprisonment.

In the nineteenth century, the law prescribed severe penalties: a maximum term of six months for atheism, blasphemy, and related offenses against religion (006-222). However, Bury observes that as a matter of fact, most trials for blasphemy during the past century were directed against publications that aroused moral rather than purely theological offense (006-244).

## Key Proponents / Opponents

- **Charles Bradlaugh** was imprisoned for blasphemy and his case contributed to the abolition of legal penalties for expressing unorthodox religious views (007-0911).
- **John Stuart Mill** argued in *On Liberty* against blasphemy laws, contending that the expression of unpopular opinions must be protected for the sake of truth and intellectual vitality.
- **The policy of enforcing the laws for blasphemy only** when public order was threatened represented a pragmatic retreat from rigorous prosecution (007-0685).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Blasphemy laws were among the most persistent obstacles to freedom of thought. Unlike heresy, which concerned doctrinal deviation within a faith community, blasphemy criminalized the expression of disbelief itself. This meant that even those who did not claim membership in a religious community could be prosecuted for statements about religion.

By the early twentieth century, Bury observed important shifts in the legal understanding of blasphemy. Courts had taken the line that it is not blasphemy to attack the fundamental doctrines of Christianity, but only "indecent" attacks constitute blasphemy (008-228). This implied a new definition of legal blasphemy, entirely contrary to the traditional understanding that any denial of Christian truth was blasphemous (008-231).

Bury remained skeptical about whether the law was really impartial and punished blasphemy only when it offended public decency (008-242). Yet he concluded that blasphemy does not endanger the liberty of thought when properly limited to preventing public nuisance rather than enforcing orthodoxy (008-260). The gradual narrowing of blasphemy from a tool of religious enforcement to a limited public-order measure represented a significant victory for freethought.
