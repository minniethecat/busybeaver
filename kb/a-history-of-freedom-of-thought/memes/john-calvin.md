# John Calvin

**John Calvin** (1509–1564) was a French theologian and the leading figure of the Reformed tradition of Protestantism. In Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, Calvin appears not as an advocate of freedom but as its notorious opponent: "Calvin's fame for intolerance is blackest" (004-137). His establishment of a theocracy at Geneva represents one of the most complete suppressions of liberty of thought in early modern history.

## Life and Background

Born in Noyon, France, Calvin studied law and was influenced by humanist scholarship before converting to Protestantism in the early 1530s. Forced to flee France due to persecution of Protestants, he settled in Geneva, where he established a form of church government that gave religious authorities control over the civil state.

Calvin did not, like [[Martin Luther|Luther]], advocate the absolute power of the civil ruler. Instead, "he stood for the control of the State by the Church — a form of government which is commonly called theocracy" (004-137). Under this system, Geneva became a city where liberty was completely crushed and false doctrines were suppressed by imprisonment, exile, and death (004-142).

## The Servetus Affair

The most famous event in Calvin's career was the execution of the Spanish physician and theologian [[Michael Servetus]] in 1553:

> The Spaniard Servetus, who had written against the dogma of the Trinity, was imprisoned at Lyons (partly through the machinations of Calvin) and having escaped came rashly to Geneva. He was tried for heresy and committed to the flames, though Geneva had no jurisdiction over him.
> (004-144–004-148)

This act, praised at the time by Melanchthon as "a memorable example to posterity," became a symbol of the intolerance inherent in the Reformation churches. In 1903, the Calvinists of Geneva felt impelled to erect an expiatory monument, in which Calvin was presented as "our great Reformer" but excused as guilty of an error "which was that of his century" (004-151–004-153).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Calvin's relation to [[freedom of thought]] is wholly negative — he is, in Bury's narrative, the archetype of Protestant intolerance. The Reformers, like the Church from which they parted, "cared nothing for freedom, they only cared for 'truth'" (004-154).

The key insight Bury draws from Calvin's example is that the Reformation did not inaugurate an era of religious liberty. The Reformers "set up the authority of the Bible instead of that of the Church, but it was the Bible according to Luther or the Bible according to Calvin" (004-119). So far as the spirit of intolerance went, "there was nothing to choose between the new and the old Churches" (004-120).

The effects of Calvin's intolerance extended far beyond Geneva. The [[Socinians]], a rationalist religious group, "were forced by the intolerance of Calvin to flee" (005-027). [[Castellio]], who criticized the burning of Servetus, "earned the malignant hatred of Calvin" (005-040).

Calvin's followers, known as Calvinists, formed the Reformed Church alongside the followers of [[Huldrych Zwingli|Zwingli]] (005-676). The traditions of "Calvinistic Geneva" continued to influence thinkers like [[Rousseau]], whose ideal State would, "in its way, have been as illiberal as any theocracy of the Middle Ages" (005-346).

## Legacy and Influence

Calvin's legacy in the history of [[freedom of thought]] is paradoxical. His theology inadvertently created conditions for resistance to authority — the Calvinist emphasis on individual conscience and the right to resist ungodly rulers laid groundwork for later political liberty. Yet in his own practice, Calvin stopped where he stood. As Bury observes, there was no inherent reason to "stop where Luther or Calvin or any of the other rebels stopped, unless" one applied the principle of private judgment consistently — something the Reformers refused to do (004-201).

| Aspect | Calvin's Position |
|--------|------------------|
| Church-State | Theocracy — Church controls the State |
| Religious Toleration | None — heresy punishable by imprisonment, exile, death |
| Biblical Authority | Bible according to Calvin is the final authority |
| Legacy for Freedom | Negative — exemplar of Protestant intolerance |
