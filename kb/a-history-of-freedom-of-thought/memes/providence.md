# Providence

Providence is the theological doctrine that God actively governs and directs the universe and human affairs according to a divine plan. In the history of freedom of thought, the concept of providence became a focal point of Enlightenment criticism, as rationalist philosophers and Deists questioned whether divine intervention in the natural order could be reconciled with reason and justice.

## Definition and Origins

The term derives from the Latin *providentia* (foresight), reflecting the belief that God foresees and provides for all creation. In Christian theology, providence encompasses both God's general governance of the natural world and specific interventions — miracles — that suspend the ordinary course of nature. The doctrine holds that nothing occurs outside God's will, and that historical events unfold according to a divine purpose (006-675).

## Historical Development

In the eighteenth century, the doctrine of providence came under sustained criticism from freethinkers who questioned its logical consistency. The Deist Thomas Chubb pointed out the moral problem: if God could break "the ordinary rules of his providence to punish the innocent for the guilty" — as in the biblical account of Elijah causing drought — then humanity had no guarantee of divine justice in this life or the next. "If the eternal rules of justice are once broken," Chubb argued, "how can we imagine any stop?" (006-360).

> If God could break in upon the ordinary rules of his providence to punish the innocent for the guilty, we have no guarantee that if he deals thus with us in this life, he will not act in the same way in the life to come, "since if the eternal rules of justice are once broken how can we imagine any stop?"
> (006-360)

Edward Gibbon employed the language of providence with devastating irony in *The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776–1788). He observed that the victory of Christianity was conventionally explained "by the convincing evidence of the doctrine and by the ruling providence of its great Author," then proceeded "with becoming submission" to examine the purely human "secondary causes" — making the hypothesis of divine interposition appear superfluous (006-675). This ironic treatment allowed Gibbon to suggest, without explicitly stating, that Christianity's rise was a natural historical development rather than a providential event.

The argument from divine design — closely related to providence — was also subjected to philosophical criticism. David Hume, in his *Dialogues on Natural Religion* (published posthumously in 1776), attacked the "argument from design" by contending that an intelligent designer would itself require explanation, leading to an infinite regress of causes (006-638). William Paley's *Natural Theology* (1802) attempted to revive the design argument by comparing nature to a watch that implies a watchmaker, but did so "without taking any account of the criticisms of Hume" (006-753).

## Key Proponents and Critics

**Proponents of Providence**:
- **William Paley** (1743–1805): Argued that contrivances in nature, particularly in the human body, proved the existence of a divine designer. His "God," as Leslie Stephen remarked, "has been civilized like man; he has become scientific and ingenious" (006-753).
- Traditional Christian apologists who saw providence as the foundation of divine justice and historical meaning.

**Critics of Providence**:
- **Thomas Chubb** (1679–1747): Deist writer who attacked the moral inconsistency of a God who breaks natural laws to punish the innocent.
- **David Hume** (1711–1776): Demonstrated that the argument from design could not logically prove the existence of a Deity, as it led to an endless chain of causation (006-638).
- **Edward Gibbon** (1737–1794): Used irony to undermine providential explanations of Christian history, treating the rise of Christianity as a purely human phenomenon (006-675).
- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804): In his *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781), demonstrated that attempts to prove God's existence through reason alone fall into contradictions, and his "destructive criticism of the argument from design and all natural theology was more complete than that of Hume" (007-0097).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The critique of providence was a significant step in the liberation of reason from theological authority. By demonstrating that natural and historical events could be explained without recourse to divine intervention, Enlightenment thinkers advanced the principle that the world operates according to discoverable natural laws. Gibbon's ironic treatment of providence modeled how a freethinker could critically examine religious claims while navigating the dangers of censorship and prosecution. Kant's philosophical demolition of natural theology marked, as Bury notes, "a significant step further in the deliverance of reason from the yoke of authority" (007-0097).
