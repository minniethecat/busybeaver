# Pericles

**Pericles** (c. 495–429 BC) was an Athenian statesman who guided [[Athens]] through the period of its greatest cultural achievement and political power. During the time she was guided by the statesman Pericles, who was personally a freethinker, Athens became the intellectual capital of the Greek world, attracting philosophers, artists, and writers from across the Mediterranean (002-116).

## Life and Background

Born into the aristocratic Alcmaeonid family, Pericles entered Athenian politics around 463 BC and rose to dominate the city's affairs for over three decades. His political program combined democratic reform at home with imperial expansion abroad. Under his leadership, Athens completed the construction of the Parthenon and other monuments of the Acropolis, supported the tragedies of Sophocles and Euripides, and hosted the philosophical inquiries of [[Anaxagoras]], [[Socrates]], and the Sophists.

Pericles' famous Funeral Oration, recorded by Thucydides, articulated the Athenian ideal of an open society in which citizens were free to live as they pleased without fear of persecution or social sanction.

## Major Contributions

Pericles' principal contributions were political and cultural rather than philosophical. He extended democratic participation by introducing pay for jury service, enabling poorer citizens to participate in the legal system. He also sponsored an ambitious building program on the Acropolis that employed thousands and produced some of the most enduring monuments of Western civilization.

His patronage of intellectuals was deliberate and strategic. He recognized that a city that welcomed freethinkers would attract the brightest minds of the age, and he cultivated an environment in which philosophical speculation could flourish alongside artistic creativity.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Pericles was personally an unbeliever in the traditional gods of the Greek pantheon — a dangerous position in a city that had previously executed [[Socrates]] for impiety. The political enemies of Pericles struck at him by attacking his associates, targeting the philosopher Anaxagoras, who taught that the sun was not a deity but a mass of flaming matter (002-121, 002-127).

> The influence of Pericles saved him [Anaxagoras] from the death penalty, though he was forced to leave Athens.
> (002-127)

This episode reveals both the limits and the possibilities of freethought in classical Athens. Even the most powerful man in the city could not prevent the prosecution of an intellectual accused of impiety — but his influence was sufficient to save the accused from execution. Pericles' protection of Anaxagoras established a precedent: political power could be deployed to shield free inquiry from religious persecution.

## Legacy and Influence

Pericles' model of a democratic society that tolerated — and even celebrated — intellectual diversity had a lasting impact on the Western political imagination. The idea that freedom of thought is essential to a flourishing civilization was given its first practical expression in Periclean Athens.

The Athenian experiment was imperfect and short-lived: within a generation of Pericles' death, Athens had condemned Socrates to death and entered a long period of decline. But the memory of the Periclean ideal survived, providing inspiration for later movements for [[freedom of thought]] and political liberty from the [[Renaissance]] to [[the-enlightenment]].
