## Life and Background

Edward Gibbon was born in Putney, England, on 8 May 1737 into a prosperous family. His childhood was marked by ill health and an irregular education, but he showed an early passion for reading and historical study. At fifteen, he entered Magdalen College, Oxford — an experience he later described in his autobiography as "the most idle and unprofitable" period of his life.

While at Oxford, Gibbon briefly converted to Roman Catholicism in 1753, a decision that scandalized his father, who promptly sent him to Lausanne, Switzerland, to be re-educated under the Calvinist pastor Daniel Pavilliard. The strategy succeeded: Gibbon returned to Protestantism within a year. His five years in Lausanne proved formative — he acquired fluency in French, immersed himself in classical and Enlightenment literature, and fell in love with Suzanne Curchod (the future Madame Necker and mother of Madame de Staël), though his father forbade the marriage.

Returning to England in 1758, Gibbon served as a captain in the Hampshire militia during the Seven Years' War (an experience he credited with giving him practical knowledge of military affairs useful for his history). He entered Parliament in 1774, sitting as a government supporter, but his political career was unremarkable. The publication of the first volume of *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* in 1776 brought him immediate fame — and notoriety. He died in London on 16 January 1794.

## Major Contributions

Gibbon's *Decline and Fall* (six volumes, 1776–1788) is among the most influential historical works ever written. Of the numerous freethinking books that appeared in England in the eighteenth century, this is the only one which is still a widely read classic (006-0661).

The work's most controversial contribution to freedom of thought was its treatment of the rise of Christianity. In what a lady friend of Dr. Johnson called "the two offensive chapters" (XV and XVI), the causes of the rise and success of Christianity are for the first time critically investigated as a simple historical phenomenon (006-0665). Like most freethinkers of the time, Gibbon thought it well to protect himself and his work against the possibility of prosecution by paying ironical lip-homage to the orthodox creed. But even if there had been no such danger, he could not have chosen a more incisive weapon for his merciless criticism of orthodox opinion than the irony which he wielded with superb ease (006-0668).

Gibbon's treatment of miracles from the purely historical point of view — he owed a great deal to [[Middleton]] — was particularly disconcerting. In the early age of Christianity, he observed, "the laws of nature were frequently suspended for the benefit of the Church. But the sages of Greece and Rome turned aside from the awful spectacle" (006-0692). His masterly exposure of the conventional history of the early Church remains in many of its most important points perfectly valid (006-0731).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Gibbon advanced freedom of thought by demonstrating that the origins of Christianity could be studied with the same critical methods applied to any other historical phenomenon. By treating the triumph of the Church not as a miracle but as a natural event produced by human causes, he secularized a domain that had previously been shielded from critical scrutiny.

Bury judged that Gibbon's "artillery has produced more effect on intelligent minds in subsequent generations than the archery of [[Voltaire]]" (006-0735). This comparison captures Gibbon's distinctive contribution: where Voltaire attacked with wit, polemic, and fury, Gibbon advanced by means of irony, meticulous scholarship, and the quiet accumulation of damning detail. His method proved harder to refute because it appeared to be nothing more than impartial history.

Gibbon's influence also operated through his memorable phrases. His description of Anglican clergy who "subscribed with a sigh or a smile" the articles of faith became proverbial for the intellectual dishonesty required by religious conformity (007-0491). His characterization of the Inquisitors as men who "defended nonsense by cruelties" distilled a moral indictment into four words (003-0212).

## Legacy and Influence

The *Decline and Fall* set a standard for critical historical writing that influenced generations of scholars. Though Gibbon had not the advantage of the minute critical labours which in the following century were expended on his sources of information, his work remains a landmark of Enlightenment historiography (006-0731).

Gibbon's method of treating religious history as subject to the same laws of evidence as secular history became a permanent acquisition of the intellectual culture. His work, along with that of [[Hume]] and [[Middleton]], completed the eighteenth-century critique of revealed religion, preparing the ground for the nineteenth-century rationalist historians who applied similar methods to biblical texts. The [[progress]] of rationalism in the century after Gibbon was built on foundations his scholarship had helped secure.
