## Definition and Origins

Deism is the belief in a supreme being who created the universe and established its natural laws, but who does not intervene through miracles, revelation, or special providence. Deists reject the authority of scripture, the divinity of Christ, and the supernatural elements of traditional religion, relying instead on reason and observation of the natural world to understand the divine.

Deism emerged from the collision of the Scientific Revolution with Christian theology. The discovery of natural laws governing the physical universe suggested a divine clockmaker who set the cosmos in motion and then stepped back. This "natural religion" was seen as the original, uncorrupted form of religion, later degraded by priestcraft and superstition. The progress of rationalism, within which deism arose, "falls naturally" into the period of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. (006-003)

## Historical Development

Deism flourished in England in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, where thinkers like John Toland, Matthew Tindal, and Anthony Collins published works arguing for a religion based on reason alone. Tindal's *Christianity as Old as the Creation* (1730) became known as the "Deist's Bible." Toleration was extended "to deists, but not to atheists" during this period. (005-016) These English deists laid the intellectual foundations that would influence the French Enlightenment.

In France, Voltaire championed deism as a rational alternative to both orthodox Christianity and atheism. He was a deist, but his deism, unlike Voltaire, "was" of a distinctly French character, militant and anticlerical. (006-553) During the French Revolution, deism received official recognition: the Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789) invoked the Supreme Being, and Robespierre's Cult of the Supreme Being attempted to establish deism as a civic religion.

Deism declined as a distinct movement in the nineteenth century, absorbed into broader currents of rationalism, Unitarianism, and agnosticism. Some freethinkers "had also come to reject deism" entirely, moving toward more radical positions. (006-589) However, its influence persisted in the separation of church and state and in the principle that religious belief should be based on reason rather than authority.

## Key Proponents

John Toland's *Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696) argued that nothing in the Gospels was contrary to or above reason. Matthew Tindal's *Christianity as Old as the Creation* asserted that natural religion was complete and perfect, rendering special revelation unnecessary. Voltaire spent his life arguing for deism against both orthodox Christianity and atheism, famously declaring that if God did not exist, it would be necessary to invent him.

In America, Thomas Jefferson and Benjamin Franklin were strongly influenced by deist ideas, and deism shaped the religious neutrality of the U.S. Constitution.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Deism served as a crucial halfway house between orthodox Christianity and freethought. By accepting the existence of God while rejecting revelation and miracles, deists could criticize religious institutions and doctrines from within a broadly theistic framework. This made deist arguments more accessible and less threatening than outright atheism, allowing rationalist ideas to reach audiences that would have rejected more radical positions.

Deism contributed directly to the development of religious toleration, the separation of church and state, and the principle that religious belief is a matter of individual reason rather than institutional authority.
