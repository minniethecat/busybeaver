# Socinianism

Socinianism was a rationalist theological movement of the Reformation era that, according to J. B. Bury, was among the most significant contributors to the cause of religious toleration. While mainstream Reformation leaders like Luther and Calvin retained the principle of coercion in matters of faith, the Socinians broke decisively with that tradition. Bury describes them as "one sect of Reformers, who in the eyes of all the others were blasphemers and of whom most people never think when they talk of the Reformation" (004-222), yet their influence on the history of freedom of thought was profound and far-reaching.

## Definition and Origins

Socinianism takes its name from Fausto Sozzini (1539–1604), known in Latin as Socinus, an Italian theologian who fled persecution to find refuge first in Transylvania and then in Poland. The movement grew out of Italian anti-Trinitarianism, which the Reformation had carried beyond the Alps: "The Reformation movement had spread to Italy, but Rome was successful in suppressing it, and many heretics fled to Switzerland" (005-025). When Calvin's Geneva proved no safer for anti-Trinitarians — Servetus was burned there in 1553 — "the anti-Trinitarian group were forced by the intolerance of Calvin to flee to Transylvania and Poland where they propagated their doctrines" (005-028).

Socinus gave this diaspora community its definitive theological shape: "The Unitarian creed was moulded by Fausto Sozzini, generally known as Socinus, and in the catechism of his sect (1574) persecution is condemned" (005-030). This repudiation of force in religion was not a tactical concession but a logical consequence of Socinian principles.

## Historical Development

The crucial difference between Socinianism and the magisterial Reformation lay in its attitude toward individual judgment. Bury explains that "unlike Luther and Calvin, the Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism would have been inconsistent with its principles" (005-033). In other words, "there was a strong rationalistic element which was lacking in the Trinitarian creeds" (005-036). Because Socinians believed that Scripture must be interpreted by each believer's reason, they could not logically coerce belief — coercion would contradict the rational freedom that their hermeneutics required.

> Unlike Luther and Calvin, the Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism would have been inconsistent with its principles.
> (005-034)

The Socinian influence on the broader toleration movement was immediate and decisive. Bury notes that "it was under the influence of the Socinian spirit that Castellion of Savoy sounded the trumpet of toleration in a pamphlet denouncing the burning of Servetus, whereby he earned the malignant hatred of Calvin" (005-038). Castellion "maintained the innocence of error and ridiculed the importance which the Churches laid on obscure questions such as predestination and the Trinity" (005-041).

Socinianism's influence radiated outward from Poland into the wider Protestant world. "For a long time the Socinians and those who came under their influence when, driven from Poland, they passed into Germany and Holland, were the only sects which advocated toleration" (005-048). From them, the principle of toleration "was adopted by the Anabaptists and by the Arminian section of the Reformed Church of Holland" (005-051). And in Holland, "the founder of the English Congregationalists, who (under the name of Independents) played such an important part in the history of the Civil War and the Commonwealth, learned the principle of liberty of conscience" (005-053).

This intellectual genealogy — from Socinus, through the Dutch Arminians, to the English Independents — demonstrates the Socinian origins of one of the most important ideas in modern political thought. Bury traces this lineage directly: the religious liberty established in Rhode Island by Roger Williams "had been ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus" (005-110).

## Key Proponents

- **Fausto Sozzini (Socinus)** (1539–1604): Italian theologian who systematized anti-Trinitarian doctrine and made toleration a core principle. Bury notes that "Socinus thought that this principle could be realized without abolishing the State Church" (005-056), envisaging "a close union between the State and the prevailing Church, combined with complete toleration for other sects" (005-058).
- **Sebastian Castellion** (1515–1563): Savoyard humanist who, under Socinian influence, wrote a landmark pamphlet against the execution of Servetus, earning Calvin's lasting enmity (005-038).
- **The Polish Brethren** (Ecclesia Minor): The organized Socinian church in Poland, which flourished until its expulsion in 1658, after which Socinians dispersed into Germany, Holland, and Transylvania.
- **John Biddle** (1615–1662): English Unitarian who translated Socinian works and was imprisoned under the Commonwealth, sometimes called the "father of English Unitarianism."

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Socinianism contributed to freedom of thought in three foundational ways. First, it established the **logical connection between rationalism and toleration**. By insisting that the individual must exercise judgment in interpreting Scripture, Socinianism made persecution not merely wrong but *incoherent*: you cannot simultaneously insist that each person must reason for themselves and then punish them for the conclusions they reach.

Second, Socinianism was the **historical bridge** between the limited toleration debates of the Reformation and the full-fledged liberty of conscience of the Enlightenment. Bury traces a direct line: the Socinian spirit inspired Castellion; the Socinian-influenced Dutch Arminians taught the English Independents; and through the Independents, the principle entered the mainstream of English and American political thought, culminating in Rhode Island's religious freedom, which was "ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus" (005-110).

Third, Socinianism proposed an institutional model for religious liberty. Unlike the Anabaptists, who wanted complete separation of church and state, "Socinus thought that this principle could be realized without abolishing the State Church" (005-056). His model — a state church combined with full toleration for dissenters — was what Bury calls the "jurisdictional" system, which became the path by which "religious liberty has been realized in European States" (005-060).

## Criticism and Opposition

Socinianism was denounced as heresy by Catholics, Lutherans, and Calvinists alike. In Poland, the Catholic reaction of the Counter-Reformation led to the expulsion of the Socinians in 1658. In Protestant lands, Socinians were often subject to legal disabilities and social ostracism. Yet the Socinian ideas proved harder to suppress than the Socinian communities: the principle of toleration they pioneered outlived the organized movement and became a cornerstone of liberal modernity.
