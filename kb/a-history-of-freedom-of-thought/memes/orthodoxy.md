## Definition and Origins

Orthodoxy, derived from the Greek *orthos* (right) and *doxa* (opinion), denotes the set of beliefs that religious authorities declare to be correct and binding. In J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, orthodoxy represents the institutional enforcement of doctrinal conformity—the standard against which heresy is defined and punished. Bury traces how the demand for orthodoxy shaped European intellectual life from the establishment of Christianity to the modern era.

The concept emerged when the early Christian church defined its core doctrines in opposition to competing interpretations. Once Christianity became the state religion of the Roman Empire, orthodoxy became a matter of law. Bury observes that by "the fifth century unorthodoxy spread considerably among the educated" classes, indicating that intellectual elites were already chafing against enforced conformity (002-149).

## Historical Development

The medieval Church perfected the machinery of orthodoxy enforcement. Bury describes how "zeal for orthodoxy, had an inconvenient instinct for scientific" inquiry that it could not entirely suppress (003-265). The tension between the demand for doctrinal conformity and the human impulse toward inquiry created a persistent conflict that Bury traces through subsequent centuries.

The Reformation did not abolish orthodoxy but multiplied it. Protestant churches established their own orthodox standards, and Bury notes that "the teaching of mediaeval orthodoxy" was challenged but "there was no explosion of" freedom—rather, one set of dogmas was replaced by another (004-029). Different regions enforced different orthodoxies, but the principle that the state should compel religious conformity remained largely unchallenged through the sixteenth century.

The Enlightenment marked the beginning of orthodoxy's decline as a social and legal institution. Thinkers began to argue openly "against the tyranny of orthodoxy—though they did not always avow" their full views for fear of persecution (006-139). Bury documents how some writers "never dropped the disguise of orthodoxy, and this" strategy of concealment itself testifies to the power orthodoxy still wielded (006-147).

The nineteenth century saw orthodoxy increasingly on the defensive. Scientific discoveries produced "results disquieting for orthodoxy" (007-0252), and historical criticism of the Bible undermined the intellectual foundations of traditional doctrine. Bury charts the gradual shift from a society in which orthodoxy was legally enforced to one in which it became merely one option among many.

## Key Proponents and Critics

- **Augustine of Hippo** developed the theological framework that justified the coercion of heretics, arguing that physical punishment could save souls. His reasoning underpinned centuries of orthodoxy enforcement.
- **John Calvin** established a rigorous system of doctrinal enforcement in Geneva, where deviation from orthodoxy was punished by death in the case of Servetus. Bury documents "Calvin's warfare against heresy" as a notorious example of orthodoxy's violence (004-144).
- **John Locke** argued in his *Letter Concerning Toleration* that civil authorities had no jurisdiction over religious belief, providing the philosophical foundation for orthodoxy's legal retreat.
- **David Friedrich Strauss** and other biblical critics subjected orthodox doctrine to historical scrutiny, showing that it rested on demonstrably human foundations (007-0252).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Orthodoxy was the primary institutional mechanism through which freedom of thought was suppressed in pre-modern Europe. By defining a fixed set of acceptable beliefs and punishing deviation, it created an intellectual environment in which independent inquiry was dangerous and conformity was rewarded.

Bury's analysis emphasizes that orthodoxy "made against authority in" intellectual matters, establishing the principle that truth was determined by institutional power rather than by evidence and reason (006-556). This inversion of the natural relationship between inquiry and belief had profound consequences for the development of science, philosophy, and literature.

The weakening of orthodoxy's grip was gradual and contested. Bury documents how thinkers navigated between "altogether, and the old orthodoxy, there are many gradations of" belief and unbelief (007-0481). The emergence of liberal theology, which "sought to interpret orthodoxy in such a" way as to accommodate modern knowledge, represented an attempt to preserve religious authority while accepting intellectual progress (007-0503).

The ultimate defeat of enforced orthodoxy—the recognition that belief must be free—represents one of the central achievements Bury celebrates in his history of freedom of thought.
