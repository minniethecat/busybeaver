## Definition and Origins

Creeds — formally authorized statements of religious doctrine — served, in Bury's account, as the institutional mechanism for defining and enforcing intellectual conformity in Christian Europe.

A creed transforms theological opinion into mandatory belief, drawing a sharp boundary between the acceptable and the heretical. Bury presents creeds not as neutral summaries of faith but as instruments of power.

Bury dryly notes the role that the "practice of advertising" has played in "establishing authoritative opinions and propagating religious creeds" (001-267), observing that "reason fortunately is able to avail herself of the same help."

## Historical Development

In the pre-Christian Roman Empire, the state was generally tolerant of diverse cults. "A State, with an official religion, but perfectly tolerant of all creeds and cults" was the ideal of some ancient arrangements (002-487).

Christianity introduced a fundamentally different model: a religion "uncompromisingly hostile to all creeds but its own and which, if it had the power, would suppress all but its own" (002-489). This exclusivist logic made the creed a weapon of intellectual warfare.

The Protestant Reformation did not abolish creeds — it multiplied them. Bury's judgment is unambiguous: "Nor did the Protestant creeds represent enlightenment. The Reformation on the Continent was as hostile to enlightenment as it was to liberty" (004-165).

However, the Reformation inadvertently undermined creedal authority. Bury observes that "the inspired record on which the creeds depend became an open book. Public attention was directed to it as never before" (004-206).

Once individuals began reading the Bible independently, they noticed discrepancies between the text and the creeds that claimed to summarize it. This tension became a powerful engine of free inquiry.

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries saw a gradual movement away from strict credal enforcement. Governments found themselves "compelled to mitigate their maintenance of one religious creed by measures of relief to other Christian sects, and the principle of exclusiveness was broken down" (004-398).

The fragmentation of Christendom into competing creeds paradoxically weakened credal authority. When multiple creeds each claimed exclusive truth, thoughtful observers began questioning whether any creed could be infallible.

By the Enlightenment, creeds were increasingly seen as human artifacts. Bury records how "experiments in new creeds" (005-453) proliferated, and the notion that religious truth could be captured in a fixed formula came under sustained attack.

## Key Proponents and Critics

### Defenders of Creeds

Creeds were defended on the grounds that they preserved doctrinal purity and maintained social cohesion. Religious authorities argued that without binding creeds, Christianity would dissolve into doctrinal chaos.

### Critics of Creeds

- **John Locke** argued the state had no business enforcing any creed, since sincere belief cannot be compelled.
- **John Milton** insisted that truth could only be discovered through free inquiry, not through submission to inherited formulas.
- **Baruch Spinoza** subjected credal statements to rational analysis, treating them as historical products rather than revealed truths.

Voltaire expressed "implacable hatred of creeds and tyrants" (007-0673), arguing that there was "not only no article in the creeds" (007-0692) that could justify persecution.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Creeds represent the institutionalization of intellectual conformity. By defining what must be believed, they simultaneously define what may not be questioned.

Bury's account suggests that the most effective solvent of credal authority was practical experience. The coexistence of multiple competing creeds within a single society demonstrated that sincere religious conviction could take many forms.

The eventual recognition that no article in any creed could justify persecution marked a decisive victory for freedom of thought over credal compulsion. Every advance in intellectual liberty required either modifying existing creeds, multiplying creeds to mutual neutralization, or rejecting credal authority altogether.
