# René Descartes

**René Descartes** (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist whose methodological rationalism helped establish the intellectual foundations of modern [[freethought]].

By excluding authority from the domain of scientific inquiry and insisting that knowledge must be built upon the independent exercise of reason, Descartes contributed decisively to the shift away from theological and traditional authority that defined the [[Scientific Revolution]] and the early [[Enlightenment]].

## Life and Background

Descartes was born in La Haye en Touraine, France, and educated at the Jesuit college of La Flèche. After studying law at Poitiers, he traveled through Europe and served as a soldier in the Dutch and Bavarian armies.

He spent most of his productive years in the Dutch Republic (1628–1649), where relative intellectual freedom allowed him to publish his major works. In 1649 he accepted an invitation from Queen Christina of Sweden, but fell ill and died in Stockholm the following year.

## Major Contributions

Descartes made lasting contributions across multiple disciplines. In mathematics, he developed analytic geometry, linking algebra and geometry through the Cartesian coordinate system. In philosophy, his *Discourse on Method* (1637) and *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641) established a new approach to knowledge based on systematic doubt and the primacy of reason.

His famous principle *cogito ergo sum* ("I think, therefore I am") placed the thinking self at the foundation of all certain knowledge. His mechanistic physics and cosmology challenged the Aristotelian framework that had dominated medieval thought.

## Excluding Authority from Science

Bury highlights Descartes as a pivotal figure in the movement to remove authority from the domain of scientific investigation:

> [006-029] scientific investigation which he did so much to stimulate. Descartes, illustrious not only as the founder of modern metaphysics but also by his original contributions to science, might seek to conciliate the orthodox, but the whole spirit of his writings was to exclude authority from the domain of scientific investigation.

Although Descartes personally sought to avoid direct conflict with the Church — he suppressed his cosmological treatise *The World* after learning of Galileo's condemnation — the methodological principle of his philosophy was inherently subversive of all appeals to authority.

By insisting that knowledge must be derived from reason rather than from tradition or revelation, Descartes provided a philosophical charter for the independence of scientific thought. Bury places him alongside [[Francis Bacon]] and [[John Locke]] as key figures in establishing the principle that reason, not authority, is the arbiter of truth.

## Influence on Spinoza

Descartes's philosophy was a major influence on the more radical rationalism of [[Baruch Spinoza]]:

> [006-077] Descartes and (in political speculation) to Hobbes, but his philosophy meant a far wider and more open breach with orthodox opinion than either of his masters had ventured on.

Spinoza adopted Descartes's commitment to geometric reasoning and rational demonstration, but pushed these principles to conclusions — including the identification of God with Nature and the denial of miracles — that went far beyond what Descartes himself had been willing to defend publicly.

## Legacy and Influence

Descartes's legacy in the history of [[freethought]] lies less in his personal religious views than in the method he established. His insistence that reason, not authority, is the arbiter of truth became a foundational principle of the Enlightenment and of liberal modernity.

The Cartesian tradition was carried forward by [[Baruch Spinoza]] and [[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz]], establishing a current in which religious claims were subject to rational scrutiny. His significance is reflected in his index entry on pages 129 and 131 [999-054].
