## Chapter I
### FREEDOM OF THOUGHT AND THE FORCES AGAINST IT
(INTRODUCTORY)

[001-001]IT is a common saying that thought is free. A man can never be hindered

[001-002]from thinking whatever he chooses so long as he conceals what he thinks.

[001-003]The working of his mind is limited only by the bounds of his experience

[001-004]and the power of his imagination. But this natural liberty of private

[001-005]thinking is of little value. It is unsatisfactory and even painful to

[001-006]the thinker himself, if he is not permitted to communicate his thoughts

[001-007]to others, and it is obviously of no value to his neighbours. Moreover

[001-008]it is extremely difficult to hide thoughts that have any power over the

[001-009]mind. If a man’s thinking leads him to call in question ideas and

[001-010]customs which regulate the behaviour of those about him, to reject

[001-011]beliefs which they hold, to see better ways of life than those they

[001-012]follow, it is almost

[001-013][8] impossible for him, if he is convinced of the truth of his own

[001-014]reasoning, not to betray by silence, chance words, or general attitude

[001-015]that he is different from them and does not share their opinions. Some

[001-016]have preferred, like Socrates, some would prefer to-day, to face death

[001-017]rather than conceal their thoughts. Thus freedom of thought, in any

[001-018]valuable sense, includes freedom of speech.

[001-019]At present, in the most civilized countries, freedom of speech is taken

[001-020]as a matter of course and seems a perfectly simple thing. We are so

[001-021]accustomed to it that we look on it as a natural right. But this right

[001-022]has been acquired only in quite recent times, and the way to its

[001-023]attainment has lain through lakes of blood. It has taken centuries to

[001-024]persuade the most enlightened peoples that liberty to publish one’s

[001-025]opinions and to discuss all questions is a good and not a bad thing.

[001-026]Human societies (there are some brilliant exceptions) have been

[001-027]generally opposed to freedom of thought, or, in other words, to new

[001-028]ideas, and it is easy to see why.

[001-029]The average brain is naturally lazy and tends to take the line of least

[001-030]resistance. The mental world of the ordinary man consists of beliefs

[001-031]which he has accepted without questioning and to which he is firmly

[001-032]attached; he is instinctively hostile to anything which

[001-033][9] would upset the established order of this familiar world. A new

[001-034]idea, inconsistent with some of the beliefs which he holds, means the

[001-035]necessity of rearranging his mind; and this process is laborious,

[001-036]requiring a painful expenditure of brain-energy. To him and his fellows,

[001-037]who form the vast majority, new ideas, and opinions which cast doubt on

[001-038]established beliefs and institutions, seem evil because they are

[001-039]disagreeable.

[001-040]The repugnance due to mere mental laziness is increased by a positive

[001-041]feeling of fear. The conservative instinct hardens into the conservative

[001-042]doctrine that the foundations of society are endangered by any

[001-043]alterations in the structure. It is only recently that men have been

[001-044]abandoning the belief that the welfare of a state depends on rigid

[001-045]stability and on the preservation of its traditions and institutions

[001-046]unchanged. Wherever that belief prevails, novel opinions are felt to be

[001-047]dangerous as well as annoying, and any one who asks inconvenient

[001-048]questions about the why and the wherefore of accepted principles is

[001-049]considered a pestilent person.

[001-050]The conservative instinct, and the conservative doctrine which is its

[001-051]consequence, are strengthened by superstition. If the social structure,

[001-052]including the whole body of customs and opinions, is associated

[001-053]intimately

[001-054][10] with religious belief and is supposed to be under divine patronage,

[001-055]criticism of the social order savours of impiety, while criticism of the

[001-056]religious belief is a direct challenge to the wrath of supernatural

[001-057]powers.

[001-058]The psychological motives which produce a conservative spirit hostile to

[001-059]new ideas are reinforced by the active opposition of certain powerful

[001-060]sections of the community, such as a class, a caste, or a priesthood,

[001-061]whose interests are bound up with the maintenance of the established

[001-062]order and the ideas on which it rests.

[001-063]Let us suppose, for instance, that a people believes that solar eclipses

[001-064]are signs employed by their Deity for the special purpose of

[001-065]communicating useful information to them, and that a clever man

[001-066]discovers the true cause of eclipses. His compatriots in the first place

[001-067]dislike his discovery because they find it very difficult to reconcile

[001-068]with their other ideas; in the second place, it disturbs them, because

[001-069]it upsets an arrangement which they consider highly advantageous to

[001-070]their community; finally, it frightens them, as an offence to their

[001-071]Divinity. The priests, one of whose functions is to interpret the divine

[001-072]signs, are alarmed and enraged at a doctrine which menaces their power.

[001-073]In prehistoric days, these motives, operating

[001-074][11] strongly, must have made change slow in communities which

[001-075]progressed, and hindered some communities from progressing at all. But

[001-076]they have continued to operate more or less throughout history,

[001-077]obstructing knowledge and progress. We can observe them at work to-day

[001-078]even in the most advanced societies, where they have no longer the power

[001-079]to arrest development or repress the publication of revolutionary

[001-080]opinions. We still meet people who consider a new idea an annoyance and

[001-081]probably a danger. Of those to whom socialism is repugnant, how many are

[001-082]there who have never examined the arguments for and against it, but turn

[001-083]away in disgust simply because the notion disturbs their mental universe

[001-084]and implies a drastic criticism on the order of things to which they are

[001-085]accustomed? And how many are there who would refuse to consider any

[001-086]proposals for altering our imperfect matrimonial institutions, because

[001-087]such an idea offends a mass of prejudice associated with religious

[001-088]sanctions? They may be right or not, but if they are, it is not their

[001-089]fault. They are actuated by the same motives which were a bar to

[001-090]progress in primitive societies. The existence of people of this

[001-091]mentality, reared in an atmosphere of freedom, side by side with others

[001-092]who are always looking out for new ideas and

[001-093][12] regretting that there are not more about, enables us to realize

[001-094]how, when public opinion was formed by the views of such men, thought

[001-095]was fettered and the impediments to knowledge enormous.

[001-096]Although the liberty to publish one’s opinions on any subject without

[001-097]regard to authority or the prejudices of one’s neighbours is now a well-

[001-098]established principle, I imagine that only the minority of those who

[001-099]would be ready to fight to the death rather than surrender it could

[001-100]defend it on rational grounds. We are apt to take for granted that

[001-101]freedom of speech is a natural and inalienable birthright of man, and

[001-102]perhaps to think that this is a sufficient answer to all that can be

[001-103]said on the other side. But it is difficult to see how such a right can

[001-104]be established.

[001-105]If a man has any “natural rights,” the right to preserve his life and

[001-106]the right to reproduce his kind are certainly such. Yet human societies

[001-107]impose upon their members restrictions in the exercise of both these

[001-108]rights. A starving man is prohibited from taking food which belongs to

[001-109]somebody else. Promiscuous reproduction is restricted by various laws or

[001-110]customs. It is admitted that society is justified in restricting these

[001-111]elementary rights, because without such restrictions an ordered society

[001-112]could not exist. If then we

[001-113][13] concede that the expression of opinion is a right of the same kind,

[001-114]it is impossible to contend that on this ground it can claim immunity

[001-115]from interference or that society acts unjustly in regulating it. But

[001-116]the concession is too large. For whereas in the other cases the

[001-117]limitations affect the conduct of every one, restrictions on freedom of

[001-118]opinion affect only the comparatively small number who have any

[001-119]opinions, revolutionary or unconventional, to express. The truth is that

[001-120]no valid argument can be founded on the conception of natural rights,

[001-121]because it involves an untenable theory of the relations between society

[001-122]and its members.

[001-123]On the other hand, those who have the responsibility of governing a

[001-124]society can argue that it is as incumbent on them to prohibit the

[001-125]circulation of pernicious opinions as to prohibit any anti-social

[001-126]actions. They can argue that a man may do far more harm by propagating

[001-127]anti-social doctrines than by stealing his neighbour’s horse or making

[001-128]love to his neighbour’s wife. They are responsible for the welfare of

[001-129]the State, and if they are convinced that an opinion is dangerous, by

[001-130]menacing the political, religious, or moral assumptions on which the

[001-131]society is based, it is their duty to protect society against it, as

[001-132]against any other danger.

[001-133][14]

[001-134]The true answer to this argument for limiting freedom of thought will

[001-135]appear in due course. It was far from obvious. A long time was needed to

[001-136]arrive at the conclusion that coercion of opinion is a mistake, and only

[001-137]a part of the world is yet convinced. That conclusion, so far as I can

[001-138]judge, is the most important ever reached by men. It was the issue of a

[001-139]continuous struggle between authority and reason—the subject of this

[001-140]volume. The word authority requires some comment.

[001-141]If you ask somebody how he knows something, he may say, “I have it on

[001-142]good authority,” or, “I read it in a book,” or, “It is a matter of

[001-143]common knowledge,” or, “I learned it at school.” Any of these replies

[001-144]means that he has accepted information from others, trusting in their

[001-145]knowledge, without verifying their statements or thinking the matter out

[001-146]for himself. And the greater part of most men’s knowledge and beliefs is

[001-147]of this kind, taken without verification from their parents, teachers,

[001-148]acquaintances, books, newspapers. When an English boy learns French, he

[001-149]takes the conjugations and the meanings of the words on the authority of

[001-150]his teacher or his grammar. The fact that in a certain place, marked on

[001-151]the map, there is a populous city called Calcutta, is for most

[001-152][15] people a fact accepted on authority. So is the existence of

[001-153]Napoleon or Julius Caesar. Familiar astronomical facts are known only in

[001-154]the same way, except by those who have studied astronomy. It is obvious

[001-155]that every one’s knowledge would be very limited indeed, if we were not

[001-156]justified in accepting facts on the authority of others.

[001-157]But we are justified only under one condition. The facts which we can

[001-158]safely accept must be capable of demonstration or verification. The

[001-159]examples I have given belong to this class. The boy can verify when he

[001-160]goes to France or is able to read a French book that the facts which he

[001-161]took on authority are true. I am confronted every day with evidence

[001-162]which proves to me that, if I took the trouble, I could verify the

[001-163]existence of Calcutta for myself. I cannot convince myself in this way

[001-164]of the existence of Napoleon, but if I have doubts about it, a simple

[001-165]process of reasoning shows me that there are hosts of facts which are

[001-166]incompatible with his non-existence. I have no doubt that the earth is

[001-167]some 93 millions of miles distant from the sun, because all astronomers

[001-168]agree that it has been demonstrated, and their agreement is only

[001-169]explicable on the supposition that this has been demonstrated and that,

[001-170]if I took the trouble to work out the calculation, I should reach the

[001-171]same result.

[001-172][16]

[001-173]But all our mental furniture is not of this kind. The thoughts of the

[001-174]average man consist not only of facts open to verification, but also of

[001-175]many beliefs and opinions which he has accepted on authority and cannot

[001-176]verify or prove. Belief in the Trinity depends on the authority of the

[001-177]Church and is clearly of a different order from belief in the existence

[001-178]of Calcutta. We cannot go behind the authority and verify or prove it.

[001-179]If we accept it, we do so because we have such implicit faith in the

[001-180]authority that we credit its assertions though incapable of proof.

[001-181]The distinction may seem so obvious as to be hardly worth making. But it

[001-182]is important to be quite clear about it. The primitive man who had

[001-183]learned from his elders that there were bears in the hills and likewise

[001-184]evil spirits, soon verified the former statement by seeing a bear, but

[001-185]if he did not happen to meet an evil spirit, it did not occur to him,

[001-186]unless he was a prodigy, that there was a distinction between the two

[001-187]statements; he would rather have argued, if he argued at all, that as

[001-188]his tribesmen were right about the bears they were sure to be right also

[001-189]about the spirits. In the Middle Ages a man who believed on authority

[001-190]that there is a city called Constantinople and that comets are portents

[001-191]signifying divine wrath, would not

[001-192][17] distinguish the nature of the evidence in the two cases. You may

[001-193]still sometimes hear arguments amounting to this: since I believe in

[001-194]Calcutta on authority, am I not entitled to believe in the Devil on

[001-195]authority?

[001-196]Now people at all times have been commanded or expected or invited to

[001-197]accept on authority alone—the authority, for instance, of public

[001-198]opinion, or a Church, or a sacred book—doctrines which are not proved or

[001-199]are not capable of proof. Most beliefs about nature and man, which were

[001-200]not founded on scientific observation, have served directly or

[001-201]indirectly religious and social interests, and hence they have been

[001-202]protected by force against the criticisms of persons who have the

[001-203]inconvenient habit of using their reason. Nobody minds if his neighbour

[001-204]disbelieves a demonstrable fact. If a sceptic denies that Napoleon

[001-205]existed, or that water is composed of oxygen and hydrogen, he causes

[001-206]amusement or ridicule. But if he denies doctrines which cannot be

[001-207]demonstrated, such as the existence of a personal God or the immortality

[001-208]of the soul, he incurs serious disapprobation and at one time he might

[001-209]have been put to death. Our mediaeval friend would have only been called

[001-210]a fool if he doubted the existence of Constantinople, but if he had

[001-211]questioned the significance of comets he

[001-212][18] might have got into trouble. It is possible that if he had been so

[001-213]mad as to deny the existence of Jerusalem he would not have escaped with

[001-214]ridicule, for Jerusalem is mentioned in the Bible.

[001-215]In the Middle Ages a large field was covered by beliefs which authority

[001-216]claimed to impose as true, and reason was warned off the ground. But

[001-217]reason cannot recognize arbitrary prohibitions or barriers, without

[001-218]being untrue to herself. The universe of experience is her province, and

[001-219]as its parts are all linked together and interdependent, it is

[001-220]impossible for her to recognize any territory on which she may not

[001-221]tread, or to surrender any of her rights to an authority whose

[001-222]credentials she has not examined and approved.

[001-223]The uncompromising assertion by reason of her absolute rights throughout

[001-224]the whole domain of thought is termed rationalism, and the slight stigma

[001-225]which is still attached to the word reflects the bitterness of the

[001-226]struggle between reason and the forces arrayed against her. The term is

[001-227]limited to the field of theology, because it was in that field that the

[001-228]self-assertion of reason was most violently and pertinaciously opposed.

[001-229]In the same way free thought, the refusal of thought to be controlled by

[001-230]any authority but its own, has a definitely theological reference.

[001-231]Throughout

[001-232][19] the conflict, authority has had great advantages. At any time the

[001-233]people who really care about reason have been a small minority, and

[001-234]probably will be so for a long time to come. Reason’s only weapon has

[001-235]been argument. Authority has employed physical and moral violence, legal

[001-236]coercion and social displeasure. Sometimes she has attempted to use the

[001-237]sword of her adversary, thereby wounding herself. Indeed the weakest

[001-238]point in the strategical position of authority was that her champions,

[001-239]being human, could not help making use of reasoning processes and the

[001-240]result was that they were divided among themselves. This gave reason her

[001-241]chance. Operating, as it were, in the enemy’s camp and professedly in

[001-242]the enemy’s cause, she was preparing her own victory.

[001-243]It may be objected that there is a legitimate domain for authority,

[001-244]consisting of doctrines which lie outside human experience and therefore

[001-245]cannot be proved or verified, but at the same time cannot be disproved.

[001-246]Of course, any number of propositions can be invented which cannot be

[001-247]disproved, and it is open to any one who possesses exuberant faith to

[001-248]believe them; but no one will maintain that they all deserve credence so

[001-249]long as their falsehood is not demonstrated. And if only some deserve

[001-250]credence, who, except reason,

[001-251][20] is to decide which? If the reply is, Authority, we are confronted

[001-252]by the difficulty that many beliefs backed by authority have been

[001-253]finally disproved and are universally abandoned. Yet some people speak

[001-254]as if we were not justified in rejecting a theological doctrine unless

[001-255]we can prove it false. But the burden of proof does not lie upon the

[001-256]rejecter. I remember a conversation in which, when some disrespectful

[001-257]remark was made about hell, a loyal friend of that establishment said

[001-258]triumphantly, “But, absurd as it may seem, you cannot disprove it.” If

[001-259]you were told that in a certain planet revolving round Sirius there is a

[001-260]race of donkeys who talk the English language and spend their time in

[001-261]discussing eugenics, you could not disprove the statement, but would it,

[001-262]on that account, have any claim to be believed? Some minds would be

[001-263]prepared to accept it, if it were reiterated often enough, through the

[001-264]potent force of suggestion. This force, exercised largely by emphatic

[001-265]repetition (the theoretical basis, as has been observed, of the modern

[001-266]practice of advertising), has played a great part in establishing

[001-267]authoritative opinions and propagating religious creeds. Reason

[001-268]fortunately is able to avail herself of the same help.

[001-269]The following sketch is confined to Western

[001-270][21] civilization. It begins with Greece and attempts to indicate the

[001-271]chief phases. It is the merest introduction to a vast and intricate

[001-272]subject, which, treated adequately, would involve not only the history

[001-273]of religion, of the Churches, of heresies, of persecution, but also the

[001-274]history of philosophy, of the natural sciences and of political

[001-275]theories. From the sixteenth century to the French Revolution nearly all

[001-276]important historical events bore in some way on the struggle for freedom

[001-277]of thought. It would require a lifetime to calculate, and many books to

[001-278]describe, all the directions and interactions of the intellectual and

[001-279]social forces which, since the fall of ancient civilization, have

[001-280]hindered and helped the emancipation of reason. All one can do, all one

[001-281]could do even in a much bigger volume than this, is to indicate the

[001-282]general course of the struggle and dwell on some particular aspects

[001-283]which the writer may happen to have specially studied.
