## Life and Background

Conyers Middleton (1683–1750) was an English clergyman and scholar who remained within the Church of England while advancing deistic arguments. He served as principal librarian of Cambridge University and was a fellow of Trinity College. Despite his clerical position, his writings placed him among the most influential critics of orthodox Christianity in the 18th century (006-436).

## Major Contributions

Middleton's most important work was *A Free Inquiry into the Miraculous Powers Which Are Supposed to Have Subsisted in the Christian Church* (1748). In this book, he posed a deceptively simple question: at what time did the Church cease to have the power of performing miracles? The question "put in a new and dangerous light" the historical foundations of Christian supernatural claims (006-444).

Bury ranks Middleton highly among the deists, describing him as "perhaps the ablest of the deists, and certainly the most scholarly" (006-437). His approach was more nuanced than many of his contemporaries. He supported Christianity on grounds of utility, arguing that even if it is an imposture, "it would be wrong to destroy it" because of its social benefits (006-438).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Middleton's contribution to freethought was primarily methodological. By treating miracles as a historical question rather than a theological one, he shifted the terms of debate. Instead of asking whether miracles *could* happen, he asked whether there was reliable historical evidence that they *did* happen—and when they stopped.

This approach had a profound influence on later writers. Edward Gibbon, in his *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, "owed a great deal to Middleton" in his treatment of early Christian miracles (006-693).

Bury notes that Gibbon's "purely historical point of view" on miracles was "particularly disconcerting" to orthodox believers, precisely because it avoided theological argument in favour of historical scrutiny (006-692).

Middleton's *Free Inquiry*, together with David Hume's essay on miracles (also published in 1748), raised the question of whether revelation "rests on a solid historical basis" (006-746). This double assault forced Christian apologists to defend the historicity of miracles rather than merely their possibility.

## Legacy and Influence

The impact of Middleton's work is visible in the responses it provoked. William Paley's *Evidences of Christianity* (1794), "the only one of the apologies of that age which is still read," was written in large part to answer the challenges posed by Hume and Middleton (006-748).

Middleton represents an important type in the history of freethought: the insider-critic who, while formally loyal to the Church, produced arguments that eroded its intellectual foundations.

His scholarly credentials and clerical status gave his criticisms a weight that purely secular writers could not command, making him a particularly dangerous adversary to orthodoxy.
