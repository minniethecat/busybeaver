## Definition and Origins

Authority, as Bury uses the term in *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to the principle of accepting beliefs on the basis of external testimony — tradition, institutional decree, sacred texts, or the pronouncements of recognized experts — rather than through independent rational inquiry. The "continuous struggle between authority and reason" is, Bury declares in his opening chapter, "the subject of this volume" (001-139).

Bury is careful to distinguish between legitimate and illegitimate appeals to authority. When someone says "I have it on good authority" or "I read it in a book," they are making a claim that can, in principle, be verified (001-142). "The meanings of the words on the authority of" a dictionary, for instance, can be checked against usage (001-149). In everyday life, we all rely on "people accepting facts on authority" because we cannot personally verify everything (001-152). "So is the existence of" distant places like Calcutta — few people have seen it, but "we are justified in accepting facts on the authority of others" when those authorities are reliable and their claims are in principle verifiable (001-156).

The problem arises when authority claims finality in domains where verification is impossible or forbidden. "Facts taken on authority are true" only insofar as the authority is competent and honest (001-161). But in matters of religion, "the opinions which he has accepted on authority" are often beyond the reach of verification entirely (001-175). The person who "believes in the Trinity depends on the authority of the" Church, which in turn depends on a chain of tradition that cannot be independently verified (001-176). "We cannot go behind the authority and verify or prove" its claims (001-180), and yet it is solely "on authority that we credit its assertions" (001-180).

## Historical Development

The dominance of authority over reason was most complete in the medieval period. "In the Middle Ages a man who believed on authority" that the Church was the custodian of divine truth had no intellectual grounds for questioning any particular doctrine (001-189). The entire framework of knowledge was grounded in authority — Scriptural, patristic, and ecclesiastical — and the function of reason was not to evaluate this framework but to explicate it.

The analogy Bury uses is instructive. To believe "Calcutta on authority" is reasonable because one could, in principle, travel to Calcutta to verify it. But "am I not entitled to believe" in religious doctrines "on authority" in the same way (001-194)? Bury's answer is no: the difference is that the existence of Calcutta is a falsifiable claim about the observable world, while religious doctrines "we accept on authority alone — the authority, for instance, of the Church — cannot be independently verified" (001-197).

Before the rise of rationalism, "the entire world was covered by beliefs which authority" had imposed and which no one thought to question (001-215). Reason's first demand was not to disprove these beliefs but simply to ask whether they could be justified independently of the authorities that promulgated them. This questioning was itself revolutionary, because authority would "render any of her rights to an authority whose" claims she could not substantiate (001-221).

The rise of modern science represented the first major breach in the wall of authority. When reason asserted "any authority but its own," it "has a definitely" revolutionary character (001-230). In "the conflict, authority has had great advantages" — institutional power, social prestige, the force of law (001-232). Yet "the strategical position of authority was that her champions" could not engage in open debate without conceding the principle that beliefs must be defended by reason (001-238).

Bury acknowledges that "there is a legitimate domain for authority" (001-243) — in matters of established scientific fact, historical record, and practical expertise. The key principle is that legitimate authority is always open to verification, while illegitimate authority demands submission without inquiry. The recognition that "many beliefs backed by authority have been" false (001-252) is the first step toward intellectual freedom.

## Key Proponents of Authority

The defenders of authority throughout history include:

- **The Medieval Church** — The most comprehensive system of intellectual authority ever constructed, claiming divine warrant for its teachings and suppressing dissent through institutional power.
- **Religious Orthodoxy** — In all its forms (Catholic, Protestant, Islamic, Jewish), orthodoxy has maintained that revealed truth must be accepted on authority rather than independently verified.
- **Political Absolutism** — The claim that kings rule by divine right and that subjects owe unquestioning obedience was the political parallel to religious authority.

The proponents of reason who challenged authority include:

- **Socrates** — His method of relentless questioning challenged Athenians to examine beliefs they had accepted on authority, for which he was executed. [[socrates]] embodied reason's refusal to defer to established opinion.
- **Galileo Galilei** — His conflict with the Church illustrated the collision between empirical inquiry and institutional authority. [[galileo-galilei]]'s telescopic observations challenged the authority of Aristotle and the Church alike.
- **René Descartes** — His method of systematic doubt was a declaration of independence from authority, insisting that knowledge must be rebuilt from indubitable foundations. [[rene-descartes]] began with the assumption that all received opinion might be false.
- **John Locke** — Extended the critique of authority from natural philosophy to politics and religion, arguing that individuals have the right and duty to judge truth for themselves. [[locke]]'s epistemology made each person the ultimate arbiter of belief.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The struggle between authority and reason is, in Bury's framework, the master narrative of intellectual history. Every advance of freedom of thought has come through challenging the claims of some authority — religious, political, or intellectual — to dictate what must be believed. The gradual displacement of authority by reason as the ultimate ground of belief is the central achievement that Bury's history documents.

The critical breakthrough was the recognition that appeals to authority are structurally different from appeals to evidence. One who "was considered the highest authority on mythology" (002-044) might be wrong, and the accumulation of "authority" behind a belief does not make it true (002-054). The mark of free thought is the willingness to question "authority or the prejudices of one's neighbors" (001-097), to demand evidence rather than accept tradition. This principle, once established, could not be confined to any single domain but eventually challenged all forms of unquestioning deference.

Bury traces how the domain of authority gradually contracted as reason expanded. From natural philosophy (science), the critique spread to biblical interpretation, then to theology itself, and finally to the very concept of revelation. Each retreat of authority created space for free inquiry, and each advance of reason demonstrated that beliefs backed by nothing but authority were vulnerable to rational dissolution.
