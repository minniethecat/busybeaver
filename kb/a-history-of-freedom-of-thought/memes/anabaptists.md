## Definition and Origins

The Anabaptists were a radical movement within the Protestant Reformation who rejected infant baptism in favor of adult (believer's) baptism, and who advocated for the separation of church and state and the principle of religious liberty. In Bury's narrative, the Anabaptists represent a crucial but ambiguous force in the history of freedom of thought — they were pioneers of the argument for religious voluntarism, yet their own vision of society was often theocratic rather than tolerant.

The movement emerged in the 1520s in Switzerland and Germany, drawing on earlier reformist impulses but pushing the logic of Reformation principles further than mainstream Reformers were willing to go. Where Luther and Calvin sought to reform the established Church in partnership with the state, the Anabaptists rejected the very idea of a state church. Religion, they insisted, was a matter of personal conviction that could not be coerced by civil authority.

## Historical Development

The Anabaptists faced persecution from both Catholic and Protestant authorities. Bury records that one defender of orthodoxy "held that Anabaptists should be put to the sword" (004-134), reflecting the consensus among both confessions that Anabaptist ideas threatened the foundations of social order. For the magisterial Reformers, the Anabaptist rejection of state authority in religious matters was as dangerous as Catholic claims of papal supremacy.

In England, Anabaptist ideas influenced the development of religious dissent. Bury notes the role played by "Anabaptists and by the Arminian section of the Reformed Church of Holland" (005-051) in shaping English nonconformity. The founder of the English Congregationalists, John Smyth, was influenced by Dutch Anabaptist thought, and through him elements of Anabaptist ecclesiology entered the mainstream of English dissent.

Bury offers a nuanced assessment of the Anabaptist contribution to religious liberty. He observes that the Anabaptists "detested the State; and the doctrine of religious liberty was not precious to them" in the modern sense (005-063). Their vision of freedom was for the gathered church of true believers, not for society at large. "Their ideal system would have been an Anabaptist theocracy; separation was the second best" (005-065). This is a crucial distinction: the Anabaptists sought liberty for themselves from state control, but did not necessarily extend that liberty to others within their own communities.

Nevertheless, the Anabaptist insistence on the voluntariness of faith — that belief cannot be compelled and that the state has no authority over conscience — proved to be one of the most fertile ideas of the Reformation era. Bury identifies this principle, even in its incomplete Anabaptist form, as a genuine advance in the theory of religious liberty.

When Bury discusses "liberty of thought" in the context of the mid-seventeenth century, he returns to the Anabaptists as one of the groups who "contemplated this system" of intellectual freedom (005-642). Though "the Anabaptists, whose spirit was intolerant, sought Separation" rather than comprehensive toleration (005-642), their insistence on the distinction between civil and religious authority created conceptual space for the later development of full religious liberty.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### Believer's Baptism
The Anabaptist rejection of infant baptism was not merely a ritual dispute but a fundamental rethinking of the nature of the Church. If baptism required a conscious profession of faith, then the Church could not be coextensive with society. This implied a separation of the religious community from the civil community that was radical for its time.

### Separation of Church and State
The Anabaptists were among the first to argue systematically that the state had no legitimate authority over religious belief. This argument, though often made from a position of weakness by a persecuted minority, laid conceptual foundations for later liberal theories of religious freedom.

### The Ambiguity of Anabaptist Toleration
Bury's careful analysis distinguishes between the Anabaptist desire for freedom *from* persecution and a principled commitment to toleration *for* others. Within their own communities, Anabaptists enforced strict moral and doctrinal discipline. Their contribution to freedom of thought was therefore partial but genuine: they established the principle that religious conviction must be voluntary, even if they did not fully embrace the implications of that principle for those who disagreed with them.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The Anabaptists occupy a paradoxical position in the history of freedom of thought. They were victims of persecution whose suffering exemplified the cruelty of enforced religious conformity, yet they were not themselves consistent advocates of toleration. Their historical significance lies less in their own practice than in the logic of their position: once it was conceded that religious belief could not be coerced, the case for persecution was fatally undermined, even if the Anabaptists themselves did not fully draw that conclusion.

The Anabaptist insistence on the gathered church of believers, separate from the state and bound only by voluntary commitment, introduced a model of religious association fundamentally different from the territorial church of medieval Christendom. This model, transmitted through English dissent and eventually into the broader liberal tradition, contributed to the development of modern religious liberty. Bury treats the Anabaptists as a necessary stage in the evolution of freedom of thought — incomplete and inconsistent, but genuinely progressive in their historical context.
