# Cicero

**Cicero** (Marcus Tullius Cicero, 106–43 BCE) was a Roman statesman, orator, and philosopher whose writings on religion, politics, and ethics influenced thinkers from [[Michel de Montaigne]] to the [[Enlightenment]]. His utilitarian view of religion — holding that official worship served social order rather than truth — and the use of his rational writings as a benchmark against revealed scripture made him a significant figure in the history of [[freethought]].

## Life and Background

Cicero was a Roman statesman, lawyer, and philosopher active during the final decades of the Roman Republic. He held the highest Roman offices, including consul in 63 BCE, and was a prolific writer whose philosophical works transmitted Greek ideas to the Latin world. His writings on rhetoric, philosophy, and politics became foundational texts for Renaissance humanism and Enlightenment thought.

## Religion as Social Utility

Cicero's most enduring contribution to the history of freethought was his utilitarian view of religion. He recognized that the official state religion was not literally true, but believed it served a necessary social function by keeping the uneducated populace in order. This position — that a false religion might be indispensable for social stability — was common among educated unbelievers in antiquity:

> [002-340] Cicero, and the view that a false religion is indispensable as a social machine was general among ancient unbelievers.

This defense of religion on grounds of utility rather than truth would recur throughout history, later articulated by political thinkers such as Machiavelli and remaining a common argument in defenses of established religion.

## Influence on Renaissance Skepticism

Cicero's philosophical writings had a profound influence on the development of Renaissance and early modern skepticism. The sixteenth-century French essayist [[Michel de Montaigne]] was shaped more by Cicero and other ancient philosophers than by Christian doctrine. When confronting the problem of death, Montaigne turned not to Christian consolations but to the wisdom of pagan antiquity:

> [004-053] It was the philosophers and wise men of antiquity, Cicero, and Seneca, and Plutarch, who moulded and possessed his mind. It is to them, and not to the consolations of Christianity, that he turns when he discusses the problem of death.

## Cicero as a Symbol of Rational Religion

During the [[French Revolution]]'s campaign of de-Christianization in the 1790s, Cicero was elevated alongside [[Socrates]] and Marcus Aurelius as an exemplar of rational, non-Christian morality. The Revolution's secularizing program taught republican morality in place of religion, and some enthusiasts predicted that philosophical paganism would replace Christianity entirely:

> [005-424] An enthusiast declared that "the religion of Socrates, Marcus Aurelius, and Cicero would soon be the religion of the world."

## Cicero vs. Revealed Scripture

In the eighteenth-century deistic controversies in England, Cicero's writings were deployed as a benchmark against which the Bible was measured. Deist critics argued that if an uninspired pagan author could write with consistent rationality, the claim of biblical infallibility became difficult to defend. The argument, as recorded in the deist literature, ran as follows:

> [006-335] "Nay, would you not tell him that his inspired book fell infinitely short of Cicero's uninspired writings, where there is no such occasion to recede from the letter?"

## Legacy and Influence

Cicero's influence on the history of freethought operates on multiple levels. His philosophical eclecticism transmitted Greek rationalism to the Latin West. His utilitarian theory of religion became a staple argument in debates about [[religious toleration]]. His prose style and moral seriousness made him a model for Renaissance humanists, and his elevation during the Enlightenment as a symbol of virtuous paganism contributed to the argument that morality does not depend on [[Christianity]]. His writings continued to be cited by freethinkers who sought to demonstrate that reason and moral seriousness could flourish independently of revealed religion.
