## Life and Background

David Hume was born in Edinburgh, Scotland, on 7 May 1711 (Old Style: 26 April) into a family of modest landed gentry. He attended the University of Edinburgh from an unusually young age — about twelve — studying classics, philosophy, and law, though he left without taking a degree. After a brief and unsuccessful attempt at a legal career, Hume devoted himself to philosophy, spending three years (1734–1737) in France, where he composed his first major work, *A Treatise of Human Nature*.

The *Treatise* (published 1739–1740) was, by Hume's own account, poorly received — "it fell dead-born from the press." Undeterred, Hume reworked his ideas into more accessible forms: the *Philosophical Essays concerning Human Understanding* (1748, later retitled *An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding*) and the *Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751). His *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion*, composed in the 1750s, were withheld from publication during his lifetime on the advice of friends who feared the consequences; they appeared posthumously in 1779.

Hume also achieved considerable fame as a historian. His *History of England* (1754–1762) became a standard work and gave him financial independence. He served briefly as secretary to the British embassy in Paris (1763–1766), where he was celebrated in Enlightenment salons, befriending figures such as [[Rousseau]] (though the friendship ended acrimoniously). He died in Edinburgh on 25 August 1776, reported by contemporaries to have faced death with the equanimity characteristic of his philosophical scepticism.

## Major Contributions

Hume is regarded as the greatest English philosopher of the eighteenth century (006-0613). His most significant contributions to the cause of freedom of thought were his critiques of two pillars of natural theology: miracles and the argument from design.

In his *Essay on Miracles* (published within the *Philosophical Essays*, 1748), Hume subjected the credibility of miracle claims to a general examination independent of theological assumptions for the first time (006-0617). Hume pointed out that there must be uniform experience against every miraculous event — otherwise it would not merit the name of miracle — and that establishing a miracle therefore requires stronger testimony than an event not contrary to experience (006-0619). He laid down the famous maxim that "no testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle unless the testimony is of such a kind that its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact which it endeavours to establish" (006-0623).

In the *Dialogues on Natural Religion*, Hume attacked the argument from design, on which deists and Christians alike relied to prove the existence of a Deity (006-0638). The argument holds that the world presents clear marks of design — endless adaptation of means to ends — which can only be explained as due to the deliberate plan of a powerful intelligence. Hume disputed the inference on the ground that a mere intelligent being is not a sufficient cause to explain the effect: the mental system of a divine mind would itself demand an explanation of its existence just as much as the material world (006-0643). He concluded that the design argument is worse than useless for the purposes of Deism or of Christianity (006-0659).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Hume advanced freedom of thought by demonstrating that the most widely accepted rational arguments for God's existence — miracles and design — could not withstand rigorous philosophical scrutiny. His criticisms of miracles, together with those of [[Middleton]], placed the debate about the historical basis of Christianity on a new footing, forcing apologists like [[Paley]] to construct more elaborate defences (006-0747).

The inadequacy of the argument from design, as a proof of God's existence, was shown by the logic of Hume and [[Kant]] well before Darwin provided the empirical refutation (007-0097). Hume's work thus created the philosophical space within which later scientific naturalism could develop. By arguing that supernatural explanations were not merely unsupported but logically incoherent, Hume shifted the burden of proof from sceptics to believers — a reversal that proved decisive for the subsequent history of freethought.

## Legacy and Influence

Though the sceptical philosophy of Hume had less influence on the general public than [[Gibbon]]'s *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, his arguments permeated the intellectual culture of the Enlightenment and beyond (006-0660). Theologians like Paley felt compelled to answer — or conspicuously ignore — his critiques, and his demolition of the design argument became a permanent resource for rationalist writers through the nineteenth century.

Hume's influence extended to the Continent. [[Kant]] acknowledged that Hume "awoke him from his dogmatic slumber," and his empiricist methodology laid groundwork for subsequent movements in philosophy, from positivism to logical empiricism. In the context of freedom of thought, Hume demonstrated that philosophical scepticism could be deployed against religious orthodoxy without the aggressive anticlericalism of the French Enlightenment — a model of temperate but uncompromising rational inquiry.
