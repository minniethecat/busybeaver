# Frederic Harrison

**Frederic Harrison** (1831–1923) was an English jurist, literary critic, and the leading English exponent of [[Auguste Comte|Comte]]'s Positive Philosophy. In the latter half of the nineteenth century, he was one of the most indefatigable workers in the cause of [[reason]] against authority (007-0198).

## Life and Background

Harrison was educated at King's College London and Wadham College, Oxford. He was called to the bar in 1858 and practiced as a barrister while simultaneously pursuing a prolific career as a writer and public intellectual. His encounter with the philosophy of Auguste Comte during his Oxford years proved formative, and he became the most prominent English advocate of Positivism.

Harrison served on several royal commissions and was active in the trade union movement, reflecting the Positivist commitment to applying scientific principles to social reform. His legal expertise and broad intellectual range made him a respected public figure in Victorian intellectual life.

## Major Contributions

As the principal English promulgator of Comte's Positive Philosophy, Harrison worked to establish a comprehensive worldview based on science rather than theology. The Positive Philosophy — like other scientific systems — attempted to show how the whole knowable world, psychical and social as well as physical, could be understood through empirical laws (007-0198).

Harrison authored numerous books and essays on topics ranging from religion and philosophy to history and literary criticism. He was a regular contributor to leading periodicals, including the *Fortnightly Review* and the *Nineteenth Century*, where he engaged in sustained polemics against theological orthodoxy.

His essay on the social function of religious belief captured a sharp critique of ecclesiastical institutions. Harrison recounted the anecdote of a workhouse master who, called to act as chaplain to a dying pauper, warned the poor man to turn his thoughts toward hell, adding: "And thankful you ought to be that you have a hell to go to" (007-0898). The anecdote illustrated, for Harrison, the way religious institutions depended on keeping the poor superstitious in order to keep them contented.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Harrison's indefatigable work in the cause of reason placed him among the most prominent English freethinkers of the late Victorian period. He argued that the Positive Philosophy — Comte's religion of humanity — offered a rational alternative to supernatural religion, one that retained the moral and social functions of traditional faith without its untenable metaphysical claims (007-0198).

Unlike some more aggressive secularists, Harrison sought to construct a positive substitute for religion rather than simply attacking it. His Positivist church — the Church of Humanity — aimed to redirect religious sentiment toward humanity itself, replacing the worship of God with the service of mankind.

Harrison was also a sharp critic of the view that free thought is peculiarly indecent in the poor, and that it is highly desirable to keep them superstitious in order to keep them contented (007-0898). His commitment to extending rational inquiry to all social classes distinguished his freethought from the more elitist versions common in his era.

## Legacy and Influence

Harrison lived to the age of ninety-one, remaining intellectually active well into the twentieth century. His long career spanned the period from the Victorian crisis of faith through the First World War, during which the intellectual landscape of England was fundamentally transformed.

While Positivism as an organized movement declined after Comte's death, Harrison's work contributed to the broader diffusion of scientific rationalism and secular humanism in English culture. His insistence that morality and social cohesion could be sustained without supernatural beliefs anticipated key themes of twentieth-century secular thought.
