## Life and Background

Pierre Charron was born in Paris in 1541. He studied law and practised as an advocate before entering the Catholic priesthood, eventually becoming a canon and theologian. He was a close friend and disciple of [[michel-de-montaigne]], whose sceptical philosophy deeply shaped his own thinking. The logical results of Montaigne's scepticism were made visible by his friend Charron (004-062).

## Major Contributions

In 1601, Charron published *De la Sagesse* (On Wisdom), a philosophical treatise that aimed to establish a system of morality independent of revealed religion. Here it is taught that true morality is not founded on religion, and the author surveys the history of Christianity to show the evils which it had produced (004-063, 004-064).

On the subject of immortality, Charron wrote that it is the most generally received doctrine, the most usefully believed, and the most weakly established by human reasons (004-065, 004-066). He modified this and some other passages in a second edition of the work, presumably to soften ecclesiastical criticism (004-068).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Charron's separation of ethics from theology was a radical intellectual move that anticipated the deism and freethought of the Enlightenment. A contemporary Jesuit placed Charron in the catalogue of the most dangerous and wicked atheists (004-069). Bury clarifies that he was really a deist, but in those days, and long after, no one scrupled to call a non-Christian deist an atheist (004-070). His book would doubtless have been suppressed or burned had not his formal position within the Church provided some protection.

## Legacy and Influence

Charron's *On Wisdom* influenced subsequent generations of French libertins and English deists. By arguing that virtuous conduct does not depend on religious belief, he helped create the intellectual space for secular morality. His work bridges Renaissance scepticism and Enlightenment rationalism, and his fate — condemned as an atheist for being a deist — illustrates the narrow boundary of tolerated opinion in early modern Europe.
