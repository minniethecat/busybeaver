# The Reformation

The **Reformation** (1517–1648) was a religious movement that began with [[Martin Luther]]'s challenge to papal authority and the sale of indulgences, and ended with the permanent division of Western Christendom into Catholic and Protestant confessions. While the Reformers themselves were not advocates of [[freedom of thought]] — Luther and [[Calvin]] proved as intolerant of dissent as the Church they defied — the Reformation inadvertently advanced the cause of [[freethought]] by breaking the institutional monopoly of the Roman Church and by legitimizing the principle of private judgment in matters of faith.

## Origins and Causes

The Reformation drew on multiple currents that had been gathering force throughout the late Middle Ages:

- **Theological grievances**: Widespread resentment of papal taxation, the sale of indulgences, and the worldliness of the Renaissance papacy created a receptive audience for reformist preaching.
- **The [[Renaissance]] and humanism**: [[Erasmus]]'s critical edition of the Greek New Testament (1516) exposed errors in the Latin Vulgate and encouraged direct engagement with Scripture, undermining the Church's claim to be the sole interpreter.
- **The printing press**: Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517) and his vernacular translation of the Bible reached an audience impossible in any earlier century. Print made the Reformation the first mass-media religious movement.
- **Political fragmentation**: The [[Holy Roman Empire]] was a patchwork of semi-sovereign principalities. Princes who resented imperial and papal authority saw in Luther's doctrine an opportunity to assert independence.

## Luther and the Principle of Private Judgment

[[Martin Luther]] (1483–1546) was an Augustinian friar and professor of theology at Wittenberg. His personal struggle with the doctrine of justification — how could a sinful human be righteous before a just God? — led him to the conviction that salvation comes through faith alone (*sola fide*), not through works or the sacramental mediation of the Church.

At the [[Diet of Worms]] (1521), summoned by [[Emperor Charles V]] to recant, Luther famously refused:

> Unless I am convinced by the testimony of Scripture or by clear reason — for I do not trust the Pope or councils alone, since it is manifest they have often erred and contradicted one another — I am bound by the Scriptures I have cited, and my conscience is captive to the Word of God.

This appeal to individual conscience and Scripture over institutional authority contained a revolutionary implication that Luther himself resisted: if every believer could interpret the Bible, what became of doctrinal uniformity? The radical [[Anabaptists]] drew this conclusion immediately, and Luther condemned them. The logic of private judgment, once released, could not be contained.

## Calvin and the Reformed Tradition

[[John Calvin]] (1509–1564) systematized Protestant theology in his *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (1536) and established [[Geneva]] as a model Reformed city. Calvin's doctrine of predestination — that God has eternally decreed the salvation of some souls (the elect) and the damnation of others — was the most intellectually rigorous statement of Reformation theology, but its practical consequence was a theocracy as intolerant as the Catholic regimes it opposed.

The execution of [[Michael Servetus]], a Spanish physician and theologian who denied the Trinity, at Geneva in 1553 with Calvin's approval, demonstrated that Protestantism had not abandoned the persecution of heresy. [[Sebastian Castellio]]'s protest against the execution — "To kill a man is not to defend a doctrine; it is to kill a man" — was one of the earliest explicit arguments for [[toleration]].

## Wars of Religion and the Road to Toleration

The Reformation triggered more than a century of religious warfare:

| Conflict | Dates | Outcome |
|----------|-------|---------|
| German Peasants' War | 1524–1525 | Crushed; Luther condemned the rebels |
| Schmalkaldic War | 1546–1547 | Imperial victory; Protestant resistance continued |
| French Wars of Religion | 1562–1598 | Ended by [[Edict of Nantes]] granting limited toleration |
| Dutch Revolt | 1568–1648 | Dutch Republic established; de facto toleration |
| Thirty Years' War | 1618–1648 | Devastated Germany; ended by [[Peace of Westphalia]] |

The exhaustion produced by these conflicts, rather than any principled commitment to liberty, gradually convinced rulers and populations that religious uniformity was not worth the cost. The [[Peace of Westphalia]] (1648) established the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* — the ruler's religion determines the territory's religion — which, while far from modern toleration, at least acknowledged that a single universal faith could not be imposed by force.

## Unintended Consequences for Freedom of Thought

Bury's assessment emphasizes that the Reformation's contribution to [[freedom of thought]] was real but paradoxical:

1. **The breakup of authority**: Once the Roman Church's monopoly was broken, no single institution could enforce doctrinal uniformity across Europe. The multiplication of sects created spaces — however narrow — for intellectual dissent.
2. **Private judgment**: Though intended only as a route to orthodoxy, the principle that the individual must interpret Scripture for himself could not be confined to the conclusions Luther or Calvin approved. [[Rationalism]] and [[deism]] grew directly from the soil of Protestant Bible-reading.
3. **The argument from futility**: The wars of religion demonstrated that persecution could not produce genuine belief — an argument that [[Locke]] would later systematize in his *Letter Concerning Toleration*.
