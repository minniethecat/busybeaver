# John Stuart Mill

**John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873) was a British philosopher and political economist. The reasoned justification of [[liberty of thought]] is due to J. S. Mill, who set it forth in his work *On Liberty*, published in 1859 (008-068). His harm principle — that "the sole end for which mankind are warranted, individually and collectively, in interfering with the liberty of action of any of their number, is self-protection" (008-013) — remains the most influential framework for limiting state power over individual conscience.

## Life and Background

Mill was the son of [[James Mill]], the Scottish utilitarian philosopher who educated his son rigorously from infancy. James Mill was a close associate of [[Jeremy Bentham]], and the younger Mill was raised to carry forward the utilitarian project. He worked for the East India Company for over thirty years and served in Parliament from 1865 to 1868, where he advocated for women's suffrage and proportional representation.

We are far from the days when the elder Mill would have been threatened with transportation to Botany Bay for irreligious opinions (007-0924). Yet even Mill's time saw prosecutions for unorthodox beliefs — including actions taken while Mill was writing his brilliant Essay (008-162).

## Major Contributions

Mill's *System of Logic* (1843) formulated the principle of uniformity of nature as the foundation on which scientific induction rests (007-0125). He was a central figure in the English Utilitarian school — [[Bentham]], [[James Mill]], J. S. Mill, [[Grote]] — who preached the greatest happiness of the greatest number (007-0957).

## On Liberty

*On Liberty* is the most systematic defense of [[freedom of thought]] ever written. Mill's argument in the second chapter proceeds through three cases: suppressed opinion may be true, false, or partly true. To the objection that we must suppress error, Mill acutely replies:

> There is the greatest difference between assuming an opinion to be true, because with every opportunity for contesting it, it has not been refuted, and assuming its truth for the purpose of not permitting its refutation.
> (008-096)

The commonest and most important case is where conflicting doctrines share the truth between them. Here Mill has little difficulty in proving the utility of supplementing one-sided popular truths by other truths (008-111). Such is the drift of Mill's main argument (008-127).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Mill did not merely argue for toleration as a pragmatic concession. He provided the philosophical justification for liberty of opinion as essential to human progress. The progress of civilization, in accordance with Mill's reasoning, depends on the free competition of ideas (008-129).

Mill applied his principles consistently. He defended the right to express even the doctrines of [[Rousseau]], which might conceivably have been suppressed as pernicious. Though current opinions were nearer to the truth than Rousseau's, there lay in Rousseau's doctrine truths that the popular view had neglected (008-118, 008-122).

While Mill was writing, the English Government instituted prosecutions for the doctrine of tyrannicide. Fortunately the prosecutions were not persisted in. Mill maintained that such a doctrine does not form an exception to the general principle of free discussion (008-162, 008-166).

## Legacy and Influence

Mill's *On Liberty* underpins modern free speech doctrine, from First Amendment jurisprudence to international human rights law. His *Subjection of Women* made the case for gender equality. His *Three Essays on Religion* (007-1065) extended rational inquiry to questions of belief.

His broader utilitarian philosophy — maximizing human well-being through rational reform — shaped progressive politics throughout the English-speaking world. Many may think that Mill unduly minimized the functions of society, underrating its claims as against the individual (008-073), but the power of his arguments has proved remarkably enduring.
