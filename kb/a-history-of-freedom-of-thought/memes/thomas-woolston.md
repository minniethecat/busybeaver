## Life and Background

Thomas Woolston was born in Northampton, England, in 1668. He entered Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge, where he distinguished himself in theology and was elected a Fellow of the college. For many years he lived the quiet life of an academic clergyman, but around 1720 he began to publish works that would make him one of the most notorious freethinkers of his age.

Woolston's first controversial publication, *The Moderator between an Infidel and an Apostate* (1725), defended the deist [[Anthony Collins]] against orthodox critics. But it was his *Six Discourses on the Miracles of our Saviour* (1727–1730) that sealed his fate. The *Discourses* not only sold enormously but attracted the hostile attention of the authorities. Unlike Collins, who escaped with comparative impunity, Woolston paid the penalty for his audacity (006-0266). Deprived of his Fellowship, prosecuted for libel, and sentenced to a fine of £100 and a year's imprisonment, he was unable to pay and died in prison in 1733 (006-0269).

## Major Contributions

Woolston's *Discourses* were a pioneering exercise in commonsense biblical criticism. Instead of arguing that miracles were impossible in principle — the approach of later rationalist critics — Woolston examined the chief miracles related in the Gospels and showed with great ability and shrewd common sense that they are absurd or unworthy of the performer (006-0273).

His method was to subject each miracle to the same practical reasoning one would apply to a contemporary claim. On the story of Jesus blasting the fig tree, he asked: "What if a yeoman of Kent should go to look for pippins in his orchard at Easter and because of a disappointment cut down his trees? What then would his neighbours make of him? Nothing less than a laughing-stock" (006-0278). On the miracle of the Pool of Bethesda, he remarked that the angels' method seemed designed "for their own diversion more than to do good to mankind — just as some throw a bone among a kennel of hounds for the pleasure of seeing them quarrel for it" (006-0287).

Woolston also employed a provocative comparative technique. Of the healing of the woman with a haemorrhage, he asked: "What if we had been told of the Pope's curing an haemorrhage like this before us, what would Protestants have said to it?" (006-0294). The strategy was devastating: it forced Protestant readers to apply the same scepticism to their own sacred texts that they readily directed at Catholic miracles.

Woolston professed no doubts of the inspiration of Scripture, and pretended to believe in the allegorical theory that the miracles were figures of Christ's mysterious operations in the soul of man — a defence borrowed from [[Origen]] (006-0302). But the allegorical pretence fooled no one; his real argument was that the literal interpretation was absurd.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Woolston occupies a distinctive place in the history of freedom of thought. He was one of the very few early English rationalists to suffer severe punishment for his views. Of the earlier rationalists, apart from the case of Woolston, the only one who was punished was Peter Annet, a schoolmaster sentenced to the pillory and hard labour for popularizing freethought (006-0857). Woolston's imprisonment and death in confinement made him a martyr figure for the deist cause.

The enormous sale of Woolston's pamphlets testifies to the appetite for rationalist criticism even among a reading public that could not follow the philosophical arguments of a [[Hume]] or a [[Locke]]. Woolston's notoriety was such that a "jolly young woman" meeting him in the street accosted him with "You old rogue, are you not hanged yet?" — to which Woolston replied, "Good woman, I know you not; pray what have I done to offend you?" "You have writ against my Saviour," she said; "what would become of my poor sinful soul if it was not for my dear Saviour?" (006-0313).

Bury noted that the fashion of some modern critics to pass over Woolston's productions as unimportant because they are "ribald" or coarse is perfectly unjust (006-0311). His vigorous criticisms vary in value, but many of them hit the nail on the head (006-0309).

## Legacy and Influence

Woolston anticipated methods that later biblical critics would develop with greater sophistication. His commonsense approach to miracle stories prefigured the more systematic critique advanced by [[David Friedrich Strauss]] in the nineteenth century, and his comparative method — asking readers to imagine the same miracle attributed to the Pope — foreshadowed the anthropological approach to comparative religion.

Woolston's fate also illustrates the uneven enforcement of laws against blasphemy and freethought in eighteenth-century England. That a Cambridge Fellow could be prosecuted, imprisoned, and left to die in confinement for publishing his opinions — while others who wrote for a learned audience escaped — revealed the class dimension of toleration. The ruling class was willing to tolerate heterodoxy among gentlemen but not the dissemination of freethought to a popular audience. This distinction would remain a live issue until [[Thomas Paine]]'s prosecution in the 1790s and beyond.
