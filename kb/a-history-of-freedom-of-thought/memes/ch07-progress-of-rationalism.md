## Chapter VII
### THE PROGRESS OF RATIONALISM
(NINETEENTH CENTURY)

[007-0001]MODERN science, heralded by the researches of Copernicus, was founded in

[007-0002]the seventeenth century, which saw the demonstration of the Copernican

[007-0003]theory, the discovery of gravitation, the discovery of the circulation

[007-0004]of the blood, and the foundation

[007-0005][177] of modern chemistry and physics. The true nature of comets was

[007-0006]ascertained, and they ceased to be regarded as signs of heavenly wrath.

[007-0007]But several generations were to pass before science became, in

[007-0008]Protestant countries, an involuntary arch-enemy of theology. Till the

[007-0009]nineteenth century, it was only in minor points, such as the movement of

[007-0010]the earth, that proved scientific facts seemed to conflict with

[007-0011]Scripture, and it was easy enough to explain away these inconsistencies

[007-0012]by a new interpretation of the sacred texts. Yet remarkable facts were

[007-0013]accumulating which, though not explained by science, seemed to menace

[007-0014]the credibility of Biblical history. If the story of Noah’s Ark and the

[007-0015]Flood is true, how was it that beasts unable to swim or fly inhabit

[007-0016]America and the islands of the Ocean? And what about the new species

[007-0017]which were constantly being found in the New World and did not exist in

[007-0018]the Old? Where did the kangaroos of Australia drop from? The only

[007-0019]explanation compatible with received theology seemed to be the

[007-0020]hypothesis of innumerable new acts of creation, later than the Flood. It

[007-0021]was in the field of natural history that scientific men of the

[007-0022]eighteenth century suffered most from the coercion of authority.

[007-0023]Linnaeus felt it in Sweden, Buffon

[007-0024][178] in France. Buffon was compelled to retract hypotheses which he put

[007-0025]forward about the formation of the earth in his Natural History (1749),

[007-0026]and to state that he believed implicitly in the Bible account of

[007-0027]Creation.

[007-0028]At the beginning of the nineteenth century Laplace worked out the

[007-0029]mechanics of the universe, on the nebular hypothesis. His results

[007-0030]dispensed, as he said to Napoleon, with the hypothesis of God, and were

[007-0031]duly denounced. His theory involved a long physical process before the

[007-0032]earth and solar system came to be formed; but this was not fatal, for a

[007-0033]little ingenuity might preserve the credit of the first chapter of

[007-0034]Genesis. Geology was to prove a more formidable enemy to the Biblical

[007-0035]story of the Creation and the Deluge. The theory of a French naturalist

[007-0036](Cuvier) that the earth had repeatedly experienced catastrophes, each of

[007-0037]which necessitated a new creative act, helped for a time to save the

[007-0038]belief in divine intervention, and Lyell, in his Principles of Geology

[007-0039](1830), while he undermined the assumption of catastrophes, by showing

[007-0040]that the earth’s history could be explained by the ordinary processes

[007-0041]which we still see in operation, yet held fast to successive acts of

[007-0042]creation. It was not till 1863 that he presented fully, in his Antiquity

[007-0043]of Man, the

[007-0044][179] evidence which showed that the human race had inhabited the earth

[007-0045]for a far longer period than could be reconciled with the record of

[007-0046]Scripture. That record might be adapted to the results of science in

[007-0047]regard not only to the earth itself but also to the plants and lower

[007-0048]animals, by explaining the word “day” in the Jewish story of creation to

[007-0049]signify some long period of time. But this way out was impossible in the

[007-0050]case of the creation of man, for the sacred chronology is quite

[007-0051]definite. An English divine of the seventeenth century ingeniously

[007-0052]calculated that man was created by the Trinity on October 23, B.C. 4004,

[007-0053]at 9 o’clock in the morning, and no reckoning of the Bible dates could

[007-0054]put the event much further back. Other evidence reinforced the

[007-0055]conclusions from geology, but geology alone was sufficient to damage

[007-0056]irretrievably the historical truth of the Jewish legend of Creation. The

[007-0057]only means of rescuing it was to suppose that God had created misleading

[007-0058]evidence for the express purpose of deceiving man.

[007-0059]Geology shook the infallibility of the Bible, but left the creation of

[007-0060]some prehistoric Adam and Eve a still admissible hypothesis. Here

[007-0061]however zoology stepped in, and pronounced upon the origin of man. It

[007-0062]was an old conjecture that the higher forms of life, including

[007-0063][180] man, had developed out of lower forms, and advanced thinkers had

[007-0064]been reaching the conclusion that the universe, as we find it, is the

[007-0065]result of a continuous process, unbroken by supernatural interference,

[007-0066]and explicable by uniform natural laws. But while the reign of law in

[007-0067]the world of non-living matter seemed to be established, the world of

[007-0068]life could be considered a field in which the theory of divine

[007-0069]intervention is perfectly valid, so long as science failed to assign

[007-0070]satisfactory causes for the origination of the various kinds of animals

[007-0071]and plants. The publication of Darwin’s Origin of Species in 1859 is,

[007-0072]therefore, a landmark not only in science but in the war between science

[007-0073]and theology. When this book appeared, Bishop Wilberforce truly said

[007-0074]that “the principle of natural selection is incompatible with the word

[007-0075]of God,” and theologians in Germany and France as well as in England

[007-0076]cried aloud against the threatened dethronement of the Deity. The

[007-0077]appearance of the Descent of Man (1871), in which the evidence for the

[007-0078]pedigree of the human race from lower animals was marshalled with

[007-0079]masterly force, renewed the outcry. The Bible said that God created man

[007-0080]in his own image, Darwin said that man descended from an ape. The

[007-0081]feelings of the orthodox world may be

[007-0082][181] expressed in the words of Mr. Gladstone: “Upon the grounds of what

[007-0083]is called evolution God is relieved of the labour of creation, and in

[007-0084]the name of unchangeable laws is discharged from governing the world.”

[007-0085]It was a discharge which, as Spencer observed, had begun with Newton’s

[007-0086]discovery of gravitation. If Darwin did not, as is now recognized,

[007-0087]supply a complete explanation of the origin of species, his researches

[007-0088]shattered the supernatural theory and confirmed the view to which many

[007-0089]able thinkers had been led that development is continuous in the living

[007-0090]as in the non-living world. Another nail was driven into the coffin of

[007-0091]Creation and the Fall of Adam, and the doctrine of redemption could only

[007-0092]be rescued by making it independent of the Jewish fable on which it was

[007-0093]founded.

[007-0094]Darwinism, as it is called, has had the larger effect of discrediting

[007-0095]the theory of the adaptation of means to ends in nature by an external

[007-0096]and infinitely powerful intelligence. The inadequacy of the argument

[007-0097]from design, as a proof of God’s existence, had been shown by the logic

[007-0098]of Hume and Kant; but the observation of the life-processes of nature

[007-0099]shows that the very analogy between nature and art, on which the

[007-0100]argument depends, breaks down. The impropriety of the analogy has been

[007-0101][182] pointed out, in a telling way, by a German writer (Lange). If a

[007-0102]man wants to shoot a hare which is in a certain field, he does not

[007-0103]procure thousands of guns, surround the field, and cause them all to be

[007-0104]fired off; or if he wants a house to live in, he does not build a whole

[007-0105]town and abandon to weather and decay all the houses but one. If he did

[007-0106]either of these things we should say he was mad or amazingly

[007-0107]unintelligent; his actions certainly would not be held to indicate a

[007-0108]powerful mind, expert in adapting means to ends. But these are the sort

[007-0109]of things that nature does. Her wastefulness in the propagation of life

[007-0110]is reckless. For the production of one life she sacrifices innumerable

[007-0111]germs. The “end” is achieved in one case out of thousands; the rule is

[007-0112]destruction and failure. If intelligence had anything to do with this

[007-0113]bungling process, it would be an intelligence infinitely low. And the

[007-0114]finished product, if regarded as a work of design, points to

[007-0115]incompetence in the designer. Take the human eye. An illustrious man of

[007-0116]science (Helmholtz) said, “If an optician sent it to me as an

[007-0117]instrument, I should send it back with reproaches for the carelessness

[007-0118]of his work and demand the return of my money. Darwin showed how the

[007-0119]phenomena might be explained as events not brought about

[007-0120][183] intentionally, but due to exceptional concurrences of

[007-0121]circumstances.

[007-0122]The phenomena of nature are a system of things which co-exist and follow

[007-0123]each other according to invariable laws. This deadly proposition was

[007-0124]asserted early in the nineteenth century to be an axiom of science. It

[007-0125]was formulated by Mill (in his System of Logic, 1843) as the foundation

[007-0126]on which scientific induction rests. It means that at any moment the

[007-0127]state of the whole universe is the effect of its state at the preceding

[007-0128]moment; the casual sequence between two successive states is not broken

[007-0129]by any arbitrary interference suppressing or altering the relation

[007-0130]between cause and effect. Some ancient Greek philosophers were convinced

[007-0131]of this principle; the work done by modern science in every field seems

[007-0132]to be a verification of it. But it need not be stated in such an

[007-0133]absolute form. Recently, scientific men have been inclined to express

[007-0134]the axiom with more reserve and less dogmatically. They are prepared to

[007-0135]recognize that it is simply a postulate without which the scientific

[007-0136]comprehension of the universe would be impossible, and they are inclined

[007-0137]to state it not as a law of causation—for the idea of causation leads

[007-0138]into metaphysics—but rather as uniformity of experience. But they are

[007-0139]not

[007-0140][184] readier to admit exceptions to this uniformity than their

[007-0141]predecessors were to admit exceptions to the law of causation.

[007-0142]The idea of development has been applied not only to nature, but to the

[007-0143]mind of man and to the history of civilization, including thought and

[007-0144]religion. The first who attempted to apply this idea methodically to the

[007-0145]whole universe was not a student of natural science, but a

[007-0146]metaphysician, Hegel. His extremely difficult philosophy had such a wide

[007-0147]influence on thought that a few words must be said about its tendency.

[007-0148]He conceived the whole of existence as what he called the Absolute Idea,

[007-0149]which is not in space or time and is compelled by the laws of its being

[007-0150]to manifest itself in the process of the world, first externalizing

[007-0151]itself in nature, and then becoming conscious of itself as spirit in

[007-0152]individual minds. His system is hence called Absolute Idealism. The

[007-0153]attraction which it exercised has probably been in great measure due to

[007-0154]the fact that it was in harmony with nineteenth-century thought, in so

[007-0155]far as it conceived the process of the world, both in nature and spirit,

[007-0156]as a necessary development from lower to higher stages. In this respect

[007-0157]indeed Hegel’s vision was limited. He treats the process as if it were

[007-0158]practically complete already, and does not take into account

[007-0159][185] the probability of further development in the future, to which

[007-0160]other thinkers of his own time were turning their attention. But what

[007-0161]concerns us here is that, while Hegel’s system is “idealistic,” finding

[007-0162]the explanation of the universe in thought and not in matter, it tended

[007-0163]as powerfully as any materialistic system to subvert orthodox beliefs.

[007-0164]It is true that some have claimed it as supporting Christianity. A

[007-0165]certain colour is lent to this by Hegel’s view that the Christian creed,

[007-0166]as the highest religion, contains doctrines which express imperfectly

[007-0167]some of the ideas of the highest philosophy—his own; along with the fact

[007-0168]that he sometimes speaks of the Absolute Idea as if it were a person,

[007-0169]though personality would be a limitation inconsistent with his

[007-0170]conception of it. But it is sufficient to observe that, whatever value

[007-0171]be assigned to Christianity, he regarded it from the superior standpoint

[007-0172]of a purely intellectual philosophy, not as a special revelation of

[007-0173]truth, but as a certain approximation to the truth which philosophy

[007-0174]alone can reach; and it may be said with some confidence that any one

[007-0175]who comes under Hegel’s spell feels that he is in possession of a theory

[007-0176]of the universe which relieves him from the need or desire of any

[007-0177]revealed religion. His influence in Germany, Russia, and elsewhere has

[007-0178]entirely made for highly unorthodox thought.

[007-0179][186]

[007-0180]Hegel was not aggressive, he was superior. His French contemporary,

[007-0181]Comte, who also thought out a comprehensive system, aggressively and

[007-0182]explicitly rejected theology as an obsolete way of explaining the

[007-0183]universe. He rejected metaphysics likewise, and all that Hegel stood

[007-0184]for, as equally useless, on the ground that metaphysicians explain

[007-0185]nothing, but merely describe phenomena in abstract terms, and that

[007-0186]questions about the origin of the world and why it exists are quite

[007-0187]beyond the reach of reason. Both theology and metaphysics are superseded

[007-0188]by science—the investigation of causes and effects and coexistences; and

[007-0189]the future progress of society will be guided by the scientific view of

[007-0190]the world which confines itself to the positive data of experience.

[007-0191]Comte was convinced that religion is a social necessity, and, to supply

[007-0192]the place of the theological religions which he pronounced to be doomed,

[007-0193]he invented a new religion—the religion of Humanity. It differs from the

[007-0194]great religions of the world in having no supernatural or non-rational

[007-0195]articles of belief, and on that account he had few adherents. But the

[007-0196]“Positive Philosophy” of Comte has exercised great influence, not least

[007-0197]in England, where its principles have been promulgated especially by Mr.

[007-0198]Frederic Harrison, who in the latter

[007-0199][187] half of the nineteenth century has been one of the most

[007-0200]indefatigable workers in the cause of reason against authority.

[007-0201]Another comprehensive system was worked out by an Englishman, Herbert

[007-0202]Spencer. Like Comte’s, it was based on science, and attempts to show

[007-0203]how, starting with a nebular universe, the whole knowable world,

[007-0204]psychical and social as well as physical, can be deduced. His Synthetic

[007-0205]Philosophy perhaps did more than anything else to make the idea of

[007-0206]evolution familiar in England.

[007-0207]I must mention one other modern explanation of the world, that of

[007-0208]Haeckel, the zoologist, professor at Jena, who may be called the prophet

[007-0209]of evolution. His Creation of Man (1868) covered the same ground as

[007-0210]Darwin’s Descent, had an enormous circulation, and was translated, I

[007-0211]believe, into fourteen languages. His World-riddles (1899) enjoys the

[007-0212]same popularity. He has taught, like Spencer, that the principle of

[007-0213]evolution applies not only to the history of nature, but also to human

[007-0214]civilization and human thought. He differs from Spencer and Comte in not

[007-0215]assuming any unknowable reality behind natural phenomena. His

[007-0216]adversaries commonly stigmatize his theory as materialism, but this is a

[007-0217]mistake. Like Spinoza he recognizes matter and mind, body and thought,

[007-0218]as

[007-0219][188] two inseparable sides of ultimate reality, which he calls God; in

[007-0220]fact, he identifies his philosophy with that of Spinoza. And he

[007-0221]logically proceeds to conceive material atoms as thinking. His idea of

[007-0222]the physical world is based on the old mechanical conception of matter,

[007-0223]which in recent years has been discredited. But Haeckel’s Monism, [1] as

[007-0224]he called his doctrine, has lately been reshaped and in its new form

[007-0225]promises to exercise wide influence on thoughtful people in Germany. I

[007-0226]will return later to this Monistic movement.

[007-0227]It had been a fundamental principle of Comte that human actions and

[007-0228]human history are as strictly subject as nature is, to the law of

[007-0229]causation. Two psychological works appeared in England in 1855 (Bain’s

[007-0230]Senses and Intellect and Spencer’s Principles of Psychology), which

[007-0231]taught that our volitions are completely determined, being the

[007-0232]inevitable consequences of chains of causes and effects. But a far

[007-0233]deeper impression was produced two years later by the first volume of

[007-0234]Buckle’s History of Civilization in England (a work of much less

[007-0235]permanent value), which attempted to apply this principle to history.

[007-0236]Men act in consequence of motives; their motives are the results of

[007-0237]preceding facts; so that “if we were acquainted with the whole of the

[007-0238]antecedents

[007-0239][189] and with all the laws of their movements, we could with unerring

[007-0240]certainty predict the whole of their immediate results.” Thus history is

[007-0241]an unbroken chain of causes and effects. Chance is excluded; it is a

[007-0242]mere name for the defects of our knowledge. Mysterious and providential

[007-0243]interference is excluded. Buckle maintained God’s existence, but

[007-0244]eliminated him from history; and his book dealt a resounding blow at the

[007-0245]theory that human actions are not submitted to the law of universal

[007-0246]causation.

[007-0247]The science of anthropology has in recent years aroused wide interest.

[007-0248]Inquiries into the condition of early man have shown (independently of

[007-0249]Darwinism) that there is nothing to be said for the view that he fell

[007-0250]from a higher to a lower state; the evidence points to a slow rise from

[007-0251]mere animality. The origin of religious beliefs has been investigated,

[007-0252]with results disquieting for orthodoxy. The researches of students of

[007-0253]anthropology and comparative religion—such as Tylor, Robertson Smith,

[007-0254]and Frazer—have gone to show that mysterious ideas and dogma and rites

[007-0255]which were held to be peculiar to the Christian revelation are derived

[007-0256]from the crude ideas of primitive religions. That the mystery of the

[007-0257]Eucharist comes from the common savage rite of eating a dead god,

[007-0258][190] that the death and resurrection of a god in human form, which form

[007-0259]the central fact of Christianity, and the miraculous birth of a Saviour

[007-0260]are features which it has in common with pagan religions—such

[007-0261]conclusions are supremely unedifying. It may be said that in themselves

[007-0262]they are not fatal to the claims of the current theology. It may be

[007-0263]held, for instance, that, as part of Christian revelation, such ideas

[007-0264]acquired a new significance and that God wisely availed himself of

[007-0265]familiar beliefs—which, though false and leading to cruel practices, he

[007-0266]himself had inspired and permitted—in order to construct a scheme of

[007-0267]redemption which should appeal to the prejudices of man. Some minds may

[007-0268]find satisfaction in this sort of explanation, but it may be suspected

[007-0269]that most of the few who study modern researches into the origin of

[007-0270]religious beliefs will feel the lines which were supposed to mark off

[007-0271]the Christian from all other faiths dissolving before their eyes.

[007-0272]The general result of the advance of science, including anthropology,

[007-0273]has been to create a coherent view of the world, in which the Christian

[007-0274]scheme, based on the notions of an unscientific age and on the arrogant

[007-0275]assumption that the universe was made for man, has no suitable or

[007-0276]reasonable place. If Paine felt this a hundred years ago, it is far

[007-0277][191] more apparent now. All minds however are not equally impressed

[007-0278]with this incongruity. There are many who will admit the proofs

[007-0279]furnished by science that the Biblical record as to the antiquity of man

[007-0280]is false, but are not affected by the incongruity between the scientific

[007-0281]and theological conceptions of the world.

[007-0282]For such minds science has only succeeded in carrying some

[007-0283]entrenchments, which may be abandoned without much harm. It has made the

[007-0284]old orthodox view of the infallibility of the Bible untenable, and upset

[007-0285]the doctrine of the Creation and Fall. But it would still be possible

[007-0286]for Christianity to maintain the supernatural claim, by modifying its

[007-0287]theory of the authority of the Bible and revising its theory of

[007-0288]redemption, if the evidence of natural science were the only group of

[007-0289]facts with which it collided. It might be argued that the law of

[007-0290]universal causation is a hypothesis inferred from experience, but that

[007-0291]experience includes the testimonies of history and must therefore take

[007-0292]account of the clear evidence of miraculous occurrences in the New

[007-0293]Testament (evidence which is valid, even if that book was not inspired).

[007-0294]Thus, a stand could be taken against the generalization of science on

[007-0295]the firm ground of historical fact. That solid ground, however, has

[007-0296]given

[007-0297][192] way, undermined by historical criticism, which has been more

[007-0298]deadly than the common-sense criticism of the eighteenth century.

[007-0299]The methodical examination of the records contained in the Bible,

[007-0300]dealing with them as if they were purely human documents, is the work of

[007-0301]the nineteenth century. Something, indeed, had already been done.

[007-0302]Spinoza, for instance (above, p. 138), and Simon, a Frenchman whose

[007-0303]books were burnt, were pioneers; and the modern criticism of the Old

[007-0304]Testament was begun by Astruc (professor of medicine at Paris), who

[007-0305]discovered an important clue for distinguishing different documents used

[007-0306]by the compiler of the Book of Genesis (1753). His German contemporary,

[007-0307]Reimarus, a student of the New Testament, anticipated the modern

[007-0308]conclusion that Jesus had no intention of founding a new religion, and

[007-0309]saw that the Gospel of St. John presents a different figure from the

[007-0310]Jesus of the other evangelists.

[007-0311]But in the nineteenth century the methods of criticism, applied by

[007-0312]German scholars to Homer and to the records of early Roman history, were

[007-0313]extended to the investigation of the Bible. The work has been done

[007-0314]principally in Germany. The old tradition that the Pentateuch was

[007-0315]written by Moses has been completely discredited. It is now

[007-0316][193] agreed unanimously by all who have studied the facts that the

[007-0317]Pentateuch was put together from a number of different documents of

[007-0318]different ages, the earliest dating from the ninth, the last from the

[007-0319]fifth, century B.C.; and there are later minor additions. An important,

[007-0320]though undesigned, contribution was made to this exposure by an

[007-0321]Englishman, Colenso, Bishop of Natal. It had been held that the oldest

[007-0322]of the documents which had been distinguished was a narrative which

[007-0323]begins in Genesis, Chapter I, but there was the difficulty that this

[007-0324]narrative seemed to be closely associated with the legislation of

[007-0325]Leviticus which could be proved to belong to the fifth century. In 1862

[007-0326]Colenso published the first part of his Pentateuch and the Book of

[007-0327]Joshua Critically Examined. His doubts of the truth of Old Testament

[007-0328]history had been awakened by a converted Zulu who asked the intelligent

[007-0329]question whether he could really believe in the story of the Flood,

[007-0330]“that all the beasts and birds and creeping things upon the earth, large

[007-0331]and small, from hot countries and cold, came thus by pairs and entered

[007-0332]into the ark with Noah? And did Noah gather food for them all, for the

[007-0333]beasts and birds of prey as well as the rest?” The Bishop then proceeded

[007-0334]to test the accuracy of the inspired books by examining

[007-0335][194] the numerical statements which they contain. The results were

[007-0336]fatal to them as historical records. Quite apart from miracles (the

[007-0337]possibility of which he did not question), he showed that the whole

[007-0338]story of the sojourn of the Israelites in Egypt and the wilderness was

[007-0339]full of absurdities and impossibilities. Colenso’s book raised a storm

[007-0340]of indignation in England—he was known as “the wicked bishop”; but on

[007-0341]the Continent its reception was very different. The portions of the

[007-0342]Pentateuch and Joshua, which he proved to be unhistorical, belonged

[007-0343]precisely to the narrative which had caused perplexity; and critics were

[007-0344]led by his results to conclude that, like the Levitical laws with which

[007-0345]it was connected, it was as late as the fifth century.

[007-0346]One of the most striking results of the researches on the Old Testament

[007-0347]has been that the Jews themselves handled their traditions freely. Each

[007-0348]of the successive documents, which were afterwards woven together, was

[007-0349]written by men who adopted a perfectly free attitude towards the older

[007-0350]traditions, and having no suspicion that they were of divine origin did

[007-0351]not bow down before their authority. It was reserved for the Christians

[007-0352]to invest with infallible authority the whole indiscriminate lump of

[007-0353]these Jewish documents, inconsistent not

[007-0354][195] only in their tendencies (since they reflect the spirit of

[007-0355]different ages), but also in some respects in substance. The examination

[007-0356]of most of the other Old Testament books has led to conclusions likewise

[007-0357]adverse to the orthodox view of their origin and character. New

[007-0358]knowledge on many points has been derived from the Babylonian literature

[007-0359]which has been recovered during the last half century. One of the

[007-0360]earliest (1872) and most sensational discoveries was that the Jews got

[007-0361]their story of the Flood from Babylonian mythology.

[007-0362]Modern criticism of the New Testament began with the stimulating works

[007-0363]of Baur and of Strauss, whose Life of Jesus (1835), in which the

[007-0364]supernatural was entirely rejected, had an immense success and caused

[007-0365]furious controversy. Both these rationalists were influenced by Hegel.

[007-0366]At the same time a classical scholar, Lachmann, laid the foundations of

[007-0367]the criticism of the Greek text of the New Testament, by issuing the

[007-0368]first scientific edition. Since then seventy years of work have led to

[007-0369]some certain results which are generally accepted.

[007-0370]In the first place, no intelligent person who has studied modern

[007-0371]criticism holds the old view that each of the four biographies of Jesus

[007-0372]is an independent work and an independent

[007-0373][196] testimony to the facts which are related. It is acknowledged that

[007-0714]The agnostic holds that there are limits to human reason, and that

[007-0715]theology lies outside those limits. Within those limits lies the world

[007-0716]with which science (including psychology) deals. Science deals entirely

[007-0717]with phenomena, and has nothing to say to the nature of the ultimate

[007-0718]reality which may lie behind phenomena. There are four possible

[007-0720]metaphysician and theologian, who are convinced not only that it exists

[007-0722]man who denies that it exists; but he must be also a metaphysician, for

[007-0723]its existence can only be disproved by metaphysical arguments. Then

[007-0724]there are those who assert that it exists but deny that we can know

[007-0725]anything about it. And finally there are those who say that we cannot

[007-0726]know whether it exists or not. These last are “agnostics” in the strict

[007-0727]sense of the term, men who profess not to know. The third class go

[007-0728]beyond phenomena in so far as they assert that there is an ultimate

[007-0729]though unknowable reality beneath phenomena. But agnostic is commonly

[007-0730]used in a wide sense so as to include the third as well as the fourth

[007-0731]class—those who assume an unknowable, as well as those who do not know

[007-0732]whether there is an unknowable or not. Comte and Spencer, for instance,

[007-0733]who believed in an unknowable, are counted as agnostics. The difference

[007-0734]between an agnostic and an atheist is that the atheist positively denies

[007-0735]the existence of a personal God, the agnostic does not believe in it.
