# Lucilio Vanini

**Lucilio Vanini** (1585–1619) was an Italian philosopher and freethinker who traveled across Europe promoting rationalist ideas in the early 17th century. His execution for atheism at Toulouse stands as one of the notable cases of persecution for freethought in early modern Europe.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

In J.B. Bury's "A History of Freedom of Thought", Vanini appears in the context of religious toleration in 17th-century France. Bury notes that while there was "rather more freedom than elsewhere under the relatively tolerant government of Henry IV and of the Cardinals Richelieu and Mazarin, till about 1660", this toleration had sharp limits. The case of Vanini demonstrated those limits: "at Toulouse (1619) Lucilio Vanini, a learned Italian who like Bruno wandered about Europe, was convicted as an atheist and blasphemer; his tongue was torn out and he was burned" [004-259]–[004-262].

Vanini's fate illustrates the precarious position of freethinkers even in relatively tolerant regimes. His execution, following closely upon Giordano Bruno's burning in 1600, shows that denial of core Christian doctrines remained a capital offense well into the 17th century.
