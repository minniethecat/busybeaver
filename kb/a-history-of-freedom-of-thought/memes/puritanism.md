# Puritanism

Puritanism was a Protestant reform movement that sought to "purify" the Church of England from residual Catholic practices. While the Puritans played a significant role in the political history of seventeenth-century England and the colonization of New England, their relationship to freedom of thought was complex and deeply contradictory. As J. B. Bury's history makes clear, Puritans were at once victims of intolerance and its perpetrators — they fled persecution only to establish regimes of their own that were equally illiberal.

## Definition and Origins

Puritanism emerged within the Church of England in the 1560s as a movement demanding further reformation beyond the Elizabethan Settlement. Puritans sought to simplify worship, eliminate episcopal hierarchy, and enforce stricter moral discipline. They were Calvinist in theology, emphasizing predestination, the sovereignty of God, and the supreme authority of Scripture. This commitment to biblical authority had direct consequences for intellectual liberty: what Scripture commanded, the magistrate must enforce.

Bury notes that under the Commonwealth in England, "the zeal of the Puritans" was directed at suppressing traditional pastimes and folk practices — what they saw as "the wicked old" customs that had survived from Catholic times (006-043). This moral rigorism extended to the intellectual sphere: the Commonwealth's Puritan rulers had little patience for heterodox opinion.

## Historical Development

At the height of their power during the English Civil War and the Commonwealth (1642–1660), the Puritans enacted some of the harshest legislation against unbelief in English history. Bury records that "Puritan intolerance, indeed, passed an ordinance in 1648, by which all who denied the Trinity, Christ's divinity, the inspiration of Scripture, or a future state, were liable to death, and persons guilty of other heresies, to imprisonment" (004-288). Although this ordinance did not lead to executions — the last burnings for heresy in England had occurred decades earlier, in 1611 — it marked the legal limit of intolerance in the English-speaking world.

The Puritan record in America was equally severe. "The Puritans who fled from the intolerance of the English Church and State and founded colonies in New England, were themselves equally intolerant, not only to Anglicans and Catholics, but to Baptists and Quakers" (005-071). They established "theocratical governments from which all who did not belong to their own sect were excluded" (005-074). This creates one of the great ironies of the history of toleration: a movement born from the experience of persecution became, when it gained power, a persecutor itself.

The Puritan theocracy provoked a reaction that ultimately advanced the cause of liberty. Roger Williams, who "had imbibed from the Dutch Arminians the idea of separation of Church from State," was "driven from Massachusetts" on account of this heresy (005-076). He founded Providence (later Rhode Island) as "a refuge for those whom the Puritan colonists persecuted" and established "a democratic constitution in which the magistrates had power only in civil matters and could not interfere with religion" (005-078). Thus the Puritan intolerance in Massachusetts gave birth, by reaction, to one of the first experiments in religious liberty in the Western world.

## Key Proponents

- **Oliver Cromwell** (1599–1658): Lord Protector of England during the Commonwealth; while personally more tolerant than many of his Puritan contemporaries, his government passed the 1648 ordinance threatening heretics with death.
- **John Winthrop** (1588–1649): Governor of the Massachusetts Bay Colony, who articulated the vision of a Puritan "city upon a hill" — a theocracy in which civil and religious authority were fused.
- **Increase Mather** (1639–1723) and **Cotton Mather** (1663–1728): Influential Puritan ministers in Massachusetts who defended the old order against the rising tide of toleration.
- **Roger Williams** (c. 1603–1683): Puritan minister turned dissenter, banished from Massachusetts for advocating the separation of church and state; founder of Rhode Island and a pivotal figure in the history of religious liberty.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Puritanism's impact on freedom of thought was largely negative but indirectly generative. In both Old and New England, Puritan rule produced a climate hostile to intellectual liberty. The Puritan emphasis on biblical literalism, enforced by the civil magistrate, left no room for the free play of reason in matters of religion. The 1648 ordinance showed how far a Protestant government could go in imitating the intolerance of the Catholic Inquisition it had once denounced.

Yet Puritanism contributed to the growth of liberty in an unintended way: by provoking its own dissidents. The experience of Puritan intolerance in Massachusetts drove Roger Williams to articulate a principled case for the separation of church and state. Williams argued that the civil magistrate had no authority in matters of conscience — a position that directly contradicted the Puritan theocracy and laid groundwork for the religious liberty clauses of the American Constitution. Bury traces this lineage: Williams's Rhode Island experiment "had been ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus" (005-110), passing through the Dutch Arminians into the English dissenting tradition and finally into American constitutional practice.

Thus Puritanism played the paradoxical role of a movement that, by enforcing orthodoxy so rigorously, stimulated the very arguments for liberty that would ultimately undo it. It was a necessary antagonist — the obstacle against which the case for toleration had to define itself.
