# Charles Fourier

**Charles Fourier** (1772–1837) was a French philosopher and utopian socialist. The optimism of Fourier went so far as to anticipate the time when the sea would be turned by human ingenuity into lemonade, when there would be 37 million poets as great as Homer, 37 million writers as great as Molière, and 37 million men of science equal to Newton (007-0964 to 007-0967).

He represents the most extravagant expression of the nineteenth-century faith in [[progress]].

## Life and Background

François Marie Charles Fourier was born in Besançon, France, the son of a cloth merchant. He received a solid education but was largely self-taught in philosophy and social theory. He spent most of his working life as a commercial clerk and traveling salesman, occupations that gave him firsthand experience of the waste and dishonesty of commercial society, which he came to detest.

Fourier lived modestly in Paris and later in Lyon, writing prolifically. His ideas attracted a small but devoted following, particularly after his death, when Fourierist communities (known as "phalansteries") were established in France and the United States, most notably Brook Farm in Massachusetts.

## Major Contributions

Fourier's social philosophy proposed the reorganization of society into self-sufficient cooperative communities called *phalanxes*, each housing about 1,600 people in a single building or "phalanstery." In these communities, work would be assigned according to individual passions and inclinations rather than economic necessity, transforming labor into a form of play.

His critique of existing civilization was penetrating. He catalogued the miseries of poverty, the wastefulness of competition, the degradation of wage labor, and the subjection of women. He argued that "civilization" — his term for the existing social order — was fundamentally irrational and would inevitably be replaced by "harmony."

Fourier's cosmology was as audacious as his social theory. He proposed that the universe was governed by a law of "passional attraction" analogous to Newton's law of gravitation, and that human passions, when properly organized, would produce social harmony just as gravitational forces produced cosmic order.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Fourier's vision extended the doctrine of historical [[progress]], which was started in France in 1750 by [[Turgot]], who made progress the organic principle of history. The idea was developed by [[Condorcet]] and seized upon by the French socialistic philosophers (007-0960 to 007-0963). Fourier took this Enlightenment inheritance to its logical extreme.

By imagining a future of unlimited human achievement, Fourier demonstrated both the liberating power and the potential excess of the idea of progress. His extravagance serves as a landmark indicating how far the nineteenth-century faith in human perfectibility could go — and why more cautious thinkers regarded the doctrine with skepticism.

## Legacy and Influence

Fourier's direct influence was limited during his lifetime, but his ideas gained significant currency after his death. Fourierist communities proliferated in the 1840s, and his critique of commercial civilization influenced later socialist and cooperative movements. [[Karl Marx]] and [[Friedrich Engels]] drew on Fourier's critique of capitalism while dismissing his utopian methods.

In the history of freethought, Fourier exemplifies the nineteenth-century tendency to replace religious eschatology with secular utopianism. His vision of a transformed world — however fantastical — expressed the conviction that [[reason]] and social reorganization could remake human existence for the better.
