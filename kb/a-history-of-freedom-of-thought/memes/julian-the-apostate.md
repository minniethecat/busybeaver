## Life and Background

Flavius Claudius Julianus, known to history as Julian the Apostate, was born in Constantinople in 331 CE, a nephew of [[constantine|Constantine the Great]]. Though raised as a Christian, he secretly embraced the old pagan gods and classical philosophy. After his cousin Constantius II died in 361, Julian became sole emperor and openly declared his allegiance to the old religion.

## Major Contributions

Under Constantine the Great and his successors, edict after edict fulminated against the worship of the old pagan gods and against heretical Christian sects (003-050, 003-051). Julian, in his brief reign (361–363 CE), sought to reverse this direction and revive the old order of things (003-052, 003-053).

As part of this reversal, Julian proclaimed universal religious toleration. However, he placed Christians at a disadvantage by forbidding them to teach in schools (003-053, 003-054, 003-055). This restriction was subtle but significant: by denying Christians access to the educational institutions of the empire, Julian aimed to marginalise the new faith without resorting to the kind of bloody persecution that had been employed against Christians themselves in earlier centuries.

## Historical Significance

Julian's reign was brief — he died in battle against the Persians in 363 — and his religious policy was only a momentary check on the Christianisation of the empire (003-055). Paganism was finally shattered by the severe laws of [[theodosius-i|Theodosius I]] at the end of the fourth century (003-056).

Yet Julian occupies an important place in the history of religious toleration. His proclamation of universal toleration, however politically motivated, demonstrated that a pre-Christian ruler could articulate a principle of religious coexistence. Bury's narrative notes that this was one of the last moments before the full establishment of Christian religious monopoly.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Julian is a paradoxical figure in the history of freedom of thought. On one hand, his policy of toleration was more liberal in principle than the persecuting edicts of the Christian emperors who preceded and followed him. On the other hand, his exclusion of Christians from teaching posts reveals that his toleration was instrumental — a tool for restoring paganism rather than a principled commitment to intellectual liberty. His reign illustrates the fragility of toleration when it is granted by imperial decree rather than grounded in a durable philosophical commitment to freedom of conscience.
