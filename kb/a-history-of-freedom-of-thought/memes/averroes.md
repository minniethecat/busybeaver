## Life and Background

Averroes — the Latinised name of Abu al-Walid Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Rushd — was born in Córdoba, Muslim Spain (al-Andalus), in 1126. He served as a qadi (judge) and physician at the courts of the Almohad caliphs. His extensive commentaries on the works of [[aristotle]] earned him the title "The Commentator" in the Latin West. Late in life he fell from favour and was banished from the court of the Spanish caliph (003-332).

## Major Contributions

Averroes' works, based on Aristotle's philosophy, propagated a small wave of rationalism in Christian countries (003-324, 003-325). He held the eternity of matter and denied the immortality of the soul; his general view may be described as pantheism (003-326, 003-327).

To avoid difficulties with the orthodox authorities of Islam, Averroes laid down the doctrine of double truth — the coexistence of two independent and contradictory truths, one philosophical and the other religious (003-328, 003-329, 003-330, 003-331). This doctrine allowed him to maintain philosophical positions that contradicted religious dogma while formally acknowledging the authority of faith.

## Influence on European Thought

In the University of Paris, Averroes' teaching produced a school of freethinkers who held that the Creation, the resurrection of the body, and other essential dogmas might be true from the standpoint of religion but are false from the standpoint of reason (003-333, 003-334, 003-335, 003-336). Bury notes the paradoxical character of this position: to a plain mind this seems much as if one said that the doctrine of immortality is true on Sundays but not on week-days, or that the Apostles' Creed is false in the drawing-room and true in the kitchen (003-337, 003-338, 003-339).

This dangerous movement was crushed, and the saving principle of double truth condemned, by Pope John XXI (003-340, 003-341). The spread of Averroistic and similar speculations called forth the theology of [[thomas-aquinas]], who enlisted Aristotle — hitherto the guide of infidelity — on the side of orthodoxy and constructed an ingenious Christian philosophy still authoritative in the Roman Church (003-342, 003-343, 003-344, 003-345).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Averroes represents one of the earliest channels through which Greek rationalism, preserved and developed in the Islamic world, re-entered Western Christendom. His doctrine of double truth, though devised as a protective measure, implicitly conceded that reason and revelation could arrive at contradictory conclusions — a concession that, over the long term, strengthened the hand of rationalist critics of ecclesiastical authority. The Averroistic movement at Paris, though suppressed, demonstrated that philosophical reason could not be indefinitely confined within the boundaries of dogma.
