# James Mill

**James Mill** (1773–1836) was a Scottish utilitarian philosopher whose loss of religious belief was prompted by his reading of Bishop Butler's *Analogy of Religion* (1736), a work intended to defend Christianity that paradoxically raised more doubts than it appeased (006-452).

## Conversion to Unbelief

Bishop [[joseph-butler|Butler]]'s *Analogy* (1736) was considered the ablest defence of the orthodox faith, but it was suspected of having the unintended effect of undermining belief in the very readers it sought to convince. This was the experience of William Pitt the Younger, and it was the *Analogy* that made James Mill an unbeliever (006-452).

## Utilitarian Philosophy

Mill became one of the leading English Utilitarian philosophers, alongside Bentham, his son [[john-stuart-mill|J. S. Mill]], and George Grote. These thinkers preached the principle of the greatest happiness of the greatest number as the supreme object of action and the basis of morality, substituting a new secular ideal for the old ideal based on theological propositions (007-0957).
