## Chapter III
### REASON IN PRISON
(THE MIDDLE AGES)

[003-001]ABOUT ten years after the Edict of [[toleration|Toleration]], Constantine the Great

[003-002]adopted Christianity. This momentous decision inaugurated

[003-003][52] a millennium in which reason was enchained, thought was enslaved,

[003-004]and knowledge made no progress.

[003-005]During the two centuries in which they had been a forbidden sect the

[003-006]Christians had claimed toleration on the ground that religious belief is

[003-007]voluntary and not a thing which can be enforced. When their faith became

[003-008]the predominant creed and had the power of the State behind it, they

[003-009]abandoned this view. They embarked on the hopeful enterprise of bringing

[003-010]about a complete uniformity in men’s opinions on the mysteries of the

[003-011]universe, and began a more or less definite policy of coercing thought.

[003-012]This policy was adopted by Emperors and Governments partly on political

[003-013]grounds; religious divisions, bitter as they were, seemed dangerous to

[003-014]the unity of the State. But the fundamental principle lay in the

[003-015]doctrine that salvation is to be found exclusively in the Christian

[003-016]Church. The profound conviction that those who did not believe in its

[003-017]doctrines would be damned eternally, and that God punishes theological

[003-018]error as if it were the most heinous of crimes, led naturally to

[003-019]persecution. It was a duty to impose on men the only true doctrine,

[003-020]seeing that their own eternal interests were at stake, and to hinder

[003-021]errors from spreading. Heretics were more

[003-022][53] than ordinary criminals and the pains that man could inflict on

[003-023]them were as nothing to the tortures awaiting them in hell. To rid the

[003-024]earth of men who, however virtuous, were, through their religious

[003-025]errors, enemies of the Almighty, was a plain duty. Their virtues were no

[003-026]excuse. We must remember that, according to the humane doctrine of the

[003-027]Christians, pagan, that is, merely human, virtues were vices, and

[003-028]infants who died unbaptized passed the rest of time in creeping on the

[003-029]floor of hell. The intolerance arising from such views could not but

[003-030]differ in kind and intensity from anything that the world had yet

[003-031]witnessed.

[003-032]Besides the logic of its doctrines, the character of its Sacred Book

[003-033]must also be held partly accountable for the intolerant principles of

[003-034]the Christian Church. It was unfortunate that the early Christians had

[003-035]included in their Scripture the Jewish writings which reflect the ideas

[003-036]of a low stage of civilization and are full of savagery. It would be

[003-037]difficult to say how much harm has been done, in corrupting the morals

[003-038]of men, by the precepts and examples of inhumanity, violence, and

[003-039]bigotry which the reverent reader of the Old Testament, implicitly

[003-040]believing in its inspiration, is bound to approve. It furnished an

[003-041]armoury for the theory of

[003-042][54] persecution. The truth is that Sacred Books are an obstacle to

[003-043]moral and intellectual progress, because they consecrate the ideas of a

[003-044]given epoch, and its customs, as divinely appointed. Christianity, by

[003-045]adopting books of a long past age, placed in the path of human

[003-046]development a particularly nasty stumbling-block. It may occur to one to

[003-047]wonder how history might have been altered —altered it surely would have

[003-048]been—if the Christians had cut Jehovah out of their programme and,

[003-049]content with the New Testament, had rejected the inspiration of the Old.

[003-050]Under Constantine the Great and his successors, edict after edict

[003-051]fulminated against the worship of the old pagan gods and against

[003-052]heretical Christian sects. Julian the Apostate, who in his brief reign

[003-053](A.D. 361–3) sought to revive the old order of things, proclaimed

[003-054]universal toleration, but he placed Christians at a disadvantage by

[003-055]forbidding them to teach in schools. This was only a momentary check.

[003-056]Paganism was finally shattered by the severe laws of Theodosius I (end

[003-057]of fourth century). It lingered on here and there for more than another

[003-058]century, especially at Rome and Athens, but had little importance. The

[003-059]Christians were more concerned in striving among themselves than in

[003-060][55] crushing the prostrate spirit of antiquity. The execution of the

[003-061]heretic Priscillian in Spain (fourth century) inaugurated the punishment

[003-062]of heresy by death. It is interesting to see a non-Christian of this age

[003-063]teaching the Christian sects that they should suffer one another.

[003-064]Themistius in an address to the Emperor Valens urged him to repeal his

[003-065]edicts against the Christians with whom he did not agree, and expounded

[003-066]a theory of toleration. “The religious beliefs of individuals are a

[003-067]field in which the authority of a government cannot be effective;

[003-068]compliance can only lead to hypocritical professions. Every faith should

[003-069]be allowed; the civil government should govern orthodox and heterodox to

[003-070]the common good. God himself plainly shows that he wishes various forms

[003-071]of worship; there are many roads by which one can reach him.”

[003-072]No father of the Church has been more esteemed or enjoyed higher

[003-073]authority than St. Augustine (died A.D. 410). He formulated the

[003-074]principle of persecution for the guidance of future generations, basing

[003-075]it on the firm foundation of Scripture—on words used by Jesus Christ in

[003-076]one of his parables, “Compel them to come in.” Till the end of the

[003-077]twelfth century the Church worked hard to suppress heterodoxies. There

[003-078]was much

[003-079][56] persecution, but it was not systematic. There is reason to think

[003-080]that in the pursuit of heresy the Church was mainly guided by

[003-081]considerations of its temporal interest, and was roused to severe action

[003-082]only when the spread of false doctrine threatened to reduce its revenues

[003-083]or seemed a menace to society. At the end of the twelfth century

[003-084]Innocent III became Pope and under him the Church of Western Europe

[003-085]reached the height of its power. He and his immediate successors are

[003-086]responsible for imagining and beginning an organized movement to sweep

[003-087]heretics out of Christendom. Languedoc in Southwestern France was

[003-088]largely populated by heretics, whose opinions were considered

[003-089]particularly offensive, known as the Albigeois. They were the subjects

[003-090]of the Count of Toulouse, and were an industrious and respectable

[003-091]people. But the Church got far too little money out of this anti-

[003-092]clerical population, and Innocent called upon the Count to extirpate

[003-093]heresy from his dominion. As he would not obey, the Pope announced a

[003-094]Crusade against the Albigeois, and offered to all who would bear a hand

[003-095]the usual rewards granted to Crusaders, including absolution from all

[003-096]their sins. A series of sanguinary wars followed in which the

[003-097]Englishman, Simon de Montfort, took part. There were

[003-098][57] wholesale burnings and hangings of men, women and children. The

[003-099]resistance of the people was broken down, though the heresy was not

[003-100]eradicated, and the struggle ended in 1229 with the complete humiliation

[003-101]of the Count of Toulouse. The important point of the episode is this:

[003-102]the Church introduced into the public law of Europe the new principle

[003-103]that a sovran held his crown on the condition that he should extirpate

[003-104]heresy. If he hesitated to persecute at the command of the Pope, he must

[003-105]be coerced; his lands were forfeited; and his dominions were thrown open

[003-106]to be seized by any one whom the Church could induce to attack him. The

[003-107]Popes thus established a theocratic system in which all other interests

[003-108]were to be subordinated to the grand duty of maintaining the purity of

[003-109]the Faith.

[003-110]But in order to root out heresy it was necessary to discover it in its

[003-111]most secret retreats. The Albigeois had been crushed, but the poison of

[003-112]their doctrine was not yet destroyed. The organized system of searching

[003-113]out heretics known as the Inquisition was founded by Pope Gregory IX

[003-114]about A.D. 1233, and fully established by a Bull of Innocent IV (A.D.

[003-115]1252) which regulated the machinery of persecution “as an integral part

[003-116]of the social edifice in every city and every

[003-117][58] State.” This powerful engine for the suppression of the freedom of

[003-118]men’s religious opinions is unique in history.

[003-119]The bishops were not equal to the new talk undertaken by the Church, and

[003-120]in every ecclesiastical province suitable monks were selected and to

[003-121]them was delegated the authority of the Pope for discovering heretics.

[003-122]These inquisitors had unlimited authority, they were subject to no

[003-123]supervision and responsible to no man. It would not have been easy to

[003-124]establish this system but for the fact that contemporary secular rulers

[003-125]had inaugurated independently a merciless legislation against heresy.

[003-126]The Emperor Frederick II, who was himself undoubtedly a freethinker,

[003-127]made laws for his extensive dominions in Italy and Germany (between 1220

[003-128]and 1235), enacting that all heretics should be outlawed, that those who

[003-129]did not recant should be burned, those who recanted should be

[003-130]imprisoned, but if they relapsed should be executed; that their property

[003-131]should be confiscated, their houses destroyed, and their children, to

[003-132]the second generation, ineligible to positions of emolument unless they

[003-133]had betrayed their father or some other heretic.

[003-134]Frederick’s legislation consecrated the stake as the proper punishment

[003-135]for heresy. This

[003-136][59] cruel form of death for that crime seems to have been first

[003-137]inflicted on heretics by a French king (1017). We must remember that in

[003-138]the Middle Ages, and much later, crimes of all kinds were punished with

[003-139]the utmost cruelty. In England in the reign of Henry VIII there is a

[003-140]case of prisoners being boiled to death. Heresy was the foulest of all

[003-141]crimes; and to prevail against it was to prevail against the legions of

[003-142]hell. The cruel enactments against heretics were strongly supported by

[003-143]the public opinion of the masses.

[003-144]When the Inquisition was fully developed it covered Western Christendom

[003-145]with a net from the meshes of which it was difficult for a heretic to

[003-146]escape. The inquisitors in the various kingdoms co-operated, and

[003-147]communicated information; there was “a chain of tribunals throughout

[003-148]continental Europe.” England stood outside the system, but from the age

[003-149]of Henry IV and Henry V the government repressed heresy by the stake

[003-150]under a special statute (A.D. 1400; repealed 1533; revived under Mary;

[003-151]finally repealed in 1676).

[003-152]In its task of imposing unity of belief the Inquisition was most

[003-153]successful in Spain. Here towards the end of the fifteenth century a

[003-154]system was instituted which had peculiarities of its own and was very

[003-155]jealous of

[003-156][60] Roman interference. One of the achievements of the Spanish

[003-157]Inquisition (which was not abolished till the nineteenth century) was to

[003-158]expel the Moriscos or converted Moors, who retained many of their old

[003-159]Mohammedan opinions and customs. It is also said to have eradicated

[003-160]Judaism and to have preserved the country from the zeal of Protestant

[003-161]missionaries. But it cannot be proved that it deserves the credit of

[003-162]having protected Spain against Protestantism, for it is quite possible

[003-163]that if the seeds of Protestant opinion had been sown they would, in any

[003-164]case, have fallen dead on an uncongenial soil. [[freethought|Freedom of thought]]

[003-165]however was entirely suppressed.

[003-166]One of the most efficacious means for hunting down heresy was the “Edict

[003-167]of Faith,” which enlisted the people in the service of the Inquisition

[003-168]and required every man to be an informer. From time to time a certain

[003-169]district was visited and an edict issued commanding those who knew

[003-170]anything of any heresy to come forward and reveal it, under fearful

[003-171]penalties temporal and spiritual. In consequence, no one was free from

[003-172]the suspicion of his neighbours or even of his own family. “No more

[003-173]ingenious device has been invented to subjugate a whole population, to

[003-174]paralyze its intellect, and to reduce it

[003-175][61] to blind obedience. It elevated delation to the rank of high

[003-176]religious duty.”

[003-177]The process employed in the trials of those accused of heresy in Spain

[003-178]rejected every reasonable means for the ascertainment of truth. The

[003-179]prisoner was assumed to be guilty, the burden of proving his innocence

[003-180]rested on him; his judge was virtually his prosecutor. All witnesses

[003-181]against him, however infamous, were admitted. The rules for allowing

[003-182]witnesses for the prosecution were lax; those for rejecting witnesses

[003-183]for the defence were rigid. Jews, Moriscos, and servants could give

[003-184]evidence against the prisoner but not for him, and the same rule applied

[003-185]to kinsmen to the fourth degree. The principle on which the Inquisition

[003-186]proceeded was that better a hundred innocent should suffer than one

[003-187]guilty person escape. Indulgences were granted to any one who

[003-188]contributed wood to the pile. But the tribunal of the Inquisition did

[003-189]not itself condemn to the stake, for the Church must not be guilty of

[003-190]the shedding of blood. The ecclesiastical judge pronounced the prisoner

[003-191]to be a heretic of whose conversion there was no hope, and handed him

[003-192]over (“relaxed” him was the official term) to the secular authority,

[003-193]asking and charging the magistrate “to treat him benignantly and

[003-194]mercifully.” But this

[003-195][62] formal plea for mercy could not be entertained by the civil power;

[003-196]it had no choice but to inflict death; if it did otherwise, it was a

[003-197]promoter of heresy. All princes and officials, according to the Canon

[003-198]Law, must punish duly and promptly heretics handed over to them by the

[003-199]Inquisition, under pain of excommunication. It is to be noted that the

[003-200]number of deaths at the stake has been much over-estimated by popular

[003-201]imagination; but the sum of suffering caused by the methods of the

[003-202]system and the punishments that fell short of death can hardly be

[003-203]exaggerated.

[003-204]The legal processes employed by the Church in these persecutions

[003-205]exercised a corrupting influence on the criminal jurisprudence of the

[003-206]Continent. Lea, the historian of the Inquisition, observes: “Of all the

[003-207]curses which the Inquisition brought in its train, this perhaps was the

[003-208]greatest—that, until the closing years of the eighteenth century,

[003-209]throughout the greater part of Europe, the inquisitorial process, as

[003-210]developed for the destruction of heresy, became the customary method of

[003-211]dealing with all who were under any accusation.”

[003-212]The Inquisitors who, as Gibbon says, “defended nonsense by cruelties,”

[003-213]are often regarded as monsters. It may be said for them and for the

[003-214]kings who did their will that

[003-215][63] they were not a bit worse than the priests and monarchs of

[003-216]primitive ages who sacrificed human beings to their deities. The Greek

[003-217]king, Agamemnon, who immolated his daughter Iphigenia to obtain

[003-218]favourable winds from the gods, was perhaps a most affectionate father,

[003-219]and the seer who advised him to do so may have been a man of high

[003-220]integrity. They acted according to their beliefs. And so in the Middle

[003-221]Ages and afterwards men of kindly temper and the purest zeal for

[003-222]morality were absolutely devoid of mercy where heresy was suspected.

[003-223]Hatred of heresy was a sort of infectious germ, generated by the

[003-224]doctrine of exclusive salvation.

[003-225]It has been observed that this dogma also injured the sense of truth. As

[003-226]man’s eternal fate was at stake, it seemed plainly legitimate or rather

[003-227]imperative to use any means to enforce the true belief—even falsehood

[003-228]and imposture. There was no scruple about the invention of miracles or

[003-229]any fictions that were edifying. A disinterested appreciation of truth

[003-230]will not begin to prevail till the seventeenth century.

[003-231]While this principle, with the associated doctrines of sin, hell, and

[003-232]the last judgment, led to such consequences, there were other doctrines

[003-233]and implications in Christianity which, forming a solid rampart against

[003-234]the

[003-235][64] advance of knowledge, blocked the paths of science in the Middle

[003-236]Ages, and obstructed its progress till the latter half of the nineteenth

[003-237]century. In every important field of scientific research, the ground was

[003-238]occupied by false views which the Church declared to be true on the

[003-239]infallible authority of the Bible. The Jewish account of Creation and

[003-240]the Fall of Man, inextricably bound up with the Christian theory of

[003-241]Redemption, excluded from free inquiry geology, zoology, and

[003-242]anthropology. The literal interpretation of the Bible involved the truth

[003-243]that the sun revolves round the earth. The Church condemned the theory

[003-244]of the antipodes. One of the charges against Servetus (who was burned in

[003-245]the sixteenth century; see below, p. 79) was that he believed the

[003-246]statement of a Greek geographer that Judea is a wretched barren country

[003-247]in spite of the fact that the Bible describes it as a land flowing with

[003-248]milk and honey. The Greek physician Hippocrates had based the study of

[003-249]medicine and disease on experience and methodical research. In the

[003-250]Middle Ages men relapsed to the primitive notions of a barbarous age.

[003-251]Bodily ailments were ascribed to occult agencies—the malice of the Devil

[003-252]or the wrath of God. St. Augustine said that the diseases of Christians

[003-253]were caused by demons,

[003-254][65] and Luther in the same way attributed them to Satan. It was only

[003-255]logical that supernatural remedies should be sought to counteract the

[003-256]effects of supernatural causes. There was an immense traffic in relics

[003-257]with miraculous virtues, and this had the advantage of bringing in a

[003-258]large revenue to the Church. Physicians were often exposed to suspicions

[003-259]of sorcery and unbelief. Anatomy was forbidden, partly perhaps on

[003-260]account of the doctrine of the resurrection of the body. The opposition

[003-261]of ecclesiastics to inoculation in the eighteenth century was a survival

[003-262]of the mediaeval view of disease. Chemistry (alchemy) was considered a

[003-263]diabolical art and in 1317 was condemned by the Pope. The long

[003-264]imprisonment of Roger Bacon (thirteenth century) who, while he professed

[003-265]zeal for orthodoxy, had an inconvenient instinct for scientific

[003-266]research, illustrates the mediaeval distrust of science.

[003-267]It is possible that the knowledge of nature would have progressed

[003-268]little, even if this distrust of science on theological grounds had not

[003-269]prevailed. For Greek science had ceased to advance five hundred years

[003-270]before Christianity became powerful. After about 200 B.C. no important

[003-271]discoveries were made. The explanation of this decay is not easy, but we

[003-272]may be sure that it is to be sought in the

[003-273][66] social conditions of the Greek and Roman world. And we may suspect

[003-274]that the social conditions of the Middle Ages would have proved

[003-275]unfavourable to the scientific spirit— the disinterested quest of

[003-276]facts—even if the controlling beliefs had not been hostile. We may

[003-277]suspect that the rebirth of science would in any case have been

[003-278]postponed till new social conditions, which began to appear in the

[003-279]thirteenth century (see next Chapter), had reached a certain maturity.

[003-280]Theological prejudice may have injured knowledge principally by its

[003-281]survival after the Middle Ages had passed away. In other words, the harm

[003-282]done by Christian doctrines, in this respect, may lie less in the

[003-283]obscurantism of the dark interval between ancient and modern

[003-284]civilization, than in the obstructions which they offered when science

[003-285]had revived in spite of them and could no longer be crushed.

[003-286]The firm belief in witchcraft, magic, and demons was inherited by the

[003-287]Middle Ages from antiquity, but it became far more lurid and made the

[003-288]world terrible. Men believed that they were surrounded by fiends

[003-289]watching for every opportunity to harm them, that pestilences, storms,

[003-290]eclipses, and famines were the work of the Devil; but they believed as

[003-291]firmly that ecclesiastical rites were capable of coping with these

[003-292]enemies. Some of the

[003-293][67] early Christian Emperors legislated against magic, but till the

[003-294]fourteenth century there was no systematic attempt to root out

[003-295]witchcraft. The fearful epidemic, known as the Black Death, which

[003-296]devastated Europe in that century, seems to have aggravated the haunting

[003-297]terror of the invisible world of demons. Trials for witchcraft

[003-298]multiplied, and for three hundred years the discovery of witchcraft and

[003-299]the destruction of those who were accused of practising it, chiefly

[003-300]women, was a standing feature of European civilization. Both the theory

[003-301]and the persecution were supported by Holy Scripture. “Thou shalt not

[003-302]suffer a witch to live” was the clear injunction of the highest

[003-303]authority. Pope Innocent VIII issued a Bull on the matter (1484) in

[003-304]which he asserted that plagues and storms are the work of witches, and

[003-305]the ablest minds believed in the reality of their devilish powers.

[003-306]No story is more painful than the persecution of witches, and nowhere

[003-307]was it more atrocious than in England and Scotland. I mention it because

[003-308]it was the direct result of theological doctrines, and because, as we

[003-309]shall see, it was rationalism which brought the long chapter of horrors

[003-310]to an end.

[003-311]In the period, then, in which the Church exercised its greatest

[003-312]influence, reason was

[003-313][68] enchained in the prison which Christianity had built around the

[003-314]human mind. It was not indeed inactive, but its activity took the form

[003-315]of heresy; or, to pursue the metaphor, those who broke chains were

[003-316]unable for the most part to scale the walls of the prison; their freedom

[003-317]extended only so far as to arrive at beliefs, which, like orthodoxy

[003-318]itself, were based on Christian mythology. There were some exceptions to

[003-319]the rule. At the end of the twelfth century a stimulus from another

[003-320]world began to make itself felt. The philosophy of Aristotle became

[003-321]known to learned men in Western Christendom; their teachers were Jews

[003-322]and Mohammedans. Among the Mohammedans there was a certain amount of

[003-323]free thought, provoked by their knowledge of ancient Greek speculation.

[003-324]The works of the freethinker Averroes (twelfth century) which were based

[003-325]on Aristotle’s philosophy, propagated a small wave of rationalism in

[003-326]Christian countries. Averroes held the eternity of matter and denied the

[003-327]immortality of the soul; his general view may be described as pantheism.

[003-328]But he sought to avoid difficulties with the orthodox authorities of

[003-329]Islam by laying down the doctrine of double truth, that is the

[003-330]coexistence of two independent and contradictory truths, the one

[003-331]philosophical, and the other religious. This

[003-332][69] did not save him from being banished from the court of the Spanish

[003-333]caliph. In the University of Paris his teaching produced a school of

[003-334]freethinkers who held that the Creation, the resurrection of the body,

[003-335]and other essential dogmas, might be true from the standpoint of

[003-336]religion but are false from the standpoint of reason. To a plain mind

[003-337]this seems much as if one said that the doctrine of immortality is true

[003-338]on Sundays but not on week-days, or that the Apostles’ Creed is false in

[003-339]the drawing-room and true in the kitchen. This dangerous movement was

[003-340]crushed, and the saving principle of double truth condemned, by Pope

[003-341]John XXI. The spread of Averroistic and similar speculations called

[003-342]forth the Theology of Thomas, of Aquino in South Italy (died 1274), a

[003-343]most subtle thinker, whose mind had a natural turn for scepticism. He

[003-344]enlisted Aristotle, hitherto the guide of infidelity, on the side of

[003-345]orthodoxy, and constructed an ingenious Christian philosophy which is

[003-346]still authoritative in the Roman Church. But Aristotle and reason are

[003-347]dangerous allies for faith, and the treatise of Thomas is perhaps more

[003-348]calculated to unsettle a believing mind by the doubts which it

[003-349]powerfully states than to quiet the scruples of a doubter by its

[003-350]solutions.

[003-351]There must always have been some private

[003-352][70] and underground unbelief here and there, which did not lead to any

[003-353]serious consequences. The blasphemous statement that the world had been

[003-354]deceived by three impostors, Moses, Jesus, and Mohammed, was current in

[003-355]the thirteenth century. It was attributed to the freethinking Emperor

[003-356]Frederick II (died 1250), who has been described as “the first modern

[003-357]man.” The same idea, in a milder form, was expressed in the story of the

[003-358]Three Rings, which is at least as old. A Mohammedan ruler, desiring to

[003-359]extort money from a rich Jew, summoned him to his court and laid a snare

[003-360]for him. “My friend,” he said, “I have often heard it reported that thou

[003-361]art a very wise man. Tell me therefore which of the three religions,

[003-362]that of the Jews, that of the Mohammedans, and that of the Christians,

[003-363]thou believest to be the truest.” The Jew saw that a trap was laid for

[003-364]him and answered as follows: “My lord, there was once a rich man who

[003-365]among his treasures had a ring of such great value that he wished to

[003-366]leave it as a perpetual heirloom to his successors. So he made a will

[003-367]that whichever of his sons should be found in possession of this ring

[003-368]after his death should be considered his heir. The son to whom he gave

[003-369]the ring acted in the same way as his father, and so the ring passed

[003-370]from hand to

[003-371][71] hand. At last it came into the possession of a man who had three

[003-372]sons whom he loved equally. Unable to make up his mind to which of them

[003-373]he should leave the ring, he promised it to each of them privately, and

[003-374]then in order to satisfy them all caused a goldsmith to make two other

[003-375]rings so closely resembling the true ring that he was unable to

[003-376]distinguish them himself. On his death-bed he gave each of them a ring,

[003-377]and each claimed to be his heir, but no one could prove his title

[003-378]because the rings were indistinguishable, and the suit at law lasts till

[003-379]this day. It is even so, my lord, with the three religions, given by God

[003-380]to the three peoples. They each think they have the true religion, but

[003-381]which of them really has it, is a question, like that of the rings,
