# Epicureanism

Epicureanism was one of the three great Hellenistic schools of philosophy that, alongside Stoicism and Skepticism, emerged from the intellectual freedom of classical Athens. According to J. B. Bury, these philosophies "had a common source in the conversations of Socrates" and "have had a deeper influence on the progress of man than any other continuous intellectual movement, at least until the rise of modern science in a new epoch of liberty" (002-284). Founded by Epicurus (341–270 BCE), Epicureanism was distinguished by its uncompromising materialism and its explicit aim of liberating the human mind from religious fear.

## Definition and Origins

Epicureanism was founded by Epicurus in Athens around 307 BCE, taught in a garden outside the city walls that became known as "the Garden." The school admitted women and slaves, a notable departure from the exclusivity of Plato's Academy and Aristotle's Lyceum. Bury observes that the doctrines of "the Epicureans, Stoics, and Sceptics all aimed at securing peace and guidance for the individual soul" (002-289), and all three were "widely propagated throughout the Greek world from the third century B.C." (002-291).

What set Epicureanism apart from its Hellenistic rivals was its explicitly anti-religious character. Unlike the Stoics, who saw reason (logos) as a divine principle pervading the universe, Epicurus was a thoroughgoing Materialist, "explaining the world by the atomic theory of Democritus and denying any divine government of the universe" (002-297). He did not deny the existence of gods outright, but held that "so far as men are concerned, his gods are as if they were not—living in some remote abode and enjoying a 'sacred and everlasting calm'" (002-300). They served merely as an example of the realization of the ideal "Epicurean life" (002-302).

## Historical Development

The core insight of Epicureanism, as Bury presents it, was that "fear" is "the fundamental motive of religion, and to free men's minds from this fear was a principal object of his teaching" (002-295). This conviction — that religion is rooted in fear of the unknown, and that knowledge of nature dispels that fear — makes Epicureanism one of the earliest systematic programs of intellectual liberation in Western thought.

In the first century BCE, Epicureanism found its greatest literary champion in the Roman poet Lucretius, who "regarded Epicurus as the great deliverer of the human race and determined to proclaim the glad tidings of his philosophy in a poem *On the Nature of the World*" (002-305). Bury describes how Lucretius, "with all the fervour of a religious enthusiast he denounces religion, sounding every note of defiance, loathing, and contempt, and branding in burning words the crimes to which it had urged man on" (002-308). It was an extraordinary irony: a philosophy that "aimed at perfect calm" was expounded with "the rapture of his enthusiasm" (002-313).

> He rides forth as a leader of the hosts of atheism against the walls of heaven. He explains the scientific arguments as if they were the radiant revelation of a new world; and the rapture of his enthusiasm is a strange accompaniment of a doctrine which aimed at perfect calm.
> (002-311, 002-314)

Bury notes, however, that the impact of Lucretius was limited by his context: "the educated Romans in the days of Lucretius were sceptical in religious matters, some of them were Epicureans, and we may suspect that not many of those who read it were shocked or influenced by the audacities of the champion of irreligion" (002-321). Nevertheless, "in the literature of free thought it must always hold an eminent place by the sincerity of its audacious, defiant spirit" (002-317).

In a later epoch, Epicurean thought resurfaced in transformed ways. During the Renaissance, the philosopher Giordano Bruno drew inspiration from "Epicurus, from whom he took the theory of the infinity of the universe" (004-240), though "Epicurean materialism was transformed into a pantheistic mysticism" (004-241) in Bruno's hands. In the seventeenth century, Thomas Hobbes "had revived materialism in its Epicurean shape" (006-061), though Bury observes he was not a champion of freedom of conscience.

## Key Proponents

- **Epicurus** (341–270 BCE): Founder of the school; taught that the goal of philosophy was *ataraxia* (tranquility), achieved by understanding nature and thereby dispelling fear of the gods and of death.
- **Lucretius** (c. 99–55 BCE): Roman poet and author of *De Rerum Natura* (*On the Nature of Things*), the most complete surviving exposition of Epicurean philosophy. Bury calls his poem "a hymn of triumph over prostrate deities" (002-316).
- **Democritus** (c. 460–370 BCE): Pre-Socratic philosopher whose atomic theory provided the physical foundation for Epicurean materialism.
- **Giordano Bruno** (1548–1600): Renaissance philosopher who adapted Epicurean ideas about the infinity of the universe within a pantheistic framework (004-240).
- **Thomas Hobbes** (1588–1679): English philosopher who revived Epicurean materialism in the seventeenth century (006-061).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Epicureanism contributed to freedom of thought in three lasting ways. First, it made the **liberation of the mind from religious fear** an explicit philosophical goal. By teaching that the universe operates according to natural laws rather than divine will, Epicurus opened a path to understanding the world without recourse to superstition — a path that would later be taken up by the Enlightenment.

Second, Epicureanism demonstrated that a **comprehensive materialist worldview** could be intellectually serious and ethically rich. Its account of nature, based on atoms and void, required no divine creator and no divine intervention. This provided a template for later materialist philosophies, from Hobbes in the seventeenth century to the scientific naturalists of the nineteenth.

Third, through Lucretius, Epicureanism bequeathed to the Western tradition one of its most powerful **literary expressions of freethought**. The *De Rerum Natura*, rediscovered in the Renaissance, continued to inspire those who questioned religious orthodoxy. Bury observed that even when Epicurean ideas resurfaced in thinkers like Bruno, the original anti-religious thrust could be redirected toward new forms of rational inquiry.

However, Epicureanism had significant limitations from the standpoint of intellectual liberty. Its aim was individual peace of mind (*ataraxia*), not social reform or political liberty. It did not, like Stoicism, develop a doctrine of natural law that could challenge unjust political authority. The Epicurean prescription — withdraw from public life, cultivate friendships, and study nature — was a personal strategy for freedom, not a program for transforming society.

## Criticism and Opposition

Epicureanism faced opposition from both religious and philosophical quarters. Early Christians regarded it as a form of godlessness, and the school was often misrepresented as advocating hedonism in the vulgar sense, rather than the refined tranquility Epicurus actually taught. The Stoics, meanwhile, rejected Epicurean materialism because it denied the providential ordering of the universe — the *logos* that was central to Stoic ethics and cosmology.

In the Roman world, Epicureanism never achieved the political influence of Stoicism. As Bury notes, "the Stoic philosophy made notable contributions to the cause of liberty and could hardly have flourished in an atmosphere where discussion was not free. It asserted the rights of individuals against public authority" (002-323). Epicureanism, by contrast, offered no comparable basis for resistance to tyranny — its advice was to retreat, not to challenge.
