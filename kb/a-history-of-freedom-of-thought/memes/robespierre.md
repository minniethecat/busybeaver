# Maximilien Robespierre

**Maximilien Robespierre** (1758–1794) was a leading figure of the [[the-french-revolution|French Revolution]] whose position on [[religion]] and [[freedom of thought]] was complex and consequential. While the Revolution is often associated with secularism and atheism, Robespierre declared against the policy of de-Christianization pursued by more radical revolutionaries, insisting instead on a deistic state religion (005-402).

## Life and Background

Born in Arras, in northern France, Robespierre was the son of a lawyer. He studied at the Collège Louis-le-Grand in Paris on a scholarship, where he excelled academically and absorbed the ideas of the [[the-enlightenment|Enlightenment]] — particularly the political philosophy of [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Rousseau]]. He practiced law in Arras before being elected as a deputy of the Third Estate to the Estates-General in 1789.

Rising rapidly through the revolutionary ranks, Robespierre became a dominant voice in the National Convention and the Committee of Public Safety. His advocacy for universal male suffrage, the abolition of slavery, and the rights of citizens made him popular with the Parisian working class — but his central role in the Reign of Terror (1793–1794) made him one of the most controversial figures in modern history.

## Major Contributions

Robespierre's political program centered on the concept of "virtue" supported by "terror" — the idea that revolutionary government required both moral legitimacy and the willingness to use force against enemies of the people. His speeches articulated a vision of a republic founded on civic virtue rather than hereditary privilege or religious authority.

His most distinctive intellectual contribution was the **Cult of the Supreme Being** (*Culte de l'Être Suprême*), a deistic civic religion he introduced in 1794. The cult celebrated a rational creator god and the immortality of the soul while rejecting the supernatural claims of Christianity and the atheism of more radical revolutionaries:

> Robespierre declared against the policy of de-Christianization pursued by Hébert and others, asserting that atheism was aristocratic.
> (005-402)

Robespierre distinguished between "atheists" who denied all religious sentiment and patriotic deists who acknowledged a supreme being and the immortality of the soul. He had those atheists who did not think like Robespierre executed (005-409).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Robespierre's relationship to [[freedom of thought]] was deeply paradoxical. He opposed the Catholic Church as an institution of oppression, yet he also opposed atheism as a threat to civic order. His Cult of the Supreme Being represented an attempt to create a state religion compatible with reason — but by enforcing it through the machinery of the Terror, he reproduced the very intolerance he claimed to oppose.

The historian [[William Godwin]] recognized this paradox: a work that had been thrown at Robespierre's feet — Godwin's *Political Justice* — argued for a more genuine liberty of thought than the revolutionary leader was willing to permit (006-816). Godwin's book is remarkable as one of the first sustained arguments for the complete abolition of state interference in matters of belief.

## Legacy and Influence

Robespierre fell from power in July 1794 (the Thermidorian Reaction) and was executed without trial — consumed by the same revolutionary violence he had helped to unleash. His attempt to impose a state religion by decree was quickly abandoned.

Yet the question he raised — whether a secular republic needs a civic religion to sustain itself — has persisted. Later thinkers from [[Auguste Comte]] to twentieth-century secular humanists would grapple with the same problem Robespierre had confronted: how to maintain social cohesion without the coercive apparatus of traditional religion.
