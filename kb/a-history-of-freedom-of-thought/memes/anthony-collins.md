# Anthony Collins

**Anthony Collins** (1676–1729) was an English country gentleman and a disciple of [[John Locke|Locke]], whose writings on freethinking and the critique of prophecy established him as one of the leading Deist controversialists of early eighteenth-century England. He argued that all religious questions should be referred to [[reason]], and complained of the general intolerance which prevailed in his time (006-256).

## Life and Background

Collins was a country gentleman of independent means, which allowed him to devote himself to philosophical and theological writing without the institutional constraints faced by clergymen or academics. His intellectual formation was decisively shaped by his association with [[John Locke]], whose empiricist philosophy and writings on toleration provided the foundation for Collins's own freethinking project.

Collins maintained correspondence with Locke and was entrusted by the philosopher with private manuscripts. He also fell under the influence of [[Pierre Bayle|Bayle]]'s skeptical method, which is evident in his first major work on freethinking.

## Major Contributions

Collins published his *Discourse of Free-thinking* in 1713, pleading for free discussion and the reference of all religious questions to reason (006-256). The work, which bore the evident influence of Bayle, argued that unrestricted rational inquiry was both a natural right and a necessary condition for discovering religious truth.

Twenty years later, in 1733, he published his *Discourse on the Grounds and Reasons of the Christian Religion*, in which he drastically exposed the weakness of the evidence for the fulfilment of prophecy. Collins demonstrated that the argument from prophecy depends on forced and unnatural figurative interpretations (006-256). This systematic critique of one of the principal arguments used to defend the truth of Revelation — alongside the miracles of the New Testament — made Collins a formidable figure in the Deist controversy.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Collins's *Discourse of Free-thinking* was a landmark defense of intellectual liberty. He maintained that the reference of all religious questions to reason was essential, and he openly complained of the general intolerance which prevailed in English society (006-256).

His critique of prophecy was particularly significant for freedom of thought because it struck at what was then considered one of the strongest rational defenses of Christianity. By exposing the forced and unnatural interpretations on which prophetic fulfillment claims depended, Collins undermined the intellectual foundations of religious authority and demonstrated that free rational inquiry could challenge doctrines long considered beyond question.

Significantly, Collins escaped with comparative impunity for his views, in contrast to contemporaries such as [[Thomas Woolston|Woolston]] who faced prosecution (006-256). His ability to publish without severe legal consequence reflected the gradual, uneven progress of toleration in Hanoverian England.

## Legacy and Influence

Collins's work contributed to the broader English Deist movement, which sought to subject revelation to the test of reason. His critique of prophecy was widely discussed and helped shift the terms of theological debate from authority-based claims to evidential reasoning.

The very fact that Collins could publish such arguments — and that the facts testifying to intolerance in his time simultaneously testified to the spread of unbelief — illustrated the dynamic he himself described: as freethinking advanced, the mechanisms of intolerance became increasingly visible and contested (006-256).
