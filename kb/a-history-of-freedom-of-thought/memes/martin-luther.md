# Martin Luther

**Martin Luther** (1483–1546) was a German theologian and Augustinian friar whose protest against Papal Indulgences launched the [[Protestant Reformation]]. The rebellion led by Luther was the result not of a revolt of reason against dogmas, but of widely spread anti-clerical feeling due to the corruption of the Church (004-103). His study of the theory of Papal Indulgences led Luther on to his theological heresies (004-107).

## Life and Background

Luther was born in Eisleben, Saxony. He entered the Augustinian order and became a professor of theology at the University of Wittenberg. His growing conviction that salvation came through faith alone — *sola fide* — brought him into conflict with the Church's teaching on indulgences and good works.

In 1517, Luther famously nailed his Ninety-five Theses to the door of the Wittenberg Castle Church, challenging the sale of indulgences. What began as a scholarly dispute escalated into a continent-wide rupture when Luther refused to recant at the Diet of Worms (1521), declaring his conscience captive to the Word of God.

## Major Contributions

Luther's translation of the Bible into German made the scriptures accessible to ordinary people, profoundly shaping the German language. His doctrine of the priesthood of all believers challenged the hierarchical structure of the medieval Church. Yet the authority he replaced the Pope with — the Bible — proved no more tolerant of dissent:

> It was the Bible according to Luther or the Bible according to Calvin. So far as the spirit of intolerance went, there was nothing to choose between the new authorities and the old.
> (004-119)

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Luther's relationship to [[freedom of thought]] is deeply paradoxical. He shattered the monopoly of the Catholic Church over European Christendom, creating space for a diversity of religious viewpoints that he himself never intended or welcomed.

Luther was quite opposed to liberty of conscience and worship, a doctrine which was inconsistent with Scripture as he read it (004-126). He might advocate the absolute power of the civil ruler in religious matters, expecting the prince to enforce true doctrine (004-137). Once reason is exercised in religious matters, there is no logical stopping point:

> Why should we stop where Luther or Calvin or any of the other rebels stopped, unless we assume that one of them was inspired?
> (004-201)

This question — posed by [[Castellion]] and the advocates of toleration — exposed the contradiction at the heart of the Reformation. By appealing to private judgment against the Pope, Luther opened a door he could not close. The principle of [[private judgment]], once admitted, led inexorably beyond Lutheranism.

## The Unintended Legacy

Unlike [[Castellion]] or later advocates of [[toleration]], Luther did not argue for freedom of conscience. He attributed dissent to the devil (003-254). With Luther as with the Pope, the Bible interpreted by the new authorities was equally fatal to those who disagreed (004-168).

Yet the Reformation he launched, by breaking the unity of Christendom and making the Bible available in vernacular languages, created the conditions in which arguments for [[freedom of thought]] could eventually flourish. The [[Socinian]] doctrines, for instance, went further than Luther in rejecting traditional dogmas — because, unlike Luther, they applied reason consistently to Scripture (005-032).

| Aspect | Luther's Position |
|--------|------------------|
| Authority | Bible alone (*sola scriptura*) |
| Toleration | Opposed — the prince should enforce true religion |
| Private judgment | Allowed against the Pope, denied to others |
| Relationship to reason | Subordinate to Scripture |

## Legacy and Influence

Luther's influence on the development of [[freedom of thought]] is indirect but immense. By demonstrating that the most powerful religious institution in Europe could be successfully challenged, he set a precedent for all subsequent dissidents — even those whose conclusions he would have condemned.

The Lutheran confession became one of the three chief religions recognized in some European states, alongside the [[Reformed]] and [[Catholic]] churches (005-557). The Peace of Westphalia (1648), which ended the religious wars Luther's movement had helped to ignite, established the principle that sovereigns could determine their territory's religion — a very limited form of toleration, but a step away from the ideal of a uniform Christendom.
