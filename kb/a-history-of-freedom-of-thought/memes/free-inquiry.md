## Definition and Origins

Free inquiry, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the principle that all questions—including those concerning religion, morality, and the social order—should be open to unrestricted rational investigation.

Bury defines free thought itself as "the refusal of thought to be constrained by authority, tradition, or prescribed conclusions" (001-229). Free inquiry is the methodological expression of this principle: the active pursuit of knowledge without predetermined limits.

The spirit of free inquiry first emerged in ancient Greece. Bury notes that the general spirit of Greek philosophy was one of "free inquiry and discussion," setting a precedent that would echo through subsequent centuries (002-091). The Socratic method—questioning every assumption and following argument wherever it leads—remains the archetype of free inquiry.

In contrast, societies dominated by religious authority characteristically "excluded from free inquiry geology, zoology, and" other fields whose findings might challenge theological doctrines (003-241). The history of freedom of thought is in large part the history of the struggle to expand the domain of free inquiry against institutional resistance.

## Historical Development

The classical Greek tradition of free inquiry was largely suppressed during the Christian Middle Ages, when ecclesiastical authority defined the boundaries of permissible investigation. Bury documents how the medieval Church excluded entire domains of knowledge "from free inquiry," including the natural sciences when they threatened doctrinal positions (003-241).

The Renaissance and Scientific Revolution gradually restored free inquiry as a governing ideal of intellectual life. The experimental method, which demanded verification rather than deference to authority, was inherently a method of free inquiry. By the seventeenth century, thinkers like Francis Bacon and René Descartes had articulated the case for systematic doubt and independent investigation.

The Enlightenment extended free inquiry from natural philosophy to religion, politics, and morality. Bury observes that knowledge and civilization "are advanced by criticism and negation, as well as by construction and positive discovery" (006-546).

This principle—that critical thinking advances civilization—applies as much to religious and political ideas as to scientific ones. The nineteenth century saw free inquiry institutionalized in universities, scientific societies, and secular publications.

## Key Proponents

- **Socrates** (c. 470–399 BCE), whose method of relentless questioning made him the founding figure of free inquiry. Bury notes that Socrates "taught those with whom he conversed" to examine their beliefs critically, embodying the principle that no assumption is beyond investigation (002-177).

- **Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677), who argued that the freedom to philosophize and inquire is essential to both individual flourishing and social peace. His *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) was a sustained argument for liberty of thought and inquiry.

- **John Locke** (1632–1704), whose epistemology emphasized that knowledge comes from experience and reason, not authority—making free inquiry the necessary path to truth. His *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) provided the philosophical framework for open investigation.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Free inquiry is both the method and the goal of intellectual liberty. Without the right to investigate freely, freedom of thought remains an abstract principle; without freedom of thought, free inquiry has no protected space in which to operate. The two are mutually reinforcing.

Bury's narrative demonstrates that every major advance in human knowledge—from Greek philosophy to modern science to biblical criticism—depended on the prior establishment of free inquiry as a legitimate intellectual practice. Conversely, every episode of intellectual repression—the Inquisition, censorship, blasphemy laws—was an attempt to restrict the scope of free inquiry.

The institutionalization of free inquiry in universities, learned societies, and publishing created permanent platforms for intellectual independence. Bury observes that these institutions, though often founded under religious auspices, gradually became vehicles for "the refusal of thought to be constrained" (001-229).
