## Definition and Origins

Miracles, in the theological sense treated by Bury, are events attributed to supernatural divine intervention that suspend or override the ordinary laws of nature. In the Christian tradition, the miracles of the New Testament — particularly those performed by Jesus and the apostles — were cited as the principal evidence for the truth of the Christian revelation. The critical examination of miracle claims became one of the central battlegrounds between rationalism and orthodox Christianity in the eighteenth century.

The early Church had shown little "scruple about the invention of miracles" (003-228) when it served polemical or evangelical purposes. The credulity of the age meant that miracle claims were rarely subjected to critical scrutiny, and the accumulation of miracle stories reinforced the authority of the Church that authenticated them.

## Historical Development

The rationalist critique of miracles developed gradually through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, reaching its fullest expression in the deist controversy. The traditional Christian apologetic rested heavily on miracles: "the fulfilment of prophecies and the miracles of the New Testament were" the twin pillars of the evidential case for Christianity (006-255). To undermine belief in miracles was therefore to strike at the foundation of revealed religion.

The most philosophically influential critique came from [[david-hume]], whose essay "Of Miracles" argued that it is always more probable that the testimony for a miracle is mistaken than that a miracle actually occurred. Hume's argument rested on the uniformity of natural law: a miracle is by definition a violation of the laws of nature, and the evidence for the uniformity of those laws is overwhelming. Some critics sought to "adopt the line of arguing that miracles are incredible or" impossible by definition (006-272).

[[conyers-middleton]]'s *Free Inquiry into Christian Miracles* (1748) applied historical criticism to the early Church's miracle claims, arguing that "the chief miracles related in the Gospels" were suspicious both in their character and in the circumstances of their recording (006-273) (006-444). Middleton asked pointedly whether early Christians could really "have the power of performing miracles" when the historical record showed that miracle claims were often fabricated for partisan purposes (006-446).

The most radical approach was that of [[thomas-woolston]], who argued that the Gospel miracles should not be taken literally at all but read as allegorical narratives. Bury records that Woolston "put" forward the provocative thesis that it was an "absurdity of the question to suppose the miracles literally" true (006-303). This allegorical interpretation, while ingenious, satisfied neither orthodox believers nor rationalist critics.

## Key Proponents of the Critique

- **David Hume** — His essay "On Miracles" (1748) provided the definitive philosophical argument against the credibility of miracle testimony. Hume's argument was, in Bury's summary, that "miracles had not been submitted to a genuine historical scrutiny" (006-618) and that the testimony for them was inherently unreliable.
- **Conyers Middleton** — His *Free Inquiry* (1748) examined the historical evidence for post-apostolic miracles and concluded that "genuine and spurious miracles are indistinguishable" (006-722), making all miracle claims equally suspect. Middleton showed that the "succession which saw the last genuine miracles performed could not" be reliably traced through church history (006-719).
- **Edward Gibbon** — Applied historical method to "Gibbon's treatment of miracles from the purely historical point of view" (006-692), treating miracle claims as historical data to be assessed by the same standards as any other testimony.

The orthodox response, exemplified by [[william-paley]], insisted that "there is no difficulty about miracles" if one accepts the existence of an omnipotent God, and Paley "bases the case for Christianity" squarely on the miracle testimony (006-764) (006-765). Paley argued that the "proof of the New Testament miracles is that the" apostles were willing to die for their testimony, which they would not do for a known falsehood (006-766). The debate between Hume and Middleton on one side and Paley on the other framed the miracle controversy for subsequent generations.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The eighteenth-century debate over miracles advanced freedom of thought in two crucial ways. First, it established the principle that religious claims — even those central to orthodox doctrine — could and must be subjected to the same standards of evidence as any other historical claim. Second, it demonstrated that rational criticism could engage with the Bible itself, not merely with the institutional Church. If the New Testament miracles could be questioned, then no religious claim was immune from rational scrutiny.

The miracle controversy also highlighted a fundamental tension in Christian apologetics. Orthodox defenders sought to use miracles as evidence for revelation, but this opened the door to rational evaluation of the evidence — and the evidence, as Hume and Middleton showed, was far weaker than the orthodox case required. In Bury's narrative, the miracle debate was a crucial step in the broader rationalist advance, demonstrating that "testimony to miracles, and the testimony seems no less" authoritative, could not withstand careful cross-examination (006-717).

By the nineteenth century, the advances of science had made miracle claims increasingly difficult to sustain. The uniformity of natural law, established by Newtonian physics and reinforced by geology and biology, left little room for supernatural intervention. The "records" of miracles came to be seen as "quite apart from miracles" in the sense of providing independent historical evidence (007-0336). Rationalists increasingly "considered miracles impossible" (007-0583), and the question shifted from whether particular miracles occurred to whether the very concept of miracle was coherent in a universe governed by natural law.
