# Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 3rd Earl of Shaftesbury

**Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 3rd Earl of Shaftesbury** (1671–1713) was an English philosopher, writer, and politician. His elegant writings on virtue, religion, and the use of ridicule in philosophical argument made him one of the most influential figures in the early English Enlightenment (006-397).

## Life and Background

Born into a prominent Whig family — his grandfather, the 1st Earl of Shaftesbury, had been a leading Exclusionist — Shaftesbury was tutored by [[John Locke]] as a child. He served in Parliament and briefly in the House of Lords before ill health forced his retirement from public life. He spent his later years in Italy and the Netherlands, devoting himself to philosophy.

Shaftesbury's major work, *Characteristics of Men, Manners, Opinions, Times* (1711), collected his earlier essays and established his literary reputation throughout Europe. His style was urbane, polished, and carefully crafted — qualities that distinguished him from the more systematic philosophers of his era.

## Major Contributions

Shaftesbury developed a philosophy of moral sense and natural virtue:

| Area | Contribution |
|------|-------------|
| Moral Philosophy | Virtue is natural to humanity; moral sense is innate |
| Religion | Attacked orthodox Christianity as degrading to human dignity |
| Aesthetics | Linked beauty, goodness, and truth in a unified vision |
| Method | Championed wit and ridicule as philosophical tools |

In his *Inquiry concerning Virtue* (1699), Shaftesbury argued that virtue is not a matter of divine command but arises naturally from human affections properly balanced and directed toward the common good (006-408).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Shaftesbury's contribution to [[freedom of thought]] lay primarily in two areas: his critique of orthodox Christianity and his defense of free inquiry through wit and ridicule.

> The Old Testament roused Shaftesbury's aversion. He attacks Scriptural religion for presenting God as a "revokable and revengeful man."
> (006-426, 006-432)

He found the doctrine of eternal punishment and the depiction of a wrathful deity deeply offensive to human dignity. For Shaftesbury, true religion must be compatible with virtue and reason — a conviction that aligned him with the [[deism]] movement while distinguishing him from its more aridly rationalist exponents.

Shaftesbury also championed the role of humor and satire in philosophical argument. He believed that false and pernicious doctrines should be tested by ridicule — if they could not withstand laughter, they did not deserve reverence. This controversial position pleased Lord Shaftesbury's sense that wit exposes what argument alone cannot reach (006-422).

## Legacy and Influence

Shaftesbury's influence on the Continental Enlightenment was considerable. His writings were admired and translated by [[Denis Diderot]] and influenced French deists. The optimism of Shaftesbury — his conviction that the universe is fundamentally benevolent and that human nature tends toward virtue — was criticized by later thinkers who saw it as insufficiently attentive to the reality of evil (006-456).

Shaftesbury famously described Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan* as a book "vomited from the jaws of the devil" — a judgment that, while colorful, reflects his conviction that the reduction of human motivation to self-interest was both false and morally dangerous (007-0616).

In the history of [[freedom of thought]], Shaftesbury represents a distinctive voice: the philosopher of civilized wit, who believed that good humor and refined taste could advance liberty of thought as effectively as systematic argument.
