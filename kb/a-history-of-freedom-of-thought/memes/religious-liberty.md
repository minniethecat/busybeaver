# Religious Liberty

**Religious liberty** — the legal and political guarantee that individuals may hold, practice, and propagate their religious beliefs without state coercion — represents in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought* the institutional embodiment of the freedom of thought principle. Bury traces the slow, contested emergence of religious liberty across Europe, treating it as both a precondition for and a product of the broader advance of rational inquiry.

## Definition and Origins

Religious liberty differs from the narrower concept of [[toleration]] in important ways. Toleration, as Bury notes, "means incomplete religious liberty" (005-013) — a concession granted by a dominant authority, revocable at will. Religious liberty, by contrast, is a right recognized in law, independent of the goodwill of the powerful. It entails not merely permission to dissent but the principle that the state has no legitimate authority over religious belief.

The concept has roots in the radical Reformation of the sixteenth century, when persecuted minorities — [[Anabaptists]], [[Socinians]], and others — began to articulate arguments for religious freedom grounded in the nature of faith itself. If belief cannot be coerced, they reasoned, then the attempt to enforce religious uniformity is both futile and impious.

## Historical Development

Bury structures much of his history around the uneven progress of religious liberty across European states (002-464). The trajectory was not a straight line:

The [[Reformation]] inadvertently advanced the cause by breaking the monopoly of the Roman Church, but the Reformers themselves largely "asserted religious liberty" only for their own beliefs (004-189). [[Luther]] and [[Calvin]] recognized the "right of private judgment" against Rome but suppressed it when it issued in conclusions they rejected (004-111).

> [004-189] principle of religious liberty. But the Reformers had asserted it only for themselves.

The seventeenth century brought important advances. The [[Edict of Nantes]] (1598) and its revocation (1685) illustrated both the possibility of religious peace and its fragility. The [[English Toleration Act]] of 1689 established a limited but durable framework for Protestant dissent. The [[American Revolution]] and the [[First Amendment]] (1791) marked a decisive break with the European pattern of established churches.

> [005-254] The achievement of religious liberty in England in the nineteenth century was the work of…

The nineteenth century saw religious liberty become the norm across the Western world, though unevenly. "Religious liberty has been realized in European countries" (005-060) to varying degrees — fully in England and America, partially and with reversals in France and Germany.

## Key Proponents

[[Roger Williams]], founder of [[Rhode Island]], made one of the earliest and most consistent arguments for religious liberty in the English-speaking world. [[John Milton]]'s *Areopagitica* (1644) defended freedom of the press but rested on principles that implied religious liberty as well.

[[Locke]]'s *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) provided the philosophical foundation for the modern concept of religious liberty, arguing that the magistrate's authority extends only to civil interests, not to the care of souls. [[Voltaire]] defended religious liberty in practice through his campaigns against miscarriages of justice like the [[Calas affair]].

> [005-545] …logical consequence of complete religious liberty. The achievement of religious liberty in Germany…

[[Mill]]'s *On Liberty* (1859) extended the argument beyond religion to cover all forms of thought and expression, but the case for religious liberty remained its paradigm instance.

## The French and German Experiences

Bury devotes particular attention to the contrasting trajectories of religious liberty in France and Germany. In France, the [[Revolution]] "established religious liberty" (005-359) in principle, but "there was a falling back" (005-273) under later regimes. The concordat of 1801 and subsequent legislation reflected an incomplete and contested settlement.

> [005-359] The Revolution established religious liberty in France. Most of the revolutionaries…

In the German states, "the history of religious liberty differs in many respects" (005-485). The principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* established by the Peace of [[Augsburg]] (1555) and confirmed at [[Westphalia]] (1648) granted rulers — not individuals — the right to choose their territory's religion. Full religious liberty in Germany was achieved only gradually, with "the accession of [[Frederick the Great]]" (005-537) to the Prussian throne marking an important advance.

> [005-552] …emphasized, that full religious liberty was for the first time, in any part of Germany, realized.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Religious liberty was both the first and the most hard-won form of intellectual freedom. Bury's narrative shows how the "conviction that religious liberty should be procured" (005-607) gradually expanded into a broader commitment to liberty of thought in all domains. Once the principle was established that the state may not dictate belief in matters of religion, the extension of that principle to science, philosophy, and politics became a matter of logical consistency.
