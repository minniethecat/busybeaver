# Anne Robert Jacques Turgot

**Anne Robert Jacques Turgot** (1727–1781) was a French economist, statesman, and philosopher. His most enduring contribution to intellectual history was founding the doctrine of historical [[progress]], which was started in France in 1750 by Turgot, who made progress the organic principle of history (007-0960 to 007-0961).

## Life and Background

Turgot was born in Paris to a distinguished Norman family with a tradition of public service. Destined initially for the Church, he studied at the Sorbonne, where he delivered his first discourses on progress in 1750.

He later abandoned his ecclesiastical career, entered the royal administration, and served as intendant of Limoges (1761–1774), where he implemented economic reforms based on physiocratic principles.

In 1774, Louis XVI appointed him Controller-General of Finances. Turgot's reform program — abolishing guilds, deregulating the grain trade, and proposing a single land tax — encountered fierce resistance from vested interests, and he was dismissed in 1776 after only twenty months in office.

## Major Contributions

Turgot's intellectual achievements span economics and the philosophy of history. As an economist, he contributed to the physiocratic school, which held that land was the sole source of wealth and advocated free trade. His *Reflections on the Formation and Distribution of Wealth* (1766) anticipated aspects of classical economics.

His most original idea, however, was the concept of historical progress as a universal law. In his 1750 discourses at the Sorbonne — *A Philosophical Review of the Successive Advances of the Human Mind* and *On Universal History* — Turgot argued that humanity passes through definite stages of development, from hunting to pastoral to agricultural to commercial society.

Each stage represented an advance in knowledge and civilization.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Turgot's theory of progress provided a secular alternative to the Christian narrative of fall and redemption. By making progress the organic principle of history, he transformed history from a record of divine providence into a story of human advancement through the exercise of [[reason]].

This conceptual shift was foundational to the Enlightenment's confidence that liberty of thought and inquiry would inevitably advance human welfare.

The doctrine of progress was subsequently developed by [[Condorcet]] in his *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind* (1793) and put forward by [[Joseph Priestley]] in England (007-0961 to 007-0962). The idea was seized upon by the French socialistic philosophers [[Saint-Simon]] and [[fourier]] (007-0963).

Turgot's progress doctrine was also powerfully reinforced by the utilitarian ideal — the greatest happiness of the greatest number — preached by [[Jeremy Bentham]], [[James Mill]], [[John Stuart Mill]], and [[Grote]] (007-0956 to 007-0959).

## Legacy and Influence

Turgot's idea of progress became one of the most influential concepts in Western thought. It underpinned Condorcet's optimistic vision of human perfectibility, Auguste Comte's positivism, and the liberal and socialist movements of the nineteenth century. The concept that history has a direction and that human effort can accelerate it remains central to modern political thought.

Though Turgot's political career as a reforming minister was brief and ended in failure, his intellectual legacy proved far more durable. Bury regarded him as the originator of an idea that would define the modern worldview.
