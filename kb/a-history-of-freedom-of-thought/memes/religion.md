## Definition and Origins

In J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, religion is examined through its institutional and doctrinal relationship with freethought. Bury traces how religious orthodoxy has historically acted as a restraint on intellectual liberty, while acknowledging that religious thinkers contributed to the development of freedom of conscience.

The ancient Greeks first raised questions about the relationship between morality and religion (002-101). Greek thinkers began to examine whether conventional religious beliefs could withstand rational scrutiny, establishing a tradition of critical inquiry that would later develop into the freethought movement.

## Historical Development

In the Roman period, religion was widely regarded as a necessary social institution. Bury notes that many Roman thinkers "did not care much whether a religion was true" (002-278), viewing it as an instrument of social order. The official religion of the State was maintained, but educated Romans considered it a matter of convention rather than conviction (002-336).

The view that "a false religion is indispensable as a social" foundation persisted from antiquity through the medieval period (002-340). This utilitarian approach to religion—defending it for social benefits regardless of truth—was challenged by Enlightenment freethinkers who sought to "free men's minds from" the idea that fear should be the "fundamental motive of religion" (002-295).

During the Enlightenment, critics argued that morality could exist independently of religious belief. The "champion of irreligion" (002-322) represented those who sought not merely to reform religion but to replace it with reason and science.

## Key Proponents

- **Spinoza** developed a rationalist critique of revealed religion, arguing for philosophical understanding of God beyond orthodox theology.
- **Pierre Bayle** advanced the argument that a society of atheists could be moral, contradicting the consensus that religion was necessary for social order.
- **Voltaire** campaigned against religious intolerance and superstition, though he remained a deist rather than an atheist.
- **Diderot** promoted secular knowledge as an alternative to religious authority.
- **John Locke** articulated the principle of religious toleration, arguing the State should not coerce belief.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Religion's impact on freedom of thought has been ambivalent. Bury documents how the official religion of the State suppressed heresy and unorthodox opinion (001-273). The Churches wielded institutional power to punish deviation from established doctrine.

However, freedom of conscience emerged partly from within religious thought. The Reformation established the precedent that individual conscience could challenge institutional authority. Bury traces how the claim of "the conscience of the individual" over external authority developed from a religious principle into a foundation for intellectual liberty (002-250). The "religious enthusiast" who "denounces religion" (002-308) was viewed as a threat to both spiritual and temporal authority.
