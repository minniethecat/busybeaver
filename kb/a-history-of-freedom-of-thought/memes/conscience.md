# Conscience

**Conscience** — the individual's inner sense of moral and religious right — is presented by J.B. Bury as the historical leverage point through which freedom of thought first gained a foothold against the claims of state and church authority. The assertion of "individual conscience, as we should say, over human authority" (002-226) was the wedge that eventually opened the door to full intellectual liberty, even though its earliest champions did not intend that outcome.

## Definition and Origins

Conscience denotes the internal faculty by which a person judges the rightness or wrongness of actions and beliefs. In the religious context of Bury's narrative, it refers specifically to the individual's direct moral conviction in matters of faith — a conviction that could conflict with the demands of ecclesiastical or civil authority.

The concept has roots in both classical philosophy and Christian theology. The Stoics spoke of an inner *daimon* guiding moral judgment; the Christian tradition, especially after Paul, treated conscience as the seat of moral knowledge implanted by God. It was this theological grounding that made conscience a politically potent claim: if God speaks through conscience, no human authority may override it.

## Historical Development

The career of conscience as a political principle began with the [[Reformation]]. When [[Luther]] at the Diet of Worms declared that to act against conscience was "neither safe nor right," he was not asserting a general right to religious freedom. Rather, he was claiming his own conscience against the Pope. Yet the principle, once stated, could not be contained:

> [002-250] …of the conscience of the individual — a claim on which the Protestants took their stand.

The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries saw conscience invoked by persecuted minorities — [[Anabaptists]], [[Socinians]], [[Quakers]] — who extended Luther's principle beyond its original scope. The "martyrs died for conscience, but not for liberty" (002-537). That is, they claimed freedom for their own conscience but did not necessarily grant it to others.

By the eighteenth century, "liberty of conscience" had become a standard demand of Enlightenment thought. [[Locke]]'s *Letter Concerning Toleration* argued that the magistrate has no authority over the care of souls because "the care of each man's salvation belongs to himself alone."

## Key Proponents

[[Luther]] and [[Calvin]] first asserted the rights of individual conscience against Rome, though they later suppressed conscience when it contradicted their own doctrines. The radical Reformation — [[Sebastian Castellio]], [[Fausto Socinus]], and the [[Anabaptists]] — extended the principle more consistently.

> [005-153] according to conscience, above all other liberties.

[[Locke]] provided the definitive philosophical case for freedom of conscience in the seventeenth century. [[Voltaire]] defended specific victims of conscience-based persecution, notably in the [[Calas affair]]. [[Mill]] in *On Liberty* gave the argument its most comprehensive secular form.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Bury treats conscience as the historical bridge from religious toleration to full intellectual liberty. The "antagonism between the conscience of the individual and the authority of the State" (002-524) drove the development of rights that eventually extended beyond religion to encompass all spheres of thought.

> [002-529] the State to respect the conscience of the individual at all costs.

The principle that "freedom of conscience is asserted as superior to all" other claims (002-496) established a precedent that secular thinkers could later invoke for freedom of speech, press, and scientific inquiry.

## Liberty of Conscience and Toleration

Bury traces how "liberty of conscience" evolved from a limited claim into a comprehensive principle (005-055). The logic of conscience pushed beyond the *cuius regio, eius religio* settlement. By the nineteenth century, "unrestricted liberty of conscience" (005-556) had become a recognized political demand, though its full realization remained uneven across Europe.
