## Life and Background

Frederick II (1712–1786), known as Frederick the Great, was King of Prussia from 1740 until his death. A member of the Hohenzollern dynasty, he was both a military commander of genius and a patron of the arts and philosophy. His court at Sanssouci attracted leading intellectuals of the European Enlightenment, most notably Voltaire.

## Major Contributions

Frederick's greatest contribution to the history of freedom of thought was his establishment of full religious liberty in Prussia. Bury identifies his accession as "the most important fact of all in hastening the realization of religious liberty in Germany" — the arrival of a rationalist on the throne of Prussia (005-537).

A few months after his accession in 1740, Frederick wrote in the margin of a state paper that "every one should be allowed to get to heaven in his own way" (005-539). His view that morality was independent of religion led to the logical consequence of complete religious liberty: "a man could be a good citizen — the only thing which the State was entitled to demand — whatever faith he might profess" (005-543).

The scope of Frederick's toleration was unprecedented. Catholics were placed on equality with Protestants, the Treaty of Westphalia was violated by extending full toleration to all forbidden sects, and "Frederick even conceived the idea of introducing Mohammedan settlers into some parts of his realm" (005-548).

Bury emphasizes the historical significance:

> It is an important fact in history, which has hardly been duly emphasized, that full religious liberty was for the first time, in any country in modern Europe, realized under a free-thinking ruler, the friend of the great "blasphemer" Voltaire.
> (005-551)

Frederick's policy was codified in the Prussian Territorial Code of 1794, which guaranteed unrestricted liberty of conscience and placed the three chief religions — Lutheran, Reformed, and Catholic — on equal footing with identical privileges (005-555).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Bury describes Frederick as "the one really tolerant ruler of the age" (006-568). This toleration had concrete consequences for persecuted freethinkers across Europe. When Rousseau's books were burned in Paris and Geneva and he was forced to flee from canton to canton, Frederick gave him protection in the Prussian principality of Neufchâtel (006-568).

Under Frederick's tolerant rule, "Prussia was an asylum for those writers who suffered for their opinions in neighbouring States" (006-892). When the freethinker Edelmann's works were burned in various cities, "he was forced to seek Frederick's protection at Berlin" (006-903).

However, Bury notes a significant limitation: Frederick "held the view which was held by so many English rationalists of the time, and is still held widely enough, that freethought is not desirable for the multitude, because they are incapable of understanding philosophy" (006-894). Toleration was, in Frederick's view, for the educated elite rather than the common people.

The contrast with other European states of the period is stark. Under Frederick's father, the philosopher Christian Wolff "was banished from Prussia for according to the moral teachings of the Chinese sage Confucius a praise which, it was thought, ought to be reserved for Christianity" (006-889). Wolff returned after Frederick's accession, illustrating the transformation that Frederick's rule brought about.

## Legacy and Influence

Frederick the Great demonstrated that religious toleration could be implemented from above by a determined ruler — even one who was personally a freethinker. His example influenced subsequent reforms in other German states and contributed to the broader European movement toward religious liberty.

The Prussian Territorial Code of 1794, which codified Frederick's principles, remained a foundation of Prussian religious policy. The contrast Bury draws between Prussia under Frederick and England under George III, France under Louis XV, or Italy "under the shadow of the Popes," underscores his historical significance (005-550).
