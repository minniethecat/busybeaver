## Life and Background

Charles Robert Darwin was born in Shrewsbury, England, on 12 February 1809 into a wealthy and intellectually distinguished family. His father, Robert Darwin, was a physician; his grandfather, Erasmus Darwin, was a noted physician and natural philosopher who had already proposed early ideas of biological transformation.

Darwin studied medicine at the University of Edinburgh before transferring to Christ's College, Cambridge, where he prepared for a career in the Anglican clergy. At Cambridge, his interest in natural history deepened under the mentorship of botanist John Stevens Henslow. In 1831, on Henslow's recommendation, Darwin accepted the position of naturalist aboard HMS *Beagle*, embarking on a five-year voyage that would provide the observational foundation for his later theory.

The *Beagle* expedition took Darwin to South America, the Galápagos Islands, Australia, and numerous oceanic islands. His meticulous observations of geological formations, fossil distributions, and the variation of species across geographical barriers planted the seeds of his evolutionary thinking. After returning to England in 1836, Darwin settled at Down House in Kent, where he spent the remaining decades of his life in intensive study, correspondence, and writing, rarely leaving his rural retreat due to chronic illness. He died on 19 April 1882 and was buried in Westminster Abbey — an honour that signaled the extent to which the intellectual establishment had come to accept, if not embrace, his revolutionary ideas (007-0925).

## Major Contributions

Darwin's most significant contribution was the theory of evolution by natural selection, first presented publicly in 1858 alongside Alfred Russel Wallace and then elaborated in *On the Origin of Species* (1859). The publication of the *Origin* is a landmark not only in science but in the war between science and theology (007-0071). The theory proposed that species are not fixed creations but descend with modification from common ancestors, driven by the mechanism of natural selection acting on random variation.

Darwin followed the *Origin* with *The Descent of Man* (1871), which explicitly extended evolutionary theory to human origins. The Bible said that God created man in his own image; Darwin said that man descended from an ape (007-0080). The feelings of the orthodox world were expressed by William Ewart Gladstone, who complained that "upon the grounds of what is called evolution God is relieved of the labour of creation, and in the name of unchangeable laws is discharged from governing the world" (007-0082).

Though Darwin did not, as is now recognized, supply a complete explanation of the origin of species, his researches shattered the supernatural theory and confirmed the view that development is continuous in the living as in the non-living world (007-0086). Another nail was driven into the coffin of Creation and the Fall of Adam, and the doctrine of redemption could only be rescued by making it independent of the Jewish fable on which it was founded (007-0091).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Darwin's work advanced freedom of thought by providing a comprehensive scientific alternative to biblical accounts of creation and human origins. Darwinism has had the larger effect of discrediting the theory of the adaptation of means to ends in nature by an external and infinitely powerful intelligence (007-0094). The inadequacy of the argument from design, as a proof of God's existence, had been shown by the logic of [[Hume]] and [[Kant]]; but Darwin's observation of the life-processes of nature demonstrated that the very analogy between nature and art, on which the design argument rested, was empirically false (007-0097).

Darwin showed how biological phenomena might be explained as events not brought about intentionally, but due to exceptional concurrences of circumstances (007-0118). This eliminated the strongest remaining argument for a divine creator and freed intellectual inquiry from the constraint of having to reconcile all natural phenomena with a theological framework.

The influence of Darwinism extended beyond biology into the broader intellectual culture. The *Descent of Man* appeared precisely in 1871, coinciding with the Broad Church movement and the emergence of a new, undogmatic Christianity (007-0689). [[Leslie Stephen]] remarked in 1873 that there was scarcely an article in the creeds which might not be contradicted with impunity — a development he attributed in part to the intellectual climate created by Darwin's work (007-0692).

## Legacy and Influence

Darwin's legacy in the history of freedom of thought is profound. By the early twentieth century, Bury could observe that "we are far from the days when Darwin's *Descent* created an uproar" and that Darwin had been buried in Westminster Abbey (007-0925). The acceptance of evolutionary theory by the establishment marked a decisive shift in the relationship between science and theology.

Darwin's ideas influenced a wide range of thinkers. [[Herbert Spencer]] developed a comprehensive philosophy of evolution applied to society and human thought (007-0212). Ernst Haeckel, the German zoologist, popularized Darwinian evolution on the Continent; his *Creation of Man* (1868) covered the same ground as Darwin's *Descent* and enjoyed an enormous circulation (007-0209). Darwinism also contributed to the historical study of religion: inquiries into the condition of early man showed, independently of Darwinism, that there is nothing to be said for the view that humanity fell from a higher to a lower state (007-0249).

The broader effect was to establish naturalistic explanation as the default mode of intellectual inquiry. After Darwin, it became increasingly difficult to invoke supernatural causes in any domain of knowledge. This methodological naturalism was perhaps Darwin's most enduring contribution to the cause of freedom of thought.
