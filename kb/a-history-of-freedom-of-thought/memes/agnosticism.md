## Definition and Origins

Agnosticism, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the position that ultimate questions—particularly the existence of God—cannot be known with certainty through human reason. Bury cites a leading writer of the nineteenth century who "held agnosticism" regarding theological questions (007-0736).

The term was coined by Thomas Henry Huxley in 1869, combining the Greek *a-* (without) and *gnosis* (knowledge). Huxley intended it to describe a scientific approach to religious questions: one should neither affirm nor deny what cannot be proved.

Bury identifies agnosticism as an intellectual posture closely related to scientific method: "And what is this but agnosticism? You may call your doubt a" philosophical position, but it is essentially the application of scientific standards of evidence to theological claims (007-0745). He further observes that agnosticism is "merely the theological phrase for agnosticism" when theologians attempt to describe the same epistemological modesty (007-0746).

## Historical Development

Agnosticism emerged in the context of nineteenth-century debates between science and religion. The success of scientific explanation in astronomy, geology, and biology made it increasingly difficult to maintain traditional religious beliefs on the basis of authority alone. However, many scientifically-minded thinkers were reluctant to embrace outright atheism.

The agnostic position offered a middle way: one could acknowledge the limits of human knowledge while remaining open to evidence. This resonated with the empirical temper of Victorian science, which emphasized what could be demonstrated rather than what could be asserted.

Darwinism played a significant role in the development of agnostic thought. By providing a naturalistic explanation for the origins of species, Darwin's work demonstrated that questions previously considered theological could be addressed through scientific methods.

## Key Proponents

- **Thomas Henry Huxley** coined the term "agnosticism" and vigorously promoted it as the proper scientific attitude toward religious questions. Huxley argued that it was intellectually dishonest to claim knowledge one did not possess.
- **Charles Darwin** described himself as an agnostic in later life, though he preferred the term to describe his epistemological position rather than as a positive creed.
- **Herbert Spencer** advanced a comprehensive philosophical system that reserved a place for the "Unknowable" while confining knowledge to the domain of science.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Agnosticism advanced freedom of thought by legitimizing doubt and uncertainty as intellectually respectable positions. Rather than being forced to choose between orthodox belief and atheism, thinkers could now occupy a principled middle ground that affirmed the value of evidence and reason.

The agnostic position challenged both religious dogmatism and dogmatic atheism. By insisting that claims about ultimate reality must meet the same standards of evidence as claims about the natural world, agnosticism contributed to the broader intellectual movement that Bury traces toward reason, toleration, and liberty of conscience.

Agnosticism also had practical implications for freedom of thought: if fundamental questions about God and the afterlife cannot be known with certainty, then no person or institution has the right to compel belief or punish unbelief.
