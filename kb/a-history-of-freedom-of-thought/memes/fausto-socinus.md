## Life and Background

Fausto Paolo Sozzini (Latinised as Faustus Socinus) was born in Siena, Italy, in 1539. He came from a family of jurists and theological dissenters; his uncle Lelio Sozzini had already raised doubts about the doctrine of the Trinity. After living in Lyon and Basel, Socinus settled in Poland, where he became the theological leader of the Polish Brethren, the Unitarian wing of the Reformed Church in Transylvania and Poland (005-028, 005-029).

## Major Contributions

The Unitarian creed was moulded by Fausto Sozzini, generally known as Socinus, and in the catechism of his sect published in 1574, persecution is condemned (005-030). The Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism by force would have been inconsistent with its own principles (005-033, 005-034). There was a strong rationalistic element in Socinian thought which was lacking in the Trinitarian creeds (005-036).

Socinus thought that the principle of religious toleration could be realized without abolishing the State Church (005-056). He contemplated a close union between the State and the prevailing Church, combined with complete toleration for other sects — a system later called the jurisdictional approach (005-057, 005-059).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

The repudiation of the use of force in the interest of religion is a direct consequence of Socinian doctrines (005-032). Under the influence of the Socinian spirit, [[castellio|Castellion]] of Savoy sounded the trumpet of toleration in a pamphlet denouncing the burning of [[michael-servetus]], earning the malignant hatred of [[john-calvin]] (005-038, 005-040).

For a long time the Socinians and those who came under their influence were the only sects which advocated toleration (005-048). The principle was adopted from them by the Anabaptists and by the Arminian section of the Reformed Church of Holland, and through the English Congregationalists it played an important part in the history of the Civil War and the Commonwealth (005-050, 005-053, 005-054). Religious liberty in Rhode Island had been ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus (005-110).

## Legacy and Influence

Bury notes that Ruffini, in his authoritative work on religious liberty, decides in favour of the jurisdictional system contemplated by Socinus (005-639, 005-641). While Socinus was a true friend of liberty of thought, his ideas laid the intellectual foundation for the toleration that would later be enshrined in European and American constitutional arrangements.
