# Augustine of Hippo

**Augustine of Hippo** (354–430) was a Church Father and Bishop of Hippo Regius in Roman North Africa. No father of the Church has been more esteemed or enjoyed higher authority than St. Augustine (003-073).

His theological writings, particularly on original sin, grace, and the relationship between church and state, established the intellectual framework of Western Christianity for over a millennium.

## Life and Background

Aurelius Augustinus was born in Thagaste (modern Algeria) to a pagan father and a Christian mother, Monica. He studied rhetoric at Carthage and taught in Rome and Milan.

After an early adherence to Manichaeism and a period of philosophical skepticism, he converted to Christianity under the influence of Ambrose of Milan. He was ordained a priest in 391 and became Bishop of Hippo in 395, serving until his death during the Vandal siege of the city in 430.

His major works include *Confessions* (c. 400), an autobiographical account of his spiritual journey, and *The City of God* (c. 426), a monumental treatise on the relationship between the earthly city and the heavenly city written in response to the sack of Rome in 410.

## Major Contributions

Augustine's theology shaped doctrines of original sin, predestination, and the nature of the Church. His concept of the "two cities" — the earthly city of human desires and the heavenly city of divine grace — provided a framework for understanding the relationship between secular and spiritual authority that would dominate medieval political thought.

On the question of suffering and natural causation, Augustine's framework was pre-scientific.

He said that the diseases of Christians were caused by demons (003-252). This supernatural explanation of illness represented a regression from the rational medical traditions of antiquity — in the Middle Ages, men relapsed to the primitive notions of a barbarous age where bodily ailments were ascribed to occult agencies, the malice of the Devil or the wrath of God (003-249 to 003-252).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Augustine's most consequential contribution to the history of freedom of thought was his formulation of the theological justification for religious persecution.

He formulated the principle of persecution for the guidance of future generations, basing it on the firm foundation of Scripture — on words used by Jesus Christ in one of his parables, "Compel them to come in" (003-073 to 003-076, quoting Luke 14:23).

This Augustinian principle echoed through the centuries.

In the late seventeenth century, when the French clergy justified the persecution of Protestants culminating in the revoking of the Edict of Nantes (1685), they appealed to St. Augustine, invoking precisely this text: "Compel them to come in" (005-287). Their arguments evoked a defence of toleration by [[Pierre Bayle]], a French Protestant who had taken refuge in Holland (005-288).

Augustine thus stands at the origin of a theological tradition that provided the intellectual justification for over a thousand years of coercion in matters of conscience — a tradition that would only be dismantled by the rise of [[rationalism]] and the arguments for [[toleration]] in the Enlightenment.

## Legacy and Influence

Augustine's influence on Western Christianity is rivaled only by that of the Apostle Paul. His theological system dominated medieval Catholicism and was a major influence on [[Martin Luther]] and [[John Calvin]], both of whom were Augustinian friars or drew heavily on his doctrines of grace and predestination.

In the history of freethought, Augustine represents the authoritative voice of orthodoxy against which later advocates of [[toleration]] and liberty of conscience — from [[Pierre Bayle]] to [[John Locke]] to [[Voltaire]] — were compelled to argue. The Augustinian "compel them to come in" was the default position that the Enlightenment had to overcome.
