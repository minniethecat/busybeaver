# Leslie Stephen

**Leslie Stephen** (1832–1904) was a British author, critic, and agnostic. He was the writer who "turned the dry light of reason on to theological opinions with the most merciless logic" in the late Victorian period (007-0739). His essays, particularly "An Agnostic's Apology" (1876), advanced the view that theological explanations of the universe, when logically pressed, collapse into confessions of ignorance — an agnosticism that Stephen argued was the only intellectually honest position.

## Life and Background

Born in London, Leslie Stephen was educated at Eton and Trinity Hall, Cambridge, where he was ordained as an Anglican clergyman. However, his intellectual development led him away from religious orthodoxy, and he eventually renounced his holy orders. This trajectory — from clergyman to agnostic — was emblematic of the crisis of faith experienced by many Victorian intellectuals confronting scientific advances and German biblical criticism.

After leaving the clergy, Stephen became a prominent literary journalist and editor. He served as the first editor of the *Dictionary of National Biography*, to which he contributed numerous biographies. He was also the father of [[Virginia Woolf]] and Vanessa Bell, central figures in the Bloomsbury Group.

His friend Sir J. F. Stephen wittily described his position as that of a disciple "who had heard the Sermon on the Mount, whose attention had not been called to the Miracles, and who died before the Resurrection" (007-0525).

## Major Contributions

Stephen's most significant contributions to the intellectual life of his time were his essays on agnosticism:

His best-known essay, "An Agnostic's Apology," published in the *Fortnightly Review* in 1876, raised the question: have the dogmas of orthodox theologians any meaning? Do they offer an intelligible reconciliation of the discords in the universe? Stephen showed in detail that the various theological explanations of the dealings of God with man, when logically pressed, issue in a confession of ignorance (007-0739).

He also wrote a major historical work: *History of English Thought in the Eighteenth Century* (1876), which traced the development of rationalist and deist ideas in England (008-402). This work remains an important source for understanding the intellectual history of English freethought.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Stephen's relation to [[freedom of thought]] was expressed through his relentless application of reason to theological claims. His core argument was that orthodoxy does not solve the mystery of existence but merely adds new difficulties:

> "Why, when no honest man will deny in private that every ultimate problem is wrapped in the profoundest mystery, do honest men proclaim in pulpits that unhesitating certainty is the duty of the most foolish and ignorant? We are a company of ignorant beings, dimly discerning light enough for our daily needs, but hopelessly differing whenever we attempt to describe the ultimate origin or end of our paths."
> (007-0747–007-0750)

The characteristic of Stephen's essays was that they were less directed to showing that orthodox theology was untrue, than to showing that there was no reality about it — that its solutions were sham solutions. If theology solved any part of the mystery, he argued, it would be welcome, but it only added new difficulties. It was "a mere edifice of moonshine" (007-0756–007-0761).

The Broad Church movement, the attempts to liberalize Christianity, to make it unsectarian and undogmatic, to find compromises between theology and science, "found no favour in Leslie Stephen's eyes, and he criticized all this with a certain contempt" (007-0770).

On the controversy about the efficacy of prayer, Stephen was characteristically incisive:

> "One phenomenon is just as much the result of fixed causes as the other; but it is easier for the imagination to suppose the interference of a divine agent to be hidden away somewhere amidst the infinitely complex play of forces, which elude our calculations in meteorological phenomena, than to believe in it where the forces are simple enough to admit of prediction."
> (007-0776–007-0782)

## Legacy and Influence

Stephen's agnosticism represented one of the purest forms of rationalist thought in Victorian England. His arguments influenced a generation of freethinkers and helped shift the intellectual climate away from theological orthodoxy toward a more skeptical and scientific worldview.

In later years, Mr. Leslie Stephen remarked in 1873 that "it may be said, with a certain plausibility, that the educated classes in England are becoming thoroughly indifferent to religion" (007-0691) — an observation that captured the gradual secularization of British intellectual life.

As recorded in the index of Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, Stephen is cited at pages 167 and 215 and following, alongside his brother J. F. Stephen (999-190, 999-191).
