# Matthew Tindal

**Matthew Tindal** (1657–1733) was a Fellow of All Souls College, Oxford, and one of the most influential English Deist writers of the early eighteenth century. His major work, *Christianity as old as the Creation* (1730), undertook to show that the Bible as a revelation is superfluous, for it adds nothing to natural religion which God revealed to man from the very first by the sole light of [[reason]] (006-319).

## Life and Background

Tindal was born in Devon and educated at Oxford, where he was elected a Fellow of All Souls in 1678. He briefly converted to Roman Catholicism during the reign of James II but returned to the Church of England, an experience that may have shaped his critical perspective on confessional claims to exclusive truth.

Tindal spent much of his later life in London, where he was part of the circle of freethinkers and Deists that included [[Anthony Collins|Collins]], [[John Toland|Toland]], and other critics of orthodox Christianity. His fellowship at All Souls provided him with financial independence and intellectual freedom to pursue his controversial writings.

## Major Contributions

*Christianity as old as the Creation* (1730) was Tindal's magnum opus and quickly became known as the "Deist's Bible." In it he argues that those who defend Revealed religion by its agreement with Natural religion, and thus set up a double government of reason and authority, fall between the two (006-319):

> It's an odd jumble to prove the truth of a book by the truth of the doctrines it contains, and at the same time conclude those doctrines to be true because contained in that book.
> (006-319)

This critique of circular reasoning in apologetics was devastating. Tindal contended that if Christianity is truly rational, then reason alone is sufficient to arrive at its moral and theological truths; if it is not rational, no amount of revelation can make it so. The Bible, on this view, becomes at best a republication of truths already accessible through natural reason.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Tindal's work advanced freedom of thought by relocating religious authority from scripture and church to the individual's rational faculty. By arguing that natural religion — accessible to all people at all times through reason — was complete and sufficient, Tindal undermined the claim of any ecclesiastical institution to possess unique access to divine truth (006-319).

His argument had profound implications for toleration: if reason is the universal foundation of religion, then no person or institution can claim the right to coerce belief. Tindal thus contributed to the philosophical case for religious liberty that was being advanced by Locke and other Enlightenment thinkers.

## Legacy and Influence

Tindal's *Christianity as old as the Creation* provoked an enormous volume of replies — over a hundred published responses — making it one of the most contested works of the Deist controversy. Its arguments shaped subsequent Deist and Unitarian thought on both sides of the Atlantic.

By the time of his death in 1733, Tindal had established himself as perhaps the most philosophically rigorous of the English Deists, and his central thesis — that revelation cannot add anything essential to a religion grounded in reason — continued to resonate through the [[the Enlightenment|Enlightenment]] and beyond.
