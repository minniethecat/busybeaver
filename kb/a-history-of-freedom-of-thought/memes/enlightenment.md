## Definition and Origins

The Enlightenment, also called the Age of Reason, was the dominant intellectual movement of eighteenth-century Europe, dedicated to the proposition that human reason should be the ultimate arbiter of truth in all domains — science, politics, religion, and morality. Bury treats the Enlightenment as the culmination of the long historical struggle between reason and authority that forms the central theme of his work.

The roots of the Enlightenment lay in the Scientific Revolution of the seventeenth century and the rationalist philosophy of Descartes, Spinoza, and Locke. By the mid-eighteenth century, these intellectual currents had coalesced into a self-conscious movement committed to liberating the human mind from tradition, superstition, and ecclesiastical control.

## Historical Development

The movement took different forms in different national contexts. In France, it was associated with the *philosophes* — Voltaire, Diderot, Montesquieu, and Rousseau — who subjected religion and political institutions to withering rational criticism. In Germany, "the age of enlightenment began in the middle of the eighteenth century" (006-886), influenced by Christian Wolff and culminating in the critical philosophy of Immanuel Kant. In Britain and Scotland, the Enlightenment took a more empirical turn with Hume, Adam Smith, and the Scottish school.

Bury notes that despite the movement's achievements, its reach was far from universal. In many parts of Europe, the established churches and traditional institutions remained as hostile to enlightenment as they had been in previous centuries. "The Protestant creeds represent enlightenment," Bury observes, yet on "the Continent was as hostile to enlightenment as it was to liberty" (004-165) (004-166).

The German Enlightenment especially contributed to historical and biblical criticism. Bury records that "the German enlightenment of this century" applied critical methods to philosophy and religion, preparing the ground for nineteenth-century rationalism (006-912). This scholarly tradition would prove essential to the development of the Higher Criticism of the Bible.

## Key Proponents

- **Voltaire** (1694–1778) — The most famous of the French *philosophes*, Voltaire tirelessly campaigned against religious intolerance, superstition, and injustice. His wit and polemical skill made him the public face of the movement.
- **Denis Diderot** (1713–1784) — Editor of the *Encyclopédie*, which sought to compile all human knowledge and disseminate it as a tool for critical thinking.
- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) — His essay "What Is Enlightenment?" defined the movement as humanity's emergence from self-imposed immaturity, captured in the motto *Sapere aude* ("Dare to know"). [[kant]]'s philosophy represented, in Bury's view, the high-water mark of the German contribution to the Enlightenment.
- [[frederick-the-great]] embodied "enlightened despotism" — the attempt to implement Enlightenment principles through royal authority, including religious toleration and legal reform.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The Enlightenment's impact on freedom of thought was transformative and enduring. By establishing reason as the highest authority, it delegitimized the traditional claims of church and state to control belief. The movement produced the first systematic arguments for freedom of speech, freedom of the press, religious toleration, and the separation of church and state — principles that would eventually be enshrined in modern constitutions.

Bury identifies the Enlightenment as a decisive turning point. The movement demonstrated that "enlightenment" could serve as the "test of enlightenment, and the man in the street is" capable of reasoned judgment (008-290). This democratic confidence in the universality of reason was itself a radical challenge to hierarchical authority.

The nineteenth-century advance of rationalism, secularism, and scientific naturalism was, in Bury's narrative, a direct continuation of Enlightenment principles. The Vatican Council's reaction "against enlightenment" (007-0661) confirmed its continuing power as a threat to ecclesiastical authority. Wherever the Enlightenment penetrated, it carried with it the conviction that enlightenment was not the privilege of an elite but the birthright of every thinking person (007-0877).
