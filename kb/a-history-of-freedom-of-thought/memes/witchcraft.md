## Definition and Origins

Witchcraft, in Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes the belief in the existence of persons — typically women — who had made a pact with the Devil and acquired supernatural powers to harm others. This belief, Bury shows, was not a marginal folk superstition but a central component of the medieval Christian worldview, actively enforced by both ecclesiastical and secular authorities. The persecution of alleged witches represents one of the most dramatic episodes in the history of thought control, and its eventual decline marks a crucial victory for rationalism over inherited fear.

Bury traces the origins of witchcraft belief to classical antiquity, noting that "the firm belief in witchcraft, magic, and demons was inherited by the Middle Ages from antiquity, but it became far more lurid" in the Christian era (003-286). The addition of the Devil as an active malevolent force, combined with the Church's teaching that he recruited human agents, transformed ancient superstitions about magic into a comprehensive demonological system that justified systematic persecution.

## Historical Development

The prosecution of witchcraft was not a constant feature of the Middle Ages. Bury observes that until "the fourteenth century there was no systematic attempt to root out witchcraft" (003-295). The turning point came with the Black Death, which "seems to have aggravated the haunting terror of the invisible world of demons" (003-295). In the wake of this catastrophe, "trials for witchcraft multiplied, and for three hundred years the discovery of witchcraft and the destruction of those who were accused of practising it" became a major function of both ecclesiastical and secular courts (003-297, 003-298).

The witch-hunts reached their peak in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the very period of the Renaissance and Reformation that we now associate with the revival of learning. Bury's account provides the sobering reminder that intellectual progress was neither uniform nor rapid: the same era that produced humanism and scientific discovery also produced the most intense persecution of alleged witches in European history.

Bury documents "the change which was silently wrought in public opinion on the subject of witchcraft" during the later seventeenth century (006-041). He notes that "the famous efforts of James I to carry out the Biblical command, 'Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live,' were outdone by the" enthusiastic persecution of his successors (006-041). Yet by the Restoration period, "the belief in witchcraft declined among educated people — though some able writers maintained it — and there were few executions" (006-045).

The decisive rejection of witchcraft persecution came in the eighteenth century. Bury records that the "laws against witchcraft were repealed in 1735" (006-050). This legal change reflected a deeper intellectual transformation: the triumph of the rationalist view that witchcraft was not a real crime but a superstition, and that persecution based on superstition was itself unjust.

Bury adds a telling epilogue: "John Wesley said with perfect truth that to disbelieve in witchcraft is to disbelieve in the Bible" (006-051). Wesley's observation was logically correct — the Old Testament explicitly commands the death penalty for witches — but it served only to highlight the growing gap between biblical literalism and enlightened opinion. By Wesley's time, even many devout Christians had quietly abandoned belief in witchcraft, demonstrating that religious belief could adapt to rational criticism even when the biblical text did not.

## Key Figures

- **James I of England** was a zealous believer in witchcraft who wrote a treatise on demonology and personally participated in witch interrogations. His reign saw vigorous enforcement of witchcraft laws.
- **John Wesley**, the founder of Methodism, was among the last prominent intellectuals to defend belief in witchcraft, arguing that to abandon it was to abandon biblical authority. Bury quotes him to illustrate the uncomfortable implications of rationalism for biblical literalists (006-051).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The witchcraft episode is particularly instructive for the history of freedom of thought because it demonstrates how a belief that was once universally held and violently enforced came to be regarded as both false and barbaric. Bury uses witchcraft as a case study in how rational criticism, gradually applied over generations, can dismantle even the most deeply entrenched systems of belief.

The mechanism of change was not a single dramatic discovery but a slow accumulation of skeptical arguments and a gradual shift in educated opinion. Once the belief in witchcraft lost the support of the intellectual elite, the legal apparatus of persecution could not sustain itself. This pattern — elite opinion shifting first, followed by legal reform, followed eventually by popular opinion — recurs throughout Bury's narrative of intellectual progress.

The repeal of the witchcraft laws in 1735 was a landmark in the legal recognition that the state should not punish beliefs that it regarded as false. This principle would later be extended from witchcraft to heresy and eventually to all matters of religious opinion. The witchcraft case thus prefigures the broader movement toward freedom of thought that Bury traces through the Enlightenment and beyond.
