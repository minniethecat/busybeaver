# Natural Religion

**Natural religion** — the belief that reason and the observation of nature, without the aid of supernatural revelation, are sufficient to establish the existence and attributes of God — occupies a crucial position in J.B. Bury's narrative of the advance of freedom of thought. It served as a halfway house between orthodox Christianity and full rationalism, allowing Enlightenment thinkers to retain religious belief while subjecting revealed religion to critical scrutiny.

## Definition and Origins

Natural religion holds that the existence of a supreme being can be inferred from the order and design evident in the natural world. Unlike revealed religion, which rests on scripture, miracles, and ecclesiastical authority, natural religion appeals solely to "the light of nature and reason." Bury describes it as the "'natural religion' of the philosophers and rationalists" (005-427).

The idea has roots in classical philosophy, particularly [[Aristotle]]'s argument for an unmoved mover and the [[Stoics]]' concept of a rational order pervading the cosmos. Medieval scholasticism, especially [[Thomas Aquinas]]'s Five Ways, developed sophisticated arguments for the existence of God based on reason alone — though always within the framework of Christian orthodoxy.

## Historical Development

Natural religion emerged as a distinct and potentially subversive position in the seventeenth century. [[Lord Herbert of Cherbury]]'s *De Veritate* (1624) articulated five "common notions" of religion knowable by reason, laying the groundwork for English [[deism]]. [[Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) applied rational criticism to the Bible while maintaining a philosophical concept of God.

> [006-192] …the question whether the Deity of natural religion — the God whose existence was proved by reason — could be identified with the God of the Bible.

The eighteenth century saw natural religion reach its fullest development. Deists like [[John Toland]], [[Matthew Tindal]], and [[Anthony Collins]] argued that Christianity, stripped of its "superfluous" supernatural elements, was nothing more than a republication of natural religion:

> [006-322] …superfluous, for it adds nothing to natural religion.

The relationship between natural religion and revealed religion became a central controversy. Orthodox defenders insisted that natural religion was insufficient for salvation; radical critics countered that revelation was not merely unnecessary but actively pernicious (006-400, 006-401).

## Key Proponents

[[Locke]]'s *Reasonableness of Christianity* (1695) attempted to demonstrate the compatibility of Christian teaching with reason, a project that inadvertently encouraged more radical deistic conclusions. [[Voltaire]], though often associated with deism, was more a critic of revealed religion than a systematic defender of natural theology.

> [006-401] …have seen, to what was called natural religion — the belief in a supreme being…

[[Hume]]'s *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (published posthumously in 1779) subjected the argument from design to devastating logical scrutiny, undermining the philosophical foundations of natural religion even as the movement reached its popular zenith. [[Rousseau]]'s "Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar" in *Émile* offered an emotionally compelling vision of natural religion grounded in sentiment rather than rational demonstration.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Natural religion advanced freedom of thought by establishing that religious belief could be justified without appeal to external authority. If reason alone could establish the essentials of religion, then the claims of churches, priests, and scriptures lost their monopoly on religious truth. This was a crucial step toward the full intellectual autonomy that Bury celebrates.

> [006-741] …consistent and compatible with natural religion. The belief in a God of reason…

By the nineteenth century, natural religion had largely given way to more thoroughgoing forms of rationalism and, among some thinkers, agnosticism. Yet its historical role was decisive: it demonstrated that religious questions could be addressed through reason alone, opening a path that later thinkers would follow beyond theism entirely.
