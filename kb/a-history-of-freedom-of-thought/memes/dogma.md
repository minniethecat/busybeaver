## Definition and Origins

Dogma, in the context of J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to religious doctrines that are proclaimed by ecclesiastical authority as absolute and unquestionable truths. The term derives from the Greek *dogma*, meaning "that which seems good" or "opinion," but in Christian usage it came to denote binding articles of faith that must be accepted without rational scrutiny. Bury presents dogmatic religion as the primary intellectual obstacle that freedom of thought had to overcome.

The establishment of Christian dogma began with the early church councils, which defined core doctrines such as the Trinity and the nature of Christ. These dogmas were not presented as subjects for discussion but as revealed truths requiring submission. Bury notes the harmful effects of dogmatic thinking on intellectual life, observing that "this dogma also injured the sense of truth" (003-225). When inquiry is subordinated to predetermined conclusions, the very concept of truth-seeking is compromised.

## Historical Development

Throughout the Middle Ages, Christian dogma provided the framework within which all intellectual activity had to operate. Philosophy was characterized as the handmaiden of theology, and scientific claims that contradicted dogma were suppressed. Bury records that the Reformation, rather than abolishing dogma, simply transferred dogmatic authority from the Pope to Scripture as interpreted by each Protestant church.

The Enlightenment marked the first sustained intellectual assault on dogmatic religion. Bury observes that the reaction against the Church was not merely "against dogmas, but of widely spread anti-clerical feeling due to the" corruption and worldliness of the clergy (004-104). However, the philosophical critique of dogma went deeper than anti-clericalism: thinkers began to argue that holding propositions as absolutely certain on the basis of authority alone was intellectually illegitimate.

The rationalist critique of dogma was sharpest in the work of John Toland and other deists. Toland argued in *Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696) that "unintelligible dogmas, cannot be accepted by reason. And if a" doctrine cannot be understood, it cannot be the basis of genuine religious belief (006-121). This rationalist standard—that religious claims must be comprehensible to be credible—struck at the foundation of dogmatic theology.

Bury documents how freethinkers "exposed without mercy the" contradictions and absurdities of "Christian dogmas" (006-150). The critical examination of specific dogmas, such as "the dogma of the Trinity" (006-201), was particularly effective because it demonstrated that supposedly revealed truths were in fact human constructions, developed over centuries through political negotiation and theological controversy.

## Key Proponents of Anti-Dogmatic Thought

- **John Toland** argued that Christianity should contain no mysteries beyond the reach of reason. His *Christianity Not Mysterious* insisted that all valid religious doctrines must be intelligible and rationally defensible (006-121).
- **Pierre Bayle** demonstrated through his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* that dogmatic certainty was incompatible with intellectual integrity. His method of exposing contradictions in traditional doctrines was widely influential.
- **Voltaire** waged a lifelong campaign against dogmatic religion, using wit and ridicule to expose the absurdities of enforced belief. He turned attention "from the dogmas of religion to the improvement" of society (006-599).
- **John Locke** provided the epistemological framework for anti-dogmatic thought, arguing in his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* that human knowledge is limited and that certainty is rarely attainable.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The struggle against dogmatic religion was central to the development of freedom of thought. Dogma represented the principle that truth is received from authority rather than discovered through inquiry. By challenging this principle, freethinkers established the alternative: that beliefs should be held provisionally, subject to revision in light of evidence and reason.

Bury highlights the irony that the opponents of religious dogma sometimes created new dogmas of their own, held "as irrationally as the dogmas of any supernatural creed" (005-461). This tendency toward dogmatic certainty, he suggests, is a persistent human temptation that freedom of thought must continually guard against. The true legacy of the Enlightenment's anti-dogmatic campaign was not the replacement of religious dogma with secular dogma, but the establishment of fallibilism—the recognition that all human knowledge is provisional and open to revision.

The gradual liberation from dogmatic thinking transformed not only religion but also science, philosophy, and politics. By insisting that claims must be supported by evidence and argument rather than by appeals to authority, the critics of dogma laid the intellectual foundations of modern liberal society.
