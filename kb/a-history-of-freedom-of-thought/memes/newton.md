## Life and Background

Isaac Newton was born in Woolsthorpe, England, in 1643. He studied at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he later became Lucasian Professor of Mathematics. A towering figure of the [[scientific-revolution]], Newton made foundational contributions to physics, mathematics, and astronomy. He served as President of the Royal Society and was knighted in 1705.

## Major Contributions

Newton's *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation. His discovery of gravitation was, as [[herbert-spencer]] observed, the beginning of a process that discharged God from governing the world in the name of unchangeable laws (007-0085). Newton's work demonstrated that celestial and terrestrial phenomena could be explained by the same mathematical principles, removing the need for continuous divine intervention in the cosmos.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

> It was a discharge which, as Spencer observed, had begun with Newton's discovery of gravitation.
> (007-0085)

Newton's mechanistic universe became a powerful model for Enlightenment thinkers. If the physical world operated according to discoverable natural laws, the same might be true of human society and morality. The idea that 37 million men of science equal to Newton might one day exist reflected the Enlightenment's faith in human progress through scientific understanding (007-0967). Though Newton himself was religious, his scientific method laid the groundwork for the rationalist challenge to revealed religion.

## Legacy and Influence

Newton's influence extended far beyond physics. His work inspired the [[the-enlightenment]] confidence in reason and natural law. Thinkers from [[voltaire]] to [[david-hume]] drew on Newtonian science to challenge traditional authority. The concept of a universe governed by discoverable laws, rather than divine will, was a foundational shift that enabled the development of modern [[freethought]] and rationalism.
