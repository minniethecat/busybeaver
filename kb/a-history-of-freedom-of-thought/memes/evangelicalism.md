# Evangelicalism

Evangelicalism was a pietistic revival movement that transformed the Church of England in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. While it reinvigorated Anglican religious life with the fervour of Methodism, its relationship to freedom of thought was, in J. B. Bury's account, unambiguously hostile. Evangelicalism represented one of the principal forces of religious reaction that checked the progress of rationalism in Britain during the decades following the French Revolution.

## Definition and Origins

Bury describes Evangelicalism as "the pietistic movement known as Evangelicalism, which Wilberforce's *Practical View of Christianity* (1797) did much to make popular" (007-0487). Originating within the Church of England but deeply influenced by the Methodist revival led by John Wesley, Evangelicalism emphasized personal conversion, the supreme authority of the Bible, the corruption of human nature, and the centrality of Christ's atonement.

The movement "introduced the spirit of Methodism within the Anglican Church, and soon put an end to the delightful type of eighteenth-century divine, who, as Gibbon says, 'subscribed with a sigh or a smile' the articles of faith" (007-0490). This transformation was not merely a change in devotional style but a fundamental shift in intellectual climate. Where the eighteenth-century clergy had often been latitudinarian — broad, tolerant, and sometimes sceptical — the Evangelicals demanded doctrinal rigor and moral seriousness.

## Historical Development

Under Evangelical influence, "the rigorous taboo of the Sabbath was revived, the theatre was denounced, the corruption of human nature became the dominant theme, and the Bible more a fetish than ever" (007-0492). Bury notes that "the success of this religious 'reaction,' as it is called, was aided, though not caused, by the common belief that the French Revolution had been mainly due to infidelity; the Revolution was taken for an object lesson showing the value of religion for keeping the people in order" (007-0495). In this political context, Evangelical piety and social conservatism reinforced each other: religious enthusiasm became a bulwark against revolutionary ideas.

> The rigorous taboo of the Sabbath was revived, the theatre was denounced, the corruption of human nature became the dominant theme, and the Bible more a fetish than ever.
> (007-0493, 007-0494)

The Evangelical dominance of the early nineteenth-century Church of England coincided with the rise of the High Church (Tractarian) movement led by John Henry Newman. Though theologically distinct — Evangelicals emphasized personal conversion while Tractarians emphasized apostolic succession and sacramental theology — the two movements were united in their hostility to freethought. "The High Church movement which marked the second quarter of the century was as hostile as Evangelicalism to the freedom of religious thought" (007-0512). Between Evangelical biblicism and Tractarian traditionalism, there was little room within the established Church for liberal or rationalist theology.

The mid-century, however, brought a decisive shift. The influence of German Biblical criticism, the philosophies of Hegel and Comte, and the publication of freethinking works such as F. W. Newman's *Phases of Faith* (1850) began to erode the Evangelical-Orthodox consensus. A new party — the Broad Church — emerged among "a few English clergymen (chiefly Oxford men) who were interested in German criticism and leaned to broad views, which to the Evangelicals and High Churchmen seemed indistinguishable from infidelity" (007-0529). In 1855, Benjamin Jowett published an edition of St. Paul's Epistles containing "an annihilating criticism of the doctrine of the Atonement, an explicit rejection of original sin" (007-0535) — ideas that would have been unthinkable under the Evangelical ascendancy a generation earlier.

## Key Proponents

- **William Wilberforce** (1759–1833): Author of *A Practical View of Christianity* (1797), which Bury identifies as a key text in popularizing Evangelicalism (007-0487). Better known for his leadership of the abolition movement, Wilberforce was also a leading figure in the Evangelical revival.
- **John Wesley** (1703–1791): Founder of Methodism, whose emphasis on personal conversion and lay preaching infused the Evangelical movement within the Church of England, though Wesley himself remained an Anglican clergyman until his death.
- **Charles Simeon** (1759–1836): Influential Evangelical preacher at Holy Trinity Church, Cambridge, who mentored generations of Anglican clergy in Evangelical theology.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Evangelicalism's impact on freedom of thought was almost entirely negative. By reviving "the corruption of human nature" as the "dominant theme" (007-0494) and treating the Bible as "more a fetish than ever" (007-0494), the movement closed off the intellectual space that eighteenth-century rationalism had opened. The latitudinarian clergyman who could "subscribe with a sigh or a smile" to the articles of faith — tolerant, urbane, and sceptical — was replaced by the earnest Evangelical who demanded literal belief and moral rigor.

The political dimension was crucial. The Evangelical reaction was "aided, though not caused" by the association of freethought with revolutionary upheaval (007-0496). In the anxious decades after 1789, religious orthodoxy was seen — and was deliberately promoted — as a safeguard of social order. Evangelicalism thus contributed to an environment in which questioning religious orthodoxy was not merely impious but politically suspect.

Yet Bury is careful to note that the religious reaction did not mean "that free thought was less prevalent, but that the beliefs of the majority were more aggressive and had powerful spokesmen, while the eighteenth-century form of rationalism fell out of fashion" (007-0500). Freethought continued, but it adapted — taking new forms in the liberal theology of Coleridge and the Broad Church movement. Evangelicalism, in this sense, was an obstacle that rationalism had to navigate around rather than confront directly. The ultimate triumph of the Broad Church and of liberal theology in the later nineteenth century suggests that Evangelicalism's hegemony, however intense, proved temporary.
