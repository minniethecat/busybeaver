## Life and Background

Marie Jean Antoine Nicolas de Caritat, Marquis de Condorcet, was born in Ribemont, France, in 1743. A gifted mathematician from an early age, he became a member of the French Academy of Sciences and was closely associated with the leading figures of the [[the-enlightenment]]. During the [[french-revolution]], he supported republican ideals but fell out of favour with the Jacobins and died in prison in 1794.

## Major Contributions

Condorcet's most influential work, *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind* (1793), articulated the doctrine of human perfectibility—the idea that mankind is capable of indefinite improvement (007-0962). This concept became a cornerstone of nineteenth-century optimism about human progress. Condorcet argued that the advances of science and the application of reason to social organization would lead to the continuous betterment of the human condition.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Condorcet's philosophy placed reason and scientific inquiry at the centre of human development. By envisioning progress as an inherent feature of human history, he provided a secular alternative to religious narratives of providence and salvation. His work embodies the Enlightenment conviction that freedom of thought is essential to the advancement of knowledge and the improvement of society.

## Legacy and Influence

> Condorcet (1793), and put forward by Priestley in England, that by the progress of the human race, a goal of ultimate perfection was being reached.
> (007-0962)

Condorcet's idea of progress influenced [[auguste-comte]] and other nineteenth-century thinkers who developed secular philosophies of history. The concept of indefinite human perfectibility became a guiding spiritual force of the nineteenth century and remains influential in modern humanist thought.
