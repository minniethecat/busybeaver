## Definition and Origins

Natural rights are rights that individuals are said to possess inherently, by virtue of their humanity or by divine endowment, rather than by grant from any government or legal system.

The concept emerged from natural law theory and became a central pillar of Enlightenment political philosophy, providing a theoretical foundation for challenging established authority and demanding liberties including freedom of thought and expression.

In modern civilized countries, freedom of speech is taken as a matter of course and seems a perfectly simple thing. People are so accustomed to it that they look on it as a natural right — but this right has been acquired only in quite recent times, and its attainment has lain through lakes of blood (001-105).

The apparent naturalness of free speech as a right obscures its hard-won historical character.

## Historical Development

The concept of natural rights gained its most influential formulation during the Enlightenment, particularly in the political philosophy of John Locke and the revolutionary declarations of the late eighteenth century. The American Declaration of Independence and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man both grounded their claims in the language of natural, inalienable rights.

However, Bury subjected the concept to rigorous philosophical scrutiny. If a man has any natural rights, the right to preserve his life and the right to reproduce his kind are certainly such — yet human societies impose upon their members restrictions in the exercise of both these rights (001-120).

A starving man is prohibited from taking food which belongs to somebody else, and promiscuous reproduction is restricted by various laws or customs, demonstrating that society is justified in restricting even the most fundamental claims of natural right.

> If a man has any "natural rights," the right to preserve his life and the right to reproduce his kind are certainly such. Yet human societies impose upon their members restrictions in the exercise of both these rights. A starving man is prohibited from taking food which belongs to somebody else. Promiscuous reproduction is restricted by various laws or customs. It is admitted that society is justified in restricting these rights.
> (001-120)

This critique exposes a fundamental weakness in natural rights theory: if even the most basic natural rights can be legitimately restricted by society, the concept loses its force as an absolute foundation for liberty.

The truth, Bury argues, is that no valid argument can be founded on the conception of natural rights, because it involves an untenable theory of the relations between society and its members (001-120).

## Key Proponents

**John Locke** (1632–1704) articulated a theory of natural rights to life, liberty, and property that profoundly influenced the American and French revolutions. His *Two Treatises of Government* argued that governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed, who possess inherent rights that no ruler may violate.

**Thomas Paine** (1737–1809), in *The Rights of Man*, popularized natural rights doctrine and applied it directly to the defense of freedom of thought and expression, arguing that intellectual liberty was among the most fundamental of human rights.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Despite its philosophical weaknesses, the doctrine of natural rights played an indispensable historical role in advancing freedom of thought. By asserting that individuals possess rights prior to and independent of the state, natural rights theory provided a powerful rhetorical and philosophical weapon against censorship, religious persecution, and authoritarian government.

The language of natural rights pervaded the great declarations of the revolutionary era and became embedded in the political culture of liberal democracies. Even critics who found the philosophical foundations of natural rights untenable acknowledged its practical importance in establishing the expectation that governments should respect individual liberty.

Bury's critique represents a more sophisticated position: freedom of thought is better defended on utilitarian and social-progress grounds than on the metaphysical assertion of natural rights. The argument that society benefits from the free circulation of ideas is stronger than the claim that individuals possess a mysterious inherent right to express them.

## Criticism and Opposition

The natural rights doctrine faced criticism from multiple directions. Edmund Burke argued that rights were historical and conventional, not abstract and universal. Jeremy Bentham famously dismissed natural rights as "nonsense upon stilts," insisting that only legally enacted rights had meaning.

Bury's own critique, grounded in the observable fact that all supposedly natural rights are routinely restricted by society, added a pragmatic dimension to the philosophical objections.
