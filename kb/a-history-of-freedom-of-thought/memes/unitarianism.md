## Definition and Origins

Unitarianism, known in its early form as Socinianism, is a Christian theological movement that denies the doctrine of the Trinity, affirming the unity of God. The movement emerged from the radical wing of the Protestant Reformation in the sixteenth century, drawing on rationalistic and humanist traditions that questioned traditional Trinitarian formulations (005-030).

The Unitarian creed was moulded by Fausto Sozzini, generally known as Socinus, an Italian theologian who systematized anti-Trinitarian thought and gave the movement its doctrinal foundation (005-030). The catechism of his sect, published in 1574, articulated a theology that broke sharply with both Catholic and mainstream Protestant orthodoxy (005-030).

## Historical Development

The Socinians, as they were originally called, exerted a far-reaching influence on European intellectual history that extended well beyond their numerical strength (004-222). Unlike Luther and Calvin, the Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism would have been inconsistent with its own principles.

There was a strong rationalistic element in Socinian thought which was lacking in the Trinitarian creeds (005-033).

For a long time the Socinians and those who came under their influence, when driven from Poland, passed into Germany and Holland, where they were the only sects which advocated toleration (005-048).

Their principles were adopted by the Anabaptists and by the Arminian section of the Reformed Church of Holland, and in Holland the founder of the English Congregationalists was influenced by their ideas of liberty of conscience (005-048).

> The Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism would have been inconsistent with its principles. In other words, there was a strong rationalistic element which was lacking in the Trinitarian creeds.
> (005-033)

## Key Proponents

**Fausto Sozzini (Socinus)** (1539–1604) was the principal theologian of the movement. Socinus thought that religious liberty could be realized without abolishing the State Church, contemplating a close union between the State and the prevailing Church, combined with complete toleration for other sects.

This system, which has been called jurisdictional, became the model under which religious liberty has been realized in European States (005-056).

**Sebastian Castellion** (1515–1563), working under the influence of the Socinian spirit, sounded the trumpet of toleration in a pamphlet denouncing the burning of Servetus, earning the malignant hatred of Calvin (005-038).

He maintained the innocence of error and ridiculed the importance which the Churches laid on obscure questions such as predestination and the Trinity, arguing that a religion is a curse if persecution is a necessary part of it (005-038).

Socinus is recognized as a true friend of liberty of thought. The historian points out that while Socinus contemplated the jurisdictional system, the Anabaptists, whose spirit was intolerant, sought Separation — a notable contrast in the history of religious liberty (005-641).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The Socinian contribution to freedom of thought operated on multiple levels. By insisting on the primacy of individual reason in interpreting Scripture, the movement undermined the authoritarian basis of doctrinal enforcement and opened a pathway toward broader intellectual liberty.

The principle of toleration advanced by Socinus was ultimately derived from his theological method: if every believer must exercise private judgment to arrive at religious truth, then coercing belief becomes not only ineffective but theologically incoherent (005-110).

This principle, carried by Socinian refugees and their sympathizers, spread through Holland, Germany, and eventually to England and the American colonies.

In Rhode Island, the principle of religious liberty was ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus. When the American colonies became independent, the Federal Constitution they set up was absolutely secular, though it was left to each member of the Union to adopt Separation or not (005-110).

The influence of this tradition persisted: Bury notes in the early twentieth century that the expression "the Trinity" jars with the whole idea and character of Bible-religion (007-0833).

> He maintained the innocence of error and ridiculed the importance which the Churches laid on obscure questions such as predestination and the Trinity. He asked, rather, whether a religion is a curse if persecution is a necessary part of it.
> (005-038)

## Criticism and Opposition

The Socinians faced severe persecution from both Catholic and Protestant authorities. Their rejection of the Trinity was regarded as a capital offense in most European jurisdictions. Driven from Poland, their main center, they became a dispersed community whose influence spread precisely because of their displacement.

Mainstream Protestant reformers, particularly Calvin, regarded the Socinian elevation of individual judgment as a dangerous precedent that would lead to the dissolution of doctrinal authority. The burning of Servetus at Geneva exemplified the hostility that anti-Trinitarian thought provoked from established religious powers.
