## Definition and Origins

The right of private judgment, in Bury's analysis, is the principle that every individual possesses the authority to interpret Scripture and form religious beliefs according to their own conscience and reason, rather than submitting to the authority of an institutional Church. This concept emerged during the Protestant Reformation as a theological claim — that the Bible is accessible to every believer — and gradually expanded into a broader assertion of intellectual autonomy.

Private judgment represents one of the most consequential ideas in the history of freedom of thought. What began as a dispute over ecclesiastical authority became the foundation for claims of universal intellectual liberty. The Reformation's insistence that the individual believer could read and interpret Scripture without priestly mediation contained within it the seeds of a far more radical principle: that each person has the right to think for themselves on all matters, religious and secular alike.

## Historical Development

The principle of private judgment first emerged as a Reformation doctrine. Martin Luther's stand at the Diet of Worms — "unless I am convinced by Scripture and plain reason, I cannot and will not recant" — asserted that individual conscience, informed by Scripture, took precedence over ecclesiastical authority. The Church had claimed a monopoly on biblical interpretation; the Reformers insisted that "the right of private judgment" belonged to every Christian (004-188).

This principle proved revolutionary in ways its originators did not anticipate. Once individuals were empowered to interpret Scripture for themselves, they inevitably reached different conclusions. Protestantism splintered into countless sects, each claiming biblical warrant for its distinctive doctrines. The logic of private judgment, once unleashed, could not be contained: if each person could interpret the Bible, why not other texts? Why not all claims to truth?

Bury traces how the concept of private judgment expanded beyond its theological origins. Reformers originally intended it as a weapon "against the Church" — a way to challenge papal authority without abandoning Christianity itself (004-188). But the principle could not be confined to this limited application. By the 18th century, "eighteenth-century divines" found themselves "who admitted the right of private judgment" extending to matters they had not intended (006-842).

The ultimate implication of private judgment was religious liberty itself. The Reformation did not initially embrace toleration; Luther and Calvin were as intolerant of theological error as the Church they opposed. Yet the logic of private judgment — that belief must be freely chosen, not coerced — gradually led toward "religious liberty and the right of private judgment" being recognized as complementary principles (004-111). What the Reformation accomplished was to open a door it could not close: once individuals claimed the right to judge doctrine for themselves, the intellectual foundation of religious coercion began to crumble.

## Key Proponents

- **Martin Luther** was the original champion of private judgment, though his version was limited to biblical interpretation under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. His refusal to recant at Worms — prioritizing conscience and Scripture over institutional authority — established the paradigm for all subsequent assertions of individual intellectual autonomy.

- **John Locke** extended the principle of private judgment into a systematic argument for toleration. His *Letter Concerning Toleration* argued that genuine belief cannot be coerced because "the nature of the understanding" is such that it cannot be compelled to assent by external force. Each person must judge truth for themselves, and no civil magistrate can do it for them.

- **Pierre Bayle** pushed the logic of private judgment further than any of his contemporaries. He argued that the rights of conscience applied even to atheists, and that a society composed entirely of atheists could be as moral and stable as a Christian one. His radical extension of private judgment to its logical conclusion made him one of the most important precursors of the Enlightenment.

- **Voltaire** championed private judgment as a practical principle rather than a theological one. His campaigns on behalf of victims of religious persecution — Calas, Sirven, La Barre — dramatized the human cost of denying individuals the right to think and believe according to their own reason.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The principle of private judgment is, in Bury's narrative, the bridge between the Reformation and the Enlightenment — between religious reform and secular liberty. The Reformation's assertion that the individual believer could interpret Scripture independently of the Church established a precedent that proved impossible to contain. Once the right of private judgment was conceded in matters of biblical interpretation, it was only a matter of time before it was extended to all questions of truth and value.

This expansion of private judgment had revolutionary consequences. If each individual has the right to judge for themselves in matters of religion, then religious coercion is illegitimate by definition — for coerced belief is not genuine belief. If religious coercion is illegitimate, then toleration is not merely a pragmatic accommodation but a moral requirement. And if toleration is morally required in religion, why not in politics, philosophy, and science?

Bury documents how this logic unfolded over centuries, from Luther's limited assertion of biblical private judgment to the full-blown liberalism of the 19th century. The principle that began as a theological weapon against the papacy ended as the intellectual foundation of modern liberty. What was originally asserted "against the Church" became "the right of private judgment" in its fullest sense — the right of every individual to think, believe, and speak according to the dictates of their own reason (004-188).
