## Chapter II
### REASON FREE
(GREECE AND ROME)

[002-001]WHEN we are asked to specify the debt which civilization owes to the

[002-002]Greeks, their

[002-003][22] achievements in literature and art naturally occur to us first of

[002-004]all. But a truer answer may be that our deepest gratitude is due to them

[002-005]as the originators of liberty of thought and discussion. For this

[002-006]freedom of spirit was not only the condition of their speculations in

[002-007]philosophy, their progress in science, their experiments in political

[002-008]institutions; it was also a condition of their literary and artistic

[002-009]excellence. Their literature, for instance, could not have been what it

[002-010]is if they had been debarred from free criticism of life. But apart from

[002-011]what they actually accomplished, even if they had not achieved the

[002-012]wonderful things they did in most of the realms of human activity, their

[002-013]assertion of the principle of liberty would place them in the highest

[002-014]rank among the benefactors of the race; for it was one of the greatest

[002-015]steps in human progress.

[002-016]We do not know enough about the earliest history of the Greeks to

[002-017]explain how it was that they attained their free outlook upon the world

[002-018]and came to possess the will and courage to set no bounds to the range

[002-019]of their criticism and curiosity. We have to take this character as a

[002-020]fact. But it must be remembered that the Greeks consisted of a large

[002-021]number of separate peoples, who varied largely in temper, customs and

[002-022]traditions,

[002-023][23] though they had important features common to all. Some were

[002-024]conservative, or backward, or unintellectual compared with others. In

[002-025]this chapter “the Greeks” does not mean all the Greeks, but only those

[002-026]who count most in the history of civilization, especially the Ionians

[002-027]and Athenians.

[002-028]Ionia in Asia Minor was the cradle of free speculation. The history of

[002-029]European science and European philosophy begins in Ionia. Here (in the

[002-030]sixth and fifth centuries B.C.) the early philosophers by using their

[002-031]reason sought to penetrate into the origin and structure of the world.

[002-032]They could not of course free their minds entirely from received

[002-033]notions, but they began the work of destroying orthodox views and

[002-034]religious faiths. Xenophanes may specially be named among these pioneers

[002-035]of thought (though he was not the most important or the ablest), because

[002-036]the toleration of his teaching illustrates the freedom of the atmosphere

[002-037]in which these men lived. He went about from city to city, calling in

[002-038]question on moral grounds the popular beliefs about the gods and

[002-039]goddesses, and ridiculing the anthropomorphic conceptions which the

[002-040]Greeks had formed of their divinities. “If oxen had hands and the

[002-041]capacities of men, they would make gods in the shape of oxen.” This

[002-042]attack on received

[002-043][24] theology was an attack on the veracity of the old poets, especially

[002-044]Homer, who was considered the highest authority on mythology. Xenophanes

[002-045]criticized him severely for ascribing to the gods acts which, committed

[002-046]by men, would be considered highly disgraceful. We do not hear that any

[002-047]attempt was made to restrain him from thus assailing traditional beliefs

[002-048]and branding Homer as immoral. We must remember that the Homeric poems

[002-049]were never supposed to be the word of God. It has been said that Homer

[002-050]was the Bible of the Greeks. The remark exactly misses the truth. The

[002-051]Greeks fortunately had no Bible, and this fact was both an expression

[002-052]and an important condition of their freedom. Homer’s poems were secular,

[002-053]not religious, and it may be noted that they are freer from immorality

[002-054]and savagery than sacred books that one could mention. Their authority

[002-055]was immense; but it was not binding like the authority of a sacred book,

[002-056]and so Homeric criticism was never hampered like Biblical criticism.

[002-057]In this connexion, notice may be taken of another expression and

[002-058]condition of freedom, the absence of sacerdotalism. The priests of the

[002-059]temples never became powerful castes, tyrannizing over the community in

[002-060]their own interests and able to silence voices raised against religious

[002-061]beliefs. The civil authorities

[002-062][25] kept the general control of public worship in their own hands, and,

[002-063]if some priestly families might have considerable influence, yet as a

[002-064]rule the priests were virtually State servants whose voice carried no

[002-065]weight except concerning the technical details of ritual.

[002-066]To return to the early philosophers, who were mostly materialists, the

[002-067]record of their speculations is an interesting chapter in the history of

[002-068]rationalism. Two great names may be selected, Heraclitus and Democritus,

[002-069]because they did more perhaps than any of the others, by sheer hard

[002-070]thinking, to train reason to look upon the universe in new ways and to

[002-071]shock the unreasoned conceptions of common sense. It was startling to be

[002-072]taught, for the first time, by Heraclitus, that the appearance of

[002-073]stability and permanence which material things present to our senses is

[002-074]a false appearance, and that the world and everything in it are changing

[002-075]every instant. Democritus performed the amazing feat of working out an

[002-076]atomic theory of the universe, which was revived in the seventeenth

[002-077]century and is connected, in the history of speculation, with the most

[002-078]modern physical and chemical theories of matter. No fantastic tales of

[002-079]creation, imposed by sacred authority, hampered these powerful brains.

[002-080]All this philosophical speculation prepared

[002-081][26] the way for the educationalists who were known as the Sophists.

[002-082]They begin to appear after the middle of the fifth century. They worked

[002-083]here and there throughout Greece, constantly travelling, training young

[002-084]men for public life, and teaching them to use their reason. As educators

[002-085]they had practical ends in view. They turned away from the problems of

[002-086]the physical universe to the problems of human life—morality and

[002-087]polities. Here they were confronted with the difficulty of

[002-088]distinguishing between truth and error, and the ablest of them

[002-089]investigated the nature of knowledge, the method of reason—logic— and

[002-090]the instrument of reason—speech. Whatever their particular theories

[002-091]might be, their general spirit was that of free inquiry and discussion.

[002-092]They sought to test everything by reason. The second half of the fifth

[002-093]century might be called the age of Illumination.

[002-094]It may be remarked that the knowledge of foreign countries which the

[002-095]Greeks had acquired had a considerable effect in promoting a sceptical

[002-096]attitude towards authority. When a man is acquainted only with the

[002-097]habits of his own country, they seem so much a matter of course that he

[002-098]ascribes them to nature, but when he travels abroad and finds totally

[002-099]different habits and standards of conduct prevailing, he begins to

[002-100]understand

[002-101][27] the power of custom; and learns that morality and religion are

[002-102]matters of latitude. This discovery tends to weaken authority, and to

[002-103]raise disquieting reflections, as in the case of one who, brought up as

[002-104]a Christian, comes to realize that, if he had been born on the Ganges or

[002-105]the Euphrates, he would have firmly believed in entirely different

[002-106]dogmas.

[002-107]Of course these movements of intellectual freedom were, as in all ages,

[002-108]confined to the minority. Everywhere the masses were exceedingly

[002-109]superstitious. They believed that the safety of their cities depended on

[002-110]the good-will of their gods. If this superstitious spirit were alarmed,

[002-111]there was always a danger that philosophical speculations might be

[002-112]persecuted. And this occurred in Athens. [[About]] the middle of the fifth

[002-113]century Athens had not only become the most powerful State in Greece,

[002-114]but was also taking the highest place in literature and art. She was a

[002-115]full-fledged democracy. Political discussion was perfectly free. At this

[002-116]time she was guided by the statesman Pericles, who was personally a

[002-117]freethinker, or at least was in touch with all the subversive

[002-118]speculations of the day. He was especially intimate with the philosopher

[002-119]Anaxagoras who had come from Ionia to teach at Athens. In regard to the

[002-120]popular gods Anaxagoras was a thorough-going

[002-121][28] unbeliever. The political enemies of Pericles struck at him by

[002-122]attacking his friend. They introduced and carried a blasphemy law, to

[002-123]the effect that unbelievers and those who taught theories about the

[002-124]celestial world might be impeached. It was easy to prove that Anaxagoras

[002-125]was a blasphemer who taught that the gods were abstractions and that the

[002-126]sun, to which the ordinary Athenian said prayers morning and evening,

[002-127]was a mass of flaming matter. The influence of Pericles saved him from

[002-128]death; he was heavily fined and left Athens for Lampsacus, where he was

[002-129]treated with consideration and honour.

[002-130]Other cases are recorded which show that anti-religious thought was

[002-131]liable to be persecuted. Protagoras, one of the greatest of the

[002-132]Sophists, published a book On the Gods, the object of which seems to

[002-133]have been to prove that one cannot know the gods by reason. The first

[002-134]words ran: “Concerning the gods, I cannot say that they exist nor yet

[002-135]that they do not exist. There are more reasons than one why we cannot

[002-136]know. There is the obscurity of the subject and there is the brevity of

[002-137]human life.” A charge of blasphemy was lodged against him and he fled

[002-138]from Athens. But there was no systematic policy of suppressing free

[002-139]thought. Copies of the work of Protagoras were collected and

[002-140][29] burned, but the book of Anaxagoras setting forth the views for

[002-141]which he had been condemned was for sale on the Athenian book-stalls at

[002-142]a popular price. Rationalistic ideas moreover were venturing to appear

[002-143]on the stage, though the dramatic performances, at the feasts of the god

[002-144]Dionysus, were religious solemnities. The poet Euripides was saturated

[002-145]with modern speculation, and, while different opinions may be held as to

[002-146]the tendencies of some of his tragedies, he often allows his characters

[002-147]to express highly unorthodox views. He was prosecuted for impiety by a

[002-148]popular politician. We may suspect that during the last thirty years of

[002-149]the fifth century unorthodoxy spread considerably among the educated

[002-150]classes. There was a large enough section of influential rationalists to

[002-151]render impossible any organized repression of liberty, and the chief

[002-152]evil of the blasphemy law was that it could be used for personal or

[002-153]party reasons. Some of the prosecutions, about which we know, were

[002-154]certainly due to such motives, others may have been prompted by genuine

[002-155]bigotry and by the fear lest sceptical thought should extend beyond the

[002-156]highly educated and leisured class. It was a generally accepted

[002-157]principle among the Greeks, and afterwards among the Romans, that

[002-158]religion was a good and necessary thing

[002-159][30] for the common people. Men who did not believe in its truth

[002-160]believed in its usefulness as a political institution, and as a rule

[002-161]philosophers did not seek to diffuse disturbing “truth” among the

[002-162]masses. It was the custom, much more than at the present day, for those

[002-163]who did not believe in the established cults to conform to them

[002-164]externally. Popular higher education was not an article in the programme

[002-165]of Greek statesmen or thinkers. And perhaps it may be argued that in the

[002-166]circumstances of the ancient world it would have been hardly

[002-167]practicable.

[002-168]There was, however, one illustrious Athenian, who thought

[002-169]differently—Socrates, the philosopher. Socrates was the greatest of the

[002-170]educationalists, but unlike the others he taught gratuitously, though he

[002-171]was a poor man. His teaching always took the form of discussion; the

[002-172]discussion often ended in no positive result, but had the effect of

[002-173]showing that some received opinion was untenable and that truth is

[002-174]difficult to ascertain. He had indeed certain definite views about

[002-175]knowledge and virtue, which are of the highest importance in the history

[002-176]of philosophy, but for our present purpose his significance lies in his

[002-177]enthusiasm for discussion and criticism. He taught those with whom he

[002-178]conversed—and he conversed indiscriminately

[002-179][31] with all who would listen to him—to bring all popular beliefs

[002-180]before the bar of reason, to approach every inquiry with an open mind,

[002-181]and not to judge by the opinion of majorities or the dictate of

[002-182]authority; in short to seek for other tests of the truth of an opinion

[002-183]than the fact that it is held by a great many people. Among his

[002-184]disciples were all the young men who were to become the leading

[002-185]philosophers of the next generation and some who played prominent parts

[002-186]in Athenian history.

[002-187]If the Athenians had had a daily press, Socrates would have been

[002-188]denounced by the journalists as a dangerous person. They had a comic

[002-189]drama, which constantly held up to ridicule philosophers and sophists

[002-190]and their vain doctrines. We possess one play (the Clouds of

[002-191]Aristophanes) in which Socrates is pilloried as a typical representative

[002-192]of impious and destructive speculations. Apart from annoyances of this

[002-193]kind, Socrates reached old age, pursuing the task of instructing his

[002-194]fellow-citizens, without any evil befalling him. Then, at the age of

[002-195]seventy, he was prosecuted as an atheist and corrupter of youth and was

[002-196]put to death (399 B.C.). It is strange that if the Athenians really

[002-197]thought him dangerous they should have suffered him so long. There can,

[002-198]I think, be

[002-199][32] little doubt that the motives of the accusation were political. [1]

[002-200]Socrates, looking at things as he did, could not be sympathetic with

[002-201]unlimited democracy, or approve of the principle that the will of the

[002-202]ignorant majority was a good guide. He was probably known to sympathize

[002-203]with those who wished to limit the franchise. When, after a struggle in

[002-204]which the constitution had been more than once overthrown, democracy

[002-205]emerged triumphant (403 B.C.), there was a bitter feeling against those

[002-206]who had not been its friends, and of these disloyal persons Socrates was

[002-207]chosen as a victim. If he had wished, he could easily have escaped. If

[002-208]he had given an undertaking to teach no more, he would almost certainly

[002-209]have been acquitted. As it was, of the 501 ordinary Athenians who were

[002-210]his judges, a very large minority voted for his acquittal. Even then, if

[002-211]he had adopted a different tone, he would not have been condemned to

[002-212]death.

[002-213]He rose to the great occasion and vindicated freedom of discussion in a

[002-214]wonderful unconventional speech. The Apology of Socrates, which was

[002-215]composed by his most brilliant pupil, Plato the philosopher, reproduces

[002-216][33] the general tenor of his defence. It is clear that he was not able

[002-217]to meet satisfactorily the charge that he did not acknowledge the gods

[002-218]worshipped by the city, and his explanations on this point are the weak

[002-219]part of his speech. But he met the accusation that he corrupted the

[002-220]minds of the young by a splendid plea for free discussion. This is the

[002-221]most valuable section of the Apology; it is as impressive to-day as

[002-222]ever. I think the two principal points which he makes are these—

[002-223](1) He maintains that the individual should at any cost refuse to be

[002-224]coerced by any human authority or tribunal into a course which his own

[002-225]mind condemns as wrong. That is, he asserts the supremacy of the

[002-226]individual conscience, as we should say, over human law. He represents

[002-227]his own life-work as a sort of religious quest; he feels convinced that

[002-228]in devoting himself to philosophical discussion he has done the bidding

[002-229]of a super-human guide; and he goes to death rather than be untrue to

[002-230]this personal conviction. “If you propose to acquit me,” he says, “on

[002-231]condition that I abandon my search for truth, I will say: I thank you, O

[002-232]Athenians, but I will obey God, who, as I believe, set me this task,

[002-233]rather than you, and so long as I have breath and strength I will never

[002-234][34] cease from my occupation with philosophy. I will continue the

[002-235]practice of accosting whomever I meet and saying to him, ‘Are you not

[002-236]ashamed of setting your heart on wealth and honours while you have no

[002-237]care for wisdom and truth and making your soul better?’ I know not what

[002-238]death is—it may be a good thing, and I am not afraid of it. But I do

[002-239]know that it is a bad thing to desert one’s post and I prefer what may

[002-240]be good to what I know to be bad.”

[002-241](2) He insists on the public value of free discussion. “In me you have a

[002-242]stimulating critic, persistently urging you with persuasion and

[002-243]reproaches, persistently testing your opinions and trying to show you

[002-244]that you are really ignorant of what you suppose you know. Daily

[002-245]discussion of the matters about which you hear me conversing is the

[002-246]highest good for man. Life that is not tested by such discussion is not

[002-247]worth living.”

[002-248]Thus in what we may call the earliest justification of liberty of

[002-249]thought we have two significant claims affirmed: the indefeasible right

[002-250]of the conscience of the individual —a claim on which later struggles

[002-251]for liberty were to turn; and the social importance of discussion and

[002-252]criticism. The former claim is not based on argument but on intuition;

[002-253]it rests in fact on the assumption

[002-254][35] of some sort of superhuman moral principle, and to those who, not

[002-255]having the same personal experience as Socrates, reject this assumption,

[002-256]his pleading does not carry weight. The second claim, after the

[002-257]experience of more than 2,000 years, can be formulated more

[002-258]comprehensively now with bearings of which he did not dream.

[002-259]The circumstances of the trial of Socrates illustrate both the tolerance

[002-260]and the intolerance which prevailed at Athens. His long immunity, the

[002-261]fact that he was at last indicted from political motives and perhaps

[002-262]personal also, the large minority in his favour, all show that thought

[002-263]was normally free, and that the mass of intolerance which existed was

[002-264]only fitfully invoked, and perhaps most often to serve other purposes. I

[002-265]may mention the case of the philosopher Aristotle, who some seventy

[002-266]years later left Athens because he was menaced by a prosecution for

[002-267]blasphemy, the charge being a pretext for attacking one who belonged to

[002-268]a certain political party. The persecution of opinion was never

[002-269]organized.

[002-270]It may seem curious that to find the persecuting spirit in Greece we

[002-271]have to turn to the philosophers. Plato, the most brilliant disciple of

[002-272]Socrates, constructed in his later years an ideal State. In this State

[002-273]he instituted

[002-274][36] a religion considerably different from the current religion, and

[002-275]proposed to compel all the citizens to believe in his gods on pain of

[002-276]death or imprisonment. All freedom of discussion was excluded under the

[002-277]cast-iron system which he conceived. But the point of interest in his

[002-278]attitude is that he did not care much whether a religion was true, but

[002-279]only whether it was morally useful; he was prepared to promote morality

[002-280]by edifying fables; and he condemned the popular mythology not because

[002-281]it was false, but because it did not make for righteousness.

[002-282]The outcome of the large freedom permitted at Athens was a series of

[002-283]philosophies which had a common source in the conversations of Socrates.

[002-284]Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, the Epicureans, the Sceptics—it may be

[002-285]maintained that the efforts of thought represented by these names have

[002-286]had a deeper influence on the progress of man than any other continuous

[002-287]intellectual movement, at least until the rise of modern science in a

[002-288]new epoch of liberty.

[002-289]The doctrines of the Epicureans, Stoics, and Sceptics all aimed at

[002-290]securing peace and guidance for the individual soul. They were widely

[002-291]propagated throughout the Greek world from the third century B.C., and

[002-292]we may say that from this time onward most

[002-293][37] well-educated Greeks were more or less rationalists. The teaching

[002-294]of Epicurus had a distinct anti-religious tendency. He considered fear

[002-295]to be the fundamental motive of religion, and to free men’s minds from

[002-296]this fear was a principal object of his teaching. He was a Materialist,

[002-297]explaining the world by the atomic theory of Democritus and denying any

[002-298]divine government of the universe. [2] He did indeed hold the existence

[002-299]of gods, but, so far as men are concerned, his gods are as if they were

[002-300]not—living in some remote abode and enjoying a “sacred and everlasting

[002-301]calm.” They just served as an example of the realization of the ideal

[002-302]Epicurean life.

[002-303]There was something in this philosophy which had the power to inspire a

[002-304]poet of singular genius to expound it in verse. The Roman Lucretius

[002-305](first century B.C.) regarded Epicurus as the great deliverer of the

[002-306]human race and determined to proclaim the glad tidings of his philosophy

[002-307]in a poem On the Nature of the World. [3] With all the fervour

[002-308][38] of a religious enthusiast he denounces religion, sounding every

[002-309]note of defiance, loathing, and contempt, and branding in burning words

[002-310]the crimes to which it had urged man on. He rides forth as a leader of

[002-311]the hosts of atheism against the walls of heaven. He explains the

[002-312]scientific arguments as if they were the radiant revelation of a new

[002-313]world; and the rapture of his enthusiasm is a strange accompaniment of a

[002-314]doctrine which aimed at perfect calm. Although the Greek thinkers had

[002-315]done all the work and the Latin poem is a hymn of triumph over prostrate

[002-316]deities, yet in the literature of free thought it must always hold an

[002-317]eminent place by the sincerity of its audacious, defiant spirit. In the

[002-318]history of rationalism its interest would be greater if it had exploded

[002-319]in the midst of an orthodox community. But the educated Romans in the

[002-320]days of Lucretius were sceptical in religious matters, some of them were

[002-321]Epicureans, and we may suspect that not many of those who read it were

[002-322]shocked or influenced by the audacities of the champion of irreligion.

[002-323]The Stoic philosophy made notable contributions to the cause of liberty

[002-324]and could hardly have flourished in an atmosphere where discussion was

[002-325]not free. It asserted the rights of individuals against public

[002-326][39] authority. Socrates had seen that laws may be unjust and that

[002-327]peoples may go wrong, but he had found no principle for the guidance of

[002-328]society. The Stoics discovered it in the law of nature, prior and

[002-329]superior to all the customs and written laws of peoples, and this

[002-330]doctrine, spreading outside Stoic circles, caught hold of the Roman

[002-331]world and affected Roman legislation.

[002-332]These philosophies have carried us from Greece to Rome. In the later

[002-333]Roman Republic and the early Empire, no restrictions were imposed on

[002-334]opinion, and these philosophies, which made the individual the first

[002-335]consideration, spread widely. Most of the leading men were unbelievers

[002-336]in the official religion of the State, but they considered it valuable

[002-337]for the purpose of keeping the uneducated populace in order. A Greek

[002-338]historian expresses high approval of the Roman policy of cultivating

[002-339]superstition for the benefit of the masses. This was the attitude of

[002-340]Cicero, and the view that a false religion is indispensable as a social

[002-341]machine was general among ancient unbelievers. It is common, in one form

[002-342]or another, to-day; at least, religions are constantly defended on the

[002-343]ground not of truth but of utility. This defence belongs to the

[002-344]statecraft of Machiavelli, who taught that religion is necessary for

[002-345]government,

[002-346][40] and that it may be the duty of a ruler to support a religion which

[002-347]he believes to be false.

[002-348]A word must be said of Lucian (second century A.D.), the last Greek man

[002-349]of letters whose writings appeal to everybody. He attacked the popular

[002-350]mythology with open ridicule. It is impossible to say whether his

[002-351]satires had any effect at the time beyond affording enjoyment to

[002-352]educated infidels who read them. Zeus in a Tragedy Part is one of the

[002-353]most effective. The situation which Lucian imagined here would be

[002-354]paralleled if a modern writer were blasphemously to represent the

[002-355]Persons of the Trinity with some eminent angels and saints discussing in

[002-356]a celestial smoke-room the alarming growth of unbelief in England and

[002-357]then by means of a telephonic apparatus overhearing a dispute between a

[002-358]freethinker and a parson on a public platform in London. The absurdities

[002-359]of anthropomorphism have never been the subject of more brilliant

[002-360]jesting than in Lucian’s satires.

[002-361]The general rule of Roman policy was to tolerate throughout the Empire

[002-362]all religions and all opinions. Blasphemy was not punished. The

[002-363]principle was expressed in the maxim of the Emperor Tiberius: “If the

[002-364]gods are insulted, let them see to it themselves.” An exception to the

[002-365]rule of tolerance

[002-366][41] was made in the case of the Christian sect, and the treatment of

[002-367]this Oriental religion may be said to have inaugurated religious

[002-368]persecution in Europe. It is a matter of interest to understand why

[002-369]Emperors who were able, humane, and not in the least fanatical, adopted

[002-370]this exceptional policy.

[002-371]For a long time the Christians were only known to those Romans who

[002-372]happened to hear of them, as a sect of the Jews. The Jewish was the one

[002-373]religion which, on account of its exclusiveness and intolerance, was

[002-374]regarded by the tolerant pagans with disfavour and suspicion. But though

[002-375]it sometimes came into collision with the Roman authorities and some

[002-376]ill-advised attacks upon it were made, it was the constant policy of the

[002-377]Emperors to let it alone and to protect the Jews against the hatred

[002-378]which their own fanaticism aroused. But while the Jewish religion was

[002-379]endured so long as it was confined to those who were born into it, the

[002-380]prospect of its dissemination raised a new question. Grave misgivings

[002-381]might arise in the mind of a ruler at seeing a creed spreading which was

[002-382]aggressively hostile to all the other creeds of the world—creeds which

[002-383]lived together in amity—and had earned for its adherents the reputation

[002-384]of being the enemies of the human race. Might not its expansion

[002-385][42] beyond the Israelites involve ultimately a danger to the Empire?

[002-386]For its spirit was incompatible with the traditions and basis of Roman

[002-387]society. The Emperor Domitian seems to have seen the question in this

[002-388]light, and he took severe measures to hinder the proselytizing of Roman

[002-389]citizens. Some of those whom he struck may have been Christians, but if

[002-390]he was aware of the distinction, there was from his point of view no

[002-391]difference. Christianity resembled Judaism, from which it sprang, in

[002-392]intolerance and in hostility towards Roman society, but it differed by

[002-393]the fact that it made many proselytes while Judaism made few.

[002-394]Under Trajan we find that the principle has been laid down that to be a

[002-395]Christian is an offence punishable by death. Henceforward Christianity

[002-396]remained an illegal religion. But in practice the law was not applied

[002-397]rigorously or logically. The Emperors desired, if possible, to extirpate

[002-398]Christianity without shedding blood. Trajan laid down that Christians

[002-399]were not to be sought out, that no anonymous charges were to be noticed,

[002-400]and that an informer who failed to make good his charge should be liable

[002-401]to be punished under the laws against calumny. Christians themselves

[002-402]recognized that this edict practically protected them. There were

[002-403][43] some executions in the second century—not many that are well

[002-404]attested—and Christians courted the pain and glory of martyrdom. There

[002-405]is evidence to show that when they were arrested their escape was often

[002-406]connived at. In general, the persecution of the Christians was rather

[002-407]provoked by the populace than desired by the authorities. The populace

[002-408]felt a horror of this mysterious Oriental sect which openly hated all

[002-409]the gods and prayed for the destruction of the world. When floods,

[002-410]famines, and especially fires occurred they were apt to be attributed to

[002-411]the black magic of the Christians.

[002-412]When any one was accused of Christianity, he was required, as a means of

[002-413]testing the truth of the charge, to offer incense to the gods or to the

[002-414]statues of deified Emperors. His compliance at once exonerated him. The

[002-415]objection of the Christians—they and the Jews were the only objectors—to

[002-416]the worship of the Emperors was, in the eyes of the Romans, one of the

[002-417]most sinister signs that their religion was dangerous. The purpose of

[002-418]this worship was to symbolize the unity and solidarity of an Empire

[002-419]which embraced so many peoples of different beliefs and different gods;

[002-420]its intention was political, to promote union and loyalty; and it is not

[002-421]surprising that those who denounced it should

[002-422][44] be suspected of a disloyal spirit. But it must be noted that there

[002-423]was no necessity for any citizen to take part in this worship. No

[002-424]conformity was required from any inhabitants of the Empire who were not

[002-425]serving the State as soldiers or civil functionaries. Thus the effect

[002-426]was to debar Christians from military and official careers.

[002-427]The Apologies for Christianity which appeared at this period (second

[002-428]century) might have helped, if the Emperors (to whom some of them were

[002-429]addressed) had read them, to confirm the view that it was a political

[002-430]danger. It would have been easy to read between the lines that, if the

[002-431]Christians ever got the upper hand, they would not spare the cults of

[002-432]the State. The contemporary work of Tatian (A Discourse to the Greeks)

[002-433]reveals what the Apologists more or less sought to disguise, invincible

[002-434]hatred towards the civilization in which they lived. Any reader of the

[002-435]Christian literature of the time could not fail to see that in a State

[002-436]where Christians had the power there would be no tolerance of other

[002-437]religious practices. [4] If the Emperors made an exception to their

[002-438]tolerant policy in the case of Christianity, their purpose was to

[002-439]safeguard tolerance.

[002-440][45]

[002-441]In the third century the religion, though still forbidden, was quite

[002-442]openly tolerated; the Church organized itself without concealment;

[002-443]ecclesiastical councils assembled without interference. There were some

[002-444]brief and local attempts at repression, there was only one grave

[002-445]persecution (begun by Decius, A.D. 250, and continued by Valerian). In

[002-446]fact, throughout this century, there were not many victims, though

[002-447]afterwards the Christians invented a whole mythology of martyrdoms. Many

[002-448]cruelties were imputed to Emperors under whom we know that the Church

[002-449]enjoyed perfect peace.

[002-450]A long period of civil confusion, in which the Empire seemed to be

[002-451]tottering to its fall, had been terminated by the Emperor Diocletian,

[002-452]who, by his radical administrative reforms, helped to preserve the Roman

[002-453]power in its integrity for another century. He desired to support his

[002-454]work of political consolidation by reviving the Roman spirit, and he

[002-455]attempted to infuse new life into the official religion. To this end he

[002-456]determined to suppress the growing influence of the Christians, who,

[002-457]though a minority, were very numerous, and he organized a persecution.

[002-458]It was long, cruel and bloody; it was the most whole-hearted, general

[002-459]and systematic effort to crush the forbidden faith. It was a

[002-460][46] failure, the Christians were now too numerous to be crushed. After

[002-461]the abdication of Diocletian, the Emperors who reigned in different

[002-462]parts of the realm did not agree as to the expediency of his policy, and

[002-463]the persecution ended by edicts of toleration (A.D. 311 and 313). These

[002-464]documents have an interest for the history of religious liberty.

[002-465]The first, issued in the eastern provinces, ran as follows:—

[002-466]“We were particularly desirous of reclaiming into the way of reason and

[002-467]nature the deluded Christians, who had renounced the religion and

[002-468]ceremonies instituted by their fathers and, presumptuously despising the

[002-469]practice of antiquity, had invented extravagant laws and opinions

[002-470]according to the dictates of their fancy, and had collected a various

[002-471]society from the different provinces of our Empire. The edicts which we

[002-472]have published to enforce the worship of the gods, having exposed many

[002-473]of the Christians to danger and distress, many having suffered death and

[002-474]many more, who still persist in their impious folly, being left

[002-475]destitute of any public exercise of religion, we are disposed to extend

[002-476]to those unhappy men the effects of our wonted clemency. We permit them,

[002-477]therefore, freely to profess their private opinions, and to assemble in

[002-478]their conventicles

[002-479][47] without fear or molestation, provided always that they preserve a

[002-480]due respect to the established laws and government.” [5]

[002-481]The second, of which Constantine was the author, known as the Edict of

[002-482]Milan, was to a similar effect, and based toleration on the Emperor’s

[002-483]care for the peace and happiness of his subjects and on the hope of

[002-484]appeasing the Deity whose seat is in heaven.

[002-485]The relations between the Roman government and the Christians raised the

[002-486]general question of persecution and freedom of conscience. A State, with

[002-487]an official religion, but perfectly tolerant of all creeds and cults,

[002-488]finds that a society had arisen in its midst which is uncompromisingly

[002-489]hostile to all creeds but its own and which, if it had the power, would

[002-490]suppress all but its own. The government, in self-defence, decides to

[002-491]check the dissemination of these subversive ideas and makes the

[002-492]profession of that creed a crime, not on account of its particular

[002-493]tenets, but on account of the social consequences of those tenets. The

[002-494]members of the society cannot without violating their consciences and

[002-495]incurring damnation abandon their exclusive doctrine. The principle of

[002-496]freedom of conscience is asserted as superior to all obligations to the

[002-497]State, and the State, confronted

[002-498][48] by this new claim, is unable to admit it. Persecution is the

[002-499]result.

[002-500]Even from the standpoint of an orthodox and loyal pagan the persecution

[002-501]of the Christians is indefensible, because blood was shed uselessly. In

[002-502]other words, it was a great mistake because it was unsuccessful. For

[002-503]persecution is a choice between two evils. The alternatives are violence

[002-504](which no reasonable defender of persecution would deny to be an evil in

[002-505]itself) and the spread of dangerous opinions. The first is chosen simply

[002-506]to avoid the second, on the ground that the second is the greater evil.

[002-507]But if the persecution is not so devised and carried out as to

[002-508]accomplish its end, then you have two evils instead of one, and nothing

[002-509]can justify this. From their point of view, the Emperors had good

[002-510]reasons for regarding Christianity as dangerous and anti-social, but

[002-511]they should either have let it alone or taken systematic measures to

[002-512]destroy it. If at an early stage they had established a drastic and

[002-513]systematic inquisition, they might possibly have exterminated it. This

[002-514]at least would have been statesmanlike. But they had no conception of

[002-515]extreme measures, and they did not understand —they had no experience to

[002-516]guide them —the sort of problem they had to deal with. They hoped to

[002-517]succeed by intimidation.

[002-518][49] Their attempts at suppression were vacillating, fitful, and

[002-519]ridiculously ineffectual. The later persecutions (of A.D. 250 and 303)

[002-520]had no prospect of success. It is particularly to be observed that no

[002-521]effort was made to suppress Christian literature.

[002-522]The higher problem whether persecution, even if it attains the desired

[002-523]end, is justifiable, was not considered. The struggle hinged on

[002-524]antagonism between the conscience of the individual and the authority

[002-525]and supposed interests of the State. It was the question which had been

[002-526]raised by Socrates, raised now on a wider platform in a more pressing

[002-527]and formidable shape: what is to happen when obedience to the law is

[002-528]inconsistent with obedience to an invisible master? Is it incumbent on

[002-529]the State to respect the conscience of the individual at all costs, or

[002-530]within what limits? The Christians did not attempt a solution, the

[002-531]general problem did not interest them. They claimed the right of freedom

[002-532]exclusively for themselves from a non-Christian government; and it is

[002-533]hardly going too far to suspect that they would have applauded the

[002-534]government if it had suppressed the Gnostic sects whom they hated and

[002-535]calumniated. In any case, when a Christian State was established, they

[002-536]would completely forget the principle which they

[002-537][50] had invoked. The martyrs died for conscience, but not for liberty.

[002-538]To-day the greatest of the Churches demands freedom of conscience in the

[002-539]modern States which she does not control, but refuses to admit that,

[002-540]where she had the power, it would be incumbent on her to concede it.

[002-541]If we review the history of classical antiquity as a whole, we may

[002-542]almost say that freedom of thought was like the air men breathed. It was

[002-543]taken for granted and nobody thought about it. If seven or eight

[002-544]thinkers at Athens were penalized for heterodoxy, in some and perhaps in

[002-545]most of these cases heterodoxy was only a pretext. They do not

[002-546]invalidate the general facts that the advance of knowledge was not

[002-547]impeded by prejudice, or science retarded by the weight of unscientific

[002-548]authority. The educated Greeks were tolerant because they were friends

[002-549]of reason and did not set up any authority to overrule reason. Opinions

[002-550]were not imposed except by argument; you were not expected to receive

[002-551]some “kingdom of heaven” like a little child, or to prostrate your

[002-552]intellect before an authority claiming to be infallible.

[002-553]But this liberty was not the result of a conscious policy or deliberate

[002-554]conviction, and therefore it was precarious. The problems

[002-555][51] of freedom of thought, religious liberty, toleration, had not been

[002-556]forced upon society and were never seriously considered. When

[002-557]Christianity confronted the Roman government, no one saw that in the

[002-558]treatment of a small, obscure, and, to pagan thinkers, uninteresting or

[002-559]repugnant sect, a principle of the deepest social importance was

[002-560]involved. A long experience of the theory and practice of persecution

[002-561]was required to base securely the theory of freedom of thought. The

[002-562]lurid policy of coercion which the Christian Church adopted, and its

[002-563]consequences, would at last compel reason to wrestle with the problem

[002-564]and discover the justification of intellectual liberty. The spirit of

[002-565]the Greeks and Romans, alive in their works, would, after a long period

[002-566]of obscuration, again enlighten the world and aid in re-establishing the

[002-567]reign of reason, which they had carelessly enjoyed without assuring its

[002-568]foundations.
