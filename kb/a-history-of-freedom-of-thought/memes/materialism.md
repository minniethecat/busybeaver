## Definition and Origins

Materialism, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the philosophical position that matter is the fundamental reality and that all phenomena, including consciousness, can be explained in terms of material interactions. In the context of Bury's narrative, materialism emerged from ancient Greek philosophy, particularly through the Epicurean school, and became a significant force in challenging religious worldviews.

The Epicurean system held that the universe consisted entirely of atoms and void, with no divine intervention in human affairs. Bury notes that "Epicurean materialism" originated in antiquity and later "was transformed into a pantheistic mysticism" during medieval transmission (004-241). This transformation illustrates how materialist ideas were often adapted or softened to survive within hostile intellectual environments.

## Historical Development

Ancient Epicurean materialism was preserved primarily through the Roman poet Lucretius, whose work *De Rerum Natura* transmitted atomistic philosophy to later ages. During the Middle Ages, materialist thought was largely suppressed by Christian orthodoxy, which insisted on the existence of immaterial souls and a transcendent God.

The revival of materialism in early modern Europe was closely tied to the scientific revolution. Bury records that Pierre Gassendi, a seventeenth-century French philosopher, "had revived materialism in its Epicurean shape" (006-061). Gassendi's work made Epicurean atomism intellectually respectable by reconciling it with Christian doctrine, though this reconciliation was often strained.

In the Enlightenment, materialism became increasingly associated with atheism and anti-clerical thought. Baron d'Holbach's *System of Nature* (1770) presented a comprehensive materialist worldview that explicitly rejected religious belief. The association between materialism and irreligion made it a target of persecution and intellectual controversy throughout the eighteenth century.

## Key Proponents

- **Epicurus** developed the original materialist philosophy in ancient Greece, arguing that the world consisted of atoms moving through void. His system denied divine providence while not explicitly denying the existence of gods.
- **Lucretius** preserved and elaborated Epicurean materialism in his poem *De Rerum Natura*, which survived as the primary vehicle for atomistic ideas through the Middle Ages.
- **Pierre Gassendi** revived Epicurean materialism in the seventeenth century, attempting to reconcile it with Christian theology. His work reintroduced atomism into European philosophy (006-061).
- **Baron d'Holbach** advanced the most uncompromising materialist philosophy of the Enlightenment, arguing that all mental phenomena could be reduced to physical processes.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Materialism contributed to freedom of thought by providing a philosophical framework that did not require supernatural explanations. By arguing that natural phenomena could be explained through material causes alone, materialists challenged the intellectual authority of religious institutions.

Bury notes that adversaries of Herbert Spencer "commonly stigmatize his theory as materialism, but this is a" mischaracterization of his broader evolutionary philosophy (007-0216). This illustrates how the label "materialism" was frequently used as a term of abuse against any thinker who questioned religious orthodoxy, even when their views were more nuanced.

The materialist challenge to religious cosmology forced a broader reconsideration of the relationship between science and faith. The gradual acceptance that the natural world could be explained without reference to divine intervention was a crucial step in the development of intellectual liberty. Materialism, by insisting on natural causation, helped establish the methodological naturalism that underlies modern scientific inquiry.

## Criticism and Opposition

Materialism faced sustained opposition from religious authorities who viewed it as incompatible with belief in God and the immortal soul. The charge of materialism was frequently leveled against freethinkers as a means of discrediting their broader philosophical positions. Even thinkers who were not strictly materialist, such as Spencer, found themselves accused of the doctrine (007-0216).

The perceived ethical implications of materialism also drew criticism. Opponents argued that if humans were merely material beings, traditional morality would collapse. This concern motivated much of the legal and social persecution of materialist philosophers during the Enlightenment and beyond.
