## Definition and Origins

Divine revelation is the theological claim that God has communicated knowledge to humanity through supernatural means — typically through inspired Scripture, prophetic utterance, or direct divine intervention — that could not be attained through reason and observation alone. In the Christian tradition, revelation was held to be the source of doctrines such as the Trinity, the Incarnation, and the Atonement, all of which were beyond the reach of unaided human reason.

The concept of revelation has roots in the Jewish prophetic tradition and was central to Christian self-understanding from the earliest period. Bury notes that the Greeks had their own notion of inspired wisdom — the pre-Socratic philosophers sometimes spoke "as if they were the radiant revelation of a new" understanding (002-312) — but the Christian claim to exclusive revelation raised the stakes considerably by making acceptance of revelation the criterion of salvation.

## Historical Development

The rationalist challenge to revelation emerged clearly in the seventeenth century, as philosophers began to insist on the primacy of reason even in religious matters. Bury identifies "the progress of rationalism" in the seventeenth century as fundamentally a challenge to "supernatural revelation" (006-003). The question was not whether God could reveal truths, but whether purported revelations could be authenticated and whether revelation could contradict reason.

[[locke]] occupied an intermediate position that proved highly influential. "While he accepted the Christian revelation, he held that revelation if" it is genuinely from God cannot contradict reason, because reason is also a divine gift (006-105). Crucially, Locke insisted that "revelation cannot give us knowledge as completely certain as" rational demonstration, because we must first use reason to determine whether a purported revelation is genuine (006-107). This principle — that reason must judge revelation — effectively subordinated revelation to rational scrutiny. Locke warned against the temptation to "throw away reason to make room for revelation" (006-108), a phrase that became a watchword of rational religion.

The orthodox position held that "the Christian revelation is not contrary to reason" (006-112) because reason and revelation have the same divine author. But this principle cut both ways: if revelation appeared to contradict reason, then either the interpretation of revelation or the claim to its divine origin must be mistaken. The deists drew the logical conclusion that a "reasonable Deity gave a revelation, its purpose must be to enlighten" humanity on matters not accessible to reason — but that no such supplementary revelation was necessary (006-122).

The deist critique, articulated most forcefully by [[matthew-tindal]], argued that "revelation is superfluous, for it adds nothing" to what reason can discover through the study of nature (006-322). If God is perfectly rational, then the laws of nature are His fullest revelation, and any claim to a supplementary scriptural revelation is both unnecessary and suspect. Tindal's argument reduced Christianity's claim to uniqueness to the proposition that the Christian revelation "is not enough to show that the revelation" comes from God — it must also be shown to contain truths beyond reason, and no such truths were forthcoming (006-744).

The radical conclusion was drawn by [[thomas-paine]], "who regarded nature as God's revelation" (006-835) and dismissed the Bible as a human fabrication. For Paine, the natural world was the only authentic revelation, accessible to all humanity regardless of culture or education, while the claim to a special scriptural revelation was a tool of priestly power. A further line of critique characterized Christianity "not as a special revelation of" God but as a human philosophical development (007-0172), arguing that the ideas "peculiar to the Christian revelation are derived" from earlier pagan and Jewish sources (007-0255).

## Key Proponents of the Critique

- **John Locke** — Held that reason must judge all claims to revelation, and that revelation cannot contradict reason. His careful balancing act provided cover for more radical positions.
- **Matthew Tindal** — Author of *Christianity as Old as the Creation* (1730), the "Deist's Bible," which argued that natural religion is complete and scriptural revelation superfluous.
- **John Toland** — In *Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696), argued that nothing in the Gospel is contrary to or above reason, effectively eliminating the category of revealed mystery.
- **Thomas Paine** — In *The Age of Reason*, rejected all claims to supernatural revelation and proclaimed nature itself as the only authentic divine communication.

Among the orthodox defenders, Bishop Butler argued against Tindal, and William Paley constructed an elaborate evidential case for Christian revelation based on miracles and prophecy. But by the nineteenth century, even many theologians conceded that revelation "is strongly affirmed" by faith but "revelation and theology" could not supply the kind of demonstrative certainty that rationalists demanded (006-562).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The debate over revelation was pivotal for freedom of thought because it addressed the fundamental question of intellectual authority. If God had revealed truths in Scripture, then those truths were binding on all humanity, and dissent from them was not free thought but rebellion against God. But if the claim to revelation could not be independently verified — if, in the end, one had to accept revelation on the authority of the Church that authenticated it — then the entire edifice of doctrinal control rested on a circular argument.

The rationalist critique of revelation exposed this circularity. By insisting that reason must authenticate any claim to revelation, it shifted the ultimate authority from the Church to the individual mind — from authority to reason, in Bury's central dichotomy. Once this step was taken, freedom of thought became not merely a practical necessity but a philosophical imperative.

An additional line of criticism that proved powerful in the nineteenth century was that revelation was incompatible with scientific progress. If, as geology and biology demonstrated, the natural world had developed through long ages of gradual change, then the simple biblical narrative of creation was "a dream, because revelation contradicts it" (008-157). The more science advanced, the more revelation appeared not as divine wisdom but as a record of pre-scientific human ignorance. The idea that revelation manifests "like a revelation from on high" (005-468) could no longer compete with the patient accumulation of knowledge through rational inquiry.
