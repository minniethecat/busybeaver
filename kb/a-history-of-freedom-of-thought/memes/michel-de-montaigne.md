## Life and Background

Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592) was born near Bordeaux, France, into a wealthy family. His father ensured he was raised speaking Latin as his first language, immersing him in classical learning from earliest childhood. After studying law, Montaigne served as a magistrate and later as mayor of Bordeaux.

At the age of thirty-eight, Montaigne retired from public life to his estate, where he began writing the *Essais* — a new literary form he invented. Writing in a personal, conversational style, he explored topics ranging from education and friendship to death and custom, always with intellectual humility and open-minded curiosity.

## Major Contributions

Montaigne's *Essais* (1580–1595) represent one of the most original achievements of Renaissance literature. The essay form itself was his invention — a mode of writing that allowed him to explore ideas tentatively, without dogmatic conclusions. His famous question — "*Que sçay-je?*" ("What do I know?") — became the motto of his skeptical philosophy.

Montaigne's skepticism was not despairing but liberating. By questioning the certainty of all knowledge claims, including religious ones, he opened space for toleration and intellectual freedom. His essays draw on extensive classical learning — Plutarch, Seneca, Lucretius — to demonstrate the diversity of human customs and beliefs, undermining claims to absolute truth.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Bury presents Montaigne as representing the "broad-mindedness of the Renaissance" (004-043). His skeptical essays exemplified the intellectual openness that characterized Renaissance humanism at its best, challenging dogmatic certainties across all domains of thought.

The logical results of Montaigne's skepticism were far-reaching: by demonstrating that human reason could not achieve certainty on fundamental questions, he inadvertently made room for a separation between the realm of faith and the realm of reason (004-061).

This skeptical fideism — accepting religious authority precisely because reason is unreliable — would be developed by later thinkers like Pierre Bayle.

Montaigne's influence flowed from the Renaissance into the early modern period. Bury traces a line from "the Renaissance, represented by Montaigne" into a new phase of intellectual development (004-074). Later, Montaigne's skeptical method influenced Blaise Pascal, who both absorbed and reacted against it (006-154).

## Legacy and Influence

Montaigne's influence extends across centuries and disciplines. His essays shaped the development of modern skepticism, influencing Descartes, Pascal, and Bayle. His tolerant, questioning spirit anticipated Enlightenment critiques of dogma. The essay form he created became one of the principal vehicles of modern intellectual life.

His anthropological curiosity — his interest in the diversity of customs and beliefs — made him an early pioneer of cultural relativism. His essay "On Cannibals" challenged European ethnocentrism and questioned the equation of custom with natural law.

## Key Works

- *Essais* (Books I–II, 1580; Book III, 1588) — The complete essays, covering philosophy, ethics, education, and human nature

- *Journal de voyage* (published posthumously) — Travel journal through Italy, revealing his observational method
