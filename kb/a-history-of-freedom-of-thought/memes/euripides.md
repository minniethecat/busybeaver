# Euripides

**Euripides** (c. 480–406 BCE) was an Athenian tragic poet who, in an age when dramatic performances were part of the religious solemnities of the feast of [[Dionysus]], was saturated with the rationalistic speculation of his time and frequently allowed his characters to voice highly unorthodox opinions (002-144).

## Freethought on the Athenian Stage

By the late fifth century BCE, rationalistic ideas were venturing to appear on the Athenian stage despite the sacred context of the dramatic festivals. Euripides was the foremost representative of this tendency. While different opinions may be held as to the ultimate tendencies of some of his tragedies, he repeatedly gave voice, through his characters, to views that challenged conventional religious assumptions (002-144).

His unorthodoxy did not go unnoticed: he was prosecuted for impiety by a popular politician, an early instance of the use of blasphemy laws to target intellectuals whose ideas threatened traditional belief (002-144).

## Historical Context

The last thirty years of the fifth century BCE saw a considerable spread of unorthodoxy among the educated classes in [[Athens]]. There was already a large enough section of influential rationalists to render impossible any organized repression of intellectual liberty, though the chief evil of the blasphemy law was that it could be exploited for personal or political ends (002-144). Euripides lived and worked at the center of this intellectual ferment, and his plays both reflected and advanced the freethinking currents of his age.
