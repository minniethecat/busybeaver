## Chapter V
### RELIGIOUS TOLERATION

[005-001]IN the third century B.C. the Indian king Asoka, a man of religious zeal

[005-002]but of tolerant spirit, confronted by the struggle between two hostile

[005-003]religions (Brahmanism and Buddhism), decided that both should be equally

[005-004]privileged and honoured in his dominions. His ordinances on the matter

[005-005]are memorable

[005-006][93] as the earliest existing Edicts of toleration. In Europe, as we

[005-007]saw, the principle of toleration was for the first time definitely

[005-008]expressed in the Roman Imperial Edicts which terminated the persecution

[005-009]of the Christians.

[005-010]The religious strife of the sixteenth century raised the question in its

[005-011]modern form, and for many generations it was one of the chief problems

[005-012]of statesmen and the subject of endless controversial pamphlets.

[005-013][[toleration|Toleration]] means incomplete religious liberty, and there are many

[005-014]degrees of it. It might be granted to certain Christian sects; it might

[005-015]be granted to Christian sects, but these alone; it might be granted to

[005-016]all religions, but not to freethinkers; or to deists, but not to

[005-017]atheists. It might mean the concession of some civil rights, but not of

[005-018]others; it might mean the exclusion of those who are tolerated from

[005-019]public offices or from certain professions. The religious liberty now

[005-020]enjoyed in Western lands has been gained through various stages of

[005-021]toleration.

[005-022]We owe the modern principle of toleration to the Italian group of

[005-023]Reformers, who rejected the doctrine of the Trinity and were the fathers

[005-024]of Unitarianism. The Reformation movement had spread to Italy, but Rome

[005-025]was successful in suppressing it, and many heretics fled to Switzerland.

[005-026]The anti-Trinitarian

[005-027][94] group were forced by the intolerance of Calvin to flee to

[005-028]Transylvania and Poland where they propagated their doctrines. The

[005-029]Unitarian creed was moulded by Fausto Sozzini, generally known as

[005-030]Socinus, and in the catechism of his sect (1574) persecution is

[005-031]condemned. This repudiation of the use of force in the interest of

[005-032]religion is a consequence of the Socinian doctrines. For, unlike Luther

[005-033]and Calvin, the Socinians conceded such a wide room to individual

[005-034]judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism

[005-035]would have been inconsistent with its principles. In other words, there

[005-036]was a strong rationalistic element which was lacking in the Trinitarian

[005-037]creeds.

[005-038]It was under the influence of the Socinian spirit that Castellion of

[005-039]Savoy sounded the trumpet of toleration in a pamphlet denouncing the

[005-040]burning of Servetus, whereby he earned the malignant hatred of Calvin.

[005-041]He maintained the innocence of error and ridiculed the importance which

[005-042]the Churches laid on obscure questions such as predestination and the

[005-043]Trinity. “To discuss the difference between the Law and the Gospel,

[005-044]gratuitous remission of sins or imputed righteousness, is as if a man

[005-045]were to discuss whether a prince was to come on horseback,

[005-046][95] or in a chariot, or dressed in white or in red.” [1] Religion is a

[005-047]curse if persecution is a necessary part of it.

[005-048]For a long time the Socinians and those who came under their influence

[005-049]when, driven from Poland, they passed into Germany and Holland, were the

[005-050]only sects which advocated toleration. It was adopted from them by the

[005-051]Anabaptists and by the Arminian section of the Reformed Church of

[005-052]Holland. And in Holland, the founder of the English Congregationalists,

[005-053]who (under the name of Independents) played such an important part in

[005-054]the history of the Civil War and the Commonwealth, learned the principle

[005-055]of liberty of conscience.

[005-056]Socinus thought that this principle could be realized without abolishing

[005-057]the State Church. He contemplated a close union between the State and

[005-058]the prevailing Church, combined with complete toleration for other

[005-059]sects. It is under this system (which has been called jurisdictional)

[005-060]that religious liberty has been realized in European States. But there

[005-061]is another and simpler method, that of separating Church from State and

[005-062]placing all religions on an equality. This was the solution which the

[005-063]Anabaptists would have preferred. They detested the State; and the

[005-064]doctrine of religious liberty was not

[005-065][96] precious to them. Their ideal system would have been an Anabaptist

[005-066]theocracy; separation was the second best.

[005-067]In Europe, public opinion was not ripe for separation, inasmuch as the

[005-068]most powerful religious bodies were alike in regarding toleration as

[005-069]wicked indifference. But it was introduced in a small corner of the new

[005-070]world beyond the Atlantic in the seventeenth century. The Puritans who

[005-071]fled from the intolerance of the English Church and State and founded

[005-072]colonies in New England, were themselves equally intolerant, not only to

[005-073]Anglicans and Catholics, but to Baptists and Quakers. They set up

[005-074]theocratical governments from which all who did not belong to their own

[005-075]sect were excluded. Roger Williams had imbibed from the Dutch Arminians

[005-076]the idea of separation of Church from State. On account of this heresy

[005-077]he was driven from Massachusetts, and he founded Providence to be a

[005-078]refuge for those whom the Puritan colonists persecuted. Here he set up a

[005-079]democratic constitution in which the magistrates had power only in civil

[005-080]matters and could not interfere with religion. Other towns were

[005-081]presently founded in Rhode Island, and a charter of Charles II (1663)

[005-082]confirmed the constitution, which secured to all citizens professing

[005-083]Christianity, of whatever

[005-084][97] form, the full enjoyment of political rights. Non-Christians were

[005-085]tolerated, but were not admitted to the political rights of Christians.

[005-086]So far, the new State fell short of perfect liberty. But the fact that

[005-087]Jews were soon admitted, notwithstanding, to full citizenship shows how

[005-088]free the atmosphere was. To Roger Williams belongs the glory of having

[005-089]founded the first modern State which was really tolerant and was based

[005-090]on the principle of taking the control of religious matters entirely out

[005-091]of the hands of the civil government.

[005-092]Toleration was also established in the Roman Catholic colony of

[005-093]Maryland, but in a different way. Through the influence of Lord

[005-094]Baltimore an Act of Toleration was passed in 1649, notable as the first

[005-095]decree, voted by a legal assembly, granting complete freedom to all

[005-096]Christians. No one professing faith in Christ was to be molested in

[005-097]regard to his religion. But the law was heavy on all outside this pale.

[005-098]Any one who blasphemed God or attacked the Trinity or any member of the

[005-099]Trinity was threatened by the penalty of death. The tolerance of

[005-100]Maryland attracted so many Protestant settlers from Virginia that the

[005-101]Protestants became a majority, and as soon as they won political

[005-102]preponderance, they introduced an Act (1654)

[005-103][98] excluding Papists and Prelatists from toleration. The rule of the

[005-104]Baltimores was restored after 1660, and the old religious freedom was

[005-105]revived, but with the accession of William III the Protestants again

[005-106]came into power and the toleration which the Catholics had instituted in

[005-107]Maryland came to an end.

[005-108]It will be observed that in both these cases freedom was incomplete; but

[005-109]it was much larger and more fundamental in Rhode Island, where it had

[005-110]been ultimately derived from the doctrine of Socinus. [2] When the

[005-111]colonies became independent of England the Federal Constitution which

[005-112]they set up was absolutely secular, but it was left to each member of

[005-113]the Union to adopt Separation or not (1789). If separation has become

[005-114]the rule in the American States, it may be largely due to the fact that

[005-115]on any other system the governments would have found it difficult to

[005-116]impose mutual tolerance on the sects. It must be added that in Maryland

[005-117]and a few southern States atheists still suffer from some political

[005-118]disabilities.

[005-119]In England, the experiment of Separation would have been tried under the

[005-120]Commonwealth, if the Independents had had their way. This policy was

[005-121]overruled by Cromwell.

[005-122][99] The new national Church included Presbyterians, Independents, and

[005-123]Baptists, but liberty of worship was granted to all Christian sects,

[005-124]except Roman Catholics and Anglicans. If the parliament had had the

[005-125]power, this toleration would have been a mere name. The Presbyterians

[005-126]regarded toleration as a work of the Devil, and would have persecuted

[005-127]the Independents if they could. But under Cromwell’s autocratic rule

[005-128]even the Anglicans lived in peace, and toleration was extended to the

[005-129]Jews. In these days, voices were raised from various quarters advocating

[005-130]toleration on general grounds. [3] The most illustrious advocate was

[005-131]Milton, the poet, who was in favour of the severance of Church from

[005-132]State.

[005-133]In Milton’s Areopagitica: a speech for the liberty of unlicensed

[005-134]printing (1644), the freedom of the Press is eloquently sustained by

[005-135]arguments which are valid for freedom of thought in general. It is shown

[005-136]that the censorship will conduce “to the discouragement of all learning

[005-137]and the stop of truth, not only by disexercising and blunting our

[005-138]abilities in what we know already, but by hindering and cropping the

[005-139]discovery that might be yet further made, both in religious

[005-140][100] and civil wisdom.” For knowledge is advanced through the utterance

[005-141]of new opinions, and truth is discovered by free discussion. If the

[005-142]waters of truth “flow not in a perpetual progression they sicken into a

[005-143]muddy pool of conformity and tradition.” Books which are authorized by

[005-144]the licensers are apt to be, as Bacon said, “but the language of the

[005-145]times,” and do not contribute to progress. The examples of the countries

[005-146]where the censorship is severe do not suggest that it is useful for

[005-147]morals: “look into Italy and Spain, whether those places be one scruple

[005-148]the better, the honester, the wiser, the chaster, since all the

[005-149]inquisitional rigour that hath been executed upon books.” Spain indeed

[005-150]could reply, “We are, what is more important, more orthodox.” It is

[005-151]interesting to notice that Milton places freedom of thought above civil

[005-152]liberty: “Give me the liberty to know, to utter, and to argue freely

[005-153]according to conscience, above all other liberties.”

[005-154]With the restoration of the Monarchy and the Anglican Church, religious

[005-155]liberty was extinguished by a series of laws against Dissenters. To the

[005-156]Revolution we owe the Act of Toleration (1689) from which the religious

[005-157]freedom which England enjoys at present is derived. It granted freedom

[005-158]of worship to Presbyterians, Congregationalists,

[005-159][101] Baptists and Quakers, but only to these; Catholics and Unitarians

[005-160]were expressly excepted and the repressive legislation of Charles II

[005-161]remained in force against them. It was a characteristically English

[005-162]measure, logically inconsistent and absurd, a mixture of tolerance and

[005-163]intolerance, but suitable to the circumstances and the state of public

[005-164]opinion at the time.

[005-165]In the same year John Locke’s famous (first) Letter concerning

[005-166]Toleration appeared in Latin. Three subsequent letters developed and

[005-167]illustrated his thesis. The main argument is based on the principle that

[005-168]the business of civil government is quite distinct from that of

[005-169]religion, that the State is a society constituted only for preserving

[005-170]and promoting the civil interests of its members —civil interests

[005-171]meaning life, liberty, health, and the possession of property. The care

[005-172]of souls is not committed to magistrates more than to other men. For the

[005-173]magistrate can only use outward force; but true religion means the

[005-174]inward persuasion of the mind, and the mind is so made that force cannot

[005-175]compel it to believe. So too it is absurd for a State to make laws to

[005-176]enforce a religion, for laws are useless without penalties, and

[005-177]penalties are impertinent because they cannot convince.

[005-178]Moreover, even if penalties could change

[005-179][102] men’s beliefs, this would not conduce to the salvation of souls.

[005-180]Would more men be saved if all blindly resigned themselves to the will

[005-181]of their rulers and accepted the religion of their country? For as the

[005-182]princes of the world are divided in religion, one country alone would be

[005-183]in the right, and all the rest of the world would have to follow their

[005-184]princes to destruction; “and that which heightens the absurdity, and

[005-185]very ill suits the notion of a deity, men would owe their eternal

[005-186]happiness or their eternal misery to the places of their nativity.” This

[005-187]is a principle on which Locke repeatedly insists. If a State is

[005-188]justified in imposing a creed, it follows that in all the lands, except

[005-189]the one or few in which the true faith prevails, it is the duty of the

[005-190]subjects to embrace a false religion. If Protestantism is promoted in

[005-191]England, Popery by the same rule will be promoted in France. “What is

[005-192]true and good in England will be true and good at Rome too, in China, or

[005-193]Geneva.” Toleration is the principle which gives to the true faith the

[005-194]best chance of prevailing.

[005-195]Locke would concede full liberty to idolaters, by whom he means the

[005-196]Indians of North America, and he makes some scathing remarks on the

[005-197]ecclesiastical zeal which forced these “innocent pagans” to forsake

[005-198][103] their ancient religion. But his toleration, though it extends

[005-199]beyond the Christian pale, is not complete. He excepts in the first

[005-200]place Roman Catholics, not on account of their theological dogmas but

[005-201]because they “teach that faith is not to be kept with heretics,” that

[005-202]“kings excommunicated forfeit their crowns and kingdoms,” and because

[005-203]they deliver themselves up to the protection and service of a foreign

[005-204]prince—the Pope. In other words, they are politically dangerous. His

[005-205]other exception is atheists. “Those are not all to be tolerated who deny

[005-206]the being of God. Promises, covenants and oaths, which are the bonds of

[005-207]human society, can have no hold upon an atheist. The taking away of God,

[005-208]though but even in thought, dissolves all. Besides also, those that by

[005-209]their atheism undermine and destroy all religion, can have no pretence

[005-210]of religion to challenge the privilege of a Toleration.”

[005-211]Thus Locke is not free from the prejudices of his time. These exceptions

[005-212]contradict his own principle that “it is absurd that things should be

[005-213]enjoined by laws which are not in men’s power to perform. And to believe

[005-214]this or that to be true does not depend upon our will.” This applies to

[005-215]Roman Catholics as to Protestants, to atheists as to deists. Locke,

[005-216]however, perhaps thought

[005-217][104] that the speculative opinion of atheism, which was uncommon in his

[005-218]day, does depend on the will. He would have excluded from his State his

[005-219]great contemporary Spinoza.

[005-220]But in spite of its limitations Locke’s Toleration is a work of the

[005-221]highest value, and its argument takes us further than its author went.

[005-222]It asserts unrestrictedly the secular principle, and its logical issue

[005-223]is Disestablishment. A Church is merely “a free and voluntary society.”

[005-224]I may notice the remark that if infidels were to be converted by force,

[005-225]it was easier for God to do it “with armies of heavenly legions than for

[005-226]any son of the Church, how potent soever, with all his dragoons.” This

[005-227]is a polite way of stating a maxim analogous to that of the Emperor

[005-228]Tiberius (above, p. 41). If false beliefs are an offence to God, it is,

[005-229]really, his affair.

[005-230]The toleration of Nonconformists was far from pleasing extreme

[005-231]Anglicans, and the influence of this party at the beginning of the

[005-232]eighteenth century menaced the liberty of Dissenters. The situation

[005-233]provoked Defoe, who was a zealous Nonconformist, to write his pamphlet,

[005-234]The Shortest Way with the Dissenters (1702), an ironical attack upon the

[005-235]principle of toleration. It pretends to show that the Dissenters are at

[005-236]heart incorrigible rebels, that a gentle policy is useless, and suggests

[005-237][105] that all preachers at conventicles should be hanged and all

[005-238]persons found attending such meetings should be banished. This

[005-239]exceedingly amusing but terribly earnest caricature of the sentiments of

[005-240]the High Anglican party at first deceived and alarmed the Dissenters

[005-241]themselves. But the High Churchmen were furious. Defoe was fined,

[005-242]exposed in the pillory three times, and sent to Newgate prison.

[005-243]But the Tory reaction was only temporary. During the eighteenth century

[005-244]a relatively tolerant spirit prevailed among the Christian sects and new

[005-245]sects were founded. The official Church became less fanatical; many of

[005-246]its leading divines were influenced by rationalistic thought. If it had

[005-247]not been for the opposition of King George III, the Catholics might have

[005-248]been freed from their disabilities before the end of the century. This

[005-249]measure, eloquently advocated by Burke and desired by Pitt, was not

[005-250]carried till 1829, and then under the threat of a revolution in Ireland.

[005-251]In the meantime legal toleration had been extended to the Unitarians in

[005-252]1813, but they were not relieved from all disabilities till the forties.

[005-253]Jews were not admitted to the full rights of citizenship till 1858.

[005-254]The achievement of religious liberty in England in the nineteenth

[005-255]century has been mainly the work of Liberals. The Liberal

[005-256][106] party has been moving towards the ultimate goal of complete

[005-257]secularization and the separation of the Church from the State— the

[005-258]logical results of Locke’s theory of civil government. The

[005-259]Disestablishment of the Church in Ireland in 1869 partly realized this

[005-260]ideal, and now more than forty years later the Liberal party is seeking

[005-261]to apply the principle to Wales. It is highly characteristic of English

[005-262]politics and English psychology that the change should be carried out in

[005-263]this piecemeal fashion. In the other countries of the British Empire the

[005-264]system of Separation prevails; there is no connection between the State

[005-265]and any sect; no Church is anything more than a voluntary society. But

[005-266]secularization has advanced under the State Church system. It is enough

[005-267]to mention the Education Act of 1870 and the abolition of religious

[005-268]tests at Universities (1871). Other gains for freedom will be noticed

[005-269]when I come to speak in another chapter of the progress of rationalism.

[005-270]If we compare the religious situation in France in the seventeenth with

[005-271]that in the eighteenth century, it seems to be sharply contrasted with

[005-272]the development in England. In England there was a great advance towards

[005-273]religious liberty, in France there was a falling away. Until 1676 the

[005-274]French Protestants

[005-275][107] (Huguenots) were tolerated; for the next hundred years they were

[005-276]outlaws. But the toleration, which their charter (the Edict of Nantes,

[005-277]1598) secured them, was of a limited kind. They were excluded, for

[005-278]instance, from the army; they were excluded from Paris and other cities

[005-279]and districts. And the liberty which they enjoyed was confined to them;

[005-280]it was not granted to any other sect. The charter was faithfully

[005-281]maintained by the two great Cardinals (Richelieu and Mazarin) who

[005-282]governed France under Louis XIII and Louis XIV, but when the latter

[005-283]assumed the active power in 1661 he began a series of laws against the

[005-284]Protestants which culminated in the revoking of the charter (1676) and

[005-285]the beginning of a Protestant persecution.

[005-286]The French clergy justified this policy by the notorious text “Compel

[005-287]them to come in,” and appealed to St. Augustine. Their arguments evoked

[005-288]a defence of toleration by Bayle, a French Protestant who had taken

[005-289]refuge in Holland. It was entitled a Philosophical Commentary on the

[005-290]text “Compel them to come in” (1686) and in importance stands beside

[005-291]Locke’s work which was being composed at the same time. Many of the

[005-292]arguments urged by the two writers are identical. They agreed, and for

[005-293]the same reasons, in excluding Roman Catholics. The

[005-294][108] most characteristic thing in Bayle’s treatise is his sceptical

[005-295]argument that, even if it were a right principle to suppress error by

[005-296]force, no truth is certain enough to justify us in applying the theory.

[005-297]We shall see (next chapter) this eminent scholar’s contribution to

[005-298]rationalism.

[005-299]Though there was an immense exodus of Protestants from France, Louis did

[005-300]not succeed in his design of extirpating heresy from his lands. In the

[005-301]eighteenth century, under Louis XV, the presence of Protestants was

[005-302]tolerated though they were outlaws; their marriages were not recognized

[005-303]as legal, and they were liable at any moment to persecution. [[About]] the

[005-304]middle of the century a literary agitation began, conducted mainly by

[005-305]rationalists, but finally supported by enlightened Catholics, to relieve

[005-306]the affliction of the oppressed sect. It resulted at last in an Edict of

[005-307]Toleration (1787), which made the position of the Protestants endurable,

[005-308]though it excluded them from certain careers.

[005-309]The most energetic and forceful leader in the campaign against

[005-310]intolerance was Voltaire (see next chapter), and his exposure of some

[005-311]glaring cases of unjust persecution did more than general arguments to

[005-312]achieve the object. The most infamous case was that of Jean Calas, a

[005-313]Protestant merchant of Toulouse, whose son committed suicide. A report

[005-314][109] was set abroad that the young man had decided to join the Catholic

[005-315]Church, and that his father, mother, and brother, filled with Protestant

[005-316]bigotry, killed him, with the help of a friend. They were all put in

[005-317]irons, tried, and condemned, though there were no arguments for their

[005-318]guilt, except the conjecture of bigotry. Jean Calas was broken on the

[005-319]wheel, his son and daughter cast into convents, his wife left to starve.

[005-320]Through the activity of Voltaire, then living near Geneva, the widow was

[005-321]induced to go to Paris, where she was kindly received, and assisted by

[005-322]eminent lawyers; a judicial inquiry was made; the Toulouse sentence was

[005-323]reversed and the King granted pensions to those who had suffered. This

[005-324]scandal could only have happened in the provinces, according to

[005-325]Voltaire: “at Paris,” he says, “fanaticism, powerful though it may be,

[005-326]is always controlled by reason.”

[005-327]The case of Sirven, though it did not end tragically, was similar, and

[005-328]the government of Toulouse was again responsible. He was accused of

[005-329]having drowned his daughter in a well to hinder her from becoming a

[005-330]Catholic, and was, with his wife, sentenced to death. Fortunately he and

[005-331]his family had escaped to Switzerland, where they persuaded Voltaire of

[005-332]their innocence. To get the sentence reversed was the work of nine

[005-333]years, and this

[005-334][110] time it was reversed at Toulouse. When Voltaire visited Paris in

[005-335]1778 he was acclaimed by crowds as the “defender of Calas and the

[005-336]Sirvens.” His disinterested practical activity against persecution was

[005-337]of far more value than the treatise on Toleration which he wrote in

[005-338]connexion with the Calas episode. It is a poor work compared with those

[005-339]of Locke and Bayle. The tolerance which he advocates is of a limited

[005-340]kind; he would confine public offices and dignities to those who belong

[005-341]to the State religion.

[005-342]But if Voltaire’s system of toleration is limited, it is wide compared

[005-343]with the religious establishment advocated by his contemporary,

[005-344]Rousseau. Though of Swiss birth, Rousseau belongs to the literature and

[005-345]history of France; but it was not for nothing that he was brought up in

[005-346]the traditions of Calvinistic Geneva. His ideal State would, in its way,

[005-347]have been little better than any theocracy. He proposed to establish a

[005-348]“civil religion” which was to be a sort of undogmatic Christianity. But

[005-349]certain dogmas, which he considered essential, were to be imposed on all

[005-350]citizens on pain of banishment. Such were the existence of a deity, the

[005-351]future bliss of the good and punishment of the bad, the duty of

[005-352]tolerance towards all those who accepted the fundamental

[005-353][111] articles of faith. It may be said that a State founded on this

[005-354]basis would be fairly inclusive—that all Christian sects and many deists

[005-355]could find a place in it. But by imposing indispensable beliefs, it

[005-356]denies the principle of toleration. The importance of Rousseau’s idea

[005-357]lies in the fact that it inspired one of the experiments in religious

[005-358]policy which were made during the French Revolution.

[005-359]The Revolution established religious liberty in France. Most of the

[005-360]leaders were unorthodox. Their rationalism was naturally of the

[005-361]eighteenth-century type, and in the preamble to the Declaration of

[005-362]Rights (1789) deism was asserted by the words “in the presence and under

[005-363]the auspices of the Supreme Being” (against which only one voice

[005-364]protested). The Declaration laid down that no one was to be vexed on

[005-365]account of his religious opinions provided he did not thereby trouble

[005-366]public order. Catholicism was retained as the “dominant” religion;

[005-367]Protestants (but not Jews) were admitted to public office. Mirabeau, the

[005-368]greatest statesman of the day, protested strongly against the use of

[005-369]words like “tolerance” and “dominant.” He said: “The most unlimited

[005-370]liberty of religion is in my eyes a right so sacred that to express it

[005-371]by the word ‘toleration’ seems to me itself a sort of tyranny,

[005-372][112] since the authority which tolerates might also not tolerate.” The

[005-373]same protest was made in Thomas Paine’s Rights of Man which appeared two

[005-374]years later: “Toleration is not the opposite of Intolerance, but is the

[005-375]counterfeit of it. Both are despotisms. The one assumes itself the right

[005-376]of withholding liberty of conscience, and the other of granting it.”

[005-377]Paine was an ardent deist, and he added: “Were a bill brought into any

[005-378]parliament, entitled ‘An Act to tolerate or grant liberty to the

[005-379]Almighty to receive the worship of a Jew or a Turk,’ or ‘to prohibit the

[005-380]Almighty from receiving it,’ all men would startle and call it

[005-381]blasphemy. There would be an uproar. The presumption of toleration in

[005-382]religious matters would then present itself unmasked.”

[005-383]The Revolution began well, but the spirit of Mirabeau was not in the

[005-384]ascendant throughout its course. The vicissitudes in religious policy

[005-385]from 1789 to 1801 have a particular interest, because they show that the

[005-386]principle of liberty of conscience was far from possessing the minds of

[005-387]the men who were proud of abolishing the intolerance of the government

[005-388]which they had overthrown. The State Church was reorganized by the Civil

[005-389]Constitution of the Clergy (1790), by which French citizens were

[005-390]forbidden to acknowledge the authority of the Pope and

[005-391][113] the appointment of Bishops was transferred to the Electors of the

[005-392]Departments, so that the commanding influence passed from the Crown to

[005-393]the nation. Doctrine and worship were not touched. Under the democratic

[005-394]Republic which succeeded the fall of the monarchy (1792–5) this

[005-395]Constitution was maintained, but a movement to dechristianize France was

[005-396]inaugurated, and the Commune of Paris ordered the churches of all

[005-397]religions to be closed. The worship of Reason, with rites modelled on

[005-398]the Catholic, was organized in Paris and the provinces. The government,

[005-399]violently anti-Catholic, did not care to use force against the prevalent

[005-400]faith; direct persecution would have weakened the national defence and

[005-401]scandalized Europe. They naïvely hoped that the superstition would

[005-402]disappear by degrees. Robespierre declared against the policy of

[005-403]unchristianizing France, and when he had the power (April, 1795), he

[005-404]established as a State religion the worship of the Supreme Being. “The

[005-405]French people recognizes the existence of the Supreme Being and the

[005-406]immortality of the Soul”; the liberty of other cults was maintained.

[005-407]Thus, for a few months, Rousseau’s idea was more or less realized. It

[005-408]meant intolerance. Atheism was regarded as a vice, and “all were

[005-409]atheists who did not think like Robespierre.”

[005-410][114]

[005-411]The democratic was succeeded by the middle-class Republic (1795–9), and

[005-412]the policy of its government was to hinder the preponderance of any one

[005-413]religious group; to hold the balance among all the creeds, but with a

[005-414]certain partiality against the strongest, the Catholic, which

[005-415]threatened, as was thought, to destroy the others or even the Republic.

[005-416]The plan was to favour the growth of new rationalistic cults, and to

[005-417]undermine revealed religion by a secular system of education.

[005-418]Accordingly the Church was separated from the State by the Constitution

[005-419]of 1795, which affirmed the liberty of all worship and withdrew from the

[005-420]Catholic clergy the salaries which the State had hitherto paid. The

[005-421]elementary schools were laicized. The Declaration of Rights, the

[005-422]articles of the Constitution, and republican morality were taught

[005-423]instead of religion. An enthusiast declared that “the religion of

[005-424]Socrates, Marcus Aurelius, and Cicero would soon be the religion of the

[005-425]world.”

[005-426]A new rationalistic religion was introduced under the name of

[005-427]Theophilanthropy. It was the “natural religion” of the philosophers and

[005-428]poets of the century, of Voltaire and the English deists—not the

[005-429]purified Christianity of Rousseau, but anterior and superior to

[005-430]Christianity. Its doctrines, briefly formulated,

[005-431][115] were: God, immortality, fraternity, humanity; no attacks on other

[005-432]religions, but respect and honour towards all; gatherings in a family,

[005-433]or in a temple, to encourage one another to practise morality. Protected

[005-434]by the government sometimes secretly, sometimes openly, it had a certain

[005-435]success among the cultivated classes.

[005-436]The idea of the lay State was popularized under this rule, and by the

[005-437]end of the century there was virtually religious peace in France. Under

[005-438]the Consulate (from 1799) the same system continued, but Napoleon ceased

[005-439]to protect Theophilanthropy. In 1801, though there seems to have been

[005-440]little discontent with the existing arrangement, Napoleon decided to

[005-441]upset it and bring the Pope upon the scene. The Catholic religion, as

[005-442]that of the majority, was again taken under the special protection of

[005-443]the State, the salaries of the clergy again paid by the nation, and the

[005-444]Papal authority over the Church again recognized within well-defined

[005-445]limits; while full toleration of other religions was maintained. This

[005-446]was the effect of the Concordat between the French Republic and the

[005-447]Pope. It is the judgment of a high authority that the nation, if it had

[005-448]been consulted, would have pronounced against the change. It may be

[005-449]doubted whether this is true. But Napoleon’s policy

[005-450][116] seems to have been prompted by the calculation that, using the

[005-451]Pope as an instrument, he could control the consciences of men, and more

[005-452]easily carry out his plans of empire.

[005-453]Apart from its ecclesiastical policies and its experiments in new creeds

[005-454]based on the principles of rationalistic thinkers, the French Revolution

[005-455]itself has an interest, in connexion with our subject, as an example of

[005-456]the coercion of reason by an intolerant faith.

[005-457]The leaders believed that, by applying certain principles, they could

[005-458]regenerate France and show the world how the lasting happiness of

[005-459]mankind can be secured. They acted in the name of reason, but their

[005-460]principles were articles of faith, which were accepted just as blindly

[005-461]and irrationally as the dogmas of any supernatural creed. One of these

[005-462]dogmas was the false doctrine of Rousseau that man is a being who is

[005-463]naturally good and loves justice and order. Another was the illusion

[005-464]that all men are equal by nature. The puerile conviction prevailed that

[005-465]legislation could completely blot out the past and radically transform

[005-466]the character of a society. “Liberty, equality, and fraternity” was as

[005-467]much a creed as the Creed of the Apostles; it hypnotized men’s minds

[005-468]like a revelation from on high; and reason had as little part in its

[005-469]propagation as in the spread

[005-470][117] of Christianity or of Protestantism. It meant anything but

[005-471]equality, fraternity, or liberty, especially liberty, when it was

[005-472]translated into action by the fanatical apostles of “Reason,” who were

[005-473]blind to the facts of human nature and defied the facts of econnomics.

[005-474]Terror, the usual instrument in propagating religions, was never more

[005-475]mercilessly applied. Any one who questioned the doctrines was a heretic

[005-476]and deserved a heretic’s fate. And, as in most religious movements, the

[005-477]milder and less unreasonable spirits succumbed to the fanatics. Never

[005-478]was the name of reason more grievously abused than by those who believed

[005-479]they were inaugurating her reign.

[005-480]Religious liberty, however, among other good things, did emerge from the

[005-481]Revolution, at first in the form of Separation, and then under the

[005-482]Concordat. The Concordat lasted for more than a century, under

[005-483]monarchies and republics, till it was abolished in December, 1905, when

[005-484]the system of Separation was introduced again.

[005-485]In the German States the history of religious liberty differs in many

[005-486]ways, but it resembles the development in France in so far as toleration

[005-487]in a limited form was at first brought about by war. The Thirty Years’

[005-488]War, which divided Germany in the first half

[005-489][118] of the seventeenth century, and in which, as in the English Civil

[005-490]War, religion and politics were mixed, was terminated by the Peace of

[005-491]Westphalia (1648). By this act, three religions, the Catholic, the

[005-492]Lutheran, and the Reformed [4] were legally recognized by the Holy Roman

[005-493]Empire, and placed on an equality; all other religious were excluded.

[005-494]But it was left to each of the German States, of which the Empire

[005-495]consisted, to tolerate or not any religion it pleased. That is, every

[005-496]prince could impose on his subjects whichever of the three religions he

[005-497]chose, and refuse to tolerate the others in his territory. But he might

[005-498]also admit one or both of the others, and he might allow the followers

[005-499]of other creeds to reside in his dominion, and practise their religion

[005-500]within the precincts of their own houses. Thus toleration varied, from

[005-501]State to State, according to the policy of each particular prince.

[005-502]As elsewhere, so in Germany, considerations of political expediency

[005-503]promoted the growth of toleration, especially in Prussia; and as

[005-504]elsewhere, theoretical advocates exercised great influence on public

[005-505]opinion. But the case for toleration was based by its German defenders

[005-506]chiefly on legal, not, as in

[005-507][119] England and France, on moral and intellectual grounds. They

[005-508]regarded it as a question of law, and discussed it from the point of

[005-509]view of the legal relations between State and Church. It had been

[005-510]considered long ago from this standpoint by an original Italian thinker,

[005-511]Marsilius of Padua (thirteenth century), who had maintained that the

[005-512]Church had no power to employ physical coercion, and that if the lay

[005-513]authority punished heretics, the punishment was inflicted for the

[005-514]violation not of divine ordinances but of the law of the State, which

[005-515]excluded heretics from its territory.

[005-516]Christian Thomasius may be taken as a leading exponent of the theory

[005-517]that religious liberty logically follows from a right conception of law.

[005-518]He laid down in a series of pamphlets (1693–1697) that the prince, who

[005-519]alone has the power of coercion, has no right to interfere in spiritual

[005-520]matters, while the clergy step beyond their province if they interfere

[005-521]in secular matters or defend their faith by any other means than

[005-522]teaching. But the secular power has no legal right to coerce heretics

[005-523]unless heresy is a crime. And heresy is not a crime, but an error; for

[005-524]it is not a matter of will. Thomasius, moreover, urges the view that the

[005-525]public welfare has nothing to gain from unity of faith, that it makes no

[005-526][120] difference what faith a man professes so long as he is loyal to

[005-527]the State. His toleration indeed is not complete. He was much influenced

[005-528]by the writings of his contemporary Locke, and he excepts from the

[005-529]benefit of toleration the same classes which Locke excepted.

[005-530]Besides the influence of the jurists, we may note that the Pietistic

[005-531]movement—a reaction of religious enthusiasm against the formal theology

[005-532]of the Lutheran divines—was animated by a spirit favourable to

[005-533]toleration; and that the cause was promoted by the leading men of

[005-534]letters, especially by Lessing, in the second half of the eighteenth

[005-535]century.

[005-536]But perhaps the most important fact of all in hastening the realization

[005-537]of religious liberty in Germany was the accession of a rationalist to

[005-538]the throne of Prussia, in the person of Frederick the Great. A few

[005-539]months after his accession (1740) he wrote in the margin of a State

[005-540]paper, in which a question of religious policy occurred, that every one

[005-541]should be allowed to get to heaven in his own way. His view that

[005-542]morality was independent of religion and therefore compatible with all

[005-543]religions, and that thus a man could be a good citizen—the only thing

[005-544]which the State was entitled to demand—whatever faith he might profess,

[005-545]led to the logical consequence of complete religious liberty. Catholics

[005-546][121] were placed on an equality with Protestants, and the Treaty of

[005-547]Westphalia was violated by the extension of full toleration to all the

[005-548]forbidden sects. Frederick even conceived the idea of introducing

[005-549]Mohammedan settlers into some parts of his realm. Contrast England under

[005-550]George III, France under Louis XV, Italy under the shadow of the Popes.

[005-551]It is an important fact in history, which has hardly been duly

[005-552]emphasized, that full religious liberty was for the first time, in any

[005-553]country in modern Europe, realized under a free-thinking ruler, the

[005-554]friend of the great “blasphemer” Voltaire.

[005-555]The policy and principles of Frederick were formulated in the Prussian

[005-556]Territorial Code of 1794, by which unrestricted liberty of conscience

[005-557]was guaranteed, and the three chief religions, the Lutheran, the

[005-558]Reformed, and the Catholic, were placed on the same footing and enjoyed

[005-559]the same privileges. The system is “jurisdictional”; only, three

[005-560]Churches here occupy the position which the Anglican Church alone

[005-561]occupies in England. The rest of Germany did not begin to move in the

[005-562]direction pointed out by Prussia until, by one of the last acts of the

[005-563]Holy Roman Empire (1803), the Westphalian settlement had been modified.

[005-564]Before the foundation of the new Empire (1870), freedom was established

[005-565]throughout Germany.

[005-566][122]

[005-567]In Austria, the Emperor Joseph II issued an Edict of Toleration in 1781,

[005-568]which may be considered a broad measure for a Catholic State at that

[005-569]time. Joseph was a sincere Catholic, but he was not impervious to the

[005-570]enlightened ideas of his age; he was an admirer of Frederick, and his

[005-571]edict was prompted by a genuinely tolerant spirit, such as had not

[005-572]inspired the English Act of 1689. It extended only to the Lutheran and

[005-573]Reformed sects and the communities of the Greek Church which had entered

[005-574]into union with Rome, and it was of a limited kind. Religious liberty

[005-575]was not established till 1867.

[005-576]The measure of Joseph applied to the Austrian States in Italy, and

[005-577]helped to prepare that country for the idea of religious freedom. It is

[005-578]notable that in Italy in the eighteenth century toleration found its

[005-579]advocate, not in a rationalist or a philosopher, but in a Catholic

[005-580]ecclesiastic, Tamburinni, who (under the name of his friend

[005-581]Trautmansdorf) published a work On Ecclesiastical and Civil Toleration

[005-582](1783). A sharp line is drawn between the provinces of the Church and

[005-583]the State, persecution and the Inquisition are condemned, coercion of

[005-584]conscience is declared inconsistent with the Christian spirit, and the

[005-585]principle is laid down that the sovran should only exercise coercion

[005-586]where

[005-587][123] the interests of public safety are concerned. Like Locke, the

[005-588]author thinks that atheism is a legitimate case for such coercion.

[005-589]The new States which Napoleon set up in Italy exhibited toleration in

[005-590]various degrees, but real liberty was first introduced in Piedmont by

[005-591]Cavour (1848), a measure which prepared the way for the full liberty

[005-592]which was one of the first-fruits of the foundation of the Italian

[005-593]kingdom in 1870. The union of Italy, with all that it meant, is the most

[005-594]signal and dramatic act in the triumph of the ideas of the modern State

[005-595]over the traditional principles of the Christian Church. Rome, which

[005-596]preserved those principles most faithfully, has offered a steadfast, we

[005-597]may say a heroic, resistance to the liberal ideas which swept Europe in

[005-598]the nineteenth century. The guides of her policy grasped thoroughly the

[005-599]danger which liberal thought meant for an institution which, founded in

[005-600]a remote past, claimed to be unchangeable and never out of date. Gregory

[005-601]XVI issued a solemn protest maintaining authority against freedom, the

[005-602]mediaeval against the modern ideal, in an Encyclical Letter (1832),

[005-603]which was intended as a rebuke to some young French Catholics (Lamennais

[005-604]and his friends) who had conceived the promising idea of transforming

[005-605]the Church by the Liberal spirit

[005-606][124] of the day. The Pope denounces “the absurd and erroneous maxim, or

[005-607]rather insanity, that liberty of conscience should be procured and

[005-608]guaranteed to every one. The path to this pernicious error is prepared

[005-609]by that full and unlimited liberty of thought which is spread abroad to

[005-610]the misfortune of Church and State and which certain persons, with

[005-611]excessive impudence, venture to represent as an advantage for religion.

[005-612]Hence comes the corruption of youth, contempt for religion and for the

[005-613]most venerable laws, and a general mental change in the world—in short

[005-614]the most deadly scourge of society; since the experience of history has

[005-615]shown that the States which have shone by their wealth and power and

[005-616]glory have perished just by this evil— immoderate freedom of opinion,

[005-617]licence of conversation, and love of novelties. With this is connected

[005-618]the liberty of publishing any writing of any kind. This is a deadly and

[005-619]execrable liberty for which we cannot feel sufficient horror, though

[005-620]some men dare to acclaim it noisily and enthusiastically.” A generation

[005-621]later Pius IX was to astonish the world by a similar manifesto—his

[005-622]Syllabus of Modern Errors (1864). Yet, notwithstanding the fundamental

[005-623]antagonism between the principles of the Church and the drift of modern

[005-624]civilization, the Papacy survives,

[005-625][125] powerful and respected, in a world where the ideas which it

[005-626]condemned have become the commonplace conditions of life.

[005-627]The progress of Western nations from the system of unity which prevailed

[005-628]in the fifteenth, to the system of liberty which was the rule in the

[005-629]nineteenth century, was slow and painful, illogical and wavering,

[005-630]generally dictated by political necessities, seldom inspired by

[005-631]deliberate conviction. We have seen how religious liberty has been

[005-632]realized, so far as the law is concerned, under two distinct systems,

[005-633]“Jurisdiction” and “Separation.” But legal toleration may coexist with

[005-634]much practical intolerance, and liberty before the law is compatible

[005-635]with serious disabilities of which the law cannot take account. For

[005-636]instance, the expression of unorthodox opinions may exclude a man from

[005-637]obtaining a secular post or hinder his advancement. The question has

[005-638]been asked, which of the two systems is more favourable to the creation

[005-639]of a tolerant social atmosphere? Ruffini (of whose excellent work on

[005-640]Religious Liberty I have made much use in this chapter) decides in

[005-641]favour of Jurisdiction. He points out that while Socinus, a true friend

[005-642]of liberty of thought, contemplated this system, the Anabaptists, whose

[005-643]spirit was intolerant, sought Separation. More important

[005-644][126] is the observation that in Germany, England, and Italy, where the

[005-645]most powerful Church or Churches are under the control of the State,

[005-646]there is more freedom, more tolerance of opinion, than in many of the

[005-647]American States where Separation prevails. A hundred years ago the

[005-648]Americans showed appalling ingratitude to Thomas Paine, who had done

[005-649]them eminent service in the War of Independence, simply because he

[005-650]published a very unorthodox book. It is notorious that free thought is

[005-651]still a serious hindrance and handicap to an American, even in most of

[005-652]the Universities. This proves that Separation is not an infallible

[005-653]receipt for producing tolerance. But I see no reason to suppose that

[005-654]public opinion in America would be different, if either the Federal

[005-655]Republic or the particular States had adopted Jurisdiction. Given legal

[005-656]liberty under either system, I should say that the tolerance of public

[005-657]opinion depends on social conditions and especially on the degree of

[005-658]culture among the educated classes.

[005-659]From this sketch it will be seen that toleration was the outcome of new

[005-660]political circumstances and necessities, brought about by the disunion

[005-661]of the Church through the Reformation. But it meant that in those States

[005-662]which granted toleration the opinion of

[005-663][127] a sufficiently influential group of the governing class was ripe

[005-664]for the change, and this new mental attitude was in a great measure due

[005-665]to the scepticism and rationalism which were diffused by the Renaissance

[005-666]movement, and which subtly and unconsciously had affected the minds of

[005-667]many who were sincerely devoted to rigidly orthodox beliefs; so

[005-668]effective is the force of suggestion. In the next two chapters the

[005-669]advance of reason at the expense of faith will be traced through the

[005-670]seventeenth, eighteenth, and nineteenth centuries.
