## Life and Background

Sebastian Castellio was born in Savoy in 1515. A scholar of Latin, Greek, and Hebrew, he was drawn to the [[reformation]] and initially worked alongside [[john-calvin]] in Geneva. However, their relationship soured over theological differences, and Castellio left Geneva to become a professor of Greek at the University of Basel, where he spent the remainder of his life.

## Major Contributions

Castellio's most important contribution to freedom of thought was his vigorous protest against the execution of [[michael-servetus]] by Calvin in 1553. His work *De Haereticis* (*Concerning Heretics*, 1554) argued that persecution for religious belief was contrary to Christian teaching and that only God could judge matters of conscience. He emerged from the spirit of the Socinian movement (005-038) and became one of the earliest and most eloquent defenders of religious toleration in the Reformation era.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

> It was the spirit of the Socinian spirit that Castellion of Savoy uttered his eloquent protest against the burning of Servetus.
> (005-038)

Castellio advanced the principle that religious error should not be punished by civil authority. He argued that to persecute a man for his beliefs is to act against the example of Christ. His defence of liberty of conscience made him a forerunner of the modern doctrine of freedom of thought and expression, and his arguments would be taken up by later advocates of toleration throughout Europe.

## Legacy and Influence

Castellio's courageous stand against Calvin placed him among the earliest champions of religious toleration in modern Europe. Though his immediate impact was limited by the dominance of orthodox Protestantism, his ideas influenced the [[socinus]] tradition and contributed to the long-term development of arguments for freedom of conscience that would eventually triumph in the [[the-enlightenment]].
