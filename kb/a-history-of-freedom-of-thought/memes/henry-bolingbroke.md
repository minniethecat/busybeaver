## Life and Background

Henry St John, 1st Viscount Bolingbroke, was born in 1678 into an aristocratic English family. He had a distinguished political career, serving as Secretary at War and later as Secretary of State under Queen Anne. After the Hanoverian succession, he was attainted for treason, and he lived long as an exile in France, where he became acquainted with leading French intellectuals including [[voltaire]] (006-489).

## Major Contributions

Bolingbroke was profoundly influenced by English thinkers, especially [[locke|John Locke]], and developed a rationalist, deistic philosophy (006-487). He wrote extensively on religion, philosophy, and politics, arguing that natural religion — belief in a Creator discoverable by reason alone — was sufficient for morality and that revealed religion was corrupted by priestcraft and superstition.

This statesman had concealed his infidelity during his lifetime except from his intimates; his rationalistic essays were published posthumously in 1754, three years after his death (006-488, 006-490).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Bolingbroke's posthumous writings became an important conduit through which English deistic ideas flowed into the French Enlightenment. Voltaire, whose literary genius converted the work of the English thinkers into a world-force, was profoundly shaped by Bolingbroke's thought during his exile in England (006-490). Bolingbroke's method of concealing heterodox opinions during his lifetime while preparing their posthumous dissemination became a model for other Enlightenment figures who faced the risk of persecution.

## Legacy and Influence

Through Voltaire, Bolingbroke's deistic rationalism spread across Europe, contributing to the intellectual foundations of the French Revolution and the broader secularisation of Western thought. His insistence that morality could be grounded in natural reason rather than revelation helped shape the agenda of the radical Enlightenment. While less remembered today than [[locke]] or [[david-hume]], Bolingbroke was a crucial bridge figure between English and Continental freethought.
