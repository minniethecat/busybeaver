# Superstition

**Superstition** — the belief in supernatural causation not grounded in reason or evidence — figures in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought* as a persistent adversary of rational inquiry. Bury traces how the critique of superstition evolved from ancient skepticism through the polemics of the Enlightenment, positioning the rejection of superstitious beliefs as essential to the liberation of human reason.

## Definition and Origins

Superstition, from the Latin *superstitio* (excessive fear of the gods), denotes beliefs and practices that attribute events to supernatural forces in ways that bypass rational explanation. In Bury's account, superstition is not merely an individual cognitive error but a social force that "strengthens" existing social structures and resists reform (001-051).

Bury distinguishes superstition from religion proper, noting that the early Christians themselves were accused of atheism by pagan contemporaries because they "rejected all or some of the superstitions which they retained" (004-204). This distinction between religion and superstition became central to later rationalist critiques.

## Historical Development

The ancient world was saturated with superstitious belief. Greek and Roman paganism, with its pantheon of gods intervening in human affairs, provided fertile ground for superstitious practices. The early Christian church, while condemning pagan superstition, introduced its own forms — belief in miracles, relics, and demonic intervention.

During the Middle Ages, superstition was deeply embedded in popular religion. Bury observes that superstition "belongs to the age which saw" particular forms of religious practice flourish (006-056), and that even the Reformation did not eliminate superstitious belief but rather reshaped it.

The Enlightenment marked a turning point. Thinkers of the eighteenth century waged systematic "warfare against superstitions" (006-486), deploying reason, science, and satire to discredit beliefs they regarded as irrational and socially harmful.

## Key Proponents

The critique of superstition attracted thinkers across centuries. [[Spinoza]] laid philosophical groundwork in the seventeenth century by applying rational analysis to biblical texts and identifying superstition with fear and ignorance. [[Voltaire]] made the campaign against superstition — *Écrasez l'infâme* — a central mission of his career, targeting miracles, ecclesiastical authority, and popular credulity.

> [006-056] this superstition belongs to the age which saw …

[[Hume]] contributed the most philosophically rigorous attack in his essay "Of Miracles," arguing that it is always more probable that a miracle report is false than that a law of nature has been violated. [[Locke]] provided epistemological foundations by delimiting the scope of human understanding.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Superstition is, in Bury's framework, antithetical to freedom of thought because it substitutes fear and authority for reason and evidence. The rationalist campaign against superstition was therefore inseparable from the broader struggle for intellectual liberty.

> [006-074] …invisible power differently, superstition.

The decline of superstition in educated circles during the nineteenth century was both a cause and a consequence of the advance of freedom of thought. As scientific explanations replaced supernatural ones, the intellectual space for free inquiry expanded proportionally.

## Criticism and Opposition

Defenders of traditional religion often pushed back against the rationalist critique of superstition by arguing that popular religious belief, even if not philosophically rigorous, served valuable social functions. Some contended that the masses required "superstition for the benefit of the masses" (002-339) — a paternalistic argument that Enlightenment thinkers like Voltaire rejected as perpetuating ignorance.
