# Progress

The **idea of progress** — that human history, taken as a whole, exhibits a cumulative improvement in knowledge, institutions, and human welfare — is one of the central themes of J.B. Bury's narrative. Bury treats the emergence of this conviction as inseparable from the growth of [[rationalism]] and the advance of [[freedom of thought]]: only when thinkers began to subject history to rational analysis, rather than viewing it as the unfolding of a divine plan or a cycle of rise and decline, could the concept of secular progress take hold.

> [006-001] …in the last three hundred years reason has been slowly but steadily destroying the authority of tradition and the tyranny of the past.

## Before the Idea of Progress

The ancient world did not possess the idea of progress as the modern era understands it. [[Plato]] and [[Aristotle]] conceived of history as cyclical — states rose, decayed, and fell in recurring patterns. The [[Stoics]] believed in a periodic conflagration that destroyed and renewed the cosmos. For [[Lucretius]] and the [[Epicureans]], human civilization had advanced from a primitive state, but this was a story of a single ascent from savagery, not an open-ended trajectory.

[[Christianity]] replaced the cyclical view with a linear one — history moved from Creation through Fall and Redemption toward the Last Judgment — but this was a supernatural drama, not a story of human achievement. [[Augustine]]'s *City of God* divided history into six ages corresponding to the six days of Creation; the present age was the last, and no further earthly improvement was to be expected.

## The Enlightenment and Secular Progress

The idea of secular, cumulative progress emerged in the [[Enlightenment]] as the combined product of the [[Scientific Revolution]] and the growing confidence in human [[reason]]. If [[Newton]] could discover the laws governing the physical universe, why could not similar methods discover the laws of society and history?

> [001-280] …helped the emancipation of reason. All one can do, all one can hope for, is to contribute something to the slow progress of enlightenment.

Key milestones in the development of the idea included:

1. **[[Fontenelle]]'s *Digression on the Ancients and Moderns*** (1688): Refuted the superiority of antiquity by arguing that nature produces minds of equal capacity in every age, and that moderns possess greater accumulated knowledge.
2. **[[Voltaire]]'s historical writings**: Rejected providential history in favor of the history of civilization — of arts, sciences, manners, and laws — treating the [[Renaissance]], the age of [[Louis XIV]], and the [[Scientific Revolution]] as peaks of human achievement.
3. **[[Condorcet]]'s *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind*** (1795): Written while in hiding from the [[French Revolution]]'s Terror, this was the most systematic statement of the Enlightenment faith in progress — ten epochs of human history, from primitive society to the anticipated future triumph of reason and equality.

## The Nineteenth Century: Progress as Science

In the nineteenth century, the idea of progress was reinforced from multiple directions:

> [006-001] …in the last three hundred years reason has been slowly but steadily destroying…

- **[[Darwin]]'s theory of evolution** (1859): Though Darwin himself was cautious about drawing social conclusions, the concept of natural selection was widely interpreted as scientific confirmation that change over time produced improvement — a biological counterpart to historical progress.
- **[[Buckle]]'s *History of Civilization in England*** (1857–1861): Attempted to make history a science by discovering statistical laws governing the advance of civilization, treating intellectual progress as the primary driver.
- **[[John Stuart Mill]]'s *On Liberty*** (1859): Provided the political theory linking progress to free discussion — progress depends on the unfettered clash of ideas, and any society that suppresses dissent stagnates.
- **[[Herbert Spencer]]'s evolutionary sociology**: Extended the concept of progress from biology to all realms — society, ethics, government — treating evolution as a universal law of increasing complexity and adaptation.

## Progress and Freedom of Thought

Bury's central argument is that progress and [[freedom of thought]] are mutually dependent. Free inquiry produces new knowledge, which improves human life; the visible improvement of life then strengthens the case for free inquiry. Conversely, whenever authority succeeds in suppressing free thought — as in the [[Middle Ages]] — knowledge stagnates and progress halts:

> [003-003] …a millennium in which reason was enchained, thought was enslaved, and knowledge made no advance.

This symbiosis has an important corollary: the idea of progress, once accepted, becomes itself a force for further liberation. If history is understood as a cumulative advance, then resistance to change and reliance on past authority are not merely conservative but *regressive* — they attempt to reverse an irreversible current.

| Period | View of History | Attitude to Progress |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Classical Antiquity | Cyclical (Plato, Stoics) | No concept of secular progress |
| Medieval Christianity | Linear but supernaturally directed (Augustine) | Earthly progress irrelevant; salvation the goal |
| Renaissance | Revival of antiquity; optimism about human capacity | Nascent — recovery, not yet advance |
| Enlightenment | Secular, cumulative | Explicit doctrine — reason improves humanity |
| 19th Century | Scientific, evolutionary | Progress as natural law (Darwin, Spencer, Buckle) |
