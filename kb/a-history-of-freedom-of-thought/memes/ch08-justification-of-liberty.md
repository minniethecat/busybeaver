## Chapter VIII
### THE JUSTIFICATION OF LIBERTY OF THOUGHT

[008-001]MOST men who have been brought up in the free atmosphere of a modern

[008-002]State sympathize with liberty in its long struggle with authority and

[008-003]may find it difficult to see that anything can be said for the

[008-004]tyrannical, and as they think extraordinarily perverse, policy by which

[008-005]communities and governments persistently sought to stifle new ideas and

[008-006]suppress free speculation. The conflict sketched in these pages appears

[008-007]as a war between light and darkness. We exclaim that altar and throne

[008-008]formed a sinister conspiracy against the progress of humanity. We look

[008-009]back with horror at the things which so many champions of reason endured

[008-010]at the hands of blind, if not malignant, bearers of authority.

[008-011]But a more or less plausible case can be made out for coercion. Let us

[008-012]take the most limited view of the lawful powers of society over its

[008-013]individual members. Let us lay down, with Mill, that “the sole end for

[008-014]which mankind are warranted, individually and collectively, in

[008-015]interfering with the liberty of action of any of their members is self-

[008-016]protection,” and that coercion is only justified

[008-017][234] for the prevention of harm to others. This is the minimum claim

[008-018]the State can make, and it will be admitted that it is not only the

[008-019]right but the duty of the State to prevent harm to its members. That is

[008-020]what it is for. Now no abstract or independent principle is

[008-021]discoverable, why liberty of speech should be a privileged form of

[008-022]liberty of action, or why society should lay down its arms of defence

[008-023]and fold its hands, when it is persuaded that harm is threatened to it

[008-024]through the speech of any of its members. The Government has to judge of

[008-025]the danger, and its judgment may be wrong; but if it is convinced that

[008-026]harm is being done, is it not its plain duty to interfere?

[008-027]This argument supplies an apology for the suppression of free opinion by

[008-028]Governments in ancient and modern times. It can be urged for the

[008-029]Inquisition, for Censorship of the Press, for Blasphemy laws, for all

[008-030]coercive measures of the kind, that, if excessive or ill-judged, they

[008-031]were intended to protect society against what their authors sincerely

[008-032]believed to be grave injury, and were simple acts of duty. (This

[008-033]apology, of course, does not extend to acts done for the sake of the

[008-034]alleged good of the victims themselves, namely, to secure their future

[008-035]salvation.)

[008-036]Nowadays we condemn all such measures

[008-037][235] and disallow the right of the State to interfere with the free

[008-038]expression of opinion. So deeply is the doctrine of liberty seated in

[008-039]our minds that we find it difficult to make allowances for the coercive

[008-040]practices of our misguided ancestors. How is this doctrine justified? It

[008-041]rests on no abstract basis, on no principle independent of society

[008-042]itself, but entirely on considerations of utility.

[008-043]We saw how Socrates indicated the social value of freedom of discussion.

[008-044]We saw how Milton observed that such freedom was necessary for the

[008-045]advance of knowledge. But in the period during which the cause of

[008-046]toleration was fought for and practically won, the argument more

[008-047]generally used was the injustice of punishing a man for opinions which

[008-048]he honestly held and could not help holding, since conviction is not a

[008-049]matter of will; in other words, the argument that error is not a crime

[008-050]and that it is therefore unjust to punish it. This argument, however,

[008-051]does not prove the case for freedom of discussion. The advocate of

[008-052]coercion may reply: We admit that it is unjust to punish a man for

[008-053]private erroneous beliefs; but it is not unjust to forbid the

[008-054]propagation of such beliefs if we are convinced that they are harmful;

[008-055]it is not unjust to punish him, not for holding them, but for publishing

[008-056]them. The truth

[008-057][236] is that, in examining principles, the word just is misleading. All

[008-058]the virtues are based on experience, physiological or social, and

[008-059]justice is no exception. Just designates a class of rules or principles

[008-060]of which the social utility has been found by experience to be paramount

[008-061]and which are recognized to be so important as to override all

[008-062]considerations of immediate expediency. And social utility is the only

[008-063]test. It is futile, therefore, to say to a Government that it acts

[008-064]unjustly in coercing opinion, unless it is shown that freedom of opinion

[008-065]is a principle of such overmastering social utility as to render other

[008-066]considerations negligible. Socrates had a true instinct in taking the

[008-067]line that freedom is valuable to society.

[008-068]The reasoned justification of liberty of thought is due to J. S. Mill,

[008-069]who set it forth in his work On Liberty, published in 1859. This book

[008-070]treats of liberty in general, and attempts to fix the frontier of the

[008-071]region in which individual freedom should be considered absolute and

[008-072]unassailable. The second chapter considers liberty of thought and

[008-073]discussion, and if many may think that Mill unduly minimized the

[008-074]functions of society, underrating its claims as against the individual,

[008-075]few will deny the justice of the chief arguments or question the general

[008-076]soundness of his conclusions.

[008-077][237]

[008-078]Pointing out that no fixed standard was recognized for testing the

[008-079]propriety of the interference on the part of the community with its

[008-080]individual members, he finds the test in self-protection, that is, the

[008-081]prevention of harm to others. He bases the proposition not on abstract

[008-082]rights, but on “utility, in the largest sense, grounded on the permanent

[008-083]interests of man as a progressive being.” He then uses the following

[008-084]argument to show that to silence opinion and discussion is always

[008-085]contrary to those permanent interests. Those who would suppress an

[008-086]opinion (it is assumed that they are honest) deny its truth, but they

[008-087]are not infallible. They may be wrong, or right, or partly wrong and

[008-088]partly right. (1) If they are wrong and the opinion they would crush is

[008-089]true, they have robbed, or done their utmost to rob, mankind of a truth.

[008-090]They will say: But we were justified, for we exercised our judgment to

[008-091]the best of our ability, and are we to be told that because our judgment

[008-092]is fallible we are not to use it? We forbade the propagation of an

[008-093]opinion which we were sure was false and pernicious; this implies no

[008-094]greater claim to infallibility than any act done by public authority. If

[008-095]we are to act at all, we must assume our own opinion to be true. To this

[008-096]Mill acutely replies: “There is the greatest difference

[008-097][238] between assuming an opinion to be true, because with every

[008-098]opportunity for contesting it it has not been refuted, and assuming its

[008-099]truth for the purpose of not permitting its refutation. Complete liberty

[008-100]of contradicting and disproving our opinion is the very condition which

[008-101]justifies us in assuming its truth for purposes of action, and on no

[008-102]other terms can a being with human faculties have any rational assurance

[008-103]of being right.”

[008-104](2) If the received opinion which it is sought to protect against the

[008-105]intrusion of error is true, the suppression of discussion is still

[008-106]contrary to general utility. A received opinion may happen to be true

[008-107](it is very seldom entirely true); but a rational certainty that it is

[008-108]so can only be secured by the fact that it has been fully canvassed but

[008-109]has not been shaken.

[008-110]Commoner and more important is (3) the case where the conflicting

[008-111]doctrines share the truth between them. Here Mill has little difficulty

[008-112]in proving the utility of supplementing one-sided popular truths by

[008-113]other truths which popular opinion omits to consider. And he observes

[008-114]that if either of the opinions which share the truth has a claim not

[008-115]merely to be tolerated but to be encouraged, it is the one which happens

[008-116]to be held by the minority, since this is the one “which

[008-117][239] for the time being represents the neglected interests.” He takes

[008-118]the doctrines of Rousseau, which might conceivably have been suppressed

[008-119]as pernicious. To the self-complacent eighteenth century those doctrines

[008-120]came as “a salutary shock, dislocating the compact mass of one-sided

[008-121]opinion.” The current opinions were indeed nearer to the truth than

[008-122]Rousseau’s, they contained much less of error; “nevertheless there lay

[008-123]in Rousseau’s doctrine, and has floated down the stream of opinion along

[008-124]with it, a considerable amount of exactly those truths which the popular

[008-125]opinion wanted; and these are the deposit which we left behind when the

[008-126]flood subsided.”

[008-127]Such is the drift of Mill’s main argument. The present writer would

[008-128]prefer to state the justification of freedom of opinion in a somewhat

[008-129]different form, though in accordance with Mill’s reasoning. The progress

[008-130]of civilization, if it is partly conditioned by circumstances beyond

[008-131]man’s control, depends more, and in an increasing measure, on things

[008-132]which are within his own power. Prominent among these are the

[008-133]advancement of knowledge and the deliberate adaptation of his habits and

[008-134]institutions to new conditions. To advance knowledge and to correct

[008-135]errors, unrestricted freedom of discussion is required.

[008-136][240] History shows that knowledge grew when speculation was perfectly

[008-137]free in Greece, and that in modern times, since restrictions on inquiry

[008-138]have been entirely removed, it has advanced with a velocity which would

[008-139]seem diabolical to the slaves of the mediaeval Church. Then, it is

[008-140]obvious that in order to readjust social customs, institutions, and

[008-141]methods to new needs and circumstances, there must be unlimited freedom

[008-142]of canvassing and criticizing them, of expressing the most unpopular

[008-143]opinions, no matter how offensive to prevailing sentiment they may be.

[008-144]If the history of civilization has any lesson to teach it is this: there

[008-145]is one supreme condition of mental and moral progress which it is

[008-146]completely within the power of man himself to secure, and that is

[008-147]perfect liberty of thought and discussion. The establishment of this

[008-148]liberty may be considered the most valuable achievement of modern

[008-149]civilization, and as a condition of social progress it should be deemed

[008-150]fundamental. The considerations of permanent utility on which it rests

[008-151]must outweigh any calculations of present advantage which from time to

[008-152]time might be thought to demand its violation.

[008-153]It is evident that this whole argument depends on the assumption that

[008-154]the progress of the race, its intellectual and moral development,

[008-155][241] is a reality and is valuable. The argument will not appeal to any

[008-156]one who holds with Cardinal Newman that “our race’s progress and

[008-157]perfectibility is a dream, because revelation contradicts it”; and he

[008-158]may consistently subscribe to the same writer’s conviction that “it

[008-159]would be a gain to this country were it vastly more superstitious, more

[008-160]bigoted, more gloomy, more fierce in its religion, than at present it

[008-161]shows itself to be.”

[008-162]While Mill was writing his brilliant Essay, which every one should read,

[008-163]the English Government of the day (1858) instituted prosecutions for the

[008-164]circulation of the doctrine that it is lawful to put tyrants to death,

[008-165]on the ground that the doctrine is immoral. Fortunately the prosecutions

[008-166]were not persisted in. Mill refers to the matter, and maintains that

[008-167]such a doctrine as tyrannicide (and, let us add, anarchy) does not form

[008-168]any exception to the rule that “there ought to exist the fullest liberty

[008-169]of professing and discussing, as a matter of ethical conviction, any

[008-170]doctrine, however immoral it may be considered.”

[008-171]Exceptions, cases where the interference of the authorities is proper,

[008-172]are only apparent, for they really come under another rule. For

[008-173]instance, if there is a direct instigation

[008-174][242] to particular acts of violence, there may be a legitimate case for

[008-175]interference. But the incitement must be deliberate and direct. If I

[008-176]write a book condemning existing societies and defending a theory of

[008-177]anarchy, and a man who reads it presently commits an outrage, it may

[008-178]clearly be established that my book made the man an anarchist and

[008-179]induced him to commit the crime, but it would be illegitimate to punish

[008-180]me or suppress the book unless it contained a direct incitement to the

[008-181]specific crime which he committed.

[008-182]It is conceivable that difficult cases might arise where a government

[008-183]might be strongly tempted, and might be urged by public clamour, to

[008-184]violate the principle of liberty. Let us suppose a case, very

[008-185]improbable, but which will make the issue clear and definite. Imagine

[008-186]that a man of highly magnetic personality, endowed with a wonderful

[008-187]power of infecting others with his own ideas however irrational, in

[008-188]short a typical religious leader, is convinced that the world will come

[008-189]to an end in the course of a few months. He goes about the country

[008-190]preaching and distributing pamphlets; his words have an electrical

[008-191]effect; and the masses of the uneducated and half-educated are persuaded

[008-192]that they have indeed only a few weeks to prepare for the day of

[008-193]Judgment. Multitudes leave their

[008-194][243] occupations, abandon their work, in order to spend the short time

[008-195]that remains in prayer and listening to the exhortations of the prophet.

[008-196]The country is paralyzed by the gigantic strike; traffic and industries

[008-197]come to a standstill. The people have a perfect legal right to give up

[008-198]their work, and the prophet has a perfect legal right to propagate his

[008-199]opinion that the end of the world is at hand —an opinion which Jesus

[008-200]Christ and his followers in their day held quite as erroneously. It

[008-201]would be said that desperate ills have desperate remedies, and there

[008-202]would be a strong temptation to suppress the fanatic. But to arrest a

[008-203]man who is not breaking the law or exhorting any one to break it, or

[008-204]causing a breach of the peace, would be an act of glaring tyranny. Many

[008-205]will hold that the evil of setting back the clock of liberty would out-

[008-206]balance all the temporary evils, great as they might be, caused by the

[008-207]propagation of a delusion. It would be absurd to deny that liberty of

[008-208]speech may sometimes cause particular harm. Every good thing sometimes

[008-209]does harm. Government, for instance, which makes fatal mistakes; law,

[008-210]which so often bears hardly and inequitably in individual cases. And can

[008-211]the Christians urge any other plea for their religion when they are

[008-212]unpleasantly reminded that it has caused untold

[008-213][244] suffering by its principle of exclusive salvation?

[008-214]Once the principle of liberty of thought is accepted as a supreme

[008-215]condition of social progress, it passes from the sphere of ordinary

[008-216]expediency into the sphere of higher expediency which we call justice.

[008-217]In other words it becomes a right on which every man should be able to

[008-218]count. The fact that this right is ultimately based on utility does not

[008-219]justify a government in curtailing it, on the ground of utility, in

[008-220]particular cases.

[008-221]The recent rather alarming inflictions of penalties for blasphemy in

[008-222]England illustrate this point. It was commonly supposed that the

[008-223]Blasphemy laws (see above, p. 139), though unrepealed, were a dead

[008-224]letter. But since December, 1911, half a dozen persons have been

[008-225]imprisoned for this offence. In these cases Christian doctrines were

[008-226]attacked by poor and more or less uneducated persons in language which

[008-227]may be described as coarse and offensive. Some of the judges seem to

[008-228]have taken the line that it is not blasphemy to attack the fundamental

[008-229]doctrines provided “the decencies of controversy” are preserved, but

[008-230]that “indecent” attacks constitute blasphemy. This implies a new

[008-231]definition of legal blasphemy, and is entirely contrary to the intention

[008-232]of the laws. Sir

[008-233][245] J. F. Stephen pointed out that the decisions of judges from the

[008-234]time of Lord Hale (XVIIth century) to the trial of Foote (1883) laid

[008-235]down the same doctrine and based it on the same principle: the doctrine

[008-236]being that it is a crime either to deny the truth of the fundamental

[008-237]doctrines of the Christian religion or to hold them up to contempt or

[008-238]ridicule; and the principle being that Christianity is a part of the law

[008-239]of the land.

[008-240]The apology offered for such prosecutions is that their object is to

[008-241]protect religious sentiment from insult and ridicule. Sir J. F. Stephen

[008-242]observed: “If the law were really impartial and punished blasphemy only,

[008-243]because it offends the feelings of believers, it ought also to punish

[008-244]such preaching as offends the feelings of unbelievers. All the more

[008-245]earnest and enthusiastic forms of religion are extremely offensive to

[008-246]those who do not believe them.” If the law does not in any sense

[008-247]recognize the truth of Christian doctrine, it would have to apply the

[008-248]same rule to the Salvation Army. In fact the law “can be explained and

[008-249]justified only on what I regard as its true principle—the principle of

[008-250]persecution.” The opponents of Christianity may justly say: If

[008-251]Christianity is false, why is it to be attacked only in polite language?

[008-252]Its goodness depends on its truth. If you

[008-253][246] grant its falsehood, you cannot maintain that it deserves special

[008-254]protection. But the law imposes no restraint on the Christian, however

[008-255]offensive his teaching may be to those who do not agree with him;

[008-256]therefore it is not based on an impartial desire to prevent the use of

[008-257]language which causes offence; therefore it is based on the hypothesis

[008-258]that Christianity is true; and therefore its principle is persecution.

[008-259]Of course, the present administration of the common law in regard to

[008-260]blasphemy does not endanger the liberty of those unbelievers who have

[008-261]the capacity for contributing to progress. But it violates the supreme

[008-262]principle of liberty of opinion and discussion. It hinders uneducated

[008-263]people from saying in the only ways in which they know how to say it,

[008-264]what those who have been brought up differently say, with impunity, far

[008-265]more effectively and far more insidiously. Some of the men who have been

[008-266]imprisoned during the last two years, only uttered in language of

[008-267]deplorable taste views that are expressed more or less politely in books

[008-268]which are in the library of a bishop unless he is a very ignorant

[008-269]person, and against which the law, if it has any validity, ought to have

[008-270]been enforced. Thus the law, as now administered, simply penalizes bad

[008-271]taste and places disabilities

[008-272][247] upon uneducated freethinkers. If their words offend their audience

[008-273]so far as to cause a disturbance, they should be prosecuted for a breach

[008-274]of public order, [1] not because their words are blasphemous. A man who

[008-275]robs or injures a church, or even an episcopal palace, is not prosecuted

[008-276]for sacrilege, but for larceny or malicious damage or something of the

[008-277]kind.

[008-278]The abolition of penalties for blasphemy was proposed in the House of

[008-279]Commons (by Bradlaugh) in 1889 and rejected. The reform is urgently

[008-280]needed. It would “prevent the recurrence at irregular intervals of

[008-281]scandalous prosecutions which have never in any one instance benefited

[008-282]any one, least of all the cause which they were intended to serve, and

[008-283]which sometimes afford a channel for the gratification of private malice

[008-284]under the cloak of religion.” [2]

[008-285]The struggle of reason against authority has ended in what appears now

[008-286]to be a decisive and permanent victory for liberty. In the most

[008-287]civilized and progressive countries, freedom of discussion is recognized

[008-288]as a

[008-289][248] fundamental principle. In fact, we may say it is accepted as a

[008-290]test of enlightenment, and the man in the street is forward in

[008-291]acknowledging that countries like Russia and Spain, where opinion is

[008-292]more or less fettered, must on that account be considered less civilized

[008-293]than their neighbours. All intellectual people who count take it for

[008-294]granted that there is no subject in heaven or earth which ought not to

[008-295]be investigated without any deference or reference to theological

[008-296]assumptions. No man of science has any fear of publishing his

[008-297]researches, whatever consequences they may involve for current beliefs.

[008-298]Criticism of religious doctrines and of political and social

[008-299]institutions is free. Hopeful people may feel confident that the victory

[008-300]is permanent; that intellectual freedom is now assured to mankind as a

[008-301]possession for ever; that the future will see the collapse of those

[008-302]forces which still work against it and its gradual diffusion in the more

[008-303]backward parts of the earth. Yet history may suggest that this prospect

[008-304]is not assured. Can we be certain that there may not come a great set-

[008-305]back? For freedom of discussion and speculation was, as we saw, fully

[008-306]realized in the Greek and Roman world, and then an unforeseen force, in

[008-307]the shape of Christianity, came in and laid chains upon the human mind

[008-308]and

[008-309][249] suppressed freedom and imposed upon man a weary struggle to

[008-310]recover the freedom which he had lost. Is it not conceivable that

[008-311]something of the same kind may occur again? that some new force,

[008-312]emerging from the unknown, may surprise the world and cause a similar

[008-313]set-back?

[008-314]The possibility cannot be denied, but there are some considerations

[008-315]which render it improbable (apart from a catastrophe sweeping away

[008-316]European culture). There are certain radical differences between the

[008-317]intellectual situation now and in antiquity. The facts known to the

[008-318]Greeks about the nature of the physical universe were few. Much that was

[008-319]taught was not proved. Compare what they knew and what we know about

[008-320]astronomy and geography—to take the two branches in which (besides

[008-321]mathematics) they made most progress. When there were so few

[008-322]demonstrated facts to work upon, there was the widest room for

[008-323]speculation. Now to suppress a number of rival theories in favour of one

[008-324]is a very different thing from suppressing whole systems of established

[008-325]facts. If one school of astronomers holds that the earth goes round the

[008-326]sun, another that the sun goes round the earth, but neither is able to

[008-327]demonstrate its proposition, it is easy for an authority, which has

[008-328]coercive power,

[008-329][250] to suppress one of them successfully. But once it is agreed by all

[008-330]astronomers that the earth goes round the sun, it is a hopeless task for

[008-331]any authority to compel men to accept a false view. In short, because

[008-332]she is in possession of a vast mass of ascertained facts about the

[008-333]nature of the universe, reason holds a much stronger position now than

[008-334]at the time when Christian theology led her captive. All these facts are

[008-335]her fortifications. Again, it is difficult to see what can arrest the

[008-336]continuous progress of knowledge in the future. In ancient times this

[008-337]progress depended on a few; nowadays, many nations take part in the

[008-338]work. A general conviction of the importance of science prevails to-day,

[008-339]which did not prevail in Greece. And the circumstance that the advance

[008-340]of material civilization depends on science is perhaps a practical

[008-341]guarantee that scientific research will not come to an abrupt halt. In

[008-342]fact science is now a social institution, as much as religion.

[008-343]But if science seems pretty safe, it is always possible that in

[008-344]countries where the scientific spirit is held in honour, nevertheless,

[008-345]serious restrictions may be laid on speculations touching social,

[008-346]political, and religious questions. Russia has men of science inferior

[008-347]to none, and Russia has its notorious censorship. It

[008-348][251] is by no means inconceivable that in lands where opinion is now

[008-349]free coercion might be introduced. If a revolutionary social movement

[008-350]prevailed, led by men inspired by faith in formulas (like the men of the

[008-351]French Revolution) and resolved to impose their creed, experience shows

[008-352]that coercion would almost inevitably be resorted to. Nevertheless,

[008-353]while it would be silly to suppose that attempts may not be made in the

[008-354]future to put back the clock, liberty is in a far more favourable

[008-355]position now than under the Roman Empire. For at that time the social

[008-356]importance of freedom of opinion was not appreciated, whereas now, in

[008-357]consequence of the long conflict which was necessary in order to re-

[008-358]establish it, men consciously realize its value. Perhaps this conviction

[008-359]will be strong enough to resist all conspiracies against liberty.

[008-360]Meanwhile, nothing should be left undone to impress upon the young that

[008-361]freedom of thought is an axiom of human progress. It may be feared,

[008-362]however, that this is not likely to be done for a long time to come. For

[008-363]our methods of early education are founded on authority. It is true that

[008-364]children are sometimes exhorted to think for themselves. But the parent

[008-365]or instructor who gives this excellent advice is confident that the

[008-366]results of the child’s thinking for

[008-367][252] himself will agree with the opinions which his elders consider

[008-368]desirable. It is assumed that he will reason from principles which have

[008-369]already been instilled into him by authority. But if his thinking for

[008-370]himself takes the form of questioning these principles, whether moral or

[008-371]religious, his parents and teachers, unless they are very exceptional

[008-372]persons, will be extremely displeased, and will certainly discourage

[008-373]him. It is, of course, only singularly promising children whose freedom

[008-374]of thought will go so far. In this sense it might be said that “distrust

[008-375]thy father and mother” is the first commandment with promise. It should

[008-376]be a part of education to explain to children, as soon as they are old

[008-377]enough to understand, when it is reasonable, and when it is not, to

[008-378]accept what they are told, on authority.

[008-380]offence has actually been given, and the penalty does not exceed

[008-381]imprisonment for three days.

[008-383]Blasphemous Libel,” in the Fortnightly Review, March, 1884, pp. 289–318.
