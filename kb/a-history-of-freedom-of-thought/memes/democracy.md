## Definition and Origins

Democracy, in Bury's usage, denotes a system of government in which political authority derives from the people, either through direct participation or through elected representatives. The concept originated in ancient Greece, where Athens developed a "full-fledged democracy" in which political discussion was "perfectly free" (002-115). This connection between democratic governance and free expression is fundamental: a democracy cannot function without citizens who are free to think, debate, and criticize.

However, democracy's relationship with freedom of thought has not always been straightforward. Bury documents how the same Athenian democracy that fostered free discussion also executed Socrates for his philosophical inquiries, revealing a tension between popular sovereignty and intellectual liberty that would recur throughout history.

## Historical Development

Ancient Athenian democracy represented the first systematic experiment in popular self-government. Every citizen could participate in the assembly, serve on juries, and hold public office. This political equality created an environment in which public debate flourished and traditional beliefs could be openly questioned. Yet the Athenian democracy also demonstrated its limits: the majority could be swayed by demagogues, and unpopular individuals — most famously Socrates — could be condemned by popular vote (002-204).

The medieval period saw the eclipse of democratic institutions in Europe, though republican city-states like Venice and Florence preserved elements of representative governance. The classical texts that described Athenian democracy were preserved and studied, keeping the concept alive even as feudal monarchy prevailed.

The democratic tradition revived with the Enlightenment. Thinkers debated whether "unlimited democracy" was desirable or whether constitutional restraints were necessary to prevent majority tyranny (002-201). Some argued for representative democracy as a compromise between popular sovereignty and the need for educated governance (006-782). The American and French Revolutions transformed democracy from a philosophical ideal into a practical political project.

The French Revolution's experiment with democracy proved complex. Under the democratic constitution, certain protections for religious minorities were maintained — "doctrine and worship were not touched" — even as the Revolution pursued its anticlerical policies (005-393). The democratic republic was succeeded by more conservative forms, and then by Napoleon, demonstrating that democracy was not a self-sustaining system but required favorable conditions (005-411).

## Key Proponents

- **Pericles**, the Athenian statesman, articulated the democratic ideal in his Funeral Oration, celebrating Athens as a model of free citizenship where every man could participate in public affairs and express his opinions freely.

- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** developed the theory of the "general will" as the foundation of democratic legitimacy. His conception of popular sovereignty influenced both the French Revolution and later democratic theory, though critics charged that his vision left insufficient room for individual dissent.

- **John Stuart Mill** advocated representative democracy as the best form of government for promoting both good governance and the moral development of citizens. He argued that democratic participation educated the public and that representative institutions provided the framework within which freedom of thought could flourish (006-782).

- **Voltaire**, though not a democrat in the modern sense, contributed to democratic thought by championing civil liberties — freedom of speech, press, and religion — that are essential to democratic governance. His campaigns against arbitrary authority helped establish the principle that government must be accountable to the governed.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The relationship between democracy and freedom of thought is reciprocal. Freedom of thought is a prerequisite for genuine democracy: citizens cannot make informed political choices unless they have access to diverse ideas and the liberty to evaluate them critically. At the same time, democratic institutions tend to protect freedom of thought, since a government accountable to the people is less likely to suppress ideas that the people wish to consider.

Bury documents both the promise and the peril of this relationship. Athenian democracy demonstrated that popular rule could coexist with vibrant intellectual life, but also showed that majorities could suppress unpopular ideas. The constitution of Athens "had been more than once overthrown," showing the fragility of democratic institutions (002-204). Modern representative democracy, by incorporating constitutional protections for minority rights, attempted to resolve this tension.

The 19th century saw democracy increasingly linked to the cause of intellectual liberty. The Reform Acts in Britain extended the franchise while simultaneously expanding the public sphere in which ideas could circulate. By Bury's time, representative democracy had become the default political aspiration of liberal reformers, seen as the system most compatible with the free exchange of ideas.
