## Definition and Origins

Skepticism is the philosophical position that questions whether certain or absolute knowledge is possible, and that advocates suspending judgment in the absence of sufficient evidence. In its Pyrrhonian form, it recommends the suspension of all belief; in its Academic form, it holds that no knowledge is possible beyond probable opinion. Both forms have been powerful allies of freethought, undermining dogmatic claims to certainty.

The skeptical tradition traces its origins to ancient Greece, particularly to Pyrrho of Elis and the later Academic skeptics. In its classical form, skepticism served as a counterweight to dogmatic philosophical systems, insisting that all claims must be subjected to rigorous scrutiny.

## Historical Development

Skepticism was revived with great effect during the Renaissance. Michel de Montaigne, whose mind "had a natural turn for scepticism," (003-343) deployed skeptical arguments to challenge the religious persecutions of his time. His essays "made for rationalism" and skepticism simultaneously. (004-058)

The "theological results of Montaigne's scepticism" (004-061) were far-reaching. By demonstrating that human reason could not achieve certainty on theological questions, skepticism paradoxically argued for toleration: if no one can be certain of their religious beliefs, no one has the right to persecute others for theirs.

In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, skepticism merged with the broader rationalist and Enlightenment movements. Pierre Bayle applied skeptical reasoning to biblical interpretation and church history. David Hume developed the most rigorous skeptical philosophy of the modern era, questioning not only religious claims but the foundations of causality and induction. The skeptical and rationalist currents "which were diffused by the Renaissance" continued to shape intellectual life into the modern era. (005-665)

## Key Proponents

Montaigne stands as the pivotal figure in reviving classical skepticism for the modern world. His influence extended to Descartes, whose method of systematic doubt, though ultimately intended to establish certain knowledge, was itself a skeptical procedure. Pierre Bayle's *Historical and Critical Dictionary* became an arsenal of skeptical arguments. David Hume's *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* subjected the design argument for God's existence to devastating skeptical critique.

In the nineteenth century, skeptical philosophy informed the development of scientific naturalism and agnosticism, with T.H. Huxley coining the term "agnostic" to describe the skeptical position on religious knowledge.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Skepticism has been one of the most powerful tools in the freethinker's arsenal. By demonstrating that claims to religious or ideological certainty cannot withstand rational scrutiny, skepticism undermines the intellectual justification for persecution and censorship. If no person or institution possesses infallible knowledge, then no person or institution has the right to impose beliefs on others by force.

This skeptical argument for toleration was central to the Enlightenment's case for freedom of thought and expression. It remains relevant today as a defense against new forms of dogmatism and intellectual authoritarianism.
