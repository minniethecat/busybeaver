## Life and Background

Titus Lucretius Carus was a Roman poet and philosopher who lived in the first century BCE (002-305). Little is known about his life beyond his monumental poem. He lived during the late Roman Republic, a period when educated Romans were increasingly sceptical in religious matters (002-320). He was a devoted follower of the Greek philosopher [[epicurus]], whom he regarded as the great deliverer of the human race (002-305).

## Major Contributions

Lucretius's sole surviving work is *De Rerum Natura* (*On the Nature of the World*), a didactic poem in six books that expounds Epicurean philosophy in Latin verse (002-304). The poem covers atomic theory, the nature of the soul, the workings of the cosmos, and the rejection of divine intervention in human affairs. With the fervour of a religious enthusiast, Lucretius denounces religion, sounding every note of defiance, loathing, and contempt (002-308). He explains scientific arguments as if they were the radiant revelation of a new world (002-312).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Lucretius occupies an eminent place in the literature of free thought by the sincerity of his audacious, defiant spirit (002-316). He rides forth as a leader of the hosts of atheism against the walls of heaven (002-311). He brands in burning words the crimes to which religion had urged humanity (002-310). Though the Greek thinkers had done all the foundational intellectual work, Lucretius's poem stands as a hymn of triumph over prostrate deities (002-315).

## Legacy and Influence

> With all the fervour of a religious enthusiast he denounces religion, sounding every note of defiance, loathing, and contempt, and branding in burning words the crimes to which it had urged man on.
> (002-308)

Lucretius preserved and transmitted Epicurean ideas to later ages. His poem survived the ancient world and influenced Renaissance and Enlightenment thinkers who sought to challenge religious orthodoxy. The materialist philosophy he championed would later find resonance in the works of thinkers like [[baron-d-holbach]] and the broader [[freethought]] movement.
