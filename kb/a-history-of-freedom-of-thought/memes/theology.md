## Definition and Origins

Theology, in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to the systematic study and formulation of religious doctrine—particularly Christian doctrine—by trained specialists. Bury treats theology as a distinct domain of intellectual activity that, historically, claimed authority over all other fields of knowledge. The conflict between freedom of thought and theological authority is a central theme running through the entire work.

It was in the field of theology that the question of intellectual liberty was first sharply raised, because it was in that field that the orthodox insisted most strenuously on the obligation to believe (001-227). Theology thus became the primary battleground for freedom of thought.

## Historical Development

In the Middle Ages, theology was regarded as the queen of the sciences, and all other disciplines were subordinate to its authority. The mediaeval principle held that reason must be subordinate to faith, and that certain propositions must be accepted as truths in the sphere of theology regardless of rational objections (006-137).

An attack on theology was, in effect, an attack on the veracity of the Church's claims to possess revealed truth (002-043). This made theological criticism among the most dangerous forms of intellectual dissent. Religion and theology were, for centuries, kept in a separate compartment, protected from the scrutiny applied to other domains of knowledge (004-036).

The Enlightenment brought a sustained challenge to theological authority. Thinkers argued that religion must be placed within the domain of reason rather than protected from it (004-049). The rationalists, who attacked theology, generally feigned to accept the core truths of Christianity while systematically undermining its intellectual foundations (006-130).

In Scotland, where theology was very powerful, resistance to Enlightenment ideas was particularly strong. Bury records that a woman was prosecuted for expressing skepticism about theological doctrine, illustrating the social and legal force that theology could still command (006-054).

Bury describes Thomas Paine as an enthusiastic adversary of revealed religion and an involuntary arch-enemy of theology (007-0008). Paine's *The Age of Reason* subjected Christian theology to a withering rational critique, arguing that natural theology—knowledge of God derived from nature—was more complete than that of revealed religion (006-779, 006-922).

## Key Proponents / Critics

- **Thomas Aquinas** systematized Christian theology in the thirteenth century, synthesizing Aristotelian philosophy with Church doctrine into a comprehensive theological framework.
- **Spinoza** subjected the Bible to rational criticism and proposed a pantheistic alternative to traditional theology, for which he was excommunicated and widely condemned.
- **Thomas Paine** launched one of the most influential popular attacks on Christian theology in *The Age of Reason*, arguing that revelation was unnecessary and that nature itself provided sufficient knowledge of God.
- **The Deists** collectively argued that theology had corrupted the simple truths of natural religion by adding layers of incomprehensible doctrine.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Theology represented the principal institutional obstacle to freedom of thought throughout most of Western history. Because theology claimed jurisdiction over ultimate questions—God, the soul, morality, the afterlife—its authority constrained inquiry across the entire range of human knowledge. The demand that reason submit to theological authority was incompatible with the principle of free inquiry.

The gradual liberation of philosophy, science, and ethics from theological control was, in Bury's account, the central achievement of the freethought tradition. Each field that successfully asserted its independence from theology expanded the domain of free thought. The argument that natural theology—knowledge derived from reason and observation—was sufficient without revelation represented a critical step toward intellectual autonomy.

Bury's narrative suggests that theology's loss of intellectual authority was not the result of any single decisive argument, but of the cumulative effect of rational criticism over centuries. The conviction that reason, rather than theological authority, should be the final arbiter of truth became the defining principle of modern intellectual life.
