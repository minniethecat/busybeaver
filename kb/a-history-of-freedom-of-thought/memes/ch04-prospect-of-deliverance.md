## Chapter IV
### PROSPECT OF DELIVERANCE
(THE RENAISSANCE AND THE REFORMATION)

[004-001]THE intellectual and social movement which was to dispel the darkness of

[004-002]the

[004-003][72] Middle Ages and prepare the way for those who would ultimately

[004-004]deliver reason from her prison, began in Italy in the thirteenth

[004-005]century. The misty veil woven of credulity and infantile naïveté which

[004-006]had hung over men’s souls and protected them from understanding either

[004-007]themselves or their relation to the world began to lift. The individual

[004-008]began to feel his separate individuality, to be conscious of his own

[004-009]value as a person apart from his race or country (as in the later ages

[004-010]of Greece and Rome); and the world around him began to emerge from the

[004-011]mists of mediaeval dreams. The change was due to the political and

[004-012]social conditions of the little Italian States, of which some were

[004-013]republics and others governed by tyrants.

[004-014]To the human world, thus unveiling itself, the individual who sought to

[004-015]make it serve his purposes required a guide; and the guide was found in

[004-016]the ancient literature of Greece and Rome. Hence the whole

[004-017]transformation, which presently extended from Italy to Northern Europe,

[004-018]is known as the Renaissance, or rebirth of classical antiquity. But the

[004-019]awakened interest in classical literature while it coloured the

[004-020]character and stimulated the growth of the movement, supplying new

[004-021]ideals and suggesting new points of view, was only the form in which the

[004-022]change of spirit

[004-023][73] began to express itself in the fourteenth century. The change might

[004-024]conceivably have taken some other shape. Its true name is Humanism.

[004-025]At the time men hardly felt that they were passing into a new age of

[004-026]civilization, nor did the culture of the Renaissance immediately produce

[004-027]any open or general intellectual rebellion against orthodox beliefs. The

[004-028]world was gradually assuming an aspect decidedly unfriendly to the

[004-029]teaching of mediaeval orthodoxy; but there was no explosion of

[004-030]hostility; it was not till the seventeenth century that war between

[004-031]religion and authority was systematically waged. The humanists were not

[004-032]hostile to theological authority or to the claims of religious dogma;

[004-033]but they had discovered a purely human curiosity about this world and it

[004-034]absorbed their interest. They idolized pagan literature which abounded

[004-035]in poisonous germs; the secular side of education became all-important;

[004-036]religion and theology were kept in a separate compartment. Some

[004-037]speculative minds, which were sensitive to the contradiction, might seek

[004-038]to reconcile the old religion with new ideas; but the general tendency

[004-039]of thinkers in the Renaissance period was to keep the two worlds

[004-040]distinct, and to practise outward conformity to the creed without any

[004-041]real intellectual submission.

[004-042][74]

[004-043]I may illustrate this double-facedness of the Renaissance by Montaigne

[004-044](second half of sixteenth century). His Essays make for rationalism, but

[004-045]contain frequent professions of orthodox Catholicism, in which he was

[004-046]perfectly sincere. There is no attempt to reconcile the two points of

[004-047]view; in fact, he takes the sceptical position that there is no bridge

[004-048]between reason and religion. The human intellect is incapable in the

[004-049]domain of theology, and religion must be placed aloft, out of reach and

[004-050]beyond the interference of reason; to be humbly accepted. But while he

[004-051]humbly accepted it, on sceptical grounds which would have induced him to

[004-052]accept Mohammadanism if he had been born in Cairo, his soul was not in

[004-053]its dominion. It was the philosophers and wise men of antiquity, Cicero,

[004-054]and Seneca, and Plutarch, who moulded and possessed his mind. It is to

[004-055]them, and not to the consolations of Christianity, that he turns when he

[004-056]discusses the problem of death. The religious wars in France which he

[004-057]witnessed and the Massacre of St. Bartholomew’s Day (1572) were

[004-058]calculated to confirm him in his scepticism. His attitude to persecution

[004-059]is expressed in the remark that “it is setting a high value on one’s

[004-060]opinions to roast men on account of them.”

[004-061]The logical results of Montaigne’s scepticism

[004-062][75] were made visible by his friend Charron, who published a book On

[004-063]Wisdom in 1601. Here it is taught that true morality is not founded on

[004-064]religion, and the author surveys the history of Christianity to show the

[004-065]evils which it had produced. He says of immortality that it is the most

[004-066]generally received doctrine, the most usefully believed, and the most

[004-067]weakly established by human reasons; but he modified this and some other

[004-068]passages in a second edition. A contemporary Jesuit placed Charron in

[004-069]the catalogue of the most dangerous and wicked atheists. He was really a

[004-070]deist; but in those days, and long after, no one scrupled to call a non-

[004-071]Christian deist an atheist. His book would doubtless have been

[004-072]suppressed and he would have suffered but for the support of King Henry

[004-073]IV. It has a particular interest because it transports us directly from

[004-074]the atmosphere of the Renaissance, represented by Montaigne, into the

[004-075]new age of more or less aggressive rationalism.

[004-076]What Humanism did in the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth centuries,

[004-077]at first in Italy, then in other countries, was to create an

[004-078]intellectual atmosphere in which the emancipation of reason could begin

[004-079]and knowledge could resume its progress. The period saw the invention of

[004-080]printing and

[004-081][76] the discovery of new parts of the globe, and these things were to

[004-082]aid powerfully in the future defeat of authority.

[004-083]But the triumph of freedom depended on other causes also; it was not to

[004-084]be brought about by the intellect alone. The chief political facts of

[004-085]the period were the decline of the power of the Pope in Europe, the

[004-086]decay of the Holy Roman Empire, and the growth of strong monarchies, in

[004-087]which worldly interests determined and dictated ecclesiastical policy,

[004-088]and from which the modern State was to develop. The success of the

[004-089]Reformation was made possible by these conditions. Its victory in North

[004-090]Germany was due to the secular interest of the princes, who profited by

[004-091]the confiscation of Church lands. In England there was no popular

[004-092]movement; the change was carried through by the government for its own

[004-093]purposes.

[004-094]The principal cause of the Reformation was the general corruption of the

[004-095]Church and the flagrancy of its oppression. For a long time the Papacy

[004-096]had had no higher aim than to be a secular power exploiting its

[004-097]spiritual authority for the purpose of promoting its worldly interests,

[004-098]by which it was exclusively governed. All the European States based

[004-099]their diplomacy on this assumption. Since the fourteenth century every

[004-100]one acknowledged

[004-101][77] the need of reforming the Church, and reform had been promised, but

[004-102]things went from bad to worse, and there was no resource but rebellion.

[004-103]The rebellion led by Luther was the result not of a revolt of reason

[004-104]against dogmas, but of widely spread anti-clerical feeling due to the

[004-105]ecclesiastical methods of extorting money, particularly by the sale of

[004-106]Indulgences, the most glaring abuse of the time. It was his study of the

[004-107]theory of Papal Indulgences that led Luther on to his theological

[004-108]heresies.

[004-109]It is an elementary error, but one which is still shared by many people

[004-110]who have read history superficially, that the Reformation established

[004-111]religious liberty and the right of private judgment. What it did was to

[004-112]bring about a new set of political and social conditions, under which

[004-113]religious liberty could ultimately be secured, and, by virtue of its

[004-114]inherent inconsistencies, to lead to results at which its leaders would

[004-115]have shuddered. But nothing was further from the minds of the leading

[004-116]Reformers than the toleration of doctrines differing from their own.

[004-117]They replaced one authority by another. They set up the authority of the

[004-118]Bible instead of that of the Church, but it was the Bible according to

[004-119]Luther or the Bible according to Calvin. So far as the spirit of

[004-120]intolerance went, there

[004-121][78] was nothing to choose between the new and the old Churches. The

[004-122]religious wars were not for the cause of freedom, but for particular

[004-123]sets of doctrines; and in France, if the Protestants had been

[004-124]victorious, it is certain that they would not have given more liberal

[004-125]terms to the Catholics than the Catholics gave to them.

[004-126]Luther was quite opposed to liberty of conscience and worship, a

[004-127]doctrine which was inconsistent with Scripture as he read it. He might

[004-128]protest against coercion and condemn the burning of heretics, when he

[004-129]was in fear that he and his party might be victims, but when he was safe

[004-130]and in power, he asserted his real view that it was the duty of the

[004-131]State to impose the true doctrine and exterminate heresy, which was an

[004-132]abomination, that unlimited obedience to their prince in religious as in

[004-133]other matters was the duty of subjects, and that the end of the State

[004-134]was to defend the faith. He held that Anabaptists should be put to the

[004-135]sword. With Protestants and Catholics alike the dogma of exclusive

[004-136]salvation led to the same place.

[004-137]Calvin’s fame for intolerance is blackest. He did not, like Luther,

[004-138]advocate the absolute power of the civil ruler; he stood for the control

[004-139]of the State by the Church—a form of government which is commonly called

[004-140]theocracy;

[004-141][79] and he established a theocracy at Geneva. Here liberty was

[004-142]completely crushed; false doctrines were put down by imprisonment,

[004-143]exile, and death. The punishment of Servetus is the most famous exploit

[004-144]of Calvin’s warfare against heresy. The Spaniard Servetus, who had

[004-145]written against the dogma of the Trinity, was imprisoned at Lyons

[004-146](partly through the machinations of Calvin) and having escaped came

[004-147]rashly to Geneva. He was tried for heresy and committed to the flames

[004-148](1553), though Geneva had no jurisdiction over him. Melanchthon, who

[004-149]formulated the principles of persecution, praised this act as a

[004-150]memorable example to posterity. Posterity however was one day to be

[004-151]ashamed of that example. In 1903 the Calvinists of Geneva felt impelled

[004-152]to erect an expiatory monument, in which Calvin “our great Reformer” is

[004-153]excused as guilty of an error “which was that of his century.”

[004-154]Thus the Reformers, like the Church from which they parted, cared

[004-155]nothing for freedom, they only cared for “truth.” If the mediaeval ideal

[004-156]was to purge the world of heretics, the object of the Protestant was to

[004-157]exclude all dissidents from his own land. The people at large were to be

[004-158]driven into a fold, to accept their faith at the command of their

[004-159]sovran. This was the principle laid down in the

[004-160][80] religious peace which (1555) composed the struggle between the

[004-161]Catholic Emperor and the Protestant German princes. It was recognized by

[004-162]Catherine de’ Medici when she massacred the French Protestants and

[004-163]signified to Queen Elizabeth that she might do likewise with English

[004-164]Catholics.

[004-165]Nor did the Protestant creeds represent enlightenment. The Reformation

[004-166]on the Continent was as hostile to enlightenment as it was to liberty;

[004-167]and science, if it seemed to contradict the Bible, has as little chance

[004-168]with Luther as with the Pope. The Bible, interpreted by the Protestants

[004-169]or the Roman Church, was equally fatal to witches. In Germany the

[004-170]development of learning received a long set-back.

[004-171]Yet the Reformation involuntarily helped the cause of liberty. The

[004-172]result was contrary to the intentions of its leaders, was indirect, and

[004-173]long delayed. In the first place, the great rent in Western

[004-174]Christianity, substituting a number of theological authorities instead

[004-175]of one—several gods, we may say, instead of one God—produced a weakening

[004-176]of ecclesiastical authority in general. The religious tradition was

[004-177]broken. In the second place, in the Protestant States, the supreme

[004-178]ecclesiastical power was vested in the sovran; the sovran had other

[004-179]interests besides those of

[004-180][81] the Church to consider; and political reasons would compel him

[004-181]sooner or later to modify the principle of ecclesiastical intolerance.

[004-182]Catholic States in the same way were forced to depart from the duty of

[004-183]not suffering heretics. The religious wars in France ended in a limited

[004-184]toleration of Protestants. The policy of Cardinal Richelieu, who

[004-185]supported the Protestant cause in Germany, illustrates how secular

[004-186]interests obstructed the cause of faith.

[004-187]Again, the intellectual justification of the Protestant rebellion

[004-188]against the Church had been the right of private judgment, that is, the

[004-189]principle of religious liberty. But the Reformers had asserted it only

[004-190]for themselves, and as soon as they had framed their own articles of

[004-191]faith, they had practically repudiated it. This was the most glaring

[004-192]inconsistency in the Protestant position; and the claim which they had

[004-193]thrust aside could not be permanently suppressed. Once more, the

[004-194]Protestant doctrines rested on an insecure foundation which no logic

[004-195]could defend, and inevitably led from one untenable position to another.

[004-196]If we are to believe on authority, why should we prefer the upstart

[004-197]dictation of the Lutheran Confession of Augsburg or the English Thirty-

[004-198]nine Articles to the venerable authority of the Church of Rome? If we

[004-199]decide against Rome, we must do so by means

[004-200][82] of reason; but once we exercise reason in the matter, why should we

[004-201]stop where Luther or Calvin or any of the other rebels stopped, unless

[004-202]we assume that one of them was inspired? If we reject superstitions

[004-203]which they rejected, there is nothing except their authority to prevent

[004-204]us from rejecting all or some of the superstitions which they retained.

[004-205]Moreover, their Bible-worship promoted results which they did not

[004-206]foresee. [1] The inspired record on which the creeds depend became an

[004-207]open book. Public attention was directed to it as never before, though

[004-208]it cannot be said to have been universally read before the nineteenth

[004-209]century. Study led to criticism, the difficulties of the dogma of

[004-210]inspiration were appreciated, and the Bible was ultimately to be

[004-211]submitted to a remorseless dissection which has altered at least the

[004-212]quality of its authority in the eyes of intelligent believers. This

[004-213]process of Biblical criticism has been conducted mainly in a Protestant

[004-214]atmosphere and the new position in which the Bible was placed by the

[004-215]Reformation must be held partly accountable. In these ways,

[004-216]Protestantism was adapted to be a stepping-stone to rationalism, and

[004-217]thus served the cause of freedom.

[004-218][83]

[004-219]That cause however was powerfully and directly promoted by one sect of

[004-220]Reformers, who in the eyes of all the others were blasphemers and of

[004-221]whom most people never think when they talk of the Reformation. I mean

[004-222]the Socinians. Of their far-reaching influence something will be said in

[004-223]the next chapter.

[004-224]Another result of the Reformation has still to be mentioned, its

[004-225]renovating effect on the Roman Church, which had now to fight for its

[004-226]existence. A new series of Popes who were in earnest about religion

[004-227]began with Paul III (1534) and reorganized the Papacy and its resources

[004-228]for a struggle of centuries. [2] The institution of the Jesuit order,

[004-229]the establishment of the Inquisition at Rome, the Council of Trent, the

[004-230]censorship of the Press (Index of Forbidden Books) were the expression

[004-231]of the new spirit and the means to cope with the new situation. The

[004-232]reformed Papacy was good fortune for believing children of the Church,

[004-233]but what here concerns us is that one of its chief objects was to

[004-234]repress freedom more effectually. Savonarola who preached right living

[004-235]at Florence had been executed (1498) under Pope Alexander VI who was a

[004-236]notorious profligate. If Savonarola had lived

[004-237][84] in the new era he might have been canonized, but Giordano Bruno was

[004-238]burned.

[004-239]Giordano Bruno had constructed a religious philosophy, based partly upon

[004-240]Epicurus, from whom he took the theory of the infinity of the universe.

[004-241]But Epicurean materialism was transformed into a pantheistic mysticism

[004-242]by the doctrine that God is the soul of matter. Accepting the recent

[004-243]discovery of Copernicus, which Catholics and Protestants alike rejected,

[004-244]that the earth revolves round the sun, Bruno took the further step of

[004-245]regarding the fixed stars as suns, each with its invisible satellites.

[004-246]He sought to come to an understanding with the Bible, which (he held)

[004-247]being intended for the vulgar had to accommodate itself to their

[004-248]prejudices. Leaving Italy, because he was suspected of heresy, he lived

[004-249]successively in Switzerland, France, England, and Germany, and in 1592,

[004-250]induced by a false friend to return to Venice he was seized by order of

[004-251]the Inquisition. Finally condemned in Rome, he was burned (1600) in the

[004-252]Campo de’ Fiori, where a monument now stands in his honour, erected some

[004-253]years ago, to the great chagrin of the Roman Church.

[004-254]Much is made of the fate of Bruno because he is one of the world’s

[004-255]famous men. No country has so illustrious a victim of that era to

[004-256]commemorate as Italy, but in other lands

[004-257][85] blood just as innocent was shed for heterodox opinions. In France

[004-258]there was rather more freedom than elsewhere under the relatively

[004-259]tolerant government of Henry IV and of the Cardinals Richelieu and

[004-260]Mazarin, till about 1660. But at Toulouse (1619) Lucilio Vanini, a

[004-261]learned Italian who like Bruno wandered about Europe, was convicted as

[004-262]an atheist and blasphemer; his tongue was torn out and he was burned.

[004-263]Protestant England, under Elizabeth and James I, did not lag behind the

[004-264]Roman Inquisition, but on account of the obscurity of the victims her

[004-265]zeal for faith has been unduly forgotten. Yet, but for an accident, she

[004-266]might have covered herself with the glory of having done to death a

[004-267]heretic not less famous than Giordano Bruno. The poet Marlowe was

[004-268]accused of atheism, but while the prosecution was hanging over him he

[004-269]was killed in a sordid quarrel in a tavern (1593). Another dramatist

[004-270](Kyd) who was implicated in the charge was put to the torture. At the

[004-271]same time Sir Walter Raleigh was prosecuted for unbelief but not

[004-272]convicted. Others were not so fortunate. Three or four persons were

[004-273]burned at Norwich in the reign of Elizabeth for unchristian doctrines,

[004-274]among them Francis Kett who had been a Fellow of Corpus Christi,

[004-275]Cambridge. Under James I, who

[004-276][86] interested himself personally in such matters, Bartholomew Legate

[004-277]was charged with holding various pestilent opinions. The king summoned

[004-278]him to his presence and asked him whether he did not pray daily to Jesus

[004-279]Christ. Legate replied he had prayed to Christ in the days of his

[004-280]ignorance, but not for the last seven years. “Away, base fellow,” said

[004-281]James, spurning him with his foot, “it shall never be said that one

[004-282]stayeth in my palace that hath never prayed to our Saviour for seven

[004-283]years together.” Legate, having been imprisoned for some time in

[004-284]Newgate, was declared an incorrigible heretic and burned at Smithfield

[004-285](1611). Just a month later, one Wightman was burned at Lichfield, by the

[004-286]Bishop of Coventry, for heterodox doctrines. It is possible that public

[004-287]opinion was shocked by these two burnings. They were the last cases in

[004-288]England of death for unbelief. Puritan intolerance, indeed, passed an

[004-289]ordinance in 1648, by which all who denied the Trinity, Christ’s

[004-290]divinity, the inspiration of Scripture, or a future state, were liable

[004-291]to death, and persons guilty of other heresies, to imprisonment. But

[004-292]this did not lead to any executions.

[004-293]The Renaissance age saw the first signs of the beginning of modern

[004-294]science, but the mediaeval prejudices against the investigation

[004-295][87] of nature were not dissipated till the seventeenth century, and in

[004-296]Italy they continued to a much later period. The history of modern

[004-297]astronomy begins in 1543, with the publication of the work of Copernicus

[004-298]revealing the truth about the motions of the earth. The appearance of

[004-299]this work is important in the history of free thought, because it raised

[004-300]a clear and definite issue between science and Scripture; and Osiander,

[004-301]who edited it (Copernicus was dying), forseeing the outcry it would

[004-302]raise, stated untruly in the preface that the earth’s motion was put

[004-303]forward only as a hypothesis. The theory was denounced by Catholics and

[004-304]Reformers, and it did not convince some men (e.g. Bacon) who were not

[004-305]influenced by theological prejudice. The observations of the Italian

[004-306]astronomer Galileo de’ Galilei demonstrated the Copernican theory beyond

[004-307]question. His telescope discovered the moons of Jupiter, and his

[004-308]observation of the spots in the sun confirmed the earth’s rotation. In

[004-309]the pulpits of Florence, where he lived under the protection of the

[004-310]Grand Duke, his sensational discoveries were condemned. “Men of Galilee,

[004-311]why stand ye gazing up into heaven?” He was then denounced to the Holy

[004-312]Office of the Inquisition by two Dominican monks. Learning that his

[004-313]investigations were being considered

[004-314][88] at Rome, Galileo went thither, confident that he would be able to

[004-315]convince the ecclesiastical authorities of the manifest truth of

[004-316]Copernicanism. He did not realize what theology was capable of. In

[004-317]February 1616 the Holy Office decided that the Copernican system was in

[004-318]itself absurd, and, in respect of Scripture, heretical. Cardinal

[004-319]Bellarmin, by the Pope’s direction, summoned Galileo and officially

[004-320]admonished him to abandon his opinion and cease to teach it, otherwise

[004-321]the Inquisition would proceed against him. Galileo promised to obey. The

[004-322]book of Copernicus was placed on the Index. It has been remarked that

[004-323]Galileo’s book on Solar Spots contains no mention of Scripture, and thus

[004-324]the Holy Office, in its decree which related to that book, passed

[004-325]judgment on a scientific, not a theological, question.

[004-326]Galileo was silenced for a while, but it was impossible for him to be

[004-327]mute for ever. Under a new Pope (Urban VIII) he looked for greater

[004-328]liberty, and there were many in the Papal circle who were well disposed

[004-329]to him. He hoped to avoid difficulties by the device of placing the

[004-330]arguments for the old and the new theories side by side, and pretending

[004-331]not to judge between them. He wrote a treatise on the two systems (the

[004-332]Ptolemaic and the Copernican) in the form

[004-333][89] of Dialogues, of which the preface declares that the purpose is to

[004-334]explain the pros and cons of the two views. But the spirit of the work

[004-335]is Copernican. He received permission, quite definite as he thought,

[004-336]from Father Riccardi (master of the Sacred Palace) to print it, and it

[004-337]appeared in 1632. The Pope however disapproved of it, the book was

[004-338]examined by a commission, and Galileo was summoned before the

[004-339]Inquisition. He was old and ill, and the humiliations which he had to

[004-340]endure are a painful story. He would probably have been more severely

[004-341]treated, if one of the members of the tribunal had not been a man of

[004-342]scientific training (Macolano, a Dominican), who was able to appreciate

[004-343]his ability. Under examination, Galileo denied that he had upheld the

[004-344]motion of the earth in the Dialogues, and asserted that he had shown the

[004-345]reasons of Copernicus to be inconclusive. This defence was in accordance

[004-346]with the statement in his preface, but contradicted his deepest

[004-347]conviction. In struggling with such a tribunal, it was the only line

[004-348]which a man who was not a hero could take. At a later session, he forced

[004-349]himself ignominiously to confess that some of the arguments on the

[004-350]Copernican side had been put too strongly and to declare himself ready

[004-351]to confute the

[004-352][90] theory. In the final examination, he was threatened with torture.

[004-353]He said that before the decree of 1616 he had held the truth of the

[004-354]Copernican system to be arguable, but since then he had held the

[004-355]Ptolemaic to be true. Next day, he publicly abjured the scientific truth

[004-356]which he had demonstrated. He was allowed to retire to the country, on

[004-357]condition that he saw no one. In the last months of his life he wrote to

[004-358]a friend to this effect: “The falsity of the Copernican system cannot be

[004-359]doubted, especially by us Catholics. It is refuted by the irrefragable

[004-360]authority of Scripture. The conjectures of Copernicus and his disciples

[004-361]were all disposed of by the one solid argument: God’s omnipotence can

[004-362]operate in infinitely various ways. If something appears to our

[004-363]observation to happen in one particular way, we must not curtail God’s

[004-364]arm, and sustain a thing in which we may be deceived.” The irony is

[004-365]evident.

[004-366]Rome did not permit the truth about the solar system to be taught till

[004-367]after the middle of the eighteenth century, and Galileo’s books remained

[004-368]on the Index till 1835. The prohibition was fatal to the study of

[004-369]natural science in Italy.

[004-370]The Roman Index reminds us of the significance of the invention of

[004-371]printing in the struggle for freedom of thought, by making

[004-372][91] it easy to propagate new ideas far and wide. Authority speedily

[004-373]realized the danger, and took measures to place its yoke on the new

[004-374]contrivance, which promised to be such a powerful ally of reason. Pope

[004-375]Alexander VI inaugurated censorship of the Press by his Bull against

[004-376]unlicensed printing (1501). In France King Henry II made printing

[004-377]without official permission punishable by death. In Germany, censorship

[004-378]was introduced in 1529. In England, under Elizabeth, books could not be

[004-379]printed without a license, and printing presses were not allowed except

[004-380]in London, Oxford, and Cambridge; the regulation of the Press was under

[004-381]the authority of the Star Chamber. Nowhere did the Press become really

[004-382]free till the nineteenth century.

[004-383]While the Reformation and the renovated Roman Church meant a reaction

[004-384]against the Renaissance, the vital changes which the Renaissance

[004-385]signified—individualism, a new intellectual attitude to the world, the

[004-386]cultivation of secular knowledge—were permanent and destined to lead,

[004-387]amid the competing intolerances of Catholic and Protestant powers, to

[004-388]the goal of liberty. We shall see how reason and the growth of knowledge

[004-389]undermined the bases of theological authority. At each step in this

[004-390]process, in which philosophical speculation, historical

[004-391][92] criticism, natural science have all taken part, the opposition

[004-392]between reason and faith deepened; doubt, clear or vague, increased; and

[004-393]secularism, derived from the Humanists, and always implying scepticism,

[004-394]whether latent or conscious, substituted an interest in the fortunes of

[004-395]the human race upon earth for the interest in a future world. And along

[004-396]with this steady intellectual advance, toleration gained ground and

[004-397]freedom won more champions. In the meantime the force of political

[004-398]circumstances was compelling governments to mitigate their maintenance

[004-399]of one religious creed by measures of relief to other Christian sects,

[004-400]and the principle of exclusiveness was broken down for reasons of

[004-401]worldly expediency. Religious liberty was an important step towards

[004-402]complete freedom of opinion.
