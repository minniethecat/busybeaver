## Definition and Origins

Persecution, in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes the systematic punishment or suppression of individuals on account of their religious or philosophical opinions. Bury identifies persecution as the principal force against which the history of freethought must be understood. He writes that the history of freedom of thought involves the Churches, of heresies, and of persecution, but also the gradual triumph of reason over intolerance (001-273).

The theoretical foundation of persecution rested on the conviction that holding false beliefs about God endangered the soul of the believer and the welfare of the community. In pagan antiquity, persecution of opinion was never systematic in the sense it would later become, though it was certainly practiced (002-268).

## Historical Development

The most significant early instance of systematic religious persecution was the Roman persecution of Christianity. Beginning intermittently under Nero, persecution in Europe became a matter of state policy during the third century (002-368). Bury notes that in general, the persecution of the Christians was rather sporadic than continuous until the mid-third century (002-406).

The first empire-wide persecution was begun by Decius in A.D. 250, and was continued under Valerian. A more thorough persecution was organized under Diocletian, beginning in A.D. 303 (002-445, 002-457). These persecutions were notable for their scale and organization, yet they ultimately failed. The persecution ended by edicts of toleration issued by Galerius in A.D. 311 and by Constantine and Licinius in A.D. 313 (002-463).

Bury presents the general question of persecution and freedom of conscience through the lens of its theoretical justifications (002-486). He analyzes the argument that to an orthodox and loyal pagan, the persecution of Christians appeared reasonable and necessary (002-500). The central dilemma Bury identifies is that persecution is a choice between two evils, which no reasonable defender of persecution would deny to be an evil in itself (002-503).

But if the persecution is not so devised and carried out as to succeed, it is an unnecessary evil. And Bury observes that the Roman persecutions were notoriously ineffectual (002-507). The later persecutions of A.D. 250 and 303 failed to halt Christian growth (002-519). The higher problem whether persecution, even if it attains the desired result, can be morally justified, remained a central question of the theory and practice of persecution throughout history (002-522, 002-560).

## Key Proponents / Opponents

- **Emperor Decius** initiated the first systematic empire-wide persecution of Christians in A.D. 250, requiring universal sacrifice to the Roman gods.
- **Emperor Diocletian** launched the most severe Roman persecution beginning in A.D. 303, destroying churches and scriptures throughout the empire.
- **Lactantius** and other early Christian apologists developed arguments against persecution that later informed the broader case for religious toleration.
- **Pierre Bayle** provided the most influential philosophical argument against religious persecution, contending that conscience cannot be coerced.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Persecution shaped the entire history of freedom of thought by creating the conditions against which freethinkers struggled. The failure of Roman persecution to suppress Christianity demonstrated the practical limits of coercion in matters of belief. However, once Christianity gained political power, it adopted the very methods of persecution it had once suffered.

Bury's analysis reveals a recurring pattern: persecution, even when systematically organized, tends to fail when it targets deeply held convictions. The ineffectiveness of persecution in achieving its stated aims—the eradication of heresy or unbelief—provided one of the strongest practical arguments for toleration.

The transition from a society that accepted persecution as legitimate to one that regarded it as a violation of fundamental rights was, in Bury's account, one of the most significant developments in Western civilization. The question of whether persecution can ever be justified, even if effective, remained a touchstone for debates about the limits of state authority over individual conscience.
