# Liberty of Conscience

Liberty of conscience — the claim that the individual has an indefeasible right to follow their own judgment in matters of belief, worship, and moral conviction — is, in J. B. Bury's account, the central thread running through the entire history of freedom of thought. From its first articulation by Socrates to its codification in Enlightenment legal codes, the idea that the inner tribunal of conscience stands above the outer tribunal of the state has been the engine driving two millennia of intellectual liberation.

## Definition and Origins

Bury locates the origin of the claim of liberty of conscience in the trial of Socrates (399 BCE). In what Bury calls "the earliest justification of liberty of thought," Socrates asserted "two significant claims": "the indefeasible right of the conscience of the individual — a claim on which later struggles for liberty were to turn; and the social importance of discussion and criticism" (002-250). The first claim was not argued from abstract principle but rested on intuition — Socrates simply knew that he must obey his "super-human guide" rather than the Athenian court.

Socrates articulated this conviction with unforgettable clarity at his trial: "If you propose to acquit me on condition that I abandon my search for truth, I will say: I thank you, O Athenians, but I will obey God, who, as I believe, set me this task, rather than you, and so long as I have breath and strength I will never cease from my occupation with philosophy" (002-231). This was the first declaration in Western history that conscience takes precedence over the commands of the state.

> Thus in what we may call the earliest justification of liberty of thought we have two significant claims affirmed: the indefeasible right of the conscience of the individual — a claim on which later struggles for liberty were to turn; and the social importance of discussion and criticism.
> (002-249, 002-252)

## Historical Development

### The Christian Paradox

The early Christians inherited the Socratic claim but applied it selectively. When confronting the Roman Empire, Christians asserted "the principle of freedom of conscience" as "superior to all obligations to the State" (002-496). The conflict between Rome and Christianity was, at its core, a clash between "the conscience of the individual and the authority and supposed interests of the State" (002-524) — the same question Socrates had raised, "raised now on a wider platform in a more pressing and formidable shape" (002-527).

Yet Bury notes a profound irony: "The martyrs died for conscience, but not for liberty. To-day the greatest of the Churches demands freedom of conscience in the modern States which she does not control, but refuses to admit that, where she had the power, it would be incumbent on her to concede it" (002-537, 002-540). The Christian claim to liberty of conscience was a claim for *themselves*, not a universal principle. When Christians gained power with Constantine, they "completely forget the principle which they had invoked" (002-536) and began persecuting others.

### The Reformation and the Socinian Bridge

Luther's Reformation, despite its rhetoric of individual judgment, did not embrace liberty of conscience. Bury is explicit: "Luther was quite opposed to liberty of conscience and worship" (004-126). The decisive turn came with the Socinians, who "conceded such a wide room to individual judgment in the interpretation of Scripture that to impose Socinianism would have been inconsistent with its principles" (005-033). From the Socinians, the principle passed to the Anabaptists, the Dutch Arminians, and the English Independents. In Holland, "the founder of the English Congregationalists... learned the principle of liberty of conscience" (005-053), bringing it into the mainstream of Anglo-American political thought.

### Enlightenment Codification

The eighteenth century saw liberty of conscience move from sectarian advocacy to legal principle. John Milton's *Areopagitica* (1644) placed "freedom of thought above civil liberty," declaring: "Give me the liberty to know, to utter, and to argue freely according to conscience, above all other liberties" (005-153). A century later, Thomas Paine sharpened this into a critique of toleration itself: "Toleration is not the opposite of Intolerance, but is the counterfeit of it. Both are despotisms. The one assumes itself the right of withholding liberty of conscience, and the other of granting it" (005-376). For Paine, liberty of conscience was not a grant from the state but a natural right that the state had no authority to give or withhold.

The first full legal realization came under Frederick the Great of Prussia, whose "Territorial Code of 1794" guaranteed "unrestricted liberty of conscience" and placed the three chief religions "on the same footing" (005-556). Bury emphasizes that this landmark was achieved "under a free-thinking ruler, the friend of the great 'blasphemer' Voltaire" (005-554) — a reminder that the path from Socrates's trial to legal liberty of conscience passed through freethought, not orthodoxy.

## Key Proponents

- **Socrates** (c. 470–399 BCE): First to assert the supremacy of individual conscience over human law, at the cost of his life (002-226).
- **Roger Williams** (c. 1603–1683): Founded Rhode Island on the principle that civil magistrates have no authority over conscience (005-076).
- **John Milton** (1608–1674): In *Areopagitica*, placed liberty of conscience above all civil liberties (005-153).
- **Thomas Paine** (1737–1809): Argued that toleration is not a right granted by the state but a counterfeit of true liberty (005-376).
- **Frederick the Great** (1712–1786): Codified unrestricted liberty of conscience in Prussian law (005-556).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Liberty of conscience is the foundation on which all other intellectual freedoms rest. Without the prior claim that the individual mind is sovereign in matters of belief, there can be no freedom of speech, no freedom of the press, and no freedom of inquiry. Bury's historical narrative traces how this claim, first made by a single Athenian philosopher facing death, gradually expanded from a personal conviction into a universal right.

The trajectory was not linear. The principle that Christians asserted against Rome they abandoned when they gained power. The Reformation, which might have been expected to champion individual conscience, proved as intolerant as the Church it challenged. It was the heterodox — the Socinians, the Anabaptists, the Independents — who kept the principle alive, transmitting it across centuries and across the Atlantic, until it found legal expression in the Enlightenment.

Bury's account suggests a sobering lesson: liberty of conscience has never been the work of majorities or established powers. It has always been championed by minorities, dissenters, and freethinkers — those who, like Socrates, were willing to obey their inner conviction even when the world commanded otherwise.
