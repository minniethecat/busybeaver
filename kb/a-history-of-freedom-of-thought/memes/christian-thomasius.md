# Christian Thomasius

**Christian Thomasius** (1655–1728) was a German jurist and philosopher who may be taken as a leading exponent of the theory that religious liberty logically follows from a right conception of law. In a series of pamphlets published between 1693 and 1697, he laid down principles that helped establish the intellectual foundations of [[toleration]] in German-speaking Europe (005-516).

## Life and Background

Thomasius was born in Leipzig, where his father was a professor of philosophy. He studied law at the University of Frankfurt an der Oder and began lecturing at Leipzig in the 1680s. His unorthodox views and willingness to lecture in German rather than Latin — a significant departure from academic convention — brought him into conflict with the university authorities.

Forced to leave Leipzig, Thomasius helped found the University of Halle in 1694, which became a center of the early German Enlightenment. At Halle, he developed his influential theories on the proper relationship between secular authority and religious belief, influenced by the writings of his contemporary [[John Locke|Locke]] (005-516).

## Major Contributions

Thomasius's central argument, developed across his 1693–1697 pamphlets, was that the prince, who alone has the power of coercion, has no right to interfere in spiritual matters. Conversely, the clergy step beyond their province if they interfere in secular matters or defend their faith by any other means than teaching (005-516).

The logical foundation of this argument was his analysis of heresy. The secular power, Thomasius maintained, has no legal right to coerce heretics unless heresy is a crime. And heresy is not a crime, but an error; for it is not a matter of will (005-524). This distinction between error and crime was revolutionary in its implications: if belief is involuntary — a matter of intellectual conviction rather than willful choice — then punishing it is both legally incoherent and morally unjust.

Thomasius further urged that public welfare has nothing to gain from unity of faith, and that it makes no difference what faith a man professes so long as he is loyal to the State (005-524).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Thomasius advanced freedom of thought by providing a secular, juristic argument for toleration that did not depend on theological premises. Unlike earlier tolerationist writers who argued on grounds of Christian charity or scriptural interpretation, Thomasius derived religious liberty from the nature of law itself (005-516).

His argument that belief cannot be coerced because it is not an act of will anticipated later liberal defenses of freedom of conscience. By separating the spheres of secular coercion and spiritual conviction, Thomasius articulated a framework that would prove foundational to Enlightenment theories of church and state.

It must be noted that his toleration was not complete: he was much influenced by Locke and excepted from the benefit of toleration the same classes which Locke excepted (005-516). Like Locke, he did not extend his principles to atheists or to Catholics whose political loyalty was suspect.

## Legacy and Influence

Thomasius's work at Halle established him as a central figure in the early German *Aufklärung* (Enlightenment). His insistence on the separation of legal coercion from religious belief influenced subsequent generations of German jurists and philosophers who elaborated more comprehensive theories of religious freedom.

His pamphlets of the 1690s, together with the influence of the jurists and the Pietistic movement, contributed to a gradual shift in German political thought away from the confessional state and toward a more secular conception of political authority (005-516).
