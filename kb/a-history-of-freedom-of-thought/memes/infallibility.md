## Definition and Origins

Infallibility, as discussed in Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the doctrine that certain religious authorities — whether the Bible, the Church, or ecclesiastical pronouncements — are incapable of error. This doctrine functioned historically as a powerful barrier to free inquiry because it placed the content of revelation beyond the reach of rational criticism. If a text or institution is infallible, any questioning of its statements becomes not merely mistaken but impious.

Bury traces the doctrine's origins to the early Church's need for an unchallengeable authority. As Christianity developed from a diverse movement into an institutional Church with binding doctrines, the claim of infallibility provided the logical foundation for enforced orthodoxy. Without infallibility, doctrinal disputes could only be resolved through persuasion and debate; with it, they could be settled by authority.

## Historical Development

The Protestant Reformation, while challenging the infallibility of the Pope and Church councils, transferred the claim of infallibility to Scripture itself. The principle of *sola scriptura* — Scripture alone as the source of religious truth — required that the Bible be treated as an errorless document. This position, Bury shows, became increasingly difficult to maintain as critical scholarship advanced.

Bury documents the penetrating criticisms leveled against the doctrine by freethinkers. One thinker went so far as to "criticize the Bible in detail. In order to maintain its infallibility, without doing violence to reason, you have, when you find irrational" elements, to resort to strained interpretations that ultimately undermine the doctrine itself (006-330).

The eighteenth-century deists and rationalists subjected the claim of infallibility to withering mockery. Bury quotes a particularly devastating passage: "(of all considering persons) against this generation and shall condemn it; for they invented but the one absurdity of infallibility, and behold a greater absurdity than infallibility is here" (006-395, 006-396). The rhetorical strategy was to make infallibility appear not merely false but absurd — a claim so extravagant that it refuted itself.

The nineteenth century brought the most powerful challenge to biblical infallibility from two directions. First, geological discoveries made it impossible to maintain a literal reading of Genesis. Bury records how "geology shook the infallibility of the Bible, but left the creation of some prehistoric Adam and Eve a still admissible hypothesis" (007-0059). Second, the development of historical criticism — particularly in Germany — demonstrated that the biblical texts had human authors who wrote in specific historical contexts with limited knowledge and particular agendas.

The cumulative effect of these criticisms was to make "the old orthodox view of the infallibility of the Bible untenable" (007-0284). Bury notes that this did not destroy religious faith, but it transformed it — forcing believers to develop more sophisticated understandings of how fallible human texts could nevertheless convey religious truth. The doctrine of infallibility, once the cornerstone of Christian apologetics, became an embarrassment that even many believers quietly abandoned.

## Key Critics

- **Baruch Spinoza** was among the first to apply systematic rational criticism to the Bible, treating it as a human document to be understood historically rather than as an infallible text to be accepted unquestioningly.
- **Thomas Paine**, in *The Age of Reason*, made "criticisms of Scripture — admissions which were calculated to damage the doctrine of the infallibility of the Bible" (006-849). His accessible style brought critical arguments to a mass audience.
- The German historical critics demonstrated that the Pentateuch had multiple authors and a complex compositional history, directly contradicting the traditional claim of Mosaic authorship and, by extension, biblical infallibility.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The doctrine of infallibility was, in Bury's analysis, the foundation on which the entire edifice of enforced orthodoxy rested. If the Bible or the Church could err, then dissent from its teachings could not automatically be condemned as error. The gradual erosion of the infallibility claim was therefore a precondition for the emergence of genuine freedom of thought in religious matters.

Bury's account reveals a strategic insight: the critics of infallibility did not need to prove that every biblical statement was false; they only needed to demonstrate that *some* were. A single proven error was sufficient to destroy the doctrine of infallibility, because infallibility is an absolute claim that cannot admit exceptions. This made the doctrine uniquely vulnerable to the accumulative effect of scholarly criticism.

The practical consequence of abandoning infallibility was the transformation of religious belief from submission to external authority into a matter of personal judgment. Once believers acknowledged that their sacred texts might contain errors, they were compelled to exercise their own reason in distinguishing truth from error — a development that placed religious belief on the same footing as any other intellectual endeavor. Bury presents this as one of the decisive achievements in the history of freedom of thought.
