## Definition and Origins

Morality, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to the principles governing right and wrong conduct. Bury's treatment of morality focuses on a critical question that animated the freethought movement: whether morality depends on religious belief, or whether it can be grounded in reason and human nature alone.

The ancient Greeks had already raised the question of the relationship between morality and religion. Socrates questioned whether actions were good because the gods commanded them, or whether the gods commanded them because they were good. This philosophical tradition recognized that morality could be investigated through reason, independent of religious authority.

## Historical Development

In the Christian era, the dominant view held that morality was inseparable from religious doctrine. It was widely assumed that without belief in God and the fear of divine punishment, human beings would lack any motivation for ethical conduct. Bury notes that freethinkers were frequently accused of immorality precisely because they rejected religious orthodoxy (002-053).

The Enlightenment transformed the debate by proposing that morality was independent of religion. Bury records that it was taught that true morality is not founded on religious belief but on rational principles accessible to all human beings (004-063). This idea was revolutionary because it implied that atheists could be moral, and that religious believers could be immoral.

The slogan that morality was independent of religion became a rallying cry for deists and freethinkers (005-542). During the French Revolution, the Constitution and republican morality were taught as alternatives to religious ethics, supported by festivals and civic ceremonies (005-422, 005-433).

Throughout the eighteenth century, morality was the guiding concern of Enlightenment thought (006-206). Thinkers separated morality from religion and identified it with reason and natural sentiment (006-069). The deists argued that morality depends on reason; the orthodox countered that reason alone was insufficient without revelation (006-204).

## Key Proponents

- **Pierre Bayle** argued most influentially that a society of atheists could be moral, challenging the long-held assumption that religion was necessary for ethics. He contended that morality derived from natural reason and social instinct, not divine command.
- **Immanuel Kant** developed a rational system of ethics grounded in the categorical imperative, demonstrating that morality could be derived from reason alone without reference to divine will.
- **John Locke** argued that moral truths were accessible through reason and that religious coercion was both ineffective and immoral.
- **The Deists** collectively advanced the view that Christianity's moral teachings were not unique and that natural religion provided a sufficient basis for ethical conduct.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The separation of morality from religion was one of the most consequential achievements of freethought. By demonstrating that ethical conduct did not require religious belief, freethinkers removed a principal argument for religious persecution. If atheists could be moral, then there was no justification for punishing unbelief on grounds of public morality.

Some critics argued that freethought would corrupt morality by removing the fear of divine punishment (006-407). However, freethinkers responded that the inducements which Christianity offered actually corrupted morality by making ethical behavior a matter of reward and punishment rather than genuine virtue (006-410).

The establishment of morality as an autonomous domain of inquiry, independent of theology, was a foundational step toward modern secular ethics. It enabled moral philosophy to develop as a rational discipline while protecting freedom of thought in religious matters. The argument that one could be good without God was, in Bury's account, among the most liberating ideas of the Enlightenment.
