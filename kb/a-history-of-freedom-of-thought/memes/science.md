## Definition and Origins

Science, as treated in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, refers to the systematic pursuit of knowledge about the natural world through observation, experiment, and rational inference. Bury traces how the rise of modern science fundamentally altered the intellectual landscape and contributed decisively to the liberation of thought from traditional authority.

European science began its distinctive development with the ancient Greeks, whose "progress in science" and "experiments in political" organization established foundational methods of rational inquiry (002-029). The Greek tradition of scientific thinking, temporarily eclipsed during the early medieval period, was revived and accelerated from the Renaissance onward.

## Historical Development

The scientific revolution of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, initiated by Copernicus and advanced by Galileo, challenged the authority of both the Church and Aristotelian philosophy. This challenge was not merely about specific facts but about the method by which truth was determined—shifting authority from tradition and revelation to observation and reason.

Bury emphasizes that modern science emerged in contrast to religious modes of explanation. The natural sciences and political philosophy developed in tandem, both drawing on empirical methods (001-274). The scientific approach of testing hypotheses against evidence provided a model for critical thinking that extended beyond the natural world into questions of morality, politics, and religion.

By the nineteenth century, science had become a powerful cultural force. The application of scientific methods to questions previously reserved for theology—particularly through Darwin's theory of evolution—marked a decisive shift in intellectual authority.

## Key Proponents

- **Copernicus** initiated the scientific revolution by proposing a heliocentric model of the solar system, challenging both religious and scientific orthodoxy.
- **Galileo Galilei** advanced the experimental method and defended Copernican astronomy against Church opposition.
- **Charles Darwin** published the *Origin of Species* in 1859, providing a naturalistic explanation for the diversity of life that challenged traditional religious accounts of creation.
- **Herbert Spencer** applied evolutionary thinking to philosophy and society, promoting a scientific worldview.
- **Thomas Huxley** vigorously defended Darwinian evolution and coined the term "agnosticism" to describe a scientific approach to religious questions.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Science advanced freedom of thought by providing an alternative path to knowledge that did not depend on religious authority or ancient texts. The scientific method—requiring evidence, welcoming criticism, and rejecting arguments from authority—embodied the principles of freethought.

The "rise of modern science" gradually undermined the intellectual monopoly of the Church (002-287). Scientific discoveries in astronomy, geology, and biology contradicted literal readings of scripture, forcing a reconsideration of the relationship between faith and reason.

Science also contributed to freethought by demonstrating the power of human reason to understand the natural world without recourse to supernatural explanations. This methodological naturalism, while not necessarily entailing atheism, established that religious explanations were not required for understanding natural phenomena.
