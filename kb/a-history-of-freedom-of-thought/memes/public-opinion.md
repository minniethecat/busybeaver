# Public Opinion

Public opinion — the collective attitudes, beliefs, and sentiments of a society — has played a dual role in the history of freedom of thought. For most of Western history, public opinion acted as a conservative force that reinforced orthodoxy and suppressed novel ideas. In the modern era, however, public opinion gradually became a vehicle for toleration, granting individuals the right to form their own beliefs.

## Definition and Origins

Public opinion refers to the prevailing views held by the majority of people in a society, as distinct from the doctrines imposed by authority. Bury observes that in the ancient world, "when public opinion was formed by the views of such men" — men hostile to new ideas — "thought was fettered and the impediments to knowledge enormous" (001-094). The weight of collective opinion, reinforced by mental laziness and fear of change, created an environment in which asking inconvenient questions was seen as dangerous to the foundations of society.

## Historical Development

### Ancient and Medieval Eras

Throughout antiquity and the Middle Ages, public opinion overwhelmingly supported religious and intellectual conformity. During the medieval Inquisition, "the cruel enactments against heretics were strongly supported by the public opinion of the masses" (003-143). Heresy was regarded as the foulest of crimes, and popular sentiment endorsed the persecution and burning of those who deviated from orthodox doctrine.

### The Seventeenth Century

By the seventeenth century, public opinion in Europe remained hostile to religious toleration. "In Europe, public opinion was not ripe for separation, inasmuch as the most powerful religious bodies were alike in regarding toleration as wicked indifference" (005-067). The principle of religious liberty found its first foothold not in the Old World but in a small corner of the New — Rhode Island, where Roger Williams established a colony based on freedom of conscience.

### The Eighteenth Century: Reason's Silent Advance

The eighteenth century witnessed a gradual but significant shift. Bury notes as "a striking illustration of the gradual encroachments of reason" the "change which was silently wrought in public opinion on the subject of witchcraft" (006-040). Where once the belief in witchcraft had been nearly universal — endorsed by monarchs like James I and zealously enforced by Puritans — educated opinion turned against it. The last trial of a witch in England occurred in 1712, and the change reflected a deeper transformation: reason was displacing superstition in the public mind.

### The Nineteenth Century: Shock and Resistance

The nineteenth century saw public opinion repeatedly challenged by new intellectual forces. "During the next few years orthodox public opinion was shocked or disturbed by the appearance of several remarkable books which criticized, ignored, or defied authority" (007-0613). These included Lyell's *Antiquity of Man* (which challenged biblical chronology), Seeley's *Ecce Homo* (which humanized Jesus), and Lecky's *History of Rationalism*. Each provoked public outrage before gradually reshaping the climate of opinion.

### The Modern Era: The Growth of Tolerance

Bury, writing in 1913, observed a decisive transformation. "In the progressive countries of Europe there has been a marked growth of tolerance (I do not mean legal toleration, but the tolerance of public opinion) during the last thirty years" (007-0918). He quoted Lord Morley's earlier assessment that "the preliminary stage has scarcely been reached — the stage in which public opinion grants to every one the unrestricted right of shaping his own beliefs, independently of those of the people who surround him," and judged that "this preliminary stage has now been passed" (007-0920). The burial of Charles Darwin in Westminster Abbey — once unthinkable — symbolized the shift.

## Key Proponents and Theorists

The role of public opinion in freedom of thought was analyzed by several major thinkers:

- **John Locke** (1632–1704): His *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) argued that religious belief could not be coerced, laying groundwork for a public opinion that respected individual conscience.
- **John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873): In *On Liberty* (1859), Mill warned against the "tyranny of the majority" — the power of public opinion to enforce conformity more effectively than law.
- **Lord Morley** (1838–1923): Documented the slow progress of toleration in public opinion, noting that even in the late nineteenth century the right to independent belief was not fully accepted.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Public opinion is both the medium through which freedom of thought advances and the barrier it must overcome. Throughout history, the tyranny of majority opinion has been a more effective censor than any law — suppressing dissent through social exclusion, professional ruin, and the sheer weight of disapproval. Yet Bury's history demonstrates that public opinion is not static: it can be transformed by the patient work of rational argument, scientific discovery, and literary expression. The transition from a public opinion that burned heretics to one that buried Darwin in Westminster Abbey represents, in Bury's account, one of the central narratives of intellectual progress.
