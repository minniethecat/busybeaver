# Broad Church

The Broad Church was a liberal movement within the Church of England in the mid-nineteenth century that sought to reconcile Christian doctrine with modern biblical criticism, science, and rational inquiry. J. B. Bury treats the Broad Church as a significant force in the history of freedom of thought, demonstrating how liberal ideas could penetrate even the institutional church and gradually transform orthodox opinion from within.

## Definition and Origins

The term "Broad Church" — though, as Bury notes, "the name did not come in till later" (007-0532) — described a group of mainly Oxford-educated Anglican clergymen who "were interested in German criticism and leaned to broad views, which to the Evangelicals and High Churchmen seemed indistinguishable from infidelity" (007-0532). Emerging in the 1850s, the movement represented an attempt to apply the methods of historical and critical scholarship to the Bible and Christian doctrine, rejecting the rigid dogmatism of both the Evangelical and Anglo-Catholic parties.

## Historical Development

### The Essays and Reviews Crisis (1860)

The Broad Church movement came to public prominence in 1860 with the publication of *Essays and Reviews*, a volume by seven writers — six of them clergymen — who "decided to defy the 'abominable system of terrorism which prevents the statement of the plainest fact'" (007-0532). The essays addressed controversial questions of biblical interpretation, the nature of miracles, and the relationship between science and religion. The volume provoked a furious reaction from orthodox churchmen, leading to ecclesiastical prosecutions of two of the contributors.

The legal outcome proved to be "a great triumph for the Broad Church party, and it is an interesting event in the history of the English State-Church" (007-0597). Lay judges, overruling the Archbishops of Canterbury and York, effectively "granted within the Church a liberty of opinion which the majority of the Church's representatives regarded as pernicious" (007-0597). This principle was formally established in 1865 by an Act of Parliament that altered the form of clerical subscription to the Thirty-nine Articles.

> This was a great triumph for the Broad Church party... Laymen decided (overruling the opinion of the Archbishops of Canterbury and York) what theological doctrines are and are not binding on a clergyman, and granted within the Church a liberty of opinion which the majority of the Church's representatives regarded as pernicious.
> (007-0597)

### Influence and Legacy

The liberal views of the Broad Churchmen gradually permeated the wider Church. "The liberal views of the Broad Churchmen and their attitude to the Bible gradually produced some effect upon those who differed most from them; and nowadays there is probably no one who would not admit, at least, that such a passage as Genesis, Chapter XIX, might have been composed without the direct inspiration of the Deity" (007-0608). This was a remarkable transformation: ideas that had been prosecuted as heretical in 1860 had, within a generation, become accepted even by theological conservatives.

Bury also notes the influence of "the Broad Church movement and of Darwinism" in creating an environment where rationalist arguments could be published "with impunity" (007-0689). By the 1870s, a "new, undogmatic Christianity was being preached in pulpits," and Leslie Stephen could observe that "there is not only no article in the creeds which may not be contradicted with impunity, but that there is none which may not be contradicted in a sermon calculated to win the reputation of orthodoxy" (007-0689).

### Criticism from Freethinkers

The Broad Church did not satisfy all advocates of intellectual liberty. **Leslie Stephen** (1832–1904), the agnostic man of letters, viewed its compromises with suspicion. "The Broad Church movement, the attempts to liberalize Christianity, to pour its old wine into new bottles, to make it unsectarian and undogmatic, to find compromises between theology and science, found no favour in Leslie Stephen's eyes, and he criticized all this with a certain contempt" (007-0767). For Stephen and other thoroughgoing rationalists, the Broad Church's halfway position — accepting critical methods while retaining Christian doctrine — was intellectually unsustainable.

## Key Proponents

- **Benjamin Jowett** (1817–1893): Master of Balliol College, Oxford, whose 1855 edition of St. Paul's Epistles "contained an annihilating criticism of the doctrine of the Atonement, an explicit rejection of original sin, and a rationalistic discussion of the question of God's existence" (007-0532). Jowett was a leading contributor to *Essays and Reviews*.
- **Frederick Temple** (1821–1902): Contributor to *Essays and Reviews* who later became Archbishop of Canterbury, illustrating how Broad Church ideas moved from the margins to the center of the Anglican establishment.
- **Rowland Williams** (1817–1870) and **Henry Bristow Wilson** (1803–1888): The two contributors prosecuted for heresy, whose eventual acquittal established the legal precedent for liberty of opinion within the Church.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The Broad Church movement advanced freedom of thought in three significant ways. First, it demonstrated that critical biblical scholarship — pioneered in Germany — could be practiced within the institutional church, not merely from outside it. Second, the legal battles over *Essays and Reviews* established the important precedent that the state would not enforce theological orthodoxy against clergymen who held liberal views (007-0597). Third, by "gradually producing some effect upon those who differed most from them" (007-0608), the Broad Churchmen helped create a climate in which even conservative believers could accept that the Bible might contain human as well as divine elements.

Bury's account suggests that the Broad Church was both a cause and a symptom of the broader liberalization of religious thought in Victorian England. Its ultimate significance lay not in its theology — which freethinkers like Stephen found insufficiently radical — but in its institutional achievement: securing a space for critical inquiry within the establishment church.
