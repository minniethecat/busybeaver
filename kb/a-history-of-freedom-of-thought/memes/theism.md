## Definition and Origins

Theism, in the context of J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes belief in a personal God who actively governs the universe and is concerned with human affairs. In antiquity, the Homeric poems were "supposed to be the word of God" and formed the basis of Greek religious education (002-049). The earliest Greek philosophers began questioning this traditional theism, with some replacing personal gods with abstract principles.

Ancient theism was inherently tied to civic order. The gods were guardians of the state, and questioning their existence was considered both impiety and political subversion. This connection between theistic belief and social order persisted throughout Western history, shaping the boundaries within which freedom of thought could operate.

## Historical Development

The rational critique of traditional theism began with the pre-Socratic philosophers and reached its classical expression in Socrates, who declared "I will obey God" rather than the Athenians, redefining the concept of divine authority (002-232). Plato and Aristotle developed philosophical theisms that conceived of God as a perfect being or unmoved mover, distinct from the anthropomorphic deities of popular religion.

With the rise of Christianity, theism became systematized into orthodox doctrine. The Church taught that "God punishes theological error," making correct belief in God a matter of eternal consequence (003-017). This doctrine of exclusive salvation gave the clergy immense power over intellectual life, as Bury documents throughout the medieval period.

During the Enlightenment, theism underwent a profound transformation. Deism emerged as a rational form of theism that accepted God as creator while rejecting revelation and miracles (006-087). Some thinkers argued that belief in God was essential for morality—"the taking away of God" was thought to remove the foundation of ethics (005-207). Yet Pierre Bayle challenged this assumption, arguing that morality and theistic belief could be separated (006-172).

## Key Proponents

- **Socrates** redefined divine obedience from institutional to personal conscience, declaring that his inner divine voice took precedence over civic commands (002-232).
- **John Locke** argued for religious toleration while affirming theism as the foundation of moral and political order. His *Letter Concerning Toleration* extended toleration "to deists as to atheists" only partially, reflecting the limits of theistic tolerance (005-215).
- **Voltaire** was a deist who "ardently combated atheism" while fiercely opposing ecclesiastical authority (006-484). His theism was rational and anticlerical.
- **Spinoza** transformed traditional theism into pantheism, identifying God with Nature. His view "may be described as pantheism" and was widely condemned as atheism by his contemporaries (003-327).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Theism's relationship with freedom of thought has been profoundly ambivalent. On one hand, the belief that an all-powerful God demands correct belief has been one of the principal sources of religious persecution throughout history. Bury documents how the doctrine that God "punishes theological error" led directly to intolerance (003-252). The fear of divine wrath motivated centuries of suppression of unorthodox ideas.

On the other hand, theistic belief also provided a foundation for arguments for toleration. Locke reasoned that genuine religious belief cannot be coerced, since God desires voluntary faith rather than forced conformity. The concept of conscience as a divine gift that must remain free from human interference became a powerful argument for intellectual liberty.

The gradual separation of theism from coercive enforcement—the recognition that belief in God must be freely chosen rather than legally mandated—represents one of the central achievements in the history of freedom of thought. This evolution transformed theism from a doctrine of control into a matter of individual conscience.

## Criticism and Opposition

Critics of traditional theism advanced several arguments. The problem of evil—"God either wishes to abolish evil" and cannot, or can but will not—posed a logical challenge to the concept of an all-powerful and benevolent deity (002-572). Epicurus formulated this dilemma in antiquity, and it remained a central argument against theism through the Enlightenment.

Atheists rejected theism entirely. The "speculative opinion of atheism" was uncommon before the Enlightenment but gradually gained ground (005-217). Deists sought a middle path, accepting a creator God while rejecting revelation, miracles, and ecclesiastical authority. Pantheists identified God with Nature, dissolving the distinction between creator and creation.
