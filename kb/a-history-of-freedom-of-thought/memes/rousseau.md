# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

**Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778) was a Genevan-born philosopher, writer, and composer whose ideas profoundly influenced the [[Enlightenment]] and the [[French Revolution]]. Though of Swiss birth, Rousseau belongs to the literature and history of France; but it was not for nothing that he was brought up in the republican atmosphere of Geneva (005-344). He contributed to the growth of freedom in a different way from his contemporaries (006-552).

## Life and Background

Rousseau was born in Geneva to a watchmaker father. His mother died shortly after his birth. Largely self-educated, he moved to Paris in his twenties and became involved with the [[Encyclopédie]] project, contributing articles on music. His early discourse *On the Arts and Sciences* (1750) won the Dijon Academy prize and established him as a major intellectual figure.

His relationship with other philosophes, particularly [[Voltaire]] and [[Diderot]], was stormy. By the 1760s his works had made him a fugitive. The authorities condemned both *Émile* and *The Social Contract* in 1762: the book was publicly burned in Paris and an order issued for Rousseau's arrest. Forced by his friends to flee, he was debarred from returning to Geneva, for the government of that canton followed the lead of Paris (006-564). After years of wandering, he eventually returned to France, where he was left unmolested till his death (006-572).

## Major Contributions

Rousseau's major works transformed European thought across multiple domains:

| Work | Year | Domain | Key Idea |
|------|------|--------|----------|
| *Discourse on Inequality* | 1755 | Political philosophy | Private property as the origin of inequality |
| *The Social Contract* | 1762 | Political theory | The general will and popular sovereignty |
| *Émile* | 1762 | Education | Natural education and developmental stages |

It was by his daring social and political theories that he challenged the established order (006-573). The proclamation that man's felicity depends not on Revelation but on social transformation — this was what Diderot and Rousseau in their different ways did so much to effect (006-601).

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Rousseau's relationship to [[freedom of thought]] is complex and paradoxical. On one hand, his political theories provided a language of popular sovereignty that inspired democratic movements. On the other, his concept of civil religion — a set of beliefs that all citizens must profess — denies the principle of toleration:

> By imposing indispensable beliefs, it denies the principle of toleration. The importance of Rousseau's idea lies in the fact that it inspired one of the experiments in religious policy during the French Revolution.
> (005-356)

Thus, for a few months during the Revolution, Rousseau's idea was more or less realized (005-407). The experiment in civil religion — the Cult of the Supreme Being under [[Robespierre]] — demonstrated the dangerous proximity between enforced civic belief and intolerance.

[[John Stuart Mill]] later addressed Rousseau's doctrines as an example of opinions that might conceivably have been suppressed as pernicious, yet which carried forward truths that the popular view had neglected (008-118, 008-122).

## Religious Views

Rousseau was a deist, but his deism, unlike that of [[Voltaire]], was religious and emotional. He regarded Christianity with a sort of reverence, even as he rejected its supernatural claims (006-553). His religion was the "purified Christianity of Rousseau" — deeply felt but anterior and superior to Christian orthodoxy (005-429).

> The religious views of Rousseau are only a minor point in his heretical speculations. It was by his daring social and political theories that he challenged the established order.
> (006-573)

Deism — whether in the semi-Christian form of Rousseau or the anti-Christian form of Voltaire — was, in Bury's view, a house built on the sand (006-582). Yet the emotional power of Rousseau's religious vision influenced Romantic and liberal religious movements throughout the nineteenth century.

## Legacy and Influence

Rousseau's influence radiated in multiple, sometimes contradictory directions. His educational ideas in *Émile* transformed pedagogy. His political theory of the general will was claimed by both democrats and authoritarians. His critique of civilization — the "false doctrine of Rousseau that man is a being who is naturally good" (005-462) — challenged the Enlightenment's faith in progress even as it contributed to it.

Without the work of Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, and their fellow-combatants, would the Catholic Church in France have been reformed? (006-605). His doctrines, which might conceivably have been suppressed as pernicious, in fact contained truths that have floated down the stream of opinion (008-122, 008-123).
