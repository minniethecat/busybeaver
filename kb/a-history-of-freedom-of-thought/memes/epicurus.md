# Epicurus

**Epicurus** (341–270 BC) was an ancient Greek philosopher whose materialist account of the universe and anti-religious teachings made him one of the most important precursors of the rationalist tradition in Western thought. Bury identifies his philosophy as having "a distinct anti-religious tendency," and Epicurus's central aim — to liberate the human mind from fear of divine punishment — aligns him directly with the history of [[freethought]].

## Life and Background

Born on the island of Samos to Athenian parents, Epicurus began studying philosophy at an early age. He was influenced by the atomistic theories of [[Democritus]] and developed his own philosophical system in Athens, where he established a school known as "the Garden." Unlike the Academy of [[Plato]] or the Lyceum of [[Aristotle]], the Garden admitted women and slaves, reflecting Epicurus's indifference to social convention. He lived a simple, communal life with his followers and died in Athens in 270 BC.

## Major Contributions

Epicurus's philosophy rested on a materialist understanding of the universe. He adopted the atomic theory of [[Democritus]], explaining all phenomena as the result of atoms moving in void, and denied any form of divine government of the world:

> [002-296] He was a Materialist, explaining the world by the atomic theory of Democritus and denying any divine government of the universe.

Although Epicurus did not deny the existence of gods outright, his conception of the divine rendered it irrelevant to human concerns:

> [002-298] He did indeed hold the existence of gods, but, so far as men are concerned, his gods are as if they were not — living in some remote abode and enjoying a "sacred and everlasting calm."

This theology — or effectively, atheology — served Epicurus's ethical purpose. If the gods neither intervene in the world nor concern themselves with human conduct, then humans have no reason to fear divine wrath. All that remains is to pursue a life of tranquil pleasure (*ataraxia*), understood not as hedonistic excess but as the absence of physical pain and mental disturbance.

## Critique of Religion

The anti-religious core of Epicurus's teaching lay in his analysis of the origins and function of religion. He identified fear as the psychological foundation of religious belief:

> [002-294] of Epicurus had a distinct anti-religious tendency. He considered fear to be the fundamental motive of religion, and to free men's minds from this fear was a principal object of his teaching.

This psychological critique of religion — that belief in gods arises from and perpetuates fear — was one of the most enduring contributions of Epicurean thought to the tradition of [[rationalism]]. It anticipated the critiques of religion advanced by later thinkers from [[Lucretius]] to [[David Hume]] and [[Baron d'Holbach]].

## Legacy and Influence

The influence of Epicurus on the history of [[freethought]] has been profound and enduring. His ideas were propagated throughout the Greek and Roman worlds from the third century BC onward, and Bury notes that from this time "most well-educated Greeks were more or less rationalists" (002-293). The Roman poet [[Lucretius]] (first century BC) regarded Epicurus as a heroic liberator of humanity and dedicated his great poem *De Rerum Natura* to expounding Epicurean philosophy:

> [002-305] (first century B.C.) regarded Epicurus as the great deliverer of the human race and determined to proclaim the glad tidings of his philosophy in a poem On the Nature of the World.

Epicurus's ideas resurfaced powerfully during the [[Renaissance]] and [[Enlightenment]]. [[Giordano Bruno]] drew on Epicurus's theory of the infinity of the universe:

> [004-240] Epicurus, from whom he took the theory of the infinity of the universe.

The atomistic and anti-religious dimensions of Epicurean philosophy contributed to the scientific revolution of the seventeenth century and the materialist philosophies of the eighteenth. In the history of [[freedom of thought]], Epicurus stands as one of the earliest and most influential thinkers to argue that the good life requires liberation from religious fear.
