## Definition and Origins

Historical criticism, as treated in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the method of subjecting religious texts—particularly the Bible—to the same critical historical analysis applied to other ancient documents. Rather than accepting Scripture as divinely inspired and therefore beyond question, historical critics examine its authorship, dating, consistency, and historical context using ordinary scholarly methods.

Bury identifies historical criticism as a distinctive achievement of modern thought, contrasting it with the pre-critical attitude of "a naïve and ignorant age" (006-012). The critical approach emerged from the confluence of Renaissance humanist scholarship, the Reformation's emphasis on reading Scripture, and the general advance of scientific method in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

## Historical Development

The process of "Biblical criticism has been conducted mainly in the Protestant countries of Europe," Bury notes, where the Reformation's emphasis on individual Bible reading paradoxically opened the text to closer scrutiny (004-213). Once the Bible was treated as a text to be studied rather than an oracle to be received, its inconsistencies and human origins became increasingly apparent.

The eighteenth century brought a more aggressive critical stance. Bury records that the Bible's authority was "undermined by historical criticism, which has been more deadly than the common-sense criticism of the eighteenth century" (007-0297–0298). The earlier deists had pointed out moral and logical problems in Scripture; the historical critics went further by demonstrating that the texts themselves were products of particular times and cultures.

The nineteenth century saw historical criticism mature into an academic discipline. Bury observes that "the havoc which science and historical criticism have wrought among traditional beliefs" was profound, destroying the foundations of biblical literalism and forcing theology to adapt or retreat (007-0422).

## Key Proponents

- **Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677), whose *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) was a landmark of early biblical criticism. Spinoza argued that the Bible must be understood in its historical context and that Moses could not have written the entire Pentateuch.
- **Richard Simon** (1638–1712), a French Oratorian priest whose critical studies of the Old and New Testaments helped found modern biblical scholarship, though his work was suppressed by the Catholic Church.
- **David Hume** (1711–1776), whose essay "On Miracles" provided a philosophical foundation for critical assessment of supernatural claims in Scripture. Bury notes that the traditional Christian evidences were reduced to "form by the criticisms of Hume and Middleton" (006-747).
- **David Friedrich Strauss** (1808–1874), whose *Life of Jesus Critically Examined* (1835) applied historical-critical methods to the Gospels, treating them as mythological constructions rather than factual reports.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Historical criticism advanced freedom of thought by demonstrating that the most heavily guarded text in Western civilization—the Bible—could be studied, questioned, and analyzed like any other human document. This was a decisive blow against the claim that religious authority was immune to rational scrutiny.

Bury emphasizes that historical criticism was "more deadly than the common-sense criticism of the eighteenth century" because it did not merely point out contradictions or moral problems; it undermined the very framework of biblical authority by revealing the human processes behind the text's composition (007-0297). Once the Bible could be seen as a historical artifact, the door was open to treating all religious claims as subjects for historical investigation.

The critical method also fostered intellectual virtues essential to free inquiry: the willingness to follow evidence wherever it leads, the suspension of preconceived conclusions, and the recognition that all texts—however sacred—are products of human hands and historical circumstances.
