## Definition and Origins

Hell, as analyzed in Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the doctrine of eternal punishment after death for those who hold incorrect religious beliefs or commit moral transgressions. The concept served as one of the most powerful psychological sanctions in the history of religion, providing supernatural enforcement for doctrinal conformity. Bury observes that "a remark was made about hell" that exposed how even casual discussion of the doctrine could be treated as threatening to the established order (001-257).

The doctrine of hell emerged from ancient Near Eastern and Greek conceptions of the underworld, transformed by Christian theology into a place of eternal conscious torment. The crucial innovation of Christian eschatology was linking damnation specifically to incorrect belief—those who held heretical views would suffer not merely temporal punishment but "the tortures awaiting them in hell" for eternity (003-023).

## Historical Development

Throughout the medieval period, the fear of hell was the Church's most potent weapon for enforcing orthodoxy. The threat of eternal torment made heresy the most serious of crimes, since it endangered not only the heretic's soul but those of others who might be led astray. Bury documents how "the cruel enactments against" heretics were justified by the greater cruelty of the hell that awaited the unrepentant (003-142).

The doctrine of hell was closely associated with related concepts of "sin, hell, and" divine judgment, forming a coherent system of supernatural rewards and punishments (003-231). This system gave the clergy enormous power, since they alone could administer the sacraments necessary to escape damnation. The "intolerance arising from" the belief in hell was direct and logical: if you truly believed your neighbor faced eternal torture for wrong belief, coercing them into orthodoxy was an act of charity (003-029).

In the Enlightenment, the doctrine of hell came under sustained rational criticism. Freethinkers argued that "the scheme of heaven and hell, with the" threat of eternal punishment, was incompatible with a benevolent deity (006-409). The poet Shelley was a particularly vocal critic; "Shelley addressed" the moral horror of eternal punishment in his writings, and his drama *Atalanta in Calydon* explored themes of divine cruelty (006-870, 007-0624).

The nineteenth century saw a "controversy about hell" that "engaged public attention," signaling the doctrine's declining hold on educated opinion (007-0787). Increasingly, freethinkers argued "that heaven and hell belong to dreamland," products of human imagination rather than divine revelation (007-0800). The gradual acceptance that it was not "essential for a clergyman to believe in eternal punishment" marked a decisive shift in the intellectual climate (007-0592).

## Key Critics

- **Percy Bysshe Shelley** was a "declared atheist" who condemned the doctrine of hell as morally abhorrent in both poetry and prose (007-0622).
- **Francis William Newman** contributed to the nineteenth-century controversy about hell, arguing that the dogma was incompatible with a rational and ethical religion (007-0787).
- **John Stuart Mill** criticized the moral logic of eternal punishment, arguing that a finite crime cannot merit infinite torment.
- **The Deists** rejected hell as a priestly invention designed to control the laity through fear.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The doctrine of hell was arguably the single most powerful obstacle to freedom of thought in Western history. The belief that unorthodox opinions would lead to "eternal hell fire" created an overwhelming incentive for intellectual conformity (006-461). The fear of damnation was far more effective than any secular punishment in suppressing dissent, since it operated on conscience rather than mere behavior.

The decline of belief in hell was therefore one of the most consequential developments in the history of freethought. As the doctrine lost its hold on educated minds, the psychological foundation of religious intolerance crumbled. If hell did not exist, there was no eternal reason to enforce orthodoxy; if "that dogma had not an intellectual basis," the entire edifice of coercive religion was undermined (007-0795).

Bury's treatment of hell illustrates a broader pattern in his history: the most effective arguments for freedom of thought were not those that directly asserted the right to dissent, but those that undermined the theological premises that made persecution seem necessary. By discrediting hell, freethinkers removed the motive for intolerance.
