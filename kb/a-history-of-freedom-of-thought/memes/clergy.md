## Definition and Origins

The clergy, in Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, designates the ordained officials of the Christian Church who held spiritual authority over doctrine and worship. The clergy were central to the enforcement of religious orthodoxy and consequently became primary targets of freethought criticism. Their distinctive position derived from the claim to mediate between God and humanity, interpreting divine will and administering salvation.

The emergence of a professional clerical class fundamentally shaped the development of intellectual history in the West. As the guardians of revealed truth, the clergy controlled education, censored books, and determined which ideas could safely circulate. This institutional power made them, depending on the period, either patrons of learning or obstacles to intellectual progress.

## Historical Development

In the medieval period, the clergy held near-absolute authority over intellectual life. The doctrine of exclusive salvation—that the Church alone could grant access to heaven—gave the clergy "enormous influence" over both rulers and subjects (007-0483). The threat of excommunication and eternal damnation provided powerful sanctions against heterodox thought.

The Reformation challenged clerical authority by asserting the priesthood of all believers, yet both Protestant and Catholic clergies continued to enforce doctrinal conformity within their respective domains. The "French clergy justified this policy" of religious uniformity by appealing to divine command (005-286). During the Revolution, the French state stripped the "Catholic clergy the salaries which the State" had previously provided, fundamentally altering the clergy's relationship to political power (005-420).

In England, the clergy's influence persisted into the nineteenth century. The requirement that Oxford and Cambridge fellows be ordained meant that "clergymen" dominated English higher education, controlling the terms of intellectual debate (007-0543). Writers "of whom six were clergymen" shaped the public discussion of religion and science, constraining the range of acceptable opinion (007-0545).

## Key Proponents and Critics

The clergy themselves were not a monolithic force. Liberal clergymen within the Church of England, including "Oxford men," sought to reconcile Christian doctrine with modern scholarship (007-0529). By the later nineteenth century, it was no longer "essential for a clergyman to believe in eternal punishment," reflecting the erosion of traditional doctrinal requirements (007-0592).

Among the clergy's most powerful critics was Voltaire, who attacked ecclesiastical authority as inherently opposed to reason and liberty. Frederick the Great's "local clergy" "but for Frederick would" have suppressed heterodox thought more aggressively (006-570). John Locke, from within a Christian framework, argued that "the clergy step beyond their province" when they sought to exercise temporal power over conscience (005-520).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The clergy's relationship to freedom of thought was fundamentally dualistic. As the custodians of revealed religion, they were the primary agents of censorship and persecution. The doctrine that "salvation is to be found exclusively in" the Church's teaching gave clerics both the motive and the means to suppress dissent (003-015). Throughout Bury's narrative, the clergy appear consistently as the institutional force most resistant to intellectual liberty.

Yet individual clergymen also contributed to the advancement of freethought. The participation of ordained scholars in biblical criticism, evolutionary biology, and historical research gradually undermined the very dogmas their office was meant to defend. Bury notes that views once considered radical "were accepted by most well-educated clergymen" by the late nineteenth century, demonstrating the clergy's gradual accommodation to modern thought (007-0545).

The struggle between clerical authority and intellectual freedom constitutes one of the central threads of Bury's history. The gradual retreat of the clergy from their position as arbiters of permissible belief represents a major victory for freedom of thought, achieved not through revolution but through the slow accumulation of critical scholarship and the expansion of the reading public.
