## Definition and Origins

Evolution by natural selection is the scientific theory explaining the diversity of life through gradual change over geological time, driven by variation and differential survival. In J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, evolution—specifically Darwinism—is treated as one of the most consequential intellectual developments of the nineteenth century for its challenge to traditional religious accounts of creation.

The publication of "Darwin's *Origin of Species* in 1859" marked a watershed moment in intellectual history (007-0071). Darwin proposed that species, including humans, arose through natural processes rather than divine creation. Bury notes that "Darwin said that man descended from" other animals, directly challenging the biblical account of humanity's special creation "in his own image" (007-0080).

## Historical Development

Darwin's work did not emerge in isolation. Earlier thinkers had proposed evolutionary ideas, but Darwin provided the mechanism—natural selection—and an overwhelming body of evidence. Bury compares Darwin's achievement to Newton's "discovery of gravitation," though he acknowledges that Darwin "did not, as is now recognized," get everything right (007-0086).

"Darwinism, as it is called, has had" effects far beyond biology (007-0094). The theory was applied to social, political, and philosophical questions, sometimes in ways Darwin himself would not have endorsed. Herbert Spencer and others developed comprehensive evolutionary philosophies that explained everything from the development of stars to the progress of civilization in evolutionary terms.

The intellectual climate shifted rapidly. Darwin's *Descent of Man* "had an enormous" circulation and influence (007-0210). By the late nineteenth century, the "Broad Church movement and Darwinism" had together reshaped religious thought in Britain (007-0689). Even those who rejected Darwin's conclusions could no longer ignore the evidence he had assembled.

Bury describes how the intellectual world had moved "far from the days when Darwin's *Descent* created an uproar" (007-0925). Within a single generation, what had been scandalous became established science.

## Key Proponents

- **Charles Darwin** published *On the Origin of Species* (1859) and *The Descent of Man* (1871), providing the evidence and mechanism for evolution. Bury notes that "Darwin showed how the" diversity of life could be explained naturalistically (007-0118).
- **Thomas Henry Huxley**, known as "Darwin's Bulldog," defended evolutionary theory in public debates and popularized its implications for religion and philosophy.
- **Herbert Spencer** developed a comprehensive philosophy of evolution, applying the principle to society, psychology, and ethics. He "accepted Darwinism" as part of his broader evolutionary worldview (007-0582).
- **Ernst Haeckel** promoted Darwinism in Germany and extended evolutionary thinking to human embryology and philosophy.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

The impact of evolution on freedom of thought was transformative. By providing a naturalistic explanation for the origin of species, Darwin "demand[ed] the return of my money" from traditional theology—he showed that religious explanations were unnecessary for understanding the natural world (007-0118).

Evolutionary theory contributed to freethought in several ways: it undermined the argument from design, which had been a principal rational support for theism; it demonstrated that complex systems could arise through natural processes without intelligent guidance; and it placed humanity firmly within the natural world rather than above it.

Bury observes that "there is nothing to" fear from accepting evolutionary science (007-0249). The theory did not destroy morality or meaning, as its critics feared, but rather opened new avenues for understanding humanity's place in nature. The successful integration of evolutionary science into mainstream thought represented a victory for reason and evidence over dogma.
