## Life and Background

Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895) was an English biologist, comparative anatomist, and scientific educator. Self-taught in many respects, he rose to become one of the most prominent public intellectuals of Victorian Britain, serving as President of the Royal Society. He earned the nickname "Darwin's Bulldog" for his fierce and effective defence of Charles Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection.

## Major Contributions

Huxley's scientific work spanned invertebrate zoology, palaeontology, and human evolution. He was among the first to argue that birds evolved from dinosaurs and that humans shared common ancestry with apes — a position that directly challenged the doctrine of special creation.

Beyond his biological research, Huxley made lasting contributions as an essayist and public lecturer on the relationship between science and religion. His 1874 essay *Animal Automatism* advanced a materialist account of consciousness, arguing that animals (including humans) could be understood as conscious automata (007-1067). In 1876, he delivered a lecture at the British Association that helped define the agnostic position for a broad public audience.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Huxley coined the term "agnostic" (from Greek *agnostos*, "unknowable") to describe his philosophical position: that the existence or nature of God is unknown and likely unknowable. The agnostic, in Huxley's formulation, neither affirms nor denies God's existence but holds that the question lies beyond the reach of human reason and empirical evidence.

Bury references Huxley in the context of the rationalist critique of miracles. Thomas Woolston, an earlier deist critic, had pointed out that the miraculous driving of devils into a herd of swine constituted an injury to property — a line of argument "as Huxley was to point out in a controversy with Gladstone" (006-275). This controversy, in which Huxley engaged the former Prime Minister on the credibility of the Gadarene swine miracle, demonstrated the application of common-sense reasoning to scriptural claims.

Huxley's essay *Animal Automatism* is cited by Bury among the key rationalist works of 1874, alongside Tyndall's Belfast Address and Clifford's *Body and Mind* (007-1067). This places Huxley at the centre of the Victorian crisis of faith, when scientific naturalism directly confronted theological orthodoxy.

## Legacy and Influence

Huxley's legacy extends beyond his scientific discoveries to his role in professionalizing science and secularizing British intellectual life. He advocated for science education as an alternative to classical and theological curricula, and his grandchildren — including the writer Aldous Huxley and the biologist Julian Huxley — continued the family tradition of scientific humanism.

The term "agnostic" that Huxley coined has outlasted its Victorian origins and remains a significant category in contemporary discussions of belief and unbelief. Huxley's insistence that scientific claims must be testable and that theological claims are not — a position he called "agnosticism" — continues to inform debates about the scope and limits of human knowledge.
