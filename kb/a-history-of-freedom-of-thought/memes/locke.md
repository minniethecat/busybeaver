# John Locke

**John Locke** (1632–1704) was an English philosopher and physician, widely regarded as the father of [[liberalism]] and one of the most influential architects of [[toleration]] in the Western intellectual tradition.

His *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) provided the first systematic philosophical argument for religious liberty grounded in the nature of civil government, rather than in mere pragmatism or fatigue with sectarian violence.

## Life and Context

Locke was born in Wrington, Somerset, to a Puritan family. He studied at Christ Church, Oxford, where he was exposed to the new experimental philosophy of [[Robert Boyle]] and the mechanistic worldview that would inform his epistemology.

As physician and secretary to [[Anthony Ashley Cooper]], the first Earl of Shaftesbury, Locke was drawn into the political turmoil of the Exclusion Crisis and spent years in exile in the [[Netherlands]] (1683–1689), where he completed his major works in the relatively tolerant atmosphere of the Dutch Republic.

The England to which Locke returned after the [[Glorious Revolution]] of 1688 had just deposed a Catholic king. Religious conflict between Anglicans, Dissenters, and Catholics had convulsed the country for over a century. It was in this context — of civil war, persecution, and fragile settlement — that Locke wrote his *Letter*.

## The *Letter Concerning Toleration*

Published anonymously in Latin in 1689 (the English translation appeared later the same year), Locke's *Letter* advances a revolutionary thesis: the civil magistrate has no authority over the care of souls.

> [005-187] it is a principle on which Locke repeatedly insists. If a State…

The core argument runs as follows:

1. **The nature of belief**: Genuine religious belief cannot be compelled by external force. "The care of souls is not committed to the civil magistrate," because faith is an inward act of the understanding.

   Penalties can produce outward conformity but not sincere conviction.

2. **The limits of civil power**: The magistrate's authority extends only to "civil concernments" — life, liberty, health, and property.

   Religious worship and doctrine lie outside this sphere.

3. **The nature of the church**: A church is a voluntary society.

   No one is born into a church; membership is freely chosen and freely revocable.

> [005-165] In the same year John Locke's famous (first) Letter concerning Toleration was published. It was a powerful argument for religious liberty, and it had a profound effect on public opinion.

### Limitations of Locke's Toleration

Locke's argument, for all its historical importance, had notable limits:

> [005-211] Thus Locke is not free from the prejudice…

- **Atheists**: Locke would not extend toleration to atheists, arguing that oaths and promises — the bonds of civil society — have no hold on one who does not believe in a punishing God.

- **Catholics**: Roman Catholics were excluded, not for their religious beliefs as such, but because their allegiance to a foreign prince (the Pope) made them, in Locke's view, a political threat.

- **Muslims and Jews**: Locke's toleration was theoretically universal across Protestant denominations, but he was ambiguous about non-Christian faiths.

> [005-215] …ts, to atheists as to deists. Locke,

These exclusions reflect the political anxieties of 1689 rather than the logical terminus of Locke's own principles. Later thinkers, including [[Pierre Bayle]] and [[Voltaire]], would push the argument for toleration beyond Locke's boundaries by demonstrating the inconsistency of excepting any class from the general principle.

## Influence on the Enlightenment

Locke's political thought shaped the [[Enlightenment]] across Europe. His *Two Treatises of Government* supplied the theoretical framework for constitutional limited government.

His *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1690) provided the empiricist epistemology — the mind as *tabula rasa*, knowledge limited to experience — that became the methodological foundation of [[rationalism]] in the eighteenth century.

> [005-258] …logical results of Locke's theory of civil government.

In [[France]], [[Voltaire]] popularized Locke's philosophy in his *Lettres philosophiques* (1734), presenting the English thinker as the antithesis of Cartesian dogmatism. [[Montesquieu]]'s *Spirit of the Laws* developed Locke's separation of powers.

In [[America]], [[Thomas Jefferson]] drew on Locke for the [[Declaration of Independence]] and the [[Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom]].

## Locke and the Growth of Rationalism

Locke's significance for [[freedom of thought]] extends beyond toleration. By arguing that reason, not authority, is the arbiter of truth, he contributed to the broader shift from revealed to natural religion:
