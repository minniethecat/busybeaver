# David Friedrich Strauss

**David Friedrich Strauss** (1808–1874) was a German theologian and writer whose *Life of Jesus* (1835) applied rationalist and mythological analysis to the New Testament, rejecting all supernatural elements in the Gospel narratives.

His work marks the beginning of modern biblical criticism and made him one of the most controversial and influential figures in the nineteenth-century conflict between [[reason]] and orthodox [[Christianity]].

## Life and Background

Strauss was born in Ludwigsburg, Württemberg, and studied theology at the University of Tübingen, where he came under the influence of the philosopher [[G. W. F. Hegel]] and the biblical critic Ferdinand Christian Baur.

After a brief period as a village pastor, he returned to Tübingen as a lecturer in philosophy. The publication of his *Life of Jesus* in 1835 ended his academic career almost immediately, as the ensuing controversy led to his dismissal. He spent the remainder of his life as a freelance writer and intellectual.

## The Life of Jesus and Modern Biblical Criticism

Strauss's *Life of Jesus* (1835) represented a watershed in the critical study of the New Testament. Building on the historical-critical methods of his teacher Baur, Strauss argued that the miraculous elements of the Gospels were not historical events but mythological expressions of early Christian religious ideas:

> [007-0363] Modern criticism of the New Testament began with the stimulating works of Baur and of Strauss, whose Life of Jesus (1835), in which the supernatural was entirely rejected, had an immense success and caused furious controversy.

Both Strauss and Baur were influenced by Hegelian philosophy, which provided a framework for understanding religious ideas as symbolic representations of philosophical truths. Strauss's mythological interpretation of the Gospels opened a new era in which the Bible could be studied as a human document subject to the same critical methods as any other ancient text.

## Persecution and Academic Consequences

The controversy provoked by Strauss's work had severe professional consequences, illustrating the institutional resistance that rationalist biblical critics faced:

> [007-0424] controversy was not the only weapon employed. Strauss was deprived of his professorship at Tübingen, and his career was ruined.

This pattern was repeated across Europe in the nineteenth century. [[Ernest Renan]], whose own *Life of Jesus* (1863) also rejected the supernatural, lost his chair at the Collège de France. The materialist philosopher [[Ludwig Büchner]] was driven from Tübingen in 1855 for his book *Force and Matter*, and an attempt was made to expel the biologist [[Ernst Haeckel]] from Jena.

The case of Strauss was thus not an isolated incident but part of a systematic effort by religious and academic authorities to suppress rationalist scholarship on the Bible and on the natural world.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Strauss's work advanced the cause of [[freedom of thought]] by demonstrating that the central texts of Christianity could and must be subjected to historical and critical scrutiny. By treating the Gospels as human documents produced within a specific cultural and religious context, Strauss challenged the doctrine of biblical infallibility at its foundations.

His mythological theory — though itself subject to scholarly revision — established the principle that the truth of Christianity must be compatible with critical reason, a principle that became central to liberal Protestant theology and to the broader freethought tradition.

## Legacy and Influence

Strauss's *Life of Jesus* was translated into English by the novelist [[George Eliot]] (Mary Ann Evans) in 1846, bringing his ideas to the English-speaking world. The rationalist biblical criticism he pioneered, carried forward by the Tübingen School and later by scholars such as [[Julius Wellhausen]] and [[Albert Schweitzer]], transformed the academic study of religion.

In the history of [[freethought]], Strauss stands as a central figure in the nineteenth-century effort to bring the methods of reason and historical criticism to bear upon the foundational texts of Western religion.
