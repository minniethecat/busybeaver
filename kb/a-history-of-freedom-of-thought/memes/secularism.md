## Definition and Origins

Secularism, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, is the principle that government, education, and public life should operate independently of religious authority. Bury describes secularism as "derived from the Humanists, and always implying scepticism, whether latent or conscious," and notes that it "substituted an interest in the fortunes of the human race upon earth for the interest in a future world" (004-392–393).

The roots of secularism lie in the Renaissance Humanist movement, which emphasized human interests and worldly concerns alongside—and eventually in place of—purely theological preoccupations. By redirecting intellectual energy toward earthly affairs, the Humanists laid the groundwork for a worldview that did not require religious sanction for every aspect of life.

The "cultivation of secular knowledge" was, in Bury's words, "permanent and destined to lead" societies away from ecclesiastical domination of thought (004-386). This shift from otherworldly to worldly concerns was a crucial precondition for the emergence of modern secular states.

## Historical Development

The secular principle advanced gradually from the seventeenth through the nineteenth centuries. John Locke's *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) provided an early and influential articulation. Bury notes that Locke's work "asserts unrestrictedly the secular principle, and its logical issue is Disestablishment," defining a church as "merely a free and voluntary society" (005-222). This represented a radical departure from the medieval assumption that religious and political authority were inseparable.

The process of "secularization and the separation of the Church from the State" accelerated during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries (005-257). In England, Bury observes that "secularization has advanced under the State Church system," suggesting that even established churches could not prevent the gradual transfer of authority to secular institutions (005-257).

The nineteenth century saw the emergence of organized secular movements. Advocates of secular education pushed for public schooling free from ecclesiastical control, a development that was "an unwelcome sign of the times" for religious authorities (007-0644). Some radicals went further, seeking to "undermine revealed religion by a secular system of education" (005-417).

## Key Proponents

- **John Locke**, whose *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) laid the philosophical foundation for the secular state by arguing that religious belief could not be coerced and that civil government had no jurisdiction over souls.
- **George Jacob Holyoake**, whom Bury calls "the apostle of 'secularism'" (007-0907). Holyoake coined the term "secularism" in 1851 to describe a system of social ethics based on human concerns rather than religious doctrine. He was imprisoned for blasphemy in his early years.
- **Charles Bradlaugh**, a prominent secularist who, alongside Holyoake, led the British freethought movement. Bury records that Bradlaugh's greatest achievement was "the securing of the right of unbelievers to sit in Parliament without taking an oath" in 1888 (007-0907).

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Secularism advanced freedom of thought by establishing that civic participation, education, and public discourse need not be governed by religious tests. By separating the spheres of religion and government, secularism created protected space for dissent, inquiry, and the free exchange of ideas.

The secular principle challenged the ancient assumption that religious uniformity was necessary for social order. The logical conclusion of the secular argument was disestablishment—the end of state-supported churches and their privileged position in public life. This in turn meant that heresy, unbelief, and freethought could no longer be treated as crimes against the state.

Secularism also transformed education. When "entirely secular" institutions demonstrated their effectiveness, the case for ecclesiastical control of learning weakened (007-1044). Control of education had long been the Church's most powerful tool for shaping belief, making the struggle over secular education a key front in the broader campaign for intellectual liberty.
