# The Renaissance

The **Renaissance** (c. 1300–1600) — "rebirth" — was the intellectual and cultural movement that revived the study of classical antiquity and began the long process of freeing European thought from ecclesiastical authority.

Bury treats the Renaissance as the turning point at which [[reason]] began to emerge from its medieval imprisonment, although the full emancipation would require centuries more:

> [004-004] …the deliverance of reason from her prison began in Italy.

## Origins in Italy

The Renaissance originated in the city-states of northern [[Italy]] — [[Florence]], [[Venice]], [[Milan]], and [[Rome]] — in the fourteenth century. Several factors converged:

- **Proximity to classical remains**: Italy was littered with Roman ruins, inscriptions, and manuscripts preserved in monastic libraries. The physical presence of antiquity stimulated curiosity.

- **Wealth and patronage**: The commercial prosperity of Italian cities created a class of patrons — the [[Medici]] of Florence, the [[Sforza]] of Milan, the [[Papal Court]] — who competed to sponsor scholars, artists, and architects.

- **Byzantine émigrés**: The decline and fall of the [[Byzantine Empire]] (culminating in the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453) drove Greek scholars westward, bringing manuscripts and knowledge of classical Greek — a language largely lost to the medieval West.

- **The universities**: Although initially bastions of scholasticism, the universities eventually became centers for the study of classical texts and, increasingly, for critical scholarship.

## Petrarch and the Humanist Spirit

[[Petrarch]] (1304–1374) is conventionally called the first humanist. His passion for recovering the lost works of [[Cicero]] and [[Livy]], his scorn for the scholastic logic-chopping of the medieval universities, and his sense of living at the beginning of a new age marked a decisive break with the medieval mindset.

Petrarch conceived of the millennium between the fall of Rome and his own time as a "dark age" — a periodization that, though crude, expressed the conviction that civilization could be advanced by deliberate effort.

The humanists who followed [[Petrarch]] — [[Boccaccio]], [[Salutati]], [[Bruni]], [[Valla]] — developed the tools of critical scholarship.

[[Lorenzo Valla]]'s demonstration that the *Donation of Constantine* — the document on which the papacy based its claim to temporal power — was an eighth-century forgery was a landmark in the application of philological analysis to authoritative texts. If the Church could be wrong about a document it had relied on for centuries, what else might it be wrong about?

## Erasmus and the Northern Renaissance

The Northern Renaissance, centered in the [[Low Countries]] and the [[Rhine]] valley, gave humanism a more explicitly religious cast. Its towering figure was [[Erasmus]] of Rotterdam (1466–1536), the most celebrated scholar in Europe:

- His *Adages* (1500, expanded throughout his life) collected and commented on thousands of classical proverbs, disseminating ancient wisdom in accessible form.

- His *Praise of Folly* (1511) satirized the corruptions of the Church, the pedantry of the scholastics, and the superstition of popular religion — though from within, as a reformer, not as an opponent of Christianity.

- His critical edition of the Greek New Testament (1516) — the *Novum Instrumentum* — exposed errors in the Vulgate and made the original text available to scholars, undermining the Church's interpretive monopoly.

Erasmus refused to join [[Luther]]'s revolt, preferring reform from within, but his work supplied the scholarly foundation on which the [[Reformation]] built. His ideal — a tolerant, rational, ethically centered Christianity — was swept aside by the dogmatic passions of the sixteenth century, but it remained an inspiration for later advocates of [[toleration]].

## The Printing Press and the Democratization of Knowledge

[[Gutenberg]]'s invention of movable-type printing (c. 1450) was the technological catalyst that transformed the Renaissance from an elite movement into an irreversible cultural shift. By 1500, printing presses operated in over 250 European cities, and an estimated 20 million books had been produced. The effect was revolutionary:

| Domain | Before Print | After Print |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Textual accuracy | Manuscripts corrupted by generations of copyists | Standardized editions, critical comparison possible |
| Access to knowledge | Books rare and expensive; learning confined to clergy and wealthy | Books affordable to merchants, professionals, students |
| Speed of dissemination | Ideas spread slowly, locally | Print could make a scholar's work known across Europe in months |
| Censorship | Informal, localized | Required organized effort (Index of Prohibited Books, 1559) |
| Vernacular literacy | Latin the language of learning | Bibles and books in vernacular languages expanded readership |

Print did not itself guarantee freedom of thought — the Catholic Church's [[Index of Prohibited Books]] and Protestant censorship proved that the new technology could serve repression as well as liberation. But by making suppression harder and more expensive, print shifted the balance incrementally in favor of free inquiry.

## The Renaissance and the Emancipation of Reason

Bury is careful not to overstate the Renaissance's direct contribution to [[freedom of thought]]. Most humanists remained personally orthodox; many sought not to overthrow Christianity but to purify it. Nevertheless, the Renaissance made three irreversible contributions:

1. **Historical consciousness**: By recovering the texts of pagan antiquity and demonstrating that civilizations rose and fell, the Renaissance relativized Christian civilization and opened the possibility of secular historical analysis.

2. **Critical method**: Philology — the comparison of manuscripts, the analysis of language change, the detection of anachronism — provided a model of evidence-based scholarship applicable to sacred as well as secular texts.

3. **Secular intellectual space**: The studia humanitatis — grammar, rhetoric, history, poetry, moral philosophy — constituted a curriculum independent of theology, creating an institutional foothold for secular learning.

> [004-078] … in which the emancipation of reason could begin…
