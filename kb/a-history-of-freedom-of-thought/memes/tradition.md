## Definition and Origins

Tradition, in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes the inherited body of customs, doctrines, and institutional practices that claim authority over human belief by virtue of their antiquity.

Bury presents tradition as one of the most persistent obstacles to free thought. Unlike explicit censorship, it operates through the subtle weight of inherited assumptions and social conformity.

The authority of tradition rests on the assumption that what has long been believed must be true. This was deeply embedded in ancient societies, where it was held that "the welfare of a state depends on rigid stability and on the preservation of its traditions and institutions unchanged" (001-045).

## Historical Development

In the ancient world, tradition was the primary guarantor of social cohesion. Bury observes that the Greek city-states were "separate peoples, who varied largely in temper, customs and traditions" (002-021).

The Greek intellectual revolution — from Ionian philosophers to Socrates — represented the first systematic challenge to traditional authority. Thinkers began to "assail traditional beliefs" (002-047) without facing legal punishment, a crucial departure from ancient norms.

The rise of Christianity introduced a new dimension to tradition. The Church accumulated sacred tradition alongside Scripture as authoritative sources of truth. Bury notes that early Christianity was "incompatible with the traditions and basis of Roman society" (002-386), yet once established, it developed formidable structures of traditional authority.

The Protestant Reformation had an ambiguous relationship with tradition. Reformers challenged Catholic ecclesiastical tradition by insisting on *sola scriptura*. Bury records that in Protestant states "the religious tradition was broken" (004-176).

Yet Reformers quickly established their own traditional frameworks. Calvin's Geneva preserved "the traditions of Calvinistic" orthodoxy with rigorous enforcement (005-346).

The Enlightenment represented the most radical challenge to traditional authority. John Milton wrote that truth "flow not in a perpetual progression they sicken into a muddy pool of conformity and tradition" (005-143).

In the nineteenth century, historical criticism was applied to the Bible. Bury documents how "the old tradition that the Pentateuch was" written by Moses came under scholarly scrutiny (007-0314).

The discovery that "the Jews themselves handled their traditions freely" (007-0347) and had "no suspicion" of their constructed nature (007-0350) undermined traditional scriptural authority.

## Key Proponents and Critics

### Defenders of Tradition

Defenders argued that inherited institutions embody accumulated wisdom no single generation could replicate. Some rationalists acknowledged tradition's pragmatic role, noting that religion "is established by law and it has a long tradition behind it" (006-440).

Others held that "some traditional religion is necessary" (006-441) for social order, regardless of its truth claims.

### Critics of Tradition

- **John Milton** condemned the "muddy pool of conformity and tradition" stifling truth (005-143).
- **Baruch Spinoza** applied rational criticism to biblical texts, treating them as historical documents rather than repositories of infallible tradition.
- **John Locke** argued individuals must exercise private judgment in religion, rejecting tradition's claim to bind conscience.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Tradition is a uniquely insidious obstacle to free thought because it shapes the very categories within which people think. Deviation from inherited norms feels unnatural even where no law forbids it.

Bury's narrative measures the progress of freedom of thought by the gradual loosening of tradition's grip on the mind, not only by the abolition of legal penalties for dissent.

The critical breakthrough came when thinkers examined tradition historically. Instead of asking whether a tradition was true, they asked how it came to be established.

Historical criticism proved the most effective solvent of traditional authority. Applied first to religious texts, then to social institutions, it demonstrated that traditions were human constructions, not timeless truths.

The achievement of free thought was not to abolish tradition but to transform it from an unquestionable authority into a subject of critical examination. Traditions, like all human institutions, must justify themselves before reason.
