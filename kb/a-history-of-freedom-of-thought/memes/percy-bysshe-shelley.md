# Percy Bysshe Shelley

**Percy Bysshe Shelley** (1792–1822) was an English Romantic poet whose radical views on religion, politics, and morality made him one of the most prominent literary advocates of [[freethought]] in the nineteenth century. His declared atheism, his public opposition to blasphemy prosecutions, and his fearless denunciation of what he called "the tyranny of Gods and Governments" established him as a central figure in the Romantic-era challenge to orthodox [[Christianity]].

## Life and Background

Shelley was born into a wealthy Sussex family and educated at Eton and Oxford, from which he was expelled in 1811 for publishing *The Necessity of Atheism*, a pamphlet arguing against the existence of God. He lived much of his adult life in self-imposed exile in Italy, where he produced his major poetic works including *Prometheus Unbound*, *Adonais*, and the political poem *The Mask of Anarchy*. His circle included other freethinking literary figures such as [[Lord Byron]] and his second wife [[Mary Shelley]]. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy at the age of twenty-nine.

## Major Contributions

Shelley's literary output combined Romantic aesthetic sensibility with radical political and religious critique. His poetic drama *Prometheus Unbound* (1820) reimagined the Greek myth as an allegory of human liberation from divine tyranny. *Queen Mab* (1813), his early philosophical poem, attacked organized religion, monarchy, and marriage as instruments of oppression. His prose works, including *A Defence of Poetry* and the pamphlet *The Necessity of Atheism*, articulated a vision of poetry as a force for moral and political transformation independent of religious revelation.

## Opposition to Blasphemy Law

Shelley was an outspoken critic of the legal persecution of freethinkers and blasphemers in England. When the publisher Daniel Isaac Eaton was prosecuted and sentenced in 1812 for publishing parts of [[Thomas Paine]]'s *The Age of Reason*, Shelley addressed a public letter of protest to the presiding judge, Lord Ellenborough:

> [006-870] permitted." The poet Shelley addressed to Lord Ellenborough a scathing letter. "Do you think to convert Mr. Eaton to your religion by embittering his existence? You might force him by torture to profess your tenets, but he could not believe in them."

This defense of Eaton's right to publish heterodox opinions aligned Shelley with the broader movement for [[freedom of speech]] and freedom of conscience that characterized radical politics in the early nineteenth century.

## Declared Atheism and Influence

Bury identifies Shelley as the greatest English poet of his era and notes that his atheism was publicly known and unapologetic:

> [007-0622] Shelley, was a declared atheist. In fearless utterance, in unfaltering zeal against the tyranny of Gods and Governments, Swinburne was like Shelley.

The comparison to [[Algernon Charles Swinburne]] links Shelley to a later generation of poets who continued the tradition of literary freethought into the Victorian period:

> [007-0624] Shelley. His drama Atalanta in Calydon (1865), even though a poet is strictly not answerable for what the persons in his drama say, yet with its denunciation of "the supreme evil, God," heralded the coming generation of agnostic and atheist voices in English literature.

Together with [[Wordsworth]] (who was a pantheist in his years of highest inspiration), Shelley represented the deep current of religious unorthodoxy running through English Romantic poetry.

## Legacy and Influence

Shelley's legacy in the history of [[freethought]] is twofold. As a poet, he demonstrated that the highest literary achievement of the age could be combined with explicit rejection of Christian orthodoxy. As a public intellectual, his defense of persecuted freethinkers and his willingness to attach his name to atheist arguments established a model of the artist as a principled dissenter from religious consensus. His influence can be traced through Swinburne and the Victorian freethought movement to the secularist tradition of the twentieth century.
