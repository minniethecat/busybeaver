## Definition and Origins

Atheism, as discussed in J.B. Bury's *A History of Freedom of Thought*, denotes the rejection of belief in deities. Bury notes that "if atheism means" the denial of God's existence, this position was uncommon before the Enlightenment (006-088). The term itself has historically been applied loosely as an accusation against any thinker who deviated from orthodox religious doctrine.

## Historical Development

In antiquity, the charge of atheism was a serious accusation. Bury records that thinkers were "accused of atheism" as a means of persecution (004-268). The prosecution of those who questioned traditional religion was common in both Greek and Roman societies.

During the Enlightenment, atheism began to emerge as a coherent philosophical position. However, the opinion of atheism "was uncommon" even among radical thinkers of the period (005-217). Most Enlightenment figures were deists rather than atheists, believing in a rational creator while rejecting revealed religion.

The fear that "atheism" would "undermine and destroy all religion" motivated centuries of legal persecution (005-209). Bury documents how the State prescribed severe punishments, including a "maximum term of six months, for atheism, blasphemy" and related offenses (006-222).

An important conceptual development came from Pierre Bayle, who argued that morality was independent of religious belief. Bayle suggested that even "the Devil, who is incapable of atheism," could not be considered moral merely by virtue of belief (006-172). This separated the question of moral behavior from religious conviction.

## Key Proponents

- **Baron d'Holbach** ardently combated atheism and promoted materialist philosophy. His works were among the most explicit statements of atheistic thought in the Enlightenment (006-484).
- **Pierre Bayle** argued for the possibility of a moral society of atheists, challenging the assumption that religion was necessary for ethics.
- **Diderot** moved from deism toward a more materialist and atheistic worldview through his work on the Encyclopédie.
- **Spinoza**, though not strictly an atheist, was widely accused of atheism for his pantheistic identification of God with Nature.

## Impact on Freedom of Thought

Atheism played a crucial role in expanding the boundaries of permissible thought. By challenging the fundamental premise of religious belief, atheists forced a reexamination of the foundations of morality and social order. The argument that "atheism and moral wickedness" were necessarily connected was gradually refuted by freethinkers (006-179).

The "hosts of atheism against the walls of heaven" represented the most radical wing of the freethought movement (002-311). While atheism remained socially dangerous well into the nineteenth century, its emergence as a legitimate philosophical position marked a milestone in the history of intellectual liberty.

The legal persecution of atheism gradually diminished as freedom of thought gained ground. The transition from atheism being a capital offense to being a protected expression of conscience represents one of the most significant achievements of the freethought tradition.
