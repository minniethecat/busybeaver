## Life and Background

Joseph Ernest Renan was born in Tréguier, Brittany, in 1823. Trained for the Catholic priesthood, he left the seminary after encountering German biblical criticism and losing his faith. He became one of the leading French scholars of Semitic languages and the history of religion. In 1862 he was appointed to the chair of Hebrew at the Collège de France.

## Major Contributions

Renan's most famous work, the *Vie de Jésus* (Life of Jesus, 1863), was a landmark in the application of historical-critical methods to the New Testament. The book treated Jesus as a purely human historical figure, rejecting all supernatural elements of the Gospel narratives. It caused an enormous scandal in Catholic France and became an international bestseller.

## Conflict with Authority

The havoc which science and historical criticism wrought among orthodox beliefs during the nineteenth century was not tamely submitted to, and controversy was not the only weapon employed (007-0422, 007-0423). Renan, whose sensational *Life of Jesus* rejected the supernatural, lost his chair in the Collège de France (007-0426, 007-0427). He was suspended from his professorship just four days after his inaugural lecture, in which he had referred to Jesus as "an incomparable man."

Bury places Renan alongside [[david-friedrich-strauss|Strauss]], who was deprived of his professorship at Tübingen, and [[ludwig-buchner|Büchner]], who was driven from Tübingen for his book on *Force and Matter* (007-0424, 007-0425, 007-0427, 007-0428). These cases together illustrate the institutional resistance that nineteenth-century rationalist scholarship faced, even in the universities of liberal states.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Renan's experience exemplifies a crucial stage in the history of freedom of thought: the transition from formal legal toleration to genuine academic freedom. Though France in the 1860s did not burn heretics, it could still deprive a scholar of his livelihood for conclusions arrived at through scholarly methods. Renan's dismissal from the Collège de France demonstrated that the battle for liberty of thought had moved from the stake and the prison to the university chair and the publishing house.

He was later reinstated after the fall of the Second Empire in 1870 and eventually became the administrator of the Collège de France, a trajectory that reflects the gradual liberalisation of French academic life over the course of the nineteenth century.
