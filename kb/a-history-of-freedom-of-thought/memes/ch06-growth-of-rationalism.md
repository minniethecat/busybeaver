## Chapter VI
### THE GROWTH OF RATIONALISM
(SEVENTEENTH AND EIGHTEENTH CENTURIES)

[006-001]DURING the last three hundred years reason has been slowly but steadily

[006-002]destroying Christian mythology and exposing the pretensions of

[006-003]supernatural revelation. The progress of rationalism falls naturally

[006-004]into two periods. (1) In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries those

[006-005]thinkers who rejected Christian theology and the book on which it relies

[006-006]were mainly influenced by the inconsistencies, contradictions, and

[006-007]absurdities which they discovered in the evidence, and by the moral

[006-008][128] difficulties of the creed. Some scientific facts were known which

[006-009]seemed to reflect on the accuracy of Revelation, but arguments based on

[006-010]science were subsidiary. (2) In the nineteenth century the discoveries

[006-011]of science in many fields bore with full force upon fabrics which had

[006-012]been constructed in a naïve and ignorant age; and historical criticism

[006-013]undermined methodically the authority of the sacred documents which had

[006-014]hitherto been exposed chiefly to the acute but unmethodical criticisms

[006-015]of common sense.

[006-016]A disinterested love of facts, without any regard to the bearing which

[006-017]those facts may have on one’s hopes or fears or destiny, is a rare

[006-018]quality in all ages, and it had been very rare indeed since the ancient

[006-019]days of Greece and Rome. It means the scientific spirit. Now in the

[006-020]seventeenth century we may say (without disrespect to a few precursors)

[006-021]that the modern study of natural science began, and in the same period

[006-022]we have a series of famous thinkers who were guided by a disinterested

[006-023]love of truth. Of the most acute minds some reached the conclusion that

[006-024]the Christian scheme of the world is irrational, and according to their

[006-025]temperament some rejected it, whilst others, like the great Frenchman

[006-026]Pascal, fell back upon an unreasoning act of faith. Bacon, who professed

[006-027][129] orthodoxy, was perhaps at heart a deist, but in any case the whole

[006-028]spirit of his writings was to exclude authority from the domain of

[006-029]scientific investigation which he did so much to stimulate. Descartes,

[006-030]illustrious not only as the founder of modern metaphysics but also by

[006-031]his original contributions to science, might seek to conciliate the

[006-032]ecclesiastical authorities—his temper was timid— but his philosophical

[006-033]method was a powerful incentive to rationalistic thought. The general

[006-034]tendency of superior intellects was to exalt reason at the expense of

[006-035]authority; and in England this principle was established so firmly by

[006-036]Locke, that throughout the theological warfare of the eighteenth century

[006-037]both parties relied on reason, and no theologian of repute assumed faith

[006-038]to be a higher faculty.

[006-039]A striking illustration of the gradual encroachments of reason is the

[006-040]change which was silently wrought in public opinion on the subject of

[006-041]witchcraft. The famous efforts of James I to carry out the Biblical

[006-042]command, “Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live,” were outdone by the

[006-043]zeal of the Puritans under the Commonwealth to suppress the wicked old

[006-044]women who had commerce with Satan. After the Restoration, the belief in

[006-045]witchcraft declined among educated people—though

[006-046][130] some able writers maintained it—and there were few executions. The

[006-047]last trial of a witch was in 1712, when some clergymen in Hertfordshire

[006-048]prosecuted Jane Wenham. The jury found her guilty, but the judge, who

[006-049]had summed up in her favour, was able to procure the remission of her

[006-050]sentence; and the laws against witchcraft were repealed in 1735. John

[006-051]Wesley said with perfect truth that to disbelieve in witchcraft is to

[006-052]disbelieve in the Bible. In France and in Holland the decline of belief

[006-053]and interest in this particular form of Satan’s activity was

[006-054]simultaneous. In Scotland, where theology was very powerful, a woman was

[006-055]burnt in 1722. It can be no mere coincidence that the general decline of

[006-056]this superstition belongs to the age which saw the rise of modern

[006-057]science and modern philosophy.

[006-058]Hobbes, who was perhaps the most brilliant English thinker of the

[006-059]seventeenth century, was a freethinker and materialist. He had come

[006-060]under the influence of his friend the French philosopher Gassendi, who

[006-061]had revived materialism in its Epicurean shape. Yet he was a champion

[006-062]not of freedom of conscience but of coercion in its most uncompromising

[006-063]form. In the political theory which he expounded in Leviathan, the

[006-064]sovran has autocratic power in the domain of doctrine,

[006-065][131] as in everything else, and it is the duty of subjects to conform

[006-066]to the religion which the sovran imposes. Religious persecution is thus

[006-067]defended, but no independent power is left to the Church. But the

[006-068]principles on which Hobbes built up his theory were rationalistic. He

[006-069]separated morality from religion and identified “the true moral

[006-070]philosophy” with the “true doctrine of the laws of nature.” What he

[006-071]really thought of religion could be inferred from his remark that the

[006-072]fanciful fear of things invisible (due to ignorance) is the natural seed

[006-073]of that feeling which, in himself, a man calls religion, but, in those

[006-074]who fear or worship the invisible power differently, superstition. In

[006-075]the reign of Charles II Hobbes was silenced and his books were burned.

[006-076]Spinoza, the Jewish philosopher of Holland, owed a great deal to

[006-077]Descartes and (in political speculation) to Hobbes, but his philosophy

[006-078]meant a far wider and more open breach with orthodox opinion than either

[006-079]of his masters had ventured on. He conceived ultimate reality, which he

[006-080]called God, as an absolutely perfect, impersonal Being, a substance

[006-081]whose nature is constituted by two “attributes”— thought and spatial

[006-082]extension. When Spinoza speaks of love of God, in which he considered

[006-083]happiness to consist, he means knowledge

[006-084][132] and contemplation of the order of nature, including human nature,

[006-085]which is subject to fixed, invariable laws. He rejects free-will and the

[006-086]“superstition,” as he calls it, of final causes in nature. If we want to

[006-087]label his philosophy, we may say that it is a form of pantheism. It has

[006-088]often been described as atheism. If atheism means, as I suppose in

[006-089]ordinary use it is generally taken to mean, rejection of a personal God,

[006-090]Spinoza was an atheist. It should be observed that in the seventeenth

[006-091]and eighteenth centuries atheist was used in the wildest way as a term

[006-092]of abuse for freethinkers, and when we read of atheists (except in

[006-093]careful writers) we may generally assume that the persons so stigmatized

[006-094]were really deists, that is, they believed in a personal God but not in

[006-095]Revelation. [1]

[006-096]Spinoza’s daring philosophy was not in harmony with the general trend of

[006-097]speculation at the time, and did not exert any profound influence on

[006-098]thought till a much later period. The thinker whose writings appealed

[006-099]most to the men of his age and were most opportune and effective was

[006-100]John Locke, who professed more or less orthodox Anglicanism. His great

[006-101]contribution to philosophy is equivalent to a very powerful defence

[006-102][133] of reason against the usurpations of authority. The object of his

[006-103]Essay on the Human Understanding (1690) is to show that all knowledge is

[006-104]derived from experience. He subordinated faith completely to reason.

[006-105]While he accepted the Christian revelation, he held that revelation if

[006-106]it contradicted the higher tribunal of reason must be rejected, and that

[006-107]revelation cannot give us knowledge as certain as the knowledge which

[006-108]reason gives. “He that takes away reason to make room for revelation

[006-109]puts out the light of both; and does much what the same as if he would

[006-110]persuade a man to put out his eyes, the better to receive the remote

[006-111]light of an invisible star by a telescope.” He wrote a book to show that

[006-112]the Christian revelation is not contrary to reason, and its title, The

[006-113]Reasonableness of Christianity, sounds the note of all religious

[006-114]controversy in England during the next hundred years. Both the orthodox

[006-115]and their opponents warmly agreed that reasonableness was the only test

[006-116]of the claims of revealed religion. It was under the direct influence of

[006-117]Locke that Toland, an Irishman who had been converted from Roman

[006-118]Catholicism, composed a sensational book, Christianity Not Mysterious

[006-119](1696). He assumes that Christianity is true and argues that there can

[006-120]be no mysteries in it, because mysteries, that

[006-121][134] is, unintelligible dogmas, cannot be accepted by reason. And if a

[006-122]reasonable Deity gave a revelation, its purpose must be to enlighten,

[006-123]not to puzzle. The assumption of the truth of Christianity was a mere

[006-124]pretence, as an intelligent reader could not fail to see. The work was

[006-125]important because it drew the logical inference from Locke’s philosophy,

[006-126]and it had a wide circulation. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu met a Turkish

[006-127]Effendi at Belgrade who asked her for news of Mr. Toland.

[006-128]It is characteristic of this stage of the struggle between reason and

[006-129]authority that (excepting the leading French thinkers in the eighteenth

[006-130]century) the rationalists, who attacked theology, generally feigned to

[006-131]acknowledge the truth of the ideas which they were assailing. They

[006-132]pretended that their speculations did not affect religion; they could

[006-133]separate the domains of reason and of faith; they could show that

[006-134]Revelation was superfluous without questioning it; they could do homage

[006-135]to orthodoxy and lay down views with which orthodoxy was irreconcilable.

[006-136]The errors which they exposed in the sphere of reason were ironically

[006-137]allowed to be truths in the sphere of theology. The mediaeval principle

[006-138]of double truth and other shifts were resorted to, in self-protection

[006-139][135] against the tyranny of orthodoxy—though they did not always avail;

[006-140]and in reading much of the rationalistic literature of this period we

[006-141]have to read between the lines. Bayle is an interesting instance.

[006-142]If Locke’s philosophy, by setting authority in its place and deriving

[006-143]all knowledge from experience, was a powerful aid to rationalism, his

[006-144]contemporary Bayle worked in the same direction by the investigation of

[006-145]history. Driven from France (see above, p. 107), he lived at Amsterdam,

[006-146]where he published his Philosophical Dictionary. He was really a

[006-147]freethinker, but he never dropped the disguise of orthodoxy, and this

[006-148]lends a particular piquancy to his work. He takes a delight in

[006-149]marshalling all the objections which heretics had made to essential

[006-150]Christian dogmas. He exposed without mercy the crimes and brutalities of

[006-151]David, and showed that this favourite of the Almighty was a person with

[006-152]whom one would refuse to shake hands. There was a great outcry at this

[006-153]unedifying candour. Bayle, in replying, adopted the attitude of

[006-154]Montaigne and Pascal, and opposed faith to reason.

[006-155]The theological virtue of faith, he said, consists in believing revealed

[006-156]truths simply and solely on God’s authority. If you believe in the

[006-157]immortality of the soul for

[006-158][136] philosophical reasons, you are orthodox, but you have no part in

[006-159]faith. The merit of faith becomes greater, in proportion as the revealed

[006-160]truth surpasses all the powers of our mind; the more incomprehensible

[006-161]the truth and the more repugnant to reason, the greater is the sacrifice

[006-162]we make in accepting it, the deeper our submission to God. Therefore a

[006-163]merciless inventory of the objections which reason has to urge against

[006-164]fundamental doctrines serves to exalt the merits of faith.

[006-165]The Dictionary was also criticized for the justice done to the moral

[006-166]excellencies of persons who denied the existence of God. Bayle replies

[006-167]that if he had been able to find any atheistical thinkers who lived bad

[006-168]lives, he would have been delighted to dwell on their vices, but he knew

[006-169]of none such. As for the criminals you meet in history, whose abominable

[006-170]actions make you tremble, their impieties and blasphemies prove they

[006-171]believed in a Divinity. This is a natural consequence of the theological

[006-172]doctrine that the Devil, who is incapable of atheism, is the instigator

[006-173]of all the sins of men. For man’s wickedness must clearly resemble that

[006-174]of the Devil and must therefore be joined to a belief in God’s

[006-175]existence, since the Devil is not an atheist. And is it not a proof of

[006-176]the infinite wisdom of God that the worst criminals

[006-177][137] are not atheists, and that most of the atheists whose names are

[006-178]recorded have been honest men? By this arrangement Providence sets

[006-179]bounds to the corruption of man; for if atheism and moral wickedness

[006-180]were united in the same persons, the societies of earth would be exposed

[006-181]to a fatal inundation of sin.

[006-182]There was much more in the same vein; and the upshot was, under the thin

[006-183]veil of serving faith, to show that the Christian dogmas were

[006-184]essentially unreasonable.

[006-185]Bayle’s work, marked by scholarship and extraordinary learning, had a

[006-186]great influence in England as well as in France. It supplied weapons to

[006-187]assailants of Christianity in both countries. At first the assault was

[006-188]carried on with most vigour and ability by the English deists, who,

[006-189]though their writings are little read now, did memorable work by their

[006-190]polemic against the authority of revealed religion.

[006-191]The controversy between the deists and their orthodox opponents turned

[006-192]on the question whether the Deity of natural religion —the God whose

[006-193]existence, as was thought, could be proved by reason—can be identified

[006-194]with the author of the Christian revelation. To the deists this seemed

[006-195]impossible. The nature of the alleged revelation seemed inconsistent

[006-196]with the character

[006-197][138] of the God to whom reason pointed. The defenders of revelation, at

[006-198]least all the most competent, agreed with the deists in making reason

[006-199]supreme, and through this reliance on reason some of them fell into

[006-200]heresies. Clarke, for instance, one of the ablest, was very unsound on

[006-201]the dogma of the Trinity. It is also to be noticed that with both

[006-202]sections the interest of morality was the principal motive. The orthodox

[006-203]held that the revealed doctrine of future rewards and punishments is

[006-204]necessary for morality; the deists, that morality depends on reason

[006-205]alone, and that revelation contains a great deal that is repugnant to

[006-206]moral ideals. Throughout the eighteenth century morality was the guiding

[006-207]consideration with Anglican Churchmen, and religious emotion, finding no

[006-208]satisfaction within the Church, was driven, as it were, outside, and

[006-209]sought an outlet in the Methodism of Wesley and Whitefield.

[006-210]Spinoza had laid down the principle that Scripture must be interpreted

[006-211]like any other book (1670), [2] and with the deists this principle was

[006-212]fundamental. In order to avoid persecution they generally veiled their

[006-213]conclusions

[006-214][139] under sufficiently thin disguises. Hitherto the Press Licensing

[006-215]Act (1662) had very effectually prevented the publication of heterodox

[006-216]works, and it is from orthodox works denouncing infidel opinions that we

[006-217]know how rationalism was spreading. But in 1695, the Press Law was

[006-218]allowed to drop, and immediately deistic literature began to appear.

[006-219]There was, however, the danger of prosecution under the Blasphemy laws.

[006-220]There were three legal weapons for coercing those who attacked

[006-221]Christianity: (1) The Ecclesiastical Courts had and have the power of

[006-222]imprisoning for a maximum term of six months, for atheism, blasphemy,

[006-223]heresy, and damnable opinions. (2) The common law as interpreted by Lord

[006-224]Chief Justice Hale in 1676, when a certain Taylor was charged with

[006-225]having said that religion was a cheat and blasphemed against Christ. The

[006-226]accused was condemned to a fine and the pillory by the Judge, who ruled

[006-227]that the Court of King’s Bench has jurisdiction in such a case, inasmuch

[006-228]as blasphemous words of the kind are an offence against the laws and the

[006-229]State, and to speak against Christianity is to speak in subversion of

[006-230]the law, since Christianity is “parcel of the laws of England.” (3) The

[006-231]statute of 1698 enacts that if any person educated in the Christian

[006-232]religion “shall by

[006-233][140] writing, printing, teaching, or advised speaking deny any one of

[006-234]the persons in the Holy Trinity to be God, or shall assert or maintain

[006-235]there are more gods than one, or shall deny the Christian religion to be

[006-236]true, or shall deny the Holy Scriptures of the Old and New Testament to

[006-237]be of divine authority,” is convicted, he shall for the first offence be

[006-238]adjudged incapable to hold any public offices or employments, and on the

[006-239]second shall lose his civil rights and be imprisoned for three years.

[006-240]This Statute expressly states as its motive the fact that “many persons

[006-241]have of late years openly avowed and published many blasphemous and

[006-242]impious opinions contrary to the doctrine and principles of the

[006-243]Christian religion.”

[006-244]As a matter of fact, most trials for blasphemy during the past two

[006-245]hundred years fall under the second head. But the new Statute of 1698

[006-246]was very intimidating, and we can easily understand how it drove

[006-247]heterodox writers to ambiguous disguises. One of these disguises was

[006-248]allegorical interpretation of Scripture. They showed that literal

[006-249]interpretation led to absurdities or to inconsistencies with the wisdom

[006-250]and justice of God, and pretended to infer that allegorical

[006-251]interpretation must be substituted. But they meant the reader to reject

[006-252]their pretended

[006-253][141] solution and draw a conclusion damaging to Revelation.

[006-254]Among the arguments used in favour of the truth of Revelation the

[006-255]fulfilment of prophecies and the miracles of the New Testament were

[006-256]conspicuous. Anthony Collins, a country gentleman who was a disciple of

[006-257]Locke, published in 1733 his Discourse on the Grounds and Reasons of the

[006-258]Christian Religion, in which he drastically exposed the weakness of the

[006-259]evidence for fulfilment of prophecy, depending as it does on forced and

[006-260]unnatural figurative interpretations. Twenty years before he had written

[006-261]a Discourse of [[freethought|Free-thinking]] (in which Bayle’s influence is evident)

[006-262]pleading for free discussion and the reference of all religious

[006-263]questions to reason. He complained of the general intolerance which

[006-264]prevailed; but the same facts which testify to intolerance testify also

[006-265]to the spread of unbelief.

[006-266]Collins escaped with comparative impunity, but Thomas Woolston, a Fellow

[006-267]of Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge, who wrote six aggressive Discourses

[006-268]on the Miracles of our Saviour (1727—1730) paid the penalty for his

[006-269]audacity. Deprived of his Fellowship, he was prosecuted for libel, and

[006-270]sentenced to a fine of £100 and a year’s imprisonment. Unable to pay, he

[006-271]died in prison. He does

[006-272][142] not adopt the line of arguing that miracles are incredible or

[006-273]impossible. He examines the chief miracles related in the Gospels, and

[006-274]shows with great ability and shrewd common sense that they are absurd or

[006-275]unworthy of the performer. He pointed out, as Huxley was to point out in

[006-276]a controversy with Gladstone, that the miraculous driving of devils into

[006-277]a herd of swine was an unwarrantable injury to somebody’s property. On

[006-278]the story of the Divine blasting of the fig tree, he remarks: “What if a

[006-279]yeoman of Kent should go to look for pippins in his orchard at Easter

[006-280](the supposed time that Jesus sought for these figs) and because of a

[006-281]disappointment cut down his trees? What then would his neighbours make

[006-282]of him? Nothing less than a laughing-stock; and if the story got into

[006-283]our Publick News, he would be the jest and ridicule of mankind.”

[006-284]Or take his comment on the miracle of the Pool of Bethesda, where an

[006-285]angel used to trouble the waters and the man who first entered the pool

[006-286]was cured of his infirmity. “An odd and a merry way of conferring a

[006-287]Divine mercy. And one would think that the angels of God did this for

[006-288]their own diversion more than to do good to mankind. Just as some throw

[006-289]a bone among a kennel of hounds for the pleasure of seeing them

[006-290][143] quarrel for it, or as others cast a piece of money among a company

[006-291]of boys for the sport of seeing them scramble for it, so was the pastime

[006-292]of the angels here.” In dealing with the healing of the woman who

[006-293]suffered from a bloody flux, he asks: “What if we had been told of the

[006-294]Pope’s curing an haemorrhage like this before us, what would Protestants

[006-295]have said to it? Why, ‘that a foolish, credulous, and superstitious

[006-296]woman had fancied herself cured of some slight indisposition, and the

[006-297]crafty Pope and his adherents, aspiring after popular applause,

[006-298]magnified the presumed cure into a miracle.’ The application of such a

[006-299]supposed story of a miracle wrought by the Pope is easy; and if

[006-300]Infidels, Jews, and Mahometans, who have no better opinion of Jesus than

[006-301]we have of the Pope, should make it, there’s no help for it.”

[006-302]Woolston professed no doubts of the inspiration of Scripture. While he

[006-303]argued that it was out of the question to suppose the miracles literally

[006-304]true, he pretended to believe in the fantastic theory that they were

[006-305]intended allegorically as figures of Christ’s mysterious operations in

[006-306]the soul of man. Origen, a not very orthodox Christian Father, had

[006-307]employed the allegorical method, and Woolston quotes him in his favour.

[006-308]His

[006-309][144] vigorous criticisms vary in value, but many of them hit the nail

[006-310]on the head, and the fashion of some modern critics to pass over

[006-311]Woolston’s productions as unimportant because they are “ribald” or

[006-312]coarse, is perfectly unjust. The pamphlets had an enormous sale, and

[006-313]Woolston’s notoriety is illustrated by the anecdote of the “jolly young

[006-314]woman” who met him walking abroad and accosted him with “You old rogue,

[006-315]are you not hanged yet?” Mr. Woolston answered, “Good woman, I know you

[006-316]not; pray what have I done to offend you?” “You have writ against my

[006-317]Saviour,” she said; “what would become of my poor sinful soul if it was

[006-318]not for my dear Saviour?”

[006-319][[About]] the same time, Matthew Tindal (a Fellow of All Souls) attacked

[006-320]Revelation from a more general point of view. In his Christianity as old

[006-321]as the Creation (1730) he undertook to show that the Bible as a

[006-322]revelation is superfluous, for it adds nothing to natural religion,

[006-323]which God revealed to man from the very first by the sole light of

[006-324]reason. He argues that those who defend Revealed religion by its

[006-325]agreement with [[deism|Natural religion]], and thus set up a double government of

[006-326]reason and authority, fall between the two. “It ’s an odd jumble,” he

[006-327]observes, “to prove the truth of a book by the truth

[006-328][145] of the doctrines it contains, and at the same time conclude those

[006-329]doctrines to be true because contained in that book.” He goes on to

[006-330]criticize the Bible in detail. In order to maintain its infallibility,

[006-331]without doing violence to reason, you have, when you find irrational

[006-332]statements, to torture them and depart from the literal sense. Would you

[006-333]think that a Mohammedan was governed by his Koran, who on all occasions

[006-334]departed from the literal sense? “Nay, would you not tell him that his

[006-335]inspired book fell infinitely short of Cicero’s uninspired writings,

[006-336]where there is no such occasion to recede from the letter?”

[006-337]As to chronological and physical errors, which seemed to endanger the

[006-338]infallibility of the Scriptures, a bishop had met the argument by

[006-339]saying, reasonably enough, that in the Bible God speaks according to the

[006-340]conceptions of those to whom he speaks, and that it is not the business

[006-341]of Revelation to rectify their opinions in such matters. Tindal made

[006-342]this rejoinder:—

[006-343]“Is there no difference between God’s not rectifying men’s sentiments in

[006-344]those matters and using himself such sentiments as needs be rectified;

[006-345]or between God’s not mending men’s logic and rhetoric where ’t is

[006-346]defective and using such himself; or between God’s

[006-347][146] not contradicting vulgar notions and confirming them by speaking

[006-348]according to them? Can infinite wisdom despair of gaining or keeping

[006-349]people’s affections without having recourse to such mean acts?”

[006-350]He exposes with considerable effect the monstrosity of the doctrine of

[006-351]exclusive salvation. Must we not consider, he asks, whether one can be

[006-352]said to be sent as a Saviour of mankind, if he comes to shut Heaven’s

[006-353]gate against those to whom, before he came, it was open provided they

[006-354]followed the dictates of their reason? He criticizes the inconsistency

[006-355]of the impartial and universal goodness of God, known to us by the light

[006-356]of nature, with acts committed by Jehovah or his prophets. Take the

[006-357]cases in which the order of nature is violated to punish men for crimes

[006-358]of which they were not guilty, such as Elijah’s hindering rain from

[006-359]falling for three years and a half. If God could break in upon the

[006-360]ordinary rules of his providence to punish the innocent for the guilty,

[006-361]we have no guarantee that if he deals thus with us in this life, he will

[006-362]not act in the same way in the life to come, “since if the eternal rules

[006-363]of justice are once broken how can we imagine any stop?” But the ideals

[006-364]of holiness and justice in the Old Testament are strange indeed. The

[006-365]holier men

[006-366][147] are represented to be, the more cruel they seem and the more

[006-367]addicted to cursing. How surprising to find the holy prophet Elisha

[006-368]cursing in the name of the Lord little children for calling him Bald-

[006-369]pate! And, what is still more surprising, two she-bears immediately

[006-370]devoured forty-two little children.

[006-371]I have remarked that theologians at this time generally took the line of

[006-372]basing Christianity on reason and not on faith. An interesting little

[006-373]book, Christianity not founded on Argument, couched in the form of a

[006-374]letter to a young gentleman at Oxford, by Henry Dodwell (Junior),

[006-375]appeared in 1741, and pointed out the dangers of such confidence in

[006-752]time. He proved (in his Natural Theology) the existence of God by the

[006-753]argument from design —without taking any account of the criticisms of

[006-754]Hume on that argument. Just as a watchmaker is inferred from a watch, so

[006-755]a divine workman is inferred from contrivances in nature. Paley takes

[006-756]his instances of such contrivance largely from the organs and

[006-757]constitution of the human body. His idea of God is that of an ingenious

[006-758]contriver dealing with rather obstinate material. Paley’s “God” (Mr.

[006-759]Leslie Stephen remarked) “has been civilized like man; he has become

[006-760]scientific and ingenious; he is superior to Watt or Priestley in

[006-761]devising mechanical and chemical contrivances, and is therefore made in

[006-762]the image of that generation of which Watt and Priestley were

[006-763]conspicuous lights.” When a God of this kind

[006-764][168] is established there is no difficulty about miracles, and it is on

[006-765]miracles that Paley bases the case for Christianity—all other arguments

[006-766]are subsidiary. And his proof of the New Testament miracles is that the

[006-767]apostles who were eye-witnesses believed in them, for otherwise they

[006-768]would not have acted and suffered in the cause of their new religion.

[006-769]Paley’s defence is the performance of an able legal adviser to the

[006-770]Almighty.

[006-771]The list of the English deistic writers of the eighteenth century closes

[006-772]with one whose name is more familiar than any of his predecessors,

[006-773]Thomas Paine. A Norfolk man, he migrated to America and played a leading

[006-774]part in the Revolution. Then he returned to England and in 1791

[006-775]published his Rights of Man in two parts. I have been considering,

[006-776]almost exclusively, freedom of thought in religion, because it may be

[006-777]taken as the thermometer for freedom of thought in general. At this

[006-778]period it was as dangerous to publish revolutionary opinions in politics

[006-779]as in theology. Paine was an enthusiastic admirer of the American

[006-780]Constitution and a supporter of the French Revolution (in which also he

[006-781]was to play a part). His Rights of Man is an indictment of the

[006-782]monarchical form of government, and a plea for representative democracy.

[006-783]It had an enormous

[006-784][169] sale, a cheap edition was issued, and the government, finding that

[006-785]it was accessible to the poorer classes, decided to prosecute. Paine

[006-786]escaped to France, and received a brilliant ovation at Calais, which

[006-787]returned him as deputy to the National Convention. His trial for high

[006-788]treason came on at the end of 1792. Among the passages in his book, on

[006-789]which the charge was founded, were these: “All hereditary government is

[006-790]in its nature tyranny.” “The time is not very distant when England will

[006-791]laugh at itself for sending to Holland, Hanover, Zell, or Brunswick for

[006-792]men” [meaning King William III and King George I] “at the expense of a

[006-793]million a year who understood neither her laws, her language, nor her

[006-794]interest, and whose capacities would scarcely have fitted them for the

[006-795]office of a parish constable. If government could be trusted to such

[006-796]hands, it must be some easy and simple thing indeed, and materials fit

[006-797]for all the purposes may be found in every town and village in England.”

[006-798]Erskine was Paine’s counsel, and he made a fine oration in defence of

[006-799]freedom of speech.

[006-800]“Constraint,” he said, “is the natural parent of resistance, and a

[006-801]pregnant proof that reason is not on the side of those who use it. You

[006-802]must all remember, gentlemen, Lucian’s pleasant story: Jupiter and a

[006-803]countryman

[006-804][170] were walking together, conversing with great freedom and

[006-805]familiarity upon the subject of heaven and earth. The countryman

[006-806]listened with attention and acquiescence while Jupiter strove only to

[006-807]convince him; but happening to hint a doubt, Jupiter turned hastily

[006-808]around and threatened him with his thunder. ‘Ah, ha!’ says the

[006-809]countryman, ‘now, Jupiter, I know that you are wrong; you are always

[006-810]wrong when you appeal to your thunder.’ This is the case with me. I can

[006-811]reason with the people of England, but I cannot fight against the

[006-812]thunder of authority.”

[006-813]Paine was found guilty and outlawed. He soon committed a new offence by

[006-814]the publication of an anti-Christian work, The Age of Reason (1794 and

[006-815]1796), which he began to write in the Paris prison into which he had

[006-816]been thrown by Robespierre. This book is remarkable as the first

[006-817]important English publication in which the Christian scheme of salvation

[006-818]and the Bible are assailed in plain language without any disguise or

[006-819]reserve. In the second place it was written in such a way as to reach

[006-820]the masses. And, thirdly, while the criticisms on the Bible are in the

[006-821]same vein as those of the earlier deists, Paine is the first to present

[006-822]with force the incongruity of the Christian scheme with the conception

[006-823]of the universe attained by astronomical science.

[006-824][171]

[006-825]“Though it is not a direct article of the Christian system that this

[006-826]world that we inhabit is the whole of the inhabitable globe, yet it is

[006-827]so worked up therewith—from what is called the Mosaic account of the

[006-828]creation, the story of Eve and the apple, and the counterpart of that

[006-829]story, the death of the Son of God—that to believe otherwise (that is,

[006-830]to believe that God created a plurality of worlds at least as numerous

[006-831]as what we call stars) renders the Christian system of faith at once

[006-832]little and ridiculous, and scatters it in the mind like feathers in the

[006-833]air. The two beliefs cannot be held together in the same mind; and he

[006-834]who thinks that he believes both has thought but little of either.”

[006-835]As an ardent deist, who regarded nature as God’s revelation, Paine was

[006-836]able to press this argument with particular force. Referring to some of

[006-837]the tales in the Old Testament, he says: “When we contemplate the

[006-838]immensity of that Being who directs and governs the incomprehensible

[006-839]Whole, of which the utmost ken of human sight can discover but a part,

[006-840]we ought to feel shame at calling such paltry stories the Word of God.”

[006-841]The book drew a reply from Bishop Watson, one of those admirable

[006-842]eighteenth-century divines, who admitted the right of private judgment

[006-843]and thought that argument

[006-844][172] should be met by argument and not by force. His reply had the

[006-845]rather significant title, An Apology for the Bible. George III remarked

[006-846]that he was not aware that any apology was needed for that book. It is a

[006-847]weak defence, but is remarkable for the concessions which it makes to

[006-848]several of Paine’s criticisms of Scripture—admissions which were

[006-849]calculated to damage the doctrine of the infallibility of the Bible.

[006-850]It was doubtless in consequence of the enormous circulation of the Age

[006-851]of Reason that a Society for the Suppression of Vice decided to

[006-852]prosecute the publisher. Unbelief was common among the ruling class, but

[006-853]the view was firmly held that religion was necessary for the populace

[006-854]and that any attempt to disseminate unbelief among the lower classes

[006-855]must be suppressed. Religion was regarded as a valuable instrument to

[006-856]keep the poor in order. It is notable that of the earlier rationalists

[006-857](apart from the case of Woolston) the only one who was punished was

[006-858]Peter Annet, a schoolmaster, who tried to popularize freethought and was

[006-859]sentenced for diffusing “diabolical” opinions to the pillory and hard

[006-860]labour (1763). Paine held that the people at large had the right of

[006-861]access to all new ideas, and he wrote so as to reach the people. Hence

[006-862]his book must be suppressed.

[006-863][173] At the trial (1797) the judge placed every obstacle in the way of

[006-864]the defence. The publisher was sentenced to a year’s imprisonment.

[006-865]This was not the end of Paine prosecutions. In 1811 a Third Part of the

[006-866]Age of Reason appeared, and Eaton the publisher was condemned to

[006-867]eighteen months’ imprisonment and to stand in the pillory once a month.

[006-868]The judge, Lord Ellenborough, said in his charge, that “to deny the

[006-869]truths of the book which is the foundation of our faith has never been

[006-870]permitted.” The poet Shelley addressed to Lord Ellenborough a scathing

[006-871]letter. “Do you think to convert Mr. Eaton to your religion by

[006-872]embittering his existence? You might force him by torture to profess

[006-873]your tenets, but he could not believe them except you should make them

[006-874]credible, which perhaps exceeds your power. Do you think to please the

[006-875]God you worship by this exhibition of your zeal? If so, the demon to

[006-876]whom some nations offer human hecatombs is less barbarous than the deity

[006-877]of civilized society!” In 1819 Richard Carlisle was prosecuted for

[006-878]publishing the Age of Reason and sentenced to a large fine and three

[006-879]years’ imprisonment. Unable to pay the fine he was kept in prison for

[006-880]three years. His wife and sister, who carried on the business

[006-881][174] and continued to sell the book, were fined and imprisoned soon

[006-882]afterwards and a whole host of shop assistants.

[006-883]If his publishers suffered in England, the author himself suffered in

[006-884]America where bigotry did all it could to make the last years of his

[006-885]life bitter.

[006-886]The age of enlightenment began in Germany in the middle of the

[006-887]eighteenth century. In most of the German States, thought was

[006-888]considerably less free than in England. Under Frederick the Great’s

[006-889]father, the philosopher Wolff was banished from Prussia for according to

[006-890]the moral teachings of the Chinese sage Confucius a praise which, it was

[006-891]thought, ought to be reserved for Christianity. He returned after the

[006-892]accession of Frederick, under whose tolerant rule Prussia was an asylum

[006-893]for those writers who suffered for their opinions in neighbouring

[006-894]States. Frederick, indeed, held the view which was held by so many

[006-895]English rationalists of the time, and is still held widely enough, that

[006-896]freethought is not desirable for the multitude, because they are

[006-897]incapable of understanding philosophy. Germany felt the influence of the

[006-898]English Deists, of the French freethinkers, and of Spinoza; but in the

[006-899]German rationalistic propaganda of this period there is nothing very

[006-900]original or interesting.

[006-901][175] The names of Edelmann and Bahrdt may be mentioned. The works of

[006-902]Edelmann, who attacked the inspiration of the Bible, were burned in

[006-903]various cities, and he was forced to seek Frederick’s protection at

[006-904]Berlin. Bahrdt was more aggressive than any other writer of the time.

[006-905]Originally a preacher, it was by slow degrees that he moved away from

[006-906]the orthodox faith. His translation of the New Testament cut short his

[006-907]ecclesiastical career. His last years were spent as an inn-keeper. His

[006-908]writings, for instance his popular Letters on the Bible, must have had a

[006-909]considerable effect, if we may judge by the hatred which he excited

[006-910]among theologians.

[006-911]It was not, however, in direct rationalistic propaganda, but in

[006-912]literature and philosophy, that the German enlightenment of this century

[006-913]expressed itself. The most illustrious men of letters, Goethe (who was

[006-914]profoundly influenced by Spinoza) and Schiller, stood outside the

[006-915]Churches, and the effect of their writings and of the whole literary

[006-916]movement of the time made for the freest treatment of human experience.

[006-917]One German thinker shook the world—the philosopher Kant. His Critic of

[006-918]Pure Reason demonstrated that when we attempt to prove by the fight of

[006-919]the intellect the existence of

[006-920][176] God and the immortality of the Soul, we fall helplessly into

[006-921]contradictions. His destructive criticism of the argument from design

[006-922]and all natural theology was more complete than that of Hume; and his

[006-923]philosophy, different though his system was, issued in the same

[006-924]practical result as that of Locke, to confine knowledge to experience.

[006-925]It is true that afterwards, in the interest of ethics, he tried to

[006-926]smuggle in by a back-door the Deity whom he had turned out by the front

[006-927]gate, but the attempt was not a success. His philosophy—while it led to

[006-928]new speculative systems in which the name of God was used to mean

[006-929]something very different from the Deistic conception—was a significant

[006-930]step further in the deliverance of reason from the yoke of authority.
