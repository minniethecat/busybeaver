# Theodosius I

**Theodosius I** (347–395 CE), also known as Theodosius the Great, was Roman Emperor from 379 to 395 CE. His reign marked the definitive establishment of Nicene Christianity as the official religion of the Roman Empire and the beginning of systematic legal suppression of pagan worship.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

In J.B. Bury's "A History of Freedom of Thought", Theodosius I represents a decisive turning point in the decline of intellectual liberty in the ancient world. Bury notes that while the Emperor Julian had briefly attempted to restore paganism and establish "universal toleration" by "forbidding [Christians] to teach in schools", this proved "only a momentary check." The critical shift came when "Paganism was finally shattered by the severe laws of Theodosius I (end of fourth century)" [003-056]–[003-057].

Although pagan practices "lingered on here and there for more than another century, especially at Rome and Athens", their public significance was effectively ended by Theodosian legislation. This legal suppression of non-Christian belief systems established a precedent for state enforcement of religious orthodoxy that would shape European intellectual life for over a millennium.
