# John Toland

**John Toland** (1670–1722) was an Irish rationalist philosopher and one of the earliest and most influential of the English [[Deism|Deists]]. His sensational book *Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696) applied the principles of [[John Locke]]'s philosophy to revealed religion, arguing that there could be nothing in Christianity that was above reason — a position that provoked intense controversy and made Toland a central figure in the early Enlightenment challenge to orthodox [[Christianity]].

## Life and Background

Toland was born in County Donegal, Ireland, and was originally raised as a Roman Catholic. According to Bury, he was converted from Catholicism before producing his most famous work:

> [006-117] Locke that Toland, an Irishman who had been converted from Roman Catholicism, composed a sensational book, Christianity Not Mysterious (1696).

He studied at the universities of Glasgow and Edinburgh and later at Leiden in the Netherlands, where he came into contact with the radical Protestant theology of the Dutch Republic. His conversion from Catholicism and his subsequent writings reflect the trajectory of rationalist critique across confessional boundaries in early modern Europe.

## Major Contributions

*Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696) was Toland's most important and controversial work. He took as his starting point Locke's epistemological principle that reason is the test of truth and applied it rigorously to the claims of revealed religion:

> [006-115] and their opponents warmly agreed that reasonableness was the only test of the claims of revealed religion.

The book argued that there could be no genuine mysteries in Christianity — anything that could not be understood by reason could not be an authentic part of the Christian religion. Although Toland formally accepted the truth of Christianity, his arguments led inexorably toward Deism or unbelief:

> [006-118] He assumes that Christianity is true and argues that there can be no mysteries in it, and that any doctrine which is mysterious must be a human addition, a pretense, as an intelligent reader could not fail to see.

The work was widely condemned as subversive. The Irish Parliament ordered it to be burned by the common hangman, and Toland fled to England to escape prosecution.

## Relation to Freedom of Thought

Toland's significance for [[freedom of thought]] lies in his role as a bridge figure between the philosophical empiricism of [[John Locke]] and the open [[Deism]] and [[freethought]] of the eighteenth century. His intellectual notoriety spread across Europe, as Bury notes with an anecdote about the book's circulation:

> [006-127] Effendi at Belgrade who asked her for news of Mr. Toland.

The fact that a Turkish official in the Balkans knew of Toland's work indicates the extraordinary reach of the early Enlightenment's printed debates. Toland later wrote *Pantheisticon* (1720) and other works that moved increasingly toward a naturalistic philosophy, influencing the development of radical thought throughout the century.

## Legacy and Influence

Toland's *Christianity Not Mysterious* was a landmark text in the history of rationalist religion. By drawing out the logical implications of Locke's philosophy, Toland made explicit what Locke himself had been careful to qualify or obscure. His work opened the door for the more openly skeptical writings of later Deists and freethinkers, such as [[Anthony Collins]], [[Matthew Tindal]], and [[Thomas Woolston]]. The trajectory from Toland's Deism to the full-throated [[freethought]] of the later eighteenth century illustrates the internal logic of [[rationalism]] when applied consistently to religious questions.
