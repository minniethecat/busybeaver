## Overview

Ghosts in *Wuthering Heights* are not merely Gothic decoration; they are the novel's most direct expression of its central thesis: that love, hatred, and will survive death. Catherine's ghost at the window in Lockwood's nightmare (003-020), the "swarming with ghosts and goblins" that Lockwood imagines (003-047), Heathcliff's obsessive summoning of Catherine's spirit (012-041), and his final, willing dissolution into her ghostly presence — these moments structure the novel's movement from social realism into something stranger and more enduring. Brontë domesticates the supernatural, making it feel less like fantasy and more like the natural extension of emotions too powerful to be contained by a single lifetime.

## Manifestations

- **Lockwood's nightmare (003-020–003-027)**: The dream of Jabez Branderham's sermon gives way to the novel's most iconic image: the child-ghost Catherine at the window, "a most melancholy voice sobbing, 'Let me in — let me in!'" and Lockwood's horrific response — rubbing her wrist against broken glass. The passage is deliberately ambiguous: is this a dream, a memory of Lockwood's own experience, or a genuine haunting?

- **Heathcliff's summoning of Catherine**: After Catherine's death, Heathcliff cries, "Catherine Earnshaw, may you not rest as long as I am living!" (012-041) and later confesses that for eighteen years he has felt her presence — "I cannot look down to this floor, but her features are shaped in the flags!" (029-042). His haunting is self-induced, a willed refusal to let her go.

- **The marsh ghost rumour**: In the final chapters, Heathcliff fabricates a story that Nelly was lost in the Black-horse Marsh — a mundane echo of the supernatural theme: stories of disappearance and haunting circulate through the community, and Heathcliff exploits them.

- **Heathcliff's death as spectral reunion**: Heathcliff dies at the open window, eyes fixed on something invisible, a "frightful, life-like gaze of exultation" (034-018). The country people later see his ghost walking with Catherine's on the moors.

## Key Passages

- **003-020–003-027**: Lockwood's nightmare of Catherine's ghost — the definitive supernatural moment, rendered with physical detail that blurs dream and waking.
- **012-041**: Heathcliff's curse: "Catherine Earnshaw, may you not rest as long as I am living!" — the invocation that makes him the active agent of haunting rather than its passive victim.
- **003-047**: Lockwood reports to Heathcliff: "the place is swarming with ghosts and goblins!" — a comic understatement that Heathcliff takes with deadly seriousness.
- **034-018–034-019**: The description of Heathcliff's corpse and the reports of his ghost — the novel's final blurring of supernatural and natural.

## Character Connections

| Character | Relationship to the Supernatural |
|-----------|--------------------------------|
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | The ghost; her spirit haunts the Heights, Heathcliff, and the novel's structure |
| **Heathcliff** | The summoner and the haunted; he wills Catherine's ghost into existence through the intensity of his grief |
| **Mr. Lockwood** | The sceptical witness; his nightmare introduces the supernatural into the novel's realistic frame |
| **Jabez Branderham** | The dream-preacher; his sermon on the 490th sin in Lockwood's dream sets the stage for the ghost |
