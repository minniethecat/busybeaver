# Earnshaw Family

The Earnshaws are the ancient yeoman family of [[Wuthering Heights]], a line that has occupied the same house since 1500. At the novel's opening, the family is in the final stages of dissolution — the name survives only in the degraded form of [[Hareton Earnshaw]], a young man raised in ignorance and servitude in the house his ancestors built. The Earnshaw story is one of decline driven by disruptive external forces ([[Heathcliff]]'s arrival), internal weakness ([[Hindley Earnshaw]]'s self-destruction), and a social order in transition.

## The Old Order

Mr. Earnshaw, the patriarch, represents the traditional values of the yeoman class: independence, rough generosity, and a certain indifference to social convention. His decision to bring the foundling Heathcliff home from [[Liverpool]] — a city associated with the slave trade and imperial commerce — disrupts the settled order of the household. His wife, [[Mrs. Earnshaw]], opposes the adoption immediately:

> "she was ready to fling it out of doors" (004-006)

Her early death leaves the household without a moderating maternal influence, and Mr. Earnshaw's own death shortly after removes the last authority capable of restraining the conflict between Heathcliff and Hindley.

The Earnshaw household before Heathcliff's arrival was, by all accounts, a functioning family unit. Catherine and Hindley were loved by their parents, and the house, though remote, was a proper home — "handsome, and very comfortable" according to the family tradition.

The dating of the Earnshaw name — "1500" — is significant. It roots the family in a pre-industrial, pre-enclosure England, the world of independent yeomen who owned their land outright and owed allegiance to no landlord. This ancient lineage makes the family's destruction within a single generation all the more shocking: centuries of continuity are undone in the space of thirty years. The carved name "Hareton Earnshaw" above the door, with its own date — "1500" — becomes a material emblem of inheritance and loss, a name that the living Hareton cannot read but which nevertheless marks his birthright.

## Hindley's Destruction

Hindley Earnshaw's story is the tragedy of a legitimate heir who destroys himself. Sent away to college after his father's death — partly to remove him from Heathcliff's influence — Hindley returns with a wife, [[Frances Earnshaw]], and assumes control of the Heights. His first act is to degrade Heathcliff to the status of a servant, denying him education and forcing him to labour in the fields — a systematic revenge for the years of displacement he suffered as a child.

But Hindley's revenge consumes him. After Frances dies of consumption, he descends into alcoholism and gambling. He neglects his son Hareton entirely, and Heathcliff — having returned wealthy and calculating — methodically encourages Hindley's vices while accumulating the debts that will eventually transfer ownership of the Heights to himself. Nelly Dean describes Hindley's decline with clinical precision:

> "He grew desperate; his sorrow was of the kind that will not lament, he neither wept nor prayed — he cursed and defied: execrated God and man" (017-018)

The financial mechanism of Hindley's ruin deserves attention. Heathcliff does not simply lend Hindley money; he becomes Hindley's mortgagee, systematically acquiring claims on every acre of Earnshaw land. The estate that had been in the family for three centuries is alienated not through conquest or legal dispute but through the slow, deliberate exploitation of a man's weakness — a form of revenge that is at once financial and deeply personal. The Heights passes from the Earnshaws not in a single dramatic moment but incrementally, drink by drink, wager by wager.

## Hareton: The Broken Heir

Hareton Earnshaw is the novel's most poignant figure of lost inheritance. The legitimate heir to Wuthering Heights, he is raised by Heathcliff in deliberate degradation — denied education, taught to curse, kept in a state of brutish ignorance — as a symbolic revenge against the Earnshaw name. Heathcliff boasts to Nelly:

> "I've got him faster than his scoundrel of a father secured me ... he'll never be able to emerge from his bathos of coarseness and ignorance. I've taught him to scorn everything extra-animal as silly and weak" (021-036)

Yet Hareton's innate decency survives his brutal upbringing. His unlikely bond with [[Catherine Linton]] — who first scorns him for his ignorance, then teaches him to read — represents the novel's only genuine movement toward redemption. Their marriage at the novel's close restores the Earnshaw name to its rightful place and signals the possibility of a new order emerging from the wreckage of the old.

Hareton's attachment to Heathcliff is one of the novel's most psychologically complex relationships. Despite being the instrument of Hareton's degradation, Heathcliff is also the only father figure Hareton has ever known. When Heathcliff dies, Hareton is the only person who grieves:

> "He sat by the corpse all night, weeping in bitter earnest" (034-016)

This grief — for the man who destroyed his life — speaks to the tangled emotional logic of the Earnshaw household, where love and cruelty, nurture and destruction, are never fully separable.

## Property and Inheritance

The Earnshaw family's decline is inseparable from questions of property. In the world of the novel, family and land are one: the name "Earnshaw" is carved into the stone of Wuthering Heights, and the house is not merely the family's residence but its identity. When Heathcliff acquires the Heights, he acquires not just a building but the material embodiment of the Earnshaw line — and his possession of it is a form of symbolic annihilation.

The novel is precise about the legal mechanisms of this transfer. Hindley's gambling debts, accumulated during his years of dissipation, are secured against the estate. Heathcliff, who has returned from his three-year absence with unexplained wealth, becomes Hindley's creditor. Upon Hindley's death — which Nelly strongly implies is assisted by Heathcliff's deliberate encouragement of his drinking — the Heights passes to Heathcliff by mortgage default. The transaction is technically legal, which makes it all the more damning: the law, like the church represented by the compliant sexton, offers no protection against a sufficiently determined predator.

The lawyer's assessment of Hareton's situation is damning in its finality:

> "His father died in debt," he said; "the whole property is mortgaged, and the sole chance for the natural heir is to allow him an opportunity of creating some interest in the creditor's heart, that he may be inclined to deal leniently towards him." (017-082)

And the result, as Nelly later confirms, is absolute:

> The guest was now the master of Wuthering Heights: he held firm possession, and proved to the attorney — who, in his turn, proved it to Mr. Linton — that Earnshaw had mortgaged every yard of land he owned for cash to supply his mania for gaming; and he, Heathcliff, was the mortgagee. In that manner Hareton, who should now be the first gentleman in the neighbourhood, was reduced to a state of complete dependence on his father's inveterate enemy; and lives in his own house as a servant, deprived of the advantage of wages: quite unable to right himself, because of his friendlessness, and his ignorance that he has been wronged. (017-092)

The Earnshaw property is eventually restored — but only through the marriage of Hareton and Cathy, which reunites the Heights with the cultivated values of the Grange. That restoration requires the erasure of Heathcliff's claim and the symbolic renewal of the Earnshaw name. The novel closes with the promise that Hareton and Cathy will move to Thrushcross Grange, leaving the Heights to "the spirits" — a final dissociation of the Earnshaw name from the house that bore it for three centuries.

## The Question of Family

The Earnshaw family raises profound questions about what family means. Heathcliff, a foundling with no known blood relation, becomes more central to the family's story than any legitimate child. His bond with [[Catherine Earnshaw]] — a bond she describes as identity itself: "I *am* Heathcliff" (009-042) — transcends both family and marriage. Mr. Earnshaw's adoption of Heathcliff, an act of compassionate impulse, destabilizes the biological family line and sets in motion the novel's entire tragic arc.

The Earnshaws are destroyed not by external enemies but by the intensity of the bonds they form and the failure of their traditional structures to contain them. In this sense, the Earnshaw story is a meditation on the limits of the patriarchal family itself — a form of social organization that, in Brontë's telling, cannot accommodate the force of desire, whether Heathcliff's vengeful passion or Catherine's declaration of merged identity. The Earnshaw name survives into the next generation, but the Earnshaw world — the world of the independent yeoman, rooted in a single house for three hundred years — does not.
