# Cathy and Hareton

The love story of [[Catherine Linton]] and [[Hareton Earnshaw]] unfolds in the
closing chapters of *Wuthering Heights* and provides the novel's single
redemptive counterpoint to the destructive passion of the first generation.
Where Catherine Earnshaw and [[Heathcliff]]'s love was wild, unbounded, and
ultimately annihilating — a force that consumed themselves and everyone around
them — Cathy and Hareton's bond is built on patience, mutual education, and
the deliberate rejection of the cycle of revenge.

## The Inherited Conflict

When Cathy first encounters Hareton, the ground between them is already
poisoned. She is the daughter of [[Edgar Linton]] and [[Catherine Earnshaw]],
born into the refinement of [[Thrushcross Grange]]; he is the son of
[[Hindley Earnshaw]], raised in neglect and degradation at [[Wuthering Heights]].
Heathcliff has deliberately cultivated Hareton's brutishness as a form of
revenge against Hindley: "He has satisfied my expectations," Heathcliff says
of Hareton. "If he were a born fool I should not enjoy it half so much. But
he's no fool; and I can sympathize with all his feelings, having felt them
myself" [021-082].

Cathy's first response to Hareton is contempt. She mocks his illiteracy and
his rough manners, refusing to acknowledge him as a cousin. "I've a relation,
a very near relation," she tells Lockwood, "and he's kept a boor, a clown, a
clod-hopper" [031-023]. This contempt wounds Hareton deeply, in ways that Cathy
only later comes to understand.

Heathcliff observes the dynamic with satisfaction, recognizing in Cathy's
contempt an echo of the class-based humiliation he himself suffered. He tells
Nelly that Cathy's scorn is "a wholesome tonic" for Hareton — a way of making
the boy feel what Heathcliff once felt, though Heathcliff notes that Hareton
"will undertake nothing against the person that offends him" [031-027]. The
difference between Hareton and the young Heathcliff is already visible: where
Heathcliff nursed his wounds in silence and plotted revenge, Hareton feels the
hurt openly and does not convert it into hatred.

## The Turning Point

The turning point comes when Cathy, isolated and imprisoned at the Heights
after Heathcliff's death, recognizes that her cruelty to Hareton mirrors the
cruelty that has shaped her own circumstances. She makes an overture —
offering to teach Hareton to read. The proposal is loaded with symbolic
significance: literacy, which Hindley used to degrade Heathcliff and which
Heathcliff then used to degrade Hareton, becomes the instrument of
reconciliation.

Hareton's response is initially defiant and wounded. He has been humiliated
by Cathy before and is slow to trust. But Cathy persists, and her persistence
is itself a break from the pattern established by the novel's first generation.
Where Catherine Earnshaw could not bridge the gap between social obligation and
spiritual truth, Cathy Linton does the patient, unglamorous work of building
a relationship day by day.

The process of reconciliation is narrated by Nelly with an attention to small
gestures. Cathy apologizes not through grand declaration but by offering
Hareton a book — a gift that acknowledges both her earlier cruelty (mocking
his illiteracy) and his desire to learn. Hareton's acceptance of the gift is
correspondingly understated: "He took it, and opened it, as if he did not know
what to do with it" [032-041]. The hesitation is itself a form of dignity — a
refusal to be easily won after being easily scorned.

## The Education Motif

Education in *Wuthering Heights* is never neutral. From the novel's earliest
chapters, it is a weapon: Hindley deprives Heathcliff of education to degrade
him; Heathcliff later deprives Hareton of education for the same purpose.
Cathy's decision to teach Hareton to read is therefore not merely an act of
kindness but a deliberate reversal of the novel's historical logic.

The choice of books is significant. Cathy and Hareton read not the religious
tracts that Joseph would approve but secular literature — a choice that
aligns their education with the world of feeling and imagination rather than
judgment and damnation. When Cathy laughs at Hareton's mispronunciations, and
he reacts not with the sullen withdrawal of the earlier chapters but with a
shared smile, the moment registers as a victory over the forces that have kept
them apart: class, family history, and the deliberate cultivation of ignorance
as an instrument of control.

## The Garden Scene

The emblem of their courtship is the garden Hareton plants for Cathy — a
plot of ground among the gooseberry bushes that he prepares and cultivates as
a gesture of devotion. When [[Joseph]] discovers the garden and destroys it
in a fit of religious fury, Hareton's devastation is absolute. Cathy's
response is to comfort him, to promise that she will teach him to plant
flowers properly, and to take his side against Joseph's tyranny. The moment
is domestic, modest, and revolutionary — a reversal of the novel's entire
trajectory of revenge and cruelty.

The garden carries a weight of symbolism disproportionate to its size. It is
an act of cultivation in a novel whose central conflict is between wildness
and civilization. Hareton's attempt to grow something — to make the earth
produce beauty rather than storm — is a gesture toward the kind of life that
Wuthering Heights has never been able to sustain. That the garden is destroyed
by Joseph, the novel's embodiment of harsh, life-denying religion, is almost
inevitable; that Cathy immediately promises to help Hareton replant it is the
novel's clearest statement that destruction is not the final word.

The culmination of their reconciliation comes in a scene of shared reading.
Nelly Dean observes them from the doorway:

> "I overheard no further distinguishable talk, but, on looking round again,
> I perceived two such radiant countenances bent over the page of the accepted
> book, that I did not doubt the treaty had been ratified on both sides; and
> the enemies were, thenceforth, sworn allies." [032-079]

The image of the two young people bent over a book together — reading, arguing,
learning — stands as the novel's final statement on what love can be when it
is freed from the toxic legacy of the past.

## Thematic Significance

Cathy and Hareton's relationship functions as a deliberate rewriting of
Catherine and Heathcliff's. The parallels are precise: both involve a young
woman of higher social standing and a young man degraded by the cruelty of
others. Both unfold at Wuthering Heights under the shadow of Heathcliff's
presence. But where Catherine chose Edgar and set in motion the chain of
destruction, Cathy chooses Hareton — and the choice heals rather than wounds.

The education motif is central to this reversal. The novel opens with a
childhood world in which education is a weapon — Hindley uses it to degrade
Heathcliff, and Heathcliff uses it to degrade Hareton. It closes with an image
of education as a gift: Cathy gives Hareton literacy, and through that gift,
he becomes her equal. The cycle of violent pedagogy is broken.

Their marriage, planned for New Year's Day, will take place not at Wuthering
Heights but at Thrushcross Grange — the house associated with civility and
order. The geographical move from the Heights to the Grange is a spatial
sealing of the novel's moral resolution: the wildness that produced the first
generation's tragedy has been tempered, not eliminated, and the future belongs
to a love that can read, plant gardens, and move between houses.

## Critical Perspectives

Critics have debated whether Cathy and Hareton's happy ending feels earned
or imposed. Some read it as a conventional Victorian marriage plot grafted onto
a novel that otherwise refuses convention; others see it as Emily Brontë's
deliberate argument that the cycles of trauma and revenge are not inevitable —
that the second generation can, through conscious effort, choose differently.

The latter reading has gained strength in recent decades, particularly among
critics attentive to the novel's treatment of education and class mobility.
Cathy and Hareton's courtship is, in this view, the novel's most radical
gesture: a demonstration that the structures of class and family that seemed
immutable in the first generation can, in fact, be transformed by individual
acts of generosity and patience. The ending is not a concession to convention
but a fulfillment of the novel's deepest argument — that love, when it takes
the form of teaching and learning rather than possession and obsession, is
capable of breaking the cycles that otherwise repeat across generations.
