## Overview

Property is the novel's hidden engine. The marriage plot, the revenge plot, and the inheritance plot are inseparable: Catherine marries Edgar for security and status, Heathcliff acquires both Wuthering Heights and Thrushcross Grange through calculated manipulation of inheritance law, and the second generation's happiness depends on the restoration of Hareton's stolen birthright. Brontë embeds the novel's emotional drama in the material reality of early nineteenth-century property law — entail, mortgage, and primogeniture — showing how legal instruments shape and destroy lives as surely as passions do.

## Manifestations

- **Heathcliff's acquisition of Wuthering Heights**: Hindley's drinking and gambling allow Heathcliff to become the mortgage-holder of Wuthering Heights. "The whole property is mortgaged, and the sole chance for the natural heir is to allow him an opportunity of creating some interest in the creditor's heart" (017-082). Hindley's self-destruction is converted into Heathcliff's wealth.

- **The Grange through Isabella**: Heathcliff marries Isabella knowing that, as Edgar's sister, she holds a claim on the Grange. The marriage is a property transaction disguised as an elopement. Isabella's flight from Heathcliff does not undo the damage — her son Linton becomes another piece of property in Heathcliff's scheme.

- **Catherine Linton as heiress**: Young Catherine is the legal heir to Thrushcross Grange. Heathcliff forces her marriage to the dying Linton Heathcliff to secure control of the Grange after Edgar's death. The forced marriage is the novel's most blatant fusion of property logic and personal cruelty.

- **Hareton's dispossession**: Hareton is the rightful heir to Wuthering Heights, but Heathcliff has raised him as a labourer on his own land. "He's not envious but emulous of your attainments" (031-033) — Hareton does not even know what has been taken from him until Catherine Linton makes him aware of it.

- **Restoration at the end**: The novel closes with Hareton and Catherine Linton engaged to be married, uniting the two properties that Heathcliff had consolidated by force. The resolution is also a restoration — property returns to its rightful line, and the cycle of appropriation ends.

## Key Passages

- **017-082**: Nelly discovers that "the whole property is mortgaged" — Hindley's debts have handed Wuthering Heights to Heathcliff.
- **017-092**: Heathcliff explains his strategy: "Earnshaw had mortgaged every yard of land he owned for ready cash." The legal mechanism of revenge laid bare.
- **010-063**: Edgar's anxiety about "the possible fact that his property, in default of heirs male, might pass into the hands of a stranger" — the gendered logic of inheritance that shapes the plot.

## Character Connections

| Character | Property Position |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Heathcliff** | Dispossessed orphan → owner of both houses. Property is his vehicle of revenge. |
| **Hindley Earnshaw** | Rightful heir who gambles away his inheritance. Property as self-destruction. |
| **Edgar Linton** | Inherited owner of the Grange; dies trying to protect his daughter's claim. |
| **Isabella Linton** | Used as a property conduit; her marriage transfers her claim to Heathcliff. |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Dispossessed heir working his own land. His name over the door (001-011) and his labour on the land are the novel's central irony. |
| **Catherine Linton** | Heiress whose forced marriage transfers the Grange to Heathcliff. |
