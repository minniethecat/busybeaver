## Overview

Marriage in *Wuthering Heights* is never about love — or rather, when it is about love, it fails. Catherine Earnshaw's decision to marry [[Edgar Linton]] rather than [[Heathcliff]], despite her famous declaration "I *am* Heathcliff" (009-077), sets in motion the novel's entire machinery of revenge, property transfer, and generational suffering. Each subsequent marriage — Isabella to Heathcliff, young Catherine to Linton Heathcliff, and finally Catherine to Hareton — functions as a strategic move in a game whose stakes are land, legacy, and the right to define what love means. [[Gimmerton]] Chapel, where these unions are solemnized, becomes a site not of sanctification but of coercion.

## The Central Marriage That Wasn't

Catherine Earnshaw and Heathcliff never marry — and this non-marriage is the novel's generative absence. Catherine chooses Edgar Linton because, as she tells [[Nelly Dean]], "he will be rich, and I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood" (009-076). She believes she can maintain her bond with Heathcliff while enjoying Edgar's social position. Nelly's question — "Why do you love him, Miss Cathy?" — produces Catherine's extraordinary answer: not a list of qualities but an ontological claim. She *is* Heathcliff; her love for Edgar is "like the foliage in the woods," while her love for Heathcliff "resembles the eternal rocks beneath" (009-077). The marriage to Edgar is, by Catherine's own account, a betrayal of her deepest self.

> [009-077] "My love for Linton is like the foliage in the woods: time will change it, I'm well aware, as winter changes the trees. My love for Heathcliff resembles the eternal rocks beneath: a source of little visible delight, but necessary."

## The Revenge Marriages

Heathcliff's return as a wealthy man enables a campaign of marital revenge. He marries [[Isabella Linton]] not from affection but to secure a claim on Thrushcross Grange and to wound Edgar. Isabella's infatuation — "I love him more than ever you loved Edgar" (010-070) — blinds her to Heathcliff's open contempt. The marriage is a catastrophe: Heathcliff hangs her dog, confines her to the Heights, and she eventually flees, pregnant, to die alone in the south.

A generation later, Heathcliff orchestrates the marriage of his sickly son Linton to young Catherine Linton. This marriage is pure coercion — Linton is dying, Catherine is trapped, and the union transfers the Grange to Heathcliff upon Edgar's death. It is the most cynical marriage in the novel, a legal instrument stripped of any pretense of affection.

## The Redemptive Marriage

The novel's final marriage — Hareton Earnshaw and the younger Catherine — offers the only note of hope. Unlike the earlier unions, it is not strategized by others but grows from genuine companionship. Catherine teaches Hareton to read; he plants her garden. Their marriage, projected at the novel's close, restores the Earnshaw name to the Heights and the Linton name to the Grange, reuniting the two houses in a union that undoes Heathcliff's revenge.

## Marriage and Property

In the world of *Wuthering Heights*, marriage is primarily a property transaction. The married woman's legal identity is subsumed into her husband's — a fact Heathcliff exploits ruthlessly. By marrying Isabella, he gains a claim on the Grange; by marrying his son to young Catherine, he secures it. Brontë, writing under a pseudonym in 1847, exposes the violence latent in the era's marriage laws: the same institution that decorates the chapel with flowers can be wielded as a weapon of dispossession.
