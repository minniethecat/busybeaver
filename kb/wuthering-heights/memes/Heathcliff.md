## Overview

Heathcliff is the central figure of *Wuthering Heights*, an enigmatic foundling brought to [[Wuthering-Heights]] by the elder Mr. Earnshaw. His passionate, all-consuming love for [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and his subsequent devastation at her marriage to [[Edgar Linton]] drive the novel's central conflict. Over the course of the narrative, Heathcliff transforms from a wild, mistreated boy into a wealthy, bitter man who systematically destroys both the Earnshaw and Linton families through calculated revenge. He is one of the most complex and controversial figures in Victorian literature — simultaneously a romantic hero, a Gothic villain, and a tragic victim of class prejudice and childhood abuse.

## Biography

Heathcliff is discovered as a dark-skinned foundling on the streets of Liverpool by Mr. Earnshaw, who brings him home to Wuthering Heights. The boy is initially resented by Hindley Earnshaw but forms an intense, inseparable bond with Catherine. After Mr. Earnshaw's death, Hindley degrades Heathcliff to the status of a servant (007-030). The defining crisis of Heathcliff's life comes when he overhears Catherine tell [[Nelly Dean]] that it would degrade her to marry him — though she loves him more than herself. He slips away into the stormy night before hearing her declaration, "I *am* Heathcliff" and "he's more myself than I am" (009-084).

Heathcliff disappears for three years, returning as a wealthy, polished gentleman determined to exact revenge. He systematically dismantles both families: he gambles Hindley into debt and takes possession of Wuthering Heights; he marries [[Isabella Linton]] to spite Edgar and secure a claim to [[Thrushcross-Grange]]; he forces the younger Catherine to marry his sickly son Linton to consolidate his property claim. By the novel's end, Heathcliff owns both estates — but finds the victory hollow. In his final days, he is haunted by Catherine's ghost, eats and sleeps little, and seems to welcome death. He dies in Catherine's old room at Wuthering Heights, the window open toward the moors (034-007).

## Relationships

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Heathcliff's soulmate and obsession. Their bond transcends conventional love; Catherine declares "he's more myself than I am" (009-084). Her choice to marry Edgar Linton for social advancement, despite loving Heathcliff, becomes the wound from which all subsequent tragedy flows. Heathcliff's grief at her death is primal and unhinged; he begs her ghost to haunt him: "I cannot live without my life! I cannot live without my soul!" (016-016).

**Edgar Linton** — Heathcliff's rival and antithesis. Edgar represents the civilized, gentle world of Thrushcross Grange that takes Catherine away from Heathcliff. Heathcliff despises Edgar's weakness and refinement, and his revenge extends to destroying Edgar's family line and seizing his property.

**Hindley Earnshaw** — Heathcliff's childhood tormentor. Hindley's systematic abuse after Mr. Earnshaw's death — degrading Heathcliff to a servant, denying him education, separating him from Catherine — plants the seeds of Heathcliff's later cruelty. In adulthood, Heathcliff exploits Hindley's alcoholism and gambling to take Wuthering Heights, completing the cycle of abuse.

**Isabella Linton** — Heathcliff's wife, married purely as an instrument of revenge. He treats her with calculated cruelty, and she eventually flees to the south of England, bearing his son Linton alone. Nelly describes him as "a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (010-077).

**Nelly Dean** — The narrator of much of Heathcliff's story. Their relationship is complex: Nelly is often critical of Heathcliff's cruelty, yet she is one of the few who witness his full arc from foundling to tormented, vengeful man.

## Character & Traits

Heathcliff embodies the Byronic hero archetype: dark, brooding, passionate, and morally ambiguous. He is defined by extremes — his love for Catherine is as absolute as his hatred for those who wronged him. Brontë uses Heathcliff to explore the boundaries between love and obsession, justice and revenge, nature and civilization.

The novel repeatedly associates Heathcliff with elemental and animalistic imagery. He is compared to "a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (010-077), and his emotional states mirror the wild Yorkshire weather. This association with untamed nature contrasts sharply with the civilized, domestic world of the Lintons, positioning Heathcliff as a force that cannot be assimilated into conventional society.

Crucially, Heathcliff is also a victim — of racism (he is repeatedly described as "dark," "gipsy"), of class prejudice, and of childhood abuse at Hindley's hands. Brontë refuses to let the reader simply condemn him, instead showing how cruelty begets cruelty across generations.

## Critical Reception

Heathcliff has divided critics since the novel's publication. Victorian reviewers were largely appalled — the *Athenaeum* called the novel "coarse" and "disagreeable." Twentieth-century critics reclaimed Heathcliff as a figure of Romantic rebellion against social constraints. Feminist readings have explored the gendered dynamics of his violence; postcolonial criticism has examined the racialized language used to describe him. He remains one of the most debated characters in English literature, inspiring countless adaptations across film, opera, and music.
