# The Second Generation Romance

The romance between [[Catherine Linton]] and [[Hareton Earnshaw]] is the final movement of *Wuthering Heights* and its only genuinely redemptive narrative arc. Where the first generation's story — [[Catherine Earnshaw]], [[Heathcliff]], [[Edgar Linton]] — is driven by passion, betrayal, and destruction, the second generation's story moves, slowly and painfully, toward reconciliation, education, and love. The pairing of Hareton and Cathy reverses the fatal patterns of their parents and offers the novel's closing image of domestic peace and renewal.

## The Forced Meeting

Cathy first encounters Hareton during her secret visits to [[Wuthering Heights]], drawn by curiosity about her cousin [[Linton Heathcliff]] and about the forbidden world of the Heights. Their initial interactions are marked by class condescension on Cathy's part: raised as the sheltered daughter of Thrushcross Grange, she cannot conceal her disdain for Hareton's coarseness and illiteracy. When she discovers that Hareton is her cousin — "My cousin Hareton?" she asks, incredulous — her shock reveals the gulf that class and upbringing have placed between them.

Heathcliff, observing their interactions, takes malicious pleasure in the irony:

> "He'll never be able to emerge from his bathos of coarseness and ignorance. I've taught him to scorn everything extra-animal as silly and weak" (021-036)

Hareton, the legitimate heir of the Heights, has been systematically brutalized into the very condition from which Heathcliff himself once suffered.

## From Antagonism to Alliance

After the deaths of Edgar Linton and Linton Heathcliff, Cathy is effectively imprisoned at the Heights — trapped in the household of the man who destroyed both her parents. Her situation is miserable: she is reduced to the status of a servant, mocked by [[Joseph]], and treated with contempt by Heathcliff. In this extremity, her relationship with Hareton begins to shift.

The turning point comes when Cathy, in a gesture of reconciliation, offers to teach Hareton to read. Hareton, who has long been ashamed of his ignorance, initially resists — his pride, the only thing Heathcliff has not taken from him, rebels against accepting help from the girl who once mocked him. But Cathy's persistence, and her growing recognition of Hareton's innate worth, gradually overcome his resistance. Lockwood, observing the reading lesson from the doorway, captures the transformed dynamic between them:

> "The male speaker began to read: he was a young man, respectably dressed and seated at a table, having a book before him. His handsome features glowed with pleasure, and his eyes kept impatiently wandering from the page to a small white hand over his shoulder, which recalled him by a smart slap on the cheek, whenever its owner detected such signs of inattention." (032-021)

Their first reading lesson — Hareton awkwardly sounding out words, Cathy patient beside him — is one of the novel's most tender scenes and a deliberate inversion of the first generation's dynamic. Where Catherine Earnshaw's relationship with Heathcliff was built on shared wildness, Cathy and Hareton's is built on shared effort toward improvement and mutual understanding.

The emotional result of this shared labour is the novel's most unambiguous image of reciprocal, tender love:

> "The task was done, not free from further blunders; but the pupil claimed a reward, and received at least five kisses; which, however, he generously returned. Then they came to the door, and from their conversation I judged they were about to issue out and have a walk on the moors." (032-022)

## Hareton's Transformation

Hareton Earnshaw's arc from brutalized ignorance to self-possessed adulthood is the novel's most sustained portrait of human resilience. The materials for his transformation are meagre but potent: an innate decency that survives years of systematic degradation, a capacity for loyalty that even Heathcliff cannot extinguish, and the catalyst of Cathy's belief that he can be more than what he has been made to be.

> "I shall not make a martyr of myself — I shall bear it as well as I can. But the worst of it is, the degradation of being supposed to be ignorant when I am not." (032-020)

The reading lessons are not merely a plot device; they are a symbolic reclamation of what was stolen. Hareton's illiteracy is Heathcliff's deliberate work — the visible mark of a childhood destroyed — and his acquisition of literacy is therefore an act of resistance. Each word he learns to read is a word Heathcliff tried to keep from him. Cathy's role as teacher reverses the gender hierarchy of the first generation: where Catherine Earnshaw was the wild girl educated (partially) by the civilizing influence of the Lintons, Cathy Linton is the educated woman who brings civilization — in the form of books, language, and patience — to the wild young man of the Heights.

Hareton's physical courage and his emotional vulnerability coexist in a way that distinguishes him from both Heathcliff and Edgar. He can fight — he proves this when he physically ejects Lockwood from the Heights — but he can also weep, as he does at Heathcliff's death. His grief for the man who destroyed him is not weakness but the mark of a capacity for attachment that Heathcliff, for all his efforts, could not crush. In Hareton, the novel finds a model of masculinity that is strong without being cruel — a resolution, of sorts, to the question posed by the first generation's destructive men.

## The Redemptive Pattern

The romance of Cathy and Hareton is constructed as a deliberate counter-narrative to the tragedy of the first generation. The parallels are exact and carefully drawn:

- Both couples involve a Catherine and a dark-haired, passionate man of the Heights.
- Both Catherines are drawn across the boundary between the Grange and the Heights.
- Both relationships are opposed by Heathcliff.

But the differences are more significant. Where Catherine Earnshaw chose social advancement over love — marrying Edgar Linton while declaring her spiritual identity with Heathcliff — young Cathy chooses Hareton despite his degradation, and works actively to lift him. Where Heathcliff responded to his degradation with vengeance, Hareton responds with the capacity to learn and to love. Where the first generation's passion consumed everything around it, the second generation's love restores what was broken.

Nelly Dean, the novel's primary narrator, marks the significance of this reversal: "They are afraid of nothing... Together they would brave Satan and all his legions" (032-045). The image of Cathy and Hareton sitting together over a book, their heads bent close in the firelight, replaces the earlier image of Catherine and Heathcliff running wild on the moors — and makes a different claim about what love can be.

## Resolution and New Beginning

The marriage of Cathy and Hareton functions as a symbolic resolution of the novel's deepest conflicts — between the Heights and the Grange, between passion and civility, between the claims of the past and the possibilities of the future. It is significant that their union reunites the two houses under the Earnshaw name (through Hareton) while incorporating the cultivated values of the Lintons (through Cathy). The new family will not be a repetition of either the wild Earnshaws or the refined Lintons but a synthesis that transcends both.

The planned move to Thrushcross Grange is equally significant. The Heights — the site of so much suffering, the house that Heathcliff made into an instrument of degradation — will be left to "the spirits" and to Joseph, the last representative of the old, harsh order. The new generation will make its home at the Grange, the house of civility and comfort — but a Grange transformed by its association with the Heights, its refinement now tempered by contact with the rougher, deeper passions of the Earnshaw world. The closing image of the novel — Lockwood pausing at the graves of Catherine, Edgar, and Heathcliff, and finding peace rather than horror — is made possible by this resolution. The dead can rest because the living have found a way forward.

The marriage of Cathy and Hareton, planned for New Year's Day, closes the novel on a note of hard-won hope. Hareton will inherit Wuthering Heights, restored to his rightful place; Cathy brings the civilization of the Grange into partnership with the strength of the Heights. Their union heals the breach between the two houses that has driven the novel's conflict from the beginning.

That this resolution is possible at all — after the devastations wrought by Heathcliff and by the self-destructive passions of the first generation — is the novel's most surprising and most generous claim. The second generation does not repeat the tragedy of the first; they learn from it, and in doing so, earn the peace that their parents could not find.
