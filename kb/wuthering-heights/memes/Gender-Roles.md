## Overview

*Wuthering Heights* systematically tests the boundaries of Victorian gender ideology. Catherine Earnshaw's famous declaration — "I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood" (009-052) — is immediately undercut by her marriage to Edgar Linton, a choice she makes for social advancement rather than love. Throughout the novel, Brontë maps gender transgression onto character: Catherine's wildness resists feminine propriety, Edgar's gentleness codes him as effeminate, Isabella's romantic naivety makes her a victim of domestic abuse, and Heathcliff's violent masculinity destroys everyone around him. The novel's gender politics are neither liberal nor conservative but fundamentally tragic — characters who conform to gender norms suffer, and those who defy them suffer more.

## Manifestations

Catherine Earnshaw embodies the central gender conflict. As a child, she is "a wild, wicked slip" (008-012) who identifies with Heathcliff because he shares her rejection of domestic constraint. Her decision to marry Edgar Linton — "he will be rich, and I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood" (009-052) — represents a calculated submission to the gendered economy of marriage. Yet her famous declaration "I *am* Heathcliff" (009-091) suggests an identity that transcends gender categories entirely.

Isabella Linton's arc illustrates the vulnerability of women within patriarchal marriage. Her elopement with Heathcliff, romanticized as escape from the constraints of Thrushcross Grange, becomes a nightmare of domestic imprisonment. Nelly receives "a short note, announcing her marriage with Heathcliff" (013-007), and within days Isabella realizes the extent of her mistake.

Edgar Linton's gentleness is consistently contrasted with Heathcliff's hyper-masculinity. Where Edgar is gentle, cultured, and domestic, Heathcliff is violent, brooding, and wild. The novel refuses to valorize either model: Edgar's gentleness makes him powerless to protect his family, while Heathcliff's brutality makes him monstrous.

## Key Passages

- **009-052**: Catherine's calculation — "he will be rich, and I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood" — articulates the marriage economy that constrains female ambition.
- **009-126**: The description of young Catherine and Hindley as "mistress" and master establishes how gender hierarchy is inscribed from childhood — "she esteemed herself a woman, and our mistress."
- **007-001**: Frances Earnshaw's arrival and immediate domestic renovation at Wuthering Heights — "she must mind and not grow wild again" — illustrates the civilizing feminine role imposed on women.

## Character Connections

- **Catherine-Earnshaw**: The primary site of gender transgression; her wildness resists feminine domesticity, but her marriage to Edgar submits to it. Her illness and death dramatize the impossibility of reconciling these positions.
- **Isabella-Linton**: Represents the fate of the romantic woman under patriarchal control; her marriage to Heathcliff functions as a cautionary tale about female desire and male violence.
- **Edgar-Linton**: His gentleness and refinement mark him as feminized in the novel's gendered economy; his powerless love for Catherine contrasts with Heathcliff's destructive passion.
- **Heathcliff**: Embodies a toxic hyper-masculinity — violence, revenge, domination — that destroys the women who love him and the men who oppose him.
