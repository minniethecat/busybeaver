## Overview

*Wuthering Heights* is a novel structured around maternal absence. Mrs. Earnshaw dies before the narrative proper begins, leaving Catherine and Hindley motherless. Frances Earnshaw dies giving birth to Hareton. Catherine Earnshaw dies in childbirth, orphaning the younger Cathy. Isabella dies young, leaving Linton Heathcliff motherless. This systematic pattern of maternal death is not incidental but foundational: it produces the novel's distinctive emotional atmosphere of unmothered violence, and ensures that the cycle of abuse passes unchecked from one generation to the next.

## Manifestations

The pattern is established immediately. Nelly Dean notes that "Mrs. Earnshaw expected him by supper-time" (004-034) — one of the few mentions of the elder Mrs. Earnshaw before her death. Her absence defines Hindley's and Catherine's childhood, leaving them in the care of a father who quickly transfers his affection to the foundling Heathcliff.

Frances Earnshaw's death is the novel's first explicit maternal tragedy: "two hours after the mother died, having never recovered sufficient consciousness to miss her child" (016-001). Hareton grows up without a mother, raised by a father consumed by grief and alcoholism, and then by Heathcliff, who deliberately ensures his degradation.

Catherine Earnshaw's death in childbirth repeats the pattern for the next generation. The younger Cathy is "a puny, seven months' child" (016-001), raised by Nelly and Edgar without a mother's presence. Isabella's death some thirteen years later completes the pattern — Linton Heathcliff is "a pale, delicate, effeminate boy" (019-001), his motherlessness inscribed on his body.

## Key Passages

- **016-001**: Catherine's death: "two hours after the mother died, having never recovered sufficient consciousness to miss her child" — the archetypal maternal death in the novel.
- **017-070**: Isabella's death: "Fortunately its mother died before the time arrived" — Nelly's darkly pragmatic assessment of Linton Heathcliff's orphanhood.
- **004-034**: Mrs. Earnshaw expecting her husband's return — one of the few textual traces of the elder matriarch before her disappearance from the narrative.

## Character Connections

- **Catherine-Earnshaw**: Both a motherless daughter and a mother who dies, she links the two generations of maternal absence.
- **Hareton-Earnshaw**: His mother Frances dies in childbirth; raised without maternal care, he becomes the novel's most improvable figure of damaged potential.
- **Catherine-Linton**: Grows up without ever knowing her mother, a gap that shapes her relationship with Nelly and her vulnerability to Heathcliff's manipulation.
- **Linton-Heathcliff**: Isabella dies when he is young; his physical frailty and emotional manipulation by his father are legible as consequences of maternal absence.
