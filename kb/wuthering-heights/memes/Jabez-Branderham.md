## Overview

The Reverend Jabez Branderham is a figure who appears only in [[Mr-Lockwood]]'s nightmare at [[Wuthering-Heights]] — a dream in which Lockwood imagines attending a sermon preached by "the famous Jabez Branderham" in the Chapel of Gimmerden Sough (003-019). Branderham's sermon on the text "Seventy Times Seven, and the First of the Seventy-First" becomes a vision of unending mutual condemnation, as the congregation rises one by one to denounce each other — and finally to denounce Branderham himself as "the sinner of the sin that no Christian need pardon" (003-022). Though Branderham is not a literal character in the novel's primary narrative, [[Joseph]] later claims kinship with him (003-049), and his dream-sermon serves as a dark parable for the cycles of judgment, revenge, and unforgiveness that define the world of the novel.

## Biography

Jabez Branderham exists entirely within the frame of Lockwood's nightmare. Forced to spend the night at Wuthering Heights after a snowstorm, Lockwood is shown to a forbidden room — the old chamber of [[Catherine-Earnshaw]] — where he reads her childhood books and eventually drifts into uneasy dreams. In the second of these dreams, Lockwood finds himself "journeying to hear the famous Jabez Branderham preach" at a chapel (003-020).

The sermon, on the text "Seventy Times Seven," extends interminably as Branderham catalogs "four hundred and ninety" distinct sins — one for each of the seventy-times-seven pardons Christ commands. Each sin is simultaneously a judgment against a member of the congregation. Branderham's rhetorical strategy turns the congregation upon itself, with each person rising to denounce their neighbor. The dream culminates when Lockwood himself is "moved to rise and denounce Jabez Branderham as the sinner of the sin that no Christian need pardon" (003-022), at which point the congregation turns on the preacher and the dream dissolves into chaos — the rapping at the window that reveals [[Catherine-Earnshaw]]'s ghost.

Joseph later adds a genealogical dimension, asking Lockwood: "Was not the Reverend Jabez Branderham akin to you on the mother's side?" (003-049). This wry connection suggests Branderham may have been a real historical preacher in the Gimmerton area whose memory persists in local lore.

## Symbolic Significance

Branderham's sermon is one of the novel's most concentrated allegorical passages. The number "seventy times seven" invokes Matthew 18:22 — Christ's command to forgive without limit — which Branderham immediately perverts into an infinite catalog of sin and condemnation. The congregation's mutual denunciation mirrors the cycles of revenge that engulf the Earnshaw and Linton families, while the dreamer's final act of accusing the accuser anticipates Heathcliff's role as both victim and perpetrator of vengeance.
