Hindley's Death marks the completion of [[Heathcliff]]'s revenge against the man who tormented him as a boy. After years of calculated financial exploitation—lending [[Hindley Earnshaw]] money for gambling and drink while slowly acquiring the mortgages on [[Wuthering Heights]]—Heathcliff watches his enemy destroy himself. Hindley dies at twenty-seven, "drunk as a lord" [017-080], leaving his son [[Hareton Earnshaw]] an orphan entirely dependent on the man who ruined his father.

## The Road to Ruin

Hindley's decline began with the death of his wife [[Frances Earnshaw]], which sent him into "a state of desperate, reckless dissipation" [ch08]. But it was Heathcliff's return that accelerated the destruction. Heathcliff deliberately cultivated Hindley's vices:

> "They sit up all night together continually, and Hindley has been borrowing money on his land, and does nothing but play and drink." [010-084]

Heathcliff's method was patient and methodical. He lent Hindley money against the value of Wuthering Heights, knowing that Hindley's addiction to gambling and drink would ensure he never repaid the debts. By the time Hindley understood what was happening, the estate was already effectively Heathcliff's.

## The Failed Ambush

Hindley's last act of defiance is a murder attempt—and a failure. On the night of [[Isabella's Escape]], Hindley arms himself with a knife and pistol, locks the doors, and waits for Heathcliff. But Heathcliff bursts through a window, disarms Hindley, and beats him savagely. The following morning, Hindley is alive but broken:

> "This morning, when I came down, about half an hour before noon, Mr. Earnshaw was sitting by the fire, deadly sick; his evil genius, almost as gaunt and ghastly, leant against the chimney." [017-048]

"His evil genius" is Heathcliff—the two men locked in a death spiral, one drinking himself to death, the other watching.

## The Death

Hindley's death is reported without sentimentality. Heathcliff tells Nelly:

> "He died true to his character: drunk as a lord. Poor lad! I'm sorry, too. One can't help missing an old companion ... He's barely twenty-seven, it seems; that's your own age: who would have thought you were born in one year?" [017-080]

The line "who would have thought you were born in one year?" is chilling—Heathcliff notes that Hindley is the same age as Nelly, and yet he has already destroyed himself. The casual cruelty of "poor lad" masks Heathcliff's satisfaction. He then elaborates:

> "I happened to leave him ten minutes yesterday afternoon, and in that interval he fastened the two doors of the house against me, and he has spent the night in drinking himself to death deliberately! We broke in this morning, for we heard him snorting like a horse; and there he was, laid over the settle: flaying and scalping would not have wakened him." [017-084]

Hindley dies as he lived in his final years: drunk, violent, and pathetic. The man who was once master of Wuthering Heights is reduced to "carrion."

## The Aftermath

The most significant consequence of Hindley's death is not financial—Heathcliff already controlled the estate—but dynastic. Hindley's son Hareton becomes Heathcliff's ward:

> Heathcliff takes charge of the boy not out of compassion but as an instrument of revenge. He deliberately raises Hareton in ignorance and degradation—"reducing him to the level of a servant"—to see the son of his enemy suffer what he himself suffered as a boy. [ch18]

This is Heathcliff's most refined act of revenge: not killing Hareton but making him into what Hindley made Heathcliff—a brute. The boy is denied education, taught to swear, and kept in a state of dependency. Heathcliff says with satisfaction:

> "Now, my bonny lad, you are *mine*! And we'll see if one tree won't grow as crooked as another, with the same wind to twist it!" [ch18]

## Significance

Hindley's death is the terminal point of the novel's first cycle of revenge:

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **Revenge completed** | The boy Hindley degraded has become the man who destroys him |
| **Cycle perpetuation** | Hareton becomes the new Heathcliff—the degraded child |
| **Class reversal** | The former master's son is now a servant in his own house |
| **Moral complexity** | Hindley was cruel but Heathcliff's revenge is disproportionate |

Hindley Earnshaw is not a sympathetic character—he abused Heathcliff as a child, neglected his own son, and drank himself to death. But his destruction is also a demonstration of Heathcliff's inhuman patience and the corrupting power of [[Revenge]].
