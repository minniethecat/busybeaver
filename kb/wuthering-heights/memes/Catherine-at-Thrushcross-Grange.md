Catherine at Thrushcross Grange is the pivotal childhood event that sets the course of the entire tragedy in Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights*. One Sunday evening, the young [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and [[Heathcliff]] escape from [[Wuthering Heights]] and spy on the [[Linton family]] at [[Thrushcross Grange]]. What begins as a childish prank ends with Catherine bitten by the Lintons' guard dog and taken into the Grange for five weeks—a period that transforms her from Heathcliff's wild companion into a young lady conscious of [[Social Class]].

## The Escape

Heathcliff recounts the adventure to [[Nelly Dean]] after returning alone to Wuthering Heights. He and Cathy, tired of the pious restrictions imposed by [[Hindley Earnshaw]] and [[Joseph]] during Sunday observance, "escaped from the wash-house to have a ramble at liberty" [006-006]. Seeing the lights of Thrushcross Grange across the moor, they decided to investigate whether the Linton children "passed their Sunday evenings standing shivering in corners" as they themselves were made to do.

## Spying Through the Window

The two children crept through the Grange's park, Catherine barefoot because her shoes had been lost in the bog. They "planted ourselves on a flower-plot under the drawing-room window" and, standing on the basement ledge, looked inside [006-006].

What they saw was a vision of a different world:

> a splendid place carpeted with crimson, and crimson-covered chairs and tables, and a pure white ceiling bordered by gold, a shower of glass-drops hanging in silver chains from the centre, and shimmering with little soft tapers. [006-006]

But the scene within was not one of refinement. [[Edgar Linton|Edgar]] and [[Isabella Linton]] were fighting over a small dog, "shrieking as if witches were running red-hot needles into her" (Isabella) while Edgar "stood on the hearth weeping silently" [006-006]. Heathcliff and Catherine "laughed outright at the petted things; we did despise them!"—a judgment that would prove bitterly ironic given Catherine's eventual marriage to Edgar [006-006].

## The Attack

When the Lintons heard the laughter, they "shot like arrows to the door." Heathcliff and Catherine fled, but Catherine fell. The Lintons' bull-dog, Skulker, seized her ankle:

> "The devil had seized her ankle, Nelly: I heard his abominable snorting. She did not yell out—no! she would have scorned to do it, if she had been spitted on the horns of a mad cow." [006-008]

Heathcliff, in a fury, thrust a stone between the dog's jaws to free Catherine. But when the servant Robert appeared, Catherine was carried into the Grange while Heathcliff—immediately identified as an outsider—was cast out.

## The Two Treatments

The contrast between how the Lintons treated the two children is the event's most pointed social commentary:

| Catherine | Heathcliff |
|-----------|-----------|
| Carried inside, feet washed with warm water | Dragged into the garden and expelled |
| Given negus, cakes, enormous slippers | Called "a little Lascar, or an American or Spanish castaway" [006-011] |
| Dried hair, combed, wheeled to the fire | "A wicked boy... quite unfit for a decent house" [006-012] |
| Made "as merry as she could be" | Told to "march directly" home alone |

Heathcliff, watching through the still-looped curtain, saw Catherine transformed before his eyes: "she is so immeasurably superior to them—to everybody on earth, is she not, Nelly?" [006-013]. But Catherine was also being drawn into the Linton world of comfort, manners, and class distinction.

## The Aftermath

Catherine remained at Thrushcross Grange for five weeks, until Christmas. When she returned to Wuthering Heights, she was changed:

> Instead of a wild, hatless little savage jumping into the house, and rushing to squeeze us all breathless, there alighted from a handsome black pony a very dignified person, with brown ringlets falling from the cover of a feathered beaver, and a long cloth habit, which she was obliged to hold up with both hands that she might sail in. [ch07]

The wild child who ran barefoot across the moors had become a young lady. The class divide that would eventually lead Catherine to choose Edgar Linton over Heathcliff was forged in this single evening.
