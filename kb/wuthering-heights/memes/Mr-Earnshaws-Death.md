## Overview

Mr. Earnshaw's death in Chapter 5 of *Wuthering Heights* is the novel's first major narrative pivot. Until this moment, the Earnshaw household at Wuthering Heights operates under the patriarch's benevolent but erratic rule — Heathcliff is favored, Hindley is jealous, and Catherine is wild but protected. The patriarch's death removes the only moderating force in the household and unleashes the full force of Hindley's resentment against Heathcliff, establishing the conditions for the hero-villain's transformation and the cascading cycle of revenge that structures the entire narrative.

## Event Sequence

### Before

Mr. Earnshaw returns from Liverpool with the foundling Heathcliff, whom he favors above his own children (Chapter 4). Hindley, displaced and resentful, is eventually sent away to college. The household settles into an uneasy equilibrium: Heathcliff and Catherine form their intense bond, while Hindley nurses his grievances from a distance.

### The Death

Mr. Earnshaw's health declines gradually. Nelly describes him as growing "weaker and weaker" through Chapter 5 (005-001), until one evening he dies quietly by the fireside, with Catherine and Heathcliff at his side. The death itself is domestic and undramatic — it is the consequences that are cataclysmic.

### After

Hindley returns for the funeral, bringing with him a wife — "He brought a wife with him" (006-001) — and immediately asserts his authority as the new master of Wuthering Heights. He relegates Heathcliff to the status of a servant, strips him of education, and begins the systematic degradation that will transform Heathcliff from a sensitive, if wild, child into the instrument of revenge that defines the novel's second half.

## Significance

Mr. Earnshaw's death functions as the origin point for the novel's central conflict. While Heathcliff's arrival from Liverpool was the precipitating cause, the patriarch's death is the enabling cause: it removes the only authority figure who protected Heathcliff. The event also establishes the novel's pattern of death as a mechanism of power transfer — each major death in *Wuthering Heights* redistributes property, status, and control, and Mr. Earnshaw's is the first in this chain.

The juxtaposition with Hindley's return — bringing a wife who will herself die in childbirth — also initiates the novel's generational structure. The patriarch's death opens the door for the first generation's dramas (Hindley's abuse, Catherine's marriage choice, Heathcliff's flight and return), which in turn produce the circumstances for the second generation's suffering and eventual redemption.

## Participants

- **Hindley-Earnshaw**: Returns from college with Frances to claim his inheritance; his transformation from resentful son to abusive master begins here.
- **Heathcliff**: Loses his protector; the patriarch's death exposes him to Hindley's unchecked cruelty.
- **Catherine-Earnshaw**: Loses her father; with Hindley as master, the domestic order that shaped her childhood is dismantled.
- **Frances-Earnshaw**: Arrives as Hindley's new wife in the immediate aftermath of the death; her presence initiates the civilizing of Wuthering Heights and her own maternal tragedy.
