## Overview

Mr. Earnshaw is the patriarch of the Earnshaw family and the master of [[Wuthering-Heights]] at the start of the narrative. His impulsive decision to bring the orphaned [[Heathcliff]] home from Liverpool sets in motion the entire chain of events that drives the novel — the intense bond between Heathcliff and [[Catherine-Earnshaw]], the jealousy of [[Hindley-Earnshaw]], and the cycle of revenge that consumes two generations. Though Mr. Earnshaw appears only briefly before his death in Chapter 5, his favoritism toward Heathcliff over his own son Hindley is the original wound from which all subsequent tragedy flows.

## Biography

Mr. Earnshaw is introduced as "the old master" when Nelly Dean recounts his fateful journey to Liverpool. Preparing to depart, he asks his children what they would like as gifts; Hindley requests a fiddle and Catherine a riding-whip. Instead, Mr. Earnshaw returns with "a dirty, ragged, black-haired child" — Heathcliff — whom he found starving on the streets of Liverpool (004-034). He tells his family that he could not bear to leave the child to perish.

From the moment of Heathcliff's arrival, Mr. Earnshaw shows marked favoritism toward the foundling, to the detriment of his own son. Hindley, who had hoped for a fiddle, received only a broken one — a symbolic displacement that foreshadows his loss of paternal affection. Mr. Earnshaw's partiality grows more pronounced over time: he tolerates Heathcliff's manipulative behavior while growing increasingly harsh toward Hindley. Nelly notes that Hindley "learned to regard his father as an oppressor rather than a friend" (005-003).

In the course of time Mr. Earnshaw began to fail. He grew increasingly frail and irritable, and his treatment of Hindley became harsher still. Hindley was eventually sent away to college — a decision Mr. Earnshaw agreed to "with a heavy spirit" (005-002), hoping distance would ease the tension. But Joseph's religious lecturing only deepened the master's anxiety about his soul, and his health continued to decline.

The end came quietly one October evening, when Catherine, then a child, sat singing to her father in his chair. "The hour came, at last, that ended Mr. Earnshaw's troubles on earth," Nelly records. "He died quietly in his chair one October evening, seated by the fire" (005-006). His death leaves a power vacuum at Wuthering Heights that Hindley, returning for the funeral with a wife in tow, will fill — with catastrophic consequences for Heathcliff.

## Relationships

**Heathcliff** — Mr. Earnshaw's relationship with Heathcliff is the novel's original catalytic bond. He finds the boy in Liverpool and immediately treats him as a favored son, naming him after a son who died in childhood. His doting affection for Heathcliff provokes Hindley's jealousy and gives Heathcliff a taste of love and belonging that will fuel his later obsession with Catherine. After Mr. Earnshaw's death, Heathcliff's place in the household changed dramatically — Hindley returned as master and "the young master had learned to regard his father as an oppressor rather than a friend" (005-003).

**Hindley Earnshaw** — Mr. Earnshaw's relationship with his biological son deteriorates sharply after Heathcliff's arrival. He perceives Hindley's resentment as ingratitude and cruelty, failing to recognize his own role in creating the rivalry. When Mr. Earnshaw once bought a couple of colts, Heathcliff took the handsomest, and when it went lame, he demanded Hindley's — a pattern of preferential treatment that Nelly recounts as typical (004-042).

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Catherine remains largely in her father's good graces, being "too mischievous and wayward for a favourite" yet not the target of his anger. She is at his bedside when he dies.

**Joseph** — The servant whose religious harangues influence Mr. Earnshaw in his final, weakening days, deepening the master's anxieties about the state of his soul (005-003).
