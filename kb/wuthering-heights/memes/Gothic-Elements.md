# Gothic Elements

*Wuthering Heights* is deeply indebted to the Gothic literary tradition that
flourished in late eighteenth- and early nineteenth-century Britain, yet Emily
Brontë's use of Gothic conventions is neither derivative nor straightforward.
She takes the familiar machinery of Gothic fiction — the haunted house, the
ghost at the window, the dark and brooding anti-hero, dreams and visions — and
reconfigures it within a psychologically realist framework. The supernatural
in *Wuthering Heights* is never fully explained away, nor is it simply
accepted; it exists in an ambiguous space that mirrors the novel's broader
refusal of easy categorization.

## Overview: What Is Gothic Fiction?

The Gothic novel emerged in Britain in the late eighteenth century with Horace Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* (1764) and flourished through the works of Ann Radcliffe, Matthew Lewis, and, later, Mary Shelley. Its defining conventions include ancient, crumbling buildings (castles, abbeys, isolated mansions); supernatural occurrences (ghosts, curses, prophetic dreams); dark, brooding villain-heroes tormented by hidden pasts; imperiled heroines imprisoned or pursued through labyrinthine spaces; and an atmosphere of terror, mystery, and the sublime. The Gothic is, at its core, a literature of transgression — it explores the boundaries between life and death, sanity and madness, nature and the supernatural, civilization and savagery.

By the time Emily Brontë wrote *Wuthering Heights* in the 1840s, the Gothic had evolved from the extravagant terrors of Radcliffe and Lewis into a more domesticated form — the Victorian sensation novel — and had been absorbed into the broader Romantic movement's fascination with extreme emotional states. Brontë's contribution was to strip away the Gothic's more theatrical elements (no trapdoors, no poison rings, no disguised monks) and relocate its essential energies — imprisonment, haunting, obsession, the violation of natural boundaries — into the landscape of the Yorkshire moors and the psychology of her characters. The result is a Gothic that feels not exotic and distant but immediate and unnervingly real: the ghosts are not visitors from another realm but extensions of emotional states so intense that the boundary between inner and outer reality collapses.

This overview provides the context for understanding the specific Gothic elements that follow: the house, the dreams, the anti-hero, and the fusion of the natural with the supernatural that is Brontë's most distinctive contribution to the tradition.

## The Haunted House

Wuthering Heights itself is a quintessential Gothic dwelling. Built in 1500
and exposed to the "atmospheric tumult" of the moorland storms, it is
described by Lockwood as having "a quantity of grotesque carving lavished
over the front" and narrow, deeply-set windows [001-002]. The house's
architecture — cramped, ancient, forbidding — embodies the Gothic principle
that buildings can absorb and project the psychological states of their
inhabitants.

Lockwood's first impression of the house is dominated by its Gothic excess:

> I paused to admire a quantity of grotesque carving lavished over the front,
> and especially about the principal door; above which, among a wilderness of
> crumbling griffins and shameless little boys, I detected the date "1500,"
> and the name "Hareton Earnshaw." [001-011]

The interior is no less Gothic. Lockwood's bedroom, once occupied by the
first Catherine, contains her childhood diary scrawled in the margins of
religious books — a classic Gothic device of discovered manuscripts that
reveal buried secrets [003-015]. The room itself becomes a site of
supernatural visitation.

## Dreams and Nightmares

Lockwood's nightmare at Wuthering Heights is one of the most powerful Gothic
set-pieces in English fiction. His dream of Jabez Branderham's interminable
sermon gives way to the apparition of Catherine's ghost at the window:

> The intense horror of nightmare came over me: I tried to draw back my arm,
> but the hand clung to it, and a most melancholy voice sobbed, "Let me in —
> let me in!" [003-027]

The ghost child's cry — "I'm come home: I'd lost my way on the moor!"
[003-042] — is a classic Gothic haunting, yet Brontë renders it with a
psychological intensity that transcends genre convention. Heathcliff's
response to Lockwood's account — throwing open the window and crying "Come
in! come in! Cathy, do come" [003-045] — transforms the Gothic ghost story
into an expression of devastating human longing.

## The Byronic Anti-Hero

Heathcliff is a reimagining of the Gothic villain-hero made famous by Ann
Radcliffe and refined by Lord Byron. Dark, brooding, of mysterious origin,
driven by passions that seem to exceed ordinary human capacity, he fits the
Byronic template. Yet Brontë complicates the type: Heathcliff's cruelty is
not glamorized but shown in its full ugliness, and his obsession with
Catherine is presented as both sublime and pathological.

Nelly Dean's assessment — "he is clearly on the verge of madness" [013-044] —
grounds the Gothic excess in psychological realism. Heathcliff is not a
supernatural being but a man destroyed by love and loss.

## The Natural Supernatural

Brontë's most distinctive Gothic innovation is her fusion of the supernatural
with the natural world. Ghosts in *Wuthering Heights* are not visitors from
another realm but seem to arise from the landscape itself. Catherine's ghost
comes not from heaven or hell but from the moor — she has "lost her way"
there. The country folk's conviction that the Heights is haunted underscores
how thoroughly the Gothic has saturated the natural world:

> "Well, it is — swarming with ghosts and goblins! You have reason enough
> to lie awake at night." [003-047]

After Heathcliff's death, the country folk report seeing his ghost
walking the moors with Catherine's, "on every rainy night" [034-022]. The
supernatural is not an intrusion upon nature but an extension of it.

## Transformation of the Gothic

Unlike conventional Gothic fiction, which typically resolves its mysteries
and restores order, *Wuthering Heights* refuses closure. The novel's final
image — the three headstones under a benign sky — has been read by some
critics as peaceful resolution and by others as Lockwood's failure to
comprehend the Gothic darkness that persists beneath the surface. This
ambiguity is Brontë's lasting contribution to the Gothic tradition: she
dissolves the boundary between the natural and the supernatural, leaving
both permanently unsettled.
