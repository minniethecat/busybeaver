## Overview

Isabella Linton is Edgar Linton's younger sister, whose romantic infatuation with [[Heathcliff]] leads her into a marriage of calculated cruelty. She serves as an innocent who wanders into the Gothic nightmare of [[Wuthering-Heights]] and barely escapes — her narrative function is to witness and testify to Heathcliff's inhumanity from within. Isabella's character arc from naive romantic to bitter, disillusioned survivor is one of the novel's sharpest illustrations of how the world of Wuthering Heights corrupts and destroys those who enter it unprepared.

## Biography

Isabella grows up at [[Thrushcross-Grange]] in comfort and refinement, sheltered alongside her brother [[Edgar Linton]]. Her first significant appearance shows her as an eleven-year-old, shrieking over a pet dog in the Linton drawing-room while Heathcliff and [[Catherine Earnshaw]] watch through the window with contempt (007-011). This initial portrait establishes her as pampered, delicate, and utterly unprepared for the harsh realities beyond the Grange.

When Heathcliff returns as a wealthy gentleman, Isabella becomes infatuated with him, misreading his brooding intensity as romantic depth. Catherine, understanding Heathcliff's true nature, warns Isabella directly: "Tell her what Heathcliff is: an unreclaimed creature, without refinement, without cultivation... he's a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (010-077). Isabella dismisses the warning as jealousy.

Isabella elopes with Heathcliff, and the marriage immediately reveals itself as a deliberate instrument of Heathcliff's revenge against Edgar. At the Heights, Isabella endures brutal treatment. She writes to Nelly: "Is Mr. Heathcliff a man? If so, is he mad? And if not, is he a devil? I sha'n't tell my reasons for making this inquiry; but I beseech you to explain, if you can, what I have married" (013-007). Her account of the household — the drunken Hindley, the snarling Joseph, the savage Hareton, and Heathcliff's calculated cruelty — provides one of the novel's most vivid depictions of the Heights as a domestic hell.

Isabella eventually escapes, fleeing to the south of England where she gives birth to [[Linton Heathcliff]]. She lives in exile for approximately twelve years, dying when Linton is about twelve. Before her death, she entrusts Linton to Edgar's care, but Heathcliff claims his son as his legal heir, bringing the sickly boy to the Heights as another piece in his revenge.

## Relationships

**Heathcliff** — Isabella's husband and tormentor. Her infatuation with him is a catastrophic misreading: she sees a Byronic hero where in fact there is a deliberate abuser. Heathcliff's treatment of Isabella is perhaps his cruelest act, as she is the most innocent of his victims. He admits that he married her solely to spite Edgar and secure a claim to the Grange, and that "she cannot reason on the subject as I can" — acknowledging his exploitation of her naivety (011-012).

**Edgar Linton** — Isabella's brother, who severs ties with her after her elopement. Edgar's refusal to aid or contact Isabella is one of his moral failures — his rigid adherence to propriety prevents him from helping the sister who most needs rescue.

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Isabella's sister-in-law, who tries to warn her about Heathcliff. Catherine's warning is motivated by genuine understanding rather than jealousy, and it proves tragically accurate.

**Linton Heathcliff** — Isabella's son, born in exile and raised without knowledge of his father. Isabella's final years are devoted to protecting Linton from Heathcliff's influence, though ultimately she fails.

## Character & Traits

Isabella begins as a naive romantic, shaped by the sentimental novels of her era. Her attraction to Heathcliff is based on a misreading of Gothic convention — she expects a dark hero and discovers a genuine monster. Her naivety is not simply foolishness; it reflects the dangerous gap between the sheltered world of the Grange and the brutal reality of the Heights.

After her ordeal, Isabella transforms into a clear-eyed, bitterly perceptive survivor. Her letters to Nelly are among the novel's most lucid passages, stripping away any romantic illusions about Heathcliff. Her escape — walking from the Heights to the Grange through deep snow, pregnant and bleeding — is an act of desperate courage that reveals a strength neither Edgar nor Heathcliff anticipated.
