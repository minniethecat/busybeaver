## Overview

Funerals in *Wuthering Heights* are never merely ceremonies of mourning — they are hinge-points where property changes hands, allegiances shift, and the past violently intrudes upon the present. From Catherine Earnshaw's burial to Heathcliff's self-orchestrated end, each funeral reshapes the social and emotional landscape of the Heights and the Grange. Brontë uses these rituals to expose the gap between the official, chapel-sanctioned version of events and the disorderly reality of grief, desire, and revenge that surrounds them.

## Key Funerals

### Catherine Earnshaw (Chapter XVI)

Catherine's death in childbirth is followed by a funeral that satisfies no one. Heathcliff, barred from the house during her final hours, keeps vigil in the garden. After the burial, he secretly opens her grave, driven by the need to see her face one last time — and, years later, bribes the sexton to open her coffin again so that his own body may be laid beside hers, the adjoining sides of their coffins removed so their dust may mingle. The official funeral at [[Gimmerton]] Chapel is a decorous public event; the real ritual — the grave-violation, the bribery, the demand for eternal proximity — happens in secret.

> [016-002] "I'll have her in my arms again, regardless of the coffin that separates us!"

### Edgar Linton (Chapter XXVIII)

Edgar Linton's funeral takes place at Gimmerton Chapel with "the concourse thickening outside the chapel porch" (015-029). It is a proper, respectable burial befitting the master of [[Thrushcross-Grange]]. But the funeral is attended by Heathcliff's shadow — he has already engineered the marriage of young Catherine to his son Linton, securing his inheritance of the Grange. Edgar's death is the final transfer of property from the Linton line to Heathcliff.

### Heathcliff (Chapter XXXIV)

Heathcliff's own death is the novel's strangest — a self-willed starvation that Nelly and [[Joseph]] are powerless to prevent. He dies in Catherine's old room at the Heights, the window open to the rain, a "frightful, life-like gaze of exultation" on his dead face. His funeral is a quiet affair; the country folk swear his ghost walks with Catherine's on the moors. He is buried in [[Gimmerton]] kirkyard beside Catherine, and beside Edgar — three graves whose arrangement speaks the novel's final, unresolved sentence about who belongs with whom.

## Funerals as Social Ritual

The funerals at Gimmerton Chapel serve a double function: they are the community's sanctioned acknowledgment of death, and they are the occasions when the novel's transgressive passions become most visible. Church bells ring, the parson presides, the congregation gathers — but outside the chapel porch, and in the kirkyard after dark, a different order of feeling asserts itself. As Nelly observes, the real mourners are often somewhere else: Heathcliff in the garden, Catherine's ghost at the window, Hareton weeping over his dead master.
