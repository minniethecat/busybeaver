# Religion and Faith

Religion in *Wuthering Heights* appears primarily in two forms: the oppressive, hypocritical Calvinism of [[Joseph]], and the deeper, more ambiguous spiritual yearning that runs through the novel's treatment of love, death, and the afterlife. Emily Brontë does not offer a simple critique of organized religion so much as she dramatizes the failure of doctrinal Christianity to accommodate the intensity of human passion — and the way religious language is repurposed by characters who exist outside its moral framework.

## Joseph's Religion

Joseph, the elderly servant at [[Wuthering Heights]], is the novel's most persistent voice of religious authority, and he is presented almost entirely as a figure of grotesque parody. His speech is a torrent of Yorkshire dialect peppered with biblical quotations, yet his piety coexists with cruelty, miserliness, and a complete lack of charity. He uses his faith as a weapon — to judge, to condemn, and to assert his own righteousness at the expense of everyone around him.

Lockwood's first encounter with Joseph sets the tone. When the snowstorm traps the narrator at the Heights, Joseph refuses him shelter with a biblical justification: "The Lord help us! ... he's noan o' t' Lord's folk" [002-018]. His Calvinist doctrine of predestination becomes an excuse for inhospitality: if you are not among the elect, you deserve nothing. Later, Joseph is shown forcing [[Catherine-Earnshaw]] and [[Heathcliff]] to endure long, punishing sermons, and he interprets every misfortune as divine punishment — usually for the sins of others rather than his own.

## Biblical Language and Allusion

Brontë herself was the daughter of an Anglican clergyman, and the novel is saturated with biblical language even where no explicit religious point is being made. Heathcliff, the character most alienated from Christian morality, frequently uses the language of damnation, hell, and heaven to express his emotional states. His love for Catherine is described in terms that appropriate religious devotion: "I cannot live without my soul" [016-011].

Catherine, for her part, frames her moral dilemma in explicitly theological language. Her famous speech contrasting her love for Edgar and Heathcliff uses heaven and hell as metaphors: her marriage to Edgar is "like the foliage in the woods" while her bond with Heathcliff resembles "the eternal rocks beneath" [009-040]. The religious framework is present but inverted — Catherine's "heaven" would be a world where she could keep both men, and her true "religion" is her bond with Heathcliff.

## Death, Burial, and the Afterlife

The novel's treatment of death and burial is deeply ambiguous in religious terms. Characters die without the consolations of faith — Catherine's death is marked by a storm and Heathcliff's desperate plea that her soul haunt him, not by prayers or last rites. Heathcliff, dying nearly two decades later, refuses the minister's visit and arranges to be buried beside Catherine with the sides of their coffins open to each other — a literal rejection of the Christian resurrection in favor of a physical, earthly reunion in the grave.

Nelly Dean, the most conventionally pious of the main characters, attempts to impose Christian interpretations on the events she narrates. After Heathcliff's death, she tells Lockwood that the country folk swear "his ghost walks" — but she herself insists "he is dead, and buried, and safe; and I'm right glad of it" [034-053]. Her relief is more superstitious than theological; the novel offers no settled Christian resolution.

## Nelly Dean's Folk Religion

Nelly Dean represents a third religious mode in the novel, distinct from both Joseph's doctrinal Calvinism and Heathcliff's passionate anti-religion. Her faith is practical, superstitious, and rooted in the folk wisdom of the Yorkshire countryside rather than in systematic theology. She believes in ghosts — or at least she does not disbelieve in them — and her moral judgments, though shaped by Christian teachings, are applied with a flexibility that Joseph would find heretical.

When Lockwood recounts his nightmare of Jabez Branderham's sermon, Nelly does not dismiss the supernatural: "I was superstitious about dreams then, and am still" [003-061]. Her faith acknowledges the uncanny without needing to reconcile it with doctrine. She is the only character who prays, who attends church, who speaks the language of Christian morality — and yet her religion is capacious enough to accommodate the strange and the inexplicable. In this, she may represent Brontë's own position: not the rejection of faith, but the refusal to let faith narrow the scope of human experience.

## The Failure of Institutional Religion

Gimmerton Chapel, the only church mentioned in the novel, is conspicuously inadequate. It is the setting for Lockwood's nightmare of Jabez Branderham's interminable sermon — a dream that satirises the tedium and irrelevance of doctrinal preaching — and for the marriages and funerals that mark the novel's key transitions. The chapel's physical decay ("the roof is in a sad state") mirrors the spiritual decay of the community it ostensibly serves.

The absence of any compassionate religious figure — no wise vicar, no charitable believer — leaves a spiritual vacuum that the novel's passions rush to fill. In this sense, *Wuthering Heights* is not an anti-religious novel; it is a novel that finds conventional religion inadequate to the scale of human suffering and desire it depicts.
