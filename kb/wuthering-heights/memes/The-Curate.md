## Overview

The curate of [[Gimmerton]] is an unnamed ecclesiastical figure who appears briefly in the early chapters of *Wuthering Heights*. He first proposes that the young [[Hindley-Earnshaw]] be sent away to college — a suggestion that [[Mr-Earnshaw|old Mr. Earnshaw]] reluctantly accepts — and later joins [[Joseph]] in reprimanding the household's neglect of religious observance. As [[Wuthering-Heights]] descends into violence and neglect under Hindley's rule, the curate quietly withdraws, ceasing his visits altogether.

## Biography

The curate is first mentioned when the old Mr. Earnshaw's health begins to fail. "At last, our curate (we had a curate then who made the living by teaching the young Lintons and Earnshaws, and farming his bit of land himself) advised that the young man [Hindley] should be sent to college" (005-002). Mr. Earnshaw agreed, though "with a heavy spirit," and Hindley departed — a removal that momentarily eased household tensions but ultimately set the stage for Hindley's return as master after his father's death.

The curate also took an interest in the children's religious education. Together with Joseph, he "reprimanded [Heathcliff's] carelessness" about attending church on Sundays (006-005). Nelly notes that the curate's influence was part of a broader attempt to impose order on the increasingly disorderly household.

But as Wuthering Heights deteriorated under Hindley's drunken rule, the curate abandoned his efforts. Nelly records with grim understatement: "an infernal house we had. The curate dropped calling, and nobody decent came near us" (008-015). His withdrawal marks a symbolic threshold — the point at which Wuthering Heights passed beyond the pale of respectable Gimmerton society.

## Significance

The curate, though barely characterized, serves as a narrative barometer of Wuthering Heights's moral descent. His initial engagement with the household represents the thin thread of community and religious order connecting the Heights to Gimmerton. His eventual abandonment of his pastoral duties toward the family signals the depth of the household's collapse under Hindley — a collapse that Heathcliff will later exploit.
