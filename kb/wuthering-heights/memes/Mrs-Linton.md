## Overview

Mrs. Linton is the matriarch of [[Thrushcross-Grange]] and the mother of [[Edgar-Linton]] and [[Isabella-Linton]]. Though she speaks few lines in the novel, her influence is felt in the sheltered, refined atmosphere of the Grange — a world utterly alien to the wild children of [[Wuthering-Heights]]. Her admission of [[Catherine-Earnshaw]] into the Grange after the bulldog incident marks a pivotal turning point: Catherine's five-week stay transforms her from a "wild, hatless little savage" into a young lady, setting the stage for her eventual marriage to Edgar and the generational tragedy that follows.

## Biography

Mrs. Linton first appears when Catherine Earnshaw and [[Heathcliff]], spying on Thrushcross Grange, are discovered after Catherine is bitten by the Lintons' bulldog. While Heathcliff is unceremoniously ejected as a "gypsy" and "a wicked boy" unfit for decent company, Mrs. Linton, together with her husband, takes Catherine in — drawn by pity for the injured child and curiosity about her identity (006-006).

During Catherine's convalescence, Mrs. Linton undertakes the project of civilizing her. Nelly notes that "old Mr. and Mrs. Linton" oversaw her transformation: Mrs. Linton "placed her spectacles on her nose and looked her over" with an appraising eye (006-008). It was on Mrs. Linton's explicit condition that her own children be kept away from Heathcliff, whom she regarded as unfit company (007-014).

Mrs. Linton's health declined in the years following Catherine's stay. She "paid us several visits, to be sure, and set things to rights" during Catherine's illness after returning to the Heights (009-125). She died sometime before the main events of the novel's second half, leaving only the elder Mr. Linton to witness Edgar's marriage and Isabella's elopement.

## Relationships

**Edgar Linton** — Her son, raised in the sheltered gentility she cultivated at the Grange. Mrs. Linton's class-consciousness shaped Edgar's early understanding of social boundaries, though he would later defy those boundaries by marrying Catherine Earnshaw.

**Isabella Linton** — Her daughter, similarly raised under her protective gaze. Isabella's later disastrous marriage to Heathcliff represents the complete inversion of everything Mrs. Linton sought to protect her children from.

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Mrs. Linton recognized something salvageable in the wild child from the Heights and undertook to refine her. Her decision to admit Catherine to the Grange — while excluding Heathcliff — inadvertently drives a wedge between the two that will have fatal consequences.

**Heathcliff** — Mrs. Linton saw Heathcliff only once, during the incident at the Grange, and her judgment was immediate and absolute: he was "a wicked boy" and a "gypsy," not fit to associate with her children (006-006/007-014). Her class prejudice becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy, as Heathcliff's later revenge targets exactly what she sought to protect.
