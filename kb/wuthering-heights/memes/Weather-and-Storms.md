## Overview

"Weathering" is built into the name *Wuthering Heights*: the house is named for "the atmospheric tumult to which its station is exposed in stormy weather" (001-010). Weather in the novel is never neutral background — it is an active narrative force that traps Lockwood at the Heights, mirrors Catherine's emotional storms, and presides over the novel's most decisive moments. The thunderstorm during which Heathcliff disappears (009-126) is not symbolic ornament but plot mechanism: it keeps Nelly awake to discover his absence. Brontë's weather is the intersection of the natural and the human — the world outside the window insisting on its presence in the world within.

## Manifestations

- **The house's name as weather (001-010)** : Lockwood's etymological gloss on "Wuthering" establishes weather as the novel's founding metaphor: "the atmospheric tumult to which its station is exposed." The Heights is defined by what it endures.

- **Snow as entrapment**: The snowstorm that traps Lockwood at the Heights (002-030–003-020) is the novel's first and most consequential weather event — without it, there is no nightmare, no ghost, no story. "The roads will be buried already" (002-056) is both a literal observation and a metaphor for the narrative to come.

- **The thunderstorm of Heathcliff's departure (009-126)** : "There was a violent wind, as well as thunder, and either one or the other split a tree off at the corner of the building." The storm externalises the emotional catastrophe — Catherine's confession, Heathcliff's overhearing, his flight — and its physical violence ("split a tree off") marks the rupture as irreversible.

- **Wind as presence**: Throughout the novel, wind is the sound of the outside pressing in — the "gusty wind" and "soughing of the firs" (002-028) are the acoustic signature of Wuthering Heights. The wind that rattles the window in Lockwood's dream is the same wind that Catherine's ghost rides.

- **Calm weather as resolution**: The novel's final image — "I lingered round them, under that benign sky" (034-028) — replaces storm with stillness. The weather has gentled; the cycle has broken.

## Key Passages

- **001-010**: The etymology of "Wuthering" — weather as the house's defining condition.
- **002-030**: Lockwood's host warns him: "Do you know that you run a risk of being lost in the marshes?" The snow that will trap him is foreshadowed.
- **009-126**: The thunderstorm of Heathcliff's departure — the novel's climactic fusion of weather and plot.
