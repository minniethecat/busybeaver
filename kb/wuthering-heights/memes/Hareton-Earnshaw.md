## Overview

Hareton Earnshaw is the son of [[Hindley Earnshaw]] and [[Frances Earnshaw]], and the last surviving male heir of the Earnshaw family. Despite being born into the landed gentry, Hareton is deliberately raised in ignorance and degradation by [[Heathcliff]] — a calculated act of revenge that mirrors Heathcliff's own mistreatment at the hands of Hareton's father, Hindley. Hareton's arc from a coarse, illiterate young man to a figure capable of tenderness and self-improvement, particularly through his relationship with the younger [[Catherine-Linton]], forms one of the novel's most hopeful counterpoints to the destructive passion of the first generation.

## Biography

Hareton is born to Hindley Earnshaw and Frances at Wuthering Heights. His mother dies of consumption shortly after his birth, plunging Hindley into alcoholism and neglect. When Mr. Earnshaw, the old master, died, his name — "Hareton Earnshaw" — is carved above the door at Wuthering Heights, alongside the date "1500," marking the ancient lineage he inherits (001-011).

After Hindley's death, Heathcliff takes control of Wuthering Heights and deliberately raises Hareton as a brute — denying him education, encouraging his worst impulses, and keeping him in a state of servile dependency. Nelly Dean observes this with horror, noting that Heathcliff takes pleasure in seeing Hareton "performing his orison *sotto voce*" in the kitchen rather than learning to read (003-059). Heathcliff boasts of his success: Hareton is "gold put to the use of paving-stones" (024-018).

Hareton's relationship with the younger Catherine Linton, who arrives at the Heights after being forced into marriage with [[Linton Heathcliff]], becomes the catalyst for his transformation. Initially, Cathy mocks his illiteracy and rough manners, but Hareton's genuine efforts to learn to read — and his quiet, steadfast devotion to her — eventually win her over. In one telling moment, Cathy writes his name on a piece of paper: "she desired me to be her ambassador" to deliver a letter addressed to "Mr. Hareton Earnshaw" (032-071). By the novel's end, Hareton and Cathy are seated together on the same bench, "leaning her hand on his shoulder" (032-080), and planning to marry and move to [[Thrushcross-Grange]], leaving the dark history of Wuthering Heights behind.

## Relationships

**Hindley Earnshaw** — Hareton's father, who loves him initially but descends into alcoholism and neglect after Frances's death. Hindley's mistreatment of Heathcliff becomes the indirect cause of Hareton's own degradation.

**Heathcliff** — Hareton's de facto guardian and tormentor. Heathcliff deliberately keeps Hareton uneducated and brutish as revenge against Hindley, yet admits a strange affection for him: "Hareton's aspect was the ghost of my immortal love; of my wild endeavours to hold my right; my degradation, my pride, my happiness, and my anguish" (033-039).

**Catherine Linton (younger)** — Initially his antagonist, later his beloved. Cathy teaches Hareton to read — reversing the degradation Heathcliff inflicted — and their union represents the novel's hope for a new generation free from the cycle of revenge. "I should be condemned in Hareton Earnshaw's heart, if not by his mouth, to the lowest pit" (032-022).

**Nelly Dean** — Nelly watches Hareton grow up and serves as a compassionate observer of his transformation. She is one of the few who sees his inherent goodness beneath the rough exterior.

**Joseph** — The dour servant at Wuthering Heights who, like Heathcliff, reinforces Hareton's coarseness through religious bullying and lack of kindness.
