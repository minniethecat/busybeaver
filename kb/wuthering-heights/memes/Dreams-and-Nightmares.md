## Overview

Dreams in *Wuthering Heights* are narrative engines. Lockwood's nightmare of Catherine's ghost at the window (003-020–003-027) is the novel's most famous scene — and its most structurally significant, setting in motion the entire embedded narrative that follows. Nelly tells Lockwood the story of the Earnshaws and Lintons because his dream has awakened his curiosity. Brontë uses dreams not as decorative Gothic flourishes but as portals: they collapse the boundary between present and past, living and dead, rational and supernatural. The Lockwood dreams in chapter 3 are framed by Catherine Earnshaw's diary — a fifteen-year-old's account of a rainy Sunday with Joseph — so that dream, text, and memory become layers in a single narrative sediment.

## Manifestations

- **Lockwood's dream of Jabez Branderham (003-021–003-023)**: Lockwood dreams he is in Gimmerden Sough, listening to Branderham preach on the subject of "the Seventy Times Seven, and the First of the Seventy-First" — the 491st sin. The congregation attacks the preacher, and Lockwood wakes. The dream prefigures the novel's themes of relentless moral accounting and violent retribution.

- **The ghost at the window (003-024–003-027)**: The second dream — or perhaps not a dream — is Catherine's child-ghost crying "Let me in — let me in!" Lockwood's violent response, rubbing her wrist against broken glass, both horrifies and implicates the reader: we, like Lockwood, are intruders in this house, and this story will not let us go.

- **Heathcliff's waking dream**: In the final chapters, Heathcliff describes a state of visionary wakefulness: "I have a strong faith in ghosts; I have a conviction that they can, and do, exist among us!" (029-XXX). His death at the open window (034-018) is the novel's climactic dream state — he dies not in sleep but in a waking vision of Catherine.

- **Dream as narrative frame**: Lockwood's nightmares in chapter 3 are the hinge on which the entire novel turns. Without them, he would have left Wuthering Heights in the morning and never heard the story. The dream is the novel's foundational act of storytelling.

## Key Passages

- **003-020–003-027**: The complete dream sequence — Branderham's sermon and the ghost at the window. The most concentrated and narratively decisive passage of supernatural writing in the novel.
- **003-021**: "Jabez had a full and attentive congregation" — the dream-sermon on the 490th sin, a surreal parody of Calvinist moral arithmetic.
- **003-025**: The second dream: "I distinguished the girl's face in the glass" — the moment of ghostly recognition.
