# Family and Kinship

Family is both the structuring principle and the primary battleground of
*Wuthering Heights*. The novel traces two generations of the Earnshaw and
Linton families across some thirty years, and nearly every major plot event —
adoption, marriage, childbirth, inheritance, death — is a family event. Yet
Brontë's treatment of family is anything but sentimental: kinship bonds are
weapons, inheritance is a means of revenge, and the family home is a site of
imprisonment as much as belonging.

## The Two Households

The novel is built around two families occupying two houses:

| Family | Home | Status | Values |
|--------|------|--------|--------|
| Earnshaw | Wuthering Heights | Old yeoman family | Wild, passionate, fierce |
| Linton | Thrushcross Grange | Gentry | Refined, cultivated, fragile |

The Earnshaw family — Mr. and Mrs. Earnshaw, their children Hindley and
Catherine, and the foundling Heathcliff — occupies the ancient farmhouse on
the moors. The Lintons — Mr. and Mrs. Linton, their children Edgar and
Isabella — inhabit the more genteel Thrushcross Grange in the valley. The
geographical separation of the two houses mirrors the social and temperamental
distance between the families, and the novel's plot is driven by the
collisions and intermarriages that breach this distance.

When Catherine and Heathcliff first glimpse Thrushcross Grange through its
windows, the Linton children are presented as beings from another world:
Edgar and Isabella are quarreling over a small dog, "shrieking as if witches
were running red-hot needles into them" — a contrast so absolute with the
rough, outdoor wildness of the Earnshaw children that it establishes the two
families as different species [006-041].

## Adoption and Displacement

The novel's founding family event is Mr. Earnshaw's adoption of Heathcliff,
a "dirty, ragged, black-haired child" [004-008] found on the streets of
Liverpool. This act of charity disrupts the Earnshaw family order: Hindley
resents the intruder who displaces him in his father's affections, and
Catherine forms an intense, almost feral bond with the newcomer.

Heathcliff's ambiguous status — neither servant nor son, neither family nor
outsider — is the novel's central family tension. Mr. Earnshaw treats him as
a favored son, but Hindley, upon inheriting, demotes him to the status of a
laborer. This oscillation between inclusion and exclusion shapes Heathcliff's
character and fuels his lifelong drive for revenge against both families.

The adoption also raises questions the novel never answers. Why does
Mr. Earnshaw, on a business trip to Liverpool, return with a child rather
than the gifts his children requested? Why does Mrs. Earnshaw, who is "ready
to fling it out of doors" [004-010], accept the child into her home? Nelly's
narration leaves these questions deliberately opaque, as if the event is as
mysterious to those who lived through it as it is to the reader.

## Marriage as Alliance and Weapon

Marriage in *Wuthering Heights* is never simply a matter of love. Catherine
marries Edgar Linton not from passion but from calculation — "he will be
rich, and I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood"
[009-076] — while her true kinship bond remains with Heathcliff. Her famous
declaration "I *am* Heathcliff" [009-076] asserts a kinship deeper than
marriage: identity itself is a family bond.

Heathcliff weaponizes marriage in his revenge plot. He marries Isabella
Linton not from affection but to gain a claim on the Linton inheritance.
He forces the marriage of his sickly son Linton to the young Catherine
Linton, securing control of Thrushcross Grange through property law.
Marriage, in Heathcliff's hands, becomes an instrument of class warfare
and family destruction.

The novel makes clear that these strategic marriages are possible because
of the legal framework that treats wives as property. Isabella, having
married Heathcliff, finds herself trapped: "I have no power to leave him"
[013-061]. Catherine Linton, once married to Linton Heathcliff, loses all
legal independence. Brontë's critique of the marriage laws that subordinate
women to their husbands runs through the novel as a dark constant, expressed
not in polemic but in the narrative consequences of each union.

## The Second Generation

The novel's second half turns to the children of the first generation's
disastrous unions: Hareton Earnshaw (Hindley's son), Catherine Linton
(Catherine Earnshaw's daughter), and Linton Heathcliff (Heathcliff's son).
These three inherit not only property but trauma, and their relationships
replay — and ultimately transform — the patterns of the previous generation.

Each child bears the weight of their parentage differently. Hareton, raised
by Heathcliff in deliberate brutishness, repeats the degradation that
Heathcliff himself suffered — but without the memory of better treatment
that fueled Heathcliff's rage. Linton Heathcliff, a "pale, delicate, and
effeminate" child [019-034], inherits his mother's fragility and his
father's manipulative intelligence, but none of Heathcliff's ferocious
will. Catherine Linton, raised in the protected world of the Grange,
inherits her mother's spirit without her mother's self-destructiveness.
Together, the three form a triangle that echoes the first generation's
dynamics while offering the possibility of a different outcome.

Hareton and the young Catherine's eventual union represents a resolution
of sorts: the Earnshaw and Linton lines are merged through a marriage not
coerced but freely chosen, and the cycle of revenge and resentment that
drove the first generation's family dynamics gives way to "the most
ordinary faces of the two, walking together" [034-023].

## Kinship and Identity

Brontë's most radical insight about family is that kinship is not simply
biological. Heathcliff, who shares no blood with the Earnshaws, is more
intensely "family" to Catherine than her own brother or husband. The bond
between Catherine and Heathcliff transcends the legal and biological
definitions of kinship, suggesting that true family is a matter of affinity
and identity rather than blood or law.

Catherine's speech to Nelly — "my love for Heathcliff resembles the eternal
rocks beneath: a source of little visible delight, but necessary" [009-084]
— distinguishes between the superficial kinship of marriage (her engagement
to Edgar) and the foundational kinship of identity (her bond with
Heathcliff). The geological metaphor is precise: biological and legal
kinship are the "visible" surface; the deeper, structural bonds are
invisible but sustaining.

At the same time, the novel shows how "real" kinship — the legal structures
of marriage, inheritance, and lineage — exercises crushing power over
individuals. The tension between these two models of family — the chosen
and the imposed — is one of the novel's deepest themes, and Brontë refuses
to resolve it. The novel ends with a marriage that is both chosen (Cathy
and Hareton's) and conventional (restoring the Earnshaw line), as if to
suggest that the best family is one in which choice and structure converge.
