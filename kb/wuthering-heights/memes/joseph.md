## Overview

Joseph is the long-serving manservant at [[Wuthering-Heights]], a figure whose presence spans the entire narrative from the arrival of [[Lockwood]] in 1801 back through [[Nelly Dean]]'s retrospective account of two generations. He is one of the few characters who witnesses the complete arc of the [[Earnshaw]] and [[Linton]] families under [[Heathcliff]]'s domination. Brontë renders his speech in a dense Yorkshire dialect that marks him as rooted in the landscape of the [[the-moors]] in a way the novel's more genteel characters are not. Though often comic in his pious grumbling, Joseph also embodies the moral and religious hypocrisy that runs through the novel, using Calvinist doctrine to justify cruelty while claiming righteousness.

## Biography

Joseph is already an old man when the narrative begins. Nelly Dean describes him as "an elderly, nay, an old man: very old, perhaps, though hale and sinewy" (001-009). He has served at Wuthering Heights since Mr. Earnshaw's time, predating Heathcliff's arrival. Under Hindley Earnshaw's degeneracy, Joseph's position grows more difficult — he is forced to endure the master's drunken rages and the humiliation of being relegated to the back-kitchen.

During Heathcliff's ascendancy, Joseph remains at the Heights, serving the new master as he once served the old. He adapts to each regime with a survivor's pragmatism, though he never ceases to voice his disapproval of the moral decay around him. Heathcliff himself tells Nelly that "Joseph and I were the only two that would rise" (008-014) — a grudging acknowledgment of the old servant's reliability amid chaos.

In the novel's final section, Joseph is still at the Heights, tending to the diminished household of Heathcliff, Hareton, and the younger Catherine. He witnesses Heathcliff's strange decline and death. After Heathcliff's death and the marriage of Hareton and Cathy, Joseph remains as housekeeper at the Heights, his world having come full circle.

## Relationships

### With Heathcliff

Joseph and Heathcliff share a complex, mutually wary relationship. Heathcliff tolerates Joseph's presence and religious mutterings with contemptuous amusement, yet relies on him to maintain the household. Joseph, in turn, regards Heathcliff as a sinner bound for damnation but accepts his authority as master of the house. Heathcliff tells Nelly he would "have the privilege of flinging Joseph off the highest gable" (006-006), yet never acts on these threats.

### With Nelly Dean

Joseph and Nelly are the two servants who bridge the novel's generations, but they could hardly be more different. Where Nelly is diplomatic and warm, Joseph is abrasive and self-righteous. They frequently clash over household matters: "Joseph and I joined at an unsociable meal" (007-019). Nelly is often Joseph's unwilling audience for his religious lectures.

### With Catherine and Hindley

The young Catherine Earnshaw delighted in "turning Joseph's religious curses into ridicule" (005-005), mocking his piety to his face. Joseph, in turn, saw her wild behaviour as evidence of damnation. Hindley's cruelty extended to Joseph, who suffered under his drunken tyranny yet maintained his post.

### With Hareton

In a rare show of care, Joseph takes a protective interest in young Hareton after Hindley's death. However, his influence — combined with Heathcliff's deliberate degradation — helps keep Hareton uneducated and coarse.

## Character and Traits

Joseph is defined by three interlocking characteristics: his religious fanaticism, his cantankerous temperament, and his Yorkshire identity.

His religion is of the harshest Calvinist strain — he sees damnation everywhere and takes grim satisfaction in foretelling it. Nelly notes him "reading his Bible near the table" (005-006) while the household descends into chaos around him. When prevented from attending church by bad weather, "Joseph must needs get up a congregation in the garret" (003-009), demonstrating both his sincere devotion and his overbearing need to impose it on others.

Brontë renders Joseph's speech phonetically in broad Yorkshire dialect, making him almost unintelligible to outsiders like Lockwood and to many readers. This linguistic barrier reinforces his rootedness in the landscape — he belongs to the moors in a way the novel's more mobile characters do not.

His temperament is memorably described as "vinegar-faced" (002-003), and he is perpetually muttering curses, complaints, and prophecies of doom. Yet beneath the gruff exterior lies a kind of steadfastness: in a house where masters come and go, die or descend into madness, Joseph endures.

## Critical Reception

Joseph has attracted critical attention primarily for Brontë's handling of his dialect. Early reviewers found his speech impenetrable; modern scholars see it as a deliberate artistic choice that roots the novel in a specific Yorkshire place and class context. Q. D. Leavis noted Joseph as part of the novel's authentic rural texture. More recent criticism has examined how Joseph's Calvinism functions as a dark mirror to the novel's broader spiritual concerns — his God of wrath and predestination echoes the deterministic cruelty of Heathcliff's revenge, raising uncomfortable questions about where justice ends and damnation begins.
