Heathcliff's Arrival as a Child is the founding event of the entire narrative of Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights*. Recounted by [[Nelly Dean]] to the convalescing [[Mr. Lockwood]], the story of how the old [[Mr. Earnshaw]] brought home a "dirty, ragged, black-haired child" [004-037] from the streets of [[Liverpool]] establishes the origin of the novel's central figure and the destructive dynamics that will consume two generations.

## The Narrative Frame

Lockwood, confined to bed at [[Thrushcross Grange]] after his ordeal at [[Wuthering Heights]], asks Nelly to explain the strange household he encountered. She begins her tale at the beginning: the summer morning when Mr. Earnshaw set out for Liverpool on foot—"sixty miles each way" [004-034]—asking his children what gifts they wished him to bring home.

## The Journey and the Foundling

Mr. Earnshaw, "the old master," was a man of "a kind heart, though he was rather severe sometimes" [004-034]. Before departing for Liverpool, he asked the young [[Hindley Earnshaw]] what gift he wanted; Hindley chose a fiddle. The six-year-old [[Catherine Earnshaw]] chose a whip. Nelly herself was promised apples and pears.

Mr. Earnshaw returned after three days, exhausted. He opened his great-coat to reveal not the promised gifts but something far stranger:

> "See here, wife! I was never so beaten with anything in my life: but you must e'en take it as a gift of God; though it's as dark almost as if it came from the devil." [004-036]

The child was "a dirty, ragged, black-haired child; big enough both to walk and talk: indeed, its face looked older than Catherine's; yet when it was set on its feet, it only stared round, and repeated over and over again some gibberish that nobody could understand" [004-037].

[[Mrs. Earnshaw]] was furious—"ready to fling it out of doors"—demanding how her husband could "bring that gipsy brat into the house, when they had their own bairns to feed and fend for" [004-037]. Mr. Earnshaw explained he had found the child "starving, and houseless, and as good as dumb, in the streets of Liverpool" where not a soul knew to whom it belonged. "Determined he would not leave it as he found it," he brought the child home [004-037].

## Rejection and Naming

The arrival was inauspicious. The promised gifts—Hindley's fiddle and Catherine's whip—had been lost or destroyed during the journey. Hindley found his fiddle "crushed to morsels in the great-coat" and "blubbered aloud," while Cathy, on learning her whip had been lost "in attending on the stranger, showed her humour by grinning and spitting at the stupid little thing" [004-038].

Both children "entirely refused to have it in bed with them, or even in their room." Nelly herself "had no more sense" and put the child on the landing, where it crept to Mr. Earnshaw's door. When discovered, Nelly was banished from the house as punishment [004-038].

On her return a few days later, the child had been integrated into the household:

> They had christened him "Heathcliff": it was the name of a son who died in childhood, and it has served him ever since, both for Christian and surname. [004-039]

This detail is significant: Heathcliff is given the name of a dead Earnshaw son, making him simultaneously family and replacement, insiders and outsider. The name functions as both Christian and surname—Heathcliff belongs nowhere and everywhere in the family structure.

## The Immediate Aftermath

Within days of his arrival, a pattern was established that would define the novel's central relationships:

| Character | Response to Heathcliff |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Mr. Earnshaw | Peculiar fondness—"he took to Heathcliff strangely, believing all he said" [ch04] |
| Catherine | Rapid bond—"Miss Cathy and he were now very thick" [004-039] |
| Hindley | Violent hatred—"Hindley hated him" [004-039] |
| Nelly | Guilty hostility—"to say the truth I did the same" [004-039] |
| Mrs. Earnshaw | Resigned tolerance after initial fury |

Hindley's hatred in particular would prove catastrophic. He "bred bad feeling in the house" and regarded Heathcliff as "a usurper of his parent's affections and his privileges" [ch04]. When Mr. Earnshaw died less than two years later, Hindley returned from college as master of Wuthering Heights and began the systematic degradation of Heathcliff that would fuel the novel's cycle of [[Revenge]].

## Thematic Significance

Heathcliff's arrival is freighted with symbolic meaning:

- **The gift that destroys**: Mr. Earnshaw's act of charity upends the entire family order. The crushed fiddle and lost whip represent the displacement of the biological children by the foundling
- **Racial otherness**: Heathcliff is repeatedly described as "dark," "gipsy," and "as if it came from the devil"—markers of racial difference in a novel deeply concerned with belonging and exclusion
- **The dead son's name**: Naming the foundling after a dead Earnshaw child suggests replacement and resurrection, but also haunting—the dead child's name lives on in a figure who will destroy the family
- **Liverpool as threshold**: The port city, hub of colonial trade, represents the outside world penetrating the closed Yorkshire world. [[Liverpool]] is where Heathcliff comes from and, in some readings, gestures toward his possible origins in the Atlantic slave trade
