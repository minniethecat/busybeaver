## Overview

Mr. Linton is the patriarch of [[Thrushcross-Grange]] during the childhood of [[Catherine-Earnshaw]] and [[Heathcliff]]. Together with his wife, [[Mrs-Linton|Mrs. Linton]], he presides over a household defined by refinement, order, and class consciousness — values that stand in stark contrast to the rough, emotionally turbulent world of [[Wuthering-Heights]]. His decision to admit Catherine into the Grange after the bulldog incident (while rejecting Heathcliff) sets in motion the social transformation that will eventually lead Catherine to choose Edgar over Heathcliff, with tragic consequences for all three.

## Biography

Mr. Linton first appears when Catherine and Heathcliff, spying on Thrushcross Grange, are discovered after the Lintons' bulldog bites Catherine. Unlike the rough inhabitants of the Heights, Mr. Linton responds with measured alarm rather than violence, exclaiming "I'll go to the gallows for this" and warning his household not to lay by the gun (006-008). He and his wife take the injured Catherine into their care while expelling Heathcliff as a "wicked boy" unfit for polite company.

During Catherine's five-week convalescence at the Grange, Mr. Linton and his wife undertake her transformation from "wild, hatless little savage" into a young lady. He "mixed a tumbler of negus" — a warm, spiced wine — as part of the domestic ritual of caring for their unexpected guest (006-013). Later, "to mend matters," he paid a visit to Wuthering Heights — a gesture of cross-estate diplomacy that underscores his gentlemanly propriety (006-014).

Mr. Linton died sometime in the years following Catherine's stay, before the main events of the novel's second half. His legacy is the genteel world of the Grange that his son [[Edgar-Linton]] inherits — a world that proves fatally vulnerable to Heathcliff's vengeful machinations.

## Relationships

**Edgar Linton** — His son and heir, who embodies the refinement and gentleness his father cultivated. Edgar's marriage to Catherine Earnshaw brings the two estates together in a union their father would likely have viewed with deep ambivalence.

**Isabella Linton** — His daughter, raised in the same sheltered gentility. Her disastrous elopement with Heathcliff represents the dark inversion of everything Mr. Linton's household stood for.

**Catherine Earnshaw** — The wild child from the Heights whom Mr. Linton reluctantly admitted into his home. His hospitality — conditional and class-bound — transformed Catherine and, in doing so, altered the course of her life.

**Heathcliff** — Mr. Linton rejected him at the door, deeming him a "gypsy" unfit for decent society. This rejection, so natural to Mr. Linton's class instincts, becomes one of the original grievances that fuel Heathcliff's lifelong vengeance.
