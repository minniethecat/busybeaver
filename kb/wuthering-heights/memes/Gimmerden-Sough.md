## Overview

Gimmerden Sough is the chapel that appears exclusively in [[Mr-Lockwood]]'s nightmare during his forced stay at [[Wuthering-Heights]] — a dream-variant of [[Gimmerton-Chapel|Gimmerton Chapel]] in which the Reverend [[Jabez-Branderham]] delivers his sermon on "Seventy Times Seven, and the First of the Seventy-First." Though Gimmerden Sough exists only within Lockwood's fevered imagination, its role in the novel is significant: the nightmare sermon, in which the congregation rises to denounce one another and finally turns upon the preacher himself, serves as a dark parable for the cycles of mutual condemnation that define the world of the Earnshaws and Lintons.

## In the Narrative

Lockwood's nightmare transports him to the "Chapel of Gimmerden Sough," where a "discourse delivered by the Reverend Jabez Branderham" is underway (003-019). Lockwood notes that he has "passed it really in my walks" — a detail that blurs the boundary between dream and reality, suggesting that Gimmerden Sough is a distorted memory of the actual Gimmerton Chapel (003-021).

The sermon proceeds as an interminable catalog of four hundred and ninety sins, each corresponding to one of the seventy-times-seven pardons Christ commands in Matthew 18:22 — but Branderham perverts the injunction to forgive into an exhaustive indictment of his congregation. The scene builds toward chaos as each worshipper rises to denounce their neighbor, culminating in Lockwood himself "moved to rise and denounce Jabez Branderham as the sinner of the sin that no Christian need pardon" (003-022).

The name "Gimmerden Sough" itself is suggestive. "Sough" is a dialect word for a boggy, marshy place or the sound of wind through reeds — an evocation of the damp, hollow landscape of the moors. The dream-chapel's name thus ties the scene to the wild, untamed geography of [[The-Moors]], even as the sermon's content ties it to the social geography of judgment and revenge.

## Symbolic Significance

Gimmerden Sough functions as a narrative prolepsis — a dream that foreshadows the novel's central conflicts. The congregation's mutual denunciation mirrors the cycles of revenge that will consume [[Heathcliff]], [[Hindley-Earnshaw]], and the families they destroy. Branderham himself, condemned as "the sinner of the sin that no Christian need pardon," prefigures Heathcliff's role as both persecutor and persecuted. And Lockwood, the outsider-dreamer who rises to accuse the accuser, anticipates the reader's own position as judge of the novel's morally compromised characters.
