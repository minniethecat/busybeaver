# Madness

Madness — in forms ranging from feverish delirium to obsessive passion to
alcoholism — pervades *Wuthering Heights*. Emily Brontë does not treat madness
as a clinical condition but as a state that arises when intense emotion
overwhelms the fragile structures of social order. Her characters do not so
much "go mad" as *become* mad — consumed by grief, fury, love, or despair
until the boundary between sanity and insanity dissolves.

Madness in *Wuthering Heights* is not a single phenomenon but a spectrum of psychological states, each linked to a different character and a different kind of breakdown. Catherine Earnshaw's madness takes the form of feverish delirium — a violent dissolution of the boundary between past and present, self and other, that manifests in hallucinations and self-destructive behavior. Heathcliff's madness is obsessive and chronic — an eighteen-year haunting that consumes him from within, visible in his refusal to eat, his violent outbursts, and his persistent vision of Catherine's ghost. Hindley's madness is alcoholic and self-destructive — a slow suicide driven by grief that Heathcliff deliberately accelerates. Together, these three forms of madness map a territory that extends from acute crisis to chronic erosion, from externally imposed suffering to self-inflicted decline.

What unites these different manifestations is their common origin in love and loss. Every instance of madness in the novel is a response to the rupture of an intense emotional bond — Catherine's separation from Heathcliff, Heathcliff's loss of Catherine, Hindley's loss of Frances. Madness is, in this sense, the price of passion: the novel suggests that those who feel most deeply are also those most vulnerable to psychological collapse. This is a radical claim in the context of Victorian fiction, which typically treated madness as a moral or hereditary defect. Brontë instead presents it as the natural — even inevitable — consequence of emotional extremity in a social world that offers no adequate outlet for such feeling.

The novel's treatment of madness also reflects the influence of Romantic ideas about the relationship between genius, passion, and insanity. The Romantic movement, with which Brontë was deeply familiar, had elevated the figure of the artist-madman — the visionary whose intense perception of reality placed him beyond the reach of ordinary sanity. In *Wuthering Heights*, this Romantic archetype is both embraced and critiqued: Heathcliff and Catherine possess the intensity of Romantic visionaries, but their madness produces not art or transcendence but destruction and death. The novel's realist framework — Nelly Dean's practical narration, Lockwood's obtuse diary — constantly pulls against the Romantic glamorization of madness, insisting on its human cost even as it acknowledges its terrible power.

## Catherine's Delirium

The novel's most extended depiction of madness is Catherine Earnshaw's
breakdown after Heathcliff's departure and her quarrel with Edgar. In a
scene of extraordinary psychological intensity, Catherine tears her pillow
with her teeth and hallucinates her own reflection. Nelly reports that the
episode began with "her feverish bewilderment to madness, and tore the pillow
with her teeth" [012-012].

Catherine's unraveling is rendered with an almost clinical specificity. She
stares at her own face in a mirror and does not recognize it:

> "Don't you see that face?" she enquired, gazing earnestly at the mirror.
> [012-020]

When Nelly tries to reason with her, the hallucination deepens. Nelly covers
the mirror with a shawl, but Catherine's perception has already broken free
of the physical world: she sees not herself but the ghosts of her childhood —
Wuthering Heights, the moors, and Heathcliff. She imagines herself back at the
Heights as a child, the intervening years of marriage and adult constraint
dissolving into fever.

Nelly Dean observes this with clinical detachment, yet her diagnosis is as
much moral as medical: she urges Edgar to "help me to convince her of her
madness. Tell her what Heathcliff is" [010-077]. Madness here is not a
disease to be cured but a truth to be confronted — Catherine's "madness"
is her inability to reconcile her love for Heathcliff with her marriage to
Edgar.

## Heathcliff's Obsession

Heathcliff's condition after Catherine's death blurs the line between grief
and madness. His refusal to eat or sleep, his violent outbursts, and his
persistent vision of Catherine's presence all suggest a mind consumed beyond
rational function. Nelly reports that "he is clearly on the verge of madness:
he was so last night at least" [013-044].

What distinguishes Heathcliff's madness from Catherine's is its duration.
Catherine's delirium is acute and episodic; Heathcliff's is chronic,
deepening over eighteen years until he achieves what he calls his "strange
change" [033-018] — a state of eerie calm that precedes his death. His
madness is not a breakdown but a *becoming*: he has been consumed so
completely by his obsession that ordinary human functioning — eating,
sleeping, social interaction — ceases to matter.

His torment is not merely psychological but supernatural in its intensity. He
confesses to Nelly that Catherine's ghost has haunted him for eighteen years,
visible to him alone: "I cannot look down to this floor, but her features are
shaped in the flags! In every cloud, in every tree — filling the air at night"
[033-014]. This is madness as a form of permanent haunting — not an episode to
be survived but a condition to be endured.

## Hindley's Decline

Hindley Earnshaw's descent into alcoholism after his wife Frances's death
represents a different form of madness: self-destructive despair. He becomes
violent, neglectful of his son Hareton, and increasingly reckless — a
"dissipation and degradation" [008-020] that Heathcliff deliberately
encourages. Hindley's madness is a slow suicide, and it serves as a foil
to Heathcliff's: where Hindley's grief destroys only himself, Heathcliff's
grief destroys everyone around him.

## Madness as Social Critique

Brontë's treatment of madness also carries a social dimension. The
characters who lose their sanity are those who cannot fit into the
ordered world of Thrushcross Grange, the Linton family, and conventional
Victorian society. Catherine's breakdown occurs when she is trapped between
two incompatible worlds; Heathcliff's obsession intensifies as he is
excluded from the social order; Hindley's decline follows his loss of
status and purpose.

In this reading, madness is not merely an individual pathology but a symptom
of a social order that demands the suppression of authentic feeling. The
truly "mad" characters are those who feel too deeply — and the "sane"
world of the Lintons is revealed as superficial and inadequate.
