## Overview

Wuthering Heights opens with a declaration of isolation: Lockwood introduces himself as a man who has come to the "solitary neighbour" (001-001) because he seeks "perfect seclusion" from society. What follows is a novel in which nearly every character is marooned — by geography, by class, by grief, or by their own choices. The Yorkshire moors are not merely a backdrop but an active agent of isolation: the snow that traps Lockwood at the Heights, the distance between the two houses, and the vast emptiness that separates both from the world of villages and cities. Isolation in this novel is never neutral — it either intensifies love to the point of fusion (Catherine and Heathcliff) or freezes it into cruelty and violence.

## Manifestations

- **Geographic isolation**: Wuthering Heights and Thrushcross Grange are embedded in the moor, cut off from Gimmerton and the larger world. The snowstorm that traps Lockwood at the Heights (002-030, 003-020) is the novel's first and most literal image of enforced isolation — a visitor who cannot leave.

- **Social isolation**: Heathcliff's orphanhood and Hindley's degradation cut him off from the social world of the Earnshaws and Lintons. His isolation is imposed before it is chosen; his subsequent choice to isolate Catherine Linton, Hareton, and Nelly at the Heights is a mirroring of his own childhood.

- **Self-imposed solitude**: Lockwood "declared he was going to seek the solitary neighbour" (001-001) and congratulates himself on his misanthropy, only to find that the Heights' inhabitants outdo him in hostility. His solitude is a performance; theirs is a condition.

- **Grief as isolation**: After Catherine's death, Heathcliff's grief isolates him from all human connection. Nelly observes his "solitary rambles on the moors" (017-071). His isolation becomes predatory — he uses it to construct his revenge, but it also consumes him.

- **The moors as symbol**: The open moor is the one space where the novel's isolation paradoxically becomes communion: Catherine and Heathcliff's childhood freedom, the elder Catherine's ghost wandering, and the younger Catherine and Hareton's reconciliation all take place on the moors. Isolation among people is suffering; solitude on the moor can be freedom.

## Key Passages

- **001-001**: "I have just returned from a visit to my landlord — the solitary neighbour that I shall be troubled with." The novel's first sentence establishes isolation as its governing condition.

- **012-037**: Catherine's delirium: "I wish I were a girl again, half savage and hardy, and free; and laughing at injuries, not maddening under them!" Her isolation at the Grange — surrounded by civility but cut off from self — is the novel's most painful portrayal of social alienation.

- **017-071**: Nelly describes Heathcliff's "solitary rambles on the moors" after Catherine's death — the image of the isolated figure on the landscape that will define the rest of the novel.

## Character Connections

| Character | Form of Isolation | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Heathcliff** | Imposed (childhood) → chosen (adulthood) | Transforms isolation into weaponised cruelty |
| **Mr. Lockwood** | Self-imposed misanthropy | Comic; he flees the isolation he claimed to seek |
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | Chosen through marriage; regretted | The isolation of the wrong choice destroys her |
| **Catherine Linton** | Imposed by Heathcliff after Isabella's flight | Forges resilience and eventually escapes |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Imposed through degradation and illiteracy | His essential decency survives isolation |
