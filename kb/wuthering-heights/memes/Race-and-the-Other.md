## Overview

Heathcliff's racial ambiguity is one of the most striking and underexplored dimensions of *Wuthering Heights*. Described repeatedly as "dark-skinned," a "gipsy," and a "little Lascar," his origins trigger visceral xenophobic reactions from nearly every character he encounters. Brontë mobilizes racial otherness as a narrative engine: Heathcliff's indeterminate origins make him perpetually legible as foreign, dangerous, and ultimately inhuman in the eyes of the novel's Yorkshire society.

## Manifestations

Heathcliff's racialization appears throughout the novel through epithets and physical description. Nelly Dean, despite her fondness for him, recalls his arrival as "a dirty, ragged, black-haired child" (004-037) and notes Mr. Earnshaw's neighbors speculated he was "a little Lascar, or an American or Spanish castaway" (006-011). Lockwood, on first meeting him, observes that Heathcliff is "a dark-skinned gipsy in aspect, in dress and manners a gentleman" (001-013). The persistent racial coding of his physical appearance — black eyes, black hair, dark complexion — operates alongside his social marginalization: the Earnshaw children initially refuse to share a bed with him, and Hindley's abuse takes on explicitly racialized contempt.

## Key Passages

- **001-013**: Lockwood's first impression — "He is a dark-skinned gipsy in aspect, in dress and manners a gentleman" — establishes the tension between Heathcliff's racialized body and his acquired social status.
- **004-037**: Nelly's account of Heathcliff's arrival as "a dirty, ragged, black-haired child" found "in the streets of Liverpool" frames him from the outset as a figure of urban and imperial otherness.
- **006-011**: The neighborhood gossip identifying him as "a little Lascar, or an American or Spanish castaway" reveals the speculative xenophobia that constructs Heathcliff as unknowable and exotic.

## Character Connections

- **Heathcliff**: The primary locus of racial otherness in the novel; his indeterminate origins (Liverpool as portal to empire) fuel his lifelong status as outsider, even after acquiring wealth and property.
- **Nelly Dean**: Her narration reproduces racial tropes despite her sympathy, describing Heathcliff in terms that emphasize his difference from the "proper" English household.
- **Mr. Earnshaw**: His act of bringing the foundling home from Liverpool positions him as a figure of imperial encounter, importing racial difference into the domestic sphere of Wuthering Heights.

## Critical Perspectives

The novel's racial dimensions have attracted significant scholarly attention. Postcolonial readings identify Heathcliff as a figure of imperial anxiety — the colonial subject who returns to dispossess the English landowner. The Liverpool connection places Heathcliff within the context of Britain's eighteenth-century slave trade and imperial commerce. Some critics read Heathcliff's racial ambiguity as deliberately irresolvable, functioning as a mirror for the prejudices of those who encounter him.
