## Overview

Books in *Wuthering Heights* are never merely objects — they are weapons, tokens, and archives of selfhood. Catherine Earnshaw's childhood diary, scrawled in the margins of a mildewed Testament (003-004–003-011), is the novel's organising textual artefact: Lockwood finds it on his nightmare-haunted night at the Heights, and it becomes the bridge between the novel's present and its buried past. Throughout the novel, books signal who has power and who is denied it — Joseph uses prayer-books to punish children; Heathcliff destroys Catherine Linton's books to isolate her; Hareton's learning to read his own name over the door ("Hareton Earnshaw, 1500") is his first step toward reclaiming his inheritance.

## Manifestations

- **Catherine's Testament (003-004–003-011)**: The "injured tome" with "Catherine Earnshaw, her book" on the fly-leaf and a date "some quarter of a century back." The margins are filled with Catherine's childhood diary — a palimpsest in which a girl's rebellion overwrites Joseph's religious text, and Lockwood's reading of both frames the entire novel.

- **Joseph's prayer-books**: Joseph forces the children to read sermons for "precisely three hours" in the freezing garret while Hindley and Frances sit by the fire (003-009). Books here are instruments of punishment, not enlightenment — and Catherine responds by "shying her book into the settle."

- **Heathcliff destroys Catherine Linton's books**: After imprisoning young Catherine at the Heights, Heathcliff systematically destroys her books — the act is a calculated assault on her identity as an educated gentlewoman and a deliberate echo of his own childhood deprivation.

- **Hareton learns to read**: The novel's resolution turns on Hareton's literacy. He begins by learning to read "Hareton Earnshaw" over the door, then graduates to the books Catherine Linton gives him. The act of reading is the act of reclaiming self — the name on the door and the name in the book are the same.

- **Lockwood as reader**: The novel's frame narrator is also its ideal reader — curious, slightly obtuse, and ultimately transformed by the story he solicits from Nelly. His reading of Catherine's diary is the novel's primal scene of reading.

## Key Passages

- **003-004**: Lockwood discovers Catherine's books in the panelled bed — "a few mildewed books piled up in one corner; and it was covered with writing scratched on the paint."
- **003-006**: The fly-leaf inscription: "Catherine Earnshaw, her book" — a declaration of ownership that echoes through the novel.
- **003-009**: The "dreadful Sunday" — Catherine's diary account of Joseph's forced Bible-reading, one of the novel's most vivid childhood scenes.

## Character Connections

| Character | Relationship to Books | Significance |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | Diarist, annotator | Her marginalia preserve her childhood self |
| **Heathcliff** | Deprived of books as a child; destroys them as an adult | Books as instruments of power and revenge |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Illiterate → reader | His literacy arc is the novel's resolution |
| **Catherine Linton** | Reader → teacher | Uses books to connect with Hareton |
| **Joseph** | Religious enforcer | Books as punishment |
| **Mr. Lockwood** | Reader of the diary | The reader-figure who frames the narrative |
