Cathy Meets Linton is the pivotal event that launches the second-generation plot of Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights*. At sixteen, the spirited [[Catherine Linton]]—raised in sheltered seclusion at [[Thrushcross Grange]]—is lured to [[Wuthering Heights]] by [[Heathcliff]]'s deliberate design and meets her cousin [[Linton Heathcliff]] for the first time. What begins as a chance encounter becomes a calculated campaign to unite the two young people and, through their union, complete Heathcliff's revenge by gaining control of both estates.

## Background

Catherine Linton—"Cathy" to distinguish her from her dead mother—grew up in almost complete isolation at Thrushcross Grange. Her father [[Edgar Linton]], "too good to be thoroughly unhappy long," raised her with "ardent, tender love" [ch17] but sheltered her from any knowledge of Wuthering Heights, Heathcliff, or the tragedies of the previous generation. She knows nothing of her mother's story, her uncle Hindley, or her cousin [[Hareton Earnshaw]].

Her only knowledge of Linton comes from an aborted meeting years earlier. When [[Isabella Linton]] died, Edgar had tried to bring the young Linton to the Grange, but Heathcliff demanded his son be delivered to him at the Heights. Cathy, then a child, had been "in high glee, eager to join her cousin" [021-001], but Linton was taken away before she could meet him.

## The Meeting

On Cathy's sixteenth birthday—which is also the anniversary of her mother's death—Edgar spends the day alone in mourning. Cathy, restless, persuades [[Nelly Dean]] to take her for a ramble on the moors. Against Nelly's better judgment, they wander further than intended and encounter Heathcliff, who tells Cathy:

> "I have a young relative of yours here—a cousin. I think you've heard me speak of Linton?" [ch21]

Heathcliff invites Cathy to the Heights to meet Linton. Nelly is suspicious, but Cathy's curiosity overcomes caution. At the Heights, Cathy discovers a sickly, petulant boy entirely unlike the romantic image she had constructed:

> "What, Linton!" cried Cathy, kindling into joyful surprise at the name. "Is that little Linton? He's taller than I am! Are you Linton?" [021-037]

Linton is weak, pale, and peevish—"a pale, delicate, effeminate boy" [ch21]. He complains about the "cursed" servants and the "hateful house" [ch21]. Cathy, who had imagined a playmate and companion, attempts to draw him out but finds him querulous and self-pitying.

Heathcliff's purpose in engineering this meeting is explicit. He tells Nelly privately:

> "My design is as honest as possible. I'll inform you of its whole scope," he said. "That the two cousins may fall in love, and get married ... She's her brother's heir, is she not?" [ch21]

The plan is brutally simple: marry Linton to Cathy, thus securing control of Thrushcross Grange through the female line, as Heathcliff already controls Wuthering Heights through Hindley's debts.

## Aftermath: The Correspondence

Edgar, on learning of the visit, forbids Cathy from returning to the Heights. He tells her of Heathcliff's true nature: "a most diabolical man, delighting to wrong and ruin those he hates" [021-084]. Cathy is "amazed at the blackness of spirit that could brood on and cover revenge for years" [021-086]—this is her first exposure to the darkness that consumed the previous generation.

But Linton has already begun a secret correspondence with Cathy. Nelly discovers "a mass of correspondence—daily almost" hidden in a locked drawer [021-098]. The letters, which Linton wrote at Heathcliff's dictation, are "foolish" love-letters "borrowed from a more experienced source" [021-098]—Heathcliff himself ghost-writing his son's courtship.

Nelly confronts Cathy and burns the letters. For a time, the correspondence ends. But the connection has been made, and Heathcliff has only begun his campaign.

## Significance

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **Second-generation catalyst** | The meeting sets the second half of the novel in motion |
| **Heathcliff as puppet-master** | He manipulates both children as instruments of revenge |
| **Cathy's innocence** | Her sheltered upbringing makes her vulnerable to Heathcliff's schemes |
| **The forced romance** | Linton is not a lover but a lure—Heathcliff's tool to capture the Grange |
| **Edgar's failure** | By hiding the past from Cathy, Edgar left her defenceless against it |

The meeting also introduces the central irony of the second generation: Cathy is drawn to Wuthering Heights by the same impulse that drew her mother—a fascination with what lies beyond the Grange's cultivated boundaries. But where Catherine Earnshaw found a soulmate in Heathcliff, Catherine Linton finds only a sickly pawn.
