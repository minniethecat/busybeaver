# Linton Family

The Lintons are the landed gentry family of [[Thrushcross Grange]], established in comfort, refinement, and social respectability. If the [[Earnshaw Family]] of [[Wuthering Heights]] represents the old yeoman order — rough, passionate, independent — the Lintons represent the cultivated gentility of the eighteenth-century landed class. Their world is one of drawing rooms, books, and polite society; their tragedy lies in their fatal entanglement with the Earnshaws and with [[Heathcliff]], a force their civility is powerless to resist.

## The Civilized Household

When [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and Heathcliff first spy on Thrushcross Grange as children, they witness a domestic scene that defines the Linton world. Heathcliff describes it to Nelly in one of the novel's most vivid passages:

> "Both of us were able to look in by standing on the basement, and clinging to the ledge, and we saw — ah! it was beautiful — a splendid place carpeted with crimson, and crimson-covered chairs and tables, and a pure white ceiling bordered by gold, a shower of glass-drops hanging in silver chains from the centre, and shimmering with little soft tapers. Old Mr. and Mrs. Linton were not there; Edgar and his sister had it entirely to themselves. Shouldn't they have been happy? We should have thought ourselves in heaven!" (006-006)

[[Edgar Linton]] and his sister [[Isabella Linton|Isabella]] are seen quarrelling — not in the violent fashion of the Heights, but petulantly — over a small dog they have "pulled" between them. The scene encapsulates the Linton character: luxury, refinement, and a certain triviality that masks deeper fragilities.

[[Mr. Linton (elder)|Mr. Linton]], the patriarch, is a magistrate — a man of legal and social standing in the district. His wife, [[Mrs. Linton]], presides over a household governed by propriety and decorum. Together they embody the values of their class: respectability, education, and a firm sense of social boundaries. When they discover the two Earnshaw children at their window, their response is telling: Catherine is injured by their bulldog, Skulker, and while the Lintons care for her wound, they recoil at Heathcliff — "a gipsy" and "a little Lascar" — whom they drive from their property.

## The Linton Patriarchs

Mr. and Mrs. Linton belong to the novel's background — they die before the main action of the story — but their influence persists through the values they instilled in their children. Mr. Linton's role as a magistrate places him at the intersection of law and social authority; he is, in the district's hierarchy, a figure of official power. Yet his death from fever, contracted while tending to his wife during her final illness, reveals a private devotion that complicates the picture of Linton coldness. Both parents die within a short space of each other, leaving Edgar and Isabella orphaned — a state they share, ironically, with the Earnshaw children and with Heathcliff himself. The novel's world is one in which parents die young and children are left to navigate the consequences of adult passions without adult guidance.

The elder Lintons' most consequential act — their rejection of Heathcliff on the night of the Grange window incident — reverberates through the entire novel. By defining Heathcliff as beyond the pale of civilized society, they reinforce the social exclusion that fuels his rage and ambition. Their class instinct — to protect their daughter from contamination by a "gipsy" child — is presented not as villainy but as the automatic response of their kind. Brontë's critique is not that the Lintons are bad people but that their virtues — propriety, discernment, the maintenance of social boundaries — are precisely what render them incapable of recognizing or responding to the force that Heathcliff represents.

## Edgar: The Civilized Suitor

Edgar Linton inherits the Grange and all its values. He is gentle, educated, devoted, and constitutionally incapable of understanding the passion that binds Catherine to Heathcliff. His courtship of Catherine is proper in every conventional sense, and his love for her is genuine and patient. After her death, Edgar devotes himself to their daughter, [[Catherine Linton|young Cathy]], raising her in seclusion at the Grange.

> "He possessed the power to depart as much as a cat possesses the power to leave a mouse half killed, or a bird half eaten." (010-012)

Yet Edgar's virtues are also his weaknesses. He is powerless against Heathcliff's calculated aggression, unable to prevent his sister Isabella's disastrous marriage or to protect his daughter from Heathcliff's schemes. He dies a broken man, his estate passing not to his own child but, through a web of manipulation, to Heathcliff's son [[Linton Heathcliff]] and ultimately to Heathcliff himself.

Edgar's tragedy is the tragedy of liberal civility confronted with a force that does not play by its rules. He challenges Heathcliff to a physical confrontation but is easily overpowered. He appeals to law but finds that Heathcliff has already suborned the lawyer. His only weapon — moral authority — is meaningless against an antagonist who operates entirely outside the moral framework that gives Edgar his identity. His deathbed scene, in which he gazes at young Cathy and sees in her face the likeness of the wife he lost, is among the novel's most melancholy passages: the civilized man dying in a world that no longer has a place for his civilization.

## Isabella: The Rebellious Daughter

Isabella Linton's infatuation with Heathcliff represents the Linton family's fatal inability to comprehend forces outside their civilized world. At eighteen, Isabella — sheltered, romantic, naive — fancies herself in love with Heathcliff. Catherine warns her bluntly: "He's a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (010-032), but Isabella, raised on sentimental novels, mistakes his dangerousness for passion. Her elopement with Heathcliff, followed by her escape from [[Wuthering Heights]] and flight to the south of England, represents the Linton family's sole act of physical defiance against the force that destroys them. She dies in exile, leaving her son Linton to be claimed by his father.

Isabella's narrative — conveyed through a long letter to Nelly and later through her first-person account of life at the Heights — is one of the novel's most vivid embedded stories. Her transformation from naive romantic to clear-eyed survivor is swift and brutal. She learns, within days of her marriage, that she has made a catastrophic error:

> "Is Mr. Heathcliff a man?" she wrote. "If so, is he mad? And if not, is he a devil? I shan't tell my reasons for making this inquiry; but I beseech you to explain, if you can, what I have married" (013-006)

Her escape — throwing her wedding ring into the fire and fleeing across the moor — is the novel's most decisive act of self-preservation. That she survives at all, and secures a life for herself and her son in the south, makes her the Linton family's sole success story — a woman who saw the abyss and chose to run rather than be consumed.

## Linton Heathcliff: The Degenerate Heir

Linton Heathcliff, the child of Isabella and Heathcliff, is the last and most pathetic figure in the Linton line. Pale, sickly, selfish, and cowardly, he combines the physical delicacy of the Lintons with none of their moral virtues. Heathcliff uses him as an instrument to secure his claim on Thrushcross Grange, forcing a marriage between the dying boy and young Cathy. Linton dies shortly after, having served his father's purpose. His death extinguishes the Linton name — though the Grange, through Cathy's eventual marriage to [[Hareton Earnshaw]], returns to something resembling its old order.

Linton Heathcliff is a study in the corruption of a child by a tyrannical parent. He is not born wicked; he is made wicked by Heathcliff's systematic emotional abuse, which teaches him that manipulation and cowardice are the only available strategies for survival. His relationship with Cathy — alternating between genuine affection and calculated betrayal — reproduces in miniature the destructive dynamics of the first generation, with Linton playing the role of the weak man who cannot protect the woman who trusts him. His death, like his life, is pathetic rather than tragic: he dies alone, unmourned, his brief existence having served only to transfer a property title from one family to another.

## The Meaning of Linton Civilization

The Linton family represents the gentility that the novel both admires and critiques. Their refinement is real, but it is also a form of weakness — a shelter that cannot withstand the elemental passions embodied by Heathcliff and the Heights. The Earnshaws are destroyed by the intensity of their bonds; the Lintons are destroyed by their inability to recognize such intensity until it is too late. Their civilization, for all its beauty, proves tragically defenseless against the forces it has excluded.

The novel's final arrangement — Cathy and Hareton preparing to marry and move to the Grange — represents a synthesis. Hareton brings the strength and rootedness of the Earnshaw yeoman tradition; Cathy brings the cultivation and gentleness of the Linton civility. Together they suggest a new kind of family, one that combines the best of both houses while escaping the fatal flaws of each. The Linton refinement, which proved so vulnerable in isolation, becomes viable when allied with the Earnshaw resilience — a guarded optimism that closes the novel's long meditation on family, class, and the possibilities of domestic life.
