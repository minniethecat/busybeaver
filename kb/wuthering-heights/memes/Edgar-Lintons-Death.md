Edgar Linton's Death is the final event in the first-generation tragedy of Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights* and the moment when [[Heathcliff]]'s long revenge reaches its material completion. Through manipulation and coercion, Heathcliff ensures that Thrushcross Grange passes not to [[Catherine Linton]] but to his own son [[Linton Heathcliff]]—and, through Linton, to himself. Edgar dies in the belief that his daughter is safely married and that his estate is protected, unaware that both are already in Heathcliff's hands.

## The Trap

The path to Edgar's death was prepared in the preceding chapters. After [[Cathy Meets Linton]], Heathcliff intensified his campaign. He used Linton's failing health as leverage, telling Cathy that Linton was dying of a broken heart because she had been forbidden to see him. When Edgar fell ill—"seven days glided away, every one marking its course by the henceforth rapid alteration of Edgar Linton's state" [ch27]—Heathcliff seized his opportunity.

He lured Cathy and [[Nelly Dean]] to [[Wuthering Heights]], imprisoned them, and refused to release Cathy until she married Linton. With Edgar dying and Cathy desperate to return to her father, Heathcliff forced the marriage through. Nelly was kept prisoner for five days while the wedding was arranged.

When Cathy finally escaped and reached the Grange, it was too late to save her father—and too late to prevent Heathcliff from taking everything.

## The Death

Edgar's death is described with the same peaceful serenity that characterised [[Catherine's Death]]—but where Catherine died in the aftermath of violent passion, Edgar dies in quiet resignation:

> He died blissfully, Mr. Lockwood: he died so. Kissing her cheek, he murmured, "I am going to her; and you, darling child, shall come to us!" [028-034]

"He died blissfully"—Nelly's choice of adverb is significant. Edgar Linton, the gentle, cultivated master of Thrushcross Grange, dies believing he will be reunited with his beloved Catherine in heaven. He does not know that his daughter has been forced into marriage with a dying boy, or that all his property will pass to Heathcliff.

Edgar's final moments are tender and domestic. He tries to comfort Cathy, telling her she will join him and her mother. The scene contrasts sharply with Heathcliff's violent grief at Catherine's death—Edgar's love is gentle, resigned, and conventional where Heathcliff's was savage and blasphemous.

## The Aftermath

Edgar's death triggers the final transfer of power. Heathcliff arrives at the Grange almost immediately:

- Linton, now Cathy's husband, inherits the Grange as Edgar's will had been altered at Heathcliff's direction
- Linton dies shortly afterward, leaving everything to his father
- Heathcliff becomes master of both Wuthering Heights and Thrushcross Grange
- Cathy is left a virtual prisoner—married to a corpse, dependent on her enemy, with no property and no protector

Heathcliff's revenge, begun decades earlier when Hindley degraded him and Catherine chose Edgar, is now materially complete. He owns everything that once belonged to both families. But at what cost? The novel's final movement—[[Heathcliff's Death]]—suggests that material victory brings no peace.

## Significance

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **End of the Linton line** | Edgar's death extinguishes the male Linton name (Linton Heathcliff dies soon after) |
| **Property consolidation** | Both estates are now under Heathcliff's control |
| **Cathy's destitution** | The spirited girl of ch21 is reduced to a penniless dependent |
| **Contrast with Catherine's death** | Edgar dies at peace; Catherine died in torment. Which is the novel's judgment? |
| **Heathcliff's pyrrhic victory** | Having won everything, Heathcliff loses interest in the victory |

Edgar Linton is often overlooked in critical commentary, overshadowed by the titanic figures of Heathcliff and Catherine. But his death is structurally crucial—it is the hinge between Heathcliff's revenge and his dissolution. After Edgar dies, Heathcliff has nothing left to destroy. The novel's remaining chapters trace his slow, strange journey toward his own death and, in the final image, his reunion with Catherine beneath the moors.
