## Overview

Mr. Lockwood is the outer frame narrator of *Wuthering Heights*, a gentleman from the south of England who rents [[Thrushcross-Grange]] from [[Heathcliff]] in 1801 seeking rural solitude. His visit to [[Wuthering-Heights]] — where he encounters the hostile household of Heathcliff, [[Hareton Earnshaw]], and the younger [[Catherine Linton]] — provokes his curiosity about his landlord's history. Bedridden with illness after his misadventure on the moors, Lockwood persuades the housekeeper [[Nelly Dean]] to tell him the story of the Earnshaw and Linton families, providing the conceit for the novel's nested narrative structure. Lockwood is a classic unreliable narrator: a self-styled misanthrope who is in fact deeply curious about others, a man who prides himself on his detachment yet falls immediately in love with the younger Cathy.

## Biography

Lockwood arrives at Thrushcross Grange in 1801, seeking seclusion after a disappointed romantic entanglement that he describes with theatrical self-pity: "I have gained the reputation of deliberate heartlessness — how undeserved, I alone can appreciate" (001-001). His first visit to Wuthering Heights is a comedy of misread social signals: he mistakes a heap of dead rabbits for pets, addresses the younger Cathy as Heathcliff's wife, and provokes the dogs to attack him.

Lockwood's second, ill-fated visit to the Heights — during a snowstorm — results in his most significant contribution to the narrative. Forced to stay the night, he sleeps in Catherine Earnshaw's old room, where he discovers her diary written in the margins of books and experiences the famous nightmare of the ghost-child at the window: "Let me in — let me in! ... I'm come home: I'd lost my way on the moor!" (003-002). Heathcliff's anguished response to Lockwood's account — weeping and begging the ghost to return — is the first crack in Heathcliff's impenetrable facade and the moment that transforms Lockwood's idle curiosity into urgent narrative desire.

Bedridden at the Grange, Lockwood becomes Nelly Dean's audience. His role as listener is not passive: he interrupts, asks questions, and occasionally draws conclusions that Nelly corrects or complicates. At the novel's end, Lockwood returns to the Grange after several months away, learns of Heathcliff's death and the younger Cathy's romance with Hareton, and visits the graves on the moor — providing the novel's closing image of tranquility: "I lingered round them, under that benign sky... and wondered how anyone could ever imagine unquiet slumbers for the sleepers in that quiet earth" (034-020).

## Relationships

**Heathcliff** — Lockwood's landlord and the object of his fascinated curiosity. Lockwood initially misreads Heathcliff as a fellow misanthrope, interpreting his reserve as kindred spirit rather than suppressed torment: "Mr. Heathcliff and I are such a suitable pair" (001-001). This misreading is Lockwood's defining characteristic — he projects his own psychology onto others and is consistently wrong.

**Nelly Dean** — Lockwood's storyteller. Their relationship is the novel's narrative engine: Lockwood's prurient interest drives Nelly's tale, and his occasional interjections remind the reader that all of this is being filtered through two layers of narration.

**Catherine Linton (young Cathy)** — Lockwood is momentarily infatuated with the younger Cathy, whom he describes as "the most exquisite creature I had ever beheld" (002-001). His romantic interest is quickly dispelled by her coldness — a comic deflation that underscores Lockwood's vanity and his status as an outsider to the world he observes.

## Character & Traits

Lockwood is Brontë's most sophisticated narrative device. By placing the story within two frames — Lockwood recounting Nelly's account — she creates deliberate distance between the reader and the events, forcing active interpretation. Lockwood's unreliability is multiple: he is an outsider to Yorkshire culture; he is self-deluding about his own motives; and he filters everything through the lens of a refined but shallow sensibility.

His comic failures to understand the Heights household serve a serious function: they demonstrate how utterly alien the world of the novel is to conventional society. Lockwood's inadequacy as a reader of character and situation mirrors the reader's own interpretive challenge. When he departs, the novel leaves us not with his perspective but with Nelly's — the insider's voice, tempered by thirty years of intimate witness.
