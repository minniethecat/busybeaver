## Overview

Hindley Earnshaw is the eldest son of the Earnshaw family and the initial antagonist of *Wuthering Heights*. His bitter resentment of the foundling [[Heathcliff]] — who displaces him in his father's affections — leads him to systematically degrade Heathcliff after inheriting [[Wuthering-Heights]]. Hindley's own self-destruction through alcoholism and gambling allows Heathcliff to take possession of the Heights, perpetuating a cycle of cruelty in which the victim becomes the perpetrator and is in turn consumed by his own creation. His son [[Hareton Earnshaw]] is raised in ignorance and degradation — a living demonstration of how abuse replicates itself across generations.

## Biography

Hindley is introduced as a fourteen-year-old boy resentful of the "gipsy brat" his father has brought home from Liverpool. When Mr. Earnshaw gives Heathcliff Hindley's horse as punishment, the breach between the boys becomes permanent (004-019). After Mr. Earnshaw's death, Hindley returns from college with his new wife, [[Frances Earnshaw]], and immediately asserts his dominance: he banishes Heathcliff from the family sitting-room, forces him to labor as a farm servant, denies him education, and separates him from [[Catherine Earnshaw]] — reducing Heathcliff to "a creature of the fields" (007-030).

Frances's death from consumption shortly after [[Hareton Earnshaw]]'s birth devastates Hindley. He descends into alcoholism, gambling, and violent rages, effectively abandoning his infant son to the care of [[Nelly Dean]]. His decline accelerates: the once-proud master of the Heights becomes "tyrannical and evil," terrorizing the household with drunken rampages. On one occasion he drops Hareton from the stairs — caught only by Heathcliff's instinctive reflex (009-018).

Heathcliff, returning after three years as a wealthy man, deliberately exploits Hindley's addiction. Through late-night card games at the Heights, Heathcliff accumulates Hindley's gambling debts until Hindley has mortgaged everything. When Hindley dies — only twenty-seven years old, his constitution destroyed by drink — Heathcliff owns Wuthering Heights outright. Hindley's son Hareton becomes Heathcliff's dependent, raised in the degradation his father intended for Heathcliff.

## Relationships

**Heathcliff** — Hindley's lifelong antagonist. As children, Hindley torments Heathcliff out of jealousy for their father's favoritism. As adults, Hindley degrades Heathcliff to a servant; Heathcliff returns to systematically destroy Hindley through his own vices. Their cycle of mutual destruction is the novel's starkest illustration of how cruelty begets cruelty.

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Hindley's sister, with whom he has a strained relationship. After becoming master of the Heights, he treats Catherine with cold distance, and her defection to the Lintons' world further isolates him.

**Frances Earnshaw** — Hindley's wife, a delicate woman of uncertain origins whose death triggers his final descent. His grief for Frances is genuine and consuming — one of the few moments when Hindley commands sympathy.

**Hareton Earnshaw** — Hindley's son, whom he effectively orphans through neglect and dissolution. Nelly observes that Hindley "neither wept nor prayed; he cursed and defied: execrated God and man, and gave himself up to reckless dissipation" (008-022).

## Character & Traits

Hindley is both villain and victim. His childhood jealousy of Heathcliff is understandable — a father replacing his own son with a foundling — but his adult cruelty is disproportionate and ultimately self-destructive. Brontë portrays Hindley's decline with clinical precision: grief → alcoholism → violence → financial ruin → death. He is a cautionary figure, illustrating the toxic consequences of unresolved resentment.

Crucially, Hindley is the origin point of the novel's revenge cycle. Heathcliff's later cruelty is modeled on Hindley's treatment of him: "I'm trying to settle how I shall pay Hindley back. I don't care how long I wait, if I can only do it at last. I hope he will not die before I do!" (007-028). When Heathcliff returns to enact this revenge, he simply applies Hindley's own methods — degradation, deprivation, dispossession — with greater intelligence and patience.
