# Narrative Architecture

*Wuthering Heights* (1847) is built on a famously complex narrative frame.
Mr. Lockwood, a gentleman tenant from the south, records in his diary the story
he hears from Nelly Dean, the housekeeper who has served both families across
three decades. Within her tale are letters, reported dreams, and second-hand
accounts — creating a Chinese-box structure of narrators within narrators
that destabilises the reader's confidence in any single version of events.

## Notes

The frame-narrative structure was controversial in 1847 — reviewers found it
"coarse" and "disagreeable" — but modern criticism recognises it as Brontë's
most radical formal innovation. By nesting stories within stories and filtering
every event through interested, fallible narrators, the novel refuses to settle
on a single truth about its characters or their motives.
