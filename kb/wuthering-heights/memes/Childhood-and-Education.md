## Overview

Childhood in *Wuthering Heights* is not a prelude to adult life but its determinant. The novel traces two generations of children — Catherine and Heathcliff, then Catherine Linton, Hareton, and Linton Heathcliff — and shows how the conditions of their upbringing produce the adults they become. Education, particularly literacy, is a central site of power: Hindley degrades Heathcliff by denying him the education of a gentleman (008-019); Heathcliff, in turn, degrades Hareton by keeping him illiterate (017-016). When Catherine Linton teaches Hareton to read in the novel's closing chapters, the act is not sentimental but revolutionary — it breaks the cycle of violent pedagogy that has driven the entire plot.

## Manifestations

- **Catherine's diary (003-004–003-011)**: Lockwood finds Catherine Earnshaw's childhood diary, scrawled in the margins of a Testament, recording a rainy Sunday in which she and Heathcliff rebelled against Joseph's forced Bible reading. The diary is the novel's most intimate glimpse of childhood as it was lived — defiant, vivid, and framed by the books that both confined and liberated the children.

- **Joseph's "education"**: Joseph's three-hour sermons and forced prayer-book readings (003-009, 003-011) represent education as punishment — a model of religious instruction that breeds rebellion rather than piety. "Shame on ye! sit ye down, ill childer! there's good books eneugh if ye'll read 'em" (003-011) is Joseph's characteristic blend of moral superiority and ineffectuality.

- **Heathcliff's degradation through denied education**: Hindley's first act of cruelty after inheriting Wuthering Heights is to strip Heathcliff of his gentleman's education. Catherine's visit to Thrushcross Grange at age twelve marks the moment education becomes class differentiation: she returns "a very dignified person" (008-019), while Heathcliff remains "an unreclaimed creature, without refinement, without cultivation."

- **Hareton's systematic degradation**: Heathcliff's revenge on Hareton is pedagogical: "Now, my bonny lad, you are mine! And we'll see if one tree won't grow as crooked as another, with the same wind to twist it!" (017-016). Hareton is denied literacy as a deliberate experiment in whether a gentleman's son can be made into a brute.

- **Catherine Linton teaches Hareton**: The novel's resolution turns on an act of teaching. Catherine Linton's decision to teach Hareton to read — mocked, resisted, then accepted — is the first freely given act of education in the novel. Their relationship reverses the cycle: education becomes not punishment but gift, not degradation but restoration.

## Key Passages

- **003-004–003-011**: Catherine's childhood diary — the foundational text of the novel's concern with childhood and education. The diary records a "dreadful Sunday" when Joseph forced the children to read sermons for three hours in the freezing garret.
- **008-019**: Catherine's transformation after five weeks at Thrushcross Grange — the education that turns a "wild, hatless little savage" into "a very dignified person."
- **017-016**: Heathcliff's declaration of pedagogical revenge on Hareton — education weaponised.

## Character Connections

| Character | Educational Experience | Outcome |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | Divided: wild childhood at Heights, civilised education at Grange | Split identity; unable to reconcile her two selves |
| **Heathcliff** | Education denied by Hindley → self-transformation during absence | Weaponised knowledge against those who denied him |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Systematically denied literacy by Heathcliff | Innate gentility survives; learns to read through love |
| **Catherine Linton** | Educated at the Grange; becomes Hareton's teacher | The novel's most hopeful figure — the teacher who breaks the cycle |
| **Joseph** | Self-appointed religious educator | His harsh pedagogy breeds rebellion |
