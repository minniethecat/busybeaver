## Overview

Confinement runs through *Wuthering Heights* as both literal event and metaphorical structure. From Lockwood's forced overnight stay at Wuthering Heights in the opening chapters to Isabella's imprisonment in her marriage, Catherine's confinement in illness, the younger Cathy's captivity under Heathcliff's control, and the graves that confine both bodies and restless spirits — the novel is punctuated by scenes of characters trapped against their will. These episodes of imprisonment mirror the larger structural confinements of class, gender, and family that bind every character, living and dead. Brontë uses the motif to expose the coercive structures of Victorian domestic life: the house that should shelter becomes a cage, the marriage that should liberate becomes a prison, and the grave — the ultimate confinement — becomes, paradoxically, the only release.

## Manifestations

The motif of imprisonment opens the novel decisively. Lockwood, attempting to leave Wuthering Heights as a snowstorm descends, finds himself trapped: "dark night coming down prematurely" (002-055) forces him to accept the household's inhospitable shelter. He is then attacked by dogs — "You might as well leave a stranger with a brood of tigers!" (001-021) — an episode that literalizes the hostile enclosure of the Heights. His second night, trapped in the oak-panelled bed, brings the nightmare of Catherine's ghost — a spirit confined to the moor for twenty years, crying to be let in (003-005).

Isabella's marriage to Heathcliff becomes the novel's most explicit depiction of domestic imprisonment. Having eloped with romantic expectations, she quickly discovers she is "a prisoner, hopeless of release" (017-070). The house that once represented escape from Thrushcross Grange becomes her cage, and her eventual flight — "I bounded, leaped, and flew down the steep road" (017-070) — is an act of desperate self-liberation, one of the novel's few successful escapes.

Catherine Earnshaw's confinement is metaphysical as well as physical. Her illness confines her to her room at Thrushcross Grange; her delirium at the window, where she imagines herself back at Wuthering Heights, collapses the distinction between imprisonment and freedom. She tears at the pillow, seeing her own reflection in "a black press" and longing for the liberty of childhood on the moors. The grave, too, is imagined as both confinement and liberation — Lockwood dreams of Catherine's ghost crying to be let in (003-005), while Heathcliff longs to be reunited with her body in the earth, opening her coffin seventeen years after her death (029-005).

The younger Cathy's captivity forms the darkest iteration of the motif. Heathcliff lures her to Wuthering Heights and imprisons her there for five days, forcing her marriage to the dying Linton Heathcliff to secure his property claim: "I shall have her locked up — she shall be placed in a room where she cannot communicate with anyone" (027-015). Nelly Dean herself is held captive for five days at the Heights (028-030), making the narrator a fellow prisoner and witness to the younger generation's ordeal. Cathy's imprisonment is particularly cruel because it is explicitly strategic — not born of passion but of cold calculation.

The grave as final confinement preoccupies the novel's closing movement. Heathcliff bribes the sexton to open Catherine's coffin, disturbs her resting place, and arranges for the adjoining sides of their coffins to be removed so their dust may mingle (029-005). His own death, found in Catherine's room with the window open toward the moors, suggests a release from the confinement of life itself. The country folk's reports of the ghosts of Heathcliff and Catherine walking the moor together invert the motif: the grave, the ultimate prison, has been transformed into a gate.

## Key Passages

- **001-021**: Lockwood's dog attack — "You might as well leave a stranger with a brood of tigers!" — the first of many episodes where Wuthering Heights functions as a trap.
- **002-055**: "Dark night coming down prematurely" — nature itself conspires to imprison Lockwood within the Heights.
- **003-005**: Catherine's ghost clawing at the window — "Let me in — let me in!" — twenty years of spectral confinement on the moor.
- **017-070**: Isabella describes herself as a prisoner within her marriage, her romantic flight having become a domestic cage.
- **027-015**: Heathcliff announces his plan to imprison the younger Cathy — an explicit statement of the captivity strategy.
- **028-030**: Nelly Dean is held captive at Wuthering Heights for five days, forced to witness Cathy's ordeal.

## Character Connections

- **Isabella-Linton**: The most explicit victim of marital imprisonment; her escape from Heathcliff is one of the novel's few successful liberations. Her letter to Nelly (chapter 13) documents the transformation of romantic fantasy into domestic prison.
- **Mr-Lockwood**: His entrapment at Wuthering Heights in the opening chapters establishes the motif and frames the entire narrative as a story told under duress. His status as an urban gentleman unfamiliar with moorland weather makes him vulnerable to the Heights' hostile enclosure.
- **Catherine-Earnshaw**: Her illness confines her physically; her marriage to Edgar Linton confines her socially; her restless ghost suggests a soul incapable of accepting even the grave's confinement.
- **Catherine-Linton**: The younger Cathy is literally imprisoned by Heathcliff at Wuthering Heights to force her marriage to Linton Heathcliff — the most brutal and strategic use of captivity in the novel.
- **Heathcliff**: Both agent and victim of confinement — he imprisons others yet is himself imprisoned by his obsession with Catherine. His final days, refusing food and company, are a self-imposed confinement leading to death.
- **Linton-Heathcliff**: A passive instrument in his father's scheme, his own sickly body is a prison from which he cannot escape.

## Critical Perspectives

The confinement motif has been central to feminist readings of *Wuthering Heights*. Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar (*The Madwoman in the Attic*, 1979) read the novel's enclosed spaces — bedrooms, coffins, the oak-panelled bed — as figures for the domestic imprisonment of Victorian women. Catherine Earnshaw's illness and death represent, in this reading, the impossibility of female desire within patriarchal structures.

Gothic criticism has focused on confinement as a defining convention of the genre. The Heights and the Grange function as what Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick terms "the claustrophobic spaces of the Gothic" — houses that trap rather than shelter, families that imprison rather than nurture. Brontë innovates within the convention by making confinement psychological as well as physical: Heathcliff's obsession is as much a prison as any locked room.

More recent ecocritical readings have examined how the moors function as the novel's only space of freedom, contrasting the open, wild landscape with the enclosed domestic interiors. The tragedy of Catherine Earnshaw, in this reading, is her transition from the liberty of the moors to the confinement of Thrushcross Grange — a spatial shift that registers her social ascent as a form of imprisonment.
