# Frame Narrative

*Wuthering Heights* is remarkable not only for its passionate content but for
the intricate narrative architecture that contains it. Emily Brontë constructs
a triple-layered frame: the outermost narrator (Lockwood) records in his diary
the story told by the inner narrator (Nelly Dean), who herself recounts the
words and actions of the primary characters. This "Chinese box" structure
creates a narrative distance that paradoxically intensifies the emotional
impact of the story it encloses.

## The Triple Frame

The narrative layers operate at three distinct removes:

1. **Lockwood's diary** (1801–1802): The outermost frame. Lockwood, a gentleman
   from the south, rents Thrushcross Grange and visits Wuthering Heights. His
   diary entries record his impressions and, crucially, his transcription of
   Nelly Dean's oral narrative. The diary itself announces the framing conceit
   from the novel's opening pages, with Catherine's marginalia discovered by
   Lockwood in her childhood books — writing that takes "the form of a regular
   diary, scrawled in an unformed, childish hand" [003-007] — serving as a
   miniature of the novel's own layered structure.

2. **Nelly Dean's tale** (c. 1771–1801): The inner frame. Nelly, the
   housekeeper who has served both the Earnshaw and Linton families, tells
   Lockwood the history of the two households. She is both participant in and
   witness to the events she describes.

3. **The primary story**: The actions and passions of Heathcliff, Catherine,
   Edgar, Isabella, Hindley, and the second generation — events that Nelly
   observed or participated in, and that Lockwood records at second hand.

## Narrative Unreliability

Each layer of the frame introduces its own unreliability. Lockwood is an
outsider who misinterprets relationships and projects his own romantic
fancies onto the characters he encounters. Nelly Dean, though more
knowledgeable, is not impartial — she has her own loyalties, prejudices,
and blind spots. She acknowledges her own limitations:

> "I am, on the whole, a very fair narrator, and I don't think I could
> improve his account." [015-001]

This self-assessment is characteristically Nelly — confident and limiting at
once. Her claim to fairness is itself a narrative position, not a guarantee
of truth. The chain of narration — character → Nelly → Lockwood → reader —
means that every event in the novel reaches us filtered through at least two
consciousnesses, neither of them fully reliable.

## The Function of Distance

The frame structure serves several critical functions in the novel:

**Credibility**: By grounding the wild, Gothic events in the prosaic frame of
Lockwood's diary, Brontë makes the supernatural elements more plausible. The
ghost at the window is not a Gothic novelist's fancy but an entry in a
gentleman's journal.

Lockwood himself is acutely aware of the generic tension his narrative creates.
When recounting his nightmare to Heathcliff, he apologizes for "having related
my ridiculous nightmare" [003-057] — the self-deprecating frame heightening,
rather than diminishing, the uncanny power of what he has witnessed.

**Irony**: The gap between the narrator's understanding and the reader's
creates dramatic irony. Lockwood's romantic interpretations are often
laughably wrong — he mistakes the young Catherine for Heathcliff's wife, fails
to recognize Hareton's resemblance to the first Catherine — and this
persistent misreading alerts the reader to the complexity beneath the surface.

**Emotional intensity**: Paradoxically, the narrative distance *intensifies*
the emotional impact. The passions of Heathcliff and Catherine, filtered
through Nelly's steady recollection and Lockwood's genteel diary, seem all
the more powerful for being contained within so many frames. The story is
like a fire burning at the center of nested boxes — the containment makes
the heat more palpable.

## A Revolutionary Structure

When *Wuthering Heights* was published in 1847, its narrative structure was
unprecedented in English fiction. No previous novel had employed such a
complex layering of narrators, or used the frame to so thoroughly interrogate
the act of storytelling itself. Brontë's innovation would influence modernist
experiments with narrative perspective nearly a century later, and the novel's
structure remains a subject of critical fascination.

The effect of this revolutionary structure is to make the reader an active
participant in meaning-making. We are never given the story directly; we must
piece it together from the accounts of narrators who are themselves characters
in the drama, each with their own investments, omissions, and failures of
understanding.
