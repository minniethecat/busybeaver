## Overview

Social class is the invisible architecture of *Wuthering Heights*, determining who may marry whom, who inherits property, and who is treated as fully human. The novel's central tragedy — Catherine's decision to marry Edgar Linton rather than Heathcliff — is an explicit calculation of class: she tells Nelly that "it would degrade me to marry Heathcliff now" (009-084), yet her heart remains with the man her brother has reduced to a servant. Brontë charts the consequences of this class calculus across two generations, tracing how property, education, and lineage conspire to produce both victims and perpetrators.

## Manifestations

Class operates on multiple levels throughout the novel:

- **Marriage as class transaction**: Catherine's choice of Edgar is explicitly framed as social advancement: "he will be rich, and I shall like to be the greatest woman of the neighbourhood" (009-084). Her fantasy that she can "aid Heathcliff to rise" (009-090) through her husband's money collapses when Heathcliff disappears.

- **The gentleman code**: Heathcliff's mysterious transformation into "a gentleman" upon his return (010-006) is the novel's cruelest class irony: the outward trappings of class — dress, speech, wealth — mask the same feral rage, now armed with the power to destroy those who excluded him.

- **Degradation and dehumanisation**: Hindley's treatment of Heathcliff after Mr. Earnshaw's death is class degradation in microcosm: stripping him of education, companions, and status, reducing him to "a labourer" on the farm. The word "degrade" echoes through the novel's pivotal scenes (009-084, 009-085, 011-047, 027-010).

- **Hareton's reversal**: The second generation inverts the pattern. Hareton, born a gentleman's son, is degraded by Heathcliff into illiteracy — precisely the fate Heathcliff suffered — yet his innate dignity and Catherine Linton's eventual recognition of it (032-005, 033-063) suggest that class is not intrinsic but imposed.

## Key Passages

- **009-084**: Catherine explains her choice to marry Edgar, not Heathcliff: "It would degrade me to marry Heathcliff now; so he shall never know how I love him: and that, not because he's handsome, Nelly, but because he's more myself than I am." The passage crystalises the class/love tension at the novel's heart.

- **009-085**: Heathcliff overhears only the word "degrade" and flees, his departure the direct consequence of class-based rejection.

- **001-013**: Lockwood's first description of Heathcliff as "a gentleman" — a judgement based entirely on externals that the reader will come to see as bitterly incomplete.

- **010-006**: Speculation about how Heathcliff transformed into "a gentleman" — the mystery at the centre of his return and the source of his new power to wound.

## Character Connections

| Character | Class Position | Arc |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| **Heathcliff** | Orphan → servant → gentleman → landlord | The novel's central class transgressor; acquires class power to avenge class wounds |
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | Gentleman's daughter → lady of the Grange | Chooses class comfort over love, and is destroyed by the choice |
| **Edgar Linton** | Born gentleman | Represents the settled, civilised world of inherited class; powerless against Heathcliff's weaponised class mobility |
| **Hindley Earnshaw** | Gentleman's heir → degraded drunk | Degrades Heathcliff to assert his own class position; consumed by the revenge he provokes |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Heir → degraded labourer → redeemed | The subject of Heathcliff's "experiment" — can innate gentility survive systematic degradation? |
