Lockwood's Arrival at Wuthering Heights is the opening event of Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights* (1847). It establishes the [[frame narrative]] structure of the novel and introduces the reader to the isolated, hostile world of the Heights through the eyes of an outsider.

## Narrative Context

The event occurs in 1801, near the end of the novel's internal chronology. [[Mr. Lockwood]], a gentleman from the south of England, has rented [[Thrushcross Grange]] from [[Heathcliff]] and rides across the [[The Moors]] to introduce himself to his landlord. His account of this visit—recorded in his diary and later relayed to [[Nelly Dean]]—forms the outermost layer of the novel's nested narrative structure.

## The Visit

Lockwood rides four miles across the moorland from Thrushcross Grange to Wuthering Heights on a stormy afternoon. Upon arrival, he announces himself to Heathcliff:

> "Mr. Lockwood, your new tenant, sir. I do myself the honour of calling as soon as possible after my arrival, to express the hope that I have not inconvenienced you by my perseverance in soliciting the occupation of Thrushcross Grange." [001-004]

Heathcliff's response is characteristically hostile. He interrupts with "Thrushcross Grange is my own, sir" and utters "walk in" through closed teeth, "expressed the sentiment, 'Go to the Deuce!'" [001-005] [001-006]. Lockwood, perversely intrigued by a man more misanthropic than himself, accepts the grudging invitation.

Inside, Lockwood observes the peculiar household: [[Joseph]], an elderly servant who mutters peevish disapproval; [[Zillah]], the housekeeper; and a pack of hostile dogs that nearly attack him. He notes the date "1500" and the name "Hareton Earnshaw" carved above the door, architectural details that hint at the house's long and troubled history. [001-010] [001-011]

## Lockwood's Character Revealed

The visit also serves to introduce Lockwood's own character. He describes himself as a misanthropist who has retreated to the Yorkshire moors after a failed romance at the seaside. When a young woman returned his affections, he confesses, he "shrunk icily into myself, like a snail; at every glance retired colder and farther." [001-014] His self-awareness about this "curious turn of disposition" [001-015] establishes him as an unreliable but self-reflective narrator.

By the visit's end, Lockwood is both repelled and fascinated. Despite Heathcliff's evident wish for "no repetition of my intrusion," Lockwood resolves: "I shall go, notwithstanding. It is astonishing how sociable I feel myself compared with him." [ch01] This determination to return sets the frame narrative in motion.

## Structural Significance

The arrival scene accomplishes several narrative functions simultaneously:

- **Establishes the frame**: Lockwood's diary provides the outermost narrative layer
- **Introduces the setting**: The physical description of Wuthering Heights as a place of "atmospheric tumult" [001-010]
- **Characterises Heathcliff**: Through Lockwood's fascinated observation of a man "more exaggeratedly reserved than myself" [001-006]
- **Foreshadows violence**: The dog attack anticipates the novel's pervasive [[Cruelty and Violence]]
- **Creates mystery**: The carved name "Hareton Earnshaw" and the date "1500" hint at untold histories
