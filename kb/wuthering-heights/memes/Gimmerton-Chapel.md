## Overview

Gimmerton Chapel is the parish church serving the village of [[Gimmerton]] and the surrounding estates of [[Wuthering-Heights]] and [[Thrushcross-Grange]]. As the site of marriages, funerals, and the spiritual life of the community, it anchors the novel's social world in the rhythms of the Anglican parish. Yet the chapel is also the setting for one of the novel's most disorienting passages — [[Mr-Lockwood]]'s nightmare of [[Jabez-Branderham]]'s sermon on "Seventy Times Seven" — which transforms this seat of religious order into a theater of mutual condemnation and judgment. The chapel thus embodies the tension between the outward propriety of Gimmerton society and the darker spiritual currents that run beneath it.

## Description

Lockwood's dream provides the most vivid description of the chapel, which he imagines as "the Chapel of Gimmerden Sough" — a humble rural church "with a raftered roof, and a flagged floor" (003-021). In waking reality, the chapel's most distinctive feature is its bell, heard tolling across the valley: "Gimmerton chapel bells were still ringing" on significant occasions (015-006). The chapel porch becomes a gathering place, with "a concourse thickening outside Gimmerton chapel porch" for weddings and funerals (015-029).

The chapel sits at a geographical boundary: "very soon after you pass the chapel, as you may have noticed, the road branches off" (010-021) — a landmark on the route between the Heights and the Grange.

## In the Narrative

The chapel appears first and most memorably in Lockwood's nightmare. Forced to spend the night at Wuthering Heights, Lockwood dreams he is "journeying to hear the famous Jabez Branderham preach" at the chapel (003-020). The dream sermon, on the text "Seventy Times Seven, and the First of the Seventy-First," becomes an interminable catalog of sins and mutual denunciations, culminating in Lockwood himself rising to denounce the preacher — a scene that captures the novel's themes of judgment, revenge, and the impossibility of forgiveness within a closed community.

In the waking world, the chapel marks significant rites of passage. It is where Catherine Earnshaw and Edgar Linton are married, and where funerals are held for the Linton family. Heathcliff, significantly, is buried not in the chapel but "in the open air, with the heath and the harebells growing over it" — a pagan burial that rejects the chapel's authority (012-050). The villagers note their surprise that Heathcliff was "neither in the chapel under the carved monument of the Lintons, nor yet outside the walls of the churchyard" but in a remote corner beside Catherine's grave (016-020).

The younger Catherine, raised in the sheltered world of Thrushcross Grange, recognizes the chapel as a landmark: "the chapel, the only building she had approached or entered, except her own home" (018-002) — a testament to the narrowness of her world.

## Associated Characters

**Jabez Branderham** — The dream-preacher whose sermon in the chapel allegorizes the novel's cycle of judgment and revenge.

**Mr. Lockwood** — The dreamer whose nightmare transforms the chapel into a space of spiritual terror.

**Heathcliff** — His burial outside the chapel walls is a final act of defiance against the social and religious order it represents.

**Edgar Linton** — Buried "in the chapel, with his family" (028-036), in accordance with Linton tradition and propriety.
