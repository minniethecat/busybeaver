# Revenge

## Overview

Revenge is the central thematic engine of *Wuthering Heights*. Heathcliff's quest for vengeance — born from his childhood mistreatment by Hindley Earnshaw and his thwarted love for Catherine — drives the novel's entire plot across two generations. Brontë presents revenge not as cathartic but as corrosive: it consumes Heathcliff's humanity and cascades suffering onto the next generation before exhausting itself.

## Manifestations

Revenge in the novel operates through multiple vectors:

- **Heathcliff vs. Hindley**: After Hindley degrades Heathcliff to a servant, Heathcliff returns years later wealthy and systematically ruins Hindley, eventually taking ownership of Wuthering Heights (010-060).
- **Heathcliff vs. Edgar Linton**: Heathcliff marries Isabella Linton partly to wound Edgar, and his manipulation of the Linton inheritance continues this revenge into the second generation.
- **Intergenerational revenge**: Heathcliff explicitly frames his treatment of Hareton Earnshaw as revenge enacted on the son for the father's sins (021-086). He states that he can "scarcely forbear" punishing Hareton as the "representative" of Hindley (033-044).

## Key Passages

> I'm trying to settle how I shall pay Hindley back. I don't care how long I wait, if I can only do it at last. I hope he will not die before I do!
>
> (010-060)

Heathcliff here articulates his revenge as a patient, long-game strategy — one that becomes the novel's primary plot engine.

> I seek no revenge on you. That is not the plan. The tyrant grinds down his slaves and they don't turn against him; they crush those beneath them.
>
> (011-038)

Heathcliff explains his philosophy of displaced vengeance — the oppressed do not strike upward but downward, a social critique embedded in the revenge narrative.

> I could not forget the words with which he had made me swear fidelity to the boy; they were perpetually recurring to me, and I had an uneasy feeling that I must take care to prevent the possibility of his ever being able to say, "You deserted me."
>
> (004-043)

Nelly Dean's reflection captures how Heathcliff weaponizes promises and loyalty, turning social bonds into instruments of revenge.

## Character Connections

- **Heathcliff** — The novel's primary agent of revenge. His entire adult life is organized around systematic payback.
- **Hindley Earnshaw** — Both perpetrator and victim of revenge: his cruelty creates the motive, and Heathcliff's retaliation ruins him.
- **Edgar Linton** — Target of Heathcliff's romantic revenge; marrying Isabella is partly retaliation against Edgar.
- **Catherine Earnshaw** — Her choice to marry Edgar is the proximate cause of Heathcliff's revenge, though it is directed at others.
