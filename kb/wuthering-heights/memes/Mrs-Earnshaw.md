# Mrs. Earnshaw

Mrs. Earnshaw is the mother of [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and [[Hindley Earnshaw]], the wife of [[Mr. Earnshaw]], and the mistress of [[Wuthering Heights]] during the years before [[Heathcliff]]'s arrival. She appears only briefly in [[Nelly Dean]]'s retrospective narrative — remembered primarily for her fierce opposition to Heathcliff's adoption — and dies young, her absence shaping the household in ways that reverberate through the entire novel.

## Opposition to Heathcliff

Mrs. Earnshaw's single significant action in the narrative is her immediate and visceral rejection of the foundling Heathcliff. When Mr. Earnshaw returns from [[Liverpool]] with the dark-skinned child, Nelly Dean recalls:

> "Mrs. Earnshaw was ready to fling it out of doors: she did fly up — asking how he could fashion to bring that gipsy brat into the house, when they had their own bairns to feed and fend for" [004-006].

Her reaction — pragmatic, protective, and unadorned by sentiment — represents the voice of conventional domestic order. Where Mr. Earnshaw acts on impulse and compassion, Mrs. Earnshaw sees the practical consequences: another mouth to feed, a stranger in the household, a disruption to her children's security. Her fury, though harsh, is not without reason, and her death soon after removes the last maternal check on the forces that will consume the family.

## Maternal Absence

Mrs. Earnshaw's early death — the cause is never specified — inaugurates the novel's persistent pattern of maternal absence. She is the first of several mothers in the novel who die young or are otherwise absent from their children's lives: [[Frances Earnshaw]], [[Catherine Earnshaw]] herself, [[Mrs. Linton]], and [[Isabella Linton]] all follow the same pattern. This recurring motif of motherlessness leaves the children of both houses — the Heights and [[Thrushcross Grange]] — without the nurturing and moderating influence that a mother might have provided.

Her death leaves the young Catherine in particular without a female guide through childhood and adolescence. Nelly Dean, the housekeeper, fills some of this role, but she is an employee, not a parent, and her authority has natural limits. The absence of Mrs. Earnshaw is one of the novel's silent structural conditions — a gap that helps explain, without excusing, the extremity of what follows.

## The Two Mrs. Earnshaws: A Generational Parallel

The parallel between Mrs. Earnshaw and [[Frances Earnshaw]], Hindley's wife, is deliberate and instructive. Both are outsiders who marry into Wuthering Heights; both die young, leaving children motherless; both are defined primarily by their opposition to disorder — Mrs. Earnshaw against the intruder Heathcliff, Frances against the degradation of the household under Hindley's decline. Yet where Mrs. Earnshaw's resistance is grounded in maternal protectiveness, Frances's is rooted in class anxiety. She is "rather thin, but young, and fresh complexioned" [008-009], and her illness and death after Hareton's birth mirror the vulnerability that haunts all the women of the Heights.

That both women share the name "Mrs. Earnshaw" and both die within the same walls reinforces the novel's sense of entrapment and repetition. The house consumes its mistresses. The maternal absence that begins with the first Mrs. Earnshaw is perpetuated by the death of the second, creating a chain of motherlessness that extends through Hareton, through the younger Catherine, through Linton Heathcliff — none of whom know a mother's care for more than the briefest span.

## A Figure in the Background

Mrs. Earnshaw is never developed as a character; she exists in the narrative only as a memory and a structural absence. Yet her brief appearance is thematically significant. Her instinct to protect her own children against the intruder speaks to a fundamental tension in the novel: the conflict between the obligations of blood and the claims of compassion. Mr. Earnshaw's adoption of Heathcliff is an act of generosity, but it comes at the cost of his own family's stability — a cost that Mrs. Earnshaw, had she lived, might have found a way to mitigate or prevent.

She remains, in the end, one of the novel's many ghosts — not a haunting presence but an absent one, whose death opened a space that would be filled, in time, by forces far beyond her imagining.

## Perceptions and Legacy

Mrs. Earnshaw exists almost entirely through the recollections of others — a figure known only by the impressions she left behind. Nelly Dean, the novel's primary narrator, recalls her with a mixture of respect and distance: she was a practical woman, concerned with the day-to-day running of the household, and her opposition to Heathcliff was framed by Nelly not as cruelty but as maternal instinct. "She did fly up," Nelly reports [004-006], the verb suggesting a sudden, almost physical eruption of protective fury rather than calculated malice.

From Mr. Earnshaw's perspective — implied rather than stated — his wife's opposition to Heathcliff registered as a challenge to his authority. His insistence on keeping the child despite her protests suggests a marriage in which the husband's will prevailed, a dynamic that would be repeated in more destructive form in the marriages of the next generation. The tension between Mr. and Mrs. Earnshaw over Heathcliff's adoption is the novel's earliest instance of a pattern that will recur throughout: a man's disruptive act of will overridden by a woman's attempt to maintain order — and the order always loses.

For Catherine and Hindley, their mother's death means she exists only as an absence. Neither child speaks of her in the narrative; she is not invoked as a memory or a standard. This silence is itself significant: Mrs. Earnshaw's legacy is not in what she taught her children but in the void her death created. In a novel so concerned with inheritance — of property, of names, of trauma — Mrs. Earnshaw bequeaths only her absence, and that absence proves to be one of the most consequential inheritances of all.

Critically, Mrs. Earnshaw has received limited attention compared to the novel's more prominent figures, yet feminist readings of the novel have increasingly recognized her as part of a pattern of silenced maternal voices. Alongside Frances Earnshaw and Mrs. Linton, she belongs to a triad of mothers whose premature deaths leave the children of both houses without maternal guidance — a structural condition that enables the unchecked expression of patriarchal violence and obsession that drives the plot. In this reading, Mrs. Earnshaw is not merely a minor character but a representative absence: the lost maternal principle whose restoration the novel's ending — Cathy and Hareton's union — only partially achieves.
