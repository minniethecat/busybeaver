## Overview

Nelly Dean (Ellen Dean) is the housekeeper at [[Thrushcross-Grange]] and the primary narrator of *Wuthering Heights*. Her story — told to the tenant [[Mr. Lockwood]] during his convalescence — spans over thirty years and three generations of the Earnshaw and Linton families. Nelly is both participant and chronicler: she nursed [[Heathcliff]], [[Catherine Earnshaw]], [[Hindley Earnshaw]], and their children, making her the novel's most knowledgeable witness. Her narrative voice — pragmatic, morally conventional, occasionally self-serving — shapes everything the reader knows about the events at [[Wuthering-Heights]] and the Grange. As such, Nelly is not merely a neutral conduit but an active interpreter whose biases and limitations are integral to the novel's meaning.

## Biography

Nelly grows up at Wuthering Heights as the daughter of the Earnshaws' nurse, making her effectively a foster-sibling to Hindley and Catherine. She is fourteen when Heathcliff arrives, and her account of his childhood forms the earliest portion of her narrative. After Mr. Earnshaw's death and Hindley's assumption of control, Nelly moves to Thrushcross Grange with Catherine upon her marriage to [[Edgar Linton]], remaining in service there for the rest of her career.

Nelly's role is far more than domestic. She is present at nearly every crisis of the novel: she witnesses Catherine's confession that she will marry Edgar (009-083); she is the intermediary through whom Isabella sends her desperate letters from the Heights (013-007); she nurses Catherine through her final illness and delivers the news of her death to Heathcliff; she raises the younger [[Catherine Linton]] as a surrogate mother; and she attempts, with mixed success, to protect young Cathy from Heathcliff's machinations.

Critically, Nelly is not a passive observer. She withholds information, makes moral judgments, and occasionally acts in ways that shape events. She conceals Heathcliff's return from Edgar for several days, arguably worsening the crisis. She is reluctant to warn Hindley about Heathcliff's gambling. Her narrative can be read as an attempt to justify her own actions and to impose moral order on chaotic events. Brontë leaves it to the reader to decide how much to trust Nelly's version of the story.

## Relationships

**Heathcliff** — Nelly's relationship with Heathcliff is complex and ambivalent. She nursed him as a child and asserts that "no one but myself was kind to him," yet she also admits to disliking him and often judges him harshly. She is one of the few characters who stands up to Heathcliff directly, yet she also fears him profoundly.

**Catherine Earnshaw** — Nelly's mistress and childhood companion. Nelly's portrayal of Catherine is notably unsympathetic: she presents Catherine as proud, willful, and hysterical, framing her love for Heathcliff as morally suspect. Some critics argue that Nelly's disapproval shapes the reader's perception of Catherine more than Catherine's own actions justify.

**Hareton Earnshaw** — Nelly was Hareton's nurse in infancy before being transferred to the Grange, and later becomes his advocate — attempting to intervene when Heathcliff degrades him and ultimately celebrating his redemption through love with the younger Cathy.

**Mr. Lockwood** — Nelly's audience, whose prurient curiosity about the Heights drives the frame narrative. Their relationship — Nelly as storyteller, Lockwood as listener — establishes the novel's layered narrative structure.

## Character & Traits

Nelly is a narrator of immense authority and questionable reliability. Her omniscience is remarkable — she records conversations verbatim thirty years after the fact, raising questions about narrative invention. Her moral framework is conventional Victorian Christianity, and she frequently interprets events as moral lessons, sometimes oversimplifying the novel's more ambiguous moments.

Yet Nelly's biases make her a richer narrator, not a weaker one. Her disapproval of Catherine's passion, her fear of Heathcliff's violence, her partiality toward the Lintons' gentility — these shape a narrative that the reader must actively interpret. As the novel's moral center, Nelly represents ordinary decency thrust into extraordinary circumstances, struggling to make sense of forces beyond her understanding.

> [013-007] "Is Mr. Heathcliff a man? If so, is he mad? And if not, is he a devil?"
