# Narrative and Storytelling

*Wuthering Heights* is among the most narratively complex novels in English. Emily Brontë structures her story as a series of embedded narratives — a story within a story within a story — mediated through multiple tellers, each with their own biases, limitations, and investments in the events they recount. The novel is not simply told; it is overheard, remembered, written down, and read back, creating a Chinese-box structure that distances the reader from the raw events while simultaneously intensifying their emotional impact through the very act of mediation.

## The Frame: Lockwood's Diary

The outermost narrative frame is [[Mr. Lockwood]]'s diary, written during his convalescence at [[Thrushcross Grange]] after his disastrous first visit to [[Wuthering Heights]]. Lockwood is the quintessential unreliable narrator — not because he lies, but because he fundamentally misunderstands what he sees. A London-bred gentleman who prides himself on his misanthropy, he consistently misreads the people he encounters: he mistakes the dead rabbits piled on the settle for pet cats, interprets Catherine Linton as Heathcliff's wife, and fails to recognise the depth of the passions swirling around him.

Lockwood's function is to be the outsider who demands explanation, and his incomprehension is the device that prompts [[Nelly Dean]] to tell the family history. His diary entries — dated, self-conscious, and mildly ironic — frame the novel as a document, a written artefact that we as readers are purportedly reading alongside the narrator himself. After Nelly finishes her tale, Lockwood records his method with characteristic self-regard:

> "I'll continue it in her own words, only a little condensed. She is, on the whole, a very fair narrator, and I don't think I could improve her style." (015-008)

## Nelly Dean: The Oral Storyteller

Nelly Dean — Ellen Dean, housekeeper first at the Heights and then at the Grange — is the novel's primary narrator and the source of nearly all the story that Lockwood records. She represents the tradition of oral storytelling: her tale unfolds as she sits by Lockwood's sickbed, speaking over several days, in a voice that is colloquial, digressive, and personal. Unlike Lockwood, Nelly was a participant in many of the events she describes; she raised [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and [[Heathcliff]], witnessed their childhood bond, and was present at the novel's key turning points.

Nelly's reliability has been debated since the novel's publication. She is not neutral: she has her own moral framework (conventional, Christian, pragmatic), her own allegiances (she favours Edgar Linton and disapproves of Heathcliff), and her own moments of culpability (she conceals Heathcliff's visits to the dying Catherine). Yet she is also the novel's most consistent moral intelligence, and her narrative — though filtered through her own perspective — conveys a complexity that she herself may not fully understand. Her own assessment of her narrative role reveals both her self-awareness and her blind spots:

> "I could scarcely refrain from smiling at this antipathy to the poor fellow [Heathcliff]; who was a waif, as lonely and helpless as any that ever breathed" (004-008)

## Embedded Narratives

Within Nelly's story are further embedded narratives. Nelly frequently quotes other characters at length — she reproduces Catherine's confession of love for Heathcliff, Heathcliff's account of his final meeting with Catherine, Isabella's letter describing her miserable life at the Heights after her elopement. These nested stories create a texture of multiple voices, each adding its own inflection to the events. And within these quoted speeches, characters themselves become storytellers: Heathcliff narrates his childhood memories, Catherine recounts her dreams, Zillah reports on the household's decline.

The effect of this narrative layering is twofold. It creates distance — we are always aware that we are receiving events at several removes — and it creates intimacy, because each narrative layer contains the emotional intensity of a speaker telling a story that matters deeply to them.

## Other Voices and Silences

Beyond Nelly and Lockwood, the novel contains a range of secondary narrators whose contributions enrich the texture of the storytelling. Isabella's letter to Nelly — a long, detailed account of her life at the Heights after her marriage to Heathcliff — is the most substantial of these. Written in Isabella's own voice, the letter provides the only first-person testimony from inside Heathcliff's household during his reign of terror. Its tone — a mixture of horror, self-recrimination, and grim wit — is distinct from Nelly's measured narration and Lockwood's ironic distance, adding a third register to the novel's vocal range.

Zillah, the servant at Wuthering Heights, provides another narrative perspective — more limited, more practical, but valuable precisely because she is not invested in the family's emotional dramas. Her accounts of the household's daily life under Heathcliff's regime offer a ground-level view that complements Nelly's more interpretative narration. Joseph's speech, rendered in nearly impenetrable Yorkshire dialect, functions as yet another narrative register — a voice from an older, harsher world whose religious severity and folk wisdom exist in permanent counterpoint to the emotional intensities of the central characters.

Perhaps most significant are the voices the novel does not allow to speak directly. Heathcliff's interior experience is reported almost entirely through others — Nelly describes him, Isabella describes him, but he rarely narrates his own experience in the sustained way that, for example, Catherine does. This narrative withholding contributes to Heathcliff's mystery: we know him through the fear, love, and incomprehension he generates in others, but his own consciousness remains partially opaque. Similarly, Catherine Earnshaw — the novel's emotional centre — speaks powerfully in her quoted moments but dies at the novel's midpoint, after which she exists only in memory and ghostly presence. The narrative structure thus enacts the novel's central theme: the impossibility of fully knowing another person.

## Temporal Structure

The novel's temporal architecture is as complex as its narrative layering. The story spans roughly thirty years — from Heathcliff's arrival at the Heights as a child in the 1770s to Lockwood's final visit in 1802 — but it is not told in chronological order. Instead, the narrative folds back upon itself repeatedly: the present-tense frame of 1801–1802 encloses Nelly's retrospective account of events from thirty years earlier, which itself contains digressions into yet earlier periods.

This temporal structure creates a distinctive reading experience. The reader knows the outcome before knowing the cause: we meet the degraded Hareton before we learn of his birthright; we encounter the haunted, spectral Heathcliff of 1801 before we understand the passion that made him so. The effect is to make the past feel simultaneously remote and achingly present — remote because it is filtered through multiple layers of memory and narration; present because its consequences are visible everywhere in the world of the frame narrative. Nelly's storytelling is not a detached historical exercise but an attempt to make Lockwood (and the reader) understand the world they are currently inhabiting — a world in which the dead are as real as the living and the past has never truly passed.

Brontë's manipulation of temporal scale is equally striking. The first-generation story — Catherine, Heathcliff, Edgar — occupies the vast majority of Nelly's narration and the reader's emotional investment, yet covers only about fifteen years. The second-generation story — Cathy, Linton, Hareton — covers a comparable span but is narrated with greater compression. And Heathcliff's final transformation, his three-day fast and death, occupies a few chapters of intense focus, as though time itself has slowed to accommodate the gravity of the moment. The variable tempo of the narration reflects the novel's implicit hierarchy of significance: the closer we come to the emotional core — the bond between Heathcliff and Catherine — the more slowly time moves.

## The Purpose of Narrative Complexity

Brontë's choice of a complex narrative structure serves several purposes. First, it mirrors the novel's thematic concern with the difficulty of knowing other people: just as the reader must piece together the truth from multiple, biased accounts, so the characters themselves struggle to understand one another. Second, the act of storytelling becomes a form of survival: Nelly tells the story to make sense of a world that has been shattered by passion and violence; Lockwood writes it down to pass the time during his convalescence; and the reader, by reading, participates in the same effort of comprehension. Third, the narrative structure enacts the temporal layering of the novel's world — a present that is haunted by an unrecoverable past, transmitted through memory and language but never fully accessible.
