Lockwood's Nightmare is one of the most famous and unsettling scenes in Emily Brontë's *Wuthering Heights* (1847). Occurring during [[Mr. Lockwood]]'s second visit to [[Wuthering Heights]], the nightmare sequence combines two disturbing dreams—one comic-horrific, one genuinely terrifying—and culminates in the apparition of [[Catherine Earnshaw]]'s ghost at the window. The scene introduces the novel's central [[Ghosts and the Supernatural]] motif and foreshadows the tragic love story at the heart of the narrative.

## Prelude: The Oak-Panelled Bed

Forced to stay overnight at Wuthering Heights by a snowstorm, [[Zillah]] leads Lockwood to a chamber that [[Heathcliff]] has forbidden to visitors. The room contains a strange oak-panelled bed—"a large oak case, with squares cut out near the top resembling coach windows" [003-002]—that resembles a small closet built into the wall. On the window ledge, Lockwood finds mildewed books and writing scratched into the paint:

> *Catherine Earnshaw*, here and there varied to *Catherine Heathcliff*, and then again to *Catherine Linton*. [003-004]

As Lockwood dozes over Catherine's childhood diary and her marginal annotations in a Testament, the names seem to come alive: "the air swarmed with Catherines" [003-005]. This blurring of reality and dream sets the stage for the nightmare to come.

## The First Dream: Jabez Branderham's Sermon

Lockwood's first dream is a satirical nightmare of religious excess. He dreams of journeying through deep snow with [[Joseph]] to hear the Reverend [[Jabez Branderham]] preach at [[Gimmerden Sough]] from the text "Seventy Times Seven." The sermon, impossibly, is divided into "four hundred and ninety parts, each fully equal to an ordinary address from the pulpit, and each discussing a separate sin" [003-021].

The dream culminates in Lockwood denouncing Branderham himself, whereupon the congregation attacks him with pilgrim's staves. The tumult is revealed to be merely "the branch of a fir-tree that touched my lattice as the blast wailed by, and rattled its dry cones against the panes" [003-025]—a natural sound transformed by the dreaming mind into violence.

## The Second Dream: Catherine's Ghost

The second dream is far more terrifying. In it, Lockwood tries to silence the rattling fir-branch by breaking the window glass, but instead of wood he grasps "the fingers of a little, ice-cold hand" [003-027]:

> "Catherine Linton," it replied, shiveringly ... "I'm come home: I'd lost my way on the moor!" [003-030]

Lockwood sees "a child's face looking through the window." In desperate terror, he rubs the child's wrist against the broken glass "till the blood ran down and soaked the bedclothes," yet still it wails "Let me in!" [003-031]. The violence of this image—a child's bleeding wrist—is one of the novel's most visceral moments.

Heathcliff, woken by Lockwood's screams, enters the room. When Lockwood leaves, Heathcliff performs one of the novel's most revealing actions:

> He got on to the bed, and wrenched open the lattice, bursting, as he pulled at it, into an uncontrollable passion of tears. "Come in! come in!" he sobbed. "Cathy, do come. Oh, do—*once* more! Oh! my heart's darling! hear me this time, Catherine, at last!" [ch03]

## Significance

The nightmare scene operates on multiple levels:

- **Narrative catalyst**: Lockwood's account of the ghost and Heathcliff's reaction prompts [[Nelly Dean]] to begin telling the full history of the Earnshaws and Lintons
- **Introduction of the supernatural**: Unlike Gothic novels that keep the supernatural ambiguous, Brontë embeds it in a dream—yet Heathcliff's response suggests the ghost is real to him
- **Catherine's eternal wanderer**: The ghost's claim to have been "walking the earth these twenty years" [003-049] establishes the novel's central tragedy—a love that cannot be contained by death
- **The window as threshold**: The window between inside and outside, living and dead, becomes a recurring symbol throughout the novel
