## Overview

Animals in *Wuthering Heights* are never merely decorative. The dogs at the Heights are part of the household's character — fierce, suspicious, and loyal only to their pack. Characters are repeatedly described in animal terms: Heathcliff is "a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (001-018), a "vulgar young ruffian" and "worse than a brute" (008-019). Isabella asks of Heathcliff, "Is he a brute?" and is answered by the hanging of her spaniel (011-042). The novel's animal imagery does not simply equate bestiality with evil — the loyal dog Skulker protects Thrushcross Grange as fiercely as the Heights' dogs protect their territory — but it consistently uses the animal-human boundary to ask what separates the civilised from the savage, and whether the distinction holds.

## Manifestations

- **The dogs of Wuthering Heights**: Lockwood's first encounter with the Heights' dogs is a near-attack (001-017). The dogs — "grim shaggy sheep-dogs" (001-018), "a brood of tigers" (001-021) — embody the house's hostility to outsiders. They are extensions of Heathcliff's own territorial ferocity.

- **Skulker and the Grange**: The Lintons' bulldog Skulker, who seizes Catherine by the ankle during her first visit (008-019), represents a different kind of animality — domestic but still dangerous, the civilised counterpart to the Heights' feral pack.

- **Heathcliff as animal**: The novel's most sustained characterisation strategy is the bestialisation of Heathcliff. He is "an unreclaimed creature" (008-019), compared to a "cuckoo" usurping the nest (004-026), and described by Isabella as "a lying fiend! a monster!" (013-047). But the imagery is ambivalent — his wolfishness is also his power, his refusal to be tamed by the forces that degraded him.

- **Isabella's spaniel**: Heathcliff hangs Isabella's dog (011-042) as a symbolic act — the destruction of the pampered pet signals what he will do to its owner. The act crystallises the novel's use of animals as proxies for human cruelty.

- **Feathers and birds**: Catherine's pillow-tearing in her delirium (012-012–012-013) — identifying feathers as belonging to a lapwing, a wild duck, a pigeon — is a catalogue of local birdlife that maps onto her fractured identity. The lapwing's nest was robbed; Catherine was similarly taken from the moors and caged in the Grange.

## Key Passages

- **001-017–001-018**: Lockwood's encounter with the Heights dogs — the introduction of the animal motif and the house's feral character.
- **008-019**: Heathcliff termed "worse than a brute" — the central animal comparison around which his character is constructed.
- **011-042**: Heathcliff hangs Isabella's spaniel — the novel's most direct act of animal cruelty as symbolic violence.
- **012-012–012-013**: Catherine's feather catalogue in her delirium — animals as markers of identity.
