# Names and Identity

No other nineteenth-century novel shares its characters' names as relentlessly
as *Wuthering Heights*. The name "Catherine" appears twice, across two
generations — mother and daughter, both headstrong, both caught between two
men and two houses. The surname "Earnshaw" attaches to three generations of
the same family. Even the given name "Linton" migrates: it begins as a surname
attached to the civilized world of Thrushcross Grange, becomes a first name
for Heathcliff's sickly son, and survives into the novel's final sentence as
part of the married name of the younger Catherine.

This onomastic economy is not incidental. Naming in *Wuthering Heights* is a
form of power — a way of claiming, disowning, and transmitting identity across
generations. The novel's [[Narrative-and-Storytelling|narrative structure]]
itself is shaped by the persistence of names: Lockwood's discovery of the
carved names on the window ledge at Wuthering Heights [003-016] is the event
that triggers the entire tale, as if the physical inscription of names
demands their story be told.

## Heathcliff: The Name Without a Surname

The novel's most striking naming act is also its most violent. When old
[[Mr-Earnshaw|Mr. Earnshaw]] brings the foundling child home from [[Liverpool]],
he gives him the name of a son who died: "Heathcliff." It is a single name —
no surname, no lineage, no place in the social order. Nelly Dean recounts the
moment plainly: "I was told to wash it, and give it clean things, and let it
sleep with the children" [004-012]. The name functions as a kind of social
death: Heathcliff is simultaneously incorporated into the Earnshaw family and
marked as forever outside it. He cannot be an Earnshaw, because Hindley
inherits that name; he cannot claim any name of his own, because his origins
are unknown.

> "They entirely refused to have it in bed with them, or even in their room; and I had no more sense, so I put it on the landing of the stairs, hoping it might be gone on the morrow." (004-003)

The consequences of this naming are structural. [[Hindley Earnshaw]]'s abuse of
Heathcliff takes the form of stripping him of the education that would have
made him a gentleman — a process that is itself a kind of dis-naming, an
erasure of the social identity that Mr. Earnshaw's original gift of the name
"son" had conferred. Hindley reduces Heathcliff to "a labourer on the
farm" and forbids him from sitting with the family or sharing their lessons
[006-025]. When Heathcliff returns three years later, he has acquired wealth
and manners but still no surname; the name "Heathcliff" becomes, in the second
half of the novel, a byword for the dark force that has swallowed both great
houses.

Isabella, writing to Nelly after her marriage, captures the horror of
Heathcliff's namelessness from within: "Is Mr. Heathcliff a man? If so, is he
mad? And if not, is he a devil? ... I do hate him — I am wretched — I have
been a fool!" [013-055]. Her use of the name "Heathcliff" — now her own
married name — is a marker of how completely she has been absorbed into his
world, her identity subsumed under his.

## Catherine and Catherine

The doubling of the Catherines is the novel's most commented-upon naming
pattern. Catherine Earnshaw, the mother, signs her childhood diary as
"Catherine Earnshaw" and later marries to become "Catherine Linton." Her
daughter, born at the moment of her death, is named "Catherine Linton" —
bearing her mother's married name from birth. When the younger Catherine
marries Linton Heathcliff, she becomes "Catherine Heathcliff"; when she is
widowed and marries [[Hareton Earnshaw]], she becomes "Catherine Earnshaw"
once more.

The naming of the child is itself significant: Edgar names the baby Catherine
after her mother [016-055], an act that simultaneously memorializes his dead
wife and ensures that the name "Catherine" will survive into the next
generation. The name is a gift of love, but it is also a chain — the younger
Catherine inherits not only her mother's name but the unresolved conflicts that
the name carries.

The circle is not mere symmetry. It is a restoration: the younger Catherine
ends the novel bearing the original name of the family that owned Wuthering
Heights before Heathcliff's intervention. The name's return to "Earnshaw" at
the novel's close is a quiet but definitive signal that the legitimate order
has been restored.

## Linton: From Surname to Weapon

The name "Linton" follows a similarly charged trajectory. Originally the
sumame of the genteel family at [[Thrushcross Grange]], it passes to
[[Catherine-Earnshaw|Catherine Earnshaw]] through her marriage to
[[Edgar Linton]]. Heathcliff, in an act of calculated cruelty, gives the name
as a first name to his son by Isabella: Linton Heathcliff. The child is
constitutionally Linton-like — pale, delicate, and emotionally manipulative —
but his legal identity as a Linton is Heathcliff's tool for claiming the
Grange. The name that once signified refinement becomes an instrument of
appropriation.

Heathcliff's use of the name is characteristically precise. He refers to his
son not as "my son" but as "my property" and treats the child's legal name as
a lever for transferring the Linton inheritance to himself. When Edgar Linton
dies, Thrushcross Grange passes to the younger Catherine, but on her marriage
to Linton Heathcliff — and Linton's subsequent death — the property falls to
Heathcliff as his son's heir. The name "Linton," once the sign of a family's
ancient gentility, becomes the mechanism of its destruction.

When the younger Catherine marries Linton Heathcliff, she is forced to bear
the Heathcliff name that her mother's generation had struggled to escape. The
marriage is Heathcliff's final revenge — the union of the Earnshaw, Linton,
and Heathcliff names under his control.

## Hareton: The Name Restored

[[Hareton Earnshaw]], the son of Hindley, bears the Earnshaw name but is
prevented by Heathcliff from claiming any of its social meaning. He cannot
read the family name carved above the door of Wuthering Heights: the name
"Earnshaw" is, to him, a sound without significance. Cathy's education of
Hareton restores the name's meaning — she teaches him to read it, and in doing
so, restores his identity.

The moment of restoration is explicitly tied to literacy and naming. When
Hareton struggles to read the inscription above the door — "Hareton Earnshaw"
carved into the stone [033-011] — Cathy guides his hand over the letters. The
physical act of tracing the name is also an act of claiming it, of making the
abstract fact of ancestry into something tangible and owned. This scene inverts
the novel's opening: where Lockwood could only spell out the names of the dead
in feverish confusion, Cathy and Hareton read the names of the living and make
them their own.

The novel's final genealogical act is Cathy and Hareton's marriage, which
unites the Earnshaw and Linton bloodlines under the Earnshaw name — a
restoration of the order that prevailed before Heathcliff's arrival. The last
line of the novel mentions only "Catherine Earnshaw" and "Hareton Earnshaw,"
the Heathcliff name having been extinguished entirely.

## The Carved Names

The novel's most explicit treatment of naming as identity occurs in the scene
at the Heights when Lockwood discovers the three names carved into the window
ledge: "Catherine Earnshaw," "Catherine Linton," and "Catherine Heathcliff"
[003-016]. The three inscriptions condense a lifetime into a single artifact:
childhood, marriage, and the name forced upon her by circumstance. Catherine
Earnshaw exists in all three names, and in none of them fully; her identity
exceeds any single name, yet she is defined by the names she bore.

Lockwood's inability to decipher these names — he finds them in a feverish
dream, the letters "swarming" before his eyes — mirrors the outsider's
disorientation that the novel's frame narrative produces. To know the names
is not to know the story; the story must be told by [[Nelly Dean]], who has
lived through the events the names compress. In this sense, the names are
the novel in miniature: inscriptions that demand narration.
