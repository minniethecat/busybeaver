## Overview

Catherine "Cathy" Linton is the daughter of [[Catherine Earnshaw]] and [[Edgar Linton]], born at [[Thrushcross-Grange]] and sheltered from the dark history of [[Wuthering-Heights]] for much of her childhood. She inherits her mother's passionate spirit and dark eyes, but her father's gentler disposition tempers her character. Cathy becomes the unwitting instrument of [[Heathcliff]]'s final act of revenge — forced into marriage with his sickly son [[Linton-Heathcliff]] to consolidate Heathcliff's claim to Thrushcross Grange — yet her resilience, sharp intelligence, and capacity for forgiveness allow her to break the generational cycle of cruelty, most powerfully in her relationship with [[Hareton-Earnshaw]].

## Biography

Cathy is born at Thrushcross Grange, and her mother dies shortly after. Her father, Edgar Linton, raises her in a protective, loving environment, never telling her of Wuthering Heights or Heathcliff. She is "as different now from your old Catherine as a sophisticated young lady from a hoyden" (014-008), yet she inherits her mother's dark eyes and fierce curiosity about the world beyond the Grange.

Her first encounter with Wuthering Heights comes through her father's attempt to keep her away from it — which only intensifies her desire to explore. At the Heights, she meets Hareton Earnshaw and initially mocks his roughness and illiteracy (020-017). Her sheltered upbringing at the Grange has given her class prejudices that clash with the raw reality of the Heights.

Heathcliff's scheme unfolds when he lures Cathy to Wuthering Heights and forces her to marry his dying son, Linton Heathcliff. The marriage gives Heathcliff legal claim to Thrushcross Grange upon Edgar Linton's death. Trapped at the Heights after both her father and husband die, Cathy finds herself reduced to a dependent in Heathcliff's household — "Mrs. Linton Heathcliff now, was suffered to stay at the Heights only on sufferance" (028-036).

In her forced confinement, Cathy's character deepens. Initially hostile toward Hareton — mocking his attempts to learn to read — she gradually comes to see his genuine effort and goodness of heart. She teaches him to read, reversing the degradation Heathcliff inflicted. Their growing affection becomes the novel's vision of a healthier love: one built on mutual growth rather than all-consuming passion. The novel ends with them planning to marry and move to Thrushcross Grange, the two estates unified not through revenge but through love.

## Relationships

**Edgar Linton** — Cathy's devoted father, who shields her from the world of Wuthering Heights. His love is tender but perhaps overprotective; Cathy's ignorance of her family history leaves her vulnerable to Heathcliff's manipulation.

**Heathcliff** — Cathy's captor and tormentor. He forces her into marriage with his son and gloats over her degradation: "Catherine Linton (the very name warms me), my bonny lass, I shall be your father to-morrow" (022-025). Yet even he is eventually worn down by her resemblance to her mother.

**Linton Heathcliff** — Cathy's cousin and first husband, a sickly, selfish boy used by his father as a tool. Cathy initially pities him, but his cowardice and complicity in Heathcliff's scheme strain any affection between them.

**Hareton Earnshaw** — Initially her antagonist, ultimately her beloved. Cathy's journey from mocking Hareton's ignorance to becoming his teacher and partner represents the novel's moral arc: "Catherine Linton was her maiden name. I nursed her" (004-015) — and in the end, she will take the name Earnshaw by choice, not coercion.

**Nelly Dean** — Cathy's nurse and confidante, who narrates much of her story to Lockwood. Nelly's affection for Cathy is deep and protective, though she is sometimes complicit in keeping secrets from her.
