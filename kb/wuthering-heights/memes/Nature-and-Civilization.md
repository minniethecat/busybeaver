## Overview

The opposition between nature and civilization is the novel's foundational binary, mapping onto every level of the narrative: Wuthering Heights versus Thrushcross Grange, Heathcliff versus Edgar Linton, wild passion versus social propriety. Brontë does not simply endorse one pole over the other; instead, she shows how each is incomplete without the other, and how the violent collision between them produces both tragedy and, in the second generation, the possibility of synthesis.

## Manifestations

- **Two houses as two worlds**: Wuthering Heights is "grotesque carving" and "a wilderness of crumbling griffins" (001-011), its name evoking storm and exposure. Thrushcross Grange is carpeted in crimson, its inhabitants sheltered, its grounds cultivated — and its children weep over a lapdog while the Heights children peer through the window like "black little imps" (008-019).

- **Heathcliff as nature incarnate**: He is repeatedly described in animalistic terms — "a fierce, pitiless, wolfish man" (001-018), "an unreclaimed creature, without refinement" (008-019) — and his return transforms him into the agent of nature's revenge against the civilisation that excluded him.

- **Catherine's split identity**: Catherine embodies the conflict internally. At the Heights she is "half savage and hardy, and free" (012-037); at the Grange she becomes "the queen of the countryside" (009-072). Her famous cry — "Nelly, I am Heathcliff!" (009-092) — is a declaration of allegiance to nature over civilisation, made on the eve of her marriage into the civilised world. The split kills her.

- **The second-generation resolution**: Catherine Linton and Hareton Earnshaw, united at the end, represent a cautious fusion: Hareton, the natural gentleman whose innate dignity survives Heathcliff's degradation experiment, and Catherine, the civilised girl who learns to value what lies beneath exteriors.

## Key Passages

- **008-019**: Catherine learns to code-switch between the Heights and the Grange: "where she heard Heathcliff termed a 'vulgar young ruffian,' and 'worse than a brute,' she took care not to act like him." The passage exposes the performativity of civilisation — Catherine is the same person in both houses, but learns what each space permits.

- **012-037**: The dying Catherine longs for her childhood self: "I wish I were a girl again, half savage and hardy, and free; and laughing at injuries, not maddening under them!" Civilisation has given her status but taken her self.

- **001-011**: Lockwood's first impression of Wuthering Heights — the wild carvings, the date "1500," the name "Hareton Earnshaw" — establishes the house as a site of deep, untamed history, untouched by the Georgian refinement of the Grange.

## Character Connections

| Character | Position on the Nature—Civilisation Axis |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| **Heathcliff** | Nature pole: "an unreclaimed creature, without refinement, without cultivation" (008-019) |
| **Edgar Linton** | Civilisation pole: "the soft thing" (009-090), cultivated, gentle, and powerless against the storm |
| **Catherine Earnshaw** | Torn between: drawn to Edgar's world but defined by Heathcliff's wildness |
| **Isabella Linton** | Civilisation → Nature: her flight to Heights represents civilisation's fatal attraction to what it fears |
| **Hareton Earnshaw** | Nature's redeemability: innate goodness survives despite systematic degradation |
