## Peace as a Condition of Liberty

Within any peaceful order there is scope for the development of individual life and the exercise of social cooperation. The Liberal vision of the good society presupposes the absence of arbitrary violence — both domestically, through the rule of law, and internationally, through relations of mutual respect among nations. Peace is not merely the absence of war but the condition in which the common good can be pursued through the common will. (006-012)

The conception of the common good as it has been explained can be realized in its fullness only through the common will. There are, of course, elements of value in the good government of a benevolent despot or of a fatherly aristocracy — but these are inherently fragile, dependent on the character of rulers rather than on the durable institutions of free [[nation|people]]s. (006-012)

## Free Trade as a Force for Peace

The school of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] was saturated with the conviction that the unfettered action of the individual is the mainspring of national progress. Central to this conviction was the belief that [[economic-liberty|free trade]] between nations would bind them together in mutual interest, making war economically irrational. The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] saw commerce as a civilizing force that would replace military competition with economic cooperation. (004-001)

This vision of peace through trade — that men who exchange goods do not exchange bullets — was one of the most influential ideas of nineteenth-century [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. It informed the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], the negotiation of trade treaties, and the broader Liberal preference for arbitration over military confrontation in international disputes.

## The Roman Peace and Its Limitations

The constructive genius of Rome devised a method of dealing with the political problems involved in expanding relations: the extension of citizenship until it included the whole free population of the Mediterranean basin. But this extension was fatal to the free self-government of a city state. The population of [[italy|Italy]] could not meet in the Forum to decide upon peace or war. The Pax Romana was a peace purchased at the price of liberty. (001-005)

This historical example illustrates a recurring Liberal theme: peace imposed by empire is not the same as peace achieved through freedom. The Liberal ideal is a peace that rests on the consent and self-determination of [[nation|people]]s, not on the supremacy of a single power.

## Peace, National Autonomy, and International Equality

Standing by national autonomy and international equality, [[liberalism|Liberalism]] is necessarily in conflict with the Imperial idea as it is ordinarily presented. But this is not to say that it is indifferent to the interests of [[british-empire|the Empire]] as a whole, to the sentiment of unity pervading its white population, or to all the possibilities involved in the bare fact that a fourth part of the human race recognizes one flag and one sovereign. (009-013)

The Liberal vision of international peace is not one of uniformity or world government but of a community of self-governing nations, each respecting the rights of the others, linked by trade and mutual interest, and resolving disputes through law rather than war. This vision is both the foundation of Liberal internationalism and its unfinished project.
