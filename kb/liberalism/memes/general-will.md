## Definition

The general will (*volonté générale*) is the concept, formulated by [[rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] in *The [[social-contract|Social Contract]]* (1762), that legitimate political authority must be derived from a collective will that aims at the common good rather than at the aggregate of private interests. It is distinguished from the "will of all" (*volonté de tous*) — the mere sum of individual preferences — by its orientation toward what is genuinely shared among citizens, and it is this orientation that, in [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s argument, makes obedience to the general will compatible with freedom: in obeying the general will, the citizen obeys only himself as a member of the sovereign body.

The doctrine was revolutionary in its implications. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] summarises [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s argument: "out of many conflicting individual wills, a common or general will could be formed. A government might be instituted as the organ of this will" (003-005). This replaced the monarchical principle — authority descending from God to king to subjects — with the democratic principle of authority ascending from the [[nation|people]] to their delegates.

## Rousseau's Formulation

[[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s argument proceeds from a fundamental problem: how can individuals be bound by law without being subjected to the will of another person? His answer is that when individuals participate in the formation of the general will, the laws they obey are, in a morally significant sense, laws they have given to themselves. The citizen who resists the general will "would be forced to be free" — compelled to abide by the terms of the social contract to which, as a rational being, they have implicitly consented.

The doctrine places sovereignty inalienably in the [[nation|people]] as a collective body — "Law is the expression of the general will" (003-014), as the French Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man and of the Citizen (1789) would later declare in Article VI, translating [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s philosophy into constitutional language. The "nation" is here "conceived as a collective whole, as a unit" (003-019), not as a mere aggregation of individuals — a move with profound consequences for the subsequent development of nationalist and democratic thought.

:::info
**General Will vs. Will of All**: [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s crucial distinction. The will of all is the sum of private interests — what individuals would choose if they voted purely on the basis of self-interest. The general will is what emerges when citizens deliberate about the common good. A vote may be unanimous and yet still express only the will of all, if each voter is thinking only of private advantage. Conversely, a minority may articulate the general will if it speaks for the [[common-good|common interest]] that others have failed to perceive.
:::

## The General Will and the Liberal Tradition

The doctrine of the general will posed an enduring challenge to the liberal tradition. On the one hand, it supplied a philosophical basis for democracy more robust than mere convenience — if legitimate law requires the participation of those subject to it, then representative government is not one option among many but a moral necessity. On the other hand, the idea that individuals could be "forced to be free" opened the door to a dangerous conflation of majority rule with moral truth, a conflation against which liberals from Benjamin Constant to Isaiah Berlin would later warn.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] engages this tension directly. He acknowledges that the general will must impose limits on the individual — "we may also restrain him from obstructing the general will; and this we have to do whenever uniformity is necessary to the end which the general will has in view" (007-006) — but insists that such restraint is justified only when it genuinely serves the common good and leaves intact "the functions, social and personal, in which [liberty's] value lies" (007-012).

The resolution, for [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], lies in the organic character of the social mind. The general will is not an external imposition on individuals but an expression of their own social nature — "the more securely [society] is founded on freedom and general willing assent, the more it is free to work out all the achievements in which the individual is feeble or helpless alone" (006-011). True community is not achieved by suppressing individuality but by harmonising individual and collective purposes.

## Limitations and Dangers

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is alert to the dangers of an unqualified doctrine of the general will. "Limitations" imposed in the name of the collective "may cramp the development of the general will, and perchance prove a serious obstacle to collective progress" (007-002). The general will, in other words, is not a fixed entity that can be identified once and for all — it develops through the very process of free discussion and experiment that a premature uniformity would foreclose.

The tension between collective authority and [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] is not one that can be resolved by a simple formula. It requires the continuous exercise of political judgment, informed by the liberal commitment to keeping open the channels of criticism, experiment, and dissent through which the general will itself is refined.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats the general will as one of the foundational concepts of democratic liberalism, but also as one requiring careful specification. In Chapter III, he traces its emergence in [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s thought and its translation into the political language of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] (003-014, 003-019). In Chapter VII, he works out its implications for the relationship between state and individual — arguing, against the laissez-faire tradition, that collective action through the state can be an expression of the general will rather than an encroachment upon it, provided the action genuinely serves a shared purpose that could not be achieved by individuals acting independently (007-006, 007-012).

The concept also informs [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s understanding of liberty itself. The general will is not the antithesis of freedom but its political expression — freedom is "founded on freedom and general willing assent" (006-011), not on isolation from the collective. The task of liberal politics is to create the conditions under which the general will can form and express itself without extinguishing the individual initiative and diversity on which social progress depends.
