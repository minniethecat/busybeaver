Religious liberty is a core element of personal liberty in the Liberal tradition. It encompasses freedom of thought, expression, and the right to worship in any form that does not inflict injury on others or breach public order (002-011). Religious liberty rests on the conception that a person's religion ranks with their own innermost thought and feelings — it is the most concrete expression of their personal attitude to life, to their kind, and to their own origin and destiny (002-011).

## The Two Dimensions of Religious Liberty

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] distinguishes between the external and internal aspects of religious liberty. Externally, it includes the liberties of thought and expression, together with the right of worship in any form which does not inflict injury on others or involve a breach of public order. This external dimension also carries an implication of decency and restraint in expression which avoids unnecessary insult to the feelings of others (002-011).

> What, then, is the primary meaning of religious liberty? Externally, I take it to include the liberties of thought and expression, and to add to these the right of worship in any form which does not inflict injury on others or involve a breach of public order.
>
> (002-011)

Internally, the spirit of religious liberty rests on the conception that religion is drenched in personality. The more religion is recognized as spiritual, the starker the contradiction that anyone should seek to impose a religion on another. Properly regarded, the attempt is not wicked, but impossible (002-011).

> There is no real religion that is not thus drenched in personality; and the more religion is recognized for spiritual the starker the contradiction is felt to be that anyone should seek to impose a religion on another. Properly regarded, the attempt is not wicked, but impossible.
>
> (002-011)

## Beyond Mere Toleration

The demand for religious liberty soon goes beyond mere toleration. Religious liberty is incomplete as long as any belief is penalized — for example, by carrying with it exclusion from office or from educational advantages. Full liberty implies full equality. On this view, toleration is only the beginning; the Liberal ideal demands that no religious belief should carry civil or political disabilities (002-011).

## Liberty of Thought and Expression

At the basis of religious liberty lies liberty of thought — freedom from inquisition into opinions that a person forms in their own mind, the inner citadel where the individual must rule. But liberty of thought is of very little avail without liberty to exchange thoughts, since thought is mainly a social product. Thus with liberty of thought goes liberty of speech and liberty of writing, printing, and peaceable discussion (002-011).

> At the basis lies liberty of thought — freedom from inquisition into opinions that a man forms in his own mind — the inner citadel where, if anywhere, the individual must rule. But liberty of thought is of very little avail without liberty to exchange thoughts — since thought is mainly a social product; and so with liberty of thought goes liberty of speech and liberty of writing, printing, and peaceable discussion.
>
> (002-011)

## Limits and Difficulties

Religious liberty is not absolute. No modern state would tolerate a form of religious worship which included cannibalism, human sacrifice, or the burning of witches. The limits of just liberty here are not easy to draw either in theory or in practice — they lead immediately to one of the points at which liberty and order may be in conflict (002-011).

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes that British practice has arrived at a workable solution: it is open to a person to preach the principles of any religion, but it is not open to them to practise such precepts as would violate the rights of others or cause a breach of the peace. Expression is free, and worship is free as far as it is the expression of personal devotion. So far as religious practices infringe the freedom — or, more generally, the rights — of others, they cannot enjoy unqualified freedom (002-011).

## Liberal Toleration and Truth

The Liberal approach to religion is connected with a broader attitude toward truth and error. The Liberal does not meet opinions which they conceive to be false with toleration, as though they did not matter. They meet them with justice, and exact for them a fair hearing as though they mattered just as much as their own (006-007). [[liberalism|Liberalism]] applies the wisdom of Gamaliel — not in a spirit of indifference, but in the full conviction of the potency of truth (006-007).
