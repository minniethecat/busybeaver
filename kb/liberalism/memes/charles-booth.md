## The Scientific Study of Poverty

Charles Booth was the most important of the empirical social investigators whose work transformed the British understanding of poverty at the end of the nineteenth century. His monumental survey, *Life and [[labour-party|Labour]] of the People in [[london|London]]*, applied statistical methods to the measurement of economic deprivation, replacing anecdote and moral assumption with systematic evidence. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] cited Booth's work as decisive in demonstrating that poverty was structural rather than individual in origin: "The careful researches of Mr. Booth in [[london|London]] and Mr. Rowntree in York, and of others in country districts, have revealed that a considerable percentage of the working classes are actually unable to earn a sum of money representing the full cost of the barest physical necessities for an average family." (007-018)

## Impact on Liberal Thought

Booth's findings were central to the intellectual transformation that made [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]] possible. By demonstrating that even the "relatively well-to-do gravitate towards this line of primary poverty in seasons of stress," (007-018) Booth undermined the laissez-faire assumption that poverty was the consequence of individual moral failure. His work showed that the competitive system itself could not guarantee a living wage to all who worked, and that "the system of industrial competition fails to meet the ethical demand embodied in the conception of the 'living wage.'" (007-018) This empirical demonstration lent weight to the philosophical arguments advanced by [[thomas-hill-green|T. H. Green]] and his followers for a positive conception of the State's responsibilities.

## Broader Influence

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] placed Booth within a wider movement of progressive thought that transformed British politics in the generation after [[gladstone|Gladstone]]. "The labours of Mr. Charles Booth and his associates had at length stated the problem of poverty in scientific terms," (009-004) he wrote, linking Booth's work to the broader current that included the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]], the [[new-unionism|New Unionism]], and the social liberalism that culminated in the [[social-reform|reform]]s of the 1906 Liberal government. Alongside the "idealism of [[william-morris|William Morris]]" and the work of "Mr. and Mrs. Sidney Webb," Booth's research helped to "clear up the relations between the organized efforts of workmen and the functions of the State." (009-004)
