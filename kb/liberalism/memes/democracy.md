## Definition

Democracy, in the liberal tradition, is not merely the rule of the majority but the institutional arrangement through which individuals exercise **shared responsibility** for the community's collective life. It is the political expression of the moral principle that each person counts equally and must therefore have an equal voice in decisions that bind all.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], drawing on [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]], emphasizes that the democratic franchise is not primarily a tool for advancing individual interests but a mechanism of moral development:

> If the adult citizen, male or female, has a right to vote, it is not so much as a means to the enforcement of his claims upon society, but rather as a means of enforcing his personal responsibility for the actions of the community.
> (005-007)

Democracy is thus an education in citizenship as much as a system of decision-making.

## Historical Development

The democratic idea has ancient roots — the city-states of classical Greece practiced [[referendum|direct democracy]] among citizens — but modern liberal democracy is a distinctively modern achievement. It emerged through a long process of extending the franchise, curtailing executive power, and establishing constitutional guarantees.

In [[england|England]], the liberal movement initially advanced democratic aims under the banner of constitutionalism rather than democracy as such. "In its early days and in its more moderate forms, it sought its ends under the guise of constitutionalism" (003-002). The seventeenth-century struggle against Stuart absolutism established parliamentary supremacy, but the franchise remained severely restricted.

The nineteenth century saw the gradual democratization of British politics through the [[reform|Reform]] Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884. Each extension of the franchise was fiercely contested, with opponents arguing that democracy would lead to mob rule and the confiscation of property. [[liberal-party|Liberals]] like [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] responded by reimagining democracy as a system of moral education rather than mere interest aggregation.

The national dimension of democracy — the demand that [[nation|people]]s govern themselves rather than be ruled by foreign powers — was also central to the liberal movement:

> A great part of the liberating movement is occupied with the struggle of entire nations against alien rule, with the revolt of Europe against Napoleon, with the struggle of Italy for freedom.
> (002-023)

From the Italian Risorgimento to the emancipation of subject [[nation|people]]s from the Ottoman [[imperialism|Empire]], the principle of [[self-determination|national self-determination]] was understood as democracy applied to international relations.

## Key Arguments

1. **Democracy as moral education**: Participation in self-government develops the capacities of judgment, responsibility, and public-spiritedness. "The problem of character is the determining issue" in the question of representative government (005-007).

2. **Democracy as the guarantor of rights**: Only a government accountable to the [[nation|people]] can be trusted to respect their rights. Arbitrary power is most effectively checked by making rulers answerable to the ruled (003-002).

3. **Democracy as the condition of liberty**: Fiscal and [[political-freedom|political liberty]] both require democratic accountability. The principle of "no taxation without representation" expresses the inseparability of liberty from democratic consent (002-007).

:::info
**Liberal vs. Illiberal Democracy**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis implies a crucial distinction. Democracy without liberal protections — free speech, association, due process — can become tyranny of the majority. Liberal democracy combines popular sovereignty with constitutional limits on what majorities may decide.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] devotes sustained attention to democracy throughout _[[liberalism|Liberalism]]_, treating it as one of the essential elements of the liberal idea. In Chapter II, he traces the connection between fiscal liberty and [[political-freedom|political liberty]], showing how the demand for consent in taxation naturally led to the demand for representation in government (002-023).

In Chapter V, through his analysis of [[gladstone|Gladstone]] and [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] develops his most sophisticated account of democracy. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s insight — that the value of democracy lies in its effects on character as much as in its institutional mechanics — is presented as the mature liberal view (005-007).

Chapter VIII extends the democratic principle to economic life. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues that any form of socialism compatible with liberalism "must be democratic. It must come from below, not from above" (008-004). The democratic principle thus serves as a criterion for distinguishing liberal [[social-reform|reform]]s from bureaucratic or authoritarian alternatives.
