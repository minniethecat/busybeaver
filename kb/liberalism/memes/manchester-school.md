## Origins and Leadership

The Manchester School was the dominant current in mid-nineteenth-century [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]], taking its name from the northern industrial city that was the centre of the [[economic-liberty|free trade]] movement. Its leaders were [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], who came to prominence through their leadership of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]]. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] described the school as "affiliated in general outlook both to the doctrine of natural liberty and to the discipline of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]," sharing "with the [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ites the thoroughly practical attitude dear to the English mind." (004-002) It had "much less to say of [[human-rights|natural rights]] than the French theorists" and was instead "saturated with the conviction that the unfettered action of the individual is the mainspring of all progress." (004-002)

## Free Trade and Peace

The Manchester School's starting-point was economic. The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], as even later tariff [[social-reform|reform]]ers admitted, were "conceived in the interest of the governing classes" and imposed "a tax on the food of the masses for the benefit of the landlords." (004-002) The [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite remedy was simple: "Give to every man the right to buy in the cheapest and sell in the dearest market ... and trade would automatically expand." (004-002) But [[economic-liberty|free trade]] was not merely an economic policy — it was the keystone of a comprehensive vision. "[[free-trade|Free Trade]] meant peace, and once taught by the example of [[england|Great Britain]]'s prosperity, other nations would follow suit, and [[free-trade|Free Trade]] would be universal." (004-002) From this flowed a connected series of practical commitments: "Nonintervention, peace, restriction of armaments, retrenchment of expenditure, reduction of taxation." (004-002)

## The Minimal State

In the [[manchester|Manchester]] doctrine, the function of government was severely circumscribed. "[[state|Government]] had to maintain order, to restrain men from violence and fraud, to hold them secure in person and property against foreign and domestic enemies, to give them redress against injury." (004-003) Beyond these core functions, the State should withdraw and allow the spontaneous order of free exchange to operate. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted, however, that the school was not purely negative — [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and his allies supported free, secular, and universal education, recognising that "education was necessary to raise the standard of intelligence, and provide the substantial equality of opportunity at the start without which the mass of men could not make use of the freedom given by the removal of legislative restrictions." (004-002)

## The Challenge from Factory Legislation

The Manchester School's commitment to [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]] brought it into direct conflict with the movement for [[factory-act|factory legislation]]. [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and [[john-bright|Bright]] "bitterly opposed" the [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]], (004-006) holding that the adult worker should be free to determine his own conditions of employment. But the facts of industrial life exposed the limits of this position. The factory system had reduced the operative to "the position of a factory hand under an employer," (004-003) and the systematic employment of women and young children "under conditions which outraged the public conscience" demonstrated that "the principle of free contract could not apply" where the bargaining power of the parties was radically unequal. (004-003) Even [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] conceded this in the case of children.

## The Transition to New Liberalism

The experience of industrial legislation led, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s narrative, not to the repudiation of liberty but to its deepening. "As experience ripened, the implications of the new legislation became clearer, and men came to see that by industrial control they were not destroying liberty but confirming it." (004-006) The Manchester School's core insight — that liberty was the foundation of progress — was preserved, but its understanding of what liberty required was transformed. "In place of the system of unfettered agreements between individual and individual which the school of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] contemplated, the industrial system which has actually grown up ... rests on conditions prescribed by the State." (004-005) This was "sometimes held to have involved the decay and death of the older [[liberalism|Liberalism]]," (004-006) but [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw it as the maturation of the liberal tradition rather than its abandonment.

## Legacy and Critique

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] offered a balanced assessment of the Manchester School's legacy. Its achievement in dismantling the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] and establishing [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as British orthodoxy was immense. Yet its "restricted view of the function of government" (004-003) proved inadequate to the realities of industrial capitalism. The distinction [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] drew between the two kinds of restraint — the protectionist tariff that favoured the strong and the factory act that protected the weak — was central to the New Liberal case. "The one is a restraint which, in the Liberal view, would operate in favour of certain industries and interests to the prejudice of others ... The other is a restraint conceived in the interest primarily of the poorer classes with the object of securing to them a more effective freedom." (004-009)
