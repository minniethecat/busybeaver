## Definition

Protectionism is the economic doctrine and policy that the state should intervene in international trade to shield domestic producers from foreign competition — principally through tariffs (taxes on imported goods), but also through quotas, subsidies, and regulatory barriers. The protective tariff raises the price of foreign goods, enabling domestic producers to charge higher prices than they could command in an open market, at the expense of consumers and of industries that rely on imported inputs.

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, the tariff was "not merely an obstruction to free enterprise, but a source of inequality as between trade and trade. Its fundamental effect is to transfer capital and labour from the objects on which they can be most profitably employed in a given locality, to objects on which they are less profitably employed, by endowing certain industries to the disadvantage of the general consumer" (002-017). The liberal attack on protection was therefore simultaneously an attack on an obstruction to economic efficiency and on an inequality — a state-conferred privilege for the few at the expense of the many.

## Protection vs. Protective Legislation

A persistent criticism of the Liberal position — [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] calls it "a very shallow wit" — was that it was inconsistent to oppose economic protection while supporting protective legislation for workers, such as factory acts, minimum wage laws, and trade union rights. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] rejects this charge decisively:

> The two things have nothing in common but that they are restraints intended to operate in the interests of somebody. The one is a restraint which, in the Liberal view, would operate in favour of certain industries and interests to the prejudice of others, and, on the whole, in favour of those who are already more fortunately placed and against the poorer classes. The other is a restraint conceived in the interest primarily of the poorer classes with the object of securing to them a more effective freedom.
> (004-008)

This distinction — between restraints that entrench privilege and restraints that expand [[positive-liberty|effective liberty]] — is fundamental to [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s social liberalism. Protectionism is condemned not because all state intervention is wrong, but because this particular intervention redistributes resources upward, from consumers and workers to protected producers and landowners.

## The Liberal Attack on Protection

The campaign against protection was one of the defining causes of nineteenth-century British liberalism. The [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], founded in 1838 by [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], mobilised a mass movement against the tariffs on imported grain that raised the price of bread for working families while enriching the landowning class. The League's success — culminating in the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 — established [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as a central plank of the Liberal platform and split the [[tory-party|Conservative Party]], whose leader [[robert-peel|Sir Robert Peel]] broke with his own party to carry repeal.

"In most countries," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes, "the attack has succeeded" — the liberal movement swept away not only the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] but the entire edifice of the old protective system, including the Navigation Laws that had restricted shipping to British vessels (002-017). Free trade became, for a generation, the settled consensus of British politics, and the prosperity of the mid-Victorian era was widely attributed to it.

## The Tariff Reform Challenge

This consensus was challenged at the turn of the twentieth century by [[joseph-chamberlain|Joseph Chamberlain]]'s campaign for Tariff Reform — a system of "imperial preference" that would impose tariffs on goods from outside the British [[imperialism|Empire]] while allowing [[economic-liberty|free trade]] within it. [[joseph-chamberlain|Chamberlain]], who had broken with [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] over [[ireland|Irish Home Rule]] in 1886, argued that protection would strengthen British industry against German and [[united-states|America]]n competition and bind the [[imperialism|Empire]] together economically.

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], writing in 1911, the Tariff Reform movement represented a direct threat to the liberal achievement. He noted with alarm that "in [[england|England]] the Protectionist reaction would undoubtedly have gained at least a temporary victory but for our dependence on foreign countries for food" (002-017) — a reminder that [[economic-liberty|free trade]] was not a permanent settlement but a politically contested position requiring constant defence.

The electoral struggles over Tariff Reform — the Liberal landslide of 1906, fought largely on the [[economic-liberty|free trade]] issue, and the constitutional crisis of 1909–1911 over Lloyd George's "People's Budget" — demonstrated that protectionism remained a live political force. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw in the Tariff Reform campaign an alliance between "plutocratic imperialism" and protectionist [[political-economy|economics]], a combination he regarded as the antithesis of genuine liberalism (009-001).

## In Liberalism

Protectionism serves in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s argument as both a historical antagonist and a recurring temptation. Historically, the triumph of [[economic-liberty|free trade]] over protection was one of liberalism's greatest victories — a demonstration that organised public opinion, mobilised around a clear principle, could defeat entrenched economic interests. But protectionism also represents a permanent danger: the coalition of "the great commercial interests" seeking favourable treatment from the state, imperialists seeking to bind the [[imperialism|Empire]] through tariffs, and nationalist sentiment hostile to international economic integration (009-001).

The challenge for liberalism, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s view, is to hold the line on [[economic-liberty|free trade]] while developing the positive case for social [[social-reform|reform]] — not as a retreat from liberal principles but as their extension. Protectionism is wrong not because the state should never intervene in the economy but because this particular intervention serves privilege rather than freedom (004-008).
