## Definition and Philosophical Foundation

The common good is the ethical and political principle that individual well-being cannot be conceived in isolation from the well-being of the community. In the organic conception of society developed by [[thomas-hill-green|T. H. Green]] and elaborated by [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L. T. Hobhouse]], "the life of the individual in turn would be something utterly different if he could be separated from society." (006-007) On this view, rights and duties are not pre-social or natural but are defined by their contribution to the shared life of the community: "His rights and his duties are alike defined by the common good." (006-008)

## Rights and the Common Good

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] developed a distinctive theory of rights that rooted them firmly in the common good rather than in abstract individual claims. "An individual right, then, cannot conflict with the common good, nor could any right exist apart from the common good." (006-008) A right was not a mere assertion of personal desire — "a mere claim is nothing. I might claim anything and everything" — but was valid only when it could be justified from an impartial standpoint that took "the good of everyone affected into account." (006-008) This provided the theoretical foundation for a liberalism that could endorse State intervention to secure the conditions of individual flourishing, since the right to liberty itself was grounded in its contribution to the common life.

## The Organic and Harmonic Conception

The organic view of society, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explained it, did not subordinate the individual to an abstract collective. "Society consists wholly of persons. It has no distinct personality separate from and superior to those of its members." (006-008) The common good was "a good in which each man has a share," consisting in the realisation of each person's "capacities of feeling, of loving, of mental and physical energy." (006-008) [[thomas-hill-green|Green]]'s phrase — that the individual "finds his own good in the common good" — captured the idea that genuine self-realisation was possible only through participation in a shared social life. (006-008)

## The Democratic Principle

The common good provided the philosophical justification for democracy. Because the common good could only be fully realised through "the common will" — the active participation of all citizens — democratic self-government was not merely a device for protecting individual interests but was integral to the moral purpose of society. "To make the rights and responsibilities of citizens real and living, and to extend them as widely as the conditions of society allow, is thus an integral part of the organic conception of society." (006-013) This also grounded [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s defence of [[self-determination|national self-determination]]: "[[nation|Nation]]al and [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]] are growths of the same root, and their historic connection rests on no accident, but on ultimate identity of idea." (006-013)

## French Revolutionary Roots

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] traced the principle of the common good to the Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man and of the Citizen (1789), which declared that "Social distinctions can only be founded on common utility." (003-011) This clause, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s reading, already contained the seed of the organic conception — the idea that inequality could be justified only by reference to the benefit of all, not by the private interest of the privileged. The [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite tradition elaborated this insight into a systematic principle of social reconstruction: "[[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] would frankly make all rights dependent on common utility, and therewith he would make it possible to examine all conflicting claims in the light of a general principle." (003-023)

## Relation to Liberty and Equality

Under the harmonic conception, equality and liberty were neither opposed nor independent but were both derived from the common good. [[equality|Equality]] required that "whatever inequality of actual treatment, of income, rank, office, consideration, there be in a good social system, it would rest, not on the interest of the favoured individual as such, but on the common good." (006-010) [[liberty|Liberty]], meanwhile, was essential because the common good was realised through the free development of personality — "the awakening of the imagination, the play of affection and passion, the strengthening and extension of rational control." (006-010) Restraint was justified only as a means of enlarging the sphere of genuine freedom for all.
