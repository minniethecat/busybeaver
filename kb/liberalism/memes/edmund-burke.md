Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Anglo-Irish statesman, parliamentarian, and political philosopher. A member of the [[whig-party|Whig Party]], Burke served in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] and was known for his support of the [[united-states|America]]n colonies' grievances, his prosecution of Warren Hastings, and his fierce opposition to the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]].

## Thought and Contributions

Burke's political philosophy rested on the principle of **prescription** — the idea that rights and institutions derive their legitimacy from long usage and inherited tradition, not from abstract rational principles. In his _Reflections on the Revolution in [[france|France]]_ (1790), Burke argued that the revolutionaries' attempt to rebuild society from first principles would end in tyranny. He contended that the "partnership" of society is between "those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born" — an organic conception that influenced later conservative thought.

Burke's critique of revolutionary rationalism made him the intellectual father of modern conservatism, but he remained a [[whig-party|Whig]] [[social-reform|reform]]er in his own terms. He supported Catholic emancipation, opposed the slave trade, and believed in cautious, evolutionary [[social-reform|reform]] rather than radical rupture.

## In Liberalism

Burke appears in the liberal tradition largely as an antagonist — the conservative foil against which progressive liberals defined themselves. [[thomas-paine]] wrote _[[rights|Rights]] of Man_ (1791) as a direct rebuttal to Burke's _Reflections_, defending popular sovereignty, representative government, and [[welfare-state|social welfare]] against Burke's traditionalism. Unlike [[thomas-paine|Paine]], who argued that rights were universal and inherent in human nature, Burke insisted that rights were inherited through national tradition — a gift of English history rather than the birthright of every human being.

This [[thomas-paine|Paine]]–Burke debate established a fault line that runs through modern politics: the tension between universal rights based on reason and particular rights rooted in historical experience. [[liberalism|Liberalism]], broadly, took [[thomas-paine|Paine]]'s side — but Burke's emphasis on social continuity and the limits of rational design has periodically resurfaced within the liberal tradition itself.
