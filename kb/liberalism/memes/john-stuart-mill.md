## Biography

John Stuart Mill was born in [[london|London]] in 1806, the eldest son of the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] philosopher [[james-mill|James Mill]], who subjected him to an extraordinarily rigorous education from infancy. By age eight he had read Herodotus, Plato, and Xenophon in Greek; by twelve he had mastered logic, political economy, and Latin literature.

Mill's intellectual trajectory was profoundly shaped by a mental crisis in his early twenties — a period of depression that led him to question the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] philosophy in which he had been raised. He emerged from this crisis with a richer, more humane liberalism that supplemented [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite rationalism with the emotional and aesthetic dimensions of human life cultivated by Wordsworth, Coleridge, and the Romantic tradition.

Mill served as a colonial administrator at [[india|India]] House (1823–1858) and was elected to [[parliament|Parliament]] in 1865 as a Liberal member for [[london|Westminster]]. His parliamentary career, though brief, was distinguished by his advocacy for women's suffrage, proportional representation, and the rights of working [[nation|people]]. He died in Avignon, [[france|France]], in 1873.

## Thought and Contributions

Mill stands, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s assessment, as one of "two great names" dominating [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]] from the mid-nineteenth century — "that of [[gladstone|Gladstone]] in the world of action, that of Mill in the world of thought" (005-001). His contribution was to deepen the philosophical foundations of liberalism while extending its practical application to new domains.

Mill's most original contribution lay in his reconception of liberty. He taught "that liberty is no mere formula of law, or of the restriction of law. There may be a tyranny of custom, a tyranny of opinion, even a tyranny of circumstance, as real as any tyranny of government and more pervasive" (006-001). This expansion of the concept of liberty beyond juridical bounds to encompass social and psychological dimensions was a decisive advance in liberal thought.

On representative government, Mill argued that the value of democratic participation lay not primarily in protecting individual interests but in developing individual character:

> If the adult citizen, male or female, has a right to vote, it is not so much as a means to the enforcement of his claims upon society, but rather as a means of enforcing his personal responsibility for the actions of the community.
> (005-007)

As a [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]], Mill faced a distinctive challenge in defending individual rights. Unwilling to appeal to [[human-rights|natural rights]] theory (which [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] had dismissed as "nonsense upon stilts"), "Mill cannot appeal to any [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]] that can be set in opposition to the public [[welfare-state|welfare]]. His method is to show that the permanent [[welfare-state|welfare]] of the public is bound up with the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]]" (005-004).

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] devotes much of Chapters V and VI to Mill's thought, treating him as the philosopher who brought liberalism closest to its mature form. Mill's teaching "brings us close to the heart of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" because it recognizes that liberty is not merely a legal formula but a condition for human development (006-001).

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is not uncritical. He notes that "so far as Mill rested his case on the distinction between self-regarding actions and actions that affect others, he was still dominated by the older individualism" (006-003). The task of the "new liberalism" that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] advocates is to preserve Mill's commitment to individual development while recognizing the inescapably social character of human life.

The foundation of Mill's conception of liberty, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] interprets it, is "the conception of thought as a growth dependent on spiritual laws, flourishing in the movement of ideas as guided by experience, reflection and feeling, corrupted by the intrusion of material considerations" (005-006). [[liberty|Liberty]], in this view, is not merely the absence of constraint but the enabling condition for the full development of human personality.
