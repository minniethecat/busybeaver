## Definition

Political liberty is the liberty of the citizen in relation to the government under which they live. It is the freedom that comes not merely from living under law, but from having a share in making and controlling that law. Where [[civil-liberties|juristic liberty]] secures the individual against arbitrary arrest, and fiscal liberty secures the individual against arbitrary taxation, [[political-freedom|political liberty]] secures the community against arbitrary rule altogether.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] traces the emergence of [[political-freedom|political liberty]] from the earlier struggles for civil and fiscal liberty, arguing that [[political-freedom|responsible government]] is the institutional expression of the principle that those who are governed must also govern.

## Historical Development

The demand for [[political-freedom|political liberty]] emerged gradually from the constitutional struggles of the seventeenth century. The English [[parliament|Parliament]]'s resistance to Stuart absolutism was directed initially at specific abuses — arbitrary taxation, imprisonment without trial — but the logic of resistance pointed inexorably towards popular control of the executive. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explains, the cry of "No taxation without representation" expressed a deeper truth:

> Hence, from the seventeenth century onwards, fiscal liberty was seen to involve what is called political liberty.
> (002-051)

The [[united-states|America]]n and [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]s of the late eighteenth century universalized this demand. Where English liberty had been the historic inheritance of a particular [[nation|people]], the [[united-states|America]]n Declaration of Independence and the French Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man asserted [[political-freedom|political liberty]] as a universal human right. The nineteenth century saw the gradual extension of the franchise across Europe and the [[united-states|America]]s, a process that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats as the working-out of principles implicit in the earliest liberal demands.

The [[reform|Reform]] Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884 in [[england|Britain]] extended the suffrage in stages, each extension being met with the argument that the newly enfranchised were not yet fit for the responsibility — an argument that [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] turned on its head by insisting that responsibility is learned only through its exercise.

## Key Arguments

1. **From fiscal to [[political-freedom|political liberty]]**: The demand that taxation require consent leads logically to the demand that government itself require consent. Arbitrary taxation and arbitrary legislation are two faces of the same despotic coin (002-051).

2. **Political liberty as a condition of all other liberties**: Without [[political-freedom|political liberty]], civil and personal liberties rest on the sufferance of the sovereign and may be withdrawn at any moment. The reign of law, the first condition of liberty, is secure only when those who make the law are accountable to those who live under it (002-059).

3. **The franchise as education in responsibility**: [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] argued that the right to vote is not primarily a means of enforcing one's claims upon society, but a means of enforcing one's personal responsibility for the actions of the community. [[democracy|Democracy]] is not merely a mechanism of consent but a school of character (005-244).

4. **The tyranny of the majority**: Political liberty contains an internal tension. Majority rule, without constitutional restraint, can become as oppressive as any despotism. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] recognized that "if personal liberty were fundamental it might only be imperilled by a so-called [[political-freedom|political liberty]] which would give to the majority unlimited powers of coercion" (005-244). The protection of minorities is therefore integral to true [[political-freedom|political liberty]].

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] devotes a full section of Chapter VI to "Political Liberty and [[popular-sovereignty|Popular Sovereignty]]," treating it as the capstone of the entire edifice of liberty. Having traced the movement from [[civil-liberties|civil liberty]] through personal, social, economic, and domestic liberty, he arrives at the question of who shall ultimately control the state that guarantees all these liberties:

> Underlying all these questions of right is the question how they are to be secured and maintained. By enforcing the responsibility of the executive and legislature to the community as a whole? Such is the general answer, and it indicates one of the lines of connection between the general theory of liberty and the doctrine of universal suffrage and the sovereignty of the people.
> (006-115)

He does not, however, treat popular sovereignty as a simple or self-validating principle. The [[nation|people]] may be "careless of their rights," "set on the conquest of others," or given to "any form of collective tyranny or folly" (006-115). The extension of the suffrage in 1884, he notes, may even have "tended for some years to arrest the development of liberty in various directions" (006-115). Political liberty is not merely a matter of counting heads but of creating the institutional and cultural conditions in which the majority governs with respect for the rights of all.

In [[gladstone|Gladstone]], [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] finds a statesman who trusted in "the recuperative force of political freedom" — the conviction that free institutions, even when they err, contain within themselves the resources for their own correction (005-234).
