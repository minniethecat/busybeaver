The American Revolution (1775–1783) was the political and military struggle by which thirteen British colonies in North [[united-states|America]] won their independence from the British Crown and established a republican form of government grounded in [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] principles. It was one of the foundational events in the history of modern liberalism — the first successful colonial revolution to produce a constitutional republic explicitly justified by the doctrine of [[natural-rights]].

## Causes and Ideological Roots

The Revolution drew deeply on the intellectual resources of the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]], particularly the English radical [[whig-party|Whig tradition]] and the French *philosophes*. [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine's]] pamphlet *Common Sense* (1776) translated complex philosophical arguments about [[popular-sovereignty]], [[social-contract|social contract]] theory, and the [[human-rights|rights of man]] into accessible prose that galvanized colonial opinion. [[thomas-paine|Paine]], an English radical who had emigrated to [[united-states|America]] only two years earlier, "gave the [[united-states|America]]n cause a universal significance" by framing it not as a parochial dispute over taxation but as a struggle for the [[human-rights|fundamental rights]] of all humanity (002-004).

The [[declaration-of-independence|Declaration of Independence]] (1776), drafted primarily by [[thomas-jefferson]], distilled these ideas into a powerful statement of liberal principles: "all men are created equal" and are "endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable [[rights|Rights]], that among these are Life, [[liberty|Liberty]] and the pursuit of Happiness." These words, which drew on [[john-locke|Locke's]] theory of [[human-rights|natural rights]] and the contractual basis of government, would reverberate through subsequent liberal and democratic movements across the world.

## Influence on British Liberalism

The American Revolution had a complex impact on British liberal thought. On the one hand, many British radicals and [[social-reform|reform]]ers — including [[charles-james-fox]], Richard Price, and the young [[jeremy-bentham]] — sympathized with the [[united-states|America]]n cause, seeing in it the vindication of principles they sought to advance at home: [[representative-government]], [[rule-of-law]], and the accountability of executive power to the legislature. Price famously declared that the American Revolution had "opened a new prospect in human affairs."

On the other hand, the Revolution exposed tensions within the liberal tradition. [[edmund-burke|Edmund Burke]], who supported the [[united-states|America]]n colonists' resistance to parliamentary overreach, nevertheless rejected the abstract rationalism of the Declaration of Independence's appeal to universal rights. This tension — between [[social-reform|reform]] rooted in historical precedent and [[social-reform|reform]] grounded in abstract principle — would define one of the central fault lines of subsequent liberal thought.

## The Constitutional Settlement

The constitutional settlement that followed the Revolution — the [[united-states-constitution|United States Constitution]] (ratified 1788) and the [[bill-of-rights]] (ratified 1791) — gave institutional form to the [[separation-of-powers]] doctrine articulated by [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]] and the principle of limited government under law. The [[united-states|America]]n experiment in republican self-government became, throughout the nineteenth century, a reference point for liberal [[social-reform|reform]]ers across Europe and Latin [[united-states|America]].

## Legacy

The American Revolution demonstrated that a [[nation|people]] could deliberately constitute its own government on liberal principles, breaking with the hereditary and customary basis of political authority that had characterized the European *ancien régime*. It inaugurated what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L.T. Hobhouse]] described as the modern period in which "society constituted on a thoroughly authoritarian basis" was slowly but surely giving way to "the new fabric of the civic State" (001-007).
