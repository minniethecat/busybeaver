## Nation and State: The Core Distinction

In Liberal thought, the nation is distinguished from the State. [[state|The State]] is a political and legal apparatus — the machinery of government, territory, and sovereignty. The nation is a human community bound by shared identity, history, language, or sentiment. The relationship between the two — what rights a nation possesses, how its boundaries are to be drawn, and when national self-government is justified — is one of the most difficult and persistent problems of Liberal politics. (002-023)

A great part of the liberating movement of the nineteenth century is occupied with the struggle of entire nations against alien rule: the revolt of Europe against [[napoleon|Napoleon]], the struggle of [[italy|Italy]] for freedom, the fate of the Christian subjects of Turkey, the emancipation of the negro, and the national movements in [[ireland|Ireland]] and in [[india|India]]. Many of these struggles present the problem of liberty in its simplest form — securing the most elementary rights for the weaker party. (002-023)

## The Nation as a Collective Life

The nation possesses a certain collective life and character. The British nation is a unity with a life of its own. But this unity is constituted by the interactions of individuals, not by something separate from and superior to them. Society consists wholly of persons and has no distinct personality separate from and superior to those of its members. (006-007)

This organic conception of the nation — neither a mere aggregation of individuals nor a mystical entity above them — is characteristic of the new [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. The nation is real, its common life is real, but its reality is constituted by the individuals who compose it and the relations among them.

## The Problem of Self-Determination

At the back of national movements, very difficult questions arise. What is a nation as distinct from a State? What sort of unity does it constitute, and what are its rights? If [[ireland|Ireland]] is a nation, is Ulster one? And if Ulster is a British and Protestant nation, what of the Catholic half of Ulster? (002-023)

History has in some cases given a practical answer. It has shown that, enjoying the gift of [[political-freedom|responsible government]], French and British populations, despite all historical quarrels and all differences of religious belief, language, and social structure, have fused into the nation of [[canada|Canada]]. History has justified the conviction that [[germany|Germany]] was a nation, and thrown ridicule on the contemptuous saying of Metternich that [[italy|Italy]] was a geographical expression. (002-023)

But how to anticipate history — what rights to concede to a people that claims to be a self-determining unit — is less easy to decide. There is no doubt that the general tendency of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] is to favour autonomy, but, faced with the problems of subdivision and the complexity of group with group, it has to rely on the concrete teaching of history and the practical insight of statesmanship to determine how the lines of autonomy are to be drawn. (002-023)

## The Empirical Test for National Autonomy

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] proposes one empirical test which seems generally applicable to the question of national self-government. Where a weaker nation incorporated with a larger or stronger one can be governed by ordinary law applicable to both parties to the union, and fulfilling all the ordinary principles of liberty, the arrangement may be the best for both parties. But where this system fails — where the government is constantly forced to resort to exceptional legislation or perhaps to de-liberalize its own institutions — the case becomes urgent. (002-023)

Under such conditions, the most liberally-minded democracy is maintaining a system which must undermine its own principles. The Assyrian conqueror, as [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]] remarks, who is depicted in the bas-reliefs leading his captive by a cord, is bound with that cord himself. He forfeits his liberty as long as he retains his power. (002-023)

## Popular Sovereignty and Individual Rights

A fundamental tension in Liberal thought concerns the relationship between the will of the nation and the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]]. Which is the ultimate authority — the will of the nation, or the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]]? If the nation deliberately decides on laws which deny the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]], ought such laws to be obeyed in the name of popular sovereignty, or to be disobeyed in the name of [[human-rights|natural rights]]? This is a real issue, and on these lines it is unfortunately quite possible for a democratic government to be tyrannical. (003-020)

This tension is at the heart of the distinction between [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and mere majoritarianism. The Liberal answer is that the will of the nation must be constrained by the rights of individuals — that democracy without liberty is tyranny by another name.

## Gladstone and the Principle of National Liberty

[[william-ewart-gladstone|William Ewart Gladstone]]'s most original work was done in the sphere of imperial relations. The maligned Majuba settlement after the First [[south-african-war|Boer War]] was an act of justice which came too late to effect a permanent undoing of mischief. All the greater was the courage of the statesman who could throw himself at that time upon the inherent force of national liberty and international fair dealing. In the case of [[ireland|Ireland]], [[gladstone|Gladstone]] again relied on the same principles. (005-002)

[[gladstone|Gladstone]] embodied the Liberal conviction that national aspirations, when they express the genuine sentiment of a people, carry a moral weight that mere force cannot permanently suppress. His approach to [[ireland|Ireland]], to [[south-africa|South Africa]], and to the broader questions of [[imperialism|Empire]] was guided by this faith in national liberty.

## Nationalism, Democracy, and Internationalism

The cause of democracy is bound up with that of internationalism. National pride, resentment, or ambition one day sweeps the public mind and diverts it from all interest in domestic progress. The next day the same function is performed no less adequately by a scare. The practice of playing on popular emotions has been reduced to a fine art which neither party scruples to employ. (009-012)

Standing by national autonomy and international equality, [[liberalism|Liberalism]] is necessarily in conflict with the Imperial idea as it is ordinarily presented. This is not to say that it is indifferent to the interests of [[british-empire|the Empire]] as a whole, to the sentiment of unity pervading its white population, or to all the possibilities involved in the bare fact that a fourth part of the human race recognizes one flag and one sovereign. (009-013)

## Nation and Empire

The Liberal attitude toward [[imperialism|Empire]] is governed by the same principles that govern its attitude toward the nation. Where Imperial rule secures peace, order, and the conditions of liberty for subject peoples, it may be defended on Liberal grounds. But where it becomes a system of domination that denies self-government to peoples capable of exercising it, it violates the very principles upon which [[liberalism|Liberalism]] rests. The extension of [[political-freedom|responsible government]] to the settler colonies — [[canada|Canada]], Australia, New Zealand, [[south-africa|South Africa]] — was the practical vindication of this principle. (009-012) (009-014)
