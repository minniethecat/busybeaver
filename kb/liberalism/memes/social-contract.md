## Definition and Background

The social contract is a theory of political obligation holding that legitimate government arises from a voluntary agreement — or "compact" — among free and equal individuals. In entering into this compact, each person surrenders a portion of their natural liberty in exchange for the security of [[human-rights|civil rights]] guaranteed by the collective power of the community. The theory provided the intellectual foundation for classical Liberal resistance to authoritarian government and for the doctrine of limited, consent-based rule. (003-006)

The contractarian framework was developed most influentially by [[Thomas Hobbes]] (in an absolutist form), [[John Locke]] (in a constitutional and rights-preserving form), and later [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]], who transformed the idea into the doctrine of the [[general-will]]. In the Liberal tradition, it was [[john-locke|Locke]]'s version — emphasising the preservation of [[natural-rights]] and the limited mandate of government — that proved most enduring.

## Core Principles

According to the social contract conception, political institutions are a restraint to which men voluntarily submit for specific, limited purposes. Before and behind the State stands the assemblage of free and equal individuals. The individual, though powerless in isolation, enters into an arrangement with others for the mutual respect of rights, instituting government to maintain those rights within the community and to guard against external assault. (003-006)

The logic of the contract sets a firm boundary on state action. [[state|Government]]'s function is "limited and definable": to maintain the [[human-rights|natural rights]] of man as accurately as the conditions of society allow, and to do nothing beside. Any further action employing the compulsory power of the State constitutes an infringement of the understanding on which government rests. As [[Thomas Paine]] expressed it, a civil right is a natural right exchanged. (003-006) In surrendering [[human-rights|natural rights]], the individual receives in return [[human-rights|civil rights]] — something less complete, perhaps, but more effective as resting on the guarantee of the collective power.

## Influence on Liberal Thought

This conception of the relations between the State and the individual long outlived the speculative theory on which it rested. It underlies the entire teaching of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] of [[free-trade]] advocacy. Its spirit was absorbed by many of the [[Utilitarianism|Utilitarians]], including [[Jeremy Bentham]] and [[James Mill]], who nonetheless rejected the language of [[human-rights|natural rights]] in favour of the principle of utility. It operated, though in diminishing force, throughout the nineteenth century and was strongly held by later continental [[liberal-party|Liberals]] who frankly abrogated its speculative foundations while resting their case on social utility. (003-007)

The earlier [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had to deal with authoritarian government in Church and State. It had to vindicate the elements of personal, civil, and [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]], and in so doing it took its stand on the [[human-rights|rights of man]]. (003-005) The contract theory gave this stand a coherent philosophical architecture: if government is a compact, then its authority is delegated, not inherent; conditional, not absolute.

## Limitations and Evolution

The strength of the contract theory lay not in its logical principles — the historical fiction of an original compact was widely criticised — but in the compactness and consistency which it gave to a view of the functions of the State that responded to certain needs of modern society. As long as those needs were uppermost, the theory was of living value. In proportion as they have been satisfied and other needs have emerged, the requirement has arisen for a fuller and sounder principle. (003-007)

By the late nineteenth century, thinkers like [[T.H. Green]] and [[Leonard Trelawny Hobhouse]] sought to move beyond the narrowly contractual view of the State. They retained the contract's insistence on consent and rights but embedded the individual within a social organism, arguing that rights are not merely pre-political possessions but are sustained and developed through the common life of society. The [[general-will]], in this revised understanding, was not a sum of individual preferences but a rational principle of the common good.
