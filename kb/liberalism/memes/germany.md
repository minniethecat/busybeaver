# Germany

Germany served as both an inspiration and a cautionary example for nineteenth-century Liberal thought. History justified the conviction that Germany was a nation, and this conviction threw ridicule on dismissive assessments such as Metternich's saying that [[italy|Italy]] was merely a geographical expression (002-023). The unification of the German states demonstrated the force of the principle of [[national-self-determination]]. Yet the militaristic and authoritarian character of the new German Empire also presented a challenge to Liberal internationalism, representing the kind of state that placed order and power above freedom.

## The German States Before Unification

Before 1871, the German-speaking lands were a patchwork of kingdoms, principalities, duchies, and free cities loosely organised into the German Confederation after the Congress of Vienna (1815). [[prussia|Prussia]] and [[austria|Austria]] competed for dominance. The revolutions of 1848 saw liberal and nationalist aspirations briefly converge in the Frankfurt [[parliament|Parliament]], which offered the German crown to the King of Prussia — an offer he contemptuously refused, dealing a lasting blow to German liberalism.

## Unification Under Prussia

Under [[otto-von-bismarck|Bismarck]]'s leadership, Prussia unified Germany through three wars: against Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), and [[france|France]] (1870–71). The resulting German Empire, proclaimed at Versailles in 1871, was a federal monarchy under Prussian hegemony. It combined modern economic dynamism with authoritarian political structures — universal [[universal-suffrage|manhood suffrage]] for the Reichstag coexisted with a government responsible to the Kaiser rather than to parliament.

## Germany and Liberalism

### The Nationalist Inspiration

For many nineteenth-century [[liberal-party|Liberals]], Germany's unification vindicated the principle that nations had a right to self-government. [[john-stuart-mill]] and other Liberal thinkers had long argued that national identity was a legitimate basis for political community, and the emergence of a unified German state seemed to confirm this belief.

### The Authoritarian Challenge

The success of Prussian militarism, however, troubled [[liberal-party|Liberals]] who valued [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] and [[internationalism|international cooperation]]. The German Empire became the exemplar of state-directed economic development, compulsory education, and [[welfare-state|social insurance]] — achievements that impressed British social [[social-reform|reform]]ers but were achieved within a framework that subordinated individual rights to state power. This tension between German efficiency and German authoritarianism would shape Liberal attitudes towards [[state-intervention|state action]] for decades.

## Germany in Hobhouse's Liberalism

In the source text, Germany appears as a dual symbol: proof that the principle of [[nation|nationality]] was a progressive force in history, but also evidence that nationalism untempered by liberal values could produce militarism and imperial rivalry. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s defence of [[internationalism|international order]] and his critique of [[imperialism]] were shaped by the German example.
