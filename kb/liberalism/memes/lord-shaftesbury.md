## The Paternalist Reformer

Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 7th Earl of Shaftesbury, was the most prominent social [[social-reform|reform]]er in nineteenth-century [[england|Britain]] and the driving parliamentary force behind the [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]] that progressively restricted the hours and conditions of industrial labour, particularly for women and children. His motivation was religious rather than liberal: an evangelical conviction that the State bore a moral responsibility to protect the weak from the strong. In the framework of [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]*, Shaftesbury occupies a crucial analytical position — he is the figure who demonstrates that the movement toward social control of industry did not originate within the liberal tradition but was, at its inception, "in accordance with the best traditions of paternal rule" (004-005).

## The Shaftesbury Paradox: Paternalism in Service of Liberty

The [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]] present what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] regarded as the pivotal case study in liberalism's evolution. Factory legislation "enjoyed a large measure of Conservative support" and "commended itself to the religious convictions of men of whom Lord Shaftesbury was the typical example" (004-005). Meanwhile, the legislation was "bitterly opposed by [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and [[john-bright|Bright]]" — the very men who were liberalism's most eloquent champions of [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] (004-006). This apparent paradox — [[tory-party|Conservatives]] expanding state power while liberals defended laissez-faire — forced a fundamental reconsideration of what liberty meant. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argued that "as experience ripened, the implications of the new legislation became clearer, and men came to see that by industrial control they were not destroying liberty but confirming it" (004-005). A new and more concrete conception of liberty emerged, one in which the State's role was not merely to refrain from interference but to create the conditions in which genuine freedom could be exercised.

## The Cleavage Across Party Lines

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], the alignment around [[factory-act|factory legislation]] was analytically decisive because it demonstrated that "the cleavage of opinion cut across the ordinary divisions of party" (004-005). [[radicalism|Radicals]] like J. Cam [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] ([[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L. T. Hobhouse]]'s own ancestor) took a leading part in the earlier legislation, while [[whig-party|Whig]] [[state|Government]]s passed the important Acts of 1833 and 1847. Shaftesbury's career thus illustrates a deeper argument of [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]*: that the history of liberty is not the story of any single party or doctrine, but the working-out of a principle that repeatedly compelled both its friends and its opponents toward positions they had not anticipated.

## Legacy

Shaftesbury's legacy is ambiguous from a liberal standpoint. His paternalism rested on premises — hierarchy, religious obligation, the duties of the strong toward the weak — that are in tension with liberal commitments to equality and autonomy. Yet his practical achievements — the restriction of child labour, the regulation of mines, the improvement of working conditions — contributed to the material conditions without which liberty for the industrial working class was an empty promise. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s treatment of him exemplifies the generous eclecticism of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]]: the willingness to recognise that "a new and more concrete conception of liberty" (004-005) could emerge from sources outside the liberal tradition itself.
