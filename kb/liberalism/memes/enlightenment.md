## Definition

The Enlightenment was the intellectual movement of the eighteenth century that elevated reason to the status of the supreme tribunal before which all claims to authority — political, religious, and intellectual — must justify themselves. Its animating conviction was that human beings, through the systematic exercise of their rational faculties, could understand the world, improve their condition, and achieve a measure of mastery over their own affairs that had been denied them by superstition, tradition, and arbitrary power.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] draws on the Enlightenment's intellectual legacy at a crucial juncture in his argument, invoking the eighteenth-century theory of a "natural harmony" between individual interest and the [[common-good|general good]]:

> But there was another side to the theory of nature which we must not ignore. If in this theory government is the marplot and authority the source of oppression and stagnation, where are the springs of social order and progress?
> (003-008)

This natural harmony thesis — the idea that free individuals pursuing their own ends would, through a kind of pre-established harmony, produce socially beneficial outcomes — was one of the Enlightenment's most influential and most contested contributions to liberal thought.

## Historical Development

The Enlightenment was a pan-European phenomenon, with distinct national variants. In [[france|France]], the *philosophes* — Voltaire, Diderot, [[rousseau|Rousseau]], [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]] — subjected the *ancien régime* to merciless critique, exposing the ir[[reason|rationality]] of inherited privilege and the cruelty of a legal system built on torture and arbitrary detention. In Scotland, thinkers like David Hume and [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] developed a more empirically grounded, less revolutionary variant of Enlightenment thought, emphasizing the unintended emergence of social order from the interactions of individuals pursuing their own interests.

In [[germany|Germany]], Immanuel Kant's essay "What Is Enlightenment?" (1784) gave the movement its canonical definition: "Enlightenment is man's emergence from his self-incurred immaturity" — the courage to use one's own understanding without the guidance of another. Kant's emphasis on *autonomy* — self-legislation — became a foundational concept for subsequent liberal thought.

The Enlightenment's political legacy was complex. The [[united-states|America]]n and [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]s both drew heavily on Enlightenment ideas, but they drew different lessons from them. The [[united-states|America]]n founders emphasized constitutional [[separation-of-powers|checks and balances]] — an Enlightenment of institutional design. The French revolutionaries, particularly in the Jacobin phase, emphasized the general will and the transformation of society — an Enlightenment of political will.

## Key Arguments

From this diverse intellectual movement, three core arguments emerge that remain central to the liberal tradition:

1. **[[reason|Reason]] against tradition**: The Enlightenment challenged the authority of tradition, revelation, and inherited status, insisting that institutions and beliefs must earn their legitimacy through rational justification. This critical stance remains central to the liberal temperament.

2. **The natural harmony thesis**: The claim that free individuals pursuing their own interests would spontaneously produce social goods was both enormously influential and, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues, ultimately untenable in its strong form. It "assumed a world that has never existed" — a world without concentrations of economic power, asymmetries of information, or the legacy of past injustice.

3. **Autonomy as the core of enlightenment**: Kant's conception of autonomy — the capacity to give oneself the law by which one lives — provided liberalism with a deeper philosophical foundation than the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] calculus of pleasure and pain. To be free is not merely to satisfy one's desires but to be the author of the principles that govern one's conduct.

:::info
**The Enlightenment and Religion**: Contrary to popular caricature, the Enlightenment was not uniformly hostile to religion. While figures like Voltaire and Diderot were fiercely anticlerical, others — [[john-locke|Locke]], Kant, and the Scottish Enlightenment thinkers — sought to reconcile reason with faith, arguing that religious belief, properly understood, was compatible with, and even required by, rational inquiry.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] engages the Enlightenment primarily through its theory of natural harmony, which he treats in Chapter III as a pivotal moment in the "movement of theory." The eighteenth-century thinkers, he argues, made an indispensable contribution by insisting that social order could be understood in naturalistic terms — as the product of human action rather than divine or royal decree (003-008).

Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is also a critic of the Enlightenment's more naive formulations. The idea that the unregulated pursuit of private interest would reliably produce public benefit was, he argues, refuted by the experience of the Industrial Revolution, which demonstrated that freedom without institutional safeguards could produce new forms of domination more oppressive than the old.

The liberal task, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] conceives it, is to preserve the Enlightenment's commitment to reason, freedom, and progress while abandoning the metaphysical assumptions — the "pre-established harmony" — that made the early liberal case for laissez-faire seem plausible. In this, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is the heir of the Enlightenment's critical spirit even when he rejects its specific economic doctrines.
