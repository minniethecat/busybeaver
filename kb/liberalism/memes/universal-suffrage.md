Universal suffrage is the principle that all adult citizens of a political community should possess the right to vote, without restriction based on property, income, sex, or race. It emerged as a central demand of democratic and liberal movements from the late eighteenth century onward and became the institutional expression of the broader idea of [[popular-sovereignty|popular sovereignty]].

## Development

The movement for universal suffrage unfolded gradually across the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. In [[england|Britain]], the process began with the [[reform|Great Reform Act of 1832]], which extended the vote to the middle classes, and continued through the [[reform|Reform]] Acts of 1867 and 1884. Full manhood suffrage was not achieved until 1918, when it was combined with the first extension of the vote to women over thirty. Equal suffrage for all adults over twenty-one was completed in 1928.

In Continental Europe, universal manhood suffrage was often introduced more abruptly — sometimes by revolutionary upheaval, sometimes by authoritarian governments seeking popular legitimacy — as in [[otto-von-bismarck|Bismarck]]'s [[germany|Germany]], where universal male suffrage for the Reichstag was enacted in 1871.

## In Liberalism

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s _[[liberalism|Liberalism]]_, universal suffrage is presented as the natural institutional corollary of popular sovereignty. "The problem of popular sovereignty pointed to the same need" (002-029) — the need for every citizen to have a voice in the laws that governed them. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], following [[john-stuart-mill]], argued that the right to vote was not merely an expression of sovereign will but a means to individual development. Political participation educated the citizen in the responsibilities of freedom, connecting "the general theory of liberty and the doctrine of universal suffrage and the [[popular-sovereignty|sovereignty of the people]]" (002-028).

This developmental justification distinguished liberal advocacy of universal suffrage from purely majoritarian arguments. For liberals in [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s tradition, the vote was a form of civic education — a way of drawing individuals into the deliberative life of the community — rather than simply a mechanism for aggregating preferences.
