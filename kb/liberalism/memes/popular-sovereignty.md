## The Doctrine of Popular Sovereignty

The doctrine of popular sovereignty rests on two principles: first, that government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed; second, that the [[nation|people]] collectively possess the right to determine the form and direction of their political institutions. In its classic formulation, "the doctrine of popular sovereignty rests on two principles. (1) It is said to reside in the nation." (003-019) This idea, developed by thinkers from [[john-locke|Locke]] to [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], transformed the conception of political authority from a divine or hereditary endowment into a trust conferred by the community upon its representatives.

## Popular Sovereignty and Liberal Theory

For the liberal tradition, popular sovereignty presented both a foundation and a problem. On the one hand, it expressed the fundamental conviction that government must be accountable to the governed — expressed in the cry "No taxation without representation." (002-007) On the other hand, an unchecked popular will could threaten the very individual rights that liberalism existed to protect. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] framed the tension: "Is popular sovereignty a practicable basis of [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]], or does it open an avenue to the tyranny of the mob?" (007-001) The liberal answer was to embed popular sovereignty within a constitutional framework of individual rights, separation of powers, and the rule of law.

## Constitutional Limitations

The problem of reconciling popular rule with [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] was central to nineteenth-century liberal thought. "Suppose the nation deliberately decides on laws which deny the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]], ought such laws to be obeyed in the name of popular sovereignty, or to be disobeyed in the name of [[human-rights|natural rights]]?" (003-020) The liberal resolution was to deny that popular sovereignty was absolute: "Popular sovereignty is not fundamental, for all government is a means to an end." (003-025) That end — the protection and development of individual personality — set limits on what any majority, however large, could legitimately do. This constitutional liberalism distinguished itself both from absolutist democracy and from authoritarian rule.

## Connection to Universal Suffrage

Popular sovereignty pointed naturally toward universal suffrage as its institutional expression. "The problem of popular sovereignty pointed to the same need" (002-029) — the need for every citizen to have a voice in the laws that governed them. Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], following [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]], insisted that the right to vote was not merely an expression of sovereign will but a means to individual development. Political participation educated the citizen in the responsibilities of freedom, connecting "the general theory of liberty and the doctrine of universal suffrage and the sovereignty of the people." (002-028)
