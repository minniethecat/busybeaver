The Poor Law was the system of poverty relief operating in [[england|England]] and Wales, fundamentally [[social-reform|reform]]ed by the Poor Law Amendment Act of 1834. The system was based on the principle that the able-bodied poor should be relieved only within the workhouse, under conditions deliberately made less attractive than the lowest independent standard of living — the principle of "less eligibility" (008-035). The Poor Law became the central point of tension between older laissez-faire [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and the emerging social [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of the twentieth century.

## The Old Poor Law and Reform

Before 1834, the Poor Law operated through a decentralized system of parish relief, including "outdoor relief" (assistance provided outside institutions) and the Speenhamland system of wage supplements. Experience of the forty years before 1834 had demonstrated what came of a system which ignored economic conditions and relied on the simple machinery of the Poor Law to deal with poverty. The [[social-reform|reform]]ers of 1834 sought to create a system that would not undermine the incentive to work (008-035).

> Experience of the forty years before 1834 had taught us what came of free resort to public funds by way of subvention to inadequate wages. It meant simply that the standard of remuneration was lowered in proportion as men could rely on public aid to make good the deficiency, while at the same time the incentives to independent labour were weakened when the pauper stood on an equal footing with the hardworking man.
>
> (008-035)

## The New Poor Law and Its Critics

The [[social-reform|reform]]ed Poor Law was deeply unpopular among the working classes, becoming a symbol of the harshness of laissez-faire [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. The system treated poverty as an individual failing rather than a social condition. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted, no one could contend that the pauper child apprenticed by Poor Law guardians to a manufacturer had any say or could have any judgment as to the work they were set to do (004-023). The aim of every sincere friend of the poor, [[liberal-party|Liberals]] increasingly recognized, must be to keep them away from the Poor Law machine through preventive social [[social-reform|reform]] (008-035).

> No one could contend that the pauper child apprenticed by Poor Law guardians to a manufacturer had any say or could have any judgment as to the work which it was set to do. It had to be protected, and experience showed that it had to be protected by law.
>
> (004-023)

## The Principle of 1834 and Its Limitations

The leading "principle of 1834" was that the lot of the pauper should be made "less eligible" than that of the independent labourer. This meant that public relief, when available, would come at the cost of personal liberty — the workhouse test enforced a loss of freedom as the price of survival. The underlying legal theory recognized a right to the bare necessaries of life, but only at the point of [[poverty|destitution]] and only on terms set by the authorities (008-035).

> It was the leading "principle of 1834" that the lot of the pauper should be made "less eligible" than that of the independent labourer. Perhaps we may express the change of opinion which has come about in our day by saying that according to the newer principle the duty of society is rather to ensure that the lot of the independent labourer be more eligible than that of the pauper.
>
> (008-035)

## Liberal Reform and the Break with Poor Law Principles

The [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]]'s social [[social-reform|reform]]s of the early twentieth century — particularly the [[old-age-pensions|Old Age Pensions]] Act of 1908 — represented a decisive break with Poor Law principles. The new departure involved providing for needs outside the stigmatizing framework of the workhouse. The pension system threw over the test of [[poverty|destitution]]: it provided a certain minimum, a basis upon which independent thrift might hope to build up a sufficiency. It was not a narcotic but a stimulus to self-help and to friendly aid or filial support (008-035).

> The Poor Law already guaranteed the aged person and the poor in general against actual starvation. But the Poor Law came into operation only at the point of sheer destitution. It failed to help those who had helped themselves. ... The pension system throws over the test of destitution. It provides a certain minimum, a basis to go upon, a foundation upon which independent thrift may hope to build up a sufficiency.
>
> (008-035)

## From Poor Relief to Social Insurance

The movement for the break-up of the Poor Law represented a fundamental shift in the conception of social obligation. Instead of redeeming the destitute after they had fallen, the community should seek to render generally available the means of avoiding [[poverty|destitution]] — though in doing so it should uniformly call on the individual for a corresponding effort. Methods included supplying a basis for private effort (as with old age [[old-age-pensions|pensions]]), State-aided insurance for sickness and unemployment, and recognizing certain forms of socially valuable labour — such as motherhood — as worthy of public remuneration (008-035).

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] articulated the emerging principle: in a State as wealthy as the [[england|United Kingdom]], every citizen should have full means of earning by socially useful labour so much material support as experience proves to be the necessary basis of a healthy, civilized existence. If the industrial system did not in fact make these means sufficiently available, the citizen had a claim — not as of charity but as of right — on the national resources to make good the deficiency (008-035).

## Significance for Liberal Thought

The transformation of the Poor Law from a deterrent system of poor relief to a framework of [[welfare-state|social insurance]] marked a decisive evolution in Liberal thought. It reflected the shift from the negative conception of liberty as non-interference to the positive conception of liberty as the conditions for self-development. The Poor Law debates crystallized the question at the heart of modern [[liberalism|Liberalism]]: whether the State's role is merely to prevent harm, or whether it extends to securing the material conditions without which liberty itself is a hollow promise.
