## Overview

Nationalism, as understood within the liberal tradition, is the recognition that shared sentiment — rooted in kinship, language, history, and mode of life — constitutes a legitimate foundation for political community. Far from being a purely destructive or chauvinistic force, nationalism in its liberal expression is presented as a natural extension of the democratic principle: just as individual freedom requires self-government, so the healthiest political units are those to which [[nation|people]] are drawn by their own deepest feelings of belonging.

> Inasmuch as the true social harmony rests on feeling and makes use of all the natural ties of kinship, of neighbourliness, of congruity of character and belief, and of language and mode of life, the best, healthiest, and most vigorous political unit is that to which men are by their own feelings strongly drawn. (009-004)

## The Justification of Nationalism

### National and Personal Freedom

The text grounds nationalism in an argument from organic social theory. The [[common good]] — the ethical aim of social life — can be fully realized only through the common will. This requires that citizens be "not only passive recipients but practical contributors" to the social order (009-004). The extension of rights and responsibilities to all citizens is thus an integral part of the organic conception of society, and simultaneously "the justification of nationalism so far as nationalism is founded on a true interpretation of history" (009-004).

> National and personal freedom are growths of the same root, and their historic connection rests on no accident, but on ultimate identity of idea. (009-004)

### The Peril of Disrupted Unity

If the natural congruence between political boundaries and national sentiment is the positive case for nationalism, the negative case rests on the harm caused when this unity is broken:

> Any breach of such unity, whether by forcible disruption or by compulsory inclusion in a larger society of alien sentiments and laws, tends to mutilate — or, at lowest, to cramp — the spontaneous development of social life. (009-004)

This principle has far-reaching implications for how liberalism approaches imperial governance, minority rights, and the constitutional structure of multi-national states.

## Nationalism and Internationalism

The liberal account of nationalism does not culminate in chauvinism or isolation. On the contrary, the text insists that democracy and internationalism are bound together:

> The cause of democracy is bound up with that of internationalism. The relation is many-sided. It is national pride, resentment, or ambition one day that sweeps the public mind and diverts it from all interest in domestic progress. The next day the same function is performed no less adequately by a scare. (009-006)

Military expenditure "eats up the public resources" that might otherwise fund social [[social-reform|reform]]. The old doctrine of absolute sovereignty is declared dead, replaced by a vision of "a complex system of government within government, authority limited by authority" (009-006).

### The World-State Vision

> The world-state of the not impossible future must be based on a free national self-direction as full and satisfying as that enjoyed by Canada or Australia within the British Empire at this moment. (009-006)

This is the liberal synthesis: national self-government and [[internationalism|international cooperation]] are not opposed but complementary. [[nation|Nation]]al loyalty, properly understood, expresses itself "less in the desire to extend territory or to count up ships and guns, and more in the endeavour to magnify the contribution of our own country to civilized life" (009-006).

## Democratic Conditions

The concluding statement of the chapter crystallizes the relationship:

> Internationalism on the one side, national self-government on the other, are the radical conditions of the growth of a social mind which is the essence, as opposed to the form, of democracy. (009-008)

This formulation places nationalism and internationalism as twin pillars supporting the democratic ideal. Neither is sufficient alone: nationalism without internationalism degenerates into militarism and imperial rivalry; internationalism without national [[self-determination|self-direction]] becomes an empty, rootless abstraction.

## Tensions and Limitations

The liberal theory of nationalism faces several inherent tensions. First, the boundary between legitimate national sentiment and chauvinistic [[imperialism]] is not always clear in practice. Second, the assumption that political units should align with national feelings can, in ethnically mixed territories, generate as many problems as it solves. Third, the appeal to "natural ties" of kinship and language risks essentializing identity in ways that liberal individualism might resist.

The text acknowledges these difficulties implicitly by insisting that nationalism must be paired with internationalism — a recognition that the national principle, left unchecked, can become the very force of militarism and conquest that liberalism opposes.

## Legacy

The liberal account of nationalism articulated in this text represents an important strand of early twentieth-century progressive thought. It sought to reconcile the evident power of national feeling — which had reshaped the political map of Europe over the preceding century — with the universalist aspirations of liberal and democratic ideals. The synthesis it proposes — national self-government within a framework of [[internationalism|international cooperation]] — anticipates, in broad outline, the post-1945 international order of sovereign nation-states cooperating through multilateral institutions.
