## Overview

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, Russia functions as the essential counter-example to liberal governance — the modern European state where the principles of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] have made the least headway. While the Liberal movement was transforming the states of Western Europe, Russia remained the archetype of "arbitrary government" in the contemporary world (002-003). [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] invokes Russia not as a subject of detailed analysis but as a rhetorical pole: the autocratic extreme against which the liberal achievement can be measured. Even so, he acknowledges at the close of his survey that Liberal principles have begun to penetrate "the Russian empire" alongside "the vast continent of Asia" (002-029).

## Autocracy and Arbitrary Government

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s discussion of arbitrary government in the second chapter of _[[liberalism|Liberalism]]_ opens with a general principle — that the first liberty to be secured is the right to be dealt with in accordance with law — and then names Russia as its primary contemporary violator. He describes "punishment by 'administrative' process in Russia at the present day" as a modern equivalent of the _lettre de cachet_ under the French _ancien régime_ (002-003). This administrative punishment, operating outside the regular courts of law and depending on "will and pleasure" rather than defined offences and regular trial, represents the antithesis of the rule of law that the English [[parliament|Parliament]] had begun to vindicate in the seventeenth century through the [[Petition of Right]] and the [[habeas-corpus|Habeas Corpus Act]] (002-003).

The broader principle at stake is what [[john-locke|Locke]] called "a standing rule to live by, common to every one of that society and made by the legislative power erected in it" (002-003). Russia, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s framework, exemplifies the society that has not yet achieved even this first and most fundamental condition of liberty.

## The Military Spirit and Liberal Progress

Russia appears a second time in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s discussion of militarism and its threat to free institutions. "Not only may the military force be directly turned against liberty, as in Russia," he writes, "but there are more subtle ways, as in Western Europe, in which the military spirit eats into free institutions and absorbs the public resources which might go to the advancement of civilization" (002-026). Here Russia represents the direct, undisguised form of military tyranny — a warning of what happens when the armed power of the state is not subordinated to civil authority and the rule of law.

Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] does not write Russia off as permanently beyond the reach of Liberal ideas. In his summary survey of the Liberal movement's global reach, he places Russia on a spectrum of liberalization: "The modern State as we see it in Europe outside Russia, in the British colonies, in North and South [[united-states|America]], as we begin to see it in the Russian empire and throughout the vast continent of Asia, is the old authoritarian society modified in greater or less degree by the absorption of Liberal principles" (002-029). Russia is at the far end of this spectrum — the place where the absorption of Liberal principles is most incipient — but the qualification "as we begin to see it" suggests that even the Russian empire was not immune to the forces of liberalization in the early twentieth century.
