Sir Robert Peel (1788–1850) was a British statesman who served twice as Prime Minister and is remembered as the founder of the modern [[tory-party|Conservative Party]], the architect of the Metropolitan Police, and — most consequentially for the history of liberalism — the Prime Minister who forced the [[repeal-of-corn-laws|repeal of the Corn Laws]] through [[parliament|Parliament]] in 1846, splitting his own party in the process.

## Early Career and the Tamworth Manifesto

Peel entered politics as a Tory, serving as Chief Secretary for [[ireland|Ireland]] (1812–1818) and Home Secretary (1822–1827, 1828–1830). His most notable achievement as Home Secretary was the establishment of the Metropolitan Police Service in 1829 — "Peelers" or "Bobbies," as they came to be known — which created the model of a professional, civilian police force that was subsequently adopted throughout [[england|Britain]] and emulated abroad.

In the Tamworth Manifesto (1834), Peel articulated a new vision for the [[tory-party|Tory Party]], one that accepted the [[reform|Reform]] Act of 1832 while committing to "the correction of proved abuses and the redress of real grievances." This document is conventionally regarded as the founding charter of the modern [[tory-party|Conservative Party]], signalling a shift from reflexive resistance to change toward a pragmatic conservatism willing to undertake necessary [[social-reform|reform]]s.

## Repeal of the Corn Laws

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were tariffs on imported grain that raised the price of bread for working families while enriching the landowning agricultural interest. The [[anti-corn-law-league]], founded in 1838 by [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], mobilized a mass movement against them. The campaign for repeal "was one of the defining causes of nineteenth-century British liberalism," and its success established [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as a central plank of the Liberal platform (004-002).

Peel's decision to support repeal was driven by a combination of conviction and circumstance. Intellectually, he had been persuaded by the free-trade arguments of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]], [[john-bright|Bright]], and the classical economists. Politically, the onset of the Irish Potato Famine (1845) made the maintenance of food tariffs morally untenable. In 1846, Peel forced repeal through [[parliament|Parliament]] with the support of the [[whig-party|Whig]] opposition, breaking with the protectionist wing of his own party.

The political cost was immense. The [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] split between Peelites — who accepted [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and followed Peel — and the protectionist rump. "The [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] split over repeal, and Peel's political career was effectively ended," as the protectionist faction, led by [[benjamin-disraeli]] and Lord George Bentinck, exacted their revenge. Yet [[william-ewart-gladstone|William Ewart Gladstone]], who had begun his parliamentary career as a Tory under Peel's mentorship, was among the Peelites who would eventually migrate to the [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]], bringing Peel's commitments to [[economic-liberty|free trade]], financial rectitude, and administrative efficiency with them.

## Peel's Liberal Legacy

Although Peel was a Conservative and a Tory by party affiliation, his legacy had profound implications for British liberalism. The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] demonstrated that a statesman of conservative instincts could, when convinced of the justice of a cause, break with the interests of his own class and party to enact a [[social-reform|reform]] that benefited the public. "The League's victory in 1846, when Prime Minister Robert Peel split his own [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] to force repeal through [[parliament|Parliament]], demonstrated the growing political power of industrial capitalism and free-trade ideology" (004-003).

Peel's career illustrates the complex relationship between conservatism and liberalism in nineteenth-century [[england|Britain]]: a statesman who began as a defender of the established order ended by advancing one of the most important liberal economic [[social-reform|reform]]s of the century.
