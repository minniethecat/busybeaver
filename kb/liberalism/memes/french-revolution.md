## The Revolution and the Birth of Liberalism

The French Revolution stands as a watershed in the development of [[new-liberalism|modern Liberalism]]. It was the most dramatic assertion of the principles that had been developing through the eighteenth century: the [[human-rights|rights of man]], the [[popular-sovereignty|sovereignty of the people]], and the rejection of authoritarian and aristocratic government. The old economic regime had been in decay throughout the eighteenth century, and the Revolution represented both the culmination of that decay and the opening of a new era of political thought. (004-001)

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s account, the Revolution belongs to the broader "movement of liberation" that characterized the modern age — a movement that was religious, political, economic, social, and ethical in its protest against authoritarian order. (002-007)

## The Revolution's Ambiguous Legacy for Liberalism

The French Revolution presented [[liberalism|Liberalism]] with both inspiration and warning. On one hand, it embodied the principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity — the rejection of arbitrary power and hereditary privilege that stood at the core of the Liberal creed. The constructive genius of earlier political experiments was now translated into a universal language of rights.

On the other hand, the Revolution's descent into the Terror and its eventual culmination in [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic despotism illustrated the dangers of revolutionary upheaval. The revolt of Europe against [[napoleon|Napoleon]] became itself a liberating movement, but it was a reaction against a revolution that had betrayed its own principles. (002-023)

## Economic Dimensions

The divorce of the labourer from the land was complete by the time the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] was formed in the 1830s. The mass of the English peasantry were landless labourers working for a weekly wage of about ten or twelve shillings. The old economic regime had been in decay throughout the eighteenth century, and the French Revolution accelerated the transformation of economic relationships across Europe. (004-001) (004-002)

## The Revolution and Economic Liberalism

The French Revolution's relationship to economic [[liberalism|Liberalism]] was complex. The revolutionaries abolished feudal dues, guild restrictions, and internal trade barriers — measures that the Physiocrats and early economic [[liberal-party|Liberals]] had long advocated. The Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man (1789) included property among the "natural and [[human-rights|imprescriptible rights]] of man," reflecting the conviction, shared by [[liberal-party|Liberals]] across Europe, that security of property was an essential safeguard of [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]]. (003-004)

Yet the Revolution also demonstrated the limits of purely economic [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. The unrestrained operation of market forces during the revolutionary period produced severe hardship among the urban poor, and the revolutionary government's attempts to fix prices by decree showed that even the most doctrinaire regimes could not escape the demand for state intervention when masses faced starvation. This tension — between the principle of free contract and the demands of [[economic-justice|social justice]] — would remain central to Liberal thought throughout the nineteenth century. (004-004)

## Revolutionary vs. Evolutionary Change in Liberal Thought

The French Revolution forced [[liberal-party|Liberals]] to confront a fundamental question: could free institutions be created by a single act of will, or must they develop gradually? The English Liberal tradition, shaped by the settlement of 1688 and the [[reform|Reform]] Bills of the nineteenth century, tended toward the evolutionary answer. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] described the typical historical method as proceeding from "step to step" — each [[social-reform|reform]] building on precedent, each extension of the franchise flowing from proven capacity for self-government. (003-002)

The French experience, by contrast, suggested that the wholesale destruction of existing institutions could produce not liberty but new forms of despotism. This contrast between English gradualism and French revolutionism became a defining theme of Liberal political thought, influencing debates about [[social-reform|reform]] strategy throughout the nineteenth century. It also informed the Liberal critique of [[socialism|Socialism]], which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw as repeating the revolutionary error of seeking to remake society by a single comprehensive act. (008-028)

## Significance in Liberal Thought

For Liberal thinkers, the French Revolution raised fundamental questions that would shape political philosophy for generations: How can popular sovereignty be reconciled with [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]]? Can a revolution establish lasting free institutions? What is the relationship between [[political-freedom|political liberty]] and economic conditions? These questions formed the intellectual background against which figures like [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], [[richard-cobden|Cobden]], and [[gladstone|Gladstone]] worked out their versions of the Liberal project. (005-003) (005-004)

The Revolution also demonstrated that liberty implies control — that freedom is not simply the absence of restraint but requires institutional forms that channel popular will without destroying the rights of individuals and minorities. This insight would become central to the new [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. (008-027)
