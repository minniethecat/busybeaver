## Definition

Toleration, in the liberal tradition, is the principled refusal to use the coercive power of the state to enforce religious conformity. It rests on two distinct but complementary foundations: a scepticism about the state's competence to adjudicate theological truth, and a positive commitment to [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] of conscience as an inviolable sphere of personal sovereignty.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] situates [[religious-liberty|toleration]] within the broader framework of religious liberty, treating [[religious-liberty|freedom of worship]] as a natural extension of freedom of thought:

> What, then, is the primary meaning of religious liberty? Externally, I take it to include the liberties of thought and expression, and to add to these the right of worship in any form which does not infringe the liberty of others.
> (002-011)

Toleration is thus not merely the grudging acceptance of difference but an affirmative recognition that spiritual matters lie beyond the legitimate reach of political power.

## Historical Development

The demand for [[religious-liberty|toleration]] emerged from the religious conflicts that convulsed Europe in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The wars of religion demonstrated, with terrible clarity, that the attempt to impose religious uniformity by force was both morally abhorrent and practically self-defeating.

Early advocates of [[religious-liberty|toleration]] — from Sebastian Castellio to [[john-locke|John Locke]] — argued that religious belief cannot be compelled because genuine faith is, by its nature, a matter of inward conviction. [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) advanced the seminal argument that "the care of souls is not committed to the civil magistrate," drawing a bright line between the proper spheres of church and state.

In the nineteenth century, the liberal movement extended the principle from the specific question of religious pluralism to the broader domain of opinion and expression. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s *On [[liberty|Liberty]]* (1859) transformed [[religious-liberty|toleration]] from a practical accommodation into a positive argument that the collision of diverse opinions is essential to the discovery of truth and the vitality of society. This argument, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes, "brings us close to the heart of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" (006-001).

## Key Arguments

1. **The limits of state competence**: The state lacks both the epistemic capacity and the moral authority to adjudicate religious truth. Toleration is therefore a recognition of the state's proper boundaries, not merely a concession to diversity.

2. **[[liberty|Liberty]] of conscience as fundamental**: The right to worship according to one's own convictions — or not to worship at all — is among the most basic of human liberties. It follows from the liberal principle that each individual is the best judge of their own good.

3. **Toleration and social peace**: The historical alternative to [[religious-liberty|toleration]] is not religious uniformity but religious war. Toleration is a condition of civil peace in a pluralistic society.

:::info
**Toleration vs. [[religious-liberty|Religious Liberty]]**: In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s framework, [[religious-liberty|toleration]] is the negative principle (the state refrains from persecution), while religious liberty is the positive right (the individual is entitled to [[religious-liberty|freedom of worship]], expression, and association). The two are complementary but analytically distinct.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats religious liberty — of which [[religious-liberty|toleration]] is the institutional mechanism — as one of the essential "elements of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" laid out in Chapter II. He traces the genealogy of the concept through the struggle for the rights of free thought and expression, presenting it as inseparable from the broader liberal commitment to individual autonomy (002-011).

The argument is deepened in Chapter VI, where [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] engages [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s contention that liberty is "no mere formula of law" but rather a condition of human flourishing that depends on the free interplay of diverse perspectives (006-001). Toleration is thus not only a negative protection against persecution but a positive condition for intellectual and moral progress.
