## Definition

Secularism is the constitutional and political principle that the authority of the state derives from the consent of the governed rather than from divine sanction, and that governmental institutions should neither enforce nor privilege any particular religious doctrine. It is distinct from atheism or irreligion: a secular state is compatible with a religious citizenry, provided that religious allegiance does not determine civil status or political right.

In the Liberal tradition, secularism emerged as a response to the centuries-long entanglement of church and state in European politics — an entanglement that, in the view of Liberal thinkers, corrupted both religion and government by subordinating spiritual conscience to political convenience and political authority to ecclesiastical interest.

## Historical Development

The roots of secularism in Western thought extend to the classical world. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes, the ancient Greek city-state knew "no opposition of church and state, no fissure between political and religious life, between the claims of the secular and the spiritual" (001-012). Citizenship and religious observance were fused in a single identity. This fusion, while productive of communal solidarity in small republics, became a source of oppression when extended to the scale of empire.

The medieval doctrine of the "two swords" — the separation of spiritual and temporal authority between Pope and Emperor — introduced a distinction that would, in time, grow into a principle of separation. But it was the [[reform|Reform]]ation and the wars of religion that followed (1524–1648) that made secularism a practical political necessity. The [[peace|Peace]] of Westphalia (1648), by recognizing the right of princes to determine the religion of their territories, began the process of disentangling political sovereignty from papal authority.

The [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] contributed the philosophical foundations. [[john-locke|Locke's]] *Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]* (1689) argued that civil government has no competence over the care of souls, and that religious coercion is both ineffective and illegitimate. This argument was developed and radicalized by later thinkers, including [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], who defended liberty of thought and discussion as the necessary condition of all intellectual and moral progress.

## Key Arguments

1. **The ineffectiveness of religious coercion**: Genuine religious belief cannot be compelled. The state may enforce outward conformity, but it cannot produce inward conviction — and it is inward conviction, not outward compliance, that religion requires (008-240).

2. **The corruption of religion by power**: When the church relies on the state to enforce its doctrines, it ceases to rely on the spiritual authority that is its true foundation. The alliance of throne and altar weakens both.

3. **The educative function of the secular state**: [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] argued that public funds diverted from armaments should be devoted to "education — free, secular, and universal" (004-199). A secular education system, free of doctrinal control, was seen as the precondition for an informed citizenry capable of exercising democratic rights responsibly.

4. **Equal citizenship**: A state that privileges one religious confession necessarily disadvantages others. Secularism is, in this sense, the political expression of the Liberal commitment to equality before the law.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] does not treat secularism as a standalone theme, but the principle pervades his analysis of the Liberal tradition. His discussion of the authoritarian state — resting on "a certain union of military force and religious or quasi-religious belief" (001-010) — frames secularism as one of the defining achievements of the Liberal revolution against authority.

In the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite tradition, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] finds a particularly clear expression of the secular ideal. The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s programme of "free, secular, and universal" education (004-199) linked secularism to the broader Liberal project of emancipation: just as [[economic-liberty|free trade]] would liberate commerce from aristocratic monopoly, secular education would liberate the mind from ecclesiastical control.

The Liberal revival of the early twentieth century, which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] chronicles with guarded optimism, was in part a reaction against the Conservative project of re-establishing "the Church at home by the endowment of doctrinal education" (009-396). The [[education-act|Education Act]] of 1902, which provided public funds to church schools, galvanized Nonconformist opposition and reminded [[liberal-party|Liberals]] that the secular principle could never be taken as permanently secure.
