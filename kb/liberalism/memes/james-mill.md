James Mill (1773–1836) was a Scottish historian, economist, and philosopher closely associated with [[jeremy-bentham|Jeremy Bentham]] and the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] school. He developed the argument that [[popular-sovereignty|popular government]] was necessary because individuals, left unchecked, would govern in their own interest rather than for the good of the greatest number (003-026). [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] worked to reconcile self-interest with the [[common-good|general good]] through moral education, holding that [[nation|people]] trained from childhood to associate the [[welfare-state|welfare]] of others with their own would come to care for the happiness of all (003-029). He was the father of [[john-stuart-mill]].

## Utilitarian Argument for Popular Government

[[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] and James Mill reasoned that men, if left to themselves — that is, if neither trained by educational discipline nor checked by responsibility — do not consider the good of the greatest number but their own good. A king with unchecked power will rule in his own interest; a class with unchecked power will rule in its own interest. The only way to secure fair consideration for the happiness of all is to allow to all an equal share of power (003-026).

> As to popular government, Bentham and James Mill reasoned after this fashion. Men, if left to themselves, that is to say, if neither trained by an educational discipline nor checked by responsibility, do not consider the good of the greatest number. They consider their own good. A king, if his power is unchecked, will rule in his own interest. A class, if its power is unchecked, will rule in its own interest. The only way to secure fair consideration for the happiness of all is to allow to all an equal share of power.
>
> (003-026)

Crucially, this argument did not rest on any inherent right of the individual to take part in government. It rested on the claim of each person to be considered in the distribution of the means of happiness, with participation in government serving as a means to that end. If one man or one class could be shown to be so much wiser and better than others that their rule would conduce more to the happiness of the greater number, then on purely [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] grounds government should be entrusted to that man or class (003-026).

## Moral Education and the General Good

[[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] devoted particular attention to the problem of reconciling self-interest with the [[common-good|general good]] — a tension at the heart of [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] ethics. His solution lay in moral education: trained from childhood to associate the good of others with his own, a man would come to care for the happiness of others as for the happiness of self. In the long run, [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] held, the two things were coincident (003-029).

> Men like James Mill, who especially worked at this side of the problem, held that they could be overcome by moral education. Trained from childhood to associate the good of others with his own, a man would come, he thought, to care for the happiness of others as for the happiness of self. For, in the long run, the two things were coincident.
>
> (003-029)

This moral-educational project was grounded in associationist psychology, which [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] developed systematically in his *Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind*. By building the right associations from childhood, the individual could be shaped into a being whose personal happiness was inseparable from the [[welfare-state|welfare]] of the community.

## Influence and Legacy

James Mill's political thought provided a crucial bridge between [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s philosophical radicalism and the more developed [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of his son, [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]]. While the younger [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] departed from his father's strict [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]]ism — expanding its conception of happiness and defending [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] on grounds beyond mere expediency — he never definitively abandoned the first principles he inherited. The elder [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s insistence on [[popular-sovereignty|popular government]] as a safeguard against class interest, and his faith in education as the instrument of moral progress, remained foundational commitments of the Liberal tradition.
