## Biography

William Ewart Gladstone was born on 29 December 1809 in Liverpool, the son of a wealthy merchant. Educated at Eton and Christ Church, Oxford, he entered [[parliament|Parliament]] in 1832 as a Tory. His early career was shaped by his deep Anglican piety and a conservative temperament, but his intellectual and political trajectory would carry him steadily toward liberalism.

The decisive break came in 1846, when [[gladstone|Gladstone]] parted from his old Conservative traditions under the leadership of [[robert-peel|Sir Robert Peel]] on the question of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] (005-001). The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] forged the Peelite faction, and for [[gladstone|Gladstone]] it inaugurated a decades-long commitment to the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite policy of financial emancipation — reducing tariffs, abolishing duties on knowledge, and establishing the fiscal foundations of a liberal economy.

[[gladstone|Gladstone]] served as Chancellor of the Exchequer four times and as Prime Minister on four separate occasions (1868–1874, 1880–1885, 1886, 1892–1894). He died at Hawarden on 19 May 1898, having dominated the public life of Victorian [[england|Britain]] for over half a century. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] sums up his character: he had "a passion for justice in the concrete which was capable of carrying him far" and "knew tyranny when he saw it, and upon it he waged unremitting and many-sided war" (005-001).

## Political Career and Domestic Reform

[[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s domestic legacy rests on three pillars: financial [[social-reform|reform]], the extension of the suffrage, and Irish land [[social-reform|reform]]. As Chancellor, he pursued a rigorous programme of retrenchment and [[economic-liberty|free trade]]. His active intervention in 1859–60 saved the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] from a humiliating surrender to the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] and "secured its financial supremacy unimpaired until 1909" (005-001). The budget of 1860, which abolished paper duties and made the press more widely accessible, was a landmark in democratic communication.

On the franchise, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] stood for the extension of the suffrage, and "it was his [[state|Government]] which, in 1884, carried the extension of the representative principle to the point at which it rested twenty-seven years later" (005-001). The [[democracy-and-representation|Representation]] of the People Act 1884 extended the household franchise to the counties, enfranchising rural labourers and reshaping the political landscape. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes that this rural enfranchisement reinforced democracy on social questions at a critical juncture (009-002).

In [[political-economy|economics]], [[gladstone|Gladstone]] "kept upon the whole to the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite principles which he acquired in middle life" (005-001). He was not sympathetic to the [[new-unionism|New Unionism]] and semi-socialistic ideas of the late 1880s, which "constituted a powerful cross current to the political work that he had immediately in hand" (005-001). Yet his thinking was not rigidly laissez-faire. In relation to Irish land, "he entered upon a new departure which threw over [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]] in a leading case where the two parties were on glaringly unequal terms" (005-001). The Irish Land Act of 1881, establishing fair rents, fixity of tenure, and free sale, represented a pragmatic willingness to intervene in property relations when justice demanded it.

## Imperial Relations and the Gladstonian Principle

[[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s most original work, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s assessment, "was done in the sphere of imperial relations" (005-002). The maligned Majuba settlement of 1881 — granting self-government to the Transvaal after the First [[south-african-war|Boer War]] — was "an act of justice which came too late to effect a permanent undoing of mischief. All the greater was the courage of the statesman who could throw himself at that time upon the inherent force of national liberty and international fair dealing" (005-002).

In the case of [[ireland|Ireland]], [[gladstone|Gladstone]] "again relied on the same principles, but another force was necessary to carry the day, a force which no man can command, the force of time" (005-002). His conversion to [[home-rule|Irish Home Rule]] in 1885–86 split [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] and defined the final phase of his career. When "Mr. [[gladstone|Gladstone]] declared for [[home-rule|Home Rule]], and the party fissure took place on false lines" (009-002). The upper and middle classes largely went over to Unionism, yet [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s personal force retained on the Liberal side a core of loyalists, and the [[home-rule|Home Rule]] cause became the defining moral crusade of late Victorian liberalism.

In international dealings, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] was a pioneer. His principle was not precisely that of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]; "he was not a non-interventionist. He took action on behalf of Greece, and would have done so on behalf of the Armenians, to save the national honour and prevent a monstrous wrong" (005-002). The Gladstonian principle may be defined by antithesis to Machiavelli, [[otto-von-bismarck|Bismarck]], and the practice of every Foreign Office: "As that practice proceeds on the principle that reasons of State justify everything, so [[gladstone|Gladstone]] proceeded on the principle that reasons of State justify nothing that is not justified already by the human conscience" (005-002).

> The statesman is for him a man charged with maintaining not only the material interests but the honour of his country. He is a citizen of the world in that he represents his nation, which is a member of the community of the world. He has to recognize rights and duties, as every representative of every other human organization has to recognize rights and duties. There is no line drawn beyond which human obligations cease. There is no gulf across which the voice of human suffering cannot be heard, beyond which massacre and torture cease to be execrable. (005-002)

The Midlothian Campaign of 1879–80 was the practical expression of this doctrine. In a series of mass public addresses — a novel form of democratic political communication — [[gladstone|Gladstone]] denounced the imperialism of Disraeli's government, condemned the [[opium-war|Opium War]] and the occupation of Egypt, and articulated a vision of international relations based on justice rather than power. "From the denunciation of the [[opium-war|Opium War]], from the exposure of the Neapolitan prisons, to his last appearance on the morrow of the Constantinople massacre this was the message which [[gladstone|Gladstone]] sought to convey" (005-002). He was before his time, and was "not always able to maintain his principle in his own Cabinet" (005-002).

## In Liberalism

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], [[gladstone|Gladstone]] and [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] are the two towering figures of mid-nineteenth-century British liberalism:

> From the middle of the nineteenth century two great names stand out in the history of British Liberalism — that of Gladstone in the world of action, that of Mill in the world of thought. (005-001)

The two men differed in much, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes, but agreed in one respect: "They had the supreme virtue of keeping their minds fresh and open to new ideas, and both of them in consequence advanced to a deeper interpretation of social life as they grew older" (005-001). This shared openness meant that neither was the prisoner of a fixed ideological system. [[gladstone|Gladstone]] began as a Conservative and ended as the champion of Irish self-government; [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] began as a [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite and ended, by his own account, as a Socialist.

[[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s contribution to liberalism was primarily moral rather than intellectual. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] assesses him as "a moral rather than an intellectual force" who "raised the whole level of public life. By habitually calling upon what was best in men, he deepened the sense of public responsibility and paved the way, half unconsciously, for the fuller exercise of the social conscience" (005-003). This moral elevation — the appeal to conscience, duty, and the common good — was essential to the liberal project as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] understood it. Where [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] supplied the philosophical architecture, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] supplied the lived example of statesmanship conducted on principle.

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s narrative of liberalism's fortunes, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] also figures as a pivot of party realignment. When he formed his second [[state|Government]] in 1880, "the old party system stood secure in [[england|Great Britain]]" and "the plain distinction of Liberal and Conservative held the field" (009-002). But the social question was already unsettling this order. [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s decision to embrace [[home-rule|Home Rule]] in 1886 reshaped the party system, and "the political fight was for the moment shifted from the social question to the single absorbing issue of [[home-rule|Home Rule]]" (009-002). [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is critical of this shift — it gave the Unionists twenty years of near-unbroken supremacy — but he credits [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s personal force in keeping the liberal flame alive through a period of fragmentation and reaction.

## Legacy and Influence

[[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s legacy extended well beyond his lifetime. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] records that his chief opponent, Lord Salisbury, "had imbibed something of his spirit, and under its influence did much to save the country from the excesses of [[imperialism|Imperialism]]" (005-002). More directly, [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s follower [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman]] "used the brief term of his power to reverse the policy of racial domination in [[south-africa|South Africa]] and to prove the value of the old Gladstonian trust in the recuperative force of political freedom" (005-002). [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Campbell-Bannerman]] himself, a man of "no marked genius but that of character," acquired in his party "the position of a [[gladstone|Gladstone]]" after his principled stand against the methods of the [[south-african-war|South African War]] (009-003).

In the ideological contests of the early twentieth century, [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s name became shorthand for the old [[liberalism|Liberalism]] that its critics claimed was obsolete — "maundering on about [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and [[gladstone|Gladstone]], and the liberty of the individual, and the rights of [[nation|nationality]], and government by the [[nation|people]]" (009-001). Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] insists that this dismissal was premature. "The Liberal who starts from the Gladstonian tradition has in large measure realized that if he is to maintain the essence of his old ideas it must be through a process of adaptation and growth" (009-004). The Gladstonian inheritance — [[economic-liberty|free trade]], peace, retrenchment, self-government, and the rule of conscience in public affairs — was not to be discarded but deepened and extended to meet the social demands of a new century.

On his retirement in 1894, the world "appeared to relapse definitely into the older ways" of power politics, and his own party "gave itself up in large measure to opposite views" (005-002). But [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s example of a statesmanship conducted on moral principle, of a political life devoted to the gradual extension of liberty and justice, remained a permanent resource for the liberal tradition.
