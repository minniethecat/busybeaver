## The War and Its Liberal Critics

The Opium War of 1839–1842 was the conflict in which [[england|Britain]] used military force to compel China to accept the importation of opium — a trade that the Chinese government had outlawed on both moral and public-health grounds. From its inception, the war was denounced by a minority of British liberals as a fundamental betrayal of the principles they claimed to uphold. [[william-ewart-gladstone|William Ewart Gladstone]], then a young Tory MP, made his name in part through his opposition to the war, arguing that it violated every principle of justice between nations. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], looking back from 1911, listed "the denunciation of the Opium War" as the first in a series of Gladstonian acts that defined a distinctive approach to international relations — one based not on *raison d'état* but on the principle that "reasons of State justify nothing that is not justified already by the human conscience" (005-002).

## The Gladstonian Principle in Foreign Policy

The Opium War served [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] as the archetypal case of what happens when foreign policy is conducted without ethical constraint. [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s opposition was not based on non-interventionism — he was not, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted, a [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite pacifist. [[gladstone|Gladstone]] "took action on behalf of Greece, and would have done so on behalf of the Armenians, to save the national honour and prevent a monstrous wrong" (005-002). The distinction was between force used in the service of justice and force used in the service of commercial exploitation. The Opium War exemplified the latter: [[england|Britain]] had gone to war to protect the profits of opium merchants at the expense of Chinese sovereignty and public health. For the Gladstonian tradition, this was the negation of everything liberalism claimed to stand for in the international sphere.

## From the Opium War to the Constantinople Massacre

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] traced a continuous line from [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s denunciation of the Opium War, through "the exposure of the Neapolitan prisons, to his last appearance on the morrow of the Constantinople massacre" (005-002). In each case, the principle was the same: "the statesman is for him a man charged with maintaining not only the material interests but the honour of his country. He is a citizen of the world in that he represents his nation, which is a member of the community of the world" (005-002). The Opium War was thus not merely a historical episode but the founding negative example against which a genuinely liberal foreign policy must define itself.

## Legacy and Historiography

The Opium War has become a touchstone in post-colonial historiography, a symbol of Western imperialism's willingness to deploy liberal rhetoric in the service of illiberal practice. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s treatment, while less radical than later anti-imperialist critiques, prefigures this tradition by insisting that foreign policy must be judged by the same moral standards as domestic policy — that there is "no gulf across which the voice of human suffering cannot be heard, beyond which massacre and torture cease to be execrable" (005-002).
