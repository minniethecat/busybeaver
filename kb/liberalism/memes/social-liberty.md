# Social Liberty

Social liberty is a foundational concept in liberal political theory, denoting a form of freedom that can be enjoyed by all members of a community equally. It is distinguished from mere absence of restraint — what might be called unsocial or absolute freedom — by the recognition that liberty for all depends on mutual limitations on conduct that causes injury to others.

## Unsocial versus Social Freedom

The distinction between unsocial and social freedom is central to understanding the liberal conception of liberty:

> There emerges a distinction between unsocial and social freedom. Unsocial freedom is the right of a man to use his powers without regard to the wishes or interests of anyone but himself. Such freedom is theoretically possible for an individual. It is antithetic to all public control. It is theoretically impossible for a plurality of individuals living in mutual contact. Socially it is a contradiction, unless the desires of all men were automatically attuned to social ends. Social freedom, then, for any epoch short of the millennium rests on restraint. (004-007)

Unsocial freedom — the freedom to act without any regard for others — cannot coexist with community life. The moment more than one person exists, their freedoms inevitably collide, and some measure of restraint becomes the condition for anyone's liberty.

## Restraint as the Foundation of Liberty

The paradox that liberty requires restraint is essential to modern liberal thought. Far from being a contradiction, the restraint of harmful conduct is precisely what secures the freedom of those who would otherwise be its victims:

> The restraint of the aggressor is the freedom of the sufferer, and only by restraint on the actions by which men injure one another do they as a whole community gain freedom in all courses of conduct that can be pursued without ultimate social disharmony. (004-007)

This principle reconciles the apparent inconsistency between opposing economic protectionism while supporting protective legislation for workers:

> The two things have nothing in common but that they are restraints intended to operate in the interests of somebody. The one is a restraint which, in the Liberal view, would operate in favour of certain industries and interests to the prejudice of others... The other is a restraint conceived in the interest primarily of the poorer classes with the object of securing to them a more effective freedom and a nearer approach to equality of conditions. (004-008)

## Social Liberty and Equality

The pursuit of social liberty leads logically toward greater equality. As liberal thinkers recognised, individualism pushed to its logical conclusions converges with socialistic remedies:

> To carry through the real principles of Liberalism, to achieve social liberty and living equality of rights, we shall have to probe still deeper. We must not assume any of the rights of property as axiomatic. We must look at their actual working and consider how they affect the life of society. (004-013)

The social liberty concept thus bridges [[classical-liberalism|classical liberal]] commitments to individual freedom with the recognition that [[positive-liberty|effective liberty]] for all requires collective action to address structural inequalities.
