## Definition

International liberty extends the Liberal critique of force from the domestic to the international sphere. If force is the basis of tyranny within the state, then force in relations between states is the basis of international tyranny. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] articulates three core propositions at the foundation of international liberty (002-025):

1. "It is of the essence of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] to oppose the use of force, the basis of all tyranny."
2. "It is one of its practical necessities to withstand the tyranny of armaments."
3. "In proportion as the world becomes free, the use of force becomes meaningless. There is no purpose in aggression if it is not to issue in one form or another of national subjection."

## Force and Tyranny

The opposition to force is not merely a preference for peaceful methods but a logical extension of Liberal first principles. If liberty means government by law rather than by arbitrary will, then international relations governed by force replicate the authoritarian structure that [[liberalism|Liberalism]] opposes domestically. The state that subjects its own citizens to the rule of law cannot consistently claim the right to subject other nations to the rule of force.

## The Tyranny of Armaments

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies two ways in which armaments threaten liberty (002-025). First, "the military force may be directly turned against liberty, as in [[russia|Russia]]," where standing armies serve as instruments of domestic repression. Second, and more subtly, "in Western Europe, the military spirit eats into free institutions and absorbs the public resources which might go to the advancement of civilization." The financial burden of armaments diverts resources from education, [[welfare-state|social welfare]], and other constructive functions of the state.

This argument connects international liberty to [[fiscal-liberty]] and to the broader [[free-trade|Cobdenite]] programme of peace, retrenchment, and [[social-reform|reform]]. The money spent on armaments is money not spent on the conditions of human flourishing.

## The Cobdenite Vision

The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] elevated international liberty to a central place in the Liberal programme. [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] argued that free commercial intercourse between nations would "engender mutual knowledge, and knit the severed [[nation|people]]s by countless ties of business interests" (004-001). [[free-trade|Free Trade]], on this view, was not merely an economic policy but the foundation of [[peace|international peace]].

The [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite vision linked [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]], colonial freedom, and international freedom as "parts of one whole" (004-001). Non-intervention, peace, restriction of armaments, retrenchment of expenditure, and reduction of taxation formed "the connected series of practical consequences" (004-001).

## Limits and Tensions

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] acknowledged that the principle of non-intervention had limits. The treatment of "weaker [[nation|people]]s" under colonial rule — where "if men say equality, they mean oppression by forms of justice" (002-024) — raised hard questions about when intervention might be justified to prevent exploitation. The Liberal approach, he argued, should "support whatever method succeeds in securing the 'coloured' man from personal violence, from the lash, from expropriation, and from gin; above all, so far as it may yet be, from the white man himself" (002-024).

These tensions between the principle of non-intervention and the duty to protect the vulnerable parallel the domestic tension between liberty and state intervention — and point toward the same constructive resolution: international liberty, like all liberty, ultimately depends on law, not mere abstention.
