## Definition

The tyranny of the majority describes a distinctive danger of democratic government: that a majority, acting through the ordinary processes of legislation, may enact laws that violate the rights of individuals or minority groups. Unlike tyranny by a monarch or an aristocracy, this form of oppression carries the moral weight of popular sanction — it is the community itself, or a majority of it, that authorizes the injustice.

This concept is central to the Liberal distinction between democracy and liberty. That a government is elected by a majority does not guarantee that it governs liberally. It is, as [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] recognized, unfortunately quite possible for a democratic government to be tyrannical. (003-020)

## Mill's Analysis

In his treatment of representative government, [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] argued that the right to vote is not a means to the enforcement of one's claims upon society — an instrumental power — but rather a means of mental and moral education. Participation in public life cultivates the citizen's capacities, but this educative function depends on institutions that protect the individual against the majority's power. (005-007)

[[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] was particularly concerned with the tendency of democratic societies to enforce social conformity. The tyranny of the majority operates not only through law but through opinion — through the pressure to think, speak, and live as the majority thinks one should. This "social tyranny" can be more oppressive than legal tyranny because it leaves fewer means of escape.

## Constitutional Safeguards

[[liberalism|Liberalism]]'s answer to the tyranny of the majority is not to reject democracy but to constitutionalize it. [[rights|Rights]] that are placed beyond the reach of ordinary legislative majorities — freedom of thought, freedom of speech, freedom of association, the right to a fair trial — constitute the boundary between democratic government and liberal government. [[state|The State]] that respects these boundaries is liberal; the State that does not is despotic, regardless of how its rulers are selected.

This is why [[liberalism|Liberalism]] has always insisted on the distinction between the will of the nation and the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]]. The question of which is the ultimate authority is not merely theoretical — it is the practical question on which the character of every democratic government depends. (003-020)

## Legacy in Liberal Thought

The concept of the tyranny of the majority has shaped Liberal constitutionalism from the nineteenth century to the present. It informs the case for:
- **Bills of rights** that constrain legislative power
- **Judicial review** as a check on majority action
- **Federalism and [[local-government|devolution]]** as means of dispersing power
- **[[protectionism|Protection]] of minority groups** against discrimination
- **Free speech and press freedom** as counters to conformist pressure

In all these forms, the Liberal response to majority tyranny is the same: liberty requires that even democratic power be limited, and the most dangerous oppressor may be not the autocrat but the crowd.
