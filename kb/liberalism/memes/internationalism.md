## Foundations in the Manchester School

Liberal internationalism has its roots in the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s vision of [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as the foundation of peace. [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and his followers argued that "Free commercial intercourse between nations would engender mutual knowledge, and knit the severed [[nation|people]]s by countless ties of business interests. [[free-trade|Free Trade]] meant peace." (004-002) This was not merely an economic doctrine but a comprehensive vision of international order in which the material interests of ordinary [[nation|people]], rather than the ambitions of rulers, would determine the relations between states. The interconnected commitments to "Nonintervention, peace, restriction of armaments, retrenchment of expenditure, reduction of taxation" formed a "connected series of practical consequences." (004-002)

## National Freedom and International Order

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], internationalism was inseparable from the principle of [[self-determination|national self-determination]]. Just as individual freedom required the capacity for [[self-determination|self-direction]], so national freedom required that [[nation|people]]s be allowed to govern themselves according to their own traditions and aspirations. "Let every [[nation|people]] be free to work out its own salvation," (004-002) was the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite principle, and [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] gave it philosophical depth by grounding it in the organic conception of society: "[[nation|Nation]]al and [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]] are growths of the same root, and their historic connection rests on no accident, but on ultimate identity of idea." (006-013) Internationalism was not the erasure of national differences but the framework within which distinct [[nation|people]]s could pursue their own development in peaceful cooperation.

## The Critique of Imperialism

Liberal internationalism stood in sharp opposition to imperialism, which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] regarded as a violation of the principles of both liberty and [[self-determination|national self-determination]]. The Liberal critique of empire — articulated most sharply by [[richard-cobden|Cobden]], [[john-bright|Bright]], and later by [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s Midlothian campaign — held that imperial adventures were financed by the many for the benefit of the few, that they corrupted domestic politics, and that they denied to subjected [[nation|people]]s the very rights that [[liberal-party|Liberals]] championed at home. "The colonies could no longer be governed in the interests of the mother country," (004-002) the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ites had argued, anticipating the [[local-government|devolution]]ary logic that would extend through [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s Irish policy to the twentieth-century Liberal commitment to self-government within [[british-empire|the Empire]].

## Internationalism and the Twentieth Century

Writing on the eve of the First World War, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] placed internationalism among the essential conditions of liberal progress. "Internationalism on the one side, national self-government on the other, are the radical conditions of the growth of a social mind," (009-015) he wrote, insisting that the enlargement of moral community beyond the nation-state was not a utopian fantasy but a practical necessity. This commitment distinguished [[liberalism|Liberalism]] both from a narrow nationalism that treated international relations as a zero-sum struggle and from an abstract cosmopolitanism that dismissed the moral significance of national communities. The liberal vision was of a world of self-governing [[nation|people]]s bound together by commerce, law, and shared commitment to peace.
