## Life and Context

Thomas Hill Green (1836–1882) was a fellow of Balliol College, [[Oxford]], and the most influential British philosopher of the Idealist school in the late nineteenth century. His brief career — he died at forty-five — nonetheless transformed the philosophical foundations of British [[Liberalism]]. Teaching at Oxford during the 1870s and early 1880s, Green confronted a Liberal tradition that had become narrowly identified with [[individualism]], [[laissez-faire]], and the negative conception of [[liberty]] as mere absence of restraint. His work supplied the philosophical architecture for a more expansive understanding of freedom and the State.

## Organic Conception of Society

Green's distinctive contribution was the organic conception of the relation between the individual and society. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] traces the development of this idea through [[John Stuart Mill]], noting that it was "a conception towards which [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] worked through his career, and which forms the starting-point of T. H. Green's philosophy alike in ethics and in politics" (006-005). Where [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] arrived at the organic view as the culmination of a lifetime's reflection, Green began there — making the interdependence of individual and community the axiomatic foundation of his entire system.

For Green, the individual was not an atom existing prior to society, nor was society a mere aggregate of independent persons. Society "consists wholly of persons. It has no distinct personality separate from and superior to those of its members" (006-007). The nation might have a collective life and character — constituted by ties of patriotism, kinship, common history, and language — but it was "not a mysterious entity over and above the forty odd millions of living souls who dwell together under a common law" (006-007). Its life was their life.

## The Common Good

From this organic starting-point, Green derived his central ethical principle: the common good. [[rights|Rights]] were not pre-political possessions of the isolated individual but claims recognised and enforced by the community because they served the good of all. "The common good to which each man's rights are subordinate is a good in which each man has a share" (006-007). This share consisted in realising one's capacities — "of feeling, of loving, of mental and physical energy" — and in realising these, the individual played his part in the social life. Or, in Green's classic formulation, "he finds his own good in the common good" (006-007).

This was not the sacrifice of the individual to the collective but the recognition that self-realisation was possible only through participation in the common life. [[liberty|Freedom]] was not the absence of interference but the positive capacity to develop one's faculties within a community that made such development possible.

## Impact on Liberalism

Green's influence on the Liberal tradition was profound and practical. Together with [[Arnold Toynbee]] and the settlement movement, Green's teaching was "setting [[liberalism|Liberalism]] free from the shackles of an individualist conception of liberty and paving the way for the legislation of our own time" (009-002). The [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]] of the Edwardian era — [[old-age-pensions]], [[National Insurance]], labour exchanges, school feeding — drew on Green's philosophical insight that the State could be an instrument of freedom rather than its enemy.

The theoretical bridge Green constructed was essential to this transformation. By showing that [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] and social cooperation were not opposites but complementary aspects of a single organic whole, he dissolved the antithesis that had paralysed earlier Liberal thinking about State action. [[Leonard Trelawny Hobhouse]]'s own synthesis — the reconciliation of [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]] with collective provision — was built on Greenian foundations.

## Legacy

Green's legacy extended far beyond academic philosophy. His students at Balliol included many of the administrators, civil servants, and social [[social-reform|reform]]ers who would staff the Liberal governments of the early twentieth century. The "Greenian" conviction — that the State exists to remove obstacles to the good life, and that liberty means the positive power to develop one's capacities — passed from Oxford common rooms into the statute book. Though Green himself died before the New Liberal programme was enacted, his ideas supplied its moral warrant.
