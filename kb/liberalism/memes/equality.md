## Definition

Equality, in the liberal tradition articulated by [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L. T. Hobhouse]], is not an abstract claim about human sameness but a concrete demand for **impartiality in law**. It is the corollary of liberty: if law is to guarantee freedom, it must apply equally to all. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] states, "if there is one law for the peer and another for the peasant, one for rich and another for poor, the law does not guarantee liberty for all. [[liberty|Liberty]] in this respect implies equality" (002-005). Without equality before the law, liberty becomes a class privilege rather than a universal right.

The liberal conception of equality is thus procedural rather than substantive — it demands equal treatment under impartial rules, not equal outcomes. This distinguishes it from socialist or egalitarian conceptions that seek to level material conditions directly.

## Historical Development

The demand for equality before the law emerged as a direct challenge to feudal and aristocratic privilege. In the medieval order, different estates — nobility, clergy, and commoners — were subject to different laws and courts. The liberal movement attacked this structure at its foundation, insisting that [[civil-liberties|legal rights]] and obligations be uniform.

The French Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man (1789) gave this principle its most famous expression, declaring that men are born free and equal in rights. The Declaration of 1793 was even more emphatic:

> All men are equal by nature and before the law.
> (003-015)

This revolutionary assertion swept away the legal architecture of the _ancien régime_ and established equality as a foundational political norm.

In [[england|England]], the evolution was more gradual but no less significant. The [[reform|Reform]] Acts of the nineteenth century progressively dismantled political inequality, while the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] philosophy of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] treated each person's happiness as counting equally in the social calculus (004-001). The school of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]], though less theoretically inclined than the French, was "saturated with the conviction that the unfettered action of the individual is the mainspring of all progress" — a conviction that presupposed equal liberty for all (004-001).

## Key Arguments

The case for equality in the liberal framework rests on three interconnected arguments:

1. **Equality as the condition of genuine liberty**: Law can only secure freedom if it is impartial. A partial law protects some at the expense of others, transforming liberty from a universal condition into a distribution of privileges (002-005).

2. **Equality as the basis of civic responsibility**: Democratic citizenship requires that each person bear an equal share of responsibility for the community's decisions. Representative government "is not so much as a means to the enforcement of his claims upon society, but rather as a means of enforcing his personal responsibility" (005-007).

3. **Equality as a requirement of justice**: The liberal commitment to justice demands that social arrangements work to the benefit of all, not merely the powerful. This principle underpins the Liberal openness to social [[social-reform|reform]] — "it must emerge from the efforts of society as a whole to secure a fuller measure of justice, and a better organization of life for all" (008-004).

:::info
**Equality vs. Uniformity**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is careful to distinguish equality from sameness. Liberal equality does not demand that all persons be treated identically in every respect — only that differences in treatment be justified by relevant differences in circumstance, not by differences in wealth, birth, or status.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats equality as inseparable from liberty throughout _[[liberalism|Liberalism]]_. In Chapter II, he presents equality as the implicit postulate of the rule of law: the moment we claim that law protects liberty, we commit ourselves to the demand that law be impartial (002-005).

In later chapters, the equality principle is extended to economic questions. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues that vast inequalities of wealth, perpetuated by inheritance laws, raise the question of whether the economic system truly serves all members of society: "Ought we to acquiesce in a condition in which the great majority are born to nothing except what they can earn by hard work, while a few are born to wealth which they have done nothing to create?" (008-012).

Crucially, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] sees the pursuit of equality not as a departure from liberal principles but as their logical fulfillment. The "harmonious whole" of liberal applications — liberty, equality, and democracy — must be shown to be "really harmonious in theory and in practice" (007-001).
