# New Unionism

**New Unionism** was a working-class movement that emerged in [[england|Britain]] during the late 1880s, distinguished from the older craft unionism by its organization of unskilled workers, its willingness to use the strike as a political weapon, and its explicit connection to socialist ideas and independent labour representation.

## Origins and Character

The movement arose in a period of intensified social questioning. The industrial slowdown after the boom of the early 1870s and growing awareness of urban poverty — documented by researchers like [[charles-booth|Charles Booth]] — created conditions for a more militant labour politics. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies the watershed moment:

> From the Dockers' strike of 1889 the New Unionism became a fighting force in public affairs, and the idea of a Labour party began to take shape. (009-002)

The [[london|London]] Dock Strike of 1889, led by Ben Tillett, Tom Mann, and John Burns, demonstrated that unskilled and casual workers could be effectively organized. Unlike the older craft unions, which represented skilled workers through friendly society benefits and cautious collective bargaining, the New Unions aimed at broad industrial organization and were willing to deploy the strike for political as well as economic objectives.

## Relation to Gladstonian Liberalism

The emergence of New Unionism created tensions within the Liberal coalition. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes that [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] himself "was not sympathetically disposed to the 'New Unionism' and the semi-socialistic ideas that came at the end of the 'eighties, which, in fact, constituted a powerful cross current to the political work that he had immediately in hand" (005-007). This divergence between Gladstonian liberalism and the new labour politics foreshadowed the eventual realignment of British politics along class lines.

## Political Consequences

The New Unionism movement, together with the intellectual work of the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] and the policy research of the Webbs, contributed to the creation of an independent [[labour-party|Labour Party]]. The defeat of the Engineers in 1897 temporarily checked the strike-based strategy, but the political impulse survived, leading eventually to the [[labour-party|Labour Representation Committee]] (1900) and the emergence of [[labour-party|Labour]] as a distinct parliamentary force.
