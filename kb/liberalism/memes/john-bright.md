## Role in the Manchester School

John Bright was, alongside [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]], the co-leader of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] — the dominant force in mid-nineteenth-century [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]]. The school was "affiliated in general outlook both to the doctrine of natural liberty and to the discipline of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]" (004-002) and championed [[economic-liberty|free trade]], non-intervention in foreign affairs, and a restricted conception of the functions of government. Bright brought to the movement a commanding eloquence that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], writing in the early twentieth century, still recalled as exemplary, invoking "the eloquence of Bright" alongside "[[richard-cobden|Cobden]]'s statistics" as the twin engines of the earlier Radical movement. (009-015)

## Opposition to Factory Legislation

Bright's commitment to [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]] led him, together with [[richard-cobden|Cobden]], to oppose the early [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]]. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] recorded that the legislation "was bitterly opposed by [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and Bright," (004-006) even as it enjoyed support from [[tory-party|Conservatives]] like [[lord-shaftesbury|Lord Shaftesbury]] and from [[radicalism|Radicals]] like J. Cam [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]. The [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]] restricted the hours and conditions of labour, particularly for women and children, and in the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s view they represented an unwarranted interference with the liberty of the individual worker and employer to determine the terms of their own bargain. This episode became a pivotal reference point in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s narrative of liberalism's evolution — the moment at which "the cleavage of opinion ... cut across the ordinary divisions of party" and a "new and more concrete conception of liberty arose." (004-006)

## The Anti-Corn Law League

Bright's national prominence was forged through his leadership of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], the mass movement that campaigned for the repeal of the protective tariffs on imported grain. The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were "conceived in the interest of the governing classes" and imposed "a tax on the food of the masses for the benefit of the landlords." (004-002) For Bright, [[economic-liberty|free trade]] was not merely an economic policy but a moral cause — the means by which the "masses had been brought to the lowest point of economic misery" (004-002) could be lifted through cheap food, expanding trade, and [[peace|international peace]]. His platform partnership with [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] united statistical rigour with moral passion, establishing the model of liberal [[social-reform|reform]] agitation that would inspire subsequent generations.

## Legacy

Bright's legacy in the Liberal tradition is complex. His resistance to [[factory-act|factory legislation]] placed him on what later liberals came to see as the wrong side of history, yet his broader vision — that liberty, peace, and prosperity were indissolubly linked — remained a vital current in Liberal thought. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s treatment is characteristic of the New Liberal ambivalence: critical of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s narrow conception of liberty, yet deeply admiring of its moral seriousness and political courage. The task for twentieth-century liberalism, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw it, was to carry forward the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s unifying vision "with the same logical cogency, the same practical resourcefulness, the same driving force" — but now informed by a richer, more concrete understanding of what liberty required. (009-015)
