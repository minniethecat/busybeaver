# House of Lords

The House of Lords is the upper chamber of the British [[parliament|Parliament]]. Throughout the nineteenth century, its power to veto legislation passed by the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] made it a central obstacle to Liberal [[social-reform|reform]]. [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s active intervention in 1859–60 saved [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] from a humiliating surrender to the Lords and secured the financial supremacy of the [[house-of-commons|lower house]] — a settlement that held until the constitutional crisis of 1909.

## Constitutional Role

The House of Lords served as a revising and delaying chamber, scrutinising legislation passed by [[house-of-commons|the Commons]]. In theory it acted as a check on hasty or radical measures; in practice, it functioned as a bulwark of conservative and landed interests against democratic [[social-reform|reform]]. To maintain the right of [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] against the Lords was, on the face of it, to adhere to old constitutional right, but to do so under the new circumstances which had made [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] representative of the nation while the Lords remained a hereditary body (003-002).

## The Nineteenth-Century Conflict

Throughout the nineteenth century, the Lords repeatedly blocked or neutered Liberal legislation. Their rejection of the [[reform|1866 Reform Bill]], their obstruction of [[home-rule|Irish Home Rule]], and their emasculation of Liberal land and education measures established a pattern of partisan obstruction. The Lords imposed a tax on the food of the masses for the benefit of the landlords through the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] (004-001), and their resistance to [[social-reform|reform]] convinced many [[liberal-party|Liberals]] that the hereditary principle was incompatible with democratic government.

## The Lords and the Corn Laws (1846)

The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 represented the most dramatic defeat of the Lords' protective function. The [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] had mobilized the manufacturing and working classes against the tariff on imported grain, which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] described as "a tax on the food of the masses for the benefit of the landlords." (004-001) Although the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] had been defended by the Lords as essential to the agricultural interest, the pressure of famine in [[ireland|Ireland]] and the political skill of [[robert-peel|Sir Robert Peel]] forced repeal through both houses.

The significance of the [[corn-laws|Corn Law repeal]] for the Lords question went far beyond the immediate issue of food prices. It demonstrated that a hereditary chamber, composed overwhelmingly of landed proprietors, could not indefinitely resist measures demanded by the representative house and supported by organized public opinion. The precedent established in 1846 — that the Lords must ultimately yield on questions of national urgency — would be invoked repeatedly by Liberal [[social-reform|reform]]ers over the following decades. (004-003)

## Gladstone's Budgetary Victory (1860)

The constitutional settlement of 1860, engineered by [[gladstone|Gladstone]] as Chancellor of the Exchequer, was a turning point in the relations between the two houses. When the Lords rejected a bill repealing the paper duties — a measure [[gladstone|Gladstone]] regarded as essential both for fiscal [[social-reform|reform]] and for the spread of cheap newspapers and political information — [[gladstone|Gladstone]] responded by incorporating all the year's financial measures into a single Finance Bill. (005-002)

The constitutional principle at stake was that the Lords could not amend money bills. By forcing the issue, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] secured a settlement affirming the financial supremacy of [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] — a principle that would hold until the crisis of 1909. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted that the victory was won under the very shadow of the Lords' pretensions, and that it maintained continuity with the old constitutional right while adapting it to the new circumstances of a representative Commons confronting a hereditary upper house. (003-002) (005-002)

## The Parliament Act of 1911

The conflict reached its climax in 1909 when the Lords rejected [[david-lloyd-george|Lloyd George]]'s "People's Budget." The resulting constitutional crisis, fought over two general elections in 1910, ended with the [[parliament-act-1911|Parliament Act of 1911]], which removed the Lords' absolute veto over legislation and replaced it with a two-year delaying power. The Liberal leader was well aware that he must deal with the House of Lords and the Plural vote if he was to gain his own ends (009-004).

## The Lords and Liberal Democracy

The House of Lords represented what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identified as one of the "unofficial brakes" on democratic governance — alongside the civil service, the permanent determined opposition of the Bench to democratic measures, and the Press (009-014). For [[liberal-party|Liberals]], the existence of a hereditary chamber with the power to block the will of the elected Commons was fundamentally at odds with the principle of [[democracy|popular sovereignty]].
