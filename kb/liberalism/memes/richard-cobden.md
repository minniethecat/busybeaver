## Overview

Richard Cobden was one of the most influential figures in nineteenth-century British liberalism. As the intellectual and organizational leader of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]], he embodied the free-trade radicalism that transformed British economic policy in the 1840s and set the terms of liberal political economy for a generation.

> The school of Cobden is affiliated in general outlook both to the doctrine of natural liberty and to the discipline of Bentham. It shared with the Benthamites the conviction that the great object of public policy was to set the individual free. (004-001)

Cobden's significance lies not only in the specific policy victory of [[corn-laws|Corn Law repeal]] (1846) but in the broader moral and political vision he articulated: a vision of [[peace|international peace]] through [[economic-liberty|free trade]], of government restrained to its proper sphere, and of the common [[nation|people]] empowered through cheap food and economic opportunity.

## The Manchester School

Cobden gave his name to a distinctive school of liberal thought — the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] — which held that the primary function of government was to secure [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]], and that the best means to this end was the removal of artificial restrictions on trade and industry.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes that this school "tended both in external and in internal affairs to a restricted view of the function of government" (004-002). In foreign policy, the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] opposed military intervention and imperial expansion, believing that commerce, not conquest, was the true source of national prosperity. In domestic affairs, it advocated for the dismantling of protective tariffs, monopolies, and all forms of state-granted privilege.

### Free Trade and Peace

The connection between [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and [[peace|international peace]] was central to Cobden's worldview. He argued that nations bound together by commercial intercourse would have no interest in war. This principle found practical expression in the Cobden–Chevalier Treaty of 1860, a landmark commercial agreement between [[england|Britain]] and [[france|France]] that substantially reduced tariffs between the two nations.

## The Anti-Corn Law Campaign

Cobden's greatest political achievement was the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], the protectionist tariffs on imported grain that kept food prices artificially high and enriched the landed aristocracy at the expense of the industrial working class.

> The first point of attack, already recognized by Adam Smith, was the landowning interest. The Corn Laws were, in the eyes of the manufacturing population, a tax on the food of the people levied for the benefit of the landowners. (004-010)

The [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], co-founded by Cobden and [[john-bright|John Bright]], was one of the first modern political pressure groups. Through mass meetings, pamphleteering, and electoral organization, it mobilized public opinion on an unprecedented scale, creating a model for democratic political action that subsequent [[social-reform|reform]] movements would follow.

## Hobhouse's Assessment

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats Cobden and his school with respect but also with critical distance. While acknowledging the historic importance of the free-trade movement, he argues that the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s restricted view of government proved inadequate to the challenges of industrial society:

> But a consistent theory of liberty could not rest wholly satisfied with the actual system under which property is held. (004-010)

The limitation of the Cobdenite position, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, was its failure to recognize that genuine liberty might require positive state action — not just the removal of obstructions but the creation of conditions in which all individuals could flourish. This insight would become central to the "new liberalism" of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
