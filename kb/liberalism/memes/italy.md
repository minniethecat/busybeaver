# Italy

Italy was a central reference point in nineteenth-century Liberal discussions of [[nation|nationality]] and self-government. The city-states of ancient Italy, alongside those of Greece, represented a distinct type of social organization that differed fundamentally from clan and communal structures. In the modern era, the movement for Italian unification under figures such as Mazzini and Cavour exemplified the Liberal principle of [[national-self-determination]], demonstrating that [[nation|people]]s bound by common culture, history, and language had a right to constitute themselves as independent nations.

## The City-State Heritage

The Italian city-states of the mediaeval and Renaissance periods — Venice, Florence, Genoa, Milan — provided a powerful historical model of self-governing political communities. Liberal thinkers in the nineteenth century frequently invoked this heritage to argue that free institutions were not a modern invention but had deep roots in European civilization. The contrast between the civic vitality of the city-states and the despotism of the post-Renaissance territorial states became a staple of [[whig-party|Whig]] historiography.

## Unification and the National Principle

The Risorgimento (1815–1871) transformed Italy from a "geographical expression" into a united kingdom. The movement drew on three distinct strands of Liberal thought: the republican nationalism of Mazzini, which emphasised popular sovereignty and democratic self-determination; the constitutional monarchism of Cavour, which sought to modernise the Sardinian state as a vehicle for national unification; and the Romantic nationalism that saw in Italian culture and history a natural claim to political independence.

For British [[liberal-party|Liberals]], Italian unification was a cause célèbre. Figures such as [[gladstone|Gladstone]] and [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] publicly supported the Italian cause, seeing in it the vindication of the principle that [[nation|nationality]] — not dynastic ambition — should determine the boundaries of states. The British government's non-intervention in the Italian wars of 1859–1860 was defended on Liberal grounds: that [[nation|people]]s should be free to determine their own political arrangements without foreign coercion.

## Italy in Liberal International Thought

The Italian case became a touchstone in Liberal debates about the scope of the principle of [[nation|nationality]]. Supporters of [[nationalism|the national principle]] pointed to Italy as evidence that cultural nations had a natural right to statehood. Critics, however, noted that the unification process had involved the absorption of culturally distinct regions and the suppression of local autonomies, raising questions about where the boundaries of a legitimate "nation" properly lay.

## Liberal Institutions After Unification

The new Italian state adopted a constitutional monarchy modelled in part on British parliamentary institutions. However, the restricted franchise, the power of the Crown, and the continued influence of the Catholic Church — which opposed the Liberal state's secularising [[social-reform|reform]]s — meant that Italian [[liberalism|Liberalism]] remained fragile and contested. The "Roman Question" (the Church's refusal to recognise the Italian state after the annexation of Rome in 1870) created a lasting tension between Liberal secularism and religious authority that would echo across Europe.
