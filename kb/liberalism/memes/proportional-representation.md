## Definition

Proportional representation (PR) is a principle of electoral design that seeks to make a legislature the mirror of the electorate as a whole, rather than the instrument of a numerical majority alone. Under majoritarian systems, a party or interest commanding a bare majority of votes may secure a near-monopoly of seats, silencing all other voices. PR aims instead to ensure that every substantial body of opinion within the community receives representation in proportion to its electoral support.

[[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] was the most prominent Liberal advocate of proportional representation in the nineteenth century. For [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], PR was not a technical question of electoral mechanics but a constitutional safeguard essential to the integrity of representative government itself.

## Historical Development

The movement for proportional representation emerged in the mid-nineteenth century as a response to the perceived defects of first-past-the-post elections. Un[[social-reform|reform]]ed electoral systems in [[england|Britain]] and elsewhere routinely produced parliaments that bore little relation to the distribution of public opinion. The [[reform|Reform]] Acts of 1832 and 1867 extended the franchise but did nothing to ensure that the newly enfranchised were fairly represented.

[[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] took up the cause in his *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]* (1861), arguing that the bare majority system was a threat to liberty itself. He was joined by thinkers such as Thomas Hare, whose scheme for the single transferable vote provided a practical mechanism, and later by Walter Bagehot and other constitutional writers.

Outside [[england|Britain]], proportional systems were adopted more rapidly. Belgium introduced PR in 1899, followed by several European nations in the early twentieth century. In [[england|Britain]], however, the cause of electoral [[social-reform|reform]] was repeatedly deferred, and PR remained an aspiration of the Liberal and later [[labour-party|Labour]] left throughout the twentieth century.

## Key Arguments

1. **[[protectionism|Protection]] of minorities**: The gravest danger of democracy, in [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s analysis, is the tyranny of the majority. A legislature elected by bare majorities in single-member districts may represent only "a portion of the [[nation|people]], however preponderant numerically," leaving substantial minorities without any voice in the making of the laws that govern them. PR is the institutional remedy for this defect (005-244).

2. **The mirror principle**: A parliament should reflect the nation. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] argued that representative government is only truly representative when the assembly contains "not a portion of the [[nation|people]]... but the whole" (005-244). Anything less is not representative government but a distorted form of elective oligarchy.

3. **The education of citizenship**: [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] believed that participation in public affairs educates the citizen in responsibility. When a voter's ballot can never affect the outcome because they live in a safe seat, the educative value of the franchise is lost. PR gives every vote weight and every voter a stake in the outcome.

4. **Reconciling liberty with democracy**: [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] was acutely aware that personal liberty and [[political-freedom|political liberty]] could come into conflict — that "if personal liberty were fundamental it might only be imperilled by a so-called [[political-freedom|political liberty]] which would give to the majority unlimited powers of coercion" (005-244). PR is one of the institutional devices by which the two can be reconciled.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] presents [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s advocacy of proportional representation as integral to his broader theory of liberty. In the chapter on "The Heart of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]," he traces the connection between [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s concern for the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]] and his institutional proposals for fair representation:

> He was, therefore, for many years anxiously concerned with the means of securing a fair hearing and fair representation to minorities, and as a pioneer of the movement for Proportional Representation he sought to make Parliament the reflection not of a portion of the people, however preponderant numerically, but of the whole.
> (005-244)

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats this not as a side-interest of [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s but as the logical conclusion of his democratic theory. If the purpose of the franchise is not merely to enforce the claims of the majority but to summon every citizen to a share of responsibility for the common life, then a system that silences minorities defeats the educative purpose of self-government from the start.

The broader implication is that liberty and democracy are not identical values. [[democracy|Democracy]] without institutional restraint can be a vehicle of coercion, and the institutions that make democracy safe for liberty — proportional representation, constitutional checks, [[local-government|devolution]] — are themselves part of the Liberal tradition.
