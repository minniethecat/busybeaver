# Henry George

Henry George (1839–1897) was an [[united-states|America]]n political economist whose work had a significant impact on progressive thought in late Victorian [[england|Britain]]. His school held by competition, but only on the basis of genuine freedom and equality for all individuals, positioning itself in a middle ground between laissez-faire [[liberalism]] and [[socialism]] (004-010).

## Economic Philosophy

George's central argument, developed in *[[progress|Progress]] and [[poverty|Poverty]]* (1879), was that the private appropriation of land rent constituted a fundamental injustice. He proposed a "single tax" on land values as the remedy — a policy that would capture the unearned increment of land value for public benefit while leaving the products of individual labour and capital untaxed. This position distinguished him from both [[karl-marx|Marxist]] collectivists (who sought to abolish private property) and orthodox laissez-faire economists (who treated land as indistinguishable from other forms of capital).

## Influence on British Liberalism

The work of Henry George awakened new interest in problems of poverty at a time when the limits of retrenchment and [[free-trade|Free Trade]] were becoming apparent (009-002). His ideas reached [[england|Britain]] through lecture tours in the 1880s and found a receptive audience among radical [[liberal-party|Liberals]] dissatisfied with Gladstonian orthodoxy. The English Land Restoration League, formed to promote Georgist principles, attracted figures who would later be active in the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] and the early [[labour-party|Labour Party]].

## Intellectual Position

George's thought occupied a distinctive position in the political economy of the late nineteenth century. Unlike the Socialists, he did not call for the collective ownership of all means of production — his critique was specifically directed at land monopoly. Unlike the orthodox [[liberal-party|Liberals]], he argued that [[free-trade|Free Trade]] alone was insufficient to address the structural causes of poverty. This "third way" approach prefigured elements of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberal]] synthesis that would emerge in [[england|Britain]] around the turn of the century, particularly in its willingness to use the taxing power of the state for redistributive ends.

## Legacy

Although the single tax movement did not achieve its legislative goals in [[england|Britain]], George's interrogation of the relationship between land ownership, economic rent, and [[economic-justice|social justice]] became a permanent element of progressive economic thought. His influence can be traced through the broader reorientation of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] toward an active, interventionist conception of the state that characterised the early twentieth century.
