# Whig Party

The Whig Party was the dominant political force in [[england|Britain]] from the late seventeenth century through the mid-nineteenth century, when it evolved into the [[liberalism|Liberal]] Party. The Whig tradition relied on constitutional precedent rather than abstract principle, valuing resistance to encroachments on parliamentary authority and continuity of development. This method was embodied in the Whig tradition and runs on to our own time as one of the elements that go to make up the working constitution of the Liberal mind — the Conservative element in [[liberalism|Liberalism]], valuable in resistance to encroachments but insufficient for purposes of reconstruction (003-002).

## Origins

The Whigs emerged in the 1670s during the Exclusion Crisis, opposing the succession of James, Duke of York (a Catholic) to the English throne. The name "Whig" — originally a term of abuse for Scottish Presbyterian rebels — came to denote those who supported parliamentary supremacy over royal prerogative. Their political philosophy drew on the constitutional settlement of 1688, which established the principle that the monarch governed by consent of [[parliament|Parliament]].

## The Whig Ascendancy

Throughout the eighteenth century, the Whigs dominated British politics under the leadership of figures such as Robert Walpole, the Pelham brothers, and Charles James Fox. They were associated with the defence of the Protestant succession, the supremacy of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]], and the expansion of British commercial and imperial power. The Whig aristocracy — great families such as the Russells, Cavendishes, and Fitzwilliams — provided much of the party's leadership and patronage.

## Reform and Evolution

The nineteenth century transformed the Whigs from an aristocratic faction into a party of [[social-reform|reform]]. The [[reform|Great Reform Act of 1832]], passed under Lord Grey's Whig government, extended the franchise and eliminated rotten boroughs. Subsequent Whig administrations under [[lord-melbourne]] and [[lord-john-russell]] pursued moderate [[social-reform|reform]]s in [[local-government|municipal government]], poor law administration, and factory regulation.

The Whigs took a leading part in early [[factory-act|factory legislation]], and Whig [[state|Government]]s passed the very important Acts of 1833 and 1847 regulating the employment of children and women in textile mills (004-005).

## Transition to the Liberal Party

By the 1850s, the Whig label was increasingly supplanted by "Liberal" as the party absorbed Peelites (free-trade [[tory-party|Conservatives]]), [[radicalism|Radicals]], and [[social-reform|reform]]ers. The formal transition occurred in 1859 when Lord Palmerston formed the first explicitly Liberal government. The [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]] inherited the Whig commitment to constitutional government while embracing a broader [[social-reform|reform]] agenda that included [[free-trade]], religious liberty, and the extension of the franchise.
