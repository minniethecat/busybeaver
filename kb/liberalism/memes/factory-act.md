## The Origins of Factory Legislation

Factory legislation in [[england|Britain]] began as a response to the appalling conditions of industrial labour — the long hours, dangerous machinery, and the widespread employment of young children in textile mills. "It is true that in the beginning factory legislation enjoyed a large measure of Conservative support," (004-005) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observed, noting a paradox: the party most associated with resistance to political [[social-reform|reform]] was often willing to regulate industrial conditions, while many [[liberal-party|Liberals]] of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] opposed such intervention as a violation of [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]]. This early alignment — [[tory-party|Tories]] regulating, [[liberal-party|Liberals]] abstaining — would gradually reverse as [[liberalism|Liberalism]] evolved toward a more socially conscious doctrine.

## The Ten Hours Act and Its Significance

The passage of the Ten Hours Act in 1847, limiting the working day for women and children to ten hours, was a watershed. "Since the disastrous period that preceded the [[repeal-of-corn-laws|Repeal of the Corn Laws]] and the passing of the Ten Hours' Act, [[social-reform|social improvement]] has been real and marked." (007-017) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] paired the Ten Hours Act with [[corn-laws|Corn Law repeal]] as the twin foundations of mid-century social progress: [[economic-liberty|free trade]] lowered the price of food, while factory legislation protected the worker from the most extreme forms of exploitation. Together, they demonstrated that [[economic-liberalism|economic liberalism]] and social protection were not incompatible but complementary.

## Factory Acts and the New Liberalism

By the early twentieth century, the principle of factory legislation had expanded far beyond its original scope. "They were to deal out factory acts, and establish wages boards," (009-001) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote of the New [[liberal-party|Liberals]], treating factory regulation as one element of a comprehensive programme of social [[social-reform|reform]]. The evolution from the limited Factory Act of 1833 to the comprehensive Factory and Workshop Act of 1878, and onward to the Trade Boards Act of 1909, traced the trajectory of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] itself — from a doctrine of negative freedom (freedom from state interference) to one of [[positive-liberty|positive freedom]] (freedom enabled by state-guaranteed minimum conditions).

## Liberty Through Restraint: The Philosophical Defence

The apparent contradiction between liberal principles and factory legislation — between freedom and regulation — was resolved by a deeper analysis of what liberty actually required. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] distinguished between "un[[social-liberty|social freedom]]," the right of a man to use his powers without regard to others, and "[[social-liberty|social freedom]]," which "rests on restraint" and is the only freedom that can be "enjoyed by all the members of a community" (004-007). The factory worker who accepted fourteen-hour shifts did so not freely but under economic compulsion: "Here was an operative possessed of no alternative means of subsistence seeking employment... If the bargain failed, the employer lost one man and had four hundred and ninety-nine to keep his mill going. During the same days the operative might have nothing to eat, and might see his children going hungry" (004-003). In such circumstances, the State's intervention was not a diminution but an enlargement of liberty — it restrained the employer's power to exploit, and thereby freed the worker from the necessity of accepting degrading terms.

This [[reason|reasoning]] extended from the most vulnerable to the general working population. "It began, as we saw, with the child, where the case was overwhelming. It went on to include the 'young person' and the woman... It drew the line at the adult male; and it is only within our own time... that legislation has avowedly undertaken the task of controlling the conditions of industry" (004-003). By the time [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote, "the law provides for the safety of the worker and the sanitary conditions of employment. It prescribes the length of the working day for women and children in factories and workshops, and for men in mines and on railways" (004-004). Each extension of regulation was driven by the same logic: "In the matter of contract true freedom postulates substantial equality between the parties" (004-003). Factory legislation was thus not a retreat from liberal principles but their logical unfolding — "a new and more concrete conception of liberty arose and many old presuppositions were challenged" (004-005).
