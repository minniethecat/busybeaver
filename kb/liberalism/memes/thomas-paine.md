# Thomas Paine

Thomas Paine (1737–1809) was an English-[[united-states|America]]n political activist and theorist whose writings on [[human-rights|natural rights]] were foundational to the Liberal tradition. He argued that [[human-rights|civil rights]] should correspond as closely as possible to [[human-rights|natural rights]], defining a civil right as "a natural right exchanged" (003-006). Paine worked within the same tradition as [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[rousseau]], conceiving political society as a voluntary arrangement among free and equal individuals for the mutual protection of their rights.

## Life and Career

Born in Thetford, Norfolk, Paine emigrated to the [[united-states|America]]n colonies in 1774 with a letter of introduction from [[benjamin-franklin]]. His pamphlet _Common Sense_ (1776) made the case for [[united-states|America]]n independence in plain, forceful prose and sold over 100,000 copies — an extraordinary figure for the time. During the Revolutionary War, he wrote the _[[united-states|America]]n Crisis_ papers to sustain morale, opening with the famous line: "These are the times that try men's souls."

Returning to Europe, Paine became embroiled in the revolutionary ferment of the 1790s. His _[[rights|Rights]] of Man_ (1791) was a defence of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] against [[edmund-burke]]'s conservative critique, arguing for popular sovereignty, representative government, and [[welfare-state|social welfare]]. Prosecuted for seditious libel, he fled to [[france|France]], where he was elected to the [[nation|Nation]]al Convention but later imprisoned during the Terror.

## Natural Rights and Civil Rights

Paine's political philosophy rested on a clear distinction between [[human-rights|natural rights]] (those inherent in human nature) and [[human-rights|civil rights]] (those recognised and protected by political society). For Paine, [[human-rights|civil rights]] should agree as nearly as possible with [[human-rights|natural rights]] — a civil right is a natural right exchanged. This contractual logic, shared with [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], made government a derivative institution whose legitimacy depended entirely on its protection of individual [[rights]].

Unlike [[edmund-burke|Burke]], who argued that rights were inherited through national tradition, Paine insisted on their universality. [[rights|Rights]] were not a gift of English history but the birthright of every human being. This universalism aligned Paine with the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] tradition and made him a hero to radicals across Europe and [[united-states|America]].

## Influence on Liberalism

Paine's influence on Liberal thought runs through several channels:

- **[[individualism|Individualism]]**: His insistence on the primacy of the individual over the state became a defining feature of classical [[liberalism]].
- **[[constitutionalism|Constitutionalism]]**: He argued that government must be limited by written constitutions expressing the will of the [[nation|people]].
- **Social Welfare**: In the second part of _[[rights|Rights]] of Man_, Paine proposed a system of [[old-age-pensions|old-age pension]]s, public education, and support for the poor — anticipating the [[social-reform|social liberalism]] of a later generation.
- **Republicanism**: His rejection of monarchy and hereditary privilege contributed to the democratic and republican currents within [[liberalism|Liberalism]].
