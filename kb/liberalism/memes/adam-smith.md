Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and political economist, widely regarded as the father of modern [[political-economy|economics]] and a foundational thinker of [[classical-liberalism|classical liberal]]ism. His two major works — *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) and *The Wealth of [[nation|Nation]]s* (1776) — together form a comprehensive account of human social life, grounded in the interactions between individual moral psychology and the spontaneous order of the market.

## Intellectual Context

Smith was a central figure of the Scottish [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]], a period of remarkable intellectual ferment that also produced [[david-hume]] (a close friend), Adam Ferguson, and Francis Hutcheson. Unlike the more radical and anti-clerical [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] of [[france|France]], the Scottish variant was more empirically grounded and less revolutionary in its political implications. Smith's thought was deeply shaped by the Newtonian method — he sought to discover the general laws governing the moral and economic realms, just as Newton had discovered the laws of the physical world.

In *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, Smith developed an account of moral psychology based on the concept of *sympathy* — the capacity to imagine oneself in another's situation and to share, in some measure, their feelings. This capacity, he argued, is the foundation of moral judgment, and the mechanism of the "impartial spectator" — the internalized perspective of a disinterested observer — provides the standard by which we evaluate our own conduct and that of others.

## Economic Liberalism and the Invisible Hand

Smith's most famous contribution to liberal thought is his analysis of the market economy in *The Wealth of [[nation|Nation]]s*. He argued that when individuals pursue their own economic interests in competitive markets, they are "led by an invisible hand" to promote the [[common-good|general good]] — a result that was no part of their original intention. "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner," Smith wrote, "but from their regard to their own interest."

This insight — that the market can coordinate independent actions into collectively beneficial outcomes without central direction — became the central argument for [[laissez-faire]] economic policy. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L.T. Hobhouse]], surveying the liberal tradition in 1911, observed that laissez-faire "emerged as a reaction against the mercantilist system of state-granted monopolies, protective tariffs, and detailed economic regulation," with Smith's *Wealth of [[nation|Nation]]s* providing "its intellectual foundations" (002-003).

## The Division of Labour and Free Trade

Smith's analysis of the division of labour — famously illustrated by the pin factory, where ten workers specializing in separate operations could produce vastly more than ten workers each making complete pins — demonstrated how specialization increases productivity. He extended this principle from the workshop to the international economy: just as individuals benefit from specializing and exchanging, so nations benefit from international trade, each producing what it can make most efficiently.

Free trade, in Smith's view, was not merely sound [[political-economy|economics]] but the economic expression of liberal principles of freedom and cooperation between [[nation|people]]s. The [[anti-corn-law-league]] and the [[manchester-school]] would later take up this argument with evangelical fervour, transforming British economic policy in the mid-nineteenth century. [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], the League's leaders, "framed [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as a moral cause — not merely sound [[political-economy|economics]] but the economic expression of liberal principles of freedom and cooperation between [[nation|people]]s" (004-002).

## Legacy and Influence

Smith's influence on subsequent liberal thought is difficult to overstate. His economic ideas were developed by David Ricardo, refined by [[john-stuart-mill]], and popularized by the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]. The free-trade consensus that dominated British politics from the repeal of the [[corn-laws]] in 1846 until the [[protectionism|Tariff Reform]] controversy of the early twentieth century was, in large measure, a Smithian inheritance.

Yet Smith was far from a doctrinaire advocate of laissez-faire in all circumstances. He recognized important exceptions — including [[common-good|public good]]s like defence, justice, and certain public works — where government action was necessary. The later caricature of Smith as a simplistic proponent of unregulated markets does violence to the subtlety of his actual position, which acknowledged both the power and the limits of market coordination.
