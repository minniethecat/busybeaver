## Definition and Scope

Personal liberty is the sphere of individual freedom that liberalism has historically sought to secure against arbitrary or coercive interference by the State. It is "a sphere most difficult to define, but the arena of the fiercest strife of passion and the deepest feelings of mankind" (002-010). At its foundation lies liberty of thought — freedom from inquisition into opinions formed within one's own mind — "the inner citadel where, if anywhere, the individual must rule" (002-010).

Since thought is largely a social product, liberty of thought is of little value without the liberty to exchange thoughts. Thus personal liberty extends naturally to liberty of speech, liberty of writing, printing, and peaceable discussion. Yet these rights are not without difficulty: there is a point at which speech becomes indistinguishable from action, and free speech may mean the right to create disorder. The limits of just liberty at this boundary are "easy to draw neither in theory nor in practice" (002-010), and they lead directly to the enduring tension between [[liberty]] and order.

## Personal Liberty and Religious Freedom

The same boundary questions arise in relation to religious liberty. That this liberty is absolute cannot be contended: no modern state would tolerate religious practices involving human sacrifice or the burning of witches. Civilised [[nation|people]]s governing less developed races habitually suppress such practices, however sincerely held. British law, while recognising some diversity in its imperial domains, does not permit every religious practice within its own jurisdiction. (002-010) The real battle for personal liberty, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] insists, has not been fought over absolute licence but over the reasonable scope of individual conscience against the claims of [[sovereignty|state authority]].

## Mill's Developmental Argument

[[John Stuart Mill]] gave the principle of personal liberty its most influential nineteenth-century formulation. For [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], personal liberty was not merely a negative protection but a condition of moral development. His treatment of representative government rested on the conviction that the right to vote was "not so much as a means to the enforcement of his claims upon society, but rather as a means of enforcing his personal responsibility for the actions of the community" (005-007). The problem of character was the determining issue: if [[nation|people]] could be spoon-fed with happiness, a benevolent [[despotism]] would be the ideal system. If they were to work out their own salvation, they must be summoned to share in directing the common life.

[[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] extended this developmental logic to the suffrage itself. To the objection that voters were ignorant and irresponsible, he answered that "to learn anything men must practise" — they must be trusted with more responsibility if they were to acquire the sense of responsibility. (005-007) The greater danger lay in excluding the mass of the population from civic rights and duties.

## The Tension with Political Liberty

[[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] recognised more clearly than any Liberal teacher before him the difference between the desire of the majority and the good of the community. He saw that the various rights for which [[liberal-party|Liberals]] pleaded might prove in practice hard to reconcile: "if personal liberty were fundamental it might only be imperilled by a so-called [[political-freedom|political liberty]] which would give to the majority unlimited powers of coercion" (005-007). [[democracy|Democracy]], unchecked, could produce a [[tyranny-of-the-majority|tyranny of the majority]] as oppressive as any despot. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] therefore became a pioneer of [[Proportional Representation]], seeking to make [[parliament|Parliament]] the reflection "not of a portion of the [[nation|people]], however preponderant numerically, but of the whole" (005-007).

## Personal Liberty and the Positive State

The [[Leonard Trelawny Hobhouse|later Liberal]] understanding of personal liberty moved beyond the purely negative conception. The collective activity of the community does not necessarily proceed by coercion or restraint; "the more securely it is founded on freedom and general willing assent, the more it is free to work out all the achievements in which the individual is feeble or powerless while combined action is strong" (006-011). Human progress is in the main social progress — the work of conscious or unconscious cooperation, in which voluntary association plays an increasing part.

[[state|The State]] is one form of association among others, distinguished by its coercive power, supremacy, and territorial claim. The "positive" conception of the State, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues, "not only involves no conflict with the true principle of personal liberty, but is necessary to its effective realization" (006-011). This reconciliation of personal freedom with collective action marks the furthest departure from the older, purely negative [[liberalism|Liberalism]].
