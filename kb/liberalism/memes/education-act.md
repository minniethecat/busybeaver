## Definition

The Education Acts of 1870 (the Forster Act) and 1902 (the Balfour Act) were the principal pieces of legislation that created and restructured the system of public elementary education in [[england|England]] and Wales. The 1870 Act established locally elected school boards with the power to build and maintain schools where voluntary (mainly church) provision was inadequate. The 1902 Act abolished the school boards, transferred responsibility for education to county and borough councils, and — most controversially — provided public funds ("rate aid") to denominational schools run by the Church of [[england|England]] and the Roman Catholic Church.

For [[liberal-party|Liberals]], the Education Acts crystallized the enduring tension between the demand for universal, secular public education on the one hand and the entrenched position of the established church in English public life on the other.

## Historical Development

The demand for universal public education was from the outset a Liberal demand. [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] argued that "a fraction of [military expenditure] devoted to education — free, secular, and universal — would do as much good as when spent on guns and ships it did harm" (004-199). Education was necessary, in their view, to "raise the standard of intelligence, and provide the substantial equality of opportunity at the start without which the mass of men could not make use of the freedom given by the removal of legislative restrictions" (004-199).

The Forster Act of 1870 was the first major legislative response to this demand. By creating school boards with the power to levy rates and build schools, it established for the first time a public system of elementary education. But it did so by "filling the gaps" in existing provision rather than by replacing the church schools that had hitherto dominated — a compromise that satisfied no party fully.

The Education Act of 1902, introduced by the Conservative government of [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse#Arthur Balfour|Arthur Balfour]], went further. It abolished the school boards and placed church schools on the rates — that is, it required local taxpayers (including Nonconformists) to fund the schools of the established church. The result, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] records, was political upheaval: "The Education Act of 1902 brought the Nonconformists into action" (009-400).

## Key Arguments

1. **Secular education as a condition of democracy**: The [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite argument that education should be "free, secular, and universal" rested on the premise that democratic citizenship requires an informed electorate. If the state outsources education to the church, it also outsources the formation of the citizen.

2. **The Nonconformist objection**: The 1902 Act was objectionable to Nonconformists not because they opposed education but because they were compelled to fund the propagation of doctrines they did not share. This was, in their view, a violation of the principle of religious liberty: the state was taxing conscience.

3. **The Conservative counter-argument**: Supporters of the 1902 Act argued that church schools provided the majority of existing places and that allowing them to collapse for want of funds would leave working-class children without any schooling at all. The practical necessity of maintaining provision, they contended, overrode the doctrinal objection.

4. **Education as the foundation of opportunity**: The Liberal argument for public education was always connected to the principle of equality of opportunity. As [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]'s programme recognized, the removal of legal restrictions on trade and employment was of little use to those who lacked the basic education to take advantage of them.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats the Education Act of 1902 as one of the events that precipitated the Liberal revival of the early twentieth century. In his account of the "shattering" of the old party system in the 1880s and 1890s, he identifies the Conservative programme of re-establishing "the Church at home by the endowment of doctrinal education" (009-396) as a defining element of the reactionary project against which [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had to reassert itself.

The Nonconformist mobilization against the 1902 Act was, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, part of a larger awakening. It was not merely a sectarian quarrel but a revival of the Liberal conviction that the state had no business enforcing religious conformity — an old Liberal principle, as old as [[john-locke|Locke's]] *Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]*, but one that had to be defended afresh in each generation.

> The Education Act of 1902 brought the Nonconformists into action. The Tariff Reform movement put Free Trade on its defence, and taught men to realize what the older economics of Liberalism had done for them.
> (009-400)

In this passage, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] places the education controversy on equal footing with the defence of [[economic-liberty|free trade]]. Both were, in his view, battles in the same war: the resistance of Liberal principles to the Conservative-imperial reaction.
