## Overview

John Locke is the philosopher with whom the liberal demand for the rule of law receives its classic formulation. In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L. T. Hobhouse]]'s analysis, Locke stands at the origin point of the juristic conception of liberty — the idea that freedom consists not in the absence of all restraint but in subjection to impartial, standing law rather than to the arbitrary will of any individual or institution.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] invokes Locke at three critical moments in his survey of liberal thought: as the voice of seventeenth-century constitutionalism, as the architect of the social [[social-contract|contract theory]] that underpinned natural-rights liberalism, and as the starting point of a tradition running "from Locke to [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[thomas-paine|Paine]]" that defined the terms of political debate for two centuries (003-006).

## Political Thought

### The Rule of Law

Locke's most enduring contribution to liberalism is his insistence that freedom and law are not opposites but complements. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes:

> "Freedom of men under government," says Locke, summing up one whole chapter of seventeenth-century controversy, "is to have a standing rule to live by, common to every one of that society and made by the legislative power erected in it."
> (002-003)

This formulation captures the essential liberal insight: arbitrary government is the enemy of liberty, and the remedy is not the abolition of government but its subjection to law. A government that acts by known rules, applicable equally to all, preserves freedom; a government that acts by caprice destroys it.

### The Social Contract

Locke's theory of political obligation rests on the idea of a contract between ruler and ruled. Political society, in this account, is "a convention arrived at for the specific purpose of securing a better order and maintaining the common safety" (003-005). [[state|Government]] is not ordained by divine right but instituted by human agreement, and its legitimacy depends on its fidelity to the purposes for which it was created.

Crucially, Locke held that the contract was "founded on a contract between king and [[nation|people]], a contract which was brought to an end if either party violated its terms" (003-005). This introduced a revolutionary principle into English political thought: the [[nation|people]] retain the right to judge whether their governors have broken trust, and to resist tyranny when they have.

### Natural Rights

Locke's political philosophy rests on a doctrine of [[human-rights|natural rights]] — rights belonging to individuals by virtue of their humanity, anterior to and independent of government. These rights set boundaries that legitimate government may not transgress. In the Lockean tradition Hobbes traces, the individual possessed rights "limited only by the corresponding rights of others" (003-006), and government existed to secure those rights more effectively than the individual could alone.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats Locke not as a figure requiring detailed exposition but as a fixed point in the liberal constellation — the philosopher who established the conceptual vocabulary within which subsequent liberal argument would be conducted. When nineteenth-century liberals spoke of "the [[human-rights|rights of man]]" or "government by consent," they were drawing on a Lockean inheritance, whether or not they acknowledged the debt.

The Lockean framework — [[human-rights|natural rights]], social contract, limited government, the rule of law — provided what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] calls the "order of ideas" (003-006) that shaped liberal thought from the Glorious Revolution through the [[united-states|America]]n and [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]s to the [[social-reform|reform]] movements of the nineteenth century. Even as later liberals, including [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] himself, came to regard the contractarian apparatus as historically and philosophically problematic, they continued to work within the normative commitments it had established.
