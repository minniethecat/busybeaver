## Overview

The United States occupied a unique position in the liberal imagination of nineteenth-century Europe. It was, simultaneously, the great experiment in democratic self-government — the proof that a large republic could sustain liberty without descending into anarchy or tyranny — and the great test case for liberal principles regarding race, slavery, and economic power. Hobhouse's references to America in *Liberalism* span three chapters and engage the country across fiscal, racial, economic, and international dimensions, treating it as both exemplar of liberal progress and source of liberal anxiety.

## Political and Historical Context

The American founding established the first large-scale modern republic, with a written constitution, a bill of rights, separation of powers, and a federal division of sovereignty. This constitutional architecture made America a model of organized liberty for European reformers who sought to reconcile freedom with order.

Hobhouse's most pointed historical reference to America concerns the origins of the republic itself. In his discussion of fiscal liberty, he observes that "George III brought things to a head in America" through arbitrary taxation, just as the Stuarts had done in England (002-007). The principle that emerged — "No taxation without representation" — crystallised the connection between fiscal liberty and political liberty. For Hobhouse, this episode demonstrated that responsible government was not merely a matter of written law but of "more direct and constant supervision" by the people over the executive (002-007).

The American federal structure, with its division of power between national and state governments, offered a model of institutional pluralism that resonated with Hobhouse's own emphasis on intermediate associations and local self-government as essential bulwarks of a free society. Where European states had developed through the gradual reform of older authoritarian structures, America had constituted itself through deliberation — a living proof that a people could design its own political forms.

## Role in Liberalism

America's role in Hobhouse's argument is distinctively ambivalent. On one side, the country stands as evidence of liberal progress on a continental scale. Surveying the reach of the liberal movement, Hobhouse places America alongside Europe as a theatre of liberal transformation: "The modern State as we see it in Europe outside Russia, in the British colonies, in North and South America ... is the old authoritarian society modified in greater or less degree by the absorption of Liberal principles" (002-029). America is, in this reading, a success story — one of the world regions where liberalism has substantively reshaped political life.

On the other side, America functions in Hobhouse's argument as a cautionary example. Its experience illustrates the limits and internal contradictions of the liberal project. Three areas receive particular attention: the unresolved problem of race, the threat of concentrated economic power, and America's place in the international order of liberal democracies.

## Race and Liberty: The American Contradiction

The deepest tension Hobhouse identifies in the American experiment is the coexistence of universalist liberal principles with racial subordination. In his discussion of local, racial, and national liberty, he addresses the American case directly:

> Somewhat similar questions arise about race, which many people wrongly confuse with nationality. So far as elementary rights are concerned there can be no question as to the attitude of Liberalism. Wherever a racial difference exposes a body of men to the denial of elementary rights, it is a Liberal duty to interfere. (002-024)

Hobhouse then turns specifically to comparative colonial and racial experience: "American experience of the negro gives, I take it, a more doubtful answer" than Cape Colony's experience with the Kaffir (002-024). He is cautiously pessimistic — not dismissing the possibility of black self-government, but warning that in the American case, "a specious extension of the white man's rights to the black may be the best way of ruining the black" (002-024). The passage reveals Hobhouse's troubled awareness that America exposed a vulnerability at the heart of the liberal tradition: the gap between declared principle and lived practice when it came to race.

## Economic Liberty: The Trust Problem

America also figures in Hobhouse's discussion of economic liberty as the prime example of the trust problem — the threat that private combinations could accumulate sufficient power to undermine the competitive conditions on which liberal economics depended. In his treatment of freedom of association, Hobhouse warns that the "history of some revolutionary societies, of some ecclesiastical organizations, even of some American trusts might be quoted to show that the danger is not imaginary" of associations becoming "a state within the state" (002-019).

The American trust problem reappears in the later discussion of laissez-faire, where Hobhouse notes that the fourth form of monopoly — industrial combination — "is one of which less has been heard in Great Britain than in the United States" (004-012). He observes that such combinations, predicted by Karl Marx as "the appointed means of dissolving the competitive system, have been kept at bay in this country by Free Trade," while "under Protection they constitute the most urgent problem of the day" (004-012). America, with its protective tariffs, here stood as a warning of what Britain might become if it abandoned free trade — a nation where competition defeated itself and private monopoly replaced the discipline of the open market.

## International Liberalism and America

In his final chapter on the future of liberalism, Hobhouse invokes America in the context of international relations and the prospects for democratic cooperation. Describing the imperialist worldview that had captured the British governing class, he notes that "the Englishman was the born ruler of the world. He might hold out a hand of friendship to the German and the American, whom he recognized as his kindred and who lived within the law" (009-001). The contrast is significant: America and Germany were the two nations Britain's imperialists regarded as peers rather than subjects — fellow members of the community of lawful, civilized states.

This sense of kinship carries forward into Hobhouse's constructive vision of a democratic international order. Envisioning a future in which Britain becomes "the centre of a group of self-governing, democratic communities," he identifies one of those communities as "a natural link with the other great commonwealth of English-speaking people" (009-013) — an unmistakable reference to the United States. America, in this vision, is not a rival but a partner in the democratic project, linked to Britain and the self-governing colonies by shared political values and a common language of liberty.

## Key Figures and Events

- **[[George III]]** — British monarch whose arbitrary taxation "brought things to a head in America" (002-007), precipitating the revolution that established the American republic and crystallised the principle of fiscal liberty.
- **[[Thomas Jefferson]]** — Architect of the Declaration of Independence, whose phrase "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" became a touchstone of the liberal conception of natural rights.
- **[[James Madison]]** — Principal author of the United States Constitution, whose design for separated powers and federalism offered a practical model of constitutional liberalism.
- **[[Alexis de Tocqueville]]** — French aristocrat whose *Democracy in America* (1835–1840) provided European liberals with the most influential analysis of American democratic society and its implications for liberty.
- **[[Abraham Lincoln]]** — American president whose leadership during the Civil War and whose Emancipation Proclamation addressed, in practice, the contradiction between liberal ideals and racial slavery that Hobhouse diagnosed in theory.

Key events:
- **[[american-revolution|American Revolution]]** (1775–1783) — The colonial rebellion that established the United States and gave political form to the principle of "no taxation without representation."
- **[[united-states-constitution|United States Constitution]]** (1787) — The founding document that made America a model of written constitutionalism and federal government.
- **[[american-civil-war|American Civil War]]** (1861–1865) — The conflict over slavery that tested whether a liberal republic "conceived in liberty" could endure its deepest internal contradiction.
