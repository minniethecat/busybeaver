## Definition and Liberal Defence

Representative government is the system in which the [[nation|people]], or a substantial portion of them, exercise political power through deputies elected at regular intervals. It is distinguished both from [[referendum|direct democracy]] (impractical in a large modern State) and from authoritarian or aristocratic government. For [[liberal-party|Liberals]], representative institutions are valued not merely as a mechanism for registering popular preferences but as a school of civic character.

[[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]]'s treatment of representative government laid down principles that would shape Liberal democratic thought for generations. If the adult citizen, male or female, has a right to vote, it is not so much as a means to the enforcement of their claims upon society as it is a means of their own mental and moral education. Participation in public affairs enlarges the mind, cultivates a sense of responsibility, and transforms the individual from a subject into a citizen. (005-007)

## The Educative Function of Democracy

The democratic thesis, in the Liberal understanding, is that the formation of a social will — the extension of intelligent interest in all manner of public things — is in itself a good, and more than that, it is a condition qualifying other goods. The extension of interest is not created by democratic forms of government, and if it neither exists nor can be brought into existence, democracy remains an empty form and may even be worse than useless. But where the capacity exists, the establishment of [[political-freedom|responsible government]] is the first condition of its development. (009-009)

This emphasis on the educative function of political participation is a distinctive feature of Liberal democratic theory. Representative government is valued not only for the decisions it produces — though [[liberal-party|Liberals]] believe these are generally better than those of despots — but for the effect it has on the character of citizens.

## Liberty and the Common Will

The question of liberty and the common will stands at the center of the Liberal theory of representative government. Social liberty rests on restraint — a person can be free to direct their own life only insofar as others are prevented from molesting and interfering. But the restraints that make liberty possible must themselves be authorized by the common will expressed through representative institutions. (007-002)

This creates a dynamic relationship: representative government is both the guardian of liberty and the expression of it. The challenge is to design institutions that are simultaneously democratic and liberal — that give effect to the popular will while protecting the rights of individuals and minorities against that will when it turns oppressive.

## The Problem of Scale

The modern State is a vast and complex organism. The individual voter feels lost among the millions, imperfectly acquainted with the devious issues and large problems of the day, and sensible of how little a solitary vote can affect their decision. What the citizen needs is organization with neighbours and fellow workers — intermediate bodies through which responsibility comes home. The development of democracy depends not only on [[universal-suffrage|adult suffrage]] and the supremacy of the elected legislature, but on all the intermediate organizations which link the individual to the whole. (009-009)

This insight connects representative government at the national level with local government, voluntary associations, and the broader fabric of civil society. Representative institutions work best when they are embedded in a dense network of intermediate bodies that connect the citizen to the State.
