## Definition

Constitutionalism is the doctrine that political power should be exercised within the constraints of a fundamental law that specifies the structure of government, demarcates the limits of its authority, and guarantees certain rights against governmental encroachment. It is distinguished from mere legality — the existence of laws — by the insistence that government itself must be governed by law, and that some norms stand above the ordinary legislative process.

In the English liberal tradition, constitutionalism developed less through the articulation of abstract principles than through the gradual accumulation of precedents, statutes, and conventions. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes:

> In England, it is true, where men are apt to be shy and unhandy in the region of theory, the Liberal movement has often sought to dispense with general principles. In its early days and in its more moderate forms, it sought its ends under the guise of constitutionalism.
> (003-002)

This pragmatism, while politically effective, had the disadvantage of obscuring the deeper philosophical commitments that constitutional arrangements were designed to protect.

## Historical Development

The roots of constitutionalism lie in the medieval idea that even the king is subject to law — a principle given institutional form in Magna Carta (1215) and developed through the common law tradition. The seventeenth-century struggle between Crown and [[parliament|Parliament]] — culminating in the Civil War, the Glorious Revolution of 1688, and the Bill of [[rights|Rights]] of 1689 — established the constitutional supremacy of [[parliament|Parliament]] and the principle that taxation requires consent.

The eighteenth century saw constitutionalism take a more explicitly philosophical form. [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]]'s *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748) articulated the theory of the separation of powers, arguing that liberty is most secure when legislative, executive, and judicial functions are lodged in distinct institutions capable of checking one another. The [[united-states|America]]n founding — with its written constitution, bill of rights, and elaborate system of [[separation-of-powers|checks and balances]] — represented the most ambitious attempt to instantiate these principles in the architecture of a state.

In nineteenth-century [[england|Britain]], constitutionalism provided the framework within which the liberal movement pursued its objectives. The [[reform|Reform]] Acts, the gradual extension of the franchise, and the curtailment of executive prerogative were all achieved through constitutional means — by changing the law rather than overthrowing it.

## Key Arguments

1. **Constitutionalism as the safeguard of liberty**: By subjecting government to law, constitutionalism ensures that power is exercised according to known rules rather than arbitrary will. The rule of law is thus the institutional expression of the liberal commitment to freedom from domination.

2. **The separation of powers**: Concentrating legislative, executive, and judicial authority in a single body or person creates the conditions for despotism. Constitutional government requires that power be divided among institutions with the capacity and incentive to resist encroachment by the others.

3. **Constitutionalism and democracy**: Constitutionalism and democracy are complementary but potentially in tension — democratic majorities may chafe at constitutional restraints that limit what they may decide. The art of liberal constitutional design lies in striking a balance that protects [[human-rights|fundamental rights]] without unduly constraining democratic [[self-determination|self-governance]].

:::info
**British vs. Continental Constitutionalism**: The British tradition of an "unwritten" constitution — a combination of statutes, conventions, and common law precedents — contrasts with the Continental and [[united-states|America]]n preference for codified constitutional documents. Both approaches aim at the same end: government limited by law. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s observation that English liberals "sought their ends under the guise of constitutionalism" reflects the British preference for incremental institutional [[social-reform|reform]] over revolutionary constitutional refounding.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] does not devote a separate chapter to constitutionalism as such, but the concept permeates the entire argument of *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]*. In Chapter III, he traces the "movement of theory" through which liberal thinkers progressively refined their understanding of the institutional conditions of liberty (003-002).

The constitutionalist strand of liberal thought receives particular attention in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s treatment of the [[whig-party|Whig tradition]] — the political current that, from the seventeenth century onward, sought to limit royal power through parliamentary supremacy, the [[judicial-independence|independence of the judiciary]], and the security of property rights. This tradition, for all its limitations — its suspicion of democracy, its defence of property qualifications for the franchise — established the institutional framework within which later, more democratic forms of liberalism could develop.
