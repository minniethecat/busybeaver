Classical liberalism is the original form of liberal political philosophy that emerged in Western Europe during the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]]. It emphasizes **[[personal-liberty|individual liberty]]**, **limited government**, the **rule of law**, **free markets**, and **equality before the law**. It is historically distinguished from social liberalism or "new liberalism" by its stronger commitment to laissez-faire economic policies and a more restricted conception of the state's role.

## Origins in Classical Antiquity

The roots of liberal governance can be traced to the city-states of classical antiquity. The Greek and [[italy|Italian city-states]] represented a new type of social organization distinct from earlier clan-based and communal structures. Unlike Oriental monarchies, these city-states were founded on civic right rather than kinship. [[state|Government]] was itself subject to law, and "law was the life of the state, willingly supported by the entire body of free citizens" (001-003). In this sense, the classical city-state constituted a community of free persons who governed themselves under established principles.

## Liberalism as Critique

Modern liberalism emerged as a protest against authoritarian rule. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L. T. Hobhouse]] observed, "the protest against that order, a protest religious, political, economic, social, and ethical, is the historic beginning of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" (001-008). In its early phases, liberalism was predominantly a critical and even destructive force, focused on removing obstacles to human progress — arbitrary government, class privilege, and economic restrictions. The question of whether liberalism was "at bottom a constructive or only a destructive principle" was a recurring theme throughout its development (001-008).

## The Rule of Law

A central tenet of classical liberalism is the **reign of law** as the guarantor of liberty. For the rule of law to secure genuine freedom, it must be impartial: "If there is one law for the [[state|Government]] and another for its subjects, one for noble and another for commoner, one for rich and another for poor, the law does not guarantee liberty for all" (002-005). [[liberty|Liberty]] in this respect implies equality, leading to liberal demands for judicial independence, accessible courts, and the abolition of class privileges.

## Liberty and Law

The relationship between law and liberty was central to classical liberal thought. A person "is not free when he is controlled by other men, but only when he is controlled by principles and rules which all society must obey, for the community is the true master of the free man" (002-009). However, classical liberals recognized two further problems: first, that the making of law might depend on the will of a sovereign or oligarchy; and second, that the content of the law might be "unjust and oppressive to some, to many, or to all except those who make it" (002-009).

## Social Hierarchy and Individual Opportunity

Classical liberalism opposed the hierarchical organization of society that reserved offices, occupations, and education to particular ranks or classes. In its extreme form, this constituted a caste system. The liberal struggle against such restrictions was simultaneously a struggle for **equality of opportunity** — "[[liberty|Freedom]] to choose and follow an occupation, if it is to become fully effective, means equality with others in the opportunities for following such occupation" (002-014). This principle of the "open road for talent" extended to restrictions of sex as well as class, forming "one application, and a very big one, of the 'open road for talent,' and to secure them both is of the essence of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" (002-015).

## Distinction from Modern Liberalism

Classical liberalism is often contrasted with modern social liberalism. While classical liberals favored minimal government intervention in economic affairs, social liberals came to accept a more active role for the state in addressing [[welfare-state|social welfare]], market failures, and economic inequality. The classical liberal tradition nevertheless provided the foundational principles — individual rights, rule of law, limited government — upon which subsequent liberal traditions built.
