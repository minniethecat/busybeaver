David Lloyd George (1863–1945) was a British Liberal statesman who, as Chancellor of the Exchequer (1908–1915) and later Prime Minister (1916–1922), introduced the most ambitious programme of social [[social-reform|reform]] in British history to that point. His "People's Budget" of 1909 and the [[national-insurance|National Insurance Act]] of 1911 made him the pre-eminent political architect of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]] — the doctrine that reconceived the state as an active agent of [[welfare-state|social welfare]] rather than merely a guarantor of negative liberty.

## Rise to Power

Lloyd George entered [[parliament|Parliament]] in 1890 as a Welsh radical, known for his opposition to the [[south-african-war|South African War]] (1899–1902) and his advocacy of Welsh disestablishment and land [[social-reform|reform]]. His political trajectory reflected the transformation of [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]] at the turn of the twentieth century: from the individualist, laissez-faire creed of [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] to a social liberalism that accepted an expanded role for the state in addressing poverty, inequality, and insecurity.

As President of the Board of Trade (1905–1908), Lloyd George established the [[wages-board|wages boards]] that set minimum wages in the so-called "sweated industries." These boards "exemplified the philosophy of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]]: the state need not own or manage industry, but it had a legitimate role in setting the conditions under which economic activity took place" (009-001).

## The People's Budget and the Constitutional Crisis

Lloyd George's 1909 Budget — the "People's Budget" — was a watershed in British political and constitutional history. It introduced a super-tax on high incomes, increased death duties on large estates, and imposed land taxes designed to capture the unearned increment in land values. The Budget was explicitly redistributive, designed to fund [[old-age-pensions]] (introduced in 1908) and an expanded naval programme.

The [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]], dominated by the [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] and the landed interest, rejected the Budget — breaking with the convention that [[house-of-lords|the Lords]] did not interfere with financial legislation passed by [[house-of-commons|the Commons]]. This triggered a constitutional crisis that culminated in the [[parliament|Parliament]] Act of 1911, which permanently removed [[house-of-lords|the Lords]]' power of veto over money bills and sharply curtailed its ability to block other legislation. "The electoral struggles over [[protectionism|Tariff Reform]] — the Liberal landslide of 1906, fought largely on the [[economic-liberty|free trade]] issue, and the constitutional crisis of 1909–1911 over Lloyd George's 'People's Budget' — demonstrated that protectionism remained a live political force" (009-001).

## National Insurance and Social Reform

The [[national-insurance|National Insurance Act of 1911]], introduced by Lloyd George as Chancellor, was "the first comprehensive scheme of its kind in [[england|Britain]] and a landmark of New Liberal social [[social-reform|reform]]." The Act provided for compulsory health insurance for workers and a limited system of unemployment insurance, funded by contributions from workers, employers, and the state. Its contributory principle — benefits were purchased through contributions, not granted as charity — distinguished it from the [[poor-law|poor law]] tradition and affirmed the liberal principle that social provision should respect the dignity and independence of the recipient.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|L.T. Hobhouse]], the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]]'s most important theorist, saw in Lloyd George's [[social-reform|reform]]s the practical realization of the liberal principle that liberty depends on social conditions: "They were to deal out factory acts, and establish wages boards," he wrote, describing the practical agenda that distinguished the [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of Asquith and Lloyd George from the laissez-faire [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of the Gladstonian era (009-001).

## Legacy

Lloyd George's social [[social-reform|reform]]s established the template for the British [[welfare-state]] that would be completed by the [[william-beveridge|Beveridge Report]] of 1942 and the post-war [[labour-party|Labour]] [[state|Government]]. By demonstrating that a liberal party could advance ambitious [[social-reform|social legislation]] without abandoning its commitments to [[economic-liberty|free trade]], [[civil-liberties|civil liberty]], and constitutional government, Lloyd George helped reconcile liberalism with the demands of a democratic industrial society.
