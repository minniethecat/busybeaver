Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher whose political theory profoundly influenced the Liberal movement. His conception of the social contract, in which political society arises from "a contract of the [[nation|people]] with one another" forming a general will, provided an alternative to [[john-locke|Locke]]'s contract between ruler and ruled (003-005). [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s ideas, along with those of [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[thomas-paine|Paine]], belonged to the school of thought that conceived political society as a voluntary restraint to which free and equal individuals submitted for specific purposes (003-005).

## The Social Contract

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s central contribution to Liberal political thought was his theory of the social contract. Unlike [[john-locke|Locke]], who conceived of a contract between king and [[nation|people]] that could be terminated if either party violated its terms, [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] held that political society was essentially a contract of the [[nation|people]] with one another — an arrangement by means of which, out of many conflicting individual wills, a common or general will could be formed (003-005). In this scheme, government was instituted as the organ of the general will, but remained subordinate to the sovereign [[nation|people]] from whom it derived its authority.

> Political society was a more artificial arrangement, a convention arrived at for the specific purpose of securing a better order and maintaining the common safety. It was, perhaps, as Locke held, founded on a contract between king and people, a contract which was brought to an end if either party violated its terms. Or, as in Rousseau's view, it was essentially a contract of the people with one another, an arrangement by means of which, out of many conflicting individual wills, a common or general will could be formed.
>
> (003-005)

## Natural Rights and Civil Rights

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] shared with [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[thomas-paine|Paine]] a common framework for understanding the transition from [[human-rights|natural rights]] to [[human-rights|civil rights]]. In the state of nature, individuals possessed rights limited only by the corresponding rights of others, but they could not effectively enforce them. By entering into political society, the individual gave up [[human-rights|natural rights]] and received in return [[human-rights|civil rights]] — something less complete in scope but more effective as resting on the guarantee of the collective power (003-005).

> In entering into the compact, the individual gave up so much of his rights as was necessitated by the condition of submitting to a common rule — so much, and no more. He gave up his natural rights and received in return civil rights, something less complete, perhaps, but more effective as resting on the guarantee of the collective power.
>
> (003-005)

This framework established a principle that became central to Liberal thought: [[human-rights|civil rights]] should agree as nearly as possible with [[human-rights|natural rights]], and any interference beyond what was necessary to accommodate conflicting claims constituted oppression. As [[thomas-paine|Paine]] would later express it, a civil right is a natural right exchanged (003-005).

## Place in the Liberal Tradition

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] belongs to the foundational period of Liberal theory, alongside thinkers from [[john-locke|Locke]] to [[thomas-paine|Paine]]. These theorists faced authoritarian government in church and state, and responded by vindicating the elements of personal, civil, and [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]]. They took their stand on the [[human-rights|rights of man]], arguing that government rested on human institution while the [[human-rights|rights of man]] rested on the law of Nature (003-005). While [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s concept of the general will would later be invoked by illiberal movements, his insistence on popular sovereignty and the contractual basis of political obligation provided essential building blocks for democratic [[liberalism|Liberalism]].
