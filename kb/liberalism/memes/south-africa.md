## Overview

South Africa became a defining test for [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]] at the turn of the twentieth century. The [[south-african-war|South African War]] (1899–1902) exposed deep divisions within [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] between imperialist and anti-imperialist wings, and the war's aftermath provided the occasion for a fundamental Liberal revival grounded in the principle of justice in international affairs.

## Political and Historical Context

At the close of the nineteenth century, the [[british-empire|British Empire]] confronted two Boer republics — the South African Republic (Transvaal) and the Orange Free State — whose independence was overrun by the forces of "plutocratic imperialism." The war that followed was marked by controversial methods including concentration camps, and it provoked fierce opposition from those [[liberal-party|Liberals]] who saw it as a violation of the Gladstonian principle that "reasons of State justify nothing that is not justified already by the human conscience." The conflict also revealed the limitations of imperial optimism: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote that "this mood of optimism did not survive the [[south-african-war|South African War]]," which "received its deathblow at Colenso and Magersfontein." (Chapter V, Chapter IX)

## Role in Liberalism

The [[south-african-war|South African War]] was the catalyst for the reawakening of Liberal conscience. [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Henry Campbell-Bannerman]]'s speech in the summer of 1901, in which he "set himself against the stream of militant sentiment and challenged in a classic phrase the methods of the war," is dated by [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] as the moment of Liberal revival. Though the speech was thought at the time to have ruined [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Campbell-Bannerman]]'s career, it "reinstated the idea of Right in the mind of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]." After the war, [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Campbell-Bannerman]] as Prime Minister used "the brief term of his power to reverse the policy of racial domination in South Africa and to prove the value of the old Gladstonian trust in the recuperative force of political freedom." The South African settlement became a demonstration that imperial relations could be conducted on principles of justice and national liberty rather than domination. (Chapter V, Chapter IX)

## Key Figures and Events

- **[[South African War]]** (1899–1902): The conflict that divided [[liberal-party|Liberals]] and ultimately revived the party's commitment to justice in international affairs
- **[[Henry Campbell-Bannerman]]**: Liberal leader whose 1901 speech against war methods catalysed the Liberal revival; as Prime Minister (1905–1908), granted responsible self-government to South Africa
- **[[William Ewart Gladstone]]**: His principle of justice between nations provided the moral framework for the anti-imperialist Liberal position; his earlier Majuba settlement after the First [[south-african-war|Boer War]] anticipated the later approach
