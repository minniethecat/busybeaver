## Definition

Individualism is the philosophical doctrine that treats the individual person — not the family, the tribe, the church, or the state — as the fundamental unit of moral and political analysis. It holds that social arrangements are ultimately justified by how they affect individual persons, and that each person possesses a sphere of autonomous action that collective authority may not legitimately invade.

In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, individualism is both the generative principle of liberalism and a doctrine that, taken to extremes, liberalism must transcend. He distinguishes between an "older individualism" that treats persons as atomistic, self-sufficient units and a richer conception that recognizes the social conditions of individual development (006-003).

## Historical Development

Individualism emerged as a distinct philosophical position during the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]], finding its most influential expression in the social contract theories of Hobbes, [[john-locke|Locke]], and [[rousseau|Rousseau]]. These thinkers sought to derive political obligation from the consent of individuals rather than from divine right or customary authority.

The [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] tradition gave individualism a different cast. [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s principle that each person counts for one and no one for more than one was radically individualistic in its egalitarianism, even as it rejected [[human-rights|natural rights]] theory. The [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite calculus treated society as an aggregate of individuals, each with an equal claim to have their pleasures and pains counted (003-024).

In the nineteenth century, the doctrine of laissez-faire gave individualism its most powerful practical expression. The school of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] maintained that individuals should be "absolutely free to compete with one another" on condition that the state "suppress force and fraud, keep property safe, and aid men in enforcing contracts" (004-006). This "older individualism" assumed that the unfettered pursuit of self-interest would, through the mechanism of competition, produce the greatest good for all.

However, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is among those liberals who recognized the limitations of this view:

> So far as Mill rested his case on the distinction between self-regarding actions and actions that affect others, he was still dominated by the older individualism.
> (006-003)

A mature liberalism, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues, must recognize that individuals develop and flourish only within a social context.

## Key Arguments

1. **The individual as the locus of value**: Individualism insists that only individuals experience pleasure, pain, fulfillment, and suffering. Social goods are ultimately reducible to goods for individual persons.

2. **The presumption of liberty**: Because each individual is the best judge of their own interests, the burden of proof always lies on those who would restrict individual freedom. The state must justify its interventions, not the individual her choices (004-006).

3. **The social critique of atomism**: The older individualism erred in treating persons as self-sufficient atoms. In reality, "a man can be free to direct his own life only in so far as others are restrained from interfering with him" (007-002). Individual freedom depends on social cooperation and legal order.

:::info
**Individualism vs. [[socialism|Collectivism]]**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] seeks a middle path between atomistic individualism (which ignores social interdependence) and collectivism (which submerges the individual in the group). His "Liberal [[socialism|Socialism]]" aims to preserve the moral primacy of the individual while recognizing the social conditions necessary for individual flourishing.
:::

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats individualism as both the source of liberalism's strength and the root of its internal tensions. The older individualism — associated with [[human-rights|natural rights]] theory and [[economic-liberalism|laissez-faire economics]] — gave liberalism its critical edge against arbitrary power and its commitment to [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]]. But it also produced an inadequate understanding of social life, treating society as a mere aggregate of independent atoms.

The mature liberal position, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] develops it, retains the moral commitment to the individual while recognizing that "social liberty rests on restraint" (007-002). Individual development requires social conditions — education, economic security, legal protection — that only collective action can provide. The task of liberalism is to harmonize individual freedom with social cooperation, not to choose between them.
