# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

[[rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose political philosophy shaped the intellectual foundations of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] and the development of modern democratic thought. His conception of the social contract and the general will provided a theoretical alternative to the older [[john-locke|Locke]]an contract between ruler and ruled.

## Social Contract Theory

In the early liberal tradition, political society was understood as an artificial arrangement established for maintaining security and order. Rousseau transformed this framework by relocating sovereignty from the monarch to the [[nation|people]] themselves:

> It was essentially a contract of the people with one another, an arrangement by means of which, out of many conflicting individual wills, a common or general will could be formed. A government might be instituted as the organ of this will, but it would, from the nature of the case, be subordinate to the people from whom it derived authority. The people were sovereign. The government was their delegate. (003-005)

This reorientation had profound implications. Where earlier [[social-contract|contract theory]] preserved a vertical relationship between ruler and ruled, Rousseau's model was fundamentally horizontal: citizens bound themselves to one another, and any government they instituted remained at all times answerable to the sovereign [[nation|people]].

## The General Will

Central to Rousseau's political philosophy is the concept of the general will (*[[general-will|volonté générale]]*) — the collective interest of the body politic, distinct from the mere aggregation of private interests. Through the social contract, individuals surrender their natural liberty in exchange for [[civil-liberties|civil liberty]], gaining the protection of the collective power while remaining free in a new, civic sense.

## Influence on Liberal Thought

Rousseau's ideas spread across the eighteenth-century Atlantic world, influencing both the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] and radical political thought in [[england|Britain]] and [[united-states|America]]. His work stood alongside that of [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[thomas-paine|Paine]] in shaping a tradition grounded in [[human-rights|natural rights]]:

> Those who from Locke to Rousseau and Paine worked with this order of ideas agreed in conceiving political society as a restraint to which men voluntarily submitted themselves for specific purposes. Political institutions were the source of subjection and inequality. Before and behind them stood the assemblage of free and equal individuals. (003-006)

This shared framework — the primacy of individual rights, the voluntary nature of political association, and the imperative of popular sovereignty — became a defining feature of the liberal tradition.
