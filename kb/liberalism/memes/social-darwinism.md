# Social Darwinism

**Social Darwinism** is a sociological theory that applies the biological concepts of natural selection and "survival of the fittest" to human societies. Associated principally with the philosopher [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]], it became a prominent ideology in the late nineteenth century, providing a pseudo-scientific justification for laissez-faire capitalism, imperial conquest, and opposition to [[welfare-state|social welfare]] legislation.

## Spencer and the Liberal Tradition

L.T. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] references [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] in a discussion of the paradox of imperial domination. The passage draws on [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s insight that the conqueror is himself bound by the act of conquest:

> The Assyrian conqueror, Mr. Herbert Spencer remarks, who is depicted in the bas-reliefs leading his captive by a cord, is bound with that cord himself. He forfeits his liberty as long as he retains his power. (007-004)

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] deploys this image to argue that a nation that rules others by force cannot itself remain free — a critique that applies both to imperialism abroad and to coercive social relations at home.

## The Limits of Laissez-Faire Individualism

The Social Darwinist worldview overlapped substantially with the doctrine of laissez-faire, which held that the State should merely "hold the ring" — suppressing force and fraud, keeping property safe, and aiding men in enforcing contracts — while leaving individuals absolutely free to compete with one another. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observed, this theory rested on the assumption that on such conditions "men should be absolutely free to compete with one another, so that their best energies should be called forth, so that each should feel himself responsible for the guidance of his own life, and exert his manhood to the utmost" (015-018).

Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] challenged this assumption at its root. The laissez-faire framework supposed a world of equal bargainers. In reality, the industrial system created vast asymmetries of power: "Here was the owner of a mill employing five hundred hands. Here was an operative possessed of no alternative means of subsistence seeking employment" (015-012). In such a context, the "freedom" to contract was largely illusory — the operative who refused the employer's terms risked seeing his children go hungry, while the employer who lost one hand could carry on with four hundred and ninety-nine. "Where was the [[positive-liberty|effective liberty]] in such an arrangement?" (015-012)

## Social Freedom versus Unsocial Freedom

Drawing on the analysis of the industrial bargain, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] articulated a crucial distinction between two conceptions of freedom. Un[[social-liberty|social freedom]] is "the right of a man to use his powers without regard to the wishes or interests of anyone but himself" — a theoretical possibility for an isolated individual but a practical impossibility for "a plurality of individuals living in mutual contact" (015-020). Social freedom, by contrast, rests on restraint: it is "the freedom to choose among those lines of activity which do not involve injury to others" (015-020). The restraint of the aggressor is the freedom of the sufferer, and it is only by such mutual restraint that "they as a whole community gain freedom in all courses of conduct that can be pursued without ultimate social disharmony" (015-022).

This distinction supplied the philosophical basis for [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s rejection of the Social Darwinist position. The survival of the fittest, applied to society, maximized un[[social-liberty|social freedom]] — the liberty of the strong to dominate the weak. But it did nothing to advance the [[social-liberty|social freedom]] that could be "enjoyed by all who dwell together" (015-022).

## Tension with Liberal Principles

Social Darwinism occupied an ambiguous position in relation to the Liberal tradition. On one hand, its emphasis on individual competition aligned with the [[classical-liberalism|classical liberal]] defence of laissez-faire. [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] himself was a vigorous critic of state intervention, and his evolutionary individualism provided a philosophical foundation for opposition to the expanding [[social-reform|social legislation]] of the late Victorian and Edwardian periods.

On the other hand, the "new liberalism" that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] championed explicitly rejected the Social Darwinist premise. Thinkers such as [[thomas-hill-green|T.H. Green]] and [[arnold-toynbee|Arnold Toynbee]] argued that social cooperation, not unrestrained competition, was the true engine of human progress — and that the state had a positive role to play in creating the conditions for individual flourishing. Where social Darwinism saw inequality as the natural and desirable outcome of superior fitness, the new liberals saw it as a product of unequal bargaining power that the community had both the right and the duty to redress.
