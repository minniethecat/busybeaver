Individual rights are the moral and legal entitlements that protect the autonomy and dignity of persons against encroachment by the state, other institutions, and other individuals. They constitute a core commitment of the liberal tradition, which holds that individuals possess certain fundamental claims that no government may legitimately violate.

## Liberty of Thought

At the center of the liberal conception of individual rights stands **freedom of thought**. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] described this as "the inner citadel where, if anywhere, the individual must rule" (002-010). [[liberty|Liberty]] of thought is not merely one right among others but the foundational right upon which other liberties depend — "liberty of thought is of the first importance" because without it, no other freedom can be meaningfully exercised (002-010). It is the indispensable condition for the "appeal of reason, of imagination, of social feeling" (006-004).

## Rights Against Hierarchical Restraints

[[liberalism|Liberalism]] has consistently opposed "those restraints on the individual which flow from the hierarchic organization of society" (002-014). These restraints, which reserve offices, occupations, and opportunities to particular ranks or classes, undermine the principle that individuals should be judged on their merits rather than their birth. The liberal commitment to individual rights thus entailed a struggle against caste systems, corporate monopolies, and class-based restrictions on economic and social participation.

## Rights of Women

The extension of individual rights to women was a natural implication of liberal principles. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argued that "rendering the wife a fully responsible individual, capable of holding property, suing and being sued" (002-021) was essential to the liberal project. The recognition of women's individual rights marked a significant expansion of the liberal conception of personhood beyond its historically male-centered origins.

## Elementary Rights and Nationality

The universality of individual rights does not negate the significance of collective identities. "So far as elementary rights are concerned there can be no question as to the right of small nationalities to equal treatment with large ones" (002-024), reflecting the liberal conviction that [[human-rights|fundamental rights]] apply equally regardless of national origin or group membership. This principle has been central to liberal arguments for minority protections and international human rights frameworks.

## Rights of Man

The liberal tradition took its stand "on the [[human-rights|rights of man]]" (003-005) as a universal foundation for political order. This commitment, articulated in documents from the French Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man to the Universal Declaration of [[human-rights|Human Rights]], holds that individuals possess rights by virtue of their humanity, not by grant of the state. However, the liberal understanding of individual rights evolved from a narrow focus on the "isolated individual" toward a recognition that individuals are also "members of a community" (003-006) whose rights must be understood in social context.
