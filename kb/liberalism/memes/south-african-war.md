## Background

By the close of the nineteenth century — which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] called "the age of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" — Liberal ideas faced their most severe crisis. The movement's faith in itself was "waxing cold" (009-001). [[liberalism|Liberalism]] appeared to have done its work, fossilised between "two very active and energetically moving grindstones — the upper grindstone of plutocratic imperialism, and the nether grindstone of social democracy" (009-001). It was in this atmosphere of Liberal decline that imperial enthusiasm swept through British politics, drawing even prominent [[liberal-party|Liberals]] toward the assertive [[imperialism]] that characterised the era.

## The Outbreak of War

The South African War (1899–1902) pitted the [[british-empire|British Empire]] against the Boer republics of the Transvaal and the Orange Free State. The conflict was rooted in tensions over mineral wealth, [[political-freedom|political rights]] for British subjects in the Boer republics, and the broader ambitions of British imperial strategy. For [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]], the war posed an acute moral challenge. "It was with the outbreak of the South African War that the new development of Conservative policy first compelled the average Liberal to consider his position" (009-003).

Within the Liberal ranks, many of the most influential men had "passed, without consciousness of the transition, under the sway of quite opposite influences. They were becoming Imperialists in their sleep, and it was only as the implications of [[imperialism|Imperialism]] became evident that they were awakened" (009-003). The Liberal Imperialists, led by figures like [[Lord Rosebery]], had accommodated themselves to the expansionist mood; the war forced a reckoning.

## Campbell-Bannerman and the Liberal Revival

The moral and political turning point came not from the Liberal Imperialists but from the party's conscience. "It needed the shock of an outspoken violation of right to stir him; and we may date the revival of the idea of justice in the party as an organized force from the speech in the summer of 1901 in which Sir [[Henry Campbell-Bannerman]] set himself against the stream of militant sentiment and challenged in a classic phrase the methods" of the war (009-003). [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Campbell-Bannerman]]'s denunciation of British conduct — particularly the use of concentration camps for Boer civilians — galvanised the party's moral opposition and marked the beginning of the Liberal recovery.

This speech was more than a tactical intervention. It reasserted the principle of [[justice]] as the foundation of Liberal foreign policy, rejecting the argument that imperial necessity could override moral constraint. It "challenged in a classic phrase the methods" (009-003) of a war prosecuted with little regard for the rights of non-combatants or the standards of civilised conduct.

## Significance for Liberal Theory

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], the South African War crystallised the tension between [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and [[imperialism]] that runs throughout the final chapter of *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]*. The war demonstrated that the imperial project was not merely an external adventure but a moral corruption of Liberal principles: militarism abroad, authoritarianism at home, and the erosion of the rule of [[law]] that [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had fought to establish. "Had [[england|Great Britain]] been an island in the mid-Pacific," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] reflects, "the onward movement would have been rapid and undeviating in its course" (009-002). The imperial entanglements of a global power distorted and delayed the natural development of democratic [[liberalism|Liberalism]].

The war also served as a filter, separating those [[liberal-party|Liberals]] who were willing to subordinate principle to power from those who insisted on the primacy of right. The Liberal revival that culminated in the 1906 landslide was forged in the moral crucible of the South African War, under [[henry-campbell-bannerman|Campbell-Bannerman]]'s leadership — a revival that, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] insisted, made the distinctive ideas of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] once again a "permanent function" in British political life. (009-004)
