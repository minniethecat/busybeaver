## Definition

Fiscal liberty is the freedom from arbitrary taxation and the corresponding right of the [[nation|people]], through their representatives, to control public finances. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explains, it is "closely connected with [[civil-liberties|juristic liberty]], and more widely felt in everyday life" (002-006). While [[civil-liberties|juristic liberty]] concerns protection from arbitrary arrest and punishment, fiscal liberty touches every citizen's material interests directly.

Fiscal liberty raises "more searching questions than [[civil-liberties|juristic liberty]]" (002-006). Unlike ordinary laws, which may remain stable for long periods, taxation must be adjustable from year to year in accordance with public needs. It is, "properly considered, for the Executive rather than the Legislature" (002-006). The liberty of the subject in fiscal matters therefore demands more than fixed laws — it requires the "more direct and constant supervision" (002-006) of the executive by the [[nation|people]]'s representatives.

## Historical Significance

The demand for fiscal liberty has been a primary driver of constitutional development. The [[stuart|Stuarts]] "brought things to a head in this country by arbitrary taxation" (002-006), and the ensuing constitutional struggles of the seventeenth century established the principle of parliamentary control over finance. [[george-iii|George III]] "brought things to a head in [[united-states|America]] by the same infallible method" (002-006), and the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]] was fought substantially on the principle of fiscal consent. The immediate cause of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] was similarly "the refusal of the nobles and the clergy to bear their share of the financial burden" (002-006).

In each case, the issue was not taxation as such but taxation without consent — the arbitrary imposition of financial burdens by a power not accountable to those who bore them.

## Link to Political Liberty

Fiscal liberty is inseparable from [[political-freedom|political liberty]] because fiscal control is inherently an executive function requiring continuous oversight rather than a one-time legislative act. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] states: "Hence the liberty of the subject in fiscal matters means the restraint of the Executive, not merely by established and written laws, but by a more direct and constant supervision. It means, in a word, [[political-freedom|responsible government]]" (002-006).

This connection explains why "we have more often heard the cry, 'No taxation without representation,' than the cry, 'No legislation without representation'" (002-006). From the seventeenth century onwards, "fiscal liberty was seen to involve what is called [[political-freedom|political liberty]]" (002-006). The demand for control over taxation became the practical engine driving the broader demand for democratic government.

## Legacy

Fiscal liberty remains a foundational principle of liberal democracy. The requirement that governments obtain parliamentary approval for taxation and expenditure — the power of the purse — is among the most durable checks on executive power. The principle that there should be no taxation without representation has expanded from its origins in resistance to royal absolutism to underpin the democratic expectation that all who pay taxes should have a voice in how they are spent.
