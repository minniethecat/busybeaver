## Definition and Core Principle

Economic justice, in the framework of [[new-liberalism|modern Liberalism]], is the principle that the economic organization of society should secure to every social function the reward sufficient to stimulate and maintain its effective performance through the life of the individual. It is the "equation between function and sustenance" — what is due not only to each individual but to each function, social or personal, engaged in the performance of useful service. (008-014)

This conception moves beyond both pure laissez-faire individualism and revolutionary socialism. It recognizes a social element in both value and production, holding that in modern industry very little can be accomplished by unaided individual effort. (008-014)

## The Social Element in Wealth

A central tenet of economic justice is that there is a social element in value and a social element in production. [[labour-party|Labour]] is minutely divided, and in proportion as it is divided it is forced to be cooperative. Men produce goods to sell, and the rate of exchange — price — is fixed by relations of demand and supply determined by complex social forces. Every producer makes use of the whole available means of civilization: the machinery devised by other minds, the human apparatus that is the gift of acquired civilization. (008-014)

Society thus provides conditions or opportunities of which one person will make much better use than another. The use to which these opportunities are put is the individual or personal element in production, which is the basis of the personal claim to reward. To maintain and stimulate this personal effort is a necessity of good economic organization. (008-014)

## Individualism Versus the Social Factor

An individualism which ignores the social factor in wealth will deplete the national resources, deprive the community of its just share in the fruits of industry, and result in a one-sided and inequitable distribution of wealth. Economic justice seeks to render what is due not only to each individual but to each function, social or personal, engaged in the performance of useful service — and this due is measured by the amount necessary to stimulate and maintain the efficient exercise of that useful function. (008-014)

> This equation between function and sustenance is the true meaning of economic equality.
>
> (008-014)

## The Primary Needs as the First Application

The first and greatest application of the principle of economic justice is to the primary needs of all citizens. If the function of society is to secure to all normal adult members the means of earning by useful work the material necessaries of a healthy and efficient life, then the conditions of social efficiency mark the minimum of industrial remuneration. Where such means fail without deliberate State action, they must be secured by means of deliberate State action. (008-024)

This standard of minimum remuneration is not charity but justice. Unlike private philanthropy — which makes one person dependent on the good graces of another and tends to diminish the value of independent effort — a form of support on which a person can count as a matter of legal right does not have the same destructive effect. It is not a handicap in favour of the unsuccessful, but an equal distance deducted from the race against fate. (008-010)

## Taxation and the Recovery of Social Value

The true function of taxation, under the principle of economic justice, is to secure to society the element in wealth that is of social origin — more broadly, all that does not owe its origin to the efforts of living individuals. When taxation based on these principles is utilized to secure healthy conditions of existence to the mass of the [[nation|people]], it is no case of robbing Peter to pay Paul. A tax which enables the State to secure a certain share of social value is not something deducted from that which the taxpayer has an unlimited right to call their own, but rather a repayment of something which was all along due to society. (008-022)

## The Wage Question and Efficiency

Against the objection that some workers are not worth a full efficiency wage, the Liberal reply is that the price which naked labour without property can command in bargaining with employers who possess property is no measure of the addition which such labour can actually make to wealth. The bargain is unequal, and low remuneration is itself a cause of low efficiency, which in turn reacts unfavourably on remuneration. Up to the efficiency minimum, a general increase of wages would positively increase the available surplus, whether that surplus goes to individuals as profits or to the State as national revenue. (008-025)

## Economic Sovereignty

The principle of economic justice implies that the State is vested with a certain overlordship over property in general and a supervisory power over industry in general. This principle of economic sovereignty stands side by side with that of economic justice as a no less fundamental conception of economic [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. For here, as elsewhere, liberty implies control. (008-027)

> The central point of Liberal economics, then, is the equation of social service and reward. This is the principle that every function of social value requires such remuneration as serves to stimulate and maintain its effective performance; that everyone who performs such a function has the right, in the strict ethical sense of that term, to such remuneration and to no more; that the residue of existing wealth should be at the disposal of the community for social purposes.
>
> (008-027)

## Role in Modern Liberalism

Economic justice underlies the movement of modern Liberal opinion. It represents a departure from the restricted view of government associated with the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]], which confined the State to maintaining order and protecting property. The newer [[liberalism|Liberalism]] recognizes that the bargain between labour and capital is unequal, and that positive measures — including taxation, wage regulation, and social provision — are required to achieve the equation of function and sustenance. (008-010) (008-025)
