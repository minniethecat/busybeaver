## Definition and Foundations

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], the rule of law — or the "reign of law" — is the most elementary condition of liberty and therefore the historical starting point of the Liberal movement. "Both logically and historically," he writes, "the first point of attack is arbitrary government, and the first liberty to be secured is the right to be dealt with in accordance with law" (002-003). Without this guarantee, the individual is "rightless" — devoid of any standing against the power of the state. The concept, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s presentation, draws directly on the seventeenth-century struggles between [[parliament|Parliament]] and the Crown in [[england|England]], which produced the [[Petition of Right]] (1628) and the [[habeas-corpus|Habeas Corpus Act]] (1679). "It is significant of much," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes, "that this first step in liberty should be in reality nothing more nor less than a demand for law" (002-003).

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] invokes [[john-locke|Locke]]'s canonical formulation of the principle: "[[liberty|Freedom]] of men under government is to have a standing rule to live by, common to every one of that society and made by the legislative power erected in it" (002-003). Three elements are embedded in this definition: law must be *standing* (not ad hoc or retrospective), *common* (applying equally to all members of society), and *legislatively made* (not imposed by executive decree).

## The Impartiality Requirement

The reign of law, however, is not sufficient to guarantee liberty on its own. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies a "tacitly postulated" condition that must be made explicit: the law must be impartial. "In assuming that the reign of law guarantees liberty to the whole community, we are assuming that it is impartial. If there is one law for the [[state|Government]] and another for its subjects, one for noble and another for commoner, one for rich and another for poor, the law does not guarantee liberty for all" (002-005).

From this principle, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] derives a series of institutional demands that have shaped the Liberal programme: "Hence the demand for the [[judicial-independence|independence of the judiciary]] to secure equality as between the [[state|Government]] and its subjects. Hence the demand for cheap procedure and accessible courts. Hence the abolition of privileges of class. Hence will come in time the demand for the abolition of the power of money to purchase skilled advocacy" (002-005). [[liberty|Liberty]], in this respect, "implies [[equality]]" — the formal equality of all persons before the law is not a separate principle from liberty but an intrinsic component of it.

## The Limits of Legal Liberty

The rule of law is foundational, but [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is careful to note that it is only a beginning. "We have seen that the reign of law is the first step to liberty," he writes, but immediately qualifies: "There may be law, and there may be no attempt, such as the Stuarts made, to set law aside, yet (1) the making and maintenance of law may depend on the will of the sovereign or of an oligarchy, and (2) the content of the law may be unjust and oppressive to some, to many, or to all except those who make it" (002-009).

The first problem points to [[political-liberty|political liberty]] — the question of who makes the law and by what authority. A society may operate entirely through formal legal procedures, yet if those procedures are controlled by a narrow oligarchy, the rule of law provides only formal, not substantive, liberty. The second problem points to the content of law itself — the historical struggle of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] has been not only to secure "liberty through law, but by the abolition of bad law and tyrannical administration" (002-009). This insight distinguishes the Liberal conception of the rule of law from a merely positivist one: law must not only be properly enacted and impartially applied, but also respect the substantive rights that make liberty meaningful.

## Historical Context

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] illustrates the absence of the rule of law through contemporary and historical examples of "arbitrary government" — "punishment by 'administrative' process in [[russia|Russia]] at the present day; imprisonment by *lettre de cachet* in [[france|France]] under the *ancien régime*; all executions by so-called martial law in times of rebellion, and the suspension of various ordinary guarantees of immediate and fair trial in [[ireland|Ireland]]" (002-003). These examples share a common structure: the executive power operates outside the regular courts of law, imposing penalties without defined offences, regular trial, or the procedural guarantees that protect the individual from the state.

The contrast, in each case, is the English constitutional tradition — the principle, won in the seventeenth century, that the Crown may not imprison, punish, or tax without parliamentary authority and due process of law. "Arbitrary government in this form was one of the first objects of attack by the English [[parliament|Parliament]]" (002-003).
