## Definition

The Theory of the Natural Order is, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s taxonomy, the first of the great theoretical frameworks underpinning Liberal thought. It is "the first of these types" of liberal theory (003-004) — an [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] doctrine that sought to ground political and moral claims not in divine revelation or inherited tradition but in the observable regularities of nature, accessible to human reason.

The theory served a critical function in the struggle against authoritarian government. While established authority "claimed supernatural sanction and divine ordinance," Liberal theory "replied in effect that the [[human-rights|rights of man]] rested on the law of Nature, and those of government on human institution" (003-004). This inversion was revolutionary: government, not the individual, was the artificial creation; the individual in a state of nature was the primordial reality.

## The Social Contract

The theory of natural order provided the philosophical foundation for social contract thinking. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explains, "the oldest 'institution' in this view was the individual, and the primordial society the natural grouping of human beings under the influence of family affection, and for the sake of mutual aid" (003-004). Political society, by contrast, was "a more artificial arrangement, a convention arrived at for the specific purpose of securing a better order and maintaining the common safety" (003-004).

Different thinkers developed the contract idea in different directions. For [[john-locke|Locke]], political society was "founded on a contract between king and [[nation|people]], a contract which was brought to an end if either party violated its terms" (003-004). For [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], it was "essentially a contract of the [[nation|people]] with one another, an arrangement by means of which, out of many conflicting individual wills, a common or general will could be formed" (003-004). In both versions, government was subordinate to the [[nation|people]] from whom it derived its authority. "The [[nation|people]] were sovereign. The government was their delegate" (003-004).

## The Harmony of Nature

The theory of natural order extended beyond [[political-freedom|political rights]] into the economic sphere, generating the doctrine of natural harmony. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] describes how "in the course of the eighteenth century, and particularly in the economic sphere, there arose a view that the conflict of wills is based on misunderstanding and ignorance, and that its mischiefs are accentuated by governmental repression" (003-008). At bottom, the theory held, "there is a natural harmony of interests" (003-008). If government maintained external order, suppressed violence, assured property, and enforced contracts, "the rest will go of itself" (003-008).

This [[natural-harmony|harmony of interests]] theory completed the natural order framework: individual rights and [[welfare-state|social welfare]] were not in tension but were, in the long run, coincident. Self-interest, enlightened and unfettered, would lead each person to conduct that served the [[common-good|public good]].

## Weaknesses and Legacy

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies "fundamental weaknesses which have both a historical and a speculative significance" (003-004) in the natural order theory. The Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man, while specifying the rights of liberty, property, security, and resistance to oppression, provided no clear principle for determining their limits. What was the supposed law of nature — "when was it written, and by whose authority?" (003-022). On what ground do we maintain that men are free or equal?

These difficulties, articulated most forcefully by [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] and the [[utilitarianism|Utilitarian]] school, led to the formation of the second major type of Liberal theory. Yet the natural order tradition left an enduring legacy: the conviction that political authority requires justification, that individuals possess rights prior to government, and that social arrangements should answer to reason rather than tradition.
