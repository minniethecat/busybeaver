## Biography

Jeremy Bentham was born in [[london|London]] in 1748. A child prodigy, he began studying Latin at age three and entered Oxford at twelve. Trained as a lawyer, he became disillusioned with the English legal system and devoted his life to philosophical and legal [[social-reform|reform]].

Bentham was the intellectual leader of the Philosophical [[radicalism|Radicals]], a group of [[social-reform|reform]]ers who applied [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] principles to law, politics, and social policy. His influence extended throughout the nineteenth century through disciples including [[james-mill|James Mill]] (whom he mentored) and [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] (whom he educated indirectly through [[james-mill|James Mill]]'s tutelage).

## Thought and Contributions

Bentham's central principle — the greatest happiness of the greatest number — provided a rational criterion for evaluating laws and institutions. This [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] calculus treated each person's pleasure and pain as equally worthy of consideration, giving it a democratic and egalitarian thrust that was radical for its time.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] critically evaluates Bentham's legacy, noting that his framework of "measurable quantities of pleasure and pain" is, "as many critics have insisted, something unreal and academic" (003-024). Yet he acknowledges the practical achievements of Benthamite [[social-reform|reform]], particularly in rationalizing the legal system and challenging entrenched privilege.

On the question of rights, Bentham famously dismissed [[human-rights|natural rights]] as "nonsense upon stilts." For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], this limitation of Benthamite thought creates a tension: "On Benthamite principles there could be no question here of indefeasible individual right" (003-029). The development of liberal thought after Bentham would need to find ways of protecting [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] without appealing to discredited [[human-rights|natural rights]] doctrines.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats Bentham as a pivotal but transitional figure. Bentham's [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]]ism gave liberalism a rational, secular foundation and a practical [[social-reform|reform]] program. But its reduction of moral life to a calculus of pleasures and pains left it ill-equipped to account for the richness of human personality and the developmental character of freedom.

The "new liberalism" that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] advocates inherits Bentham's commitment to rational [[social-reform|reform]] and universal [[welfare-state|welfare]] while rejecting his mechanistic psychology. [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]]'s achievement — and [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s project — was to build a liberalism that could accommodate both Bentham's practical [[social-reform|reform]]ism and a richer conception of human development.
