## Definition

Imperialism, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] uses the term, denotes more than the mere possession of colonies or the administration of an empire. It refers to a specific political doctrine — ascendant in [[england|Britain]] from roughly the 1880s through the Edwardian period — that glorified imperial power as an end in itself, advocated the consolidation and economic integration of the [[british-empire|British Empire]] through tariffs and military force, and promoted a conception of national greatness at odds with the liberal commitment to self-government and international equality.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] describes it as "a distinct reactionary policy with a definite and not incoherent creed of its own, an ideal which in its best expression... is certain to exercise a powerful attraction on many generous minds — the ideal of the efficient, disciplined nation, centre and dominating force of a powerful, self-contained, militant empire" (009-002). This is imperialism as a rival *Weltanschauung* — not merely an administrative arrangement but a vision of the good society that competes directly with the liberal vision.

## The Liberal Critique

The liberal objection to imperialism rests on several interconnected grounds. The first is constitutional: empire concentrates power in the executive, inflates the military establishment, and removes decisions of war and peace from parliamentary scrutiny. The second is economic: the "imperial preference" campaign sought to replace [[economic-liberty|free trade]] with a system of protective tariffs within [[british-empire|the Empire]], enriching privileged interests at the expense of consumers — "plutocratic imperialism" in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s pointed phrase (009-001). The third is moral: imperialism denies to subject [[nation|people]]s the rights of self-government that liberalism claims as universal — and, in doing so, corrupts the political culture of the imperial power itself.

"Standing by national autonomy and international equality," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] writes, "[[liberalism|Liberalism]] is necessarily in conflict with the Imperial idea as it is ordinarily presented" (009-013). The conflict is not incidental but principled: the same commitment to liberty that requires democratic government at home requires respect for the self-determination of [[nation|people]]s abroad.

:::info
**The [[south-african-war|South African War]] as Watershed**: The [[south-african-war|Boer War]] (1899–1902) crystallised the division between Liberal Imperialists and "pro-Boer" [[radicalism|Radicals]] within [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]]. The war's conduct — concentration camps, scorched-earth tactics, massive expenditure — exposed the illiberal logic of imperial conquest, while the settlement after 1902 (granting responsible self-government to the former Boer republics) demonstrated that Gladstonian principles could still guide policy. For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], the war was both a warning and a vindication.
:::

## The Liberal Imperialist Temptation

One of the most revealing passages in *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]* concerns not the straightforward opponents of liberal principles but those liberals who were seduced by imperialism without recognising what was happening. "In the Liberal ranks many of the most influential men had passed, without consciousness of the transition, under the sway of quite opposite influences. They were becoming Imperialists in their sleep, and it was only as the implications of Imperialism became evident that they were awakened" (009-003).

The "Liberal Imperialists" — figures like Lord Rosebery, R.B. Haldane, and H.H. Asquith — argued that empire and liberalism were compatible: that [[england|Britain]] could govern its dependencies in accordance with liberal principles, extending the blessings of free institutions to [[nation|people]]s not yet ready for self-government. The [[south-african-war|South African War]] (1899–1902) shattered this complacency. "It was with the outbreak of the [[south-african-war|South African War]] that the new development of Conservative thought first took coherent shape" (009-003), but also that the incompatibility of imperial warfare with liberal values became impossible to ignore.

## Gladstone and the Alternative Imperial Vision

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] presents [[gladstone|Gladstone]] as the exemplar of a genuinely liberal approach to imperial questions — one grounded in "the inherent force of national liberty and international fair dealing" (005-002). [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s "most original work was done in the sphere of imperial relations": the Majuba settlement (granting self-government to the Transvaal after the First [[south-african-war|Boer War]]), the advocacy of [[ireland|Irish Home Rule]], and the consistent application of the principle that subject [[nation|people]]s had the same moral claim to self-government as Englishmen.

This Gladstonian tradition — empire as trust, self-government as goal, conciliation rather than coercion — stood in sharp contrast to the "efficient, disciplined nation" ideal of the imperialists. Where the imperialists saw [[british-empire|the Empire]] as a source of national strength to be consolidated and exploited, [[gladstone|Gladstone]] saw it as a field of moral responsibility, to be administered in the interests of its inhabitants and gradually devolved as they acquired the capacity for self-rule.

## In Liberalism

Imperialism occupies a privileged position in the architecture of [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s argument: it is the closing threat, the force against which he mobilises the entire intellectual apparatus of social liberalism in the final chapter. The book's ninth chapter, "The Future of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]," opens with a diagnosis of crisis — Liberal defeats at the polls, but "something much worse": a loss of faith, "the air of a creed that is becoming fossilized as an extinct form, a fossil that occupied, moreover, an awkward position between two very active and energetically moving grindstones — the upper grindstone of plutocratic imperialism, and the nether grindstone of socialism" (009-001).

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s response is to argue that imperialism is not merely a political threat but a philosophical error — a misunderstanding of what national greatness consists in. [[democracy|Democracy]], he insists, is "bound up with that of internationalism" (009-012); a nation that seeks to dominate others will find its own domestic liberty corroded by the habits of command and coercion that empire requires. The true work of liberalism, in foreign as in domestic policy, is to build the conditions under which free [[nation|people]]s can cooperate without domination — "national autonomy and international equality" (009-013).
