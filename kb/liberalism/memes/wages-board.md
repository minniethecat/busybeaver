## From Laissez-Faire to Statutory Protection

The establishment of wages boards represented a decisive break with the laissez-faire orthodoxy that had dominated Liberal economic thinking through the mid-nineteenth century. "It drew the line at the adult male," (004-003) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observed of earlier [[factory-act|factory legislation]], noting that the state had long been willing to regulate the labour of women and children while leaving adult men to the mercy of the market. The extension of statutory protection to male workers — the result of "a controversy waged for many years within the trade union world itself" (004-003) — marked a turning point in the relationship between the state and the labour market.

## The Trade Boards Act of 1909

The legislative breakthrough came with the Trade Boards Act of 1909, which established boards composed of employer representatives, worker representatives, and independent members with the power to fix minimum wages in industries where pay was exceptionally low and collective bargaining was weak. "It enables wages boards to establish a legal minimum wage in scheduled industries which will undoubtedly grow in number," (004-004) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote, anticipating the expansion of the system. The Act applied initially to four trades — chain-making, ready-made tailoring, paper-box making, and machine-made lace — all characterized by sweated labour and a predominantly female workforce.

## Wages Boards and the New Liberalism

The wages boards exemplified the philosophy of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]]: the state need not own or manage industry, but it had a legitimate role in setting the conditions under which economic activity took place. "They were to deal out factory acts, and establish wages boards," (009-001) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] summarized, describing the practical agenda of social [[social-reform|reform]] that distinguished [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] of Asquith and Lloyd George from the laissez-faire [[liberalism|Liberalism]] of [[gladstone|Gladstone]]. By establishing a floor below which wages could not fall, the boards affirmed the principle that the state had a responsibility to ensure that [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]] did not become, for the weakest, the freedom to starve.
