## Overview

India occupied a complex position in British Liberal thought. It was simultaneously the "jewel in the crown" of the [[british-empire|British Empire]] — governed as a dependency — and a nation whose claims to self-determination tested the universality of liberal principles. The tension between imperial governance and liberal ideals manifested in debates about Indian self-government, the limits of religious liberty under colonial law, and the place of non-European [[nation|people]]s within the liberal vision of national freedom.

## Political and Historical Context

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identified the national movement in India as part of "the liberating movement" that occupied "a great part" of modern history, comparing it to the struggles of [[italy|Italy]], [[ireland|Ireland]], and other European nations for freedom. He recognised that India, like [[ireland|Ireland]], raised the problem of liberty "in its simplest form" — "a question of securing the most elementary rights for the weaker party." Yet the British Raj also illustrated the contradictions of liberal empire: British law in India recognised practices such as polygamy that English law would not tolerate, reflecting the tension between cultural accommodation and liberal norms. (Chapter II)

## Role in Liberalism

India forced British [[liberal-party|Liberals]] to confront the limits of their own principles. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argued that the general tendency of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] is "to favour autonomy," and he proposed an empirical test for imperial relations: where a weaker nation "can be governed by ordinary law applicable to both parties to the union, and fulfilling all the ordinary principles of liberty, the arrangement may be the best for both parties. But where this system fails, where the government is constantly forced to resort to exceptional legislation or perhaps to de-liberalize its own institutions, the case becomes urgent." India's governance under special legislation rather than ordinary law presented precisely this urgency. The Indian national movement thus became, like [[ireland|Ireland]], a measure of whether [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]] could extend its principles universally or would confine them to European [[nation|people]]s. (Chapter II, Chapter VII)

## Key Figures and Events

- **[[Indian National Congress]]**: Founded 1885; the principal vehicle of the Indian national movement demanding greater self-government
- **[[John Morley]]**: Liberal Secretary of State for India (1905–1910) who introduced limited constitutional [[social-reform|reform]]s (Morley-Minto [[reform|Reform]]s, 1909)
- **Indian Councils Acts**: Series of legislative measures (1861, 1892, 1909) gradually expanding Indian participation in governance
