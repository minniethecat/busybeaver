## Definition and Origins

The welfare state is a political and economic system in which the government assumes responsibility for the basic welfare of its citizens through a range of social programmes. In the context of [[british-political-tradition|British Liberalism]], the welfare state emerged from the recognition — articulated by [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] and the New [[liberal-party|Liberals]] — that the State had a positive duty to secure "the conditions of self-maintenance for the normal healthy citizen." (008-005) This marked a decisive break from the older laissez-faire orthodoxy, which had confined the State to the minimal functions of maintaining order and enforcing contracts.

## The New Liberal Rationale

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argued that the conditions of modern industrial society made it impossible for the average worker to secure a living wage through competition alone. "Experience goes to show that the wage of the average worker, as fixed by competition, is not and is not likely to become sufficient to cover all the fortunes and misfortunes of life, to provide for sickness, accident, unemployment and old age." (008-007) This was not a matter of individual moral failing but of structural economic inequality — the "bargain is unequal, and low remuneration is itself a cause of low efficiency." (008-024)

The New Liberal solution was to establish a social minimum through State action: "It provides a certain minimum, a basis to go upon, a foundation upon which independent thrift may hope to build up a sufficiency." (008-008) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] was careful to characterise this not as charity but as the recognition of a right: "It is not a narcotic but a stimulus to self help and to friendly aid or filial support." (008-008)

## The Old Age Pensions Act

The [[old-age-pensions|Old Age Pensions]] Act represented, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis, a pivotal shift in principle. The existing [[poor-law|Poor Law]] "already guaranteed the aged person and the poor in general against actual starvation. But the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] came into operation only at the point of sheer [[poverty|destitution]]." (008-008) The pension system "throws over the test of [[poverty|destitution]]," (008-008) recognising that the aged citizen had a claim on the common resources not as an object of charity but as a matter of right. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] framed this as part of a broader reconception: "the duty of society is rather to ensure that the lot of the independent labourer be more eligible than that of the pauper." (008-012)

## From Destitution to Prevention

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] extended the principle beyond [[old-age-pensions|old-age pension]]s to a general programme of social insurance and preventive provision. The "movement for the break up of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]" (008-008) sought to replace a system that intervened only at the point of collapse with one that rendered "generally available the means of avoiding [[poverty|destitution]]." (008-008) He cited the case of the widowed or deserted mother: under the newer conception, her care of children was to be recognised as "doing better service to the community and one more worthy of pecuniary remuneration" than wage labour outside the home. (008-009) Public money in such cases ceased to be a "dole" and became "a payment for a civic service." (008-009)

## Economic Justice and the Social Surplus

The welfare state, for [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], was not an act of spoliation — "robbing Peter to pay Paul" — but a recovery of wealth that was socially created and properly belonged to the community. "Peter is not robbed. Apart from the tax it is he who would be robbing the State." (008-024) Urban land values, monopoly profits, and inherited wealth all contained elements of social origin that justified collective appropriation for public purposes. The true function of taxation was "to secure to society the element in wealth that is of social origin." (008-024) Welfare expenditure was thus the practical expression of economic justice — "the equation of social service and reward." (008-028)

## The Liberal Middle Way

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] positioned the welfare state as a distinctively Liberal project, distinct both from laissez-faire individualism and from full state socialism. "A Liberal [[socialism|Socialism]]," he wrote, "must clearly fulfil two conditions. In the first place, it must be democratic. It must come from below, not from above. ... And, secondly ... it must make its account with the human individual. It must give the average man free play in the personal life for which he really cares." (008-005) The welfare state was the institutional expression of this balance — enlarging freedom through collective provision, and making liberty real by securing its material preconditions.
