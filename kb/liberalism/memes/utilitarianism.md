## Origins and the Greatest Happiness Principle

Utilitarianism emerged as the second major type of Liberal theory, arising in part from the difficulties encountered by the doctrine of [[natural-rights]]. (003-021) [[Jeremy Bentham]], who spent the greater part of his life elaborating the greatest happiness principle as a basis of social reconstruction, was fully alive to those difficulties. The alleged [[human-rights|rights of man]] were for him "so many anarchical fallacies" — they were "founded on no clearly assignable principle, and admitted of no demonstration" (003-022). What was the supposed law of nature? When was it written, and by whose authority?

Against the metaphysical vagueness of [[human-rights|natural rights]], [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] offered a principle of great clarity and simplicity: "An action is good which tends to promote the greatest possible happiness of the greatest possible number of those affected by it" (003-023). As with an action, so with an institution or a social system. That which conforms to this principle is useful and right; that which conflicts with it is harmful and wrong. (003-023) The appeal was to observable consequences — pleasure and pain — rather than to abstract entitlements.

## The Logic of the Benthamite System

[[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s system rested on a distinctive set of commitments. In its [[individualism]], **equality** was fundamental: "Everybody is to count for one, nobody for more than one, for everyone can feel pain and pleasure" (003-025). **[[liberty|Liberty]]**, by contrast, was not fundamental — it was a means to an end. **Popular sovereignty** was not fundamental either, for "all government is a means to an end" (003-025). Yet, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observes, "the school of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], upon the whole, stood by both liberty and democracy" (003-025).

The [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite argument for [[popular-sovereignty|popular government]] was prudential. Men, if left to themselves — "if neither trained by an educational discipline nor checked by responsibility — do not consider the good of the greatest number. They consider their own good" (003-026). A king unchecked will rule in his own interest; a class unchecked will rule in its own interest. "The only way to secure fair consideration for the happiness of all is to allow to all an equal share of power" (003-026). [[democracy|Democracy]] was thus justified not as a natural right but as a practical safeguard against the abuse of power.

## Liberty in the Utilitarian Framework

On [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite principles, there could be "no question here of indefeasible individual right" (003-029). Indeed, there were possibilities of a thoroughgoing [[socialism]] or of an authoritarian paternalism within the [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite framework. But two considerations pushed in the opposite direction. One arose from the circumstances of the day: [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], originally of conservative temper, was "driven into [[radicalism|Radicalism]] comparatively late in life by the indifference or hostility of the governing classes to his schemes of [[social-reform|reform]]" (003-029). [[state|Government]], as he saw it, was a close corporation with a vested interest hostile to the [[common-good|public good]].

Thus, though their starting-point was different, "the [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ites arrived at practical results not notably divergent from those of the doctrine of natural liberty" (003-030). The two influences worked together in the formation of the school that exercised so notable an influence on English [[liberalism|Liberalism]] during the [[social-reform|reform]] period.

## John Stuart Mill's Refinement

[[John Stuart Mill]] gave utilitarianism its most sophisticated Liberal expression. As a utilitarian, [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] could not appeal to "any [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]] that can be set in opposition to the public [[welfare-state|welfare]]" (005-004). His method was to show that "the permanent [[welfare-state|welfare]] of the public is bound up with the [[individual-rights|rights of the individual]]" (005-004). On occasions, the immediate expediency of the public might be served by ignoring [[individual-rights|personal rights]] — but if the rule of expediency were followed, "there would be neither right nor law at all. There would be no fixed rules in social life, and nothing to which men could trust in guiding their conduct" (005-004).

For [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]], the utilitarian question of right resolved itself into a question of principle: what claim is it, "in general and as a matter of principle," advisable for society to recognise and enforce? His answer — delivered in *On [[liberty|Liberty]]* and *Utilitarianism* — was that personal liberty, freedom of thought and discussion, and representative government were utility-maximising institutions, not because they satisfied individual preferences but because they were indispensable conditions of human development and the long-term improvement of character.

## Influence on English Liberalism

Utilitarianism provided the intellectual engine room for a vast programme of [[reform]]: legal rationalisation, [[constitutional-reform|parliamentary reform]], [[economic-liberty|free trade]], and administrative efficiency. The school of [[Richard Cobden|Cobden]] was "affiliated in general outlook both to the doctrine of natural liberty and to the discipline of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]" (004-001). Together, the [[human-rights|natural rights]] tradition and the utilitarian calculus shaped nineteenth-century English [[liberalism|Liberalism]], each compensating for the weaknesses of the other — the former supplying moral passion, the latter providing practical rigour.

The legacy of utilitarianism persisted well beyond [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]. Its spirit was absorbed by the [[Manchester School]] of [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and operated throughout the nineteenth century. (003-007) Even as its philosophical foundations were challenged by [[T.H. Green]] and the Idealist school, its insistence that institutions must justify themselves by their effects on human [[welfare-state|welfare]] remained central to the Liberal tradition.
