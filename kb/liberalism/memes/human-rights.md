## The Rights of Man in Liberal Theory

The concept of human rights — historically articulated as the "rights of man" or "natural rights" — provided early liberalism with its most powerful rhetorical and philosophical weapon against arbitrary government. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote, liberalism "had to vindicate the elements of personal, civil, and [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]]; and in so doing it took its stand on the rights of man, and, in proportion as it was forced to be constructive, on the supposed harmony of the natural order" (003-005). The appeal to rights enabled liberals to argue that individuals possessed entitlements that no government could legitimately abrogate — that political authority was constrained by a moral order independent of human will.

The logical structure of natural rights theory was straightforward and radical: "the rights of man rested on the law of Nature, and those of government on human institution" (003-005). [[state|Government]] was an artifact, created to serve human purposes; the rights of individuals were not granted by government but recognised by it. This inversion of the traditional relationship between ruler and ruled was among the most consequential intellectual moves in the history of political thought.

## Natural Rights and the Social Contract

The doctrine of natural rights was closely associated with [[social-contract]] theory, which provided an account of how pre-[[political-freedom|political rights]] were transformed into civil rights through the establishment of government. In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s summary: the individual "gave up his natural rights and received in return civil rights, something less complete, perhaps, but more effective as resting on the guarantee of the collective power" (003-006).

This framework established a criterion for evaluating political arrangements: "If you would discover, then, what the civil rights of man in society should be, you must inquire what are the natural rights of man, and how far they are unavoidably modified in accommodating the conflicting claims of men with one another" (003-006). The best constitution was that which preserved natural rights to the greatest extent compatible with social order. As [[thomas-paine]] expressed it, "a civil right is a natural right exchanged" (quoted at 003-006).

## The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen

The most influential codification of natural rights theory was the French **Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man and of the Citizen** (1789), which declared that "the end of every political association is the conservation of the natural and imprescriptible rights of man. These rights are liberty, property, security (*la sûreté*), and resistance to oppression" (003-011).

The Declaration's Article IV provided a classic liberal definition of liberty: "[[liberty|Liberty]] consists in the power to do anything that does not injure others; thus, the exercise of the natural rights of every man has only such limits as assure to other members of society the enjoyment of the same rights" (003-013). This formulation — liberty limited only by the equal liberty of others — remains one of the most enduring statements of the liberal understanding of freedom.

## The British and French Traditions Compared

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted an important difference between the British and French approaches to rights. British liberalism "has much less to say of natural rights than the French theorists" (004-001), preferring to ground its claims in historical precedent and the common law rather than abstract philosophical deduction. The English tradition spoke of the "rights of Englishmen" — liberties inherited from Magna Carta and the common law — rather than the universal "rights of man."

This difference in idiom should not obscure the underlying convergence of principle. Whether justified by natural law or by constitutional history, both traditions insisted that individuals possess protections against arbitrary power that the state cannot justly override. The [[petition-of-right]], the [[habeas-corpus]] Act, and the English Bill of [[rights|Rights]] (1689) served many of the same functions as the French Declaration, even as they employed a different rhetorical register.

## The Development and Critique of Natural Rights

The theory of natural rights has undergone significant development and critique within the liberal tradition itself. By the nineteenth century, the historical school (influenced by [[edmund-burke]]) and the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] school (led by [[jeremy-bentham]], who dismissed natural rights as "nonsense upon stilts") had called into question the philosophical foundations of rights talk.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] acknowledged the force of these critiques while defending the practical value of rights-based [[reason|reasoning]]. He observed that "the theory of the natural rights of the individual is thus supplemented by a theory of the mutual harmony of individual and social needs, and, so completed, forms a conception of human society which is prima facie workable, which, in fact, contains important elements of truth" (003-008). The mature liberal view did not abandon rights but re-conceived them as socially embedded rather than metaphysically grounded — "responsive to the needs of a great class, and to many of the requirements of society as a whole" (003-008).

## Human Rights in the Twentieth Century and Beyond

The twentieth century witnessed a remarkable revival and expansion of the human rights framework. The **Universal Declaration of Human Rights** (1948) translated the eighteenth-century language of natural rights into the idiom of international law. The **European Convention on Human Rights** (1950) created enforceable legal mechanisms for holding states accountable to rights standards — realising, in institutional form, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s insistence that rights must be secured by "the guarantee of the collective power" (003-006).

Contemporary human rights discourse extends well beyond the [[classical-liberalism|classical liberal]] catalogue of civil and [[political-freedom|political rights]] to encompass economic, social, and cultural rights — the rights to education, healthcare, and an adequate standard of living. This expansion reflects the logic of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]] that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] himself advanced, which held that liberty could not be meaningfully enjoyed without the material conditions that make freedom effective.
