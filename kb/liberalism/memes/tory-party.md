## Overview

The Tory Party occupied a central place in the political landscape against which British liberalism defined itself. From the late seventeenth century through the nineteenth century, Toryism was the political expression of resistance to change — the defense of monarchy, established church, landed property, and traditional hierarchy against the successive waves of liberal [[social-reform|reform]].

> We have to note only that what modern democracy has to face is no mere inertia of tradition. It is a distinct reactionary policy with a definite basis, finding its inspiration in a reasoned defence of authority, of the value of institutions and of tradition. (009-002)

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats Toryism not merely as a political opponent but as a coherent intellectual tradition with its own "reasoned defence of authority." Understanding this tradition is essential to understanding what liberalism was fighting against — and, in some respects, what it absorbed and transformed.

## Origins and Development

The Tories first emerged as a political grouping in the late seventeenth century, during the Exclusion Crisis (1678–1681), when they defended the hereditary right of James, Duke of York (a Catholic), to succeed to the throne. The name "Tory" itself was originally a term of abuse, derived from the Irish word *tóraidhe* (outlaw, pursued man), applied to those who supported the royal prerogative against [[parliament|Parliament]].

Through the eighteenth century, the Tories were associated with the interests of the landed gentry and the established Church of [[england|England]]. They opposed the extension of the franchise, religious [[religious-liberty|toleration]] for Dissenters and Catholics, and the [[social-reform|reform]] of parliamentary representation.

## Toryism and the Liberal Challenge

The great [[reform|Reform]] Act of 1832, which expanded the franchise and eliminated "rotten boroughs," marked a decisive defeat for the old Tory order. Yet Toryism proved remarkably adaptive. Under the leadership of [[robert-peel|Sir Robert Peel]], the party began a transformation that would eventually produce the modern Conservative Party.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes that in the later nineteenth century, under Benjamin Disraeli's leadership, Conservatism developed a positive program of its own — one that combined acceptance of democratic institutions with an appeal to national pride, imperial sentiment, and social [[social-reform|reform]]:

> On the other hand, in the Liberal ranks many of the most influential men had passed, without consciousness of the transition, under the sway of quite opposite influences. They were becoming Imperialists, and as Imperialists they began to think in terms of power rather than of freedom. (009-003)

This passage captures a crucial dialectic in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s analysis: as the nineteenth century progressed, liberalism and conservatism began to converge in troubling ways. [[liberal-party|Liberals]] adopted imperial and authoritarian habits of thought, while Tories learned to speak the language of democracy and social [[social-reform|reform]]. The clarity of the old battle lines was lost.

## The Tory Legacy in Liberal Thought

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s treatment of Toryism is notable for its analytical sophistication. He does not simply dismiss the Tory tradition as an obstacle to be overcome. Rather, he recognizes that certain Tory insights — the value of continuity, the importance of institutional wisdom, the dangers of abstract rationalism in politics — posed genuine challenges that liberalism needed to address.

The organic conception of society that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] himself advocates — in which society is understood as a living whole rather than a mechanical aggregate of individuals — owes something to the Tory critique of liberal atomism, even as he deploys it in the service of liberal ends.

## Key Principles

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Authority** | The defense of established authority — monarchical, ecclesiastical, and social |
| **Tradition** | Preference for inherited institutions over abstract rational design |
| **Hierarchy** | Acceptance of social gradation as natural and beneficial |
| **Landed Interest** | [[protectionism|Protection]] of agricultural and property interests against industrial and commercial challengers |
| **Established Church** | Defense of the Church of [[england|England]]'s privileged position |
| **[[imperialism|Imperial Preference]]** | Support for empire, protectionism, and national assertion abroad |
