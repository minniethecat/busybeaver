## Definition

Laissez-faire is the economic doctrine holding that the state's role in economic life should be confined to the most minimal functions — what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] describes as "holding the ring" for private economic activity. The metaphor is precise: the state provides the framework of law and order within which private actors compete, but it does not itself enter the contest or direct its outcome.

> We have seen that the theory of laissez-faire assumed that the State would hold the ring. That is to say, it would suppress force and fraud, keep property secure, and aid men in enforcing contracts.
> (004-006)

This doctrine was the dominant economic philosophy of nineteenth-century British liberalism, associated above all with the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and the campaign against the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]. Yet [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] devotes an entire chapter of *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]* to its critical examination, arguing that laissez-faire, whatever its historical merits, is an insufficient basis for liberal policy in the conditions of modern industrial society.

## Historical Development

Laissez-faire emerged as a reaction against the mercantilist system of state-granted monopolies, protective tariffs, and detailed economic regulation that characterized the early modern period. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s *The Wealth of [[nation|Nation]]s* (1776) provided its intellectual foundations, arguing that the pursuit of private interest, mediated by competitive markets, tends to produce outcomes that serve the [[common-good|general good]] more effectively than any government planner could design.

In [[england|Britain]], laissez-faire became the rallying cry of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] (founded 1838), which campaigned against the tariffs that kept food prices high for the benefit of landed interests. [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], the League's leaders, framed [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as a moral cause — not merely sound [[political-economy|economics]] but the economic expression of liberal principles of freedom and cooperation between [[nation|people]]s.

Yet even in its nineteenth-century heyday, the doctrine was never applied with complete consistency. [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]] regulating working conditions, public health legislation, and state support for education all represented departures from strict laissez-faire, justified by liberals on the grounds that they corrected market failures or protected the vulnerable.

## Key Arguments

1. **The case for laissez-faire**: [[state|Government]] intervention distorts the natural operation of markets, creates opportunities for rent-seeking, and substitutes the judgment of officials for the dispersed knowledge embodied in market prices. Free exchange is the economic counterpart to [[political-freedom|political liberty]].

2. **The limits of laissez-faire**: The doctrine presupposes conditions — equality of bargaining power, perfect information, absence of externalities — that do not hold in real economies. "The theory of laissez-faire," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues, "assumed a world that has never existed" — a world of independent producers negotiating on roughly equal terms.

3. **The liberal alternative**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] proposes that liberalism should move beyond the sterile opposition of laissez-faire and state socialism toward a conception of the state as the guarantor of the conditions under which [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] can be meaningfully exercised by all, not merely the privileged few.

:::info
**The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and Its Critics**: The term "[[manchester-school|Manchester School]]" was coined by Benjamin Disraeli as a term of abuse for the free-trade advocates led by [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and [[john-bright|Bright]]. It later became a convenient label for the philosophy of laissez-faire, though [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] takes care to distinguish the historical [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] from the simplified caricature of "[[manchester-school|Manchesterism]]us" that later critics attacked.
:::

## In Liberalism

Chapter IV of *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]*, titled simply "Laissez-Faire," is the most sustained critical treatment of any single concept in the book. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] begins by carefully reconstructing the theory in its strongest form, acknowledging its historical role in dismantling mercantilist restrictions and its philosophical affinity with liberal principles of freedom and individual responsibility.

He then proceeds to dismantle it, not by rejecting the value of [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]] but by showing that the conditions laissez-faire presupposes — a rough equality of bargaining power, the absence of coercion in economic relationships — cannot be taken for granted. Where the factory owner can dictate terms to the starving worker, the language of "free contract" obscures a relation of domination.

> The "let alone" theory, Hobhouse concludes, is "a misleading guide to the true relation of the State to the economic order." The real question is not whether the state should intervene but *how* and *to what ends*.
