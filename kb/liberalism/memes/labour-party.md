## Overview

The Labour Party emerged as the political expression of the organized working class in [[england|Britain]] at the turn of the twentieth century. Rooted in the trade union movement and the [[new-unionism|New Unionism]] of the late 1880s, the party grew from a parliamentary pressure group into an independent political force that would eventually displace the [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]] as the principal party of the left. In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s narrative, the Labour Party appears both as a product of liberal ideas — the emancipation of trade unions being "a movement to liberty through equality" (002-019) — and as a new challenge that [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had to accommodate through cooperation on the basis of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] finance and social [[social-reform|reform]] (009-003).

## Origins in the New Unionism

The intellectual and organizational roots of the Labour Party lie in the transformation of British trade unionism during the late nineteenth century. The emancipation of trade unions, "extending over the period from 1824 to 1906, and perhaps not yet complete, was in the main a liberating movement, because combination was necessary to place the workman on something approaching terms of equality with the employer" (002-019). This legal emancipation created the institutional foundation upon which a labour political movement could be built.

The decisive catalyst, however, was the [[new-unionism|New Unionism]] of the late 1880s — a movement that organized unskilled workers alongside the traditional craft unions. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies the Dockers' strike of 1889 as the turning point: "From the Dockers' strike of 1889 the [[new-unionism|New Unionism]] became a fighting force in public affairs, and the idea of a Labour party began to take shape" (009-002). [[socialism|Socialism]], which had "begun to influence organized labour" and "inspired the more generous minds among the artisans with the determination to grapple with the problem of the unskilled workmen," provided the ideological impetus (009-002).

Not all [[liberal-party|Liberals]] welcomed this development. [[William Ewart Gladstone]], while a great [[social-reform|reform]]er in matters of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] and Irish land, "was not sympathetically disposed to the '[[new-unionism|New Unionism]]' and the semi-socialistic ideas that came at the end of the 'eighties, which, in fact, constituted a powerful cross current to the political work that he had immediately in hand" (005-001). The [[new-unionism|New Unionism]] thus represented a force that pushed beyond the limits of Gladstonian [[liberalism|Liberalism]] even as it shared [[liberalism|Liberalism]]'s commitment to improving the condition of the working class.

## Alliance with Liberalism

Despite early tensions, the Labour Party and [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] found common ground in the early years of the twentieth century. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] describes this cooperative relationship as grounded in shared economic principles: "The Socialists of practical politics, the Labour Party, found that they could by no means dispense with the discipline of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]. [[free-trade|Free Trade]] finance was to be the basis of social [[social-reform|reform]]. [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and Labour learned to cooperate in resisting delusive promises of remedies for unemployment and in maintaining the right of free international exchange" (009-003).

A decisive moment in cementing this alliance came from an unexpected quarter — the judiciary. A series of legal decisions that weakened the position of trade unions had the paradoxical effect of strengthening the political resolve of organized labour: "In destroying the old position of the [[trade-union|Trade Unions]], the judges created the modern Labour party and cemented its alliance with [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" (009-003). The Taff Vale judgment of 1901 and the Osborne judgment of 1909 were instrumental in convincing labour leaders that political representation was indispensable to the defence of trade union interests.

The alliance was not merely tactical. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw it as expressing a deeper convergence rooted in the logic of democratic politics: "the growing cooperation of political [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and Labour, which in the last few years has replaced the antagonism of the 'nineties, is no mere accident of temporary political convenience, but has its roots deep in the necessities of [[democracy|Democracy]]" (008-028). Both movements, in his view, were working toward a reconciliation of [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] with [[economic-justice|social justice]], even if they approached the problem from different starting points.

## Key Figures

- **[[Keir Hardie]]** — Scottish socialist and first independent Labour MP (1892); a founder of the Independent Labour Party (1893) and first parliamentary leader of the Labour Party.
- **[[Ramsay MacDonald]]** — Secretary of the Labour Representation Committee from 1900; later became the first Labour Prime Minister in 1924.
- **[[John Burns]]** — Trade unionist and Liberal-Labour MP; a leader of the 1889 Dockers' strike before entering [[parliament|Parliament]].

## Legacy

The Labour Party's rise was the most significant structural transformation of British party politics in the twentieth century. It absorbed the working-class vote that nineteenth-century [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had cultivated, and by the 1920s it had supplanted the [[liberal-party|Liberals]] as the main opposition to the [[tory-party|Conservatives]]. The party's displacement of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] was not, however, a repudiation of Liberal achievements: the [[welfare-state|welfare]] state that Labour extended after 1945 rested on foundations laid by the New Liberal governments of 1906–1914, and the Labour Party's commitment to parliamentary democracy, [[civil-liberties|civil liberty]], and progressive taxation all bore the imprint of the liberal tradition. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s hope that economic [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and organized Labour could converge "along the path to a point where they will meet" (008-028) was in one sense vindicated — even if the institutional vehicle for that convergence proved to be the Labour Party rather than [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] he had championed.
