# British Empire

The **British Empire** was the global system of territories, colonies, protectorates, and dominions under British sovereignty, which reached its territorial zenith in the early twentieth century. In the context of liberal political thought, the Empire served as both a field for the application of liberal principles of self-government and a source of tension between [[internationalism|liberal internationalism]] and imperial consolidation.

## Liberal Perspectives on Empire

L.T. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s *[[liberalism|Liberalism]]* (1911) engages the Empire primarily through the lens of colonial self-government and the tension between [[internationalism|liberal internationalism]] and imperial consolidation. Writing in the aftermath of the [[south-african-war|South African War]] and the [[protectionism|Tariff Reform]] debate, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] distinguishes between two visions of empire: an authoritarian, protectionist imperialism and a democratic commonwealth of self-governing nations:

> The Colonial Empire as it stands is in substance the creation of the older Liberalism. It is founded on self-government, and self-government is the root from which the existing sentiment of unity has sprung. (007-027)

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues that the self-governing colonies — notably including [[canada|Canada]] and [[australia|Australia]] — represent the most democratic communities in the world, whose "natural sympathies are not with the [[tory-party|Conservatives]], but with the most [[progress|Progress]]ive parties in the [[england|United Kingdom]]" (007-027). This positions the Empire not as an instrument of domination but as a potential framework for democratic [[internationalism|international cooperation]].

## The Social Factor in Imperial Wealth

In his discussion of economic justice, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] briefly references the Empire in the context of socially-created value, noting that a portion of [[london|London]] site values is attributable not merely to the city but to "the British empire" as a whole — an illustration of how economic value is collectively produced across political boundaries (008-011).

## Liberalism, Internationalism, and the Imperial Idea

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] was clear about the fundamental tension between liberal principles and the imperial project as conventionally understood. "Standing by national autonomy and international equality," he wrote, "[[liberalism|Liberalism]] is necessarily in conflict with the Imperial idea as it is ordinarily presented" (020-007). This was not, however, a counsel of indifference to the Empire as it actually existed. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] distinguished carefully between the coercive imperialism of conquest and subjugation and the democratic commonwealth represented by the self-governing Dominions.

The liberal internationalist case for [[imperialism|Empire]], such as it was, rested on the possibility of a voluntary association of self-governing nations united by common institutions and mutual interest rather than by force. This vision was fundamentally at odds with the protectionist imperialism advocated by [[joseph-chamberlain|Joseph Chamberlain]] and the [[protectionism|Tariff Reform]] League, which sought to bind the Empire through preferential tariffs — a policy that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] regarded as a betrayal of both [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and the principles of self-government.

## The Paradox of Imperial Rule

In his discussion of national autonomy, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] drew on [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]] to articulate a paradox central to the liberal critique of empire: the ruler who dominates others is himself unfree. "The Assyrian conqueror, [[herbert-spencer|Mr. Herbert Spencer]] remarks, who is depicted in the bas-reliefs leading his captive by a cord, is bound with that cord himself. He forfeits his liberty as long as he retains his power" (007-004). The application to the British Empire was clear: a nation that ruled others by force could not itself remain a free society, for the habits of domination abroad would inevitably corrode the practice of liberty at home. This argument — that imperialism was self-defeating not merely politically but morally — represented the deepest liberal objection to the imperial project.
