## From Laissez-Faire to Social Action

Social reform represented the practical working-out of the theoretical shift from laissez-faire individualism to the positive conception of the State. The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] had confined government to the protection of person and property; the New [[liberal-party|Liberals]], informed by the experience of industrial society and the empirical findings of social investigators, concluded that genuine freedom required more. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] traced the evolution through the [[factory-act|factory legislation]]: "as experience ripened, the implications of the new legislation became clearer, and men came to see that by industrial control they were not destroying liberty but confirming it." (004-006) This was the philosophical basis of the social reform programme: restraint was not the enemy of liberty but its necessary condition when it prevented the strong from exploiting the weak.

## The New Liberal Reform Agenda

The social reform agenda that crystallised in Edwardian [[england|Britain]] encompassed [[old-age-pensions|old-age pension]]s, national insurance against sickness and unemployment, labour exchanges, wages boards for sweated industries, school meals, and medical inspection of schoolchildren. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] presented these not as discrete measures but as elements of a coherent philosophy: "The sum and substance of the changes that I have mentioned may be expressed in the principle that the individual cannot stand alone, but that between him and the State there is a reciprocal obligation." (007-018) [[state|The State]]'s role was to secure the "means of maintaining a civilized standard of life," while the individual's duty was to work and contribute. It was this synthesis of rights and responsibilities that distinguished Liberal social reform from both laissez-faire individualism and state socialism.

## Reform and the Liberal Revival

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] situated social reform within the broader Liberal revival that followed the [[south-african-war|Boer War]]. "The tide has by no means spent itself," he wrote of the reform movement, noting that it "flows in a steady stream towards social amelioration and democratic government." (009-005) The [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]]'s landslide victory in 1906 and the subsequent programme of social legislation — the [[old-age-pensions|Old Age Pensions]] Act, the Trade Boards Act, the [[national-insurance|National Insurance Act]] — represented the high-water mark of this current. Crucially, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] saw this not as the abandonment of the older Liberal tradition but as its fulfilment: "the Liberal who starts from the Gladstonian tradition has in large measure realized that if he is to maintain the essence of his old ideas it must be through a process of adaptation and growth." (009-005)

## The Liberal-Socialist Convergence

The social reform programme brought [[liberalism|Liberalism]] into close cooperation with the emerging [[labour-party|Labour]] movement. "[[liberalism|Liberalism]] and [[labour-party|Labour]] learned to cooperate," [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observed, finding common ground in their commitment to using the instruments of the State to address the material conditions of working-class life. (009-004) The distinctive Liberal contribution was to frame reform in the language of individual rights and duties rather than class interest: the purpose of social legislation was not to supersede individual effort but to make it possible — "to give the average man free play in the personal life for which he really cares." (008-005)
