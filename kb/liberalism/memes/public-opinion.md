## Definition

Public Opinion — the collective judgment of citizens on matters of common concern — a force that liberalism both empowers through free speech and seeks to educate through democratic participation

This concept occupies a distinctive position within the liberal tradition. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] engages with it at multiple points in _[[liberalism|Liberalism]]_, treating it as one of the elements that must be harmonized within a coherent liberal synthesis.

## Historical Development

The concept emerged from [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] political thought and was refined through the political struggles of the nineteenth century. The French Declaration of 1793 gave emphatic expression to principles of equality and popular sovereignty that informed the broader liberal movement (003-015).

In [[england|England]], liberal ideas developed within a constitutional framework. "In its early days and in its more moderate forms, it sought its ends under the guise of constitutionalism" (003-002), gradually extending the scope of individual and collective liberty.

## Key Arguments

1. **Liberal foundation**: This concept addresses a fundamental question about the relationship between the individual and the political order. The liberal answer emphasizes voluntary association and consent rather than coercion or inherited obligation.

2. **Practical limits**: The application of this principle encounters practical tensions with other liberal commitments. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] asks, "Are these different applications compatible? Will they work together to make that harmonious whole of which it is easy enough to talk in abstract terms?" (007-001).

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] addresses this concept in connection with his broader argument about the harmony of liberal principles. He maintains that "social liberty rests on restraint" and that individual freedom is possible only through social cooperation (007-002).

The challenge for liberalism is to maintain the internal coherence of its various commitments — to show that liberty, equality, democracy, and justice are not competing values but complementary aspects of a unified social ideal. This concept contributes one thread to that larger argument.
