## The Corn Laws and Class Interest

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], protective tariffs on imported grain designed to maintain high prices for domestic producers, were "conceived in the interest of the governing classes" (004-001) — the landowning aristocracy that dominated [[parliament|Parliament]] before the [[reform|Reform]] Act of 1832. By keeping the price of bread artificially high, they imposed a regressive tax on the industrial working class while enriching the rentier class. "As even the tariff [[social-reform|reform]]ers of our own day admit," (004-001) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] observed, the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] represented the subordination of public [[welfare-state|welfare]] to private class interest, making their repeal a moral as well as an economic imperative.

## The Anti-Corn Law League

The campaign for repeal was led by the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], founded in 1838 by [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]]. It was the first modern political pressure group, employing mass meetings, pamphleteering, and parliamentary lobbying on an unprecedented scale. "The divorce of the labourer from the land was complete at the time when the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] was formed," (004-002) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted, observing that the industrial transformation of [[england|England]] had created a new constituency — urban workers and manufacturers — whose interests were directly opposed to the protectionist regime. The League's victory in 1846, when Prime Minister [[robert-peel|Robert Peel]] split his own [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] to force repeal through [[parliament|Parliament]], demonstrated the growing political power of industrial capitalism and free-trade ideology.

## Free Trade and the Manchester School

The intellectual foundations of the repeal movement were supplied by the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]], whose central conviction was that free commercial intercourse served not only economic efficiency but [[peace|international peace]] and domestic justice. "The school of [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] is affiliated in general outlook both to the doctrine of natural liberty and to the discipline of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]," (015-006) [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] wrote, noting its "thoroughly practical attitude dear to the English mind" and its relative lack of interest in the abstract declarations of [[human-rights|natural rights]] that characterized French liberal theory (015-006).

The [[manchester|Manchester]] doctrine held that [[economic-liberty|free trade]] would benefit all classes, not merely manufacturers. Cheap bread meant higher real wages for workers. Open markets meant expanding employment. And the mutual interdependence created by international commerce would, the [[manchester|Manchester]] men believed, make war between trading nations unthinkable — a conviction that made the [[corn-laws|Corn Law repeal]] not merely an economic [[social-reform|reform]] but a contribution to the cause of peace.

## Distinguishing Free Trade from Protectionism

A crucial dimension of the repeal's legacy was the sharp distinction it established, in the Liberal mind, between legitimate protective legislation and illegitimate economic protectionism. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] insisted:

> It is a very shallow wit that taunts contemporary Liberalism with inconsistency in opposing economic protection while it supports protective legislation for the manual labourer. The two things have nothing in common but that they are restraints intended to operate in the interests of somebody. (015-022)

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were a restraint "conceived in favour of those who are already more fortunately placed and against the poorer classes" (015-022). Factory legislation, by contrast, was a restraint "conceived in the interest primarily of the poorer classes with the object of securing to them a more effective freedom and a nearer approach to equality of conditions" (015-022). This distinction — between restraints that entrenched privilege and restraints that expanded [[positive-liberty|effective liberty]] — became a permanent feature of Liberal political economy in the decades after 1846.

## Significance for Liberal Thought

The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] became a foundational myth of Liberal political economy. It appeared to vindicate the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s conviction that [[economic-liberty|free trade]] served not only economic efficiency but [[peace|international peace]] and domestic justice. The [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite argument — that free commercial intercourse between nations would engender mutual knowledge and that [[economic-liberty|free trade]] meant peace — gave the repeal a significance extending far beyond the price of bread. It established an enduring alliance between [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and [[economic-liberty|free trade]] that would define British politics for the next half-century.
