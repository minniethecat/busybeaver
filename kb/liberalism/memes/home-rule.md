## Overview

Home Rule is the doctrine that a distinct national or regional community within a larger political union should possess autonomous governing institutions for its domestic affairs. In British liberal thought, it emerged as the application of the principle of [[self-government]] to the constitutional structure of the [[england|United Kingdom]] itself — most notably in relation to [[ireland|Ireland]], where the demand for a devolved parliament became the defining political controversy of the late Victorian era.

> Now, we are not concerned here to follow up the devious windings of modern Conservatism. We have to note only that what modern democracy has to face is no mere inertia of tradition. It is a distinct reactionary policy with a definite and not incoherent creed of its own... (008-001)

## Historical Development

### The Irish Question and Gladstone

The Home Rule movement crystallized around the Irish demand for legislative autonomy. When [[William Ewart Gladstone]] formed his second [[state|Government]] in 1880, the old party system stood secure — but a new leaven was at work. Industrial progress slowed, new ideas of social [[social-reform|reform]] emerged from the teaching of [[thomas-hill-green|Green]] and the enthusiasm of [[arnold-toynbee|Toynbee]], and the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] brought [[socialism]] into contact with practical politics.

> At this stage the Irish question came to a head. Mr. Gladstone declared for Home Rule, and the party fissure took place on false lines. The upper and middle classes in the main went over to Unionism, but they took with them a section of the Radicals, while Mr. Gladstone's personal force retained on the Liberal side a number of men whose insight into the needs of democracy was by no means profound. The political fight was for the moment shifted from the social question to the single absorbing issue of Home Rule, and the new Unionist party enjoyed twenty years of almost unbroken supremacy. (008-003)

### Constitutional Implications

The Home Rule controversy reshaped British party politics. The [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]] split in 1886, with [[liberal-unionist-party|Liberal Unionists]] joining the [[tory-party|Conservatives]] to oppose Irish self-government. This realignment delayed social [[social-reform|reform]] and embedded the Irish question at the centre of British political life for a generation. The author notes that "had the Home Rule issue stood alone it might have been settled in 1892, but meanwhile in the later 'eighties the social question had become insistent" (008-003).

## Home Rule as an Integral Element of Democracy

Home Rule cannot be understood in isolation from the broader democratic project. The text presents it as fundamentally connected to the organization of a genuinely participatory political order:

> He is aware that the problem of Home Rule and of devolution generally is an integral part of the organization of democracy. (009-001)

This reflects a deeper liberal conviction — that democracy requires intermediate institutions linking the individual citizen to the vast and complex modern state. The individual voter "feels himself lost among the millions" and needs organization with neighbours and fellow workers through which responsibility "comes home to him":

> The development of social interest — and that is democracy — depends not only on adult suffrage and the supremacy of the elected legislature, but on all the intermediate organizations which link the individual to the whole. This is one among the reasons why devolution and the revival of local government, at present crushed in this country by a centralized bureaucracy, are of the essence of democratic progress. (009-005)

## Home Rule and the Imperial Context

The liberal approach to Home Rule extended beyond domestic politics to the governance of the [[british-empire|British Empire]]. The text argues that the Colonial [[imperialism|Empire]] is "in substance the creation of the older [[liberalism|Liberalism]]" and "founded on self-government" (009-007). The self-governing colonies — including "the most democratic communities in the world" — favoured Home Rule and set the pace in [[social-reform|social legislation]]:

> Standing by national autonomy and international equality, Liberalism is necessarily in conflict with the Imperial idea as it is ordinarily presented. But this is not to say that it is indifferent to the interests of the Empire as a whole... The problem of our time is to devise means for the more concrete and living expression of this sentiment without impairing the rights of self-government on which it depends. (009-007)

The liberal vision was of a democratic alliance of self-governing communities — a model "on no mean scale, of the International State" (009-007).

## Critique of Centralization

The text levels a sharp critique of centralized bureaucracy, arguing that it crushes local government and, with it, the school of civic responsibility. [[progress|Progress]] itself "is blocked by the very competition of many causes for the first place in the advance. Here, again, [[local-government|devolution]] will help us" (009-009). The author calls for "a fuller cooperation among those of genuine democratic feeling" and "a clearer sense of the necessity of cooperation between all who profess and call themselves democrats" (009-009).

## Legacy and Significance

Home Rule left an enduring mark on liberal constitutional thought. Its core insight — that democratic self-government requires devolved institutions close to the [[nation|people]] — remains relevant to contemporary debates about federalism, [[local-government|devolution]], and the organization of multi-national states. Though the specific Irish settlement took a different constitutional form than [[gladstone|Gladstone]] envisioned, the principle that distinct national communities deserve autonomous political institutions within a broader framework of cooperation became a settled element of the liberal tradition.
