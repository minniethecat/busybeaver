## Historical Role in the Liberal Narrative

From the smallest social unit to the largest, a great part of the liberating movement of the nineteenth century is occupied with the struggle of entire nations against alien rule. Among the formative experiences of this movement was the revolt of Europe against Napoleon, whose conquests had spread French dominion across the continent and provoked the national awakenings that would define European politics for a century. (002-023)

Napoleon appears in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s narrative not as a subject of study in his own right, but as the catalyst for the national and liberal movements that arose in reaction to his empire. The struggle of [[italy|Italy]] for freedom, the fate of various subject [[nation|people]]s, and the national movements in [[ireland|Ireland]] and [[india|India]] all belong to this broader dynamic of liberation from alien domination. (002-023)

## The Napoleonic Wars and Their Economic Aftermath

The Napoleonic Wars had profound economic consequences for [[england|Britain]]. The rise of machine industry since 1760 had already destroyed the old domestic system and reduced the operative in the towns to the position of a factory hand. [[england|Britain]] enjoyed a monopoly of manufacture for two generations after the Napoleonic war, which made the road to wealth easy for employers while leaving workers vulnerable. (004-002)

This post-war economic context gave urgency to the questions that the new [[liberalism|Liberalism]] would eventually address. The factory system brought matters to a head through the systematic employment of women and young children under conditions that outraged the public conscience — a situation that even adherents of free contract like [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] could not defend when it came to children. (004-002)

## Bonapartism and Liberal Constitutionalism

The Napoleonic regime represented what [[liberalism|Liberalism]] defined itself against. Where [[liberalism|Liberalism]] sought to establish government by discussion, limited powers, and the rule of law, Bonapartism substituted personal rule backed by plebiscitary acclamation. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] noted that the theory of the modern State as a sovereign authority, erected by force and ruling by force, was not derived from the tradition of English liberty but from the imperial jurists of Rome — a tradition reinforced by the spectacle of Napoleonic absolutism. (003-001)

The Liberal counter-model insisted that the State's authority derived not from conquest or personal charisma but from the community it served. The theory which stood in sharp contrast to the Roman conception was that of the English constitutionalists: government as a trustee, its powers limited by the purposes for which it was instituted (003-003). Napoleon's career demonstrated the danger of concentrating sovereign power in a single individual, however brilliant — a lesson that reinforced the Liberal commitment to parliamentary checks and the separation of powers.

## Napoleon's Continental System and British Trade

The Continental System, Napoleon's attempt to economically strangle [[england|Britain]] by closing European ports to British goods, had paradoxical effects on the development of [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. By disrupting normal trade, it intensified the economic distress that fed radical and [[social-reform|reform]] movements in [[england|Britain]]. It also stimulated the smuggling that kept alive free-trade principles in practice even as governments attempted to suppress them. (004-001)

The post-war settlement brought a reaction against liberal ideas across the Continent, but the economic forces that the Napoleonic era had accelerated — industrialisation, urbanisation, the growth of a commercial middle class — could not be reversed. These forces would eventually provide the social basis for Liberal and [[social-reform|reform]] movements throughout Europe in the decades after Waterloo.

## Significance for Liberalism

The struggle against Napoleonic domination exemplified what [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] called "the problem of liberty in its simplest form" — the question of securing the most elementary rights for the weaker party. The revolt against Napoleon was part of a broader pattern that included the emancipation of the negro, the national movement in [[ireland|Ireland]], and the struggle of the Christian subjects of Turkey. All shared the character of a fight for basic rights against overwhelming power. (002-023)
