## Definition

[[nation|Nation]]al liberty is the application of the principle of self-government to nations and [[nation|people]]s. Just as [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]] requires that the person be governed by law rather than by the arbitrary will of another, so national liberty requires that a [[nation|people]] be governed by institutions answerable to themselves rather than imposed by an external power. It is, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s framework, the largest-scale expression of the liberal impulse — the movement from the liberty of the individual through the liberty of the locality to the liberty of the nation.

[[nation|Nation]]al liberty must be distinguished from nationalism. The former is a defensive principle: the right of a [[nation|people]] not to be ruled by aliens. The latter is often an aggressive principle: the assertion of one nation's superiority or its right to dominate others. Liberal thought has consistently supported the first while viewing the second with suspicion.

## Historical Development

The nineteenth century was the great age of national liberation movements. The revolt of Europe against [[napoleon|Napoleon]], the struggle of [[italy|Italy]] for unification, the emancipation of Greece from Ottoman rule, and the national movements in [[ireland|Ireland]] and [[india|India]] all presented "the problem of liberty in its simplest form" — the demand of a [[nation|people]] to govern themselves:

> A great part of the liberating movement is occupied with the struggle of entire nations against alien rule, with the revolt of Europe against Napoleon, with the struggle of Italy for freedom, with the fate of the Christian subjects of Turkey, with the emancipation of the negro, with the national movement in Ireland and in India.
> (007-097)

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] acknowledges that these struggles raise difficult questions. What is a nation as distinct from a state? If [[ireland|Ireland]] is a nation, is Ulster one? The answers, he suggests, are practical rather than theoretical, to be worked out through "the concrete teaching of history and the practical insight of statesmanship" (007-097).

## Key Arguments

1. **[[nation|Nation]]al liberty as the extension of [[personal-liberty|individual liberty]]**: A [[nation|people]] deprived of self-government is in the position of an individual deprived of [[human-rights|civil rights]]. The denial of national liberty degrades both the governed and, ultimately, the governor. As [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]] observed, "The Assyrian conqueror who is depicted in the bas-reliefs leading his captive by a cord, is bound with that cord himself" (007-097).

2. **The empirical test for autonomy**: Where a weaker nation incorporated into a larger one can be governed by ordinary law applicable to both, the union may serve both parties. But where the stronger power must resort to "exceptional legislation" or "de-liberalize its own institutions" to maintain control, the case for separation becomes urgent: "Under such conditions the most liberally-minded democracy is maintaining a system which must undermine its own principles" (007-097).

3. **The distinction between nation and race**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] warns against confusing [[nation|nationality]] with race. The rights of a nation to self-government do not imply racial hierarchy. In relations with "weaker [[nation|people]]s," liberal principles require the protection of elementary rights — freedom from violence, expropriation, and exploitation — rather than the imposition of Western political forms that may destroy traditional social structures (007-097).

## In Liberalism

[[nation|Nation]]al liberty features prominently in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s portrait of [[gladstone|Gladstone]]. [[gladstone|Gladstone]]'s commitment to Irish [[home-rule|Home Rule]] and his defence of Greek and Armenian national causes were expressions of a consistent principle:

> All the greater was the courage of the statesman who could throw himself at that time upon the inherent force of national liberty and international fair dealing.
> (005-234)

The Gladstonian principle, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] defines it, is that "reasons of State justify nothing that is not justified already by the human conscience" (005-234). Applied to national questions, this means that the interests of the state can never justify the oppression of a [[nation|people]]. The statesman represents a nation that is "a member of the community of the world," with obligations that extend beyond national borders.

In the twentieth century, the principle of national liberty was taken up in [[woodrow-wilson|Woodrow Wilson]]'s doctrine of self-determination and, after the Second World War, in the decolonization movement that reshaped the political map of Asia and Africa.
