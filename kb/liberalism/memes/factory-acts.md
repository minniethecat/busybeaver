## Definition

The Factory Acts were a series of legislative measures enacted in [[england|Britain]] beginning in 1802 that progressively regulated industrial working conditions. Starting with protections for child apprentices in cotton mills, the legislation expanded over the course of the nineteenth century to limit working hours for women and children, mandate safety standards, and eventually cover most categories of industrial employment. By the early twentieth century, the Factory Acts had grown into a comprehensive industrial code governing the relationship between employer and operative.

In the history of liberal thought, the Factory Acts mark a decisive turning point. They represent the moment at which liberalism began to question the adequacy of unrestricted [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]] and to accept that the state has a legitimate role in protecting the weaker party in economic relations.

## Historical Development

The first Factory Act of 1802, prompted by the appalling conditions in cotton mills — where pauper children were worked for sixteen hours a day — was modest in scope and poorly enforced. But it established a precedent that would be built upon repeatedly over the following century. The Factory Act of 1833 created a professional inspectorate; the Act of 1844 limited the working day for women to twelve hours and required machinery fencing; the Act of 1847 established the ten-hour day for women and children in textile mills.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] situates this development within the broader trajectory of liberal thought:

> The old restrictions had not been formally withdrawn before a new process of regulation began. The conditions produced by the new factory system shocked the public conscience; and as early as 1802 we find the first of a long series of laws, out of which has grown an industrial code that year by year follows the life of the operative, in his relations with his employer, into more minute detail.
> (008-083)

## Key Arguments

1. **The tension with [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]]**: The Factory Acts directly challenged the [[classical-liberalism|classical liberal]] commitment to [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]]. If two sane adults freely agree to terms of employment, on what grounds can the state intervene? The early Factory Acts dodged this question by focusing on children, whom even [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] conceded stood in a different category. But as legislation extended to adult women and eventually to adult men, the theoretical tension became impossible to ignore (008-083).

2. **From doubt to advocacy**: The first Factory Acts were "contemplated with doubt and distrust by many men of Liberal sympathies" precisely because they infringed [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]]. But over time, "men of the keenest Liberal sympathies have come not merely to accept but eagerly to advance the extension of public control in the industrial sphere" (008-083). This shift was not, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s view, a betrayal of liberal principles but a deepening of them.

3. **The social conscience and collective responsibility**: The Factory Acts expressed a growing recognition that liberty cannot be purely negative — it requires positive conditions. The "collective responsibility in the matter of the education and even the feeding of children, the housing of the industrial population, the care of the sick and aged, the provision of the means of regular employment" flowed from the same moral impulse that had earlier driven the abolition of slavery and the extension of the franchise (008-083).

## In Liberalism

The Factory Acts occupy a pivotal position in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s argument for a "new [[liberalism|Liberalism]]." They illustrate the transition from the negative liberty of the classical economists — the removal of external restraints — to the positive liberty of the twentieth century — the creation of conditions in which every person can develop their capacities.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] poses the question squarely: does this expansion of state control represent "a change of principle or of application"? His answer is that it is a change of application. The principle remains the same — the liberation of human potential — but the application changes as the obstacles to that liberation change. In an age of concentrated industrial power, the unregulated [[economic-liberty|freedom of contract]] had become a means by which the strong oppressed the weak. The Factory Acts restored the balance.

This argument would prove enormously influential, shaping the [[liberal-party|Liberal Party]]'s social [[social-reform|reform]]s of 1906–1914 — including [[old-age-pensions|old-age pension]]s, national insurance, and labour exchanges — which [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] himself helped to theorize.
