## Definition

The Petition of Right was a major English constitutional document passed by [[parliament|Parliament]] in 1628 and reluctantly accepted by Charles I. It set out four specific grievances against the Crown — arbitrary imprisonment, taxation without parliamentary consent, the billeting of soldiers on civilians, and the use of martial law against civilians — and demanded that each be remedied. It is, together with Magna Carta (1215) and the [[habeas-corpus|Habeas Corpus Act]] (1679), one of the foundational documents of English constitutional liberty.

In the liberal tradition, the Petition of Right marks the first systematic parliamentary attempt to subject the royal prerogative to law. It was, as [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] emphasizes, the opening salvo in the seventeenth-century struggle that would establish the principle of government under law.

## Historical Development

By the 1620s, Charles I's financial and military policies had brought the Crown into open conflict with [[parliament|Parliament]]. To fund his foreign wars without summoning [[parliament|Parliament]] — which might refuse supply or demand concessions — Charles resorted to forced loans, imprisoning those who refused to pay. He billeted soldiers on private houses and declared martial law in coastal areas to facilitate military preparations.

Sir Edward Coke, the great common lawyer turned parliamentarian, led the drafting of the Petition of Right as a restatement of ancient liberties that the King was bound to respect. The Petition did not create new law; rather, it declared what the law already was, citing Magna Carta and other medieval precedents to demonstrate that Charles's actions were not merely unpopular but unlawful.

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] places the Petition at the very beginning of the liberal story:

> Arbitrary government in this form was one of the first objects of attack by the English Parliament in the seventeenth century, and this first liberty of the subject was vindicated by the Petition of Right, and again by the Habeas Corpus Act.
> (002-039)

The Petition did not, in itself, solve the problem. Charles accepted it only under financial duress, and within a year he was evading its provisions. The struggle between Crown and [[parliament|Parliament]] continued through the Civil War, the Restoration, and the Glorious Revolution. But the Petition established the terms on which the contest would be fought: law against prerogative, [[parliament|Parliament]] against the Crown, the rights of the subject against the claims of the state.

## Key Arguments

1. **The primacy of legal liberty**: The Petition of Right is the first major expression of what would become the foundational liberal insight — that the first liberty is the right to be dealt with in accordance with law. Before freedom of speech, freedom of religion, or freedom of association, there must be security of the person against arbitrary power (002-039).

2. **[[rights|Rights]] as restatements, not creations**: The Petition's method — declaring ancient liberties rather than inventing new ones — reflects a deep feature of English constitutional thought. [[rights|Rights]] are not granted by the sovereign; they are inherent, and the sovereign's obligation is to recognize and respect them. This conception would later be taken up by the [[united-states|America]]n revolutionaries and incorporated into the Bill of [[rights|Rights]].

3. **Law as the foundation of liberty**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] draws from the Petition's story a conclusion that applies to the whole liberal tradition: "It is significant of much that this first step in liberty should be in reality nothing more nor less than a demand for law" (002-039). [[liberty|Liberty]] is achieved not by abolishing law but by ensuring that law binds the ruler as well as the ruled.

## In Liberalism

For [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]], the Petition of Right inaugurates the liberal method: the systematic subjection of power to principle. Every subsequent liberal achievement — the abolition of slavery, the [[factory-acts|Factory Acts]], the enfranchisement of women — follows the same logic. An existing practice is challenged not because it is unpopular but because it is inconsistent with principles that society already professes.

The Petition's influence extends far beyond [[england|England]]. It was cited by the [[united-states|America]]n colonists in their resistance to British taxation, and its principles were incorporated into the US Constitution's provisions on habeas corpus, due process, and the quartering of soldiers. The [[declaration-of-independence|Declaration of Independence]] itself is, in form, a petition of right — a statement of grievances against a sovereign who has violated the compact between ruler and ruled.
