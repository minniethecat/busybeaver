## Overview

Manchester was the industrial heart of nineteenth-century [[england|England]] and the intellectual home of the [[economic-liberty|free trade]] movement that shaped classical [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. The city gave its name to the "[[manchester-school|Manchester School]]" — the group of economic and political thinkers, led by [[Richard Cobden]] and [[John Bright]], who argued that unrestricted commerce, non-intervention in foreign affairs, and minimal government were the foundations of both prosperity and peace.

## Political and Historical Context

Manchester's rapid growth as a manufacturing centre during the Industrial Revolution created a new class of industrialists and merchants who resented the landed aristocracy's political dominance. The city became the headquarters of the [[Anti-Corn Law League]] (founded 1838), which campaigned successfully for the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846. [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] himself worked for "finally emancipating Manchester from manorial rights that have no place in the nineteenth century," linking the city's local grievances to the national campaign for [[economic-liberty|free trade]]. (Chapter III)

## Role in Liberalism

The [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s philosophy underlies the laissez-faire tradition within [[liberalism|Liberalism]]. Its teaching held that the unfettered action of the individual is "the mainspring of all progress," starting from economic premises: [[economic-liberty|free trade]] would expand commerce, reduce the cost of living, and knit nations together in peaceful interdependence. The School advocated a connected series of principles — [[personal-liberty|personal freedom]], colonial freedom, international freedom — with practical consequences including non-intervention, peace, reduced armaments, and lower taxation. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] acknowledged that the Manchester doctrine contained "elements of a more constructive view" than its critics allowed, particularly in [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]'s support for universal education. However, he judged that the movement's restricted view of government proved inadequate to address the social costs of industrialisation, including child labour and urban poverty. (Chapter III, Chapter VIII)

## Key Figures and Events

- **[[Richard Cobden]]** (1804–1865): Leader of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], principal exponent of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s [[economic-liberty|free trade]] philosophy
- **[[John Bright]]** (1811–1889): [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]'s parliamentary ally and fellow Manchester-based advocate of [[economic-liberty|free trade]] and non-intervention
- **[[Anti-Corn Law League]]**: Founded in Manchester, 1838; achieved repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], 1846
- **[[Manchester School]]**: The economic and political doctrine identified with Manchester's industrial and commercial class
