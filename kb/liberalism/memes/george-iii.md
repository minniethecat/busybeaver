# George III

George III (1738–1820) was King of [[england|Great Britain]] and [[ireland|Ireland]] from 1760 until his death. His reign, one of the longest in British history, encompassed the [[american-revolution|American Revolution]], the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]ary and [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars, and the beginnings of the Industrial Revolution. In the history of liberal thought, George III occupies a significant place as the monarch whose fiscal policies inadvertently helped to crystallise the principle that liberty requires [[political-freedom|responsible government]].

## Fiscal Liberty and the American Crisis

The connection between taxation and [[political-freedom|political liberty]] was forged in the crucible of seventeenth-century English constitutional conflicts, but it was George III's government that demonstrated the principle's transatlantic reach. The attempt to raise revenue from the [[united-states|America]]n colonies without their consent provoked a crisis that would permanently reshape the relationship between executive power and popular sovereignty:

> George III brought things to a head in America by the same infallible method. The immediate cause of the French Revolution was the refusal of the nobles and the clergy to bear their share of the financial burden. But fiscal liberty raises more searching questions than juristic liberty. It is not enough that taxes should be fixed by a law applying universally and impartially, for taxes vary from year to year in accordance with public needs, and while other laws may remain stable and unchanged for an indefinite period, taxation must, in the nature of the case, be adjustable. (002-007)

The [[united-states|America]]n colonists' rallying cry of "no taxation without representation" expressed a demand that went beyond the mere amount or legality of particular taxes. It asserted that fiscal policy must be subject to direct and constant popular supervision — or, in short, that liberty requires [[political-freedom|responsible government]].

## The Enclosure of the Commons

The reign of George III also coincided with the acceleration of the enclosure movement, which transformed the English rural landscape and dispossessed the peasantry of their traditional access to common land. This process, which had begun in the fifteenth century, was completed "between the reigns of George II and Queen Victoria" (004-011). The concentration of land ownership that resulted would later become a central target of liberal and radical criticism, from [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]'s campaign for [[economic-liberty|free trade]] in land to the land nationalisation proposals of [[henry-george|Henry George]] and the social liberals.

## Legacy for Liberal Thought

George III's significance for the liberal tradition is primarily negative but instructive. His reign illustrated, in the most dramatic fashion, the consequences of executive power unrestrained by popular consent. The [[united-states|America]]n crisis under his rule gave practical urgency to the abstract principles of the social contract theorists, demonstrating that the fiscal and political dimensions of liberty were inseparable. The demand for representative control over taxation — born in the struggles against Stuart absolutism and brought to fruition in the conflict with George III's government — remains a cornerstone of liberal constitutionalism.
