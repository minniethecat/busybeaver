## Origins and Purpose

The Anti-Corn Law League was formed in 1838 in [[manchester|Manchester]] as a response to the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], the protective tariffs on imported grain that had been imposed in the aftermath of the [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars. In [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s account, the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] "were conceived in the interest of the governing classes" and "frankly imposed a tax on the food of the masses for the benefit of the landlords." (004-002) The League's formation occurred at a moment of acute economic distress: "The divorce of the labourer from the land was complete at the time when the Anti-Corn Law League was formed. The mass of the English peasantry were landless labourers working for a weekly wage of about ten or twelve shillings, and often for a good deal less." (004-003)

## Leaders and Methods

The League was led by [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]], two [[manchester|Manchester]] manufacturers who became the most prominent Radical politicians of their generation. It pioneered modern techniques of mass political agitation — public meetings, pamphleteering, petition campaigns, and the strategic use of statistics — that would serve as a model for [[social-reform|reform]] movements throughout the following century. [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] provided the analytical rigour, grounding the case for [[economic-liberty|free trade]] in detailed economic data, while [[john-bright|Bright]] contributed the moral passion and oratory that [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] still recalled as "the eloquence of [[john-bright|Bright]]." (009-015)

## The Case for Repeal

The League's argument was simultaneously economic and moral. Economically, the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] raised the price of bread — the staple food of the working poor — and diverted resources from the industrial economy to an inefficient agricultural sector. Morally, the laws represented class legislation of the most flagrant kind: a transfer of wealth from the many to the few, enforced by the power of the State. "Give to every man the right to buy in the cheapest and sell in the dearest market," (004-002) urged the [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite, and the natural harmony of interests would ensure prosperity for all. Free trade was inseparable from peace, for "Free commercial intercourse between nations would engender mutual knowledge, and knit the severed [[nation|people]]s by countless ties of business interests." (004-002)

## Success and Repeal

The League achieved its objective with the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 by [[robert-peel|Sir Robert Peel]]'s Conservative government — a political earthquake that split the [[tory-party|Conservative Party]] and paved the way for a generation of Liberal ascendancy. The repeal was the decisive victory of the industrial and commercial interest over the landed interest, and it established [[economic-liberty|free trade]] as the economic orthodoxy of Victorian [[england|Britain]]. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] considered it the crowning achievement of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and the proof that organised public opinion, when driven by moral conviction and economic reason, could overcome entrenched privilege.

## Legacy in Liberal Thought

The Anti-Corn Law League's significance extended beyond its immediate goal. It established a model of liberal [[social-reform|reform]] — peaceful, democratic, evidence-based, and morally framed — that shaped British political culture for generations. [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] acknowledged its limitations while affirming its fundamental importance: the League had demonstrated that liberty, equality, and prosperity were not opposed but complementary, and that democratic agitation could achieve what deference and oligarchy could not. The task for the twentieth century, as he saw it, was to carry forward "the same logical cogency, the same practical resourcefulness, the same driving force that inspired the earlier [[radicalism|Radicals]]." (009-015)
