## Definition

National Insurance is a system of state-administered [[welfare-state|social insurance]] in which working [[nation|people]], employers, and the state collectively contribute to a fund that provides income support during periods of sickness, unemployment, invalidity, and old age. It differs from poor relief in being contributory rather than charitable: the beneficiary receives support as a matter of right, purchased through contributions, rather than as a dole conditioned on [[poverty|destitution]].

The National Insurance Act of 1911, introduced by [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse#David Lloyd George|David Lloyd George]] as Chancellor of the Exchequer in [[william-ewart-gladstone#Herbert Henry Asquith|H.H. Asquith's]] Liberal [[state|Government]], was the first comprehensive scheme of its kind in [[england|Britain]] and a landmark of New Liberal social [[social-reform|reform]].

## Historical Development

The movement for [[welfare-state|social insurance]] in [[england|Britain]] has its origins in the perceived inadequacy of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]. The "principle of 1834" — that the lot of the pauper should be made "less eligible" than that of the independent labourer — had produced a system that deterred [[poverty|destitution]] only by threatening the workhouse. By the late nineteenth century, as the researches of [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse#Charles Booth|Charles Booth]] and [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse#Seebohm Rowntree|Seebohm Rowntree]] revealed the extent of poverty among the working population, it was clear that the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] was failing to meet the needs of a civilized society.

The [[old-age-pensions|Old Age Pensions]] Act of 1908 marked the first decisive break with [[poor-law|Poor Law]] principles. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explains, the pension system "throws over the test of [[poverty|destitution]]. It provides a certain minimum, a basis to go upon, a foundation upon which independent thrift may hope to build up a sufficiency" (008-343). It was, he insisted, "not a narcotic but a stimulus to self help."

The National Insurance Act of 1911 extended this logic from old age to sickness, invalidity, and unemployment. Modelled in part on the German system introduced by [[otto-von-bismarck|Bismarck]] in the 1880s, the British scheme was adapted to Liberal principles: it was administered through friendly societies and trade unions rather than a central bureaucracy, preserving the element of voluntary association within a compulsory framework.

## Key Arguments

1. **Insurance, not charity**: The defining innovation of National Insurance was that it replaced the charitable logic of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] with the contractual logic of insurance. The beneficiary had paid for their entitlement; the state was not dispensing alms but honouring a bargain.

2. **The insufficiency of self-help**: The competitive system, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] argues, fails to deliver the "living wage" necessary for a worker to save independently against all the contingencies of life. "The increase of wages does not appear to be by any means proportionate to the general growth of wealth" (008-320). If the system cannot provide the means of independence, the state must step in to supply what the market cannot.

3. **A basis for thrift, not a substitute for it**: [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] is careful to distinguish National Insurance from a "dole." The state provides "a substructure on which it would be possible for him to rear the fabric of a real sufficiency" (008-349). The individual is still required to work and save; the difference is that the foundation on which they build is secure.

4. **Reciprocal obligation**: National Insurance embodies the principle "that the individual cannot stand alone, but that between him and the State there is a reciprocal obligation" (008-320). The citizen owes society the duty of industrious self-maintenance; society owes the citizen the conditions in which that duty can be fulfilled.

## In Liberalism

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] treats National Insurance as one expression of the "Liberal [[socialism|Socialism]]" that he defends as the logical extension of Liberal principles into the social and economic sphere. Writing before the 1911 Act had passed into law, he describes the movement toward State-aided insurance as an experiment already underway:

> Another method is that of State-aided insurance, and on these lines Liberal legislators have been experimenting in the hope of dealing with sickness, invalidity, and one portion of the problem of unemployment.
> (008-345)

Crucially, [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] insists that such measures are not philanthropy but justice. The question, in his view, is not whether the state should be charitable to the poor but whether the existing distribution of the social product is just. The "living wage," the Old Age Pension, and National Insurance are all attempts to answer that question in practical legislation.

The broader significance of National Insurance in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s argument is that it demonstrates how far [[liberalism|Liberalism]] had travelled from the laissez-faire orthodoxy of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]. The state that guarantees liberty is not a minimal state but a state that intervenes actively to secure the material conditions without which liberty is a mockery.
