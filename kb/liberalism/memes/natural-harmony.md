## Definition

The doctrine of Natural Harmony holds that there exists a spontaneous coincidence between individual self-interest and the [[common-good|public good]]. As [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] explains, the theory arose in the eighteenth-century economic sphere from the observation that "if all artificial barriers are removed, [the individual] will find the occupation which best suits his capacities, and this will be the occupation in which he will be most productive, and therefore, socially, most valuable" (003-008).

The doctrine was the economic complement to the political theory of [[natural-order|natural order]]. Where the natural order theory established that individuals possess rights prior to government, the natural harmony theory established that individuals pursuing those rights would, in aggregate, produce socially beneficial outcomes without the need for governmental direction.

## The Mechanism of Harmony

The theory's mechanism is elegantly simple: each individual, guided by self-interest, will follow "the lines of greatest productivity" (003-008). To sell goods, one must produce "things which others need, things, therefore, of social value" (003-008). To maximize profit, one will make "that for which he can obtain the highest price, and this will be that for which, at the particular time and place and in relation to his particular capacities, there is the greatest need" (003-008). To earn the best wage, the worker will "find the employer who will pay him best, and that will be the employer to whom he can do the best service" (003-008).

The conclusion is sweeping: "Self-interest, if enlightened and unfettered, will, in short, lead him to conduct coincident with public interest. There is, in this sense, a natural harmony between the individual and society" (003-008).

## Government's Role: The Ring-Keeper

The natural harmony doctrine prescribed a sharply limited role for government. True, the harmony "might require a certain amount of education and enlightenment to make it effective. What it did not require was governmental 'interference,' which would always hamper the causes making for its smooth and effectual operation" (003-008). The memorable formulation was that "[[state|Government]] must keep the ring, and leave it for individuals to play out the game" (003-008).

This minimal-state vision aligned with the [[laissez-faire]] [[political-economy|economics]] of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and provided the intellectual foundation for [[free-trade|Free Trade]] advocacy. If natural harmony was real, then protective tariffs, trade restrictions, and industrial regulation were not merely inefficient but actually counterproductive — they disrupted the spontaneous order through which individual effort was naturally channelled toward social benefit.

## The Cobdenite Application

The [[richard-cobden|Cobdenite]] school adopted the natural harmony doctrine as the basis for its practical programme. [[richard-cobden|Cobden]] and his followers were "saturated with the conviction that the unfettered action of the individual is the mainspring of all progress" (004-001). [[free-trade|Free Trade]], on this view, was not merely good [[political-economy|economics]] — it was the mechanism through which the natural harmony of interests would be realized on a global scale, knitting nations together through commercial interdependence and thereby rendering war irrational.

The [[richard-cobden|Cobden]]ite programme linked domestic [[economic-liberty|economic freedom]] with [[peace|international peace]]. If trade were freed, national antagonisms — "aroused and maintained by a protective system" (004-001) — would dissolve. [[free-trade|Free Trade]] meant peace.

## Limitations

[[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] notes that the natural harmony doctrine, "so completed, forms a conception of human society which is prima facie workable, which, in fact, contains important elements of truth" (003-008), but its limitations became apparent with industrial experience. Factory conditions, urban squalor, and the vulnerability of workers to economic fluctuation demonstrated that the unfettered pursuit of self-interest did not always produce social harmony. The [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] and subsequent [[social-reform|social legislation]] represented a recognition that some forms of "interference" were necessary to secure the conditions under which liberty could be genuinely exercised.

The natural harmony doctrine thus occupies an ambivalent position in Liberal history: it provided the intellectual energy for the great nineteenth-century achievements of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] and the dismantling of mercantilist restrictions, but its limitations spurred the development of the [[new-liberalism|New Liberalism]] that would define the early twentieth century.
