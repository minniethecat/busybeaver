Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman (1836–1908) was a British Liberal statesman who served as Prime Minister from 1905 to 1908. A follower of [[gladstone|Gladstone]], he used the brief term of his power to reverse the policy of racial domination in [[south-africa|South Africa]] and to prove the value of the Gladstonian trust in the recuperative force of political freedom (005-020). [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]] identifies Campbell-Bannerman as the figure who revived the idea of justice in [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] at its lowest ebb.

## The South Africa Speech

Campbell-Bannerman's defining moment came in the summer of 1901, during the [[south-african-war|South African War]], when he set himself against the stream of militant sentiment and challenged the methods of the war in what became a classic phrase. At the time, the speech was supposed to have irretrievably ruined his political career. Instead, it proved to be the turning point both for him and for his party (009-031).

> It was with the outbreak of the South African War that the new development of Conservative policy first compelled the average Liberal to consider his position. It needed the shock of an outspoken violation of right to stir him; and we may date the revival of the idea of justice in the party as an organized force from the speech in the summer of 1901 in which Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman set himself against the stream of militant sentiment and challenged in a classic phrase the methods of the war.
>
> (009-031)

From the day of this speech, Campbell-Bannerman — a man with no marked genius but that of character and the insight which character gave into the minds of his followers — acquired in his party the position of a [[gladstone|Gladstone]]. His name, hitherto greeted with indifference, became a recognized signal for the cheers of a political meeting. This was, in [[leonard-trelawny-hobhouse|Hobhouse]]'s assessment, the first and fundamental victory: the reinstatement of the idea of Right in the mind of [[liberalism|Liberalism]] (009-031).

## Gladstonian Legacy

Campbell-Bannerman was a direct heir to the Gladstonian tradition in foreign and imperial policy. Where [[gladstone|Gladstone]] had insisted that reasons of State justify nothing that is not justified already by the human conscience, Campbell-Bannerman applied the same principle in the aftermath of imperial overreach. His government's restoration of self-government to the former Boer republics demonstrated that trust in political freedom could succeed where military domination had failed (005-020).

> His follower, Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman, used the brief term of his power to reverse the policy of racial domination in South Africa and to prove the value of the old Gladstonian trust in the recuperative force of political freedom.
>
> (005-020)

## Liberal Revival

Campbell-Bannerman's premiership, though brief, coincided with and helped to catalyze a broader Liberal revival. The Conservative disillusionment that followed the [[south-african-war|South African War]] unlocked the floodgates for the advancing tide of Liberal recovery. Under Campbell-Bannerman's leadership, [[liberal-party|the Liberal Party]] rediscovered its moral purpose and laid the groundwork for the great [[social-reform|reform]]ing government of [[herbert-henry-asquith|Asquith]] that would follow. The alliance between [[liberalism|Liberalism]] and the emerging [[labour-party|Labour Party]], the reassertion of [[free-trade|Free Trade]], and the commitment to social [[social-reform|reform]] all took shape during his tenure.
