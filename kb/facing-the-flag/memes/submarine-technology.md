Submarine technology occupies a distinctive place in *Facing the Flag*, extending Jules Verne's lifelong fascination with underwater vessels. While less celebrated than the *Nautilus* of *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*, the submarine tug employed by the pirates of [[Back Cup]] represents a crucial narrative and thematic element.

## The Submarine Tug

The most significant underwater vessel in the novel is the submersible tug used to tow the schooner [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]] into the concealed entrance of [[Back Cup]]. This vessel represents a fusion of maritime stealth and practical engineering.

> Alongside — a submersible boat, a submarine tug, worked by a screw set in motion by a dynamo. (013-036)

The submarine tug solves a critical operational problem for [[Ker Karraje]]'s organization: how to bring a surface vessel through a submerged entrance into a hidden cavern without detection. The tug submerges, attaches to the schooner's hull, and tows it underwater through the narrow channel that leads into Back Cup's interior lagoon.

## Engineering Context

Verne's depiction of the submarine tug reflects the state of submarine engineering at the close of the nineteenth century:

| Technical Element | Novel Depiction | Historical Context |
|-------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Propulsion | Screw propeller, electric dynamo | Electric submarines were experimental in the 1890s |
| Function | Tug and tow operations | Real submarine tugs were in early development |
| Stealth | Underwater approach to hidden entrance | Submarine concealment was a key military interest |

> Why is the Ebba obliged to have recourse to this submarine towing system? Why is she not able to enter the cavern under sail or steam? (013-039)

The narrator's question highlights the technical mystery at the heart of Back Cup's concealment. The submarine tug is not merely a convenience but a necessity — the entrance to the cavern is entirely submerged, accessible only from beneath the surface.

## Thematic Significance

### Technology as Both Tool and Threat

The submarine technology in *Facing the Flag* embodies a recurring Verne theme: technological innovation as a double-edged sword. The submarine tug enables [[Ker Karraje]]'s illicit operations, while the [[Fulgurator]] — another technological marvel — threatens global security. Both technologies are morally neutral; their ethical valence derives entirely from who controls them.

### The Hidden and the Visible

Submarine operation is fundamentally about concealment — movement unseen beneath the waves. This mirrors the novel's broader concern with hidden identities ([[Ker Karraje]] posing as [[count-dartigas]]), hidden locations ([[Back Cup]]), and hidden knowledge ([[Thomas Roch]]'s closely guarded secret). The submarine is the physical manifestation of the novel's epistemology of concealment.

### Continuity with the Verne Canon

The submarine tug connects *Facing the Flag* to Verne's most famous creation, the *Nautilus*. Where Captain Nemo's submarine represented individual genius and moral complexity, the Back Cup submarine tug represents collective criminal enterprise. The contrast underscores the darker tone of this later novel — technology has passed from romantic adventure into strategic threat.

## Narrative Function

The submarine tug appears at pivotal moments in the plot, particularly during the climactic [[Escape from Back Cup]] sequence. Its role in the pirates' comings and goings makes it essential to the novel's logistics of concealment and revelation.
