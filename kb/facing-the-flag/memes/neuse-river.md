## Neuse River

The Neuse River is a waterway in [[North Carolina]], United States, that plays a crucial geographical role in the opening chapters of Jules Verne's *Facing the Flag* (1896). [[Healthful House]], the private sanatorium where [[Thomas Roch]] is confined, is situated on the estuary of the Neuse, and the river serves as the route by which [[Captain Spade]] and the crew of the [[Ebba Schooner]] execute the kidnapping that launches the novel's central conflict.

## In the Novel

The Neuse River is introduced in the novel's opening description of [[North Carolina]]'s geography. [[Healthful House]] is situated on the right bank of the river, near the point where it empties into [[Pamlico Sound]]:

> "The capital of North Carolina is situated on the estuary of the Neuse River, which empties itself into Pamlico Sound." (001-014)

The river's estuary provides the sanatorium with a pleasant setting — cool breezes sweep across the water, and the wide mouth of the Neuse gives the institution a sense of openness and tranquility. This peaceful geography, however, also makes [[Healthful House]] vulnerable to approach from the sea:

> "At the back lay the wide estuary of the Neuse, swept by the cool breezes of Pamlico Sound." (001-018)

The Neuse River is the route of the kidnapping. On the night of the abduction, the [[Ebba Schooner]] lies anchored at the mouth of the Neuse, and the raiding party uses the riverbank to access [[Healthful House]]:

> "The schooner had anchored at the entrance to the Neuse, at the foot of the hill upon which Healthful House was situated." (003-022)

After the kidnapping, [[Simon Hart]] — bound and gagged — is carried along the right bank of the Neuse to a waiting boat. The river becomes the first leg of his journey into captivity, the familiar waterway giving way to the open expanse of [[Pamlico Sound]] and eventually the [[Atlantic Ocean]]:

> "They skirted the right bank of the Neuse." (003-031)

Throughout his initial disorientation, Hart uses the Neuse as a geographical anchor. He recognizes the feel of the river's current, the distinctive motion of the water that tells him he is still in familiar territory — at least for the moment:

> "I know that I am afloat on the Neuse, though I was conveyed a short distance in a boat." (008-011)

The river also serves as a narrative boundary. As the kidnappers' boat moves down the Neuse toward [[Pamlico Sound]], Hart watches the banks become indistinct, the landmarks of his known world fading into the darkness:

> "The left bank of the sinuous Neuse became more and more indistinct." (004-047)

## Connections

The Neuse River connects to multiple key elements:

- **[[Healthful House]]**: Located on the right bank of the Neuse estuary.
- **[[North Carolina]]**: The state through which the Neuse flows.
- **[[Pamlico Sound]]**: The body of water into which the Neuse empties, forming the escape route.
- **[[Ebba Schooner]]**: Anchored at the mouth of the Neuse during the kidnapping.
- **[[Simon Hart]]**: Carried along the Neuse's bank as a captive, his last contact with familiar territory.
