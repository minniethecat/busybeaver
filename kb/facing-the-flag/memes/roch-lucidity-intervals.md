Thomas Roch's Intervals of Lucidity were the alternating periods of sanity and madness that defined his mental condition throughout *Facing the Flag*. During these intervals, the inventor's true character — his intelligence, his [[patriotism]], and his capacity for moral reasoning — briefly surfaced before being submerged again by his monomaniacal obsession with the [[Fulgurator]] and its monetary value.

## The Pattern of Madness and Sanity

Roch's mental state followed a recognizable pattern. For extended periods, he was consumed by his fixation on the [[Fulgurator]], demanding payment, suspecting conspiracy, and raging against the governments that had rejected him. Then, unpredictably, the cloud would lift:

> His reason was affected only on the one point of his invention. On all other subjects he was perfectly sane. (001-011)

During these lucid intervals, Roch could discuss subjects unrelated to his invention with clarity and intelligence. He recognized [[Simon Hart]] (as Gaydon), responded appropriately to social cues, and even displayed flashes of the patriotic feeling that would ultimately determine his fate.

## The Climactic Lucidity

The novel's most consequential interval of lucidity occurred during the [[final-naval-battle|final battle]], when the sight of the [[Tricolor Flag]] triggered not merely a return to sanity but a transformation of character. In that moment, Roch's lucidity was not a remission of madness but its cure — the reassertion of an identity that his obsession had suppressed but never extinguished.
