[003-001] Or perhaps the Count had been inspired by curiosity to meet [[thomas-roch|Thomas Roch]]? This curiosity would have been legitimate and natural enough in view of the universal renown of the French inventor. Fancy--a [[genius-and-madness|mad genius]] who claimed that his discoveries were destined to revolutionize the methods of modern military art!

[003-002] As he had notified [[director-of-healthful-house|the director]] he would do, the [[count-d-artigas|Count d'Artigas]] presented himself in the afternoon at the door of [[healthful-house|Healthful House]], accompanied by [[captain-spade|Captain Spade]], the commander of the _Ebba_.

[003-003] In conformity with orders given, both were admitted and conducted to the office of the director. The latter received his distinguished visitor with _empressement_, placed himself at his disposal, and intimated his intention of personally conducting him over the establishment, not being willing to concede to anybody else the honor of being his _cicerone_. The Count on his part was profuse in the expression of his thanks for the considerations extended to him.

[003-004] They went over the common rooms and private habitations of the establishment, the director prattling unceasingly about the care with which the patients were tended--much better care, if he was to be believed, than they could possibly have had in the bosoms of their families--and priding himself upon the results achieved, and which had earned for the place its well-merited success.

[003-005] The [[count-dartigas|Count d'Artigas]] listened to his ceaseless chatter with apparent interest, probably in order the better to dissemble the real motive of his visit. However, after going the rounds for an hour he ventured to remark: "Have you not among your patients, sir, one anent whom there was a great deal of talk some time ago, and whose presence here contributed in no small measure to attract public attention to Healthful House?"

[003-006] "You refer to Thomas Roch, I presume, Count?" queried the director.

[003-007] "Precisely--that Frenchman--that inventor--whose mental condition is said to be very precarious."

[003-008] "Very precarious, Count, and happily so, perhaps! In my opinion humanity has nothing to gain by his discoveries, the application of which would increase the already too numerous means of destruction."

[003-009] "You speak wisely, sir, and I entirely agree with you. Real progress does not lie in that direction, and I regard as inimical to society all those who seek to follow it. But has this inventor entirely lost the use of his intellectual faculties?"

[003-010] "Entirely, no; save as regards the ordinary things of life. In this respect he no longer possesses either comprehension or responsibility.

[003-011] His genius as an inventor, however, remains intact; it has survived his moral degeneracy, and, had his insensate demands been complied with, I have no doubt he would have produced a new war engine--which the world can get along very well without."

[003-012] "Very well without, as you say, sir," re-echoed the [[ker-karraje|Count d'Artigas]], and Captain Spade nodded approval.

[003-013] "But you will be able to judge for yourself, Count, for here is the pavilion occupied by Thomas Roch. If his [[imprisonment-and-captivity|confinement]] is well justified from the point of view of public security he is none the less treated with all the consideration due to him and the attention which his condition necessitates. Besides, Healthful House is beyond the reach of indiscreet persons who might...."

[003-014] The director completed the phrase with a significant motion of his head--which brought an imperceptible smile to the lips of the stranger.

[003-015] "But," asked the Count, "is Thomas Roch never left alone?"

[003-016] "Never, Count, never. He has a permanent attendant in whom we have implicit confidence, who speaks his language and keeps the closest possible watch upon him. If in some way or other some indication relative to his discovery were to escape him, it would be immediately noted down and its value would be passed upon by those competent to judge."

[003-017] Here the Count d'Artigas stole a rapid and meaning glance at Captain Spade, who responded with a gesture which said plainly enough: "I understand." And had any one observed the captain during the visit, they could not have failed to remark that he examined with the greatest minuteness that portion of the park surrounding Pavilion No. 17, and the different paths leading to the latter--probably in view of some prearranged scheme.

[003-018] The garden of the pavilion was near the high wall surrounding the property, from the foot of which on the other side the hill sloped gently to the right bank of [[neuse-river|the Neuse]].

[003-019] The pavilion itself was a one-story building surmounted by a terrace in the Italian style. It contained two rooms and an ante-room with strongly-barred windows. On each side and in rear of the habitation were clusters of fine trees, which were then in full leaf. In front was a cool, green velvety lawn, ornamented with shrubs and brilliantly tinted flowers. The whole garden extended over about half an acre, and was reserved exclusively for the use of Thomas Roch, who was free to wander about it at pleasure under the [[surveillance-and-observation|surveillance]] of his guardian.

[003-020] When the Count d'Artigas, Captain Spade, and the director entered the garden, the first person they saw was [[warder-of-healthful-house|the warder]] [[gaydon|Gaydon]], standing at the door of the pavilion. Unnoticed by the director the Count d'Artigas eyed the attendant with singular persistence.

[003-021] It was not the first time that strangers had come to see the occupant of Pavilion No. 17, for the French inventor was justly regarded as the most interesting inmate of Healthful House. Nevertheless, [[simon-hart|Gaydon]]'s attention was attracted by the originality of the type presented by the two visitors, of whose nationality he was ignorant. If the name of the Count d'Artigas was not unfamiliar to him, he had never had occasion to meet that wealthy gentleman during the latter's sojourn in the eastern ports. He therefore had no idea as to who the Count was.

[003-022] Neither was he aware that [[ebba-schooner|the schooner]] _Ebba_ was then anchored at the entrance to the Neuse, at the foot of the hill upon which Healthful House was situated.

[003-023] "Gaydon," demanded the director, "where is Thomas Roch?"

[003-024] "Yonder," replied the warder, pointing to a man who was walking meditatively under the trees in rear of the pavilion.

[003-025] "The Count d'Artigas has been authorized to visit Healthful House," the director explained; "and does not wish to go away without having seen Thomas Roch, who was lately the subject of a good deal too much discussion."

[003-026] "And who would be talked about a great deal more," added the Count, "had the Federal Government not taken the precaution to confine him in this establishment."

[003-027] "A necessary precaution, Count."

[003-028] "Necessary, as you observe, Mr. Director. It is better for the peace of the world that his secret should die with him."

[003-029] After having glanced at the Count d'Artigas, Gaydon had not uttered a word; but preceding the two strangers he walked towards the clump of trees where the inventor was pacing back and forth.

[003-030] Thomas Roch paid no attention to them. He appeared to be oblivious of their presence.

[003-031] Meanwhile, Captain Spade, while being careful not to excite suspicion, had been minutely examining the immediate surroundings of the pavilion and the end of the park in which it was situated. From the top of the sloping alleys he could easily distinguish the peak of a mast which showed above the wall of the park. He recognized the peak at a glance as being that of the _Ebba_, and knew therefore that the wall at this part skirted the right bank of the Neuse.

[003-032] The Count d'Artigas' whole attention was concentrated upon the French inventor. The latter's health appeared to have suffered in no way from his eighteen months' confinement; but his queer attitude, his incoherent gestures, his haggard eye, and his indifference to what was passing around him testified only too plainly to the degeneration of his mental faculties.

[003-033] At length Thomas Roch dropped into a seat and with the end of a switch traced in the sand of the alley the outline of a fortification. Then kneeling down he made a number of little mounds that were evidently intended to represent bastions. He next plucked some leaves from a neighboring tree and stuck them in the mounds like so many tiny flags. All this was done with the utmost seriousness and without any attention whatever being paid to the onlookers.

[003-034] It was the amusement of a child, but a child would have lacked this characteristic gravity.

[003-035] "Is he then absolutely mad?" demanded the Count d'Artigas, who in spite of his habitual impassibility appeared to be somewhat disappointed.

[003-036] "I warned you, Count, that nothing could be obtained from him."

[003-037] "Couldn't he at least pay some attention to us?"

[003-038] "It would perhaps be difficult to induce him to do so."

[003-039] Then turning to the attendant: "Speak to him, Gaydon. Perhaps he will answer you."

[003-040] "Oh! he'll answer me right enough, sir, never fear," replied Gaydon.

[003-041] He went up to the inventor and touching him on the shoulder, said gently: "Thomas Roch!"

[003-042] The latter raised his head, and of the persons present he doubtless saw but his keeper, though Captain Spade had come up and all formed a circle about him.

[003-043] "Thomas Roch," continued Gaydon, speaking in English, "here are some visitors to see you. They are interested in your health--in your work."

[003-044] The last word alone seemed to rouse him from his indifference.

[003-045] "My work?" he replied, also in English, which he spoke like a native.

[003-046] Then taking a pebble between his index finger and bent thumb, as a boy plays at marbles, he projected it against one of the little sand-heaps. It scattered, and he jumped for joy.

[003-047] "Blown to pieces! The bastion is blown to pieces! My explosive has destroyed everything at one blow!" he shouted, the light of triumph flashing in his eyes.

[003-048] "You see," said the director, addressing the Count d'Artigas. "The idea of his invention never leaves him."

[003-049] "And it will die with him," affirmed the attendant.

[003-050] "Couldn't you, Gaydon, get him to talk about his fulgurator?" asked his chief.

[003-051] "I will try, if you order me to do so, sir."

[003-052] "Well, I do order you, for I think it might interest the Count d'Artigas."

[003-053] "Certainly," assented the Count, whose physiognomy betrayed no sign of the sentiments which were agitating him.

[003-054] "I ought to warn you that I risk bringing on another fit," observed Gaydon.

[003-055] "You can drop the conversation when you consider it prudent. Tell Thomas Roch that a foreigner wishes to negotiate with him for the purchase of his fulgurator."

[003-056] "But are you not afraid he may give his secret away?" questioned the Count.

[003-057] He spoke with such vivacity that Gaydon could not restrain a glance of distrust, which, however, did not appear to disturb the equanimity of that impenetrable nobleman.

[003-058] "No fear of that," said the warder. "No promise would induce him to divulge his secret. Until the millions he demands are counted into his hand he will remain as mute as a stone."

[003-059] "I don't happen to be carrying those millions about me," remarked the Count quietly.

[003-060] Gaydon again touched Roch on the shoulder and repeated: "Thomas Roch, here are some foreigners who are anxious to acquire your invention."

[003-061] The madman started.

[003-062] "My invention?" he cried. "My deflagrator?"

[003-063] And his growing animation plainly indicated the imminence of the fit that Gaydon had been apprehensive about, and which questions of this character invariably brought on.

[003-064] "How much will you give me for it--how much?" continued Roch. "How much--how much?"

[003-065] "Ten million dollars," replied Gaydon.

[003-066] "Ten millions! Ten millions! A fulgurator ten million times more powerful than anything hitherto invented! Ten millions for an autopropulsive projectile which, when it explodes, destroys everything in sight within a radius of over twelve thousand square yards! Ten millions for the only deflagrator that can provoke its explosion! Why, all the wealth of the world wouldn't suffice to purchase the secret of my engine, and rather than sell it at such a price I would cut my tongue in half with my teeth. Ten millions, when it is worth a billion--a billion--a billion!"

[003-067] It was clear that Roch had lost all notion of things, and had Gaydon offered him ten billions the madman would have replied in exactly the same manner.
