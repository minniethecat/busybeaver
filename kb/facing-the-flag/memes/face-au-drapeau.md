## *[[facing-the-flag-novel|face au drapeau]]*

*Face au drapeau* is the original French edition of [[jules-verne|jules verne]]'s novel, published in 1896. An English translation was published in the United States the following year under the title *Facing the Flag* by F. Tennyson Neely (1897), with a second English translation appearing as *For the Flag* from Sampson & Low in [[england|england]].

> "Facing the Flag — number V044 in the T&M listing of Verne's works — is an anonymous translation of Face au drapeau (1896)." (001-002)

## In the Novel

The original French title *Face au drapeau* — literally "Facing the Flag" or "Before the Flag" — carries a stronger patriotic connotation than either English translation fully captures. In the French context of 1896, the title would have evoked the figure of the soldier standing before the regimental colors, a powerful symbol of national duty and allegiance.

This original title is thematically significant, as the novel's climax hinges on Roch's reaction to the French tricolor. The flag — *le drapeau* — triggers the inventor's dormant [[patriotism|patriotism]] and leads him to sacrifice himself rather than allow his weapon to be used against [[french-government|france]]. The original title thus encodes the novel's central moral moment in a way that the English translations, by omitting the definite article, somewhat obscure.

The novel was written during a period when Verne was moving away from the optimistic scientism of his earlier *Voyages extraordinaires* toward darker, more ambivalent treatments of technology. *Face au drapeau* represents this transitional phase, combining the adventure narrative structure of Verne's earlier work with the anxieties [[About|about]] technological destruction that would characterize his later novels.

## Related Characters

- **Jules Verne**: The author, who was 68 years old when this novel was published.
- **[[thomas-roch|thomas roch]]**: The novel's protagonist, whose relationship to France mirrors Verne's own complex patriotism.

The original French text remains an important document for understanding Verne's evolving attitudes toward science, nationalism, and the moral responsibilities of the inventor at the close of the nineteenth century.
