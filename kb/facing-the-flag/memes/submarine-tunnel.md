## Submarine Tunnel

The submarine tunnel is the critical infrastructural feature of [[Back Cup]] in Jules Verne's *Facing the Flag* (1896). This submerged passage — bored through the base of the rocky islet — provides the sole maritime access between the open ocean and [[Back Cup]]'s interior cavern. The tunnel serves simultaneously as entrance, exit, and security checkpoint, making it both the pirates' greatest asset and their most vulnerable point.

> "As to the length of the submarine tunnel by which communication is obtained with the sea, I estimate it at about one hundred and fifty feet." (014-044)

## In the Novel

### Engineering and Design

The submarine tunnel is a feat of pirate engineering, carved through the limestone base of [[Back Cup]] below the waterline. Its submerged nature ensures that the cavern remains invisible to external observation — no surface entrance betrays the hidden world within:

> "The tug had disappeared through a liquid archway formed by the base of the rock. A tunnel, bored through the islet, gave access to the interior of Back Cup." (014-043)

The tunnel's dimensions accommodate the pirates' submarine tug, which serves as both transport and cargo vessel for the hidden base. The water level within the tunnel is controlled by the tides, creating a natural flushing mechanism that circulates fresh seawater through the cavern's artificial beach.

### Strategic Function

The tunnel's design embodies the pirates' security philosophy: total invisibility achieved through total inaccessibility. No vessel can enter [[Back Cup]] without navigating the submerged passage, and no external observer can detect the entrance from the surface:

> "It is impossible to escape through that submarine tunnel, or through the hole in the dome. We never know what the future may have in store for us." (020-035)

This absolute control over access makes the tunnel both a prison and a fortress. For the captives — [[Simon Hart]] and [[Thomas Roch]] — it represents the impossibility of escape:

> "It is pretty certain therefore that there is no other way of getting out besides the submarine tunnel." (021-021)

### The Tunnel as Narrative Device

The tunnel functions narratively as a threshold between worlds. Crossing through it — as [[Simon Hart]] does when first brought to [[Back Cup]] — marks the passage from the known world of nations, navies, and law to the hidden world of piracy, secrecy, and captivity:

> "The tug had disappeared through a liquid archway formed by the base of the rock. A tunnel, bored through the islet, gave access to the interior of Back Cup." (014-043)

The tunnel's final narrative role comes at the novel's climax, when the destruction of [[Back Cup]] seals the passage forever, entombing the pirates' lair beneath the Atlantic:

> "Day and night the tunnel is guarded by a detachment of the crew, under the command of Captain Spade." (025-001)

## Related Characters

- **[[Captain Spade]]**: The warden of the tunnel, responsible for controlling all maritime access to [[Back Cup]].
- **[[count-dartigas]]**: The strategist who chose [[Back Cup]] specifically for its tunnel-secured inaccessibility.
- **[[Engineer Serko]]**: The technical overseer of the tunnel's operation and the tug's navigation through it.
- **[[Simon Hart]]**: The captive observer whose study of the tunnel represents his ongoing calculation of escape possibilities.

## Key Passages

The tunnel's discovery during Hart's first approach to [[Back Cup]] establishes the islet's hidden nature:

> "The screw was worked with electricity, and a strong current rushed through the submarine tunnel. It is pretty certain therefore that there is no other way of getting out." (021-021)

The tunnel's role in the pirate economy — enabling covert raids and undetected returns — is essential to [[count-dartigas]]'s criminal enterprise:

> "Having without recourse to the submarine tunnel to get there, and day and night the tug is ready to put to sea at a moment's notice." (025-001)
