## British Admiralty

The British Admiralty is one of the governmental bodies that [[thomas-roch|thomas roch]] approached with his [[fulgurator|fulgurator]] invention in [[jules-verne|jules verne]]'s *Facing the Flag* (1896). The Admiralty's handling of Roch — more pragmatic than [[french-government|france]]'s rejection but ultimately unsuccessful — represents a middle ground between outright refusal and protective custody in the novel's spectrum of governmental responses.

> "The English being practical people, did not at first repulse Thomas Roch. They sounded him and tried to get round him; but Roch would listen to nothing. His secret was worth millions, and these millions he would have, or they would not have his secret. The Admiralty at last declined to have anything more to do with him." (002-006)

## In the Novel

The British Admiralty's engagement with Roch followed the pattern of rejection already established by France and Germany, but with an important distinction: the British were the first to recognize that negotiation, rather than outright dismissal, might be the appropriate approach to a genius inventor with a potentially revolutionary weapon.

The Admiralty's negotiators "sounded him and tried to get round him," attempting the pragmatic approach of extracting the secret through persuasion and careful maneuvering. However, they encountered Roch's immovable demand: his secret was worth millions, and he would not part with it for less. The Admiralty's practicality, encountering the inventor's inflexibility, led to the same outcome as the other governments — refusal.

> "As the result of this second rebuff Roch's anger became coupled with hatred — an instinctive hatred of humanity — especially after his pourparlers with the British Admiralty came to naught." (002-006)

## Members

- **British Admiralty Negotiators**: Unnamed officials who attempted to negotiate with Roch and "get round him."
- **The Admiralty Board**: The governing body that ultimately declined further involvement with Roch.

The British Admiralty episode serves as a turning point in Roch's psychological deterioration. Verne frames this rejection as particularly damaging because the British had initially seemed receptive — the hope of acceptance, followed by failure, deepened Roch's alienation more profoundly than the outright refusals of France and Germany had done.

The episode also reflects the geopolitical context of the 1890s, when Britain's naval supremacy was being challenged by new military technologies. The Admiralty's interest in [[roch-fulgurator-invention|the fulgurator]], even if ultimately aborted, reflects the strategic anxiety that attended the end of the nineteenth century's naval arms race.
