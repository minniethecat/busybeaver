The Pamlico Sound Lighthouse was a navigational landmark at the entrance to [[Pamlico Sound]] that marked the transition from the sheltered waters of [[North Carolina]]'s inner coast to the open [[Atlantic Ocean]]. It was one of the last American landmarks visible to the crew of the [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]] as they began their [[ebba-voyage-to-back-cup|voyage to Back Cup]].

## Role in the Voyage

The Lighthouse served as a critical navigational reference for vessels entering and leaving [[Pamlico Sound]]. For the [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]], navigating at night to avoid detection, the lighthouse's beam would have been both a guide and a hazard — useful for fixing position, but also a reminder that the channel was monitored and potentially watched.

The passage of the lighthouse marked the point of no return for the kidnapping voyage. Once the [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]] cleared the sound and entered the [[Atlantic Ocean]], the isolation of [[Thomas Roch]] and [[Simon Hart]] was complete — beyond the reach of American jurisdiction and the surveillance that had been organized after the kidnapping.

## Symbolic Significance

The lighthouse embodies the tension between visibility and concealment that runs throughout *Facing the Flag*. It is a fixed point of reference in a narrative defined by hidden passages, secret bases, and covert operations. Its beam, illuminating the darkness through which the [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]] sails, is a reminder that even in a world of shadows, there are still points of light — and that those who sail past them may never return.
