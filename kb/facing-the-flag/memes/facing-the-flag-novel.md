## Facing the Flag (novel)

*Facing the Flag* (original French title *Face au drapeau*, 1896) is a novel by [[jules-verne|jules verne]], numbered V044 in the T&M listing of Verne's works. It was first published in English as an anonymous translation by F. Tennyson Neely in the United States in 1897. A second English translation, entitled *For the Flag* and translated by Mrs. Cashel Hoey, was published by Sampson & Low in [[england|england]] the same year.

## In the Novel

*Facing the Flag* belongs to Verne's late period, a phase of his career marked by darker themes and increasing pessimism [[About|about]] technology. Unlike the optimistic scientific adventure of his earlier works like *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea* or *From the Earth to the Moon*, this novel presents technological innovation as a source of danger — a weapon so terrible that no nation can be trusted with it.

The novel is structured around [[thomas-roch|thomas roch]]'s invention of the "[[fulgurator|fulgurator]]," a weapon of mass destruction, and the efforts of various parties — governments and criminals alike — to obtain its secret. The narrative is told in the first person by [[simon-hart|simon hart]], a French engineer who poses as Roch's keeper "Gaydon" at a sanatorium in [[north-carolina|north carolina]], and who is kidnapped along with Roch when [[count-dartigas|the pirate]] [[ker-karraje|ker karraje]] abducts the inventor.

The novel incorporates elements of the espionage thriller, the maritime adventure, and the psychological case study. Its exploration of the relationship between [[genius-and-madness|genius and madness]], and its treatment of [[patriotism|patriotism]] as both a virtue and a vulnerability, distinguish it from Verne's more purely adventurous works.

## Related Characters

- **Simon Hart**: The novel's narrator.

## Key Passages

*Facing the Flag* was published during a period when Verne was increasingly concerned with the destructive potential of modern technology. The novel's dark vision of a weapon that could "revolutionize the methods of modern military art" reflects the anxieties of the pre-World War I era, anticipating the catastrophic technologies that would emerge in the twentieth century.
