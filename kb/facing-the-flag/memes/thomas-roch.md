## Thomas Roch

Thomas Roch is a French inventor and the central figure of Jules Verne's *Facing the Flag* (1896). A man of exceptional genius, Roch created the "[[fulgurator]]" — a devastating military weapon of immense power. However, after his invention was repeatedly rejected by multiple governments, his mental condition deteriorated, and he was confined at [[healthful-house|Healthful House]], a private sanatorium in [[north-carolina|North Carolina]].

> "Thomas Roch, let it be again remarked — this point cannot be too often insisted upon — incapable though he was of comprehending and performing the ordinary acts of life, was in full possession of his mental faculties, of his genius as an inventor, when the dominant idea, the fixed obsession of his discovery, took possession of his mind." (002-008)

## Role in the Story

Roch's invention drives the entire narrative of *Facing the Flag*. After being rejected by France, Germany, and Britain, and then confined by the United States, Roch becomes the target of an elaborate kidnapping plot. the pirate [[Ker Karraje|Ker Karraje]], posing as Count d'Artigas, abducts both Roch and his attendant [[gaydon]] to obtain the secret of [[roch-fulgurator-invention|the fulgurator]].

Roch's mental illness is complex: he is lucid and brilliant regarding his invention, but disconnected from ordinary reality. This duality makes him simultaneously the most powerful and most vulnerable character in the novel.

> "Billions! Billions!" (004-005)

## Relationships

- **Simon Hart (Gaydon)**: Roch's keeper at Healthful House, who is actually the engineer Simon Hart in disguise. Hart protects Roch and ultimately thwarts [[pirate-crew|the pirates]]' plans.

- **Count d'Artigas (Ker Karraje)**: The pirate who kidnaps Roch to exploit his invention. Poses as a wealthy foreign nobleman to gain access to Healthful House.

- **[[engineer-serko|Engineer Serko]]**: Ker Karraje's technical expert, who studies Roch's work and attempts to extract the fulgurator's secret.

## Key Passages

Roch's patriotic fury at the thought of his invention being used against France reveals the depth of character beneath his apparent madness:

> "The sentiment of [[patriotism]], which is the very essence of the citizen — who before belonging to himself belongs to his country — became extinct in the soul of the disappointed inventor." (002-005)

Throughout the novel, Roch's cries of "Billions!" serve as a haunting refrain, capturing his obsession with the monetary value of his discovery and his refusal to part with it without full compensation.
