## Rejection of [[fulgurator|roch's invention]]

The rejection of [[thomas-roch|thomas roch]]'s fulgurator by multiple governments is the backstory event that sets the stage for all subsequent action in [[jules-verne|jules verne]]'s *Facing the Flag* (1896). This cascade of refusals — by [[french-government|france]], Germany, Britain, and ultimately the United States — drove the inventor into [[mental-alienation|mental alienation]] and created the conditions for his exploitation by [[count-dartigas|the pirate]] [[ker-karraje|ker karraje]].

> "Thomas Roch's invention having been refused by the commission, steps ought to have been taken to prevent him from offering it elsewhere. Nothing of the kind was done, and there a great mistake was made." (002-001)

## In the Story

The rejection unfolded in stages. First, Roch offered his invention to the French government, which — through a formal commission — refused it. This initial rejection struck at Roch's core identity: as a French citizen, he had expected his own nation to recognize his genius. The commission's refusal was not merely a professional setback but a wound to his patriotic pride.

Roch then crossed the French frontier and offered [[roch-fulgurator-invention|the fulgurator]] to Germany. There too his exorbitant financial demands led to immediate refusal. The German military authorities, absorbed in the development of a new ballistic engine, dismissed the French inventor's claims.

Britain was next. The [[british-admiralty|british admiralty]] was more pragmatic, attempting to negotiate and "sound him out," but Roch's inflexible demand for millions proved insurmountable. The Admiralty ultimately "declined to have anything more to do with him."

> "As the result of this second rebuff Roch's anger became coupled with hatred — an instinctive hatred of humanity — especially after his pourparlers with the British Admiralty came to naught." (002-006)

## Participants

- **Thomas Roch**: The rejected inventor, whose mental state deteriorated with each refusal.
- **French Government Commission**: The first to reject the fulgurator.
- **German Military Authorities**: Rejected the invention in favor of their own ballistic program.
- **British Admiralty**: Attempted negotiation but ultimately refused.
- **United States Government**: Did not reject but instead confined Roch for protection.

## Consequences

The cumulative effect of these rejections was catastrophic for Roch. His [[patriotism|patriotism]] eroded, replaced by an "instinctive hatred of humanity." His mental condition worsened, and his fixation on the fulgurator's monetary value intensified. By the time the United States chose to sequester him at [[healthful-house|healthful house]] rather than negotiate, Roch had become a figure of international concern — a [[genius-and-madness|mad genius]] whose invention could change the balance of global military power, and who had no remaining loyalty to any nation.
