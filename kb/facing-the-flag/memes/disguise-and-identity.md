Disguise and identity form a pervasive thematic pattern in *Facing the Flag*. Nearly every major character operates under a false or concealed identity, creating a narrative world in which appearances are systematically deceptive and the truth must be uncovered through observation and deduction.

## The Layered Masquerade

The novel's identity deceptions operate at multiple levels. [[Simon Hart]] adopts the alias "Gaydon" to work as Roch's keeper at [[Healthful House]]. [[Ker Karraje]] presents himself as "Count d'Artigas," a wealthy yachtsman, to gain access to the sanatorium. The [[Ebba Schooner|Ebba]] is disguised as a pleasure yacht while serving as a pirate vessel. Even [[Back Cup]] itself wears a disguise — appearing from the outside as a barren, uninhabited rock while concealing an elaborate pirate base within.

This layering of deceptions creates a narrative of nested revelations. Hart, himself disguised, must penetrate the disguises of others — a task that requires the careful observation and deduction that define his role as narrator.

## Identity and Nationality

Disguise in the novel often involves the concealment or assumption of national identity. [[Ker Karraje]]'s "Count d'Artigas" persona is explicitly transnational — a citizen of the world of wealth and leisure, owing allegiance to no nation. This contrasts with [[Simon Hart]]'s unwavering French identity, which persists beneath his "Gaydon" disguise and ultimately proves stronger than any assumed role.

## The Restoration of True Identity

The novel's resolution involves the stripping away of disguises and the restoration of true identities. Karraje is revealed as a pirate, not a count. Roch, facing the [[Tricolor Flag]], sheds his mercenary persona and reclaims his identity as a Frenchman. The explosion of [[Back Cup]] is, in symbolic terms, the destruction of the novel's architecture of deception — a return to the world of visible, accountable truth.
