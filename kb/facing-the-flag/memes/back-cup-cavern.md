## Back Cup Cavern

The cavern within [[Back Cup]] is one of the most elaborately described subterranean settings in Jules Verne's fiction. In *Facing the Flag* (1896), the interior of this rocky islet — located off the coast of [[Bermuda]] — has been hollowed out and transformed into a hidden pirate base, complete with living quarters, workshops, and a sophisticated infrastructure for electricity, ventilation, and maritime access.

> "The size of the cavern can be judged from these approximate figures: length, three hundred feet; breadth, one hundred and fifty feet; height, eighty feet." (014-045)

## In the Novel

### Discovery and Layout

[[Simon Hart]] provides the novel's primary description of the cavern after being brought to [[Back Cup]] as a prisoner. His careful observation reveals an underground world of surprising scale and organization. The cavern is not a natural void but an engineered space, shaped by human labor into a functional habitat:

> "This part of the cavern, which is known as the Beehive, is occupied by the quarters of the crew. This designation is justified by the rows of cells excavated in the limestone wall, and which are piled one upon the other." (015-003)

The eastern side of the cavern presents a different character — less densely developed, with natural rock formations and open spaces that serve as common areas and storage:

> "The lay of the cavern to the east is very different. There the rock rises in a gentle slope to a height of about sixty feet." (015-004)

### Infrastructure and Technology

The cavern's infrastructure reflects the technical sophistication of [[count-dartigas]]'s operation. Electricity, generated by the tidal action of the ocean, powers the interior lighting:

> "I am not surprised that electricity is employed in lighting the interior of the cavern, as it is also used in the submarine tug's propulsion." (014-028)

Ventilation is provided by natural fissures in the rock, supplemented by mechanical systems:

> "A light breeze, which seems to come from every direction, causes the flame of the lamp to flicker. I soon grow accustomed to the atmosphere that envelops the cavern and comes from a hole in the dome." (014-040)

The cavern's artificial beach, created by the [[submarine-tunnel]]'s water inflow, serves as a small harbor for the pirates' vessels.

### Psychology of the Cavern

The cavern functions as more than a physical setting — it is a psychological space of imprisonment and isolation. Its vast dimensions paradoxically reinforce the prisoners' sense of confinement:

> "If I am a prisoner in the cavern I am at least not imprisoned in a narrow cell. I can walk about at ease in this immense cavity." (014-037)

The cavern's hidden nature — invisible from the sea, accessible only through the submerged tunnel — creates an atmosphere of absolute seclusion. No external authority can reach [[Back Cup]]'s interior without navigating the tunnel, making the cavern a near-perfect refuge for [[count-dartigas]]'s pirate crew.

## Related Characters

- **[[count-dartigas]]**: The master of [[Back Cup]], who has transformed the cavern into an impregnable pirate base.
- **[[Simon Hart]]**: The novel's narrator, whose detailed observations of the cavern provide the reader's primary window into this hidden world.
- **[[Engineer Serko]]**: Oversees the cavern's technical infrastructure, including the electrical and ventilation systems.
- **[[Captain Spade]]**: Commands the maritime access to the cavern through the [[submarine-tunnel]].
- **[[Thomas Roch]]**: The cavern's most valuable prisoner, housed in a pavilion within this underground domain.

## Key Passages

The cavern's hidden nature is essential to the novel's plot:

> "This, however, is not the only peculiarity of Back Cup. One would never imagine that its walls hide from exterior view an excavation large enough to contain not only the crew but all the material and machinery of a submarine tug." (015-002)

Verne situates [[Back Cup]]'s cavern within the tradition of great natural caverns, noting its modest scale compared to others:

> "I remember that there are caverns of larger dimensions both in the Old and the New Worlds. But those are the works of nature, while this is the work of man — or rather of pirates." (014-046)
