## French Government Commission

The French Government Commission is the body that first evaluated and rejected [[thomas-roch|thomas roch]]'s [[fulgurator|fulgurator]] invention in [[jules-verne|jules verne]]'s *Facing the Flag* (1896). As the initial official response to Roch's work, the commission's rejection set in motion the chain of events that led to the inventor's mental deterioration, his international wanderings, and ultimately his exploitation by [[count-dartigas|the pirate]] [[ker-karraje|ker karraje]].

> "Thomas Roch's invention having been refused by the commission, steps ought to have been taken to prevent him from offering it elsewhere. Nothing of the kind was done, and there a great mistake was made." (002-001)

## In the Novel

The French commission's rejection of [[roch-fulgurator-invention|the fulgurator]] is presented in the novel as both a technical judgment and a moral failure. On the technical level, Verne implies that the commission failed to recognize the weapon's potential — a failure of imagination that the novel's events would prove catastrophic. On the moral level, the French government's failure to take "steps to prevent Roch from offering it elsewhere" represents a dereliction of duty.

The narrator's judgment is unequivocal: "there a great mistake was made." By rejecting Roch without securing the secret of his invention — either through purchase, protection, or even sequestration — France left a weapon of mass destruction free to circulate among rival powers and criminal enterprises. The commission's shortsightedness is the novel's original sin, from which all subsequent calamities flow.

The rejection struck at the heart of Roch's identity as a French citizen. Verne frames the inventor's [[patriotism|patriotism]] as the "essence of the citizen," and the French commission's refusal was not merely a professional setback but a wound to that essential identity. It was this wound, more than any other, that extinguished Roch's loyalty and set him wandering across Europe in search of a nation that would value his genius.

## Members

The commission members are not individually named in the novel. They exist as an institutional entity — a faceless bureaucracy whose collective judgment carried consequences far beyond what any individual member could have anticipated.

The French Government Commission represents a recurring theme in Verne's work: the tension between individual genius and institutional inertia. The commission's failure to recognize Roch's invention mirrors similar failures in Verne's other novels, where visionary individuals find themselves thwarted by the limited imagination of official bodies.
