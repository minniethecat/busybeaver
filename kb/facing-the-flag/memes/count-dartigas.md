Count d'Artigas is the mastermind behind the pirate operations centered on [[Back Cup]] and the primary antagonist of Jules Verne's *Facing the Flag* (1896). He appears initially as a wealthy and distinguished foreign nobleman visiting [[Healthful House]] to tour the facility, but this refined persona conceals his true identity: Ker Karraje, one of the most notorious pirates of the era, who seeks to obtain the secret of [[Thomas Roch]]'s [[Fulgurator]].

## The Dual Identity

The Count's elaborate deception is one of the novel's central dramatic devices. At [[Healthful House]], he presents as a wealthy, eccentric nobleman interested in scientific matters — the perfect cover for gaining access to [[Thomas Roch]]. The revelation of his true identity transforms the narrative from a mystery of abduction into a tale of international piracy.

> "The Count d'Artigas listened to his ceaseless chatter with apparent interest, probably in order the better to dissemble the real motive of his visit." (003-011)

| Identity | Role | Setting |
|----------|------|---------|
| Count d'Artigas | Wealthy foreign nobleman, patron of science | Healthful House, public appearances |
| Ker Karraje | Pirate leader, captain of criminal enterprise | Back Cup, the [[Ebba Schooner]] |

The newspaper account discovered by Simon Hart reveals the full scope of the deception:

> "The pirate Ker Karraje." (016-041)

> That man's name was Ker Karraje. (016-049)

> It goes without saying that no one imagined that Count d'Artigas was none other than Ker Karraje, the former pirate of the Pacific. (017-008)

## Criminal Career

Karraje's rise to infamy follows a pattern of escalating criminal ambition. His method was calculated: seize control of a vessel, recruit a crew of pirates, and establish a secure base from which to launch attacks across the world's oceans.

> With Engineer Serko and Captain Spade, Ker Karraje managed to seize a ship in the Pacific, and having succeeded in shipping a crew of villains like himself, commenced a career of piracy. (016-052)

Initially operating in the Pacific, international naval pressure eventually forced Karraje to relocate. The disappearance of his vessel coincided with a temporary lull in piracy — but as Simon Hart discovers, Karraje had simply moved his operations to the [[Atlantic Ocean]], establishing the impregnable base at [[Back Cup]].

> Although the whereabouts of Ker Karraje's vessel, commanded by Captain Spade, were unknown, there was reason to believe it was somewhere in the Atlantic. (016-055)

Karraje is described as possibly a Malay by origin, though the narrative notes this is of little consequence compared to the scope of his criminal enterprise. His command of advanced technology — the [[Ebba Schooner]], the [[Submarine Technology|submarine tug]], and the hidden cavern — distinguishes him from the stereotypical pirates of earlier adventure fiction.

## Role in the Story

### The Healthful House Deception

The Count's scheme unfolds methodically. He first visits [[Healthful House]] to assess [[Thomas Roch]]'s condition and the security of the facility. When he confirms that Roch's invention is real and that the inventor is psychologically vulnerable, he orchestrates a daring nighttime kidnapping:

- Captain Spade leads the raid that captures both [[Thomas Roch]] and his keeper Simon Hart (Gaydon)
- The captives are transported aboard the [[Ebba Schooner]] to [[Back Cup]]
- Roch is kept in isolation, his mental state carefully managed to extract the Fulgurator's secret

The Count's calculated, impassive demeanor is frequently noted by the narrator:

> "The Count d'Artigas was impassible as usual, though his brow had darkened, but the captain shook his head in a manner that implied plainly: 'Decidedly there is nothing to hope from this poor devil!'" (004-003)

### The Back Cup Operation

At [[Back Cup]], the Count commands a sophisticated paramilitary organization. He directs [[Engineer Serko]]'s technical operations, oversees Captain Spade's maritime activities, and personally manages the delicate task of extracting the [[Fulgurator]]'s secret from a mentally unstable inventor.

The Count's treatment of [[Thomas Roch]] is marked by calculated respect. Unlike [[Engineer Serko]], who views Roch with contempt, the Count recognizes that the inventor's fragile mental state requires careful handling. He plays the role of the sympathetic patron, never pressing too hard while ensuring that the Fulgurator's secrets are steadily extracted.

## The Fulgurator Plot

The Count's ultimate goal is to weaponize [[Thomas Roch]]'s invention. The [[Fulgurator]] — an explosive of unprecedented power — would transform the Back Cup pirates from maritime criminals into an unassailable naval force. His ambition extends beyond mere piracy; he envisions a political order in which his command of the Fulgurator makes him the equal of any nation.

His careful plan unravels when the international community learns of the threat. The [[Escape from Back Cup]] and the subsequent [[back-cup-explosion]] destroy his carefully constructed empire. The weapon he sought to control becomes the instrument of his destruction — a fitting end for a character whose ambition consistently exceeded his reach.

## Character Analysis

Karraje embodies a modernized, technological form of criminality that distinguishes him from the romantic pirates of earlier fiction:

1. **Strategic Patience**: Unlike impulsive buccaneers, the Count plans his operations over months and years
2. **Technological Sophistication**: His command of the submarine tug and hidden cavern places him at the cutting edge of maritime engineering
3. **Organizational Hierarchy**: His structured command system — with [[Engineer Serko]] and Captain Spade as specialized subordinates — mirrors a corporate or military structure
4. **Psychological Manipulation**: His handling of [[Thomas Roch]] demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of mental vulnerability

## Key Passages

The Count's public persona is carefully maintained throughout the novel. His composure rarely cracks, even when circumstances deteriorate:

> "The Count d'Artigas listened to his ceaseless chatter with apparent interest..." (003-011)

The revelation of his true identity in Chapter XVI transforms every prior interaction with the Count into retrospective evidence of his duplicity. The polite nobleman discussing scientific matters at [[Healthful House]] is revealed as a calculating predator, and every courteous gesture is re-read as intelligence gathering.
