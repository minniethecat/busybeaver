Verne's Late Period (roughly 1890–1905) represents the final phase of [[Jules Verne]]'s career, during which *Facing the Flag* (1896) was written. This period is characterized by darker themes, technological pessimism, and more complex moral frameworks than the optimistic adventure narratives of his earlier works.

## Characteristics

The late-period novels share several distinguishing features:
- **Technological pessimism**: inventions are as likely to destroy as to benefit
- **Moral ambiguity**: protagonists are flawed, antagonists may have legitimate grievances
- **Political engagement**: explicit treatment of international relations, nationalism, and power
- **Psychological depth**: greater attention to mental states, obsession, and madness

*Facing the Flag* exemplifies all of these tendencies. The [[Fulgurator]] is purely destructive. [[Thomas Roch]] is a deeply flawed protagonist whose [[patriotism]] is dormant rather than active. The novel engages directly with Franco-German rivalry, American power, and international law. And Roch's [[mental-alienation]] is central to the plot.
