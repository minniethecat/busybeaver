The Warder of [[Healthful House]] is a minor but symbolically significant figure in *Facing the Flag*. Assigned to watch over [[Thomas Roch]], the Warder embodies the sanatorium's dual function: protection and confinement. His constant presence is a reminder that Roch, for all his genius, is not free.

> Therefore a warder was detailed to keep close watch over the new inmate. (001-021)

## Role in the Sanatorium

The Warder's duties at [[Healthful House]] include monitoring [[Thomas Roch]]'s movements, preventing any attempt at self-harm or escape, and reporting on the inventor's mental state to the sanatorium's administration. The position requires constant vigilance — Roch's condition is unpredictable, and his value (both to the French government, which hopes to prevent his secret from escaping, and to potential adversaries who would exploit it) makes him a high-stakes charge.

The Warder operates within the institution's hierarchy, answerable to the [[Director of Healthful House]] and working alongside Simon Hart (Gaydon), Roch's designated keeper. Unlike Hart, who develops a complex relationship with his charge, the Warder remains anonymous — defined by function rather than personality.

## The Keeper-Warder Distinction

The narrative distinguishes between the keeper — Simon Hart, who adopts the identity of Gaydon — and the warder, a more generic guard role. This distinction matters:

| Role | Holder | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| Keeper | Simon Hart (as Gaydon) | Personal attendant, constant companion |
| Warder | Anonymous | Institutional guard, surveillance |

> The engineer, become Gaydon the warder. (002-020)

This passage's conflation of "Gaydon" with "warder" suggests the fluidity of roles within the institution. The keeper and the warder are distinct in function but overlap in their shared task of watching over [[Thomas Roch]].

## Symbolic Function

The Warder represents the institutional dimension of Roch's captivity — the impersonal, bureaucratic surveillance that constrains the inventor as much as any physical barrier. Unlike the pirates who later hold Roch for explicit criminal purposes, the Warder's surveillance is ostensibly for Roch's own protection. The novel invites reflection on whether there is, in practice, a meaningful difference between these two forms of confinement.
