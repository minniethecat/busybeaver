[009-001] But hark! If I mistake not--no, it was not imagination--I hear footsteps. Some one is approaching the side of the compartment where the door is. One of the crew no doubt. Are they going to let me out at last? I can now hear voices. A conversation is going on outside the door, but it is carried on in a language that I do not understand. I shout to them--I shout again, but no answer is vouchsafed.

[009-002] There is nothing to do, then, but wait, wait, wait! I keep repeating the word and it rings in my ears like a bell.

[009-003] Let me try to calculate how long I have been here. The ship must have been under way for at least four or five hours. I reckon it must be past midnight, but I cannot tell, for unfortunately my watch is of no use to me in this Cimmerian darkness.

[009-004] Now, if we have been going for five hours, we must have cleared [[pamlico-sound|Pamlico Sound]], whether we issued by Ocracoke or Hatteras inlet, and must be off the coast a good mile, at least. Yet I haven't felt any motion from the swell of the sea.

[009-005] It is inexplicable, incredible! Come now, have I made a mistake? Am I the dupe of an illusion? Am I not imprisoned in the hold of a ship under way?

[009-006] Another hour has passed and the movement of the ship suddenly ceases; I realize perfectly that she is stationary. Has she reached her destination? In this event we can only be in one of the coast ports to the north or south of Pamlico Sound. But why should [[thomas-roch|Thomas Roch]] be landed again? The [[kidnapping-of-roch|abduction]] must soon have been discovered, and our kidnappers would run the greatest risk of falling into the hands of the authorities if they attempted to disembark.

[009-007] However this may be, if the vessel is coming to anchor I shall hear the noise of the chain as it is paid out, and feel the jerk as the ship is brought up. I know that sound and that jerk well from experience, and I am bound to hear and feel them in a minute or two.

[009-008] I wait--I listen.

[009-009] A dead and disquieting silence reigns on board. I begin to wonder whether I am not the only living being in the ship.

[009-010] Now I feel an irresistible torpor coming over me. The air is vitiated.

[009-011] I cannot breathe. My chest is bursting. I try to resist, but it is impossible to do so. The temperature rises to such a degree that I am compelled to divest myself of part of my clothing. Then I lie me down in a corner. My heavy eyelids close, and I sink into a prostration that eventually forces me into heavy slumber.

[009-012] How long have I been asleep? I cannot say. Is it night? Is it day? I know not. I remark, however, that I breathe more easily, and that the air is no longer poisoned carbonic acid.

[009-013] Was the air renewed while I slept? Has the door been opened? Has anybody been in here?

[009-014] Yes, here is the proof of it!

[009-015] In feeling about, my hand has come in contact with a mug filled with a liquid that exhales an inviting odor. I raise it to my lips, which, are burning, for I am suffering such an agony of thirst that I would even drink brackish water.

[009-016] It is ale--an ale of excellent quality--which refreshes and comforts me, and I drain the pint to the last drop.

[009-017] But if they have not condemned me to die of thirst, neither have they condemned me to die of hunger, I suppose?

[009-018] No, for in one of the corners I find a basket, and this basket contains some bread and cold meat.

[009-019] I fall to, eating greedily, and my strength little by little returns.

[009-020] Decidedly, I am not so abandoned as I thought I was. Some one entered this obscure hole, and the open door admitted a little of the oxygen from the outside, without which I should have been suffocated. Then the wherewithal to quench my thirst and appease the pangs of hunger was placed within my reach.

[009-021] How much longer will this incarceration last? Days? Months? I cannot estimate the hours that have elapsed since I fell asleep, nor have I any idea as to what time of the day or night it may be. I was careful to wind up my watch, though, and perhaps by feeling the hands--Yes, I think the little hand marks eight o'clock--in the morning, no doubt.

[009-022] What I do know, however, is that the ship is not in motion. There is not the slightest quiver.

[009-023] Hours and hours, weary, interminable hours go by, and I wonder whether they are again waiting till night comes on to renew my stock of air and provisions. Yes, they are waiting to take advantage of my slumbers. But this time I am resolved to resist. I will feign to be asleep--and I shall know how to force an answer from whoever enters!

[009-024] CHAPTER VI.

[009-025] ON DECK.

[009-026] Here I am in the open air, breathing freely once more. I have at last been hauled out of that stifling box and taken on deck. I gaze around me in every direction and see no sign of land. On every hand is that circular line which defines earth and sky. No, there is not even a speck of land to be seen to the west, where the coast of North America extends for thousands of miles.

[009-027] The setting sun now throws but slanting rays upon the bosom of the ocean. It must be about six o'clock in the evening. I take out my watch and it marks thirteen minutes past six.

[009-028] As I have already mentioned, I waited for the door of my prison to open, thoroughly resolved not to fall asleep again, but to spring upon the first person who entered and force him to answer my questions. I was not aware then that it was day, but it was, and hour after hour passed and no one came. I began to suffer again from hunger and thirst, for I had not preserved either bite or sup.

[009-029] As soon as I awoke I felt that the ship was in motion again, after having, I calculated, remained stationary since the previous day--no doubt in some lonely creek, since I had not heard or felt her come to anchor.

[009-030] A few minutes ago--it must therefore have been six o'clock--I again heard footsteps on the other side of the iron wall of my compartment.

[009-031] Was anybody coming to my cell? Yes, for I heard the creaking of the bolts as they were drawn back, and then the door opened, and the darkness in which I had been plunged since the first hour of my [[imprisonment-and-captivity|captivity]] was illumined by the light of a lantern.

[009-032] Two men, whom I had no time to look at, entered and seized me by the arms. A thick cloth was thrown over my head, which was enveloped in such a manner that I could see absolutely nothing.

[009-033] What did it all mean? What were they going to do with me? I struggled, but they held me in an iron grasp. I questioned them, but they made no reply. The men spoke to each other in a language that I could not understand, and had never heard before.

[009-034] They stood upon no ceremony with me. It is true I was only a madhouse [[warder-of-healthful-house|warder]], and they probably did not consider it necessary to do so; but I question very much whether [[gaydon|Simon Hart]], the engineer, would have received any more courtesy at their hands.

[009-035] This time, however, no attempt was made to gag me nor to bind either my arms or legs. I was simply restrained by main force from breaking away from them.

[009-036] In a moment I was dragged out of the compartment and pushed along a narrow passage. Next, the steps of a metallic stairway resounded under our feet. Then the fresh air blew in my face and I inhaled it with avidity.

[009-037] Finally they took their hands from off me, and I found myself free. I immediately tore the cloth off my head and gazed about me.

[009-038] I am on board a schooner which is ripping through the water at a great rate and leaving a long white trail behind her.

[009-039] I had to clutch at one of the stays for support, dazzled as I was by the light after my forty-eight hours' imprisonment in complete obscurity.

[009-040] On the deck a dozen men with rough, weather-beaten faces come and go--very dissimilar types of men, to whom it would be impossible to attribute any particular nationality. They scarcely take any notice of me.

[009-041] As to [[ebba-schooner|the schooner]], I estimate that she registers from two hundred and fifty to three hundred tons. She has a fairly wide beam, her masts are strong and lofty, and her large spread of canvas must carry her along at a spanking rate in a good breeze.

[009-042] Aft, a grizzly-faced man is at the wheel, and he is keeping her head to the sea that is running pretty high.

[009-043] I try to find out the name of the vessel, but it is not to be seen anywhere, even on the life-buoys.

[009-044] I walk up to one of the sailors and inquire: "What is the name of this ship?"

[009-045] No answer, and I fancy the man does not understand me.

[009-046] "Where is the captain?" I continue.

[009-047] But the sailor pays no more heed to this than he did to the previous question.

[009-048] I turn on my heel and go forward.

[009-049] Above the forward hatchway a bell is suspended. Maybe the name of the schooner is engraved upon it. I examine it, but can find no name upon it.

[009-050] I then return to the stern and address the man at the wheel. He gazes at me sourly, shrugs his shoulders, and bending, grasps the spokes of the wheel solidly, and brings the schooner, which had been headed off by a large wave from port, stem on to sea again.

[009-051] Seeing that nothing is to be got from that quarter, I turn away and look about to see if I can find Thomas Roch, but I do not perceive him anywhere. Is he not on board? He must be. They could have had no reason for carrying me off alone. No one could have had any idea that I was [[simon-hart|Simon Hart]], the engineer, and even had they known it what interest could they have had in me, and what could they expect of me?

[009-052] Therefore, as Roch is not on deck, I conclude that he is locked in one of the cabins, and trust he has met with better treatment than his ex-guardian.

[009-053] But what is this--and how on earth could I have failed to notice it before? How is this schooner moving? Her sails are furled--there is not an inch of canvas set--the wind has fallen, and the few puffs that occasionally come from the east are unfavorable, in view of the fact that we are going in that very direction. And yet the schooner speeds through the sea, her bows down, throwing off clouds of foam, and leaving a long, milky, undulating trail in her wake.

[009-054] Is she a steam-yacht? No--there is not a smokestack about her. Is she propelled by electricity--by a battery of accumulators, or by piles of great power that work her screw and send her along at this rate?

[009-055] I can come to no other conclusion. In any case she must be fitted with a screw, and by leaning over the stern I shall be able to see it, and can find out what sets it working afterwards.

[009-056] The man at the wheel watches me ironically as I approach, but makes no effort to prevent me from looking over.

[009-057] I gaze long and earnestly, but there is no foaming and seething of the water such as is invariably caused by the revolutions of the screw--naught but the long white furrow that a sailing vessel leaves behind is discernible in the schooner's wake.
