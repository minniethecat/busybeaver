## Battle Between the Sword and the Tug

The battle between the submarine "Sword" and the pursuing tug is the climactic naval engagement in [[jules-verne|jules verne]]'s *Facing the Flag* (1896). This confrontation represents the collision between [[ker-karraje|ker karraje]]'s pirate technology and the naval forces of lawful authority, and it forms the backdrop against which [[thomas-roch|thomas roch]] makes his fateful decision.

> "Battle Between the 'Sword' and the Tug" (Chapter XIV title, 014-000)

## In the Story

The battle occurs in the waters near [[back-cup|back cup]], where Ker Karraje's submarine boat — which he has armed with a weapon derived from [[roch-[[fulgurator|fulgurator]]-invention|roch's fulgurator]] technology — encounters a pursuing vessel. The tug, representing the forces of law, has tracked [[pirate-crew|the pirates]] to their lair and now threatens to expose and destroy their operation.

The engagement demonstrates the terrifying power of Roch's invention. The submarine, named the "Sword," deploys its fulgurator-derived weaponry against the surface vessel. The asymmetry of the confrontation — a hidden submarine against a conventional surface ship — showcases the technological revolution that Roch's invention represents and the danger of such power in criminal hands.

> "[[engineer-serko|engineer serko]], who was well versed in his profession, and was a clever mechanic to boot, and who had made a special study of submarine craft, proposed to Ker Karraje that they should construct one of these boats in order to continue their criminal exploits with greater secrecy and effectiveness." (017-004)

## Participants

- **Ker Karraje**: Commands [[count-dartigas|the pirate]] forces during the engagement.
- **Engineer Serko**: Operates the submarine's technical systems during battle.
- **[[simon-hart|simon hart]]**: Witnesses the engagement from within the pirate base, documenting its outcome.
- **Thomas Roch**: Present during the events, his reaction to the battle proves decisive.

## Consequences

The battle serves as the catalyst for the novel's resolution. Facing the destruction that his invention enables, Roch experiences a moment of moral clarity. The sight of armed conflict — and particularly the threat his weapon poses to the forces representing lawful authority — triggers his dormant [[patriotism|patriotism]]. In the novel's climactic sequence, Roch turns the fulgurator against the pirates themselves, choosing to face the flag of [[french-government|france]] rather than aid those who would use his creation for evil.

The battle thus functions as both action setpiece and moral crucible, forcing each character to reveal their true nature under the pressure of combat.
