Naval blockade and interdiction represent the institutional military response to [[Ker Karraje]]'s piracy in *Facing the Flag*. The international naval coalition that converges on [[Back Cup]] employs blockade tactics — the cordoning of maritime approaches, the interception of vessels, and the concentration of force at a strategic chokepoint — to contain and ultimately destroy the pirate threat.

## The Coalition Blockade

The blockade of [[Back Cup]] was a multilateral operation involving warships from [[France]], [[British Admiralty|Britain]], and the [[US Navy|United States]], among others. The coalition's approach reflected the international consensus that [[Ker Karraje]] was a threat to all maritime nations and that unilateral action by any single power would be insufficient.

The tactical challenge was the [[submarine-tunnel]] — the hidden passage through which the pirates could theoretically escape even a close blockade. The coalition's solution was to combine surface blockade with direct assault, forcing the confrontation that led to the [[back-cup-explosion|destruction of Back Cup]].

## Blockade in Nineteenth-Century Context

Verne's depiction of the naval blockade reflects late nineteenth-century naval doctrine, in which the close blockade of enemy ports was a standard operational concept. The novel updates this concept for the age of submarine technology — the [[submarine-tunnel]] represents a vulnerability in traditional blockade strategy that foreshadows the twentieth century's submarine warfare.
