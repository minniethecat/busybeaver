## Pacific Ocean

The Pacific Ocean, particularly its western reaches, is the original theater of [[Ker Karraje]]'s criminal career in Jules Verne's *Facing the Flag* (1896). Before establishing his hidden base at [[Back Cup]] in the [[Atlantic Ocean|Atlantic]], Karraje operated as a pirate in the Pacific, building the reputation and crew that would later serve his more ambitious schemes.

## In the Novel

The Pacific Ocean enters the narrative through the novel's elaborate backstory, revealed in the newspaper account that [[Simon Hart]] discovers during his captivity. The account traces Karraje's origins to the Pacific piracy boom of the late 1880s:

> "Eight or nine years ago, the West Pacific was infested by pirates who attacked vessels, plundered them, and murdered their crews." (016-045)

Karraje's method was calculated and ruthless. With his two key subordinates — [[Engineer Serko]] and [[Captain Spade]] — he seized a ship in the Pacific, recruited a crew of like-minded criminals, and launched a systematic campaign of maritime predation:

> "In that part of the Pacific Ocean where piracy is still carried on with a certain success, Karraje managed to seize a ship." (016-052)

International naval pressure eventually forced Karraje to abandon the Pacific. The disappearance of his vessel coincided with a temporary lull in piracy, leading authorities to believe the threat had been eliminated:

> "Had he abandoned the Pacific for other seas? Would this pirate, who had acquired such a terrible notoriety, resume his depredations in other latitudes?" (016-055)

The answer, as the novel reveals, was yes. Karraje relocated to the [[Atlantic Ocean]], established the impregnable base at [[Back Cup]], and continued his criminal enterprise with even greater sophistication. The Pacific phase of his career, however, established both his reputation and his methodology — the pattern of seizing vessels, recruiting crews, and operating from hidden bases that would define his later operations.

The narrator's final assessment connects the Pacific to the larger arc of Karraje's criminal biography:

> "After sweeping the West Pacific the pirate and his companions shifted their field of operations to the Atlantic." (024-054)

## Connections

The Pacific Ocean connects to several key narrative elements:

- **[[Ker Karraje|Count d'Artigas]]**: Began his piracy career in the Pacific before relocating to the Atlantic.
- **[[Engineer Serko]]**: Karraje's technical officer, present from the Pacific days.
- **[[Captain Spade]]**: Karraje's maritime commander, part of the original Pacific crew.
- **[[Atlantic Ocean]]**: The ocean to which Karraje relocated, and the setting for the novel's main action.
- **[[Pirate Crew of Back Cup]]**: Many crew members were recruited during the Pacific phase.
- **[[Ebba Schooner]]**: The vessel that Karraje used to transit from Pacific to Atlantic operations.

## Key Passages

The Pacific Ocean's criminal history sets up the novel's central conflict:

> "Eight or nine years ago, the West Pacific was infested by pirates who attacked vessels, plundered them, and murdered their crews." (016-045)

The strategic shift from Pacific to Atlantic is the key to understanding Karraje's evolution from regional pirate to international threat:

> "Had he abandoned the Pacific for other seas?" (016-055)

The novel's epilogue connects the two oceans as successive chapters in Karraje's career:

> "After sweeping the West Pacific the pirate and his companions shifted their field of operations to the Atlantic." (024-054)
