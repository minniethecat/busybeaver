# Rouen

The Norman manufacturing city that serves as the setting for the central drama of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" — a place of exile, suffering, and industrial ugliness that provides the dark contrast to the remembered splendors of [[seville|Seville]].

## Geography and Location

Rouen, the historic capital of Normandy, lies on the Seine River about eighty kilometers from the English Channel. By the 1830s, it was one of [[tours|France]]'s principal industrial cities, its prosperity built on cotton textiles and maritime trade. The "slender spires" (005-190) of its Gothic cathedral — immortalized by Monet a generation after Balzac — and the surviving medieval quarters of the old town coexisted with factories, warehouses, and a growing working-class population.

## In the Novel

Rouen plays no direct role in the plot of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* — [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has never been there — but it features prominently in her literary imagination. The poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" opens with the Spanish heroine singing in a squalid Norman setting:

> Paquita sang; the murky town beneath
> Was Rouen, whence the slender spires rise
> To chew the storm with teeth.
> Rouen so hideous, noisy, full of rage — (005-190)

The description is amplified in the narrative: "And here followed a magnificent description of Rouen — where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] had never been — written with the affected brutality which, a little later, inspired so many imitations of Juvenal; a contrast drawn between the life of a manufacturing town and the careless life of Spain, between the love of Heaven and of human beauty, and the worship of machinery, in short, between poetry and sordid moneymaking" (005-191).

Balzac's parenthetical observation — "where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] had never been" — is crucial. Rouen in the poem is a product of literary imagination rather than experience, a city reconstructed from Romantic common[[place-index|places]] about industrial ugliness. The irony is that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own provincial [[sancerre|Sancerre]] — which she experiences daily and which drives her to despair — is never directly depicted in her [[poetry|verse]]; she escapes to an imaginary Spain and an imagined Normandy, both equally unreal.

## The Cathedral

The Cathedral of Rouen provides the setting for the poem's most emotionally charged scene: the parting between Paquita and her Norman artillery captain. "Nothing could be more dainty than the description of the parting between the Spanish girl and the Normandy Captain of Artillery, who, in the delirium of [[love|passion]] ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ed with feeling worthy of Byron, exacted from Paquita a vow of absolute fidelity, in the Cathedral at Rouen in front of the alter of the Blessed Virgin" (005-195).

The choice of the Cathedral is richly suggestive. It is at once a symbol of medieval faith and architectural sublimity — the antithesis of the industrial "rage" that surrounds it — and the setting for a vow that, within the poem's logic, cannot be kept. The Virgin who "though a Maid is a woman, and never forgives / When lovers are false to their vows" (005-196) presides over a scene whose irony would not have been lost on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s readers — or on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] herself.

## Paquita's Suffering

Paquita's solitude in Rouen occupies a substantial portion of the poem: "A large part of the poem was devoted to describing Paquita's sufferings when alone in Rouen waiting till the campaign was over; she stood writhing at the window bars as she watched happy couples go by; she sup[[newspaper-press|press]]ed her [[love|passion]] in her heart with a determination that consumed her; she lived on narcotics, and exhausted herself in dreams" (005-197).

This portrait — the woman at the window, watching life pass by, consuming herself in longing — is unmistakably a projection of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own condition. The "window bars" of Rouen are the turrets of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]; the "happy couples" are the world of [[paris|Paris]] from which she is excluded; the "narcotics" are the literary compositions and antique collections with which she fills her provincial days. The [[place-index|geography]] of the poem maps directly onto the [[place-index|geography]] of the novel: Rouen is [[sancerre|Sancerre]], and Paquita is [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]].

## A Later Mention

Rouen appears once more in the novel, in a simile that underscores its mundane accessibility. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] watches M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] depart for America, the little man is described as embarking "as briskly as if it were to go down to Rouen in a steamboat" (005-530). The casual comparison — a transatlantic voyage equated with a routine steamboat trip down the Seine — emphasizes both the shrinking of the world in the age of steam and the particular ordinariness of Rouen as a destination. It is a city one reaches by steamboat, a place of business and transit, the antithesis of the poetic [[seville|Seville]] that exists only in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s imagination.

## Literary Significance

Rouen in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* illustrates Balzac's sophisticated handling of the relationship between lived experience and literary invention. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has never seen Rouen, but she writes about it with conviction — and the poem, by the narrator's own admission, contains passages of genuine power ("magnificent description," "feeling worthy of Byron"). The gap between [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s knowledge and her imagination is the gap in which provincial literary ambition breeds: a borrowed vocabulary, an imagined world, a [[genius-concept|talent]] that might, in different circumstances, have found its proper subject.
