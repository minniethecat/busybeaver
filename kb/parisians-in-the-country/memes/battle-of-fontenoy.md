# Battle of Fontenoy

The Battle of Fontenoy (May 11, 1745) was a French victory during the War of the Austrian Succession, fought near Tournai in the Austrian Netherlands (modern Belgium). Under the command of Marshal Maurice de Saxe, French forces defeated a British-Dutch-Hanoverian army led by the Duke of Cumberland. In Balzac's fiction, [[fontenoy-sancerre|Fontenoy]] serves as a touchstone of pre-revolutionary aristocratic glory — a world of military honor that the commercial society of the 1830s has rendered obsolete.

## The Battle

The French army, numbering approximately 50,000 men under the brilliant Marshal de Saxe (himself suffering from dropsy and carried about the field in a wicker cart), faced a Pragmatic Army of roughly equal size. The battle is famous for the alleged exchange of courtesies between the opposing officers: "Messieurs les Anglais, tirez les premiers" ("Gentlemen of England, fire first") — though historians debate whether these words were actually spoken.

The French victory secured the Austrian Netherlands and contributed to the favorable Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748). [[fontenoy-sancerre|Fontenoy]] marked one of the last great triumphs of the *ancien régime*'s military establishment before the disasters of the Seven Years' War.

## In Balzac's Fiction

[[fontenoy-sancerre|Fontenoy]] appears in Balzac's work as a historical reference point — part of the deep French past that his [[person-index|characters]] invoke to measure the distance between heroic and commercial ages. The battle represents a form of glory — aristocratic, martial, honor-based — that stands in stark contrast to the commercial ambition embodied by [[person-index|characters]] like [[felix-gaudissart]]. Where [[fontenoy-sancerre|Fontenoy]] was won by noblemen leading regiments, the battles of Balzac's era are fought with [[prospectus|prospectuses]], [[subscription-publishing|newspaper subscription]]s, and insurance policies.
