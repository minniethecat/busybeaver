# Literary Hack

The literary hack is the writer who lives by his pen not as a vocation but as a trade — the *homme de lettres mercenaire* whose daily production is "like the stone of Sisyphus" and whose facility condemns him to permanent mediocrity. Balzac's extended passage on the subject in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is one of the most famous anatomies of literary labor in 19th-century fiction.

## Definition and Origin

Balzac introduces the concept through the figure of [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], returning to [[paris|Paris]] after his summer at [[anzy|Anzy]]: "In [[paris|Paris]] once more, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] had, in a few weeks, lost the im[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of the happy time he had spent at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]]. This is why: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] lived by his pen" (005-836). The phrase "lived by his pen" is not neutral description but accusation, and Balzac immediately elaborates: "In this century, especially since the triumph of the bourgeoisie — the commonplace, money-saving citizen — who takes good care not to imitate Francis I or Louis XIV — to live by the pen is a form of penal servitude to which a galley-slave would prefer death. To live by the pen means to create — to create today, and tomorrow, and incessantly — or to seem to create; and the imitation costs as dear as the reality" (005-837).

The passage is remarkable for its equation of literary production with forced labor. The writer who lives by his pen is not free but enslaved — his creativity is extracted from him on a schedule, and the distinction between genuine creation and its simulation collapses, because "the imitation costs as dear as the reality." The hack is the writer for whom the means (writing) has consumed the end (art).


> In Paris, in our day, hackwork cuts a man off from every pretension to a literary position. When he can do no more, or no longer cares for advancement, the man who can write becomes a journalist and a hack.
>
> (005-837)

The central metaphor is mythological. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s daily newspaper article "was like the stone of Sisyphus, and which came every Monday, crashing down on to the feather of his pen" (005-837). The image of the stone "crashing down" onto the "feather" — the massive weight of obligation crushing the delicate instrument of creation — condenses the hack's predicament into a single violent collision. Sisyphus at least has the dignity of eternal punishment; the hack merely has Mondays.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac distinguishes carefully between the hack and the artist. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] "put no artistic conscientiousness into his work. This man of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] had a facility, a carelessness, if you call it so, which ranked him with those writers who are mere scriveners, literary hacks. In [[paris|Paris]], in our day, hackwork cuts a man off from every pretension to a literary position. When he can do no more, or no longer cares for advancement, the man who can write becomes a journalist and a hack" (005-837). The [[civilization|progress]]ion is fatal: the man who can write becomes a journalist, and the journalist becomes a hack. Facility is not an asset but a trap — the easier writing comes, the less motive there is to write well.

The hack's life, Balzac concedes, "is not unpleasing" (005-838). [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] enjoys the perquisites of his position: "boxes at all the theatres; the sale of the books he reviewed or left unreviewed paid for his gloves." He "took toll from vanity in the form of drawings or pictures. Every day had its engagements to dinner, every night its theatre, every morning was filled up with callers, visits, and lounging" (005-838). But these pleasures are not rewards for achievement; they are compensation for the surrender of ambition. The hack is paid in pleasures rather than in recognition, and the pleasures themselves become monotonous.

The economics of hackwork are structurally inadequate. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] earns "from seven to eight hundred francs a month" (005-839), a sum that should be sufficient but never is, because the hack's life demands the expenditure of a gentleman on the income of a clerk. The four "indigent months" — January, April, July, October — when rent comes due, punctuate the year with predictable crises that can never be escaped, only deferred.

## Dexterity Versus Art

Balzac's most important theoretical distinction in the passage is between two kinds of literary work, embodied in two kinds of critic. "Criticism is as fatal to the critic as seeing two sides to a question is to a pleader. In these professions the judgment is undermined, the mind loses its lucid rectitude. The writer lives by taking sides. Thus, we may distinguish two kinds of criticism, as in painting we may distinguish art from practical dexterity" (005-1064).

The first kind of criticism — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s kind — "is the ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of judgments formed at random in a more or less witty way, just as an advocate pleads in court on the most contradictory briefs." It is "well adapted to indolent brains, to men devoid of the sublime faculty of imagination, or, possessed of it indeed, but lacking courage to cultivate it" (005-1064).

The second kind — the kind practiced by Claude Vignon — "is a science. It necessitates a thorough comprehension of each work, a lucid insight into the tendencies of the age, the adoption of a system, and faith in fixed principles." The critic of this type "is then a magistrate of [[concept-index|ideas]], the censor of his time; he fulfils a sacred function; while in the former case he is but an acrobat who turns somersaults for a living so long as he had a leg to stand on" (005-1067).

The judgment is devastating and final: "Between Claude Vignon and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] lay the gulf that divides mere dexterity from art" (005-1067). The literary hack is not a failed artist but a different species — a creature of "dexterity" rather than "art," of facility rather than vision, of the marketplace rather than the museum.

## Social Context

The literary hack emerges from the specific economic conditions of post-1830 [[paris|Paris]]. The triumph of the bourgeoisie produced a literary marketplace driven by quantity rather than quality — "the triumph of the bourgeoisie — the commonplace, money-saving citizen" (005-837) — in which the writer who could produce rapidly and reliably was more valuable than the writer who produced slowly and brilliantly. The hack is the industrial worker of the literary economy, and his product is not art but copy.

## In the Novels

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is Balzac's definitive literary hack, and his career across *La Comédie Humaine* traces the type's inevitable trajectory: early promise, temporary success, permanent mediocrity. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], watching him work, "had ere long taken literary measure of her idol" and discovered that "[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s pen could never be trusted to as a resource" (005-1068). The hack is not tragic, because he lacks the stature for tragedy; he is merely exhausted.
