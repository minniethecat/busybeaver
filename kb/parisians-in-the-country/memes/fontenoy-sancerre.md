# Fontenoy

A small village or hamlet below the hilltop town of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], situated among the vineyards of the [[loire|Loire]] valley — an "almost suburb" that is one of the two satellite settlements (with [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]]) in the immediate orbit of the town.

## Geography and Location

Fontenoy lies below [[sancerre|Sancerre]], on the slopes that descend from the hilltop toward the [[loire|Loire]]. Balzac groups it with [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]] as one of "a few villages, Fontenoy and [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]], almost suburbs" [005-004] of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], situated in the belt of vineyards that rings the town. The qualifying "almost" is significant: these are not true suburbs in the modern sense, but agricultural hamlets close enough to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] to fall within its social and economic shadow, yet distinct enough to have their own names and identities. The villages are described as "reminding us by their situation of the smiling vineyards about Neuchatel in Switzerland" [005-004] — a landscape of cultivated slopes and picturesque settlement.

## The Battle of Fontenoy — A Cautionary Distinction

This Fontenoy must be carefully distinguished from the famous battle site of Fontenoy in present-day Belgium (Hainaut [[provincial-life|province]]), where on 11 May 1745, the French army under Marshal de Saxe defeated the British, Dutch, and Austrian forces in one of the most celebrated battles of the War of the Austrian Succession. That Fontenoy is immortalized in French military history — and in the famous (if perhaps apocryphal) exchange: "Messieurs les Anglais, tirez les premiers."

## The La Baudraye Connection

The two Fontenoys are, however, connected in the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] family history. The narrative of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* records that "Under Louis XV [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Milaud de la Baudraye]], from being a mere squire, was made Chevalier, and had influence enough to obtain for his son a cornet's commission in the Musketeers. This officer perished at Fontenoy, leaving a child, to whom King Louis XVI subsequently granted the privileges, by patent, of a farmer-general, in remembrance of his father's death on the field of battle" [005-015].

This passage refers to the *Belgian* Fontenoy, the battle site — not the village below [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. The death of the Chevalier's son at the [[battle-of-fontenoy|Battle of Fontenoy]] was the sacrifice that earned the family royal favor, leading to the lucrative post of farmer-general that established the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] fortune. The [[sancerre|Sancerre]] Fontenoy shares only a name with this battlefield — a coincidence that adds a layer of historical resonance to an otherwise modest wine-growing hamlet.

## In the Novel

The Fontenoy below [[sancerre|Sancerre]] appears only once in the novel's descriptive passages, in the same paragraph that introduces [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]] [005-004]. Its minimal presence in the narrative is itself telling: Fontenoy is a place so small, so absorbed into the landscape of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]'s vineyards, that it barely registers as a distinct location. It exists as part of the backdrop — the "smiling vineyards" [005-004] that make the [[sancerre|Sancerre]] region beautiful to visitors but suffocating to its ambitious inhabitants.

## Economic Character

Like the rest of the [[sancerre|Sancerre]] region, Fontenoy is a wine-growing settlement. "Outside the ramparts lies a belt of vineyards. Wine forms the chief industry and the most important trade of the country" [005-004]. The village's economy would have been entirely bound up with viticulture — the cultivation, harvest, and processing of the grapes that produced "several vintages of high-class wine full of aroma" [005-004]. Fontenoy winegrowers would have shipped their produce through the [[saint-thibault|port of Saint-Thibault]], participating in the local economy that sustained [[sancerre|Sancerre]] despite its gradual commercial decline relative to the right bank.

## Social Significance

Fontenoy's "almost suburb" status illuminates the social [[place-index|geography]] of provincial [[tours|France]]. The village is close enough to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] to be under its watchful eye — the "officious eye which makes private life almost a public concern" [005-007] — and yet removed enough to have a separate identity. Like [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]], it represents the intermediate space between town and country, the zone where [[sancerre|Sancerre]]'s influence fades into the agricultural hinterland that sustains it.
