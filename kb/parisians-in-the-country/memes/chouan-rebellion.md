# Chouan Rebellion

The Chouan Rebellion (Chouannerie) was a royalist insurgency waged in Brittany, Maine, Normandy, and the Vendée against the French Republic and later Napoleon's Consulate, spanning from 1793 to approximately 1800. The rebellion forms the historical backdrop for [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s dramatic tale of the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]], a Chouan envoy captured and imprisoned by Napoleonic authorities.

## Historical Background

Following the execution of Louis XVI and the radicalization of the Revolution, counter-revolutionary uprisings erupted across western [[tours|France]]. The Chouans — named from the nocturnal owl (*chouette*) whose call they used as a signal — were irregular royalist fighters operating in small bands under local chiefs. Unlike the large Catholic and Royal Army of the Vendée, the Chouans practiced guerrilla warfare: ambushes, raids on stagecoaches and supply convoys, and assassinations of republican officials. The rebellion persisted through the Directory and into the Consulate, requiring sustained military and police attention from [[paris|Paris]].

## The Chevalier de Beauvoir Episode

In the novel, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] recounts a story attributed to [[charles-nodier|Charles Nodier]], set "not long after the 18th Brumaire" — the coup that brought Napoleon to power as First Consul in November 1799. At this time "there was a call to arms in Brittany and la Vendée," and the First Consul, "anxious before all things for peace in [[tours|France]], opened negotiations with the rebel chiefs, and took energetic military measures; but, while combining his plans of campaign with the insinuating charm of Italian diplomacy, he also set the Machiavelian springs of the police in movement, Fouché then being at its head." (PN: 1350)

A young man of the Maillé family, the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]], was dispatched by the Chouans from Brittany to Saumur "to open communications between certain magnates of that town and its environs and the leaders of the Royalist party." Traveling by boat disguised as a master mariner, he was arrested on the day he landed. Despite having his "passport and papers all in order," the authorities imprisoned him provisionally — "in those days of liberty the agents of the powers in authority cared little enough for what we now regard as legal." (PN: 1352-1354)

Transferred to the Castle of l'Escarpe, a fortress "perched on very high rocks" with "precipices for its trenches," [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] charmed the Corsican commandant and his wife, which aroused the husband's jealousy. Thrown into a dungeon and fettered, [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] staged an escape with the help of a treacherous jailer — only to discover that the rope provided was too short, presumably cut to plunge him to his death on the rocks below. Awaiting the turnkey's return, [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] "hit him such a tremendous blow on the head that the wretch fell in a heap without a cry; the bar had cracked his skull." Donning the dead man's clothes and mimicking his walk, [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] walked out through the main gate at dawn and escaped. (PN: 1356-1400)

## Fouché's Police State

The anecdote highlights the role of Fouché, Napoleon's Minister of Police, in sup[[newspaper-press|press]]ing the Chouan threat. The "blind belief that the spies had in their instructions, which were unfortunately only too minute" reflects the extensive intelligence apparatus deployed against the royalists. The tale exemplifies the arbitrary detention and merciless policing that characterized the Consulate's approach to internal security. (PN: 1350, 1354)

## In the Novel

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] tells the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] story as part of the evening entertainment at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], positioning it as a tale of adultery and revenge — a veiled commentary on his own situation with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] and [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]]. The story's [[concept-index|themes]] of imprisonment, jealousy, and cunning escape parallel the novel's larger concerns with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s entrapment in [[marriage-as-institution|provincial marriage]] and her eventual flight to [[paris|Paris]]. "It did not seem to strike either the lawyer or [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] that there was in this narrative the least allusion that should apply to them." (PN: 1402)
