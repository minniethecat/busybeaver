# Saint-Satur

Saint-Satur is the village — or, as Balzac describes it, the "suburb" — lying directly below the hilltop town of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] on the left bank of the [[loire|Loire]]. It is the location of the [[la-baudraye-estate|La Baudraye manor]] and the home of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]], nicknamed "the [[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]." (PN 711, 819)

## Geography / Location

Saint-Satur sits at the foot of the hill crowned by [[sancerre|Sancerre]], one of several villages — along with [[fontenoy-sancerre|Fontenoy]] — described as "almost suburbs" of the town. The area is characterized by vineyards that remind Balzac of "the smiling vineyards about Neuchâtel in Switzerland." (PN 691)

Below the Great Ramparts that encircle [[sancerre|Sancerre]] lies a belt of vineyards; Saint-Satur and its neighboring hamlets are embedded within this wine-growing landscape. The village lies within ten minutes of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] proper, connected by paths including the steep "Breakneck Alley" (le Casse-cou), a stone-strewn track shut in by high vineyard banks and descending from Caesar's Gate. (PN 711, 879)

The [[loire|Loire]] flows through the flats below Saint-Satur, leaving a yellow alluvium that is extremely fertile. The town of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] above commands a panorama over the [[loire|Loire]] valley extending for forty leagues. (PN 685, 727)

## Description

Saint-Satur is presented as a modest wine-growing village typical of the left-bank [[loire|Loire]] landscape in the [[berry|Berry]] region. Its most notable feature is the [[la-baudraye-estate|La Baudraye manor]], "a town-house and country-house in one," with turrets visible above the vineyards from the Mall at [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. (PN 711)

From the Mall, the local gentry would contemplate "the abode of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] while [[jealousy|envy]]ing the ten or twelve privileged [[person-index|persons]] who might spend their afternoons with the Queen of the neighborhood." (PN 879) The vineyards of Saint-Satur produce high-class vintages full of aroma, "so nearly resembling the wines of Burgundy, that the vulgar palate is deceived." (PN 691)

## Historical Significance

Saint-Satur belongs to the historic [[provincial-life|province]] of [[berry|Le Berry]]. The [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] fief, situated within its bounds, carries a lineage dating to the Crusades. However, the present [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] family are "a substitute for those [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in the chief events of the history of [[berry|Le Berry]]." (PN 711)

The broader economic shift of trade to the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]] affected Saint-Satur as part of the left-bank community facing gradual marginalization. (PN 695–697)

## In the Novel

Saint-Satur is the provincial stage for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s intellectual ascendancy and emotional frustration. At [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] in Saint-Satur, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] holds her salon, attracting admirers from across the region while alienating the women of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. Her pretensions earn her the nickname "the [[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]." (PN 819, 845)

When she remarks that she would rather have an empty bowl than one with anything in it, "this speech produced a terrible effect on [[sancerre|Sancerre]], and was cruelly retorted on the [[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]." (PN 845) Saint-Satur also contrasts with the grander [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], which the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s acquire later; the move marks a shift in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s circumstances. (PN 1082)
