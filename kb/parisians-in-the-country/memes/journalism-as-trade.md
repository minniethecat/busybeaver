# Journalism as Trade

Journalism as a trade, as distinct from the Press as an institution, is one of Balzac's most sustained and bitter subjects in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. Through the figure of [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], Balzac anatomizes the journalist's economics, his daily discipline, and the moral degradation that the profession inflicts on those who practice it.

## Definition and Origin

Balzac distinguishes between two understandings of [[newspaper-press|journalism]]. The Press as an institution is, as [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] mockingly declares, "not a mere tool, not a mere trade. From the point of view of the politician, the Press is an Institution." But [[newspaper-press|journalism]] as practiced — the daily production of copy for money — is something else entirely: a trade that consumes the [[genius-concept|talent]]s it exploits. The novel's phrase for [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is precise: "the man who can write becomes a journalist and a hack" (005-837). Journalism is not a profession one enters; it is a condition into which one falls.

## Balzac's Depiction: The Economics of the Trade

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s financial life is mapped with the precision of a case study. "He earned from seven to eight hundred francs a month, a sum which he found quite insufficient for the prodigality peculiar to the impecunious" (005-839). The sum is substantial by the standards of the 1830s — a provincial family could live comfortably on a fraction of it — but the journalist's life demands expenditures that outrun his income. "The months of January, April, July, and October were, as he phrased it, his indigent months. The rent and the porter's account cleaned him out" (005-840). The quarterly rhythm of crisis is as predictable as the seasons, and as inescapable.

The journalist is caught in a structural contradiction: his position demands the display of prosperity (cabs, cigars, dinners, mistresses) while his income guarantees poverty. "[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] took no fewer hackney cabs, spend a hundred francs in breakfasts all the same, smoked thirty francs' worth of cigars, and could never refuse the mistress of a day a dinner or a new dress" (005-840). The journalist's consumption is not indulgence but professional necessity — he must appear successful to be successful, and the appearance of success consumes the substance of it.

## The Daily Grind

The journalist's labor is structured by the relentless periodicity of the [[newspaper-press|press]]. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s daily newspaper article "was like the stone of Sisyphus, and which came every Monday, crashing down on to the feather of his pen" (005-837). The image is one of Balzac's most com[[newspaper-press|press]]ed and violent: the mythological stone of eternal punishment crashing onto the "feather" — the delicate, almost weightless instrument of writing. The journalist is crushed by the very tool of his trade.

The volume of production is staggering. Besides his daily newspaper article, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] "worked for three or four literary magazines" (005-837), producing a serial, two novels a year, and miscellaneous articles. "And yet, to reach this position, Étienne had struggled for ten years" (005-838). The reward for a decade of struggle is not leisure or recognition but the right to continue struggling at a slightly higher level.

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is explicit about the toll. He "looked eight-and-forty, though he was no more than thirty-seven. He was already bald, and had assumed a Byronic air in harmony with his early decay and the lines furrowed in his face by overindulgence in champagne. He ascribed these signs-manual of dissipation to the severities of a literary life, declaring that the Press was murderous; and he gave it to be understood that it consumed superior [[genius-concept|talent]]s, so as to lend a grace to his exhaustion."


> In this century, especially since the triumph of the bourgeoisie—the commonplace, money-saving citizen—who takes good care not to imitate Francis I or Louis XIV—to live by the pen is a form of penal servitude to which a galley-slave would prefer death. To live by the pen means to create—to create today, and tomorrow, and incessantly—or to seem to create; and the imitation costs as dear as the reality.
>
> (005-837)


## Moral Degradation

The journalist's moral deterioration is as systematic as his financial one. "Criticism is as fatal to the critic as seeing two sides to a question is to a pleader. In these professions the judgment is undermined, the mind loses its lucid rectitude. The writer lives by taking sides" (005-1064). The journalist who must produce opinions on demand ceases to have opinions at all; he has only positions, adopted and discarded according to the needs of the moment.

The degradation is visible in the journalist's mercenary flexibility. "Men have been known to transfer their services from one paper to another without being at the pains to consider that the opinions of the new sheet must be diametrically antagonistic to those of the old. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] could smile to see [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] with one article on the Legitimist side and one on the side of the new dynasty, both on the same occasion. She admired the maxim he preached: 'We are the attorneys of public opinion'" [005-1065, 005-1066]. The phrase "attorneys of public opinion" is [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s attempt to dignify prostitution as professionalism — but the novel does not let the rationalization stand.

## Dexterity Versus Art

Balzac's most important distinction is between two kinds of journalistic work. The first kind — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s kind — "is the ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of judgments formed at random in a more or less witty way, just as an advocate pleads in court on the most contradictory briefs." It is "well adapted to indolent brains, to men devoid of the sublime faculty of imagination, or, possessed of it indeed, but lacking courage to cultivate it" (005-1064).

The second kind "is a science. It necessitates a thorough comprehension of each work, a lucid insight into the tendencies of the age, the adoption of a system, and faith in fixed principles — that is to say, a scheme of jurisprudence, a summing-up, and a verdict. The critic is then a magistrate of [[concept-index|ideas]], the censor of his time; he fulfils a sacred function; while in the former case he is but an acrobat who turns somersaults for a living so long as he had a leg to stand on" (005-1067).

The judgment is final: "Between Claude Vignon and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] lay the gulf that divides mere dexterity from art" (005-1067). The journalist is an acrobat, a performer, a mercenary; the critic is a magistrate, a judge, a priest. The distinction is not one of degree but of kind — and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is on the wrong side of it.

## Social Context

Journalism as a trade emerged in [[tours|France]] after the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]], when the relaxation of [[newspaper-press|press]] censorship and the expansion of the reading public created a demand for copy that could only be met by professionalizing the production of text. The journalist was a new social type — neither an artist nor a clerk, neither a gentleman nor a worker — and his ambiguous status was the source of both his freedom and his degradation.

## In the Novels

*The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* contains Balzac's most concentrated analysis of [[newspaper-press|journalism]] as a trade, but the subject is one of the great [[concept-index|themes]] of *La Comédie Humaine*. *Lost Illusions* traces the corruption of Lucien de Rubempré by the [[paris|Parisian]] [[newspaper-press|press]]; *A Prince of Bohemia* anatomizes the journalist's social world; and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] himself appears across multiple novels as the enduring example of [[genius-concept|talent]] destroyed by the trade that exploits it.
