# Clos Margaritis

The Clos Margaritis is the celebrated vineyard of [[margaritis]] in [[vouvray]], producing what its owner proudly calls "the head of [[vouvray|Vouvray]]" (002-070) — a wine of such richness and quality that, according to local [[honor|reputation]], it is "passed off as Madeira in [[paris|Paris]]" (002-070). The vineyard and its wine are central to the comic plot of *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, serving as the bait in the transaction through which the mad wine-grower outwits the great commercial traveler [[felix-gaudissart]].

## Geography and Location

The Clos Margaritis is situated in the commune of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], in the heart of the [[touraine|Touraine]] wine country on the right bank of the [[loire]]. The term *clos* designates a walled or enclosed vineyard, a form of viticultural parceling characteristic of the region that often indicates a site of particular quality — a plot whose soil, aspect, and microclimate produce distinctive wines that command premium prices.

The vineyard surrounds [[la-fuye]], [[la-fuye|the Margaritis house]], integrating residence and production in the pattern typical of the [[touraine|Touraine]] vigneron. From the house, [[margaritis|Margaritis]] can survey his vines, study the sky, and calculate the effects of weather on his precious crop — activities that, in his case, have become the obsessive focus of a disordered mind.

## The Wine

[[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s boasts about his wine are among the comic highlights of his dialogue with [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]. He presents the Clos Margaritis as the pinnacle of [[vouvray|Vouvray]] production: "'You are here in the headquarters of [[vouvray|Vouvray]],' said the master, pointing to his vines. 'The *clos* [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'" (002-070). The vineyard's [[honor|reputation]] extends beyond [[touraine|Touraine]]; in [[paris|Paris]], its wine is sufficiently esteemed to be "passed off as Madeira" (002-070), the fortified Portuguese wine that was the benchmark of quality in the early nineteenth century.

The 1819 vintage holds a particular place in [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s obsession. It is this vintage that he repeatedly invokes, the mythical wine that he offers to sell to [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] — two casks of a vintage that may or may not exist, from a vineyard whose products are real but whose owner's grasp of commercial reality is not. The 1819 vintage becomes a kind of MacGuffin: the object of desire that drives the comic plot, sought by [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], promised by [[margaritis|Margaritis]], and ultimately vanishing into the labyrinth of the madman's delusions.

The comic irony of the Clos Margaritis is that the wine is genuinely excellent. [[margaritis|Margaritis]] is not a charlatan in the sense of selling inferior goods; his madness takes the specific form of a [[madness|monomania]]cal fixation on his vineyard and its products. He has real wine, from a real *clos*, of genuine quality. What he does not have — what he cannot have — is the capacity to conduct a normal commercial transaction. The two casks [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is tricked into buying represent not a fraud in the ordinary sense but the collision between commercial rationality and the impenetrable logic of obsession.

## In the Novel

The Clos Margaritis is the objective correlative of [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s madness — the one subject on which his mind, otherwise wandering through delusion and non sequitur, can focus with sustained intensity. When [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] mentions business, [[margaritis|Margaritis]] responds with "I pacified Calabria when Murat was King" (002-060). But when the subject turns to wine, to his vineyard, to the 1819 vintage, [[margaritis|Margaritis]] becomes coherent, even shrewd. The vineyard is both the content of his [[madness|monomania]] and its excuse: a man who produces "the head of [[vouvray|Vouvray]]" is entitled, in the logic of the region, to a certain eccentricity.

> You were in business, monsieur?
>
> (002-115)


The commercial negotiation between [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] and [[margaritis|Margaritis]] over the wine is the centerpiece of the comic plot. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], having come to [[vouvray|Vouvray]] to sell insurance, finds himself instead buying wine — a reversal of roles that signals his defeat at the hands of provincial cunning. The contract he extracts from [[margaritis|Margaritis]] — "signed, after reading, a contract to deliver two casks of wine of Clos Margaritis" (002-115) — is worthless, the product of a negotiation between a confidence man and a madman, each operating in a different reality.

The vineyard also serves a deeper thematic purpose in Balzac's anatomy of commerce. The Clos Margaritis represents authentic value — the product of land, labor, and tradition — as distinct from the abstract financial instruments [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] peddles. When [[margaritis|Margaritis]] boasts of his wine, he is speaking of something real, something that can be tasted, something whose quality can be verified by the senses. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s "[[life-insurance|intellectual capital insurance]]" is mere words; [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s wine is substance. That the madman has the real product and [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] the empty [[eloquence|rhetoric]] is one of Balzac's most pointed ironies.
