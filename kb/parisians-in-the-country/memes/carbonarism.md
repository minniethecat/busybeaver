# Carbonarism

The French Carbonari (Charbonnerie) were a network of secret revolutionary societies active in the early 1820s, organized on the model of the Italian Carbonari. Their aim was the overthrow of the Bourbon monarchy. In Balzac's fiction, Carbonarism deepens the characterization of Felix [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], linking [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] to the conspiratorial underground of post-Napoleonic [[tours|France]].

## Definition and Origin

Carbonarism originated in Italy, where the Carbonari ("charcoal burners") developed as a secret society with elaborate rituals, drawn from the guild traditions of charcoal-makers, aimed at resisting Napoleonic rule and later the restored Italian monarchies. The movement spread to [[tours|France]] around 1820-1822, where it was adopted by liberal and Bonapartist elements — particularly army officers, students, and members of the urban middle classes — who sought to overthrow the [[bourbon-restoration|Bourbon Restoration]].

The French Charbonnerie was organized into local cells called *ventes* (from the Italian *vendite*, meaning "sales" or "lodges"), with a hierarchical command structure, secret passwords, initiation rituals, and an elaborate system of internal communication. At its peak, it may have counted between 20,000 and 40,000 members, concentrated particularly in [[paris|Paris]], Alsace, and the western departments.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac's treatment of Carbonarism in *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* is brief and embedded within a comic portrait. The revelation that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] "had ere now dabbled in the plots of the French carbonari" (002-031) is presented not as a mark of political seriousness but as one more credential in his eclectic portfolio:

> He made no claim on the Republicans. In the first place, he himself had a leaning to Republican notions — the only views according to the Gaudissart philosophy that could bring about rational equality; and then Gaudissart had ere now dabbled in the plots of the French carbonari. He had even been arrested, but released for lack of evidence. (002-031)

The passage is a miniature of [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s character: his Republicanism is a philosophical preference, his Carbonarism a youthful adventure, and his present political identity purely sartorial — "he now only needed a particular shape of cap and long spurs to be representative of the Republic" (002-031). The arrest — a potentially grave biographical fact — is undercut by the phrase "released for lack of evidence."

## Social Context

The French Carbonari launched several unsuccessful uprisings in 1821-1822, notably the failed insurrection at Belfort (January 1822) and the conspiracy of the Four Sergeants of La Rochelle (executed September 1822). These failures, combined with effective police infiltration, effectively broke the movement by 1823. The Restoration government's re[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of Carbonarism was severe, but the movement contributed to the culture of liberal opposition that culminated in the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]].

Many prominent figures of the Liberal and Republican opposition — including the young La Fayette — were associated with the Charbonnerie, lending the movement a patina of revolutionary gl[[love|amour]] that Balzac exploits for comic purposes. By the time of the novel's action (the mid-1830s), Carbonarism was a safely historical reference.

## In the Novels

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s Carbonarist past serves several narrative functions. It establishes his credentials with the Republican newspaper *[[le-mouvement|Le Mouvement]]*, for which he sells subscriptions alongside the Saint-Simonian *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]* (002-030). It exemplifies his ideological flexibility: he invokes his conspiratorial past when dealing with Republicans while presenting himself as a man of commerce when dealing with customers. The detail that he was "arrested, but released for lack of evidence" is consistent with his characterization as a man who skates on the surface of things — always involved, never committed, escaping the consequences of his engagements.
