# Le Morvan

Le Morvan is a mountainous region in Burgundy, forming part of the modern department of Nievre, that plays a small but significant role in the [[literary-hoax|literary mystification]] at the heart of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. It is through the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]*, a local newspaper, that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]]'s poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" first reaches the public, and the region subsequently becomes one of the four departments that proudly claim the mysterious poet [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] as their own.

## Geography and Location

Le Morvan is a granitic massif situated in the heart of Burgundy, straddling the departments of Nievre, Saone-et-[[loire|Loire]], Cote-d'Or, and Yonne. It is a region of wooded hills, small lakes, and dispersed hamlets — a pays of ancient geology and sparse population, quite unlike the vineyard slopes of the [[loire|Loire Valley]] or the limestone plains of [[berry]]. Its principal town, Chateau-[[chateau-chinon|Chinon]], occupies a central position in the massif and serves as the administrative and cultural hub of the Morvan.

The region's remoteness and its distinctive character — a "mountain" country in the heart of [[tours|France]] — made it a plausible candidate for the romantic mystification of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]. A Spanish poet dying young in the Morvan was a story that appealed to local [[vanity|pride]] precisely because it was improbable.

## In the Novel

The Morvan enters the novel through the publication history of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]ous poem. Balzac tells us that the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* was one of the provincial newspapers that published "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne," alongside journals in [[nevers]], [[bourges]], and [[sancerre]]. The poem, attributed to the mysterious [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] — supposedly "born at [[bourges|Bourges]] in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner" [005-192] — became a minor sensation across central [[tours|France]].

The region's connection to the [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] legend deepened with the biographical detail that the poet had allegedly died at Chateau-[[chateau-chinon|Chinon]], the principal town of the Morvan. Balzac notes that "Chateau-[[chateau-chinon|Chinon]], where the fictional poet [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] allegedly died, is in the Morvan" [005-070]. This detail cemented the region's claim to the poet: if [[bourges|Bourges]] could boast his birth, the Morvan could claim his death — and death, for a Romantic poet, is the greater distinction.

Balzac describes the scope of the [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] phenomenon with characteristic irony: "the whole countryside — [[nevers|Nevers]] and [[sancerre|Sancerre]], Le Morvan and [[berry|Le Berry]] — was priding itself on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]]" [005-070]. Four departments, each ignorant of the true authorship, competed for the reflected glory of a poet who did not exist. The Morvan's participation in this collective self-deception illustrates Balzac's theme of [[vanity|provincial vanity]] — the hunger of the regions for cultural significance, for a local celebrity to rival [[george-sand]] or the poets of [[paris|Paris]].

The *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* represents the provincial [[newspaper-press|press]] in its role as a vehicle of local identity. These small newspapers, springing up across [[tours|France]] in the 1820s and 1830s, served not only to disseminate news but to construct and reinforce regional [[vanity|pride]]. That the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* published [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s poem — and presumably defended the authenticity of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] — [[place-index|places]] it within the network of provincial [[newspaper-press|journalism]] that Balzac anatomizes elsewhere in his fiction, most notably in *Lost Illusions*.
