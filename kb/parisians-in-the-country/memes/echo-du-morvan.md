# L'Écho du Morvan

*L'Écho du Morvan* was a provincial periodical that, in Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, published [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]. The periodical, which "for eighteen months maintained its existence in spite of provincial indifference," serves in the novel as the vehicle through which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary ambition escapes the confines of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and enters — however briefly — the wider literary world. It is emblematic of the provincial [[newspaper-press|press]] culture of Restoration and early [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]], in which small-circulation periodicals provided one of the few available outlets for local [[genius-concept|talent]].

## Background

Provincial periodicals flourished in 1820s and 1830s [[tours|France]], despite severe economic constraints. Small print runs, limited subscription bases, and intense competition from [[paris|Paris]] publications made their existence precarious. Most were short-lived. *L'Écho du Morvan* belongs to this ecosystem: a regional review serving the departments of the Nièvre and its neighbors, publishing poetry, local news, and literary notices. Its name evokes [[le-morvan|the Morvan]], the granite massif in Burgundy whose isolation and distinctive culture made it a recognizable regional brand.

## The Morvan Region

[[le-morvan|Le Morvan]] is a mountainous region in central Burgundy, spanning parts of the modern departments of Nièvre, Saône-et-[[loire|Loire]], Yonne, and Côte-d'Or. In Balzac's time, it was known for its remoteness, its wet climate, and the distinctive character of its inhabitants — [[le-morvan|the Morvan]]diaux, who were stereotyped as sturdy, taciturn, and resistant to outside influence. The region features in the novel's [[place-index|geography]]: "[[nevers|Nevers]] and [[sancerre|Sancerre]], [[le-morvan|Le Morvan]] and [[berry|Le Berry]]" are grouped together as the territory where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]]'s fame spread.

## In the Novel

The periodical first enters the narrative through its publication of "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne." After the departure of the [[vicomte-de-chargeboeuf|Vicomte de Chargeboeuf]], the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] advises [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to "exhale her evil thoughts in [[poetry|verse]]" (005-279). The resulting poem — the story of a Spanish girl's love for a Norman artillery captain who leaves for the Russian campaign — appears in *L'Écho du Morvan* under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]:

> Moulins, Nevers, Bourges were searched to find Jan Diaz; but Dinah was impenetrable.
>
> (005-360)

The periodical's reach, while modest, is significant enough to create a genuine mystery. The search across three provincial centers for the poem's author testifies to the cultural hunger of [[provincial-life|the provinces]] and the capacity of even a small review to generate literary celebrity within a regional circuit.

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] later collects [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]'s works into a small volume printed by Desroziers at [[moulins|Moulins]] — two hundred copies, of which one hundred and fifty sold, "about fifty in each department" (005-376). This distribution across three departments maps the effective reach of provincial literary culture: a triangle of readership bounded by the [[loire|Loire]] and [[le-morvan|the Morvan]], sufficient to sustain a local [[honor|reputation]] but utterly disconnected from the [[paris|Parisian]] center.
