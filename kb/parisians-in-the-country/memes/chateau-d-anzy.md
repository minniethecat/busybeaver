# Chateau d'Anzy

The Renaissance castle on the banks of the [[loire|Loire]], about two leagues above [[sancerre|Sancerre]], built by the great French architect [[philibert-de-lorme|Philibert de l'Orme]] — a masterpiece of sixteenth-century architecture purchased by [[monsieur-milaud|Monsieur Milaud]] de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] in 1827 and the setting for key scenes in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*.

## Geography and Location

The Chateau d'Anzy stands "on the banks of the [[loire|Loire]], about two leagues above [[sancerre|Sancerre]]" (005-019). This position, overlooking the great river, [[place-index|places]] it among the celebrated chain of [[loire|Loire Valley]] chateaux that define the architectural heritage of central [[tours|France]]. The distance of two leagues (roughly eight kilometers or five miles) from [[sancerre|Sancerre]] gives it a distinct identity — not a suburban appendage like [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]], but a proper country seat with the isolation and grandeur befitting a major estate.

The grounds include lawns, formal gardens, and a commanding view. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] arrive, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] "walked her guests round and round the lawn, ornamented with large vases of flowers, which lay in front of the Chateau d'Anzy" (005-223), suggesting the formal landscaping appropriate to a Renaissance palace.

## Architectural Significance

The chateau was "built by [[philibert-de-lorme|Philibert de l'Orme]], the admiration of every connoisseur" (005-019). [[philibert-de-lorme|Philibert de l'Orme]] (c. 1514-1570) was one of the great masters of the French Renaissance, architect to Henri II and Catherine de Medici, creator of the Chateau d'Anet and portions of Fontainebleau and the Tuileries. His work at [[anzy|Anzy]] [[place-index|places]] the chateau in the first rank of [[loire|Loire Valley]] architecture. Balzac's account of the purchase captures both the magnitude of the acquisition and the sacrifice it required:

> By paying four hundred thousand francs down, and binding himself to further instalments, which compelled him to live for six years on the air as it came, to use his own expression, he was able to purchase the estate of Anzy on the banks of the Loire, about two leagues above Sancerre, and its magnificent castle built by Philibert de l'Orme, the admiration of every connoisseur, and for five centuries the property of the Uxelles family. At last he was one of the great landowners of the province!
>
> (005-055)

A fact Balzac emphasizes with the phrase "the admiration of every connoisseur" — a rare superlative in a novel otherwise steeped in provincial irony.

## Historical Background

For five centuries before the [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] purchase, the estate had been "the property of the Uxelles family" (005-019). The Uxelles were a distinguished noble house whose name appears elsewhere in Balzac's *Comedie Humaine* — the Marquise d'Uxelles is a recurring figure in [[paris|Parisian]] [[society|high society]]. The transfer of a property held by the same family since the Middle Ages to a descendant of Calvinist sheriffs and farmer-generals represents the social transformation Balzac chronicles throughout his work: the displacement of ancient aristocratic families by the new money of the bourgeoisie.

The purchase was the culmination of [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]'s lifelong ambition. "By paying four hundred thousand francs down, and binding himself to further instalments, which compelled him to live for six years on the air as it came, to use his own ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion, he was able to purchase the [[anzy|estate of Anzy]]" (005-019). The acquisition, completed in 1827, transformed [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|the little vine-owner]] into "one of the great landowners of the [[provincial-life|province]]" (005-019) — though at the cost of subjecting his household to "unconfessed penuriousness till 1835" (005-019).

## In the Novel

The Chateau d'Anzy serves as the setting for the most significant encounters between [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] and the visiting [[paris|Parisian]]s. After the arrival of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] had "for the last year played the chatelaine, and spent the winter only at [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-220), her relocation to the grander [[anzy|Anzy]] signaling her ambition to perform the role of a country aristocrat entertaining men of metropolitan distinction.

It is at [[anzy|Anzy]] that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] first encounters [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] in her carefully curated persona: "they beheld, on the garden steps of [[anzy|Anzy]], a lady dressed in thin black cashmere with a deep tucker, in effect like a riding-habit cut short" (005-221), her "artistic costume" serving as "a cloak for the romantic affectations of the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" (005-221). The chateau is where the comedy of provincial [[ambition|aspiration]] and [[paris|Parisian]] condescension plays out — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] exchange a glance at "the affectations of their too amiable hostess" (005-221), and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] mentally converts [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s lament about provincial life into newspaper copy worth "ten thousand francs" (005-226).

The chateau's piano "ends its asthmatic life at the Chateau d'Anzy" (005-225) — a detail that encapsulates the slow death of cultural ambition in provincial isolation. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] herself, in a moment of bitter self-awareness, describes the aridity of life at [[anzy|Anzy]]: "Utter despair or dull resignation — there is no third alternative; that is the arid soil in which our existence is rooted" (005-224).

## The Purchase as Plot Point

The acquisition of [[anzy|Anzy]] is the key to understanding [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]'s character. "His [[land-hunger|land mania]] allowed him to think of nothing but the [[anzy|estate of Anzy]]" (005-082), the narrator tells us. The patient years of trotting to [[saint-thibault|Saint-Thibault]], the sordid economy, the advantageous marriage — all were means to this single end. The chateau is thus not merely a setting but the visible proof of a life's work, the materialization of provincial avarice in stone and proportion.
