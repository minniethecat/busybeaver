# Monsieur Camusot

Monsieur Camusot, referred to as "old Camusot," is a député (member of the [[french-chamber-of-deputies|Chamber of Deputies]]) and a figure of political influence in [[paris|Paris]]. Connected by marriage to the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] family, he represents the intersection of commerce, politics, and the demimonde that Balzac so frequently depicted in *La Comédie Humaine*.

## Identity and Appearance

Camusot is identified as "the député who lived so long with [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]]" (005-877), placing him within the political class of Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]]. His long liaison with [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]], an actress or courtesan of the period, marks him as a man of the world whose private life intersected with the theatrical and [[lorette|demi-mondaine]] circles of [[paris|Paris]]. He is the brother-in-law of [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] (005-877), the wealthy notary and businessman, through marriage to [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]'s eldest daughter (005-878).

## Character and Behavior

The narrator presents Camusot through the recollections of other [[person-index|characters]]. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] remarks that "old Camusot married little Daddy [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]'s eldest daughter, and they had high times together" (005-878), suggesting a convivial and perhaps dissolute past. His long association with [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]] indicates a man who moved comfortably between respectable political society and the more libertine circles of [[paris|Parisian]] entertainment. The reference to "high times" hints at the exuberant social life of the [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie under the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]].


The web of family connections that defines the [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie links Camusot to the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] notarial dynasty:

> Cardot is the brother-in-law of old Camusot, the depute who lived so long with Fanny Beaupre.
>
> (005-877)

## Social Relations

Camusot's family network extends through the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] clan: his wife is [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]'s eldest daughter, making him the brother-in-law of the notary. Through this connection, he belongs to "a family holding a fine political position" (005-877). His son by a first marriage, also named Monsieur Camusot, serves as a Councillor of the Supreme Court (005-903). This younger Camusot's stepmother — Camusot's second wife — is [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]'s sister (005-903), further entwining the Camusot and [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] families. The younger Camusot reportedly gave "a far from flattering account of the journalist" [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] (005-903), warning the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] family about the unsuitability of the match.

## Role in the Plot

Camusot functions in the narrative primarily as a source of political patronage. When the scheme to marry [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] to [[felicie-cardot|Félicie Cardot]] is being advanced, his name is invoked as a source of professional advancement: "by Camusot's interest, you may get an appointment as librarian to some public office where there is no library" (005-880). This vignette illustrates the system of political patronage and family connections that Balzac repeatedly anatomized — where a député's influence could secure sinecures for relatives and allies. Camusot himself does not appear directly in the story; his presence is felt through the network of obligations and opportunities that his position generates.

## Narrative Assessment

Camusot is not directly characterized by the narrator; instead, he is constructed through the speech of other [[person-index|characters]], particularly [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and the references made by the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] family. This indirect portraiture is characteristic of Balzac's method for secondary [[person-index|characters]] who belong to the broader world of *La Comédie Humaine* — figures who need not appear on stage because their [[honor|reputation]] and influence precede them.

## Historical and Literary Context

The character of Camusot appears across multiple novels of Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*, making him one of the author's recurring [[person-index|characters]] — a technique Balzac pioneered to create a unified fictional world. As a député, he represents the political class of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], a period when the franchise was limited to wealthy property-owners and political influence was closely tied to family and financial networks. His connection to [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]] [[place-index|places]] him within the world of the [[paris|Parisian]] *lorette* and theatrical demimonde, a social milieu Balzac explored extensively in works such as *A Harlot High and Low* (*Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes*) and *Cousin Bette*.
