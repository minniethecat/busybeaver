# Ambition

The fundamental motive force in Balzac's fictional uni[[poetry|verse]]. Whether commercial, literary, or romantic, ambition drives his [[person-index|characters]] to act — and, more often than not, to fail. Balzac treats ambition not as a moral failing but as a structural condition of post-revolutionary [[society]], where traditional hierarchies have collapsed and everyone is free to want everything.

In the linked worlds of [[felix-gaudissart]] and [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]], ambition takes different forms — commercial, intellectual, romantic — but leads both [[person-index|characters]] toward the same confrontation with their own limits.

## Definition and Origin

Ambition in Restoration [[tours|France]] was shaped by the legacy of the Revolution and the Napoleonic era, which had demonstrated that [[genius-concept|talent]] and determination could overcome birth and privilege. The [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]'s bourgeois ethos further encouraged the pursuit of wealth and status through commercial means. Yet Balzac, a perpetual striver who never achieved financial stability, understood the gap between ambition and fulfillment better than most. His [[person-index|characters]] are defined not by what they achieve but by how they manage the distance between their desires and their realities.

## Balzac's Depiction

Ambition takes different forms across the two stories, forming a comparative anatomy of human striving:

### Commercial Ambition

In *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, ambition is commercial and exuberant. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] dreams of making "twenty to thirty thousand francs out of one round" and rising to the status of his friend [[finot|Finot]], "who has thirty thousand francs a year, and will get himself made a peer" (002-052). His ambition ex[[newspaper-press|press]]es itself as Napoleonic fantasy:

> "Why shouldn't I be Baron Gaudissart and Peer of France? Has not Monsieur Popinot been made a Count?" (002-056)

### Provincial-Feminine Ambition

In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]]'s ambition is at first literary — "she cast longing looks at [[paris|Paris]], aspiring to fame" (005-189) — and later romantic, as her desire for recognition transforms into a desire for love. Her ambition burns itself out against the limits of provincial life:

> Fits of fury when she saw herself condemned never to escape from La Baudraye and Sancerre. (005-103)

### Parisian-Cynical Ambition

[[etienne-lousteau]] represents ambition in its exhausted, cynical form — a man who has achieved enough to despise the striving of others but not enough to be satisfied. His is the ambition of the insider who has seen through the game but cannot stop playing.

### Typology of Ambition

| Form | Character | Goal | Outcome |
|------|-----------|------|---------|
| Commercial | [[felix-gaudissart]] | Wealth, peerage | Duped by a madman; resilience |
| Literary/Romantic | [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]] | Fame, love | Affair, disillusionment |
| Cynical | [[etienne-lousteau]] | Status, pleasure | Achieved but hollow |
| Ascetic | [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye]] | Land, control | Success through negation |

## Social Context

Balzac's treatment of ambition reflects the distinctive anxieties of the 1830s: the fear of mediocrity in a society that promised greatness to all, the tension between provincial origins and [[paris|Parisian]] destinations, and the gendered asymmetry of ambition. Men could pursue it publicly through commerce, politics, or the professions; women had to channel it through marriage, literary [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]s, or clandestine affairs. The gap between these channels is itself a subject of Balzac's social criticism.

## In the Novels

Every major character is defined by their relationship to ambition. The structure of both novellas traces an arc from hopeful striving to ironic comeuppance: [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is humiliated by a madman; [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] exchanges one form of dependency for another. Yet Balzac's conclusion is not simply moralistic. Ambition, even when it fails, is what gives his [[person-index|characters]] their energy and their interest. The alternative — the stolid contentment of the provincial bourgeoisie — is presented as a kind of living death.
