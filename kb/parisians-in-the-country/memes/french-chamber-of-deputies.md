# French Chamber of Deputies

The French Chamber of Deputies (*Chambre des deputes*) was the elected lower house of the French Parliament under the Charter of 1814 (Restoration) and the Charter of 1830 ([[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]). It shared legislative power with the Chamber of Peers (the upper house, composed of hereditary and life peers appointed by the king) and the monarch. In Balzac's *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country*, the Chamber of Deputies represents the summit of political ambition for provincial notables, the object of lousteau's journalistic satire, and the institution whose composition [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] critiques as fundamentally degraded by the commercialization of politics.

## Background

Under the Restoration (1814–1830), the Chamber of Deputies was elected by a narrow franchise restricted to the wealthiest taxpayers — roughly 100,000 voters in a nation of 30 million. Deputies served five-year terms. The Chamber's powers were substantial but not sovereign: it could initiate legislation, vote on budgets, and address petitions to the king, but the monarch retained executive authority and the right to dissolve the Chamber. The [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]] expanded the franchise modestly (to about 170,000 voters), abolished [[newspaper-press|press]] censorship, and increased the Chamber's prestige — but also, in Balzac's view, inaugurated an era of political corruption in which parliamentary seats could be bought, sold, and traded like any other commodity.

## In the Novel

The Chamber of Deputies is a pervasive presence in both stories. In *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] practices parliamentary [[eloquence|oratory]] before his mistress Jenny, aping the [[eloquence|rhetoric]] of the Tribune: "Gentlemen, the Press is not a mere tool, not a mere trade. From the point of view of the politician, the Press is an Institution (002-053)..." [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] dreams of becoming a deputy and eventually a peer, like his friend [[monsieur-popinot|Popinot]] who "was yesterday made Minister of Commerce." The Chamber represents the ultimate career horizon for the ambitious commercial class.

In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the Chamber is the object of [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s famous critique. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] promotes [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] as an electoral candidate, the doctor demurs: "Only a physician without a practice could care to be returned as a deputy. Nominate statesmen, thinkers, men whose knowledge is universal, and who are capable of placing themselves on the high level which a legislator should occupy. That is what is lacking in our Chambers, and what our country needs" (005-496).

The narrator provides the historical diagnosis behind [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s lament: "since 1830 the nomination of parochial dignitaries has increased so greatly that real statesmen are becoming rare indeed in the lower chamber (005-009)." The post-1830 expansion of local government patronage — the "nomination of parochial dignitaries" — has flooded the Chamber with placemen and local notables whose primary qualification is their usefulness to the ministry rather than their capacity for statesmanship. This is the system that has produced men like [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], who uses his political ambitions purely as vehicles for financial recovery and social climbing.

[[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s own path to the Chamber — he stands as a candidate before being diverted to the Upper House by the death of the Duc d'Orleans — illustrates the transactional nature of Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] politics. His membership on the "Council for the Department since 1826" and his tax qualification make him eligible; his opinions are whatever the Ministry requires. The Chamber, in Balzac's satirical vision, is not a deliberative body but a marketplace where loyalties are bought with collectorship appointments, [[legion-of-honor|Legion of Honor]] ribbons, and peerages.
