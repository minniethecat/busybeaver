# Bourbon Restoration

The Bourbon Restoration (*Restauration*) was the period of French history from 1814 to 1830 (interrupted by the Hundred Days in 1815) during which the House of Bourbon returned to the throne in the [[person-index|persons]] of Louis XVIII (1814–1824) and Charles X (1824–1830). The Restoration attempted to reconcile the principles of the *ancien régime* with the irreversible changes of the Revolution and Empire, creating a hybrid society of old [[nobility]], new money, and unresolved grievances. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the Restoration is not merely background: it is the economic mechanism that determines the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] family's fortunes, the political arena in which M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] operates, and the source of the social tensions that drive [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s story.

## Historical Background

Louis XVIII, the brother of Louis XVI, was restored to the throne in April 1814 following Napoleon's first abdication. He granted the Constitutional Charter of 1814, which established a limited monarchy with a bicameral legislature and guaranteed certain revolutionary gains — equality before the law, the Napoleonic Code, and the security of property, including lands nationalized during the Revolution. This attempt at compromise was challenged from the right by ultraroyalists seeking a full return to pre-revolutionary order, and from the left by liberals and Bonapartists. The assassination of the Duc de [[berry|Berry]] (1820) pushed the regime rightward. Charles X's accession in 1824 intensified the reaction, culminating in the July Ordinances of 1830 that triggered the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution]].

## The Émigré Indemnity and Aristocratic Finance

One of the Restoration's signal measures was the Law of Indemnity to Émigrés (1825), known as the *milliard des émigrés* (the "billion for the émigrés"), which compensated nobles whose lands had been confiscated during the Revolution. This law, and the system of aristocratic claims and [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s it created, is the financial engine of Balzac's plot. The indemnity transformed old [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s into negotiable instruments and made the settlement of émigré claims a form of political currency.

## In the Novel

The Restoration provides the political and economic framework for the entire [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] story. Balzac introduces the period through the lens of M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s calculations:

> From 1802 to 1815 young La Baudraye added several plots to his vineyards, and devoted himself to the culture of the vine. The Restoration seemed to him at first so insecure that he dared not go to Paris to claim his debts; but after Napoleon's death he tried to turn his father's collection of autographs into money.
>
> (005-065)

The "autographs" are the I.O.U.s and receipts that the elder [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] had collected from émigré nobles he assisted during the Revolution — [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s that become politically collectible only under the Restoration. The winegrower's journey to [[paris|Paris]] to recover [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s from the [[duc-de-navarreins|Duc de Navarreins]] "and others" is a study in how the Restoration's political economy functioned:

> It will be as well to eliminate from this story, in which moral developments play the principal part, the baser material interests which alone occupied Monsieur de la Baudraye, by briefly relating the results of his negotiations in Paris. This will also throw light on certain mysterious phenomena of contemporary history, and the underground difficulties in matters of politics which hampered the Ministry at the time of the Restoration.
>
> (005-083)

This passage is Balzac's explicit claim that the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] financial story illuminates the "mysterious phenomena of contemporary history" — the Restoration was a regime built on such transactions between old aristocratic [[usury-and-debt|debt]]ors and new claimants. The deal eventually struck — [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] receives a tax collectorship, twenty thousand francs from [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], and the [[legion-of-honor|Legion of Honor]], in exchange for surrendering his claims on the [[duc-de-navarreins|Duc de Navarreins]] (005-121) — is a miniature of Restoration political economy: titles, honors, and offices traded for the quiet settlement of inconvenient aristocratic [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s.

The Restoration's social texture pervades the novel. The emigration and return of the nobility, the precarious position of families like the [[monsieur-milaud|Milaud]]s who had profited from revolution-era land purchases, the church's renewed institutional power (the Archbishop of [[bourges|Bourges]] arranging [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s marriage), and the tension between "old" and "new" nobility — all are Restoration phenomena. When the novel's events reach their denouement in the 1840s, M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] has become "a Count, a Peer of [[tours|France]], and Commander of the [[legion-of-honor|Legion of Honor]]" (005-710) — the Restoration having provided the ladder for his ascent from provincial winegrower to member of the hereditary upper chamber.
