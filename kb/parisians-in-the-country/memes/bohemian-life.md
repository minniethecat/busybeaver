# Bohemian Life

Bohemian life (*la bohème*) is the [[paris|Parisian]] literary demi-monde that Balzac anatomizes through the existence of [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]] — a world of underpaid writers, kept women, chronic [[usury-and-debt|debt]], and improvised pleasure that constitutes a parallel society to bourgeois domesticity. Balzac intervenes directly in the narrative to justify the detailing of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s circumstances:

> These details of Lousteau's life and fortune are indispensable, for this penury and this bohemian existence of a man to whom Parisian luxury had become a necessity, were fated to have a cruel influence on Dinah's life.
>
> (005-847)

## Definition and Origin

Balzac introduces the term "bohemia" explicitly in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* when he describes the consequences of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s return to [[paris|Paris]] after his idyll at [[anzy|Anzy]]: "These details of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s life and fortune are indispensable, for this penury and this bohemian existence of a man to whom [[paris|Parisian]] luxury had become a necessity, were fated to have a cruel influence on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s life. Those to whom the bohemia of [[paris|Paris]] is familiar will now understand how it was that, by the end of a fortnight, the journalist, up to his ears in the literary environment, could laugh about his Baroness with his friends and even with [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]]" (005-847). The term is used with the casualness of a recognized social category — "the bohemia of [[paris|Paris]]" is assumed to be familiar to the reader, a world with its own [[place-index|geography]], economy, and moral code.

The bohemian type emerged in 1830s [[paris|Paris]] as a byproduct of the expansion of the [[newspaper-press|press]] and the literary marketplace. Young men from [[provincial-life|the provinces]] — like [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], who came from [[sancerre|Sancerre]] — flocked to [[paris|the capital]] to make their fortunes by the pen, and found themselves suspended between the poverty of the garret and the luxury of the salon, belonging to neither the working class nor the bourgeoisie.

## Balzac's Depiction

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s bohemian existence is mapped with precise geographical and economic detail. He "lived in the [[rue-des-martyrs|Rue des Martyrs]] in pretty ground-floor rooms with a garden, and splendidly furnished" — rooms let for twelve hundred francs a year (005-840). His finances follow a desperate rhythm: "The months of January, April, July, and October were, as he phrased it, his indigent months. The rent and the porter's account cleaned him out" (005-840). Despite earning "from seven to eight hundred francs a month," he "could no more find a hundred francs on his chimneypiece now, when he was making seven or eight hundred francs a month, than he could in 1822, when he was hardly getting two hundred" [005-839, 005-840].

The bohemian economy is one of perpetual consumption and perpetual scarcity. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] "took no fewer hackney cabs, spend a hundred francs in breakfasts all the same, smoked thirty francs' worth of cigars, and could never refuse the mistress of a day a dinner or a new dress" (005-840). He "dipped so deeply into the fluctuating earnings of the following months" (005-840) that solvency was structurally impossible. The bohemian lives beyond his means as a matter of principle — or rather, as a matter of the social performance that his position demands.

The social world of bohemia is defined by a network of fellow writers and artists. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s intimate circle includes "Nathan or [[bixiou|Bixiou]], as they sat smoking in his scrap of garden, looking out on an evergreen lawn as big as a dinner-table" (005-841). Their conversation is world-weary and self-mocking: "What will be the end of us? White hairs are giving us respectful hints!" (005-842). Nathan's reply — "Lord! we shall marry when we choose to give as much thought to the matter as we give to a drama or a novel" (005-843) — captures the bohemian's simultaneous contempt for and [[jealousy|envy]] of bourgeois domesticity.

The bohemian's relationship to women is triangulated between the respectable woman ([[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]), the lorette ([[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]]), and the passing fancy. "And [[florine|Florine]]?" [[bixiou|Bixiou]] retorts to Nathan's marriage fantasy, and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] answers for all of them: "Oh, we all have a [[florine|Florine]]" [005-844, 005-845]. The lorette is the bohemian's necessary counterpart — the woman who shares his world of improvisation and impermanence.

## Social Context

Bohemian life in Balzac's [[paris|Paris]] is enabled by the expansion of the periodical [[newspaper-press|press]] and the literary marketplace after the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]]. The proliferation of newspapers and reviews created a demand for copy that could be met by men like [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] — [[genius-concept|talent]]ed but undisciplined, capable of producing "his daily contribution to a newspaper, which was like the stone of Sisyphus" (005-837), but incapable of the sustained labor that produces lasting work. The bohemian is the product of a literary economy that rewards speed and volume over quality.

The bohemian world also reflects the transformation of [[paris|Paris]] under the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. The [[place-index|geography]] of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s life — the [[rue-des-martyrs|Rue des Martyrs]], the Church of Notre-Dame de [[lorette|Lorette]], the Café Riche (where he and [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] dined daily, running up "five hundred francs" in [[usury-and-debt|debt]] for a fortnight (005-1052)), the Café Anglais — maps a new [[paris|Paris]] of commercial pleasure, distinct from the aristocratic salons of the Faubourg Saint-Germain.

Balzac's treatment of bohemia is both satirical and sympathetic. He exposes the self-deception and moral carelessness of the bohemian — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s casual cruelty to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], his exploitation of [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]], his willingness to "laugh about his Baroness with his friends" (005-847). But he also registers the genuine exhaustion of the bohemian life: "Tired, sometimes, by the incessant vicissitudes of a literary life, and as much bored by amusement as a courtesan, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] would get out of the tideway and sit on the bank" (005-841). The bohemian is as trapped by his freedom as the provincial is by her constraints.

## In the Novels

*The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is Balzac's most detailed portrait of literary bohemia, but the world recurs throughout *La Comédie Humaine* — in *Lost Illusions*, *A Prince of Bohemia*, and *Cousin Bette*. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is one of Balzac's definitive bohemians, and his trajectory — from [[ambition|provincial ambition]] to [[paris|Parisian]] cynicism to permanent mediocrity — is the bohemian life cycle in miniature. Late in the novel, after the birth of his child with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] "found himself at the house of a friend's mistress in the heart of bohemia" (005-1097), drawn back to the world that defines him despite his pretense at domesticity.
