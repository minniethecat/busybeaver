# Madame Hadot

Madame Hadot (Marie-Adélaïde Barthélemy-Hadot, 1763–1821) was a French novelist active during the Consulate and First Empire periods. She is invoked in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* during the literary conversation at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], where her name serves as a benchmark for popular fiction of the Napoleonic era.

## Identity and Appearance

Madame Hadot is identified by the hostess of the château as "an authoress, who lived at the time of the Consulate" (005-594). Her full literary name, Madame Barthélemy-Hadot, [[place-index|places]] her among the recognized novelists of the late Revolutionary and Napoleonic periods. The appellation "of La Charité" (005-593) may refer to her association with the charitable or pious [[concept-index|themes]] that featured in some of her work, or to a specific geographic origin.

## Character and Behavior

As a historical figure referenced rather than a character, Madame Hadot's personality is not directly portrayed. Her inclusion in the conversation at [[anzy|Anzy]] — alongside "a Councillor of State, or Madame Barthélemy-Hadot, or the late lamented Desforges, or Sewrin" (005-571) — positions her within the literary culture of the Empire period, when wood-engraved vignettes were a mark of a book's prestige and the identity of an anonymous author could be deduced from such material clues.

## Social Relations

Madame Hadot belonged to the generation of popular novelists who bridged the gap between the sentimental fiction of the late eighteenth century and the emerging [[romanticism|Romantic movement]]. Her contemporaries included Desforges (Pierre-Jean-Baptiste Choudard Desforges, 1746–1806), a playwright and novelist, and Sewrin (Charles-Augustin Sewrin, 1771–1853), a librettist and songwriter — both mentioned alongside her in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s catalogue of possible authors (005-571).

## Role in the Plot

Madame Hadot's name arises during a scene of literary detection at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]]. When the assembled company examines an old book with wood-engraved vignettes, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] speculates on its authorship: "It may be by Madame Hadot" (005-592). The Présidente's puzzled response — "What has Madame Hadot of La Charité to do with it?" (005-593) — underscores the provincial ignorance of literary figures that the [[paris|Parisian]] visitors alternately mock and exploit. Her name thus functions as a minor signifier of the cultural distance between [[paris|Paris]] and [[provincial-life|the provinces]], a theme that runs throughout the novel.


The identification of an anonymous author becomes a small drama of provincial incomprehension and [[paris|Parisian]] knowingness:

> "It may be by Madame Hadot," replied Lousteau.

## Narrative Assessment

The reference to Madame Hadot is fleeting and functional; Balzac uses her as an authenticating detail — the kind of specific, real-world reference that grounds his fictional uni[[poetry|verse]] in historical reality. That her name requires explanation for the provincial [[person-index|characters]] (005-594) while being casually deployed by the [[paris|Parisian]] [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] reinforces the novel's cultural [[place-index|geography]].

## Historical and Literary Context

Marie-Adélaïde Barthélemy-Hadot was a prolific author of sentimental and Gothic novels, including *Les Deux Héros* (1801), *La Tour du Louvre* (1803), and *Clémence de Duras* (1807). She was part of a generation of women writers — alongside Mesdames de Genlis, Cottin, and de Souza — who dominated the French fiction market in the decades before the full emergence of [[romanticism|Romanticism]]. Her popularity waned after the Restoration, and by the time Balzac wrote *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* (published 1837, set c. 1830), she was a figure of an earlier literary epoch, her name carrying the slightly antiquated flavor that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s antiquarian curiosity exploits. The wood-engraved vignettes that prompt the identification of her authorship were a characteristic feature of Empire-era book production, before the shift to steel engraving and lithography in the 1820s.
