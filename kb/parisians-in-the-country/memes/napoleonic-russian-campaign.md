# Napoleon's Russian Campaign

Napoleon's Russian Campaign (1812) was the catastrophic invasion of Russia by the Grande Armée, which entered Russia with over 600,000 men and returned with fewer than 100,000. The campaign marked the turning point of the [[napoleonic-wars|Napoleonic Wars]] and the beginning of the Emperor's downfall. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the campaign appears as a literary motif in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne," where it serves as the force of historical destiny that tears lovers apart and transforms returning soldiers into spectral figures.

## Historical Background

By 1812, Napoleon's Continental System — the economic blockade of Britain — had created tensions with Tsar Alexander I, who withdrew from the system in 1810. Napoleon assembled the largest army Europe had yet seen, drawing troops from across his empire, and crossed the Niemen River into Russian territory in June 1812. The campaign was conceived as a short, decisive strike to compel Russian compliance. Instead, the Russian army's strategic retreat, scorched-earth tactics, and the vast distances of the Russian interior drew the French into a fatal trap.

## Key Events

The campaign's major battles included Smolensk (August) and the pyrrhic French victory at Borodino (September 7). Napoleon entered Moscow on September 14 to find the city largely abandoned and soon in flames. After waiting in vain for Russian surrender negotiations, Napoleon ordered the retreat in October. The Grande Armée's withdrawal through the Russian winter became one of military history's great catastrophes: soldiers died of cold, starvation, and Cossack attacks. The Berezina crossing (November 26–29) became synonymous with disaster. Napoleon abandoned his army in December to return to [[paris|Paris]] and shore up his political position.

## Consequences and Impact

The campaign destroyed the Grande Armée as a fighting force and emboldened Napoleon's enemies. Prussia and Austria joined Russia in the Sixth Coalition, leading to Napoleon's defeat at Leipzig (1813), the invasion of [[tours|France]] (1814), and his first abdication. The Russian campaign became a cultural touchstone of Romantic-era imagination — a narrative of glory turned to frozen disaster that Balzac's generation knew intimately. Veterans of the campaign, marked by frostbite and trauma, were living witnesses throughout Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]].

## In the Novel

The Russian Campaign enters Balzac's novel through [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne," published under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]. The poem tells the story of a Spanish girl who follows a Norman artillery captain to [[rouen|Rouen]], only to be separated from him by the call of imperial war:

> And yet she could quit the joys of Seville, its woods and fields of orange-trees, for a Norman soldier who won her love and carried her away to his hearth and home. She did not weep for her Andalusia, the Soldier was her whole joy... But the day came when he was compelled to start for Russia in the footsteps of the great Emperor.
>
> (005-331)

The parting scene — staged in [[rouen|Rouen]] Cathedral before the altar of the Virgin — exacts from Paquita a vow of absolute fidelity. The poem then traces her solitary suffering during the campaign, waiting "writhing at the window bars as she watched happy couples go by" (005-338). The soldier's return is described as a kind of living death:

> But he, pale and frozen by the cold of Russia, chilled to the very marrow, met his yearning fair one with a melancholy smile.
>
> (005-345)

The Russian cold becomes a transformative agent — not merely a physical condition but a spiritual death. The soldier who returns is not the man who left. Balzac's use of the campaign as a narrative device reveals its cultural status: by 1836, the Russian disaster was both a known historical fact and a ready-made Romantic symbol of love thwarted by the machinery of empire. The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]'s response to the poem — "Write no more. You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet" (005-358) — acknowledges the authentic power of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s imaginative projection into this historical trauma.
