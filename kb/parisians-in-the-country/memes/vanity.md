# Vanity

A minor vice elevated by Balzac to a major explanatory principle. Vanity — the desire for social recognition, the fear of appearing inferior, the pleasure of being envied — drives much of the behavior in his novels, from [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s Napoleonic poses to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary pretensions.

## Definition and Origin

Vanity occupies an ambiguous place in the moral taxonomy of the 19th century. Christian morality condemned it as a sin; bourgeois morality treated it as a weakness; but commercial society increasingly depended on it as a motor of consumption. Balzac's treatment reflects all three perspectives while adding a fourth: vanity as a form of social intelligence, a sensitivity to the opinions of others that is essential to navigating a complex social world.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac treats vanity with characteristic irony. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] "struck an attitude in front of Jenny, looking at her haughtily, one hand in his waistcoat, and his head half turned in a Napoleonic pose" (002-061) — a gesture of pure theatrical vanity that is simultaneously absurd and effective. The commercial traveler understands that to sell products, he must first sell himself, and selling oneself requires a calculated performance of self-regard.

In the provincial context, vanity takes more constrained forms. The de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] family acted "out of a feeling of vanity and imaginary pride, quite intelligible" (005-018). [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary circle is, in part, a theater of vanity — a stage on which local notables can display their cultivation. In her more honest moments, her "idleness, or perhaps sheer vanity, revealed the secret of her [[ambition|aspiration]]s" (005-124). The provincial variant of vanity is the desire not to be thought provincial, a desire that paradoxically confirms the provincial condition.

## Social Context

Balzac's period saw the democratization of vanity. No longer the prerogative of aristocrats displaying inherited status, vanity became available to anyone who could afford the right clothes, the right furnishings, the right opinions. The commercial traveler was both an agent and a beneficiary of this democratization, bringing the material tokens of vanity — fashionable hats, [[paris|Parisian]] novelties — to customers eager to distinguish themselves from their neighbors. As [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] observes, vanity is the mechanism by which society holds us: "You hold on to us by appealing to our vanity, our love of appearances" (005-1047).

## In the Novel

Vanity operates at every level of the narrative. It motivates [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s self-presentation, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary ambitions, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s cynicism, and the provincial bourgeoisie's emulation of [[paris|Parisian]] manners. It is, in Balzac's world, as fundamental a human motive as love or greed — and perhaps more reliable.
