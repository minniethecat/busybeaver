# Comte de Fontaine

The Comte de Fontaine is a significant though offstage figure in "The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]," representing the aristocratic world into which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s childhood friend Anna Grossetete has married, and against which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own compromised social position is measured. His name recurs throughout the novel as a metonym for the closed, legitimist [[society|high society]] of Restoration [[paris|Paris]] — a world to which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] desperately aspires but cannot, in her [[provincial-metamorphosis|provincial degradation]], gain entry. As a recurring character across Balzac's La Comedie Humaine, he belongs to an extensive fictional uni[[poetry|verse]] in which he plays a more active role in other narratives.

## Identity and Position in Restoration Society

The Comte de Fontaine is a figure of established aristocratic prestige in Restoration [[tours|France]]. His third son married Anna Grossetete — [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s bosom friend from [[mademoiselle-chamarolles|Mademoiselle Chamarolles]]' school in [[bourges|Bourges]] — "thanks to a fine fortune":

> Anna, thanks to a fine fortune, had married the Comte de Fontaine's third son. Thus those ladies who visited at La Baudraye were perpetually piqued by Dinah's success in leading the fashion; do what they would, they were always behind, or, as they say on the turf, distanced.
>
> (005-061)

This marriage elevates Anna into the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] and grants her access to the highest echelons of [[society|Parisian society]]. The Comte's position within the legitimist aristocracy is indicated by the company his daughter-in-law keeps at the theater: "the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] and the [[vandenesse|Comtesse Marie de Vandenesse]]" (005-1022), the latter being another recurring figure in the Comedie Humaine whose social standing is unimpeachable. Anna's husband rose to become "Director-General under the Minister of Finance" (005-119), a position that combines aristocratic pedigree with administrative power in the Restoration state apparatus. The de Fontaine family thus embodies the fusion of old [[nobility]] with new governmental influence that characterized the Restoration's political settlement.

## The de Fontaine Family in the Novel

The de Fontaine name functions in the novel primarily through its association with Anna and as a marker of the social gulf separating [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] from her former equal. Madame Piedefier, "flattered through her daughter, also allowed herself to say such things — 'My daughter, who is a very [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], was writing yesterday to Madame de Fontaine such and such a thing'" (005-074). This name-dropping reveals both the provincial hunger for [[paris|Parisian]] connection and the actual social distance: writing letters to Madame de Fontaine is not the same as being received by her. Anna, as [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]], maintains her connection to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] for a time — she keeps [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s [[autograph-collecting|autograph album]] for three months, filling it with signatures from Rossini, Meyerbeer, Victor Hugo, Lamartine, [[george-sand|George Sand]], and other luminaries (005-258). Yet this graciousness has its limits. The decisive rupture occurs when "[[anna-grosset|Anna de Fontaine]] had turned her head away in order to avoid seeing her bosom friend at the [[mademoiselle-chamarolles|Chamarolles]]' school" (005-1193). This cut direct, delivered in a theater, crystallizes for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] the full extent of her social fall — from the respectability of a married baroness to the notoriety of a journalist's public mistress.

## Contrast with Dinah de la Baudraye

The de Fontaine connection provides the novel's most painful calibration of social distance. In 1829, Anna visits [[sancerre|Sancerre]] as [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]], and the comparison devastates [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]: "Anna, who at school had been far less handsome than [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], now, as [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]], was a thousand times handsomer than the Baronne de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]" (005-119). Anna arrives in an elegant traveling chaise with a lady's maid, spends six thousand francs a year on herself alone — "as much as kept the whole household at [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-119). The narrator observes that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] "saw herself as her friend saw her — and Anna found her altered beyond recognition" (005-119). Anna's superiority, the text notes, "all lay on the surface, while her own were forever buried" (005-120). This encounter precipitates [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s turn to writing poetry under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], her first step toward the literary [[ambition]] that will eventually deliver her into [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s hands. The de Fontaine marriage thus represents both the path not taken and the social standard by which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s choices will be judged.

## Role as Social Antagonist

Though he never appears directly, the Comte de Fontaine and his family function as a collective social antagonist in the novel's moral economy. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], at the theater, finds herself in a box "next to that which Anna Grossetete had taken" and the two friends do not even acknowledge each other (005-1022), the de Fontaine party "received some of the most distinguished men of fashion" while [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] sits alone, "overpoweringly conscious of being the object of general attention" (005-1022-1023). This scene dramatizes the rigid social exclusion that attended sexual scandal in Restoration society. Later, when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] contemplates her rehabilitation as Comtesse de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]], her first thought is of revenge: "I am a countess ... I shall have the peer's blue hammer-cloth on my carriage, and the leaders of the literary world in my drawing-room — and I will look at her!" (005-1194). The de Fontaine snub is the wound that, more than any moral consideration, motivates [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s return to respectability.

## The Comte de Fontaine Across La Comedie Humaine

The Comte de Fontaine is one of Balzac's recurring [[person-index|characters]], appearing substantially in several works that predate and contextualize "The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]." He is a central figure in "Le Bal de Sceaux" (1829), where he is presented as a stern legitimist nobleman of the old school, the father of Emilie de Fontaine, whose [[vanity|pride]]ful rejection of suitable suitors forms the novella's plot. He also appears in "Les Chouans" (1829), Balzac's novel of the counter-revolutionary wars in Brittany, establishing his credentials as a loyal servant of the Bourbon cause. In the broader architecture of La Comedie Humaine, the Comte de Fontaine represents the old aristocracy's attempt to maintain its position and values in post-Revolutionary [[tours|France]] — a project that Balzac treats with a mixture of respect for its dignity and clear-eyed recognition of its anachronism. His third son's marriage to the wealthy bourgeois Anna Grossetete exemplifies the pattern of aristocratic-bourgeois alliance that Balzac anatomized across his entire fictional uni[[poetry|verse]]: old names seeking new money, new money purchasing social prestige.

## Narrative Assessment

In "The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]," the de Fontaine name functions less as a characterization than as a social signifier. Balzac expects his readers to recognize the Comte de Fontaine from his other works — to understand, without explicit exposition, what it means that Anna has married into this family. The de Fontaine connection is the novel's shorthand for everything [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has lost: aristocratic society, [[paris|Parisian]] polish, the protections of legitimate marriage, and the regard of women of her own class. When [[anna-grosset|Anna de Fontaine]] turns away her head, she is not merely snubbing a former friend; she is enforcing the social law that Balzac spent his entire career documenting — the law that a woman who steps outside the bounds of conjugal propriety forfeits the protection of her class, no matter how superior her mind or how refined her sensibilities.

## Historical and Literary Context

The position of the de Fontaine family in Restoration society reflects the historical reality of the French aristocracy after 1815. The [[bourbon-restoration|Bourbon Restoration]] attempted to reconstruct a social order shattered by the Revolution, but the attempt was always compromised by the irreversible transformations of the Revolutionary and Napoleonic periods. Marriages between impoverished aristocrats and wealthy bourgeois heiresses — like the union between the Comte de Fontaine's third son and Anna Grossetete — were a defining feature of this era, simultaneously preserving and diluting the old nobility. Balzac, himself a social climber who added the particle "de" to his name, was fascinated by these transactions and their psychological and moral consequences. The Comte de Fontaine, as a recurring character across multiple novels, embodies the historical type of the legitimist grand seigneur whose authority, though still formidable, is being steadily eroded by the economic and social forces of the nineteenth century.
