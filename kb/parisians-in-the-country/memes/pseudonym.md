# Pseudonym

Pseudonymous publication in Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is simultaneously a plot mechanism, a study in female authorship, and a satire of literary celebrity — [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] publishes as "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]," a fabricated Spanish poet whose invented biography becomes more real to the provincial public than the woman who created it.

## Definition and Origin

Pseudonymous publication was a common practice in 19th-century [[tours|France]], particularly for women writers. [[george-sand|George Sand]] ([[george-sand|Aurore Dupin]]) was the most famous example, but countless lesser-known women adopted male or foreign [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]s to circumvent the prejudices against female authorship. The [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] allowed a woman to be judged by her work rather than her sex, but it also created a split between the public author and the private woman — a split that could become psychologically unbearable.

## Balzac's Depiction: The Fabrication of Jan Diaz

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]ous career begins with her poem *Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne*, published in the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* under the name [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]. The choice of a Spanish identity is significant: Spain represented [[love|passion]], exoticism, and a certain literary gl[[love|amour]] in 1830s [[tours|France]], and the name allowed [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to claim an emotional intensity that would have been unseemly in a provincial Frenchwoman.

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s devoted admirer, transforms the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] into a full-scale literary hoax. He "collected the works of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] in a small volume printed by Desroziers at [[moulins|Moulins]]" and "wrote a little notice of the author, too early snatched from the world of letters, which was amusing to those who were in the secret" (005-191). The fabricated biography is a masterpiece of provincial mythmaking: [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] was "born at [[bourges|Bourges]] in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner" (005-192). The memoir includes "the names of the professors at the [[bourges|Bourges]] College" and "his deceased schoolfellows, such as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]], and other famous natives of the [[provincial-life|province]], who, it is said, knew the dreamy, melancholy boy, and his precocious bent towards poetry" (005-192).

The detail is the point. The biography fabricates a complete life — birthplace, parentage, education, friendships, literary influences, tragic early death — and in doing so, creates a person more real to the reading public than [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] herself. "From this point of view, however, the memoir of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], born at [[bourges|Bourges]] in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner, may very likely some day deceive the compiler of some *Universal Biography*" (005-192). Balzac's narrator observes this with amusement, but the observation contains a serious point: literary [[honor|reputation]] is always to some degree a fabrication, and the public prefers a good story to the truth.

## The Economics of Pseudonymous Fame

"Of this little yellow paper volume two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty were sold — about fifty in each department. This average of tender and poetic souls in three departments of [[tours|France]] is enough to revive the enthusiasm of writers as to the *Furia [[tours|France]]se*" (005-193). The numbers are precise and comically modest: two hundred copies, three departments, fifty readers per department. Balzac is satirizing the vanity of provincial literary ambition — the "tender and poetic souls" who constitute the entire audience for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s work — but he is also registering the real exhilaration of seeing one's words in print, of being read, of being talked about.

The [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] allows [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to experience "the intoxicating delights of success" (005-195) while remaining physically and socially invisible. She is admired by three departments "under the black cloak of the anonymous" (005-195), a phrase that captures both the protection and the thrill of [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]ity. But the thrill is shadowed by fear: "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] dreaded the malignity of [[society|provincial society]], where more than one woman, if the secret should slip out, would certainly find points of resemblance between the writer and Paquita" (005-195). The [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] is a mask that can slip.

## The Reveal

The secret cannot be kept. "[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], warned by his fellow-schoolfellows, who could not remember [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], waited for information from [[sancerre|Sancerre]], and learned that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] was a [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] assumed by a woman" (005-195). The revelation transforms [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s local [[honor|reputation]]: "in and around [[sancerre|Sancerre]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] became the rage; she was the future rival of [[george-sand|George Sand]]" (005-196).

The revelation of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s deception unfolds with the quiet efficiency of provincial gossip:

> "Lousteau, warned by his fellow-schoolfellows, who could not remember Jan Diaz, waited for information from Sancerre, and learned that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym assumed by a woman."
>
> (005-195)

The sentence is characteristically Balzacian in its com[[newspaper-press|press]]ion: a single line contains the entire arc of discovery — the suspicion, the inquiry, the confirmation — and the revelation transforms [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] from a mysterious Spanish poet into something far more interesting: a provincial woman who has successfully passed herself off as a foreign man.


But the transformation is double-edged. Her husband, "little [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]," feels "her glory a mortal blow. He alone knew the secret source of 'Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne'" (005-200). The poem, which the public reads as exotic fantasy, is in fact autobiographical — a cry of sexual frustration that her husband alone can decode. "When this terrible work was spoken of, everybody said of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] — 'Poor woman! Poor soul!' The women rejoiced in being able to pity her who had so long op[[newspaper-press|press]]ed them" (005-200). The [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]], which was meant to protect [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], ends by exposing her.

## Social Context

Women's use of [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]s in 19th-century [[tours|France]] was a response to a literary marketplace that was structurally hostile to female authorship. Women who published under their own names risked their [[honor|reputation]]s; the woman writer was assumed to be immodest, unwomanly, or worse. [[george-sand|George Sand]]'s adoption of a male [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] and male clothing was the most extreme version of a strategy that many women followed in more modest forms.

Balzac's treatment of the [[literary-hoax|Jan Diaz hoax]] is both satirical and sympathetic. He mocks the provincial machinery of literary [[honor|reputation]] — the fabricated biography, the two hundred copies, the credulous public — but he also registers the genuine creative impulse that drives [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to write, and the genuine courage it takes for a provincial woman to send her words into the world, even under a borrowed name.

## In the Novels

The [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] episode is one of Balzac's most sustained treatments of the theme of [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]]ity, but it resonates with his larger interest in the gap between public [[honor|reputation]] and private identity. Across *La Comédie Humaine*, [[person-index|characters]] adopt false names, conceal their origins, and construct public selves that bear little relation to private realities. The [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] is both a necessary strategy for survival in a hostile world and a form of self-betrayal — a mask that, once assumed, cannot easily be removed.
