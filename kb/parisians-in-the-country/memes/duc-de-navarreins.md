# Duc de Navarreins

The Duc de Navarreins is a high-ranking aristocrat of the [[bourbon-restoration|Bourbon Restoration]], serving as First Gentleman of the Bedchamber to King Charles X. A recurring figure across Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*, he appears in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* primarily as the principal [[usury-and-debt|debt]]or to [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]'s father, whose claims against the Duke drive part of the early plot.

## Identity and Appearance

The Duc de Navarreins occupies one of the most prestigious court positions under the Restoration monarchy: "first gentleman of the Bedchamber to the King" (005-046). His name stands at the head of the catalogue of great aristocratic houses — "the Navarreins, Cadignans, Langeais, Blamont-Chauvrys, Chaulieus" — that form "the Aristocracy of the 'Human Comedy'" (004-002). Balzac enlisted [[ferdinand-de-gramont|Ferdinand de Gramont]], a gentleman and heraldist, to devise coats of arms and mottoes for these families, placing Navarreins at the summit of this feudal symbolic order.

## Character and Behavior

The Duke's character is revealed indirectly through the diplomacy surrounding his unpaid [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s. When [[newspaper-press|press]]ed by [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] for repayment, his intermediary [[monsieur-des-lupeaulx|des Lupeaulx]] explains that "the Duc de Navarreins, who is, as you know, first gentleman of the Bedchamber to the King, does not repudiate his [[usury-and-debt|debt]]; but he cannot — Now, be reasonable" (005-046). This evasion, couched in the language of political necessity — "We are emerging from the pit of the Revolution. — And you yourself are noble — He simply cannot pay" (005-046) — reveals the Restoration aristocracy's reliance on status and political exigency to defer obligations that would be enforced against lesser men.

The aristocratic logic of noble in[[usury-and-debt|debt]]edness is delivered by [[monsieur-des-lupeaulx|des Lupeaulx]] with the full authority of the Restoration state behind him:

> "Well, monsieur, you understand the position of your debtors; they stand high in the King's good graces, but they have no money, and are obliged to make a good show.—Again, you know the difficulties of the political situation. The aristocracy has to be rehabilitated in the face of a very strong force of the third estate. The King's idea—and France does him scant justice—is to create a peerage as a national institution analogous to the English peerage. To realize this grand idea we need years—and millions.—Noblesse oblige. The Duc de Navarreins, who is, as you know, first gentleman of the Bedchamber to the King, does not repudiate his debt; but he cannot—Now, be reasonable.—Consider the state of politics. We are emerging from the pit of the Revolution.—And you yourself are noble—He simply cannot pay—"
>
> (005-046)

## Social Relations

The Duke occupies the pinnacle of Restoration noble society. He is "the principal [[usury-and-debt|debt]]or threatened by [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]" (005-033), placing him in an asymmetrical relationship with the provincial vine-grower. His affairs are managed through intermediaries: the Minister and [[monsieur-des-lupeaulx|des Lupeaulx]] negotiate on his behalf, and the Cardinal-Archbishop of [[bourges|Bourges]] is involved in arranging the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] marriage as part of the settlement strategy. The Duke's [[usury-and-debt|debt]] is linked to "indemnities" paid to the aristocracy — compensation for properties confiscated during the Revolution — making him emblematic of the Restoration's attempt to restore the ancien régime's economic foundations.

## Role in the Plot

The Duke of Navarreins never appears directly on stage; his role is entirely off-stage, yet consequential. [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s attempts to recover his father's claims against "the Duc de Navarreins 'and others'" (005-022) motivate his journey to [[paris|Paris]] and his dealings with the Restoration government. The [[usury-and-debt|debt]] becomes leverage: [[monsieur-des-lupeaulx|des Lupeaulx]] offers to arrange the recovery in exchange for [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] "restoring to Monseigneur the Duc de Navarreins all your claims on him" (005-052) — effectively a political favor dressed as [[usury-and-debt|debt]] forgiveness. The Duke thus functions as a symbolic figure representing the old aristocracy's financial entanglement with the rising bourgeoisie, a theme Balzac explored throughout *La Comédie Humaine*.

## Narrative Assessment

The narrator treats the Duke with the ironic detachment characteristic of Balzac's portrayal of the Restoration aristocracy. The repeated phrase "and others" — [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s own dismissive locution for his aristocratic [[usury-and-debt|debt]]ors (005-022)(005-027) — undercuts the grandeur of the Duke's titles. The elaborate justification offered by [[monsieur-des-lupeaulx|des Lupeaulx]], with its appeals to "Noblesse oblige" and the "grand idea" of creating a peerage (005-046), is presented as transparently self-serving [[eloquence|rhetoric]], exposing the gap between aristocratic pretension and financial reality.

## Historical and Literary Context

The Duc de Navarreins belongs to the interconnected uni[[poetry|verse]] of *La Comédie Humaine*, where he appears across multiple novels as a representative of the Faubourg Saint-Germain aristocracy. His financial embarrassment reflects the economic reality of the Restoration [[nobility]], many of whom held titles and court positions but struggled with the loss of revolutionary-era confiscations. The "indemnities" referenced in the novel refer to the 1825 Émigré Indemnity Law (the *milliard des émigrés*), which compensated nobles for properties seized during the Revolution — a controversial measure that Balzac, ever the analyst of money and class, wove into his fictional uni[[poetry|verse]]. The Duke's armorial bearings, devised by [[ferdinand-de-gramont|Ferdinand de Gramont]], form part of Balzac's elaborate heraldic system that lent his fictional aristocracy the texture of historical authenticity.
