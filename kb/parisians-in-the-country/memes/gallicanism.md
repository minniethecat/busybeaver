# Gallicanism

The tradition of ecclesiastical autonomy within the French Catholic Church, which asserted the liberties of the Gallican (French) Church against the centralizing claims of the papacy. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, Gallicanism is embodied — and elegized — in the character of the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]], a clergyman of the old school whose type, Balzac tells us, has disappeared from [[tours|France]].

## Definition and Origin

Gallicanism was a distinctively French tradition of church governance that reached its definitive ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion in the Declaration of the Clergy of [[tours|France]] (1682), drafted by Bossuet. The Declaration asserted key principles: the pope had authority in spiritual but not temporal matters; general councils were superior to the pope; the ancient liberties of the French Church were inviolable; and papal judgments were not irreformable without the consent of the Church.

The Gallican tradition was shaped by the peculiar relationship between the French crown and the French church. The Concordat of Bologna (1516) gave the king the right to nominate bishops, creating a French episcopate that was institutionally dependent on the monarchy and culturally distinct from Rome. Gallican clergy tended to be drawn from the aristocracy and educated in the classical-humanist tradition; they were often men of the world, comfortable in salon society, and more concerned with pastoral duties and local civility than with theological controversy.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac's portrait of the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] is among his most affectionate character sketches. The curé of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] is presented as a survivor from an earlier era:

> The Abbé Duret, Curé of Sancerre, an old man of a lost type of clergy in France, a man of the world with a liking for cards, had not dared to indulge this taste in so liberal a district as Sancerre; he, therefore, was delighted at Madame de la Baudraye's coming, and they got on together to admiration. (005-063)

The key phrase is "a lost type of clergy in [[tours|France]]." The Abbé belongs to the Gallican tradition: a "man of the world," comfortable in the drawing room, attached to the gentle pleasures of society — cards, conversation, good company. His piety is real but unemphatic; his vocation is ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ed through pastoral care and social presence.

Balzac emphasizes Duret's worldly wisdom. He is the only figure who understands both [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary ambitions and her husband's dangerous character: "The wise old priest had more than once endeavored to enlighten [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] as to her husband's character, telling her that the man could hate" (005-197). His advice about her poetry — "You will find such relief as those who write epitaphs or elegies over those whom they have lost. Pain is soothed in the heart as lines surge up in the brain" (005-124) — reflects the Gallican clergyman's comfort with human frailty.

## Social Context

The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]'s status as a "lost type" reflects a genuine historical transformation. The [[french-revolution-reign-of-terror|French Revolution]] devastated the Gallican Church: clergy were required to swear allegiance to the Civil Constitution (1790), church property was nationalized, and many clergy were executed or exiled. The Concordat of 1801 restored the Church but on terms that strengthened papal authority.

Under the Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], French Catholicism underwent a profound reorientation. The ultramontane movement — so called because it looked "beyond the mountains" to Rome — gained strength, emphasizing papal authority, devotional piety, and doctrinal orthodoxy. The old Gallican type — the worldly, cultivated, card-playing priest — was supplanted by a new model of clergy: more zealous, more Roman, more distant from secular culture.

## In the Novels

The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] serves as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s most perceptive counselor. Alone among her circle, he understands that her literary ambitions are driven by despair, and alone he dares to tell her to stop: "Write no more. You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet" (005-185). This advice is characteristically Gallican in its humane realism — the priest who has seen the world knows the poetic vocation is incompatible with feminine happiness.

Duret was also the only one besides [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] who "ever knew the clue" to the "tragicomedy" being enacted in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s house (005-124). His death removes the last restraint on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s public ambitions — "he would certainly have prevented her rushing into public life" (005-197) — marking the end of an era in which the old Gallican clergy exercised a moderating influence on [[society|provincial society]].
