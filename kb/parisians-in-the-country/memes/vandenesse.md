# Marie de Vandenesse

The Comtesse Marie de Vandenesse is a figure from the highest circles of [[parisian-society|Parisian aristocracy]] in Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*. In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, she appears only briefly, but her history with [[nathan|Raoul Nathan]] casts a long shadow over the novel's central drama.

## The Nathan Affair

Marie de Vandenesse was, before the events of the novel, the lover of the playwright and journalist [[nathan|Raoul Nathan]]. The affair brought her to the brink of social ruin. As [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] recounts to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]], the Comtesse "was within an ace of committing the utmost folly for a more celebrated man than [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] — for Nathan" (005-1036). What saved her was not her own prudence but her husband's intervention: the Comte de Vandenesse, through what [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] calls "admirable management," steered his wife back to respectability, and now "they do not even recognize each other" (005-1036) — the former lovers passing as strangers.


The Vandenesse family motto, devised by [[ferdinand-de-gramont|Ferdinand de Gramont]] for Balzac, encodes an aristocratic code of refusal in two words:

> What profound knowledge of the old feudal spirit is to be seen in the motto of the Bauséants, *Pulchrè sedens, melius agens*; in that of the Espards, *Des partem leonis*; in that of the Vandenesses, *Ne se vend*. And what elegance in the thousand details of the learned symbolism which will always show how far accuracy has been carried in my work, to which you, the poet, have contributed.
>
> (004-002)

## Appearance in the Novel

Marie de Vandenesse makes a single but significant appearance in the novel, at the first-night performance of Nathan's play. She attends alongside the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] (the former [[anna-grosset|Anna Grossetête]]) and "received" the Comtesse de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s party in the adjacent theatre box (005-1022). The scene is one of silent social judgment: [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], attending as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s mistress, finds herself seated near the very women whose respectability she has forfeited. The proximity of Marie de Vandenesse — a woman who narrowly escaped the same fate — underscores the precariousness of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s position.

## The Vandenesse Family

The Vandenesse family motto, referenced in the novel's dedication, is "Ne se vend" — "Does not sell itself" (004-002). The motto encapsulates the aristocratic [[vanity|pride]] that distinguishes families like the Vandenesses from the commercial and journalistic classes represented by [[person-index|characters]] like [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]. In the social taxonomy of Balzac's world, the Vandenesses occupy the stratum of old nobility — a position that Marie's near-fall makes all the more precarious.
