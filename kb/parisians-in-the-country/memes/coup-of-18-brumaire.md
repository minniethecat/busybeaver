# Coup of 18 Brumaire

The political coup that brought Napoleon Bonaparte to power as First Consul, explicitly named in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s story of the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]] as the political context for the Chouan rebellion.

## Historical Background

By 1799, the Directory — the five-man executive that had governed [[tours|France]] since 1795 — was widely discredited. Corruption, military setbacks, and political instability had eroded its authority. On 9 November 1799 (18 Brumaire Year VIII in the revolutionary calendar), Napoleon Bonaparte, supported by key political figures including Sieyès and his brother Lucien, dissolved the Directory and the Council of Five Hundred. A new constitution established the Consulate, with Napoleon as First Consul — effectively a military dictatorship wrapped in republican forms.

## In the Novel

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] introduces his tale of the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]] with the explicit marker: "Not long after the 18th Brumaire, there was, as you know, a call to arms in Brittany and la Vendée. The First Consul, anxious before all things for peace in [[tours|France]], opened negotiations with the rebel chiefs, and took energetic military measures" (005-1350).

## Fouché's Police

The passage immediately connects the Consulate to the police state: Napoleon "also set the Machiavelian springs of the police in movement, Fouché then being at its head" (005-1350). Joseph Fouché, the ruthless Minister of Police who served both the Revolution and Napoleon, is invoked as the dark genius behind the surveillance and re[[newspaper-press|press]]ion that frames the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]]'s story.

## Significance in Balzac's World

The coup of 18 Brumaire marks the transition from the revolutionary decade to the Napoleonic era that shapes the world of Balzac's [[person-index|characters]]. It represents the moment when military glory and authoritarian efficiency replaced republican idealism — a shift whose consequences are still being negotiated in the Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]] of the novels. [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s father navigated this transition, returning to [[tours|France]] "tired of hoping" under the Consulate when "his prospects were but slender" (005-721).
