# Revue des Deux Mondes

The *Revue des Deux Mondes* (Review of the Two Worlds) was a bi-monthly literary and political journal founded in 1829 by Prosper Mauroy and Pierre de Segur-Dupeyron. Under the editorship of Francois Buloz from 1831, it became the most prestigious and enduring periodical of 19th-century [[tours|France]], publishing works by the leading literary and intellectual figures of the age: [[george-sand|George Sand]], Alfred de Musset, Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve, [[honor|Honor]]e de Balzac himself, and many others. In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the *Revue* appears as both a benchmark of critical authority and an object of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s professional cynicism.

## Background

The *Revue des Deux Mondes* derived its name from its ambition to bridge [[tours|France]] and America — the "two worlds" of the Old and New hemispheres. Under Buloz, it shifted focus primarily to French and European literature, politics, and cultural commentary, becoming the journal of record for the French intellectual establishment. It was known for its seriousness, its rigorous critical standards, and its substantial payments to contributors. The *Revue* serialized novels, published long-form essays, and maintained a staff of distinguished critics who shaped literary [[honor|reputation]] in the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] era.

## In the Novel

The *Revue des Deux Mondes* appears twice in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, both times filtered through [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s sardonic perspective. During his performance of the mock romance *Olympia, or Roman [[revenge|Revenge]]*, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] pauses to remark on the Empire-era literary style: "At that time, to quote the words of one of the most imbecile critics in the *Revue des Deux Mondes*, literature was content with a clear sketch and the simple outline of all antique statues." This offhand dismissal serves multiple functions: it positions [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] (and by extension Balzac) as superior to the *Revue*'s critical establishment, and it performs the insider knowledge that im[[newspaper-press|press]]es [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s provincial audience.

The second reference is more significant. In the novel's climactic breakup scene — when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] tells [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] she is returning to her husband — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] delivers a long speech about Benjamin Constant's novel *Adolphe* and its critical reception:

"You have read Benjamin Constant's book very diligently; you have even studied the last critique on it; but you have read with a woman's eyes."

The "last critique" is identified by the narrator as "this fine essay by the only real critic who has written in the *Revue des Deux Mondes*" — a detailed analysis of *Adolphe* that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has used as her moral guide for understanding her own situation. The essay, which reads *Adolphe* as a tragedy of both lovers rather than a simple story of male ingratitude, has shaped [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s self-understanding but, according to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], she has misread it by identifying only with Ellenore (the abandoned mistress) rather than also with Adolphe (the man who sacrificed his career for love).

The *Revue des Deux Mondes* critique of *Adolphe* thus becomes the intellectual framework through which the novel's central relationship is interpreted by its own [[person-index|characters]]. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has made herself an Ellenore by following a critical paradigm; [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] insists she should have seen both sides. The *Revue* is the cultural mediator that makes this self-consciousness possible — the conduit through which [[paris|Parisian]] literary discourse penetrates [[provincial-life|the provinces]] and provides the scripts by which provincial lives are lived.
