# Tours

Tours is at once a real city and a narrative hinge in Balzac's [[place-index|geography]] of the [[loire|Loire Valley]] — the place where the [[paris|Parisian]] traveler pauses before plunging deeper into [[provincial-life|the provinces]], and where the violence sup[[newspaper-press|press]]ed beneath provincial respectability erupts into judicial spectacle. For [[felix-gaudissart]], it is a last outpost of urban comfort before the fatal encounter at [[vouvray]]; for the legal machinery of Restoration France, it is the seat of the assize court that pronounces sentence upon the unspeakable. As Balzac's narrative moves south and east from [[paris|the capital]], Tours marks the boundary where [[paris|Parisian]] sophistication thins into something harder, stranger, and more dangerous.

## Location and Geography

Tours is situated on the [[loire|Loire River]] in the historic [[touraine|province of Touraine]], in central France, lying between Orléans and the Atlantic coast (002-079). The city occupies a strategic position on the great river highway that linked the interior of France to the sea — a position that, in Balzac's day, placed it at the intersection of the north-south trade routes from [[paris|Paris]] to Bordeaux and the east-west river traffic of the [[loire|Loire]].

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] approaches Tours from the northeast, having spent "forty-five days in beating the county lying between [[paris|Paris]] and [[blois|Blois]]" (002-067) before arriving at what he clearly regards as a staging-post rather than a destination. He stays at the Faisan hotel, mounts his horse on the river quay, and rides out toward [[vouvray|Vouvray]] (002-079) — all of which establishes Tours as a node in [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s circuit, a place of transit rather than settlement.

The city's relationship to the surrounding countryside is defined by the contrast Balzac draws repeatedly between [[touraine|Touraine]]'s softness and the sharper character of its inhabitants. "Transplant the native of [[touraine|Touraine]], and his qualities develop and lead to great things," Balzac writes, citing Rabelais, Descartes, and others (002-081). "But, left at home, the countryman of [[touraine|Touraine]], so remarkable elsewhere, remains like the Indian on his rug, like the Turk on his divan" (002-081). Tours, as the urban capital of this region, concentrates both its cultivated ease and its corrosive indolence.

## History and Regional Character

Tours was one of the ancient capitals of the French monarchy, and Balzac insists on this royal heritage as the source of the Tourangeau temperament — "an intellect as keen and polished as it must inevitably be in a [[provincial-life|province]] where the kings of France long held their court; an ardent, artistic, poetical, and luxurious nature, but prompt to forget its first impulse" (002-080).

By the period of the novels — the late 1820s through the 1830s, under the [[bourbon-restoration|Bourbon Restoration]] and the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] — Tours had settled into the comfortable rhythms of a provincial administrative center. Its royal past was memory; its present was defined by the wine trade, the river, and the operations of the centralized French state. The suspension bridges at [[cosne|Cosne]] and [[saint-thibault|Saint-Thibault]], completed after the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]], had improved communications between [[paris|Paris]] and the south (005-002), altering Tours's role as a transit point while preserving its essential character.

Balzac's characterization of [[touraine|Touraine]] as "the true Abbey of Thelema, so much praised in Gargantua's book" (002-081) — a place of sensual ease, good cheer, and moral relaxation — applies with particular force to Tours, where the convergence of river commerce, judicial authority, and [[society|provincial society]] created a distinctive urban culture of urbane self-satisfaction.

## Economic and Social Life

Tours in Balzac's fiction is a city of commerce, but commerce of a particular kind — the wine trade. When [[margaritis|Margaritis]], the [[madness|monomania]]c of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], declares that "There is a gentleman here, from [[paris|Paris]], who has come to make champagne at Tours" (002-193), he is speaking from his delusion, but the reference to champagne production at Tours reflects the city's genuine role as a center of the [[loire|Loire]] wine industry. The region's vineyards, producing the white wines of [[vouvray|Vouvray]] and the sparkling wines of the [[loire|Loire]], were a mainstay of the local economy.

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] himself represents a newer form of commerce — the intangible commerce of insurance, [[subscription-publishing|newspaper subscription]]s, and [[speculation|financial speculation]] that was the hallmark of [[paris|Parisian]] capitalism under the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. His arrival at Tours, fresh from his successes at [[blois|Blois]] and Orléans, marks the city as a frontier between the old provincial economy and the new commercial order. That he treats Tours merely as a hotel stop before venturing into [[vouvray|Vouvray]] suggests that the city itself was already open to the [[paris|Article Paris]]; the real resistance lay deeper in the countryside.

The "rich and populous district" (002-079) surrounding Tours supplied the city with both its wealth and its character. The mix of retired soldiers ([[monsieur-mitouflet|Mitouflet]], the discharged Grenadier of the Imperial Guard (002-085)), small landowners, dyers, and vintners that Balzac depicts in the [[vouvray|Vouvray]] episode mirrors the social composition of Tours itself — a bourgeoisie of modest means and local consequence, suspicious of [[saint-simonism|Saint-Simonian doctrine]]s and [[paris|Parisian]] innovations.

## In Gaudissart the Great

In *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, Tours is the threshold between the familiar territory of [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s success and the unknown danger of [[provincial-life|the provinces]]. The day before leaving for Tours, [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] writes to Mademoiselle [[jenny-courand|Jenny Courand]] a long, boastful letter cataloguing his triumphs: sixty-two Ternaux shawls at Orléans, sixty-two *[[le-mouvement|Mouvement]]* subscriptions across his entire journey, a hundred *Globe* subscriptions, two thousand *[[journal-des-enfants|Children's Magazine]]* subscriptions [002-067 through 002-075].

He announces his intention to proceed to Tours and then to "try my hand on the deadliest country from the point of view of intelligence and speculation" (002-077) — meaning [[vouvray|Vouvray]] and its environs. The letter establishes Tours as the last civilized stop before the wilderness: a place where one can stay at a proper hotel (the Faisan), eat a proper meal, and compose a proper letter before descending into a region where, as events will prove, a madman can outwit the greatest commercial traveler of the age.

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is described setting out "one morning from the Faisan hotel, where he put up at Tours, and went to [[vouvray|Vouvray]], a rich and populous district where the public mind seemed to him to be open to conviction" (002-079). He rides "along the river quay on his nag, thinking no more of the speeches he was about to make than an actor thinks of the part he has played a hundred times" (002-079). In this moment of complacency, Tours recedes behind him as he enters the landscape that will undo his commercial infallibility.

The episode at Tours thus functions as a structural pivot: before Tours, [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is triumphant; after Tours, he meets [[margaritis]] and is comprehensively defeated. The city marks the limit of the [[paris|Parisian]] salesman's dominion.

## In The Muse of the Department

In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, Tours appears in a darker register, as the seat of judicial authority and the site of a notorious execution. The story is told by [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], the public prosecutor of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], during the journey from [[paris|Paris]]:

> "She was sentenced and executed at Tours," replied the lawyer; "but her repentance and piety had attracted interest in spite of her monstrous crime." (005-460)

The crime in question — a woman who murdered her husband in the Saint-Pierre-des-Corps quarter of Tours, salted his body, packed it into casks, and threw it piece by piece into the [[loire|Loire]] [005-456, 005-457] — is one of the most gruesome anecdotes in the novel. That it is recounted as drawing-room conversation among the travelers to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] suggests both the casual brutality of provincial justice and the gothic undercurrents beneath the placid surface of [[touraine|Touraine]] society.

The story resonates thematically with the novel's central concerns. The husband-murderess of Tours is the dark double of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]], whose own form of marital violence is psychological rather than physical. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] observes, in response to [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s reflections on the inadequacy of human justice in domestic matters, that "the victim has in many cases been for so long the tormentor that the crime would sometimes seem almost excusable if the accused could tell all" (005-462), she is thinking of her own marriage — but the Tours execution provides the dramatic context that makes her remark possible.

Tours also appears in the novel as a center of administrative patronage. A character recounts being "promised the post of Saint-Pierre-des-Corps at Tours" (005-456), and another was "promised the post of Receiver here at [[sancerre|Sancerre]]" while passing through Tours (005-445). The city's function as a node in the Restoration bureaucracy — where posts are promised, careers are made or broken, and the machinery of the state processes the lives of its subjects — is essential to Balzac's portrait of provincial France as a system of interlocking dependencies.
