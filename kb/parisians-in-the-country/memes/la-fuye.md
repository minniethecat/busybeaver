# La Fuye

La Fuye is the unpretentious country house of [[margaritis]], situated at the head of the [[vallee-coquette]] in [[vouvray|Vouvray]]. Balzac describes it with careful architectural precision as "not particularly remarkable" (002-055), yet it is one of the most memorable settings in Balzac's comic fiction — the stage for the extended dialogue in which the mad wine-grower outwits the great commercial traveler [[felix-gaudissart]] at his own game.

## Geography and Location

La Fuye occupies a privileged position at the head of the Vallee Coquette, the first house one encounters when entering the valley from the [[vouvray|Vouvray]] side. Its situation [[place-index|places]] it within the commune of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], in the department of Indre-et-[[loire|Loire]], in the heart of the [[touraine|Touraine]] wine country. The house is "surrounded by a vineyard" (002-045), integrating it into the viticultural landscape that defines the region's economy and identity.

The position at the valley's head gives La Fuye a commanding prospect over the winding course of the Vallee Coquette, while its modest scale keeps it firmly within the register of the smallholding vigneron rather than the grand cru proprietor.

## Description and Features

Balzac's description of La Fuye is uncharacteristically detailed, fixing the modest house in the reader's imagination before the comic drama unfolds within:

> "It was called La Fuye, and was not particularly remarkable. On the ground floor was a large paneled sitting-room, with a bedroom on each side for the master and mistress. The entrance was through a hall, where they dined, opening into the kitchen. This ground floor, quite lacking the external elegance for which even the humblest dwellings in Touraine are noted, was crowned by attics, to which an outside stair led up, built against one of the gable ends, and covered by a lean-to roof. A small garden, full of marigolds, seringa, and elder, divided the house from the vineyard."
>
> (002-109)

The architecture is legible as social document: the downstairs paneling speaks of vanished prosperity, the outside stair and lean-to roof of parsimonious upkeep, the marigolds and elder of a garden that has become an afterthought. La Fuye is not a property on display but a working house, and its modesty is the perfect setting for the madman who will outwit [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] at his own game.


Balzac's description of La Fuye is uncharacteristically detailed for such a modest dwelling, suggesting its narrative importance. The ground floor comprises "a large paneled sitting-room, with a bedroom on each side for the master and mistress" (002-055). The entrance is "through a hall, where they dined, opening into the kitchen" (002-055) — a layout typical of the [[touraine|Touraine]] smallholder's house, where domestic life centers on the practical circulation between cooking, eating, and receiving spaces.

The architecture is notably plain: "This ground floor, quite lacking the external elegance for which even the humblest dwellings in [[touraine|Touraine]] are noted, was crowned by attics, to which an outside stair led up, built against one of the gable ends, and covered by a lean-to roof" (002-055). This lack of elegance is itself significant — it marks La Fuye as a working property rather than a showpiece, the home of a man whose wealth lies in his vines, not his walls.

The garden is modest but charming: "A small garden, full of marigolds, seringa, and elder, divided the house from the vineyard" (002-055). The marigolds (soucis), seringa (mock-orange), and elder (sureau) are plants of the cottage garden, neither rare nor expensive, suggesting a household that values simple, fragrant pleasures. The courtyard contains "the buildings for the wine[[newspaper-press|press]]es and storage" (002-055), along with poultry — the working apparatus of a vigneron's life.

The drawing-room, where the central comic scene takes place, features "a yellow Utrecht velvet chair by the window" (002-057), in which [[margaritis|Margaritis]] sits when [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] enters. The room is the domestic theatre for one of the most elaborately sustained comic dialogues in the *Comédie Humaine*.

## In the Novel

La Fuye is the setting for the novel's central comic set-piece. It is here that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], directed by the mischievous [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]], meets [[margaritis|Margaritis]] and launches into his elaborate sales pitch, entirely unaware that he is addressing a madman. The scene is structured as a theatrical performance: [[margaritis|Margaritis]] sits in his yellow velvet chair (002-057), "settling himself in the attitude of a man sitting for his portrait" (002-059); [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] "fidgeting with his watch key, which he twisted round and round" (002-058), deploys his full repertoire of commercial [[eloquence|rhetoric]].

Three women — [[madame-margaritis]], [[madame-vernier]], and Madame Fontanieu — retreat to [[madame-margaritis|Madame Margaritis]]'s bedroom, "leaving the door open so as to hear all that went on, and intervene in case of need" (002-057). [[monsieur-vernier|Monsieur Vernier]] enters "quietly from the vineyard" through the window (002-057), completing the hidden audience. The scene is a play within a play, with [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] as the unwitting actor and the [[vouvray|Vouvray]] locals as the knowing spectators.

The dialogue that follows is a masterpiece of comic misprision. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] expounds on [[paris|the capital]]ization of [[genius-concept|talent]] and the discounting of time; [[margaritis|Margaritis]] responds with non sequiturs about having "pacified Calabria when Murat was King" (002-060). The house, with its open door and window, its hidden listeners, and its mad master playing the banker, becomes a theatre of provincial cunning triumphing over [[paris|Parisian]] sophistry.
