# July Revolution of 1830

The July Revolution of 1830 (July 27–29, the *Trois Glorieuses*) marks the invisible watershed in Balzac's fictional world. It overthrew the reactionary Charles X, last of the Bourbon kings, and installed Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King," inaugurating the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy (1830–1848)]]. For Balzac's [[person-index|characters]] — particularly [[felix-gaudissart]] — 1830 represents a rupture in the commercial and ideological order, a moment when old certainties dissolved and new opportunities emerged. (002-028)

## The Event

The revolution was triggered by Charles X's July Ordinances, which dissolved the [[french-chamber-of-deputies|Chamber of Deputies]], restricted [[newspaper-press|press]] freedom, and narrowed the electorate. Over three days (July 27–29, 1830), [[paris|Parisian]] crowds — workers, students, former soldiers of the Napoleonic Guard — erected barricades and fought the royal troops. Charles X abdicated on August 2 and fled to England; Louis-Philippe d'Orléans accepted the throne on August 9.

## In Balzac's Fiction

For [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], 1830 is the year that changed everything:

> Until 1830 Gaudissart the Great remained faithful to the Article Paris. But in that year, the Saint-Simonian mania of the day reacted on Gaudissart the Great. (002-029, 002-020)

> The Saint-Simonians were so loud in their praises of Gaudissart the Great, showed him so much attention, that he believed in their doctrine. (002-030)

The revolution opened a brief window in which [[saint-simonism]] — the gospel of industrial [[civilization|progress]] and social reorganization — seemed plausible. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] was momentarily converted:

> According to the Gaudissart philosophy, only views that could bring about the reign of commerce and of the commercial interest in France were the true ones. (002-031)

But his flirtation with ideology was short-lived. Commerce reclaimed him, and he returned to selling [[life-insurance]] and [[subscription-publishing|newspaper subscription]]s as the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]'s bourgeois order consolidated.

## Broader Significance

The July Revolution of 1830 shapes the background of both *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* and *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. It represents the triumph of the bourgeoisie — the class Balzac anatomized with such precision — and the definitive end of the aristocratic restoration attempted by Charles X. For Balzac, who was both a social conservative and an unsparing realist, the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] represented a world in which money had fully supplanted birth as the organizing principle of society.
