# Seville

The Andalusian city that figures in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* not as a physical setting but as a literary dream — the idealized birthplace of Paquita, heroine of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s confessional poem, and the embodiment of everything that provincial [[sancerre|Sancerre]] is not.

## In the Novel

Seville is encountered exclusively through [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s poem "Paquita la Sevillane" (Paquita the Sevillian), published in the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]. The poem opens with a ballad contrasting the sensual richness of Spain with the gray poverty of northern [[tours|France]]:

> Ah! if you knew the fragrant plain,
> The air, the sky, of golden Spain,
> Its fervid noons, its balmy spring,
> Sad daughters of the northern gloom,
> Of love, of heav'n, of native home,
> You never would presume to sing! (005-188)

The poem goes on to establish Seville as Paquita's origin and essence:

> Seville, you see, had been her native home,
> Seville, where skies are blue and evening sweet.
> She, at thirteen, the sovereign of the town,
> Had lovers at her feet.
> For her three Toreadors had gone to death
> Or victory; the prize to be a kiss —
> One kiss from those red lips of sweetest breath —
> A longed-for touch of bliss! (005-192)

The exoticism is deliberate and programmatic: toreadors, orange trees, moonlit dances — the full apparatus of French Romantic Spain, derived not from experience ([[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has never left [[tours|France]]) but from the literary conventions of Byron, Hugo, and Merimee.

## Paquita's Exile

The narrative arc of the poem turns on the contrast between Seville and the northern city ([[rouen|Rouen]]) to which Paquita is transported by love. "And yet she could quit the joys of Seville, its woods and fields of orange-trees, for a Norman soldier who won her love and carried her away to his hearth and home. She did not weep for her Andalusia, the Soldier was her whole joy" (005-193). Seville becomes the paradise lost, the remembered warmth that makes the cold of Normandy — and by extension, the cold of provincial [[tours|France]] — endurable only through love.

This structure — the [[love|passion]]ate southerner exiled to the grim north — is transparently autobiographical. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], the ambitious young woman of [[berry|Berry]], married to a cold and miserly husband, projects her own sense of exile onto Paquita. Where Paquita has lost Seville for [[rouen|Rouen]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has lost the promise of [[paris|Paris]] for [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. The orange groves of Andalusia are the [[literary-circle|literary salon]]s of [[paris|the capital]]; the Norman soldier is M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] — a substitution whose absurdity would not have escaped [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s conscious mind.

## Literary Context

"Paquita la Sevillane" belongs to the wave of French literary Hispanism that flourished in the 1820s and 1830s. Hugo's *Hernani* and *Ruy Blas*, Merimee's *Carmen* and *Theatre de Clara Gazul*, and Musset's *Contes d'Espagne et d'Italie* all exploited the French fascination with a romanticized Spain — a land imagined as [[love|passion]]ate, violent, Catholic, and untouched by the gray rationality of the modern north. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s Seville is entirely of this tradition: a confection of literary convention rather than observed reality.

Balzac underlines this with the observation that, after the two poems, "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]], feeling herself a poet, had a light on her brow and a flash in her eyes that made her handsomer than ever. She cast longing looks at [[paris|Paris]], aspiring to fame — and fell back into her den of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-206). The Seville of the poem is not a real place but a projection of [[paris|Paris]] — the true object of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s longing, the city where her [[genius-concept|talent]]s might be recognized and her beauty appreciated.

## "Paquita la Sevillane" in the Plot

The poem becomes a source of both fame and vulnerability for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]. Its success — "a great ferment in the departments of the Allier, the Nievre, and the [[bourges|Cher]]" (005-187) — establishes her as a provincial literary celebrity. But the poem also contains the encoded confession of her marital misery, and "she alone knew the secret source of 'Paquita la Sevillane'" (005-220). When her husband and her circle discuss the poem, "when this terrible work was spoken of, everybody said of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] — 'Poor woman! Poor soul!'" (005-220) — a pity that mortifies rather than comforts.

The poem also serves as a test of literary discernment. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] later makes a reference that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] interprets as "a reflection on 'Paquita la Sevillane'" (005-425), she looks at M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] "by an ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion that gave him a chill" (005-425) — the poem, and by extension its Spanish setting, having become so entwined with her identity that any criticism of one is an attack on the other.

## Cultural Significance

Seville in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* exemplifies the role of an imaginary elsewhere in provincial consciousness. For [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], Spain is not a country but an escape hatch — a landscape of the mind where [[love|passion]] is permitted and [[genius-concept|talent]] recognized. That she has never seen Seville is not a limitation but the condition of its power: like all Romantic elsewheres, its vividness depends on distance. To know Seville would be to lose it; to dream of it is to survive [[sancerre|Sancerre]].
