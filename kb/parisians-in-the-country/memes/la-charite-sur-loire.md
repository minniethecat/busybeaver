# La Charite-sur-Loire

A town on the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]], within the social orbit of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], that functions in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* as a source of provincial admirers and a convenient destination for strategic absences.

## Geography and Location

La Charite-sur-Loire lies on the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]], downstream from [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and [[cosne|Cosne]]. It sits within the radius of "ten leagues" that defines the social catchment area of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s salon. Balzac groups it with the right-bank towns from which "men came" to pay court to the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]: "men came from [[cosne|Cosne]], from la Charite, and from [[nevers|Nevers]], on the right bank" [005-072].

## Historical Background

La Charite-sur-Loire has a distinguished medieval history centered on the Cluniac priory of La Charite, founded in the eleventh century and once one of the most important monastic establishments in [[tours|France]]. The town was a major stop on the pilgrimage route to Santiago de Compostela. By Balzac's time, however, the town had settled into the quieter rhythms of provincial life, notable in the novel primarily for its proximity to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and its role in the local social economy.

## In the Novel

La Charite appears in the novel in two significant passages. The first establishes it as part of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s extended social network: "men came from [[cosne|Cosne]], from la Charite, and from [[nevers|Nevers]], on the right bank; from Lere, Vailly, Argent, Blancafort, and Aubigny, on the left bank, to be introduced to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]], as they used in Switzerland, to be introduced to Madame de Stael" [005-072]. The comparison to Madame de Stael — the great intellectual hostess of Coppet — is of course ironic, the provincial scale of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s celebrity made comic by the list of tiny towns from which her admirers travel.

The second appearance is more pointed. During the hunting party at [[anzy|Anzy]], when [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]] M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] has arranged to be absent from his official duties — presumably to be near [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] — M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] has also absented himself: "And [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]] is gone to La Charite!" [005-283]. [[gatien-boirouge|Gatien Boirouge]] reveals this with dramatic emphasis, the implication being that the husband's departure to La Charite on business clears the field for M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]]'s romantic maneuvering. La Charite thus serves as a narrative convenience — the plausible destination for an errand that removes an inconvenient husband from the scene.

A third, minor appearance comes in the literary-society episode, when [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], deflecting a question about the authorship of a novel, invokes "[[madame-hadot|Madame Hadot]] of La Charite" [005-340]. This reference — to a minor female author of the Consulate period — draws a blank from the provincial ladies, who have never heard of her and are shocked that "women write in the Emperor's time" [005-341]. La Charite here becomes associated, however tangentially, with the long history of provincial female authorship that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own career (as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]) both continues and transcends.

## Social Significance

In Balzac's [[place-index|geography]], the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]] represents commercial modernity — [[cosne|Cosne]] has overtaken [[sancerre|Sancerre]] in population and trade — and La Charite, as a right-bank town, partakes of this association. The men who come from La Charite to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s salon are representatives of this more dynamic world, their journey across the river a small act of cultural pilgrimage to the left bank's historic but fading grandeur.
