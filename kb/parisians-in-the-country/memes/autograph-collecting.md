# Autograph Collecting

The nineteenth-century practice of assembling handwritten inscriptions, signatures, and original contributions from celebrated figures into a personal album. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s autograph album serves as both a marker of cultural [[ambition|aspiration]] and a tangible connection to the world of [[paris|Parisian]] celebrity that her provincial existence denies her.

## Definition and Origin

The autograph album emerged as a distinctive cultural form in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, facilitated by rising literacy, the expansion of celebrity culture, and the Romantic cult of the author's personality. The practice was particularly associated with women: young ladies kept albums in which friends and visitors inscribed [[poetry|verse]]s and sentiments, while more ambitious collectors sought the signatures of celebrated writers, musicians, and actors.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac devotes an extended passage to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s album, treating it with anthropological curiosity and gentle irony:

> Madame de la Baudraye had caught the mania for autographs; she possessed an oblong volume which deserved the name of album better than most, as two-thirds of the pages were still blank. (005-258)

The detail that "two-thirds of the pages were still blank" is quietly devastating — the album, like the ambitions it represents, is largely empty. The collection is enumerated with the precision of an inventory, including "a line from Rossini, six bars written by Meyerbeer, the four lines that Victor Hugo writes in every album, a [[poetry|verse]] from Lamartine...Calypso ne pouvait se consoler du depart d'Ulysse written by [[george-sand|George Sand]], Scribe's famous lines on the Umbrella, a sentence from [[charles-nodier|Charles Nodier]], an outline of distance by Jules Dupré, the signature of David d'Angers, and three notes written by Hector Berlioz" (005-258).

The narrator cannot resist noting the mechanical quality of some contributions — "the four lines that Victor Hugo writes in every album" carries an edge of deflation, suggesting the celebrity's contribution is as formulaic as the collector's devotion.

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] added "a song by Lacenaire — a much coveted autograph, two lines from Fieschi, and an extremely short note from Napoleon" (005-258). Lacenaire was a notorious criminal-poet, Fieschi a would-be regicide — their inclusion alongside Rossini and Hugo blurs the line between celebrity and notoriety, art and crime.

## Social Context

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was "the only person for ten leagues round who owned an album" (005-259), though "within the last two years, however, several young ladies had acquired such books." The album was a fashion spreading from [[paris|Paris]] outward, following the same trajectory as [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s "[[paris|Article Paris]]."

The elder [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s collection of autographs embodied a peculiar philosophy of mingled [[usury-and-debt|debt]] and vanity:

> After Napoleon's death he tried to turn his father's collection of autographs into money, though not understanding the deep philosophy which had thus mixed up I.O.U.s and copies of verses.
>
> (005-022)

## In the Novels

The album provides the occasion for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s first intimate exchange with [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], to whom she presents the volume for a contribution — resulting in his cynical poem "Spleen" (005-296). The album also figures in one of the novel's darkest moments. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] later threatens to use a compromising letter as a weapon, the narrator comments: "That autograph is one of those weapons which an athlete in the circus cannot afford to lay down. That note proves that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] has no heart" (005-1084). The shift from album-autograph to letter-autograph reveals [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s corruption and [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s naivety.
