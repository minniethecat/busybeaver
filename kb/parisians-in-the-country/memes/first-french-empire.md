# First French Empire

The Napoleonic imperial regime that serves as the defining historical reference point for a generation of Balzac's [[person-index|characters]] — the era against which they measure their ambitions and from which they derive their styles, careers, and disillusionments.

## Historical Background

The First Empire was proclaimed in 1804 when Napoleon Bonaparte, already First Consul for life, crowned himself Emperor of the French. The regime lasted until Napoleon's first abdication in 1814, with a brief return during the Hundred Days in 1815. It was characterized by continuous warfare across Europe, administrative centralization, the creation of the Napoleonic nobility, and the establishment of institutions — the Bank of [[tours|France]], the [[legion-of-honor|Legion of Honor]], the Civil Code — that long outlasted the Emperor himself.

## In Gaudissart the Great

The Empire is a constant stylistic and psychological reference point. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] himself strikes "a Napoleonic pose" — "one hand in his waistcoat, and his head half turned" [002-137]. The ex-Grenadier [[monsieur-mitouflet|Mitouflet]], who keeps the [[soleil-dor-vouvray|Soleil d'Or]] inn, "had served in the Imperial Guard" [002-460], lending him an aura of military authority. The Empire represents a lost world of masculine energy and decisive action, against which the commercial hustle of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] seems diminished.

## In The Muse of the Department

[[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] is repeatedly identified as a man of the Empire: "in the days of the Empire he had been a charming ballad-singer; it was this accomplishment that had won him the high position of Paymaster-General of the forces" [005-815]. His "frivolous spirit and light tone of the Empire had become ponderous" under the Restoration [005-815]. The [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]] story is set during the Napoleonic police state, with the First Consul deploying "the Machiavelian springs of the police" under Fouché [005-1350].

## The Napoleonic Nobility

Napoleon created a new aristocracy of merit and service, granting titles, arms, and endowments. The dedication to [[ferdinand-de-gramont|Ferdinand de Gramont]] notes that the Armorial of the Études includes "even the arms of the Empire" [005-673]. This Napoleonic nobility exists in tension with the old feudal aristocracy, creating a complex social landscape that [[person-index|characters]] navigate throughout the Human Comedy.

## Cultural Legacy

Napoleon's autograph — "an extremely short note from Napoleon" — appears in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s album [005-1172], alongside other cultural treasures. For Balzac's [[person-index|characters]], Napoleon is not merely a historical figure but a living presence, a standard of greatness against which they measure their own achievements. The "great Emperor" of Paquita's poem sends her soldier to Russia [005-996], turning the imperial wars into romantic material.
