# Madness

Madness in Balzac's *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* serves simultaneously as comic engine, clinical case study, and philosophical mirror — the "more than half-crazy" [[margaritis|Margaritis]], whose fixed [[concept-index|ideas]] parody [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s obsessions, is the character who, in a supreme Balzacian irony, outwits the great [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] at his own game.

## Definition and Origin

Balzac introduces [[margaritis|Margaritis]] with clinical precision: "a more than half-crazy creature named [[margaritis|Margaritis]]" (002-094). The phrase "more than half-crazy" is characteristically Balzacian — it refuses the clean binary of sanity and madness in favor of a graduated spectrum. [[margaritis|Margaritis]] is not a lunatic in the clinical sense; he "drank, ate, and walked out like any man in perfect health" (002-100). His madness is specific, intermittent, and thematically legible: it takes the form of a monomania that mirrors and mocks the monomania of capitalism itself.

## Balzac's Depiction

The clinical catalogue of [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s symptoms is meticulously compiled. He "would always go out as soon as it began to rain, to walk bareheaded among his vines" (002-095). Indoors, "he was perpetually asking for the newspaper; just to satisfy him, his wife or the maidservant would give him an old *Journal d'Indre-et-[[loire|Loire]]* and for seven years he had never discovered that it was always the same copy" (002-095). His response to social inquiries is random or non sequitur: to "How are you today, [[monsieur-margaritis|Monsieur Margaritis]]?" he replies "I have shaved, and you?"; to "Are you better, monsieur?" he answers "Jerusalem! Jerusalem!" [002-098, 002-099].

His most dangerous symptom is seasonal: "Twice or thrice in the course of five years, always about the time of the equinox, he had flown into a rage at this remark, had drawn a knife, and shrieked, 'That hussy disgraces me!'" (002-099). His wife, [[madame-margaritis|Madame Margaritis]], "certainly ran some risk in living with a man who, among other manias, insisted on always having two long knives about him, not unfrequently threatening her with them" (002-094). The detail of the equinox — the moment when day and night are balanced — suggests a cosmic rhythm to [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s madness, as if his mind were a microclimate subject to its own seasons.

The fixed [[concept-index|ideas]] that structure [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s madness — the two casks of wine that must be preserved, the sack of flour, the seven-year-old newspaper — are not arbitrary. He "insisted on always having a sack of flour in the house, and on keeping two casks of wine from the vintage, never allowing anyone to touch either the flour or the wine" (002-101). They are parodic versions of the obsessions that drive the sane: property, information, wealth. Madness, in Balzac's rendering, is not the opposite of commercial reason but its reductio ad absurdum.

## The Comic Mirror

Balzac's treatment of madness in *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* is primarily comic, but the comedy is philosophically pointed. The central joke is structural: [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], the great commercial traveler, the man who "had learned to study the secrets of the heart" and could "open the most closely tied money bags," is routed by a madman. [[monsieur-vernier|Monsieur Vernier]], the dyer who orchestrates the practical joke, sees the opportunity immediately: "At [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s very first words [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]] determined to let him loose on [[margaritis|old Margaritis]]" (002-104).

The scene that follows is a duet of mutually incomprehensible monomanias. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] delivers his pitch for [[life-insurance|intellectual capital insurance]] with mechanical eloquence: "to such men, we may capitalize the future, we may discount their [[genius-concept|talent]]s, by discounting what? — why, their time" (002-122). [[margaritis|Margaritis]] responds with equal fixation on his wine: "Will you take my two puncheons? I will give you favorable terms" (002-160). Each man hears the other only through the filter of his own obsession. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] cannot perceive [[margaritis|Margaritis]]'s madness because he is himself in the grip of a monomania — the monomania of commerce, of [[eloquence|persuasion]], of "the gift of the gab."

The madman's logic is not random but systematically inverted. When [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] speaks of capital, [[margaritis|Margaritis]] seizes on etymology: "the etymology of capital is caput — head. — Heh? — The Head of [[vouvray|Vouvray]] — the connection is obvious" (002-158). When [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] describes the insurance policy, [[margaritis|Margaritis]] translates it into his own idiolect: "I was the founder of the [[banque-territoriale|Banque Territoriale]], Rue des Fossés-Montmartre in [[paris|Paris]], in 1798" (002-138). The madman has a complete, internally coherent world in which he is a banker, a painter, a pacifier of Calabria — a world that mirrors [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s own self-aggrandizing fictions.

## Social Context

Madness in 19th-century [[tours|France]] was being reconceptualized by the emerging discipline of psychiatry. The work of Philippe Pinel, Jean-Étienne Esquirol, and others had established the idea that madness was a medical condition rather than a moral failing or demonic possession. Balzac was aware of these developments — the novel mentions "Dubuisson, Esquirol, Blanche, or other mad-doctors" (002-094) — but his treatment of [[margaritis|Margaritis]] owes more to the older tradition of the wise fool, the madman whose insanity grants him a truth-telling license denied to the sane.

[[madame-margaritis|Madame Margaritis]]'s decision to keep her husband at home rather than commit him to an asylum is explicitly economic: "the aversion is well known which country folks feel for paying a hundred louis, or perhaps a thousand crowns, the price charged at [[charenton|Charenton]] or in a private asylum" (002-094). The care of the mad in provincial [[tours|France]] is not a medical question but a financial one, and the family bears the burden that the state will not. [[madame-margaritis|Madame Margaritis]] "preferred, with lofty indignation, to keep her three thousand francs and her goodman" (002-094) — a sentence in which love and thrift are inextricably intertwined.

## In the Novels

[[margaritis|Margaritis]] is one of Balzac's most memorable comic creations, but he also belongs to a larger Balzacian gallery of monomaniacs — [[person-index|characters]] like Père Grandet (avarice), Baron Hulot (lust), and Cousin Bette ([[revenge|vengeance]]) whose fixed [[concept-index|ideas]] drive them to destruction. What distinguishes [[margaritis|Margaritis]] is that his monomania is harmless; indeed, it is productive — his wine is excellent, and his madness protects him from the predatory rationality of [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]. The madman, in this comic fable, is wiser than the salesman.
