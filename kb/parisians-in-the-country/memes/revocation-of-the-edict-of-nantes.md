# Revocation of the Edict of Nantes

The Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (October 1685) was Louis XIV's decree — the Edict of Fontainebleau — that revoked the religious and civil liberties granted to French Protestants ([[calvinism-in-france|Huguenots]]) by Henri IV's Edict of Nantes (1598). This act of state-enforced Catholic conformity triggered one of the largest population displacements in early modern Europe, as hundreds of thousands of [[calvinism-in-france|Huguenots]] fled [[tours|France]]. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the Revocation is the founding moment of the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] family's social position: their wealth, title, and very identity originate in their ancestors' strategic conversion during this persecution.

## Historical Background

The Edict of Nantes had ended the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]] by granting Protestants substantial rights: freedom of conscience, the right to worship in designated [[place-index|places]], and access to public office. Over the course of the 17th century, these rights were [[civilization|progress]]ively eroded under Louis XIII and Louis XIV through a policy of *dragonnades* (billeting troops in Protestant homes) and legal harassment. By the 1680s, the monarchy determined that religious unity was essential to absolutist rule. The Revocation formally abolished all Protestant rights, ordered the destruction of Protestant churches, and mandated Catholic baptism for all children — while forbidding emigration, on pain of the galleys for men and imprisonment for women.

## Key Events

The Revocation was not a single event but the culmination of decades of escalating persecution. The *dragonnades* of the early 1680s produced mass forced conversions through terror. When the Edict of Fontainebleau was signed on October 22, 1685, it declared Protestantism legally extinct in [[tours|France]]. Protestant pastors were given fifteen days to leave the country; their congregations were forbidden to follow. Despite the ban on emigration, an estimated 200,000 to 400,000 [[calvinism-in-france|Huguenots]] fled to England, the Dutch Republic, Prussia, Switzerland, and the Americas, taking skills, capital, and Protestant networks with them.

## Consequences and Impact

The Revocation had profound demographic, economic, and diplomatic consequences. [[tours|France]] lost a substantial portion of its merchant and artisan class; the "Protestant international" of Huguenot exiles became a permanent source of hostility to Bourbon [[tours|France]]. Within [[tours|France]], a crypto-Protestant culture persisted in regions like the Cévennes, leading to the Camisard revolt (1702–1704). The Revocation also created a class of "new converts" (*nouveaux convertis*) whose sincerity was perpetually suspect — a social ambiguity that Balzac directly exploits in his fiction.

## In the Novel

Balzac makes the Revocation the foundational moment of the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] family's social ascent. The family's entire position — their name, their arms, their claim to nobility — originates in an opportunistic conversion during the persecution:

> In the time of Louis XIV a certain sheriff named Milaud, whose forefathers had been furious Calvinists, was converted at the time of the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. To encourage this movement in one of the strongholds of Calvinism, the King gave said Milaud a good appointment in the "Waters and Forests," granted him arms and the title of Sire (or Lord) de la Baudraye, with the fief of the old and genuine La Baudrayes. The descendants of the famous Captain la Baudraye fell, sad to say, into one of the snares laid for heretics by the new decrees, and were hanged — an unworthy deed of the great King's.
>
> (005-051)

This passage is remarkable for its density of social critique. The [[monsieur-milaud|Milaud]]s — "furious Calvinists" turned loyal Catholics — acquire their nobility through a betrayal of faith, while the "genuine" [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s are executed as heretics. The irony is doubled by the narrator's parenthetical condemnation: "an unworthy deed of the great King's." The [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] legacy is thus built on a double foundation: the opportunistic conversion of one line and the extermination of another.

The theme echoes throughout the novel. The Piédefer family, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s ancestors, survived the same persecution through obscurity:

> the first upholders of Calvinism, and who, thanks to their obscurity or to some compromise with Heaven, had escaped from the persecutions under Louis XIV.
>
> (005-070)

The coat-of-arms granted to the converted [[monsieur-milaud|Milaud]]s carries an insult built into its heraldic motto, "Deo sic patet fides et hominibus" — "Thus faith lies open to God and to men" — described by Balzac as "inflicted on the converted Calvinist by Hozier the satirical" (005-375). The Revocation's legacy thus persists as a permanent irony inscribed in the family's own escutcheon.
