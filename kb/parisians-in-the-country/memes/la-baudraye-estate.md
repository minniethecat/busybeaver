# La Baudraye (Estate)

[[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], the residence of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] and her husband, is both a physical location and a symbolic space: the setting of a provincial woman's ambitions, frustrations, and aesthetic self-fashioning. Its architecture, history, and furnishings embody the novel's [[concept-index|themes]] of social imposture and the transformation of the domestic interior into a theater of identity.

## Geography and Location

[[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] is situated "within ten minutes of the town, and in the village, or, if you will, the suburb of [[saint-satur|Saint-Satur]]" (005-010). The village is one of those below [[sancerre|Sancerre]] that remind the narrator "by their situation of the smiling vineyards about Neuchâtel in Switzerland" (005-001).

The estate is visible from the Mall, [[sancerre|Sancerre]]'s promenade, where "the turrets of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-041) can be seen in the distance.

Visitors from [[sancerre|Sancerre]] could reach it by way of the *Casse-cou* (Breakneck Alley). This path ran "down the steepest part of the hillside, thickly strewn with stones, and shut in by the high banks of the vineyards on each side" (005-041), which "much abridged" the distance.

## Historical Background

The house occupies the site of the original La Baudraye manor. The original [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s were "those [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in the chief events of the history of [[berry|Le Berry]]" (005-010).

Balzac's description of the estate captures both its [[place-index|geography]] and the genealogical imposture at its heart:

> The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town-house and country-house in one, within ten minutes of the town, and in the village, or, if you will, the suburb of Saint-Satur. The La Baudrayes of the present day have, as is frequently the case, thrust themselves in, and are but a substitute for those La Baudrayes whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in the chief events of the history of Le Berry.
>
> (005-013)

This noble Protestant family lost their property through persecution. They "fell, sad to say, into one of the snares laid for heretics by the new decrees, and were hanged" (005-011). The fief was granted by Louis XIV to the converted Calvinist [[monsieur-milaud|Milaud]], who took the name and arms of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]].

After the Revolution, in 1800, "tired of hoping, and perhaps tired of lending," the ex-farmer-general "returned to [[sancerre|Sancerre]], bought back [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] out of a feeling of vanity and imaginary [[vanity|pride]], quite intelligible in a sheriff's grandson" (005-012).

The present house thus embodies the novel's central theme of genealogical imposture. The [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s of the present day "have, as is frequently the case, thrust themselves in, and are but a substitute" for the authentic line (005-010).

## Description

Balzac's description of the house is filtered through [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s transformation of it. She spent "the seven or eight thousand francs required by [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] for improvements in the house" (005-047) to redecorate "her rooms in various styles — Medieval, Louis XIV, and Pompadour" (005-047).

The ground floor — "the anteroom, the dining-room, the two drawing-rooms, and the boudoir" (005-037) — became a private museum. "Every spot even to the staircase" was crammed with "masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments" (005-037).

The house also had "five precious acres now wasted in pleasure grounds round [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-047), with flowerbeds that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] reviewed daily.

The estate's external features included turrets visible from the Mall (005-041) and a lawn where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] would walk "round and round... after dinner" (005-041) with her admirers, dispensing consolation to each in turn. The house was large enough to accommodate [[madame-piedefier|Madame Piédefer]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s mother, who "came to live with her daughter" (005-030) after the marriage.

## Economic and Social Context

The estate was the center of M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s vineyard operations. He had "added several plots to his vineyards" between 1802 and 1815, "and devoted himself to the culture of the vine" (005-015).

The combined fortunes of the household, including [[madame-piedefier|Madame Piédefer]]'s annuity, "amounted to an acknowledged income of about fifteen thousand francs" (005-030). This was a respectable but not lavish provincial establishment.

M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s stinginess was a constant source of tension: he gave [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] "two hundred francs a month for her personal expenses" (005-048). He complained that household costs had risen from his bachelor fifteen hundred francs a year to six thousand.

## In the Novel

[[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] is the primary setting of the novel's first half: the scene of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s salon, her literary circle, her card parties, her romantic intrigues. It is the stage on which she performs her identity as the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] of [[sancerre|Sancerre]].

When the household moves to the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], the rearrangement of her collected treasures "derived added value from the splendid setting which [[philibert-de-lorme|Philibert de Lorme]] seemed to have planned on purpose for this museum" (005-078). [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] is left behind, a monument to the earlier phase of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s career.

The return to [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] at the novel's end signifies the closing of the circle: [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is back where she started, her [[paris|Parisian]] adventure concluded, her provincial identity reasserted.
