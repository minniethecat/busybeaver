# Félicie Cardot

Félicie Cardot is the daughter of the prosperous [[paris|Parisian]] notary Maître [[madame-cardot|Cardot]], and a pivotal figure in the marriage plot that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] pursues as an escape from poverty before [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s unexpected arrival in [[paris|Paris]]. A young woman marked by a secret pregnancy and a shy, [[melancholy]] disposition, Félicie serves as both a symbol of bourgeois respectability and a victim of the cynical calculations that govern the [[paris|Parisian]] marriage market in Balzac's world.

## Identity and Appearance

Félicie Cardot is introduced as "a tall, fair girl with blue eyes, at once shy and languishing" (005-893). She is the daughter of a wealthy notary, Maître [[madame-cardot|Cardot]], and his wife, a woman Balzac describes as "a bigot" with "lips like two faded narrow pink ribbons" — a "penitential scrubber" made of wood, according to [[malaga|Malaga]] (005-879)(005-880). Félicie's mother, née Chiffreville, comes from a family of chemical manufacturers — what [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] ironically calls "the aristocracy of these days! Potash, I tell you!" (005-879). Félicie has an elder brother, the fourth clerk in his father's office, and a younger sister aged twelve (005-893). Behind her shy demeanor, she carries a secret: she is pregnant by a deceased head clerk, a penniless young man who "entrapped the daughter in order to marry into the business" but died of pleurisy caught leaving the theatre (005-873).

## Character and Behavior

Félicie is characterized by a quiet, emotional vulnerability that sets her apart from the calculating world of [[paris|Parisian]] matchmaking. Within three weeks of meeting [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], at her fourth dinner with him, she "carried him a cup of coffee where he stood in the window recess, and said in a low voice, with tears in her eyes: 'I will devote my whole life, monsieur, to thanking you for your sacrifice in favor of a poor girl—'" (005-894). This moment reveals her awareness of her compromised situation and her gratitude toward anyone who would accept her. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] himself is momentarily touched by the sincerity of her ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion: "'She would make a good man happy,' thought he, [[newspaper-press|press]]ing her hand in reply" (005-896). Unlike the scheming women of the [[paris|Parisian]] demi-monde, Félicie presents a genuine, if naive, emotional transparency. Her tears flow freely — when the marriage collapses, she follows her mother out of [[rue-des-martyrs|Lousteau's apartment]] "crying too, for she had become accustomed to Étienne" (005-932).


The [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]-[[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] marriage alliance anchors the domestic economy of Balzac's [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie:

> "Yes," said Lousteau, "old Camusot married little Daddy Cardot's eldest daughter, and they had high times together!"
>
> (005-878)

## Social Relations

Félicie's social world is defined entirely by her family and its connections. Her father, Maître [[madame-cardot|Cardot]], is the brother-in-law of old [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]], the député who lived with [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]] (005-877). Her mother, [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]], is a domineering bigot who, in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s cynical fantasy, is "a woman capable of killing her daughter if she knew" the secret of the pregnancy (005-879). [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]] exercises strict moral surveillance over her daughter's marriage prospects, visiting [[rue-des-martyrs|Lousteau's apartment]] unannounced to inspect his living arrangements, and delivering lengthy moral lectures on the dangers of liaisons with respectable women (005-900). Félicie has no agency in the marriage negotiations conducted on her behalf; she is a passive figure whose fate is decided by her father's urgency, her mother's moralism, and the suitor's calculations. After the collapse of the engagement to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], she is transferred to the head clerk — "the late head-clerk's deputy for two years" — who "agrees to take the girl with the business" (005-982).

## Role in the Plot

Félicie functions as the mechanism by which [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] nearly escapes his financial desperation through a mercenary marriage. [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]], acting as intermediary, presents the opportunity: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] would marry "a very consolable widow" — Félicie, already pregnant — and receive twelve thousand francs a year, a house in the Rue Saint-Lazare, and future prospects of fifteen thousand francs more (005-877). The marriage appeals to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] precisely because Félicie's misconduct leaves him "as free as a bachelor" (005-882). The plot reaches its crisis when [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]] and Félicie visit [[rue-des-martyrs|Lousteau's apartment]] unexpectedly on the very day [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] arrives from [[sancerre|Sancerre]] (005-921). Félicie and her mother "crept into a corner on seeing Étienne enter with a woman," witnessing his intimacy with the Baroness (005-921). Confronted with this scene, [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]] declares: "If my daughter were to die of it, she should never be the wife of such a man as you" (005-931). The marriage collapses within hours; the head clerk re[[place-index|places]] [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] as Félicie's suitor. Even after this disaster, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] cynically plans to salvage the situation by claiming he has seduced Félicie at Saint-Eustache and that she "bears in her bosom the pledge of our affection" (005-935).

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac presents Félicie with a measure of sympathy rare in his treatment of bourgeois [[person-index|characters]]. Her vulnerability and genuine emotion contrast sharply with the cold calculations surrounding her. Where [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] sees her as a financial instrument and her mother sees a jewel to be protected from moral contamination, Balzac allows the reader to glimpse a real young woman trapped by circumstances she did not create. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s reaction to her — being "touched" by her sincerity — briefly humanizes even the cynical journalist, before his calculating nature reasserts itself. The narrative treats Félicie's fate — being passed from one clerk to another along with the notary's business — as a grim illustration of how bourgeois marriage functions as a property transaction, where women and practices are transferred together. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] herself later uses Félicie as a point of comparison, telling [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]: "we will find you a better match than a Félicie Cardot" (005-1210), a remark that simultaneously denigrates the notary's daughter and reveals [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own entanglement in the same social logic of competitive [[marriage-as-institution|matrimony]].

## Historical and Literary Context

Félicie represents a recurrent type in Balzac's Comédie Humaine: the bourgeois daughter whose marriage is a commercial transaction managed by her family. Her situation — an unplanned pregnancy that must be concealed through a hasty marriage — reflects the harsh sexual double standard of Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]], where a woman's "fall" could destroy her social existence while men's indiscretions were tolerated or even celebrated. The [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] family, with its connections to the [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]]s (who appear across multiple Balzac novels), forms part of the dense web of [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeois families whose intermarriages and financial dealings constitute the social fabric of the Comédie Humaine. Félicie's mother, the bigoted [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]] née Chiffreville, embodies the alliance between the old Catholic [[bourgeoisie]] and the new industrial wealth of chemical manufacturing, a fusion characteristic of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]'s ruling class.
