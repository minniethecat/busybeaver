# Moulins

The prefectural city of the Allier department in central [[tours|France]], which figures in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* as a center of provincial printing and one of the towns where the search for the mysterious poet "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]" was conducted.

## Geography and Location

Moulins is situated on the Allier River, in the historic [[provincial-life|province]] of Bourbonnais, roughly sixty kilometers southwest of [[nevers|Nevers]] and seventy kilometers southeast of [[bourges|Bourges]]. It serves as the administrative seat (prefecture) of the Allier department. In Balzac's [[place-index|geography]] of provincial [[tours|France]], Moulins belongs to the network of central French cities — [[bourges|Bourges]], [[nevers|Nevers]], Moulins — that form the cultural catchment area within which the story of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] unfolds.

## Historical Background

Moulins was the historic capital of the Duchy of Bourbonnais and became the seat of a prefecture during the Napoleonic administrative reorganization of [[tours|France]]. By the 1830s, the period of the novel, Moulins was a modest provincial capital with the institutions typical of such towns: a prefecture, courts, and — crucially for the plot — printing establishments capable of producing literary works for regional circulation.

## In the Novel

Moulins plays a key role in the literary subplot of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. After [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] publishes her poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" in the *[[echo-du-morvan|Echo du Morvan]]* under the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], a search begins across the region to discover the poet's true identity: "Moulins, [[nevers|Nevers]], [[bourges|Bourges]] were searched to find [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]; but [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was impenetrable" [005-207]. The listing of Moulins first among the three cities is significant — the search was conducted across the full span of central French provincial cities, and Moulins, as a prefectural seat, had its own literary circles and local intellectuals who would have been curious about the anonymous poet in their midst.

Later, M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] takes advantage of the post-1830 liberalization of attitudes toward women writers to collect and publish the works of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]: "[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] took advantage of this outbreak of freedom to collect the works of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] in a small volume printed by Desroziers at Moulins" [005-209]. The choice of Desroziers — a provincial printer rather than a [[paris|Parisian]] publishing house — reflects both the regional nature of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s celebrity and the limitations of her ambition. A [[paris|Paris]] publication would have risked exposure; a Moulins edition preserves the mystique of the [[literary-hoax|pseudonym]] while satisfying local [[vanity|pride]].

De [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]]'s volume included "a little notice of the author, too early snatched from the world of letters" [005-209], along with the two long poems, three sonnets, and the tale *Carola*. "Of this little yellow paper volume two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty were sold — about fifty in each department" [005-211]. The three departments — the Nievre, the [[bourges|Cher]], and the Allier — correspond to the three cities searched for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]: [[nevers|Nevers]], [[bourges|Bourges]], and Moulins.

## Literary and Social Significance

The role of Moulins in the novel highlights the infrastructure of provincial literary life in nineteenth-century [[tours|France]]. Before the total centralization of French publishing in [[paris|Paris]], provincial printers like Desroziers served local markets, producing editions for regional consumption. Moulins represents this semi-autonomous provincial culture — not metropolitan, but not entirely dependent on [[paris|Paris]] for its literary life.

The printer Desroziers himself is a figure worthy of note: his willingness to publish the collected works of an unknown and possibly fictitious poet speaks to the hunger of provincial printers for any material that might sell to the "tender and poetic souls" [005-211] of the departments.
