# Saint-Thibault

Saint-Thibault is the small river-port on the [[loire|Loire]] that serves the hilltop town of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. It is the commercial lifeline of the district — the point where wine is shipped out, oak staves and other produce are landed, and the economic fortunes of the left-bank vineyards meet the trade routes of the [[loire|Loire]] valley. At the time of the novel, a suspension bridge already connected Saint-Thibault to [[cosne|Cosne]] on the right bank. (PN 687)

## Geography / Location

Saint-Thibault lies on the left bank of the [[loire|Loire]], directly below the hill of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. The town of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] sits atop a chain of hills, the last of the range that gives variety to the [[nivernais|Nivernais]], at sufficient distance from the river that the port of Saint-Thibault developed as a separate settlement. The [[loire|Loire]] floods the flats at the foot of the slopes, leaving a yellow alluvium that is extremely fertile. (PN 685–687) By the 1830s, suspension bridges had been built both at [[cosne|Cosne]] and at Saint-Thibault, replacing the former ferry crossing; travelers from [[paris|Paris]] to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] by the southern road could now cross directly rather than being ferried over. (PN 687)

## Description

Balzac describes Saint-Thibault as a "little river-port" that "thrives on the life of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]." (PN 687) The port's activities center on the wine trade: the vintages of the [[sancerre|Sancerre]] district — several high-class wines full of aroma, so nearly resembling the wines of Burgundy that "the vulgar palate is deceived" — are shipped from Saint-Thibault to the wineshops of [[paris|Paris]], the quick market indispensable for liquor that will not keep for more than seven or eight years. (PN 691) In addition to wine, oak staves are landed at Saint-Thibault, along with "all the produce brought from the upper and lower [[loire|Loire]]." (PN 687) The port thus functions as both an export point for the region's celebrated vintage and an import point for materials essential to the wine industry itself.

## Historical Significance

The construction of the suspension bridges at [[cosne|Cosne]] and Saint-Thibault represented a significant infrastructure improvement, attributed in the novel to the House of Orléans' care for "substantial improvements, though somewhat after the fashion of a husband who makes his wife presents out of her marriage portion." (PN 687) The bridge replaced the older ferry system and facilitated travel between [[paris|Paris]] and [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. Nevertheless, the novel notes that the course of trade had already shifted to the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]], and [[sancerre|Sancerre]] — despite its two bridges — was being left "more and more lonely." Within half a century the roles of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and [[cosne|Cosne]] had re[[poetry|verse]]d: [[sancerre|Sancerre]] numbered three thousand five hundred inhabitants at most, while [[cosne|Cosne]] had grown to more than six thousand. (PN 695–697)

## In the Novel

Saint-Thibault is intimately associated with [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]'s commercial obsessions. He is repeatedly seen "trotting to Saint-Thibault and attending to his business, like a merchant living on his vineyards." (PN 799) When his wife [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] launches into one of her intellectual performances — holding forth on "Negro emancipation or the improvement of convict prisons" — the little man "would take up his little blue cap and vanish without a sound, in the certainty of being able to get to Saint-Thibault to see off a cargo of puncheons, and return an hour later to find the discussion approaching a close." (PN 851) The port thus serves as both the real center of M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s economic life and his escape from the exhausting brilliance of his [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]].
