# Melancholy

The characteristic mood of Balzac's protagonists. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s Spleen poem, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s despair — each responds differently to the gap between desire and reality (005-177).

## Context in Balzac's Fiction

This concept appears in Balzac's "[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country" — comprising *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* and *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* — two stories set in the [[loire|Loire Valley]] and [[paris|Paris]] during the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy (1830–1848)]].

## In the Novels

Balzac uses this concept to illuminate the social, psychological, and historical realities of post-Revolutionary and post-Napoleonic [[tours|France]]. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] was known as a "dreamy, melancholy boy" with a precocious bent (005-192). [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], consumed by melancholy which she concealed with great effort (005-1229), suffered from a "deep and persistent melancholy, though disguised by the Countess" (005-1247). She eventually threw herself into charitable works to cure her melancholy (005-1257). In each case, melancholy marks the gap between provincial reality and [[paris|Parisian]] dreams.


> “But he, pale and frozen by the cold of Russia, chilled to the very marrow, met his yearning fair one with a melancholy smile.”
>
> (005-177)
