# Irony

Balzac's master trope pervading both stories — from [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s Napoleonic pretensions punctured by a madman to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s literary ambitions ending in domestic recapture.

## Context in Balzac's Fiction

This concept appears in Balzac's "[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country" — comprising *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* and *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* — two stories set in the [[loire|Loire Valley]] and [[paris|Paris]] during the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy (1830–1848)]].


> Mitouflet glanced at his customer, twice, with calm irony.
>
> (002-265)

## In the Novels

Balzac uses this concept to illuminate the social, psychological, and historical realities of post-Revolutionary and post-Napoleonic [[tours|France]]. In *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, the innkeeper looks at his customer "twice, with calm irony" after the madman episode (002-265). In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s irony is met and matched by [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own sarcastic speech (005-235); the journalist responds "with grave irony" to provincial pretensions (005-451). He writes his reports "seriously or in irony, according to the mood of the moment" (005-1064). Throughout, irony serves as both a weapon of the sophisticated [[paris|Parisian]] and a defense for the provincial against [[paris|Parisian]] condescension.
