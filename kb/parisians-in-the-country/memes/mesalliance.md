# Mesalliance

In the social vocabulary of 19th-century [[tours|France]], a *mésalliance* was a marriage that degraded the higher-status partner by uniting them with someone of inferior birth, fortune, or condition. Balzac, the great anatomist of marriage as economic and social institution, made the mésalliance one of his recurring [[concept-index|themes]] — and in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, he explores its paradoxes through a union that is simultaneously a mésalliance and its opposite.

## Definition and Origin

The term *mésalliance* (from *mes-*, "wrongly," and *alliance*, "union") entered the French social lexicon during the ancien régime, when the aristocracy policed its boundaries against infiltration by commoners. A mésalliance was typically the marriage of a noble to a non-noble, or of a wealthy family to one without fortune. In post-revolutionary [[tours|France]], where nobility and wealth no longer coincided, the concept became both more fraught and more ambiguous: a title without money might marry money without a title, and which party degraded which became a matter of perspective.

## Balzac's Depiction

The marriage of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah Piédefer]] to Polydore [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Milaud de la Baudraye]] is a mésalliance in two directions. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], "the daughter of one of the citizen families," is a "mere plebeian" — "the daughter of a mother who was dependent on the settlement of Piédefer's estate" (005-012). She marries a nobleman. But M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s title is recent and purchased: his grandfather was a sheriff named [[monsieur-milaud|Milaud]], a converted Calvinist who received arms and a fief from Louis XIV (005-011). The name itself is borrowed from the "old and genuine [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s" who "fell" to persecution. In the eyes of the true aristocracy, the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s of the present day "have, as is frequently the case, thrust themselves in, and are but a substitute for those [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in the chief events of the history of [[berry|Le Berry]]" (005-010).

The irony is sharpened by the husband's physical inadequacy: "a sallow and almost diaphanous creature" with false calves and thin legs, a man the [[provincial-life|province]] mockingly calls "little [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]" (005-031). The marriage is "imprudent" (005-020) in the local estimation — not because of the social disparity but because of the grotesque mismatch between a beautiful, brilliant seventeen-year-old and a sickly, miserly forty-three-year-old.

## Social Context

Under the Restoration, marriage remained the primary mechanism for transferring and consolidating wealth and status. The Civil Code (Code Napoléon) had stripped women of legal autonomy, making marriage an existential decision. For a woman of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s circumstances — brilliant, beautiful, but without fortune — marriage was a trap with only one exit. The Archbishop of [[bourges|Bourges]], her patron, "was anxious to see her married," but "every family to whom the prelate made advances took fright at a damsel gifted with the looks of a princess" who would bring only "a thousand crowns a year" (005-012). When M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] appeared, "[[madame-piedefier|Madame Piédefer]], a devotee at high services, had almost made up her own mind and her daughter's to take the first comer with well-lined pockets — the first *chien coiffé*, as they say in [[berry|Le Berry]]" (005-013).

## In the Novel

The mésalliance theme pervades the novel's marriage plots. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s opportunistic engagement to [[felicie-cardot|Félicie Cardot]] is a mésalliance in re[[poetry|verse]]: the [[paris|Parisian]] journalist, penniless and debauched, marries a notary's daughter for money. [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] counsels him cynically: "If you were to marry a lamb without spot, it might be a light woman by the end of two years. What is the damage? — an anticipated dividend!" (005-175). Every marriage in the novel is a balance sheet, and every mésalliance is a bet on future returns.

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s mésalliance is the engine of the plot: her frustration within it fuels her literary ambition, her salon, and ultimately her adultery. The novel asks whether any marriage — mesalliance or otherwise — can satisfy a woman of superior gifts in a society that offers her no other career.


> Sancerre at that time could boast of a Superior Woman, long misprized indeed, but now, about 1836, enjoying a pretty extensive local reputation.
>
> (005-011)
