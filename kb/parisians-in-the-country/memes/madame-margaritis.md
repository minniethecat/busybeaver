# Madame Margaritis

The wife of the half-crazy [[monsieur-margaritis|Monsieur Margaritis]], Madame Margaritis is "universally admired" in [[vouvray|Vouvray]] for her patient management of her husband's madness (002-094).

## Identity and Appearance

Madame Margaritis lives in [[vouvray|Vouvray]] with her mentally ill husband. She is described as "universally admired" by her neighbors (002-094), a testament to the forbearance she displays in the difficult circumstances of her marriage. The [[margaritis|Margaritis]] household includes a vineyard, the "clos [[margaritis|Margaritis]]," over which her husband maintains his delusions of business grandeur (002-153). She is connected to the local society through her friendship with [[madame-vernier|Madame Vernier]] and the other wives of [[vouvray|Vouvray]].

## Character and Behavior

Madame Margaritis's life is defined by the constant labor of managing her husband's condition. She "generally told him that she had sold all the wine in the cellar" (002-101)—a necessary fiction to calm his delusions about business affairs. The narrator notes that she deals with "all the fretfulness of a madman" (002-101) on a daily basis.

Her patience is tested before [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s visit, when she has "had more difficulty than usual in keeping him quiet" (002-102). When the practical joke leads to threats of legal action from the humiliated [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], Madame Margaritis is "afraid of an action" and works to resolve the situation (002-321), demonstrating her practical concern for the family's welfare.

## Social Relations

Madame Margaritis's primary relationship is with her husband, **[[monsieur-margaritis|Monsieur Margaritis]]**, whose madness defines her social role as caretaker. She is friendly with **[[madame-vernier|Madame Vernier]]**, who brings [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] to visit: "My wife was just going to call on Madame Margaritis with a neighbor of ours" (002-107). The visit is described as the three women going "into Madame Margaritis' room, leaving the door open" (002-114), suggesting a domestic intimacy among the women of [[vouvray|Vouvray]].

She is also connected to the wider community—when the [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]]s' practical joke threatens to escalate into a legal dispute, the local society rallies to protect her from the consequences.

## Role in the Plot

Madame Margaritis is a silent but essential figure in the comedy of "[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]." While her husband and [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] engage in their absurd dialogue, she remains in the background, having done her best to manage the situation. Her earlier difficulty in "keeping him quiet" (002-102) is what sets up the encounter—had she succeeded, the comic set-piece could not have happened.

At the story's resolution, her fear of legal consequences (002-321) motivates the community to patch up the quarrel between [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] and [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]], making her the indirect instrument of the story's peaceful conclusion. Her role thus brackets the comic action: her exertions enable it, and her anxiety resolves it.

## Narrative Assessment

> "Universally admired; for Madame Margaritis certainly ran some risk in living with a madman" (002-094) — the narrator's concise summary of her social standing, linking admiration to the perceived danger of her domestic situation.

> "With all the fretfulness of a madman" (002-101) — Balzac's unsparing language for Margaritis's condition, by implication showing what Madame Margaritis endures.

## Historical and Literary Context

Madame Margaritis represents the 19th-century figure of the long-suffering wife, a type that appears throughout Balzac's fiction. Unlike some of Balzac's more complex heroines, she is defined almost entirely by her relationship to her husband—her identity is Mrs. [[margaritis|Margaritis]], caretaker of the madman. This reflects the legal and social reality of married women in post-Revolutionary [[tours|France]], where the Napoleonic Code subordinated wives to their husbands. Yet Balzac also grants her the dignity of community admiration and a practical effectiveness that contrasts with her husband's helpless grandiosity.
