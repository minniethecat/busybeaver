# Antiquarianism

The [[love|passion]] for "antiquities" that seized [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] is treated by Balzac with characteristic ambivalence: it is at once a genuine aesthetic education, a symptom of provincial boredom, and a form of intellectual consumption that substitutes for the life she cannot live. The description of her collections provides some of the novel's richest art-historical content, embedding [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] in the material culture of Renaissance and early modern [[tours|France]].

## Definition and Origin

Antiquarianism as a systematic pursuit emerged in the Renaissance but acquired new energy in early 19th-century [[tours|France]], fueled by several forces: the dispersal of aristocratic and ecclesiastical collections during the Revolution, the Romantic fascination with the medieval past, the rise of the art market, and the pioneering work of collectors and scholars like Alexandre du Sommerard, whose collection of medieval and Renaissance objects at the Hôtel de Cluny (opened to the public in 1832) became a model for the domestic museum. Balzac directly references Sommerard: [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s "catacombs full of old things, piled up as Sommerard used to pile them" (005-037).

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] transformed her provincial house into a private museum that astonished visitors:

> By the end of five or six years the anteroom, the dining-room, the two drawing-rooms, and the boudoir which Dinah had arranged on the ground floor of La Baudraye, every spot even to the staircase, were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments. These surroundings, which were called *queer* by the neighbors, were quite in harmony with Dinah.
>
> (005-083)

## Balzac's Depiction

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s antiquarian [[love|passion]] is presented as an extension of her intellectual ambition. "Once fairly established as a [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was eager to prove her devotion to the most remarkable creations of art. She threw herself into the propaganda of the romantic school, including, under Art, poetry and painting, literature and sculpture, furniture and the opera. Thus she became a medievalist" (005-036). The [[civilization|progress]]ion is revealing: "medievalist" is a type, a social performance, as much as an authentic interest.

Yet the objects themselves are real, and Balzac catalogues them with the precision of a sale-room inventory. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] acquires "the Rougets' furniture, sold at [[issoudun|Issoudun]] early in 1824" and purchases "some very good things at [[nivernais|Nivernais]] and the Haute-[[loire|Loire]]" (005-036). "By the end of five or six years the anteroom, the dining-room, the two drawing-rooms, and the boudoir which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] had arranged on the ground floor of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], every spot even to the staircase, were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments" (005-037).

The catalogue of artists and craftsmen that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] could lecture upon is a roll-call of French decorative arts: "Jean Goujon, Michel Columb, Germain Pilon, Boulle, Van Huysum, and Boucher, the great native painter of [[berry|Le Berry]]; on Clodion, the carver of wood, on Venetian mirrors, on Brustolone, an Italian tenor who was the Michelangelo of boxwood and holm oak; on the thirteenth, fourteenth, fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth centuries, on the glazes of Bernard de Palissy, the enamels of Petitot, the engravings of Albrecht Dürer -- whom she called Dür; on illuminations on vellum, on Gothic architecture, early decorated, flamboyant and pure -- enough to turn an old man's brain and fire a young man with enthusiasm" (005-037).

## Social Context

The antiquarian market of the 1820s and 1830s was a buyer's paradise. "These 'antiquities' at that time cost much less than modern furniture" (005-037). The Revolution had flooded the market with objets d'art stripped from churches and châteaux, and provincial [[tours|France]] was full of unrecognized treasures. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s collecting is economically feasible precisely because the objects are undervalued — a situation that would change dramatically as the century [[civilization|progress]]ed and the market for French decorative arts matured.

## In the Novel

The antiquities serve multiple narrative functions. They are the setting for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s salon, im[[newspaper-press|press]]ing visitors who "came expecting something unusual; and they found their expectations surpassed" (005-037). The move to [[anzy|Anzy]] occasions "the rearrangement of her collected treasures and curiosities, which derived added value from the splendid setting which [[philibert-de-lorme|Philibert de Lorme]] seemed to have planned on purpose for this museum" (005-078). Her husband sardonically complains in [[paris|Paris]]: "my wife ruins me in curiosities and china images" (005-180) — a complaint that reveals both [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s genuine acquisitiveness and her husband's performative miserliness.

The [[love|passion]] for antiquities is also a sublimation. Denied romantic fulfillment and intellectual recognition, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] fills her house with beautiful objects and fills her conversation with the erudition they prompt. The collection is her alternative to the life she cannot have — and, in Balzac's knowing treatment, a form of consumption that ultimately consumes the collector.
