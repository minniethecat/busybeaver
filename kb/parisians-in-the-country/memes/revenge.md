# Revenge

Revenge in Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* operates simultaneously as narrative engine, psychological study, and social critique. The novel's central dinner-party scene is structured as a competition of revenge tales, each more extreme than the last, and the character of [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]] embodies a quieter but no less devastating form of methodical, financial vengeance.

## Definition and Origin

Revenge in 19th-century French fiction is a legacy of both the aristocratic code of honor and the Romantic fascination with extreme [[love|passion]]s. Balzac inherits the revenge tale from the Gothic tradition, from the *contes cruels* of the eighteenth century, and from the oral culture of provincial storytelling. But he transforms it into something distinctively modern: revenge in Balzac is not merely a plot device but a mode of social analysis, revealing the power relations that structure marriage, class, and gender.

## Balzac's Depiction: The Competition of Tales

The revenge sequence at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]] is precipitated by a theoretical debate about adultery. When [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] denounces [[paris|Parisian]] literature for making adultery its central subject, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] defends it by enumerating the great works of Western literature that all "turn on adultery" — Homer, Milton, the Psalms, Molière, Racine (005-344). The theoretical debate gives way to a practical one, and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] issues the challenge: "If [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] had not just ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ed such vehement disapproval of the immorality of stories in which the matrimonial compact is violated, I could tell you of a husband's revenge" (005-358).

What follows is a sequence of four tales, each escalating in horror. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] tells [[charles-nodier|Charles Nodier]]'s story of the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]], a Chouan prisoner whose affair with the commandant's wife provokes a revenge of exquisite cruelty: the commandant arranges a fake escape, providing a rope that stops short a hundred feet above the rocks. [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Beauvoir]] discovers the trap at the last moment and after "a brief meditation on this skilfully-planned revenge" (005-386), kills the treacherous turnkey and escapes.

[[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] follows with the celebrated *Grande Bretêche*, in which a husband walls his wife's lover alive in a cupboard and forces her to swear on a crucifix that no one is there — "You swore on that crucifix that there was no one in that closet!" (005-392). The lover starves to death standing up.

[[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] contributes a Spanish tale in which a jealous husband identifies his wife's infidelity by a mole on her arm, has the arm amputated, and presents it to the surgeon who had unwittingly revealed the identifying mark — before stabbing him to the heart with the words "Opium grows for all!" (005-441).

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], despite his earlier protests against immoral stories, tells of a [[tours|Tours]] woman who murdered her husband, salted his body, packed it in casks, and disposed of it piecemeal in the [[loire|Loire]] — keeping only the head "in pickle in one of the casks" because "it was too heavy" to carry (005-457).

The four tales form a kind of anthology of marital revenge: the psychological trap, the gothic immurement, the surgical retribution, and the grotesque butchery. They function simultaneously as entertainment, as warning, and as sublimated ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of the sexual tensions among the listeners. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] had planned the storytelling as a strategic provocation — "we will tell sundry little anecdotes of wives caught out by their husbands, killed, murdered under the most terrible circumstances. Then we shall see the faces that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] and de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] will make."

## La Baudraye: Methodical Revenge

Beyond the sensational tales, the novel presents a quieter but more sustained study of revenge in the character of [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]. The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] warns [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] explicitly: "Do not confound hatred and vengeance. They are two different sentiments. One is the instinct of small minds; the other is the outcome of law which great souls obey. God is avenged, but He does not hate" (005-199). The warning is prophetic. [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s revenge on his wife for her adultery is not violent but financial and social: he waits, he accumulates wealth, and when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is abandoned and desperate, he offers the terms of her return on his own conditions. When asked about the Duke of Bracciano's caged-husband revenge, [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] delivers the novel's coldest judgment: "do you call that revenge? — Our laws and our society are far more cruel" (005-461).

> Pooh!" said little La Baudraye, "the Duke of Bracciano, whom his wife puts into a cage, and to whom she shows herself every night in the arms of her lover, will kill her—and do you call that revenge?—Our laws and our society are far more cruel.

## Social Context

The revenge tales in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* reflect the asymmetrical power relations of 19th-century marriage. A husband's revenge on an unfaithful wife could be legal, social, and economic as well as physical; a wife's revenge on an unfaithful husband was far more constrained. The tales cluster around the moment of discovery and the husband's response — the moment when the legal and social order is most violently reasserted.

## In the Novels

The revenge competition at [[anzy|Anzy]] is one of Balzac's most virtuosic set pieces, demonstrating his command of tone (from gothic horror to black comedy to philosophical reflection), his ability to manage ensemble scenes, and his understanding of narrative as a form of social transaction. The tales are not merely told; they are deployed as moves in a complex game of seduction, intimidation, and self-revelation.
