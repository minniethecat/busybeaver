# Romanticism

The literary and artistic movement that swept across Europe in the early nineteenth century, revolutionizing conceptions of art and the artist. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, Romanticism functions as both a cultural context and a specific practice — a set of tastes, references, and postures that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] adopts as the equipment of the "[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]."

## Definition and Origin

French Romanticism emerged in the 1820s as a reaction against the neoclassical orthodoxy that had dominated French letters since the seventeenth century. Victor Hugo's preface to *Cromwell* (1827) served as a manifesto, calling for the abolition of the classical unities, the mixing of tragic and comic modes, and the liberation of language from aristocratic decorum. The movement was broader than literature alone: in painting, Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* (1830) embodied the Romantic fusion of political energy and artistic innovation; in music, Berlioz's *Symphonie fantastique* (1830) translated Romantic [[love|passion]] into orchestral form.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac presents Romanticism in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* as a cultural program adopted by [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] with characteristic thoroughness:

> Once fairly established as a Superior Woman, Dinah was eager to prove her devotion to the most remarkable creations of art. She threw herself into the propaganda of the romantic school, including, under Art, poetry and painting, literature and sculpture, furniture and the opera. Thus she became a medievalist. (005-082)

The word "propaganda" is significant: Balzac presents Romanticism not as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s spontaneous aesthetic response but as a cause she adopts and a position she takes. Her [[antiquarianism|medievalism]] — the enthusiasm for Gothic architecture, Renaissance furniture, and the material culture of pre-Revolutionary [[tours|France]] — is the provincial version of the Romantic cult of the Middle Ages popularized by Hugo and Walter Scott.

Her poem "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne" is described as emerging from "the new school, just then bringing out its eccentric [[poetry|verse]], full of vitality and imagery" (005-128). The poem's "gloomy vigor," running to "about six hundred lines" with "the masculine utterance of inex[[newspaper-press|press]]ible grief" (005-184), is a perfect exercise in Byronic Romantic convention. The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]'s warning — "Write no more. You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet" (005-185) — captures the Romantic anxiety about the incompatibility of artistic vocation and feminine identity.

## Social Context

Romanticism in provincial [[tours|France]] was necessarily a mediated phenomenon. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] encountered the movement through books, journals, and the reports of travelers. Her Romanticism was secondhand — a fact that Balzac registers without mockery, as the condition of provincial intellectual life. When "the glory of [[george-sand|George Sand]] was reflected on [[berry|Le Berry]]" after 1830, it "elicited a vast number of tenth Muses, young girls or young wives tempted from a silent life by the bait of glory" (005-190) — [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] foremost among them.

The Romantic cult of feeling provided a vocabulary for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s discontent. Her salon at [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], with its antique furniture and elevated conversation, was a Romantic space — an attempt to create, in the midst of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], the conditions of [[paris|Parisian]] literary society. Her album, filled with autographs from Hugo, Lamartine, and [[george-sand|George Sand]] (005-258), was a Romantic object — a portable connection to the world of celebrity from which [[place-index|geography]] excluded her.

## In the Novels

Romanticism pervades *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* at multiple levels. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s medievalist [[antiquarianism|collecting]] (005-083), her Byronic poetry, and her Sandist sentimentality are ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ions of the movement's central [[concept-index|themes]]: emotion over reason, the individual over society, the past over the present. Her furnishings — "catacombs full of old things" referencing Jean Goujon, Germain Pilon, Boulle, and Albrecht Dürer (005-083) — are the material culture of the Romantic sensibility.

[[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]] represents another face of Romanticism: its degeneration into professional cynicism. Where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is a Romantic by conviction, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is one by trade — a producer of the "literature of the ready pen" who "took toll from vanity in the form of drawings or pictures" (005-838). The confrontation between provincial Romantic sincerity and [[paris|Parisian]] literary commerce is one of the novel's central dramas.
