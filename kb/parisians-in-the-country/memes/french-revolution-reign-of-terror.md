# French Revolution and Reign of Terror

The French Revolution (1789-1799) and its most violent phase, the Reign of Terror (1793-1794), fundamentally restructured French society by abolishing feudal privileges, nationalizing church and émigré properties, and executing thousands — including the King and many aristocrats — on the scaffold. In Balzac's novel, the Revolution's legacy of confiscated land and disrupted inheritance shapes the material fortunes of every major character.

## Historical Background

The Revolution began in 1789 with the convocation of the Estates-General and the Declaration of the Rights of Man. By 1792 the monarchy had been abolished, and in January 1793 Louis XVI was executed. The Reign of Terror, orchestrated by the Committee of Public Safety under Robespierre, saw mass executions of perceived enemies of the Republic, including members of the Convention itself who fell victim to political purges. The Terror ended with Robespierre's own execution in July 1794 (9 Thermidor), but the Revolution's property redistribution — the sale of *biens nationaux* (nationalized lands) — remained a permanent transformation of French economic life.

## Nationalization of Lands

Two categories of property were nationalized: church lands (from 1789) and émigré lands (confiscated from nobles who fled the country). These properties were sold at auction, often at bargain prices, creating a new class of landowners from among the bourgeoisie and prosperous peasants. This redistribution is the economic foundation of the novel's plot:

- **The Piédefer family**: Moïse Piédefer, a Calvinist cloth merchant, "bought up the nationalized land, pulled down abbeys and churches with all the zeal of his ancestors, oddly enough, and married a Catholic, the only daughter of a member of the Convention who had perished on the scaffold." (PN: 733) This acquisition and marriage created the fortune that would later support his daughter [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]].

- **The [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye estate]]**: The elder [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s estate was "nationalized and sold in 1793." Decades later, "thanks to sordid economy and an indemnity paid him for the estate belonging to his father," his son Polydore was able to purchase the magnificent [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]] from the financially ruined Uxelles family. (PN: 797) The indemnity — a Restoration policy — re[[poetry|verse]]d the Revolution's confiscation in financial terms, allowing [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] to acquire what had been lost.

## The Scaffold and Social Memory

The Terror's executions haunt the novel's genealogical background. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s maternal grandfather "perished on the scaffold" as a member of the Convention — a victim of the Terror's internal purges, suggesting he was a Girondin or otherwise fell afoul of the radical Jacobins. This death left his daughter with few resources, forcing her into a marriage of convenience with the elderly [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]. The Revolution thus shaped not only property relations but also family histories and marriage strategies for two subsequent generations. (PN: 733)

## In the Novel

The Revolution is an ever-present but rarely discussed force in the novel's world. The [[person-index|characters]]' fortunes — [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s indemnity-funded land purchases, the Piédefers' Revolution-acquired wealth, the Uxelles family's ruin that made [[anzy|Anzy]] available — all trace back to the upheavals of 1789-1794. [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] alludes to the period with grim humor, comparing censorship under Napoleon to those "who went to the scaffold in 1793." The novel treats the Revolution not as a political argument but as an accomplished fact whose economic consequences continue to shape the lives of the living. (PN: 733, 797, 1682)
