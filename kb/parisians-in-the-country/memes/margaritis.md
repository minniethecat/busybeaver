# Margaritis

The accidental antagonist of *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*: an old man whose mental infirmity proves to be the perfect defense against [[advertisement|commercial persuasion]]. Margaritis is one of Balzac's most original comic creations — a madman whose madness mirrors the logic of commerce so precisely that the distinction between sanity and [[madness|insanity]] becomes momentarily unreadable.

## Identity and Appearance

Margaritis is an elderly inhabitant of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], known to the locals for his peculiar mental condition. His madness is not violent or chaotic but systematic: he is obsessed with business, endlessly proposing and discussing commercial schemes that exist only in his imagination. "The inexplicable caprices of this worthy's [[madness|insanity]]" are, the narrator notes, "closely connected with the course of my story" (002-095). He lives with his wife, who manages him with a mixture of exasperation and protective affection.


The village of [[vouvray|Vouvray]] harbors its own peculiar resident, a man whose madness is the carefully guarded secret of the commune:

> At the head of a delightful valley, known as the Vallée coquette, from its curves and bends, new at every step, and each more charming than the last, whether you go up or down the winding slope, there dwelt, in a little house surrounded by a vineyard, a more than half-crazy creature named Margaritis. This man, an Italian by birth, was married, but had no children, and his wife took care of him with a degree of courage that was universally admired; for Madame Margaritis certainly ran some risk in living with a man who, among other manias, insisted on always having two long knives about him, not unfrequently threatening her with them. But who does not know the admirable devotion with which country people care for afflicted creatures, perhaps in consequence of the discredit that attaches to a middle-class wife if she abandons her child or her husband to the tender mercies of a public asylum?
>
> (002-094)

## Character and Behavior

Margaritis's madness takes a specific and revealing form: he is perpetually engaged in imaginary commercial transactions. He "would always go out as soon as it began to rain, to walk bareheaded" — one of several "conspicuous vagaries" (002-095) that mark his detachment from conventional reality. But his obsession with business makes him, paradoxically, the perfect interlocutor for [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]: he speaks [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s language, recognizes his [[eloquence|rhetoric]]al moves, and responds with an enthusiasm that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] mistakes for genuine commercial interest.

## Social Relations

Margaritis's primary relationship is with his wife, who protects him from the consequences of his madness while enduring its daily demands. His interaction with [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is the comic high point of the story: two [[madness|monomania]]cs — one obsessed with selling, the other with buying — conducting a transaction that exists entirely in the gap between their respective delusions. The locals of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], particularly [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]], understand the situation and direct [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] toward Margaritis as a form of revenge against the intrusive [[paris|Parisian]].

## Role in the Plot

Margaritis serves as the instrument of [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s comic downfall. Directed toward the old man by [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]], [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] delivers his most elaborate sales pitch to an audience that cannot possibly understand it — or rather, understands it all too well in his own peculiar way. The revelation that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] has been outwitted by a madman is the story's culminating irony: [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]], that "bold pioneer of civilization" (002-007), is defeated not by superior cunning but by the sheer unassailable logic of [[madness|insanity]].

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac's treatment of Margaritis is a tour de force of comic narration. The madman is never merely ridiculous; his madness, like Don Quixote's, has a kind of integrity that exposes the madness lurking within normal commercial behavior. If [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s sales pitches are not themselves a form of organized delusion, what are they? Margaritis holds up a mirror to commerce and reveals its underlying irrationality.

## Historical and Literary Context

Margaritis belongs to the tradition of the wise fool — the character whose apparent irrationality conceals a deeper truth. He also reflects 19th-century medical and literary interest in [[madness|monomania]], a concept that Balzac explored throughout *La Comédie Humaine*. In a world where everyone is obsessed with something — money, fame, love — Margaritis's madness is only a more extreme version of the normal condition.
