# Château-Chinon

Château-Chinon, a small town in [[le-morvan|the Morvan]] massif of the Nièvre department, appears in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* not as an actual setting but as a narrative detail in a fabricated biography. It is the place where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] — the invented Spanish poet behind whom [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] concealed her authorship — was said to have died. The choice of location is itself significant: [[le-morvan|the Morvan]]'s isolation makes it the perfect setting for an obscure poet's unremarked death.

## Geography and Location

Château-Chinon is situated in the heart of [[le-morvan|the Morvan]], a granite massif in Burgundy known for its dense forests, harsh climate, and sparse population. The town lies in the Nièvre department, roughly 70 kilometers east of [[nevers|Nevers]] and 100 kilometers west of Dijon. In the 1830s, [[le-morvan|the Morvan]] was one of the most isolated regions of central [[tours|France]] — a place of subsistence agriculture, extensive woodlands, and limited communication with the outside world. It was, in Balzac's phrase, part of the rustic [[tours|France]] that resisted [[civilization|Parisian civilization]].

## Historical Background

The [[le-morvan|Morvan]] region had a distinctive identity within Burgundy. Its economy was based on forestry, cattle-raising, and the wet-nursing trade ([[le-morvan|Morvan]] women were famously employed as wet-nurses for [[paris|Parisian]] infants). Château-Chinon itself was a small administrative center, the seat of a sub-prefecture after the Revolution. The region's remoteness and poverty made it a byword for provincial obscurity — precisely the quality Balzac exploits in the [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] fiction.

## In the Novel

Château-Chinon appears in the mock-biography of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] composed by [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] as part of the literary hoax. The biography, printed by Desroziers at [[moulins|Moulins]], recounted the poet's "last hours, full of misery and despair," noting that "he died near Château-Chinon, unknown to all, even to the woman he had loved" (005-065). The detail is carefully crafted for pathos: a Spanish poet, son of a prisoner of war, dying in the remote [[le-morvan|Morvan]], far from his imagined [[seville|Seville]]. The [[place-index|geography]] reinforces the fiction: "the whole countryside — [[nevers|Nevers]] and [[sancerre|Sancerre]], [[le-morvan|Le Morvan]] and [[berry|Le Berry]] — was priding itself on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]], and lauding her under the name of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]" (005-072).

The conjunction of "[[le-morvan|Le Morvan]]" with [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and [[berry|Le Berry]] in this passage shows how the [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] legend had woven [[le-morvan|the Morvan]] into the regional literary [[place-index|geography]], creating a fictional poet whose tragic end in Château-Chinon was part of the local [[vanity|pride]] in having produced — or at least having buried — a genius. The fact that "two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty were sold — about fifty in each department" of the Nièvre, the Bourbonnais, the [[bourges|Cher]], and [[le-morvan|the Morvan]] (005-065) confirms the regional distribution of the hoax.

## Significance

Château-Chinon's role in the novel is emblematic of Balzac's technique of embedding fictional creations in real [[place-index|geography]]. The [[le-morvan|Morvan]]'s remoteness authenticates the fiction: of course a poet would die unknown in such a place. The detail also serves the hoax's sentimental logic — the tragic Spanish exile expiring in the cold forests of Burgundy is a Romantic cliché that Balzac both deploys and ironizes. The town's very obscurity makes it the perfect final resting place for a poet who never existed.
