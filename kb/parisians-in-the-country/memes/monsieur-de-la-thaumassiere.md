# Monsieur de la Thaumassière

A minor but memorably comic figure in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, whose single recorded action — a yawn — is sufficient to banish him permanently from [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s intellectual circle. His brief appearance illuminates both the pretensions of provincial literary culture and the precarious social dynamics of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s salon.

## Identity and Appearance

Monsieur de la Thaumassière is introduced as "one young landowner, and the dandy of Sancerre" (005-110). His family background is distinguished: he is the grandson of Gaspard Thaumas de la Thaumassière, the real 17th-century historian who wrote the authoritative *Histoire de [[berry|Berry]]*. This lineage gives him a certain hereditary standing in the region, though his claim to local prominence rests more on ancestral [[honor|reputation]] than on any demonstrated accomplishment of his own. Balzac offers no physical description of the young dandy; his identity is defined entirely by his social position, his ambition, and his catastrophic failure of etiquette.


The definitive moment of Thaumassiere's provincial disgrace is rendered with Balzacian comic precision:

> One young landowner, and the dandy of Sancerre, fell away from Dinah's good graces in consequence of some rash demonstrations. After soliciting the honor of admission to this little circle, where he flattered himself he could snatch the blossom from the constituted authorities who guarded it, he was so unfortunate as to yawn in the middle of an explanation Dinah was favoring him with—for the fourth time, it is true—of the philosophy of Kant. Monsieur de la Thaumassiere, the grandson of the historian of Le Berry, was thenceforth regarded as a man entirely bereft of soul and brains.
>
> (005-090)

## Character and Behavior

De la Thaumassière embodies the type of the young provincial dandy — a man of some local standing and personal vanity who believes his social graces entitle him to whatever he desires. Balzac notes that he "flattered himself he could snatch the blossom from the constituted authorities who guarded it" (005-110), meaning he assumed he could win [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s favor away from the established triumvirate of [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]], [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], and the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]. This confidence proves entirely misplaced. Unlike the three official devotees, who submitted to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s "exorbitant demands on their mind and attention" (005-110) in the hope of eventual romantic reward, de la Thaumassière lacked either the patience or the strategic discipline to endure the intellectual rigors of her salon.

His fatal misstep is at once trivial and definitive: "he was so unfortunate as to yawn in the middle of an explanation [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was favoring him with — for the fourth time, it is true — of the philosophy of Kant" (005-110). The yawn is an involuntary betrayal of boredom, and in the hothouse atmosphere of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s circle, a single moment of imperfect attention is unforgivable.

## Social Relations

De la Thaumassière sought admission to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s inner circle as an aspirant, positioning himself as a rival to the established admirers — [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]] [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]], the tax collector [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], and the elderly [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]. His ambition was to displace these "constituted authorities" and claim the central place in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s attention. The three regular devotees maintained their position through a disciplined system of mutual jealousy and strategic endurance; they took turns suffering [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s lectures and even contrived escapes to the card table through the Abbé's tactical protests. De la Thaumassière, as an outsider, possessed neither their network of mutual support nor their hard-won tolerance for repetition, and his solitary bid for admission collapsed at its first real test.

## Role in the Plot

De la Thaumassière's role in the narrative is limited to a single paragraph, but that paragraph is richly functional. His disastrous audition serves at least three purposes. First, it demonstrates the genuine demands [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] placed on her admirers — her intellectual pretensions were not merely a backdrop but an active requirement, enforced through social exclusion. Second, it reveals the strategic patience of the three official devotees, who endured what the dandy could not. Third, the episode provides one of the novel's most economical comic set-pieces: the young man who came to conquer leaves defeated by German idealism, his [[honor|reputation]] for "soul and brains" permanently destroyed by an involuntary physiological reflex.

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac's handling of the episode is characteristically double-edged. The narrator's parenthetical aside — "for the fourth time, it is true" — distributes the absurdity evenly: if de la Thaumassière is ridiculous for yawning, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is perhaps more ridiculous for delivering her fourth lecture on Kant to a provincial audience of dubious philosophical aptitude. The verdict on de la Thaumassière — "thenceforth regarded as a man entirely bereft of soul and brains" (005-110) — is presented with a straight face that invites the reader to smile at the disproportion between the crime and its punishment. A yawn, in this world, is not merely a breach of manners but evidence of metaphysical nullity.

## Historical and Literary Context

De la Thaumassière's connection to Gaspard Thaumas de la Thaumassière, the real historian of [[berry|Le Berry]], anchors the fictional character in authentic regional history. The historical Thaumassière (1631–1702) was a jurist and scholar whose *Histoire de [[berry|Berry]]* (1689) remained a standard reference work. By making his fictional dandy the grandson of this genuine figure, Balzac plays a familiar game of blending invented [[person-index|characters]] with documented lineages — lending verisimilitude to his provincial world while gently mocking the gap between ancestral achievement and present triviality. The Kant episode also registers a broader cultural moment: the early 19th-century vogue for German philosophy in French intellectual circles, popularized by figures like Madame de Staël in *De l'Allemagne* (1813), which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s provincial salon both imitates and travesties.
