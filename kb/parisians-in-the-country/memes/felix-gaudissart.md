# Félix Gaudissart

The eponymous "great" [[commercial-traveler]] of Balzac's novella, a character who transforms the mundane profession of [[commercial-traveler|traveling salesman]] into a theatrical performance of [[eloquence|persuasion]] and self-invention. Gaudissart operates in the world of [[life-insurance]] and [[newspaper-press]] subscriptions, peddling the instruments of modern [[civilization]] to the reluctant [[provincial-life|province]]s. He is both a type — the commercial traveler as social phenomenon — and an individual, whose vanity, energy, and ultimate vulnerability make him one of Balzac's most memorable creations. His encounter with the madman [[margaritis]] in [[vouvray|Vouvray]] produces one of the *Comédie Humaine*'s finest comic set-pieces.

## Identity and Appearance

Gaudissart is a commercial traveler, a profession he has elevated to an art form. His "line" is hats, but his real trade is [[eloquence|persuasion]] itself. Balzac describes him with the precision of a naturalist:

> Note his olive-green overcoat, his cloak, his morocco stock, his pipe, his blue-striped cotton shirt. In that figure, so genuinely original that it can stand friction, how many different natures you may discover. (002-011)

His physical presence is a carefully constructed instrument of his trade — every detail of his appearance designed to im[[newspaper-press|press]], disarm, or dominate. He was "a man of eight-and-thirty" (002-063), at the height of his powers, a "[[prospectus|living prospectus]]" whose very person constituted an advertisement.

## Character and Behavior

Gaudissart's defining trait is his eloquence — "the eloquence of a hot-water tap turned on for ever" (002-005). He is tireless, optimistic, and utterly convinced of his own greatness. Balzac presents him as:

> An ignorant instructor, a magician who has faith in his own spells, a short-sighted and believing prophet, who has never seen to the bottom of things and never will; he learns only the names of men and places, only the surface of things; and yet he is the bold pioneer of the transactions which embody to the humblest hamlet the genius of civilization. (002-004)

His vanity is theatrical and Napoleonic in scale:

> He struck an attitude in front of Jenny, looking at her haughtily, one hand in his waistcoat, and his head half turned in a Napoleonic pose. (002-061)

Beneath the bluster lies genuine commercial genius and boundless [[ambition]]:

> "Why shouldn't I be Baron Gaudissart and Peer of France? Has not Monsieur Popinot been made a Count?" (002-056)

## Social Relations

Gaudissart operates within a network of commercial and romantic relationships. His mistress [[jenny-courand]] provides a [[paris|Parisian]] base; the directors of the [[insurance-company]] are his employers and admirers; his friend [[finot|Finot]], a successful journalist, represents the social summit to which he aspires. In [[vouvray|Vouvray]], he encounters [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]], the local innkeeper, and [[margaritis]], the madman who becomes his unwitting antagonist.

> Jenny was Gaudissart's tyrant; he was seeing her home to the Rue d'Artois, where Gaudissart and Jenny went up to the fourth floor. (002-035, 002-044)

Gaudissart's relationships are fundamentally transactional — he relates to others as potential customers, competitors, or stepping-stones toward his ambition of joining the peerage.

## Role in the Plot

Gaudissart's arrival in [[vouvray|Vouvray]] sets the entire plot in motion. Sent to sell insurance and [[subscription-publishing|newspaper subscription]]s to the provincial population, he encounters resistance from the locals and is directed toward [[margaritis|Margaritis]], whom he mistakes for a wealthy potential customer:

> "I have come, monsieur," Gaudissart went on, "to request the assistance of your name and influence in the district." (002-090)

> And Gaudissart unrolled the frippery of his eloquence. (002-093)

His elaborate sales pitch to a man who cannot understand it constitutes the comic center of the story. The madman [[margaritis|Margaritis]] and his wife [[madame-margaritis|Madame Margaritis]] play their parts with devastating precision:

> They played their parts so well that Gaudissart had no suspicions, and rushed headlong into the snare. (002-108)

His eventual humiliation — learning that he has been duped by a madman — is both a personal defeat and a symbolic one: [[paris|Parisian]] commercial sophistication is not invincible. "But, get the better of Gaudissart? Never! never!" (002-060) proves to be hollow bravado.

## Narrative Assessment

> Is not the commercial traveler — a being unknown in earlier times — one of the most curious types produced by the manners and customs of this age? (002-002)

Balzac's narrative voice oscillates between admiration for Gaudissart's energy and irony at his limitations. He is "the bold pioneer of the transactions which embody to the humblest hamlet the genius of civilization" (002-007), yet he remains fundamentally superficial. The narrator both celebrates Gaudissart's vitality and exposes the hollowness of his pretensions, creating a rich comic portrait that transcends mere satire.

## Historical and Literary Context

Gaudissart belongs to Balzac's great gallery of [[madness|monomania]]cs — [[person-index|characters]] defined by a single dominating [[love|passion]]. Like Grandet with his gold or Balthazar Claës with his scientific research, Gaudissart is consumed by his commercial vocation. He represents a distinctly modern type: the professional persuader whose success depends not on what he knows but on how effectively he communicates.

The year 1830 marks a turning point in his career:

> Until 1830 Gaudissart the Great remained faithful to the article Paris. But in that year, the Saint-Simonian mania of the day reacted on Gaudissart the Great. (002-029, 002-020)

The [[saint-simonism]] movement, with its gospel of industrial [[civilization|progress]], briefly seduces him before he returns to his true faith — commerce. In this, Gaudissart anticipates the advertising men, public relations experts, and sales gurus of later eras.
