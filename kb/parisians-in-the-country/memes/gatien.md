# Gatien

The provincial young man in love: a figure of pathos and gentle satire in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, whose romantic devotion to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] illustrates the asymmetries of desire that structure [[society|provincial society]].

## Identity and Appearance

[[gatien-boirouge|Gatien Boirouge]] — referred to as both "Gatien" and "[[gatien-boirouge|Gatien de Boirouge]]" (005-256) — is the son of [[monsieur-boirouge|Monsieur Boirouge]], [[president-boirouge|the Presiding Judge of Sancerre]]. His role as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s devoted courier is captured in a characteristic scene from the [[paris|Parisian]] visitors' arrival:

> The Parisians went to spend three days at Anzy, fetched in a sort of wagonette driven by Gatien himself. The young man, under a genuine illusion, spoke of Madame de la Baudraye not only as the handsomest woman in those parts, a woman so superior that she might give George Sand a qualm, but as a woman who would produce a great sensation in Paris.
>
> (005-222) His full designation as "Gatien de Boirouge" reflects the provincial pretension to aristocratic particle, a telling detail about his family's social aspirations. As the child of a local judicial notable, he enjoys a certain social position without possessing the personal distinction or Parisian polish to transcend it. Balzac presents him as a typical provincial youth — well-meaning, somewhat naive, and susceptible to the glamour of an older, more sophisticated woman.

## Character and Behavior

Gatien's love for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is ardent, sincere, and utterly hopeless. He is part of the constellation of provincial admirers — [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]], the Abbe Duret, various local notables — who orbit around [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] without ever coming close to possessing her. His eagerness to serve her manifests in practical errands: "She made [[gatien-boirouge|Gatien Boirouge]] write to the great physician" to arrange [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s visit (005-215), and when the [[paris|Parisian]]s seemed about to escape, the locals "despatched [[gatien-boirouge|Gatien Boirouge]], who had no vineyards, to beg [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]]" to arrange a hunting party (005-221).

His youthful enthusiasm is endearing but transparent. He "nudged [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s elbow" in "anticipation of their sarcasms" (005-229), revealing both his social nervousness and his hope that the [[paris|Parisian]] visitor will share his disdain for the local bores. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] performs for the visitors, Gatien exclaims to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]: "She wants to see you deputy!" and calls her simply "An angel!" (005-254). His adoration is so naked that others exploit it: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], in an elaborate joke, makes a speech "addressing Gatien, 'who, anxious to forefend a danger, reveals small secrets'" (005-292).

Yet Gatien is not merely comic. His persistence and loyalty are genuine. At the hunting party, "the receiver of taxes, and Gatien came barefoot, like robbers" (005-470), demonstrating a willingness to endure discomfort for the social occasion. He functions as a bearer of local gossip: Gatien tells [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] what "my papa said to me" about [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s [[honor|reputation]] (005-272), and when [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] questions him, Gatien retorts, "you are ignorant of provincial life" (005-279).

## Social Relations

As [[monsieur-boirouge|the Presiding Judge]]'s son, Gatien moves within the restricted circles of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] society. His relationship to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is defined by the social gap between them — she is a married woman of established [[honor|reputation]], he is a young man of no particular accomplishment. His father's position gives him access to her circle but cannot give him what he truly wants.

Gatien's mother plays a role in his social positioning: she wishes "that she might see her Gatien in the good graces of this [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" (005-205), treating [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s favor as a form of social capital. When the [[paris|Parisian]]s arrive, Gatien drives "a sort of wagonette" to transport them (005-222), performing the role of the dutiful provincial escort. His conversation with the [[paris|Parisian]]s at the chateau reveals his provincial perspective: "You who live in [[paris|Paris]]," he says to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], "meet as many different women as there are days in the year" (005-285), his remark betraying both curiosity and [[jealousy|envy]].

## Role in the Plot

Gatien functions as a measure of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s attractions and of provincial limitations. His devotion to her underscores what is at stake in her choice between provincial admiration and [[paris|Parisian]] [[love|passion]]. He also represents the future of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] — the generation that will inherit the town's institutions without inheriting the energy or ambition to transform them.

He appears at key narrative moments as a witness and facilitator. At the hunting party, he guides the [[paris|Parisian]]s: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] "and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] left the two great men and Gatien, under the guidance of a keeper" (005-277). He asks eagerly of the night's events: "what has happened in the course of the night?" (005-467). When challenged by [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] about the provincial rumor mill, Gatien counters with the insouciance of local knowledge: "Do you suppose I could rest till it was all settled?" (005-281).

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac treats Gatien with a mixture of affection and irony. His love is genuine, but it is also a form of provincialism — a [[love|passion]] that cannot imagine its object beyond the horizons of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. He is not ridiculed, but he is not taken entirely seriously either. His question to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] — "what do you think of her?" (005-252) — encapsulates his role: a provincial admirer seeking validation from a [[paris|Parisian]] authority.

The narrative gently exposes his naivete. His laughter "at his own wit" (005-294), his transparent stratagems, and his inability to see himself as others see him all mark him as a figure of affectionate comedy. Yet there is pathos too: in a world of calculated seductions and [[paris|Parisian]] sophistication, Gatien's unguarded devotion — however futile — represents something genuine.

## Historical and Literary Context

Gatien belongs to the tradition of the young provincial lover in French fiction — a type that extends from Stendhal's Julien Sorel (in his more naive moments) to Flaubert's Frederic Moreau. He is distinguished by his modesty: unlike the ambitious provincials who drive so many 19th-century plots, Gatien wants only what he cannot have, and his ambition extends no further than his desire. His presence as the judge's son also reflects the social reality of provincial [[tours|France]], where the judiciary formed a distinct social caste and judicial families dominated local society.
