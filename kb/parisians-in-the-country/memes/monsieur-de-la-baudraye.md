# Monsieur de la Baudraye

One of Balzac's most memorable grotesques: a man whose physical deformity mirrors his emotional incapacity, yet whose financial cunning ensures that he always retains the upper hand. Monsieur de la Baudraye is the provincial husband as trap — the legal-economic reality that frustrates all of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s attempts at transcendence.

## Identity and Appearance

Born Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, he inherits a title and a modest estate that he has expanded through patient accumulation. His physical description is one of Balzac's most elaborate exercises in the grotesque: "his legs were so thin that, for mere decency, he wore false calves, whose thighs were like the arms of an average man, whose body was not unlike that of a cockchafer" (005-079). His appearance functions as a visible sign of his moral nature — just as his body is stunted and artificial, so is his capacity for genuine human connection.

## Character and Behavior

Monsieur de la Baudraye's ruling [[love|passion]] is money. After his marriage, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] discovers "the Arctic coldness of a provincial miser whenever money was in question" (005-100). He is not cruel in any active sense — he does not beat his wife or openly humiliate her — but his utter indifference to her emotional and intellectual needs constitutes a form of passive cruelty. His response to her request for money is to "put it plainly" that there will be no more than was agreed under the [[marriage-as-institution|marriage settlement]] (005-100).

## Social Relations

His marriage to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is the defining relationship of his life, and it is conducted entirely on his terms. He tolerates her literary pretensions because they keep her occupied; he tolerates her affair with [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] because it relieves him of conjugal obligations he has no interest in fulfilling. His relationship to the [[sancerre|Sancerre]] community is defined by his wealth and his title — he commands a certain grudging respect, but no affection.

## Role in the Plot

Monsieur de la Baudraye is the immovable object against which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s ambition repeatedly shatters. His "imprudent marriage" (005-028) — imprudent from the perspective of social calculation — sets the entire plot in motion. He is not an active antagonist but a structural one: his existence as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s husband is the condition that defines every choice she makes.

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac's treatment of Monsieur de la Baudraye is pitiless but not entirely unsympathetic. The narrator notes that even this grotesque figure has his reasons: he married to secure an heir, to consolidate his property, to achieve the social standing that a wife confers. He is, in his own way, as much a prisoner of provincial life as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] — but a prisoner who has made his peace with his cell.

## Historical and Literary Context

Monsieur de la Baudraye belongs to Balzac's gallery of [[madness|monomania]]cal misers, sharing DNA with Grandet and Gobseck. But he is a miser of a different order — his avarice is not the [[love|passion]]ate hoarding of Grandet but the cold calculation of a man who treats all human relationships as balance-sheet entries. He represents the provincial bourgeoisie at its least attractive: narrow, calculating, and immune to the claims of beauty or feeling.

Balzac's description of Monsieur de la Baudraye is a masterpiece of the grotesque, rendering physical deformity as a visible sign of moral poverty:

> Monsieur de la Baudraye, whose legs were so thin that, for mere decency, he wore false calves, whose thighs were like the arms of an average man, whose body was not unlike that of a cockchafer, would have been an advantageous foil to the Bailli de Ferrette. As he walked, the little vine-owner's leg-pads often twisted round on to his shins, so little did he make a secret of them, and he would thank anyone who warned him of this little mishap.
>
> (005-079)
