# Death of the Duc d'Orléans (1842)

The death of Ferdinand Philippe, Duc d'Orleans, the eldest son of King Louis-Philippe I and heir-presumptive to the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], occurred on July 13, 1842, when his carriage horses bolted on the road to Neuilly. The prince, attempting to jump clear, struck his head and died hours later at age thirty-one. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, this seemingly distant event is the catalyst that resolves the novel's central marital plot and elevates the improbable figure of little [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] to the peerage.

## Background

Ferdinand Philippe d'Orleans (born 1810) was widely regarded as the most promising figure of the Orleans dynasty. A decorated military officer who had served in Algeria, he was popular, liberal-minded, and seen as the future of the constitutional monarchy. His sudden, senseless death — essentially a traffic accident — plunged the nation into mourning and created a succession crisis. His infant son, the Comte de [[paris|Paris]], became the new heir, but the death necessitated a meeting of the Chambers to address the regency question and the composition of the Upper House.

## Political Consequences

The vacancy created by the Duc d'Orleans's death had immediate ramifications for the composition of the peerage. New peers were needed to strengthen the dynastic position in the Upper House. The Regency Bill, debated in the Chamber of Peers and [[french-chamber-of-deputies|Chamber of Deputies]], required reliable royalist votes. It was in this political environment that provincial notables with the right combination of wealth, loyalty, and pliability — men like [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]] — found their path to the Luxembourg Palace suddenly cleared of obstacles.

## In the Novel

Balzac connects the Duc d'Orleans's death to [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s elevation with remarkable narrative economy. The narrator notes that [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] "had been a member of the Council for the Department since 1826, and now, paying ten thousand francs in taxes, he was doubly qualified for a peerage under the conditions of the new legislation." He had put himself forward as a candidate for the peerage, provided he were not first elected to the [[french-chamber-of-deputies|Chamber of Deputies]]. Then:

"The fatal accident which in 1842 deprived the House of Orleans of the heir-presumptive having necessitated a meeting of the Chambers...little [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] came to present his titles to the Upper House sooner than he had expected."

The timing is crucial to the plot. [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s peerage is what enables the reconciliation with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]. Now "a Count, a Peer of [[tours|France]], and Commander of the [[legion-of-honor|Legion of Honor]]," he wishes "to cut a figure with a wife and handsomely appointed house." He writes to her, offering their [[paris|Paris]] mansion, sixty thousand francs for decoration, and her own income. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], comparing her future as a peeress with her present as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s neglected mistress, chooses respectability. The death of the heir-presumptive thus directly precipitates the novel's denouement.

The reference also underscores the fragility of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] itself. The Orleans dynasty, dependent on a single capable heir, could be destabilized by a runaway horse — a fragility that mirrors the precariousness of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s own physical and social position. The "insect" (as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] calls him) ascends to the peerage through a combination of money, patience, and the accident that killed a prince.
