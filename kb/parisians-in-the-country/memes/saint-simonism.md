# Saint-Simonism

## Definition

Saint-Simonism was a social, political, and quasi-religious doctrine that emerged from the [[concept-index|ideas]] of Claude Henri de Rouvroy, Comte de Saint-Simon (1760–1825). After Saint-Simon's death, his followers — notably Barthélemy Prosper Enfantin and Saint-Amand Bazard — systematized his teachings into a coherent movement. The doctrine advocated for the reorganization of society along industrial and scientific lines, with productive labor replacing hereditary privilege as the basis of social standing.

In Balzac's *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country*, Saint-Simonism appears as one of the competing ideologies circulating in the post-Revolutionary intellectual marketplace, alongside Republicanism, [[fourierism|Fourierism]], and other reform movements.

> The Globe was originally the organ of the doctrines of Saint-Simon.
>
> (002-030)

## Core Principles

The Saint-Simonian system rested on several key [[concept-index|ideas]]:

1. **Industrial reorganization**: [[society|Society]] should be organized around productive labor rather than inherited status. "To each according to his capacity, to each capacity according to its works" became the movement's famous slogan.

2. **The elevation of the *industriel*** class: Engineers, bankers, manufacturers, and artists — those who produced tangible value — should replace the old aristocratic and clerical elites.

3. **A new spiritual authority**: The Saint-Simonians envisioned a secular priesthood of scientists and artists who would provide moral and intellectual guidance, replacing the Catholic Church's traditional role.

4. **The emancipation of women**: The movement advocated for female equality, symbolized by the search for a "Female Messiah" who would complete the Saint-Simonian couple.

> Talking about the free woman and Saint-Simonism, and antagonism, and Fourierism.
>
> (002-064)

## Balzac's Treatment

Balzac's attitude toward Saint-Simonism in *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* is characteristically ironic. The movement appears not as a serious political program but as one more commodity in [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s portfolio — bundled alongside insurance policies and children's magazines.

> He went every morning to be Saint-Simonized at the Globe office.
>
> (002-032)

The verb "Saint-Simonized" — used casually in connection with [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s morning routine — captures Balzac's satirical perspective. The commercial traveler absorbs ideology the way he might take coffee, as part of his professional equipment rather than a matter of conviction. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s engagement with Saint-Simonism is performative: he needs enough familiarity with the doctrine to sell subscriptions to *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]*, but his real allegiance is to the sale itself.

The local notables in [[provincial-life|the provinces]] view Saint-Simonism with hostility:

> The doctrines of Saint-Simon were held particularly odious.
>
> (002-084)

This reaction reflects the genuine anxiety that Saint-Simonian [[concept-index|ideas]] provoked in conservative provincial [[tours|France]] — particularly the movement's critique of property rights and traditional family structures.

## Connections in the Novel

The Saint-Simonian thread connects several elements of the novel:

- **[[le-globe|Le Globe]]**: Originally a liberal newspaper, *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]* became the official organ of Saint-Simonism in 1830. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] sells subscriptions to it.

- **[[le-mouvement|Le Mouvement]]**: The Republican rival to *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]*, representing the competing political tendency that Saint-Simonism opposed.

- **[[felix-gaudissart|Félix Gaudissart]]**: The commercial traveler whose ideological promiscuity — he handles Royalist, Republican, and Saint-Simonian publications with equal enthusiasm — embodies Balzac's theme of commerce dissolving political conviction.

> The fathers of the Saint-Simonian doctrine.
>
> (002-190)

## Historical Significance

Saint-Simonism had a brief but intense period of influence in early 1830s [[tours|France]] before collapsing into internal schisms and state persecution. Its legacy, however, proved durable: Saint-Simonian [[concept-index|ideas]] influenced the development of French socialism, industrial banking (the Crédit Mobilier was founded by former Saint-Simonians), and the technocratic tradition in French political thought. The movement's emphasis on infrastructure investment — canals, railways, banking networks — anticipated the economic modernization of the Second Empire.

Balzac captures the movement at its peak of visibility, when Saint-Simonian preachers in distinctive blue tunics were a familiar sight in [[paris|Paris]] and *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]* circulated the doctrine to a growing readership. His satirical treatment reflects both the movement's genuine cultural prominence and the novelist's skepticism toward systematic ideologies.
