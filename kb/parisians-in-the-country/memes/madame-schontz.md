# Madame Schontz

A [[paris|Parisian]] lorette — the knowing, pragmatic woman of the pleasure class — who serves as [[etienne-lousteau]]'s mistress and unlikely ally in his matrimonial ambitions. Unlike the sentimental heroines of provincial romance, Madame Schontz actively promotes her lover's marriage to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]], recognizing with the clarity of a practiced calculator that the economics of love in [[paris|Paris]] reward cooperation, not jealousy.

## Identity and Appearance

Madame Schontz is a member of the [[paris|Parisian]] demi-monde, the class of [[lorette|lorettes]] who inhabited the ambiguous social space between respectability and prostitution in 1830s [[tours|France]]. The narrator describes her as "a pretty enough woman to justify her position in the world" (005-846), a blunt economic assessment that treats beauty as a form of capital — a recurring Balzacian theme. She belongs to the world of [[florine|Florine]]s and Schontzes, women who "exerted their power over a very limited circle" (005-1018) yet exercised real influence within that sphere.

Her lodgings, her possessions, and her acquaintance place her within the middling ranks of the [[lorette|kept woman]]. She can lend "some plate" when [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] needs to make an im[[newspaper-press|press]]ion (005-947), and she moves easily among the notary's wife [[madame-cardot]]'s circle (005-879), suggesting a social position carefully maintained through strategic connections.

## Character and Behavior

Madame Schontz is defined by pragmatic intelligence rather than romantic [[love|passion]]. She "really loved him for himself" (005-857), the narrator insists, but her love is of a distinctly worldly variety — clear-eyed, calculating, and free of the destructive jealousy that Balzac's male narrators typically attribute to women. When she picks up a letter from [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], her first observation is not emotional but practical: "Carriage paid! It must have come from [[sancerre|Sancerre]]" (005-861). In that single exclamation, Balzac captures an entire worldview — the lorette who reads postmarks as financial data.

She "took a real interest in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s affairs" (005-866), a phrase that in Balzac's vocabulary suggests not sentimental attachment but strategic engagement. Her encouragement of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s marriage to the wealthy provincial is an act of clear-eyed self-interest dressed as generosity. A married lover with a rich wife can maintain his mistress more reliably than a struggling bachelor journalist — this is the arithmetic of the [[paris|Parisian]] demi-monde, and Schontz performs it instinctively.

## Social Relations

Madame Schontz's social world intersects with the respectable bourgeoisie through [[madame-cardot]], the notary's wife, with whom she discusses [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s prospects (005-879). This connection is significant — it demonstrates that the barrier between the demi-monde and the bourgeoisie was permeable in practice, whatever the official morality of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] might dictate.

Her relationship with [[etienne-lousteau]] is the central axis of her existence but not, in any conventional sense, a romance. She is simultaneously his mistress, his confidante, and his business adviser — a triple role that speaks to the functional flexibility of the lorette position. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] departs for [[sancerre|Sancerre]], he is found "smoking a cigar and thinking of Madame Schontz" (005-845), a tableau that suggests comfort rather than [[love|passion]], habit rather than hunger.

Her connection to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]] is that of shadow to substance. Schontz knows of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] through [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s letters and schemes; [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] remains largely unaware of Schontz's existence. This asymmetry of knowledge is characteristic of Balzac's triangular relationships, in which the [[paris|Parisian]] party invariably possesses the superior intelligence.

## Role in the Plot

Madame Schontz functions as a knowing, worldly counterpoint to provincial innocence. While [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]] pursues romantic ideals through her affair with [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], Schontz calculates practical outcomes. Her approval of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s marriage scheme helps normalize what might otherwise appear as cynical fortune-hunting — if even the mistress approves, the narrator implies, the arrangement must possess a certain worldly logic.

She provides comic relief and serves as a confidante for [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s schemes. Her reappearance at the story's end — "And a mistress," said Madame Schontz, smiling. "Here you are, more than ever in my toils" (005-986) — underscores the persistence of [[paris|Parisian]] entanglements. Even after the provincial conquest, the [[paris|Parisian]] mistress remains, her smile suggesting that she has understood the game better than anyone from the beginning.

The scene in which she identifies [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s letter by its paper and style — "A woman's letter! The paper, the style..." (005-859) — is a small masterpiece of social observation. In that moment, Schontz performs a forensic reading worthy of any detective, deducing the identity and intentions of her rival from material evidence alone.

## Narrative Assessment

> "Carriage paid! It must have come from Sancerre" (005-861) — the narrator allows Schontz to reveal her entire worldview in a single practical observation, treating the postmark as a document of social geography.

> "A woman's letter! The paper, the style..." (005-859) — Schontz as literary critic, reading the material signs of female authorship with professional precision.

The narrator treats Schontz with amused respect rather than moral condemnation. She is no mere obstacle to romance but a clear-eyed participant in the social economy of desire — one of Balzac's gallery of women who understand that love in [[paris|Paris]] is a form of commerce and conduct themselves accordingly.

## Historical and Literary Context

The lorette was a recognized social type in 19th-century [[paris|Paris]], named after the Church of Notre-Dame-de-[[lorette|Lorette]] in the neighborhood where many such women lived. Immortalized in Balzac's *La Comedie humaine* and later analyzed by contemporaries such as Alexandre Parent-Duchatelet, the lorette occupied an intermediate position between the respectable woman and the registered prostitute — a creation, as Balzac recognized, of the city's unequal economic structures and its expanding market for pleasure.

Schontz belongs to a gallery of Balzacian courtesans — including Coralie, [[florine|Florine]], and Esther Gobseck — who exercise surprising agency within their constrained circumstances. Her pragmatic encouragement of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s marriage anticipates the "arrangement" marriages of the later 19th century, in which husband and mistress, wife and lover, settled into stable quadrants of mutual accommodation. In her clear-sighted acceptance of the economic basis of all human relations, Schontz embodies one of Balzac's deepest convictions: that sentiment is never entirely separable from self-interest.
