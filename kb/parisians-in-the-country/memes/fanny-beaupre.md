# Fanny Beaupré

Fanny Beaupré is a [[paris|Parisian]] actress and courtesan who becomes [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]]'s companion during the final, deteriorating phase of his relationship with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]], and remains with him through the period of his deepest poverty after [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s departure. Previously the mistress of the député [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]], Fanny represents the last in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s sequence of lorette attachments — a trajectory that traces his descent from youthful [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]nism into middle-aged destitution.

## Identity and Appearance

Fanny Beaupré is identified as an actress and courtesan, part of the network of [[lorette|lorettes]] that populates the theatrical and demi-monde circles of [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[paris|Paris]]. Balzac provides no extended physical description of Fanny, though her profession as an actress implies a certain standard of beauty and performative skill. She is noted as having been the mistress of "old [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]], the député who lived so long with Fanny Beaupré" (005-877), establishing her as a woman of some standing in the demi-monde — [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] being a figure of considerable wealth and political influence. By the time she becomes [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s companion, she is described as a woman whose "salary was pledged to pay her [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s" and who had "nothing left to pawn" (005-1261), indicating the financial precarity that characterized even established [[lorette|lorettes]] when attached to declining men. Her connection to [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] [[place-index|places]] her within the dense web of relationships linking the courtesan class to the [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie: [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] is the brother-in-law of [[madame-cardot|Cardot]], whose daughter Félicie [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] nearly marries.

## Character and Behavior

Fanny Beaupré's character is sketched through her function in the plot rather than through direct psychological portraiture. She appears first as the object of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s infidelity to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]: on the day of Mid-Lent 1840, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], "believing her [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to be ill, had engaged himself for that evening to Fanny Beaupré" (005-1107). When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], disguised in fancy dress, goes to spy on him at the ball, "the journalist, warned by a friend, had behaved so as to deceive the poor woman, only too ready to be deceived" (005-1107). This episode establishes Fanny as the beneficiary of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s habitual deception — the woman he turns to when he believes himself free of domestic surveillance. By 1843, after [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has returned to her husband and re-entered fashionable society, Fanny has become [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s established partner: he is described as "Fanny Beaupré's attaché" (005-1260) and is "living as a husband with Fanny Beaupré" (005-1253). The term "attaché" carries a sardonic charge, suggesting that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] has become the dependent appendage of a woman who, as an actress-courtesan, ought by social convention to be dependent on him. Her financial desperation mirrors and compounds his own: "Fanny Beaupré had nothing left to pawn, and her salary was pledged to pay her [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s" (005-1261), completing the picture of mutual impoverishment.

## Social Relations

Fanny Beaupré's social position is defined by her successive connections to men of diminishing status. Her past as the mistress of [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] — a wealthy député and member of the judicial nobility — [[place-index|places]] her in the upper tier of the demi-monde (005-877). Her present as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s companion represents a significant step down: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is a journalist of fading [[honor|reputation]], chronically in[[usury-and-debt|debt]]ed, and by 1843 reduced to "the sharpest poverty of all — poverty unconfessed" (005-1260). Fanny's relationships connect her to the extended [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]-[[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] family network: [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] married "little Daddy [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]'s eldest daughter" (005-878), linking Fanny indirectly to the [[felicie-cardot|Félicie Cardot]] marriage plot. She occupies the same world as the other [[lorette|lorettes]] of the novel — [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]], [[malaga|Malaga]], [[florine|Florine]] — though she is distinguished from them by her longevity in the trade and her attachment to a man whose decline she shares rather than escapes. [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], the loyal attorney-general who serves as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s devoted admirer, is the one who reveals Fanny's role to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]: "He is living as a husband with Fanny Beaupré" (005-1253), a disclosure that "served, however, to cure [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] of her melancholy" (005-1257).

## Role in the Plot

Fanny Beaupré serves two crucial functions in the novel's architecture. First, she is the instrument of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s final disillusionment. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], now the Comtesse de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] and moving in [[society|high society]], still feels occasional pangs of nostalgia for [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]]'s revelation about Fanny breaks the spell: the knowledge that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] has replaced her with another courtesan "cured" [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] and propelled her fully into the "whirl of fashion" (005-1257). Second, Fanny represents the terminus of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s downward trajectory. At the novel's end, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], stripped of his elegant furniture and facing a legal sale of his possessions, is living with Fanny Beaupré in a state of mutual destitution — she with nothing to pawn, he borrowing a hundred francs from [[bixiou|Bixiou]], "the poorest of his friends" (005-1261). Fanny is thus both the cause of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s liberation and the symptom of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s ruin: she is the woman he ends up with when all his other options — the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] marriage, the [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] arrangement — have collapsed.


[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s descent into shared poverty with Fanny Beaupré marks the terminus of his trajectory:

> In the early spring, which, by some whim of our planets, smiled on Paris in the first week of March in 1843, making the Champs-Élysées green and leafy before Longchamp, Fanny Beaupré's attaché had seen Madame de la Baudraye several times without being seen by her. More than once he was stung to the heart by one of those promptings of jealousy and envy familiar to those who are born and bred provincials, when he beheld his former mistress comfortably ensconced in a handsome carriage, well dressed, with dreamy eyes, and his two little boys, one at each window. He accused himself with all the more virulence because he was waging war with the sharpest poverty of all—poverty unconfessed.
>
> (005-1260)

## Narrative Assessment

The narrative treats Fanny Beaupré without the vivid individuation it grants to [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] or even [[malaga|Malaga]]; she remains a somewhat shadowy figure, defined more by her structural position than by her personality. This relative anonymity is itself significant: by the time [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] reaches Fanny, he has exhausted his capacity for the kind of intense entanglement that characterized his relationships with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] and even with [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]]. Fanny is what remains when [[love|passion]], ambition, and calculation have all run their course — a companion in destitution rather than a conquest or a scheme. The narrator's observation that "[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] dined and breakfasted and smoked as if he were a rich man" (005-1261) even as Fanny had nothing left to pawn underscores the pathos of their situation: both are maintaining the performance of prosperity long after the substance has vanished. The phrase "Fanny Beaupré's attaché" (005-1260) encapsulates the inversion of expected gender roles: the [[lorette|kept woman]] has become the keeper, and the man of letters has become the dependent.

## Historical and Literary Context

Fanny Beaupré belongs to the category of the "aging courtesan" — a type Balzac explored across the Comédie Humaine with a mixture of clinical detachment and com[[love|passion]]ate curiosity. The economic trajectory of women like Fanny was well understood in [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[paris|Paris]]: a courtesan's earning power declined with her youth, and her financial security depended on either accumulating sufficient capital during her peak years or attaching herself permanently to a wealthy protector. Fanny's situation — her salary pledged to [[usury-and-debt|debt]], her possessions pawned — suggests she failed at both strategies. Her successive attachments from [[monsieur-camusot|Camusot]] (wealthy, established) to [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] (impoverished, declining) trace the typical downward mobility of the lorette who outlives her market value. Balzac's treatment of Fanny, however, is notably free of the moralizing that might accompany such a story in a lesser novelist's hands. She is presented simply as a fact of [[paris|Parisian]] life: women like Fanny exist, they attach themselves to men like [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], and the two descend together into the anonymity from which both had briefly emerged.
