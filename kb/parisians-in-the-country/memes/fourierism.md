# Fourierism

The utopian socialist system of Charles Fourier, one of the most imaginative and idiosyncratic doctrines to emerge from the post-Revolutionary intellectual ferment. In Balzac's *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, Fourierism appears only as a passing reference — a single word in a list — yet its presence illuminates both the historical moment and [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s relationship to [[concept-index|ideas]].

## Definition and Origin

Charles Fourier (1772–1837) was a French philosopher who developed one of the most elaborate systems of utopian socialism. Working as a commercial clerk in Lyon, Fourier came to believe that the existing social order systematically frustrated human happiness.

His alternative was the "phalanstery" (*phalanstère*), a self-sufficient cooperative community of exactly 1,620 [[person-index|persons]], in which work would be organized according to natural human "[[love|passion]]s" rather than economic necessity.

Fourier's system was remarkable for its specificity and strangeness. He claimed the Earth would be transformed into a paradise with six moons, that the oceans would turn to lemonade, and that humans would develop new organs including a tail with an eye at its tip.

These speculations made Fourier easy to ridicule, but his critique of commercial society — its waste, fraud, and monotony — was penetrating. His [[concept-index|ideas]] attracted a dedicated following in the 1830s and 1840s, particularly after his death in 1837.

## Balzac's Depiction

Fourierism enters *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* through a single line of dialogue, in which [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] invokes the intellectual currents of the day to im[[newspaper-press|press]] his mistress [[jenny-courand|Jenny Courand]]:

> "Ah, you don't know anything about the free woman and Saint-Simonism, and antagonism, and Fourierism, and criticism, and determined push — well it is — in short, it is ten francs on every subscription, Madame Gaudissart." (002-064)

The sentence is a masterpiece of comic characterization. Fourierism appears in a list of intellectual fashions — [[saint-simonism|Saint-Simonism]], "antagonism," "criticism" — that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] rattles off without understanding. The list culminates in his characteristic reduction of all [[concept-index|ideas]] to commercial value: "in short, it is ten francs on every subscription." For [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], Fourierism is not a vision of human liberation but a product to be sold.

## Social Context

Fourierism was one of several utopian socialist doctrines alongside [[saint-simonism|Saint-Simonism]], Owenism, and the Christian socialism of Lamennais that competed for the allegiance of the French intellectual public in the 1830s. Unlike [[saint-simonism|Saint-Simonism]], which briefly achieved wide circulation through its control of *[[le-globe|Le Globe]]*, Fourierism remained more marginal during the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. Its real moment of influence would come in the 1840s, when Fourierist [[concept-index|ideas]] inspired experimental communities in the United States, including Brook Farm in Massachusetts.

## In the Novels

Fourierism's brief appearance contributes to the satire of ideological consumption. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] treats Fourier's elaborate system as exactly equivalent to Saint-Simon's industrial reorganization, the Republicans' political program, and the Insurance Company's actuarial tables — all are "articles" to be "got up" and sold. Balzac's narrator describes [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] as one who "knows only the names of men and [[place-index|places]], only the surface of things" (002-004) — and Fourierism, in [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s mouth, is reduced to exactly that superficiality.
