# Madame Cardot

The wife of a [[paris|Paris]] notary and mother seeking a suitable husband for her daughter. Her pragmatic approach to marriage and social climbing made her both an obstacle and an opportunity in [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]]'s financial calculations.

## Identity and Appearance

Madame Cardot belonged to the respectable [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie — the wife of a notary, a profession that in Balzac's world represented solid middle-class wealth and social connections. [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] described her as someone in whose eyes one could "read a look of property" [005-880], suggesting both her mercenary nature and her [[ambition|social ambition]]s.

## Character and Behavior

A calculating matriarch, Madame Cardot "looked upon her son-in-law as her own property" [005-897] and was determined to place her daughter advantageously. Her interest in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] was entirely practical: a journalist with connections could be shaped into an acceptable husband.

She was described as "dreadful" by those who encountered her determination [005-932], and her visit to [[rue-des-martyrs|Lousteau's apartment]] caused him considerable anxiety. Yet she was also shrewd: when [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s true situation became clear, "the notary relies on your honor" [005-984] became the polite fiction that ended the negotiation.

## Social Relations

Her social network included [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] (the courtesan who served as an intermediary), her husband the notary, and the broader circle of [[paris|Parisian]] bourgeoisie seeking advantageous marriages for their children. She "had ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ed a wish to see the famous man" — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] — before committing to any arrangement [005-903].

## Role in the Plot

Madame Cardot represents the marriage market in its most explicit form. Her pursuit of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] as a potential son-in-law — and his escape from the arrangement — parallels the novel's central plot of marriage as a financial transaction. She embodies the [[paris|Parisian]] version of [[ambition|social ambition]] that contrasts with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]]'s provincial romanticism.

## Narrative Assessment

> The dreadful Madame Cardot got into her hackney-coach again, having failed in her errand [005-932]

The narrator treats her as a comic figure — the pushy bourgeoise — but also acknowledges the legitimacy of her concerns within the social system Balzac anatomizes.
