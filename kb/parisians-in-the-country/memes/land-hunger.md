# Land Hunger

The obsessive [[love|passion]] for land acquisition — *la faim de la terre* — that Balzac identifies as a defining drive of the provincial bourgeoisie. In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the concept is embodied with almost clinical precision in the character of [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]], whose acquisition of the [[anzy|estate of Anzy]] represents the triumph of a [[madness|monomania]] that excludes all other human concerns, including marriage, family, and social life.

## Definition and Origin

Land hunger is a concept that Balzac develops across *La Comédie Humaine* as part of his larger theory of human motivation. In the Balzacian anthropology, every human being is driven by a dominant [[love|passion]] — a *monomanie* — that organizes all their energies and renders them incapable of attending to any interest outside its scope. In the case of the provincial bourgeois, the dominant [[love|passion]] is frequently the acquisition of land.

The phenomenon had deep roots in French social history. Land ownership was the traditional basis of social status, and the [[french-revolution-reign-of-terror|French Revolution]] had only intensified its symbolic importance by abolishing feudal privileges while preserving — indeed, democratizing — property rights. For the post-Revolutionary bourgeoisie, land represented not merely economic value but social legitimacy: the acquisition of an estate, particularly one with a historic château, was a way of purchasing the aristocratic standing that birth had denied.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac introduces the concept in a passage that is at once analytical and sardonic:

> Those who are familiar with the manias of country folks will recognize in him the land-hunger which becomes such a consuming passion to the exclusion of every other; a sort of avarice displayed in the sight of the sun, which often leads to ruin by a want of balance between the interest on mortgages and the products of the soil. (005-056)

Several elements of this passage repay attention. First, the diagnosis of land hunger as a "mania" — a pathological fixation akin to the collector's obsession or the gambler's compulsion. Second, the phrase "to the exclusion of every other" — land hunger does not coexist with other interests; it consumes them. Third, the observation that this [[love|passion]] "often leads to ruin" — the land-hungry man overextends himself, burdening his acquisitions with mortgages that the land itself cannot service. The [[love|passion]] for land, like all Balzacian [[madness|monomania]]s, is self-destructive.

The phrase "avarice displayed in the sight of the sun" is particularly striking. Avarice, traditionally, is associated with the miser who hoards gold in darkness — a secret, shameful vice. Land hunger, by contrast, is avarice made visible, avarice that parades its object under the open sky. The land-hungry man does not hide his wealth; he displays it, because the display is the point — the estate is a trophy as much as an investment.

## The Man Who Embodied It

[[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]] is the novel's definitive study of the type. A "sallow and almost diaphanous creature" (005-078), physically negligible to the point of caricature, he compensates for his bodily insignificance through the relentless acquisition of property. His biography is a chronicle of land purchases:

> From 1802 to 1815 young La Baudraye added several plots to his vineyards, and devoted himself to the culture of the vine. (005-022)

But the real triumph comes later:

> By paying four hundred thousand francs down, and binding himself to further instalments, which compelled him to live for six years on the air as it came, to use his own expression, he was able to purchase the estate of Anzy on the banks of the Loire, about two leagues above Sancerre, and its magnificent castle built by Philibert de l'Orme, the admiration of every connoisseur, and for five centuries the property of the Uxelles family. (005-055)

The acquisition of [[anzy|Anzy]] is the masterpiece of la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s life — a historic château, built by a celebrated Renaissance architect, long held by a noble family, now possessed by a man whose own name, "thrust in" as a substitute for the extinct [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]s of the Crusades, is itself a forgery. The irony is Balzacian in its completeness: the land-hungry man acquires the shell of nobility while remaining, in his person, as ignoble as ever.

The cost of this triumph is paid by his wife. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] finds herself "reduced to unconfessed penuriousness till 1835" (005-055), her husband's capital entirely absorbed by the land that is his true [[love|passion]]. The marriage is a financial convenience, a means of accumulating more property; the wife is an ornament useful for securing social acceptance but irrelevant to the real project of acquisition.

## In the Novels

Land hunger drives the plot of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* in subtle but decisive ways. [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]'s indifference to his wife's literary ambitions — he tolerates her [[antiquarianism|collecting]] because "they gave him an appearance of sacrificing a few crowns to his wife's taste" while "his land mania allowed him to think of nothing but the [[anzy|estate of Anzy]]" (005-082) — creates the emotional vacuum that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] fills with her salon and, eventually, her affair with [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]].

When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s adultery is discovered, la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s response is governed entirely by considerations of property. He invokes the entail he has created as his supreme achievement — "Have I not created an entail?" (005-1090) — and his forgiveness of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is conditional on her cooperation in the project of accumulating and preserving land. The novel's resolution, in which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is reintegrated into [[society|provincial society]] and her husband continues his acquisitions, suggests that land hunger is the one [[love|passion]] in the novel that achieves its objects without tragic penalty.
