# Rue des Martyrs

The Rue des Martyrs, between the Church of Notre-Dame de [[lorette|Lorette]] and the Rue du Faubourg-Montmartre in the 9th arrondissement, is where the journalist [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]] occupies "pretty ground-floor rooms with a garden, and splendidly furnished." (PN 2218) This apartment is the principal [[paris|Parisian]] stage for the second half of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. (PN 2350–2364)

## Geography / Location

The Rue des Martyrs runs up the slope of Montmartre from the Rue du Faubourg-Montmartre toward the Butte. The Church of Notre-Dame de [[lorette|Lorette]], completed in 1836, stands at the foot of the street and gives its name to the *[[lorette|Lorette]]s* of the neighborhood.

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s mistress [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] lives "in the Rue Fléchier, a stone's throw from [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]," within the same [[lorette|Lorette]] quarter. (PN 2230) The apartment is a ground-floor dwelling with a "scrap of garden" looking out on "an evergreen lawn as big as a dinner-table." (PN 2220) The rent is twelve hundred francs a year, the rooms "splendidly furnished" on credit. (PN 2218)

## Description

Lousteau's apartment embodies the [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]n elegance and chronic impecuniosity of the [[paris|Parisian]] literary man of the 1830s. The rooms bear "the stamp of fashion" — a superficial elegance concealing "extreme poverty." [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], on arrival, discovers "none of the necessaries of life." (PN 2429)

The journalist earns seven to eight hundred francs a month but can "no more find a hundred francs on his chimneypiece now... than he could in 1822." (PN 2218) The months of January, April, July, and October are his "indigent months" — the rent and porter's account clean him out.

The tiny garden provides a retreat where [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], "tired by the incessant vicissitudes of a literary life," would sit with his confidants Nathan and [[bixiou|Bixiou]]. (PN 2220)

## Historical Significance

The Rue des Martyrs and the surrounding [[lorette|Lorette]] quarter represent a specific moment in [[paris|Parisian]] social [[place-index|geography]] of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. The proximity of Lousteau's apartment to the Rue Fléchier, and the easy passage between [[bohemian-life|literary bohemia]] and the world of the *[[lorette|Lorette]]s*, define the moral landscape in which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s provincial illusions are broken.

Balzac's detailed accounting of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s finances — the rent, the upholsterer's credit, the quarterly cycle of penury and splurge — constitutes a document of the material conditions of [[paris|Parisian]] literary life in the 1830s. (PN 2218–2232)

## In the Novel

The Rue des Martyrs apartment is the setting for the novel's most concentrated dramatic sequence. In January 1837, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] arrives unannounced from [[anzy|Anzy]] with her luggage and the news that [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is to be a father. Simultaneously, [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]], mother of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s betrothed Félicie, is inspecting the apartment. (PN 2350–2364)

The confrontation is catastrophic. [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]], seeing [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] embrace [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] in the mirror, declares "If my daughter were to die of it, she should never be the wife of such a man as you," and departs. (PN 2402–2404) [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s marriage to an heiress is destroyed in an instant.

That evening, [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] summons [[bixiou|Bixiou]] — "the man who, in all [[paris|Paris]], most delighted in such practical jokes" — to stage a theatrical intervention. [[bixiou|Bixiou]] delivers a brutal monologue on [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s ruined prospects while [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], concealed in the bedroom, hears everything. (PN 2463–2467)

The apartment is also where [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] gives birth to a son, attended by [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] and Duriau. (PN 2687) The birth triggers the novel's final farce: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s grotesque birth announcements, sup[[newspaper-press|press]]ed by [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]]. (PN 2691–2701)
