# Anna Grossetête

A school friend and confidante of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]], who later marries into the [[parisian-society|Parisian aristocracy]] as the Baronne de Fontaine, becoming a mirror of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s own thwarted ambitions.

## Identity and Appearance

Anna Grossetête was a fellow pupil of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah Piédefer]] at the Demoiselles [[mademoiselle-chamarolles|Chamarolles]]' school in [[bourges|Bourges]], where the two formed an intimate friendship [005-060]. Thanks to a fine fortune, Anna married the third son of the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]], entering the ranks of the [[paris|Parisian]] nobility as the Baronne de Fontaine [005-061]. This advantageous match placed her in the highest circles of [[paris|Paris]] society, a position that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] herself had once aspired to but could never attain.

## Character and Behavior

Anna served as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s lifeline to the fashionable world. In the early years of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s marriage, she maintained an active correspondence with Anna, who helped her stay current with [[paris|Parisian]] fashions and the latest developments of luxury, enabling [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] to avoid becoming "careless or the victim of bad taste" [005-060].

The friendship was founded on shared literary and romantic sensibilities — as young women, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] and Anna together "sketched" a poetical ideal of the superior man [005-110], a shared fantasy that shaped [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s later disappointment with provincial life.

## Social Relations

Anna is the daughter of a wealthy family who attended [[mademoiselle-chamarolles|Mademoiselle Chamarolles]]' school alongside [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] [005-060]. Her advantageous marriage to the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]]'s third son elevated her to the [[parisian-society|Parisian aristocracy]] [005-061]. The friendship between the two women frays over time: when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] travels to [[paris|Paris]] as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s mistress and attends a first-night performance at the theatre, Anna's box is adjacent to hers. The two intimate friends "did not even bow; neither chose to acknowledge the other" [005-1022], a silent testament to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s fall from respectability.

## Role in the Plot

Anna Grossetête functions as a foil to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]. Both women began with similar gifts of intelligence and beauty, educated at the same school. Anna's fortune allowed her to marry into [[parisian-society|Parisian aristocracy]], while [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], lacking wealth, was compelled to accept the frail and calculating [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Milaud de la Baudraye]]. The parallel trajectories highlight the arbitrary role of fortune in determining a provincial woman's fate. Anna's final appearance — seated in the adjacent theatre box, refusing to acknowledge her former friend — crystallizes [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s social ostracism [005-1022].

## Narrative Assessment

## Historical and Literary Context

Anna Grossetête represents a recurring Balzacian type: the provincial woman who, through wealth or marriage, successfully makes the transition to [[society|Parisian society]]. Unlike [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], who attempts the same ascent through literary ambition and an illicit liaison, Anna's path is legitimized by fortune and marriage — a commentary on the limited avenues available to women in post-Napoleonic [[tours|France]].
