# Monsieur Gorju

Monsieur Gorju serves as the mayor of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] during the period of the novel. Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his family — particularly his wife and his daughter Euphémie — participate in the social world centered on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s salon and the visits of illustrious outsiders to the town.

## Identity and Appearance

Monsieur Gorju holds the office of mayor of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] (005-493), placing him among the principal civic dignitaries of the small provincial town. Under the Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], the office of mayor in a commune like [[sancerre|Sancerre]] conferred considerable local standing, making the Gorju family part of the town's elite. His daughter, Mademoiselle Euphémie Gorju, is described as "the owner of a fairly good fortune" (005-701), indicating that the Gorjus belong to the prosperous provincial bourgeoisie.


> “He is certainly a great physician,” said the Présidente, coming back to Madame de Clagny, Madame Popinot-Chandier, and Madame Gorju, the Mayor’s wife.
>
> (005-493)

## Character and Behavior

As a character who never takes the stage directly, Gorju's personality must be inferred from his family's conduct. His wife, Madame Gorju, is depicted as a timorous figure who approaches [[madame-de-clagny|Madame de Clagny]] "timidly" (005-533), suggesting a family unaccustomed to the intellectual audacity of the [[paris|Parisian]] visitors who frequent [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s circle. The family's interest in securing a medical opinion from [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] for their daughter (005-701) positions them as practical provincials concerned with marriage and health — the standard preoccupations of Balzac's provincial bourgeoisie.

## Social Relations

As mayor, Gorju presides over the municipal hierarchy of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], placing him alongside other local notables: [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] the tax collector, [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]], and the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] the curé. His wife, Madame Gorju, circulates among the wives of these dignitaries, appearing in company with "[[madame-de-clagny|Madame de Clagny]], [[madame-popinot-chandier|Madame Popinot-Chandier]], and Madame Gorju, the Mayor's wife" (005-493). His daughter Euphémie Gorju attracts the professional attention of [[horace-bianchon|Doctor Bianchon]], who observes her "figure" with clinical concern (005-703) — a detail that [[place-index|places]] the Gorju family within the medical gaze of the [[paris|Parisian]] visitor, emblematic of the encounter between provincial domesticity and metropolitan expertise.

## Role in the Plot

The Gorju family functions collectively as part of the provincial backdrop against which the drama of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] unfolds. Madame Gorju's timid question — "Do you understand in the least?" (005-533) — captures the bewilderment of [[society|provincial society]] confronted with the intellectual pretensions and literary conversation of the [[paris|Parisian]] visitors. The family's appearance in the narrative serves to illustrate the social gulf between [[sancerre|Sancerre]]'s local elite and the cosmopolitan world represented by [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]].

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac does not grant Monsieur Gorju direct narrative attention; he is present only through the reflected light of his wife and daughter. This is characteristic of the author's treatment of minor provincial officials — necessary furniture of the social scene, but unworthy of the extended psychological portraiture reserved for figures of greater complexity or dramatic consequence.

## Historical and Literary Context

The figure of the provincial mayor is a staple of Balzac's fictional uni[[poetry|verse]], from the grasping Monsieur Grandet of Saumur to the ambitious functionaries of *The Civil Service* (*Les Employés*). Under the municipal system established by the Revolution and modified under Napoleon and the Restoration, the mayor was appointed by the central government rather than elected, making the position a mark of regime loyalty and local influence. Gorju, as an off-stage figure, represents the anonymous mass of provincial notables who populate the background of *La Comédie Humaine* — types rather than individuals, their presence necessary to establish the social density of Balzac's world.
