# Vouvray

[[margaritis|Margaritis]], in one of his lucid intervals, delivers an expert's appreciation of the local wine — a speech that captures both the [[vanity|pride]] and the commercial cunning of the Vouvray vigneron:

> "The only fault of the Vouvray wine, monsieur, is that it cannot be used as an ordinaire, as a table wine. It is too generous, too strong; and it is sold in Paris as Madeira after being doctored with brandy. Our wine is so rich that many of the Paris merchants, when the French crop is insufficient for Holland and Belgium, buy our wine to mix with the wine grown about Paris, and so manufacture a Bordeaux wine."
>
> (002-156)

The irony is double: the lunatic's wine-lore is perfectly accurate — Vouvray was indeed used to fortify inferior wines — and [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]], who has come to sell the [[eloquence|rhetoric]] of [[civilization|progress]], finds himself being sold a [[eloquence|rhetoric]] of terroir that is, in its way, no less sophisticated than his own.


A rich and populous wine-producing district near [[tours|Tours]] in the [[loire|Loire Valley]], serving as the primary setting for [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]'s commercial expedition in the first story of *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country*.

## Location and Geography

Vouvray is situated near [[tours|Tours]], in the historical [[touraine|province of Touraine]], within the [[loire|Loire Valley]] region of central [[tours|France]]. The district lies along the [[loire|Loire River]], forming part of the rich wine-growing countryside that Balzac depicts as both prosperous and provincial (002-079).

## Historical Background

The region of Vouvray belongs to [[touraine|Touraine]], a historical French [[provincial-life|province]] known since the medieval period for its vineyards and châteaux. By the early nineteenth century — the period of Balzac's narrative — the area had developed a [[honor|reputation]] for its white wines, which were traded throughout [[tours|France]]. The district's identity was defined by its viticulture, with vineyards passing through generations of local families (002-105).

## In the Novel

Balzac presents Vouvray as a wealthy but insular community whose residents possess a distinctly provincial character. The "whole borough of Vouvray" is portrayed as tightly knit, where news travels quickly and outsiders are viewed with a mixture of curiosity and suspicion (002-292). The local population includes "twenty well-to-do houses" whose purses [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] hopes to open (002-105), as well as a cast of "merry wives" who form the social fabric of the town (002-108).

The district is characterized as "rich and populous" (002-079), suggesting a community of substance rather than the poverty sometimes associated with provincial [[tours|France]]. Balzac emphasizes the local [[vanity|pride]] in Vouvray wine and the established social order that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]], the [[paris|Parisian]] commercial traveler, attempts to penetrate.

## Economy and Society

Wine production is the central economic activity of Vouvray. The local wine is praised as being capable of great longevity — "when old enough, it is a potion to bring a dead man to life" (002-178). The district's prosperity rests on its vineyards, with the wine trade forming the backbone of the local economy. The "Head of Vouvray" serves as a metaphorical and literal meeting place for the community (002-153) (002-158).

Socially, Vouvray is home to a distinct type: the "indigenous wag" — a local wit whose humor and skepticism define the community's response to outsiders (002-084). The prime figure of this type is [[margaritis|Margaritis]], "the life and soul of the district" (002-085), whose madness takes the form of an elaborate pretense that ultimately defeats [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s commercial ambitions.

## Role in the Plot

Vouvray functions as the stage upon which the central conflict of "[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]" unfolds. It is here that the [[paris|Parisian]] commercial traveler encounters resistance not from economic conditions but from the distinctive provincial character of its inhabitants. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] "put up at [[tours|Tours]], and went to Vouvray" expecting to conquer the market (002-079), only to be outmaneuvered by the local wit [[margaritis|Margaritis]].

The town becomes the site of [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s humiliation: the entire community mobilizes against him, with a dyer declaring "you shall fight at Vouvray" (002-290), and the borough stirring with anticipation of the confrontation (002-292). Vouvray thus embodies the broader theme of the collection: the collision between [[paris|Parisian]] commercial modernity and the entrenched ways of provincial French life.
