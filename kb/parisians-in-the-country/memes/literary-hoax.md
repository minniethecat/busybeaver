# Literary Hoax

The literary hoax — a fabricated author, a false biography, a text attributed to an invented person — was a feature of 19th-century literary culture that Balzac exploited to brilliant effect in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s creation of "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]," the melancholy Spanish poet who supposedly died young in [[le-morvan|the Morvan]] wilderness, is simultaneously a strategy of concealment, a gesture of ambition, and the occasion for one of the novel's most sustained social comedies.

## Definition and Origin

The literary hoax or mystification has a long tradition, from the Ossian poems of James Macpherson to Prosper Mérimée's *La Guzla* (1827), a collection of supposed Illyrian folk ballads that deceived even Pushkin. In the French context, pseudonymous and anonymous publication was particularly important for women writers, who faced both social disapproval and critical condescension. Balzac captures the provincial ferment that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s hoax produced:

> This strange production caused a great ferment in the departments of the Allier, the Nièvre, and the Cher, proud to possess a poet capable of rivalry with the glories of Paris. "Paquita la Sevillane," by Jan Diaz, was published in the *Écho du Morvan*, a review which for eighteen months maintained its existence in spite of provincial indifference. Some knowing persons at Nevers declared that Jan Diaz was making fun of the new school, just then bringing out its eccentric verse, full of vitality and imagery, and of brilliant effects produced by defying the Muse under pretext of adapting German, English, and Romanesque mannerisms.
>
> (005-128)

[[pseudonym|Pseudonym]]ous and anonymous publication was particularly important for women writers, who faced both social disapproval and critical condescension. [[george-sand|George Sand]] ([[george-sand|Aurore Dupin]]) was the most famous example, but the practice was widespread. The hoax went further than the pseudonym: it created a complete fictional identity, often with a tragic biography designed to capture the public's sympathy.

## Balzac's Depiction

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s "Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne," published under the name [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] in the *Écho du [[le-morvan|Morvan]]*, is a poem of "gloomy vigor" running to "about six hundred lines" that "alarmed the woman who found herself admired by three departments, under the black cloak of the anonymous" (005-060). The poem is autobiographical in ways that would be scandalous if the authorship were known: "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] shuddered with shame at having made 'copy' of some of her woes" (005-060).

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]] and [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s devoted admirer, takes the hoax to its logical conclusion. He collects "the works of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] in a small volume printed by Desroziers at [[moulins|Moulins]]" and writes "a little notice of the author, too early snatched from the world of letters" (005-065). The biography is a masterpiece of invention: "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], born at [[bourges|Bourges]] in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner" — complete with "the names of the professors at the [[bourges|Bourges]] College" and "his deceased schoolfellows, such as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]], and other famous natives of the [[provincial-life|province]]" (005-065). The poet was said to have "died near [[chateau-chinon|Château-Chinon]], unknown to all, even to the woman he had loved" (005-065). Balzac describes this as a "practical joke" (005-065) — capital so long as the secret holds, but one that would "fall rather flat if subsequently the poet stands confessed."

The hoax succeeds brilliantly, at least for a time: "Nathan was taken in as well as several of his fellow-countrymen of [[berry|Le Berry]], and wrote an article on the [[genius-concept|great man]], in which he credited him with all the fine qualities we discover in those who are dead and buried" (005-066). The second poem, "The Mass-Oak," a legend of the [[nivernais|Nivernais]], was published to "remove any evil im[[newspaper-press|press]]ion, in case any unforeseen chance should betray her name" (005-063).

## Social Context

The Jan Diaz hoax must be understood in the context of female authorship in the 1830s. After the [[july-revolution-1830|Revolution of 1830]], "very strange doctrines were proclaimed as to the part women should play in society" (005-064). [[george-sand|George Sand]]'s celebrity had "elicited the fact that [[tours|France]] has a perfectly enormous number of superior women" (005-009). But writing was still a transgressive act for a provincial wife. The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]] warned [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]: "Write no more... You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet" (005-062). The pseudonym was armor — not only against scandal but against the Abbé's prophecy.

## In the Novel

The [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] episode functions on multiple levels. It is a study in provincial literary ambition and its absurdities: "Two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty were sold — about fifty in each department. This average of tender and poetic souls in three departments of [[tours|France]]..." (005-065). It is a comedy of errors: [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], "warned by his fellow-schoolfellows, who could not remember [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]], waited for information from [[sancerre|Sancerre]], and learned that [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]] was a pseudonym assumed by a woman" (005-067). And it is a personal tragedy: M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]] "alone knew the secret source of 'Paquita la [[seville|Sevilla]]ne'" (005-072) — the poem was a confession of his wife's sexual frustration, and his knowledge of this fact gave him a weapon of "implacable" revenge (005-071).
