# Superior Woman (Femme Supérieure)

The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] (*femme supérieure*) is a social type that Balzac explicitly names, anatomizes, and satirizes in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* — the intelligent, cultivated woman whose very superiority becomes her prison when she is condemned to live in [[provincial-life|the provinces]].

## Definition and Origin

Balzac introduces the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] with a Latin tag that frames her as both exceptional and absurd. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] is presented as "the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] of the borough, *dux femina fasti*" — the woman who leads the parade (005-010). The phrase situates the type within the classical tradition of the exceptional woman who rules over men, but Balzac immediately undercuts the dignity of the epithet by revealing that the "plan" hatched in her brain serves "personal interest." The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], in Balzac's anatomy, is never entirely separable from the vanity and performance that create her.

The type is also explicitly linked to the literary celebrity of [[george-sand|George Sand]], whose success "elicited the fact that [[tours|France]] has a perfectly enormous number of superior women" (005-012). Balzac treats *[[sandism|Sandism]]* — "this [[sandism|leprosy of sentimentality]]" — as both a genuine cultural phenomenon and a kind of mass delusion: "the woman attacked by it bases her assumption of superiority on feelings scorned; she is a [[bluestocking|bluestocking of sentiment]]" (005-012). The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] of [[provincial-life|the provinces]] is, in part, a provincial imitation of a [[paris|Parisian]] original.

> In point of fact, this plan, of very doubtful outcome, was hatched in the brain of the Superior Woman of the borough, *dux femina fasti*, but with a view to personal interest. This idea was so widely rooted in this lady's past life, and so entirely comprehended her future prospects, that it can scarcely be understood without some sketch of her antecedent career.
>
> (005-010)

Before the label "[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" became fixed, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was known by a cruder nickname: "the [[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]" (005-066). This earlier designation, with its classical pretension and geographical specificity, captures the mockery that surrounded provincial female intellect. The shift from "[[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]" to "[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" marks the success of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s campaign to be taken seriously — a campaign that depends entirely on the admiration of the men who surround her.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac's treatment of the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] is a study in the social construction of [[honor|reputation]]. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] becomes a [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] not because she possesses any extraordinary genius but because four provincial men — the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]], [[vicomte-de-chargeboeuf|Vicomte de Chargeboeuf]], [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], and [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] — "were the first to go into ecstasies over [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s cultivation, good taste, and refinement, and pronounced her a woman of most superior mind" (005-065). The mechanism is comically transparent: the men flatter [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] performs for them, and the performance confirms their flattery.

The women of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] see through the charade. They "said to each other, '[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] must laugh at us behind our back'" and "ended by denying a superiority — after all, merely comparative! — which emphasized their ignorance, and did not forgive it" (005-066). [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s social isolation is the price of her role: "Where the whole population is hunchbacked, a straight shape is the monstrosity; [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was regarded as monstrous and dangerous, and she found herself in a desert" (005-066).

The label becomes self-reinforcing. When [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] explains that "You are too superior a woman to be liked by other women" (005-068), and [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] adds that "Women cannot forgive so much superiority" (005-070), the flattery becomes the explanation for social failure, and the social failure becomes proof of superiority. Balzac traces how the phrase passes from the admirers to the husband: "Men said to [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]: 'You who have such a [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] for a wife are very fortunate' — And at last he himself would say: 'I who have a [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] for a wife, am very fortunate'" [005-072, 005-073]. [[madame-piedefier|Madame Piédefer]], [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s mother, completes the circuit: "My daughter, who is a very [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], was writing yesterday to Madame de Fontaine" (005-074). Balzac's verdict is lapidary: "Those who know the world — [[tours|France]], [[paris|Paris]] — know how true it is that many celebrities are thus created" (005-075).

The psychological cost of playing the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] is profound. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] "soon began to listen to herself, enjoyed haranguing her audience, and at last regarded her friends as the chorus in a tragedy, there only to give her her cues. In fact, she had a very fine collection of phrases and [[concept-index|ideas]], derived either from books or by assimilating the opinions of her companions, and thus became a sort of mechanical instrument, going off on a round of phrases as soon as some chance remark released the spring" (005-080). The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], created by admiration, is destroyed by it — reduced to "a mechanical instrument" performing for her captive audience.

## The Degradation of the Role

Balzac shows how the role of [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] degrades the woman who plays it. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s admirers, tired of keeping "their minds up to the level of the 'high range of discussion,'" secretly long for escape. They deploy the old priest as a decoy: "The [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]]'s rubber made another pleasing variety on the monologues of the oracle" (005-085). The "three rivals, tired of keeping their minds up to the level" would "turn with ingratiating hints to the old priest" (005-085), who "lent himself very readily to the little trick" (005-087). The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]], oblivious, continues to perform for an audience that has slipped away to the card table.

The role also distorts [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s self-presentation. "[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]], accustomed to take the stage, acquired an indefinable theatrical and domineering manner, the air of a prima donna coming forward on the boards, of which ironical smiles would soon have cured her in [[paris|the capital]]" (005-117). When she finally meets [[paris|Parisian]]s — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] and [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] — the "terrible awakening" comes "like the fall of an avalanche from a mountain. In one day she was crushed by a frightful comparison" (005-118).

Even late in the novel, after her affair, disillusionment, and return to her husband, the label clings to her. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] acts "with superiority of the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" as she escorts [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] to [[cosne|Cosne]] (005-833), and after her return to wealth she is "consumed by melancholy, which she concealed with the determination of a really superior woman" (005-1229). At the novel's close, she is mockingly referred to as "the disenchanted '[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]'" (005-1299) — the quotation marks signaling that the label has become an epitaph.

> Though consumed by melancholy, which she concealed with the determination of a really superior woman, Dinah was charming, witty, and above all, young again in her court mourning.
>
> (005-1229)

## Social Context

The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] is a product of the unique social conditions of post-Revolutionary [[tours|France]]. In a country where "all [[paris|Paris]] is the Court, and all the country is the town" (005-108), the provincial woman is cut off from the standards and correctives of [[paris|the capital]]. She has "no standard of comparison" (005-113) and thus "fell into the snares that surround the provincial woman" (005-113). The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] is, paradoxically, created by the very provincial conditions she despises — she is what happens when intelligence and ambition are denied a metropolitan stage.

The type also reflects the gender politics of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] can rule over a small circle of provincial admirers, but she cannot escape the fundamental constraints of her sex: she is defined by her husband, dependent on his money, and judged by standards she did not create. Her superiority is, in the end, merely performative — "her apparent superiority was unreal, while her unseen superiority was genuine. This whimsical medley is commoner than [[person-index|people]] think" (005-106).

## In the Novels

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] is the definitive [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] in Balzac's fiction, but the type resonates across *La Comédie Humaine*. The mechanism that creates her — provincial admiration, female [[jealousy|envy]], male flattery, and self-deception — is Balzac's template for understanding how social [[honor|reputation]] is manufactured in the absence of authentic achievement. The [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] is both a satirical portrait and a sympathetic one: Balzac anatomizes her vanity but also registers her genuine suffering, her "unseen superiority" that circumstances never allow to flourish.
