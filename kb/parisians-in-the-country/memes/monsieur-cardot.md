# Monsieur Cardot

A [[paris|Paris]] notary — a solid, respectable figure of the professional bourgeoisie — who sought a husband of at least thirty years of age for his daughter. His brief entanglement in [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s matrimonial schemes reveals the transactional nature of [[paris|Parisian]] marriage arrangements.

## Identity and Appearance

Monsieur Cardot was a notary by profession, placing him firmly in the upper reaches of the [[paris|Parisian]] middle class. Notaries in 19th-century [[tours|France]] handled property transfers, marriage contracts, and wills — they were the gatekeepers of bourgeois wealth and propriety. His wife, [[madame-cardot|Madame Cardot]], conducted the active search for a son-in-law, but [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] himself "hurries matters on, urges forward" when it came to settling his daughter [005-898].

## Role in the Plot

When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s financial difficulties mounted, [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] proposed him as a potential match for the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] daughter. [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] "wants a man of thirty at least" [005-877], and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] fit the description. The scheme collapsed when [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s true circumstances became known, and "the notary relies on your honor" [005-984] became the face-saving conclusion.

[[madame-cardot|Cardot]] and his wife represent the marriage market in its institutional form — the notary whose office processed the contracts that turned romantic attachments into property arrangements.
