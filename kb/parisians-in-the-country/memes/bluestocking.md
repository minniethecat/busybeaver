# Bluestocking

The pejorative term for a woman who cultivates intellect, learning, or literary ambition — traditionally a figure of satire and social unease. Balzac engages with the bluestocking type throughout his work, and in *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country* he offers an original variation: the "bluestocking of sentiment," a Sandist woman of feeling who is, he assures us, "rather less of a bore" than the intellectual original.

## Definition and Origin

The term "bluestocking" derives from the Blue Stockings [[society|Society]], an informal [[literary-circle|literary salon]] hosted in mid-eighteenth-century London by Elizabeth Montagu and other cultivated women. The name originated from the blue worsted stockings worn by the botanist Benjamin Stillingfleet, who attended the gatherings in informal dress — and was applied, initially without malice, to the society itself. Over time, however, "bluestocking" (and its French equivalent, *bas-bleu*) acquired a derogatory edge, denoting a woman whose intellectual pretensions exceeded her actual [[genius-concept|talent]] and violated the norms of feminine modesty.

In [[tours|France]], the type had a distinguished literary pedigree. Molière's *Les Femmes savantes* (1672) had established the comic template: the learned woman as domestic tyrant, substituting pedantry for affection and alienating her husband through intellectual vanity. The revolutionary period and the early nineteenth century produced real-life bluestockings of considerable achievement — Germaine de Staël most prominently — but the satirical type remained available for anyone who wished to police the boundaries of female intellectual life.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac's treatment of the bluestocking in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is characteristically double. In his analysis of [[sandism|Sandism]], he introduces a taxonomic distinction that is implicitly a defense of the type he is about to embody:

> She is a bluestocking of sentiment; and she is rather less of a bore, love to some extent neutralizing literature. (005-012)

The distinction is ingenious. The traditional bluestocking bores through intellect; the Sandist bluestocking, by grounding her superiority in feeling rather than learning, at least offers the entertainment of emotional drama. "[[love|Love]] to some extent neutralizing literature" — the phrase suggests that the presence of genuine erotic feeling redeems, or partly redeems, the pretension inherent in female literary [[ambition|aspiration]].

But Balzac's irony cuts both ways. The phrase "bluestocking of sentiment" is itself a diminishment — a way of saying that even in the domain of feeling, the ambitious woman is performing a role, constructing a self, rather than simply being. The bluestocking, whether intellectual or sentimental, is always suspected of inauthenticity.

Later in the novel, the term appears in a [[paris|Parisian]] context. Describing the circle that sustains [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], Balzac observes:

> Bluestockings, beginners in every walk of life, actresses at the outset or the close of a career, publishers and authors, all make much of these writers of the ready pen. (005-838)

Here "bluestockings" appears in a list of types who sustain the literary economy — the women who host salons, flatter journalists, and provide the social infrastructure of [[parisian-literary-life|literary Paris]]. The bluestocking is not merely an object of satire but a functional element of the cultural system.

## Social Context

The French nineteenth century was deeply ambivalent about female intellect. On one hand, the salon tradition — from Madame Geoffrin in the eighteenth century to Madame Récamier in the early nineteenth — demonstrated that women could exercise significant cultural influence through conversation and patronage. On the other, the professionalization of literary life, the expansion of the [[newspaper-press|press]], and the ideology of separate spheres combined to make female authorship a dubious enterprise. The woman who published was suspected of self-display; the woman who cultivated learning was suspected of neglecting her domestic duties.

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s position is defined by these tensions. She is "attainted and convicted of pedantry, because she spoke grammatically" (005-066). Her intelligence, rather than being recognized as a gift, is treated as a social offense — a violation of the provincial code that requires women to be pleasant, not clever. The nickname "[[provincial-muse|Sappho of Saint-Satur]]" (005-066) captures the provincial ambivalence: it acknowledges her literary ambition while situating it in a tradition of female transgression.

## In the Novels

[[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s entire career can be read as a negotiation with the bluestocking label. She adopts the posture of the [[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]] — cultivated, literate, contemptuous of provincial mediocrity — and in doing so invites the very hostility the label encodes. Her retreat into [[literary-hoax|anonymous publication]] (as [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Jan Diaz]]) reflects an awareness that overt female authorship carries social penalties. Her eventual migration to [[paris|Paris]], where she pursues a career as a woman of letters under the protection of [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], represents an attempt to escape the provincial constraints on female intellect — but [[paris|Paris]] presents its own version of the same problem, in which the bluestocking is tolerated only as a component of the literary economy.

Balzac's final judgment is characteristically unresolved. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] embodies the bluestocking's tragic dimension — the woman whose intelligence and ambition exceed the social forms available to her — while remaining, in her provincial incarnation, an object of gentle satire. The "bluestocking of sentiment" is both a diagnosis and a defense, capturing the double bind of the woman who seeks distinction through the cultivation of feeling in a society that demands women to feel without seeking distinction.
