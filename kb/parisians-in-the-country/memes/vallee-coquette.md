# Vallée Coquette

The Vallée Coquette is a picturesque valley in the wine-growing district of [[vouvray|Vouvray]], near [[tours|Tours]] in [[touraine|Touraine]]. It provides the setting for the comic centerpiece of *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* — [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]]'s disastrous encounter with the mad vigneron [[margaritis|Margaritis]]. (PN 171)

## Geography / Location

The Vallée Coquette lies within [[vouvray|Vouvray]], on the right bank of the [[loire|Loire]] a few kilometers east of [[tours|Tours]]. The district is described as "a rich and populous district" approached by the river quay from [[tours|Tours]]. (PN 163)

The landscape is characterized by vineyard terraces rising in steps above the [[loire|Loire]] valley. The softness of the atmosphere and simplicity of manners define [[touraine|Touraine]], which Balzac calls "the true Abbey of Thelema, so much praised in Gargantua's book." (PN 167) The area's wines are celebrated; the 1819 vintage of [[clos-margaritis|Clos Margaritis]] was so fine that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] mistakes it for "genuine Madeira." (PN 315)

## Description

Balzac presents the valleys of [[vouvray|Vouvray]] as "the happy valleys" of [[touraine|Touraine]] — a landscape of extraordinary charm shaped by centuries of viticulture. The vineyards climb the slopes in terraces, linked by paths that lead "from terrace to terrace, from vine to vine." (PN 509)

The region's beauty is inseparable from its wine culture: the vines define the topography, and the cellars and [[newspaper-press|press]]es of each clos punctuate the valley floor. The Tourangeau character is "an ardent, artistic, poetical, and luxurious nature, but prompt to forget its first impulse." (PN 165) It is the "land of good stories and jokes played between neighbors." (PN 173)

## Historical Significance

[[vouvray|Vouvray]] has been a wine-producing region since the Middle Ages. The fictional [[clos-margaritis|Clos Margaritis]] represents the small independent vigneron holdings of the region. (PN 311) The Vallée Coquette is a literary invention of Balzac, its name evoking the flirtatious, winding beauty of the [[touraine|Touraine]] countryside.

## In the Novel

The Vallée Coquette hosts the most memorable episode of *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*. After being tricked by [[monsieur-vernier|Monsieur Vernier]] into visiting the madman [[margaritis|Margaritis]], [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] follows him into the vineyard terraces. (PN 509)

The scene is watched with delight from the window by [[madame-vernier|Madame Vernier]] and company, who see "the two men from the window gesticulating, haranguing, standing still, and going on again, talking vehemently." (PN 511) The mad vigneron plies [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] with heady wine and subjects him to a lunatic parody of negotiation — claiming to have "pacified Calabria when Murat was King." (PN 237)

The encounter culminates in a double swindle: [[margaritis|Margaritis]] subscribes to the *[[journal-des-enfants|Children's Magazine]]* while [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] buys two casks of [[clos-margaritis|Clos Margaritis]] wine. The valley witnesses the ironic reversal of the great commercial traveler's usual triumph. (PN 517–521)
