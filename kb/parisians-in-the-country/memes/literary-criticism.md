# Literary Criticism

Balzac's sophisticated theory distinguishing two kinds of criticism — superficial wit and rigorous science. The "five literary senses": Invention, Style, Thought, Learning, and Feeling (005-651).

## Context

Balzac inserts a theoretical passage in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* distinguishing two kinds of criticism. The first is "the ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of judgments formed at random in a more or less witty way." The second is "a science" requiring "a thorough comprehension of each work, a lucid insight into the tendencies of the age." The critic is "a magistrate of [[concept-index|ideas]], the censor of his time (005-1067)."


> “For my part, I am alarmed at the progress of public knowledge in the matter of literature,” said Bianchon.
>
> (005-651)

## In the Novel

Claude Vignon and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] are contrasted as representing these two types of criticism. Vignon represents the rigorous, scientific approach; [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] the facile, witty approach that reduces criticism to a commodity. The "five literary senses" — Invention, Style, Thought, Learning, and Feeling — are enumerated as the critic's evaluative criteria.
