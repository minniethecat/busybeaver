# Bourgeoisie

The rising middle class of [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]] — the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]]s, the financiers, the wine merchants — whose values and money increasingly dominate Balzac's social landscape.

## Context in Balzac's Fiction

This concept appears in Balzac's "[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country" — comprising *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]* and *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* — two stories set in the [[loire|Loire Valley]] and [[paris|Paris]] during the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy (1830–1848)]].

Balzac anatomizes the material basis of the journalist's existence with a metaphor of penal servitude:

> In this century, especially since the triumph of the bourgeoisie—the commonplace, money-saving citizen—who takes good care not to imitate Francis I or Louis XIV—to live by the pen is a form of penal servitude to which a galley-slave would prefer death.
>
> (005-837)

## In the Novels

Balzac uses this concept to illuminate the social, psychological, and historical realities of post-Revolutionary and post-Napoleonic [[tours|France]], where traditional hierarchies have collapsed and new forms of power — commercial, journalistic, political — are emerging.
