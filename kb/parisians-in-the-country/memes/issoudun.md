# Issoudun

A town in the department of the Indre, in the historic [[berry|province of Berry]], that figures in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* as the site of an auction of the Rouget family furniture — the sale that launched [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s celebrated collection of [[antiquarianism|antiquities]].

## Geography and Location

Issoudun lies on the River Theols in the Indre department, roughly thirty-five kilometers southwest of [[bourges|Bourges]] and about eighty kilometers from [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. It is situated in the southern part of the [[berry|Berry]] region, in the heart of what was once the [[provincial-life|province]]'s agricultural and wine-growing heartland. By the early nineteenth century, Issoudun was a substantial market town, known within Balzac's fictional uni[[poetry|verse]] as the setting for his earlier novel *La Rabouilleuse* (also known as *A Bachelor's Establishment*), in which the Rouget family plays a central role.

## Historical Background

Issoudun has a rich medieval history, dominated in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries by a powerful collegiate church and a royal castle. During the Hundred Years' War, the town was contested between French and English forces. By Balzac's time, it had settled into the character of a provincial market town — notable within his fiction for the Rouget family, whose fortunes and furniture become key plot elements across multiple novels of the *Comedie Humaine*.

## The Rouget Furniture

The Rougets were a family of some wealth and eccentricity in Issoudun, their story fully recounted in *La Rabouilleuse*. The dispersal of their possessions — including a significant collection of furniture — is briefly mentioned in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* as a formative event in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s development as a collector. Early in her marriage, she "had become possessed of the Rougets' furniture, sold at Issoudun early in 1824" [005-075]. This purchase was made possible by M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s early generosity toward his young wife: "he allowed her to take the five precious acres now wasted in pleasure grounds round [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], and paid, almost with generosity, the seven or eight thousand francs required by [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] for improvements in the house, enabling her to buy the furniture at the Rougets' sale at Issoudun" [005-109].

The Rougets' furniture became the nucleus of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s collection. She "purchased some very good things at [[nivernais|Nivernais]] and the Haute-[[loire|Loire]]" [005-075] to supplement it, and by the end of five or six years "the anteroom, the dining-room, the two drawing-rooms, and the boudoir which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] had arranged on the ground floor of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], every spot even to the staircase, were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments" [005-076]. The collection spanned centuries: medieval, Renaissance, Louis XIV, and Pompadour, including works attributed to Jean Goujon, Germain Pilon, Boulle, Van Huysum, and Bernard de Palissy.

## In the Novel

The Issoudun auction serves several narrative functions. First, it marks the beginning of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s transformation from a young bride into the "[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] — the collection of [[antiquarianism|antiquities]] being both a genuine [[love|passion]] and a strategic performance of cultivated taste. Second, the Rouget furniture connects *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* to the wider Balzacian uni[[poetry|verse]], the Rougets being major [[person-index|characters]] in *La Rabouilleuse* — a typical Balzacian device that enriches each individual novel through cross-reference to others.

Third, the purchase itself reveals the dynamics of the [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] marriage. M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s apparent generosity in funding the purchases masked his deeper calculation: "These fancies found favor in the eyes of [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]]; they gave him an appearance of sacrificing a few crowns to his wife's taste. In point of fact, his [[land-hunger|land mania]] allowed him to think of nothing but the [[anzy|estate of Anzy]]" [005-075]. The Issoudun furniture thus becomes an instrument of marital management — a relatively inexpensive way to keep a young wife occupied while the husband pursued his real [[love|passion]] for land.

## Economic and Cultural Significance

The mention of the Issoudun sale is also a document of the broader phenomenon of antique [[antiquarianism|collecting]] in Restoration [[tours|France]]. The 1820s were a period when medieval and Renaissance artifacts, devalued by the Revolution and its aftermath, began to be appreciated and collected by a new generation — a trend that Balzac, an avid collector himself, observed at first hand. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s purchases at Issoudun anticipate the broader Gothic and Renaissance revivals that would characterize French taste in the 1830s and 1840s.
