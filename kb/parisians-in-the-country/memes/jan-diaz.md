## Identity and Biography

Jan Diaz was a pseudonym assumed by [[dinah-de-la-baudraye]], under which she published her poetry and gained literary recognition across three French departments. (005-222) The fictional biography, devised by Dinah and Monsieur de Clagny, presented Jan Diaz as born at Bourges in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner. (005-192)

The fabricated "memoir" of Jan Diaz was sufficiently detailed to deceive future compilers of literary biographies. It included the names of his professors at the Bourges College, his deceased schoolfellows — among them [[etienne-lousteau]] and Bianchon, other famous natives of the province — and described him as a dreamy, melancholy boy with a precocious bent towards poetry. (005-192)

## Literary Works

Under the name Jan Diaz, Dinah published several works that caused a ferment in the departments of the Allier, the Nièvre, and the Cher. (005-128)

**"Paquita la Sevillane"** — A poem of about six hundred lines, published in the *[[echo-du-morvan]]*, telling the story of a Spanish girl from Seville who follows a Norman soldier to Rouen, only to descend into vice after his departure for the Russian campaign. (005-128) The poem was written with a gloomy vigor that alarmed even its author, once she reflected on how much of her own suffering she had exposed. (005-154)

**"Le Chêne de la Messe" (The Mass-Oak)** — A charming poem in two cantos based on a Nivernais legend in which a priest halts a battle between the people of Nevers and Saint-Saulge by saying Mass under an oak in the forest of Faye. (005-186) Though "immeasurably superior" to "Paquita la Sevillane," this poem was far less admired. (005-188)

Other works attributed to the Diaz persona included an elegy called "Tristesse" (Melancholy), written during his supposed school days; three sonnets; a description of the Cathedral and the House of Jacques Coeur at [[bourges]]; and an unfinished tale called *Carola*, said to be the work he was engaged on at the time of his death. (005-192)

## The Literary Hoax

The Jan Diaz persona was an elaborate literary hoax. Monsieur de Clagny took advantage of the post-1830 enthusiasm for provincial literary talent to collect the works of Jan Diaz in a small volume printed by [[desroziers-publisher]] at [[moulins]]. (005-191) He wrote a biographical notice "amusing to those who were in the secret." (005-191) Two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty were sold — about fifty in each of the three departments. (005-191)

The hoax extended to inventing the poet's last hours, "full of misery and despair," near [[chateau-chinon]], where he supposedly died "unknown to all, even to the woman he had loved." (005-192)

> Such practical jokes, capital so long as the author remains unknown, fall rather flat if subsequently the poet stands confessed. (005-191)

## Revelation

The truth was discovered when [[etienne-lousteau]], warned by his fellow-schoolfellows who could not remember any Jan Diaz, made inquiries and learned that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym assumed by a woman. (005-195) By this time, Madame de la Baudraye had become the rage in the region around Sancerre, lauded as the future rival of George Sand. (005-200)

When the Parisians — Doctor Bianchon and the journalist Lousteau — heard that the beautiful Madame de la Baudraye was Jan Diaz, they visited her at Anzy. (005-222) Dinah had enjoyed the intoxicating delights of success while dreading exposure, having made "copy" of some of her own woes in the verse. (005-154)
