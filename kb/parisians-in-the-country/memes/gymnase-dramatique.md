# Théâtre du Gymnase-Dramatique

The Théâtre du Gymnase-Dramatique (often called simply the Gymnase) was a [[paris|Paris]] boulevard theatre opened in 1820 on the Boulevard Bonne-Nouvelle. It was one of the most important theatres of the Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] periods, known for its repertoire of comedies, vaudevilles, and melodramas — the popular theatrical forms that competed with the more prestigious state-sponsored theatres. In Balzac's *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country*, the Gymnase appears in both constituent stories, serving as a [[paris|Parisian]] landmark associated with pleasure, spectacle, and the theatricality of urban life.

## Background

The Gymnase was founded under the patronage of the Duchesse de [[berry|Berry]] and originally called the [[theatre-du-gymnase|Théâtre du Gymnase]] de Marie Stuart. After the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]], it was renamed the Théâtre du Gymnase-Dramatique. The theatre seated approximately 1,200 spectators and was distinguished by its elegant architecture and its [[honor|reputation]] for well-mounted productions. Under the direction of M. Poirson (1820–1844), it became a launching pad for many of the period's most successful playwrights, including Eugène Scribe, whose "well-made plays" defined the theatrical aesthetic of the period. Its location on the Boulevard Bonne-Nouvelle placed it at the heart of the boulevard theatre district — the commercial entertainment zone that contrasted with the state-subsidized theatres of the Comédie-Française and the Odéon.

## In the Novel

The Gymnase appears in two distinct roles across the two stories collected in *[[paris|Parisian]]s in the Country*.

### Gaudissart the Great

In the opening scene of *[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart the Great]]*, the Gymnase is the setting for [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s evening with [[jenny-courand|Jenny Courand]]:

> Every truly great man loves to be tyrannized over by some feeble creature, and Jenny was Gaudissart's tyrant; he was seeing her home at eleven o'clock from the Gymnase theatre, where he had taken her in full dress to a private box on the first tier.
>
> (002-021)

The detail is precise: a private box on the first tier is the visible marker of [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s prosperity and his effort to im[[newspaper-press|press]]. The theatre here is a site of courtship and display — [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] performing the role of the gallant [[paris|Parisian]]. Jenny's later demand that [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] furnish her room with the proceeds of his provincial sales links the theatre directly to the commercial plot: the Gymnase box is an investment in the relationship that the provincial "Children" (subscriptions) must fund.

### The Muse of the Department

In the later novel, the Gymnase reappears as the venue where [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s story "La Grande Bretêche" achieved theatrical adaptation:

> Among the stock of narratives he had in store, for every clever man has a fund of anecdotes as Madame de la Baudraye had a collection of phrases, the doctor chose that which is known as "La Grande Bretêche," and is so famous indeed, that it was put on the stage at the Gymnase-Dramatique under the title of *Valentine*.
>
> (005-444)

This reference performs multiple functions. It authenticates [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s story by attaching it to a real [[paris|Paris]] institution; it creates a self-referential loop in which Balzac's fiction references its own adaptations; and it contrasts the Gymnase — the theatre of popular entertainment — with the provincial salon at [[anzy|Anzy]] where the story is being retold. The story that "was then new to the inhabitants of the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]]" (005-444) had already been consumed and forgotten by [[paris|Paris]] audiences, measuring the cultural distance between capital and [[provincial-life|province]] with theatrical precision.
