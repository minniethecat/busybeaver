# Adultery

Adultery in Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is simultaneously a plot mechanism, a philosophical problem, and a narrative occasion — the novel is structured around [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s adulterous relationship with [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], but it also contains one of the most extended theoretical discussions of adultery in 19th-century fiction.

## Definition and Origin

In 19th-century [[tours|France]], adultery was not merely a private moral failing but a public and legal transgression with severe consequences, especially for women. The Napoleonic Code gave husbands extensive powers over wives, and a woman's adultery could result in imprisonment, while a husband's was merely a fine. This legal asymmetry is the invisible architecture behind every Balzacian treatment of the subject.

## Balzac's Depiction

The centerpiece of Balzac's treatment of adultery in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is the after-dinner conversation at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], which functions as both theoretical debate and narrative showcase. The Public Prosecutor, [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], opens the discussion with a bitter attack on [[paris|Parisian]] literature: "for some time past the books you have written, you [[paris|Paris]] authors, your farces, your dramas, all your atrocious literature, turn on adultery" (005-343).

[[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s reply is one of the most famous passages in the novel. He defends adulterous love as the central subject of all great literature: "if you condemn them, you must condemn Homer, whose *Iliad* turns on Helen of Troy; you must condemn Milton's *Paradise Lost*. Eve and her serpent seem to me a pretty little case of symbolical adultery; you must sup[[newspaper-press|press]] the Psalms of David, inspired by the highly adulterous love affairs of that Louis XIV of Judah; you must make a bonfire of *Mithridate*, *le Tartuffe*, *l'École des Femmes*, *Phèdre*, *Andromaque*, *le Mariage de Figaro*, Dante's *Inferno*, Petrarch's Sonnets, all the works of Jean-Jacques Rousseau, the romances of the Middle Ages, the History of [[tours|France]], and of Rome" (005-344). The catalogue is deliberately excessive — and deliberately persuasive. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s argument is that to condemn adultery in literature is to condemn literature itself.

When [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] protests that "Much loss that would be!" (005-345), [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] escalates the argument to theological blasphemy. He argues that Christianity itself is "founded in adultery," since "in the opinion of King Herod, and of Pilate as representing the Roman Empire, Joseph's wife figured as an adulteress" (005-347). The journalist pushes the argument to its logical extreme: "a Buddhist as he smokes his pipe may very well assert that the Christian religion is founded in adultery" (005-351). The discussion is both a display of [[paris|Parisian]] intellectual daring and a seduction strategy — [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] is performing for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], and she is captivated.

> To adultery!—For thus, monsieur, a Buddhist as he smokes his pipe may very well assert that the Christian religion is founded in adultery; as we believe that Muhammad is an impostor; that his Koran is an epitome of the Old Testament and the Gospels; and that God never had the least intention of constituting that camel-driver His Prophet.
>
> (005-351)


## The Competition of Revenge Tales

The theoretical debate gives way to a practical one. [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] observes that "among the ranks of deceived husbands there are some whose attitude is not devoid of energy, men who, at a crisis, can be very dramatic" (005-355), and [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] seizes the opening: "If [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]] had not just ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ed such vehement disapproval of the immorality of stories in which the matrimonial compact is violated, I could tell you of a husband's revenge" (005-358).

What follows is a sequence of four adultery-revenge narratives, each more extreme than the last. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] tells [[charles-nodier|Charles Nodier]]'s story of the [[chevalier-de-beauvoir|Chevalier de Beauvoir]] — a prisoner tricked by a jealous commandant into a fake escape that would have meant a fatal fall from a castle tower [005-363 to 005-388]. [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] follows with the celebrated tale of *La Grande Bretêche*, in which a husband walls up his wife's lover alive in a closet (005-392). [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]] contributes a Spanish tale in which a husband amputates his wife's arm after identifying it by a distinctive mole [005-394 to 005-442]. And [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]], despite his earlier protestations, relates the story of a [[tours|Tours]] woman who murdered her husband, salted his body, and disposed of it piecemeal — keeping the head "in pickle in one of the casks" because "it was too heavy" to throw in the river [005-456 to 005-458].

The four tales form a kind of anthology of [[revenge|marital revenge]], moving from the psychological (the commandant's trap) to the gothic (the walled-up lover) to the surgical (the amputated arm) to the grotesque (the pickled husband). They function simultaneously as entertainment, as warning, and as sublimated ex[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of the sexual tensions among the listeners. The conversation about adultery has become an enactment of the [[love|passion]]s it purports to discuss.

## Social Context

Adultery in Balzac's world is inseparable from the institution of marriage as a property arrangement. Marriages in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* are financial transactions: [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] marries [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]] because he is "an unhoped-for good match" (005-098), and she discovers in him only "the Artic coldness of a provincial miser whenever money was in question" (005-100). Adultery becomes, in this framework, both a rebellion against the commodification of women and a confirmation of it — [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] escapes one form of economic dependence only to enter another with [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]].

The social consequences of adultery are asymmetrical. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s affair becomes public, she has "broken forever with society" (005-1029) — a punishment visited on the woman alone. [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], by contrast, returns to [[paris|Paris]] and his [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]n life without social penalty. The novel's ending, in which [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] returns to her wealthy husband as a countess while [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] remains a struggling journalist, registers the irony: adultery, the great romantic rebellion, ends in the restoration of the very property relations it was meant to escape.

## In the Novels

*The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* is among Balzac's most sustained studies of adultery, but the theme runs through all of *La Comédie Humaine*. The novel's unique contribution is to frame adultery as simultaneously the great subject of literature and the great subject of life — to show that the stories we tell about illicit love are not merely reflections of reality but templates for how we live.
