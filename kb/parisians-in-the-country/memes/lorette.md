# Lorette

The lorette is a distinct social type of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] — the [[paris|Parisian]] kept woman or demi-mondaine whose name derives from the Church of Notre-Dame de Lorette, around which these women lived. Balzac defines the type explicitly in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* and uses it to construct a parallel social world that mirrors and mocks respectable society.

## Definition and Origin

Balzac provides the etymology and sociology of the term in a single, definitive sentence: "Like all those women who get the name in [[paris|Paris]] of *Lorettes*, from the Church of Notre Dame de Lorette, round about which they dwell, she lived in the Rue Fléchier, a stone's throw from [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]" (005-846). The definition is geographical and economic: the lorette is defined by where she lives (the neighborhood around the church in the 9th arrondissement) and by her economic relation to men (she is "kept" rather than married, but maintains a pretense of romantic attachment).

The lorette occupies a specific position in the hierarchy of 19th-century [[paris|Parisian]] women. She is not a common prostitute but a *demi-mondaine* — a woman of the demi-monde, the shadow society that parallels respectable society. She is not a *grande courtisane* of the type immortalized by Dumas fils in *La Dame aux Camélias*; she is more modest, more provisional, more specifically tied to the [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]n literary world of which she is the necessary counterpart.

## Balzac's Depiction

[[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] is the definitive lorette of *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*. She is introduced as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s mistress: "[[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]] was a pretty enough woman to put a very high price on the interest on her beauty, while reserving absolute ownership for [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], the man of her heart" (005-846). The phrase "absolute ownership" is ironic — [[madame-schontz|Schontz]] sells the "interest" on her beauty while retaining [[paris|the capital]] — but it also captures the lorette's paradoxical position: she is simultaneously property and proprietor, dependent and autonomous.

[[madame-schontz|Schontz]] "took a [[vanity|pride]] and delight in teasing her friends by boasting of having a Wit for her lover" (005-846). The lorette values her lover not primarily for his money but for his cultural capital — his status as a writer, a journalist, a "Wit." This is what distinguishes the lorette from the ordinary prostitute: she participates in the symbolic economy of literature and fame, and her relationship with her lover is as much about shared [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]n identity as about financial support.

The lorette's world is a network of similar women. [[madame-schontz|Schontz]] knows [[malaga|Malaga]], the mistress of the notary [[madame-cardot|Cardot]], who provides inside information about the [[madame-cardot|Cardot]] family's marriage schemes. The women share intelligence, compete for protectors, and move between lovers with pragmatic fluidity. When [[madame-schontz|Schontz]] decides to leave [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] for a wealthier protector named Arthur, she announces it with professional calm: "I will be faithful to him and cut [[malaga|Malaga]]'s acquaintance" (005-847). The lorette's relationships with other lorettes are governed by the same calculus of interest that governs her relationships with men.

The lorette's function in the novel is not merely decorative. [[madame-schontz|Schontz]] actively manages [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]]'s life — she proposes his marriage to [[felicie-cardot|Félicie Cardot]], negotiates with the notary, and when the marriage falls through, delivers the news with brutal clarity. She is more practical, more worldly, and in many ways more honest than the respectable [[person-index|characters]] who surround her.

## The Parallel Social World

Balzac constructs the world of the lorettes as a parallel society that mirrors respectable society in every particular. The lorettes have their own [[place-index|geography]] (the quarter around Notre-Dame de Lorette, the Rue Fléchier), their own economy (the exchange of beauty for maintenance), their own social networks ([[madame-schontz|Schontz]], [[malaga|Malaga]], [[florine|Florine]], [[fanny-beaupre|Fanny Beaupré]]), and their own moral code. When [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] says "Oh, we all have a [[florine|Florine]]" (005-845), he is acknowledging that the lorette is as essential to the [[bohemian-life|bohemia]]n writer as the wife is to the bourgeois husband.

The parallel is made explicit when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], the respectable provincial wife turned adulterous mistress, descends into the lorette's world. She disguises herself to attend the Opera balls "where she met courtesans" (005-1097), and [[malaga|Malaga]] cruelly mocks her for wearing nothing but black. The respectable woman who has lost her respectability finds that she has entered a world whose rules she does not understand and whose inhabitants regard her with contempt.

## Social Context

The lorette is a product of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]'s specific social and economic conditions. The expansion of [[paris|Paris]], the growth of the [[newspaper-press|press]] and the literary marketplace, the concentration of wealth in [[paris|the capital]], and the relaxation of aristocratic moral codes all contributed to the emergence of a class of women who lived outside the structures of marriage but within the structures of the market. The neighborhood around Notre-Dame de Lorette, built in the 1820s and 1830s, was part of the new [[paris|Paris]] of speculation and pleasure — a district of modest apartments, small shops, and easy anonymity.

## In the Novels

The lorette appears throughout *La Comédie Humaine*, from Coralie in *Lost Illusions* to Josépha in *Cousin Bette*. Balzac's treatment of the type is characteristically double: he registers the lorette's vulgarity and mercenary calculation, but he also grants her a kind of honesty denied to the respectable women who commit the same sins under the cover of marriage. [[madame-schontz|Madame Schontz]], who openly trades her beauty for maintenance, is less hypocritical than [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]], who disguises the same transaction as love.


> The novelty of the position hid its disastrous side; Dinah regarded reciprocated love as the absolution of her sin; she did not yet look beyond the walls of these rooms.
>
> (005-947)
