# French Intervention in Spain 1823

The French intervention in Spain (April–November 1823) was a military expedition authorized by the Congress of Verona and led by the Duc d'Angoulême, crossing the Pyrenees with approximately 100,000 French troops — the "Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis" — to restore the absolutist rule of King Ferdinand VII, who had been forced to accept a liberal constitution by Spanish revolutionaries in 1820. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the expedition is a minor but significant detail in [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]]'s professional trajectory: the expedition that nearly sent him to Spain before a timely posting to [[sancerre|Sancerre]] changed his life.

## Historical Background

After Napoleon's defeat, Ferdinand VII was restored to the Spanish throne in 1814. He promptly abolished the liberal Constitution of 1812 and re-established absolutist rule. In January 1820, a military revolt led by Colonel Rafael del Riego forced Ferdinand to restore the constitution, inaugurating the *Trienio Liberal* (Liberal Triennium, 1820–1823). The spectacle of a constitutional monarchy on [[tours|France]]'s southern border alarmed the conservative powers of the Holy Alliance. At the Congress of Verona (1822), the European powers authorized [[tours|France]] to intervene and restore Ferdinand's absolute authority.

## Key Events

The French army under the Duc d'Angoulême crossed the Bidassoa River on April 7, 1823. Meeting minimal resistance — the campaign was framed as a restoration of legitimate monarchy rather than a foreign conquest — the French forces advanced rapidly. Madrid fell in May. The liberal government retreated to Cádiz, taking Ferdinand with them as a hostage. The French besieged Cádiz, and the fortified city of Trocadero fell on August 31, 1823 — a victory commemorated in [[paris|Paris]] by the Place du Trocadéro. Ferdinand was liberated in October and immediately began a brutal re[[newspaper-press|press]]ion of liberals that would last a decade.

## Consequences and Impact

The expedition was a triumph for the Restoration monarchy, demonstrating that Bourbon [[tours|France]] could project military power and serve as the enforcer of conservative order in Europe. It also exposed deep divisions in French society: liberals viewed the expedition as a betrayal of constitutional principles; ultraroyalists celebrated it as the vindication of divine-right monarchy. The ease of the campaign contrasted sharply with Napoleon's disastrous Spanish ulcer (1808–1814) and helped rehabilitate the French army's prestige after 1815.

## In the Novel

The Spanish expedition enters Balzac's narrative through a single, almost throwaway line in [[monsieur-gravier|Monsieur Gravier]]'s backstory. [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], the Receiver-General at [[sancerre|Sancerre]], has just told a lurid tale set in Napoleonic-occupied Madrid, drawing on his own experience in Spain. After the story, he reveals:

> At the time of the expedition sent out to restore Ferdinand VII I was appointed to a place in Spain; but, happily for me, I got no further than Tours when I was promised the post of Receiver here at Sancerre.
>
> (005-450)

This brief mention accomplishes several narrative purposes. It establishes [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s connection to Spain — lending credibility to his Madrid story — while also explaining how he came to settle in [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and enter [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s circle. The "happily for me" carries an ironic charge: the expedition that was a triumph for French arms would have been, for [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]] personally, a lesser fate than the provincial post where he became one of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s admirers. The line also dates the action: [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s arrival in [[sancerre|Sancerre]] occurred around 1823, placing the novel's backstory precisely in the Restoration period.

In [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s story-within-the-story, the Spanish setting of French military occupation under Murat (1808) provides a complementary image — the earlier, Napoleonic occupation — creating a diptych of French interventions in Spain that spans the Empire and the Restoration.
