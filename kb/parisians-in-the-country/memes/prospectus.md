# Prospectus

A document — and, in Balzac's extended metaphor, a person — that embodies the fusion of commercial promotion and intellectual production characteristic of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. The [[advertisement|prospectus]] advertises a product, an idea, or a financial scheme, but it also makes a claim on the reader's imagination and credulity.

## Definition and Origin

The [[advertisement|prospectus]] emerged as a distinct genre in the early 19th century alongside the expansion of joint-stock companies, literary publishing, and periodical [[newspaper-press|journalism]]. It was a printed circular that described a forthcoming publication, a new business venture, or a financial offering, combining factual information with persuasive [[eloquence|rhetoric]]. Balzac himself wrote numerous [[advertisement|prospectus]]es for his ill-fated publishing ventures, and his intimate knowledge of the form informs his fictional treatment.

## Balzac's Depiction

Balzac's most original contribution to the concept is the figure of the "living prospectus." [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is described as exactly this: a human being who functions as a walking advertisement. The directors of the Insurance Company recognize "the [[genius-concept|talent]]s of this living prospectus" (002-030) and deploy him accordingly. The [[advertisement|prospectus]] as a genre is also subject to satirical analysis: "the excise has levied a tax on the intellect; it has exactly measured the acreage of advertisements, has assessed the [[advertisement|prospectus]], and weighed thought" (002-025). The state's taxation of the [[advertisement|prospectus]] represents the official recognition — and regulation — of persuasive speech as a taxable commodity.

## Social Context

The [[advertisement|prospectus]] flourished in an era of expanding literacy, growing [[speculation|financial speculation]], and the democratization of investment. It was the primary tool through which joint-stock companies, publishers, and political movements reached a mass audience. The [[advertisement|prospectus]]'s characteristic blend of factual claim and [[eloquence|rhetoric]]al excess made it both essential to modern commerce and inherently suspect — a tension that Balzac exploits throughout his work.

## In the Novel

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] is the living embodiment of the [[advertisement|prospectus]]. His entire being — his dress, his speech, his gestures — functions as an advertisement for the products he represents. Yet Balzac also extends the concept metaphorically: the intellectual commodities produced in [[paris|Paris]] are "roasted with [[advertisement|prospectus]]es, spitted on flattery" (002-028), suggesting that in a commercial society, all discourse tends toward the condition of the [[advertisement|prospectus]].
