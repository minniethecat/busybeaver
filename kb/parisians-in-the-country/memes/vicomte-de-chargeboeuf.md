# Vicomte de Chargeboeuf

The Vicomte de Chargeboeuf served as sous-préfet (sub-prefect) of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and was a prominent figure in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]]'s salon. An aristocratic gentleman of good family, he was one of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s principal admirers and for a time the object of her romantic consideration, before his professional ambitions took him away from the district.

## Identity and Appearance

The Vicomte de Chargeboeuf held the post of sous-préfet in [[sancerre|Sancerre]], representing the state administration in the provincial town. He was "a gentleman of good family, whose appearance and manners" [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] liked (005-110). As a member of the minor provincial aristocracy serving in the prefectoral administration, he embodied the type of the career functionary who moved between postings as his family connections and political fortunes dictated.

## Character and Behavior

Despite [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s appreciation for his appearance and social graces, the Vicomte's character was marked by a fundamental coldness. [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] recognized in him a "cold nature, selfishness, and narrow ambition, never rising above a prefecture and a good marriage" (005-110). These qualities ultimately repelled her, even as she considered him as a potential romantic partner. His character is further illuminated by an earlier episode: "at a word from his family, who were alarmed lest he should be killed for an intrigue, the Vicomte had already deserted a woman he had loved in the town where he previously had been Sous-préfet" (005-110), revealing a man who placed career and family expectations above personal attachments.

## Social Relations

The Vicomte was a regular in [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]]'s drawing-room, where he joined "Messieurs de Chargeboeuf, [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], and de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]]" among the circle of male admirers who gathered around [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] (005-085). Together with [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]], de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]], the [[abbe-duret|Abbé Duret]], and other local notables, he formed part of the intellectual and social [[literary-circle|coterie]] that the "[[provincial-muse|Superior Woman]]" of [[sancerre|Sancerre]] assembled to relieve the tedium of provincial life. As [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s cousin, he occupied a position of familiarity within her social world that distinguished him from her other suitors.

## Role in the Plot

Among [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s admirers, the Vicomte occupied a unique position: when she surveyed her suitors, "she owned to a preference for Monsieur de Chargeboeuf" (005-110). He came closest to her romantic ideal among the provincial men who surrounded her. However, the relationship never materialized — "when [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] saw herself condemned to six years' residence at [[sancerre|Sancerre]] she was on the point of accepting the devotion of Monsieur le Vicomte de Chargeboeuf; but he was appointed to a prefecture and left the district" (005-111). His departure in or before 1829 proved a turning point for [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]: "after the departure of Monsieur de Chargeboeuf, she was excited by the anticipation of a little pleasure; she was expecting the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]]" (005-119). The visit of her old school-friend [[anna-grosset|Anna Grossetête]], now the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]], became a "strangely disastrous" encounter that painfully highlighted [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s provincial condition. Later, "her patience and her resignation alike broken by the departure of the Vicomte de Chargeboeuf, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] took the worthy Abbé's advice to exhale her evil thoughts in [[poetry|verse]]" (005-126), a turn toward poetry that marked her evolution as the "[[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]."

## Narrative Assessment

> When, stirred by the involuntary temptations suggested by the homage she received, she asked herself, "If I had to make a choice, who should it be?" she owned to a preference for Monsieur de Chargeboeuf, a gentleman of good family, whose appearance and manners she liked, but whose cold nature, selfishness, and narrow ambition, never rising above a prefecture and a good marriage, repelled her. (005-110)

The narrator's retrospective assessment also notes his role as a lost source of comfort: "when she had looked for consolation, the consoler, Monsieur de Chargeboeuf had left her" (005-212). This framing presents the Vicomte as yet another in the series of disappointments that shaped [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s early married life — present enough to offer the possibility of escape, but insufficient in character and commitment to provide it.

## Historical and Literary Context

The Vicomte de Chargeboeuf represents a recurring Balzacian type: the provincial functionary of aristocratic origin whose ambitions are calibrated to the narrow world of prefectoral postings and advantageous marriages. The prefectoral system under the Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] created a class of administrators who moved between provincial towns, and Balzac frequently uses such figures to illustrate the contrast between [[paris|Parisian]] and provincial life. The Vicomte's departure — dictated by career advancement — and [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s subsequent turn to poetry exemplify the constraints of provincial existence that Balzac explored throughout *La Comédie Humaine*.
