# Gazette des Tribunaux

The *Gazette des Tribunaux* was a French daily newspaper founded in 1826–1827, devoted to reporting on legal proceedings, court cases, and criminal trials. It was one of the first French publications to specialize in judicial reporting, and its detailed accounts of sensational crimes made it enormously popular across the country. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]], [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]] of [[sancerre|Sancerre]], denounces the *Gazette* as "one of the worst abuses of the Press" — a condemnation that reveals as much about [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]]'s professional anxieties as about the newspaper itself.

## Background

The *Gazette des Tribunaux* was founded during the late Restoration, a period of rapid expansion in the French [[newspaper-press|press]]. Its innovation was to treat court proceedings not merely as legal records but as public spectacle — narratives of crime and punishment that satisfied and stimulated the public's appetite for the sensational. The paper published verbatim transcripts of trials, descriptions of crime scenes, and biographical sketches of accused criminals. Its success inspired imitators and made "police news" (*faits divers*) a staple of French [[newspaper-press|journalism]].

## Role and Function

The *Gazette des Tribunaux* occupied a liminal position between official record and popular entertainment. For legal professionals, it was a useful compendium of case law and judicial practice. For the general public, it was a daily source of crime narratives that transformed the courtroom into a theater of social transgression and punishment. Critics argued that its detailed reporting of crimes served to disseminate the very behaviors it purported to condemn — spreading criminal "techniques" to provincial readers who would otherwise have remained ignorant of them.

## In the Novel

The *Gazette des Tribunaux* appears in a pivotal conversation scene at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]], after the assembled company has been trading lurid stories of adulterous wives and vengeful husbands. When [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Madame de la Baudraye]] asks whether such adventures could occur "now, and in [[tours|France]]," M. de [[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]] launches into an indignant reply:

> "Dear me!" cried Clagny, "of the ten or twelve startling crimes that are annually committed in France, quite half are mixed up with circumstances at least as extraordinary as these, and often outdoing them in romantic details. Indeed, is not this proved by the reports in the *Gazette des Tribunaux* — the Police news — in my opinion, one of the worst abuses of the Press? This newspaper, which was started only in 1826 or '27, was not in existence when I began my professional career, and the facts of the crime I am about to speak of were not known beyond the limits of the department where it was committed."
>
> (005-470)

[[monsieur-de-clagny|Clagny]]'s speech is a complex piece of characterization. As Public Prosecutor, he has professional reason to resent a newspaper that publicizes the crimes he prosecutes, removing them from the controlled sphere of the courtroom into the uncontrolled sphere of public discussion. His denunciation of the *Gazette* as an "abuse" of [[newspaper-press|press]] freedom reflects the anxieties of a legal establishment that preferred crime to remain a local affair — "not known beyond the limits of the department." Yet he immediately proceeds to tell his own sensational crime story (the woman who salted her husband's body and threw it piece by piece into the [[loire|Loire]]), performing exactly the function he condemns in the newspaper. The irony is characteristic of Balzac: [[monsieur-de-clagny|the Public Prosecutor]] and the *Gazette des Tribunaux* are, in effect, in the same business — the narration of sensational transgression for an audience hungry for it.

The *Gazette* thus functions in the novel as both a specific institution and a symbol: of the [[newspaper-press|press]]'s power to dissolve provincial boundaries, of the commercialization of crime as entertainment, and of the hypocrisy of those who consume the very narratives they claim to deplore.
