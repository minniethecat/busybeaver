# Peninsular War

The Peninsular War (1808-1814) was Napoleon's military campaign to conquer Spain and Portugal, which devolved into a brutal guerrilla war and became the Emperor's "Spanish ulcer." In *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the French occupation of Madrid provides the exotic and dangerous setting for [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s dramatic stories, while also explaining the career trajectory of several [[person-index|characters]].

## Historical Background

In 1808, Napoleon forced the abdication of the Spanish Bourbons and installed his brother Joseph as King of Spain. The Spanish population rose in revolt, launching a protracted guerrilla war that tied down hundreds of thousands of French troops. The war was marked by atrocities on both sides, the British intervention under Wellington, and a savage irregular conflict that foreshadowed 20th-century warfare. The French occupation of Madrid under Marshal Murat, Grand Duke of Berg, was a period of lavish imperial display alongside simmering Spanish resentment and violence.

## The French Occupation of Madrid

[[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s story, set during the early days of the occupation, opens with a grand fête: "Some little time after his entry into Madrid, the Grand Duke of Berg invited the magnates of [[paris|the capital]] to an entertainment given to the newly conquered city by the French army. In spite of the splendor of the affair, the Spaniards were not very cheerful; their ladies hardly danced at all." The "imperial magnificence" of Murat's entertainment, illuminated gardens, and forced gaiety cannot conceal the hostility beneath: the French officers discuss "the not too hopeful outlook prognosticated by the conduct of the Spaniards present." (PN: 1416-1418)

## The Surgeon-Major's Ordeal

The centerpiece of [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s tale is the story of Béga, a French surgeon-major, who is kidnapped one night on the streets of Madrid, bundled into a carriage, and forced to deliver a noblewoman's illegitimate child while her husband sleeps in the adjoining room. The episode is a study in the savage codes of Spanish honor under occupation:

- The husband's jealous vigilance is described as "all the vigilance of Spanish jealousy." (PN: 1430)
- The lover threatens Béga with a dagger, and when the infant proves dead, the surgeon narrowly escapes being poisoned via doctored lemonade. (PN: 1462, 1468)
- Béga later mentions to his fellow officers a mole on the woman's arm — and a hidden Spaniard overhears this fatal indiscretion. (PN: 1480)

The revenge is swift and terrible. The Spanish husband appears at Béga's bedside, wakes him, and "took from under his cloak the arm of a woman, freshly amputated, and held it out to Béga, pointing to a mole like that he had so rashly described. 'Is it the same?' he asked." He then stabbed Béga to the heart. (PN: 1506-1508)

## The Amputation and Its Aftermath

The story's coda reveals the fate of the woman whose arm was severed. Years later, at a ball in [[tours|Tours]], [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]] encounters a Spanish grandee and his wife, "whose right arm was perfectly motionless." When [[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]] asks what caused her loss, she replies simply: "I lost it in the war of independence." The ironic understatement — a husband amputating his wife's arm as punishment for infidelity, passed off as a war wound — encapsulates the novel's treatment of the Peninsular War as a theater of extreme [[love|passion]]s where normal civilized restraints were suspended. (PN: 1516-1518)

## In the Novel

[[monsieur-gravier|Gravier]]'s Peninsular War experiences are central to his character. A former "charming ballad-singer" who became Paymaster-General of the forces under the Empire, he "mixed himself up in certain important matters in Spain with generals at that time in opposition," leading to his exile to the provincial receivership at [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. (PN: 815) His Spanish stories — told with relish at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]] — serve to establish his worldly credentials against the [[paris|Parisian]] journalists, even as [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]] dismisses them as implausible. The tale's [[concept-index|themes]] of adultery, jealousy, and violent revenge mirror the novel's underlying tension between [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]], [[etienne-lousteau|Lousteau]], and the watchful [[monsieur-de-clagny|Monsieur de Clagny]].
