# Baron de Fontaine

The third son of the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]] and the husband of [[anna-grosset|Anna Grossetête]], the Baron de Fontaine is a figure who never appears directly in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]* but whose existence exerts a decisive [[newspaper-press|press]]ure on its plot. His marriage to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s school friend — and the life of [[paris|Parisian]] elegance that marriage makes possible — serves as the living measure of everything [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] has been denied.

## Identity and Appearance

The Baron de Fontaine is identified as "the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]]'s third son," a cadet of one of Balzac's recurring noble families, who rose to the position of Director-General under the Minister of Finance (005-120). No physical description of him appears in the novel, for the simple reason that he never sets foot in [[sancerre|Sancerre]] and is never depicted in a scene. His identity in the narrative is entirely positional: he is defined by his title, his administrative rank, and his marriage to a woman of fortune. This absence is itself significant — the Baron is a structural placeholder, the [[paris|Parisian]] husband who exists offstage while his wife's social performance drives the novel's central comparison.

## Character and Behavior

What little can be inferred about the Baron's character comes from two gestures. The first is his marriage itself: "Anna, thanks to a fine fortune, had married the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]]'s third son" (005-120). The phrasing makes clear that the match was financially motivated — the Baron, a younger son without prospects of the principal inheritance, secured his position through a strategic marriage to a wealthy provincial heiress. The second is his decision, after his father's death, to take leave from his duties and escort Anna on a tour of Italy (005-120). This suggests a man who understands the social obligations of his class and who, within the limits of an arranged marriage, performs the role of the attentive husband competently.

## Social Relations

The Baron's marriage to [[anna-grosset|Anna Grossetête]] [[place-index|places]] him at the intersection of provincial wealth and [[parisian-society|Parisian aristocracy]]. His wife maintains a correspondence with [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah de la Baudraye]] that, in the early years of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s marriage, serves as her primary connection to the fashionable world. Through Anna, the Baron is linked — however indirectly — to the [[society|provincial society]] of [[sancerre|Sancerre]]. In [[paris|Paris]], the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] moves in elevated circles: at the theatre she is accompanied by "the [[vandenesse|Comtesse Marie de Vandenesse]]" and receives "some of the most distinguished men of fashion" (005-1020). The Baron's own social network, while unseen, is implied by his wife's connections and his position within the Finance ministry. As a member of the Fontaine family, he belongs to a dynasty that appears across multiple Balzac novels, most notably *Le Bal de Sceaux*, where the [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]]'s rigid social calculus is laid bare.

## Role in the Plot

The Baron de Fontaine's most consequential contribution to the plot occurs entirely through his wife. In 1829, while en route to Italy, Anna stops at [[sancerre|Sancerre]] to visit her old school friend. The effect on [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] is devastating: "Anna stepped out of an elegant traveling chaise loaded with [[paris|Paris]] milliners' boxes, and she had with her a lady's maid, whose airs quite frightened [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]. All the difference between a woman of [[paris|Paris]] and a provincial was at once evident to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s intelligent eye" (005-120). Anna, who had been the less beautiful of the two at school, now appeared "a thousand times handsomer" as the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] — an effect made possible entirely by her husband's position and wealth. The Baron reappears in the narrative through his wife's presence at the theatre first-night years later, when the [[anna-grosset|Baronne de Fontaine]] occupies a box adjacent to [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s and the two former friends refuse to acknowledge each other (005-1020). On both occasions, the Baron is physically absent but socially present — it is his name, his title, his income, and his position that confer upon Anna the standing from which she can look down on her former friend.

## Narrative Assessment

Balzac never judges the Baron de Fontaine directly; the character is too marginal to warrant explicit narrative commentary. But the novel's judgment is delivered through the suffering he enables. The Baron's marriage to Anna is the counterfactual of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s life: what [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] might have achieved had she, like Anna, possessed a "fine fortune" to trade for a [[paris|Parisian]] title. Instead, [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]] was compelled to marry the grotesque [[monsieur-de-la-baudraye|Monsieur de la Baudraye]], and her subsequent humiliation at Anna's hands — "[[anna-grosset|Anna de Fontaine]] had turned her head away in order to avoid seeing her bosom friend" — is, in a sense, the Baron's doing, since it is his name and position that confer upon Anna the power to deliver such a snub. The novel's implicit verdict is that the Baron de Fontaine, for all his administrative eminence, is an instrument of the same social machinery that crushes [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]].

## Historical and Literary Context

The Baron de Fontaine belongs to the Balzacian type of the minor aristocrat-administrator: a figure who combines hereditary status with bureaucratic function in the service of the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]]. His position as Director-General under the Minister of Finance [[place-index|places]] him within the administrative elite that Balzac anatomized across *La Comédie Humaine* — men who managed the machinery of the state while profiting from its operation. Such positions were highly coveted under the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], conferring immense discretionary power over taxation, customs, and public expenditure while offering plentiful opportunities for patronage and self-enrichment.

The Fontaine family itself is one of Balzac's most thoroughly documented noble houses. The [[comte-de-fontaine|Comte de Fontaine]], the Baron's father, appears in *Le Bal de Sceaux* as a man who has made his peace with the post-Revolutionary order and who deploys his children in a calculated campaign of marital alliances designed to restore the family's fortunes. In that novel, the eldest daughter Émilie de Fontaine rejects a series of suitors for insufficient social standing, only to marry a man she later discovers to be a shopkeeper — a bitter lesson in the dangers of aristocratic [[vanity|pride]].

The Baron's own marriage to [[anna-grosset|Anna Grossetête]] follows the family pattern exactly: a younger son with a title but limited means marries a provincial heiress, trading prestige for capital. Unlike his sister Émilie's disastrously romantic matching, the Baron's union is a sober, successful transaction — precisely the sort of alliance his father would have approved.

> In twenty-four hours the friends had exchanged many confidences; and the Parisian, seeing herself so far superior to the phoenix of Mademoiselle Chamarolles' school, showed her provincial friend such kindness, such attentions, while giving her certain explanations, as were so many stabs to Dinah, though she perfectly understood that Anna's advantages all lay on the surface, while her own were forever buried.
>
> (005-120)
 The arrangement is a microcosm of the social history of Restoration and [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]] [[tours|France]], in which the old aristocracy and the new monied classes negotiated a pragmatic coexistence through the institution of marriage. In this light, the Baron de Fontaine is not merely a minor character in a provincial novel but a representative figure in Balzac's comprehensive sociology of post-Revolutionary [[tours|France]].
