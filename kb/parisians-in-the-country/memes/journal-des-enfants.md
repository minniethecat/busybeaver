# Journal des Enfants

The *Journal des Enfants* (Children's Magazine) was a real French periodical for children founded in 1832, which in Balzac's novel serves as [[felix-gaudissart|the commercial traveler]] [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s flagship product — a six-franc annual subscription that he peddles from town to town across the [[loire|Loire]] valley.

## Background

The *Journal des Enfants* was launched during the [[july-monarchy|July Monarchy]], a period of explosive growth in periodical publishing. Balzac's narrator describes the broader economic context: "What has not been done in [[tours|France]] since 1830 to stimulate the zeal, the conceit of the intelligent and [[civilization|progress]]ive masses?" The magazine was part of a wave of intellectual commodification in which advertising, [[advertisement|prospectus]]es, and commercial travelers harvested subscriptions from provincial households — "the fried bacon of advertisement and [[advertisement|prospectus]] by which the departmental mouse is tempted into the editor's trap." (PN: 65, 71)

[[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] himself frames the magazine as filling a genuine gap: "Just think, the little ones have never had a paper of their own." It occupied a niche "something between the sugarplum and the picture-book, the two eternal cravings of childhood." (PN: 111, 157)

## Gaudissart's Sales Strategy

The novel provides a detailed anatomy of the children's-magazine subscription business. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] explains the sales technique to his mistress [[jenny-courand|Jenny Courand]]:

> "I have to push a most capital idea, a magazine that is to be brought out for children. In our walk of life a traveler, when he has worked up a town and got, let us say, ten subscriptions to the Children's Magazine, says I have got ten Children... I get five sous on each subscription. But, Jenny, I shall get thousands of Children." (PN: 99, 107, 111)

The magazine's commercial success rests on a psychological insight about parent-child dynamics: "You show the picture to the mother on the sly, so that the child wants to see; then, of course, the child sees; and he tugs at mamma's skirts till he gets his paper, because 'Daddy has hisn paper.' Mamma's gown cost twenty francs, and she does not want it torn by the brat; the paper costs but six francs, that is cheaper; so the subscription is dragged out." (PN: 157)

The production values are key to the sale: "one number a month, two columns, contributors of the highest literary rank, got up in the best style, good paper, illustrations from drawings by our first artists, genuine India paper proofs, and colors that will not fade." (PN: 158)

## In the Novel

The *Journal des Enfants* is the plot device that drives the novel's opening act. [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s boast of having secured "two thousand Children between [[paris|Paris]] and [[blois|Blois]]" establishes his commercial prowess and fuels his overconfidence entering [[vouvray|Vouvray]], where the madman [[margaritis|Margaritis]] outwits him. (PN: 157)

The magazine's subscription economy creates key plot points:

- **The [[margaritis|Margaritis]] deal**: [[margaritis|Margaritis]] pays seven francs for a subscription — but only after [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]] agrees to buy two casks of wine, a poisoned bargain that makes the "children's paper" subscription the vehicle for [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s humiliation. (PN: 517)

- **The [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]] reconciliation**: After [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s duel with [[monsieur-vernier|Vernier]] over the [[margaritis|Margaritis]] affair, the dyer promises: "Monsieur, we will get you twenty subscribers to the children's paper" — converting the magazine into an instrument of social peace and commercial redemption. (PN: 635)

Throughout, "Children" serves as [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s slang for subscriptions, creating comic misunderstandings ([[jenny-courand|Jenny Courand]]'s indignation at [[felix-gaudissart|Gaudissart]]'s "children") that underline the novel's satire of how commercial language colonizes human relations.
