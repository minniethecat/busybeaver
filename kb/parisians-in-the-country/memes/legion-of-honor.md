# Legion of Honor

The Legion of Honor (*Légion d'honneur*) is the French national order of merit, established by Napoleon Bonaparte on May 19, 1802, to recognize distinguished military and civil service. Unlike the aristocratic orders of the *ancien régime*, the Legion was open to all ranks and classes — a characteristically Napoleonic fusion of meritocratic principle with hierarchical form. In Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, the Legion's red ribbon appears repeatedly as a visible token of social status: it is bought, sold, worn, and envied, functioning as the portable currency of honor in a commercial society.

## Background

Napoleon created the Legion to reconcile the Revolutionary principle of *carrière ouverte aux [[genius-concept|talent]]s* (careers open to [[genius-concept|talent]]) with the human appetite for distinction. Membership was organized in ranks: Chevalier (Knight), Officier (Officer), Commandeur (Commander), Grand Officier (Grand Officer), and Grand Aigle (Grand Eagle, later Grand Cross). The distinctive red ribbon of the Legion, worn in the buttonhole, was instantly recognizable and universally understood as a sign of recognized merit — though the grounds of recognition were infinitely flexible.

## In the Novel

The Legion of Honor appears at two key points in *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, serving different narrative functions. First, it is the sweetener in M. de la [[la-baudraye|Baudraye]]'s Restoration financial settlement:

> "The collectorship at Sancerre is vacant; a certain paymaster-general of the forces has a claim on it, but he has no chance of getting it; you have the chance — and no claim. You will get the place. You will hold it for three months, you will then resign, and Monsieur Gravier will give twenty thousand francs for it. In addition, the Order of the Legion of Honor will be conferred on you."
>
> "Well, that is something," said the winegrower, tempted by the money rather than by the red ribbon.
>
> (005-123)

The exchange is remarkable for its cynical precision. The Legion of Honor is explicitly listed as one item in a commercial transaction: a collectorship plus twenty thousand francs plus the ribbon, in exchange for the cancellation of aristocratic [[usury-and-debt|debt]]s. [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s preference for money over honor — "tempted by the money rather than by the red ribbon" — encapsulates Balzac's vision of the Restoration as a regime in which even honor has a cash price.

The second appearance of the ribbon marks Dr. [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s introduction:

> Horace Bianchon, who wore the ribbon of the Legion of Honor, was fat and burly, as beseems a fashionable physician, with a patriarchal air, his hair thick and long, a prominent brow, the frame of a hard worker, and the calm expression of a philosopher.
>
> (005-430)

[[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]]'s ribbon is presented as a natural attribute of his person — worn, not mentioned — a silent statement of achieved status that contrasts with [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s purchased decoration. The difference between the earned ribbon ([[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]], the Officer) and the traded ribbon ([[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]], eventually rising to Commander by the novel's end) traces the moral arc of the Restoration's honors system.

By the novel's conclusion, the Legion has completed its work as a marker of [[la-baudraye|La Baudraye]]'s ascent:

> Monsieur de la Baudraye, then, a Count, a Peer of France, and Commander of the Legion of Honor, was vain enough to wish to cut a figure with a wife and handsomely appointed house.
>
> (005-710)

The three titles — Count, Peer, Commander — form a trinity of Restoration social achievement, the Legion's red ribbon the most visible and universally recognized of the three.
