# Chevalier de Beauvoir

Charles-Félix-Théodore, Chevalier de Beauvoir, is a minor but vividly drawn character in Balzac's *The [[provincial-muse|Muse of the Department]]*, appearing in an embedded story told by the physician [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] during the evening gathering at the [[anzy|Château d'Anzy]]. His tale — of a loyalist gentleman imprisoned, interrogated, and ultimately aided in escape — serves as a romantic interlude within the novel's social satire, illustrating the persistence of aristocratic codes of [[honor]] and courage even under revolutionary duress.

## Identity and Background

The Chevalier's full name is given as Charles-Félix-Théodore, Chevalier de Beauvoir, and he is identified as "cousin to Madame la Duchesse" (005-371), connecting him to the highest ranks of the French aristocracy. He was a member of the Maillé family, sent by the Chouans — the royalist insurgents of western [[tours|France]] — from Brittany to Saumur during the revolutionary period to establish communications between royalist sympathizers (005-365).

Beauvoir "had studied his part well": he assumed a false identity, "appealed to the family whose name he had borrowed, persisted in his false address, and stood his examination so successfully" (005-365) that he initially evaded detection. This careful preparation reveals a man of intelligence and nerve, capable of the dangerous game of political deception in a time of civil war.

## Imprisonment at l'Escarpe

Beauvoir was subsequently "transferred, in obedience to further orders, to the Castle of l'Escarpe, a name which sufficiently indicates its situation" (005-366). This fortress, "perched on very high rocks" with "precipices on every side" (005-366), was reached only by a narrow path. Its dramatic situation — suggestive of an eagle's eyrie — made it an ideal prison for a man whose escape the authorities were determined to prevent.

Despite his circumstances, Beauvoir's character as "a loyal gentleman" is emphasized. He was "also a very handsome youth" with "attractive features, a dashing air, a pleasing address, and extraordinary strength" — "well made, active, and daring" (005-367). These qualities, which made him a formidable adversary, also made him a sympathetic figure to those who encountered him.

## Imprisonment and Escape

Beauvoir's treatment grew harsher: "the commandant thought himself justified in treating his prisoner with excessive severity" (005-368), and Beauvoir was "eating his heart out, for death alone could set him free" (005-371). The narrative captures the psychological toll of solitary confinement on a man of action and spirit.

His escape was facilitated by a turnkey who, one morning, instead of simply delivering food, lingered and revealed that he had been "paid to help you to get away" (005-374). The jailer warned that any suspicion would result in his own execution, underscoring the mortal stakes of the enterprise. The escape route involved a splayed recess "under the deep cornice that ran round the top of the tower, between the brackets that supported the embrasures" (005-375) — a precarious architectural detail that served as the means of Beauvoir's liberation.

When confronted with the question of his true identity, Beauvoir responded with defiance

> The Chevalier de Beauvoir—I now remember his name—had studied his part well; he appealed to the family whose name he had borrowed, persisted in his false address, and stood his examination so boldly that he would have been set at large but for the blind belief that the spies had in their instructions, which were unfortunately only too minute.
>
> (005-365)
: "Well, and supposing I were the Chevalier de Beauvoir, what should I gain by that?" (005-373) — a rejoinder that captures both his courage and his awareness that his name, however distinguished, offered no protection in revolutionary [[tours|France]].

## Significance in the Novel

Beauvoir's story is told by [[horace-bianchon|Bianchon]] as part of the evening's entertainment at [[anzy|Anzy]] — a tale of adventure and aristocratic honor offered to a provincial audience hungry for romantic drama. Within the novel's structure, the embedded narrative provides a counterpoint to the domestic comedy of [[dinah-de-la-baudraye|Dinah]]'s salon: a glimpse of a larger, more dangerous world where names, identities, and loyalties were matters of life and death. The Chevalier de Beauvoir, though a minor figure, represents the old [[tours|France]] of chivalry and sacrifice that Balzac's generation could still remember and romanticize.
