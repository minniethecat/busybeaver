# Darts

## Overview

The darts used in the Whistlefield Maze murders are modified [[air-rifle|airgun]] darts — small, feathered projectiles designed for target shooting — repurposed as lethal weapons by dipping their tips in [[curare]]. Discovered at [[the-maze|the Maze]] shortly after the discovery of Roger Shandon's body, these darts provide the first concrete forensic evidence in the investigation. Their unusual staining (brown instead of the usual gaudy colours) and the rusty powder clinging to their fibres alert [[Sir Clinton Driffield]] to the murder method.

The darts bridge two domains: the recreational world of Arthur Hawkhurst's target shooting and the lethal world of premeditated murder. Their discovery transforms what initially appeared to be a mysterious death into a case of deliberate poisoning by projectile.

## Role in the Novel

### Discovery at the Maze

The darts are found by a constable searching the Maze grounds shortly after the bodies are discovered. He reports finding "the lid of a tin box" and:

> "Some darts that Mr. Hawkhurst uses for that airgun o' his when he's shootin' at a target." (003-104)

The immediate association with Arthur Hawkhurst is significant: the darts are identified as his before any forensic analysis has been performed.

The constable counts "one … two … three" darts, suggesting the murderer fired multiple shots — consistent with the "dull concussions" Vera Forrest heard while lost in the Maze.

### Sir Clinton's Examination

When Sir Clinton examines the recovered darts, he immediately notices anomalies. He observes that the woollen feathering "instead of showing the usual gaudy colours, was stained brown; and a rusty powder seemed to cling to the fibre. A tiny patch of the same pigment showed on the points of the darts" (004-058). His laconic comment — "Airgun darts, evidently" (004-057) — understates the gravity of the find. The staining is the first indication that these are not ordinary target darts but something far more sinister.

Sir Clinton characteristically does not state the obvious: that the brown staining is almost certainly dried blood or poison residue. His method is to accumulate observations silently, assembling the picture in his own mind before revealing it to others.

### The Tin Box

The tin box whose lid is found with the darts is itself evidence. It suggests the murderer carried the darts in a portable container — pre-prepared, ready for use — rather than improvising at the scene. This level of preparation reinforces the conclusion that the murders were planned, not impulsive.

### The Dart That Saved a Life

In a later attack, the dart's physical trajectory becomes a matter of life and death. When a dart strikes Vera Forrest's watch-bracelet, the chain deflects it just enough to prevent a full embedment:

> "By the merest luck, the dart hit the chain of her watch-bracelet. It got down between the links and made a nasty wound all the same; but it didn't quite embed itself in the flesh. So there's just the chance that the dose of poison injected may not reach the fatal amount." (012-088)

This narrow escape — where the difference between a fatal wound and survival is measured in millimetres of bracelet chain — underscores the darts' potency as a killing method, as well as the element of chance that runs through the investigation.

### Forensic Significance

The darts serve as the physical link between the air-rifle and the poison. Without them, Sir Clinton would know a murder occurred but not how. With them, he can trace backward to the weapon (airgun darts imply an airgun) and forward to the poison (stained darts imply a toxic substance, later confirmed as curare). They are the keystone evidence that makes the entire forensic reconstruction possible.

## Analysis

The darts in *Murder in the Maze* embody a recurring theme in Connington's detective fiction: the transformation of ordinary objects into instruments of death. Airgun darts are recreational items, associated with garden parties and friendly competition. By dipping them in curare, the murderer weaponises the mundane — a gesture that mirrors the broader novelistic theme of evil concealed beneath the placid surface of English country life.

The darts also represent a specific subtype of Golden Age clue: the "significant object" whose true nature is visible from the start but only properly understood later. The constable who finds the darts sees them as "darts that Mr. Hawkhurst uses"; Sir Clinton sees them as evidence of murder. The gap between these two interpretations is the space in which the detective's superior reasoning operates.
