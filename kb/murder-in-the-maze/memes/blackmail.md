# Blackmail

## Overview

Blackmail — the extortion of money or advantage through the threat of revealing damaging information — is a persistent undercurrent in *Murder in the Maze*. It appears as a motive, a subplot, and a character test: who is vulnerable to exposure, who is capable of applying pressure, and who would kill rather than pay. The novel deploys blackmail not as the central crime but as the ambient moral atmosphere in which murder becomes possible.

In the Golden Age detective novel, blackmail is the crime of the respectable classes. Unlike burglary or assault, it operates through knowledge and social vulnerability — the threat to expose a secret that would destroy a reputation, a career, or a fortune. The Whistlefield household, composed of men with pasts they prefer not to discuss, is fertile ground for this kind of predation.

## Role in the Novel

### The Blackmailer in the Household

The first mention of blackmail comes early, when Neville Shandon offers to deal with a problem his brother Roger has been handling. "Blackmail, I suppose?" Neville asks, with professional detachment. "I'll have a talk with him, if you like. Perhaps my persuasive style — the harsh lines about his mouth deepened — would help to convert him to honesty" (001-015). The barrister's "persuasive style" is clearly a euphemism for intimidation — the blackmailer threatened with counter-pressure from a man who has made his career breaking witnesses.

The identity of the blackmailer and the nature of the secret are left tantalisingly vague in this early exchange, but the implication is clear: someone in or near the Shandon household has discovered something about Roger's past and is demanding payment for silence.

### Tim Costock's Attempt

The blackmail subplot crystallises around Tim Costock, a man from Roger Shandon's past who arrives at Whistlefield with demands. Sir Clinton intercepts him and makes his assessment plain: "So you came home as soon as you got out, to blackmail him? That's obvious. You needn't protest, Costock. It's really not of any importance, for I'm quite convinced that you didn't reach the stage of negotiations, so there's no harm done" (005-054). Costock's blackmail attempt is amateurish and abortive — he is, as Sir Clinton later characterises him, "a miserable creature who couldn't screw himself up to murder if he tried" (018-067).

Despite Costock's ineffectuality, his presence establishes an important principle: Roger Shandon had secrets worth blackmailing over. The money he made in South Africa and South America, the "exact methods which had led to his fortune were never discussed by him" (001-005), created vulnerabilities that outlived his return to England.

### Blackmail as Misdirection

Sir Clinton uses the blackmail thread as a deliberate distraction. By appearing to take Costock seriously as a suspect, he keeps the real investigation moving without alerting the actual murderer. "I had him watched; but I never really suspected him of anything beyond a futile attempt at blackmailing Roger Shandon. He hadn't even the nerve for that" (018-067). The blackmail subplot is, in narrative terms, a red herring — a plausible alternative explanation that must be investigated and eliminated before the true solution can emerge.

## Analysis

Blackmail in *Murder in the Maze* functions as the crime that shadows murder — less lethal, but equally revealing of character. Those who blackmail and those who pay blackmail share a moral kinship: both operate outside the law, and both have secrets they would rather keep hidden. The novel suggests, without stating explicitly, that the line between blackmailer and murderer is thinner than either would admit.

Connington's treatment of blackmail is notably unsensational. Costock is not a melodramatic villain but a failed opportunist — "a miserable creature" who lacks the nerve even for effective extortion. This deflation of the blackmailer figure is characteristic of the novel's commitment to psychological realism over Gothic excess. Real crime, Connington implies, is often this shabby: not master criminals executing perfect schemes, but weak men grasping at desperate chances.
