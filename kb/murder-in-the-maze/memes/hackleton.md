# Hackleton

## Overview

Hackleton is a figure whose past conflict with the Shandon family — referred to throughout the novel as "the Hackleton case" — provides background context for the Maze murders. The nature of this case, revealed gradually through the investigation, involves financial dealings that left Hackleton with both a grudge against the Shandons and, potentially, a motive for violence.

The Hackleton case serves as a narrative device that expands the suspect pool beyond the immediate household. If the murders were revenge for a past wrong, the killer need not be a family member or beneficiary — opening possibilities that Sir Clinton must systematically evaluate and eliminate.

## Role in the Plot

Hackleton's significance lies primarily in what his case represents: the possibility that the Maze murders are not a family crime but an external act of vengeance. Sir Clinton considers this possibility seriously — "Hackleton's got money enough to buy some unscrupulous fellow with brains; and this gone-under intellectual might have hit on the trick" (008-077) — before ultimately determining that the evidence points elsewhere.
