# Suspicion

## Overview

Suspicion is both an investigative tool and a narrative force in *Murder in the Maze*. As a tool, it directs Sir Clinton Driffield's attention toward possible perpetrators; as a narrative force, it shapes the reader's experience of the story, creating a shifting landscape of doubt in which every character seems potentially guilty. The novel carefully manages suspicion, directing it first toward one suspect, then another, before the final resolution identifies the true killer.

The management of suspicion is central to the Golden Age detective story's contract with the reader: all clues must be fairly presented, yet the solution must surprise. Connington achieves this by making suspicion a dynamic, distributed phenomenon — it moves between characters as new evidence emerges, and no single suspect commands it exclusively until the final revelation.

## Role in the Novel

### Arthur Hawkhurst: The Obvious Suspect

Arthur Hawkhurst attracts suspicion from the earliest chapters. He owns the air-rifle; he is the acknowledged expert marksman; he is seen carrying the weapon near the time of the murders; he knows about the curare in the museum; his behaviour is hostile and erratic. Sir Clinton acknowledges the strength of the case against him: "I'm a bit doubtful about young Hawkhurst, to tell you the truth" (008-097).

Yet Connington carefully plants reasons to doubt Arthur's guilt. His hostility during the curare retrieval, while suspicious, is also consistent with an innocent person objecting to high-handed police procedures. His airgun expertise, while damning, makes him almost too obvious a suspect — a meta-textual clue that the experienced reader learns to distrust.

### The Shifting Focus of Suspicion

Suspicion in the novel does not remain fixed. It shifts from Arthur to Ardsley (who has his own curare supply and knows the Maze), to Torrance (whose forgery and flight preparations suggest guilt), to Ernest Shandon (whose broken alibi is the investigation's decisive turn). Each shift is motivated by new evidence, and each suspect has a plausible case against them — until Sir Clinton's final synthesis identifies the one whose guilt satisfies all the evidence.

### Suspicion as Psychological Burden

The novel also explores the psychological toll of being under suspicion. Torrance's anguished explanation of why he did not flee — "I'd have been marked down as the murderer if I'd stirred a step" (015-039) — reveals how suspicion constrains behaviour even when it is undeserved. The innocent, knowing they are watched, may act guiltily; the guilty, knowing the same, may perform innocence. This feedback loop between suspicion and behaviour is one of the novel's most psychologically acute observations.

### The Reader's Suspicion

Connington manages the reader's suspicion with particular care. Information is released in a sequence that makes each suspect seem plausible in turn, ensuring that the true solution — while fairly clued — remains surprising. The technique is essentially theatrical: the reader's suspicion is directed by the same mechanism that directs characters' suspicion, creating an immersive experience in which the reader participates in the investigation.

## Analysis

Suspicion in *Murder in the Maze* operates as a distributed cognitive state — it is not the property of any single character but a field that surrounds the entire cast. This distribution is essential to the detective story's structure: if suspicion were concentrated on one character from the start, there would be no puzzle. The detective's job is to focus suspicion gradually, eliminating the innocent until only the guilty remain.

Connington's treatment of suspicion also has a philosophical dimension. The novel suggests that suspicion, unchecked by evidence, is indistinguishable from prejudice. Sir Clinton's method — "fluid" convictions, readiness to "reconsider things at any time" (018-062) — is an antidote to premature certainty. Suspicion is necessary to investigation, but it must be disciplined by evidence and open to revision.
