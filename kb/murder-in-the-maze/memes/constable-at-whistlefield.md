# The Constable at Whistlefield

## Overview

The local police constable assigned to search the Maze grounds after the discovery of the bodies makes one of the investigation's most significant finds: the darts and tin lid that become the first physical evidence of the murder method. His methodical search — the kind of routine police work that detective fiction often minimises in favour of brilliant deduction — demonstrates the importance of thoroughness in criminal investigation.

The constable's report — "'Tis the lid of a tin box — one o' these round 'uns. And here's some darts that Mr. Hawkhurst uses for that airgun o' his when he's shootin' at a target" (003-104) — is precise and factual. He identifies the objects, notes their association with Arthur Hawkhurst, and counts them ("one … two … three"). His testimony exemplifies the value of the ordinary police officer whose observations provide the foundation for the detective's deductions.

## Role in the Plot

The constable's discovery of the darts transforms the investigation. Before this find, the deaths could have been attributed to natural causes or an unknown method. After it, the investigation has a weapon (the darts imply an airgun), a poison (the staining implies toxic contamination), and a suspect (the darts are associated with Arthur Hawkhurst). The constable does not draw these conclusions — that is Sir Clinton's job — but he provides the evidence that makes them possible.
