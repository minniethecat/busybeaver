# Forgery

## Overview

Forgery — specifically, the forging of a cheque — surfaces as a significant subplot in the Whistlefield Maze investigation. The discovery that a cheque bearing Roger Shandon's signature may have been forged introduces a financial motive into what initially appears to be a purely violent crime. The forgery connects to Howard Torrance, Roger Shandon's secretary, whose financial difficulties and access to Shandon's papers make him a natural suspect.

The forgery thread exemplifies Connington's technique of layering financial crime beneath violent crime, creating a web of motives that the detective must untangle before the murderer's identity can be established.

## Role in the Novel

### Discovery of the Forged Cheque

The forged cheque emerges during the investigation of Roger Shandon's financial affairs. As Sir Clinton examines the dead man's papers — looking for motives among the beneficiaries of his will — the irregularity of a particular cheque comes to light. The possibility that someone close to Shandon was systematically defrauding him adds a new dimension to the case: the Maze murders may not have been a crime of passion or revenge, but the culmination of a financial scheme on the verge of exposure.

### Torrance's Position

Howard Torrance, as Shandon's secretary, had both opportunity and motive. The Squire notes that Torrance had been "clearing up preparations for a bolt" (015-039) — packing to flee — when the murders occurred. Torrance's explanation — that he was leaving because of the forgery, not the murders — is plausible but damning. His admission that he "hadn't an alibi" for the time of the murders (015-039) compounds the suspicion, even as Sir Clinton ultimately determines that the forgery and the murders, while connected, are not the work of the same hand.

### Motive and Misdirection

The forgery serves a dual narrative function. On one level, it provides genuine motive for one suspect, deepening the mystery. On another, it functions as misdirection — the reader, and some characters, may assume the forger must be the murderer, when in fact the two crimes have different perpetrators. Sir Clinton's ability to separate these threads — to see that financial crime and murder, though intertwined, are distinct — demonstrates his sophistication as a detective.

## Analysis

The forgery subplot in *Murder in the Maze* reflects the Golden Age detective story's fascination with financial crime as the "respectable" motive. Unlike passion or madness, financial gain operates through calculation and deception — qualities that make it a natural companion to the rational-detection model. Connington, whose own background included scientific training, was particularly drawn to crimes that reward methodical investigation, and forgery — with its paper trail, handwriting analysis, and bank records — is the quintessential crime of documents.

The forgery also serves as a vehicle for Sir Clinton's distinctive method. Rather than treating financial irregularity as a smoking gun, he examines it as one thread among many, testing how (and whether) it connects to the murders. This refusal to jump to conclusions — to let each piece of evidence find its own place in the picture — is presented as the mark of true detective genius.
