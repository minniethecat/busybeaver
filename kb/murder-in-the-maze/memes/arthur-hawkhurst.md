# Arthur Hawkhurst

## Overview

Arthur Hawkhurst is Roger Shandon's nephew, a beneficiary of his will, and the owner of the air-rifle used in the Maze murders. An expert marksman who "happens to be able to beat everyone else" at shooting (001-069), Arthur is the novel's most obvious suspect: he possesses the weapon, the skill, and the access to curare that the murders required.

Arthur's behaviour throughout the investigation is hostile and erratic. He objects strenuously to the removal of the curare from the museum ("You've no right to take it: I object", 007-059), carries his airgun openly despite the obvious suspicion it attracts, and generally acts in ways that seem designed to incriminate himself. Whether this behaviour indicates guilt or merely a difficult personality is one of the questions Sir Clinton must answer.

## Role in the Plot

### The Obvious Suspect

Arthur accumulates suspicion from the earliest chapters. The butler reports seeing him "passing the window with his airgun in his hand" (003-037) shortly before the murders. The darts found at the Maze are identified as "darts that Mr. Hawkhurst uses for that airgun o' his" (003-104). His marksmanship is acknowledged by all. His knowledge of the curare in the museum is confirmed when he identifies it immediately: "It's curare — Indian arrow-poison" (007-059).

### The Problem of Obviousness

Sir Clinton's hesitation about Arthur is significant: "I'm a bit doubtful about young Hawkhurst, to tell you the truth" (008-097). The case against Arthur is almost too strong — every piece of evidence points toward him, which in a fair-play detective story often signals that the obvious suspect is a red herring. Sir Clinton's doubt is methodological: correlation is not causation, and a person can be unpleasant, skilled, and near the crime scene without being guilty.

### The Curare Retrieval Confrontation

The scene at the museum, when Wendover and Ardsley attempt to remove the curare, is Arthur's most extended appearance. His hostility — fixing "his eyes on the toxicologist's hands, as though he feared that Ardsley might remove some of the stuff under their very eyes" (007-053) — and his explicit objection ("I don't care about this way of doing things") deepen suspicion against him. Yet his behaviour is also consistent with an innocent person who distrusts the police and resents their intrusion.

## Analysis

Arthur Hawkhurst represents the "obvious suspect" problem in detective fiction. Every clue points to him, yet the conventions of the genre suggest that the obvious suspect is never guilty. Connington exploits this convention to create productive ambiguity: the reader cannot decide whether Arthur is guilty (the evidence is strong) or innocent (the narrative structure argues against it).

Arthur's characterisation — hostile, erratic, skilled with weapons — draws on post-World War I anxieties about young men who had learned to kill and returned to civilian life unsettled. The reference to his "attack of sleepy sickness" (encephalitis lethargica) and the subsequent change in his temper (008-097) suggests a neurological basis for his difficult personality, a detail consistent with Connington's scientific interests.
