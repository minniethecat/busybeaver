# The Air-Rifle

## Overview

The air-rifle (also referred to as an "airgun" throughout the novel) is the murder weapon used to kill Roger and Neville Shandon in the Whistlefield Maze. It fires curare-tipped darts with a near-silent discharge, making it an ideal weapon for concealed attacks. The weapon belongs to Arthur Hawkhurst, a keen airgun marksman who uses it for target practice. Its distinctive auditory signature — a "dull concussion" followed by a "metallic rasping" as the spring is recharged — becomes a crucial piece of evidence in reconstructing the murders.

The air-rifle appears throughout the investigation: first as a recreational item during the tennis party, then as the suspected murder weapon, and finally as the key to understanding both the method and the killer's identity.

## Role in the Novel

### The Murder Method

The air-rifle's role as a murder weapon crystallises when Vera Forrest, having heard the dull reports in the Maze, suddenly connects the sound to the weapon: "That was the noise she had heard — the dull report of an air-rifle! And the metallic rasping was the grating of the spring as the murderer recharged his weapon" (003-038). This recognition is pivotal: it identifies both the weapon type and the method of the attack.

The weapon's near-silence is central to the crime. Unlike a firearm, an air-rifle produces only a muffled thud, inaudible beyond the immediate vicinity of the Maze. Sir Clinton Driffield later observes: "An airgun's fairly silent; and that curare evidently kills quickly. It's not the sort of thing an ordinary rough would think of" (008-077). The combination of silent weapon and fast-acting poison makes the murders possible in broad daylight, with other people nearby.

### Arthur Hawkhurst's Weapon

The air-rifle is explicitly linked to Arthur Hawkhurst from the earliest chapters. The butler reports: "Mr. Hawkhurst, he went out quite early on. I saw him passing the window with his airgun in his hand" (003-037). Later, when Arthur enters a room carrying the weapon, Vera Forrest gives "a slight exclamation" (005-100), her trauma from the Maze now associating the air-rifle with murder.

Arthur's proficiency with the airgun is noted early: during the tennis party, he "bored everyone stiff" with the shooting tournament, "except Arthur, who happens to be able to beat everyone else" (001-069). This marksmanship becomes suspicious when combined with his access to curare and his presence near the Maze.

### A Weapon of Concealment

The air-rifle represents a deliberate choice of method. As Sir Clinton notes, the killer selected a weapon that would not draw attention — silent, recoilless, and small enough to hide. The darts fired from it leave no bullet and produce minimal bleeding, complicating the initial investigation. Only when the darts are discovered at the Maze does the full nature of the weapon become clear.

## Analysis

The air-rifle in *Murder in the Maze* exemplifies a recurring Golden Age detective fiction trope: the ingenious murder method. Unlike the conventional firearms of thrillers, the air-rifle is a domestic object — associated with target practice and recreation — transformed into a lethal weapon. This dual nature (sporting equipment / murder weapon) creates dramatic irony: the characters handle and discuss the airgun casually before the murders, never suspecting its deadly potential.

The weapon also serves as a narrative device linking Arthur Hawkhurst to the crime. Every appearance of the air-rifle is associated with Arthur: he carries it, he is its owner, he is the acknowledged expert shot. The reader, following Sir Clinton's method, must decide whether this association is evidence of guilt or a red herring — a question the detective himself wrestles with throughout the investigation.
