# Curare

## Overview

Curare is a South American arrow poison that acts as the lethal agent in the Whistlefield Maze murders. A powerful paralysing substance derived from plant alkaloids, curare kills by blocking motor nerve endings, causing rapid respiratory failure without affecting consciousness. In the novel, the murderer dips air-rifle darts in curare, combining a silent delivery mechanism with a fast-acting toxin to create a nearly undetectable killing method.

The poison enters the investigation when Sir Clinton Driffield consults Dr. Ardsley, a local toxicologist. Through Ardsley's expertise, curare is identified as the substance on the stained darts found at the Maze, and its local availability — specifically, a specimen pot in Roger Shandon's museum at Whistlefield — becomes a crucial line of inquiry.

## Role in the Novel

### Identification of the Poison

The investigation's turning point comes when Sir Clinton takes the recovered darts to Dr. Ardsley for analysis. The toxicologist immediately recognises the staining and conducts a live demonstration: "Its physiological effect is a powerful paralysing action on the motor nerve endings supplying striated muscle, but it has no action on the excitability of the muscle. You saw the actual results in that experiment" (006-055). The demonstration is both scientific and dramatic — Ardsley injects a small animal, which dies within moments, confirming the darts' lethality.

Ardsley provides precise toxicological data: "A quarter of a grain of protocurarine would kill a ten-stone man quite easily. There was far more than a fatal dose of curare on that dart" (006-060). This establishes that the murder was not accidental or experimental but deliberately lethal.

### Three Forms of Curare

Ardsley's expertise reveals that curare comes in three commercial forms, distinguished by their packaging: "Para curare you can buy in bamboo tubes; calabash curare is packed in gourds; and what they call pot curare is sold in earthenware pots" (006-064). The specimen at Whistlefield is "pot curare" — the form that gives the novel's key clue its name: the "Pot of Curare" that Sir Clinton races to secure.

### The Whistlefield Source

A critical revelation comes when Ardsley recalls that Roger Shandon kept a pot of curare in his private museum at Whistlefield: "Roger Shandon had a sort of museum up at Whistlefield — stuff he had picked up on his travels — rubbish mostly. But I remember he had a pot of curare amongst it" (006-076). Ardsley admits he himself borrowed from the specimen: "I remember that once, a while ago, I ran short of curare and I borrowed Roger's specimen and took some of it" (006-079).

This admission transforms the investigation. The curare, previously an exotic mystery, is now local and accessible. Multiple people — Ardsley, the Shandon household, any visitor to the museum — could have obtained it. Sir Clinton immediately dispatches Wendover and Ardsley to Whistlefield to secure the remaining curare before the murderer can dispose of it.

### The Scramble for Evidence

The race to secure the pot of curare is one of the investigation's most urgent sequences. Sir Clinton tells Wendover: "You must get that stuff into your hands without a second's delay" (006-094). At Whistlefield, Arthur Hawkhurst objects strenuously to its removal: "You've no right to take it: I object" (007-059). His hostility — combined with his known skill with airguns — sharpens suspicion against him.

Wendover notes that "some of the stuff had been removed" from the pot (007-069), confirming the murderer's access. The curare thus serves a dual narrative function: as both the physiological cause of death and the investigative thread that draws suspicion toward specific individuals.

## Analysis

Curare in *Murder in the Maze* represents a sophisticated escalation from the conventional poisons of detective fiction. Where arsenic and cyanide suggest domestic murders of passion or greed, curare is exotic, scientific, and requires specialised knowledge. Its selection as the murder weapon narrows the suspect pool to those with access to both the poison and the knowledge of its properties — a classic Golden Age detective fiction constraint that turns forensic science into a filter for suspects.

The poison also functions as a thematic mirror of the Maze itself. Just as the Maze conceals the killer through physical architecture, curare conceals the method through biochemical subtlety. Both require expert knowledge to navigate: Sir Clinton needs Ardsley's toxicology just as he needs Stenness's familiarity with the Maze's layout.
