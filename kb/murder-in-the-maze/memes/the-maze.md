# The Maze

## Overview

The Maze at Whistlefield is a hedge labyrinth on the grounds of the Shandon family estate, located down by the river, close to the boathouse. A relic of an earlier era when such garden features were fashionable, it serves as the primary setting for the murders of Roger and Neville Shandon, and becomes the focal point of [[Sir Clinton Driffield]]'s investigation. The Maze's physical properties — its impenetrable twelve-foot hedges, its "island-pattern" design, and its two isolated centres — make it both an ideal location for a concealed murder and a central plot device around which the mystery unfolds. The killer exploits these features to fire [[darts]] from an [[air-rifle]] at close range while remaining completely hidden by the towering hedges.

As described in Chapter II, the Maze at Whistlefield was "a labyrinth far exceeding in complexity its kindred at Hatfield and Hampton Court" (002-006). It covered about half an acre of ground, yet compressed more than half a mile of pathways within that limited space. Its twelve-foot hedges were impenetrably thick, and its "island-pattern" design meant that "incautious explorers might wander by the hour through its tiny archipelago without gaining a foot towards the innermost recesses" (002-006).

## Role in the Novel

### The Murders

The Maze serves as the scene of the novel's central crimes. In the opening chapters, both Roger Shandon and Neville Shandon are found dead in the Maze's twin centres — Roger in Helen's Bower and Neville at the Pool of Narcissus (002-031). The murders occur while Vera Forrest and Howard Torrance are inside the Maze, separated and lost in its winding paths. Vera hears the dull reports of the murder weapon — later identified as an air-rifle — but the Maze's towering hedges prevent her from seeing the killer (002-038).

The choice of the Maze as a murder scene is no accident. As Sir Clinton Driffield observes:

> "Absolute privacy guaranteed by the nature of the affair. No one could see through those hedges. The murderer can creep up to within lethal distance, come almost face to face with his victim, and yet remain absolutely invisible." (003-049)

This makes the Maze a uniquely effective setting for a crime in which the killer exploits physical concealment to its fullest.

### The Labyrinth as Narrative Device

Beyond its role as a crime scene, the Maze functions as a narrative device that heightens tension and isolates characters. Vera Forrest's experience inside the Maze — hearing footsteps, the crack of a twig, knowing the murderer is still nearby — creates some of the novel's most suspenseful passages:

> "With the dying out of her own footfalls, the silence of the Maze impressed itself on her; and she strained her ears to catch the sound of Howard as he moved somewhere beyond these impenetrable green living walls." (002-075)

Her terror is amplified by the Maze's architecture: "shut in among these towering walls, isolated from all help in the intricacies of the Maze, it would be an easy business to silence her finally" (002-024).

The Maze also complicates the investigation. Because its pathways twist and double back, determining the killer's movements and escape route becomes a critical forensic challenge. Sir Clinton notes that the murderer "didn't go straight out of the Maze immediately. Probably he found Miss Forrest and myself blocking the road again and again as we wandered about" (003-009), demonstrating how the Maze's structure directly shapes the sequence of events.

### Later Attacks

The Maze remains significant throughout the novel as the site of subsequent attacks. The pattern of murders "in the Maze" establishes it not merely as a location but as a recurring motif — the place where the killer feels most emboldened to strike, confident in the labyrinth's concealment.

## Analysis

In the context of Golden Age detective fiction, the Whistlefield Maze is a classic example of the "locked room" variant known as the "enclosed space" mystery. Unlike a traditional locked room, the Maze is physically open — anyone could enter or leave — but its disorienting architecture creates a psychological enclosure. The killer's ability to navigate it confidently, while victims and witnesses struggle, becomes a crucial clue.

The Maze also carries mythological resonance. The reference to the horn hung at the entrance — a device for lost visitors to summon help — echoes the myth of Theseus and the Minotaur (002-007). Both the classical labyrinth and the Whistlefield Maze contain a monster at their centre; in Connington's novel, the monster is human.

The two centres of the Maze — Helen's Bower and the Pool of Narcissus — are themselves symbolically named. Helen's Bower suggests a place of romantic seclusion, while Narcissus's Pool evokes self-absorption and death. That the two brothers die in separate centres may reflect their estranged relationship, each isolated even in death.
