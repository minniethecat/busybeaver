# The Chief Constable

## Overview

The local Chief Constable represents official police authority in the Maze murder investigation, but his role is primarily facilitative: he provides resources, arranges access, and defers to Sir Clinton Driffield's superior analytical abilities. His presence in the novel reflects the Golden Age convention of the official police as competent but unbrilliant — reliable for routine work but incapable of the deductive leaps that solve complex cases.

The Chief Constable's professional relationship with Sir Clinton — respectful, cooperative, without rivalry — models the ideal relationship between amateur expertise and official authority. Unlike detectives who clash with the police, Sir Clinton works with them, and the Chief Constable's willingness to recognise superior ability ensures the investigation proceeds without jurisdictional friction.

## Role in the Plot

The Chief Constable handles the administrative and procedural dimensions of the case: securing the crime scene, arranging the inquest, managing the constables assigned to surveillance. His competence in these matters frees Sir Clinton to focus on the analytical work of detection. He is not a rival detective but a professional colleague, and his presence legitimises Sir Clinton's involvement without threatening it.
