# Fingerprint

## Overview

Fingerprint identification — the matching of unique ridge patterns left by human fingers on smooth surfaces — was an established forensic technique by the 1920s setting of *Murder in the Maze*. Sir Clinton Driffield employs fingerprint evidence as a precautionary tool in the Whistlefield investigation, though the novel realistically acknowledges its limitations when a cunning murderer has taken steps to obscure or dilute the evidence.

Unlike the fingerprint-centric detective stories of the period, Connington treats finger marks as one tool among many — useful but rarely decisive. The murderer's deliberate contamination of the physical evidence (organising a group shooting competition to overlay his own prints with those of others) demonstrates an awareness of forensic methods that elevates the criminal's intelligence.

## Role in the Novel

### The Cigar-Case

The most explicit use of fingerprint evidence occurs when Sir Clinton recovers Ernest Shandon's cigar-case from the Maze. Wendover immediately thinks of fingerprints: "You think the murderer may have picked it up, and you'll get his fingerprints from it? It's a nice smooth surface" (011-026). Sir Clinton's response, however, reveals his deeper strategic thinking — he suspects the cigar-case was left deliberately as a plant, and the fingerprints on it may be part of a larger deception.

### Contaminated Evidence

The novel's most sophisticated engagement with fingerprint evidence comes in Sir Clinton's final exposition. He explains that Ernest Shandon, the real murderer, had anticipated forensic examination: "It seems friend Ernest had carefully organised a grand airgun shooting competition on the morning of the day he killed his brothers. He'd had the guns passed from hand to hand, so that the fingermarks of nearly everyone were printed on them in addition to his own" (018-071). This mass contamination of the evidence rendered fingerprint analysis nearly useless — exactly as Ernest intended.

Sir Clinton also notes that Ernest likely used gloves when handling the tin box of darts: "I was sure he'd use gloves in touching it" (018-071). The murderer's forensic awareness extends even to the precautions against leaving prints on the poison container.

### A Precaution, Not a Solution

Throughout the investigation, Sir Clinton treats fingerprint evidence as a supplementary tool rather than a primary method. "I hardly troubled to examine it for fingermarks" (018-071), he admits of the dart box. His confidence derives from logical deduction, not physical traces — a choice that distinguishes his method from the more mechanically-minded detectives of contemporary fiction.

## Analysis

The treatment of fingerprint evidence in *Murder in the Maze* reflects a transitional moment in forensic science. By the 1920s, fingerprinting was accepted as valid evidence in English courts, but its limitations were equally well understood by both police and criminals. Connington's depiction of a murderer who actively contaminates fingerprint evidence represents a sophisticated engagement with forensic epistemology — the recognition that physical evidence can be manipulated as readily as testimonial evidence.

This theme resonates with the novel's broader interest in the distinction between genuine and fabricated clues. Just as the "Ariadne's clue" thread is planted to mislead, fingerprint evidence can be arranged to incriminate or exculpate. The detective's task is not simply to collect prints but to interpret their context — who left them, when, and with what intention.
