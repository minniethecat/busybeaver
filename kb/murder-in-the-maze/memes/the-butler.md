# The Butler

## Overview

The butler at Whistlefield is a minor but significant witness in the Maze murder investigation. His testimony — delivered without apparent bias or interpretive overlay — provides Sir Clinton with the kind of routine observational data that becomes crucial when assembled with other evidence. His report that Arthur Hawkhurst was seen "passing the window with his airgun in his hand" (003-037) is offered without suspicion: it is simply what he saw in the course of his duties.

The butler's value as a witness lies precisely in his lack of investigative agenda. He reports what he observed; he does not speculate about what it means. This makes his testimony more reliable than that of witnesses who have theories to advance or positions to defend.

## Role in the Plot

The butler's most significant contribution is his testimony about the movements of household members on the day of the murders. His detailed account of who was where — Roger Shandon leaving the house, Neville Shandon following, Arthur Hawkhurst passing the window with his airgun — establishes the timeline that Sir Clinton uses to test alibis and reconstruct the sequence of events.
