# Howard Torrance

## Overview

Howard Torrance is Roger Shandon's private secretary, employed at Whistlefield and entrusted with access to Shandon's financial papers. His position — close to the victim, familiar with his affairs, and present at the estate during the murders — makes him a natural suspect. When a forged cheque is discovered among Roger Shandon's papers, Torrance's financial difficulties and his access to Shandon's signature transform him from a peripheral figure into a central one.

Torrance's plight illustrates the novel's theme of circumstantial evidence. His actions — packing to flee, lacking an alibi, being present near the Maze — are all explainable individually. Together, they form a pattern that could convince a jury. Sir Clinton's task is to determine whether this pattern points to the forger, the murderer, or both.

## Role in the Plot

### The Secretary

Torrance's position as secretary gives him access to Roger Shandon's private affairs. He knows the contents of the will, the state of the estate, and the relationships among the beneficiaries. This knowledge makes him both a valuable witness (he can confirm or correct others' accounts) and a potential suspect (his knowledge could facilitate crime).

### The Forgery

The discovery of a forged cheque among Shandon's papers brings Torrance under direct suspicion. The forgery suggests someone was defrauding Roger Shandon, and Torrance — with his financial access and his known money troubles — is the obvious candidate. His admission that he was "clearing up preparations for a bolt" (015-039) at the time of the murders confirms both his guilt in the forgery and his vulnerability to suspicion in the murders.

### The Self-Interested Witness

Torrance's explanation for remaining after the murders — despite having planned to flee — reveals the psychology of the innocent suspect: "I was in the middle of clearing up preparations for a bolt … and suddenly came the affair in the Maze. Could I bolt then? Not likely. I'd have been marked down as the murderer if I'd stirred a step" (015-039). His awareness of how his actions would be interpreted — correctly — constrains his behaviour. The innocent man, knowing he looks guilty, may act in ways that confirm suspicion.

## Analysis

Howard Torrance embodies the "guilty of something else" archetype in detective fiction. He is indeed a criminal — a forger who defrauded his employer — but he is not a murderer. Sir Clinton's ability to distinguish between the two crimes, recognising that financial dishonesty and lethal violence have different perpetrators, is a testament to his analytical precision.

Torrance also illustrates a recurring Golden Age theme: the respectable man driven to crime by financial pressure. His forgery is not presented as villainy but as weakness — a desperate measure by a man who could not maintain his position honestly. The novel's treatment of Torrance is notably compassionate; his forgery is acknowledged but not judged, and his innocence of the murders is established without melodrama.
