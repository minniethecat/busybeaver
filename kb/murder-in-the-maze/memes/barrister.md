# Barrister

## Overview

The barrister is a specialist courtroom advocate in the English legal system, distinct from the solicitor who prepares cases and instructs counsel. In *Murder in the Maze*, the profession is personified by Neville Shandon KC, a King's Counsel whose formidable reputation as a cross-examiner makes him one of the leading figures of the Bar. The barrister's craft — relentless questioning, concealed traps, the dismantling of witness testimony — becomes a thematic parallel to Sir Clinton Driffield's own method of logical investigation.

Neville Shandon's professional identity is inseparable from his character. His success derives from "a certain hardness of character coupled with an abundance of energy" (001-005); his cross-examinations are described as "brutal and domineering" (001-005). The barrister's skills of interrogation and deduction mirror the detective's, creating a structural symmetry between the legal and investigative realms.

## Role in the Novel

### Neville Shandon, King's Counsel

Neville Shandon stands at the pinnacle of his profession. His handling of the Hackleton case demonstrates his mastery: "It had shown Neville Shandon at his best, relentlessly driving the defendants into one damaging admission after another" (001-011). The case has consumed months of work, and Neville's personal investment in its outcome is absolute: "It's taken me months of work to see my way through it; and if I happened to break down before it comes to a finish, I doubt if a junior could take it on with anything like success" (001-012).

The barrister's professional pride is evident in his frustration with his brother Ernest's ignorance of the Hackleton case. "Do you never read newspapers, Ernest?" he demands, "quite unnettled by his brother's ignorance of one of the greatest cases in which he himself had taken a leading part" (001-011). This pride is not vanity — it reflects the barrister's complete absorption in his work, a trait that gives depth to his character beyond the mere function of "first victim."

### The Hackleton Prosecution After Neville

Neville's murder has immediate consequences for the Hackleton trial. As Wendover later remarks: "Shandon's junior isn't making much out of it, so far as I can see. Old man Hackleton has every reason to be content with the removal of Neville Shandon. He's having it all his own way in the case now — far too clever for the poor barrister. He'll get off scot-free, or I'm much mistaken" (012-006). This observation transforms Neville's death from a personal tragedy into a crime with professional ramifications: the murderer has not only killed a man but effectively scuttled a major prosecution.

The barrister's removal creates a motive that extends beyond the Shandon family. Hackleton himself benefits directly from Neville's death, adding an external dimension to what initially appears to be a family-centered crime.

### The Barrister's Method and the Detective's Method

Neville's professional technique — "I'll have a talk with him, if you like. Perhaps my persuasive style would help to convert him to honesty" (001-015) — is described with characteristic irony. The "harsh lines about his mouth" deepen as he speaks, suggesting that "persuasion" in the barrister's vocabulary means something closer to intimidation. This hardness, which made Neville feared in the courtroom, also made him enemies.

The parallel between barrister and detective is explicit: both professions require the ability to see through deception, to construct logical chains from fragmented evidence, and to break down the resistance of those who dissemble. Sir Clinton's method of investigation is, in essence, a barrister's cross-examination conducted outside the courtroom.

## Analysis

The barrister in Golden Age detective fiction frequently serves a dual function: as a character whose professional skills mirror the detective's, and as a victim whose death has ripple effects beyond the personal. Connington, writing from his own experience (he was a chemist, not a lawyer, but knew the legal world well), uses Neville Shandon's profession to enrich the motive landscape of the novel.

Neville's barristerial hardness — the quality that made him successful — also made him vulnerable. A man who has "driven defendants into damaging admissions" for decades has inevitably created enemies. The novel leaves open the possibility that Neville, not his brother Roger, was the intended target — a question that his profession makes more plausible.

The posthumous impact on the Hackleton trial also serves a structural purpose: it demonstrates that murder is not a self-contained event but one that radiates outward, affecting institutions, cases, and lives far beyond the immediate crime scene.
