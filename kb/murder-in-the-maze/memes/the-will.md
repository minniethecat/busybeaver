# The Will

## Overview

Roger Shandon's will is a deceptively simple document: "All the property is to be divided equally between Neville Shandon, Ernest Shandon, Miss Hawkhurst, and Arthur Hawkhurst" (013-059). This equal division among four relatives — two Shandon brothers and two Hawkhurst siblings — creates a financial landscape in which inheritance provides motive for all four beneficiaries and therefore, paradoxically, for none. If everyone gains equally from Roger's death, no single beneficiary has a unique financial incentive to kill him.

The will's significance is not in identifying a single greedy heir but in establishing that financial motive was diffused across the family. Sir Clinton must look beyond simple inheritance for the true motive — and when he does, he finds it in the more complex dynamics of family hatred, forged cheques, and the fear of exposure.

## Role in the Novel

### The Equal Division

The will's equal division is confirmed by Mr. Shandon's solicitor: "The will's simple enough. All the property is to be divided equally between Neville Shandon, Ernest Shandon, Miss Hawkhurst, and Arthur Hawkhurst. There's no entail or anything of that kind" (013-059). Sir Clinton specifically inquires about a "residuary legatee" — someone who would inherit if all named beneficiaries predeceased Roger — but none exists (013-068).

The absence of a residuary legatee is significant. If the killer hoped to inherit by eliminating other beneficiaries, the murder of Neville Shandon (who died alongside Roger) would have been unnecessary — Neville would have inherited and then could have been killed separately. The double murder suggests a motive other than simple inheritance.

### Motive and Counter-Motive

The will creates a structure of countervailing incentives. Each beneficiary stands to gain from Roger's death but also from the deaths of the other beneficiaries — yet the sequence of murders (Roger and Neville together, then attacks on others) does not follow a clean inheritance logic. The killer is not systematically eliminating co-heirs.

Sir Clinton uses the will as a negative filter: it provides motive for everyone, so it cannot be the decisive factor. The true motive, he concludes, must lie elsewhere — in the forgery, in family hatred, in the specific relationship between the killer and the victims.

### The Will as Context

Beyond its direct role in the investigation, the will establishes the financial context of the Shandon family. Roger Shandon was wealthy; his death redistributes significant assets. The financial stakes give the investigation urgency and explain why so many people have reasons — if not to kill, at least to be relieved by Roger's death.

## Analysis

The will in *Murder in the Maze* exemplifies Connington's preference for complex over simple motives. A will that leaves everything to one person would make that person the obvious suspect; a will that divides equally among four diffuses suspicion while preserving the possibility of financial motive. This is characteristic of the Golden Age detective story's approach to motive: it should be present but not obvious, real but not decisive.

The will also functions as a narrative device that structures the suspect pool. The four beneficiaries form a natural quartet of suspects, and the investigation can be charted as a process of elimination across this set. Sir Clinton's method — testing each beneficiary against means, opportunity, and alternative motives — is facilitated by the will's clean enumeration of the interested parties.
