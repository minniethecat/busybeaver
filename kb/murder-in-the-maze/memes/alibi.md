# Alibi

## Overview

The concept of the alibi — proof that a suspect was elsewhere at the time of a crime — is fundamental to the investigation of the Whistlefield Maze murders. In a case where the killer could strike silently and invisibly from within the Maze's hedges, establishing who was where becomes Sir Clinton Driffield's primary investigative tool. The alibi problem in the novel is complicated by the Maze's architecture: witnesses inside the Maze cannot see each other, and anyone claiming to have been elsewhere at the critical moment must have their story verified independently.

The alibi investigation ultimately narrows the suspect pool and provides the deductive framework for Sir Clinton's final reconstruction of events. The breaking of Ernest Shandon's alibi — the discovery that a bicycle could cover the distance between his supposed location and the Maze — is the investigation's decisive turning point.

## Role in the Novel

### The Investigative Framework

Sir Clinton approaches the Maze murders methodically, treating alibi verification as a filter: "Of course my convictions were quite fluid still. I was quite prepared to reconsider things at any time. The next point was to go over the alibis as well as I could. Wendover gave me a hand with that" (018-062). This systematic approach is characteristic of his deductive method — eliminate the impossible by establishing where each suspect demonstrably was, and the possible becomes progressively clearer.

The alibi check proceeds person by person through the household and visitors. Each individual's claimed location is tested against witness statements and physical constraints. Where contradictions emerge — as with Ernest Shandon's claimed whereabouts — Sir Clinton pursues them relentlessly.

### Ernest Shandon's Broken Alibi

The investigation's critical breakthrough comes when Sir Clinton identifies a flaw in Ernest Shandon's alibi. Ernest had claimed to be at a location distant from the Maze at the time of the murders, but Sir Clinton demonstrates that "once you bring in the bicycle, Ernest Shandon's alibi falls to bits" (018-071). A bicycle could cover the distance quickly enough for Ernest to reach the Maze, commit the murders, and return without being missed.

This forensic geography — measuring distances, testing travel times — is presented as pure deductive work. No single witness contradicts Ernest; the alibi is broken by reasoning alone, a hallmark of the Golden Age detective story's faith in rational analysis.

### Howard Torrance's Anxious Alibi

Howard Torrance provides a contrasting case: his behaviour suggests he fears being suspected, yet his alibi holds. As Torrance himself says when the murders are discovered: "I was in the middle of clearing up preparations for a bolt … and suddenly came the affair in the Maze. Could I bolt then? Not likely. I'd have been marked down as the murderer if I'd stirred a step" (015-039). His awareness of how his actions would be interpreted — and his decision to stay despite the risk — demonstrates the psychological dimension of the alibi concept: not only where one was, but how one's movements will be read by others.

### The Alibi as Narrative Filter

Throughout the novel, the alibi concept serves as a narrative device that structures the suspect pool. At each new attack, Sir Clinton revisits the alibis, checking who could have been present. The narrowing of possibilities — from many suspects to one — mirrors the reader's own deductive process, making the investigation transparent and engaging.

## Analysis

The alibi in *Murder in the Maze* operates on two levels. At the surface, it is a practical investigative tool — the detective's method for eliminating suspects. But at a deeper level, it functions as a thematic expression of the novel's concern with visibility and concealment. The Maze makes everyone potentially invisible; the alibi is the counter-force, the mechanism for making people visible through verification of their movements.

Connington's treatment of alibis reflects the legal culture of early twentieth-century Britain, where alibi evidence was subject to specific procedural rules. Sir Clinton's methodical verification — cross-checking times, testing travel distances, questioning witness reliability — mirrors actual police practice of the period while dramatising it for fiction.
