# The Affair in the Maze

## Overview

The Affair in the Maze is the double murder of [[roger-shandon|Roger Shandon]] and [[neville-shandon|Neville Shandon]] in the twin centres of the [[the-maze|Whistlefield Maze]] on a single afternoon in late spring. Both brothers are killed by [[darts|curare-tipped darts]] fired from an [[air-rifle|airgun]] at close range — a method that exploits the Maze's twelve-foot hedges to provide the killer with absolute visual concealment. The crime is witnessed aurally by [[vera-forrest|Vera Forrest]], who is lost in the labyrinth at the time, and the bodies are discovered by her companion [[howard-torrance|Howard Torrance]].

The Affair in the Maze is the novel's inciting event and its central puzzle. It is a crime of extraordinary audacity: two victims killed in an enclosed space, in broad daylight, while other people are present within the same labyrinth. The apparent impossibility of the crime — how could the killer enter, shoot, and escape without being seen? — drives the entire investigation and establishes [[sir-clinton-driffield|Sir Clinton Driffield]]'s deductive challenge.

## Sequence of Events

### Prelude: Decisions to Enter the Maze

The chain of events begins in the [[whistlefield|Whistlefield]] study. [[ernest-shandon|Ernest Shandon]], asked to suggest a quiet place for Neville to review his [[the-hackleton-case|Hackleton case]] notes, proposes the Maze: "The thing like the one at Hampton Court, down by the river, close to the boathouse" [001-047]. Neville accepts, declaring "Right. I'll go to Helen's Bower" [001-049].

Roger, overhearing, independently resolves to use the Maze for an afternoon rest. He stakes a claim to the preferred centre:

> "I think I'll try the Maze myself this afternoon. I feel a bit sleepy; and it's quiet in there. I shan't disturb you. But if it's all the same to you, I'll take Helen's Bower myself. I'm used to a chair there; it suits me. You can go to Narcissus's Pool instead." [001-084]

Neville agrees without objection. Neither brother is aware that Vera Forrest and Howard Torrance will shortly decide to "try our luck" with the Maze [002-024], nor that [[arthur-hawkhurst|Arthur Hawkhurst]] — fresh from a bruising quarrel with Roger over his piano-playing — has been seen "coming out of the gun-room with a nasty look in his eye and an airgun in his hand" [002-042].

### The Maze: Into the Labyrinth

Vera and Howard enter the Maze by its riverside gate. The physical properties of the labyrinth are established with forensic precision:

> The Whistlefield Maze was a labyrinth far exceeding in complexity its kindred at Hatfield and Hampton Court. Its twelve-foot hedges were impenetrably thick; and in its design it followed the "island-pattern" to such an extent that incautious explorers might wander by the hour through its tiny archipelago without gaining a foot towards the innermost recesses. [002-053]

The pair separate at the first junction, Vera taking the left path and Howard the right, agreeing that the first to reach either centre wins [002-068]. Vera is soon hopelessly lost, her bearings obliterated by the continuous windings: "When she thought of taking the sun as a reference-point, it was too late; for by that time she had lost all notion of her whereabouts" [002-073].

### The Murders

Alone in the silence, Vera strains to hear Howard's movements. Instead, she hears something far more sinister:

> First came a dull thud, which unconsciously she recognised as familiar, though she could not identify it at the moment. Then, almost at the same instant, a man's voice gave an inarticulate cry in which surprise, pain, and anger seemed to be mingled. A moment of silence, then a peculiar metallic grating reached her ears, followed by a second thud and a fresh cry of pain. Again came the familiar metallic rasping, yet another of these familiar dull concussions, and then, lower this time, a last cry. [002-078]

Vera does not see anything. The towering hedges, as Sir Clinton later notes, provide "absolute privacy guaranteed by the nature of the affair. No one could see through those hedges" [003-049]. She is an earwitness, recording sensory data whose full significance she will not grasp until later, when the word "airgun" triggers the delayed recognition that the "dull concussions" were the reports of an air-rifle and the "metallic rasping" was the grating of its spring being recharged [003-038].

### Discovery

Howard, trying to reach Vera through the Maze, blunders into one of the centres and finds a body. His shout rings through the labyrinth:

> "Murder! See that no one gets away from the Maze!" [002-091]

He has found Roger Shandon in [[helens-bower|Helen's Bower]]. Neville's body is discovered shortly afterwards in the [[pool-of-narcissus|Pool of Narcissus]]. Both have been killed by the same method. The Maze, moments ago a place of idle recreation, has become a double crime scene.

A figure is heard running in the alleys of the Maze — footsteps that cease abruptly when Vera calls out [002-099–002-100]. The killer is still inside the labyrinth, moving through its concealment, and Vera remains terrifyingly vulnerable. She is not harmed. The killer's restraint — or his inability to locate her in the identical passages — spares her life.

## Significance

The Affair in the Maze is the novel's narrative and structural centre. It is the event that transforms a domestic drama — brothers talking in a study, guests playing tennis, a nephew practising the piano — into a criminal investigation. Every subsequent chapter is a response to this event: the gathering of evidence, the testing of hypotheses, the elimination of suspects, and the final identification of the killer.

The crime's audacity is also its clue. Only someone with intimate knowledge of the Maze could navigate it quickly enough to kill two men and escape detection; only someone familiar with the household's movements could time the murders to coincide with Vera and Howard's presence as unwitting alibi-witnesses. The killer's apparent invisibility is itself evidence of familiarity — a paradox that Sir Clinton exploits in his reconstruction.

## Analysis

Connington's choice of a hedge maze as the murder scene is a masterstroke of Golden Age construction. The Maze is simultaneously enclosed and open, private and accessible. It provides the killer with the visual concealment of an indoor crime while offering the multiple access points of an outdoor one. This ambiguity is essential to the puzzle: the crime reads as impossible (how did the killer escape unseen?) until the reader understands that the Maze's very properties — its "island-pattern," its twelve-foot hedges, its disorienting pathways — make the impossible not only possible but inevitable.

The murders also dramatise a central theme of the novel: the gap between sensory data and understanding. Vera hears everything and comprehends nothing. The dull concussions, the metallic rasping, the running footsteps — each is a piece of the truth, accurately recorded but meaningless until interpreted. Sir Clinton's role is not to discover new facts but to arrange existing ones into a coherent pattern. The Affair in the Maze is the raw data; the remaining sixteen chapters are the analysis.

The symbolic dimension of the two centres should not be overlooked. Helen's Bower — named for the most beautiful woman in classical myth, whose abduction launched a war — and the Pool of Narcissus, named for the youth who drowned gazing at his own reflection, are both sites of fatal self-absorption. The two brothers die in separate centres, each isolated in death as they were in life: Roger the worldly adventurer, Neville the relentless prosecutor, united only by shared parentage and a shared killer.
