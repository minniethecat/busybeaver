# Ben Burton

> "When Ben Burton, who succeeded him at the bat, made a good hit, he reached third base." (002-049)

## Role in the Club

Ben Burton played shortstop for the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]], a demanding position that put him in the center of the defensive action. He [[baseball-equipment|bat]]ted after [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]] in the lineup (002-049), suggesting he was considered one of the team's more reliable hitters. His role extended beyond the field — he was a known figure in the community whose [[national-game|baseball]] slang ("a basket of goose eggs") [[alice-howell|Alice Howell]] quoted to her father (001-016).

## Key Appearances

Burton appears throughout the novel's [[baseball-match|baseball contest]]s, most prominently in the opening [[baseball-match|match]] against the [[jonesville-nine|Jonesville Nine]] in Chapter 2. His performance there was mixed: he "made a good hit" to reach third base (002-049) but also "muffed" two ground [[baseball-equipment|ball]]s at shortstop (002-050), errors that cost the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpas]] [[scoring-baseball|runs]].

The second muff was particularly costly — it allowed "[[larry-boyne|Larry]] to come home and [[neddie-ellis|Ellis]] to get to first base" (002-050). However, Burton partially redeemed himself when he "neatly caught out a [[foul-ball|foul fly]]" by [[berthelet|Berthelet]] (002-050), a play described as partially retrieving his reputation.

In the later innings, Burton was "put out in attempting to steal from first to second base" (002-051), capping a difficult day for him personally as the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpas]] fell behind 15–8.

## Playing Style

As shortstop, Burton had "many opportunities to do himself credit" (007-044). The position required quick reflexes to field ground [[baseball-equipment|ball]]s and strong throws to first base. His errors in the [[the-great-match|Jonesville match]] — two muffed [[ground-ball|grounder]]s — highlight the defensive challenges of 19th-century [[national-game|baseball]], where [[fielding-baseball|fielding]] was barehanded or with minimal glove protection.

Burton also contributed on offense, [[baseball-equipment|bat]]ting in the middle of the order and attempting to steal bases. His aggressive [[base-running|baserunning]], while not always successful, reflected the more daring style of 19th-century [[national-game|baseball]].
