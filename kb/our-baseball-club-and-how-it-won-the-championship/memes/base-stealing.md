**Base stealing** (or "stealing bases") is the offensive tactic of a baserunner advancing to the next base during the pitcher's delivery, without the aid of a hit, walk, or defensive error. It requires speed, timing, and the ability to read the pitcher and catcher. In the Catalpa Nine's era, base stealing was a high-risk, high-reward maneuver that could change the course of an inning.

## The Art of Stealing

Successful base stealing was a mark of an aggressive and skilled baserunner. The runner had to judge the pitcher's motion, get a good jump, and beat the catcher's throw:

> In the eighth inning, he made a beautiful run, stealing two bases just as easy as falling off a log. (007-044)

A stolen base was celebrated as a display of individual daring that contributed to the team's offensive momentum.

## The Risk of Getting Caught

For every successful steal, there was the ignominy of being thrown out. The margin between triumph and disaster was thin:

> Al Heaton, who led for the home nine, was put out in attempting to steal from first to second base, and Ben Burton, who followed him, met with a similar disaster. (002-051)

> Charlie attempted to steal to second base but was thrown out by Billy Webb, and the ardor of the spirits of Catalpa was consequently soon dampened. (011-035)

## Stealing and the Catcher

The catcher was the primary obstacle to a successful steal. A strong, accurate throw from behind the plate could cut down even the fastest runner:

> Larry Boyne was the first man to the bat for the Catalpas in the sixth inning, and he secured his base on called balls, but fell before Charlie Webb's throwing, while trying to steal to the second bag. (011-052)

## Strategic Value

Base stealing was more than athletic display — it was a strategic weapon. A runner on second base was in "scoring position," meaning a base hit could drive him home. By stealing second, a baserunner doubled the offense's chance of scoring. This made the steal attempt a calculated gamble: the reward of advancing into scoring position versus the risk of recording an out on the basepaths.
