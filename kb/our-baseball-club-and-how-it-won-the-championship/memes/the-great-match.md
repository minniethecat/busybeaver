# The Great Match

> "The great baseball match was set for two o'clock, that afternoon." (001-001)

## The Build-Up

The match between the Catalpa Nine and the Jonesville club was the event the entire town had anticipated. Alice Howell woke on the morning of the game and "ran to the window to see what were the signs of the sky" (001-001), anxious that weather might postpone the contest. This was to be "the last game of the season, and the decision of the championship" (001-005).

The Catalpas were in "splendid form" (001-016), and the community had turned out in force. Spectators streamed in from the surrounding countryside — "strings of buggies, lumber-wagons and other vehicles; and not a few sightseers jogged along on horseback" (002-001).

## The Game

The match unfolded as a tense, closely followed contest. The Jonesville Nine opened strongly, scoring two runs in the first innings (002-033). The Catalpas responded, and the score seesawed. "The winning of the toss was deemed a matter of importance by the sympathetic friends of the home club" (002-031), showing how every advantage was eagerly seized.

The Jonesville fielding proved decisive. Their "fielding...was far superior to what their previous encounters had shown" (002-009), and the Catalpa batters struggled against Sam Ellis's pitching. Ben Burton's two muffed ground balls at shortstop (002-050) cost the Catalpas dearly, and though he partially redeemed himself with a caught foul fly, the damage was done.

## The Aftermath

The final score — though not given in full — heavily favored Jonesville. The Catalpas had been beaten decisively, just as they had been in the previous year's championship game (24-13). Walking off the field, the Catalpa players faced the grim reality: "the championship of the whole State would belong to the Jonesville boys" (002-076) unless the club could reorganize and improve.

The defeat catalyzed the reorganization of the Catalpa club in Chapter 3, making this match the pivotal event that set the novel's plot in motion.
