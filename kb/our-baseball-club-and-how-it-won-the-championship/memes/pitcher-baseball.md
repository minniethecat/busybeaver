# Pitcher (Baseball)

> "Sam Ellis, the champion pitcher of the Jonesvilles, cannot hold a candle to Bill Van Orman, the Dean County Nine's catcher." (003-004)

## Definition

The pitcher in 19th-century baseball delivered the ball to the batter, but the role differed significantly from modern pitching. Pitchers threw underhand or sidearm, and the emphasis was more on fairness and control than on velocity and deception. Over the course of the 1880s — the era in which the novel is set — pitching was evolving from a purely "delivery" role toward the strategic position it would become.

## Pitching Style and Tactics

Pitchers of this era were beginning to experiment with deceptive deliveries. The Calumets' pitcher was known for his "puzzling curves" (011-035), a reference to the nascent art of the curveball. When batters "wildly struck the air three times" (011-035), it was as much a testament to the pitcher's skill as to the batter's failure.

Effective pitching was critical to a team's success. Sam Ellis of the Jonesville club was described as the "champion pitcher" (002-033), while Bill Van Orman, though primarily a catcher for Dean County, was "the big pitcher of the Dean County Nine" (002-034). The versatility expected of players meant that a team's best catcher might also be among its best pitchers.

## Notable Pitchers in the Narrative

- **Sam Ellis**: Champion pitcher of the Jonesville club (002-033, 002-049)
- **Bill Van Orman**: Big pitcher for the Dean County Nine (002-034), also a catcher (003-004)
- **Harris**: Pitcher for the Black Hawks, whom the Catalpas faced (007-044)
- **Samuel Morse**: Pitcher for the Calumets (011-006)
- **Neddie Ellis**: Described as "as good a pitcher as there is in the country" (004-013)
