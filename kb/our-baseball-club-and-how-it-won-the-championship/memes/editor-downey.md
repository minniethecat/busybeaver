## Identity

Editor Downey was the editor and publisher of [[the-catalpa-leaf|The Catalpa Leaf]], the only daily newspaper in [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]], Illinois. A man of enterprise and occasional comical dishevelment, Downey was the town's primary source of information — especially during the [[national-game|baseball]] season, when his office window became [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]'s de facto [[scoring-baseball|score]]board (007-034).

> PN: (007-034)

## Role

### Newspaperman

As editor of [[the-catalpa-leaf|The Catalpa Leaf]], Downey was responsible for gathering and disseminating news to the citizens of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]. His paper was the town's daily record, and during the [[championship-baseball|championship]] campaign, it became something more — a real-time sports information service (010-015).

> PN: (010-015)

### Bulletin Board Master

Downey's most visible role was as the keeper of the telegraph bulletins. When [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]] sent dispatches from distant [[baseball-equipment|ball]] fields, it was Downey who received them and posted them — on large sheets of white paper with bold black lettering — from his office window to the [[baseball-spectators|crowd]] gathered below. The scene was repeated throughout the season: (007-039).

> PN: (007-039)

> Editor Downey put out of his office window his uncovered head, every hair of which stood up with excitement, as he bawled, "Sixth inning, Catalpas, no runs; Black Hawks, two!"

### Comic Figure

Downey's bulletin-posting was not without mishaps. His most memorable moment came when he made an error in the inning-by-inning scoring, forgetting to include an inning's results — and hastily corrected himself in the face of the [[baseball-spectators|crowd]]'s groans and catcalls:

> "It was a mistake. I forgot to put on the sixth inning. Catalpas, one; Galenas, nix!"

His tousled hair, ink-smudged face, and flustered corrections made him a beloved figure — the town's reliable-yet-fallible newsman, doing his best under the pressure of a community hanging on every telegraphic word.

### Gatekeeper of News

Downey exercised editorial judgment about what to publish. When discouraging news arrived by wire late at night about the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpas]] being beaten by the [[zoo-zoo-nine|Zoo-Zoo Nine]], he "did not think it worth while to post on his bulletin board" — preferring to let the town sleep rather than spread bad news after hours.

## In the Story

Downey appears primarily in the chapters describing the community's reception of [[national-game|baseball]] news. He is at his office window during the [[state-championship-catalpa-calumets|championship game]] bulletins, his head bare to the elements, his hair standing on end with excitement. A grimy young imp on the roof of [[the-catalpa-leaf|The Leaf]] building assists him by hoisting flags to signal important news.

His role is essential to the novel's depiction of how a 19th-century small town followed distant events — the editor was the bridge between the telegraph wire and the public, transforming electronic dots and dashes into the shared experience of the [[baseball-spectators|crowd]] below.
