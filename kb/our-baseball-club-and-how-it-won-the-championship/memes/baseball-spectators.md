# Baseball Spectators

> "The criticism of many a loud-talking spectator." (002-009)

## Who Attended

A 19th-century baseball match drew spectators from every stratum of the community. On game day, "a steady stream of people poured into town" (002-002), arriving by "strings of buggies, lumber-wagons and other vehicles; and not a few sightseers jogged along on horseback" (002-001). Farmers, townspeople, professionals, and laborers all gathered at the fair grounds.

Women were a significant portion of the audience. The Introduction notes that "even among the gentler sex, who may be regarded as spectators only of the game, there is to be found much sound information and an intelligent acquaintance with the details of baseball playing" (P01-001). Alice Howell's passionate following of the Catalpa club exemplifies the educated female spectator of the era.

## Spectator Behavior

Spectators were not passive observers. They "chaffed each other vociferously and exchanged learned comments on the playing" (002-003), turning the crowd into an active participant in the event. The "criticism of many a loud-talking spectator" (002-009) was a constant feature of the match, creating an atmosphere of shared expertise and partisan passion.

The crowd's mood shifted with the game's fortunes. "A murmur of approbation from the spectators, who were now warming up to the contest" (002-033) marked good plays, while "the spirits of the sympathetic spectators rose perceptibly" (002-043) when the home team rallied.

## The Partisan Crowd

Spectators were overwhelmingly partisan toward their local club. The "sympathetic friends of the home club" (002-033) cheered every success and groaned at every failure. The crowd was said to be "sympathetic spectators" (002-043) — the word "sympathetic" carrying its 19th-century meaning of sharing the feelings of the players.

For the players, the crowd's presence could be inspiring or intimidating. Playing before "the eyes of the multitude" (002-008) added pressure, as every error was witnessed and judged by hundreds of knowledgeable observers.

## Social Gathering

A baseball match was as much a social occasion as a sporting event. People came from surrounding towns, reuniting with friends and exchanging news. The gathering at the fair grounds was one of the major community events of the season, rivaling agricultural fairs and traveling circuses in drawing crowds.
