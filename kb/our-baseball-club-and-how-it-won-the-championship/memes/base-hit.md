A **Base Hit** (also called a **safe hit**) is the fundamental offensive play in baseball: a ball struck by the batter that lands in fair territory and enables the batter to reach first base (or beyond) safely, without the aid of a defensive error or a fielder's choice. It is the primary mechanism by which offensive players advance on the bases and by which runs are scored.

## Definition and Mechanics

A base hit occurs when the batter strikes a pitched ball into fair territory and reaches base before the defensive team can field the ball and make a play. The hit is credited to the batter's statistics and is distinguished from:

- Reaching base on a **field error** (muffed ball)
- Reaching base on a **fielder's choice** (defense chose to put out another runner)
- Reaching base on **called balls** (a base on balls, or walk)

The distinction was well understood by the players and spectators of the Catalpa club's era, as the narrative's detailed account of each inning demonstrates:

> Lew Morris, tall, handsome and sinewy, deserved the praises lavished upon him, as he stood, modestly but confidently, to open the innings for the Catalpas. But, to the great disappointment of his admirers, he failed to make a hit and was sent to first base on three called balls. (002-036)

## Types of Base Hits

The narrative provides rich examples of the varieties of base hitting prevalent in 19th-century baseball:

| Type | Description | Example from the Text |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Ground ball** | Ball struck along the ground | "a ground ball to third base" (002-047) |
| **Air ball / Fly ball** | Ball struck high into the air | "a magnificent air ball so high that it seemed lost in the misty blue of the October sky" (002-063) |
| **Line drive / Right fielder** | Ball hit sharply to a specific field | "a splendid right fielder" (002-047) |
| **Slow ball** | Softly hit ball that lands between fielders | "a slow ball to center field" (002-050) |
| **Safe hit** | A clean hit allowing the batter to reach base | "Wheeler made a capital safe hit to center field" (002-047) |
| **Infield hit** | Ball that stays within the infield but allows batter to reach | "an infield hit" (013-052) |

## The Hit in Game Strategy

Base hits were the engine of run production. The narrative's play-by-play accounts show how hits cascaded to produce scoring:

> Charlie King justified the expectations of his friends by striking a tremendous ball to right field, on which Lew Morris tallied one, but in trying to get to second base, was put out by Studley in excellent style. Hart Stirling followed, making the first quarter, and Will Sprague went to second base on a strong hit to right field, which brought Stirling home. (002-036)

A well-executed base hit could completely shift the momentum of an inning. [[larry-boyne|Larry Boyne]] demonstrated this power when, in the critical sixth inning, he "made a grand hit to the center field... on which he made first base and brought home Alexander and Studley, who were on the second and third bases, respectively" (002-062).

## Distinguished from Other Offensive Outcomes

The narrative carefully distinguishes between achievements reached by hitting and those reached by other means:

- **Called balls (walks)**: Sam Alexander "walked to first base on called balls, and Martin cleverly made his home run" — the walk advanced the runner without a hit (002-046)
- **Muffed balls (errors)**: Ben Burton's repeated errors allowed Jonesville runners to reach base and advance, producing runs without hits
- **Foul tips**: John Brubaker "went out on a foul tip" — the opposite of a fair hit (002-036)
