## Event Summary

A Pride Has a Fall is the second game of the three-game championship series between the Catalpa Nine and the Calumets, played in Chicago under foggy conditions. Despite leading early by 3–0 after one inning and holding a lead through four innings, the Catalpas collapsed in the fifth inning — surrendering four runs to the Calumets — and ultimately fell 7–9. The defeat leveled the series at one game apiece and set up a decisive third game at Catalpa. The game was marked by Ben Burton's disruptive behavior, internal team tensions, and the humbling lesson that gave the chapter its name.

> PN: (001-014)

## Historical Context

Following the Catalpa Nine's victory over the Galenas in the semifinal (chronicled in How the Good News Came) and their arrival in Chicago for the championship series, this game was the middle contest of a three-game set. The Catalpas had won the opening game, putting them one victory away from the championship. The game was played in a vast Chicago amphitheater before a large crowd, with the visiting Catalpas having gained local fame for their earlier exploits.

## Narrative

### A Bad Omen in the Fog

The day began under inauspicious conditions — fog and dampness covered Chicago as the Catalpa Nine shivered in the chilly air before their preliminary practice. Someone remarked it was a bad day for Catalpa, though Larry Boyne laughed off the superstition. The gloom deepened when Ben Burton disappeared before the game, returning flushed and red-faced — suspected by teammate Sam Morrison of visiting pool rooms.

### The Game Begins

The two teams met under brightening skies, with the crowd at the vast amphitheater — larger than the previous day's due to the visiting nine's growing reputation — giving "The Cats" a cheery welcome. The Catalpas lost the toss, which Neddie Ellis called another evil sign. Despite the omens, the Catalpas started strong.

### Early Dominance

The Catalpas came out swinging in the first inning, scoring three runs and taking an early lead. Captain Hiram justified his conviction that the team would do well by hitting the first ball pitched for a base hit. The Catalpas held a lead of 6–3 through four innings.

### The Fifth Inning Collapse

The game turned in the decisive fifth inning. The Calumets mounted their comeback, scoring four runs to surge ahead. The Catalpa defense faltered — Ben Burton's distracted play contributed, and the team's earlier confidence evaporated. What had looked like a commanding position dissolved into a 6–7 deficit.

### Aftermath: The Decisive Game Is Set

Following the defeat, Captain Hiram Porter approached the Calumet captain with a challenge:

> Meet us at Catalpa. Meet us at Catalpa, and we will try hard to retrieve the ill fortune of this day.

The agreement had already been reached that the third and concluding game of the championship series would be played at Catalpa if the Calumets won the second game. With the series now tied 1–1, the stage was set for the climactic final game on Catalpa's home ground.

## Team Dynamics

The game exposed fissures within the Catalpa Nine. Ben Burton's pre-game disappearance and subsequent lackluster play drew suspicion from his teammates. Sam Morrison in particular believed Burton had been visiting pool rooms, and the internal discord appeared to affect the team's performance — the Catalpas committed five errors to the Calumets' seven, but the Calumets' runs came at more critical moments.

## Significance in the Story

This game represents the critical turning point in the championship narrative. The title "Pride Has a Fall" captures the moral dimension — the Catalpas' overconfidence, particularly Ben Burton's, meets its comeuppance. The defeat strips away complacency and sets up the dramatic necessity of a third and deciding game at home. By losing, the Catalpas learn humility, and the stakes for the final game at Catalpa are raised to their highest pitch.

## In the Book

The event is narrated in Chapter XIII of the novel ("Pride Has a Fall"). The chapter's box-score ending (reproducing the full game summary as it appeared on bulletin boards) is one of the novel's most distinctive structural features, treating the baseball game with the formal statistical gravity of a professional contest.
