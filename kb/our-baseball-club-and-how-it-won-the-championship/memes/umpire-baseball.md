# Umpire (Baseball)

> "The umpire called 'six balls.'" (011-036)

## Role and Authority

The umpire was the sole on-field authority during a 19th-century baseball match. He called balls and strikes, ruled on fair and foul balls, and determined whether runners were safe or out. His decisions were final, though spectators and players alike freely voiced their opinions of his judgment.

In the Catalpa-Calumet match, the umpire from the Black Hawks "took his place as umpire" (011-010) — a neutral official brought in from a third club to ensure impartiality. When the umpire "called to the umpire 'play!'" (011-031), the game was officially underway under his authority.

## The Umpire's Calls

The umpire's calls structured the tactical rhythm of the game:

- **Strikes**: "The third strike called by the umpire" (011-039) retired a batter, one of the most consequential calls in the sport
- **Balls**: "The umpire called 'six balls'" (011-036), awarding the batter a walk to first base. The six-ball count (rather than the modern four) was standard in 19th-century baseball
- **Called balls and strikes**: Multiple references to "called balls successively called by the umpire" (011-049) show how the umpire's judgment directly shaped the outcome of at-bats

## The Umpire's Place

The umpire's role was both respected and criticized. "The criticism of many a loud-talking spectator" (002-009) was directed not only at players but at officials. The umpire had to maintain order amid the "sympathetic spectators" (002-043) whose partisan passions could spill over into the field of play.

## In the Narrative

The umpire appears most prominently in Chapter 11 during the Catalpa-Calumet match, where his calls on strikes, balls, and outs drive the dramatic tension of a close contest. His presence as an impartial arbiter from a neutral club reflects the organized but informal nature of 19th-century amateur baseball governance.
