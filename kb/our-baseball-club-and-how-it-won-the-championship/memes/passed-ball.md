A **passed ball** is a defensive misplay in baseball charged against the catcher when a pitched ball that should have been caught with ordinary effort gets past, allowing baserunners to advance. In the Catalpa Nine's era, passed balls were a significant factor in the flow of the game, often enabling runs that would not otherwise have scored.

## Passed Balls in the Catalpa Contests

Passed balls occurred frequently enough to play a decisive role in the outcomes of games, enabling baserunners to advance into scoring position or even cross home plate:

> Next, Martin got to first base on a slow ball to right field, and then home on passed balls. (002-048)

> During the passage of the ball, however, Morrison came home, and Ellis subsequently tallied on a passed ball. (002-050)

## Advancing on Passed Balls

A passed ball was particularly valuable to the offense because it allowed runners to advance without the batter needing to make contact. An alert baserunner could take an extra base on a passed ball, turning a routine pitch into a scoring opportunity:

> While Jamie Kennedy was at the bat, a passed ball allowed Shoff to complete three quarters of his homeward journey. (011-037)

> While Neddie Ellis was at the bat, a passed ball allowed Larry to get around to third base. (013-050)

## Strategic Considerations

Managers and captains sometimes instructed batters to wait for a good pitch and accept the risk of a passed ball:

> He had been ordered not to strike at a ball until it was put where he asked for it, and to take the chances of the catcher of the Catalpas having a passed ball charged to him. (011-056)

The passed ball was thus both a defensive failure and an offensive opportunity, a reminder that in 19th-century baseball, every pitch — even one the batter did not swing at — could change the game.

## Distinction from Wild Pitch

In modern scoring, a passed ball is distinguished from a wild pitch — the former being the catcher's fault, the latter the pitcher's. In the Catalpa Nine's era, the distinction was less formalized, but the practical effect was the same: a ball that got away from the catcher allowed baserunners to advance at no cost to the offense.
