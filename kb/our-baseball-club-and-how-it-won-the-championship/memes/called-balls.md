**Called balls** was the 19th-century baseball term for what modern baseball calls a "walk" or "base on balls." When a pitcher failed to deliver the ball within the strike zone on four occasions, the umpire "called" those pitches as balls, and the batter was awarded first base. The called ball was a fundamental mechanism of the game, balancing the contest between pitcher and batter.

## How Called Balls Worked

The rule was straightforward: four pitches outside the strike zone entitled the batter to first base. Pitchers who could not find the strike zone paid the price:

> He failed to make a hit and was sent to first base on three called balls. (002-036)

(The reference to "three" called balls reflects the evolving rules of the era, when the number of balls required for a walk varied before settling at four.)

## Called Balls in Catalpa's Games

The Catalpa Nine's contests featured numerous instances of batters reaching base on called balls. A patient batter could work the count and take his base without ever swinging:

> Rob Peabody secured first base on called balls, and was followed at the bat by Shoff who hit a grounder to Hart Stirling. (011-044)

> Larry Boyne was the first man to the bat for the Catalpas in the sixth inning, and he secured his base on called balls. (011-052)

## Called Balls vs. Strikes

The dynamic between called balls and called strikes was the engine of every at-bat. A pitcher who threw too many balls risked giving free passes; a batter who waited too long risked being called out on strikes:

> Charlie King went out on called balls, and, amidst cries of "another whitewash!" the side was retired. (002-061)

The called ball was thus both an opportunity for the offense and a hazard for the defense, demanding control and discipline from both pitcher and batter alike.

## Historical Context

In 19th-century baseball, the number of balls required for a walk changed over time — early rules required as many as nine balls. By the era of the Catalpa Nine, the modern standard of four balls (or occasionally three, depending on local rules) was taking hold. The called ball, together with the called strike, formed the strike zone that defined the game's central confrontation.
