The **Final Game** was the decisive championship contest between the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]] and the [[calumets|Calumets]], played at the Diamond Field in Catalpa. It represented the culmination of a long and fiercely contested season, and its outcome determined the state baseball championship. The game was played against a backdrop of intense civic pride, personal drama — including the exposure of [[ben-burton|Ben Burton]]'s attempted game-throwing conspiracy — and the concentrated hopes of an entire town.

## Context

The championship series between the Catalpas and the Calumets had been "already made sufficiently notable by the narrowness of the margin left for the two contestants" (017-001). The two clubs had traded victories throughout the season, setting the stage for a winner-take-all final match. Adding to the drama was the recent discovery that Ben Burton, one of the Catalpa Nine's own players, had conspired with [[hank-jackson|Hank Jackson]] to throw a game for betting profit — a scandal that had become "town-talk, but (thanks to the discretion of the players) not known outside of Catalpa" (017-001).

> It was a great day for baseball when the far-famed Calumet club came to Catalpa to play the home nine. (017-001)

## The Occasion

The Calumets arrived by the evening train on the day before the contest and were met at the station by the greater part of the Catalpa club, who escorted their visitors to the hotel — a display of the sportsmanship that characterized the rivalry despite its intensity (017-001).

The morning of the game saw the entire town mobilized:

> Every man who had a flag hung it out to the breeze. Jedediah Van Orman, "The Lily's" father, took up a collection from the willing shopkeepers and bought a supply of powder, with which he proceeded to fire a salute from four anvils, the only artillery then accessible in the town. (012-027)

## Spectators and Atmosphere

The crowd assembled in force, drawn from every quarter of Catalpa and the surrounding countryside:

- **[[judge-howell|Judge Howell]]** attended with his carriage, his daughter **[[alice-howell|Alice Howell]]**, and his sister Miss Anstress — the latter "grimly admitting, as if under great mental pressure, that she did hope that the Catalpas would beat" (017-004)
- **"Rough and Ready"** — the old veteran — was "alert and spry as a lad of nineteen, making himself very busy trimming the flags, inspecting the grounds, and running of errands for the players, conscious that but for him the game could not go on" (017-004)
- **Tom Selby**, editor of *The Leaf*, posted bulletins from [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]] and his father at frequent intervals, maintaining public connection with the contest (012-027)

A great and tumultuous cheer erupted when the two nines, clad in their uniforms, emerged from the unpainted little buildings near the judges' stand (017-004).

## The Contest

The game was played at the **Diamond Field**, the premier baseball venue in Catalpa. The contest was closely fought, with the Calumets demonstrating superior fielding and the Catalpas relying on their batting strength — a tactical dynamic that had defined the season. In one critical inning:

> John Brubaker hit a ball to Kennedy who sent it to first base in a manner that won the plaudits of the crowds intently watching the contest from the seats around the huge amphitheater. Captain Porter hit a fly to left field which was captured by McWilliams in wonderfully fine style, and Ben Burton struck out. (013-052)

The Calumets extended their lead with disciplined play: Kennedy made a base hit, advanced on a passed ball, reached third on Burton's error, and Jamie scored on McWilliams's out at first base (013-052).

## Aftermath

The final result sent Catalpa into a state of collective euphoria:

> The excitement waxed high as the contest proceeded, and when the final result was reached, the town was fairly mad with joy. The event had eclipsed everything of the kind that had happened during the season. (012-027)

> Victory brooded over Catalpa, and in every house as the red sun went down, that night, there was but one theme of conversation — baseball. (012-027)

The championship victory represented more than athletic achievement: it was a validation of the town's identity, a repudiation of the conspiracy that had threatened to undermine the club from within, and the triumphant conclusion to a season that had begun with the humble [[first-practice|First Practice]] months earlier.
