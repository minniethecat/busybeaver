**[[al-heaton|Heaton]]'s flouring mills** were a manufacturing business in [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]], owned by Mr. [[al-heaton|Heaton]], the father of [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]]. The mill was located near the dam on the Stone River and served as one of the town's principal industrial establishments.

## Mr. Heaton's Counting-Room

The mill complex contained Mr. [[al-heaton|Heaton]]'s counting-room, an office where the miller conducted his business affairs. It was here that significant decisions about the [[national-game|baseball]] club were made:

> And when, his little errands done, he found his way to Mr. Heaton's counting-room in the mills near the dam, he had made up his mind that Jonesville had no claim on him and that he belonged no more to Jonesville than he did to Catalpa. (004-008)

## The Miller and the Nine

Mr. [[al-heaton|Heaton]] was frequently identified by his occupation. When he delivered the joyous news of the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]]'s victory at [[sandy-key|Sandy Key]], he was described as "the beaming miller" reading a telegram to an anxious [[baseball-spectators|crowd]] at the telegraph office (007-025).

## Role in the Town

[[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]] was "a busy manufacturing and milling community" (001-011), and the men who worked in "the flouring mills and the lumberyards along the river" (001-012) formed part of the town's industrial backbone. [[al-heaton|Heaton]]'s mills were a key part of this economy, and messengers regularly hurried between the mill and the telegraph office with news and instructions (015-005).

## Connection to the Baseball Club

Mr. [[al-heaton|Heaton]]'s position as a mill owner gave him the means and standing to be one of the principal backers of the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]]. His counting-room served as an informal headquarters where [[national-game|baseball]] matters were discussed and decisions were made, linking the town's industrial life with its sporting ambitions.
