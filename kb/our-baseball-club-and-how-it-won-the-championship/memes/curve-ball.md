The **curve ball** was one of the most effective and feared weapons in 19th-century pitching. By imparting spin to the ball, a skilled pitcher could make it deviate from a straight path — curving in toward the batter, away from the batter, or dropping as it approached the plate. Mastering and hitting the curve ball separated accomplished pitchers and batters from mere participants.

## The Mysteries of the Curve

Pitchers who commanded the curve ball were regarded with a mixture of admiration and suspicion. The physics of the curve were not universally understood, lending the pitch an air of mystery:

> The Calumets now went to work with a will at the beginning of their second inning, and, after receiving some hints from Jamie Kennedy, who assumed to know a little about the mysteries of King's curves. (011-036)

## King's Curves and Morse's Out-Curves

Each pitcher had his own signature curve. Charlie King of the Catalpas and Sam Morse both employed deceptive curves that confounded opposing batters:

> He made a second attempt, however, and was retired without hitting the ball, Sam Morse's out-curves being more intricate than anything that he had yet encountered. (011-046)

The "out-curve" moved away from the batter, while the "in-curve" or "inward curve" moved toward the batter's hands:

> Hart Stirling was deceived by a few sharp inward curves from the pitcher of the Calumets and retired to his seat without hitting a ball. (013-039)

## The Deceptive Curves of the Catalpas' Pitcher

The curve ball was not merely a physical feat but a psychological weapon. Batters who had been "deceived" by curves lost confidence at the plate:

> McWilliams went down before the deceptive curves of the Catalpas' pitcher. (017-012)

## Countering the Curve

Experienced batters learned to read the spin and anticipate the break. The key was watching the ball out of the pitcher's hand and adjusting mid-swing. When a batter successfully hit a curve ball — "stepping backwards a few inches and hitting it with all his might" (011-058) — it was a triumph of skill over deception, celebrated by teammates and spectators alike.
