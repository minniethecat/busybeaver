**Rough and Ready** — whose given name was **Tony May** — was one of the most memorable characters of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]], Illinois: an aged loafer, errand man, Civil War veteran, and devoted [[national-game|baseball]] enthusiast. Instantly recognizable by his ever-present coonskin cap, worn "in the heats of summer as in winter" (009-001), Rough and Ready was a fixture of the town's streets, bridge, and [[national-game|baseball]] grounds, serving as an unofficial messenger, news-bearer, and one-man cheering section for the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]].

## Identity

### Name and Origin

His nickname derived from his own habitual self-description:

> Tony May, an aged and disreputable loafer in the store where this debate was taking place... was usually called "Rough and Ready" because of his frequent use of that phrase as applied to himself. (003-006)

He was fond of excusing his intrusions into public discourse with the refrain "'Scuse me, Jedge, I didn't mean to be interruptious, but you know I'm rough and ready, rough and ready, Jedge" (003-006). [[alice-howell|Alice Howell]] affectionately shortened the nickname to "Roughy" — a familiarity he accepted with evident pleasure (009-013).

### The Coonskin Cap

Rough and Ready's coonskin cap was his signature, worn regardless of season — a detail that the narrator emphasizes repeatedly, noting he wore it "in the heats of summer as in winter" (009-001) and that he would pull it down over his shaggy brows when embarrassed or retreating from social scrutiny (003-006).

### Family

Rough and Ready had a son, Fremont May, who played as the right fielder for the [[dean-county-nine|Dean County Nine]]. [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]] acknowledged this connection during the town's [[national-game|baseball]] [[reorganization-catalpa-club|reorganization]] de[[baseball-equipment|bat]]e, noting that Fremont "has certainly distinguished himself, before now, as the right fielder of the [[dean-county-nine|Dean County Nine]]" (003-007). Rough and Ready credited [[larry-boyne|Larry Boyne]] with teaching his son the game:

> "Larry! why, it was him what give my boy all the points he has got in the game, and you may lay your bottom dollar that that boy is goin' to be the all-firedest batter in the Stone River country." (009-004)

## Role in the Story

### Town Messenger and News-Bearer

Rough and Ready served as [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]'s informal communications network, carrying bundles, running errands, and — most importantly — relaying [[national-game|baseball]] news. When storekeepers wanted an errand run, they hung out a little blue flag, and Rough and Ready would "shamble in" to see what was wanted (009-001).

He was the first to bring news of the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]]'s victory at [[galena|Galena]] to [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]], meeting [[judge-howell|the Judge]] at the train station:

> "Any baggage, Jedge? carry it as cheap as anybody. Our nine has flaxed out the Galenas — eight to three! Big thing, Jedge! Lemme take that gripsack. Great day for old Catalpa, Jedge." (010-038)

### Baseball Enthusiast

Rough and Ready's passion for [[national-game|baseball]] was absolute. When [[alice-howell|Alice Howell]] shared early-game news from her window, his response was immediate and physical:

> "Hooray for our side!" cried Rough and Ready, exultingly. "I have heard it from the gentle Miss Ally. Our boys have made ten runs in the first three innings, and the Galena fellows have made one — one whole one." (009-016)

Such was his absorption in the game that he would forget his errands entirely: "If I didn't go and leave that ther bundle of sheetin' in [[judge-howell|the Judge]]'s dooryard! 'Pears to me as if that pesky [[national-game|baseball]] had knocked my wits clean out" (009-018).

### At the Championship Final

At the climactic final game against the [[calumets|Calumets]], Rough and Ready was everywhere at once:

> There, too, was old Rough and Ready, alert and spry as a lad of nineteen, making himself very busy trimming the flags, inspecting the grounds, and running of errands for the players, conscious that but for him the game could not go on. (017-004)

His omnipresence at the Diamond Field reflected his self-appointed role as the club's most devoted supporter.

## Character and Speech

Rough and Ready's speech was marked by vivid colloquialisms and a distinctive voice:

| Phrase | Context |
|--------|---------|
| "Right as a trivet, Jedge" | Expressing agreement with [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]] (003-012) |
| "I'm rough and ready, you know" | His signature self-description (003-006) |
| "Sarves me right!" | After being rebuked by Miss Anstress (009-012) |
| "our nine has flaxed out the [[galena|Galenas]]" | Reporting victory to [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]] (010-038) |
| "that gal never went back on [[baseball-positions|the nine]]" | Praising [[alice-howell|Alice Howell]]'s loyalty (010-007) |

Despite his low social standing, [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]] treated him with a measure of respect, acknowledging his "enthusiasm" and his right to participate in the civic [[national-game|baseball]] discussion (003-007). Rough and Ready repaid this with fierce loyalty, declaring of [[judge-howell|the Judge]]: "I allow that the Jedge here speaks the senterments of the community" (003-012).

## Significance

Rough and Ready represents the democratic spirit of 19th-century [[national-game|baseball]] — the idea that the game belonged to everyone, from district judges to aged loafers in coonskin caps. His unwavering support for the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]], his practical usefulness as town messenger, and his irrepressible enthusiasm made him, in his own eyes and in the eyes of many townspeople, an essential part of the [[championship-baseball|championship]] campaign. He embodies the community-wide investment in the [[national-game|baseball]] club that was [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]'s defining characteristic.
