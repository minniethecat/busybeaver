The **After the Battle** period refers to the weeks following the Catalpa Nine's humiliating defeat by the Jonesville club. The loss ignited a passionate town-wide debate about what the club needed to succeed — divided between those who argued for more skill and training, and those who insisted on raw physical strength.

## The Town's Reaction

The defeat struck Catalpa deeply:

> To say that the town of Catalpa was very deeply mortified by this latest and most signal defeat of the favorite Nine would be a mild way of putting the case. For weeks afterwards, nothing was talked of in the place but the disgraceful overthrow of the Catalpa Nine. (003-001)

Two factions quickly formed: one blaming the Catalpas for inadequate training, the other excusing them as simply outmatched by the physically superior Jonesville farmers (003-001).

## Skill vs. Muscle: The Great Debate

The town's discussion crystallized around a fundamental disagreement about the nature of baseball excellence.

Squire Mead led the argument for skill:

> "I tell you it is not mere brute muscle that our fellows want, it's not brawn, but skill, that they must acquire before they can stand up against the baseball players of this part of the country. Let them pay more attention to work, and less to frills, and they will come out all right." (003-002)

Dr. Selby countered with the case for physical power, citing his son Tom — "not only a wiry and agile player in the field, but he was the best oarsman on the river, and he could lift a barrel of flour, properly slung, 'without turning a hair'" (003-003). He also praised Bill Van Orman, the Dean County catcher, who "could stave in the panel of that door with one blow of his fist, but who gets about the bases as spry as a cat" (003-004).

## Judge Howell's Intervention

Judge Howell offered a reconciling position that carried the day:

> "What we want, gentlemen, is muscle and training. I am confident that in this good town of Catalpa there are more than nine young men who can give time to the practice necessary for the purpose, and who are endowed by nature with the requisite powers for the development of first-rate baseball players." (003-005)

His speech drew an impulsive endorsement from Tony May ("Rough and Ready"), the town loafer, who burst out: "you know I'm rough and ready, rough and ready, Jedge, and that 'ere remark of yourn does seem to be about the fust sensible thing I've hearn in this 'ere jag of words" (003-006).

## A Strategic Proposal: Larry Boyne

The debate took a practical turn when Judge Howell raised the possibility of recruiting talent from outside Catalpa. He specifically inquired about Larry Boyne of the Jonesville Nine (003-007). Storekeeper Jason Elderkin provided the first intelligence on the young Irish player:

> "He lives with his mother over to Sugar Grove, and started in to read law with 'Squire Welby, over to Dean Center; but he had to give it up on account of his father's being killed by being crushed under a tree that he was felling." (003-009)

This revelation — that Boyne was being paid by the Jonesvilles — stirred fresh controversy, with some arguing that paid players would make the club professional rather than amateur (003-010).

## Significance

The "After the Battle" debate was a pivotal moment in the Catalpa club's development. The town's intense engagement demonstrated how deeply baseball had become woven into community identity. Judge Howell's middle-ground philosophy — combining training with natural ability — would shape the club's approach to reorganization, and the mention of Larry Boyne foreshadowed his eventual recruitment as a cornerstone of the reformed Catalpa Nine.
