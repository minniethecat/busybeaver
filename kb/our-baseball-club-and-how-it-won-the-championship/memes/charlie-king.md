# Charlie King

> "Let them wait until Al Heaton and Charlie King get after them." (002-034)

## Role in the Club

Charlie King was a player for the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]], frequently mentioned alongside [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]] as one of the club's reliable performers. His name was invoked by supporters as a source of confidence — "let them wait until [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]] and Charlie King get after them" (002-034) — indicating that King was viewed as a difference-maker capable of turning a game around.

King appeared in the [[baseball-equipment|bat]]ting order and contributed defensively. In the [[the-great-match|Jonesville match]], he "sharply followed Morris to first base" (002-049) though he was forced out. He later "hit an air [[baseball-equipment|ball]] which was caught by [[alexander|Alexander]] at shortstop" (002-051), one of several [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]] [[baseball-equipment|bat]]ters who struggled against the [[jonesville-nine|Jonesville]] [[fielding-baseball|fielding]].

## Key Appearances

In the Chapter 2 [[baseball-match|match]] against [[jonesville-nine|Jonesville]], King's day at the plate reflected the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpas]]' overall difficulties. His "air [[baseball-equipment|ball]]" caught at shortstop (002-051) ended one scoring opportunity. Defensively, he was involved in a play where "[[sam-morrison|Morrison]] came home on a [[baseball-equipment|ball]] muffed by Charlie King" (002-066) — an error that cost the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpas]] a run at a critical moment.

Despite these setbacks, King remained a valued member of the club. His name was consistently mentioned among the core players, and his role in the reorganized [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa club]] continued beyond the opening chapters.

## In the Narrative

King represents the rank-and-file player on the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa club]] — not the star (like [[al-heaton|Heaton]]) or the prominent figure (like [[ben-burton|Burton]]), but a reliable contributor whose presence was essential to [[fielding-baseball|fielding]] a competitive nine. His occasional errors and his continued place on the team reflect the reality of amateur [[national-game|baseball]], where players were community members first and athletes second.
