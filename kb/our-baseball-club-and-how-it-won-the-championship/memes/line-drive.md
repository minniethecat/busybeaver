A **line drive** (also called a "liner," "line ball," or "line hit") is a batted ball struck sharply on a low, straight trajectory. Unlike the high arc of the fly ball or the low bounce of the ground ball, the line drive travels with speed and minimal arc, making it one of the most difficult types of contact for fielders to handle. A well-struck liner could elude infielders before they could react and split the outfielders before they could close the gap.

## The Liner in the Catalpa Nine's Games

Line drives featured prominently in the Catalpa Nine's contests, producing both the game's most thrilling hits and its most heartbreaking defensive lapses:

> Rob Peabody followed and hit a liner to Neddie Ellis who misjudged the distance, and the ball went over his head and allowed Rob to make two bases, while Handy got safely home. (011-050)

> Sam Morrison made a long line hit to center field for three bases, and a slight glimmer of hope dawned in the breasts of the sons of Catalpa. (013-058)

## The Line Ball Caught

Not all line drives fell safely. A sharply hit liner could be caught for an out if the fielder reacted quickly enough:

> The gallant captain took his place at the bat, and hit a line ball which was neatly captured by John Brubaker, who received a round of applause. (013-013)

> Darius Ayres hit a liner to the left field which was very cleverly caught by Sam Morrison; and the inning was then brought to an end. (013-048)

## The Fumbled Liner

The speed of the line drive made it difficult to field cleanly. Even when a fielder got into position, the velocity of the ball could cause a fumble:

> Jamie Kennedy, who hit a line ball to Sam Morrison, who fumbled it and allowed the base runner to reach the first bag safely. (017-018)

## Distinction from Other Batted Ball Types

The line drive occupied a distinct place in the taxonomy of batted balls. It was faster than a ground ball, which relied on placement and spin to evade fielders. It was lower and harder than a fly ball, which gave fielders time to position themselves underneath it. The liner was the batter's most aggressive stroke — a ball hit with such authority that the defense's only hope was to be standing in exactly the right place.
