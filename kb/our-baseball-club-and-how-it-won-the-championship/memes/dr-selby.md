## Identity

Dr. Selby was the staid and dignified village apothecary of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]](002-079) — the town's pharmacist and a respected elder figure. He walked the streets of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]] with the quiet authority of a learned man in a small town, serving as confidant to [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]]'s family and as a steady presence in the community's public gatherings.(010-006)

> Dr. Selby, the staid and dignified village town apothecary.

His professional role as apothecary placed him at the center of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]'s daily life. In an era when the apothecary was both pharmacist and informal medical adviser, Dr. Selby was a man the town trusted.

## Role

### Town Observer and Philosopher

Dr. Selby served as a calm, sometimes ironic observer of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]]'s [[national-game|baseball]] mania. When others grew heated about the team's fortunes, he maintained a cheerful detachment.(009-017) During the tense gathering for bulletins from the [[state-championship-catalpa-calumets|championship game]], he remarked with characteristic understatement about the editor's disheveled appearance:

> Mr. Downey's hair looks as if he was laboring under great excitement. Every individual hair is standing on end, as if he were charged with electricity.

### War Veteran

Dr. Selby was a Civil War veteran, having served at Port Hudson and Vicksburg — major [[baseball-equipment|bat]]tles of the Western theater. This experience gave him perspective on the relative importance of [[national-game|baseball]] victories and defeats:

> I was in the war, myself, you know; was at Port Hudson and Vicksburg.

His war service lent gravity to his character and explained his philosophical detachment from the [[national-game|baseball]] frenzy that consumed his fellow townspeople.

### Father of a Ballplayer

Dr. Selby's son, [[tom-selby|Tom Selby]], was one of the rising players in the [[dean-county-nine|Dean County Nine]](003-003). The Doctor took pride in his son's athletic accomplishments — [[tom-shoff|Tom]] was not only "a wiry and agile player in the field" but also "the best oarsman on the river" and strong enough to lift a barrel of flour. Dr. Selby's support of his son's [[national-game|baseball]] aspirations was steadfast, even when others criticized the less aristocratic [[dean-county-nine|Dean County club]].

## In the Story

Dr. Selby appears throughout the novel in the community scenes that surround the [[national-game|baseball]] action. He walks alongside [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]]'s carriage, trades observations with [[judge-howell|the Judge]] about the team's prospects, and joins the gatherings at [[the-catalpa-leaf|The Catalpa Leaf]]'s bulletin board. During the "[[how-the-good-news-came|How the Good News Came]]" sequence, he sits in the carriage with [[alice-howell|Alice]] and [[miss-ida-boardman|Miss Ida]], providing running commentary on the bulletins and keeping the young women company through the tense afternoon.

His most characteristic moments come when others are agitated — he responds to [[miss-ida-boardman|Miss Ida]]'s superstitious worries with gentle skepticism, and to the [[baseball-spectators|crowd]]'s mood swings with the equanimity of a man who has seen far worse.
