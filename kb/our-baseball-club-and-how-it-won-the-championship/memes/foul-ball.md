# Foul Ball

> "Berthelet then went out on a foul tip leaving Simpson on the base." (002-049)

## Definition

A foul ball in 19th-century baseball was a batted ball that traveled outside the field of fair play. Unlike the modern game where foul balls are strikes (except with two strikes), the rules of the 1880s treated foul balls differently. Various types of fouls had distinct outcomes.

## Types of Fouls

The novel describes several distinct types of foul plays:

**Foul Tip**: When a batter barely contacted the ball and it went directly to the catcher. A foul tip that was caught meant the batter was out. Berthelet "went out on a foul tip" (002-049), and earlier Morrison met the same fate — "Morrison was caught out on a foul fly" (002-063).

**Foul Fly**: A ball hit into the air in foul territory. When "Ben Burton neatly caught" such a ball, the batter was retired (002-050). Larry Boyne also "tipped a foul fly which Al Heaton caught" (002-055), demonstrating how pitchers and catchers worked together to produce these outs.

**Muffed Foul**: Not all foul flies were caught cleanly. When a fielder dropped a foul ball, it was recorded as a "muff" (002-060), and the batter could reach base safely.

## Strategic Significance

Foul outs were critical moments in a game. Catching a foul fly required alertness and skill from fielders — it was often the catcher or third baseman who made these plays. In a close contest, a failed catch on a foul ("muffed shamefully, first by Martin" at 002-060) could swing the momentum.
