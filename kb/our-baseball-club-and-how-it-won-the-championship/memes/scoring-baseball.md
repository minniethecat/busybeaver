# Scoring (Baseball)

> "The score then stood, Jonesvilles, 15; Catalpas, 10." (002-051)

## How Runs Were Scored

In a 19th-century baseball match, a run was scored when a baserunner successfully advanced around all four bases and touched home plate. Runs could be scored through clean hits, errors by the opposing fielders ("muffs"), or walks ("called balls"). The Catalpa Nine once "scored three runs in their inning" (002-060) — a productive inning that could shift the momentum of the entire match.

## The Scoreboard

Scores were tracked inning by inning. At the close of an inning, the tally would be announced: "the inning closed with a score of ten for the Jonesville boys" (002-049). Spectators tracked the numbers obsessively, as when "the score being 15 for the Jonesvilles and five for the Catalpas" (002-060) signaled a lopsided contest.

A "decisive game for the championship" (001-015) had seen the Jonesville Nine win "by a score of 24 to 13 — an overwhelming defeat for the Catalpas" (001-015), demonstrating how scores crystallized the humiliation or triumph of a contest.

## The Drama of Scoring

Scoring created dramatic moments. When "Martin cleverly made his home run, scoring one for the Jonesvilles" (002-046), the tension escalated. Catchers and pitchers could change the scoring dynamic: a pitcher who "added one more to the score of the Jonesvilles" (002-032) by allowing a hit could shift the game's direction.

The emotional rhythm of scoring was relentless — supporters would nervously calculate if their team could overcome a deficit, as when the home nine added "three to their score" and became "a little more hopeful" (002-064).

## Whitewashes and Shutouts

A scoreless inning — being "whitewashed" (002-045) — was a particular humiliation. The threat of being held scoreless motivated desperate play, and close scores made for gripping contests that the novel's spectators and readers alike followed intently.
