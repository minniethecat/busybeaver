# Baseball Equipment

> "The ball dropped near the home base." (002-033)

## The Ball

The 19th-century baseball was a hand-stitched leather-covered sphere with a rubber or cork core. Unlike the standardized modern baseball, balls of this era varied in weight, size, and liveliness. The novel depicts the ball's behavior in detail: "Simpson struck the ball straight into the pitcher's hands" (002-043), "the ball dropped near the home base" (002-033), and "the flying sphere almost struck" a player (013-049).

A well-struck ball could travel far — "like a cannon shot" (011-032) — but balls could also behave unpredictably, taking odd bounces or dying on the rough turf of the agricultural grounds.

## The Bat

Bats of this era were typically hand-carved from ash or hickory, heavier and less tapered than modern bats. Batters aimed for contact rather than power, and the emphasis was on placing the ball where fielders were not. A "straight ball" (011-032) was one struck cleanly along the ground, while hits to the outfield were valued for advancing baserunners.

The novel describes various batting outcomes: "a hot grounder" (011-033), "a swift grounder" (013-021), and "a slow ball to right field" (002-048), suggesting that bat control — the ability to direct the ball — was prized more than raw power.

## Gloves and Protective Gear

In the 1880s, fielders' gloves were thin leather coverings that offered minimal protection — more akin to modern batting gloves than fielding gloves. Catchers in particular played with little protection compared to modern standards. Al Heaton, the Catalpa catcher, played barehanded or with rudimentary mitts. This made the catcher's role especially demanding and elevated the status of those who excelled at it.

The phrase "he went out on a foul tip to Al Heaton, catcher" (002-066) takes on added significance considering the catcher's minimal protection — catching a sharply fouled ball required both courage and skill.

## Field Equipment

The baseball field itself was marked out on whatever open ground was available. At Catalpa, "the Fair Grounds" with its "dead level plain, admirably adapted for a baseball field" (002-001) provided the venue. Unlike modern fields with permanent dugouts, fences, and manicured infields, 19th-century diamonds were temporary arrangements marked by stakes or stones.

The bases were likely canvas bags filled with sand or sawdust, pegged to the ground. Home plate was a simple iron plate or flat stone. Foul lines were marked with lime or chalk, and the outfield was bounded by whatever natural or manmade obstacles existed — in Catalpa's case, "a high board fence" (011-004).
