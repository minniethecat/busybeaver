## Identity

Miss Ida Boardman was [[alice-howell|Alice Howell]]'s closest friend and frequent companion in the novel. Young, sharp-tongued, and never at a loss for a sardonic observation, Ida provided a counterpoint to [[alice-howell|Alice]]'s earnestness — a skeptical, worldly voice amid the town's [[national-game|baseball]] enthusiasm. She was one of the young women of [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]] who attended games, followed the bulletins, and participated in the social rituals that surrounded the team's [[championship-baseball|championship]] campaign.

> PN: (007-023)

## Role

### Alice's Confidante and Comic Foil

Ida served as [[alice-howell|Alice]]'s constant companion, sharing carriage rides, attending games, and waiting for bulletins together. Where [[alice-howell|Alice]] was hopeful and romantic, Ida was skeptical and droll. When [[alice-howell|Alice]] trusted in good omens, Ida voiced distrust. Their contrasting temperaments enriched the novel's community scenes:

> PN: (012-008)

> "Why," she said, "at the end of the fourth inning, yesterday, the Catalpas were three to the Calumets' one — just leading them by two. And you know what happened then?"

[[alice-howell|Alice]] called her "as much of a doubting Thomas as [[ben-burton|Ben Burton]]" — a characterization that Ida did not dispute.

> PN: (010-048)

### Social Critic

Ida's sharp tongue extended to the town's institutions. She "uttered sarcastic remarks about the editor's exuberant comments in the morning paper regarding the coming [[baseball-match|contest]] in [[galena|Galena]]." When the telegraph operator Miss Millicent Murch withheld news, Ida muttered:

> "Hateful old thing! She has had that message all the time and said nothing about it until she got good and ready."

### Faithful Supporter

Despite her skepticism, Ida was a loyal supporter of the [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa Nine]]. She attended games, traveled with [[alice-howell|Alice]] to inspect the [[national-game|baseball]] grounds, and followed the bulletins with genuine investment in the outcome. Her sarcasm masked but did not negate her enthusiasm — she was, in her own way, as committed to the team as anyone in [[catalpa-nine|Catalpa]].

## In the Story

Ida appears throughout the novel as [[alice-howell|Alice]]'s companion. During the "[[how-the-good-news-came|How the Good News Came]]" sequence, she sits with [[alice-howell|Alice]] and [[dr-selby|Dr. Selby]] in [[judge-howell|Judge Howell]]'s carriage, providing a running skeptical commentary on the bulletins while clearly sharing in the town's joy at the final victory. She accompanies [[alice-howell|Alice]] to inspect the [[national-game|baseball]] grounds in [[galena|Galena]] under the guidance of [[al-heaton|Al Heaton]], where the party toured the facility ahead of a big game.

Ida's most characteristic moment comes when, despite having predicted nothing, she claims vindication after the first favorable bulletin:

> "What did I tell you, Alice," said Miss Ida, regardless of the fact that she had told her nothing. "Didn't I say that the Catalpas would win?"

This moment captures Ida perfectly: skeptical before the fact, triumphant after it, and cheerfully inconsistent throughout.
