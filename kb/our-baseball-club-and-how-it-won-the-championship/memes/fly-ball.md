A **fly ball** (also called an "air ball" or simply a "fly") is a batted ball struck high into the air. It is one of the primary types of contact in baseball, standing in contrast to the ground ball. Fielders attempt to catch fly balls before they touch the ground to record outs, making the fly ball a critical element of both batting strategy and defensive play.

## Fly Balls in Play

In the Catalpa Nine's games, fly balls featured prominently in both offensive and defensive situations. A well-struck fly ball could advance runners or clear the bases, while a caught fly ball ended an at-bat:

> Larry Boyne followed with a winged ball which he sent flying to the right field and which enabled him to reach second base and brought Alexander and Studley home. (002-050)

> He moistened his hands and, with the avowed intention of losing the ball, he made a plunge, and, as Al Heaton from his perch remarked, "hit the ball on the nose" and sent it flying over the center field. (011-043)

## Fly Outs

The most common outcome of a fly ball was the fly out, where a fielder caught the ball before it landed:

> Alice's eyes were fixed on Larry Boyne who ended the eighth inning by sending a fly ball to McWilliams and so going out. (011-059)

> John Brubaker hit a fly to Peabody in the right field; the latter captured the ball and also made a fine double play as Neddie Ellis tried to come home on it. (013-029)

## The Magnificent Air Ball

A particularly well-struck fly was described in vivid terms:

> He sent a magnificent air ball so high that it seemed lost in the clouds before it began its descent to earth. (002-063)

This description captures the 19th-century baseball idiom — a towering fly ball was both a display of batting prowess and a moment of suspense as fielders tracked its descent.

## Distinction from Ground Balls

The fly ball and the ground ball represented opposite batting outcomes. While a ground ball stayed low and relied on speed and placement to evade infielders, the fly ball challenged outfielders to judge its flight and make the catch. Together with the ground ball and the foul ball, the fly ball completed the taxonomy of batted ball types recognized in the Catalpa Nine's era of baseball.
