# The National Game

> "When we consider how strong a hold the pastime of baseball playing has upon our people, it is a little surprising that more frequent use of the game, as a framework, has not been made by writers of fiction." (P01-001)

## Definition

"The National Game" was the term used in the late 19th century to describe baseball's unique status in American culture. It was not merely a sport but a shared cultural institution — one that cut across social classes, regions, and even genders. The Introduction to the novel notes that "there are very few Americans, certainly very few of the younger generation, who are not only familiar with the nomenclature and rules of baseball, but are enthusiastic lovers of the sport" (P01-001).

## Cultural Reach

Baseball's appeal extended beyond male players to female spectators as well. "Even among the gentler sex, who may be regarded as spectators only of the game, there is to be found much sound information and an intelligent acquaintance with the details of baseball playing" (P01-001). Alice Howell, the novel's female lead, is described as "an ardent lover of the American game" (001-001), demonstrating that educated young women of the era took genuine interest in baseball.

The game was seen as character-building. The Introduction praises the "hearty and wholesomely taught boy" who "knows everything worth knowing about the game, the famous players, the historic contests, and the notable features of the sport, as practiced in various sections of the republic" (P01-001).

## In Fiction

The novel uses baseball as a narrative framework — the Introduction explicitly notes that the "slender plot should be threaded on a baseball match" (P01-002). The story illustrates "The National Game" with "a story of outdoor life, founded on fact and incidentally introducing personages which are not wholly creatures of his imagination" (P01-002).

For the town of Catalpa, baseball was a source of community pride and identity. Even Judge Howell, a skeptical elder, comes to recognize that the town must support its club: the game demanded that communities invest in their teams to compete with regional rivals.

## Baseball Vocabulary in Daily Life

The novel shows how baseball terminology had infiltrated everyday speech. Alice "mingle[s] the slang of the game with her mother's mode of speech" (001-009), highlighting how thoroughly baseball had saturated American vernacular by the 1880s. The "noble game" (001-010) was a common frame of reference for communities across the country.
