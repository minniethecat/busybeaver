A **fair ball** is a batted ball that lands within the field of play, between the foul lines extending from home plate through first and third bases. It is the defining condition of a legal hit — without a fair ball, no base hit, home run, or run can be scored. The distinction between fair and foul is one of the most fundamental rules in baseball.

## Hitting Fairly

In the Catalpa Nine's contests, hitting the ball fairly was celebrated as a mark of batting skill. The fair ball represented clean, legitimate contact:

> He opened the inning in magnificent style by hitting the ball fairly and the flying sphere almost struck the left field foul line. (013-049)

This passage illustrates the fine margin between a fair ball and a foul — the ball was struck so close to the foul line that it nearly crossed into foul territory.

## Fair vs. Foul

The fair/foul distinction governed the fundamental rhythm of each at-bat. A foul tip ended an at-bat (as a strikeout on the third strike), while a fair ball put the ball into play and required the defense to act:

> Berthelet then went out on a foul tip leaving Simpson on the base. (002-049)

In contrast, a fair ball required fielders to make a play — throw to a base, tag a runner, or make a catch. The entire defensive alignment shifted based on whether the ball was ruled fair or foul.

## Strategic Importance

Hitting the ball fairly was not merely about making contact — it required placing the ball where fielders could not easily reach it. A ground ball hit fairly to an open part of the infield could become a base hit; a fly ball hit fairly to the outfield gaps could become an extra-base hit. The batter's challenge was to hit the ball fairly while simultaneously avoiding the fielders, making the fair ball the gateway to all offensive production in the game.
