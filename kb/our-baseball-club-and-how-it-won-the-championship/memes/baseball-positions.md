# Baseball Positions

> "The Catalpas were well chosen with an eye to symmetry and uniformity. They were all brawny and athletic young fellows." (002-008)

## The Nine Positions

A 19th-century baseball club fielded nine players, each with a distinct defensive position. The novel names many of these positions through its characters, giving insight into how the roles were understood by players and spectators of the era.

### Pitcher

The player who delivers the ball to the batter. The position was important enough that regions produced "champion pitchers" (002-033) whose reputations preceded them. Pitchers of this era were beginning to develop deceptive deliveries — the Calumets' pitcher was known for his "puzzling curves" that left Catalpa batters flailing (011-035).

### Catcher

Stationed behind home plate, the catcher was often the team's star player. Al Heaton, "the stalwart catcher" of the Catalpas, drew "the eyes of the multitude" (002-008). The position demanded athleticism and leadership.

### Basemen

The first baseman fielded throws to first base. Hiram Porter was the Catalpa first baseman, described as "an honor man in his class at the seminary" (002-041). The second baseman, like Hart Stirling (002-033), covered the area between first and second base. The third baseman handled balls hit down the line — Larry Boyne played "the new third baseman of the Catalpa Nine" (006-022).

### Shortstop

The shortstop covered the critical area between second and third base. Ben Burton played shortstop for the Catalpas, and "being at shortstop, had many opportunities to do himself credit" (007-044). Hank Jackson was the "burly shortstop" for Dean County (007-030).

### Outfielders

The outfield consisted of left, center, and right fielders who tracked down fly balls and line drives. The outfielders appear less frequently by name in the novel, but their role was essential to the defensive alignment, as when batters aimed to place "the ball far enough into the outfield" (011-049).

## Team Composition

Teams were "well chosen with an eye to symmetry and uniformity" (002-008). The Catalpa Nine was drawn from "students in the Seminary, young fellows in the law and county offices of the town, and sons of gentlemen of leisure" (001-013), reflecting the social composition of amateur clubs.
