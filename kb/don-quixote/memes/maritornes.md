## Overview

Maritornes is the servant at Juan Palomeque's inn, one of the novel's most vividly drawn minor characters. She is described with characteristic Cervantine bluntness: broad-faced, flat-nosed, blind in one eye, and not quite sound in the other — yet she is also generous, good-natured, and the only person at the inn who offers Don Quixote water after his beating [016-029]. Her name, meaning "Mary of the Towers" or possibly "troubled Mary," hints at her role in the comic chaos that unfolds around her. She is at the center of one of the novel's most celebrated comic set-pieces: the inn bedroom farce of Chapters 16–17, where she agrees to meet a muleteer in the dark and becomes the unwitting object of Don Quixote's chivalric fantasy.

## In Don Quixote

The inn episode involving Maritornes is a tour de force of physical comedy. Don Quixote, lying battered in his bed, fantasizes that the inn is a castle and that the innkeeper's daughter is a princess in love with him. Maritornes, sneaking into the dark garret to meet the muleteer, is seized by Don Quixote and pulled into his bed, where he delivers an elaborate courtly speech while she struggles in his grip [016-119]. The muleteer, enraged, attacks Don Quixote; the innkeeper rushes in with a lamp; and a full-scale brawl erupts in pitch darkness [016-179]. Through it all, Maritornes — frightened, bruised, and entirely innocent of the literary delusions that have engulfed her — hides under the bed and prays for deliverance.

Cervantes' treatment of Maritornes is revealing. She is poor, plain, and socially marginal, yet she is also the most genuinely compassionate figure at the inn. When Don Quixote is beaten senseless after the brawl, Maritornes wordlessly brings him water and applies what remedies she knows [017-252]. Her kindness, unaccompanied by any rhetoric or pretension, offers a quiet counterpoint to the elaborate courtesies of chivalric romance.

## Key Passages

> "Maritornes — for that was the name of the Asturian — promised to come and stay with him as long as he pleased... She was broad-faced, flat-nosed, blind in one eye and not very sound in the other, but the grace of her person supplied all her defects." [016-029]

> "Maritornes was fretting and sweating at finding herself held so fast by Don Quixote, and not understanding what he was talking about, she struggled to get free without a word." [016-179]

> "The compassionate Maritornes, seeing him so exhausted, thought to help him with a drink of water, and brought him some from the well, fresh drawn, as it was the coldest time of the night." [017-252]

## Interpretation

Maritornes is one of Cervantes' most humane creations. In a novel concerned with the gap between ideal and reality, she represents reality at its least idealized — and its most honest. Her physical description is unflattering; her social position is abject; yet she is the character who most consistently acts with simple kindness. The contrast between Don Quixote's elaborate fiction (she is a princess, the inn is a castle) and the brute fact of Maritornes — a tired servant doing a secret favor for a muleteer — is the novel's comic method in miniature. But Cervantes goes further: he makes Maritornes admirable, not just comic. Her water-and-compresses treatment of Don Quixote is more genuinely chivalric than anything he does in the chapter.
