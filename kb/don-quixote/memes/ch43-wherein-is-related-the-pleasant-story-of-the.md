[043-002]Ah me, Love’s mariner am I

[043-003]On Love’s deep ocean sailing;

[043-004]I know not where the haven lies,

[043-005]I dare not hope to gain it.

[043-006]One solitary distant star

[043-007]Is all I have to guide me,

[043-008]A brighter orb than those of old

[043-009]That Palinurus lighted.

[043-010]And vaguely drifting am I borne,

[043-011]I know not where it leads me;

[043-012]I fix my gaze on it alone,

[043-013]Of all beside it heedless.

[043-014]But over-cautious prudery,

[043-015]And coyness cold and cruel,

[043-016]When most I need it, these, like clouds,

[043-017]Its longed-for light refuse me.

[043-018]Bright star, goal of my yearning eyes

[043-019]As thou above me beamest,

[043-020]When thou shalt hide thee from my sight

[043-021]I’ll know that death is near me.

[043-022]The singer had got so far when it struck Dorothea that it was not fair

[043-023]to let Clara miss hearing such a sweet voice, so, shaking her from side

[043-024]to side, she woke her, saying:

[043-025]“Forgive me, child, for waking thee, but I do so that thou mayest have

[043-026]the pleasure of hearing the best voice thou hast ever heard, perhaps,

[043-027]in all thy life.”

[043-028]Clara awoke quite drowsy, and not understanding at the moment what

[043-029]Dorothea said, asked her what it was; she repeated what she had said,

[043-030]and Clara became attentive at once; but she had hardly heard two lines,

[043-031]as the singer continued, when a strange trembling seized her, as if she

[043-032]were suffering from a severe attack of quartan ague, and throwing her

[043-033]arms round Dorothea she said:

[043-034]“Ah, dear lady of my soul and life! why did you wake me? The greatest

[043-035]kindness fortune could do me now would be to close my eyes and ears so

[043-036]as neither to see or hear that unhappy musician.”

[043-037]“What art thou talking about, child?” said Dorothea. “Why, they say

[043-038]this singer is a muleteer!”

[043-039]“Nay, he is the lord of many places,” replied Clara, “and that one in

[043-040]my heart which he holds so firmly shall never be taken from him, unless

[043-041]he be willing to surrender it.”

[043-042]Dorothea was amazed at the ardent language of the girl, for it seemed

[043-043]to be far beyond such experience of life as her tender years gave any

[043-044]promise of, so she said to her:

[043-045]“You speak in such a way that I cannot understand you, Señora Clara;

[043-046]explain yourself more clearly, and tell me what is this you are saying

[043-047]about hearts and places and this musician whose voice has so moved you?

[043-048]But do not tell me anything now; I do not want to lose the pleasure I

[043-049]get from listening to the singer by giving my attention to your

[043-050]transports, for I perceive he is beginning to sing a new strain and a

[043-051]new air.”

[043-052]“Let him, in Heaven’s name,” returned Clara; and not to hear him she

[043-053]stopped both ears with her hands, at which Dorothea was again

[043-054]surprised; but turning her attention to the song she found that it ran

[043-055]in this fashion:

[043-056]Sweet Hope, my stay,

[043-057]That onward to the goal of thy intent

[043-058]Dost make thy way,

[043-059]Heedless of hindrance or impediment,

[043-060]Have thou no fear

[043-061]If at each step thou findest death is near.

[043-062]No victory,

[043-063]No joy of triumph doth the faint heart know;

[043-064]Unblest is he

[043-065]That a bold front to Fortune dares not show,

[043-066]But soul and sense

[043-067]In bondage yieldeth up to indolence.

[043-068]If Love his wares

[043-069]Do dearly sell, his right must be contest;

[043-070]What gold compares

[043-071]With that whereon his stamp he hath imprest?

[043-072]And all men know

[043-073]What costeth little that we rate but low.

[043-074]Love resolute

[043-075]Knows not the word “impossibility;”

[043-076]And though my suit

[043-077]Beset by endless obstacles I see,

[043-078]Yet no despair

[043-079]Shall hold me bound to earth while heaven is there.

[043-080]Here the voice ceased and Clara’s sobs began afresh, all which excited

[043-081]Dorothea’s curiosity to know what could be the cause of singing so

[043-082]sweet and weeping so bitter, so she again asked her what it was she was

[043-083]going to say before. On this Clara, afraid that Luscinda might overhear

[043-084]her, winding her arms tightly round Dorothea put her mouth so close to

[043-085]her ear that she could speak without fear of being heard by anyone

[043-086]else, and said:

[043-087]“This singer, dear señora, is the son of a gentleman of Aragon, lord of

[043-088]two villages, who lives opposite my father’s house at Madrid; and

[043-089]though my father had curtains to the windows of his house in winter,

[043-090]and lattice-work in summer, in some way—I know not how—this gentleman,

[043-091]who was pursuing his studies, saw me, whether in church or elsewhere, I

[043-092]cannot tell, and, in fact, fell in love with me, and gave me to know it

[043-093]from the windows of his house, with so many signs and tears that I was

[043-094]forced to believe him, and even to love him, without knowing what it

[043-095]was he wanted of me. One of the signs he used to make me was to link

[043-096]one hand in the other, to show me he wished to marry me; and though I

[043-097]should have been glad if that could be, being alone and motherless I

[043-098]knew not whom to open my mind to, and so I left it as it was, showing

[043-099]him no favour, except when my father, and his too, were from home, to

[043-100]raise the curtain or the lattice a little and let him see me plainly,

[043-101]at which he would show such delight that he seemed as if he were going

[043-102]mad. Meanwhile the time for my father’s departure arrived, which he

[043-103]became aware of, but not from me, for I had never been able to tell him

[043-104]of it. He fell sick, of grief I believe, and so the day we were going

[043-105]away I could not see him to take farewell of him, were it only with the

[043-106]eyes. But after we had been two days on the road, on entering the

[043-107]posada of a village a day’s journey from this, I saw him at the inn

[043-108]door in the dress of a muleteer, and so well disguised, that if I did

[043-109]not carry his image graven on my heart it would have been impossible

[043-110]for me to recognise him. But I knew him, and I was surprised, and glad;

[043-111]he watched me, unsuspected by my father, from whom he always hides

[043-112]himself when he crosses my path on the road, or in the posadas where we

[043-113]halt; and, as I know what he is, and reflect that for love of me he

[043-114]makes this journey on foot in all this hardship, I am ready to die of

[043-115]sorrow; and where he sets foot there I set my eyes. I know not with

[043-116]what object he has come; or how he could have got away from his father,

[043-117]who loves him beyond measure, having no other heir, and because he

[043-118]deserves it, as you will perceive when you see him. And moreover, I can

[043-119]tell you, all that he sings is out of his own head; for I have heard

[043-120]them say he is a great scholar and poet; and what is more, every time I

[043-121]see him or hear him sing I tremble all over, and am terrified lest my

[043-122]father should recognise him and come to know of our loves. I have never

[043-123]spoken a word to him in my life; and for all that I love him so that I

[043-124]could not live without him. This, dear señora, is all I have to tell

[043-125]you about the musician whose voice has delighted you so much; and from

[043-126]it alone you might easily perceive he is no muleteer, but a lord of

[043-127]hearts and towns, as I told you already.”

[043-128]“Say no more, Doña Clara,” said Dorothea at this, at the same time

[043-129]kissing her a thousand times over, “say no more, I tell you, but wait

[043-130]till day comes; when I trust in God to arrange this affair of yours so

[043-131]that it may have the happy ending such an innocent beginning deserves.”

[043-132]“Ah, señora,” said Doña Clara, “what end can be hoped for when his

[043-133]father is of such lofty position, and so wealthy, that he would think I

[043-134]was not fit to be even a servant to his son, much less wife? And as to

[043-135]marrying without the knowledge of my father, I would not do it for all

[043-136]the world. I would not ask anything more than that this youth should go

[043-137]back and leave me; perhaps with not seeing him, and the long distance

[043-138]we shall have to travel, the pain I suffer now may become easier;

[043-139]though I daresay the remedy I propose will do me very little good. I

[043-140]don’t know how the devil this has come about, or how this love I have

[043-141]for him got in; I such a young girl, and he such a mere boy; for I

[043-142]verily believe we are both of an age, and I am not sixteen yet; for I

[043-143]will be sixteen Michaelmas Day, next, my father says.”

[043-144]Dorothea could not help laughing to hear how like a child Doña Clara

[043-145]spoke. “Let us go to sleep now, señora,” said she, “for the little of

[043-146]the night that I fancy is left to us: God will soon send us daylight,

[043-147]and we will set all to rights, or it will go hard with me.”

[043-148]With this they fell asleep, and deep silence reigned all through the

[043-149]inn. The only persons not asleep were the landlady’s daughter and her

[043-150]servant Maritornes, who, knowing the weak point of Don Quixote’s

[043-151]humour, and that he was outside the inn mounting guard in armour and on

[043-152]horseback, resolved, the pair of them, to play some trick upon him, or

[043-153]at any rate to amuse themselves for a while by listening to his

[043-154]nonsense. As it so happened there was not a window in the whole inn

[043-155]that looked outwards except a hole in the wall of a straw-loft through

[043-156]which they used to throw out the straw. At this hole the two

[043-157]demi-damsels posted themselves, and observed Don Quixote on his horse,

[043-158]leaning on his pike and from time to time sending forth such deep and

[043-159]doleful sighs, that he seemed to pluck up his soul by the roots with

[043-160]each of them; and they could hear him, too, saying in a soft, tender,

[043-161]loving tone, “Oh my lady Dulcinea del Toboso, perfection of all beauty,

[043-162]summit and crown of discretion, treasure house of grace, depositary of

[043-163]virtue, and finally, ideal of all that is good, honourable, and

[043-164]delectable in this world! What is thy grace doing now? Art thou,

[043-165]perchance, mindful of thy enslaved knight who of his own free will hath

[043-166]exposed himself to so great perils, and all to serve thee? Give me

[043-167]tidings of her, oh luminary of the three faces! Perhaps at this moment,

[043-168]envious of hers, thou art regarding her, either as she paces to and fro

[043-169]some gallery of her sumptuous palaces, or leans over some balcony,

[043-170]meditating how, whilst preserving her purity and greatness, she may

[043-171]mitigate the tortures this wretched heart of mine endures for her sake,

[043-172]what glory should recompense my sufferings, what repose my toil, and

[043-173]lastly what death my life, and what reward my services? And thou, oh

[043-174]sun, that art now doubtless harnessing thy steeds in haste to rise

[043-175]betimes and come forth to see my lady; when thou seest her I entreat of

[043-176]thee to salute her on my behalf: but have a care, when thou shalt see

[043-177]her and salute her, that thou kiss not her face; for I shall be more

[043-178]jealous of thee than thou wert of that light-footed ingrate that made

[043-179]thee sweat and run so on the plains of Thessaly, or on the banks of the

[043-182]Don Quixote had got so far in his pathetic speech when the landlady’s

[043-183]daughter began to signal to him, saying, “Señor, come over here,

[043-184]please.”

[043-185]At these signals and voice Don Quixote turned his head and saw by the

[043-186]light of the moon, which then was in its full splendour, that someone

[043-187]was calling to him from the hole in the wall, which seemed to him to be

[043-190]suggested itself to his imagination that, as on the former occasion,

[043-191]the fair damsel, the daughter of the lady of the castle, overcome by

[043-192]love for him, was once more endeavouring to win his affections; and

[043-193]with this idea, not to show himself discourteous, or ungrateful, he

[043-194]turned Rocinante’s head and approached the hole, and as he perceived

[043-195]the two wenches he said:

[043-196]“I pity you, beauteous lady, that you should have directed your

[043-197]thoughts of love to a quarter from whence it is impossible that such a

[043-198]return can be made to you as is due to your great merit and gentle

[043-199]birth, for which you must not blame this unhappy knight-errant whom

[043-200]love renders incapable of submission to any other than her whom, the

[043-201]first moment his eyes beheld her, he made absolute mistress of his

[043-202]soul. Forgive me, noble lady, and retire to your apartment, and do not,

[043-203]by any further declaration of your passion, compel me to show myself

[043-204]more ungrateful; and if, of the love you bear me, you should find that

[043-205]there is anything else in my power wherein I can gratify you, provided

[043-206]it be not love itself, demand it of me; for I swear to you by that

[043-207]sweet absent enemy of mine to grant it this instant, though it be that

[043-208]you require of me a lock of Medusa’s hair, which was all snakes, or

[043-209]even the very beams of the sun shut up in a vial.”

[043-210]“My mistress wants nothing of that sort, sir knight,” said Maritornes

[043-211]at this.

[043-212]“What then, discreet dame, is it that your mistress wants?” replied Don

[043-213]Quixote.

[043-214]“Only one of your fair hands,” said Maritornes, “to enable her to vent

[043-215]over it the great passion, passion which has brought her to this

[043-216]loophole, so much to the risk of her honour; for if the lord her father

[043-218]“I should like to see that tried,” said Don Quixote; “but he had better

[043-219]beware of that, if he does not want to meet the most disastrous end

[043-221]limbs of a love-stricken daughter.”

[043-222]Maritornes felt sure that Don Quixote would present the hand she had

[043-223]asked, and making up her mind what to do, she got down from the hole

[043-224]and went into the stable, where she took the halter of Sancho Panza’s

[043-225]ass, and in all haste returned to the hole, just as Don Quixote had

[043-226]planted himself standing on Rocinante’s saddle in order to reach the

[043-227]grated window where he supposed the lovelorn damsel to be; and giving

[043-228]her his hand, he said, “Lady, take this hand, or rather this scourge of

[043-229]the evil-doers of the earth; take, I say, this hand which no other hand

[043-231]my entire body. I present it to you, not that you may kiss it, but that

[043-235]“That we shall see presently,” said Maritornes, and making a running

[043-236]knot on the halter, she passed it over his wrist and coming down from

[043-239]Don Quixote, feeling the roughness of the rope on his wrist, exclaimed,

[043-240]“Your grace seems to be grating rather than caressing my hand; treat it

[043-241]not so harshly, for it is not to blame for the offence my resolution

[043-242]has given you, nor is it just to wreak all your vengeance on so small a

[043-243]part; remember that one who loves so well should not revenge herself so

[043-245]But there was nobody now to listen to these words of Don Quixote’s, for

[043-246]as soon as Maritornes had tied him she and the other made off, ready to

[043-248]impossible for him to release himself.

[043-251]mighty fear and dread of being left hanging by the arm if Rocinante

[043-254]disposition of Rocinante, he had good reason to expect that he would
