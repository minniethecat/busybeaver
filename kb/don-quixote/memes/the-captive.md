# The Captive (Ruy Pérez de Viedma)

**Ruy Pérez de Viedma**, referred to throughout the novel simply as "**the Captive**" (*el Cautivo*), arrives at [[juan-palomeque|Juan Palomeque]]'s inn accompanied by a veiled Moorish woman, [[zoraida|Zoraida]]. A former infantry captain who spent years as a slave in Algiers, he tells one of the novel's most famous interpolated tales — a narrative that draws heavily on Cervantes's own five years of captivity in North Africa.

## Character Profile

The Captive is introduced at the inn as a man whose travel-worn appearance suggests hardship and dignity in equal measure. He is accompanied by Zoraida, whose beauty and exotic dress immediately draw attention. When asked her name, the Captive replies with careful diplomacy, protecting her dignity while acknowledging her otherness:

> "Don Fernando asked the captive what her name was, and he replied that she was called Zoraida"[037-241]

He is a soldier by profession — a captain of infantry — and his narrative reveals a man shaped by military service, religious faith, and the brutal experience of captivity. He survived Algiers through a combination of patience, cunning, and an unlikely alliance with Zoraida, who arranged his escape.

## The Captive's Tale

Spanning chapters 39–41, the Captive's story is the longest interpolated narrative in *Don Quixote* and one of the most autobiographical passages Cervantes ever wrote. Like Cervantes himself, the Captive:

- Served in the Spanish military under Don John of Austria
- Fought at the Battle of Lepanto (1571)
- Was captured by Ottoman corsairs and enslaved in Algiers
- Made multiple escape attempts before being ransomed

The narrative provides a detailed account of life in the *baños* (slave prisons) of Algiers, the hierarchy among captives, and the complex negotiations of ransom and escape:

> "filled with joy, and the only captive among so many who rejoiced at the coming of the ship"[039-129]

## Zoraida

The Captive's escape is made possible by **Zoraida**, the daughter of a wealthy Moor, Agi Morato. Having secretly converted to Christianity, Zoraida lowers a bundle of money and jewels from her window to the Captive, enabling his ransom and their joint escape. Her decision to abandon her father, her wealth, and her country for a man she barely knows is one of the novel's most dramatic acts of faith.

She communicates with the Captive through letters written in broken Spanish, taught to her by a Christian slave. The Captive describes her as both brave and vulnerable — a woman who risked everything for a religion she had never been able to practice openly.

## Reunion at the Inn

The Captive's story reaches its emotional climax when a judge staying at the same inn is revealed to be his long-lost brother, Don Juan Pérez de Viedma. The recognition scene — triggered by the Captive mentioning their father's name — is one of Cervantes's most moving passages:

> "The instant the captive mentioned the name of Don Pedro de Viedma, the judge changed color"[039-242]

The brothers' reunion, after decades of separation and the Captive's years of enslavement, brings the interpolated tale to a satisfying closure and integrates it into the main narrative.

## Thematic Significance

The Captive's tale serves multiple functions. It injects historical realism into a novel dominated by chivalric fantasy, grounding the narrative in the actual Mediterranean conflicts of Cervantes's era. It also embodies the theme of endurance through faith — the Captive survives not through knightly valor but through patience, quick thinking, and Zoraida's love. His story stands as a counterpoint to Don Quixote's: real suffering, real courage, and a happy ending earned through genuine hardship rather than delusion.
