[065-001]IN WHICH IS CONTINUED THE ADVENTURE OF THE KNIGHT OF THE GROVE,

[065-002]TOGETHER WITH THE SENSIBLE, ORIGINAL, AND TRANQUIL COLLOQUY THAT PASSED

BETWEEN THE TWO SQUIRES

p13a.jpg (126K)


[065-003]The knights and the squires made two parties, these telling the story

[065-004]of their lives, the others the story of their loves; but the history

[065-005]relates first of all the conversation of the servants, and afterwards

[065-006]takes up that of the masters; and it says that, withdrawing a little

[065-007]from the others, he of the Grove said to Sancho, “A hard life it is we

[065-008]lead and live, señor, we that are squires to knights-errant; verily, we

[065-009]eat our bread in the sweat of our faces, which is one of the curses God

[065-010]laid on our first parents.”

[065-011]“It may be said, too,” added Sancho, “that we eat it in the chill of

[065-012]our bodies; for who gets more heat and cold than the miserable squires

[065-013]of knight-errantry? Even so it would not be so bad if we had something

[065-014]to eat, for woes are lighter if there’s bread; but sometimes we go a

[065-015]day or two without breaking our fast, except with the wind that blows.”

[065-016]“All that,” said he of the Grove, “may be endured and put up with when

[065-017]we have hopes of reward; for, unless the knight-errant he serves is

[065-018]excessively unlucky, after a few turns the squire will at least find

[065-019]himself rewarded with a fine government of some island or some fair

[065-020]county.”

[065-021]“I,” said Sancho, “have already told my master that I shall be content

[065-022]with the government of some island, and he is so noble and generous

[065-023]that he has promised it to me ever so many times.”

[065-024]“I,” said he of the Grove, “shall be satisfied with a canonry for my

[065-025]services, and my master has already assigned me one.”

[065-026]“Your master,” said Sancho, “no doubt is a knight in the Church line,

[065-027]and can bestow rewards of that sort on his good squire; but mine is

[065-028]only a layman; though I remember some clever, but, to my mind,

[065-029]designing people, strove to persuade him to try and become an

[065-030]archbishop. He, however, would not be anything but an emperor; but I

[065-031]was trembling all the time lest he should take a fancy to go into the

[065-032]Church, not finding myself fit to hold office in it; for I may tell

[065-033]you, though I seem a man, I am no better than a beast for the Church.”

[065-034]“Well, then, you are wrong there,” said he of the Grove; “for those

[065-035]island governments are not all satisfactory; some are awkward, some are

[065-036]poor, some are dull, and, in short, the highest and choicest brings

[065-037]with it a heavy burden of cares and troubles which the unhappy wight to

[065-038]whose lot it has fallen bears upon his shoulders. Far better would it

[065-039]be for us who have adopted this accursed service to go back to our own

[065-040]houses, and there employ ourselves in pleasanter occupations—in hunting

[065-041]or fishing, for instance; for what squire in the world is there so poor

[065-042]as not to have a hack and a couple of greyhounds and a fishingrod to

[065-043]amuse himself with in his own village?”

[065-044]“I am not in want of any of those things,” said Sancho; “to be sure I

[065-045]have no hack, but I have an ass that is worth my master’s horse twice

[065-046]over; God send me a bad Easter, and that the next one I am to see, if I

[065-047]would swap, even if I got four bushels of barley to boot. You will

[065-048]laugh at the value I put on my Dapple—for dapple is the colour of my

[065-049]beast. As to greyhounds, I can’t want for them, for there are enough

[065-050]and to spare in my town; and, moreover, there is more pleasure in sport

[065-051]when it is at other people’s expense.”

[065-052]“In truth and earnest, sir squire,” said he of the Grove, “I have made

[065-053]up my mind and determined to have done with these drunken vagaries of

[065-054]these knights, and go back to my village, and bring up my children; for

[065-055]I have three, like three Oriental pearls.”

[065-056]“I have two,” said Sancho, “that might be presented before the Pope

[065-057]himself, especially a girl whom I am breeding up for a countess, please

[065-058]God, though in spite of her mother.”

[065-059]“And how old is this lady that is being bred up for a countess?” asked

[065-060]he of the Grove.

[065-061]“Fifteen, a couple of years more or less,” answered Sancho; “but she is

[065-062]as tall as a lance, and as fresh as an April morning, and as strong as

[065-063]a porter.”

[065-064]“Those are gifts to fit her to be not only a countess but a nymph of

[065-065]the greenwood,” said he of the Grove; “whoreson strumpet! what pith the

[065-066]rogue must have!”

[065-067]To which Sancho made answer, somewhat sulkily, “She’s no strumpet, nor

[065-068]was her mother, nor will either of them be, please God, while I live;

[065-069]speak more civilly; for one bred up among knights-errant, who are

[065-070]courtesy itself, your words don’t seem to me to be very becoming.”

[065-071]“O how little you know about compliments, sir squire,” returned he of

[065-072]the Grove. “What! don’t you know that when a horseman delivers a good

[065-073]lance thrust at the bull in the plaza, or when anyone does anything

[065-074]very well, the people are wont to say, ‘Ha, whoreson rip! how well he

[065-075]has done it!’ and that what seems to be abuse in the expression is high

[065-076]praise? Disown sons and daughters, señor, who don’t do what deserves

[065-077]that compliments of this sort should be paid to their parents.”

[065-078]“I do disown them,” replied Sancho, “and in this way, and by the same

[065-079]reasoning, you might call me and my children and my wife all the

[065-080]strumpets in the world, for all they do and say is of a kind that in

[065-081]the highest degree deserves the same praise; and to see them again I

[065-082]pray God to deliver me from mortal sin, or, what comes to the same

[065-083]thing, to deliver me from this perilous calling of squire into which I

[065-084]have fallen a second time, decayed and beguiled by a purse with a

[065-085]hundred ducats that I found one day in the heart of the Sierra Morena;

[065-086]and the devil is always putting a bag full of doubloons before my eyes,

[065-087]here, there, everywhere, until I fancy at every stop I am putting my

[065-088]hand on it, and hugging it, and carrying it home with me, and making

[065-089]investments, and getting interest, and living like a prince; and so

[065-090]long as I think of this I make light of all the hardships I endure with

[065-091]this simpleton of a master of mine, who, I well know, is more of a

[065-092]madman than a knight.”

[065-093]“There’s why they say that ‘covetousness bursts the bag,’” said he of

[065-094]the Grove; “but if you come to talk of that sort, there is not a

[065-095]greater one in the world than my master, for he is one of those of whom

[065-096]they say, ‘the cares of others kill the ass;’ for, in order that

[065-097]another knight may recover the senses he has lost, he makes a madman of

[065-098]himself and goes looking for what, when found, may, for all I know, fly

[065-099]in his own face.” “And is he in love perchance?” asked Sancho.

[065-100]“He is,” said of the Grove, “with one Casildea de Vandalia, the rawest

[065-101]and best roasted lady the whole world could produce; but that rawness

[065-102]is not the only foot he limps on, for he has greater schemes rumbling

[065-103]in his bowels, as will be seen before many hours are over.”

[065-104]“There’s no road so smooth but it has some hole or hindrance in it,”

[065-105]said Sancho; “in other houses they cook beans, but in mine it’s by the

[065-106]potful; madness will have more followers and hangers-on than sound

[065-107]sense; but if there be any truth in the common saying, that to have

[065-108]companions in trouble gives some relief, I may take consolation from

[065-109]you, inasmuch as you serve a master as crazy as my own.”

[065-110]“Crazy but valiant,” replied he of the Grove, “and more roguish than

[065-111]crazy or valiant.”

[065-112]“Mine is not that,” said Sancho; “I mean he has nothing of the rogue in

[065-113]him; on the contrary, he has the soul of a pitcher; he has no thought

[065-114]of doing harm to anyone, only good to all, nor has he any malice

[065-115]whatever in him; a child might persuade him that it is night at

[065-116]noonday; and for this simplicity I love him as the core of my heart,

[065-117]and I can’t bring myself to leave him, let him do ever such foolish

[065-118]things.”

[065-119]“For all that, brother and señor,” said he of the Grove, “if the blind

[065-120]lead the blind, both are in danger of falling into the pit. It is

[065-121]better for us to beat a quiet retreat and get back to our own quarters;

[065-122]for those who seek adventures don’t always find good ones.”

[065-123]Sancho kept spitting from time to time, and his spittle seemed somewhat

[065-124]ropy and dry, observing which the compassionate squire of the Grove

[065-125]said, “It seems to me that with all this talk of ours our tongues are

[065-126]sticking to the roofs of our mouths; but I have a pretty good loosener

[065-127]hanging from the saddle-bow of my horse,” and getting up he came back

[065-128]the next minute with a large bota of wine and a pasty half a yard

[065-129]across; and this is no exaggeration, for it was made of a house rabbit

[065-130]so big that Sancho, as he handled it, took it to be made of a goat, not

[065-131]to say a kid, and looking at it he said, “And do you carry this with

[065-132]you, señor?”

[065-133]“Why, what are you thinking about?” said the other; “do you take me for

[065-134]some paltry squire? I carry a better larder on my horse’s croup than a

[065-135]general takes with him when he goes on a march.”

[065-136]Sancho ate without requiring to be pressed, and in the dark bolted

[065-137]mouthfuls like the knots on a tether, and said he, “You are a proper

[065-138]trusty squire, one of the right sort, sumptuous and grand, as this

[065-139]banquet shows, which, if it has not come here by magic art, at any rate

[065-140]has the look of it; not like me, unlucky beggar, that have nothing more

[065-141]in my alforjas than a scrap of cheese, so hard that one might brain a

[065-142]giant with it, and, to keep it company, a few dozen carobs and as many

[065-143]more filberts and walnuts; thanks to the austerity of my master, and

[065-144]the idea he has and the rule he follows, that knights-errant must not

[065-145]live or sustain themselves on anything except dried fruits and the

[065-146]herbs of the field.”

[065-147]“By my faith, brother,” said he of the Grove, “my stomach is not made

[065-148]for thistles, or wild pears, or roots of the woods; let our masters do

[065-149]as they like, with their chivalry notions and laws, and eat what those

[065-150]enjoin; I carry my prog-basket and this bota hanging to the saddle-bow,

[065-151]whatever they may say; and it is such an object of worship with me, and

[065-152]I love it so, that there is hardly a moment but I am kissing and

[065-153]embracing it over and over again;” and so saying he thrust it into

[065-154]Sancho’s hands, who raising it aloft pointed to his mouth, gazed at the

[065-155]stars for a quarter of an hour; and when he had done drinking let his

[065-156]head fall on one side, and giving a deep sigh, exclaimed, “Ah, whoreson

[065-157]rogue, how catholic it is!”

[065-158]“There, you see,” said he of the Grove, hearing Sancho’s exclamation,

[065-159]“how you have called this wine whoreson by way of praise.”

[065-160]“Well,” said Sancho, “I own it, and I grant it is no dishonour to call

[065-161]anyone whoreson when it is to be understood as praise. But tell me,

[065-162]señor, by what you love best, is this Ciudad Real wine?”

[065-163]“O rare wine-taster!” said he of the Grove; “nowhere else indeed does

[065-164]it come from, and it has some years’ age too.”

[065-165]“Leave me alone for that,” said Sancho; “never fear but I’ll hit upon

[065-166]the place it came from somehow. What would you say, sir squire, to my

[065-167]having such a great natural instinct in judging wines that you have

[065-168]only to let me smell one and I can tell positively its country, its

[065-169]kind, its flavour and soundness, the changes it will undergo, and

[065-170]everything that appertains to a wine? But it is no wonder, for I have

[065-171]had in my family, on my father’s side, the two best wine-tasters that

[065-172]have been known in La Mancha for many a long year, and to prove it I’ll

[065-173]tell you now a thing that happened them. They gave the two of them some

[065-174]wine out of a cask, to try, asking their opinion as to the condition,

[065-175]quality, goodness or badness of the wine. One of them tried it with the

[065-176]tip of his tongue, the other did no more than bring it to his nose. The

[065-177]first said the wine had a flavour of iron, the second said it had a

[065-178]stronger flavour of cordovan. The owner said the cask was clean, and

[065-179]that nothing had been added to the wine from which it could have got a

[065-180]flavour of either iron or leather. Nevertheless, these two great

[065-181]wine-tasters held to what they had said. Time went by, the wine was

[065-182]sold, and when they came to clean out the cask, they found in it a

[065-183]small key hanging to a thong of cordovan; see now if one who comes of

[065-184]the same stock has not a right to give his opinion in such like cases.”

[065-185]“Therefore, I say,” said he of the Grove, “let us give up going in

[065-186]quest of adventures, and as we have loaves let us not go looking for

[065-187]cakes, but return to our cribs, for God will find us there if it be his

[065-188]will.”

[065-189]“Until my master reaches Saragossa,” said Sancho, “I’ll remain in his

[065-190]service; after that we’ll see.”

[065-191]The end of it was that the two squires talked so much and drank so much

[065-192]that sleep had to tie their tongues and moderate their thirst, for to

[065-193]quench it was impossible; and so the pair of them fell asleep clinging

[065-194]to the now nearly empty bota and with half-chewed morsels in their

[065-195]mouths; and there we will leave them for the present, to relate what

[065-196]passed between the Knight of the Grove and him of the Rueful

[065-197]Countenance.
