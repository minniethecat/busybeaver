OF THE SECOND SET OF COUNSELS DON QUIXOTE GAVE SANCHO PANZA

p43a.jpg (129K)


[095-001]Who, hearing the foregoing discourse of Don Quixote, would not have set

[095-002]him down for a person of great good sense and greater rectitude of

[095-003]purpose? But, as has been frequently observed in the course of this

[095-004]great history, he only talked nonsense when he touched on chivalry, and

[095-005]in discussing all other subjects showed that he had a clear and

[095-006]unbiassed understanding; so that at every turn his acts gave the lie to

[095-007]his intellect, and his intellect to his acts; but in the case of these

[095-008]second counsels that he gave Sancho, he showed himself to have a lively

[095-009]turn of humour, and displayed conspicuously his wisdom, and also his

[095-010]folly.

[095-011]Sancho listened to him with the deepest attention, and endeavoured to

[095-012]fix his counsels in his memory, like one who meant to follow them and

[095-013]by their means bring the full promise of his government to a happy

[095-014]issue. Don Quixote, then, went on to say:

[095-015]“With regard to the mode in which thou shouldst govern thy person and

[095-016]thy house, Sancho, the first charge I have to give thee is to be clean,

[095-017]and to cut thy nails, not letting them grow as some do, whose ignorance

[095-018]makes them fancy that long nails are an ornament to their hands, as if

[095-019]those excrescences they neglect to cut were nails, and not the talons

[095-020]of a lizard-catching kestrel—a filthy and unnatural abuse.

[095-021]“Go not ungirt and loose, Sancho; for disordered attire is a sign of an

[095-022]unstable mind, unless indeed the slovenliness and slackness is to be

[095-023]set down to craft, as was the common opinion in the case of Julius

[095-024]Cæsar.

[095-025]“Ascertain cautiously what thy office may be worth; and if it will

[095-026]allow thee to give liveries to thy servants, give them respectable and

[095-027]serviceable, rather than showy and gay ones, and divide them between

[095-028]thy servants and the poor; that is to say, if thou canst clothe six

[095-029]pages, clothe three and three poor men, and thus thou wilt have pages

[095-030]for heaven and pages for earth; the vainglorious never think of this

[095-031]new mode of giving liveries.

[095-032]“Eat not garlic nor onions, lest they find out thy boorish origin by

[095-033]the smell; walk slowly and speak deliberately, but not in such a way as

[095-034]to make it seem thou art listening to thyself, for all affectation is

[095-035]bad.

[095-036]“Dine sparingly and sup more sparingly still; for the health of the

[095-037]whole body is forged in the workshop of the stomach.

[095-038]“Be temperate in drinking, bearing in mind that wine in excess keeps

[095-039]neither secrets nor promises.

[095-040]“Take care, Sancho, not to chew on both sides, and not to eruct in

[095-041]anybody’s presence.”

[095-042]“Eruct!” said Sancho; “I don’t know what that means.”

[095-043]“To eruct, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “means to belch, and that is one

[095-044]of the filthiest words in the Spanish language, though a very

[095-045]expressive one; and therefore nice folk have had recourse to the Latin,

[095-046]and instead of belch say eruct, and instead of belches say eructations;

[095-047]and if some do not understand these terms it matters little, for custom

[095-048]will bring them into use in the course of time, so that they will be

[095-049]readily understood; this is the way a language is enriched; custom and

[095-050]the public are all-powerful there.”

[095-051]“In truth, señor,” said Sancho, “one of the counsels and cautions I

[095-052]mean to bear in mind shall be this, not to belch, for I’m constantly

[095-053]doing it.”

[095-054]“Eruct, Sancho, not belch,” said Don Quixote.

[095-055]“Eruct, I shall say henceforth, and I swear not to forget it,” said

[095-056]Sancho.

[095-057]“Likewise, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “thou must not mingle such a

[095-058]quantity of proverbs in thy discourse as thou dost; for though proverbs

[095-059]are short maxims, thou dost drag them in so often by the head and

[095-060]shoulders that they savour more of nonsense than of maxims.”

[095-061]“God alone can cure that,” said Sancho; “for I have more proverbs in me

[095-062]than a book, and when I speak they come so thick together into my mouth

[095-063]that they fall to fighting among themselves to get out; that’s why my

[095-064]tongue lets fly the first that come, though they may not be pat to the

[095-065]purpose. But I’ll take care henceforward to use such as befit the

[095-066]dignity of my office; for ‘in a house where there’s plenty, supper is

[095-067]soon cooked,’ and ‘he who binds does not wrangle,’ and ‘the

[095-068]bell-ringer’s in a safe berth,’ and ‘giving and keeping require

[095-069]brains.’”

[095-070]“That’s it, Sancho!” said Don Quixote; “pack, tack, string proverbs

[095-071]together; nobody is hindering thee! ‘My mother beats me, and I go on

[095-072]with my tricks.’ I am bidding thee avoid proverbs, and here in a second

[095-073]thou hast shot out a whole litany of them, which have as much to do

[095-074]with what we are talking about as ‘over the hills of Úbeda.’ Mind,

[095-075]Sancho, I do not say that a proverb aptly brought in is objectionable;

[095-076]but to pile up and string together proverbs at random makes

[095-077]conversation dull and vulgar.

[095-078]“When thou ridest on horseback, do not go lolling with thy body on the

[095-079]back of the saddle, nor carry thy legs stiff or sticking out from the

[095-080]horse’s belly, nor yet sit so loosely that one would suppose thou wert

[095-081]on Dapple; for the seat on a horse makes gentlemen of some and grooms

[095-082]of others.

[095-083]“Be moderate in thy sleep; for he who does not rise early does not get

[095-084]the benefit of the day; and remember, Sancho, diligence is the mother

[095-085]of good fortune, and indolence, its opposite, never yet attained the

[095-086]object of an honest ambition.

[095-087]“The last counsel I will give thee now, though it does not tend to

[095-088]bodily improvement, I would have thee carry carefully in thy memory,

[095-089]for I believe it will be no less useful to thee than those I have given

[095-090]thee already, and it is this—never engage in a dispute about families,

[095-091]at least in the way of comparing them one with another; for necessarily

[095-092]one of those compared will be better than the other, and thou wilt be

[095-093]hated by the one thou hast disparaged, and get nothing in any shape

[095-094]from the one thou hast exalted.

[095-095]“Thy attire shall be hose of full length, a long jerkin, and a cloak a

[095-096]trifle longer; loose breeches by no means, for they are becoming

[095-097]neither for gentlemen nor for governors.

[095-098]“For the present, Sancho, this is all that has occurred to me to advise

[095-099]thee; as time goes by and occasions arise my instructions shall follow,

[095-100]if thou take care to let me know how thou art circumstanced.”

[095-101]“Señor,” said Sancho, “I see well enough that all these things your

[095-102]worship has said to me are good, holy, and profitable; but what use

[095-103]will they be to me if I don’t remember one of them? To be sure that

[095-104]about not letting my nails grow, and marrying again if I have the

[095-105]chance, will not slip out of my head; but all that other hash, muddle,

[095-106]and jumble—I don’t and can’t recollect any more of it than of last

[095-107]year’s clouds; so it must be given me in writing; for though I can’t

[095-108]either read or write, I’ll give it to my confessor, to drive it into me

[095-109]and remind me of it whenever it is necessary.”

[095-110]“Ah, sinner that I am!” said Don Quixote, “how bad it looks in

[095-111]governors not to know how to read or write; for let me tell thee,

[095-112]Sancho, when a man knows not how to read, or is left-handed, it argues

[095-113]one of two things; either that he was the son of exceedingly mean and

[095-114]lowly parents, or that he himself was so incorrigible and

[095-115]ill-conditioned that neither good company nor good teaching could make

[095-116]any impression on him. It is a great defect that thou labourest under,

[095-117]and therefore I would have thee learn at any rate to sign thy name.”

[095-118]“I can sign my name well enough,” said Sancho, “for when I was steward

[095-119]of the brotherhood in my village I learned to make certain letters,

[095-120]like the marks on bales of goods, which they told me made out my name.

[095-121]Besides I can pretend my right hand is disabled and make someone else

[095-122]sign for me, for ‘there’s a remedy for everything except death;’ and as

[095-123]I shall be in command and hold the staff, I can do as I like; moreover,

[095-124]‘he who has the alcalde for his father—,’ and I’ll be governor, and

[095-125]that’s higher than alcalde. Only come and see! Let them make light of

[095-126]me and abuse me; ‘they’ll come for wool and go back shorn;’ ‘whom God

[095-127]loves, his house is known to Him;’ ‘the silly sayings of the rich pass

[095-128]for saws in the world;’ and as I’ll be rich, being a governor, and at

[095-129]the same time generous, as I mean to be, no fault will be seen in me.

[095-130]‘Only make yourself honey and the flies will suck you;’ ‘as much as

[095-131]thou hast so much art thou worth,’ as my grandmother used to say; and

[095-132]‘thou canst have no revenge of a man of substance.’”

[095-133]“Oh, God’s curse upon thee, Sancho!” here exclaimed Don Quixote; “sixty

[095-134]thousand devils fly away with thee and thy proverbs! For the last hour

[095-135]thou hast been stringing them together and inflicting the pangs of

[095-136]torture on me with every one of them. Those proverbs will bring thee to

[095-137]the gallows one day, I promise thee; thy subjects will take the

[095-138]government from thee, or there will be revolts among them. Tell me,

[095-139]where dost thou pick them up, thou booby? How dost thou apply them,

[095-140]thou blockhead? For with me, to utter one and make it apply properly, I

[095-141]have to sweat and labour as if I were digging.”

[095-142]“By God, master mine,” said Sancho, “your worship is making a fuss

[095-143]about very little. Why the devil should you be vexed if I make use of

[095-144]what is my own? And I have got nothing else, nor any other stock in

[095-145]trade except proverbs and more proverbs; and here are three just this

[095-146]instant come into my head, pat to the purpose and like pears in a

[095-147]basket; but I won’t repeat them, for ‘sage silence is called Sancho.’”

[095-148]“That, Sancho, thou art not,” said Don Quixote; “for not only art thou

[095-149]not sage silence, but thou art pestilent prate and perversity; still I

[095-150]would like to know what three proverbs have just now come into thy

[095-151]memory, for I have been turning over mine own—and it is a good one—and

[095-152]none occurs to me.”

[095-153]“What can be better,” said Sancho, “than ‘never put thy thumbs between

[095-154]two back teeth;’ and ‘to “_get out of my house_” and “_what do you want

[095-155]with my wife?_” there is no answer;’ and ‘whether the pitcher hits the

[095-156]stone, or the stone the pitcher, it’s a bad business for the pitcher;’

[095-157]all which fit to a hair? For no one should quarrel with his governor,

[095-158]or him in authority over him, because he will come off the worst, as he

[095-159]does who puts his finger between two back and if they are not back

[095-160]teeth it makes no difference, so long as they are teeth; and to

[095-161]whatever the governor may say there’s no answer, any more than to ‘get

[095-162]out of my house’ and ‘what do you want with my wife?’ and then, as for

[095-163]that about the stone and the pitcher, a blind man could see that. So

[095-164]that he ‘who sees the mote in another’s eye had need to see the beam in

[095-165]his own,’ that it be not said of himself, ‘the dead woman was

[095-166]frightened at the one with her throat cut;’ and your worship knows well

[095-167]that ‘the fool knows more in his own house than the wise man in

[095-168]another’s.’”

[095-169]“Nay, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “the fool knows nothing, either in his

[095-170]own house or in anybody else’s, for no wise structure of any sort can

[095-171]stand on a foundation of folly; but let us say no more about it,

[095-172]Sancho, for if thou governest badly, thine will be the fault and mine

[095-173]the shame; but I comfort myself with having done my duty in advising

[095-174]thee as earnestly and as wisely as I could; and thus I am released from

[095-175]my obligations and my promise. God guide thee, Sancho, and govern thee

[095-176]in thy government, and deliver me from the misgiving I have that thou

[095-177]wilt turn the whole island upside down, a thing I might easily prevent

[095-178]by explaining to the duke what thou art and telling him that all that

[095-179]fat little person of thine is nothing else but a sack full of proverbs

[095-180]and sauciness.”

[095-181]“Señor,” said Sancho, “if your worship thinks I’m not fit for this

[095-182]government, I give it up on the spot; for the mere black of the nail of

[095-183]my soul is dearer to me than my whole body; and I can live just as

[095-184]well, simple Sancho, on bread and onions, as governor, on partridges

[095-185]and capons; and what’s more, while we’re asleep we’re all equal, great

[095-186]and small, rich and poor. But if your worship looks into it, you will

[095-187]see it was your worship alone that put me on to this business of

[095-188]governing; for I know no more about the government of islands than a

[095-189]buzzard; and if there’s any reason to think that because of my being a

[095-190]governor the devil will get hold of me, I’d rather go Sancho to heaven

[095-191]than governor to hell.”

[095-192]“By God, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “for those last words thou hast

[095-193]uttered alone, I consider thou deservest to be governor of a thousand

[095-194]islands. Thou hast good natural instincts, without which no knowledge

[095-195]is worth anything; commend thyself to God, and try not to swerve in the

[095-196]pursuit of thy main object; I mean, always make it thy aim and fixed

[095-197]purpose to do right in all matters that come before thee, for heaven

[095-198]always helps good intentions; and now let us go to dinner, for I think

[095-199]my lord and lady are waiting for us.”
