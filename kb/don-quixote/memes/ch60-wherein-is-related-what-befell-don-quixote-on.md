WHEREIN IS RELATED WHAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE ON HIS WAY TO SEE HIS LADY

DULCINEA DEL TOBOSO

p08a.jpg (65K)


[060-001]“Blessed be Allah the all-powerful!” says Hamete Benengeli on beginning

[060-002]this eighth chapter; “blessed be Allah!” he repeats three times; and he

[060-003]says he utters these thanksgivings at seeing that he has now got Don

[060-004]Quixote and Sancho fairly afield, and that the readers of his

[060-005]delightful history may reckon that the achievements and humours of Don

[060-006]Quixote and his squire are now about to begin; and he urges them to

[060-007]forget the former chivalries of the ingenious gentleman and to fix

[060-008]their eyes on those that are to come, which now begin on the road to El

[060-009]Toboso, as the others began on the plains of Montiel; nor is it much

[060-010]that he asks in consideration of all he promises, and so he goes on to

[060-011]say:

[060-012]Don Quixote and Sancho were left alone, and the moment Samson took his

[060-013]departure, Rocinante began to neigh, and Dapple to sigh, which, by both

[060-014]knight and squire, was accepted as a good sign and a very happy omen;

[060-015]though, if the truth is to be told, the sighs and brays of Dapple were

[060-016]louder than the neighings of the hack, from which Sancho inferred that

[060-017]his good fortune was to exceed and overtop that of his master,

[060-018]building, perhaps, upon some judicial astrology that he may have known,

[060-019]though the history says nothing about it; all that can be said is, that

[060-020]when he stumbled or fell, he was heard to say he wished he had not come

[060-021]out, for by stumbling or falling there was nothing to be got but a

[060-022]damaged shoe or a broken rib; and, fool as he was, he was not much

[060-023]astray in this.

[060-024]Said Don Quixote, “Sancho, my friend, night is drawing on upon us as we

[060-025]go, and more darkly than will allow us to reach El Toboso by daylight;

[060-026]for there I am resolved to go before I engage in another adventure, and

[060-027]there I shall obtain the blessing and generous permission of the

[060-028]peerless Dulcinea, with which permission I expect and feel assured that

[060-029]I shall conclude and bring to a happy termination every perilous

[060-030]adventure; for nothing in life makes knights-errant more valorous than

[060-031]finding themselves favoured by their ladies.”

p08b.jpg (283K)


[060-032]“So I believe,” replied Sancho; “but I think it will be difficult for

[060-033]your worship to speak with her or see her, at any rate where you will

[060-034]be able to receive her blessing; unless, indeed, she throws it over the

[060-035]wall of the yard where I saw her the time before, when I took her the

[060-036]letter that told of the follies and mad things your worship was doing

[060-037]in the heart of Sierra Morena.”

[060-038]“Didst thou take that for a yard wall, Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[060-039]“where or at which thou sawest that never sufficiently extolled grace

[060-040]and beauty? It must have been the gallery, corridor, or portico of some

[060-041]rich and royal palace.”

[060-042]“It might have been all that,” returned Sancho, “but to me it looked

[060-043]like a wall, unless I am short of memory.”

[060-044]“At all events, let us go there, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “for, so

[060-045]that I see her, it is the same to me whether it be over a wall, or at a

[060-046]window, or through the chink of a door, or the grate of a garden; for

[060-047]any beam of the sun of her beauty that reaches my eyes will give light

[060-048]to my reason and strength to my heart, so that I shall be unmatched and

[060-049]unequalled in wisdom and valour.”

[060-050]“Well, to tell the truth, señor,” said Sancho, “when I saw that sun of

[060-051]the lady Dulcinea del Toboso, it was not bright enough to throw out

[060-052]beams at all; it must have been, that as her grace was sifting that

[060-053]wheat I told you of, the thick dust she raised came before her face

[060-054]like a cloud and dimmed it.”

[060-055]“What! dost thou still persist, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “in saying,

[060-056]thinking, believing, and maintaining that my lady Dulcinea was sifting

[060-057]wheat, that being an occupation and task entirely at variance with what

[060-058]is and should be the employment of persons of distinction, who are

[060-059]constituted and reserved for other avocations and pursuits that show

[060-060]their rank a bowshot off? Thou hast forgotten, O Sancho, those lines of

[060-061]our poet wherein he paints for us how, in their crystal abodes, those

[060-062]four nymphs employed themselves who rose from their loved Tagus and

[060-063]seated themselves in a verdant meadow to embroider those tissues which

[060-064]the ingenious poet there describes to us, how they were worked and

[060-065]woven with gold and silk and pearls; and something of this sort must

[060-066]have been the employment of my lady when thou sawest her, only that the

[060-067]spite which some wicked enchanter seems to have against everything of

[060-068]mine changes all those things that give me pleasure, and turns them

[060-069]into shapes unlike their own; and so I fear that in that history of my

[060-070]achievements which they say is now in print, if haply its author was

[060-071]some sage who is an enemy of mine, he will have put one thing for

[060-072]another, mingling a thousand lies with one truth, and amusing himself

[060-073]by relating transactions which have nothing to do with the sequence of

[060-074]a true history. O envy, root of all countless evils, and cankerworm of

[060-075]the virtues! All the vices, Sancho, bring some kind of pleasure with

[060-076]them; but envy brings nothing but irritation, bitterness, and rage.”

[060-077]“So I say too,” replied Sancho; “and I suspect in that legend or

[060-078]history of us that the bachelor Samson Carrasco told us he saw, my

[060-079]honour goes dragged in the dirt, knocked about, up and down, sweeping

[060-080]the streets, as they say. And yet, on the faith of an honest man, I

[060-081]never spoke ill of any enchanter, and I am not so well off that I am to

[060-082]be envied; to be sure, I am rather sly, and I have a certain spice of

[060-083]the rogue in me; but all is covered by the great cloak of my

[060-084]simplicity, always natural and never acted; and if I had no other merit

[060-085]save that I believe, as I always do, firmly and truly in God, and all

[060-086]the holy Roman Catholic Church holds and believes, and that I am a

[060-087]mortal enemy of the Jews, the historians ought to have mercy on me and

[060-088]treat me well in their writings. But let them say what they like; naked

[060-089]was I born, naked I find myself, I neither lose nor gain; nay, while I

[060-090]see myself put into a book and passed on from hand to hand over the

[060-091]world, I don’t care a fig, let them say what they like of me.”

[060-092]“That, Sancho,” returned Don Quixote, “reminds me of what happened to a

[060-093]famous poet of our own day, who, having written a bitter satire against

[060-094]all the courtesan ladies, did not insert or name in it a certain lady

[060-095]of whom it was questionable whether she was one or not. She, seeing she

[060-096]was not in the list of the poet, asked him what he had seen in her that

[060-097]he did not include her in the number of the others, telling him he must

[060-098]add to his satire and put her in the new part, or else look out for the

[060-099]consequences. The poet did as she bade him, and left her without a

[060-100]shred of reputation, and she was satisfied by getting fame though it

[060-101]was infamy. In keeping with this is what they relate of that shepherd

[060-102]who set fire to the famous temple of Diana, by repute one of the seven

[060-103]wonders of the world, and burned it with the sole object of making his

[060-104]name live in after ages; and, though it was forbidden to name him, or

[060-105]mention his name by word of mouth or in writing, lest the object of his

[060-106]ambition should be attained, nevertheless it became known that he was

[060-107]called Erostratus. And something of the same sort is what happened in

[060-108]the case of the great emperor Charles V. and a gentleman in Rome. The

[060-109]emperor was anxious to see that famous temple of the Rotunda, called in

[060-110]ancient times the temple ‘of all the gods,’ but now-a-days, by a better

[060-111]nomenclature, ‘of all the saints,’ which is the best preserved building

[060-112]of all those of pagan construction in Rome, and the one which best

[060-113]sustains the reputation of mighty works and magnificence of its

[060-114]founders. It is in the form of a half orange, of enormous dimensions,

[060-115]and well lighted, though no light penetrates it save that which is

[060-116]admitted by a window, or rather round skylight, at the top; and it was

[060-117]from this that the emperor examined the building. A Roman gentleman

[060-118]stood by his side and explained to him the skilful construction and

[060-119]ingenuity of the vast fabric and its wonderful architecture, and when

[060-120]they had left the skylight he said to the emperor, ‘A thousand times,

[060-121]your Sacred Majesty, the impulse came upon me to seize your Majesty in

[060-122]my arms and fling myself down from yonder skylight, so as to leave

[060-123]behind me in the world a name that would last for ever.’ ‘I am thankful

[060-124]to you for not carrying such an evil thought into effect,’ said the

[060-125]emperor, ‘and I shall give you no opportunity in future of again

[060-126]putting your loyalty to the test; and I therefore forbid you ever to

[060-127]speak to me or to be where I am; and he followed up these words by

[060-128]bestowing a liberal bounty upon him. My meaning is, Sancho, that the

[060-129]desire of acquiring fame is a very powerful motive. What, thinkest

[060-130]thou, was it that flung Horatius in full armour down from the bridge

[060-131]into the depths of the Tiber? What burned the hand and arm of Mutius?

[060-132]What impelled Curtius to plunge into the deep burning gulf that opened

[060-133]in the midst of Rome? What, in opposition to all the omens that

[060-134]declared against him, made Julius Cæsar cross the Rubicon? And to come

[060-135]to more modern examples, what scuttled the ships, and left stranded and

[060-136]cut off the gallant Spaniards under the command of the most courteous

[060-137]Cortés in the New World? All these and a variety of other great

[060-138]exploits are, were and will be, the work of fame that mortals desire as

[060-139]a reward and a portion of the immortality their famous deeds deserve;

[060-140]though we Catholic Christians and knights-errant look more to that

[060-141]future glory that is everlasting in the ethereal regions of heaven than

[060-142]to the vanity of the fame that is to be acquired in this present

[060-143]transitory life; a fame that, however long it may last, must after all

[060-144]end with the world itself, which has its own appointed end. So that, O

[060-145]Sancho, in what we do we must not overpass the bounds which the

[060-146]Christian religion we profess has assigned to us. We have to slay pride

[060-147]in giants, envy by generosity and nobleness of heart, anger by calmness

[060-148]of demeanour and equanimity, gluttony and sloth by the spareness of our

[060-149]diet and the length of our vigils, lust and lewdness by the loyalty we

[060-150]preserve to those whom we have made the mistresses of our thoughts,

[060-151]indolence by traversing the world in all directions seeking

[060-152]opportunities of making ourselves, besides Christians, famous knights.

[060-153]Such, Sancho, are the means by which we reach those extremes of praise

[060-154]that fair fame carries with it.”

[060-155]“All that your worship has said so far,” said Sancho, “I have

[060-156]understood quite well; but still I would be glad if your worship would

[060-157]dissolve a doubt for me, which has just this minute come into my mind.”

[060-158]“Solve, thou meanest, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “say on, in God’s

[060-159]name, and I will answer as well as I can.”

[060-160]“Tell me, señor,” Sancho went on to say, “those Julys or Augusts, and

[060-161]all those venturous knights that you say are now dead—where are they

[060-162]now?”

[060-163]“The heathens,” replied Don Quixote, “are, no doubt, in hell; the

[060-164]Christians, if they were good Christians, are either in purgatory or in

[060-165]heaven.”

[060-166]“Very good,” said Sancho; “but now I want to know—the tombs where the

[060-167]bodies of those great lords are, have they silver lamps before them, or

[060-168]are the walls of their chapels ornamented with crutches,

[060-169]winding-sheets, tresses of hair, legs and eyes in wax? Or what are they

[060-170]ornamented with?”

[060-171]To which Don Quixote made answer: “The tombs of the heathens were

[060-172]generally sumptuous temples; the ashes of Julius Cæsar’s body were

[060-173]placed on the top of a stone pyramid of vast size, which they now call

[060-174]in Rome Saint Peter’s needle. The emperor Hadrian had for a tomb a

[060-175]castle as large as a good-sized village, which they called the _Moles

[060-176]Adriani_, and is now the castle of St. Angelo in Rome. The queen

[060-177]Artemisia buried her husband Mausolus in a tomb which was reckoned one

[060-178]of the seven wonders of the world; but none of these tombs, or of the

[060-179]many others of the heathens, were ornamented with winding-sheets or any

[060-180]of those other offerings and tokens that show that they who are buried

[060-181]there are saints.”

[060-182]“That’s the point I’m coming to,” said Sancho; “and now tell me, which

[060-183]is the greater work, to bring a dead man to life or to kill a giant?”

[060-184]“The answer is easy,” replied Don Quixote; “it is a greater work to

[060-185]bring to life a dead man.”

[060-186]“Now I have got you,” said Sancho; “in that case the fame of them who

[060-187]bring the dead to life, who give sight to the blind, cure cripples,

[060-188]restore health to the sick, and before whose tombs there are lamps

[060-189]burning, and whose chapels are filled with devout folk on their knees

[060-190]adoring their relics be a better fame in this life and in the other

[060-191]than that which all the heathen emperors and knights-errant that have

[060-192]ever been in the world have left or may leave behind them?”

[060-193]“That I grant, too,” said Don Quixote.

[060-194]“Then this fame, these favours, these privileges, or whatever you call

[060-195]it,” said Sancho, “belong to the bodies and relics of the saints who,

[060-196]with the approbation and permission of our holy mother Church, have

[060-197]lamps, tapers, winding-sheets, crutches, pictures, eyes and legs, by

[060-198]means of which they increase devotion and add to their own Christian

[060-199]reputation. Kings carry the bodies or relics of saints on their

[060-200]shoulders, and kiss bits of their bones, and enrich and adorn their

[060-201]oratories and favourite altars with them.”

[060-202]“What wouldst thou have me infer from all thou hast said, Sancho?”

[060-203]asked Don Quixote.

[060-204]“My meaning is,” said Sancho, “let us set about becoming saints, and we

[060-205]shall obtain more quickly the fair fame we are striving after; for you

[060-206]know, señor, yesterday or the day before yesterday (for it is so lately

[060-207]one may say so) they canonised and beatified two little barefoot

[060-208]friars, and it is now reckoned the greatest good luck to kiss or touch

[060-209]the iron chains with which they girt and tortured their bodies, and

[060-210]they are held in greater veneration, so it is said, than the sword of

[060-211]Roland in the armoury of our lord the King, whom God preserve. So that,

[060-212]señor, it is better to be an humble little friar of no matter what

[060-213]order, than a valiant knight-errant; with God a couple of dozen of

[060-214]penance lashings are of more avail than two thousand lance-thrusts, be

[060-215]they given to giants, or monsters, or dragons.”

[060-216]“All that is true,” returned Don Quixote, “but we cannot all be friars,

[060-217]and many are the ways by which God takes his own to heaven; chivalry is

[060-218]a religion, there are sainted knights in glory.”

[060-219]“Yes,” said Sancho, “but I have heard say that there are more friars in

[060-220]heaven than knights-errant.”

[060-221]“That,” said Don Quixote, “is because those in religious orders are

[060-222]more numerous than knights.”

[060-223]“The errants are many,” said Sancho.

[060-224]“Many,” replied Don Quixote, “but few they who deserve the name of

[060-225]knights.”

[060-226]With these, and other discussions of the same sort, they passed that

[060-227]night and the following day, without anything worth mention happening

[060-228]to them, whereat Don Quixote was not a little dejected; but at length

[060-229]the next day, at daybreak, they descried the great city of El Toboso,

[060-230]at the sight of which Don Quixote’s spirits rose and Sancho’s fell, for

[060-231]he did not know Dulcinea’s house, nor in all his life had he ever seen

[060-232]her, any more than his master; so that they were both uneasy, the one

[060-233]to see her, the other at not having seen her, and Sancho was at a loss

[060-234]to know what he was to do when his master sent him to El Toboso. In the

[060-235]end, Don Quixote made up his mind to enter the city at nightfall, and

[060-236]they waited until the time came among some oak trees that were near El

[060-237]Toboso; and when the moment they had agreed upon arrived, they made

[060-238]their entrance into the city, where something happened them that may

[060-239]fairly be called something.
