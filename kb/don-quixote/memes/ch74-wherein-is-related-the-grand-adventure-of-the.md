WHEREIN IS RELATED THE GRAND ADVENTURE OF THE CAVE OF MONTESINOS IN THE

[074-001]HEART OF LA MANCHA, WHICH THE VALIANT DON QUIXOTE BROUGHT TO A HAPPY

TERMINATION

p22a.jpg (112K)


[074-002]Many and great were the attentions shown to Don Quixote by the newly

[074-003]married couple, who felt themselves under an obligation to him for

[074-004]coming forward in defence of their cause; and they exalted his wisdom

[074-005]to the same level with his courage, rating him as a Cid in arms, and a

[074-006]Cicero in eloquence. Worthy Sancho enjoyed himself for three days at

[074-007]the expense of the pair, from whom they learned that the sham wound was

[074-008]not a scheme arranged with the fair Quiteria, but a device of

[074-009]Basilio’s, who counted on exactly the result they had seen; he

[074-010]confessed, it is true, that he had confided his idea to some of his

[074-011]friends, so that at the proper time they might aid him in his purpose

[074-012]and insure the success of the deception.

p22b.jpg (344K)


[074-013]“That,” said Don Quixote, “is not and ought not to be called deception

[074-014]which aims at virtuous ends;” and the marriage of lovers he maintained

[074-015]to be a most excellent end, reminding them, however, that love has no

[074-016]greater enemy than hunger and constant want; for love is all gaiety,

[074-017]enjoyment, and happiness, especially when the lover is in the

[074-018]possession of the object of his love, and poverty and want are the

[074-019]declared enemies of all these; which he said to urge Señor Basilio to

[074-020]abandon the practice of those accomplishments he was skilled in, for

[074-021]though they brought him fame, they brought him no money, and apply

[074-022]himself to the acquisition of wealth by legitimate industry, which will

[074-023]never fail those who are prudent and persevering. The poor man who is a

[074-024]man of honour (if indeed a poor man can be a man of honour) has a jewel

[074-025]when he has a fair wife, and if she is taken from him, his honour is

[074-026]taken from him and slain. The fair woman who is a woman of honour, and

[074-027]whose husband is poor, deserves to be crowned with the laurels and

[074-028]crowns of victory and triumph. Beauty by itself attracts the desires of

[074-029]all who behold it, and the royal eagles and birds of towering flight

[074-030]stoop on it as on a dainty lure; but if beauty be accompanied by want

[074-031]and penury, then the ravens and the kites and other birds of prey

[074-032]assail it, and she who stands firm against such attacks well deserves

[074-033]to be called the crown of her husband. “Remember, O prudent Basilio,”

[074-034]added Don Quixote, “it was the opinion of a certain sage, I know not

[074-035]whom, that there was not more than one good woman in the whole world;

[074-036]and his advice was that each one should think and believe that this one

[074-037]good woman was his own wife, and in this way he would live happy. I

[074-038]myself am not married, nor, so far, has it ever entered my thoughts to

[074-039]be so; nevertheless I would venture to give advice to anyone who might

[074-040]ask it, as to the mode in which he should seek a wife such as he would

[074-041]be content to marry. The first thing I would recommend him, would be to

[074-042]look to good name rather than to wealth, for a good woman does not win

[074-043]a good name merely by being good, but by letting it be seen that she is

[074-044]so, and open looseness and freedom do much more damage to a woman’s

[074-045]honour than secret depravity. If you take a good woman into your house

[074-046]it will be an easy matter to keep her good, and even to make her still

[074-047]better; but if you take a bad one you will find it hard work to mend

[074-048]her, for it is no very easy matter to pass from one extreme to another.

[074-049]I do not say it is impossible, but I look upon it as difficult.”

[074-050]Sancho, listening to all this, said to himself, “This master of mine,

[074-051]when I say anything that has weight and substance, says I might take a

[074-052]pulpit in hand, and go about the world preaching fine sermons; but I

[074-053]say of him that, when he begins stringing maxims together and giving

[074-054]advice not only might he take a pulpit in hand, but two on each finger,

[074-055]and go into the market-places to his heart’s content. Devil take you

[074-056]for a knight-errant, what a lot of things you know! I used to think in

[074-057]my heart that the only thing he knew was what belonged to his chivalry;

[074-058]but there is nothing he won’t have a finger in.”

[074-059]Sancho muttered this somewhat aloud, and his master overheard him, and

[074-060]asked, “What art thou muttering there, Sancho?”

[074-061]“I’m not saying anything or muttering anything,” said Sancho; “I was

[074-062]only saying to myself that I wish I had heard what your worship has

[074-063]said just now before I married; perhaps I’d say now, ‘The ox that’s

[074-064]loose licks himself well.’”

[074-065]“Is thy Teresa so bad then, Sancho?”

[074-066]“She is not very bad,” replied Sancho; “but she is not very good; at

[074-067]least she is not as good as I could wish.”

[074-068]“Thou dost wrong, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “to speak ill of thy wife;

[074-069]for after all she is the mother of thy children.” “We are quits,”

[074-070]returned Sancho; “for she speaks ill of me whenever she takes it into

[074-071]her head, especially when she is jealous; and Satan himself could not

[074-072]put up with her then.”

[074-073]In fine, they remained three days with the newly married couple, by

[074-074]whom they were entertained and treated like kings. Don Quixote begged

[074-075]the fencing licentiate to find him a guide to show him the way to the

[074-076]cave of Montesinos, as he had a great desire to enter it and see with

[074-077]his own eyes if the wonderful tales that were told of it all over the

[074-078]country were true. The licentiate said he would get him a cousin of his

[074-079]own, a famous scholar, and one very much given to reading books of

[074-080]chivalry, who would have great pleasure in conducting him to the mouth

[074-081]of the very cave, and would show him the lakes of Ruidera, which were

[074-082]likewise famous all over La Mancha, and even all over Spain; and he

[074-083]assured him he would find him entertaining, for he was a youth who

[074-084]could write books good enough to be printed and dedicated to princes.

[074-085]The cousin arrived at last, leading an ass in foal, with a pack-saddle

[074-086]covered with a parti-coloured carpet or sackcloth; Sancho saddled

[074-087]Rocinante, got Dapple ready, and stocked his alforjas, along with which

[074-088]went those of the cousin, likewise well filled; and so, commending

[074-089]themselves to God and bidding farewell to all, they set out, taking the

[074-090]road for the famous cave of Montesinos.

[074-091]On the way Don Quixote asked the cousin of what sort and character his

[074-092]pursuits, avocations, and studies were, to which he replied that he was

[074-093]by profession a humanist, and that his pursuits and studies were making

[074-094]books for the press, all of great utility and no less entertainment to

[074-095]the nation. One was called “The Book of Liveries,” in which he

[074-096]described seven hundred and three liveries, with their colours,

[074-097]mottoes, and ciphers, from which gentlemen of the court might pick and

[074-098]choose any they fancied for festivals and revels, without having to go

[074-099]a-begging for them from anyone, or puzzling their brains, as the saying

[074-100]is, to have them appropriate to their objects and purposes; “for,” said

[074-101]he, “I give the jealous, the rejected, the forgotten, the absent, what

[074-102]will suit them, and fit them without fail. I have another book, too,

[074-103]which I shall call ‘Metamorphoses, or the Spanish Ovid,’ one of rare

[074-104]and original invention, for imitating Ovid in burlesque style, I show

[074-105]in it who the Giralda of Seville and the Angel of the Magdalena were,

[074-106]what the sewer of Vecinguerra at Cordova was, what the bulls of

[074-107]Guisando, the Sierra Morena, the Leganitos and Lavapies fountains at

[074-108]Madrid, not forgetting those of the Piojo, of the Cano Dorado, and of

[074-109]the Priora; and all with their allegories, metaphors, and changes, so

[074-110]that they are amusing, interesting, and instructive, all at once.

[074-111]Another book I have which I call ‘The Supplement to Polydore Vergil,’

[074-112]which treats of the invention of things, and is a work of great

[074-113]erudition and research, for I establish and elucidate elegantly some

[074-114]things of great importance which Polydore omitted to mention. He forgot

[074-115]to tell us who was the first man in the world that had a cold in his

[074-116]head, and who was the first to try salivation for the French disease,

[074-117]but I give it accurately set forth, and quote more than five-and-twenty

[074-118]authors in proof of it, so you may perceive I have laboured to good

[074-119]purpose and that the book will be of service to the whole world.”

[074-120]Sancho, who had been very attentive to the cousin’s words, said to him,

[074-121]“Tell me, señor—and God give you luck in printing your books—can you

[074-122]tell me (for of course you know, as you know everything) who was the

[074-123]first man that scratched his head? For to my thinking it must have been

[074-124]our father Adam.”

[074-125]“So it must,” replied the cousin; “for there is no doubt but Adam had a

[074-126]head and hair; and being the first man in the world he would have

[074-127]scratched himself sometimes.”

[074-128]“So I think,” said Sancho; “but now tell me, who was the first tumbler

[074-129]in the world?”

[074-130]“Really, brother,” answered the cousin, “I could not at this moment say

[074-131]positively without having investigated it; I will look it up when I go

[074-132]back to where I have my books, and will satisfy you the next time we

[074-133]meet, for this will not be the last time.”

[074-134]“Look here, señor,” said Sancho, “don’t give yourself any trouble about

[074-135]it, for I have just this minute hit upon what I asked you. The first

[074-136]tumbler in the world, you must know, was Lucifer, when they cast or

[074-137]pitched him out of heaven; for he came tumbling into the bottomless

[074-138]pit.”

[074-139]“You are right, friend,” said the cousin; and said Don Quixote,

[074-140]“Sancho, that question and answer are not thine own; thou hast heard

[074-141]them from someone else.”

[074-142]“Hold your peace, señor,” said Sancho; “faith, if I take to asking

[074-143]questions and answering, I’ll go on from this till to-morrow morning.

[074-144]Nay! to ask foolish things and answer nonsense I needn’t go looking for

[074-145]help from my neighbours.”

[074-146]“Thou hast said more than thou art aware of, Sancho,” said Don Quixote;

[074-147]“for there are some who weary themselves out in learning and proving

[074-148]things that, after they are known and proved, are not worth a farthing

[074-149]to the understanding or memory.”

[074-150]In this and other pleasant conversation the day went by, and that night

[074-151]they put up at a small hamlet whence it was not more than two leagues

[074-152]to the cave of Montesinos, so the cousin told Don Quixote, adding, that

[074-153]if he was bent upon entering it, it would be requisite for him to

[074-154]provide himself with ropes, so that he might be tied and lowered into

[074-155]its depths. Don Quixote said that even if it reached to the bottomless

[074-156]pit he meant to see where it went to; so they bought about a hundred

[074-157]fathoms of rope, and next day at two in the afternoon they arrived at

[074-158]the cave, the mouth of which is spacious and wide, but full of thorn

[074-159]and wild-fig bushes and brambles and briars, so thick and matted that

[074-160]they completely close it up and cover it over.

[074-161]On coming within sight of it the cousin, Sancho, and Don Quixote

[074-162]dismounted, and the first two immediately tied the latter very firmly

[074-163]with the ropes, and as they were girding and swathing him Sancho said

[074-164]to him, “Mind what you are about, master mine; don’t go burying

[074-165]yourself alive, or putting yourself where you’ll be like a bottle put

[074-166]to cool in a well; it’s no affair or business of your worship’s to

[074-167]become the explorer of this, which must be worse than a Moorish

[074-168]dungeon.”

[074-169]“Tie me and hold thy peace,” said Don Quixote, “for an emprise like

[074-170]this, friend Sancho, was reserved for me;” and said the guide, “I beg

[074-171]of you, Señor Don Quixote, to observe carefully and examine with a

[074-172]hundred eyes everything that is within there; perhaps there may be some

[074-173]things for me to put into my book of ‘Transformations.’”

[074-174]“The drum is in hands that will know how to beat it well enough,” said

[074-175]Sancho Panza.

[074-176]When he had said this and finished the tying (which was not over the

[074-177]armour but only over the doublet) Don Quixote observed, “It was

[074-178]careless of us not to have provided ourselves with a small cattle-bell

[074-179]to be tied on the rope close to me, the sound of which would show that

[074-180]I was still descending and alive; but as that is out of the question

[074-181]now, in God’s hand be it to guide me;” and forthwith he fell on his

[074-182]knees and in a low voice offered up a prayer to heaven, imploring God

[074-183]to aid him and grant him success in this to all appearance perilous and

[074-184]untried adventure, and then exclaimed aloud, “O mistress of my actions

[074-185]and movements, illustrious and peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, if so be

[074-186]the prayers and supplications of this fortunate lover can reach thy

[074-187]ears, by thy incomparable beauty I entreat thee to listen to them, for

[074-188]they but ask thee not to refuse me thy favour and protection now that I

[074-189]stand in such need of them. I am about to precipitate, to sink, to

[074-190]plunge myself into the abyss that is here before me, only to let the

[074-191]world know that while thou dost favour me there is no impossibility I

[074-192]will not attempt and accomplish.” With these words he approached the

[074-193]cavern, and perceived that it was impossible to let himself down or

[074-194]effect an entrance except by sheer force or cleaving a passage; so

[074-195]drawing his sword he began to demolish and cut away the brambles at the

[074-196]mouth of the cave, at the noise of which a vast multitude of crows and

[074-197]choughs flew out of it so thick and so fast that they knocked Don

[074-198]Quixote down; and if he had been as much of a believer in augury as he

[074-199]was a Catholic Christian he would have taken it as a bad omen and

[074-200]declined to bury himself in such a place. He got up, however, and as

[074-201]there came no more crows, or night-birds like the bats that flew out at

[074-202]the same time with the crows, the cousin and Sancho giving him rope, he

[074-203]lowered himself into the depths of the dread cavern; and as he entered

[074-204]it Sancho sent his blessing after him, making a thousand crosses over

[074-205]him and saying, “God, and the Peña de Francia, and the Trinity of Gaeta

[074-206]guide thee, flower and cream of knights-errant. There thou goest, thou

[074-207]dare-devil of the earth, heart of steel, arm of brass; once more, God

[074-208]guide thee and send thee back safe, sound, and unhurt to the light of

[074-209]this world thou art leaving to bury thyself in the darkness thou art

[074-210]seeking there;” and the cousin offered up almost the same prayers and

[074-211]supplications.

[074-212]Don Quixote kept calling to them to give him rope and more rope, and

[074-213]they gave it out little by little, and by the time the calls, which

[074-214]came out of the cave as out of a pipe, ceased to be heard they had let

[074-215]down the hundred fathoms of rope. They were inclined to pull Don

[074-216]Quixote up again, as they could give him no more rope; however, they

[074-217]waited about half an hour, at the end of which time they began to

[074-218]gather in the rope again with great ease and without feeling any

[074-219]weight, which made them fancy Don Quixote was remaining below; and

[074-220]persuaded that it was so, Sancho wept bitterly, and hauled away in

[074-221]great haste in order to settle the question. When, however, they had

[074-222]come to, as it seemed, rather more than eighty fathoms they felt a

[074-223]weight, at which they were greatly delighted; and at last, at ten

[074-224]fathoms more, they saw Don Quixote distinctly, and Sancho called out to

[074-225]him, saying, “Welcome back, señor, for we had begun to think you were

[074-226]going to stop there to found a family.” But Don Quixote answered not a

[074-227]word, and drawing him out entirely they perceived he had his eyes shut

[074-228]and every appearance of being fast asleep.

[074-229]They stretched him on the ground and untied him, but still he did not

[074-230]awake; however, they rolled him back and forwards and shook and pulled

[074-231]him about, so that after some time he came to himself, stretching

[074-232]himself just as if he were waking up from a deep and sound sleep, and

[074-233]looking about him he said, “God forgive you, friends; ye have taken me

[074-234]away from the sweetest and most delightful existence and spectacle that

[074-235]ever human being enjoyed or beheld. Now indeed do I know that all the

[074-236]pleasures of this life pass away like a shadow and a dream, or fade

[074-237]like the flower of the field. O ill-fated Montesinos! O sore-wounded

[074-238]Durandarte! O unhappy Belerma! O tearful Guadiana, and ye O hapless

[074-239]daughters of Ruidera who show in your waves the tears that flowed from

[074-240]your beauteous eyes!”

[074-241]The cousin and Sancho Panza listened with deep attention to the words

[074-242]of Don Quixote, who uttered them as though with immense pain he drew

[074-243]them up from his very bowels. They begged of him to explain himself,

[074-244]and tell them what he had seen in that hell down there.

[074-245]“Hell do you call it?” said Don Quixote; “call it by no such name, for

[074-246]it does not deserve it, as ye shall soon see.”

[074-247]He then begged them to give him something to eat, as he was very

[074-248]hungry. They spread the cousin’s sackcloth on the grass, and put the

[074-249]stores of the alforjas into requisition, and all three sitting down

[074-250]lovingly and sociably, they made a luncheon and a supper of it all in

[074-251]one; and when the sackcloth was removed, Don Quixote of La Mancha said,

[074-252]“Let no one rise, and attend to me, my sons, both of you.”
