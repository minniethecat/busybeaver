## Definition

Rocinante is Don Quixote's horse, and like his master, he is a figure of tragicomic transformation. The name itself is a Cervantine joke: *rocín* means a nag or broken-down workhorse, while *ante* signifies "before" or "formerly" — so Rocinante is "formerly a nag," a common horse elevated to knightly status by the mere addition of a suffix. Alonso Quijano spends four days devising the name before setting out, treating the christening of his horse with the same gravity he applies to renaming himself [001-120]. Rocinante is described as having more ailments than a goat and a leaner frame than a worn-out hack, yet in Don Quixote's eyes he surpasses the legendary Babieca of El Cid and is worthy of poetic commemoration.

## In Don Quixote

Rocinante is present in nearly every adventure Don Quixote undertakes, serving as both a literal mount and a symbol of his master's condition. The horse suffers alongside Don Quixote — thrown by windmill sails, beaten by muleteers, and generally subjected to the same physical indignities his master endures. Rocinante's name is among the first things Don Quixote declares when he arrives at the inn-turned-castle [002-154], and the horse's independent desires — notably, his attempt to "take some refreshment" with the Yanguesan mares [015-000] — repeatedly create complications for his master.

Cervantes even includes comic verses in the prefatory material, where Rocinante speaks in his own voice: "I am that Rocinante fa—, / Great-grandson of Babieca" [P01-1836]. This metafictional touch — a horse with a literary pedigree, speaking in verse — reinforces Rocinante's dual status as both a comic prop and a serious commentary on the nature of identity: if a nag can be a warhorse because his owner says so, what else might be transformed by the power of naming?

## Key Passages

> "He then went to have a look at his nag; and though the animal had more cracks in its hoof than a groat and more ailments than Gonnella's horse... it seemed to him that Alexander's Bucephalus and the Cid's Babieca were not to be compared with it. Four days he spent in considering what name to give it... and so after having designed many others, struck out, and rejected, he decided upon calling him Rocinante, a name, to his thinking, lofty, sonorous, and significant of what he had been when he was a nag — before becoming what he now was — the first and foremost of all the hacks in the world." [001-120]

> "'For I am that Rocinante fa—, / Great-grandson of Babieca...' — the comic verses from the prefatory sonnet 'On Sancho Panza and Rocinante'" [P01-1836]

## Critical Context

Rocinante is not merely a joke about a skinny horse. He embodies the novel's central paradox: transformation through language. Don Quixote cannot transform his actual circumstances — his armor is cardboard, his lady is a peasant, his horse is a nag — but he can transform their names. Rocinante's elevation from rocín to steed mirrors Alonso Quijano's elevation to Don Quixote, Aldonza Lorenzo's to Dulcinea, and the inn's to a castle. In this light, Rocinante is not just a comic sidekick but the novel's most compact symbol of the creative, world-making power of language itself.
