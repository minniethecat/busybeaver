OF THE STRANGE MANNER IN WHICH DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA WAS CARRIED

[047-001]AWAY ENCHANTED, TOGETHER WITH OTHER REMARKABLE INCIDENTS

c47a.jpg (181K)


[047-002]When Don Quixote saw himself caged and hoisted on the cart in this way,

[047-003]he said, “Many grave histories of knights-errant have I read; but never

[047-004]yet have I read, seen, or heard of their carrying off enchanted

[047-005]knights-errant in this fashion, or at the slow pace that these lazy,

[047-006]sluggish animals promise; for they always take them away through the

[047-007]air with marvellous swiftness, enveloped in a dark thick cloud, or on a

[047-008]chariot of fire, or it may be on some hippogriff or other beast of the

[047-009]kind; but to carry me off like this on an ox-cart! By God, it puzzles

[047-010]me! But perhaps the chivalry and enchantments of our day take a

[047-011]different course from that of those in days gone by; and it may be,

[047-012]too, that as I am a new knight in the world, and the first to revive

[047-013]the already forgotten calling of knight-adventurers, they may have

[047-014]newly invented other kinds of enchantments and other modes of carrying

[047-015]off the enchanted. What thinkest thou of the matter, Sancho my son?”

c47b.jpg (357K)


[047-016]“I don’t know what to think,” answered Sancho, “not being as well read

[047-017]as your worship in errant writings; but for all that I venture to say

[047-018]and swear that these apparitions that are about us are not quite

[047-019]catholic.”

[047-020]“Catholic!” said Don Quixote. “Father of me! how can they be Catholic

[047-021]when they are all devils that have taken fantastic shapes to come and

[047-022]do this, and bring me to this condition? And if thou wouldst prove it,

[047-023]touch them, and feel them, and thou wilt find they have only bodies of

[047-024]air, and no consistency except in appearance.”

[047-025]“By God, master,” returned Sancho, “I have touched them already; and

[047-026]that devil, that goes about there so busily, has firm flesh, and

[047-027]another property very different from what I have heard say devils have,

[047-028]for by all accounts they all smell of brimstone and other bad smells;

[047-029]but this one smells of amber half a league off.” Sancho was here

[047-030]speaking of Don Fernando, who, like a gentleman of his rank, was very

[047-031]likely perfumed as Sancho said.

[047-032]“Marvel not at that, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote; “for let me

[047-033]tell thee devils are crafty; and even if they do carry odours about

[047-034]with them, they themselves have no smell, because they are spirits; or,

[047-035]if they have any smell, they cannot smell of anything sweet, but of

[047-036]something foul and fetid; and the reason is that as they carry hell

[047-037]with them wherever they go, and can get no ease whatever from their

[047-038]torments, and as a sweet smell is a thing that gives pleasure and

[047-039]enjoyment, it is impossible that they can smell sweet; if, then, this

[047-040]devil thou speakest of seems to thee to smell of amber, either thou art

[047-041]deceiving thyself, or he wants to deceive thee by making thee fancy he

[047-042]is not a devil.”

[047-043]Such was the conversation that passed between master and man; and Don

[047-044]Fernando and Cardenio, apprehensive of Sancho’s making a complete

[047-045]discovery of their scheme, towards which he had already gone some way,

[047-046]resolved to hasten their departure, and calling the landlord aside,

[047-047]they directed him to saddle Rocinante and put the pack-saddle on

[047-048]Sancho’s ass, which he did with great alacrity. In the meantime the

[047-049]curate had made an arrangement with the officers that they should bear

[047-050]them company as far as his village, he paying them so much a day.

[047-051]Cardenio hung the buckler on one side of the bow of Rocinante’s saddle

[047-052]and the basin on the other, and by signs commanded Sancho to mount his

[047-053]ass and take Rocinante’s bridle, and at each side of the cart he placed

[047-054]two officers with their muskets; but before the cart was put in motion,

[047-055]out came the landlady and her daughter and Maritornes to bid Don

[047-056]Quixote farewell, pretending to weep with grief at his misfortune; and

[047-057]to them Don Quixote said:

[047-058]“Weep not, good ladies, for all these mishaps are the lot of those who

[047-059]follow the profession I profess; and if these reverses did not befall

[047-060]me I should not esteem myself a famous knight-errant; for such things

[047-061]never happen to knights of little renown and fame, because nobody in

[047-062]the world thinks about them; to valiant knights they do, for these are

[047-063]envied for their virtue and valour by many princes and other knights

[047-064]who compass the destruction of the worthy by base means. Nevertheless,

[047-065]virtue is of herself so mighty, that, in spite of all the magic that

[047-066]Zoroaster its first inventor knew, she will come victorious out of

[047-067]every trial, and shed her light upon the earth as the sun does upon the

[047-068]heavens. Forgive me, fair ladies, if, through inadvertence, I have in

[047-069]aught offended you; for intentionally and wittingly I have never done

[047-070]so to any; and pray to God that he deliver me from this captivity to

[047-071]which some malevolent enchanter has consigned me; and should I find

[047-072]myself released therefrom, the favours that ye have bestowed upon me in

[047-073]this castle shall be held in memory by me, that I may acknowledge,

[047-074]recognise, and requite them as they deserve.”

[047-075]While this was passing between the ladies of the castle and Don

[047-076]Quixote, the curate and the barber bade farewell to Don Fernando and

[047-077]his companions, to the captain, his brother, and the ladies, now all

[047-078]made happy, and in particular to Dorothea and Luscinda. They all

[047-079]embraced one another, and promised to let each other know how things

[047-080]went with them, and Don Fernando directed the curate where to write to

[047-081]him, to tell him what became of Don Quixote, assuring him that there

[047-082]was nothing that could give him more pleasure than to hear of it, and

[047-083]that he too, on his part, would send him word of everything he thought

[047-084]he would like to know, about his marriage, Zoraida’s baptism, Don

[047-085]Luis’s affair, and Luscinda’s return to her home. The curate promised

[047-086]to comply with his request carefully, and they embraced once more, and

[047-087]renewed their promises.

[047-088]The landlord approached the curate and handed him some papers, saying

[047-089]he had discovered them in the lining of the valise in which the novel

[047-090]of “The Ill-advised Curiosity” had been found, and that he might take

[047-091]them all away with him as their owner had not since returned; for, as

[047-092]he could not read, he did not want them himself. The curate thanked

[047-093]him, and opening them he saw at the beginning of the manuscript the

[047-094]words, “Novel of Rinconete and Cortadillo,” by which he perceived that

[047-095]it was a novel, and as that of “The Ill-advised Curiosity” had been

[047-096]good he concluded this would be so too, as they were both probably by

[047-097]the same author; so he kept it, intending to read it when he had an

[047-098]opportunity. He then mounted and his friend the barber did the same,

[047-099]both masked, so as not to be recognised by Don Quixote, and set out

[047-100]following in the rear of the cart. The order of march was this: first

[047-101]went the cart with the owner leading it; at each side of it marched the

[047-102]officers of the Brotherhood, as has been said, with their muskets; then

[047-103]followed Sancho Panza on his ass, leading Rocinante by the bridle; and

[047-104]behind all came the curate and the barber on their mighty mules, with

[047-105]faces covered, as aforesaid, and a grave and serious air, measuring

[047-106]their pace to suit the slow steps of the oxen. Don Quixote was seated

[047-107]in the cage, with his hands tied and his feet stretched out, leaning

[047-108]against the bars as silent and as patient as if he were a stone statue

[047-109]and not a man of flesh. Thus slowly and silently they made, it might

[047-110]be, two leagues, until they reached a valley which the carter thought a

[047-111]convenient place for resting and feeding his oxen, and he said so to

[047-112]the curate, but the barber was of opinion that they ought to push on a

[047-113]little farther, as at the other side of a hill which appeared close by

[047-114]he knew there was a valley that had more grass and much better than the

[047-115]one where they proposed to halt; and his advice was taken and they

[047-116]continued their journey.

[047-117]Just at that moment the curate, looking back, saw coming on behind them

[047-118]six or seven mounted men, well found and equipped, who soon overtook

[047-119]them, for they were travelling, not at the sluggish, deliberate pace of

[047-120]oxen, but like men who rode canons’ mules, and in haste to take their

[047-121]noontide rest as soon as possible at the inn which was in sight not a

[047-122]league off. The quick travellers came up with the slow, and courteous

[047-123]salutations were exchanged; and one of the new comers, who was, in

[047-124]fact, a canon of Toledo and master of the others who accompanied him,

[047-125]observing the regular order of the procession, the cart, the officers,

[047-126]Sancho, Rocinante, the curate and the barber, and above all Don Quixote

[047-127]caged and confined, could not help asking what was the meaning of

[047-128]carrying the man in that fashion; though, from the badges of the

[047-129]officers, he already concluded that he must be some desperate

[047-130]highwayman or other malefactor whose punishment fell within the

[047-131]jurisdiction of the Holy Brotherhood. One of the officers to whom he

[047-132]had put the question, replied, “Let the gentleman himself tell you the

[047-133]meaning of his going this way, señor, for we do not know.”

[047-134]Don Quixote overheard the conversation and said, “Haply, gentlemen, you

[047-135]are versed and learned in matters of errant chivalry? Because if you

[047-136]are I will tell you my misfortunes; if not, there is no good in my

[047-137]giving myself the trouble of relating them;” but here the curate and

[047-138]the barber, seeing that the travellers were engaged in conversation

[047-139]with Don Quixote, came forward, in order to answer in such a way as to

[047-140]save their stratagem from being discovered.

[047-141]The canon, replying to Don Quixote, said, “In truth, brother, I know

[047-142]more about books of chivalry than I do about Villalpando’s elements of

[047-143]logic; so if that be all, you may safely tell me what you please.”

[047-144]“In God’s name, then, señor,” replied Don Quixote; “if that be so, I

[047-145]would have you know that I am held enchanted in this cage by the envy

[047-146]and fraud of wicked enchanters; for virtue is more persecuted by the

[047-147]wicked than loved by the good. I am a knight-errant, and not one of

[047-148]those whose names Fame has never thought of immortalising in her

[047-149]record, but of those who, in defiance and in spite of envy itself, and

[047-150]all the magicians that Persia, or Brahmans that India, or Gymnosophists

[047-151]that Ethiopia ever produced, will place their names in the temple of

[047-152]immortality, to serve as examples and patterns for ages to come,

[047-153]whereby knights-errant may see the footsteps in which they must tread

[047-154]if they would attain the summit and crowning point of honour in arms.”

[047-155]“What Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha says,” observed the curate, “is

[047-156]the truth; for he goes enchanted in this cart, not from any fault or

[047-157]sins of his, but because of the malevolence of those to whom virtue is

[047-158]odious and valour hateful. This, señor, is the Knight of the Rueful

[047-159]Countenance, if you have ever heard him named, whose valiant

[047-160]achievements and mighty deeds shall be written on lasting brass and

[047-161]imperishable marble, notwithstanding all the efforts of envy to obscure

[047-162]them and malice to hide them.”

[047-163]When the canon heard both the prisoner and the man who was at liberty

[047-164]talk in such a strain he was ready to cross himself in his

[047-165]astonishment, and could not make out what had befallen him; and all his

[047-166]attendants were in the same state of amazement.

[047-167]At this point Sancho Panza, who had drawn near to hear the

[047-168]conversation, said, in order to make everything plain, “Well, sirs, you

[047-169]may like or dislike what I am going to say, but the fact of the matter

[047-170]is, my master, Don Quixote, is just as much enchanted as my mother. He

[047-171]is in his full senses, he eats and he drinks, and he has his calls like

[047-172]other men and as he had yesterday, before they caged him. And if that’s

[047-173]the case, what do they mean by wanting me to believe that he is

[047-174]enchanted? For I have heard many a one say that enchanted people

[047-175]neither eat, nor sleep, nor talk; and my master, if you don’t stop him,

[047-176]will talk more than thirty lawyers.” Then turning to the curate he

[047-177]exclaimed, “Ah, señor curate, señor curate! do you think I don’t know

[047-178]you? Do you think I don’t guess and see the drift of these new

[047-179]enchantments? Well then, I can tell you I know you, for all your face

[047-180]is covered, and I can tell you I am up to you, however you may hide

[047-181]your tricks. After all, where envy reigns virtue cannot live, and where

[047-182]there is niggardliness there can be no liberality. Ill betide the

[047-183]devil! if it had not been for your worship my master would be married

[047-184]to the Princess Micomicona this minute, and I should be a count at

[047-185]least; for no less was to be expected, as well from the goodness of my

[047-186]master, him of the Rueful Countenance, as from the greatness of my

[047-187]services. But I see now how true it is what they say in these parts,

[047-188]that the wheel of fortune turns faster than a mill-wheel, and that

[047-189]those who were up yesterday are down to-day. I am sorry for my wife and

[047-190]children, for when they might fairly and reasonably expect to see their

[047-191]father return to them a governor or viceroy of some island or kingdom,

[047-192]they will see him come back a horse-boy. I have said all this, señor

[047-193]curate, only to urge your paternity to lay to your conscience your

[047-194]ill-treatment of my master; and have a care that God does not call you

[047-195]to account in another life for making a prisoner of him in this way,

[047-196]and charge against you all the succours and good deeds that my lord Don

[047-197]Quixote leaves undone while he is shut up.

[047-198]“Trim those lamps there!” exclaimed the barber at this; “so you are of

[047-199]the same fraternity as your master, too, Sancho? By God, I begin to see

[047-200]that you will have to keep him company in the cage, and be enchanted

[047-201]like him for having caught some of his humour and chivalry. It was an

[047-202]evil hour when you let yourself be got with child by his promises, and

[047-203]that island you long so much for found its way into your head.”

[047-204]“I am not with child by anyone,” returned Sancho, “nor am I a man to

[047-205]let myself be got with child, if it was by the King himself. Though I

[047-206]am poor I am an old Christian, and I owe nothing to nobody, and if I

[047-207]long for an island, other people long for worse. Each of us is the son

[047-208]of his own works; and being a man I may come to be pope, not to say

[047-209]governor of an island, especially as my master may win so many that he

[047-210]will not know whom to give them to. Mind how you talk, master barber;

[047-211]for shaving is not everything, and there is some difference between

[047-212]Peter and Peter. I say this because we all know one another, and it

[047-213]will not do to throw false dice with me; and as to the enchantment of

[047-214]my master, God knows the truth; leave it as it is; it only makes it

[047-215]worse to stir it.”

[047-216]The barber did not care to answer Sancho lest by his plain speaking he

[047-217]should disclose what the curate and he himself were trying so hard to

[047-218]conceal; and under the same apprehension the curate had asked the canon

[047-219]to ride on a little in advance, so that he might tell him the mystery

[047-220]of this man in the cage, and other things that would amuse him. The

[047-221]canon agreed, and going on ahead with his servants, listened with

[047-222]attention to the account of the character, life, madness, and ways of

[047-223]Don Quixote, given him by the curate, who described to him briefly the

[047-224]beginning and origin of his craze, and told him the whole story of his

[047-225]adventures up to his being confined in the cage, together with the plan

[047-226]they had of taking him home to try if by any means they could discover

[047-227]a cure for his madness. The canon and his servants were surprised anew

[047-228]when they heard Don Quixote’s strange story, and when it was finished

[047-229]he said, “To tell the truth, señor curate, I for my part consider what

[047-230]they call books of chivalry to be mischievous to the State; and though,

[047-231]led by idle and false taste, I have read the beginnings of almost all

[047-232]that have been printed, I never could manage to read any one of them

[047-233]from beginning to end; for it seems to me they are all more or less the

[047-234]same thing; and one has nothing more in it than another; this no more

[047-235]than that. And in my opinion this sort of writing and composition is of

[047-236]the same species as the fables they call the Milesian, nonsensical

[047-237]tales that aim solely at giving amusement and not instruction, exactly

[047-238]the opposite of the apologue fables which amuse and instruct at the

[047-239]same time. And though it may be the chief object of such books to

[047-240]amuse, I do not know how they can succeed, when they are so full of

[047-241]such monstrous nonsense. For the enjoyment the mind feels must come

[047-242]from the beauty and harmony which it perceives or contemplates in the

[047-243]things that the eye or the imagination brings before it; and nothing

[047-244]that has any ugliness or disproportion about it can give any pleasure.

[047-245]What beauty, then, or what proportion of the parts to the whole, or of

[047-246]the whole to the parts, can there be in a book or fable where a lad of

[047-247]sixteen cuts down a giant as tall as a tower and makes two halves of

[047-248]him as if he was an almond cake? And when they want to give us a

[047-249]picture of a battle, after having told us that there are a million of

[047-250]combatants on the side of the enemy, let the hero of the book be

[047-251]opposed to them, and we have perforce to believe, whether we like it or

[047-252]not, that the said knight wins the victory by the single might of his

[047-253]strong arm. And then, what shall we say of the facility with which a

[047-254]born queen or empress will give herself over into the arms of some

[047-255]unknown wandering knight? What mind, that is not wholly barbarous and

[047-256]uncultured, can find pleasure in reading of how a great tower full of

[047-257]knights sails away across the sea like a ship with a fair wind, and

[047-258]will be to-night in Lombardy and to-morrow morning in the land of

[047-259]Prester John of the Indies, or some other that Ptolemy never described

[047-260]nor Marco Polo saw? And if, in answer to this, I am told that the

[047-261]authors of books of the kind write them as fiction, and therefore are

[047-262]not bound to regard niceties of truth, I would reply that fiction is

[047-263]all the better the more it looks like truth, and gives the more

[047-264]pleasure the more probability and possibility there is about it. Plots

[047-265]in fiction should be wedded to the understanding of the reader, and be

[047-266]constructed in such a way that, reconciling impossibilities, smoothing

[047-267]over difficulties, keeping the mind on the alert, they may surprise,

[047-268]interest, divert, and entertain, so that wonder and delight joined may

[047-269]keep pace one with the other; all which he will fail to effect who

[047-270]shuns verisimilitude and truth to nature, wherein lies the perfection

[047-271]of writing. I have never yet seen any book of chivalry that puts

[047-272]together a connected plot complete in all its numbers, so that the

[047-273]middle agrees with the beginning, and the end with the beginning and

[047-274]middle; on the contrary, they construct them with such a multitude of

[047-275]members that it seems as though they meant to produce a chimera or

[047-276]monster rather than a well-proportioned figure. And besides all this

[047-277]they are harsh in their style, incredible in their achievements,

[047-278]licentious in their amours, uncouth in their courtly speeches, prolix

[047-279]in their battles, silly in their arguments, absurd in their travels,

[047-280]and, in short, wanting in everything like intelligent art; for which

[047-281]reason they deserve to be banished from the Christian commonwealth as a

[047-282]worthless breed.”

[047-283]The curate listened to him attentively and felt that he was a man of

[047-284]sound understanding, and that there was good reason in what he said; so

[047-285]he told him that, being of the same opinion himself, and bearing a

[047-286]grudge to books of chivalry, he had burned all Don Quixote’s, which

[047-287]were many; and gave him an account of the scrutiny he had made of them,

[047-288]and of those he had condemned to the flames and those he had spared,

[047-289]with which the canon was not a little amused, adding that though he had

[047-290]said so much in condemnation of these books, still he found one good

[047-291]thing in them, and that was the opportunity they afforded to a gifted

[047-292]intellect for displaying itself; for they presented a wide and spacious

[047-293]field over which the pen might range freely, describing shipwrecks,

[047-294]tempests, combats, battles, portraying a valiant captain with all the

[047-295]qualifications requisite to make one, showing him sagacious in

[047-296]foreseeing the wiles of the enemy, eloquent in speech to encourage or

[047-297]restrain his soldiers, ripe in counsel, rapid in resolve, as bold in

[047-298]biding his time as in pressing the attack; now picturing some sad

[047-299]tragic incident, now some joyful and unexpected event; here a beauteous

[047-300]lady, virtuous, wise, and modest; there a Christian knight, brave and

[047-301]gentle; here a lawless, barbarous braggart; there a courteous prince,

[047-302]gallant and gracious; setting forth the devotion and loyalty of

[047-303]vassals, the greatness and generosity of nobles. “Or again,” said he,

[047-304]“the author may show himself to be an astronomer, or a skilled

[047-305]cosmographer, or musician, or one versed in affairs of state, and

[047-306]sometimes he will have a chance of coming forward as a magician if he

[047-307]likes. He can set forth the craftiness of Ulysses, the piety of Æneas,

[047-308]the valour of Achilles, the misfortunes of Hector, the treachery of

[047-309]Sinon, the friendship of Euryalus, the generosity of Alexander, the

[047-310]boldness of Cæsar, the clemency and truth of Trajan, the fidelity of

[047-311]Zopyrus, the wisdom of Cato, and in short all the faculties that serve

[047-312]to make an illustrious man perfect, now uniting them in one individual,

[047-313]again distributing them among many; and if this be done with charm of

[047-314]style and ingenious invention, aiming at the truth as much as possible,

[047-315]he will assuredly weave a web of bright and varied threads that, when

[047-316]finished, will display such perfection and beauty that it will attain

[047-317]the worthiest object any writing can seek, which, as I said before, is

[047-318]to give instruction and pleasure combined; for the unrestricted range

[047-319]of these books enables the author to show his powers, epic, lyric,

[047-320]tragic, or comic, and all the moods the sweet and winning arts of poesy

[047-321]and oratory are capable of; for the epic may be written in prose just

[047-322]as well as in verse.”
