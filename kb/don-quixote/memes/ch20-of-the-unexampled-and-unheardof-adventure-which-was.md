[020-001]OF THE UNEXAMPLED AND UNHEARD-OF ADVENTURE WHICH WAS ACHIEVED BY THE

VALIANT DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA WITH LESS PERIL THAN ANY EVER ACHIEVED

BY ANY FAMOUS KNIGHT IN THE WORLD

[020-002]“It cannot be, señor, but that this grass is a proof that there must be

[020-003]hard by some spring or brook to give it moisture, so it would be well

[020-004]to move a little farther on, that we may find some place where we may

[020-005]quench this terrible thirst that plagues us, which beyond a doubt is

[020-006]more distressing than hunger.”

[020-007]The advice seemed good to Don Quixote, and, he leading Rocinante by the

[020-008]bridle and Sancho the ass by the halter, after he had packed away upon

[020-009]him the remains of the supper, they advanced the meadow feeling their

[020-010]way, for the darkness of the night made it impossible to see anything;

[020-011]but they had not gone two hundred paces when a loud noise of water, as

[020-012]if falling from great rocks, struck their ears. The sound cheered them

[020-013]greatly; but halting to make out by listening from what quarter it came

[020-014]they heard unseasonably another noise which spoiled the satisfaction

[020-015]the sound of the water gave them, especially for Sancho, who was by

[020-016]nature timid and faint-hearted. They heard, I say, strokes falling with

[020-017]a measured beat, and a certain rattling of iron and chains that,

[020-018]together with the furious din of the water, would have struck terror

[020-019]into any heart but Don Quixote’s. The night was, as has been said,

[020-020]dark, and they had happened to reach a spot in among some tall trees,

[020-021]whose leaves stirred by a gentle breeze made a low ominous sound; so

[020-022]that, what with the solitude, the place, the darkness, the noise of the

[020-023]water, and the rustling of the leaves, everything inspired awe and

[020-024]dread; more especially as they perceived that the strokes did not

[020-025]cease, nor the wind lull, nor morning approach; to all which might be

[020-026]added their ignorance as to where they were.

[020-027]But Don Quixote, supported by his intrepid heart, leaped on Rocinante,

[020-028]and bracing his buckler on his arm, brought his pike to the slope, and

[020-029]said, “Friend Sancho, know that I by Heaven’s will have been born in

[020-030]this our iron age to revive in it the age of gold, or the golden as it

[020-031]is called; I am he for whom perils, mighty achievements, and valiant

[020-032]deeds are reserved; I am, I say again, he who is to revive the Knights

[020-033]of the Round Table, the Twelve of France and the Nine Worthies; and he

[020-034]who is to consign to oblivion the Platirs, the Tablantes, the Olivantes

[020-035]and Tirantes, the Phœbuses and Belianises, with the whole herd of

[020-036]famous knights-errant of days gone by, performing in these in which I

[020-037]live such exploits, marvels, and feats of arms as shall obscure their

[020-038]brightest deeds. Thou dost mark well, faithful and trusty squire, the

[020-039]gloom of this night, its strange silence, the dull confused murmur of

[020-040]those trees, the awful sound of that water in quest of which we came,

[020-041]that seems as though it were precipitating and dashing itself down from

[020-042]the lofty mountains of the Moon, and that incessant hammering that

[020-043]wounds and pains our ears; which things all together and each of itself

[020-044]are enough to instil fear, dread, and dismay into the breast of Mars

[020-045]himself, much more into one not used to hazards and adventures of the

[020-046]kind. Well, then, all this that I put before thee is but an incentive

[020-047]and stimulant to my spirit, making my heart burst in my bosom through

[020-048]eagerness to engage in this adventure, arduous as it promises to be;

[020-049]therefore tighten Rocinante’s girths a little, and God be with thee;

[020-050]wait for me here three days and no more, and if in that time I come not

[020-051]back, thou canst return to our village, and thence, to do me a favour

[020-052]and a service, thou wilt go to El Toboso, where thou shalt say to my

[020-053]incomparable lady Dulcinea that her captive knight hath died in

[020-054]attempting things that might make him worthy of being called hers.”

[020-055]When Sancho heard his master’s words he began to weep in the most

[020-056]pathetic way, saying:

[020-057]“Señor, I know not why your worship wants to attempt this so dreadful

[020-058]adventure; it is night now, no one sees us here, we can easily turn

[020-059]about and take ourselves out of danger, even if we don’t drink for

[020-060]three days to come; and as there is no one to see us, all the less will

[020-061]there be anyone to set us down as cowards; besides, I have many a time

[020-062]heard the curate of our village, whom your worship knows well, preach

[020-063]that he who seeks danger perishes in it; so it is not right to tempt

[020-064]God by trying so tremendous a feat from which there can be no escape

[020-065]save by a miracle, and Heaven has performed enough of them for your

[020-066]worship in delivering you from being blanketed as I was, and bringing

[020-067]you out victorious and safe and sound from among all those enemies that

[020-068]were with the dead man; and if all this does not move or soften that

[020-069]hard heart, let this thought and reflection move it, that you will have

[020-070]hardly quitted this spot when from pure fear I shall yield my soul up

[020-071]to anyone that will take it. I left home and wife and children to come

[020-072]and serve your worship, trusting to do better and not worse; but as

[020-073]covetousness bursts the bag, it has rent my hopes asunder, for just as

[020-074]I had them highest about getting that wretched unlucky island your

[020-075]worship has so often promised me, I see that instead and in lieu of it

[020-076]you mean to desert me now in a place so far from human reach: for God’s

[020-077]sake, master mine, deal not so unjustly by me, and if your worship will

[020-078]not entirely give up attempting this feat, at least put it off till

[020-079]morning, for by what the lore I learned when I was a shepherd tells me

[020-080]it cannot want three hours of dawn now, because the mouth of the Horn

[020-081]is overhead and makes midnight in the line of the left arm.”

[020-082]“How canst thou see, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “where it makes that

[020-083]line, or where this mouth or this occiput is that thou talkest of, when

[020-084]the night is so dark that there is not a star to be seen in the whole

[020-085]heaven?”

[020-086]“That’s true,” said Sancho, “but fear has sharp eyes, and sees things

[020-087]underground, much more above in heavens; besides, there is good reason

[020-088]to show that it now wants but little of day.”

[020-089]“Let it want what it may,” replied Don Quixote, “it shall not be said

[020-090]of me now or at any time that tears or entreaties turned me aside from

[020-091]doing what was in accordance with knightly usage; and so I beg of thee,

[020-092]Sancho, to hold thy peace, for God, who has put it into my heart to

[020-093]undertake now this so unexampled and terrible adventure, will take care

[020-094]to watch over my safety and console thy sorrow; what thou hast to do is

[020-095]to tighten Rocinante’s girths well, and wait here, for I shall come

[020-096]back shortly, alive or dead.”

[020-097]Sancho perceiving it his master’s final resolve, and how little his

[020-098]tears, counsels, and entreaties prevailed with him, determined to have

[020-100]daylight; and so, while tightening the girths of the horse, he quietly

[020-101]and without being felt, with his ass’ halter tied both Rocinante’s

[020-102]legs, so that when Don Quixote strove to go he was unable as the horse

[020-103]could only move by jumps. Seeing the success of his trick, Sancho Panza

[020-104]said:

[020-105]“See there, señor! Heaven, moved by my tears and prayers, has so

[020-106]ordered it that Rocinante cannot stir; and if you will be obstinate,

[020-107]and spur and strike him, you will only provoke fortune, and kick, as

[020-108]they say, against the pricks.”

[020-109]Don Quixote at this grew desperate, but the more he drove his heels

[020-110]into the horse, the less he stirred him; and not having any suspicion

[020-111]of the tying, he was fain to resign himself and wait till daybreak or

[020-112]until Rocinante could move, firmly persuaded that all this came of

[020-113]something other than Sancho’s ingenuity. So he said to him, “As it is

[020-114]so, Sancho, and as Rocinante cannot move, I am content to wait till

[020-115]dawn smiles upon us, even though I weep while it delays its coming.”

[020-116]“There is no need to weep,” answered Sancho, “for I will amuse your

[020-117]worship by telling stories from this till daylight, unless indeed you

[020-118]like to dismount and lie down to sleep a little on the green grass

[020-119]after the fashion of knights-errant, so as to be fresher when day comes

[020-120]and the moment arrives for attempting this extraordinary adventure you

[020-121]are looking forward to.”

[020-122]“What art thou talking about dismounting or sleeping for?” said Don

[020-123]Quixote. “Am I, thinkest thou, one of those knights that take their

[020-124]rest in the presence of danger? Sleep thou who art born to sleep, or do

[020-125]as thou wilt, for I will act as I think most consistent with my

[020-126]character.”

[020-127]“Be not angry, master mine,” replied Sancho, “I did not mean to say

[020-129]saddle and the other on the cantle so that he held his master’s left

[020-131]so much afraid was he of the strokes which still resounded with a

[020-132]regular beat. Don Quixote bade him tell some story to amuse him as he

[020-144]quarter, and that we should go back by some other road, since nobody

[020-145]forces us to follow this in which so many terrors affright us.”

[020-146]“Go on with thy story, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and leave the choice

[020-147]of our road to my care.”

[020-148]“I say then,” continued Sancho, “that in a village of Estremadura there

[020-149]was a goat-shepherd—that is to say, one who tended goats—which shepherd

[020-151]was in love with a shepherdess called Torralva, which shepherdess

[020-152]called Torralva was the daughter of a rich grazier, and this rich

[020-153]grazier—”

[020-154]“If that is the way thou tellest thy tale, Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[020-155]“repeating twice all thou hast to say, thou wilt not have done these

[020-156]two days; go straight on with it, and tell it like a reasonable man, or

[020-157]else say nothing.”

[020-158]“Tales are always told in my country in the very way I am telling

[020-159]this,” answered Sancho, “and I cannot tell it in any other, nor is it

[020-160]right of your worship to ask me to make new customs.”

[020-161]“Tell it as thou wilt,” replied Don Quixote; “and as fate will have it

[020-162]that I cannot help listening to thee, go on.”

[020-163]“And so, lord of my soul,” continued Sancho, as I have said, this

[020-165]buxom lass with something of the look of a man about her, for she had

[020-166]little moustaches; I fancy I see her now.”

[020-167]“Then you knew her?” said Don Quixote.

[020-168]“I did not know her,” said Sancho, “but he who told me the story said

[020-169]it was so true and certain that when I told it to another I might

[020-170]safely declare and swear I had seen it all myself. And so in course of

[020-171]time, the devil, who never sleeps and puts everything in confusion,

[020-172]contrived that the love the shepherd bore the shepherdess turned into

[020-173]hatred and ill-will, and the reason, according to evil tongues, was

[020-174]some little jealousy she caused him that crossed the line and

[020-175]trespassed on forbidden ground; and so much did the shepherd hate her

[020-176]from that time forward that, in order to escape from her, he determined

[020-177]to quit the country and go where he should never set eyes on her again.

[020-178]Torralva, when she found herself spurned by Lope, was immediately

[020-179]smitten with love for him, though she had never loved him before.”

[020-180]“That is the natural way of women,” said Don Quixote, “to scorn the one

[020-181]that loves them, and love the one that hates them: go on, Sancho.”

[020-182]“It came to pass,” said Sancho, “that the shepherd carried out his

[020-183]intention, and driving his goats before him took his way across the

[020-184]plains of Estremadura to pass over into the Kingdom of Portugal.

[020-185]Torralva, who knew of it, went after him, and on foot and barefoot

[020-187]scrip round her neck, in which she carried, it is said, a bit of

[020-189]paint for her face; but let her carry what she did, I am not going to

[020-190]trouble myself to prove it; all I say is, that the shepherd, they say,

[020-192]time swollen and almost overflowing its banks, and at the spot he came

[020-193]to there was neither ferry nor boat nor anyone to carry him or his

[020-194]flock to the other side, at which he was much vexed, for he perceived

[020-195]that Torralva was approaching and would give him great annoyance with

[020-196]her tears and entreaties; however, he went looking about so closely

[020-197]that he discovered a fisherman who had alongside of him a boat so small

[020-198]that it could only hold one person and one goat; but for all that he

[020-199]spoke to him and agreed with him to carry himself and his three hundred

[020-200]goats across. The fisherman got into the boat and carried one goat

[020-201]over; he came back and carried another over; he came back again, and

[020-202]again brought over another—let your worship keep count of the goats the

[020-206]miry and slippery, and the fisherman lost a great deal of time in going

[020-207]and coming; still he returned for another goat, and another, and

[020-208]another.”

[020-209]“Take it for granted he brought them all across,” said Don Quixote,

[020-210]“and don’t keep going and coming in this way, or thou wilt not make an

[020-211]end of bringing them over this twelvemonth.”

[020-212]“How many have gone across so far?” said Sancho.

[020-213]“How the devil do I know?” replied Don Quixote.

[020-214]“There it is,” said Sancho, “what I told you, that you must keep a good

[020-217]“How can that be?” said Don Quixote; “is it so essential to the story

[020-220]“No, señor, not a bit,” replied Sancho; “for when I asked your worship

[020-221]to tell me how many goats had crossed, and you answered you did not

[020-222]know, at that very instant all I had to say passed away out of my

[020-223]memory, and, faith, there was much virtue in it, and entertainment.”

[020-224]“So, then,” said Don Quixote, “the story has come to an end?”

[020-225]“As much as my mother has,” said Sancho.

[020-226]“In truth,” said Don Quixote, “thou hast told one of the rarest
