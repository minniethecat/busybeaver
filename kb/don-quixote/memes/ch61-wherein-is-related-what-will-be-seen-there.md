WHEREIN IS RELATED WHAT WILL BE SEEN THERE

p09a.jpg (79K)


[061-001]’Twas at the very midnight hour—more or less—when Don Quixote and

[061-002]Sancho quitted the wood and entered El Toboso. The town was in deep

[061-003]silence, for all the inhabitants were asleep, and stretched on the

[061-004]broad of their backs, as the saying is. The night was darkish, though

[061-005]Sancho would have been glad had it been quite dark, so as to find in

[061-006]the darkness an excuse for his blundering. All over the place nothing

[061-007]was to be heard except the barking of dogs, which deafened the ears of

[061-008]Don Quixote and troubled the heart of Sancho. Now and then an ass

[061-009]brayed, pigs grunted, cats mewed, and the various noises they made

[061-010]seemed louder in the silence of the night; all which the enamoured

[061-011]knight took to be of evil omen; nevertheless he said to Sancho,

[061-012]“Sancho, my son, lead on to the palace of Dulcinea, it may be that we

[061-013]shall find her awake.”

[061-014]“Body of the sun! what palace am I to lead to,” said Sancho, “when what

[061-015]I saw her highness in was only a very little house?”

[061-016]“Most likely she had then withdrawn into some small apartment of her

[061-017]palace,” said Don Quixote, “to amuse herself with damsels, as great

[061-018]ladies and princesses are accustomed to do.”

[061-019]“Señor,” said Sancho, “if your worship will have it in spite of me that

[061-020]the house of my lady Dulcinea is a palace, is this an hour, think you,

[061-021]to find the door open; and will it be right for us to go knocking till

[061-022]they hear us and open the door; making a disturbance and confusion all

[061-023]through the household? Are we going, do you fancy, to the house of our

[061-024]wenches, like gallants who come and knock and go in at any hour,

[061-025]however late it may be?”

[061-026]“Let us first of all find out the palace for certain,” replied Don

[061-027]Quixote, “and then I will tell thee, Sancho, what we had best do; but

[061-028]look, Sancho, for either I see badly, or that dark mass that one sees

[061-029]from here should be Dulcinea’s palace.”

[061-030]“Then let your worship lead the way,” said Sancho, “perhaps it may be

[061-031]so; though I see it with my eyes and touch it with my hands, I’ll

[061-032]believe it as much as I believe it is daylight now.”

[061-033]Don Quixote took the lead, and having gone a matter of two hundred

[061-034]paces he came upon the mass that produced the shade, and found it was a

[061-035]great tower, and then he perceived that the building in question was no

[061-036]palace, but the chief church of the town, and said he, “It’s the church

[061-037]we have lit upon, Sancho.”

[061-038]“So I see,” said Sancho, “and God grant we may not light upon our

[061-039]graves; it is no good sign to find oneself wandering in a graveyard at

[061-040]this time of night; and that, after my telling your worship, if I don’t

[061-041]mistake, that the house of this lady will be in an alley without an

[061-042]outlet.”

[061-043]“The curse of God on thee for a blockhead!” said Don Quixote; “where

[061-044]hast thou ever heard of castles and royal palaces being built in alleys

[061-045]without an outlet?”

[061-046]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “every country has a way of its own; perhaps

[061-047]here in El Toboso it is the way to build palaces and grand buildings in

[061-048]alleys; so I entreat your worship to let me search about among these

[061-049]streets or alleys before me, and perhaps, in some corner or other, I

[061-050]may stumble on this palace—and I wish I saw the dogs eating it for

[061-051]leading us such a dance.”

[061-052]“Speak respectfully of what belongs to my lady, Sancho,” said Don

[061-053]Quixote; “let us keep the feast in peace, and not throw the rope after

[061-054]the bucket.”

[061-055]“I’ll hold my tongue,” said Sancho, “but how am I to take it patiently

[061-056]when your worship wants me, with only once seeing the house of our

[061-057]mistress, to know always, and find it in the middle of the night, when

[061-058]your worship can’t find it, who must have seen it thousands of times?”

[061-059]“Thou wilt drive me to desperation, Sancho,” said Don Quixote. “Look

[061-060]here, heretic, have I not told thee a thousand times that I have never

[061-061]once in my life seen the peerless Dulcinea or crossed the threshold of

[061-062]her palace, and that I am enamoured solely by hearsay and by the great

[061-063]reputation she bears for beauty and discretion?”

[061-064]“I hear it now,” returned Sancho; “and I may tell you that if you have

[061-065]not seen her, no more have I.”

[061-066]“That cannot be,” said Don Quixote, “for, at any rate, thou saidst, on

[061-067]bringing back the answer to the letter I sent by thee, that thou sawest

[061-068]her sifting wheat.”

[061-069]“Don’t mind that, señor,” said Sancho; “I must tell you that my seeing

[061-070]her and the answer I brought you back were by hearsay too, for I can no

[061-071]more tell who the lady Dulcinea is than I can hit the sky.”

[061-072]“Sancho, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “there are times for jests and

[061-073]times when jests are out of place; if I tell thee that I have neither

[061-074]seen nor spoken to the lady of my heart, it is no reason why thou

[061-075]shouldst say thou hast not spoken to her or seen her, when the contrary

[061-076]is the case, as thou well knowest.”

[061-077]While the two were engaged in this conversation, they perceived someone

[061-078]with a pair of mules approaching the spot where they stood, and from

[061-079]the noise the plough made, as it dragged along the ground, they guessed

[061-080]him to be some labourer who had got up before daybreak to go to his

[061-081]work, and so it proved to be. He came along singing the ballad that

[061-082]says-

[061-083]Ill did ye fare, ye men of France,

[061-084]In Roncesvalles chase—

[061-085]“May I die, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, when he heard him, “if any good

[061-086]will come to us to-night! Dost thou not hear what that clown is

[061-087]singing?”

[061-088]“I do,” said Sancho, “but what has Roncesvalles chase to do with what

[061-089]we have in hand? He might just as well be singing the ballad of

[061-090]Calainos, for any good or ill that can come to us in our business.”

[061-091]By this time the labourer had come up, and Don Quixote asked him, “Can

[061-092]you tell me, worthy friend, and God speed you, whereabouts here is the

[061-093]palace of the peerless princess Doña Dulcinea del Toboso?”

[061-094]“Señor,” replied the lad, “I am a stranger, and I have been only a few

[061-095]days in the town, doing farm work for a rich farmer. In that house

[061-096]opposite there live the curate of the village and the sacristan, and

[061-097]both or either of them will be able to give your worship some account

[061-098]of this lady princess, for they have a list of all the people of El

[061-099]Toboso; though it is my belief there is not a princess living in the

[061-100]whole of it; many ladies there are, of quality, and in her own house

[061-101]each of them may be a princess.”

[061-102]“Well, then, she I am inquiring for will be one of these, my friend,”

[061-103]said Don Quixote.

[061-104]“May be so,” replied the lad; “God be with you, for here comes the

[061-105]daylight;” and without waiting for any more of his questions, he

[061-106]whipped on his mules.

[061-107]Sancho, seeing his master downcast and somewhat dissatisfied, said to

[061-108]him, “Señor, daylight will be here before long, and it will not do for

[061-109]us to let the sun find us in the street; it will be better for us to

[061-110]quit the city, and for your worship to hide in some forest in the

[061-111]neighbourhood, and I will come back in the daytime, and I won’t leave a

[061-112]nook or corner of the whole village that I won’t search for the house,

[061-113]castle, or palace, of my lady, and it will be hard luck for me if I

[061-114]don’t find it; and as soon as I have found it I will speak to her

[061-115]grace, and tell her where and how your worship is waiting for her to

[061-116]arrange some plan for you to see her without any damage to her honour

[061-117]and reputation.”

[061-118]“Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “thou hast delivered a thousand sentences

[061-119]condensed in the compass of a few words; I thank thee for the advice

[061-120]thou hast given me, and take it most gladly. Come, my son, let us go

[061-121]look for some place where I may hide, while thou dost return, as thou

[061-122]sayest, to seek, and speak with my lady, from whose discretion and

[061-123]courtesy I look for favours more than miraculous.”

[061-124]Sancho was in a fever to get his master out of the town, lest he should

[061-125]discover the falsehood of the reply he had brought to him in the Sierra

[061-126]Morena on behalf of Dulcinea; so he hastened their departure, which

[061-127]they took at once, and two miles out of the village they found a forest

[061-128]or thicket wherein Don Quixote ensconced himself, while Sancho returned

[061-129]to the city to speak to Dulcinea, in which embassy things befell him

[061-130]which demand fresh attention and a new chapter.
