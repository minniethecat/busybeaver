[126-001]OF HOW DON QUIXOTE FELL SICK, AND OF THE WILL HE MADE, AND HOW HE DIED

p74a.jpg (96K)


[126-002]As nothing that is man’s can last for ever, but all tends ever

[126-003]downwards from its beginning to its end, and above all man’s life, and

[126-004]as Don Quixote’s enjoyed no special dispensation from heaven to stay

[126-005]its course, its end and close came when he least looked for it.

[126-006]For—whether it was of the dejection the thought of his defeat produced,

[126-007]or of heaven’s will that so ordered it—a fever settled upon him and

[126-008]kept him in his bed for six days, during which he was often visited by

[126-009]his friends the curate, the bachelor, and the barber, while his good

[126-010]squire Sancho Panza never quitted his bedside. They, persuaded that it

[126-011]was grief at finding himself vanquished, and the object of his heart,

[126-012]the liberation and disenchantment of Dulcinea, unattained, that kept

[126-013]him in this state, strove by all the means in their power to cheer him

[126-014]up; the bachelor bidding him take heart and get up to begin his

[126-015]pastoral life, for which he himself, he said, had already composed an

[126-016]eclogue that would take the shine out of all Sannazaro had ever

[126-017]written, and had bought with his own money two famous dogs to guard the

[126-018]flock, one called Barcino and the other Butron, which a herdsman of

[126-019]Quintanar had sold him.

[126-020]But for all this Don Quixote could not shake off his sadness. His

[126-021]friends called in the doctor, who felt his pulse and was not very well

[126-022]satisfied with it, and said that in any case it would be well for him

[126-023]to attend to the health of his soul, as that of his body was in a bad

[126-024]way. Don Quixote heard this calmly; but not so his housekeeper, his

[126-025]niece, and his squire, who fell weeping bitterly, as if they had him

[126-026]lying dead before them. The doctor’s opinion was that melancholy and

[126-027]depression were bringing him to his end. Don Quixote begged them to

[126-028]leave him to himself, as he had a wish to sleep a little. They obeyed,

[126-029]and he slept at one stretch, as the saying is, more than six hours, so

[126-030]that the housekeeper and niece thought he was going to sleep for ever.

[126-031]But at the end of that time he woke up, and in a loud voice exclaimed,

[126-032]“Blessed be Almighty God, who has shown me such goodness. In truth his

[126-033]mercies are boundless, and the sins of men can neither limit them nor

[126-034]keep them back!”

[126-035]The niece listened with attention to her uncle’s words, and they struck

[126-036]her as more coherent than what usually fell from him, at least during

[126-037]his illness, so she asked, “What are you saying, señor? Has anything

[126-038]strange occurred? What mercies or what sins of men are you talking of?”

[126-039]“The mercies, niece,” said Don Quixote, “are those that God has this

[126-040]moment shown me, and with him, as I said, my sins are no impediment to

[126-041]them. My reason is now free and clear, rid of the dark shadows of

[126-042]ignorance that my unhappy constant study of those detestable books of

[126-043]chivalry cast over it. Now I see through their absurdities and

[126-044]deceptions, and it only grieves me that this destruction of my

[126-045]illusions has come so late that it leaves me no time to make some

[126-046]amends by reading other books that might be a light to my soul. Niece,

[126-047]I feel myself at the point of death, and I would fain meet it in such a

[126-048]way as to show that my life has not been so ill that I should leave

[126-049]behind me the name of a madman; for though I have been one, I would not

[126-050]that the fact should be made plainer at my death. Call in to me, my

[126-051]dear, my good friends the curate, the bachelor Samson Carrasco, and

[126-052]Master Nicholas the barber, for I wish to confess and make my will.”

[126-053]But his niece was saved the trouble by the entrance of the three. The

[126-054]instant Don Quixote saw them he exclaimed, “Good news for you, good

[126-055]sirs, that I am no longer Don Quixote of La Mancha, but Alonso Quixano,

[126-056]whose way of life won for him the name of Good. Now am I the enemy of

[126-057]Amadis of Gaul and of the whole countless troop of his descendants;

[126-058]odious to me now are all the profane stories of knight-errantry; now I

[126-059]perceive my folly, and the peril into which reading them brought me;

[126-060]now, by God’s mercy schooled into my right senses, I loathe them.”

[126-061]When the three heard him speak in this way, they had no doubt whatever

[126-062]that some new craze had taken possession of him; and said Samson,

[126-063]“What? Señor Don Quixote! Now that we have intelligence of the lady

[126-064]Dulcinea being disenchanted, are you taking this line; now, just as we

[126-065]are on the point of becoming shepherds, to pass our lives singing, like

[126-066]princes, are you thinking of turning hermit? Hush, for heaven’s sake,

[126-067]be rational and let’s have no more nonsense.”

[126-068]“All that nonsense,” said Don Quixote, “that until now has been a

[126-069]reality to my hurt, my death will, with heaven’s help, turn to my good.

[126-070]I feel, sirs, that I am rapidly drawing near death; a truce to jesting;

[126-071]let me have a confessor to confess me, and a notary to make my will;

[126-072]for in extremities like this, man must not trifle with his soul; and

[126-073]while the curate is confessing me let someone, I beg, go for the

[126-074]notary.”

[126-075]They looked at one another, wondering at Don Quixote’s words; but,

[126-076]though uncertain, they were inclined to believe him, and one of the

[126-077]signs by which they came to the conclusion he was dying was this so

[126-078]sudden and complete return to his senses after having been mad; for to

[126-079]the words already quoted he added much more, so well expressed, so

[126-080]devout, and so rational, as to banish all doubt and convince them that

[126-081]he was sound of mind. The curate turned them all out, and left alone

[126-082]with him confessed him. The bachelor went for the notary and returned

[126-083]shortly afterwards with him and with Sancho, who, having already

[126-084]learned from the bachelor the condition his master was in, and finding

[126-085]the housekeeper and niece weeping, began to blubber and shed tears.

[126-086]The confession over, the curate came out saying, “Alonso Quixano the

[126-087]Good is indeed dying, and is indeed in his right mind; we may now go in

[126-088]to him while he makes his will.”

[126-089]This news gave a tremendous impulse to the brimming eyes of the

[126-090]housekeeper, niece, and Sancho Panza his good squire, making the tears

[126-091]burst from their eyes and a host of sighs from their hearts; for of a

[126-092]truth, as has been said more than once, whether as plain Alonso Quixano

[126-093]the Good, or as Don Quixote of La Mancha, Don Quixote was always of a

[126-094]gentle disposition and kindly in all his ways, and hence he was

[126-095]beloved, not only by those of his own house, but by all who knew him.

[126-096]The notary came in with the rest, and as soon as the preamble of the

[126-097]will had been set out and Don Quixote had commended his soul to God

[126-098]with all the devout formalities that are usual, coming to the bequests,

[126-099]he said, “Item, it is my will that, touching certain moneys in the

[126-100]hands of Sancho Panza (whom in my madness I made my squire), inasmuch

[126-101]as between him and me there have been certain accounts and debits and

[126-102]credits, no claim be made against him, nor any account demanded of him

[126-103]in respect of them; but that if anything remain over and above, after

[126-104]he has paid himself what I owe him, the balance, which will be but

[126-105]little, shall be his, and much good may it do him; and if, as when I

[126-106]was mad I had a share in giving him the government of an island, so,

[126-107]now that I am in my senses, I could give him that of a kingdom, it

[126-108]should be his, for the simplicity of his character and the fidelity of

[126-109]his conduct deserve it.” And then, turning to Sancho, he said, “Forgive

[126-110]me, my friend, that I led thee to seem as mad as myself, making thee

[126-111]fall into the same error I myself fell into, that there were and still

[126-112]are knights-errant in the world.”

[126-113]“Ah!” said Sancho weeping, “don’t die, master, but take my advice and

[126-114]live many years; for the foolishest thing a man can do in this life is

[126-115]to let himself die without rhyme or reason, without anybody killing

[126-116]him, or any hands but melancholy’s making an end of him. Come, don’t be

[126-117]lazy, but get up from your bed and let us take to the fields in

[126-118]shepherd’s trim as we agreed. Perhaps behind some bush we shall find

[126-119]the lady Dulcinea disenchanted, as fine as fine can be. If it be that

[126-120]you are dying of vexation at having been vanquished, lay the blame on

[126-121]me, and say you were overthrown because I had girthed Rocinante badly;

[126-122]besides you must have seen in your books of chivalry that it is a

[126-123]common thing for knights to upset one another, and for him who is

[126-124]conquered to-day to be conqueror to-morrow.”

[126-125]“Very true,” said Samson, “and good Sancho Panza’s view of these cases

[126-126]is quite right.”

[126-127]“Sirs, not so fast,” said Don Quixote, “‘in last year’s nests there are

[126-128]no birds this year.’ I was mad, now I am in my senses; I was Don

[126-129]Quixote of La Mancha, I am now, as I said, Alonso Quixano the Good; and

[126-130]may my repentance and sincerity restore me to the esteem you used to

[126-131]have for me; and now let Master Notary proceed.

[126-132]“Item, I leave all my property absolutely to Antonia Quixana my niece,

[126-133]here present, after all has been deducted from the most available

[126-134]portion of it that may be required to satisfy the bequests I have made.

[126-135]And the first disbursement I desire to be made is the payment of the

[126-136]wages I owe for the time my housekeeper has served me, with twenty

[126-137]ducats, over and above, for a gown. The curate and the bachelor Samson

[126-138]Carrasco, now present, I appoint my executors.

[126-139]“Item, it is my wish that if Antonia Quixana, my niece, desires to

[126-140]marry, she shall marry a man of whom it shall be first of all

[126-141]ascertained by information taken that he does not know what books of

[126-142]chivalry are; and if it should be proved that he does, and if, in spite

[126-143]of this, my niece insists upon marrying him, and does marry him, then

[126-144]that she shall forfeit the whole of what I have left her, which my

[126-145]executors shall devote to works of charity as they please.

[126-146]“Item, I entreat the aforesaid gentlemen my executors, that, if any

[126-147]happy chance should lead them to discover the author who is said to

[126-148]have written a history now going about under the title of ‘Second Part

[126-149]of the Achievements of Don Quixote of La Mancha,’ they beg of him on my

[126-150]behalf as earnestly as they can to forgive me for having been, without

[126-151]intending it, the cause of his writing so many and such monstrous

[126-152]absurdities as he has written in it; for I am leaving the world with a

[126-153]feeling of compunction at having provoked him to write them.”

[126-154]With this he closed his will, and a faintness coming over him he

[126-155]stretched himself out at full length on the bed. All were in a flutter

[126-156]and made haste to relieve him, and during the three days he lived after

[126-157]that on which he made his will he fainted away very often. The house

[126-158]was all in confusion; but still the niece ate and the housekeeper drank

[126-159]and Sancho Panza enjoyed himself; for inheriting property wipes out or

[126-160]softens down in the heir the feeling of grief the dead man might be

[126-161]expected to leave behind him.

[126-162]At last Don Quixote’s end came, after he had received all the

[126-163]sacraments, and had in full and forcible terms expressed his

[126-164]detestation of books of chivalry. The notary was there at the time, and

[126-165]he said that in no book of chivalry had he ever read of any

[126-166]knight-errant dying in his bed so calmly and so like a Christian as Don

[126-167]Quixote, who amid the tears and lamentations of all present yielded up

[126-168]his spirit, that is to say died. On perceiving it the curate begged the

[126-169]notary to bear witness that Alonso Quixano the Good, commonly called

[126-170]Don Quixote of La Mancha, had passed away from this present life, and

[126-171]died naturally; and said he desired this testimony in order to remove

[126-172]the possibility of any other author save Cide Hamete Benengeli bringing

[126-173]him to life again falsely and making interminable stories out of his

[126-174]achievements.

[126-175]Such was the end of the Ingenious Gentleman of La Mancha, whose village

[126-176]Cide Hamete would not indicate precisely, in order to leave all the

[126-177]towns and villages of La Mancha to contend among themselves for the

[126-178]right to adopt him and claim him as a son, as the seven cities of

[126-179]Greece contended for Homer. The lamentations of Sancho and the niece

[126-180]and housekeeper are omitted here, as well as the new epitaphs upon his

[126-181]tomb; Samson Carrasco, however, put the following lines:

[126-182]A doughty gentleman lies here;

[126-183]A stranger all his life to fear;

[126-184]Nor in his death could Death prevail,

[126-185]In that last hour, to make him quail.

[126-186]He for the world but little cared;

[126-187]And at his feats the world was scared;

[126-188]A crazy man his life he passed,

[126-189]But in his senses died at last.

[126-190]And said most sage Cide Hamete to his pen, “Rest here, hung up by this

[126-191]brass wire, upon this shelf, O my pen, whether of skilful make or

[126-192]clumsy cut I know not; here shalt thou remain long ages hence, unless

[126-193]presumptuous or malignant story-tellers take thee down to profane thee.

[126-194]But ere they touch thee warn them, and, as best thou canst, say to

[126-195]them:

[126-196]Hold off! ye weaklings; hold your hands!

[126-197]Adventure it let none,

[126-198]For this emprise, my lord the king,

[126-199]Was meant for me alone.

[126-200]For me alone was Don Quixote born, and I for him; it was his to act,

[126-201]mine to write; we two together make but one, notwithstanding and in

[126-202]spite of that pretended Tordesillesque writer who has ventured or would

[126-203]venture with his great, coarse, ill-trimmed ostrich quill to write the

[126-204]achievements of my valiant knight;—no burden for his shoulders, nor

[126-205]subject for his frozen wit: whom, if perchance thou shouldst come to

[126-206]know him, thou shalt warn to leave at rest where they lie the weary

[126-207]mouldering bones of Don Quixote, and not to attempt to carry him off,

[126-208]in opposition to all the privileges of death, to Old Castile, making

[126-209]him rise from the grave where in reality and truth he lies stretched at

[126-210]full length, powerless to make any third expedition or new sally; for

[126-211]the two that he has already made, so much to the enjoyment and approval

[126-212]of everybody to whom they have become known, in this as well as in

[126-213]foreign countries, are quite sufficient for the purpose of turning into

[126-214]ridicule the whole of those made by the whole set of the

[126-215]knights-errant; and so doing shalt thou discharge thy Christian

[126-216]calling, giving good counsel to one that bears ill-will to thee. And I

[126-217]shall remain satisfied, and proud to have been the first who has ever

[126-218]enjoyed the fruit of his writings as fully as he could desire; for my

[126-219]desire has been no other than to deliver over to the detestation of

[126-220]mankind the false and foolish tales of the books of chivalry, which,

[126-221]thanks to that of my true Don Quixote, are even now tottering, and

[126-222]doubtless doomed to fall for ever. Farewell.”
