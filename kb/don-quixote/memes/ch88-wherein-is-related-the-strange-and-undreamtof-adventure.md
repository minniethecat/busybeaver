[088-001]WHEREIN IS RELATED THE STRANGE AND UNDREAMT-OF ADVENTURE OF THE

[088-002]DISTRESSED DUENNA, ALIAS THE COUNTESS TRIFALDI, TOGETHER WITH A LETTER

[088-003]WHICH SANCHO PANZA WROTE TO HIS WIFE, TERESA PANZA

p36a.jpg (150K)


[088-004]The duke had a majordomo of a very facetious and sportive turn, and he

[088-005]it was that played the part of Merlin, made all the arrangements for

[088-006]the late adventure, composed the verses, and got a page to represent

[088-007]Dulcinea; and now, with the assistance of his master and mistress, he

[088-008]got up another of the drollest and strangest contrivances that can be

[088-009]imagined.

[088-010]The duchess asked Sancho the next day if he had made a beginning with

[088-011]his penance task which he had to perform for the disenchantment of

[088-012]Dulcinea. He said he had, and had given himself five lashes overnight.

[088-013]The duchess asked him what he had given them with.

[088-014]He said with his hand.

[088-015]“That,” said the duchess, “is more like giving oneself slaps than

[088-016]lashes; I am sure the sage Merlin will not be satisfied with such

[088-017]tenderness; worthy Sancho must make a scourge with claws, or a

[088-018]cat-o’-nine tails, that will make itself felt; for it’s with blood that

[088-019]letters enter, and the release of so great a lady as Dulcinea will not

[088-020]be granted so cheaply, or at such a paltry price; and remember, Sancho,

[088-021]that works of charity done in a lukewarm and half-hearted way are

[088-022]without merit and of no avail.”

[088-023]To which Sancho replied, “If your ladyship will give me a proper

[088-024]scourge or cord, I’ll lay on with it, provided it does not hurt too

[088-025]much; for you must know, boor as I am, my flesh is more cotton than

[088-026]hemp, and it won’t do for me to destroy myself for the good of anybody

[088-027]else.”

[088-028]“So be it by all means,” said the duchess; “to-morrow I’ll give you a

[088-029]scourge that will be just the thing for you, and will accommodate

[088-030]itself to the tenderness of your flesh, as if it was its own sister.”

[088-031]Then said Sancho, “Your highness must know, dear lady of my soul, that

[088-032]I have a letter written to my wife, Teresa Panza, giving her an account

[088-033]of all that has happened me since I left her; I have it here in my

[088-034]bosom, and there’s nothing wanting but to put the address to it; I’d be

[088-035]glad if your discretion would read it, for I think it runs in the

[088-036]governor style; I mean the way governors ought to write.”

[088-037]“And who dictated it?” asked the duchess.

[088-038]“Who should have dictated but myself, sinner as I am?” said Sancho.

[088-039]“And did you write it yourself?” said the duchess.

[088-040]“That I didn’t,” said Sancho; “for I can neither read nor write, though

[088-041]I can sign my name.”

[088-042]“Let us see it,” said the duchess, “for never fear but you display in

[088-043]it the quality and quantity of your wit.”

[088-044]Sancho drew out an open letter from his bosom, and the duchess, taking

[088-045]it, found it ran in this fashion:

[088-046]SANCHO PANZA’S LETTER TO HIS WIFE, TERESA PANZA

[088-047]If I was well whipped I went mounted like a gentleman; if I have got a

[088-048]good government it is at the cost of a good whipping. Thou wilt not

[088-049]understand this just now, my Teresa; by-and-by thou wilt know what it

[088-050]means. I may tell thee, Teresa, I mean thee to go in a coach, for that

[088-051]is a matter of importance, because every other way of going is going on

[088-052]all-fours. Thou art a governor’s wife; take care that nobody speaks

[088-053]evil of thee behind thy back. I send thee here a green hunting suit

[088-054]that my lady the duchess gave me; alter it so as to make a petticoat

[088-055]and bodice for our daughter. Don Quixote, my master, if I am to believe

[088-056]what I hear in these parts, is a madman of some sense, and a droll

[088-057]blockhead, and I am in no way behind him. We have been in the cave of

[088-058]Montesinos, and the sage Merlin has laid hold of me for the

[088-059]disenchantment of Dulcinea del Toboso, her that is called Aldonza

[088-060]Lorenzo over there. With three thousand three hundred lashes, less

[088-061]five, that I’m to give myself, she will be left as entirely

[088-062]disenchanted as the mother that bore her. Say nothing of this to

[088-063]anyone; for, make thy affairs public, and some will say they are white

[088-064]and others will say they are black. I shall leave this in a few days

[088-065]for my government, to which I am going with a mighty great desire to

[088-066]make money, for they tell me all new governors set out with the same

[088-067]desire; I will feel the pulse of it and will let thee know if thou art

[088-068]to come and live with me or not. Dapple is well and sends many

[088-069]remembrances to thee; I am not going to leave him behind though they

[088-070]took me away to be Grand Turk. My lady the duchess kisses thy hands a

[088-071]thousand times; do thou make a return with two thousand, for as my

[088-072]master says, nothing costs less or is cheaper than civility. God has

[088-073]not been pleased to provide another valise for me with another hundred

[088-074]crowns, like the one the other day; but never mind, my Teresa, the

[088-075]bell-ringer is in safe quarters, and all will come out in the scouring

[088-076]of the government; only it troubles me greatly what they tell me—that

[088-077]once I have tasted it I will eat my hands off after it; and if that is

[088-078]so it will not come very cheap to me; though to be sure the maimed have

[088-079]a benefice of their own in the alms they beg for; so that one way or

[088-080]another thou wilt be rich and in luck. God give it to thee as he can,

[088-081]and keep me to serve thee. From this castle, the 20th of July, 1614.

[088-082]Thy husband, the governor,

SANCHO PANZA

[088-083]When she had done reading the letter the duchess said to Sancho, “On

[088-084]two points the worthy governor goes rather astray; one is in saying or

[088-085]hinting that this government has been bestowed upon him for the lashes

[088-086]that he is to give himself, when he knows (and he cannot deny it) that

[088-087]when my lord the duke promised it to him nobody ever dreamt of such a

[088-088]thing as lashes; the other is that he shows himself here to be very

[088-089]covetous; and I would not have him a money-seeker, for ‘covetousness

[088-090]bursts the bag,’ and the covetous governor does ungoverned justice.”

[088-091]“I don’t mean it that way, señora,” said Sancho; “and if you think the

[088-092]letter doesn’t run as it ought to do, it’s only to tear it up and make

[088-093]another; and maybe it will be a worse one if it is left to my

[088-094]gumption.”

[088-095]“No, no,” said the duchess, “this one will do, and I wish the duke to

[088-096]see it.”

[088-097]With this they betook themselves to a garden where they were to dine,

[088-098]and the duchess showed Sancho’s letter to the duke, who was highly

[088-099]delighted with it. They dined, and after the cloth had been removed and

[088-100]they had amused themselves for a while with Sancho’s rich conversation,

[088-101]the melancholy sound of a fife and harsh discordant drum made itself

[088-102]heard. All seemed somewhat put out by this dull, confused, martial

[088-103]harmony, especially Don Quixote, who could not keep his seat from pure

[088-104]disquietude; as to Sancho, it is needless to say that fear drove him to

[088-105]his usual refuge, the side or the skirts of the duchess; and indeed and

[088-106]in truth the sound they heard was a most doleful and melancholy one.

[088-107]While they were still in uncertainty they saw advancing towards them

[088-108]through the garden two men clad in mourning robes so long and flowing

[088-109]that they trailed upon the ground. As they marched they beat two great

[088-110]drums which were likewise draped in black, and beside them came the

[088-111]fife player, black and sombre like the others. Following these came a

[088-112]personage of gigantic stature enveloped rather than clad in a gown of

[088-113]the deepest black, the skirt of which was of prodigious dimensions.

[088-114]Over the gown, girdling or crossing his figure, he had a broad baldric

[088-115]which was also black, and from which hung a huge scimitar with a black

[088-116]scabbard and furniture. He had his face covered with a transparent

[088-117]black veil, through which might be descried a very long beard as white

[088-118]as snow. He came on keeping step to the sound of the drums with great

[088-119]gravity and dignity; and, in short, his stature, his gait, the

[088-120]sombreness of his appearance and his following might well have struck

[088-121]with astonishment, as they did, all who beheld him without knowing who

[088-122]he was. With this measured pace and in this guise he advanced to kneel

[088-123]before the duke, who, with the others, awaited him standing. The duke,

[088-124]however, would not on any account allow him to speak until he had

[088-125]risen. The prodigious scarecrow obeyed, and standing up, removed the

[088-126]veil from his face and disclosed the most enormous, the longest, the

[088-127]whitest and the thickest beard that human eyes had ever beheld until

[088-128]that moment, and then fetching up a grave, sonorous voice from the

[088-129]depths of his broad, capacious chest, and fixing his eyes on the duke,

[088-130]he said:

[088-131]“Most high and mighty señor, my name is Trifaldin of the White Beard; I

[088-132]am squire to the Countess Trifaldi, otherwise called the Distressed

[088-133]Duenna, on whose behalf I bear a message to your highness, which is

[088-134]that your magnificence will be pleased to grant her leave and

[088-135]permission to come and tell you her trouble, which is one of the

[088-136]strangest and most wonderful that the mind most familiar with trouble

[088-137]in the world could have imagined; but first she desires to know if the

[088-138]valiant and never vanquished knight, Don Quixote of La Mancha, is in

[088-139]this your castle, for she has come in quest of him on foot and without

[088-140]breaking her fast from the kingdom of Kandy to your realms here; a

[088-141]thing which may and ought to be regarded as a miracle or set down to

[088-142]enchantment; she is even now at the gate of this fortress or plaisance,

[088-143]and only waits for your permission to enter. I have spoken.” And with

[088-144]that he coughed, and stroked down his beard with both his hands, and

[088-145]stood very tranquilly waiting for the response of the duke, which was

[088-146]to this effect: “Many days ago, worthy squire Trifaldin of the White

[088-147]Beard, we heard of the misfortune of my lady the Countess Trifaldi,

[088-148]whom the enchanters have caused to be called the Distressed Duenna. Bid

[088-149]her enter, O stupendous squire, and tell her that the valiant knight

[088-150]Don Quixote of La Mancha is here, and from his generous disposition she

[088-151]may safely promise herself every protection and assistance; and you may

[088-152]tell her, too, that if my aid be necessary it will not be withheld, for

[088-153]I am bound to give it to her by my quality of knight, which involves

[088-154]the protection of women of all sorts, especially widowed, wronged, and

[088-155]distressed dames, such as her ladyship seems to be.”

[088-156]On hearing this Trifaldin bent the knee to the ground, and making a

[088-157]sign to the fifer and drummers to strike up, he turned and marched out

[088-158]of the garden to the same notes and at the same pace as when he

[088-159]entered, leaving them all amazed at his bearing and solemnity. Turning

[088-160]to Don Quixote, the duke said, “After all, renowned knight, the mists

[088-161]of malice and ignorance are unable to hide or obscure the light of

[088-162]valour and virtue. I say so, because your excellence has been barely

[088-163]six days in this castle, and already the unhappy and the afflicted come

[088-164]in quest of you from lands far distant and remote, and not in coaches

[088-165]or on dromedaries, but on foot and fasting, confident that in that

[088-166]mighty arm they will find a cure for their sorrows and troubles; thanks

[088-167]to your great achievements, which are circulated all over the known

[088-168]earth.”

[088-169]“I wish, señor duke,” replied Don Quixote, “that blessed ecclesiastic,

[088-170]who at table the other day showed such ill-will and bitter spite

[088-171]against knights-errant, were here now to see with his own eyes whether

[088-172]knights of the sort are needed in the world; he would at any rate learn

[088-173]by experience that those suffering any extraordinary affliction or

[088-174]sorrow, in extreme cases and unusual misfortunes do not go to look for

[088-175]a remedy to the houses of jurists or village sacristans, or to the

[088-176]knight who has never attempted to pass the bounds of his own town, or

[088-177]to the indolent courtier who only seeks for news to repeat and talk of,

[088-178]instead of striving to do deeds and exploits for others to relate and

[088-179]record. Relief in distress, help in need, protection for damsels,

[088-180]consolation for widows, are to be found in no sort of persons better

[088-181]than in knights-errant; and I give unceasing thanks to heaven that I am

[088-182]one, and regard any misfortune or suffering that may befall me in the

[088-183]pursuit of so honourable a calling as endured to good purpose. Let this

[088-184]duenna come and ask what she will, for I will effect her relief by the

[088-185]might of my arm and the dauntless resolution of my bold heart.”
