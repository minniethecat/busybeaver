OF THE WONDERFUL THINGS THE INCOMPARABLE DON QUIXOTE SAID HE SAW IN THE

[075-001]PROFOUND CAVE OF MONTESINOS, THE IMPOSSIBILITY AND MAGNITUDE OF WHICH

CAUSE THIS ADVENTURE TO BE DEEMED APOCRYPHAL

p23a.jpg (148K)


[075-002]It was about four in the afternoon when the sun, veiled in clouds, with

[075-003]subdued light and tempered beams, enabled Don Quixote to relate,

[075-004]without heat or inconvenience, what he had seen in the cave of

[075-005]Montesinos to his two illustrious hearers, and he began as follows:

[075-006]“A matter of some twelve or fourteen times a man’s height down in this

[075-007]pit, on the right-hand side, there is a recess or space, roomy enough

[075-008]to contain a large cart with its mules. A little light reaches it

[075-009]through some chinks or crevices, communicating with it and open to the

[075-010]surface of the earth. This recess or space I perceived when I was

[075-011]already growing weary and disgusted at finding myself hanging suspended

[075-012]by the rope, travelling downwards into that dark region without any

[075-013]certainty or knowledge of where I was going, so I resolved to enter it

[075-014]and rest myself for a while. I called out, telling you not to let out

[075-015]more rope until I bade you, but you cannot have heard me. I then

[075-016]gathered in the rope you were sending me, and making a coil or pile of

[075-017]it I seated myself upon it, ruminating and considering what I was to do

[075-018]to lower myself to the bottom, having no one to hold me up; and as I

[075-019]was thus deep in thought and perplexity, suddenly and without

[075-020]provocation a profound sleep fell upon me, and when I least expected

[075-021]it, I know not how, I awoke and found myself in the midst of the most

[075-022]beautiful, delightful meadow that nature could produce or the most

[075-023]lively human imagination conceive. I opened my eyes, I rubbed them, and

[075-024]found I was not asleep but thoroughly awake. Nevertheless, I felt my

[075-025]head and breast to satisfy myself whether it was I myself who was there

[075-026]or some empty delusive phantom; but touch, feeling, the collected

[075-027]thoughts that passed through my mind, all convinced me that I was the

[075-028]same then and there that I am this moment. Next there presented itself

[075-029]to my sight a stately royal palace or castle, with walls that seemed

[075-030]built of clear transparent crystal; and through two great doors that

[075-031]opened wide therein, I saw coming forth and advancing towards me a

[075-032]venerable old man, clad in a long gown of mulberry-coloured serge that

[075-033]trailed upon the ground. On his shoulders and breast he had a green

[075-034]satin collegiate hood, and covering his head a black Milanese bonnet,

[075-035]and his snow-white beard fell below his girdle. He carried no arms

[075-036]whatever, nothing but a rosary of beads bigger than fair-sized

[075-037]filberts, each tenth bead being like a moderate ostrich egg; his

[075-038]bearing, his gait, his dignity and imposing presence held me spellbound

[075-039]and wondering. He approached me, and the first thing he did was to

[075-040]embrace me closely, and then he said to me, ‘For a long time now, O

[075-041]valiant knight Don Quixote of La Mancha, we who are here enchanted in

[075-042]these solitudes have been hoping to see thee, that thou mayest make

[075-043]known to the world what is shut up and concealed in this deep cave,

[075-044]called the cave of Montesinos, which thou hast entered, an achievement

[075-045]reserved for thy invincible heart and stupendous courage alone to

[075-046]attempt. Come with me, illustrious sir, and I will show thee the

[075-047]marvels hidden within this transparent castle, whereof I am the alcaide

[075-048]and perpetual warden; for I am Montesinos himself, from whom the cave

[075-049]takes its name.’

[075-050]“The instant he told me he was Montesinos, I asked him if the story

[075-051]they told in the world above here was true, that he had taken out the

[075-052]heart of his great friend Durandarte from his breast with a little

[075-053]dagger, and carried it to the lady Belerma, as his friend when at the

[075-054]point of death had commanded him. He said in reply that they spoke the

[075-055]truth in every respect except as to the dagger, for it was not a

[075-056]dagger, nor little, but a burnished poniard sharper than an awl.”

[075-057]“That poniard must have been made by Ramon de Hoces the Sevillian,”

[075-058]said Sancho.

[075-059]“I do not know,” said Don Quixote; “it could not have been by that

[075-060]poniard maker, however, because Ramon de Hoces was a man of yesterday,

[075-061]and the affair of Roncesvalles, where this mishap occurred, was long

[075-062]ago; but the question is of no great importance, nor does it affect or

[075-063]make any alteration in the truth or substance of the story.”

[075-064]“That is true,” said the cousin; “continue, Señor Don Quixote, for I am

[075-065]listening to you with the greatest pleasure in the world.”

[075-066]“And with no less do I tell the tale,” said Don Quixote; “and so, to

[075-067]proceed—the venerable Montesinos led me into the palace of crystal,

[075-068]where, in a lower chamber, strangely cool and entirely of alabaster,

[075-069]was an elaborately wrought marble tomb, upon which I beheld, stretched

[075-070]at full length, a knight, not of bronze, or marble, or jasper, as are

[075-071]seen on other tombs, but of actual flesh and bone. His right hand

[075-072](which seemed to me somewhat hairy and sinewy, a sign of great strength

[075-073]in its owner) lay on the side of his heart; but before I could put any

[075-074]question to Montesinos, he, seeing me gazing at the tomb in amazement,

[075-075]said to me, ‘This is my friend Durandarte, flower and mirror of the

[075-076]true lovers and valiant knights of his time. He is held enchanted here,

[075-077]as I myself and many others are, by that French enchanter Merlin, who,

[075-078]they say, was the devil’s son; but my belief is, not that he was the

[075-079]devil’s son, but that he knew, as the saying is, a point more than the

[075-080]devil. How or why he enchanted us, no one knows, but time will tell,

[075-081]and I suspect that time is not far off. What I marvel at is, that I

[075-082]know it to be as sure as that it is now day, that Durandarte ended his

[075-083]life in my arms, and that, after his death, I took out his heart with

[075-084]my own hands; and indeed it must have weighed more than two pounds,

[075-085]for, according to naturalists, he who has a large heart is more largely

[075-086]endowed with valour than he who has a small one. Then, as this is the

[075-087]case, and as the knight did really die, how comes it that he now moans

[075-088]and sighs from time to time, as if he were still alive?’

[075-089]“As he said this, the wretched Durandarte cried out in a loud voice:

[075-090]O cousin Montesinos!

[075-091]’Twas my last request of thee,

[075-092]When my soul hath left the body,

[075-093]And that lying dead I be,

[075-094]With thy poniard or thy dagger

[075-095]Cut the heart from out my breast,

[075-096]And bear it to Belerma.

[075-097]This was my last request.”

[075-098]“On hearing which, the venerable Montesinos fell on his knees before

[075-099]the unhappy knight, and with tearful eyes exclaimed, ‘Long since, Señor

[075-100]Durandarte, my beloved cousin, long since have I done what you bade me

[075-101]on that sad day when I lost you; I took out your heart as well as I

[075-102]could, not leaving an atom of it in your breast, I wiped it with a lace

[075-103]handkerchief, and I took the road to France with it, having first laid

[075-104]you in the bosom of the earth with tears enough to wash and cleanse my

[075-105]hands of the blood that covered them after wandering among your bowels;

[075-106]and more by token, O cousin of my soul, at the first village I came to

[075-107]after leaving Roncesvalles, I sprinkled a little salt upon your heart

[075-108]to keep it sweet, and bring it, if not fresh, at least pickled, into

[075-109]the presence of the lady Belerma, whom, together with you, myself,

[075-110]Guadiana your squire, the duenna Ruidera and her seven daughters and

[075-111]two nieces, and many more of your friends and acquaintances, the sage

[075-112]Merlin has been keeping enchanted here these many years; and although

[075-113]more than five hundred have gone by, not one of us has died; Ruidera

[075-114]and her daughters and nieces alone are missing, and these, because of

[075-115]the tears they shed, Merlin, out of the compassion he seems to have

[075-116]felt for them, changed into so many lakes, which to this day in the

[075-117]world of the living, and in the province of La Mancha, are called the

[075-118]Lakes of Ruidera. The seven daughters belong to the kings of Spain and

[075-119]the two nieces to the knights of a very holy order called the Order of

[075-120]St. John. Guadiana your squire, likewise bewailing your fate, was

[075-121]changed into a river of his own name, but when he came to the surface

[075-122]and beheld the sun of another heaven, so great was his grief at finding

[075-123]he was leaving you, that he plunged into the bowels of the earth;

[075-124]however, as he cannot help following his natural course, he from time

[075-125]to time comes forth and shows himself to the sun and the world. The

[075-126]lakes aforesaid send him their waters, and with these, and others that

[075-127]come to him, he makes a grand and imposing entrance into Portugal; but

[075-128]for all that, go where he may, he shows his melancholy and sadness, and

[075-129]takes no pride in breeding dainty choice fish, only coarse and

[075-130]tasteless sorts, very different from those of the golden Tagus. All

[075-131]this that I tell you now, O cousin mine, I have told you many times

[075-132]before, and as you make no answer, I fear that either you believe me

[075-133]not, or do not hear me, whereat I feel God knows what grief. I have now

[075-134]news to give you, which, if it serves not to alleviate your sufferings,

[075-135]will not in any wise increase them. Know that you have here before you

[075-136](open your eyes and you will see) that great knight of whom the sage

[075-137]Merlin has prophesied such great things; that Don Quixote of La Mancha

[075-138]I mean, who has again, and to better purpose than in past times,

[075-139]revived in these days knight-errantry, long since forgotten, and by

[075-140]whose intervention and aid it may be we shall be disenchanted; for

[075-141]great deeds are reserved for great men.’

[075-142]“‘And if that may not be,’ said the wretched Durandarte in a low and

[075-143]feeble voice, ‘if that may not be, then, my cousin, I say “patience and

[075-144]shuffle;”’ and turning over on his side, he relapsed into his former

[075-145]silence without uttering another word.

[075-146]“And now there was heard a great outcry and lamentation, accompanied by

[075-147]deep sighs and bitter sobs. I looked round, and through the crystal

[075-148]wall I saw passing through another chamber a procession of two lines of

[075-149]fair damsels all clad in mourning, and with white turbans of Turkish

[075-150]fashion on their heads. Behind, in the rear of these, there came a

[075-151]lady, for so from her dignity she seemed to be, also clad in black,

[075-152]with a white veil so long and ample that it swept the ground. Her

[075-153]turban was twice as large as the largest of any of the others; her

[075-154]eyebrows met, her nose was rather flat, her mouth was large but with

[075-155]ruddy lips, and her teeth, of which at times she allowed a glimpse,

[075-156]were seen to be sparse and ill-set, though as white as peeled almonds.

[075-157]She carried in her hands a fine cloth, and in it, as well as I could

[075-158]make out, a heart that had been mummied, so parched and dried was it.

[075-159]Montesinos told me that all those forming the procession were the

[075-160]attendants of Durandarte and Belerma, who were enchanted there with

[075-161]their master and mistress, and that the last, she who carried the heart

[075-162]in the cloth, was the lady Belerma, who, with her damsels, four days in

[075-163]the week went in procession singing, or rather weeping, dirges over the

[075-164]body and miserable heart of his cousin; and that if she appeared to me

[075-165]somewhat ill-favoured or not so beautiful as fame reported her, it was

[075-166]because of the bad nights and worse days that she passed in that

[075-167]enchantment, as I could see by the great dark circles round her eyes,

[075-168]and her sickly complexion; ‘her sallowness, and the rings round her

[075-169]eyes,’ said he, ‘are not caused by the periodical ailment usual with

[075-170]women, for it is many months and even years since she has had any, but

[075-171]by the grief her own heart suffers because of that which she holds in

[075-172]her hand perpetually, and which recalls and brings back to her memory

[075-173]the sad fate of her lost lover; were it not for this, hardly would the

[075-174]great Dulcinea del Toboso, so celebrated in all these parts, and even

[075-175]in the world, come up to her for beauty, grace, and gaiety.’

[075-176]“‘Hold hard!’ said I at this, ‘tell your story as you ought, Señor Don

[075-177]Montesinos, for you know very well that all comparisons are odious, and

[075-178]there is no occasion to compare one person with another; the peerless

[075-179]Dulcinea del Toboso is what she is, and the lady Doña Belerma is what

[075-180]_she_ is and has been, and that’s enough.’ To which he made answer,

[075-181]‘Forgive me, Señor Don Quixote; I own I was wrong and spoke unadvisedly

[075-182]in saying that the lady Dulcinea could scarcely come up to the lady

[075-183]Belerma; for it were enough for me to have learned, by what means I

[075-184]know not, that you are her knight, to make me bite my tongue out before

[075-185]I compared her to anything save heaven itself.’ After this apology

[075-186]which the great Montesinos made me, my heart recovered itself from the

[075-187]shock I had received in hearing my lady compared with Belerma.”

[075-188]“Still I wonder,” said Sancho, “that your worship did not get upon the

[075-189]old fellow and bruise every bone of him with kicks, and pluck his beard

[075-190]until you didn’t leave a hair in it.”

[075-191]“Nay, Sancho, my friend,” said Don Quixote, “it would not have been

[075-192]right in me to do that, for we are all bound to pay respect to the

[075-193]aged, even though they be not knights, but especially to those who are,

[075-194]and who are enchanted; I only know I gave him as good as he brought in

[075-195]the many other questions and answers we exchanged.”

[075-196]“I cannot understand, Señor Don Quixote,” remarked the cousin here,

[075-197]“how it is that your worship, in such a short space of time as you have

[075-198]been below there, could have seen so many things, and said and answered

[075-199]so much.”

[075-200]“How long is it since I went down?” asked Don Quixote.

[075-201]“Little better than an hour,” replied Sancho.

[075-202]“That cannot be,” returned Don Quixote, “because night overtook me

[075-203]while I was there, and day came, and it was night again and day again

[075-204]three times; so that, by my reckoning, I have been three days in those

[075-205]remote regions beyond our ken.”

[075-206]“My master must be right,” replied Sancho; “for as everything that has

[075-207]happened to him is by enchantment, maybe what seems to us an hour would

[075-208]seem three days and nights there.”

[075-209]“That’s it,” said Don Quixote.

[075-210]“And did your worship eat anything all that time, señor?” asked the

[075-211]cousin.

[075-212]“I never touched a morsel,” answered Don Quixote, “nor did I feel

[075-213]hunger, or think of it.”

[075-214]“And do the enchanted eat?” said the cousin.

[075-215]“They neither eat,” said Don Quixote; “nor are they subject to the

[075-216]greater excrements, though it is thought that their nails, beards, and

[075-217]hair grow.”

[075-218]“And do the enchanted sleep, now, señor?” asked Sancho.

[075-219]“Certainly not,” replied Don Quixote; “at least, during those three

[075-220]days I was with them not one of them closed an eye, nor did I either.”

[075-221]“The proverb, ‘Tell me what company thou keepest and I’ll tell thee

[075-222]what thou art,’ is to the point here,” said Sancho; “your worship keeps

[075-223]company with enchanted people that are always fasting and watching;

[075-224]what wonder is it, then, that you neither eat nor sleep while you are

[075-225]with them? But forgive me, señor, if I say that of all this you have

[075-226]told us now, may God take me—I was just going to say the devil—if I

[075-227]believe a single particle.”

[075-228]“What!” said the cousin, “has Señor Don Quixote, then, been lying? Why,

[075-229]even if he wished it he has not had time to imagine and put together

[075-230]such a host of lies.”

[075-231]“I don’t believe my master lies,” said Sancho.

[075-232]“If not, what dost thou believe?” asked Don Quixote.

[075-233]“I believe,” replied Sancho, “that this Merlin, or those enchanters who

[075-234]enchanted the whole crew your worship says you saw and discoursed with

[075-235]down there, stuffed your imagination or your mind with all this

[075-236]rigmarole you have been treating us to, and all that is still to come.”

[075-237]“All that might be, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote; “but it is not so,

[075-238]for everything that I have told you I saw with my own eyes, and touched

[075-239]with my own hands. But what will you say when I tell you now how, among

[075-240]the countless other marvellous things Montesinos showed me (of which at

[075-241]leisure and at the proper time I will give thee an account in the

[075-242]course of our journey, for they would not be all in place here), he

[075-243]showed me three country girls who went skipping and capering like goats

[075-244]over the pleasant fields there, and the instant I beheld them I knew

[075-245]one to be the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, and the other two those

[075-246]same country girls that were with her and that we spoke to on the road

[075-247]from El Toboso! I asked Montesinos if he knew them, and he told me he

[075-248]did not, but he thought they must be some enchanted ladies of

[075-249]distinction, for it was only a few days before that they had made their

[075-250]appearance in those meadows; but I was not to be surprised at that,

[075-251]because there were a great many other ladies there of times past and

[075-252]present, enchanted in various strange shapes, and among them he had

[075-253]recognised Queen Guinevere and her dame Quintañona, she who poured out

[075-254]the wine for Lancelot when he came from Britain.”

[075-255]When Sancho Panza heard his master say this he was ready to take leave

[075-256]of his senses, or die with laughter; for, as he knew the real truth

[075-257]about the pretended enchantment of Dulcinea, in which he himself had

[075-258]been the enchanter and concocter of all the evidence, he made up his

[075-259]mind at last that, beyond all doubt, his master was out of his wits and

[075-260]stark mad, so he said to him, “It was an evil hour, a worse season, and

[075-261]a sorrowful day, when your worship, dear master mine, went down to the

[075-262]other world, and an unlucky moment when you met with Señor Montesinos,

[075-263]who has sent you back to us like this. You were well enough here above

[075-264]in your full senses, such as God had given you, delivering maxims and

[075-265]giving advice at every turn, and not as you are now, talking the

[075-266]greatest nonsense that can be imagined.”

[075-267]“As I know thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “I heed not thy words.”

[075-268]“Nor I your worship’s,” said Sancho, “whether you beat me or kill me

[075-269]for those I have spoken, and will speak if you don’t correct and mend

[075-270]your own. But tell me, while we are still at peace, how or by what did

[075-271]you recognise the lady our mistress; and if you spoke to her, what did

[075-272]you say, and what did she answer?”

[075-273]“I recognised her,” said Don Quixote, “by her wearing the same garments

[075-274]she wore when thou didst point her out to me. I spoke to her, but she

[075-275]did not utter a word in reply; on the contrary, she turned her back on

[075-276]me and took to flight, at such a pace that crossbow bolt could not have

[075-277]overtaken her. I wished to follow her, and would have done so had not

[075-278]Montesinos recommended me not to take the trouble as it would be

[075-279]useless, particularly as the time was drawing near when it would be

[075-280]necessary for me to quit the cavern. He told me, moreover, that in

[075-281]course of time he would let me know how he and Belerma, and Durandarte,

[075-282]and all who were there, were to be disenchanted. But of all I saw and

[075-283]observed down there, what gave me most pain was, that while Montesinos

[075-284]was speaking to me, one of the two companions of the hapless Dulcinea

[075-285]approached me on one without my having seen her coming, and with tears

[075-286]in her eyes said to me, in a low, agitated voice, ‘My lady Dulcinea del

[075-287]Toboso kisses your worship’s hands, and entreats you to do her the

[075-288]favour of letting her know how you are; and, being in great need, she

[075-289]also entreats your worship as earnestly as she can to be so good as to

[075-290]lend her half a dozen reals, or as much as you may have about you, on

[075-291]this new dimity petticoat that I have here; and she promises to repay

[075-292]them very speedily.’ I was amazed and taken aback by such a message,

[075-293]and turning to Señor Montesinos I asked him, ‘Is it possible, Señor

[075-294]Montesinos, that persons of distinction under enchantment can be in

[075-295]need?’ To which he replied, ‘Believe me, Señor Don Quixote, that which

[075-296]is called need is to be met with everywhere, and penetrates all

[075-297]quarters and reaches everyone, and does not spare even the enchanted;

[075-298]and as the lady Dulcinea del Toboso sends to beg those six reals, and

[075-299]the pledge is to all appearance a good one, there is nothing for it but

[075-300]to give them to her, for no doubt she must be in some great strait.’ ‘I

[075-301]will take no pledge of her,’ I replied, ‘nor yet can I give her what

[075-302]she asks, for all I have is four reals; which I gave (they were those

[075-303]which thou, Sancho, gavest me the other day to bestow in alms upon the

[075-304]poor I met along the road), and I said, ‘Tell your mistress, my dear,

[075-305]that I am grieved to the heart because of her distresses, and wish I

[075-306]was a Fucar to remedy them, and that I would have her know that I

[075-307]cannot be, and ought not be, in health while deprived of the happiness

[075-308]of seeing her and enjoying her discreet conversation, and that I

[075-309]implore her as earnestly as I can, to allow herself to be seen and

[075-310]addressed by this her captive servant and forlorn knight. Tell her,

[075-311]too, that when she least expects it she will hear it announced that I

[075-312]have made an oath and vow after the fashion of that which the Marquis

[075-313]of Mantua made to avenge his nephew Baldwin, when he found him at the

[075-314]point of death in the heart of the mountains, which was, not to eat

[075-315]bread off a tablecloth, and other trifling matters which he added,

[075-316]until he had avenged him; and I will make the same to take no rest, and

[075-317]to roam the seven regions of the earth more thoroughly than the Infante

[075-318]Don Pedro of Portugal ever roamed them, until I have disenchanted her.’

[075-319]‘All that and more, you owe my lady,’ the damsel’s answer to me, and

[075-320]taking the four reals, instead of making me a curtsey she cut a caper,

[075-321]springing two full yards into the air.”

[075-322]“O blessed God!” exclaimed Sancho aloud at this, “is it possible that

[075-323]such things can be in the world, and that enchanters and enchantments

[075-324]can have such power in it as to have changed my master’s right senses

[075-325]into a craze so full of absurdity! O señor, señor, for God’s sake,

[075-326]consider yourself, have a care for your honour, and give no credit to

[075-327]this silly stuff that has left you scant and short of wits.”

[075-328]“Thou talkest in this way because thou lovest me, Sancho,” said Don

[075-329]Quixote; “and not being experienced in the things of the world,

[075-330]everything that has some difficulty about it seems to thee impossible;

[075-331]but time will pass, as I said before, and I will tell thee some of the

[075-332]things I saw down there which will make thee believe what I have

[075-333]related now, the truth of which admits of neither reply nor question.”
