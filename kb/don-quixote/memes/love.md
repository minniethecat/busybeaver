## Knightly Love

In the chivalric code Don Quixote adopts, love is not optional but constitutive. A knight without a lady is incomplete: "for a knight-errant without love was like a tree without leaves or fruit, or a body without a soul" (001-139). This conviction drives him to invent Dulcinea del Toboso — an idealized version of a neighbouring farm-girl, Aldonza Lorenzo — before setting out on his first sally.

The invention of Dulcinea is both comic and poignant. Don Quixote's description of his imaginary lady borrows the conventional hyperbole of courtly poetry while the narrator quietly reminds us of the mundane reality:

> Her name is Dulcinea, her country El Toboso — a village of La Mancha — her rank at the very least a princess, for she is my queen and lady, and her beauty superhuman, for in her are made real all the impossible and chimerical attributes of beauty which poets bestow upon their ladies. (013-024)

## Love as Delusion

Don Quixote's love for Dulcinea is an extension of his madness. He has never spoken to her, yet he constructs an elaborate emotional reality around her. His devotion is entirely literary — modeled on the loves of Amadis of Gaul for Oriana and of Palmerin for Polinarda. The love is real in its effects (it motivates his adventures) while being entirely fictional in its object.

> Presently he broke out again, as if he were love-stricken in earnest, "O Princess Dulcinea, lady of this captive heart, sorely hast thou afflicted me in driving me forth with the rigor of thy scorn." (002-046)

## Real Love: The Interpolated Tales

Cervantes contrasts Don Quixote's artificial courtly love with the real loves depicted in the novel's interpolated tales. Cardenio's madness over Lucinda, the Captive's story of Zoraida, and the tale of the Curious Impertinent all present love as something genuinely disruptive — capable of madness, sacrifice, and transformation — without the mediation of chivalric convention.

| Type of Love | Representative | Basis |
|-------------|---------------|-------|
| Courtly (imagined) | Don Quixote → Dulcinea | Literary convention |
| Jealous (real) | Cardenio → Luscinda | Betrayal by friend |
| Forbidden | Captive → Zoraida | Cross-cultural, cross-faith |
| Marital | Camacho's wedding | Social convention vs. true love |

## The Enchantment of Dulcinea

In Part II, Sancho Panza attempts to pass off three peasant girls as Dulcinea and her ladies. Don Quixote sees only coarse country wenches and concludes that enchanters have transformed his lady — a moment that reveals both the pathos and the ingenuity of his delusion. He can perceive only what his literary framework allows, even when the "real" Dulcinea stands before him.
