## Origins and Recruitment

Sancho Panza is introduced as a poor labourer, a neighbour of Alonso Quixano, whom Don Quixote persuades to serve as his squire. The recruitment is transactional — Don Quixote promises him the governorship of an island:

> Among other things, he told him he ought to be ready to go with him gladly, because at any moment an adventure might occur that would win an island, and leave him as governor. With these and the like promises Sancho Panza (for so the labourer was called) left wife and children, and engaged himself as esquire to his neighbour. (007-097)

Sancho is mounted on a donkey — a detail that becomes a running joke, since no chivalric romance features a squire on ass-back. Cervantes deliberately subverts the convention: "the ingenious gentleman had not been able to remember in any history of chivalry of any knight-errant taking with him an esquire mounted on ass-back" (007-114).

## Character and Speech

Sancho Panza is defined by his speech — a ceaseless flow of proverbs that both exasperates and amuses his master. Where Don Quixote speaks in the elevated language of chivalric romance, Sancho speaks in the vernacular of the Castilian peasant. His proverbs are practical, concrete, and often only tangentially relevant.

This linguistic contrast embodies the novel's central tension between idealism and realism. Sancho sees windmills where Don Quixote sees giants:

> "Look, your worship," said Sancho; "what we see there are not giants but windmills, and what seem to be their arms are the sails that turned by the wind make the millstone go." (008-013)

Yet his loyalty to his master is absolute. Despite being tossed in a blanket, beaten, and repeatedly humiliated, Sancho remains at Don Quixote's side through both expeditions.

## Governorship

Sancho's dream of governing an island is eventually realized — or rather, staged — in Part II, when the Duke and Duchess arrange for him to govern the fictional "island" of Barataria. His tenure as governor reveals unexpected wisdom:

| Episode | Sancho's Role | Outcome |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Windmill battle | Witness and victim | Tossed with his ass |
| Blanketing at inn | Innocent bystander | Tossed in a blanket |
| Barataria governorship | Governor | Rules wisely then resigns |
| Don Quixote's illness | Faithful squire | Weeps at his master's bedside |

His judgments as governor are shrewd and proverb-laden, demonstrating practical intelligence despite his lack of education. When he voluntarily resigns the governorship — "I was not born to be a governor" (054-042) — it is an act of self-knowledge that his master would have done well to imitate sooner.

## Faithful Companion

Sancho's relationship with Don Quixote evolves from mercenary transaction to genuine affection. By the novel's end, he has internalized something of his master's worldview even as he remains firmly grounded. When Don Quixote lies dying, Sancho weeps and begs him not to die — the squire who began as a comic foil has become the story's emotional anchor.
