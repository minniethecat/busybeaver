WHICH TREATS OF THE CHARACTER AND PURSUITS OF THE FAMOUS GENTLEMAN DON

QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA

[001-001]In a village of La Mancha, the name of which I have no desire to call

[001-002]to mind, there lived not long since one of those gentlemen that keep a

[001-003]lance in the lance-rack, an old buckler, a lean hack, and a greyhound

[001-004]for coursing. An olla of rather more beef than mutton, a salad on most

[001-005]nights, scraps on Saturdays, lentils on Fridays, and a pigeon or so

[001-006]extra on Sundays, made away with three-quarters of his income. The rest

[001-007]of it went in a doublet of fine cloth and velvet breeches and shoes to

[001-008]match for holidays, while on week-days he made a brave figure in his

[001-009]best homespun. He had in his house a housekeeper past forty, a niece

[001-010]under twenty, and a lad for the field and market-place, who used to

[001-011]saddle the hack as well as handle the bill-hook. The age of this

[001-012]gentleman of ours was bordering on fifty; he was of a hardy habit,

[001-013]spare, gaunt-featured, a very early riser and a great sportsman. They

[001-014]will have it his surname was Quixada or Quesada (for here there is some

[001-015]difference of opinion among the authors who write on the subject),

[001-016]although from reasonable conjectures it seems plain that he was called

[001-017]Quexana. This, however, is of but little importance to our tale; it

[001-018]will be enough not to stray a hair’s breadth from the truth in the

[001-019]telling of it.

[001-020]You must know, then, that the above-named gentleman whenever he was at

[001-021]leisure (which was mostly all the year round) gave himself up to

[001-022]reading books of chivalry with such ardour and avidity that he almost

[001-023]entirely neglected the pursuit of his field-sports, and even the

[001-024]management of his property; and to such a pitch did his eagerness and

[001-025]infatuation go that he sold many an acre of tillageland to buy books of

[001-026]chivalry to read, and brought home as many of them as he could get. But

[001-027]of all there were none he liked so well as those of the famous

[001-028]Feliciano de Silva’s composition, for their lucidity of style and

[001-029]complicated conceits were as pearls in his sight, particularly when in

[001-030]his reading he came upon courtships and cartels, where he often found

[001-031]passages like “_the reason of the unreason with which my reason is

[001-032]afflicted so weakens my reason that with reason I murmur at your

[001-033]beauty;” or again, “the high heavens, that of your divinity divinely

[001-034]fortify you with the stars, render you deserving of the desert your

[001-035]greatness deserves_.” Over conceits of this sort the poor gentleman

[001-036]lost his wits, and used to lie awake striving to understand them and

[001-037]worm the meaning out of them; what Aristotle himself could not have

[001-038]made out or extracted had he come to life again for that special

[001-039]purpose. He was not at all easy about the wounds which Don Belianis

[001-040]gave and took, because it seemed to him that, great as were the

[001-041]surgeons who had cured him, he must have had his face and body covered

[001-042]all over with seams and scars. He commended, however, the author’s way

[001-043]of ending his book with the promise of that interminable adventure, and

[001-044]many a time was he tempted to take up his pen and finish it properly as

[001-045]is there proposed, which no doubt he would have done, and made a

[001-046]successful piece of work of it too, had not greater and more absorbing

[001-047]thoughts prevented him.

[001-048]Many an argument did he have with the curate of his village (a learned

[001-049]man, and a graduate of Siguenza) as to which had been the better

[001-050]knight, Palmerin of England or Amadis of Gaul. Master Nicholas, the

[001-051]village barber, however, used to say that neither of them came up to

[001-052]the Knight of Phœbus, and that if there was any that could compare with

[001-053]_him_ it was Don Galaor, the brother of Amadis of Gaul, because he had

[001-054]a spirit that was equal to every occasion, and was no finikin knight,

[001-055]nor lachrymose like his brother, while in the matter of valour he was

[001-056]not a whit behind him. In short, he became so absorbed in his books

[001-057]that he spent his nights from sunset to sunrise, and his days from dawn

[001-058]to dark, poring over them; and what with little sleep and much reading

[001-059]his brains got so dry that he lost his wits. His fancy grew full of

[001-060]what he used to read about in his books, enchantments, quarrels,

[001-061]battles, challenges, wounds, wooings, loves, agonies, and all sorts of

[001-062]impossible nonsense; and it so possessed his mind that the whole fabric

[001-063]of invention and fancy he read of was true, that to him no history in

[001-064]the world had more reality in it. He used to say the Cid Ruy Diaz was a

[001-065]very good knight, but that he was not to be compared with the Knight of

[001-066]the Burning Sword who with one back-stroke cut in half two fierce and

[001-067]monstrous giants. He thought more of Bernardo del Carpio because at

[001-068]Roncesvalles he slew Roland in spite of enchantments, availing himself

[001-069]of the artifice of Hercules when he strangled Antæus the son of Terra

[001-070]in his arms. He approved highly of the giant Morgante, because,

[001-071]although of the giant breed which is always arrogant and

[001-072]ill-conditioned, he alone was affable and well-bred. But above all he

[001-073]admired Reinaldos of Montalban, especially when he saw him sallying

[001-074]forth from his castle and robbing everyone he met, and when beyond the

[001-075]seas he stole that image of Mahomet which, as his history says, was

[001-076]entirely of gold. To have a bout of kicking at that traitor of a

[001-077]Ganelon he would have given his housekeeper, and his niece into the

[001-078]bargain.

[001-079]In short, his wits being quite gone, he hit upon the strangest notion

[001-080]that ever madman in this world hit upon, and that was that he fancied

[001-081]it was right and requisite, as well for the support of his own honour

[001-082]as for the service of his country, that he should make a knight-errant

[001-083]of himself, roaming the world over in full armour and on horseback in

[001-084]quest of adventures, and putting in practice himself all that he had

[001-085]read of as being the usual practices of knights-errant; righting every

[001-086]kind of wrong, and exposing himself to peril and danger from which, in

[001-087]the issue, he was to reap eternal renown and fame. Already the poor man

[001-088]saw himself crowned by the might of his arm Emperor of Trebizond at

[001-089]least; and so, led away by the intense enjoyment he found in these

[001-090]pleasant fancies, he set himself forthwith to put his scheme into

[001-091]execution.

[001-092]The first thing he did was to clean up some armour that had belonged to

[001-093]his great-grandfather, and had been for ages lying forgotten in a

[001-094]corner eaten with rust and covered with mildew. He scoured and polished

[001-095]it as best he could, but he perceived one great defect in it, that it

[001-096]had no closed helmet, nothing but a simple morion. This deficiency,

[001-097]however, his ingenuity supplied, for he contrived a kind of half-helmet

[001-098]of pasteboard which, fitted on to the morion, looked like a whole one.

[001-099]It is true that, in order to see if it was strong and fit to stand a

[001-100]cut, he drew his sword and gave it a couple of slashes, the first of

[001-101]which undid in an instant what had taken him a week to do. The ease

[001-102]with which he had knocked it to pieces disconcerted him somewhat, and

[001-103]to guard against that danger he set to work again, fixing bars of iron

[001-104]on the inside until he was satisfied with its strength; and then, not

[001-105]caring to try any more experiments with it, he passed it and adopted it

[001-106]as a helmet of the most perfect construction.

[001-107]He next proceeded to inspect his hack, which, with more quartos than a

[001-108]real and more blemishes than the steed of Gonela, that “_tantum pellis

[001-109]et ossa fuit_,” surpassed in his eyes the Bucephalus of Alexander or

[001-110]the Babieca of the Cid. Four days were spent in thinking what name to

[001-111]give him, because (as he said to himself) it was not right that a horse

[001-112]belonging to a knight so famous, and one with such merits of his own,

[001-113]should be without some distinctive name, and he strove to adapt it so

[001-114]as to indicate what he had been before belonging to a knight-errant,

[001-115]and what he then was; for it was only reasonable that, his master

[001-116]taking a new character, he should take a new name, and that it should

[001-117]be a distinguished and full-sounding one, befitting the new order and

[001-118]calling he was about to follow. And so, after having composed, struck

[001-119]out, rejected, added to, unmade, and remade a multitude of names out of

[001-120]his memory and fancy, he decided upon calling him Rocinante, a name, to

[001-121]his thinking, lofty, sonorous, and significant of his condition as a

[001-122]hack before he became what he now was, the first and foremost of all

[001-123]the hacks in the world.

[001-124]Having got a name for his horse so much to his taste, he was anxious to

[001-125]get one for himself, and he was eight days more pondering over this

[001-126]point, till at last he made up his mind to call himself “Don Quixote,”

[001-127]whence, as has been already said, the authors of this veracious history

[001-128]have inferred that his name must have been beyond a doubt Quixada, and

[001-129]not Quesada as others would have it. Recollecting, however, that the

[001-130]valiant Amadis was not content to call himself curtly Amadis and

[001-131]nothing more, but added the name of his kingdom and country to make it

[001-132]famous, and called himself Amadis of Gaul, he, like a good knight,

[001-133]resolved to add on the name of his, and to style himself Don Quixote of

[001-134]La Mancha, whereby, he considered, he described accurately his origin

[001-135]and country, and did honour to it in taking his surname from it.

[001-136]So then, his armour being furbished, his morion turned into a helmet,

[001-137]his hack christened, and he himself confirmed, he came to the

[001-138]conclusion that nothing more was needed now but to look out for a lady

[001-139]to be in love with; for a knight-errant without love was like a tree

[001-140]without leaves or fruit, or a body without a soul. As he said to

[001-141]himself, “If, for my sins, or by my good fortune, I come across some

[001-142]giant hereabouts, a common occurrence with knights-errant, and

[001-143]overthrow him in one onslaught, or cleave him asunder to the waist, or,

[001-144]in short, vanquish and subdue him, will it not be well to have someone

[001-145]I may send him to as a present, that he may come in and fall on his

[001-146]knees before my sweet lady, and in a humble, submissive voice say, ‘I

[001-147]am the giant Caraculiambro, lord of the island of Malindrania,

[001-148]vanquished in single combat by the never sufficiently extolled knight

[001-149]Don Quixote of La Mancha, who has commanded me to present myself before

[001-150]your Grace, that your Highness dispose of me at your pleasure’?” Oh,

[001-151]how our good gentleman enjoyed the delivery of this speech, especially

[001-152]when he had thought of someone to call his Lady! There was, so the

[001-153]story goes, in a village near his own a very good-looking farm-girl

[001-154]with whom he had been at one time in love, though, so far as is known,

[001-155]she never knew it nor gave a thought to the matter. Her name was

[001-156]Aldonza Lorenzo, and upon her he thought fit to confer the title of

[001-157]Lady of his Thoughts; and after some search for a name which should not

[001-158]be out of harmony with her own, and should suggest and indicate that of

[001-159]a princess and great lady, he decided upon calling her Dulcinea del

[001-160]Toboso—she being of El Toboso—a name, to his mind, musical, uncommon,

[001-161]and significant, like all those he had already bestowed upon himself

[001-162]and the things belonging to him.
