OF THE INTERVIEW THE CURATE AND THE BARBER HAD WITH DON QUIXOTE ABOUT

[053-001]HIS MALADY

p01a.jpg (156K)


[053-002]Cide Hamete Benengeli, in the Second Part of this history, and third

[053-003]sally of Don Quixote, says that the curate and the barber remained

[053-004]nearly a month without seeing him, lest they should recall or bring

[053-005]back to his recollection what had taken place. They did not, however,

[053-006]omit to visit his niece and housekeeper, and charge them to be careful

[053-007]to treat him with attention, and give him comforting things to eat, and

[053-008]such as were good for the heart and the brain, whence, it was plain to

[053-009]see, all his misfortune proceeded. The niece and housekeeper replied

[053-010]that they did so, and meant to do so with all possible care and

[053-011]assiduity, for they could perceive that their master was now and then

[053-012]beginning to show signs of being in his right mind. This gave great

[053-013]satisfaction to the curate and the barber, for they concluded they had

[053-014]taken the right course in carrying him off enchanted on the ox-cart, as

[053-015]has been described in the First Part of this great as well as accurate

[053-016]history, in the last chapter thereof. So they resolved to pay him a

[053-017]visit and test the improvement in his condition, although they thought

[053-018]it almost impossible that there could be any; and they agreed not to

[053-019]touch upon any point connected with knight-errantry so as not to run

[053-020]the risk of reopening wounds which were still so tender.

[053-021]They came to see him consequently, and found him sitting up in bed in a

[053-022]green baize waistcoat and a red Toledo cap, and so withered and dried

[053-023]up that he looked as if he had been turned into a mummy. They were very

[053-024]cordially received by him; they asked him after his health, and he

[053-025]talked to them about himself very naturally and in very well-chosen

[053-026]language. In the course of their conversation they fell to discussing

[053-027]what they call State-craft and systems of government, correcting this

[053-028]abuse and condemning that, reforming one practice and abolishing

[053-029]another, each of the three setting up for a new legislator, a modern

[053-030]Lycurgus, or a brand-new Solon; and so completely did they remodel the

[053-031]State, that they seemed to have thrust it into a furnace and taken out

[053-032]something quite different from what they had put in; and on all the

[053-033]subjects they dealt with, Don Quixote spoke with such good sense that

[053-034]the pair of examiners were fully convinced that he was quite recovered

[053-035]and in his full senses.

[053-036]The niece and housekeeper were present at the conversation and could

[053-037]not find words enough to express their thanks to God at seeing their

[053-038]master so clear in his mind; the curate, however, changing his original

[053-039]plan, which was to avoid touching upon matters of chivalry, resolved to

[053-040]test Don Quixote’s recovery thoroughly, and see whether it were genuine

[053-041]or not; and so, from one subject to another, he came at last to talk of

[053-042]the news that had come from the capital, and, among other things, he

[053-043]said it was considered certain that the Turk was coming down with a

[053-044]powerful fleet, and that no one knew what his purpose was, or when the

[053-045]great storm would burst; and that all Christendom was in apprehension

[053-046]of this, which almost every year calls us to arms, and that his Majesty

[053-047]had made provision for the security of the coasts of Naples and Sicily

[053-048]and the island of Malta.

[053-049]To this Don Quixote replied, “His Majesty has acted like a prudent

[053-050]warrior in providing for the safety of his realms in time, so that the

[053-051]enemy may not find him unprepared; but if my advice were taken I would

[053-052]recommend him to adopt a measure which at present, no doubt, his

[053-053]Majesty is very far from thinking of.”

[053-054]The moment the curate heard this he said to himself, “God keep thee in

[053-055]his hand, poor Don Quixote, for it seems to me thou art precipitating

[053-056]thyself from the height of thy madness into the profound abyss of thy

[053-057]simplicity.”

[053-058]But the barber, who had the same suspicion as the curate, asked Don

[053-059]Quixote what would be his advice as to the measures that he said ought

[053-060]to be adopted; for perhaps it might prove to be one that would have to

[053-061]be added to the list of the many impertinent suggestions that people

[053-062]were in the habit of offering to princes.

[053-063]“Mine, master shaver,” said Don Quixote, “will not be impertinent, but,

[053-064]on the contrary, pertinent.”

[053-065]“I don’t mean that,” said the barber, “but that experience has shown

[053-066]that all or most of the expedients which are proposed to his Majesty

[053-067]are either impossible, or absurd, or injurious to the King and to the

[053-068]kingdom.”

[053-069]“Mine, however,” replied Don Quixote, “is neither impossible nor

[053-070]absurd, but the easiest, the most reasonable, the readiest and most

[053-071]expeditious that could suggest itself to any projector’s mind.”

[053-072]“You take a long time to tell it, Señor Don Quixote,” said the curate.

[053-073]“I don’t choose to tell it here, now,” said Don Quixote, “and have it

[053-074]reach the ears of the lords of the council to-morrow morning, and some

[053-075]other carry off the thanks and rewards of my trouble.”

[053-076]“For my part,” said the barber, “I give my word here and before God

[053-077]that I will not repeat what your worship says, to King, Rook or earthly

[053-078]man—an oath I learned from the ballad of the curate, who, in the

[053-079]prelude, told the king of the thief who had robbed him of the hundred

[053-080]gold crowns and his pacing mule.”

[053-081]“I am not versed in stories,” said Don Quixote; “but I know the oath is

[053-082]a good one, because I know the barber to be an honest fellow.”

[053-083]“Even if he were not,” said the curate, “I will go bail and answer for

[053-084]him that in this matter he will be as silent as a dummy, under pain of

[053-085]paying any penalty that may be pronounced.”

[053-086]“And who will be security for you, señor curate?” said Don Quixote.

[053-087]“My profession,” replied the curate, “which is to keep secrets.”

[053-088]“Ods body!” said Don Quixote at this, “what more has his Majesty to do

[053-089]but to command, by public proclamation, all the knights-errant that are

[053-090]scattered over Spain to assemble on a fixed day in the capital, for

[053-091]even if no more than half a dozen come, there may be one among them who

[053-092]alone will suffice to destroy the entire might of the Turk. Give me

[053-093]your attention and follow me. Is it, pray, any new thing for a single

[053-094]knight-errant to demolish an army of two hundred thousand men, as if

[053-095]they all had but one throat or were made of sugar paste? Nay, tell me,

[053-096]how many histories are there filled with these marvels? If only (in an

[053-097]evil hour for me: I don’t speak for anyone else) the famous Don

[053-098]Belianis were alive now, or anyone of the innumerable progeny of Amadis

[053-099]of Gaul! If any these were alive to-day, and were to come face to face

[053-100]with the Turk, by my faith, I would not give much for the Turk’s

[053-101]chance. But God will have regard for his people, and will provide

[053-102]someone, who, if not so valiant as the knights-errant of yore, at least

[053-103]will not be inferior to them in spirit; but God knows what I mean, and

[053-104]I say no more.”

[053-105]“Alas!” exclaimed the niece at this, “may I die if my master does not

[053-106]want to turn knight-errant again;” to which Don Quixote replied, “A

[053-107]knight-errant I shall die, and let the Turk come down or go up when he

[053-108]likes, and in as strong force as he can, once more I say, God knows

[053-109]what I mean.” But here the barber said, “I ask your worships to give me

[053-110]leave to tell a short story of something that happened in Seville,

[053-111]which comes so pat to the purpose just now that I should like greatly

[053-112]to tell it.” Don Quixote gave him leave, and the rest prepared to

[053-113]listen, and he began thus:

[053-114]“In the madhouse at Seville there was a man whom his relations had

[053-115]placed there as being out of his mind. He was a graduate of Osuna in

[053-116]canon law; but even if he had been of Salamanca, it was the opinion of

[053-117]most people that he would have been mad all the same. This graduate,

[053-118]after some years of confinement, took it into his head that he was sane

[053-119]and in his full senses, and under this impression wrote to the

[053-120]Archbishop, entreating him earnestly, and in very correct language, to

[053-121]have him released from the misery in which he was living; for by God’s

[053-122]mercy he had now recovered his lost reason, though his relations, in

[053-123]order to enjoy his property, kept him there, and, in spite of the

[053-124]truth, would make him out to be mad until his dying day. The

[053-125]Archbishop, moved by repeated sensible, well-written letters, directed

[053-126]one of his chaplains to make inquiry of the madhouse as to the truth of

[053-127]the licentiate’s statements, and to have an interview with the madman

[053-128]himself, and, if it should appear that he was in his senses, to take

[053-129]him out and restore him to liberty. The chaplain did so, and the

[053-130]governor assured him that the man was still mad, and that though he

[053-131]often spoke like a highly intelligent person, he would in the end break

[053-132]out into nonsense that in quantity and quality counterbalanced all the

[053-133]sensible things he had said before, as might be easily tested by

[053-134]talking to him. The chaplain resolved to try the experiment, and

[053-135]obtaining access to the madman conversed with him for an hour or more,

[053-136]during the whole of which time he never uttered a word that was

[053-137]incoherent or absurd, but, on the contrary, spoke so rationally that

[053-138]the chaplain was compelled to believe him to be sane. Among other

[053-139]things, he said the governor was against him, not to lose the presents

[053-140]his relations made him for reporting him still mad but with lucid

[053-141]intervals; and that the worst foe he had in his misfortune was his

[053-142]large property; for in order to enjoy it his enemies disparaged and

[053-143]threw doubts upon the mercy our Lord had shown him in turning him from

[053-144]a brute beast into a man. In short, he spoke in such a way that he cast

[053-145]suspicion on the governor, and made his relations appear covetous and

[053-146]heartless, and himself so rational that the chaplain determined to take

[053-147]him away with him that the Archbishop might see him, and ascertain for

[053-148]himself the truth of the matter. Yielding to this conviction, the

[053-149]worthy chaplain begged the governor to have the clothes in which the

[053-150]licentiate had entered the house given to him. The governor again bade

[053-151]him beware of what he was doing, as the licentiate was beyond a doubt

[053-152]still mad; but all his cautions and warnings were unavailing to

[053-153]dissuade the chaplain from taking him away. The governor, seeing that

[053-154]it was the order of the Archbishop, obeyed, and they dressed the

[053-155]licentiate in his own clothes, which were new and decent. He, as soon

[053-156]as he saw himself clothed like one in his senses, and divested of the

[053-157]appearance of a madman, entreated the chaplain to permit him in charity

[053-158]to go and take leave of his comrades the madmen. The chaplain said he

[053-159]would go with him to see what madmen there were in the house; so they

[053-160]went upstairs, and with them some of those who were present.

[053-161]Approaching a cage in which there was a furious madman, though just at

[053-162]that moment calm and quiet, the licentiate said to him, ‘Brother, think

[053-163]if you have any commands for me, for I am going home, as God has been

[053-164]pleased, in his infinite goodness and mercy, without any merit of mine,

[053-165]to restore me my reason. I am now cured and in my senses, for with

[053-166]God’s power nothing is impossible. Have strong hope and trust in him,

[053-167]for as he has restored me to my original condition, so likewise he will

[053-168]restore you if you trust in him. I will take care to send you some good

[053-169]things to eat; and be sure you eat them; for I would have you know I am

[053-170]convinced, as one who has gone through it, that all this madness of

[053-171]ours comes of having the stomach empty and the brains full of wind.

[053-172]Take courage! take courage! for despondency in misfortune breaks down

[053-173]health and brings on death.’

[053-174]“To all these words of the licentiate another madman in a cage opposite

[053-175]that of the furious one was listening; and raising himself up from an

[053-176]old mat on which he lay stark naked, he asked in a loud voice who it

[053-177]was that was going away cured and in his senses. The licentiate

[053-178]answered, ‘It is I, brother, who am going; I have now no need to remain

[053-179]here any longer, for which I return infinite thanks to Heaven that has

[053-180]had so great mercy upon me.’

[053-181]“‘Mind what you are saying, licentiate; don’t let the devil deceive

[053-182]you,’ replied the madman. ‘Keep quiet, stay where you are, and you will

[053-183]save yourself the trouble of coming back.’

[053-184]“‘I know I am cured,’ returned the licentiate, ‘and that I shall not

[053-185]have to go stations again.’

[053-186]“‘You cured!’ said the madman; ‘well, we shall see; God be with you;

[053-187]but I swear to you by Jupiter, whose majesty I represent on earth, that

[053-188]for this crime alone, which Seville is committing to-day in releasing

[053-189]you from this house, and treating you as if you were in your senses, I

[053-190]shall have to inflict such a punishment on it as will be remembered for

[053-191]ages and ages, amen. Dost thou not know, thou miserable little

[053-192]licentiate, that I can do it, being, as I say, Jupiter the Thunderer,

[053-193]who hold in my hands the fiery bolts with which I am able and am wont

[053-194]to threaten and lay waste the world? But in one way only will I punish

[053-195]this ignorant town, and that is by not raining upon it, nor on any part

[053-196]of its district or territory, for three whole years, to be reckoned

[053-197]from the day and moment when this threat is pronounced. Thou free, thou

[053-198]cured, thou in thy senses! and I mad, I disordered, I bound! I will as

[053-199]soon think of sending rain as of hanging myself.

[053-200]“Those present stood listening to the words and exclamations of the

[053-201]madman; but our licentiate, turning to the chaplain and seizing him by

[053-202]the hands, said to him, ‘Be not uneasy, señor; attach no importance to

[053-203]what this madman has said; for if he is Jupiter and will not send rain,

[053-204]I, who am Neptune, the father and god of the waters, will rain as often

[053-205]as it pleases me and may be needful.’

[053-206]“The governor and the bystanders laughed, and at their laughter the

[053-207]chaplain was half ashamed, and he replied, ‘For all that, Señor

[053-208]Neptune, it will not do to vex Señor Jupiter; remain where you are, and

[053-209]some other day, when there is a better opportunity and more time, we

[053-210]will come back for you.’ So they stripped the licentiate, and he was

[053-211]left where he was; and that’s the end of the story.”

[053-212]“So that’s the story, master barber,” said Don Quixote, “which came in

[053-213]so pat to the purpose that you could not help telling it? Master

[053-214]shaver, master shaver! how blind is he who cannot see through a sieve.

[053-215]Is it possible that you do not know that comparisons of wit with wit,

[053-216]valour with valour, beauty with beauty, birth with birth, are always

[053-217]odious and unwelcome? I, master barber, am not Neptune, the god of the

[053-218]waters, nor do I try to make anyone take me for an astute man, for I am

[053-219]not one. My only endeavour is to convince the world of the mistake it

[053-220]makes in not reviving in itself the happy time when the order of

[053-221]knight-errantry was in the field. But our depraved age does not deserve

[053-222]to enjoy such a blessing as those ages enjoyed when knights-errant took

[053-223]upon their shoulders the defence of kingdoms, the protection of

[053-224]damsels, the succour of orphans and minors, the chastisement of the

[053-225]proud, and the recompense of the humble. With the knights of these

[053-226]days, for the most part, it is the damask, brocade, and rich stuffs

[053-227]they wear, that rustle as they go, not the chain mail of their armour;

[053-228]no knight now-a-days sleeps in the open field exposed to the inclemency

[053-229]of heaven, and in full panoply from head to foot; no one now takes a

[053-230]nap, as they call it, without drawing his feet out of the stirrups, and

[053-231]leaning upon his lance, as the knights-errant used to do; no one now,

[053-232]issuing from the wood, penetrates yonder mountains, and then treads the

[053-233]barren, lonely shore of the sea—mostly a tempestuous and stormy one—and

[053-234]finding on the beach a little bark without oars, sail, mast, or

[053-235]tackling of any kind, in the intrepidity of his heart flings himself

[053-236]into it and commits himself to the wrathful billows of the deep sea,

[053-237]that one moment lift him up to heaven and the next plunge him into the

[053-238]depths; and opposing his breast to the irresistible gale, finds

[053-239]himself, when he least expects it, three thousand leagues and more away

[053-240]from the place where he embarked; and leaping ashore in a remote and

[053-241]unknown land has adventures that deserve to be written, not on

[053-242]parchment, but on brass. But now sloth triumphs over energy, indolence

[053-243]over exertion, vice over virtue, arrogance over courage, and theory

[053-244]over practice in arms, which flourished and shone only in the golden

[053-245]ages and in knights-errant. For tell me, who was more virtuous and more

[053-246]valiant than the famous Amadis of Gaul? Who more discreet than Palmerin

[053-247]of England? Who more gracious and easy than Tirante el Blanco? Who more

[053-248]courtly than Lisuarte of Greece? Who more slashed or slashing than Don

[053-249]Belianis? Who more intrepid than Perion of Gaul? Who more ready to face

[053-250]danger than Felixmarte of Hircania? Who more sincere than Esplandian?

[053-251]Who more impetuous than Don Cirongilio of Thrace? Who more bold than

[053-252]Rodamonte? Who more prudent than King Sobrino? Who more daring than

[053-253]Reinaldos? Who more invincible than Roland? and who more gallant and

[053-254]courteous than Ruggiero, from whom the dukes of Ferrara of the present

[053-255]day are descended, according to Turpin in his ‘Cosmography.’ All these

[053-256]knights, and many more that I could name, señor curate, were

[053-257]knights-errant, the light and glory of chivalry. These, or such as

[053-258]these, I would have to carry out my plan, and in that case his Majesty

[053-259]would find himself well served and would save great expense, and the

[053-260]Turk would be left tearing his beard. And so I will stay where I am, as

[053-261]the chaplain does not take me away; and if Jupiter, as the barber has

[053-262]told us, will not send rain, here am I, and I will rain when I please.

[053-263]I say this that Master Basin may know that I understand him.”

[053-264]“Indeed, Señor Don Quixote,” said the barber, “I did not mean it in

[053-265]that way, and, so help me God, my intention was good, and your worship

[053-266]ought not to be vexed.”

[053-267]“As to whether I ought to be vexed or not,” returned Don Quixote, “I

[053-268]myself am the best judge.”

[053-269]Hereupon the curate observed, “I have hardly said a word as yet; and I

[053-270]would gladly be relieved of a doubt, arising from what Don Quixote has

[053-271]said, that worries and works my conscience.”

[053-272]“The señor curate has leave for more than that,” returned Don Quixote,

[053-273]“so he may declare his doubt, for it is not pleasant to have a doubt on

[053-274]one’s conscience.”

[053-275]“Well then, with that permission,” said the curate, “I say my doubt is

[053-276]that, all I can do, I cannot persuade myself that the whole pack of

[053-277]knights-errant you, Señor Don Quixote, have mentioned, were really and

[053-278]truly persons of flesh and blood, that ever lived in the world; on the

[053-279]contrary, I suspect it to be all fiction, fable, and falsehood, and

[053-280]dreams told by men awakened from sleep, or rather still half asleep.”

[053-281]“That is another mistake,” replied Don Quixote, “into which many have

[053-282]fallen who do not believe that there ever were such knights in the

[053-283]world, and I have often, with divers people and on divers occasions,

[053-284]tried to expose this almost universal error to the light of truth.

[053-285]Sometimes I have not been successful in my purpose, sometimes I have,

[053-286]supporting it upon the shoulders of the truth; which truth is so clear

[053-287]that I can almost say I have with my own eyes seen Amadis of Gaul, who

[053-288]was a man of lofty stature, fair complexion, with a handsome though

[053-289]black beard, of a countenance between gentle and stern in expression,

[053-290]sparing of words, slow to anger, and quick to put it away from him; and

[053-291]as I have depicted Amadis, so I could, I think, portray and describe

[053-292]all the knights-errant that are in all the histories in the world; for

[053-293]by the perception I have that they were what their histories describe,

[053-294]and by the deeds they did and the dispositions they displayed, it is

[053-295]possible, with the aid of sound philosophy, to deduce their features,

[053-296]complexion, and stature.”

[053-297]“How big, in your worship’s opinion, may the giant Morgante have been,

[053-298]Señor Don Quixote?” asked the barber.

[053-299]“With regard to giants,” replied Don Quixote, “opinions differ as to

[053-300]whether there ever were any or not in the world; but the Holy

[053-301]Scripture, which cannot err by a jot from the truth, shows us that

[053-302]there were, when it gives us the history of that big Philistine,

[053-303]Goliath, who was seven cubits and a half in height, which is a huge

[053-304]size. Likewise, in the island of Sicily, there have been found

[053-305]leg-bones and arm-bones so large that their size makes it plain that

[053-306]their owners were giants, and as tall as great towers; geometry puts

[053-307]this fact beyond a doubt. But, for all that, I cannot speak with

[053-308]certainty as to the size of Morgante, though I suspect he cannot have

[053-309]been very tall; and I am inclined to be of this opinion because I find

[053-310]in the history in which his deeds are particularly mentioned, that he

[053-311]frequently slept under a roof and as he found houses to contain him, it

[053-312]is clear that his bulk could not have been anything excessive.”

[053-313]“That is true,” said the curate, and yielding to the enjoyment of

[053-314]hearing such nonsense, he asked him what was his notion of the features

[053-315]of Reinaldos of Montalban, and Don Roland and the rest of the Twelve

[053-316]Peers of France, for they were all knights-errant.

[053-317]“As for Reinaldos,” replied Don Quixote, “I venture to say that he was

[053-318]broad-faced, of ruddy complexion, with roguish and somewhat prominent

[053-319]eyes, excessively punctilious and touchy, and given to the society of

[053-320]thieves and scapegraces. With regard to Roland, or Rotolando, or

[053-321]Orlando (for the histories call him by all these names), I am of

[053-322]opinion, and hold, that he was of middle height, broad-shouldered,

[053-323]rather bow-legged, swarthy-complexioned, red-bearded, with a hairy body

[053-324]and a severe expression of countenance, a man of few words, but very

[053-325]polite and well-bred.”

[053-326]“If Roland was not a more graceful person than your worship has

[053-327]described,” said the curate, “it is no wonder that the fair Lady

[053-328]Angelica rejected him and left him for the gaiety, liveliness, and

[053-329]grace of that budding-bearded little Moor to whom she surrendered

[053-330]herself; and she showed her sense in falling in love with the gentle

[053-331]softness of Medoro rather than the roughness of Roland.”

[053-332]“That Angelica, señor curate,” returned Don Quixote, “was a giddy

[053-333]damsel, flighty and somewhat wanton, and she left the world as full of

[053-334]her vagaries as of the fame of her beauty. She treated with scorn a

[053-335]thousand gentlemen, men of valour and wisdom, and took up with a

[053-336]smooth-faced sprig of a page, without fortune or fame, except such

[053-337]reputation for gratitude as the affection he bore his friend got for

[053-338]him. The great poet who sang her beauty, the famous Ariosto, not caring

[053-339]to sing her adventures after her contemptible surrender (which probably

[053-340]were not over and above creditable), dropped her where he says:

[053-341]How she received the sceptre of Cathay,

[053-342]Some bard of defter quill may sing some day;

[053-343]and this was no doubt a kind of prophecy, for poets are also called

[053-344]_vates_, that is to say diviners; and its truth was made plain; for

[053-345]since then a famous Andalusian poet has lamented and sung her tears,

[053-346]and another famous and rare poet, a Castilian, has sung her beauty.”

[053-347]“Tell me, Señor Don Quixote,” said the barber here, “among all those

[053-348]who praised her, has there been no poet to write a satire on this Lady

[053-349]Angelica?”

[053-350]“I can well believe,” replied Don Quixote, “that if Sacripante or
