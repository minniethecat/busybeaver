[044-001]IN WHICH ARE CONTINUED THE UNHEARD-OF ADVENTURES OF THE INN

c44a.jpg (144K)


[044-002]So loud, in fact, were the shouts of Don Quixote, that the landlord

[044-003]opening the gate of the inn in all haste, came out in dismay, and ran

[044-004]to see who was uttering such cries, and those who were outside joined

[044-005]him. Maritornes, who had been by this time roused up by the same

[044-006]outcry, suspecting what it was, ran to the loft and, without anyone

[044-007]seeing her, untied the halter by which Don Quixote was suspended, and

[044-008]down he came to the ground in the sight of the landlord and the

[044-009]travellers, who approaching asked him what was the matter with him that

[044-010]he shouted so. He without replying a word took the rope off his wrist,

[044-011]and rising to his feet leaped upon Rocinante, braced his buckler on his

[044-012]arm, put his lance in rest, and making a considerable circuit of the

[044-013]plain came back at a half-gallop exclaiming:

[044-014]“Whoever shall say that I have been enchanted with just cause, provided

[044-015]my lady the Princess Micomicona grants me permission to do so, I give

[044-016]him the lie, challenge him and defy him to single combat.”

[044-017]The newly arrived travellers were amazed at the words of Don Quixote;

[044-018]but the landlord removed their surprise by telling them who he was, and

[044-019]not to mind him as he was out of his senses. They then asked the

[044-020]landlord if by any chance a youth of about fifteen years of age had

[044-021]come to that inn, one dressed like a muleteer, and of such and such an

[044-022]appearance, describing that of Doña Clara’s lover. The landlord replied

[044-023]that there were so many people in the inn he had not noticed the person

[044-024]they were inquiring for; but one of them observing the coach in which

[044-025]the Judge had come, said, “He is here no doubt, for this is the coach

[044-026]he is following: let one of us stay at the gate, and the rest go in to

[044-027]look for him; or indeed it would be as well if one of us went round the

[044-028]inn, lest he should escape over the wall of the yard.” “So be it,” said

[044-029]another; and while two of them went in, one remained at the gate and

[044-030]the other made the circuit of the inn; observing all which, the

[044-031]landlord was unable to conjecture for what reason they were taking all

[044-032]these precautions, though he understood they were looking for the youth

[044-033]whose description they had given him.

[044-034]It was by this time broad daylight; and for that reason, as well as in

[044-035]consequence of the noise Don Quixote had made, everybody was awake and

[044-036]up, but particularly Doña Clara and Dorothea; for they had been able to

[044-037]sleep but badly that night, the one from agitation at having her lover

[044-038]so near her, the other from curiosity to see him. Don Quixote, when he

[044-039]saw that not one of the four travellers took any notice of him or

[044-040]replied to his challenge, was furious and ready to die with indignation

[044-041]and wrath; and if he could have found in the ordinances of chivalry

[044-042]that it was lawful for a knight-errant to undertake or engage in

[044-043]another enterprise, when he had plighted his word and faith not to

[044-044]involve himself in any until he had made an end of the one to which he

[044-045]was pledged, he would have attacked the whole of them, and would have

[044-046]made them return an answer in spite of themselves. But considering that

[044-047]it would not become him, nor be right, to begin any new emprise until

[044-048]he had established Micomicona in her kingdom, he was constrained to

[044-049]hold his peace and wait quietly to see what would be the upshot of the

[044-050]proceedings of those same travellers; one of whom found the youth they

[044-051]were seeking lying asleep by the side of a muleteer, without a thought

[044-052]of anyone coming in search of him, much less finding him.

[044-053]The man laid hold of him by the arm, saying, “It becomes you well

[044-054]indeed, Señor Don Luis, to be in the dress you wear, and well the bed

[044-055]in which I find you agrees with the luxury in which your mother reared

[044-056]you.”

[044-057]The youth rubbed his sleepy eyes and stared for a while at him who held

[044-058]him, but presently recognised him as one of his father’s servants, at

[044-059]which he was so taken aback that for some time he could not find or

[044-060]utter a word; while the servant went on to say, “There is nothing for

[044-061]it now, Señor Don Luis, but to submit quietly and return home, unless

[044-062]it is your wish that my lord, your father, should take his departure

[044-063]for the other world, for nothing else can be the consequence of the

[044-064]grief he is in at your absence.”

[044-065]“But how did my father know that I had gone this road and in this

[044-066]dress?” said Don Luis.

[044-067]“It was a student to whom you confided your intentions,” answered the

[044-068]servant, “that disclosed them, touched with pity at the distress he saw

[044-069]your father suffer on missing you; he therefore despatched four of his

[044-070]servants in quest of you, and here we all are at your service, better

[044-071]pleased than you can imagine that we shall return so soon and be able

[044-072]to restore you to those eyes that so yearn for you.”

[044-073]“That shall be as I please, or as heaven orders,” returned Don Luis.

[044-074]“What can you please or heaven order,” said the other, “except to agree

[044-075]to go back? Anything else is impossible.”

[044-076]All this conversation between the two was overheard by the muleteer at

[044-077]whose side Don Luis lay, and rising, he went to report what had taken

[044-078]place to Don Fernando, Cardenio, and the others, who had by this time

[044-079]dressed themselves; and told them how the man had addressed the youth

[044-080]as “Don,” and what words had passed, and how he wanted him to return to

[044-081]his father, which the youth was unwilling to do. With this, and what

[044-082]they already knew of the rare voice that heaven had bestowed upon him,

[044-083]they all felt very anxious to know more particularly who he was, and

[044-084]even to help him if it was attempted to employ force against him; so

[044-085]they hastened to where he was still talking and arguing with his

[044-086]servant. Dorothea at this instant came out of her room, followed by

[044-087]Doña Clara all in a tremor; and calling Cardenio aside, she told him in

[044-088]a few words the story of the musician and Doña Clara, and he at the

[044-089]same time told her what had happened, how his father’s servants had

[044-090]come in search of him; but in telling her so, he did not speak low

[044-091]enough but that Doña Clara heard what he said, at which she was so much

[044-092]agitated that had not Dorothea hastened to support her she would have

[044-093]fallen to the ground. Cardenio then bade Dorothea return to her room,

[044-094]as he would endeavour to make the whole matter right, and they did as

[044-095]he desired. All the four who had come in quest of Don Luis had now come

[044-096]into the inn and surrounded him, urging him to return and console his

[044-097]father at once and without a moment’s delay. He replied that he could

[044-098]not do so on any account until he had concluded some business in which

[044-099]his life, honour, and heart were at stake. The servants pressed him,

[044-100]saying that most certainly they would not return without him, and that

[044-101]they would take him away whether he liked it or not.

[044-102]“You shall not do that,” replied Don Luis, “unless you take me dead;

[044-103]though however you take me, it will be without life.”

[044-104]By this time most of those in the inn had been attracted by the

[044-105]dispute, but particularly Cardenio, Don Fernando, his companions, the

[044-106]Judge, the curate, the barber, and Don Quixote; for he now considered

[044-107]there was no necessity for mounting guard over the castle any longer.

[044-108]Cardenio being already acquainted with the young man’s story, asked the

[044-109]men who wanted to take him away, what object they had in seeking to

[044-110]carry off this youth against his will.

[044-111]“Our object,” said one of the four, “is to save the life of his father,

[044-112]who is in danger of losing it through this gentleman’s disappearance.”

[044-113]Upon this Don Luis exclaimed, “There is no need to make my affairs

[044-114]public here; I am free, and I will return if I please; and if not, none

[044-115]of you shall compel me.”

[044-116]“Reason will compel your worship,” said the man, “and if it has no

[044-117]power over you, it has power over us, to make us do what we came for,

[044-118]and what it is our duty to do.”

[044-119]“Let us hear what the whole affair is about,” said the Judge at this;

[044-120]but the man, who knew him as a neighbour of theirs, replied, “Do you

[044-121]not know this gentleman, Señor Judge? He is the son of your neighbour,

[044-122]who has run away from his father’s house in a dress so unbecoming his

[044-123]rank, as your worship may perceive.”

[044-124]The judge on this looked at him more carefully and recognised him, and

[044-125]embracing him said, “What folly is this, Señor Don Luis, or what can

[044-126]have been the cause that could have induced you to come here in this

[044-127]way, and in this dress, which so ill becomes your condition?”

[044-128]Tears came into the eyes of the young man, and he was unable to utter a

[044-129]word in reply to the Judge, who told the four servants not to be

[044-130]uneasy, for all would be satisfactorily settled; and then taking Don

[044-131]Luis by the hand, he drew him aside and asked the reason of his having

[044-132]come there.

[044-133]But while he was questioning him they heard a loud outcry at the gate

[044-134]of the inn, the cause of which was that two of the guests who had

[044-135]passed the night there, seeing everybody busy about finding out what it

[044-136]was the four men wanted, had conceived the idea of going off without

[044-137]paying what they owed; but the landlord, who minded his own affairs

[044-138]more than other people’s, caught them going out of the gate and

[044-139]demanded his reckoning, abusing them for their dishonesty with such

[044-140]language that he drove them to reply with their fists, and so they

[044-141]began to lay on him in such a style that the poor man was forced to cry

[044-142]out, and call for help. The landlady and her daughter could see no one

[044-143]more free to give aid than Don Quixote, and to him the daughter said,

[044-144]“Sir knight, by the virtue God has given you, help my poor father, for

[044-145]two wicked men are beating him to a mummy.”

[044-146]To which Don Quixote very deliberately and phlegmatically replied,

[044-147]“Fair damsel, at the present moment your request is inopportune, for I

[044-148]am debarred from involving myself in any adventure until I have brought

[044-149]to a happy conclusion one to which my word has pledged me; but that

[044-150]which I can do for you is what I will now mention: run and tell your

[044-151]father to stand his ground as well as he can in this battle, and on no

[044-152]account to allow himself to be vanquished, while I go and request

[044-153]permission of the Princess Micomicona to enable me to succour him in

[044-154]his distress; and if she grants it, rest assured I will relieve him

[044-155]from it.”

[044-156]“Sinner that I am,” exclaimed Maritornes, who stood by; “before you

[044-157]have got your permission my master will be in the other world.”

[044-158]“Give me leave, señora, to obtain the permission I speak of,” returned

[044-159]Don Quixote; “and if I get it, it will matter very little if he is in

[044-160]the other world; for I will rescue him thence in spite of all the same

[044-161]world can do; or at any rate I will give you such a revenge over those

[044-162]who shall have sent him there that you will be more than moderately

[044-163]satisfied;” and without saying anything more he went and knelt before

[044-164]Dorothea, requesting her Highness in knightly and errant phrase to be

[044-165]pleased to grant him permission to aid and succour the castellan of

[044-166]that castle, who now stood in grievous jeopardy. The princess granted

[044-167]it graciously, and he at once, bracing his buckler on his arm and

[044-168]drawing his sword, hastened to the inn-gate, where the two guests were

[044-169]still handling the landlord roughly; but as soon as he reached the spot

[044-170]he stopped short and stood still, though Maritornes and the landlady

[044-171]asked him why he hesitated to help their master and husband.

[044-172]“I hesitate,” said Don Quixote, “because it is not lawful for me to

[044-173]draw sword against persons of squirely condition; but call my squire

[044-174]Sancho to me; for this defence and vengeance are his affair and

[044-175]business.”

[044-176]Thus matters stood at the inn-gate, where there was a very lively

[044-177]exchange of fisticuffs and punches, to the sore damage of the landlord

[044-178]and to the wrath of Maritornes, the landlady, and her daughter, who

[044-179]were furious when they saw the pusillanimity of Don Quixote, and the

[044-180]hard treatment their master, husband and father was undergoing. But let

[044-181]us leave him there; for he will surely find someone to help him, and if

[044-182]not, let him suffer and hold his tongue who attempts more than his

[044-183]strength allows him to do; and let us go back fifty paces to see what

[044-184]Don Luis said in reply to the Judge whom we left questioning him

[044-185]privately as to his reasons for coming on foot and so meanly dressed.

[044-186]To which the youth, pressing his hand in a way that showed his heart

[044-187]was troubled by some great sorrow, and shedding a flood of tears, made

[044-188]answer:

[044-189]“Señor, I have no more to tell you than that from the moment when,

[044-190]through heaven’s will and our being near neighbours, I first saw Doña

[044-191]Clara, your daughter and my lady, from that instant I made her the

[044-192]mistress of my will, and if yours, my true lord and father, offers no

[044-193]impediment, this very day she shall become my wife. For her I left my

[044-194]father’s house, and for her I assumed this disguise, to follow her

[044-195]whithersoever she may go, as the arrow seeks its mark or the sailor the

[044-196]pole-star. She knows nothing more of my passion than what she may have

[044-197]learned from having sometimes seen from a distance that my eyes were

[044-198]filled with tears. You know already, señor, the wealth and noble birth

[044-199]of my parents, and that I am their sole heir; if this be a sufficient

[044-200]inducement for you to venture to make me completely happy, accept me at

[044-201]once as your son; for if my father, influenced by other objects of his

[044-202]own, should disapprove of this happiness I have sought for myself, time

[044-203]has more power to alter and change things, than human will.”

[044-204]With this the love-smitten youth was silent, while the Judge, after

[044-205]hearing him, was astonished, perplexed, and surprised, as well at the

[044-206]manner and intelligence with which Don Luis had confessed the secret of

[044-207]his heart, as at the position in which he found himself, not knowing

[044-208]what course to take in a matter so sudden and unexpected. All the

[044-209]answer, therefore, he gave him was to bid him to make his mind easy for

[044-210]the present, and arrange with his servants not to take him back that

[044-211]day, so that there might be time to consider what was best for all

[044-212]parties. Don Luis kissed his hands by force, nay, bathed them with his

[044-213]tears, in a way that would have touched a heart of marble, not to say

[044-214]that of the Judge, who, as a shrewd man, had already perceived how

[044-215]advantageous the marriage would be to his daughter; though, were it

[044-216]possible, he would have preferred that it should be brought about with

[044-217]the consent of the father of Don Luis, who he knew looked for a title

[044-218]for his son.

[044-219]The guests had by this time made peace with the landlord, for, by

[044-220]persuasion and Don Quixote’s fair words more than by threats, they had

[044-221]paid him what he demanded, and the servants of Don Luis were waiting

[044-222]for the end of the conversation with the Judge and their master’s

[044-223]decision, when the devil, who never sleeps, contrived that the barber,

[044-224]from whom Don Quixote had taken Mambrino’s helmet, and Sancho Panza the

[044-225]trappings of his ass in exchange for those of his own, should at this

[044-226]instant enter the inn; which said barber, as he led his ass to the

[044-227]stable, observed Sancho Panza engaged in repairing something or other

[044-228]belonging to the pack-saddle; and the moment he saw it he knew it, and

[044-229]made bold to attack Sancho, exclaiming, “Ho, sir thief, I have caught

[044-230]you! hand over my basin and my pack-saddle, and all my trappings that

[044-231]you robbed me of.”

[044-232]Sancho, finding himself so unexpectedly assailed, and hearing the abuse

[044-233]poured upon him, seized the pack-saddle with one hand, and with the

[044-234]other gave the barber a cuff that bathed his teeth in blood. The

[044-235]barber, however, was not so ready to relinquish the prize he had made

[044-236]in the pack-saddle; on the contrary, he raised such an outcry that

[044-237]everyone in the inn came running to know what the noise and quarrel

[044-238]meant. “Here, in the name of the king and justice!” he cried, “this

[044-239]thief and highwayman wants to kill me for trying to recover my

[044-240]property.”

[044-241]“You lie,” said Sancho, “I am no highwayman; it was in fair war my

[044-242]master Don Quixote won these spoils.”

[044-243]Don Quixote was standing by at the time, highly pleased to see his

[044-244]squire’s stoutness, both offensive and defensive, and from that time

[044-245]forth he reckoned him a man of mettle, and in his heart resolved to dub

[044-246]him a knight on the first opportunity that presented itself, feeling

[044-247]sure that the order of chivalry would be fittingly bestowed upon him.

[044-248]In the course of the altercation, among other things the barber said,

[044-249]“Gentlemen, this pack-saddle is mine as surely as I owe God a death,

[044-250]and I know it as well as if I had given birth to it, and here is my ass

[044-251]in the stable who will not let me lie; only try it, and if it does not

[044-252]fit him like a glove, call me a rascal; and what is more, the same day

[044-253]I was robbed of this, they robbed me likewise of a new brass basin,

[044-254]never yet handselled, that would fetch a crown any day.”

[044-255]At this Don Quixote could not keep himself from answering; and

[044-256]interposing between the two, and separating them, he placed the

[044-257]pack-saddle on the ground, to lie there in sight until the truth was

[044-258]established, and said, “Your worships may perceive clearly and plainly

[044-259]the error under which this worthy squire lies when he calls a basin

[044-260]which was, is, and shall be the helmet of Mambrino which I won from him

[044-261]in fair war, and made myself master of by legitimate and lawful

[044-262]possession. With the pack-saddle I do not concern myself; but I may

[044-263]tell you on that head that my squire Sancho asked my permission to

[044-264]strip off the caparison of this vanquished poltroon’s steed, and with

[044-265]it adorn his own; I allowed him, and he took it; and as to its having

[044-266]been changed from a caparison into a pack-saddle, I can give no

[044-267]explanation except the usual one, that such transformations will take

[044-268]place in adventures of chivalry. To confirm all which, run, Sancho my

[044-269]son, and fetch hither the helmet which this good fellow calls a basin.”

[044-270]“Egad, master,” said Sancho, “if we have no other proof of our case

[044-271]than what your worship puts forward, Mambrino’s helmet is just as much

[044-272]a basin as this good fellow’s caparison is a pack-saddle.”

[044-273]“Do as I bid thee,” said Don Quixote; “it cannot be that everything in

[044-274]this castle goes by enchantment.”

[044-275]Sancho hastened to where the basin was, and brought it back with him,

[044-276]and when Don Quixote saw it, he took hold of it and said:

[044-277]“Your worships may see with what a face this squire can assert that

[044-278]this is a basin and not the helmet I told you of; and I swear by the

[044-279]order of chivalry I profess, that this helmet is the identical one I

[044-280]took from him, without anything added to or taken from it.”

[044-281]“There is no doubt of that,” said Sancho, “for from the time my master

[044-282]won it until now he has only fought one battle in it, when he let loose

[044-283]those unlucky men in chains; and if it had not been for this

[044-284]basin-helmet he would not have come off over well that time, for there

[044-285]was plenty of stone-throwing in that affair.”
