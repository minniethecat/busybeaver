[034-002]“It is commonly said that an army looks ill without its general and a

[034-003]castle without its castellan, and I say that a young married woman

[034-004]looks still worse without her husband unless there are very good

[034-005]reasons for it. I find myself so ill at ease without you, and so

[034-006]incapable of enduring this separation, that unless you return quickly I

[034-007]shall have to go for relief to my parents’ house, even if I leave yours

[034-008]without a protector; for the one you left me, if indeed he deserved

[034-009]that title, has, I think, more regard to his own pleasure than to what

[034-010]concerns you: as you are possessed of discernment I need say no more to

[034-011]you, nor indeed is it fitting I should say more.”

[034-012]Anselmo received this letter, and from it he gathered that Lothario had

[034-013]already begun his task and that Camilla must have replied to him as he

[034-014]would have wished; and delighted beyond measure at such intelligence he

[034-015]sent word to her not to leave his house on any account, as he would

[034-016]very shortly return. Camilla was astonished at Anselmo’s reply, which

[034-017]placed her in greater perplexity than before, for she neither dared to

[034-018]remain in her own house, nor yet to go to her parents’; for in

[034-019]remaining her virtue was imperilled, and in going she was opposing her

[034-020]husband’s commands. Finally she decided upon what was the worse course

[034-021]for her, to remain, resolving not to fly from the presence of Lothario,

[034-022]that she might not give food for gossip to her servants; and she now

[034-023]began to regret having written as she had to her husband, fearing he

[034-024]might imagine that Lothario had perceived in her some lightness which

[034-025]had impelled him to lay aside the respect he owed her; but confident of

[034-026]her rectitude she put her trust in God and in her own virtuous

[034-027]intentions, with which she hoped to resist in silence all the

[034-028]solicitations of Lothario, without saying anything to her husband so as

[034-029]not to involve him in any quarrel or trouble; and she even began to

[034-030]consider how to excuse Lothario to Anselmo when he should ask her what

[034-031]it was that induced her to write that letter. With these resolutions,

[034-032]more honourable than judicious or effectual, she remained the next day

[034-033]listening to Lothario, who pressed his suit so strenuously that

[034-034]Camilla’s firmness began to waver, and her virtue had enough to do to

[034-035]come to the rescue of her eyes and keep them from showing signs of a

[034-036]certain tender compassion which the tears and appeals of Lothario had

[034-037]awakened in her bosom. Lothario observed all this, and it inflamed him

[034-038]all the more. In short he felt that while Anselmo’s absence afforded

[034-039]time and opportunity he must press the siege of the fortress, and so he

[034-040]assailed her self-esteem with praises of her beauty, for there is

[034-041]nothing that more quickly reduces and levels the castle towers of fair

[034-042]women’s vanity than vanity itself upon the tongue of flattery. In fact

[034-043]with the utmost assiduity he undermined the rock of her purity with

[034-044]such engines that had Camilla been of brass she must have fallen. He

[034-045]wept, he entreated, he promised, he flattered, he importuned, he

[034-046]pretended with so much feeling and apparent sincerity, that he

[034-047]overthrew the virtuous resolves of Camilla and won the triumph he least

[034-048]expected and most longed for. Camilla yielded, Camilla fell; but what

[034-049]wonder if the friendship of Lothario could not stand firm? A clear

[034-050]proof to us that the passion of love is to be conquered only by flying

[034-051]from it, and that no one should engage in a struggle with an enemy so

[034-052]mighty; for divine strength is needed to overcome his human power.

[034-053]Leonela alone knew of her mistress’s weakness, for the two false

[034-054]friends and new lovers were unable to conceal it. Lothario did not care

[034-055]to tell Camilla the object Anselmo had in view, nor that he had

[034-056]afforded him the opportunity of attaining such a result, lest she

[034-057]should undervalue his love and think that it was by chance and without

[034-058]intending it and not of his own accord that he had made love to her.

[034-059]A few days later Anselmo returned to his house and did not perceive

[034-060]what it had lost, that which he so lightly treated and so highly

[034-061]prized. He went at once to see Lothario, and found him at home; they

[034-062]embraced each other, and Anselmo asked for the tidings of his life or

[034-063]his death.

[034-064]“The tidings I have to give thee, Anselmo my friend,” said Lothario,

[034-065]“are that thou dost possess a wife that is worthy to be the pattern and

[034-066]crown of all good wives. The words that I have addressed to her were

[034-067]borne away on the wind, my promises have been despised, my presents

[034-068]have been refused, such feigned tears as I shed have been turned into

[034-069]open ridicule. In short, as Camilla is the essence of all beauty, so is

[034-070]she the treasure-house where purity dwells, and gentleness and modesty

[034-073]I have had no need to touch it, for the chastity of Camilla yields not

[034-074]to things so base as gifts or promises. Be content, Anselmo, and

[034-075]refrain from making further proof; and as thou hast passed dryshod

[034-078]new embarrassments, or with another pilot make trial of the goodness

[034-093]unwilling to take the trouble of writing the verses he would compose

[034-094]them himself.

[034-095]“That will not be necessary,” said Lothario, “for the muses are not

[034-096]such enemies of mine but that they visit me now and then in the course

[034-126]At midnight, in the silence, when the eyes

[034-127]Of happier mortals balmy slumbers close,

[034-128]The weary tale of my unnumbered woes

[034-129]To Chloris and to Heaven is wont to rise.

[034-133]In broken accents and in burning sighs.

[034-135]And on the earth pours down his midday beams,

[034-137]And with the night again goes up my moan.

[034-142]sincerity so manifest. On which Camilla said, “Then all that

[034-143]love-smitten poets say is true?”

[034-153]one, or, more correctly speaking, less bad; but you can easily judge,

[034-155]I know that I am doomed; death is to me

[034-156]As certain as that thou, ungrateful fair,

[034-157]Dead at thy feet shouldst see me lying, ere

[034-160]Bereft of life, fame, favour, even there

[034-162]Deep graven in my breast for all to see.

[034-164]To save me from the fate my truth entails,

[034-165]Truth that to thy hard heart its vigour owes.

[034-166]Alas for him that under lowering skies,

[034-167]In peril o’er a trackless ocean sails,
