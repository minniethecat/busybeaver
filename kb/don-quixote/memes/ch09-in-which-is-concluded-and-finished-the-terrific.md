IN WHICH IS CONCLUDED AND FINISHED THE TERRIFIC BATTLE BETWEEN THE

GALLANT BISCAYAN AND THE VALIANT MANCHEGAN

c09a.jpg (142K)


[009-001]In the First Part of this history we left the valiant Biscayan and the

[009-002]renowned Don Quixote with drawn swords uplifted, ready to deliver two

[009-003]such furious slashing blows that if they had fallen full and fair they

[009-004]would at least have split and cleft them asunder from top to toe and

[009-005]laid them open like a pomegranate; and at this so critical point the

[009-006]delightful history came to a stop and stood cut short without any

[009-007]intimation from the author where what was missing was to be found.

[009-008]This distressed me greatly, because the pleasure derived from having

[009-009]read such a small portion turned to vexation at the thought of the poor

[009-010]chance that presented itself of finding the large part that, so it

[009-011]seemed to me, was missing of such an interesting tale. It appeared to

[009-012]me to be a thing impossible and contrary to all precedent that so good

[009-013]a knight should have been without some sage to undertake the task of

[009-014]writing his marvellous achievements; a thing that was never wanting to

[009-015]any of those knights-errant who, they say, went after adventures; for

[009-016]every one of them had one or two sages as if made on purpose, who not

[009-017]only recorded their deeds but described their most trifling thoughts

[009-018]and follies, however secret they might be; and such a good knight could

[009-019]not have been so unfortunate as not to have what Platir and others like

[009-020]him had in abundance. And so I could not bring myself to believe that

[009-021]such a gallant tale had been left maimed and mutilated, and I laid the

[009-022]blame on Time, the devourer and destroyer of all things, that had

[009-023]either concealed or consumed it.

[009-024]On the other hand, it struck me that, inasmuch as among his books there

[009-025]had been found such modern ones as “The Enlightenment of Jealousy” and

[009-026]the “Nymphs and Shepherds of Henares,” his story must likewise be

[009-027]modern, and that though it might not be written, it might exist in the

[009-028]memory of the people of his village and of those in the neighbourhood.

[009-029]This reflection kept me perplexed and longing to know really and truly

[009-030]the whole life and wondrous deeds of our famous Spaniard, Don Quixote

[009-031]of La Mancha, light and mirror of Manchegan chivalry, and the first

[009-032]that in our age and in these so evil days devoted himself to the labour

[009-033]and exercise of the arms of knight-errantry, righting wrongs,

[009-034]succouring widows, and protecting damsels of that sort that used to

[009-035]ride about, whip in hand, on their palfreys, with all their virginity

[009-036]about them, from mountain to mountain and valley to valley—for, if it

[009-037]were not for some ruffian, or boor with a hood and hatchet, or

[009-038]monstrous giant, that forced them, there were in days of yore damsels

[009-039]that at the end of eighty years, in all which time they had never slept

[009-040]a day under a roof, went to their graves as much maids as the mothers

[009-041]that bore them. I say, then, that in these and other respects our

[009-042]gallant Don Quixote is worthy of everlasting and notable praise, nor

[009-043]should it be withheld even from me for the labour and pains spent in

[009-044]searching for the conclusion of this delightful history; though I know

[009-045]well that if Heaven, chance and good fortune had not helped me, the

[009-046]world would have remained deprived of an entertainment and pleasure

[009-047]that for a couple of hours or so may well occupy him who shall read it

[009-048]attentively. The discovery of it occurred in this way.

[009-049]One day, as I was in the Alcana of Toledo, a boy came up to sell some

[009-050]pamphlets and old papers to a silk mercer, and, as I am fond of reading

[009-051]even the very scraps of paper in the streets, led by this natural bent

[009-052]of mine I took up one of the pamphlets the boy had for sale, and saw

[009-053]that it was in characters which I recognised as Arabic, and as I was

[009-054]unable to read them though I could recognise them, I looked about to

[009-055]see if there were any Spanish-speaking Morisco at hand to read them for

[009-056]me; nor was there any great difficulty in finding such an interpreter,

[009-057]for even had I sought one for an older and better language I should

[009-058]have found him. In short, chance provided me with one, who when I told

[009-059]him what I wanted and put the book into his hands, opened it in the

[009-060]middle and after reading a little in it began to laugh. I asked him

[009-061]what he was laughing at, and he replied that it was at something the

[009-062]book had written in the margin by way of a note. I bade him tell it to

[009-063]me; and he still laughing said, “In the margin, as I told you, this is

[009-064]written: ‘_This Dulcinea del Toboso so often mentioned in this history,

[009-065]had, they say, the best hand of any woman in all La Mancha for salting

[009-066]pigs_.’”

[009-067]When I heard Dulcinea del Toboso named, I was struck with surprise and

[009-068]amazement, for it occurred to me at once that these pamphlets contained

[009-069]the history of Don Quixote. With this idea I pressed him to read the

[009-070]beginning, and doing so, turning the Arabic offhand into Castilian, he

[009-071]told me it meant, “_History of Don Quixote of La Mancha, written by Cid

[009-072]Hamete Benengeli, an Arab historian_.” It required great caution to

[009-073]hide the joy I felt when the title of the book reached my ears, and

[009-074]snatching it from the silk mercer, I bought all the papers and

[009-075]pamphlets from the boy for half a real; and if he had had his wits

[009-076]about him and had known how eager I was for them, he might have safely

[009-077]calculated on making more than six reals by the bargain. I withdrew at

[009-078]once with the Morisco into the cloister of the cathedral, and begged

[009-079]him to turn all these pamphlets that related to Don Quixote into the

[009-080]Castilian tongue, without omitting or adding anything to them, offering

[009-081]him whatever payment he pleased. He was satisfied with two arrobas of

[009-082]raisins and two bushels of wheat, and promised to translate them

[009-083]faithfully and with all despatch; but to make the matter easier, and

[009-084]not to let such a precious find out of my hands, I took him to my

[009-085]house, where in little more than a month and a half he translated the

[009-086]whole just as it is set down here.

[009-087]In the first pamphlet the battle between Don Quixote and the Biscayan

[009-088]was drawn to the very life, they planted in the same attitude as the

[009-089]history describes, their swords raised, and the one protected by his

[009-090]buckler, the other by his cushion, and the Biscayan’s mule so true to

[009-091]nature that it could be seen to be a hired one a bowshot off. The

[009-092]Biscayan had an inscription under his feet which said, “_Don Sancho de

[009-093]Azpeitia_,” which no doubt must have been his name; and at the feet of

[009-094]Rocinante was another that said, “_Don Quixote_.” Rocinante was

[009-095]marvellously portrayed, so long and thin, so lank and lean, with so

[009-096]much backbone and so far gone in consumption, that he showed plainly

[009-097]with what judgment and propriety the name of Rocinante had been

[009-098]bestowed upon him. Near him was Sancho Panza holding the halter of his

[009-099]ass, at whose feet was another label that said, “Sancho Zancas,” and

[009-100]according to the picture, he must have had a big belly, a short body,

[009-101]and long shanks, for which reason, no doubt, the names of Panza and

[009-102]Zancas were given him, for by these two surnames the history several

[009-103]times calls him. Some other trifling particulars might be mentioned,

[009-104]but they are all of slight importance and have nothing to do with the

[009-105]true relation of the history; and no history can be bad so long as it

[009-106]is true.

[009-107]If against the present one any objection be raised on the score of its

[009-108]truth, it can only be that its author was an Arab, as lying is a very

[009-109]common propensity with those of that nation; though, as they are such

[009-110]enemies of ours, it is conceivable that there were omissions rather

[009-111]than additions made in the course of it. And this is my own opinion;

[009-112]for, where he could and should give freedom to his pen in praise of so

[009-113]worthy a knight, he seems to me deliberately to pass it over in

[009-114]silence; which is ill done and worse contrived, for it is the business

[009-115]and duty of historians to be exact, truthful, and wholly free from

[009-116]passion, and neither interest nor fear, hatred nor love, should make

[009-117]them swerve from the path of truth, whose mother is history, rival of

[009-118]time, storehouse of deeds, witness for the past, example and counsel

[009-119]for the present, and warning for the future. In this I know will be

[009-120]found all that can be desired in the pleasantest, and if it be wanting

[009-121]in any good quality, I maintain it is the fault of its hound of an

[009-122]author and not the fault of the subject. To be brief, its Second Part,

[009-123]according to the translation, began in this way:

[009-124]With trenchant swords upraised and poised on high, it seemed as though

[009-125]the two valiant and wrathful combatants stood threatening heaven, and

[009-126]earth, and hell, with such resolution and determination did they bear

[009-127]themselves. The fiery Biscayan was the first to strike a blow, which

[009-128]was delivered with such force and fury that had not the sword turned in

[009-129]its course, that single stroke would have sufficed to put an end to the

[009-130]bitter struggle and to all the adventures of our knight; but that good

[009-131]fortune which reserved him for greater things, turned aside the sword

[009-132]of his adversary, so that although it smote him upon the left shoulder,

[009-133]it did him no more harm than to strip all that side of its armour,

[009-134]carrying away a great part of his helmet with half of his ear, all

[009-135]which with fearful ruin fell to the ground, leaving him in a sorry

[009-136]plight.

[009-137]Good God! Who is there that could properly describe the rage that

[009-138]filled the heart of our Manchegan when he saw himself dealt with in

[009-139]this fashion? All that can be said is, it was such that he again raised

[009-140]himself in his stirrups, and, grasping his sword more firmly with both

[009-141]hands, he came down on the Biscayan with such fury, smiting him full

[009-142]over the cushion and over the head, that—even so good a shield proving

[009-143]useless—as if a mountain had fallen on him, he began to bleed from

[009-144]nose, mouth, and ears, reeling as if about to fall backwards from his

[009-145]mule, as no doubt he would have done had he not flung his arms about

[009-146]its neck; at the same time, however, he slipped his feet out of the

[009-147]stirrups and then unclasped his arms, and the mule, taking fright at

[009-148]the terrible blow, made off across the plain, and with a few plunges

[009-149]flung its master to the ground. Don Quixote stood looking on very

[009-150]calmly, and, when he saw him fall, leaped from his horse and with great

[009-151]briskness ran to him, and, presenting the point of his sword to his

[009-152]eyes, bade him surrender, or he would cut his head off. The Biscayan

[009-153]was so bewildered that he was unable to answer a word, and it would

[009-154]have gone hard with him, so blind was Don Quixote, had not the ladies

[009-155]in the coach, who had hitherto been watching the combat in great

[009-156]terror, hastened to where he stood and implored him with earnest

[009-157]entreaties to grant them the great grace and favour of sparing their

[009-158]squire’s life; to which Don Quixote replied with much gravity and

[009-159]dignity, “In truth, fair ladies, I am well content to do what ye ask of

[009-160]me; but it must be on one condition and understanding, which is that

[009-161]this knight promise me to go to the village of El Toboso, and on my

[009-162]behalf present himself before the peerless lady Dulcinea, that she deal

[009-163]with him as shall be most pleasing to her.”

[009-164]The terrified and disconsolate ladies, without discussing Don Quixote’s

[009-165]demand or asking who Dulcinea might be, promised that their squire

[009-166]should do all that had been commanded.

[009-167]“Then, on the faith of that promise,” said Don Quixote, “I shall do him

[009-168]no further harm, though he well deserves it of me.”
