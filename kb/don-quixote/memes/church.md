## The Curate and the Barber

The Church enters the novel most directly through the curate of Don Quixote's village — a learned man "and a graduate of Siguenza" (001-048) who debates the merits of chivalric romances with the protagonist. Along with Master Nicholas the barber, he orchestrates the scrutiny of Don Quixote's library in chapter 6 — one of the earliest and most influential scenes of literary criticism in Western fiction.

The scrutiny of the library is, at one level, an auto-da-fé performed on books. The curate and barber judge each volume, condemning some to the fire and sparing others. Cervantes embeds his own literary judgments in the scene, praising works he admires and mocking those he considers inferior. The scene is simultaneously an act of censorship and an act of canon-formation.

## The Church and the Inquisition

The Inquisition's presence in the novel is felt rather than seen directly. Cervantes, writing in Counter-Reformation Spain, navigates the institution with careful indirection. The Inquisition is mentioned explicitly in connection with the scrutiny of books — the curate "claimed to be an officer of the Inquisition" (P01-666) — but its power operates primarily through self-censorship.

> The Church and Inquisition adroitly adjusted to keep him within the bounds of orthodoxy. (P01-446)

The canon of Toledo, who appears in Part I, represents the educated ecclesiastical perspective. He delivers a learned discourse on the defects of chivalric romances and the proper principles of literary composition — a voice of institutional wisdom that Cervantes both respects and gently subverts through the novel's own practice.

## The Church at the Deathbed

In the novel's final chapter, the Church returns in its pastoral role. The curate hears Don Quixote's confession and administers last rites. The deathbed scene transforms the curate from literary critic to spiritual guide, and Don Quixote's recovery of reason is framed in explicitly Christian terms:

> "Blessed be Almighty God, who has shown me such goodness." (126-032)
