[013-002]But hardly had day begun to show itself through the balconies of the

[013-003]east, when five of the six goatherds came to rouse Don Quixote and tell

[013-004]him that if he was still of a mind to go and see the famous burial of

[013-005]Chrysostom they would bear him company. Don Quixote, who desired

[013-006]nothing better, rose and ordered Sancho to saddle and pannel at once,

[013-007]which he did with all despatch, and with the same they all set out

[013-008]forthwith. They had not gone a quarter of a league when at the meeting

[013-009]of two paths they saw coming towards them some six shepherds dressed in

[013-010]black sheepskins and with their heads crowned with garlands of cypress

[013-011]and bitter oleander. Each of them carried a stout holly staff in his

[013-012]hand, and along with them there came two men of quality on horseback in

[013-013]handsome travelling dress, with three servants on foot accompanying

[013-014]them. Courteous salutations were exchanged on meeting, and inquiring

[013-015]one of the other which way each party was going, they learned that all

[013-016]were bound for the scene of the burial, so they went on all together.

[013-017]One of those on horseback addressing his companion said to him, “It

[013-018]seems to me, Señor Vivaldo, that we may reckon as well spent the delay

[013-019]we shall incur in seeing this remarkable funeral, for remarkable it

[013-020]cannot but be judging by the strange things these shepherds have told

[013-021]us, of both the dead shepherd and homicide shepherdess.”

[013-022]“So I think too,” replied Vivaldo, “and I would delay not to say a day,

[013-023]but four, for the sake of seeing it.”

[013-024]Don Quixote asked them what it was they had heard of Marcela and

[013-025]Chrysostom. The traveller answered that the same morning they had met

[013-026]these shepherds, and seeing them dressed in this mournful fashion they

[013-027]had asked them the reason of their appearing in such a guise; which one

[013-028]of them gave, describing the strange behaviour and beauty of a

[013-029]shepherdess called Marcela, and the loves of many who courted her,

[013-030]together with the death of that Chrysostom to whose burial they were

[013-031]going. In short, he repeated all that Pedro had related to Don Quixote.

[013-032]This conversation dropped, and another was commenced by him who was

[013-033]called Vivaldo asking Don Quixote what was the reason that led him to

[013-034]go armed in that fashion in a country so peaceful. To which Don Quixote

[013-035]replied, “The pursuit of my calling does not allow or permit me to go

[013-036]in any other fashion; easy life, enjoyment, and repose were invented

[013-037]for soft courtiers, but toil, unrest, and arms were invented and made

[013-038]for those alone whom the world calls knights-errant, of whom I, though

[013-039]unworthy, am the least of all.”

[013-040]The instant they heard this all set him down as mad, and the better to

[013-041]settle the point and discover what kind of madness his was, Vivaldo

[013-042]proceeded to ask him what knights-errant meant.

[013-043]“Have not your worships,” replied Don Quixote, “read the annals and

[013-044]histories of England, in which are recorded the famous deeds of King

[013-045]Arthur, whom we in our popular Castilian invariably call King Artus,

[013-046]with regard to whom it is an ancient tradition, and commonly received

[013-047]all over that kingdom of Great Britain, that this king did not die, but

[013-048]was changed by magic art into a raven, and that in process of time he

[013-049]is to return to reign and recover his kingdom and sceptre; for which

[013-050]reason it cannot be proved that from that time to this any Englishman

[013-051]ever killed a raven? Well, then, in the time of this good king that

[013-052]famous order of chivalry of the Knights of the Round Table was

[013-053]instituted, and the amour of Don Lancelot of the Lake with the Queen

[013-054]Guinevere occurred, precisely as is there related, the go-between and

[013-055]confidante therein being the highly honourable dame Quintañona, whence

[013-056]came that ballad so well known and widely spread in our Spain—

[013-057]O never surely was there knight

[013-058]So served by hand of dame,

[013-059]As served was he Sir Lancelot hight

[013-060]When he from Britain came—

[013-061]with all the sweet and delectable course of his achievements in love

[013-062]and war. Handed down from that time, then, this order of chivalry went

[013-063]on extending and spreading itself over many and various parts of the

[013-064]world; and in it, famous and renowned for their deeds, were the mighty

[013-065]Amadis of Gaul with all his sons and descendants to the fifth

[013-066]generation, and the valiant Felixmarte of Hircania, and the never

[013-067]sufficiently praised Tirante el Blanco, and in our own days almost we

[013-068]have seen and heard and talked with the invincible knight Don Belianis

[013-069]of Greece. This, then, sirs, is to be a knight-errant, and what I have

[013-070]spoken of is the order of his chivalry, of which, as I have already

[013-071]said, I, though a sinner, have made profession, and what the aforesaid

[013-072]knights professed that same do I profess, and so I go through these

[013-073]solitudes and wilds seeking adventures, resolved in soul to oppose my

[013-074]arm and person to the most perilous that fortune may offer me in aid of

[013-075]the weak and needy.”

[013-076]By these words of his the travellers were able to satisfy themselves of

[013-077]Don Quixote’s being out of his senses and of the form of madness that

[013-078]overmastered him, at which they felt the same astonishment that all

[013-079]felt on first becoming acquainted with it; and Vivaldo, who was a

[013-080]person of great shrewdness and of a lively temperament, in order to

[013-081]beguile the short journey which they said was required to reach the

[013-082]mountain, the scene of the burial, sought to give him an opportunity of

[013-083]going on with his absurdities. So he said to him, “It seems to me,

[013-084]Señor Knight-errant, that your worship has made choice of one of the

[013-085]most austere professions in the world, and I imagine even that of the

[013-086]Carthusian monks is not so austere.”

[013-087]“As austere it may perhaps be,” replied our Don Quixote, “but so

[013-088]necessary for the world I am very much inclined to doubt. For, if the

[013-089]truth is to be told, the soldier who executes what his captain orders

[013-090]does no less than the captain himself who gives the order. My meaning,

[013-091]is, that churchmen in peace and quiet pray to Heaven for the welfare of

[013-092]the world, but we soldiers and knights carry into effect what they pray

[013-093]for, defending it with the might of our arms and the edge of our

[013-094]swords, not under shelter but in the open air, a target for the

[013-095]intolerable rays of the sun in summer and the piercing frosts of

[013-096]winter. Thus are we God’s ministers on earth and the arms by which his

[013-097]justice is done therein. And as the business of war and all that

[013-098]relates and belongs to it cannot be conducted without exceeding great

[013-099]sweat, toil, and exertion, it follows that those who make it their

[013-100]profession have undoubtedly more labour than those who in tranquil

[013-101]peace and quiet are engaged in praying to God to help the weak. I do

[013-102]not mean to say, nor does it enter into my thoughts, that the

[013-103]knight-errant’s calling is as good as that of the monk in his cell; I

[013-104]would merely infer from what I endure myself that it is beyond a doubt

[013-105]a more laborious and a more belaboured one, a hungrier and thirstier, a

[013-106]wretcheder, raggeder, and lousier; for there is no reason to doubt that

[013-107]the knights-errant of yore endured much hardship in the course of their

[013-108]lives. And if some of them by the might of their arms did rise to be

[013-109]emperors, in faith it cost them dear in the matter of blood and sweat;

[013-110]and if those who attained to that rank had not had magicians and sages

[013-111]to help them they would have been completely baulked in their ambition

[013-112]and disappointed in their hopes.”

[013-113]“That is my own opinion,” replied the traveller; “but one thing among

[013-114]many others seems to me very wrong in knights-errant, and that is that

[013-115]when they find themselves about to engage in some mighty and perilous

[013-116]adventure in which there is manifest danger of losing their lives, they

[013-117]never at the moment of engaging in it think of commending themselves to

[013-118]God, as is the duty of every good Christian in like peril; instead of

[013-119]which they commend themselves to their ladies with as much devotion as

[013-120]if these were their gods, a thing which seems to me to savour somewhat

[013-121]of heathenism.”

[013-122]“Sir,” answered Don Quixote, “that cannot be on any account omitted,

[013-123]and the knight-errant would be disgraced who acted otherwise: for it is

[013-124]usual and customary in knight-errantry that the knight-errant, who on

[013-125]engaging in any great feat of arms has his lady before him, should turn

[013-126]his eyes towards her softly and lovingly, as though with them

[013-127]entreating her to favour and protect him in the hazardous venture he is

[013-128]about to undertake, and even though no one hear him, he is bound to say

[013-129]certain words between his teeth, commending himself to her with all his

[013-130]heart, and of this we have innumerable instances in the histories. Nor

[013-131]is it to be supposed from this that they are to omit commending

[013-132]themselves to God, for there will be time and opportunity for doing so

[013-133]while they are engaged in their task.”

[013-134]“For all that,” answered the traveller, “I feel some doubt still,

[013-135]because often I have read how words will arise between two

[013-136]knights-errant, and from one thing to another it comes about that their

[013-137]anger kindles and they wheel their horses round and take a good stretch

[013-138]of field, and then without any more ado at the top of their speed they

[013-139]come to the charge, and in mid-career they are wont to commend

[013-140]themselves to their ladies; and what commonly comes of the encounter is

[013-141]that one falls over the haunches of his horse pierced through and

[013-142]through by his antagonist’s lance, and as for the other, it is only by

[013-143]holding on to the mane of his horse that he can help falling to the

[013-144]ground; but I know not how the dead man had time to commend himself to

[013-145]God in the course of such rapid work as this; it would have been better

[013-146]if those words which he spent in commending himself to his lady in the

[013-147]midst of his career had been devoted to his duty and obligation as a

[013-148]Christian. Moreover, it is my belief that all knights-errant have not

[013-149]ladies to commend themselves to, for they are not all in love.”

[013-150]“That is impossible,” said Don Quixote: “I say it is impossible that

[013-151]there could be a knight-errant without a lady, because to such it is as

[013-152]natural and proper to be in love as to the heavens to have stars: most

[013-153]certainly no history has been seen in which there is to be found a

[013-154]knight-errant without an amour, and for the simple reason that without

[013-155]one he would be held no legitimate knight but a bastard, and one who

[013-156]had gained entrance into the stronghold of the said knighthood, not by

[013-157]the door, but over the wall like a thief and a robber.”

[013-158]“Nevertheless,” said the traveller, “if I remember rightly, I think I

[013-159]have read that Don Galaor, the brother of the valiant Amadis of Gaul,

[013-160]never had any special lady to whom he might commend himself, and yet he

[013-161]was not the less esteemed, and was a very stout and famous knight.”

[013-162]To which our Don Quixote made answer, “Sir, one solitary swallow does

[013-163]not make summer; moreover, I know that knight was in secret very deeply

[013-164]in love; besides which, that way of falling in love with all that took

[013-165]his fancy was a natural propensity which he could not control. But, in

[013-166]short, it is very manifest that he had one alone whom he made mistress

[013-167]of his will, to whom he commended himself very frequently and very

[013-168]secretly, for he prided himself on being a reticent knight.”

[013-169]“Then if it be essential that every knight-errant should be in love,”

[013-170]said the traveller, “it may be fairly supposed that your worship is so,

[013-171]as you are of the order; and if you do not pride yourself on being as

[013-172]reticent as Don Galaor, I entreat you as earnestly as I can, in the

[013-173]name of all this company and in my own, to inform us of the name,

[013-174]country, rank, and beauty of your lady, for she will esteem herself

[013-175]fortunate if all the world knows that she is loved and served by such a

[013-176]knight as your worship seems to be.”

[013-177]At this Don Quixote heaved a deep sigh and said, “I cannot say

[013-178]positively whether my sweet enemy is pleased or not that the world

[013-179]should know I serve her; I can only say in answer to what has been so

[013-180]courteously asked of me, that her name is Dulcinea, her country El

[013-181]Toboso, a village of La Mancha, her rank must be at least that of a

[013-182]princess, since she is my queen and lady, and her beauty superhuman,

[013-183]since all the impossible and fanciful attributes of beauty which the

[013-184]poets apply to their ladies are verified in her; for her hairs are

[013-185]gold, her forehead Elysian fields, her eyebrows rainbows, her eyes

[013-186]suns, her cheeks roses, her lips coral, her teeth pearls, her neck

[013-187]alabaster, her bosom marble, her hands ivory, her fairness snow, and

[013-188]what modesty conceals from sight such, I think and imagine, as rational

[013-189]reflection can only extol, not compare.”

[013-190]“We should like to know her lineage, race, and ancestry,” said Vivaldo.

[013-191]To which Don Quixote replied, “She is not of the ancient Roman Curtii,

[013-192]Caii, or Scipios, nor of the modern Colonnas or Orsini, nor of the

[013-193]Moncadas or Requesenes of Catalonia, nor yet of the Rebellas or

[013-194]Villanovas of Valencia; Palafoxes, Nuzas, Rocabertis, Corellas, Lunas,

[013-195]Alagones, Urreas, Foces, or Gurreas of Aragon; Cerdas, Manriques,

[013-196]Mendozas, or Guzmans of Castile; Alencastros, Pallas, or Meneses of

[013-197]Portugal; but she is of those of El Toboso of La Mancha, a lineage that

[013-198]though modern, may furnish a source of gentle blood for the most

[013-199]illustrious families of the ages that are to come, and this let none

[013-200]dispute with me save on the condition that Zerbino placed at the foot

[013-201]of the trophy of Orlando’s arms, saying,

[013-202]These let none move

[013-203]Who dareth not his might with Roland prove.”

[013-204]“Although mine is of the Cachopins of Laredo,” said the traveller, “I

[013-205]will not venture to compare it with that of El Toboso of La Mancha,

[013-206]though, to tell the truth, no such surname has until now ever reached

[013-207]my ears.”

[013-208]“What!” said Don Quixote, “has that never reached them?”

[013-209]The rest of the party went along listening with great attention to the

[013-210]conversation of the pair, and even the very goatherds and shepherds

[013-211]perceived how exceedingly out of his wits our Don Quixote was. Sancho

[013-212]Panza alone thought that what his master said was the truth, knowing

[013-213]who he was and having known him from his birth; and all that he felt

[013-214]any difficulty in believing was that about the fair Dulcinea del

[013-215]Toboso, because neither any such name nor any such princess had ever

[013-216]come to his knowledge though he lived so close to El Toboso. They were

[013-217]going along conversing in this way, when they saw descending a gap

[013-218]between two high mountains some twenty shepherds, all clad in

[013-219]sheepskins of black wool, and crowned with garlands which, as

[013-220]afterwards appeared, were, some of them of yew, some of cypress. Six of

[013-221]the number were carrying a bier covered with a great variety of flowers

[013-222]and branches, on seeing which one of the goatherds said, “Those who

[013-223]come there are the bearers of Chrysostom’s body, and the foot of that

[013-224]mountain is the place where he ordered them to bury him.” They

[013-225]therefore made haste to reach the spot, and did so by the time those

[013-226]who came had laid the bier upon the ground, and four of them with sharp

[013-227]pickaxes were digging a grave by the side of a hard rock. They greeted

[013-228]each other courteously, and then Don Quixote and those who accompanied

[013-229]him turned to examine the bier, and on it, covered with flowers, they

[013-230]saw a dead body in the dress of a shepherd, to all appearance of one

[013-231]thirty years of age, and showing even in death that in life he had been

[013-232]of comely features and gallant bearing. Around him on the bier itself

[013-233]were laid some books, and several papers open and folded; and those who

[013-234]were looking on as well as those who were opening the grave and all the

[013-235]others who were there preserved a strange silence, until one of those

[013-236]who had borne the body said to another, “Observe carefully, Ambrosio if

[013-237]this is the place Chrysostom spoke of, since you are anxious that what

[013-238]he directed in his will should be so strictly complied with.”

[013-243]honourable as it was devoted, and here it was that at last Marcela

[013-244]ended by scorning and rejecting him so as to bring the tragedy of his

[013-245]wretched life to a close; here, in memory of misfortunes so great, he

[013-246]desired to be laid in the bowels of eternal oblivion.” Then turning to

[013-248]which you are looking with compassionate eyes, was the abode of a soul

[013-249]on which Heaven bestowed a vast share of its riches. That is the body

[013-250]of Chrysostom, who was unrivalled in wit, unequalled in courtesy,

[013-251]unapproached in gentle bearing, a phœnix in friendship, generous

[013-252]without limit, grave without arrogance, gay without vulgarity, and, in

[013-253]short, first in all that constitutes goodness and second to none in all

[013-254]that makes up misfortune. He loved deeply, he was hated; he adored, he

[013-255]was scorned; he wooed a wild beast, he pleaded with marble, he pursued

[013-256]the wind, he cried to the wilderness, he served ingratitude, and for

[013-260]to consign them to the fire after having consigned his body to the

[013-261]earth.”
