WHICH RELATES HOW THEY LEARNED THE WAY IN WHICH THEY WERE TO DISENCHANT

[086-001]THE PEERLESS DULCINEA DEL TOBOSO, WHICH IS ONE OF THE RAREST ADVENTURES

IN THIS BOOK

p34a.jpg (141K)


[086-002]Great was the pleasure the duke and duchess took in the conversation of

[086-003]Don Quixote and Sancho Panza; and, more bent than ever upon the plan

[086-004]they had of practising some jokes upon them that should have the look

[086-005]and appearance of adventures, they took as their basis of action what

[086-006]Don Quixote had already told them about the cave of Montesinos, in

[086-007]order to play him a famous one. But what the duchess marvelled at above

[086-008]all was that Sancho’s simplicity could be so great as to make him

[086-009]believe as absolute truth that Dulcinea had been enchanted, when it was

[086-010]he himself who had been the enchanter and trickster in the business.

[086-011]Having, therefore, instructed their servants in everything they were to

[086-012]do, six days afterwards they took him out to hunt, with as great a

[086-013]retinue of huntsmen and beaters as a crowned king.

[086-014]They presented Don Quixote with a hunting suit, and Sancho with another

[086-015]of the finest green cloth; but Don Quixote declined to put his on,

[086-016]saying that he must soon return to the hard pursuit of arms, and could

[086-017]not carry wardrobes or stores with him. Sancho, however, took what they

[086-018]gave him, meaning to sell it at the first opportunity.

[086-019]The appointed day having arrived, Don Quixote armed himself, and Sancho

[086-020]arrayed himself, and mounted on his Dapple (for he would not give him

[086-021]up though they offered him a horse), he placed himself in the midst of

[086-022]the troop of huntsmen. The duchess came out splendidly attired, and Don

[086-023]Quixote, in pure courtesy and politeness, held the rein of her palfrey,

[086-024]though the duke wanted not to allow him; and at last they reached a

[086-025]wood that lay between two high mountains, where, after occupying

[086-026]various posts, ambushes, and paths, and distributing the party in

[086-027]different positions, the hunt began with great noise, shouting, and

[086-028]hallooing, so that, between the baying of the hounds and the blowing of

[086-029]the horns, they could not hear one another. The duchess dismounted, and

[086-030]with a sharp boar-spear in her hand posted herself where she knew the

[086-031]wild boars were in the habit of passing. The duke and Don Quixote

[086-032]likewise dismounted and placed themselves one at each side of her.

[086-033]Sancho took up a position in the rear of all without dismounting from

[086-034]Dapple, whom he dared not desert lest some mischief should befall him.

[086-035]Scarcely had they taken their stand in a line with several of their

[086-036]servants, when they saw a huge boar, closely pressed by the hounds and

[086-037]followed by the huntsmen, making towards them, grinding his teeth and

[086-038]tusks, and scattering foam from his mouth. As soon as he saw him Don

[086-039]Quixote, bracing his shield on his arm, and drawing his sword, advanced

[086-040]to meet him; the duke with boar-spear did the same; but the duchess

[086-041]would have gone in front of them all had not the duke prevented her.

[086-042]Sancho alone, deserting Dapple at the sight of the mighty beast, took

[086-043]to his heels as hard as he could and strove in vain to mount a tall

[086-044]oak. As he was clinging to a branch, however, half-way up in his

[086-045]struggle to reach the top, the bough, such was his ill-luck and hard

[086-046]fate, gave way, and caught in his fall by a broken limb of the oak, he

[086-047]hung suspended in the air unable to reach the ground. Finding himself

[086-048]in this position, and that the green coat was beginning to tear, and

[086-049]reflecting that if the fierce animal came that way he might be able to

[086-050]get at him, he began to utter such cries, and call for help so

[086-051]earnestly, that all who heard him and did not see him felt sure he must

[086-052]be in the teeth of some wild beast. In the end the tusked boar fell

[086-053]pierced by the blades of the many spears they held in front of him; and

[086-054]Don Quixote, turning round at the cries of Sancho, for he knew by them

[086-055]that it was he, saw him hanging from the oak head downwards, with

[086-056]Dapple, who did not forsake him in his distress, close beside him; and

[086-057]Cide Hamete observes that he seldom saw Sancho Panza without seeing

[086-058]Dapple, or Dapple without seeing Sancho Panza; such was their

[086-059]attachment and loyalty one to the other. Don Quixote went over and

[086-060]unhooked Sancho, who, as soon as he found himself on the ground, looked

[086-061]at the rent in his huntingcoat and was grieved to the heart, for he

[086-062]thought he had got a patrimonial estate in that suit.

[086-063]Meanwhile they had slung the mighty boar across the back of a mule, and

[086-064]having covered it with sprigs of rosemary and branches of myrtle, they

[086-065]bore it away as the spoils of victory to some large field-tents which

[086-066]had been pitched in the middle of the wood, where they found the tables

[086-067]laid and dinner served, in such grand and sumptuous style that it was

[086-068]easy to see the rank and magnificence of those who had provided it.

[086-069]Sancho, as he showed the rents in his torn suit to the duchess,

[086-070]observed, “If we had been hunting hares, or after small birds, my coat

[086-071]would have been safe from being in the plight it’s in; I don’t know

[086-072]what pleasure one can find in lying in wait for an animal that may take

[086-073]your life with his tusk if he gets at you. I recollect having heard an

[086-074]old ballad sung that says,

[086-075]By bears be thou devoured, as erst

[086-076]Was famous Favila.”

[086-077]“That,” said Don Quixote, “was a Gothic king, who, going a-hunting, was

[086-078]devoured by a bear.”

[086-079]“Just so,” said Sancho; “and I would not have kings and princes expose

[086-080]themselves to such dangers for the sake of a pleasure which, to my

[086-081]mind, ought not to be one, as it consists in killing an animal that has

[086-082]done no harm whatever.”

[086-083]“Quite the contrary, Sancho; you are wrong there,” said the duke; “for

[086-084]hunting is more suitable and requisite for kings and princes than for

[086-085]anybody else. The chase is the emblem of war; it has stratagems, wiles,

[086-086]and crafty devices for overcoming the enemy in safety; in it extreme

[086-087]cold and intolerable heat have to be borne, indolence and sleep are

[086-088]despised, the bodily powers are invigorated, the limbs of him who

[086-089]engages in it are made supple, and, in a word, it is a pursuit which

[086-090]may be followed without injury to anyone and with enjoyment to many;

[086-091]and the best of it is, it is not for everybody, as field-sports of

[086-092]other sorts are, except hawking, which also is only for kings and great

[086-093]lords. Reconsider your opinion therefore, Sancho, and when you are

[086-094]governor take to hunting, and you will find the good of it.”

[086-095]“Nay,” said Sancho, “the good governor should have a broken leg and

[086-096]keep at home;” it would be a nice thing if, after people had been at

[086-097]the trouble of coming to look for him on business, the governor were to

[086-098]be away in the forest enjoying himself; the government would go on

[086-099]badly in that fashion. By my faith, señor, hunting and amusements are

[086-100]more fit for idlers than for governors; what I intend to amuse myself

[086-101]with is playing all fours at Eastertime, and bowls on Sundays and

[086-102]holidays; for these huntings don’t suit my condition or agree with my

[086-103]conscience.”

[086-104]“God grant it may turn out so,” said the duke; “because it’s a long

[086-105]step from saying to doing.”

[086-106]“Be that as it may,” said Sancho, “‘pledges don’t distress a good

[086-107]payer,’ and ‘he whom God helps does better than he who gets up early,’

[086-108]and ‘it’s the tripes that carry the feet and not the feet the tripes;’

[086-109]I mean to say that if God gives me help and I do my duty honestly, no

[086-110]doubt I’ll govern better than a gerfalcon. Nay, let them only put a

[086-111]finger in my mouth, and they’ll see whether I can bite or not.”

[086-112]“The curse of God and all his saints upon thee, thou accursed Sancho!”

[086-113]exclaimed Don Quixote; “when will the day come—as I have often said to

[086-114]thee—when I shall hear thee make one single coherent, rational remark

[086-115]without proverbs? Pray, your highnesses, leave this fool alone, for he

[086-116]will grind your souls between, not to say two, but two thousand

[086-117]proverbs, dragged in as much in season, and as much to the purpose

[086-118]as—may God grant as much health to him, or to me if I want to listen to

[086-119]them!”

[086-120]“Sancho Panza’s proverbs,” said the duchess, “though more in number

[086-121]than the Greek Commander’s, are not therefore less to be esteemed for

[086-122]the conciseness of the maxims. For my own part, I can say they give me

[086-123]more pleasure than others that may be better brought in and more

[086-124]seasonably introduced.”

[086-125]In pleasant conversation of this sort they passed out of the tent into

[086-126]the wood, and the day was spent in visiting some of the posts and

[086-127]hiding-places, and then night closed in, not, however, as brilliantly

[086-128]or tranquilly as might have been expected at the season, for it was

[086-129]then midsummer; but bringing with it a kind of haze that greatly aided

[086-130]the project of the duke and duchess; and thus, as night began to fall,

[086-131]and a little after twilight set in, suddenly the whole wood on all four

[086-132]sides seemed to be on fire, and shortly after, here, there, on all

[086-133]sides, a vast number of trumpets and other military instruments were

[086-134]heard, as if several troops of cavalry were passing through the wood.

[086-135]The blaze of the fire and the noise of the warlike instruments almost

[086-136]blinded the eyes and deafened the ears of those that stood by, and

[086-137]indeed of all who were in the wood. Then there were heard repeated

[086-138]lelilies after the fashion of the Moors when they rush to battle;

[086-139]trumpets and clarions brayed, drums beat, fifes played, so unceasingly

[086-140]and so fast that he could not have had any senses who did not lose them

[086-141]with the confused din of so many instruments. The duke was astounded,

[086-142]the duchess amazed, Don Quixote wondering, Sancho Panza trembling, and

[086-143]indeed, even they who were aware of the cause were frightened. In their

[086-144]fear, silence fell upon them, and a postillion, in the guise of a

[086-145]demon, passed in front of them, blowing, in lieu of a bugle, a huge

[086-146]hollow horn that gave out a horrible hoarse note.

[086-147]“Ho there! brother courier,” cried the duke, “who are you? Where are

[086-148]you going? What troops are these that seem to be passing through the

[086-149]wood?”

[086-150]To which the courier replied in a harsh, discordant voice, “I am the

[086-151]devil; I am in search of Don Quixote of La Mancha; those who are coming

[086-152]this way are six troops of enchanters, who are bringing on a triumphal

[086-153]car the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso; she comes under enchantment,

[086-154]together with the gallant Frenchman Montesinos, to give instructions to

[086-155]Don Quixote as to how, she the said lady, may be disenchanted.”

[086-156]“If you were the devil, as you say and as your appearance indicates,”

[086-157]said the duke, “you would have known the said knight Don Quixote of La

[086-158]Mancha, for you have him here before you.”

[086-159]“By God and upon my conscience,” said the devil, “I never observed it,

[086-160]for my mind is occupied with so many different things that I was

[086-161]forgetting the main thing I came about.”

[086-162]“This demon must be an honest fellow and a good Christian,” said

[086-163]Sancho; “for if he wasn’t he wouldn’t swear by God and his conscience;

[086-164]I feel sure now there must be good souls even in hell itself.”

[086-165]Without dismounting, the demon then turned to Don Quixote and said,

[086-166]“The unfortunate but valiant knight Montesinos sends me to thee, the

[086-167]Knight of the Lions (would that I saw thee in their claws), bidding me

[086-168]tell thee to wait for him wherever I may find thee, as he brings with

[086-169]him her whom they call Dulcinea del Toboso, that he may show thee what

[086-170]is needful in order to disenchant her; and as I came for no more I need

[086-171]stay no longer; demons of my sort be with thee, and good angels with

[086-172]these gentles;” and so saying he blew his huge horn, turned about and

[086-173]went off without waiting for a reply from anyone.

[086-174]They all felt fresh wonder, but particularly Sancho and Don Quixote;

[086-175]Sancho to see how, in defiance of the truth, they would have it that

[086-176]Dulcinea was enchanted; Don Quixote because he could not feel sure

[086-177]whether what had happened to him in the cave of Montesinos was true or

[086-178]not; and as he was deep in these cogitations the duke said to him, “Do

[086-179]you mean to wait, Señor Don Quixote?”

[086-180]“Why not?” replied he; “here will I wait, fearless and firm, though all

[086-181]hell should come to attack me.”

[086-182]“Well then, if I see another devil or hear another horn like the last,

[086-183]I’ll wait here as much as in Flanders,” said Sancho.

[086-184]Night now closed in more completely, and many lights began to flit

[086-185]through the wood, just as those fiery exhalations from the earth, that

[086-186]look like shooting-stars to our eyes, flit through the heavens; a

[086-187]frightful noise, too, was heard, like that made by the solid wheels the

[086-188]ox-carts usually have, by the harsh, ceaseless creaking of which, they

[086-189]say, the bears and wolves are put to flight, if there happen to be any

[086-190]where they are passing. In addition to all this commotion, there came a

[086-191]further disturbance to increase the tumult, for now it seemed as if in

[086-192]truth, on all four sides of the wood, four encounters or battles were

[086-193]going on at the same time; in one quarter resounded the dull noise of a

[086-194]terrible cannonade, in another numberless muskets were being

[086-195]discharged, the shouts of the combatants sounded almost close at hand,

[086-196]and farther away the Moorish lelilies were raised again and again. In a

[086-197]word, the bugles, the horns, the clarions, the trumpets, the drums, the

[086-198]cannon, the musketry, and above all the tremendous noise of the carts,

[086-199]all made up together a din so confused and terrific that Don Quixote

[086-200]had need to summon up all his courage to brave it; but Sancho’s gave

[086-201]way, and he fell fainting on the skirt of the duchess’s robe, who let

[086-202]him lie there and promptly bade them throw water in his face. This was

[086-203]done, and he came to himself by the time that one of the carts with the

[086-204]creaking wheels reached the spot. It was drawn by four plodding oxen

[086-205]all covered with black housings; on each horn they had fixed a large

[086-206]lighted wax taper, and on the top of the cart was constructed a raised

[086-207]seat, on which sat a venerable old man with a beard whiter than the

[086-208]very snow, and so long that it fell below his waist; he was dressed in

[086-209]a long robe of black buckram; for as the cart was thickly set with a

[086-210]multitude of candles it was easy to make out everything that was on it.

[086-211]Leading it were two hideous demons, also clad in buckram, with

[086-212]countenances so frightful that Sancho, having once seen them, shut his

[086-213]eyes so as not to see them again. As soon as the cart came opposite the

[086-214]spot the old man rose from his lofty seat, and standing up said in a

[086-215]loud voice, “I am the sage Lirgandeo,” and without another word the

[086-216]cart then passed on. Behind it came another of the same form, with

[086-217]another aged man enthroned, who, stopping the cart, said in a voice no

[086-218]less solemn than that of the first, “I am the sage Alquife, the great

[086-219]friend of Urganda the Unknown,” and passed on. Then another cart came

[086-220]by at the same pace, but the occupant of the throne was not old like

[086-221]the others, but a man stalwart and robust, and of a forbidding

[086-222]countenance, who as he came up said in a voice far hoarser and more

[086-223]devilish, “I am the enchanter Archelaus, the mortal enemy of Amadis of

[086-224]Gaul and all his kindred,” and then passed on. Having gone a short

[086-225]distance the three carts halted and the monotonous noise of their

[086-226]wheels ceased, and soon after they heard another, not noise, but sound

[086-227]of sweet, harmonious music, of which Sancho was very glad, taking it to

[086-228]be a good sign; and said he to the duchess, from whom he did not stir a

[086-229]step, or for a single instant, “Señora, where there’s music there can’t

[086-230]be mischief.”

[086-231]“Nor where there are lights and it is bright,” said the duchess; to

[086-232]which Sancho replied, “Fire gives light, and it’s bright where there

[086-233]are bonfires, as we see by those that are all round us and perhaps may

[086-234]burn us; but music is a sign of mirth and merrymaking.”

[086-235]“That remains to be seen,” said Don Quixote, who was listening to all

[086-236]that passed; and he was right, as is shown in the following chapter.
