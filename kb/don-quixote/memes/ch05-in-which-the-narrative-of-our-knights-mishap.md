[005-001]IN WHICH THE NARRATIVE OF OUR KNIGHT’S MISHAP IS CONTINUED

[005-002]Finding, then, that, in fact he could not move, he thought himself of

[005-003]having recourse to his usual remedy, which was to think of some passage

[005-004]in his books, and his craze brought to his mind that about Baldwin and

[005-005]the Marquis of Mantua, when Carloto left him wounded on the

[005-006]mountainside, a story known by heart by the children, not forgotten by

[005-007]the young men, and lauded and even believed by the old folk; and for

[005-008]all that not a whit truer than the miracles of Mahomet. This seemed to

[005-009]him to fit exactly the case in which he found himself, so, making a

[005-010]show of severe suffering, he began to roll on the ground and with

[005-011]feeble breath repeat the very words which the wounded knight of the

[005-012]wood is said to have uttered:

[005-013]Where art thou, lady mine, that thou

[005-014]My sorrow dost not rue?

[005-015]Thou canst not know it, lady mine,

[005-016]Or else thou art untrue.

[005-017]And so he went on with the ballad as far as the lines:

[005-018]O noble Marquis of Mantua,

[005-019]My Uncle and liege lord!

[005-020]As chance would have it, when he had got to this line there happened to

[005-021]come by a peasant from his own village, a neighbour of his, who had

[005-022]been with a load of wheat to the mill, and he, seeing the man stretched

[005-023]there, came up to him and asked him who he was and what was the matter

[005-024]with him that he complained so dolefully.

[005-025]Don Quixote was firmly persuaded that this was the Marquis of Mantua,

[005-026]his uncle, so the only answer he made was to go on with his ballad, in

[005-027]which he told the tale of his misfortune, and of the loves of the

[005-028]Emperor’s son and his wife all exactly as the ballad sings it.

[005-029]The peasant stood amazed at hearing such nonsense, and relieving him of

[005-030]the visor, already battered to pieces by blows, he wiped his face,

[005-031]which was covered with dust, and as soon as he had done so he

[005-032]recognised him and said, “Señor Quixada” (for so he appears to have

[005-033]been called when he was in his senses and had not yet changed from a

[005-034]quiet country gentleman into a knight-errant), “who has brought your

[005-035]worship to this pass?” But to all questions the other only went on with

[005-036]his ballad.

[005-037]Seeing this, the good man removed as well as he could his breastplate

[005-038]and backpiece to see if he had any wound, but he could perceive no

[005-039]blood nor any mark whatever. He then contrived to raise him from the

[005-040]ground, and with no little difficulty hoisted him upon his ass, which

[005-041]seemed to him to be the easiest mount for him; and collecting the arms,

[005-042]even to the splinters of the lance, he tied them on Rocinante, and

[005-043]leading him by the bridle and the ass by the halter he took the road

[005-044]for the village, very sad to hear what absurd stuff Don Quixote was

[005-045]talking.

[005-046]Nor was Don Quixote less so, for what with blows and bruises he could

[005-047]not sit upright on the ass, and from time to time he sent up sighs to

[005-048]heaven, so that once more he drove the peasant to ask what ailed him.

[005-049]And it could have been only the devil himself that put into his head

[005-050]tales to match his own adventures, for now, forgetting Baldwin, he

[005-051]bethought himself of the Moor Abindarraez, when the Alcaide of

[005-052]Antequera, Rodrigo de Narvaez, took him prisoner and carried him away

[005-053]to his castle; so that when the peasant again asked him how he was and

[005-054]what ailed him, he gave him for reply the same words and phrases that

[005-055]the captive Abindarraez gave to Rodrigo de Narvaez, just as he had read

[005-056]the story in the “Diana” of Jorge de Montemayor where it is written,

[005-057]applying it to his own case so aptly that the peasant went along

[005-058]cursing his fate that he had to listen to such a lot of nonsense; from

[005-059]which, however, he came to the conclusion that his neighbour was mad,

[005-060]and so made all haste to reach the village to escape the wearisomeness

[005-061]of this harangue of Don Quixote’s; who, at the end of it, said, “Señor

[005-062]Don Rodrigo de Narvaez, your worship must know that this fair Xarifa I

[005-063]have mentioned is now the lovely Dulcinea del Toboso, for whom I have

[005-064]done, am doing, and will do the most famous deeds of chivalry that in

[005-065]this world have been seen, are to be seen, or ever shall be seen.”

[005-066]To this the peasant answered, “Señor—sinner that I am!—cannot your

[005-067]worship see that I am not Don Rodrigo de Narvaez nor the Marquis of

[005-068]Mantua, but Pedro Alonso your neighbour, and that your worship is

[005-069]neither Baldwin nor Abindarraez, but the worthy gentleman Señor

[005-070]Quixada?”

[005-071]“I know who I am,” replied Don Quixote, “and I know that I may be not

[005-072]only those I have named, but all the Twelve Peers of France and even

[005-073]all the Nine Worthies, since my achievements surpass all that they have

[005-074]done all together and each of them on his own account.”

[005-075]With this talk and more of the same kind they reached the village just

[005-076]as night was beginning to fall, but the peasant waited until it was a

[005-077]little later that the belaboured gentleman might not be seen riding in

[005-078]such a miserable trim. When it was what seemed to him the proper time

[005-079]he entered the village and went to Don Quixote’s house, which he found

[005-080]all in confusion, and there were the curate and the village barber, who

[005-081]were great friends of Don Quixote, and his housekeeper was saying to

[005-082]them in a loud voice, “What does your worship think can have befallen

[005-083]my master, Señor Licentiate Pero Perez?” for so the curate was called;

[005-084]“it is three days now since anything has been seen of him, or the hack,

[005-085]or the buckler, lance, or armour. Miserable me! I am certain of it, and

[005-086]it is as true as that I was born to die, that these accursed books of

[005-087]chivalry he has, and has got into the way of reading so constantly,

[005-088]have upset his reason; for now I remember having often heard him saying

[005-089]to himself that he would turn knight-errant and go all over the world

[005-090]in quest of adventures. To the devil and Barabbas with such books, that

[005-091]have brought to ruin in this way the finest understanding there was in

[005-092]all La Mancha!”

[005-093]The niece said the same, and, more: “You must know, Master

[005-094]Nicholas”—for that was the name of the barber—“it was often my uncle’s

[005-095]way to stay two days and nights together poring over these unholy books

[005-096]of misventures, after which he would fling the book away and snatch up

[005-097]his sword and fall to slashing the walls; and when he was tired out he

[005-098]would say he had killed four giants like four towers; and the sweat

[005-099]that flowed from him when he was weary he said was the blood of the

[005-100]wounds he had received in battle; and then he would drink a great jug

[005-101]of cold water and become calm and quiet, saying that this water was a

[005-102]most precious potion which the sage Esquife, a great magician and

[005-103]friend of his, had brought him. But I take all the blame upon myself

[005-104]for never having told your worships of my uncle’s vagaries, that you

[005-105]might put a stop to them before things had come to this pass, and burn

[005-106]all these accursed books—for he has a great number—that richly deserve

[005-107]to be burned like heretics.”

[005-108]“So say I too,” said the curate, “and by my faith to-morrow shall not

[005-109]pass without public judgment upon them, and may they be condemned to

[005-110]the flames lest they lead those that read to behave as my good friend

[005-111]seems to have behaved.”

[005-112]All this the peasant heard, and from it he understood at last what was

[005-113]the matter with his neighbour, so he began calling aloud, “Open, your

[005-114]worships, to Señor Baldwin and to Señor the Marquis of Mantua, who

[005-115]comes badly wounded, and to Señor Abindarraez, the Moor, whom the

[005-116]valiant Rodrigo de Narvaez, the Alcaide of Antequera, brings captive.”

[005-117]At these words they all hurried out, and when they recognised their

[005-118]friend, master, and uncle, who had not yet dismounted from the ass

[005-119]because he could not, they ran to embrace him.

[005-120]“Hold!” said he, “for I am badly wounded through my horse’s fault;

[005-121]carry me to bed, and if possible send for the wise Urganda to cure and

[005-122]see to my wounds.”

[005-123]“See there! plague on it!” cried the housekeeper at this: “did not my

[005-124]heart tell the truth as to which foot my master went lame of? To bed

[005-125]with your worship at once, and we will contrive to cure you here

[005-126]without fetching that Hurgada. A curse I say once more, and a hundred

[005-127]times more, on those books of chivalry that have brought your worship

[005-128]to such a pass.”

[005-129]They carried him to bed at once, and after searching for his wounds

[005-130]could find none, but he said they were all bruises from having had a

[005-131]severe fall with his horse Rocinante when in combat with ten giants,

[005-132]the biggest and the boldest to be found on earth.

[005-133]“So, so!” said the curate, “are there giants in the dance? By the sign

[005-134]of the Cross I will burn them to-morrow before the day is over.”

[005-135]They put a host of questions to Don Quixote, but his only answer to all

[005-136]was—give him something to eat, and leave him to sleep, for that was

[005-137]what he needed most. They did so, and the curate questioned the peasant

[005-138]at great length as to how he had found Don Quixote. He told him, and

[005-139]the nonsense he had talked when found and on the way home, all which

[005-140]made the licentiate the more eager to do what he did the next day,

[005-141]which was to summon his friend the barber, Master Nicholas, and go with

[005-142]him to Don Quixote’s house.
