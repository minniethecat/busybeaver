[079-001]WHEREIN IT IS SHOWN WHO MASTER PEDRO AND HIS APE WERE, TOGETHER WITH

[079-002]THE MISHAP DON QUIXOTE HAD IN THE BRAYING ADVENTURE, WHICH HE DID NOT

CONCLUDE AS HE WOULD HAVE LIKED OR AS HE HAD EXPECTED

p27a.jpg (135K)


[079-003]Cide Hamete, the chronicler of this great history, begins this chapter

[079-004]with these words, “I swear as a Catholic Christian;” with regard to

[079-005]which his translator says that Cide Hamete’s swearing as a Catholic

[079-006]Christian, he being—as no doubt he was—a Moor, only meant that, just as

[079-007]a Catholic Christian taking an oath swears, or ought to swear, what is

[079-008]true, and tell the truth in what he avers, so he was telling the truth,

[079-009]as much as if he swore as a Catholic Christian, in all he chose to

[079-010]write about Quixote, especially in declaring who Master Pedro was and

[079-011]what was the divining ape that astonished all the villages with his

[079-012]divinations. He says, then, that he who has read the First Part of this

[079-013]history will remember well enough the Gines de Pasamonte whom, with

[079-014]other galley slaves, Don Quixote set free in the Sierra Morena: a

[079-015]kindness for which he afterwards got poor thanks and worse payment from

[079-016]that evil-minded, ill-conditioned set. This Gines de Pasamonte—Don

[079-017]Ginesillo de Parapilla, Don Quixote called him—it was that stole Dapple

[079-018]from Sancho Panza; which, because by the fault of the printers neither

[079-019]the how nor the when was stated in the First Part, has been a puzzle to

[079-020]a good many people, who attribute to the bad memory of the author what

[079-021]was the error of the press. In fact, however, Gines stole him while

[079-022]Sancho Panza was asleep on his back, adopting the plan and device that

[079-023]Brunello had recourse to when he stole Sacripante’s horse from between

[079-024]his legs at the siege of Albracca; and, as has been told, Sancho

[079-025]afterwards recovered him. This Gines, then, afraid of being caught by

[079-026]the officers of justice, who were looking for him to punish him for his

[079-027]numberless rascalities and offences (which were so many and so great

[079-028]that he himself wrote a big book giving an account of them), resolved

[079-029]to shift his quarters into the kingdom of Aragon, and cover up his left

[079-030]eye, and take up the trade of a puppet-showman; for this, as well as

[079-031]juggling, he knew how to practise to perfection. From some released

[079-032]Christians returning from Barbary, it so happened, he bought the ape,

[079-033]which he taught to mount upon his shoulder on his making a certain

[079-034]sign, and to whisper, or seem to do so, in his ear. Thus prepared,

[079-035]before entering any village whither he was bound with his show and his

[079-036]ape, he used to inform himself at the nearest village, or from the most

[079-037]likely person he could find, as to what particular things had happened

[079-038]there, and to whom; and bearing them well in mind, the first thing he

[079-039]did was to exhibit his show, sometimes one story, sometimes another,

[079-040]but all lively, amusing, and familiar. As soon as the exhibition was

[079-041]over he brought forward the accomplishments of his ape, assuring the

[079-042]public that he divined all the past and the present, but as to the

[079-043]future he had no skill. For each question answered he asked two reals,

[079-044]and for some he made a reduction, just as he happened to feel the pulse

[079-045]of the questioners; and when now and then he came to houses where

[079-046]things that he knew of had happened to the people living there, even if

[079-047]they did not ask him a question, not caring to pay for it, he would

[079-048]make the sign to the ape and then declare that it had said so and so,

[079-049]which fitted the case exactly. In this way he acquired a prodigious

[079-050]name and all ran after him; on other occasions, being very crafty, he

[079-051]would answer in such a way that the answers suited the questions; and

[079-052]as no one cross-questioned him or pressed him to tell how his ape

[079-053]divined, he made fools of them all and filled his pouch. The instant he

[079-054]entered the inn he knew Don Quixote and Sancho, and with that knowledge

[079-055]it was easy for him to astonish them and all who were there; but it

[079-056]would have cost him dear had Don Quixote brought down his hand a little

[079-057]lower when he cut off King Marsilio’s head and destroyed all his

[079-058]horsemen, as related in the preceeding chapter.

[079-059]So much for Master Pedro and his ape; and now to return to Don Quixote

[079-060]of La Mancha. After he had left the inn he determined to visit, first

[079-061]of all, the banks of the Ebro and that neighbourhood, before entering

[079-062]the city of Saragossa, for the ample time there was still to spare

[079-063]before the jousts left him enough for all. With this object in view he

[079-064]followed the road and travelled along it for two days, without meeting

[079-065]any adventure worth committing to writing until on the third day, as he

[079-066]was ascending a hill, he heard a great noise of drums, trumpets, and

[079-067]musket-shots. At first he imagined some regiment of soldiers was

[079-068]passing that way, and to see them he spurred Rocinante and mounted the

[079-069]hill. On reaching the top he saw at the foot of it over two hundred

[079-070]men, as it seemed to him, armed with weapons of various sorts, lances,

[079-071]crossbows, partisans, halberds, and pikes, and a few muskets and a

[079-072]great many bucklers. He descended the slope and approached the band

[079-073]near enough to see distinctly the flags, make out the colours and

[079-074]distinguish the devices they bore, especially one on a standard or

[079-075]ensign of white satin, on which there was painted in a very life-like

[079-076]style an ass like a little sard, with its head up, its mouth open and

[079-077]its tongue out, as if it were in the act and attitude of braying; and

[079-078]round it were inscribed in large characters these two lines—

[079-079]They did not bray in vain,

[079-080]Our alcaldes twain.

[079-081]From this device Don Quixote concluded that these people must be from

[079-082]the braying town, and he said so to Sancho, explaining to him what was

[079-083]written on the standard. At the same time be observed that the man who

[079-084]had told them about the matter was wrong in saying that the two who

[079-085]brayed were regidors, for according to the lines of the standard they

[079-086]were alcaldes. To which Sancho replied, “Señor, there’s nothing to

[079-087]stick at in that, for maybe the regidors who brayed then came to be

[079-088]alcaldes of their town afterwards, and so they may go by both titles;

[079-089]moreover, it has nothing to do with the truth of the story whether the

[079-090]brayers were alcaldes or regidors, provided at any rate they did bray;

[079-091]for an alcalde is just as likely to bray as a regidor.” They perceived,

[079-092]in short, clearly that the town which had been twitted had turned out

[079-093]to do battle with some other that had jeered it more than was fair or

[079-094]neighbourly.

[079-095]Don Quixote proceeded to join them, not a little to Sancho’s

[079-096]uneasiness, for he never relished mixing himself up in expeditions of

[079-097]that sort. The members of the troop received him into the midst of

[079-098]them, taking him to be someone who was on their side. Don Quixote,

[079-099]putting up his visor, advanced with an easy bearing and demeanour to

[079-100]the standard with the ass, and all the chief men of the army gathered

[079-101]round him to look at him, staring at him with the usual amazement that

[079-102]everybody felt on seeing him for the first time. Don Quixote, seeing

[079-103]them examining him so attentively, and that none of them spoke to him

[079-104]or put any question to him, determined to take advantage of their

[079-105]silence; so, breaking his own, he lifted up his voice and said, “Worthy

[079-106]sirs, I entreat you as earnestly as I can not to interrupt an argument

[079-107]I wish to address to you, until you find it displeases or wearies you;

[079-108]and if that come to pass, on the slightest hint you give me I will put

[079-109]a seal upon my lips and a gag upon my tongue.”

[079-110]They all bade him say what he liked, for they would listen to him

[079-111]willingly.

[079-112]With this permission Don Quixote went on to say, “I, sirs, am a

[079-113]knight-errant whose calling is that of arms, and whose profession is to

[079-114]protect those who require protection, and give help to such as stand in

[079-115]need of it. Some days ago I became acquainted with your misfortune and

[079-116]the cause which impels you to take up arms again and again to revenge

[079-117]yourselves upon your enemies; and having many times thought over your

[079-118]business in my mind, I find that, according to the laws of combat, you

[079-119]are mistaken in holding yourselves insulted; for a private individual

[079-120]cannot insult an entire community; unless it be by defying it

[079-121]collectively as a traitor, because he cannot tell who in particular is

[079-122]guilty of the treason for which he defies it. Of this we have an

[079-123]example in Don Diego Ordoñez de Lara, who defied the whole town of

[079-124]Zamora, because he did not know that Vellido Dolfos alone had committed

[079-125]the treachery of slaying his king; and therefore he defied them all,

[079-126]and the vengeance and the reply concerned all; though, to be sure,

[079-127]Señor Don Diego went rather too far, indeed very much beyond the limits

[079-128]of a defiance; for he had no occasion to defy the dead, or the waters,

[079-129]or the fishes, or those yet unborn, and all the rest of it as set

[079-130]forth; but let that pass, for when anger breaks out there’s no father,

[079-131]governor, or bridle to check the tongue. The case being, then, that no

[079-132]one person can insult a kingdom, province, city, state, or entire

[079-133]community, it is clear there is no reason for going out to avenge the

[079-134]defiance of such an insult, inasmuch as it is not one. A fine thing it

[079-135]would be if the people of the clock town were to be at loggerheads

[079-136]every moment with everyone who called them by that name,—or the

[079-137]Cazoleros, Berengeneros, Ballenatos, Jaboneros, or the bearers of all

[079-138]the other names and titles that are always in the mouth of the boys and

[079-139]common people! It would be a nice business indeed if all these

[079-140]illustrious cities were to take huff and revenge themselves and go

[079-141]about perpetually making trombones of their swords in every petty

[079-142]quarrel! No, no; God forbid! There are four things for which sensible

[079-143]men and well-ordered States ought to take up arms, draw their swords,

[079-144]and risk their persons, lives, and properties. The first is to defend

[079-145]the Catholic faith; the second, to defend one’s life, which is in

[079-146]accordance with natural and divine law; the third, in defence of one’s

[079-147]honour, family, and property; the fourth, in the service of one’s king

[079-148]in a just war; and if to these we choose to add a fifth (which may be

[079-149]included in the second), in defence of one’s country. To these five, as

[079-150]it were capital causes, there may be added some others that may be just

[079-151]and reasonable, and make it a duty to take up arms; but to take them up

[079-152]for trifles and things to laugh at and be amused by rather than

[079-153]offended, looks as though he who did so was altogether wanting in

[079-154]common sense. Moreover, to take an unjust revenge (and there cannot be

[079-155]any just one) is directly opposed to the sacred law that we

[079-156]acknowledge, wherein we are commanded to do good to our enemies and to

[079-157]love them that hate us; a command which, though it seems somewhat

[079-158]difficult to obey, is only so to those who have in them less of God

[079-159]than of the world, and more of the flesh than of the spirit; for Jesus

[079-160]Christ, God and true man, who never lied, and could not and cannot lie,

[079-161]said, as our law-giver, that his yoke was easy and his burden light; he

[079-162]would not, therefore, have laid any command upon us that it was

[079-163]impossible to obey. Thus, sirs, you are bound to keep quiet by human

[079-164]and divine law.”

[079-165]“The devil take me,” said Sancho to himself at this, “but this master

[079-166]of mine is a theologian; or, if not, faith, he’s as like one as one egg

[079-167]is like another.”

[079-168]Don Quixote stopped to take breath, and, observing that silence was

[079-169]still preserved, had a mind to continue his discourse, and would have

[079-170]done so had not Sancho interposed with his smartness; for he, seeing

[079-171]his master pause, took the lead, saying, “My lord Don Quixote of La

[079-172]Mancha, who once was called the Knight of the Rueful Countenance, but

[079-173]now is called the Knight of the Lions, is a gentleman of great

[079-174]discretion who knows Latin and his mother tongue like a bachelor, and

[079-175]in everything that he deals with or advises proceeds like a good

[079-176]soldier, and has all the laws and ordinances of what they call combat

[079-177]at his fingers’ ends; so you have nothing to do but to let yourselves

[079-178]be guided by what he says, and on my head be it if it is wrong. Besides

[079-179]which, you have been told that it is folly to take offence at merely

[079-180]hearing a bray. I remember when I was a boy I brayed as often as I had

[079-181]a fancy, without anyone hindering me, and so elegantly and naturally

[079-182]that when I brayed all the asses in the town would bray; but I was none

[079-183]the less for that the son of my parents who were greatly respected; and

[079-184]though I was envied because of the gift by more than one of the high

[079-185]and mighty ones of the town, I did not care two farthings for it; and

[079-186]that you may see I am telling the truth, wait a bit and listen, for

[079-187]this art, like swimming, once learnt is never forgotten;” and then,

[079-188]taking hold of his nose, he began to bray so vigorously that all the

[079-189]valleys around rang again.

[079-190]One of those, however, that stood near him, fancying he was mocking

[079-191]them, lifted up a long staff he had in his hand and smote him such a

[079-192]blow with it that Sancho dropped helpless to the ground. Don Quixote,

[079-193]seeing him so roughly handled, attacked the man who had struck him

[079-194]lance in hand, but so many thrust themselves between them that he could

[079-195]not avenge him. Far from it, finding a shower of stones rained upon

[079-196]him, and crossbows and muskets unnumbered levelled at him, he wheeled

[079-197]Rocinante round and, as fast as his best gallop could take him, fled

[079-198]from the midst of them, commending himself to God with all his heart to

[079-199]deliver him out of this peril, in dread every step of some ball coming

[079-200]in at his back and coming out at his breast, and every minute drawing

[079-201]his breath to see whether it had gone from him. The members of the

[079-202]band, however, were satisfied with seeing him take to flight, and did

[079-203]not fire on him. They put up Sancho, scarcely restored to his senses,

[079-204]on his ass, and let him go after his master; not that he was

[079-205]sufficiently in his wits to guide the beast, but Dapple followed the

[079-206]footsteps of Rocinante, from whom he could not remain a moment

[079-207]separated. Don Quixote having got some way off looked back, and seeing

[079-208]Sancho coming, waited for him, as he perceived that no one followed

[079-209]him. The men of the troop stood their ground till night, and as the

[079-210]enemy did not come out to battle, they returned to their town exulting;

[079-211]and had they been aware of the ancient custom of the Greeks, they would

[079-212]have erected a trophy on the spot.
