[107-001]OF WHAT BEFELL SANCHO ON THE ROAD, AND OTHER THINGS THAT CANNOT BE

SURPASSED

[107-002]The length of time he delayed with Ricote prevented Sancho from

[107-003]reaching the duke’s castle that day, though he was within half a league

[107-004]of it when night, somewhat dark and cloudy, overtook him. This,

[107-005]however, as it was summer time, did not give him much uneasiness, and

[107-006]he turned aside out of the road intending to wait for morning; but his

[107-007]ill luck and hard fate so willed it that as he was searching about for

[107-008]a place to make himself as comfortable as possible, he and Dapple fell

[107-009]into a deep dark hole that lay among some very old buildings. As he

[107-010]fell he commended himself with all his heart to God, fancying he was

[107-011]not going to stop until he reached the depths of the bottomless pit;

[107-012]but it did not turn out so, for at little more than thrice a man’s

[107-013]height Dapple touched bottom, and he found himself sitting on him

[107-014]without having received any hurt or damage whatever. He felt himself

[107-015]all over and held his breath to try whether he was quite sound or had a

[107-016]hole made in him anywhere, and finding himself all right and whole and

[107-017]in perfect health he was profuse in his thanks to God our Lord for the

[107-018]mercy that had been shown him, for he made sure he had been broken into

[107-019]a thousand pieces. He also felt along the sides of the pit with his

[107-020]hands to see if it were possible to get out of it without help, but he

[107-021]found they were quite smooth and afforded no hold anywhere, at which he

[107-022]was greatly distressed, especially when he heard how pathetically and

[107-023]dolefully Dapple was bemoaning himself, and no wonder he complained,

[107-024]nor was it from ill-temper, for in truth he was not in a very good

[107-025]case. “Alas,” said Sancho, “what unexpected accidents happen at every

[107-026]step to those who live in this miserable world! Who would have said

[107-027]that one who saw himself yesterday sitting on a throne, governor of an

[107-028]island, giving orders to his servants and his vassals, would see

[107-029]himself to-day buried in a pit without a soul to help him, or servant

[107-030]or vassal to come to his relief? Here must we perish with hunger, my

[107-031]ass and myself, if indeed we don’t die first, he of his bruises and

[107-032]injuries, and I of grief and sorrow. At any rate I’ll not be as lucky

[107-033]as my master Don Quixote of La Mancha, when he went down into the cave

[107-034]of that enchanted Montesinos, where he found people to make more of him

[107-035]than if he had been in his own house; for it seems he came in for a

[107-036]table laid out and a bed ready made. There he saw fair and pleasant

[107-037]visions, but here I’ll see, I imagine, toads and adders. Unlucky wretch

[107-038]that I am, what an end my follies and fancies have come to! They’ll

[107-039]take up my bones out of this, when it is heaven’s will that I’m found,

[107-040]picked clean, white and polished, and my good Dapple’s with them, and

[107-041]by that, perhaps, it will be found out who we are, at least by such as

[107-042]have heard that Sancho Panza never separated from his ass, nor his ass

[107-043]from Sancho Panza. Unlucky wretches, I say again, that our hard fate

[107-044]should not let us die in our own country and among our own people,

[107-045]where if there was no help for our misfortune, at any rate there would

[107-046]be someone to grieve for it and to close our eyes as we passed away! O

[107-047]comrade and friend, how ill have I repaid thy faithful services!

[107-048]Forgive me, and entreat Fortune, as well as thou canst, to deliver us

[107-049]out of this miserable strait we are both in; and I promise to put a

[107-050]crown of laurel on thy head, and make thee look like a poet laureate,

[107-051]and give thee double feeds.”

[107-052]In this strain did Sancho bewail himself, and his ass listened to him,

[107-053]but answered him never a word, such was the distress and anguish the

[107-054]poor beast found himself in. At length, after a night spent in bitter

[107-055]moanings and lamentations, day came, and by its light Sancho perceived

[107-056]that it was wholly impossible to escape out of that pit without help,

[107-057]and he fell to bemoaning his fate and uttering loud shouts to find out

[107-058]if there was anyone within hearing; but all his shouting was only

[107-059]crying in the wilderness, for there was not a soul anywhere in the

[107-060]neighbourhood to hear him, and then at last he gave himself up for

[107-061]dead. Dapple was lying on his back, and Sancho helped him to his feet,

[107-062]which he was scarcely able to keep; and then taking a piece of bread

[107-063]out of his alforjas which had shared their fortunes in the fall, he

[107-064]gave it to the ass, to whom it was not unwelcome, saying to him as if

[107-065]he understood him, “With bread all sorrows are less.”

[107-066]And now he perceived on one side of the pit a hole large enough to

[107-067]admit a person if he stooped and squeezed himself into a small compass.

[107-068]Sancho made for it, and entered it by creeping, and found it wide and

[107-069]spacious on the inside, which he was able to see as a ray of sunlight

[107-070]that penetrated what might be called the roof showed it all plainly. He

[107-071]observed too that it opened and widened out into another spacious

[107-072]cavity; seeing which he made his way back to where the ass was, and

[107-073]with a stone began to pick away the clay from the hole until in a short

[107-074]time he had made room for the beast to pass easily, and this

[107-075]accomplished, taking him by the halter, he proceeded to traverse the

[107-076]cavern to see if there was any outlet at the other end. He advanced,

[107-077]sometimes in the dark, sometimes without light, but never without fear;

[107-078]“God Almighty help me!” said he to himself; “this that is a

[107-079]misadventure to me would make a good adventure for my master Don

[107-080]Quixote. He would have been sure to take these depths and dungeons for

[107-081]flowery gardens or the palaces of Galiana, and would have counted upon

[107-082]issuing out of this darkness and imprisonment into some blooming

[107-083]meadow; but I, unlucky that I am, hopeless and spiritless, expect at

[107-084]every step another pit deeper than the first to open under my feet and

[107-085]swallow me up for good; ‘welcome evil, if thou comest alone.’”

[107-086]In this way and with these reflections he seemed to himself to have

[107-087]travelled rather more than half a league, when at last he perceived a

[107-088]dim light that looked like daylight and found its way in on one side,

[107-089]showing that this road, which appeared to him the road to the other

[107-090]world, led to some opening.

[107-091]Here Cide Hamete leaves him, and returns to Don Quixote, who in high

[107-092]spirits and satisfaction was looking forward to the day fixed for the

[107-093]battle he was to fight with him who had robbed Doña Rodriguez’s

[107-094]daughter of her honour, for whom he hoped to obtain satisfaction for

[107-095]the wrong and injury shamefully done to her. It came to pass, then,

[107-096]that having sallied forth one morning to practise and exercise himself

[107-097]in what he would have to do in the encounter he expected to find

[107-098]himself engaged in the next day, as he was putting Rocinante through

[107-099]his paces or pressing him to the charge, he brought his feet so close

[107-100]to a pit that but for reining him in tightly it would have been

[107-101]impossible for him to avoid falling into it. He pulled him up, however,

[107-102]without a fall, and coming a little closer examined the hole without

[107-103]dismounting; but as he was looking at it he heard loud cries proceeding

[107-104]from it, and by listening attentively was able to make out that he who

[107-105]uttered them was saying, “Ho, above there! is there any Christian that

[107-106]hears me, or any charitable gentleman that will take pity on a sinner

[107-107]buried alive, on an unfortunate disgoverned governor?”

[107-108]It struck Don Quixote that it was the voice of Sancho Panza he heard,

[107-109]whereat he was taken aback and amazed, and raising his own voice as

[107-110]much as he could, he cried out, “Who is below there? Who is that

[107-111]complaining?”

[107-112]“Who should be here, or who should complain,” was the answer, “but the

[107-113]forlorn Sancho Panza, for his sins and for his ill-luck governor of the

[107-114]island of Barataria, squire that was to the famous knight Don Quixote

[107-115]of La Mancha?”

[107-116]When Don Quixote heard this his amazement was redoubled and his

[107-117]perturbation grew greater than ever, for it suggested itself to his

[107-118]mind that Sancho must be dead, and that his soul was in torment down

[107-119]there; and carried away by this idea he exclaimed, “I conjure thee by

[107-120]everything that as a Catholic Christian I can conjure thee by, tell me

[107-121]who thou art; and if thou art a soul in torment, tell me what thou

[107-122]wouldst have me do for thee; for as my profession is to give aid and

[107-123]succour to those that need it in this world, it will also extend to

[107-124]aiding and succouring the distressed of the other, who cannot help

[107-125]themselves.”

[107-126]“In that case,” answered the voice, “your worship who speaks to me must

[107-127]be my master Don Quixote of La Mancha; nay, from the tone of the voice

[107-128]it is plain it can be nobody else.”

[107-129]“Don Quixote I am,” replied Don Quixote, “he whose profession it is to

[107-130]aid and succour the living and the dead in their necessities; wherefore

[107-131]tell me who thou art, for thou art keeping me in suspense; because, if

[107-132]thou art my squire Sancho Panza, and art dead, since the devils have

[107-133]not carried thee off, and thou art by God’s mercy in purgatory, our

[107-134]holy mother the Roman Catholic Church has intercessory means sufficient

[107-135]to release thee from the pains thou art in; and I for my part will

[107-136]plead with her to that end, so far as my substance will go; without

[107-137]further delay, therefore, declare thyself, and tell me who thou art.”

[107-138]“By all that’s good,” was the answer, “and by the birth of whomsoever

[107-139]your worship chooses, I swear, Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha, that I

[107-140]am your squire Sancho Panza, and that I have never died all my life;

[107-141]but that, having given up my government for reasons that would require

[107-142]more time to explain, I fell last night into this pit where I am now,

[107-143]and Dapple is witness and won’t let me lie, for more by token he is

[107-144]here with me.”

[107-145]Nor was this all; one would have fancied the ass understood what Sancho

[107-146]said, because that moment he began to bray so loudly that the whole

[107-147]cave rang again.

[107-148]“Famous testimony!” exclaimed Don Quixote; “I know that bray as well as

[107-149]if I was its mother, and thy voice too, my Sancho. Wait while I go to

[107-150]the duke’s castle, which is close by, and I will bring someone to take

[107-151]thee out of this pit into which thy sins no doubt have brought thee.”

[107-152]“Go, your worship,” said Sancho, “and come back quick for God’s sake;

[107-153]for I cannot bear being buried alive any longer, and I’m dying of

[107-154]fear.”

[107-155]Don Quixote left him, and hastened to the castle to tell the duke and

[107-156]duchess what had happened Sancho, and they were not a little astonished

[107-157]at it; they could easily understand his having fallen, from the

[107-158]confirmatory circumstance of the cave which had been in existence there

[107-159]from time immemorial; but they could not imagine how he had quitted the

[107-160]government without their receiving any intimation of his coming. To be

[107-161]brief, they fetched ropes and tackle, as the saying is, and by dint of

[107-162]many hands and much labour they drew up Dapple and Sancho Panza out of

[107-163]the darkness into the light of day. A student who saw him remarked,

[107-164]“That’s the way all bad governors should come out of their governments,

[107-165]as this sinner comes out of the depths of the pit, dead with hunger,

[107-166]pale, and I suppose without a farthing.”

[107-167]Sancho overheard him and said, “It is eight or ten days, brother

[107-168]growler, since I entered upon the government of the island they gave

[107-169]me, and all that time I never had a bellyful of victuals, no not for an

[107-170]hour; doctors persecuted me and enemies crushed my bones; nor had I any

[107-171]opportunity of taking bribes or levying taxes; and if that be the case,

[107-172]as it is, I don’t deserve, I think, to come out in this fashion; but

[107-173]‘man proposes and God disposes;’ and God knows what is best, and what

[107-174]suits each one best; and ‘as the occasion, so the behaviour;’ and ‘let

[107-175]nobody say “I won’t drink of this water;”’ and ‘where one thinks there

[107-176]are flitches, there are no pegs;’ God knows my meaning and that’s

[107-177]enough; I say no more, though I could.”

[107-178]“Be not angry or annoyed at what thou hearest, Sancho,” said Don

[107-179]Quixote, “or there will never be an end of it; keep a safe conscience

[107-180]and let them say what they like; for trying to stop slanderers’ tongues

[107-181]is like trying to put gates to the open plain. If a governor comes out

[107-182]of his government rich, they say he has been a thief; and if he comes

[107-183]out poor, that he has been a noodle and a blockhead.”

[107-184]“They’ll be pretty sure this time,” said Sancho, “to set me down for a

[107-185]fool rather than a thief.”

[107-186]Thus talking, and surrounded by boys and a crowd of people, they

[107-187]reached the castle, where in one of the corridors the duke and duchess

[107-188]stood waiting for them; but Sancho would not go up to see the duke

[107-189]until he had first put up Dapple in the stable, for he said he had

[107-190]passed a very bad night in his last quarters; then he went upstairs to

[107-191]see his lord and lady, and kneeling before them he said, “Because it

[107-192]was your highnesses’ pleasure, not because of any desert of my own, I

[107-193]went to govern your island of Barataria, which ‘I entered naked, and

[107-194]naked I find myself; I neither lose nor gain.’ Whether I have governed

[107-195]well or ill, I have had witnesses who will say what they think fit. I

[107-196]have answered questions, I have decided causes, and always dying of

[107-197]hunger, for Doctor Pedro Recio of Tirteafuera, the island and governor

[107-198]doctor, would have it so. Enemies attacked us by night and put us in a

[107-199]great quandary, but the people of the island say they came off safe and

[107-200]victorious by the might of my arm; and may God give them as much health

[107-201]as there’s truth in what they say. In short, during that time I have

[107-202]weighed the cares and responsibilities governing brings with it, and by

[107-203]my reckoning I find my shoulders can’t bear them, nor are they a load

[107-204]for my loins or arrows for my quiver; and so, before the government

[107-205]threw me over I preferred to throw the government over; and yesterday

[107-206]morning I left the island as I found it, with the same streets, houses,

[107-207]and roofs it had when I entered it. I asked no loan of anybody, nor did

[107-208]I try to fill my pocket; and though I meant to make some useful laws, I

[107-209]made hardly any, as I was afraid they would not be kept; for in that

[107-210]case it comes to the same thing to make them or not to make them. I

[107-211]quitted the island, as I said, without any escort except my ass; I fell

[107-212]into a pit, I pushed on through it, until this morning by the light of

[107-213]the sun I saw an outlet, but not so easy a one but that, had not heaven

[107-214]sent me my master Don Quixote, I’d have stayed there till the end of

[107-215]the world. So now my lord and lady duke and duchess, here is your

[107-216]governor Sancho Panza, who in the bare ten days he has held the

[107-217]government has come by the knowledge that he would not give anything to

[107-218]be governor, not to say of an island, but of the whole world; and that

[107-219]point being settled, kissing your worships’ feet, and imitating the

[107-220]game of the boys when they say, ‘leap thou, and give me one,’ I take a

[107-221]leap out of the government and pass into the service of my master Don

[107-222]Quixote; for after all, though in it I eat my bread in fear and

[107-223]trembling, at any rate I take my fill; and for my part, so long as I’m

[107-224]full, it’s all alike to me whether it’s with carrots or with

[107-225]partridges.”

[107-226]Here Sancho brought his long speech to an end, Don Quixote having been

[107-227]the whole time in dread of his uttering a host of absurdities; and when

[107-228]he found him leave off with so few, he thanked heaven in his heart. The

[107-229]duke embraced Sancho and told him he was heartily sorry he had given up

[107-230]the government so soon, but that he would see that he was provided with

[107-231]some other post on his estate less onerous and more profitable. The

[107-232]duchess also embraced him, and gave orders that he should be taken good

[107-233]care of, as it was plain to see he had been badly treated and worse

[107-234]bruised.
