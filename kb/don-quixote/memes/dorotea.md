# Dorotea

**Dorotea** is a young woman of exceptional beauty, intelligence, and resourcefulness who appears during the Sierra Morena arc of Part I. Discovered by the curate and barber while they search for Don Quixote, she agrees to play the role of **Princess Micomicona** — a fictional African princess seeking a champion — to help lure the knight back to his village. Her own story, however, is one of genuine tragedy and eventual triumph.

## Character Profile

Dorotea is introduced as a peasant girl washing her feet in a stream — but the narrator immediately signals that she is no ordinary peasant:

> "you feel, fairest Dorothea (for that is the name of this unhappy lady)"[028-256]

She is the daughter of a wealthy farmer, educated above her station, and sufficiently "shrewd and sprightly" to improvise an elaborate fictional kingdom on the spot:

> "Dorothea, who was shrewd and sprightly, seeing the simplicity of the curate and the barber, took the opportunity of carrying on the jest"[030-023]

Her beauty is described in extravagant terms throughout the narrative — when Cardenio first hears her name, "he showed fresh agitation" — but Cervantes emphasizes her intelligence above her appearance.

## Her Story: Betrayal by Don Fernando

Dorotea's personal narrative is one of the novel's most compelling subplots. She tells how [[don-fernando|Don Fernando]], the younger son of a duke, courted her with promises of marriage, seduced her, and then abandoned her to pursue [[luscinda|Luscinda]]. Her account is direct and emotionally powerful:

> "I, Dorothea, am he who witnessed the wrongs I suffer"[029-034]

Unlike many literary heroines of the period, Dorotea does not merely lament her fate — she pursues Don Fernando into the mountains, determined to confront him or die. Her active pursuit of justice distinguishes her from more passive female characters in the novel.

## Princess Micomicona

The curate recruits Dorotea to impersonate **Princess Micomicona of Guinea**, who has supposedly traveled to Spain seeking a knight to slay a giant and restore her kingdom. Dorotea's improvisation is masterful — she invents a detailed genealogy, a giant named Pandafilando, and a plea for Don Quixote's aid:

> "this is my story; it only remains for me to tell you that I have come to this kingdom in search of a remedy"[030-157]

Her performance is so convincing that Sancho Panza is genuinely distressed when Don Quixote initially refuses to undertake the quest (because he had not yet completed his penance). The scene is comic and poignant: a wronged woman pretending to be a princess, manipulating a madman who believes every word.

## Resolution

Dorotea's story resolves at Juan Palomeque's inn, where all the Sierra Morena characters converge. Don Fernando, confronted by Dorotea's presence and Cardenio's testimony, repents and agrees to honor his promise of marriage. Dorotea accepts him — a resolution that has troubled modern readers but reflects the social realities of Golden Age Spain, where a woman's honor depended on marriage.

## Thematic Significance

Dorotea represents the tension between passive virtue and active agency. She is wronged as completely as any character in the novel, yet she refuses to be a victim. Her willingness to perform — as Princess Micomicona — mirrors the novel's broader concern with identity, disguise, and the permeable boundary between truth and fiction.
