## Overview

*Amadís de Gaula* is the foundational Spanish chivalric romance, compiled and revised by Garci Rodríguez de Montalvo and first published in Zaragoza in 1508. The work narrates the adventures of Amadís, the prototypical knight-errant, and his squire Gandalin. For Don Quixote, Amadís is not merely a book but a template for how a knight should live, fight, and love — he explicitly models his penance in the Sierra Morena on Amadís's "beautiful penitence" on the Peña Pobre.

## In Don Quixote

Amadís de Gaula is the most frequently invoked chivalric romance in Cervantes' novel. It functions as the yardstick against which Don Quixote measures all knightly conduct. In the opening chapter, Alonso Quijano's obsession with Amadís drives his transformation into Don Quixote [001-050]. The novel's library scrutiny scene (Chapter 6) treats Amadís with surprising respect — the curate and barber debate whether to burn it, and it is ultimately spared as "the best of all the books of this kind" [006-024]. Throughout both parts, Don Quixote repeatedly invokes Amadís as precedent for his own actions, from his vow of silence [025-097] to his defense of knightly love [013-065].

## Literary Significance

Cervantes' relationship with Amadís is ambivalent and sophisticated. The book is simultaneously the object of parody (Don Quixote's absurd imitation of Amadís's penance) and a work the author genuinely admires (the curate spares it from the fire). This duality — mocking the chivalric mode while respecting its literary achievements — is central to *Don Quixote*'s status as both the death of the chivalric romance and the birth of the modern novel. The intertextual relationship with Amadís allows Cervantes to critique not just a genre but an entire worldview, while also acknowledging the narrative power that made that worldview so seductive in the first place.
