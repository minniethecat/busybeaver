[033-001]IN WHICH IS RELATED THE NOVEL OF “THE ILL-ADVISED CURIOSITY”

[033-002]In Florence, a rich and famous city of Italy in the province called

[033-003]Tuscany, there lived two gentlemen of wealth and quality, Anselmo and

[033-004]Lothario, such great friends that by way of distinction they were

[033-005]called by all that knew them “The Two Friends.” They were unmarried,

[033-006]young, of the same age and of the same tastes, which was enough to

[033-007]account for the reciprocal friendship between them. Anselmo, it is

[033-008]true, was somewhat more inclined to seek pleasure in love than

[033-009]Lothario, for whom the pleasures of the chase had more attraction; but

[033-010]on occasion Anselmo would forego his own tastes to yield to those of

[033-011]Lothario, and Lothario would surrender his to fall in with those of

[033-012]Anselmo, and in this way their inclinations kept pace one with the

[033-013]other with a concord so perfect that the best regulated clock could not

[033-014]surpass it.

[033-015]Anselmo was deep in love with a high-born and beautiful maiden of the

[033-016]same city, the daughter of parents so estimable, and so estimable

[033-017]herself, that he resolved, with the approval of his friend Lothario,

[033-018]without whom he did nothing, to ask her of them in marriage, and did

[033-019]so, Lothario being the bearer of the demand, and conducting the

[033-020]negotiation so much to the satisfaction of his friend that in a short

[033-021]time he was in possession of the object of his desires, and Camilla so

[033-022]happy in having won Anselmo for her husband, that she gave thanks

[033-023]unceasingly to heaven and to Lothario, by whose means such good fortune

[033-024]had fallen to her. The first few days, those of a wedding being usually

[033-025]days of merry-making, Lothario frequented his friend Anselmo’s house as

[033-026]he had been wont, striving to do honour to him and to the occasion, and

[033-027]to gratify him in every way he could; but when the wedding days were

[033-028]over and the succession of visits and congratulations had slackened, he

[033-029]began purposely to leave off going to the house of Anselmo, for it

[033-030]seemed to him, as it naturally would to all men of sense, that friends’

[033-031]houses ought not to be visited after marriage with the same frequency

[033-032]as in their masters’ bachelor days: because, though true and genuine

[033-033]friendship cannot and should not be in any way suspicious, still a

[033-034]married man’s honour is a thing of such delicacy that it is held liable

[033-035]to injury from brothers, much more from friends. Anselmo remarked the

[033-036]cessation of Lothario’s visits, and complained of it to him, saying

[033-037]that if he had known that marriage was to keep him from enjoying his

[033-038]society as he used, he would have never married; and that, if by the

[033-039]thorough harmony that subsisted between them while he was a bachelor

[033-040]they had earned such a sweet name as that of “The Two Friends,” he

[033-041]should not allow a title so rare and so delightful to be lost through a

[033-042]needless anxiety to act circumspectly; and so he entreated him, if such

[033-043]a phrase was allowable between them, to be once more master of his

[033-044]house and to come in and go out as formerly, assuring him that his wife

[033-045]Camilla had no other desire or inclination than that which he would

[033-046]wish her to have, and that knowing how sincerely they loved one another

[033-047]she was grieved to see such coldness in him.

[033-048]To all this and much more that Anselmo said to Lothario to persuade him

[033-049]to come to his house as he had been in the habit of doing, Lothario

[033-050]replied with so much prudence, sense, and judgment, that Anselmo was

[033-051]satisfied of his friend’s good intentions, and it was agreed that on

[033-052]two days in the week, and on holidays, Lothario should come to dine

[033-053]with him; but though this arrangement was made between them Lothario

[033-054]resolved to observe it no further than he considered to be in

[033-055]accordance with the honour of his friend, whose good name was more to

[033-056]him than his own. He said, and justly, that a married man upon whom

[033-057]heaven had bestowed a beautiful wife should consider as carefully what

[033-058]friends he brought to his house as what female friends his wife

[033-059]associated with, for what cannot be done or arranged in the

[033-060]market-place, in church, at public festivals or at stations

[033-061](opportunities that husbands cannot always deny their wives), may be

[033-062]easily managed in the house of the female friend or relative in whom

[033-063]most confidence is reposed. Lothario said, too, that every married man

[033-064]should have some friend who would point out to him any negligence he

[033-065]might be guilty of in his conduct, for it will sometimes happen that

[033-066]owing to the deep affection the husband bears his wife either he does

[033-067]not caution her, or, not to vex her, refrains from telling her to do or

[033-068]not to do certain things, doing or avoiding which may be a matter of

[033-069]honour or reproach to him; and errors of this kind he could easily

[033-070]correct if warned by a friend. But where is such a friend to be found

[033-071]as Lothario would have, so judicious, so loyal, and so true?

[033-072]Of a truth I know not; Lothario alone was such a one, for with the

[033-073]utmost care and vigilance he watched over the honour of his friend, and

[033-074]strove to diminish, cut down, and reduce the number of days for going

[033-075]to his house according to their agreement, lest the visits of a young

[033-076]man, wealthy, high-born, and with the attractions he was conscious of

[033-077]possessing, at the house of a woman so beautiful as Camilla, should be

[033-078]regarded with suspicion by the inquisitive and malicious eyes of the

[033-079]idle public. For though his integrity and reputation might bridle

[033-080]slanderous tongues, still he was unwilling to hazard either his own

[033-081]good name or that of his friend; and for this reason most of the days

[033-082]agreed upon he devoted to some other business which he pretended was

[033-083]unavoidable; so that a great portion of the day was taken up with

[033-084]complaints on one side and excuses on the other. It happened, however,

[033-085]that on one occasion when the two were strolling together outside the

[033-086]city, Anselmo addressed the following words to Lothario.

[033-087]“Thou mayest suppose, Lothario my friend, that I am unable to give

[033-088]sufficient thanks for the favours God has rendered me in making me the

[033-089]son of such parents as mine were, and bestowing upon me with no niggard

[033-090]hand what are called the gifts of nature as well as those of fortune,

[033-091]and above all for what he has done in giving me thee for a friend and

[033-092]Camilla for a wife—two treasures that I value, if not as highly as I

[033-093]ought, at least as highly as I am able. And yet, with all these good

[033-094]things, which are commonly all that men need to enable them to live

[033-095]happily, I am the most discontented and dissatisfied man in the whole

[033-096]world; for, I know not how long since, I have been harassed and

[033-097]oppressed by a desire so strange and so unusual, that I wonder at

[033-098]myself and blame and chide myself when I am alone, and strive to stifle

[033-099]it and hide it from my own thoughts, and with no better success than if

[033-100]I were endeavouring deliberately to publish it to all the world; and

[033-101]as, in short, it must come out, I would confide it to thy safe keeping,

[033-102]feeling sure that by this means, and by thy readiness as a true friend

[033-103]to afford me relief, I shall soon find myself freed from the distress

[033-104]it causes me, and that thy care will give me happiness in the same

[033-105]degree as my own folly has caused me misery.”

[033-106]The words of Anselmo struck Lothario with astonishment, unable as he

[033-107]was to conjecture the purport of such a lengthy preamble; and though he

[033-108]strove to imagine what desire it could be that so troubled his friend,

[033-109]his conjectures were all far from the truth, and to relieve the anxiety

[033-110]which this perplexity was causing him, he told him he was doing a

[033-111]flagrant injustice to their great friendship in seeking circuitous

[033-112]methods of confiding to him his most hidden thoughts, for he well knew

[033-113]he might reckon upon his counsel in diverting them, or his help in

[033-114]carrying them into effect.

[033-115]“That is the truth,” replied Anselmo, “and relying upon that I will

[033-116]tell thee, friend Lothario, that the desire which harasses me is that

[033-117]of knowing whether my wife Camilla is as good and as perfect as I think

[033-118]her to be; and I cannot satisfy myself of the truth on this point

[033-119]except by testing her in such a way that the trial may prove the purity

[033-120]of her virtue as the fire proves that of gold; because I am persuaded,

[033-121]my friend, that a woman is virtuous only in proportion as she is or is

[033-122]not tempted; and that she alone is strong who does not yield to the

[033-123]promises, gifts, tears, and importunities of earnest lovers; for what

[033-124]thanks does a woman deserve for being good if no one urges her to be

[033-125]bad, and what wonder is it that she is reserved and circumspect to whom

[033-126]no opportunity is given of going wrong and who knows she has a husband

[033-127]that will take her life the first time he detects her in an

[033-128]impropriety? I do not therefore hold her who is virtuous through fear

[033-129]or want of opportunity in the same estimation as her who comes out of

[033-130]temptation and trial with a crown of victory; and so, for these reasons

[033-131]and many others that I could give thee to justify and support the

[033-132]opinion I hold, I am desirous that my wife Camilla should pass this

[033-133]crisis, and be refined and tested by the fire of finding herself wooed

[033-134]and by one worthy to set his affections upon her; and if she comes out,

[033-135]as I know she will, victorious from this struggle, I shall look upon my

[033-136]good fortune as unequalled, I shall be able to say that the cup of my

[033-137]desire is full, and that the virtuous woman of whom the sage says ‘Who

[033-138]shall find her?’ has fallen to my lot. And if the result be the

[033-139]contrary of what I expect, in the satisfaction of knowing that I have

[033-140]been right in my opinion, I shall bear without complaint the pain which

[033-141]my so dearly bought experience will naturally cause me. And, as nothing

[033-142]of all thou wilt urge in opposition to my wish will avail to keep me

[033-143]from carrying it into effect, it is my desire, friend Lothario, that

[033-144]thou shouldst consent to become the instrument for effecting this

[033-145]purpose that I am bent upon, for I will afford thee opportunities to

[033-146]that end, and nothing shall be wanting that I may think necessary for

[033-147]the pursuit of a virtuous, honourable, modest and high-minded woman.

[033-148]And among other reasons, I am induced to entrust this arduous task to

[033-149]thee by the consideration that if Camilla be conquered by thee the

[033-150]conquest will not be pushed to extremes, but only far enough to account

[033-151]that accomplished which from a sense of honour will be left undone;

[033-152]thus I shall not be wronged in anything more than intention, and my

[033-153]wrong will remain buried in the integrity of thy silence, which I know

[033-154]well will be as lasting as that of death in what concerns me. If,

[033-155]therefore, thou wouldst have me enjoy what can be called life, thou

[033-156]wilt at once engage in this love struggle, not lukewarmly nor

[033-157]slothfully, but with the energy and zeal that my desire demands, and

[033-158]with the loyalty our friendship assures me of.”

[033-159]Such were the words Anselmo addressed to Lothario, who listened to them

[033-160]with such attention that, except to say what has been already

[033-161]mentioned, he did not open his lips until the other had finished. Then

[033-162]perceiving that he had no more to say, after regarding him for a while,

[033-163]as one would regard something never before seen that excited wonder and

[033-164]amazement, he said to him, “I cannot persuade myself, Anselmo my

[033-165]friend, that what thou hast said to me is not in jest; if I thought

[033-166]that thou wert speaking seriously I would not have allowed thee to go

[033-167]so far; so as to put a stop to thy long harangue by not listening to

[033-168]thee I verily suspect that either thou dost not know me, or I do not

[033-169]know thee; but no, I know well thou art Anselmo, and thou knowest that

[033-170]I am Lothario; the misfortune is, it seems to me, that thou art not the

[033-171]Anselmo thou wert, and must have thought that I am not the Lothario I

[033-172]should be; for the things that thou hast said to me are not those of

[033-173]that Anselmo who was my friend, nor are those that thou demandest of me

[033-174]what should be asked of the Lothario thou knowest. True friends will

[033-175]prove their friends and make use of them, as a poet has said, _usque ad

[033-176]aras;_ whereby he meant that they will not make use of their friendship

[033-177]in things that are contrary to God’s will. If this, then, was a

[033-178]heathen’s feeling about friendship, how much more should it be a

[033-179]Christian’s, who knows that the divine must not be forfeited for the

[033-180]sake of any human friendship? And if a friend should go so far as to

[033-181]put aside his duty to Heaven to fulfil his duty to his friend, it

[033-182]should not be in matters that are trifling or of little moment, but in

[033-183]such as affect the friend’s life and honour. Now tell me, Anselmo, in

[033-184]which of these two art thou imperilled, that I should hazard myself to

[033-185]gratify thee, and do a thing so detestable as that thou seekest of me?

[033-186]Neither forsooth; on the contrary, thou dost ask of me, so far as I

[033-187]understand, to strive and labour to rob thee of honour and life, and to

[033-188]rob myself of them at the same time; for if I take away thy honour it

[033-189]is plain I take away thy life, as a man without honour is worse than

[033-190]dead; and being the instrument, as thou wilt have it so, of so much

[033-191]wrong to thee, shall not I, too, be left without honour, and

[033-192]consequently without life? Listen to me, Anselmo my friend, and be not

[033-193]impatient to answer me until I have said what occurs to me touching the

[033-194]object of thy desire, for there will be time enough left for thee to

[033-195]reply and for me to hear.”

[033-196]“Be it so,” said Anselmo, “say what thou wilt.”

[033-197]Lothario then went on to say, “It seems to me, Anselmo, that thine is

[033-198]just now the temper of mind which is always that of the Moors, who can

[033-199]never be brought to see the error of their creed by quotations from the

[033-200]Holy Scriptures, or by reasons which depend upon the examination of the

[033-201]understanding or are founded upon the articles of faith, but must have

[033-202]examples that are palpable, easy, intelligible, capable of proof, not

[033-203]admitting of doubt, with mathematical demonstrations that cannot be

[033-204]denied, like, ‘_If equals be taken from equals, the remainders are

[033-205]equal:_’ and if they do not understand this in words, and indeed they

[033-206]do not, it has to be shown to them with the hands, and put before their

[033-207]eyes, and even with all this no one succeeds in convincing them of the

[033-208]truth of our holy religion. This same mode of proceeding I shall have

[033-209]to adopt with thee, for the desire which has sprung up in thee is so

[033-210]absurd and remote from everything that has a semblance of reason, that

[033-211]I feel it would be a waste of time to employ it in reasoning with thy

[033-212]simplicity, for at present I will call it by no other name; and I am

[033-213]even tempted to leave thee in thy folly as a punishment for thy

[033-214]pernicious desire; but the friendship I bear thee, which will not allow

[033-215]me to desert thee in such manifest danger of destruction, keeps me from

[033-216]dealing so harshly by thee. And that thou mayest clearly see this, say,

[033-217]Anselmo, hast thou not told me that I must force my suit upon a modest

[033-218]woman, decoy one that is virtuous, make overtures to one that is

[033-219]pure-minded, pay court to one that is prudent? Yes, thou hast told me

[033-220]so. Then, if thou knowest that thou hast a wife, modest, virtuous,

[033-221]pure-minded and prudent, what is it that thou seekest? And if thou

[033-222]believest that she will come forth victorious from all my attacks—as

[033-223]doubtless she would—what higher titles than those she possesses now

[033-224]dost thou think thou canst bestow upon her then, or in what will she be

[033-225]better then than she is now? Either thou dost not hold her to be what

[033-226]thou sayest, or thou knowest not what thou dost demand. If thou dost

[033-227]not hold her to be what thou sayest, why dost thou seek to prove her

[033-228]instead of treating her as guilty in the way that may seem best to

[033-229]thee? but if she be as virtuous as thou believest, it is an

[033-230]uncalled-for proceeding to make trial of truth itself, for, after

[033-231]trial, it will but be in the same estimation as before. Thus, then, it

[033-232]is conclusive that to attempt things from which harm rather than

[033-233]advantage may come to us is the part of unreasoning and reckless minds,

[033-234]more especially when they are things which we are not forced or

[033-235]compelled to attempt, and which show from afar that it is plainly

[033-236]madness to attempt them.

[033-237]“Difficulties are attempted either for the sake of God or for the sake

[033-238]of the world, or for both; those undertaken for God’s sake are those

[033-239]which the saints undertake when they attempt to live the lives of

[033-240]angels in human bodies; those undertaken for the sake of the world are

[033-241]those of the men who traverse such a vast expanse of water, such a

[033-242]variety of climates, so many strange countries, to acquire what are

[033-243]called the blessings of fortune; and those undertaken for the sake of

[033-244]God and the world together are those of brave soldiers, who no sooner

[033-245]do they see in the enemy’s wall a breach as wide as a cannon ball could

[033-246]make, than, casting aside all fear, without hesitating, or heeding the

[033-247]manifest peril that threatens them, borne onward by the desire of

[033-248]defending their faith, their country, and their king, they fling

[033-249]themselves dauntlessly into the midst of the thousand opposing deaths

[033-250]that await them. Such are the things that men are wont to attempt, and

[033-251]there is honour, glory, gain, in attempting them, however full of

[033-252]difficulty and peril they may be; but that which thou sayest it is thy

[033-253]wish to attempt and carry out will not win thee the glory of God nor

[033-254]the blessings of fortune nor fame among men; for even if the issue be

[033-255]as thou wouldst have it, thou wilt be no happier, richer, or more

[033-256]honoured than thou art this moment; and if it be otherwise thou wilt be

[033-257]reduced to misery greater than can be imagined, for then it will avail

[033-258]thee nothing to reflect that no one is aware of the misfortune that has

[033-259]befallen thee; it will suffice to torture and crush thee that thou

[033-260]knowest it thyself. And in confirmation of the truth of what I say, let

[033-261]me repeat to thee a stanza made by the famous poet Luigi Tansillo at

[033-262]the end of the first part of his ‘Tears of Saint Peter,’ which says

[033-263]thus:

[033-264]The anguish and the shame but greater grew

[033-265]In Peter’s heart as morning slowly came;

[033-266]No eye was there to see him, well he knew,

[033-267]Yet he himself was to himself a shame;

[033-268]Exposed to all men’s gaze, or screened from view,

[033-269]A noble heart will feel the pang the same;

[033-270]A prey to shame the sinning soul will be,

[033-271]Though none but heaven and earth its shame can see.

[033-272]Thus by keeping it secret thou wilt not escape thy sorrow, but rather

[033-273]thou wilt shed tears unceasingly, if not tears of the eyes, tears of

[033-274]blood from the heart, like those shed by that simple doctor our poet

[033-275]tells us of, that tried the test of the cup, which the wise Rinaldo,

[033-276]better advised, refused to do; for though this may be a poetic fiction

[033-277]it contains a moral lesson worthy of attention and study and imitation.

[033-278]Moreover by what I am about to say to thee thou wilt be led to see the

[033-279]great error thou wouldst commit.

[033-280]“Tell me, Anselmo, if Heaven or good fortune had made thee master and

[033-281]lawful owner of a diamond of the finest quality, with the excellence

[033-282]and purity of which all the lapidaries that had seen it had been

[033-283]satisfied, saying with one voice and common consent that in purity,

[033-284]quality, and fineness, it was all that a stone of the kind could

[033-285]possibly be, thou thyself too being of the same belief, as knowing

[033-286]nothing to the contrary, would it be reasonable in thee to desire to

[033-287]take that diamond and place it between an anvil and a hammer, and by

[033-288]mere force of blows and strength of arm try if it were as hard and as

[033-289]fine as they said? And if thou didst, and if the stone should resist so

[033-290]silly a test, that would add nothing to its value or reputation; and if

[033-291]it were broken, as it might be, would not all be lost? Undoubtedly it

[033-292]would, leaving its owner to be rated as a fool in the opinion of all.

[033-293]Consider, then, Anselmo my friend, that Camilla is a diamond of the

[033-294]finest quality as well in thy estimation as in that of others, and that

[033-295]it is contrary to reason to expose her to the risk of being broken; for

[033-296]if she remains intact she cannot rise to a higher value than she now

[033-297]possesses; and if she give way and be unable to resist, bethink thee

[033-298]now how thou wilt be deprived of her, and with what good reason thou

[033-299]wilt complain of thyself for having been the cause of her ruin and

[033-300]thine own. Remember there is no jewel in the world so precious as a

[033-301]chaste and virtuous woman, and that the whole honour of women consists

[033-302]in reputation; and since thy wife’s is of that high excellence that

[033-303]thou knowest, wherefore shouldst thou seek to call that truth in

[033-304]question? Remember, my friend, that woman is an imperfect animal, and

[033-305]that impediments are not to be placed in her way to make her trip and

[033-306]fall, but that they should be removed, and her path left clear of all

[033-307]obstacles, so that without hindrance she may run her course freely to

[033-308]attain the desired perfection, which consists in being virtuous.

[033-309]Naturalists tell us that the ermine is a little animal which has a fur

[033-310]of purest white, and that when the hunters wish to take it, they make

[033-311]use of this artifice. Having ascertained the places which it frequents

[033-312]and passes, they stop the way to them with mud, and then rousing it,

[033-313]drive it towards the spot, and as soon as the ermine comes to the mud

[033-314]it halts, and allows itself to be taken captive rather than pass

[033-315]through the mire, and spoil and sully its whiteness, which it values

[033-316]more than life and liberty. The virtuous and chaste woman is an ermine,

[033-317]and whiter and purer than snow is the virtue of modesty; and he who

[033-318]wishes her not to lose it, but to keep and preserve it, must adopt a

[033-319]course different from that employed with the ermine; he must not put

[033-320]before her the mire of the gifts and attentions of persevering lovers,

[033-321]because perhaps—and even without a perhaps—she may not have sufficient

[033-322]virtue and natural strength in herself to pass through and tread under

[033-323]foot these impediments; they must be removed, and the brightness of

[033-324]virtue and the beauty of a fair fame must be put before her. A virtuous

[033-325]woman, too, is like a mirror, of clear shining crystal, liable to be

[033-326]tarnished and dimmed by every breath that touches it. She must be

[033-327]treated as relics are; adored, not touched. She must be protected and

[033-328]prized as one protects and prizes a fair garden full of roses and

[033-329]flowers, the owner of which allows no one to trespass or pluck a

[033-330]blossom; enough for others that from afar and through the iron grating

[033-331]they may enjoy its fragrance and its beauty. Finally let me repeat to

[033-332]thee some verses that come to my mind; I heard them in a modern comedy,

[033-333]and it seems to me they bear upon the point we are discussing. A

[033-334]prudent old man was giving advice to another, the father of a young

[033-335]girl, to lock her up, watch over her and keep her in seclusion, and

[033-336]among other arguments he used these:

[033-337]Woman is a thing of glass;

[033-338]But her brittleness ’tis best

[033-339]Not too curiously to test:

[033-340]Who knows what may come to pass?

[033-341]Breaking is an easy matter,

[033-342]And it’s folly to expose

[033-343]What you cannot mend to blows;

[033-344]What you can’t make whole to shatter.

[033-345]This, then, all may hold as true,

[033-346]And the reason’s plain to see;

[033-347]For if Danaës there be,

[033-348]There are golden showers too.

[033-349]“All that I have said to thee so far, Anselmo, has had reference to

[033-350]what concerns thee; now it is right that I should say something of what

[033-351]regards myself; and if I be prolix, pardon me, for the labyrinth into

[033-352]which thou hast entered and from which thou wouldst have me extricate

[033-353]thee makes it necessary.

[033-354]“Thou dost reckon me thy friend, and thou wouldst rob me of honour, a

[033-355]thing wholly inconsistent with friendship; and not only dost thou aim

[033-356]at this, but thou wouldst have me rob thee of it also. That thou

[033-357]wouldst rob me of it is clear, for when Camilla sees that I pay court

[033-358]to her as thou requirest, she will certainly regard me as a man without

[033-359]honour or right feeling, since I attempt and do a thing so much opposed
