## Overview

*Tirant lo Blanc* is a chivalric romance written in Catalan by the Valencian knight Joanot Martorell, first published in Valencia in 1490. Unlike the fantastical excesses of most chivalric romances, Tirant is noted for its realism: the hero dies in bed, the knights eat and sleep, and military strategy receives detailed attention. This combination of chivalric adventure with everyday detail made it unique among its contemporaries and earned it a singular distinction in Cervantes' novel.

## In Don Quixote

In the famous library scrutiny scene of Chapter 6, the curate reacts to *Tirant lo Blanc* with uncharacteristic enthusiasm. While dozens of other chivalric romances are consigned to the bonfire, Tirant is spared with a shout of joy — the curate declares it a treasure of delightful reading [006-142]. This moment stands out as Cervantes' most unambiguous endorsement of any chivalric text within his own novel. The work is praised as a model of what the genre could be when grounded in human reality, prefiguring Cervantes' own narrative innovations. Later in the novel, Tirant is invoked alongside Amadís as a benchmark of knightly excellence [013-067], and in Part II it is cited once more as a standard of graceful style [053-247].

## Literary Significance

The curate's outburst over *Tirant lo Blanc* is one of the most revealing literary-critical moments in *Don Quixote*. Cervantes, speaking through the curate, values Tirant not for its chivalric grandeur but for its human details — the amours, the witticisms, the stratagems. This is the same quality Cervantes brings to his own novel: knights who get beaten up, squires who complain about hunger, love that is sometimes absurd and sometimes genuine. The passage is also self-referential: the curate mentions "the Empress in love with her squire Hippolytus," an unconventional love plot that parallels Cervantes' own interest in desire across social boundaries. By sparing Tirant while burning its peers, Cervantes signals that the problem with chivalric romance was not the genre itself, but the failure to infuse it with observed human reality.
