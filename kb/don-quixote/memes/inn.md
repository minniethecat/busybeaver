## A Castle in Disguise

The inn is one of the novel's most important locations, appearing repeatedly throughout Part I. Don Quixote consistently perceives it as a castle, converting its mundane features into chivalric equivalents: the innkeeper becomes the castellan, prostitutes become noble damsels, and the horn of a swineherd becomes a ceremonial trumpet.

This perceptual transformation is not incidental but systematic. When Don Quixote first arrives at the inn, Cervantes narrates from both perspectives simultaneously — the narrator and reader see an inn, while Don Quixote sees a castle. The result is a double vision that defines the novel's comic method.

## The Knighting Ceremony

Don Quixote's vigil of arms and subsequent knighting take place at the inn. The innkeeper, "a man of some weight" who "had himself been something of a rogue in his young days" (003-013), plays along with the madman's delusion, performing a burlesque ceremony in the inn's courtyard. The scene establishes the pattern of the novel: Don Quixote's chivalric imagination imposes itself on a reality that is simultaneously accommodating and mocking.

## Social Crossroads

Beyond its comic function, the inn serves as the novel's principal social crossroads. Cervantes brings together characters from every stratum of Spanish society within its walls:

| Social Class | Characters at the Inn |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Gentry | Don Quixote, Dorotea, Don Fernando |
| Clergy | The curate, the barber |
| Peasantry | Sancho Panza, Maritornes |
| Military | The Captain (Captive's Tale) |
| Outcasts | Galley slaves (discussed) |
| Moorish | Zoraida |

The inn becomes the setting for the "judgment of the books" (ch. 32), the reading of the Tale of Foolish Curiosity, and the reunion of the lovers Cardenio, Luscinda, Don Fernando, and Dorotea. It is the novel's centripetal point — all roads lead to the inn.

## Don Quixote's Battles at the Inn

Two of Don Quixote's most farcical episodes occur at the inn. In the first, his battle with the wineskins — mistaking them for a giant — results in a flood of red wine. In the second, the famous "blanketing" of Sancho Panza occurs in the inn yard as payment for an unpaid bill, while Don Quixote watches helplessly from outside the walls.
