OF HOW DON QUIXOTE AND SANCHO REACHED THEIR VILLAGE

p72a.jpg (155K)


[124-001]All that day Don Quixote and Sancho remained in the village and inn

[124-002]waiting for night, the one to finish off his task of scourging in the

[124-003]open country, the other to see it accomplished, for therein lay the

[124-004]accomplishment of his wishes. Meanwhile there arrived at the hostelry a

[124-005]traveller on horseback with three or four servants, one of whom said to

[124-006]him who appeared to be the master, “Here, Señor Don Álvaro Tarfe, your

[124-007]worship may take your siesta to-day; the quarters seem clean and cool.”

[124-008]When he heard this Don Quixote said to Sancho, “Look here, Sancho; on

[124-009]turning over the leaves of that book of the Second Part of my history I

[124-010]think I came casually upon this name of Don Álvaro Tarfe.”

[124-011]“Very likely,” said Sancho; “we had better let him dismount, and

[124-012]by-and-by we can ask about it.”

[124-013]The gentleman dismounted, and the landlady gave him a room on the

[124-014]ground floor opposite Don Quixote’s and adorned with painted serge

[124-015]hangings of the same sort. The newly arrived gentleman put on a summer

[124-016]coat, and coming out to the gateway of the hostelry, which was wide and

[124-017]cool, addressing Don Quixote, who was pacing up and down there, he

[124-018]asked, “In what direction is your worship bound, gentle sir?”

[124-019]“To a village near this which is my own village,” replied Don Quixote;

[124-020]“and your worship, where are you bound for?”

[124-021]“I am going to Granada, señor,” said the gentleman, “to my own

[124-022]country.”

[124-023]“And a goodly country,” said Don Quixote; “but will your worship do me

[124-024]the favour of telling me your name, for it strikes me it is of more

[124-025]importance to me to know it than I can tell you.”

[124-026]“My name is Don Álvaro Tarfe,” replied the traveller.

[124-027]To which Don Quixote returned, “I have no doubt whatever that your

[124-028]worship is that Don Álvaro Tarfe who appears in print in the Second

[124-029]Part of the history of Don Quixote of La Mancha, lately printed and

[124-030]published by a new author.”

[124-031]“I am the same,” replied the gentleman; “and that same Don Quixote, the

[124-032]principal personage in the said history, was a very great friend of

[124-033]mine, and it was I who took him away from home, or at least induced him

[124-034]to come to some jousts that were to be held at Saragossa, whither I was

[124-035]going myself; indeed, I showed him many kindnesses, and saved him from

[124-036]having his shoulders touched up by the executioner because of his

[124-037]extreme rashness.”

[124-038]“Tell me, Señor Don Álvaro,” said Don Quixote, “am I at all like that

[124-039]Don Quixote you talk of?”

[124-040]“No indeed,” replied the traveller, “not a bit.”

[124-041]“And that Don Quixote—” said our one, “had he with him a squire called

[124-042]Sancho Panza?”

[124-043]“He had,” said Don Álvaro; “but though he had the name of being very

[124-044]droll, I never heard him say anything that had any drollery in it.”

[124-045]“That I can well believe,” said Sancho at this, “for to come out with

[124-046]drolleries is not in everybody’s line; and that Sancho your worship

[124-047]speaks of, gentle sir, must be some great scoundrel, dunderhead, and

[124-048]thief, all in one; for I am the real Sancho Panza, and I have more

[124-049]drolleries than if it rained them; let your worship only try; come

[124-050]along with me for a year or so, and you will find they fall from me at

[124-051]every turn, and so rich and so plentiful that though mostly I don’t

[124-052]know what I am saying I make everybody that hears me laugh. And the

[124-053]real Don Quixote of La Mancha, the famous, the valiant, the wise, the

[124-054]lover, the righter of wrongs, the guardian of minors and orphans, the

[124-055]protector of widows, the killer of damsels, he who has for his sole

[124-056]mistress the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, is this gentleman before

[124-057]you, my master; all other Don Quixotes and all other Sancho Panzas are

[124-058]dreams and mockeries.”

[124-059]“By God I believe it,” said Don Álvaro; “for you have uttered more

[124-060]drolleries, my friend, in the few words you have spoken than the other

[124-061]Sancho Panza in all I ever heard from him, and they were not a few. He

[124-062]was more greedy than well-spoken, and more dull than droll; and I am

[124-063]convinced that the enchanters who persecute Don Quixote the Good have

[124-064]been trying to persecute me with Don Quixote the Bad. But I don’t know

[124-065]what to say, for I am ready to swear I left him shut up in the Casa del

[124-066]Nuncio at Toledo, and here another Don Quixote turns up, though a very

[124-067]different one from mine.”

[124-068]“I don’t know whether I am good,” said Don Quixote, “but I can safely

[124-069]say I am not ‘the Bad;’ and to prove it, let me tell you, Señor Don

[124-070]Álvaro Tarfe, I have never in my life been in Saragossa; so far from

[124-071]that, when it was told me that this imaginary Don Quixote had been

[124-072]present at the jousts in that city, I declined to enter it, in order to

[124-073]drag his falsehood before the face of the world; and so I went on

[124-074]straight to Barcelona, the treasure-house of courtesy, haven of

[124-075]strangers, asylum of the poor, home of the valiant, champion of the

[124-076]wronged, pleasant exchange of firm friendships, and city unrivalled in

[124-077]site and beauty. And though the adventures that befell me there are not

[124-078]by any means matters of enjoyment, but rather of regret, I do not

[124-079]regret them, simply because I have seen it. In a word, Señor Don Álvaro

[124-080]Tarfe, I am Don Quixote of La Mancha, the one that fame speaks of, and

[124-081]not the unlucky one that has attempted to usurp my name and deck

[124-082]himself out in my ideas. I entreat your worship by your devoir as a

[124-083]gentleman to be so good as to make a declaration before the alcalde of

[124-084]this village that you never in all your life saw me until now, and that

[124-085]neither am I the Don Quixote in print in the Second Part, nor this

[124-086]Sancho Panza, my squire, the one your worship knew.”

[124-087]“That I will do most willingly,” replied Don Álvaro; “though it amazes

[124-088]me to find two Don Quixotes and two Sancho Panzas at once, as much

[124-089]alike in name as they differ in demeanour; and again I say and declare

[124-090]that what I saw I cannot have seen, and that what happened me cannot

[124-091]have happened.”

[124-092]“No doubt your worship is enchanted, like my lady Dulcinea del Toboso,”

[124-093]said Sancho; “and would to heaven your disenchantment rested on my

[124-094]giving myself another three thousand and odd lashes like what I’m

[124-095]giving myself for her, for I’d lay them on without looking for

[124-096]anything.”

[124-097]“I don’t understand that about the lashes,” said Don Álvaro. Sancho

[124-098]replied that it was a long story to tell, but he would tell him if they

[124-099]happened to be going the same road.

[124-100]By this dinner-time arrived, and Don Quixote and Don Álvaro dined

[124-101]together. The alcalde of the village came by chance into the inn

[124-102]together with a notary, and Don Quixote laid a petition before him,

[124-103]showing that it was requisite for his rights that Don Álvaro Tarfe, the

[124-104]gentleman there present, should make a declaration before him that he

[124-105]did not know Don Quixote of La Mancha, also there present, and that he

[124-106]was not the one that was in print in a history entitled “Second Part of

[124-107]Don Quixote of La Mancha, by one Avellaneda of Tordesillas.” The

[124-108]alcalde finally put it in legal form, and the declaration was made with

[124-109]all the formalities required in such cases, at which Don Quixote and

[124-110]Sancho were in high delight, as if a declaration of the sort was of any

[124-111]great importance to them, and as if their words and deeds did not

[124-112]plainly show the difference between the two Don Quixotes and the two

[124-113]Sanchos. Many civilities and offers of service were exchanged by Don

[124-114]Álvaro and Don Quixote, in the course of which the great Manchegan

[124-115]displayed such good taste that he disabused Don Álvaro of the error he

[124-116]was under; and he, on his part, felt convinced he must have been

[124-117]enchanted, now that he had been brought in contact with two such

[124-118]opposite Don Quixotes.

[124-119]Evening came, they set out from the village, and after about half a

[124-120]league two roads branched off, one leading to Don Quixote’s village,

[124-121]the other the road Don Álvaro was to follow. In this short interval Don

[124-122]Quixote told him of his unfortunate defeat, and of Dulcinea’s

[124-123]enchantment and the remedy, all which threw Don Álvaro into fresh

[124-124]amazement, and embracing Don Quixote and Sancho, he went his way, and

[124-125]Don Quixote went his. That night he passed among trees again in order

[124-126]to give Sancho an opportunity of working out his penance, which he did

[124-127]in the same fashion as the night before, at the expense of the bark of

[124-128]the beech trees much more than of his back, of which he took such good

[124-129]care that the lashes would not have knocked off a fly had there been

[124-130]one there. The duped Don Quixote did not miss a single stroke of the

[124-131]count, and he found that together with those of the night before they

[124-132]made up three thousand and twenty-nine. The sun apparently had got up

[124-133]early to witness the sacrifice, and with his light they resumed their

[124-134]journey, discussing the deception practised on Don Álvaro, and saying

[124-135]how well done it was to have taken his declaration before a magistrate

[124-136]in such an unimpeachable form. That day and night they travelled on,

[124-137]nor did anything worth mention happen to them, unless it was that in

[124-138]the course of the night Sancho finished off his task, whereat Don

[124-139]Quixote was beyond measure joyful. He watched for daylight, to see if

[124-140]along the road he should fall in with his already disenchanted lady

[124-141]Dulcinea; and as he pursued his journey there was no woman he met that

[124-142]he did not go up to, to see if she was Dulcinea del Toboso, as he held

[124-143]it absolutely certain that Merlin’s promises could not lie. Full of

[124-144]these thoughts and anxieties, they ascended a rising ground wherefrom

[124-145]they descried their own village, at the sight of which Sancho fell on

[124-146]his knees exclaiming, “Open thine eyes, longed-for home, and see how

[124-147]thy son Sancho Panza comes back to thee, if not very rich, very well

[124-148]whipped! Open thine arms and receive, too, thy son Don Quixote, who, if

[124-149]he comes vanquished by the arm of another, comes victor over himself,

[124-150]which, as he himself has told me, is the greatest victory anyone can

[124-151]desire. I’m bringing back money, for if I was well whipped, I went

[124-152]mounted like a gentleman.”

p72b.jpg (375K)


[124-153]“Have done with these fooleries,” said Don Quixote; “let us push on

[124-154]straight and get to our own place, where we will give free range to our

[124-155]fancies, and settle our plans for our future pastoral life.”

[124-156]With this they descended the slope and directed their steps to their

[124-157]village.
