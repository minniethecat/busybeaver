OF WHAT HAPPENED DON QUIXOTE ON HIS WAY TO BARCELONA

p60a.jpg (129K)


[112-001]It was a fresh morning giving promise of a cool day as Don Quixote

[112-002]quitted the inn, first of all taking care to ascertain the most direct

[112-003]road to Barcelona without touching upon Saragossa; so anxious was he to

[112-004]make out this new historian, who they said abused him so, to be a liar.

[112-005]Well, as it fell out, nothing worthy of being recorded happened him for

[112-006]six days, at the end of which, having turned aside out of the road, he

[112-007]was overtaken by night in a thicket of oak or cork trees; for on this

[112-008]point Cide Hamete is not as precise as he usually is on other matters.

[112-009]Master and man dismounted from their beasts, and as soon as they had

[112-010]settled themselves at the foot of the trees, Sancho, who had had a good

[112-011]noontide meal that day, let himself, without more ado, pass the gates

[112-012]of sleep. But Don Quixote, whom his thoughts, far more than hunger,

[112-013]kept awake, could not close an eye, and roamed in fancy to and fro

[112-014]through all sorts of places. At one moment it seemed to him that he was

[112-015]in the cave of Montesinos and saw Dulcinea, transformed into a country

[112-016]wench, skipping and mounting upon her she-ass; again that the words of

[112-017]the sage Merlin were sounding in his ears, setting forth the conditions

[112-018]to be observed and the exertions to be made for the disenchantment of

[112-019]Dulcinea. He lost all patience when he considered the laziness and want

[112-020]of charity of his squire Sancho; for to the best of his belief he had

[112-021]only given himself five lashes, a number paltry and disproportioned to

[112-022]the vast number required. At this thought he felt such vexation and

[112-023]anger that he reasoned the matter thus: “If Alexander the Great cut the

[112-024]Gordian knot, saying, ‘To cut comes to the same thing as to untie,’ and

[112-025]yet did not fail to become lord paramount of all Asia, neither more nor

[112-026]less could happen now in Dulcinea’s disenchantment if I scourge Sancho

[112-027]against his will; for, if it is the condition of the remedy that Sancho

[112-028]shall receive three thousand and odd lashes, what does it matter to me

[112-029]whether he inflicts them himself, or someone else inflicts them, when

[112-030]the essential point is that he receives them, let them come from

[112-031]whatever quarter they may?”

[112-032]With this idea he went over to Sancho, having first taken Rocinante’s

[112-033]reins and arranged them so as to be able to flog him with them, and

[112-034]began to untie the points (the common belief is he had but one in

[112-035]front) by which his breeches were held up; but the instant he

[112-036]approached him Sancho woke up in his full senses and cried out, “What

[112-037]is this? Who is touching me and untrussing me?”

[112-038]“It is I,” said Don Quixote, “and I come to make good thy shortcomings

[112-039]and relieve my own distresses; I come to whip thee, Sancho, and wipe

[112-040]off some portion of the debt thou hast undertaken. Dulcinea is

[112-041]perishing, thou art living on regardless, I am dying of hope deferred;

[112-042]therefore untruss thyself with a good will, for mine it is, here, in

[112-043]this retired spot, to give thee at least two thousand lashes.”

[112-044]“Not a bit of it,” said Sancho; “let your worship keep quiet, or else

[112-045]by the living God the deaf shall hear us; the lashes I pledged myself

[112-046]to must be voluntary and not forced upon me, and just now I have no

[112-047]fancy to whip myself; it is enough if I give you my word to flog and

[112-048]flap myself when I have a mind.”

[112-049]“It will not do to leave it to thy courtesy, Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[112-050]“for thou art hard of heart and, though a clown, tender of flesh;” and

[112-051]at the same time he strove and struggled to untie him.

[112-052]Seeing this Sancho got up, and grappling with his master he gripped him

[112-053]with all his might in his arms, giving him a trip with the heel

[112-054]stretched him on the ground on his back, and pressing his right knee on

[112-055]his chest held his hands in his own so that he could neither move nor

[112-056]breathe.

[112-057]“How now, traitor!” exclaimed Don Quixote. “Dost thou revolt against

[112-058]thy master and natural lord? Dost thou rise against him who gives thee

[112-059]his bread?”

[112-060]“I neither put down king, nor set up king,” said Sancho; “I only stand

[112-061]up for myself who am my own lord; if your worship promises me to be

[112-062]quiet, and not to offer to whip me now, I’ll let you go free and

[112-063]unhindered; if not—

[112-064]Traitor and Doña Sancha’s foe,

[112-065]Thou diest on the spot.”

[112-066]Don Quixote gave his promise, and swore by the life of his thoughts not

[112-067]to touch so much as a hair of his garments, and to leave him entirely

[112-068]free and to his own discretion to whip himself whenever he pleased.

[112-069]Sancho rose and removed some distance from the spot, but as he was

[112-070]about to place himself leaning against another tree he felt something

[112-071]touch his head, and putting up his hands encountered somebody’s two

[112-072]feet with shoes and stockings on them. He trembled with fear and made

[112-073]for another tree, where the very same thing happened to him, and he

[112-074]fell a-shouting, calling upon Don Quixote to come and protect him. Don

[112-075]Quixote did so, and asked him what had happened to him, and what he was

[112-076]afraid of. Sancho replied that all the trees were full of men’s feet

[112-077]and legs. Don Quixote felt them, and guessed at once what it was, and

[112-078]said to Sancho, “Thou hast nothing to be afraid of, for these feet and

[112-079]legs that thou feelest but canst not see belong no doubt to some

[112-080]outlaws and freebooters that have been hanged on these trees; for the

[112-081]authorities in these parts are wont to hang them up by twenties and

[112-082]thirties when they catch them; whereby I conjecture that I must be near

[112-083]Barcelona;” and it was, in fact, as he supposed; with the first light

[112-084]they looked up and saw that the fruit hanging on those trees were

[112-085]freebooters’ bodies.

[112-086]And now day dawned; and if the dead freebooters had scared them, their

[112-087]hearts were no less troubled by upwards of forty living ones, who all

[112-088]of a sudden surrounded them, and in the Catalan tongue bade them stand

[112-089]and wait until their captain came up. Don Quixote was on foot with his

[112-090]horse unbridled and his lance leaning against a tree, and in short

[112-091]completely defenceless; he thought it best therefore to fold his arms

[112-092]and bow his head and reserve himself for a more favourable occasion and

[112-093]opportunity. The robbers made haste to search Dapple, and did not leave

[112-094]him a single thing of all he carried in the alforjas and in the valise;

[112-095]and lucky it was for Sancho that the duke’s crowns and those he brought

[112-096]from home were in a girdle that he wore round him; but for all that

[112-097]these good folk would have stripped him, and even looked to see what he

[112-098]had hidden between the skin and flesh, but for the arrival at that

[112-099]moment of their captain, who was about thirty-four years of age

[112-100]apparently, strongly built, above the middle height, of stern aspect

[112-101]and swarthy complexion. He was mounted upon a powerful horse, and had

[112-102]on a coat of mail, with four of the pistols they call petronels in that

[112-103]country at his waist. He saw that his squires (for so they call those

[112-104]who follow that trade) were about to rifle Sancho Panza, but he ordered

[112-105]them to desist and was at once obeyed, so the girdle escaped. He

[112-106]wondered to see the lance leaning against the tree, the shield on the

[112-107]ground, and Don Quixote in armour and dejected, with the saddest and

[112-108]most melancholy face that sadness itself could produce; and going up to

[112-109]him he said, “Be not so cast down, good man, for you have not fallen

[112-110]into the hands of any inhuman Busiris, but into Roque Guinart’s, which

[112-111]are more merciful than cruel.”

[112-112]“The cause of my dejection,” returned Don Quixote, “is not that I have

[112-113]fallen into thy hands, O valiant Roque, whose fame is bounded by no

[112-114]limits on earth, but that my carelessness should have been so great

[112-115]that thy soldiers should have caught me unbridled, when it is my duty,

[112-116]according to the rule of knight-errantry which I profess, to be always

[112-117]on the alert and at all times my own sentinel; for let me tell thee,

[112-118]great Roque, had they found me on my horse, with my lance and shield,

[112-119]it would not have been very easy for them to reduce me to submission,

[112-120]for I am Don Quixote of La Mancha, he who hath filled the whole world

[112-121]with his achievements.”

[112-122]Roque Guinart at once perceived that Don Quixote’s weakness was more

[112-123]akin to madness than to swagger; and though he had sometimes heard him

[112-124]spoken of, he never regarded the things attributed to him as true, nor

[112-125]could he persuade himself that such a humour could become dominant in

[112-126]the heart of man; he was extremely glad, therefore, to meet him and

[112-127]test at close quarters what he had heard of him at a distance; so he

[112-128]said to him, “Despair not, valiant knight, nor regard as an untoward

[112-129]fate the position in which thou findest thyself; it may be that by

[112-130]these slips thy crooked fortune will make itself straight; for heaven

[112-131]by strange circuitous ways, mysterious and incomprehensible to man,

[112-132]raises up the fallen and makes rich the poor.”

[112-133]Don Quixote was about to thank him, when they heard behind them a noise

[112-134]as of a troop of horses; there was, however, but one, riding on which

[112-135]at a furious pace came a youth, apparently about twenty years of age,

[112-136]clad in green damask edged with gold and breeches and a loose frock,

[112-137]with a hat looped up in the Walloon fashion, tight-fitting polished

[112-138]boots, gilt spurs, dagger and sword, and in his hand a musketoon, and a

[112-139]pair of pistols at his waist.

[112-140]Roque turned round at the noise and perceived this comely figure, which

[112-141]drawing near thus addressed him, “I came in quest of thee, valiant

[112-142]Roque, to find in thee if not a remedy at least relief in my

[112-143]misfortune; and not to keep thee in suspense, for I see thou dost not

[112-144]recognise me, I will tell thee who I am; I am Claudia Jeronima, the

[112-145]daughter of Simon Forte, thy good friend, and special enemy of Clauquel

[112-146]Torrellas, who is thine also as being of the faction opposed to thee.

[112-147]Thou knowest that this Torrellas has a son who is called, or at least

[112-148]was not two hours since, Don Vicente Torrellas. Well, to cut short the

[112-149]tale of my misfortune, I will tell thee in a few words what this youth

[112-150]has brought upon me. He saw me, he paid court to me, I listened to him,

[112-151]and, unknown to my father, I loved him; for there is no woman, however

[112-152]secluded she may live or close she may be kept, who will not have

[112-153]opportunities and to spare for following her headlong impulses. In a

[112-154]word, he pledged himself to be mine, and I promised to be his, without

[112-155]carrying matters any further. Yesterday I learned that, forgetful of

[112-156]his pledge to me, he was about to marry another, and that he was to go

[112-157]this morning to plight his troth, intelligence which overwhelmed and

[112-158]exasperated me; my father not being at home I was able to adopt this

[112-159]costume you see, and urging my horse to speed I overtook Don Vicente

[112-160]about a league from this, and without waiting to utter reproaches or

[112-161]hear excuses I fired this musket at him, and these two pistols besides,

[112-162]and to the best of my belief I must have lodged more than two bullets

[112-163]in his body, opening doors to let my honour go free, enveloped in his

[112-164]blood. I left him there in the hands of his servants, who did not dare

[112-165]and were not able to interfere in his defence, and I come to seek from

[112-166]thee a safe-conduct into France, where I have relatives with whom I can

[112-167]live; and also to implore thee to protect my father, so that Don

[112-168]Vicente’s numerous kinsmen may not venture to wreak their lawless

[112-169]vengeance upon him.”

[112-170]Roque, filled with admiration at the gallant bearing, high spirit,

[112-171]comely figure, and adventure of the fair Claudia, said to her, “Come,

[112-172]señora, let us go and see if thy enemy is dead; and then we will

[112-173]consider what will be best for thee.” Don Quixote, who had been

[112-174]listening to what Claudia said and Roque Guinart said in reply to her,

[112-175]exclaimed, “Nobody need trouble himself with the defence of this lady,

[112-176]for I take it upon myself. Give me my horse and arms, and wait for me

[112-177]here; I will go in quest of this knight, and dead or alive I will make

[112-178]him keep his word plighted to so great beauty.”

[112-179]“Nobody need have any doubt about that,” said Sancho, “for my master

[112-180]has a very happy knack of matchmaking; it’s not many days since he

[112-181]forced another man to marry, who in the same way backed out of his

[112-182]promise to another maiden; and if it had not been for his persecutors

[112-183]the enchanters changing the man’s proper shape into a lacquey’s the

[112-184]said maiden would not be one this minute.”

[112-185]Roque, who was paying more attention to the fair Claudia’s adventure

[112-186]than to the words of master or man, did not hear them; and ordering his

[112-187]squires to restore to Sancho everything they had stripped Dapple of, he

[112-188]directed them to return to the place where they had been quartered

[112-189]during the night, and then set off with Claudia at full speed in search

[112-190]of the wounded or slain Don Vicente. They reached the spot where

[112-191]Claudia met him, but found nothing there save freshly spilt blood;

[112-192]looking all round, however, they descried some people on the slope of a

[112-193]hill above them, and concluded, as indeed it proved to be, that it was

[112-194]Don Vicente, whom either dead or alive his servants were removing to

[112-195]attend to his wounds or to bury him. They made haste to overtake them,

[112-196]which, as the party moved slowly, they were able to do with ease. They

[112-197]found Don Vicente in the arms of his servants, whom he was entreating

[112-198]in a broken feeble voice to leave him there to die, as the pain of his

[112-199]wounds would not suffer him to go any farther. Claudia and Roque threw

[112-200]themselves off their horses and advanced towards him; the servants were

[112-201]overawed by the appearance of Roque, and Claudia was moved by the sight

[112-202]of Don Vicente, and going up to him half tenderly half sternly, she

[112-203]seized his hand and said to him, “Hadst thou given me this according to

[112-204]our compact thou hadst never come to this pass.”

[112-205]The wounded gentleman opened his all but closed eyes, and recognising

[112-206]Claudia said, “I see clearly, fair and mistaken lady, that it is thou

[112-207]that hast slain me, a punishment not merited or deserved by my feelings

[112-208]towards thee, for never did I mean to, nor could I, wrong thee in

[112-209]thought or deed.”

[112-210]“It is not true, then,” said Claudia, “that thou wert going this

[112-211]morning to marry Leonora the daughter of the rich Balvastro?”

[112-212]“Assuredly not,” replied Don Vicente; “my cruel fortune must have

[112-213]carried those tidings to thee to drive thee in thy jealousy to take my

[112-214]life; and to assure thyself of this, press my hands and take me for thy

[112-215]husband if thou wilt; I have no better satisfaction to offer thee for

[112-216]the wrong thou fanciest thou hast received from me.”

[112-217]Claudia wrung his hands, and her own heart was so wrung that she lay

[112-218]fainting on the bleeding breast of Don Vicente, whom a death spasm

[112-219]seized the same instant. Roque was in perplexity and knew not what to

[112-220]do; the servants ran to fetch water to sprinkle their faces, and

[112-221]brought some and bathed them with it. Claudia recovered from her

[112-222]fainting fit, but not so Don Vicente from the paroxysm that had

[112-223]overtaken him, for his life had come to an end. On perceiving this,

[112-224]Claudia, when she had convinced herself that her beloved husband was no

[112-225]more, rent the air with her sighs and made the heavens ring with her

[112-226]lamentations; she tore her hair and scattered it to the winds, she beat

[112-227]her face with her hands and showed all the signs of grief and sorrow

[112-228]that could be conceived to come from an afflicted heart. “Cruel,

[112-229]reckless woman!” she cried, “how easily wert thou moved to carry out a

[112-230]thought so wicked! O furious force of jealousy, to what desperate

[112-231]lengths dost thou lead those that give thee lodging in their bosoms! O

[112-232]husband, whose unhappy fate in being mine hath borne thee from the

[112-233]marriage bed to the grave!”

[112-234]So vehement and so piteous were the lamentations of Claudia that they

[112-235]drew tears from Roque’s eyes, unused as they were to shed them on any

[112-236]occasion. The servants wept, Claudia swooned away again and again, and

[112-237]the whole place seemed a field of sorrow and an abode of misfortune. In

[112-238]the end Roque Guinart directed Don Vicente’s servants to carry his body

[112-239]to his father’s village, which was close by, for burial. Claudia told

[112-240]him she meant to go to a monastery of which an aunt of hers was abbess,

[112-241]where she intended to pass her life with a better and everlasting

[112-242]spouse. He applauded her pious resolution, and offered to accompany her

[112-243]whithersoever she wished, and to protect her father against the kinsmen

[112-244]of Don Vicente and all the world, should they seek to injure him.

[112-245]Claudia would not on any account allow him to accompany her; and

[112-246]thanking him for his offers as well as she could, took leave of him in

[112-247]tears. The servants of Don Vicente carried away his body, and Roque

[112-248]returned to his comrades, and so ended the love of Claudia Jeronima;

[112-249]but what wonder, when it was the insuperable and cruel might of

[112-250]jealousy that wove the web of her sad story?

[112-251]Roque Guinart found his squires at the place to which he had ordered

[112-252]them, and Don Quixote on Rocinante in the midst of them delivering a

[112-253]harangue to them in which he urged them to give up a mode of life so

[112-254]full of peril, as well to the soul as to the body; but as most of them

[112-255]were Gascons, rough lawless fellows, his speech did not make much

[112-256]impression on them. Roque on coming up asked Sancho if his men had

[112-257]returned and restored to him the treasures and jewels they had stripped

[112-258]off Dapple. Sancho said they had, but that three kerchiefs that were

[112-259]worth three cities were missing.

[112-260]“What are you talking about, man?” said one of the bystanders; “I have

[112-261]got them, and they are not worth three reals.”

[112-262]“That is true,” said Don Quixote; “but my squire values them at the

[112-263]rate he says, as having been given me by the person who gave them.”

[112-264]Roque Guinart ordered them to be restored at once; and making his men

[112-265]fall in in line he directed all the clothing, jewellery, and money that

[112-266]they had taken since the last distribution to be produced; and making a

[112-267]hasty valuation, and reducing what could not be divided into money, he

[112-268]made shares for the whole band so equitably and carefully, that in no

[112-269]case did he exceed or fall short of strict distributive justice.

[112-270]When this had been done, and all left satisfied, Roque observed to Don

[112-271]Quixote, “If this scrupulous exactness were not observed with these

[112-272]fellows there would be no living with them.”

[112-273]Upon this Sancho remarked, “From what I have seen here, justice is such

[112-274]a good thing that there is no doing without it, even among the thieves

[112-275]themselves.”

[112-276]One of the squires heard this, and raising the butt-end of his

[112-277]harquebuss would no doubt have broken Sancho’s head with it had not

[112-278]Roque Guinart called out to him to hold his hand. Sancho was frightened

[112-279]out of his wits, and vowed not to open his lips so long as he was in

[112-280]the company of these people.

[112-281]At this instant one or two of those squires who were posted as

[112-282]sentinels on the roads, to watch who came along them and report what

[112-283]passed to their chief, came up and said, “Señor, there is a great troop

[112-284]of people not far off coming along the road to Barcelona.”

[112-285]To which Roque replied, “Hast thou made out whether they are of the

[112-286]sort that are after us, or of the sort we are after?”

[112-287]“The sort we are after,” said the squire.

[112-288]“Well then, away with you all,” said Roque, “and bring them here to me

[112-289]at once without letting one of them escape.”

[112-290]They obeyed, and Don Quixote, Sancho, and Roque, left by themselves,

[112-291]waited to see what the squires brought, and while they were waiting

[112-292]Roque said to Don Quixote, “It must seem a strange sort of life to

[112-293]Señor Don Quixote, this of ours, strange adventures, strange incidents,

[112-294]and all full of danger; and I do not wonder that it should seem so, for

[112-295]in truth I must own there is no mode of life more restless or anxious

[112-296]than ours. What led me into it was a certain thirst for vengeance,

[112-297]which is strong enough to disturb the quietest hearts. I am by nature

[112-298]tender-hearted and kindly, but, as I said, the desire to revenge myself

[112-299]for a wrong that was done me so overturns all my better impulses that I

[112-300]keep on in this way of life in spite of what conscience tells me; and

[112-301]as one depth calls to another, and one sin to another sin, revenges

[112-302]have linked themselves together, and I have taken upon myself not only

[112-303]my own but those of others: it pleases God, however, that, though I see

[112-304]myself in this maze of entanglements, I do not lose all hope of

[112-305]escaping from it and reaching a safe port.”

[112-306]Don Quixote was amazed to hear Roque utter such excellent and just

[112-307]sentiments, for he did not think that among those who followed such

[112-308]trades as robbing, murdering, and waylaying, there could be anyone

[112-309]capable of a virtuous thought, and he said in reply, “Señor Roque, the

[112-310]beginning of health lies in knowing the disease and in the sick man’s

[112-311]willingness to take the medicines which the physician prescribes; you

[112-312]are sick, you know what ails you, and heaven, or more properly speaking

[112-313]God, who is our physician, will administer medicines that will cure

[112-314]you, and cure gradually, and not of a sudden or by a miracle; besides,

[112-315]sinners of discernment are nearer amendment than those who are fools;

[112-316]and as your worship has shown good sense in your remarks, all you have

[112-317]to do is to keep up a good heart and trust that the weakness of your

[112-318]conscience will be strengthened. And if you have any desire to shorten

[112-319]the journey and put yourself easily in the way of salvation, come with

[112-320]me, and I will show you how to become a knight-errant, a calling

[112-321]wherein so many hardships and mishaps are encountered that if they be

[112-322]taken as penances they will lodge you in heaven in a trice.”

[112-323]Roque laughed at Don Quixote’s exhortation, and changing the

[112-324]conversation he related the tragic affair of Claudia Jeronima, at which

[112-325]Sancho was extremely grieved; for he had not found the young woman’s

[112-326]beauty, boldness, and spirit at all amiss.

[112-327]And now the squires despatched to make the prize came up, bringing with

[112-328]them two gentlemen on horseback, two pilgrims on foot, and a coach full

[112-329]of women with some six servants on foot and on horseback in attendance

[112-330]on them, and a couple of muleteers whom the gentlemen had with them.

[112-331]The squires made a ring round them, both victors and vanquished

[112-332]maintaining profound silence, waiting for the great Roque Guinart to

[112-333]speak. He asked the gentlemen who they were, whither they were going,

[112-334]and what money they carried with them; “Señor,” replied one of them,

[112-335]“we are two captains of Spanish infantry; our companies are at Naples,

[112-336]and we are on our way to embark in four galleys which they say are at

[112-337]Barcelona under orders for Sicily; and we have about two or three

[112-338]hundred crowns, with which we are, according to our notions, rich and

[112-339]contented, for a soldier’s poverty does not allow a more extensive

[112-340]hoard.”

[112-341]Roque asked the pilgrims the same questions he had put to the captains,

[112-342]and was answered that they were going to take ship for Rome, and that

[112-343]between them they might have about sixty reals. He asked also who was

[112-344]in the coach, whither they were bound and what money they had, and one

[112-345]of the men on horseback replied, “The persons in the coach are my lady

[112-346]Doña Guiomar de Quiñones, wife of the regent of the Vicaria at Naples,

[112-347]her little daughter, a handmaid and a duenna; we six servants are in

[112-348]attendance upon her, and the money amounts to six hundred crowns.”
