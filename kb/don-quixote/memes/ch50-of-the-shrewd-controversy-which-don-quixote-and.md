[050-001]OF THE SHREWD CONTROVERSY WHICH DON QUIXOTE AND THE CANON HELD,

TOGETHER WITH OTHER INCIDENTS

[050-002]“A good joke, that!” returned Don Quixote. “Books that have been

[050-003]printed with the king’s licence, and with the approbation of those to

[050-004]whom they have been submitted, and read with universal delight, and

[050-005]extolled by great and small, rich and poor, learned and ignorant,

[050-006]gentle and simple, in a word by people of every sort, of whatever rank

[050-007]or condition they may be—that these should be lies! And above all when

[050-008]they carry such an appearance of truth with them; for they tell us the

[050-009]father, mother, country, kindred, age, place, and the achievements,

[050-010]step by step, and day by day, performed by such a knight or knights!

[050-011]Hush, sir; utter not such blasphemy; trust me I am advising you now to

[050-012]act as a sensible man should; only read them, and you will see the

[050-013]pleasure you will derive from them. For, come, tell me, can there be

[050-014]anything more delightful than to see, as it were, here now displayed

[050-015]before us a vast lake of bubbling pitch with a host of snakes and

[050-016]serpents and lizards, and ferocious and terrible creatures of all sorts

[050-017]swimming about in it, while from the middle of the lake there comes a

[050-018]plaintive voice saying: ‘Knight, whosoever thou art who beholdest this

[050-019]dread lake, if thou wouldst win the prize that lies hidden beneath

[050-020]these dusky waves, prove the valour of thy stout heart and cast thyself

[050-021]into the midst of its dark burning waters, else thou shalt not be

[050-022]worthy to see the mighty wonders contained in the seven castles of the

[050-023]seven Fays that lie beneath this black expanse;’ and then the knight,

[050-024]almost ere the awful voice has ceased, without stopping to consider,

[050-025]without pausing to reflect upon the danger to which he is exposing

[050-026]himself, without even relieving himself of the weight of his massive

[050-027]armour, commending himself to God and to his lady, plunges into the

[050-028]midst of the boiling lake, and when he little looks for it, or knows

[050-029]what his fate is to be, he finds himself among flowery meadows, with

[050-030]which the Elysian fields are not to be compared.

[050-031]“The sky seems more transparent there, and the sun shines with a

[050-032]strange brilliancy, and a delightful grove of green leafy trees

[050-033]presents itself to the eyes and charms the sight with its verdure,

[050-034]while the ear is soothed by the sweet untutored melody of the countless

[050-035]birds of gay plumage that flit to and fro among the interlacing

[050-036]branches. Here he sees a brook whose limpid waters, like liquid

[050-037]crystal, ripple over fine sands and white pebbles that look like sifted

[050-038]gold and purest pearls. There he perceives a cunningly wrought fountain

[050-039]of many-coloured jasper and polished marble; here another of rustic

[050-040]fashion where the little mussel-shells and the spiral white and yellow

[050-041]mansions of the snail disposed in studious disorder, mingled with

[050-042]fragments of glittering crystal and mock emeralds, make up a work of

[050-043]varied aspect, where art, imitating nature, seems to have outdone it.

c50c.jpg (334K)


[050-044]“Suddenly there is presented to his sight a strong castle or gorgeous

[050-045]palace with walls of massy gold, turrets of diamond and gates of

[050-046]jacinth; in short, so marvellous is its structure that though the

[050-047]materials of which it is built are nothing less than diamonds,

[050-048]carbuncles, rubies, pearls, gold, and emeralds, the workmanship is

[050-049]still more rare. And after having seen all this, what can be more

[050-050]charming than to see how a bevy of damsels comes forth from the gate of

[050-051]the castle in gay and gorgeous attire, such that, were I to set myself

[050-052]now to depict it as the histories describe it to us, I should never

[050-053]have done; and then how she who seems to be the first among them all

[050-054]takes the bold knight who plunged into the boiling lake by the hand,

[050-055]and without addressing a word to him leads him into the rich palace or

[050-056]castle, and strips him as naked as when his mother bore him, and bathes

[050-057]him in lukewarm water, and anoints him all over with sweet-smelling

[050-058]unguents, and clothes him in a shirt of the softest sendal, all scented

[050-059]and perfumed, while another damsel comes and throws over his shoulders

[050-060]a mantle which is said to be worth at the very least a city, and even

[050-061]more? How charming it is, then, when they tell us how, after all this,

[050-062]they lead him to another chamber where he finds the tables set out in

[050-063]such style that he is filled with amazement and wonder; to see how they

[050-064]pour out water for his hands distilled from amber and sweet-scented

[050-065]flowers; how they seat him on an ivory chair; to see how the damsels

[050-066]wait on him all in profound silence; how they bring him such a variety

[050-067]of dainties so temptingly prepared that the appetite is at a loss which

[050-068]to select; to hear the music that resounds while he is at table, by

[050-069]whom or whence produced he knows not. And then when the repast is over

[050-070]and the tables removed, for the knight to recline in the chair, picking

[050-071]his teeth perhaps as usual, and a damsel, much lovelier than any of the

[050-072]others, to enter unexpectedly by the chamber door, and herself by his

[050-073]side, and begin to tell him what the castle is, and how she is held

[050-074]enchanted there, and other things that amaze the knight and astonish

[050-075]the readers who are perusing his history.

[050-076]“But I will not expatiate any further upon this, as it may be gathered

[050-077]from it that whatever part of whatever history of a knight-errant one

[050-078]reads, it will fill the reader, whoever he be, with delight and wonder;

[050-079]and take my advice, sir, and, as I said before, read these books and

[050-080]you will see how they will banish any melancholy you may feel and raise

[050-081]your spirits should they be depressed. For myself I can say that since

[050-082]I have been a knight-errant I have become valiant, polite, generous,

[050-083]well-bred, magnanimous, courteous, dauntless, gentle, patient, and have

[050-084]learned to bear hardships, imprisonments, and enchantments; and though

[050-085]it be such a short time since I have seen myself shut up in a cage like

[050-086]a madman, I hope by the might of my arm, if heaven aid me and fortune

[050-087]thwart me not, to see myself king of some kingdom where I may be able

[050-088]to show the gratitude and generosity that dwell in my heart; for by my

[050-089]faith, señor, the poor man is incapacitated from showing the virtue of

[050-090]generosity to anyone, though he may possess it in the highest degree;

[050-091]and gratitude that consists of disposition only is a dead thing, just

[050-092]as faith without works is dead. For this reason I should be glad were

[050-093]fortune soon to offer me some opportunity of making myself an emperor,

[050-094]so as to show my heart in doing good to my friends, particularly to

[050-095]this poor Sancho Panza, my squire, who is the best fellow in the world;

[050-096]and I would gladly give him a county I have promised him this ever so

[050-097]long, only that I am afraid he has not the capacity to govern his

[050-098]realm.”

[050-099]Sancho partly heard these last words of his master, and said to him,

[050-100]“Strive hard you, Señor Don Quixote, to give me that county so often

[050-101]promised by you and so long looked for by me, for I promise you there

[050-102]will be no want of capacity in me to govern it; and even if there is, I

[050-103]have heard say there are men in the world who farm seigniories, paying

[050-104]so much a year, and they themselves taking charge of the government,

[050-105]while the lord, with his legs stretched out, enjoys the revenue they

[050-106]pay him, without troubling himself about anything else. That’s what

[050-107]I’ll do, and not stand haggling over trifles, but wash my hands at once

[050-108]of the whole business, and enjoy my rents like a duke, and let things

[050-109]go their own way.”

[050-110]“That, brother Sancho,” said the canon, “only holds good as far as the

[050-111]enjoyment of the revenue goes; but the lord of the seigniory must

[050-112]attend to the administration of justice, and here capacity and sound

[050-113]judgment come in, and above all a firm determination to find out the

[050-114]truth; for if this be wanting in the beginning, the middle and the end

[050-115]will always go wrong; and God as commonly aids the honest intentions of

[050-116]the simple as he frustrates the evil designs of the crafty.”

[050-117]“I don’t understand those philosophies,” returned Sancho Panza; “all I

[050-118]know is I would I had the county as soon as I shall know how to govern

[050-119]it; for I have as much soul as another, and as much body as anyone, and

[050-120]I shall be as much king of my realm as any other of his; and being so I

[050-121]should do as I liked, and doing as I liked I should please myself, and

[050-122]pleasing myself I should be content, and when one is content he has

[050-123]nothing more to desire, and when one has nothing more to desire there

[050-124]is an end of it; so let the county come, and God be with you, and let

[050-125]us see one another, as one blind man said to the other.”

[050-126]“That is not bad philosophy thou art talking, Sancho,” said the canon;

[050-127]“but for all that there is a good deal to be said on this matter of

[050-128]counties.”

[050-129]To which Don Quixote returned, “I know not what more there is to be

[050-130]said; I only guide myself by the example set me by the great Amadis of

[050-131]Gaul, when he made his squire count of the Insula Firme; and so,

[050-132]without any scruples of conscience, I can make a count of Sancho Panza,

[050-133]for he is one of the best squires that ever knight-errant had.”

[050-134]The canon was astonished at the methodical nonsense (if nonsense be

[050-135]capable of method) that Don Quixote uttered, at the way in which he had

[050-136]described the adventure of the knight of the lake, at the impression

[050-137]that the deliberate lies of the books he read had made upon him, and

[050-138]lastly he marvelled at the simplicity of Sancho, who desired so eagerly

[050-139]to obtain the county his master had promised him.

[050-140]By this time the canon’s servants, who had gone to the inn to fetch the

[050-141]sumpter mule, had returned, and making a carpet and the green grass of

[050-142]the meadow serve as a table, they seated themselves in the shade of

[050-143]some trees and made their repast there, that the carter might not be

[050-144]deprived of the advantage of the spot, as has been already said. As

[050-145]they were eating they suddenly heard a loud noise and the sound of a

[050-146]bell that seemed to come from among some brambles and thick bushes that

[050-147]were close by, and the same instant they observed a beautiful goat,

[050-148]spotted all over black, white, and brown, spring out of the thicket

[050-149]with a goatherd after it, calling to it and uttering the usual cries to

[050-150]make it stop or turn back to the fold. The fugitive goat, scared and

[050-151]frightened, ran towards the company as if seeking their protection and

[050-152]then stood still, and the goatherd coming up seized it by the horns and

[050-153]began to talk to it as if it were possessed of reason and

[050-154]understanding: “Ah wanderer, wanderer, Spotty, Spotty; how have you

[050-155]gone limping all this time? What wolves have frightened you, my

[050-156]daughter? Won’t you tell me what is the matter, my beauty? But what

[050-157]else can it be except that you are a she, and cannot keep quiet? A

[050-158]plague on your humours and the humours of those you take after! Come

[050-159]back, come back, my darling; and if you will not be so happy, at any

[050-160]rate you will be safe in the fold or with your companions; for if you

[050-161]who ought to keep and lead them, go wandering astray, what will become

[050-162]of them?”

[050-163]The goatherd’s talk amused all who heard it, but especially the canon,

[050-164]who said to him, “As you live, brother, take it easy, and be not in

[050-165]such a hurry to drive this goat back to the fold; for, being a female,

[050-166]as you say, she will follow her natural instinct in spite of all you

[050-167]can do to prevent it. Take this morsel and drink a sup, and that will

[050-168]soothe your irritation, and in the meantime the goat will rest

[050-169]herself,” and so saying, he handed him the loins of a cold rabbit on a

[050-170]fork.

[050-171]The goatherd took it with thanks, and drank and calmed himself, and

[050-172]then said, “I should be sorry if your worships were to take me for a

[050-173]simpleton for having spoken so seriously as I did to this animal; but

[050-174]the truth is there is a certain mystery in the words I used. I am a

[050-175]clown, but not so much of one but that I know how to behave to men and

[050-176]to beasts.”

[050-177]“That I can well believe,” said the curate, “for I know already by

[050-178]experience that the woods breed men of learning, and shepherds’ huts

[050-179]harbour philosophers.”

[050-180]“At all events, señor,” returned the goatherd, “they shelter men of

[050-181]experience; and that you may see the truth of this and grasp it, though

[050-182]I may seem to put myself forward without being asked, I will, if it

[050-183]will not tire you, gentlemen, and you will give me your attention for a

[050-184]little, tell you a true story which will confirm this gentleman’s word

[050-185](and he pointed to the curate) as well as my own.”

[050-186]To this Don Quixote replied, “Seeing that this affair has a certain

[050-187]colour of chivalry about it, I for my part, brother, will hear you most

[050-188]gladly, and so will all these gentlemen, from the high intelligence

[050-189]they possess and their love of curious novelties that interest, charm,

[050-190]and entertain the mind, as I feel quite sure your story will do. So

[050-191]begin, friend, for we are all prepared to listen.”

[050-192]“I draw my stakes,” said Sancho, “and will retreat with this pasty to

[050-193]the brook there, where I mean to victual myself for three days; for I

[050-194]have heard my lord, Don Quixote, say that a knight-errant’s squire

[050-195]should eat until he can hold no more, whenever he has the chance,

[050-196]because it often happens them to get by accident into a wood so thick

[050-197]that they cannot find a way out of it for six days; and if the man is

[050-198]not well filled or his alforjas well stored, there he may stay, as very

[050-199]often he does, turned into a dried mummy.”

[050-200]“Thou art in the right of it, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “go where thou

[050-201]wilt and eat all thou canst, for I have had enough, and only want to

[050-202]give my mind its refreshment, as I shall by listening to this good

[050-203]fellow’s story.”

[050-204]“It is what we shall all do,” said the canon; and then begged the

[050-205]goatherd to begin the promised tale.

[050-206]The goatherd gave the goat which he held by the horns a couple of slaps

[050-207]on the back, saying, “Lie down here beside me, Spotty, for we have time

[050-208]enough to return to our fold.” The goat seemed to understand him, for

[050-209]as her master seated himself, she stretched herself quietly beside him

[050-210]and looked up in his face to show him she was all attention to what he

[050-211]was going to say, and then in these words he began his story.
