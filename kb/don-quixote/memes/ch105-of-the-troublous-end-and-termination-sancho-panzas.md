[105-001]OF THE TROUBLOUS END AND TERMINATION SANCHO PANZA’S GOVERNMENT CAME TO

p53a.jpg (109K)


[105-002]To fancy that in this life anything belonging to it will remain for

[105-003]ever in the same state is an idle fancy; on the contrary, in it

[105-004]everything seems to go in a circle, I mean round and round. The spring

[105-005]succeeds the summer, the summer the fall, the fall the autumn, the

[105-006]autumn the winter, and the winter the spring, and so time rolls with

[105-007]never-ceasing wheel. Man’s life alone, swifter than time, speeds onward

[105-008]to its end without any hope of renewal, save it be in that other life

[105-009]which is endless and boundless. Thus saith Cide Hamete the Mahometan

[105-010]philosopher; for there are many that by the light of nature alone,

[105-011]without the light of faith, have a comprehension of the fleeting nature

[105-012]and instability of this present life and the endless duration of that

[105-013]eternal life we hope for; but our author is here speaking of the

[105-014]rapidity with which Sancho’s government came to an end, melted away,

[105-015]disappeared, vanished as it were in smoke and shadow. For as he lay in

[105-016]bed on the night of the seventh day of his government, sated, not with

[105-017]bread and wine, but with delivering judgments and giving opinions and

[105-018]making laws and proclamations, just as sleep, in spite of hunger, was

[105-019]beginning to close his eyelids, he heard such a noise of bell-ringing

[105-020]and shouting that one would have fancied the whole island was going to

[105-021]the bottom. He sat up in bed and remained listening intently to try if

[105-022]he could make out what could be the cause of so great an uproar; not

[105-023]only, however, was he unable to discover what it was, but as countless

[105-024]drums and trumpets now helped to swell the din of the bells and shouts,

[105-025]he was more puzzled than ever, and filled with fear and terror; and

[105-026]getting up he put on a pair of slippers because of the dampness of the

[105-027]floor, and without throwing a dressing gown or anything of the kind

[105-028]over him he rushed out of the door of his room, just in time to see

[105-029]approaching along a corridor a band of more than twenty persons with

[105-030]lighted torches and naked swords in their hands, all shouting out, “To

[105-031]arms, to arms, señor governor, to arms! The enemy is in the island in

[105-032]countless numbers, and we are lost unless your skill and valour come to

[105-033]our support.”

[105-034]Keeping up this noise, tumult, and uproar, they came to where Sancho

[105-035]stood dazed and bewildered by what he saw and heard, and as they

[105-036]approached one of them called out to him, “Arm at once, your lordship,

[105-037]if you would not have yourself destroyed and the whole island lost.”

[105-038]“What have I to do with arming?” said Sancho. “What do I know about

[105-039]arms or supports? Better leave all that to my master Don Quixote, who

[105-040]will settle it and make all safe in a trice; for I, sinner that I am,

[105-041]God help me, don’t understand these scuffles.”

[105-042]“Ah, señor governor,” said another, “what slackness of mettle this is!

[105-043]Arm yourself; here are arms for you, offensive and defensive; come out

[105-044]to the plaza and be our leader and captain; it falls upon you by right,

[105-045]for you are our governor.”

[105-046]“Arm me then, in God’s name,” said Sancho, and they at once produced

[105-047]two large shields they had come provided with, and placed them upon him

[105-048]over his shirt, without letting him put on anything else, one shield in

[105-049]front and the other behind, and passing his arms through openings they

[105-050]had made, they bound him tight with ropes, so that there he was walled

[105-051]and boarded up as straight as a spindle and unable to bend his knees or

[105-052]stir a single step. In his hand they placed a lance, on which he leant

[105-053]to keep himself from falling, and as soon as they had him thus fixed

[105-054]they bade him march forward and lead them on and give them all courage;

[105-055]for with him for their guide and lamp and morning star, they were sure

[105-056]to bring their business to a successful issue.

p53b.jpg (332K)


[105-057]“How am I to march, unlucky being that I am?” said Sancho, “when I

[105-058]can’t stir my knee-caps, for these boards I have bound so tight to my

[105-059]body won’t let me. What you must do is carry me in your arms, and lay

[105-060]me across or set me upright in some postern, and I’ll hold it either

[105-061]with this lance or with my body.”

[105-062]“On, señor governor!” cried another, “it is fear more than the boards

[105-063]that keeps you from moving; make haste, stir yourself, for there is no

[105-064]time to lose; the enemy is increasing in numbers, the shouts grow

[105-065]louder, and the danger is pressing.”

[105-066]Urged by these exhortations and reproaches the poor governor made an

[105-067]attempt to advance, but fell to the ground with such a crash that he

[105-068]fancied he had broken himself all to pieces. There he lay like a

[105-069]tortoise enclosed in its shell, or a side of bacon between two

[105-070]kneading-troughs, or a boat bottom up on the beach; nor did the gang of

[105-071]jokers feel any compassion for him when they saw him down; so far from

[105-072]that, extinguishing their torches they began to shout afresh and to

[105-073]renew the calls to arms with such energy, trampling on poor Sancho, and

[105-074]slashing at him over the shield with their swords in such a way that,

[105-075]if he had not gathered himself together and made himself small and

[105-076]drawn in his head between the shields, it would have fared badly with

[105-077]the poor governor, as, squeezed into that narrow compass, he lay,

[105-078]sweating and sweating again, and commending himself with all his heart

[105-079]to God to deliver him from his present peril. Some stumbled over him,

[105-080]others fell upon him, and one there was who took up a position on top

[105-081]of him for some time, and from thence as if from a watchtower issued

[105-082]orders to the troops, shouting out, “Here, our side! Here the enemy is

[105-083]thickest! Hold the breach there! Shut that gate! Barricade those

[105-084]ladders! Here with your stink-pots of pitch and resin, and kettles of

[105-085]boiling oil! Block the streets with feather beds!” In short, in his

[105-086]ardour he mentioned every little thing, and every implement and engine

[105-087]of war by means of which an assault upon a city is warded off, while

[105-088]the bruised and battered Sancho, who heard and suffered all, was saying

[105-089]to himself, “O if it would only please the Lord to let the island be

[105-090]lost at once, and I could see myself either dead or out of this

[105-091]torture!” Heaven heard his prayer, and when he least expected it he

[105-092]heard voices exclaiming, “Victory, victory! The enemy retreats beaten!

[105-093]Come, señor governor, get up, and come and enjoy the victory, and

[105-094]divide the spoils that have been won from the foe by the might of that

[105-095]invincible arm.”

[105-096]“Lift me up,” said the wretched Sancho in a woebegone voice. They

[105-097]helped him to rise, and as soon as he was on his feet said, “The enemy

[105-098]I have beaten you may nail to my forehead; I don’t want to divide the

[105-099]spoils of the foe, I only beg and entreat some friend, if I have one,

[105-100]to give me a sup of wine, for I’m parched with thirst, and wipe me dry,

[105-101]for I’m turning to water.”

[105-102]They rubbed him down, fetched him wine and unbound the shields, and he

[105-103]seated himself upon his bed, and with fear, agitation, and fatigue he

[105-104]fainted away. Those who had been concerned in the joke were now sorry

[105-105]they had pushed it so far; however, the anxiety his fainting away had

[105-106]caused them was relieved by his returning to himself. He asked what

[105-107]o’clock it was; they told him it was just daybreak. He said no more,

[105-108]and in silence began to dress himself, while all watched him, waiting

[105-109]to see what the haste with which he was putting on his clothes meant.

[105-110]He got himself dressed at last, and then, slowly, for he was sorely

[105-111]bruised and could not go fast, he proceeded to the stable, followed by

[105-112]all who were present, and going up to Dapple embraced him and gave him

[105-113]a loving kiss on the forehead, and said to him, not without tears in

[105-114]his eyes, “Come along, comrade and friend and partner of my toils and

[105-115]sorrows; when I was with you and had no cares to trouble me except

[105-116]mending your harness and feeding your little carcass, happy were my

[105-117]hours, my days, and my years; but since I left you, and mounted the

[105-118]towers of ambition and pride, a thousand miseries, a thousand troubles,

[105-119]and four thousand anxieties have entered into my soul;” and all the

[105-120]while he was speaking in this strain he was fixing the pack-saddle on

[105-121]the ass, without a word from anyone. Then having Dapple saddled, he,

[105-122]with great pain and difficulty, got up on him, and addressing himself

[105-123]to the majordomo, the secretary, the head-carver, and Pedro Recio the

[105-124]doctor and several others who stood by, he said, “Make way, gentlemen,

[105-125]and let me go back to my old freedom; let me go look for my past life,

[105-126]and raise myself up from this present death. I was not born to be a

[105-127]governor or protect islands or cities from the enemies that choose to

[105-128]attack them. Ploughing and digging, vinedressing and pruning, are more

[105-129]in my way than defending provinces or kingdoms. Saint Peter is very

[105-130]well at Rome; I mean each of us is best following the trade he was born

[105-131]to. A reaping-hook fits my hand better than a governor’s sceptre; I’d

[105-132]rather have my fill of gazpacho than be subject to the misery of a

[105-133]meddling doctor who kills me with hunger, and I’d rather lie in summer

[105-134]under the shade of an oak, and in winter wrap myself in a double

[105-135]sheepskin jacket in freedom, than go to bed between holland sheets and

[105-136]dress in sables under the restraint of a government. God be with your

[105-137]worships, and tell my lord the duke that ‘naked I was born, naked I

[105-138]find myself, I neither lose nor gain;’ I mean that without a farthing I

[105-139]came into this government, and without a farthing I go out of it, very

[105-140]different from the way governors commonly leave other islands. Stand

[105-141]aside and let me go; I have to plaster myself, for I believe every one

[105-142]of my ribs is crushed, thanks to the enemies that have been trampling

[105-143]over me to-night.”

[105-144]“That is unnecessary, señor governor,” said Doctor Recio, “for I will

[105-145]give your worship a draught against falls and bruises that will soon

[105-146]make you as sound and strong as ever; and as for your diet I promise

[105-147]your worship to behave better, and let you eat plentifully of whatever

[105-148]you like.”

[105-149]“You spoke late,” said Sancho. “I’d as soon turn Turk as stay any

[105-150]longer. Those jokes won’t pass a second time. By God I’d as soon remain

[105-151]in this government, or take another, even if it was offered me between

[105-152]two plates, as fly to heaven without wings. I am of the breed of the

[105-153]Panzas, and they are every one of them obstinate, and if they once say

[105-154]‘odds,’ odds it must be, no matter if it is evens, in spite of all the

[105-155]world. Here in this stable I leave the ant’s wings that lifted me up

[105-156]into the air for the swifts and other birds to eat me, and let’s take

[105-157]to level ground and our feet once more; and if they’re not shod in

[105-158]pinked shoes of cordovan, they won’t want for rough sandals of hemp;

[105-159]‘every ewe to her like,’ ‘and let no one stretch his leg beyond the

[105-160]length of the sheet;’ and now let me pass, for it’s growing late with

[105-161]me.”

[105-162]To this the majordomo said, “Señor governor, we would let your worship

[105-163]go with all our hearts, though it sorely grieves us to lose you, for

[105-164]your wit and Christian conduct naturally make us regret you; but it is

[105-165]well known that every governor, before he leaves the place where he has

[105-166]been governing, is bound first of all to render an account. Let your

[105-167]worship do so for the ten days you have held the government, and then

[105-168]you may go and the peace of God go with you.”

[105-169]“No one can demand it of me,” said Sancho, “but he whom my lord the

[105-170]duke shall appoint; I am going to meet him, and to him I will render an

[105-171]exact one; besides, when I go forth naked as I do, there is no other

[105-172]proof needed to show that I have governed like an angel.”

[105-173]“By God the great Sancho is right,” said Doctor Recio, “and we should

[105-174]let him go, for the duke will be beyond measure glad to see him.”

[105-175]They all agreed to this, and allowed him to go, first offering to bear

[105-176]him company and furnish him with all he wanted for his own comfort or

[105-177]for the journey. Sancho said he did not want anything more than a

[105-178]little barley for Dapple, and half a cheese and half a loaf for

[105-179]himself; for the distance being so short there was no occasion for any

[105-180]better or bulkier provant. They all embraced him, and he with tears

[105-181]embraced all of them, and left them filled with admiration not only at

[105-182]his remarks but at his firm and sensible resolution.
