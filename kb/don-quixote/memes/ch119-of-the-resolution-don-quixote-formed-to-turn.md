OF THE RESOLUTION DON QUIXOTE FORMED TO TURN SHEPHERD AND TAKE TO A

LIFE IN THE FIELDS WHILE THE YEAR FOR WHICH HE HAD GIVEN HIS WORD WAS

[119-001]RUNNING ITS COURSE; WITH OTHER EVENTS TRULY DELECTABLE AND HAPPY

p67a.jpg (145K)


[119-002]If a multitude of reflections used to harass Don Quixote before he had

[119-003]been overthrown, a great many more harassed him since his fall. He was

[119-004]under the shade of a tree, as has been said, and there, like flies on

[119-005]honey, thoughts came crowding upon him and stinging him. Some of them

[119-006]turned upon the disenchantment of Dulcinea, others upon the life he was

[119-007]about to lead in his enforced retirement. Sancho came up and spoke in

[119-008]high praise of the generous disposition of the lacquey Tosilos.

[119-009]“Is it possible, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that thou dost still think

[119-010]that he yonder is a real lacquey? Apparently it has escaped thy memory

[119-011]that thou hast seen Dulcinea turned and transformed into a peasant

[119-012]wench, and the Knight of the Mirrors into the bachelor Carrasco; all

[119-013]the work of the enchanters that persecute me. But tell me now, didst

[119-014]thou ask this Tosilos, as thou callest him, what has become of

[119-015]Altisidora, did she weep over my absence, or has she already consigned

[119-016]to oblivion the love thoughts that used to afflict her when I was

[119-017]present?”

[119-018]“The thoughts that I had,” said Sancho, “were not such as to leave time

[119-019]for asking fool’s questions. Body o’ me, señor! is your worship in a

[119-020]condition now to inquire into other people’s thoughts, above all love

[119-021]thoughts?”

[119-022]“Look ye, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “there is a great difference

[119-023]between what is done out of love and what is done out of gratitude. A

[119-024]knight may very possibly be proof against love; but it is impossible,

[119-025]strictly speaking, for him to be ungrateful. Altisidora, to all

[119-026]appearance, loved me truly; she gave me the three kerchiefs thou

[119-027]knowest of; she wept at my departure, she cursed me, she abused me,

[119-028]casting shame to the winds she bewailed herself in public; all signs

[119-029]that she adored me; for the wrath of lovers always ends in curses. I

[119-030]had no hopes to give her, nor treasures to offer her, for mine are

[119-031]given to Dulcinea, and the treasures of knights-errant are like those

[119-032]of the fairies,’ illusory and deceptive; all I can give her is the

[119-033]place in my memory I keep for her, without prejudice, however, to that

[119-034]which I hold devoted to Dulcinea, whom thou art wronging by thy

[119-035]remissness in whipping thyself and scourging that flesh—would that I

[119-036]saw it eaten by wolves—which would rather keep itself for the worms

[119-037]than for the relief of that poor lady.”

[119-038]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “if the truth is to be told, I cannot persuade

[119-039]myself that the whipping of my backside has anything to do with the

[119-040]disenchantment of the enchanted; it is like saying, ‘If your head aches

[119-041]rub ointment on your knees;’ at any rate I’ll make bold to swear that

[119-042]in all the histories dealing with knight-errantry that your worship has

[119-043]read you have never come across anybody disenchanted by whipping; but

[119-044]whether or no I’ll whip myself when I have a fancy for it, and the

[119-045]opportunity serves for scourging myself comfortably.”

[119-046]“God grant it,” said Don Quixote; “and heaven give thee grace to take

[119-047]it to heart and own the obligation thou art under to help my lady, who

[119-048]is thine also, inasmuch as thou art mine.”

[119-049]As they pursued their journey talking in this way they came to the very

[119-050]same spot where they had been trampled on by the bulls. Don Quixote

[119-051]recognised it, and said he to Sancho, “This is the meadow where we came

[119-052]upon those gay shepherdesses and gallant shepherds who were trying to

[119-053]revive and imitate the pastoral Arcadia there, an idea as novel as it

[119-054]was happy, in emulation whereof, if so be thou dost approve of it,

[119-055]Sancho, I would have ourselves turn shepherds, at any rate for the time

[119-056]I have to live in retirement. I will buy some ewes and everything else

[119-057]requisite for the pastoral calling; and, I under the name of the

[119-058]shepherd Quixotize and thou as the shepherd Panzino, we will roam the

[119-059]woods and groves and meadows singing songs here, lamenting in elegies

[119-060]there, drinking of the crystal waters of the springs or limpid brooks

[119-061]or flowing rivers. The oaks will yield us their sweet fruit with

[119-062]bountiful hand, the trunks of the hard cork trees a seat, the willows

[119-063]shade, the roses perfume, the widespread meadows carpets tinted with a

[119-064]thousand dyes; the clear pure air will give us breath, the moon and

[119-065]stars lighten the darkness of the night for us, song shall be our

[119-066]delight, lamenting our joy, Apollo will supply us with verses, and love

[119-067]with conceits whereby we shall make ourselves famed for ever, not only

[119-068]in this but in ages to come.”

[119-069]“Egad,” said Sancho, “but that sort of life squares, nay corners, with

[119-070]my notions; and what is more the bachelor Samson Carrasco and Master

[119-071]Nicholas the barber won’t have well seen it before they’ll want to

[119-072]follow it and turn shepherds along with us; and God grant it may not

[119-073]come into the curate’s head to join the sheepfold too, he’s so jovial

[119-074]and fond of enjoying himself.”

[119-075]“Thou art in the right of it, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “and the

[119-076]bachelor Samson Carrasco, if he enters the pastoral fraternity, as no

[119-077]doubt he will, may call himself the shepherd Samsonino, or perhaps the

[119-078]shepherd Carrascon; Nicholas the barber may call himself Niculoso, as

[119-079]old Boscan formerly was called Nemoroso; as for the curate I don’t know

[119-080]what name we can fit to him unless it be something derived from his

[119-081]title, and we call him the shepherd Curiambro. For the shepherdesses

[119-082]whose lovers we shall be, we can pick names as we would pears; and as

[119-083]my lady’s name does just as well for a shepherdess’s as for a

[119-084]princess’s, I need not trouble myself to look for one that will suit

[119-085]her better; to thine, Sancho, thou canst give what name thou wilt.”

[119-086]“I don’t mean to give her any but Teresona,” said Sancho, “which will

[119-087]go well with her stoutness and with her own right name, as she is

[119-088]called Teresa; and then when I sing her praises in my verses I’ll show

[119-089]how chaste my passion is, for I’m not going to look ‘for better bread

[119-090]than ever came from wheat’ in other men’s houses. It won’t do for the

[119-091]curate to have a shepherdess, for the sake of good example; and if the

[119-092]bachelor chooses to have one, that is his look-out.”

[119-093]“God bless me, Sancho my friend!” said Don Quixote, “what a life we

[119-094]shall lead! What hautboys and Zamora bagpipes we shall hear, what

[119-095]tabors, timbrels, and rebecks! And then if among all these different

[119-096]sorts of music that of the albogues is heard, almost all the pastoral

[119-097]instruments will be there.”

[119-098]“What are albogues?” asked Sancho, “for I never in my life heard tell

[119-099]of them or saw them.”

[119-100]“Albogues,” said Don Quixote, “are brass plates like candlesticks that

[119-101]struck against one another on the hollow side make a noise which, if

[119-102]not very pleasing or harmonious, is not disagreeable and accords very

[119-103]well with the rude notes of the bagpipe and tabor. The word albogue is

[119-104]Morisco, as are all those in our Spanish tongue that begin with _al;_

[119-105]for example, _almohaza, almorzar, alhombra, alguacil, alhucema,

[119-106]almacen, alcancia_, and others of the same sort, of which there are not

[119-107]many more; our language has only three that are Morisco and end in _i_,

[119-108]which are _borceguí, zaquizamí_, and _maravedí. Alhelí_ and _alfaquí_

[119-109]are seen to be Arabic, as well by the _al_ at the beginning as by the

[119-110]_í_ they end with. I mention this incidentally, the chance allusion to

[119-111]albogues having reminded me of it; and it will be of great assistance

[119-112]to us in the perfect practice of this calling that I am something of a

[119-113]poet, as thou knowest, and that besides the bachelor Samson Carrasco is

[119-114]an accomplished one. Of the curate I say nothing; but I will wager he

[119-115]has some spice of the poet in him, and no doubt Master Nicholas too,

[119-116]for all barbers, or most of them, are guitar players and stringers of

[119-117]verses. I will bewail my separation; thou shalt glorify thyself as a

[119-118]constant lover; the shepherd Carrascon will figure as a rejected one,

[119-119]and the curate Curiambro as whatever may please him best; and so all

[119-120]will go as gaily as heart could wish.”

[119-121]To this Sancho made answer, “I am so unlucky, señor, that I’m afraid

[119-122]the day will never come when I’ll see myself at such a calling. O what

[119-123]neat spoons I’ll make when I’m a shepherd! What messes, creams,

[119-124]garlands, pastoral odds and ends! And if they don’t get me a name for

[119-125]wisdom, they’ll not fail to get me one for ingenuity. My daughter

[119-126]Sanchica will bring us our dinner to the pasture. But stay—she’s

[119-127]good-looking, and shepherds there are with more mischief than

[119-128]simplicity in them; I would not have her ‘come for wool and go back

[119-129]shorn;’ love-making and lawless desires are just as common in the

[119-130]fields as in the cities, and in shepherds’ shanties as in royal

[119-131]palaces; ‘do away with the cause, you do away with the sin;’ ‘if eyes

[119-132]don’t see hearts don’t break’ and ‘better a clear escape than good

[119-133]men’s prayers.’”

[119-134]“A truce to thy proverbs, Sancho,” exclaimed Don Quixote; “any one of

[119-135]those thou hast uttered would suffice to explain thy meaning; many a

[119-136]time have I recommended thee not to be so lavish with proverbs and to

[119-137]exercise some moderation in delivering them; but it seems to me it is

[119-138]only ‘preaching in the desert;’ ‘my mother beats me and I go on with my

[119-139]tricks.”

[119-140]“It seems to me,” said Sancho, “that your worship is like the common

[119-141]saying, ‘Said the frying-pan to the kettle, Get away, blackbreech.’ You

[119-142]chide me for uttering proverbs, and you string them in couples

[119-143]yourself.”

[119-144]“Observe, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “I bring in proverbs to the

[119-145]purpose, and when I quote them they fit like a ring to the finger; thou

[119-146]bringest them in by the head and shoulders, in such a way that thou

[119-147]dost drag them in, rather than introduce them; if I am not mistaken, I

[119-148]have told thee already that proverbs are short maxims drawn from the

[119-149]experience and observation of our wise men of old; but the proverb that

[119-150]is not to the purpose is a piece of nonsense and not a maxim. But

[119-151]enough of this; as nightfall is drawing on let us retire some little

[119-152]distance from the high road to pass the night; what is in store for us

[119-153]to-morrow God knoweth.”

[119-154]They turned aside, and supped late and poorly, very much against

[119-155]Sancho’s will, who turned over in his mind the hardships attendant upon

[119-156]knight-errantry in woods and forests, even though at times plenty

[119-157]presented itself in castles and houses, as at Don Diego de Miranda’s,

[119-158]at the wedding of Camacho the Rich, and at Don Antonio Moreno’s; he

[119-159]reflected, however, that it could not be always day, nor always night;

[119-160]and so that night he passed in sleeping, and his master in waking.
