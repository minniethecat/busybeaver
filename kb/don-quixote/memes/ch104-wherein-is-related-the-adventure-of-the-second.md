WHEREIN IS RELATED THE ADVENTURE OF THE SECOND DISTRESSED OR AFFLICTED

[104-001]DUENNA, OTHERWISE CALLED DOÑA RODRIGUEZ

p52a.jpg (131K)


[104-002]Cide Hamete relates that Don Quixote being now cured of his scratches

[104-003]felt that the life he was leading in the castle was entirely

[104-004]inconsistent with the order of chivalry he professed, so he determined

[104-005]to ask the duke and duchess to permit him to take his departure for

[104-006]Saragossa, as the time of the festival was now drawing near, and he

[104-007]hoped to win there the suit of armour which is the prize at festivals

[104-008]of the sort. But one day at table with the duke and duchess, just as he

[104-009]was about to carry his resolution into effect and ask for their

[104-010]permission, lo and behold suddenly there came in through the door of

[104-011]the great hall two women, as they afterwards proved to be, draped in

[104-012]mourning from head to foot, one of whom approaching Don Quixote flung

[104-013]herself at full length at his feet, pressing her lips to them, and

[104-014]uttering moans so sad, so deep, and so doleful that she put all who

[104-015]heard and saw her into a state of perplexity; and though the duke and

[104-016]duchess supposed it must be some joke their servants were playing off

[104-017]upon Don Quixote, still the earnest way the woman sighed and moaned and

[104-018]wept puzzled them and made them feel uncertain, until Don Quixote,

[104-019]touched with compassion, raised her up and made her unveil herself and

[104-020]remove the mantle from her tearful face. She complied and disclosed

[104-021]what no one could have ever anticipated, for she disclosed the

[104-022]countenance of Doña Rodriguez, the duenna of the house; the other

[104-023]female in mourning being her daughter, who had been made a fool of by

[104-024]the rich farmer’s son. All who knew her were filled with astonishment,

[104-025]and the duke and duchess more than any; for though they thought her a

[104-026]simpleton and a weak creature, they did not think her capable of crazy

[104-027]pranks. Doña Rodriguez, at length, turning to her master and mistress

[104-028]said to them, “Will your excellences be pleased to permit me to speak

[104-029]to this gentleman for a moment, for it is requisite I should do so in

[104-030]order to get successfully out of the business in which the boldness of

[104-031]an evil-minded clown has involved me?”

[104-032]The duke said that for his part he gave her leave, and that she might

[104-033]speak with Señor Don Quixote as much as she liked.

[104-034]She then, turning to Don Quixote and addressing herself to him said,

[104-035]“Some days since, valiant knight, I gave you an account of the

[104-036]injustice and treachery of a wicked farmer to my dearly beloved

[104-037]daughter, the unhappy damsel here before you, and you promised me to

[104-038]take her part and right the wrong that has been done her; but now it

[104-039]has come to my hearing that you are about to depart from this castle in

[104-040]quest of such fair adventures as God may vouchsafe to you; therefore,

[104-041]before you take the road, I would that you challenge this froward

[104-042]rustic, and compel him to marry my daughter in fulfillment of the

[104-043]promise he gave her to become her husband before he seduced her; for to

[104-044]expect that my lord the duke will do me justice is to ask pears from

[104-045]the elm tree, for the reason I stated privately to your worship; and so

[104-046]may our Lord grant you good health and forsake us not.”

[104-047]To these words Don Quixote replied very gravely and solemnly, “Worthy

[104-048]duenna, check your tears, or rather dry them, and spare your sighs, for

[104-049]I take it upon myself to obtain redress for your daughter, for whom it

[104-050]would have been better not to have been so ready to believe lovers’

[104-051]promises, which are for the most part quickly made and very slowly

[104-052]performed; and so, with my lord the duke’s leave, I will at once go in

[104-053]quest of this inhuman youth, and will find him out and challenge him

[104-054]and slay him, if so be he refuses to keep his promised word; for the

[104-055]chief object of my profession is to spare the humble and chastise the

[104-056]proud; I mean, to help the distressed and destroy the oppressors.”

[104-057]“There is no necessity,” said the duke, “for your worship to take the

[104-058]trouble of seeking out the rustic of whom this worthy duenna complains,

[104-059]nor is there any necessity, either, for asking my leave to challenge

[104-060]him; for I admit him duly challenged, and will take care that he is

[104-061]informed of the challenge, and accepts it, and comes to answer it in

[104-062]person to this castle of mine, where I shall afford to both a fair

[104-063]field, observing all the conditions which are usually and properly

[104-064]observed in such trials, and observing too justice to both sides, as

[104-065]all princes who offer a free field to combatants within the limits of

[104-066]their lordships are bound to do.”

[104-067]“Then with that assurance and your highness’s good leave,” said Don

[104-068]Quixote, “I hereby for this once waive my privilege of gentle blood,

[104-069]and come down and put myself on a level with the lowly birth of the

[104-070]wrong-doer, making myself equal with him and enabling him to enter into

[104-071]combat with me; and so, I challenge and defy him, though absent, on the

[104-072]plea of his malfeasance in breaking faith with this poor damsel, who

[104-073]was a maiden and now by his misdeed is none; and say that he shall

[104-074]fulfill the promise he gave her to become her lawful husband, or else

[104-075]stake his life upon the question.”

[104-076]And then plucking off a glove he threw it down in the middle of the

[104-077]hall, and the duke picked it up, saying, as he had said before, that he

[104-078]accepted the challenge in the name of his vassal, and fixed six days

[104-079]thence as the time, the courtyard of the castle as the place, and for

[104-080]arms the customary ones of knights, lance and shield and full armour,

[104-081]with all the other accessories, without trickery, guile, or charms of

[104-082]any sort, and examined and passed by the judges of the field. “But

[104-083]first of all,” he said, “it is requisite that this worthy duenna and

[104-084]unworthy damsel should place their claim for justice in the hands of

[104-085]Don Quixote; for otherwise nothing can be done, nor can the said

[104-086]challenge be brought to a lawful issue.”

[104-087]“I do so place it,” replied the duenna.

[104-088]“And I too,” added her daughter, all in tears and covered with shame

[104-089]and confusion.

[104-090]This declaration having been made, and the duke having settled in his

[104-091]own mind what he would do in the matter, the ladies in black withdrew,

[104-092]and the duchess gave orders that for the future they were not to be

[104-093]treated as servants of hers, but as lady adventurers who came to her

[104-094]house to demand justice; so they gave them a room to themselves and

[104-095]waited on them as they would on strangers, to the consternation of the

[104-096]other women-servants, who did not know where the folly and imprudence

[104-097]of Doña Rodriguez and her unlucky daughter would stop.

[104-098]And now, to complete the enjoyment of the feast and bring the dinner to

[104-099]a satisfactory end, lo and behold the page who had carried the letters

[104-100]and presents to Teresa Panza, the wife of the governor Sancho, entered

[104-101]the hall; and the duke and duchess were very well pleased to see him,

[104-102]being anxious to know the result of his journey; but when they asked

[104-103]him the page said in reply that he could not give it before so many

[104-104]people or in a few words, and begged their excellences to be pleased to

[104-105]let it wait for a private opportunity, and in the meantime amuse

[104-106]themselves with these letters; and taking out the letters he placed

[104-107]them in the duchess’s hand. One bore by way of address, _Letter for my

[104-108]lady the Duchess So-and-so, of I don’t know where; and the other To my

[104-109]husband Sancho Panza, governor of the island of Barataria, whom God

[104-110]prosper longer than me_. The duchess’s bread would not bake, as the

[104-111]saying is, until she had read her letter; and having looked over it

[104-112]herself and seen that it might be read aloud for the duke and all

[104-113]present to hear, she read out as follows.

[104-114]TERESA PANZA’S LETTER TO THE DUCHESS.

[104-115]The letter your highness wrote me, my lady, gave me great pleasure, for

[104-116]indeed I found it very welcome. The string of coral beads is very fine,

[104-117]and my husband’s hunting suit does not fall short of it. All this

[104-118]village is very much pleased that your ladyship has made a governor of

[104-119]my good man Sancho; though nobody will believe it, particularly the

[104-120]curate, and Master Nicholas the barber, and the bachelor Samson

[104-121]Carrasco; but I don’t care for that, for so long as it is true, as it

[104-122]is, they may all say what they like; though, to tell the truth, if the

[104-123]coral beads and the suit had not come I would not have believed it

[104-124]either; for in this village everybody thinks my husband a numskull, and

[104-125]except for governing a flock of goats, they cannot fancy what sort of

[104-126]government he can be fit for. God grant it, and direct him according as

[104-127]he sees his children stand in need of it. I am resolved with your

[104-128]worship’s leave, lady of my soul, to make the most of this fair day,

[104-129]and go to Court to stretch myself at ease in a coach, and make all

[104-130]those I have envying me already burst their eyes out; so I beg your

[104-131]excellence to order my husband to send me a small trifle of money, and

[104-132]to let it be something to speak of, because one’s expenses are heavy at

[104-133]the Court; for a loaf costs a real, and meat thirty maravedis a pound,

[104-134]which is beyond everything; and if he does not want me to go let him

[104-135]tell me in time, for my feet are on the fidgets to be off; and my

[104-136]friends and neighbours tell me that if my daughter and I make a figure

[104-137]and a brave show at Court, my husband will come to be known far more by

[104-138]me than I by him, for of course plenty of people will ask, “Who are

[104-139]those ladies in that coach?” and some servant of mine will answer, “The

[104-140]wife and daughter of Sancho Panza, governor of the island of

[104-141]Barataria;” and in this way Sancho will become known, and I’ll be

[104-142]thought well of, and “to Rome for everything.” I am as vexed as vexed

[104-143]can be that they have gathered no acorns this year in our village; for

[104-144]all that I send your highness about half a peck that I went to the wood

[104-145]to gather and pick out one by one myself, and I could find no bigger

[104-146]ones; I wish they were as big as ostrich eggs.

[104-147]Let not your high mightiness forget to write to me; and I will take

[104-148]care to answer, and let you know how I am, and whatever news there

[104-149]may be in this place, where I remain, praying our Lord to have your

[104-150]highness in his keeping and not to forget me.

[104-151]Sancha my daughter, and my son, kiss your worship’s hands.

[104-152]She who would rather see your ladyship than write to you,

[104-153]Your servant,

[104-154]TERESA PANZA.

[104-155]All were greatly amused by Teresa Panza’s letter, but particularly the

[104-156]duke and duchess; and the duchess asked Don Quixote’s opinion whether

[104-157]they might open the letter that had come for the governor, which she

[104-158]suspected must be very good. Don Quixote said that to gratify them he

[104-159]would open it, and did so, and found that it ran as follows.

[104-160]TERESA PANZA’S LETTER TO HER HUSBAND SANCHO PANZA.

[104-161]I got thy letter, Sancho of my soul, and I promise thee and swear as a

[104-162]Catholic Christian that I was within two fingers’ breadth of going mad

[104-163]I was so happy. I can tell thee, brother, when I came to hear that thou

[104-164]wert a governor I thought I should have dropped dead with pure joy; and

[104-165]thou knowest they say sudden joy kills as well as great sorrow; and as

[104-166]for Sanchica thy daughter, she leaked from sheer happiness. I had

[104-167]before me the suit thou didst send me, and the coral beads my lady the

[104-168]duchess sent me round my neck, and the letters in my hands, and there

[104-169]was the bearer of them standing by, and in spite of all this I verily

[104-170]believed and thought that what I saw and handled was all a dream; for

[104-171]who could have thought that a goatherd would come to be a governor of

[104-172]islands? Thou knowest, my friend, what my mother used to say, that one

[104-173]must live long to see much; I say it because I expect to see more if I

[104-174]live longer; for I don’t expect to stop until I see thee a farmer of

[104-175]taxes or a collector of revenue, which are offices where, though the

[104-176]devil carries off those who make a bad use of them, still they make and

[104-177]handle money. My lady the duchess will tell thee the desire I have to

[104-178]go to the Court; consider the matter and let me know thy pleasure; I

[104-179]will try to do honour to thee by going in a coach.

[104-180]Neither the curate, nor the barber, nor the bachelor, nor even the

[104-181]sacristan, can believe that thou art a governor, and they say the

[104-182]whole thing is a delusion or an enchantment affair, like everything

[104-183]belonging to thy master Don Quixote; and Samson says he must go in

[104-184]search of thee and drive the government out of thy head and the

[104-185]madness out of Don Quixote’s skull; I only laugh, and look at my

[104-186]string of beads, and plan out the dress I am going to make for our

[104-187]daughter out of thy suit. I sent some acorns to my lady the

[104-188]duchess; I wish they had been gold. Send me some strings of pearls

[104-189]if they are in fashion in that island. Here is the news of the

[104-190]village; La Berrueca has married her daughter to a good-for-nothing

[104-191]painter, who came here to paint anything that might turn up. The

[104-192]council gave him an order to paint his Majesty’s arms over the door

[104-193]of the town-hall; he asked two ducats, which they paid him in

[104-194]advance; he worked for eight days, and at the end of them had

[104-195]nothing painted, and then said he had no turn for painting such

[104-196]trifling things; he returned the money, and for all that has

[104-197]married on the pretence of being a good workman; to be sure he has

[104-198]now laid aside his paint-brush and taken a spade in hand, and goes

[104-199]to the field like a gentleman. Pedro Lobo’s son has received the

[104-200]first orders and tonsure, with the intention of becoming a priest.

[104-201]Minguilla, Mingo Silvato’s granddaughter, found it out, and has

[104-202]gone to law with him on the score of having given her promise of

[104-203]marriage. Evil tongues say she is with child by him, but he denies

[104-204]it stoutly. There are no olives this year, and there is not a drop

[104-205]of vinegar to be had in the whole village. A company of soldiers

[104-206]passed through here; when they left they took away with them three

[104-207]of the girls of the village; I will not tell thee who they are;

[104-208]perhaps they will come back, and they will be sure to find those

[104-209]who will take them for wives with all their blemishes, good or bad.

[104-210]Sanchica is making bonelace; she earns eight maravedis a day clear,

[104-211]which she puts into a moneybox as a help towards house furnishing;

[104-212]but now that she is a governor’s daughter thou wilt give her a

[104-213]portion without her working for it. The fountain in the plaza has

[104-214]run dry. A flash of lightning struck the gibbet, and I wish they

[104-215]all lit there. I look for an answer to this, and to know thy mind

[104-216]about my going to the Court; and so, God keep thee longer than me,

[104-217]or as long, for I would not leave thee in this world without me.

[104-218]Thy wife,

[104-219]TERESA PANZA.

[104-220]The letters were applauded, laughed over, relished, and admired; and

[104-221]then, as if to put the seal to the business, the courier arrived,

[104-222]bringing the one Sancho sent to Don Quixote, and this, too, was read

[104-223]out, and it raised some doubts as to the governor’s simplicity. The

[104-224]duchess withdrew to hear from the page about his adventures in Sancho’s

[104-225]village, which he narrated at full length without leaving a single

[104-226]circumstance unmentioned. He gave her the acorns, and also a cheese

[104-227]which Teresa had given him as being particularly good and superior to

[104-228]those of Tronchon. The duchess received it with greatest delight, in

[104-229]which we will leave her, to describe the end of the government of the

[104-230]great Sancho Panza, flower and mirror of all governors of islands.
