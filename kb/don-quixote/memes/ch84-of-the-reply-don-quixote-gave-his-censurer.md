[084-003]Don Quixote, then, having risen to his feet, trembling from head to

[084-004]foot like a man dosed with mercury, said in a hurried, agitated voice,

[084-005]“The place I am in, the presence in which I stand, and the respect I

[084-006]have and always have had for the profession to which your worship

[084-007]belongs, hold and bind the hands of my just indignation; and as well

[084-008]for these reasons as because I know, as everyone knows, that a

[084-009]gownsman’s weapon is the same as a woman’s, the tongue, I will with

[084-010]mine engage in equal combat with your worship, from whom one might have

[084-011]expected good advice instead of foul abuse. Pious, well-meant reproof

[084-012]requires a different demeanour and arguments of another sort; at any

[084-013]rate, to have reproved me in public, and so roughly, exceeds the bounds

[084-014]of proper reproof, for that comes better with gentleness than with

[084-015]rudeness; and it is not seemly to call the sinner roundly blockhead and

[084-016]booby, without knowing anything of the sin that is reproved. Come, tell

[084-017]me, for which of the stupidities you have observed in me do you condemn

[084-018]and abuse me, and bid me go home and look after my house and wife and

[084-019]children, without knowing whether I have any? Is nothing more needed

[084-020]than to get a footing, by hook or by crook, in other people’s houses to

[084-021]rule over the masters (and that, perhaps, after having been brought up

[084-022]in all the straitness of some seminary, and without having ever seen

[084-023]more of the world than may lie within twenty or thirty leagues round),

[084-024]to fit one to lay down the law rashly for chivalry, and pass judgment

[084-025]on knights-errant? Is it, haply, an idle occupation, or is the time

[084-026]ill-spent that is spent in roaming the world in quest, not of its

[084-027]enjoyments, but of those arduous toils whereby the good mount upwards

[084-028]to the abodes of everlasting life? If gentlemen, great lords, nobles,

[084-029]men of high birth, were to rate me as a fool I should take it as an

[084-030]irreparable insult; but I care not a farthing if clerks who have never

[084-031]entered upon or trod the paths of chivalry should think me foolish.

[084-032]Knight I am, and knight I will die, if such be the pleasure of the Most

[084-033]High. Some take the broad road of overweening ambition; others that of

[084-034]mean and servile flattery; others that of deceitful hypocrisy, and some

[084-035]that of true religion; but I, led by my star, follow the narrow path of

[084-036]knight-errantry, and in pursuit of that calling I despise wealth, but

[084-037]not honour. I have redressed injuries, righted wrongs, punished

[084-038]insolences, vanquished giants, and crushed monsters; I am in love, for

[084-039]no other reason than that it is incumbent on knights-errant to be so;

[084-040]but though I am, I am no carnal-minded lover, but one of the chaste,

[084-041]platonic sort. My intentions are always directed to worthy ends, to do

[084-042]good to all and evil to none; and if he who means this, does this, and

[084-043]makes this his practice deserves to be called a fool, it is for your

[084-044]highnesses to say, O most excellent duke and duchess.”

[084-045]“Good, by God!” cried Sancho; “say no more in your own defence, master

[084-046]mine, for there’s nothing more in the world to be said, thought, or

[084-047]insisted on; and besides, when this gentleman denies, as he has, that

[084-048]there are or ever have been any knights-errant in the world, is it any

[084-049]wonder if he knows nothing of what he has been talking about?”

[084-050]“Perhaps, brother,” said the ecclesiastic, “you are that Sancho Panza

[084-051]that is mentioned, to whom your master has promised an island?”

[084-052]“Yes, I am,” said Sancho, “and what’s more, I am one who deserves it as

[084-053]much as anyone; I am one of the sort—‘Attach thyself to the good, and

[084-054]thou wilt be one of them,’ and of those, ‘Not with whom thou art bred,

[084-055]but with whom thou art fed,’ and of those, ‘Who leans against a good

[084-056]tree, a good shade covers him;’ I have leant upon a good master, and I

[084-057]have been for months going about with him, and please God I shall be

[084-058]just such another; long life to him and long life to me, for neither

[084-059]will he be in any want of empires to rule, or I of islands to govern.”

[084-060]“No, Sancho my friend, certainly not,” said the duke, “for in the name

[084-061]of Señor Don Quixote I confer upon you the government of one of no

[084-062]small importance that I have at my disposal.”

[084-063]“Go down on thy knees, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and kiss the feet of

[084-064]his excellence for the favour he has bestowed upon thee.”

[084-065]Sancho obeyed, and on seeing this the ecclesiastic stood up from table

[084-066]completely out of temper, exclaiming, “By the gown I wear, I am almost

[084-067]inclined to say that your excellence is as great a fool as these

[084-068]sinners. No wonder they are mad, when people who are in their senses

[084-069]sanction their madness! I leave your excellence with them, for so long

[084-070]as they are in the house, I will remain in my own, and spare myself the

[084-071]trouble of reproving what I cannot remedy;” and without uttering

[084-072]another word, or eating another morsel, he went off, the entreaties of

[084-073]the duke and duchess being entirely unavailing to stop him; not that

[084-074]the duke said much to him, for he could not, because of the laughter

[084-075]his uncalled-for anger provoked.

[084-076]When he had done laughing, he said to Don Quixote, “You have replied on

[084-077]your own behalf so stoutly, Sir Knight of the Lions, that there is no

[084-078]occasion to seek further satisfaction for this, which, though it may

[084-079]look like an offence, is not so at all, for, as women can give no

[084-080]offence, no more can ecclesiastics, as you very well know.”

[084-081]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, “and the reason is, that he who is

[084-082]not liable to offence cannot give offence to anyone. Women, children,

[084-083]and ecclesiastics, as they cannot defend themselves, though they may

[084-084]receive offence cannot be insulted, because between the offence and the

[084-085]insult there is, as your excellence very well knows, this difference:

[084-086]the insult comes from one who is capable of offering it, and does so,

[084-087]and maintains it; the offence may come from any quarter without

[084-088]carrying insult. To take an example: a man is standing unsuspectingly

[084-089]in the street and ten others come up armed and beat him; he draws his

[084-090]sword and quits himself like a man, but the number of his antagonists

[084-091]makes it impossible for him to effect his purpose and avenge himself;

[084-092]this man suffers an offence but not an insult. Another example will

[084-093]make the same thing plain: a man is standing with his back turned,

[084-094]another comes up and strikes him, and after striking him takes to

[084-095]flight, without waiting an instant, and the other pursues him but does

[084-096]not overtake him; he who received the blow received an offence, but not

[084-097]an insult, because an insult must be maintained. If he who struck him,

[084-098]though he did so sneakingly and treacherously, had drawn his sword and

[084-099]stood and faced him, then he who had been struck would have received

[084-100]offence and insult at the same time; offence because he was struck

[084-101]treacherously, insult because he who struck him maintained what he had

[084-102]done, standing his ground without taking to flight. And so, according

[084-103]to the laws of the accursed duel, I may have received offence, but not

[084-104]insult, for neither women nor children can maintain it, nor can they

[084-105]wound, nor have they any way of standing their ground, and it is just

[084-106]the same with those connected with religion; for these three sorts of

[084-107]persons are without arms offensive or defensive, and so, though

[084-108]naturally they are bound to defend themselves, they have no right to

[084-109]offend anybody; and though I said just now I might have received

[084-110]offence, I say now certainly not, for he who cannot receive an insult

[084-111]can still less give one; for which reasons I ought not to feel, nor do

[084-112]I feel, aggrieved at what that good man said to me; I only wish he had

[084-113]stayed a little longer, that I might have shown him the mistake he

[084-114]makes in supposing and maintaining that there are not and never have

[084-115]been any knights-errant in the world; had Amadis or any of his

[084-116]countless descendants heard him say as much, I am sure it would not

[084-117]have gone well with his worship.”

[084-118]“I will take my oath of that,” said Sancho; “they would have given him

[084-119]a slash that would have slit him down from top to toe like a

[084-120]pomegranate or a ripe melon; they were likely fellows to put up with

[084-121]jokes of that sort! By my faith, I’m certain if Reinaldos of Montalvan

[084-122]had heard the little man’s words he would have given him such a spank

[084-123]on the mouth that he wouldn’t have spoken for the next three years; ay,

[084-124]let him tackle them, and he’ll see how he’ll get out of their hands!”

[084-125]The duchess, as she listened to Sancho, was ready to die with laughter,

[084-126]and in her own mind she set him down as droller and madder than his

[084-127]master; and there were a good many just then who were of the same

[084-128]opinion.

[084-129]Don Quixote finally grew calm, and dinner came to an end, and as the

[084-130]cloth was removed four damsels came in, one of them with a silver

[084-131]basin, another with a jug also of silver, a third with two fine white

[084-132]towels on her shoulder, and the fourth with her arms bared to the

[084-133]elbows, and in her white hands (for white they certainly were) a round

[084-134]ball of Naples soap. The one with the basin approached, and with arch

[084-135]composure and impudence, thrust it under Don Quixote’s chin, who,

[084-136]wondering at such a ceremony, said never a word, supposing it to be the

[084-137]custom of that country to wash beards instead of hands; he therefore

[084-138]stretched his out as far as he could, and at the same instant the jug

[084-139]began to pour and the damsel with the soap rubbed his beard briskly,

[084-140]raising snow-flakes, for the soap lather was no less white, not only

[084-141]over the beard, but all over the face, and over the eyes of the

[084-142]submissive knight, so that they were perforce obliged to keep shut. The

[084-143]duke and duchess, who had not known anything about this, waited to see

[084-144]what came of this strange washing. The barber damsel, when she had him

[084-145]a hand’s breadth deep in lather, pretended that there was no more

[084-146]water, and bade the one with the jug go and fetch some, while Señor Don

[084-147]Quixote waited. She did so, and Don Quixote was left the strangest and

[084-148]most ludicrous figure that could be imagined. All those present, and

[084-149]there were a good many, were watching him, and as they saw him there

[084-150]with half a yard of neck, and that uncommonly brown, his eyes shut, and

[084-151]his beard full of soap, it was a great wonder, and only by great

[084-152]discretion, that they were able to restrain their laughter. The

[084-153]damsels, the concocters of the joke, kept their eyes down, not daring

[084-154]to look at their master and mistress; and as for them, laughter and

[084-155]anger struggled within them, and they knew not what to do, whether to

[084-156]punish the audacity of the girls, or to reward them for the amusement

[084-157]they had received from seeing Don Quixote in such a plight.

[084-158]At length the damsel with the jug returned and they made an end of

[084-159]washing Don Quixote, and the one who carried the towels very

[084-160]deliberately wiped him and dried him; and all four together making him

[084-161]a profound obeisance and curtsey, they were about to go, when the duke,

[084-162]lest Don Quixote should see through the joke, called out to the one

[084-163]with the basin saying, “Come and wash me, and take care that there is

[084-164]water enough.” The girl, sharp-witted and prompt, came and placed the

[084-165]basin for the duke as she had done for Don Quixote, and they soon had

[084-166]him well soaped and washed, and having wiped him dry they made their

[084-167]obeisance and retired. It appeared afterwards that the duke had sworn

[084-168]that if they had not washed him as they had Don Quixote he would have

[084-169]punished them for their impudence, which they adroitly atoned for by

[084-170]soaping him as well.

[084-171]Sancho observed the ceremony of the washing very attentively, and said

[084-172]to himself, “God bless me, if it were only the custom in this country

[084-173]to wash squires’ beards too as well as knights’. For by God and upon my

[084-174]soul I want it badly; and if they gave me a scrape of the razor besides

[084-175]I’d take it as a still greater kindness.”

[084-176]“What are you saying to yourself, Sancho?” asked the duchess.

[084-177]“I was saying, señora,” he replied, “that in the courts of other

[084-178]princes, when the cloth is taken away, I have always heard say they

[084-179]give water for the hands, but not lye for the beard; and that shows it

[084-180]is good to live long that you may see much; to be sure, they say too

[084-181]that he who lives a long life must undergo much evil, though to undergo

[084-182]a washing of that sort is pleasure rather than pain.”

[084-183]“Don’t be uneasy, friend Sancho,” said the duchess; “I will take care

[084-184]that my damsels wash you, and even put you in the tub if necessary.”

[084-185]“I’ll be content with the beard,” said Sancho, “at any rate for the

[084-186]present; and as for the future, God has decreed what is to be.”

[084-187]“Attend to worthy Sancho’s request, seneschal,” said the duchess, “and

[084-188]do exactly what he wishes.”

[084-189]The seneschal replied that Señor Sancho should be obeyed in everything;

[084-190]and with that he went away to dinner and took Sancho along with him,

[084-191]while the duke and duchess and Don Quixote remained at table discussing

[084-192]a great variety of things, but all bearing on the calling of arms and

[084-193]knight-errantry.

[084-194]The duchess begged Don Quixote, as he seemed to have a retentive

[084-195]memory, to describe and portray to her the beauty and features of the

[084-196]lady Dulcinea del Toboso, for, judging by what fame trumpeted abroad of

[084-197]her beauty, she felt sure she must be the fairest creature in the

[084-198]world, nay, in all La Mancha.

[084-199]Don Quixote sighed on hearing the duchess’s request, and said, “If I

[084-200]could pluck out my heart, and lay it on a plate on this table here

[084-201]before your highness’s eyes, it would spare my tongue the pain of

[084-202]telling what can hardly be thought of, for in it your excellence would

[084-203]see her portrayed in full. But why should I attempt to depict and

[084-204]describe in detail, and feature by feature, the beauty of the peerless

[084-205]Dulcinea, the burden being one worthy of other shoulders than mine, an

[084-206]enterprise wherein the pencils of Parrhasius, Timantes, and Apelles,

[084-207]and the graver of Lysippus ought to be employed, to paint it in

[084-208]pictures and carve it in marble and bronze, and Ciceronian and

[084-209]Demosthenian eloquence to sound its praises?”

[084-210]“What does Demosthenian mean, Señor Don Quixote?” said the duchess; “it

[084-211]is a word I never heard in all my life.”

[084-212]“Demosthenian eloquence,” said Don Quixote, “means the eloquence of

[084-213]Demosthenes, as Ciceronian means that of Cicero, who were the two most

[084-214]eloquent orators in the world.”

[084-215]“True,” said the duke; “you must have lost your wits to ask such a

[084-216]question. Nevertheless, Señor Don Quixote would greatly gratify us if

[084-217]he would depict her to us; for never fear, even in an outline or sketch

[084-218]she will be something to make the fairest envious.”

[084-219]“I would do so certainly,” said Don Quixote, “had she not been blurred

[084-220]to my mind’s eye by the misfortune that fell upon her a short time

[084-221]since, one of such a nature that I am more ready to weep over it than

[084-222]to describe it. For your highnesses must know that, going a few days

[084-223]back to kiss her hands and receive her benediction, approbation, and

[084-224]permission for this third sally, I found her altogether a different

[084-225]being from the one I sought; I found her enchanted and changed from a

[084-226]princess into a peasant, from fair to foul, from an angel into a devil,

[084-227]from fragrant to pestiferous, from refined to clownish, from a

[084-228]dignified lady into a jumping tomboy, and, in a word, from Dulcinea del

[084-229]Toboso into a coarse Sayago wench.”

[084-230]“God bless me!” said the duke aloud at this, “who can have done the

[084-231]world such an injury? Who can have robbed it of the beauty that

[084-232]gladdened it, of the grace and gaiety that charmed it, of the modesty

[084-233]that shed a lustre upon it?”

[084-234]“Who?” replied Don Quixote; “who could it be but some malignant

[084-235]enchanter of the many that persecute me out of envy—that accursed race

[084-236]born into the world to obscure and bring to naught the achievements of

[084-237]the good, and glorify and exalt the deeds of the wicked? Enchanters

[084-238]have persecuted me, enchanters persecute me still, and enchanters will

[084-239]continue to persecute me until they have sunk me and my lofty chivalry

[084-240]in the deep abyss of oblivion; and they injure and wound me where they

[084-241]know I feel it most. For to deprive a knight-errant of his lady is to

[084-242]deprive him of the eyes he sees with, of the sun that gives him light,

[084-243]of the food whereby he lives. Many a time before have I said it, and I

[084-244]say it now once more, a knight-errant without a lady is like a tree

[084-245]without leaves, a building without a foundation, or a shadow without

[084-246]the body that causes it.”

[084-247]“There is no denying it,” said the duchess; “but still, if we are to

[084-248]believe the history of Don Quixote that has come out here lately with

[084-249]general applause, it is to be inferred from it, if I mistake not, that

[084-250]you never saw the lady Dulcinea, and that the said lady is nothing in

[084-251]the world but an imaginary lady, one that you yourself begot and gave

[084-252]birth to in your brain, and adorned with whatever charms and

[084-253]perfections you chose.”

[084-254]“There is a good deal to be said on that point,” said Don Quixote; “God

[084-255]knows whether there be any Dulcinea or not in the world, or whether she

[084-256]is imaginary or not imaginary; these are things the proof of which must

[084-257]not be pushed to extreme lengths. I have not begotten nor given birth

[084-258]to my lady, though I behold her as she needs must be, a lady who

[084-259]contains in herself all the qualities to make her famous throughout the

[084-260]world, beautiful without blemish, dignified without haughtiness, tender

[084-261]and yet modest, gracious from courtesy and courteous from good

[084-262]breeding, and lastly, of exalted lineage, because beauty shines forth

[084-263]and excels with a higher degree of perfection upon good blood than in

[084-264]the fair of lowly birth.”

[084-265]“That is true,” said the duke; “but Señor Don Quixote will give me

[084-266]leave to say what I am constrained to say by the story of his exploits

[084-267]that I have read, from which it is to be inferred that, granting there

[084-268]is a Dulcinea in El Toboso, or out of it, and that she is in the

[084-269]highest degree beautiful as you have described her to us, as regards

[084-270]the loftiness of her lineage she is not on a par with the Orianas,

[084-271]Alastrajareas, Madasimas, or others of that sort, with whom, as you

[084-272]well know, the histories abound.”

[084-273]“To that I may reply,” said Don Quixote, “that Dulcinea is the daughter

[084-274]of her own works, and that virtues rectify blood, and that lowly virtue

[084-275]is more to be regarded and esteemed than exalted vice. Dulcinea,

[084-276]besides, has that within her that may raise her to be a crowned and

[084-277]sceptred queen; for the merit of a fair and virtuous woman is capable

[084-278]of performing greater miracles; and virtually, though not formally, she

[084-279]has in herself higher fortunes.”

[084-280]“I protest, Señor Don Quixote,” said the duchess, “that in all you say,

[084-281]you go most cautiously and lead in hand, as the saying is; henceforth I

[084-282]will believe myself, and I will take care that everyone in my house

[084-283]believes, even my lord the duke if needs be, that there is a Dulcinea

[084-284]in El Toboso, and that she is living to-day, and that she is beautiful

[084-285]and nobly born and deserves to have such a knight as Señor Don Quixote

[084-286]in her service, and that is the highest praise that it is in my power

[084-287]to give her or that I can think of. But I cannot help entertaining a

[084-288]doubt, and having a certain grudge against Sancho Panza; the doubt is

[084-289]this, that the aforesaid history declares that the said Sancho Panza,

[084-290]when he carried a letter on your worship’s behalf to the said lady

[084-291]Dulcinea, found her sifting a sack of wheat; and more by token it says

[084-292]it was red wheat; a thing which makes me doubt the loftiness of her

[084-293]lineage.”

[084-294]To this Don Quixote made answer, “Señora, your highness must know that

[084-295]everything or almost everything that happens me transcends the ordinary

[084-296]limits of what happens to other knights-errant; whether it be that it

[084-297]is directed by the inscrutable will of destiny, or by the malice of

[084-298]some jealous enchanter. Now it is an established fact that all or most

[084-299]famous knights-errant have some special gift, one that of being proof

[084-300]against enchantment, another that of being made of such invulnerable

[084-301]flesh that he cannot be wounded, as was the famous Roland, one of the

[084-302]twelve peers of France, of whom it is related that he could not be

[084-303]wounded except in the sole of his left foot, and that it must be with

[084-304]the point of a stout pin and not with any other sort of weapon

[084-305]whatever; and so, when Bernardo del Carpio slew him at Roncesvalles,

[084-306]finding that he could not wound him with steel, he lifted him up from

[084-307]the ground in his arms and strangled him, calling to mind seasonably

[084-308]the death which Hercules inflicted on Antæus, the fierce giant that

[084-309]they say was the son of Terra. I would infer from what I have mentioned

[084-310]that perhaps I may have some gift of this kind, not that of being

[084-311]invulnerable, because experience has many times proved to me that I am

[084-312]of tender flesh and not at all impenetrable; nor that of being proof

[084-313]against enchantment, for I have already seen myself thrust into a cage,

[084-314]in which all the world would not have been able to confine me except by

[084-315]force of enchantments. But as I delivered myself from that one, I am

[084-316]inclined to believe that there is no other that can hurt me; and so,

[084-317]these enchanters, seeing that they cannot exert their vile craft

[084-318]against my person, revenge themselves on what I love most, and seek to

[084-319]rob me of life by maltreating that of Dulcinea in whom I live; and

[084-320]therefore I am convinced that when my squire carried my message to her,

[084-321]they changed her into a common peasant girl, engaged in such a mean

[084-322]occupation as sifting wheat; I have already said, however, that that

[084-323]wheat was not red wheat, nor wheat at all, but grains of orient pearl.

[084-324]And as a proof of all this, I must tell your highnesses that, coming to

[084-325]El Toboso a short time back, I was altogether unable to discover the

[084-326]palace of Dulcinea; and that the next day, though Sancho, my squire,

[084-327]saw her in her own proper shape, which is the fairest in the world, to

[084-328]me she appeared to be a coarse, ill-favoured farm-wench, and by no

[084-329]means a well-spoken one, she who is propriety itself. And so, as I am

[084-330]not and, so far as one can judge, cannot be enchanted, she it is that

[084-331]is enchanted, that is smitten, that is altered, changed, and

[084-332]transformed; in her have my enemies revenged themselves upon me, and

[084-333]for her shall I live in ceaseless tears, until I see her in her

[084-334]pristine state. I have mentioned this lest anybody should mind what

[084-335]Sancho said about Dulcinea’s winnowing or sifting; for, as they changed

[084-336]her to me, it is no wonder if they changed her to him. Dulcinea is

[084-337]illustrious and well-born, and of one of the gentle families of El

[084-338]Toboso, which are many, ancient, and good. Therein, most assuredly, not

[084-339]small is the share of the peerless Dulcinea, through whom her town will

[084-340]be famous and celebrated in ages to come, as Troy was through Helen,

[084-341]and Spain through La Cava, though with a better title and tradition.

[084-342]For another thing; I would have your graces understand that Sancho

[084-343]Panza is one of the drollest squires that ever served knight-errant;
