## Character and Origins

Don Quixote begins the novel as Alonso Quixano, a gentleman of modest means living "in a village of La Mancha, the name of which I have no desire to call to mind" (001-001). Cervantes describes him with precise physical detail:

> The age of this gentleman of ours was bordering on fifty; he was of a hardy habit, spare, gaunt-featured, a very early riser and a great sportsman. (001-012)

His surname is deliberately left uncertain — Quixada, Quesada, or Quexana — a metafictional gesture that establishes the novel's playful relationship with truth from its opening paragraphs.

Quixano's descent into madness is driven by obsessive reading. He "gave himself up to reading books of chivalry with such ardour and avidity that he almost entirely neglected the pursuit of his field-sports, and even the management of his property" (001-021). The obsession escalates until "he sold many an acre of tillageland to buy books of chivalry to read" (001-025).

The transformation is completed when he declares himself a knight-errant. He renames his horse Rocinante, chooses a lady-love ([[dulcinea-del-toboso]]), and sets out on his first sally.

## Knight-Errant

Don Quixote's conception of knighthood draws entirely from literary models, particularly Amadis of Gaul and Palmerin of England. His mission, as he repeatedly declares, is "to right every kind of wrong, and to expose himself to peril and danger from which, in the issue, he was to reap eternal renown and fame" (002-014).

His investiture as a knight occurs at an inn he mistakes for a castle — the first of many perceptual errors that structure the novel's comedy. The innkeeper, amused by his madness, performs a mock ceremony:

| Event | Chapter | Location | Perceptual Error |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------------|
| First sally | Ch. 2 | Road from La Mancha | Seeks adventures alone |
| Knighting | Ch. 3 | Inn | Inn → Castle |
| Windmill battle | Ch. 8 | Plains of La Mancha | Windmills → Giants |
| Galley slaves freed | Ch. 22 | Road | Slaves → Oppressed knights |

## Two Expeditions

The novel follows Don Quixote through two major expeditions. In Part I (1605), he travels alone on his first sally and returns home beaten. His second sally, accompanied by his newly-recruited squire [[sancho-panza]], produces the novel's most famous episodes, including the windmill adventure.

> At this point they came in sight of thirty or forty windmills that there are on that plain, and as soon as Don Quixote saw them he said to his squire, "Fortune is arranging matters for us better than we could have shaped our desires ourselves, for look there, friend Sancho Panza, where thirty or more monstrous giants present themselves." (008-002)

The windmills episode encapsulates the core dynamic of the novel: Don Quixote's literary imagination transforms mundane reality into chivalric adventure, while Sancho Panza's practical vision sees only windmills. Charging with his lance, Don Quixote is caught by a sail and hurled across the plain — defeated but undeterred.

Part II (1615) deepens the metafictional dimension, as Don Quixote and Sancho discover that their earlier adventures have been published and widely read. Characters they encounter have already formed opinions of them from the book — a device that makes Don Quixote both protagonist and reader of his own life.

## Death and Redemption

Don Quixote's story ends with his return to sanity and his death. A fever settles upon him, and after a long sleep, he awakens with his reason restored:

> "Blessed be Almighty God, who has shown me such goodness. In truth his mercies are boundless, and the sins of men can neither limit them nor keep them back!" (126-032)

He renounces his chivalric identity and reclaims his birth name. The transformation is recorded with legal precision:

> "I am no longer Don Quixote of La Mancha, but Alonso Quixano, whose way of life won for him the name of Good." (126-055)

His death is witnessed and documented — the curate "begged the notary to bear witness that Alonso Quixano the Good, commonly called Don Quixote of La Mancha, had passed away from this present life" (126-169). The narrator Cide Hamete then lays down his pen, declaring: "For me alone was Don Quixote born, and I for him; it was his to act, mine to write" (126-200).
