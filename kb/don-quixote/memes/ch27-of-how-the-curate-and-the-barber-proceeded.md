[027-002]The curate’s plan did not seem a bad one to the barber, but on the

[027-003]contrary so good that they immediately set about putting it in

[027-004]execution. They begged a petticoat and hood of the landlady, leaving

[027-005]her in pledge a new cassock of the curate’s; and the barber made a

[027-006]beard out of a grey-brown or red ox-tail in which the landlord used to

[027-007]stick his comb. The landlady asked them what they wanted these things

[027-008]for, and the curate told her in a few words about the madness of Don

[027-009]Quixote, and how this disguise was intended to get him away from the

[027-010]mountain where he then was. The landlord and landlady immediately came

[027-011]to the conclusion that the madman was their guest, the balsam man and

[027-012]master of the blanketed squire, and they told the curate all that had

[027-013]passed between him and them, not omitting what Sancho had been so

[027-014]silent about. Finally the landlady dressed up the curate in a style

[027-015]that left nothing to be desired; she put on him a cloth petticoat with

[027-016]black velvet stripes a palm broad, all slashed, and a bodice of green

[027-017]velvet set off by a binding of white satin, which as well as the

[027-018]petticoat must have been made in the time of king Wamba. The curate

[027-019]would not let them hood him, but put on his head a little quilted linen

[027-020]cap which he used for a night-cap, and bound his forehead with a strip

[027-021]of black silk, while with another he made a mask with which he

[027-022]concealed his beard and face very well. He then put on his hat, which

[027-023]was broad enough to serve him for an umbrella, and enveloping himself

[027-024]in his cloak seated himself woman-fashion on his mule, while the barber

[027-025]mounted his with a beard down to the waist of mingled red and white,

[027-026]for it was, as has been said, the tail of a clay-red ox.

[027-027]They took leave of all, and of the good Maritornes, who, sinner as she

[027-028]was, promised to pray a rosary of prayers that God might grant them

[027-029]success in such an arduous and Christian undertaking as that they had

[027-030]in hand. But hardly had he sallied forth from the inn when it struck

[027-031]the curate that he was doing wrong in rigging himself out in that

[027-032]fashion, as it was an indecorous thing for a priest to dress himself

[027-033]that way even though much might depend upon it; and saying so to the

[027-034]barber he begged him to change dresses, as it was fitter he should be

[027-035]the distressed damsel, while he himself would play the squire’s part,

[027-036]which would be less derogatory to his dignity; otherwise he was

[027-037]resolved to have nothing more to do with the matter, and let the devil

[027-038]take Don Quixote. Just at this moment Sancho came up, and on seeing the

[027-039]pair in such a costume he was unable to restrain his laughter; the

[027-040]barber, however, agreed to do as the curate wished, and, altering their

[027-041]plan, the curate went on to instruct him how to play his part and what

[027-042]to say to Don Quixote to induce and compel him to come with them and

[027-043]give up his fancy for the place he had chosen for his idle penance. The

[027-044]barber told him he could manage it properly without any instruction,

[027-045]and as he did not care to dress himself up until they were near where

[027-046]Don Quixote was, he folded up the garments, and the curate adjusted his

[027-047]beard, and they set out under the guidance of Sancho Panza, who went

[027-048]along telling them of the encounter with the madman they met in the

[027-049]Sierra, saying nothing, however, about the finding of the valise and

[027-050]its contents; for with all his simplicity the lad was a trifle

[027-051]covetous.

[027-052]The next day they reached the place where Sancho had laid the

[027-053]broom-branches as marks to direct him to where he had left his master,

[027-054]and recognising it he told them that here was the entrance, and that

[027-055]they would do well to dress themselves, if that was required to deliver

[027-056]his master; for they had already told him that going in this guise and

[027-057]dressing in this way were of the highest importance in order to rescue

[027-058]his master from the pernicious life he had adopted; and they charged

[027-059]him strictly not to tell his master who they were, or that he knew

[027-060]them, and should he ask, as ask he would, if he had given the letter to

[027-061]Dulcinea, to say that he had, and that, as she did not know how to

[027-062]read, she had given an answer by word of mouth, saying that she

[027-063]commanded him, on pain of her displeasure, to come and see her at once;

[027-064]and it was a very important matter for himself, because in this way and

[027-065]with what they meant to say to him they felt sure of bringing him back

[027-066]to a better mode of life and inducing him to take immediate steps to

[027-067]become an emperor or monarch, for there was no fear of his becoming an

[027-068]archbishop. All this Sancho listened to and fixed it well in his

[027-069]memory, and thanked them heartily for intending to recommend his master

[027-070]to be an emperor instead of an archbishop, for he felt sure that in the

[027-071]way of bestowing rewards on their squires emperors could do more than

[027-072]archbishops-errant. He said, too, that it would be as well for him to

[027-073]go on before them to find him, and give him his lady’s answer; for that

[027-074]perhaps might be enough to bring him away from the place without

[027-075]putting them to all this trouble. They approved of what Sancho

[027-076]proposed, and resolved to wait for him until he brought back word of

[027-077]having found his master.

[027-078]Sancho pushed on into the glens of the Sierra, leaving them in one

[027-079]through which there flowed a little gentle rivulet, and where the rocks

[027-080]and trees afforded a cool and grateful shade. It was an August day with

[027-081]all the heat of one, and the heat in those parts is intense, and the

[027-082]hour was three in the afternoon, all which made the spot the more

[027-083]inviting and tempted them to wait there for Sancho’s return, which they

[027-084]did. They were reposing, then, in the shade, when a voice unaccompanied

[027-085]by the notes of any instrument, but sweet and pleasing in its tone,

[027-086]reached their ears, at which they were not a little astonished, as the

[027-087]place did not seem to them likely quarters for one who sang so well;

[027-088]for though it is often said that shepherds of rare voice are to be

[027-089]found in the woods and fields, this is rather a flight of the poet’s

[027-090]fancy than the truth. And still more surprised were they when they

[027-091]perceived that what they heard sung were the verses not of rustic

[027-092]shepherds, but of the polished wits of the city; and so it proved, for

[027-093]the verses they heard were these:

[027-094]What makes my quest of happiness seem vain?

[027-095]Disdain.

[027-096]What bids me to abandon hope of ease?

[027-097]Jealousies.

[027-098]What holds my heart in anguish of suspense?

[027-099]Absence.

[027-100]If that be so, then for my grief

[027-101]Where shall I turn to seek relief,

[027-102]When hope on every side lies slain

[027-103]By Absence, Jealousies, Disdain?

[027-104]What the prime cause of all my woe doth prove?

[027-105]Love.

[027-106]What at my glory ever looks askance?

[027-107]Chance.

[027-108]Whence is permission to afflict me given?

[027-109]Heaven.

[027-110]If that be so, I but await

[027-111]The stroke of a resistless fate,

[027-112]Since, working for my woe, these three,

[027-113]Love, Chance and Heaven, in league I see.

[027-114]What must I do to find a remedy?

[027-115]Die.

[027-116]What is the lure for love when coy and strange?

[027-117]Change.

[027-118]What, if all fail, will cure the heart of sadness?

[027-119]Madness.

[027-120]If that be so, it is but folly

[027-121]To seek a cure for melancholy:

[027-122]Ask where it lies; the answer saith

[027-123]In Change, in Madness, or in Death.

[027-124]The hour, the summer season, the solitary place, the voice and skill of

[027-125]the singer, all contributed to the wonder and delight of the two

[027-126]listeners, who remained still waiting to hear something more; finding,

[027-127]however, that the silence continued some little time, they resolved to

[027-128]go in search of the musician who sang with so fine a voice; but just as

[027-129]they were about to do so they were checked by the same voice, which

[027-130]once more fell upon their ears, singing this

SONNET

[027-131]When heavenward, holy Friendship, thou didst go

[027-132]Soaring to seek thy home beyond the sky,

[027-133]And take thy seat among the saints on high,

[027-134]It was thy will to leave on earth below

[027-135]Thy semblance, and upon it to bestow

[027-136]Thy veil, wherewith at times hypocrisy,

[027-137]Parading in thy shape, deceives the eye,

[027-138]And makes its vileness bright as virtue show.

[027-139]Friendship, return to us, or force the cheat

[027-140]That wears it now, thy livery to restore,

[027-141]By aid whereof sincerity is slain.

[027-142]If thou wilt not unmask thy counterfeit,

[027-143]This earth will be the prey of strife once more,

[027-144]As when primæval discord held its reign.

[027-145]The song ended with a deep sigh, and again the listeners remained

[027-146]waiting attentively for the singer to resume; but perceiving that the

[027-147]music had now turned to sobs and heart-rending moans they determined to

[027-148]find out who the unhappy being could be whose voice was as rare as his

[027-149]sighs were piteous, and they had not proceeded far when on turning the

[027-150]corner of a rock they discovered a man of the same aspect and

[027-151]appearance as Sancho had described to them when he told them the story

[027-152]of Cardenio. He, showing no astonishment when he saw them, stood still

[027-153]with his head bent down upon his breast like one in deep thought,

[027-154]without raising his eyes to look at them after the first glance when

[027-155]they suddenly came upon him. The curate, who was aware of his

[027-156]misfortune and recognised him by the description, being a man of good

[027-157]address, approached him and in a few sensible words entreated and urged

[027-158]him to quit a life of such misery, lest he should end it there, which

[027-159]would be the greatest of all misfortunes. Cardenio was then in his

[027-160]right mind, free from any attack of that madness which so frequently

[027-161]carried him away, and seeing them dressed in a fashion so unusual among

[027-162]the frequenters of those wilds, could not help showing some surprise,

[027-163]especially when he heard them speak of his case as if it were a

[027-164]well-known matter (for the curate’s words gave him to understand as

[027-165]much) so he replied to them thus:

[027-166]“I see plainly, sirs, whoever you may be, that Heaven, whose care it is

[027-167]to succour the good, and even the wicked very often, here, in this

[027-168]remote spot, cut off from human intercourse, sends me, though I deserve

[027-169]it not, those who seek to draw me away from this to some better

[027-170]retreat, showing me by many and forcible arguments how unreasonably I

[027-171]act in leading the life I do; but as they know, that if I escape from

[027-172]this evil I shall fall into another still greater, perhaps they will

[027-173]set me down as a weak-minded man, or, what is worse, one devoid of

[027-174]reason; nor would it be any wonder, for I myself can perceive that the

[027-175]effect of the recollection of my misfortunes is so great and works so

[027-176]powerfully to my ruin, that in spite of myself I become at times like a

[027-177]stone, without feeling or consciousness; and I come to feel the truth

[027-178]of it when they tell me and show me proofs of the things I have done

[027-179]when the terrible fit overmasters me; and all I can do is bewail my lot

[027-180]in vain, and idly curse my destiny, and plead for my madness by telling

[027-181]how it was caused, to any that care to hear it; for no reasonable

[027-182]beings on learning the cause will wonder at the effects; and if they

[027-183]cannot help me at least they will not blame me, and the repugnance they

[027-184]feel at my wild ways will turn into pity for my woes. If it be, sirs,

[027-185]that you are here with the same design as others have come with, before

[027-186]you proceed with your wise arguments, I entreat you to hear the story

[027-187]of my countless misfortunes, for perhaps when you have heard it you

[027-188]will spare yourselves the trouble you would take in offering

[027-189]consolation to grief that is beyond the reach of it.”

[027-190]As they, both of them, desired nothing more than to hear from his own

[027-191]lips the cause of his suffering, they entreated him to tell it,

[027-192]promising not to do anything for his relief or comfort that he did not

[027-193]wish; and thereupon the unhappy gentleman began his sad story in nearly

[027-194]the same words and manner in which he had related it to Don Quixote and

[027-195]the goatherd a few days before, when, through Master Elisabad, and Don

[027-196]Quixote’s scrupulous observance of what was due to chivalry, the tale

[027-197]was left unfinished, as this history has already recorded; but now

[027-198]fortunately the mad fit kept off, allowed him to tell it to the end;

[027-199]and so, coming to the incident of the note which Don Fernando had found

[027-200]in the volume of “Amadis of Gaul,” Cardenio said that he remembered it

[027-201]perfectly and that it was in these words:

[027-202]“_Luscinda to Cardenio._

[027-203]“Every day I discover merits in you that oblige and compel me to hold

[027-204]you in higher estimation; so if you desire to relieve me of this

[027-205]obligation without cost to my honour, you may easily do so. I have a

[027-206]father who knows you and loves me dearly, who without putting any

[027-207]constraint on my inclination will grant what will be reasonable for you

[027-208]to have, if it be that you value me as you say and as I believe you

[027-209]do.”

[027-210]“By this letter I was induced, as I told you, to demand Luscinda for my

[027-211]wife, and it was through it that Luscinda came to be regarded by Don

[027-212]Fernando as one of the most discreet and prudent women of the day, and

[027-213]this letter it was that suggested his design of ruining me before mine

[027-214]could be carried into effect. I told Don Fernando that all Luscinda’s

[027-215]father was waiting for was that mine should ask her of him, which I did

[027-216]not dare to suggest to him, fearing that he would not consent to do so;

[027-217]not because he did not know perfectly well the rank, goodness, virtue,

[027-218]and beauty of Luscinda, and that she had qualities that would do honour

[027-219]to any family in Spain, but because I was aware that he did not wish me

[027-220]to marry so soon, before seeing what the Duke Ricardo would do for me.

[027-221]In short, I told him I did not venture to mention it to my father, as

[027-222]well on account of that difficulty, as of many others that discouraged

[027-223]me though I knew not well what they were, only that it seemed to me

[027-224]that what I desired was never to come to pass. To all this Don Fernando

[027-225]answered that he would take it upon himself to speak to my father, and

[027-226]persuade him to speak to Luscinda’s father. O, ambitious Marius! O,

[027-227]cruel Catiline! O, wicked Sylla! O, perfidious Ganelon! O, treacherous

[027-228]Vellido! O, vindictive Julian! O, covetous Judas! Traitor, cruel,

[027-229]vindictive, and perfidious, wherein had this poor wretch failed in his

[027-232]what counsels did I give that had not the furtherance of thy honour and

[027-233]welfare for their aim? But, woe is me, wherefore do I complain? for

[027-234]sure it is that when misfortunes spring from the stars, descending from

[027-235]on high they fall upon us with such fury and violence that no power on

[027-238]intelligent, bound to me by gratitude for my services, one that could

[027-241]that was not even yet in my possession? But laying aside these useless

[027-242]and unavailing reflections, let us take up the broken thread of my

[027-243]unhappy story.

[027-244]“To proceed, then: Don Fernando finding my presence an obstacle to the

[027-245]execution of his treacherous and wicked design, resolved to send me to

[027-246]his elder brother under the pretext of asking money from him to pay for

[027-247]six horses which, purposely, and with the sole object of sending me

[027-248]away that he might the better carry out his infernal scheme, he had

[027-249]purchased the very day he offered to speak to my father, and the price

[027-250]of which he now desired me to fetch. Could I have anticipated this

[027-252]that, I offered with the greatest pleasure to go at once, in my

[027-253]satisfaction at the good bargain that had been made. That night I spoke

[027-254]with Luscinda, and told her what had been agreed upon with Don

[027-255]Fernando, and how I had strong hopes of our fair and reasonable wishes

[027-257]Fernando, bade me try to return speedily, as she believed the

[027-258]fulfilment of our desires would be delayed only so long as my father

[027-343]losing the prize I had won by so many years of love and devotion, lent

[027-347]had brought me the letter, and fortune was pleased to be for once so
