RAREST ADVENTURES RELATED IN THIS VERACIOUS HISTORY

[023-002]Seeing himself served in this way, Don Quixote said to his squire, “I

[023-003]have always heard it said, Sancho, that to do good to boors is to throw

[023-004]water into the sea. If I had believed thy words, I should have avoided

[023-005]this trouble; but it is done now, it is only to have patience and take

[023-006]warning for the future.”

c23b.jpg (318K)


[023-007]“Your worship will take warning as much as I am a Turk,” returned

[023-008]Sancho; “but, as you say this mischief might have been avoided if you

[023-009]had believed me, believe me now, and a still greater one will be

[023-010]avoided; for I tell you chivalry is of no account with the Holy

[023-011]Brotherhood, and they don’t care two maravedis for all the

[023-012]knights-errant in the world; and I can tell you I fancy I hear their

[023-013]arrows whistling past my ears this minute.”

[023-014]“Thou art a coward by nature, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “but lest thou

[023-015]shouldst say I am obstinate, and that I never do as thou dost advise,

[023-016]this once I will take thy advice, and withdraw out of reach of that

[023-017]fury thou so dreadest; but it must be on one condition, that never, in

[023-018]life or in death, thou art to say to anyone that I retired or withdrew

[023-019]from this danger out of fear, but only in compliance with thy

[023-020]entreaties; for if thou sayest otherwise thou wilt lie therein, and

[023-021]from this time to that, and from that to this, I give thee lie, and say

[023-022]thou liest and wilt lie every time thou thinkest or sayest it; and

[023-023]answer me not again; for at the mere thought that I am withdrawing or

[023-024]retiring from any danger, above all from this, which does seem to carry

[023-025]some little shadow of fear with it, I am ready to take my stand here

[023-026]and await alone, not only that Holy Brotherhood you talk of and dread,

[023-027]but the brothers of the twelve tribes of Israel, and the Seven

[023-028]Maccabees, and Castor and Pollux, and all the brothers and brotherhoods

[023-029]in the world.”

[023-030]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “to retire is not to flee, and there is no

[023-031]wisdom in waiting when danger outweighs hope, and it is the part of

[023-032]wise men to preserve themselves to-day for to-morrow, and not risk all

[023-033]in one day; and let me tell you, though I am a clown and a boor, I have

[023-034]got some notion of what they call safe conduct; so repent not of having

[023-035]taken my advice, but mount Rocinante if you can, and if not I will help

[023-036]you; and follow me, for my mother-wit tells me we have more need of

[023-037]legs than hands just now.”

[023-038]Don Quixote mounted without replying, and, Sancho leading the way on

[023-039]his ass, they entered the side of the Sierra Morena, which was close

[023-040]by, as it was Sancho’s design to cross it entirely and come out again

[023-041]at El Viso or Almodóvar del Campo, and hide for some days among its

[023-042]crags so as to escape the search of the Brotherhood should they come to

[023-043]look for them. He was encouraged in this by perceiving that the stock

[023-044]of provisions carried by the ass had come safe out of the fray with the

[023-045]galley slaves, a circumstance that he regarded as a miracle, seeing how

[023-046]they pillaged and ransacked.

[023-047]That night they reached the very heart of the Sierra Morena, where it

[023-048]seemed prudent to Sancho to pass the night and even some days, at least

[023-049]as many as the stores he carried might last, and so they encamped

[023-050]between two rocks and among some cork trees; but fatal destiny, which,

[023-051]according to the opinion of those who have not the light of the true

[023-052]faith, directs, arranges, and settles everything in its own way, so

[023-053]ordered it that Gines de Pasamonte, the famous knave and thief who by

[023-054]the virtue and madness of Don Quixote had been released from the chain,

[023-055]driven by fear of the Holy Brotherhood, which he had good reason to

[023-056]dread, resolved to take hiding in the mountains; and his fate and fear

[023-057]led him to the same spot to which Don Quixote and Sancho Panza had been

[023-058]led by theirs, just in time to recognise them and leave them to fall

[023-059]asleep: and as the wicked are always ungrateful, and necessity leads to

[023-060]evildoing, and immediate advantage overcomes all considerations of the

[023-061]future, Gines, who was neither grateful nor well-principled, made up

[023-062]his mind to steal Sancho Panza’s ass, not troubling himself about

[023-063]Rocinante, as being a prize that was no good either to pledge or sell.

[023-064]While Sancho slept he stole his ass, and before day dawned he was far

[023-065]out of reach.

[023-066]Aurora made her appearance bringing gladness to the earth but sadness

[023-067]to Sancho Panza, for he found that his Dapple was missing, and seeing

[023-068]himself bereft of him he began the saddest and most doleful lament in

[023-069]the world, so loud that Don Quixote awoke at his exclamations and heard

[023-070]him saying, “O son of my bowels, born in my very house, my children’s

[023-071]plaything, my wife’s joy, the envy of my neighbours, relief of my

[023-072]burdens, and lastly, half supporter of myself, for with the

[023-073]six-and-twenty maravedis thou didst earn me daily I met half my

[023-074]charges.”

[023-075]Don Quixote, when he heard the lament and learned the cause, consoled

[023-076]Sancho with the best arguments he could, entreating him to be patient,

[023-077]and promising to give him a letter of exchange ordering three out of

[023-078]five ass-colts that he had at home to be given to him. Sancho took

[023-079]comfort at this, dried his tears, suppressed his sobs, and returned

[023-080]thanks for the kindness shown him by Don Quixote. He on his part was

[023-081]rejoiced to the heart on entering the mountains, as they seemed to him

[023-082]to be just the place for the adventures he was in quest of. They

[023-083]brought back to his memory the marvellous adventures that had befallen

[023-084]knights-errant in like solitudes and wilds, and he went along

[023-085]reflecting on these things, so absorbed and carried away by them that

[023-086]he had no thought for anything else.

[023-087]Nor had Sancho any other care (now that he fancied he was travelling in

[023-088]a safe quarter) than to satisfy his appetite with such remains as were

[023-089]left of the clerical spoils, and so he marched behind his master laden

[023-090]with what Dapple used to carry, emptying the sack and packing his

[023-091]paunch, and so long as he could go that way, he would not have given a

[023-092]farthing to meet with another adventure.

[023-093]While so engaged he raised his eyes and saw that his master had halted,

[023-094]and was trying with the point of his pike to lift some bulky object

[023-095]that lay upon the ground, on which he hastened to join him and help him

[023-096]if it were needful, and reached him just as with the point of the pike

[023-097]he was raising a saddle-pad with a valise attached to it, half or

[023-098]rather wholly rotten and torn; but so heavy were they that Sancho had

[023-099]to help to take them up, and his master directed him to see what the

[023-100]valise contained. Sancho did so with great alacrity, and though the

[023-101]valise was secured by a chain and padlock, from its torn and rotten

[023-102]condition he was able to see its contents, which were four shirts of

[023-103]fine holland, and other articles of linen no less curious than clean;

[023-104]and in a handkerchief he found a good lot of gold crowns, and as soon

[023-105]as he saw them he exclaimed:

[023-106]“Blessed be all Heaven for sending us an adventure that is good for

[023-107]something!”

[023-108]Searching further he found a little memorandum book richly bound; this

[023-109]Don Quixote asked of him, telling him to take the money and keep it for

[023-110]himself. Sancho kissed his hands for the favour, and cleared the valise

[023-111]of its linen, which he stowed away in the provision sack. Considering

[023-112]the whole matter, Don Quixote observed:

[023-113]“It seems to me, Sancho—and it is impossible it can be otherwise—that

[023-114]some strayed traveller must have crossed this sierra and been attacked

[023-115]and slain by footpads, who brought him to this remote spot to bury

[023-116]him.”

[023-117]“That cannot be,” answered Sancho, “because if they had been robbers

[023-118]they would not have left this money.”

[023-119]“Thou art right,” said Don Quixote, “and I cannot guess or explain what

[023-120]this may mean; but stay; let us see if in this memorandum book there is

[023-121]anything written by which we may be able to trace out or discover what

[023-122]we want to know.”

[023-123]He opened it, and the first thing he found in it, written roughly but

[023-124]in a very good hand, was a sonnet, and reading it aloud that Sancho

[023-125]might hear it, he found that it ran as follows:

SONNET

[023-126]Or Love is lacking in intelligence,

[023-127]Or to the height of cruelty attains,

[023-128]Or else it is my doom to suffer pains

[023-129]Beyond the measure due to my offence.

[023-130]But if Love be a God, it follows thence

[023-131]That he knows all, and certain it remains

[023-132]No God loves cruelty; then who ordains

[023-133]This penance that enthrals while it torments?

[023-134]It were a falsehood, Chloe, thee to name;

[023-135]Such evil with such goodness cannot live;

[023-136]And against Heaven I dare not charge the blame,

[023-137]I only know it is my fate to die.

[023-138]To him who knows not whence his malady

[023-139]A miracle alone a cure can give.

[023-140]“There is nothing to be learned from that rhyme,” said Sancho, “unless

[023-141]by that clue there’s in it, one may draw out the ball of the whole

[023-142]matter.”

[023-143]“What clue is there?” said Don Quixote.

[023-144]“I thought your worship spoke of a clue in it,” said Sancho.

[023-145]“I only said Chloe,” replied Don Quixote; “and that no doubt, is the

[023-146]name of the lady of whom the author of the sonnet complains; and,

[023-147]faith, he must be a tolerable poet, or I know little of the craft.”

[023-148]“Then your worship understands rhyming too?”

[023-149]“And better than thou thinkest,” replied Don Quixote, “as thou shalt

[023-150]see when thou carriest a letter written in verse from beginning to end

[023-151]to my lady Dulcinea del Toboso, for I would have thee know, Sancho,

[023-152]that all or most of the knights-errant in days of yore were great

[023-153]troubadours and great musicians, for both of these accomplishments, or

[023-154]more properly speaking gifts, are the peculiar property of

[023-155]lovers-errant: true it is that the verses of the knights of old have

[023-156]more spirit than neatness in them.”

[023-157]“Read more, your worship,” said Sancho, “and you will find something

[023-158]that will enlighten us.”

[023-159]Don Quixote turned the page and said, “This is prose and seems to be a

[023-160]letter.”

[023-161]“A correspondence letter, señor?”

[023-162]“From the beginning it seems to be a love letter,” replied Don Quixote.

[023-163]“Then let your worship read it aloud,” said Sancho, “for I am very fond

[023-164]of love matters.”

[023-165]“With all my heart,” said Don Quixote, and reading it aloud as Sancho

[023-166]had requested him, he found it ran thus:

[023-167]Thy false promise and my sure misfortune carry me to a place whence the

[023-168]news of my death will reach thy ears before the words of my complaint.

[023-169]Ungrateful one, thou hast rejected me for one more wealthy, but not

[023-170]more worthy; but if virtue were esteemed wealth I should neither envy

[023-171]the fortunes of others nor weep for misfortunes of my own. What thy

[023-172]beauty raised up thy deeds have laid low; by it I believed thee to be

[023-173]an angel, by them I know thou art a woman. Peace be with thee who hast

[023-174]sent war to me, and Heaven grant that the deceit of thy husband be ever

[023-175]hidden from thee, so that thou repent not of what thou hast done, and I

[023-176]reap not a revenge I would not have.

[023-177]When he had finished the letter, Don Quixote said, “There is less to be

[023-178]gathered from this than from the verses, except that he who wrote it is

[023-179]some rejected lover;” and turning over nearly all the pages of the book

[023-180]he found more verses and letters, some of which he could read, while

[023-181]others he could not; but they were all made up of complaints, laments,

[023-182]misgivings, desires and aversions, favours and rejections, some

[023-183]rapturous, some doleful. While Don Quixote examined the book, Sancho

[023-184]examined the valise, not leaving a corner in the whole of it or in the

[023-185]pad that he did not search, peer into, and explore, or seam that he did

[023-186]not rip, or tuft of wool that he did not pick to pieces, lest anything

[023-187]should escape for want of care and pains; so keen was the covetousness

[023-188]excited in him by the discovery of the crowns, which amounted to near a

[023-189]hundred; and though he found no more booty, he held the blanket

[023-190]flights, balsam vomits, stake benedictions, carriers’ fisticuffs,

[023-191]missing alforjas, stolen coat, and all the hunger, thirst, and

[023-192]weariness he had endured in the service of his good master, cheap at

[023-193]the price; as he considered himself more than fully indemnified for all

[023-194]by the payment he received in the gift of the treasure-trove.

[023-195]The Knight of the Rueful Countenance was still very anxious to find out

[023-196]who the owner of the valise could be, conjecturing from the sonnet and

[023-197]letter, from the money in gold, and from the fineness of the shirts,

[023-198]that he must be some lover of distinction whom the scorn and cruelty of

[023-199]his lady had driven to some desperate course; but as in that

[023-200]uninhabited and rugged spot there was no one to be seen of whom he

[023-201]could inquire, he saw nothing else for it but to push on, taking

[023-202]whatever road Rocinante chose—which was where he could make his

[023-203]way—firmly persuaded that among these wilds he could not fail to meet

[023-204]some rare adventure. As he went along, then, occupied with these

[023-205]thoughts, he perceived on the summit of a height that rose before their

[023-206]eyes a man who went springing from rock to rock and from tussock to

[023-207]tussock with marvellous agility. As well as he could make out he was

[023-208]unclad, with a thick black beard, long tangled hair, and bare legs and

[023-209]feet, his thighs were covered by breeches apparently of tawny velvet

[023-210]but so ragged that they showed his skin in several places.

[023-211]He was bareheaded, and notwithstanding the swiftness with which he

[023-212]passed as has been described, the Knight of the Rueful Countenance

[023-213]observed and noted all these trifles, and though he made the attempt,

[023-214]he was unable to follow him, for it was not granted to the feebleness

[023-215]of Rocinante to make way over such rough ground, he being, moreover,

[023-216]slow-paced and sluggish by nature. Don Quixote at once came to the

[023-217]conclusion that this was the owner of the saddle-pad and of the valise,

[023-218]and made up his mind to go in search of him, even though he should have

[023-219]to wander a year in those mountains before he found him, and so he

[023-220]directed Sancho to take a short cut over one side of the mountain,

[023-221]while he himself went by the other, and perhaps by this means they

[023-222]might light upon this man who had passed so quickly out of their sight.

[023-223]“I could not do that,” said Sancho, “for when I separate from your

[023-224]worship fear at once lays hold of me, and assails me with all sorts of

[023-225]panics and fancies; and let what I now say be a notice that from this

[023-226]time forth I am not going to stir a finger’s width from your presence.”

[023-227]“It shall be so,” said he of the Rueful Countenance, “and I am very

[023-228]glad that thou art willing to rely on my courage, which will never fail

[023-229]thee, even though the soul in thy body fail thee; so come on now behind

[023-230]me slowly as well as thou canst, and make lanterns of thine eyes; let

[023-231]us make the circuit of this ridge; perhaps we shall light upon this man

[023-232]that we saw, who no doubt is no other than the owner of what we found.”

[023-233]To which Sancho made answer, “Far better would it be not to look for

[023-234]him, for, if we find him, and he happens to be the owner of the money,

[023-235]it is plain I must restore it; it would be better, therefore, that

[023-236]without taking this needless trouble, I should keep possession of it

[023-237]until in some other less meddlesome and officious way the real owner

[023-238]may be discovered; and perhaps that will be when I shall have spent it,

[023-239]and then the king will hold me harmless.”

[023-240]“Thou art wrong there, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “for now that we have

[023-241]a suspicion who the owner is, and have him almost before us, we are

[023-242]bound to seek him and make restitution; and if we do not see him, the

[023-243]strong suspicion we have as to his being the owner makes us as guilty

[023-244]as if he were so; and so, friend Sancho, let not our search for him

[023-245]give thee any uneasiness, for if we find him it will relieve mine.”

[023-246]And so saying he gave Rocinante the spur, and Sancho followed him on

[023-247]foot and loaded, and after having partly made the circuit of the

[023-248]mountain they found lying in a ravine, dead and half devoured by dogs

[023-249]and pecked by jackdaws, a mule saddled and bridled, all which still

[023-250]further strengthened their suspicion that he who had fled was the owner

[023-251]of the mule and the saddle-pad.

[023-252]As they stood looking at it they heard a whistle like that of a

[023-253]shepherd watching his flock, and suddenly on their left there appeared

[023-254]a great number of goats and behind them on the summit of the mountain

[023-255]the goatherd in charge of them, a man advanced in years. Don Quixote

[023-256]called aloud to him and begged him to come down to where they stood. He

[023-257]shouted in return, asking what had brought them to that spot, seldom or

[023-258]never trodden except by the feet of goats, or of the wolves and other

[023-259]wild beasts that roamed around. Sancho in return bade him come down,

[023-260]and they would explain all to him.

[023-261]The goatherd descended, and reaching the place where Don Quixote stood,

[023-262]he said, “I will wager you are looking at that hack mule that lies dead

[023-263]in the hollow there, and, faith, it has been lying there now these six

[023-264]months; tell me, have you come upon its master about here?”

[023-265]“We have come upon nobody,” answered Don Quixote, “nor on anything

[023-266]except a saddle-pad and a little valise that we found not far from

[023-267]this.”

[023-268]“I found it too,” said the goatherd, “but I would not lift it nor go

[023-269]near it for fear of some ill-luck or being charged with theft, for the

[023-270]devil is crafty, and things rise up under one’s feet to make one fall

[023-271]without knowing why or wherefore.”

[023-272]“That’s exactly what I say,” said Sancho; “I found it too, and I would

[023-273]not go within a stone’s throw of it; there I left it, and there it lies

[023-274]just as it was, for I don’t want a dog with a bell.”

[023-275]“Tell me, good man,” said Don Quixote, “do you know who is the owner of

[023-276]this property?”

[023-277]“All I can tell you,” said the goatherd, “is that about six months ago,

[023-278]more or less, there arrived at a shepherd’s hut three leagues, perhaps,

[023-279]away from this, a youth of well-bred appearance and manners, mounted on

[023-280]that same mule which lies dead here, and with the same saddle-pad and

[023-281]valise which you say you found and did not touch. He asked us what part

[023-283]where we now are; and so in truth it is, for if you push on half a

[023-284]league farther, perhaps you will not be able to find your way out; and

[023-285]I am wondering how you have managed to come here, for there is no road

[023-287]answer the youth turned about and made for the place we pointed out to

[023-288]him, leaving us all charmed with his good looks, and wondering at his

[023-289]question and the haste with which we saw him depart in the direction of

[023-290]the sierra; and after that we saw him no more, until some days

[023-291]afterwards he crossed the path of one of our shepherds, and without

[023-292]saying a word to him, came up to him and gave him several cuffs and

[023-293]kicks, and then turned to the ass with our provisions and took all the

[023-294]bread and cheese it carried, and having done this made off back again

[023-297]most remote portion of this sierra, at the end of which we found him

[023-299]with great gentleness, with his dress now torn and his face so

[023-300]disfigured and burned by the sun, that we hardly recognised him but

[023-303]courteously, and in a few well-spoken words he told us not to wonder at

[023-304]seeing him going about in this guise, as it was binding upon him in

[023-305]order that he might work out a penance which for his many sins had been

[023-306]imposed upon him. We asked him to tell us who he was, but we were never

[023-307]able to find out from him: we begged of him too, when he was in want of

[023-308]food, which he could not do without, to tell us where we should find

[023-309]him, as we would bring it to him with all good-will and readiness; or

[023-310]if this were not to his taste, at least to come and ask it of us and

[023-311]not take it by force from the shepherds. He thanked us for the offer,

[023-312]begged pardon for the late assault, and promised for the future to ask

[023-313]it in God’s name without offering violence to anybody. As for fixed

[023-314]abode, he said he had no other than that which chance offered wherever

[023-315]night might overtake him; and his words ended in an outburst of weeping

[023-316]so bitter that we who listened to him must have been very stones had we

[023-317]not joined him in it, comparing what we saw of him the first time with

[023-318]what we saw now; for, as I said, he was a graceful and gracious youth,

[023-319]and in his courteous and polished language showed himself to be of good

[023-320]birth and courtly breeding, and rustics as we were that listened to

[023-321]him, even to our rusticity his gentle bearing sufficed to make it

[023-322]plain.

[023-323]“But in the midst of his conversation he stopped and became silent,

[023-324]keeping his eyes fixed upon the ground for some time, during which we

[023-325]stood still waiting anxiously to see what would come of this

[023-326]abstraction; and with no little pity, for from his behaviour, now

[023-327]staring at the ground with fixed gaze and eyes wide open without moving

[023-328]an eyelid, again closing them, compressing his lips and raising his

[023-329]eyebrows, we could perceive plainly that a fit of madness of some kind

[023-330]had come upon him; and before long he showed that what we imagined was

[023-331]the truth, for he arose in a fury from the ground where he had thrown

[023-332]himself, and attacked the first he found near him with such rage and

[023-333]fierceness that if we had not dragged him off him, he would have beaten

[023-334]or bitten him to death, all the while exclaiming, ‘Oh faithless

[023-335]Fernando, here, here shalt thou pay the penalty of the wrong thou hast

[023-336]done me; these hands shall tear out that heart of thine, abode and

[023-337]dwelling of all iniquity, but of deceit and fraud above all; and to

[023-338]these he added other words all in effect upbraiding this Fernando and

[023-339]charging him with treachery and faithlessness.

[023-340]“We forced him to release his hold with no little difficulty, and

[023-341]without another word he left us, and rushing off plunged in among these

[023-342]brakes and brambles, so as to make it impossible for us to follow him;

[023-343]from this we suppose that madness comes upon him from time to time, and

[023-344]that someone called Fernando must have done him a wrong of a grievous

[023-345]nature such as the condition to which it had brought him seemed to

[023-346]show. All this has been since then confirmed on those occasions, and

[023-347]they have been many, on which he has crossed our path, at one time to

[023-348]beg the shepherds to give him some of the food they carry, at another

[023-349]to take it from them by force; for when there is a fit of madness upon

[023-350]him, even though the shepherds offer it freely, he will not accept it

[023-351]but snatches it from them by dint of blows; but when he is in his
