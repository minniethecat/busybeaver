[111-001]WHEREIN IS RELATED THE STRANGE THING, WHICH MAY BE REGARDED AS AN

[111-002]ADVENTURE, THAT HAPPENED DON QUIXOTE

p59a.jpg (126K)


[111-003]A clear limpid spring which they discovered in a cool grove relieved

[111-004]Don Quixote and Sancho of the dust and fatigue due to the unpolite

[111-005]behaviour of the bulls, and by the side of this, having turned Dapple

[111-006]and Rocinante loose without headstall or bridle, the forlorn pair,

[111-007]master and man, seated themselves. Sancho had recourse to the larder of

[111-008]his alforjas and took out of them what he called the prog; Don Quixote

[111-009]rinsed his mouth and bathed his face, by which cooling process his

[111-010]flagging energies were revived. Out of pure vexation he remained

[111-011]without eating, and out of pure politeness Sancho did not venture to

[111-012]touch a morsel of what was before him, but waited for his master to act

[111-013]as taster. Seeing, however, that, absorbed in thought, he was

[111-014]forgetting to carry the bread to his mouth, he said never a word, and

[111-015]trampling every sort of good breeding under foot, began to stow away in

[111-016]his paunch the bread and cheese that came to his hand.

p59b.jpg (370K)


[111-017]“Eat, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote; “support life, which is of

[111-018]more consequence to thee than to me, and leave me to die under the pain

[111-019]of my thoughts and pressure of my misfortunes. I was born, Sancho, to

[111-020]live dying, and thou to die eating; and to prove the truth of what I

[111-021]say, look at me, printed in histories, famed in arms, courteous in

[111-022]behaviour, honoured by princes, courted by maidens; and after all, when

[111-023]I looked forward to palms, triumphs, and crowns, won and earned by my

[111-024]valiant deeds, I have this morning seen myself trampled on, kicked, and

[111-025]crushed by the feet of unclean and filthy animals. This thought blunts

[111-026]my teeth, paralyses my jaws, cramps my hands, and robs me of all

[111-027]appetite for food; so much so that I have a mind to let myself die of

[111-028]hunger, the cruelest death of all deaths.”

[111-029]“So then,” said Sancho, munching hard all the time, “your worship does

[111-030]not agree with the proverb that says, ‘Let Martha die, but let her die

[111-031]with a full belly.’ I, at any rate, have no mind to kill myself; so far

[111-032]from that, I mean to do as the cobbler does, who stretches the leather

[111-033]with his teeth until he makes it reach as far as he wants. I’ll stretch

[111-034]out my life by eating until it reaches the end heaven has fixed for it;

[111-035]and let me tell you, señor, there’s no greater folly than to think of

[111-036]dying of despair as your worship does; take my advice, and after eating

[111-037]lie down and sleep a bit on this green grass-mattress, and you will see

[111-038]that when you awake you’ll feel something better.”

[111-039]Don Quixote did as he recommended, for it struck him that Sancho’s

[111-040]reasoning was more like a philosopher’s than a blockhead’s, and said

[111-041]he, “Sancho, if thou wilt do for me what I am going to tell thee my

[111-042]ease of mind would be more assured and my heaviness of heart not so

[111-043]great; and it is this; to go aside a little while I am sleeping in

[111-044]accordance with thy advice, and, making bare thy carcase to the air, to

[111-045]give thyself three or four hundred lashes with Rocinante’s reins, on

[111-046]account of the three thousand and odd thou art to give thyself for the

[111-047]disenchantment of Dulcinea; for it is a great pity that the poor lady

[111-048]should be left enchanted through thy carelessness and negligence.”

[111-049]“There is a good deal to be said on that point,” said Sancho; “let us

[111-050]both go to sleep now, and after that, God has decreed what will happen.

[111-051]Let me tell your worship that for a man to whip himself in cold blood

[111-052]is a hard thing, especially if the stripes fall upon an ill-nourished

[111-053]and worse-fed body. Let my lady Dulcinea have patience, and when she is

[111-054]least expecting it, she will see me made a riddle of with whipping, and

[111-055]‘until death it’s all life;’ I mean that I have still life in me, and

[111-056]the desire to make good what I have promised.”

[111-057]Don Quixote thanked him, and ate a little, and Sancho a good deal, and

[111-058]then they both lay down to sleep, leaving those two inseparable friends

[111-059]and comrades, Rocinante and Dapple, to their own devices and to feed

[111-060]unrestrained upon the abundant grass with which the meadow was

[111-061]furnished. They woke up rather late, mounted once more and resumed

[111-062]their journey, pushing on to reach an inn which was in sight,

[111-063]apparently a league off. I say an inn, because Don Quixote called it

[111-064]so, contrary to his usual practice of calling all inns castles. They

[111-065]reached it, and asked the landlord if they could put up there. He said

[111-066]yes, with as much comfort and as good fare as they could find in

[111-067]Saragossa. They dismounted, and Sancho stowed away his larder in a room

[111-068]of which the landlord gave him the key. He took the beasts to the

[111-069]stable, fed them, and came back to see what orders Don Quixote, who was

[111-070]seated on a bench at the door, had for him, giving special thanks to

[111-071]heaven that this inn had not been taken for a castle by his master.

[111-072]Supper-time came, and they repaired to their room, and Sancho asked the

[111-073]landlord what he had to give them for supper. To this the landlord

[111-074]replied that his mouth should be the measure; he had only to ask what

[111-075]he would; for that inn was provided with the birds of the air and the

[111-076]fowls of the earth and the fish of the sea.

[111-077]“There’s no need of all that,” said Sancho; “if they’ll roast us a

[111-078]couple of chickens we’ll be satisfied, for my master is delicate and

[111-079]eats little, and I’m not over and above gluttonous.”

[111-080]The landlord replied he had no chickens, for the kites had stolen them.

[111-081]“Well then,” said Sancho, “let señor landlord tell them to roast a

[111-082]pullet, so that it is a tender one.”

[111-083]“Pullet! My father!” said the landlord; “indeed and in truth it’s only

[111-084]yesterday I sent over fifty to the city to sell; but saving pullets ask

[111-085]what you will.”

[111-086]“In that case,” said Sancho, “you will not be without veal or kid.”

[111-087]“Just now,” said the landlord, “there’s none in the house, for it’s all

[111-088]finished; but next week there will be enough and to spare.”

[111-089]“Much good that does us,” said Sancho; “I’ll lay a bet that all these

[111-090]short-comings are going to wind up in plenty of bacon and eggs.”

[111-091]“By God,” said the landlord, “my guest’s wits must be precious dull; I

[111-092]tell him I have neither pullets nor hens, and he wants me to have eggs!

[111-093]Talk of other dainties, if you please, and don’t ask for hens again.”

[111-094]“Body o’ me!” said Sancho, “let’s settle the matter; say at once what

[111-095]you have got, and let us have no more words about it.”

[111-096]“In truth and earnest, señor guest,” said the landlord, “all I have is

[111-097]a couple of cow-heels like calves’ feet, or a couple of calves’ feet

[111-098]like cowheels; they are boiled with chick-peas, onions, and bacon, and

[111-099]at this moment they are crying ‘Come eat me, come eat me.”

[111-100]“I mark them for mine on the spot,” said Sancho; “let nobody touch

[111-101]them; I’ll pay better for them than anyone else, for I could not wish

[111-102]for anything more to my taste; and I don’t care a pin whether they are

[111-103]feet or heels.”

[111-104]“Nobody shall touch them,” said the landlord; “for the other guests I

[111-105]have, being persons of high quality, bring their own cook and caterer

[111-106]and larder with them.”

[111-107]“If you come to people of quality,” said Sancho, “there’s nobody more

[111-108]so than my master; but the calling he follows does not allow of larders

[111-109]or store-rooms; we lay ourselves down in the middle of a meadow, and

[111-110]fill ourselves with acorns or medlars.”

[111-111]Here ended Sancho’s conversation with the landlord, Sancho not caring

[111-112]to carry it any farther by answering him; for he had already asked him

[111-113]what calling or what profession it was his master was of.

[111-114]Supper-time having come, then, Don Quixote betook himself to his room,

[111-115]the landlord brought in the stew-pan just as it was, and he sat himself

[111-116]down to sup very resolutely. It seems that in another room, which was

[111-117]next to Don Quixote’s, with nothing but a thin partition to separate

[111-118]it, he overheard these words, “As you live, Señor Don Jeronimo, while

[111-119]they are bringing supper, let us read another chapter of the Second

[111-120]Part of ‘Don Quixote of La Mancha.’”

[111-121]The instant Don Quixote heard his own name he started to his feet and

[111-122]listened with open ears to catch what they said about him, and heard

[111-123]the Don Jeronimo who had been addressed say in reply, “Why would you

[111-124]have us read that absurd stuff, Don Juan, when it is impossible for

[111-125]anyone who has read the First Part of the history of ‘Don Quixote of La

[111-126]Mancha’ to take any pleasure in reading this Second Part?”

[111-127]“For all that,” said he who was addressed as Don Juan, “we shall do

[111-128]well to read it, for there is no book so bad but it has something good

[111-129]in it. What displeases me most in it is that it represents Don Quixote

[111-130]as now cured of his love for Dulcinea del Toboso.”

[111-131]On hearing this Don Quixote, full of wrath and indignation, lifted up

[111-132]his voice and said, “Whoever he may be who says that Don Quixote of La

[111-133]Mancha has forgotten or can forget Dulcinea del Toboso, I will teach

[111-134]him with equal arms that what he says is very far from the truth; for

[111-135]neither can the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso be forgotten, nor can

[111-136]forgetfulness have a place in Don Quixote; his motto is constancy, and

[111-137]his profession to maintain the same with his life and never wrong it.”

[111-138]“Who is this that answers us?” said they in the next room.

[111-139]“Who should it be,” said Sancho, “but Don Quixote of La Mancha himself,

[111-140]who will make good all he has said and all he will say; for pledges

[111-141]don’t trouble a good payer.”

[111-142]Sancho had hardly uttered these words when two gentlemen, for such they

[111-143]seemed to be, entered the room, and one of them, throwing his arms

[111-144]round Don Quixote’s neck, said to him, “Your appearance cannot leave

[111-145]any question as to your name, nor can your name fail to identify your

[111-146]appearance; unquestionably, señor, you are the real Don Quixote of La

[111-147]Mancha, cynosure and morning star of knight-errantry, despite and in

[111-148]defiance of him who has sought to usurp your name and bring to naught

[111-149]your achievements, as the author of this book which I here present to

[111-150]you has done;” and with this he put a book which his companion carried

[111-151]into the hands of Don Quixote, who took it, and without replying began

[111-152]to run his eye over it; but he presently returned it saying, “In the

[111-153]little I have seen I have discovered three things in this author that

[111-154]deserve to be censured. The first is some words that I have read in the

[111-155]preface; the next that the language is Aragonese, for sometimes he

[111-156]writes without articles; and the third, which above all stamps him as

[111-157]ignorant, is that he goes wrong and departs from the truth in the most

[111-158]important part of the history, for here he says that my squire Sancho

[111-159]Panza’s wife is called Mari Gutierrez, when she is called nothing of

[111-160]the sort, but Teresa Panza; and when a man errs on such an important

[111-161]point as this there is good reason to fear that he is in error on every

[111-162]other point in the history.”

[111-163]“A nice sort of historian, indeed!” exclaimed Sancho at this; “he must

[111-164]know a deal about our affairs when he calls my wife Teresa Panza, Mari

[111-165]Gutierrez; take the book again, señor, and see if I am in it and if he

[111-166]has changed my name.”

[111-167]“From your talk, friend,” said Don Jeronimo, “no doubt you are Sancho

[111-168]Panza, Señor Don Quixote’s squire.”

[111-169]“Yes, I am,” said Sancho; “and I’m proud of it.”

[111-170]“Faith, then,” said the gentleman, “this new author does not handle you

[111-171]with the decency that displays itself in your person; he makes you out

[111-172]a heavy feeder and a fool, and not in the least droll, and a very

[111-173]different being from the Sancho described in the First Part of your

[111-174]master’s history.”

[111-175]“God forgive him,” said Sancho; “he might have left me in my corner

[111-176]without troubling his head about me; ‘let him who knows how ring the

[111-177]bells; ‘Saint Peter is very well in Rome.’”

[111-178]The two gentlemen pressed Don Quixote to come into their room and have

[111-179]supper with them, as they knew very well there was nothing in that inn

[111-180]fit for one of his sort. Don Quixote, who was always polite, yielded to

[111-181]their request and supped with them. Sancho stayed behind with the stew.

[111-182]and invested with plenary delegated authority seated himself at the

[111-183]head of the table, and the landlord sat down with him, for he was no

[111-184]less fond of cow-heel and calves’ feet than Sancho was.

[111-185]While at supper Don Juan asked Don Quixote what news he had of the lady

[111-186]Dulcinea del Toboso, was she married, had she been brought to bed, or

[111-187]was she with child, or did she in maidenhood, still preserving her

[111-188]modesty and delicacy, cherish the remembrance of the tender passion of

[111-189]Señor Don Quixote?

[111-190]To this he replied, “Dulcinea is a maiden still, and my passion more

[111-191]firmly rooted than ever, our intercourse unsatisfactory as before, and

[111-192]her beauty transformed into that of a foul country wench;” and then he

[111-193]proceeded to give them a full and particular account of the enchantment

[111-194]of Dulcinea, and of what had happened him in the cave of Montesinos,

[111-195]together with what the sage Merlin had prescribed for her

[111-196]disenchantment, namely the scourging of Sancho.

[111-197]Exceedingly great was the amusement the two gentlemen derived from

[111-198]hearing Don Quixote recount the strange incidents of his history; and

[111-199]if they were amazed by his absurdities they were equally amazed by the

[111-200]elegant style in which he delivered them. On the one hand they regarded

[111-201]him as a man of wit and sense, and on the other he seemed to them a

[111-202]maundering blockhead, and they could not make up their minds

[111-203]whereabouts between wisdom and folly they ought to place him.

[111-204]Sancho having finished his supper, and left the landlord in the X

[111-205]condition, repaired to the room where his master was, and as he came in

[111-206]said, “May I die, sirs, if the author of this book your worships have

[111-207]got has any mind that we should agree; as he calls me glutton

[111-208](according to what your worships say) I wish he may not call me

[111-209]drunkard too.”

[111-210]“But he does,” said Don Jeronimo; “I cannot remember, however, in what

[111-211]way, though I know his words are offensive, and what is more, lying, as

[111-212]I can see plainly by the physiognomy of the worthy Sancho before me.”

[111-213]“Believe me,” said Sancho, “the Sancho and the Don Quixote of this

[111-214]history must be different persons from those that appear in the one

[111-215]Cide Hamete Benengeli wrote, who are ourselves; my master valiant,

[111-216]wise, and true in love, and I simple, droll, and neither glutton nor

[111-217]drunkard.”

[111-218]“I believe it,” said Don Juan; “and were it possible, an order should

[111-219]be issued that no one should have the presumption to deal with anything

[111-220]relating to Don Quixote, save his original author Cide Hamete; just as

[111-221]Alexander commanded that no one should presume to paint his portrait

[111-222]save Apelles.”

[111-223]“Let him who will paint me,” said Don Quixote; “but let him not abuse

[111-224]me; for patience will often break down when they heap insults upon it.”

[111-225]“None can be offered to Señor Don Quixote,” said Don Juan, “that he

[111-226]himself will not be able to avenge, if he does not ward it off with the

[111-227]shield of his patience, which, I take it, is great and strong.”

[111-228]A considerable portion of the night passed in conversation of this

[111-229]sort, and though Don Juan wished Don Quixote to read more of the book

[111-230]to see what it was all about, he was not to be prevailed upon, saying

[111-231]that he treated it as read and pronounced it utterly silly; and, if by

[111-232]any chance it should come to its author’s ears that he had it in his

[111-233]hand, he did not want him to flatter himself with the idea that he had

[111-234]read it; for our thoughts, and still more our eyes, should keep

[111-235]themselves aloof from what is obscene and filthy.

[111-236]They asked him whither he meant to direct his steps. He replied, to

[111-237]Saragossa, to take part in the harness jousts which were held in that

[111-238]city every year. Don Juan told him that the new history described how

[111-239]Don Quixote, let him be who he might, took part there in a tilting at

[111-240]the ring, utterly devoid of invention, poor in mottoes, very poor in

[111-241]costume, though rich in sillinesses.

[111-242]“For that very reason,” said Don Quixote, “I will not set foot in

[111-243]Saragossa; and by that means I shall expose to the world the lie of

[111-244]this new history writer, and people will see that I am not the Don

[111-245]Quixote he speaks of.”

[111-246]“You will do quite right,” said Don Jeronimo; “and there are other

[111-247]jousts at Barcelona in which Señor Don Quixote may display his

[111-248]prowess.”

[111-249]“That is what I mean to do,” said Don Quixote; “and as it is now time,

[111-250]I pray your worships to give me leave to retire to bed, and to place

[111-251]and retain me among the number of your greatest friends and servants.”

[111-252]“And me too,” said Sancho; “maybe I’ll be good for something.”

[111-253]With this they exchanged farewells, and Don Quixote and Sancho retired

[111-254]to their room, leaving Don Juan and Don Jeronimo amazed to see the

[111-255]medley he made of his good sense and his craziness; and they felt

[111-256]thoroughly convinced that these, and not those their Aragonese author

[111-257]described, were the genuine Don Quixote and Sancho. Don Quixote rose

[111-258]betimes, and bade adieu to his hosts by knocking at the partition of

[111-259]the other room. Sancho paid the landlord magnificently, and recommended

[111-260]him either to say less about the providing of his inn or to keep it

[111-261]better provided.
