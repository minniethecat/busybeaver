OF WHAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE WITH A DISCREET GENTLEMAN OF LA MANCHA

p16a.jpg (85K)


[068-001]Don Quixote pursued his journey in the high spirits, satisfaction, and

[068-002]self-complacency already described, fancying himself the most valorous

[068-003]knight-errant of the age in the world because of his late victory. All

[068-004]the adventures that could befall him from that time forth he regarded

[068-005]as already done and brought to a happy issue; he made light of

[068-006]enchantments and enchanters; he thought no more of the countless

[068-007]drubbings that had been administered to him in the course of his

[068-008]knight-errantry, nor of the volley of stones that had levelled half his

[068-009]teeth, nor of the ingratitude of the galley slaves, nor of the audacity

[068-010]of the Yanguesans and the shower of stakes that fell upon him; in

[068-011]short, he said to himself that could he discover any means, mode, or

[068-012]way of disenchanting his lady Dulcinea, he would not envy the highest

[068-013]fortune that the most fortunate knight-errant of yore ever reached or

[068-014]could reach.

[068-015]He was going along entirely absorbed in these fancies, when Sancho said

[068-016]to him, “Isn’t it odd, señor, that I have still before my eyes that

[068-017]monstrous enormous nose of my gossip, Tom Cecial?”

[068-018]“And dost thou, then, believe, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that the

[068-019]Knight of the Mirrors was the bachelor Carrasco, and his squire Tom

[068-020]Cecial thy gossip?”

[068-021]“I don’t know what to say to that,” replied Sancho; “all I know is that

[068-022]the tokens he gave me about my own house, wife and children, nobody

[068-023]else but himself could have given me; and the face, once the nose was

[068-024]off, was the very face of Tom Cecial, as I have seen it many a time in

[068-025]my town and next door to my own house; and the sound of the voice was

[068-026]just the same.”

[068-027]“Let us reason the matter, Sancho,” said Don Quixote. “Come now, by

[068-028]what process of thinking can it be supposed that the bachelor Samson

[068-029]Carrasco would come as a knight-errant, in arms offensive and

[068-030]defensive, to fight with me? Have I ever been by any chance his enemy?

[068-031]Have I ever given him any occasion to owe me a grudge? Am I his rival,

[068-032]or does he profess arms, that he should envy the fame I have acquired

[068-033]in them?”

[068-034]“Well, but what are we to say, señor,” returned Sancho, “about that

[068-035]knight, whoever he is, being so like the bachelor Carrasco, and his

[068-036]squire so like my gossip, Tom Cecial? And if that be enchantment, as

[068-037]your worship says, was there no other pair in the world for them to

[068-038]take the likeness of?”

[068-039]“It is all,” said Don Quixote, “a scheme and plot of the malignant

[068-040]magicians that persecute me, who, foreseeing that I was to be

[068-041]victorious in the conflict, arranged that the vanquished knight should

[068-042]display the countenance of my friend the bachelor, in order that the

[068-043]friendship I bear him should interpose to stay the edge of my sword and

[068-044]might of my arm, and temper the just wrath of my heart; so that he who

[068-045]sought to take my life by fraud and falsehood should save his own. And

[068-046]to prove it, thou knowest already, Sancho, by experience which cannot

[068-047]lie or deceive, how easy it is for enchanters to change one countenance

[068-048]into another, turning fair into foul, and foul into fair; for it is not

[068-049]two days since thou sawest with thine own eyes the beauty and elegance

[068-050]of the peerless Dulcinea in all its perfection and natural harmony,

[068-051]while I saw her in the repulsive and mean form of a coarse country

[068-052]wench, with cataracts in her eyes and a foul smell in her mouth; and

[068-053]when the perverse enchanter ventured to effect so wicked a

[068-054]transformation, it is no wonder if he effected that of Samson Carrasco

[068-055]and thy gossip in order to snatch the glory of victory out of my grasp.

[068-056]For all that, however, I console myself, because, after all, in

[068-057]whatever shape he may have been, I have been victorious over my enemy.”

[068-058]“God knows what’s the truth of it all,” said Sancho; and knowing as he

[068-059]did that the transformation of Dulcinea had been a device and

[068-060]imposition of his own, his master’s illusions were not satisfactory to

[068-061]him; but he did not like to reply lest he should say something that

[068-062]might disclose his trickery.

[068-063]As they were engaged in this conversation they were overtaken by a man

[068-064]who was following the same road behind them, mounted on a very handsome

[068-065]flea-bitten mare, and dressed in a gaban of fine green cloth, with

[068-066]tawny velvet facings, and a montera of the same velvet. The trappings

[068-067]of the mare were of the field and jineta fashion, and of mulberry

[068-068]colour and green. He carried a Moorish cutlass hanging from a broad

[068-069]green and gold baldric; the buskins were of the same make as the

[068-070]baldric; the spurs were not gilt, but lacquered green, and so brightly

[068-071]polished that, matching as they did the rest of his apparel, they

[068-072]looked better than if they had been of pure gold.

[068-073]When the traveller came up with them he saluted them courteously, and

[068-074]spurring his mare was passing them without stopping, but Don Quixote

[068-075]called out to him, “Gallant sir, if so be your worship is going our

[068-076]road, and has no occasion for speed, it would be a pleasure to me if we

[068-077]were to join company.”

[068-078]“In truth,” replied he on the mare, “I would not pass you so hastily

[068-079]but for fear that horse might turn restive in the company of my mare.”

[068-080]“You may safely hold in your mare, señor,” said Sancho in reply to

[068-081]this, “for our horse is the most virtuous and well-behaved horse in the

[068-082]world; he never does anything wrong on such occasions, and the only

[068-083]time he misbehaved, my master and I suffered for it sevenfold; I say

[068-084]again your worship may pull up if you like; for if she was offered to

[068-085]him between two plates the horse would not hanker after her.”

[068-086]The traveller drew rein, amazed at the trim and features of Don

[068-087]Quixote, who rode without his helmet, which Sancho carried like a

[068-088]valise in front of Dapple’s pack-saddle; and if the man in green

[068-089]examined Don Quixote closely, still more closely did Don Quixote

[068-090]examine the man in green, who struck him as being a man of

[068-091]intelligence. In appearance he was about fifty years of age, with but

[068-092]few grey hairs, an aquiline cast of features, and an expression between

[068-093]grave and gay; and his dress and accoutrements showed him to be a man

[068-094]of good condition. What he in green thought of Don Quixote of La Mancha

[068-095]was that a man of that sort and shape he had never yet seen; he

[068-096]marvelled at the length of his hair, his lofty stature, the lankness

[068-097]and sallowness of his countenance, his armour, his bearing and his

[068-098]gravity—a figure and picture such as had not been seen in those regions

[068-099]for many a long day.

[068-100]Don Quixote saw very plainly the attention with which the traveller was

[068-101]regarding him, and read his curiosity in his astonishment; and

[068-102]courteous as he was and ready to please everybody, before the other

[068-103]could ask him any question he anticipated him by saying, “The

[068-104]appearance I present to your worship being so strange and so out of the

[068-105]common, I should not be surprised if it filled you with wonder; but you

[068-106]will cease to wonder when I tell you, as I do, that I am one of those

[068-107]knights who, as people say, go seeking adventures. I have left my home,

[068-108]I have mortgaged my estate, I have given up my comforts, and committed

[068-109]myself to the arms of Fortune, to bear me whithersoever she may please.

[068-110]My desire was to bring to life again knight-errantry, now dead, and for

[068-111]some time past, stumbling here, falling there, now coming down

[068-112]headlong, now raising myself up again, I have carried out a great

[068-113]portion of my design, succouring widows, protecting maidens, and giving

[068-114]aid to wives, orphans, and minors, the proper and natural duty of

[068-115]knights-errant; and, therefore, because of my many valiant and

[068-116]Christian achievements, I have been already found worthy to make my way

[068-117]in print to well-nigh all, or most, of the nations of the earth. Thirty

[068-118]thousand volumes of my history have been printed, and it is on the

[068-119]high-road to be printed thirty thousand thousands of times, if heaven

[068-120]does not put a stop to it. In short, to sum up all in a few words, or

[068-121]in a single one, I may tell you I am Don Quixote of La Mancha,

[068-122]otherwise called ‘The Knight of the Rueful Countenance;’ for though

[068-123]self-praise is degrading, I must perforce sound my own sometimes, that

[068-124]is to say, when there is no one at hand to do it for me. So that,

[068-125]gentle sir, neither this horse, nor this lance, nor this shield, nor

[068-126]this squire, nor all these arms put together, nor the sallowness of my

[068-127]countenance, nor my gaunt leanness, will henceforth astonish you, now

[068-128]that you know who I am and what profession I follow.”

[068-129]With these words Don Quixote held his peace, and, from the time he took

[068-130]to answer, the man in green seemed to be at a loss for a reply; after a

[068-131]long pause, however, he said to him, “You were right when you saw

[068-132]curiosity in my amazement, sir knight; but you have not succeeded in

[068-133]removing the astonishment I feel at seeing you; for although you say,

[068-134]señor, that knowing who you are ought to remove it, it has not done so;

[068-135]on the contrary, now that I know, I am left more amazed and astonished

[068-136]than before. What! is it possible that there are knights-errant in the

[068-137]world in these days, and histories of real chivalry printed? I cannot

[068-138]realise the fact that there can be anyone on earth now-a-days who aids

[068-139]widows, or protects maidens, or defends wives, or succours orphans; nor

[068-140]should I believe it had I not seen it in your worship with my own eyes.

[068-141]Blessed be heaven! for by means of this history of your noble and

[068-142]genuine chivalrous deeds, which you say has been printed, the countless

[068-143]stories of fictitious knights-errant with which the world is filled, so

[068-144]much to the injury of morality and the prejudice and discredit of good

[068-145]histories, will have been driven into oblivion.”

[068-146]“There is a good deal to be said on that point,” said Don Quixote, “as

[068-147]to whether the histories of the knights-errant are fiction or not.”

[068-148]“Why, is there anyone who doubts that those histories are false?” said

[068-149]the man in green.

[068-150]“I doubt it,” said Don Quixote, “but never mind that just now; if our

[068-151]journey lasts long enough, I trust in God I shall show your worship

[068-152]that you do wrong in going with the stream of those who regard it as a

[068-153]matter of certainty that they are not true.”

[068-154]From this last observation of Don Quixote’s, the traveller began to

[068-155]have a suspicion that he was some crazy being, and was waiting for him

[068-156]to confirm it by something further; but before they could turn to any

[068-157]new subject Don Quixote begged him to tell him who he was, since he

[068-158]himself had rendered account of his station and life. To this, he in

[068-159]the green gaban replied “I, Sir Knight of the Rueful Countenance, am a

[068-160]gentleman by birth, native of the village where, please God, we are

[068-161]going to dine to-day; I am more than fairly well off, and my name is

[068-162]Don Diego de Miranda. I pass my life with my wife, children, and

[068-163]friends; my pursuits are hunting and fishing, but I keep neither hawks

[068-164]nor greyhounds, nothing but a tame partridge or a bold ferret or two; I

[068-165]have six dozen or so of books, some in our mother tongue, some Latin,

[068-166]some of them history, others devotional; those of chivalry have not as

[068-167]yet crossed the threshold of my door; I am more given to turning over

[068-168]the profane than the devotional, so long as they are books of honest

[068-169]entertainment that charm by their style and attract and interest by the

[068-170]invention they display, though of these there are very few in Spain.

[068-171]Sometimes I dine with my neighbours and friends, and often invite them;

[068-172]my entertainments are neat and well served without stint of anything. I

[068-173]have no taste for tattle, nor do I allow tattling in my presence; I pry

[068-174]not into my neighbours’ lives, nor have I lynx-eyes for what others do.

[068-175]I hear mass every day; I share my substance with the poor, making no

[068-176]display of good works, lest I let hypocrisy and vainglory, those

[068-177]enemies that subtly take possession of the most watchful heart, find an

[068-178]entrance into mine. I strive to make peace between those whom I know to

[068-179]be at variance; I am the devoted servant of Our Lady, and my trust is

[068-180]ever in the infinite mercy of God our Lord.”

[068-181]Sancho listened with the greatest attention to the account of the

[068-182]gentleman’s life and occupation; and thinking it a good and a holy

[068-183]life, and that he who led it ought to work miracles, he threw himself

[068-184]off Dapple, and running in haste seized his right stirrup and kissed

[068-185]his foot again and again with a devout heart and almost with tears.

[068-186]Seeing this the gentleman asked him, “What are you about, brother? What

[068-187]are these kisses for?”

[068-188]“Let me kiss,” said Sancho, “for I think your worship is the first

[068-189]saint in the saddle I ever saw all the days of my life.”

[068-190]“I am no saint,” replied the gentleman, “but a great sinner; but you

[068-191]are, brother, for you must be a good fellow, as your simplicity shows.”

[068-192]Sancho went back and regained his pack-saddle, having extracted a laugh

[068-193]from his master’s profound melancholy, and excited fresh amazement in

[068-194]Don Diego. Don Quixote then asked him how many children he had, and

[068-195]observed that one of the things wherein the ancient philosophers, who

[068-196]were without the true knowledge of God, placed the _summum bonum_ was

[068-197]in the gifts of nature, in those of fortune, in having many friends,

[068-198]and many and good children.

[068-199]“I, Señor Don Quixote,” answered the gentleman, “have one son, without

[068-200]whom, perhaps, I should count myself happier than I am, not because he

[068-201]is a bad son, but because he is not so good as I could wish. He is

[068-202]eighteen years of age; he has been for six at Salamanca studying Latin

[068-203]and Greek, and when I wished him to turn to the study of other sciences

[068-204]I found him so wrapped up in that of poetry (if that can be called a

[068-205]science) that there is no getting him to take kindly to the law, which

[068-206]I wished him to study, or to theology, the queen of them all. I would

[068-207]like him to be an honour to his family, as we live in days when our

[068-208]kings liberally reward learning that is virtuous and worthy; for

[068-209]learning without virtue is a pearl on a dunghill. He spends the whole

[068-210]day in settling whether Homer expressed himself correctly or not in

[068-211]such and such a line of the Iliad, whether Martial was indecent or not

[068-212]in such and such an epigram, whether such and such lines of Virgil are

[068-213]to be understood in this way or in that; in short, all his talk is of

[068-214]the works of these poets, and those of Horace, Perseus, Juvenal, and

[068-215]Tibullus; for of the moderns in our own language he makes no great

[068-216]account; but with all his seeming indifference to Spanish poetry, just

[068-217]now his thoughts are absorbed in making a gloss on four lines that have

[068-218]been sent him from Salamanca, which I suspect are for some poetical

[068-219]tournament.”

[068-220]To all this Don Quixote said in reply, “Children, señor, are portions

[068-221]of their parents’ bowels, and therefore, be they good or bad, are to be

[068-222]loved as we love the souls that give us life; it is for the parents to

[068-223]guide them from infancy in the ways of virtue, propriety, and worthy

[068-224]Christian conduct, so that when grown up they may be the staff of their

[068-225]parents’ old age, and the glory of their posterity; and to force them

[068-226]to study this or that science I do not think wise, though it may be no

[068-227]harm to persuade them; and when there is no need to study for the sake

[068-228]of _pane lucrando_, and it is the student’s good fortune that heaven

[068-229]has given him parents who provide him with it, it would be my advice to

[068-230]them to let him pursue whatever science they may see him most inclined

[068-231]to; and though that of poetry is less useful than pleasurable, it is

[068-232]not one of those that bring discredit upon the possessor. Poetry,

[068-233]gentle sir, is, as I take it, like a tender young maiden of supreme

[068-234]beauty, to array, bedeck, and adorn whom is the task of several other

[068-235]maidens, who are all the rest of the sciences; and she must avail

[068-236]herself of the help of all, and all derive their lustre from her. But

[068-237]this maiden will not bear to be handled, nor dragged through the

[068-238]streets, nor exposed either at the corners of the market-places, or in

[068-239]the closets of palaces. She is the product of an Alchemy of such virtue

[068-240]that he who is able to practise it, will turn her into pure gold of

[068-241]inestimable worth. He that possesses her must keep her within bounds,

[068-242]not permitting her to break out in ribald satires or soulless sonnets.

[068-243]She must on no account be offered for sale, unless, indeed, it be in

[068-244]heroic poems, moving tragedies, or sprightly and ingenious comedies.

[068-245]She must not be touched by the buffoons, nor by the ignorant vulgar,

[068-246]incapable of comprehending or appreciating her hidden treasures. And do

[068-247]not suppose, señor, that I apply the term vulgar here merely to

[068-248]plebeians and the lower orders; for everyone who is ignorant, be he

[068-249]lord or prince, may and should be included among the vulgar. He, then,

[068-250]who shall embrace and cultivate poetry under the conditions I have

[068-251]named, shall become famous, and his name honoured throughout all the

[068-252]civilised nations of the earth. And with regard to what you say, señor,

[068-253]of your son having no great opinion of Spanish poetry, I am inclined to

[068-254]think that he is not quite right there, and for this reason: the great

[068-255]poet Homer did not write in Latin, because he was a Greek, nor did

[068-256]Virgil write in Greek, because he was a Latin; in short, all the

[068-257]ancient poets wrote in the language they imbibed with their mother’s

[068-258]milk, and never went in quest of foreign ones to express their sublime

[068-259]conceptions; and that being so, the usage should in justice extend to

[068-260]all nations, and the German poet should not be undervalued because he

[068-261]writes in his own language, nor the Castilian, nor even the Biscayan,

[068-262]for writing in his. But your son, señor, I suspect, is not prejudiced

[068-263]against Spanish poetry, but against those poets who are mere Spanish

[068-264]verse writers, without any knowledge of other languages or sciences to

[068-265]adorn and give life and vigour to their natural inspiration; and yet

[068-266]even in this he may be wrong; for, according to a true belief, a poet

[068-267]is born one; that is to say, the poet by nature comes forth a poet from

[068-268]his mother’s womb; and following the bent that heaven has bestowed upon

[068-269]him, without the aid of study or art, he produces things that show how

[068-270]truly he spoke who said, ‘_Est Deus in nobis_,’ etc. At the same time,

[068-271]I say that the poet by nature who calls in art to his aid will be a far

[068-272]better poet, and will surpass him who tries to be one relying upon his

[068-273]knowledge of art alone. The reason is, that art does not surpass

[068-274]nature, but only brings it to perfection; and thus, nature combined

[068-275]with art, and art with nature, will produce a perfect poet. To bring my

[068-276]argument to a close, I would say then, gentle sir, let your son go on

[068-277]as his star leads him, for being so studious as he seems to be, and

[068-278]having already successfully surmounted the first step of the sciences,

[068-279]which is that of the languages, with their help he will by his own

[068-280]exertions reach the summit of polite literature, which so well becomes

[068-281]an independent gentleman, and adorns, honours, and distinguishes him,

[068-282]as much as the mitre does the bishop, or the gown the learned

[068-283]counsellor. If your son write satires reflecting on the honour of

[068-284]others, chide and correct him, and tear them up; but if he compose

[068-285]discourses in which he rebukes vice in general, in the style of Horace,

[068-286]and with elegance like his, commend him; for it is legitimate for a

[068-287]poet to write against envy and lash the envious in his verse, and the

[068-288]other vices too, provided he does not single out individuals; there

[068-289]are, however, poets who, for the sake of saying something spiteful,

[068-290]would run the risk of being banished to the coast of Pontus. If the

[068-291]poet be pure in his morals, he will be pure in his verses too; the pen

[068-292]is the tongue of the mind, and as the thought engendered there, so will

[068-293]be the things that it writes down. And when kings and princes observe

[068-294]this marvellous science of poetry in wise, virtuous, and thoughtful

[068-295]subjects, they honour, value, exalt them, and even crown them with the

[068-296]leaves of that tree which the thunderbolt strikes not, as if to show

[068-297]that they whose brows are honoured and adorned with such a crown are

[068-298]not to be assailed by anyone.”

[068-299]He of the green gaban was filled with astonishment at Don Quixote’s

[068-300]argument, so much so that he began to abandon the notion he had taken

[068-301]up about his being crazy. But in the middle of the discourse, it being

[068-302]not very much to his taste, Sancho had turned aside out of the road to

[068-303]beg a little milk from some shepherds, who were milking their ewes hard

[068-304]by; and just as the gentleman, highly pleased, was about to renew the

[068-305]conversation, Don Quixote, raising his head, perceived a cart covered

[068-306]with royal flags coming along the road they were travelling; and

[068-307]persuaded that this must be some new adventure, he called aloud to

[068-308]Sancho to come and bring him his helmet. Sancho, hearing himself

[068-309]called, quitted the shepherds, and, prodding Dapple vigorously, came up

[068-310]to his master, to whom there fell a terrific and desperate adventure.
