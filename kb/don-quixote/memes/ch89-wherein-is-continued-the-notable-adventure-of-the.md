WHEREIN IS CONTINUED THE NOTABLE ADVENTURE OF THE DISTRESSED DUENNA

p37a.jpg (94K)


[089-001]The duke and duchess were extremely glad to see how readily Don Quixote

[089-002]fell in with their scheme; but at this moment Sancho observed, “I hope

[089-003]this señora duenna won’t be putting any difficulties in the way of the

[089-004]promise of my government; for I have heard a Toledo apothecary, who

[089-005]talked like a goldfinch, say that where duennas were mixed up nothing

[089-006]good could happen. God bless me, how he hated them, that same

[089-007]apothecary! And so what I’m thinking is, if all duennas, of whatever

[089-008]sort or condition they may be, are plagues and busybodies, what must

[089-009]they be that are distressed, like this Countess Three-skirts or

[089-010]Three-tails!—for in my country skirts or tails, tails or skirts, it’s

[089-011]all one.”

[089-012]“Hush, friend Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “since this lady duenna comes

[089-013]in quest of me from such a distant land she cannot be one of those the

[089-014]apothecary meant; moreover this is a countess, and when countesses

[089-015]serve as duennas it is in the service of queens and empresses, for in

[089-016]their own houses they are mistresses paramount and have other duennas

[089-017]to wait on them.”

[089-018]To this Doña Rodriguez, who was present, made answer, “My lady the

[089-019]duchess has duennas in her service that might be countesses if it was

[089-020]the will of fortune; ‘but laws go as kings like;’ let nobody speak ill

[089-021]of duennas, above all of ancient maiden ones; for though I am not one

[089-022]myself, I know and am aware of the advantage a maiden duenna has over

[089-023]one that is a widow; but ‘he who clipped us has kept the scissors.’”

[089-024]“For all that,” said Sancho, “there’s so much to be clipped about

[089-025]duennas, so my barber said, that ‘it will be better not to stir the

[089-026]rice even though it sticks.’”

[089-027]“These squires,” returned Doña Rodriguez, “are always our enemies; and

[089-028]as they are the haunting spirits of the antechambers and watch us at

[089-029]every step, whenever they are not saying their prayers (and that’s

[089-030]often enough) they spend their time in tattling about us, digging up

[089-031]our bones and burying our good name. But I can tell these walking

[089-032]blocks that we will live in spite of them, and in great houses too,

[089-033]though we die of hunger and cover our flesh, be it delicate or not,

[089-034]with widow’s weeds, as one covers or hides a dunghill on a procession

[089-035]day. By my faith, if it were permitted me and time allowed, I could

[089-036]prove, not only to those here present, but to all the world, that there

[089-037]is no virtue that is not to be found in a duenna.”

[089-038]“I have no doubt,” said the duchess, “that my good Doña Rodriguez is

[089-039]right, and very much so; but she had better bide her time for fighting

[089-040]her own battle and that of the rest of the duennas, so as to crush the

[089-041]calumny of that vile apothecary, and root out the prejudice in the

[089-042]great Sancho Panza’s mind.”

[089-043]To which Sancho replied, “Ever since I have sniffed the governorship I

[089-044]have got rid of the humours of a squire, and I don’t care a wild fig

[089-045]for all the duennas in the world.”

[089-046]They would have carried on this duenna dispute further had they not

[089-047]heard the notes of the fife and drums once more, from which they

[089-048]concluded that the Distressed Duenna was making her entrance. The

[089-049]duchess asked the duke if it would be proper to go out to receive her,

[089-050]as she was a countess and a person of rank.

[089-051]“In respect of her being a countess,” said Sancho, before the duke

[089-052]could reply, “I am for your highnesses going out to receive her; but in

[089-053]respect of her being a duenna, it is my opinion you should not stir a

[089-054]step.”

[089-055]“Who bade thee meddle in this, Sancho?” said Don Quixote.

[089-056]“Who, señor?” said Sancho; “I meddle for I have a right to meddle, as a

[089-057]squire who has learned the rules of courtesy in the school of your

[089-058]worship, the most courteous and best-bred knight in the whole world of

[089-059]courtliness; and in these things, as I have heard your worship say, as

[089-060]much is lost by a card too many as by a card too few, and to one who

[089-061]has his ears open, few words.”

[089-062]“Sancho is right,” said the duke; “we’ll see what the countess is like,

[089-063]and by that measure the courtesy that is due to her.”

[089-064]And now the drums and fife made their entrance as before; and here the

[089-065]author brought this short chapter to an end and began the next,

[089-066]following up the same adventure, which is one of the most notable in

[089-067]the history.
