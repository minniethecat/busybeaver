[096-001]HOW SANCHO PANZA WAS CONDUCTED TO HIS GOVERNMENT, AND OF THE STRANGE

ADVENTURE THAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE IN THE CASTLE

p44a.jpg (140K)


[096-002]It is stated, they say, in the true original of this history, that when

[096-003]Cide Hamete came to write this chapter, his interpreter did not

[096-004]translate it as he wrote it—that is, as a kind of complaint the Moor

[096-005]made against himself for having taken in hand a story so dry and of so

[096-006]little variety as this of Don Quixote, for he found himself forced to

[096-007]speak perpetually of him and Sancho, without venturing to indulge in

[096-008]digressions and episodes more serious and more interesting. He said,

[096-009]too, that to go on, mind, hand, pen always restricted to writing upon

[096-010]one single subject, and speaking through the mouths of a few

[096-011]characters, was intolerable drudgery, the result of which was never

[096-012]equal to the author’s labour, and that to avoid this he had in the

[096-013]First Part availed himself of the device of novels, like “The

[096-014]Ill-advised Curiosity,” and “The Captive Captain,” which stand, as it

[096-015]were, apart from the story; the others are given there being incidents

[096-016]which occurred to Don Quixote himself and could not be omitted. He also

[096-017]thought, he says, that many, engrossed by the interest attaching to the

[096-018]exploits of Don Quixote, would take none in the novels, and pass them

[096-019]over hastily or impatiently without noticing the elegance and art of

[096-020]their composition, which would be very manifest were they published by

[096-021]themselves and not as mere adjuncts to the crazes of Don Quixote or the

[096-022]simplicities of Sancho. Therefore in this Second Part he thought it

[096-023]best not to insert novels, either separate or interwoven, but only

[096-024]episodes, something like them, arising out of the circumstances the

[096-025]facts present; and even these sparingly, and with no more words than

[096-026]suffice to make them plain; and as he confines and restricts himself to

[096-027]the narrow limits of the narrative, though he has ability; capacity,

[096-028]and brains enough to deal with the whole universe, he requests that his

[096-029]labours may not be despised, and that credit be given him, not alone

[096-030]for what he writes, but for what he has refrained from writing.

[096-031]And so he goes on with his story, saying that the day Don Quixote gave

[096-032]the counsels to Sancho, the same afternoon after dinner he handed them

[096-033]to him in writing so that he might get someone to read them to him.

[096-034]They had scarcely, however, been given to him when he let them drop,

[096-035]and they fell into the hands of the duke, who showed them to the

[096-036]duchess and they were both amazed afresh at the madness and wit of Don

[096-037]Quixote. To carry on the joke, then, the same evening they despatched

[096-038]Sancho with a large following to the village that was to serve him for

[096-039]an island. It happened that the person who had him in charge was a

[096-040]majordomo of the duke’s, a man of great discretion and humour—and there

[096-041]can be no humour without discretion—and the same who played the part of

[096-042]the Countess Trifaldi in the comical way that has been already

[096-043]described; and thus qualified, and instructed by his master and

[096-044]mistress as to how to deal with Sancho, he carried out their scheme

[096-045]admirably. Now it came to pass that as soon as Sancho saw this

[096-046]majordomo he seemed in his features to recognise those of the Trifaldi,

[096-047]and turning to his master, he said to him, “Señor, either the devil

[096-048]will carry me off, here on this spot, righteous and believing, or your

[096-049]worship will own to me that the face of this majordomo of the duke’s

[096-050]here is the very face of the Distressed One.”

[096-051]Don Quixote regarded the majordomo attentively, and having done so,

[096-052]said to Sancho, “There is no reason why the devil should carry thee

[096-053]off, Sancho, either righteous or believing—and what thou meanest by

[096-054]that I know not; the face of the Distressed One is that of the

[096-055]majordomo, but for all that the majordomo is not the Distressed One;

[096-056]for his being so would involve a mighty contradiction; but this is not

[096-057]the time for going into questions of the sort, which would be involving

[096-058]ourselves in an inextricable labyrinth. Believe me, my friend, we must

[096-059]pray earnestly to our Lord that he deliver us both from wicked wizards

[096-060]and enchanters.”

[096-061]“It is no joke, señor,” said Sancho, “for before this I heard him

[096-062]speak, and it seemed exactly as if the voice of the Trifaldi was

[096-063]sounding in my ears. Well, I’ll hold my peace; but I’ll take care to be

[096-064]on the look-out henceforth for any sign that may be seen to confirm or

[096-065]do away with this suspicion.”

[096-066]“Thou wilt do well, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and thou wilt let me

[096-067]know all thou discoverest, and all that befalls thee in thy

[096-068]government.”

[096-069]Sancho at last set out attended by a great number of people. He was

[096-070]dressed in the garb of a lawyer, with a gaban of tawny watered camlet

[096-071]over all and a montera cap of the same material, and mounted a la

[096-072]gineta upon a mule. Behind him, in accordance with the duke’s orders,

[096-073]followed Dapple with brand new ass-trappings and ornaments of silk, and

[096-074]from time to time Sancho turned round to look at his ass, so well

[096-075]pleased to have him with him that he would not have changed places with

[096-076]the emperor of Germany. On taking leave he kissed the hands of the duke

[096-077]and duchess and got his master’s blessing, which Don Quixote gave him

[096-078]with tears, and he received blubbering.

[096-079]Let worthy Sancho go in peace, and good luck to him, Gentle Reader; and

[096-080]look out for two bushels of laughter, which the account of how he

[096-081]behaved himself in office will give thee. In the meantime turn thy

[096-082]attention to what happened his master the same night, and if thou dost

[096-083]not laugh thereat, at any rate thou wilt stretch thy mouth with a grin;

[096-084]for Don Quixote’s adventures must be honoured either with wonder or

[096-085]with laughter.

[096-086]It is recorded, then, that as soon as Sancho had gone, Don Quixote felt

[096-087]his loneliness, and had it been possible for him to revoke the mandate

[096-088]and take away the government from him he would have done so. The

[096-089]duchess observed his dejection and asked him why he was melancholy;

[096-090]because, she said, if it was for the loss of Sancho, there were

[096-091]squires, duennas, and damsels in her house who would wait upon him to

[096-092]his full satisfaction.

[096-093]“The truth is, señora,” replied Don Quixote, “that I do feel the loss

[096-094]of Sancho; but that is not the main cause of my looking sad; and of all

[096-095]the offers your excellence makes me, I accept only the good-will with

[096-096]which they are made, and as to the remainder I entreat of your

[096-097]excellence to permit and allow me alone to wait upon myself in my

[096-098]chamber.”

[096-099]“Indeed, Señor Don Quixote,” said the duchess, “that must not be; four

[096-100]of my damsels, as beautiful as flowers, shall wait upon you.”

[096-101]“To me,” said Don Quixote, “they will not be flowers, but thorns to

[096-102]pierce my heart. They, or anything like them, shall as soon enter my

[096-103]chamber as fly. If your highness wishes to gratify me still further,

[096-104]though I deserve it not, permit me to please myself, and wait upon

[096-105]myself in my own room; for I place a barrier between my inclinations

[096-106]and my virtue, and I do not wish to break this rule through the

[096-107]generosity your highness is disposed to display towards me; and, in

[096-108]short, I will sleep in my clothes, sooner than allow anyone to undress

[096-109]me.”

[096-110]“Say no more, Señor Don Quixote, say no more,” said the duchess; “I

[096-111]assure you I will give orders that not even a fly, not to say a damsel,

[096-112]shall enter your room. I am not the one to undermine the propriety of

[096-113]Señor Don Quixote, for it strikes me that among his many virtues the

[096-114]one that is pre-eminent is that of modesty. Your worship may undress

[096-115]and dress in private and in your own way, as you please and when you

[096-116]please, for there will be no one to hinder you; and in your chamber you

[096-117]will find all the utensils requisite to supply the wants of one who

[096-118]sleeps with his door locked, to the end that no natural needs compel

[096-119]you to open it. May the great Dulcinea del Toboso live a thousand

[096-120]years, and may her fame extend all over the surface of the globe, for

[096-121]she deserves to be loved by a knight so valiant and so virtuous; and

[096-122]may kind heaven infuse zeal into the heart of our governor Sancho Panza

[096-123]to finish off his discipline speedily, so that the world may once more

[096-124]enjoy the beauty of so grand a lady.”

[096-125]To which Don Quixote replied, “Your highness has spoken like what you

[096-126]are; from the mouth of a noble lady nothing bad can come; and Dulcinea

[096-127]will be more fortunate, and better known to the world by the praise of

[096-128]your highness than by all the eulogies the greatest orators on earth

[096-129]could bestow upon her.”

[096-130]“Well, well, Señor Don Quixote,” said the duchess, it is nearly

[096-131]supper-time, and the duke is probably waiting; come let us go to

[096-132]supper, and retire to rest early, for the journey you made yesterday

[096-133]from Kandy was not such a short one but that it must have caused you

[096-134]some fatigue.”

[096-135]“I feel none, señora,” said Don Quixote, “for I would go so far as to

[096-136]swear to your excellence that in all my life I never mounted a quieter

[096-137]beast, or a pleasanter paced one, than Clavileño; and I don’t know what

[096-138]could have induced Malambruno to discard a steed so swift and so

[096-139]gentle, and burn it so recklessly as he did.”

[096-140]“Probably,” said the duchess, “repenting of the evil he had done to the

[096-141]Trifaldi and company, and others, and the crimes he must have committed

[096-142]as a wizard and enchanter, he resolved to make away with all the

[096-143]instruments of his craft; and so burned Clavileño as the chief one, and

[096-144]that which mainly kept him restless, wandering from land to land; and

[096-145]by its ashes and the trophy of the placard the valour of the great Don

[096-146]Quixote of La Mancha is established for ever.”

[096-147]Don Quixote renewed his thanks to the duchess; and having supped,

[096-148]retired to his chamber alone, refusing to allow anyone to enter with

[096-149]him to wait on him, such was his fear of encountering temptations that

[096-150]might lead or drive him to forget his chaste fidelity to his lady

[096-151]Dulcinea; for he had always present to his mind the virtue of Amadis,

[096-152]that flower and mirror of knights-errant. He locked the door behind

[096-153]him, and by the light of two wax candles undressed himself, but as he

[096-154]was taking off his stockings—O disaster unworthy of such a

[096-155]personage!—there came a burst, not of sighs, or anything belying his

[096-156]delicacy or good breeding, but of some two dozen stitches in one of his

[096-157]stockings, that made it look like a window-lattice. The worthy

[096-158]gentleman was beyond measure distressed, and at that moment he would

[096-159]have given an ounce of silver to have had half a drachm of green silk

[096-160]there; I say green silk, because the stockings were green.

[096-161]Here Cide Hamete exclaimed as he was writing, “O poverty, poverty! I

[096-162]know not what could have possessed the great Cordovan poet to call thee

[096-163]‘holy gift ungratefully received.’ Although a Moor, I know well enough

[096-164]from the intercourse I have had with Christians that holiness consists

[096-165]in charity, humility, faith, obedience, and poverty; but for all that,

[096-166]I say he must have a great deal of godliness who can find any

[096-167]satisfaction in being poor; unless, indeed, it be the kind of poverty

[096-168]one of their greatest saints refers to, saying, ‘possess all things as

[096-169]though ye possessed them not;’ which is what they call poverty in

[096-170]spirit. But thou, that other poverty—for it is of thee I am speaking

[096-171]now—why dost thou love to fall out with gentlemen and men of good birth

[096-172]more than with other people? Why dost thou compel them to smear the

[096-173]cracks in their shoes, and to have the buttons of their coats, one

[096-174]silk, another hair, and another glass? Why must their ruffs be always

[096-175]crinkled like endive leaves, and not crimped with a crimping iron?”

[096-176](From this we may perceive the antiquity of starch and crimped ruffs.)

[096-177]Then he goes on: “Poor gentleman of good family! always cockering up

[096-178]his honour, dining miserably and in secret, and making a hypocrite of

[096-179]the toothpick with which he sallies out into the street after eating

[096-180]nothing to oblige him to use it! Poor fellow, I say, with his nervous

[096-181]honour, fancying they perceive a league off the patch on his shoe, the

[096-182]sweat-stains on his hat, the shabbiness of his cloak, and the hunger of

[096-183]his stomach!”

[096-184]All this was brought home to Don Quixote by the bursting of his

[096-185]stitches; however, he comforted himself on perceiving that Sancho had

[096-186]left behind a pair of travelling boots, which he resolved to wear the

[096-187]next day. At last he went to bed, out of spirits and heavy at heart, as

[096-188]much because he missed Sancho as because of the irreparable disaster to

[096-189]his stockings, the stitches of which he would have even taken up with

[096-190]silk of another colour, which is one of the greatest signs of poverty a

[096-191]gentleman can show in the course of his never-failing embarrassments.

[096-192]He put out the candles; but the night was warm and he could not sleep;

[096-193]he rose from his bed and opened slightly a grated window that looked

[096-194]out on a beautiful garden, and as he did so he perceived and heard

[096-195]people walking and talking in the garden. He set himself to listen

[096-196]attentively, and those below raised their voices so that he could hear

[096-197]these words:

[096-198]“Urge me not to sing, Emerencia, for thou knowest that ever since this

[096-199]stranger entered the castle and my eyes beheld him, I cannot sing but

[096-200]only weep; besides my lady is a light rather than a heavy sleeper, and

[096-201]I would not for all the wealth of the world that she found us here; and

[096-202]even if she were asleep and did not waken, my singing would be in vain,

[096-203]if this strange Æneas, who has come into my neighbourhood to flout me,

[096-204]sleeps on and wakens not to hear it.”

[096-205]“Heed not that, dear Altisidora,” replied a voice; “the duchess is no

[096-206]doubt asleep, and everybody in the house save the lord of thy heart and

[096-207]disturber of thy soul; for just now I perceived him open the grated

[096-208]window of his chamber, so he must be awake; sing, my poor sufferer, in

[096-209]a low sweet tone to the accompaniment of thy harp; and even if the

[096-210]duchess hears us we can lay the blame on the heat of the night.”

[096-211]“That is not the point, Emerencia,” replied Altisidora, “it is that I

[096-212]would not that my singing should lay bare my heart, and that I should

[096-213]be thought a light and wanton maiden by those who know not the mighty

[096-214]power of love; but come what may; better a blush on the cheeks than a

[096-215]sore in the heart;” and here a harp softly touched made itself heard.

[096-216]As he listened to all this Don Quixote was in a state of breathless

[096-217]amazement, for immediately the countless adventures like this, with

[096-218]windows, gratings, gardens, serenades, lovemakings, and languishings,

[096-219]that he had read of in his trashy books of chivalry, came to his mind.

[096-220]He at once concluded that some damsel of the duchess’s was in love with

[096-221]him, and that her modesty forced her to keep her passion secret. He

[096-222]trembled lest he should fall, and made an inward resolution not to

[096-223]yield; and commending himself with all his might and soul to his lady

[096-224]Dulcinea he made up his mind to listen to the music; and to let them

[096-225]know he was there he gave a pretended sneeze, at which the damsels were

[096-226]not a little delighted, for all they wanted was that Don Quixote should

[096-227]hear them. So having tuned the harp, Altisidora, running her hand

[096-228]across the strings, began this ballad:

[096-305]Here the lay of the heart-stricken Altisidora came to an end, while the

[096-306]warmly wooed Don Quixote began to feel alarm; and with a deep sigh he

[096-307]said to himself, “O that I should be such an unlucky knight that no

[096-308]damsel can set eyes on me but falls in love with me! O that the

[096-309]peerless Dulcinea should be so unfortunate that they cannot let her

[096-310]enjoy my incomparable constancy in peace! What would ye with her, ye

[096-311]queens? Why do ye persecute her, ye empresses? Why ye pursue her, ye

[096-312]virgins of from fourteen to fifteen? Leave the unhappy being to

[096-313]triumph, rejoice and glory in the lot love has been pleased to bestow

[096-314]upon her in surrendering my heart and yielding up my soul to her. Ye

[096-315]love-smitten host, know that to Dulcinea only I am dough and

[096-316]sugar-paste, flint to all others; for her I am honey, for you aloes.

[096-317]For me Dulcinea alone is beautiful, wise, virtuous, graceful, and

[096-318]high-bred, and all others are ill-favoured, foolish, light, and

[096-319]low-born. Nature sent me into the world to be hers and no other’s;

[096-320]Altisidora may weep or sing, the lady for whose sake they belaboured me

[096-321]in the castle of the enchanted Moor may give way to despair, but I must

[096-322]be Dulcinea’s, boiled or roast, pure, courteous, and chaste, in spite

[096-323]of all the magic-working powers on earth.” And with that he shut the

[096-324]window with a bang, and, as much out of temper and out of sorts as if

[096-325]some great misfortune had befallen him, stretched himself on his bed,

[096-326]where we will leave him for the present, as the great Sancho Panza, who

[096-327]is about to set up his famous government, now demands our attention.
