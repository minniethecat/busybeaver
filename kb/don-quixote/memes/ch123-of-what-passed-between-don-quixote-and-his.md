OF WHAT PASSED BETWEEN DON QUIXOTE AND HIS SQUIRE SANCHO ON THE WAY TO

THEIR VILLAGE

p71a.jpg (82K)


[123-001]The vanquished and afflicted Don Quixote went along very downcast in

[123-002]one respect and very happy in another. His sadness arose from his

[123-003]defeat, and his satisfaction from the thought of the virtue that lay in

[123-004]Sancho, as had been proved by the resurrection of Altisidora; though it

[123-005]was with difficulty he could persuade himself that the love-smitten

[123-006]damsel had been really dead. Sancho went along anything but cheerful,

[123-007]for it grieved him that Altisidora had not kept her promise of giving

[123-008]him the smocks; and turning this over in his mind he said to his

[123-009]master, “Surely, señor, I’m the most unlucky doctor in the world;

[123-010]there’s many a physician that, after killing the sick man he had to

[123-011]cure, requires to be paid for his work, though it is only signing a bit

[123-012]of a list of medicines, that the apothecary and not he makes up, and,

[123-013]there, his labour is over; but with me though to cure somebody else

[123-014]costs me drops of blood, smacks, pinches, pinproddings, and whippings,

[123-015]nobody gives me a farthing. Well, I swear by all that’s good if they

[123-016]put another patient into my hands, they’ll have to grease them for me

[123-017]before I cure him; for, as they say, ‘it’s by his singing the abbot

[123-018]gets his dinner,’ and I’m not going to believe that heaven has bestowed

[123-019]upon me the virtue I have, that I should be dealing it out to others

[123-020]all for nothing.”

[123-021]“Thou art right, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote, “and Altisidora

[123-022]has behaved very badly in not giving thee the smocks she promised; and

[123-023]although that virtue of thine is _gratis data_—as it has cost thee no

[123-024]study whatever, any more than such study as thy personal sufferings may

[123-025]be—I can say for myself that if thou wouldst have payment for the

[123-026]lashes on account of the disenchant of Dulcinea, I would have given it

[123-027]to thee freely ere this. I am not sure, however, whether payment will

[123-028]comport with the cure, and I would not have the reward interfere with

[123-029]the medicine. I think there will be nothing lost by trying it; consider

[123-030]how much thou wouldst have, Sancho, and whip thyself at once, and pay

[123-031]thyself down with thine own hand, as thou hast money of mine.”

[123-032]At this proposal Sancho opened his eyes and his ears a palm’s breadth

[123-033]wide, and in his heart very readily acquiesced in whipping himself, and

[123-034]said he to his master, “Very well then, señor, I’ll hold myself in

[123-035]readiness to gratify your worship’s wishes if I’m to profit by it; for

[123-036]the love of my wife and children forces me to seem grasping. Let your

[123-037]worship say how much you will pay me for each lash I give myself.”

[123-038]“If Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “I were to requite thee as the

[123-039]importance and nature of the cure deserves, the treasures of Venice,

[123-040]the mines of Potosi, would be insufficient to pay thee. See what thou

[123-041]hast of mine, and put a price on each lash.”

[123-042]“Of them,” said Sancho, “there are three thousand three hundred and

[123-043]odd; of these I have given myself five, the rest remain; let the five

[123-044]go for the odd ones, and let us take the three thousand three hundred,

[123-045]which at a quarter real apiece (for I will not take less though the

[123-046]whole world should bid me) make three thousand three hundred quarter

[123-047]reals; the three thousand are one thousand five hundred half reals,

[123-048]which make seven hundred and fifty reals; and the three hundred make a

[123-049]hundred and fifty half reals, which come to seventy-five reals, which

[123-050]added to the seven hundred and fifty make eight hundred and twenty-five

[123-051]reals in all. These I will stop out of what I have belonging to your

[123-052]worship, and I’ll return home rich and content, though well whipped,

[123-053]for ‘there’s no taking trout’—but I say no more.”

[123-054]“O blessed Sancho! O dear Sancho!” said Don Quixote; “how we shall be

[123-055]bound to serve thee, Dulcinea and I, all the days of our lives that

[123-056]heaven may grant us! If she returns to her lost shape (and it cannot be

[123-057]but that she will) her misfortune will have been good fortune, and my

[123-058]defeat a most happy triumph. But look here, Sancho; when wilt thou

[123-059]begin the scourging? For if thou wilt make short work of it, I will

[123-060]give thee a hundred reals over and above.”

[123-061]“When?” said Sancho; “this night without fail. Let your worship order

[123-062]it so that we pass it out of doors and in the open air, and I’ll

[123-063]scarify myself.”

[123-064]Night, longed for by Don Quixote with the greatest anxiety in the

[123-065]world, came at last, though it seemed to him that the wheels of

[123-066]Apollo’s car had broken down, and that the day was drawing itself out

[123-067]longer than usual, just as is the case with lovers, who never make the

[123-068]reckoning of their desires agree with time. They made their way at

[123-069]length in among some pleasant trees that stood a little distance from

[123-070]the road, and there vacating Rocinante’s saddle and Dapple’s

[123-071]pack-saddle, they stretched themselves on the green grass and made

[123-072]their supper off Sancho’s stores, and he making a powerful and flexible

[123-073]whip out of Dapple’s halter and headstall retreated about twenty paces

[123-074]from his master among some beech trees. Don Quixote seeing him march

[123-075]off with such resolution and spirit, said to him, “Take care, my

[123-076]friend, not to cut thyself to pieces; allow the lashes to wait for one

[123-077]another, and do not be in so great a hurry as to run thyself out of

[123-078]breath midway; I mean, do not lay on so strenuously as to make thy life

[123-079]fail thee before thou hast reached the desired number; and that thou

[123-080]mayest not lose by a card too much or too little, I will station myself

[123-081]apart and count on my rosary here the lashes thou givest thyself. May

[123-082]heaven help thee as thy good intention deserves.”

[123-083]“‘Pledges don’t distress a good payer,’” said Sancho; “I mean to lay on

[123-084]in such a way as without killing myself to hurt myself, for in that, no

[123-085]doubt, lies the essence of this miracle.”

[123-086]He then stripped himself from the waist upwards, and snatching up the

[123-087]rope he began to lay on and Don Quixote to count the lashes. He might

[123-088]have given himself six or eight when he began to think the joke no

[123-089]trifle, and its price very low; and holding his hand for a moment, he

[123-090]told his master that he cried off on the score of a blind bargain, for

[123-091]each of those lashes ought to be paid for at the rate of half a real

[123-092]instead of a quarter.

[123-093]“Go on, Sancho my friend, and be not disheartened,” said Don Quixote;

[123-094]“for I double the stakes as to price.”

[123-095]“In that case,” said Sancho, “in God’s hand be it, and let it rain

[123-096]lashes.” But the rogue no longer laid them on his shoulders, but laid

[123-097]on to the trees, with such groans every now and then, that one would

[123-098]have thought at each of them his soul was being plucked up by the

[123-099]roots. Don Quixote, touched to the heart, and fearing he might make an

[123-100]end of himself, and that through Sancho’s imprudence he might miss his

[123-101]own object, said to him, “As thou livest, my friend, let the matter

[123-102]rest where it is, for the remedy seems to me a very rough one, and it

[123-103]will be well to have patience; ‘Zamora was not won in an hour.’ If I

[123-104]have not reckoned wrong thou hast given thyself over a thousand lashes;

[123-105]that is enough for the present; ‘for the ass,’ to put it in homely

[123-106]phrase, ‘bears the load, but not the overload.’”

[123-107]“No, no, señor,” replied Sancho; “it shall never be said of me, ‘The

[123-108]money paid, the arms broken;’ go back a little further, your worship,

[123-109]and let me give myself at any rate a thousand lashes more; for in a

[123-110]couple of bouts like this we shall have finished off the lot, and there

[123-111]will be even cloth to spare.”

[123-112]“As thou art in such a willing mood,” said Don Quixote, “may heaven aid

[123-113]thee; lay on and I’ll retire.”

[123-114]Sancho returned to his task with so much resolution that he soon had

[123-115]the bark stripped off several trees, such was the severity with which

[123-116]he whipped himself; and one time, raising his voice, and giving a beech

[123-117]a tremendous lash, he cried out, “Here dies Samson, and all with him!”

[123-118]At the sound of his piteous cry and of the stroke of the cruel lash,

[123-119]Don Quixote ran to him at once, and seizing the twisted halter that

[123-120]served him for a courbash, said to him, “Heaven forbid, Sancho my

[123-121]friend, that to please me thou shouldst lose thy life, which is needed

[123-122]for the support of thy wife and children; let Dulcinea wait for a

[123-123]better opportunity, and I will content myself with a hope soon to be

[123-124]realised, and have patience until thou hast gained fresh strength so as

[123-125]to finish off this business to the satisfaction of everybody.”

[123-126]“As your worship will have it so, señor,” said Sancho, “so be it; but

[123-127]throw your cloak over my shoulders, for I’m sweating and I don’t want

[123-128]to take cold; it’s a risk that novice disciplinants run.”

[123-129]Don Quixote obeyed, and stripping himself covered Sancho, who slept

[123-130]until the sun woke him; they then resumed their journey, which for the

[123-131]time being they brought to an end at a village that lay three leagues

[123-132]farther on. They dismounted at a hostelry which Don Quixote recognised

[123-133]as such and did not take to be a castle with moat, turrets, portcullis,

[123-134]and drawbridge; for ever since he had been vanquished he talked more

[123-135]rationally about everything, as will be shown presently. They quartered

[123-136]him in a room on the ground floor, where in place of leather hangings

[123-137]there were pieces of painted serge such as they commonly use in

[123-138]villages. On one of them was painted by some very poor hand the Rape of

[123-139]Helen, when the bold guest carried her off from Menelaus, and on the

[123-140]other was the story of Dido and Æneas, she on a high tower, as though

[123-141]she were making signals with a half sheet to her fugitive guest who was

[123-142]out at sea flying in a frigate or brigantine. He noticed in the two

[123-143]stories that Helen did not go very reluctantly, for she was laughing

[123-144]slyly and roguishly; but the fair Dido was shown dropping tears the

[123-145]size of walnuts from her eyes. Don Quixote as he looked at them

[123-146]observed, “Those two ladies were very unfortunate not to have been born

[123-147]in this age, and I unfortunate above all men not to have been born in

[123-148]theirs. Had I fallen in with those gentlemen, Troy would not have been

[123-149]burned or Carthage destroyed, for it would have been only for me to

[123-150]slay Paris, and all these misfortunes would have been avoided.”

[123-151]“I’ll lay a bet,” said Sancho, “that before long there won’t be a

[123-152]tavern, roadside inn, hostelry, or barber’s shop where the story of our

[123-153]doings won’t be painted up; but I’d like it painted by the hand of a

[123-154]better painter than painted these.”

[123-155]“Thou art right, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “for this painter is like

[123-156]Orbaneja, a painter there was at Úbeda, who when they asked him what he

[123-157]was painting, used to say, ‘Whatever it may turn out; and if he chanced

[123-158]to paint a cock he would write under it, ‘This is a cock,’ for fear

[123-159]they might think it was a fox. The painter or writer, for it’s all the

[123-160]same, who published the history of this new Don Quixote that has come

[123-161]out, must have been one of this sort I think, Sancho, for he painted or

[123-162]wrote ‘whatever it might turn out;’ or perhaps he is like a poet called

[123-163]Mauleon that was about the Court some years ago, who used to answer at

[123-164]haphazard whatever he was asked, and on one asking him what _Deum de

[123-165]Deo_ meant, he replied _Dé donde diere_. But, putting this aside, tell

[123-166]me, Sancho, hast thou a mind to have another turn at thyself to-night,

[123-167]and wouldst thou rather have it indoors or in the open air?”

[123-168]“Egad, señor,” said Sancho, “for what I’m going to give myself, it

[123-169]comes all the same to me whether it is in a house or in the fields;

[123-170]still I’d like it to be among trees; for I think they are company for

[123-171]me and help me to bear my pain wonderfully.”

[123-172]“And yet it must not be, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote; “but, to

[123-173]enable thee to recover strength, we must keep it for our own village;

[123-174]for at the latest we shall get there the day after to-morrow.”

[123-175]Sancho said he might do as he pleased; but that for his own part he

[123-176]would like to finish off the business quickly before his blood cooled

[123-177]and while he had an appetite, because “in delay there is apt to be

[123-178]danger” very often, and “praying to God and plying the hammer,” and

[123-179]“one take was better than two I’ll give thee’s,” and “a sparrow in the

[123-180]hand than a vulture on the wing.”

[123-181]“For God’s sake, Sancho, no more proverbs!” exclaimed Don Quixote; “it

[123-182]seems to me thou art becoming _sicut erat_ again; speak in a plain,

[123-183]simple, straight-forward way, as I have often told thee, and thou wilt

[123-184]find the good of it.”

[123-185]“I don’t know what bad luck it is of mine,” said Sancho, “but I can’t

[123-186]utter a word without a proverb that is not as good as an argument to my

[123-187]mind; however, I mean to mend if I can;” and so for the present the

[123-188]conversation ended.
