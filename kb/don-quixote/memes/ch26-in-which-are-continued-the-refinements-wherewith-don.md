IN WHICH ARE CONTINUED THE REFINEMENTS WHEREWITH DON QUIXOTE PLAYED THE

PART OF A LOVER IN THE SIERRA MORENA

c26a.jpg (111K)


[026-001]Returning to the proceedings of him of the Rueful Countenance when he

[026-002]found himself alone, the history says that when Don Quixote had

[026-003]completed the performance of the somersaults or capers, naked from the

[026-004]waist down and clothed from the waist up, and saw that Sancho had gone

[026-005]off without waiting to see any more crazy feats, he climbed up to the

[026-006]top of a high rock, and there set himself to consider what he had

[026-007]several times before considered without ever coming to any conclusion

[026-008]on the point, namely whether it would be better and more to his purpose

[026-009]to imitate the outrageous madness of Roland, or the melancholy madness

[026-010]of Amadis; and communing with himself he said:

[026-011]“What wonder is it if Roland was so good a knight and so valiant as

[026-012]everyone says he was, when, after all, he was enchanted, and nobody

[026-013]could kill him save by thrusting a corking pin into the sole of his

[026-014]foot, and he always wore shoes with seven iron soles? Though cunning

[026-015]devices did not avail him against Bernardo del Carpio, who knew all

[026-016]about them, and strangled him in his arms at Roncesvalles. But putting

[026-017]the question of his valour aside, let us come to his losing his wits,

[026-018]for certain it is that he did lose them in consequence of the proofs he

[026-019]discovered at the fountain, and the intelligence the shepherd gave him

[026-020]of Angelica having slept more than two siestas with Medoro, a little

[026-021]curly-headed Moor, and page to Agramante. If he was persuaded that this

[026-022]was true, and that his lady had wronged him, it is no wonder that he

[026-023]should have gone mad; but I, how am I to imitate him in his madness,

[026-024]unless I can imitate him in the cause of it? For my Dulcinea, I will

[026-025]venture to swear, never saw a Moor in her life, as he is, in his proper

[026-026]costume, and she is this day as the mother that bore her, and I should

[026-027]plainly be doing her a wrong if, fancying anything else, I were to go

[026-028]mad with the same kind of madness as Roland the Furious. On the other

[026-029]hand, I see that Amadis of Gaul, without losing his senses and without

[026-030]doing anything mad, acquired as a lover as much fame as the most

[026-031]famous; for, according to his history, on finding himself rejected by

[026-032]his lady Oriana, who had ordered him not to appear in her presence

[026-033]until it should be her pleasure, all he did was to retire to the Peña

[026-034]Pobre in company with a hermit, and there he took his fill of weeping

[026-035]until Heaven sent him relief in the midst of his great grief and need.

[026-036]And if this be true, as it is, why should I now take the trouble to

[026-037]strip stark naked, or do mischief to these trees which have done me no

[026-038]harm, or why am I to disturb the clear waters of these brooks which

[026-039]will give me to drink whenever I have a mind? Long live the memory of

[026-040]Amadis and let him be imitated so far as is possible by Don Quixote of

[026-041]La Mancha, of whom it will be said, as was said of the other, that if

[026-042]he did not achieve great things, he died in attempting them; and if I

[026-043]am not repulsed or rejected by my Dulcinea, it is enough for me, as I

[026-044]have said, to be absent from her. And so, now to business; come to my

[026-045]memory ye deeds of Amadis, and show me how I am to begin to imitate

[026-046]you. I know already that what he chiefly did was to pray and commend

[026-047]himself to God; but what am I to do for a rosary, for I have not got

[026-048]one?”

[026-049]And then it occurred to him how he might make one, and that was by

[026-050]tearing a great strip off the tail of his shirt which hung down, and

[026-051]making eleven knots on it, one bigger than the rest, and this served

[026-052]him for a rosary all the time he was there, during which he repeated

[026-053]countless ave-marias. But what distressed him greatly was not having

[026-054]another hermit there to confess him and receive consolation from; and

[026-055]so he solaced himself with pacing up and down the little meadow, and

[026-056]writing and carving on the bark of the trees and on the fine sand a

[026-057]multitude of verses all in harmony with his sadness, and some in praise

[026-058]of Dulcinea; but, when he was found there afterwards, the only ones

[026-059]completely legible that could be discovered were those that follow

[026-060]here:

[026-061]Ye on the mountainside that grow,

[026-062]Ye green things all, trees, shrubs, and bushes,

[026-063]Are ye aweary of the woe

[026-064]That this poor aching bosom crushes?

[026-065]If it disturb you, and I owe

[026-066]Some reparation, it may be a

[026-067]Defence for me to let you know

[026-068]Don Quixote’s tears are on the flow,

[026-069]And all for distant Dulcinea

[026-070]Del Toboso.

[026-071]The lealest lover time can show,

[026-072]Doomed for a lady-love to languish,

[026-073]Among these solitudes doth go,

[026-074]A prey to every kind of anguish.

[026-075]Why Love should like a spiteful foe

[026-076]Thus use him, he hath no idea,

[026-077]But hogsheads full—this doth he know—

[026-078]Don Quixote’s tears are on the flow,

[026-079]And all for distant Dulcinea

[026-080]Del Toboso.

[026-081]Adventure-seeking doth he go

[026-082]Up rugged heights, down rocky valleys,

[026-083]But hill or dale, or high or low,

[026-084]Mishap attendeth all his sallies:

[026-085]Love still pursues him to and fro,

[026-086]And plies his cruel scourge—ah me! a

[026-087]Relentless fate, an endless woe;

[026-088]Don Quixote’s tears are on the flow,

[026-089]And all for distant Dulcinea

[026-090]Del Toboso.

[026-091]The addition of “Del Toboso” to Dulcinea’s name gave rise to no little

[026-092]laughter among those who found the above lines, for they suspected Don

[026-093]Quixote must have fancied that unless he added “del Toboso” when he

[026-094]introduced the name of Dulcinea the verse would be unintelligible;

[026-095]which was indeed the fact, as he himself afterwards admitted. He wrote

[026-096]many more, but, as has been said, these three verses were all that

[026-097]could be plainly and perfectly deciphered. In this way, and in sighing

[026-098]and calling on the fauns and satyrs of the woods and the nymphs of the

[026-099]streams, and Echo, moist and mournful, to answer, console, and hear

[026-100]him, as well as in looking for herbs to sustain him, he passed his time

[026-101]until Sancho’s return; and had that been delayed three weeks, as it was

[026-102]three days, the Knight of the Rueful Countenance would have worn such

[026-103]an altered countenance that the mother that bore him would not have

[026-104]known him: and here it will be well to leave him, wrapped up in sighs

[026-105]and verses, to relate how Sancho Panza fared on his mission.

[026-106]As for him, coming out upon the high road, he made for El Toboso, and

[026-107]the next day reached the inn where the mishap of the blanket had

[026-108]befallen him. As soon as he recognised it he felt as if he were once

[026-109]more living through the air, and he could not bring himself to enter it

[026-110]though it was an hour when he might well have done so, for it was

[026-111]dinner-time, and he longed to taste something hot as it had been all

[026-112]cold fare with him for many days past. This craving drove him to draw

[026-113]near to the inn, still undecided whether to go in or not, and as he was

[026-114]hesitating there came out two persons who at once recognised him, and

[026-115]said one to the other:

[026-116]“Señor licentiate, is not he on the horse there Sancho Panza who, our

[026-117]adventurer’s housekeeper told us, went off with her master as esquire?”

[026-118]“So it is,” said the licentiate, “and that is our friend Don Quixote’s

[026-119]horse;” and if they knew him so well it was because they were the

[026-120]curate and the barber of his own village, the same who had carried out

[026-121]the scrutiny and sentence upon the books; and as soon as they

[026-122]recognised Sancho Panza and Rocinante, being anxious to hear of Don

[026-123]Quixote, they approached, and calling him by his name the curate said,

[026-124]“Friend Sancho Panza, where is your master?”

[026-125]Sancho recognised them at once, and determined to keep secret the place

[026-126]and circumstances where and under which he had left his master, so he

[026-127]replied that his master was engaged in a certain quarter on a certain

[026-128]matter of great importance to him which he could not disclose for the

[026-129]eyes in his head.

[026-130]“Nay, nay,” said the barber, “if you don’t tell us where he is, Sancho

[026-131]Panza, we will suspect as we suspect already, that you have murdered

[026-132]and robbed him, for here you are mounted on his horse; in fact, you

[026-133]must produce the master of the hack, or else take the consequences.”

[026-134]“There is no need of threats with me,” said Sancho, “for I am not a man

[026-135]to rob or murder anybody; let his own fate, or God who made him, kill

[026-136]each one; my master is engaged very much to his taste doing penance in

[026-137]the midst of these mountains;” and then, offhand and without stopping,

[026-138]he told them how he had left him, what adventures had befallen him, and

[026-139]how he was carrying a letter to the lady Dulcinea del Toboso, the

[026-140]daughter of Lorenzo Corchuelo, with whom he was over head and ears in

[026-141]love. They were both amazed at what Sancho Panza told them; for though

[026-142]they were aware of Don Quixote’s madness and the nature of it, each

[026-143]time they heard of it they were filled with fresh wonder. They then

[026-144]asked Sancho Panza to show them the letter he was carrying to the lady

[026-145]Dulcinea del Toboso. He said it was written in a notebook, and that his

[026-146]master’s directions were that he should have it copied on paper at the

[026-147]first village he came to. On this the curate said if he showed it to

[026-148]him, he himself would make a fair copy of it. Sancho put his hand into

[026-149]his bosom in search of the notebook but could not find it, nor, if he

[026-150]had been searching until now, could he have found it, for Don Quixote

[026-151]had kept it, and had never given it to him, nor had he himself thought

[026-152]of asking for it. When Sancho discovered he could not find the book his

[026-153]face grew deadly pale, and in great haste he again felt his body all

[026-154]over, and seeing plainly it was not to be found, without more ado he

[026-155]seized his beard with both hands and plucked away half of it, and then,

[026-156]as quick as he could and without stopping, gave himself half a dozen

[026-157]cuffs on the face and nose till they were bathed in blood.

[026-158]Seeing this, the curate and the barber asked him what had happened him

[026-159]that he gave himself such rough treatment.

[026-160]“What should happen to me?” replied Sancho, “but to have lost from one

[026-161]hand to the other, in a moment, three ass-colts, each of them like a

[026-162]castle?”

[026-163]“How is that?” said the barber.

[026-164]“I have lost the notebook,” said Sancho, “that contained the letter to

[026-165]Dulcinea, and an order signed by my master in which he directed his

[026-166]niece to give me three ass-colts out of four or five he had at home;”

[026-167]and he then told them about the loss of Dapple.

[026-168]The curate consoled him, telling him that when his master was found he

[026-169]would get him to renew the order, and make a fresh draft on paper, as

[026-170]was usual and customary; for those made in notebooks were never

[026-171]accepted or honoured.

[026-172]Sancho comforted himself with this, and said if that were so the loss

[026-173]of Dulcinea’s letter did not trouble him much, for he had it almost by

[026-174]heart, and it could be taken down from him wherever and whenever they

[026-175]liked.

[026-176]“Repeat it then, Sancho,” said the barber, “and we will write it down

[026-177]afterwards.”

[026-178]Sancho Panza stopped to scratch his head to bring back the letter to

[026-179]his memory, and balanced himself now on one foot, now the other, one

[026-180]moment staring at the ground, the next at the sky, and after having

[026-181]half gnawed off the end of a finger and kept them in suspense waiting

[026-182]for him to begin, he said, after a long pause, “By God, señor

[026-183]licentiate, devil a thing can I recollect of the letter; but it said at

[026-184]the beginning, ‘Exalted and scrubbing Lady.’”

[026-185]“It cannot have said ‘scrubbing,’” said the barber, “but ‘superhuman’

[026-186]or ‘sovereign.’”

[026-187]“That is it,” said Sancho; “then, as well as I remember, it went on,

[026-188]‘The wounded, and wanting of sleep, and the pierced, kisses your

[026-189]worship’s hands, ungrateful and very unrecognised fair one; and it said

[026-190]something or other about health and sickness that he was sending her;

[026-191]and from that it went tailing off until it ended with ‘Yours till

[026-192]death, the Knight of the Rueful Countenance.’”

[026-193]It gave them no little amusement, both of them, to see what a good

[026-194]memory Sancho had, and they complimented him greatly upon it, and

[026-195]begged him to repeat the letter a couple of times more, so that they

[026-196]too might get it by heart to write it out by-and-by. Sancho repeated it

[026-197]three times, and as he did, uttered three thousand more absurdities;

[026-198]then he told them more about his master but he never said a word about

[026-199]the blanketing that had befallen himself in that inn, into which he

[026-200]refused to enter. He told them, moreover, how his lord, if he brought

[026-201]him a favourable answer from the lady Dulcinea del Toboso, was to put

[026-202]himself in the way of endeavouring to become an emperor, or at least a

[026-203]monarch; for it had been so settled between them, and with his personal

[026-204]worth and the might of his arm it was an easy matter to come to be one:

[026-205]and how on becoming one his lord was to make a marriage for him (for he

[026-206]would be a widower by that time, as a matter of course) and was to give

[026-207]him as a wife one of the damsels of the empress, the heiress of some

[026-208]rich and grand state on the mainland, having nothing to do with islands

[026-209]of any sort, for he did not care for them now. All this Sancho

[026-210]delivered with so much composure—wiping his nose from time to time—and

[026-211]with so little common-sense that his two hearers were again filled with

[026-212]wonder at the force of Don Quixote’s madness that could run away with

[026-213]this poor man’s reason. They did not care to take the trouble of

[026-214]disabusing him of his error, as they considered that since it did not

[026-215]in any way hurt his conscience it would be better to leave him in it,

[026-216]and they would have all the more amusement in listening to his

[026-217]simplicities; and so they bade him pray to God for his lord’s health,

[026-218]as it was a very likely and a very feasible thing for him in course of

[026-219]time to come to be an emperor, as he said, or at least an archbishop or

[026-220]some other dignitary of equal rank.

[026-221]To which Sancho made answer, “If fortune, sirs, should bring things

[026-222]about in such a way that my master should have a mind, instead of being

[026-223]an emperor, to be an archbishop, I should like to know what

[026-224]archbishops-errant commonly give their squires?”

[026-225]“They commonly give them,” said the curate, some simple benefice or

[026-226]cure, or some place as sacristan which brings them a good fixed income,

[026-227]not counting the altar fees, which may be reckoned at as much more.”

[026-228]“But for that,” said Sancho, “the squire must be unmarried, and must

[026-229]know, at any rate, how to help at mass, and if that be so, woe is me,

[026-230]for I am married already and I don’t know the first letter of the A B

[026-231]C. What will become of me if my master takes a fancy to be an

[026-232]archbishop and not an emperor, as is usual and customary with

[026-233]knights-errant?”

[026-234]“Be not uneasy, friend Sancho,” said the barber, “for we will entreat

[026-235]your master, and advise him, even urging it upon him as a case of

[026-236]conscience, to become an emperor and not an archbishop, because it will

[026-237]be easier for him as he is more valiant than lettered.”

[026-238]“So I have thought,” said Sancho; “though I can tell you he is fit for

[026-239]anything: what I mean to do for my part is to pray to our Lord to place

[026-240]him where it may be best for him, and where he may be able to bestow

[026-241]most favours upon me.”

[026-242]“You speak like a man of sense,” said the curate, “and you will be

[026-243]acting like a good Christian; but what must now be done is to take

[026-244]steps to coax your master out of that useless penance you say he is

[026-245]performing; and we had best turn into this inn to consider what plan to

[026-246]adopt, and also to dine, for it is now time.”

[026-247]Sancho said they might go in, but that he would wait there outside, and

[026-248]that he would tell them afterwards the reason why he was unwilling, and

[026-249]why it did not suit him to enter it; but he begged them to bring him

[026-250]out something to eat, and to let it be hot, and also to bring barley

[026-251]for Rocinante. They left him and went in, and presently the barber

[026-252]brought him out something to eat. By-and-by, after they had between

[026-253]them carefully thought over what they should do to carry out their

[026-254]object, the curate hit upon an idea very well adapted to humour Don

[026-255]Quixote, and effect their purpose; and his notion, which he explained

[026-256]to the barber, was that he himself should assume the disguise of a

[026-257]wandering damsel, while the other should try as best he could to pass

[026-258]for a squire, and that they should thus proceed to where Don Quixote

[026-259]was, and he, pretending to be an aggrieved and distressed damsel,

[026-260]should ask a favour of him, which as a valiant knight-errant he could

[026-261]not refuse to grant; and the favour he meant to ask him was that he

[026-262]should accompany her whither she would conduct him, in order to redress

[026-263]a wrong which a wicked knight had done her, while at the same time she

[026-264]should entreat him not to require her to remove her mask, nor ask her

[026-265]any question touching her circumstances until he had righted her with

[026-266]the wicked knight. And he had no doubt that Don Quixote would comply

[026-267]with any request made in these terms, and that in this way they might

[026-268]remove him and take him to his own village, where they would endeavour

[026-269]to find out if his extraordinary madness admitted of any kind of

[026-270]remedy.
