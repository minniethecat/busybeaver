OF WHAT HAPPENED TO THE INGENIOUS GENTLEMAN IN THE INN WHICH HE TOOK TO

[016-002]The innkeeper, seeing Don Quixote slung across the ass, asked Sancho

[016-003]what was amiss with him. Sancho answered that it was nothing, only that

[016-004]he had fallen down from a rock and had his ribs a little bruised. The

[016-005]innkeeper had a wife whose disposition was not such as those of her

[016-006]calling commonly have, for she was by nature kind-hearted and felt for

[016-007]the sufferings of her neighbours, so she at once set about tending Don

[016-008]Quixote, and made her young daughter, a very comely girl, help her in

[016-009]taking care of her guest. There was besides in the inn, as servant, an

[016-010]Asturian lass with a broad face, flat poll, and snub nose, blind of one

[016-011]eye and not very sound in the other. The elegance of her shape, to be

[016-012]sure, made up for all her defects; she did not measure seven palms from

[016-013]head to foot, and her shoulders, which overweighted her somewhat, made

[016-014]her contemplate the ground more than she liked. This graceful lass,

[016-015]then, helped the young girl, and the two made up a very bad bed for Don

[016-016]Quixote in a garret that showed evident signs of having formerly served

[016-017]for many years as a straw-loft, in which there was also quartered a

[016-018]carrier whose bed was placed a little beyond our Don Quixote’s, and,

[016-019]though only made of the pack-saddles and cloths of his mules, had much

[016-020]the advantage of it, as Don Quixote’s consisted simply of four rough

[016-021]boards on two not very even trestles, a mattress, that for thinness

[016-022]might have passed for a quilt, full of pellets which, were they not

[016-023]seen through the rents to be wool, would to the touch have seemed

[016-024]pebbles in hardness, two sheets made of buckler leather, and a coverlet

[016-025]the threads of which anyone that chose might have counted without

[016-026]missing one in the reckoning.

[016-027]On this accursed bed Don Quixote stretched himself, and the hostess and

[016-028]her daughter soon covered him with plasters from top to toe, while

[016-029]Maritornes—for that was the name of the Asturian—held the light for

[016-030]them, and while plastering him, the hostess, observing how full of

[016-031]wheals Don Quixote was in some places, remarked that this had more the

[016-032]look of blows than of a fall.

[016-033]It was not blows, Sancho said, but that the rock had many points and

[016-034]projections, and that each of them had left its mark. “Pray, señora,”

[016-035]he added, “manage to save some tow, as there will be no want of someone

[016-036]to use it, for my loins too are rather sore.”

[016-037]“Then you must have fallen too,” said the hostess.

[016-038]“I did not fall,” said Sancho Panza, “but from the shock I got at

[016-039]seeing my master fall, my body aches so that I feel as if I had had a

[016-040]thousand thwacks.”

[016-041]“That may well be,” said the young girl, “for it has many a time

[016-042]happened to me to dream that I was falling down from a tower and never

[016-043]coming to the ground, and when I awoke from the dream to find myself as

[016-044]weak and shaken as if I had really fallen.”

[016-045]“There is the point, señora,” replied Sancho Panza, “that I without

[016-046]dreaming at all, but being more awake than I am now, find myself with

[016-047]scarcely less wheals than my master, Don Quixote.”

[016-048]“How is the gentleman called?” asked Maritornes the Asturian.

[016-049]“Don Quixote of La Mancha,” answered Sancho Panza, “and he is a

[016-050]knight-adventurer, and one of the best and stoutest that have been seen

[016-051]in the world this long time past.”

[016-052]“What is a knight-adventurer?” said the lass.

[016-053]“Are you so new in the world as not to know?” answered Sancho Panza.

[016-054]“Well, then, you must know, sister, that a knight-adventurer is a thing

[016-055]that in two words is seen drubbed and emperor, that is to-day the most

[016-056]miserable and needy being in the world, and to-morrow will have two or

[016-057]three crowns of kingdoms to give his squire.”

[016-058]“Then how is it,” said the hostess, “that belonging to so good a master

[016-059]as this, you have not, to judge by appearances, even so much as a

[016-060]county?”

[016-061]“It is too soon yet,” answered Sancho, “for we have only been a month

[016-062]going in quest of adventures, and so far we have met with nothing that

[016-063]can be called one, for it will happen that when one thing is looked for

[016-064]another thing is found; however, if my master Don Quixote gets well of

[016-065]this wound, or fall, and I am left none the worse of it, I would not

[016-066]change my hopes for the best title in Spain.”

[016-067]To all this conversation Don Quixote was listening very attentively,

[016-068]and sitting up in bed as well as he could, and taking the hostess by

[016-069]the hand he said to her, “Believe me, fair lady, you may call yourself

[016-070]fortunate in having in this castle of yours sheltered my person, which

[016-071]is such that if I do not myself praise it, it is because of what is

[016-072]commonly said, that self-praise debaseth; but my squire will inform you

[016-073]who I am. I only tell you that I shall preserve for ever inscribed on

[016-074]my memory the service you have rendered me in order to tender you my

[016-075]gratitude while life shall last me; and would to Heaven love held me

[016-076]not so enthralled and subject to its laws and to the eyes of that fair

[016-077]ingrate whom I name between my teeth, but that those of this lovely

[016-078]damsel might be the masters of my liberty.”

[016-079]The hostess, her daughter, and the worthy Maritornes listened in

[016-080]bewilderment to the words of the knight-errant; for they understood

[016-081]about as much of them as if he had been talking Greek, though they

[016-082]could perceive they were all meant for expressions of good-will and

[016-083]blandishments; and not being accustomed to this kind of language, they

[016-084]stared at him and wondered to themselves, for he seemed to them a man

[016-085]of a different sort from those they were used to, and thanking him in

[016-086]pothouse phrase for his civility they left him, while the Asturian gave

[016-087]her attention to Sancho, who needed it no less than his master.

[016-088]The carrier had made an arrangement with her for recreation that night,

[016-089]and she had given him her word that when the guests were quiet and the

[016-090]family asleep she would come in search of him and meet his wishes

[016-091]unreservedly. And it is said of this good lass that she never made

[016-092]promises of the kind without fulfilling them, even though she made them

[016-093]in a forest and without any witness present, for she plumed herself

[016-094]greatly on being a lady and held it no disgrace to be in such an

[016-095]employment as servant in an inn, because, she said, misfortunes and

[016-096]ill-luck had brought her to that position. The hard, narrow, wretched,

[016-097]rickety bed of Don Quixote stood first in the middle of this star-lit

[016-098]stable, and close beside it Sancho made his, which merely consisted of

[016-099]a rush mat and a blanket that looked as if it was of threadbare canvas

[016-100]rather than of wool. Next to these two beds was that of the carrier,

[016-101]made up, as has been said, of the pack-saddles and all the trappings of

[016-102]the two best mules he had, though there were twelve of them, sleek,

[016-103]plump, and in prime condition, for he was one of the rich carriers of

[016-104]Arévalo, according to the author of this history, who particularly

[016-105]mentions this carrier because he knew him very well, and they even say

[016-106]was in some degree a relation of his; besides which Cid Hamete

[016-107]Benengeli was a historian of great research and accuracy in all things,

[016-108]as is very evident since he would not pass over in silence those that

[016-109]have been already mentioned, however trifling and insignificant they

[016-110]might be, an example that might be followed by those grave historians

[016-111]who relate transactions so curtly and briefly that we hardly get a

[016-112]taste of them, all the substance of the work being left in the inkstand

[016-113]from carelessness, perverseness, or ignorance. A thousand blessings on

[016-114]the author of “Tablante de Ricamonte” and that of the other book in

[016-115]which the deeds of the Conde Tomillas are recounted; with what

[016-116]minuteness they describe everything!

[016-117]To proceed, then: after having paid a visit to his team and given them

[016-118]their second feed, the carrier stretched himself on his pack-saddles

[016-119]and lay waiting for his conscientious Maritornes. Sancho was by this

[016-120]time plastered and had lain down, and though he strove to sleep the

[016-121]pain of his ribs would not let him, while Don Quixote with the pain of

[016-122]his had his eyes as wide open as a hare’s.

[016-123]The inn was all in silence, and in the whole of it there was no light

[016-124]except that given by a lantern that hung burning in the middle of the

[016-125]gateway. This strange stillness, and the thoughts, always present to

[016-126]our knight’s mind, of the incidents described at every turn in the

[016-127]books that were the cause of his misfortune, conjured up to his

[016-128]imagination as extraordinary a delusion as can well be conceived, which

[016-129]was that he fancied himself to have reached a famous castle (for, as

[016-130]has been said, all the inns he lodged in were castles to his eyes), and

[016-131]that the daughter of the innkeeper was daughter of the lord of the

[016-132]castle, and that she, won by his high-bred bearing, had fallen in love

[016-133]with him, and had promised to come to his bed for a while that night

[016-134]without the knowledge of her parents; and holding all this fantasy that

[016-135]he had constructed as solid fact, he began to feel uneasy and to

[016-136]consider the perilous risk which his virtue was about to encounter, and

[016-137]he resolved in his heart to commit no treason to his lady Dulcinea del

[016-138]Toboso, even though the queen Guinevere herself and the dame Quintañona

[016-139]should present themselves before him.

[016-140]While he was taken up with these vagaries, then, the time and the

[016-141]hour—an unlucky one for him—arrived for the Asturian to come, who in

[016-142]her smock, with bare feet and her hair gathered into a fustian coif,

[016-143]with noiseless and cautious steps entered the chamber where the three

[016-144]were quartered, in quest of the carrier; but scarcely had she gained

[016-145]the door when Don Quixote perceived her, and sitting up in his bed in

[016-146]spite of his plasters and the pain of his ribs, he stretched out his

[016-147]arms to receive his beauteous damsel. The Asturian, who went all

[016-148]doubled up and in silence with her hands before her feeling for her

[016-149]lover, encountered the arms of Don Quixote, who grasped her tightly by

[016-150]the wrist, and drawing her towards him, while she dared not utter a

[016-151]word, made her sit down on the bed. He then felt her smock, and

[016-152]although it was of sackcloth it appeared to him to be of the finest and

[016-153]softest silk: on her wrists she wore some glass beads, but to him they

[016-154]had the sheen of precious Orient pearls: her hair, which in some

[016-155]measure resembled a horse’s mane, he rated as threads of the brightest

[016-156]gold of Araby, whose refulgence dimmed the sun himself: her breath,

[016-157]which no doubt smelt of yesterday’s stale salad, seemed to him to

[016-158]diffuse a sweet aromatic fragrance from her mouth; and, in short, he

[016-159]drew her portrait in his imagination with the same features and in the

[016-160]same style as that which he had seen in his books of the other

[016-161]princesses who, smitten by love, came with all the adornments that are

[016-162]here set down, to see the sorely wounded knight; and so great was the

[016-163]poor gentleman’s blindness that neither touch, nor smell, nor anything

[016-164]else about the good lass that would have made any but a carrier vomit,

[016-165]were enough to undeceive him; on the contrary, he was persuaded he had

[016-166]the goddess of beauty in his arms, and holding her firmly in his grasp

[016-167]he went on to say in low, tender voice:

[016-168]“Would that I found myself, lovely and exalted lady, in a position to

[016-169]repay such a favour as that which you, by the sight of your great

[016-170]beauty, have granted me; but fortune, which is never weary of

[016-171]persecuting the good, has chosen to place me upon this bed, where I lie

[016-172]so bruised and broken that though my inclination would gladly comply

[016-173]with yours it is impossible; besides, to this impossibility another yet

[016-174]greater is to be added, which is the faith that I have pledged to the

[016-175]peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, sole lady of my most secret thoughts; and

[016-176]were it not that this stood in the way I should not be so insensible a

[016-177]knight as to miss the happy opportunity which your great goodness has

[016-178]offered me.”

[016-179]Maritornes was fretting and sweating at finding herself held so fast by

[016-180]Don Quixote, and not understanding or heeding the words he addressed to

[016-181]her, she strove without speaking to free herself. The worthy carrier,

[016-182]whose unholy thoughts kept him awake, was aware of his doxy the moment

[016-183]she entered the door, and was listening attentively to all Don Quixote

[016-184]said; and jealous that the Asturian should have broken her word with

[016-185]him for another, drew nearer to Don Quixote’s bed and stood still to

[016-186]see what would come of this talk which he could not understand; but

[016-187]when he perceived the wench struggling to get free and Don Quixote

[016-188]striving to hold her, not relishing the joke he raised his arm and

[016-189]delivered such a terrible cuff on the lank jaws of the amorous knight

[016-190]that he bathed all his mouth in blood, and not content with this he

[016-191]mounted on his ribs and with his feet tramped all over them at a pace

[016-192]rather smarter than a trot. The bed which was somewhat crazy and not

[016-193]very firm on its feet, unable to support the additional weight of the

[016-194]carrier, came to the ground, and at the mighty crash of this the

[016-195]innkeeper awoke and at once concluded that it must be some brawl of

[016-196]Maritornes’, because after calling loudly to her he got no answer. With

[016-197]this suspicion he got up, and lighting a lamp hastened to the quarter

[016-198]where he had heard the disturbance. The wench, seeing that her master

[016-199]was coming and knowing that his temper was terrible, frightened and

[016-200]panic-stricken made for the bed of Sancho Panza, who still slept, and

[016-201]crouching upon it made a ball of herself.

[016-202]The innkeeper came in exclaiming, “Where art thou, strumpet? Of course

[016-203]this is some of thy work.” At this Sancho awoke, and feeling this mass

[016-204]almost on top of him fancied he had the nightmare and began to

[016-205]distribute fisticuffs all round, of which a certain share fell upon

[016-206]Maritornes, who, irritated by the pain and flinging modesty aside, paid

[016-207]back so many in return to Sancho that she woke him up in spite of

[016-208]himself. He then, finding himself so handled, by whom he knew not,

[016-209]raising himself up as well as he could, grappled with Maritornes, and

[016-210]he and she between them began the bitterest and drollest scrimmage in

[016-211]the world. The carrier, however, perceiving by the light of the

[016-212]innkeeper candle how it fared with his ladylove, quitting Don Quixote,

[016-213]ran to bring her the help she needed; and the innkeeper did the same

[016-214]but with a different intention, for his was to chastise the lass, as he

[016-215]believed that beyond a doubt she alone was the cause of all the

[016-216]harmony. And so, as the saying is, cat to rat, rat to rope, rope to

[016-217]stick, the carrier pounded Sancho, Sancho the lass, she him, and the

[016-218]innkeeper her, and all worked away so briskly that they did not give

[016-219]themselves a moment’s rest; and the best of it was that the innkeeper’s

[016-220]lamp went out, and as they were left in the dark they all laid on one

[016-221]upon the other in a mass so unmercifully that there was not a sound

[016-222]spot left where a hand could light.

[016-223]It so happened that there was lodging that night in the inn a

[016-224]caudrillero of what they call the Old Holy Brotherhood of Toledo, who,

[016-225]also hearing the extraordinary noise of the conflict, seized his staff

[016-226]and the tin case with his warrants, and made his way in the dark into

[016-227]the room crying: “Hold! in the name of the Jurisdiction! Hold! in the

[016-228]name of the Holy Brotherhood!”

[016-229]The first that he came upon was the pummelled Don Quixote, who lay

[016-230]stretched senseless on his back upon his broken-down bed, and, his hand

[016-231]falling on the beard as he felt about, he continued to cry, “Help for

[016-232]the Jurisdiction!” but perceiving that he whom he had laid hold of did

[016-233]not move or stir, he concluded that he was dead and that those in the

[016-234]room were his murderers, and with this suspicion he raised his voice

[016-235]still higher, calling out, “Shut the inn gate; see that no one goes

[016-236]out; they have killed a man here!” This cry startled them all, and each

[016-237]dropped the contest at the point at which the voice reached him. The

[016-238]innkeeper retreated to his room, the carrier to his pack-saddles, the

[016-239]lass to her crib; the unlucky Don Quixote and Sancho alone were unable

[016-240]to move from where they were. The cuadrillero on this let go Don

[016-241]Quixote’s beard, and went out to look for a light to search for and

[016-242]apprehend the culprits; but not finding one, as the innkeeper had

[016-243]purposely extinguished the lantern on retreating to his room, he was

[016-244]compelled to have recourse to the hearth, where after much time and

[016-245]trouble he lit another lamp.
