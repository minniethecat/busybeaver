[036-001]Just at that instant the landlord, who was standing at the gate of the

[036-002]inn, exclaimed, “Here comes a fine troop of guests; if they stop here

[036-003]we may say _gaudeamus_.”

[036-004]“What are they?” said Cardenio.

[036-005]“Four men,” said the landlord, “riding _à la jineta_, with lances and

[036-006]bucklers, and all with black veils, and with them there is a woman in

[036-007]white on a side-saddle, whose face is also veiled, and two attendants

[036-008]on foot.”

[036-009]“Are they very near?” said the curate.

[036-010]“So near,” answered the landlord, “that here they come.”

[036-011]Hearing this Dorothea covered her face, and Cardenio retreated into Don

[036-012]Quixote’s room, and they hardly had time to do so before the whole

[036-013]party the host had described entered the inn, and the four that were on

[036-014]horseback, who were of highbred appearance and bearing, dismounted, and

[036-015]came forward to take down the woman who rode on the side-saddle, and

[036-016]one of them taking her in his arms placed her in a chair that stood at

[036-017]the entrance of the room where Cardenio had hidden himself. All this

[036-018]time neither she nor they had removed their veils or spoken a word,

[036-019]only on sitting down on the chair the woman gave a deep sigh and let

[036-020]her arms fall like one that was ill and weak. The attendants on foot

[036-021]then led the horses away to the stable. Observing this the curate,

[036-022]curious to know who these people in such a dress and preserving such

[036-023]silence were, went to where the servants were standing and put the

[036-024]question to one of them, who answered him.

[036-025]“Faith, sir, I cannot tell you who they are, I only know they seem to

[036-026]be people of distinction, particularly he who advanced to take the lady

[036-027]you saw in his arms; and I say so because all the rest show him

[036-028]respect, and nothing is done except what he directs and orders.”

[036-029]“And the lady, who is she?” asked the curate.

[036-030]“That I cannot tell you either,” said the servant, “for I have not seen

[036-031]her face all the way: I have indeed heard her sigh many times and utter

[036-032]such groans that she seems to be giving up the ghost every time; but it

[036-033]is no wonder if we do not know more than we have told you, as my

[036-034]comrade and I have only been in their company two days, for having met

[036-035]us on the road they begged and persuaded us to accompany them to

[036-036]Andalusia, promising to pay us well.”

[036-037]“And have you heard any of them called by his name?” asked the curate.

[036-038]“No, indeed,” replied the servant; “they all preserve a marvellous

[036-039]silence on the road, for not a sound is to be heard among them except

[036-040]the poor lady’s sighs and sobs, which make us pity her; and we feel

[036-041]sure that wherever it is she is going, it is against her will, and as

[036-042]far as one can judge from her dress she is a nun or, what is more

[036-043]likely, about to become one; and perhaps it is because taking the vows

[036-044]is not of her own free will, that she is so unhappy as she seems to

[036-045]be.”

[036-046]“That may well be,” said the curate, and leaving them he returned to

[036-047]where Dorothea was, who, hearing the veiled lady sigh, moved by natural

[036-048]compassion drew near to her and said, “What are you suffering from,

[036-049]señora? If it be anything that women are accustomed and know how to

[036-050]relieve, I offer you my services with all my heart.”

[036-051]To this the unhappy lady made no reply; and though Dorothea repeated

[036-052]her offers more earnestly she still kept silence, until the gentleman

[036-053]with the veil, who, the servant said, was obeyed by the rest,

[036-054]approached and said to Dorothea, “Do not give yourself the trouble,

[036-055]señora, of making any offers to that woman, for it is her way to give

[036-056]no thanks for anything that is done for her; and do not try to make her

[036-057]answer unless you want to hear some lie from her lips.”

[036-058]“I have never told a lie,” was the immediate reply of her who had been

[036-059]silent until now; “on the contrary, it is because I am so truthful and

[036-060]so ignorant of lying devices that I am now in this miserable condition;

[036-061]and this I call you yourself to witness, for it is my unstained truth

[036-062]that has made you false and a liar.”

[036-063]Cardenio heard these words clearly and distinctly, being quite close to

[036-064]the speaker, for there was only the door of Don Quixote’s room between

[036-065]them, and the instant he did so, uttering a loud exclamation he cried,

[036-066]“Good God! what is this I hear? What voice is this that has reached my

[036-067]ears?” Startled at the voice the lady turned her head; and not seeing

[036-068]the speaker she stood up and attempted to enter the room; observing

[036-069]which the gentleman held her back, preventing her from moving a step.

[036-070]In her agitation and sudden movement the silk with which she had

[036-071]covered her face fell off and disclosed a countenance of incomparable

[036-072]and marvellous beauty, but pale and terrified; for she kept turning her

[036-073]eyes, everywhere she could direct her gaze, with an eagerness that made

[036-074]her look as if she had lost her senses, and so marked that it excited

[036-075]the pity of Dorothea and all who beheld her, though they knew not what

[036-076]caused it. The gentleman grasped her firmly by the shoulders, and being

[036-077]so fully occupied with holding her back, he was unable to put a hand to

[036-078]his veil which was falling off, as it did at length entirely, and

[036-079]Dorothea, who was holding the lady in her arms, raising her eyes saw

[036-080]that he who likewise held her was her husband, Don Fernando. The

[036-081]instant she recognised him, with a prolonged plaintive cry drawn from

[036-082]the depths of her heart, she fell backwards fainting, and but for the

[036-083]barber being close by to catch her in his arms, she would have fallen

[036-084]completely to the ground. The curate at once hastened to uncover her

[036-085]face and throw water on it, and as he did so Don Fernando, for he it

[036-086]was who held the other in his arms, recognised her and stood as if

[036-087]death-stricken by the sight; not, however, relaxing his grasp of

[036-088]Luscinda, for it was she that was struggling to release herself from

[036-089]his hold, having recognised Cardenio by his voice, as he had recognised

[036-090]her. Cardenio also heard Dorothea’s cry as she fell fainting, and

[036-091]imagining that it came from his Luscinda burst forth in terror from the

[036-092]room, and the first thing he saw was Don Fernando with Luscinda in his

[036-093]arms. Don Fernando, too, knew Cardenio at once; and all three,

[036-094]Luscinda, Cardenio, and Dorothea, stood in silent amazement scarcely

[036-095]knowing what had happened to them.

[036-096]They gazed at one another without speaking, Dorothea at Don Fernando,

[036-097]Don Fernando at Cardenio, Cardenio at Luscinda, and Luscinda at

[036-098]Cardenio. The first to break silence was Luscinda, who thus addressed

[036-099]Don Fernando: “Leave me, Señor Don Fernando, for the sake of what you

[036-100]owe to yourself; if no other reason will induce you, leave me to cling

[036-101]to the wall of which I am the ivy, to the support from which neither

[036-102]your importunities, nor your threats, nor your promises, nor your gifts

[036-103]have been able to detach me. See how Heaven, by ways strange and hidden

[036-104]from our sight, has brought me face to face with my true husband; and

[036-105]well you know by dear-bought experience that death alone will be able

[036-106]to efface him from my memory. May this plain declaration, then, lead

[036-107]you, as you can do nothing else, to turn your love into rage, your

[036-108]affection into resentment, and so to take my life; for if I yield it up

[036-109]in the presence of my beloved husband I count it well bestowed; it may

[036-110]be by my death he will be convinced that I kept my faith to him to the

[036-111]last moment of life.”

[036-112]Meanwhile Dorothea had come to herself, and had heard Luscinda’s words,

[036-113]by means of which she divined who she was; but seeing that Don Fernando

[036-114]did not yet release her or reply to her, summoning up her resolution as

[036-115]well as she could she rose and knelt at his feet, and with a flood of

[036-116]bright and touching tears addressed him thus:

[036-117]“If, my lord, the beams of that sun that thou holdest eclipsed in thine

[036-118]arms did not dazzle and rob thine eyes of sight thou wouldst have seen

[036-119]by this time that she who kneels at thy feet is, so long as thou wilt

[036-120]have it so, the unhappy and unfortunate Dorothea. I am that lowly

[036-121]peasant girl whom thou in thy goodness or for thy pleasure wouldst

[036-122]raise high enough to call herself thine; I am she who in the seclusion

[036-123]of innocence led a contented life until at the voice of thy

[036-124]importunity, and thy true and tender passion, as it seemed, she opened

[036-125]the gates of her modesty and surrendered to thee the keys of her

[036-126]liberty; a gift received by thee but thanklessly, as is clearly shown

[036-127]by my forced retreat to the place where thou dost find me, and by thy

[036-128]appearance under the circumstances in which I see thee. Nevertheless, I

[036-129]would not have thee suppose that I have come here driven by my shame;

[036-130]it is only grief and sorrow at seeing myself forgotten by thee that

[036-131]have led me. It was thy will to make me thine, and thou didst so follow

[036-132]thy will, that now, even though thou repentest, thou canst not help

[036-133]being mine. Bethink thee, my lord, the unsurpassable affection I bear

[036-134]thee may compensate for the beauty and noble birth for which thou

[036-135]wouldst desert me. Thou canst not be the fair Luscinda’s because thou

[036-136]art mine, nor can she be thine because she is Cardenio’s; and it will

[036-137]be easier, remember, to bend thy will to love one who adores thee, than

[036-138]to lead one to love thee who abhors thee now. Thou didst address

[036-139]thyself to my simplicity, thou didst lay siege to my virtue, thou wert

[036-140]not ignorant of my station, well dost thou know how I yielded wholly to

[036-141]thy will; there is no ground or reason for thee to plead deception, and

[036-142]if it be so, as it is, and if thou art a Christian as thou art a

[036-143]gentleman, why dost thou by such subterfuges put off making me as happy

[036-144]at last as thou didst at first? And if thou wilt not have me for what I

[036-145]am, thy true and lawful wife, at least take and accept me as thy slave,

[036-146]for so long as I am thine I will count myself happy and fortunate. Do

[036-147]not by deserting me let my shame become the talk of the gossips in the

[036-148]streets; make not the old age of my parents miserable; for the loyal

[036-149]services they as faithful vassals have ever rendered thine are not

[036-150]deserving of such a return; and if thou thinkest it will debase thy

[036-151]blood to mingle it with mine, reflect that there is little or no

[036-152]nobility in the world that has not travelled the same road, and that in

[036-153]illustrious lineages it is not the woman’s blood that is of account;

[036-154]and, moreover, that true nobility consists in virtue, and if thou art

[036-155]wanting in that, refusing me what in justice thou owest me, then even I

[036-156]have higher claims to nobility than thine. To make an end, señor, these

[036-157]are my last words to thee: whether thou wilt, or wilt not, I am thy

[036-158]wife; witness thy words, which must not and ought not to be false, if

[036-159]thou dost pride thyself on that for want of which thou scornest me;

[036-160]witness the pledge which thou didst give me, and witness Heaven, which

[036-161]thou thyself didst call to witness the promise thou hadst made me; and

[036-162]if all this fail, thy own conscience will not fail to lift up its

[036-163]silent voice in the midst of all thy gaiety, and vindicate the truth of

[036-164]what I say and mar thy highest pleasure and enjoyment.”

[036-165]All this and more the injured Dorothea delivered with such earnest

[036-166]feeling and such tears that all present, even those who came with Don

[036-167]Fernando, were constrained to join her in them. Don Fernando listened

[036-168]to her without replying, until, ceasing to speak, she gave way to such

[036-169]sobs and sighs that it must have been a heart of brass that was not

[036-170]softened by the sight of so great sorrow. Luscinda stood regarding her

[036-171]with no less compassion for her sufferings than admiration for her

[036-172]intelligence and beauty, and would have gone to her to say some words

[036-173]of comfort to her, but was prevented by Don Fernando’s grasp which held

[036-174]her fast. He, overwhelmed with confusion and astonishment, after

[036-175]regarding Dorothea for some moments with a fixed gaze, opened his arms,

[036-176]and, releasing Luscinda, exclaimed:

[036-177]“Thou hast conquered, fair Dorothea, thou hast conquered, for it is

[036-178]impossible to have the heart to deny the united force of so many

[036-179]truths.”

[036-180]Luscinda in her feebleness was on the point of falling to the ground

[036-181]when Don Fernando released her, but Cardenio, who stood near, having

[036-182]retreated behind Don Fernando to escape recognition, casting fear aside

[036-183]and regardless of what might happen, ran forward to support her, and

[036-184]said as he clasped her in his arms, “If Heaven in its compassion is

[036-185]willing to let thee rest at last, mistress of my heart, true, constant,

[036-186]and fair, nowhere canst thou rest more safely than in these arms that

[036-187]now receive thee, and received thee before when fortune permitted me to

[036-188]call thee mine.”

[036-189]At these words Luscinda looked up at Cardenio, at first beginning to

[036-190]recognise him by his voice and then satisfying herself by her eyes that

[036-191]it was he, and hardly knowing what she did, and heedless of all

[036-192]considerations of decorum, she flung her arms around his neck and

[036-193]pressing her face close to his, said, “Yes, my dear lord, you are the

[036-194]true master of this your slave, even though adverse fate interpose

[036-195]again, and fresh dangers threaten this life that hangs on yours.”

[036-196]A strange sight was this for Don Fernando and those that stood around,

[036-197]filled with surprise at an incident so unlooked for. Dorothea fancied

[036-198]that Don Fernando changed colour and looked as though he meant to take

[036-199]vengeance on Cardenio, for she observed him put his hand to his sword;

[036-200]and the instant the idea struck her, with wonderful quickness she

[036-201]clasped him round the knees, and kissing them and holding him so as to

[036-202]prevent his moving, she said, while her tears continued to flow, “What

[036-203]is it thou wouldst do, my only refuge, in this unforeseen event? Thou

[036-204]hast thy wife at thy feet, and she whom thou wouldst have for thy wife

[036-205]is in the arms of her husband: reflect whether it will be right for

[036-206]thee, whether it will be possible for thee to undo what Heaven has

[036-207]done, or whether it will be becoming in thee to seek to raise her to be

[036-208]thy mate who in spite of every obstacle, and strong in her truth and

[036-209]constancy, is before thine eyes, bathing with the tears of love the

[036-210]face and bosom of her lawful husband. For God’s sake I entreat of thee,

[036-211]for thine own I implore thee, let not this open manifestation rouse thy

[036-212]anger; but rather so calm it as to allow these two lovers to live in

[036-213]peace and quiet without any interference from thee so long as Heaven

[036-214]permits them; and in so doing thou wilt prove the generosity of thy

[036-215]lofty noble spirit, and the world shall see that with thee reason has

[036-216]more influence than passion.”

[036-217]All the time Dorothea was speaking, Cardenio, though he held Luscinda

[036-218]in his arms, never took his eyes off Don Fernando, determined, if he

[036-219]saw him make any hostile movement, to try and defend himself and resist

[036-220]as best he could all who might assail him, though it should cost him

[036-221]his life. But now Don Fernando’s friends, as well as the curate and the

[036-222]barber, who had been present all the while, not forgetting the worthy

[036-223]Sancho Panza, ran forward and gathered round Don Fernando, entreating

[036-224]him to have regard for the tears of Dorothea, and not suffer her

[036-225]reasonable hopes to be disappointed, since, as they firmly believed,

[036-226]what she said was but the truth; and bidding him observe that it was

[036-227]not, as it might seem, by accident, but by a special disposition of

[036-228]Providence that they had all met in a place where no one could have

[036-229]expected a meeting. And the curate bade him remember that only death

[036-230]could part Luscinda from Cardenio; that even if some sword were to

[036-231]separate them they would think their death most happy; and that in a

[036-232]case that admitted of no remedy his wisest course was, by conquering

[036-233]and putting a constraint upon himself, to show a generous mind, and of

[036-234]his own accord suffer these two to enjoy the happiness Heaven had

[036-235]granted them. He bade him, too, turn his eyes upon the beauty of

[036-236]Dorothea and he would see that few if any could equal much less excel

[036-237]her; while to that beauty should be added her modesty and the

[036-238]surpassing love she bore him. But besides all this, he reminded him

[036-239]that if he prided himself on being a gentleman and a Christian, he

[036-240]could not do otherwise than keep his plighted word; and that in doing

[036-241]so he would obey God and meet the approval of all sensible people, who

[036-242]know and recognised it to be the privilege of beauty, even in one of

[036-243]humble birth, provided virtue accompany it, to be able to raise itself

[036-244]to the level of any rank, without any slur upon him who places it upon

[036-245]an equality with himself; and furthermore that when the potent sway of

[036-246]passion asserts itself, so long as there be no mixture of sin in it, he

[036-247]is not to be blamed who gives way to it.

[036-248]To be brief, they added to these such other forcible arguments that Don

[036-249]Fernando’s manly heart, being after all nourished by noble blood, was

[036-250]touched, and yielded to the truth which, even had he wished it, he

[036-251]could not gainsay; and he showed his submission, and acceptance of the

[036-252]good advice that had been offered to him, by stooping down and

[036-253]embracing Dorothea, saying to her, “Rise, dear lady, it is not right

[036-254]that what I hold in my heart should be kneeling at my feet; and if

[036-255]until now I have shown no sign of what I own, it may have been by

[036-256]Heaven’s decree in order that, seeing the constancy with which you love

[036-257]me, I may learn to value you as you deserve. What I entreat of you is

[036-258]that you reproach me not with my transgression and grievous

[036-259]wrong-doing; for the same cause and force that drove me to make you

[036-260]mine impelled me to struggle against being yours; and to prove this,

[036-261]turn and look at the eyes of the now happy Luscinda, and you will see

[036-262]in them an excuse for all my errors: and as she has found and gained

[036-263]the object of her desires, and I have found in you what satisfies all

[036-264]my wishes, may she live in peace and contentment as many happy years

[036-265]with her Cardenio, as on my knees I pray Heaven to allow me to live

[036-266]with my Dorothea;” and with these words he once more embraced her and

[036-267]pressed his face to hers with so much tenderness that he had to take

[036-268]great heed to keep his tears from completing the proof of his love and

[036-269]repentance in the sight of all. Not so Luscinda, and Cardenio, and

[036-270]almost all the others, for they shed so many tears, some in their own

[036-271]happiness, some at that of the others, that one would have supposed a

[036-272]heavy calamity had fallen upon them all. Even Sancho Panza was weeping;

[036-273]though afterwards he said he only wept because he saw that Dorothea was

[036-274]not as he fancied the queen Micomicona, of whom he expected such great

[036-275]favours. Their wonder as well as their weeping lasted some time, and

[036-276]then Cardenio and Luscinda went and fell on their knees before Don

[036-277]Fernando, returning him thanks for the favour he had rendered them in

[036-278]language so grateful that he knew not how to answer them, and raising

[036-279]them up embraced them with every mark of affection and courtesy.

[036-280]He then asked Dorothea how she had managed to reach a place so far

[036-281]removed from her own home, and she in a few fitting words told all that

[036-282]she had previously related to Cardenio, with which Don Fernando and his

[036-283]companions were so delighted that they wished the story had been

[036-284]longer; so charmingly did Dorothea describe her misadventures. When she

[036-285]had finished Don Fernando recounted what had befallen him in the city

[036-286]after he had found in Luscinda’s bosom the paper in which she declared

[036-287]that she was Cardenio’s wife, and never could be his. He said he meant

[036-288]to kill her, and would have done so had he not been prevented by her

[036-289]parents, and that he quitted the house full of rage and shame, and

[036-290]resolved to avenge himself when a more convenient opportunity should

[036-291]offer. The next day he learned that Luscinda had disappeared from her

[036-292]father’s house, and that no one could tell whither she had gone.

[036-293]Finally, at the end of some months he ascertained that she was in a

[036-294]convent and meant to remain there all the rest of her life, if she were

[036-295]not to share it with Cardenio; and as soon as he had learned this,

[036-296]taking these three gentlemen as his companions, he arrived at the place

[036-297]where she was, but avoided speaking to her, fearing that if it were

[036-298]known he was there stricter precautions would be taken in the convent;

[036-299]and watching a time when the porter’s lodge was open he left two to

[036-300]guard the gate, and he and the other entered the convent in quest of

[036-301]Luscinda, whom they found in the cloisters in conversation with one of

[036-302]the nuns, and carrying her off without giving her time to resist, they

[036-303]reached a place with her where they provided themselves with what they

[036-304]required for taking her away; all which they were able to do in

[036-305]complete safety, as the convent was in the country at a considerable

[036-306]distance from the city. He added that when Luscinda found herself in

[036-307]his power she lost all consciousness, and after returning to herself

[036-308]did nothing but weep and sigh without speaking a word; and thus in

[036-309]silence and tears they reached that inn, which for him was reaching

[036-310]heaven where all the mischances of earth are over and at an end.
