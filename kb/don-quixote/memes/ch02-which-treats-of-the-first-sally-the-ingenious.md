[002-001]These preliminaries settled, he did not care to put off any longer the

[002-002]execution of his design, urged on to it by the thought of all the world

[002-003]was losing by his delay, seeing what wrongs he intended to right,

[002-004]grievances to redress, injustices to repair, abuses to remove, and

[002-005]duties to discharge. So, without giving notice of his intention to

[002-006]anyone, and without anybody seeing him, one morning before the dawning

[002-007]of the day (which was one of the hottest of the month of July) he

[002-008]donned his suit of armour, mounted Rocinante with his patched-up helmet

[002-009]on, braced his buckler, took his lance, and by the back door of the

[002-010]yard sallied forth upon the plain in the highest contentment and

[002-011]satisfaction at seeing with what ease he had made a beginning with his

[002-012]grand purpose. But scarcely did he find himself upon the open plain,

[002-013]when a terrible thought struck him, one all but enough to make him

[002-014]abandon the enterprise at the very outset. It occurred to him that he

[002-015]had not been dubbed a knight, and that according to the law of chivalry

[002-016]he neither could nor ought to bear arms against any knight; and that

[002-017]even if he had been, still he ought, as a novice knight, to wear white

[002-018]armour, without a device upon the shield until by his prowess he had

[002-019]earned one. These reflections made him waver in his purpose, but his

[002-020]craze being stronger than any reasoning, he made up his mind to have

[002-021]himself dubbed a knight by the first one he came across, following the

[002-022]example of others in the same case, as he had read in the books that

[002-023]brought him to this pass. As for white armour, he resolved, on the

[002-024]first opportunity, to scour his until it was whiter than an ermine; and

[002-025]so comforting himself he pursued his way, taking that which his horse

[002-026]chose, for in this he believed lay the essence of adventures.

[002-027]Thus setting out, our new-fledged adventurer paced along, talking to

[002-028]himself and saying, “Who knows but that in time to come, when the

[002-029]veracious history of my famous deeds is made known, the sage who writes

[002-030]it, when he has to set forth my first sally in the early morning, will

[002-031]do it after this fashion? ‘Scarce had the rubicund Apollo spread o’er

[002-032]the face of the broad spacious earth the golden threads of his bright

[002-033]hair, scarce had the little birds of painted plumage attuned their

[002-034]notes to hail with dulcet and mellifluous harmony the coming of the

[002-035]rosy Dawn, that, deserting the soft couch of her jealous spouse, was

[002-036]appearing to mortals at the gates and balconies of the Manchegan

[002-037]horizon, when the renowned knight Don Quixote of La Mancha, quitting

[002-038]the lazy down, mounted his celebrated steed Rocinante and began to

[002-039]traverse the ancient and famous Campo de Montiel;’” which in fact he

[002-040]was actually traversing. “Happy the age, happy the time,” he continued,

[002-041]“in which shall be made known my deeds of fame, worthy to be moulded in

[002-042]brass, carved in marble, limned in pictures, for a memorial for ever.

[002-043]And thou, O sage magician, whoever thou art, to whom it shall fall to

[002-044]be the chronicler of this wondrous history, forget not, I entreat thee,

[002-045]my good Rocinante, the constant companion of my ways and wanderings.”

[002-046]Presently he broke out again, as if he were love-stricken in earnest,

[002-047]“O Princess Dulcinea, lady of this captive heart, a grievous wrong hast

[002-048]thou done me to drive me forth with scorn, and with inexorable obduracy

[002-049]banish me from the presence of thy beauty. O lady, deign to hold in

[002-050]remembrance this heart, thy vassal, that thus in anguish pines for love

[002-051]of thee.”

[002-052]So he went on stringing together these and other absurdities, all in

[002-053]the style of those his books had taught him, imitating their language

[002-054]as well as he could; and all the while he rode so slowly and the sun

[002-055]mounted so rapidly and with such fervour that it was enough to melt his

[002-056]brains if he had any. Nearly all day he travelled without anything

[002-057]remarkable happening to him, at which he was in despair, for he was

[002-058]anxious to encounter someone at once upon whom to try the might of his

[002-059]strong arm.

[002-060]Writers there are who say the first adventure he met with was that of

[002-061]Puerto Lapice; others say it was that of the windmills; but what I have

[002-062]ascertained on this point, and what I have found written in the annals

[002-063]of La Mancha, is that he was on the road all day, and towards nightfall

[002-064]his hack and he found themselves dead tired and hungry, when, looking

[002-065]all around to see if he could discover any castle or shepherd’s shanty

[002-066]where he might refresh himself and relieve his sore wants, he perceived

[002-067]not far out of his road an inn, which was as welcome as a star guiding

[002-068]him to the portals, if not the palaces, of his redemption; and

[002-069]quickening his pace he reached it just as night was setting in. At the

[002-070]door were standing two young women, girls of the district as they call

[002-071]them, on their way to Seville with some carriers who had chanced to

[002-072]halt that night at the inn; and as, happen what might to our

[002-073]adventurer, everything he saw or imagined seemed to him to be and to

[002-074]happen after the fashion of what he read of, the moment he saw the inn

[002-075]he pictured it to himself as a castle with its four turrets and

[002-076]pinnacles of shining silver, not forgetting the drawbridge and moat and

[002-077]all the belongings usually ascribed to castles of the sort. To this

[002-078]inn, which to him seemed a castle, he advanced, and at a short distance

[002-079]from it he checked Rocinante, hoping that some dwarf would show himself

[002-080]upon the battlements, and by sound of trumpet give notice that a knight

[002-081]was approaching the castle. But seeing that they were slow about it,

[002-082]and that Rocinante was in a hurry to reach the stable, he made for the

[002-083]inn door, and perceived the two gay damsels who were standing there,

[002-084]and who seemed to him to be two fair maidens or lovely ladies taking

[002-085]their ease at the castle gate.

[002-086]At this moment it so happened that a swineherd who was going through

[002-087]the stubbles collecting a drove of pigs (for, without any apology, that

[002-088]is what they are called) gave a blast of his horn to bring them

[002-089]together, and forthwith it seemed to Don Quixote to be what he was

[002-090]expecting, the signal of some dwarf announcing his arrival; and so with

[002-091]prodigious satisfaction he rode up to the inn and to the ladies, who,

[002-092]seeing a man of this sort approaching in full armour and with lance and

[002-093]buckler, were turning in dismay into the inn, when Don Quixote,

[002-094]guessing their fear by their flight, raising his pasteboard visor,

[002-095]disclosed his dry dusty visage, and with courteous bearing and gentle

[002-096]voice addressed them, “Your ladyships need not fly or fear any

[002-097]rudeness, for that it belongs not to the order of knighthood which I

[002-098]profess to offer to anyone, much less to highborn maidens as your

[002-099]appearance proclaims you to be.” The girls were looking at him and

[002-100]straining their eyes to make out the features which the clumsy visor

[002-101]obscured, but when they heard themselves called maidens, a thing so

[002-102]much out of their line, they could not restrain their laughter, which

[002-103]made Don Quixote wax indignant, and say, “Modesty becomes the fair, and

[002-104]moreover laughter that has little cause is great silliness; this,

[002-105]however, I say not to pain or anger you, for my desire is none other

[002-106]than to serve you.”

[002-107]The incomprehensible language and the unpromising looks of our cavalier

[002-108]only increased the ladies’ laughter, and that increased his irritation,

[002-109]and matters might have gone farther if at that moment the landlord had

[002-110]not come out, who, being a very fat man, was a very peaceful one. He,

[002-111]seeing this grotesque figure clad in armour that did not match any more

[002-112]than his saddle, bridle, lance, buckler, or corselet, was not at all

[002-113]indisposed to join the damsels in their manifestations of amusement;

[002-114]but, in truth, standing in awe of such a complicated armament, he

[002-115]thought it best to speak him fairly, so he said, “Señor Caballero, if

[002-116]your worship wants lodging, bating the bed (for there is not one in the

[002-117]inn) there is plenty of everything else here.” Don Quixote, observing

[002-118]the respectful bearing of the Alcaide of the fortress (for so innkeeper

[002-119]and inn seemed in his eyes), made answer, “Sir Castellan, for me

[002-120]anything will suffice, for

[002-121]‘My armour is my only wear,

[002-122]My only rest the fray.’”

[002-123]The host fancied he called him Castellan because he took him for a

[002-124]“worthy of Castile,” though he was in fact an Andalusian, and one from

[002-125]the strand of San Lucar, as crafty a thief as Cacus and as full of

[002-126]tricks as a student or a page. “In that case,” said he,

[002-127]“‘Your bed is on the flinty rock,

[002-128]Your sleep to watch alway;’

[002-129]and if so, you may dismount and safely reckon upon any quantity of

[002-130]sleeplessness under this roof for a twelvemonth, not to say for a

[002-131]single night.” So saying, he advanced to hold the stirrup for Don

[002-132]Quixote, who got down with great difficulty and exertion (for he had

[002-133]not broken his fast all day), and then charged the host to take great

[002-134]care of his horse, as he was the best bit of flesh that ever ate bread

[002-135]in this world. The landlord eyed him over but did not find him as good

[002-136]as Don Quixote said, nor even half as good; and putting him up in the

[002-137]stable, he returned to see what might be wanted by his guest, whom the

[002-138]damsels, who had by this time made their peace with him, were now

[002-139]relieving of his armour. They had taken off his breastplate and

[002-140]backpiece, but they neither knew nor saw how to open his gorget or

[002-141]remove his make-shift helmet, for he had fastened it with green

[002-142]ribbons, which, as there was no untying the knots, required to be cut.

[002-143]This, however, he would not by any means consent to, so he remained all

[002-144]the evening with his helmet on, the drollest and oddest figure that can

[002-145]be imagined; and while they were removing his armour, taking the

[002-146]baggages who were about it for ladies of high degree belonging to the

[002-147]castle, he said to them with great sprightliness:

[002-148]“Oh, never, surely, was there knight

[002-149]So served by hand of dame,

[002-150]As served was he, Don Quixote hight,

[002-151]When from his town he came;

[002-152]With maidens waiting on himself,

[002-153]Princesses on his hack—

[002-154]—or Rocinante, for that, ladies mine, is my horse’s name, and Don

[002-155]Quixote of La Mancha is my own; for though I had no intention of

[002-156]declaring myself until my achievements in your service and honour had

[002-157]made me known, the necessity of adapting that old ballad of Lancelot to

[002-158]the present occasion has given you the knowledge of my name altogether

[002-159]prematurely. A time, however, will come for your ladyships to command

[002-160]and me to obey, and then the might of my arm will show my desire to

[002-161]serve you.”

[002-162]The girls, who were not used to hearing rhetoric of this sort, had

[002-163]nothing to say in reply; they only asked him if he wanted anything to

[002-164]eat. “I would gladly eat a bit of something,” said Don Quixote, “for I

[002-165]feel it would come very seasonably.” The day happened to be a Friday,

[002-166]and in the whole inn there was nothing but some pieces of the fish they

[002-167]call in Castile “abadejo,” in Andalusia “bacallao,” and in some places

[002-168]“curadillo,” and in others “troutlet;” so they asked him if he thought

[002-169]he could eat troutlet, for there was no other fish to give him. “If

[002-170]there be troutlets enough,” said Don Quixote, “they will be the same

[002-171]thing as a trout; for it is all one to me whether I am given eight

[002-172]reals in small change or a piece of eight; moreover, it may be that

[002-173]these troutlets are like veal, which is better than beef, or kid, which

[002-174]is better than goat. But whatever it be let it come quickly, for the

[002-175]burden and pressure of arms cannot be borne without support to the

[002-176]inside.” They laid a table for him at the door of the inn for the sake

[002-177]of the air, and the host brought him a portion of ill-soaked and worse

[002-178]cooked stockfish, and a piece of bread as black and mouldy as his own

[002-179]armour; but a laughable sight it was to see him eating, for having his

[002-180]helmet on and the beaver up, he could not with his own hands put

[002-181]anything into his mouth unless someone else placed it there, and this

[002-182]service one of the ladies rendered him. But to give him anything to

[002-183]drink was impossible, or would have been so had not the landlord bored

[002-184]a reed, and putting one end in his mouth poured the wine into him

[002-185]through the other; all which he bore with patience rather than sever

[002-186]the ribbons of his helmet.

[002-187]While this was going on there came up to the inn a sowgelder, who, as

[002-188]he approached, sounded his reed pipe four or five times, and thereby

[002-189]completely convinced Don Quixote that he was in some famous castle, and

[002-190]that they were regaling him with music, and that the stockfish was

[002-191]trout, the bread the whitest, the wenches ladies, and the landlord the

[002-192]castellan of the castle; and consequently he held that his enterprise

[002-193]and sally had been to some purpose. But still it distressed him to

[002-194]think he had not been dubbed a knight, for it was plain to him he could

[002-195]not lawfully engage in any adventure without receiving the order of

[002-196]knighthood.
