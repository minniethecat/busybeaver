[042-002]With these words the captive held his peace, and Don Fernando said to

[042-003]him, “In truth, captain, the manner in which you have related this

[042-004]remarkable adventure has been such as befitted the novelty and

[042-005]strangeness of the matter. The whole story is curious and uncommon, and

[042-006]abounds with incidents that fill the hearers with wonder and

[042-007]astonishment; and so great is the pleasure we have found in listening

[042-008]to it that we should be glad if it were to begin again, even though

[042-009]to-morrow were to find us still occupied with the same tale.” And while

[042-010]he said this Cardenio and the rest of them offered to be of service to

[042-011]him in any way that lay in their power, and in words and language so

[042-012]kindly and sincere that the captain was much gratified by their

[042-013]good-will. In particular Don Fernando offered, if he would go back with

[042-014]him, to get his brother the marquis to become godfather at the baptism

[042-015]of Zoraida, and on his own part to provide him with the means of making

[042-016]his appearance in his own country with the credit and comfort he was

[042-017]entitled to. For all this the captive returned thanks very courteously,

[042-018]although he would not accept any of their generous offers.

[042-019]By this time night closed in, and as it did, there came up to the inn a

[042-020]coach attended by some men on horseback, who demanded accommodation; to

[042-021]which the landlady replied that there was not a hand’s breadth of the

[042-022]whole inn unoccupied.

[042-023]“Still, for all that,” said one of those who had entered on horseback,

[042-024]“room must be found for his lordship the Judge here.”

[042-025]At this name the landlady was taken aback, and said, “Señor, the fact

[042-026]is I have no beds; but if his lordship the Judge carries one with him,

[042-027]as no doubt he does, let him come in and welcome; for my husband and I

[042-028]will give up our room to accommodate his worship.”

[042-029]“Very good, so be it,” said the squire; but in the meantime a man had

[042-030]got out of the coach whose dress indicated at a glance the office and

[042-031]post he held, for the long robe with ruffled sleeves that he wore

[042-032]showed that he was, as his servant said, a Judge of appeal. He led by

[042-033]the hand a young girl in a travelling dress, apparently about sixteen

[042-034]years of age, and of such a high-bred air, so beautiful and so

[042-035]graceful, that all were filled with admiration when she made her

[042-036]appearance, and but for having seen Dorothea, Luscinda, and Zoraida,

[042-037]who were there in the inn, they would have fancied that a beauty like

[042-038]that of this maiden’s would have been hard to find. Don Quixote was

[042-039]present at the entrance of the Judge with the young lady, and as soon

[042-040]as he saw him he said, “Your worship may with confidence enter and take

[042-041]your ease in this castle; for though the accommodation be scanty and

[042-042]poor, there are no quarters so cramped or inconvenient that they cannot

[042-043]make room for arms and letters; above all if arms and letters have

[042-044]beauty for a guide and leader, as letters represented by your worship

[042-045]have in this fair maiden, to whom not only ought castles to throw

[042-046]themselves open and yield themselves up, but rocks should rend

[042-047]themselves asunder and mountains divide and bow themselves down to give

[042-048]her a reception. Enter, your worship, I say, into this paradise, for

[042-049]here you will find stars and suns to accompany the heaven your worship

[042-050]brings with you, here you will find arms in their supreme excellence,

[042-051]and beauty in its highest perfection.”

[042-052]The Judge was struck with amazement at the language of Don Quixote,

[042-053]whom he scrutinized very carefully, no less astonished by his figure

[042-054]than by his talk; and before he could find words to answer him he had a

[042-055]fresh surprise, when he saw opposite to him Luscinda, Dorothea, and

[042-056]Zoraida, who, having heard of the new guests and of the beauty of the

[042-057]young lady, had come to see her and welcome her; Don Fernando,

[042-058]Cardenio, and the curate, however, greeted him in a more intelligible

[042-059]and polished style. In short, the Judge made his entrance in a state of

[042-060]bewilderment, as well with what he saw as what he heard, and the fair

[042-061]ladies of the inn gave the fair damsel a cordial welcome. On the whole

[042-062]he could perceive that all who were there were people of quality; but

[042-063]with the figure, countenance, and bearing of Don Quixote he was at his

[042-064]wits’ end; and all civilities having been exchanged, and the

[042-065]accommodation of the inn inquired into, it was settled, as it had been

[042-066]before settled, that all the women should retire to the garret that has

[042-067]been already mentioned, and that the men should remain outside as if to

[042-068]guard them; the Judge, therefore, was very well pleased to allow his

[042-069]daughter, for such the damsel was, to go with the ladies, which she did

[042-070]very willingly; and with part of the host’s narrow bed and half of what

[042-071]the Judge had brought with him, they made a more comfortable

[042-072]arrangement for the night than they had expected.

[042-073]The captive, whose heart had leaped within him the instant he saw the

[042-074]Judge, telling him somehow that this was his brother, asked one of the

[042-075]servants who accompanied him what his name was, and whether he knew

[042-076]from what part of the country he came. The servant replied that he was

[042-077]called the Licentiate Juan Perez de Viedma, and that he had heard it

[042-078]said he came from a village in the mountains of Leon. From this

[042-079]statement, and what he himself had seen, he felt convinced that this

[042-080]was his brother who had adopted letters by his father’s advice; and

[042-081]excited and rejoiced, he called Don Fernando and Cardenio and the

[042-082]curate aside, and told them how the matter stood, assuring them that

[042-083]the judge was his brother. The servant had further informed him that he

[042-084]was now going to the Indies with the appointment of Judge of the

[042-085]Supreme Court of Mexico; and he had learned, likewise, that the young

[042-086]lady was his daughter, whose mother had died in giving birth to her,

[042-087]and that he was very rich in consequence of the dowry left to him with

[042-088]the daughter. He asked their advice as to what means he should adopt to

[042-089]make himself known, or to ascertain beforehand whether, when he had

[042-090]made himself known, his brother, seeing him so poor, would be ashamed

[042-091]of him, or would receive him with a warm heart.

[042-092]“Leave it to me to find out that,” said the curate; “though there is no

[042-093]reason for supposing, señor captain, that you will not be kindly

[042-094]received, because the worth and wisdom that your brother’s bearing

[042-095]shows him to possess do not make it likely that he will prove haughty

[042-096]or insensible, or that he will not know how to estimate the accidents

[042-097]of fortune at their proper value.”

[042-098]“Still,” said the captain, “I would not make myself known abruptly, but

[042-099]in some indirect way.”

[042-100]“I have told you already,” said the curate, “that I will manage it in a

[042-101]way to satisfy us all.”

[042-102]By this time supper was ready, and they all took their seats at the

[042-103]table, except the captive, and the ladies, who supped by themselves in

[042-104]their own room. In the middle of supper the curate said:

[042-105]“I had a comrade of your worship’s name, Señor Judge, in

[042-106]Constantinople, where I was a captive for several years, and that same

[042-107]comrade was one of the stoutest soldiers and captains in the whole

[042-108]Spanish infantry; but he had as large a share of misfortune as he had

[042-109]of gallantry and courage.”

[042-110]“And how was the captain called, señor?” asked the Judge.

[042-111]“He was called Ruy Perez de Viedma,” replied the curate, “and he was

[042-112]born in a village in the mountains of Leon; and he mentioned a

[042-113]circumstance connected with his father and his brothers which, had it

[042-114]not been told me by so truthful a man as he was, I should have set down

[042-115]as one of those fables the old women tell over the fire in winter; for

[042-116]he said his father had divided his property among his three sons and

[042-117]had addressed words of advice to them sounder than any of Cato’s. But I

[042-118]can say this much, that the choice he made of going to the wars was

[042-119]attended with such success, that by his gallant conduct and courage,

[042-120]and without any help save his own merit, he rose in a few years to be

[042-121]captain of infantry, and to see himself on the high-road and in

[042-122]position to be given the command of a corps before long; but Fortune

[042-123]was against him, for where he might have expected her favour he lost

[042-124]it, and with it his liberty, on that glorious day when so many

[042-125]recovered theirs, at the battle of Lepanto. I lost mine at the Goletta,

[042-126]and after a variety of adventures we found ourselves comrades at

[042-127]Constantinople. Thence he went to Algiers, where he met with one of the

[042-128]most extraordinary adventures that ever befell anyone in the world.”

[042-129]Here the curate went on to relate briefly his brother’s adventure with

[042-130]Zoraida; to all which the Judge gave such an attentive hearing that he

[042-131]never before had been so much of a hearer. The curate, however, only

[042-132]went so far as to describe how the Frenchmen plundered those who were

[042-133]in the boat, and the poverty and distress in which his comrade and the

[042-134]fair Moor were left, of whom he said he had not been able to learn what

[042-135]became of them, or whether they had reached Spain, or been carried to

[042-136]France by the Frenchmen.

[042-137]The captain, standing a little to one side, was listening to all the

[042-138]curate said, and watching every movement of his brother, who, as soon

[042-139]as he perceived the curate had made an end of his story, gave a deep

[042-140]sigh and said with his eyes full of tears, “Oh, señor, if you only knew

[042-141]what news you have given me and how it comes home to me, making me show

[042-142]how I feel it with these tears that spring from my eyes in spite of all

[042-143]my worldly wisdom and self-restraint! That brave captain that you speak

[042-144]of is my eldest brother, who, being of a bolder and loftier mind than

[042-145]my other brother or myself, chose the honourable and worthy calling of

[042-146]arms, which was one of the three careers our father proposed to us, as

[042-147]your comrade mentioned in that fable you thought he was telling you. I

[042-148]followed that of letters, in which God and my own exertions have raised

[042-149]me to the position in which you see me. My second brother is in Peru,

[042-150]so wealthy that with what he has sent to my father and to me he has

[042-151]fully repaid the portion he took with him, and has even furnished my

[042-152]father’s hands with the means of gratifying his natural generosity,

[042-153]while I too have been enabled to pursue my studies in a more becoming

[042-154]and creditable fashion, and so to attain my present standing. My father

[042-155]is still alive, though dying with anxiety to hear of his eldest son,

[042-156]and he prays God unceasingly that death may not close his eyes until he

[042-157]has looked upon those of his son; but with regard to him what surprises

[042-158]me is, that having so much common sense as he had, he should have

[042-159]neglected to give any intelligence about himself, either in his

[042-160]troubles and sufferings, or in his prosperity, for if his father or any

[042-161]of us had known of his condition he need not have waited for that

[042-162]miracle of the reed to obtain his ransom; but what now disquiets me is

[042-163]the uncertainty whether those Frenchmen may have restored him to

[042-164]liberty, or murdered him to hide the robbery. All this will make me

[042-165]continue my journey, not with the satisfaction in which I began it, but

[042-166]in the deepest melancholy and sadness. Oh dear brother! that I only

[042-167]knew where thou art now, and I would hasten to seek thee out and

[042-168]deliver thee from thy sufferings, though it were to cost me suffering

[042-169]myself! Oh that I could bring news to our old father that thou art

[042-170]alive, even wert thou in the deepest dungeon of Barbary; for his wealth

[042-171]and my brother’s and mine would rescue thee thence! Oh beautiful and

[042-172]generous Zoraida, that I could repay thy goodness to a brother! That I

[042-173]could be present at the new birth of thy soul, and at thy bridal that

[042-174]would give us all such happiness!”

[042-175]All this and more the Judge uttered with such deep emotion at the news

[042-176]he had received of his brother that all who heard him shared in it,

[042-177]showing their sympathy with his sorrow. The curate, seeing, then, how

[042-178]well he had succeeded in carrying out his purpose and the captain’s

[042-179]wishes, had no desire to keep them unhappy any longer, so he rose from

[042-180]the table and going into the room where Zoraida was he took her by the

[042-181]hand, Luscinda, Dorothea, and the Judge’s daughter following her. The

[042-182]captain was waiting to see what the curate would do, when the latter,

[042-183]taking him with the other hand, advanced with both of them to where the

[042-184]Judge and the other gentlemen were and said, “Let your tears cease to

[042-185]flow, Señor Judge, and the wish of your heart be gratified as fully as

[042-186]you could desire, for you have before you your worthy brother and your

[042-187]good sister-in-law. He whom you see here is the Captain Viedma, and

[042-188]this is the fair Moor who has been so good to him. The Frenchmen I told

[042-189]you of have reduced them to the state of poverty you see that you may

[042-190]show the generosity of your kind heart.”

[042-191]The captain ran to embrace his brother, who placed both hands on his

[042-192]breast so as to have a good look at him, holding him a little way off

[042-193]but as soon as he had fully recognised him he clasped him in his arms

[042-194]so closely, shedding such tears of heartfelt joy, that most of those

[042-195]present could not but join in them. The words the brothers exchanged,

[042-196]the emotion they showed can scarcely be imagined, I fancy, much less

[042-197]put down in writing. They told each other in a few words the events of

[042-198]their lives; they showed the true affection of brothers in all its

[042-199]strength; then the judge embraced Zoraida, putting all he possessed at

[042-200]her disposal; then he made his daughter embrace her, and the fair

[042-201]Christian and the lovely Moor drew fresh tears from every eye. And

[042-202]there was Don Quixote observing all these strange proceedings

[042-203]attentively without uttering a word, and attributing the whole to

[042-204]chimeras of knight-errantry. Then they agreed that the captain and

[042-205]Zoraida should return with his brother to Seville, and send news to his

[042-206]father of his having been delivered and found, so as to enable him to

[042-207]come and be present at the marriage and baptism of Zoraida, for it was

[042-208]impossible for the Judge to put off his journey, as he was informed

[042-209]that in a month from that time the fleet was to sail from Seville for

[042-210]New Spain, and to miss the passage would have been a great

[042-211]inconvenience to him. In short, everybody was well pleased and glad at

[042-212]the captive’s good fortune; and as now almost two-thirds of the night

[042-213]were past, they resolved to retire to rest for the remainder of it. Don

[042-214]Quixote offered to mount guard over the castle lest they should be

[042-215]attacked by some giant or other malevolent scoundrel, covetous of the

[042-216]great treasure of beauty the castle contained. Those who understood him

[042-217]returned him thanks for this service, and they gave the Judge an

[042-218]account of his extraordinary humour, with which he was not a little

[042-219]amused. Sancho Panza alone was fuming at the lateness of the hour for

[042-220]retiring to rest; and he of all was the one that made himself most

[042-221]comfortable, as he stretched himself on the trappings of his ass,

[042-222]which, as will be told farther on, cost him so dear.

[042-223]The ladies, then, having retired to their chamber, and the others

[042-224]having disposed themselves with as little discomfort as they could, Don
