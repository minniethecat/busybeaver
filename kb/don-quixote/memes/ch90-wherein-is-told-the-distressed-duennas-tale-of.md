[090-001]WHEREIN IS TOLD THE DISTRESSED DUENNA’S TALE OF HER MISFORTUNES

p38a.jpg (54K)


[090-002]Following the melancholy musicians there filed into the garden as many

[090-003]as twelve duennas, in two lines, all dressed in ample mourning robes

[090-004]apparently of milled serge, with hoods of fine white gauze so long that

[090-005]they allowed only the border of the robe to be seen. Behind them came

[090-006]the Countess Trifaldi, the squire Trifaldin of the White Beard leading

[090-007]her by the hand, clad in the finest unnapped black baize, such that,

[090-008]had it a nap, every tuft would have shown as big as a Martos chickpea;

[090-009]the tail, or skirt, or whatever it might be called, ended in three

[090-010]points which were borne up by the hands of three pages, likewise

[090-011]dressed in mourning, forming an elegant geometrical figure with the

[090-012]three acute angles made by the three points, from which all who saw the

[090-013]peaked skirt concluded that it must be because of it the countess was

[090-014]called Trifaldi, as though it were Countess of the Three Skirts; and

[090-015]Benengeli says it was so, and that by her right name she was called the

[090-016]Countess Lobuna, because wolves bred in great numbers in her country;

[090-017]and if, instead of wolves, they had been foxes, she would have been

[090-018]called the Countess Zorruna, as it was the custom in those parts for

[090-019]lords to take distinctive titles from the thing or things most abundant

[090-020]in their dominions; this countess, however, in honour of the new

[090-021]fashion of her skirt, dropped Lobuna and took up Trifaldi.

[090-022]The twelve duennas and the lady came on at procession pace, their faces

[090-023]being covered with black veils, not transparent ones like Trifaldin’s,

[090-024]but so close that they allowed nothing to be seen through them. As soon

[090-025]as the band of duennas was fully in sight, the duke, the duchess, and

[090-026]Don Quixote stood up, as well as all who were watching the slow-moving

[090-027]procession. The twelve duennas halted and formed a lane, along which

[090-028]the Distressed One advanced, Trifaldin still holding her hand. On

[090-029]seeing this the duke, the duchess, and Don Quixote went some twelve

[090-030]paces forward to meet her. She then, kneeling on the ground, said in a

[090-031]voice hoarse and rough, rather than fine and delicate, “May it please

[090-032]your highnesses not to offer such courtesies to this your servant, I

[090-033]should say to this your handmaid, for I am in such distress that I

[090-034]shall never be able to make a proper return, because my strange and

[090-035]unparalleled misfortune has carried off my wits, and I know not

[090-036]whither; but it must be a long way off, for the more I look for them

[090-037]the less I find them.”

[090-038]“He would be wanting in wits, señora countess,” said the duke, “who did

[090-039]not perceive your worth by your person, for at a glance it may be seen

[090-040]it deserves all the cream of courtesy and flower of polite usage;” and

[090-041]raising her up by the hand he led her to a seat beside the duchess, who

[090-042]likewise received her with great urbanity. Don Quixote remained silent,

[090-043]while Sancho was dying to see the features of Trifaldi and one or two

[090-044]of her many duennas; but there was no possibility of it until they

[090-045]themselves displayed them of their own accord and free will.

[090-046]All kept still, waiting to see who would break silence, which the

[090-047]Distressed Duenna did in these words: “I am confident, most mighty

[090-048]lord, most fair lady, and most discreet company, that my most miserable

[090-049]misery will be accorded a reception no less dispassionate than generous

[090-050]and condolent in your most valiant bosoms, for it is one that is enough

[090-051]to melt marble, soften diamonds, and mollify the steel of the most

[090-052]hardened hearts in the world; but ere it is proclaimed to your hearing,

[090-053]not to say your ears, I would fain be enlightened whether there be

[090-054]present in this society, circle, or company, that knight

[090-055]immaculatissimus, Don Quixote de la Manchissima, and his squirissimus

[090-056]Panza.”

[090-057]“The Panza is here,” said Sancho, before anyone could reply, “and Don

[090-058]Quixotissimus too; and so, most distressedest Duenissima, you may say

[090-059]what you willissimus, for we are all readissimus to do you any

[090-060]servissimus.”

[090-061]On this Don Quixote rose, and addressing the Distressed Duenna, said,

[090-062]“If your sorrows, afflicted lady, can indulge in any hope of relief

[090-063]from the valour or might of any knight-errant, here are mine, which,

[090-064]feeble and limited though they be, shall be entirely devoted to your

[090-065]service. I am Don Quixote of La Mancha, whose calling it is to give aid

[090-066]to the needy of all sorts; and that being so, it is not necessary for

[090-067]you, señora, to make any appeal to benevolence, or deal in preambles,

[090-068]only to tell your woes plainly and straightforwardly: for you have

[090-069]hearers that will know how, if not to remedy them, to sympathise with

[090-070]them.”

[090-071]On hearing this, the Distressed Duenna made as though she would throw

[090-072]herself at Don Quixote’s feet, and actually did fall before them and

[090-073]said, as she strove to embrace them, “Before these feet and legs I cast

[090-074]myself, O unconquered knight, as before, what they are, the foundations

[090-075]and pillars of knight-errantry; these feet I desire to kiss, for upon

[090-076]their steps hangs and depends the sole remedy for my misfortune, O

[090-077]valorous errant, whose veritable achievements leave behind and eclipse

[090-078]the fabulous ones of the Amadises, Esplandians, and Belianises!” Then

[090-079]turning from Don Quixote to Sancho Panza, and grasping his hands, she

[090-080]said, “O thou, most loyal squire that ever served knight-errant in this

[090-081]present age or ages past, whose goodness is more extensive than the

[090-082]beard of Trifaldin my companion here of present, well mayest thou boast

[090-083]thyself that, in serving the great Don Quixote, thou art serving,

[090-084]summed up in one, the whole host of knights that have ever borne arms

[090-085]in the world. I conjure thee, by what thou owest to thy most loyal

[090-086]goodness, that thou wilt become my kind intercessor with thy master,

[090-087]that he speedily give aid to this most humble and most unfortunate

[090-088]countess.”

[090-089]To this Sancho made answer, “As to my goodness, señora, being as long

[090-090]and as great as your squire’s beard, it matters very little to me; may

[090-091]I have my soul well bearded and moustached when it comes to quit this

[090-092]life, that’s the point; about beards here below I care little or

[090-093]nothing; but without all these blandishments and prayers, I will beg my

[090-094]master (for I know he loves me, and, besides, he has need of me just

[090-095]now for a certain business) to help and aid your worship as far as he

[090-096]can; unpack your woes and lay them before us, and leave us to deal with

[090-097]them, for we’ll be all of one mind.”

[090-098]The duke and duchess, as it was they who had made the experiment of

[090-099]this adventure, were ready to burst with laughter at all this, and

[090-100]between themselves they commended the clever acting of the Trifaldi,

[090-101]who, returning to her seat, said, “Queen Doña Maguncia reigned over the

[090-102]famous kingdom of Kandy, which lies between the great Trapobana and the

[090-103]Southern Sea, two leagues beyond Cape Comorin. She was the widow of

[090-104]King Archipiela, her lord and husband, and of their marriage they had

[090-105]issue the Princess Antonomasia, heiress of the kingdom; which Princess

[090-106]Antonomasia was reared and brought up under my care and direction, I

[090-107]being the oldest and highest in rank of her mother’s duennas. Time

[090-108]passed, and the young Antonomasia reached the age of fourteen, and such

[090-109]a perfection of beauty, that nature could not raise it higher. Then, it

[090-110]must not be supposed her intelligence was childish; she was as

[090-111]intelligent as she was fair, and she was fairer than all the world; and

[090-112]is so still, unless the envious fates and hard-hearted sisters three

[090-113]have cut for her the thread of life. But that they have not, for Heaven

[090-114]will not suffer so great a wrong to Earth, as it would be to pluck

[090-115]unripe the grapes of the fairest vineyard on its surface. Of this

[090-116]beauty, to which my poor feeble tongue has failed to do justice,

[090-117]countless princes, not only of that country, but of others, were

[090-118]enamoured, and among them a private gentleman, who was at the court,

[090-119]dared to raise his thoughts to the heaven of so great beauty, trusting

[090-120]to his youth, his gallant bearing, his numerous accomplishments and

[090-121]graces, and his quickness and readiness of wit; for I may tell your

[090-122]highnesses, if I am not wearying you, that he played the guitar so as

[090-123]to make it speak, and he was, besides, a poet and a great dancer, and

[090-124]he could make birdcages so well, that by making them alone he might

[090-125]have gained a livelihood, had he found himself reduced to utter

[090-126]poverty; and gifts and graces of this kind are enough to bring down a

[090-127]mountain, not to say a tender young girl. But all his gallantry, wit,

[090-128]and gaiety, all his graces and accomplishments, would have been of

[090-129]little or no avail towards gaining the fortress of my pupil, had not

[090-130]the impudent thief taken the precaution of gaining me over first.

[090-131]First, the villain and heartless vagabond sought to win my good-will

[090-132]and purchase my compliance, so as to get me, like a treacherous warder,

[090-133]to deliver up to him the keys of the fortress I had in charge. In a

[090-134]word, he gained an influence over my mind, and overcame my resolutions

[090-135]with I know not what trinkets and jewels he gave me; but it was some

[090-136]verses I heard him singing one night from a grating that opened on the

[090-137]street where he lived, that, more than anything else, made me give way

[090-138]and led to my fall; and if I remember rightly they ran thus:

[090-139]From that sweet enemy of mine

[090-140]My bleeding heart hath had its wound;

[090-141]And to increase the pain I’m bound

[090-142]To suffer and to make no sign.

[090-143]The lines seemed pearls to me and his voice sweet as syrup; and

[090-144]afterwards, I may say ever since then, looking at the misfortune into

[090-145]which I have fallen, I have thought that poets, as Plato advised, ought

[090-146]to be banished from all well-ordered States; at least the amatory ones,

[090-147]for they write verses, not like those of ‘The Marquis of Mantua,’ that

[090-148]delight and draw tears from the women and children, but sharp-pointed

[090-149]conceits that pierce the heart like soft thorns, and like the lightning

[090-150]strike it, leaving the raiment uninjured. Another time he sang:

[090-151]Come Death, so subtly veiled that I

[090-152]Thy coming know not, how or when,

[090-153]Lest it should give me life again

[090-154]To find how sweet it is to die.

[090-155]—and other verses and burdens of the same sort, such as enchant when

[090-156]sung and fascinate when written. And then, when they condescend to

[090-157]compose a sort of verse that was at that time in vogue in Kandy, which

[090-158]they call seguidillas! Then it is that hearts leap and laughter breaks

[090-159]forth, and the body grows restless and all the senses turn quicksilver.

[090-160]And so I say, sirs, that these troubadours richly deserve to be

[090-161]banished to the isles of the lizards. Though it is not they that are in

[090-162]fault, but the simpletons that extol them, and the fools that believe

[090-163]in them; and had I been the faithful duenna I should have been, his

[090-164]stale conceits would have never moved me, nor should I have been taken

[090-165]in by such phrases as ‘in death I live,’ ‘in ice I burn,’ ‘in flames I

[090-166]shiver,’ ‘hopeless I hope,’ ‘I go and stay,’ and paradoxes of that sort

[090-167]which their writings are full of. And then when they promise the Phœnix

[090-168]of Arabia, the crown of Ariadne, the horses of the Sun, the pearls of

[090-169]the South, the gold of Tibar, and the balsam of Panchaia! Then it is

[090-170]they give a loose to their pens, for it costs them little to make

[090-171]promises they have no intention or power of fulfilling. But where am I

[090-172]wandering to? Woe is me, unfortunate being! What madness or folly leads

[090-173]me to speak of the faults of others, when there is so much to be said

[090-174]about my own? Again, woe is me, hapless that I am! it was not verses

[090-175]that conquered me, but my own simplicity; it was not music made me

[090-176]yield, but my own imprudence; my own great ignorance and little caution

[090-177]opened the way and cleared the path for Don Clavijo’s advances, for

[090-178]that was the name of the gentleman I have referred to; and so, with my

[090-179]help as go-between, he found his way many a time into the chamber of

[090-180]the deceived Antonomasia (deceived not by him but by me) under the

[090-181]title of a lawful husband; for, sinner though I was, I would not have

[090-182]allowed him to approach the edge of her shoe-sole without being her

[090-183]husband. No, no, not that; marriage must come first in any business of

[090-184]this sort that I take in hand. But there was one hitch in this case,

[090-185]which was that of inequality of rank, Don Clavijo being a private

[090-186]gentleman, and the Princess Antonomasia, as I said, heiress to the

[090-187]kingdom. The entanglement remained for some time a secret, kept hidden

[090-188]by my cunning precautions, until I perceived that a certain expansion

[090-189]of waist in Antonomasia must before long disclose it, the dread of

[090-190]which made us all there take counsel together, and it was agreed that

[090-191]before the mischief came to light, Don Clavijo should demand

[090-192]Antonomasia as his wife before the Vicar, in virtue of an agreement to

[090-193]marry him made by the princess, and drafted by my wit in such binding

[090-194]terms that the might of Samson could not have broken it. The necessary

[090-195]steps were taken; the Vicar saw the agreement, and took the lady’s

[090-196]confession; she confessed everything in full, and he ordered her into

[090-197]the custody of a very worthy alguacil of the court.”

[090-198]“Are there alguacils of the court in Kandy, too,” said Sancho at this,

[090-199]“and poets, and seguidillas? I swear I think the world is the same all

[090-200]over! But make haste, Señora Trifaldi; for it is late, and I am dying

[090-201]to know the end of this long story.”

[090-202]“I will,” replied the countess.
