# Samson Carrasco

**Samson Carrasco** (Spanish: *Sansón Carrasco*) is a pivotal figure in Part II of *Don Quixote*. A bachelor from the University of Salamanca, he learns of Don Quixote's first sally through the published chronicle by Cide Hamete Benengeli. Determined to cure the knight's madness, Carrasco twice disguises himself as a knight-errant and challenges Don Quixote to single combat — first as the **Knight of the Mirrors** (*El Caballero de los Espejos*) and later as the **Knight of the White Moon** (*El Caballero de la Blanca Luna*).

## Character Profile

Carrasco is introduced as a clever, somewhat mischievous young scholar. The narrator describes him as "of no great bodily size" but sharp of wit:

> "for though he was called Samson, he was no giant, being of no great bodily size, though he was a great wag"[055-029]

His name — Samson — is itself an ironic joke: the biblical Samson was known for physical strength; Carrasco's power lies entirely in his intellect and cunning. He is described as "the arch wag," a prankster who derives genuine amusement from the knight's delusions:

> "The arch wag Samson came forward, and embracing him as he had done before, said in a loud voice..."[059-137]

## Role in the Narrative

### Messenger of Fame

Carrasco serves as the bridge between Part I and Part II. Having read the published adventures, he brings news to Don Quixote and Sancho that their exploits are now famous throughout Spain — a development that drives much of Part II's metafictional humor. In a key conversation, he discusses the reception of the chronicle:

> "it is so plain that there is nothing in it difficult or obscure: children handle it, youths read it, men understand it, and old people applaud it"[055-180]

Carrasco also reveals the book's commercial success and the public's mixed reactions, noting some readers complain that Sancho is "too credulous" while others find the inserted tales digressive. This literary-critical dialogue makes Carrasco the novel's internal representative of the reading public.

### Knight of the Mirrors

Carrasco's first attempt to defeat Don Quixote occurs in Chapter 60 (Chapter 14 of Part II in traditional numbering). Disguised as the Knight of the Mirrors — with shining mirrors covering his armor — he challenges Don Quixote but is unexpectedly defeated when Rocinante stumbles and the bachelor falls from his horse. This defeat only deepens his determination.

### Knight of the White Moon

His second and successful challenge comes near the novel's end, on the beach of Barcelona. Now calling himself the Knight of the White Moon, Carrasco defeats Don Quixote and, as the victor's condition, compels the knight to return to his village and abandon knight-errantry for one year — a condition that ultimately leads to Don Quixote's deathbed recovery of sanity.

## Relationship with Other Characters

Samson is a close associate of [[the-priest|the Priest]] and [[master-nicholas|Master Nicholas]], the barber — together they form the "home team" conspiring to cure Don Quixote's madness. Carrasco's methods are more theatrical and aggressive than his co-conspirators' gentler approaches.

He also develops a playful rapport with [[sancho-panza|Sancho Panza]], engaging in long conversations about governance, fame, and practical wisdom. Sancho, for his part, holds Carrasco's learning in genuine respect:

> "honours change manners, and it may be that when I find myself a governor I won't know the mother that bore me"[056-136]

## Thematic Significance

Samson Carrasco embodies the novel's central tension between reality and fiction. He enters Don Quixote's fictional world — dressing as a knight-errant, adopting a chivalric name, using the language of romance — specifically to demolish it. Yet in doing so, he validates the very logic he seeks to destroy: to cure Quixote, he must become Quixote.

His dual identity as both Scholar and Knight mirrors Don Quixote's own transformation from Alonso Quijano, the gentleman, into Don Quixote, the knight. The difference is that Carrasco can take off the armor whenever he wishes; for Quixote, the armor has become his skin.
