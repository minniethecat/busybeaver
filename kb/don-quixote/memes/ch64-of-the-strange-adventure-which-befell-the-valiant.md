OF THE STRANGE ADVENTURE WHICH BEFELL THE VALIANT DON QUIXOTE WITH THE

BOLD KNIGHT OF THE MIRRORS

p12a.jpg (98K)


[064-001]The night succeeding the day of the encounter with Death, Don Quixote

[064-002]and his squire passed under some tall shady trees, and Don Quixote at

[064-003]Sancho’s persuasion ate a little from the store carried by Dapple, and

[064-004]over their supper Sancho said to his master, “Señor, what a fool I

[064-005]should have looked if I had chosen for my reward the spoils of the

[064-006]first adventure your worship achieved, instead of the foals of the

[064-007]three mares. After all, ‘a sparrow in the hand is better than a vulture

[064-008]on the wing.’”

[064-009]“At the same time, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “if thou hadst let me

[064-010]attack them as I wanted, at the very least the emperor’s gold crown and

[064-011]Cupid’s painted wings would have fallen to thee as spoils, for I should

[064-012]have taken them by force and given them into thy hands.”

[064-013]“The sceptres and crowns of those play-actor emperors,” said Sancho,

[064-014]“were never yet pure gold, but only brass foil or tin.”

[064-015]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, “for it would not be right that the

[064-016]accessories of the drama should be real, instead of being mere fictions

[064-017]and semblances, like the drama itself; towards which, Sancho—and, as a

[064-018]necessary consequence, towards those who represent and produce it—I

[064-019]would that thou wert favourably disposed, for they are all instruments

[064-020]of great good to the State, placing before us at every step a mirror in

[064-021]which we may see vividly displayed what goes on in human life; nor is

[064-022]there any similitude that shows us more faithfully what we are and

[064-023]ought to be than the play and the players. Come, tell me, hast thou not

[064-024]seen a play acted in which kings, emperors, pontiffs, knights, ladies,

[064-025]and divers other personages were introduced? One plays the villain,

[064-026]another the knave, this one the merchant, that the soldier, one the

[064-027]sharp-witted fool, another the foolish lover; and when the play is

[064-028]over, and they have put off the dresses they wore in it, all the actors

[064-029]become equal.”

[064-030]“Yes, I have seen that,” said Sancho.

[064-031]“Well then,” said Don Quixote, “the same thing happens in the comedy

[064-032]and life of this world, where some play emperors, others popes, and, in

[064-033]short, all the characters that can be brought into a play; but when it

[064-034]is over, that is to say when life ends, death strips them all of the

[064-035]garments that distinguish one from the other, and all are equal in the

[064-036]grave.”

[064-037]“A fine comparison!” said Sancho; “though not so new but that I have

[064-038]heard it many and many a time, as well as that other one of the game of

[064-039]chess; how, so long as the game lasts, each piece has its own

[064-040]particular office, and when the game is finished they are all mixed,

[064-041]jumbled up and shaken together, and stowed away in the bag, which is

[064-042]much like ending life in the grave.”

[064-043]“Thou art growing less doltish and more shrewd every day, Sancho,” said

[064-044]Don Quixote.

[064-045]“Ay,” said Sancho; “it must be that some of your worship’s shrewdness

[064-046]sticks to me; land that, of itself, is barren and dry, will come to

[064-047]yield good fruit if you dung it and till it; what I mean is that your

[064-048]worship’s conversation has been the dung that has fallen on the barren

[064-049]soil of my dry wit, and the time I have been in your service and

[064-050]society has been the tillage; and with the help of this I hope to yield

[064-051]fruit in abundance that will not fall away or slide from those paths of

[064-052]good breeding that your worship has made in my parched understanding.”

[064-053]Don Quixote laughed at Sancho’s affected phraseology, and perceived

[064-054]that what he said about his improvement was true, for now and then he

[064-055]spoke in a way that surprised him; though always, or mostly, when

[064-056]Sancho tried to talk fine and attempted polite language, he wound up by

[064-057]toppling over from the summit of his simplicity into the abyss of his

[064-058]ignorance; and where he showed his culture and his memory to the

[064-059]greatest advantage was in dragging in proverbs, no matter whether they

[064-060]had any bearing or not upon the subject in hand, as may have been seen

[064-061]already and will be noticed in the course of this history.

[064-062]In conversation of this kind they passed a good part of the night, but

[064-063]Sancho felt a desire to let down the curtains of his eyes, as he used

[064-064]to say when he wanted to go to sleep; and stripping Dapple he left him

[064-065]at liberty to graze his fill. He did not remove Rocinante’s saddle, as

[064-066]his master’s express orders were, that so long as they were in the

[064-067]field or not sleeping under a roof Rocinante was not to be stripped—the

[064-068]ancient usage established and observed by knights-errant being to take

[064-069]off the bridle and hang it on the saddle-bow, but to remove the saddle

[064-070]from the horse—never! Sancho acted accordingly, and gave him the same

[064-071]liberty he had given Dapple, between whom and Rocinante there was a

[064-072]friendship so unequalled and so strong, that it is handed down by

[064-073]tradition from father to son, that the author of this veracious history

[064-074]devoted some special chapters to it, which, in order to preserve the

[064-075]propriety and decorum due to a history so heroic, he did not insert

[064-076]therein; although at times he forgets this resolution of his and

[064-077]describes how eagerly the two beasts would scratch one another when

[064-078]they were together and how, when they were tired or full, Rocinante

[064-079]would lay his neck across Dapple’s, stretching half a yard or more on

[064-080]the other side, and the pair would stand thus, gazing thoughtfully on

[064-081]the ground, for three days, or at least so long as they were left

[064-082]alone, or hunger did not drive them to go and look for food. I may add

[064-083]that they say the author left it on record that he likened their

[064-084]friendship to that of Nisus and Euryalus, and Pylades and Orestes; and

[064-085]if that be so, it may be perceived, to the admiration of mankind, how

[064-086]firm the friendship must have been between these two peaceful animals,

[064-087]shaming men, who preserve friendships with one another so badly. This

[064-088]was why it was said-

[064-089]For friend no longer is there friend;

[064-090]The reeds turn lances now.

[064-091]And someone else has sung—

[064-092]Friend to friend the bug, etc.

[064-093]and let no one fancy that the author was at all astray when he compared

[064-094]the friendship of these animals to that of men; for men have received

[064-095]many lessons from beasts, and learned many important things, as, for

[064-096]example, the clyster from the stork, vomit and gratitude from the dog,

[064-097]watchfulness from the crane, foresight from the ant, modesty from the

[064-098]elephant, and loyalty from the horse.

[064-099]Sancho at last fell asleep at the foot of a cork tree, while Don

[064-100]Quixote dozed at that of a sturdy oak; but a short time only had

[064-101]elapsed when a noise he heard behind him awoke him, and rising up

[064-102]startled, he listened and looked in the direction the noise came from,

[064-103]and perceived two men on horseback, one of whom, letting himself drop

[064-104]from the saddle, said to the other, “Dismount, my friend, and take the

[064-105]bridles off the horses, for, so far as I can see, this place will

[064-106]furnish grass for them, and the solitude and silence my love-sick

[064-107]thoughts need of.” As he said this he stretched himself upon the

[064-108]ground, and as he flung himself down, the armour in which he was clad

[064-109]rattled, whereby Don Quixote perceived that he must be a knight-errant;

[064-110]and going over to Sancho, who was asleep, he shook him by the arm and

[064-111]with no small difficulty brought him back to his senses, and said in a

[064-112]low voice to him, “Brother Sancho, we have got an adventure.”

[064-113]“God send us a good one,” said Sancho; “and where may her ladyship the

[064-114]adventure be?”

[064-115]“Where, Sancho?” replied Don Quixote; “turn thine eyes and look, and

[064-116]thou wilt see stretched there a knight-errant, who, it strikes me, is

[064-117]not over and above happy, for I saw him fling himself off his horse and

[064-118]throw himself on the ground with a certain air of dejection, and his

[064-119]armour rattled as he fell.”

[064-120]“Well,” said Sancho, “how does your worship make out that to be an

[064-121]adventure?”

[064-122]“I do not mean to say,” returned Don Quixote, “that it is a complete

[064-123]adventure, but that it is the beginning of one, for it is in this way

[064-124]adventures begin. But listen, for it seems he is tuning a lute or

[064-125]guitar, and from the way he is spitting and clearing his chest he must

[064-126]be getting ready to sing something.”

[064-127]“Faith, you are right,” said Sancho, “and no doubt he is some enamoured

[064-128]knight.”

[064-129]“There is no knight-errant that is not,” said Don Quixote; “but let us

[064-130]listen to him, for, if he sings, by that thread we shall extract the

[064-131]ball of his thoughts; because out of the abundance of the heart the

[064-132]mouth speaketh.”

[064-133]Sancho was about to reply to his master, but the Knight of the Grove’s

[064-134]voice, which was neither very bad nor very good, stopped him, and

[064-135]listening attentively the pair heard him sing this

SONNET

[064-136]Your pleasure, prithee, lady mine, unfold;

[064-137]Declare the terms that I am to obey;

[064-138]My will to yours submissively I mould,

[064-139]And from your law my feet shall never stray.

[064-140]Would you I die, to silent grief a prey?

[064-141]Then count me even now as dead and cold;

[064-142]Would you I tell my woes in some new way?

[064-143]Then shall my tale by Love itself be told.

[064-144]The unison of opposites to prove,

[064-145]Of the soft wax and diamond hard am I;

[064-146]But still, obedient to the laws of love,

[064-147]Here, hard or soft, I offer you my breast,

[064-148]Whate’er you grave or stamp thereon shall rest

[064-149]Indelible for all eternity.

[064-150]With an “Ah me!” that seemed to be drawn from the inmost recesses of

[064-151]his heart, the Knight of the Grove brought his lay to an end, and

[064-152]shortly afterwards exclaimed in a melancholy and piteous voice, “O

[064-153]fairest and most ungrateful woman on earth! What! can it be, most

[064-154]serene Casildea de Vandalia, that thou wilt suffer this thy captive

[064-155]knight to waste away and perish in ceaseless wanderings and rude and

[064-156]arduous toils? It is not enough that I have compelled all the knights

[064-157]of Navarre, all the Leonese, all the Tartesians, all the Castilians,

[064-158]and finally all the knights of La Mancha, to confess thee the most

[064-159]beautiful in the world?”

[064-160]“Not so,” said Don Quixote at this, “for I am of La Mancha, and I have

[064-161]never confessed anything of the sort, nor could I nor should I confess

[064-162]a thing so much to the prejudice of my lady’s beauty; thou seest how

[064-163]this knight is raving, Sancho. But let us listen, perhaps he will tell

[064-164]us more about himself.”

[064-165]“That he will,” returned Sancho, “for he seems in a mood to bewail

[064-166]himself for a month at a stretch.”

[064-167]But this was not the case, for the Knight of the Grove, hearing voices

[064-168]near him, instead of continuing his lamentation, stood up and exclaimed

[064-169]in a distinct but courteous tone, “Who goes there? What are you? Do you

[064-170]belong to the number of the happy or of the miserable?”

[064-171]“Of the miserable,” answered Don Quixote.

[064-172]“Then come to me,” said he of the Grove, “and rest assured that it is

[064-173]to woe itself and affliction itself you come.”

[064-174]Don Quixote, finding himself answered in such a soft and courteous

[064-175]manner, went over to him, and so did Sancho.

[064-176]The doleful knight took Don Quixote by the arm, saying, “Sit down here,

[064-177]sir knight; for, that you are one, and of those that profess

[064-178]knight-errantry, it is to me a sufficient proof to have found you in

[064-179]this place, where solitude and night, the natural couch and proper

[064-180]retreat of knights-errant, keep you company.” To which Don made answer,

[064-181]“A knight I am of the profession you mention, and though sorrows,

[064-182]misfortunes, and calamities have made my heart their abode, the

[064-183]compassion I feel for the misfortunes of others has not been thereby

[064-184]banished from it. From what you have just now sung I gather that yours

[064-185]spring from love, I mean from the love you bear that fair ingrate you

[064-186]named in your lament.”

[064-187]In the meantime, they had seated themselves together on the hard ground

[064-188]peaceably and sociably, just as if, as soon as day broke, they were not

[064-189]going to break one another’s heads.

[064-190]“Are you, sir knight, in love perchance?” asked he of the Grove of Don

[064-191]Quixote.

[064-192]“By mischance I am,” replied Don Quixote; “though the ills arising from

[064-193]well-bestowed affections should be esteemed favours rather than

[064-194]misfortunes.”

[064-195]“That is true,” returned he of the Grove, “if scorn did not unsettle

[064-196]our reason and understanding, for if it be excessive it looks like

[064-197]revenge.”

[064-198]“I was never scorned by my lady,” said Don Quixote.

[064-199]“Certainly not,” said Sancho, who stood close by, “for my lady is as a

[064-200]lamb, and softer than a roll of butter.”

[064-201]“Is this your squire?” asked he of the Grove.

[064-202]“He is,” said Don Quixote.

[064-203]“I never yet saw a squire,” said he of the Grove, “who ventured to

[064-204]speak when his master was speaking; at least, there is mine, who is as

[064-205]big as his father, and it cannot be proved that he has ever opened his

[064-206]lips when I am speaking.”

[064-207]“By my faith then,” said Sancho, “I have spoken, and am fit to speak,

[064-208]in the presence of one as much, or even—but never mind—it only makes it

[064-209]worse to stir it.”

[064-210]The squire of the Grove took Sancho by the arm, saying to him, “Let us

[064-211]two go where we can talk in squire style as much as we please, and

[064-212]leave these gentlemen our masters to fight it out over the story of

[064-213]their loves; and, depend upon it, daybreak will find them at it without

[064-214]having made an end of it.”

[064-215]“So be it by all means,” said Sancho; “and I will tell your worship who

[064-216]I am, that you may see whether I am to be reckoned among the number of

[064-217]the most talkative squires.”

[064-218]With this the two squires withdrew to one side, and between them there

[064-219]passed a conversation as droll as that which passed between their

[064-220]masters was serious.
