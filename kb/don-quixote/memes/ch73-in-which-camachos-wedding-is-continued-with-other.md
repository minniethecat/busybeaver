[073-001]IN WHICH CAMACHO’S WEDDING IS CONTINUED, WITH OTHER DELIGHTFUL

INCIDENTS

p21a.jpg (118K)


[073-002]While Don Quixote and Sancho were engaged in the discussion set forth

[073-003]the last chapter, they heard loud shouts and a great noise, which were

[073-004]uttered and made by the men on the mares as they went at full gallop,

[073-005]shouting, to receive the bride and bridegroom, who were approaching

[073-006]with musical instruments and pageantry of all sorts around them, and

[073-007]accompanied by the priest and the relatives of both, and all the most

[073-008]distinguished people of the surrounding villages. When Sancho saw the

[073-009]bride, he exclaimed, “By my faith, she is not dressed like a country

[073-010]girl, but like some fine court lady; egad, as well as I can make out,

[073-011]the patena she wears rich coral, and her green Cuenca stuff is

[073-012]thirty-pile velvet; and then the white linen trimming—by my oath, but

[073-013]it’s satin! Look at her hands—jet rings on them! May I never have luck

[073-014]if they’re not gold rings, and real gold, and set with pearls as white

[073-015]as a curdled milk, and every one of them worth an eye of one’s head!

[073-016]Whoreson baggage, what hair she has! if it’s not a wig, I never saw

[073-017]longer or fairer all the days of my life. See how bravely she bears

[073-018]herself—and her shape! Wouldn’t you say she was like a walking palm

[073-019]tree loaded with clusters of dates? for the trinkets she has hanging

[073-020]from her hair and neck look just like them. I swear in my heart she is

[073-021]a brave lass, and fit ‘to pass over the banks of Flanders.’”

[073-022]Don Quixote laughed at Sancho’s boorish eulogies and thought that,

[073-023]saving his lady Dulcinea del Toboso, he had never seen a more beautiful

[073-024]woman. The fair Quiteria appeared somewhat pale, which was, no doubt,

[073-025]because of the bad night brides always pass dressing themselves out for

[073-026]their wedding on the morrow. They advanced towards a theatre that stood

[073-027]on one side of the meadow decked with carpets and boughs, where they

[073-028]were to plight their troth, and from which they were to behold the

[073-029]dances and plays; but at the moment of their arrival at the spot they

[073-030]heard a loud outcry behind them, and a voice exclaiming, “Wait a

[073-031]little, ye, as inconsiderate as ye are hasty!” At these words all

[073-032]turned round, and perceived that the speaker was a man clad in what

[073-033]seemed to be a loose black coat garnished with crimson patches like

[073-034]flames. He was crowned (as was presently seen) with a crown of gloomy

[073-035]cypress, and in his hand he held a long staff. As he approached he was

[073-036]recognised by everyone as the gay Basilio, and all waited anxiously to

[073-037]see what would come of his words, in dread of some catastrophe in

[073-038]consequence of his appearance at such a moment. He came up at last

[073-039]weary and breathless, and planting himself in front of the bridal pair,

[073-040]drove his staff, which had a steel spike at the end, into the ground,

[073-041]and, with a pale face and eyes fixed on Quiteria, he thus addressed her

[073-042]in a hoarse, trembling voice:

[073-043]“Well dost thou know, ungrateful Quiteria, that according to the holy

[073-044]law we acknowledge, so long as live thou canst take no husband; nor art

[073-045]thou ignorant either that, in my hopes that time and my own exertions

[073-046]would improve my fortunes, I have never failed to observe the respect

[073-047]due to thy honour; but thou, casting behind thee all thou owest to my

[073-048]true love, wouldst surrender what is mine to another whose wealth

[073-049]serves to bring him not only good fortune but supreme happiness; and

[073-050]now to complete it (not that I think he deserves it, but inasmuch as

[073-051]heaven is pleased to bestow it upon him), I will, with my own hands, do

[073-052]away with the obstacle that may interfere with it, and remove myself

[073-053]from between you. Long live the rich Camacho! many a happy year may he

[073-054]live with the ungrateful Quiteria! and let the poor Basilio die,

[073-055]Basilio whose poverty clipped the wings of his happiness, and brought

[073-056]him to the grave!”

[073-057]And so saying, he seized the staff he had driven into the ground, and

[073-058]leaving one half of it fixed there, showed it to be a sheath that

[073-059]concealed a tolerably long rapier; and, what may be called its hilt

[073-060]being planted in the ground, he swiftly, coolly, and deliberately threw

[073-061]himself upon it, and in an instant the bloody point and half the steel

[073-062]blade appeared at his back, the unhappy man falling to the earth bathed

[073-063]in his blood, and transfixed by his own weapon.

[073-064]His friends at once ran to his aid, filled with grief at his misery and

[073-065]sad fate, and Don Quixote, dismounting from Rocinante, hastened to

[073-066]support him, and took him in his arms, and found he had not yet ceased

[073-067]to breathe. They were about to draw out the rapier, but the priest who

[073-068]was standing by objected to its being withdrawn before he had confessed

[073-069]him, as the instant of its withdrawal would be that of this death.

[073-070]Basilio, however, reviving slightly, said in a weak voice, as though in

[073-071]pain, “If thou wouldst consent, cruel Quiteria, to give me thy hand as

[073-072]my bride in this last fatal moment, I might still hope that my rashness

[073-073]would find pardon, as by its means I attained the bliss of being

[073-074]thine.”

[073-075]Hearing this the priest bade him think of the welfare of his soul

[073-076]rather than of the cravings of the body, and in all earnestness implore

[073-077]God’s pardon for his sins and for his rash resolve; to which Basilio

[073-078]replied that he was determined not to confess unless Quiteria first

[073-079]gave him her hand in marriage, for that happiness would compose his

[073-080]mind and give him courage to make his confession.

[073-081]Don Quixote hearing the wounded man’s entreaty, exclaimed aloud that

[073-082]what Basilio asked was just and reasonable, and moreover a request that

[073-083]might be easily complied with; and that it would be as much to Señor

[073-084]Camacho’s honour to receive the lady Quiteria as the widow of the brave

[073-085]Basilio as if he received her direct from her father.

[073-086]“In this case,” said he, “it will be only to say ‘yes,’ and no

[073-087]consequences can follow the utterance of the word, for the nuptial

[073-088]couch of this marriage must be the grave.”

[073-089]Camacho was listening to all this, perplexed and bewildered and not

[073-090]knowing what to say or do; but so urgent were the entreaties of

[073-091]Basilio’s friends, imploring him to allow Quiteria to give him her

[073-092]hand, so that his soul, quitting this life in despair, should not be

[073-093]lost, that they moved, nay, forced him, to say that if Quiteria were

[073-094]willing to give it he was satisfied, as it was only putting off the

[073-095]fulfillment of his wishes for a moment. At once all assailed Quiteria

[073-096]and pressed her, some with prayers, and others with tears, and others

[073-097]with persuasive arguments, to give her hand to poor Basilio; but she,

[073-098]harder than marble and more unmoved than any statue, seemed unable or

[073-099]unwilling to utter a word, nor would she have given any reply had not

[073-100]the priest bade her decide quickly what she meant to do, as Basilio now

[073-101]had his soul at his teeth, and there was no time for hesitation.

[073-102]On this the fair Quiteria, to all appearance distressed, grieved, and

[073-103]repentant, advanced without a word to where Basilio lay, his eyes

[073-104]already turned in his head, his breathing short and painful, murmuring

[073-105]the name of Quiteria between his teeth, and apparently about to die

[073-106]like a heathen and not like a Christian. Quiteria approached him, and

[073-107]kneeling, demanded his hand by signs without speaking. Basilio opened

[073-108]his eyes and gazing fixedly at her, said, “O Quiteria, why hast thou

[073-109]turned compassionate at a moment when thy compassion will serve as a

[073-110]dagger to rob me of life, for I have not now the strength left either

[073-111]to bear the happiness thou givest me in accepting me as thine, or to

[073-112]suppress the pain that is rapidly drawing the dread shadow of death

[073-113]over my eyes? What I entreat of thee, O thou fatal star to me, is that

[073-114]the hand thou demandest of me and wouldst give me, be not given out of

[073-115]complaisance or to deceive me afresh, but that thou confess and declare

[073-116]that without any constraint upon thy will thou givest it to me as to

[073-117]thy lawful husband; for it is not meet that thou shouldst trifle with

[073-118]me at such a moment as this, or have recourse to falsehoods with one

[073-119]who has dealt so truly by thee.”

[073-120]While uttering these words he showed such weakness that the bystanders

[073-121]expected each return of faintness would take his life with it. Then

[073-122]Quiteria, overcome with modesty and shame, holding in her right hand

[073-123]the hand of Basilio, said, “No force would bend my will; as freely,

[073-124]therefore, as it is possible for me to do so, I give thee the hand of a

[073-125]lawful wife, and take thine if thou givest it to me of thine own free

[073-126]will, untroubled and unaffected by the calamity thy hasty act has

[073-127]brought upon thee.”

[073-128]“Yes, I give it,” said Basilio, “not agitated or distracted, but with

[073-129]unclouded reason that heaven is pleased to grant me, thus do I give

[073-130]myself to be thy husband.”

[073-131]“And I give myself to be thy wife,” said Quiteria, “whether thou livest

[073-132]many years, or they carry thee from my arms to the grave.”

[073-133]“For one so badly wounded,” observed Sancho at this point, “this young

[073-134]man has a great deal to say; they should make him leave off billing and

[073-135]cooing, and attend to his soul; for to my thinking he has it more on

[073-136]his tongue than at his teeth.”

[073-137]Basilio and Quiteria having thus joined hands, the priest, deeply moved

[073-138]and with tears in his eyes, pronounced the blessing upon them, and

[073-139]implored heaven to grant an easy passage to the soul of the newly

[073-140]wedded man, who, the instant he received the blessing, started nimbly

[073-141]to his feet and with unparalleled effrontery pulled out the rapier that

[073-142]had been sheathed in his body. All the bystanders were astounded, and

[073-143]some, more simple than inquiring, began shouting, “A miracle, a

[073-144]miracle!” But Basilio replied, “No miracle, no miracle; only a trick, a

[073-145]trick!” The priest, perplexed and amazed, made haste to examine the

[073-146]wound with both hands, and found that the blade had passed, not through

[073-147]Basilio’s flesh and ribs, but through a hollow iron tube full of blood,

[073-148]which he had adroitly fixed at the place, the blood, as was afterwards

[073-149]ascertained, having been so prepared as not to congeal. In short, the

[073-150]priest and Camacho and most of those present saw they were tricked and

[073-151]made fools of. The bride showed no signs of displeasure at the

[073-152]deception; on the contrary, hearing them say that the marriage, being

[073-153]fraudulent, would not be valid, she said that she confirmed it afresh,

[073-154]whence they all concluded that the affair had been planned by agreement

[073-155]and understanding between the pair, whereat Camacho and his supporters

[073-156]were so mortified that they proceeded to revenge themselves by

[073-157]violence, and a great number of them drawing their swords attacked

[073-158]Basilio, in whose protection as many more swords were in an instant

[073-159]unsheathed, while Don Quixote taking the lead on horseback, with his

[073-160]lance over his arm and well covered with his shield, made all give way

[073-161]before him. Sancho, who never found any pleasure or enjoyment in such

[073-162]doings, retreated to the wine-jars from which he had taken his

[073-163]delectable skimmings, considering that, as a holy place, that spot

[073-164]would be respected.

[073-165]“Hold, sirs, hold!” cried Don Quixote in a loud voice; “we have no

[073-166]right to take vengeance for wrongs that love may do to us: remember

[073-167]love and war are the same thing, and as in war it is allowable and

[073-168]common to make use of wiles and stratagems to overcome the enemy, so in

[073-169]the contests and rivalries of love the tricks and devices employed to

[073-170]attain the desired end are justifiable, provided they be not to the

[073-171]discredit or dishonour of the loved object. Quiteria belonged to

[073-172]Basilio and Basilio to Quiteria by the just and beneficent disposal of

[073-173]heaven. Camacho is rich, and can purchase his pleasure when, where, and

[073-174]as it pleases him. Basilio has but this ewe-lamb, and no one, however

[073-175]powerful he may be, shall take her from him; these two whom God hath

[073-176]joined man cannot separate; and he who attempts it must first pass the

[073-177]point of this lance;” and so saying he brandished it so stoutly and

[073-178]dexterously that he overawed all who did not know him.

[073-179]But so deep an impression had the rejection of Quiteria made on

[073-180]Camacho’s mind that it banished her at once from his thoughts; and so

[073-181]the counsels of the priest, who was a wise and kindly disposed man,

[073-182]prevailed with him, and by their means he and his partisans were

[073-183]pacified and tranquillised, and to prove it put up their swords again,

[073-184]inveighing against the pliancy of Quiteria rather than the craftiness

[073-185]of Basilio; Camacho maintaining that, if Quiteria as a maiden had such

[073-186]a love for Basilio, she would have loved him too as a married woman,

[073-187]and that he ought to thank heaven more for having taken her than for

[073-188]having given her.

[073-189]Camacho and those of his following, therefore, being consoled and

[073-190]pacified, those on Basilio’s side were appeased; and the rich Camacho,

[073-191]to show that he felt no resentment for the trick, and did not care

[073-192]about it, desired the festival to go on just as if he were married in

[073-193]reality. Neither Basilio, however, nor his bride, nor their followers

[073-194]would take any part in it, and they withdrew to Basilio’s village; for

[073-195]the poor, if they are persons of virtue and good sense, have those who

[073-196]follow, honour, and uphold them, just as the rich have those who

[073-197]flatter and dance attendance on them. With them they carried Don

[073-198]Quixote, regarding him as a man of worth and a stout one. Sancho alone

[073-199]had a cloud on his soul, for he found himself debarred from waiting for

[073-200]Camacho’s splendid feast and festival, which lasted until night; and

[073-201]thus dragged away, he moodily followed his master, who accompanied

[073-202]Basilio’s party, and left behind him the flesh-pots of Egypt; though in

[073-203]his heart he took them with him, and their now nearly finished

[073-204]skimmings that he carried in the bucket conjured up visions before his

[073-205]eyes of the glory and abundance of the good cheer he was losing. And

[073-206]so, vexed and dejected though not hungry, without dismounting from

[073-207]Dapple he followed in the footsteps of Rocinante.
