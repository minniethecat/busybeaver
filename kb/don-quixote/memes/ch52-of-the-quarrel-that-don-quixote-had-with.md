[052-003]The goatherd’s tale gave great satisfaction to all the hearers, and the

[052-004]canon especially enjoyed it, for he had remarked with particular

[052-005]attention the manner in which it had been told, which was as unlike the

[052-006]manner of a clownish goatherd as it was like that of a polished city

[052-007]wit; and he observed that the curate had been quite right in saying

[052-008]that the woods bred men of learning. They all offered their services to

[052-009]Eugenio but he who showed himself most liberal in this way was Don

[052-010]Quixote, who said to him, “Most assuredly, brother goatherd, if I found

[052-011]myself in a position to attempt any adventure, I would, this very

[052-012]instant, set out on your behalf, and would rescue Leandra from that

[052-013]convent (where no doubt she is kept against her will), in spite of the

[052-014]abbess and all who might try to prevent me, and would place her in your

[052-015]hands to deal with her according to your will and pleasure, observing,

[052-016]however, the laws of chivalry which lay down that no violence of any

[052-017]kind is to be offered to any damsel. But I trust in God our Lord that

[052-018]the might of one malignant enchanter may not prove so great but that

[052-019]the power of another better disposed may prove superior to it, and then

[052-020]I promise you my support and assistance, as I am bound to do by my

[052-021]profession, which is none other than to give aid to the weak and

[052-022]needy.”

[052-023]The goatherd eyed him, and noticing Don Quixote’s sorry appearance and

[052-024]looks, he was filled with wonder, and asked the barber, who was next

[052-025]him, “Señor, who is this man who makes such a figure and talks in such

[052-026]a strain?”

[052-027]“Who should it be,” said the barber, “but the famous Don Quixote of La

[052-028]Mancha, the undoer of injustice, the righter of wrongs, the protector

[052-029]of damsels, the terror of giants, and the winner of battles?”

[052-030]“That,” said the goatherd, “sounds like what one reads in the books of

[052-031]the knights-errant, who did all that you say this man does; though it

[052-032]is my belief that either you are joking, or else this gentleman has

[052-033]empty lodgings in his head.”

[052-034]“You are a great scoundrel,” said Don Quixote, “and it is you who are

[052-035]empty and a fool. I am fuller than ever was the whoreson bitch that

[052-036]bore you;” and passing from words to deeds, he caught up a loaf that

[052-037]was near him and sent it full in the goatherd’s face, with such force

[052-038]that he flattened his nose; but the goatherd, who did not understand

[052-039]jokes, and found himself roughly handled in such good earnest, paying

[052-040]no respect to carpet, tablecloth, or diners, sprang upon Don Quixote,

[052-041]and seizing him by the throat with both hands would no doubt have

[052-042]throttled him, had not Sancho Panza that instant come to the rescue,

[052-043]and grasping him by the shoulders flung him down on the table, smashing

[052-044]plates, breaking glasses, and upsetting and scattering everything on

[052-045]it. Don Quixote, finding himself free, strove to get on top of the

[052-046]goatherd, who, with his face covered with blood, and soundly kicked by

[052-047]Sancho, was on all fours feeling about for one of the table-knives to

[052-048]take a bloody revenge with. The canon and the curate, however,

[052-049]prevented him, but the barber so contrived it that he got Don Quixote

[052-050]under him, and rained down upon him such a shower of fisticuffs that

[052-051]the poor knight’s face streamed with blood as freely as his own. The

[052-052]canon and the curate were bursting with laughter, the officers were

[052-053]capering with delight, and both the one and the other hissed them on as

[052-054]they do dogs that are worrying one another in a fight. Sancho alone was

[052-055]frantic, for he could not free himself from the grasp of one of the

[052-056]canon’s servants, who kept him from going to his master’s assistance.

[052-057]At last, while they were all, with the exception of the two bruisers

[052-058]who were mauling each other, in high glee and enjoyment, they heard a

[052-059]trumpet sound a note so doleful that it made them all look in the

[052-060]direction whence the sound seemed to come. But the one that was most

[052-061]excited by hearing it was Don Quixote, who though sorely against his

[052-062]will he was under the goatherd, and something more than pretty well

[052-063]pummelled, said to him, “Brother devil (for it is impossible but that

[052-064]thou must be one since thou hast had might and strength enough to

[052-065]overcome mine), I ask thee to agree to a truce for but one hour for the

[052-066]solemn note of yonder trumpet that falls on our ears seems to me to

[052-067]summon me to some new adventure.” The goatherd, who was by this time

[052-068]tired of pummelling and being pummelled, released him at once, and Don

[052-069]Quixote rising to his feet and turning his eyes to the quarter where

[052-070]the sound had been heard, suddenly saw coming down the slope of a hill

[052-071]several men clad in white like penitents.

[052-072]The fact was that the clouds had that year withheld their moisture from

[052-073]the earth, and in all the villages of the district they were organising

[052-074]processions, rogations, and penances, imploring God to open the hands

[052-075]of his mercy and send the rain; and to this end the people of a village

[052-076]that was hard by were going in procession to a holy hermitage there was

[052-077]on one side of that valley. Don Quixote when he saw the strange garb of

[052-078]the penitents, without reflecting how often he had seen it before, took

[052-079]it into his head that this was a case of adventure, and that it fell to

[052-080]him alone as a knight-errant to engage in it; and he was all the more

[052-081]confirmed in this notion, by the idea that an image draped in black

[052-082]they had with them was some illustrious lady that these villains and

[052-083]discourteous thieves were carrying off by force. As soon as this

[052-084]occurred to him he ran with all speed to Rocinante who was grazing at

[052-085]large, and taking the bridle and the buckler from the saddle-bow, he

[052-086]had him bridled in an instant, and calling to Sancho for his sword he

[052-087]mounted Rocinante, braced his buckler on his arm, and in a loud voice

[052-088]exclaimed to those who stood by, “Now, noble company, ye shall see how

[052-089]important it is that there should be knights in the world professing

[052-090]the order of knight-errantry; now, I say, ye shall see, by the

[052-091]deliverance of that worthy lady who is borne captive there, whether

[052-092]knights-errant deserve to be held in estimation,” and so saying he

[052-093]brought his legs to bear on Rocinante—for he had no spurs—and at a full

[052-094]canter (for in all this veracious history we never read of Rocinante

[052-095]fairly galloping) set off to encounter the penitents, though the

[052-096]curate, the canon, and the barber ran to prevent him. But it was out of

[052-097]their power, nor did he even stop for the shouts of Sancho calling

[052-098]after him, “Where are you going, Señor Don Quixote? What devils have

[052-099]possessed you to set you on against our Catholic faith? Plague take me!

[052-100]mind, that is a procession of penitents, and the lady they are carrying

[052-101]on that stand there is the blessed image of the immaculate Virgin. Take

[052-102]care what you are doing, señor, for this time it may be safely said you

[052-103]don’t know what you are about.” Sancho laboured in vain, for his master

[052-104]was so bent on coming to quarters with these sheeted figures and

[052-105]releasing the lady in black that he did not hear a word; and even had

[052-106]he heard, he would not have turned back if the king had ordered him. He

[052-107]came up with the procession and reined in Rocinante, who was already

[052-108]anxious enough to slacken speed a little, and in a hoarse, excited

[052-109]voice he exclaimed, “You who hide your faces, perhaps because you are

[052-110]not good subjects, pay attention and listen to what I am about to say

[052-111]to you.” The first to halt were those who were carrying the image, and

[052-112]one of the four ecclesiastics who were chanting the Litany, struck by

[052-113]the strange figure of Don Quixote, the leanness of Rocinante, and the

[052-114]other ludicrous peculiarities he observed, said in reply to him,

[052-115]“Brother, if you have anything to say to us say it quickly, for these

[052-116]brethren are whipping themselves, and we cannot stop, nor is it

[052-117]reasonable we should stop to hear anything, unless indeed it is short

[052-118]enough to be said in two words.”

[052-119]“I will say it in one,” replied Don Quixote, “and it is this; that at

[052-120]once, this very instant, ye release that fair lady whose tears and sad

[052-121]aspect show plainly that ye are carrying her off against her will, and

[052-122]that ye have committed some scandalous outrage against her; and I, who

[052-123]was born into the world to redress all such like wrongs, will not

[052-124]permit you to advance another step until you have restored to her the

[052-125]liberty she pines for and deserves.”

[052-126]From these words all the hearers concluded that he must be a madman,

[052-127]and began to laugh heartily, and their laughter acted like gunpowder on

[052-128]Don Quixote’s fury, for drawing his sword without another word he made

[052-129]a rush at the stand. One of those who supported it, leaving the burden

[052-130]to his comrades, advanced to meet him, flourishing a forked stick that

[052-131]he had for propping up the stand when resting, and with this he caught

[052-132]a mighty cut Don Quixote made at him that severed it in two; but with

[052-133]the portion that remained in his hand he dealt such a thwack on the

[052-134]shoulder of Don Quixote’s sword arm (which the buckler could not

[052-135]protect against the clownish assault) that poor Don Quixote came to the

[052-136]ground in a sad plight.

[052-137]Sancho Panza, who was coming on close behind puffing and blowing,

[052-138]seeing him fall, cried out to his assailant not to strike him again,

[052-139]for he was a poor enchanted knight, who had never harmed anyone all the

[052-140]days of his life; but what checked the clown was, not Sancho’s

[052-141]shouting, but seeing that Don Quixote did not stir hand or foot; and

[052-142]so, fancying he had killed him, he hastily hitched up his tunic under

[052-143]his girdle and took to his heels across the country like a deer.

[052-144]By this time all Don Quixote’s companions had come up to where he lay;

[052-145]but the processionists seeing them come running, and with them the

[052-146]officers of the Brotherhood with their crossbows, apprehended mischief,

[052-147]and clustering round the image, raised their hoods, and grasped their

[052-148]scourges, as the priests did their tapers, and awaited the attack,

[052-149]resolved to defend themselves and even to take the offensive against

[052-150]their assailants if they could. Fortune, however, arranged the matter

[052-151]better than they expected, for all Sancho did was to fling himself on

[052-152]his master’s body, raising over him the most doleful and laughable

[052-153]lamentation that ever was heard, for he believed he was dead. The

[052-154]curate was known to another curate who walked in the procession, and

[052-155]their recognition of one another set at rest the apprehensions of both

[052-156]parties; the first then told the other in two words who Don Quixote

[052-157]was, and he and the whole troop of penitents went to see if the poor

[052-158]gentleman was dead, and heard Sancho Panza saying, with tears in his

[052-159]eyes, “Oh flower of chivalry, that with one blow of a stick hast ended

[052-163]misdeeds! Oh thou, generous above all the Alexanders, since for only

[052-164]eight months of service thou hast given me the best island the sea

[052-165]girds or surrounds! Humble with the proud, haughty with the humble,

[052-166]encounterer of dangers, endurer of outrages, enamoured without reason,

[052-167]imitator of the good, scourge of the wicked, enemy of the mean, in

[052-168]short, knight-errant, which is all that can be said!”

[052-183]simplicities, they placed Don Quixote in the cart as before. The

[052-184]procession once more formed itself in order and proceeded on its road;

[052-185]the goatherd took his leave of the party; the officers of the

[052-186]Brotherhood declined to go any farther, and the curate paid them what

[052-193]his oxen and made Don Quixote comfortable on a truss of hay, and at his

[052-195]of six days they reached Don Quixote’s village, and entered it about

[052-196]the middle of the day, which it so happened was a Sunday, and the

[052-197]people were all in the plaza, through which Don Quixote’s cart passed.

[052-198]They all flocked to see what was in the cart, and when they recognised

[052-199]their townsman they were filled with amazement, and a boy ran off to

[052-200]bring the news to his housekeeper and his niece that their master and

[052-201]uncle had come back all lean and yellow and stretched on a truss of hay

[052-202]on an ox-cart. It was piteous to hear the cries the two good ladies

[052-203]raised, how they beat their breasts and poured out fresh maledictions

[052-204]on those accursed books of chivalry; all which was renewed when they

[052-205]saw Don Quixote coming in at the gate.

[052-206]At the news of Don Quixote’s arrival Sancho Panza’s wife came running,

[052-207]for she by this time knew that her husband had gone away with him as

[052-208]his squire, and on seeing Sancho, the first thing she asked him was if

[052-209]the ass was well. Sancho replied that he was, better than his master

[052-210]was.

[052-211]“Thanks be to God,” said she, “for being so good to me; but now tell

[052-212]me, my friend, what have you made by your squirings? What gown have you

[052-213]brought me back? What shoes for your children?”

[052-214]“I bring nothing of that sort, wife,” said Sancho; “though I bring

[052-215]other things of more consequence and value.”

[052-216]“I am very glad of that,” returned his wife; “show me these things of

[052-217]more value and consequence, my friend; for I want to see them to cheer

[052-218]my heart that has been so sad and heavy all these ages that you have

[052-219]been away.”

[052-220]“I will show them to you at home, wife,” said Sancho; “be content for

[052-221]the present; for if it please God that we should again go on our

[052-222]travels in search of adventures, you will soon see me a count, or

[052-223]governor of an island, and that not one of those everyday ones, but the

[052-224]best that is to be had.”

[052-225]“Heaven grant it, husband,” said she, “for indeed we have need of it.

[052-226]But tell me, what’s this about islands, for I don’t understand it?”

[052-227]“Honey is not for the mouth of the ass,” returned Sancho; “all in good

[052-228]time thou shalt see, wife—nay, thou wilt be surprised to hear thyself

[052-229]called ‘your ladyship’ by all thy vassals.”

[052-230]“What are you talking about, Sancho, with your ladyships, islands, and

[052-231]vassals?” returned Teresa Panza—for so Sancho’s wife was called, though

[052-232]they were not relations, for in La Mancha it is customary for wives to

[052-235]is enough that I am telling you the truth, so shut your mouth. But I

[052-236]may tell you this much by the way, that there is nothing in the world

[052-237]more delightful than to be a person of consideration, squire to a

[052-238]knight-errant, and a seeker of adventures. To be sure most of those one

[052-239]finds do not end as pleasantly as one could wish, for out of a hundred,

[052-240]ninety-nine will turn out cross and contrary. I know it by experience,

[052-242]for all that, it is a fine thing to be on the look-out for what may

[052-243]happen, crossing mountains, searching woods, climbing rocks, visiting

[052-244]castles, putting up at inns, all at free quarters, and devil take the

[052-246]While this conversation passed between Sancho Panza and his wife, Don

[052-247]Quixote’s housekeeper and niece took him in and undressed him and laid

[052-248]him in his old bed. He eyed them askance, and could not make out where

[052-249]he was. The curate charged his niece to be very careful to make her

[052-250]uncle comfortable and to keep a watch over him lest he should make his

[052-252]bring him home. On this the pair once more lifted up their voices and

[052-257]found himself somewhat better, and as they feared so it fell out.

[052-387]some service to Your Excellency, as from many parts I am urged to send

[052-388]him off, so as to dispel the loathing and disgust caused by another Don

[052-389]Quixote who, under the name of Second Part, has run masquerading

[052-390]through the whole world. And he who has shown the greatest longing for

[052-391]him has been the great Emperor of China, who wrote me a letter in

[052-392]Chinese a month ago and sent it by a special courier. He asked me, or

[052-393]to be truthful, he begged me to send him Don Quixote, for he intended

[052-394]to found a college where the Spanish tongue would be taught, and it was

[052-395]his wish that the book to be read should be the History of Don Quixote.

[052-396]He also added that I should go and be the rector of this college. I

[052-397]asked the bearer if His Majesty had afforded a sum in aid of my travel

[052-398]expenses. He answered, “No, not even in thought.”

[052-399]“Then, brother,” I replied, “you can return to your China, post haste

[052-400]or at whatever haste you are bound to go, as I am not fit for so long a

[052-401]travel and, besides being ill, I am very much without money, while

[052-402]Emperor for Emperor and Monarch for Monarch, I have at Naples the great

[052-403]Count of Lemos, who, without so many petty titles of colleges and

[052-404]rectorships, sustains me, protects me and does me more favour than I

[052-420]God bless me, gentle (or it may be plebeian) reader, how eagerly must

[052-421]thou be looking forward to this preface, expecting to find there

[052-422]retaliation, scolding, and abuse against the author of the second Don

[052-423]Quixote—I mean him who was, they say, begotten at Tordesillas and born

[052-424]at Tarragona! Well then, the truth is, I am not going to give thee that

[052-425]satisfaction; for, though injuries stir up anger in humbler breasts, in

[052-426]mine the rule must admit of an exception. Thou wouldst have me call him

[052-427]ass, fool, and malapert, but I have no such intention; let his offence

[052-428]be his punishment, with his bread let him eat it, and there’s an end of

[052-429]it. What I cannot help taking amiss is that he charges me with being

[052-430]old and one-handed, as if it had been in my power to keep time from

[052-431]passing over me, or as if the loss of my hand had been brought about in

[052-432]some tavern, and not on the grandest occasion the past or present has

[052-433]seen, or the future can hope to see. If my wounds have no beauty to the

[052-434]beholder’s eye, they are, at least, honourable in the estimation of

[052-435]those who know where they were received; for the soldier shows to

[052-436]greater advantage dead in battle than alive in flight; and so strongly

[052-437]is this my feeling, that if now it were proposed to perform an

[052-438]impossibility for me, I would rather have had my share in that mighty

[052-439]action, than be free from my wounds this minute without having been

[052-440]present at it. Those the soldier shows on his face and breast are stars

[052-441]that direct others to the heaven of honour and ambition of merited

[052-442]praise; and moreover it is to be observed that it is not with grey

[052-443]hairs that one writes, but with the understanding, and that commonly

[052-444]improves with years. I take it amiss, too, that he calls me envious,

[052-445]and explains to me, as if I were ignorant, what envy is; for really and

[052-446]truly, of the two kinds there are, I only know that which is holy,

[052-447]noble, and high-minded; and if that be so, as it is, I am not likely to

[052-448]attack a priest, above all if, in addition, he holds the rank of

[052-449]familiar of the Holy Office. And if he said what he did on account of

[052-450]him on whose behalf it seems he spoke, he is entirely mistaken; for I

[052-451]worship the genius of that person, and admire his works and his

[052-452]unceasing and strenuous industry. After all, I am grateful to this

[052-453]gentleman, the author, for saying that my novels are more satirical

[052-454]than exemplary, but that they are good; for they could not be that

[052-455]unless there was a little of everything in them.

[052-456]I suspect thou wilt say that I am taking a very humble line, and

[052-457]keeping myself too much within the bounds of my moderation, from a

[052-458]feeling that additional suffering should not be inflicted upon a

[052-459]sufferer, and that what this gentleman has to endure must doubtless be

[052-460]very great, as he does not dare to come out into the open field and

[052-461]broad daylight, but hides his name and disguises his country as if he

[052-462]had been guilty of some lese majesty. If perchance thou shouldst come

[052-463]to know him, tell him from me that I do not hold myself aggrieved; for

[052-464]I know well what the temptations of the devil are, and that one of the

[052-465]greatest is putting it into a man’s head that he can write and print a

[052-466]book by which he will get as much fame as money, and as much money as

[052-467]fame; and to prove it I will beg of you, in your own sprightly,

[052-468]pleasant way, to tell him this story.

[052-469]There was a madman in Seville who took to one of the drollest

[052-470]absurdities and vagaries that ever madman in the world gave way to. It

[052-471]was this: he made a tube of reed sharp at one end, and catching a dog

[052-472]in the street, or wherever it might be, he with his foot held one of

[052-473]its legs fast, and with his hand lifted up the other, and as best he

[052-474]could fixed the tube where, by blowing, he made the dog as round as a

[052-475]ball; then holding it in this position, he gave it a couple of slaps on

[052-476]the belly, and let it go, saying to the bystanders (and there were

[052-477]always plenty of them): “Do your worships think, now, that it is an

[052-478]easy thing to blow up a dog?”—Does your worship think now, that it is

[052-479]an easy thing to write a book?

[052-480]And if this story does not suit him, you may, dear reader, tell him

[052-481]this one, which is likewise of a madman and a dog.

[052-482]In Cordova there was another madman, whose way it was to carry a piece

[052-483]of marble slab or a stone, not of the lightest, on his head, and when

[052-484]he came upon any unwary dog he used to draw close to him and let the

[052-485]weight fall right on top of him; on which the dog in a rage, barking

[052-486]and howling, would run three streets without stopping. It so happened,

[052-487]however, that one of the dogs he discharged his load upon was a

[052-488]cap-maker’s dog, of which his master was very fond. The stone came down

[052-489]hitting it on the head, the dog raised a yell at the blow, the master

[052-490]saw the affair and was wroth, and snatching up a measuring-yard rushed

[052-491]out at the madman and did not leave a sound bone in his body, and at

[052-492]every stroke he gave him he said, “You dog, you thief! my lurcher!

[052-493]Don’t you see, you brute, that my dog is a lurcher?” and so, repeating

[052-494]the word “lurcher” again and again, he sent the madman away beaten to a

[052-495]jelly. The madman took the lesson to heart, and vanished, and for more

[052-496]than a month never once showed himself in public; but after that he

[052-497]came out again with his old trick and a heavier load than ever. He came

[052-498]up to where there was a dog, and examining it very carefully without

[052-499]venturing to let the stone fall, he said: “This is a lurcher; ware!” In

[052-500]short, all the dogs he came across, be they mastiffs or terriers, he

[052-501]said were lurchers; and he discharged no more stones. Maybe it will be
