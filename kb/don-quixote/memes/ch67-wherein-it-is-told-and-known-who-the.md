WHEREIN IT IS TOLD AND KNOWN WHO THE KNIGHT OF THE MIRRORS AND HIS

SQUIRE WERE

p15a.jpg (122K)


[067-001]Don Quixote went off satisfied, elated, and vain-glorious in the

[067-002]highest degree at having won a victory over such a valiant knight as he

[067-003]fancied him of the Mirrors to be, and one from whose knightly word he

[067-004]expected to learn whether the enchantment of his lady still continued;

[067-005]inasmuch as the said vanquished knight was bound, under the penalty of

[067-006]ceasing to be one, to return and render him an account of what took

[067-007]place between him and her. But Don Quixote was of one mind, he of the

[067-008]Mirrors of another, for he just then had no thought of anything but

[067-009]finding some village where he could plaster himself, as has been said

[067-010]already. The history goes on to say, then, that when the bachelor

[067-011]Samson Carrasco recommended Don Quixote to resume his knight-errantry

[067-012]which he had laid aside, it was in consequence of having been

[067-013]previously in conclave with the curate and the barber on the means to

[067-014]be adopted to induce Don Quixote to stay at home in peace and quiet

[067-015]without worrying himself with his ill-starred adventures; at which

[067-016]consultation it was decided by the unanimous vote of all, and on the

[067-017]special advice of Carrasco, that Don Quixote should be allowed to go,

[067-018]as it seemed impossible to restrain him, and that Samson should sally

[067-019]forth to meet him as a knight-errant, and do battle with him, for there

[067-020]would be no difficulty about a cause, and vanquish him, that being

[067-021]looked upon as an easy matter; and that it should be agreed and settled

[067-022]that the vanquished was to be at the mercy of the victor. Then, Don

[067-023]Quixote being vanquished, the bachelor knight was to command him to

[067-024]return to his village and his house, and not quit it for two years, or

[067-025]until he received further orders from him; all which it was clear Don

[067-026]Quixote would unhesitatingly obey, rather than contravene or fail to

[067-027]observe the laws of chivalry; and during the period of his seclusion he

[067-028]might perhaps forget his folly, or there might be an opportunity of

[067-029]discovering some ready remedy for his madness. Carrasco undertook the

[067-030]task, and Tom Cecial, a gossip and neighbour of Sancho Panza’s, a

[067-031]lively, feather-headed fellow, offered himself as his squire. Carrasco

[067-032]armed himself in the fashion described, and Tom Cecial, that he might

[067-033]not be known by his gossip when they met, fitted on over his own

[067-034]natural nose the false masquerade one that has been mentioned; and so

[067-035]they followed the same route Don Quixote took, and almost came up with

[067-036]him in time to be present at the adventure of the cart of Death and

[067-037]finally encountered them in the grove, where all that the sagacious

[067-038]reader has been reading about took place; and had it not been for the

[067-039]extraordinary fancies of Don Quixote, and his conviction that the

[067-040]bachelor was not the bachelor, señor bachelor would have been

[067-041]incapacitated for ever from taking his degree of licentiate, all

[067-042]through not finding nests where he thought to find birds.

[067-043]Tom Cecial, seeing how ill they had succeeded, and what a sorry end

[067-044]their expedition had come to, said to the bachelor, “Sure enough, Señor

[067-045]Samson Carrasco, we are served right; it is easy enough to plan and set

[067-046]about an enterprise, but it is often a difficult matter to come well

[067-047]out of it. Don Quixote a madman, and we sane; he goes off laughing,

[067-048]safe, and sound, and you are left sore and sorry! I’d like to know now

[067-049]which is the madder, he who is so because he cannot help it, or he who

[067-050]is so of his own choice?”

[067-051]To which Samson replied, “The difference between the two sorts of

[067-052]madmen is, that he who is so will he nil he, will be one always, while

[067-053]he who is so of his own accord can leave off being one whenever he

[067-054]likes.”

[067-055]“In that case,” said Tom Cecial, “I was a madman of my own accord when

[067-056]I volunteered to become your squire, and, of my own accord, I’ll leave

[067-057]off being one and go home.”

[067-058]“That’s your affair,” returned Samson, “but to suppose that I am going

[067-059]home until I have given Don Quixote a thrashing is absurd; and it is

[067-060]not any wish that he may recover his senses that will make me hunt him

[067-061]out now, but a wish for the sore pain I am in with my ribs won’t let me

[067-062]entertain more charitable thoughts.”

[067-063]Thus discoursing, the pair proceeded until they reached a town where it

[067-064]was their good luck to find a bone-setter, with whose help the

[067-065]unfortunate Samson was cured. Tom Cecial left him and went home, while

[067-066]he stayed behind meditating vengeance; and the history will return to

[067-067]him again at the proper time, so as not to omit making merry with Don

[067-068]Quixote now.
