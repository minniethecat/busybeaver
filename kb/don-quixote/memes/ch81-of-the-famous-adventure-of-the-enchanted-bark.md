OF THE FAMOUS ADVENTURE OF THE ENCHANTED BARK

p29a.jpg (127K)


[081-001]By stages as already described or left undescribed, two days after

[081-002]quitting the grove Don Quixote and Sancho reached the river Ebro, and

[081-003]the sight of it was a great delight to Don Quixote as he contemplated

[081-004]and gazed upon the charms of its banks, the clearness of its stream,

[081-005]the gentleness of its current and the abundance of its crystal waters;

[081-006]and the pleasant view revived a thousand tender thoughts in his mind.

[081-007]Above all, he dwelt upon what he had seen in the cave of Montesinos;

[081-008]for though Master Pedro’s ape had told him that of those things part

[081-009]was true, part false, he clung more to their truth than to their

[081-010]falsehood, the very reverse of Sancho, who held them all to be

[081-011]downright lies.

[081-012]As they were thus proceeding, then, they discovered a small boat,

[081-013]without oars or any other gear, that lay at the water’s edge tied to

[081-014]the stem of a tree growing on the bank. Don Quixote looked all round,

[081-015]and seeing nobody, at once, without more ado, dismounted from Rocinante

[081-016]and bade Sancho get down from Dapple and tie both beasts securely to

[081-017]the trunk of a poplar or willow that stood there. Sancho asked him the

[081-018]reason of this sudden dismounting and tying. Don Quixote made answer,

[081-019]“Thou must know, Sancho, that this bark is plainly, and without the

[081-020]possibility of any alternative, calling and inviting me to enter it,

[081-021]and in it go to give aid to some knight or other person of distinction

[081-022]in need of it, who is no doubt in some sore strait; for this is the way

[081-023]of the books of chivalry and of the enchanters who figure and speak in

[081-024]them. When a knight is involved in some difficulty from which he cannot

[081-025]be delivered save by the hand of another knight, though they may be at

[081-026]a distance of two or three thousand leagues or more one from the other,

[081-027]they either take him up on a cloud, or they provide a bark for him to

[081-028]get into, and in less than the twinkling of an eye they carry him where

[081-029]they will and where his help is required; and so, Sancho, this bark is

[081-030]placed here for the same purpose; this is as true as that it is now

[081-031]day, and ere this one passes tie Dapple and Rocinante together, and

[081-032]then in God’s hand be it to guide us; for I would not hold back from

[081-033]embarking, though barefooted friars were to beg me.”

[081-034]“As that’s the case,” said Sancho, “and your worship chooses to give in

[081-035]to these—I don’t know if I may call them absurdities—at every turn,

[081-036]there’s nothing for it but to obey and bow the head, bearing in mind

[081-037]the proverb, ‘Do as thy master bids thee, and sit down to table with

[081-038]him;’ but for all that, for the sake of easing my conscience, I warn

[081-039]your worship that it is my opinion this bark is no enchanted one, but

[081-040]belongs to some of the fishermen of the river, for they catch the best

[081-041]shad in the world here.”

[081-042]As Sancho said this, he tied the beasts, leaving them to the care and

[081-043]protection of the enchanters with sorrow enough in his heart. Don

[081-044]Quixote bade him not be uneasy about deserting the animals, “for he who

[081-045]would carry themselves over such longinquous roads and regions would

[081-046]take care to feed them.”

[081-047]“I don’t understand that logiquous,” said Sancho, “nor have I ever

[081-048]heard the word all the days of my life.”

[081-049]“Longinquous,” replied Don Quixote, “means far off; but it is no wonder

[081-050]thou dost not understand it, for thou art not bound to know Latin, like

[081-051]some who pretend to know it and don’t.”

[081-052]“Now they are tied,” said Sancho; “what are we to do next?”

[081-053]“What?” said Don Quixote, “cross ourselves and weigh anchor; I mean,

[081-054]embark and cut the moorings by which the bark is held;” and the bark

[081-055]began to drift away slowly from the bank. But when Sancho saw himself

[081-056]somewhere about two yards out in the river, he began to tremble and

[081-057]give himself up for lost; but nothing distressed him more than hearing

[081-058]Dapple bray and seeing Rocinante struggling to get loose, and said he

[081-059]to his master, “Dapple is braying in grief at our leaving him, and

[081-060]Rocinante is trying to escape and plunge in after us. O dear friends,

[081-061]peace be with you, and may this madness that is taking us away from

[081-062]you, turned into sober sense, bring us back to you.” And with this he

[081-063]fell weeping so bitterly, that Don Quixote said to him, sharply and

[081-064]angrily, “What art thou afraid of, cowardly creature? What art thou

[081-065]weeping at, heart of butter-paste? Who pursues or molests thee, thou

[081-066]soul of a tame mouse? What dost thou want, unsatisfied in the very

[081-067]heart of abundance? Art thou, perchance, tramping barefoot over the

[081-068]Riphaean mountains, instead of being seated on a bench like an archduke

[081-069]on the tranquil stream of this pleasant river, from which in a short

[081-070]space we shall come out upon the broad sea? But we must have already

[081-071]emerged and gone seven hundred or eight hundred leagues; and if I had

[081-072]here an astrolabe to take the altitude of the pole, I could tell thee

[081-073]how many we have travelled, though either I know little, or we have

[081-074]already crossed or shall shortly cross the equinoctial line which parts

[081-075]the two opposite poles midway.”

[081-076]“And when we come to that line your worship speaks of,” said Sancho,

[081-077]“how far shall we have gone?”

[081-078]“Very far,” said Don Quixote, “for of the three hundred and sixty

[081-079]degrees that this terraqueous globe contains, as computed by Ptolemy,

[081-080]the greatest cosmographer known, we shall have travelled one-half when

[081-081]we come to the line I spoke of.”

[081-082]“By God,” said Sancho, “your worship gives me a nice authority for what

[081-083]you say, putrid Dolly something transmogrified, or whatever it is.”

[081-084]Don Quixote laughed at the interpretation Sancho put upon “computed,”

[081-085]and the name of the cosmographer Ptolemy, and said he, “Thou must know,

[081-086]Sancho, that with the Spaniards and those who embark at Cadiz for the

[081-087]East Indies, one of the signs they have to show them when they have

[081-088]passed the equinoctial line I told thee of, is, that the lice die upon

[081-089]everybody on board the ship, and not a single one is left, or to be

[081-090]found in the whole vessel if they gave its weight in gold for it; so,

[081-091]Sancho, thou mayest as well pass thy hand down thy thigh, and if thou

[081-092]comest upon anything alive we shall be no longer in doubt; if not, then

[081-093]we have crossed.”

[081-094]“I don’t believe a bit of it,” said Sancho; “still, I’ll do as your

[081-095]worship bids me; though I don’t know what need there is for trying

[081-096]these experiments, for I can see with my own eyes that we have not

[081-097]moved five yards away from the bank, or shifted two yards from where

[081-098]the animals stand, for there are Rocinante and Dapple in the very same

[081-099]place where we left them; and watching a point, as I do now, I swear by

[081-100]all that’s good, we are not stirring or moving at the pace of an ant.”

[081-101]“Try the test I told thee of, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and don’t

[081-102]mind any other, for thou knowest nothing about colures, lines,

[081-103]parallels, zodiacs, ecliptics, poles, solstices, equinoxes, planets,

[081-104]signs, bearings, the measures of which the celestial and terrestrial

[081-105]spheres are composed; if thou wert acquainted with all these things, or

[081-106]any portion of them, thou wouldst see clearly how many parallels we

[081-107]have cut, what signs we have seen, and what constellations we have left

[081-108]behind and are now leaving behind. But again I tell thee, feel and

[081-109]hunt, for I am certain thou art cleaner than a sheet of smooth white

[081-110]paper.”

[081-111]Sancho felt, and passing his hand gently and carefully down to the

[081-112]hollow of his left knee, he looked up at his master and said, “Either

[081-113]the test is a false one, or we have not come to where your worship

[081-114]says, nor within many leagues of it.”

[081-115]“Why, how so?” asked Don Quixote; “hast thou come upon aught?”

[081-116]“Ay, and aughts,” replied Sancho; and shaking his fingers he washed his

[081-117]whole hand in the river along which the boat was quietly gliding in

[081-118]midstream, not moved by any occult intelligence or invisible enchanter,

[081-119]but simply by the current, just there smooth and gentle.

[081-120]They now came in sight of some large water mills that stood in the

[081-121]middle of the river, and the instant Don Quixote saw them he cried out,

[081-122]“Seest thou there, my friend? there stands the castle or fortress,

[081-123]where there is, no doubt, some knight in durance, or ill-used queen, or

[081-124]infanta, or princess, in whose aid I am brought hither.”

[081-125]“What the devil city, fortress, or castle is your worship talking

[081-126]about, señor?” said Sancho; “don’t you see that those are mills that

[081-127]stand in the river to grind corn?”

[081-128]“Hold thy peace, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “though they look like

[081-129]mills they are not so; I have already told thee that enchantments

[081-130]transform things and change their proper shapes; I do not mean to say

[081-131]they really change them from one form into another, but that it seems

[081-132]as though they did, as experience proved in the transformation of

[081-133]Dulcinea, sole refuge of my hopes.”

[081-134]By this time, the boat, having reached the middle of the stream, began

[081-135]to move less slowly than hitherto. The millers belonging to the mills,

[081-136]when they saw the boat coming down the river, and on the point of being

[081-137]sucked in by the draught of the wheels, ran out in haste, several of

[081-138]them, with long poles to stop it, and being all mealy, with faces and

[081-139]garments covered with flour, they presented a sinister appearance. They

[081-140]raised loud shouts, crying, “Devils of men, where are you going to? Are

[081-141]you mad? Do you want to drown yourselves, or dash yourselves to pieces

[081-142]among these wheels?”

[081-143]“Did I not tell thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote at this, “that we had

[081-144]reached the place where I am to show what the might of my arm can do?

[081-145]See what ruffians and villains come out against me; see what monsters

[081-146]oppose me; see what hideous countenances come to frighten us! You shall

[081-147]soon see, scoundrels!” And then standing up in the boat he began in a

[081-148]loud voice to hurl threats at the millers, exclaiming, “Ill-conditioned

[081-149]and worse-counselled rabble, restore to liberty and freedom the person

[081-150]ye hold in durance in this your fortress or prison, high or low or of

[081-151]whatever rank or quality he be, for I am Don Quixote of La Mancha,

[081-152]otherwise called the Knight of the Lions, for whom, by the disposition

[081-153]of heaven above, it is reserved to give a happy issue to this

[081-154]adventure;” and so saying he drew his sword and began making passes in

[081-155]the air at the millers, who, hearing but not understanding all this

[081-156]nonsense, strove to stop the boat, which was now getting into the

[081-157]rushing channel of the wheels. Sancho fell upon his knees devoutly

[081-158]appealing to heaven to deliver him from such imminent peril; which it

[081-159]did by the activity and quickness of the millers, who, pushing against

[081-160]the boat with their poles, stopped it, not, however, without upsetting

[081-161]and throwing Don Quixote and Sancho into the water; and lucky it was

[081-162]for Don Quixote that he could swim like a goose, though the weight of

[081-163]his armour carried him twice to the bottom; and had it not been for the

[081-164]millers, who plunged in and hoisted them both out, it would have been

[081-165]Troy town with the pair of them. As soon as, more drenched than

[081-166]thirsty, they were landed, Sancho went down on his knees and with

[081-167]clasped hands and eyes raised to heaven, prayed a long and fervent

[081-168]prayer to God to deliver him evermore from the rash projects and

[081-169]attempts of his master. The fishermen, the owners of the boat, which

[081-170]the mill-wheels had knocked to pieces, now came up, and seeing it

[081-171]smashed they proceeded to strip Sancho and to demand payment for it

[081-172]from Don Quixote; but he with great calmness, just as if nothing had

[081-173]happened him, told the millers and fishermen that he would pay for the

[081-174]bark most cheerfully, on condition that they delivered up to him, free

[081-175]and unhurt, the person or persons that were in durance in that castle

[081-176]of theirs.

[081-177]“What persons or what castle art thou talking of, madman? Art thou for

[081-178]carrying off the people who come to grind corn in these mills?”

[081-179]“That’s enough,” said Don Quixote to himself, “it would be preaching in

[081-180]the desert to attempt by entreaties to induce this rabble to do any

[081-181]virtuous action. In this adventure two mighty enchanters must have

[081-182]encountered one another, and one frustrates what the other attempts;

[081-183]one provided the bark for me, and the other upset me; God help us, this

[081-184]world is all machinations and schemes at cross purposes one with the

[081-185]other. I can do no more.” And then turning towards the mills he said

[081-186]aloud, “Friends, whoe’er ye be that are immured in that prison, forgive

[081-187]me that, to my misfortune and yours, I cannot deliver you from your

[081-188]misery; this adventure is doubtless reserved and destined for some

[081-189]other knight.”

[081-190]So saying he settled with the fishermen, and paid fifty reals for the

[081-191]boat, which Sancho handed to them very much against the grain, saying,

[081-192]“With a couple more bark businesses like this we shall have sunk our

[081-193]whole capital.”

[081-194]The fishermen and the millers stood staring in amazement at the two

[081-195]figures, so very different to all appearance from ordinary men, and

[081-196]were wholly unable to make out the drift of the observations and

[081-197]questions Don Quixote addressed to them; and coming to the conclusion

[081-198]that they were madmen, they left them and betook themselves, the

[081-199]millers to their mills, and the fishermen to their huts. Don Quixote

[081-200]and Sancho returned to their beasts, and to their life of beasts, and

[081-201]so ended the adventure of the enchanted bark.
