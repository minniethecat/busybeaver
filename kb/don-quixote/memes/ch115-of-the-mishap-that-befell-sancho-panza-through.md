OF THE MISHAP THAT BEFELL SANCHO PANZA THROUGH THE VISIT TO THE

[115-001]GALLEYS, AND THE STRANGE ADVENTURE OF THE FAIR MORISCO

p63a.jpg (151K)


[115-002]Profound were Don Quixote’s reflections on the reply of the enchanted

[115-003]head, not one of them, however, hitting on the secret of the trick, but

[115-004]all concentrated on the promise, which he regarded as a certainty, of

[115-005]Dulcinea’s disenchantment. This he turned over in his mind again and

[115-006]again with great satisfaction, fully persuaded that he would shortly

[115-007]see its fulfillment; and as for Sancho, though, as has been said, he

[115-008]hated being a governor, still he had a longing to be giving orders and

[115-009]finding himself obeyed once more; this is the misfortune that being in

[115-010]authority, even in jest, brings with it.

[115-011]To resume; that afternoon their host Don Antonio Moreno and his two

[115-012]friends, with Don Quixote and Sancho, went to the galleys. The

[115-013]commandant had been already made aware of his good fortune in seeing

[115-014]two such famous persons as Don Quixote and Sancho, and the instant they

[115-015]came to the shore all the galleys struck their awnings and the clarions

[115-016]rang out. A skiff covered with rich carpets and cushions of crimson

[115-017]velvet was immediately lowered into the water, and as Don Quixote

[115-018]stepped on board of it, the leading galley fired her gangway gun, and

[115-019]the other galleys did the same; and as he mounted the starboard ladder

[115-020]the whole crew saluted him (as is the custom when a personage of

[115-021]distinction comes on board a galley) by exclaiming “Hu, hu, hu,” three

[115-022]times. The general, for so we shall call him, a Valencian gentleman of

[115-023]rank, gave him his hand and embraced him, saying, “I shall mark this

[115-024]day with a white stone as one of the happiest I can expect to enjoy in

[115-025]my lifetime, since I have seen Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha, pattern

[115-026]and image wherein we see contained and condensed all that is worthy in

[115-027]knight-errantry.”

[115-028]Don Quixote delighted beyond measure with such a lordly reception,

[115-029]replied to him in words no less courteous. All then proceeded to the

[115-030]poop, which was very handsomely decorated, and seated themselves on the

[115-031]bulwark benches; the boatswain passed along the gangway and piped all

[115-032]hands to strip, which they did in an instant. Sancho, seeing such a

[115-033]number of men stripped to the skin, was taken aback, and still more

[115-034]when he saw them spread the awning so briskly that it seemed to him as

[115-035]if all the devils were at work at it; but all this was cakes and fancy

[115-036]bread to what I am going to tell now. Sancho was seated on the

[115-037]captain’s stage, close to the aftermost rower on the right-hand side.

[115-038]He, previously instructed in what he was to do, laid hold of Sancho,

[115-039]hoisting him up in his arms, and the whole crew, who were standing

[115-040]ready, beginning on the right, proceeded to pass him on, whirling him

[115-041]along from hand to hand and from bench to bench with such rapidity that

[115-042]it took the sight out of poor Sancho’s eyes, and he made quite sure

[115-043]that the devils themselves were flying away with him; nor did they

[115-044]leave off with him until they had sent him back along the left side and

[115-045]deposited him on the poop; and the poor fellow was left bruised and

[115-046]breathless and all in a sweat, and unable to comprehend what it was

[115-047]that had happened to him.

[115-048]Don Quixote when he saw Sancho’s flight without wings asked the general

[115-049]if this was a usual ceremony with those who came on board the galleys

[115-050]for the first time; for, if so, as he had no intention of adopting them

[115-051]as a profession, he had no mind to perform such feats of agility, and

[115-052]if anyone offered to lay hold of him to whirl him about, he vowed to

[115-053]God he would kick his soul out; and as he said this he stood up and

[115-054]clapped his hand upon his sword. At this instant they struck the awning

[115-055]and lowered the yard with a prodigious rattle. Sancho thought heaven

[115-056]was coming off its hinges and going to fall on his head, and full of

[115-057]terror he ducked it and buried it between his knees; nor were Don

[115-058]Quixote’s knees altogether under control, for he too shook a little,

[115-059]squeezed his shoulders together and lost colour. The crew then hoisted

[115-060]the yard with the same rapidity and clatter as when they lowered it,

[115-061]all the while keeping silence as though they had neither voice nor

[115-062]breath. The boatswain gave the signal to weigh anchor, and leaping upon

[115-063]the middle of the gangway began to lay on to the shoulders of the crew

[115-064]with his courbash or whip, and to haul out gradually to sea.

[115-065]When Sancho saw so many red feet (for such he took the oars to be)

[115-066]moving all together, he said to himself, “It’s these that are the real

[115-067]chanted things, and not the ones my master talks of. What can those

[115-068]wretches have done to be so whipped; and how does that one man who goes

[115-069]along there whistling dare to whip so many? I declare this is hell, or

[115-070]at least purgatory!”

[115-071]Don Quixote, observing how attentively Sancho regarded what was going

[115-072]on, said to him, “Ah, Sancho my friend, how quickly and cheaply might

[115-073]you finish off the disenchantment of Dulcinea, if you would strip to

[115-074]the waist and take your place among those gentlemen! Amid the pain and

[115-075]sufferings of so many you would not feel your own much; and moreover

[115-076]perhaps the sage Merlin would allow each of these lashes, being laid on

[115-077]with a good hand, to count for ten of those which you must give

[115-078]yourself at last.”

[115-079]The general was about to ask what these lashes were, and what was

[115-080]Dulcinea’s disenchantment, when a sailor exclaimed, “Monjui signals

[115-081]that there is an oared vessel off the coast to the west.”

[115-082]On hearing this the general sprang upon the gangway crying, “Now then,

[115-083]my sons, don’t let her give us the slip! It must be some Algerine

[115-084]corsair brigantine that the watchtower signals to us.” The three others

[115-085]immediately came alongside the chief galley to receive their orders.

[115-086]The general ordered two to put out to sea while he with the other kept

[115-087]in shore, so that in this way the vessel could not escape them. The

[115-088]crews plied the oars driving the galleys so furiously that they seemed

[115-089]to fly. The two that had put out to sea, after a couple of miles

[115-090]sighted a vessel which, so far as they could make out, they judged to

[115-091]be one of fourteen or fifteen banks, and so she proved. As soon as the

[115-092]vessel discovered the galleys she went about with the object and in the

[115-093]hope of making her escape by her speed; but the attempt failed, for the

[115-094]chief galley was one of the fastest vessels afloat, and overhauled her

[115-095]so rapidly that they on board the brigantine saw clearly there was no

[115-096]possibility of escaping, and the rais therefore would have had them

[115-097]drop their oars and give themselves up so as not to provoke the captain

[115-098]in command of our galleys to anger. But chance, directing things

[115-099]otherwise, so ordered it that just as the chief galley came close

[115-100]enough for those on board the vessel to hear the shouts from her

[115-101]calling on them to surrender, two Toraquis, that is to say two Turks,

[115-102]both drunken, that with a dozen more were on board the brigantine,

[115-103]discharged their muskets, killing two of the soldiers that lined the

[115-104]sides of our vessel. Seeing this the general swore he would not leave

[115-105]one of those he found on board the vessel alive, but as he bore down

[115-106]furiously upon her she slipped away from him underneath the oars. The

[115-107]galley shot a good way ahead; those on board the vessel saw their case

[115-108]was desperate, and while the galley was coming about they made sail,

[115-109]and by sailing and rowing once more tried to sheer off; but their

[115-110]activity did not do them as much good as their rashness did them harm,

[115-111]for the galley coming up with them in a little more than half a mile

[115-112]threw her oars over them and took the whole of them alive. The other

[115-113]two galleys now joined company and all four returned with the prize to

[115-114]the beach, where a vast multitude stood waiting for them, eager to see

[115-115]what they brought back. The general anchored close in, and perceived

[115-116]that the viceroy of the city was on the shore. He ordered the skiff to

[115-117]push off to fetch him, and the yard to be lowered for the purpose of

[115-118]hanging forthwith the rais and the rest of the men taken on board the

[115-119]vessel, about six-and-thirty in number, all smart fellows and most of

[115-120]them Turkish musketeers. He asked which was the rais of the brigantine,

[115-121]and was answered in Spanish by one of the prisoners (who afterwards

[115-122]proved to be a Spanish renegade), “This young man, señor, that you see

[115-123]here is our rais,” and he pointed to one of the handsomest and most

[115-124]gallant-looking youths that could be imagined. He did not seem to be

[115-125]twenty years of age.

[115-126]“Tell me, dog,” said the general, “what led thee to kill my soldiers,

[115-127]when thou sawest it was impossible for thee to escape? Is that the way

[115-128]to behave to chief galleys? Knowest thou not that rashness is not

[115-129]valour? Faint prospects of success should make men bold, but not rash.”

[115-130]The rais was about to reply, but the general could not at that moment

[115-131]listen to him, as he had to hasten to receive the viceroy, who was now

[115-132]coming on board the galley, and with him certain of his attendants and

[115-133]some of the people.

[115-134]“You have had a good chase, señor general,” said the viceroy.

[115-135]“Your excellency shall soon see how good, by the game strung up to this

[115-136]yard,” replied the general.

[115-137]“How so?” returned the viceroy.

[115-138]“Because,” said the general, “against all law, reason, and usages of

[115-139]war they have killed on my hands two of the best soldiers on board

[115-140]these galleys, and I have sworn to hang every man that I have taken,

[115-141]but above all this youth who is the rais of the brigantine,” and he

[115-142]pointed to him as he stood with his hands already bound and the rope

[115-143]round his neck, ready for death.

[115-144]The viceroy looked at him, and seeing him so well-favoured, so

[115-145]graceful, and so submissive, he felt a desire to spare his life, the

[115-146]comeliness of the youth furnishing him at once with a letter of

[115-147]recommendation. He therefore questioned him, saying, “Tell me, rais,

[115-148]art thou Turk, Moor, or renegade?”

[115-149]To which the youth replied, also in Spanish, “I am neither Turk, nor

[115-150]Moor, nor renegade.”

[115-151]“What art thou, then?” said the viceroy.

[115-152]“A Christian woman,” replied the youth.

[115-153]“A woman and a Christian, in such a dress and in such circumstances! It

[115-154]is more marvellous than credible,” said the viceroy.

[115-155]“Suspend the execution of the sentence,” said the youth; “your

[115-156]vengeance will not lose much by waiting while I tell you the story of

[115-157]my life.”

[115-158]What heart could be so hard as not to be softened by these words, at

[115-159]any rate so far as to listen to what the unhappy youth had to say? The

[115-160]general bade him say what he pleased, but not to expect pardon for his

[115-161]flagrant offence. With this permission the youth began in these words.

[115-162]“Born of Morisco parents, I am of that nation, more unhappy than wise,

[115-163]upon which of late a sea of woes has poured down. In the course of our

[115-164]misfortune I was carried to Barbary by two uncles of mine, for it was

[115-165]in vain that I declared I was a Christian, as in fact I am, and not a

[115-166]mere pretended one, or outwardly, but a true Catholic Christian. It

[115-167]availed me nothing with those charged with our sad expatriation to

[115-168]protest this, nor would my uncles believe it; on the contrary, they

[115-169]treated it as an untruth and a subterfuge set up to enable me to remain

[115-170]behind in the land of my birth; and so, more by force than of my own

[115-171]will, they took me with them. I had a Christian mother, and a father

[115-172]who was a man of sound sense and a Christian too; I imbibed the

[115-173]Catholic faith with my mother’s milk, I was well brought up, and

[115-174]neither in word nor in deed did I, I think, show any sign of being a

[115-175]Morisco. To accompany these virtues, for such I hold them, my beauty,

[115-176]if I possess any, grew with my growth; and great as was the seclusion

[115-177]in which I lived it was not so great but that a young gentleman, Don

[115-178]Gaspar Gregorio by name, eldest son of a gentleman who is lord of a

[115-179]village near ours, contrived to find opportunities of seeing me. How he

[115-180]saw me, how we met, how his heart was lost to me, and mine not kept

[115-181]from him, would take too long to tell, especially at a moment when I am

[115-182]in dread of the cruel cord that threatens me interposing between tongue

[115-183]and throat; I will only say, therefore, that Don Gregorio chose to

[115-184]accompany me in our banishment. He joined company with the Moriscoes

[115-185]who were going forth from other villages, for he knew their language

[115-186]very well, and on the voyage he struck up a friendship with my two

[115-187]uncles who were carrying me with them; for my father, like a wise and

[115-188]far-sighted man, as soon as he heard the first edict for our expulsion,

[115-189]quitted the village and departed in quest of some refuge for us abroad.

[115-190]He left hidden and buried, at a spot of which I alone have knowledge, a

[115-191]large quantity of pearls and precious stones of great value, together

[115-192]with a sum of money in gold cruzadoes and doubloons. He charged me on

[115-193]no account to touch the treasure, if by any chance they expelled us

[115-194]before his return. I obeyed him, and with my uncles, as I have said,

[115-195]and others of our kindred and neighbours, passed over to Barbary, and

[115-196]the place where we took up our abode was Algiers, much the same as if

[115-197]we had taken it up in hell itself. The king heard of my beauty, and

[115-198]report told him of my wealth, which was in some degree fortunate for

[115-199]me. He summoned me before him, and asked me what part of Spain I came

[115-200]from, and what money and jewels I had. I mentioned the place, and told

[115-201]him the jewels and money were buried there; but that they might easily

[115-202]be recovered if I myself went back for them. All this I told him, in

[115-203]dread lest my beauty and not his own covetousness should influence him.

[115-204]While he was engaged in conversation with me, they brought him word

[115-205]that in company with me was one of the handsomest and most graceful

[115-206]youths that could be imagined. I knew at once that they were speaking

[115-207]of Don Gaspar Gregorio, whose comeliness surpasses the most highly

[115-208]vaunted beauty. I was troubled when I thought of the danger he was in,

[115-209]for among those barbarous Turks a fair youth is more esteemed than a

[115-210]woman, be she ever so beautiful. The king immediately ordered him to be

[115-211]brought before him that he might see him, and asked me if what they

[115-212]said about the youth was true. I then, almost as if inspired by heaven,

[115-213]told him it was, but that I would have him to know it was not a man,

[115-214]but a woman like myself, and I entreated him to allow me to go and

[115-215]dress her in the attire proper to her, so that her beauty might be seen

[115-216]to perfection, and that she might present herself before him with less

[115-217]embarrassment. He bade me go by all means, and said that the next day

[115-218]we should discuss the plan to be adopted for my return to Spain to

[115-219]carry away the hidden treasure. I saw Don Gaspar, I told him the danger

[115-220]he was in if he let it be seen he was a man, I dressed him as a Moorish

[115-221]woman, and that same afternoon I brought him before the king, who was

[115-222]charmed when he saw him, and resolved to keep the damsel and make a

[115-223]present of her to the Grand Signor; and to avoid the risk she might run

[115-224]among the women of his seraglio, and distrustful of himself, he

[115-225]commanded her to be placed in the house of some Moorish ladies of rank

[115-226]who would protect and attend to her; and thither he was taken at once.

[115-227]What we both suffered (for I cannot deny that I love him) may be left

[115-228]to the imagination of those who are separated if they love one another

[115-229]dearly. The king then arranged that I should return to Spain in this

[115-230]brigantine, and that two Turks, those who killed your soldiers, should

[115-231]accompany me. There also came with me this Spanish renegade”—and here

[115-232]she pointed to him who had first spoken—“whom I know to be secretly a

[115-233]Christian, and to be more desirous of being left in Spain than of

[115-234]returning to Barbary. The rest of the crew of the brigantine are Moors

[115-235]and Turks, who merely serve as rowers. The two Turks, greedy and

[115-236]insolent, instead of obeying the orders we had to land me and this

[115-237]renegade in Christian dress (with which we came provided) on the first

[115-238]Spanish ground we came to, chose to run along the coast and make some

[115-239]prize if they could, fearing that if they put us ashore first, we

[115-240]might, in case of some accident befalling us, make it known that the

[115-241]brigantine was at sea, and thus, if there happened to be any galleys on

[115-242]the coast, they might be taken. We sighted this shore last night, and

[115-243]knowing nothing of these galleys, we were discovered, and the result

[115-244]was what you have seen. To sum up, there is Don Gregorio in woman’s

[115-245]dress, among women, in imminent danger of his life; and here am I, with

[115-246]hands bound, in expectation, or rather in dread, of losing my life, of

[115-247]which I am already weary. Here, sirs, ends my sad story, as true as it

[115-248]is unhappy; all I ask of you is to allow me to die like a Christian,

[115-249]for, as I have already said, I am not to be charged with the offence of

[115-250]which those of my nation are guilty;” and she stood silent, her eyes

[115-251]filled with moving tears, accompanied by plenty from the bystanders.

[115-252]The viceroy, touched with compassion, went up to her without speaking

[115-253]and untied the cord that bound the hands of the Moorish girl.

[115-254]But all the while the Morisco Christian was telling her strange story,

[115-255]an elderly pilgrim, who had come on board of the galley at the same

[115-256]time as the viceroy, kept his eyes fixed upon her; and the instant she

[115-257]ceased speaking he threw himself at her feet, and embracing them said

[115-258]in a voice broken by sobs and sighs, “O Ana Felix, my unhappy daughter,

[115-259]I am thy father Ricote, come back to look for thee, unable to live

[115-260]without thee, my soul that thou art!”

[115-261]At these words of his, Sancho opened his eyes and raised his head,

[115-262]which he had been holding down, brooding over his unlucky excursion;

[115-263]and looking at the pilgrim he recognised in him that same Ricote he met

[115-264]the day he quitted his government, and felt satisfied that this was his

[115-265]daughter. She being now unbound embraced her father, mingling her tears

[115-266]with his, while he addressing the general and the viceroy said, “This,

[115-267]sirs, is my daughter, more unhappy in her adventures than in her name.

[115-268]She is Ana Felix, surnamed Ricote, celebrated as much for her own

[115-269]beauty as for my wealth. I quitted my native land in search of some

[115-270]shelter or refuge for us abroad, and having found one in Germany I

[115-271]returned in this pilgrim’s dress, in the company of some other German

[115-272]pilgrims, to seek my daughter and take up a large quantity of treasure

[115-273]I had left buried. My daughter I did not find, the treasure I found and

[115-274]have with me; and now, in this strange roundabout way you have seen, I

[115-275]find the treasure that more than all makes me rich, my beloved

[115-276]daughter. If our innocence and her tears and mine can with strict

[115-277]justice open the door to clemency, extend it to us, for we never had

[115-278]any intention of injuring you, nor do we sympathise with the aims of

[115-279]our people, who have been justly banished.”

[115-280]“I know Ricote well,” said Sancho at this, “and I know too that what he

[115-281]says about Ana Felix being his daughter is true; but as to those other

[115-282]particulars about going and coming, and having good or bad intentions,

[115-283]I say nothing.”

[115-284]While all present stood amazed at this strange occurrence the general

[115-285]said, “At any rate your tears will not allow me to keep my oath; live,

[115-286]fair Ana Felix, all the years that heaven has allotted you; but these

[115-287]rash insolent fellows must pay the penalty of the crime they have

[115-288]committed;” and with that he gave orders to have the two Turks who had

[115-289]killed his two soldiers hanged at once at the yard-arm. The viceroy,

[115-290]however, begged him earnestly not to hang them, as their behaviour

[115-291]savoured rather of madness than of bravado. The general yielded to the

[115-292]viceroy’s request, for revenge is not easily taken in cold blood. They

[115-293]then tried to devise some scheme for rescuing Don Gaspar Gregorio from

[115-294]the danger in which he had been left. Ricote offered for that object

[115-295]more than two thousand ducats that he had in pearls and gems; they

[115-296]proposed several plans, but none so good as that suggested by the

[115-297]renegade already mentioned, who offered to return to Algiers in a small

[115-298]vessel of about six banks, manned by Christian rowers, as he knew

[115-299]where, how, and when he could and should land, nor was he ignorant of

[115-300]the house in which Don Gaspar was staying. The general and the viceroy

[115-301]had some hesitation about placing confidence in the renegade and

[115-302]entrusting him with the Christians who were to row, but Ana Felix said

[115-303]she could answer for him, and her father offered to go and pay the

[115-304]ransom of the Christians if by any chance they should not be

[115-305]forthcoming. This, then, being agreed upon, the viceroy landed, and Don

[115-306]Antonio Moreno took the fair Morisco and her father home with him, the

[115-307]viceroy charging him to give them the best reception and welcome in his

[115-308]power, while on his own part he offered all that house contained for

[115-309]their entertainment; so great was the good-will and kindliness the

[115-310]beauty of Ana Felix had infused into his heart.
