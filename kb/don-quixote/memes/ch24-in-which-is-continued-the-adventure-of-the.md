IN WHICH IS CONTINUED THE ADVENTURE OF THE SIERRA MORENA

c24a.jpg (151K)


[024-001]The history relates that it was with the greatest attention Don Quixote

[024-002]listened to the ragged knight of the Sierra, who began by saying:

[024-003]“Of a surety, señor, whoever you are, for I know you not, I thank you

[024-004]for the proofs of kindness and courtesy you have shown me, and would I

[024-005]were in a condition to requite with something more than good-will that

[024-006]which you have displayed towards me in the cordial reception you have

[024-007]given me; but my fate does not afford me any other means of returning

[024-008]kindnesses done me save the hearty desire to repay them.”

[024-009]“Mine,” replied Don Quixote, “is to be of service to you, so much so

[024-010]that I had resolved not to quit these mountains until I had found you,

[024-011]and learned of you whether there is any kind of relief to be found for

[024-012]that sorrow under which from the strangeness of your life you seem to

[024-013]labour; and to search for you with all possible diligence, if search

[024-014]had been necessary. And if your misfortune should prove to be one of

[024-015]those that refuse admission to any sort of consolation, it was my

[024-016]purpose to join you in lamenting and mourning over it, so far as I

[024-017]could; for it is still some comfort in misfortune to find one who can

[024-018]feel for it. And if my good intentions deserve to be acknowledged with

[024-019]any kind of courtesy, I entreat you, señor, by that which I perceive

[024-020]you possess in so high a degree, and likewise conjure you by whatever

[024-021]you love or have loved best in life, to tell me who you are and the

[024-022]cause that has brought you to live or die in these solitudes like a

[024-023]brute beast, dwelling among them in a manner so foreign to your

[024-024]condition as your garb and appearance show. And I swear,” added Don

[024-025]Quixote, “by the order of knighthood which I have received, and by my

[024-026]vocation of knight-errant, if you gratify me in this, to serve you with

[024-027]all the zeal my calling demands of me, either in relieving your

[024-028]misfortune if it admits of relief, or in joining you in lamenting it as

[024-029]I promised to do.”

[024-030]The Knight of the Thicket, hearing him of the Rueful Countenance talk

[024-031]in this strain, did nothing but stare at him, and stare at him again,

[024-032]and again survey him from head to foot; and when he had thoroughly

[024-033]examined him, he said to him:

[024-034]“If you have anything to give me to eat, for God’s sake give it me, and

[024-035]after I have eaten I will do all you ask in acknowledgment of the

[024-036]goodwill you have displayed towards me.”

[024-037]Sancho from his sack, and the goatherd from his pouch, furnished the

[024-038]Ragged One with the means of appeasing his hunger, and what they gave

[024-039]him he ate like a half-witted being, so hastily that he took no time

[024-040]between mouthfuls, gorging rather than swallowing; and while he ate

[024-041]neither he nor they who observed him uttered a word. As soon as he had

[024-042]done he made signs to them to follow him, which they did, and he led

[024-043]them to a green plot which lay a little farther off round the corner of

[024-044]a rock. On reaching it he stretched himself upon the grass, and the

[024-045]others did the same, all keeping silence, until the Ragged One,

[024-046]settling himself in his place, said:

[024-047]“If it is your wish, sirs, that I should disclose in a few words the

[024-048]surpassing extent of my misfortunes, you must promise not to break the

[024-049]thread of my sad story with any question or other interruption, for the

[024-050]instant you do so the tale I tell will come to an end.”

[024-051]These words of the Ragged One reminded Don Quixote of the tale his

[024-052]squire had told him, when he failed to keep count of the goats that had

[024-053]crossed the river and the story remained unfinished; but to return to

[024-054]the Ragged One, he went on to say:

[024-055]“I give you this warning because I wish to pass briefly over the story

[024-056]of my misfortunes, for recalling them to memory only serves to add

[024-057]fresh ones, and the less you question me the sooner shall I make an end

[024-058]of the recital, though I shall not omit to relate anything of

[024-059]importance in order fully to satisfy your curiosity.”

[024-060]Don Quixote gave the promise for himself and the others, and with this

[024-061]assurance he began as follows:

[024-062]“My name is Cardenio, my birthplace one of the best cities of this

[024-063]Andalusia, my family noble, my parents rich, my misfortune so great

[024-064]that my parents must have wept and my family grieved over it without

[024-065]being able by their wealth to lighten it; for the gifts of fortune can

[024-066]do little to relieve reverses sent by Heaven. In that same country

[024-067]there was a heaven in which love had placed all the glory I could

[024-068]desire; such was the beauty of Luscinda, a damsel as noble and as rich

[024-069]as I, but of happier fortunes, and of less firmness than was due to so

[024-070]worthy a passion as mine. This Luscinda I loved, worshipped, and adored

[024-071]from my earliest and tenderest years, and she loved me in all the

[024-072]innocence and sincerity of childhood. Our parents were aware of our

[024-073]feelings, and were not sorry to perceive them, for they saw clearly

[024-074]that as they ripened they must lead at last to a marriage between us, a

[024-075]thing that seemed almost prearranged by the equality of our families

[024-076]and wealth. We grew up, and with our growth grew the love between us,

[024-077]so that the father of Luscinda felt bound for propriety’s sake to

[024-078]refuse me admission to his house, in this perhaps imitating the parents

[024-079]of that Thisbe so celebrated by the poets, and this refusal but added

[024-080]love to love and flame to flame; for though they enforced silence upon

[024-081]our tongues they could not impose it upon our pens, which can make

[024-082]known the heart’s secrets to a loved one more freely than tongues; for

[024-083]many a time the presence of the object of love shakes the firmest will

[024-084]and strikes dumb the boldest tongue. Ah heavens! how many letters did I

[024-085]write her, and how many dainty modest replies did I receive! how many

[024-086]ditties and love-songs did I compose in which my heart declared and

[024-087]made known its feelings, described its ardent longings, revelled in its

[024-088]recollections and dallied with its desires! At length growing impatient

[024-089]and feeling my heart languishing with longing to see her, I resolved to

[024-090]put into execution and carry out what seemed to me the best mode of

[024-091]winning my desired and merited reward, to ask her of her father for my

[024-092]lawful wife, which I did. To this his answer was that he thanked me for

[024-093]the disposition I showed to do honour to him and to regard myself as

[024-094]honoured by the bestowal of his treasure; but that as my father was

[024-095]alive it was his by right to make this demand, for if it were not in

[024-096]accordance with his full will and pleasure, Luscinda was not to be

[024-097]taken or given by stealth. I thanked him for his kindness, reflecting

[024-098]that there was reason in what he said, and that my father would assent

[024-099]to it as soon as I should tell him, and with that view I went the very

[024-100]same instant to let him know what my desires were. When I entered the

[024-101]room where he was I found him with an open letter in his hand, which,

[024-102]before I could utter a word, he gave me, saying, ‘By this letter thou

[024-103]wilt see, Cardenio, the disposition the Duke Ricardo has to serve

[024-104]thee.’ This Duke Ricardo, as you, sirs, probably know already, is a

[024-105]grandee of Spain who has his seat in the best part of this Andalusia. I

[024-106]took and read the letter, which was couched in terms so flattering that

[024-107]even I myself felt it would be wrong in my father not to comply with

[024-108]the request the duke made in it, which was that he would send me

[024-109]immediately to him, as he wished me to become the companion, not

[024-110]servant, of his eldest son, and would take upon himself the charge of

[024-111]placing me in a position corresponding to the esteem in which he held

[024-112]me. On reading the letter my voice failed me, and still more when I

[024-113]heard my father say, ‘Two days hence thou wilt depart, Cardenio, in

[024-114]accordance with the duke’s wish, and give thanks to God who is opening

[024-115]a road to thee by which thou mayest attain what I know thou dost

[024-116]deserve; and to these words he added others of fatherly counsel. The

[024-117]time for my departure arrived; I spoke one night to Luscinda, I told

[024-118]her all that had occurred, as I did also to her father, entreating him

[024-119]to allow some delay, and to defer the disposal of her hand until I

[024-120]should see what the Duke Ricardo sought of me: he gave me the promise,

[024-121]and she confirmed it with vows and swoonings unnumbered. Finally, I

[024-122]presented myself to the duke, and was received and treated by him so

[024-123]kindly that very soon envy began to do its work, the old servants

[024-124]growing envious of me, and regarding the duke’s inclination to show me

[024-125]favour as an injury to themselves. But the one to whom my arrival gave

[024-126]the greatest pleasure was the duke’s second son, Fernando by name, a

[024-127]gallant youth, of noble, generous, and amorous disposition, who very

[024-128]soon made so intimate a friend of me that it was remarked by everybody;

[024-129]for though the elder was attached to me, and showed me kindness, he did

[024-130]not carry his affectionate treatment to the same length as Don

[024-131]Fernando. It so happened, then, that as between friends no secret

[024-132]remains unshared, and as the favour I enjoyed with Don Fernando had

[024-133]grown into friendship, he made all his thoughts known to me, and in

[024-134]particular a love affair which troubled his mind a little. He was

[024-135]deeply in love with a peasant girl, a vassal of his father’s, the

[024-136]daughter of wealthy parents, and herself so beautiful, modest,

[024-137]discreet, and virtuous, that no one who knew her was able to decide in

[024-138]which of these respects she was most highly gifted or most excelled.

[024-139]The attractions of the fair peasant raised the passion of Don Fernando

[024-140]to such a point that, in order to gain his object and overcome her

[024-141]virtuous resolutions, he determined to pledge his word to her to become

[024-142]her husband, for to attempt it in any other way was to attempt an

[024-143]impossibility. Bound to him as I was by friendship, I strove by the

[024-144]best arguments and the most forcible examples I could think of to

[024-145]restrain and dissuade him from such a course; but perceiving I produced

[024-146]no effect I resolved to make the Duke Ricardo, his father, acquainted

[024-147]with the matter; but Don Fernando, being sharp-witted and shrewd,

[024-148]foresaw and apprehended this, perceiving that by my duty as a good

[024-149]servant I was bound not to keep concealed a thing so much opposed to

[024-150]the honour of my lord the duke; and so, to mislead and deceive me, he

[024-151]told me he could find no better way of effacing from his mind the

[024-152]beauty that so enslaved him than by absenting himself for some months,

[024-153]and that he wished the absence to be effected by our going, both of us,

[024-154]to my father’s house under the pretence, which he would make to the

[024-155]duke, of going to see and buy some fine horses that there were in my

[024-156]city, which produces the best in the world. When I heard him say so,

[024-157]even if his resolution had not been so good a one I should have hailed

[024-158]it as one of the happiest that could be imagined, prompted by my

[024-159]affection, seeing what a favourable chance and opportunity it offered

[024-160]me of returning to see my Luscinda. With this thought and wish I

[024-161]commended his idea and encouraged his design, advising him to put it

[024-162]into execution as quickly as possible, as, in truth, absence produced

[024-163]its effect in spite of the most deeply rooted feelings. But, as

[024-164]afterwards appeared, when he said this to me he had already enjoyed the

[024-165]peasant girl under the title of husband, and was waiting for an

[024-166]opportunity of making it known with safety to himself, being in dread

[024-167]of what his father the duke would do when he came to know of his folly.

[024-168]It happened, then, that as with young men love is for the most part

[024-169]nothing more than appetite, which, as its final object is enjoyment,

[024-170]comes to an end on obtaining it, and that which seemed to be love takes

[024-171]to flight, as it cannot pass the limit fixed by nature, which fixes no

[024-172]limit to true love—what I mean is that after Don Fernando had enjoyed

[024-173]this peasant girl his passion subsided and his eagerness cooled, and if

[024-174]at first he feigned a wish to absent himself in order to cure his love,

[024-175]he was now in reality anxious to go to avoid keeping his promise.

[024-176]“The duke gave him permission, and ordered me to accompany him; we

[024-177]arrived at my city, and my father gave him the reception due to his

[024-178]rank; I saw Luscinda without delay, and, though it had not been dead or

[024-179]deadened, my love gathered fresh life. To my sorrow I told the story of

[024-180]it to Don Fernando, for I thought that in virtue of the great

[024-181]friendship he bore me I was bound to conceal nothing from him. I

[024-182]extolled her beauty, her gaiety, her wit, so warmly, that my praises

[024-183]excited in him a desire to see a damsel adorned by such attractions. To

[024-184]my misfortune I yielded to it, showing her to him one night by the

[024-185]light of a taper at a window where we used to talk to one another. As

[024-186]she appeared to him in her dressing-gown, she drove all the beauties he

[024-187]had seen until then out of his recollection; speech failed him, his

[024-188]head turned, he was spell-bound, and in the end love-smitten, as you

[024-189]will see in the course of the story of my misfortune; and to inflame

[024-190]still further his passion, which he hid from me and revealed to Heaven

[024-191]alone, it so happened that one day he found a note of hers entreating

[024-192]me to demand her of her father in marriage, so delicate, so modest, and

[024-193]so tender, that on reading it he told me that in Luscinda alone were

[024-194]combined all the charms of beauty and understanding that were

[024-195]distributed among all the other women in the world. It is true, and I

[024-196]own it now, that though I knew what good cause Don Fernando had to

[024-197]praise Luscinda, it gave me uneasiness to hear these praises from his

[024-198]mouth, and I began to fear, and with reason to feel distrust of him,

[024-199]for there was no moment when he was not ready to talk of Luscinda, and

[024-200]he would start the subject himself even though he dragged it in

[024-201]unseasonably, a circumstance that aroused in me a certain amount of

[024-202]jealousy; not that I feared any change in the constancy or faith of

[024-203]Luscinda; but still my fate led me to forebode what she assured me

[024-204]against. Don Fernando contrived always to read the letters I sent to

[024-205]Luscinda and her answers to me, under the pretence that he enjoyed the

[024-206]wit and sense of both. It so happened, then, that Luscinda having

[024-207]begged of me a book of chivalry to read, one that she was very fond of,

[024-208]Amadis of Gaul—”

[024-209]Don Quixote no sooner heard a book of chivalry mentioned, than he said:

[024-210]“Had your worship told me at the beginning of your story that the Lady

[024-211]Luscinda was fond of books of chivalry, no other laudation would have

[024-212]been requisite to impress upon me the superiority of her understanding,

[024-213]for it could not have been of the excellence you describe had a taste

[024-214]for such delightful reading been wanting; so, as far as I am concerned,

[024-215]you need waste no more words in describing her beauty, worth, and

[024-216]intelligence; for, on merely hearing what her taste was, I declare her

[024-217]to be the most beautiful and the most intelligent woman in the world;

[024-218]and I wish your worship had, along with Amadis of Gaul, sent her the

[024-219]worthy Don Rugel of Greece, for I know the Lady Luscinda would greatly

[024-220]relish Daraida and Garaya, and the shrewd sayings of the shepherd

[024-221]Darinel, and the admirable verses of his bucolics, sung and delivered

[024-222]by him with such sprightliness, wit, and ease; but a time may come when

[024-223]this omission can be remedied, and to rectify it nothing more is needed

[024-224]than for your worship to be so good as to come with me to my village,

[024-225]for there I can give you more than three hundred books which are the

[024-226]delight of my soul and the entertainment of my life;—though it occurs

[024-227]to me that I have not got one of them now, thanks to the spite of

[024-228]wicked and envious enchanters;—but pardon me for having broken the

[024-229]promise we made not to interrupt your discourse; for when I hear

[024-230]chivalry or knights-errant mentioned, I can no more help talking about

[024-231]them than the rays of the sun can help giving heat, or those of the

[024-232]moon moisture; pardon me, therefore, and proceed, for that is more to

[024-233]the purpose now.”

[024-234]While Don Quixote was saying this, Cardenio allowed his head to fall

[024-235]upon his breast, and seemed plunged in deep thought; and though twice

[024-236]Don Quixote bade him go on with his story, he neither looked up nor

[024-237]uttered a word in reply; but after some time he raised his head and

[024-238]said, “I cannot get rid of the idea, nor will anyone in the world

[024-239]remove it, or make me think otherwise—and he would be a blockhead who

[024-240]would hold or believe anything else than that that arrant knave Master

[024-241]Elisabad made free with Queen Madasima.”

[024-242]“That is not true, by all that’s good,” said Don Quixote in high wrath,

[024-243]turning upon him angrily, as his way was; “and it is a very great

[024-244]slander, or rather villainy. Queen Madasima was a very illustrious

[024-245]lady, and it is not to be supposed that so exalted a princess would

[024-246]have made free with a quack; and whoever maintains the contrary lies

[024-247]like a great scoundrel, and I will give him to know it, on foot or on

[024-248]horseback, armed or unarmed, by night or by day, or as he likes best.”

[024-249]Cardenio was looking at him steadily, and his mad fit having now come

[024-250]upon him, he had no disposition to go on with his story, nor would Don

[024-251]Quixote have listened to it, so much had what he had heard about

[024-252]Madasima disgusted him. Strange to say, he stood up for her as if she

[024-253]were in earnest his veritable born lady; to such a pass had his unholy

[024-254]books brought him. Cardenio, then, being, as I said, now mad, when he

[024-255]heard himself given the lie, and called a scoundrel and other insulting

[024-256]names, not relishing the jest, snatched up a stone that he found near

[024-257]him, and with it delivered such a blow on Don Quixote’s breast that he

[024-258]laid him on his back. Sancho Panza, seeing his master treated in this

[024-259]fashion, attacked the madman with his closed fist; but the Ragged One

[024-260]received him in such a way that with a blow of his fist he stretched

[024-261]him at his feet, and then mounting upon him crushed his ribs to his own

[024-262]satisfaction; the goatherd, who came to the rescue, shared the same

[024-263]fate; and having beaten and pummelled them all he left them and quietly

[024-264]withdrew to his hiding-place on the mountain. Sancho rose, and with the

[024-265]rage he felt at finding himself so belaboured without deserving it, ran

[024-266]to take vengeance on the goatherd, accusing him of not giving them

[024-267]warning that this man was at times taken with a mad fit, for if they

[024-268]had known it they would have been on their guard to protect themselves.

[024-269]The goatherd replied that he had said so, and that if he had not heard

[024-270]him, that was no fault of his. Sancho retorted, and the goatherd

[024-271]rejoined, and the altercation ended in their seizing each other by the

[024-272]beard, and exchanging such fisticuffs that if Don Quixote had not made

[024-273]peace between them, they would have knocked one another to pieces.

[024-274]“Leave me alone, Sir Knight of the Rueful Countenance,” said Sancho,

[024-275]grappling with the goatherd, “for of this fellow, who is a clown like

[024-276]myself, and no dubbed knight, I can safely take satisfaction for the

[024-277]affront he has offered me, fighting with him hand to hand like an

[024-278]honest man.”

[024-279]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, “but I know that he is not to blame

[024-280]for what has happened.”

[024-281]With this he pacified them, and again asked the goatherd if it would be

[024-282]possible to find Cardenio, as he felt the greatest anxiety to know the

[024-283]end of his story. The goatherd told him, as he had told him before,

[024-284]that there was no knowing of a certainty where his lair was; but that

[024-285]if he wandered about much in that neighbourhood he could not fail to

[024-286]fall in with him either in or out of his senses.
