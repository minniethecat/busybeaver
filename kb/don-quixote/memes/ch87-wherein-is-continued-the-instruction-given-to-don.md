WHEREIN IS CONTINUED THE INSTRUCTION GIVEN TO DON QUIXOTE TOUCHING THE

[087-001]DISENCHANTMENT OF DULCINEA, TOGETHER WITH OTHER MARVELLOUS INCIDENTS

p35a.jpg (108K)


[087-002]They saw advancing towards them, to the sound of this pleasing music,

[087-003]what they call a triumphal car, drawn by six grey mules with white

[087-004]linen housings, on each of which was mounted a penitent, robed also in

[087-005]white, with a large lighted wax taper in his hand. The car was twice

[087-006]or, perhaps, three times as large as the former ones, and in front and

[087-007]on the sides stood twelve more penitents, all as white as snow and all

[087-008]with lighted tapers, a spectacle to excite fear as well as wonder; and

[087-009]on a raised throne was seated a nymph draped in a multitude of

[087-010]silver-tissue veils with an embroidery of countless gold spangles

[087-011]glittering all over them, that made her appear, if not richly, at least

[087-012]brilliantly, apparelled. She had her face covered with thin transparent

[087-013]sendal, the texture of which did not prevent the fair features of a

[087-014]maiden from being distinguished, while the numerous lights made it

[087-015]possible to judge of her beauty and of her years, which seemed to be

[087-016]not less than seventeen but not to have yet reached twenty. Beside her

[087-017]was a figure in a robe of state, as they call it, reaching to the feet,

[087-018]while the head was covered with a black veil. But the instant the car

[087-019]was opposite the duke and duchess and Don Quixote the music of the

[087-020]clarions ceased, and then that of the lutes and harps on the car, and

[087-021]the figure in the robe rose up, and flinging it apart and removing the

[087-022]veil from its face, disclosed to their eyes the shape of Death itself,

[087-023]fleshless and hideous, at which sight Don Quixote felt uneasy, Sancho

[087-024]frightened, and the duke and duchess displayed a certain trepidation.

[087-025]Having risen to its feet, this living death, in a sleepy voice and with

[087-026]a tongue hardly awake, held forth as follows:

p35b.jpg (232K)


[087-027]I am that Merlin who the legends say

[087-028]The devil had for father, and the lie

[087-029]Hath gathered credence with the lapse of time.

[087-030]Of magic prince, of Zoroastric lore

[087-031]Monarch and treasurer, with jealous eye

[087-032]I view the efforts of the age to hide

[087-033]The gallant deeds of doughty errant knights,

[087-034]Who are, and ever have been, dear to me.

[087-035]Enchanters and magicians and their kind

[087-036]Are mostly hard of heart; not so am I;

[087-037]For mine is tender, soft, compassionate,

[087-038]And its delight is doing good to all.

[087-039]In the dim caverns of the gloomy Dis,

[087-040]Where, tracing mystic lines and characters,

[087-041]My soul abideth now, there came to me

[087-042]The sorrow-laden plaint of her, the fair,

[087-043]The peerless Dulcinea del Toboso.

[087-044]I knew of her enchantment and her fate,

[087-045]From high-born dame to peasant wench transformed

[087-046]And touched with pity, first I turned the leaves

[087-047]Of countless volumes of my devilish craft,

[087-048]And then, in this grim grisly skeleton

[087-049]Myself encasing, hither have I come

[087-050]To show where lies the fitting remedy

[087-051]To give relief in such a piteous case.

[087-052]O thou, the pride and pink of all that I wear

[087-053]The adamantine steel! O shining light,

[087-054]O beacon, polestar, path and guide of all

[087-055]Who, scorning slumber and the lazy down,

[087-056]Adopt the toilsome life of bloodstained arms!

[087-057]To thee, great hero who all praise transcends,

[087-058]La Mancha’s lustre and Iberia’s star,

[087-059]Don Quixote, wise as brave, to thee I say—

[087-060]For peerless Dulcinea del Toboso

[087-061]Her pristine form and beauty to regain,

[087-062]’Tis needful that thy esquire Sancho shall,

[087-063]On his own sturdy buttocks bared to heaven,

[087-064]Three thousand and three hundred lashes lay,

[087-065]And that they smart and sting and hurt him well.

[087-066]Thus have the authors of her woe resolved.

[087-067]And this is, gentles, wherefore I have come.

[087-068]“By all that’s good,” exclaimed Sancho at this, “I’ll just as soon give

[087-069]myself three stabs with a dagger as three, not to say three thousand,

[087-070]lashes. The devil take such a way of disenchanting! I don’t see what my

[087-071]backside has got to do with enchantments. By God, if Señor Merlin has

[087-072]not found out some other way of disenchanting the lady Dulcinea del

[087-073]Toboso, she may go to her grave enchanted.”

[087-074]“But I’ll take you, Don Clown stuffed with garlic,” said Don Quixote,

[087-075]“and tie you to a tree as naked as when your mother brought you forth,

[087-076]and give you, not to say three thousand three hundred, but six thousand

[087-077]six hundred lashes, and so well laid on that they won’t be got rid of

[087-078]if you try three thousand three hundred times; don’t answer me a word

[087-079]or I’ll tear your soul out.”

[087-080]On hearing this Merlin said, “That will not do, for the lashes worthy

[087-081]Sancho has to receive must be given of his own free will and not by

[087-082]force, and at whatever time he pleases, for there is no fixed limit

[087-083]assigned to him; but it is permitted him, if he likes to commute by

[087-084]half the pain of this whipping, to let them be given by the hand of

[087-085]another, though it may be somewhat weighty.”

[087-086]“Not a hand, my own or anybody else’s, weighty or weighable, shall

[087-087]touch me,” said Sancho. “Was it I that gave birth to the lady Dulcinea

[087-088]del Toboso, that my backside is to pay for the sins of her eyes? My

[087-089]master, indeed, that’s a part of her—for, he’s always calling her ‘my

[087-090]life’ and ‘my soul,’ and his stay and prop—may and ought to whip

[087-091]himself for her and take all the trouble required for her

[087-092]disenchantment. But for me to whip myself! Abernuncio!”

[087-093]As soon as Sancho had done speaking the nymph in silver that was at the

[087-094]side of Merlin’s ghost stood up, and removing the thin veil from her

[087-095]face disclosed one that seemed to all something more than exceedingly

[087-096]beautiful; and with a masculine freedom from embarrassment and in a

[087-097]voice not very like a lady’s, addressing Sancho directly, said, “Thou

[087-098]wretched squire, soul of a pitcher, heart of a cork tree, with bowels

[087-099]of flint and pebbles; if, thou impudent thief, they bade thee throw

[087-100]thyself down from some lofty tower; if, enemy of mankind, they asked

[087-101]thee to swallow a dozen of toads, two of lizards, and three of adders;

[087-102]if they wanted thee to slay thy wife and children with a sharp

[087-103]murderous scimitar, it would be no wonder for thee to show thyself

[087-104]stubborn and squeamish. But to make a piece of work about three

[087-105]thousand three hundred lashes, what every poor little charity-boy gets

[087-106]every month—it is enough to amaze, astonish, astound the compassionate

[087-107]bowels of all who hear it, nay, all who come to hear it in the course

[087-108]of time. Turn, O miserable, hard-hearted animal, turn, I say, those

[087-109]timorous owl’s eyes upon these of mine that are compared to radiant

[087-110]stars, and thou wilt see them weeping trickling streams and rills, and

[087-111]tracing furrows, tracks, and paths over the fair fields of my cheeks.

[087-112]Let it move thee, crafty, ill-conditioned monster, to see my blooming

[087-113]youth—still in its teens, for I am not yet twenty—wasting and withering

[087-114]away beneath the husk of a rude peasant wench; and if I do not appear

[087-115]in that shape now, it is a special favour Señor Merlin here has granted

[087-116]me, to the sole end that my beauty may soften thee; for the tears of

[087-117]beauty in distress turn rocks into cotton and tigers into ewes. Lay on

[087-118]to that hide of thine, thou great untamed brute, rouse up thy lusty

[087-119]vigour that only urges thee to eat and eat, and set free the softness

[087-120]of my flesh, the gentleness of my nature, and the fairness of my face.

[087-121]And if thou wilt not relent or come to reason for me, do so for the

[087-122]sake of that poor knight thou hast beside thee; thy master I mean,

[087-123]whose soul I can this moment see, how he has it stuck in his throat not

[087-124]ten fingers from his lips, and only waiting for thy inflexible or

[087-125]yielding reply to make its escape by his mouth or go back again into

[087-126]his stomach.”

[087-127]Don Quixote on hearing this felt his throat, and turning to the duke he

[087-128]said, “By God, señor, Dulcinea says true, I have my soul stuck here in

[087-129]my throat like the nut of a crossbow.”

[087-130]“What say you to this, Sancho?” said the duchess.

[087-131]“I say, señora,” returned Sancho, “what I said before; as for the

[087-132]lashes, abernuncio!”

[087-133]“Abrenuncio, you should say, Sancho, and not as you do,” said the duke.

[087-134]“Let me alone, your highness,” said Sancho. “I’m not in a humour now to

[087-135]look into niceties or a letter more or less, for these lashes that are

[087-136]to be given me, or I’m to give myself, have so upset me, that I don’t

[087-137]know what I’m saying or doing. But I’d like to know of this lady, my

[087-138]lady Dulcinea del Toboso, where she learned this way she has of asking

[087-139]favours. She comes to ask me to score my flesh with lashes, and she

[087-140]calls me soul of a pitcher, and great untamed brute, and a string of

[087-141]foul names that the devil is welcome to. Is my flesh brass? or is it

[087-142]anything to me whether she is enchanted or not? Does she bring with her

[087-143]a basket of fair linen, shirts, kerchiefs, socks—not that I wear any—to

[087-144]coax me? No, nothing but one piece of abuse after another, though she

[087-145]knows the proverb they have here that ‘an ass loaded with gold goes

[087-146]lightly up a mountain,’ and that ‘gifts break rocks,’ and ‘praying to

[087-147]God and plying the hammer,’ and that ‘one “take” is better than two

[087-148]“I’ll give thee’s.”’ Then there’s my master, who ought to stroke me

[087-149]down and pet me to make me turn wool and carded cotton; he says if he

[087-150]gets hold of me he’ll tie me naked to a tree and double the tale of

[087-151]lashes on me. These tender-hearted gentry should consider that it’s not

[087-152]merely a squire, but a governor they are asking to whip himself; just

[087-153]as if it was ‘drink with cherries.’ Let them learn, plague take them,

[087-154]the right way to ask, and beg, and behave themselves; for all times are

[087-155]not alike, nor are people always in good humour. I’m now ready to burst

[087-156]with grief at seeing my green coat torn, and they come to ask me to

[087-157]whip myself of my own free will, I having as little fancy for it as for

[087-158]turning cacique.”

[087-159]“Well then, the fact is, friend Sancho,” said the duke, “that unless

[087-160]you become softer than a ripe fig, you shall not get hold of the

[087-161]government. It would be a nice thing for me to send my islanders a

[087-162]cruel governor with flinty bowels, who won’t yield to the tears of

[087-163]afflicted damsels or to the prayers of wise, magisterial, ancient

[087-164]enchanters and sages. In short, Sancho, either you must be whipped by

[087-165]yourself, or they must whip you, or you shan’t be governor.”

[087-166]“Señor,” said Sancho, “won’t two days’ grace be given me in which to

[087-167]consider what is best for me?”

[087-168]“No, certainly not,” said Merlin; “here, this minute, and on the spot,

[087-169]the matter must be settled; either Dulcinea will return to the cave of

[087-170]Montesinos and to her former condition of peasant wench, or else in her

[087-171]present form shall be carried to the Elysian fields, where she will

[087-172]remain waiting until the number of stripes is completed.”

[087-173]“Now then, Sancho!” said the duchess, “show courage, and gratitude for

[087-174]your master Don Quixote’s bread that you have eaten; we are all bound

[087-175]to oblige and please him for his benevolent disposition and lofty

[087-176]chivalry. Consent to this whipping, my son; to the devil with the

[087-177]devil, and leave fear to milksops, for ‘a stout heart breaks bad luck,’

[087-178]as you very well know.”

[087-179]To this Sancho replied with an irrelevant remark, which, addressing

[087-180]Merlin, he made to him, “Will your worship tell me, Señor Merlin—when

[087-181]that courier devil came up he gave my master a message from Señor

[087-182]Montesinos, charging him to wait for him here, as he was coming to

[087-183]arrange how the lady Doña Dulcinea del Toboso was to be disenchanted;

[087-184]but up to the present we have not seen Montesinos, nor anything like

[087-185]him.”

[087-186]To which Merlin made answer, “The devil, Sancho, is a blockhead and a

[087-187]great scoundrel; I sent him to look for your master, but not with a

[087-188]message from Montesinos but from myself; for Montesinos is in his cave

[087-189]expecting, or more properly speaking, waiting for his disenchantment;

[087-190]for there’s the tail to be skinned yet for him; if he owes you

[087-191]anything, or you have any business to transact with him, I’ll bring him

[087-192]to you and put him where you choose; but for the present make up your

[087-193]mind to consent to this penance, and believe me it will be very good

[087-194]for you, for soul as well for body—for your soul because of the charity

[087-195]with which you perform it, for your body because I know that you are of

[087-196]a sanguine habit and it will do you no harm to draw a little blood.”

[087-197]“There are a great many doctors in the world; even the enchanters are

[087-198]doctors,” said Sancho; “however, as everybody tells me the same

[087-199]thing—though I can’t see it myself—I say I am willing to give myself

[087-200]the three thousand three hundred lashes, provided I am to lay them on

[087-201]whenever I like, without any fixing of days or times; and I’ll try and

[087-202]get out of debt as quickly as I can, that the world may enjoy the

[087-203]beauty of the lady Dulcinea del Toboso; as it seems, contrary to what I

[087-204]thought, that she is beautiful after all. It must be a condition, too,

[087-205]that I am not to be bound to draw blood with the scourge, and that if

[087-206]any of the lashes happen to be fly-flappers they are to count. Item,

[087-207]that, in case I should make any mistake in the reckoning, Señor Merlin,

[087-208]as he knows everything, is to keep count, and let me know how many are

[087-209]still wanting or over the number.”

[087-210]“There will be no need to let you know of any over,” said Merlin,

[087-211]“because, when you reach the full number, the lady Dulcinea will at

[087-212]once, and that very instant, be disenchanted, and will come in her

[087-213]gratitude to seek out the worthy Sancho, and thank him, and even reward

[087-214]him for the good work. So you have no cause to be uneasy about stripes

[087-215]too many or too few; heaven forbid I should cheat anyone of even a hair

[087-216]of his head.”

[087-217]“Well then, in God’s hands be it,” said Sancho; “in the hard case I’m

[087-218]in I give in; I say I accept the penance on the conditions laid down.”

[087-219]The instant Sancho uttered these last words the music of the clarions

[087-220]struck up once more, and again a host of muskets were discharged, and

[087-221]Don Quixote hung on Sancho’s neck kissing him again and again on the

[087-222]forehead and cheeks. The duchess and the duke expressed the greatest

[087-223]satisfaction, the car began to move on, and as it passed the fair

[087-224]Dulcinea bowed to the duke and duchess and made a low curtsey to

[087-225]Sancho.

[087-226]And now bright smiling dawn came on apace; the flowers of the field,

[087-227]revived, raised up their heads, and the crystal waters of the brooks,

[087-228]murmuring over the grey and white pebbles, hastened to pay their

[087-229]tribute to the expectant rivers; the glad earth, the unclouded sky, the

[087-230]fresh breeze, the clear light, each and all showed that the day that

[087-231]came treading on the skirts of morning would be calm and bright. The

[087-232]duke and duchess, pleased with their hunt and at having carried out

[087-233]their plans so cleverly and successfully, returned to their castle

[087-234]resolved to follow up their joke; for to them there was no reality that

[087-235]could afford them more amusement.
