WHEREIN IS RELATED THE CRAFTY DEVICE SANCHO ADOPTED TO ENCHANT THE LADY

[062-001]DULCINEA, AND OTHER INCIDENTS AS LUDICROUS AS THEY ARE TRUE

p10a.jpg (142K)


[062-002]When the author of this great history comes to relate what is set down

[062-003]in this chapter he says he would have preferred to pass it over in

[062-004]silence, fearing it would not be believed, because here Don Quixote’s

[062-005]madness reaches the confines of the greatest that can be conceived, and

[062-006]even goes a couple of bowshots beyond the greatest. But after all,

[062-007]though still under the same fear and apprehension, he has recorded it

[062-008]without adding to the story or leaving out a particle of the truth, and

[062-009]entirely disregarding the charges of falsehood that might be brought

[062-010]against him; and he was right, for the truth may run fine but will not

[062-011]break, and always rises above falsehood as oil above water; and so,

[062-012]going on with his story, he says that as soon as Don Quixote had

[062-013]ensconced himself in the forest, oak grove, or wood near El Toboso, he

[062-014]bade Sancho return to the city, and not come into his presence again

[062-015]without having first spoken on his behalf to his lady, and begged of

[062-016]her that it might be her good pleasure to permit herself to be seen by

[062-017]her enslaved knight, and deign to bestow her blessing upon him, so that

[062-018]he might thereby hope for a happy issue in all his encounters and

[062-019]difficult enterprises. Sancho undertook to execute the task according

[062-020]to the instructions, and to bring back an answer as good as the one he

[062-021]brought back before.

[062-022]“Go, my son,” said Don Quixote, “and be not dazed when thou findest

[062-023]thyself exposed to the light of that sun of beauty thou art going to

[062-024]seek. Happy thou, above all the squires in the world! Bear in mind, and

[062-025]let it not escape thy memory, how she receives thee; if she changes

[062-026]colour while thou art giving her my message; if she is agitated and

[062-027]disturbed at hearing my name; if she cannot rest upon her cushion,

[062-028]shouldst thou haply find her seated in the sumptuous state chamber

[062-029]proper to her rank; and should she be standing, observe if she poises

[062-030]herself now on one foot, now on the other; if she repeats two or three

[062-031]times the reply she gives thee; if she passes from gentleness to

[062-032]austerity, from asperity to tenderness; if she raises her hand to

[062-033]smooth her hair though it be not disarranged. In short, my son, observe

[062-034]all her actions and motions, for if thou wilt report them to me as they

[062-035]were, I will gather what she hides in the recesses of her heart as

[062-036]regards my love; for I would have thee know, Sancho, if thou knowest it

[062-037]not, that with lovers the outward actions and motions they give way to

[062-038]when their loves are in question are the faithful messengers that carry

[062-039]the news of what is going on in the depths of their hearts. Go, my

[062-040]friend, may better fortune than mine attend thee, and bring thee a

[062-041]happier issue than that which I await in dread in this dreary

[062-042]solitude.”

[062-043]“I will go and return quickly,” said Sancho; “cheer up that little

[062-044]heart of yours, master mine, for at the present moment you seem to have

[062-045]got one no bigger than a hazel nut; remember what they say, that a

[062-046]stout heart breaks bad luck, and that where there are no fletches there

[062-047]are no pegs; and moreover they say, the hare jumps up where it’s not

[062-048]looked for. I say this because, if we could not find my lady’s palaces

[062-049]or castles to-night, now that it is daylight I count upon finding them

[062-050]when I least expect it, and once found, leave it to me to manage her.”

[062-051]“Verily, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “thou dost always bring in thy

[062-052]proverbs happily, whatever we deal with; may God give me better luck in

[062-053]what I am anxious about.”

[062-054]With this, Sancho wheeled about and gave Dapple the stick, and Don

[062-055]Quixote remained behind, seated on his horse, resting in his stirrups

[062-056]and leaning on the end of his lance, filled with sad and troubled

[062-057]forebodings; and there we will leave him, and accompany Sancho, who

[062-058]went off no less serious and troubled than he left his master; so much

[062-059]so, that as soon as he had got out of the thicket, and looking round

[062-060]saw that Don Quixote was not within sight, he dismounted from his ass,

[062-061]and seating himself at the foot of a tree began to commune with

[062-062]himself, saying, “Now, brother Sancho, let us know where your worship

[062-063]is going. Are you going to look for some ass that has been lost? Not at

[062-064]all. Then what are you going to look for? I am going to look for a

[062-065]princess, that’s all; and in her for the sun of beauty and the whole

[062-066]heaven at once. And where do you expect to find all this, Sancho?

[062-067]Where? Why, in the great city of El Toboso. Well, and for whom are you

[062-068]going to look for her? For the famous knight Don Quixote of La Mancha,

[062-069]who rights wrongs, gives food to those who thirst and drink to the

[062-070]hungry. That’s all very well, but do you know her house, Sancho? My

[062-071]master says it will be some royal palace or grand castle. And have you

[062-072]ever seen her by any chance? Neither I nor my master ever saw her. And

[062-073]does it strike you that it would be just and right if the El Toboso

[062-074]people, finding out that you were here with the intention of going to

[062-075]tamper with their princesses and trouble their ladies, were to come and

[062-076]cudgel your ribs, and not leave a whole bone in you? They would,

[062-077]indeed, have very good reason, if they did not see that I am under

[062-078]orders, and that ‘you are a messenger, my friend, no blame belongs to

[062-079]you.’ Don’t you trust to that, Sancho, for the Manchegan folk are as

[062-080]hot-tempered as they are honest, and won’t put up with liberties from

[062-081]anybody. By the Lord, if they get scent of you, it will be worse for

[062-082]you, I promise you. Be off, you scoundrel! Let the bolt fall. Why

[062-083]should I go looking for three feet on a cat, to please another man; and

[062-084]what is more, when looking for Dulcinea will be looking for Marica in

[062-085]Ravena, or the bachelor in Salamanca? The devil, the devil and nobody

[062-086]else, has mixed me up in this business!”

[062-087]Such was the soliloquy Sancho held with himself, and all the conclusion

[062-088]he could come to was to say to himself again, “Well, there’s remedy for

[062-089]everything except death, under whose yoke we have all to pass, whether

[062-090]we like it or not, when life’s finished. I have seen by a thousand

[062-091]signs that this master of mine is a madman fit to be tied, and for that

[062-092]matter, I too, am not behind him; for I’m a greater fool than he is

[062-093]when I follow him and serve him, if there’s any truth in the proverb

[062-094]that says, ‘Tell me what company thou keepest, and I’ll tell thee what

[062-095]thou art,’ or in that other, ‘Not with whom thou art bred, but with

[062-096]whom thou art fed.’ Well then, if he be mad, as he is, and with a

[062-097]madness that mostly takes one thing for another, and white for black,

[062-098]and black for white, as was seen when he said the windmills were

[062-099]giants, and the monks’ mules dromedaries, flocks of sheep armies of

[062-100]enemies, and much more to the same tune, it will not be very hard to

[062-101]make him believe that some country girl, the first I come across here,

[062-102]is the lady Dulcinea; and if he does not believe it, I’ll swear it; and

[062-103]if he should swear, I’ll swear again; and if he persists I’ll persist

[062-104]still more, so as, come what may, to have my quoit always over the peg.

[062-105]Maybe, by holding out in this way, I may put a stop to his sending me

[062-106]on messages of this kind another time; or maybe he will think, as I

[062-107]suspect he will, that one of those wicked enchanters, who he says have

[062-108]a spite against him, has changed her form for the sake of doing him an

[062-109]ill turn and injuring him.”

[062-110]With this reflection Sancho made his mind easy, counting the business

[062-111]as good as settled, and stayed there till the afternoon so as to make

[062-112]Don Quixote think he had time enough to go to El Toboso and return; and

[062-113]things turned out so luckily for him that as he got up to mount Dapple,

[062-114]he spied, coming from El Toboso towards the spot where he stood, three

[062-115]peasant girls on three colts, or fillies—for the author does not make

[062-116]the point clear, though it is more likely they were she-asses, the

[062-117]usual mount with village girls; but as it is of no great consequence,

[062-118]we need not stop to prove it.

[062-119]To be brief, the instant Sancho saw the peasant girls, he returned full

[062-120]speed to seek his master, and found him sighing and uttering a thousand

[062-121]passionate lamentations. When Don Quixote saw him he exclaimed, “What

[062-122]news, Sancho, my friend? Am I to mark this day with a white stone or a

[062-123]black?”

[062-124]“Your worship,” replied Sancho, “had better mark it with ruddle, like

[062-125]the inscriptions on the walls of class rooms, that those who see it may

[062-126]see it plain.”

[062-127]“Then thou bringest good news,” said Don Quixote.

[062-128]“So good,” replied Sancho, “that your worship has only to spur

[062-129]Rocinante and get out into the open field to see the lady Dulcinea del

[062-130]Toboso, who, with two others, damsels of hers, is coming to see your

[062-131]worship.”

[062-132]“Holy God! what art thou saying, Sancho, my friend?” exclaimed Don

[062-133]Quixote. “Take care thou art not deceiving me, or seeking by false joy

[062-134]to cheer my real sadness.”

[062-135]“What could I get by deceiving your worship,” returned Sancho,

[062-136]“especially when it will so soon be shown whether I tell the truth or

[062-137]not? Come, señor, push on, and you will see the princess our mistress

[062-138]coming, robed and adorned—in fact, like what she is. Her damsels and

[062-139]she are all one glow of gold, all bunches of pearls, all diamonds, all

[062-140]rubies, all cloth of brocade of more than ten borders; with their hair

[062-141]loose on their shoulders like so many sunbeams playing with the wind;

[062-142]and moreover, they come mounted on three piebald cackneys, the finest

[062-143]sight ever you saw.”

[062-144]“Hackneys, you mean, Sancho,” said Don Quixote.

[062-145]“There is not much difference between cackneys and hackneys,” said

[062-146]Sancho; “but no matter what they come on, there they are, the finest

[062-147]ladies one could wish for, especially my lady the princess Dulcinea,

[062-148]who staggers one’s senses.”

[062-149]“Let us go, Sancho, my son,” said Don Quixote, “and in guerdon of this

[062-150]news, as unexpected as it is good, I bestow upon thee the best spoil I

[062-151]shall win in the first adventure I may have; or if that does not

[062-152]satisfy thee, I promise thee the foals I shall have this year from my

[062-153]three mares that thou knowest are in foal on our village common.”

[062-154]“I’ll take the foals,” said Sancho; “for it is not quite certain that

[062-155]the spoils of the first adventure will be good ones.”

[062-156]By this time they had cleared the wood, and saw the three village

[062-157]lasses close at hand. Don Quixote looked all along the road to El

[062-158]Toboso, and as he could see nobody except the three peasant girls, he

[062-159]was completely puzzled, and asked Sancho if it was outside the city he

[062-160]had left them.

[062-161]“How outside the city?” returned Sancho. “Are your worship’s eyes in

[062-162]the back of your head, that you can’t see that they are these who are

[062-163]coming here, shining like the very sun at noonday?”

[062-164]“I see nothing, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “but three country girls on

[062-165]three jackasses.”

[062-166]“Now, may God deliver me from the devil!” said Sancho, “and can it be

[062-167]that your worship takes three hackneys—or whatever they’re called—as

[062-168]white as the driven snow, for jackasses? By the Lord, I could tear my

[062-169]beard if that was the case!”

[062-170]“Well, I can only say, Sancho, my friend,” said Don Quixote, “that it

[062-171]is as plain they are jackasses—or jennyasses—as that I am Don Quixote,

[062-172]and thou Sancho Panza: at any rate, they seem to me to be so.”

[062-173]“Hush, señor,” said Sancho, “don’t talk that way, but open your eyes,

[062-174]and come and pay your respects to the lady of your thoughts, who is

[062-175]close upon us now;” and with these words he advanced to receive the

[062-176]three village lasses, and dismounting from Dapple, caught hold of one

[062-177]of the asses of the three country girls by the halter, and dropping on

[062-178]both knees on the ground, he said, “Queen and princess and duchess of

[062-179]beauty, may it please your haughtiness and greatness to receive into

[062-180]your favour and good-will your captive knight who stands there turned

[062-181]into marble stone, and quite stupefied and benumbed at finding himself

[062-182]in your magnificent presence. I am Sancho Panza, his squire, and he the

[062-183]vagabond knight Don Quixote of La Mancha, otherwise called ‘The Knight

[062-184]of the Rueful Countenance.’”

[062-185]Don Quixote had by this time placed himself on his knees beside Sancho,

[062-186]and, with eyes starting out of his head and a puzzled gaze, was

[062-187]regarding her whom Sancho called queen and lady; and as he could see

[062-188]nothing in her except a village lass, and not a very well-favoured one,

[062-189]for she was platter-faced and snub-nosed, he was perplexed and

[062-190]bewildered, and did not venture to open his lips. The country girls, at

[062-191]the same time, were astonished to see these two men, so different in

[062-192]appearance, on their knees, preventing their companion from going on.

[062-193]She, however, who had been stopped, breaking silence, said angrily and

[062-194]testily, “Get out of the way, bad luck to you, and let us pass, for we

[062-195]are in a hurry.”

[062-196]To which Sancho returned, “Oh, princess and universal lady of El

[062-197]Toboso, is not your magnanimous heart softened by seeing the pillar and

[062-198]prop of knight-errantry on his knees before your sublimated presence?”

[062-199]On hearing this, one of the others exclaimed, “Woa then! why, I’m

[062-200]rubbing thee down, she-ass of my father-in-law! See how the lordlings

[062-201]come to make game of the village girls now, as if we here could not

[062-202]chaff as well as themselves. Go your own way, and let us go ours, and

[062-203]it will be better for you.”

[062-204]“Get up, Sancho,” said Don Quixote at this; “I see that fortune, ‘with

[062-205]evil done to me unsated still,’ has taken possession of all the roads

[062-206]by which any comfort may reach ‘this wretched soul’ that I carry in my

[062-207]flesh. And thou, highest perfection of excellence that can be desired,

[062-208]utmost limit of grace in human shape, sole relief of this afflicted

[062-209]heart that adores thee, though the malign enchanter that persecutes me

[062-210]has brought clouds and cataracts on my eyes, and to them, and them

[062-211]only, transformed thy unparagoned beauty and changed thy features into

[062-212]those of a poor peasant girl, if so be he has not at the same time

[062-213]changed mine into those of some monster to render them loathsome in thy

[062-214]sight, refuse not to look upon me with tenderness and love; seeing in

[062-215]this submission that I make on my knees to thy transformed beauty the

[062-216]humility with which my soul adores thee.”

[062-217]“Hey-day! My grandfather!” cried the girl, “much I care for your

[062-218]love-making! Get out of the way and let us pass, and we’ll thank you.”

[062-219]Sancho stood aside and let her go, very well pleased to have got so

[062-220]well out of the hobble he was in. The instant the village lass who had

[062-221]done duty for Dulcinea found herself free, prodding her “cackney” with

[062-222]a spike she had at the end of a stick, she set off at full speed across

[062-223]the field. The she-ass, however, feeling the point more acutely than

[062-224]usual, began cutting such capers, that it flung the lady Dulcinea to

[062-225]the ground; seeing which, Don Quixote ran to raise her up, and Sancho

[062-226]to fix and girth the pack-saddle, which also had slipped under the

[062-227]ass’s belly. The pack-saddle being secured, as Don Quixote was about to

[062-228]lift up his enchanted mistress in his arms and put her upon her beast,

[062-229]the lady, getting up from the ground, saved him the trouble, for, going

[062-230]back a little, she took a short run, and putting both hands on the

[062-231]croup of the ass she dropped into the saddle more lightly than a

[062-232]falcon, and sat astride like a man, whereat Sancho said, “Rogue! but

[062-233]our lady is lighter than a lanner, and might teach the cleverest

[062-234]Cordovan or Mexican how to mount; she cleared the back of the saddle in

[062-235]one jump, and without spurs she is making the hackney go like a zebra;

[062-236]and her damsels are no way behind her, for they all fly like the wind;”

[062-237]which was the truth, for as soon as they saw Dulcinea mounted, they

[062-238]pushed on after her, and sped away without looking back, for more than

[062-239]half a league.

[062-240]Don Quixote followed them with his eyes, and when they were no longer

[062-241]in sight, he turned to Sancho and said, “How now, Sancho? thou seest

[062-242]how I am hated by enchanters! And see to what a length the malice and

[062-243]spite they bear me go, when they seek to deprive me of the happiness it

[062-244]would give me to see my lady in her own proper form. The fact is I was

[062-245]born to be an example of misfortune, and the target and mark at which

[062-246]the arrows of adversity are aimed and directed. Observe too, Sancho,

[062-247]that these traitors were not content with changing and transforming my

[062-248]Dulcinea, but they transformed and changed her into a shape as mean and

[062-249]ill-favoured as that of the village girl yonder; and at the same time

[062-250]they robbed her of that which is such a peculiar property of ladies of

[062-251]distinction, that is to say, the sweet fragrance that comes of being

[062-252]always among perfumes and flowers. For I must tell thee, Sancho, that

[062-253]when I approached to put Dulcinea upon her hackney (as thou sayest it

[062-254]was, though to me it appeared a she-ass), she gave me a whiff of raw

[062-255]garlic that made my head reel, and poisoned my very heart.”

[062-256]“O scum of the earth!” cried Sancho at this, “O miserable, spiteful

[062-257]enchanters! O that I could see you all strung by the gills, like

[062-258]sardines on a twig! Ye know a great deal, ye can do a great deal, and

[062-259]ye do a great deal more. It ought to have been enough for you, ye

[062-260]scoundrels, to have changed the pearls of my lady’s eyes into oak

[062-261]galls, and her hair of purest gold into the bristles of a red ox’s

[062-262]tail, and in short, all her features from fair to foul, without

[062-263]meddling with her smell; for by that we might somehow have found out

[062-264]what was hidden underneath that ugly rind; though, to tell the truth, I

[062-265]never perceived her ugliness, but only her beauty, which was raised to

[062-266]the highest pitch of perfection by a mole she had on her right lip,

[062-267]like a moustache, with seven or eight red hairs like threads of gold,

[062-268]and more than a palm long.”

[062-269]“From the correspondence which exists between those of the face and

[062-270]those of the body,” said Don Quixote, “Dulcinea must have another mole

[062-271]resembling that on the thick of the thigh on that side on which she has

[062-272]the one on her face; but hairs of the length thou hast mentioned are

[062-273]very long for moles.”

[062-274]“Well, all I can say is there they were as plain as could be,” replied

[062-275]Sancho.

[062-276]“I believe it, my friend,” returned Don Quixote; “for nature bestowed

[062-277]nothing on Dulcinea that was not perfect and well-finished; and so, if

[062-278]she had a hundred moles like the one thou hast described, in her they

[062-279]would not be moles, but moons and shining stars. But tell me, Sancho,

[062-280]that which seemed to me to be a pack-saddle as thou wert fixing it, was

[062-281]it a flat-saddle or a side-saddle?”

[062-282]“It was neither,” replied Sancho, “but a jineta saddle, with a field

[062-283]covering worth half a kingdom, so rich is it.”

[062-284]“And that I could not see all this, Sancho!” said Don Quixote; “once

[062-285]more I say, and will say a thousand times, I am the most unfortunate of

[062-286]men.”

[062-287]Sancho, the rogue, had enough to do to hide his laughter, at hearing

[062-288]the simplicity of the master he had so nicely befooled. At length,

[062-289]after a good deal more conversation had passed between them, they

[062-290]remounted their beasts, and followed the road to Saragossa, which they

[062-291]expected to reach in time to take part in a certain grand festival

[062-292]which is held every year in that illustrious city; but before they got

[062-293]there things happened to them, so many, so important, and so strange,

[062-294]that they deserve to be recorded and read, as will be seen farther on.
