[018-001]IN WHICH IS RELATED THE DISCOURSE SANCHO PANZA HELD WITH HIS MASTER,

[018-002]DON QUIXOTE, AND OTHER ADVENTURES WORTH RELATING

c18a.jpg (79K)


[018-003]Sancho reached his master so limp and faint that he could not urge on

[018-004]his beast. When Don Quixote saw the state he was in he said, “I have

[018-005]now come to the conclusion, good Sancho, that this castle or inn is

[018-006]beyond a doubt enchanted, because those who have so atrociously

[018-007]diverted themselves with thee, what can they be but phantoms or beings

[018-008]of another world? and I hold this confirmed by having noticed that when

[018-009]I was by the wall of the yard witnessing the acts of thy sad tragedy,

[018-010]it was out of my power to mount upon it, nor could I even dismount from

[018-011]Rocinante, because they no doubt had me enchanted; for I swear to thee

[018-012]by the faith of what I am that if I had been able to climb up or

[018-013]dismount, I would have avenged thee in such a way that those braggart

[018-014]thieves would have remembered their freak for ever, even though in so

[018-015]doing I knew that I contravened the laws of chivalry, which, as I have

[018-016]often told thee, do not permit a knight to lay hands on him who is not

[018-017]one, save in case of urgent and great necessity in defence of his own

[018-018]life and person.”

[018-019]“I would have avenged myself too if I could,” said Sancho, “whether I

[018-020]had been dubbed knight or not, but I could not; though for my part I am

[018-021]persuaded those who amused themselves with me were not phantoms or

[018-022]enchanted men, as your worship says, but men of flesh and bone like

[018-023]ourselves; and they all had their names, for I heard them name them

[018-024]when they were tossing me, and one was called Pedro Martinez, and

[018-025]another Tenorio Hernandez, and the innkeeper, I heard, was called Juan

[018-026]Palomeque the Left-handed; so that, señor, your not being able to leap

[018-027]over the wall of the yard or dismount from your horse came of something

[018-028]else besides enchantments; and what I make out clearly from all this

[018-029]is, that these adventures we go seeking will in the end lead us into

[018-030]such misadventures that we shall not know which is our right foot; and

[018-031]that the best and wisest thing, according to my small wits, would be

[018-032]for us to return home, now that it is harvest-time, and attend to our

[018-033]business, and give over wandering from Zeca to Mecca and from pail to

[018-034]bucket, as the saying is.”

[018-035]“How little thou knowest about chivalry, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote;

[018-036]“hold thy peace and have patience; the day will come when thou shalt

[018-037]see with thine own eyes what an honourable thing it is to wander in the

[018-038]pursuit of this calling; nay, tell me, what greater pleasure can there

[018-039]be in the world, or what delight can equal that of winning a battle,

[018-040]and triumphing over one’s enemy? None, beyond all doubt.”

[018-041]“Very likely,” answered Sancho, “though I do not know it; all I know is

[018-042]that since we have been knights-errant, or since your worship has been

[018-043]one (for I have no right to reckon myself one of so honourable a

[018-044]number) we have never won any battle except the one with the Biscayan,

[018-045]and even out of that your worship came with half an ear and half a

[018-046]helmet the less; and from that till now it has been all cudgellings and

[018-047]more cudgellings, cuffs and more cuffs, I getting the blanketing over

[018-048]and above, and falling in with enchanted persons on whom I cannot

[018-049]avenge myself so as to know what the delight, as your worship calls it,

[018-050]of conquering an enemy is like.”

[018-051]“That is what vexes me, and what ought to vex thee, Sancho,” replied

[018-052]Don Quixote; “but henceforward I will endeavour to have at hand some

[018-053]sword made by such craft that no kind of enchantments can take effect

[018-054]upon him who carries it, and it is even possible that fortune may

[018-055]procure for me that which belonged to Amadis when he was called ‘The

[018-056]Knight of the Burning Sword,’ which was one of the best swords that

[018-057]ever knight in the world possessed, for, besides having the said

[018-058]virtue, it cut like a razor, and there was no armour, however strong

[018-059]and enchanted it might be, that could resist it.”

[018-060]“Such is my luck,” said Sancho, “that even if that happened and your

[018-061]worship found some such sword, it would, like the balsam, turn out

[018-062]serviceable and good for dubbed knights only, and as for the squires,

[018-063]they might sup sorrow.”

[018-064]“Fear not that, Sancho,” said Don Quixote: “Heaven will deal better by

[018-065]thee.”

[018-066]Thus talking, Don Quixote and his squire were going along, when, on the

[018-067]road they were following, Don Quixote perceived approaching them a

[018-068]large and thick cloud of dust, on seeing which he turned to Sancho and

[018-069]said:

[018-070]“This is the day, Sancho, on which will be seen the boon my fortune is

[018-071]reserving for me; this, I say, is the day on which as much as on any

[018-072]other shall be displayed the might of my arm, and on which I shall do

[018-073]deeds that shall remain written in the book of fame for all ages to

[018-074]come. Seest thou that cloud of dust which rises yonder? Well, then, all

[018-075]that is churned up by a vast army composed of various and countless

[018-076]nations that comes marching there.”

[018-077]“According to that there must be two,” said Sancho, “for on this

[018-078]opposite side also there rises just such another cloud of dust.”

[018-079]Don Quixote turned to look and found that it was true, and rejoicing

[018-080]exceedingly, he concluded that they were two armies about to engage and

[018-081]encounter in the midst of that broad plain; for at all times and

[018-082]seasons his fancy was full of the battles, enchantments, adventures,

[018-083]crazy feats, loves, and defiances that are recorded in the books of

[018-084]chivalry, and everything he said, thought, or did had reference to such

[018-085]things. Now the cloud of dust he had seen was raised by two great

[018-086]droves of sheep coming along the same road in opposite directions,

[018-087]which, because of the dust, did not become visible until they drew

[018-088]near, but Don Quixote asserted so positively that they were armies that

[018-089]Sancho was led to believe it and say, “Well, and what are we to do,

[018-090]señor?”

[018-091]“What?” said Don Quixote: “give aid and assistance to the weak and

[018-092]those who need it; and thou must know, Sancho, that this which comes

[018-093]opposite to us is conducted and led by the mighty emperor Alifanfaron,

[018-094]lord of the great isle of Trapobana; this other that marches behind me

[018-095]is that of his enemy the king of the Garamantas, Pentapolin of the Bare

[018-096]Arm, for he always goes into battle with his right arm bare.”

[018-097]“But why are these two lords such enemies?”

[018-098]“They are at enmity,” replied Don Quixote, “because this Alifanfaron is

[018-099]a furious pagan and is in love with the daughter of Pentapolin, who is

[018-100]a very beautiful and moreover gracious lady, and a Christian, and her

[018-101]father is unwilling to bestow her upon the pagan king unless he first

[018-102]abandons the religion of his false prophet Mahomet, and adopts his

[018-103]own.”

[018-104]“By my beard,” said Sancho, “but Pentapolin does quite right, and I

[018-105]will help him as much as I can.”

[018-106]“In that thou wilt do what is thy duty, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “for

[018-107]to engage in battles of this sort it is not requisite to be a dubbed

[018-108]knight.”

[018-109]“That I can well understand,” answered Sancho; “but where shall we put

[018-110]this ass where we may be sure to find him after the fray is over? for I

[018-111]believe it has not been the custom so far to go into battle on a beast

[018-112]of this kind.”

[018-113]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, “and what you had best do with him is

[018-114]to leave him to take his chance whether he be lost or not, for the

[018-115]horses we shall have when we come out victors will be so many that even

[018-116]Rocinante will run a risk of being changed for another. But attend to

[018-117]me and observe, for I wish to give thee some account of the chief

[018-118]knights who accompany these two armies; and that thou mayest the better

[018-119]see and mark, let us withdraw to that hillock which rises yonder,

[018-120]whence both armies may be seen.”

[018-121]They did so, and placed themselves on a rising ground from which the

[018-122]two droves that Don Quixote made armies of might have been plainly seen

[018-123]if the clouds of dust they raised had not obscured them and blinded the

[018-124]sight; nevertheless, seeing in his imagination what he did not see and

[018-125]what did not exist, he began thus in a loud voice:

[018-126]“That knight whom thou seest yonder in yellow armour, who bears upon

[018-127]his shield a lion crowned crouching at the feet of a damsel, is the

[018-128]valiant Laurcalco, lord of the Silver Bridge; that one in armour with

[018-129]flowers of gold, who bears on his shield three crowns argent on an

[018-130]azure field, is the dreaded Micocolembo, grand duke of Quirocia; that

[018-131]other of gigantic frame, on his right hand, is the ever dauntless

[018-132]Brandabarbaran de Boliche, lord of the three Arabias, who for armour

[018-133]wears that serpent skin, and has for shield a gate which, according to

[018-134]tradition, is one of those of the temple that Samson brought to the

[018-135]ground when by his death he revenged himself upon his enemies. But turn

[018-136]thine eyes to the other side, and thou shalt see in front and in the

[018-137]van of this other army the ever victorious and never vanquished Timonel

[018-138]of Carcajona, prince of New Biscay, who comes in armour with arms

[018-139]quartered azure, vert, white, and yellow, and bears on his shield a cat

[018-140]or on a field tawny with a motto which says _Miau_, which is the

[018-141]beginning of the name of his lady, who according to report is the

[018-142]peerless Miaulina, daughter of the duke Alfeniquen of the Algarve; the

[018-143]other, who burdens and presses the loins of that powerful charger and

[018-144]bears arms white as snow and a shield blank and without any device, is

[018-145]a novice knight, a Frenchman by birth, Pierres Papin by name, lord of

[018-146]the baronies of Utrique; that other, who with iron-shod heels strikes

[018-147]the flanks of that nimble parti-coloured zebra, and for arms bears

[018-148]azure vair, is the mighty duke of Nerbia, Espartafilardo del Bosque,

[018-149]who bears for device on his shield an asparagus plant with a motto in

[018-150]Castilian that says, _‘Rastrea mi suerte’_.” And so he went on naming a

[018-151]number of knights of one squadron or the other out of his imagination,

[018-152]and to all he assigned off-hand their arms, colours, devices, and

[018-153]mottoes, carried away by the illusions of his unheard-of craze; and

[018-154]without a pause, he continued, “People of divers nations compose this

[018-155]squadron in front; here are those that drink of the sweet waters of the

[018-156]famous Xanthus, those that scour the woody Massilian plains, those that

[018-157]sift the pure fine gold of Arabia Felix, those that enjoy the famed

[018-158]cool banks of the crystal Thermodon, those that in many and various

[018-159]ways divert the streams of the golden Pactolus, the Numidians,

[018-160]faithless in their promises, the Persians renowned in archery, the

[018-161]Parthians and the Medes that fight as they fly, the Arabs that ever

[018-162]shift their dwellings, the Scythians as cruel as they are fair, the

[018-163]Ethiopians with pierced lips, and an infinity of other nations whose

[018-164]features I recognise and descry, though I cannot recall their names. In

[018-165]this other squadron there come those that drink of the crystal streams

[018-166]of the olive-bearing Betis, those that make smooth their countenances

[018-167]with the water of the ever rich and golden Tagus, those that rejoice in

[018-168]the fertilising flow of the divine Genil, those that roam the Tartesian

[018-169]plains abounding in pasture, those that take their pleasure in the

[018-170]Elysian meadows of Jerez, the rich Manchegans crowned with ruddy ears

[018-171]of corn, the wearers of iron, old relics of the Gothic race, those that

[018-172]bathe in the Pisuerga renowned for its gentle current, those that feed

[018-173]their herds along the spreading pastures of the winding Guadiana famed

[018-174]for its hidden course, those that tremble with the cold of the pineclad

[018-175]Pyrenees or the dazzling snows of the lofty Apennine; in a word, as

[018-176]many as all Europe includes and contains.”

[018-177]Good God! what a number of countries and nations he named! giving to

[018-178]each its proper attributes with marvellous readiness; brimful and

[018-179]saturated with what he had read in his lying books! Sancho Panza hung

[018-180]upon his words without speaking, and from time to time turned to try if

[018-181]he could see the knights and giants his master was describing, and as

[018-182]he could not make out one of them he said to him:

[018-183]“Señor, devil take it if there’s a sign of any man you talk of, knight

[018-184]or giant, in the whole thing; maybe it’s all enchantment, like the

[018-185]phantoms last night.”

[018-186]“How canst thou say that!” answered Don Quixote; “dost thou not hear

[018-187]the neighing of the steeds, the braying of the trumpets, the roll of

[018-188]the drums?”

[018-189]“I hear nothing but a great bleating of ewes and sheep,” said Sancho;

[018-190]which was true, for by this time the two flocks had come close.

[018-191]“The fear thou art in, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “prevents thee from

[018-192]seeing or hearing correctly, for one of the effects of fear is to

[018-193]derange the senses and make things appear different from what they are;

[018-194]if thou art in such fear, withdraw to one side and leave me to myself,

[018-195]for alone I suffice to bring victory to that side to which I shall give

[018-196]my aid;” and so saying he gave Rocinante the spur, and putting the

[018-197]lance in rest, shot down the slope like a thunderbolt. Sancho shouted

[018-198]after him, crying, “Come back, Señor Don Quixote; I vow to God they are

[018-199]sheep and ewes you are charging! Come back! Unlucky the father that

[018-200]begot me! what madness is this! Look, there is no giant, nor knight,

[018-201]nor cats, nor arms, nor shields quartered or whole, nor vair azure or

[018-202]bedevilled. What are you about? Sinner that I am before God!” But not

[018-203]for all these entreaties did Don Quixote turn back; on the contrary he

[018-204]went on shouting out, “Ho, knights, ye who follow and fight under the

[018-205]banners of the valiant emperor Pentapolin of the Bare Arm, follow me

[018-206]all; ye shall see how easily I shall give him his revenge over his

[018-207]enemy Alifanfaron of the Trapobana.”

[018-208]So saying, he dashed into the midst of the squadron of ewes, and began

[018-209]spearing them with as much spirit and intrepidity as if he were

[018-210]transfixing mortal enemies in earnest. The shepherds and drovers

[018-211]accompanying the flock shouted to him to desist; seeing it was no use,

[018-212]they ungirt their slings and began to salute his ears with stones as

[018-213]big as one’s fist. Don Quixote gave no heed to the stones, but, letting

[018-214]drive right and left kept saying:

[018-215]“Where art thou, proud Alifanfaron? Come before me; I am a single

[018-216]knight who would fain prove thy prowess hand to hand, and make thee

[018-217]yield thy life a penalty for the wrong thou dost to the valiant

[018-218]Pentapolin Garamanta.” Here came a sugar-plum from the brook that

[018-219]struck him on the side and buried a couple of ribs in his body. Feeling

[018-220]himself so smitten, he imagined himself slain or badly wounded for

[018-221]certain, and recollecting his liquor he drew out his flask, and putting

[018-222]it to his mouth began to pour the contents into his stomach; but ere he

[018-223]had succeeded in swallowing what seemed to him enough, there came

[018-224]another almond which struck him on the hand and on the flask so fairly

[018-225]that it smashed it to pieces, knocking three or four teeth and grinders

[018-226]out of his mouth in its course, and sorely crushing two fingers of his

[018-227]hand. Such was the force of the first blow and of the second, that the

[018-228]poor knight in spite of himself came down backwards off his horse. The

[018-229]shepherds came up, and felt sure they had killed him; so in all haste

[018-230]they collected their flock together, took up the dead beasts, of which

[018-231]there were more than seven, and made off without waiting to ascertain

[018-232]anything further.

[018-233]All this time Sancho stood on the hill watching the crazy feats his

[018-234]master was performing, and tearing his beard and cursing the hour and

[018-235]the occasion when fortune had made him acquainted with him. Seeing him,

[018-236]then, brought to the ground, and that the shepherds had taken

[018-237]themselves off, he ran to him and found him in very bad case, though

[018-238]not unconscious; and said he:

[018-239]“Did I not tell you to come back, Señor Don Quixote; and that what you

[018-240]were going to attack were not armies but droves of sheep?”

[018-241]“That’s how that thief of a sage, my enemy, can alter and falsify

[018-242]things,” answered Don Quixote; “thou must know, Sancho, that it is a

[018-243]very easy matter for those of his sort to make us believe what they

[018-244]choose; and this malignant being who persecutes me, envious of the

[018-245]glory he knew I was to win in this battle, has turned the squadrons of

[018-246]the enemy into droves of sheep. At any rate, do this much, I beg of

[018-247]thee, Sancho, to undeceive thyself, and see that what I say is true;

[018-248]mount thy ass and follow them quietly, and thou shalt see that when

[018-249]they have gone some little distance from this they will return to their

[018-250]original shape and, ceasing to be sheep, become men in all respects as

[018-251]I described them to thee at first. But go not just yet, for I want thy

[018-252]help and assistance; come hither, and see how many of my teeth and

[018-253]grinders are missing, for I feel as if there was not one left in my

[018-254]mouth.”

[018-255]Sancho came so close that he almost put his eyes into his mouth; now

[018-256]just at that moment the balsam had acted on the stomach of Don Quixote,

[018-257]so, at the very instant when Sancho came to examine his mouth, he

[018-258]discharged all its contents with more force than a musket, and full

[018-259]into the beard of the compassionate squire.

[018-260]“Holy Mary!” cried Sancho, “what is this that has happened me? Clearly

[018-261]this sinner is mortally wounded, as he vomits blood from the mouth;”

[018-262]but considering the matter a little more closely he perceived by the

[018-263]colour, taste, and smell, that it was not blood but the balsam from the

[018-264]flask which he had seen him drink; and he was taken with such a

[018-265]loathing that his stomach turned, and he vomited up his inside over his

[018-266]very master, and both were left in a precious state. Sancho ran to his

[018-267]ass to get something wherewith to clean himself, and relieve his

[018-268]master, out of his alforjas; but not finding them, he well-nigh took

[018-269]leave of his senses, and cursed himself anew, and in his heart resolved

[018-270]to quit his master and return home, even though he forfeited the wages

[018-271]of his service and all hopes of the promised island.

[018-272]Don Quixote now rose, and putting his left hand to his mouth to keep

[018-273]his teeth from falling out altogether, with the other he laid hold of

[018-274]the bridle of Rocinante, who had never stirred from his master’s

[018-275]side—so loyal and well-behaved was he—and betook himself to where the

[018-276]squire stood leaning over his ass with his hand to his cheek, like one

[018-277]in deep dejection. Seeing him in this mood, looking so sad, Don Quixote

[018-278]said to him:

[018-279]“Bear in mind, Sancho, that one man is no more than another, unless he

[018-280]does more than another; all these tempests that fall upon us are signs

[018-281]that fair weather is coming shortly, and that things will go well with

[018-282]us, for it is impossible for good or evil to last for ever; and hence

[018-283]it follows that the evil having lasted long, the good must be now nigh

[018-284]at hand; so thou must not distress thyself at the misfortunes which

[018-285]happen to me, since thou hast no share in them.”

[018-286]“How have I not?” replied Sancho; “was he whom they blanketed yesterday

[018-287]perchance any other than my father’s son? and the alforjas that are

[018-288]missing to-day with all my treasures, did they belong to any other but

[018-289]myself?”

[018-290]“What! are the alforjas missing, Sancho?” said Don Quixote.

[018-291]“Yes, they are missing,” answered Sancho.

[018-292]“In that case we have nothing to eat to-day,” replied Don Quixote.

[018-293]“It would be so,” answered Sancho, “if there were none of the herbs

[018-294]your worship says you know in these meadows, those with which

[018-295]knights-errant as unlucky as your worship are wont to supply such-like

[018-296]shortcomings.”

[018-297]“For all that,” answered Don Quixote, “I would rather have just now a

[018-298]quarter of bread, or a loaf and a couple of pilchards’ heads, than all

[018-299]the herbs described by Dioscorides, even with Doctor Laguna’s notes.

[018-300]Nevertheless, Sancho the Good, mount thy beast and come along with me,

[018-301]for God, who provides for all things, will not fail us (more especially

[018-302]when we are so active in his service as we are), since he fails not the

[018-303]midges of the air, nor the grubs of the earth, nor the tadpoles of the

[018-304]water, and is so merciful that he maketh his sun to rise on the good

[018-305]and on the evil, and sendeth rain on the unjust and on the just.”

[018-306]“Your worship would make a better preacher than knight-errant,” said

[018-307]Sancho.

[018-308]“Knights-errant knew and ought to know everything, Sancho,” said Don

[018-309]Quixote; “for there were knights-errant in former times as well

[018-310]qualified to deliver a sermon or discourse in the middle of an

[018-311]encampment, as if they had graduated in the University of Paris;

[018-312]whereby we may see that the lance has never blunted the pen, nor the

[018-313]pen the lance.”

[018-314]“Well, be it as your worship says,” replied Sancho; “let us be off now

[018-315]and find some place of shelter for the night, and God grant it may be

[018-316]somewhere where there are no blankets, nor blanketeers, nor phantoms,

[018-317]nor enchanted Moors; for if there are, may the devil take the whole

[018-318]concern.”

[018-319]“Ask that of God, my son,” said Don Quixote; and do thou lead on where

[018-320]thou wilt, for this time I leave our lodging to thy choice; but reach

[018-321]me here thy hand, and feel with thy finger, and find out how many of my

[018-322]teeth and grinders are missing from this right side of the upper jaw,

[018-323]for it is there I feel the pain.”

[018-324]Sancho put in his fingers, and feeling about asked him, “How many

[018-325]grinders used your worship have on this side?”

[018-326]“Four,” replied Don Quixote, “besides the back-tooth, all whole and

[018-327]quite sound.”

[018-328]“Mind what you are saying, señor.”

[018-329]“I say four, if not five,” answered Don Quixote, “for never in my life

[018-330]have I had tooth or grinder drawn, nor has any fallen out or been

[018-331]destroyed by any decay or rheum.”

[018-332]“Well, then,” said Sancho, “in this lower side your worship has no more

[018-333]than two grinders and a half, and in the upper neither a half nor any

[018-334]at all, for it is all as smooth as the palm of my hand.”

[018-335]“Luckless that I am!” said Don Quixote, hearing the sad news his squire

[018-336]gave him; “I had rather they despoiled me of an arm, so it were not the

[018-337]sword-arm; for I tell thee, Sancho, a mouth without teeth is like a

[018-338]mill without a millstone, and a tooth is much more to be prized than a

[018-339]diamond; but we who profess the austere order of chivalry are liable to

[018-340]all this. Mount, friend, and lead the way, and I will follow thee at

[018-341]whatever pace thou wilt.”

[018-342]Sancho did as he bade him, and proceeded in the direction in which he

[018-343]thought he might find refuge without quitting the high road, which was

[018-344]there very much frequented. As they went along, then, at a slow

[018-345]pace—for the pain in Don Quixote’s jaws kept him uneasy and

[018-346]ill-disposed for speed—Sancho thought it well to amuse and divert him

[018-347]by talk of some kind, and among the things he said to him was that

[018-348]which will be told in the following chapter.
