[037-001]IN WHICH IS CONTINUED THE STORY OF THE FAMOUS PRINCESS MICOMICONA, WITH

OTHER DROLL ADVENTURES

[037-002]To all this Sancho listened with no little sorrow at heart to see how

[037-003]his hopes of dignity were fading away and vanishing in smoke, and how

[037-004]the fair Princess Micomicona had turned into Dorothea, and the giant

[037-005]into Don Fernando, while his master was sleeping tranquilly, totally

[037-006]unconscious of all that had come to pass. Dorothea was unable to

[037-007]persuade herself that her present happiness was not all a dream;

[037-008]Cardenio was in a similar state of mind, and Luscinda’s thoughts ran in

[037-009]the same direction. Don Fernando gave thanks to Heaven for the favour

[037-010]shown to him and for having been rescued from the intricate labyrinth

[037-011]in which he had been brought so near the destruction of his good name

[037-012]and of his soul; and in short everybody in the inn was full of

[037-013]contentment and satisfaction at the happy issue of such a complicated

[037-014]and hopeless business. The curate as a sensible man made sound

[037-015]reflections upon the whole affair, and congratulated each upon his good

[037-016]fortune; but the one that was in the highest spirits and good humour

[037-017]was the landlady, because of the promise Cardenio and the curate had

[037-018]given her to pay for all the losses and damage she had sustained

[037-019]through Don Quixote’s means. Sancho, as has been already said, was the

[037-020]only one who was distressed, unhappy, and dejected; and so with a long

[037-021]face he went in to his master, who had just awoke, and said to him:

[037-022]“Sir Rueful Countenance, your worship may as well sleep on as much as

[037-023]you like, without troubling yourself about killing any giant or

[037-024]restoring her kingdom to the princess; for that is all over and settled

[037-025]now.”

[037-026]“I should think it was,” replied Don Quixote, “for I have had the most

[037-027]prodigious and stupendous battle with the giant that I ever remember

[037-028]having had all the days of my life; and with one back-stroke—swish!—I

[037-029]brought his head tumbling to the ground, and so much blood gushed forth

[037-030]from him that it ran in rivulets over the earth like water.”

[037-031]“Like red wine, your worship had better say,” replied Sancho; “for I

[037-032]would have you know, if you don’t know it, that the dead giant is a

[037-033]hacked wine-skin, and the blood four-and-twenty gallons of red wine

[037-034]that it had in its belly, and the cut-off head is the bitch that bore

[037-035]me; and the devil take it all.”

[037-036]“What art thou talking about, fool?” said Don Quixote; “art thou in thy

[037-037]senses?”

[037-038]“Let your worship get up,” said Sancho, “and you will see the nice

[037-039]business you have made of it, and what we have to pay; and you will see

[037-040]the queen turned into a private lady called Dorothea, and other things

[037-041]that will astonish you, if you understand them.”

[037-042]“I shall not be surprised at anything of the kind,” returned Don

[037-043]Quixote; “for if thou dost remember the last time we were here I told

[037-044]thee that everything that happened here was a matter of enchantment,

[037-045]and it would be no wonder if it were the same now.”

[037-046]“I could believe all that,” replied Sancho, “if my blanketing was the

[037-047]same sort of thing also; only it wasn’t, but real and genuine; for I

[037-048]saw the landlord, who is here to-day, holding one end of the blanket

[037-049]and jerking me up to the skies very neatly and smartly, and with as

[037-050]much laughter as strength; and when it comes to be a case of knowing

[037-051]people, I hold for my part, simple and sinner as I am, that there is no

[037-052]enchantment about it at all, but a great deal of bruising and bad

[037-053]luck.”

[037-054]“Well, well, God will give a remedy,” said Don Quixote; “hand me my

[037-055]clothes and let me go out, for I want to see these transformations and

[037-056]things thou speakest of.”

[037-057]Sancho fetched him his clothes; and while he was dressing, the curate

[037-058]gave Don Fernando and the others present an account of Don Quixote’s

[037-059]madness and of the stratagem they had made use of to withdraw him from

[037-060]that Pena Pobre where he fancied himself stationed because of his

[037-061]lady’s scorn. He described to them also nearly all the adventures that

[037-062]Sancho had mentioned, at which they marvelled and laughed not a little,

[037-063]thinking it, as all did, the strangest form of madness a crazy

[037-064]intellect could be capable of. But now, the curate said, that the lady

[037-065]Dorothea’s good fortune prevented her from proceeding with their

[037-066]purpose, it would be necessary to devise or discover some other way of

[037-067]getting him home.

[037-068]Cardenio proposed to carry out the scheme they had begun, and suggested

[037-069]that Luscinda would act and support Dorothea’s part sufficiently well.

[037-070]“No,” said Don Fernando, “that must not be, for I want Dorothea to

[037-071]follow out this idea of hers; and if the worthy gentleman’s village is

[037-072]not very far off, I shall be happy if I can do anything for his

[037-073]relief.”

[037-074]“It is not more than two days’ journey from this,” said the curate.

[037-075]“Even if it were more,” said Don Fernando, “I would gladly travel so

[037-076]far for the sake of doing so good a work.”

[037-077]At this moment Don Quixote came out in full panoply, with Mambrino’s

[037-078]helmet, all dinted as it was, on his head, his buckler on his arm, and

[037-079]leaning on his staff or pike. The strange figure he presented filled

[037-080]Don Fernando and the rest with amazement as they contemplated his lean

[037-081]yellow face half a league long, his armour of all sorts, and the

[037-082]solemnity of his deportment. They stood silent waiting to see what he

[037-083]would say, and he, fixing his eyes on the fair Dorothea, addressed her

[037-084]with great gravity and composure:

[037-085]“I am informed, fair lady, by my squire here that your greatness has

[037-086]been annihilated and your being abolished, since, from a queen and lady

[037-087]of high degree as you used to be, you have been turned into a private

[037-088]maiden. If this has been done by the command of the magician king your

[037-089]father, through fear that I should not afford you the aid you need and

[037-090]are entitled to, I may tell you he did not know and does not know half

[037-091]the mass, and was little versed in the annals of chivalry; for, if he

[037-092]had read and gone through them as attentively and deliberately as I

[037-093]have, he would have found at every turn that knights of less renown

[037-094]than mine have accomplished things more difficult: it is no great

[037-095]matter to kill a whelp of a giant, however arrogant he may be; for it

[037-096]is not many hours since I myself was engaged with one, and—I will not

[037-097]speak of it, that they may not say I am lying; time, however, that

[037-098]reveals all, will tell the tale when we least expect it.”

[037-099]“You were engaged with a couple of wine-skins, and not a giant,” said

[037-100]the landlord at this; but Don Fernando told him to hold his tongue and

[037-101]on no account interrupt Don Quixote, who continued, “I say in

[037-102]conclusion, high and disinherited lady, that if your father has brought

[037-103]about this metamorphosis in your person for the reason I have

[037-104]mentioned, you ought not to attach any importance to it; for there is

[037-105]no peril on earth through which my sword will not force a way, and with

[037-106]it, before many days are over, I will bring your enemy’s head to the

[037-107]ground and place on yours the crown of your kingdom.”

[037-108]Don Quixote said no more, and waited for the reply of the princess, who

[037-109]aware of Don Fernando’s determination to carry on the deception until

[037-110]Don Quixote had been conveyed to his home, with great ease of manner

[037-111]and gravity made answer, “Whoever told you, valiant Knight of the

[037-112]Rueful Countenance, that I had undergone any change or transformation

[037-113]did not tell you the truth, for I am the same as I was yesterday. It is

[037-114]true that certain strokes of good fortune, that have given me more than

[037-115]I could have hoped for, have made some alteration in me; but I have not

[037-116]therefore ceased to be what I was before, or to entertain the same

[037-117]desire I have had all through of availing myself of the might of your

[037-118]valiant and invincible arm. And so, señor, let your goodness reinstate

[037-119]the father that begot me in your good opinion, and be assured that he

[037-120]was a wise and prudent man, since by his craft he found out such a sure

[037-121]and easy way of remedying my misfortune; for I believe, señor, that had

[037-122]it not been for you I should never have lit upon the good fortune I now

[037-123]possess; and in this I am saying what is perfectly true; as most of

[037-124]these gentlemen who are present can fully testify. All that remains is

[037-125]to set out on our journey to-morrow, for to-day we could not make much

[037-126]way; and for the rest of the happy result I am looking forward to, I

[037-127]trust to God and the valour of your heart.”

[037-128]So said the sprightly Dorothea, and on hearing her Don Quixote turned

[037-129]to Sancho, and said to him, with an angry air, “I declare now, little

[037-130]Sancho, thou art the greatest little villain in Spain. Say, thief and

[037-131]vagabond, hast thou not just now told me that this princess had been

[037-132]turned into a maiden called Dorothea, and that the head which I am

[037-133]persuaded I cut off from a giant was the bitch that bore thee, and

[037-134]other nonsense that put me in the greatest perplexity I have ever been

[037-135]in all my life? I vow” (and here he looked to heaven and ground his

[037-136]teeth) “I have a mind to play the mischief with thee, in a way that

[037-137]will teach sense for the future to all lying squires of knights-errant

[037-138]in the world.”

[037-139]“Let your worship be calm, señor,” returned Sancho, “for it may well be

[037-140]that I have been mistaken as to the change of the lady princess

[037-141]Micomicona; but as to the giant’s head, or at least as to the piercing

[037-142]of the wine-skins, and the blood being red wine, I make no mistake, as

[037-143]sure as there is a God; because the wounded skins are there at the head

[037-144]of your worship’s bed, and the wine has made a lake of the room; if not

[037-145]you will see when the eggs come to be fried; I mean when his worship

[037-146]the landlord calls for all the damages: for the rest, I am heartily

[037-147]glad that her ladyship the queen is as she was, for it concerns me as

[037-148]much as anyone.”

[037-149]“I tell thee again, Sancho, thou art a fool,” said Don Quixote;

[037-150]“forgive me, and that will do.”

[037-151]“That will do,” said Don Fernando; “let us say no more about it; and as

[037-152]her ladyship the princess proposes to set out to-morrow because it is

[037-153]too late to-day, so be it, and we will pass the night in pleasant

[037-154]conversation, and to-morrow we will all accompany Señor Don Quixote;

[037-155]for we wish to witness the valiant and unparalleled achievements he is

[037-156]about to perform in the course of this mighty enterprise which he has

[037-157]undertaken.”

[037-158]“It is I who shall wait upon and accompany you,” said Don Quixote; “and

[037-159]I am much gratified by the favour that is bestowed upon me, and the

[037-160]good opinion entertained of me, which I shall strive to justify or it

[037-161]shall cost me my life, or even more, if it can possibly cost me more.”

[037-162]Many were the compliments and expressions of politeness that passed

[037-163]between Don Quixote and Don Fernando; but they were brought to an end

[037-164]by a traveller who at this moment entered the inn, and who seemed from

[037-165]his attire to be a Christian lately come from the country of the Moors,

[037-166]for he was dressed in a short-skirted coat of blue cloth with

[037-167]half-sleeves and without a collar; his breeches were also of blue

[037-168]cloth, and his cap of the same colour, and he wore yellow buskins and

[037-169]had a Moorish cutlass slung from a baldric across his breast. Behind

[037-170]him, mounted upon an ass, there came a woman dressed in Moorish

[037-171]fashion, with her face veiled and a scarf on her head, and wearing a

[037-172]little brocaded cap, and a mantle that covered her from her shoulders

[037-173]to her feet. The man was of a robust and well-proportioned frame, in

[037-174]age a little over forty, rather swarthy in complexion, with long

[037-175]moustaches and a full beard, and, in short, his appearance was such

[037-176]that if he had been well dressed he would have been taken for a person

[037-177]of quality and good birth. On entering he asked for a room, and when

[037-178]they told him there was none in the inn he seemed distressed, and

[037-179]approaching her who by her dress seemed to be a Moor, he took her down

[037-180]from the saddle in his arms. Luscinda, Dorothea, the landlady, her

[037-181]daughter and Maritornes, attracted by the strange, and to them entirely

[037-182]new costume, gathered round her; and Dorothea, who was always kindly,

[037-183]courteous, and quick-witted, perceiving that both she and the man who

[037-184]had brought her were annoyed at not finding a room, said to her, “Do

[037-185]not be put out, señora, by the discomfort and want of luxuries here,

[037-186]for it is the way of road-side inns to be without them; still, if you

[037-187]will be pleased to share our lodging with us (pointing to Luscinda)

[037-188]perhaps you will have found worse accommodation in the course of your

[037-189]journey.”

[037-190]To this the veiled lady made no reply; all she did was to rise from her

[037-191]seat, crossing her hands upon her bosom, bowing her head and bending

[037-192]her body as a sign that she returned thanks. From her silence they

[037-193]concluded that she must be a Moor and unable to speak a Christian

[037-194]tongue.

[037-195]At this moment the captive came up, having been until now otherwise

[037-196]engaged, and seeing that they all stood round his companion and that

[037-197]she made no reply to what they addressed to her, he said, “Ladies, this

[037-198]damsel hardly understands my language and can speak none but that of

[037-199]her own country, for which reason she does not and cannot answer what

[037-200]has been asked of her.”

[037-201]“Nothing has been asked of her,” returned Luscinda; “she has only been

[037-202]offered our company for this evening and a share of the quarters we

[037-203]occupy, where she shall be made as comfortable as the circumstances

[037-204]allow, with the good-will we are bound to show all strangers that stand

[037-205]in need of it, especially if it be a woman to whom the service is

[037-206]rendered.”

[037-207]“On her part and my own, señora,” replied the captive, “I kiss your

[037-208]hands, and I esteem highly, as I ought, the favour you have offered,

[037-209]which, on such an occasion and coming from persons of your appearance,

[037-210]is, it is plain to see, a very great one.”

[037-211]“Tell me, señor,” said Dorothea, “is this lady a Christian or a Moor?

[037-212]for her dress and her silence lead us to imagine that she is what we

[037-213]could wish she was not.”

[037-214]“In dress and outwardly,” said he, “she is a Moor, but at heart she is

[037-215]a thoroughly good Christian, for she has the greatest desire to become

[037-216]one.”

[037-217]“Then she has not been baptised?” returned Luscinda.

[037-218]“There has been no opportunity for that,” replied the captive, “since

[037-219]she left Algiers, her native country and home; and up to the present

[037-220]she has not found herself in any such imminent danger of death as to

[037-221]make it necessary to baptise her before she has been instructed in all

[037-222]the ceremonies our holy mother Church ordains; but, please God, ere

[037-223]long she shall be baptised with the solemnity befitting her which is

[037-224]higher than her dress or mine indicates.”

[037-225]By these words he excited a desire in all who heard him, to know who

[037-226]the Moorish lady and the captive were, but no one liked to ask just

[037-227]then, seeing that it was a fitter moment for helping them to rest

[037-228]themselves than for questioning them about their lives. Dorothea took

[037-229]the Moorish lady by the hand and leading her to a seat beside herself,

[037-230]requested her to remove her veil. She looked at the captive as if to

[037-231]ask him what they meant and what she was to do. He said to her in

[037-232]Arabic that they asked her to take off her veil, and thereupon she

[037-233]removed it and disclosed a countenance so lovely, that to Dorothea she

[037-234]seemed more beautiful than Luscinda, and to Luscinda more beautiful

[037-235]than Dorothea, and all the bystanders felt that if any beauty could

[037-236]compare with theirs it was the Moorish lady’s, and there were even

[037-237]those who were inclined to give it somewhat the preference. And as it

[037-238]is the privilege and charm of beauty to win the heart and secure

[037-239]good-will, all forthwith became eager to show kindness and attention to

[037-240]the lovely Moor.

[037-241]Don Fernando asked the captive what her name was, and he replied that

[037-242]it was Lela Zoraida; but the instant she heard him, she guessed what

[037-243]the Christian had asked, and said hastily, with some displeasure and

[037-244]energy, “No, not Zoraida; Maria, Maria!” giving them to understand that

[037-245]she was called “Maria” and not “Zoraida.” These words, and the touching

[037-246]earnestness with which she uttered them, drew more than one tear from

[037-247]some of the listeners, particularly the women, who are by nature

[037-248]tender-hearted and compassionate. Luscinda embraced her affectionately,

[037-249]saying, “Yes, yes, Maria, Maria,” to which the Moor replied, “Yes, yes,

[037-250]Maria; Zoraida macange,” which means “not Zoraida.”

[037-251]Night was now approaching, and by the orders of those who accompanied

[037-252]Don Fernando the landlord had taken care and pains to prepare for them

[037-253]the best supper that was in his power. The hour therefore having

[037-254]arrived they all took their seats at a long table like a refectory one,

[037-255]for round or square table there was none in the inn, and the seat of

[037-256]honour at the head of it, though he was for refusing it, they assigned

[037-257]to Don Quixote, who desired the lady Micomicona to place herself by his

[037-258]side, as he was her protector. Luscinda and Zoraida took their places

[037-259]next her, opposite to them were Don Fernando and Cardenio, and next the

[037-260]captive and the other gentlemen, and by the side of the ladies, the

[037-261]curate and the barber. And so they supped in high enjoyment, which was

[037-262]increased when they observed Don Quixote leave off eating, and, moved

[037-263]by an impulse like that which made him deliver himself at such length

[037-264]when he supped with the goatherds, begin to address them:

[037-265]“Verily, gentlemen, if we reflect upon it, great and marvellous are the

[037-266]things they see, who make profession of the order of knight-errantry.

[037-267]Say, what being is there in this world, who entering the gate of this

[037-268]castle at this moment, and seeing us as we are here, would suppose or

[037-269]imagine us to be what we are? Who would say that this lady who is

[037-270]beside me was the great queen that we all know her to be, or that I am

[037-271]that Knight of the Rueful Countenance, trumpeted far and wide by the

[037-272]mouth of Fame? Now, there can be no doubt that this art and calling

[037-273]surpasses all those that mankind has invented, and is the more

[037-274]deserving of being held in honour in proportion as it is the more

[037-275]exposed to peril. Away with those who assert that letters have the

[037-276]preeminence over arms; I will tell them, whosoever they may be, that

[037-277]they know not what they say. For the reason which such persons commonly

[037-278]assign, and upon which they chiefly rest, is, that the labours of the

[037-279]mind are greater than those of the body, and that arms give employment

[037-280]to the body alone; as if the calling were a porter’s trade, for which

[037-281]nothing more is required than sturdy strength; or as if, in what we who

[037-282]profess them call arms, there were not included acts of vigour for the

[037-283]execution of which high intelligence is requisite; or as if the soul of

[037-284]the warrior, when he has an army, or the defence of a city under his

[037-285]care, did not exert itself as much by mind as by body. Nay; see whether

[037-286]by bodily strength it be possible to learn or divine the intentions of

[037-287]the enemy, his plans, stratagems, or obstacles, or to ward off

[037-288]impending mischief; for all these are the work of the mind, and in them

[037-289]the body has no share whatever. Since, therefore, arms have need of the

[037-290]mind, as much as letters, let us see now which of the two minds, that

[037-291]of the man of letters or that of the warrior, has most to do; and this

[037-292]will be seen by the end and goal that each seeks to attain; for that

[037-293]purpose is the more estimable which has for its aim the nobler object.

[037-294]The end and goal of letters—I am not speaking now of divine letters,

[037-295]the aim of which is to raise and direct the soul to Heaven; for with an

[037-296]end so infinite no other can be compared—I speak of human letters, the

[037-297]end of which is to establish distributive justice, give to every man

[037-298]that which is his, and see and take care that good laws are observed:

[037-299]an end undoubtedly noble, lofty, and deserving of high praise, but not

[037-300]such as should be given to that sought by arms, which have for their

[037-301]end and object peace, the greatest boon that men can desire in this

[037-302]life. The first good news the world and mankind received was that which

[037-303]the angels announced on the night that was our day, when they sang in

[037-304]the air, ‘Glory to God in the highest, and peace on earth to men of

[037-305]good-will;’ and the salutation which the great Master of heaven and

[037-306]earth taught his disciples and chosen followers when they entered any

[037-307]house, was to say, ‘Peace be on this house;’ and many other times he

[037-308]said to them, ‘My peace I give unto you, my peace I leave you, peace be

[037-309]with you;’ a jewel and a precious gift given and left by such a hand: a

[037-310]jewel without which there can be no happiness either on earth or in

[037-311]heaven. This peace is the true end of war; and war is only another name

[037-312]for arms. This, then, being admitted, that the end of war is peace, and

[037-313]that so far it has the advantage of the end of letters, let us turn to

[037-314]the bodily labours of the man of letters, and those of him who follows

[037-315]the profession of arms, and see which are the greater.”

[037-316]Don Quixote delivered his discourse in such a manner and in such

[037-317]correct language, that for the time being he made it impossible for any

[037-318]of his hearers to consider him a madman; on the contrary, as they were

[037-319]mostly gentlemen, to whom arms are an appurtenance by birth, they

[037-320]listened to him with great pleasure as he continued: “Here, then, I say

[037-321]is what the student has to undergo; first of all poverty: not that all

[037-322]are poor, but to put the case as strongly as possible: and when I have

[037-323]said that he endures poverty, I think nothing more need be said about

[037-324]his hard fortune, for he who is poor has no share of the good things of

[037-325]life. This poverty he suffers from in various ways, hunger, or cold, or

[037-326]nakedness, or all together; but for all that it is not so extreme but

[037-327]that he gets something to eat, though it may be at somewhat

[037-328]unseasonable hours and from the leavings of the rich; for the greatest

[037-329]misery of the student is what they themselves call ‘going out for

[037-330]soup,’ and there is always some neighbour’s brazier or hearth for them,

[037-331]which, if it does not warm, at least tempers the cold to them, and

[037-332]lastly, they sleep comfortably at night under a roof. I will not go

[037-333]into other particulars, as for example want of shirts, and no

[037-334]superabundance of shoes, thin and threadbare garments, and gorging

[037-335]themselves to surfeit in their voracity when good luck has treated them

[037-336]to a banquet of some sort. By this road that I have described, rough

[037-337]and hard, stumbling here, falling there, getting up again to fall

[037-338]again, they reach the rank they desire, and that once attained, we have

[037-339]seen many who have passed these Syrtes and Scyllas and Charybdises, as

[037-340]if borne flying on the wings of favouring fortune; we have seen them, I

[037-341]say, ruling and governing the world from a chair, their hunger turned

[037-342]into satiety, their cold into comfort, their nakedness into fine

[037-343]raiment, their sleep on a mat into repose in holland and damask, the

[037-344]justly earned reward of their virtue; but, contrasted and compared with

[037-345]what the warrior undergoes, all they have undergone falls far short of

[037-346]it, as I am now about to show.”
