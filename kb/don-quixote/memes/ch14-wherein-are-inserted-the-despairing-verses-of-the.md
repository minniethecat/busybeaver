THE LAY OF CHRYSOSTOM

[014-002]Since thou dost in thy cruelty desire

[014-003]The ruthless rigour of thy tyranny

[014-004]From tongue to tongue, from land to land proclaimed,

[014-005]The very Hell will I constrain to lend

[014-006]This stricken breast of mine deep notes of woe

[014-007]To serve my need of fitting utterance.

[014-008]And as I strive to body forth the tale

[014-009]Of all I suffer, all that thou hast done,

[014-010]Forth shall the dread voice roll, and bear along

[014-011]Shreds from my vitals torn for greater pain.

[014-012]Then listen, not to dulcet harmony,

[014-013]But to a discord wrung by mad despair

[014-014]Out of this bosom’s depths of bitterness,

[014-015]To ease my heart and plant a sting in thine.

[014-016]The lion’s roar, the fierce wolf’s savage howl,

[014-017]The horrid hissing of the scaly snake,

[014-018]The awesome cries of monsters yet unnamed,

[014-019]The crow’s ill-boding croak, the hollow moan

[014-020]Of wild winds wrestling with the restless sea,

[014-021]The wrathful bellow of the vanquished bull,

[014-022]The plaintive sobbing of the widowed dove,

[014-023]The envied owl’s sad note, the wail of woe

[014-024]That rises from the dreary choir of Hell,

[014-025]Commingled in one sound, confusing sense,

[014-026]Let all these come to aid my soul’s complaint,

[014-027]For pain like mine demands new modes of song.

[014-028]No echoes of that discord shall be heard

[014-029]Where Father Tagus rolls, or on the banks

[014-030]Of olive-bordered Betis; to the rocks

[014-031]Or in deep caverns shall my plaint be told,

[014-032]And by a lifeless tongue in living words;

[014-033]Or in dark valleys or on lonely shores,

[014-034]Where neither foot of man nor sunbeam falls;

[014-035]Or in among the poison-breathing swarms

[014-036]Of monsters nourished by the sluggish Nile.

[014-037]For, though it be to solitudes remote

[014-038]The hoarse vague echoes of my sorrows sound

[014-039]Thy matchless cruelty, my dismal fate

[014-040]Shall carry them to all the spacious world.

[014-041]Disdain hath power to kill, and patience dies

[014-042]Slain by suspicion, be it false or true;

[014-043]And deadly is the force of jealousy;

[014-044]Long absence makes of life a dreary void;

[014-045]No hope of happiness can give repose

[014-046]To him that ever fears to be forgot;

[014-047]And death, inevitable, waits in hall.

[014-048]But I, by some strange miracle, live on

[014-049]A prey to absence, jealousy, disdain;

[014-050]Racked by suspicion as by certainty;

[014-051]Forgotten, left to feed my flame alone.

[014-052]And while I suffer thus, there comes no ray

[014-053]Of hope to gladden me athwart the gloom;

[014-054]Nor do I look for it in my despair;

[014-055]But rather clinging to a cureless woe,

[014-056]All hope do I abjure for evermore.

[014-057]Can there be hope where fear is? Were it well,

[014-058]When far more certain are the grounds of fear?

[014-059]Ought I to shut mine eyes to jealousy,

[014-060]If through a thousand heart-wounds it appears?

[014-061]Who would not give free access to distrust,

[014-062]Seeing disdain unveiled, and—bitter change!—

[014-063]All his suspicions turned to certainties,

[014-064]And the fair truth transformed into a lie?

[014-065]Oh, thou fierce tyrant of the realms of love,

[014-066]Oh, Jealousy! put chains upon these hands,

[014-067]And bind me with thy strongest cord, Disdain.

[014-068]But, woe is me! triumphant over all,

[014-069]My sufferings drown the memory of you.

[014-070]And now I die, and since there is no hope

[014-071]Of happiness for me in life or death,

[014-072]Still to my fantasy I’ll fondly cling.

[014-073]I’ll say that he is wise who loveth well,

[014-074]And that the soul most free is that most bound

[014-075]In thraldom to the ancient tyrant Love.

[014-076]I’ll say that she who is mine enemy

[014-077]In that fair body hath as fair a mind,

[014-078]And that her coldness is but my desert,

[014-079]And that by virtue of the pain he sends

[014-080]Love rules his kingdom with a gentle sway.

[014-081]Thus, self-deluding, and in bondage sore,

[014-082]And wearing out the wretched shred of life

[014-083]To which I am reduced by her disdain,

[014-084]I’ll give this soul and body to the winds,

[014-085]All hopeless of a crown of bliss in store.

[014-086]Thou whose injustice hath supplied the cause

[014-087]That makes me quit the weary life I loathe,

[014-088]As by this wounded bosom thou canst see

[014-089]How willingly thy victim I become,

[014-090]Let not my death, if haply worth a tear,

[014-091]Cloud the clear heaven that dwells in thy bright eyes;

[014-092]I would not have thee expiate in aught

[014-093]The crime of having made my heart thy prey;

[014-094]But rather let thy laughter gaily ring

[014-095]And prove my death to be thy festival.

[014-096]Fool that I am to bid thee! well I know

[014-097]Thy glory gains by my untimely end.

[014-098]And now it is the time; from Hell’s abyss

[014-099]Come thirsting Tantalus, come Sisyphus

[014-100]Heaving the cruel stone, come Tityus

[014-101]With vulture, and with wheel Ixion come,

[014-102]And come the sisters of the ceaseless toil;

[014-103]And all into this breast transfer their pains,

[014-104]And (if such tribute to despair be due)

[014-105]Chant in their deepest tones a doleful dirge

[014-106]Over a corse unworthy of a shroud.

[014-107]Let the three-headed guardian of the gate,

[014-108]And all the monstrous progeny of hell,

[014-109]The doleful concert join: a lover dead

[014-110]Methinks can have no fitter obsequies.

[014-111]Lay of despair, grieve not when thou art gone

[014-112]Forth from this sorrowing heart: my misery

[014-113]Brings fortune to the cause that gave thee birth;

[014-114]Then banish sadness even in the tomb.

[014-115]The “Lay of Chrysostom” met with the approbation of the listeners,

[014-116]though the reader said it did not seem to him to agree with what he had

[014-117]heard of Marcela’s reserve and propriety, for Chrysostom complained in

[014-118]it of jealousy, suspicion, and absence, all to the prejudice of the

[014-119]good name and fame of Marcela; to which Ambrosio replied as one who

[014-120]knew well his friend’s most secret thoughts, “Señor, to remove that

[014-121]doubt I should tell you that when the unhappy man wrote this lay he was

[014-122]away from Marcela, from whom he had voluntarily separated himself, to

[014-123]try if absence would act with him as it is wont; and as everything

[014-124]distresses and every fear haunts the banished lover, so imaginary

[014-125]jealousies and suspicions, dreaded as if they were true, tormented

[014-126]Chrysostom; and thus the truth of what report declares of the virtue of

[014-127]Marcela remains unshaken, and with her envy itself should not and

[014-128]cannot find any fault save that of being cruel, somewhat haughty, and

[014-129]very scornful.”

[014-130]“That is true,” said Vivaldo; and as he was about to read another paper

[014-131]of those he had preserved from the fire, he was stopped by a marvellous

[014-132]vision (for such it seemed) that unexpectedly presented itself to their

[014-133]eyes; for on the summit of the rock where they were digging the grave

[014-134]there appeared the shepherdess Marcela, so beautiful that her beauty

[014-135]exceeded its reputation. Those who had never till then beheld her gazed

[014-136]upon her in wonder and silence, and those who were accustomed to see

[014-137]her were not less amazed than those who had never seen her before. But

[014-138]the instant Ambrosio saw her he addressed her, with manifest

[014-139]indignation:

[014-140]“Art thou come, by chance, cruel basilisk of these mountains, to see if

[014-141]in thy presence blood will flow from the wounds of this wretched being

[014-142]thy cruelty has robbed of life; or is it to exult over the cruel work

[014-143]of thy humours that thou art come; or like another pitiless Nero to

[014-144]look down from that height upon the ruin of his Rome in embers; or in

[014-145]thy arrogance to trample on this ill-fated corpse, as the ungrateful

[014-146]daughter trampled on her father Tarquin’s? Tell us quickly for what

[014-147]thou art come, or what it is thou wouldst have, for, as I know the

[014-148]thoughts of Chrysostom never failed to obey thee in life, I will make

[014-149]all these who call themselves his friends obey thee, though he be

[014-150]dead.”

[014-151]“I come not, Ambrosio for any of the purposes thou hast named,” replied

[014-152]Marcela, “but to defend myself and to prove how unreasonable are all

[014-153]those who blame me for their sorrow and for Chrysostom’s death; and

[014-154]therefore I ask all of you that are here to give me your attention, for

[014-155]it will not take much time or many words to bring the truth home to

[014-156]persons of sense. Heaven has made me, so you say, beautiful, and so

[014-157]much so that in spite of yourselves my beauty leads you to love me; and

[014-158]for the love you show me you say, and even urge, that I am bound to

[014-159]love you. By that natural understanding which God has given me I know

[014-160]that everything beautiful attracts love, but I cannot see how, by

[014-161]reason of being loved, that which is loved for its beauty is bound to

[014-162]love that which loves it; besides, it may happen that the lover of that

[014-163]which is beautiful may be ugly, and ugliness being detestable, it is

[014-164]very absurd to say, “I love thee because thou art beautiful, thou must

[014-165]love me though I be ugly.” But supposing the beauty equal on both

[014-166]sides, it does not follow that the inclinations must be therefore

[014-167]alike, for it is not every beauty that excites love, some but pleasing

[014-168]the eye without winning the affection; and if every sort of beauty

[014-169]excited love and won the heart, the will would wander vaguely to and

[014-170]fro unable to make choice of any; for as there is an infinity of

[014-171]beautiful objects there must be an infinity of inclinations, and true

[014-172]love, I have heard it said, is indivisible, and must be voluntary and

[014-173]not compelled. If this be so, as I believe it to be, why do you desire

[014-174]me to bend my will by force, for no other reason but that you say you

[014-175]love me? Nay—tell me—had Heaven made me ugly, as it has made me

[014-176]beautiful, could I with justice complain of you for not loving me?

[014-177]Moreover, you must remember that the beauty I possess was no choice of

[014-178]mine, for, be it what it may, Heaven of its bounty gave it me without

[014-179]my asking or choosing it; and as the viper, though it kills with it,

[014-180]does not deserve to be blamed for the poison it carries, as it is a

[014-181]gift of nature, neither do I deserve reproach for being beautiful; for

[014-182]beauty in a modest woman is like fire at a distance or a sharp sword;

[014-183]the one does not burn, the other does not cut, those who do not come

[014-184]too near. Honour and virtue are the ornaments of the mind, without

[014-185]which the body, though it be so, has no right to pass for beautiful;

[014-186]but if modesty is one of the virtues that specially lend a grace and

[014-187]charm to mind and body, why should she who is loved for her beauty part

[014-188]with it to gratify one who for his pleasure alone strives with all his

[014-189]might and energy to rob her of it? I was born free, and that I might

[014-190]live in freedom I chose the solitude of the fields; in the trees of the

[014-191]mountains I find society, the clear waters of the brooks are my

[014-192]mirrors, and to the trees and waters I make known my thoughts and

[014-193]charms. I am a fire afar off, a sword laid aside. Those whom I have

[014-194]inspired with love by letting them see me, I have by words undeceived,

[014-195]and if their longings live on hope—and I have given none to Chrysostom

[014-196]or to any other—it cannot justly be said that the death of any is my

[014-197]doing, for it was rather his own obstinacy than my cruelty that killed

[014-198]him; and if it be made a charge against me that his wishes were

[014-199]honourable, and that therefore I was bound to yield to them, I answer

[014-200]that when on this very spot where now his grave is made he declared to

[014-201]me his purity of purpose, I told him that mine was to live in perpetual

[014-202]solitude, and that the earth alone should enjoy the fruits of my

[014-203]retirement and the spoils of my beauty; and if, after this open avowal,

[014-204]he chose to persist against hope and steer against the wind, what

[014-205]wonder is it that he should sink in the depths of his infatuation? If I

[014-206]had encouraged him, I should be false; if I had gratified him, I should

[014-207]have acted against my own better resolution and purpose. He was

[014-208]persistent in spite of warning, he despaired without being hated.

[014-209]Bethink you now if it be reasonable that his suffering should be laid

[014-210]to my charge. Let him who has been deceived complain, let him give way

[014-211]to despair whose encouraged hopes have proved vain, let him flatter

[014-212]himself whom I shall entice, let him boast whom I shall receive; but

[014-213]let not him call me cruel or homicide to whom I make no promise, upon

[014-214]whom I practise no deception, whom I neither entice nor receive. It has

[014-215]not been so far the will of Heaven that I should love by fate, and to

[014-216]expect me to love by choice is idle. Let this general declaration serve

[014-217]for each of my suitors on his own account, and let it be understood

[014-218]from this time forth that if anyone dies for me it is not of jealousy

[014-219]or misery he dies, for she who loves no one can give no cause for

[014-220]jealousy to any, and candour is not to be confounded with scorn. Let

[014-221]him who calls me wild beast and basilisk, leave me alone as something

[014-222]noxious and evil; let him who calls me ungrateful, withhold his

[014-223]service; who calls me wayward, seek not my acquaintance; who calls me

[014-224]cruel, pursue me not; for this wild beast, this basilisk, this

[014-225]ungrateful, cruel, wayward being has no kind of desire to seek, serve,

[014-226]know, or follow them. If Chrysostom’s impatience and violent passion

[014-227]killed him, why should my modest behaviour and circumspection be

[014-228]blamed? If I preserve my purity in the society of the trees, why should

[014-229]he who would have me preserve it among men, seek to rob me of it? I

[014-230]have, as you know, wealth of my own, and I covet not that of others; my

[014-231]taste is for freedom, and I have no relish for constraint; I neither

[014-232]love nor hate anyone; I do not deceive this one or court that, or

[014-233]trifle with one or play with another. The modest converse of the

[014-234]shepherd girls of these hamlets and the care of my goats are my

[014-235]recreations; my desires are bounded by these mountains, and if they

[014-236]ever wander hence it is to contemplate the beauty of the heavens, steps

[014-237]by which the soul travels to its primeval abode.”

[014-238]With these words, and not waiting to hear a reply, she turned and

[014-239]passed into the thickest part of a wood that was hard by, leaving all

[014-240]who were there lost in admiration as much of her good sense as of her

[014-241]beauty. Some—those wounded by the irresistible shafts launched by her

[014-242]bright eyes—made as though they would follow her, heedless of the frank

[014-243]declaration they had heard; seeing which, and deeming this a fitting

[014-244]occasion for the exercise of his chivalry in aid of distressed damsels,

[014-245]Don Quixote, laying his hand on the hilt of his sword, exclaimed in a

[014-246]loud and distinct voice:

[014-247]“Let no one, whatever his rank or condition, dare to follow the

[014-248]beautiful Marcela, under pain of incurring my fierce indignation. She

[014-249]has shown by clear and satisfactory arguments that little or no fault

[014-250]is to be found with her for the death of Chrysostom, and also how far

[014-251]she is from yielding to the wishes of any of her lovers, for which

[014-252]reason, instead of being followed and persecuted, she should in justice

[014-253]be honoured and esteemed by all the good people of the world, for she

[014-254]shows that she is the only woman in it that holds to such a virtuous

[014-255]resolution.”

[014-256]Whether it was because of the threats of Don Quixote, or because

[014-257]Ambrosio told them to fulfil their duty to their good friend, none of

[014-258]the shepherds moved or stirred from the spot until, having finished the

[014-259]grave and burned Chrysostom’s papers, they laid his body in it, not

[014-260]without many tears from those who stood by. They closed the grave with

[014-261]a heavy stone until a slab was ready which Ambrosio said he meant to

[014-262]have prepared, with an epitaph which was to be to this effect:

[014-263]Beneath the stone before your eyes

[014-264]The body of a lover lies;

[014-265]In life he was a shepherd swain,

[014-266]In death a victim to disdain.

[014-267]Ungrateful, cruel, coy, and fair,

[014-268]Was she that drove him to despair,

[014-269]And Love hath made her his ally

[014-270]For spreading wide his tyranny.

[014-271]They then strewed upon the grave a profusion of flowers and branches,

[014-272]and all expressing their condolence with his friend Ambrosio, took

[014-273]their leave. Vivaldo and his companion did the same; and Don Quixote

[014-274]bade farewell to his hosts and to the travellers, who pressed him to

[014-275]come with them to Seville, as being such a convenient place for finding

[014-276]adventures, for they presented themselves in every street and round

[014-277]every corner oftener than anywhere else. Don Quixote thanked them for

[014-278]their advice and for the disposition they showed to do him a favour,

[014-279]and said that for the present he would not, and must not go to Seville

[014-280]until he had cleared all these mountains of highwaymen and robbers, of

[014-281]whom report said they were full. Seeing his good intention, the

[014-282]travellers were unwilling to press him further, and once more bidding

[014-283]him farewell, they left him and pursued their journey, in the course of

[014-284]which they did not fail to discuss the story of Marcela and Chrysostom

[014-285]as well as the madness of Don Quixote. He, on his part, resolved to go

[014-286]in quest of the shepherdess Marcela, and make offer to her of all the

[014-287]service he could render her; but things did not fall out with him as he

[014-288]expected, according to what is related in the course of this veracious

[014-289]history, of which the Second Part ends here.
