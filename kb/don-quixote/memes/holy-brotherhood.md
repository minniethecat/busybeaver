## The Santa Hermandad

The Holy Brotherhood (Santa Hermandad) was a real institution in Cervantes' Spain — a rural police force charged with maintaining order on the kingdom's highways. In *Don Quixote*, they represent the secular arm of the law, the institutional reality against which the knight's chivalric fantasies repeatedly collide.

Their most significant appearance comes in the aftermath of Don Quixote's liberation of the galley slaves. Having freed a dozen convicted criminals from the king's chain, Don Quixote has committed an actual crime under Spanish law. The Holy Brotherhood pursues him, and the threat of arrest hangs over the latter portion of Part I.

## Law vs. Chivalry

The Brotherhood's pursuit of Don Quixote exposes the fundamental incompatibility between chivalric morality and civil law. Don Quixote frees the galley slaves because his code demands that knights "right every kind of wrong" and liberate the oppressed. The Brotherhood pursues him because the same act constitutes a criminal offence — aiding the escape of convicted felons.

> The patrol of the Holy Brotherhood, hearing the uproar, came up to see what was the matter. (045-022)

The confrontation is never fully resolved in legal terms. Don Quixote escapes formal prosecution through a combination of luck, social status, and the intervention of others who recognize his madness. The novel acknowledges the law's claims while sidestepping their consequences — a pattern that reflects Cervantes' own complex relationship with Spanish institutions.
