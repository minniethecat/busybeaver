[055-001]OF THE LAUGHABLE CONVERSATION THAT PASSED BETWEEN DON QUIXOTE, SANCHO

[055-002]PANZA, AND THE BACHELOR SAMSON CARRASCO

p03a.jpg (131K)


[055-003]Don Quixote remained very deep in thought, waiting for the bachelor

[055-004]Carrasco, from whom he was to hear how he himself had been put into a

[055-005]book as Sancho said; and he could not persuade himself that any such

[055-006]history could be in existence, for the blood of the enemies he had

[055-007]slain was not yet dry on the blade of his sword, and now they wanted to

[055-008]make out that his mighty achievements were going about in print. For

[055-009]all that, he fancied some sage, either a friend or an enemy, might, by

[055-010]the aid of magic, have given them to the press; if a friend, in order

[055-011]to magnify and exalt them above the most famous ever achieved by any

[055-012]knight-errant; if an enemy, to bring them to naught and degrade them

[055-013]below the meanest ever recorded of any low squire, though as he said to

[055-014]himself, the achievements of squires never were recorded. If, however,

[055-015]it were the fact that such a history were in existence, it must

[055-016]necessarily, being the story of a knight-errant, be grandiloquent,

[055-017]lofty, imposing, grand and true. With this he comforted himself

[055-018]somewhat, though it made him uncomfortable to think that the author was

[055-019]a Moor, judging by the title of “Cide;” and that no truth was to be

[055-020]looked for from Moors, as they are all impostors, cheats, and schemers.

[055-021]He was afraid he might have dealt with his love affairs in some

[055-022]indecorous fashion, that might tend to the discredit and prejudice of

[055-023]the purity of his lady Dulcinea del Toboso; he would have had him set

[055-024]forth the fidelity and respect he had always observed towards her,

[055-025]spurning queens, empresses, and damsels of all sorts, and keeping in

[055-026]check the impetuosity of his natural impulses. Absorbed and wrapped up

[055-027]in these and divers other cogitations, he was found by Sancho and

[055-028]Carrasco, whom Don Quixote received with great courtesy.

[055-029]The bachelor, though he was called Samson, was of no great bodily size,

[055-030]but he was a very great wag; he was of a sallow complexion, but very

[055-031]sharp-witted, somewhere about four-and-twenty years of age, with a

[055-032]round face, a flat nose, and a large mouth, all indications of a

[055-033]mischievous disposition and a love of fun and jokes; and of this he

[055-034]gave a sample as soon as he saw Don Quixote, by falling on his knees

[055-035]before him and saying, “Let me kiss your mightiness’s hand, Señor Don

[055-036]Quixote of La Mancha, for, by the habit of St. Peter that I wear,

[055-037]though I have no more than the first four orders, your worship is one

[055-038]of the most famous knights-errant that have ever been, or will be, all

[055-039]the world over. A blessing on Cide Hamete Benengeli, who has written

[055-040]the history of your great deeds, and a double blessing on that

[055-041]connoisseur who took the trouble of having it translated out of the

[055-042]Arabic into our Castilian vulgar tongue for the universal entertainment

[055-043]of the people!”

[055-044]Don Quixote made him rise, and said, “So, then, it is true that there

[055-045]is a history of me, and that it was a Moor and a sage who wrote it?”

[055-046]“So true is it, señor,” said Samson, “that my belief is there are more

[055-047]than twelve thousand volumes of the said history in print this very

[055-048]day. Only ask Portugal, Barcelona, and Valencia, where they have been

[055-049]printed, and moreover there is a report that it is being printed at

[055-050]Antwerp, and I am persuaded there will not be a country or language in

[055-051]which there will not be a translation of it.”

[055-052]“One of the things,” here observed Don Quixote, “that ought to give

[055-053]most pleasure to a virtuous and eminent man is to find himself in his

[055-054]lifetime in print and in type, familiar in people’s mouths with a good

[055-055]name; I say with a good name, for if it be the opposite, then there is

[055-056]no death to be compared to it.”

[055-057]“If it goes by good name and fame,” said the bachelor, “your worship

[055-058]alone bears away the palm from all the knights-errant; for the Moor in

[055-059]his own language, and the Christian in his, have taken care to set

[055-060]before us your gallantry, your high courage in encountering dangers,

[055-061]your fortitude in adversity, your patience under misfortunes as well as

[055-062]wounds, the purity and continence of the platonic loves of your worship

[055-063]and my lady Doña Dulcinea del Toboso—”

[055-064]“I never heard my lady Dulcinea called Doña,” observed Sancho here;

[055-065]“nothing more than the lady Dulcinea del Toboso; so here already the

[055-066]history is wrong.”

[055-067]“That is not an objection of any importance,” replied Carrasco.

[055-068]“Certainly not,” said Don Quixote; “but tell me, señor bachelor, what

[055-069]deeds of mine are they that are made most of in this history?”

[055-070]“On that point,” replied the bachelor, “opinions differ, as tastes do;

[055-071]some swear by the adventure of the windmills that your worship took to

[055-072]be Briareuses and giants; others by that of the fulling mills; one

[055-073]cries up the description of the two armies that afterwards took the

[055-074]appearance of two droves of sheep; another that of the dead body on its

[055-075]way to be buried at Segovia; a third says the liberation of the galley

[055-076]slaves is the best of all, and a fourth that nothing comes up to the

[055-077]affair with the Benedictine giants, and the battle with the valiant

[055-078]Biscayan.”

[055-079]“Tell me, señor bachelor,” said Sancho at this point, “does the

[055-080]adventure with the Yanguesans come in, when our good Rocinante went

[055-081]hankering after dainties?”

[055-082]“The sage has left nothing in the ink-bottle,” replied Samson; “he

[055-083]tells all and sets down everything, even to the capers that worthy

[055-084]Sancho cut in the blanket.”

[055-085]“I cut no capers in the blanket,” returned Sancho; “in the air I did,

[055-086]and more of them than I liked.”

[055-087]“There is no human history in the world, I suppose,” said Don Quixote,

[055-088]“that has not its ups and downs, but more than others such as deal with

[055-089]chivalry, for they can never be entirely made up of prosperous

[055-090]adventures.”

[055-091]“For all that,” replied the bachelor, “there are those who have read

[055-092]the history who say they would have been glad if the author had left

[055-093]out some of the countless cudgellings that were inflicted on Señor Don

[055-094]Quixote in various encounters.”

[055-095]“That’s where the truth of the history comes in,” said Sancho.

[055-096]“At the same time they might fairly have passed them over in silence,”

[055-097]observed Don Quixote; “for there is no need of recording events which

[055-098]do not change or affect the truth of a history, if they tend to bring

[055-099]the hero of it into contempt. Æneas was not in truth and earnest so

[055-100]pious as Virgil represents him, nor Ulysses so wise as Homer describes

[055-101]him.”

[055-102]“That is true,” said Samson; “but it is one thing to write as a poet,

[055-103]another to write as a historian; the poet may describe or sing things,

[055-104]not as they were, but as they ought to have been; but the historian has

[055-105]to write them down, not as they ought to have been, but as they were,

[055-106]without adding anything to the truth or taking anything from it.”

[055-107]“Well then,” said Sancho, “if this señor Moor goes in for telling the

[055-108]truth, no doubt among my master’s drubbings mine are to be found; for

[055-109]they never took the measure of his worship’s shoulders without doing

[055-110]the same for my whole body; but I have no right to wonder at that, for,

[055-111]as my master himself says, the members must share the pain of the

[055-112]head.”

[055-113]“You are a sly dog, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “i’ faith, you have no

[055-114]want of memory when you choose to remember.”

[055-115]“If I were to try to forget the thwacks they gave me,” said Sancho, “my

[055-116]weals would not let me, for they are still fresh on my ribs.”

[055-117]“Hush, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and don’t interrupt the bachelor,

[055-118]whom I entreat to go on and tell all that is said about me in this

[055-119]history.”

[055-120]“And about me,” said Sancho, “for they say, too, that I am one of the

[055-121]principal presonages in it.”

[055-122]“Personages, not presonages, friend Sancho,” said Samson.

[055-123]“What! Another word-catcher!” said Sancho; “if that’s to be the way we

[055-124]shall not make an end in a lifetime.”

[055-125]“May God shorten mine, Sancho,” returned the bachelor, “if you are not

[055-126]the second person in the history, and there are even some who would

[055-127]rather hear you talk than the cleverest in the whole book; though there

[055-128]are some, too, who say you showed yourself over-credulous in believing

[055-129]there was any possibility in the government of that island offered you

[055-130]by Señor Don Quixote.”

[055-131]“There is still sunshine on the wall,” said Don Quixote; “and when

[055-132]Sancho is somewhat more advanced in life, with the experience that

[055-133]years bring, he will be fitter and better qualified for being a

[055-134]governor than he is at present.”

[055-135]“By God, master,” said Sancho, “the island that I cannot govern with

[055-136]the years I have, I’ll not be able to govern with the years of

[055-137]Methuselah; the difficulty is that the said island keeps its distance

[055-138]somewhere, I know not where; and not that there is any want of head in

[055-139]me to govern it.”

[055-140]“Leave it to God, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “for all will be and

[055-141]perhaps better than you think; no leaf on the tree stirs but by God’s

[055-142]will.”

[055-143]“That is true,” said Samson; “and if it be God’s will, there will not

[055-144]be any want of a thousand islands, much less one, for Sancho to

[055-145]govern.”

[055-146]“I have seen governors in these parts,” said Sancho, “that are not to

[055-147]be compared to my shoe-sole; and for all that they are called ‘your

[055-148]lordship’ and served on silver.”

[055-149]“Those are not governors of islands,” observed Samson, “but of other

[055-150]governments of an easier kind: those that govern islands must at least

[055-151]know grammar.”

[055-152]“I could manage the gram well enough,” said Sancho; “but for the mar I

[055-153]have neither leaning nor liking, for I don’t know what it is; but

[055-154]leaving this matter of the government in God’s hands, to send me

[055-155]wherever it may be most to his service, I may tell you, señor bachelor

[055-156]Samson Carrasco, it has pleased me beyond measure that the author of

[055-157]this history should have spoken of me in such a way that what is said

[055-158]of me gives no offence; for, on the faith of a true squire, if he had

[055-159]said anything about me that was at all unbecoming an old Christian,

[055-160]such as I am, the deaf would have heard of it.”

[055-161]“That would be working miracles,” said Samson.

[055-162]“Miracles or no miracles,” said Sancho, “let everyone mind how he

[055-163]speaks or writes about people, and not set down at random the first

[055-164]thing that comes into his head.”

[055-165]“One of the faults they find with this history,” said the bachelor, “is

[055-166]that its author inserted in it a novel called ‘The Ill-advised

[055-167]Curiosity;’ not that it is bad or ill-told, but that it is out of place

[055-168]and has nothing to do with the history of his worship Señor Don

[055-169]Quixote.”

[055-170]“I will bet the son of a dog has mixed the cabbages and the baskets,”

[055-171]said Sancho.

[055-172]“Then, I say,” said Don Quixote, “the author of my history was no sage,

[055-173]but some ignorant chatterer, who, in a haphazard and heedless way, set

[055-174]about writing it, let it turn out as it might, just as Orbaneja, the

[055-175]painter of Úbeda, used to do, who, when they asked him what he was

[055-176]painting, answered, ‘What it may turn out.’ Sometimes he would paint a

[055-177]cock in such a fashion, and so unlike, that he had to write alongside

[055-178]of it in Gothic letters, ‘This is a cock;’ and so it will be with my

[055-179]history, which will require a commentary to make it intelligible.”

[055-180]“No fear of that,” returned Samson, “for it is so plain that there is

[055-181]nothing in it to puzzle over; the children turn its leaves, the young

[055-182]people read it, the grown men understand it, the old folk praise it; in

[055-183]a word, it is so thumbed, and read, and got by heart by people of all

[055-184]sorts, that the instant they see any lean hack, they say, ‘There goes

[055-185]Rocinante.’ And those that are most given to reading it are the pages,

[055-186]for there is not a lord’s ante-chamber where there is not a ‘Don

[055-187]Quixote’ to be found; one takes it up if another lays it down; this one

[055-188]pounces upon it, and that begs for it. In short, the said history is

[055-189]the most delightful and least injurious entertainment that has been

[055-190]hitherto seen, for there is not to be found in the whole of it even the

[055-191]semblance of an immodest word, or a thought that is other than

[055-192]Catholic.”

[055-193]“To write in any other way,” said Don Quixote, “would not be to write

[055-194]truth, but falsehood, and historians who have recourse to falsehood

[055-195]ought to be burned, like those who coin false money; and I know not

[055-196]what could have led the author to have recourse to novels and

[055-197]irrelevant stories, when he had so much to write about in mine; no

[055-198]doubt he must have gone by the proverb ‘with straw or with hay, &c.,’

[055-199]for by merely setting forth my thoughts, my sighs, my tears, my lofty

[055-200]purposes, my enterprises, he might have made a volume as large, or

[055-201]larger than all the works of El Tostado would make up. In fact, the

[055-202]conclusion I arrive at, señor bachelor, is, that to write histories, or

[055-203]books of any kind, there is need of great judgment and a ripe

[055-204]understanding. To give expression to humour, and write in a strain of

[055-205]graceful pleasantry, is the gift of great geniuses. The cleverest

[055-206]character in comedy is the clown, for he who would make people take him

[055-207]for a fool, must not be one. History is in a measure a sacred thing,

[055-208]for it should be true, and where the truth is, there God is; but

[055-209]notwithstanding this, there are some who write and fling books

[055-210]broadcast on the world as if they were fritters.”

[055-211]“There is no book so bad but it has something good in it,” said the

[055-212]bachelor.

[055-213]“No doubt of that,” replied Don Quixote; “but it often happens that

[055-214]those who have acquired and attained a well-deserved reputation by

[055-215]their writings, lose it entirely, or damage it in some degree, when

[055-216]they give them to the press.”

[055-217]“The reason of that,” said Samson, “is, that as printed works are

[055-218]examined leisurely, their faults are easily seen; and the greater the

[055-219]fame of the writer, the more closely are they scrutinised. Men famous

[055-220]for their genius, great poets, illustrious historians, are always, or

[055-221]most commonly, envied by those who take a particular delight and

[055-222]pleasure in criticising the writings of others, without having produced

[055-223]any of their own.”

[055-224]“That is no wonder,” said Don Quixote; “for there are many divines who

[055-225]are no good for the pulpit, but excellent in detecting the defects or

[055-226]excesses of those who preach.”

[055-227]“All that is true, Señor Don Quixote,” said Carrasco; “but I wish such

[055-228]fault-finders were more lenient and less exacting, and did not pay so

[055-229]much attention to the spots on the bright sun of the work they grumble

[055-230]at; for if _aliquando bonus dormitat Homerus_, they should remember how

[055-231]long he remained awake to shed the light of his work with as little

[055-232]shade as possible; and perhaps it may be that what they find fault with

[055-233]may be moles, that sometimes heighten the beauty of the face that bears

[055-234]them; and so I say very great is the risk to which he who prints a book

[055-235]exposes himself, for of all impossibilities the greatest is to write

[055-236]one that will satisfy and please all readers.”

[055-237]“That which treats of me must have pleased few,” said Don Quixote.

[055-238]“Quite the contrary,” said the bachelor; “for, as _stultorum infinitum

[055-239]est numerus_, innumerable are those who have relished the said history;

[055-240]but some have brought a charge against the author’s memory, inasmuch as

[055-241]he forgot to say who the thief was who stole Sancho’s Dapple; for it is

[055-242]not stated there, but only to be inferred from what is set down, that

[055-243]he was stolen, and a little farther on we see Sancho mounted on the

[055-244]same ass, without any reappearance of it. They say, too, that he forgot

[055-245]to state what Sancho did with those hundred crowns that he found in the

[055-246]valise in the Sierra Morena, as he never alludes to them again, and

[055-247]there are many who would be glad to know what he did with them, or what

[055-248]he spent them on, for it is one of the serious omissions of the work.”

[055-249]“Señor Samson, I am not in a humour now for going into accounts or

[055-250]explanations,” said Sancho; “for there’s a sinking of the stomach come

[055-251]over me, and unless I doctor it with a couple of sups of the old stuff

[055-252]it will put me on the thorn of Santa Lucia. I have it at home, and my

[055-253]old woman is waiting for me; after dinner I’ll come back, and will

[055-254]answer you and all the world every question you may choose to ask, as

[055-255]well about the loss of the ass as about the spending of the hundred

[055-256]crowns;” and without another word or waiting for a reply he made off

[055-257]home.

[055-258]Don Quixote begged and entreated the bachelor to stay and do penance

[055-259]with him. The bachelor accepted the invitation and remained, a couple

[055-260]of young pigeons were added to the ordinary fare, at dinner they talked

[055-261]chivalry, Carrasco fell in with his host’s humour, the banquet came to

[055-262]an end, they took their afternoon sleep, Sancho returned, and their

[055-263]conversation was resumed.
