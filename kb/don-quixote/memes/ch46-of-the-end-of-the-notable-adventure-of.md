OF THE END OF THE NOTABLE ADVENTURE OF THE OFFICERS OF THE HOLY

[046-001]BROTHERHOOD; AND OF THE GREAT FEROCITY OF OUR WORTHY KNIGHT, DON

QUIXOTE

[046-002]While Don Quixote was talking in this strain, the curate was

[046-003]endeavouring to persuade the officers that he was out of his senses, as

[046-004]they might perceive by his deeds and his words, and that they need not

[046-005]press the matter any further, for even if they arrested him and carried

[046-006]him off, they would have to release him by-and-by as a madman; to which

[046-007]the holder of the warrant replied that he had nothing to do with

[046-008]inquiring into Don Quixote’s madness, but only to execute his

[046-009]superior’s orders, and that once taken they might let him go three

[046-010]hundred times if they liked.

[046-011]“For all that,” said the curate, “you must not take him away this time,

[046-012]nor will he, it is my opinion, let himself be taken away.”

[046-013]In short, the curate used such arguments, and Don Quixote did such mad

[046-014]things, that the officers would have been more mad than he was if they

[046-015]had not perceived his want of wits, and so they thought it best to

[046-016]allow themselves to be pacified, and even to act as peacemakers between

[046-017]the barber and Sancho Panza, who still continued their altercation with

[046-018]much bitterness. In the end they, as officers of justice, settled the

[046-019]question by arbitration in such a manner that both sides were, if not

[046-020]perfectly contented, at least to some extent satisfied; for they

[046-021]changed the pack-saddles, but not the girths or head-stalls; and as to

[046-022]Mambrino’s helmet, the curate, under the rose and without Don Quixote’s

[046-023]knowing it, paid eight reals for the basin, and the barber executed a

[046-024]full receipt and engagement to make no further demand then or

[046-025]thenceforth for evermore, amen. These two disputes, which were the most

[046-026]important and gravest, being settled, it only remained for the servants

[046-027]of Don Luis to consent that three of them should return while one was

[046-028]left to accompany him whither Don Fernando desired to take him; and

[046-029]good luck and better fortune, having already begun to solve

[046-030]difficulties and remove obstructions in favour of the lovers and

[046-031]warriors of the inn, were pleased to persevere and bring everything to

[046-032]a happy issue; for the servants agreed to do as Don Luis wished; which

[046-033]gave Doña Clara such happiness that no one could have looked into her

[046-034]face just then without seeing the joy of her heart. Zoraida, though she

[046-035]did not fully comprehend all she saw, was grave or gay without knowing

[046-036]why, as she watched and studied the various countenances, but

[046-037]particularly her Spaniard’s, whom she followed with her eyes and clung

[046-038]to with her soul. The gift and compensation which the curate gave the

[046-039]barber had not escaped the landlord’s notice, and he demanded Don

[046-040]Quixote’s reckoning, together with the amount of the damage to his

[046-041]wine-skins, and the loss of his wine, swearing that neither Rocinante

[046-042]nor Sancho’s ass should leave the inn until he had been paid to the

[046-043]very last farthing. The curate settled all amicably, and Don Fernando

[046-044]paid; though the Judge had also very readily offered to pay the score;

[046-045]and all became so peaceful and quiet that the inn no longer reminded

[046-046]one of the discord of Agramante’s camp, as Don Quixote said, but of the

[046-047]peace and tranquillity of the days of Octavianus: for all which it was

[046-048]the universal opinion that their thanks were due to the great zeal and

[046-049]eloquence of the curate, and to the unexampled generosity of Don

[046-050]Fernando.

[046-051]Finding himself now clear and quit of all quarrels, his squire’s as

[046-052]well as his own, Don Quixote considered that it would be advisable to

[046-053]continue the journey he had begun, and bring to a close that great

[046-054]adventure for which he had been called and chosen; and with this high

[046-055]resolve he went and knelt before Dorothea, who, however, would not

[046-056]allow him to utter a word until he had risen; so to obey her he rose,

[046-057]and said, “It is a common proverb, fair lady, that ‘diligence is the

[046-058]mother of good fortune,’ and experience has often shown in important

[046-059]affairs that the earnestness of the negotiator brings the doubtful case

[046-060]to a successful termination; but in nothing does this truth show itself

[046-061]more plainly than in war, where quickness and activity forestall the

[046-062]devices of the enemy, and win the victory before the foe has time to

[046-063]defend himself. All this I say, exalted and esteemed lady, because it

[046-064]seems to me that for us to remain any longer in this castle now is

[046-065]useless, and may be injurious to us in a way that we shall find out

[046-066]some day; for who knows but that your enemy the giant may have learned

[046-067]by means of secret and diligent spies that I am going to destroy him,

[046-068]and if the opportunity be given him he may seize it to fortify himself

[046-069]in some impregnable castle or stronghold, against which all my efforts

[046-070]and the might of my indefatigable arm may avail but little? Therefore,

[046-071]lady, let us, as I say, forestall his schemes by our activity, and let

[046-072]us depart at once in quest of fair fortune; for your highness is only

[046-073]kept from enjoying it as fully as you could desire by my delay in

[046-074]encountering your adversary.”

[046-075]Don Quixote held his peace and said no more, calmly awaiting the reply

[046-076]of the beauteous princess, who, with commanding dignity and in a style

[046-077]adapted to Don Quixote’s own, replied to him in these words, “I give

[046-078]you thanks, sir knight, for the eagerness you, like a good knight to

[046-079]whom it is a natural obligation to succour the orphan and the needy,

[046-080]display to afford me aid in my sore trouble; and heaven grant that your

[046-081]wishes and mine may be realised, so that you may see that there are

[046-082]women in this world capable of gratitude; as to my departure, let it be

[046-083]forthwith, for I have no will but yours; dispose of me entirely in

[046-084]accordance with your good pleasure; for she who has once entrusted to

[046-085]you the defence of her person, and placed in your hands the recovery of

[046-086]her dominions, must not think of offering opposition to that which your

[046-087]wisdom may ordain.”

[046-088]“On, then, in God’s name,” said Don Quixote; “for, when a lady humbles

[046-089]herself to me, I will not lose the opportunity of raising her up and

[046-090]placing her on the throne of her ancestors. Let us depart at once, for

[046-091]the common saying that in delay there is danger, lends spurs to my

[046-092]eagerness to take the road; and as neither heaven has created nor hell

[046-093]seen any that can daunt or intimidate me, saddle Rocinante, Sancho, and

[046-094]get ready thy ass and the queen’s palfrey, and let us take leave of the

[046-095]castellan and these gentlemen, and go hence this very instant.”

[046-096]Sancho, who was standing by all the time, said, shaking his head, “Ah!

[046-097]master, master, there is more mischief in the village than one hears

[046-098]of, begging all good bodies’ pardon.”

[046-099]“What mischief can there be in any village, or in all the cities of the

[046-100]world, you booby, that can hurt my reputation?” said Don Quixote.

[046-101]“If your worship is angry,” replied Sancho, “I will hold my tongue and

[046-102]leave unsaid what as a good squire I am bound to say, and what a good

[046-103]servant should tell his master.”

[046-104]“Say what thou wilt,” returned Don Quixote, “provided thy words be not

[046-105]meant to work upon my fears; for thou, if thou fearest, art behaving

[046-106]like thyself; but I like myself, in not fearing.”

[046-107]“It is nothing of the sort, as I am a sinner before God,” said Sancho,

[046-108]“but that I take it to be sure and certain that this lady, who calls

[046-109]herself queen of the great kingdom of Micomicon, is no more so than my

[046-110]mother; for, if she was what she says, she would not go rubbing noses

[046-111]with one that is here every instant and behind every door.”

[046-112]Dorothea turned red at Sancho’s words, for the truth was that her

[046-113]husband Don Fernando had now and then, when the others were not

[046-114]looking, gathered from her lips some of the reward his love had earned,

[046-115]and Sancho seeing this had considered that such freedom was more like a

[046-116]courtesan than a queen of a great kingdom; she, however, being unable

[046-117]or not caring to answer him, allowed him to proceed, and he continued,

[046-118]“This I say, señor, because, if after we have travelled roads and

[046-119]highways, and passed bad nights and worse days, one who is now enjoying

[046-120]himself in this inn is to reap the fruit of our labours, there is no

[046-121]need for me to be in a hurry to saddle Rocinante, put the pad on the

[046-122]ass, or get ready the palfrey; for it will be better for us to stay

[046-123]quiet, and let every jade mind her spinning, and let us go to dinner.”

[046-124]Good God, what was the indignation of Don Quixote when he heard the

[046-125]audacious words of his squire! So great was it, that in a voice

[046-126]inarticulate with rage, with a stammering tongue, and eyes that flashed

[046-127]living fire, he exclaimed, “Rascally clown, boorish, insolent, and

[046-128]ignorant, ill-spoken, foul-mouthed, impudent backbiter and slanderer!

[046-129]Hast thou dared to utter such words in my presence and in that of these

[046-130]illustrious ladies? Hast thou dared to harbour such gross and shameless

[046-131]thoughts in thy muddled imagination? Begone from my presence, thou born

[046-132]monster, storehouse of lies, hoard of untruths, garner of knaveries,

[046-133]inventor of scandals, publisher of absurdities, enemy of the respect

[046-134]due to royal personages! Begone, show thyself no more before me under

[046-135]pain of my wrath;” and so saying he knitted his brows, puffed out his

[046-136]cheeks, gazed around him, and stamped on the ground violently with his

[046-137]right foot, showing in every way the rage that was pent up in his

[046-138]heart; and at his words and furious gestures Sancho was so scared and

[046-139]terrified that he would have been glad if the earth had opened that

[046-140]instant and swallowed him, and his only thought was to turn round and

[046-141]make his escape from the angry presence of his master.

[046-142]But the ready-witted Dorothea, who by this time so well understood Don

[046-143]Quixote’s humour, said, to mollify his wrath, “Be not irritated at the

[046-144]absurdities your good squire has uttered, Sir Knight of the Rueful

[046-145]Countenance, for perhaps he did not utter them without cause, and from

[046-146]his good sense and Christian conscience it is not likely that he would

[046-147]bear false witness against anyone. We may therefore believe, without

[046-148]any hesitation, that since, as you say, sir knight, everything in this

[046-149]castle goes and is brought about by means of enchantment, Sancho, I

[046-150]say, may possibly have seen, through this diabolical medium, what he

[046-151]says he saw so much to the detriment of my modesty.”

[046-152]“I swear by God Omnipotent,” exclaimed Don Quixote at this, “your

[046-153]highness has hit the point; and that some vile illusion must have come

[046-154]before this sinner of a Sancho, that made him see what it would have

[046-155]been impossible to see by any other means than enchantments; for I know

[046-156]well enough, from the poor fellow’s goodness and harmlessness, that he

[046-157]is incapable of bearing false witness against anybody.”

[046-158]“True, no doubt,” said Don Fernando, “for which reason, Señor Don

[046-159]Quixote, you ought to forgive him and restore him to the bosom of your

[046-160]favour, _sicut erat in principio_, before illusions of this sort had

[046-161]taken away his senses.”

[046-162]Don Quixote said he was ready to pardon him, and the curate went for

[046-163]Sancho, who came in very humbly, and falling on his knees begged for

[046-164]the hand of his master, who having presented it to him and allowed him

[046-165]to kiss it, gave him his blessing and said, “Now, Sancho my son, thou

[046-166]wilt be convinced of the truth of what I have many a time told thee,

[046-167]that everything in this castle is done by means of enchantment.”

[046-168]“So it is, I believe,” said Sancho, “except the affair of the blanket,

[046-169]which came to pass in reality by ordinary means.”

[046-170]“Believe it not,” said Don Quixote, “for had it been so, I would have

[046-171]avenged thee that instant, or even now; but neither then nor now could

[046-172]I, nor have I seen anyone upon whom to avenge thy wrong.”

[046-173]They were all eager to know what the affair of the blanket was, and the

[046-174]landlord gave them a minute account of Sancho’s flights, at which they

[046-175]laughed not a little, and at which Sancho would have been no less out

[046-176]of countenance had not his master once more assured him it was all

[046-177]enchantment. For all that his simplicity never reached so high a pitch

[046-178]that he could persuade himself it was not the plain and simple truth,

[046-179]without any deception whatever about it, that he had been blanketed by

[046-180]beings of flesh and blood, and not by visionary and imaginary phantoms,

[046-181]as his master believed and protested.

[046-182]The illustrious company had now been two days in the inn; and as it

[046-183]seemed to them time to depart, they devised a plan so that, without

[046-184]giving Dorothea and Don Fernando the trouble of going back with Don

[046-185]Quixote to his village under pretence of restoring Queen Micomicona,

[046-186]the curate and the barber might carry him away with them as they

[046-187]proposed, and the curate be able to take his madness in hand at home;

[046-188]and in pursuance of their plan they arranged with the owner of an

[046-189]oxcart who happened to be passing that way to carry him after this

[046-190]fashion. They constructed a kind of cage with wooden bars, large enough

[046-191]to hold Don Quixote comfortably; and then Don Fernando and his

[046-192]companions, the servants of Don Luis, and the officers of the

[046-193]Brotherhood, together with the landlord, by the directions and advice

[046-194]of the curate, covered their faces and disguised themselves, some in

[046-195]one way, some in another, so as to appear to Don Quixote quite

[046-196]different from the persons he had seen in the castle. This done, in

[046-197]profound silence they entered the room where he was asleep, taking his

[046-198]rest after the past frays, and advancing to where he was sleeping

[046-199]tranquilly, not dreaming of anything of the kind happening, they seized

[046-200]him firmly and bound him fast hand and foot, so that, when he awoke

[046-201]startled, he was unable to move, and could only marvel and wonder at

[046-202]the strange figures he saw before him; upon which he at once gave way

[046-203]to the idea which his crazed fancy invariably conjured up before him,

[046-204]and took it into his head that all these shapes were phantoms of the

[046-205]enchanted castle, and that he himself was unquestionably enchanted as

[046-206]he could neither move nor help himself; precisely what the curate, the

[046-207]concoctor of the scheme, expected would happen. Of all that were there

[046-208]Sancho was the only one who was at once in his senses and in his own

[046-209]proper character, and he, though he was within very little of sharing

[046-210]his master’s infirmity, did not fail to perceive who all these

[046-211]disguised figures were; but he did not dare to open his lips until he

[046-212]saw what came of this assault and capture of his master; nor did the

[046-213]latter utter a word, waiting to the upshot of his mishap; which was

[046-214]that bringing in the cage, they shut him up in it and nailed the bars

[046-215]so firmly that they could not be easily burst open.

[046-216]They then took him on their shoulders, and as they passed out of the

[046-217]room an awful voice—as much so as the barber, not he of the pack-saddle

[046-218]but the other, was able to make it—was heard to say, “O Knight of the

[046-219]Rueful Countenance, let not this captivity in which thou art placed

[046-220]afflict thee, for this must needs be, for the more speedy

[046-221]accomplishment of the adventure in which thy great heart has engaged

[046-222]thee; the which shall be accomplished when the raging Manchegan lion

[046-223]and the white Tobosan dove shall be linked together, having first

[046-224]humbled their haughty necks to the gentle yoke of matrimony. And from

[046-225]this marvellous union shall come forth to the light of the world brave

[046-226]whelps that shall rival the ravening claws of their valiant father; and

[046-227]this shall come to pass ere the pursuer of the flying nymph shall in

[046-228]his swift natural course have twice visited the starry signs. And thou,

[046-229]O most noble and obedient squire that ever bore sword at side, beard on

[046-230]face, or nose to smell with, be not dismayed or grieved to see the

[046-231]flower of knight-errantry carried away thus before thy very eyes; for

[046-232]soon, if it so please the Framer of the universe, thou shalt see

[046-233]thyself exalted to such a height that thou shalt not know thyself, and

[046-234]the promises which thy good master has made thee shall not prove false;

[046-235]and I assure thee, on the authority of the sage Mentironiana, that thy

[046-236]wages shall be paid thee, as thou shalt see in due season. Follow then

[046-237]the footsteps of the valiant enchanted knight, for it is expedient that

[046-238]thou shouldst go to the destination assigned to both of you; and as it

[046-239]is not permitted to me to say more, God be with thee; for I return to

[046-240]that place I wot of;” and as he brought the prophecy to a close he

[046-241]raised his voice to a high pitch, and then lowered it to such a soft

[046-242]tone, that even those who knew it was all a joke were almost inclined

[046-243]to take what they heard seriously.

[046-244]Don Quixote was comforted by the prophecy he heard, for he at once

[046-245]comprehended its meaning perfectly, and perceived it was promised to

[046-246]him that he should see himself united in holy and lawful matrimony with

[046-247]his beloved Dulcinea del Toboso, from whose blessed womb should proceed

[046-248]the whelps, his sons, to the eternal glory of La Mancha; and being

[046-249]thoroughly and firmly persuaded of this, he lifted up his voice, and

[046-250]with a deep sigh exclaimed, “Oh thou, whoever thou art, who hast

[046-251]foretold me so much good, I implore of thee that on my part thou

[046-252]entreat that sage enchanter who takes charge of my interests, that he

[046-253]leave me not to perish in this captivity in which they are now carrying

[046-254]me away, ere I see fulfilled promises so joyful and incomparable as

[046-255]those which have been now made me; for, let this but come to pass, and

[046-256]I shall glory in the pains of my prison, find comfort in these chains

[046-257]wherewith they bind me, and regard this bed whereon they stretch me,

[046-258]not as a hard battle-field, but as a soft and happy nuptial couch; and

[046-259]touching the consolation of Sancho Panza, my squire, I rely upon his

[046-260]goodness and rectitude that he will not desert me in good or evil

[046-261]fortune; for if, by his ill luck or mine, it may not happen to be in my

[046-262]power to give him the island I have promised, or any equivalent for it,

[046-263]at least his wages shall not be lost; for in my will, which is already

[046-264]made, I have declared the sum that shall be paid to him, measured, not

[046-265]by his many faithful services, but by the means at my disposal.”

[046-266]Sancho bowed his head very respectfully and kissed both his hands, for,

[046-267]being tied together, he could not kiss one; and then the apparitions

[046-268]lifted the cage upon their shoulders and fixed it upon the ox-cart.
