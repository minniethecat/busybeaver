[071-001]IN WHICH IS RELATED THE ADVENTURE OF THE ENAMOURED SHEPHERD, TOGETHER

WITH OTHER TRULY DROLL INCIDENTS

p19a.jpg (131K)


[071-002]Don Quixote had gone but a short distance beyond Don Diego’s village,

[071-003]when he fell in with a couple of either priests or students, and a

[071-004]couple of peasants, mounted on four beasts of the ass kind. One of the

[071-005]students carried, wrapped up in a piece of green buckram by way of a

[071-006]portmanteau, what seemed to be a little linen and a couple of pairs of

[071-007]ribbed stockings; the other carried nothing but a pair of new

[071-008]fencing-foils with buttons. The peasants carried divers articles that

[071-009]showed they were on their way from some large town where they had

[071-010]bought them, and were taking them home to their village; and both

[071-011]students and peasants were struck with the same amazement that

[071-012]everybody felt who saw Don Quixote for the first time, and were dying

[071-013]to know who this man, so different from ordinary men, could be. Don

[071-014]Quixote saluted them, and after ascertaining that their road was the

[071-015]same as his, made them an offer of his company, and begged them to

[071-016]slacken their pace, as their young asses travelled faster than his

[071-017]horse; and then, to gratify them, he told them in a few words who he

[071-018]was and the calling and profession he followed, which was that of a

[071-019]knight-errant seeking adventures in all parts of the world. He informed

[071-020]them that his own name was Don Quixote of La Mancha, and that he was

[071-021]called, by way of surname, the Knight of the Lions.

[071-022]All this was Greek or gibberish to the peasants, but not so to the

[071-023]students, who very soon perceived the crack in Don Quixote’s pate; for

[071-024]all that, however, they regarded him with admiration and respect, and

[071-025]one of them said to him, “If you, sir knight, have no fixed road, as it

[071-026]is the way with those who seek adventures not to have any, let your

[071-027]worship come with us; you will see one of the finest and richest

[071-028]weddings that up to this day have ever been celebrated in La Mancha, or

[071-029]for many a league round.”

[071-030]Don Quixote asked him if it was some prince’s, that he spoke of it in

[071-031]this way. “Not at all,” said the student; “it is the wedding of a

[071-032]farmer and a farmer’s daughter, he the richest in all this country, and

[071-033]she the fairest mortal ever set eyes on. The display with which it is

[071-034]to be attended will be something rare and out of the common, for it

[071-035]will be celebrated in a meadow adjoining the town of the bride, who is

[071-036]called, _par excellence_, Quiteria the fair, as the bridegroom is

[071-037]called Camacho the rich. She is eighteen, and he twenty-two, and they

[071-038]are fairly matched, though some knowing ones, who have all the

[071-039]pedigrees in the world by heart, will have it that the family of the

[071-040]fair Quiteria is better than Camacho’s; but no one minds that

[071-041]now-a-days, for wealth can solder a great many flaws. At any rate,

[071-042]Camacho is free-handed, and it is his fancy to screen the whole meadow

[071-043]with boughs and cover it in overhead, so that the sun will have hard

[071-044]work if he tries to get in to reach the grass that covers the soil. He

[071-045]has provided dancers too, not only sword but also bell-dancers, for in

[071-046]his own town there are those who ring the changes and jingle the bells

[071-047]to perfection; of shoe-dancers I say nothing, for of them he has

[071-048]engaged a host. But none of these things, nor of the many others I have

[071-049]omitted to mention, will do more to make this a memorable wedding than

[071-050]the part which I suspect the despairing Basilio will play in it. This

[071-051]Basilio is a youth of the same village as Quiteria, and he lived in the

[071-052]house next door to that of her parents, of which circumstance Love took

[071-053]advantage to reproduce to the word the long-forgotten loves of Pyramus

[071-054]and Thisbe; for Basilio loved Quiteria from his earliest years, and she

[071-055]responded to his passion with countless modest proofs of affection, so

[071-056]that the loves of the two children, Basilio and Quiteria, were the talk

[071-057]and the amusement of the town. As they grew up, the father of Quiteria

[071-058]made up his mind to refuse Basilio his wonted freedom of access to the

[071-059]house, and to relieve himself of constant doubts and suspicions, he

[071-060]arranged a match for his daughter with the rich Camacho, as he did not

[071-061]approve of marrying her to Basilio, who had not so large a share of the

[071-062]gifts of fortune as of nature; for if the truth be told ungrudgingly,

[071-063]he is the most agile youth we know, a mighty thrower of the bar, a

[071-064]first-rate wrestler, and a great ball-player; he runs like a deer, and

[071-065]leaps better than a goat, bowls over the nine-pins as if by magic,

[071-066]sings like a lark, plays the guitar so as to make it speak, and, above

[071-067]all, handles a sword as well as the best.”

[071-068]“For that excellence alone,” said Don Quixote at this, “the youth

[071-069]deserves to marry, not merely the fair Quiteria, but Queen Guinevere

[071-070]herself, were she alive now, in spite of Launcelot and all who would

[071-071]try to prevent it.”

[071-072]“Say that to my wife,” said Sancho, who had until now listened in

[071-073]silence, “for she won’t hear of anything but each one marrying his

[071-074]equal, holding with the proverb ‘each ewe to her like.’ What I would

[071-075]like is that this good Basilio (for I am beginning to take a fancy to

[071-076]him already) should marry this lady Quiteria; and a blessing and good

[071-077]luck—I meant to say the opposite—on people who would prevent those who

[071-078]love one another from marrying.”

[071-079]“If all those who love one another were to marry,” said Don Quixote,

[071-080]“it would deprive parents of the right to choose, and marry their

[071-081]children to the proper person and at the proper time; and if it was

[071-082]left to daughters to choose husbands as they pleased, one would be for

[071-083]choosing her father’s servant, and another, someone she has seen

[071-084]passing in the street and fancies gallant and dashing, though he may be

[071-085]a drunken bully; for love and fancy easily blind the eyes of the

[071-086]judgment, so much wanted in choosing one’s way of life; and the

[071-087]matrimonial choice is very liable to error, and it needs great caution

[071-088]and the special favour of heaven to make it a good one. He who has to

[071-089]make a long journey, will, if he is wise, look out for some trusty and

[071-090]pleasant companion to accompany him before he sets out. Why, then,

[071-091]should not he do the same who has to make the whole journey of life

[071-092]down to the final halting-place of death, more especially when the

[071-093]companion has to be his companion in bed, at board, and everywhere, as

[071-094]the wife is to her husband? The companionship of one’s wife is no

[071-095]article of merchandise, that, after it has been bought, may be

[071-096]returned, or bartered, or changed; for it is an inseparable accident

[071-097]that lasts as long as life lasts; it is a noose that, once you put it

[071-098]round your neck, turns into a Gordian knot, which, if the scythe of

[071-099]Death does not cut it, there is no untying. I could say a great deal

[071-100]more on this subject, were I not prevented by the anxiety I feel to

[071-101]know if the señor licentiate has anything more to tell about the story

[071-102]of Basilio.”

[071-103]To this the student, bachelor, or, as Don Quixote called him,

[071-104]licentiate, replied, “I have nothing whatever to say further, but that

[071-105]from the moment Basilio learned that the fair Quiteria was to be

[071-106]married to Camacho the rich, he has never been seen to smile, or heard

[071-107]to utter rational word, and he always goes about moody and dejected,

[071-108]talking to himself in a way that shows plainly he is out of his senses.

[071-109]He eats little and sleeps little, and all he eats is fruit, and when he

[071-110]sleeps, if he sleeps at all, it is in the field on the hard earth like

[071-111]a brute beast. Sometimes he gazes at the sky, at other times he fixes

[071-112]his eyes on the earth in such an abstracted way that he might be taken

[071-113]for a clothed statue, with its drapery stirred by the wind. In short,

[071-114]he shows such signs of a heart crushed by suffering, that all we who

[071-115]know him believe that when to-morrow the fair Quiteria says ‘yes,’ it

[071-116]will be his sentence of death.”

[071-117]“God will guide it better,” said Sancho, “for God who gives the wound

[071-118]gives the salve; nobody knows what will happen; there are a good many

[071-119]hours between this and to-morrow, and any one of them, or any moment,

[071-120]the house may fall; I have seen the rain coming down and the sun

[071-121]shining all at one time; many a one goes to bed in good health who

[071-122]can’t stir the next day. And tell me, is there anyone who can boast of

[071-123]having driven a nail into the wheel of fortune? No, faith; and between

[071-124]a woman’s ‘yes’ and ‘no’ I wouldn’t venture to put the point of a pin,

[071-125]for there would not be room for it; if you tell me Quiteria loves

[071-126]Basilio heart and soul, then I’ll give him a bag of good luck; for

[071-127]love, I have heard say, looks through spectacles that make copper seem

[071-128]gold, poverty wealth, and bleary eyes pearls.”

[071-129]“What art thou driving at, Sancho? curses on thee!” said Don Quixote;

[071-130]“for when thou takest to stringing proverbs and sayings together, no

[071-131]one can understand thee but Judas himself, and I wish he had thee. Tell

[071-132]me, thou animal, what dost thou know about nails or wheels, or anything

[071-133]else?”

[071-134]“Oh, if you don’t understand me,” replied Sancho, “it is no wonder my

[071-135]words are taken for nonsense; but no matter; I understand myself, and I

[071-136]know I have not said anything very foolish in what I have said; only

[071-137]your worship, señor, is always gravelling at everything I say, nay,

[071-138]everything I do.”

[071-139]“Cavilling, not gravelling,” said Don Quixote, “thou prevaricator of

[071-140]honest language, God confound thee!”

[071-141]“Don’t find fault with me, your worship,” returned Sancho, “for you

[071-142]know I have not been bred up at court or trained at Salamanca, to know

[071-143]whether I am adding or dropping a letter or so in my words. Why! God

[071-144]bless me, it’s not fair to force a Sayago-man to speak like a Toledan;

[071-145]maybe there are Toledans who do not hit it off when it comes to

[071-146]polished talk.”

[071-147]“That is true,” said the licentiate, “for those who have been bred up

[071-148]in the Tanneries and the Zocodover cannot talk like those who are

[071-149]almost all day pacing the cathedral cloisters, and yet they are all

[071-150]Toledans. Pure, correct, elegant and lucid language will be met with in

[071-151]men of courtly breeding and discrimination, though they may have been

[071-152]born in Majalahonda; I say of discrimination, because there are many

[071-153]who are not so, and discrimination is the grammar of good language, if

[071-154]it be accompanied by practice. I, sirs, for my sins have studied canon

[071-155]law at Salamanca, and I rather pique myself on expressing my meaning in

[071-156]clear, plain, and intelligible language.”

[071-157]“If you did not pique yourself more on your dexterity with those foils

[071-158]you carry than on dexterity of tongue,” said the other student, “you

[071-159]would have been head of the degrees, where you are now tail.”

[071-160]“Look here, bachelor Corchuelo,” returned the licentiate, “you have the

[071-161]most mistaken idea in the world about skill with the sword, if you

[071-162]think it useless.”

[071-163]“It is no idea on my part, but an established truth,” replied

[071-164]Corchuelo; “and if you wish me to prove it to you by experiment, you

[071-165]have swords there, and it is a good opportunity; I have a steady hand

[071-166]and a strong arm, and these joined with my resolution, which is not

[071-167]small, will make you confess that I am not mistaken. Dismount and put

[071-168]in practice your positions and circles and angles and science, for I

[071-169]hope to make you see stars at noonday with my rude raw swordsmanship,

[071-170]in which, next to God, I place my trust that the man is yet to be born

[071-171]who will make me turn my back, and that there is not one in the world I

[071-172]will not compel to give ground.”

[071-173]“As to whether you turn your back or not, I do not concern myself,”

[071-174]replied the master of fence; “though it might be that your grave would

[071-175]be dug on the spot where you planted your foot the first time; I mean

[071-176]that you would be stretched dead there for despising skill with the

[071-177]sword.”

[071-178]“We shall soon see,” replied Corchuelo, and getting off his ass

[071-179]briskly, he drew out furiously one of the swords the licentiate carried

[071-180]on his beast.

[071-181]“It must not be that way,” said Don Quixote at this point; “I will be

[071-182]the director of this fencing match, and judge of this often disputed

[071-183]question;” and dismounting from Rocinante and grasping his lance, he

[071-184]planted himself in the middle of the road, just as the licentiate, with

[071-185]an easy, graceful bearing and step, advanced towards Corchuelo, who

[071-186]came on against him, darting fire from his eyes, as the saying is. The

[071-187]other two of the company, the peasants, without dismounting from their

[071-188]asses, served as spectators of the mortal tragedy. The cuts, thrusts,

[071-189]down strokes, back strokes and doubles, that Corchuelo delivered were

[071-190]past counting, and came thicker than hops or hail. He attacked like an

[071-191]angry lion, but he was met by a tap on the mouth from the button of the

[071-192]licentiate’s sword that checked him in the midst of his furious onset,

[071-193]and made him kiss it as if it were a relic, though not as devoutly as

[071-194]relics are and ought to be kissed. The end of it was that the

[071-195]licentiate reckoned up for him by thrusts every one of the buttons of

[071-196]the short cassock he wore, tore the skirts into strips, like the tails

[071-197]of a cuttlefish, knocked off his hat twice, and so completely tired him

[071-198]out, that in vexation, anger, and rage, he took the sword by the hilt

[071-199]and flung it away with such force, that one of the peasants that were

[071-200]there, who was a notary, and who went for it, made an affidavit

[071-201]afterwards that he sent it nearly three-quarters of a league, which

[071-202]testimony will serve, and has served, to show and establish with all

[071-203]certainty that strength is overcome by skill.

[071-204]Corchuelo sat down wearied, and Sancho approaching him said, “By my

[071-205]faith, señor bachelor, if your worship takes my advice, you will never

[071-206]challenge anyone to fence again, only to wrestle and throw the bar, for

[071-207]you have the youth and strength for that; but as for these fencers as

[071-208]they call them, I have heard say they can put the point of a sword

[071-209]through the eye of a needle.”

[071-210]“I am satisfied with having tumbled off my donkey,” said Corchuelo,

[071-211]“and with having had the truth I was so ignorant of proved to me by

[071-212]experience;” and getting up he embraced the licentiate, and they were

[071-213]better friends than ever; and not caring to wait for the notary who had

[071-214]gone for the sword, as they saw he would be a long time about it, they

[071-215]resolved to push on so as to reach the village of Quiteria, to which

[071-216]they all belonged, in good time.

[071-217]During the remainder of the journey the licentiate held forth to them

[071-218]on the excellences of the sword, with such conclusive arguments, and

[071-219]such figures and mathematical proofs, that all were convinced of the

[071-220]value of the science, and Corchuelo cured of his dogmatism.

[071-221]It grew dark; but before they reached the town it seemed to them all as

[071-222]if there was a heaven full of countless glittering stars in front of

[071-223]it. They heard, too, the pleasant mingled notes of a variety of

[071-224]instruments, flutes, drums, psalteries, pipes, tabors, and timbrels,

[071-225]and as they drew near they perceived that the trees of a leafy arcade

[071-226]that had been constructed at the entrance of the town were filled with

[071-227]lights unaffected by the wind, for the breeze at the time was so gentle

[071-228]that it had not power to stir the leaves on the trees. The musicians

[071-229]were the life of the wedding, wandering through the pleasant grounds in

[071-230]separate bands, some dancing, others singing, others playing the

[071-231]various instruments already mentioned. In short, it seemed as though

[071-232]mirth and gaiety were frisking and gambolling all over the meadow.

[071-233]Several other persons were engaged in erecting raised benches from

[071-234]which people might conveniently see the plays and dances that were to

[071-235]be performed the next day on the spot dedicated to the celebration of

[071-236]the marriage of Camacho the rich and the obsequies of Basilio. Don

[071-237]Quixote would not enter the village, although the peasant as well as

[071-238]the bachelor pressed him; he excused himself, however, on the grounds,

[071-239]amply sufficient in his opinion, that it was the custom of

[071-240]knights-errant to sleep in the fields and woods in preference to towns,

[071-241]even were it under gilded ceilings; and so turned aside a little out of

[071-242]the road, very much against Sancho’s will, as the good quarters he had

[071-243]enjoyed in the castle or house of Don Diego came back to his mind.
