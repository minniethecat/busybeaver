[082-001]OF DON QUIXOTE’S ADVENTURE WITH A FAIR HUNTRESS

p30a.jpg (134K)


[082-002]They reached their beasts in low spirits and bad humour enough, knight

[082-003]and squire, Sancho particularly, for with him what touched the stock of

[082-004]money touched his heart, and when any was taken from him he felt as if

[082-005]he was robbed of the apples of his eyes. In fine, without exchanging a

[082-006]word, they mounted and quitted the famous river, Don Quixote absorbed

[082-007]in thoughts of his love, Sancho in thinking of his advancement, which

[082-008]just then, it seemed to him, he was very far from securing; for, fool

[082-009]as he was, he saw clearly enough that his master’s acts were all or

[082-010]most of them utterly senseless; and he began to cast about for an

[082-011]opportunity of retiring from his service and going home some day,

[082-012]without entering into any explanations or taking any farewell of him.

[082-013]Fortune, however, ordered matters after a fashion very much the

[082-014]opposite of what he contemplated.

[082-015]It so happened that the next day towards sunset, on coming out of a

[082-016]wood, Don Quixote cast his eyes over a green meadow, and at the far end

[082-017]of it observed some people, and as he drew nearer saw that it was a

[082-018]hawking party. Coming closer, he distinguished among them a lady of

[082-019]graceful mien, on a pure white palfrey or hackney caparisoned with

[082-020]green trappings and a silver-mounted side-saddle. The lady was also in

[082-021]green, and so richly and splendidly dressed that splendour itself

[082-022]seemed personified in her. On her left hand she bore a hawk, a proof to

[082-023]Don Quixote’s mind that she must be some great lady and the mistress of

[082-024]the whole hunting party, which was the fact; so he said to Sancho, “Run

[082-025]Sancho, my son, and say to that lady on the palfrey with the hawk that

[082-026]I, the Knight of the Lions, kiss the hands of her exalted beauty, and

[082-027]if her excellence will grant me leave I will go and kiss them in person

[082-028]and place myself at her service for aught that may be in my power and

[082-029]her highness may command; and mind, Sancho, how thou speakest, and take

[082-030]care not to thrust in any of thy proverbs into thy message.”

p30b.jpg (334K)


[082-031]“You’ve got a likely one here to thrust any in!” said Sancho; “leave me

[082-032]alone for that! Why, this is not the first time in my life I have

[082-033]carried messages to high and exalted ladies.”

[082-034]“Except that thou didst carry to the lady Dulcinea,” said Don Quixote,

[082-035]“I know not that thou hast carried any other, at least in my service.”

[082-036]“That is true,” replied Sancho; “but pledges don’t distress a good

[082-037]payer, and in a house where there’s plenty supper is soon cooked; I

[082-038]mean there’s no need of telling or warning me about anything; for I’m

[082-039]ready for everything and know a little of everything.”

[082-040]“That I believe, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “go and good luck to thee,

[082-041]and God speed thee.”

[082-042]Sancho went off at top speed, forcing Dapple out of his regular pace,

[082-043]and came to where the fair huntress was standing, and dismounting knelt

[082-044]before her and said, “Fair lady, that knight that you see there, the

[082-045]Knight of the Lions by name, is my master, and I am a squire of his,

[082-046]and at home they call me Sancho Panza. This same Knight of the Lions,

[082-047]who was called not long since the Knight of the Rueful Countenance,

[082-048]sends by me to say may it please your highness to give him leave that,

[082-049]with your permission, approbation, and consent, he may come and carry

[082-050]out his wishes, which are, as he says and I believe, to serve your

[082-051]exalted loftiness and beauty; and if you give it, your ladyship will do

[082-052]a thing which will redound to your honour, and he will receive a most

[082-053]distinguished favour and happiness.”

[082-054]“You have indeed, squire,” said the lady, “delivered your message with

[082-055]all the formalities such messages require; rise up, for it is not right

[082-056]that the squire of a knight so great as he of the Rueful Countenance,

[082-057]of whom we have heard a great deal here, should remain on his knees;

[082-058]rise, my friend, and bid your master welcome to the services of myself

[082-059]and the duke my husband, in a country house we have here.”

[082-060]Sancho got up, charmed as much by the beauty of the good lady as by her

[082-061]high-bred air and her courtesy, but, above all, by what she had said

[082-062]about having heard of his master, the Knight of the Rueful Countenance;

[082-063]for if she did not call him Knight of the Lions it was no doubt because

[082-064]he had so lately taken the name. “Tell me, brother squire,” asked the

[082-065]duchess (whose title, however, is not known), “this master of yours, is

[082-066]he not one of whom there is a history extant in print, called ‘The

[082-067]Ingenious Gentleman, Don Quixote of La Mancha,’ who has for the lady of

[082-068]his heart a certain Dulcinea del Toboso?”

[082-069]“He is the same, señora,” replied Sancho; “and that squire of his who

[082-070]figures, or ought to figure, in the said history under the name of

[082-071]Sancho Panza, is myself, unless they have changed me in the cradle, I

[082-072]mean in the press.”

[082-073]“I am rejoiced at all this,” said the duchess; “go, brother Panza, and

[082-074]tell your master that he is welcome to my estate, and that nothing

[082-075]could happen to me that could give me greater pleasure.”

[082-076]Sancho returned to his master mightily pleased with this gratifying

[082-077]answer, and told him all the great lady had said to him, lauding to the

[082-078]skies, in his rustic phrase, her rare beauty, her graceful gaiety, and

[082-079]her courtesy. Don Quixote drew himself up briskly in his saddle, fixed

[082-080]himself in his stirrups, settled his visor, gave Rocinante the spur,

[082-081]and with an easy bearing advanced to kiss the hands of the duchess,

[082-082]who, having sent to summon the duke her husband, told him while Don

[082-083]Quixote was approaching all about the message; and as both of them had

[082-084]read the First Part of this history, and from it were aware of Don

[082-085]Quixote’s crazy turn, they awaited him with the greatest delight and

[082-086]anxiety to make his acquaintance, meaning to fall in with his humour

[082-087]and agree with everything he said, and, so long as he stayed with them,

[082-088]to treat him as a knight-errant, with all the ceremonies usual in the

[082-089]books of chivalry they had read, for they themselves were very fond of

[082-090]them.

[082-091]Don Quixote now came up with his visor raised, and as he seemed about

[082-092]to dismount Sancho made haste to go and hold his stirrup for him; but

[082-093]in getting down off Dapple he was so unlucky as to hitch his foot in

[082-094]one of the ropes of the pack-saddle in such a way that he was unable to

[082-095]free it, and was left hanging by it with his face and breast on the

[082-096]ground. Don Quixote, who was not used to dismount without having the

[082-097]stirrup held, fancying that Sancho had by this time come to hold it for

[082-098]him, threw himself off with a lurch and brought Rocinante’s saddle

[082-099]after him, which was no doubt badly girthed, and saddle and he both

[082-100]came to the ground; not without discomfiture to him and abundant curses

[082-101]muttered between his teeth against the unlucky Sancho, who had his foot

[082-102]still in the shackles. The duke ordered his huntsmen to go to the help

[082-103]of knight and squire, and they raised Don Quixote, sorely shaken by his

[082-104]fall; and he, limping, advanced as best he could to kneel before the

[082-105]noble pair. This, however, the duke would by no means permit; on the

[082-106]contrary, dismounting from his horse, he went and embraced Don Quixote,

[082-107]saying, “I am grieved, Sir Knight of the Rueful Countenance, that your

[082-108]first experience on my ground should have been such an unfortunate one

[082-109]as we have seen; but the carelessness of squires is often the cause of

[082-110]worse accidents.”

[082-111]“That which has happened me in meeting you, mighty prince,” replied Don

[082-112]Quixote, “cannot be unfortunate, even if my fall had not stopped short

[082-113]of the depths of the bottomless pit, for the glory of having seen you

[082-114]would have lifted me up and delivered me from it. My squire, God’s

[082-115]curse upon him, is better at unloosing his tongue in talking

[082-116]impertinence than in tightening the girths of a saddle to keep it

[082-117]steady; but however I may be, fallen or raised up, on foot or on

[082-118]horseback, I shall always be at your service and that of my lady the

[082-119]duchess, your worthy consort, worthy queen of beauty and paramount

[082-120]princess of courtesy.”

[082-121]“Gently, Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha,” said the duke; “where my lady

[082-122]Doña Dulcinea del Toboso is, it is not right that other beauties should

[082-123]be praised.”

[082-124]Sancho, by this time released from his entanglement, was standing by,

[082-125]and before his master could answer he said, “There is no denying, and

[082-126]it must be maintained, that my lady Dulcinea del Toboso is very

[082-127]beautiful; but the hare jumps up where one least expects it; and I have

[082-128]heard say that what we call nature is like a potter that makes vessels

[082-129]of clay, and he who makes one fair vessel can as well make two, or

[082-130]three, or a hundred; I say so because, by my faith, my lady the duchess

[082-131]is in no way behind my mistress the lady Dulcinea del Toboso.”

[082-132]Don Quixote turned to the duchess and said, “Your highness may conceive

[082-133]that never had knight-errant in this world a more talkative or a

[082-134]droller squire than I have, and he will prove the truth of what I say,

[082-135]if your highness is pleased to accept of my services for a few days.”

[082-136]To which the duchess made answer, “that worthy Sancho is droll I

[082-137]consider a very good thing, because it is a sign that he is shrewd; for

[082-138]drollery and sprightliness, Señor Don Quixote, as you very well know,

[082-139]do not take up their abode with dull wits; and as good Sancho is droll

[082-140]and sprightly I here set him down as shrewd.”

[082-141]“And talkative,” added Don Quixote.

[082-142]“So much the better,” said the duke, “for many droll things cannot be

[082-143]said in few words; but not to lose time in talking, come, great Knight

[082-144]of the Rueful Countenance—”

[082-145]“Of the Lions, your highness must say,” said Sancho, “for there is no

[082-146]Rueful Countenance nor any such character now.”

[082-147]“He of the Lions be it,” continued the duke; “I say, let Sir Knight of

[082-148]the Lions come to a castle of mine close by, where he shall be given

[082-149]that reception which is due to so exalted a personage, and which the

[082-150]duchess and I are wont to give to all knights-errant who come there.”

[082-151]By this time Sancho had fixed and girthed Rocinante’s saddle, and Don

[082-152]Quixote having got on his back and the duke mounted a fine horse, they

[082-153]placed the duchess in the middle and set out for the castle. The

[082-154]duchess desired Sancho to come to her side, for she found infinite

[082-155]enjoyment in listening to his shrewd remarks. Sancho required no

[082-156]pressing, but pushed himself in between them and the duke, who thought

[082-157]it rare good fortune to receive such a knight-errant and such a homely

[082-158]squire in their castle.
