[021-002]HELMET, TOGETHER WITH OTHER THINGS THAT HAPPENED TO OUR INVINCIBLE

[021-003]It now began to rain a little, and Sancho was for going into the

[021-004]fulling mills, but Don Quixote had taken such an abhorrence to them on

[021-005]account of the late joke that he would not enter them on any account;

[021-006]so turning aside to right they came upon another road, different from

[021-007]that which they had taken the night before. Shortly afterwards Don

[021-008]Quixote perceived a man on horseback who wore on his head something

[021-009]that shone like gold, and the moment he saw him he turned to Sancho and

[021-010]said:

[021-011]“I think, Sancho, there is no proverb that is not true, all being

[021-012]maxims drawn from experience itself, the mother of all the sciences,

[021-013]especially that one that says, ‘Where one door shuts, another opens.’ I

[021-014]say so because if last night fortune shut the door of the adventure we

[021-015]were looking for against us, cheating us with the fulling mills, it now

[021-016]opens wide another one for another better and more certain adventure,

[021-017]and if I do not contrive to enter it, it will be my own fault, and I

[021-018]cannot lay it to my ignorance of fulling mills, or the darkness of the

[021-019]night. I say this because, if I mistake not, there comes towards us one

[021-020]who wears on his head the helmet of Mambrino, concerning which I took

[021-021]the oath thou rememberest.”

[021-022]“Mind what you say, your worship, and still more what you do,” said

[021-023]Sancho, “for I don’t want any more fulling mills to finish off fulling

[021-024]and knocking our senses out.”

[021-025]“The devil take thee, man,” said Don Quixote; “what has a helmet to do

[021-026]with fulling mills?”

[021-027]“I don’t know,” replied Sancho, “but, faith, if I might speak as I

[021-028]used, perhaps I could give such reasons that your worship would see you

[021-029]were mistaken in what you say.”

[021-030]“How can I be mistaken in what I say, unbelieving traitor?” returned

[021-031]Don Quixote; “tell me, seest thou not yonder knight coming towards us

[021-032]on a dappled grey steed, who has upon his head a helmet of gold?”

[021-033]“What I see and make out,” answered Sancho, “is only a man on a grey

[021-034]ass like my own, who has something that shines on his head.”

[021-035]“Well, that is the helmet of Mambrino,” said Don Quixote; “stand to one

[021-036]side and leave me alone with him; thou shalt see how, without saying a

[021-037]word, to save time, I shall bring this adventure to an issue and

[021-038]possess myself of the helmet I have so longed for.”

[021-039]“I will take care to stand aside,” said Sancho; “but God grant, I say

[021-040]once more, that it may be marjoram and not fulling mills.”

[021-041]“I have told thee, brother, on no account to mention those fulling

[021-042]mills to me again,” said Don Quixote, “or I vow—and I say no more—I’ll

[021-043]full the soul out of you.”

[021-044]Sancho held his peace in dread lest his master should carry out the vow

[021-045]he had hurled like a bowl at him.

[021-046]The fact of the matter as regards the helmet, steed, and knight that

[021-047]Don Quixote saw, was this. In that neighbourhood there were two

[021-048]villages, one of them so small that it had neither apothecary’s shop

[021-049]nor barber, which the other that was close to it had, so the barber of

[021-050]the larger served the smaller, and in it there was a sick man who

[021-051]required to be bled and another man who wanted to be shaved, and on

[021-052]this errand the barber was going, carrying with him a brass basin; but

[021-053]as luck would have it, as he was on the way it began to rain, and not

[021-054]to spoil his hat, which probably was a new one, he put the basin on his

[021-055]head, and being clean it glittered at half a league’s distance. He rode

[021-056]upon a grey ass, as Sancho said, and this was what made it seem to Don

[021-057]Quixote to be a dapple-grey steed and a knight and a golden helmet; for

[021-058]everything he saw he made to fall in with his crazy chivalry and

[021-059]ill-errant notions; and when he saw the poor knight draw near, without

[021-060]entering into any parley with him, at Rocinante’s top speed he bore

[021-061]down upon him with the pike pointed low, fully determined to run him

[021-062]through and through, and as he reached him, without checking the fury

[021-063]of his charge, he cried to him:

[021-064]“Defend thyself, miserable being, or yield me of thine own accord that

[021-065]which is so reasonably my due.”

[021-066]The barber, who without any expectation or apprehension of it saw this

[021-067]apparition coming down upon him, had no other way of saving himself

[021-068]from the stroke of the lance but to let himself fall off his ass; and

[021-069]no sooner had he touched the ground than he sprang up more nimbly than

[021-070]a deer and sped away across the plain faster than the wind.

[021-071]He left the basin on the ground, with which Don Quixote contented

[021-072]himself, saying that the pagan had shown his discretion and imitated

[021-073]the beaver, which finding itself pressed by the hunters bites and cuts

[021-074]off with its teeth that for which, by its natural instinct, it knows it

[021-075]is pursued.

[021-076]He told Sancho to pick up the helmet, and he taking it in his hands

[021-077]said:

[021-078]“By God the basin is a good one, and worth a real of eight if it is

[021-079]worth a maravedis,” and handed it to his master, who immediately put it

[021-080]on his head, turning it round, now this way, now that, in search of

[021-081]fitment, and not finding it he said, “Clearly the pagan to whose

[021-082]measure this famous head-piece was first forged must have had a very

[021-083]large head; but the worst of it is half of it is wanting.”

[021-084]When Sancho heard him call the basin a headpiece he was unable to

[021-085]restrain his laughter, but remembering his master’s wrath he checked

[021-086]himself in the midst of it.

[021-087]“What art thou laughing at, Sancho?” said Don Quixote.

[021-088]“I am laughing,” said he, “to think of the great head the pagan must

[021-089]have had who owned this helmet, for it looks exactly like a regular

[021-090]barber’s basin.”

[021-091]“Dost thou know what I suspect, Sancho?” said Don Quixote; “that this

[021-092]wonderful piece of this enchanted helmet must by some strange accident

[021-093]have come into the hands of someone who was unable to recognise or

[021-094]realise its value, and who, not knowing what he did, and seeing it to

[021-095]be of the purest gold, must have melted down one half for the sake of

[021-096]what it might be worth, and of the other made this which is like a

[021-097]barber’s basin as thou sayest; but be it as it may, to me who recognise

[021-098]it, its transformation makes no difference, for I will set it to rights

[021-099]at the first village where there is a blacksmith, and in such style

[021-100]that that helmet the god of smithies forged for the god of battles

[021-101]shall not surpass it or even come up to it; and in the meantime I will

[021-102]wear it as well as I can, for something is better than nothing; all the

[021-103]more as it will be quite enough to protect me from any chance blow of a

[021-104]stone.”

[021-105]“That is,” said Sancho, “if it is not shot with a sling as they were in

[021-106]the battle of the two armies, when they signed the cross on your

[021-107]worship’s grinders and smashed the flask with that blessed draught that

[021-108]made me vomit my bowels up.”

[021-109]“It does not grieve me much to have lost it,” said Don Quixote, “for

[021-110]thou knowest, Sancho, that I have the receipt in my memory.”

[021-111]“So have I,” answered Sancho, “but if ever I make it, or try it again

[021-112]as long as I live, may this be my last hour; moreover, I have no

[021-113]intention of putting myself in the way of wanting it, for I mean, with

[021-114]all my five senses, to keep myself from being wounded or from wounding

[021-115]anyone: as to being blanketed again I say nothing, for it is hard to

[021-116]prevent mishaps of that sort, and if they come there is nothing for it

[021-117]but to squeeze our shoulders together, hold our breath, shut our eyes,

[021-118]and let ourselves go where luck and the blanket may send us.”

[021-119]“Thou art a bad Christian, Sancho,” said Don Quixote on hearing this,

[021-120]“for once an injury has been done thee thou never forgettest it: but

[021-121]know that it is the part of noble and generous hearts not to attach

[021-122]importance to trifles. What lame leg hast thou got by it, what broken

[021-123]rib, what cracked head, that thou canst not forget that jest? For jest

[021-124]and sport it was, properly regarded, and had I not seen it in that

[021-125]light I would have returned and done more mischief in revenging thee

[021-126]than the Greeks did for the rape of Helen, who, if she were alive now,

[021-127]or if my Dulcinea had lived then, might depend upon it she would not be

[021-128]so famous for her beauty as she is;” and here he heaved a sigh and sent

[021-129]it aloft; and said Sancho, “Let it pass for a jest as it cannot be

[021-130]revenged in earnest, but I know what sort of jest and earnest it was,

[021-131]and I know it will never be rubbed out of my memory any more than off

[021-132]my shoulders. But putting that aside, will your worship tell me what

[021-133]are we to do with this dapple-grey steed that looks like a grey ass,

[021-134]which that Martino that your worship overthrew has left deserted here?

[021-135]for, from the way he took to his heels and bolted, he is not likely

[021-136]ever to come back for it; and by my beard but the grey is a good one.”

[021-137]“I have never been in the habit,” said Don Quixote, “of taking spoil of

[021-138]those whom I vanquish, nor is it the practice of chivalry to take away

[021-139]their horses and leave them to go on foot, unless indeed it be that the

[021-140]victor have lost his own in the combat, in which case it is lawful to

[021-141]take that of the vanquished as a thing won in lawful war; therefore,

[021-142]Sancho, leave this horse, or ass, or whatever thou wilt have it to be;

[021-143]for when its owner sees us gone hence he will come back for it.”

[021-144]“God knows I should like to take it,” returned Sancho, “or at least to

[021-145]change it for my own, which does not seem to me as good a one: verily

[021-146]the laws of chivalry are strict, since they cannot be stretched to let

[021-147]one ass be changed for another; I should like to know if I might at

[021-148]least change trappings.”

[021-149]“On that head I am not quite certain,” answered Don Quixote, “and the

[021-150]matter being doubtful, pending better information, I say thou mayest

[021-151]change them, if so be thou hast urgent need of them.”

[021-152]“So urgent is it,” answered Sancho, “that if they were for my own

[021-153]person I could not want them more;” and forthwith, fortified by this

[021-154]licence, he effected the _mutatio capparum_, and rigged out his beast

[021-155]to the ninety-nines and making quite another thing of it. This done,

[021-156]they broke their fast on the remains of the spoils of war plundered

[021-157]from the sumpter mule, and drank of the brook that flowed from the

[021-158]fulling mills, without casting a look in that direction, in such

[021-159]loathing did they hold them for the alarm they had caused them; and,

[021-160]all anger and gloom removed, they mounted and, without taking any fixed

[021-161]road (not to fix upon any being the proper thing for true

[021-162]knights-errant), they set out, guided by Rocinante’s will, which

[021-163]carried along with it that of his master, not to say that of the ass,

[021-164]which always followed him wherever he led, lovingly and sociably;

[021-165]nevertheless they returned to the high road, and pursued it at a

[021-166]venture without any other aim.

[021-167]As they went along, then, in this way Sancho said to his master,

[021-168]“Señor, would your worship give me leave to speak a little to you? For

[021-169]since you laid that hard injunction of silence on me several things

[021-170]have gone to rot in my stomach, and I have now just one on the tip of

[021-171]my tongue that I don’t want to be spoiled.”

[021-172]“Say, on, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and be brief in thy discourse,

[021-173]for there is no pleasure in one that is long.”

[021-174]“Well then, señor,” returned Sancho, “I say that for some days past I

[021-175]have been considering how little is got or gained by going in search of

[021-176]these adventures that your worship seeks in these wilds and

[021-177]cross-roads, where, even if the most perilous are victoriously

[021-178]achieved, there is no one to see or know of them, and so they must be

[021-179]left untold for ever, to the loss of your worship’s object and the

[021-180]credit they deserve; therefore it seems to me it would be better

[021-181](saving your worship’s better judgment) if we were to go and serve some

[021-182]emperor or other great prince who may have some war on hand, in whose

[021-183]service your worship may prove the worth of your person, your great

[021-184]might, and greater understanding, on perceiving which the lord in whose

[021-185]service we may be will perforce have to reward us, each according to

[021-186]his merits; and there you will not be at a loss for someone to set down

[021-187]your achievements in writing so as to preserve their memory for ever.

[021-188]Of my own I say nothing, as they will not go beyond squirely limits,

[021-189]though I make bold to say that, if it be the practice in chivalry to

[021-190]write the achievements of squires, I think mine must not be left out.”

[021-191]“Thou speakest not amiss, Sancho,” answered Don Quixote, “but before

[021-192]that point is reached it is requisite to roam the world, as it were on

[021-193]probation, seeking adventures, in order that, by achieving some, name

[021-194]and fame may be acquired, such that when he betakes himself to the

[021-195]court of some great monarch the knight may be already known by his

[021-196]deeds, and that the boys, the instant they see him enter the gate of

[021-197]the city, may all follow him and surround him, crying, ‘This is the

[021-198]Knight of the Sun’—or the Serpent, or any other title under which he

[021-199]may have achieved great deeds. ‘This,’ they will say, ‘is he who

[021-200]vanquished in single combat the gigantic Brocabruno of mighty strength;

[021-201]he who delivered the great Mameluke of Persia out of the long

[021-202]enchantment under which he had been for almost nine hundred years.’ So

[021-203]from one to another they will go proclaiming his achievements; and

[021-204]presently at the tumult of the boys and the others the king of that

[021-205]kingdom will appear at the windows of his royal palace, and as soon as

[021-206]he beholds the knight, recognising him by his arms and the device on

[021-207]his shield, he will as a matter of course say, ‘What ho! Forth all ye,

[021-208]the knights of my court, to receive the flower of chivalry who cometh

[021-209]hither!’ At which command all will issue forth, and he himself,

[021-210]advancing half-way down the stairs, will embrace him closely, and

[021-211]salute him, kissing him on the cheek, and will then lead him to the

[021-212]queen’s chamber, where the knight will find her with the princess her

[021-213]daughter, who will be one of the most beautiful and accomplished

[021-214]damsels that could with the utmost pains be discovered anywhere in the

[021-215]known world. Straightway it will come to pass that she will fix her

[021-216]eyes upon the knight and he his upon her, and each will seem to the

[021-217]other something more divine than human, and, without knowing how or why

[021-218]they will be taken and entangled in the inextricable toils of love, and

[021-219]sorely distressed in their hearts not to see any way of making their

[021-220]pains and sufferings known by speech. Thence they will lead him, no

[021-221]doubt, to some richly adorned chamber of the palace, where, having

[021-222]removed his armour, they will bring him a rich mantle of scarlet

[021-223]wherewith to robe himself, and if he looked noble in his armour he will

[021-224]look still more so in a doublet. When night comes he will sup with the

[021-225]king, queen, and princess; and all the time he will never take his eyes

[021-226]off her, stealing stealthy glances, unnoticed by those present, and she

[021-227]will do the same, and with equal cautiousness, being, as I have said, a

[021-228]damsel of great discretion. The tables being removed, suddenly through

[021-229]the door of the hall there will enter a hideous and diminutive dwarf

[021-230]followed by a fair dame, between two giants, who comes with a certain

[021-231]adventure, the work of an ancient sage; and he who shall achieve it

[021-232]shall be deemed the best knight in the world.

[021-233]“The king will then command all those present to essay it, and none

[021-234]will bring it to an end and conclusion save the stranger knight, to the

[021-235]great enhancement of his fame, whereat the princess will be overjoyed

[021-236]and will esteem herself happy and fortunate in having fixed and placed

[021-237]her thoughts so high. And the best of it is that this king, or prince,

[021-238]or whatever he is, is engaged in a very bitter war with another as

[021-239]powerful as himself, and the stranger knight, after having been some

[021-240]days at his court, requests leave from him to go and serve him in the

[021-241]said war. The king will grant it very readily, and the knight will

[021-242]courteously kiss his hands for the favour done to him; and that night

[021-243]he will take leave of his lady the princess at the grating of the

[021-244]chamber where she sleeps, which looks upon a garden, and at which he

[021-245]has already many times conversed with her, the go-between and

[021-246]confidante in the matter being a damsel much trusted by the princess.

[021-247]He will sigh, she will swoon, the damsel will fetch water, much

[021-248]distressed because morning approaches, and for the honour of her lady

[021-249]he would not that they were discovered; at last the princess will come

[021-250]to herself and will present her white hands through the grating to the

[021-251]knight, who will kiss them a thousand and a thousand times, bathing

[021-252]them with his tears. It will be arranged between them how they are to

[021-253]inform each other of their good or evil fortunes, and the princess will

[021-254]entreat him to make his absence as short as possible, which he will

[021-255]promise to do with many oaths; once more he kisses her hands, and takes

[021-256]his leave in such grief that he is well-nigh ready to die. He betakes

[021-257]him thence to his chamber, flings himself on his bed, cannot sleep for

[021-258]sorrow at parting, rises early in the morning, goes to take leave of

[021-259]the king, queen, and princess, and, as he takes his leave of the pair,

[021-260]it is told him that the princess is indisposed and cannot receive a

[021-261]visit; the knight thinks it is from grief at his departure, his heart

[021-262]is pierced, and he is hardly able to keep from showing his pain. The

[021-263]confidante is present, observes all, goes to tell her mistress, who

[021-264]listens with tears and says that one of her greatest distresses is not

[021-265]knowing who this knight is, and whether he is of kingly lineage or not;

[021-266]the damsel assures her that so much courtesy, gentleness, and gallantry

[021-267]of bearing as her knight possesses could not exist in any save one who

[021-268]was royal and illustrious; her anxiety is thus relieved, and she

[021-269]strives to be of good cheer lest she should excite suspicion in her

[021-270]parents, and at the end of two days she appears in public. Meanwhile

[021-271]the knight has taken his departure; he fights in the war, conquers the

[021-272]king’s enemy, wins many cities, triumphs in many battles, returns to

[021-273]the court, sees his lady where he was wont to see her, and it is agreed

[021-274]that he shall demand her in marriage of her parents as the reward of

[021-275]his services; the king is unwilling to give her, as he knows not who he

[021-276]is, but nevertheless, whether carried off or in whatever other way it

[021-277]may be, the princess comes to be his bride, and her father comes to

[021-278]regard it as very good fortune; for it so happens that this knight is

[021-279]proved to be the son of a valiant king of some kingdom, I know not

[021-280]what, for I fancy it is not likely to be on the map. The father dies,

[021-281]the princess inherits, and in two words the knight becomes king. And

[021-282]here comes in at once the bestowal of rewards upon his squire and all

[021-283]who have aided him in rising to so exalted a rank. He marries his

[021-284]squire to a damsel of the princess’s, who will be, no doubt, the one

[021-285]who was confidante in their amour, and is daughter of a very great

[021-286]duke.”

[021-287]“That’s what I want, and no mistake about it!” said Sancho. “That’s

[021-288]what I’m waiting for; for all this, word for word, is in store for your

[021-289]worship under the title of the Knight of the Rueful Countenance.”

[021-290]“Thou needst not doubt it, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “for in the

[021-291]same manner, and by the same steps as I have described here,

[021-292]knights-errant rise and have risen to be kings and emperors; all we

[021-293]want now is to find out what king, Christian or pagan, is at war and

[021-294]has a beautiful daughter; but there will be time enough to think of

[021-295]that, for, as I have told thee, fame must be won in other quarters

[021-296]before repairing to the court. There is another thing, too, that is

[021-297]wanting; for supposing we find a king who is at war and has a beautiful

[021-298]daughter, and that I have won incredible fame throughout the universe,

[021-299]I know not how it can be made out that I am of royal lineage, or even

[021-300]second cousin to an emperor; for the king will not be willing to give

[021-301]me his daughter in marriage unless he is first thoroughly satisfied on

[021-302]this point, however much my famous deeds may deserve it; so that by

[021-303]this deficiency I fear I shall lose what my arm has fairly earned. True

[021-304]it is I am a gentleman of known house, of estate and property, and

[021-305]entitled to the five hundred sueldos mulet; and it may be that the sage

[021-306]who shall write my history will so clear up my ancestry and pedigree

[021-307]that I may find myself fifth or sixth in descent from a king; for I

[021-308]would have thee know, Sancho, that there are two kinds of lineages in

[021-309]the world; some there be tracing and deriving their descent from kings

[021-310]and princes, whom time has reduced little by little until they end in a

[021-311]point like a pyramid upside down; and others who spring from the common

[021-312]herd and go on rising step by step until they come to be great lords;

[021-313]so that the difference is that the one were what they no longer are,

[021-314]and the others are what they formerly were not. And I may be of such

[021-315]that after investigation my origin may prove great and famous, with

[021-316]which the king, my father-in-law that is to be, ought to be satisfied;

[021-317]and should he not be, the princess will so love me that even though she

[021-318]well knew me to be the son of a water-carrier, she will take me for her

[021-319]lord and husband in spite of her father; if not, then it comes to

[021-320]seizing her and carrying her off where I please; for time or death will

[021-321]put an end to the wrath of her parents.”

[021-322]“It comes to this, too,” said Sancho, “what some naughty people say,

[021-323]‘Never ask as a favour what thou canst take by force;’ though it would

[021-324]fit better to say, ‘A clear escape is better than good men’s prayers.’

[021-325]I say so because if my lord the king, your worship’s father-in-law,

[021-326]will not condescend to give you my lady the princess, there is nothing

[021-327]for it but, as your worship says, to seize her and transport her. But

[021-328]the mischief is that until peace is made and you come into the peaceful

[021-329]enjoyment of your kingdom, the poor squire is famishing as far as

[021-330]rewards go, unless it be that the confidante damsel that is to be his

[021-331]wife comes with the princess, and that with her he tides over his bad

[021-332]luck until Heaven otherwise orders things; for his master, I suppose,

[021-333]may as well give her to him at once for a lawful wife.”

[021-334]“Nobody can object to that,” said Don Quixote.

[021-335]“Then since that may be,” said Sancho, “there is nothing for it but to

[021-336]commend ourselves to God, and let fortune take what course it will.”

[021-337]“God guide it according to my wishes and thy wants,” said Don Quixote,

[021-338]“and mean be he who thinks himself mean.”

[021-339]“In God’s name let him be so,” said Sancho: “I am an old Christian, and

[021-340]to fit me for a count that’s enough.”

[021-341]“And more than enough for thee,” said Don Quixote; “and even wert thou

[021-342]not, it would make no difference, because I being the king can easily

[021-343]give thee nobility without purchase or service rendered by thee, for

[021-344]when I make thee a count, then thou art at once a gentleman; and they

[021-345]may say what they will, but by my faith they will have to call thee

[021-346]‘your lordship,’ whether they like it or not.”

[021-347]“Not a doubt of it; and I’ll know how to support the tittle,” said

[021-348]Sancho.

[021-349]“Title thou shouldst say, not tittle,” said his master.

[021-350]“So be it,” answered Sancho. “I say I will know how to behave, for once

[021-351]in my life I was beadle of a brotherhood, and the beadle’s gown sat so

[021-352]well on me that all said I looked as if I was to be steward of the same

[021-353]brotherhood. What will it be, then, when I put a duke’s robe on my

[021-354]back, or dress myself in gold and pearls like a count? I believe

[021-355]they’ll come a hundred leagues to see me.”

[021-356]“Thou wilt look well,” said Don Quixote, “but thou must shave thy beard

[021-357]often, for thou hast it so thick and rough and unkempt, that if thou

[021-358]dost not shave it every second day at least, they will see what thou

[021-359]art at the distance of a musket shot.”

[021-360]“What more will it be,” said Sancho, “than having a barber, and keeping

[021-361]him at wages in the house? and even if it be necessary, I will make him

[021-362]go behind me like a nobleman’s equerry.”

[021-363]“Why, how dost thou know that noblemen have equerries behind them?”

[021-364]asked Don Quixote.

[021-365]“I will tell you,” answered Sancho. “Years ago I was for a month at the

[021-366]capital and there I saw taking the air a very small gentleman who they

[021-367]said was a very great man, and a man following him on horseback in

[021-368]every turn he took, just as if he was his tail. I asked why this man

[021-369]did not join the other man, instead of always going behind him; they

[021-370]answered me that he was his equerry, and that it was the custom with

[021-371]nobles to have such persons behind them, and ever since then I know it,

[021-372]for I have never forgotten it.”

[021-373]“Thou art right,” said Don Quixote, “and in the same way thou mayest

[021-374]carry thy barber with thee, for customs did not come into use all

[021-375]together, nor were they all invented at once, and thou mayest be the

[021-376]first count to have a barber to follow him; and, indeed, shaving one’s

[021-377]beard is a greater trust than saddling one’s horse.”

[021-378]“Let the barber business be my look-out,” said Sancho; “and your

[021-379]worship’s be it to strive to become a king, and make me a count.”

[021-380]“So it shall be,” answered Don Quixote, and raising his eyes he saw

[021-381]what will be told in the following chapter.
