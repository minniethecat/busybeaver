IN WHICH IS RELATED THE UNFORTUNATE ADVENTURE THAT DON QUIXOTE FELL IN

WITH WHEN HE FELL OUT WITH CERTAIN HEARTLESS YANGUESANS

c15a.jpg (81K)


[015-001]The sage Cid Hamete Benengeli relates that as soon as Don Quixote took

[015-002]leave of his hosts and all who had been present at the burial of

[015-003]Chrysostom, he and his squire passed into the same wood which they had

[015-004]seen the shepherdess Marcela enter, and after having wandered for more

[015-005]than two hours in all directions in search of her without finding her,

[015-006]they came to a halt in a glade covered with tender grass, beside which

[015-007]ran a pleasant cool stream that invited and compelled them to pass

[015-008]there the hours of the noontide heat, which by this time was beginning

[015-009]to come on oppressively. Don Quixote and Sancho dismounted, and turning

[015-010]Rocinante and the ass loose to feed on the grass that was there in

[015-011]abundance, they ransacked the alforjas, and without any ceremony very

[015-012]peacefully and sociably master and man made their repast on what they

[015-013]found in them.

c15b.jpg (376K)


[015-014]Sancho had not thought it worth while to hobble Rocinante, feeling

[015-015]sure, from what he knew of his staidness and freedom from incontinence,

[015-016]that all the mares in the Cordova pastures would not lead him into an

[015-017]impropriety. Chance, however, and the devil, who is not always asleep,

[015-018]so ordained it that feeding in this valley there was a drove of

[015-019]Galician ponies belonging to certain Yanguesan carriers, whose way it

[015-020]is to take their midday rest with their teams in places and spots where

[015-021]grass and water abound; and that where Don Quixote chanced to be suited

[015-022]the Yanguesans’ purpose very well. It so happened, then, that Rocinante

[015-023]took a fancy to disport himself with their ladyships the ponies, and

[015-024]abandoning his usual gait and demeanour as he scented them, he, without

[015-025]asking leave of his master, got up a briskish little trot and hastened

[015-026]to make known his wishes to them; they, however, it seemed, preferred

[015-027]their pasture to him, and received him with their heels and teeth to

[015-028]such effect that they soon broke his girths and left him naked without

[015-029]a saddle to cover him; but what must have been worse to him was that

[015-030]the carriers, seeing the violence he was offering to their mares, came

[015-031]running up armed with stakes, and so belaboured him that they brought

[015-032]him sorely battered to the ground.

[015-033]By this time Don Quixote and Sancho, who had witnessed the drubbing of

[015-034]Rocinante, came up panting, and said Don Quixote to Sancho:

[015-035]“So far as I can see, friend Sancho, these are not knights but base

[015-036]folk of low birth: I mention it because thou canst lawfully aid me in

[015-037]taking due vengeance for the insult offered to Rocinante before our

[015-038]eyes.”

[015-039]“What the devil vengeance can we take,” answered Sancho, “if they are

[015-040]more than twenty, and we no more than two, or, indeed, perhaps not more

[015-041]than one and a half?”

[015-042]“I count for a hundred,” replied Don Quixote, and without more words he

[015-043]drew his sword and attacked the Yanguesans and excited and impelled by

[015-044]the example of his master, Sancho did the same; and to begin with, Don

[015-045]Quixote delivered a slash at one of them that laid open the leather

[015-046]jerkin he wore, together with a great portion of his shoulder. The

[015-047]Yanguesans, seeing themselves assaulted by only two men while they were

[015-048]so many, betook themselves to their stakes, and driving the two into

[015-049]the middle they began to lay on with great zeal and energy; in fact, at

[015-050]the second blow they brought Sancho to the ground, and Don Quixote

[015-051]fared the same way, all his skill and high mettle availing him nothing,

[015-052]and fate willed it that he should fall at the feet of Rocinante, who

[015-053]had not yet risen; whereby it may be seen how furiously stakes can

[015-054]pound in angry boorish hands.

c15c.jpg (362K)


[015-055]Then, seeing the mischief they had done, the Yanguesans with all the

[015-056]haste they could loaded their team and pursued their journey, leaving

[015-057]the two adventurers a sorry sight and in sorrier mood.

[015-058]Sancho was the first to come to, and finding himself close to his

[015-059]master he called to him in a weak and doleful voice, “Señor Don

[015-060]Quixote, ah, Señor Don Quixote!”

[015-061]“What wouldst thou, brother Sancho?” answered Don Quixote in the same

[015-062]feeble suffering tone as Sancho.

[015-063]“I would like, if it were possible,” answered Sancho Panza, “your

[015-064]worship to give me a couple of sups of that potion of the fiery Blas,

[015-065]if it be that you have any to hand there; perhaps it will serve for

[015-066]broken bones as well as for wounds.”

[015-067]“If I only had it here, wretch that I am, what more should we want?”

[015-068]said Don Quixote; “but I swear to thee, Sancho Panza, on the faith of a

[015-069]knight-errant, ere two days are over, unless fortune orders otherwise,

[015-070]I mean to have it in my possession, or my hand will have lost its

[015-071]cunning.”

[015-072]“But in how many does your worship think we shall have the use of our

[015-073]feet?” answered Sancho Panza.

[015-074]“For myself I must say I cannot guess how many,” said the battered

[015-075]knight Don Quixote; “but I take all the blame upon myself, for I had no

[015-076]business to put hand to sword against men who where not dubbed knights

[015-077]like myself, and so I believe that in punishment for having

[015-078]transgressed the laws of chivalry the God of battles has permitted this

[015-079]chastisement to be administered to me; for which reason, brother

[015-080]Sancho, it is well thou shouldst receive a hint on the matter which I

[015-081]am now about to mention to thee, for it is of much importance to the

[015-082]welfare of both of us. It is that when thou shalt see rabble of this

[015-083]sort offering us insult thou art not to wait till I draw sword against

[015-084]them, for I shall not do so at all; but do thou draw sword and chastise

[015-085]them to thy heart’s content, and if any knights come to their aid and

[015-086]defence I will take care to defend thee and assail them with all my

[015-087]might; and thou hast already seen by a thousand signs and proofs what

[015-088]the might of this strong arm of mine is equal to”—so uplifted had the

[015-089]poor gentleman become through the victory over the stout Biscayan.

[015-090]But Sancho did not so fully approve of his master’s admonition as to

[015-091]let it pass without saying in reply, “Señor, I am a man of peace, meek

[015-092]and quiet, and I can put up with any affront because I have a wife and

[015-093]children to support and bring up; so let it be likewise a hint to your

[015-094]worship, as it cannot be a mandate, that on no account will I draw

[015-095]sword either against clown or against knight, and that here before God

[015-096]I forgive the insults that have been offered me, whether they have

[015-097]been, are, or shall be offered me by high or low, rich or poor, noble

[015-098]or commoner, not excepting any rank or condition whatsoever.”

[015-099]To all which his master said in reply, “I wish I had breath enough to

[015-100]speak somewhat easily, and that the pain I feel on this side would

[015-101]abate so as to let me explain to thee, Panza, the mistake thou makest.

[015-102]Come now, sinner, suppose the wind of fortune, hitherto so adverse,

[015-103]should turn in our favour, filling the sails of our desires so that

[015-104]safely and without impediment we put into port in some one of those

[015-105]islands I have promised thee, how would it be with thee if on winning

[015-106]it I made thee lord of it? Why, thou wilt make it well-nigh impossible

[015-107]through not being a knight nor having any desire to be one, nor

[015-108]possessing the courage nor the will to avenge insults or defend thy

[015-109]lordship; for thou must know that in newly conquered kingdoms and

[015-110]provinces the minds of the inhabitants are never so quiet nor so well

[015-111]disposed to the new lord that there is no fear of their making some

[015-112]move to change matters once more, and try, as they say, what chance may

[015-113]do for them; so it is essential that the new possessor should have good

[015-114]sense to enable him to govern, and valour to attack and defend himself,

[015-115]whatever may befall him.”

[015-116]“In what has now befallen us,” answered Sancho, “I’d have been well

[015-117]pleased to have that good sense and that valour your worship speaks of,

[015-118]but I swear on the faith of a poor man I am more fit for plasters than

[015-119]for arguments. See if your worship can get up, and let us help

[015-120]Rocinante, though he does not deserve it, for he was the main cause of

[015-121]all this thrashing. I never thought it of Rocinante, for I took him to

[015-122]be a virtuous person and as quiet as myself. After all, they say right

[015-123]that it takes a long time to come to know people, and that there is

[015-124]nothing sure in this life. Who would have said that, after such mighty

[015-125]slashes as your worship gave that unlucky knight-errant, there was

[015-126]coming, travelling post and at the very heels of them, such a great

[015-127]storm of sticks as has fallen upon our shoulders?”

[015-128]“And yet thine, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “ought to be used to such

[015-129]squalls; but mine, reared in soft cloth and fine linen, it is plain

[015-130]they must feel more keenly the pain of this mishap, and if it were not

[015-131]that I imagine—why do I say imagine?—know of a certainty that all these

[015-132]annoyances are very necessary accompaniments of the calling of arms, I

[015-133]would lay me down here to die of pure vexation.”

[015-134]To this the squire replied, “Señor, as these mishaps are what one reaps

[015-135]of chivalry, tell me if they happen very often, or if they have their

[015-136]own fixed times for coming to pass; because it seems to me that after

[015-137]two harvests we shall be no good for the third, unless God in his

[015-138]infinite mercy helps us.”

[015-139]“Know, friend Sancho,” answered Don Quixote, “that the life of

[015-140]knights-errant is subject to a thousand dangers and reverses, and

[015-141]neither more nor less is it within immediate possibility for

[015-142]knights-errant to become kings and emperors, as experience has shown in

[015-143]the case of many different knights with whose histories I am thoroughly

[015-144]acquainted; and I could tell thee now, if the pain would let me, of

[015-145]some who simply by might of arm have risen to the high stations I have

[015-146]mentioned; and those same, both before and after, experienced divers

[015-147]misfortunes and miseries; for the valiant Amadis of Gaul found himself

[015-148]in the power of his mortal enemy Arcalaus the magician, who, it is

[015-149]positively asserted, holding him captive, gave him more than two

[015-150]hundred lashes with the reins of his horse while tied to one of the

[015-151]pillars of a court; and moreover there is a certain recondite author of

[015-152]no small authority who says that the Knight of Phœbus, being caught in

[015-153]a certain pitfall, which opened under his feet in a certain castle, on

[015-154]falling found himself bound hand and foot in a deep pit underground,

[015-155]where they administered to him one of those things they call clysters,

[015-156]of sand and snow-water, that well-nigh finished him; and if he had not

[015-157]been succoured in that sore extremity by a sage, a great friend of his,

[015-158]it would have gone very hard with the poor knight; so I may well suffer

[015-159]in company with such worthy folk, for greater were the indignities

[015-160]which they had to suffer than those which we suffer. For I would have

[015-161]thee know, Sancho, that wounds caused by any instruments which happen

[015-162]by chance to be in hand inflict no indignity, and this is laid down in

[015-163]the law of the duel in express words: if, for instance, the cobbler

[015-164]strikes another with the last which he has in his hand, though it be in

[015-165]fact a piece of wood, it cannot be said for that reason that he whom he

[015-166]struck with it has been cudgelled. I say this lest thou shouldst

[015-167]imagine that because we have been drubbed in this affray we have

[015-168]therefore suffered any indignity; for the arms those men carried, with

[015-169]which they pounded us, were nothing more than their stakes, and not one

[015-170]of them, so far as I remember, carried rapier, sword, or dagger.”

[015-171]“They gave me no time to see that much,” answered Sancho, “for hardly

[015-172]had I laid hand on my tizona when they signed the cross on my shoulders

[015-173]with their sticks in such style that they took the sight out of my eyes

[015-174]and the strength out of my feet, stretching me where I now lie, and

[015-175]where thinking of whether all those stake-strokes were an indignity or

[015-176]not gives me no uneasiness, which the pain of the blows does, for they

[015-177]will remain as deeply impressed on my memory as on my shoulders.”

[015-178]“For all that let me tell thee, brother Panza,” said Don Quixote, “that

[015-179]there is no recollection which time does not put an end to, and no pain

[015-180]which death does not remove.”

[015-181]“And what greater misfortune can there be,” replied Panza, “than the

[015-182]one that waits for time to put an end to it and death to remove it? If

[015-183]our mishap were one of those that are cured with a couple of plasters,

[015-184]it would not be so bad; but I am beginning to think that all the

[015-185]plasters in a hospital almost won’t be enough to put us right.”

[015-186]“No more of that: pluck strength out of weakness, Sancho, as I mean to

[015-187]do,” returned Don Quixote, “and let us see how Rocinante is, for it

[015-188]seems to me that not the least share of this mishap has fallen to the

[015-189]lot of the poor beast.”

[015-190]“There is nothing wonderful in that,” replied Sancho, “since he is a

[015-191]knight-errant too; what I wonder at is that my beast should have come

[015-192]off scot-free where we come out scotched.”

[015-193]“Fortune always leaves a door open in adversity in order to bring

[015-194]relief to it,” said Don Quixote; “I say so because this little beast

[015-195]may now supply the want of Rocinante, carrying me hence to some castle

[015-196]where I may be cured of my wounds. And moreover I shall not hold it any

[015-197]dishonour to be so mounted, for I remember having read how the good old

[015-198]Silenus, the tutor and instructor of the gay god of laughter, when he

[015-199]entered the city of the hundred gates, went very contentedly mounted on

[015-200]a handsome ass.”

[015-201]“It may be true that he went mounted as your worship says,” answered

[015-202]Sancho, “but there is a great difference between going mounted and

[015-203]going slung like a sack of manure.”

[015-204]To which Don Quixote replied, “Wounds received in battle confer honour

[015-205]instead of taking it away; and so, friend Panza, say no more, but, as I

[015-206]told thee before, get up as well as thou canst and put me on top of thy

[015-207]beast in whatever fashion pleases thee best, and let us go hence ere

[015-208]night come on and surprise us in these wilds.”

[015-209]“And yet I have heard your worship say,” observed Panza, “that it is

[015-210]very meet for knights-errant to sleep in wastes and deserts, and that

[015-211]they esteem it very good fortune.”

[015-212]“That is,” said Don Quixote, “when they cannot help it, or when they

[015-213]are in love; and so true is this that there have been knights who have

[015-214]remained two years on rocks, in sunshine and shade and all the

[015-215]inclemencies of heaven, without their ladies knowing anything of it;

[015-216]and one of these was Amadis, when, under the name of Beltenebros, he

[015-217]took up his abode on the Peña Pobre for—I know not if it was eight

[015-218]years or eight months, for I am not very sure of the reckoning; at any

[015-219]rate he stayed there doing penance for I know not what pique the

[015-220]Princess Oriana had against him; but no more of this now, Sancho, and

[015-221]make haste before a mishap like Rocinante’s befalls the ass.”

[015-222]“The very devil would be in it in that case,” said Sancho; and letting

[015-223]off thirty “ohs,” and sixty sighs, and a hundred and twenty

[015-224]maledictions and execrations on whomsoever it was that had brought him

[015-225]there, he raised himself, stopping half-way bent like a Turkish bow

[015-226]without power to bring himself upright, but with all his pains he

[015-227]saddled his ass, who too had gone astray somewhat, yielding to the

[015-228]excessive licence of the day; he next raised up Rocinante, and as for

[015-229]him, had he possessed a tongue to complain with, most assuredly neither

[015-230]Sancho nor his master would have been behind him.

[015-231]To be brief, Sancho fixed Don Quixote on the ass and secured Rocinante

[015-232]with a leading rein, and taking the ass by the halter, he proceeded

[015-233]more or less in the direction in which it seemed to him the high road

[015-234]might be; and, as chance was conducting their affairs for them from

[015-235]good to better, he had not gone a short league when the road came in

[015-236]sight, and on it he perceived an inn, which to his annoyance and to the

[015-237]delight of Don Quixote must needs be a castle. Sancho insisted that it

[015-238]was an inn, and his master that it was not one, but a castle, and the

[015-239]dispute lasted so long that before the point was settled they had time

[015-240]to reach it, and into it Sancho entered with all his team without any

[015-241]further controversy.
