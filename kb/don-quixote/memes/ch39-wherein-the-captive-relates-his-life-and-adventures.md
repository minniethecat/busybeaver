WHEREIN THE CAPTIVE RELATES HIS LIFE AND ADVENTURES

c39a.jpg (137K)


[039-001]My family had its origin in a village in the mountains of Leon, and

[039-002]nature had been kinder and more generous to it than fortune; though in

[039-003]the general poverty of those communities my father passed for being

[039-004]even a rich man; and he would have been so in reality had he been as

[039-005]clever in preserving his property as he was in spending it. This

[039-006]tendency of his to be liberal and profuse he had acquired from having

[039-007]been a soldier in his youth, for the soldier’s life is a school in

[039-008]which the niggard becomes free-handed and the free-handed prodigal; and

[039-009]if any soldiers are to be found who are misers, they are monsters of

[039-010]rare occurrence. My father went beyond liberality and bordered on

[039-011]prodigality, a disposition by no means advantageous to a married man

[039-012]who has children to succeed to his name and position. My father had

[039-013]three, all sons, and all of sufficient age to make choice of a

[039-014]profession. Finding, then, that he was unable to resist his propensity,

[039-015]he resolved to divest himself of the instrument and cause of his

[039-016]prodigality and lavishness, to divest himself of wealth, without which

[039-017]Alexander himself would have seemed parsimonious; and so calling us all

[039-018]three aside one day into a room, he addressed us in words somewhat to

[039-019]the following effect:

[039-020]“My sons, to assure you that I love you, no more need be known or said

[039-021]than that you are my sons; and to encourage a suspicion that I do not

[039-022]love you, no more is needed than the knowledge that I have no

[039-023]self-control as far as preservation of your patrimony is concerned;

[039-024]therefore, that you may for the future feel sure that I love you like a

[039-025]father, and have no wish to ruin you like a stepfather, I propose to do

[039-026]with you what I have for some time back meditated, and after mature

[039-027]deliberation decided upon. You are now of an age to choose your line of

[039-028]life or at least make choice of a calling that will bring you honour

[039-029]and profit when you are older; and what I have resolved to do is to

[039-030]divide my property into four parts; three I will give to you, to each

[039-031]his portion without making any difference, and the other I will retain

[039-032]to live upon and support myself for whatever remainder of life Heaven

[039-033]may be pleased to grant me. But I wish each of you on taking possession

[039-034]of the share that falls to him to follow one of the paths I shall

[039-035]indicate. In this Spain of ours there is a proverb, to my mind very

[039-036]true—as they all are, being short aphorisms drawn from long practical

[039-037]experience—and the one I refer to says, ‘The church, or the sea, or the

[039-038]king’s house;’ as much as to say, in plainer language, whoever wants to

[039-039]flourish and become rich, let him follow the church, or go to sea,

[039-040]adopting commerce as his calling, or go into the king’s service in his

[039-041]household, for they say, ‘Better a king’s crumb than a lord’s favour.’

[039-042]I say so because it is my will and pleasure that one of you should

[039-043]follow letters, another trade, and the third serve the king in the

[039-044]wars, for it is a difficult matter to gain admission to his service in

[039-045]his household, and if war does not bring much wealth it confers great

[039-046]distinction and fame. Eight days hence I will give you your full shares

[039-047]in money, without defrauding you of a farthing, as you will see in the

[039-048]end. Now tell me if you are willing to follow out my idea and advice as

[039-049]I have laid it before you.”

[039-050]Having called upon me as the eldest to answer, I, after urging him not

[039-051]to strip himself of his property but to spend it all as he pleased, for

[039-052]we were young men able to gain our living, consented to comply with his

[039-053]wishes, and said that mine were to follow the profession of arms and

[039-054]thereby serve God and my king. My second brother having made the same

[039-055]proposal, decided upon going to the Indies, embarking the portion that

[039-056]fell to him in trade. The youngest, and in my opinion the wisest, said

[039-057]he would rather follow the church, or go to complete his studies at

[039-058]Salamanca. As soon as we had come to an understanding, and made choice

[039-059]of our professions, my father embraced us all, and in the short time he

[039-060]mentioned carried into effect all he had promised; and when he had

[039-061]given to each his share, which as well as I remember was three thousand

[039-062]ducats apiece in cash (for an uncle of ours bought the estate and paid

[039-063]for it down, not to let it go out of the family), we all three on the

[039-064]same day took leave of our good father; and at the same time, as it

[039-065]seemed to me inhuman to leave my father with such scanty means in his

[039-066]old age, I induced him to take two of my three thousand ducats, as the

[039-067]remainder would be enough to provide me with all a soldier needed. My

[039-068]two brothers, moved by my example, gave him each a thousand ducats, so

[039-069]that there was left for my father four thousand ducats in money,

[039-070]besides three thousand, the value of the portion that fell to him which

[039-071]he preferred to retain in land instead of selling it. Finally, as I

[039-072]said, we took leave of him, and of our uncle whom I have mentioned, not

[039-073]without sorrow and tears on both sides, they charging us to let them

[039-074]know whenever an opportunity offered how we fared, whether well or ill.

[039-075]We promised to do so, and when he had embraced us and given us his

[039-076]blessing, one set out for Salamanca, the other for Seville, and I for

[039-077]Alicante, where I had heard there was a Genoese vessel taking in a

[039-078]cargo of wool for Genoa.

[039-079]It is now some twenty-two years since I left my father’s house, and all

[039-080]that time, though I have written several letters, I have had no news

[039-081]whatever of him or of my brothers; my own adventures during that period

[039-082]I will now relate briefly. I embarked at Alicante, reached Genoa after

[039-083]a prosperous voyage, and proceeded thence to Milan, where I provided

[039-084]myself with arms and a few soldier’s accoutrements; thence it was my

[039-085]intention to go and take service in Piedmont, but as I was already on

[039-086]the road to Alessandria della Paglia, I learned that the great Duke of

[039-087]Alva was on his way to Flanders. I changed my plans, joined him, served

[039-088]under him in the campaigns he made, was present at the deaths of the

[039-089]Counts Egmont and Horn, and was promoted to be ensign under a famous

[039-090]captain of Guadalajara, Diego de Urbina by name. Some time after my

[039-091]arrival in Flanders news came of the league that his Holiness Pope Pius

[039-092]V. of happy memory, had made with Venice and Spain against the common

[039-093]enemy, the Turk, who had just then with his fleet taken the famous

[039-094]island of Cyprus, which belonged to the Venetians, a loss deplorable

[039-095]and disastrous. It was known as a fact that the Most Serene Don John of

[039-096]Austria, natural brother of our good king Don Philip, was coming as

[039-097]commander-in-chief of the allied forces, and rumours were abroad of the

[039-098]vast warlike preparations which were being made, all which stirred my

[039-099]heart and filled me with a longing to take part in the campaign which

[039-100]was expected; and though I had reason to believe, and almost certain

[039-101]promises, that on the first opportunity that presented itself I should

[039-102]be promoted to be captain, I preferred to leave all and betake myself,

[039-103]as I did, to Italy; and it was my good fortune that Don John had just

[039-104]arrived at Genoa, and was going on to Naples to join the Venetian

[039-105]fleet, as he afterwards did at Messina. I may say, in short, that I

[039-106]took part in that glorious expedition, promoted by this time to be a

[039-107]captain of infantry, to which honourable charge my good luck rather

[039-108]than my merits raised me; and that day—so fortunate for Christendom,

[039-109]because then all the nations of the earth were disabused of the error

[039-110]under which they lay in imagining the Turks to be invincible on sea—on

[039-111]that day, I say, on which the Ottoman pride and arrogance were broken,

[039-112]among all that were there made happy (for the Christians who died that

[039-113]day were happier than those who remained alive and victorious) I alone

[039-114]was miserable; for, instead of some naval crown that I might have

[039-115]expected had it been in Roman times, on the night that followed that

[039-116]famous day I found myself with fetters on my feet and manacles on my

[039-117]hands.

[039-118]It happened in this way: El Uchali, the king of Algiers, a daring and

[039-119]successful corsair, having attacked and taken the leading Maltese

[039-120]galley (only three knights being left alive in it, and they badly

[039-121]wounded), the chief galley of John Andrea, on board of which I and my

[039-122]company were placed, came to its relief, and doing as was bound to do

[039-123]in such a case, I leaped on board the enemy’s galley, which, sheering

[039-124]off from that which had attacked it, prevented my men from following

[039-125]me, and so I found myself alone in the midst of my enemies, who were in

[039-126]such numbers that I was unable to resist; in short I was taken, covered

[039-127]with wounds; El Uchali, as you know, sirs, made his escape with his

[039-128]entire squadron, and I was left a prisoner in his power, the only sad

[039-129]being among so many filled with joy, and the only captive among so many

[039-130]free; for there were fifteen thousand Christians, all at the oar in the

[039-131]Turkish fleet, that regained their longed-for liberty that day.

[039-132]They carried me to Constantinople, where the Grand Turk, Selim, made my

[039-133]master general at sea for having done his duty in the battle and

[039-134]carried off as evidence of his bravery the standard of the Order of

[039-135]Malta. The following year, which was the year seventy-two, I found

[039-136]myself at Navarino rowing in the leading galley with the three

[039-137]lanterns. There I saw and observed how the opportunity of capturing the

[039-138]whole Turkish fleet in harbour was lost; for all the marines and

[039-139]janizzaries that belonged to it made sure that they were about to be

[039-140]attacked inside the very harbour, and had their kits and pasamaques, or

[039-141]shoes, ready to flee at once on shore without waiting to be assailed,

[039-142]in so great fear did they stand of our fleet. But Heaven ordered it

[039-143]otherwise, not for any fault or neglect of the general who commanded on

[039-144]our side, but for the sins of Christendom, and because it was God’s

[039-145]will and pleasure that we should always have instruments of punishment

[039-146]to chastise us. As it was, El Uchali took refuge at Modon, which is an

[039-147]island near Navarino, and landing forces fortified the mouth of the

[039-148]harbour and waited quietly until Don John retired. On this expedition

[039-149]was taken the galley called the Prize, whose captain was a son of the

[039-150]famous corsair Barbarossa. It was taken by the chief Neapolitan galley

[039-151]called the She-wolf, commanded by that thunderbolt of war, that father

[039-152]of his men, that successful and unconquered captain Don Álvaro de

[039-153]Bazan, Marquis of Santa Cruz; and I cannot help telling you what took

[039-154]place at the capture of the Prize.

[039-155]The son of Barbarossa was so cruel, and treated his slaves so badly,

[039-156]that, when those who were at the oars saw that the She-wolf galley was

[039-157]bearing down upon them and gaining upon them, they all at once dropped

[039-158]their oars and seized their captain who stood on the stage at the end

[039-159]of the gangway shouting to them to row lustily; and passing him on from

[039-160]bench to bench, from the poop to the prow, they so bit him that before

[039-161]he had got much past the mast his soul had already got to hell; so

[039-162]great, as I said, was the cruelty with which he treated them, and the

[039-163]hatred with which they hated him.

[039-164]We returned to Constantinople, and the following year, seventy-three,

[039-165]it became known that Don John had seized Tunis and taken the kingdom

[039-166]from the Turks, and placed Muley Hamet in possession, putting an end to

[039-167]the hopes which Muley Hamida, the cruelest and bravest Moor in the

[039-168]world, entertained of returning to reign there. The Grand Turk took the

[039-169]loss greatly to heart, and with the cunning which all his race possess,

[039-170]he made peace with the Venetians (who were much more eager for it than

[039-171]he was), and the following year, seventy-four, he attacked the Goletta

[039-172]and the fort which Don John had left half built near Tunis. While all

[039-173]these events were occurring, I was labouring at the oar without any

[039-174]hope of freedom; at least I had no hope of obtaining it by ransom, for

[039-175]I was firmly resolved not to write to my father telling him of my

[039-176]misfortunes. At length the Goletta fell, and the fort fell, before

[039-177]which places there were seventy-five thousand regular Turkish soldiers,

[039-178]and more than four hundred thousand Moors and Arabs from all parts of

[039-179]Africa, and in the train of all this great host such munitions and

[039-180]engines of war, and so many pioneers that with their hands they might

[039-181]have covered the Goletta and the fort with handfuls of earth. The first

[039-182]to fall was the Goletta, until then reckoned impregnable, and it fell,

[039-183]not by any fault of its defenders, who did all that they could and

[039-184]should have done, but because experiment proved how easily

[039-185]entrenchments could be made in the desert sand there; for water used to

[039-186]be found at two palms depth, while the Turks found none at two yards;

[039-187]and so by means of a quantity of sandbags they raised their works so

[039-188]high that they commanded the walls of the fort, sweeping them as if

[039-189]from a cavalier, so that no one was able to make a stand or maintain

[039-190]the defence.

[039-191]It was a common opinion that our men should not have shut themselves up

[039-192]in the Goletta, but should have waited in the open at the

[039-193]landing-place; but those who say so talk at random and with little

[039-194]knowledge of such matters; for if in the Goletta and in the fort there

[039-195]were barely seven thousand soldiers, how could such a small number,

[039-196]however resolute, sally out and hold their own against numbers like

[039-197]those of the enemy? And how is it possible to help losing a stronghold

[039-198]that is not relieved, above all when surrounded by a host of determined

[039-199]enemies in their own country? But many thought, and I thought so too,

[039-200]that it was special favour and mercy which Heaven showed to Spain in

[039-201]permitting the destruction of that source and hiding place of mischief,

[039-202]that devourer, sponge, and moth of countless money, fruitlessly wasted

[039-203]there to no other purpose save preserving the memory of its capture by

[039-204]the invincible Charles V.; as if to make that eternal, as it is and

[039-205]will be, these stones were needed to support it. The fort also fell;

[039-206]but the Turks had to win it inch by inch, for the soldiers who defended

[039-207]it fought so gallantly and stoutly that the number of the enemy killed

[039-208]in twenty-two general assaults exceeded twenty-five thousand. Of three

[039-209]hundred that remained alive not one was taken unwounded, a clear and

[039-210]manifest proof of their gallantry and resolution, and how sturdily they

[039-211]had defended themselves and held their post. A small fort or tower

[039-212]which was in the middle of the lagoon under the command of Don Juan

[039-213]Zanoguera, a Valencian gentleman and a famous soldier, capitulated upon

[039-214]terms. They took prisoner Don Pedro Puertocarrero, commandant of the

[039-215]Goletta, who had done all in his power to defend his fortress, and took

[039-216]the loss of it so much to heart that he died of grief on the way to

[039-217]Constantinople, where they were carrying him a prisoner. They also took

[039-218]the commandant of the fort, Gabrio Cerbellon by name, a Milanese

[039-219]gentleman, a great engineer and a very brave soldier. In these two

[039-220]fortresses perished many persons of note, among whom was Pagano Doria,

[039-221]knight of the Order of St. John, a man of generous disposition, as was

[039-222]shown by his extreme liberality to his brother, the famous John Andrea

[039-223]Doria; and what made his death the more sad was that he was slain by

[039-224]some Arabs to whom, seeing that the fort was now lost, he entrusted

[039-225]himself, and who offered to conduct him in the disguise of a Moor to

[039-226]Tabarca, a small fort or station on the coast held by the Genoese

[039-227]employed in the coral fishery. These Arabs cut off his head and carried

[039-228]it to the commander of the Turkish fleet, who proved on them the truth

[039-229]of our Castilian proverb, that “though the treason may please, the

[039-230]traitor is hated;” for they say he ordered those who brought him the

[039-231]present to be hanged for not having brought him alive.

[039-232]Among the Christians who were taken in the fort was one named Don Pedro

[039-233]de Aguilar, a native of some place, I know not what, in Andalusia, who

[039-234]had been ensign in the fort, a soldier of great repute and rare

[039-235]intelligence, who had in particular a special gift for what they call

[039-236]poetry. I say so because his fate brought him to my galley and to my

[039-237]bench, and made him a slave to the same master; and before we left the

[039-238]port this gentleman composed two sonnets by way of epitaphs, one on the

[039-239]Goletta and the other on the fort; indeed, I may as well repeat them,

[039-240]for I have them by heart, and I think they will be liked rather than

[039-241]disliked.

[039-242]The instant the captive mentioned the name of Don Pedro de Aguilar, Don

[039-243]Fernando looked at his companions and they all three smiled; and when

[039-244]he came to speak of the sonnets one of them said, “Before your worship

[039-245]proceeds any further I entreat you to tell me what became of that Don

[039-246]Pedro de Aguilar you have spoken of.”

[039-247]“All I know is,” replied the captive, “that after having been in

[039-248]Constantinople two years, he escaped in the disguise of an Arnaut, in

[039-249]company with a Greek spy; but whether he regained his liberty or not I

[039-250]cannot tell, though I fancy he did, because a year afterwards I saw the

[039-251]Greek at Constantinople, though I was unable to ask him what the result

[039-252]of the journey was.”

[039-253]“Well then, you are right,” returned the gentleman, “for that Don Pedro

[039-254]is my brother, and he is now in our village in good health, rich,

[039-255]married, and with three children.”

[039-256]“Thanks be to God for all the mercies he has shown him,” said the

[039-257]captive; “for to my mind there is no happiness on earth to compare with

[039-258]recovering lost liberty.”

[039-259]“And what is more,” said the gentleman, “I know the sonnets my brother

[039-260]made.”

[039-261]“Then let your worship repeat them,” said the captive, “for you will

[039-262]recite them better than I can.”

[039-263]“With all my heart,” said the gentleman; “that on the Goletta runs

[039-264]thus.”
