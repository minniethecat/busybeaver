WHICH TREATS OF THE STRANGE AND DELIGHTFUL ADVENTURE THAT BEFELL THE

CURATE AND THE BARBER IN THE SAME SIERRA

[028-001]Happy and fortunate were the times when that most daring knight Don

[028-002]Quixote of La Mancha was sent into the world; for by reason of his

[028-003]having formed a resolution so honourable as that of seeking to revive

[028-004]and restore to the world the long-lost and almost defunct order of

[028-005]knight-errantry, we now enjoy in this age of ours, so poor in light

[028-006]entertainment, not only the charm of his veracious history, but also of

[028-007]the tales and episodes contained in it which are, in a measure, no less

[028-008]pleasing, ingenious, and truthful, than the history itself; which,

[028-009]resuming its thread, carded, spun, and wound, relates that just as the

[028-010]curate was going to offer consolation to Cardenio, he was interrupted

[028-011]by a voice that fell upon his ear saying in plaintive tones:

[028-012]“O God! is it possible I have found a place that may serve as a secret

[028-013]grave for the weary load of this body that I support so unwillingly? If

[028-014]the solitude these mountains promise deceives me not, it is so; ah! woe

[028-015]is me! how much more grateful to my mind will be the society of these

[028-016]rocks and brakes that permit me to complain of my misfortune to Heaven,

[028-017]than that of any human being, for there is none on earth to look to for

[028-018]counsel in doubt, comfort in sorrow, or relief in distress!”

[028-019]All this was heard distinctly by the curate and those with him, and as

[028-020]it seemed to them to be uttered close by, as indeed it was, they got up

[028-021]to look for the speaker, and before they had gone twenty paces they

[028-022]discovered behind a rock, seated at the foot of an ash tree, a youth in

[028-023]the dress of a peasant, whose face they were unable at the moment to

[028-024]see as he was leaning forward, bathing his feet in the brook that

[028-025]flowed past. They approached so silently that he did not perceive them,

[028-026]being fully occupied in bathing his feet, which were so fair that they

[028-027]looked like two pieces of shining crystal brought forth among the other

[028-028]stones of the brook. The whiteness and beauty of these feet struck them

[028-029]with surprise, for they did not seem to have been made to crush clods

[028-030]or to follow the plough and the oxen as their owner’s dress suggested;

[028-031]and so, finding they had not been noticed, the curate, who was in

[028-032]front, made a sign to the other two to conceal themselves behind some

[028-033]fragments of rock that lay there; which they did, observing closely

[028-034]what the youth was about. He had on a loose double-skirted dark brown

[028-035]jacket bound tight to his body with a white cloth; he wore besides

[028-036]breeches and gaiters of brown cloth, and on his head a brown montera;

[028-037]and he had the gaiters turned up as far as the middle of the leg, which

[028-038]verily seemed to be of pure alabaster.

[028-039]As soon as he had done bathing his beautiful feet, he wiped them with a

[028-040]towel he took from under the montera, on taking off which he raised his

[028-041]face, and those who were watching him had an opportunity of seeing a

[028-042]beauty so exquisite that Cardenio said to the curate in a whisper:

[028-043]“As this is not Luscinda, it is no human creature but a divine being.”

[028-044]The youth then took off the montera, and shaking his head from side to

[028-045]side there broke loose and spread out a mass of hair that the beams of

[028-046]the sun might have envied; by this they knew that what had seemed a

[028-047]peasant was a lovely woman, nay the most beautiful the eyes of two of

[028-048]them had ever beheld, or even Cardenio’s if they had not seen and known

[028-049]Luscinda, for he afterwards declared that only the beauty of Luscinda

[028-050]could compare with this. The long auburn tresses not only covered her

[028-051]shoulders, but such was their length and abundance, concealed her all

[028-052]round beneath their masses, so that except the feet nothing of her form

[028-053]was visible. She now used her hands as a comb, and if her feet had

[028-054]seemed like bits of crystal in the water, her hands looked like pieces

[028-055]of driven snow among her locks; all which increased not only the

[028-056]admiration of the three beholders, but their anxiety to learn who she

[028-057]was. With this object they resolved to show themselves, and at the stir

[028-058]they made in getting upon their feet the fair damsel raised her head,

[028-059]and parting her hair from before her eyes with both hands, she looked

[028-060]to see who had made the noise, and the instant she perceived them she

[028-061]started to her feet, and without waiting to put on her shoes or gather

[028-062]up her hair, hastily snatched up a bundle as though of clothes that she

[028-063]had beside her, and, scared and alarmed, endeavoured to take flight;

[028-064]but before she had gone six paces she fell to the ground, her delicate

[028-065]feet being unable to bear the roughness of the stones; seeing which,

[028-066]the three hastened towards her, and the curate addressing her first

[028-067]said:

[028-068]“Stay, señora, whoever you may be, for those whom you see here only

[028-069]desire to be of service to you; you have no need to attempt a flight so

[028-070]heedless, for neither can your feet bear it, nor we allow it.”

[028-071]Taken by surprise and bewildered, she made no reply to these words.

[028-072]They, however, came towards her, and the curate taking her hand went on

[028-073]to say:

[028-074]“What your dress would hide, señora, is made known to us by your hair;

[028-075]a clear proof that it can be no trifling cause that has disguised your

[028-076]beauty in a garb so unworthy of it, and sent it into solitudes like

[028-077]these where we have had the good fortune to find you, if not to relieve

[028-078]your distress, at least to offer you comfort; for no distress, so long

[028-079]as life lasts, can be so oppressive or reach such a height as to make

[028-080]the sufferer refuse to listen to comfort offered with good intention.

[028-081]And so, señora, or señor, or whatever you prefer to be, dismiss the

[028-082]fears that our appearance has caused you and make us acquainted with

[028-083]your good or evil fortunes, for from all of us together, or from each

[028-084]one of us, you will receive sympathy in your trouble.”

[028-085]While the curate was speaking, the disguised damsel stood as if

[028-086]spell-bound, looking at them without opening her lips or uttering a

[028-087]word, just like a village rustic to whom something strange that he has

[028-088]never seen before has been suddenly shown; but on the curate addressing

[028-089]some further words to the same effect to her, sighing deeply she broke

[028-090]silence and said:

[028-091]“Since the solitude of these mountains has been unable to conceal me,

[028-092]and the escape of my dishevelled tresses will not allow my tongue to

[028-093]deal in falsehoods, it would be idle for me now to make any further

[028-094]pretence of what, if you were to believe me, you would believe more out

[028-095]of courtesy than for any other reason. This being so, I say I thank

[028-096]you, sirs, for the offer you have made me, which places me under the

[028-097]obligation of complying with the request you have made of me; though I

[028-098]fear the account I shall give you of my misfortunes will excite in you

[028-099]as much concern as compassion, for you will be unable to suggest

[028-100]anything to remedy them or any consolation to alleviate them. However,

[028-101]that my honour may not be left a matter of doubt in your minds, now

[028-102]that you have discovered me to be a woman, and see that I am young,

[028-103]alone, and in this dress, things that taken together or separately

[028-104]would be enough to destroy any good name, I feel bound to tell what I

[028-105]would willingly keep secret if I could.”

[028-106]All this she who was now seen to be a lovely woman delivered without

[028-107]any hesitation, with so much ease and in so sweet a voice that they

[028-108]were not less charmed by her intelligence than by her beauty, and as

[028-109]they again repeated their offers and entreaties to her to fulfil her

[028-110]promise, she without further pressing, first modestly covering her feet

[028-111]and gathering up her hair, seated herself on a stone with the three

[028-112]placed around her, and, after an effort to restrain some tears that

[028-113]came to her eyes, in a clear and steady voice began her story thus:

[028-114]“In this Andalusia there is a town from which a duke takes a title

[028-115]which makes him one of those that are called Grandees of Spain. This

[028-116]nobleman has two sons, the elder heir to his dignity and apparently to

[028-117]his good qualities; the younger heir to I know not what, unless it be

[028-118]the treachery of Vellido and the falsehood of Ganelon. My parents are

[028-119]this lord’s vassals, lowly in origin, but so wealthy that if birth had

[028-120]conferred as much on them as fortune, they would have had nothing left

[028-121]to desire, nor should I have had reason to fear trouble like that in

[028-122]which I find myself now; for it may be that my ill fortune came of

[028-123]theirs in not having been nobly born. It is true they are not so low

[028-124]that they have any reason to be ashamed of their condition, but neither

[028-125]are they so high as to remove from my mind the impression that my

[028-126]mishap comes of their humble birth. They are, in short, peasants, plain

[028-127]homely people, without any taint of disreputable blood, and, as the

[028-128]saying is, old rusty Christians, but so rich that by their wealth and

[028-129]free-handed way of life they are coming by degrees to be considered

[028-130]gentlefolk by birth, and even by position; though the wealth and

[028-131]nobility they thought most of was having me for their daughter; and as

[028-132]they have no other child to make their heir, and are affectionate

[028-133]parents, I was one of the most indulged daughters that ever parents

[028-134]indulged.

[028-135]“I was the mirror in which they beheld themselves, the staff of their

[028-136]old age, and the object in which, with submission to Heaven, all their

[028-137]wishes centred, and mine were in accordance with theirs, for I knew

[028-138]their worth; and as I was mistress of their hearts, so was I also of

[028-139]their possessions. Through me they engaged or dismissed their servants;

[028-140]through my hands passed the accounts and returns of what was sown and

[028-141]reaped; the oil-mills, the wine-presses, the count of the flocks and

[028-142]herds, the beehives, all in short that a rich farmer like my father has

[028-143]or can have, I had under my care, and I acted as steward and mistress

[028-144]with an assiduity on my part and satisfaction on theirs that I cannot

[028-145]well describe to you. The leisure hours left to me after I had given

[028-146]the requisite orders to the head-shepherds, overseers, and other

[028-147]labourers, I passed in such employments as are not only allowable but

[028-148]necessary for young girls, those that the needle, embroidery cushion,

[028-149]and spinning wheel usually afford, and if to refresh my mind I quitted

[028-150]them for a while, I found recreation in reading some devotional book or

[028-151]playing the harp, for experience taught me that music soothes the

[028-152]troubled mind and relieves weariness of spirit. Such was the life I led

[028-153]in my parents’ house and if I have depicted it thus minutely, it is not

[028-154]out of ostentation, or to let you know that I am rich, but that you may

[028-155]see how, without any fault of mine, I have fallen from the happy

[028-156]condition I have described, to the misery I am in at present. The truth

[028-157]is, that while I was leading this busy life, in a retirement that might

[028-158]compare with that of a monastery, and unseen as I thought by any except

[028-159]the servants of the house (for when I went to Mass it was so early in

[028-160]the morning, and I was so closely attended by my mother and the women

[028-161]of the household, and so thickly veiled and so shy, that my eyes

[028-162]scarcely saw more ground than I trod on), in spite of all this, the

[028-163]eyes of love, or idleness, more properly speaking, that the lynx’s

[028-164]cannot rival, discovered me, with the help of the assiduity of Don

[028-165]Fernando; for that is the name of the younger son of the duke I told

[028-166]of.”

[028-167]The moment the speaker mentioned the name of Don Fernando, Cardenio

[028-168]changed colour and broke into a sweat, with such signs of emotion that

[028-169]the curate and the barber, who observed it, feared that one of the mad

[028-170]fits which they heard attacked him sometimes was coming upon him; but

[028-171]Cardenio showed no further agitation and remained quiet, regarding the

[028-172]peasant girl with fixed attention, for he began to suspect who she was.

[028-173]She, however, without noticing the excitement of Cardenio, continuing

[028-174]her story, went on to say:

[028-175]“And they had hardly discovered me, when, as he owned afterwards, he

[028-176]was smitten with a violent love for me, as the manner in which it

[028-177]displayed itself plainly showed. But to shorten the long recital of my

[028-178]woes, I will pass over in silence all the artifices employed by Don

[028-179]Fernando for declaring his passion for me. He bribed all the household,

[028-180]he gave and offered gifts and presents to my parents; every day was

[028-181]like a holiday or a merry-making in our street; by night no one could

[028-182]sleep for the music; the love letters that used to come to my hand, no

[028-183]one knew how, were innumerable, full of tender pleadings and pledges,

[028-184]containing more promises and oaths than there were letters in them; all

[028-185]which not only did not soften me, but hardened my heart against him, as

[028-186]if he had been my mortal enemy, and as if everything he did to make me

[028-187]yield were done with the opposite intention. Not that the high-bred

[028-188]bearing of Don Fernando was disagreeable to me, or that I found his

[028-189]importunities wearisome; for it gave me a certain sort of satisfaction

[028-190]to find myself so sought and prized by a gentleman of such distinction,

[028-191]and I was not displeased at seeing my praises in his letters (for

[028-192]however ugly we women may be, it seems to me it always pleases us to

[028-193]hear ourselves called beautiful) but that my own sense of right was

[028-194]opposed to all this, as well as the repeated advice of my parents, who

[028-195]now very plainly perceived Don Fernando’s purpose, for he cared very

[028-196]little if all the world knew it. They told me they trusted and confided

[028-197]their honour and good name to my virtue and rectitude alone, and bade

[028-198]me consider the disparity between Don Fernando and myself, from which I

[028-199]might conclude that his intentions, whatever he might say to the

[028-200]contrary, had for their aim his own pleasure rather than my advantage;

[028-201]and if I were at all desirous of opposing an obstacle to his

[028-202]unreasonable suit, they were ready, they said, to marry me at once to

[028-203]anyone I preferred, either among the leading people of our own town, or

[028-204]of any of those in the neighbourhood; for with their wealth and my good

[028-205]name, a match might be looked for in any quarter. This offer, and their

[028-206]sound advice strengthened my resolution, and I never gave Don Fernando

[028-207]a word in reply that could hold out to him any hope of success, however

[028-208]remote.

[028-209]“All this caution of mine, which he must have taken for coyness, had

[028-210]apparently the effect of increasing his wanton appetite—for that is the

[028-211]name I give to his passion for me; had it been what he declared it to

[028-212]be, you would not know of it now, because there would have been no

[028-213]occasion to tell you of it. At length he learned that my parents were

[028-214]contemplating marriage for me in order to put an end to his hopes of

[028-215]obtaining possession of me, or at least to secure additional protectors

[028-216]to watch over me, and this intelligence or suspicion made him act as

[028-217]you shall hear. One night, as I was in my chamber with no other

[028-218]companion than a damsel who waited on me, with the doors carefully

[028-219]locked lest my honour should be imperilled through any carelessness, I

[028-220]know not nor can conceive how it happened, but, with all this seclusion

[028-221]and these precautions, and in the solitude and silence of my

[028-222]retirement, I found him standing before me, a vision that so astounded

[028-223]me that it deprived my eyes of sight, and my tongue of speech. I had no

[028-224]power to utter a cry, nor, I think, did he give me time to utter one,

[028-225]as he immediately approached me, and taking me in his arms (for,

[028-226]overwhelmed as I was, I was powerless, I say, to help myself), he began

[028-227]to make such professions to me that I know not how falsehood could have

[028-228]had the power of dressing them up to seem so like truth; and the

[028-229]traitor contrived that his tears should vouch for his words, and his

[028-230]sighs for his sincerity.

[028-231]“I, a poor young creature alone, ill versed among my people in cases

[028-232]such as this, began, I know not how, to think all these lying

[028-233]protestations true, though without being moved by his sighs and tears

[028-234]to anything more than pure compassion; and so, as the first feeling of

[028-235]bewilderment passed away, and I began in some degree to recover myself,

[028-236]I said to him with more courage than I thought I could have possessed,

[028-237]‘If, as I am now in your arms, señor, I were in the claws of a fierce

[028-238]lion, and my deliverance could be procured by doing or saying anything

[028-239]to the prejudice of my honour, it would no more be in my power to do it

[028-240]or say it, than it would be possible that what was should not have

[028-241]been; so then, if you hold my body clasped in your arms, I hold my soul

[028-242]secured by virtuous intentions, very different from yours, as you will

[028-243]see if you attempt to carry them into effect by force. I am your

[028-244]vassal, but I am not your slave; your nobility neither has nor should

[028-245]have any right to dishonour or degrade my humble birth; and low-born

[028-246]peasant as I am, I have my self-respect as much as you, a lord and

[028-247]gentleman: with me your violence will be to no purpose, your wealth

[028-248]will have no weight, your words will have no power to deceive me, nor

[028-249]your sighs or tears to soften me: were I to see any of the things I

[028-250]speak of in him whom my parents gave me as a husband, his will should

[028-251]be mine, and mine should be bounded by his; and my honour being

[028-252]preserved even though my inclinations were not would willingly yield

[028-253]him what you, señor, would now obtain by force; and this I say lest you

[028-254]should suppose that any but my lawful husband shall ever win anything

[028-255]of me.’ ‘If that,’ said this disloyal gentleman, ‘be the only scruple

[028-256]you feel, fairest Dorothea’ (for that is the name of this unhappy

[028-257]being), ‘see here I give you my hand to be yours, and let Heaven, from

[028-258]which nothing is hid, and this image of Our Lady you have here, be

[028-259]witnesses of this pledge.’”

[028-260]When Cardenio heard her say she was called Dorothea, he showed fresh

[028-261]agitation and felt convinced of the truth of his former suspicion, but

[028-262]he was unwilling to interrupt the story, and wished to hear the end of

[028-263]what he already all but knew, so he merely said:

[028-264]“What! is Dorothea your name, señora? I have heard of another of the

[028-265]same name who can perhaps match your misfortunes. But proceed;

[028-266]by-and-by I may tell you something that will astonish you as much as it

[028-267]will excite your compassion.”

[028-268]Dorothea was struck by Cardenio’s words as well as by his strange and

[028-269]miserable attire, and begged him if he knew anything concerning her to

[028-270]tell it to her at once, for if fortune had left her any blessing it was

[028-271]courage to bear whatever calamity might fall upon her, as she felt sure

[028-272]that none could reach her capable of increasing in any degree what she

[028-273]endured already.

[028-274]“I would not let the occasion pass, señora,” replied Cardenio, “of

[028-275]telling you what I think, if what I suspect were the truth, but so far

[028-276]there has been no opportunity, nor is it of any importance to you to

[028-277]know it.”

[028-278]“Be it as it may,” replied Dorothea, “what happened in my story was

[028-279]that Don Fernando, taking an image that stood in the chamber, placed it

[028-280]as a witness of our betrothal, and with the most binding words and

[028-281]extravagant oaths gave me his promise to become my husband; though

[028-282]before he had made an end of pledging himself I bade him consider well

[028-283]what he was doing, and think of the anger his father would feel at

[028-284]seeing him married to a peasant girl and one of his vassals; I told him

[028-285]not to let my beauty, such as it was, blind him, for that was not

[028-286]enough to furnish an excuse for his transgression; and if in the love

[028-287]he bore me he wished to do me any kindness, it would be to leave my lot

[028-288]to follow its course at the level my condition required; for marriages

[028-289]so unequal never brought happiness, nor did they continue long to

[028-290]afford the enjoyment they began with.

[028-291]“All this that I have now repeated I said to him, and much more which I

[028-292]cannot recollect; but it had no effect in inducing him to forego his

[028-293]purpose; he who has no intention of paying does not trouble himself

[028-294]about difficulties when he is striking the bargain. At the same time I

[028-295]argued the matter briefly in my own mind, saying to myself, ‘I shall

[028-296]not be the first who has risen through marriage from a lowly to a lofty

[028-297]station, nor will Don Fernando be the first whom beauty or, as is more

[028-298]likely, a blind attachment, has led to mate himself below his rank.

[028-299]Then, since I am introducing no new usage or practice, I may as well

[028-300]avail myself of the honour that chance offers me, for even though his

[028-301]inclination for me should not outlast the attainment of his wishes, I

[028-302]shall be, after all, his wife before God. And if I strive to repel him

[028-303]by scorn, I can see that, fair means failing, he is in a mood to use

[028-304]force, and I shall be left dishonoured and without any means of proving

[028-305]my innocence to those who cannot know how innocently I have come to be

[028-306]in this position; for what arguments would persuade my parents that

[028-307]this gentleman entered my chamber without my consent?’

[028-308]“All these questions and answers passed through my mind in a moment;

[028-309]but the oaths of Don Fernando, the witnesses he appealed to, the tears

[028-310]he shed, and lastly the charms of his person and his high-bred grace,

[028-311]which, accompanied by such signs of genuine love, might well have

[028-312]conquered a heart even more free and coy than mine—these were the

[028-313]things that more than all began to influence me and lead me unawares to

[028-314]my ruin. I called my waiting-maid to me, that there might be a witness

[028-315]on earth besides those in Heaven, and again Don Fernando renewed and

[028-316]repeated his oaths, invoked as witnesses fresh saints in addition to

[028-317]the former ones, called down upon himself a thousand curses hereafter

[028-318]should he fail to keep his promise, shed more tears, redoubled his

[028-319]sighs and pressed me closer in his arms, from which he had never

[028-320]allowed me to escape; and so I was left by my maid, and ceased to be

[028-321]one, and he became a traitor and a perjured man.

[028-322]“The day which followed the night of my misfortune did not come so

[028-323]quickly, I imagine, as Don Fernando wished, for when desire has

[028-324]attained its object, the greatest pleasure is to fly from the scene of

[028-325]pleasure. I say so because Don Fernando made all haste to leave me, and

[028-326]by the adroitness of my maid, who was indeed the one who had admitted

[028-327]him, gained the street before daybreak; but on taking leave of me he

[028-328]told me, though not with as much earnestness and fervour as when he

[028-329]came, that I might rest assured of his faith and of the sanctity and

[028-330]sincerity of his oaths; and to confirm his words he drew a rich ring

[028-331]off his finger and placed it upon mine. He then took his departure and

[028-332]I was left, I know not whether sorrowful or happy; all I can say is, I

[028-333]was left agitated and troubled in mind and almost bewildered by what

[028-334]had taken place, and I had not the spirit, or else it did not occur to

[028-335]me, to chide my maid for the treachery she had been guilty of in

[028-336]concealing Don Fernando in my chamber; for as yet I was unable to make

[028-337]up my mind whether what had befallen me was for good or evil. I told

[028-338]Don Fernando at parting, that as I was now his, he might see me on

[028-339]other nights in the same way, until it should be his pleasure to let

[028-340]the matter become known; but, except the following night, he came no

[028-341]more, nor for more than a month could I catch a glimpse of him in the

[028-342]street or in church, while I wearied myself with watching for one;

[028-343]although I knew he was in the town, and almost every day went out

[028-344]hunting, a pastime he was very fond of. I remember well how sad and

[028-345]dreary those days and hours were to me; I remember well how I began to

[028-346]doubt as they went by, and even to lose confidence in the faith of Don

[028-347]Fernando; and I remember, too, how my maid heard those words in reproof

[028-348]of her audacity that she had not heard before, and how I was forced to

[028-349]put a constraint on my tears and on the expression of my countenance,

[028-350]not to give my parents cause to ask me why I was so melancholy, and

[028-351]drive me to invent falsehoods in reply. But all this was suddenly

[028-352]brought to an end, for the time came when all such considerations were

[028-353]disregarded, and there was no further question of honour, when my
