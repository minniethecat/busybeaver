# Teresa Panza

**Teresa Panza** (*Teresa Cascajo* by birth) is the wife of [[sancho-panza|Sancho Panza]] and the mother of their daughter, Sanchica. A peasant woman of blunt common sense and deep practicality, Teresa serves as the domestic anchor that Sancho's adventures continually strain against. In Part II, her correspondence with her husband during his governorship of Barataria provides some of the novel's most vivid glimpses into rural Spanish life.

## Character Profile

Teresa is introduced indirectly — first mentioned by name as the person who will notice Sancho's absence — and her full character emerges in Part II. She is illiterate, practical to the point of suspicion, and fiercely protective of her family's modest but honest station. When Sancho speaks ambitiously of governorships and titles, Teresa cuts through his pretensions with earthy clarity:

> "called Teresa Panza, though by right I ought to be called Teresa Cascajo; but kings go where the laws lead them, and I am content with this name without any 'Doña' tacked on to it"[057-119]

Her refusal to adopt the honorific *Doña* — which Sancho eagerly embraces for her — encapsulates her character: she knows who she is and sees no reason to pretend otherwise.

## The Letters

Teresa's correspondence forms one of Part II's most charming subplots. When Sancho becomes governor of Barataria and the [[the-duchess|Duchess]] sends her a letter, Teresa responds with a dictated message that reveals her entire worldview — practical, suspicious of aristocratic gifts, and more concerned with acorns and cheese than with titles:

> "All were greatly amused by Teresa Panza's letter, but particularly the duke and duchess"[104-155]

She also receives letters from Sancho describing his governorship, to which she responds with news of the village: who is marrying whom, the price of olives, and gentle reminders that her husband should remember his origins. These letters ground the novel's most fantastical episodes in the soil of everyday life.

## Sanchica

Teresa's daughter, Sanchica (also called Mari Sancha), appears alongside her mother. Teresa's ambitions for her daughter are revealing: she wants Sanchica to marry a local man of equal station, not to rise through Sancho's improbable political career. When the Duchess offers to find Sanchica a noble match, Teresa's response is characteristically skeptical — a countess who stumbles will be mocked; a peasant girl who stumbles will be understood.

## Relationship with Sancho

Teresa's marriage to Sancho is affectionate but not idealized. She tolerates his adventures, worries about his safety, and occasionally scolds him for his foolishness. Yet she also takes genuine pride in his success — when gifts arrive from Barataria, she displays them to the village with undisguised pleasure. Her love for Sancho is the love of someone who knows her husband's flaws intimately and accepts them anyway:

> "I mean my Teresa Panza, whom I love better than my own eyelids"[122-213]

— Sancho's declaration, which Teresa would likely receive with an eye-roll and a demand that he bring home something useful from his next adventure.

## Thematic Significance

Teresa Panza represents the world that Don Quixote and Sancho leave behind — a world of labor, scarcity, and practical wisdom that neither chivalric idealism nor political ambition can erase. Her skepticism about Sancho's governorship proves prescient: the adventure ends, and Sancho returns home to the wife who, all along, had the better measure of reality.
