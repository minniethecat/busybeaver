[097-001]OF HOW THE GREAT SANCHO PANZA TOOK POSSESSION OF HIS ISLAND, AND OF HOW

HE MADE A BEGINNING IN GOVERNING

p45a.jpg (141K)


[097-002]O perpetual discoverer of the antipodes, torch of the world, eye of

[097-003]heaven, sweet stimulator of the water-coolers! Thimbraeus here, Phœbus

[097-004]there, now archer, now physician, father of poetry, inventor of music;

[097-005]thou that always risest and, notwithstanding appearances, never

[097-006]settest! To thee, O Sun, by whose aid man begetteth man, to thee I

[097-007]appeal to help me and lighten the darkness of my wit that I may be able

[097-008]to proceed with scrupulous exactitude in giving an account of the great

[097-009]Sancho Panza’s government; for without thee I feel myself weak, feeble,

[097-010]and uncertain.

[097-011]To come to the point, then—Sancho with all his attendants arrived at a

[097-012]village of some thousand inhabitants, and one of the largest the duke

[097-013]possessed. They informed him that it was called the island of

[097-014]Barataria, either because the name of the village was Baratario, or

[097-015]because of the joke by way of which the government had been conferred

[097-016]upon him. On reaching the gates of the town, which was a walled one,

[097-017]the municipality came forth to meet him, the bells rang out a peal, and

[097-018]the inhabitants showed every sign of general satisfaction; and with

[097-019]great pomp they conducted him to the principal church to give thanks to

[097-020]God, and then with burlesque ceremonies they presented him with the

[097-021]keys of the town, and acknowledged him as perpetual governor of the

[097-022]island of Barataria. The costume, the beard, and the fat squat figure

[097-023]of the new governor astonished all those who were not in on the secret,

[097-024]and even all who were, and they were not a few. Finally, leading him

[097-025]out of the church they carried him to the judgment seat and seated him

[097-026]on it, and the duke’s majordomo said to him, “It is an ancient custom

[097-027]in this island, señor governor, that he who comes to take possession of

[097-028]this famous island is bound to answer a question which shall be put to

[097-029]him, and which must be a somewhat knotty and difficult one; and by his

[097-030]answer the people take the measure of their new governor’s wit, and

[097-031]hail with joy or deplore his arrival accordingly.”

[097-032]While the majordomo was making this speech Sancho was gazing at several

[097-033]large letters inscribed on the wall opposite his seat, and as he could

[097-034]not read he asked what that was that was painted on the wall. The

[097-035]answer was, “Señor, there is written and recorded the day on which your

[097-036]lordship took possession of this island, and the inscription says,

[097-037]‘This day, the so-and-so of such-and-such a month and year, Señor Don

[097-038]Sancho Panza took possession of this island; many years may he enjoy

[097-039]it.’”

[097-040]“And whom do they call Don Sancho Panza?” asked Sancho.

[097-041]“Your lordship,” replied the majordomo; “for no other Panza but the one

[097-042]who is now seated in that chair has ever entered this island.”

[097-043]“Well then, let me tell you, brother,” said Sancho, “I haven’t got the

[097-044]‘Don,’ nor has anyone of my family ever had it; my name is plain Sancho

[097-045]Panza, and Sancho was my father’s name, and Sancho was my grandfather’s

[097-046]and they were all Panzas, without any Dons or Doñas tacked on; I

[097-047]suspect that in this island there are more Dons than stones; but never

[097-048]mind; God knows what I mean, and maybe if my government lasts four days

[097-049]I’ll weed out these Dons that no doubt are as great a nuisance as the

[097-050]midges, they’re so plenty. Let the majordomo go on with his question,

[097-051]and I’ll give the best answer I can, whether the people deplore or

[097-052]not.”

[097-053]At this instant there came into court two old men, one carrying a cane

[097-054]by way of a walking-stick, and the one who had no stick said, “Señor,

[097-055]some time ago I lent this good man ten gold-crowns in gold to gratify

[097-056]him and do him a service, on the condition that he was to return them

[097-057]to me whenever I should ask for them. A long time passed before I asked

[097-058]for them, for I would not put him to any greater straits to return them

[097-059]than he was in when I lent them to him; but thinking he was growing

[097-060]careless about payment I asked for them once and several times; and not

[097-061]only will he not give them back, but he denies that he owes them, and

[097-062]says I never lent him any such crowns; or if I did, that he repaid

[097-063]them; and I have no witnesses either of the loan, or the payment, for

[097-064]he never paid me; I want your worship to put him to his oath, and if he

[097-065]swears he returned them to me I forgive him the debt here and before

[097-066]God.”

[097-067]“What say you to this, good old man, you with the stick?” said Sancho.

[097-068]To which the old man replied, “I admit, señor, that he lent them to me;

[097-069]but let your worship lower your staff, and as he leaves it to my oath,

[097-070]I’ll swear that I gave them back, and paid him really and truly.”

[097-071]The governor lowered the staff, and as he did so the old man who had

[097-072]the stick handed it to the other old man to hold for him while he

[097-073]swore, as if he found it in his way; and then laid his hand on the

[097-074]cross of the staff, saying that it was true the ten crowns that were

[097-075]demanded of him had been lent him; but that he had with his own hand

[097-076]given them back into the hand of the other, and that he, not

[097-077]recollecting it, was always asking for them.

[097-078]Seeing this the great governor asked the creditor what answer he had to

[097-079]make to what his opponent said. He said that no doubt his debtor had

[097-080]told the truth, for he believed him to be an honest man and a good

[097-081]Christian, and he himself must have forgotten when and how he had given

[097-082]him back the crowns; and that from that time forth he would make no

[097-083]further demand upon him.

[097-084]The debtor took his stick again, and bowing his head left the court.

[097-085]Observing this, and how, without another word, he made off, and

[097-086]observing too the resignation of the plaintiff, Sancho buried his head

[097-087]in his bosom and remained for a short space in deep thought, with the

[097-088]forefinger of his right hand on his brow and nose; then he raised his

[097-089]head and bade them call back the old man with the stick, for he had

[097-090]already taken his departure. They brought him back, and as soon as

[097-091]Sancho saw him he said, “Honest man, give me that stick, for I want

[097-092]it.”

[097-093]“Willingly,” said the old man; “here it is señor,” and he put it into

[097-094]his hand.

[097-095]Sancho took it and, handing it to the other old man, said to him, “Go,

[097-096]and God be with you; for now you are paid.”

[097-097]“I, señor!” returned the old man; “why, is this cane worth ten

[097-098]gold-crowns?”

[097-099]“Yes,” said the governor, “or if not I am the greatest dolt in the

[097-100]world; now you will see whether I have got the headpiece to govern a

[097-101]whole kingdom;” and he ordered the cane to be broken in two, there, in

[097-102]the presence of all. It was done, and in the middle of it they found

[097-103]ten gold-crowns. All were filled with amazement, and looked upon their

[097-104]governor as another Solomon. They asked him how he had come to the

[097-105]conclusion that the ten crowns were in the cane; he replied, that

[097-106]observing how the old man who swore gave the stick to his opponent

[097-107]while he was taking the oath, and swore that he had really and truly

[097-108]given him the crowns, and how as soon as he had done swearing he asked

[097-109]for the stick again, it came into his head that the sum demanded must

[097-110]be inside it; and from this he said it might be seen that God sometimes

[097-111]guides those who govern in their judgments, even though they may be

[097-112]fools; besides he had himself heard the curate of his village mention

[097-113]just such another case, and he had so good a memory, that if it was not

[097-114]that he forgot everything he wished to remember, there would not be

[097-115]such a memory in all the island. To conclude, the old men went off, one

[097-116]crestfallen, and the other in high contentment, all who were present

[097-117]were astonished, and he who was recording the words, deeds, and

[097-118]movements of Sancho could not make up his mind whether he was to look

[097-119]upon him and set him down as a fool or as a man of sense.

[097-120]As soon as this case was disposed of, there came into court a woman

[097-121]holding on with a tight grip to a man dressed like a well-to-do cattle

[097-122]dealer, and she came forward making a great outcry and exclaiming,

[097-123]“Justice, señor governor, justice! and if I don’t get it on earth I’ll

[097-124]go look for it in heaven. Señor governor of my soul, this wicked man

[097-125]caught me in the middle of the fields here and used my body as if it

[097-126]was an ill-washed rag, and, woe is me! got from me what I had kept

[097-127]these three-and-twenty years and more, defending it against Moors and

[097-128]Christians, natives and strangers; and I always as hard as an oak, and

[097-129]keeping myself as pure as a salamander in the fire, or wool among the

[097-130]brambles, for this good fellow to come now with clean hands to handle

[097-131]me!”

[097-132]“It remains to be proved whether this gallant has clean hands or not,”

[097-133]said Sancho; and turning to the man he asked him what he had to say in

[097-134]answer to the woman’s charge.

[097-135]He all in confusion made answer, “Sirs, I am a poor pig dealer, and

[097-136]this morning I left the village to sell (saving your presence) four

[097-137]pigs, and between dues and cribbings they got out of me little less

[097-138]than the worth of them. As I was returning to my village I fell in on

[097-139]the road with this good dame, and the devil who makes a coil and a mess

[097-140]out of everything, yoked us together. I paid her fairly, but she not

[097-141]contented laid hold of me and never let go until she brought me here;

[097-142]she says I forced her, but she lies by the oath I swear or am ready to

[097-143]swear; and this is the whole truth and every particle of it.”

[097-144]The governor on this asked him if he had any money in silver about him;

[097-145]he said he had about twenty ducats in a leather purse in his bosom. The

[097-146]governor bade him take it out and hand it to the complainant; he obeyed

[097-147]trembling; the woman took it, and making a thousand salaams to all and

[097-148]praying to God for the long life and health of the señor governor who

[097-149]had such regard for distressed orphans and virgins, she hurried out of

[097-150]court with the purse grasped in both her hands, first looking, however,

[097-151]to see if the money it contained was silver.

[097-152]As soon as she was gone Sancho said to the cattle dealer, whose tears

[097-153]were already starting and whose eyes and heart were following his

[097-154]purse, “Good fellow, go after that woman and take the purse from her,

[097-155]by force even, and come back with it here;” and he did not say it to

[097-156]one who was a fool or deaf, for the man was off like a flash of

[097-157]lightning, and ran to do as he was bid.

[097-158]All the bystanders waited anxiously to see the end of the case, and

[097-159]presently both man and woman came back at even closer grips than

[097-160]before, she with her petticoat up and the purse in the lap of it, and

[097-161]he struggling hard to take it from her, but all to no purpose, so stout

[097-162]was the woman’s defence, she all the while crying out, “Justice from

[097-163]God and the world! see here, señor governor, the shamelessness and

[097-164]boldness of this villain, who in the middle of the town, in the middle

[097-165]of the street, wanted to take from me the purse your worship bade him

[097-166]give me.”

[097-167]“And did he take it?” asked the governor.

[097-168]“Take it!” said the woman; “I’d let my life be taken from me sooner

[097-169]than the purse. A pretty child I’d be! It’s another sort of cat they

[097-170]must throw in my face, and not that poor scurvy knave. Pincers and

[097-171]hammers, mallets and chisels would not get it out of my grip; no, nor

[097-172]lions’ claws; the soul from out of my body first!”

[097-173]“She is right,” said the man; “I own myself beaten and powerless; I

[097-174]confess I haven’t the strength to take it from her;” and he let go his

[097-175]hold of her.

[097-176]Upon this the governor said to the woman, “Let me see that purse, my

[097-177]worthy and sturdy friend.” She handed it to him at once, and the

[097-178]governor returned it to the man, and said to the unforced mistress of

[097-179]force, “Sister, if you had shown as much, or only half as much, spirit

[097-180]and vigour in defending your body as you have shown in defending that

[097-181]purse, the strength of Hercules could not have forced you. Be off, and

[097-182]God speed you, and bad luck to you, and don’t show your face in all

[097-183]this island, or within six leagues of it on any side, under pain of two

[097-184]hundred lashes; be off at once, I say, you shameless, cheating shrew.”

[097-185]The woman was cowed and went off disconsolately, hanging her head; and

[097-186]the governor said to the man, “Honest man, go home with your money, and

[097-187]God speed you; and for the future, if you don’t want to lose it, see

[097-188]that you don’t take it into your head to yoke with anybody.” The man

[097-189]thanked him as clumsily as he could and went his way, and the

[097-190]bystanders were again filled with admiration at their new governor’s

[097-191]judgments and sentences.

[097-192]Next, two men, one apparently a farm labourer, and the other a tailor,

[097-193]for he had a pair of shears in his hand, presented themselves before

[097-194]him, and the tailor said, “Señor governor, this labourer and I come

[097-195]before your worship by reason of this honest man coming to my shop

[097-196]yesterday (for saving everybody’s presence I’m a passed tailor, God be

[097-197]thanked), and putting a piece of cloth into my hands and asking me,

[097-198]‘Señor, will there be enough in this cloth to make me a cap?’ Measuring

[097-199]the cloth I said there would. He probably suspected—as I supposed, and

[097-200]I supposed right—that I wanted to steal some of the cloth, led to think

[097-201]so by his own roguery and the bad opinion people have of tailors; and

[097-202]he told me to see if there would be enough for two. I guessed what he

[097-203]would be at, and I said ‘yes.’ He, still following up his original

[097-204]unworthy notion, went on adding cap after cap, and I ‘yes’ after ‘yes,’

[097-205]until we got as far as five. He has just this moment come for them; I

[097-206]gave them to him, but he won’t pay me for the making; on the contrary,

[097-207]he calls upon me to pay him, or else return his cloth.”

[097-208]“Is all this true, brother?” said Sancho.

[097-209]“Yes,” replied the man; “but will your worship make him show the five

[097-210]caps he has made me?”

[097-211]“With all my heart,” said the tailor; and drawing his hand from under

[097-212]his cloak he showed five caps stuck upon the five fingers of it, and

[097-213]said, “there are the caps this good man asks for; and by God and upon

[097-214]my conscience I haven’t a scrap of cloth left, and I’ll let the work be

[097-215]examined by the inspectors of the trade.”

[097-216]All present laughed at the number of caps and the novelty of the suit;

[097-217]Sancho set himself to think for a moment, and then said, “It seems to

[097-218]me that in this case it is not necessary to deliver long-winded

[097-219]arguments, but only to give off-hand the judgment of an honest man; and

[097-220]so my decision is that the tailor lose the making and the labourer the

[097-221]cloth, and that the caps go to the prisoners in the gaol, and let there

[097-222]be no more about it.”

[097-223]If the previous decision about the cattle dealer’s purse excited the

[097-224]admiration of the bystanders, this provoked their laughter; however,

[097-225]the governor’s orders were after all executed. All this, having been

[097-226]taken down by his chronicler, was at once despatched to the duke, who

[097-227]was looking out for it with great eagerness; and here let us leave the

[097-228]good Sancho; for his master, sorely troubled in mind by Altisidora’s

[097-229]music, has pressing claims upon us now.
