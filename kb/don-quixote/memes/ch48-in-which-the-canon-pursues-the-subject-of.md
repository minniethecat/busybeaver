[048-001]IN WHICH THE CANON PURSUES THE SUBJECT OF THE BOOKS OF CHIVALRY, WITH

OTHER MATTERS WORTHY OF HIS WIT

c48a.jpg (80K)


[048-002]“It is as you say, señor canon,” said the curate; “and for that reason

[048-003]those who have hitherto written books of the sort deserve all the more

[048-004]censure for writing without paying any attention to good taste or the

[048-005]rules of art, by which they might guide themselves and become as famous

[048-006]in prose as the two princes of Greek and Latin poetry are in verse.”

[048-007]“I myself, at any rate,” said the canon, “was once tempted to write a

[048-008]book of chivalry in which all the points I have mentioned were to be

[048-009]observed; and if I must own the truth I have more than a hundred sheets

[048-010]written; and to try if it came up to my own opinion of it, I showed

[048-011]them to persons who were fond of this kind of reading, to learned and

[048-012]intelligent men as well as to ignorant people who cared for nothing but

[048-013]the pleasure of listening to nonsense, and from all I obtained

[048-014]flattering approval; nevertheless I proceeded no farther with it, as

[048-015]well because it seemed to me an occupation inconsistent with my

[048-016]profession, as because I perceived that the fools are more numerous

[048-017]than the wise; and, though it is better to be praised by the wise few

[048-018]than applauded by the foolish many, I have no mind to submit myself to

[048-019]the stupid judgment of the silly public, to whom the reading of such

[048-020]books falls for the most part.

[048-021]“But what most of all made me hold my hand and even abandon all idea of

[048-022]finishing it was an argument I put to myself taken from the plays that

[048-023]are acted now-a-days, which was in this wise: if those that are now in

[048-024]vogue, as well those that are pure invention as those founded on

[048-025]history, are, all or most of them, downright nonsense and things that

[048-026]have neither head nor tail, and yet the public listens to them with

[048-027]delight, and regards and cries them up as perfection when they are so

[048-028]far from it; and if the authors who write them, and the players who act

[048-029]them, say that this is what they must be, for the public wants this and

[048-030]will have nothing else; and that those that go by rule and work out a

[048-031]plot according to the laws of art will only find some half-dozen

[048-032]intelligent people to understand them, while all the rest remain blind

[048-033]to the merit of their composition; and that for themselves it is better

[048-034]to get bread from the many than praise from the few; then my book will

[048-035]fare the same way, after I have burnt off my eyebrows in trying to

[048-036]observe the principles I have spoken of, and I shall be ‘the tailor of

[048-037]the corner.’ And though I have sometimes endeavoured to convince actors

[048-038]that they are mistaken in this notion they have adopted, and that they

[048-039]would attract more people, and get more credit, by producing plays in

[048-040]accordance with the rules of art, than by absurd ones, they are so

[048-041]thoroughly wedded to their own opinion that no argument or evidence can

[048-042]wean them from it.

[048-043]“I remember saying one day to one of these obstinate fellows, ‘Tell me,

[048-044]do you not recollect that a few years ago, there were three tragedies

[048-045]acted in Spain, written by a famous poet of these kingdoms, which were

[048-046]such that they filled all who heard them with admiration, delight, and

[048-047]interest, the ignorant as well as the wise, the masses as well as the

[048-048]higher orders, and brought in more money to the performers, these three

[048-049]alone, than thirty of the best that have been since produced?’

[048-050]“‘No doubt,’ replied the actor in question, ‘you mean the “Isabella,”

[048-051]the “Phyllis,” and the “Alexandra.”’

[048-052]“‘Those are the ones I mean,’ said I; ‘and see if they did not observe

[048-053]the principles of art, and if, by observing them, they failed to show

[048-054]their superiority and please all the world; so that the fault does not

[048-055]lie with the public that insists upon nonsense, but with those who

[048-056]don’t know how to produce something else. “The Ingratitude Revenged”

[048-057]was not nonsense, nor was there any in “The Numantia,” nor any to be

[048-058]found in “The Merchant Lover,” nor yet in “The Friendly Fair Foe,” nor

[048-059]in some others that have been written by certain gifted poets, to their

[048-060]own fame and renown, and to the profit of those that brought them out;’

[048-061]some further remarks I added to these, with which, I think, I left him

[048-062]rather dumbfoundered, but not so satisfied or convinced that I could

[048-063]disabuse him of his error.”

[048-064]“You have touched upon a subject, señor canon,” observed the curate

[048-065]here, “that has awakened an old enmity I have against the plays in

[048-066]vogue at the present day, quite as strong as that which I bear to the

[048-067]books of chivalry; for while the drama, according to Tully, should be

[048-068]the mirror of human life, the model of manners, and the image of the

[048-069]truth, those which are presented now-a-days are mirrors of nonsense,

[048-070]models of folly, and images of lewdness. For what greater nonsense can

[048-071]there be in connection with what we are now discussing than for an

[048-072]infant to appear in swaddling clothes in the first scene of the first

[048-073]act, and in the second a grown-up bearded man? Or what greater

[048-074]absurdity can there be than putting before us an old man as a

[048-075]swashbuckler, a young man as a poltroon, a lackey using fine language,

[048-076]a page giving sage advice, a king plying as a porter, a princess who is

[048-077]a kitchen-maid? And then what shall I say of their attention to the

[048-078]time in which the action they represent may or can take place, save

[048-079]that I have seen a play where the first act began in Europe, the second

[048-080]in Asia, the third finished in Africa, and no doubt, had it been in

[048-081]four acts, the fourth would have ended in America, and so it would have

[048-082]been laid in all four quarters of the globe? And if truth to life is

[048-083]the main thing the drama should keep in view, how is it possible for

[048-084]any average understanding to be satisfied when the action is supposed

[048-085]to pass in the time of King Pepin or Charlemagne, and the principal

[048-086]personage in it they represent to be the Emperor Heraclius who entered

[048-087]Jerusalem with the cross and won the Holy Sepulchre, like Godfrey of

[048-088]Bouillon, there being years innumerable between the one and the other?

[048-089]or, if the play is based on fiction and historical facts are

[048-090]introduced, or bits of what occurred to different people and at

[048-091]different times mixed up with it, all, not only without any semblance

[048-092]of probability, but with obvious errors that from every point of view

[048-093]are inexcusable? And the worst of it is, there are ignorant people who

[048-094]say that this is perfection, and that anything beyond this is affected

[048-095]refinement. And then if we turn to sacred dramas—what miracles they

[048-096]invent in them! What apocryphal, ill-devised incidents, attributing to

[048-097]one saint the miracles of another! And even in secular plays they

[048-098]venture to introduce miracles without any reason or object except that

[048-099]they think some such miracle, or transformation as they call it, will

[048-100]come in well to astonish stupid people and draw them to the play. All

[048-101]this tends to the prejudice of the truth and the corruption of history,

[048-102]nay more, to the reproach of the wits of Spain; for foreigners who

[048-103]scrupulously observe the laws of the drama look upon us as barbarous

[048-104]and ignorant, when they see the absurdity and nonsense of the plays we

[048-105]produce. Nor will it be a sufficient excuse to say that the chief

[048-106]object well-ordered governments have in view when they permit plays to

[048-107]be performed in public is to entertain the people with some harmless

[048-108]amusement occasionally, and keep it from those evil humours which

[048-109]idleness is apt to engender; and that, as this may be attained by any

[048-110]sort of play, good or bad, there is no need to lay down laws, or bind

[048-111]those who write or act them to make them as they ought to be made,

[048-112]since, as I say, the object sought for may be secured by any sort. To

[048-113]this I would reply that the same end would be, beyond all comparison,

[048-114]better attained by means of good plays than by those that are not so;

[048-115]for after listening to an artistic and properly constructed play, the

[048-116]hearer will come away enlivened by the jests, instructed by the serious

[048-117]parts, full of admiration at the incidents, his wits sharpened by the

[048-118]arguments, warned by the tricks, all the wiser for the examples,

[048-119]inflamed against vice, and in love with virtue; for in all these ways a

[048-120]good play will stimulate the mind of the hearer be he ever so boorish

[048-121]or dull; and of all impossibilities the greatest is that a play endowed

[048-122]with all these qualities will not entertain, satisfy, and please much

[048-123]more than one wanting in them, like the greater number of those which

[048-124]are commonly acted now-a-days. Nor are the poets who write them to be

[048-125]blamed for this; for some there are among them who are perfectly well

[048-126]aware of their faults, and know what they ought to do; but as plays

[048-127]have become a salable commodity, they say, and with truth, that the

[048-128]actors will not buy them unless they are after this fashion; and so the

[048-129]poet tries to adapt himself to the requirements of the actor who is to

[048-130]pay him for his work. And that this is the truth may be seen by the

[048-131]countless plays that a most fertile wit of these kingdoms has written,

[048-132]with so much brilliancy, so much grace and gaiety, such polished

[048-133]versification, such choice language, such profound reflections, and in

[048-134]a word, so rich in eloquence and elevation of style, that he has filled

[048-135]the world with his fame; and yet, in consequence of his desire to suit

[048-136]the taste of the actors, they have not all, as some of them have, come

[048-137]as near perfection as they ought. Others write plays with such

[048-138]heedlessness that, after they have been acted, the actors have to fly

[048-139]and abscond, afraid of being punished, as they often have been, for

[048-140]having acted something offensive to some king or other, or insulting to

[048-141]some noble family. All which evils, and many more that I say nothing

[048-142]of, would be removed if there were some intelligent and sensible person

[048-143]at the capital to examine all plays before they were acted, not only

[048-144]those produced in the capital itself, but all that were intended to be

[048-145]acted in Spain; without whose approval, seal, and signature, no local

[048-146]magistracy should allow any play to be acted. In that case actors would

[048-147]take care to send their plays to the capital, and could act them in

[048-148]safety, and those who write them would be more careful and take more

[048-149]pains with their work, standing in awe of having to submit it to the

[048-150]strict examination of one who understood the matter; and so good plays

[048-151]would be produced and the objects they aim at happily attained; as well

[048-152]the amusement of the people, as the credit of the wits of Spain, the

[048-153]interest and safety of the actors, and the saving of trouble in

[048-154]inflicting punishment on them. And if the same or some other person

[048-155]were authorised to examine the newly written books of chivalry, no

[048-156]doubt some would appear with all the perfections you have described,

[048-157]enriching our language with the gracious and precious treasure of

[048-158]eloquence, and driving the old books into obscurity before the light of

[048-159]the new ones that would come out for the harmless entertainment, not

[048-160]merely of the idle but of the very busiest; for the bow cannot be

[048-161]always bent, nor can weak human nature exist without some lawful

[048-162]amusement.”

[048-163]The canon and the curate had proceeded thus far with their

[048-164]conversation, when the barber, coming forward, joined them, and said to

[048-165]the curate, “This is the spot, señor licentiate, that I said was a good

[048-166]one for fresh and plentiful pasture for the oxen, while we take our

[048-167]noontide rest.”

[048-168]“And so it seems,” returned the curate, and he told the canon what he

[048-169]proposed to do, on which he too made up his mind to halt with them,

[048-170]attracted by the aspect of the fair valley that lay before their eyes;

[048-171]and to enjoy it as well as the conversation of the curate, to whom he

[048-172]had begun to take a fancy, and also to learn more particulars about the

[048-173]doings of Don Quixote, he desired some of his servants to go on to the

[048-174]inn, which was not far distant, and fetch from it what eatables there

[048-175]might be for the whole party, as he meant to rest for the afternoon

[048-176]where he was; to which one of his servants replied that the sumpter

[048-177]mule, which by this time ought to have reached the inn, carried

[048-178]provisions enough to make it unnecessary to get anything from the inn

[048-179]except barley.

[048-180]“In that case,” said the canon, “take all the beasts there, and bring

[048-181]the sumpter mule back.”

[048-182]While this was going on, Sancho, perceiving that he could speak to his

[048-183]master without having the curate and the barber, of whom he had his

[048-184]suspicions, present all the time, approached the cage in which Don

[048-185]Quixote was placed, and said, “Señor, to ease my conscience I want to

[048-186]tell you the state of the case as to your enchantment, and that is that

[048-187]these two here, with their faces covered, are the curate of our village

[048-188]and the barber; and I suspect they have hit upon this plan of carrying

[048-189]you off in this fashion, out of pure envy because your worship

[048-190]surpasses them in doing famous deeds; and if this be the truth it

[048-191]follows that you are not enchanted, but hoodwinked and made a fool of.

[048-192]And to prove this I want to ask you one thing; and if you answer me as

[048-193]I believe you will answer, you will be able to lay your finger on the

[048-194]trick, and you will see that you are not enchanted but gone wrong in

[048-195]your wits.”

[048-196]“Ask what thou wilt, Sancho my son,” returned Don Quixote, “for I will

[048-197]satisfy thee and answer all thou requirest. As to what thou sayest,

[048-198]that these who accompany us yonder are the curate and the barber, our

[048-199]neighbours and acquaintances, it is very possible that they may seem to

[048-200]be those same persons; but that they are so in reality and in fact,

[048-201]believe it not on any account; what thou art to believe and think is

[048-202]that, if they look like them, as thou sayest, it must be that those who

[048-203]have enchanted me have taken this shape and likeness; for it is easy

[048-204]for enchanters to take any form they please, and they may have taken

[048-205]those of our friends in order to make thee think as thou dost, and lead

[048-206]thee into a labyrinth of fancies from which thou wilt find no escape

[048-207]though thou hadst the cord of Theseus; and they may also have done it

[048-208]to make me uncertain in my mind, and unable to conjecture whence this

[048-209]evil comes to me; for if on the one hand thou dost tell me that the

[048-210]barber and curate of our village are here in company with us, and on

[048-211]the other I find myself shut up in a cage, and know in my heart that no

[048-212]power on earth that was not supernatural would have been able to shut

[048-213]me in, what wouldst thou have me say or think, but that my enchantment

[048-214]is of a sort that transcends all I have ever read of in all the

[048-215]histories that deal with knights-errant that have been enchanted? So

[048-216]thou mayest set thy mind at rest as to the idea that they are what thou

[048-217]sayest, for they are as much so as I am a Turk. But touching thy desire

[048-218]to ask me something, say on, and I will answer thee, though thou

[048-219]shouldst ask questions from this till to-morrow morning.”

[048-220]“May Our Lady be good to me!” said Sancho, lifting up his voice; “and

[048-221]is it possible that your worship is so thick of skull and so short of

[048-222]brains that you cannot see that what I say is the simple truth, and

[048-223]that malice has more to do with your imprisonment and misfortune than

[048-224]enchantment? But as it is so, I will prove plainly to you that you are

[048-225]not enchanted. Now tell me, so may God deliver you from this

[048-226]affliction, and so may you find yourself when you least expect it in

[048-227]the arms of my lady Dulcinea—”

[048-228]“Leave off conjuring me,” said Don Quixote, “and ask what thou wouldst

[048-229]know; I have already told thee I will answer with all possible

[048-230]precision.”

[048-231]“That is what I want,” said Sancho; “and what I would know, and have

[048-232]you tell me, without adding or leaving out anything, but telling the

[048-233]whole truth as one expects it to be told, and as it is told, by all who

[048-234]profess arms, as your worship professes them, under the title of

[048-235]knights-errant—”

[048-236]“I tell thee I will not lie in any particular,” said Don Quixote;

[048-237]“finish thy question; for in truth thou weariest me with all these

[048-238]asseverations, requirements, and precautions, Sancho.”

[048-239]“Well, I rely on the goodness and truth of my master,” said Sancho;

[048-240]“and so, because it bears upon what we are talking about, I would ask,

[048-241]speaking with all reverence, whether since your worship has been shut

[048-242]up and, as you think, enchanted in this cage, you have felt any desire

[048-243]or inclination to go anywhere, as the saying is?”

[048-244]“I do not understand ‘going anywhere,’” said Don Quixote; “explain

[048-245]thyself more clearly, Sancho, if thou wouldst have me give an answer to

[048-246]the point.”

[048-247]“Is it possible,” said Sancho, “that your worship does not understand

[048-248]‘going anywhere’? Why, the schoolboys know that from the time they were

[048-249]babes. Well then, you must know I mean have you had any desire to do

[048-250]what cannot be avoided?”

[048-251]“Ah! now I understand thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “yes, often, and

[048-252]even this minute; get me out of this strait, or all will not go right.”
