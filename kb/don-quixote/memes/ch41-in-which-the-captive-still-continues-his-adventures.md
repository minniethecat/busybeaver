IN WHICH THE CAPTIVE STILL CONTINUES HIS ADVENTURES

c41a.jpg (106K)


[041-001]Before fifteen days were over our renegade had already purchased an

[041-002]excellent vessel with room for more than thirty persons; and to make

[041-003]the transaction safe and lend a colour to it, he thought it well to

[041-004]make, as he did, a voyage to a place called Shershel, twenty leagues

[041-005]from Algiers on the Oran side, where there is an extensive trade in

[041-006]dried figs. Two or three times he made this voyage in company with the

[041-007]Tagarin already mentioned. The Moors of Aragon are called Tagarins in

[041-008]Barbary, and those of Granada Mudéjares; but in the Kingdom of Fez they

[041-009]call the Mudéjares Elches, and they are the people the king chiefly

[041-010]employs in war. To proceed: every time he passed with his vessel he

[041-011]anchored in a cove that was not two crossbow shots from the garden

[041-012]where Zoraida was waiting; and there the renegade, together with the

[041-013]two Moorish lads that rowed, used purposely to station himself, either

[041-014]going through his prayers, or else practising as a part what he meant

[041-015]to perform in earnest. And thus he would go to Zoraida’s garden and ask

[041-016]for fruit, which her father gave him, not knowing him; but though, as

[041-017]he afterwards told me, he sought to speak to Zoraida, and tell her who

[041-018]he was, and that by my orders he was to take her to the land of the

[041-019]Christians, so that she might feel satisfied and easy, he had never

[041-020]been able to do so; for the Moorish women do not allow themselves to be

[041-021]seen by any Moor or Turk, unless their husband or father bid them: with

[041-022]Christian captives they permit freedom of intercourse and

[041-023]communication, even more than might be considered proper. But for my

[041-024]part I should have been sorry if he had spoken to her, for perhaps it

[041-025]might have alarmed her to find her affairs talked of by renegades. But

[041-026]God, who ordered it otherwise, afforded no opportunity for our

[041-027]renegade’s well-meant purpose; and he, seeing how safely he could go to

[041-028]Shershel and return, and anchor when and how and where he liked, and

[041-029]that the Tagarin his partner had no will but his, and that, now I was

[041-030]ransomed, all we wanted was to find some Christians to row, told me to

[041-031]look out for any I should be willing to take with me, over and above

[041-032]those who had been ransomed, and to engage them for the next Friday,

[041-033]which he fixed upon for our departure. On this I spoke to twelve

[041-034]Spaniards, all stout rowers, and such as could most easily leave the

[041-035]city; but it was no easy matter to find so many just then, because

[041-036]there were twenty ships out on a cruise and they had taken all the

[041-037]rowers with them; and these would not have been found were it not that

[041-038]their master remained at home that summer without going to sea in order

[041-039]to finish a galliot that he had upon the stocks. To these men I said

[041-040]nothing more than that the next Friday in the evening they were to come

[041-041]out stealthily one by one and hang about Hadji Morato’s garden, waiting

[041-042]for me there until I came. These directions I gave each one separately,

[041-043]with orders that if they saw any other Christians there they were not

[041-044]to say anything to them except that I had directed them to wait at that

[041-045]spot.

[041-046]This preliminary having been settled, another still more necessary step

[041-047]had to be taken, which was to let Zoraida know how matters stood that

[041-048]she might be prepared and forewarned, so as not to be taken by surprise

[041-049]if we were suddenly to seize upon her before she thought the

[041-050]Christians’ vessel could have returned. I determined, therefore, to go

[041-051]to the garden and try if I could speak to her; and the day before my

[041-052]departure I went there under the pretence of gathering herbs. The first

[041-053]person I met was her father, who addressed me in the language that all

[041-054]over Barbary and even in Constantinople is the medium between captives

[041-055]and Moors, and is neither Morisco nor Castilian, nor of any other

[041-056]nation, but a mixture of all languages, by means of which we can all

[041-057]understand one another. In this sort of language, I say, he asked me

[041-058]what I wanted in his garden, and to whom I belonged. I replied that I

[041-059]was a slave of the Arnaut Mami (for I knew as a certainty that he was a

[041-060]very great friend of his), and that I wanted some herbs to make a

[041-061]salad. He asked me then whether I were on ransom or not, and what my

[041-062]master demanded for me. While these questions and answers were

[041-063]proceeding, the fair Zoraida, who had already perceived me some time

[041-064]before, came out of the house in the garden, and as Moorish women are

[041-065]by no means particular about letting themselves be seen by Christians,

[041-066]or, as I have said before, at all coy, she had no hesitation in coming

[041-067]to where her father stood with me; moreover her father, seeing her

[041-068]approaching slowly, called to her to come. It would be beyond my power

[041-069]now to describe to you the great beauty, the high-bred air, the

[041-070]brilliant attire of my beloved Zoraida as she presented herself before

[041-071]my eyes. I will content myself with saying that more pearls hung from

[041-072]her fair neck, her ears, and her hair than she had hairs on her head.

[041-073]On her ankles, which as is customary were bare, she had carcajes (for

[041-074]so bracelets or anklets are called in Morisco) of the purest gold, set

[041-075]with so many diamonds that she told me afterwards her father valued

[041-076]them at ten thousand doubloons, and those she had on her wrists were

[041-077]worth as much more. The pearls were in profusion and very fine, for the

[041-078]highest display and adornment of the Moorish women is decking

[041-079]themselves with rich pearls and seed-pearls; and of these there are

[041-080]therefore more among the Moors than among any other people. Zoraida’s

[041-081]father had to the reputation of possessing a great number, and the

[041-082]purest in all Algiers, and of possessing also more than two hundred

[041-083]thousand Spanish crowns; and she, who is now mistress of me only, was

[041-084]mistress of all this. Whether thus adorned she would have been

[041-085]beautiful or not, and what she must have been in her prosperity, may be

[041-086]imagined from the beauty remaining to her after so many hardships; for,

[041-087]as everyone knows, the beauty of some women has its times and its

[041-088]seasons, and is increased or diminished by chance causes; and naturally

[041-089]the emotions of the mind will heighten or impair it, though indeed more

[041-090]frequently they totally destroy it. In a word she presented herself

[041-091]before me that day attired with the utmost splendour, and supremely

[041-092]beautiful; at any rate, she seemed to me the most beautiful object I

[041-093]had ever seen; and when, besides, I thought of all I owed to her I felt

[041-094]as though I had before me some heavenly being come to earth to bring me

[041-095]relief and happiness.

[041-096]As she approached her father told her in his own language that I was a

[041-097]captive belonging to his friend the Arnaut Mami, and that I had come

[041-098]for salad.

[041-099]She took up the conversation, and in that mixture of tongues I have

[041-100]spoken of she asked me if I was a gentleman, and why I was not

[041-101]ransomed.

[041-102]I answered that I was already ransomed, and that by the price it might

[041-103]be seen what value my master set on me, as they had given one thousand

[041-104]five hundred zoltanis for me; to which she replied, “Hadst thou been my

[041-105]father’s, I can tell thee, I would not have let him part with thee for

[041-106]twice as much, for you Christians always tell lies about yourselves and

[041-107]make yourselves out poor to cheat the Moors.”

[041-108]“That may be, lady,” said I; “but indeed I dealt truthfully with my

[041-109]master, as I do and mean to do with everybody in the world.”

[041-110]“And when dost thou go?” said Zoraida.

[041-111]“To-morrow, I think,” said I, “for there is a vessel here from France

[041-112]which sails to-morrow, and I think I shall go in her.”

[041-113]“Would it not be better,” said Zoraida, “to wait for the arrival of

[041-114]ships from Spain and go with them and not with the French who are not

[041-115]your friends?”

[041-116]“No,” said I; “though if there were intelligence that a vessel were now

[041-117]coming from Spain it is true I might, perhaps, wait for it; however, it

[041-118]is more likely I shall depart to-morrow, for the longing I feel to

[041-119]return to my country and to those I love is so great that it will not

[041-120]allow me to wait for another opportunity, however more convenient, if

[041-121]it be delayed.”

[041-122]“No doubt thou art married in thine own country,” said Zoraida, “and

[041-123]for that reason thou art anxious to go and see thy wife.”

[041-124]“I am not married,” I replied, “but I have given my promise to marry on

[041-125]my arrival there.”

[041-126]“And is the lady beautiful to whom thou hast given it?” said Zoraida.

[041-127]“So beautiful,” said I, “that, to describe her worthily and tell thee

[041-128]the truth, she is very like thee.”

[041-129]At this her father laughed very heartily and said, “By Allah,

[041-130]Christian, she must be very beautiful if she is like my daughter, who

[041-131]is the most beautiful woman in all this kingdom: only look at her well

[041-132]and thou wilt see I am telling the truth.”

[041-133]Zoraida’s father as the better linguist helped to interpret most of

[041-134]these words and phrases, for though she spoke the bastard language,

[041-135]that, as I have said, is employed there, she expressed her meaning more

[041-136]by signs than by words.

[041-137]While we were still engaged in this conversation, a Moor came running

[041-138]up, exclaiming that four Turks had leaped over the fence or wall of the

[041-139]garden, and were gathering the fruit though it was not yet ripe. The

[041-140]old man was alarmed and Zoraida too, for the Moors commonly, and, so to

[041-141]speak, instinctively have a dread of the Turks, but particularly of the

[041-142]soldiers, who are so insolent and domineering to the Moors who are

[041-143]under their power that they treat them worse than if they were their

[041-144]slaves. Her father said to Zoraida, “Daughter, retire into the house

[041-145]and shut thyself in while I go and speak to these dogs; and thou,

[041-146]Christian, pick thy herbs, and go in peace, and Allah bring thee safe

[041-147]to thy own country.”

[041-148]I bowed, and he went away to look for the Turks, leaving me alone with

[041-149]Zoraida, who made as if she were about to retire as her father bade

[041-150]her; but the moment he was concealed by the trees of the garden,

[041-151]turning to me with her eyes full of tears she said, “Tameji, cristiano,

[041-152]tameji?” that is to say, “Art thou going, Christian, art thou going?”

[041-153]I made answer, “Yes, lady, but not without thee, come what may: be on

[041-154]the watch for me on the next Juma, and be not alarmed when thou seest

[041-155]us; for most surely we shall go to the land of the Christians.”

[041-156]This I said in such a way that she understood perfectly all that passed

[041-157]between us, and throwing her arm round my neck she began with feeble

[041-158]steps to move towards the house; but as fate would have it (and it

[041-159]might have been very unfortunate if Heaven had not otherwise ordered

[041-160]it), just as we were moving on in the manner and position I have

[041-161]described, with her arm round my neck, her father, as he returned after

[041-162]having sent away the Turks, saw how we were walking and we perceived

[041-163]that he saw us; but Zoraida, ready and quickwitted, took care not to

[041-164]remove her arm from my neck, but on the contrary drew closer to me and

[041-165]laid her head on my breast, bending her knees a little and showing all

[041-166]the signs and tokens of fainting, while I at the same time made it seem

[041-167]as though I were supporting her against my will. Her father came

[041-168]running up to where we were, and seeing his daughter in this state

[041-169]asked what was the matter with her; she, however, giving no answer, he

[041-170]said, “No doubt she has fainted in alarm at the entrance of those

[041-171]dogs,” and taking her from mine he drew her to his own breast, while

[041-172]she sighing, her eyes still wet with tears, said again, “Ameji,

[041-173]cristiano, ameji”—“Go, Christian, go.” To this her father replied,

[041-174]“There is no need, daughter, for the Christian to go, for he has done

[041-175]thee no harm, and the Turks have now gone; feel no alarm, there is

[041-176]nothing to hurt thee, for as I say, the Turks at my request have gone

[041-177]back the way they came.”

[041-178]“It was they who terrified her, as thou hast said, señor,” said I to

[041-179]her father; “but since she tells me to go, I have no wish to displease

[041-180]her: peace be with thee, and with thy leave I will come back to this

[041-181]garden for herbs if need be, for my master says there are nowhere

[041-182]better herbs for salad than here.”

[041-183]“Come back for any thou hast need of,” replied Hadji Morato; “for my

[041-184]daughter does not speak thus because she is displeased with thee or any

[041-185]Christian: she only meant that the Turks should go, not thou; or that

[041-186]it was time for thee to look for thy herbs.”

[041-187]With this I at once took my leave of both; and she, looking as though

[041-188]her heart were breaking, retired with her father. While pretending to

[041-189]look for herbs I made the round of the garden at my ease, and studied

[041-190]carefully all the approaches and outlets, and the fastenings of the

[041-191]house and everything that could be taken advantage of to make our task

[041-192]easy.

[041-193]Having done so I went and gave an account of all that had taken place

[041-194]to the renegade and my comrades, and looked forward with impatience to

[041-195]the hour when, all fear at an end, I should find myself in possession

[041-196]of the prize which fortune held out to me in the fair and lovely

[041-197]Zoraida. The time passed at length, and the appointed day we so longed

[041-198]for arrived; and, all following out the arrangement and plan which,

[041-199]after careful consideration and many a long discussion, we had decided

[041-200]upon, we succeeded as fully as we could have wished; for on the Friday

[041-201]following the day upon which I spoke to Zoraida in the garden, the

[041-202]renegade anchored his vessel at nightfall almost opposite the spot

[041-203]where she was. The Christians who were to row were ready and in hiding

[041-204]in different places round about, all waiting for me, anxious and

[041-205]elated, and eager to attack the vessel they had before their eyes; for

[041-206]they did not know the renegade’s plan, but expected that they were to

[041-207]gain their liberty by force of arms and by killing the Moors who were

[041-208]on board the vessel. As soon, then, as I and my comrades made our

[041-209]appearance, all those that were in hiding seeing us came and joined us.

[041-210]It was now the time when the city gates are shut, and there was no one

[041-211]to be seen in all the space outside. When we were collected together we

[041-212]debated whether it would be better first to go for Zoraida, or to make

[041-213]prisoners of the Moorish rowers who rowed in the vessel; but while we

[041-214]were still uncertain our renegade came up asking us what kept us, as it

[041-215]was now the time, and all the Moors were off their guard and most of

[041-216]them asleep. We told him why we hesitated, but he said it was of more

[041-217]importance first to secure the vessel, which could be done with the

[041-218]greatest ease and without any danger, and then we could go for Zoraida.

[041-219]We all approved of what he said, and so without further delay, guided

[041-220]by him we made for the vessel, and he leaping on board first, drew his

[041-221]cutlass and said in Morisco, “Let no one stir from this if he does not

[041-222]want it to cost him his life.” By this almost all the Christians were

[041-223]on board, and the Moors, who were fainthearted, hearing their captain

[041-224]speak in this way, were cowed, and without any one of them taking to

[041-225]his arms (and indeed they had few or hardly any) they submitted without

[041-226]saying a word to be bound by the Christians, who quickly secured them,

[041-227]threatening them that if they raised any kind of outcry they would be

[041-228]all put to the sword. This having been accomplished, and half of our

[041-229]party being left to keep guard over them, the rest of us, again taking

[041-230]the renegade as our guide, hastened towards Hadji Morato’s garden, and

[041-231]as good luck would have it, on trying the gate it opened as easily as

[041-232]if it had not been locked; and so, quite quietly and in silence, we

[041-233]reached the house without being perceived by anybody. The lovely

[041-234]Zoraida was watching for us at a window, and as soon as she perceived

[041-235]that there were people there, she asked in a low voice if we were

[041-236]“Nizarani,” as much as to say or ask if we were Christians. I answered

[041-237]that we were, and begged her to come down. As soon as she recognised me

[041-238]she did not delay an instant, but without answering a word came down

[041-239]immediately, opened the door and presented herself before us all, so

[041-240]beautiful and so richly attired that I cannot attempt to describe her.

[041-241]The moment I saw her I took her hand and kissed it, and the renegade

[041-242]and my two comrades did the same; and the rest, who knew nothing of the

[041-243]circumstances, did as they saw us do, for it only seemed as if we were

[041-244]returning thanks to her, and recognising her as the giver of our

[041-245]liberty. The renegade asked her in the Morisco language if her father

[041-246]was in the house. She replied that he was and that he was asleep.

[041-247]“Then it will be necessary to waken him and take him with us,” said the

[041-248]renegade, “and everything of value in this fair mansion.”

[041-249]“Nay,” said she, “my father must not on any account be touched, and

[041-250]there is nothing in the house except what I shall take, and that will

[041-251]be quite enough to enrich and satisfy all of you; wait a little and you

[041-252]shall see,” and so saying she went in, telling us she would return

[041-253]immediately and bidding us keep quiet without making any noise.

[041-254]I asked the renegade what had passed between them, and when he told me,

[041-255]I declared that nothing should be done except in accordance with the

[041-256]wishes of Zoraida, who now came back with a little trunk so full of

[041-257]gold crowns that she could scarcely carry it. Unfortunately her father

[041-258]awoke while this was going on, and hearing a noise in the garden, came

[041-259]to the window, and at once perceiving that all those who were there

[041-260]were Christians, raising a prodigiously loud outcry, he began to call

[041-261]out in Arabic, “Christians, Christians! thieves, thieves!” by which

[041-262]cries we were all thrown into the greatest fear and embarrassment; but

[041-263]the renegade seeing the danger we were in and how important it was for

[041-264]him to effect his purpose before we were heard, mounted with the utmost

[041-265]quickness to where Hadji Morato was, and with him went some of our

[041-266]party; I, however, did not dare to leave Zoraida, who had fallen almost

[041-267]fainting in my arms. To be brief, those who had gone upstairs acted so

[041-268]promptly that in an instant they came down, carrying Hadji Morato with

[041-269]his hands bound and a napkin tied over his mouth, which prevented him

[041-270]from uttering a word, warning him at the same time that to attempt to

[041-271]speak would cost him his life. When his daughter caught sight of him

[041-272]she covered her eyes so as not to see him, and her father was

[041-273]horror-stricken, not knowing how willingly she had placed herself in

[041-274]our hands. But it was now most essential for us to be on the move, and

[041-275]carefully and quickly we regained the vessel, where those who had

[041-276]remained on board were waiting for us in apprehension of some mishap

[041-277]having befallen us. It was barely two hours after night set in when we

[041-278]were all on board the vessel, where the cords were removed from the

[041-279]hands of Zoraida’s father, and the napkin from his mouth; but the

[041-280]renegade once more told him not to utter a word, or they would take his

[041-281]life. He, when he saw his daughter there, began to sigh piteously, and

[041-282]still more when he perceived that I held her closely embraced and that

[041-283]she lay quiet without resisting or complaining, or showing any

[041-284]reluctance; nevertheless he remained silent lest they should carry into

[041-285]effect the repeated threats the renegade had addressed to him.

[041-286]Finding herself now on board, and that we were about to give way with

[041-287]the oars, Zoraida, seeing her father there, and the other Moors bound,

[041-288]bade the renegade ask me to do her the favour of releasing the Moors

[041-289]and setting her father at liberty, for she would rather drown herself

[041-290]in the sea than suffer a father that had loved her so dearly to be

[041-291]carried away captive before her eyes and on her account. The renegade

[041-292]repeated this to me, and I replied that I was very willing to do so;

[041-293]but he replied that it was not advisable, because if they were left

[041-294]there they would at once raise the country and stir up the city, and

[041-295]lead to the despatch of swift cruisers in pursuit, and our being taken,

[041-296]by sea or land, without any possibility of escape; and that all that

[041-297]could be done was to set them free on the first Christian ground we

[041-298]reached. On this point we all agreed; and Zoraida, to whom it was

[041-299]explained, together with the reasons that prevented us from doing at

[041-300]once what she desired, was satisfied likewise; and then in glad silence

[041-301]and with cheerful alacrity each of our stout rowers took his oar, and

[041-302]commending ourselves to God with all our hearts, we began to shape our

[041-303]course for the island of Majorca, the nearest Christian land. Owing,

[041-304]however, to the Tramontana rising a little, and the sea growing

[041-305]somewhat rough, it was impossible for us to keep a straight course for

[041-306]Majorca, and we were compelled to coast in the direction of Oran, not

[041-307]without great uneasiness on our part lest we should be observed from

[041-308]the town of Shershel, which lies on that coast, not more than sixty

[041-309]miles from Algiers. Moreover we were afraid of meeting on that course

[041-310]one of the galliots that usually come with goods from Tetuan; although

[041-311]each of us for himself and all of us together felt confident that, if

[041-312]we were to meet a merchant galliot, so that it were not a cruiser, not

[041-313]only should we not be lost, but that we should take a vessel in which

[041-314]we could more safely accomplish our voyage. As we pursued our course

[041-315]Zoraida kept her head between my hands so as not to see her father, and

[041-316]I felt that she was praying to Lela Marien to help us.

[041-317]We might have made about thirty miles when daybreak found us some three

[041-318]musket-shots off the land, which seemed to us deserted, and without

[041-319]anyone to see us. For all that, however, by hard rowing we put out a

[041-320]little to sea, for it was now somewhat calmer, and having gained about

[041-321]two leagues the word was given to row by batches, while we ate

[041-322]something, for the vessel was well provided; but the rowers said it was

[041-323]not a time to take any rest; let food be served out to those who were

[041-324]not rowing, but they would not leave their oars on any account. This

[041-325]was done, but now a stiff breeze began to blow, which obliged us to

[041-326]leave off rowing and make sail at once and steer for Oran, as it was

[041-327]impossible to make any other course. All this was done very promptly,

[041-328]and under sail we ran more than eight miles an hour without any fear,

[041-329]except that of coming across some vessel out on a roving expedition. We

[041-330]gave the Moorish rowers some food, and the renegade comforted them by

[041-331]telling them that they were not held as captives, as we should set them

[041-332]free on the first opportunity.

[041-333]The same was said to Zoraida’s father, who replied, “Anything else,

[041-334]Christian, I might hope for or think likely from your generosity and

[041-335]good behaviour, but do not think me so simple as to imagine you will

[041-336]give me my liberty; for you would have never exposed yourselves to the

[041-337]danger of depriving me of it only to restore it to me so generously,

[041-338]especially as you know who I am and the sum you may expect to receive

[041-339]on restoring it; and if you will only name that, I here offer you all

[041-340]you require for myself and for my unhappy daughter there; or else for

[041-341]her alone, for she is the greatest and most precious part of my soul.”

[041-342]As he said this he began to weep so bitterly that he filled us all with

[041-343]compassion and forced Zoraida to look at him, and when she saw him

[041-344]weeping she was so moved that she rose from my feet and ran to throw

[041-345]her arms round him, and pressing her face to his, they both gave way to

[041-346]such an outburst of tears that several of us were constrained to keep

[041-347]them company.

[041-348]But when her father saw her in full dress and with all her jewels about

[041-349]her, he said to her in his own language, “What means this, my daughter?

[041-350]Last night, before this terrible misfortune in which we are plunged

[041-351]befell us, I saw thee in thy everyday and indoor garments; and now,

[041-352]without having had time to attire thyself, and without my bringing thee

[041-353]any joyful tidings to furnish an occasion for adorning and bedecking

[041-354]thyself, I see thee arrayed in the finest attire it would be in my
