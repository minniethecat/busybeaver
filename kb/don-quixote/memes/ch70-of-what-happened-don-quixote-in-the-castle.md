OF WHAT HAPPENED DON QUIXOTE IN THE CASTLE OR HOUSE OF THE KNIGHT OF

[070-001]THE GREEN GABAN, TOGETHER WITH OTHER MATTERS OUT OF THE COMMON

p18a.jpg (133K)


[070-002]Don Quixote found Don Diego de Miranda’s house built in village style,

[070-003]with his arms in rough stone over the street door; in the patio was the

[070-004]store-room, and at the entrance the cellar, with plenty of wine-jars

[070-005]standing round, which, coming from El Toboso, brought back to his

[070-006]memory his enchanted and transformed Dulcinea; and with a sigh, and not

[070-007]thinking of what he was saying, or in whose presence he was, he

[070-008]exclaimed-

[070-009]“O ye sweet treasures, to my sorrow found!

[070-010]Once sweet and welcome when ’twas heaven’s good-will.

[070-011]“O ye Tobosan jars, how ye bring back to my memory the

[070-012]sweet object of my bitter regrets!”

p18b.jpg (300K)


[070-013]The student poet, Don Diego’s son, who had come out with his mother to

[070-014]receive him, heard this exclamation, and both mother and son were

[070-015]filled with amazement at the extraordinary figure he presented; he,

[070-016]however, dismounting from Rocinante, advanced with great politeness to

[070-017]ask permission to kiss the lady’s hand, while Don Diego said, “Señora,

[070-018]pray receive with your wonted kindness Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha,

[070-019]whom you see before you, a knight-errant, and the bravest and wisest in

[070-020]the world.”

[070-021]The lady, whose name was Doña Christina, received him with every sign

[070-022]of good-will and great courtesy, and Don Quixote placed himself at her

[070-023]service with an abundance of well-chosen and polished phrases. Almost

[070-024]the same civilities were exchanged between him and the student, who

[070-025]listening to Don Quixote, took him to be a sensible, clear-headed

[070-026]person.

[070-027]Here the author describes minutely everything belonging to Don Diego’s

[070-028]mansion, putting before us in his picture the whole contents of a rich

[070-029]gentleman-farmer’s house; but the translator of the history thought it

[070-030]best to pass over these and other details of the same sort in silence,

[070-031]as they are not in harmony with the main purpose of the story, the

[070-032]strong point of which is truth rather than dull digressions.

[070-033]They led Don Quixote into a room, and Sancho removed his armour,

[070-034]leaving him in loose Walloon breeches and chamois-leather doublet, all

[070-035]stained with the rust of his armour; his collar was a falling one of

[070-036]scholastic cut, without starch or lace, his buskins buff-coloured, and

[070-037]his shoes polished. He wore his good sword, which hung in a baldric of

[070-038]sea-wolf’s skin, for he had suffered for many years, they say, from an

[070-039]ailment of the kidneys; and over all he threw a long cloak of good grey

[070-040]cloth. But first of all, with five or six buckets of water (for as

[070-041]regard the number of buckets there is some dispute), he washed his head

[070-042]and face, and still the water remained whey-coloured, thanks to

[070-043]Sancho’s greediness and purchase of those unlucky curds that turned his

[070-044]master so white. Thus arrayed, and with an easy, sprightly, and gallant

[070-045]air, Don Quixote passed out into another room, where the student was

[070-046]waiting to entertain him while the table was being laid; for on the

[070-047]arrival of so distinguished a guest, Doña Christina was anxious to show

[070-048]that she knew how and was able to give a becoming reception to those

[070-049]who came to her house.

[070-050]While Don Quixote was taking off his armour, Don Lorenzo (for so Don

[070-051]Diego’s son was called) took the opportunity to say to his father,

[070-052]“What are we to make of this gentleman you have brought home to us,

[070-053]sir? For his name, his appearance, and your describing him as a

[070-054]knight-errant have completely puzzled my mother and me.”

[070-055]“I don’t know what to say, my son,” replied. Don Diego; “all I can tell

[070-056]thee is that I have seen him act the acts of the greatest madman in the

[070-057]world, and heard him make observations so sensible that they efface and

[070-058]undo all he does; do thou talk to him and feel the pulse of his wits,

[070-059]and as thou art shrewd, form the most reasonable conclusion thou canst

[070-060]as to his wisdom or folly; though, to tell the truth, I am more

[070-061]inclined to take him to be mad than sane.”

[070-062]With this Don Lorenzo went away to entertain Don Quixote as has been

[070-063]said, and in the course of the conversation that passed between them

[070-064]Don Quixote said to Don Lorenzo, “Your father, Señor Don Diego de

[070-065]Miranda, has told me of the rare abilities and subtle intellect you

[070-066]possess, and, above all, that you are a great poet.”

[070-067]“A poet, it may be,” replied Don Lorenzo, “but a great one, by no

[070-068]means. It is true that I am somewhat given to poetry and to reading

[070-069]good poets, but not so much so as to justify the title of ‘great’ which

[070-070]my father gives me.”

[070-071]“I do not dislike that modesty,” said Don Quixote; “for there is no

[070-072]poet who is not conceited and does not think he is the best poet in the

[070-073]world.”

[070-074]“There is no rule without an exception,” said Don Lorenzo; “there may

[070-075]be some who are poets and yet do not think they are.”

[070-076]“Very few,” said Don Quixote; “but tell me, what verses are those which

[070-077]you have now in hand, and which your father tells me keep you somewhat

[070-078]restless and absorbed? If it be some gloss, I know something about

[070-079]glosses, and I should like to hear them; and if they are for a poetical

[070-080]tournament, contrive to carry off the second prize; for the first

[070-081]always goes by favour or personal standing, the second by simple

[070-082]justice; and so the third comes to be the second, and the first,

[070-083]reckoning in this way, will be third, in the same way as licentiate

[070-084]degrees are conferred at the universities; but, for all that, the title

[070-085]of first is a great distinction.”

[070-086]“So far,” said Don Lorenzo to himself, “I should not take you to be a

[070-087]madman; but let us go on.” So he said to him, “Your worship has

[070-088]apparently attended the schools; what sciences have you studied?”

[070-089]“That of knight-errantry,” said Don Quixote, “which is as good as that

[070-090]of poetry, and even a finger or two above it.”

[070-091]“I do not know what science that is,” said Don Lorenzo, “and until now

[070-092]I have never heard of it.”

[070-093]“It is a science,” said Don Quixote, “that comprehends in itself all or

[070-094]most of the sciences in the world, for he who professes it must be a

[070-095]jurist, and must know the rules of justice, distributive and equitable,

[070-096]so as to give to each one what belongs to him and is due to him. He

[070-097]must be a theologian, so as to be able to give a clear and distinctive

[070-098]reason for the Christian faith he professes, wherever it may be asked

[070-099]of him. He must be a physician, and above all a herbalist, so as in

[070-100]wastes and solitudes to know the herbs that have the property of

[070-101]healing wounds, for a knight-errant must not go looking for someone to

[070-102]cure him at every step. He must be an astronomer, so as to know by the

[070-103]stars how many hours of the night have passed, and what clime and

[070-104]quarter of the world he is in. He must know mathematics, for at every

[070-105]turn some occasion for them will present itself to him; and, putting it

[070-106]aside that he must be adorned with all the virtues, cardinal and

[070-107]theological, to come down to minor particulars, he must, I say, be able

[070-108]to swim as well as Nicholas or Nicolao the Fish could, as the story

[070-109]goes; he must know how to shoe a horse, and repair his saddle and

[070-110]bridle; and, to return to higher matters, he must be faithful to God

[070-111]and to his lady; he must be pure in thought, decorous in words,

[070-112]generous in works, valiant in deeds, patient in suffering,

[070-113]compassionate towards the needy, and, lastly, an upholder of the truth

[070-114]though its defence should cost him his life. Of all these qualities,

[070-115]great and small, is a true knight-errant made up; judge then, Señor Don

[070-116]Lorenzo, whether it be a contemptible science which the knight who

[070-117]studies and professes it has to learn, and whether it may not compare

[070-118]with the very loftiest that are taught in the schools.”

[070-119]“If that be so,” replied Don Lorenzo, “this science, I protest,

[070-120]surpasses all.”

[070-121]“How, if that be so?” said Don Quixote.

[070-122]“What I mean to say,” said Don Lorenzo, “is, that I doubt whether there

[070-123]are now, or ever were, any knights-errant, and adorned with such

[070-124]virtues.”

[070-125]“Many a time,” replied Don Quixote, “have I said what I now say once

[070-126]more, that the majority of the world are of opinion that there never

[070-127]were any knights-errant in it; and as it is my opinion that, unless

[070-128]heaven by some miracle brings home to them the truth that there were

[070-129]and are, all the pains one takes will be in vain (as experience has

[070-130]often proved to me), I will not now stop to disabuse you of the error

[070-131]you share with the multitude. All I shall do is to pray to heaven to

[070-132]deliver you from it, and show you how beneficial and necessary

[070-133]knights-errant were in days of yore, and how useful they would be in

[070-134]these days were they but in vogue; but now, for the sins of the people,

[070-135]sloth and indolence, gluttony and luxury are triumphant.”

[070-136]“Our guest has broken out on our hands,” said Don Lorenzo to himself at

[070-137]this point; “but, for all that, he is a glorious madman, and I should

[070-138]be a dull blockhead to doubt it.”

[070-139]Here, being summoned to dinner, they brought their colloquy to a close.

[070-140]Don Diego asked his son what he had been able to make out as to the

[070-141]wits of their guest. To which he replied, “All the doctors and clever

[070-142]scribes in the world will not make sense of the scrawl of his madness;

[070-143]he is a madman full of streaks, full of lucid intervals.”

[070-144]They went in to dinner, and the repast was such as Don Diego said on

[070-145]the road he was in the habit of giving to his guests, neat, plentiful,

[070-146]and tasty; but what pleased Don Quixote most was the marvellous silence

[070-147]that reigned throughout the house, for it was like a Carthusian

[070-148]monastery.

[070-149]When the cloth had been removed, grace said and their hands washed, Don

[070-150]Quixote earnestly pressed Don Lorenzo to repeat to him his verses for

[070-151]the poetical tournament, to which he replied, “Not to be like those

[070-152]poets who, when they are asked to recite their verses, refuse, and when

[070-153]they are not asked for them vomit them up, I will repeat my gloss, for

[070-154]which I do not expect any prize, having composed it merely as an

[070-155]exercise of ingenuity.”

[070-156]“A discerning friend of mine,” said Don Quixote, “was of opinion that

[070-157]no one ought to waste labour in glossing verses; and the reason he gave

[070-158]was that the gloss can never come up to the text, and that often or

[070-159]most frequently it wanders away from the meaning and purpose aimed at

[070-160]in the glossed lines; and besides, that the laws of the gloss were too

[070-161]strict, as they did not allow interrogations, nor ‘said he,’ nor ‘I

[070-162]say,’ nor turning verbs into nouns, or altering the construction, not

[070-163]to speak of other restrictions and limitations that fetter

[070-164]gloss-writers, as you no doubt know.”

[070-165]“Verily, Señor Don Quixote,” said Don Lorenzo, “I wish I could catch

[070-166]your worship tripping at a stretch, but I cannot, for you slip through

[070-167]my fingers like an eel.”

[070-168]“I don’t understand what you say, or mean by slipping,” said Don

[070-169]Quixote.

[070-170]“I will explain myself another time,” said Don Lorenzo; “for the

[070-171]present pray attend to the glossed verses and the gloss, which run

[070-172]thus:

[070-173]Could ‘was’ become an ‘is’ for me,

[070-174]Then would I ask no more than this;

[070-175]Or could, for me, the time that is

[070-176]Become the time that is to be!—

GLOSS

[070-177]Dame Fortune once upon a day

[070-178]To me was bountiful and kind;

[070-179]But all things change; she changed her mind,

[070-180]And what she gave she took away.

[070-181]O Fortune, long I’ve sued to thee;

[070-182]The gifts thou gavest me restore,

[070-183]For, trust me, I would ask no more,

[070-184]Could ‘was’ become an ‘is’ for me.

[070-185]No other prize I seek to gain,

[070-186]No triumph, glory, or success,

[070-187]Only the long-lost happiness,

[070-188]The memory whereof is pain.

[070-189]One taste, methinks, of bygone bliss

[070-190]The heart-consuming fire might stay;

[070-191]And, so it come without delay,

[070-192]Then would I ask no more than this.

[070-193]I ask what cannot be, alas!

[070-194]That time should ever be, and then

[070-195]Come back to us, and be again,

[070-196]No power on earth can bring to pass;

[070-197]For fleet of foot is he, I wis,

[070-198]And idly, therefore, do we pray

[070-199]That what for aye hath left us may

[070-200]Become for us the time that is.

[070-201]Perplexed, uncertain, to remain

[070-202]’Twixt hope and fear, is death, not life;

[070-203]’Twere better, sure, to end the strife,

[070-204]And dying, seek release from pain.

[070-205]And yet, thought were the best for me.

[070-206]Anon the thought aside I fling,

[070-207]And to the present fondly cling,

[070-208]And dread the time that is to be.”

[070-209]When Don Lorenzo had finished reciting his gloss, Don Quixote stood up,

[070-210]and in a loud voice, almost a shout, exclaimed as he grasped Don

[070-211]Lorenzo’s right hand in his, “By the highest heavens, noble youth, but

[070-212]you are the best poet on earth, and deserve to be crowned with laurel,

[070-213]not by Cyprus or by Gaeta—as a certain poet, God forgive him, said—but

[070-214]by the Academies of Athens, if they still flourished, and by those that

[070-215]flourish now, Paris, Bologna, Salamanca. Heaven grant that the judges

[070-216]who rob you of the first prize—that Phœbus may pierce them with his

[070-217]arrows, and the Muses never cross the thresholds of their doors. Repeat

[070-218]me some of your long-measure verses, señor, if you will be so good, for

[070-219]I want thoroughly to feel the pulse of your rare genius.”

[070-220]Is there any need to say that Don Lorenzo enjoyed hearing himself

[070-221]praised by Don Quixote, albeit he looked upon him as a madman? power of

[070-222]flattery, how far-reaching art thou, and how wide are the bounds of thy

[070-223]pleasant jurisdiction! Don Lorenzo gave a proof of it, for he complied

[070-224]with Don Quixote’s request and entreaty, and repeated to him this

[070-225]sonnet on the fable or story of Pyramus and Thisbe.

SONNET

[070-226]The lovely maid, she pierces now the wall;

[070-227]Heart-pierced by her young Pyramus doth lie;

[070-228]And Love spreads wing from Cyprus isle to fly,

[070-229]A chink to view so wondrous great and small.

[070-230]There silence speaketh, for no voice at all

[070-231]Can pass so strait a strait; but love will ply

[070-232]Where to all other power ’twere vain to try;

[070-233]For love will find a way whate’er befall.

[070-234]Impatient of delay, with reckless pace

[070-235]The rash maid wins the fatal spot where she

[070-236]Sinks not in lover’s arms but death’s embrace.

[070-237]So runs the strange tale, how the lovers twain

[070-238]One sword, one sepulchre, one memory,

[070-239]Slays, and entombs, and brings to life again.

[070-240]“Blessed be God,” said Don Quixote when he had heard Don Lorenzo’s

[070-241]sonnet, “that among the hosts there are of irritable poets I have found

[070-242]one consummate one, which, señor, the art of this sonnet proves to me

[070-243]that you are!”

[070-244]For four days was Don Quixote most sumptuously entertained in Don

[070-245]Diego’s house, at the end of which time he asked his permission to

[070-246]depart, telling him he thanked him for the kindness and hospitality he

[070-247]had received in his house, but that, as it did not become

[070-248]knights-errant to give themselves up for long to idleness and luxury,

[070-249]he was anxious to fulfill the duties of his calling in seeking

[070-250]adventures, of which he was informed there was an abundance in that

[070-251]neighbourhood, where he hoped to employ his time until the day came

[070-252]round for the jousts at Saragossa, for that was his proper destination;

[070-253]and that, first of all, he meant to enter the cave of Montesinos, of

[070-254]which so many marvellous things were reported all through the country,

[070-255]and at the same time to investigate and explore the origin and true

[070-256]source of the seven lakes commonly called the lakes of Ruidera.

[070-257]Don Diego and his son commended his laudable resolution, and bade him

[070-258]furnish himself with all he wanted from their house and belongings, as

[070-259]they would most gladly be of service to him; which, indeed, his

[070-260]personal worth and his honourable profession made incumbent upon them.

[070-261]The day of his departure came at length, as welcome to Don Quixote as

[070-262]it was sad and sorrowful to Sancho Panza, who was very well satisfied

[070-263]with the abundance of Don Diego’s house, and objected to return to the

[070-264]starvation of the woods and wilds and the short-commons of his

[070-265]ill-stocked alforjas; these, however, he filled and packed with what he

[070-266]considered needful. On taking leave, Don Quixote said to Don Lorenzo,

[070-267]“I know not whether I have told you already, but if I have I tell you

[070-268]once more, that if you wish to spare yourself fatigue and toil in

[070-269]reaching the inaccessible summit of the temple of fame, you have

[070-270]nothing to do but to turn aside out of the somewhat narrow path of

[070-271]poetry and take the still narrower one of knight-errantry, wide enough,

[070-272]however, to make you an emperor in the twinkling of an eye.”

[070-273]In this speech Don Quixote wound up the evidence of his madness, but

[070-274]still better in what he added when he said, “God knows, I would gladly

[070-275]take Don Lorenzo with me to teach him how to spare the humble, and

[070-276]trample the proud under foot, virtues that are part and parcel of the

[070-277]profession I belong to; but since his tender age does not allow of it,

[070-278]nor his praiseworthy pursuits permit it, I will simply content myself

[070-279]with impressing it upon your worship that you will become famous as a

[070-280]poet if you are guided by the opinion of others rather than by your

[070-281]own; because no fathers or mothers ever think their own children

[070-282]ill-favoured, and this sort of deception prevails still more strongly

[070-283]in the case of the children of the brain.”

[070-284]Both father and son were amazed afresh at the strange medley Don

[070-285]Quixote talked, at one moment sense, at another nonsense, and at the

[070-286]pertinacity and persistence he displayed in going through thick and

[070-287]thin in quest of his unlucky adventures, which he made the end and aim

[070-288]of his desires. There was a renewal of offers of service and

[070-289]civilities, and then, with the gracious permission of the lady of the

[070-290]castle, they took their departure, Don Quixote on Rocinante, and Sancho

[070-291]on Dapple.
