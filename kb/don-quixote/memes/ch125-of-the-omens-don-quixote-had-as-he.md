[125-001]OF THE OMENS DON QUIXOTE HAD AS HE ENTERED HIS OWN VILLAGE, AND OTHER

INCIDENTS THAT EMBELLISH AND GIVE A COLOUR TO THIS GREAT HISTORY

p73a.jpg (141K)


[125-002]At the entrance of the village, so says Cide Hamete, Don Quixote saw

[125-003]two boys quarrelling on the village threshing-floor, one of whom said

[125-004]to the other, “Take it easy, Periquillo; thou shalt never see it again

[125-005]as long as thou livest.”

[125-006]Don Quixote heard this, and said he to Sancho, “Dost thou not mark,

[125-007]friend, what that boy said, ‘Thou shalt never see it again as long as

[125-008]thou livest’?”

[125-009]“Well,” said Sancho, “what does it matter if the boy said so?”

[125-010]“What!” said Don Quixote, “dost thou not see that, applied to the

[125-011]object of my desires, the words mean that I am never to see Dulcinea

[125-012]more?”

[125-013]Sancho was about to answer, when his attention was diverted by seeing a

[125-014]hare come flying across the plain pursued by several greyhounds and

[125-015]sportsmen. In its terror it ran to take shelter and hide itself under

[125-016]Dapple. Sancho caught it alive and presented it to Don Quixote, who was

[125-017]saying, “_Malum signum, malum signum!_ a hare flies, greyhounds chase

[125-018]it, Dulcinea appears not.”

[125-019]“Your worship’s a strange man,” said Sancho; “let’s take it for granted

[125-020]that this hare is Dulcinea, and these greyhounds chasing it the

[125-021]malignant enchanters who turned her into a country wench; she flies,

[125-022]and I catch her and put her into your worship’s hands, and you hold her

[125-023]in your arms and cherish her; what bad sign is that, or what ill omen

[125-024]is there to be found here?”

[125-025]The two boys who had been quarrelling came over to look at the hare,

[125-026]and Sancho asked one of them what their quarrel was about. He was

[125-027]answered by the one who had said, “Thou shalt never see it again as

[125-028]long as thou livest,” that he had taken a cage full of crickets from

[125-029]the other boy, and did not mean to give it back to him as long as he

[125-030]lived. Sancho took out four cuartos from his pocket and gave them to

[125-031]the boy for the cage, which he placed in Don Quixote’s hands, saying,

[125-032]“There, señor! there are the omens broken and destroyed, and they have

[125-033]no more to do with our affairs, to my thinking, fool as I am, than with

[125-034]last year’s clouds; and if I remember rightly I have heard the curate

[125-035]of our village say that it does not become Christians or sensible

[125-036]people to give any heed to these silly things; and even you yourself

[125-037]said the same to me some time ago, telling me that all Christians who

[125-038]minded omens were fools; but there’s no need of making words about it;

[125-039]let us push on and go into our village.”

[125-040]The sportsmen came up and asked for their hare, which Don Quixote gave

[125-041]them. They then went on, and upon the green at the entrance of the town

[125-042]they came upon the curate and the bachelor Samson Carrasco busy with

[125-043]their breviaries. It should be mentioned that Sancho had thrown, by way

[125-044]of a sumpter-cloth, over Dapple and over the bundle of armour, the

[125-045]buckram robe painted with flames which they had put upon him at the

[125-046]duke’s castle the night Altisidora came back to life. He had also fixed

[125-047]the mitre on Dapple’s head, the oddest transformation and decoration

[125-048]that ever ass in the world underwent. They were at once recognised by

[125-049]both the curate and the bachelor, who came towards them with open arms.

[125-050]Don Quixote dismounted and received them with a close embrace; and the

[125-051]boys, who are lynxes that nothing escapes, spied out the ass’s mitre

[125-052]and came running to see it, calling out to one another, “Come here,

[125-053]boys, and see Sancho Panza’s ass figged out finer than Mingo, and Don

[125-054]Quixote’s beast leaner than ever.”

[125-055]So at length, with the boys capering round them, and accompanied by the

[125-056]curate and the bachelor, they made their entrance into the town, and

[125-057]proceeded to Don Quixote’s house, at the door of which they found his

[125-058]housekeeper and niece, whom the news of his arrival had already

[125-059]reached. It had been brought to Teresa Panza, Sancho’s wife, as well,

[125-060]and she with her hair all loose and half naked, dragging Sanchica her

[125-061]daughter by the hand, ran out to meet her husband; but seeing him

[125-062]coming in by no means as good case as she thought a governor ought to

[125-063]be, she said to him, “How is it you come this way, husband? It seems to

[125-064]me you come tramping and footsore, and looking more like a disorderly

[125-065]vagabond than a governor.”

[125-066]“Hold your tongue, Teresa,” said Sancho; “often ‘where there are pegs

[125-067]there are no flitches;’ let’s go into the house and there you’ll hear

[125-068]strange things. I bring money, and that’s the main thing, got by my own

[125-069]industry without wronging anybody.”

[125-070]“You bring the money, my good husband,” said Teresa, “and no matter

[125-071]whether it was got this way or that; for, however you may have got it,

[125-072]you’ll not have brought any new practice into the world.”

[125-073]Sanchica embraced her father and asked him if he brought her anything,

[125-074]for she had been looking out for him as for the showers of May; and she

[125-075]taking hold of him by the girdle on one side, and his wife by the hand,

[125-076]while the daughter led Dapple, they made for their house, leaving Don

[125-077]Quixote in his, in the hands of his niece and housekeeper, and in the

[125-078]company of the curate and the bachelor.

[125-079]Don Quixote at once, without any regard to time or season, withdrew in

[125-080]private with the bachelor and the curate, and in a few words told them

[125-081]of his defeat, and of the engagement he was under not to quit his

[125-082]village for a year, which he meant to keep to the letter without

[125-083]departing a hair’s breadth from it, as became a knight-errant bound by

[125-084]scrupulous good faith and the laws of knight-errantry; and of how he

[125-085]thought of turning shepherd for that year, and taking his diversion in

[125-086]the solitude of the fields, where he could with perfect freedom give

[125-087]range to his thoughts of love while he followed the virtuous pastoral

[125-088]calling; and he besought them, if they had not a great deal to do and

[125-089]were not prevented by more important business, to consent to be his

[125-090]companions, for he would buy sheep enough to qualify them for

[125-091]shepherds; and the most important point of the whole affair, he could

[125-092]tell them, was settled, for he had given them names that would fit them

[125-093]to a T. The curate asked what they were. Don Quixote replied that he

[125-094]himself was to be called the shepherd Quixotize and the bachelor the

[125-095]shepherd Carrascon, and the curate the shepherd Curambro, and Sancho

[125-096]Panza the shepherd Pancino.

[125-097]Both were astounded at Don Quixote’s new craze; however, lest he should

[125-098]once more make off out of the village from them in pursuit of his

[125-099]chivalry, they trusting that in the course of the year he might be

[125-100]cured, fell in with his new project, applauded his crazy idea as a

[125-101]bright one, and offered to share the life with him. “And what’s more,”

[125-102]said Samson Carrasco, “I am, as all the world knows, a very famous

[125-103]poet, and I’ll be always making verses, pastoral, or courtly, or as it

[125-104]may come into my head, to pass away our time in those secluded regions

[125-105]where we shall be roaming. But what is most needful, sirs, is that each

[125-106]of us should choose the name of the shepherdess he means to glorify in

[125-107]his verses, and that we should not leave a tree, be it ever so hard,

[125-108]without writing up and carving her name on it, as is the habit and

[125-109]custom of love-smitten shepherds.”

[125-110]“That’s the very thing,” said Don Quixote; “though I am relieved from

[125-111]looking for the name of an imaginary shepherdess, for there’s the

[125-112]peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, the glory of these brooksides, the

[125-113]ornament of these meadows, the mainstay of beauty, the cream of all the

[125-114]graces, and, in a word, the being to whom all praise is appropriate, be

[125-115]it ever so hyperbolical.”

[125-116]“Very true,” said the curate; “but we the others must look about for

[125-117]accommodating shepherdesses that will answer our purpose one way or

[125-118]another.”

[125-119]“And,” added Samson Carrasco, “if they fail us, we can call them by the

[125-120]names of the ones in print that the world is filled with, Fílidas,

[125-121]Amarilises, Dianas, Fleridas, Galateas, Belisardas; for as they sell

[125-122]them in the market-places we may fairly buy them and make them our own.

[125-123]If my lady, or I should say my shepherdess, happens to be called Ana,

[125-124]I’ll sing her praises under the name of Anarda, and if Francisca, I’ll

[125-125]call her Francenia, and if Lucia, Lucinda, for it all comes to the same

[125-126]thing; and Sancho Panza, if he joins this fraternity, may glorify his

[125-127]wife Teresa Panza as Teresaina.”

[125-128]Don Quixote laughed at the adaptation of the name, and the curate

[125-129]bestowed vast praise upon the worthy and honourable resolution he had

[125-130]made, and again offered to bear him company all the time that he could

[125-131]spare from his imperative duties. And so they took their leave of him,

[125-132]recommending and beseeching him to take care of his health and treat

[125-133]himself to a suitable diet.

[125-134]It so happened his niece and the housekeeper overheard all the three of

[125-135]them said; and as soon as they were gone they both of them came in to

[125-136]Don Quixote, and said the niece, “What’s this, uncle? Now that we were

[125-137]thinking you had come back to stay at home and lead a quiet respectable

[125-138]life there, are you going to get into fresh entanglements, and turn

[125-139]‘young shepherd, thou that comest here, young shepherd going there?’

[125-140]Nay! indeed ‘the straw is too hard now to make pipes of.’”

[125-141]“And,” added the housekeeper, “will your worship be able to bear, out

[125-142]in the fields, the heats of summer, and the chills of winter, and the

[125-143]howling of the wolves? Not you; for that’s a life and a business for

[125-144]hardy men, bred and seasoned to such work almost from the time they

[125-145]were in swaddling-clothes. Why, to make choice of evils, it’s better to

[125-146]be a knight-errant than a shepherd! Look here, señor; take my

[125-147]advice—and I’m not giving it to you full of bread and wine, but

[125-148]fasting, and with fifty years upon my head—stay at home, look after

[125-149]your affairs, go often to confession, be good to the poor, and upon my

[125-150]soul be it if any evil comes to you.”

[125-151]“Hold your peace, my daughters,” said Don Quixote; “I know very well

[125-152]what my duty is; help me to bed, for I don’t feel very well; and rest

[125-153]assured that, knight-errant now or wandering shepherd to be, I shall

[125-154]never fail to have a care for your interests, as you will see in the

[125-155]end.” And the good wenches (for that they undoubtedly were), the

[125-156]housekeeper and niece, helped him to bed, where they gave him something

[125-157]to eat and made him as comfortable as possible.
