IN WHICH SANCHO PANZA GIVES A SATISFACTORY REPLY TO THE DOUBTS AND

[056-001]QUESTIONS OF THE BACHELOR SAMSON CARRASCO, TOGETHER WITH OTHER MATTERS

WORTH KNOWING AND TELLING

p04a.jpg (143K)


[056-002]Sancho came back to Don Quixote’s house, and returning to the late

[056-003]subject of conversation, he said, “As to what Señor Samson said, that

[056-004]he would like to know by whom, or how, or when my ass was stolen, I say

[056-005]in reply that the same night we went into the Sierra Morena, flying

[056-006]from the Holy Brotherhood after that unlucky adventure of the galley

[056-007]slaves, and the other of the corpse that was going to Segovia, my

[056-008]master and I ensconced ourselves in a thicket, and there, my master

[056-009]leaning on his lance, and I seated on my Dapple, battered and weary

[056-010]with the late frays we fell asleep as if it had been on four feather

[056-011]mattresses; and I in particular slept so sound, that, whoever he was,

[056-012]he was able to come and prop me up on four stakes, which he put under

[056-013]the four corners of the pack-saddle in such a way that he left me

[056-014]mounted on it, and took away Dapple from under me without my feeling

[056-015]it.”

p04b.jpg (270K)


[056-016]“That is an easy matter,” said Don Quixote, “and it is no new

[056-017]occurrence, for the same thing happened to Sacripante at the siege of

[056-018]Albracca; the famous thief, Brunello, by the same contrivance, took his

[056-019]horse from between his legs.”

[056-020]“Day came,” continued Sancho, “and the moment I stirred the stakes gave

[056-021]way and I fell to the ground with a mighty come down; I looked about

[056-022]for the ass, but could not see him; the tears rushed to my eyes and I

[056-023]raised such a lamentation that, if the author of our history has not

[056-024]put it in, he may depend upon it he has left out a good thing. Some

[056-025]days after, I know not how many, travelling with her ladyship the

[056-026]Princess Micomicona, I saw my ass, and mounted upon him, in the dress

[056-027]of a gipsy, was that Gines de Pasamonte, the great rogue and rascal

[056-028]that my master and I freed from the chain.”

[056-029]“That is not where the mistake is,” replied Samson; “it is, that before

[056-030]the ass has turned up, the author speaks of Sancho as being mounted on

[056-031]it.”

[056-032]“I don’t know what to say to that,” said Sancho, “unless that the

[056-033]historian made a mistake, or perhaps it might be a blunder of the

[056-034]printer’s.”

[056-035]“No doubt that’s it,” said Samson; “but what became of the hundred

[056-036]crowns? Did they vanish?”

[056-037]To which Sancho answered, “I spent them for my own good, and my wife’s,

[056-038]and my children’s, and it is they that have made my wife bear so

[056-039]patiently all my wanderings on highways and byways, in the service of

[056-040]my master, Don Quixote; for if after all this time I had come back to

[056-041]the house without a rap and without the ass, it would have been a poor

[056-042]look-out for me; and if anyone wants to know anything more about me,

[056-043]here I am, ready to answer the king himself in person; and it is no

[056-044]affair of anyone’s whether I took or did not take, whether I spent or

[056-045]did not spend; for the whacks that were given me in these journeys were

[056-046]to be paid for in money, even if they were valued at no more than four

[056-047]maravedis apiece, another hundred crowns would not pay me for half of

[056-048]them. Let each look to himself and not try to make out white black, and

[056-049]black white; for each of us is as God made him, aye, and often worse.”

[056-050]“I will take care,” said Carrasco, “to impress upon the author of the

[056-051]history that, if he prints it again, he must not forget what worthy

[056-052]Sancho has said, for it will raise it a good span higher.”

[056-053]“Is there anything else to correct in the history, señor bachelor?”

[056-054]asked Don Quixote.

[056-055]“No doubt there is,” replied he; “but not anything that will be of the

[056-056]same importance as those I have mentioned.”

[056-057]“Does the author promise a second part at all?” said Don Quixote.

[056-058]“He does promise one,” replied Samson; “but he says he has not found

[056-059]it, nor does he know who has got it; and we cannot say whether it will

[056-060]appear or not; and so, on that head, as some say that no second part

[056-061]has ever been good, and others that enough has been already written

[056-062]about Don Quixote, it is thought there will be no second part; though

[056-063]some, who are jovial rather than saturnine, say, ‘Let us have more

[056-064]Quixotades, let Don Quixote charge and Sancho chatter, and no matter

[056-065]what it may turn out, we shall be satisfied with that.’”

[056-066]“And what does the author mean to do?” said Don Quixote.

[056-067]“What?” replied Samson; “why, as soon as he has found the history which

[056-068]he is now searching for with extraordinary diligence, he will at once

[056-069]give it to the press, moved more by the profit that may accrue to him

[056-070]from doing so than by any thought of praise.”

[056-071]Whereat Sancho observed, “The author looks for money and profit, does

[056-072]he? It will be a wonder if he succeeds, for it will be only hurry,

[056-073]hurry, with him, like the tailor on Easter Eve; and works done in a

[056-074]hurry are never finished as perfectly as they ought to be. Let master

[056-075]Moor, or whatever he is, pay attention to what he is doing, and I and

[056-076]my master will give him as much grouting ready to his hand, in the way

[056-077]of adventures and accidents of all sorts, as would make up not only one

[056-078]second part, but a hundred. The good man fancies, no doubt, that we are

[056-079]fast asleep in the straw here, but let him hold up our feet to be shod

[056-080]and he will see which foot it is we go lame on. All I say is, that if

[056-081]my master would take my advice, we would be now afield, redressing

[056-082]outrages and righting wrongs, as is the use and custom of good

[056-083]knights-errant.”

[056-084]Sancho had hardly uttered these words when the neighing of Rocinante

[056-085]fell upon their ears, which neighing Don Quixote accepted as a happy

[056-086]omen, and he resolved to make another sally in three or four days from

[056-087]that time. Announcing his intention to the bachelor, he asked his

[056-088]advice as to the quarter in which he ought to commence his expedition,

[056-089]and the bachelor replied that in his opinion he ought to go to the

[056-090]kingdom of Aragon, and the city of Saragossa, where there were to be

[056-091]certain solemn joustings at the festival of St. George, at which he

[056-092]might win renown above all the knights of Aragon, which would be

[056-093]winning it above all the knights of the world. He commended his very

[056-094]praiseworthy and gallant resolution, but admonished him to proceed with

[056-095]greater caution in encountering dangers, because his life did not

[056-096]belong to him, but to all those who had need of him to protect and aid

[056-097]them in their misfortunes.

[056-098]“There’s where it is, what I abominate, Señor Samson,” said Sancho

[056-099]here; “my master will attack a hundred armed men as a greedy boy would

[056-100]half a dozen melons. Body of the world, señor bachelor! there is a time

[056-101]to attack and a time to retreat, and it is not to be always ‘Santiago,

[056-102]and close Spain!’ Moreover, I have heard it said (and I think by my

[056-103]master himself, if I remember rightly) that the mean of valour lies

[056-104]between the extremes of cowardice and rashness; and if that be so, I

[056-105]don’t want him to fly without having good reason, or to attack when the

[056-106]odds make it better not. But, above all things, I warn my master that

[056-107]if he is to take me with him it must be on the condition that he is to

[056-108]do all the fighting, and that I am not to be called upon to do anything

[056-109]except what concerns keeping him clean and comfortable; in this I will

[056-110]dance attendance on him readily; but to expect me to draw sword, even

[056-111]against rascally churls of the hatchet and hood, is idle. I don’t set

[056-112]up to be a fighting man, Señor Samson, but only the best and most loyal

[056-113]squire that ever served knight-errant; and if my master Don Quixote, in

[056-114]consideration of my many faithful services, is pleased to give me some

[056-115]island of the many his worship says one may stumble on in these parts,

[056-116]I will take it as a great favour; and if he does not give it to me, I

[056-117]was born like everyone else, and a man must not live in dependence on

[056-118]anyone except God; and what is more, my bread will taste as well, and

[056-119]perhaps even better, without a government than if I were a governor;

[056-120]and how do I know but that in these governments the devil may have

[056-121]prepared some trip for me, to make me lose my footing and fall and

[056-122]knock my grinders out? Sancho I was born and Sancho I mean to die. But

[056-123]for all that, if heaven were to make me a fair offer of an island or

[056-124]something else of the kind, without much trouble and without much risk,

[056-125]I am not such a fool as to refuse it; for they say, too, ‘when they

[056-126]offer thee a heifer, run with a halter; and ‘when good luck comes to

[056-127]thee, take it in.’”

[056-128]“Brother Sancho,” said Carrasco, “you have spoken like a professor;

[056-129]but, for all that, put your trust in God and in Señor Don Quixote, for

[056-130]he will give you a kingdom, not to say an island.”

[056-131]“It is all the same, be it more or be it less,” replied Sancho; “though

[056-132]I can tell Señor Carrasco that my master would not throw the kingdom he

[056-133]might give me into a sack all in holes; for I have felt my own pulse

[056-134]and I find myself sound enough to rule kingdoms and govern islands; and

[056-135]I have before now told my master as much.”

[056-136]“Take care, Sancho,” said Samson; “honours change manners, and perhaps

[056-137]when you find yourself a governor you won’t know the mother that bore

[056-138]you.”

[056-139]“That may hold good of those that are born in the ditches,” said

[056-140]Sancho, “not of those who have the fat of an old Christian four fingers

[056-141]deep on their souls, as I have. Nay, only look at my disposition, is

[056-142]that likely to show ingratitude to anyone?”

[056-143]“God grant it,” said Don Quixote; “we shall see when the government

[056-144]comes; and I seem to see it already.”

[056-145]He then begged the bachelor, if he were a poet, to do him the favour of

[056-146]composing some verses for him conveying the farewell he meant to take

[056-147]of his lady Dulcinea del Toboso, and to see that a letter of her name

[056-148]was placed at the beginning of each line, so that, at the end of the

[056-149]verses, “Dulcinea del Toboso” might be read by putting together the

[056-150]first letters. The bachelor replied that although he was not one of the

[056-151]famous poets of Spain, who were, they said, only three and a half, he

[056-152]would not fail to compose the required verses; though he saw a great

[056-153]difficulty in the task, as the letters which made up the name were

[056-154]seventeen; so, if he made four ballad stanzas of four lines each, there

[056-155]would be a letter over, and if he made them of five, what they called

[056-156]decimas or redondillas, there were three letters short; nevertheless he

[056-157]would try to drop a letter as well as he could, so that the name

[056-158]“Dulcinea del Toboso” might be got into four ballad stanzas.

[056-159]“It must be, by some means or other,” said Don Quixote, “for unless the

[056-160]name stands there plain and manifest, no woman would believe the verses

[056-161]were made for her.”

[056-162]They agreed upon this, and that the departure should take place in

[056-163]three days from that time. Don Quixote charged the bachelor to keep it

[056-164]a secret, especially from the curate and Master Nicholas, and from his

[056-165]niece and the housekeeper, lest they should prevent the execution of

[056-166]his praiseworthy and valiant purpose. Carrasco promised all, and then

[056-167]took his leave, charging Don Quixote to inform him of his good or evil

[056-168]fortunes whenever he had an opportunity; and thus they bade each other

[056-169]farewell, and Sancho went away to make the necessary preparations for

[056-170]their expedition.
