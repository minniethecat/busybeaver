[109-001]WHICH TREATS OF HOW DON QUIXOTE TOOK LEAVE OF THE DUKE, AND OF WHAT

[109-002]FOLLOWED WITH THE WITTY AND IMPUDENT ALTISIDORA, ONE OF THE DUCHESS’S

DAMSELS

p57a.jpg (119K)


[109-003]Don Quixote now felt it right to quit a life of such idleness as he was

[109-004]leading in the castle; for he fancied that he was making himself sorely

[109-005]missed by suffering himself to remain shut up and inactive amid the

[109-006]countless luxuries and enjoyments his hosts lavished upon him as a

[109-007]knight, and he felt too that he would have to render a strict account

[109-008]to heaven of that indolence and seclusion; and so one day he asked the

[109-009]duke and duchess to grant him permission to take his departure. They

[109-010]gave it, showing at the same time that they were very sorry he was

[109-011]leaving them.

p57b.jpg (370K)


[109-012]The duchess gave his wife’s letters to Sancho Panza, who shed tears

[109-013]over them, saying, “Who would have thought that such grand hopes as the

[109-014]news of my government bred in my wife Teresa Panza’s breast would end

[109-015]in my going back now to the vagabond adventures of my master Don

[109-016]Quixote of La Mancha? Still I’m glad to see my Teresa behaved as she

[109-017]ought in sending the acorns, for if she had not sent them I’d have been

[109-018]sorry, and she’d have shown herself ungrateful. It is a comfort to me

[109-019]that they can’t call that present a bribe; for I had got the government

[109-020]already when she sent them, and it’s but reasonable that those who have

[109-021]had a good turn done them should show their gratitude, if it’s only

[109-022]with a trifle. After all I went into the government naked, and I come

[109-023]out of it naked; so I can say with a safe conscience—and that’s no

[109-024]small matter—‘naked I was born, naked I find myself, I neither lose nor

[109-025]gain.’”

[109-026]Thus did Sancho soliloquise on the day of their departure, as Don

[109-027]Quixote, who had the night before taken leave of the duke and duchess,

[109-028]coming out made his appearance at an early hour in full armour in the

[109-029]courtyard of the castle. The whole household of the castle were

[109-030]watching him from the corridors, and the duke and duchess, too, came

[109-031]out to see him. Sancho was mounted on his Dapple, with his alforjas,

[109-032]valise, and proven supremely happy because the duke’s majordomo, the

[109-033]same that had acted the part of the Trifaldi, had given him a little

[109-034]purse with two hundred gold crowns to meet the necessary expenses of

[109-035]the road, but of this Don Quixote knew nothing as yet. While all were,

[109-036]as has been said, observing him, suddenly from among the duennas and

[109-037]handmaidens the impudent and witty Altisidora lifted up her voice and

[109-038]said in pathetic tones:

[109-039]Give ear, cruel knight;

[109-040]Draw rein; where’s the need

[109-041]Of spurring the flanks

[109-042]Of that ill-broken steed?

[109-043]From what art thou flying?

[109-044]No dragon I am,

[109-045]Not even a sheep,

[109-046]But a tender young lamb.

[109-047]Thou hast jilted a maiden

[109-048]As fair to behold

[109-049]As nymph of Diana

[109-050]Or Venus of old.

[109-051]Bireno, Æneas, what worse shall I call thee?

[109-052]Barabbas go with thee! All evil befall thee!

[109-053]In thy claws, ruthless robber,

[109-054]Thou bearest away

[109-055]The heart of a meek

[109-056]Loving maid for thy prey,

[109-057]Three kerchiefs thou stealest,

[109-058]And garters a pair,

[109-059]From legs than the whitest

[109-060]Of marble more fair;

[109-061]And the sighs that pursue thee

[109-062]Would burn to the ground

[109-063]Two thousand Troy Towns,

[109-064]If so many were found.

[109-065]Bireno, Æneas, what worse shall I call thee?

[109-066]Barabbas go with thee! All evil befall thee!

[109-067]May no bowels of mercy

[109-068]To Sancho be granted,

[109-069]And thy Dulcinea

[109-070]Be left still enchanted,

[109-071]May thy falsehood to me

[109-072]Find its punishment in her,

[109-073]For in my land the just

[109-074]Often pays for the sinner.

[109-075]May thy grandest adventures

[109-076]Discomfitures prove,

[109-077]May thy joys be all dreams,

[109-078]And forgotten thy love.

[109-079]Bireno, Æneas, what worse shall I call thee?

[109-080]Barabbas go with thee! All evil befall thee!

[109-081]May thy name be abhorred

[109-082]For thy conduct to ladies,

[109-083]From London to England,

[109-084]From Seville to Cadiz;

[109-085]May thy cards be unlucky,

[109-086]Thy hands contain ne’er a

[109-087]King, seven, or ace

[109-088]When thou playest primera;

[109-089]When thy corns are cut

[109-090]May it be to the quick;

[109-091]When thy grinders are drawn

[109-092]May the roots of them stick.

[109-093]Bireno, Æneas, what worse shall I call thee?

[109-094]Barabbas go with thee! All evil befall thee!

[109-095]All the while the unhappy Altisidora was bewailing herself in the above

[109-096]strain Don Quixote stood staring at her; and without uttering a word in

[109-097]reply to her he turned round to Sancho and said, “Sancho my friend, I

[109-098]conjure thee by the life of thy forefathers tell me the truth; say,

[109-099]hast thou by any chance taken the three kerchiefs and the garters this

[109-100]love-sick maid speaks of?”

[109-101]To this Sancho made answer, “The three kerchiefs I have; but the

[109-102]garters, as much as ‘over the hills of Úbeda.’”

[109-103]The duchess was amazed at Altisidora’s assurance; she knew that she was

[109-104]bold, lively, and impudent, but not so much so as to venture to make

[109-105]free in this fashion; and not being prepared for the joke, her

[109-106]astonishment was all the greater. The duke had a mind to keep up the

[109-107]sport, so he said, “It does not seem to me well done in you, sir

[109-108]knight, that after having received the hospitality that has been

[109-109]offered you in this very castle, you should have ventured to carry off

[109-110]even three kerchiefs, not to say my handmaid’s garters. It shows a bad

[109-111]heart and does not tally with your reputation. Restore her garters, or

[109-112]else I defy you to mortal combat, for I am not afraid of rascally

[109-113]enchanters changing or altering my features as they changed his who

[109-114]encountered you into those of my lacquey, Tosilos.”

[109-115]“God forbid,” said Don Quixote, “that I should draw my sword against

[109-116]your illustrious person from which I have received such great favours.

[109-117]The kerchiefs I will restore, as Sancho says he has them; as to the

[109-118]garters that is impossible, for I have not got them, neither has he;

[109-119]and if your handmaiden here will look in her hiding-places, depend upon

[109-120]it she will find them. I have never been a thief, my lord duke, nor do

[109-121]I mean to be so long as I live, if God cease not to have me in his

[109-122]keeping. This damsel by her own confession speaks as one in love, for

[109-123]which I am not to blame, and therefore need not ask pardon, either of

[109-124]her or of your excellence, whom I entreat to have a better opinion of

[109-125]me, and once more to give me leave to pursue my journey.”

[109-126]“And may God so prosper it, Señor Don Quixote,” said the duchess, “that

[109-127]we may always hear good news of your exploits; God speed you; for the

[109-128]longer you stay, the more you inflame the hearts of the damsels who

[109-129]behold you; and as for this one of mine, I will so chastise her that

[109-130]she will not transgress again, either with her eyes or with her words.”

[109-131]“One word and no more, O valiant Don Quixote, I ask you to hear,” said

[109-132]Altisidora, “and that is that I beg your pardon about the theft of the

[109-133]garters; for by God and upon my soul I have got them on, and I have

[109-134]fallen into the same blunder as he did who went looking for his ass

[109-135]being all the while mounted on it.”

[109-136]“Didn’t I say so?” said Sancho. “I’m a likely one to hide thefts! Why

[109-137]if I wanted to deal in them, opportunities came ready enough to me in

[109-138]my government.”

[109-139]Don Quixote bowed his head, and saluted the duke and duchess and all

[109-140]the bystanders, and wheeling Rocinante round, Sancho following him on

[109-141]Dapple, he rode out of the castle, shaping his course for Saragossa.
