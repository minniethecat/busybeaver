WHEREIN IS SET FORTH WHO THE ENCHANTERS AND EXECUTIONERS WERE WHO

[102-001]FLOGGED THE DUENNA AND PINCHED DON QUIXOTE, AND ALSO WHAT BEFELL THE

[102-002]PAGE WHO CARRIED THE LETTER TO TERESA PANZA, SANCHO PANZA’S WIFE

p50a.jpg (104K)


[102-003]Cide Hamete, the painstaking investigator of the minute points of this

[102-004]veracious history, says that when Doña Rodriguez left her own room to

[102-005]go to Don Quixote’s, another duenna who slept with her observed her,

[102-006]and as all duennas are fond of prying, listening, and sniffing, she

[102-007]followed her so silently that the good Rodriguez never perceived it;

[102-008]and as soon as the duenna saw her enter Don Quixote’s room, not to fail

[102-009]in a duenna’s invariable practice of tattling, she hurried off that

[102-010]instant to report to the duchess how Doña Rodriguez was closeted with

[102-011]Don Quixote. The duchess told the duke, and asked him to let her and

[102-012]Altisidora go and see what the said duenna wanted with Don Quixote. The

[102-013]duke gave them leave, and the pair cautiously and quietly crept to the

[102-014]door of the room and posted themselves so close to it that they could

[102-015]hear all that was said inside. But when the duchess heard how the

[102-016]Rodriguez had made public the Aranjuez of her issues she could not

[102-017]restrain herself, nor Altisidora either; and so, filled with rage and

[102-018]thirsting for vengeance, they burst into the room and tormented Don

[102-019]Quixote and flogged the duenna in the manner already described; for

[102-020]indignities offered to their charms and self-esteem mightily provoke

[102-021]the anger of women and make them eager for revenge. The duchess told

[102-022]the duke what had happened, and he was much amused by it; and she, in

[102-023]pursuance of her design of making merry and diverting herself with Don

[102-024]Quixote, despatched the page who had played the part of Dulcinea in the

[102-025]negotiations for her disenchantment (which Sancho Panza in the cares of

[102-026]government had forgotten all about) to Teresa Panza his wife with her

[102-027]husband’s letter and another from herself, and also a great string of

[102-028]fine coral beads as a present.

[102-029]Now the history says this page was very sharp and quick-witted; and

[102-030]eager to serve his lord and lady he set off very willingly for Sancho’s

[102-031]village. Before he entered it he observed a number of women washing in

[102-032]a brook, and asked them if they could tell him whether there lived

[102-033]there a woman of the name of Teresa Panza, wife of one Sancho Panza,

[102-034]squire to a knight called Don Quixote of La Mancha. At the question a

[102-035]young girl who was washing stood up and said, “Teresa Panza is my

[102-036]mother, and that Sancho is my father, and that knight is our master.”

[102-037]“Well then, miss,” said the page, “come and show me where your mother

[102-038]is, for I bring her a letter and a present from your father.”

[102-039]“That I will with all my heart, señor,” said the girl, who seemed to be

[102-040]about fourteen, more or less; and leaving the clothes she was washing

[102-041]to one of her companions, and without putting anything on her head or

[102-042]feet, for she was bare-legged and had her hair hanging about her, away

[102-043]she skipped in front of the page’s horse, saying, “Come, your worship,

[102-044]our house is at the entrance of the town, and my mother is there,

[102-045]sorrowful enough at not having had any news of my father this ever so

[102-046]long.”

[102-047]“Well,” said the page, “I am bringing her such good news that she will

[102-048]have reason to thank God.”

[102-049]And then, skipping, running, and capering, the girl reached the town,

[102-050]but before going into the house she called out at the door, “Come out,

[102-051]mother Teresa, come out, come out; here’s a gentleman with letters and

[102-052]other things from my good father.” At these words her mother Teresa

[102-053]Panza came out spinning a bundle of flax, in a grey petticoat (so short

[102-054]was it one would have fancied “they to her shame had cut it short”), a

[102-055]grey bodice of the same stuff, and a smock. She was not very old,

[102-056]though plainly past forty, strong, healthy, vigorous, and sun-dried;

[102-057]and seeing her daughter and the page on horseback, she exclaimed,

[102-058]“What’s this, child? What gentleman is this?”

[102-059]“A servant of my lady, Doña Teresa Panza,” replied the page; and

[102-060]suiting the action to the word he flung himself off his horse, and with

[102-061]great humility advanced to kneel before the lady Teresa, saying, “Let

[102-062]me kiss your hand, Señora Doña Teresa, as the lawful and only wife of

[102-063]Señor Don Sancho Panza, rightful governor of the island of Barataria.”

[102-064]“Ah, señor, get up, do that,” said Teresa; “for I’m not a bit of a

[102-065]court lady, but only a poor country woman, the daughter of a

[102-066]clodcrusher, and the wife of a squire-errant and not of any governor at

[102-067]all.”

[102-068]“You are,” said the page, “the most worthy wife of a most arch-worthy

[102-069]governor; and as a proof of what I say accept this letter and this

[102-070]present;” and at the same time he took out of his pocket a string of

[102-071]coral beads with gold clasps, and placed it on her neck, and said,

[102-072]“This letter is from his lordship the governor, and the other as well

[102-073]as these coral beads from my lady the duchess, who sends me to your

[102-074]worship.”

[102-075]Teresa stood lost in astonishment, and her daughter just as much, and

[102-076]the girl said, “May I die but our master Don Quixote’s at the bottom of

[102-077]this; he must have given father the government or county he so often

[102-078]promised him.”

[102-079]“That is the truth,” said the page; “for it is through Señor Don

[102-080]Quixote that Señor Sancho is now governor of the island of Barataria,

[102-081]as will be seen by this letter.”

[102-082]“Will your worship read it to me, noble sir?” said Teresa; “for though

[102-083]I can spin I can’t read, not a scrap.”

[102-084]“Nor I either,” said Sanchica; “but wait a bit, and I’ll go and fetch

[102-085]someone who can read it, either the curate himself or the bachelor

[102-086]Samson Carrasco, and they’ll come gladly to hear any news of my

[102-087]father.”

[102-088]“There is no need to fetch anybody,” said the page; “for though I can’t

[102-089]spin I can read, and I’ll read it;” and so he read it through, but as

[102-090]it has been already given it is not inserted here; and then he took out

[102-091]the other one from the duchess, which ran as follows:

[102-092]Friend Teresa,—Your husband Sancho’s good qualities, of heart as well

[102-093]as of head, induced and compelled me to request my husband the duke to

[102-094]give him the government of one of his many islands. I am told he

[102-095]governs like a gerfalcon, of which I am very glad, and my lord the

[102-096]duke, of course, also; and I am very thankful to heaven that I have not

[102-097]made a mistake in choosing him for that same government; for I would

[102-098]have Señora Teresa know that a good governor is hard to find in this

[102-099]world and may God make me as good as Sancho’s way of governing.

[102-100]Herewith I send you, my dear, a string of coral beads with gold clasps;

[102-101]I wish they were Oriental pearls; but “he who gives thee a bone does

[102-102]not wish to see thee dead;” a time will come when we shall become

[102-103]acquainted and meet one another, but God knows the future. Commend me

[102-104]to your daughter Sanchica, and tell her from me to hold herself in

[102-105]readiness, for I mean to make a high match for her when she least

[102-106]expects it. They tell me there are big acorns in your village; send me

[102-107]a couple of dozen or so, and I shall value them greatly as coming from

[102-108]your hand; and write to me at length to assure me of your health and

[102-109]well-being; and if there be anything you stand in need of, it is but to

[102-110]open your mouth, and that shall be the measure; and so God keep you.

[102-111]From this place.

[102-112]Your loving friend,

[102-113]THE DUCHESS.

[102-114]“Ah, what a good, plain, lowly lady!” said Teresa when she heard the

[102-115]letter; “that I may be buried with ladies of that sort, and not the

[102-116]gentlewomen we have in this town, that fancy because they are

[102-117]gentlewomen the wind must not touch them, and go to church with as much

[102-118]airs as if they were queens, no less, and seem to think they are

[102-119]disgraced if they look at a farmer’s wife! And see here how this good

[102-120]lady, for all she’s a duchess, calls me ‘friend,’ and treats me as if I

[102-121]was her equal—and equal may I see her with the tallest church-tower in

[102-122]La Mancha! And as for the acorns, señor, I’ll send her ladyship a peck

[102-123]and such big ones that one might come to see them as a show and a

[102-124]wonder. And now, Sanchica, see that the gentleman is comfortable; put

[102-125]up his horse, and get some eggs out of the stable, and cut plenty of

[102-126]bacon, and let’s give him his dinner like a prince; for the good news

[102-127]he has brought, and his own bonny face deserve it all; and meanwhile

[102-128]I’ll run out and give the neighbours the news of our good luck, and

[102-129]father curate, and Master Nicholas the barber, who are and always have

[102-130]been such friends of thy father’s.”

[102-131]“That I will, mother,” said Sanchica; “but mind, you must give me half

[102-132]of that string; for I don’t think my lady the duchess could have been

[102-133]so stupid as to send it all to you.”

[102-134]“It is all for thee, my child,” said Teresa; “but let me wear it round

[102-135]my neck for a few days; for verily it seems to make my heart glad.”

[102-136]“You will be glad too,” said the page, “when you see the bundle there

[102-137]is in this portmanteau, for it is a suit of the finest cloth, that the

[102-138]governor only wore one day out hunting and now sends, all for Señora

[102-139]Sanchica.”

[102-140]“May he live a thousand years,” said Sanchica, “and the bearer as many,

[102-141]nay two thousand, if needful.”

[102-142]With this Teresa hurried out of the house with the letters, and with

[102-143]the string of beads round her neck, and went along thrumming the

[102-144]letters as if they were a tambourine, and by chance coming across the

[102-145]curate and Samson Carrasco she began capering and saying, “None of us

[102-146]poor now, faith! We’ve got a little government! Ay, let the finest fine

[102-147]lady tackle me, and I’ll give her a setting down!”

[102-148]“What’s all this, Teresa Panza,” said they; “what madness is this, and

[102-149]what papers are those?”

[102-150]“The madness is only this,” said she, “that these are the letters of

[102-151]duchesses and governors, and these I have on my neck are fine coral

[102-152]beads, with ave-marias and paternosters of beaten gold, and I am a

[102-153]governess.”

[102-154]“God help us,” said the curate, “we don’t understand you, Teresa, or

[102-155]know what you are talking about.”

[102-156]“There, you may see it yourselves,” said Teresa, and she handed them

[102-157]the letters.

[102-158]The curate read them out for Samson Carrasco to hear, and Samson and he

[102-159]regarded one another with looks of astonishment at what they had read,

[102-160]and the bachelor asked who had brought the letters. Teresa in reply

[102-161]bade them come with her to her house and they would see the messenger,

[102-162]a most elegant youth, who had brought another present which was worth

[102-163]as much more. The curate took the coral beads from her neck and

[102-164]examined them again and again, and having satisfied himself as to their

[102-165]fineness he fell to wondering afresh, and said, “By the gown I wear I

[102-166]don’t know what to say or think of these letters and presents; on the

[102-167]one hand I can see and feel the fineness of these coral beads, and on

[102-168]the other I read how a duchess sends to beg for a couple of dozen of

[102-169]acorns.”

[102-170]“Square that if you can,” said Carrasco; “well, let’s go and see the

[102-171]messenger, and from him we’ll learn something about this mystery that

[102-172]has turned up.”

[102-173]They did so, and Teresa returned with them. They found the page sifting

[102-174]a little barley for his horse, and Sanchica cutting a rasher of bacon

[102-175]to be paved with eggs for his dinner. His looks and his handsome

[102-176]apparel pleased them both greatly; and after they had saluted him

[102-177]courteously, and he them, Samson begged him to give them his news, as

[102-178]well of Don Quixote as of Sancho Panza, for, he said, though they had

[102-179]read the letters from Sancho and her ladyship the duchess, they were

[102-180]still puzzled and could not make out what was meant by Sancho’s

[102-181]government, and above all of an island, when all or most of those in

[102-182]the Mediterranean belonged to his Majesty.

[102-183]To this the page replied, “As to Señor Sancho Panza’s being a governor

[102-184]there is no doubt whatever; but whether it is an island or not that he

[102-185]governs, with that I have nothing to do; suffice it that it is a town

[102-186]of more than a thousand inhabitants; with regard to the acorns I may

[102-187]tell you my lady the duchess is so unpretending and unassuming that,

[102-188]not to speak of sending to beg for acorns from a peasant woman, she has

[102-189]been known to send to ask for the loan of a comb from one of her

[102-190]neighbours; for I would have your worships know that the ladies of

[102-191]Aragon, though they are just as illustrious, are not so punctilious and

[102-192]haughty as the Castilian ladies; they treat people with greater

[102-193]familiarity.”

[102-194]In the middle of this conversation Sanchica came in with her skirt full

[102-195]of eggs, and said she to the page, “Tell me, señor, does my father wear

[102-196]trunk-hose since he has been governor?”

[102-197]“I have not noticed,” said the page; “but no doubt he wears them.”

[102-198]“Ah! my God!” said Sanchica, “what a sight it must be to see my father

[102-199]in tights! Isn’t it odd that ever since I was born I have had a longing

[102-200]to see my father in trunk-hose?”

[102-201]“As things go you will see that if you live,” said the page; “by God he

[102-202]is in the way to take the road with a sunshade if the government only

[102-203]lasts him two months more.”

[102-204]The curate and the bachelor could see plainly enough that the page

[102-205]spoke in a waggish vein; but the fineness of the coral beads, and the

[102-206]hunting suit that Sancho sent (for Teresa had already shown it to them)

[102-207]did away with the impression; and they could not help laughing at

[102-208]Sanchica’s wish, and still more when Teresa said, “Señor curate, look

[102-209]about if there’s anybody here going to Madrid or Toledo, to buy me a

[102-210]hooped petticoat, a proper fashionable one of the best quality; for

[102-211]indeed and indeed I must do honour to my husband’s government as well

[102-212]as I can; nay, if I am put to it and have to, I’ll go to Court and set

[102-213]a coach like all the world; for she who has a governor for her husband

[102-214]may very well have one and keep one.”

[102-215]“And why not, mother!” said Sanchica; “would to God it were to-day

[102-216]instead of to-morrow, even though they were to say when they saw me

[102-217]seated in the coach with my mother, ‘See that rubbish, that

[102-218]garlic-stuffed fellow’s daughter, how she goes stretched at her ease in

[102-219]a coach as if she was a she-pope!’ But let them tramp through the mud,

[102-220]and let me go in my coach with my feet off the ground. Bad luck to

[102-221]backbiters all over the world; ‘let me go warm and the people may

[102-222]laugh.’ Do I say right, mother?”

[102-223]“To be sure you do, my child,” said Teresa; “and all this good luck,

[102-224]and even more, my good Sancho foretold me; and thou wilt see, my

[102-225]daughter, he won’t stop till he has made me a countess; for to make a

[102-226]beginning is everything in luck; and as I have heard thy good father

[102-227]say many a time (for besides being thy father he’s the father of

[102-228]proverbs too), ‘When they offer thee a heifer, run with a halter; when

[102-229]they offer thee a government, take it; when they would give thee a

[102-230]county, seize it; when they say, “Here, here!” to thee with something

[102-231]good, swallow it.’ Oh no! go to sleep, and don’t answer the strokes of

[102-232]good fortune and the lucky chances that are knocking at the door of

[102-233]your house!”

[102-234]“And what do I care,” added Sanchica, “whether anybody says when he

[102-235]sees me holding my head up, ‘The dog saw himself in hempen breeches,’

[102-236]and the rest of it?”

[102-237]Hearing this the curate said, “I do believe that all this family of the

[102-238]Panzas are born with a sackful of proverbs in their insides, every one

[102-239]of them; I never saw one of them that does not pour them out at all

[102-240]times and on all occasions.”

[102-241]“That is true,” said the page, “for Señor Governor Sancho utters them

[102-242]at every turn; and though a great many of them are not to the purpose,

[102-243]still they amuse one, and my lady the duchess and the duke praise them

[102-244]highly.”

[102-245]“Then you still maintain that all this about Sancho’s government is

[102-246]true, señor,” said the bachelor, “and that there actually is a duchess

[102-247]who sends him presents and writes to him? Because we, although we have

[102-248]handled the present and read the letters, don’t believe it and suspect

[102-249]it to be something in the line of our fellow-townsman Don Quixote, who

[102-250]fancies that everything is done by enchantment; and for this reason I

[102-251]am almost ready to say that I’d like to touch and feel your worship to

[102-252]see whether you are a mere ambassador of the imagination or a man of

[102-253]flesh and blood.”

[102-254]“All I know, sirs,” replied the page, “is that I am a real ambassador,

[102-255]and that Señor Sancho Panza is governor as a matter of fact, and that

[102-256]my lord and lady the duke and duchess can give, and have given him this

[102-257]same government, and that I have heard it said Sancho Panza bears

[102-258]himself very stoutly therein; whether there be any enchantment in all

[102-259]this or not, it is for your worships to settle between you; for that’s

[102-260]all I know by the oath I swear, and that is by the life of my parents

[102-261]whom I have still alive, and love dearly.”

[102-262]“It may be so,” said the bachelor; “but _dubitat Augustinus_.”

[102-263]“Doubt who will,” said the page; “what I have told you is the truth,

[102-264]and that will always rise above falsehood as oil above water; if not

[102-265]_operibus credite, et non verbis_. Let one of you come with me, and he

[102-266]will see with his eyes what he does not believe with his ears.”

[102-267]“It’s for me to make that trip,” said Sanchica; “take me with you,

[102-268]señor, behind you on your horse; for I’ll go with all my heart to see

[102-269]my father.”

[102-270]“Governors’ daughters,” said the page, “must not travel along the roads

[102-271]alone, but accompanied by coaches and litters and a great number of

[102-272]attendants.”

[102-273]“By God,” said Sanchica, “I can go just as well mounted on a she-ass as

[102-274]in a coach; what a dainty lass you must take me for!”

[102-275]“Hush, girl,” said Teresa; “you don’t know what you’re talking about;

[102-276]the gentleman is quite right, for ‘as the time so the behaviour;’ when

[102-277]it was Sancho it was ‘Sancha;’ when it is governor it’s ‘señora;’ I

[102-278]don’t know if I’m right.”

[102-279]“Señora Teresa says more than she is aware of,” said the page; “and now

[102-280]give me something to eat and let me go at once, for I mean to return

[102-281]this evening.”

[102-282]“Come and do penance with me,” said the curate at this; “for Señora

[102-283]Teresa has more will than means to serve so worthy a guest.”

[102-284]The page refused, but had to consent at last for his own sake; and the

[102-285]curate took him home with him very gladly, in order to have an

[102-286]opportunity of questioning him at leisure about Don Quixote and his

[102-287]doings. The bachelor offered to write the letters in reply for Teresa;

[102-288]but she did not care to let him mix himself up in her affairs, for she

[102-289]thought him somewhat given to joking; and so she gave a cake and a

[102-290]couple of eggs to a young acolyte who was a penman, and he wrote for

[102-291]her two letters, one for her husband and the other for the duchess,

[102-292]dictated out of her own head, which are not the worst inserted in this

[102-293]great history, as will be seen farther on.
