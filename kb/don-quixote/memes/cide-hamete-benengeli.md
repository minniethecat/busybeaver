## Overview

Cide Hamete Benengeli is one of the most ingenious narrative inventions in *Don Quixote*: the purported original author of the history, an Arab historian whose manuscript Cervantes (or the novel's first-person narrator) discovers in a Toledo marketplace and hires a morisco to translate. The name itself is a bilingual joke — *Cide* (from Arabic *sīdī*, "my lord"), *Hamete* (a variant of Hamid), and *Benengeli* (a play on *berenjena*, "eggplant" in Spanish) — so the "author" is roughly "Lord Hamid Eggplant." Cervantes uses Cide Hamete to constantly remind the reader that the story is a translation of a translation, filtered through an unreliable narrator whose motives (as a Moor writing about Christians) are perpetually suspect.

## In Don Quixote

Cide Hamete is introduced in Chapter 9 of Part I, when the narrative breaks off mid-battle (Don Quixote and the Biscayan, swords raised) because the narrator claims his source text has run out. The discovery of Cide Hamete's Arabic manuscript in the Toledo marketplace — and the morisco's translation of it — is the novel's first and most famous metafictional intervention [009-000]. From this point forward, Cide Hamete is invoked periodically as the authority behind the narrative, but always with ironic distance: the narrator questions his accuracy, his motives, and his taste. In Part II, Cide Hamete becomes more present, sometimes addressing the reader directly. At the novel's end, he speaks in his own voice to declare the story definitively finished and to warn off any sequel-writers [126-172].

## Key Passages

> "Cide Hamete Benengeli, in the Second Part of this history, says that on coming to write this chapter the translator did not render the original as it was written..." [055-039]

> "A blessing on Cide Hamete Benengeli, who has written the history of your great deeds, and a second blessing on that curious-minded gentleman who had it translated from the Arabic into our vulgar Castilian for the universal entertainment of mankind." [055-039]

> "For me alone was Don Quixote born, and I for him; it was his to act, mine to write; we two together make but one, in spite of that Tordesillesque pretender who has dared, and may dare again, to pen the deeds of my valiant knight — a burden too heavy for his shoulders..." [126-172]

## Interpretation

Cide Hamete is far more than a joke about eggplants. He is Cervantes' masterstroke of narrative self-consciousness. By attributing the story to an Arab historian, Cervantes creates a triple filter — Arabic original → morisco translation → Castilian narration — that undermines any claim to historical truth. This is not merely playful; it is philosophically radical for 1605. The device allows Cervantes to satirize the conventions of chivalric romance (which invariably claimed to be true histories translated from ancient manuscripts) while also raising genuine questions about authorship, translation, and the constructed nature of all narrative. Cide Hamete is, in effect, Cervantes' way of signing the novel without signing it — a pseudonym that conceals and reveals at once.
