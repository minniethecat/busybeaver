OF THE BRISTLY ADVENTURE THAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE

p68a.jpg (119K)


[120-001]The night was somewhat dark, for though there was a moon in the sky it

[120-002]was not in a quarter where she could be seen; for sometimes the lady

[120-003]Diana goes on a stroll to the antipodes, and leaves the mountains all

[120-004]black and the valleys in darkness. Don Quixote obeyed nature so far as

[120-005]to sleep his first sleep, but did not give way to the second, very

[120-006]different from Sancho, who never had any second, because with him sleep

[120-007]lasted from night till morning, wherein he showed what a sound

[120-008]constitution and few cares he had. Don Quixote’s cares kept him

[120-009]restless, so much so that he awoke Sancho and said to him, “I am

[120-010]amazed, Sancho, at the unconcern of thy temperament. I believe thou art

[120-011]made of marble or hard brass, incapable of any emotion or feeling

[120-012]whatever. I lie awake while thou sleepest, I weep while thou singest, I

[120-013]am faint with fasting while thou art sluggish and torpid from pure

[120-014]repletion. It is the duty of good servants to share the sufferings and

[120-015]feel the sorrows of their masters, if it be only for the sake of

[120-016]appearances. See the calmness of the night, the solitude of the spot,

[120-017]inviting us to break our slumbers by a vigil of some sort. Rise as thou

[120-018]livest, and retire a little distance, and with a good heart and

[120-019]cheerful courage give thyself three or four hundred lashes on account

[120-020]of Dulcinea’s disenchantment score; and this I entreat of thee, making

[120-021]it a request, for I have no desire to come to grips with thee a second

[120-022]time, as I know thou hast a heavy hand. As soon as thou hast laid them

[120-023]on we will pass the rest of the night, I singing my separation, thou

[120-024]thy constancy, making a beginning at once with the pastoral life we are

[120-025]to follow at our village.”

[120-026]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “I’m no monk to get up out of the middle of my

[120-027]sleep and scourge myself, nor does it seem to me that one can pass from

[120-028]one extreme of the pain of whipping to the other of music. Will your

[120-029]worship let me sleep, and not worry me about whipping myself? or you’ll

[120-030]make me swear never to touch a hair of my doublet, not to say my

[120-031]flesh.”

[120-032]“O hard heart!” said Don Quixote, “O pitiless squire! O bread

[120-033]ill-bestowed and favours ill-acknowledged, both those I have done thee

[120-034]and those I mean to do thee! Through me hast thou seen thyself a

[120-035]governor, and through me thou seest thyself in immediate expectation of

[120-036]being a count, or obtaining some other equivalent title, for I—_post

[120-037]tenebras spero lucem_.”

[120-038]“I don’t know what that is,” said Sancho; “all I know is that so long

[120-039]as I am asleep I have neither fear nor hope, trouble nor glory; and

[120-040]good luck betide him that invented sleep, the cloak that covers over

[120-041]all a man’s thoughts, the food that removes hunger, the drink that

[120-042]drives away thirst, the fire that warms the cold, the cold that tempers

[120-043]the heat, and, to wind up with, the universal coin wherewith everything

[120-044]is bought, the weight and balance that makes the shepherd equal with

[120-045]the king and the fool with the wise man. Sleep, I have heard say, has

[120-046]only one fault, that it is like death; for between a sleeping man and a

[120-047]dead man there is very little difference.”

[120-048]“Never have I heard thee speak so elegantly as now, Sancho,” said Don

[120-049]Quixote; “and here I begin to see the truth of the proverb thou dost

[120-050]sometimes quote, ‘Not with whom thou art bred, but with whom thou art

[120-051]fed.’”

[120-052]“Ha, by my life, master mine,” said Sancho, “it’s not I that am

[120-053]stringing proverbs now, for they drop in pairs from your worship’s

[120-054]mouth faster than from mine; only there is this difference between mine

[120-055]and yours, that yours are well-timed and mine are untimely; but anyhow,

[120-056]they are all proverbs.”

[120-057]At this point they became aware of a harsh indistinct noise that seemed

[120-058]to spread through all the valleys around. Don Quixote stood up and laid

[120-059]his hand upon his sword, and Sancho ensconced himself under Dapple and

[120-060]put the bundle of armour on one side of him and the ass’s pack-saddle

[120-061]on the other, in fear and trembling as great as Don Quixote’s

[120-062]perturbation. Each instant the noise increased and came nearer to the

[120-063]two terrified men, or at least to one, for as to the other, his courage

[120-064]is known to all. The fact of the matter was that some men were taking

[120-065]above six hundred pigs to sell at a fair, and were on their way with

[120-066]them at that hour, and so great was the noise they made and their

[120-067]grunting and blowing, that they deafened the ears of Don Quixote and

[120-068]Sancho Panza, and they could not make out what it was. The wide-spread

[120-069]grunting drove came on in a surging mass, and without showing any

[120-070]respect for Don Quixote’s dignity or Sancho’s, passed right over the

[120-071]pair of them, demolishing Sancho’s entrenchments, and not only

[120-072]upsetting Don Quixote but sweeping Rocinante off his feet into the

[120-073]bargain; and what with the trampling and the grunting, and the pace at

[120-074]which the unclean beasts went, pack-saddle, armour, Dapple and

[120-075]Rocinante were left scattered on the ground and Sancho and Don Quixote

[120-076]at their wits’ end.

[120-077]Sancho got up as well as he could and begged his master to give him his

[120-078]sword, saying he wanted to kill half a dozen of those dirty unmannerly

[120-079]pigs, for he had by this time found out that that was what they were.

[120-080]“Let them be, my friend,” said Don Quixote; “this insult is the penalty

[120-081]of my sin; and it is the righteous chastisement of heaven that jackals

[120-082]should devour a vanquished knight, and wasps sting him and pigs trample

[120-083]him under foot.”

[120-084]“I suppose it is the chastisement of heaven, too,” said Sancho, “that

[120-085]flies should prick the squires of vanquished knights, and lice eat

[120-086]them, and hunger assail them. If we squires were the sons of the

[120-087]knights we serve, or their very near relations, it would be no wonder

[120-088]if the penalty of their misdeeds overtook us, even to the fourth

[120-089]generation. But what have the Panzas to do with the Quixotes? Well,

[120-090]well, let’s lie down again and sleep out what little of the night

[120-091]there’s left, and God will send us dawn and we shall be all right.”

[120-092]“Sleep thou, Sancho,” returned Don Quixote, “for thou wast born to

[120-093]sleep as I was born to watch; and during the time it now wants of dawn

[120-094]I will give a loose rein to my thoughts, and seek a vent for them in a

[120-095]little madrigal which, unknown to thee, I composed in my head last

[120-096]night.”

[120-097]“I should think,” said Sancho, “that the thoughts that allow one to

[120-098]make verses cannot be of great consequence; let your worship string

[120-099]verses as much as you like and I’ll sleep as much as I can;” and

[120-100]forthwith, taking the space of ground he required, he muffled himself

[120-101]up and fell into a sound sleep, undisturbed by bond, debt, or trouble

[120-102]of any sort. Don Quixote, propped up against the trunk of a beech or a

[120-103]cork tree—for Cide Hamete does not specify what kind of tree it

[120-104]was—sang in this strain to the accompaniment of his own sighs:

[120-105]When in my mind

[120-106]I muse, O Love, upon thy cruelty,

[120-107]To death I flee,

[120-108]In hope therein the end of all to find.

[120-109]But drawing near

[120-110]That welcome haven in my sea of woe,

[120-111]Such joy I know,

[120-112]That life revives, and still I linger here.

[120-113]Thus life doth slay,

[120-114]And death again to life restoreth me;

[120-115]Strange destiny,

[120-116]That deals with life and death as with a play!

[120-117]He accompanied each verse with many sighs and not a few tears, just

[120-118]like one whose heart was pierced with grief at his defeat and his

[120-119]separation from Dulcinea.

[120-120]And now daylight came, and the sun smote Sancho on the eyes with his

[120-121]beams. He awoke, roused himself up, shook himself and stretched his

[120-122]lazy limbs, and seeing the havoc the pigs had made with his stores he

[120-123]cursed the drove, and more besides. Then the pair resumed their

[120-124]journey, and as evening closed in they saw coming towards them some ten

[120-125]men on horseback and four or five on foot. Don Quixote’s heart beat

[120-126]quick and Sancho’s quailed with fear, for the persons approaching them

[120-127]carried lances and bucklers, and were in very warlike guise. Don

[120-128]Quixote turned to Sancho and said, “If I could make use of my weapons,

[120-129]and my promise had not tied my hands, I would count this host that

[120-130]comes against us but cakes and fancy bread; but perhaps it may prove

[120-131]something different from what we apprehend.” The men on horseback now

[120-132]came up, and raising their lances surrounded Don Quixote in silence,

[120-133]and pointed them at his back and breast, menacing him with death. One

[120-134]of those on foot, putting his finger to his lips as a sign to him to be

[120-135]silent, seized Rocinante’s bridle and drew him out of the road, and the

[120-136]others driving Sancho and Dapple before them, and all maintaining a

[120-137]strange silence, followed in the steps of the one who led Don Quixote.

[120-138]The latter two or three times attempted to ask where they were taking

[120-139]him to and what they wanted, but the instant he began to open his lips

[120-140]they threatened to close them with the points of their lances; and

[120-141]Sancho fared the same way, for the moment he seemed about to speak one

[120-142]of those on foot punched him with a goad, and Dapple likewise, as if he

[120-143]too wanted to talk. Night set in, they quickened their pace, and the

[120-144]fears of the two prisoners grew greater, especially as they heard

[120-145]themselves assailed with—“Get on, ye Troglodytes;” “Silence, ye

[120-146]barbarians;” “March, ye cannibals;” “No murmuring, ye Scythians;”

[120-147]“Don’t open your eyes, ye murderous Polyphemes, ye blood-thirsty

[120-148]lions,” and suchlike names with which their captors harassed the ears

[120-149]of the wretched master and man. Sancho went along saying to himself,

[120-150]“We, tortolites, barbers, animals! I don’t like those names at all;

[120-151]‘it’s in a bad wind our corn is being winnowed;’ ‘misfortune comes upon

[120-152]us all at once like sticks on a dog,’ and God grant it may be no worse

[120-153]than them that this unlucky adventure has in store for us.”

[120-154]Don Quixote rode completely dazed, unable with the aid of all his wits

[120-155]to make out what could be the meaning of these abusive names they

[120-156]called them, and the only conclusion he could arrive at was that there

[120-157]was no good to be hoped for and much evil to be feared. And now, about

[120-158]an hour after midnight, they reached a castle which Don Quixote saw at

[120-159]once was the duke’s, where they had been but a short time before. “God

[120-160]bless me!” said he, as he recognised the mansion, “what does this mean?

[120-161]It is all courtesy and politeness in this house; but with the

[120-162]vanquished good turns into evil, and evil into worse.”

[120-163]They entered the chief court of the castle and found it prepared and

[120-164]fitted up in a style that added to their amazement and doubled their

[120-165]fears, as will be seen in the following chapter.
