[113-001]OF WHAT HAPPENED DON QUIXOTE ON ENTERING BARCELONA, TOGETHER WITH OTHER

MATTERS THAT PARTAKE OF THE TRUE RATHER THAN OF THE INGENIOUS

p61a.jpg (143K)


[113-002]Don Quixote passed three days and three nights with Roque, and had he

[113-003]passed three hundred years he would have found enough to observe and

[113-004]wonder at in his mode of life. At daybreak they were in one spot, at

[113-005]dinner-time in another; sometimes they fled without knowing from whom,

[113-006]at other times they lay in wait, not knowing for what. They slept

[113-007]standing, breaking their slumbers to shift from place to place. There

[113-008]was nothing but sending out spies and scouts, posting sentinels and

[113-009]blowing the matches of harquebusses, though they carried but few, for

[113-010]almost all used flintlocks. Roque passed his nights in some place or

[113-011]other apart from his men, that they might not know where he was, for

[113-012]the many proclamations the viceroy of Barcelona had issued against his

[113-013]life kept him in fear and uneasiness, and he did not venture to trust

[113-014]anyone, afraid that even his own men would kill him or deliver him up

[113-015]to the authorities; of a truth, a weary miserable life! At length, by

[113-016]unfrequented roads, short cuts, and secret paths, Roque, Don Quixote,

[113-017]and Sancho, together with six squires, set out for Barcelona. They

[113-018]reached the strand on Saint John’s Eve during the night; and Roque,

[113-019]after embracing Don Quixote and Sancho (to whom he presented the ten

[113-020]crowns he had promised but had not until then given), left them with

[113-021]many expressions of good-will on both sides.

[113-022]Roque went back, while Don Quixote remained on horseback, just as he

[113-023]was, waiting for day, and it was not long before the countenance of the

[113-024]fair Aurora began to show itself at the balconies of the east,

[113-025]gladdening the grass and flowers, if not the ear, though to gladden

[113-026]that too there came at the same moment a sound of clarions and drums,

[113-027]and a din of bells, and a tramp, tramp, and cries of “Clear the way

[113-028]there!” of some runners, that seemed to issue from the city.

p61b.jpg (271K)


[113-029]The dawn made way for the sun that with a face broader than a buckler

[113-030]began to rise slowly above the low line of the horizon; Don Quixote and

[113-031]Sancho gazed all round them; they beheld the sea, a sight until then

[113-032]unseen by them; it struck them as exceedingly spacious and broad, much

[113-033]more so than the lakes of Ruidera which they had seen in La Mancha.

[113-034]They saw the galleys along the beach, which, lowering their awnings,

[113-035]displayed themselves decked with streamers and pennons that trembled in

[113-036]the breeze and kissed and swept the water, while on board the bugles,

[113-037]trumpets, and clarions were sounding and filling the air far and near

[113-038]with melodious warlike notes. Then they began to move and execute a

[113-039]kind of skirmish upon the calm water, while a vast number of horsemen

[113-040]on fine horses and in showy liveries, issuing from the city, engaged on

[113-041]their side in a somewhat similar movement. The soldiers on board the

[113-042]galleys kept up a ceaseless fire, which they on the walls and forts of

[113-043]the city returned, and the heavy cannon rent the air with the

[113-044]tremendous noise they made, to which the gangway guns of the galleys

[113-045]replied. The bright sea, the smiling earth, the clear air—though at

[113-046]times darkened by the smoke of the guns—all seemed to fill the whole

[113-047]multitude with unexpected delight. Sancho could not make out how it was

[113-048]that those great masses that moved over the sea had so many feet.

[113-049]And now the horsemen in livery came galloping up with shouts and

[113-050]outlandish cries and cheers to where Don Quixote stood amazed and

[113-051]wondering; and one of them, he to whom Roque had sent word, addressing

[113-052]him exclaimed, “Welcome to our city, mirror, beacon, star and cynosure

[113-053]of all knight-errantry in its widest extent! Welcome, I say, valiant

[113-054]Don Quixote of La Mancha; not the false, the fictitious, the

[113-055]apocryphal, that these latter days have offered us in lying histories,

[113-056]but the true, the legitimate, the real one that Cide Hamete Benengeli,

[113-057]flower of historians, has described to us!”

[113-058]Don Quixote made no answer, nor did the horsemen wait for one, but

[113-059]wheeling again with all their followers, they began curvetting round

[113-060]Don Quixote, who, turning to Sancho, said, “These gentlemen have

[113-061]plainly recognised us; I will wager they have read our history, and

[113-062]even that newly printed one by the Aragonese.”

[113-063]The cavalier who had addressed Don Quixote again approached him and

[113-064]said, “Come with us, Señor Don Quixote, for we are all of us your

[113-065]servants and great friends of Roque Guinart’s;” to which Don Quixote

[113-066]returned, “If courtesy breeds courtesy, yours, sir knight, is daughter

[113-067]or very nearly akin to the great Roque’s; carry me where you please; I

[113-068]will have no will but yours, especially if you deign to employ it in

[113-069]your service.”

p61c.jpg (448K)


[113-070]The cavalier replied with words no less polite, and then, all closing

[113-071]in around him, they set out with him for the city, to the music of the

[113-072]clarions and the drums. As they were entering it, the wicked one, who

[113-073]is the author of all mischief, and the boys who are wickeder than the

[113-074]wicked one, contrived that a couple of these audacious irrepressible

[113-075]urchins should force their way through the crowd, and lifting up, one

[113-076]of them Dapple’s tail and the other Rocinante’s, insert a bunch of

[113-077]furze under each. The poor beasts felt the strange spurs and added to

[113-078]their anguish by pressing their tails tight, so much so that, cutting a

[113-079]multitude of capers, they flung their masters to the ground. Don

[113-080]Quixote, covered with shame and out of countenance, ran to pluck the

[113-081]plume from his poor jade’s tail, while Sancho did the same for Dapple.

[113-082]His conductors tried to punish the audacity of the boys, but there was

[113-083]no possibility of doing so, for they hid themselves among the hundreds

[113-084]of others that were following them. Don Quixote and Sancho mounted once

[113-085]more, and with the same music and acclamations reached their

[113-086]conductor’s house, which was large and stately, that of a rich

[113-087]gentleman, in short; and there for the present we will leave them, for

[113-088]such is Cide Hamete’s pleasure.
