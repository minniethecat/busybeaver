OF THE SECOND SALLY OF OUR WORTHY KNIGHT DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA

[007-001]At this instant Don Quixote began shouting out, “Here, here, valiant

[007-002]knights! here is need for you to put forth the might of your strong

[007-003]arms, for they of the Court are gaining the mastery in the tourney!”

[007-004]Called away by this noise and outcry, they proceeded no farther with

[007-005]the scrutiny of the remaining books, and so it is thought that “The

[007-006]Carolea,” “The Lion of Spain,” and “The Deeds of the Emperor,” written

[007-007]by Don Luis de Ávila, went to the fire unseen and unheard; for no doubt

[007-008]they were among those that remained, and perhaps if the curate had seen

[007-009]them they would not have undergone so severe a sentence.

[007-010]When they reached Don Quixote he was already out of bed, and was still

[007-011]shouting and raving, and slashing and cutting all round, as wide awake

[007-012]as if he had never slept.

[007-013]They closed with him and by force got him back to bed, and when he had

[007-014]become a little calm, addressing the curate, he said to him, “Of a

[007-015]truth, Señor Archbishop Turpin, it is a great disgrace for us who call

[007-016]ourselves the Twelve Peers, so carelessly to allow the knights of the

[007-017]Court to gain the victory in this tourney, we the adventurers having

[007-018]carried off the honour on the three former days.”

[007-019]“Hush, gossip,” said the curate; “please God, the luck may turn, and

[007-020]what is lost to-day may be won to-morrow; for the present let your

[007-021]worship have a care of your health, for it seems to me that you are

[007-022]over-fatigued, if not badly wounded.”

[007-023]“Wounded no,” said Don Quixote, “but bruised and battered no doubt, for

[007-024]that bastard Don Roland has cudgelled me with the trunk of an oak tree,

[007-025]and all for envy, because he sees that I alone rival him in his

[007-026]achievements. But I should not call myself Reinaldos of Montalvan did

[007-027]he not pay me for it in spite of all his enchantments as soon as I rise

[007-028]from this bed. For the present let them bring me something to eat, for

[007-029]that, I feel, is what will be more to my purpose, and leave it to me to

[007-030]avenge myself.”

[007-031]They did as he wished; they gave him something to eat, and once more he

[007-032]fell asleep, leaving them marvelling at his madness.

[007-033]That night the housekeeper burned to ashes all the books that were in

[007-034]the yard and in the whole house; and some must have been consumed that

[007-035]deserved preservation in everlasting archives, but their fate and the

[007-036]laziness of the examiner did not permit it, and so in them was verified

[007-037]the proverb that the innocent suffer for the guilty.

[007-038]One of the remedies which the curate and the barber immediately applied

[007-039]to their friend’s disorder was to wall up and plaster the room where

[007-040]the books were, so that when he got up he should not find them

[007-041](possibly the cause being removed the effect might cease), and they

[007-042]might say that a magician had carried them off, room and all; and this

[007-043]was done with all despatch. Two days later Don Quixote got up, and the

[007-044]first thing he did was to go and look at his books, and not finding the

[007-045]room where he had left it, he wandered from side to side looking for

[007-046]it. He came to the place where the door used to be, and tried it with

[007-047]his hands, and turned and twisted his eyes in every direction without

[007-048]saying a word; but after a good while he asked his housekeeper

[007-049]whereabouts was the room that held his books.

[007-050]The housekeeper, who had been already well instructed in what she was

[007-051]to answer, said, “What room or what nothing is it that your worship is

[007-052]looking for? There are neither room nor books in this house now, for

[007-053]the devil himself has carried all away.”

[007-054]“It was not the devil,” said the niece, “but a magician who came on a

[007-055]cloud one night after the day your worship left this, and dismounting

[007-056]from a serpent that he rode he entered the room, and what he did there

[007-057]I know not, but after a little while he made off, flying through the

[007-058]roof, and left the house full of smoke; and when we went to see what he

[007-059]had done we saw neither book nor room: but we remember very well, the

[007-060]housekeeper and I, that on leaving, the old villain said in a loud

[007-061]voice that, for a private grudge he owed the owner of the books and the

[007-062]room, he had done mischief in that house that would be discovered

[007-063]by-and-by: he said too that his name was the Sage Muñaton.”

[007-064]“He must have said Friston,” said Don Quixote.

[007-065]“I don’t know whether he called himself Friston or Friton,” said the

[007-066]housekeeper, “I only know that his name ended with ‘ton.’”

[007-067]“So it does,” said Don Quixote, “and he is a sage magician, a great

[007-068]enemy of mine, who has a spite against me because he knows by his arts

[007-069]and lore that in process of time I am to engage in single combat with a

[007-070]knight whom he befriends and that I am to conquer, and he will be

[007-071]unable to prevent it; and for this reason he endeavours to do me all

[007-072]the ill turns that he can; but I promise him it will be hard for him to

[007-073]oppose or avoid what is decreed by Heaven.”

[007-074]“Who doubts that?” said the niece; “but, uncle, who mixes you up in

[007-075]these quarrels? Would it not be better to remain at peace in your own

[007-076]house instead of roaming the world looking for better bread than ever

[007-077]came of wheat, never reflecting that many go for wool and come back

[007-078]shorn?”

[007-079]“Oh, niece of mine,” replied Don Quixote, “how much astray art thou in

[007-080]thy reckoning: ere they shear me I shall have plucked away and stripped

[007-081]off the beards of all who dare to touch only the tip of a hair of

[007-082]mine.”

[007-083]The two were unwilling to make any further answer, as they saw that his

[007-084]anger was kindling.

[007-085]In short, then, he remained at home fifteen days very quietly without

[007-086]showing any signs of a desire to take up with his former delusions, and

[007-087]during this time he held lively discussions with his two gossips, the

[007-088]curate and the barber, on the point he maintained, that knights-errant

[007-089]were what the world stood most in need of, and that in him was to be

[007-090]accomplished the revival of knight-errantry. The curate sometimes

[007-091]contradicted him, sometimes agreed with him, for if he had not observed

[007-092]this precaution he would have been unable to bring him to reason.

[007-093]Meanwhile Don Quixote worked upon a farm labourer, a neighbour of his,

[007-094]an honest man (if indeed that title can be given to him who is poor),

[007-095]but with very little wit in his pate. In a word, he so talked him over,

[007-096]and with such persuasions and promises, that the poor clown made up his

[007-097]mind to sally forth with him and serve him as esquire. Don Quixote,

[007-098]among other things, told him he ought to be ready to go with him

[007-099]gladly, because any moment an adventure might occur that might win an

[007-100]island in the twinkling of an eye and leave him governor of it. On

[007-101]these and the like promises Sancho Panza (for so the labourer was

[007-102]called) left wife and children, and engaged himself as esquire to his

[007-103]neighbour.

[007-104]Don Quixote next set about getting some money; and selling one thing

[007-105]and pawning another, and making a bad bargain in every case, he got

[007-106]together a fair sum. He provided himself with a buckler, which he

[007-107]begged as a loan from a friend, and, restoring his battered helmet as

[007-108]best he could, he warned his squire Sancho of the day and hour he meant

[007-109]to set out, that he might provide himself with what he thought most

[007-110]needful. Above all, he charged him to take alforjas with him. The other

[007-111]said he would, and that he meant to take also a very good ass he had,

[007-112]as he was not much given to going on foot. About the ass, Don Quixote

[007-113]hesitated a little, trying whether he could call to mind any

[007-114]knight-errant taking with him an esquire mounted on ass-back, but no

[007-115]instance occurred to his memory. For all that, however, he determined

[007-116]to take him, intending to furnish him with a more honourable mount when

[007-117]a chance of it presented itself, by appropriating the horse of the

[007-118]first discourteous knight he encountered. Himself he provided with

[007-119]shirts and such other things as he could, according to the advice the

[007-120]host had given him; all which being done, without taking leave, Sancho

[007-121]Panza of his wife and children, or Don Quixote of his housekeeper and

[007-122]niece, they sallied forth unseen by anybody from the village one night,

[007-123]and made such good way in the course of it that by daylight they held

[007-124]themselves safe from discovery, even should search be made for them.

[007-125]Sancho rode on his ass like a patriarch, with his alforjas and bota,

[007-126]and longing to see himself soon governor of the island his master had

[007-127]promised him. Don Quixote decided upon taking the same route and road

[007-128]he had taken on his first journey, that over the Campo de Montiel,

[007-129]which he travelled with less discomfort than on the last occasion, for,

[007-130]as it was early morning and the rays of the sun fell on them obliquely,

[007-131]the heat did not distress them.

[007-132]And now said Sancho Panza to his master, “Your worship will take care,

[007-133]Señor Knight-errant, not to forget about the island you have promised

[007-134]me, for be it ever so big I’ll be equal to governing it.”

[007-135]To which Don Quixote replied, “Thou must know, friend Sancho Panza,

[007-136]that it was a practice very much in vogue with the knights-errant of

[007-137]old to make their squires governors of the islands or kingdoms they

[007-138]won, and I am determined that there shall be no failure on my part in

[007-139]so liberal a custom; on the contrary, I mean to improve upon it, for

[007-140]they sometimes, and perhaps most frequently, waited until their squires

[007-141]were old, and then when they had had enough of service and hard days

[007-142]and worse nights, they gave them some title or other, of count, or at

[007-143]the most marquis, of some valley or province more or less; but if thou

[007-144]livest and I live, it may well be that before six days are over, I may

[007-145]have won some kingdom that has others dependent upon it, which will be

[007-146]just the thing to enable thee to be crowned king of one of them. Nor

[007-147]needst thou count this wonderful, for things and chances fall to the

[007-148]lot of such knights in ways so unexampled and unexpected that I might

[007-149]easily give thee even more than I promise thee.”

[007-150]“In that case,” said Sancho Panza, “if I should become a king by one of

[007-151]those miracles your worship speaks of, even Juana Gutierrez, my old

[007-152]woman, would come to be queen and my children infantes.”

[007-153]“Well, who doubts it?” said Don Quixote.

[007-154]“I doubt it,” replied Sancho Panza, “because for my part I am persuaded

[007-155]that though God should shower down kingdoms upon earth, not one of them

[007-156]would fit the head of Mari Gutierrez. Let me tell you, señor, she is

[007-157]not worth two maravedis for a queen; countess will fit her better, and

[007-158]that only with God’s help.”

[007-159]“Leave it to God, Sancho,” returned Don Quixote, “for he will give her

[007-160]what suits her best; but do not undervalue thyself so much as to come

[007-161]to be content with anything less than being governor of a province.”

[007-162]“I will not, señor,” answered Sancho, “specially as I have a man of

[007-163]such quality for a master in your worship, who will know how to give me

[007-164]all that will be suitable for me and that I can bear.”
