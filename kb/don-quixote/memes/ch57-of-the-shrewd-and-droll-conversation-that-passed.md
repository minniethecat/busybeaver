OF THE SHREWD AND DROLL CONVERSATION THAT PASSED BETWEEN SANCHO PANZA

[057-001]AND HIS WIFE TERESA PANZA, AND OTHER MATTERS WORTHY OF BEING DULY

RECORDED

p05a.jpg (129K)


[057-002]The translator of this history, when he comes to write this fifth

[057-003]chapter, says that he considers it apocryphal, because in it Sancho

[057-004]Panza speaks in a style unlike that which might have been expected from

[057-005]his limited intelligence, and says things so subtle that he does not

[057-006]think it possible he could have conceived them; however, desirous of

[057-007]doing what his task imposed upon him, he was unwilling to leave it

[057-008]untranslated, and therefore he went on to say:

[057-009]Sancho came home in such glee and spirits that his wife noticed his

[057-010]happiness a bowshot off, so much so that it made her ask him, “What

[057-011]have you got, Sancho friend, that you are so glad?”

[057-012]To which he replied, “Wife, if it were God’s will, I should be very

[057-013]glad not to be so well pleased as I show myself.”

[057-014]“I don’t understand you, husband,” said she, “and I don’t know what you

[057-015]mean by saying you would be glad, if it were God’s will, not to be well

[057-016]pleased; for, fool as I am, I don’t know how one can find pleasure in

[057-017]not having it.”

[057-018]“Hark ye, Teresa,” replied Sancho, “I am glad because I have made up my

[057-019]mind to go back to the service of my master Don Quixote, who means to

[057-020]go out a third time to seek for adventures; and I am going with him

[057-021]again, for my necessities will have it so, and also the hope that

[057-022]cheers me with the thought that I may find another hundred crowns like

[057-023]those we have spent; though it makes me sad to have to leave thee and

[057-024]the children; and if God would be pleased to let me have my daily

[057-025]bread, dry-shod and at home, without taking me out into the byways and

[057-026]cross-roads—and he could do it at small cost by merely willing it—it is

[057-027]clear my happiness would be more solid and lasting, for the happiness I

[057-028]have is mingled with sorrow at leaving thee; so that I was right in

[057-029]saying I would be glad, if it were God’s will, not to be well pleased.”

[057-030]“Look here, Sancho,” said Teresa; “ever since you joined on to a

[057-031]knight-errant you talk in such a roundabout way that there is no

[057-032]understanding you.”

[057-033]“It is enough that God understands me, wife,” replied Sancho; “for he

[057-034]is the understander of all things; that will do; but mind, sister, you

[057-035]must look to Dapple carefully for the next three days, so that he may

[057-036]be fit to take arms; double his feed, and see to the pack-saddle and

[057-037]other harness, for it is not to a wedding we are bound, but to go round

[057-038]the world, and play at give and take with giants and dragons and

[057-039]monsters, and hear hissings and roarings and bellowings and howlings;

[057-040]and even all this would be lavender, if we had not to reckon with

[057-041]Yanguesans and enchanted Moors.”

[057-042]“I know well enough, husband,” said Teresa, “that squires-errant don’t

[057-043]eat their bread for nothing, and so I will be always praying to our

[057-044]Lord to deliver you speedily from all that hard fortune.”

[057-045]“I can tell you, wife,” said Sancho, “if I did not expect to see myself

[057-046]governor of an island before long, I would drop down dead on the spot.”

[057-047]“Nay, then, husband,” said Teresa; “let the hen live, though it be with

[057-048]her pip, live, and let the devil take all the governments in the world;

[057-049]you came out of your mother’s womb without a government, you have lived

[057-050]until now without a government, and when it is God’s will you will go,

[057-051]or be carried, to your grave without a government. How many there are

[057-052]in the world who live without a government, and continue to live all

[057-053]the same, and are reckoned in the number of the people. The best sauce

[057-054]in the world is hunger, and as the poor are never without that, they

[057-055]always eat with a relish. But mind, Sancho, if by good luck you should

[057-056]find yourself with some government, don’t forget me and your children.

[057-057]Remember that Sanchico is now full fifteen, and it is right he should

[057-058]go to school, if his uncle the abbot has a mind to have him trained for

[057-059]the Church. Consider, too, that your daughter Mari-Sancha will not die

[057-060]of grief if we marry her; for I have my suspicions that she is as eager

[057-061]to get a husband as you to get a government; and, after all, a daughter

[057-062]looks better ill married than well whored.”

[057-063]“By my faith,” replied Sancho, “if God brings me to get any sort of a

[057-064]government, I intend, wife, to make such a high match for Mari-Sancha

[057-065]that there will be no approaching her without calling her ‘my lady.”

[057-066]“Nay, Sancho,” returned Teresa; “marry her to her equal, that is the

[057-067]safest plan; for if you put her out of wooden clogs into high-heeled

[057-068]shoes, out of her grey flannel petticoat into hoops and silk gowns, out

[057-069]of the plain ‘Marica’ and ‘thou,’ into ‘Doña So-and-so’ and ‘my lady,’

[057-070]the girl won’t know where she is, and at every turn she will fall into

[057-071]a thousand blunders that will show the thread of her coarse homespun

[057-072]stuff.”

[057-073]“Tut, you fool,” said Sancho; “it will be only to practise it for two

[057-074]or three years; and then dignity and decorum will fit her as easily as

[057-075]a glove; and if not, what matter? Let her be ‘my lady,’ and never mind

[057-076]what happens.”

[057-077]“Keep to your own station, Sancho,” replied Teresa; “don’t try to raise

[057-078]yourself higher, and bear in mind the proverb that says, ‘wipe the nose

[057-079]of your neigbbour’s son, and take him into your house.’ A fine thing it

[057-080]would be, indeed, to marry our Maria to some great count or grand

[057-081]gentleman, who, when the humour took him, would abuse her and call her

[057-082]clown-bred and clodhopper’s daughter and spinning wench. I have not

[057-083]been bringing up my daughter for that all this time, I can tell you,

[057-084]husband. Do you bring home money, Sancho, and leave marrying her to my

[057-085]care; there is Lope Tocho, Juan Tocho’s son, a stout, sturdy young

[057-086]fellow that we know, and I can see he does not look sour at the girl;

[057-087]and with him, one of our own sort, she will be well married, and we

[057-088]shall have her always under our eyes, and be all one family, parents

[057-089]and children, grandchildren and sons-in-law, and the peace and blessing

[057-090]of God will dwell among us; so don’t you go marrying her in those

[057-091]courts and grand palaces where they won’t know what to make of her, or

[057-092]she what to make of herself.”

[057-093]“Why, you idiot and wife for Barabbas,” said Sancho, “what do you mean

[057-094]by trying, without why or wherefore, to keep me from marrying my

[057-095]daughter to one who will give me grandchildren that will be called

[057-096]‘your lordship’? Look ye, Teresa, I have always heard my elders say

[057-097]that he who does not know how to take advantage of luck when it comes

[057-098]to him, has no right to complain if it gives him the go-by; and now

[057-099]that it is knocking at our door, it will not do to shut it out; let us

[057-100]go with the favouring breeze that blows upon us.”

[057-101]It is this sort of talk, and what Sancho says lower down, that made the

[057-102]translator of the history say he considered this chapter apocryphal.

[057-103]“Don’t you see, you animal,” continued Sancho, “that it will be well

[057-104]for me to drop into some profitable government that will lift us out of

[057-105]the mire, and marry Mari-Sancha to whom I like; and you yourself will

[057-106]find yourself called ‘Doña Teresa Panza,’ and sitting in church on a

[057-107]fine carpet and cushions and draperies, in spite and in defiance of all

[057-108]the born ladies of the town? No, stay as you are, growing neither

[057-109]greater nor less, like a tapestry figure—Let us say no more about it,

[057-110]for Sanchica shall be a countess, say what you will.”

[057-111]“Are you sure of all you say, husband?” replied Teresa. “Well, for all

[057-112]that, I am afraid this rank of countess for my daughter will be her

[057-113]ruin. You do as you like, make a duchess or a princess of her, but I

[057-114]can tell you it will not be with my will and consent. I was always a

[057-115]lover of equality, brother, and I can’t bear to see people give

[057-116]themselves airs without any right. They called me Teresa at my baptism,

[057-117]a plain, simple name, without any additions or tags or fringes of Dons

[057-118]or Doñas; Cascajo was my father’s name, and as I am your wife, I am

[057-119]called Teresa Panza, though by right I ought to be called Teresa

[057-120]Cascajo; but ‘kings go where laws like,’ and I am content with this

[057-121]name without having the ‘Don’ put on top of it to make it so heavy that

[057-122]I cannot carry it; and I don’t want to make people talk about me when

[057-123]they see me go dressed like a countess or governor’s wife; for they

[057-124]will say at once, ‘See what airs the slut gives herself! Only yesterday

[057-125]she was always spinning flax, and used to go to mass with the tail of

[057-126]her petticoat over her head instead of a mantle, and there she goes

[057-127]to-day in a hooped gown with her broaches and airs, as if we didn’t

[057-128]know her!’ If God keeps me in my seven senses, or five, or whatever

[057-129]number I have, I am not going to bring myself to such a pass; go you,

[057-130]brother, and be a government or an island man, and swagger as much as

[057-131]you like; for by the soul of my mother, neither my daughter nor I are

[057-132]going to stir a step from our village; a respectable woman should have

[057-133]a broken leg and keep at home; and to be busy at something is a

[057-134]virtuous damsel’s holiday; be off to your adventures along with your

[057-135]Don Quixote, and leave us to our misadventures, for God will mend them

[057-136]for us according as we deserve it. I don’t know, I’m sure, who fixed

[057-137]the ‘Don’ to him, what neither his father nor grandfather ever had.”

[057-138]“I declare thou hast a devil of some sort in thy body!” said Sancho.

[057-139]“God help thee, what a lot of things thou hast strung together, one

[057-140]after the other, without head or tail! What have Cascajo, and the

[057-141]broaches and the proverbs and the airs, to do with what I say? Look

[057-142]here, fool and dolt (for so I may call you, when you don’t understand

[057-143]my words, and run away from good fortune), if I had said that my

[057-144]daughter was to throw herself down from a tower, or go roaming the

[057-145]world, as the Infanta Doña Urraca wanted to do, you would be right in

[057-146]not giving way to my will; but if in an instant, in less than the

[057-147]twinkling of an eye, I put the ‘Don’ and ‘my lady’ on her back, and

[057-148]take her out of the stubble, and place her under a canopy, on a dais,

[057-149]and on a couch, with more velvet cushions than all the Almohades of

[057-150]Morocco ever had in their family, why won’t you consent and fall in

[057-151]with my wishes?”

[057-152]“Do you know why, husband?” replied Teresa; “because of the proverb

[057-153]that says ‘who covers thee, discovers thee.’ At the poor man people

[057-154]only throw a hasty glance; on the rich man they fix their eyes; and if

[057-155]the said rich man was once on a time poor, it is then there is the

[057-156]sneering and the tattle and spite of backbiters; and in the streets

[057-157]here they swarm as thick as bees.”

[057-158]“Look here, Teresa,” said Sancho, “and listen to what I am now going to

[057-159]say to you; maybe you never heard it in all your life; and I do not

[057-160]give my own notions, for what I am about to say are the opinions of his

[057-161]reverence the preacher, who preached in this town last Lent, and who

[057-162]said, if I remember rightly, that all things present that our eyes

[057-163]behold, bring themselves before us, and remain and fix themselves on

[057-164]our memory much better and more forcibly than things past.”

[057-165]These observations which Sancho makes here are the other ones on

[057-166]account of which the translator says he regards this chapter as

[057-167]apocryphal, inasmuch as they are beyond Sancho’s capacity.

[057-168]“Whence it arises,” he continued, “that when we see any person well

[057-169]dressed and making a figure with rich garments and retinue of servants,

[057-170]it seems to lead and impel us perforce to respect him, though memory

[057-171]may at the same moment recall to us some lowly condition in which we

[057-172]have seen him, but which, whether it may have been poverty or low

[057-173]birth, being now a thing of the past, has no existence; while the only

[057-174]thing that has any existence is what we see before us; and if this

[057-175]person whom fortune has raised from his original lowly state (these

[057-176]were the very words the padre used) to his present height of

[057-177]prosperity, be well bred, generous, courteous to all, without seeking

[057-178]to vie with those whose nobility is of ancient date, depend upon it,

[057-179]Teresa, no one will remember what he was, and everyone will respect

[057-180]what he is, except indeed the envious, from whom no fair fortune is

[057-181]safe.”

[057-182]“I do not understand you, husband,” replied Teresa; “do as you like,

[057-183]and don’t break my head with any more speechifying and rethoric; and if

[057-184]you have revolved to do what you say—”

[057-185]“Resolved, you should say, woman,” said Sancho, “not revolved.”

[057-186]“Don’t set yourself to wrangle with me, husband,” said Teresa; “I speak

[057-187]as God pleases, and don’t deal in out-of-the-way phrases; and I say if

[057-188]you are bent upon having a government, take your son Sancho with you,

[057-189]and teach him from this time on how to hold a government; for sons

[057-190]ought to inherit and learn the trades of their fathers.”

[057-191]“As soon as I have the government,” said Sancho, “I will send for him

[057-192]by post, and I will send thee money, of which I shall have no lack, for

[057-193]there is never any want of people to lend it to governors when they

[057-194]have not got it; and do thou dress him so as to hide what he is and

[057-195]make him look what he is to be.”

[057-196]“You send the money,” said Teresa, “and I’ll dress him up for you as

[057-197]fine as you please.”

[057-198]“Then we are agreed that our daughter is to be a countess,” said

[057-199]Sancho.

[057-200]“The day that I see her a countess,” replied Teresa, “it will be the

[057-201]same to me as if I was burying her; but once more I say do as you

[057-202]please, for we women are born to this burden of being obedient to our

[057-203]husbands, though they be dogs;” and with this she began to weep in

[057-204]earnest, as if she already saw Sanchica dead and buried.

[057-205]Sancho consoled her by saying that though he must make her a countess,

[057-206]he would put it off as long as possible. Here their conversation came

[057-207]to an end, and Sancho went back to see Don Quixote, and make

[057-208]arrangements for their departure.
