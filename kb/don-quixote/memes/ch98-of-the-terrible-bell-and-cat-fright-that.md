OF THE TERRIBLE BELL AND CAT FRIGHT THAT DON QUIXOTE GOT IN THE COURSE

[098-001]OF THE ENAMOURED ALTISIDORA’S WOOING

p46a.jpg (58K)


[098-002]We left Don Quixote wrapped up in the reflections which the music of

[098-003]the enamourned maid Altisidora had given rise to. He went to bed with

[098-004]them, and just like fleas they would not let him sleep or get a

[098-005]moment’s rest, and the broken stitches of his stockings helped them.

[098-006]But as Time is fleet and no obstacle can stay his course, he came

[098-007]riding on the hours, and morning very soon arrived. Seeing which Don

[098-008]Quixote quitted the soft down, and, nowise slothful, dressed himself in

[098-009]his chamois suit and put on his travelling boots to hide the disaster

[098-010]to his stockings. He threw over him his scarlet mantle, put on his head

[098-011]a montera of green velvet trimmed with silver edging, flung across his

[098-012]shoulder the baldric with his good trenchant sword, took up a large

[098-013]rosary that he always carried with him, and with great solemnity and

[098-014]precision of gait proceeded to the antechamber where the duke and

[098-015]duchess were already dressed and waiting for him. But as he passed

[098-016]through a gallery, Altisidora and the other damsel, her friend, were

[098-017]lying in wait for him, and the instant Altisidora saw him she pretended

[098-018]to faint, while her friend caught her in her lap, and began hastily

[098-019]unlacing the bosom of her dress.

[098-020]Don Quixote observed it, and approaching them said, “I know very well

[098-021]what this seizure arises from.”

[098-022]“I know not from what,” replied the friend, “for Altisidora is the

[098-023]healthiest damsel in all this house, and I have never heard her

[098-024]complain all the time I have known her. A plague on all the

[098-025]knights-errant in the world, if they be all ungrateful! Go away, Señor

[098-026]Don Quixote; for this poor child will not come to herself again so long

[098-027]as you are here.”

p46b.jpg (320K)


[098-028]To which Don Quixote returned, “Do me the favour, señora, to let a lute

[098-029]be placed in my chamber to-night; and I will comfort this poor maiden

[098-030]to the best of my power; for in the early stages of love a prompt

[098-031]disillusion is an approved remedy;” and with this he retired, so as not

[098-032]to be remarked by any who might see him there.

[098-033]He had scarcely withdrawn when Altisidora, recovering from her swoon,

[098-034]said to her companion, “The lute must be left, for no doubt Don Quixote

[098-035]intends to give us some music; and being his it will not be bad.”

[098-036]They went at once to inform the duchess of what was going on, and of

[098-037]the lute Don Quixote asked for, and she, delighted beyond measure,

[098-038]plotted with the duke and her two damsels to play him a trick that

[098-039]should be amusing but harmless; and in high glee they waited for night,

[098-040]which came quickly as the day had come; and as for the day, the duke

[098-041]and duchess spent it in charming conversation with Don Quixote.

[098-042]When eleven o’clock came, Don Quixote found a guitar in his chamber; he

[098-043]tried it, opened the window, and perceived that some persons were

[098-044]walking in the garden; and having passed his fingers over the frets of

[098-045]the guitar and tuned it as well as he could, he spat and cleared his

[098-046]chest, and then with a voice a little hoarse but full-toned, he sang

[098-047]the following ballad, which he had himself that day composed:

[098-048]Mighty Love the hearts of maidens

[098-049]Doth unsettle and perplex,

[098-050]And the instrument he uses

[098-051]Most of all is idleness.

[098-052]Sewing, stitching, any labour,

[098-053]Having always work to do,

[098-054]To the poison Love instilleth

[098-055]Is the antidote most sure.

[098-056]And to proper-minded maidens

[098-057]Who desire the matron’s name

[098-058]Modesty’s a marriage portion,

[098-059]Modesty their highest praise.

[098-060]Men of prudence and discretion,

[098-061]Courtiers gay and gallant knights,

[098-062]With the wanton damsels dally,

[098-063]But the modest take to wife.

[098-064]There are passions, transient, fleeting,

[098-065]Loves in hostelries declar’d,

[098-066]Sunrise loves, with sunset ended,

[098-067]When the guest hath gone his way.

[098-068]Love that springs up swift and sudden,

[098-069]Here to-day, to-morrow flown,

[098-070]Passes, leaves no trace behind it,

[098-071]Leaves no image on the soul.

[098-072]Painting that is laid on painting

[098-073]Maketh no display or show;

[098-074]Where one beauty’s in possession

[098-075]There no other can take hold.

[098-076]Dulcinea del Toboso

[098-077]Painted on my heart I wear;

[098-078]Never from its tablets, never,

[098-079]Can her image be eras’d.

[098-080]The quality of all in lovers

[098-081]Most esteemed is constancy;

[098-082]’Tis by this that love works wonders,

[098-083]This exalts them to the skies.

[098-084]Don Quixote had got so far with his song, to which the duke, the

[098-085]duchess, Altisidora, and nearly the whole household of the castle were

[098-086]listening, when all of a sudden from a gallery above that was exactly

[098-087]over his window they let down a cord with more than a hundred bells

[098-088]attached to it, and immediately after that discharged a great sack full

[098-089]of cats, which also had bells of smaller size tied to their tails. Such

[098-090]was the din of the bells and the squalling of the cats, that though the

[098-091]duke and duchess were the contrivers of the joke they were startled by

[098-092]it, while Don Quixote stood paralysed with fear; and as luck would have

[098-093]it, two or three of the cats made their way in through the grating of

[098-094]his chamber, and flying from one side to the other, made it seem as if

[098-095]there was a legion of devils at large in it. They extinguished the

[098-096]candles that were burning in the room, and rushed about seeking some

[098-097]way of escape; the cord with the large bells never ceased rising and

[098-098]falling; and most of the people of the castle, not knowing what was

[098-099]really the matter, were at their wits’ end with astonishment. Don

[098-100]Quixote sprang to his feet, and drawing his sword, began making passes

[098-101]at the grating, shouting out, “Avaunt, malignant enchanters! avaunt, ye

[098-102]witchcraft-working rabble! I am Don Quixote of La Mancha, against whom

[098-103]your evil machinations avail not nor have any power.” And turning upon

[098-104]the cats that were running about the room, he made several cuts at

[098-105]them. They dashed at the grating and escaped by it, save one that,

[098-106]finding itself hard pressed by the slashes of Don Quixote’s sword, flew

[098-107]at his face and held on to his nose tooth and nail, with the pain of

[098-108]which he began to shout his loudest. The duke and duchess hearing this,

[098-109]and guessing what it was, ran with all haste to his room, and as the

[098-110]poor gentleman was striving with all his might to detach the cat from

[098-111]his face, they opened the door with a master-key and went in with

[098-112]lights and witnessed the unequal combat. The duke ran forward to part

[098-113]the combatants, but Don Quixote cried out aloud, “Let no one take him

[098-114]from me; leave me hand to hand with this demon, this wizard, this

[098-115]enchanter; I will teach him, I myself, who Don Quixote of La Mancha

[098-116]is.” The cat, however, never minding these threats, snarled and held

[098-117]on; but at last the duke pulled it off and flung it out of the window.

[098-118]Don Quixote was left with a face as full of holes as a sieve and a nose

[098-119]not in very good condition, and greatly vexed that they did not let him

[098-120]finish the battle he had been so stoutly fighting with that villain of

[098-121]an enchanter. They sent for some oil of John’s wort, and Altisidora

[098-122]herself with her own fair hands bandaged all the wounded parts; and as

[098-123]she did so she said to him in a low voice. “All these mishaps have

[098-124]befallen thee, hardhearted knight, for the sin of thy insensibility and

[098-125]obstinacy; and God grant thy squire Sancho may forget to whip himself,

[098-126]so that that dearly beloved Dulcinea of thine may never be released

[098-127]from her enchantment, that thou mayest never come to her bed, at least

[098-128]while I who adore thee am alive.”

[098-129]To all this Don Quixote made no answer except to heave deep sighs, and

[098-130]then stretched himself on his bed, thanking the duke and duchess for

[098-131]their kindness, not because he stood in any fear of that bell-ringing

[098-132]rabble of enchanters in cat shape, but because he recognised their good

[098-133]intentions in coming to his rescue. The duke and duchess left him to

[098-134]repose and withdrew greatly grieved at the unfortunate result of the

[098-135]joke; as they never thought the adventure would have fallen so heavy on

[098-136]Don Quixote or cost him so dear, for it cost him five days of

[098-137]confinement to his bed, during which he had another adventure,

[098-138]pleasanter than the late one, which his chronicler will not relate just

[098-139]now in order that he may turn his attention to Sancho Panza, who was

[098-140]proceeding with great diligence and drollery in his government.
