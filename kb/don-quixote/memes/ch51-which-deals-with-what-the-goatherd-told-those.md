WHICH DEALS WITH WHAT THE GOATHERD TOLD THOSE WHO WERE CARRYING OFF DON

[051-001]Three leagues from this valley there is a village which, though small,

[051-002]is one of the richest in all this neighbourhood, and in it there lived

[051-003]a farmer, a very worthy man, and so much respected that, although to be

[051-004]so is the natural consequence of being rich, he was even more respected

[051-005]for his virtue than for the wealth he had acquired. But what made him

[051-006]still more fortunate, as he said himself, was having a daughter of such

[051-007]exceeding beauty, rare intelligence, gracefulness, and virtue, that

[051-008]everyone who knew her and beheld her marvelled at the extraordinary

[051-009]gifts with which heaven and nature had endowed her. As a child she was

[051-010]beautiful, she continued to grow in beauty, and at the age of sixteen

[051-011]she was most lovely. The fame of her beauty began to spread abroad

[051-012]through all the villages around—but why do I say the villages around,

[051-013]merely, when it spread to distant cities, and even made its way into

[051-014]the halls of royalty and reached the ears of people of every class, who

[051-015]came from all sides to see her as if to see something rare and curious,

[051-016]or some wonder-working image?

[051-017]Her father watched over her and she watched over herself; for there are

[051-018]no locks, or guards, or bolts that can protect a young girl better than

[051-019]her own modesty. The wealth of the father and the beauty of the

[051-020]daughter led many neighbours as well as strangers to seek her for a

[051-021]wife; but he, as one might well be who had the disposal of so rich a

[051-022]jewel, was perplexed and unable to make up his mind to which of her

[051-023]countless suitors he should entrust her. I was one among the many who

[051-024]felt a desire so natural, and, as her father knew who I was, and I was

[051-025]of the same town, of pure blood, in the bloom of life, and very rich in

[051-026]possessions, I had great hopes of success. There was another of the

[051-027]same place and qualifications who also sought her, and this made her

[051-028]father’s choice hang in the balance, for he felt that on either of us

[051-029]his daughter would be well bestowed; so to escape from this state of

[051-030]perplexity he resolved to refer the matter to Leandra (for that is the

[051-031]name of the rich damsel who has reduced me to misery), reflecting that

[051-032]as we were both equal it would be best to leave it to his dear daughter

[051-033]to choose according to her inclination—a course that is worthy of

[051-034]imitation by all fathers who wish to settle their children in life. I

[051-035]do not mean that they ought to leave them to make a choice of what is

[051-036]contemptible and bad, but that they should place before them what is

[051-037]good and then allow them to make a good choice as they please. I do not

[051-038]know which Leandra chose; I only know her father put us both off with

[051-039]the tender age of his daughter and vague words that neither bound him

[051-040]nor dismissed us. My rival is called Anselmo and I myself Eugenio—that

[051-041]you may know the names of the personages that figure in this tragedy,

[051-042]the end of which is still in suspense, though it is plain to see it

[051-043]must be disastrous.

[051-044]About this time there arrived in our town one Vicente de la Roca, the

[051-045]son of a poor peasant of the same town, the said Vicente having

[051-046]returned from service as a soldier in Italy and divers other parts. A

[051-047]captain who chanced to pass that way with his company had carried him

[051-048]off from our village when he was a boy of about twelve years, and now

[051-049]twelve years later the young man came back in a soldier’s uniform,

[051-050]arrayed in a thousand colours, and all over glass trinkets and fine

[051-051]steel chains. To-day he would appear in one gay dress, to-morrow in

[051-052]another; but all flimsy and gaudy, of little substance and less worth.

[051-053]The peasant folk, who are naturally malicious, and when they have

[051-054]nothing to do can be malice itself, remarked all this, and took note of

[051-055]his finery and jewellery, piece by piece, and discovered that he had

[051-056]three suits of different colours, with garters and stockings to match;

[051-057]but he made so many arrangements and combinations out of them, that if

[051-058]they had not counted them, anyone would have sworn that he had made a

[051-059]display of more than ten suits of clothes and twenty plumes. Do not

[051-060]look upon all this that I am telling you about the clothes as uncalled

[051-061]for or spun out, for they have a great deal to do with the story. He

[051-062]used to seat himself on a bench under the great poplar in our plaza,

[051-063]and there he would keep us all hanging open-mouthed on the stories he

[051-064]told us of his exploits. There was no country on the face of the globe

[051-065]he had not seen, nor battle he had not been engaged in; he had killed

[051-066]more Moors than there are in Morocco and Tunis, and fought more single

[051-067]combats, according to his own account, than Garcilaso, Diego García de

[051-068]Paredes and a thousand others he named, and out of all he had come

[051-069]victorious without losing a drop of blood. On the other hand he showed

[051-070]marks of wounds, which, though they could not be made out, he said were

[051-071]gunshot wounds received in divers encounters and actions. Lastly, with

[051-072]monstrous impudence he used to say “you” to his equals and even those

[051-073]who knew what he was, and declare that his arm was his father and his

[051-074]deeds his pedigree, and that being a soldier he was as good as the king

[051-075]himself. And to add to these swaggering ways he was a trifle of a

[051-076]musician, and played the guitar with such a flourish that some said he

[051-077]made it speak; nor did his accomplishments end here, for he was

[051-078]something of a poet too, and on every trifle that happened in the town

[051-079]he made a ballad a league long.

[051-080]This soldier, then, that I have described, this Vicente de la Roca,

[051-081]this bravo, gallant, musician, poet, was often seen and watched by

[051-082]Leandra from a window of her house which looked out on the plaza. The

[051-083]glitter of his showy attire took her fancy, his ballads bewitched her

[051-084](for he gave away twenty copies of every one he made), the tales of his

[051-085]exploits which he told about himself came to her ears; and in short, as

[051-086]the devil no doubt had arranged it, she fell in love with him before

[051-087]the presumption of making love to her had suggested itself to him; and

[051-088]as in love-affairs none are more easily brought to an issue than those

[051-089]which have the inclination of the lady for an ally, Leandra and Vicente

[051-090]came to an understanding without any difficulty; and before any of her

[051-091]numerous suitors had any suspicion of her design, she had already

[051-092]carried it into effect, having left the house of her dearly beloved

[051-093]father (for mother she had none), and disappeared from the village with

[051-094]the soldier, who came more triumphantly out of this enterprise than out

[051-095]of any of the large number he laid claim to. All the village and all

[051-096]who heard of it were amazed at the affair; I was aghast, Anselmo

[051-097]thunderstruck, her father full of grief, her relations indignant, the

[051-098]authorities all in a ferment, the officers of the Brotherhood in arms.

[051-099]They scoured the roads, they searched the woods and all quarters, and

[051-100]at the end of three days they found the flighty Leandra in a mountain

[051-101]cave, stript to her shift, and robbed of all the money and precious

[051-102]jewels she had carried away from home with her.

[051-103]They brought her back to her unhappy father, and questioned her as to

[051-104]her misfortune, and she confessed without pressure that Vicente de la

[051-105]Roca had deceived her, and under promise of marrying her had induced

[051-106]her to leave her father’s house, as he meant to take her to the richest

[051-107]and most delightful city in the whole world, which was Naples; and that

[051-108]she, ill-advised and deluded, had believed him, and robbed her father,

[051-109]and handed over all to him the night she disappeared; and that he had

[051-110]carried her away to a rugged mountain and shut her up in the cave where

[051-111]they had found her. She said, moreover, that the soldier, without

[051-112]robbing her of her honour, had taken from her everything she had, and

[051-113]made off, leaving her in the cave, a thing that still further surprised

[051-114]everybody. It was not easy for us to credit the young man’s continence,

[051-115]but she asserted it with such earnestness that it helped to console her

[051-116]distressed father, who thought nothing of what had been taken since the

[051-117]jewel that once lost can never be recovered had been left to his

[051-118]daughter. The same day that Leandra made her appearance her father

[051-119]removed her from our sight and took her away to shut her up in a

[051-120]convent in a town near this, in the hope that time may wear away some

[051-121]of the disgrace she has incurred. Leandra’s youth furnished an excuse

[051-122]for her fault, at least with those to whom it was of no consequence

[051-123]whether she was good or bad; but those who knew her shrewdness and

[051-124]intelligence did not attribute her misdemeanour to ignorance but to

[051-125]wantonness and the natural disposition of women, which is for the most

[051-126]part flighty and ill-regulated.

[051-127]Leandra withdrawn from sight, Anselmo’s eyes grew blind, or at any rate

[051-128]found nothing to look at that gave them any pleasure, and mine were in

[051-129]darkness without a ray of light to direct them to anything enjoyable

[051-130]while Leandra was away. Our melancholy grew greater, our patience grew

[051-131]less; we cursed the soldier’s finery and railed at the carelessness of

[051-132]Leandra’s father. At last Anselmo and I agreed to leave the village and

[051-133]come to this valley; and, he feeding a great flock of sheep of his own,

[051-134]and I a large herd of goats of mine, we pass our life among the trees,

[051-135]giving vent to our sorrows, together singing the fair Leandra’s

[051-136]praises, or upbraiding her, or else sighing alone, and to heaven

[051-137]pouring forth our complaints in solitude. Following our example, many

[051-138]more of Leandra’s lovers have come to these rude mountains and adopted

[051-139]our mode of life, and they are so numerous that one would fancy the

[051-140]place had been turned into the pastoral Arcadia, so full is it of

[051-141]shepherds and sheep-folds; nor is there a spot in it where the name of

[051-142]the fair Leandra is not heard. Here one curses her and calls her

[051-143]capricious, fickle, and immodest, there another condemns her as frail

[051-144]and frivolous; this pardons and absolves her, that spurns and reviles

[051-145]her; one extols her beauty, another assails her character, and in short

[051-146]all abuse her, and all adore her, and to such a pitch has this general

[051-147]infatuation gone that there are some who complain of her scorn without

[051-148]ever having exchanged a word with her, and even some that bewail and

[051-149]mourn the raging fever of jealousy, for which she never gave anyone

[051-150]cause, for, as I have already said, her misconduct was known before her

[051-151]passion. There is no nook among the rocks, no brookside, no shade

[051-152]beneath the trees that is not haunted by some shepherd telling his woes

[051-153]to the breezes; wherever there is an echo it repeats the name of

[051-154]Leandra; the mountains ring with “Leandra,” “Leandra” murmur the

[051-155]brooks, and Leandra keeps us all bewildered and bewitched, hoping

[051-156]without hope and fearing without knowing what we fear. Of all this

[051-157]silly set the one that shows the least and also the most sense is my

[051-158]rival Anselmo, for having so many other things to complain of, he only

[051-159]complains of separation, and to the accompaniment of a rebeck, which he

[051-160]plays admirably, he sings his complaints in verses that show his

[051-161]ingenuity. I follow another, easier, and to my mind wiser course, and

[051-162]that is to rail at the frivolity of women, at their inconstancy, their

[051-163]double dealing, their broken promises, their unkept pledges, and in

[051-164]short the want of reflection they show in fixing their affections and

[051-165]inclinations. This, sirs, was the reason of words and expressions I

[051-166]made use of to this goat when I came up just now; for as she is a

[051-167]female I have a contempt for her, though she is the best in all my

[051-168]fold. This is the story I promised to tell you, and if I have been

[051-169]tedious in telling it, I will not be slow to serve you; my hut is close

[051-170]by, and I have fresh milk and dainty cheese there, as well as a variety

[051-171]of toothsome fruit, no less pleasing to the eye than to the palate.
