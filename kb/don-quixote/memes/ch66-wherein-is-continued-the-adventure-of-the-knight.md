WHEREIN IS CONTINUED THE ADVENTURE OF THE KNIGHT OF THE GROVE

p14a.jpg (120K)


[066-001]Among the things that passed between Don Quixote and the Knight of the

[066-002]Wood, the history tells us he of the Grove said to Don Quixote, “In

[066-003]fine, sir knight, I would have you know that my destiny, or, more

[066-004]properly speaking, my choice led me to fall in love with the peerless

[066-005]Casildea de Vandalia. I call her peerless because she has no peer,

[066-006]whether it be in bodily stature or in the supremacy of rank and beauty.

[066-007]This same Casildea, then, that I speak of, requited my honourable

[066-008]passion and gentle aspirations by compelling me, as his stepmother did

[066-009]Hercules, to engage in many perils of various sorts, at the end of each

[066-010]promising me that, with the end of the next, the object of my hopes

[066-011]should be attained; but my labours have gone on increasing link by link

[066-012]until they are past counting, nor do I know what will be the last one

[066-013]that is to be the beginning of the accomplishment of my chaste desires.

[066-014]On one occasion she bade me go and challenge the famous giantess of

[066-015]Seville, La Giralda by name, who is as mighty and strong as if made of

[066-016]brass, and though never stirring from one spot, is the most restless

[066-017]and changeable woman in the world. I came, I saw, I conquered, and I

[066-018]made her stay quiet and behave herself, for nothing but north winds

[066-019]blew for more than a week. Another time I was ordered to lift those

[066-020]ancient stones, the mighty bulls of Guisando, an enterprise that might

[066-021]more fitly be entrusted to porters than to knights. Again, she bade me

[066-022]fling myself into the cavern of Cabra—an unparalleled and awful

[066-023]peril—and bring her a minute account of all that is concealed in those

[066-024]gloomy depths. I stopped the motion of the Giralda, I lifted the bulls

[066-025]of Guisando, I flung myself into the cavern and brought to light the

[066-026]secrets of its abyss; and my hopes are as dead as dead can be, and her

[066-027]scorn and her commands as lively as ever. To be brief, last of all she

[066-028]has commanded me to go through all the provinces of Spain and compel

[066-029]all the knights-errant wandering therein to confess that she surpasses

[066-030]all women alive to-day in beauty, and that I am the most valiant and

[066-031]the most deeply enamoured knight on earth; in support of which claim I

[066-032]have already travelled over the greater part of Spain, and have there

[066-033]vanquished several knights who have dared to contradict me; but what I

[066-034]most plume and pride myself upon is having vanquished in single combat

[066-035]that so famous knight Don Quixote of La Mancha, and made him confess

[066-036]that my Casildea is more beautiful than his Dulcinea; and in this one

[066-037]victory I hold myself to have conquered all the knights in the world;

[066-038]for this Don Quixote that I speak of has vanquished them all, and I

[066-039]having vanquished him, his glory, his fame, and his honour have passed

[066-040]and are transferred to my person; for

[066-041]The more the vanquished hath of fair renown,

[066-042]The greater glory gilds the victor’s crown.

[066-043]Thus the innumerable achievements of the said Don Quixote are now set

[066-044]down to my account and have become mine.”

[066-045]Don Quixote was amazed when he heard the Knight of the Grove, and was a

[066-046]thousand times on the point of telling him he lied, and had the lie

[066-047]direct already on the tip of his tongue; but he restrained himself as

[066-048]well as he could, in order to force him to confess the lie with his own

[066-049]lips; so he said to him quietly, “As to what you say, sir knight, about

[066-050]having vanquished most of the knights of Spain, or even of the whole

[066-051]world, I say nothing; but that you have vanquished Don Quixote of La

[066-052]Mancha I consider doubtful; it may have been some other that resembled

[066-053]him, although there are few like him.”

[066-054]“How! not vanquished?” said he of the Grove; “by the heaven that is

[066-055]above us I fought Don Quixote and overcame him and made him yield; and

[066-056]he is a man of tall stature, gaunt features, long, lank limbs, with

[066-057]hair turning grey, an aquiline nose rather hooked, and large black

[066-058]drooping moustaches; he does battle under the name of ‘The

[066-059]Countenance,’ and he has for squire a peasant called Sancho Panza; he

[066-060]presses the loins and rules the reins of a famous steed called

[066-061]Rocinante; and lastly, he has for the mistress of his will a certain

[066-062]Dulcinea del Toboso, once upon a time called Aldonza Lorenzo, just as I

[066-063]call mine Casildea de Vandalia because her name is Casilda and she is

[066-064]of Andalusia. If all these tokens are not enough to vindicate the truth

[066-065]of what I say, here is my sword, that will compel incredulity itself to

[066-066]give credence to it.”

[066-067]“Calm yourself, sir knight,” said Don Quixote, “and give ear to what I

[066-068]am about to say to you. I would have you know that this Don Quixote you

[066-069]speak of is the greatest friend I have in the world; so much so that I

[066-070]may say I regard him in the same light as my own person; and from the

[066-071]precise and clear indications you have given I cannot but think that he

[066-072]must be the very one you have vanquished. On the other hand, I see with

[066-073]my eyes and feel with my hands that it is impossible it can have been

[066-074]the same; unless indeed it be that, as he has many enemies who are

[066-075]enchanters, and one in particular who is always persecuting him,

[066-076]someone of these may have taken his shape in order to allow himself to

[066-077]be vanquished, so as to defraud him of the fame that his exalted

[066-078]achievements as a knight have earned and acquired for him throughout

[066-079]the known world. And in confirmation of this, I must tell you, too,

[066-080]that it is but ten hours since these said enchanters his enemies

[066-081]transformed the shape and person of the fair Dulcinea del Toboso into a

[066-082]foul and mean village lass, and in the same way they must have

[066-083]transformed Don Quixote; and if all this does not suffice to convince

[066-084]you of the truth of what I say, here is Don Quixote himself, who will

[066-085]maintain it by arms, on foot or on horseback or in any way you please.”

[066-086]And so saying he stood up and laid his hand on his sword, waiting to

[066-087]see what the Knight of the Grove would do, who in an equally calm voice

[066-088]said in reply, “Pledges don’t distress a good payer; he who has

[066-089]succeeded in vanquishing you once when transformed, Sir Don Quixote,

[066-090]may fairly hope to subdue you in your own proper shape; but as it is

[066-091]not becoming for knights to perform their feats of arms in the dark,

[066-092]like highwaymen and bullies, let us wait till daylight, that the sun

[066-093]may behold our deeds; and the conditions of our combat shall be that

[066-094]the vanquished shall be at the victor’s disposal, to do all that he may

[066-095]enjoin, provided the injunction be such as shall be becoming a knight.”

[066-096]“I am more than satisfied with these conditions and terms,” replied Don

[066-097]Quixote; and so saying, they betook themselves to where their squires

[066-098]lay, and found them snoring, and in the same posture they were in when

[066-099]sleep fell upon them. They roused them up, and bade them get the horses

[066-100]ready, as at sunrise they were to engage in a bloody and arduous single

[066-101]combat; at which intelligence Sancho was aghast and thunderstruck,

[066-102]trembling for the safety of his master because of the mighty deeds he

[066-103]had heard the squire of the Grove ascribe to his; but without a word

[066-104]the two squires went in quest of their cattle; for by this time the

[066-105]three horses and the ass had smelt one another out, and were all

[066-106]together.

[066-107]On the way, he of the Grove said to Sancho, “You must know, brother,

[066-108]that it is the custom with the fighting men of Andalusia, when they are

[066-109]godfathers in any quarrel, not to stand idle with folded arms while

[066-110]their godsons fight; I say so to remind you that while our masters are

[066-111]fighting, we, too, have to fight, and knock one another to shivers.”

[066-112]“That custom, sir squire,” replied Sancho, “may hold good among those

[066-113]bullies and fighting men you talk of, but certainly not among the

[066-114]squires of knights-errant; at least, I have never heard my master speak

[066-115]of any custom of the sort, and he knows all the laws of knight-errantry

[066-116]by heart; but granting it true that there is an express law that

[066-117]squires are to fight while their masters are fighting, I don’t mean to

[066-118]obey it, but to pay the penalty that may be laid on peacefully minded

[066-119]squires like myself; for I am sure it cannot be more than two pounds of

[066-120]wax, and I would rather pay that, for I know it will cost me less than

[066-121]the lint I shall be at the expense of to mend my head, which I look

[066-122]upon as broken and split already; there’s another thing that makes it

[066-123]impossible for me to fight, that I have no sword, for I never carried

[066-124]one in my life.”

[066-125]“I know a good remedy for that,” said he of the Grove; “I have here two

[066-126]linen bags of the same size; you shall take one, and I the other, and

[066-127]we will fight at bag blows with equal arms.”

[066-128]“If that’s the way, so be it with all my heart,” said Sancho, “for that

[066-129]sort of battle will serve to knock the dust out of us instead of

[066-130]hurting us.”

[066-131]“That will not do,” said the other, “for we must put into the bags, to

[066-132]keep the wind from blowing them away, half a dozen nice smooth pebbles,

[066-133]all of the same weight; and in this way we shall be able to baste one

[066-134]another without doing ourselves any harm or mischief.”

[066-135]“Body of my father!” said Sancho, “see what marten and sable, and pads

[066-136]of carded cotton he is putting into the bags, that our heads may not be

[066-137]broken and our bones beaten to jelly! But even if they are filled with

[066-138]toss silk, I can tell you, señor, I am not going to fight; let our

[066-139]masters fight, that’s their lookout, and let us drink and live; for

[066-140]time will take care to ease us of our lives, without our going to look

[066-141]for fillips so that they may be finished off before their proper time

[066-142]comes and they drop from ripeness.”

[066-143]“Still,” returned he of the Grove, “we must fight, if it be only for

[066-144]half an hour.”

[066-145]“By no means,” said Sancho; “I am not going to be so discourteous or so

[066-146]ungrateful as to have any quarrel, be it ever so small, with one I have

[066-147]eaten and drunk with; besides, who the devil could bring himself to

[066-148]fight in cold blood, without anger or provocation?”

[066-149]“I can remedy that entirely,” said he of the Grove, “and in this way:

[066-150]before we begin the battle, I will come up to your worship fair and

[066-151]softly, and give you three or four buffets, with which I shall stretch

[066-152]you at my feet and rouse your anger, though it were sleeping sounder

[066-153]than a dormouse.”

[066-154]“To match that plan,” said Sancho, “I have another that is not a whit

[066-155]behind it; I will take a cudgel, and before your worship comes near

[066-156]enough to waken my anger I will send yours so sound to sleep with

[066-157]whacks, that it won’t waken unless it be in the other world, where it

[066-158]is known that I am not a man to let my face be handled by anyone; let

[066-159]each look out for the arrow—though the surer way would be to let

[066-160]everyone’s anger sleep, for nobody knows the heart of anyone, and a man

[066-161]may come for wool and go back shorn; God gave his blessing to peace and

[066-162]his curse to quarrels; if a hunted cat, surrounded and hard pressed,

[066-163]turns into a lion, God knows what I, who am a man, may turn into; and

[066-164]so from this time forth I warn you, sir squire, that all the harm and

[066-165]mischief that may come of our quarrel will be put down to your

[066-166]account.”

[066-167]“Very good,” said he of the Grove; “God will send the dawn and we shall

[066-168]be all right.”

[066-169]And now gay-plumaged birds of all sorts began to warble in the trees,

[066-170]and with their varied and gladsome notes seemed to welcome and salute

[066-171]the fresh morn that was beginning to show the beauty of her countenance

[066-172]at the gates and balconies of the east, shaking from her locks a

[066-173]profusion of liquid pearls; in which dulcet moisture bathed, the

[066-174]plants, too, seemed to shed and shower down a pearly spray, the willows

[066-175]distilled sweet manna, the fountains laughed, the brooks babbled, the

[066-176]woods rejoiced, and the meadows arrayed themselves in all their glory

[066-177]at her coming. But hardly had the light of day made it possible to see

[066-178]and distinguish things, when the first object that presented itself to

[066-179]the eyes of Sancho Panza was the squire of the Grove’s nose, which was

[066-180]so big that it almost overshadowed his whole body. It is, in fact,

[066-181]stated, that it was of enormous size, hooked in the middle, covered

[066-182]with warts, and of a mulberry colour like an egg-plant; it hung down

[066-183]two fingers’ length below his mouth, and the size, the colour, the

[066-184]warts, and the bend of it, made his face so hideous, that Sancho, as he

[066-185]looked at him, began to tremble hand and foot like a child in

[066-186]convulsions, and he vowed in his heart to let himself be given two

[066-187]hundred buffets, sooner than be provoked to fight that monster. Don

[066-188]Quixote examined his adversary, and found that he already had his

[066-189]helmet on and visor lowered, so that he could not see his face; he

[066-190]observed, however, that he was a sturdily built man, but not very tall

[066-191]in stature. Over his armour he wore a surcoat or cassock of what seemed

[066-192]to be the finest cloth of gold, all bespangled with glittering mirrors

[066-193]like little moons, which gave him an extremely gallant and splendid

[066-194]appearance; above his helmet fluttered a great quantity of plumes,

[066-195]green, yellow, and white, and his lance, which was leaning against a

[066-196]tree, was very long and stout, and had a steel point more than a palm

[066-197]in length.

[066-198]Don Quixote observed all, and took note of all, and from what he saw

[066-199]and observed he concluded that the said knight must be a man of great

[066-200]strength, but he did not for all that give way to fear, like Sancho

[066-201]Panza; on the contrary, with a composed and dauntless air, he said to

[066-202]the Knight of the Mirrors, “If, sir knight, your great eagerness to

[066-203]fight has not banished your courtesy, by it I would entreat you to

[066-204]raise your visor a little, in order that I may see if the comeliness of

[066-205]your countenance corresponds with that of your equipment.”

[066-206]“Whether you come victorious or vanquished out of this emprise, sir

[066-207]knight,” replied he of the Mirrors, “you will have more than enough

[066-208]time and leisure to see me; and if now I do not comply with your

[066-209]request, it is because it seems to me I should do a serious wrong to

[066-210]the fair Casildea de Vandalia in wasting time while I stopped to raise

[066-211]my visor before compelling you to confess what you are already aware I

[066-212]maintain.”

[066-213]“Well then,” said Don Quixote, “while we are mounting you can at least

[066-214]tell me if I am that Don Quixote whom you said you vanquished.”

[066-215]“To that we answer you,” said he of the Mirrors, “that you are as like

[066-216]the very knight I vanquished as one egg is like another, but as you say

[066-217]enchanters persecute you, I will not venture to say positively whether

[066-218]you are the said person or not.”

[066-219]“That,” said Don Quixote, “is enough to convince me that you are under

[066-220]a deception; however, entirely to relieve you of it, let our horses be

[066-221]brought, and in less time than it would take you to raise your visor,

[066-222]if God, my lady, and my arm stand me in good stead, I shall see your

[066-223]face, and you shall see that I am not the vanquished Don Quixote you

[066-224]take me to be.”

[066-225]With this, cutting short the colloquy, they mounted, and Don Quixote

[066-226]wheeled Rocinante round in order to take a proper distance to charge

[066-227]back upon his adversary, and he of the Mirrors did the same; but Don

[066-228]Quixote had not moved away twenty paces when he heard himself called by

[066-229]the other, and, each returning half-way, he of the Mirrors said to him,

[066-230]“Remember, sir knight, that the terms of our combat are, that the

[066-231]vanquished, as I said before, shall be at the victor’s disposal.”

[066-232]“I am aware of it already,” said Don Quixote; “provided what is

[066-233]commanded and imposed upon the vanquished be things that do not

[066-234]transgress the limits of chivalry.”

[066-235]“That is understood,” replied he of the Mirrors.

[066-236]At this moment the extraordinary nose of the squire presented itself to

[066-237]Don Quixote’s view, and he was no less amazed than Sancho at the sight;

[066-238]insomuch that he set him down as a monster of some kind, or a human

[066-239]being of some new species or unearthly breed. Sancho, seeing his master

[066-240]retiring to run his course, did not like to be left alone with the nosy

[066-241]man, fearing that with one flap of that nose on his own the battle

[066-242]would be all over for him and he would be left stretched on the ground,

[066-243]either by the blow or with fright; so he ran after his master, holding

[066-244]on to Rocinante’s stirrup-leather, and when it seemed to him time to

[066-245]turn about, he said, “I implore of your worship, señor, before you turn

[066-246]to charge, to help me up into this cork tree, from which I will be able

[066-247]to witness the gallant encounter your worship is going to have with

[066-248]this knight, more to my taste and better than from the ground.”

[066-249]“It seems to me rather, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that thou wouldst

[066-250]mount a scaffold in order to see the bulls without danger.”

[066-251]“To tell the truth,” returned Sancho, “the monstrous nose of that

[066-252]squire has filled me with fear and terror, and I dare not stay near

[066-253]him.”

[066-254]“It is,” said Don Quixote, “such a one that were I not what I am it

[066-255]would terrify me too; so, come, I will help thee up where thou wilt.”

[066-256]While Don Quixote waited for Sancho to mount into the cork tree he of

[066-257]the Mirrors took as much ground as he considered requisite, and,

[066-258]supposing Don Quixote to have done the same, without waiting for any

[066-259]sound of trumpet or other signal to direct them, he wheeled his horse,

[066-260]which was not more agile or better-looking than Rocinante, and at his

[066-261]top speed, which was an easy trot, he proceeded to charge his enemy;

[066-262]seeing him, however, engaged in putting Sancho up, he drew rein, and

[066-263]halted in mid career, for which his horse was very grateful, as he was

[066-264]already unable to go. Don Quixote, fancying that his foe was coming

[066-265]down upon him flying, drove his spurs vigorously into Rocinante’s lean

[066-266]flanks and made him scud along in such style that the history tells us

[066-267]that on this occasion only was he known to make something like running,

[066-268]for on all others it was a simple trot with him; and with this

[066-269]unparalleled fury he bore down where he of the Mirrors stood digging

[066-270]his spurs into his horse up to buttons, without being able to make him

[066-271]stir a finger’s length from the spot where he had come to a standstill

[066-272]in his course. At this lucky moment and crisis, Don Quixote came upon

[066-273]his adversary, in trouble with his horse, and embarrassed with his

[066-274]lance, which he either could not manage, or had no time to lay in rest.

[066-275]Don Quixote, however, paid no attention to these difficulties, and in

[066-276]perfect safety to himself and without any risk encountered him of the

[066-277]Mirrors with such force that he brought him to the ground in spite of

[066-278]himself over the haunches of his horse, and with so heavy a fall that

[066-279]he lay to all appearance dead, not stirring hand or foot. The instant

[066-280]Sancho saw him fall he slid down from the cork tree, and made all haste

[066-281]to where his master was, who, dismounting from Rocinante, went and

[066-282]stood over him of the Mirrors, and unlacing his helmet to see if he was

[066-283]dead, and to give him air if he should happen to be alive, he saw—who

[066-284]can say what he saw, without filling all who hear it with astonishment,

[066-285]wonder, and awe? He saw, the history says, the very countenance, the

[066-286]very face, the very look, the very physiognomy, the very effigy, the

[066-287]very image of the bachelor Samson Carrasco! As soon as he saw it he

[066-288]called out in a loud voice, “Make haste here, Sancho, and behold what

[066-289]thou art to see but not to believe; quick, my son, and learn what magic

[066-290]can do, and wizards and enchanters are capable of.”

[066-291]Sancho came up, and when he saw the countenance of the bachelor

[066-292]Carrasco, he fell to crossing himself a thousand times, and blessing

[066-293]himself as many more. All this time the prostrate knight showed no

[066-294]signs of life, and Sancho said to Don Quixote, “It is my opinion,

[066-295]señor, that in any case your worship should take and thrust your sword

[066-296]into the mouth of this one here that looks like the bachelor Samson

[066-297]Carrasco; perhaps in him you will kill one of your enemies, the

[066-298]enchanters.”

[066-299]“Thy advice is not bad,” said Don Quixote, “for of enemies the fewer

[066-300]the better;” and he was drawing his sword to carry into effect Sancho’s

[066-301]counsel and suggestion, when the squire of the Mirrors came up, now

[066-302]without the nose which had made him so hideous, and cried out in a loud

[066-303]voice, “Mind what you are about, Señor Don Quixote; that is your

[066-304]friend, the bachelor Samson Carrasco, you have at your feet, and I am

[066-305]his squire.”

[066-306]“And the nose?” said Sancho, seeing him without the hideous feature he

[066-307]had before; to which he replied, “I have it here in my pocket,” and

[066-308]putting his hand into his right pocket, he pulled out a masquerade nose

[066-309]of varnished pasteboard of the make already described; and Sancho,

[066-310]examining him more and more closely, exclaimed aloud in a voice of

[066-311]amazement, “Holy Mary be good to me! Isn’t it Tom Cecial, my neighbour

[066-312]and gossip?”

[066-313]“Why, to be sure I am!” returned the now unnosed squire; “Tom Cecial I

[066-314]am, gossip and friend Sancho Panza; and I’ll tell you presently the

[066-315]means and tricks and falsehoods by which I have been brought here; but

[066-316]in the meantime, beg and entreat of your master not to touch, maltreat,

[066-317]wound, or slay the Knight of the Mirrors whom he has at his feet;

[066-318]because, beyond all dispute, it is the rash and ill-advised bachelor

[066-319]Samson Carrasco, our fellow townsman.”

[066-320]At this moment he of the Mirrors came to himself, and Don Quixote

[066-321]perceiving it, held the naked point of his sword over his face, and

[066-322]said to him, “You are a dead man, knight, unless you confess that the

[066-323]peerless Dulcinea del Toboso excels your Casildea de Vandalia in

[066-324]beauty; and in addition to this you must promise, if you should survive

[066-325]this encounter and fall, to go to the city of El Toboso and present

[066-326]yourself before her on my behalf, that she deal with you according to

[066-327]her good pleasure; and if she leaves you free to do yours, you are in

[066-328]like manner to return and seek me out (for the trail of my mighty deeds

[066-329]will serve you as a guide to lead you to where I may be), and tell me

[066-330]what may have passed between you and her—conditions which, in

[066-331]accordance with what we stipulated before our combat, do not transgress

[066-332]the just limits of knight-errantry.”

[066-333]“I confess,” said the fallen knight, “that the dirty tattered shoe of

[066-334]the lady Dulcinea del Toboso is better than the ill-combed though clean

[066-335]beard of Casildea; and I promise to go and to return from her presence

[066-336]to yours, and to give you a full and particular account of all you

[066-337]demand of me.”

[066-338]“You must also confess and believe,” added Don Quixote, “that the

[066-339]knight you vanquished was not and could not be Don Quixote of La

[066-340]Mancha, but someone else in his likeness, just as I confess and believe

[066-341]that you, though you seem to be the bachelor Samson Carrasco, are not

[066-342]so, but some other resembling him, whom my enemies have here put before

[066-343]me in his shape, in order that I may restrain and moderate the

[066-344]vehemence of my wrath, and make a gentle use of the glory of my

[066-345]victory.”

[066-346]“I confess, hold, and think everything to be as you believe, hold, and
