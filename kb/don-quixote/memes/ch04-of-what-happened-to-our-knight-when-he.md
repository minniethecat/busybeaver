OF WHAT HAPPENED TO OUR KNIGHT WHEN HE LEFT THE INN

[004-001]Day was dawning when Don Quixote quitted the inn, so happy, so gay, so

[004-002]exhilarated at finding himself now dubbed a knight, that his joy was

[004-003]like to burst his horse-girths. However, recalling the advice of his

[004-004]host as to the requisites he ought to carry with him, especially that

[004-005]referring to money and shirts, he determined to go home and provide

[004-006]himself with all, and also with a squire, for he reckoned upon securing

[004-007]a farm-labourer, a neighbour of his, a poor man with a family, but very

[004-008]well qualified for the office of squire to a knight. With this object

[004-009]he turned his horse’s head towards his village, and Rocinante, thus

[004-010]reminded of his old quarters, stepped out so briskly that he hardly

[004-011]seemed to tread the earth.

[004-012]He had not gone far, when out of a thicket on his right there seemed to

[004-013]come feeble cries as of someone in distress, and the instant he heard

[004-014]them he exclaimed, “Thanks be to heaven for the favour it accords me,

[004-015]that it so soon offers me an opportunity of fulfilling the obligation I

[004-016]have undertaken, and gathering the fruit of my ambition. These cries,

[004-017]no doubt, come from some man or woman in want of help, and needing my

[004-018]aid and protection;” and wheeling, he turned Rocinante in the direction

[004-019]whence the cries seemed to proceed. He had gone but a few paces into

[004-020]the wood, when he saw a mare tied to an oak, and tied to another, and

[004-021]stripped from the waist upwards, a youth of about fifteen years of age,

[004-022]from whom the cries came. Nor were they without cause, for a lusty

[004-023]farmer was flogging him with a belt and following up every blow with

[004-024]scoldings and commands, repeating, “Your mouth shut and your eyes

[004-025]open!” while the youth made answer, “I won’t do it again, master mine;

[004-026]by God’s passion I won’t do it again, and I’ll take more care of the

[004-027]flock another time.”

[004-028]Seeing what was going on, Don Quixote said in an angry voice,

[004-029]“Discourteous knight, it ill becomes you to assail one who cannot

[004-030]defend himself; mount your steed and take your lance” (for there was a

[004-031]lance leaning against the oak to which the mare was tied), “and I will

[004-032]make you know that you are behaving as a coward.” The farmer, seeing

[004-033]before him this figure in full armour brandishing a lance over his

[004-034]head, gave himself up for dead, and made answer meekly, “Sir Knight,

[004-035]this youth that I am chastising is my servant, employed by me to watch

[004-036]a flock of sheep that I have hard by, and he is so careless that I lose

[004-037]one every day, and when I punish him for his carelessness and knavery

[004-038]he says I do it out of niggardliness, to escape paying him the wages I

[004-039]owe him, and before God, and on my soul, he lies.”

[004-040]“Lies before me, base clown!” said Don Quixote. “By the sun that shines

[004-041]on us I have a mind to run you through with this lance. Pay him at once

[004-042]without another word; if not, by the God that rules us I will make an

[004-043]end of you, and annihilate you on the spot; release him instantly.”

[004-044]The farmer hung his head, and without a word untied his servant, of

[004-045]whom Don Quixote asked how much his master owed him.

[004-046]He replied, nine months at seven reals a month. Don Quixote added it

[004-047]up, found that it came to sixty-three reals, and told the farmer to pay

[004-048]it down immediately, if he did not want to die for it.

[004-049]The trembling clown replied that as he lived and by the oath he had

[004-050]sworn (though he had not sworn any) it was not so much; for there were

[004-051]to be taken into account and deducted three pairs of shoes he had given

[004-052]him, and a real for two blood-lettings when he was sick.

[004-053]“All that is very well,” said Don Quixote; “but let the shoes and the

[004-054]blood-lettings stand as a setoff against the blows you have given him

[004-055]without any cause; for if he spoiled the leather of the shoes you paid

[004-056]for, you have damaged that of his body, and if the barber took blood

[004-057]from him when he was sick, you have drawn it when he was sound; so on

[004-058]that score he owes you nothing.”

[004-059]“The difficulty is, Sir Knight, that I have no money here; let Andres

[004-060]come home with me, and I will pay him all, real by real.”

[004-061]“I go with him!” said the youth. “Nay, God forbid! No, señor, not for

[004-062]the world; for once alone with me, he would flay me like a Saint

[004-063]Bartholomew.”

[004-064]“He will do nothing of the kind,” said Don Quixote; “I have only to

[004-065]command, and he will obey me; and as he has sworn to me by the order of

[004-066]knighthood which he has received, I leave him free, and I guarantee the

[004-067]payment.”

[004-068]“Consider what you are saying, señor,” said the youth; “this master of

[004-069]mine is not a knight, nor has he received any order of knighthood; for

[004-070]he is Juan Haldudo the Rich, of Quintanar.”

[004-071]“That matters little,” replied Don Quixote; “there may be Haldudos

[004-072]knights; moreover, everyone is the son of his works.”

[004-073]“That is true,” said Andres; “but this master of mine—of what works is

[004-074]he the son, when he refuses me the wages of my sweat and labour?”

[004-075]“I do not refuse, brother Andres,” said the farmer, “be good enough to

[004-076]come along with me, and I swear by all the orders of knighthood there

[004-077]are in the world to pay you as I have agreed, real by real, and

[004-078]perfumed.”

[004-079]“For the perfumery I excuse you,” said Don Quixote; “give it to him in

[004-080]reals, and I shall be satisfied; and see that you do as you have sworn;

[004-081]if not, by the same oath I swear to come back and hunt you out and

[004-082]punish you; and I shall find you though you should lie closer than a

[004-083]lizard. And if you desire to know who it is lays this command upon you,

[004-084]that you be more firmly bound to obey it, know that I am the valorous

[004-085]Don Quixote of La Mancha, the undoer of wrongs and injustices; and so,

[004-086]God be with you, and keep in mind what you have promised and sworn

[004-087]under those penalties that have been already declared to you.”

[004-088]So saying, he gave Rocinante the spur and was soon out of reach. The

[004-089]farmer followed him with his eyes, and when he saw that he had cleared

[004-090]the wood and was no longer in sight, he turned to his boy Andres, and

[004-091]said, “Come here, my son, I want to pay you what I owe you, as that

[004-092]undoer of wrongs has commanded me.”

[004-093]“My oath on it,” said Andres, “your worship will be well advised to

[004-094]obey the command of that good knight—may he live a thousand years—for,

[004-095]as he is a valiant and just judge, by Roque, if you do not pay me, he

[004-096]will come back and do as he said.”

[004-097]“My oath on it, too,” said the farmer; “but as I have a strong

[004-098]affection for you, I want to add to the debt in order to add to the

[004-099]payment;” and seizing him by the arm, he tied him up again, and gave

[004-100]him such a flogging that he left him for dead.

[004-101]“Now, Master Andres,” said the farmer, “call on the undoer of wrongs;

[004-102]you will find he won’t undo that, though I am not sure that I have

[004-103]quite done with you, for I have a good mind to flay you alive.” But at

[004-104]last he untied him, and gave him leave to go look for his judge in

[004-105]order to put the sentence pronounced into execution.

[004-106]Andres went off rather down in the mouth, swearing he would go to look

[004-107]for the valiant Don Quixote of La Mancha and tell him exactly what had

[004-108]happened, and that all would have to be repaid him sevenfold; but for

[004-109]all that, he went off weeping, while his master stood laughing.

[004-110]Thus did the valiant Don Quixote right that wrong, and, thoroughly

[004-111]satisfied with what had taken place, as he considered he had made a

[004-112]very happy and noble beginning with his knighthood, he took the road

[004-113]towards his village in perfect self-content, saying in a low voice,

[004-114]“Well mayest thou this day call thyself fortunate above all on earth, O

[004-115]Dulcinea del Toboso, fairest of the fair! since it has fallen to thy

[004-116]lot to hold subject and submissive to thy full will and pleasure a

[004-117]knight so renowned as is and will be Don Quixote of La Mancha, who, as

[004-118]all the world knows, yesterday received the order of knighthood, and

[004-119]hath to-day righted the greatest wrong and grievance that ever

[004-120]injustice conceived and cruelty perpetrated: who hath to-day plucked

[004-121]the rod from the hand of yonder ruthless oppressor so wantonly lashing

[004-122]that tender child.”

[004-123]He now came to a road branching in four directions, and immediately he

[004-124]was reminded of those cross-roads where knights-errant used to stop to

[004-125]consider which road they should take. In imitation of them he halted

[004-126]for a while, and after having deeply considered it, he gave Rocinante

[004-127]his head, submitting his own will to that of his hack, who followed out

[004-128]his first intention, which was to make straight for his own stable.

[004-129]After he had gone about two miles Don Quixote perceived a large party

[004-130]of people, who, as afterwards appeared, were some Toledo traders, on

[004-131]their way to buy silk at Murcia. There were six of them coming along

[004-132]under their sunshades, with four servants mounted, and three muleteers

[004-133]on foot. Scarcely had Don Quixote descried them when the fancy

[004-134]possessed him that this must be some new adventure; and to help him to

[004-135]imitate as far as he could those passages he had read of in his books,

[004-136]here seemed to come one made on purpose, which he resolved to attempt.

[004-137]So with a lofty bearing and determination he fixed himself firmly in

[004-138]his stirrups, got his lance ready, brought his buckler before his

[004-139]breast, and planting himself in the middle of the road, stood waiting

[004-140]the approach of these knights-errant, for such he now considered and

[004-141]held them to be; and when they had come near enough to see and hear, he

[004-142]exclaimed with a haughty gesture, “All the world stand, unless all the

[004-143]world confess that in all the world there is no maiden fairer than the

[004-144]Empress of La Mancha, the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso.”

[004-145]The traders halted at the sound of this language and the sight of the

[004-146]strange figure that uttered it, and from both figure and language at

[004-147]once guessed the craze of their owner; they wished, however, to learn

[004-148]quietly what was the object of this confession that was demanded of

[004-149]them, and one of them, who was rather fond of a joke and was very

[004-150]sharp-witted, said to him, “Sir Knight, we do not know who this good

[004-151]lady is that you speak of; show her to us, for, if she be of such

[004-152]beauty as you suggest, with all our hearts and without any pressure we

[004-153]will confess the truth that is on your part required of us.”

[004-154]“If I were to show her to you,” replied Don Quixote, “what merit would

[004-155]you have in confessing a truth so manifest? The essential point is that

[004-156]without seeing her you must believe, confess, affirm, swear, and defend

[004-157]it; else ye have to do with me in battle, ill-conditioned, arrogant

[004-158]rabble that ye are; and come ye on, one by one as the order of

[004-159]knighthood requires, or all together as is the custom and vile usage of

[004-160]your breed, here do I bide and await you relying on the justice of the

[004-161]cause I maintain.”

[004-162]“Sir Knight,” replied the trader, “I entreat your worship in the name

[004-163]of this present company of princes, that, to save us from charging our

[004-164]consciences with the confession of a thing we have never seen or heard

[004-165]of, and one moreover so much to the prejudice of the Empresses and

[004-166]Queens of the Alcarria and Estremadura, your worship will be pleased to

[004-167]show us some portrait of this lady, though it be no bigger than a grain

[004-168]of wheat; for by the thread one gets at the ball, and in this way we

[004-169]shall be satisfied and easy, and you will be content and pleased; nay,

[004-170]I believe we are already so far agreed with you that even though her

[004-171]portrait should show her blind of one eye, and distilling vermilion and

[004-172]sulphur from the other, we would nevertheless, to gratify your worship,

[004-173]say all in her favour that you desire.”

[004-174]“She distils nothing of the kind, vile rabble,” said Don Quixote,

[004-175]burning with rage, “nothing of the kind, I say, only ambergris and

[004-176]civet in cotton; nor is she one-eyed or humpbacked, but straighter than

[004-177]a Guadarrama spindle: but ye must pay for the blasphemy ye have uttered

[004-178]against beauty like that of my lady.”

[004-179]And so saying, he charged with levelled lance against the one who had

[004-180]spoken, with such fury and fierceness that, if luck had not contrived

[004-181]that Rocinante should stumble midway and come down, it would have gone

[004-182]hard with the rash trader. Down went Rocinante, and over went his

[004-183]master, rolling along the ground for some distance; and when he tried

[004-184]to rise he was unable, so encumbered was he with lance, buckler, spurs,

[004-185]helmet, and the weight of his old armour; and all the while he was

[004-186]struggling to get up he kept saying, “Fly not, cowards and caitiffs!

[004-187]stay, for not by my fault, but my horse’s, am I stretched here.”

[004-188]One of the muleteers in attendance, who could not have had much good

[004-189]nature in him, hearing the poor prostrate man blustering in this style,

[004-190]was unable to refrain from giving him an answer on his ribs; and coming

[004-191]up to him he seized his lance, and having broken it in pieces, with one

[004-192]of them he began so to belabour our Don Quixote that, notwithstanding

[004-193]and in spite of his armour, he milled him like a measure of wheat. His

[004-194]masters called out not to lay on so hard and to leave him alone, but

[004-195]the muleteer’s blood was up, and he did not care to drop the game until

[004-196]he had vented the rest of his wrath, and gathering up the remaining

[004-197]fragments of the lance he finished with a discharge upon the unhappy

[004-198]victim, who all through the storm of sticks that rained on him never

[004-199]ceased threatening heaven, and earth, and the brigands, for such they

[004-200]seemed to him. At last the muleteer was tired, and the traders

[004-201]continued their journey, taking with them matter for talk about the

[004-202]poor fellow who had been cudgelled. He when he found himself alone made

[004-203]another effort to rise; but if he was unable when whole and sound, how

[004-204]was he to rise after having been thrashed and well-nigh knocked to

[004-205]pieces? And yet he esteemed himself fortunate, as it seemed to him that

[004-206]this was a regular knight-errant’s mishap, and entirely, he considered,

[004-207]the fault of his horse. However, battered in body as he was, to rise

[004-208]was beyond his power.
