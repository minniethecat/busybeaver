[093-001]And now night came, and with it the appointed time for the arrival of

[093-002]the famous horse Clavileño, the non-appearance of which was already

[093-003]beginning to make Don Quixote uneasy, for it struck him that, as

[093-004]Malambruno was so long about sending it, either he himself was not the

[093-005]knight for whom the adventure was reserved, or else Malambruno did not

[093-006]dare to meet him in single combat. But lo! suddenly there came into the

[093-007]garden four wild-men all clad in green ivy bearing on their shoulders a

[093-008]great wooden horse. They placed it on its feet on the ground, and one

[093-009]of the wild-men said, “Let the knight who has heart for it mount this

[093-010]machine.”

[093-011]Here Sancho exclaimed, “I don’t mount, for neither have I the heart nor

[093-012]am I a knight.”

[093-013]“And let the squire, if he has one,” continued the wild-man, “take his

[093-014]seat on the croup, and let him trust the valiant Malambruno; for by no

[093-015]sword save his, nor by the malice of any other, shall he be assailed.

[093-016]It is but to turn this peg the horse has in his neck, and he will bear

[093-017]them through the air to where Malambruno awaits them; but lest the vast

[093-018]elevation of their course should make them giddy, their eyes must be

[093-019]covered until the horse neighs, which will be the sign of their having

[093-020]completed their journey.”

[093-021]With these words, leaving Clavileño behind them, they retired with easy

[093-022]dignity the way they came. As soon as the Distressed One saw the horse,

[093-023]almost in tears she exclaimed to Don Quixote, “Valiant knight, the

[093-024]promise of Malambruno has proved trustworthy; the horse has come, our

[093-025]beards are growing, and by every hair in them all of us implore thee to

[093-026]shave and shear us, as it is only mounting him with thy squire and

[093-027]making a happy beginning with your new journey.”

[093-028]“That I will, Señora Countess Trifaldi,” said Don Quixote, “most gladly

[093-029]and with right goodwill, without stopping to take a cushion or put on

[093-030]my spurs, so as not to lose time, such is my desire to see you and all

[093-031]these duennas shaved clean.”

[093-032]“That I won’t,” said Sancho, “with good-will or bad-will, or any way at

[093-033]all; and if this shaving can’t be done without my mounting on the

[093-034]croup, my master had better look out for another squire to go with him,

[093-035]and these ladies for some other way of making their faces smooth; I’m

[093-036]no witch to have a taste for travelling through the air. What would my

[093-037]islanders say when they heard their governor was going, strolling about

[093-038]on the winds? And another thing, as it is three thousand and odd

[093-039]leagues from this to Kandy, if the horse tires, or the giant takes

[093-040]huff, we’ll be half a dozen years getting back, and there won’t be isle

[093-041]or island in the world that will know me: and so, as it is a common

[093-042]saying ‘in delay there’s danger,’ and ‘when they offer thee a heifer

[093-043]run with a halter,’ these ladies’ beards must excuse me; ‘Saint Peter

[093-044]is very well in Rome;’ I mean I am very well in this house where so

[093-045]much is made of me, and I hope for such a good thing from the master as

[093-046]to see myself a governor.”

[093-047]“Friend Sancho,” said the duke at this, “the island that I have

[093-048]promised you is not a moving one, or one that will run away; it has

[093-049]roots so deeply buried in the bowels of the earth that it will be no

[093-050]easy matter to pluck it up or shift it from where it is; you know as

[093-051]well as I do that there is no sort of office of any importance that is

[093-052]not obtained by a bribe of some kind, great or small; well then, that

[093-053]which I look to receive for this government is that you go with your

[093-054]master Don Quixote, and bring this memorable adventure to a conclusion;

[093-055]and whether you return on Clavileño as quickly as his speed seems to

[093-056]promise, or adverse fortune brings you back on foot travelling as a

[093-057]pilgrim from hostel to hostel and from inn to inn, you will always find

[093-058]your island on your return where you left it, and your islanders with

[093-059]the same eagerness they have always had to receive you as their

[093-060]governor, and my good-will will remain the same; doubt not the truth of

[093-061]this, Señor Sancho, for that would be grievously wronging my

[093-062]disposition to serve you.”

[093-063]“Say no more, señor,” said Sancho; “I am a poor squire and not equal to

[093-064]carrying so much courtesy; let my master mount; bandage my eyes and

[093-065]commit me to God’s care, and tell me if I may commend myself to our

[093-066]Lord or call upon the angels to protect me when we go towering up

[093-067]there.”

[093-068]To this the Trifaldi made answer, “Sancho, you may freely commend

[093-069]yourself to God or whom you will; for Malambruno though an enchanter is

[093-070]a Christian, and works his enchantments with great circumspection,

[093-071]taking very good care not to fall out with anyone.”

[093-072]“Well then,” said Sancho, “God and the most holy Trinity of Gaeta give

[093-073]me help!”

[093-074]“Since the memorable adventure of the fulling mills,” said Don Quixote,

[093-075]“I have never seen Sancho in such a fright as now; were I as

[093-076]superstitious as others his abject fear would cause me some little

[093-077]trepidation of spirit. But come here, Sancho, for with the leave of

[093-078]these gentles I would say a word or two to thee in private;” and

[093-079]drawing Sancho aside among the trees of the garden and seizing both his

[093-080]hands he said, “Thou seest, brother Sancho, the long journey we have

[093-081]before us, and God knows when we shall return, or what leisure or

[093-082]opportunities this business will allow us; I wish thee therefore to

[093-083]retire now to thy chamber, as though thou wert going to fetch something

[093-084]required for the road, and in a trice give thyself if it be only five

[093-085]hundred lashes on account of the three thousand three hundred to which

[093-086]thou art bound; it will be all to the good, and to make a beginning

[093-087]with a thing is to have it half finished.”

[093-088]“By God,” said Sancho, “but your worship must be out of your senses!

[093-089]This is like the common saying, ‘You see me with child, and you want me

[093-090]a virgin.’ Just as I’m about to go sitting on a bare board, your

[093-091]worship would have me score my backside! Indeed, your worship is not

[093-092]reasonable. Let us be off to shave these duennas; and on our return I

[093-093]promise on my word to make such haste to wipe off all that’s due as

[093-094]will satisfy your worship; I can’t say more.”

[093-095]“Well, I will comfort myself with that promise, my good Sancho,”

[093-096]replied Don Quixote, “and I believe thou wilt keep it; for indeed

[093-097]though stupid thou art veracious.”

[093-098]“I’m not voracious,” said Sancho, “only peckish; but even if I was a

[093-099]little, still I’d keep my word.”

[093-100]With this they went back to mount Clavileño, and as they were about to

[093-101]do so Don Quixote said, “Cover thine eyes, Sancho, and mount; for one

[093-102]who sends for us from lands so far distant cannot mean to deceive us

[093-103]for the sake of the paltry glory to be derived from deceiving persons

[093-104]who trust in him; though all should turn out the contrary of what I

[093-105]hope, no malice will be able to dim the glory of having undertaken this

[093-106]exploit.”

[093-107]“Let us be off, señor,” said Sancho, “for I have taken the beards and

[093-108]tears of these ladies deeply to heart, and I shan’t eat a bit to relish

[093-109]it until I have seen them restored to their former smoothness. Mount,

[093-110]your worship, and blindfold yourself, for if I am to go on the croup,

[093-111]it is plain the rider in the saddle must mount first.”

[093-112]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, and, taking a handkerchief out of his

[093-113]pocket, he begged the Distressed One to bandage his eyes very

[093-114]carefully; but after having them bandaged he uncovered them again,

[093-115]saying, “If my memory does not deceive me, I have read in Virgil of the

[093-116]Palladium of Troy, a wooden horse the Greeks offered to the goddess

[093-117]Pallas, which was big with armed knights, who were afterwards the

[093-118]destruction of Troy; so it would be as well to see, first of all, what

[093-119]Clavileño has in his stomach.”

[093-120]“There is no occasion,” said the Distressed One; “I will be bail for

[093-121]him, and I know that Malambruno has nothing tricky or treacherous about

[093-122]him; you may mount without any fear, Señor Don Quixote; on my head be

[093-123]it if any harm befalls you.”

[093-124]Don Quixote thought that to say anything further with regard to his

[093-125]safety would be putting his courage in an unfavourable light; and so,

[093-126]without more words, he mounted Clavileño, and tried the peg, which

[093-127]turned easily; and as he had no stirrups and his legs hung down, he

[093-128]looked like nothing so much as a figure in some Roman triumph painted

[093-129]or embroidered on a Flemish tapestry.

[093-130]Much against the grain, and very slowly, Sancho proceeded to mount,

[093-131]and, after settling himself as well as he could on the croup, found it

[093-132]rather hard, and not at all soft, and asked the duke if it would be

[093-133]possible to oblige him with a pad of some kind, or a cushion; even if

[093-134]it were off the couch of his lady the duchess, or the bed of one of the

[093-135]pages; as the haunches of that horse were more like marble than wood.

[093-136]On this the Trifaldi observed that Clavileño would not bear any kind of

[093-137]harness or trappings, and that his best plan would be to sit sideways

[093-138]like a woman, as in that way he would not feel the hardness so much.

[093-139]Sancho did so, and, bidding them farewell, allowed his eyes to be

[093-140]bandaged, but immediately afterwards uncovered them again, and looking

[093-141]tenderly and tearfully on those in the garden, bade them help him in

[093-142]his present strait with plenty of Paternosters and Ave Marias, that God

[093-143]might provide someone to say as many for them, whenever they found

[093-144]themselves in a similar emergency.

[093-145]At this Don Quixote exclaimed, “Art thou on the gallows, thief, or at

[093-146]thy last moment, to use pitiful entreaties of that sort? Cowardly,

[093-147]spiritless creature, art thou not in the very place the fair Magalona

[093-148]occupied, and from which she descended, not into the grave, but to

[093-149]become Queen of France; unless the histories lie? And I who am here

[093-150]beside thee, may I not put myself on a par with the valiant Pierres,

[093-151]who pressed this very spot that I now press? Cover thine eyes, cover

[093-152]thine eyes, abject animal, and let not thy fear escape thy lips, at

[093-153]least in my presence.”

[093-154]“Blindfold me,” said Sancho; “as you won’t let me commend myself or be

[093-155]commended to God, is it any wonder if I am afraid there is a region of

[093-156]devils about here that will carry us off to Peralvillo?”

[093-157]They were then blindfolded, and Don Quixote, finding himself settled to

[093-158]his satisfaction, felt for the peg, and the instant he placed his

[093-159]fingers on it, all the duennas and all who stood by lifted up their

[093-160]voices exclaiming, “God guide thee, valiant knight! God be with thee,

[093-161]intrepid squire! Now, now ye go cleaving the air more swiftly than an

[093-162]arrow! Now ye begin to amaze and astonish all who are gazing at you

[093-163]from the earth! Take care not to wobble about, valiant Sancho! Mind

[093-164]thou fall not, for thy fall will be worse than that rash youth’s who

[093-165]tried to steer the chariot of his father the Sun!”

[093-166]As Sancho heard the voices, clinging tightly to his master and winding

[093-167]his arms round him, he said, “Señor, how do they make out we are going

[093-168]up so high, if their voices reach us here and they seem to be speaking

[093-169]quite close to us?”

[093-170]“Don’t mind that, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “for as affairs of this

[093-171]sort, and flights like this are out of the common course of things, you

[093-172]can see and hear as much as you like a thousand leagues off; but don’t

[093-173]squeeze me so tight or thou wilt upset me; and really I know not what

[093-174]thou hast to be uneasy or frightened at, for I can safely swear I never

[093-175]mounted a smoother-going steed all the days of my life; one would fancy

[093-176]we never stirred from one place. Banish fear, my friend, for indeed

[093-177]everything is going as it ought, and we have the wind astern.”

[093-178]“That’s true,” said Sancho, “for such a strong wind comes against me on

[093-179]this side, that it seems as if people were blowing on me with a

[093-180]thousand pair of bellows;” which was the case; they were puffing at him

[093-181]with a great pair of bellows; for the whole adventure was so well

[093-182]planned by the duke, the duchess, and their majordomo, that nothing was

[093-183]omitted to make it perfectly successful.

[093-184]Don Quixote now, feeling the blast, said, “Beyond a doubt, Sancho, we

[093-185]must have already reached the second region of the air, where the hail

[093-186]and snow are generated; the thunder, the lightning, and the

[093-187]thunderbolts are engendered in the third region, and if we go on

[093-188]ascending at this rate, we shall shortly plunge into the region of

[093-189]fire, and I know not how to regulate this peg, so as not to mount up

[093-190]where we shall be burned.”

[093-191]And now they began to warm their faces, from a distance, with tow that

[093-192]could be easily set on fire and extinguished again, fixed on the end of

[093-193]a cane. On feeling the heat Sancho said, “May I die if we are not

[093-194]already in that fire place, or very near it, for a good part of my

[093-195]beard has been singed, and I have a mind, señor, to uncover and see

[093-196]whereabouts we are.”

[093-197]“Do nothing of the kind,” said Don Quixote; “remember the true story of

[093-198]the licentiate Torralva that the devils carried flying through the air

[093-199]riding on a stick with his eyes shut; who in twelve hours reached Rome

[093-200]and dismounted at Torre di Nona, which is a street of the city, and saw

[093-201]the whole sack and storming and the death of Bourbon, and was back in

[093-202]Madrid the next morning, where he gave an account of all he had seen;

[093-203]and he said moreover that as he was going through the air, the devil

[093-204]bade him open his eyes, and he did so, and saw himself so near the body

[093-205]of the moon, so it seemed to him, that he could have laid hold of it

[093-206]with his hand, and that he did not dare to look at the earth lest he

[093-207]should be seized with giddiness. So that, Sancho, it will not do for us

[093-208]to uncover ourselves, for he who has us in charge will be responsible

[093-209]for us; and perhaps we are gaining an altitude and mounting up to

[093-210]enable us to descend at one swoop on the kingdom of Kandy, as the saker

[093-211]or falcon does on the heron, so as to seize it however high it may

[093-212]soar; and though it seems to us not half an hour since we left the

[093-213]garden, believe me we must have travelled a great distance.”

[093-214]“I don’t know how that may be,” said Sancho; “all I know is that if the

[093-215]Señora Magallanes or Magalona was satisfied with this croup, she could

[093-216]not have been very tender of flesh.”

[093-217]The duke, the duchess, and all in the garden were listening to the

[093-218]conversation of the two heroes, and were beyond measure amused by it;

[093-219]and now, desirous of putting a finishing touch to this rare and

[093-220]well-contrived adventure, they applied a light to Clavileño’s tail with

[093-221]some tow, and the horse, being full of squibs and crackers, immediately

[093-222]blew up with a prodigious noise, and brought Don Quixote and Sancho

[093-223]Panza to the ground half singed. By this time the bearded band of

[093-224]duennas, the Trifaldi and all, had vanished from the garden, and those

[093-225]that remained lay stretched on the ground as if in a swoon. Don Quixote

[093-226]and Sancho got up rather shaken, and, looking about them, were filled

[093-227]with amazement at finding themselves in the same garden from which they

[093-228]had started, and seeing such a number of people stretched on the

[093-229]ground; and their astonishment was increased when at one side of the

[093-230]garden they perceived a tall lance planted in the ground, and hanging

[093-231]from it by two cords of green silk a smooth white parchment on which

[093-232]there was the following inscription in large gold letters: “The

[093-233]illustrious knight Don Quixote of La Mancha has, by merely attempting

[093-234]it, finished and concluded the adventure of the Countess Trifaldi,

[093-235]otherwise called the Distressed Duenna; Malambruno is now satisfied on

[093-236]every point, the chins of the duennas are now smooth and clean, and

[093-237]King Don Clavijo and Queen Antonomasia in their original form; and when

[093-238]the squirely flagellation shall have been completed, the white dove

[093-239]shall find herself delivered from the pestiferous gerfalcons that

[093-240]persecute her, and in the arms of her beloved mate; for such is the

[093-241]decree of the sage Merlin, arch-enchanter of enchanters.”

[093-242]As soon as Don Quixote had read the inscription on the parchment he

[093-243]perceived clearly that it referred to the disenchantment of Dulcinea,

[093-244]and returning hearty thanks to heaven that he had with so little danger

[093-245]achieved so grand an exploit as to restore to their former complexion

[093-246]the countenances of those venerable duennas, he advanced towards the

[093-247]duke and duchess, who had not yet come to themselves, and taking the

[093-248]duke by the hand he said, “Be of good cheer, worthy sir, be of good

[093-249]cheer; it’s nothing at all; the adventure is now over and without any

[093-250]harm done, as the inscription fixed on this post shows plainly.”

[093-251]The duke came to himself slowly and like one recovering consciousness

[093-252]after a heavy sleep, and the duchess and all who had fallen prostrate

[093-253]about the garden did the same, with such demonstrations of wonder and

[093-254]amazement that they would have almost persuaded one that what they

[093-255]pretended so adroitly in jest had happened to them in reality. The duke

[093-256]read the placard with half-shut eyes, and then ran to embrace Don

[093-257]Quixote with open arms, declaring him to be the best knight that had

[093-258]ever been seen in any age. Sancho kept looking about for the Distressed

[093-259]One, to see what her face was like without the beard, and if she was as

[093-260]fair as her elegant person promised; but they told him that, the

[093-261]instant Clavileño descended flaming through the air and came to the

[093-262]ground, the whole band of duennas with the Trifaldi vanished, and that

[093-263]they were already shaved and without a stump left.

[093-264]The duchess asked Sancho how he had fared on that long journey, to

[093-265]which Sancho replied, “I felt, señora, that we were flying through the

[093-266]region of fire, as my master told me, and I wanted to uncover my eyes

[093-267]for a bit; but my master, when I asked leave to uncover myself, would

[093-268]not let me; but as I have a little bit of curiosity about me, and a

[093-269]desire to know what is forbidden and kept from me, quietly and without

[093-270]anyone seeing me I drew aside the handkerchief covering my eyes ever so

[093-271]little, close to my nose, and from underneath looked towards the earth,

[093-272]and it seemed to me that it was altogether no bigger than a grain of

[093-273]mustard seed, and that the men walking on it were little bigger than

[093-274]hazel nuts; so you may see how high we must have got to then.”

[093-275]To this the duchess said, “Sancho, my friend, mind what you are saying;

[093-276]it seems you could not have seen the earth, but only the men walking on

[093-277]it; for if the earth looked to you like a grain of mustard seed, and

[093-278]each man like a hazel nut, one man alone would have covered the whole

[093-279]earth.”

[093-280]“That is true,” said Sancho, “but for all that I got a glimpse of a bit

[093-281]of one side of it, and saw it all.”

[093-282]“Take care, Sancho,” said the duchess, “with a bit of one side one does

[093-283]not see the whole of what one looks at.”

[093-284]“I don’t understand that way of looking at things,” said Sancho; “I

[093-285]only know that your ladyship will do well to bear in mind that as we

[093-286]were flying by enchantment so I might have seen the whole earth and all

[093-287]the men by enchantment whatever way I looked; and if you won’t believe

[093-288]this, no more will you believe that, uncovering myself nearly to the

[093-289]eyebrows, I saw myself so close to the sky that there was not a palm

[093-290]and a half between me and it; and by everything that I can swear by,

[093-291]señora, it is mighty great! And it so happened we came by where the

[093-292]seven goats are, and by God and upon my soul, as in my youth I was a

[093-293]goatherd in my own country, as soon as I saw them I felt a longing to

[093-294]be among them for a little, and if I had not given way to it I think

[093-295]I’d have burst. So I come and take, and what do I do? without saying

[093-296]anything to anybody, not even to my master, softly and quietly I got

[093-297]down from Clavileño and amused myself with the goats—which are like

[093-298]violets, like flowers—for nigh three-quarters of an hour; and Clavileño

[093-299]never stirred or moved from one spot.”

[093-300]“And while the good Sancho was amusing himself with the goats,” said

[093-301]the duke, “how did Señor Don Quixote amuse himself?”

[093-302]To which Don Quixote replied, “As all these things and such like

[093-303]occurrences are out of the ordinary course of nature, it is no wonder

[093-304]that Sancho says what he does; for my own part I can only say that I

[093-305]did not uncover my eyes either above or below, nor did I see sky or

[093-306]earth or sea or shore. It is true I felt that I was passing through the

[093-307]region of the air, and even that I touched that of fire; but that we

[093-308]passed farther I cannot believe; for the region of fire being between

[093-309]the heaven of the moon and the last region of the air, we could not

[093-310]have reached that heaven where the seven goats Sancho speaks of are

[093-311]without being burned; and as we were not burned, either Sancho is lying

[093-312]or Sancho is dreaming.”

[093-313]“I am neither lying nor dreaming,” said Sancho; “only ask me the tokens

[093-314]of those same goats, and you’ll see by that whether I’m telling the

[093-315]truth or not.”

[093-316]“Tell us them then, Sancho,” said the duchess.

[093-317]“Two of them,” said Sancho, “are green, two blood-red, two blue, and

[093-318]one a mixture of all colours.”

[093-319]“An odd sort of goat, that,” said the duke; “in this earthly region of

[093-320]ours we have no such colours; I mean goats of such colours.”

[093-321]“That’s very plain,” said Sancho; “of course there must be a difference

[093-322]between the goats of heaven and the goats of the earth.”

[093-323]“Tell me, Sancho,” said the duke, “did you see any he-goat among those

[093-324]goats?”

[093-325]“No, señor,” said Sancho; “but I have heard say that none ever passed

[093-326]the horns of the moon.”

[093-327]They did not care to ask him anything more about his journey, for they

[093-328]saw he was in the vein to go rambling all over the heavens giving an

[093-329]account of everything that went on there, without having ever stirred

[093-330]from the garden. Such, in short, was the end of the adventure of the

[093-331]Distressed Duenna, which gave the duke and duchess laughing matter not

[093-332]only for the time being, but for all their lives, and Sancho something

[093-333]to talk about for ages, if he lived so long; but Don Quixote, coming

[093-334]close to his ear, said to him, “Sancho, as you would have us believe

[093-335]what you saw in heaven, I require you to believe me as to what I saw in

[093-336]the cave of Montesinos; I say no more.”
