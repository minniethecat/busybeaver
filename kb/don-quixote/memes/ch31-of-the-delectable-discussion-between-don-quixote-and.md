[031-003]“All that is not unsatisfactory to me,” said Don Quixote. “Go on; thou

[031-004]didst reach her; and what was that queen of beauty doing? Surely thou

[031-005]didst find her stringing pearls, or embroidering some device in gold

[031-006]thread for this her enslaved knight.”

[031-007]“I did not,” said Sancho, “but I found her winnowing two bushels of

[031-008]wheat in the yard of her house.”

[031-009]“Then depend upon it,” said Don Quixote, “the grains of that wheat were

[031-010]pearls when touched by her hands; and didst thou look, friend? was it

[031-011]white wheat or brown?”

[031-012]“It was neither, but red,” said Sancho.

[031-013]“Then I promise thee,” said Don Quixote, “that, winnowed by her hands,

[031-014]beyond a doubt the bread it made was of the whitest; but go on; when

[031-015]thou gavest her my letter, did she kiss it? Did she place it on her

[031-016]head? Did she perform any ceremony befitting it, or what did she do?”

[031-017]“When I went to give it to her,” replied Sancho, “she was hard at it

[031-018]swaying from side to side with a lot of wheat she had in the sieve, and

[031-019]she said to me, ‘Lay the letter, friend, on the top of that sack, for I

[031-020]cannot read it until I have done sifting all this.”

[031-021]“Discreet lady!” said Don Quixote; “that was in order to read it at her

[031-022]leisure and enjoy it; proceed, Sancho; while she was engaged in her

[031-023]occupation what converse did she hold with thee? What did she ask about

[031-024]me, and what answer didst thou give? Make haste; tell me all, and let

[031-025]not an atom be left behind in the ink-bottle.”

[031-026]“She asked me nothing,” said Sancho; “but I told her how your worship

[031-027]was left doing penance in her service, naked from the waist up, in

[031-028]among these mountains like a savage, sleeping on the ground, not eating

[031-029]bread off a tablecloth nor combing your beard, weeping and cursing your

[031-030]fortune.”

[031-031]“In saying I cursed my fortune thou saidst wrong,” said Don Quixote;

[031-032]“for rather do I bless it and shall bless it all the days of my life

[031-033]for having made me worthy of aspiring to love so lofty a lady as

[031-034]Dulcinea del Toboso.”

[031-035]“And so lofty she is,” said Sancho, “that she overtops me by more than

[031-036]a hand’s-breadth.”

[031-037]“What! Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “didst thou measure with her?”

[031-038]“I measured in this way,” said Sancho; “going to help her to put a sack

[031-039]of wheat on the back of an ass, we came so close together that I could

[031-040]see she stood more than a good palm over me.”

[031-041]“Well!” said Don Quixote, “and doth she not of a truth accompany and

[031-042]adorn this greatness with a thousand million charms of mind! But one

[031-043]thing thou wilt not deny, Sancho; when thou camest close to her didst

[031-044]thou not perceive a Sabæan odour, an aromatic fragrance, a, I know not

[031-045]what, delicious, that I cannot find a name for; I mean a redolence, an

[031-046]exhalation, as if thou wert in the shop of some dainty glover?”

[031-047]“All I can say is,” said Sancho, “that I did perceive a little odour,

[031-048]something goaty; it must have been that she was all in a sweat with

[031-049]hard work.”

[031-050]“It could not be that,” said Don Quixote, “but thou must have been

[031-051]suffering from cold in the head, or must have smelt thyself; for I know

[031-052]well what would be the scent of that rose among thorns, that lily of

[031-053]the field, that dissolved amber.”

[031-054]“Maybe so,” replied Sancho; “there often comes from myself that same

[031-055]odour which then seemed to me to come from her grace the lady Dulcinea;

[031-056]but that’s no wonder, for one devil is like another.”

[031-057]“Well then,” continued Don Quixote, “now she has done sifting the corn

[031-058]and sent it to the mill; what did she do when she read the letter?”

[031-059]“As for the letter,” said Sancho, “she did not read it, for she said

[031-060]she could neither read nor write; instead of that she tore it up into

[031-061]small pieces, saying that she did not want to let anyone read it lest

[031-062]her secrets should become known in the village, and that what I had

[031-063]told her by word of mouth about the love your worship bore her, and the

[031-064]extraordinary penance you were doing for her sake, was enough; and, to

[031-065]make an end of it, she told me to tell your worship that she kissed

[031-066]your hands, and that she had a greater desire to see you than to write

[031-067]to you; and that therefore she entreated and commanded you, on sight of

[031-068]this present, to come out of these thickets, and to have done with

[031-069]carrying on absurdities, and to set out at once for El Toboso, unless

[031-070]something else of greater importance should happen, for she had a great

[031-071]desire to see your worship. She laughed greatly when I told her how

[031-072]your worship was called The Knight of the Rueful Countenance; I asked

[031-073]her if that Biscayan the other day had been there; and she told me he

[031-074]had, and that he was an honest fellow; I asked her too about the galley

[031-075]slaves, but she said she had not seen any as yet.”

[031-076]“So far all goes well,” said Don Quixote; “but tell me what jewel was

[031-077]it that she gave thee on taking thy leave, in return for thy tidings of

[031-078]me? For it is a usual and ancient custom with knights and ladies errant

[031-079]to give the squires, damsels, or dwarfs who bring tidings of their

[031-080]ladies to the knights, or of their knights to the ladies, some rich

[031-081]jewel as a guerdon for good news, and acknowledgment of the message.”

[031-082]“That is very likely,” said Sancho, “and a good custom it was, to my

[031-083]mind; but that must have been in days gone by, for now it would seem to

[031-084]be the custom only to give a piece of bread and cheese; because that

[031-085]was what my lady Dulcinea gave me over the top of the yard-wall when I

[031-086]took leave of her; and more by token it was sheep’s-milk cheese.”

[031-087]“She is generous in the extreme,” said Don Quixote, “and if she did not

[031-088]give thee a jewel of gold, no doubt it must have been because she had

[031-089]not one to hand there to give thee; but sleeves are good after Easter;

[031-090]I shall see her and all shall be made right. But knowest thou what

[031-091]amazes me, Sancho? It seems to me thou must have gone and come through

[031-092]the air, for thou hast taken but little more than three days to go to

[031-093]El Toboso and return, though it is more than thirty leagues from here

[031-094]to there. From which I am inclined to think that the sage magician who

[031-095]is my friend, and watches over my interests (for of necessity there is

[031-096]and must be one, or else I should not be a right knight-errant), that

[031-097]this same, I say, must have helped thee to travel without thy

[031-098]knowledge; for some of these sages will catch up a knight-errant

[031-099]sleeping in his bed, and without his knowing how or in what way it

[031-100]happened, he wakes up the next day more than a thousand leagues away

[031-101]from the place where he went to sleep. And if it were not for this,

[031-102]knights-errant would not be able to give aid to one another in peril,

[031-103]as they do at every turn. For a knight, maybe, is fighting in the

[031-104]mountains of Armenia with some dragon, or fierce serpent, or another

[031-105]knight, and gets the worst of the battle, and is at the point of death;

[031-106]but when he least looks for it, there appears over against him on a

[031-107]cloud, or chariot of fire, another knight, a friend of his, who just

[031-108]before had been in England, and who takes his part, and delivers him

[031-109]from death; and at night he finds himself in his own quarters supping

[031-110]very much to his satisfaction; and yet from one place to the other will

[031-111]have been two or three thousand leagues. And all this is done by the

[031-112]craft and skill of the sage enchanters who take care of those valiant

[031-113]knights; so that, friend Sancho, I find no difficulty in believing that

[031-114]thou mayest have gone from this place to El Toboso and returned in such

[031-115]a short time, since, as I have said, some friendly sage must have

[031-116]carried thee through the air without thee perceiving it.”

[031-117]“That must have been it,” said Sancho, “for indeed Rocinante went like

[031-118]a gipsy’s ass with quicksilver in his ears.”

[031-119]“Quicksilver!” said Don Quixote, “aye and what is more, a legion of

[031-120]devils, folk that can travel and make others travel without being

[031-121]weary, exactly as the whim seizes them. But putting this aside, what

[031-122]thinkest thou I ought to do about my lady’s command to go and see her?

[031-123]For though I feel that I am bound to obey her mandate, I feel too that

[031-124]I am debarred by the boon I have accorded to the princess that

[031-125]accompanies us, and the law of chivalry compels me to have regard for

[031-126]my word in preference to my inclination; on the one hand the desire to

[031-127]see my lady pursues and harasses me, on the other my solemn promise and

[031-128]the glory I shall win in this enterprise urge and call me; but what I

[031-129]think I shall do is to travel with all speed and reach quickly the

[031-130]place where this giant is, and on my arrival I shall cut off his head,

[031-131]and establish the princess peacefully in her realm, and forthwith I

[031-132]shall return to behold the light that lightens my senses, to whom I

[031-133]shall make such excuses that she will be led to approve of my delay,

[031-134]for she will see that it entirely tends to increase her glory and fame;

[031-135]for all that I have won, am winning, or shall win by arms in this life,

[031-136]comes to me of the favour she extends to me, and because I am hers.”

[031-137]“Ah! what a sad state your worship’s brains are in!” said Sancho. “Tell

[031-138]me, señor, do you mean to travel all that way for nothing, and to let

[031-139]slip and lose so rich and great a match as this where they give as a

[031-140]portion a kingdom that in sober truth I have heard say is more than

[031-141]twenty thousand leagues round about, and abounds with all things

[031-142]necessary to support human life, and is bigger than Portugal and

[031-143]Castile put together? Peace, for the love of God! Blush for what you

[031-144]have said, and take my advice, and forgive me, and marry at once in the

[031-145]first village where there is a curate; if not, here is our licentiate

[031-146]who will do the business beautifully; remember, I am old enough to give

[031-147]advice, and this I am giving comes pat to the purpose; for a sparrow in

[031-148]the hand is better than a vulture on the wing, and he who has the good

[031-149]to his hand and chooses the bad, that the good he complains of may not

[031-150]come to him.”

[031-151]“Look here, Sancho,” said Don Quixote. “If thou art advising me to

[031-152]marry, in order that immediately on slaying the giant I may become

[031-153]king, and be able to confer favours on thee, and give thee what I have

[031-154]promised, let me tell thee I shall be able very easily to satisfy thy

[031-155]desires without marrying; for before going into battle I will make it a

[031-156]stipulation that, if I come out of it victorious, even I do not marry,

[031-157]they shall give me a portion of the kingdom, that I may bestow it upon

[031-158]whomsoever I choose, and when they give it to me upon whom wouldst thou

[031-159]have me bestow it but upon thee?”

[031-160]“That is plain speaking,” said Sancho; “but let your worship take care

[031-161]to choose it on the seacoast, so that if I don’t like the life, I may

[031-162]be able to ship off my black vassals and deal with them as I have said;

[031-163]don’t mind going to see my lady Dulcinea now, but go and kill this

[031-164]giant and let us finish off this business; for by God it strikes me it

[031-165]will be one of great honour and great profit.”

[031-166]“I hold thou art in the right of it, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “and I

[031-167]will take thy advice as to accompanying the princess before going to

[031-168]see Dulcinea; but I counsel thee not to say anything to anyone, or to

[031-169]those who are with us, about what we have considered and discussed, for

[031-170]as Dulcinea is so decorous that she does not wish her thoughts to be

[031-171]known it is not right that I or anyone for me should disclose them.”

[031-172]“Well then, if that be so,” said Sancho, “how is it that your worship

[031-173]makes all those you overcome by your arm go to present themselves

[031-174]before my lady Dulcinea, this being the same thing as signing your name

[031-175]to it that you love her and are her lover? And as those who go must

[031-176]perforce kneel before her and say they come from your worship to submit

[031-177]themselves to her, how can the thoughts of both of you be hid?”

[031-178]“O, how silly and simple thou art!” said Don Quixote; “seest thou not,

[031-179]Sancho, that this tends to her greater exaltation? For thou must know

[031-180]that according to our way of thinking in chivalry, it is a high honour

[031-181]to a lady to have many knights-errant in her service, whose thoughts

[031-182]never go beyond serving her for her own sake, and who look for no other

[031-183]reward for their great and true devotion than that she should be

[031-184]willing to accept them as her knights.”

[031-185]“It is with that kind of love,” said Sancho, “I have heard preachers

[031-186]say we ought to love our Lord, for himself alone, without being moved

[031-187]by the hope of glory or the fear of punishment; though for my part, I

[031-188]would rather love and serve him for what he could do.”

[031-189]“The devil take thee for a clown!” said Don Quixote, “and what shrewd

[031-190]things thou sayest at times! One would think thou hadst studied.”

[031-191]“In faith, then, I cannot even read.”

[031-192]Master Nicholas here called out to them to wait a while, as they wanted

[031-193]to halt and drink at a little spring there was there. Don Quixote drew

[031-194]up, not a little to the satisfaction of Sancho, for he was by this time

[031-195]weary of telling so many lies, and in dread of his master catching him

[031-196]tripping, for though he knew that Dulcinea was a peasant girl of El

[031-197]Toboso, he had never seen her in all his life. Cardenio had now put on

[031-198]the clothes which Dorothea was wearing when they found her, and though

[031-199]they were not very good, they were far better than those he put off.

[031-200]They dismounted together by the side of the spring, and with what the

[031-201]curate had provided himself with at the inn they appeased, though not

[031-202]very well, the keen appetite they all of them brought with them.

[031-203]While they were so employed there happened to come by a youth passing

[031-204]on his way, who stopping to examine the party at the spring, the next

[031-205]moment ran to Don Quixote and clasping him round the legs, began to

[031-206]weep freely, saying, “O, señor, do you not know me? Look at me well; I

[031-207]am that lad Andres that your worship released from the oak-tree where I

[031-208]was tied.”

[031-209]Don Quixote recognised him, and taking his hand he turned to those

[031-210]present and said: “That your worships may see how important it is to

[031-211]have knights-errant to redress the wrongs and injuries done by

[031-212]tyrannical and wicked men in this world, I may tell you that some days

[031-213]ago passing through a wood, I heard cries and piteous complaints as of

[031-214]a person in pain and distress; I immediately hastened, impelled by my

[031-215]bounden duty, to the quarter whence the plaintive accents seemed to me

[031-216]to proceed, and I found tied to an oak this lad who now stands before

[031-217]you, which in my heart I rejoice at, for his testimony will not permit

[031-218]me to depart from the truth in any particular. He was, I say, tied to

[031-219]an oak, naked from the waist up, and a clown, whom I afterwards found

[031-220]to be his master, was scarifying him by lashes with the reins of his

[031-221]mare. As soon as I saw him I asked the reason of so cruel a

[031-222]flagellation. The boor replied that he was flogging him because he was

[031-223]his servant and because of carelessness that proceeded rather from

[031-224]dishonesty than stupidity; on which this boy said, ‘Señor, he flogs me

[031-225]only because I ask for my wages.’ The master made I know not what

[031-226]speeches and explanations, which, though I listened to them, I did not

[031-227]accept. In short, I compelled the clown to unbind him, and to swear he

[031-228]would take him with him, and pay him real by real, and perfumed into

[031-229]the bargain. Is not all this true, Andres my son? Didst thou not mark

[031-230]with what authority I commanded him, and with what humility he promised

[031-231]to do all I enjoined, specified, and required of him? Answer without

[031-232]hesitation; tell these gentlemen what took place, that they may see

[031-233]that it is as great an advantage as I say to have knights-errant

[031-234]abroad.”

[031-235]“All that your worship has said is quite true,” answered the lad; “but

[031-236]the end of the business turned out just the opposite of what your

[031-237]worship supposes.”

[031-238]“How! the opposite?” said Don Quixote; “did not the clown pay thee

[031-239]then?”

[031-240]“Not only did he not pay me,” replied the lad, “but as soon as your

[031-241]worship had passed out of the wood and we were alone, he tied me up

[031-242]again to the same oak and gave me a fresh flogging, that left me like a

[031-243]flayed Saint Bartholomew; and every stroke he gave me he followed up

[031-244]with some jest or gibe about having made a fool of your worship, and

[031-245]but for the pain I was suffering I should have laughed at the things he

[031-246]said. In short he left me in such a condition that I have been until

[031-247]now in a hospital getting cured of the injuries which that rascally

[031-248]clown inflicted on me then; for all which your worship is to blame; for

[031-249]if you had gone your own way and not come where there was no call for

[031-250]you, nor meddled in other people’s affairs, my master would have been

[031-251]content with giving me one or two dozen lashes, and would have then

[031-252]loosed me and paid me what he owed me; but when your worship abused him

[031-253]so out of measure, and gave him so many hard words, his anger was

[031-254]kindled; and as he could not revenge himself on you, as soon as he saw

[031-255]you had left him the storm burst upon me in such a way, that I feel as

[031-256]if I should never be a man again.”

[031-257]“The mischief,” said Don Quixote, “lay in my going away; for I should

[031-258]not have gone until I had seen thee paid; because I ought to have known

[031-259]well by long experience that there is no clown who will keep his word

[031-260]if he finds it will not suit him to keep it; but thou rememberest,

[031-261]Andres, that I swore if he did not pay thee I would go and seek him,

[031-262]and find him though he were to hide himself in the whale’s belly.”

[031-263]“That is true,” said Andres; “but it was of no use.”

[031-264]“Thou shalt see now whether it is of use or not,” said Don Quixote; and

[031-265]so saying, he got up hastily and bade Sancho bridle Rocinante, who was

[031-266]browsing while they were eating. Dorothea asked him what he meant to

[031-267]do. He replied that he meant to go in search of this clown and chastise

[031-268]him for such iniquitous conduct, and see Andres paid to the last

[031-269]maravedi, despite and in the teeth of all the clowns in the world. To

[031-270]which she replied that he must remember that in accordance with his

[031-271]promise he could not engage in any enterprise until he had concluded

[031-272]hers; and that as he knew this better than anyone, he should restrain

[031-273]his ardour until his return from her kingdom.

[031-274]“That is true,” said Don Quixote, “and Andres must have patience until

[031-275]my return as you say, señora; but I once more swear and promise not to

[031-276]stop until I have seen him avenged and paid.”

[031-277]“I have no faith in those oaths,” said Andres; “I would rather have now

[031-278]something to help me to get to Seville than all the revenges in the

[031-279]world; if you have here anything to eat that I can take with me, give

[031-280]it me, and God be with your worship and all knights-errant; and may

[031-281]their errands turn out as well for themselves as they have for me.”

[031-282]Sancho took out from his store a piece of bread and another of cheese,

[031-283]and giving them to the lad he said, “Here, take this, brother Andres,

[031-284]for we have all of us a share in your misfortune.”

[031-285]“Why, what share have you got?”

[031-286]“This share of bread and cheese I am giving you,” answered Sancho; “and

[031-287]God knows whether I shall feel the want of it myself or not; for I

[031-288]would have you know, friend, that we squires to knights-errant have to

[031-289]bear a great deal of hunger and hard fortune, and even other things

[031-290]more easily felt than told.”

[031-291]Andres seized his bread and cheese, and seeing that nobody gave him

[031-292]anything more, bent his head, and took hold of the road, as the saying

[031-293]is. However, before leaving he said, “For the love of God, sir

[031-294]knight-errant, if you ever meet me again, though you may see them

[031-295]cutting me to pieces, give me no aid or succour, but leave me to my

[031-296]misfortune, which will not be so great but that a greater will come to

[031-297]me by being helped by your worship, on whom and all the knights-errant

[031-298]that have ever been born God send his curse.”

[031-299]Don Quixote was getting up to chastise him, but he took to his heels at

[031-300]such a pace that no one attempted to follow him; and mightily

[031-301]chapfallen was Don Quixote at Andres’ story, and the others had to take

[031-302]great care to restrain their laughter so as not to put him entirely out

[031-303]of countenance.
