## Overview

Master Nicholas is the barber-surgeon of the unnamed village in La Mancha where Alonso Quijano lives. Along with the curate Pero Pérez, he forms the rationalist foil to Don Quixote's romantic delusions. He participates in the famous scrutiny of the library (Chapter 6), where his practical sensibility contrasts with the curate's bookish knowledge — Nicholas is the one who knows which chivalric romances were popular in the village. He also plays a crucial role in the rescue mission to the Sierra Morena, disguising himself as a distressed damsel (with a beard made of an ox-tail) to lure Don Quixote back to the village [027-000].

## In Don Quixote

Master Nicholas appears throughout Part I as a problem-solver. In the library scrutiny, he serves as the curate's assistant, handing books down one by one for judgment [006-012]. His literary opinions are more populist than the curate's — he knows which books the villagers read and borrows a volume of *Don Cirongilio of Thrace* that the curate condemns. In the Sierra Morena arc, Nicholas and the curate devise the plan to disguise themselves and persuade Don Quixote to return home. Nicholas takes the lead in costuming, donning a skirt and padding to play the part of a princess in distress — a comic performance that underscores the novel's theme of identity as performance [027-000]. His most memorable moment may be at the inn in Chapters 44–45, where he becomes entangled in the debate over Mambrino's helmet (the barber's basin), defending his fellow barber and insisting on the basin interpretation.

## Key Passages

> "He then directed the barber to give him the books one by one, so that they might see what they were about, for there might be some among them that did not deserve the punishment of the fire." [006-012]

> "'Nay, sir,' said the barber, 'I too have heard say that it is the best of all the books of this kind that have been written; and so, as something exceptional in its line, it should be pardoned.'" [006-029]

## Relationship to Don Quixote

Master Nicholas is a friend of Alonso Quijano, not a villain. His participation in the book-burning, the rescue mission, and the various schemes to bring Don Quixote home is motivated by genuine concern. Unlike some of the novel's crueler jesters (the Duke and Duchess in Part II), Nicholas and the curate are trying to cure a friend. The question Cervantes subtly raises — whether the cure is worse than the disease, whether Don Quixote's madness is more life-giving than the village's sanity — applies to Nicholas as much as to anyone else in the novel.
