[117-001]WHEREIN IS MADE KNOWN WHO THE KNIGHT OF THE WHITE MOON WAS; LIKEWISE

[117-002]DON GREGORIO’S RELEASE, AND OTHER EVENTS

p65a.jpg (149K)


[117-003]Don Antonio Moreno followed the Knight of the White Moon, and a number

[117-004]of boys followed him too, nay pursued him, until they had him fairly

[117-005]housed in a hostel in the heart of the city. Don Antonio, eager to make

[117-006]his acquaintance, entered also; a squire came out to meet him and

[117-007]remove his armour, and he shut himself into a lower room, still

[117-008]attended by Don Antonio, whose bread would not bake until he had found

[117-009]out who he was. He of the White Moon, seeing then that the gentleman

[117-010]would not leave him, said, “I know very well, señor, what you have come

[117-011]for; it is to find out who I am; and as there is no reason why I should

[117-012]conceal it from you, while my servant here is taking off my armour I

[117-013]will tell you the true state of the case, without leaving out anything.

[117-014]You must know, señor, that I am called the bachelor Samson Carrasco. I

[117-015]am of the same village as Don Quixote of La Mancha, whose craze and

[117-016]folly make all of us who know him feel pity for him, and I am one of

[117-017]those who have felt it most; and persuaded that his chance of recovery

[117-018]lay in quiet and keeping at home and in his own house, I hit upon a

[117-019]device for keeping him there. Three months ago, therefore, I went out

[117-020]to meet him as a knight-errant, under the assumed name of the Knight of

[117-021]the Mirrors, intending to engage him in combat and overcome him without

[117-022]hurting him, making it the condition of our combat that the vanquished

[117-023]should be at the disposal of the victor. What I meant to demand of him

[117-024](for I regarded him as vanquished already) was that he should return to

[117-025]his own village, and not leave it for a whole year, by which time he

[117-026]might be cured. But fate ordered it otherwise, for he vanquished me and

[117-027]unhorsed me, and so my plan failed. He went his way, and I came back

[117-028]conquered, covered with shame, and sorely bruised by my fall, which was

[117-029]a particularly dangerous one. But this did not quench my desire to meet

[117-030]him again and overcome him, as you have seen to-day. And as he is so

[117-031]scrupulous in his observance of the laws of knight-errantry, he will,

[117-032]no doubt, in order to keep his word, obey the injunction I have laid

[117-033]upon him. This, señor, is how the matter stands, and I have nothing

[117-034]more to tell you. I implore of you not to betray me, or tell Don

[117-035]Quixote who I am; so that my honest endeavours may be successful, and

[117-036]that a man of excellent wits—were he only rid of the fooleries of

[117-037]chivalry—may get them back again.”

[117-038]“O señor,” said Don Antonio, “may God forgive you the wrong you have

[117-039]done the whole world in trying to bring the most amusing madman in it

[117-040]back to his senses. Do you not see, señor, that the gain by Don

[117-041]Quixote’s sanity can never equal the enjoyment his crazes give? But my

[117-042]belief is that all the señor bachelor’s pains will be of no avail to

[117-043]bring a man so hopelessly cracked to his senses again; and if it were

[117-044]not uncharitable, I would say may Don Quixote never be cured, for by

[117-045]his recovery we lose not only his own drolleries, but his squire Sancho

[117-046]Panza’s too, any one of which is enough to turn melancholy itself into

[117-047]merriment. However, I’ll hold my peace and say nothing to him, and

[117-048]we’ll see whether I am right in my suspicion that Señor Carrasco’s

[117-049]efforts will be fruitless.”

[117-050]The bachelor replied that at all events the affair promised well, and

[117-051]he hoped for a happy result from it; and putting his services at Don

[117-052]Antonio’s commands he took his leave of him; and having had his armour

[117-053]packed at once upon a mule, he rode away from the city the same day on

[117-054]the horse he rode to battle, and returned to his own country without

[117-055]meeting any adventure calling for record in this veracious history.

[117-056]Don Antonio reported to the viceroy what Carrasco told him, and the

[117-057]viceroy was not very well pleased to hear it, for with Don Quixote’s

[117-058]retirement there was an end to the amusement of all who knew anything

[117-059]of his mad doings.

[117-060]Six days did Don Quixote keep his bed, dejected, melancholy, moody and

[117-061]out of sorts, brooding over the unhappy event of his defeat. Sancho

[117-062]strove to comfort him, and among other things he said to him, “Hold up

[117-063]your head, señor, and be of good cheer if you can, and give thanks to

[117-064]heaven that if you have had a tumble to the ground you have not come

[117-065]off with a broken rib; and, as you know that ‘where they give they

[117-066]take,’ and that ‘there are not always fletches where there are pegs,’ a

[117-067]fig for the doctor, for there’s no need of him to cure this ailment.

[117-068]Let us go home, and give over going about in search of adventures in

[117-069]strange lands and places; rightly looked at, it is I that am the

[117-070]greater loser, though it is your worship that has had the worse usage.

[117-071]With the government I gave up all wish to be a governor again, but I

[117-072]did not give up all longing to be a count; and that will never come to

[117-073]pass if your worship gives up becoming a king by renouncing the calling

[117-074]of chivalry; and so my hopes are going to turn into smoke.”

[117-075]“Peace, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “thou seest my suspension and

[117-076]retirement is not to exceed a year; I shall soon return to my honoured

[117-077]calling, and I shall not be at a loss for a kingdom to win and a county

[117-078]to bestow on thee.”

[117-079]“May God hear it and sin be deaf,” said Sancho; “I have always heard

[117-080]say that ‘a good hope is better than a bad holding.”

[117-081]As they were talking Don Antonio came in looking extremely pleased and

[117-082]exclaiming, “Reward me for my good news, Señor Don Quixote! Don

[117-083]Gregorio and the renegade who went for him have come ashore—ashore do I

[117-084]say? They are by this time in the viceroy’s house, and will be here

[117-085]immediately.”

[117-086]Don Quixote cheered up a little and said, “Of a truth I am almost ready

[117-087]to say I should have been glad had it turned out just the other way,

[117-088]for it would have obliged me to cross over to Barbary, where by the

[117-089]might of my arm I should have restored to liberty, not only Don

[117-090]Gregorio, but all the Christian captives there are in Barbary. But what

[117-091]am I saying, miserable being that I am? Am I not he that has been

[117-092]conquered? Am I not he that has been overthrown? Am I not he who must

[117-093]not take up arms for a year? Then what am I making professions for;

[117-094]what am I bragging about; when it is fitter for me to handle the

[117-095]distaff than the sword?”

[117-096]“No more of that, señor,” said Sancho; “‘let the hen live, even though

[117-097]it be with her pip;’ ‘to-day for thee and to-morrow for me;’ in these

[117-098]affairs of encounters and whacks one must not mind them, for he that

[117-099]falls to-day may get up to-morrow; unless indeed he chooses to lie in

[117-100]bed, I mean gives way to weakness and does not pluck up fresh spirit

[117-101]for fresh battles; let your worship get up now to receive Don Gregorio;

[117-102]for the household seems to be in a bustle, and no doubt he has come by

[117-103]this time;” and so it proved, for as soon as Don Gregorio and the

[117-104]renegade had given the viceroy an account of the voyage out and home,

[117-105]Don Gregorio, eager to see Ana Felix, came with the renegade to Don

[117-106]Antonio’s house. When they carried him away from Algiers he was in

[117-107]woman’s dress; on board the vessel, however, he exchanged it for that

[117-108]of a captive who escaped with him; but in whatever dress he might be he

[117-109]looked like one to be loved and served and esteemed, for he was

[117-110]surpassingly well-favoured, and to judge by appearances some seventeen

[117-111]or eighteen years of age. Ricote and his daughter came out to welcome

[117-112]him, the father with tears, the daughter with bashfulness. They did not

[117-113]embrace each other, for where there is deep love there will never be

[117-114]overmuch boldness. Seen side by side, the comeliness of Don Gregorio

[117-115]and the beauty of Ana Felix were the admiration of all who were

[117-116]present. It was silence that spoke for the lovers at that moment, and

[117-117]their eyes were the tongues that declared their pure and happy

[117-118]feelings. The renegade explained the measures and means he had adopted

[117-119]to rescue Don Gregorio, and Don Gregorio at no great length, but in a

[117-120]few words, in which he showed that his intelligence was in advance of

[117-121]his years, described the peril and embarrassment he found himself in

[117-122]among the women with whom he had sojourned. To conclude, Ricote

[117-123]liberally recompensed and rewarded as well the renegade as the men who

[117-124]had rowed; and the renegade effected his readmission into the body of

[117-125]the Church and was reconciled with it, and from a rotten limb became by

[117-126]penance and repentance a clean and sound one.

[117-127]Two days later the viceroy discussed with Don Antonio the steps they

[117-128]should take to enable Ana Felix and her father to stay in Spain, for it

[117-129]seemed to them there could be no objection to a daughter who was so

[117-130]good a Christian and a father to all appearance so well disposed

[117-131]remaining there. Don Antonio offered to arrange the matter at the

[117-132]capital, whither he was compelled to go on some other business, hinting

[117-133]that many a difficult affair was settled there with the help of favour

[117-134]and bribes.

[117-135]“Nay,” said Ricote, who was present during the conversation, “it will

[117-136]not do to rely upon favour or bribes, because with the great Don

[117-137]Bernardino de Velasco, Conde de Salazar, to whom his Majesty has

[117-138]entrusted our expulsion, neither entreaties nor promises, bribes nor

[117-139]appeals to compassion, are of any use; for though it is true he mingles

[117-140]mercy with justice, still, seeing that the whole body of our nation is

[117-141]tainted and corrupt, he applies to it the cautery that burns rather

[117-142]than the salve that soothes; and thus, by prudence, sagacity, care and

[117-143]the fear he inspires, he has borne on his mighty shoulders the weight

[117-144]of this great policy and carried it into effect, all our schemes and

[117-145]plots, importunities and wiles, being ineffectual to blind his Argus

[117-146]eyes, ever on the watch lest one of us should remain behind in

[117-147]concealment, and like a hidden root come in course of time to sprout

[117-148]and bear poisonous fruit in Spain, now cleansed, and relieved of the

[117-149]fear in which our vast numbers kept it. Heroic resolve of the great

[117-150]Philip the Third, and unparalleled wisdom to have entrusted it to the

[117-151]said Don Bernardino de Velasco!”

[117-152]“At any rate,” said Don Antonio, “when I am there I will make all

[117-153]possible efforts, and let heaven do as pleases it best; Don Gregorio

[117-154]will come with me to relieve the anxiety which his parents must be

[117-155]suffering on account of his absence; Ana Felix will remain in my house

[117-156]with my wife, or in a monastery; and I know the viceroy will be glad

[117-157]that the worthy Ricote should stay with him until we see what terms I

[117-158]can make.”

[117-159]The viceroy agreed to all that was proposed; but Don Gregorio on

[117-160]learning what had passed declared he could not and would not on any

[117-161]account leave Ana Felix; however, as it was his purpose to go and see

[117-162]his parents and devise some way of returning for her, he fell in with

[117-163]the proposed arrangement. Ana Felix remained with Don Antonio’s wife,

[117-164]and Ricote in the viceroy’s house.

[117-165]The day for Don Antonio’s departure came; and two days later that for

[117-166]Don Quixote’s and Sancho’s, for Don Quixote’s fall did not suffer him

[117-167]to take the road sooner. There were tears and sighs, swoonings and

[117-168]sobs, at the parting between Don Gregorio and Ana Felix. Ricote offered

[117-169]Don Gregorio a thousand crowns if he would have them, but he would not

[117-170]take any save five which Don Antonio lent him and he promised to repay

[117-171]at the capital. So the two of them took their departure, and Don

[117-172]Quixote and Sancho afterwards, as has been already said, Don Quixote

[117-173]without his armour and in travelling gear, and Sancho on foot, Dapple

[117-174]being loaded with the armour.
