[078-001]WHEREIN IS CONTINUED THE DROLL ADVENTURE OF THE PUPPET-SHOWMAN,

TOGETHER WITH OTHER THINGS IN TRUTH RIGHT GOOD

p26a.jpg (157K)


[078-002]All were silent, Tyrians and Trojans; I mean all who were watching the

[078-003]show were hanging on the lips of the interpreter of its wonders, when

[078-004]drums and trumpets were heard to sound inside it and cannon to go off.

[078-005]The noise was soon over, and then the boy lifted up his voice and said,

[078-006]“This true story which is here represented to your worships is taken

[078-007]word for word from the French chronicles and from the Spanish ballads

[078-008]that are in everybody’s mouth, and in the mouth of the boys about the

[078-009]streets. Its subject is the release by Señor Don Gaiferos of his wife

[078-010]Melisendra, when a captive in Spain at the hands of the Moors in the

[078-011]city of Sansueña, for so they called then what is now called Saragossa;

[078-012]and there you may see how Don Gaiferos is playing at the tables, just

[078-013]as they sing it—

[078-014]At tables playing Don Gaiferos sits,

[078-015]For Melisendra is forgotten now.

[078-016]And that personage who appears there with a crown on his head and a

[078-017]sceptre in his hand is the Emperor Charlemagne, the supposed father of

[078-018]Melisendra, who, angered to see his son-in-law’s inaction and

[078-019]unconcern, comes in to chide him; and observe with what vehemence and

[078-020]energy he chides him, so that you would fancy he was going to give him

[078-021]half a dozen raps with his sceptre; and indeed there are authors who

[078-022]say he did give them, and sound ones too; and after having said a great

[078-023]deal to him about imperilling his honour by not effecting the release

[078-024]of his wife, he said, so the tale runs,

[078-025]Enough I’ve said, see to it now.

[078-026]Observe, too, how the emperor turns away, and leaves Don Gaiferos

[078-027]fuming; and you see now how in a burst of anger, he flings the table

[078-028]and the board far from him and calls in haste for his armour, and asks

[078-029]his cousin Don Roland for the loan of his sword, Durindana, and how Don

[078-030]Roland refuses to lend it, offering him his company in the difficult

[078-031]enterprise he is undertaking; but he, in his valour and anger, will not

[078-032]accept it, and says that he alone will suffice to rescue his wife, even

[078-033]though she were imprisoned deep in the centre of the earth, and with

[078-034]this he retires to arm himself and set out on his journey at once. Now

[078-035]let your worships turn your eyes to that tower that appears there,

[078-036]which is supposed to be one of the towers of the alcazar of Saragossa,

[078-037]now called the Aljaferia; that lady who appears on that balcony dressed

[078-038]in Moorish fashion is the peerless Melisendra, for many a time she used

[078-039]to gaze from thence upon the road to France, and seek consolation in

[078-040]her captivity by thinking of Paris and her husband. Observe, too, a new

[078-041]incident which now occurs, such as, perhaps, never was seen. Do you not

[078-042]see that Moor, who silently and stealthily, with his finger on his lip,

[078-043]approaches Melisendra from behind? Observe now how he prints a kiss

[078-044]upon her lips, and what a hurry she is in to spit, and wipe them with

[078-045]the white sleeve of her smock, and how she bewails herself, and tears

[078-046]her fair hair as though it were to blame for the wrong. Observe, too,

[078-047]that the stately Moor who is in that corridor is King Marsilio of

[078-048]Sansueña, who, having seen the Moor’s insolence, at once orders him

[078-049](though his kinsman and a great favourite of his) to be seized and

[078-050]given two hundred lashes, while carried through the streets of the city

[078-051]according to custom, with criers going before him and officers of

[078-052]justice behind; and here you see them come out to execute the sentence,

[078-053]although the offence has been scarcely committed; for among the Moors

[078-054]there are no indictments nor remands as with us.”

[078-055]Here Don Quixote called out, “Child, child, go straight on with your

[078-056]story, and don’t run into curves and slants, for to establish a fact

[078-057]clearly there is need of a great deal of proof and confirmation;” and

[078-058]said Master Pedro from within, “Boy, stick to your text and do as the

[078-059]gentleman bids you; it’s the best plan; keep to your plain song, and

[078-060]don’t attempt harmonies, for they are apt to break down from being over

[078-061]fine.”

[078-062]“I will,” said the boy, and he went on to say, “This figure that you

[078-063]see here on horseback, covered with a Gascon cloak, is Don Gaiferos

[078-064]himself, whom his wife, now avenged of the insult of the amorous Moor,

[078-065]and taking her stand on the balcony of the tower with a calmer and more

[078-066]tranquil countenance, has perceived without recognising him; and she

[078-067]addresses her husband, supposing him to be some traveller, and holds

[078-068]with him all that conversation and colloquy in the ballad that runs—

[078-069]If you, sir knight, to France are bound,

[078-070]Oh! for Gaiferos ask—

[078-071]which I do not repeat here because prolixity begets disgust; suffice it

[078-072]to observe how Don Gaiferos discovers himself, and that by her joyful

[078-073]gestures Melisendra shows us she has recognised him; and what is more,

[078-074]we now see she lowers herself from the balcony to place herself on the

[078-075]haunches of her good husband’s horse. But ah! unhappy lady, the edge of

[078-076]her petticoat has caught on one of the bars of the balcony and she is

[078-077]left hanging in the air, unable to reach the ground. But you see how

[078-078]compassionate heaven sends aid in our sorest need; Don Gaiferos

[078-079]advances, and without minding whether the rich petticoat is torn or

[078-080]not, he seizes her and by force brings her to the ground, and then with

[078-081]one jerk places her on the haunches of his horse, astraddle like a man,

[078-082]and bids her hold on tight and clasp her arms round his neck, crossing

[078-083]them on his breast so as not to fall, for the lady Melisendra was not

[078-084]used to that style of riding. You see, too, how the neighing of the

[078-085]horse shows his satisfaction with the gallant and beautiful burden he

[078-086]bears in his lord and lady. You see how they wheel round and quit the

[078-087]city, and in joy and gladness take the road to Paris. Go in peace, O

[078-088]peerless pair of true lovers! May you reach your longed-for fatherland

[078-089]in safety, and may fortune interpose no impediment to your prosperous

[078-090]journey; may the eyes of your friends and kinsmen behold you enjoying

[078-091]in peace and tranquillity the remaining days of your life—and that they

[078-092]may be as many as those of Nestor!”

[078-093]Here Master Pedro called out again and said, “Simplicity, boy! None of

[078-094]your high flights; all affectation is bad.”

[078-095]The interpreter made no answer, but went on to say, “There was no want

[078-096]of idle eyes, that see everything, to see Melisendra come down and

[078-097]mount, and word was brought to King Marsilio, who at once gave orders

[078-098]to sound the alarm; and see what a stir there is, and how the city is

[078-099]drowned with the sound of the bells pealing in the towers of all the

[078-100]mosques.”

[078-101]“Nay, nay,” said Don Quixote at this; “on that point of the bells

[078-102]Master Pedro is very inaccurate, for bells are not in use among the

[078-103]Moors; only kettledrums, and a kind of small trumpet somewhat like our

[078-104]clarion; to ring bells this way in Sansueña is unquestionably a great

[078-105]absurdity.”

[078-106]On hearing this, Master Pedro stopped ringing, and said, “Don’t look

[078-107]into trifles, Señor Don Quixote, or want to have things up to a pitch

[078-108]of perfection that is out of reach. Are there not almost every day a

[078-109]thousand comedies represented all round us full of thousands of

[078-110]inaccuracies and absurdities, and, for all that, they have a successful

[078-111]run, and are listened to not only with applause, but with admiration

[078-112]and all the rest of it? Go on, boy, and don’t mind; for so long as I

[078-113]fill my pouch, no matter if I show as many inaccuracies as there are

[078-114]motes in a sunbeam.”

[078-115]“True enough,” said Don Quixote; and the boy went on: “See what a

[078-116]numerous and glittering crowd of horsemen issues from the city in

[078-117]pursuit of the two faithful lovers, what a blowing of trumpets there

[078-118]is, what sounding of horns, what beating of drums and tabors; I fear me

[078-119]they will overtake them and bring them back tied to the tail of their

[078-120]own horse, which would be a dreadful sight.”

[078-121]Don Quixote, however, seeing such a swarm of Moors and hearing such a

[078-122]din, thought it would be right to aid the fugitives, and standing up he

[078-123]exclaimed in a loud voice, “Never, while I live, will I permit foul

[078-124]play to be practised in my presence on such a famous knight and

[078-125]fearless lover as Don Gaiferos. Halt! ill-born rabble, follow him not

[078-126]nor pursue him, or ye will have to reckon with me in battle!” and

[078-127]suiting the action to the word, he drew his sword, and with one bound

[078-128]placed himself close to the show, and with unexampled rapidity and fury

[078-129]began to shower down blows on the puppet troop of Moors, knocking over

[078-130]some, decapitating others, maiming this one and demolishing that; and

[078-131]among many more he delivered one down stroke which, if Master Pedro had

[078-132]not ducked, made himself small, and got out of the way, would have

[078-133]sliced off his head as easily as if it had been made of almond-paste.

[078-134]Master Pedro kept shouting, “Hold hard! Señor Don Quixote! can’t you

[078-135]see they’re not real Moors you’re knocking down and killing and

[078-136]destroying, but only little pasteboard figures! Look—sinner that I

[078-137]am!—how you’re wrecking and ruining all that I’m worth!” But in spite

[078-138]of this, Don Quixote did not leave off discharging a continuous rain of

[078-139]cuts, slashes, downstrokes, and backstrokes, and at length, in less

[078-140]than the space of two credos, he brought the whole show to the ground,

[078-141]with all its fittings and figures shivered and knocked to pieces, King

[078-142]Marsilio badly wounded, and the Emperor Charlemagne with his crown and

[078-143]head split in two. The whole audience was thrown into confusion, the

[078-144]ape fled to the roof of the inn, the cousin was frightened, and even

[078-145]Sancho Panza himself was in mighty fear, for, as he swore after the

[078-146]storm was over, he had never seen his master in such a furious passion.

[078-147]The complete destruction of the show being thus accomplished, Don

[078-148]Quixote became a little calmer, said, “I wish I had here before me now

[078-149]all those who do not or will not believe how useful knights-errant are

[078-150]in the world; just think, if I had not been here present, what would

[078-151]have become of the brave Don Gaiferos and the fair Melisendra! Depend

[078-152]upon it, by this time those dogs would have overtaken them and

[078-153]inflicted some outrage upon them. So, then, long live knight-errantry

[078-154]beyond everything living on earth this day!”

[078-155]“Let it live, and welcome,” said Master Pedro at this in a feeble

[078-156]voice, “and let me die, for I am so unfortunate that I can say with

[078-157]King Don Rodrigo—

[078-158]Yesterday was I lord of Spain

[078-159]To-day I’ve not a turret left

[078-160]That I may call mine own.

[078-161]Not half an hour, nay, barely a minute ago, I saw myself lord of kings

[078-162]and emperors, with my stables filled with countless horses, and my

[078-163]trunks and bags with gay dresses unnumbered; and now I find myself

[078-164]ruined and laid low, destitute and a beggar, and above all without my

[078-165]ape, for, by my faith, my teeth will have to sweat for it before I have

[078-166]him caught; and all through the reckless fury of sir knight here, who,

[078-167]they say, protects the fatherless, and rights wrongs, and does other

[078-168]charitable deeds; but whose generous intentions have been found wanting

[078-169]in my case only, blessed and praised be the highest heavens! Verily,

[078-170]knight of the rueful figure he must be to have disfigured mine.”

[078-171]Sancho Panza was touched by Master Pedro’s words, and said to him,

[078-172]“Don’t weep and lament, Master Pedro; you break my heart; let me tell

[078-173]you my master, Don Quixote, is so catholic and scrupulous a Christian

[078-174]that, if he can make out that he has done you any wrong, he will own

[078-175]it, and be willing to pay for it and make it good, and something over

[078-176]and above.”

[078-177]“Only let Señor Don Quixote pay me for some part of the work he has

[078-178]destroyed,” said Master Pedro, “and I would be content, and his worship

[078-179]would ease his conscience, for he cannot be saved who keeps what is

[078-180]another’s against the owner’s will, and makes no restitution.”

[078-181]“That is true,” said Don Quixote; “but at present I am not aware that I

[078-182]have got anything of yours, Master Pedro.”

[078-183]“What!” returned Master Pedro; “and these relics lying here on the bare

[078-184]hard ground—what scattered and shattered them but the invincible

[078-185]strength of that mighty arm? And whose were the bodies they belonged to

[078-186]but mine? And what did I get my living by but by them?”

[078-187]“Now am I fully convinced,” said Don Quixote, “of what I had many a

[078-188]time before believed; that the enchanters who persecute me do nothing

[078-189]more than put figures like these before my eyes, and then change and

[078-190]turn them into what they please. In truth and earnest, I assure you

[078-191]gentlemen who now hear me, that to me everything that has taken place

[078-192]here seemed to take place literally, that Melisendra was Melisendra,

[078-193]Don Gaiferos Don Gaiferos, Marsilio Marsilio, and Charlemagne

[078-194]Charlemagne. That was why my anger was roused; and to be faithful to my

[078-195]calling as a knight-errant I sought to give aid and protection to those

[078-196]who fled, and with this good intention I did what you have seen. If the

[078-197]result has been the opposite of what I intended, it is no fault of

[078-198]mine, but of those wicked beings that persecute me; but, for all that,

[078-199]I am willing to condemn myself in costs for this error of mine, though

[078-200]it did not proceed from malice; let Master Pedro see what he wants for

[078-201]the spoiled figures, for I agree to pay it at once in good and current

[078-202]money of Castile.”

[078-203]Master Pedro made him a bow, saying, “I expected no less of the rare

[078-204]Christianity of the valiant Don Quixote of La Mancha, true helper and

[078-205]protector of all destitute and needy vagabonds; master landlord here

[078-206]and the great Sancho Panza shall be the arbitrators and appraisers

[078-207]between your worship and me of what these dilapidated figures are worth

[078-208]or may be worth.”

[078-209]The landlord and Sancho consented, and then Master Pedro picked up from

[078-210]the ground King Marsilio of Saragossa with his head off, and said,

[078-211]“Here you see how impossible it is to restore this king to his former

[078-212]state, so I think, saving your better judgments, that for his death,

[078-213]decease, and demise, four reals and a half may be given me.”

[078-214]“Proceed,” said Don Quixote.

[078-215]“Well then, for this cleavage from top to bottom,” continued Master

[078-216]Pedro, taking up the split Emperor Charlemagne, “it would not be much

[078-217]if I were to ask five reals and a quarter.”

[078-218]“It’s not little,” said Sancho.

[078-219]“Nor is it much,” said the landlord; “make it even, and say five

[078-220]reals.”

[078-221]“Let him have the whole five and a quarter,” said Don Quixote; “for the

[078-222]sum total of this notable disaster does not stand on a quarter more or

[078-223]less; and make an end of it quickly, Master Pedro, for it’s getting on

[078-224]to supper-time, and I have some hints of hunger.”

[078-225]“For this figure,” said Master Pedro, “that is without a nose, and

[078-226]wants an eye, and is the fair Melisendra, I ask, and I am reasonable in

[078-227]my charge, two reals and twelve maravedis.”

[078-228]“The very devil must be in it,” said Don Quixote, “if Melisendra and

[078-229]her husband are not by this time at least on the French border, for the

[078-230]horse they rode on seemed to me to fly rather than gallop; so you

[078-231]needn’t try to sell me the cat for the hare, showing me here a noseless

[078-232]Melisendra when she is now, may be, enjoying herself at her ease with

[078-233]her husband in France. God help every one to his own, Master Pedro, and

[078-234]let us all proceed fairly and honestly; and now go on.”

[078-235]Master Pedro, perceiving that Don Quixote was beginning to wander, and

[078-236]return to his original fancy, was not disposed to let him escape, so he

[078-237]said to him, “This cannot be Melisendra, but must be one of the damsels

[078-238]that waited on her; so if I’m given sixty maravedis for her, I’ll be

[078-239]content and sufficiently paid.”

[078-240]And so he went on, putting values on ever so many more smashed figures,

[078-241]which, after the two arbitrators had adjusted them to the satisfaction

[078-242]of both parties, came to forty reals and three-quarters; and over and

[078-243]above this sum, which Sancho at once disbursed, Master Pedro asked for

[078-244]two reals for his trouble in catching the ape.

[078-245]“Let him have them, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “not to catch the ape,

[078-246]but to get drunk; and two hundred would I give this minute for the good

[078-247]news, to anyone who could tell me positively, that the lady Doña

[078-248]Melisandra and Señor Don Gaiferos were now in France and with their own

[078-249]people.”

[078-250]“No one could tell us that better than my ape,” said Master Pedro; “but

[078-251]there’s no devil that could catch him now; I suspect, however, that

[078-252]affection and hunger will drive him to come looking for me to-night;

[078-253]but to-morrow will soon be here and we shall see.”

[078-254]In short, the puppet-show storm passed off, and all supped in peace and

[078-255]good fellowship at Don Quixote’s expense, for he was the height of

[078-256]generosity. Before it was daylight the man with the lances and halberds

[078-257]took his departure, and soon after daybreak the cousin and the page

[078-258]came to bid Don Quixote farewell, the former returning home, the latter

[078-259]resuming his journey, towards which, to help him, Don Quixote gave him

[078-260]twelve reals. Master Pedro did not care to engage in any more palaver

[078-261]with Don Quixote, whom he knew right well; so he rose before the sun,

[078-262]and having got together the remains of his show and caught his ape, he

[078-263]too went off to seek his adventures. The landlord, who did not know Don

[078-264]Quixote, was as much astonished at his mad freaks as at his generosity.

[078-265]To conclude, Sancho, by his master’s orders, paid him very liberally,

[078-266]and taking leave of him they quitted the inn at about eight in the

[078-267]morning and took to the road, where we will leave them to pursue their

[078-268]journey, for this is necessary in order to allow certain other matters

[078-269]to be set forth, which are required to clear up this famous history.
