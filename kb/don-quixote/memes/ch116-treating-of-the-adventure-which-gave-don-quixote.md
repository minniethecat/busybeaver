TREATING OF THE ADVENTURE WHICH GAVE DON QUIXOTE MORE UNHAPPINESS THAN

ALL THAT HAD HITHERTO BEFALLEN HIM

p64a.jpg (80K)


[116-001]The wife of Don Antonio Moreno, so the history says, was extremely

[116-002]happy to see Ana Felix in her house. She welcomed her with great

[116-003]kindness, charmed as well by her beauty as by her intelligence; for in

[116-004]both respects the fair Morisco was richly endowed, and all the people

[116-005]of the city flocked to see her as though they had been summoned by the

[116-006]ringing of the bells.

[116-007]Don Quixote told Don Antonio that the plan adopted for releasing Don

[116-008]Gregorio was not a good one, for its risks were greater than its

[116-009]advantages, and that it would be better to land himself with his arms

[116-010]and horse in Barbary; for he would carry him off in spite of the whole

[116-011]Moorish host, as Don Gaiferos carried off his wife Melisendra.

[116-012]“Remember, your worship,” observed Sancho on hearing him say so, “Señor

[116-013]Don Gaiferos carried off his wife from the mainland, and took her to

[116-014]France by land; but in this case, if by chance we carry off Don

[116-015]Gregorio, we have no way of bringing him to Spain, for there’s the sea

[116-016]between.”

[116-017]“There’s a remedy for everything except death,” said Don Quixote; “if

[116-018]they bring the vessel close to the shore we shall be able to get on

[116-019]board though all the world strive to prevent us.”

[116-020]“Your worship hits it off mighty well and mighty easy,” said Sancho;

[116-021]“but ‘it’s a long step from saying to doing;’ and I hold to the

[116-022]renegade, for he seems to me an honest good-hearted fellow.”

[116-023]Don Antonio then said that if the renegade did not prove successful,

[116-024]the expedient of the great Don Quixote’s expedition to Barbary should

[116-025]be adopted. Two days afterwards the renegade put to sea in a light

[116-026]vessel of six oars a-side manned by a stout crew, and two days later

[116-027]the galleys made sail eastward, the general having begged the viceroy

[116-028]to let him know all about the release of Don Gregorio and about Ana

[116-029]Felix, and the viceroy promised to do as he requested.

[116-030]One morning as Don Quixote went out for a stroll along the beach,

[116-031]arrayed in full armour (for, as he often said, that was “his only gear,

[116-032]his only rest the fray,” and he never was without it for a moment), he

[116-033]saw coming towards him a knight, also in full armour, with a shining

[116-034]moon painted on his shield, who, on approaching sufficiently near to be

[116-035]heard, said in a loud voice, addressing himself to Don Quixote,

[116-036]“Illustrious knight, and never sufficiently extolled Don Quixote of La

[116-037]Mancha, I am the Knight of the White Moon, whose unheard-of

[116-038]achievements will perhaps have recalled him to thy memory. I come to do

[116-039]battle with thee and prove the might of thy arm, to the end that I make

[116-040]thee acknowledge and confess that my lady, let her be who she may, is

[116-041]incomparably fairer than thy Dulcinea del Toboso. If thou dost

[116-042]acknowledge this fairly and openly, thou shalt escape death and save me

[116-043]the trouble of inflicting it upon thee; if thou fightest and I vanquish

[116-044]thee, I demand no other satisfaction than that, laying aside arms and

[116-045]abstaining from going in quest of adventures, thou withdraw and betake

[116-046]thyself to thine own village for the space of a year, and live there

[116-047]without putting hand to sword, in peace and quiet and beneficial

[116-048]repose, the same being needful for the increase of thy substance and

[116-049]the salvation of thy soul; and if thou dost vanquish me, my head shall

[116-050]be at thy disposal, my arms and horse thy spoils, and the renown of my

[116-051]deeds transferred and added to thine. Consider which will be thy best

[116-052]course, and give me thy answer speedily, for this day is all the time I

[116-053]have for the despatch of this business.”

[116-054]Don Quixote was amazed and astonished, as well at the Knight of the

[116-055]White Moon’s arrogance, as at his reason for delivering the defiance,

[116-056]and with calm dignity he answered him, “Knight of the White Moon, of

[116-057]whose achievements I have never heard until now, I will venture to

[116-058]swear you have never seen the illustrious Dulcinea; for had you seen

[116-059]her I know you would have taken care not to venture yourself upon this

[116-060]issue, because the sight would have removed all doubt from your mind

[116-061]that there ever has been or can be a beauty to be compared with hers;

[116-062]and so, not saying you lie, but merely that you are not correct in what

[116-063]you state, I accept your challenge, with the conditions you have

[116-064]proposed, and at once, that the day you have fixed may not expire; and

[116-065]from your conditions I except only that of the renown of your

[116-066]achievements being transferred to me, for I know not of what sort they

[116-067]are nor what they may amount to; I am satisfied with my own, such as

[116-068]they be. Take, therefore, the side of the field you choose, and I will

[116-069]do the same; and to whom God shall give it may Saint Peter add his

[116-070]blessing.”

[116-071]The Knight of the White Moon had been seen from the city, and it was

[116-072]told the viceroy how he was in conversation with Don Quixote. The

[116-073]viceroy, fancying it must be some fresh adventure got up by Don Antonio

[116-074]Moreno or some other gentleman of the city, hurried out at once to the

[116-075]beach accompanied by Don Antonio and several other gentlemen, just as

[116-076]Don Quixote was wheeling Rocinante round in order to take up the

[116-077]necessary distance. The viceroy upon this, seeing that the pair of them

[116-078]were evidently preparing to come to the charge, put himself between

[116-079]them, asking them what it was that led them to engage in combat all of

[116-080]a sudden in this way. The Knight of the White Moon replied that it was

[116-081]a question of precedence of beauty; and briefly told him what he had

[116-082]said to Don Quixote, and how the conditions of the defiance agreed upon

[116-083]on both sides had been accepted. The viceroy went over to Don Antonio,

[116-084]and asked in a low voice did he know who the Knight of the White Moon

[116-085]was, or was it some joke they were playing on Don Quixote. Don Antonio

[116-086]replied that he neither knew who he was nor whether the defiance was in

[116-087]joke or in earnest. This answer left the viceroy in a state of

[116-088]perplexity, not knowing whether he ought to let the combat go on or

[116-089]not; but unable to persuade himself that it was anything but a joke he

[116-090]fell back, saying, “If there be no other way out of it, gallant

[116-091]knights, except to confess or die, and Don Quixote is inflexible, and

[116-092]your worship of the White Moon still more so, in God’s hand be it, and

[116-093]fall on.”

[116-094]He of the White Moon thanked the viceroy in courteous and well-chosen

[116-095]words for the permission he gave them, and so did Don Quixote, who

[116-096]then, commending himself with all his heart to heaven and to his

[116-097]Dulcinea, as was his custom on the eve of any combat that awaited him,

[116-098]proceeded to take a little more distance, as he saw his antagonist was

[116-099]doing the same; then, without blast of trumpet or other warlike

[116-100]instrument to give them the signal to charge, both at the same instant

[116-101]wheeled their horses; and he of the White Moon, being the swifter, met

[116-102]Don Quixote after having traversed two-thirds of the course, and there

[116-103]encountered him with such violence that, without touching him with his

[116-104]lance (for he held it high, to all appearance purposely), he hurled Don

[116-105]Quixote and Rocinante to the earth, a perilous fall. He sprang upon him

[116-106]at once, and placing the lance over his visor said to him, “You are

[116-107]vanquished, sir knight, nay dead unless you admit the conditions of our

[116-108]defiance.”

[116-109]Don Quixote, bruised and stupefied, without raising his visor said in a

[116-110]weak feeble voice as if he were speaking out of a tomb, “Dulcinea del

[116-111]Toboso is the fairest woman in the world, and I the most unfortunate

[116-112]knight on earth; it is not fitting that this truth should suffer by my

[116-113]feebleness; drive your lance home, sir knight, and take my life, since

[116-114]you have taken away my honour.”

[116-115]“That will I not, in sooth,” said he of the White Moon; “live the fame

[116-116]of the lady Dulcinea’s beauty undimmed as ever; all I require is that

[116-117]the great Don Quixote retire to his own home for a year, or for so long

[116-118]a time as shall by me be enjoined upon him, as we agreed before

[116-119]engaging in this combat.”

[116-120]The viceroy, Don Antonio, and several others who were present heard all

[116-121]this, and heard too how Don Quixote replied that so long as nothing in

[116-122]prejudice of Dulcinea was demanded of him, he would observe all the

[116-123]rest like a true and loyal knight. The engagement given, he of the

[116-124]White Moon wheeled about, and making obeisance to the viceroy with a

[116-125]movement of the head, rode away into the city at a half gallop. The

[116-126]viceroy bade Don Antonio hasten after him, and by some means or other

[116-127]find out who he was. They raised Don Quixote up and uncovered his face,

[116-128]and found him pale and bathed with sweat.

p64b.jpg (344K)


[116-129]Rocinante from the mere hard measure he had received lay unable to stir

[116-130]for the present. Sancho, wholly dejected and woebegone, knew not what

[116-131]to say or do. He fancied that all was a dream, that the whole business

[116-132]was a piece of enchantment. Here was his master defeated, and bound not

[116-133]to take up arms for a year. He saw the light of the glory of his

[116-134]achievements obscured; the hopes of the promises lately made him swept

[116-135]away like smoke before the wind; Rocinante, he feared, was crippled for

[116-136]life, and his master’s bones out of joint; for if he were only shaken

[116-137]out of his madness it would be no small luck. In the end they carried

[116-138]him into the city in a hand-chair which the viceroy sent for, and

[116-139]thither the viceroy himself returned, eager to ascertain who this

[116-140]Knight of the White Moon was who had left Don Quixote in such a sad

[116-141]plight.
