WHICH TELLS HOW ADVENTURES CAME CROWDING ON DON QUIXOTE IN SUCH NUMBERS

[110-001]THAT THEY GAVE ONE ANOTHER NO BREATHING-TIME

p58a.jpg (105K)


[110-002]When Don Quixote saw himself in open country, free, and relieved from

[110-003]the attentions of Altisidora, he felt at his ease, and in fresh spirits

[110-004]to take up the pursuit of chivalry once more; and turning to Sancho, he

[110-005]said, “Freedom, Sancho, is one of the most precious gifts that heaven

[110-006]has bestowed upon men; no treasures that the earth holds buried or the

[110-007]sea conceals can compare with it; for freedom, as for honour, life may

[110-008]and should be ventured; and on the other hand, captivity is the

[110-009]greatest evil that can fall to the lot of man. I say this, Sancho,

[110-010]because thou hast seen the good cheer, the abundance we have enjoyed in

[110-011]this castle we are leaving; well then, amid those dainty banquets and

[110-012]snow-cooled beverages I felt as though I were undergoing the straits of

[110-013]hunger, because I did not enjoy them with the same freedom as if they

[110-014]had been mine own; for the sense of being under an obligation to return

[110-015]benefits and favours received is a restraint that checks the

[110-016]independence of the spirit. Happy he, to whom heaven has given a piece

[110-017]of bread for which he is not bound to give thanks to any but heaven

[110-018]itself!”

[110-019]“For all your worship says,” said Sancho, “it is not becoming that

[110-020]there should be no thanks on our part for two hundred gold crowns that

[110-021]the duke’s majordomo has given me in a little purse which I carry next

[110-022]my heart, like a warming plaster or comforter, to meet any chance

[110-023]calls; for we shan’t always find castles where they’ll entertain us;

[110-024]now and then we may light upon roadside inns where they’ll cudgel us.”

[110-025]In conversation of this sort the knight and squire errant were pursuing

[110-026]their journey, when, after they had gone a little more than half a

[110-027]league, they perceived some dozen men dressed like labourers stretched

[110-028]upon their cloaks on the grass of a green meadow eating their dinner.

[110-029]They had beside them what seemed to be white sheets concealing some

[110-030]objects under them, standing upright or lying flat, and arranged at

[110-031]intervals. Don Quixote approached the diners, and, saluting them

[110-032]courteously first, he asked them what it was those cloths covered.

[110-033]“Señor,” answered one of the party, “under these cloths are some images

[110-034]carved in relief intended for a retablo we are putting up in our

[110-035]village; we carry them covered up that they may not be soiled, and on

[110-036]our shoulders that they may not be broken.”

[110-037]“With your good leave,” said Don Quixote, “I should like to see them;

[110-038]for images that are carried so carefully no doubt must be fine ones.”

[110-039]“I should think they were!” said the other; “let the money they cost

[110-040]speak for that; for as a matter of fact there is not one of them that

[110-041]does not stand us in more than fifty ducats; and that your worship may

[110-042]judge; wait a moment, and you shall see with your own eyes;” and

[110-043]getting up from his dinner he went and uncovered the first image, which

[110-044]proved to be one of Saint George on horseback with a serpent writhing

[110-045]at his feet and the lance thrust down its throat with all that

[110-046]fierceness that is usually depicted. The whole group was one blaze of

[110-047]gold, as the saying is. On seeing it Don Quixote said, “That knight was

[110-048]one of the best knights-errant the army of heaven ever owned; he was

[110-049]called Don Saint George, and he was moreover a defender of maidens. Let

[110-050]us see this next one.”

[110-051]The man uncovered it, and it was seen to be that of Saint Martin on his

[110-052]horse, dividing his cloak with the beggar. The instant Don Quixote saw

[110-053]it he said, “This knight too was one of the Christian adventurers, but

[110-054]I believe he was generous rather than valiant, as thou mayest perceive,

[110-055]Sancho, by his dividing his cloak with the beggar and giving him half

[110-056]of it; no doubt it was winter at the time, for otherwise he would have

[110-057]given him the whole of it, so charitable was he.”

[110-058]“It was not that, most likely,” said Sancho, “but that he held with the

[110-059]proverb that says, ‘For giving and keeping there’s need of brains.’”

[110-060]Don Quixote laughed, and asked them to take off the next cloth,

[110-061]underneath which was seen the image of the patron saint of the Spains

[110-062]seated on horseback, his sword stained with blood, trampling on Moors

[110-063]and treading heads underfoot; and on seeing it Don Quixote exclaimed,

[110-064]“Ay, this is a knight, and of the squadrons of Christ! This one is

[110-065]called Don Saint James the Moorslayer, one of the bravest saints and

[110-066]knights the world ever had or heaven has now.”

[110-067]They then raised another cloth which it appeared covered Saint Paul

[110-068]falling from his horse, with all the details that are usually given in

[110-069]representations of his conversion. When Don Quixote saw it, rendered in

[110-070]such lifelike style that one would have said Christ was speaking and

[110-071]Paul answering, “This,” he said, “was in his time the greatest enemy

[110-072]that the Church of God our Lord had, and the greatest champion it will

[110-073]ever have; a knight-errant in life, a steadfast saint in death, an

[110-074]untiring labourer in the Lord’s vineyard, a teacher of the Gentiles,

[110-075]whose school was heaven, and whose instructor and master was Jesus

[110-076]Christ himself.”

[110-077]There were no more images, so Don Quixote bade them cover them up

[110-078]again, and said to those who had brought them, “I take it as a happy

[110-079]omen, brothers, to have seen what I have; for these saints and knights

[110-080]were of the same profession as myself, which is the calling of arms;

[110-081]only there is this difference between them and me, that they were

[110-082]saints, and fought with divine weapons, and I am a sinner and fight

[110-083]with human ones. They won heaven by force of arms, for heaven suffereth

[110-084]violence; and I, so far, know not what I have won by dint of my

[110-085]sufferings; but if my Dulcinea del Toboso were to be released from

[110-086]hers, perhaps with mended fortunes and a mind restored to itself I

[110-087]might direct my steps in a better path than I am following at present.”

[110-088]“May God hear and sin be deaf,” said Sancho to this.

[110-089]The men were filled with wonder, as well at the figure as at the words

[110-090]of Don Quixote, though they did not understand one half of what he

[110-091]meant by them. They finished their dinner, took their images on their

[110-092]backs, and bidding farewell to Don Quixote resumed their journey.

[110-093]Sancho was amazed afresh at the extent of his master’s knowledge, as

[110-094]much as if he had never known him, for it seemed to him that there was

[110-095]no story or event in the world that he had not at his fingers’ ends and

[110-096]fixed in his memory, and he said to him, “In truth, master mine, if

[110-097]this that has happened to us to-day is to be called an adventure, it

[110-098]has been one of the sweetest and pleasantest that have befallen us in

[110-099]the whole course of our travels; we have come out of it unbelaboured

[110-100]and undismayed, neither have we drawn sword nor have we smitten the

[110-101]earth with our bodies, nor have we been left famishing; blessed be God

[110-102]that he has let me see such a thing with my own eyes!”

[110-103]“Thou sayest well, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “but remember all times

[110-104]are not alike nor do they always run the same way; and these things the

[110-105]vulgar commonly call omens, which are not based upon any natural

[110-106]reason, will by him who is wise be esteemed and reckoned happy

[110-107]accidents merely. One of these believers in omens will get up of a

[110-108]morning, leave his house, and meet a friar of the order of the blessed

[110-109]Saint Francis, and, as if he had met a griffin, he will turn about and

[110-110]go home. With another Mendoza the salt is spilt on his table, and gloom

[110-111]is spilt over his heart, as if nature was obliged to give warning of

[110-112]coming misfortunes by means of such trivial things as these. The wise

[110-113]man and the Christian should not trifle with what it may please heaven

[110-114]to do. Scipio on coming to Africa stumbled as he leaped on shore; his

[110-115]soldiers took it as a bad omen; but he, clasping the soil with his

[110-116]arms, exclaimed, ‘Thou canst not escape me, Africa, for I hold thee

[110-117]tight between my arms.’ Thus, Sancho, meeting those images has been to

[110-118]me a most happy occurrence.”

[110-119]“I can well believe it,” said Sancho; “but I wish your worship would

[110-120]tell me what is the reason that the Spaniards, when they are about to

[110-121]give battle, in calling on that Saint James the Moorslayer, say

[110-122]‘Santiago and close Spain!’ Is Spain, then, open, so that it is needful

[110-123]to close it; or what is the meaning of this form?”

[110-124]“Thou art very simple, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “God, look you, gave

[110-125]that great knight of the Red Cross to Spain as her patron saint and

[110-126]protector, especially in those hard struggles the Spaniards had with

[110-127]the Moors; and therefore they invoke and call upon him as their

[110-128]defender in all their battles; and in these he has been many a time

[110-129]seen beating down, trampling under foot, destroying and slaughtering

[110-130]the Hagarene squadrons in the sight of all; of which fact I could give

[110-131]thee many examples recorded in truthful Spanish histories.”

[110-132]Sancho changed the subject, and said to his master, “I marvel, señor,

[110-133]at the boldness of Altisidora, the duchess’s handmaid; he whom they

[110-134]call Love must have cruelly pierced and wounded her; they say he is a

[110-135]little blind urchin who, though blear-eyed, or more properly speaking

[110-136]sightless, if he aims at a heart, be it ever so small, hits it and

[110-137]pierces it through and through with his arrows. I have heard it said

[110-138]too that the arrows of Love are blunted and robbed of their points by

[110-139]maidenly modesty and reserve; but with this Altisidora it seems they

[110-140]are sharpened rather than blunted.”

[110-141]“Bear in mind, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that love is influenced by

[110-142]no consideration, recognises no restraints of reason, and is of the

[110-143]same nature as death, that assails alike the lofty palaces of kings and

[110-144]the humble cabins of shepherds; and when it takes entire possession of

[110-145]a heart, the first thing it does is to banish fear and shame from it;

[110-146]and so without shame Altisidora declared her passion, which excited in

[110-147]my mind embarrassment rather than commiseration.”

[110-148]“Notable cruelty!” exclaimed Sancho; “unheard-of ingratitude! I can

[110-149]only say for myself that the very smallest loving word of hers would

[110-150]have subdued me and made a slave of me. The devil! What a heart of

[110-151]marble, what bowels of brass, what a soul of mortar! But I can’t

[110-152]imagine what it is that this damsel saw in your worship that could have

[110-153]conquered and captivated her so. What gallant figure was it, what bold

[110-154]bearing, what sprightly grace, what comeliness of feature, which of

[110-155]these things by itself, or what all together, could have made her fall

[110-156]in love with you? For indeed and in truth many a time I stop to look at

[110-157]your worship from the sole of your foot to the topmost hair of your

[110-158]head, and I see more to frighten one than to make one fall in love;

[110-159]moreover I have heard say that beauty is the first and main thing that

[110-160]excites love, and as your worship has none at all, I don’t know what

[110-161]the poor creature fell in love with.”

[110-162]“Recollect, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “there are two sorts of

[110-163]beauty, one of the mind, the other of the body; that of the mind

[110-164]displays and exhibits itself in intelligence, in modesty, in honourable

[110-165]conduct, in generosity, in good breeding; and all these qualities are

[110-166]possible and may exist in an ugly man; and when it is this sort of

[110-167]beauty and not that of the body that is the attraction, love is apt to

[110-168]spring up suddenly and violently. I, Sancho, perceive clearly enough

[110-169]that I am not beautiful, but at the same time I know I am not hideous;

[110-170]and it is enough for an honest man not to be a monster to be an object

[110-171]of love, if only he possesses the endowments of mind I have mentioned.”

[110-172]While engaged in this discourse they were making their way through a

[110-173]wood that lay beyond the road, when suddenly, without expecting

[110-174]anything of the kind, Don Quixote found himself caught in some nets of

[110-175]green cord stretched from one tree to another; and unable to conceive

[110-176]what it could be, he said to Sancho, “Sancho, it strikes me this affair

[110-177]of these nets will prove one of the strangest adventures imaginable.

[110-178]May I die if the enchanters that persecute me are not trying to

[110-179]entangle me in them and delay my journey, by way of revenge for my

[110-180]obduracy towards Altisidora. Well then let me tell them that if these

[110-181]nets, instead of being green cord, were made of the hardest diamonds,

[110-182]or stronger than that wherewith the jealous god of blacksmiths enmeshed

[110-183]Venus and Mars, I would break them as easily as if they were made of

[110-184]rushes or cotton threads.” But just as he was about to press forward

[110-185]and break through all, suddenly from among some trees two shepherdesses

[110-186]of surpassing beauty presented themselves to his sight—or at least

[110-187]damsels dressed like shepherdesses, save that their jerkins and sayas

[110-188]were of fine brocade; that is to say, the sayas were rich farthingales

[110-189]of gold embroidered tabby. Their hair, that in its golden brightness

[110-190]vied with the beams of the sun itself, fell loose upon their shoulders

[110-191]and was crowned with garlands twined with green laurel and red

[110-192]everlasting; and their years to all appearance were not under fifteen

[110-193]nor above eighteen.

[110-194]Such was the spectacle that filled Sancho with amazement, fascinated

[110-195]Don Quixote, made the sun halt in his course to behold them, and held

[110-196]all four in a strange silence. One of the shepherdesses, at length, was

[110-197]the first to speak and said to Don Quixote, “Hold, sir knight, and do

[110-198]not break these nets; for they are not spread here to do you any harm,

[110-199]but only for our amusement; and as I know you will ask why they have

[110-200]been put up, and who we are, I will tell you in a few words. In a

[110-201]village some two leagues from this, where there are many people of

[110-202]quality and rich gentlefolk, it was agreed upon by a number of friends

[110-203]and relations to come with their wives, sons and daughters, neighbours,

[110-204]friends and kinsmen, and make holiday in this spot, which is one of the

[110-205]pleasantest in the whole neighbourhood, setting up a new pastoral

[110-206]Arcadia among ourselves, we maidens dressing ourselves as shepherdesses

[110-207]and the youths as shepherds. We have prepared two eclogues, one by the

[110-208]famous poet Garcilasso, the other by the most excellent Camoens, in its

[110-209]own Portuguese tongue, but we have not as yet acted them. Yesterday was

[110-210]the first day of our coming here; we have a few of what they say are

[110-211]called field-tents pitched among the trees on the bank of an ample

[110-212]brook that fertilises all these meadows; last night we spread these

[110-213]nets in the trees here to snare the silly little birds that startled by

[110-214]the noise we make may fly into them. If you please to be our guest,

[110-215]señor, you will be welcomed heartily and courteously, for here just now

[110-216]neither care nor sorrow shall enter.”

[110-217]She held her peace and said no more, and Don Quixote made answer, “Of a

[110-218]truth, fairest lady, Actaeon when he unexpectedly beheld Diana bathing

[110-219]in the stream could not have been more fascinated and wonderstruck than

[110-220]I at the sight of your beauty. I commend your mode of entertainment,

[110-221]and thank you for the kindness of your invitation; and if I can serve

[110-222]you, you may command me with full confidence of being obeyed, for my

[110-223]profession is none other than to show myself grateful, and ready to

[110-224]serve persons of all conditions, but especially persons of quality such

[110-225]as your appearance indicates; and if, instead of taking up, as they

[110-226]probably do, but a small space, these nets took up the whole surface of

[110-227]the globe, I would seek out new worlds through which to pass, so as not

[110-228]to break them; and that ye may give some degree of credence to this

[110-229]exaggerated language of mine, know that it is no less than Don Quixote

[110-230]of La Mancha that makes this declaration to you, if indeed it be that

[110-231]such a name has reached your ears.”

[110-232]“Ah! friend of my soul,” instantly exclaimed the other shepherdess,

[110-233]“what great good fortune has befallen us! Seest thou this gentleman we

[110-234]have before us? Well then let me tell thee he is the most valiant and

[110-235]the most devoted and the most courteous gentleman in all the world,

[110-236]unless a history of his achievements that has been printed and I have

[110-237]read is telling lies and deceiving us. I will lay a wager that this

[110-238]good fellow who is with him is one Sancho Panza his squire, whose

[110-239]drolleries none can equal.”

[110-240]“That’s true,” said Sancho; “I am that same droll and squire you speak

[110-241]of, and this gentleman is my master Don Quixote of La Mancha, the same

[110-242]that’s in the history and that they talk about.”

[110-243]“Oh, my friend,” said the other, “let us entreat him to stay; for it

[110-244]will give our fathers and brothers infinite pleasure; I too have heard

[110-245]just what thou hast told me of the valour of the one and the drolleries

[110-246]of the other; and what is more, of him they say that he is the most

[110-247]constant and loyal lover that was ever heard of, and that his lady is

[110-248]one Dulcinea del Toboso, to whom all over Spain the palm of beauty is

[110-249]awarded.”

[110-250]“And justly awarded,” said Don Quixote, “unless, indeed, your

[110-251]unequalled beauty makes it a matter of doubt. But spare yourselves the

[110-252]trouble, ladies, of pressing me to stay, for the urgent calls of my

[110-253]profession do not allow me to take rest under any circumstances.”

[110-254]At this instant there came up to the spot where the four stood a

[110-255]brother of one of the two shepherdesses, like them in shepherd costume,

[110-256]and as richly and gaily dressed as they were. They told him that their

[110-257]companion was the valiant Don Quixote of La Mancha, and the other

[110-258]Sancho his squire, of whom he knew already from having read their

[110-259]history. The gay shepherd offered him his services and begged that he

[110-260]would accompany him to their tents, and Don Quixote had to give way and

[110-261]comply. And now the game was started, and the nets were filled with a

[110-262]variety of birds that deceived by the colour fell into the danger they

[110-263]were flying from. Upwards of thirty persons, all gaily attired as

[110-264]shepherds and shepherdesses, assembled on the spot, and were at once

[110-265]informed who Don Quixote and his squire were, whereat they were not a

[110-266]little delighted, as they knew of him already through his history. They

[110-267]repaired to the tents, where they found tables laid out, and choicely,

[110-268]plentifully, and neatly furnished. They treated Don Quixote as a person

[110-269]of distinction, giving him the place of honour, and all observed him,

[110-270]and were full of astonishment at the spectacle. At last the cloth being

[110-271]removed, Don Quixote with great composure lifted up his voice and said:

[110-272]“One of the greatest sins that men are guilty of is—some will say

[110-273]pride—but I say ingratitude, going by the common saying that hell is

[110-274]full of ingrates. This sin, so far as it has lain in my power, I have

[110-275]endeavoured to avoid ever since I have enjoyed the faculty of reason;

[110-276]and if I am unable to requite good deeds that have been done me by

[110-277]other deeds, I substitute the desire to do so; and if that be not

[110-278]enough I make them known publicly; for he who declares and makes known

[110-279]the good deeds done to him would repay them by others if it were in his

[110-280]power, and for the most part those who receive are the inferiors of

[110-281]those who give. Thus, God is superior to all because he is the supreme

[110-282]giver, and the offerings of man fall short by an infinite distance of

[110-283]being a full return for the gifts of God; but gratitude in some degree

[110-284]makes up for this deficiency and shortcoming. I therefore, grateful for

[110-285]the favour that has been extended to me here, and unable to make a

[110-286]return in the same measure, restricted as I am by the narrow limits of

[110-287]my power, offer what I can and what I have to offer in my own way; and

[110-288]so I declare that for two full days I will maintain in the middle of

[110-289]this highway leading to Saragossa, that these ladies disguised as

[110-290]shepherdesses, who are here present, are the fairest and most courteous

[110-291]maidens in the world, excepting only the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso,

[110-292]sole mistress of my thoughts, be it said without offence to those who

[110-293]hear me, ladies and gentlemen.”

[110-294]On hearing this Sancho, who had been listening with great attention,

[110-295]cried out in a loud voice, “Is it possible there is anyone in the world

[110-296]who will dare to say and swear that this master of mine is a madman?

[110-297]Say, gentlemen shepherds, is there a village priest, be he ever so wise

[110-298]or learned, who could say what my master has said; or is there

[110-299]knight-errant, whatever renown he may have as a man of valour, that

[110-300]could offer what my master has offered now?”

[110-301]Don Quixote turned upon Sancho, and with a countenance glowing with

[110-302]anger said to him, “Is it possible, Sancho, there is anyone in the

[110-303]whole world who will say thou art not a fool, with a lining to match,

[110-304]and I know not what trimmings of impertinence and roguery? Who asked

[110-305]thee to meddle in my affairs, or to inquire whether I am a wise man or

[110-306]a blockhead? Hold thy peace; answer me not a word; saddle Rocinante if

[110-307]he be unsaddled; and let us go to put my offer into execution; for with

[110-308]the right that I have on my side thou mayest reckon as vanquished all

[110-309]who shall venture to question it;” and in a great rage, and showing his

[110-310]anger plainly, he rose from his seat, leaving the company lost in

[110-311]wonder, and making them feel doubtful whether they ought to regard him

[110-312]as a madman or a rational being. In the end, though they sought to

[110-313]dissuade him from involving himself in such a challenge, assuring him

[110-314]they admitted his gratitude as fully established, and needed no fresh

[110-315]proofs to be convinced of his valiant spirit, as those related in the

[110-316]history of his exploits were sufficient, still Don Quixote persisted in

[110-317]his resolve; and mounted on Rocinante, bracing his buckler on his arm

[110-318]and grasping his lance, he posted himself in the middle of a high road

[110-319]that was not far from the green meadow. Sancho followed on Dapple,

[110-320]together with all the members of the pastoral gathering, eager to see

[110-321]what would be the upshot of his vainglorious and extraordinary

[110-322]proposal.

[110-323]Don Quixote, then, having, as has been said, planted himself in the

[110-324]middle of the road, made the welkin ring with words to this effect: “Ho

[110-325]ye travellers and wayfarers, knights, squires, folk on foot or on

[110-326]horseback, who pass this way or shall pass in the course of the next

[110-327]two days! Know that Don Quixote of La Mancha, knight-errant, is posted

[110-328]here to maintain by arms that the beauty and courtesy enshrined in the

[110-329]nymphs that dwell in these meadows and groves surpass all upon earth,

[110-330]putting aside the lady of my heart, Dulcinea del Toboso. Wherefore, let

[110-331]him who is of the opposite opinion come on, for here I await him.”

[110-332]Twice he repeated the same words, and twice they fell unheard by any

[110-333]adventurer; but fate, that was guiding affairs for him from better to

[110-334]better, so ordered it that shortly afterwards there appeared on the

[110-335]road a crowd of men on horseback, many of them with lances in their

[110-336]hands, all riding in a compact body and in great haste. No sooner had

[110-337]those who were with Don Quixote seen them than they turned about and

[110-338]withdrew to some distance from the road, for they knew that if they

[110-339]stayed some harm might come to them; but Don Quixote with intrepid

[110-340]heart stood his ground, and Sancho Panza shielded himself with

[110-341]Rocinante’s hind-quarters. The troop of lancers came up, and one of

[110-342]them who was in advance began shouting to Don Quixote, “Get out of the

[110-343]way, you son of the devil, or these bulls will knock you to pieces!”

[110-344]“Rabble!” returned Don Quixote, “I care nothing for bulls, be they the

[110-345]fiercest Jarama breeds on its banks. Confess at once, scoundrels, that

[110-346]what I have declared is true; else ye have to deal with me in combat.”

[110-347]The herdsman had no time to reply, nor Don Quixote to get out of the

[110-348]way even if he wished; and so the drove of fierce bulls and tame

[110-349]bullocks, together with the crowd of herdsmen and others who were

[110-350]taking them to be penned up in a village where they were to be run the

[110-351]next day, passed over Don Quixote and over Sancho, Rocinante and

[110-352]Dapple, hurling them all to the earth and rolling them over on the

[110-353]ground. Sancho was left crushed, Don Quixote scared, Dapple belaboured

[110-354]and Rocinante in no very sound condition.

[110-355]They all got up, however, at length, and Don Quixote in great haste,

[110-356]stumbling here and falling there, started off running after the drove,

[110-357]shouting out, “Hold! stay! ye rascally rabble, a single knight awaits

[110-358]you, and he is not of the temper or opinion of those who say, ‘For a

[110-359]flying enemy make a bridge of silver.’” The retreating party in their

[110-360]haste, however, did not stop for that, or heed his menaces any more

[110-361]than last year’s clouds. Weariness brought Don Quixote to a halt, and

[110-362]more enraged than avenged he sat down on the road to wait until Sancho,

[110-363]Rocinante and Dapple came up. When they reached him master and man

[110-364]mounted once more, and without going back to bid farewell to the mock

[110-365]or imitation Arcadia, and more in humiliation than contentment, they

[110-366]continued their journey.
