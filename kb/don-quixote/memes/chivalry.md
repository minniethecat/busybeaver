# Chivalry

> In the long history of European culture, few codes of conduct have inspired as much literature — or as much satire — as the code of chivalry.

## Definition

The chivalric code was the moral and behavioral system that governed the medieval knight. It demanded courage in battle, loyalty to one's lord, protection of the weak, courtly love toward noble ladies, and defense of the Christian faith. By the time Cervantes wrote *Don Quixote* in the early 17th century, the age of knights was long past, but the romances of chivalry — fantastical tales of knights-errant, giants, enchanters, and impossible quests — were wildly popular in Spain. Cervantes' novel is simultaneously a parody of these romances and a profound meditation on what the chivalric ideals mean in a world that no longer needs them.

## In Don Quixote

Chivalry is the engine that drives the entire novel. Don Quixote, whose real name is Alonso Quijano, has read so many books of chivalry that "his brain dried up and he came to lose his wits" (P01-417). He decides to become a knight-errant himself, reviving what he sees as a lost golden age of honor and valor. The novel's central comic device is the collision between Don Quixote's chivalric worldview and the mundane reality of early 17th-century Spain — windmills become giants, inns become castles, and peasant women become princesses.

Cervantes' treatment of chivalry is complex. On one level, the novel mercilessly satirizes the absurdity of applying a medieval code to the modern world. Don Quixote's chivalric delusions lead him into beatings, humiliations, and near-death experiences. On another level, Cervantes shows genuine affection for the ideals of chivalry — courage, honor, the defense of the helpless — and through Don Quixote's madness, these ideals sometimes produce moments of genuine nobility. As one critic noted, "his madness makes him virtuous" (P01-1285).

## Key Passages

> "[H]e gave himself up so wholly to the reading of romances of chivalry, that it was a thing not to be believed, how he was transported and lost in them" (P01-417)

The novel opens by establishing that Don Quixote's obsession with chivalric literature is the root of his madness. This passage sets up the central tension: chivalry as a literary ideal that can consume and transform a human mind.

> "He pondered and pondered over this with so much earnestness that he lost his senses, for everything he read took possession of his imagination" (P01-464)

The transformative power of chivalric literature is so complete that Don Quixote can no longer distinguish between fiction and reality.

> "The age of chivalry is past. Nowadays there is no need of knights-errant, for there are no giants, no dragons, no distressed damsels to rescue." (003-085)

This passage, spoken by a character who encounters Don Quixote on the road, articulates the novel's central argument: chivalry's time has passed, yet Don Quixote's commitment to it exposes both the beauty and the absurdity of refusing to accept that.

## Related Characters

- **Don Quixote** — The embodiment of chivalric ideals taken to their logical extreme. He genuinely believes he is a knight-errant and that the code of chivalry obligates him to right wrongs wherever he finds them.
- **Sancho Panza** — Don Quixote's squire represents the practical, earthly counterpoint to chivalric idealism. He follows Don Quixote for the promise of an island to govern, not out of any belief in knight-errantry.
- **The Priest and the Barber** — They conduct the scrutiny of Don Quixote's library of chivalric romances (Part I, Ch. 6), debating which books should be burned — a literal attempt to cure Don Quixote by destroying the source of his chivalric delusion.
