WHEREIN IS CONTINUED THE ACCOUNT OF HOW SANCHO PANZA CONDUCTED HIMSELF

IN HIS GOVERNMENT

p47a.jpg (139K)


[099-001]The history says that from the justice court they carried Sancho to a

[099-002]sumptuous palace, where in a spacious chamber there was a table laid

[099-003]out with royal magnificence. The clarions sounded as Sancho entered the

[099-004]room, and four pages came forward to present him with water for his

[099-005]hands, which Sancho received with great dignity. The music ceased, and

[099-006]Sancho seated himself at the head of the table, for there was only that

[099-007]seat placed, and no more than one cover laid. A personage, who it

[099-008]appeared afterwards was a physician, placed himself standing by his

[099-009]side with a whalebone wand in his hand. They then lifted up a fine

[099-010]white cloth covering fruit and a great variety of dishes of different

[099-011]sorts; one who looked like a student said grace, and a page put a laced

[099-012]bib on Sancho, while another who played the part of head carver placed

[099-013]a dish of fruit before him. But hardly had he tasted a morsel when the

[099-014]man with the wand touched the plate with it, and they took it away from

[099-015]before him with the utmost celerity. The carver, however, brought him

[099-016]another dish, and Sancho proceeded to try it; but before he could get

[099-017]at it, not to say taste it, already the wand had touched it and a page

[099-018]had carried it off with the same promptitude as the fruit. Sancho

[099-019]seeing this was puzzled, and looking from one to another asked if this

[099-020]dinner was to be eaten after the fashion of a jugglery trick.

[099-021]To this he with the wand replied, “It is not to be eaten, señor

[099-022]governor, except as is usual and customary in other islands where there

[099-023]are governors. I, señor, am a physician, and I am paid a salary in this

[099-024]island to serve its governors as such, and I have a much greater regard

[099-025]for their health than for my own, studying day and night and making

[099-026]myself acquainted with the governor’s constitution, in order to be able

[099-027]to cure him when he falls sick. The chief thing I have to do is to

[099-028]attend at his dinners and suppers and allow him to eat what appears to

[099-029]me to be fit for him, and keep from him what I think will do him harm

[099-030]and be injurious to his stomach; and therefore I ordered that plate of

[099-031]fruit to be removed as being too moist, and that other dish I ordered

[099-032]to be removed as being too hot and containing many spices that

[099-033]stimulate thirst; for he who drinks much kills and consumes the radical

[099-034]moisture wherein life consists.”

[099-035]“Well then,” said Sancho, “that dish of roast partridges there that

[099-036]seems so savoury will not do me any harm.”

[099-037]To this the physician replied, “Of those my lord the governor shall not

[099-038]eat so long as I live.”

[099-039]“Why so?” said Sancho.

[099-040]“Because,” replied the doctor, “our master Hippocrates, the polestar

[099-041]and beacon of medicine, says in one of his aphorisms _omnis saturatio

[099-042]mala, perdicis autem pessima_, which means ‘all repletion is bad, but

[099-043]that of partridge is the worst of all.”

[099-044]“In that case,” said Sancho, “let señor doctor see among the dishes

[099-045]that are on the table what will do me most good and least harm, and let

[099-046]me eat it, without tapping it with his stick; for by the life of the

[099-047]governor, and so may God suffer me to enjoy it, but I’m dying of

[099-048]hunger; and in spite of the doctor and all he may say, to deny me food

[099-049]is the way to take my life instead of prolonging it.”

[099-050]“Your worship is right, señor governor,” said the physician; “and

[099-051]therefore your worship, I consider, should not eat of those stewed

[099-052]rabbits there, because it is a furry kind of food; if that veal were

[099-053]not roasted and served with pickles, you might try it; but it is out of

[099-054]the question.”

[099-055]“That big dish that is smoking farther off,” said Sancho, “seems to me

[099-056]to be an olla podrida, and out of the diversity of things in such

[099-057]ollas, I can’t fail to light upon something tasty and good for me.”

[099-058]“_Absit_,” said the doctor; “far from us be any such base thought!

[099-059]There is nothing in the world less nourishing than an olla podrida; to

[099-060]canons, or rectors of colleges, or peasants’ weddings with your ollas

[099-061]podridas, but let us have none of them on the tables of governors,

[099-062]where everything that is present should be delicate and refined; and

[099-063]the reason is, that always, everywhere and by everybody, simple

[099-064]medicines are more esteemed than compound ones, for we cannot go wrong

[099-065]in those that are simple, while in the compound we may, by merely

[099-066]altering the quantity of the things composing them. But what I am of

[099-067]opinion the governor should eat now in order to preserve and fortify

[099-068]his health is a hundred or so of wafer cakes and a few thin slices of

[099-069]conserve of quinces, which will settle his stomach and help his

[099-070]digestion.”

[099-071]Sancho on hearing this threw himself back in his chair and surveyed the

[099-072]doctor steadily, and in a solemn tone asked him what his name was and

[099-073]where he had studied.

[099-074]He replied, “My name, señor governor, is Doctor Pedro Recio de Aguero I

[099-075]am a native of a place called Tirteafuera which lies between Caracuel

[099-076]and Almodóvar del Campo, on the right-hand side, and I have the degree

[099-077]of doctor from the university of Osuna.”

[099-078]To which Sancho, glowing all over with rage, returned, “Then let Doctor

[099-079]Pedro Recio de Malaguero, native of Tirteafuera, a place that’s on the

[099-080]right-hand side as we go from Caracuel to Almodóvar del Campo, graduate

[099-081]of Osuna, get out of my presence at once; or I swear by the sun I’ll

[099-082]take a cudgel, and by dint of blows, beginning with him, I’ll not leave

[099-083]a doctor in the whole island; at least of those I know to be ignorant;

[099-084]for as to learned, wise, sensible physicians, them I will reverence and

[099-085]honour as divine persons. Once more I say let Pedro Recio get out of

[099-086]this or I’ll take this chair I am sitting on and break it over his

[099-087]head. And if they call me to account for it, I’ll clear myself by

[099-088]saying I served God in killing a bad doctor—a general executioner. And

[099-089]now give me something to eat, or else take your government; for a trade

[099-090]that does not feed its master is not worth two beans.”

[099-091]The doctor was dismayed when he saw the governor in such a passion, and

[099-092]he would have made a Tirteafuera out of the room but that the same

[099-093]instant a post-horn sounded in the street; and the carver putting his

[099-094]head out of the window turned round and said, “It’s a courier from my

[099-095]lord the duke, no doubt with some despatch of importance.”

[099-096]The courier came in all sweating and flurried, and taking a paper from

[099-097]his bosom, placed it in the governor’s hands. Sancho handed it to the

[099-098]majordomo and bade him read the superscription, which ran thus:

[099-099]_To Don Sancho Panza, Governor of the Island of Barataria, into his own

[099-100]hands or those of his secretary._

[099-101]Sancho when he heard this said, “Which of you is my secretary?” “I am,

[099-102]señor,” said one of those present, “for I can read and write, and am a

[099-103]Biscayan.” “With that addition,” said Sancho, “you might be secretary

[099-104]to the emperor himself; open this paper and see what it says.” The

[099-105]new-born secretary obeyed, and having read the contents said the matter

[099-106]was one to be discussed in private. Sancho ordered the chamber to be

[099-107]cleared, the majordomo and the carver only remaining; so the doctor and

[099-108]the others withdrew, and then the secretary read the letter, which was

[099-109]as follows:

[099-110]It has come to my knowledge, Señor Don Sancho Panza, that certain

[099-111]enemies of mine and of the island are about to make a furious attack

[099-112]upon it some night, I know not when. It behoves you to be on the alert

[099-113]and keep watch, that they surprise you not. I also know by trustworthy

[099-114]spies that four persons have entered the town in disguise in order to

[099-115]take your life, because they stand in dread of your great capacity;

[099-116]keep your eyes open and take heed who approaches you to address you,

[099-117]and eat nothing that is presented to you. I will take care to send you

[099-118]aid if you find yourself in difficulty, but in all things you will act

[099-119]as may be expected of your judgment. From this place, the Sixteenth of

[099-120]August, at four in the morning.

[099-121]Your friend,

[099-122]THE DUKE

[099-123]Sancho was astonished, and those who stood by made believe to be so

[099-124]too, and turning to the majordomo he said to him, “What we have got to

[099-125]do first, and it must be done at once, is to put Doctor Recio in the

[099-126]lock-up; for if anyone wants to kill me it is he, and by a slow death

[099-127]and the worst of all, which is hunger.”

[099-128]“Likewise,” said the carver, “it is my opinion your worship should not

[099-129]eat anything that is on this table, for the whole was a present from

[099-130]some nuns; and as they say, ‘behind the cross there’s the devil.’”

[099-131]“I don’t deny it,” said Sancho; “so for the present give me a piece of

[099-132]bread and four pounds or so of grapes; no poison can come in them; for

[099-133]the fact is I can’t go on without eating; and if we are to be prepared

[099-134]for these battles that are threatening us we must be well provisioned;

[099-135]for it is the tripes that carry the heart and not the heart the tripes.

[099-136]And you, secretary, answer my lord the duke and tell him that all his

[099-137]commands shall be obeyed to the letter, as he directs; and say from me

[099-138]to my lady the duchess that I kiss her hands, and that I beg of her not

[099-139]to forget to send my letter and bundle to my wife Teresa Panza by a

[099-140]messenger; and I will take it as a great favour and will not fail to

[099-141]serve her in all that may lie within my power; and as you are about it

[099-142]you may enclose a kiss of the hand to my master Don Quixote that he may

[099-143]see I am grateful bread; and as a good secretary and a good Biscayan

[099-144]you may add whatever you like and whatever will come in best; and now

[099-145]take away this cloth and give me something to eat, and I’ll be ready to

[099-146]meet all the spies and assassins and enchanters that may come against

[099-147]me or my island.”

[099-148]At this instant a page entered saying, “Here is a farmer on business,

[099-149]who wants to speak to your lordship on a matter of great importance, he

[099-150]says.”

[099-151]“It’s very odd,” said Sancho, “the ways of these men on business; is it

[099-152]possible they can be such fools as not to see that an hour like this is

[099-153]no hour for coming on business? We who govern and we who are judges—are

[099-154]we not men of flesh and blood, and are we not to be allowed the time

[099-155]required for taking rest, unless they’d have us made of marble? By God

[099-156]and on my conscience, if the government remains in my hands (which I

[099-157]have a notion it won’t), I’ll bring more than one man on business to

[099-158]order. However, tell this good man to come in; but take care first of

[099-159]all that he is not some spy or one of my assassins.”

[099-160]“No, my lord,” said the page, “for he looks like a simple fellow, and

[099-161]either I know very little or he is as good as good bread.”

[099-162]“There is nothing to be afraid of,” said the majordomo, “for we are all

[099-163]here.”

[099-164]“Would it be possible, carver,” said Sancho, “now that Doctor Pedro

[099-165]Recio is not here, to let me eat something solid and substantial, if it

[099-166]were even a piece of bread and an onion?”

[099-167]“To-night at supper,” said the carver, “the shortcomings of the dinner

[099-168]shall be made good, and your lordship shall be fully contented.”

[099-169]“God grant it,” said Sancho.

[099-170]The farmer now came in, a well-favoured man that one might see a

[099-171]thousand leagues off was an honest fellow and a good soul. The first

[099-172]thing he said was, “Which is the lord governor here?”

[099-173]“Which should it be,” said the secretary, “but he who is seated in the

[099-174]chair?”

[099-175]“Then I humble myself before him,” said the farmer; and going on his

[099-176]knees he asked for his hand, to kiss it. Sancho refused it, and bade

[099-177]him stand up and say what he wanted. The farmer obeyed, and then said,

[099-178]“I am a farmer, señor, a native of Miguelturra, a village two leagues

[099-179]from Ciudad Real.”

[099-180]“Another Tirteafuera!” said Sancho; “say on, brother; I know

[099-181]Miguelturra very well I can tell you, for it’s not very far from my own

[099-182]town.”

[099-183]“The case is this, señor,” continued the farmer, “that by God’s mercy I

[099-184]am married with the leave and licence of the holy Roman Catholic

[099-185]Church; I have two sons, students, and the younger is studying to

[099-186]become bachelor, and the elder to be licentiate; I am a widower, for my

[099-187]wife died, or more properly speaking, a bad doctor killed her on my

[099-188]hands, giving her a purge when she was with child; and if it had

[099-189]pleased God that the child had been born, and was a boy, I would have

[099-190]put him to study for doctor, that he might not envy his brothers the

[099-191]bachelor and the licentiate.”

[099-192]“So that if your wife had not died, or had not been killed, you would

[099-193]not now be a widower,” said Sancho.

[099-194]“No, señor, certainly not,” said the farmer.

[099-195]“We’ve got that much settled,” said Sancho; “get on, brother, for it’s

[099-196]more bed-time than business-time.”

[099-197]“Well then,” said the farmer, “this son of mine who is going to be a

[099-198]bachelor, fell in love in the said town with a damsel called Clara

[099-199]Perlerina, daughter of Andres Perlerino, a very rich farmer; and this

[099-200]name of Perlerines does not come to them by ancestry or descent, but

[099-201]because all the family are paralytics, and for a better name they call

[099-202]them Perlerines; though to tell the truth the damsel is as fair as an

[099-203]Oriental pearl, and like a flower of the field, if you look at her on

[099-204]the right side; on the left not so much, for on that side she wants an

[099-205]eye that she lost by small-pox; and though her face is thickly and

[099-206]deeply pitted, those who love her say they are not pits that are there,

[099-207]but the graves where the hearts of her lovers are buried. She is so

[099-208]cleanly that not to soil her face she carries her nose turned up, as

[099-209]they say, so that one would fancy it was running away from her mouth;

[099-210]and with all this she looks extremely well, for she has a wide mouth;

[099-211]and but for wanting ten or a dozen teeth and grinders she might compare

[099-212]and compete with the comeliest. Of her lips I say nothing, for they are

[099-213]so fine and thin that, if lips might be reeled, one might make a skein

[099-214]of them; but being of a different colour from ordinary lips they are

[099-215]wonderful, for they are mottled, blue, green, and purple—let my lord

[099-216]the governor pardon me for painting so minutely the charms of her who

[099-217]some time or other will be my daughter; for I love her, and I don’t

[099-218]find her amiss.”

[099-219]“Paint what you will,” said Sancho; “I enjoy your painting, and if I

[099-220]had dined there could be no dessert more to my taste than your

[099-221]portrait.”

[099-222]“That I have still to furnish,” said the farmer; “but a time will come

[099-223]when we may be able if we are not now; and I can tell you, señor, if I

[099-224]could paint her gracefulness and her tall figure, it would astonish

[099-225]you; but that is impossible because she is bent double with her knees

[099-226]up to her mouth; but for all that it is easy to see that if she could

[099-227]stand up she’d knock her head against the ceiling; and she would have

[099-228]given her hand to my bachelor ere this, only that she can’t stretch it

[099-229]out, for it’s contracted; but still one can see its elegance and fine

[099-230]make by its long furrowed nails.”

[099-231]“That will do, brother,” said Sancho; “consider you have painted her

[099-232]from head to foot; what is it you want now? Come to the point without

[099-233]all this beating about the bush, and all these scraps and additions.”

[099-234]“I want your worship, señor,” said the farmer, “to do me the favour of

[099-235]giving me a letter of recommendation to the girl’s father, begging him

[099-236]to be so good as to let this marriage take place, as we are not

[099-237]ill-matched either in the gifts of fortune or of nature; for to tell

[099-238]the truth, señor governor, my son is possessed of a devil, and there is

[099-239]not a day but the evil spirits torment him three or four times; and

[099-240]from having once fallen into the fire, he has his face puckered up like

[099-241]a piece of parchment, and his eyes watery and always running; but he

[099-242]has the disposition of an angel, and if it was not for belabouring and

[099-243]pummelling himself he’d be a saint.”

[099-244]“Is there anything else you want, good man?” said Sancho.

[099-245]“There’s another thing I’d like,” said the farmer, “but I’m afraid to

[099-246]mention it; however, out it must; for after all I can’t let it be

[099-247]rotting in my breast, come what may. I mean, señor, that I’d like your

[099-248]worship to give me three hundred or six hundred ducats as a help to my

[099-249]bachelor’s portion, to help him in setting up house; for they must, in

[099-250]short, live by themselves, without being subject to the interferences

[099-251]of their fathers-in-law.”

[099-252]“Just see if there’s anything else you’d like,” said Sancho, “and don’t

[099-253]hold back from mentioning it out of bashfulness or modesty.”

[099-254]“No, indeed there is not,” said the farmer.

[099-255]The moment he said this the governor started to his feet, and seizing

[099-256]the chair he had been sitting on exclaimed, “By all that’s good, you

[099-257]ill-bred, boorish Don Bumpkin, if you don’t get out of this at once and

[099-258]hide yourself from my sight, I’ll lay your head open with this chair.

[099-259]You whoreson rascal, you devil’s own painter, and is it at this hour

[099-260]you come to ask me for six hundred ducats! How should I have them, you

[099-261]stinking brute? And why should I give them to you if I had them, you

[099-262]knave and blockhead? What have I to do with Miguelturra or the whole

[099-263]family of the Perlerines? Get out I say, or by the life of my lord the

[099-264]duke I’ll do as I said. You’re not from Miguelturra, but some knave

[099-265]sent here from hell to tempt me. Why, you villain, I have not yet had

[099-266]the government half a day, and you want me to have six hundred ducats

[099-267]already!”

[099-268]The carver made signs to the farmer to leave the room, which he did

[099-269]with his head down, and to all appearance in terror lest the governor

[099-270]should carry his threats into effect, for the rogue knew very well how

[099-271]to play his part.

[099-272]But let us leave Sancho in his wrath, and peace be with them all; and

[099-273]let us return to Don Quixote, whom we left with his face bandaged and

[099-274]doctored after the cat wounds, of which he was not cured for eight

[099-275]days; and on one of these there befell him what Cide Hamete promises to

[099-276]relate with that exactitude and truth with which he is wont to set

[099-277]forth everything connected with this great history, however minute it

[099-278]may be.
