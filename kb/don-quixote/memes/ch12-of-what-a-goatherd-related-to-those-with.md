OF WHAT A GOATHERD RELATED TO THOSE WITH DON QUIXOTE

c12a.jpg (143K)


[012-001]Just then another young man, one of those who fetched their provisions

[012-002]from the village, came up and said, “Do you know what is going on in

[012-003]the village, comrades?”

[012-004]“How could we know it?” replied one of them.

[012-005]“Well, then, you must know,” continued the young man, “this morning

[012-006]that famous student-shepherd called Chrysostom died, and it is rumoured

[012-007]that he died of love for that devil of a village girl the daughter of

[012-008]Guillermo the Rich, she that wanders about the wolds here in the dress

[012-009]of a shepherdess.”

[012-010]“You mean Marcela?” said one.

[012-011]“Her I mean,” answered the goatherd; “and the best of it is, he has

[012-012]directed in his will that he is to be buried in the fields like a Moor,

[012-013]and at the foot of the rock where the Cork-tree spring is, because, as

[012-014]the story goes (and they say he himself said so), that was the place

[012-015]where he first saw her. And he has also left other directions which the

[012-016]clergy of the village say should not and must not be obeyed because

[012-017]they savour of paganism. To all which his great friend Ambrosio the

[012-018]student, he who, like him, also went dressed as a shepherd, replies

[012-019]that everything must be done without any omission according to the

[012-020]directions left by Chrysostom, and about this the village is all in

[012-021]commotion; however, report says that, after all, what Ambrosio and all

[012-022]the shepherds his friends desire will be done, and to-morrow they are

[012-023]coming to bury him with great ceremony where I said. I am sure it will

[012-024]be something worth seeing; at least I will not fail to go and see it

[012-025]even if I knew I should not return to the village to-morrow.”

[012-026]“We will do the same,” answered the goatherds, “and cast lots to see

[012-027]who must stay to mind the goats of all.”

[012-028]“Thou sayest well, Pedro,” said one, “though there will be no need of

[012-029]taking that trouble, for I will stay behind for all; and don’t suppose

[012-030]it is virtue or want of curiosity in me; it is that the splinter that

[012-031]ran into my foot the other day will not let me walk.”

[012-032]“For all that, we thank thee,” answered Pedro.

[012-033]Don Quixote asked Pedro to tell him who the dead man was and who the

[012-034]shepherdess, to which Pedro replied that all he knew was that the dead

[012-035]man was a wealthy gentleman belonging to a village in those mountains,

[012-036]who had been a student at Salamanca for many years, at the end of which

[012-037]he returned to his village with the reputation of being very learned

[012-038]and deeply read. “Above all, they said, he was learned in the science

[012-039]of the stars and of what went on yonder in the heavens and the sun and

[012-040]the moon, for he told us of the cris of the sun and moon to exact

[012-041]time.”

[012-042]“Eclipse it is called, friend, not cris, the darkening of those two

[012-043]luminaries,” said Don Quixote; but Pedro, not troubling himself with

[012-044]trifles, went on with his story, saying, “Also he foretold when the

[012-045]year was going to be one of abundance or estility.”

[012-046]“Sterility, you mean,” said Don Quixote.

[012-047]“Sterility or estility,” answered Pedro, “it is all the same in the

[012-048]end. And I can tell you that by this his father and friends who

[012-049]believed him grew very rich because they did as he advised them,

[012-050]bidding them ‘sow barley this year, not wheat; this year you may sow

[012-051]pulse and not barley; the next there will be a full oil crop, and the

[012-052]three following not a drop will be got.’”

[012-053]“That science is called astrology,” said Don Quixote.

[012-054]“I do not know what it is called,” replied Pedro, “but I know that he

[012-055]knew all this and more besides. But, to make an end, not many months

[012-056]had passed after he returned from Salamanca, when one day he appeared

[012-057]dressed as a shepherd with his crook and sheepskin, having put off the

[012-058]long gown he wore as a scholar; and at the same time his great friend,

[012-059]Ambrosio by name, who had been his companion in his studies, took to

[012-060]the shepherd’s dress with him. I forgot to say that Chrysostom, who is

[012-061]dead, was a great man for writing verses, so much so that he made

[012-062]carols for Christmas Eve, and plays for Corpus Christi, which the young

[012-063]men of our village acted, and all said they were excellent. When the

[012-064]villagers saw the two scholars so unexpectedly appearing in shepherd’s

[012-065]dress, they were lost in wonder, and could not guess what had led them

[012-066]to make so extraordinary a change. About this time the father of our

[012-067]Chrysostom died, and he was left heir to a large amount of property in

[012-068]chattels as well as in land, no small number of cattle and sheep, and a

[012-069]large sum of money, of all of which the young man was left dissolute

[012-070]owner, and indeed he was deserving of it all, for he was a very good

[012-071]comrade, and kind-hearted, and a friend of worthy folk, and had a

[012-072]countenance like a benediction. Presently it came to be known that he

[012-073]had changed his dress with no other object than to wander about these

[012-074]wastes after that shepherdess Marcela our lad mentioned a while ago,

[012-075]with whom the deceased Chrysostom had fallen in love. And I must tell

[012-076]you now, for it is well you should know it, who this girl is; perhaps,

[012-077]and even without any perhaps, you will not have heard anything like it

[012-078]all the days of your life, though you should live more years than

[012-079]sarna.”

[012-080]“Say Sarra,” said Don Quixote, unable to endure the goatherd’s

[012-081]confusion of words.

[012-082]“The sarna lives long enough,” answered Pedro; “and if, señor, you must

[012-083]go finding fault with words at every step, we shall not make an end of

[012-084]it this twelvemonth.”

[012-085]“Pardon me, friend,” said Don Quixote; “but, as there is such a

[012-086]difference between sarna and Sarra, I told you of it; however, you have

[012-087]answered very rightly, for sarna lives longer than Sarra: so continue

[012-088]your story, and I will not object any more to anything.”

[012-089]“I say then, my dear sir,” said the goatherd, “that in our village

[012-090]there was a farmer even richer than the father of Chrysostom, who was

[012-091]named Guillermo, and upon whom God bestowed, over and above great

[012-092]wealth, a daughter at whose birth her mother died, the most respected

[012-093]woman there was in this neighbourhood; I fancy I can see her now with

[012-094]that countenance which had the sun on one side and the moon on the

[012-095]other; and moreover active, and kind to the poor, for which I trust

[012-096]that at the present moment her soul is in bliss with God in the other

[012-097]world. Her husband Guillermo died of grief at the death of so good a

[012-098]wife, leaving his daughter Marcela, a child and rich, to the care of an

[012-099]uncle of hers, a priest and prebendary in our village. The girl grew up

[012-100]with such beauty that it reminded us of her mother’s, which was very

[012-101]great, and yet it was thought that the daughter’s would exceed it; and

[012-102]so when she reached the age of fourteen to fifteen years nobody beheld

[012-103]her but blessed God that had made her so beautiful, and the greater

[012-104]number were in love with her past redemption. Her uncle kept her in

[012-105]great seclusion and retirement, but for all that the fame of her great

[012-106]beauty spread so that, as well for it as for her great wealth, her

[012-107]uncle was asked, solicited, and importuned, to give her in marriage not

[012-108]only by those of our town but of those many leagues round, and by the

[012-109]persons of highest quality in them. But he, being a good Christian man,

[012-110]though he desired to give her in marriage at once, seeing her to be old

[012-111]enough, was unwilling to do so without her consent, not that he had any

[012-112]eye to the gain and profit which the custody of the girl’s property

[012-113]brought him while he put off her marriage; and, faith, this was said in

[012-114]praise of the good priest in more than one set in the town. For I would

[012-115]have you know, Sir Errant, that in these little villages everything is

[012-116]talked about and everything is carped at, and rest assured, as I am,

[012-117]that the priest must be over and above good who forces his parishioners

[012-118]to speak well of him, especially in villages.”

[012-119]“That is the truth,” said Don Quixote; “but go on, for the story is

[012-120]very good, and you, good Pedro, tell it with very good grace.”

[012-121]“May that of the Lord not be wanting to me,” said Pedro; “that is the

[012-122]one to have. To proceed; you must know that though the uncle put before

[012-123]his niece and described to her the qualities of each one in particular

[012-124]of the many who had asked her in marriage, begging her to marry and

[012-125]make a choice according to her own taste, she never gave any other

[012-126]answer than that she had no desire to marry just yet, and that being so

[012-127]young she did not think herself fit to bear the burden of matrimony. At

[012-128]these, to all appearance, reasonable excuses that she made, her uncle

[012-129]ceased to urge her, and waited till she was somewhat more advanced in

[012-130]age and could mate herself to her own liking. For, said he—and he said

[012-131]quite right—parents are not to settle children in life against their

[012-132]will. But when one least looked for it, lo and behold! one day the

[012-133]demure Marcela makes her appearance turned shepherdess; and, in spite

[012-134]of her uncle and all those of the town that strove to dissuade her,

[012-135]took to going a-field with the other shepherd-lasses of the village,

[012-136]and tending her own flock. And so, since she appeared in public, and

[012-137]her beauty came to be seen openly, I could not well tell you how many

[012-138]rich youths, gentlemen and peasants, have adopted the costume of

[012-139]Chrysostom, and go about these fields making love to her. One of these,

[012-140]as has been already said, was our deceased friend, of whom they say

[012-141]that he did not love but adore her. But you must not suppose, because

[012-142]Marcela chose a life of such liberty and independence, and of so little

[012-143]or rather no retirement, that she has given any occasion, or even the

[012-144]semblance of one, for disparagement of her purity and modesty; on the

[012-145]contrary, such and so great is the vigilance with which she watches

[012-146]over her honour, that of all those that court and woo her not one has

[012-147]boasted, or can with truth boast, that she has given him any hope

[012-148]however small of obtaining his desire. For although she does not avoid

[012-149]or shun the society and conversation of the shepherds, and treats them

[012-150]courteously and kindly, should any one of them come to declare his

[012-151]intention to her, though it be one as proper and holy as that of

[012-152]matrimony, she flings him from her like a catapult. And with this kind

[012-153]of disposition she does more harm in this country than if the plague

[012-154]had got into it, for her affability and her beauty draw on the hearts

[012-155]of those that associate with her to love her and to court her, but her

[012-156]scorn and her frankness bring them to the brink of despair; and so they

[012-157]know not what to say save to proclaim her aloud cruel and hard-hearted,

[012-158]and other names of the same sort which well describe the nature of her

[012-159]character; and if you should remain here any time, señor, you would

[012-160]hear these hills and valleys resounding with the laments of the

[012-161]rejected ones who pursue her. Not far from this there is a spot where

[012-162]there are a couple of dozen of tall beeches, and there is not one of

[012-163]them but has carved and written on its smooth bark the name of Marcela,

[012-164]and above some a crown carved on the same tree as though her lover

[012-165]would say more plainly that Marcela wore and deserved that of all human

[012-166]beauty. Here one shepherd is sighing, there another is lamenting; there

[012-167]love songs are heard, here despairing elegies. One will pass all the

[012-168]hours of the night seated at the foot of some oak or rock, and there,

[012-169]without having closed his weeping eyes, the sun finds him in the

[012-170]morning bemused and bereft of sense; and another without relief or

[012-171]respite to his sighs, stretched on the burning sand in the full heat of

[012-172]the sultry summer noontide, makes his appeal to the compassionate

[012-173]heavens, and over one and the other, over these and all, the beautiful

[012-174]Marcela triumphs free and careless. And all of us that know her are

[012-175]waiting to see what her pride will come to, and who is to be the happy

[012-176]man that will succeed in taming a nature so formidable and gaining

[012-177]possession of a beauty so supreme. All that I have told you being such

[012-178]well-established truth, I am persuaded that what they say of the cause

[012-179]of Chrysostom’s death, as our lad told us, is the same. And so I advise

[012-180]you, señor, fail not to be present to-morrow at his burial, which will

[012-181]be well worth seeing, for Chrysostom had many friends, and it is not

[012-182]half a league from this place to where he directed he should be

[012-183]buried.”

[012-184]“I will make a point of it,” said Don Quixote, “and I thank you for the

[012-185]pleasure you have given me by relating so interesting a tale.”

[012-186]“Oh,” said the goatherd, “I do not know even the half of what has

[012-187]happened to the lovers of Marcela, but perhaps to-morrow we may fall in

[012-188]with some shepherd on the road who can tell us; and now it will be well

[012-189]for you to go and sleep under cover, for the night air may hurt your

[012-190]wound, though with the remedy I have applied to you there is no fear of

[012-191]an untoward result.”

[012-192]Sancho Panza, who was wishing the goatherd’s loquacity at the devil, on

[012-193]his part begged his master to go into Pedro’s hut to sleep. He did so,

[012-194]and passed all the rest of the night in thinking of his lady Dulcinea,

[012-195]in imitation of the lovers of Marcela. Sancho Panza settled himself

[012-196]between Rocinante and his ass, and slept, not like a lover who had been

[012-197]discarded, but like a man who had been soundly kicked.
