[019-001]OF THE SHREWD DISCOURSE WHICH SANCHO HELD WITH HIS MASTER, AND OF THE

[019-002]ADVENTURE THAT BEFELL HIM WITH A DEAD BODY, TOGETHER WITH OTHER NOTABLE

OCCURRENCES

[019-003]“It seems to me, señor, that all these mishaps that have befallen us of

[019-004]late have been without any doubt a punishment for the offence committed

[019-005]by your worship against the order of chivalry in not keeping the oath

[019-006]you made not to eat bread off a tablecloth or embrace the queen, and

[019-007]all the rest of it that your worship swore to observe until you had

[019-008]taken that helmet of Malandrino’s, or whatever the Moor is called, for

[019-009]I do not very well remember.”

[019-010]“Thou art very right, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “but to tell the

[019-011]truth, it had escaped my memory; and likewise thou mayest rely upon it

[019-012]that the affair of the blanket happened to thee because of thy fault in

[019-013]not reminding me of it in time; but I will make amends, for there are

[019-014]ways of compounding for everything in the order of chivalry.”

[019-015]“Why! have I taken an oath of some sort, then?” said Sancho.

[019-016]“It makes no matter that thou hast not taken an oath,” said Don

[019-017]Quixote; “suffice it that I see thou art not quite clear of complicity;

[019-018]and whether or no, it will not be ill done to provide ourselves with a

[019-019]remedy.”

[019-020]“In that case,” said Sancho, “mind that your worship does not forget

[019-021]this as you did the oath; perhaps the phantoms may take it into their

[019-022]heads to amuse themselves once more with me; or even with your worship

[019-023]if they see you so obstinate.”

[019-024]While engaged in this and other talk, night overtook them on the road

[019-025]before they had reached or discovered any place of shelter; and what

[019-026]made it still worse was that they were dying of hunger, for with the

[019-027]loss of the alforjas they had lost their entire larder and

[019-028]commissariat; and to complete the misfortune they met with an adventure

[019-029]which without any invention had really the appearance of one. It so

[019-030]happened that the night closed in somewhat darkly, but for all that

[019-031]they pushed on, Sancho feeling sure that as the road was the king’s

[019-032]highway they might reasonably expect to find some inn within a league

[019-033]or two. Going along, then, in this way, the night dark, the squire

[019-034]hungry, the master sharp-set, they saw coming towards them on the road

[019-035]they were travelling a great number of lights which looked exactly like

[019-036]stars in motion. Sancho was taken aback at the sight of them, nor did

[019-037]Don Quixote altogether relish them: the one pulled up his ass by the

[019-038]halter, the other his hack by the bridle, and they stood still,

[019-039]watching anxiously to see what all this would turn out to be, and found

[019-040]that the lights were approaching them, and the nearer they came the

[019-041]greater they seemed, at which spectacle Sancho began to shake like a

[019-042]man dosed with mercury, and Don Quixote’s hair stood on end; he,

[019-043]however, plucking up spirit a little, said:

[019-044]“This, no doubt, Sancho, will be a most mighty and perilous adventure,

[019-045]in which it will be needful for me to put forth all my valour and

[019-046]resolution.”

[019-047]“Unlucky me!” answered Sancho; “if this adventure happens to be one of

[019-048]phantoms, as I am beginning to think it is, where shall I find the ribs

[019-049]to bear it?”

[019-050]“Be they phantoms ever so much,” said Don Quixote, “I will not permit

[019-051]them to touch a thread of thy garments; for if they played tricks with

[019-052]thee the time before, it was because I was unable to leap the walls of

[019-053]the yard; but now we are on a wide plain, where I shall be able to

[019-054]wield my sword as I please.”

[019-055]“And if they enchant and cripple you as they did the last time,” said

[019-056]Sancho, “what difference will it make being on the open plain or not?”

[019-057]“For all that,” replied Don Quixote, “I entreat thee, Sancho, to keep a

[019-058]good heart, for experience will tell thee what mine is.”

[019-059]“I will, please God,” answered Sancho, and the two retiring to one side

[019-060]of the road set themselves to observe closely what all these moving

[019-061]lights might be; and very soon afterwards they made out some twenty

[019-062]encamisados, all on horseback, with lighted torches in their hands, the

[019-063]awe-inspiring aspect of whom completely extinguished the courage of

[019-064]Sancho, who began to chatter with his teeth like one in the cold fit of

[019-065]an ague; and his heart sank and his teeth chattered still more when

[019-066]they perceived distinctly that behind them there came a litter covered

[019-067]over with black and followed by six more mounted figures in mourning

[019-068]down to the very feet of their mules—for they could perceive plainly

[019-069]they were not horses by the easy pace at which they went. And as the

[019-070]encamisados came along they muttered to themselves in a low plaintive

[019-071]tone. This strange spectacle at such an hour and in such a solitary

[019-072]place was quite enough to strike terror into Sancho’s heart, and even

[019-073]into his master’s; and (save in Don Quixote’s case) did so, for all

[019-074]Sancho’s resolution had now broken down. It was just the opposite with

[019-075]his master, whose imagination immediately conjured up all this to him

[019-076]vividly as one of the adventures of his books.

[019-077]He took it into his head that the litter was a bier on which was borne

[019-078]some sorely wounded or slain knight, to avenge whom was a task reserved

[019-079]for him alone; and without any further reasoning he laid his lance in

[019-080]rest, fixed himself firmly in his saddle, and with gallant spirit and

[019-081]bearing took up his position in the middle of the road where the

[019-082]encamisados must of necessity pass; and as soon as he saw them near at

[019-083]hand he raised his voice and said:

[019-084]“Halt, knights, or whosoever ye may be, and render me account of who ye

[019-085]are, whence ye come, where ye go, what it is ye carry upon that bier,

[019-086]for, to judge by appearances, either ye have done some wrong or some

[019-087]wrong has been done to you, and it is fitting and necessary that I

[019-088]should know, either that I may chastise you for the evil ye have done,

[019-089]or else that I may avenge you for the injury that has been inflicted

[019-090]upon you.”

[019-091]“We are in haste,” answered one of the encamisados, “and the inn is far

[019-092]off, and we cannot stop to render you such an account as you demand;”

[019-093]and spurring his mule he moved on.

[019-094]Don Quixote was mightily provoked by this answer, and seizing the mule

[019-095]by the bridle he said, “Halt, and be more mannerly, and render an

[019-096]account of what I have asked of you; else, take my defiance to combat,

[019-097]all of you.”

[019-098]The mule was shy, and was so frightened at her bridle being seized that

[019-099]rearing up she flung her rider to the ground over her haunches. An

[019-100]attendant who was on foot, seeing the encamisado fall, began to abuse

[019-101]Don Quixote, who now moved to anger, without any more ado, laying his

[019-102]lance in rest charged one of the men in mourning and brought him badly

[019-103]wounded to the ground, and as he wheeled round upon the others the

[019-104]agility with which he attacked and routed them was a sight to see, for

[019-105]it seemed just as if wings had that instant grown upon Rocinante, so

[019-106]lightly and proudly did he bear himself. The encamisados were all timid

[019-107]folk and unarmed, so they speedily made their escape from the fray and

[019-108]set off at a run across the plain with their lighted torches, looking

[019-109]exactly like maskers running on some gala or festival night. The

[019-110]mourners, too, enveloped and swathed in their skirts and gowns, were

[019-111]unable to bestir themselves, and so with entire safety to himself Don

[019-112]Quixote belaboured them all and drove them off against their will, for

[019-113]they all thought it was no man but a devil from hell come to carry away

[019-114]the dead body they had in the litter.

[019-115]Sancho beheld all this in astonishment at the intrepidity of his lord,

[019-116]and said to himself, “Clearly this master of mine is as bold and

[019-117]valiant as he says he is.”

[019-118]A burning torch lay on the ground near the first man whom the mule had

[019-119]thrown, by the light of which Don Quixote perceived him, and coming up

[019-120]to him he presented the point of the lance to his face, calling on him

[019-121]to yield himself prisoner, or else he would kill him; to which the

[019-122]prostrate man replied, “I am prisoner enough as it is; I cannot stir,

[019-123]for one of my legs is broken: I entreat you, if you be a Christian

[019-124]gentleman, not to kill me, which will be committing grave sacrilege,

[019-125]for I am a licentiate and I hold first orders.”

[019-126]“Then what the devil brought you here, being a churchman?” said Don

[019-127]Quixote.

[019-128]“What, señor?” said the other. “My bad luck.”

[019-129]“Then still worse awaits you,” said Don Quixote, “if you do not satisfy

[019-130]me as to all I asked you at first.”

[019-131]“You shall be soon satisfied,” said the licentiate; “you must know,

[019-132]then, that though just now I said I was a licentiate, I am only a

[019-133]bachelor, and my name is Alonzo Lopez; I am a native of Alcobendas, I

[019-134]come from the city of Baeza with eleven others, priests, the same who

[019-135]fled with the torches, and we are going to the city of Segovia

[019-136]accompanying a dead body which is in that litter, and is that of a

[019-137]gentleman who died in Baeza, where he was interred; and now, as I said,

[019-138]we are taking his bones to their burial-place, which is in Segovia,

[019-139]where he was born.”

[019-140]“And who killed him?” asked Don Quixote.

[019-141]“God, by means of a malignant fever that took him,” answered the

[019-142]bachelor.

[019-143]“In that case,” said Don Quixote, “the Lord has relieved me of the task

[019-144]of avenging his death had any other slain him; but, he who slew him

[019-145]having slain him, there is nothing for it but to be silent, and shrug

[019-146]one’s shoulders; I should do the same were he to slay myself; and I

[019-147]would have your reverence know that I am a knight of La Mancha, Don

[019-148]Quixote by name, and it is my business and calling to roam the world

[019-149]righting wrongs and redressing injuries.”

[019-150]“I do not know how that about righting wrongs can be,” said the

[019-151]bachelor, “for from straight you have made me crooked, leaving me with

[019-152]a broken leg that will never see itself straight again all the days of

[019-153]its life; and the injury you have redressed in my case has been to

[019-154]leave me injured in such a way that I shall remain injured for ever;

[019-155]and the height of misadventure it was to fall in with you who go in

[019-156]search of adventures.”

[019-157]“Things do not all happen in the same way,” answered Don Quixote; “it

[019-158]all came, Sir Bachelor Alonzo Lopez, of your going, as you did, by

[019-159]night, dressed in those surplices, with lighted torches, praying,

[019-160]covered with mourning, so that naturally you looked like something evil

[019-161]and of the other world; and so I could not avoid doing my duty in

[019-162]attacking you, and I should have attacked you even had I known

[019-163]positively that you were the very devils of hell, for such I certainly

[019-164]believed and took you to be.”

[019-165]“As my fate has so willed it,” said the bachelor, “I entreat you, sir

[019-166]knight-errant, whose errand has been such an evil one for me, to help

[019-167]me to get from under this mule that holds one of my legs caught between

[019-168]the stirrup and the saddle.”

[019-169]“I would have talked on till to-morrow,” said Don Quixote; “how long

[019-170]were you going to wait before telling me of your distress?”

[019-171]He at once called to Sancho, who, however, had no mind to come, as he

[019-172]was just then engaged in unloading a sumpter mule, well laden with

[019-173]provender, which these worthy gentlemen had brought with them. Sancho

[019-174]made a bag of his coat, and, getting together as much as he could, and

[019-175]as the bag would hold, he loaded his beast, and then hastened to obey

[019-176]his master’s call, and helped him to remove the bachelor from under the

[019-177]mule; then putting him on her back he gave him the torch, and Don

[019-178]Quixote bade him follow the track of his companions, and beg pardon of

[019-179]them on his part for the wrong which he could not help doing them.

[019-180]And said Sancho, “If by chance these gentlemen should want to know who

[019-181]was the hero that served them so, your worship may tell them that he is

[019-182]the famous Don Quixote of La Mancha, otherwise called the Knight of the

[019-183]Rueful Countenance.”

[019-184]The bachelor then took his departure.

[019-185]I forgot to mention that before he did so he said to Don Quixote,

[019-186]“Remember that you stand excommunicated for having laid violent hands

[019-187]on a holy thing, _juxta illud, si quis, suadente diabolo_.”

[019-188]“I do not understand that Latin,” answered Don Quixote, “but I know

[019-189]well I did not lay hands, only this pike; besides, I did not think I

[019-190]was committing an assault upon priests or things of the Church, which,

[019-191]like a Catholic and faithful Christian as I am, I respect and revere,

[019-192]but upon phantoms and spectres of the other world; but even so, I

[019-193]remember how it fared with Cid Ruy Diaz when he broke the chair of the

[019-194]ambassador of that king before his Holiness the Pope, who

[019-195]excommunicated him for the same; and yet the good Roderick of Vivar

[019-196]bore himself that day like a very noble and valiant knight.”

[019-197]On hearing this the bachelor took his departure, as has been said,

[019-198]without making any reply; and Don Quixote asked Sancho what had induced

[019-199]him to call him the “Knight of the Rueful Countenance” more then than

[019-200]at any other time.

[019-201]“I will tell you,” answered Sancho; “it was because I have been looking

[019-202]at you for some time by the light of the torch held by that

[019-203]unfortunate, and verily your worship has got of late the most

[019-204]ill-favoured countenance I ever saw: it must be either owing to the

[019-205]fatigue of this combat, or else to the want of teeth and grinders.”

[019-206]“It is not that,” replied Don Quixote, “but because the sage whose duty

[019-207]it will be to write the history of my achievements must have thought it

[019-208]proper that I should take some distinctive name as all knights of yore

[019-209]did; one being ‘He of the Burning Sword,’ another ‘He of the Unicorn,’

[019-210]this one ‘He of the Damsels,’ that ‘He of the Phœnix,’ another ‘The

[019-211]Knight of the Griffin,’ and another ‘He of the Death,’ and by these

[019-212]names and designations they were known all the world round; and so I

[019-213]say that the sage aforesaid must have put it into your mouth and mind

[019-214]just now to call me ‘The Knight of the Rueful Countenance,’ as I intend

[019-215]to call myself from this day forward; and that the said name may fit me

[019-216]better, I mean, when the opportunity offers, to have a very rueful

[019-217]countenance painted on my shield.”

[019-218]“There is no occasion, señor, for wasting time or money on making that

[019-219]countenance,” said Sancho; “for all that need be done is for your

[019-220]worship to show your own, face to face, to those who look at you, and

[019-221]without anything more, either image or shield, they will call you ‘Him

[019-222]of the Rueful Countenance’ and believe me I am telling you the truth,

[019-223]for I assure you, señor (and in good part be it said), hunger and the

[019-224]loss of your grinders have given you such an ill-favoured face that, as

[019-225]I say, the rueful picture may be very well spared.”

[019-226]Don Quixote laughed at Sancho’s pleasantry; nevertheless he resolved to

[019-227]call himself by that name, and have his shield or buckler painted as he

[019-228]had devised.

[019-229]Don Quixote would have looked to see whether the body in the litter

[019-230]were bones or not, but Sancho would not have it, saying:

[019-231]“Señor, you have ended this perilous adventure more safely for yourself

[019-232]than any of those I have seen: perhaps these people, though beaten and

[019-233]routed, may bethink themselves that it is a single man that has beaten

[019-234]them, and feeling sore and ashamed of it may take heart and come in

[019-235]search of us and give us trouble enough. The ass is in proper trim, the

[019-236]mountains are near at hand, hunger presses, we have nothing more to do

[019-237]but make good our retreat, and, as the saying is, the dead to the grave

[019-238]and the living to the loaf.”

[019-239]And driving his ass before him he begged his master to follow, who,

[019-240]feeling that Sancho was right, did so without replying; and after

[019-241]proceeding some little distance between two hills they found themselves

[019-242]in a wide and retired valley, where they alighted, and Sancho unloaded

[019-243]his beast, and stretched upon the green grass, with hunger for sauce,

[019-244]they breakfasted, dined, lunched, and supped all at once, satisfying

[019-245]their appetites with more than one store of cold meat which the dead

[019-246]man’s clerical gentlemen (who seldom put themselves on short allowance)

[019-247]had brought with them on their sumpter mule. But another piece of

[019-248]ill-luck befell them, which Sancho held the worst of all, and that was

[019-249]that they had no wine to drink, nor even water to moisten their lips;

[019-250]and as thirst tormented them, Sancho, observing that the meadow where

[019-251]they were was full of green and tender grass, said what will be told in

[019-252]the following chapter.
