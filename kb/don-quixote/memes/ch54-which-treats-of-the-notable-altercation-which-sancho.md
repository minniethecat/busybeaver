WHICH TREATS OF THE NOTABLE ALTERCATION WHICH SANCHO PANZA HAD WITH DON

[054-001]QUIXOTE’S NIECE, AND HOUSEKEEPER, TOGETHER WITH OTHER DROLL MATTERS

p02a.jpg (159K)


[054-002]The history relates that the outcry Don Quixote, the curate, and the

[054-003]barber heard came from the niece and the housekeeper exclaiming to

[054-004]Sancho, who was striving to force his way in to see Don Quixote while

[054-005]they held the door against him, “What does the vagabond want in this

[054-006]house? Be off to your own, brother, for it is you, and no one else,

[054-007]that delude my master, and lead him astray, and take him tramping about

[054-008]the country.”

[054-009]To which Sancho replied, “Devil’s own housekeeper! it is I who am

[054-010]deluded, and led astray, and taken tramping about the country, and not

[054-011]thy master! He has carried me all over the world, and you are mightily

[054-012]mistaken. He enticed me away from home by a trick, promising me an

[054-013]island, which I am still waiting for.”

[054-014]“May evil islands choke thee, thou detestable Sancho,” said the niece;

[054-015]“What are islands? Is it something to eat, glutton and gormandiser that

[054-016]thou art?”

[054-017]“It is not something to eat,” replied Sancho, “but something to govern

[054-018]and rule, and better than four cities or four judgeships at court.”

[054-019]“For all that,” said the housekeeper, “you don’t enter here, you bag of

[054-020]mischief and sack of knavery; go govern your house and dig your

[054-021]seed-patch, and give over looking for islands or shylands.”

[054-022]The curate and the barber listened with great amusement to the words of

[054-023]the three; but Don Quixote, uneasy lest Sancho should blab and blurt

[054-024]out a whole heap of mischievous stupidities, and touch upon points that

[054-025]might not be altogether to his credit, called to him and made the other

[054-026]two hold their tongues and let him come in. Sancho entered, and the

[054-027]curate and the barber took their leave of Don Quixote, of whose

[054-028]recovery they despaired when they saw how wedded he was to his crazy

[054-029]ideas, and how saturated with the nonsense of his unlucky chivalry; and

[054-030]said the curate to the barber, “You will see, gossip, that when we are

[054-031]least thinking of it, our gentleman will be off once more for another

[054-032]flight.”

[054-033]“I have no doubt of it,” returned the barber; “but I do not wonder so

[054-034]much at the madness of the knight as at the simplicity of the squire,

[054-035]who has such a firm belief in all that about the island, that I suppose

[054-036]all the exposures that could be imagined would not get it out of his

[054-037]head.”

[054-038]“God help them,” said the curate; “and let us be on the look-out to see

[054-039]what comes of all these absurdities of the knight and squire, for it

[054-040]seems as if they had both been cast in the same mould, and the madness

[054-041]of the master without the simplicity of the man would not be worth a

[054-042]farthing.”

[054-043]“That is true,” said the barber, “and I should like very much to know

[054-044]what the pair are talking about at this moment.”

[054-045]“I promise you,” said the curate, “the niece or the housekeeper will

[054-046]tell us by-and-by, for they are not the ones to forget to listen.”

[054-047]Meanwhile Don Quixote shut himself up in his room with Sancho, and when

[054-048]they were alone he said to him, “It grieves me greatly, Sancho, that

[054-049]thou shouldst have said, and sayest, that I took thee out of thy

[054-050]cottage, when thou knowest I did not remain in my house. We sallied

[054-051]forth together, we took the road together, we wandered abroad together;

[054-052]we have had the same fortune and the same luck; if they blanketed thee

[054-053]once, they belaboured me a hundred times, and that is the only

[054-054]advantage I have of thee.”

[054-055]“That was only reasonable,” replied Sancho, “for, by what your worship

[054-056]says, misfortunes belong more properly to knights-errant than to their

[054-057]squires.”

[054-058]“Thou art mistaken, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “according to the maxim

[054-059]_quando caput dolet_, etc.”

[054-060]“I don’t understand any language but my own,” said Sancho.

[054-061]“I mean to say,” said Don Quixote, “that when the head suffers all the

[054-062]members suffer; and so, being thy lord and master, I am thy head, and

[054-063]thou a part of me as thou art my servant; and therefore any evil that

[054-064]affects or shall affect me should give thee pain, and what affects thee

[054-065]give pain to me.”

[054-066]“It should be so,” said Sancho; “but when I was blanketed as a member,

[054-067]my head was on the other side of the wall, looking on while I was

[054-068]flying through the air, and did not feel any pain whatever; and if the

[054-069]members are obliged to feel the suffering of the head, it should be

[054-070]obliged to feel their sufferings.”

[054-071]“Dost thou mean to say now, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that I did not

[054-072]feel when they were blanketing thee? If thou dost, thou must not say so

[054-073]or think so, for I felt more pain then in spirit than thou didst in

[054-074]body. But let us put that aside for the present, for we shall have

[054-075]opportunities enough for considering and settling the point; tell me,

[054-076]Sancho my friend, what do they say about me in the village here? What

[054-077]do the common people think of me? What do the hidalgos? What do the

[054-078]caballeros? What do they say of my valour; of my achievements; of my

[054-079]courtesy? How do they treat the task I have undertaken in reviving and

[054-080]restoring to the world the now forgotten order of chivalry? In short,

[054-081]Sancho, I would have thee tell me all that has come to thine ears on

[054-082]this subject; and thou art to tell me, without adding anything to the

[054-083]good or taking away anything from the bad; for it is the duty of loyal

[054-084]vassals to tell the truth to their lords just as it is and in its

[054-085]proper shape, not allowing flattery to add to it or any idle deference

[054-086]to lessen it. And I would have thee know, Sancho, that if the naked

[054-087]truth, undisguised by flattery, came to the ears of princes, times

[054-088]would be different, and other ages would be reckoned iron ages more

[054-089]than ours, which I hold to be the golden of these latter days. Profit

[054-090]by this advice, Sancho, and report to me clearly and faithfully the

[054-091]truth of what thou knowest touching what I have demanded of thee.”

[054-092]“That I will do with all my heart, master,” replied Sancho, “provided

[054-093]your worship will not be vexed at what I say, as you wish me to say it

[054-094]out in all its nakedness, without putting any more clothes on it than

[054-095]it came to my knowledge in.”

[054-096]“I will not be vexed at all,” returned Don Quixote; “thou mayest speak

[054-097]freely, Sancho, and without any beating about the bush.”

[054-098]“Well then,” said he, “first of all, I have to tell you that the common

[054-099]people consider your worship a mighty great madman, and me no less a

[054-100]fool. The hidalgos say that, not keeping within the bounds of your

[054-101]quality of gentleman, you have assumed the ‘Don,’ and made a knight of

[054-102]yourself at a jump, with four vine-stocks and a couple of acres of

[054-103]land, and never a shirt to your back. The caballeros say they do not

[054-104]want to have hidalgos setting up in opposition to them, particularly

[054-105]squire hidalgos who polish their own shoes and darn their black

[054-106]stockings with green silk.”

[054-107]“That,” said Don Quixote, “does not apply to me, for I always go well

[054-108]dressed and never patched; ragged I may be, but ragged more from the

[054-109]wear and tear of arms than of time.”

[054-110]“As to your worship’s valour, courtesy, accomplishments, and task,

[054-111]there is a variety of opinions. Some say, ‘mad but droll;’ others,

[054-112]‘valiant but unlucky;’ others, ‘courteous but meddling,’ and then they

[054-113]go into such a number of things that they don’t leave a whole bone

[054-114]either in your worship or in myself.”

[054-115]“Recollect, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that wherever virtue exists in

[054-116]an eminent degree it is persecuted. Few or none of the famous men that

[054-117]have lived escaped being calumniated by malice. Julius Cæsar, the

[054-118]boldest, wisest, and bravest of captains, was charged with being

[054-119]ambitious, and not particularly cleanly in his dress, or pure in his

[054-120]morals. Of Alexander, whose deeds won him the name of Great, they say

[054-121]that he was somewhat of a drunkard. Of Hercules, him of the many

[054-122]labours, it is said that he was lewd and luxurious. Of Don Galaor, the

[054-123]brother of Amadis of Gaul, it was whispered that he was

[054-124]over-quarrelsome, and of his brother that he was lachrymose. So that, O

[054-125]Sancho, amongst all these calumnies against good men, mine may be let

[054-126]pass, since they are no more than thou hast said.”

[054-127]“That’s just where it is, body of my father!”

[054-128]“Is there more, then?” asked Don Quixote.

[054-129]“There’s the tail to be skinned yet,” said Sancho; “all so far is cakes

[054-130]and fancy bread; but if your worship wants to know all about the

[054-131]calumnies they bring against you, I will fetch you one this instant who

[054-132]can tell you the whole of them without missing an atom; for last night

[054-133]the son of Bartholomew Carrasco, who has been studying at Salamanca,

[054-134]came home after having been made a bachelor, and when I went to welcome

[054-135]him, he told me that your worship’s history is already abroad in books,

[054-136]with the title of THE INGENIOUS GENTLEMAN DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA; and

[054-137]he says they mention me in it by my own name of Sancho Panza, and the

[054-138]lady Dulcinea del Toboso too, and divers things that happened to us

[054-139]when we were alone; so that I crossed myself in my wonder how the

[054-140]historian who wrote them down could have known them.”

[054-141]“I promise thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “the author of our history

[054-142]will be some sage enchanter; for to such nothing that they choose to

[054-143]write about is hidden.”

[054-144]“What!” said Sancho, “a sage and an enchanter! Why, the bachelor Samson

[054-145]Carrasco (that is the name of him I spoke of) says the author of the

[054-146]history is called Cide Hamete Berengena.”

[054-147]“That is a Moorish name,” said Don Quixote.

[054-148]“May be so,” replied Sancho; “for I have heard say that the Moors are

[054-149]mostly great lovers of berengenas.”

[054-150]“Thou must have mistaken the surname of this ‘Cide’—which means in

[054-151]Arabic ‘Lord’—Sancho,” observed Don Quixote.

[054-152]“Very likely,” replied Sancho, “but if your worship wishes me to fetch

[054-153]the bachelor I will go for him in a twinkling.”

[054-154]“Thou wilt do me a great pleasure, my friend,” said Don Quixote, “for

[054-155]what thou hast told me has amazed me, and I shall not eat a morsel that

[054-156]will agree with me until I have heard all about it.”

[054-157]“Then I am off for him,” said Sancho; and leaving his master he went in

[054-158]quest of the bachelor, with whom he returned in a short time, and, all

[054-159]three together, they had a very droll colloquy.
