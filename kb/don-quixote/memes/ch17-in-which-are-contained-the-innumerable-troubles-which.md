IN WHICH ARE CONTAINED THE INNUMERABLE TROUBLES WHICH THE BRAVE DON

[017-001]QUIXOTE AND HIS GOOD SQUIRE SANCHO PANZA ENDURED IN THE INN, WHICH TO

HIS MISFORTUNE HE TOOK TO BE A CASTLE

c17a.jpg (87K)


[017-002]By this time Don Quixote had recovered from his swoon; and in the same

[017-003]tone of voice in which he had called to his squire the day before when

[017-004]he lay stretched “in the vale of the stakes,” he began calling to him

[017-005]now, “Sancho, my friend, art thou asleep? sleepest thou, friend

[017-006]Sancho?”

[017-007]“How can I sleep, curses on it!” returned Sancho discontentedly and

[017-008]bitterly, “when it is plain that all the devils have been at me this

[017-009]night?”

[017-010]“Thou mayest well believe that,” answered Don Quixote, “because, either

[017-011]I know little, or this castle is enchanted, for thou must know—but this

[017-012]that I am now about to tell thee thou must swear to keep secret until

[017-013]after my death.”

[017-014]“I swear it,” answered Sancho.

[017-015]“I say so,” continued Don Quixote, “because I hate taking away anyone’s

[017-016]good name.”

[017-017]“I say,” replied Sancho, “that I swear to hold my tongue about it till

[017-018]the end of your worship’s days, and God grant I may be able to let it

[017-019]out to-morrow.”

[017-020]“Do I do thee such injuries, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that thou

[017-021]wouldst see me dead so soon?”

[017-022]“It is not for that,” replied Sancho, “but because I hate keeping

[017-023]things long, and I don’t want them to grow rotten with me from

[017-024]over-keeping.”

[017-025]“At any rate,” said Don Quixote, “I have more confidence in thy

[017-026]affection and good nature; and so I would have thee know that this

[017-027]night there befell me one of the strangest adventures that I could

[017-028]describe, and to relate it to thee briefly thou must know that a little

[017-029]while ago the daughter of the lord of this castle came to me, and that

[017-030]she is the most elegant and beautiful damsel that could be found in the

[017-031]wide world. What I could tell thee of the charms of her person! of her

[017-032]lively wit! of other secret matters which, to preserve the fealty I owe

[017-033]to my lady Dulcinea del Toboso, I shall pass over unnoticed and in

[017-034]silence! I will only tell thee that, either fate being envious of so

[017-035]great a boon placed in my hands by good fortune, or perhaps (and this

[017-036]is more probable) this castle being, as I have already said, enchanted,

[017-037]at the time when I was engaged in the sweetest and most amorous

[017-038]discourse with her, there came, without my seeing or knowing whence it

[017-039]came, a hand attached to some arm of some huge giant, that planted such

[017-040]a cuff on my jaws that I have them all bathed in blood, and then

[017-041]pummelled me in such a way that I am in a worse plight than yesterday

[017-042]when the carriers, on account of Rocinante’s misbehaviour, inflicted on

[017-043]us the injury thou knowest of; whence conjecture that there must be

[017-044]some enchanted Moor guarding the treasure of this damsel’s beauty, and

[017-045]that it is not for me.”

[017-046]“Not for me either,” said Sancho, “for more than four hundred Moors

[017-047]have so thrashed me that the drubbing of the stakes was cakes and

[017-048]fancy-bread to it. But tell me, señor, what do you call this excellent

[017-049]and rare adventure that has left us as we are left now? Though your

[017-050]worship was not so badly off, having in your arms that incomparable

[017-051]beauty you spoke of; but I, what did I have, except the heaviest whacks

[017-052]I think I had in all my life? Unlucky me and the mother that bore me!

[017-053]for I am not a knight-errant and never expect to be one, and of all the

[017-054]mishaps, the greater part falls to my share.”

[017-055]“Then thou hast been thrashed too?” said Don Quixote.

[017-056]“Didn’t I say so? worse luck to my line!” said Sancho.

[017-057]“Be not distressed, friend,” said Don Quixote, “for I will now make the

[017-058]precious balsam with which we shall cure ourselves in the twinkling of

[017-059]an eye.”

[017-060]By this time the cuadrillero had succeeded in lighting the lamp, and

[017-061]came in to see the man that he thought had been killed; and as Sancho

[017-062]caught sight of him at the door, seeing him coming in his shirt, with a

[017-063]cloth on his head, and a lamp in his hand, and a very forbidding

[017-064]countenance, he said to his master, “Señor, can it be that this is the

[017-065]enchanted Moor coming back to give us more castigation if there be

[017-066]anything still left in the ink-bottle?”

[017-067]“It cannot be the Moor,” answered Don Quixote, “for those under

[017-068]enchantment do not let themselves be seen by anyone.”

[017-069]“If they don’t let themselves be seen, they let themselves be felt,”

[017-070]said Sancho; “if not, let my shoulders speak to the point.”

[017-071]“Mine could speak too,” said Don Quixote, “but that is not a sufficient

[017-072]reason for believing that what we see is the enchanted Moor.”

[017-073]The officer came up, and finding them engaged in such a peaceful

[017-074]conversation, stood amazed; though Don Quixote, to be sure, still lay

[017-075]on his back unable to move from pure pummelling and plasters. The

[017-076]officer turned to him and said, “Well, how goes it, good man?”

[017-077]“I would speak more politely if I were you,” replied Don Quixote; “is

[017-078]it the way of this country to address knights-errant in that style, you

[017-079]booby?”

[017-080]The cuadrillero finding himself so disrespectfully treated by such a

[017-081]sorry-looking individual, lost his temper, and raising the lamp full of

[017-082]oil, smote Don Quixote such a blow with it on the head that he gave him

[017-083]a badly broken pate; then, all being in darkness, he went out, and

[017-084]Sancho Panza said, “That is certainly the enchanted Moor, Señor, and he

[017-085]keeps the treasure for others, and for us only the cuffs and

[017-086]lamp-whacks.”

[017-087]“That is the truth,” answered Don Quixote, “and there is no use in

[017-088]troubling oneself about these matters of enchantment or being angry or

[017-089]vexed at them, for as they are invisible and visionary we shall find no

[017-090]one on whom to avenge ourselves, do what we may; rise, Sancho, if thou

[017-091]canst, and call the alcaide of this fortress, and get him to give me a

[017-092]little oil, wine, salt, and rosemary to make the salutiferous balsam,

[017-093]for indeed I believe I have great need of it now, because I am losing

[017-094]much blood from the wound that phantom gave me.”

[017-095]Sancho got up with pain enough in his bones, and went after the

[017-096]innkeeper in the dark, and meeting the officer, who was looking to see

[017-097]what had become of his enemy, he said to him, “Señor, whoever you are,

[017-098]do us the favour and kindness to give us a little rosemary, oil, salt,

[017-099]and wine, for it is wanted to cure one of the best knights-errant on

[017-100]earth, who lies on yonder bed wounded by the hands of the enchanted

[017-101]Moor that is in this inn.”

[017-102]When the officer heard him talk in this way, he took him for a man out

[017-103]of his senses, and as day was now beginning to break, he opened the inn

[017-104]gate, and calling the host, he told him what this good man wanted. The

[017-105]host furnished him with what he required, and Sancho brought it to Don

[017-106]Quixote, who, with his hand to his head, was bewailing the pain of the

[017-107]blow of the lamp, which had done him no more harm than raising a couple

[017-108]of rather large lumps, and what he fancied blood was only the sweat

[017-109]that flowed from him in his sufferings during the late storm. To be

[017-110]brief, he took the materials, of which he made a compound, mixing them

[017-111]all and boiling them a good while until it seemed to him they had come

[017-112]to perfection. He then asked for some vial to pour it into, and as

[017-113]there was not one in the inn, he decided on putting it into a tin

[017-114]oil-bottle or flask of which the host made him a free gift; and over

[017-115]the flask he repeated more than eighty paternosters and as many more

[017-116]ave-marias, salves, and credos, accompanying each word with a cross by

[017-117]way of benediction, at all which there were present Sancho, the

[017-118]innkeeper, and the cuadrillero; for the carrier was now peacefully

[017-119]engaged in attending to the comfort of his mules.

[017-120]This being accomplished, he felt anxious to make trial himself, on the

[017-121]spot, of the virtue of this precious balsam, as he considered it, and

[017-122]so he drank near a quart of what could not be put into the flask and

[017-123]remained in the pigskin in which it had been boiled; but scarcely had

[017-124]he done drinking when he began to vomit in such a way that nothing was

[017-125]left in his stomach, and with the pangs and spasms of vomiting he broke

[017-126]into a profuse sweat, on account of which he bade them cover him up and

[017-127]leave him alone. They did so, and he lay sleeping more than three

[017-128]hours, at the end of which he awoke and felt very great bodily relief

[017-129]and so much ease from his bruises that he thought himself quite cured,

[017-130]and verily believed he had hit upon the balsam of Fierabras; and that

[017-131]with this remedy he might thenceforward, without any fear, face any

[017-132]kind of destruction, battle, or combat, however perilous it might be.

[017-133]Sancho Panza, who also regarded the amendment of his master as

[017-134]miraculous, begged him to give him what was left in the pigskin, which

[017-135]was no small quantity. Don Quixote consented, and he, taking it with

[017-136]both hands, in good faith and with a better will, gulped down and

[017-137]drained off very little less than his master. But the fact is, that the

[017-138]stomach of poor Sancho was of necessity not so delicate as that of his

[017-139]master, and so, before vomiting, he was seized with such gripings and

[017-140]retchings, and such sweats and faintness, that verily and truly he

[017-141]believed his last hour had come, and finding himself so racked and

[017-142]tormented he cursed the balsam and the thief that had given it to him.

[017-143]Don Quixote seeing him in this state said, “It is my belief, Sancho,

[017-144]that this mischief comes of thy not being dubbed a knight, for I am

[017-145]persuaded this liquor cannot be good for those who are not so.”

[017-146]“If your worship knew that,” returned Sancho—“woe betide me and all my

[017-147]kindred!—why did you let me taste it?”

[017-148]At this moment the draught took effect, and the poor squire began to

[017-149]discharge both ways at such a rate that the rush mat on which he had

[017-150]thrown himself and the canvas blanket he had covering him were fit for

[017-151]nothing afterwards. He sweated and perspired with such paroxysms and

[017-152]convulsions that not only he himself but all present thought his end

[017-153]had come. This tempest and tribulation lasted about two hours, at the

[017-154]end of which he was left, not like his master, but so weak and

[017-155]exhausted that he could not stand. Don Quixote, however, who, as has

[017-156]been said, felt himself relieved and well, was eager to take his

[017-157]departure at once in quest of adventures, as it seemed to him that all

[017-158]the time he loitered there was a fraud upon the world and those in it

[017-159]who stood in need of his help and protection, all the more when he had

[017-160]the security and confidence his balsam afforded him; and so, urged by

[017-161]this impulse, he saddled Rocinante himself and put the pack-saddle on

[017-162]his squire’s beast, whom likewise he helped to dress and mount the ass;

[017-163]after which he mounted his horse and turning to a corner of the inn he

[017-164]laid hold of a pike that stood there, to serve him by way of a lance.

[017-165]All that were in the inn, who were more than twenty persons, stood

[017-166]watching him; the innkeeper’s daughter was likewise observing him, and

[017-167]he too never took his eyes off her, and from time to time fetched a

[017-168]sigh that he seemed to pluck up from the depths of his bowels; but they

[017-169]all thought it must be from the pain he felt in his ribs; at any rate

[017-170]they who had seen him plastered the night before thought so.

[017-171]As soon as they were both mounted, at the gate of the inn, he called to

[017-172]the host and said in a very grave and measured voice, “Many and great

[017-173]are the favours, Señor Alcaide, that I have received in this castle of

[017-174]yours, and I remain under the deepest obligation to be grateful to you

[017-175]for them all the days of my life; if I can repay them in avenging you

[017-176]of any arrogant foe who may have wronged you, know that my calling is

[017-177]no other than to aid the weak, to avenge those who suffer wrong, and to

[017-178]chastise perfidy. Search your memory, and if you find anything of this

[017-179]kind you need only tell me of it, and I promise you by the order of

[017-180]knighthood which I have received to procure you satisfaction and

[017-181]reparation to the utmost of your desire.”

[017-182]The innkeeper replied to him with equal calmness, “Sir Knight, I do not

[017-183]want your worship to avenge me of any wrong, because when any is done

[017-184]me I can take what vengeance seems good to me; the only thing I want is

[017-185]that you pay me the score that you have run up in the inn last night,

[017-186]as well for the straw and barley for your two beasts, as for supper and

[017-187]beds.”

[017-188]“Then this is an inn?” said Don Quixote.

[017-189]“And a very respectable one,” said the innkeeper.

[017-190]“I have been under a mistake all this time,” answered Don Quixote, “for

[017-191]in truth I thought it was a castle, and not a bad one; but since it

[017-192]appears that it is not a castle but an inn, all that can be done now is

[017-193]that you should excuse the payment, for I cannot contravene the rule of

[017-194]knights-errant, of whom I know as a fact (and up to the present I have

[017-195]read nothing to the contrary) that they never paid for lodging or

[017-196]anything else in the inn where they might be; for any hospitality that

[017-197]might be offered them is their due by law and right in return for the

[017-198]insufferable toil they endure in seeking adventures by night and by

[017-199]day, in summer and in winter, on foot and on horseback, in hunger and

[017-200]thirst, cold and heat, exposed to all the inclemencies of heaven and

[017-201]all the hardships of earth.”

[017-202]“I have little to do with that,” replied the innkeeper; “pay me what

[017-203]you owe me, and let us have no more talk of chivalry, for all I care

[017-204]about is to get my money.”

[017-205]“You are a stupid, scurvy innkeeper,” said Don Quixote, and putting

[017-206]spurs to Rocinante and bringing his pike to the slope he rode out of

[017-207]the inn before anyone could stop him, and pushed on some distance

[017-208]without looking to see if his squire was following him.

[017-209]The innkeeper when he saw him go without paying him ran to get payment

[017-210]of Sancho, who said that as his master would not pay neither would he,

[017-211]because, being as he was squire to a knight-errant, the same rule and

[017-212]reason held good for him as for his master with regard to not paying

[017-213]anything in inns and hostelries. At this the innkeeper waxed very

[017-214]wroth, and threatened if he did not pay to compel him in a way that he

[017-215]would not like. To which Sancho made answer that by the law of chivalry

[017-216]his master had received he would not pay a rap, though it cost him his

[017-217]life; for the excellent and ancient usage of knights-errant was not

[017-218]going to be violated by him, nor should the squires of such as were yet

[017-219]to come into the world ever complain of him or reproach him with

[017-220]breaking so just a privilege.

[017-221]The ill-luck of the unfortunate Sancho so ordered it that among the

[017-222]company in the inn there were four woolcarders from Segovia, three

[017-223]needle-makers from the Colt of Cordova, and two lodgers from the Fair

[017-224]of Seville, lively fellows, tender-hearted, fond of a joke, and

[017-225]playful, who, almost as if instigated and moved by a common impulse,

[017-226]made up to Sancho and dismounted him from his ass, while one of them

[017-227]went in for the blanket of the host’s bed; but on flinging him into it

[017-228]they looked up, and seeing that the ceiling was somewhat lower than

[017-229]what they required for their work, they decided upon going out into the

[017-230]yard, which was bounded by the sky, and there, putting Sancho in the

[017-231]middle of the blanket, they began to raise him high, making sport with

[017-232]him as they would with a dog at Shrovetide.

[017-233]The cries of the poor blanketed wretch were so loud that they reached

[017-234]the ears of his master, who, halting to listen attentively, was

[017-235]persuaded that some new adventure was coming, until he clearly

[017-236]perceived that it was his squire who uttered them. Wheeling about he

[017-237]came up to the inn with a laborious gallop, and finding it shut went

[017-238]round it to see if he could find some way of getting in; but as soon as

[017-239]he came to the wall of the yard, which was not very high, he discovered

[017-240]the game that was being played with his squire. He saw him rising and

[017-241]falling in the air with such grace and nimbleness that, had his rage

[017-242]allowed him, it is my belief he would have laughed. He tried to climb

[017-243]from his horse on to the top of the wall, but he was so bruised and

[017-244]battered that he could not even dismount; and so from the back of his

[017-245]horse he began to utter such maledictions and objurgations against

[017-246]those who were blanketing Sancho as it would be impossible to write

[017-247]down accurately: they, however, did not stay their laughter or their

[017-248]work for this, nor did the flying Sancho cease his lamentations,

[017-249]mingled now with threats, now with entreaties but all to little

[017-250]purpose, or none at all, until from pure weariness they left off. They

[017-251]then brought him his ass, and mounting him on top of it they put his

[017-252]jacket round him; and the compassionate Maritornes, seeing him so

[017-253]exhausted, thought fit to refresh him with a jug of water, and that it

[017-254]might be all the cooler she fetched it from the well. Sancho took it,

[017-255]and as he was raising it to his mouth he was stopped by the cries of

[017-256]his master exclaiming, “Sancho, my son, drink not water; drink it not,

[017-257]my son, for it will kill thee; see, here I have the blessed balsam (and

[017-258]he held up the flask of liquor), and with drinking two drops of it thou

[017-259]wilt certainly be restored.”

[017-260]At these words Sancho turned his eyes asquint, and in a still louder

[017-261]voice said, “Can it be your worship has forgotten that I am not a

[017-262]knight, or do you want me to end by vomiting up what bowels I have left

[017-263]after last night? Keep your liquor in the name of all the devils, and

[017-264]leave me to myself!” and at one and the same instant he left off

[017-265]talking and began drinking; but as at the first sup he perceived it was

[017-266]water he did not care to go on with it, and begged Maritornes to fetch

[017-267]him some wine, which she did with right good will, and paid for it with

[017-268]her own money; for indeed they say of her that, though she was in that

[017-269]line of life, there was some faint and distant resemblance to a

[017-270]Christian about her. When Sancho had done drinking he dug his heels

[017-271]into his ass, and the gate of the inn being thrown open he passed out

[017-272]very well pleased at having paid nothing and carried his point, though

[017-273]it had been at the expense of his usual sureties, his shoulders. It is

[017-274]true that the innkeeper detained his alforjas in payment of what was

[017-275]owing to him, but Sancho took his departure in such a flurry that he

[017-276]never missed them. The innkeeper, as soon as he saw him off, wanted to

[017-277]bar the gate close, but the blanketers would not agree to it, for they

[017-278]were fellows who would not have cared two farthings for Don Quixote,

[017-279]even had he been really one of the knights-errant of the Round Table.
