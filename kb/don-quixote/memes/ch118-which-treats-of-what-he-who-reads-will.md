[118-001]WHICH TREATS OF WHAT HE WHO READS WILL SEE, OR WHAT HE WHO HAS IT READ

TO HIM WILL HEAR

p66a.jpg (125K)


[118-002]As he left Barcelona, Don Quixote turned gaze upon the spot where he

[118-003]had fallen. “Here Troy was,” said he; “here my ill-luck, not my

[118-004]cowardice, robbed me of all the glory I had won; here Fortune made me

[118-005]the victim of her caprices; here the lustre of my achievements was

[118-006]dimmed; here, in a word, fell my happiness never to rise again.”

p66b.jpg (251K)


[118-007]“Señor,” said Sancho on hearing this, “it is the part of brave hearts

[118-008]to be patient in adversity just as much as to be glad in prosperity; I

[118-009]judge by myself, for, if when I was a governor I was glad, now that I

[118-010]am a squire and on foot I am not sad; and I have heard say that she

[118-011]whom commonly they call Fortune is a drunken whimsical jade, and, what

[118-012]is more, blind, and therefore neither sees what she does, nor knows

[118-013]whom she casts down or whom she sets up.”

[118-014]“Thou art a great philosopher, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “thou

[118-015]speakest very sensibly; I know not who taught thee. But I can tell thee

[118-016]there is no such thing as Fortune in the world, nor does anything which

[118-017]takes place there, be it good or bad, come about by chance, but by the

[118-018]special preordination of heaven; and hence the common saying that ‘each

[118-019]of us is the maker of his own Fortune.’ I have been that of mine; but

[118-020]not with the proper amount of prudence, and my self-confidence has

[118-021]therefore made me pay dearly; for I ought to have reflected that

[118-022]Rocinante’s feeble strength could not resist the mighty bulk of the

[118-023]Knight of the White Moon’s horse. In a word, I ventured it, I did my

[118-024]best, I was overthrown, but though I lost my honour I did not lose nor

[118-025]can I lose the virtue of keeping my word. When I was a knight-errant,

[118-026]daring and valiant, I supported my achievements by hand and deed, and

[118-027]now that I am a humble squire I will support my words by keeping the

[118-028]promise I have given. Forward then, Sancho my friend, let us go to keep

[118-029]the year of the novitiate in our own country, and in that seclusion we

[118-030]shall pick up fresh strength to return to the by me never-forgotten

[118-031]calling of arms.”

[118-032]“Señor,” returned Sancho, “travelling on foot is not such a pleasant

[118-033]thing that it makes me feel disposed or tempted to make long marches.

[118-034]Let us leave this armour hung up on some tree, instead of someone that

[118-035]has been hanged; and then with me on Dapple’s back and my feet off the

[118-036]ground we will arrange the stages as your worship pleases to measure

[118-037]them out; but to suppose that I am going to travel on foot, and make

[118-038]long ones, is to suppose nonsense.”

[118-039]“Thou sayest well, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “let my armour be hung up

[118-040]for a trophy, and under it or round it we will carve on the trees what

[118-041]was inscribed on the trophy of Roland’s armour-

[118-042]These let none move

[118-043]Who dareth not his might with Roland prove.”

[118-044]“That’s the very thing,” said Sancho; “and if it was not that we should

[118-045]feel the want of Rocinante on the road, it would be as well to leave

[118-046]him hung up too.”

[118-047]“And yet, I had rather not have either him or the armour hung up,” said

[118-048]Don Quixote, “that it may not be said, ‘for good service a bad

[118-049]return.’”

[118-050]“Your worship is right,” said Sancho; “for, as sensible people hold,

[118-051]‘the fault of the ass must not be laid on the pack-saddle;’ and, as in

[118-052]this affair the fault is your worship’s, punish yourself and don’t let

[118-053]your anger break out against the already battered and bloody armour, or

[118-054]the meekness of Rocinante, or the tenderness of my feet, trying to make

[118-055]them travel more than is reasonable.”

p66c.jpg (389K)


[118-056]In converse of this sort the whole of that day went by, as did the four

[118-057]succeeding ones, without anything occurring to interrupt their journey,

[118-058]but on the fifth as they entered a village they found a great number of

[118-059]people at the door of an inn enjoying themselves, as it was a holiday.

[118-060]Upon Don Quixote’s approach a peasant called out, “One of these two

[118-061]gentlemen who come here, and who don’t know the parties, will tell us

[118-062]what we ought to do about our wager.”

[118-063]“That I will, certainly,” said Don Quixote, “and according to the

[118-064]rights of the case, if I can manage to understand it.”

[118-065]“Well, here it is, worthy sir,” said the peasant; “a man of this

[118-066]village who is so fat that he weighs twenty stone challenged another, a

[118-067]neighbour of his, who does not weigh more than nine, to run a race. The

[118-068]agreement was that they were to run a distance of a hundred paces with

[118-069]equal weights; and when the challenger was asked how the weights were

[118-070]to be equalised he said that the other, as he weighed nine stone,

[118-071]should put eleven in iron on his back, and that in this way the twenty

[118-072]stone of the thin man would equal the twenty stone of the fat one.”

[118-073]“Not at all,” exclaimed Sancho at once, before Don Quixote could

[118-074]answer; “it’s for me, that only a few days ago left off being a

[118-075]governor and a judge, as all the world knows, to settle these doubtful

[118-076]questions and give an opinion in disputes of all sorts.”

[118-077]“Answer in God’s name, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote, “for I am

[118-078]not fit to give crumbs to a cat, my wits are so confused and upset.”

[118-079]With this permission Sancho said to the peasants who stood clustered

[118-080]round him, waiting with open mouths for the decision to come from his,

[118-081]“Brothers, what the fat man requires is not in reason, nor has it a

[118-082]shadow of justice in it; because, if it be true, as they say, that the

[118-083]challenged may choose the weapons, the other has no right to choose

[118-084]such as will prevent and keep him from winning. My decision, therefore,

[118-085]is that the fat challenger prune, peel, thin, trim and correct himself,

[118-086]and take eleven stone of his flesh off his body, here or there, as he

[118-087]pleases, and as suits him best; and being in this way reduced to nine

[118-088]stone weight, he will make himself equal and even with nine stone of

[118-089]his opponent, and they will be able to run on equal terms.”

[118-090]“By all that’s good,” said one of the peasants as he heard Sancho’s

[118-091]decision, “but the gentleman has spoken like a saint, and given

[118-092]judgment like a canon! But I’ll be bound the fat man won’t part with an

[118-093]ounce of his flesh, not to say eleven stone.”

[118-094]“The best plan will be for them not to run,” said another, “so that

[118-095]neither the thin man break down under the weight, nor the fat one strip

[118-096]himself of his flesh; let half the wager be spent in wine, and let’s

[118-097]take these gentlemen to the tavern where there’s the best, and ‘over me

[118-098]be the cloak when it rains.’”

[118-099]“I thank you, sirs,” said Don Quixote; “but I cannot stop for an

[118-100]instant, for sad thoughts and unhappy circumstances force me to seem

[118-101]discourteous and to travel apace;” and spurring Rocinante he pushed on,

[118-102]leaving them wondering at what they had seen and heard, at his own

[118-103]strange figure and at the shrewdness of his servant, for such they took

[118-104]Sancho to be; and another of them observed, “If the servant is so

[118-105]clever, what must the master be? I’ll bet, if they are going to

[118-106]Salamanca to study, they’ll come to be alcaldes of the Court in a

[118-107]trice; for it’s a mere joke—only to read and read, and have interest

[118-108]and good luck; and before a man knows where he is he finds himself with

[118-109]a staff in his hand or a mitre on his head.”

[118-110]That night master and man passed out in the fields in the open air, and

[118-111]the next day as they were pursuing their journey they saw coming

[118-112]towards them a man on foot with alforjas at the neck and a javelin or

[118-113]spiked staff in his hand, the very cut of a foot courier; who, as soon

[118-114]as he came close to Don Quixote, increased his pace and half running

[118-115]came up to him, and embracing his right thigh, for he could reach no

[118-116]higher, exclaimed with evident pleasure, “O Señor Don Quixote of La

[118-117]Mancha, what happiness it will be to the heart of my lord the duke when

[118-118]he knows your worship is coming back to his castle, for he is still

[118-119]there with my lady the duchess!”

[118-120]“I do not recognise you, friend,” said Don Quixote, “nor do I know who

[118-121]you are, unless you tell me.”

[118-122]“I am Tosilos, my lord the duke’s lacquey, Señor Don Quixote,” replied

[118-123]the courier; “he who refused to fight your worship about marrying the

[118-124]daughter of Doña Rodriguez.”

[118-125]“God bless me!” exclaimed Don Quixote; “is it possible that you are the

[118-126]one whom mine enemies the enchanters changed into the lacquey you speak

[118-127]of in order to rob me of the honour of that battle?”

[118-128]“Nonsense, good sir!” said the messenger; “there was no enchantment or

[118-129]transformation at all; I entered the lists just as much lacquey Tosilos

[118-130]as I came out of them lacquey Tosilos. I thought to marry without

[118-131]fighting, for the girl had taken my fancy; but my scheme had a very

[118-132]different result, for as soon as your worship had left the castle my

[118-133]lord the duke had a hundred strokes of the stick given me for having

[118-134]acted contrary to the orders he gave me before engaging in the combat;

[118-135]and the end of the whole affair is that the girl has become a nun, and

[118-136]Doña Rodriguez has gone back to Castile, and I am now on my way to

[118-137]Barcelona with a packet of letters for the viceroy which my master is

[118-138]sending him. If your worship would like a drop, sound though warm, I

[118-139]have a gourd here full of the best, and some scraps of Tronchon cheese

[118-140]that will serve as a provocative and wakener of your thirst if so be it

[118-141]is asleep.”

[118-142]“I take the offer,” said Sancho; “no more compliments about it; pour

[118-143]out, good Tosilos, in spite of all the enchanters in the Indies.”

[118-144]“Thou art indeed the greatest glutton in the world, Sancho,” said Don

[118-145]Quixote, “and the greatest booby on earth, not to be able to see that

[118-146]this courier is enchanted and this Tosilos a sham one; stop with him

[118-147]and take thy fill; I will go on slowly and wait for thee to come up

[118-148]with me.”

[118-149]The lacquey laughed, unsheathed his gourd, unwalletted his scraps, and

[118-150]taking out a small loaf of bread he and Sancho seated themselves on the

[118-151]green grass, and in peace and good fellowship finished off the contents

[118-152]of the alforjas down to the bottom, so resolutely that they licked the

[118-153]wrapper of the letters, merely because it smelt of cheese.

[118-154]Said Tosilos to Sancho, “Beyond a doubt, Sancho my friend, this master

[118-155]of thine ought to be a madman.”

[118-156]“Ought!” said Sancho; “he owes no man anything; he pays for everything,

[118-157]particularly when the coin is madness. I see it plain enough, and I

[118-158]tell him so plain enough; but what’s the use? especially now that it is

[118-159]all over with him, for here he is beaten by the Knight of the White

[118-160]Moon.”

[118-161]Tosilos begged him to explain what had happened him, but Sancho replied

[118-162]that it would not be good manners to leave his master waiting for him;

[118-163]and that some other day if they met there would be time enough for

[118-164]that; and then getting up, after shaking his doublet and brushing the

[118-165]crumbs out of his beard, he drove Dapple on before him, and bidding

[118-166]adieu to Tosilos left him and rejoined his master, who was waiting for

[118-167]him under the shade of a tree.
