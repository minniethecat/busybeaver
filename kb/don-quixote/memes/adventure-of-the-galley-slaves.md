## The Chain Gang

Don Quixote and Sancho encounter "some dozen men on foot strung together by the neck, like beads, on a great iron chain, and all with manacles on their hands" (022-006). Sancho immediately identifies them as galley slaves — criminals condemned to serve in the king's galleys. Don Quixote's response is shaped entirely by his chivalric framework:

> "How by force?" asked Don Quixote; "is it possible that the king uses force against anyone?" (022-013)

Sancho explains the legal reality: these are convicted criminals serving lawful sentences. But Don Quixote's categorical thinking cannot accommodate this distinction. In his world, anyone being taken somewhere by force and "not of their own will" (022-018) is oppressed and must be freed.

## The Liberation

Don Quixote interviews each prisoner, collecting their life stories. The rogues' gallery includes a lovesick youth, a man who confessed under torture, and the notorious Ginés de Pasamonte — a clever and dangerous criminal who claims to be writing his own autobiography.

Despite the guards' protests, Don Quixote attacks and frees the prisoners. His justification is pure knight-errantry:

| Knightly Principle | Legal Reality |
|-------------------|---------------|
| "It is a harsh thing to make slaves of those whom God and nature made free" | Convicted criminals serving sentences |
| The oppressed must be liberated | The king's justice must be respected |
| Knighthood compels intervention | Prudence demands non-interference |

## The Ingratitude

Once freed, the convicts refuse Don Quixote's demand that they present themselves to Dulcinea in gratitude. Ginés de Pasamonte organizes the others, and they stone their liberator — pelting Don Quixote and Sancho with rocks, stripping them of some possessions, and fleeing. The knight who liberated the oppressed is attacked by the liberated.

The episode is one of the novel's sharpest critiques of Don Quixote's worldview. His moral absolutism — "it is my duty to help the oppressed" — produces consequences he cannot control and refuses to anticipate. The freed criminals do not reform; they simply continue their criminal lives. Ginés de Pasamonte later reappears to steal Sancho's donkey.
