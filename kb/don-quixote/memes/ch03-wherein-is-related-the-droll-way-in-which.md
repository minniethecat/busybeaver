WHEREIN IS RELATED THE DROLL WAY IN WHICH DON QUIXOTE HAD HIMSELF

DUBBED A KNIGHT

[003-001]Harassed by this reflection, he made haste with his scanty pothouse

[003-002]supper, and having finished it called the landlord, and shutting

[003-003]himself into the stable with him, fell on his knees before him, saying,

[003-004]“From this spot I rise not, valiant knight, until your courtesy grants

[003-005]me the boon I seek, one that will redound to your praise and the

[003-006]benefit of the human race.” The landlord, seeing his guest at his feet

[003-007]and hearing a speech of this kind, stood staring at him in

[003-008]bewilderment, not knowing what to do or say, and entreating him to

[003-009]rise, but all to no purpose until he had agreed to grant the boon

[003-010]demanded of him. “I looked for no less, my lord, from your High

[003-011]Magnificence,” replied Don Quixote, “and I have to tell you that the

[003-012]boon I have asked and your liberality has granted is that you shall dub

[003-013]me knight to-morrow morning, and that to-night I shall watch my arms in

[003-014]the chapel of this your castle; thus to-morrow, as I have said, will be

[003-015]accomplished what I so much desire, enabling me lawfully to roam

[003-016]through all the four quarters of the world seeking adventures on behalf

[003-017]of those in distress, as is the duty of chivalry and of knights-errant

[003-018]like myself, whose ambition is directed to such deeds.”

[003-019]The landlord, who, as has been mentioned, was something of a wag, and

[003-020]had already some suspicion of his guest’s want of wits, was quite

[003-021]convinced of it on hearing talk of this kind from him, and to make

[003-022]sport for the night he determined to fall in with his humour. So he

[003-023]told him he was quite right in pursuing the object he had in view, and

[003-024]that such a motive was natural and becoming in cavaliers as

[003-025]distinguished as he seemed and his gallant bearing showed him to be;

[003-026]and that he himself in his younger days had followed the same

[003-027]honourable calling, roaming in quest of adventures in various parts of

[003-028]the world, among others the Curing-grounds of Malaga, the Isles of

[003-029]Riaran, the Precinct of Seville, the Little Market of Segovia, the

[003-030]Olivera of Valencia, the Rondilla of Granada, the Strand of San Lucar,

[003-031]the Colt of Cordova, the Taverns of Toledo, and divers other quarters,

[003-032]where he had proved the nimbleness of his feet and the lightness of his

[003-033]fingers, doing many wrongs, cheating many widows, ruining maids and

[003-034]swindling minors, and, in short, bringing himself under the notice of

[003-035]almost every tribunal and court of justice in Spain; until at last he

[003-036]had retired to this castle of his, where he was living upon his

[003-037]property and upon that of others; and where he received all

[003-038]knights-errant of whatever rank or condition they might be, all for the

[003-039]great love he bore them and that they might share their substance with

[003-040]him in return for his benevolence. He told him, moreover, that in this

[003-041]castle of his there was no chapel in which he could watch his armour,

[003-042]as it had been pulled down in order to be rebuilt, but that in a case

[003-043]of necessity it might, he knew, be watched anywhere, and he might watch

[003-044]it that night in a courtyard of the castle, and in the morning, God

[003-045]willing, the requisite ceremonies might be performed so as to have him

[003-046]dubbed a knight, and so thoroughly dubbed that nobody could be more so.

[003-047]He asked if he had any money with him, to which Don Quixote replied

[003-048]that he had not a farthing, as in the histories of knights-errant he

[003-049]had never read of any of them carrying any. On this point the landlord

[003-050]told him he was mistaken; for, though not recorded in the histories,

[003-051]because in the author’s opinion there was no need to mention anything

[003-052]so obvious and necessary as money and clean shirts, it was not to be

[003-053]supposed therefore that they did not carry them, and he might regard it

[003-054]as certain and established that all knights-errant (about whom there

[003-055]were so many full and unimpeachable books) carried well-furnished

[003-056]purses in case of emergency, and likewise carried shirts and a little

[003-057]box of ointment to cure the wounds they received. For in those plains

[003-058]and deserts where they engaged in combat and came out wounded, it was

[003-059]not always that there was someone to cure them, unless indeed they had

[003-060]for a friend some sage magician to succour them at once by fetching

[003-061]through the air upon a cloud some damsel or dwarf with a vial of water

[003-062]of such virtue that by tasting one drop of it they were cured of their

[003-063]hurts and wounds in an instant and left as sound as if they had not

[003-064]received any damage whatever. But in case this should not occur, the

[003-065]knights of old took care to see that their squires were provided with

[003-066]money and other requisites, such as lint and ointments for healing

[003-067]purposes; and when it happened that knights had no squires (which was

[003-068]rarely and seldom the case) they themselves carried everything in

[003-069]cunning saddle-bags that were hardly seen on the horse’s croup, as if

[003-070]it were something else of more importance, because, unless for some

[003-071]such reason, carrying saddle-bags was not very favourably regarded

[003-072]among knights-errant. He therefore advised him (and, as his godson so

[003-073]soon to be, he might even command him) never from that time forth to

[003-074]travel without money and the usual requirements, and he would find the

[003-075]advantage of them when he least expected it.

[003-076]Don Quixote promised to follow his advice scrupulously, and it was

[003-077]arranged forthwith that he should watch his armour in a large yard at

[003-078]one side of the inn; so, collecting it all together, Don Quixote placed

[003-079]it on a trough that stood by the side of a well, and bracing his

[003-080]buckler on his arm he grasped his lance and began with a stately air to

[003-081]march up and down in front of the trough, and as he began his march

[003-082]night began to fall.

[003-083]The landlord told all the people who were in the inn about the craze of

[003-084]his guest, the watching of the armour, and the dubbing ceremony he

[003-085]contemplated. Full of wonder at so strange a form of madness, they

[003-086]flocked to see it from a distance, and observed with what composure he

[003-087]sometimes paced up and down, or sometimes, leaning on his lance, gazed

[003-088]on his armour without taking his eyes off it for ever so long; and as

[003-089]the night closed in with a light from the moon so brilliant that it

[003-090]might vie with his that lent it, everything the novice knight did was

[003-091]plainly seen by all.

[003-092]Meanwhile one of the carriers who were in the inn thought fit to water

[003-093]his team, and it was necessary to remove Don Quixote’s armour as it lay

[003-094]on the trough; but he seeing the other approach hailed him in a loud

[003-095]voice, “O thou, whoever thou art, rash knight that comest to lay hands

[003-096]on the armour of the most valorous errant that ever girt on sword, have

[003-097]a care what thou dost; touch it not unless thou wouldst lay down thy

[003-098]life as the penalty of thy rashness.” The carrier gave no heed to these

[003-099]words (and he would have done better to heed them if he had been

[003-100]heedful of his health), but seizing it by the straps flung the armour

[003-101]some distance from him. Seeing this, Don Quixote raised his eyes to

[003-102]heaven, and fixing his thoughts, apparently, upon his lady Dulcinea,

[003-103]exclaimed, “Aid me, lady mine, in this the first encounter that

[003-104]presents itself to this breast which thou holdest in subjection; let

[003-105]not thy favour and protection fail me in this first jeopardy;” and,

[003-106]with these words and others to the same purpose, dropping his buckler

[003-107]he lifted his lance with both hands and with it smote such a blow on

[003-108]the carrier’s head that he stretched him on the ground, so stunned that

[003-109]had he followed it up with a second there would have been no need of a

[003-110]surgeon to cure him. This done, he picked up his armour and returned to

[003-111]his beat with the same serenity as before.

[003-112]Shortly after this, another, not knowing what had happened (for the

[003-113]carrier still lay senseless), came with the same object of giving water

[003-114]to his mules, and was proceeding to remove the armour in order to clear

[003-115]the trough, when Don Quixote, without uttering a word or imploring aid

[003-116]from anyone, once more dropped his buckler and once more lifted his

[003-117]lance, and without actually breaking the second carrier’s head into

[003-118]pieces, made more than three of it, for he laid it open in four. At the

[003-119]noise all the people of the inn ran to the spot, and among them the

[003-120]landlord. Seeing this, Don Quixote braced his buckler on his arm, and

[003-121]with his hand on his sword exclaimed, “O Lady of Beauty, strength and

[003-122]support of my faint heart, it is time for thee to turn the eyes of thy

[003-123]greatness on this thy captive knight on the brink of so mighty an

[003-124]adventure.” By this he felt himself so inspired that he would not have

[003-125]flinched if all the carriers in the world had assailed him. The

[003-126]comrades of the wounded perceiving the plight they were in began from a

[003-127]distance to shower stones on Don Quixote, who screened himself as best

[003-128]he could with his buckler, not daring to quit the trough and leave his

[003-129]armour unprotected. The landlord shouted to them to leave him alone,

[003-130]for he had already told them that he was mad, and as a madman he would

[003-131]not be accountable even if he killed them all. Still louder shouted Don

[003-132]Quixote, calling them knaves and traitors, and the lord of the castle,

[003-133]who allowed knights-errant to be treated in this fashion, a villain and

[003-134]a low-born knight whom, had he received the order of knighthood, he

[003-135]would call to account for his treachery. “But of you,” he cried, “base

[003-136]and vile rabble, I make no account; fling, strike, come on, do all ye

[003-137]can against me, ye shall see what the reward of your folly and

[003-138]insolence will be.” This he uttered with so much spirit and boldness

[003-139]that he filled his assailants with a terrible fear, and as much for

[003-140]this reason as at the persuasion of the landlord they left off stoning

[003-141]him, and he allowed them to carry off the wounded, and with the same

[003-142]calmness and composure as before resumed the watch over his armour.

[003-143]But these freaks of his guest were not much to the liking of the

[003-144]landlord, so he determined to cut matters short and confer upon him at

[003-145]once the unlucky order of knighthood before any further misadventure

[003-146]could occur; so, going up to him, he apologised for the rudeness which,

[003-147]without his knowledge, had been offered to him by these low people,

[003-148]who, however, had been well punished for their audacity. As he had

[003-149]already told him, he said, there was no chapel in the castle, nor was

[003-150]it needed for what remained to be done, for, as he understood the

[003-151]ceremonial of the order, the whole point of being dubbed a knight lay

[003-152]in the accolade and in the slap on the shoulder, and that could be

[003-153]administered in the middle of a field; and that he had now done all

[003-154]that was needful as to watching the armour, for all requirements were

[003-155]satisfied by a watch of two hours only, while he had been more than

[003-156]four about it. Don Quixote believed it all, and told him he stood there

[003-157]ready to obey him, and to make an end of it with as much despatch as

[003-158]possible; for, if he were again attacked, and felt himself to be dubbed

[003-159]knight, he would not, he thought, leave a soul alive in the castle,

[003-160]except such as out of respect he might spare at his bidding.

[003-161]Thus warned and menaced, the castellan forthwith brought out a book in

[003-162]which he used to enter the straw and barley he served out to the

[003-163]carriers, and, with a lad carrying a candle-end, and the two damsels

[003-164]already mentioned, he returned to where Don Quixote stood, and bade him

[003-165]kneel down. Then, reading from his account-book as if he were repeating

[003-166]some devout prayer, in the middle of his delivery he raised his hand

[003-167]and gave him a sturdy blow on the neck, and then, with his own sword, a

[003-168]smart slap on the shoulder, all the while muttering between his teeth

[003-169]as if he was saying his prayers. Having done this, he directed one of

[003-170]the ladies to gird on his sword, which she did with great

[003-171]self-possession and gravity, and not a little was required to prevent a

[003-172]burst of laughter at each stage of the ceremony; but what they had

[003-173]already seen of the novice knight’s prowess kept their laughter within

[003-174]bounds. On girding him with the sword the worthy lady said to him, “May

[003-175]God make your worship a very fortunate knight, and grant you success in

[003-176]battle.” Don Quixote asked her name in order that he might from that

[003-177]time forward know to whom he was beholden for the favour he had

[003-178]received, as he meant to confer upon her some portion of the honour he

[003-179]acquired by the might of his arm. She answered with great humility that

[003-180]she was called La Tolosa, and that she was the daughter of a cobbler of

[003-181]Toledo who lived in the stalls of Sanchobienaya, and that wherever she

[003-182]might be she would serve and esteem him as her lord. Don Quixote said

[003-183]in reply that she would do him a favour if thenceforward she assumed

[003-184]the “Don” and called herself Doña Tolosa. She promised she would, and

[003-185]then the other buckled on his spur, and with her followed almost the

[003-186]same conversation as with the lady of the sword. He asked her name, and

[003-187]she said it was La Molinera, and that she was the daughter of a

[003-188]respectable miller of Antequera; and of her likewise Don Quixote

[003-189]requested that she would adopt the “Don” and call herself Doña

[003-190]Molinera, making offers to her further services and favours.

[003-191]Having thus, with hot haste and speed, brought to a conclusion these

[003-192]never-till-now-seen ceremonies, Don Quixote was on thorns until he saw

[003-193]himself on horseback sallying forth in quest of adventures; and

[003-194]saddling Rocinante at once he mounted, and embracing his host, as he

[003-195]returned thanks for his kindness in knighting him, he addressed him in

[003-196]language so extraordinary that it is impossible to convey an idea of it

[003-197]or report it. The landlord, to get him out of the inn, replied with no

[003-198]less rhetoric though with shorter words, and without calling upon him

[003-199]to pay the reckoning let him go with a Godspeed.
