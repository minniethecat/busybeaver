[100-001]OF WHAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE WITH DOÑA RODRIGUEZ, THE DUCHESS’S DUENNA,

TOGETHER WITH OTHER OCCURRENCES WORTHY OF RECORD AND ETERNAL

REMEMBRANCE

p48a.jpg (131K)


[100-002]Exceedingly moody and dejected was the sorely wounded Don Quixote, with

[100-003]his face bandaged and marked, not by the hand of God, but by the claws

[100-004]of a cat, mishaps incidental to knight-errantry.

p48b.jpg (316K)


[100-005]Six days he remained without appearing in public, and one night as he

[100-006]lay awake thinking of his misfortunes and of Altisidora’s pursuit of

[100-007]him, he perceived that someone was opening the door of his room with a

[100-008]key, and he at once made up his mind that the enamoured damsel was

[100-009]coming to make an assault upon his chastity and put him in danger of

[100-010]failing in the fidelity he owed to his lady Dulcinea del Toboso. “No,”

[100-011]said he, firmly persuaded of the truth of his idea (and he said it loud

[100-012]enough to be heard), “the greatest beauty upon earth shall not avail to

[100-013]make me renounce my adoration of her whom I bear stamped and graved in

[100-014]the core of my heart and the secret depths of my bowels; be thou, lady

[100-015]mine, transformed into a clumsy country wench, or into a nymph of

[100-016]golden Tagus weaving a web of silk and gold, let Merlin or Montesinos

[100-017]hold thee captive where they will; where’er thou art, thou art mine,

[100-018]and where’er I am, must be thine.” The very instant he had uttered

[100-019]these words, the door opened. He stood up on the bed wrapped from head

[100-020]to foot in a yellow satin coverlet, with a cap on his head, and his

[100-021]face and his moustaches tied up, his face because of the scratches, and

[100-022]his moustaches to keep them from drooping and falling down, in which

[100-023]trim he looked the most extraordinary scarecrow that could be

[100-024]conceived. He kept his eyes fixed on the door, and just as he was

[100-025]expecting to see the love-smitten and unhappy Altisidora make her

[100-026]appearance, he saw coming in a most venerable duenna, in a long

[100-027]white-bordered veil that covered and enveloped her from head to foot.

[100-028]Between the fingers of her left hand she held a short lighted candle,

[100-029]while with her right she shaded it to keep the light from her eyes,

[100-030]which were covered by spectacles of great size, and she advanced with

[100-031]noiseless steps, treading very softly.

[100-032]Don Quixote kept an eye upon her from his watchtower, and observing her

[100-033]costume and noting her silence, he concluded that it must be some witch

[100-034]or sorceress that was coming in such a guise to work him some mischief,

[100-035]and he began crossing himself at a great rate. The spectre still

[100-036]advanced, and on reaching the middle of the room, looked up and saw the

[100-037]energy with which Don Quixote was crossing himself; and if he was

[100-038]scared by seeing such a figure as hers, she was terrified at the sight

[100-039]of his; for the moment she saw his tall yellow form with the coverlet

[100-040]and the bandages that disfigured him, she gave a loud scream, and

[100-041]exclaiming, “Jesus! what’s this I see?” let fall the candle in her

[100-042]fright, and then finding herself in the dark, turned about to make off,

[100-043]but stumbling on her skirts in her consternation, she measured her

[100-044]length with a mighty fall.

[100-045]Don Quixote in his trepidation began saying, “I conjure thee, phantom,

[100-046]or whatever thou art, tell me what thou art and what thou wouldst with

[100-047]me. If thou art a soul in torment, say so, and all that my powers can

[100-048]do I will do for thee; for I am a Catholic Christian and love to do

[100-049]good to all the world, and to this end I have embraced the order of

[100-050]knight-errantry to which I belong, the province of which extends to

[100-051]doing good even to souls in purgatory.”

[100-052]The unfortunate duenna hearing herself thus conjured, by her own fear

[100-053]guessed Don Quixote’s and in a low plaintive voice answered, “Señor Don

[100-054]Quixote—if so be you are indeed Don Quixote—I am no phantom or spectre

[100-055]or soul in purgatory, as you seem to think, but Doña Rodriguez, duenna

[100-056]of honour to my lady the duchess, and I come to you with one of those

[100-057]grievances your worship is wont to redress.”

[100-058]“Tell me, Señora Doña Rodriguez,” said Don Quixote, “do you perchance

[100-059]come to transact any go-between business? Because I must tell you I am

[100-060]not available for anybody’s purpose, thanks to the peerless beauty of

[100-061]my lady Dulcinea del Toboso. In short, Señora Doña Rodriguez, if you

[100-062]will leave out and put aside all love messages, you may go and light

[100-063]your candle and come back, and we will discuss all the commands you

[100-064]have for me and whatever you wish, saving only, as I said, all

[100-065]seductive communications.”

[100-066]“I carry nobody’s messages, señor,” said the duenna; “little you know

[100-067]me. Nay, I’m not far enough advanced in years to take to any such

[100-068]childish tricks. God be praised I have a soul in my body still, and all

[100-069]my teeth and grinders in my mouth, except one or two that the colds, so

[100-070]common in this Aragon country, have robbed me of. But wait a little,

[100-071]while I go and light my candle, and I will return immediately and lay

[100-072]my sorrows before you as before one who relieves those of all the

[100-073]world;” and without staying for an answer she quitted the room and left

[100-074]Don Quixote tranquilly meditating while he waited for her. A thousand

[100-075]thoughts at once suggested themselves to him on the subject of this new

[100-076]adventure, and it struck him as being ill done and worse advised in him

[100-077]to expose himself to the danger of breaking his plighted faith to his

[100-078]lady; and said he to himself, “Who knows but that the devil, being wily

[100-079]and cunning, may be trying now to entrap me with a duenna, having

[100-080]failed with empresses, queens, duchesses, marchionesses, and

[100-081]countesses? Many a time have I heard it said by many a man of sense

[100-082]that he will sooner offer you a flat-nosed wench than a roman-nosed

[100-083]one; and who knows but this privacy, this opportunity, this silence,

[100-084]may awaken my sleeping desires, and lead me in these my latter years to

[100-085]fall where I have never tripped? In cases of this sort it is better to

[100-086]flee than to await the battle. But I must be out of my senses to think

[100-087]and utter such nonsense; for it is impossible that a long, white-hooded

[100-088]spectacled duenna could stir up or excite a wanton thought in the most

[100-089]graceless bosom in the world. Is there a duenna on earth that has fair

[100-090]flesh? Is there a duenna in the world that escapes being ill-tempered,

[100-091]wrinkled, and prudish? Avaunt, then, ye duenna crew, undelightful to

[100-092]all mankind. Oh, but that lady did well who, they say, had at the end

[100-093]of her reception room a couple of figures of duennas with spectacles

[100-094]and lace-cushions, as if at work, and those statues served quite as

[100-095]well to give an air of propriety to the room as if they had been real

[100-096]duennas.”

[100-097]So saying he leaped off the bed, intending to close the door and not

[100-098]allow Señora Rodriguez to enter; but as he went to shut it Señora

[100-099]Rodriguez returned with a wax candle lighted, and having a closer view

[100-100]of Don Quixote, with the coverlet round him, and his bandages and

[100-101]night-cap, she was alarmed afresh, and retreating a couple of paces,

[100-102]exclaimed, “Am I safe, sir knight? for I don’t look upon it as a sign

[100-103]of very great virtue that your worship should have got up out of bed.”

[100-104]“I may well ask the same, señora,” said Don Quixote; “and I do ask

[100-105]whether I shall be safe from being assailed and forced?”

[100-106]“Of whom and against whom do you demand that security, sir knight?”

[100-107]said the duenna.

[100-108]“Of you and against you I ask it,” said Don Quixote; “for I am not

[100-109]marble, nor are you brass, nor is it now ten o’clock in the morning,

[100-110]but midnight, or a trifle past it I fancy, and we are in a room more

[100-111]secluded and retired than the cave could have been where the

[100-112]treacherous and daring Æneas enjoyed the fair soft-hearted Dido. But

[100-113]give me your hand, señora; I require no better protection than my own

[100-114]continence, and my own sense of propriety; as well as that which is

[100-115]inspired by that venerable head-dress;” and so saying he kissed her

[100-116]right hand and took it in his own, she yielding it to him with equal

[100-117]ceremoniousness. And here Cide Hamete inserts a parenthesis in which he

[100-118]says that to have seen the pair marching from the door to the bed,

[100-119]linked hand in hand in this way, he would have given the best of the

[100-120]two tunics he had.

[100-121]Don Quixote finally got into bed, and Doña Rodriguez took her seat on a

[100-122]chair at some little distance from his couch, without taking off her

[100-123]spectacles or putting aside the candle. Don Quixote wrapped the

[100-124]bedclothes round him and covered himself up completely, leaving nothing

[100-125]but his face visible, and as soon as they had both regained their

[100-126]composure he broke silence, saying, “Now, Señora Doña Rodriguez, you

[100-127]may unbosom yourself and out with everything you have in your sorrowful

[100-128]heart and afflicted bowels; and by me you shall be listened to with

[100-129]chaste ears, and aided by compassionate exertions.”

[100-130]“I believe it,” replied the duenna; “from your worship’s gentle and

[100-131]winning presence only such a Christian answer could be expected. The

[100-132]fact is, then, Señor Don Quixote, that though you see me seated in this

[100-133]chair, here in the middle of the kingdom of Aragon, and in the attire

[100-134]of a despised outcast duenna, I am from the Asturias of Oviedo, and of

[100-135]a family with which many of the best of the province are connected by

[100-136]blood; but my untoward fate and the improvidence of my parents, who, I

[100-137]know not how, were unseasonably reduced to poverty, brought me to the

[100-138]court of Madrid, where as a provision and to avoid greater misfortunes,

[100-139]my parents placed me as seamstress in the service of a lady of quality,

[100-140]and I would have you know that for hemming and sewing I have never been

[100-141]surpassed by any all my life. My parents left me in service and

[100-142]returned to their own country, and a few years later went, no doubt, to

[100-143]heaven, for they were excellent good Catholic Christians. I was left an

[100-144]orphan with nothing but the miserable wages and trifling presents that

[100-145]are given to servants of my sort in palaces; but about this time,

[100-146]without any encouragement on my part, one of the esquires of the

[100-147]household fell in love with me, a man somewhat advanced in years,

[100-148]full-bearded and personable, and above all as good a gentleman as the

[100-149]king himself, for he came of a mountain stock. We did not carry on our

[100-150]loves with such secrecy but that they came to the knowledge of my lady,

[100-151]and she, not to have any fuss about it, had us married with the full

[100-152]sanction of the holy mother Roman Catholic Church, of which marriage a

[100-153]daughter was born to put an end to my good fortune, if I had any; not

[100-154]that I died in childbirth, for I passed through it safely and in due

[100-155]season, but because shortly afterwards my husband died of a certain

[100-156]shock he received, and had I time to tell you of it I know your worship

[100-157]would be surprised;” and here she began to weep bitterly and said,

[100-158]“Pardon me, Señor Don Quixote, if I am unable to control myself, for

[100-159]every time I think of my unfortunate husband my eyes fill up with

[100-160]tears. God bless me, with what an air of dignity he used to carry my

[100-161]lady behind him on a stout mule as black as jet! for in those days they

[100-162]did not use coaches or chairs, as they say they do now, and ladies rode

[100-163]behind their squires. This much at least I cannot help telling you,

[100-164]that you may observe the good breeding and punctiliousness of my worthy

[100-165]husband. As he was turning into the Calle de Santiago in Madrid, which

[100-166]is rather narrow, one of the alcaldes of the Court, with two alguacils

[100-167]before him, was coming out of it, and as soon as my good squire saw him

[100-168]he wheeled his mule about and made as if he would turn and accompany

[100-169]him. My lady, who was riding behind him, said to him in a low voice,

[100-170]‘What are you about, you sneak, don’t you see that I am here?’ The

[100-171]alcalde like a polite man pulled up his horse and said to him,

[100-172]‘Proceed, señor, for it is I, rather, who ought to accompany my lady

[100-173]Doña Casilda’—for that was my mistress’s name. Still my husband, cap in

[100-174]hand, persisted in trying to accompany the alcalde, and seeing this my

[100-175]lady, filled with rage and vexation, pulled out a big pin, or, I rather

[100-176]think, a bodkin, out of her needle-case and drove it into his back with

[100-177]such force that my husband gave a loud yell, and writhing fell to the

[100-178]ground with his lady. Her two lacqueys ran to rise her up, and the

[100-179]alcalde and the alguacils did the same; the Guadalajara gate was all in

[100-180]commotion—I mean the idlers congregated there; my mistress came back on

[100-181]foot, and my husband hurried away to a barber’s shop protesting that he

[100-182]was run right through the guts. The courtesy of my husband was noised

[100-183]abroad to such an extent, that the boys gave him no peace in the

[100-184]street; and on this account, and because he was somewhat shortsighted,

[100-185]my lady dismissed him; and it was chagrin at this I am convinced beyond

[100-186]a doubt that brought on his death. I was left a helpless widow, with a

[100-187]daughter on my hands growing up in beauty like the sea-foam; at length,

[100-188]however, as I had the character of being an excellent needlewoman, my

[100-189]lady the duchess, then lately married to my lord the duke, offered to

[100-190]take me with her to this kingdom of Aragon, and my daughter also, and

[100-191]here as time went by my daughter grew up and with her all the graces in

[100-192]the world; she sings like a lark, dances quick as thought, foots it

[100-193]like a gipsy, reads and writes like a schoolmaster, and does sums like

[100-194]a miser; of her neatness I say nothing, for the running water is not

[100-195]purer, and her age is now, if my memory serves me, sixteen years five

[100-196]months and three days, one more or less. To come to the point, the son

[100-197]of a very rich farmer, living in a village of my lord the duke’s not

[100-198]very far from here, fell in love with this girl of mine; and in short,

[100-199]how I know not, they came together, and under the promise of marrying

[100-200]her he made a fool of my daughter, and will not keep his word. And

[100-201]though my lord the duke is aware of it (for I have complained to him,

[100-202]not once but many and many a time, and entreated him to order the

[100-203]farmer to marry my daughter), he turns a deaf ear and will scarcely

[100-204]listen to me; the reason being that as the deceiver’s father is so

[100-205]rich, and lends him money, and is constantly going security for his

[100-206]debts, he does not like to offend or annoy him in any way. Now, señor,

[100-207]I want your worship to take it upon yourself to redress this wrong

[100-208]either by entreaty or by arms; for by what all the world says you came

[100-209]into it to redress grievances and right wrongs and help the

[100-210]unfortunate. Let your worship put before you the unprotected condition

[100-211]of my daughter, her youth, and all the perfections I have said she

[100-212]possesses; and before God and on my conscience, out of all the damsels

[100-213]my lady has, there is not one that comes up to the sole of her shoe,

[100-214]and the one they call Altisidora, and look upon as the boldest and

[100-215]gayest of them, put in comparison with my daughter, does not come

[100-216]within two leagues of her. For I would have you know, señor, all is not

[100-217]gold that glitters, and that same little Altisidora has more

[100-218]forwardness than good looks, and more impudence than modesty; besides

[100-219]being not very sound, for she has such a disagreeable breath that one

[100-220]cannot bear to be near her for a moment; and even my lady the

[100-221]duchess—but I’ll hold my tongue, for they say that walls have ears.”

[100-222]“For heaven’s sake, Doña Rodriguez, what ails my lady the duchess?”

[100-223]asked Don Quixote.

[100-224]“Adjured in that way,” replied the duenna, “I cannot help answering the

[100-225]question and telling the whole truth. Señor Don Quixote, have you

[100-226]observed the comeliness of my lady the duchess, that smooth complexion

[100-227]of hers like a burnished polished sword, those two cheeks of milk and

[100-228]carmine, that gay lively step with which she treads or rather seems to

[100-229]spurn the earth, so that one would fancy she went radiating health

[100-230]wherever she passed? Well then, let me tell you she may thank, first of

[100-231]all God, for this, and next, two issues that she has, one in each leg,

[100-232]by which all the evil humours, of which the doctors say she is full,

[100-233]are discharged.”

[100-234]“Blessed Virgin!” exclaimed Don Quixote; “and is it possible that my

[100-235]lady the duchess has drains of that sort? I would not have believed it

[100-236]if the barefoot friars had told it me; but as the lady Doña Rodriguez

[100-237]says so, it must be so. But surely such issues, and in such places, do

[100-238]not discharge humours, but liquid amber. Verily, I do believe now that

[100-239]this practice of opening issues is a very important matter for the

[100-240]health.”

[100-241]Don Quixote had hardly said this, when the chamber door flew open with

[100-242]a loud bang, and with the start the noise gave her Doña Rodriguez let

[100-243]the candle fall from her hand, and the room was left as dark as a

[100-244]wolf’s mouth, as the saying is. Suddenly the poor duenna felt two hands

[100-245]seize her by the throat, so tightly that she could not croak, while

[100-246]someone else, without uttering a word, very briskly hoisted up her

[100-247]petticoats, and with what seemed to be a slipper began to lay on so

[100-248]heartily that anyone would have felt pity for her; but although Don

[100-249]Quixote felt it he never stirred from his bed, but lay quiet and

[100-250]silent, nay apprehensive that his turn for a drubbing might be coming.

[100-251]Nor was the apprehension an idle one; for leaving the duenna (who did

[100-252]not dare to cry out) well basted, the silent executioners fell upon Don

[100-253]Quixote, and stripping him of the sheet and the coverlet, they pinched

[100-254]him so fast and so hard that he was driven to defend himself with his

[100-255]fists, and all this in marvellous silence. The battle lasted nearly

[100-256]half an hour, and then the phantoms fled; Doña Rodriguez gathered up

[100-257]her skirts, and bemoaning her fate went out without saying a word to

[100-258]Don Quixote, and he, sorely pinched, puzzled, and dejected, remained

[100-259]alone, and there we will leave him, wondering who could have been the

[100-260]perverse enchanter who had reduced him to such a state; but that shall

[100-261]be told in due season, for Sancho claims our attention, and the

[100-262]methodical arrangement of the story demands it.
