[072-001]WHEREIN AN ACCOUNT IS GIVEN OF THE WEDDING OF CAMACHO THE RICH,

TOGETHER WITH THE INCIDENT OF BASILIO THE POOR

p20a.jpg (125K)


[072-002]Scarce had the fair Aurora given bright Phœbus time to dry the liquid

[072-003]pearls upon her golden locks with the heat of his fervent rays, when

[072-004]Don Quixote, shaking off sloth from his limbs, sprang to his feet and

[072-005]called to his squire Sancho, who was still snoring; seeing which Don

[072-006]Quixote ere he roused him thus addressed him: “Happy thou, above all

[072-007]the dwellers on the face of the earth, that, without envying or being

[072-008]envied, sleepest with tranquil mind, and that neither enchanters

[072-009]persecute nor enchantments affright. Sleep, I say, and will say a

[072-010]hundred times, without any jealous thoughts of thy mistress to make

[072-011]thee keep ceaseless vigils, or any cares as to how thou art to pay the

[072-012]debts thou owest, or find to-morrow’s food for thyself and thy needy

[072-013]little family, to interfere with thy repose. Ambition breaks not thy

[072-014]rest, nor doth this world’s empty pomp disturb thee, for the utmost

[072-015]reach of thy anxiety is to provide for thy ass, since upon my shoulders

[072-016]thou hast laid the support of thyself, the counterpoise and burden that

[072-017]nature and custom have imposed upon masters. The servant sleeps and the

[072-018]master lies awake thinking how he is to feed him, advance him, and

[072-019]reward him. The distress of seeing the sky turn brazen, and withhold

[072-020]its needful moisture from the earth, is not felt by the servant but by

[072-021]the master, who in time of scarcity and famine must support him who has

[072-022]served him in times of plenty and abundance.”

p20b.jpg (365K)


[072-023]To all this Sancho made no reply because he was asleep, nor would he

[072-024]have wakened up so soon as he did had not Don Quixote brought him to

[072-025]his senses with the butt of his lance. He awoke at last, drowsy and

[072-026]lazy, and casting his eyes about in every direction, observed, “There

[072-027]comes, if I don’t mistake, from the quarter of that arcade a steam and

[072-028]a smell a great deal more like fried rashers than galingale or thyme; a

[072-029]wedding that begins with smells like that, by my faith, ought to be

[072-030]plentiful and unstinting.”

[072-031]“Have done, thou glutton,” said Don Quixote; “come, let us go and

[072-032]witness this bridal, and see what the rejected Basilio does.”

[072-033]“Let him do what he likes,” returned Sancho; “be he not poor, he would

[072-034]marry Quiteria. To make a grand match for himself, and he without a

[072-035]farthing; is there nothing else? Faith, señor, it’s my opinion the poor

[072-036]man should be content with what he can get, and not go looking for

[072-037]dainties in the bottom of the sea. I will bet my arm that Camacho could

[072-038]bury Basilio in reals; and if that be so, as no doubt it is, what a

[072-039]fool Quiteria would be to refuse the fine dresses and jewels Camacho

[072-040]must have given her and will give her, and take Basilio’s bar-throwing

[072-041]and sword-play. They won’t give a pint of wine at the tavern for a good

[072-042]cast of the bar or a neat thrust of the sword. Talents and

[072-043]accomplishments that can’t be turned into money, let Count Dirlos have

[072-044]them; but when such gifts fall to one that has hard cash, I wish my

[072-045]condition of life was as becoming as they are. On a good foundation you

[072-046]can raise a good building, and the best foundation in the world is

[072-047]money.”

[072-048]“For God’s sake, Sancho,” said Don Quixote here, “stop that harangue;

[072-049]it is my belief, if thou wert allowed to continue all thou beginnest

[072-050]every instant, thou wouldst have no time left for eating or sleeping;

[072-051]for thou wouldst spend it all in talking.”

[072-052]“If your worship had a good memory,” replied Sancho, “you would

[072-053]remember the articles of our agreement before we started from home this

[072-054]last time; one of them was that I was to be let say all I liked, so

[072-055]long as it was not against my neighbour or your worship’s authority;

[072-056]and so far, it seems to me, I have not broken the said article.”

[072-057]“I remember no such article, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “and even if it

[072-058]were so, I desire you to hold your tongue and come along; for the

[072-059]instruments we heard last night are already beginning to enliven the

[072-060]valleys again, and no doubt the marriage will take place in the cool of

[072-061]the morning, and not in the heat of the afternoon.”

[072-062]Sancho did as his master bade him, and putting the saddle on Rocinante

[072-063]and the pack-saddle on Dapple, they both mounted and at a leisurely

[072-064]pace entered the arcade. The first thing that presented itself to

[072-065]Sancho’s eyes was a whole ox spitted on a whole elm tree, and in the

[072-066]fire at which it was to be roasted there was burning a middling-sized

[072-067]mountain of faggots, and six stewpots that stood round the blaze had

[072-068]not been made in the ordinary mould of common pots, for they were six

[072-069]half wine-jars, each fit to hold the contents of a slaughter-house;

[072-070]they swallowed up whole sheep and hid them away in their insides

[072-071]without showing any more sign of them than if they were pigeons.

[072-072]Countless were the hares ready skinned and the plucked fowls that hung

[072-073]on the trees for burial in the pots, numberless the wildfowl and game

[072-074]of various sorts suspended from the branches that the air might keep

[072-075]them cool. Sancho counted more than sixty wine skins of over six

[072-076]gallons each, and all filled, as it proved afterwards, with generous

[072-077]wines. There were, besides, piles of the whitest bread, like the heaps

[072-078]of corn one sees on the threshing-floors. There was a wall made of

[072-079]cheeses arranged like open brick-work, and two cauldrons full of oil,

[072-080]bigger than those of a dyer’s shop, served for cooking fritters, which

[072-081]when fried were taken out with two mighty shovels, and plunged into

[072-082]another cauldron of prepared honey that stood close by. Of cooks and

[072-083]cook-maids there were over fifty, all clean, brisk, and blithe. In the

[072-084]capacious belly of the ox were a dozen soft little sucking-pigs, which,

[072-085]sewn up there, served to give it tenderness and flavour. The spices of

[072-086]different kinds did not seem to have been bought by the pound but by

[072-087]the quarter, and all lay open to view in a great chest. In short, all

[072-088]the preparations made for the wedding were in rustic style, but

[072-089]abundant enough to feed an army.

[072-090]Sancho observed all, contemplated all, and everything won his heart.

[072-091]The first to captivate and take his fancy were the pots, out of which

[072-092]he would have very gladly helped himself to a moderate pipkinful; then

[072-093]the wine skins secured his affections; and lastly, the produce of the

[072-094]frying-pans, if, indeed, such imposing cauldrons may be called

[072-095]frying-pans; and unable to control himself or bear it any longer, he

[072-096]approached one of the busy cooks and civilly but hungrily begged

[072-097]permission to soak a scrap of bread in one of the pots; to which the

[072-098]cook made answer, “Brother, this is not a day on which hunger is to

[072-099]have any sway, thanks to the rich Camacho; get down and look about for

[072-100]a ladle and skim off a hen or two, and much good may they do you.”

[072-101]“I don’t see one,” said Sancho.

[072-102]“Wait a bit,” said the cook; “sinner that I am! how particular and

[072-103]bashful you are!” and so saying, he seized a bucket and plunging it

[072-104]into one of the half jars took up three hens and a couple of geese, and

[072-105]said to Sancho, “Fall to, friend, and take the edge off your appetite

[072-106]with these skimmings until dinner-time comes.”

p20d.jpg (351K)


[072-107]“I have nothing to put them in,” said Sancho.

[072-108]“Well then,” said the cook, “take spoon and all; for Camacho’s wealth

[072-109]and happiness furnish everything.”

[072-110]While Sancho fared thus, Don Quixote was watching the entrance, at one

[072-111]end of the arcade, of some twelve peasants, all in holiday and gala

[072-112]dress, mounted on twelve beautiful mares with rich handsome field

[072-113]trappings and a number of little bells attached to their petrals, who,

[072-114]marshalled in regular order, ran not one but several courses over the

[072-115]meadow, with jubilant shouts and cries of “Long live Camacho and

[072-116]Quiteria! he as rich as she is fair; and she the fairest on earth!”

[072-117]Hearing this, Don Quixote said to himself, “It is easy to see these

[072-118]folk have never seen my Dulcinea del Toboso; for if they had they would

[072-119]be more moderate in their praises of this Quiteria of theirs.”

[072-120]Shortly after this, several bands of dancers of various sorts began to

[072-121]enter the arcade at different points, and among them one of

[072-122]sword-dancers composed of some four-and-twenty lads of gallant and

[072-123]high-spirited mien, clad in the finest and whitest of linen, and with

[072-124]handkerchiefs embroidered in various colours with fine silk; and one of

[072-125]those on the mares asked an active youth who led them if any of the

[072-126]dancers had been wounded. “As yet, thank God, no one has been wounded,”

[072-127]said he, “we are all safe and sound;” and he at once began to execute

[072-128]complicated figures with the rest of his comrades, with so many turns

[072-129]and so great dexterity, that although Don Quixote was well used to see

[072-130]dances of the same kind, he thought he had never seen any so good as

[072-131]this. He also admired another that came in composed of fair young

[072-132]maidens, none of whom seemed to be under fourteen or over eighteen

[072-133]years of age, all clad in green stuff, with their locks partly braided,

[072-134]partly flowing loose, but all of such bright gold as to vie with the

[072-135]sunbeams, and over them they wore garlands of jessamine, roses,

[072-136]amaranth, and honeysuckle. At their head were a venerable old man and

[072-137]an ancient dame, more brisk and active, however, than might have been

[072-138]expected from their years. The notes of a Zamora bagpipe accompanied

[072-139]them, and with modesty in their countenances and in their eyes, and

[072-140]lightness in their feet, they looked the best dancers in the world.

p20e.jpg (361K)


[072-141]Following these there came an artistic dance of the sort they call

[072-142]“speaking dances.” It was composed of eight nymphs in two files, with

[072-143]the god Cupid leading one and Interest the other, the former furnished

[072-144]with wings, bow, quiver and arrows, the latter in a rich dress of gold

[072-145]and silk of divers colours. The nymphs that followed Love bore their

[072-146]names written on white parchment in large letters on their backs.

[072-147]“Poetry” was the name of the first, “Wit” of the second, “Birth” of the

[072-148]third, and “Valour” of the fourth. Those that followed Interest were

[072-149]distinguished in the same way; the badge of the first announced

[072-150]“Liberality,” that of the second “Largess,” the third “Treasure,” and

[072-151]the fourth “Peaceful Possession.” In front of them all came a wooden

[072-152]castle drawn by four wild men, all clad in ivy and hemp stained green,

[072-153]and looking so natural that they nearly terrified Sancho. On the front

[072-154]of the castle and on each of the four sides of its frame it bore the

[072-155]inscription “Castle of Caution.” Four skillful tabor and flute players

[072-156]accompanied them, and the dance having been opened, Cupid, after

[072-157]executing two figures, raised his eyes and bent his bow against a

[072-158]damsel who stood between the turrets of the castle, and thus addressed

[072-159]her:

[072-160]I am the mighty God whose sway

[072-161]Is potent over land and sea.

[072-162]The heavens above us own me; nay,

[072-163]The shades below acknowledge me.

[072-164]I know not fear, I have my will,

[072-165]Whate’er my whim or fancy be;

[072-166]For me there’s no impossible,

[072-167]I order, bind, forbid, set free.

[072-168]Having concluded the stanza he discharged an arrow at the top of the

[072-169]castle, and went back to his place. Interest then came forward and went

[072-170]through two more figures, and as soon as the tabors ceased, he said:

[072-171]But mightier than Love am I,

[072-172]Though Love it be that leads me on,

[072-173]Than mine no lineage is more high,

[072-174]Or older, underneath the sun.

[072-175]To use me rightly few know how,

[072-176]To act without me fewer still,

[072-177]For I am Interest, and I vow

[072-178]For evermore to do thy will.

[072-179]Interest retired, and Poetry came forward, and when she had gone

[072-180]through her figures like the others, fixing her eyes on the damsel of

[072-181]the castle, she said:

[072-182]With many a fanciful conceit,

[072-183]Fair Lady, winsome Poesy

[072-184]Her soul, an offering at thy feet,

[072-185]Presents in sonnets unto thee.

[072-186]If thou my homage wilt not scorn,

[072-187]Thy fortune, watched by envious eyes,

[072-188]On wings of poesy upborne

[072-189]Shall be exalted to the skies.

[072-190]Poetry withdrew, and on the side of Interest Liberality advanced, and

[072-191]after having gone through her figures, said:

[072-192]To give, while shunning each extreme,

[072-193]The sparing hand, the over-free,

[072-194]Therein consists, so wise men deem,

[072-195]The virtue Liberality.

[072-196]But thee, fair lady, to enrich,

[072-197]Myself a prodigal I’ll prove,

[072-198]A vice not wholly shameful, which

[072-199]May find its fair excuse in love.

[072-200]In the same manner all the characters of the two bands advanced and

[072-201]retired, and each executed its figures, and delivered its verses, some

[072-202]of them graceful, some burlesque, but Don Quixote’s memory (though he

[072-203]had an excellent one) only carried away those that have been just

[072-204]quoted. All then mingled together, forming chains and breaking off

[072-205]again with graceful, unconstrained gaiety; and whenever Love passed in

[072-206]front of the castle he shot his arrows up at it, while Interest broke

[072-207]gilded pellets against it. At length, after they had danced a good

[072-208]while, Interest drew out a great purse, made of the skin of a large

[072-209]brindled cat and to all appearance full of money, and flung it at the

[072-210]castle, and with the force of the blow the boards fell asunder and

[072-211]tumbled down, leaving the damsel exposed and unprotected. Interest and

[072-212]the characters of his band advanced, and throwing a great chain of gold

[072-213]over her neck pretended to take her and lead her away captive, on

[072-214]seeing which, Love and his supporters made as though they would release

[072-215]her, the whole action being to the accompaniment of the tabors and in

[072-216]the form of a regular dance. The wild men made peace between them, and

[072-217]with great dexterity readjusted and fixed the boards of the castle, and

[072-218]the damsel once more ensconced herself within; and with this the dance

[072-219]wound up, to the great enjoyment of the beholders.

[072-220]Don Quixote asked one of the nymphs who it was that had composed and

[072-221]arranged it. She replied that it was a beneficiary of the town who had

[072-222]a nice taste in devising things of the sort. “I will lay a wager,” said

[072-223]Don Quixote, “that the same bachelor or beneficiary is a greater friend

[072-224]of Camacho’s than of Basilio’s, and that he is better at satire than at

[072-225]vespers; he has introduced the accomplishments of Basilio and the

[072-226]riches of Camacho very neatly into the dance.” Sancho Panza, who was

[072-227]listening to all this, exclaimed, “The king is my cock; I stick to

[072-228]Camacho.” “It is easy to see thou art a clown, Sancho,” said Don

[072-229]Quixote, “and one of that sort that cry ‘Long life to the conqueror.’”

[072-230]“I don’t know of what sort I am,” returned Sancho, “but I know very

[072-231]well I’ll never get such elegant skimmings off Basilio’s pots as these

[072-232]I have got off Camacho’s;” and he showed him the bucketful of geese and

[072-233]hens, and seizing one began to eat with great gaiety and appetite,

[072-234]saying, “A fig for the accomplishments of Basilio! As much as thou hast

[072-235]so much art thou worth, and as much as thou art worth so much hast

[072-236]thou. As a grandmother of mine used to say, there are only two families

[072-237]in the world, the Haves and the Haven’ts; and she stuck to the Haves;

[072-238]and to this day, Señor Don Quixote, people would sooner feel the pulse

[072-239]of ‘Have,’ than of ‘Know;’ an ass covered with gold looks better than a

[072-240]horse with a pack-saddle. So once more I say I stick to Camacho, the

[072-241]bountiful skimmings of whose pots are geese and hens, hares and

[072-242]rabbits; but of Basilio’s, if any ever come to hand, or even to foot,

[072-243]they’ll be only rinsings.”

[072-244]“Hast thou finished thy harangue, Sancho?” said Don Quixote. “Of course

[072-245]I have finished it,” replied Sancho, “because I see your worship takes

[072-246]offence at it; but if it was not for that, there was work enough cut

[072-247]out for three days.”

[072-248]“God grant I may see thee dumb before I die, Sancho,” said Don Quixote.

[072-249]“At the rate we are going,” said Sancho, “I’ll be chewing clay before

[072-250]your worship dies; and then, maybe, I’ll be so dumb that I’ll not say a

[072-251]word until the end of the world, or, at least, till the day of

[072-252]judgment.”

[072-253]“Even should that happen, O Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “thy silence

[072-254]will never come up to all thou hast talked, art talking, and wilt talk

[072-255]all thy life; moreover, it naturally stands to reason, that my death

[072-256]will come before thine; so I never expect to see thee dumb, not even

[072-257]when thou art drinking or sleeping, and that is the utmost I can say.”

[072-258]“In good faith, señor,” replied Sancho, “there’s no trusting that

[072-259]fleshless one, I mean Death, who devours the lamb as soon as the sheep,

[072-260]and, as I have heard our curate say, treads with equal foot upon the

[072-261]lofty towers of kings and the lowly huts of the poor. That lady is more

[072-262]mighty than dainty, she is in no way squeamish, she devours all and is

[072-263]ready for all, and fills her alforjas with people of all sorts, ages,

[072-264]and ranks. She is no reaper that sleeps out the noontide; at all times

[072-265]she is reaping and cutting down, as well the dry grass as the green;

[072-266]she never seems to chew, but bolts and swallows all that is put before

[072-267]her, for she has a canine appetite that is never satisfied; and though

[072-268]she has no belly, she shows she has a dropsy and is athirst to drink

[072-269]the lives of all that live, as one would drink a jug of cold water.”

[072-270]“Say no more, Sancho,” said Don Quixote at this; “don’t try to better

[072-271]it, and risk a fall; for in truth what thou hast said about death in

[072-272]thy rustic phrase is what a good preacher might have said. I tell thee,

[072-273]Sancho, if thou hadst discretion equal to thy mother wit, thou mightst

[072-274]take a pulpit in hand, and go about the world preaching fine sermons.”

[072-275]“He preaches well who lives well,” said Sancho, “and I know no more

[072-276]theology than that.”

[072-277]“Nor needst thou,” said Don Quixote, “but I cannot conceive or make out

[072-278]how it is that, the fear of God being the beginning of wisdom, thou,

[072-279]who art more afraid of a lizard than of him, knowest so much.”

[072-280]“Pass judgment on your chivalries, señor,” returned Sancho, “and don’t

[072-281]set yourself up to judge of other men’s fears or braveries, for I am as

[072-282]good a fearer of God as my neighbours; but leave me to despatch these

[072-283]skimmings, for all the rest is only idle talk that we shall be called

[072-284]to account for in the other world;” and so saying, he began a fresh

[072-285]attack on the bucket, with such a hearty appetite that he aroused Don

[072-286]Quixote’s, who no doubt would have helped him had he not been prevented

[072-287]by what must be told farther on.
