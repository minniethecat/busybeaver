[114-001]WHICH DEALS WITH THE ADVENTURE OF THE ENCHANTED HEAD, TOGETHER WITH

OTHER TRIVIAL MATTERS WHICH CANNOT BE LEFT UNTOLD

[114-002]Don Quixote’s host was one Don Antonio Moreno by name, a gentleman of

[114-003]wealth and intelligence, and very fond of diverting himself in any fair

[114-004]and good-natured way; and having Don Quixote in his house he set about

[114-005]devising modes of making him exhibit his mad points in some harmless

[114-006]fashion; for jests that give pain are no jests, and no sport is worth

[114-007]anything if it hurts another. The first thing he did was to make Don

[114-008]Quixote take off his armour, and lead him, in that tight chamois suit

[114-009]we have already described and depicted more than once, out on a balcony

[114-010]overhanging one of the chief streets of the city, in full view of the

[114-011]crowd and of the boys, who gazed at him as they would at a monkey. The

[114-012]cavaliers in livery careered before him again as though it were for him

[114-013]alone, and not to enliven the festival of the day, that they wore it,

[114-014]and Sancho was in high delight, for it seemed to him that, how he knew

[114-015]not, he had fallen upon another Camacho’s wedding, another house like

[114-016]Don Diego de Miranda’s, another castle like the duke’s. Some of Don

[114-017]Antonio’s friends dined with him that day, and all showed honour to Don

[114-018]Quixote and treated him as a knight-errant, and he becoming puffed up

[114-019]and exalted in consequence could not contain himself for satisfaction.

[114-020]Such were the drolleries of Sancho that all the servants of the house,

[114-021]and all who heard him, were kept hanging upon his lips. While at table

[114-022]Don Antonio said to him, “We hear, worthy Sancho, that you are so fond

[114-023]of manjar blanco and forced-meat balls, that if you have any left, you

[114-024]keep them in your bosom for the next day.”

[114-025]“No, señor, that’s not true,” said Sancho, “for I am more cleanly than

[114-026]greedy, and my master Don Quixote here knows well that we two are used

[114-027]to live for a week on a handful of acorns or nuts. To be sure, if it so

[114-028]happens that they offer me a heifer, I run with a halter; I mean, I eat

[114-029]what I’m given, and make use of opportunities as I find them; but

[114-030]whoever says that I’m an out-of-the-way eater or not cleanly, let me

[114-031]tell him that he is wrong; and I’d put it in a different way if I did

[114-032]not respect the honourable beards that are at the table.”

[114-033]“Indeed,” said Don Quixote, “Sancho’s moderation and cleanliness in

[114-034]eating might be inscribed and graved on plates of brass, to be kept in

[114-035]eternal remembrance in ages to come. It is true that when he is hungry

[114-036]there is a certain appearance of voracity about him, for he eats at a

[114-037]great pace and chews with both jaws; but cleanliness he is always

[114-038]mindful of; and when he was governor he learned how to eat daintily, so

[114-039]much so that he eats grapes, and even pomegranate pips, with a fork.”

[114-040]“What!” said Don Antonio, “has Sancho been a governor?”

[114-041]“Ay,” said Sancho, “and of an island called Barataria. I governed it to

[114-042]perfection for ten days; and lost my rest all the time; and learned to

[114-043]look down upon all the governments in the world; I got out of it by

[114-044]taking to flight, and fell into a pit where I gave myself up for dead,

[114-045]and out of which I escaped alive by a miracle.”

[114-046]Don Quixote then gave them a minute account of the whole affair of

[114-047]Sancho’s government, with which he greatly amused his hearers.

[114-048]On the cloth being removed Don Antonio, taking Don Quixote by the hand,

[114-049]passed with him into a distant room in which there was nothing in the

[114-050]way of furniture except a table, apparently of jasper, resting on a

[114-051]pedestal of the same, upon which was set up, after the fashion of the

[114-052]busts of the Roman emperors, a head which seemed to be of bronze. Don

[114-053]Antonio traversed the whole apartment with Don Quixote and walked round

[114-054]the table several times, and then said, “Now, Señor Don Quixote, that I

[114-055]am satisfied that no one is listening to us, and that the door is shut,

[114-056]I will tell you of one of the rarest adventures, or more properly

[114-057]speaking strange things, that can be imagined, on condition that you

[114-058]will keep what I say to you in the remotest recesses of secrecy.”

[114-059]“I swear it,” said Don Quixote, “and for greater security I will put a

[114-060]flag-stone over it; for I would have you know, Señor Don Antonio” (he

[114-061]had by this time learned his name), “that you are addressing one who,

[114-062]though he has ears to hear, has no tongue to speak; so that you may

[114-063]safely transfer whatever you have in your bosom into mine, and rely

[114-064]upon it that you have consigned it to the depths of silence.”

[114-065]“In reliance upon that promise,” said Don Antonio, “I will astonish you

[114-066]with what you shall see and hear, and relieve myself of some of the

[114-067]vexation it gives me to have no one to whom I can confide my secrets,

[114-068]for they are not of a sort to be entrusted to everybody.”

[114-069]Don Quixote was puzzled, wondering what could be the object of such

[114-070]precautions; whereupon Don Antonio taking his hand passed it over the

[114-071]bronze head and the whole table and the pedestal of jasper on which it

[114-072]stood, and then said, “This head, Señor Don Quixote, has been made and

[114-073]fabricated by one of the greatest magicians and wizards the world ever

[114-074]saw, a Pole, I believe, by birth, and a pupil of the famous Escotillo

[114-075]of whom such marvellous stories are told. He was here in my house, and

[114-076]for a consideration of a thousand crowns that I gave him he constructed

[114-077]this head, which has the property and virtue of answering whatever

[114-078]questions are put to its ear. He observed the points of the compass, he

[114-079]traced figures, he studied the stars, he watched favourable moments,

[114-080]and at length brought it to the perfection we shall see to-morrow, for

[114-081]on Fridays it is mute, and this being Friday we must wait till the next

[114-082]day. In the interval your worship may consider what you would like to

[114-083]ask it; and I know by experience that in all its answers it tells the

[114-084]truth.”

[114-085]Don Quixote was amazed at the virtue and property of the head, and was

[114-086]inclined to disbelieve Don Antonio; but seeing what a short time he had

[114-087]to wait to test the matter, he did not choose to say anything except

[114-088]that he thanked him for having revealed to him so mighty a secret. They

[114-089]then quitted the room, Don Antonio locked the door, and they repaired

[114-090]to the chamber where the rest of the gentlemen were assembled. In the

[114-091]meantime Sancho had recounted to them several of the adventures and

[114-092]accidents that had happened his master.

[114-093]That afternoon they took Don Quixote out for a stroll, not in his

[114-094]armour but in street costume, with a surcoat of tawny cloth upon him,

[114-095]that at that season would have made ice itself sweat. Orders were left

[114-096]with the servants to entertain Sancho so as not to let him leave the

[114-097]house. Don Quixote was mounted, not on Rocinante, but upon a tall mule

[114-098]of easy pace and handsomely caparisoned. They put the surcoat on him,

[114-099]and on the back, without his perceiving it, they stitched a parchment

[114-100]on which they wrote in large letters, “This is Don Quixote of La

[114-101]Mancha.” As they set out upon their excursion the placard attracted the

[114-102]eyes of all who chanced to see him, and as they read out, “This is Don

[114-103]Quixote of La Mancha,” Don Quixote was amazed to see how many people

[114-104]gazed at him, called him by his name, and recognised him, and turning

[114-105]to Don Antonio, who rode at his side, he observed to him, “Great are

[114-106]the privileges knight-errantry involves, for it makes him who professes

[114-107]it known and famous in every region of the earth; see, Don Antonio,

[114-108]even the very boys of this city know me without ever having seen me.”

[114-109]“True, Señor Don Quixote,” returned Don Antonio; “for as fire cannot be

[114-110]hidden or kept secret, virtue cannot escape being recognised; and that

[114-111]which is attained by the profession of arms shines distinguished above

[114-112]all others.”

[114-113]It came to pass, however, that as Don Quixote was proceeding amid the

[114-114]acclamations that have been described, a Castilian, reading the

[114-115]inscription on his back, cried out in a loud voice, “The devil take

[114-116]thee for a Don Quixote of La Mancha! What! art thou here, and not dead

[114-117]of the countless drubbings that have fallen on thy ribs? Thou art mad;

[114-118]and if thou wert so by thyself, and kept thyself within thy madness, it

[114-119]would not be so bad; but thou hast the gift of making fools and

[114-120]blockheads of all who have anything to do with thee or say to thee.

[114-121]Why, look at these gentlemen bearing thee company! Get thee home,

[114-122]blockhead, and see after thy affairs, and thy wife and children, and

[114-123]give over these fooleries that are sapping thy brains and skimming away

[114-124]thy wits.”

[114-125]“Go your own way, brother,” said Don Antonio, “and don’t offer advice

[114-126]to those who don’t ask you for it. Señor Don Quixote is in his full

[114-127]senses, and we who bear him company are not fools; virtue is to be

[114-128]honoured wherever it may be found; go, and bad luck to you, and don’t

[114-129]meddle where you are not wanted.”

[114-130]“By God, your worship is right,” replied the Castilian; “for to advise

[114-131]this good man is to kick against the pricks; still for all that it

[114-132]fills me with pity that the sound wit they say the blockhead has in

[114-133]everything should dribble away by the channel of his knight-errantry;

[114-134]but may the bad luck your worship talks of follow me and all my

[114-135]descendants, if, from this day forth, though I should live longer than

[114-136]Methuselah, I ever give advice to anybody even if he asks me for it.”

[114-137]The advice-giver took himself off, and they continued their stroll; but

[114-138]so great was the press of the boys and people to read the placard, that

[114-139]Don Antonio was forced to remove it as if he were taking off something

[114-140]else.

[114-141]Night came and they went home, and there was a ladies’ dancing party,

[114-142]for Don Antonio’s wife, a lady of rank and gaiety, beauty and wit, had

[114-143]invited some friends of hers to come and do honour to her guest and

[114-144]amuse themselves with his strange delusions. Several of them came, they

[114-145]supped sumptuously, the dance began at about ten o’clock. Among the

[114-146]ladies were two of a mischievous and frolicsome turn, and, though

[114-147]perfectly modest, somewhat free in playing tricks for harmless

[114-148]diversion’s sake. These two were so indefatigable in taking Don Quixote

[114-149]out to dance that they tired him down, not only in body but in spirit.

[114-150]It was a sight to see the figure Don Quixote made, long, lank, lean,

[114-151]and yellow, his garments clinging tight to him, ungainly, and above all

[114-152]anything but agile.

p62c.jpg (342K)


[114-153]The gay ladies made secret love to him, and he on his part secretly

[114-154]repelled them, but finding himself hard pressed by their blandishments

[114-155]he lifted up his voice and exclaimed, “_Fugite, partes adversæ!_ Leave

[114-156]me in peace, unwelcome overtures; avaunt, with your desires, ladies,

[114-157]for she who is queen of mine, the peerless Dulcinea del Toboso, suffers

[114-158]none but hers to lead me captive and subdue me;” and so saying he sat

[114-159]down on the floor in the middle of the room, tired out and broken down

[114-160]by all this exertion in the dance.

[114-161]Don Antonio directed him to be taken up bodily and carried to bed, and

[114-162]the first that laid hold of him was Sancho, saying as he did so, “In an

[114-163]evil hour you took to dancing, master mine; do you fancy all mighty men

[114-164]of valour are dancers, and all knights-errant given to capering? If you

[114-165]do, I can tell you you are mistaken; there’s many a man would rather

[114-166]undertake to kill a giant than cut a caper. If it had been the

[114-167]shoe-fling you were at I could take your place, for I can do the

[114-168]shoe-fling like a gerfalcon; but I’m no good at dancing.”

[114-169]With these and other observations Sancho set the whole ball-room

[114-170]laughing, and then put his master to bed, covering him up well so that

[114-171]he might sweat out any chill caught after his dancing.

[114-172]The next day Don Antonio thought he might as well make trial of the

[114-173]enchanted head, and with Don Quixote, Sancho, and two others, friends

[114-174]of his, besides the two ladies that had tired out Don Quixote at the

[114-175]ball, who had remained for the night with Don Antonio’s wife, he locked

[114-176]himself up in the chamber where the head was. He explained to them the

[114-177]property it possessed and entrusted the secret to them, telling them

[114-178]that now for the first time he was going to try the virtue of the

[114-179]enchanted head; but except Don Antonio’s two friends no one else was

[114-180]privy to the mystery of the enchantment, and if Don Antonio had not

[114-181]first revealed it to them they would have been inevitably reduced to

[114-182]the same state of amazement as the rest, so artfully and skilfully was

[114-183]it contrived.

[114-184]The first to approach the ear of the head was Don Antonio himself, and

[114-185]in a low voice but not so low as not to be audible to all, he said to

[114-186]it, “Head, tell me by the virtue that lies in thee what am I at this

[114-187]moment thinking of?”

[114-188]The head, without any movement of the lips, answered in a clear and

[114-189]distinct voice, so as to be heard by all, “I cannot judge of thoughts.”

[114-190]All were thunderstruck at this, and all the more so as they saw that

[114-191]there was nobody anywhere near the table or in the whole room that

[114-192]could have answered. “How many of us are here?” asked Don Antonio once

[114-193]more; and it was answered him in the same way softly, “Thou and thy

[114-194]wife, with two friends of thine and two of hers, and a famous knight

[114-195]called Don Quixote of La Mancha, and a squire of his, Sancho Panza by

[114-196]name.”

[114-197]Now there was fresh astonishment; now everyone’s hair was standing on

[114-198]end with awe; and Don Antonio retiring from the head exclaimed, “This

[114-199]suffices to show me that I have not been deceived by him who sold thee

[114-200]to me, O sage head, talking head, answering head, wonderful head! Let

[114-201]someone else go and put what question he likes to it.”

[114-202]And as women are commonly impulsive and inquisitive, the first to come

[114-203]forward was one of the two friends of Don Antonio’s wife, and her

[114-204]question was, “Tell me, Head, what shall I do to be very beautiful?”

[114-205]and the answer she got was, “Be very modest.”

[114-206]“I question thee no further,” said the fair querist.

[114-207]Her companion then came up and said, “I should like to know, Head,

[114-208]whether my husband loves me or not;” the answer given to her was,

[114-209]“Think how he uses thee, and thou mayest guess;” and the married lady

[114-210]went off saying, “That answer did not need a question; for of course

[114-211]the treatment one receives shows the disposition of him from whom it is

[114-212]received.”

[114-213]Then one of Don Antonio’s two friends advanced and asked it, “Who am

[114-214]I?” “Thou knowest,” was the answer. “That is not what I ask thee,” said

[114-215]the gentleman, “but to tell me if thou knowest me.” “Yes, I know thee,

[114-216]thou art Don Pedro Noriz,” was the reply.

[114-217]“I do not seek to know more,” said the gentleman, “for this is enough

[114-218]to convince me, O Head, that thou knowest everything;” and as he

[114-219]retired the other friend came forward and asked it, “Tell me, Head,

[114-220]what are the wishes of my eldest son?”

[114-221]“I have said already,” was the answer, “that I cannot judge of wishes;

[114-222]however, I can tell thee the wish of thy son is to bury thee.”

[114-223]“That’s ‘what I see with my eyes I point out with my finger,’” said the

[114-224]gentleman, “so I ask no more.”

[114-225]Don Antonio’s wife came up and said, “I know not what to ask thee,

[114-226]Head; I would only seek to know of thee if I shall have many years of

[114-227]enjoyment of my good husband;” and the answer she received was, “Thou

[114-228]shalt, for his vigour and his temperate habits promise many years of

[114-229]life, which by their intemperance others so often cut short.”

[114-230]Then Don Quixote came forward and said, “Tell me, thou that answerest,

[114-231]was that which I describe as having happened to me in the cave of

[114-232]Montesinos the truth or a dream? Will Sancho’s whipping be accomplished

[114-233]without fail? Will the disenchantment of Dulcinea be brought about?”

[114-234]“As to the question of the cave,” was the reply, “there is much to be

[114-235]said; there is something of both in it. Sancho’s whipping will proceed

[114-236]leisurely. The disenchantment of Dulcinea will attain its due

[114-237]consummation.”

[114-238]“I seek to know no more,” said Don Quixote; “let me but see Dulcinea

[114-239]disenchanted, and I will consider that all the good fortune I could

[114-240]wish for has come upon me all at once.”

[114-241]The last questioner was Sancho, and his questions were, “Head, shall I

[114-242]by any chance have another government? Shall I ever escape from the

[114-243]hard life of a squire? Shall I get back to see my wife and children?”

[114-244]To which the answer came, “Thou shalt govern in thy house; and if thou

[114-245]returnest to it thou shalt see thy wife and children; and on ceasing to

[114-246]serve thou shalt cease to be a squire.”

[114-247]“Good, by God!” said Sancho Panza; “I could have told myself that; the

[114-248]prophet Perogrullo could have said no more.”

[114-249]“What answer wouldst thou have, beast?” said Don Quixote; “is it not

[114-250]enough that the replies this head has given suit the questions put to

[114-251]it?”

[114-252]“Yes, it is enough,” said Sancho; “but I should have liked it to have

[114-253]made itself plainer and told me more.”

[114-254]The questions and answers came to an end here, but not the wonder with

[114-255]which all were filled, except Don Antonio’s two friends who were in the

[114-256]secret. This Cide Hamete Benengeli thought fit to reveal at once, not

[114-257]to keep the world in suspense, fancying that the head had some strange

[114-258]magical mystery in it. He says, therefore, that on the model of another

[114-259]head, the work of an image maker, which he had seen at Madrid, Don

[114-260]Antonio made this one at home for his own amusement and to astonish

[114-261]ignorant people; and its mechanism was as follows. The table was of

[114-262]wood painted and varnished to imitate jasper, and the pedestal on which

[114-263]it stood was of the same material, with four eagles’ claws projecting

[114-264]from it to support the weight more steadily. The head, which resembled

[114-265]a bust or figure of a Roman emperor, and was coloured like bronze, was

[114-266]hollow throughout, as was the table, into which it was fitted so

[114-267]exactly that no trace of the joining was visible. The pedestal of the

[114-268]table was also hollow and communicated with the throat and neck of the

[114-269]head, and the whole was in communication with another room underneath

[114-270]the chamber in which the head stood. Through the entire cavity in the

[114-271]pedestal, table, throat and neck of the bust or figure, there passed a

[114-272]tube of tin carefully adjusted and concealed from sight. In the room

[114-273]below corresponding to the one above was placed the person who was to

[114-274]answer, with his mouth to the tube, and the voice, as in an

[114-275]ear-trumpet, passed from above downwards, and from below upwards, the

[114-276]words coming clearly and distinctly; it was impossible, thus, to detect

[114-277]the trick. A nephew of Don Antonio’s, a smart sharp-witted student, was

[114-278]the answerer, and as he had been told beforehand by his uncle who the

[114-279]persons were that would come with him that day into the chamber where

[114-280]the head was, it was an easy matter for him to answer the first

[114-281]question at once and correctly; the others he answered by guess-work,

[114-282]and, being clever, cleverly. Cide Hamete adds that this marvellous

[114-283]contrivance stood for some ten or twelve days; but that, as it became

[114-284]noised abroad through the city that he had in his house an enchanted

[114-285]head that answered all who asked questions of it, Don Antonio, fearing

[114-286]it might come to the ears of the watchful sentinels of our faith,

[114-287]explained the matter to the inquisitors, who commanded him to break it

[114-288]up and have done with it, lest the ignorant vulgar should be

[114-289]scandalised. By Don Quixote, however, and by Sancho the head was still

[114-290]held to be an enchanted one, and capable of answering questions, though

[114-291]more to Don Quixote’s satisfaction than Sancho’s.

[114-292]The gentlemen of the city, to gratify Don Antonio and also to do the

[114-293]honours to Don Quixote, and give him an opportunity of displaying his

[114-294]folly, made arrangements for a tilting at the ring in six days from

[114-295]that time, which, however, for reason that will be mentioned hereafter,

[114-296]did not take place.

[114-297]Don Quixote took a fancy to stroll about the city quietly and on foot,

[114-298]for he feared that if he went on horseback the boys would follow him;

[114-299]so he and Sancho and two servants that Don Antonio gave him set out for

[114-300]a walk. Thus it came to pass that going along one of the streets Don

[114-301]Quixote lifted up his eyes and saw written in very large letters over a

[114-302]door, “Books printed here,” at which he was vastly pleased, for until

[114-303]then he had never seen a printing office, and he was curious to know

[114-304]what it was like. He entered with all his following, and saw them

[114-305]drawing sheets in one place, correcting in another, setting up type

[114-306]here, revising there; in short all the work that is to be seen in great

[114-307]printing offices. He went up to one case and asked what they were about

[114-308]there; the workmen told him, he watched them with wonder, and passed

[114-309]on. He approached one man, among others, and asked him what he was

[114-310]doing. The workman replied, “Señor, this gentleman here” (pointing to a

[114-311]man of prepossessing appearance and a certain gravity of look) “has

[114-312]translated an Italian book into our Spanish tongue, and I am setting it

[114-313]up in type for the press.”

[114-314]“What is the title of the book?” asked Don Quixote; to which the author

[114-315]replied, “Señor, in Italian the book is called _Le Bagatelle_.”

[114-316]“And what does _Le Bagatelle_ import in our Spanish?” asked Don

[114-317]Quixote.

[114-318]“_Le Bagatelle_,” said the author, “is as though we should say in

[114-319]Spanish _Los Juguetes;_ but though the book is humble in name it has

[114-320]good solid matter in it.”

[114-321]“I,” said Don Quixote, “have some little smattering of Italian, and I

[114-322]plume myself on singing some of Ariosto’s stanzas; but tell me, señor—I

[114-323]do not say this to test your ability, but merely out of curiosity—have

[114-324]you ever met with the word _pignatta_ in your book?”

[114-325]“Yes, often,” said the author.

[114-326]“And how do you render that in Spanish?”

[114-327]“How should I render it,” returned the author, “but by _olla_?”

[114-328]“Body o’ me,” exclaimed Don Quixote, “what a proficient you are in the

[114-329]Italian language! I would lay a good wager that where they say in

[114-330]Italian _piace_ you say in Spanish _place_, and where they say _piu_

[114-331]you say _mas_, and you translate _sù_ by _arriba_ and _giù_ by

[114-332]_abajo_.”

[114-333]“I translate them so of course,” said the author, “for those are their

[114-334]proper equivalents.”

[114-335]“I would venture to swear,” said Don Quixote, “that your worship is not

[114-336]known in the world, which always begrudges their reward to rare wits

[114-337]and praiseworthy labours. What talents lie wasted there! What genius

[114-338]thrust away into corners! What worth left neglected! Still it seems to

[114-339]me that translation from one language into another, if it be not from

[114-340]the queens of languages, the Greek and the Latin, is like looking at

[114-341]Flemish tapestries on the wrong side; for though the figures are

[114-342]visible, they are full of threads that make them indistinct, and they

[114-343]do not show with the smoothness and brightness of the right side; and

[114-344]translation from easy languages argues neither ingenuity nor command of

[114-345]words, any more than transcribing or copying out one document from

[114-346]another. But I do not mean by this to draw the inference that no credit

[114-347]is to be allowed for the work of translating, for a man may employ

[114-348]himself in ways worse and less profitable to himself. This estimate

[114-349]does not include two famous translators, Doctor Cristóbal de Figueroa,
