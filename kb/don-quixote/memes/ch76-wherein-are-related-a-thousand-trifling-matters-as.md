[076-001]WHEREIN ARE RELATED A THOUSAND TRIFLING MATTERS, AS TRIVIAL AS THEY ARE

NECESSARY TO THE RIGHT UNDERSTANDING OF THIS GREAT HISTORY

p24a.jpg (137K)


[076-002]He who translated this great history from the original written by its

[076-003]first author, Cide Hamete Benengeli, says that on coming to the chapter

[076-004]giving the adventures of the cave of Montesinos he found written on the

[076-005]margin of it, in Hamete’s own hand, these exact words:

[076-006]“I cannot convince or persuade myself that everything that is written

[076-007]in the preceding chapter could have precisely happened to the valiant

[076-008]Don Quixote; and for this reason, that all the adventures that have

[076-009]occurred up to the present have been possible and probable; but as for

[076-010]this one of the cave, I see no way of accepting it as true, as it

[076-011]passes all reasonable bounds. For me to believe that Don Quixote could

[076-012]lie, he being the most truthful gentleman and the noblest knight of his

[076-013]time, is impossible; he would not have told a lie though he were shot

[076-014]to death with arrows. On the other hand, I reflect that he related and

[076-015]told the story with all the circumstances detailed, and that he could

[076-016]not in so short a space have fabricated such a vast complication of

[076-017]absurdities; if, then, this adventure seems apocryphal, it is no fault

[076-018]of mine; and so, without affirming its falsehood or its truth, I write

[076-019]it down. Decide for thyself in thy wisdom, reader; for I am not bound,

[076-020]nor is it in my power, to do more; though certain it is they say that

[076-021]at the time of his death he retracted, and said he had invented it,

[076-022]thinking it matched and tallied with the adventures he had read of in

[076-023]his histories.” And then he goes on to say:

[076-024]The cousin was amazed as well at Sancho’s boldness as at the patience

[076-025]of his master, and concluded that the good temper the latter displayed

[076-026]arose from the happiness he felt at having seen his lady Dulcinea, even

[076-027]enchanted as she was; because otherwise the words and language Sancho

[076-028]had addressed to him deserved a thrashing; for indeed he seemed to him

[076-029]to have been rather impudent to his master, to whom he now observed,

[076-030]“I, Señor Don Quixote of La Mancha, look upon the time I have spent in

[076-031]travelling with your worship as very well employed, for I have gained

[076-032]four things in the course of it; the first is that I have made your

[076-033]acquaintance, which I consider great good fortune; the second, that I

[076-034]have learned what the cave of Montesinos contains, together with the

[076-035]transformations of Guadiana and of the lakes of Ruidera; which will be

[076-036]of use to me for the Spanish Ovid that I have in hand; the third, to

[076-037]have discovered the antiquity of cards, that they were in use at least

[076-038]in the time of Charlemagne, as may be inferred from the words you say

[076-039]Durandarte uttered when, at the end of that long spell while Montesinos

[076-040]was talking to him, he woke up and said, ‘Patience and shuffle.’ This

[076-041]phrase and expression he could not have learned while he was enchanted,

[076-042]but only before he had become so, in France, and in the time of the

[076-043]aforesaid emperor Charlemagne. And this demonstration is just the thing

[076-044]for me for that other book I am writing, the ‘Supplement to Polydore

[076-045]Vergil on the Invention of Antiquities;’ for I believe he never thought

[076-046]of inserting that of cards in his book, as I mean to do in mine, and it

[076-047]will be a matter of great importance, particularly when I can cite so

[076-048]grave and veracious an authority as Señor Durandarte. And the fourth

[076-049]thing is, that I have ascertained the source of the river Guadiana,

[076-050]heretofore unknown to mankind.”

[076-051]“You are right,” said Don Quixote; “but I should like to know, if by

[076-052]God’s favour they grant you a licence to print those books of

[076-053]yours—which I doubt—to whom do you mean to dedicate them?”

[076-054]“There are lords and grandees in Spain to whom they can be dedicated,”

[076-055]said the cousin.

[076-056]“Not many,” said Don Quixote; “not that they are unworthy of it, but

[076-057]because they do not care to accept books and incur the obligation of

[076-058]making the return that seems due to the author’s labour and courtesy.

[076-059]One prince I know who makes up for all the rest, and more—how much

[076-060]more, if I ventured to say, perhaps I should stir up envy in many a

[076-061]noble breast; but let this stand over for some more convenient time,

[076-062]and let us go and look for some place to shelter ourselves in

[076-063]to-night.”

[076-064]“Not far from this,” said the cousin, “there is a hermitage, where

[076-065]there lives a hermit, who they say was a soldier, and who has the

[076-066]reputation of being a good Christian and a very intelligent and

[076-067]charitable man. Close to the hermitage he has a small house which he

[076-068]built at his own cost, but though small it is large enough for the

[076-069]reception of guests.”

[076-070]“Has this hermit any hens, do you think?” asked Sancho.

[076-071]“Few hermits are without them,” said Don Quixote; “for those we see

[076-072]now-a-days are not like the hermits of the Egyptian deserts who were

[076-073]clad in palm-leaves, and lived on the roots of the earth. But do not

[076-074]think that by praising these I am disparaging the others; all I mean to

[076-075]say is that the penances of those of the present day do not come up to

[076-076]the asceticism and austerity of former times; but it does not follow

[076-077]from this that they are not all worthy; at least I think them so; and

[076-078]at the worst the hypocrite who pretends to be good does less harm than

[076-079]the open sinner.”

[076-080]At this point they saw approaching the spot where they stood a man on

[076-081]foot, proceeding at a rapid pace, and beating a mule loaded with lances

[076-082]and halberds. When he came up to them, he saluted them and passed on

[076-083]without stopping. Don Quixote called to him, “Stay, good fellow; you

[076-084]seem to be making more haste than suits that mule.”

[076-085]“I cannot stop, señor,” answered the man; “for the arms you see I carry

[076-086]here are to be used to-morrow, so I must not delay; God be with you.

[076-087]But if you want to know what I am carrying them for, I mean to lodge

[076-088]to-night at the inn that is beyond the hermitage, and if you be going

[076-089]the same road you will find me there, and I will tell you some curious

[076-090]things; once more God be with you;” and he urged on his mule at such a

[076-091]pace that Don Quixote had no time to ask him what these curious things

[076-092]were that he meant to tell them; and as he was somewhat inquisitive,

[076-093]and always tortured by his anxiety to learn something new, he decided

[076-094]to set out at once, and go and pass the night at the inn instead of

[076-095]stopping at the hermitage, where the cousin would have had them halt.

[076-096]Accordingly they mounted and all three took the direct road for the

[076-097]inn, which they reached a little before nightfall. On the road the

[076-098]cousin proposed they should go up to the hermitage to drink a sup. The

[076-099]instant Sancho heard this he steered his Dapple towards it, and Don

[076-100]Quixote and the cousin did the same; but it seems Sancho’s bad luck so

[076-101]ordered it that the hermit was not at home, for so a sub-hermit they

[076-102]found in the hermitage told them. They called for some of the best. She

[076-103]replied that her master had none, but that if they liked cheap water

[076-104]she would give it with great pleasure.

[076-105]“If I found any in water,” said Sancho, “there are wells along the road

[076-106]where I could have had enough of it. Ah, Camacho’s wedding, and

[076-107]plentiful house of Don Diego, how often do I miss you!”

[076-108]Leaving the hermitage, they pushed on towards the inn, and a little

[076-109]farther they came upon a youth who was pacing along in front of them at

[076-110]no great speed, so that they overtook him. He carried a sword over his

[076-111]shoulder, and slung on it a budget or bundle of his clothes apparently,

[076-112]probably his breeches or pantaloons, and his cloak and a shirt or two;

[076-113]for he had on a short jacket of velvet with a gloss like satin on it in

[076-114]places, and had his shirt out; his stockings were of silk, and his

[076-115]shoes square-toed as they wear them at court. His age might have been

[076-116]eighteen or nineteen; he was of a merry countenance, and to all

[076-117]appearance of an active habit, and he went along singing seguidillas to

[076-118]beguile the wearisomeness of the road. As they came up with him he was

[076-119]just finishing one, which the cousin got by heart and they say ran

[076-120]thus—

[076-121]I’m off to the wars

[076-122]For the want of pence,

[076-123]Oh, had I but money

[076-124]I’d show more sense.

[076-125]The first to address him was Don Quixote, who said, “You travel very

[076-126]airily, sir gallant; whither bound, may we ask, if it is your pleasure

[076-127]to tell us?”

[076-128]To which the youth replied, “The heat and my poverty are the reason of

[076-129]my travelling so airily, and it is to the wars that I am bound.”

[076-130]“How poverty?” asked Don Quixote; “the heat one can understand.”

[076-131]“Señor,” replied the youth, “in this bundle I carry velvet pantaloons

[076-132]to match this jacket; if I wear them out on the road, I shall not be

[076-133]able to make a decent appearance in them in the city, and I have not

[076-134]the wherewithal to buy others; and so for this reason, as well as to

[076-135]keep myself cool, I am making my way in this fashion to overtake some

[076-136]companies of infantry that are not twelve leagues off, in which I shall

[076-137]enlist, and there will be no want of baggage trains to travel with

[076-138]after that to the place of embarkation, which they say will be

[076-139]Carthagena; I would rather have the King for a master, and serve him in

[076-140]the wars, than serve a court pauper.”

[076-141]“And did you get any bounty, now?” asked the cousin.

[076-142]“If I had been in the service of some grandee of Spain or personage of

[076-143]distinction,” replied the youth, “I should have been safe to get it;

[076-144]for that is the advantage of serving good masters, that out of the

[076-145]servants’ hall men come to be ancients or captains, or get a good

[076-146]pension. But I, to my misfortune, always served place-hunters and

[076-147]adventurers, whose keep and wages were so miserable and scanty that

[076-148]half went in paying for the starching of one’s collars; it would be a

[076-149]miracle indeed if a page volunteer ever got anything like a reasonable

[076-150]bounty.”

[076-151]“And tell me, for heaven’s sake,” asked Don Quixote, “is it possible,

[076-152]my friend, that all the time you served you never got any livery?”

[076-153]“They gave me two,” replied the page; “but just as when one quits a

[076-154]religious community before making profession, they strip him of the

[076-155]dress of the order and give him back his own clothes, so did my masters

[076-156]return me mine; for as soon as the business on which they came to court

[076-157]was finished, they went home and took back the liveries they had given

[076-158]merely for show.”

[076-159]“What spilorceria!—as an Italian would say,” said Don Quixote; “but for

[076-160]all that, consider yourself happy in having left court with as worthy

[076-161]an object as you have, for there is nothing on earth more honourable or

[076-162]profitable than serving, first of all God, and then one’s king and

[076-163]natural lord, particularly in the profession of arms, by which, if not

[076-164]more wealth, at least more honour is to be won than by letters, as I

[076-165]have said many a time; for though letters may have founded more great

[076-166]houses than arms, still those founded by arms have I know not what

[076-167]superiority over those founded by letters, and a certain splendour

[076-168]belonging to them that distinguishes them above all. And bear in mind

[076-169]what I am now about to say to you, for it will be of great use and

[076-170]comfort to you in time of trouble; it is, not to let your mind dwell on

[076-171]the adverse chances that may befall you; for the worst of all is death,

[076-172]and if it be a good death, the best of all is to die. They asked Julius

[076-173]Cæsar, the valiant Roman emperor, what was the best death. He answered,

[076-174]that which is unexpected, which comes suddenly and unforeseen; and

[076-175]though he answered like a pagan, and one without the knowledge of the

[076-176]true God, yet, as far as sparing our feelings is concerned, he was

[076-177]right; for suppose you are killed in the first engagement or skirmish,

[076-178]whether by a cannon ball or blown up by mine, what matters it? It is

[076-179]only dying, and all is over; and according to Terence, a soldier shows

[076-180]better dead in battle, than alive and safe in flight; and the good

[076-181]soldier wins fame in proportion as he is obedient to his captains and

[076-182]those in command over him. And remember, my son, that it is better for

[076-183]the soldier to smell of gunpowder than of civet, and that if old age

[076-184]should come upon you in this honourable calling, though you may be

[076-185]covered with wounds and crippled and lame, it will not come upon you

[076-186]without honour, and that such as poverty cannot lessen; especially now

[076-187]that provisions are being made for supporting and relieving old and

[076-188]disabled soldiers; for it is not right to deal with them after the

[076-189]fashion of those who set free and get rid of their black slaves when

[076-190]they are old and useless, and, turning them out of their houses under

[076-191]the pretence of making them free, make them slaves to hunger, from

[076-192]which they cannot expect to be released except by death. But for the

[076-193]present I won’t say more than get ye up behind me on my horse as far as

[076-194]the inn, and sup with me there, and to-morrow you shall pursue your

[076-195]journey, and God give you as good speed as your intentions deserve.”

[076-196]The page did not accept the invitation to mount, though he did that to

[076-197]supper at the inn; and here they say Sancho said to himself, “God be

[076-198]with you for a master; is it possible that a man who can say things so

[076-199]many and so good as he has said just now, can say that he saw the

[076-200]impossible absurdities he reports about the cave of Montesinos? Well,

[076-201]well, we shall see.”

[076-202]And now, just as night was falling, they reached the inn, and it was

[076-203]not without satisfaction that Sancho perceived his master took it for a

[076-204]real inn, and not for a castle as usual. The instant they entered Don

[076-205]Quixote asked the landlord after the man with the lances and halberds,

[076-206]and was told that he was in the stable seeing to his mule; which was

[076-207]what Sancho and the cousin proceeded to do for their beasts, giving the

[076-208]best manger and the best place in the stable to Rocinante.
