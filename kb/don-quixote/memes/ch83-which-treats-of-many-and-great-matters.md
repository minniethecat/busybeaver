WHICH TREATS OF MANY AND GREAT MATTERS

p31a.jpg (155K)


[083-001]Supreme was the satisfaction that Sancho felt at seeing himself, as it

[083-002]seemed, an established favourite with the duchess, for he looked

[083-003]forward to finding in her castle what he had found in Don Diego’s house

[083-004]and in Basilio’s; he was always fond of good living, and always seized

[083-005]by the forelock any opportunity of feasting himself whenever it

[083-006]presented itself. The history informs us, then, that before they

[083-007]reached the country house or castle, the duke went on in advance and

[083-008]instructed all his servants how they were to treat Don Quixote; and so

[083-009]the instant he came up to the castle gates with the duchess, two

[083-010]lackeys or equerries, clad in what they call morning gowns of fine

[083-011]crimson satin reaching to their feet, hastened out, and catching Don

[083-012]Quixote in their arms before he saw or heard them, said to him, “Your

[083-013]highness should go and take my lady the duchess off her horse.”

p31b.jpg (334K)


[083-014]Don Quixote obeyed, and great bandying of compliments followed between

[083-015]the two over the matter; but in the end the duchess’s determination

[083-016]carried the day, and she refused to get down or dismount from her

[083-017]palfrey except in the arms of the duke, saying she did not consider

[083-018]herself worthy to impose so unnecessary a burden on so great a knight.

[083-019]At length the duke came out to take her down, and as they entered a

[083-020]spacious court two fair damsels came forward and threw over Don

[083-021]Quixote’s shoulders a large mantle of the finest scarlet cloth, and at

[083-022]the same instant all the galleries of the court were lined with the

[083-023]men-servants and women-servants of the household, crying, “Welcome,

[083-024]flower and cream of knight-errantry!” while all or most of them flung

[083-025]pellets filled with scented water over Don Quixote and the duke and

[083-026]duchess; at all which Don Quixote was greatly astonished, and this was

[083-027]the first time that he thoroughly felt and believed himself to be a

[083-028]knight-errant in reality and not merely in fancy, now that he saw

[083-029]himself treated in the same way as he had read of such knights being

[083-030]treated in days of yore.

[083-031]Sancho, deserting Dapple, hung on to the duchess and entered the

[083-032]castle, but feeling some twinges of conscience at having left the ass

[083-033]alone, he approached a respectable duenna who had come out with the

[083-034]rest to receive the duchess, and in a low voice he said to her, “Señora

[083-035]Gonzalez, or however your grace may be called—”

[083-036]“I am called Doña Rodriguez de Grijalba,” replied the duenna; “what is

[083-037]your will, brother?” To which Sancho made answer, “I should be glad if

[083-038]your worship would do me the favour to go out to the castle gate, where

[083-039]you will find a grey ass of mine; make them, if you please, put him in

[083-040]the stable, or put him there yourself, for the poor little beast is

[083-041]rather easily frightened, and cannot bear being alone at all.”

[083-042]“If the master is as wise as the man,” said the duenna, “we have got a

[083-043]fine bargain. Be off with you, brother, and bad luck to you and him who

[083-044]brought you here; go, look after your ass, for we, the duennas of this

[083-045]house, are not used to work of that sort.”

[083-046]“Well then, in troth,” returned Sancho, “I have heard my master, who is

[083-047]the very treasure-finder of stories, telling the story of Lancelot when

[083-048]he came from Britain, say that ladies waited upon him and duennas upon

[083-049]his hack; and, if it comes to my ass, I wouldn’t change him for Señor

[083-050]Lancelot’s hack.”

[083-051]“If you are a jester, brother,” said the duenna, “keep your drolleries

[083-052]for some place where they’ll pass muster and be paid for; for you’ll

[083-053]get nothing from me but a fig.”

[083-054]“At any rate, it will be a very ripe one,” said Sancho, “for you won’t

[083-055]lose the trick in years by a point too little.”

[083-056]“Son of a bitch,” said the duenna, all aglow with anger, “whether I’m

[083-057]old or not, it’s with God I have to reckon, not with you, you

[083-058]garlic-stuffed scoundrel!” and she said it so loud, that the duchess

[083-059]heard it, and turning round and seeing the duenna in such a state of

[083-060]excitement, and her eyes flaming so, asked whom she was wrangling with.

[083-061]“With this good fellow here,” said the duenna, “who has particularly

[083-062]requested me to go and put an ass of his that is at the castle gate

[083-063]into the stable, holding it up to me as an example that they did the

[083-064]same I don’t know where—that some ladies waited on one Lancelot, and

[083-065]duennas on his hack; and what is more, to wind up with, he called me

[083-066]old.”

[083-067]“That,” said the duchess, “I should have considered the greatest

[083-068]affront that could be offered me;” and addressing Sancho, she said to

[083-069]him, “You must know, friend Sancho, that Doña Rodriguez is very

[083-070]youthful, and that she wears that hood more for authority and custom’s

[083-071]sake than because of her years.”

[083-072]“May all the rest of mine be unlucky,” said Sancho, “if I meant it that

[083-073]way; I only spoke because the affection I have for my ass is so great,

[083-074]and I thought I could not commend him to a more kind-hearted person

[083-075]than the lady Doña Rodriguez.”

[083-076]Don Quixote, who was listening, said to him, “Is this proper

[083-077]conversation for the place, Sancho?”

[083-078]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “every one must mention what he wants wherever

[083-079]he may be; I thought of Dapple here, and I spoke of him here; if I had

[083-080]thought of him in the stable I would have spoken there.”

[083-081]On which the duke observed, “Sancho is quite right, and there is no

[083-082]reason at all to find fault with him; Dapple shall be fed to his

[083-083]heart’s content, and Sancho may rest easy, for he shall be treated like

[083-084]himself.”

[083-085]While this conversation, amusing to all except Don Quixote, was

[083-086]proceeding, they ascended the staircase and ushered Don Quixote into a

[083-087]chamber hung with rich cloth of gold and brocade; six damsels relieved

[083-088]him of his armour and waited on him like pages, all of them prepared

[083-089]and instructed by the duke and duchess as to what they were to do, and

[083-090]how they were to treat Don Quixote, so that he might see and believe

[083-091]they were treating him like a knight-errant. When his armour was

[083-092]removed, there stood Don Quixote in his tight-fitting breeches and

[083-093]chamois doublet, lean, lanky, and long, with cheeks that seemed to be

[083-094]kissing each other inside; such a figure, that if the damsels waiting

[083-095]on him had not taken care to check their merriment (which was one of

[083-096]the particular directions their master and mistress had given them),

[083-097]they would have burst with laughter. They asked him to let himself be

[083-098]stripped that they might put a shirt on him, but he would not on any

[083-099]account, saying that modesty became knights-errant just as much as

[083-100]valour. However, he said they might give the shirt to Sancho; and

[083-101]shutting himself in with him in a room where there was a sumptuous bed,

[083-102]he undressed and put on the shirt; and then, finding himself alone with

[083-103]Sancho, he said to him, “Tell me, thou new-fledged buffoon and old

[083-104]booby, dost thou think it right to offend and insult a duenna so

[083-105]deserving of reverence and respect as that one just now? Was that a

[083-106]time to bethink thee of thy Dapple, or are these noble personages

[083-107]likely to let the beasts fare badly when they treat their owners in

[083-108]such elegant style? For God’s sake, Sancho, restrain thyself, and don’t

[083-109]show the thread so as to let them see what a coarse, boorish texture

[083-110]thou art of. Remember, sinner that thou art, the master is the more

[083-111]esteemed the more respectable and well-bred his servants are; and that

[083-112]one of the greatest advantages that princes have over other men is that

[083-113]they have servants as good as themselves to wait on them. Dost thou not

[083-114]see—shortsighted being that thou art, and unlucky mortal that I

[083-115]am!—that if they perceive thee to be a coarse clown or a dull

[083-116]blockhead, they will suspect me to be some impostor or swindler? Nay,

[083-117]nay, Sancho friend, keep clear, oh, keep clear of these

[083-118]stumbling-blocks; for he who falls into the way of being a chatterbox

[083-119]and droll, drops into a wretched buffoon the first time he trips;

[083-120]bridle thy tongue, consider and weigh thy words before they escape thy

[083-121]mouth, and bear in mind we are now in quarters whence, by God’s help,

[083-122]and the strength of my arm, we shall come forth mightily advanced in

[083-123]fame and fortune.”

[083-124]Sancho promised him with much earnestness to keep his mouth shut, and

[083-125]to bite off his tongue before he uttered a word that was not altogether

[083-126]to the purpose and well considered, and told him he might make his mind

[083-127]easy on that point, for it should never be discovered through him what

[083-128]they were.

[083-129]Don Quixote dressed himself, put on his baldric with his sword, threw

[083-130]the scarlet mantle over his shoulders, placed on his head a montera of

[083-131]green satin that the damsels had given him, and thus arrayed passed out

[083-132]into the large room, where he found the damsels drawn up in double

[083-133]file, the same number on each side, all with the appliances for washing

[083-134]the hands, which they presented to him with profuse obeisances and

[083-135]ceremonies. Then came twelve pages, together with the seneschal, to

[083-136]lead him to dinner, as his hosts were already waiting for him. They

[083-137]placed him in the midst of them, and with much pomp and stateliness

[083-138]they conducted him into another room, where there was a sumptuous table

[083-139]laid with but four covers. The duchess and the duke came out to the

[083-140]door of the room to receive him, and with them a grave ecclesiastic,

[083-141]one of those who rule noblemen’s houses; one of those who, not being

[083-142]born magnates themselves, never know how to teach those who are how to

[083-143]behave as such; one of those who would have the greatness of great folk

[083-144]measured by their own narrowness of mind; one of those who, when they

[083-145]try to introduce economy into the household they rule, lead it into

[083-146]meanness. One of this sort, I say, must have been the grave churchman

[083-147]who came out with the duke and duchess to receive Don Quixote.

[083-148]A vast number of polite speeches were exchanged, and at length, taking

[083-149]Don Quixote between them, they proceeded to sit down to table. The duke

[083-150]pressed Don Quixote to take the head of the table, and, though he

[083-151]refused, the entreaties of the duke were so urgent that he had to

[083-152]accept it.

[083-153]The ecclesiastic took his seat opposite to him, and the duke and

[083-154]duchess those at the sides. All this time Sancho stood by, gaping with

[083-155]amazement at the honour he saw shown to his master by these illustrious

[083-156]persons; and observing all the ceremonious pressing that had passed

[083-157]between the duke and Don Quixote to induce him to take his seat at the

[083-158]head of the table, he said, “If your worship will give me leave I will

[083-159]tell you a story of what happened in my village about this matter of

[083-160]seats.”

[083-161]The moment Sancho said this Don Quixote trembled, making sure that he

[083-162]was about to say something foolish. Sancho glanced at him, and guessing

[083-163]his thoughts, said, “Don’t be afraid of my going astray, señor, or

[083-164]saying anything that won’t be pat to the purpose; I haven’t forgotten

[083-165]the advice your worship gave me just now about talking much or little,

[083-166]well or ill.”

[083-167]“I have no recollection of anything, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “say

[083-168]what thou wilt, only say it quickly.”

[083-169]“Well then,” said Sancho, “what I am going to say is so true that my

[083-170]master Don Quixote, who is here present, will keep me from lying.”

[083-171]“Lie as much as thou wilt for all I care, Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[083-172]“for I am not going to stop thee, but consider what thou art going to

[083-173]say.”

[083-174]“I have so considered and reconsidered,” said Sancho, “that the

[083-175]bell-ringer’s in a safe berth; as will be seen by what follows.”

[083-176]“It would be well,” said Don Quixote, “if your highnesses would order

[083-177]them to turn out this idiot, for he will talk a heap of nonsense.”

[083-178]“By the life of the duke, Sancho shall not be taken away from me for a

[083-179]moment,” said the duchess; “I am very fond of him, for I know he is

[083-180]very discreet.”

[083-181]“Discreet be the days of your holiness,” said Sancho, “for the good

[083-182]opinion you have of my wit, though there’s none in me; but the story I

[083-183]want to tell is this. There was an invitation given by a gentleman of

[083-184]my town, a very rich one, and one of quality, for he was one of the

[083-185]Alamos of Medina del Campo, and married to Doña Mencia de Quiñones, the

[083-186]daughter of Don Alonso de Marañon, Knight of the Order of Santiago,

[083-187]that was drowned at the Herradura—him there was that quarrel about

[083-188]years ago in our village, that my master Don Quixote was mixed up in,

[083-189]to the best of my belief, that Tomasillo the scapegrace, the son of

[083-190]Balbastro the smith, was wounded in.—Isn’t all this true, master mine?

[083-191]As you live, say so, that these gentlefolk may not take me for some

[083-192]lying chatterer.”

[083-193]“So far,” said the ecclesiastic, “I take you to be more a chatterer

[083-194]than a liar; but I don’t know what I shall take you for by-and-by.”

[083-195]“Thou citest so many witnesses and proofs, Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[083-196]“that I have no choice but to say thou must be telling the truth; go

[083-197]on, and cut the story short, for thou art taking the way not to make an

[083-198]end for two days to come.”

[083-199]“He is not to cut it short,” said the duchess; “on the contrary, for my

[083-200]gratification, he is to tell it as he knows it, though he should not

[083-201]finish it these six days; and if he took so many they would be to me

[083-202]the pleasantest I ever spent.”

[083-203]“Well then, sirs, I say,” continued Sancho, “that this same gentleman,

[083-204]whom I know as well as I do my own hands, for it’s not a bowshot from

[083-205]my house to his, invited a poor but respectable labourer—”

[083-206]“Get on, brother,” said the churchman; “at the rate you are going you

[083-207]will not stop with your story short of the next world.”

[083-208]“I’ll stop less than half-way, please God,” said Sancho; “and so I say

[083-209]this labourer, coming to the house of the gentleman I spoke of that

[083-210]invited him—rest his soul, he is now dead; and more by token he died

[083-211]the death of an angel, so they say; for I was not there, for just at

[083-212]that time I had gone to reap at Tembleque—”

[083-213]“As you live, my son,” said the churchman, “make haste back from

[083-214]Tembleque, and finish your story without burying the gentleman, unless

[083-215]you want to make more funerals.”

[083-216]“Well then, it so happened,” said Sancho, “that as the pair of them

[083-217]were going to sit down to table—and I think I can see them now plainer

[083-218]than ever—”

[083-219]Great was the enjoyment the duke and duchess derived from the

[083-220]irritation the worthy churchman showed at the long-winded, halting way

[083-221]Sancho had of telling his story, while Don Quixote was chafing with

[083-222]rage and vexation.

[083-223]“So, as I was saying,” continued Sancho, “as the pair of them were

[083-224]going to sit down to table, as I said, the labourer insisted upon the

[083-225]gentleman’s taking the head of the table, and the gentleman insisted

[083-226]upon the labourer’s taking it, as his orders should be obeyed in his

[083-227]house; but the labourer, who plumed himself on his politeness and good

[083-228]breeding, would not on any account, until the gentleman, out of

[083-229]patience, putting his hands on his shoulders, compelled him by force to

[083-230]sit down, saying, ‘Sit down, you stupid lout, for wherever I sit will

[083-231]be the head to you; and that’s the story, and, troth, I think it hasn’t

[083-232]been brought in amiss here.”

[083-233]Don Quixote turned all colours, which, on his sunburnt face, mottled it

[083-234]till it looked like jasper. The duke and duchess suppressed their

[083-235]laughter so as not altogether to mortify Don Quixote, for they saw

[083-236]through Sancho’s impertinence; and to change the conversation, and keep

[083-237]Sancho from uttering more absurdities, the duchess asked Don Quixote

[083-238]what news he had of the lady Dulcinea, and if he had sent her any

[083-239]presents of giants or miscreants lately, for he could not but have

[083-240]vanquished a good many.

[083-241]To which Don Quixote replied, “Señora, my misfortunes, though they had

[083-242]a beginning, will never have an end. I have vanquished giants and I

[083-243]have sent her caitiffs and miscreants; but where are they to find her

[083-244]if she is enchanted and turned into the most ill-favoured peasant wench

[083-245]that can be imagined?”

[083-246]“I don’t know,” said Sancho Panza; “to me she seems the fairest

[083-247]creature in the world; at any rate, in nimbleness and jumping she won’t

[083-248]give in to a tumbler; by my faith, señora duchess, she leaps from the

[083-249]ground on to the back of an ass like a cat.”

[083-250]“Have you seen her enchanted, Sancho?” asked the duke.

[083-251]“What, seen her!” said Sancho; “why, who the devil was it but myself

[083-252]that first thought of the enchantment business? She is as much

[083-253]enchanted as my father.”

[083-254]The ecclesiastic, when he heard them talking of giants and caitiffs and

[083-255]enchantments, began to suspect that this must be Don Quixote of La

[083-256]Mancha, whose story the duke was always reading; and he had himself

[083-257]often reproved him for it, telling him it was foolish to read such

[083-258]fooleries; and becoming convinced that his suspicion was correct,

[083-259]addressing the duke, he said very angrily to him, “Señor, your

[083-260]excellence will have to give account to God for what this good man

[083-261]does. This Don Quixote, or Don Simpleton, or whatever his name is,

[083-262]cannot, I imagine, be such a blockhead as your excellence would have

[083-263]him, holding out encouragement to him to go on with his vagaries and

[083-264]follies.” Then turning to address Don Quixote he said, “And you,

[083-265]num-skull, who put it into your head that you are a knight-errant, and

[083-266]vanquish giants and capture miscreants? Go your ways in a good hour,

[083-267]and in a good hour be it said to you. Go home and bring up your

[083-268]children if you have any, and attend to your business, and give over

[083-269]going wandering about the world, gaping and making a laughing-stock of

[083-270]yourself to all who know you and all who don’t. Where, in heaven’s

[083-271]name, have you discovered that there are or ever were knights-errant?

[083-272]Where are there giants in Spain or miscreants in La Mancha, or

[083-273]enchanted Dulcineas, or all the rest of the silly things they tell

[083-274]about you?”

[083-275]Don Quixote listened attentively to the reverend gentleman’s words, and

[083-276]as soon as he perceived he had done speaking, regardless of the

[083-277]presence of the duke and duchess, he sprang to his feet with angry

[083-278]looks and an agitated countenance, and said—But the reply deserves a

[083-279]chapter to itself.
