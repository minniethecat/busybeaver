[045-001]IN WHICH THE DOUBTFUL QUESTION OF MAMBRINO’S HELMET AND THE PACK-SADDLE

[045-002]IS FINALLY SETTLED, WITH OTHER ADVENTURES THAT OCCURRED IN TRUTH AND

EARNEST

c45a.jpg (154K)


[045-003]“What do you think now, gentlemen,” said the barber, “of what these

[045-004]gentles say, when they want to make out that this is a helmet?”

[045-005]“And whoever says the contrary,” said Don Quixote, “I will let him know

[045-006]he lies if he is a knight, and if he is a squire that he lies again a

[045-007]thousand times.”

[045-008]Our own barber, who was present at all this, and understood Don

[045-009]Quixote’s humour so thoroughly, took it into his head to back up his

[045-010]delusion and carry on the joke for the general amusement; so addressing

[045-011]the other barber he said:

[045-012]“Señor barber, or whatever you are, you must know that I belong to your

[045-013]profession too, and have had a licence to practise for more than twenty

[045-014]years, and I know the implements of the barber craft, every one of

[045-015]them, perfectly well; and I was likewise a soldier for some time in the

[045-016]days of my youth, and I know also what a helmet is, and a morion, and a

[045-017]headpiece with a visor, and other things pertaining to soldiering, I

[045-018]meant to say to soldiers’ arms; and I say—saving better opinions and

[045-019]always with submission to sounder judgments—that this piece we have now

[045-020]before us, which this worthy gentleman has in his hands, not only is no

[045-021]barber’s basin, but is as far from being one as white is from black,

[045-022]and truth from falsehood; I say, moreover, that this, although it is a

[045-023]helmet, is not a complete helmet.”

[045-024]“Certainly not,” said Don Quixote, “for half of it is wanting, that is

[045-025]to say the beaver.”

[045-026]“It is quite true,” said the curate, who saw the object of his friend

[045-027]the barber; and Cardenio, Don Fernando and his companions agreed with

[045-028]him, and even the Judge, if his thoughts had not been so full of Don

[045-029]Luis’s affair, would have helped to carry on the joke; but he was so

[045-030]taken up with the serious matters he had on his mind that he paid

[045-031]little or no attention to these facetious proceedings.

[045-032]“God bless me!” exclaimed their butt the barber at this; “is it

[045-033]possible that such an honourable company can say that this is not a

[045-034]basin but a helmet? Why, this is a thing that would astonish a whole

[045-035]university, however wise it might be! That will do; if this basin is a

[045-036]helmet, why, then the pack-saddle must be a horse’s caparison, as this

[045-037]gentleman has said.”

[045-038]“To me it looks like a pack-saddle,” said Don Quixote; “but I have

[045-039]already said that with that question I do not concern myself.”

[045-040]“As to whether it be pack-saddle or caparison,” said the curate, “it is

[045-041]only for Señor Don Quixote to say; for in these matters of chivalry all

[045-042]these gentlemen and I bow to his authority.”

[045-043]“By God, gentlemen,” said Don Quixote, “so many strange things have

[045-044]happened to me in this castle on the two occasions on which I have

[045-045]sojourned in it, that I will not venture to assert anything positively

[045-046]in reply to any question touching anything it contains; for it is my

[045-047]belief that everything that goes on within it goes by enchantment. The

[045-048]first time, an enchanted Moor that there is in it gave me sore trouble,

[045-049]nor did Sancho fare well among certain followers of his; and last night

[045-050]I was kept hanging by this arm for nearly two hours, without knowing

[045-051]how or why I came by such a mishap. So that now, for me to come forward

[045-052]to give an opinion in such a puzzling matter, would be to risk a rash

[045-053]decision. As regards the assertion that this is a basin and not a

[045-054]helmet I have already given an answer; but as to the question whether

[045-055]this is a pack-saddle or a caparison I will not venture to give a

[045-056]positive opinion, but will leave it to your worships’ better judgment.

[045-057]Perhaps as you are not dubbed knights like myself, the enchantments of

[045-058]this place have nothing to do with you, and your faculties are

[045-059]unfettered, and you can see things in this castle as they really and

[045-060]truly are, and not as they appear to me.”

[045-061]“There can be no question,” said Don Fernando on this, “but that Señor

[045-062]Don Quixote has spoken very wisely, and that with us rests the decision

[045-063]of this matter; and that we may have surer ground to go on, I will take

[045-064]the votes of the gentlemen in secret, and declare the result clearly

[045-065]and fully.”

[045-066]To those who were in on the secret of Don Quixote’s humour all this

[045-067]afforded great amusement; but to those who knew nothing about it, it

[045-068]seemed the greatest nonsense in the world, in particular to the four

[045-069]servants of Don Luis, as well as to Don Luis himself, and to three

[045-070]other travellers who had by chance come to the inn, and had the

[045-071]appearance of officers of the Holy Brotherhood, as indeed they were;

[045-072]but the one who above all was at his wits’ end was the barber whose

[045-073]basin, there before his very eyes, had been turned into Mambrino’s

[045-074]helmet, and whose pack-saddle he had no doubt whatever was about to

[045-075]become a rich caparison for a horse. All laughed to see Don Fernando

[045-076]going from one to another collecting the votes, and whispering to them

[045-077]to give him their private opinion whether the treasure over which there

[045-078]had been so much fighting was a pack-saddle or a caparison; but after

[045-079]he had taken the votes of those who knew Don Quixote, he said aloud,

[045-080]“The fact is, my good fellow, that I am tired collecting such a number

[045-081]of opinions, for I find that there is not one of whom I ask what I

[045-082]desire to know, who does not tell me that it is absurd to say that this

[045-083]is the pack-saddle of an ass, and not the caparison of a horse, nay, of

[045-084]a thoroughbred horse; so you must submit, for, in spite of you and your

[045-085]ass, this is a caparison and no pack-saddle, and you have stated and

[045-086]proved your case very badly.”

[045-087]“May I never share heaven,” said the poor barber, “if your worships are

[045-088]not all mistaken; and may my soul appear before God as that appears to

[045-089]me a pack-saddle and not a caparison; but, ‘laws go,’—I say no more;

[045-090]and indeed I am not drunk, for I am fasting, except it be from sin.”

[045-091]The simple talk of the barber did not afford less amusement than the

[045-092]absurdities of Don Quixote, who now observed:

[045-093]“There is no more to be done now than for each to take what belongs to

[045-094]him, and to whom God has given it, may St. Peter add his blessing.”

[045-095]But said one of the four servants, “Unless, indeed, this is a

[045-096]deliberate joke, I cannot bring myself to believe that men so

[045-097]intelligent as those present are, or seem to be, can venture to declare

[045-098]and assert that this is not a basin, and that not a pack-saddle; but as

[045-099]I perceive that they do assert and declare it, I can only come to the

[045-100]conclusion that there is some mystery in this persistence in what is so

[045-101]opposed to the evidence of experience and truth itself; for I swear

[045-102]by”—and here he rapped out a round oath—“all the people in the world

[045-103]will not make me believe that this is not a barber’s basin and that a

[045-104]jackass’s pack-saddle.”

[045-105]“It might easily be a she-ass’s,” observed the curate.

[045-106]“It is all the same,” said the servant; “that is not the point; but

[045-107]whether it is or is not a pack-saddle, as your worships say.”

[045-108]On hearing this one of the newly arrived officers of the Brotherhood,

[045-109]who had been listening to the dispute and controversy, unable to

[045-110]restrain his anger and impatience, exclaimed, “It is a pack-saddle as

[045-111]sure as my father is my father, and whoever has said or will say

[045-112]anything else must be drunk.”

[045-113]“You lie like a rascally clown,” returned Don Quixote; and lifting his

[045-114]pike, which he had never let out of his hand, he delivered such a blow

[045-115]at his head that, had not the officer dodged it, it would have

[045-116]stretched him at full length. The pike was shivered in pieces against

[045-117]the ground, and the rest of the officers, seeing their comrade

[045-118]assaulted, raised a shout, calling for help for the Holy Brotherhood.

[045-119]The landlord, who was of the fraternity, ran at once to fetch his staff

[045-120]of office and his sword, and ranged himself on the side of his

[045-121]comrades; the servants of Don Luis clustered round him, lest he should

[045-122]escape from them in the confusion; the barber, seeing the house turned

[045-123]upside down, once more laid hold of his pack-saddle and Sancho did the

[045-124]same; Don Quixote drew his sword and charged the officers; Don Luis

[045-125]cried out to his servants to leave him alone and go and help Don

[045-126]Quixote, and Cardenio and Don Fernando, who were supporting him; the

[045-127]curate was shouting at the top of his voice, the landlady was

[045-128]screaming, her daughter was wailing, Maritornes was weeping, Dorothea

[045-129]was aghast, Luscinda terror-stricken, and Doña Clara in a faint. The

[045-130]barber cudgelled Sancho, and Sancho pommelled the barber; Don Luis gave

[045-131]one of his servants, who ventured to catch him by the arm to keep him

[045-132]from escaping, a cuff that bathed his teeth in blood; the Judge took

[045-133]his part; Don Fernando had got one of the officers down and was

[045-134]belabouring him heartily; the landlord raised his voice again calling

[045-135]for help for the Holy Brotherhood; so that the whole inn was nothing

[045-136]but cries, shouts, shrieks, confusion, terror, dismay, mishaps,

[045-137]sword-cuts, fisticuffs, cudgellings, kicks, and bloodshed; and in the

[045-138]midst of all this chaos, complication, and general entanglement, Don

[045-139]Quixote took it into his head that he had been plunged into the thick

[045-140]of the discord of Agramante’s camp; and, in a voice that shook the inn

[045-141]like thunder, he cried out:

[045-142]“Hold all, let all sheathe their swords, let all be calm and attend to

[045-143]me as they value their lives!”

[045-144]All paused at his mighty voice, and he went on to say, “Did I not tell

[045-145]you, sirs, that this castle was enchanted, and that a legion or so of

[045-146]devils dwelt in it? In proof whereof I call upon you to behold with

[045-147]your own eyes how the discord of Agramante’s camp has come hither, and

[045-148]been transferred into the midst of us. See how they fight, there for

[045-149]the sword, here for the horse, on that side for the eagle, on this for

[045-150]the helmet; we are all fighting, and all at cross purposes. Come then,

[045-151]you, Señor Judge, and you, señor curate; let the one represent King

[045-152]Agramante and the other King Sobrino, and make peace among us; for by

[045-153]God Almighty it is a sorry business that so many persons of quality as

[045-154]we are should slay one another for such trifling cause.” The officers,

[045-155]who did not understand Don Quixote’s mode of speaking, and found

[045-156]themselves roughly handled by Don Fernando, Cardenio, and their

[045-157]companions, were not to be appeased; the barber was, however, for both

[045-158]his beard and his pack-saddle were the worse for the struggle; Sancho

[045-159]like a good servant obeyed the slightest word of his master; while the

[045-160]four servants of Don Luis kept quiet when they saw how little they

[045-161]gained by not being so. The landlord alone insisted upon it that they

[045-162]must punish the insolence of this madman, who at every turn raised a

[045-163]disturbance in the inn; but at length the uproar was stilled for the

[045-164]present; the pack-saddle remained a caparison till the day of judgment,

[045-165]and the basin a helmet and the inn a castle in Don Quixote’s

[045-166]imagination.

[045-167]All having been now pacified and made friends by the persuasion of the

[045-168]Judge and the curate, the servants of Don Luis began again to urge him

[045-169]to return with them at once; and while he was discussing the matter

[045-170]with them, the Judge took counsel with Don Fernando, Cardenio, and the

[045-171]curate as to what he ought to do in the case, telling them how it

[045-172]stood, and what Don Luis had said to him. It was agreed at length that

[045-173]Don Fernando should tell the servants of Don Luis who he was, and that

[045-174]it was his desire that Don Luis should accompany him to Andalusia,

[045-175]where he would receive from the marquis his brother the welcome his

[045-176]quality entitled him to; for, otherwise, it was easy to see from the

[045-177]determination of Don Luis that he would not return to his father at

[045-178]present, though they tore him to pieces. On learning the rank of Don

[045-179]Fernando and the resolution of Don Luis the four then settled it

[045-180]between themselves that three of them should return to tell his father

[045-181]how matters stood, and that the other should remain to wait upon Don

[045-182]Luis, and not leave him until they came back for him, or his father’s

[045-183]orders were known. Thus by the authority of Agramante and the wisdom of

[045-184]King Sobrino all this complication of disputes was arranged; but the

[045-185]enemy of concord and hater of peace, feeling himself slighted and made

[045-186]a fool of, and seeing how little he had gained after having involved

[045-187]them all in such an elaborate entanglement, resolved to try his hand

[045-188]once more by stirring up fresh quarrels and disturbances.

[045-189]It came about in this wise: the officers were pacified on learning the

[045-190]rank of those with whom they had been engaged, and withdrew from the

[045-191]contest, considering that whatever the result might be they were likely

[045-192]to get the worst of the battle; but one of them, the one who had been

[045-193]thrashed and kicked by Don Fernando, recollected that among some

[045-194]warrants he carried for the arrest of certain delinquents, he had one

[045-195]against Don Quixote, whom the Holy Brotherhood had ordered to be

[045-196]arrested for setting the galley slaves free, as Sancho had, with very

[045-197]good reason, apprehended. Suspecting how it was, then, he wished to

[045-198]satisfy himself as to whether Don Quixote’s features corresponded; and

[045-199]taking a parchment out of his bosom he lit upon what he was in search

[045-200]of, and setting himself to read it deliberately, for he was not a quick

[045-201]reader, as he made out each word he fixed his eyes on Don Quixote, and

[045-202]went on comparing the description in the warrant with his face, and

[045-203]discovered that beyond all doubt he was the person described in it. As

[045-204]soon as he had satisfied himself, folding up the parchment, he took the

[045-205]warrant in his left hand and with his right seized Don Quixote by the

[045-206]collar so tightly that he did not allow him to breathe, and shouted

[045-207]aloud, “Help for the Holy Brotherhood! and that you may see I demand it

[045-208]in earnest, read this warrant which says this highwayman is to be

[045-209]arrested.”

[045-210]The curate took the warrant and saw that what the officer said was

[045-211]true, and that it agreed with Don Quixote’s appearance, who, on his

[045-212]part, when he found himself roughly handled by this rascally clown,

[045-213]worked up to the highest pitch of wrath, and all his joints cracking

[045-214]with rage, with both hands seized the officer by the throat with all

[045-215]his might, so that had he not been helped by his comrades he would have

[045-216]yielded up his life ere Don Quixote released his hold. The landlord,

[045-217]who had perforce to support his brother officers, ran at once to aid

[045-218]them. The landlady, when she saw her husband engaged in a fresh

[045-219]quarrel, lifted up her voice afresh, and its note was immediately

[045-220]caught up by Maritornes and her daughter, calling upon heaven and all

[045-221]present for help; and Sancho, seeing what was going on, exclaimed, “By

[045-222]the Lord, it is quite true what my master says about the enchantments

[045-223]of this castle, for it is impossible to live an hour in peace in it!”

[045-224]Don Fernando parted the officer and Don Quixote, and to their mutual

[045-225]contentment made them relax the grip by which they held, the one the

[045-226]coat collar, the other the throat of his adversary; for all this,

[045-227]however, the officers did not cease to demand their prisoner and call

[045-228]on them to help, and deliver him over bound into their power, as was

[045-229]required for the service of the King and of the Holy Brotherhood, on

[045-230]whose behalf they again demanded aid and assistance to effect the

[045-231]capture of this robber and footpad of the highways.

[045-232]Don Quixote smiled when he heard these words, and said very calmly,

[045-233]“Come now, base, ill-born brood; call ye it highway robbery to give

[045-234]freedom to those in bondage, to release the captives, to succour the

[045-235]miserable, to raise up the fallen, to relieve the needy? Infamous

[045-236]beings, who by your vile grovelling intellects deserve that heaven

[045-237]should not make known to you the virtue that lies in knight-errantry,

[045-238]or show you the sin and ignorance in which ye lie when ye refuse to

[045-239]respect the shadow, not to say the presence, of any knight-errant! Come

[045-240]now; band, not of officers, but of thieves; footpads with the licence

[045-241]of the Holy Brotherhood; tell me who was the ignoramus who signed a

[045-242]warrant of arrest against such a knight as I am? Who was he that did

[045-243]not know that knights-errant are independent of all jurisdictions, that

[045-244]their law is their sword, their charter their prowess, and their edicts

[045-245]their will? Who, I say again, was the fool that knows not that there

[045-246]are no letters patent of nobility that confer such privileges or

[045-247]exemptions as a knight-errant acquires the day he is dubbed a knight,

[045-248]and devotes himself to the arduous calling of chivalry? What

[045-249]knight-errant ever paid poll-tax, duty, queen’s pin-money, king’s dues,

[045-250]toll or ferry? What tailor ever took payment of him for making his

[045-251]clothes? What castellan that received him in his castle ever made him

[045-252]pay his shot? What king did not seat him at his table? What damsel was

[045-253]not enamoured of him and did not yield herself up wholly to his will

[045-254]and pleasure? And, lastly, what knight-errant has there been, is there,

[045-255]or will there ever be in the world, not bold enough to give,

[045-256]single-handed, four hundred cudgellings to four hundred officers of the

[045-257]Holy Brotherhood if they come in his way?”
