OF MATTERS RELATING AND BELONGING TO THIS ADVENTURE AND TO THIS

MEMORABLE HISTORY

p40a.jpg (129K)


[092-001]Verily and truly all those who find pleasure in histories like this

[092-002]ought show their gratitude to Cide Hamete, its original author, for the

[092-003]scrupulous care he has taken to set before us all its minute

[092-004]particulars, not leaving anything, however trifling it may be, that he

[092-005]does not make clear and plain. He portrays the thoughts, he reveals the

[092-006]fancies, he answers implied questions, clears up doubts, sets

[092-007]objections at rest, and, in a word, makes plain the smallest points the

[092-008]most inquisitive can desire to know. O renowned author! O happy Don

[092-009]Quixote! O famous famous droll Sancho! All and each, may ye live

[092-010]countless ages for the delight and amusement of the dwellers on earth!

[092-011]The history goes on to say that when Sancho saw the Distressed One

[092-012]faint he exclaimed: “I swear by the faith of an honest man and the

[092-013]shades of all my ancestors the Panzas, that never I did see or hear of,

[092-014]nor has my master related or conceived in his mind, such an adventure

[092-015]as this. A thousand devils—not to curse thee—take thee, Malambruno, for

[092-016]an enchanter and a giant! Couldst thou find no other sort of punishment

[092-017]for these sinners but bearding them? Would it not have been better—it

[092-018]would have been better for them—to have taken off half their noses from

[092-019]the middle upwards, even though they’d have snuffled when they spoke,

[092-020]than to have put beards on them? I’ll bet they have not the means of

[092-021]paying anybody to shave them.”

[092-022]“That is the truth, señor,” said one of the twelve; “we have not the

[092-023]money to get ourselves shaved, and so we have, some of us, taken to

[092-024]using sticking-plasters by way of an economical remedy, for by applying

[092-025]them to our faces and plucking them off with a jerk we are left as bare

[092-026]and smooth as the bottom of a stone mortar. There are, to be sure,

[092-027]women in Kandy that go about from house to house to remove down, and

[092-028]trim eyebrows, and make cosmetics for the use of the women, but we, the

[092-029]duennas of my lady, would never let them in, for most of them have a

[092-030]flavour of agents that have ceased to be principals; and if we are not

[092-031]relieved by Señor Don Quixote we shall be carried to our graves with

[092-032]beards.”

[092-033]“I will pluck out my own in the land of the Moors,” said Don Quixote,

[092-034]“if I don’t cure yours.”

[092-035]At this instant the Trifaldi recovered from her swoon and said, “The

[092-036]chink of that promise, valiant knight, reached my ears in the midst of

[092-037]my swoon, and has been the means of reviving me and bringing back my

[092-038]senses; and so once more I implore you, illustrious errant, indomitable

[092-039]sir, to let your gracious promises be turned into deeds.”

[092-040]“There shall be no delay on my part,” said Don Quixote. “Bethink you,

[092-041]señora, of what I must do, for my heart is most eager to serve you.”

[092-042]“The fact is,” replied the Distressed One, “it is five thousand

[092-043]leagues, a couple more or less, from this to the kingdom of Kandy, if

[092-044]you go by land; but if you go through the air and in a straight line,

[092-045]it is three thousand two hundred and twenty-seven. You must know, too,

[092-046]that Malambruno told me that, whenever fate provided the knight our

[092-047]deliverer, he himself would send him a steed far better and with less

[092-048]tricks than a post-horse; for he will be that same wooden horse on

[092-049]which the valiant Pierres carried off the fair Magalona; which said

[092-050]horse is guided by a peg he has in his forehead that serves for a

[092-051]bridle, and flies through the air with such rapidity that you would

[092-052]fancy the very devils were carrying him. This horse, according to

[092-053]ancient tradition, was made by Merlin. He lent him to Pierres, who was

[092-054]a friend of his, and who made long journeys with him, and, as has been

[092-055]said, carried off the fair Magalona, bearing her through the air on its

[092-056]haunches and making all who beheld them from the earth gape with

[092-057]astonishment; and he never lent him save to those whom he loved or

[092-058]those who paid him well; and since the great Pierres we know of no one

[092-059]having mounted him until now. From him Malambruno stole him by his

[092-060]magic art, and he has him now in his possession, and makes use of him

[092-061]in his journeys which he constantly makes through different parts of

[092-062]the world; he is here to-day, to-morrow in France, and the next day in

[092-063]Potosi; and the best of it is the said horse neither eats nor sleeps

[092-064]nor wears out shoes, and goes at an ambling pace through the air

[092-065]without wings, so that he whom he has mounted upon him can carry a cup

[092-066]full of water in his hand without spilling a drop, so smoothly and

[092-067]easily does he go, for which reason the fair Magalona enjoyed riding

[092-068]him greatly.”

[092-069]“For going smoothly and easily,” said Sancho at this, “give me my

[092-070]Dapple, though he can’t go through the air; but on the ground I’ll back

[092-071]him against all the amblers in the world.”

[092-072]They all laughed, and the Distressed One continued: “And this same

[092-073]horse, if so be that Malambruno is disposed to put an end to our

[092-074]sufferings, will be here before us ere the night shall have advanced

[092-075]half an hour; for he announced to me that the sign he would give me

[092-076]whereby I might know that I had found the knight I was in quest of,

[092-077]would be to send me the horse wherever he might be, speedily and

[092-078]promptly.”

[092-079]“And how many is there room for on this horse?” asked Sancho.

[092-080]“Two,” said the Distressed One, “one in the saddle, and the other on

[092-081]the croup; and generally these two are knight and squire, when there is

[092-082]no damsel that’s being carried off.”

[092-083]“I’d like to know, Señora Distressed One,” said Sancho, “what is the

[092-084]name of this horse?”

[092-085]“His name,” said the Distressed One, “is not the same as Bellerophon’s

[092-086]horse that was called Pegasus, or Alexander the Great’s, called

[092-087]Bucephalus, or Orlando Furioso’s, the name of which was Brigliador, nor

[092-088]yet Bayard, the horse of Reinaldos of Montalvan, nor Frontino like

[092-089]Ruggiero’s, nor Bootes or Peritoa, as they say the horses of the sun

[092-090]were called, nor is he called Orelia, like the horse on which the

[092-091]unfortunate Rodrigo, the last king of the Goths, rode to the battle

[092-092]where he lost his life and his kingdom.”

[092-093]“I’ll bet,” said Sancho, “that as they have given him none of these

[092-094]famous names of well-known horses, no more have they given him the name

[092-095]of my master’s Rocinante, which for being apt surpasses all that have

[092-096]been mentioned.”

[092-097]“That is true,” said the bearded countess, “still it fits him very

[092-098]well, for he is called Clavileño the Swift, which name is in accordance

[092-099]with his being made of wood, with the peg he has in his forehead, and

[092-100]with the swift pace at which he travels; and so, as far as name goes,

[092-101]he may compare with the famous Rocinante.”

[092-102]“I have nothing to say against his name,” said Sancho; “but with what

[092-103]sort of bridle or halter is he managed?”

[092-104]“I have said already,” said the Trifaldi, “that it is with a peg, by

[092-105]turning which to one side or the other the knight who rides him makes

[092-106]him go as he pleases, either through the upper air, or skimming and

[092-107]almost sweeping the earth, or else in that middle course that is sought

[092-108]and followed in all well-regulated proceedings.”

[092-109]“I’d like to see him,” said Sancho; “but to fancy I’m going to mount

[092-110]him, either in the saddle or on the croup, is to ask pears of the elm

[092-111]tree. A good joke indeed! I can hardly keep my seat upon Dapple, and on

[092-112]a pack-saddle softer than silk itself, and here they’d have me hold on

[092-113]upon haunches of plank without pad or cushion of any sort! Gad, I have

[092-114]no notion of bruising myself to get rid of anyone’s beard; let each one

[092-115]shave himself as best he can; I’m not going to accompany my master on

[092-116]any such long journey; besides, I can’t give any help to the shaving of

[092-117]these beards as I can to the disenchantment of my lady Dulcinea.”

[092-118]“Yes, you can, my friend,” replied the Trifaldi; “and so much, that

[092-119]without you, so I understand, we shall be able to do nothing.”

[092-120]“In the king’s name!” exclaimed Sancho, “what have squires got to do

[092-121]with the adventures of their masters? Are they to have the fame of such

[092-122]as they go through, and we the labour? Body o’ me! if the historians

[092-123]would only say, ‘Such and such a knight finished such and such an

[092-124]adventure, but with the help of so and so, his squire, without which it

[092-125]would have been impossible for him to accomplish it;’ but they write

[092-126]curtly, “Don Paralipomenon of the Three Stars accomplished the

[092-127]adventure of the six monsters;’ without mentioning such a person as his

[092-128]squire, who was there all the time, just as if there was no such being.

[092-129]Once more, sirs, I say my master may go alone, and much good may it do

[092-130]him; and I’ll stay here in the company of my lady the duchess; and

[092-131]maybe when he comes back, he will find the lady Dulcinea’s affair ever

[092-132]so much advanced; for I mean in leisure hours, and at idle moments, to

[092-133]give myself a spell of whipping without so much as a hair to cover me.”

[092-134]“For all that you must go if it be necessary, my good Sancho,” said the

[092-135]duchess, “for they are worthy folk who ask you; and the faces of these

[092-136]ladies must not remain overgrown in this way because of your idle

[092-137]fears; that would be a hard case indeed.”

[092-138]“In the king’s name, once more!” said Sancho; “If this charitable work

[092-139]were to be done for the sake of damsels in confinement or

[092-140]charity-girls, a man might expose himself to some hardships; but to

[092-141]bear it for the sake of stripping beards off duennas! Devil take it!

[092-142]I’d sooner see them all bearded, from the highest to the lowest, and

[092-143]from the most prudish to the most affected.”

[092-144]“You are very hard on duennas, Sancho my friend,” said the duchess;

[092-145]“you incline very much to the opinion of the Toledo apothecary. But

[092-146]indeed you are wrong; there are duennas in my house that may serve as

[092-147]patterns of duennas; and here is my Doña Rodriguez, who will not allow

[092-148]me to say otherwise.”

[092-149]“Your excellence may say it if you like,” said the Rodriguez; “for God

[092-150]knows the truth of everything; and whether we duennas are good or bad,

[092-151]bearded or smooth, we are our mothers’ daughters like other women; and

[092-152]as God sent us into the world, he knows why he did, and on his mercy I

[092-153]rely, and not on anybody’s beard.”

[092-154]“Well, Señora Rodriguez, Señora Trifaldi, and present company,” said

[092-155]Don Quixote, “I trust in Heaven that it will look with kindly eyes upon

[092-156]your troubles, for Sancho will do as I bid him. Only let Clavileño come

[092-157]and let me find myself face to face with Malambruno, and I am certain

[092-158]no razor will shave you more easily than my sword shall shave

[092-159]Malambruno’s head off his shoulders; for ‘God bears with the wicked,

[092-160]but not for ever.’”

[092-161]“Ah!” exclaimed the Distressed One at this, “may all the stars of the

[092-162]celestial regions look down upon your greatness with benign eyes,

[092-163]valiant knight, and shed every prosperity and valour upon your heart,

[092-164]that it may be the shield and safeguard of the abused and downtrodden

[092-165]race of duennas, detested by apothecaries, sneered at by squires, and

[092-166]made game of by pages. Ill betide the jade that in the flower of her

[092-167]youth would not sooner become a nun than a duenna! Unfortunate beings

[092-168]that we are, we duennas! Though we may be descended in the direct male

[092-169]line from Hector of Troy himself, our mistresses never fail to address

[092-170]us as ‘you’ if they think it makes queens of them. O giant Malambruno,

[092-171]though thou art an enchanter, thou art true to thy promises. Send us

[092-172]now the peerless Clavileño, that our misfortune may be brought to an

[092-173]end; for if the hot weather sets in and these beards of ours are still

[092-174]there, alas for our lot!”

[092-175]The Trifaldi said this in such a pathetic way that she drew tears from

[092-176]the eyes of all and even Sancho’s filled up; and he resolved in his

[092-177]heart to accompany his master to the uttermost ends of the earth, if so

[092-178]be the removal of the wool from those venerable countenances depended

[092-179]upon it.
