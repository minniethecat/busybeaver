## The Choice of a Lady's Home

When Alonso Quixano reinvents himself as Don Quixote, he must choose a lady-love — a requirement of the chivalric code he has internalized. The choice falls on a farm-girl he was once "in love with" from a neighbouring village:

> Close by the village where he lived there was a very good-looking farm-girl with whom he had been at one time in love, though, so far as is known, she never knew or noticed it. Her name was Aldonza Lorenzo, and upon her he thought fit to confer the title of Lady of his Thoughts; and hunting about for a name that should not be at variance with her own, and yet should suggest a princess and a great lady, he decided upon calling her Dulcinea del Toboso — she being of El Toboso — a name, to his mind, musical, uncommon, and significant. (001-154)

The name is a literary creation layered upon a geographic reality. Cervantes chooses a real town — El Toboso, in the province of Toledo — giving his fictional knight a real-world anchor in the Castilian landscape.

## The Journey to El Toboso

In Part II, Sancho Panza is sent to El Toboso to find Dulcinea and deliver Don Quixote's message of devotion. The journey becomes one of the novel's most complex episodes. Sancho, knowing that Dulcinea does not exist, must improvise. Arriving near the village at night, he encounters three peasant girls on donkeys and presents them to Don Quixote as Dulcinea and her ladies.

Don Quixote sees only coarse country wenches. The gap between his imagined Dulcinea — "a princess, for she is my queen and lady, and her beauty superhuman" — and the reality of three farm girls on donkeys produces a crisis that echoes throughout the rest of Part II. He concludes that enchanters have transformed his lady: the logic of chivalric romance provides the only explanation his mind can accept.

## A Real Town, A Fictional Lady

El Toboso exists on the map. Cervantes' choice of a real, unremarkable Manchegan village grounds the novel's fantasy in provincial reality. The juxtaposition is deliberate: the most idealized figure in the novel is associated with one of its most ordinary places. This tension between the real and the ideal — the farm-girl who is a princess, the village that is a court — is the novel's central preoccupation made geographic.
