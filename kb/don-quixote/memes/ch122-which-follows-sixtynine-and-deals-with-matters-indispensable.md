[122-001]WHICH FOLLOWS SIXTY-NINE AND DEALS WITH MATTERS INDISPENSABLE FOR THE

CLEAR COMPREHENSION OF THIS HISTORY

p70a.jpg (131K)


[122-002]Sancho slept that night in a cot in the same chamber with Don Quixote,

[122-003]a thing he would have gladly excused if he could for he knew very well

[122-004]that with questions and answers his master would not let him sleep, and

[122-005]he was in no humour for talking much, as he still felt the pain of his

[122-006]late martyrdom, which interfered with his freedom of speech; and it

[122-007]would have been more to his taste to sleep in a hovel alone, than in

[122-008]that luxurious chamber in company. And so well founded did his

[122-009]apprehension prove, and so correct was his anticipation, that scarcely

[122-010]had his master got into bed when he said, “What dost thou think of

[122-011]to-night’s adventure, Sancho? Great and mighty is the power of

[122-012]cold-hearted scorn, for thou with thine own eyes hast seen Altisidora

[122-013]slain, not by arrows, nor by the sword, nor by any warlike weapon, nor

[122-014]by deadly poisons, but by the thought of the sternness and scorn with

[122-015]which I have always treated her.”

[122-016]“She might have died and welcome,” said Sancho, “when she pleased and

[122-017]how she pleased; and she might have left me alone, for I never made her

[122-018]fall in love or scorned her. I don’t know nor can I imagine how the

[122-019]recovery of Altisidora, a damsel more fanciful than wise, can have, as

[122-020]I have said before, anything to do with the sufferings of Sancho Panza.

[122-021]Now I begin to see plainly and clearly that there are enchanters and

[122-022]enchanted people in the world; and may God deliver me from them, since

[122-023]I can’t deliver myself; and so I beg of your worship to let me sleep

[122-024]and not ask me any more questions, unless you want me to throw myself

[122-025]out of the window.”

[122-026]“Sleep, Sancho my friend,” said Don Quixote, “if the pinprodding and

[122-027]pinches thou hast received and the smacks administered to thee will let

[122-028]thee.”

[122-029]“No pain came up to the insult of the smacks,” said Sancho, “for the

[122-030]simple reason that it was duennas, confound them, that gave them to me;

[122-031]but once more I entreat your worship to let me sleep, for sleep is

[122-032]relief from misery to those who are miserable when awake.”

[122-033]“Be it so, and God be with thee,” said Don Quixote.

[122-034]They fell asleep, both of them, and Cide Hamete, the author of this

[122-035]great history, took this opportunity to record and relate what it was

[122-036]that induced the duke and duchess to get up the elaborate plot that has

[122-037]been described. The bachelor Samson Carrasco, he says, not forgetting

[122-038]how he as the Knight of the Mirrors had been vanquished and overthrown

[122-039]by Don Quixote, which defeat and overthrow upset all his plans,

[122-040]resolved to try his hand again, hoping for better luck than he had

[122-041]before; and so, having learned where Don Quixote was from the page who

[122-042]brought the letter and present to Sancho’s wife, Teresa Panza, he got

[122-043]himself new armour and another horse, and put a white moon upon his

[122-044]shield, and to carry his arms he had a mule led by a peasant, not by

[122-045]Tom Cecial his former squire for fear he should be recognised by Sancho

[122-046]or Don Quixote. He came to the duke’s castle, and the duke informed him

[122-047]of the road and route Don Quixote had taken with the intention of being

[122-048]present at the jousts at Saragossa. He told him, too, of the jokes he

[122-049]had practised upon him, and of the device for the disenchantment of

[122-050]Dulcinea at the expense of Sancho’s backside; and finally he gave him

[122-051]an account of the trick Sancho had played upon his master, making him

[122-052]believe that Dulcinea was enchanted and turned into a country wench;

[122-053]and of how the duchess, his wife, had persuaded Sancho that it was he

[122-054]himself who was deceived, inasmuch as Dulcinea was really enchanted; at

[122-055]which the bachelor laughed not a little, and marvelled as well at the

[122-056]sharpness and simplicity of Sancho as at the length to which Don

[122-057]Quixote’s madness went. The duke begged of him if he found him (whether

[122-058]he overcame him or not) to return that way and let him know the result.

[122-059]This the bachelor did; he set out in quest of Don Quixote, and not

[122-060]finding him at Saragossa, he went on, and how he fared has been already

[122-061]told. He returned to the duke’s castle and told him all, what the

[122-062]conditions of the combat were, and how Don Quixote was now, like a

[122-063]loyal knight-errant, returning to keep his promise of retiring to his

[122-064]village for a year, by which time, said the bachelor, he might perhaps

[122-065]be cured of his madness; for that was the object that had led him to

[122-066]adopt these disguises, as it was a sad thing for a gentleman of such

[122-067]good parts as Don Quixote to be a madman. And so he took his leave of

[122-068]the duke, and went home to his village to wait there for Don Quixote,

[122-069]who was coming after him. Thereupon the duke seized the opportunity of

[122-070]practising this mystification upon him; so much did he enjoy everything

[122-071]connected with Sancho and Don Quixote. He had the roads about the

[122-072]castle far and near, everywhere he thought Don Quixote was likely to

[122-073]pass on his return, occupied by large numbers of his servants on foot

[122-074]and on horseback, who were to bring him to the castle, by fair means or

[122-075]foul, if they met him. They did meet him, and sent word to the duke,

[122-076]who, having already settled what was to be done, as soon as he heard of

[122-077]his arrival, ordered the torches and lamps in the court to be lit and

[122-078]Altisidora to be placed on the catafalque with all the pomp and

[122-079]ceremony that has been described, the whole affair being so well

[122-080]arranged and acted that it differed but little from reality. And Cide

[122-081]Hamete says, moreover, that for his part he considers the concocters of

[122-082]the joke as crazy as the victims of it, and that the duke and duchess

[122-083]were not two fingers’ breadth removed from being something like fools

[122-084]themselves when they took such pains to make game of a pair of fools.

[122-085]As for the latter, one was sleeping soundly and the other lying awake

[122-086]occupied with his desultory thoughts, when daylight came to them

[122-087]bringing with it the desire to rise; for the lazy down was never a

[122-088]delight to Don Quixote, victor or vanquished. Altisidora, come back

[122-089]from death to life as Don Quixote fancied, following up the freak of

[122-090]her lord and lady, entered the chamber, crowned with the garland she

[122-091]had worn on the catafalque and in a robe of white taffeta embroidered

[122-092]with gold flowers, her hair flowing loose over her shoulders, and

[122-093]leaning upon a staff of fine black ebony. Don Quixote, disconcerted and

[122-094]in confusion at her appearance, huddled himself up and well-nigh

[122-095]covered himself altogether with the sheets and counterpane of the bed,

[122-096]tongue-tied, and unable to offer her any civility. Altisidora seated

[122-097]herself on a chair at the head of the bed, and, after a deep sigh, said

[122-098]to him in a feeble, soft voice, “When women of rank and modest maidens

[122-099]trample honour under foot, and give a loose to the tongue that breaks

[122-100]through every impediment, publishing abroad the inmost secrets of their

[122-101]hearts, they are reduced to sore extremities. Such a one am I, Señor

[122-102]Don Quixote of La Mancha, crushed, conquered, love-smitten, but yet

[122-103]patient under suffering and virtuous, and so much so that my heart

[122-104]broke with grief and I lost my life. For the last two days I have been

[122-105]dead, slain by the thought of the cruelty with which thou hast treated

[122-106]me, obdurate knight,

[122-107]O harder thou than marble to my plaint;

[122-108]or at least believed to be dead by all who saw me; and had it not been

[122-109]that Love, taking pity on me, let my recovery rest upon the sufferings

[122-110]of this good squire, there I should have remained in the other world.”

[122-111]“Love might very well have let it rest upon the sufferings of my ass,

[122-112]and I should have been obliged to him,” said Sancho. “But tell me,

[122-113]señora—and may heaven send you a tenderer lover than my master—what did

[122-114]you see in the other world? What goes on in hell? For of course that’s

[122-115]where one who dies in despair is bound for.”

[122-116]“To tell you the truth,” said Altisidora, “I cannot have died outright,

[122-117]for I did not go into hell; had I gone in, it is very certain I should

[122-118]never have come out again, do what I might. The truth is, I came to the

[122-119]gate, where some dozen or so of devils were playing tennis, all in

[122-120]breeches and doublets, with falling collars trimmed with Flemish

[122-121]bonelace, and ruffles of the same that served them for wristbands, with

[122-122]four fingers’ breadth of the arms exposed to make their hands look

[122-123]longer; in their hands they held rackets of fire; but what amazed me

[122-124]still more was that books, apparently full of wind and rubbish, served

[122-125]them for tennis balls, a strange and marvellous thing; this, however,

[122-126]did not astonish me so much as to observe that, although with players

[122-127]it is usual for the winners to be glad and the losers sorry, there in

[122-128]that game all were growling, all were snarling, and all were cursing

[122-129]one another.” “That’s no wonder,” said Sancho; “for devils, whether

[122-130]playing or not, can never be content, win or lose.”

[122-131]“Very likely,” said Altisidora; “but there is another thing that

[122-132]surprises me too, I mean surprised me then, and that was that no ball

[122-133]outlasted the first throw or was of any use a second time; and it was

[122-134]wonderful the constant succession there was of books, new and old. To

[122-135]one of them, a brand-new, well-bound one, they gave such a stroke that

[122-136]they knocked the guts out of it and scattered the leaves about. ‘Look

[122-137]what book that is,’ said one devil to another, and the other replied,

[122-138]‘It is the “Second Part of the History of Don Quixote of La Mancha,”

[122-139]not by Cide Hamete, the original author, but by an Aragonese who by his

[122-140]own account is of Tordesillas.’ ‘Out of this with it,’ said the first,

[122-141]‘and into the depths of hell with it out of my sight.’ ‘Is it so bad?’

[122-142]said the other. ‘So bad is it,’ said the first, ‘that if I had set

[122-143]myself deliberately to make a worse, I could not have done it.’ They

[122-144]then went on with their game, knocking other books about; and I, having

[122-145]heard them mention the name of Don Quixote whom I love and adore so,

[122-146]took care to retain this vision in my memory.”

[122-147]“A vision it must have been, no doubt,” said Don Quixote, “for there is

[122-148]no other I in the world; this history has been going about here for

[122-149]some time from hand to hand, but it does not stay long in any, for

[122-150]everybody gives it a taste of his foot. I am not disturbed by hearing

[122-151]that I am wandering in a fantastic shape in the darkness of the pit or

[122-152]in the daylight above, for I am not the one that history treats of. If

[122-153]it should be good, faithful, and true, it will have ages of life; but

[122-154]if it should be bad, from its birth to its burial will not be a very

[122-155]long journey.”

[122-156]Altisidora was about to proceed with her complaint against Don Quixote,

[122-157]when he said to her, “I have several times told you, señora, that it

[122-158]grieves me you should have set your affections upon me, as from mine

[122-159]they can only receive gratitude, but no return. I was born to belong to

[122-160]Dulcinea del Toboso, and the fates, if there are any, dedicated me to

[122-161]her; and to suppose that any other beauty can take the place she

[122-162]occupies in my heart is to suppose an impossibility. This frank

[122-163]declaration should suffice to make you retire within the bounds of your

[122-164]modesty, for no one can bind himself to do impossibilities.”

[122-165]Hearing this, Altisidora, with a show of anger and agitation,

[122-166]exclaimed, “God’s life! Don Stockfish, soul of a mortar, stone of a

[122-167]date, more obstinate and obdurate than a clown asked a favour when he

[122-168]has his mind made up, if I fall upon you I’ll tear your eyes out! Do

[122-169]you fancy, Don Vanquished, Don Cudgelled, that I died for your sake?

[122-170]All that you have seen to-night has been make-believe; I’m not the

[122-171]woman to let the black of my nail suffer for such a camel, much less

[122-172]die!”

[122-173]“That I can well believe,” said Sancho; “for all that about lovers

[122-174]pining to death is absurd; they may talk of it, but as for doing

[122-175]it—Judas may believe that!”

[122-176]While they were talking, the musician, singer, and poet, who had sung

[122-177]the two stanzas given above came in, and making a profound obeisance to

[122-178]Don Quixote said, “Will your worship, sir knight, reckon and retain me

[122-179]in the number of your most faithful servants, for I have long been a

[122-180]great admirer of yours, as well because of your fame as because of your

[122-181]achievements?” “Will your worship tell me who you are,” replied Don

[122-182]Quixote, “so that my courtesy may be answerable to your deserts?” The

[122-183]young man replied that he was the musician and songster of the night

[122-184]before. “Of a truth,” said Don Quixote, “your worship has a most

[122-185]excellent voice; but what you sang did not seem to me very much to the

[122-186]purpose; for what have Garcilasso’s stanzas to do with the death of

[122-187]this lady?”

[122-188]“Don’t be surprised at that,” returned the musician; “for with the

[122-189]callow poets of our day the way is for every one to write as he pleases

[122-190]and pilfer where he chooses, whether it be germane to the matter or

[122-191]not, and now-a-days there is no piece of silliness they can sing or

[122-192]write that is not set down to poetic licence.”

[122-193]Don Quixote was about to reply, but was prevented by the duke and

[122-194]duchess, who came in to see him, and with them there followed a long

[122-195]and delightful conversation, in the course of which Sancho said so many

[122-196]droll and saucy things that he left the duke and duchess wondering not

[122-197]only at his simplicity but at his sharpness. Don Quixote begged their

[122-198]permission to take his departure that same day, inasmuch as for a

[122-199]vanquished knight like himself it was fitter he should live in a

[122-200]pig-sty than in a royal palace. They gave it very readily, and the

[122-201]duchess asked him if Altisidora was in his good graces.

[122-202]He replied, “Señora, let me tell your ladyship that this damsel’s

[122-203]ailment comes entirely of idleness, and the cure for it is honest and

[122-204]constant employment. She herself has told me that lace is worn in hell;

[122-205]and as she must know how to make it, let it never be out of her hands;

[122-206]for when she is occupied in shifting the bobbins to and fro, the image

[122-207]or images of what she loves will not shift to and fro in her thoughts;

[122-208]this is the truth, this is my opinion, and this is my advice.”

[122-209]“And mine,” added Sancho; “for I never in all my life saw a lace-maker

[122-210]that died for love; when damsels are at work their minds are more set

[122-211]on finishing their tasks than on thinking of their loves. I speak from

[122-212]my own experience; for when I’m digging I never think of my old woman;

[122-213]I mean my Teresa Panza, whom I love better than my own eyelids.” “You

[122-214]say well, Sancho,” said the duchess, “and I will take care that my

[122-215]Altisidora employs herself henceforward in needlework of some sort; for

[122-216]she is extremely expert at it.” “There is no occasion to have recourse

[122-217]to that remedy, señora,” said Altisidora; “for the mere thought of the

[122-218]cruelty with which this vagabond villain has treated me will suffice to

[122-219]blot him out of my memory without any other device; with your

[122-220]highness’s leave I will retire, not to have before my eyes, I won’t say

[122-221]his rueful countenance, but his abominable, ugly looks.” “That reminds

[122-222]me of the common saying, that ‘he that rails is ready to forgive,’”

[122-223]said the duke.

[122-224]Altisidora then, pretending to wipe away her tears with a handkerchief,

[122-225]made an obeisance to her master and mistress and quitted the room.

[122-226]“Ill luck betide thee, poor damsel,” said Sancho, “ill luck betide

[122-227]thee! Thou hast fallen in with a soul as dry as a rush and a heart as

[122-228]hard as oak; had it been me, i’faith ‘another cock would have crowed to

[122-229]thee.’”

[122-230]So the conversation came to an end, and Don Quixote dressed himself and

[122-231]dined with the duke and duchess, and set out the same evening.
