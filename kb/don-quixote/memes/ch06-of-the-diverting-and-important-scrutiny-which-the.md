OF THE DIVERTING AND IMPORTANT SCRUTINY WHICH THE CURATE AND THE BARBER

MADE IN THE LIBRARY OF OUR INGENIOUS GENTLEMAN

c06a.jpg (92K)


[006-001]He was still sleeping; so the curate asked the niece for the keys of

[006-002]the room where the books, the authors of all the mischief, were, and

[006-003]right willingly she gave them. They all went in, the housekeeper with

[006-004]them, and found more than a hundred volumes of big books very well

[006-005]bound, and some other small ones. The moment the housekeeper saw them

[006-006]she turned about and ran out of the room, and came back immediately

[006-007]with a saucer of holy water and a sprinkler, saying, “Here, your

[006-008]worship, señor licentiate, sprinkle this room; don’t leave any magician

[006-009]of the many there are in these books to bewitch us in revenge for our

[006-010]design of banishing them from the world.”

[006-011]The simplicity of the housekeeper made the licentiate laugh, and he

[006-012]directed the barber to give him the books one by one to see what they

[006-013]were about, as there might be some to be found among them that did not

[006-014]deserve the penalty of fire.

[006-015]“No,” said the niece, “there is no reason for showing mercy to any of

[006-016]them; they have every one of them done mischief; better fling them out

[006-017]of the window into the court and make a pile of them and set fire to

[006-018]them; or else carry them into the yard, and there a bonfire can be made

[006-019]without the smoke giving any annoyance.” The housekeeper said the same,

[006-020]so eager were they both for the slaughter of those innocents, but the

[006-021]curate would not agree to it without first reading at any rate the

[006-022]titles.

[006-023]The first that Master Nicholas put into his hand was “The four books of

[006-024]Amadis of Gaul.” “This seems a mysterious thing,” said the curate,

[006-025]“for, as I have heard say, this was the first book of chivalry printed

[006-026]in Spain, and from this all the others derive their birth and origin;

[006-027]so it seems to me that we ought inexorably to condemn it to the flames

[006-028]as the founder of so vile a sect.”

[006-029]“Nay, sir,” said the barber, “I too, have heard say that this is the

[006-030]best of all the books of this kind that have been written, and so, as

[006-031]something singular in its line, it ought to be pardoned.”

[006-032]“True,” said the curate; “and for that reason let its life be spared

[006-033]for the present. Let us see that other which is next to it.”

[006-034]“It is,” said the barber, “the ‘Sergas de Esplandian,’ the lawful son

[006-035]of Amadis of Gaul.”

[006-036]“Then verily,” said the curate, “the merit of the father must not be

[006-037]put down to the account of the son. Take it, mistress housekeeper; open

[006-038]the window and fling it into the yard and lay the foundation of the

[006-039]pile for the bonfire we are to make.”

[006-040]The housekeeper obeyed with great satisfaction, and the worthy

[006-041]“Esplandian” went flying into the yard to await with all patience the

[006-042]fire that was in store for him.

[006-043]“Proceed,” said the curate.

[006-044]“This that comes next,” said the barber, “is ‘Amadis of Greece,’ and,

[006-045]indeed, I believe all those on this side are of the same Amadis

[006-046]lineage.”

[006-047]“Then to the yard with the whole of them,” said the curate; “for to

[006-048]have the burning of Queen Pintiquiniestra, and the shepherd Darinel and

[006-049]his eclogues, and the bedevilled and involved discourses of his author,

[006-050]I would burn with them the father who begot me if he were going about

[006-051]in the guise of a knight-errant.”

[006-052]“I am of the same mind,” said the barber.

[006-053]“And so am I,” added the niece.

[006-054]“In that case,” said the housekeeper, “here, into the yard with them!”

[006-055]They were handed to her, and as there were many of them, she spared

[006-056]herself the staircase, and flung them down out of the window.

[006-057]“Who is that tub there?” said the curate.

[006-058]“This,” said the barber, “is ‘Don Olivante de Laura.’”

[006-059]“The author of that book,” said the curate, “was the same that wrote

[006-060]‘The Garden of Flowers,’ and truly there is no deciding which of the

[006-061]two books is the more truthful, or, to put it better, the less lying;

[006-062]all I can say is, send this one into the yard for a swaggering fool.”

[006-063]“This that follows is ‘Florismarte of Hircania,’” said the barber.

[006-064]“Señor Florismarte here?” said the curate; “then by my faith he must

[006-065]take up his quarters in the yard, in spite of his marvellous birth and

[006-066]visionary adventures, for the stiffness and dryness of his style

[006-067]deserve nothing else; into the yard with him and the other, mistress

[006-068]housekeeper.”

[006-069]“With all my heart, señor,” said she, and executed the order with great

[006-070]delight.

[006-071]“This,” said the barber, “is ‘The Knight Platir.’”

[006-072]“An old book that,” said the curate, “but I find no reason for clemency

[006-073]in it; send it after the others without appeal;” which was done.

[006-074]Another book was opened, and they saw it was entitled, “The Knight of

[006-075]the Cross.”

[006-076]“For the sake of the holy name this book has,” said the curate, “its

[006-077]ignorance might be excused; but then, they say, ‘behind the cross

[006-078]there’s the devil;’ to the fire with it.”

[006-079]Taking down another book, the barber said, “This is ‘The Mirror of

[006-080]Chivalry.’”

[006-081]“I know his worship,” said the curate; “that is where Señor Reinaldos

[006-082]of Montalvan figures with his friends and comrades, greater thieves

[006-083]than Cacus, and the Twelve Peers of France with the veracious historian

[006-084]Turpin; however, I am not for condemning them to more than perpetual

[006-085]banishment, because, at any rate, they have some share in the invention

[006-086]of the famous Matteo Boiardo, whence too the Christian poet Ludovico

[006-087]Ariosto wove his web, to whom, if I find him here, and speaking any

[006-088]language but his own, I shall show no respect whatever; but if he

[006-089]speaks his own tongue I will put him upon my head.”

[006-090]“Well, I have him in Italian,” said the barber, “but I do not

[006-091]understand him.”

[006-092]“Nor would it be well that you should understand him,” said the curate,

[006-093]“and on that score we might have excused the Captain if he had not

[006-094]brought him into Spain and turned him into Castilian. He robbed him of

[006-095]a great deal of his natural force, and so do all those who try to turn

[006-096]books written in verse into another language, for, with all the pains

[006-097]they take and all the cleverness they show, they never can reach the

[006-098]level of the originals as they were first produced. In short, I say

[006-099]that this book, and all that may be found treating of those French

[006-100]affairs, should be thrown into or deposited in some dry well, until

[006-101]after more consideration it is settled what is to be done with them;

[006-102]excepting always one ‘Bernardo del Carpio’ that is going about, and

[006-103]another called ‘Roncesvalles;’ for these, if they come into my hands,

[006-104]shall pass at once into those of the housekeeper, and from hers into

[006-105]the fire without any reprieve.”

[006-106]To all this the barber gave his assent, and looked upon it as right and

[006-107]proper, being persuaded that the curate was so staunch to the Faith and

[006-108]loyal to the Truth that he would not for the world say anything opposed

[006-109]to them. Opening another book he saw it was “Palmerin de Oliva,” and

[006-110]beside it was another called “Palmerin of England,” seeing which the

[006-111]licentiate said, “Let the Olive be made firewood of at once and burned

[006-112]until no ashes even are left; and let that Palm of England be kept and

[006-113]preserved as a thing that stands alone, and let such another case be

[006-114]made for it as that which Alexander found among the spoils of Darius

[006-115]and set aside for the safe keeping of the works of the poet Homer. This

[006-116]book, gossip, is of authority for two reasons, first because it is very

[006-117]good, and secondly because it is said to have been written by a wise

[006-118]and witty king of Portugal. All the adventures at the Castle of

[006-119]Miraguarda are excellent and of admirable contrivance, and the language

[006-120]is polished and clear, studying and observing the style befitting the

[006-121]speaker with propriety and judgment. So then, provided it seems good to

[006-122]you, Master Nicholas, I say let this and ‘Amadis of Gaul’ be remitted

[006-123]the penalty of fire, and as for all the rest, let them perish without

[006-124]further question or query.”

[006-125]“Nay, gossip,” said the barber, “for this that I have here is the

[006-126]famous ‘Don Belianis.’”

[006-127]“Well,” said the curate, “that and the second, third, and fourth parts

[006-128]all stand in need of a little rhubarb to purge their excess of bile,

[006-129]and they must be cleared of all that stuff about the Castle of Fame and

[006-130]other greater affectations, to which end let them be allowed the

[006-131]over-seas term, and, according as they mend, so shall mercy or justice

[006-132]be meted out to them; and in the mean time, gossip, do you keep them in

[006-133]your house and let no one read them.”

[006-134]“With all my heart,” said the barber; and not caring to tire himself

[006-135]with reading more books of chivalry, he told the housekeeper to take

[006-136]all the big ones and throw them into the yard. It was not said to one

[006-137]dull or deaf, but to one who enjoyed burning them more than weaving the

[006-138]broadest and finest web that could be; and seizing about eight at a

[006-139]time, she flung them out of the window.

[006-140]In carrying so many together she let one fall at the feet of the

[006-141]barber, who took it up, curious to know whose it was, and found it

[006-142]said, “History of the Famous Knight, Tirante el Blanco.”

[006-143]“God bless me!” said the curate with a shout, “‘Tirante el Blanco’

[006-144]here! Hand it over, gossip, for in it I reckon I have found a treasury

[006-145]of enjoyment and a mine of recreation. Here is Don Kyrieleison of

[006-146]Montalvan, a valiant knight, and his brother Thomas of Montalvan, and

[006-147]the knight Fonseca, with the battle the bold Tirante fought with the

[006-148]mastiff, and the witticisms of the damsel Placerdemivida, and the loves

[006-149]and wiles of the widow Reposada, and the empress in love with the

[006-150]squire Hipolito—in truth, gossip, by right of its style it is the best

[006-151]book in the world. Here knights eat and sleep, and die in their beds,

[006-152]and make their wills before dying, and a great deal more of which there

[006-153]is nothing in all the other books. Nevertheless, I say he who wrote it,

[006-154]for deliberately composing such fooleries, deserves to be sent to the

[006-155]galleys for life. Take it home with you and read it, and you will see

[006-156]that what I have said is true.”

[006-157]“As you will,” said the barber; “but what are we to do with these

[006-158]little books that are left?”

[006-159]“These must be, not chivalry, but poetry,” said the curate; and opening

[006-160]one he saw it was the “Diana” of Jorge de Montemayor, and, supposing

[006-161]all the others to be of the same sort, “these,” he said, “do not

[006-162]deserve to be burned like the others, for they neither do nor can do

[006-163]the mischief the books of chivalry have done, being books of

[006-164]entertainment that can hurt no one.”

[006-165]“Ah, señor!” said the niece, “your worship had better order these to be

[006-166]burned as well as the others; for it would be no wonder if, after being

[006-167]cured of his chivalry disorder, my uncle, by reading these, took a

[006-168]fancy to turn shepherd and range the woods and fields singing and

[006-169]piping; or, what would be still worse, to turn poet, which they say is

[006-170]an incurable and infectious malady.”

[006-171]“The damsel is right,” said the curate, “and it will be well to put

[006-172]this stumbling-block and temptation out of our friend’s way. To begin,

[006-173]then, with the ‘Diana’ of Montemayor. I am of opinion it should not be

[006-174]burned, but that it should be cleared of all that about the sage

[006-175]Felicia and the magic water, and of almost all the longer pieces of

[006-176]verse: let it keep, and welcome, its prose and the honour of being the

[006-177]first of books of the kind.”

[006-178]“This that comes next,” said the barber, “is the ‘Diana,’ entitled the

[006-179]‘Second Part, by the Salamancan,’ and this other has the same title,

[006-180]and its author is Gil Polo.”

[006-181]“As for that of the Salamancan,” replied the curate, “let it go to

[006-182]swell the number of the condemned in the yard, and let Gil Polo’s be

[006-183]preserved as if it came from Apollo himself: but get on, gossip, and

[006-184]make haste, for it is growing late.”

[006-185]“This book,” said the barber, opening another, “is the ten books of the

[006-186]‘Fortune of Love,’ written by Antonio de Lofraso, a Sardinian poet.”

[006-187]“By the orders I have received,” said the curate, “since Apollo has

[006-188]been Apollo, and the Muses have been Muses, and poets have been poets,

[006-189]so droll and absurd a book as this has never been written, and in its

[006-190]way it is the best and the most singular of all of this species that

[006-191]have as yet appeared, and he who has not read it may be sure he has

[006-192]never read what is delightful. Give it here, gossip, for I make more

[006-193]account of having found it than if they had given me a cassock of

[006-194]Florence stuff.”

[006-195]He put it aside with extreme satisfaction, and the barber went on,

[006-196]“These that come next are ‘The Shepherd of Iberia,’ ‘Nymphs of

[006-197]Henares,’ and ‘The Enlightenment of Jealousy.’”

[006-198]“Then all we have to do,” said the curate, “is to hand them over to the

[006-199]secular arm of the housekeeper, and ask me not why, or we shall never

[006-200]have done.”

[006-201]“This next is the ‘Pastor de Fílida.’”

[006-202]“No Pastor that,” said the curate, “but a highly polished courtier; let

[006-203]it be preserved as a precious jewel.”

[006-204]“This large one here,” said the barber, “is called ‘The Treasury of

[006-205]various Poems.’”

[006-206]“If there were not so many of them,” said the curate, “they would be

[006-207]more relished: this book must be weeded and cleansed of certain

[006-208]vulgarities which it has with its excellences; let it be preserved

[006-209]because the author is a friend of mine, and out of respect for other

[006-210]more heroic and loftier works that he has written.”

[006-211]“This,” continued the barber, “is the ‘Cancionero’ of Lopez de

[006-212]Maldonado.”

[006-213]“The author of that book, too,” said the curate, “is a great friend of

[006-214]mine, and his verses from his own mouth are the admiration of all who

[006-215]hear them, for such is the sweetness of his voice that he enchants when

[006-216]he chants them: it gives rather too much of its eclogues, but what is

[006-217]good was never yet plentiful: let it be kept with those that have been

[006-218]set apart. But what book is that next it?”

[006-219]“The ‘Galatea’ of Miguel de Cervantes,” said the barber.

[006-220]“That Cervantes has been for many years a great friend of mine, and to

[006-221]my knowledge he has had more experience in reverses than in verses. His

[006-222]book has some good invention in it, it presents us with something but

[006-223]brings nothing to a conclusion: we must wait for the Second Part it

[006-224]promises: perhaps with amendment it may succeed in winning the full

[006-225]measure of grace that is now denied it; and in the mean time do you,

[006-226]señor gossip, keep it shut up in your own quarters.”

[006-227]“Very good,” said the barber; “and here come three together, the

[006-228]‘Araucana’ of Don Alonso de Ercilla, the ‘Austriada’ of Juan Rufo,

[006-229]Justice of Cordova, and the ‘Montserrate’ of Christobal de Virués, the

[006-230]Valencian poet.”

[006-231]“These three books,” said the curate, “are the best that have been

[006-232]written in Castilian in heroic verse, and they may compare with the

[006-233]most famous of Italy; let them be preserved as the richest treasures of

[006-234]poetry that Spain possesses.”

[006-235]The curate was tired and would not look into any more books, and so he

[006-236]decided that, “contents uncertified,” all the rest should be burned;

[006-237]but just then the barber held open one, called “The Tears of Angelica.”

[006-238]“I should have shed tears myself,” said the curate when he heard the

[006-239]title, “had I ordered that book to be burned, for its author was one of

[006-240]the famous poets of the world, not to say of Spain, and was very happy

[006-241]in the translation of some of Ovid’s fables.”
