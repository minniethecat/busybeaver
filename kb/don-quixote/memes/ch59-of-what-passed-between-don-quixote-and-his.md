[059-001]OF WHAT PASSED BETWEEN DON QUIXOTE AND HIS SQUIRE, TOGETHER WITH OTHER

VERY NOTABLE INCIDENTS

p07a.jpg (140K)


[059-002]The instant the housekeeper saw Sancho Panza shut himself in with her

[059-003]master, she guessed what they were about; and suspecting that the

[059-004]result of the consultation would be a resolve to undertake a third

[059-005]sally, she seized her mantle, and in deep anxiety and distress, ran to

[059-006]find the bachelor Samson Carrasco, as she thought that, being a

[059-007]well-spoken man, and a new friend of her master’s, he might be able to

[059-008]persuade him to give up any such crazy notion. She found him pacing the

[059-009]patio of his house, and, perspiring and flurried, she fell at his feet

[059-010]the moment she saw him.

[059-011]Carrasco, seeing how distressed and overcome she was, said to her,

[059-012]“What is this, mistress housekeeper? What has happened to you? One

[059-013]would think you heart-broken.”

[059-014]“Nothing, Señor Samson,” said she, “only that my master is breaking

[059-015]out, plainly breaking out.”

[059-016]“Whereabouts is he breaking out, señora?” asked Samson; “has any part

[059-017]of his body burst?”

[059-018]“He is only breaking out at the door of his madness,” she replied; “I

[059-019]mean, dear señor bachelor, that he is going to break out again (and

[059-020]this will be the third time) to hunt all over the world for what he

[059-021]calls ventures, though I can’t make out why he gives them that name.

[059-022]The first time he was brought back to us slung across the back of an

[059-023]ass, and belaboured all over; and the second time he came in an

[059-024]ox-cart, shut up in a cage, in which he persuaded himself he was

[059-025]enchanted, and the poor creature was in such a state that the mother

[059-026]that bore him would not have known him; lean, yellow, with his eyes

[059-027]sunk deep in the cells of his skull; so that to bring him round again,

[059-028]ever so little, cost me more than six hundred eggs, as God knows, and

[059-029]all the world, and my hens too, that won’t let me tell a lie.”

[059-030]“That I can well believe,” replied the bachelor, “for they are so good

[059-031]and so fat, and so well-bred, that they would not say one thing for

[059-032]another, though they were to burst for it. In short then, mistress

[059-033]housekeeper, that is all, and there is nothing the matter, except what

[059-034]it is feared Don Quixote may do?”

[059-035]“No, señor,” said she.

[059-036]“Well then,” returned the bachelor, “don’t be uneasy, but go home in

[059-037]peace; get me ready something hot for breakfast, and while you are on

[059-038]the way say the prayer of Santa Apollonia, that is if you know it; for

[059-039]I will come presently and you will see miracles.”

[059-040]“Woe is me,” cried the housekeeper, “is it the prayer of Santa

[059-041]Apollonia you would have me say? That would do if it was the toothache

[059-042]my master had; but it is in the brains, what he has got.”

[059-043]“I know what I am saying, mistress housekeeper; go, and don’t set

[059-044]yourself to argue with me, for you know I am a bachelor of Salamanca,

[059-045]and one can’t be more of a bachelor than that,” replied Carrasco; and

[059-046]with this the housekeeper retired, and the bachelor went to look for

[059-047]the curate, and arrange with him what will be told in its proper place.

[059-048]While Don Quixote and Sancho were shut up together, they had a

[059-049]discussion which the history records with great precision and

[059-050]scrupulous exactness. Sancho said to his master, “Señor, I have educed

[059-051]my wife to let me go with your worship wherever you choose to take me.”

[059-052]“Induced, you should say, Sancho,” said Don Quixote; “not educed.”

[059-053]“Once or twice, as well as I remember,” replied Sancho, “I have begged

[059-054]of your worship not to mend my words, if so be as you understand what I

[059-055]mean by them; and if you don’t understand them to say ‘Sancho,’ or

[059-056]‘devil,’ ‘I don’t understand thee; and if I don’t make my meaning

[059-057]plain, then you may correct me, for I am so focile—”

[059-058]“I don’t understand thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote at once; “for I

[059-059]know not what ‘I am so focile’ means.”

[059-060]“‘So focile’ means I am so much that way,” replied Sancho.

[059-061]“I understand thee still less now,” said Don Quixote.

[059-062]“Well, if you can’t understand me,” said Sancho, “I don’t know how to

[059-063]put it; I know no more, God help me.”

[059-064]“Oh, now I have hit it,” said Don Quixote; “thou wouldst say thou art

[059-065]so docile, tractable, and gentle that thou wilt take what I say to

[059-066]thee, and submit to what I teach thee.”

[059-067]“I would bet,” said Sancho, “that from the very first you understood

[059-068]me, and knew what I meant, but you wanted to put me out that you might

[059-069]hear me make another couple of dozen blunders.”

[059-070]“May be so,” replied Don Quixote; “but to come to the point, what does

[059-071]Teresa say?”

[059-072]“Teresa says,” replied Sancho, “that I should make sure with your

[059-073]worship, and ‘let papers speak and beards be still,’ for ‘he who binds

[059-074]does not wrangle,’ since one ‘take’ is better than two ‘I’ll give

[059-075]thee’s;’ and I say a woman’s advice is no great thing, and he who won’t

[059-076]take it is a fool.”

[059-077]“And so say I,” said Don Quixote; “continue, Sancho my friend; go on;

[059-078]you talk pearls to-day.”

[059-079]“The fact is,” continued Sancho, “that, as your worship knows better

[059-080]than I do, we are all of us liable to death, and to-day we are, and

[059-081]to-morrow we are not, and the lamb goes as soon as the sheep, and

[059-082]nobody can promise himself more hours of life in this world than God

[059-083]may be pleased to give him; for death is deaf, and when it comes to

[059-084]knock at our life’s door, it is always urgent, and neither prayers, nor

[059-085]struggles, nor sceptres, nor mitres, can keep it back, as common talk

[059-086]and report say, and as they tell us from the pulpits every day.”

[059-087]“All that is very true,” said Don Quixote; “but I cannot make out what

[059-088]thou art driving at.”

[059-089]“What I am driving at,” said Sancho, “is that your worship settle some

[059-090]fixed wages for me, to be paid monthly while I am in your service, and

[059-091]that the same be paid me out of your estate; for I don’t care to stand

[059-092]on rewards which either come late, or ill, or never at all; God help me

[059-093]with my own. In short, I would like to know what I am to get, be it

[059-094]much or little; for the hen will lay on one egg, and many littles make

[059-095]a much, and so long as one gains something there is nothing lost. To be

[059-096]sure, if it should happen (what I neither believe nor expect) that your

[059-097]worship were to give me that island you have promised me, I am not so

[059-098]ungrateful nor so grasping but that I would be willing to have the

[059-099]revenue of such island valued and stopped out of my wages in due

[059-100]promotion.”

[059-101]“Sancho, my friend,” replied Don Quixote, “sometimes proportion may be

[059-102]as good as promotion.”

[059-103]“I see,” said Sancho; “I’ll bet I ought to have said proportion, and

[059-104]not promotion; but it is no matter, as your worship has understood me.”

[059-105]“And so well understood,” returned Don Quixote, “that I have seen into

[059-106]the depths of thy thoughts, and know the mark thou art shooting at with

[059-107]the countless shafts of thy proverbs. Look here, Sancho, I would

[059-108]readily fix thy wages if I had ever found any instance in the histories

[059-109]of the knights-errant to show or indicate, by the slightest hint, what

[059-110]their squires used to get monthly or yearly; but I have read all or the

[059-111]best part of their histories, and I cannot remember reading of any

[059-112]knight-errant having assigned fixed wages to his squire; I only know

[059-113]that they all served on reward, and that when they least expected it,

[059-114]if good luck attended their masters, they found themselves recompensed

[059-115]with an island or something equivalent to it, or at the least they were

[059-116]left with a title and lordship. If with these hopes and additional

[059-117]inducements you, Sancho, please to return to my service, well and good;

[059-118]but to suppose that I am going to disturb or unhinge the ancient usage

[059-119]of knight-errantry, is all nonsense. And so, my Sancho, get you back to

[059-120]your house and explain my intentions to your Teresa, and if she likes

[059-121]and you like to be on reward with me, _bene quidem;_ if not, we remain

[059-122]friends; for if the pigeon-house does not lack food, it will not lack

[059-123]pigeons; and bear in mind, my son, that a good hope is better than a

[059-124]bad holding, and a good grievance better than a bad compensation. I

[059-125]speak in this way, Sancho, to show you that I can shower down proverbs

[059-126]just as well as yourself; and in short, I mean to say, and I do say,

[059-127]that if you don’t like to come on reward with me, and run the same

[059-128]chance that I run, God be with you and make a saint of you; for I shall

[059-129]find plenty of squires more obedient and painstaking, and not so

[059-130]thickheaded or talkative as you are.”

[059-131]When Sancho heard his master’s firm, resolute language, a cloud came

[059-132]over the sky with him and the wings of his heart drooped, for he had

[059-133]made sure that his master would not go without him for all the wealth

[059-134]of the world; and as he stood there dumbfoundered and moody, Samson

[059-135]Carrasco came in with the housekeeper and niece, who were anxious to

[059-136]hear by what arguments he was about to dissuade their master from going

[059-137]to seek adventures. The arch wag Samson came forward, and embracing him

[059-138]as he had done before, said with a loud voice, “O flower of

[059-139]knight-errantry! O shining light of arms! O honour and mirror of the

[059-140]Spanish nation! may God Almighty in his infinite power grant that any

[059-141]person or persons, who would impede or hinder thy third sally, may find

[059-142]no way out of the labyrinth of their schemes, nor ever accomplish what

[059-143]they most desire!” And then, turning to the housekeeper, he said,

[059-144]“Mistress housekeeper may just as well give over saying the prayer of

[059-145]Santa Apollonia, for I know it is the positive determination of the

[059-146]spheres that Señor Don Quixote shall proceed to put into execution his

[059-147]new and lofty designs; and I should lay a heavy burden on my conscience

[059-148]did I not urge and persuade this knight not to keep the might of his

[059-149]strong arm and the virtue of his valiant spirit any longer curbed and

[059-150]checked, for by his inactivity he is defrauding the world of the

[059-151]redress of wrongs, of the protection of orphans, of the honour of

[059-152]virgins, of the aid of widows, and of the support of wives, and other

[059-153]matters of this kind appertaining, belonging, proper and peculiar to

[059-154]the order of knight-errantry. On, then, my lord Don Quixote, beautiful

[059-155]and brave, let your worship and highness set out to-day rather than

[059-156]to-morrow; and if anything be needed for the execution of your purpose,

[059-157]here am I ready in person and purse to supply the want; and were it

[059-158]requisite to attend your magnificence as squire, I should esteem it the

[059-159]happiest good fortune.”

[059-160]At this, Don Quixote, turning to Sancho, said, “Did I not tell thee,

[059-161]Sancho, there would be squires enough and to spare for me? See now who

[059-162]offers to become one; no less than the illustrious bachelor Samson

[059-163]Carrasco, the perpetual joy and delight of the courts of the Salamancan

[059-164]schools, sound in body, discreet, patient under heat or cold, hunger or

[059-165]thirst, with all the qualifications requisite to make a knight-errant’s

[059-166]squire! But heaven forbid that, to gratify my own inclination, I should

[059-167]shake or shatter this pillar of letters and vessel of the sciences, and

[059-168]cut down this towering palm of the fair and liberal arts. Let this new

[059-169]Samson remain in his own country, and, bringing honour to it, bring

[059-170]honour at the same time on the grey heads of his venerable parents; for

[059-171]I will be content with any squire that comes to hand, as Sancho does

[059-172]not deign to accompany me.”

[059-173]“I do deign,” said Sancho, deeply moved and with tears in his eyes; “it

[059-174]shall not be said of me, master mine,” he continued, “‘the bread eaten

[059-175]and the company dispersed.’ Nay, I come of no ungrateful stock, for all

[059-176]the world knows, but particularly my own town, who the Panzas from whom

[059-177]I am descended were; and, what is more, I know and have learned, by

[059-178]many good words and deeds, your worship’s desire to show me favour; and

[059-179]if I have been bargaining more or less about my wages, it was only to

[059-180]please my wife, who, when she sets herself to press a point, no hammer

[059-181]drives the hoops of a cask as she drives one to do what she wants; but,

[059-182]after all, a man must be a man, and a woman a woman; and as I am a man

[059-183]anyhow, which I can’t deny, I will be one in my own house too, let who

[059-184]will take it amiss; and so there’s nothing more to do but for your

[059-185]worship to make your will with its codicil in such a way that it can’t

[059-186]be provoked, and let us set out at once, to save Señor Samson’s soul

[059-187]from suffering, as he says his conscience obliges him to persuade your

[059-188]worship to sally out upon the world a third time; so I offer again to

[059-189]serve your worship faithfully and loyally, as well and better than all

[059-190]the squires that served knights-errant in times past or present.”

[059-191]The bachelor was filled with amazement when he heard Sancho’s

[059-192]phraseology and style of talk, for though he had read the first part of

[059-193]his master’s history he never thought that he could be so droll as he

[059-194]was there described; but now, hearing him talk of a “will and codicil

[059-195]that could not be provoked,” instead of “will and codicil that could

[059-196]not be revoked,” he believed all he had read of him, and set him down

[059-197]as one of the greatest simpletons of modern times; and he said to

[059-198]himself that two such lunatics as master and man the world had never

[059-199]seen. In fine, Don Quixote and Sancho embraced one another and made

[059-200]friends, and by the advice and with the approval of the great Carrasco,

[059-201]who was now their oracle, it was arranged that their departure should

[059-202]take place three days thence, by which time they could have all that

[059-203]was requisite for the journey ready, and procure a closed helmet, which

[059-204]Don Quixote said he must by all means take. Samson offered him one, as

[059-205]he knew a friend of his who had it would not refuse it to him, though

[059-206]it was more dingy with rust and mildew than bright and clean like

[059-207]burnished steel.

[059-208]The curses which both housekeeper and niece poured out on the bachelor

[059-209]were past counting; they tore their hair, they clawed their faces, and

[059-210]in the style of the hired mourners that were once in fashion, they

[059-211]raised a lamentation over the departure of their master and uncle, as

[059-212]if it had been his death. Samson’s intention in persuading him to sally

[059-213]forth once more was to do what the history relates farther on; all by

[059-214]the advice of the curate and barber, with whom he had previously

[059-215]discussed the subject. Finally, then, during those three days, Don

[059-216]Quixote and Sancho provided themselves with what they considered

[059-217]necessary, and Sancho having pacified his wife, and Don Quixote his

[059-218]niece and housekeeper, at nightfall, unseen by anyone except the

[059-219]bachelor, who thought fit to accompany them half a league out of the

[059-220]village, they set out for El Toboso, Don Quixote on his good Rocinante

[059-221]and Sancho on his old Dapple, his alforjas furnished with certain

[059-222]matters in the way of victuals, and his purse with money that Don

[059-223]Quixote gave him to meet emergencies. Samson embraced him, and

[059-224]entreated him to let him hear of his good or evil fortunes, so that he

[059-225]might rejoice over the former or condole with him over the latter, as

[059-226]the laws of friendship required. Don Quixote promised him he would do

[059-227]so, and Samson returned to the village, and the other two took the road

[059-228]for the great city of El Toboso.
