WHAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE WITH CERTAIN GOATHERDS

c11a.jpg (173K)


[011-001]He was cordially welcomed by the goatherds, and Sancho, having as best

[011-002]he could put up Rocinante and the ass, drew towards the fragrance that

[011-003]came from some pieces of salted goat simmering in a pot on the fire;

[011-004]and though he would have liked at once to try if they were ready to be

[011-005]transferred from the pot to the stomach, he refrained from doing so as

[011-006]the goatherds removed them from the fire, and laying sheepskins on the

[011-007]ground, quickly spread their rude table, and with signs of hearty

[011-008]good-will invited them both to share what they had. Round the skins six

[011-009]of the men belonging to the fold seated themselves, having first with

[011-010]rough politeness pressed Don Quixote to take a seat upon a trough which

[011-011]they placed for him upside down. Don Quixote seated himself, and Sancho

[011-012]remained standing to serve the cup, which was made of horn. Seeing him

[011-013]standing, his master said to him:

[011-014]“That thou mayest see, Sancho, the good that knight-errantry contains

[011-015]in itself, and how those who fill any office in it are on the high road

[011-016]to be speedily honoured and esteemed by the world, I desire that thou

[011-017]seat thyself here at my side and in the company of these worthy people,

[011-018]and that thou be one with me who am thy master and natural lord, and

[011-019]that thou eat from my plate and drink from whatever I drink from; for

[011-020]the same may be said of knight-errantry as of love, that it levels

[011-021]all.”

[011-022]“Great thanks,” said Sancho, “but I may tell your worship that provided

[011-023]I have enough to eat, I can eat it as well, or better, standing, and by

[011-024]myself, than seated alongside of an emperor. And indeed, if the truth

[011-025]is to be told, what I eat in my corner without form or fuss has much

[011-026]more relish for me, even though it be bread and onions, than the

[011-027]turkeys of those other tables where I am forced to chew slowly, drink

[011-028]little, wipe my mouth every minute, and cannot sneeze or cough if I

[011-029]want or do other things that are the privileges of liberty and

[011-030]solitude. So, señor, as for these honours which your worship would put

[011-031]upon me as a servant and follower of knight-errantry, exchange them for

[011-032]other things which may be of more use and advantage to me; for these,

[011-033]though I fully acknowledge them as received, I renounce from this

[011-034]moment to the end of the world.”

[011-035]“For all that,” said Don Quixote, “thou must seat thyself, because him

[011-036]who humbleth himself God exalteth;” and seizing him by the arm he

[011-037]forced him to sit down beside himself.

[011-038]The goatherds did not understand this jargon about squires and

[011-039]knights-errant, and all they did was to eat in silence and stare at

[011-040]their guests, who with great elegance and appetite were stowing away

[011-041]pieces as big as one’s fist. The course of meat finished, they spread

[011-042]upon the sheepskins a great heap of parched acorns, and with them they

[011-043]put down a half cheese harder than if it had been made of mortar. All

[011-044]this while the horn was not idle, for it went round so constantly, now

[011-045]full, now empty, like the bucket of a water-wheel, that it soon drained

[011-046]one of the two wine-skins that were in sight. When Don Quixote had

[011-047]quite appeased his appetite he took up a handful of the acorns, and

[011-048]contemplating them attentively delivered himself somewhat in this

[011-049]fashion:

[011-050]“Happy the age, happy the time, to which the ancients gave the name of

[011-051]golden, not because in that fortunate age the gold so coveted in this

[011-052]our iron one was gained without toil, but because they that lived in it

[011-053]knew not the two words “_mine_” and “_thine_”! In that blessed age all

[011-054]things were in common; to win the daily food no labour was required of

[011-055]any save to stretch forth his hand and gather it from the sturdy oaks

[011-056]that stood generously inviting him with their sweet ripe fruit. The

[011-057]clear streams and running brooks yielded their savoury limpid waters in

[011-058]noble abundance. The busy and sagacious bees fixed their republic in

[011-059]the clefts of the rocks and hollows of the trees, offering without

[011-060]usance the plenteous produce of their fragrant toil to every hand. The

[011-061]mighty cork trees, unenforced save of their own courtesy, shed the

[011-062]broad light bark that served at first to roof the houses supported by

[011-063]rude stakes, a protection against the inclemency of heaven alone. Then

[011-064]all was peace, all friendship, all concord; as yet the dull share of

[011-065]the crooked plough had not dared to rend and pierce the tender bowels

[011-066]of our first mother that without compulsion yielded from every portion

[011-067]of her broad fertile bosom all that could satisfy, sustain, and delight

[011-068]the children that then possessed her. Then was it that the innocent and

[011-069]fair young shepherdess roamed from vale to vale and hill to hill, with

[011-070]flowing locks, and no more garments than were needful modestly to cover

[011-071]what modesty seeks and ever sought to hide. Nor were their ornaments

[011-072]like those in use to-day, set off by Tyrian purple, and silk tortured

[011-073]in endless fashions, but the wreathed leaves of the green dock and ivy,

[011-074]wherewith they went as bravely and becomingly decked as our Court dames

[011-075]with all the rare and far-fetched artifices that idle curiosity has

[011-076]taught them. Then the love-thoughts of the heart clothed themselves

[011-077]simply and naturally as the heart conceived them, nor sought to commend

[011-078]themselves by forced and rambling verbiage. Fraud, deceit, or malice

[011-079]had then not yet mingled with truth and sincerity. Justice held her

[011-080]ground, undisturbed and unassailed by the efforts of favour and of

[011-081]interest, that now so much impair, pervert, and beset her. Arbitrary

[011-082]law had not yet established itself in the mind of the judge, for then

[011-083]there was no cause to judge and no one to be judged. Maidens and

[011-084]modesty, as I have said, wandered at will alone and unattended, without

[011-085]fear of insult from lawlessness or libertine assault, and if they were

[011-086]undone it was of their own will and pleasure. But now in this hateful

[011-087]age of ours not one is safe, not though some new labyrinth like that of

[011-088]Crete conceal and surround her; even there the pestilence of gallantry

[011-089]will make its way to them through chinks or on the air by the zeal of

[011-090]its accursed importunity, and, despite of all seclusion, lead them to

[011-091]ruin. In defence of these, as time advanced and wickedness increased,

[011-092]the order of knights-errant was instituted, to defend maidens, to

[011-093]protect widows and to succour the orphans and the needy. To this order

[011-094]I belong, brother goatherds, to whom I return thanks for the

[011-095]hospitality and kindly welcome ye offer me and my squire; for though by

[011-096]natural law all living are bound to show favour to knights-errant, yet,

[011-097]seeing that without knowing this obligation ye have welcomed and

[011-098]feasted me, it is right that with all the good-will in my power I

[011-099]should thank you for yours.”

[011-100]All this long harangue (which might very well have been spared) our

[011-101]knight delivered because the acorns they gave him reminded him of the

[011-102]golden age; and the whim seized him to address all this unnecessary

[011-103]argument to the goatherds, who listened to him gaping in amazement

[011-104]without saying a word in reply. Sancho likewise held his peace and ate

[011-105]acorns, and paid repeated visits to the second wine-skin, which they

[011-106]had hung up on a cork tree to keep the wine cool.

[011-107]Don Quixote was longer in talking than the supper in finishing, at the

[011-108]end of which one of the goatherds said, “That your worship, señor

[011-109]knight-errant, may say with more truth that we show you hospitality

[011-110]with ready good-will, we will give you amusement and pleasure by making

[011-111]one of our comrades sing: he will be here before long, and he is a very

[011-112]intelligent youth and deep in love, and what is more he can read and

[011-113]write and play on the rebeck to perfection.”

[011-114]The goatherd had hardly done speaking, when the notes of the rebeck

[011-115]reached their ears; and shortly after, the player came up, a very

[011-116]good-looking young man of about two-and-twenty. His comrades asked him

[011-117]if he had supped, and on his replying that he had, he who had already

[011-118]made the offer said to him:

[011-119]“In that case, Antonio, thou mayest as well do us the pleasure of

[011-120]singing a little, that the gentleman, our guest, may see that even in

[011-121]the mountains and woods there are musicians: we have told him of thy

[011-122]accomplishments, and we want thee to show them and prove that we say

[011-123]true; so, as thou livest, pray sit down and sing that ballad about thy

[011-124]love that thy uncle the prebendary made thee, and that was so much

[011-125]liked in the town.”

[011-126]“With all my heart,” said the young man, and without waiting for more

[011-127]pressing he seated himself on the trunk of a felled oak, and tuning his

[011-128]rebeck, presently began to sing to these words.

[011-129]ANTONIO’S BALLAD

[011-130]Thou dost love me well, Olalla;

[011-131]Well I know it, even though

[011-132]Love’s mute tongues, thine eyes, have never

[011-133]By their glances told me so.

[011-134]For I know my love thou knowest,

[011-135]Therefore thine to claim I dare:

[011-136]Once it ceases to be secret,

[011-137]Love need never feel despair.

[011-138]True it is, Olalla, sometimes

[011-139]Thou hast all too plainly shown

[011-140]That thy heart is brass in hardness,

[011-141]And thy snowy bosom stone.

[011-142]Yet for all that, in thy coyness,

[011-143]And thy fickle fits between,

[011-144]Hope is there—at least the border

[011-145]Of her garment may be seen.

[011-146]Lures to faith are they, those glimpses,

[011-147]And to faith in thee I hold;

[011-148]Kindness cannot make it stronger,

[011-149]Coldness cannot make it cold.

[011-150]If it be that love is gentle,

[011-151]In thy gentleness I see

[011-152]Something holding out assurance

[011-153]To the hope of winning thee.

[011-154]If it be that in devotion

[011-155]Lies a power hearts to move,

[011-156]That which every day I show thee,

[011-157]Helpful to my suit should prove.

[011-158]Many a time thou must have noticed—

[011-159]If to notice thou dost care—

[011-160]How I go about on Monday

[011-161]Dressed in all my Sunday wear.

[011-162]Love’s eyes love to look on brightness;

[011-163]Love loves what is gaily drest;

[011-164]Sunday, Monday, all I care is

[011-165]Thou shouldst see me in my best.

[011-166]No account I make of dances,

[011-167]Or of strains that pleased thee so,

[011-168]Keeping thee awake from midnight

[011-169]Till the cocks began to crow;

[011-170]Or of how I roundly swore it

[011-171]That there’s none so fair as thou;

[011-172]True it is, but as I said it,

[011-173]By the girls I’m hated now.

[011-174]For Teresa of the hillside

[011-175]At my praise of thee was sore;

[011-176]Said, “You think you love an angel;

[011-177]It’s a monkey you adore;

[011-178]“Caught by all her glittering trinkets,

[011-179]And her borrowed braids of hair,

[011-180]And a host of made-up beauties

[011-181]That would Love himself ensnare.”

[011-182]’Twas a lie, and so I told her,

[011-183]And her cousin at the word

[011-184]Gave me his defiance for it;

[011-185]And what followed thou hast heard.

[011-186]Mine is no high-flown affection,

[011-187]Mine no passion _par amours_—

[011-188]As they call it—what I offer

[011-189]Is an honest love, and pure.

[011-190]Cunning cords the holy Church has,

[011-191]Cords of softest silk they be;

[011-192]Put thy neck beneath the yoke, dear;

[011-193]Mine will follow, thou wilt see.

[011-194]Else—and once for all I swear it

[011-195]By the saint of most renown—

[011-196]If I ever quit the mountains,

[011-197]’Twill be in a friar’s gown.

[011-198]Here the goatherd brought his song to an end, and though Don Quixote

[011-199]entreated him to sing more, Sancho had no mind that way, being more

[011-200]inclined for sleep than for listening to songs; so said he to his

[011-201]master, “Your worship will do well to settle at once where you mean to

[011-202]pass the night, for the labour these good men are at all day does not

[011-203]allow them to spend the night in singing.”

[011-204]“I understand thee, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote; “I perceive clearly

[011-205]that those visits to the wine-skin demand compensation in sleep rather

[011-206]than in music.”

[011-207]“It’s sweet to us all, blessed be God,” said Sancho.

[011-208]“I do not deny it,” replied Don Quixote; “but settle thyself where thou

[011-209]wilt; those of my calling are more becomingly employed in watching than

[011-210]in sleeping; still it would be as well if thou wert to dress this ear

[011-211]for me again, for it is giving me more pain than it need.”

[011-212]Sancho did as he bade him, but one of the goatherds, seeing the wound,

[011-213]told him not to be uneasy, as he would apply a remedy with which it

[011-214]would be soon healed; and gathering some leaves of rosemary, of which

[011-215]there was a great quantity there, he chewed them and mixed them with a

[011-216]little salt, and applying them to the ear he secured them firmly with a

[011-217]bandage, assuring him that no other treatment would be required, and so

[011-218]it proved.
