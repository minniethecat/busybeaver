INTRODUCTION

PREFARATORY

[P01-116]It was with considerable reluctance that I abandoned in favour of the

[P01-117]present undertaking what had long been a favourite project: that of a

[P01-118]new edition of Shelton’s “Don Quixote,” which has now become a somewhat

[P01-119]scarce book. There are some—and I confess myself to be one—for whom

[P01-120]Shelton’s racy old version, with all its defects, has a charm that no

[P01-121]modern translation, however skilful or correct, could possess. Shelton

[P01-122]had the inestimable advantage of belonging to the same generation as

[P01-123]Cervantes; “Don Quixote” had to him a vitality that only a contemporary

[P01-124]could feel; it cost him no dramatic effort to see things as Cervantes

[P01-125]saw them; there is no anachronism in his language; he put the Spanish

[P01-126]of Cervantes into the English of Shakespeare. Shakespeare himself most

[P01-127]likely knew the book; he may have carried it home with him in his

[P01-128]saddle-bags to Stratford on one of his last journeys, and under the

[P01-129]mulberry tree at New Place joined hands with a kindred genius in its

[P01-130]pages.

[P01-131]But it was soon made plain to me that to hope for even a moderate

[P01-132]popularity for Shelton was vain. His fine old crusted English would, no

[P01-133]doubt, be relished by a minority, but it would be only by a minority.

[P01-134]His warmest admirers must admit that he is not a satisfactory

[P01-135]representative of Cervantes. His translation of the First Part was very

[P01-136]hastily made and was never revised by him. It has all the freshness and

[P01-137]vigour, but also a full measure of the faults, of a hasty production.

[P01-138]It is often very literal—barbarously literal frequently—but just as

[P01-139]often very loose. He had evidently a good colloquial knowledge of

[P01-140]Spanish, but apparently not much more. It never seems to occur to him

[P01-141]that the same translation of a word will not suit in every case.

[P01-142]It is often said that we have no satisfactory translation of “Don

[P01-143]Quixote.” To those who are familiar with the original, it savours of

[P01-144]truism or platitude to say so, for in truth there can be no thoroughly

[P01-145]satisfactory translation of “Don Quixote” into English or any other

[P01-146]language. It is not that the Spanish idioms are so utterly

[P01-147]unmanageable, or that the untranslatable words, numerous enough no

[P01-148]doubt, are so superabundant, but rather that the sententious terseness

[P01-149]to which the humour of the book owes its flavour is peculiar to

[P01-150]Spanish, and can at best be only distantly imitated in any other

[P01-151]tongue.

[P01-152]The history of our English translations of “Don Quixote” is

[P01-153]instructive. Shelton’s, the first in any language, was made,

[P01-154]apparently, about 1608, but not published till 1612. This of course was

[P01-155]only the First Part. It has been asserted that the Second, published in

[P01-156]1620, is not the work of Shelton, but there is nothing to support the

[P01-157]assertion save the fact that it has less spirit, less of what we

[P01-158]generally understand by “go,” about it than the first, which would be

[P01-159]only natural if the first were the work of a young man writing

[P01-160]_currente calamo_, and the second that of a middle-aged man writing for

[P01-161]a bookseller. On the other hand, it is closer and more literal, the

[P01-162]style is the same, the very same translations, or mistranslations,

[P01-163]occur in it, and it is extremely unlikely that a new translator would,

[P01-164]by suppressing his name, have allowed Shelton to carry off the credit.

[P01-165]In 1687 John Phillips, Milton’s nephew, produced a “Don Quixote” “made

[P01-166]English,” he says, “according to the humour of our modern language.”

[P01-167]His “Quixote” is not so much a translation as a travesty, and a

[P01-168]travesty that for coarseness, vulgarity, and buffoonery is almost

[P01-169]unexampled even in the literature of that day.

[P01-170]Ned Ward’s “Life and Notable Adventures of Don Quixote, merrily

[P01-171]translated into Hudibrastic Verse” (1700), can scarcely be reckoned a

[P01-172]translation, but it serves to show the light in which “Don Quixote” was

[P01-173]regarded at the time.

[P01-174]A further illustration may be found in the version published in 1712 by

[P01-175]Peter Motteux, who had then recently combined tea-dealing with

[P01-176]literature. It is described as “translated from the original by several

[P01-177]hands,” but if so all Spanish flavour has entirely evaporated under the

[P01-178]manipulation of the several hands. The flavour that it has, on the

[P01-179]other hand, is distinctly Franco-cockney. Anyone who compares it

[P01-180]carefully with the original will have little doubt that it is a

[P01-181]concoction from Shelton and the French of Filleau de Saint Martin, eked

[P01-182]out by borrowings from Phillips, whose mode of treatment it adopts. It

[P01-183]is, to be sure, more decent and decorous, but it treats “Don Quixote”

[P01-184]in the same fashion as a comic book that cannot be made too comic.

[P01-185]To attempt to improve the humour of “Don Quixote” by an infusion of

[P01-186]cockney flippancy and facetiousness, as Motteux’s operators did, is not

[P01-187]merely an impertinence like larding a sirloin of prize beef, but an

[P01-188]absolute falsification of the spirit of the book, and it is a proof of

[P01-189]the uncritical way in which “Don Quixote” is generally read that this

[P01-190]worse than worthless translation—worthless as failing to represent,

[P01-191]worse than worthless as misrepresenting—should have been favoured as it

[P01-192]has been.

[P01-193]It had the effect, however, of bringing out a translation undertaken

[P01-194]and executed in a very different spirit, that of Charles Jervas, the

[P01-195]portrait painter, and friend of Pope, Swift, Arbuthnot, and Gay. Jervas

[P01-196]has been allowed little credit for his work, indeed it may be said

[P01-197]none, for it is known to the world in general as Jarvis’s. It was not

[P01-198]published until after his death, and the printers gave the name

[P01-199]according to the current pronunciation of the day. It has been the most

[P01-200]freely used and the most freely abused of all the translations. It has

[P01-201]seen far more editions than any other, it is admitted on all hands to

[P01-202]be by far the most faithful, and yet nobody seems to have a good word

[P01-203]to say for it or for its author. Jervas no doubt prejudiced readers

[P01-204]against himself in his preface, where among many true words about

[P01-205]Shelton, Stevens, and Motteux, he rashly and unjustly charges Shelton

[P01-206]with having translated not from the Spanish, but from the Italian

[P01-207]version of Franciosini, which did not appear until ten years after

[P01-208]Shelton’s first volume. A suspicion of incompetence, too, seems to have

[P01-209]attached to him because he was by profession a painter and a mediocre

[P01-210]one (though he has given us the best portrait we have of Swift), and

[P01-211]this may have been strengthened by Pope’s remark that he “translated

[P01-212]‘Don Quixote’ without understanding Spanish.” He has been also charged

[P01-213]with borrowing from Shelton, whom he disparaged. It is true that in a

[P01-214]few difficult or obscure passages he has followed Shelton, and gone

[P01-215]astray with him; but for one case of this sort, there are fifty where

[P01-216]he is right and Shelton wrong. As for Pope’s dictum, anyone who

[P01-217]examines Jervas’s version carefully, side by side with the original,

[P01-218]will see that he was a sound Spanish scholar, incomparably a better one

[P01-219]than Shelton, except perhaps in mere colloquial Spanish. He was, in

[P01-220]fact, an honest, faithful, and painstaking translator, and he has left

[P01-221]a version which, whatever its shortcomings may be, is singularly free

[P01-222]from errors and mistranslations.

[P01-223]The charge against it is that it is stiff, dry—“wooden” in a word,—and

[P01-224]no one can deny that there is a foundation for it. But it may be

[P01-225]pleaded for Jervas that a good deal of this rigidity is due to his

[P01-226]abhorrence of the light, flippant, jocose style of his predecessors. He

[P01-227]was one of the few, very few, translators that have shown any

[P01-228]apprehension of the unsmiling gravity which is the essence of Quixotic

[P01-229]humour; it seemed to him a crime to bring Cervantes forward smirking

[P01-230]and grinning at his own good things, and to this may be attributed in a

[P01-231]great measure the ascetic abstinence from everything savouring of

[P01-232]liveliness which is the characteristic of his translation. In most

[P01-233]modern editions, it should be observed, his style has been smoothed and

[P01-234]smartened, but without any reference to the original Spanish, so that

[P01-235]if he has been made to read more agreeably he has also been robbed of

[P01-236]his chief merit of fidelity.

[P01-237]Smollett’s version, published in 1755, may be almost counted as one of

[P01-238]these. At any rate it is plain that in its construction Jervas’s

[P01-239]translation was very freely drawn upon, and very little or probably no

[P01-240]heed given to the original Spanish.

[P01-241]The later translations may be dismissed in a few words. George Kelly’s,

[P01-242]which appeared in 1769, “printed for the Translator,” was an impudent

[P01-243]imposture, being nothing more than Motteux’s version with a few of the

[P01-244]words, here and there, artfully transposed; Charles Wilmot’s (1774) was

[P01-245]only an abridgment like Florian’s, but not so skilfully executed; and

[P01-246]the version published by Miss Smirke in 1818, to accompany her

[P01-247]brother’s plates, was merely a patchwork production made out of former

[P01-248]translations. On the latest, Mr. A. J. Duffield’s, it would be in every

[P01-249]sense of the word impertinent in me to offer an opinion here. I had not

[P01-250]even seen it when the present undertaking was proposed to me, and since

[P01-251]then I may say vidi tantum, having for obvious reasons resisted the

[P01-252]temptation which Mr. Duffield’s reputation and comely volumes hold out

[P01-253]to every lover of Cervantes.

[P01-254]From the foregoing history of our translations of “Don Quixote,” it

[P01-255]will be seen that there are a good many people who, provided they get

[P01-256]the mere narrative with its full complement of facts, incidents, and

[P01-257]adventures served up to them in a form that amuses them, care very

[P01-258]little whether that form is the one in which Cervantes originally

[P01-259]shaped his ideas. On the other hand, it is clear that there are many

[P01-260]who desire to have not merely the story he tells, but the story as he

[P01-261]tells it, so far at least as differences of idiom and circumstances

[P01-262]permit, and who will give a preference to the conscientious translator,

[P01-263]even though he may have acquitted himself somewhat awkwardly.

[P01-264]But after all there is no real antagonism between the two classes;

[P01-265]there is no reason why what pleases the one should not please the

[P01-266]other, or why a translator who makes it his aim to treat “Don Quixote”

[P01-267]with the respect due to a great classic, should not be as acceptable

[P01-268]even to the careless reader as the one who treats it as a famous old

[P01-269]jest-book. It is not a question of caviare to the general, or, if it

[P01-270]is, the fault rests with him who makes so. The method by which

[P01-271]Cervantes won the ear of the Spanish people ought, mutatis mutandis, to

[P01-272]be equally effective with the great majority of English readers. At any

[P01-273]rate, even if there are readers to whom it is a matter of indifference,

[P01-274]fidelity to the method is as much a part of the translator’s duty as

[P01-275]fidelity to the matter. If he can please all parties, so much the

[P01-276]better; but his first duty is to those who look to him for as faithful

[P01-277]a representation of his author as it is in his power to give them,

[P01-278]faithful to the letter so long as fidelity is practicable, faithful to

[P01-279]the spirit so far as he can make it.

[P01-280]My purpose here is not to dogmatise on the rules of translation, but to

[P01-281]indicate those I have followed, or at least tried to the best of my

[P01-282]ability to follow, in the present instance. One which, it seems to me,

[P01-283]cannot be too rigidly followed in translating “Don Quixote,” is to

[P01-284]avoid everything that savours of affectation. The book itself is,

[P01-285]indeed, in one sense a protest against it, and no man abhorred it more

[P01-286]than Cervantes. For this reason, I think, any temptation to use

[P01-287]antiquated or obsolete language should be resisted. It is after all an

[P01-288]affectation, and one for which there is no warrant or excuse. Spanish

[P01-289]has probably undergone less change since the seventeenth century than

[P01-290]any language in Europe, and by far the greater and certainly the best

[P01-291]part of “Don Quixote” differs but little in language from the

[P01-292]colloquial Spanish of the present day. Except in the tales and Don

[P01-293]Quixote’s speeches, the translator who uses the simplest and plainest

[P01-294]everyday language will almost always be the one who approaches nearest

[P01-295]to the original.

[P01-296]Seeing that the story of “Don Quixote” and all its characters and

[P01-297]incidents have now been for more than two centuries and a half familiar

[P01-298]as household words in English mouths, it seems to me that the old

[P01-299]familiar names and phrases should not be changed without good reason.

[P01-300]Of course a translator who holds that “Don Quixote” should receive the

[P01-301]treatment a great classic deserves, will feel himself bound by the

[P01-302]injunction laid upon the Morisco in Chap. IX not to omit or add

[P01-303]anything.

CERVANTES

[P01-304]Four generations had laughed over “Don Quixote” before it occurred to

[P01-305]anyone to ask, who and what manner of man was this Miguel de Cervantes

[P01-306]Saavedra whose name is on the title-page; and it was too late for a

[P01-307]satisfactory answer to the question when it was proposed to add a life

[P01-308]of the author to the London edition published at Lord Carteret’s

[P01-309]instance in 1738. All traces of the personality of Cervantes had by

[P01-310]that time disappeared. Any floating traditions that may once have

[P01-311]existed, transmitted from men who had known him, had long since died

[P01-312]out, and of other record there was none; for the sixteenth and

[P01-313]seventeenth centuries were incurious as to “the men of the time,” a

[P01-314]reproach against which the nineteenth has, at any rate, secured itself,

[P01-315]if it has produced no Shakespeare or Cervantes. All that Mayans y

[P01-316]Siscar, to whom the task was entrusted, or any of those who followed

[P01-317]him, Rios, Pellicer, or Navarrete, could do was to eke out the few

[P01-318]allusions Cervantes makes to himself in his various prefaces with such

[P01-319]pieces of documentary evidence bearing upon his life as they could

[P01-320]find.

[P01-321]This, however, has been done by the last-named biographer to such good

[P01-322]purpose that he has superseded all predecessors. Thoroughness is the

[P01-323]chief characteristic of Navarrete’s work. Besides sifting, testing, and

[P01-324]methodising with rare patience and judgment what had been previously

[P01-325]brought to light, he left, as the saying is, no stone unturned under

[P01-326]which anything to illustrate his subject might possibly be found.

[P01-327]Navarrete has done all that industry and acumen could do, and it is no

[P01-328]fault of his if he has not given us what we want. What Hallam says of

[P01-329]Shakespeare may be applied to the almost parallel case of Cervantes:

[P01-330]“It is not the register of his baptism, or the draft of his will, or

[P01-331]the orthography of his name that we seek; no letter of his writing, no

[P01-332]record of his conversation, no character of him drawn ... by a

[P01-333]contemporary has been produced.”

[P01-334]It is only natural, therefore, that the biographers of Cervantes,

[P01-335]forced to make brick without straw, should have recourse largely to

[P01-336]conjecture, and that conjecture should in some instances come by

[P01-337]degrees to take the place of established fact. All that I propose to do

[P01-338]here is to separate what is matter of fact from what is matter of

[P01-339]conjecture, and leave it to the reader’s judgment to decide whether the

[P01-340]data justify the inference or not.

[P01-341]The men whose names by common consent stand in the front rank of

[P01-342]Spanish literature, Cervantes, Lope de Vega, Quevedo, Calderon,

[P01-343]Garcilaso de la Vega, the Mendozas, Gongora, were all men of ancient

[P01-344]families, and, curiously, all, except the last, of families that traced

[P01-345]their origin to the same mountain district in the North of Spain. The

[P01-346]family of Cervantes is commonly said to have been of Galician origin,

[P01-347]and unquestionably it was in possession of lands in Galicia at a very

[P01-348]early date; but I think the balance of the evidence tends to show that

[P01-349]the “solar,” the original site of the family, was at Cervatos in the

[P01-350]north-west corner of Old Castile, close to the junction of Castile,

[P01-351]Leon, and the Asturias. As it happens, there is a complete history of

[P01-352]the Cervantes family from the tenth century down to the seventeenth

[P01-353]extant under the title of “Illustrious Ancestry, Glorious Deeds, and

[P01-354]Noble Posterity of the Famous Nuno Alfonso, Alcaide of Toledo,” written

[P01-355]in 1648 by the industrious genealogist Rodrigo Mendez Silva, who

[P01-356]availed himself of a manuscript genealogy by Juan de Mena, the poet

[P01-357]laureate and historiographer of John II.

[P01-358]The origin of the name Cervantes is curious. Nuno Alfonso was almost as

[P01-359]distinguished in the struggle against the Moors in the reign of Alfonso

[P01-360]VII as the Cid had been half a century before in that of Alfonso VI,

[P01-361]and was rewarded by divers grants of land in the neighbourhood of

[P01-362]Toledo. On one of his acquisitions, about two leagues from the city, he

[P01-363]built himself a castle which he called Cervatos, because “he was lord

[P01-364]of the solar of Cervatos in the Montana,” as the mountain region

[P01-365]extending from the Basque Provinces to Leon was always called. At his

[P01-366]death in battle in 1143, the castle passed by his will to his son

[P01-367]Alfonso Munio, who, as territorial or local surnames were then coming

[P01-368]into vogue in place of the simple patronymic, took the additional name

[P01-369]of Cervatos. His eldest son Pedro succeeded him in the possession of

[P01-370]the castle, and followed his example in adopting the name, an

[P01-371]assumption at which the younger son, Gonzalo, seems to have taken

[P01-372]umbrage.

[P01-373]Everyone who has paid even a flying visit to Toledo will remember the

[P01-374]ruined castle that crowns the hill above the spot where the bridge of

[P01-375]Alcántara spans the gorge of the Tagus, and with its broken outline and

[P01-376]crumbling walls makes such an admirable pendant to the square solid

[P01-377]Alcazar towering over the city roofs on the opposite side. It was

[P01-378]built, or as some say restored, by Alfonso VI shortly after his

[P01-379]occupation of Toledo in 1085, and called by him San Servando after a

[P01-380]Spanish martyr, a name subsequently modified into San Servan (in which

[P01-381]form it appears in the “Poem of the Cid”), San Servantes, and San

[P01-382]Cervantes: with regard to which last the “Handbook for Spain” warns its

[P01-383]readers against the supposition that it has anything to do with the

[P01-384]author of “Don Quixote.” Ford, as all know who have taken him for a

[P01-385]companion and counsellor on the roads of Spain, is seldom wrong in

[P01-386]matters of literature or history. In this instance, however, he is in

[P01-387]error. It has everything to do with the author of “Don Quixote,” for it

[P01-388]is in fact these old walls that have given to Spain the name she is

[P01-389]proudest of to-day. Gonzalo, above mentioned, it may be readily

[P01-390]conceived, did not relish the appropriation by his brother of a name to

[P01-391]which he himself had an equal right, for though nominally taken from

[P01-392]the castle, it was in reality derived from the ancient territorial

[P01-393]possession of the family, and as a set-off, and to distinguish himself

[P01-394](diferenciarse) from his brother, he took as a surname the name of the

[P01-395]castle on the bank of the Tagus, in the building of which, according to

[P01-396]a family tradition, his great-grandfather had a share.

[P01-397]Both brothers founded families. The Cervantes branch had more tenacity;

[P01-398]it sent offshoots in various directions, Andalusia, Estremadura,

[P01-399]Galicia, and Portugal, and produced a goodly line of men distinguished

[P01-400]in the service of Church and State. Gonzalo himself, and apparently a

[P01-401]son of his, followed Ferdinand III in the great campaign of 1236-48

[P01-402]that gave Cordova and Seville to Christian Spain and penned up the

[P01-403]Moors in the kingdom of Granada, and his descendants intermarried with

[P01-404]some of the noblest families of the Peninsula and numbered among them

[P01-405]soldiers, magistrates, and Church dignitaries, including at least two

[P01-406]cardinal-archbishops.

[P01-407]Of the line that settled in Andalusia, Deigo de Cervantes, Commander of

[P01-408]the Order of Santiago, married Juana Avellaneda, daughter of Juan Arias

[P01-409]de Saavedra, and had several sons, of whom one was Gonzalo Gomez,

[P01-410]Corregidor of Jerez and ancestor of the Mexican and Columbian branches

[P01-411]of the family; and another, Juan, whose son Rodrigo married Doña Leonor

[P01-412]de Cortinas, and by her had four children, Rodrigo, Andrea, Luisa, and

[P01-413]Miguel, our author.

[P01-414]The pedigree of Cervantes is not without its bearing on “Don Quixote.”

[P01-415]A man who could look back upon an ancestry of genuine knights-errant

[P01-416]extending from well-nigh the time of Pelayo to the siege of Granada was

[P01-417]likely to have a strong feeling on the subject of the sham chivalry of

[P01-418]the romances. It gives a point, too, to what he says in more than one

[P01-419]place about families that have once been great and have tapered away

[P01-420]until they have come to nothing, like a pyramid. It was the case of his

[P01-421]own.

[P01-422]He was born at Alcalá de Henares and baptised in the church of Santa

[P01-423]Maria Mayor on the 9th of October, 1547. Of his boyhood and youth we

[P01-424]know nothing, unless it be from the glimpse he gives us in the preface

[P01-425]to his “Comedies” of himself as a boy looking on with delight while

[P01-426]Lope de Rueda and his company set up their rude plank stage in the

[P01-427]plaza and acted the rustic farces which he himself afterwards took as

[P01-428]the model of his interludes. This first glimpse, however, is a

[P01-429]significant one, for it shows the early development of that love of the

[P01-430]drama which exercised such an influence on his life and seems to have

[P01-431]grown stronger as he grew older, and of which this very preface,

[P01-432]written only a few months before his death, is such a striking proof.

[P01-433]He gives us to understand, too, that he was a great reader in his

[P01-434]youth; but of this no assurance was needed, for the First Part of “Don

[P01-435]Quixote” alone proves a vast amount of miscellaneous reading, romances

[P01-436]of chivalry, ballads, popular poetry, chronicles, for which he had no

[P01-437]time or opportunity except in the first twenty years of his life; and

[P01-438]his misquotations and mistakes in matters of detail are always, it may

[P01-439]be noticed, those of a man recalling the reading of his boyhood.

[P01-440]Other things besides the drama were in their infancy when Cervantes was

[P01-441]a boy. The period of his boyhood was in every way a transition period

[P01-442]for Spain. The old chivalrous Spain had passed away. The new Spain was

[P01-443]the mightiest power the world had seen since the Roman Empire and it

[P01-444]had not yet been called upon to pay the price of its greatness. By the

[P01-445]policy of Ferdinand and Ximenez the sovereign had been made absolute,

[P01-446]and the Church and Inquisition adroitly adjusted to keep him so. The

[P01-447]nobles, who had always resisted absolutism as strenuously as they had

[P01-448]fought the Moors, had been divested of all political power, a like fate

[P01-449]had befallen the cities, the free constitutions of Castile and Aragon

[P01-450]had been swept away, and the only function that remained to the Cortés

[P01-451]was that of granting money at the King’s dictation.

[P01-452]The transition extended to literature. Men who, like Garcilaso de la

[P01-453]Vega and Diego Hurtado de Mendoza, followed the Italian wars, had

[P01-454]brought back from Italy the products of the post-Renaissance

[P01-455]literature, which took root and flourished and even threatened to

[P01-456]extinguish the native growths. Damon and Thyrsis, Phyllis and Chloe had

[P01-457]been fairly naturalised in Spain, together with all the devices of

[P01-458]pastoral poetry for investing with an air of novelty the idea of a

[P01-459]dispairing shepherd and inflexible shepherdess. As a set-off against

[P01-460]this, the old historical and traditional ballads, and the true

[P01-461]pastorals, the songs and ballads of peasant life, were being collected

[P01-462]assiduously and printed in the cancioneros that succeeded one another

[P01-463]with increasing rapidity. But the most notable consequence, perhaps, of

[P01-464]the spread of printing was the flood of romances of chivalry that had

[P01-465]continued to pour from the press ever since Garci Ordoñez de Montalvo

[P01-466]had resuscitated “Amadis of Gaul” at the beginning of the century.

[P01-467]For a youth fond of reading, solid or light, there could have been no

[P01-468]better spot in Spain than Alcalá de Henares in the middle of the

[P01-469]sixteenth century. It was then a busy, populous university town,

[P01-470]something more than the enterprising rival of Salamanca, and altogether

[P01-471]a very different place from the melancholy, silent, deserted Alcalá the

[P01-472]traveller sees now as he goes from Madrid to Saragossa. Theology and

[P01-473]medicine may have been the strong points of the university, but the

[P01-474]town itself seems to have inclined rather to the humanities and light

[P01-475]literature, and as a producer of books Alcalá was already beginning to

[P01-476]compete with the older presses of Toledo, Burgos, Salamanca and

[P01-477]Seville.

[P01-478]A pendant to the picture Cervantes has given us of his first playgoings

[P01-479]might, no doubt, have been often seen in the streets of Alcalá at that

[P01-480]time; a bright, eager, tawny-haired boy peering into a book-shop where

[P01-481]the latest volumes lay open to tempt the public, wondering, it may be,

[P01-482]what that little book with the woodcut of the blind beggar and his boy,

[P01-483]that called itself “Vida de Lazarillo de Tormes, segunda impresion,”

[P01-484]could be about; or with eyes brimming over with merriment gazing at one

[P01-485]of those preposterous portraits of a knight-errant in outrageous

[P01-486]panoply and plumes with which the publishers of chivalry romances loved

[P01-487]to embellish the title-pages of their folios. If the boy was the father

[P01-488]of the man, the sense of the incongruous that was strong at fifty was

[P01-489]lively at ten, and some such reflections as these may have been the

[P01-490]true genesis of “Don Quixote.”

[P01-491]For his more solid education, we are told, he went to Salamanca. But

[P01-492]why Rodrigo de Cervantes, who was very poor, should have sent his son

[P01-493]to a university a hundred and fifty miles away when he had one at his

[P01-494]own door, would be a puzzle, if we had any reason for supposing that he

[P01-495]did so. The only evidence is a vague statement by Professor Tomas

[P01-496]Gonzalez, that he once saw an old entry of the matriculation of a

[P01-497]Miguel de Cervantes. This does not appear to have been ever seen again;

[P01-498]but even if it had, and if the date corresponded, it would prove

[P01-499]nothing, as there were at least two other Miguels born about the middle

[P01-500]of the century; one of them, moreover, a Cervantes Saavedra, a cousin,

[P01-501]no doubt, who was a source of great embarrassment to the biographers.

[P01-502]That he was a student neither at Salamanca nor at Alcalá is best proved

[P01-503]by his own works. No man drew more largely upon experience than he did,

[P01-504]and he has nowhere left a single reminiscence of student life—for the

[P01-505]“Tia Fingida,” if it be his, is not one—nothing, not even “a college

[P01-506]joke,” to show that he remembered days that most men remember best. All

[P01-507]that we know positively about his education is that Juan Lopez de

[P01-508]Hoyos, a professor of humanities and belles-lettres of some eminence,

[P01-509]calls him his “dear and beloved pupil.” This was in a little collection

[P01-510]of verses by different hands on the death of Isabel de Valois, second

[P01-511]queen of Philip II., published by the professor in 1569, to which

[P01-512]Cervantes contributed four pieces, including an elegy, and an epitaph

[P01-513]in the form of a sonnet. It is only by a rare chance that a “Lycidas”

[P01-514]finds its way into a volume of this sort, and Cervantes was no Milton.

[P01-515]His verses are no worse than such things usually are; so much, at

[P01-516]least, may be said for them.

[P01-517]By the time the book appeared he had left Spain, and, as fate ordered

[P01-518]it, for twelve years, the most eventful ones of his life. Giulio,

[P01-519]afterwards Cardinal, Acquaviva had been sent at the end of 1568 to

[P01-520]Philip II. by the Pope on a mission, partly of condolence, partly

[P01-521]political, and on his return to Rome, which was somewhat brusquely

[P01-522]expedited by the King, he took Cervantes with him as his camarero

[P01-523](chamberlain), the office he himself held in the Pope’s household. The

[P01-524]post would no doubt have led to advancement at the Papal Court had

[P01-525]Cervantes retained it, but in the summer of 1570 he resigned it and

[P01-526]enlisted as a private soldier in Captain Diego Urbina’s company,

[P01-527]belonging to Don Miguel de Moncada’s regiment, but at that time forming

[P01-528]a part of the command of Marc Antony Colonna. What impelled him to this

[P01-529]step we know not, whether it was distaste for the career before him, or

[P01-530]purely military enthusiasm. It may well have been the latter, for it

[P01-1060]Nine editions of the First Part of “Don Quixote” had already appeared

[P01-1061]before Cervantes died, thirty thousand copies in all, according to his

[P01-1062]own estimate, and a tenth was printed at Barcelona the year after his

[P01-1063]death. So large a number naturally supplied the demand for some time,

[P01-1064]but by 1634 it appears to have been exhausted; and from that time down

[P01-1065]to the present day the stream of editions has continued to flow rapidly

[P01-1066]and regularly. The translations show still more clearly in what request

[P01-1067]the book has been from the very outset. In seven years from the

[P01-1068]completion of the work it had been translated into the four leading

[P01-1069]languages of Europe. Except the Bible, in fact, no book has been so

[P01-1070]widely diffused as “Don Quixote.” The “Imitatio Christi” may have been

[P01-1071]translated into as many different languages, and perhaps “Robinson

[P01-1072]Crusoe” and the “Vicar of Wakefield” into nearly as many, but in

[P01-1073]multiplicity of translations and editions “Don Quixote” leaves them all

[P01-1074]far behind.

[P01-1075]Still more remarkable is the character of this wide diffusion. “Don

[P01-1076]Quixote” has been thoroughly naturalised among people whose ideas about

[P01-1077]knight-errantry, if they had any at all, were of the vaguest, who had

[P01-1078]never seen or heard of a book of chivalry, who could not possibly feel

[P01-1079]the humour of the burlesque or sympathise with the author’s purpose.

[P01-1080]Another curious fact is that this, the most cosmopolitan book in the

[P01-1081]world, is one of the most intensely national. “Manon Lescaut” is not

[P01-1082]more thoroughly French, “Tom Jones” not more English, “Rob Roy” not

[P01-1083]more Scotch, than “Don Quixote” is Spanish, in character, in ideas, in

[P01-1084]sentiment, in local colour, in everything. What, then, is the secret of

[P01-1085]this unparalleled popularity, increasing year by year for well-nigh

[P01-1086]three centuries? One explanation, no doubt, is that of all the books in

[P01-1087]the world, “Don Quixote” is the most catholic. There is something in it

[P01-1088]for every sort of reader, young or old, sage or simple, high or low. As

[P01-1089]Cervantes himself says with a touch of pride, “It is thumbed and read

[P01-1090]and got by heart by people of all sorts; the children turn its leaves,

[P01-1091]the young people read it, the grown men understand it, the old folk

[P01-1092]praise it.”

[P01-1093]But it would be idle to deny that the ingredient which, more than its

[P01-1094]humour, or its wisdom, or the fertility of invention or knowledge of

[P01-1095]human nature it displays, has insured its success with the multitude,

[P01-1096]is the vein of farce that runs through it. It was the attack upon the

[P01-1097]sheep, the battle with the wine-skins, Mambrino’s helmet, the balsam of

[P01-1098]Fierabras, Don Quixote knocked over by the sails of the windmill,

[P01-1099]Sancho tossed in the blanket, the mishaps and misadventures of master

[P01-1100]and man, that were originally the great attraction, and perhaps are so

[P01-1101]still to some extent with the majority of readers. It is plain that

[P01-1102]“Don Quixote” was generally regarded at first, and indeed in Spain for

[P01-1103]a long time, as little more than a queer droll book, full of laughable

[P01-1104]incidents and absurd situations, very amusing, but not entitled to much

[P01-1105]consideration or care. All the editions printed in Spain from 1637 to

[P01-1106]1771, when the famous printer Ibarra took it up, were mere trade

[P01-1107]editions, badly and carelessly printed on vile paper and got up in the

[P01-1108]style of chap-books intended only for popular use, with, in most

[P01-1109]instances, uncouth illustrations and clap-trap additions by the

[P01-1110]publisher.

[P01-1111]To England belongs the credit of having been the first country to

[P01-1112]recognise the right of “Don Quixote” to better treatment than this. The

[P01-1113]London edition of 1738, commonly called Lord Carteret’s from having

[P01-1114]been suggested by him, was not a mere _édition de luxe_. It produced

[P01-1115]“Don Quixote” in becoming form as regards paper and type, and

[P01-1116]embellished with plates which, if not particularly happy as

[P01-1117]illustrations, were at least well intentioned and well executed, but it

[P01-1118]also aimed at correctness of text, a matter to which nobody except the

[P01-1119]editors of the Valencia and Brussels editions had given even a passing

[P01-1120]thought; and for a first attempt it was fairly successful, for though

[P01-1121]some of its emendations are inadmissible, a good many of them have been

[P01-1122]adopted by all subsequent editors.

[P01-1124]remarkable change of sentiment with regard to “Don Quixote.” A vast

[P01-1125]number of its admirers began to grow ashamed of laughing over it. It

[P01-1126]became almost a crime to treat it as a humorous book. The humour was

[P01-1127]not entirely denied, but, according to the new view, it was rated as an

[P01-1128]altogether secondary quality, a mere accessory, nothing more than the

[P01-1129]stalking-horse under the presentation of which Cervantes shot his

[P01-1130]philosophy or his satire, or whatever it was he meant to shoot; for on

[P01-1132]he aimed at was not the books of chivalry. He said emphatically in the

[P01-1133]preface to the First Part and in the last sentence of the Second, that

[P01-1135]to advanced criticism, made it clear that his object must have been

[P01-1139]poetry and the spirit of prose; and perhaps German philosophy never

[P01-1148]never could see the facts before his eyes, and the troglodyte who could

[P01-1149]see nothing else. But to suppose Cervantes deliberately setting himself

[P01-1151]something not only very unlike the age in which he lived, but

[P01-1152]altogether unlike Cervantes himself, who would have been the first to

[P01-1154]The extraordinary influence of the romances of chivalry in his day is

[P01-1187]would have imagined the whole nation to have been nothing less than a

[P01-1188]race of knight-errants. But after the world became a little acquainted

[P01-1189]with that notable history, the man that was seen in that once

[P01-1190]celebrated drapery was pointed at as a Don Quixote, and found himself

[P01-1202]circumstances and consequences, is mischievous to its owner, and a very

[P01-1204]distinguish between the one kind and the other, no doubt “Don Quixote”

DEDICATION OF PART I

[P01-1907]TO THE DUKE OF BEJAR, MARQUIS OF GIBRALEON, COUNT OF BENALCAZAR AND

[P01-1908]BANARES, VICECOUNT OF THE PUEBLA DE ALCOCER, MASTER OF THE TOWNS OF

[P01-1909]CAPILLA, CURIEL AND BURGUILLOS

[P01-1910]In belief of the good reception and honours that Your Excellency

[P01-1911]bestows on all sort of books, as prince so inclined to favor good arts,

[P01-1912]chiefly those who by their nobleness do not submit to the service and

[P01-1913]bribery of the vulgar, I have determined bringing to light The

[P01-1914]Ingenious Gentleman Don Quixote of la Mancha, in shelter of Your

[P01-1915]Excellency’s glamorous name, to whom, with the obeisance I owe to such

[P01-1916]grandeur, I pray to receive it agreeably under his protection, so that

[P01-1917]in this shadow, though deprived of that precious ornament of elegance

[P01-1918]and erudition that clothe the works composed in the houses of those who

[P01-1919]know, it dares appear with assurance in the judgment of some who,

[P01-1920]trespassing the bounds of their own ignorance, use to condemn with more

[P01-1921]rigour and less justice the writings of others. It is my earnest hope

[P01-1922]that Your Excellency’s good counsel in regard to my honourable purpose,

[P01-1923]will not disdain the littleness of so humble a service.

[P01-1924]Miguel de Cervantes
