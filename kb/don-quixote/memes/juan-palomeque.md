# Juan Palomeque

**Juan Palomeque** is the innkeeper whose establishment becomes the narrative pressure-cooker of Part I. Over the course of a single night and day at his inn, the novel's four separated lovers reunite, the Captive tells his tale, Don Quixote battles wineskins in his sleep, and the Holy Brotherhood attempts an arrest — all under Palomeque's harried but generally good-natured supervision.

## Character Profile

Palomeque is a man of business rather than romance. He runs an inn — not a castle, as Don Quixote insists — and his primary concerns are practical: beds, meals, payment, and keeping the peace among an increasingly chaotic collection of guests. Unlike many innkeepers in picaresque literature, he is not portrayed as corrupt or predatory; he is simply a working man whose establishment has been invaded by an improbable concentration of aristocrats, lovers, soldiers, and madmen.

His wife and daughter assist in running the inn, and Palomeque's domestic life provides a grounding contrast to the high drama unfolding under his roof. He has literary tastes, too — he keeps a collection of chivalric romances, which the curate discovers and which leads to a discussion about the nature of fiction with the inn's assembled company.

## The Convergence

Palomeque's inn becomes the novel's great convergence point when, in rapid succession, the following characters arrive:

- **Don Quixote and Sancho Panza** — already guests
- **The Curate and the Barber** — in pursuit of Quixote
- **Cardenio** — the love-mad youth
- **Dorotea** — disguised as Princess Micomicona
- **Don Fernando and Luscinda** — the cause of everyone's misery
- **The Captive and Zoraida** — escaped from Algiers
- **The Judge** — the Captive's long-lost brother
- **Officers of the Holy Brotherhood** — investigating Quixote's crimes

The innkeeper manages this impossible situation with a combination of pragmatism and bemusement, providing food, wine, and beds while the aristocrats sort out their emotional entanglements.

## The Wineskin Battle

Palomeque's most famous moment comes when Don Quixote, dreaming that he is battling giants, attacks wineskins hanging near his bed, drenching the room in red wine:

> "'We want my friend's housekeeper and niece here now'"[032-097]

The innkeeper's exasperation at the ruined wine — a businessman's entirely reasonable response to a madman destroying his inventory — is one of the novel's most purely comic moments.

## Thematic Significance

Juan Palomeque represents the ordinary world that chivalric romance disrupts. He is neither villain nor hero — just a man trying to run an inn that has somehow become the stage for a dozen intersecting dramas. His very ordinariness makes him one of Cervantes's most sympathetic minor characters: the only sane man in a building full of people acting out stories.
