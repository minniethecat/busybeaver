# The Duke

**The Duke** (*el Duque*) is a central figure in Part II of *Don Quixote*. With his wife [[the-duchess|the Duchess]], he transforms Don Quixote's final adventure into an extended theatrical performance at his country estate. Having read the published account of the knight's first sally, the Duke recognizes Don Quixote and Sancho immediately and decides to turn their visit into a source of entertainment.

## Character Profile

The Duke is a wealthy nobleman with a country house, extensive hunting grounds, and a household staff large enough to stage elaborate productions. He is intelligent, well-read (having enjoyed Cide Hamete Benengeli's chronicle), and shares his wife's taste for refined cruelty disguised as hospitality.

Cervantes offers his sharpest satire of the aristocracy through the Duke: a man with every material comfort who amuses himself by manipulating the vulnerable. Unlike the innkeeper who plays along with Don Quixote's fantasies out of good-natured amusement, the Duke's games are calculated and often cruel.

## Recognition and Welcome

The Duke's first encounter with Don Quixote occurs during a boar hunt. Recognizing the gaunt knight and his round squire immediately, he stages an elaborate welcome:

> "Don Quixote of La Mancha," said the duke; "where my lady the duchess is, with the courtly hospitality she owes to all knights-errant that come her way"[082-121]

The Duke insists on treating Don Quixote with full chivalric ceremony — seating him at the head of the table, addressing him with formal titles, and assigning servants to wait on him exclusively. Every gesture is calculated to validate the knight's delusions and prolong the comedy:

> "So much the better," said the duke, "for many droll things cannot be said without the aid of the knight; let him be knighted, let Sir Knight of the Lions and his squire be our guests"[082-142]

## Sancho's Governorship

The Duke's most elaborate scheme is the granting of an "island" for Sancho to govern. Planting the suggestion casually ("the duke and I are wont to give to all who serve us well"), he arranges for Sancho to be installed as governor of Barataria — a town on his own estate. The entire episode is a scripted farce:

- The townspeople have been instructed to treat Sancho as a genuine governor while creating impossible situations
- Sancho is starved, terrorized with a mock invasion, and forced to adjudicate absurd legal disputes
- Throughout, the Duke receives reports and laughs at Sancho's expense

This cruelty is the novel's sharpest indictment of the leisure class. The Duke has everything — and uses it to torment a peasant for entertainment.

## Relationship with Don Quixote

The Duke's relationship with Don Quixote is entirely parasitic. He offers the knight everything his fantasy requires — a castle, titled hosts, solemn recognition of his chivalric identity — and extracts maximum entertainment from the resulting display. When Don Quixote departs, the Duke has experienced no transformation; he simply returns to his normal routine, having lost an amusing diversion.

## Thematic Significance

The Duke represents the corruption of power without purpose. Unlike Don Quixote, whose madness is directed toward justice and the defense of the weak, the Duke uses his considerable resources solely for his own amusement. The contrast is deliberate: the madman has better values than the sane nobleman.
