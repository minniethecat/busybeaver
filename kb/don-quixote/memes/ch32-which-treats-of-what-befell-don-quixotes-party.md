[032-001]WHICH TREATS OF WHAT BEFELL DON QUIXOTE’S PARTY AT THE INN

c32a.jpg (132K)


[032-002]Their dainty repast being finished, they saddled at once, and without

[032-003]any adventure worth mentioning they reached next day the inn, the

[032-004]object of Sancho Panza’s fear and dread; but though he would have

[032-005]rather not entered it, there was no help for it. The landlady, the

[032-006]landlord, their daughter, and Maritornes, when they saw Don Quixote and

[032-007]Sancho coming, went out to welcome them with signs of hearty

[032-008]satisfaction, which Don Quixote received with dignity and gravity, and

[032-009]bade them make up a better bed for him than the last time: to which the

[032-010]landlady replied that if he paid better than he did the last time she

[032-011]would give him one fit for a prince. Don Quixote said he would, so they

[032-012]made up a tolerable one for him in the same garret as before; and he

[032-013]lay down at once, being sorely shaken and in want of sleep.

[032-014]No sooner was the door shut upon him than the landlady made at the

[032-015]barber, and seizing him by the beard, said:

[032-016]“By my faith you are not going to make a beard of my tail any longer;

[032-017]you must give me back my tail, for it is a shame the way that thing of

[032-018]my husband’s goes tossing about on the floor; I mean the comb that I

[032-019]used to stick in my good tail.”

[032-020]But for all she tugged at it the barber would not give it up until the

[032-021]licentiate told him to let her have it, as there was now no further

[032-022]occasion for that stratagem, because he might declare himself and

[032-023]appear in his own character, and tell Don Quixote that he had fled to

[032-024]this inn when those thieves the galley slaves robbed him; and should he

[032-025]ask for the princess’s squire, they could tell him that she had sent

[032-026]him on before her to give notice to the people of her kingdom that she

[032-027]was coming, and bringing with her the deliverer of them all. On this

[032-028]the barber cheerfully restored the tail to the landlady, and at the

[032-029]same time they returned all the accessories they had borrowed to effect

[032-030]Don Quixote’s deliverance. All the people of the inn were struck with

[032-031]astonishment at the beauty of Dorothea, and even at the comely figure

[032-032]of the shepherd Cardenio. The curate made them get ready such fare as

[032-033]there was in the inn, and the landlord, in hope of better payment,

[032-034]served them up a tolerably good dinner. All this time Don Quixote was

[032-035]asleep, and they thought it best not to waken him, as sleeping would

[032-036]now do him more good than eating.

[032-037]While at dinner, the company consisting of the landlord, his wife,

[032-038]their daughter, Maritornes, and all the travellers, they discussed the

[032-039]strange craze of Don Quixote and the manner in which he had been found;

[032-040]and the landlady told them what had taken place between him and the

[032-041]carrier; and then, looking round to see if Sancho was there, when she

[032-042]saw he was not, she gave them the whole story of his blanketing, which

[032-043]they received with no little amusement. But on the curate observing

[032-044]that it was the books of chivalry which Don Quixote had read that had

[032-045]turned his brain, the landlord said:

[032-046]“I cannot understand how that can be, for in truth to my mind there is

[032-047]no better reading in the world, and I have here two or three of them,

[032-048]with other writings that are the very life, not only of myself but of

[032-049]plenty more; for when it is harvest-time, the reapers flock here on

[032-050]holidays, and there is always one among them who can read and who takes

[032-051]up one of these books, and we gather round him, thirty or more of us,

[032-052]and stay listening to him with a delight that makes our grey hairs grow

[032-053]young again. At least I can say for myself that when I hear of what

[032-054]furious and terrible blows the knights deliver, I am seized with the

[032-055]longing to do the same, and I would like to be hearing about them night

[032-056]and day.”

[032-057]“And I just as much,” said the landlady, “because I never have a quiet

[032-058]moment in my house except when you are listening to someone reading;

[032-059]for then you are so taken up that for the time being you forget to

[032-060]scold.”

[032-061]“That is true,” said Maritornes; “and, faith, I relish hearing these

[032-062]things greatly too, for they are very pretty; especially when they

[032-063]describe some lady or another in the arms of her knight under the

[032-064]orange trees, and the duenna who is keeping watch for them half dead

[032-065]with envy and fright; all this I say is as good as honey.”

[032-066]“And you, what do you think, young lady?” said the curate turning to

[032-067]the landlord’s daughter.

[032-068]“I don’t know indeed, señor,” said she; “I listen too, and to tell the

[032-069]truth, though I do not understand it, I like hearing it; but it is not

[032-070]the blows that my father likes that I like, but the laments the knights

[032-071]utter when they are separated from their ladies; and indeed they

[032-072]sometimes make me weep with the pity I feel for them.”

[032-073]“Then you would console them if it was for you they wept, young lady?”

[032-074]said Dorothea.

[032-075]“I don’t know what I should do,” said the girl; “I only know that there

[032-076]are some of those ladies so cruel that they call their knights tigers

[032-077]and lions and a thousand other foul names: and Jesus! I don’t know what

[032-078]sort of folk they can be, so unfeeling and heartless, that rather than

[032-079]bestow a glance upon a worthy man they leave him to die or go mad. I

[032-080]don’t know what is the good of such prudery; if it is for honour’s

[032-081]sake, why not marry them? That’s all they want.”

[032-082]“Hush, child,” said the landlady; “it seems to me thou knowest a great

[032-083]deal about these things, and it is not fit for girls to know or talk so

[032-084]much.”

[032-085]“As the gentleman asked me, I could not help answering him,” said the

[032-086]girl.

[032-087]“Well then,” said the curate, “bring me these books, señor landlord,

[032-088]for I should like to see them.”

[032-089]“With all my heart,” said he, and going into his own room he brought

[032-090]out an old valise secured with a little chain, on opening which the

[032-091]curate found in it three large books and some manuscripts written in a

[032-092]very good hand. The first that he opened he found to be “Don Cirongilio

[032-093]of Thrace,” and the second “Don Felixmarte of Hircania,” and the other

[032-094]the “History of the Great Captain Gonzalo Hernandez de Cordova, with

[032-095]the Life of Diego García de Paredes.”

[032-096]When the curate read the two first titles he looked over at the barber

[032-097]and said, “We want my friend’s housekeeper and niece here now.”

[032-098]“Nay,” said the barber, “I can do just as well to carry them to the

[032-099]yard or to the hearth, and there is a very good fire there.”

[032-100]“What! your worship would burn my books!” said the landlord.

[032-101]“Only these two,” said the curate, “Don Cirongilio, and Felixmarte.”

[032-102]“Are my books, then, heretics or phlegmatics that you want to burn

[032-103]them?” said the landlord.

[032-104]“Schismatics you mean, friend,” said the barber, “not phlegmatics.”

[032-105]“That’s it,” said the landlord; “but if you want to burn any, let it be

[032-106]that about the Great Captain and that Diego García; for I would rather

[032-107]have a child of mine burnt than either of the others.”

[032-108]“Brother,” said the curate, “those two books are made up of lies, and

[032-109]are full of folly and nonsense; but this of the Great Captain is a true

[032-110]history, and contains the deeds of Gonzalo Hernandez of Cordova, who by

[032-111]his many and great achievements earned the title all over the world of

[032-112]the Great Captain, a famous and illustrious name, and deserved by him

[032-113]alone; and this Diego García de Paredes was a distinguished knight of

[032-114]the city of Trujillo in Estremadura, a most gallant soldier, and of

[032-115]such bodily strength that with one finger he stopped a mill-wheel in

[032-116]full motion; and posted with a two-handed sword at the foot of a bridge

[032-117]he kept the whole of an immense army from passing over it, and achieved

[032-118]such other exploits that if, instead of his relating them himself with

[032-119]the modesty of a knight and of one writing his own history, some free

[032-120]and unbiased writer had recorded them, they would have thrown into the

[032-121]shade all the deeds of the Hectors, Achilleses, and Rolands.”

[032-122]“Tell that to my father,” said the landlord. “There’s a thing to be

[032-123]astonished at! Stopping a mill-wheel! By God your worship should read

[032-124]what I have read of Felixmarte of Hircania, how with one single

[032-125]backstroke he cleft five giants asunder through the middle as if they

[032-126]had been made of bean-pods like the little friars the children make;

[032-127]and another time he attacked a very great and powerful army, in which

[032-128]there were more than a million six hundred thousand soldiers, all armed

[032-129]from head to foot, and he routed them all as if they had been flocks of

[032-130]sheep.”

c32c.jpg (341K)


[032-131]“And then, what do you say to the good Cirongilio of Thrace, that was

[032-132]so stout and bold; as may be seen in the book, where it is related that

[032-133]as he was sailing along a river there came up out of the midst of the

[032-134]water against him a fiery serpent, and he, as soon as he saw it, flung

[032-135]himself upon it and got astride of its scaly shoulders, and squeezed

[032-136]its throat with both hands with such force that the serpent, finding he

[032-137]was throttling it, had nothing for it but to let itself sink to the

[032-138]bottom of the river, carrying with it the knight who would not let go

[032-139]his hold; and when they got down there he found himself among palaces

[032-140]and gardens so pretty that it was a wonder to see; and then the serpent

[032-141]changed itself into an old ancient man, who told him such things as

[032-142]were never heard. Hold your peace, señor; for if you were to hear this

[032-143]you would go mad with delight. A couple of figs for your Great Captain

[032-144]and your Diego García!”

[032-145]Hearing this Dorothea said in a whisper to Cardenio, “Our landlord is

[032-146]almost fit to play a second part to Don Quixote.”

[032-147]“I think so,” said Cardenio, “for, as he shows, he accepts it as a

[032-148]certainty that everything those books relate took place exactly as it

[032-149]is written down; and the barefooted friars themselves would not

[032-150]persuade him to the contrary.”

[032-151]“But consider, brother,” said the curate once more, “there never was

[032-152]any Felixmarte of Hircania in the world, nor any Cirongilio of Thrace,

[032-153]or any of the other knights of the same sort, that the books of

[032-154]chivalry talk of; the whole thing is the fabrication and invention of

[032-155]idle wits, devised by them for the purpose you describe of beguiling

[032-156]the time, as your reapers do when they read; for I swear to you in all

[032-157]seriousness there never were any such knights in the world, and no such

[032-158]exploits or nonsense ever happened anywhere.”

[032-159]“Try that bone on another dog,” said the landlord; “as if I did not

[032-160]know how many make five, and where my shoe pinches me; don’t think to

[032-161]feed me with pap, for by God I am no fool. It is a good joke for your

[032-162]worship to try and persuade me that everything these good books say is

[032-163]nonsense and lies, and they printed by the license of the Lords of the

[032-164]Royal Council, as if they were people who would allow such a lot of

[032-165]lies to be printed all together, and so many battles and enchantments

[032-166]that they take away one’s senses.”

[032-167]“I have told you, friend,” said the curate, “that this is done to

[032-168]divert our idle thoughts; and as in well-ordered states games of chess,

[032-169]fives, and billiards are allowed for the diversion of those who do not

[032-170]care, or are not obliged, or are unable to work, so books of this kind

[032-171]are allowed to be printed, on the supposition that, what indeed is the

[032-172]truth, there can be nobody so ignorant as to take any of them for true

[032-173]stories; and if it were permitted me now, and the present company

[032-174]desired it, I could say something about the qualities books of chivalry

[032-175]should possess to be good ones, that would be to the advantage and even

[032-176]to the taste of some; but I hope the time will come when I can

[032-177]communicate my ideas to someone who may be able to mend matters; and in

[032-178]the meantime, señor landlord, believe what I have said, and take your

[032-179]books, and make up your mind about their truth or falsehood, and much

[032-180]good may they do you; and God grant you may not fall lame of the same

[032-181]foot your guest Don Quixote halts on.”

[032-182]“No fear of that,” returned the landlord; “I shall not be so mad as to

[032-183]make a knight-errant of myself; for I see well enough that things are

[032-184]not now as they used to be in those days, when they say those famous

[032-185]knights roamed about the world.”

[032-186]Sancho had made his appearance in the middle of this conversation, and

[032-187]he was very much troubled and cast down by what he heard said about

[032-188]knights-errant being now no longer in vogue, and all books of chivalry

[032-189]being folly and lies; and he resolved in his heart to wait and see what

[032-190]came of this journey of his master’s, and if it did not turn out as

[032-191]happily as his master expected, he determined to leave him and go back

[032-192]to his wife and children and his ordinary labour.

[032-193]The landlord was carrying away the valise and the books, but the curate

[032-194]said to him, “Wait; I want to see what those papers are that are

[032-195]written in such a good hand.” The landlord taking them out handed them

[032-196]to him to read, and he perceived they were a work of about eight sheets

[032-197]of manuscript, with, in large letters at the beginning, the title of

[032-198]“Novel of the Ill-advised Curiosity.” The curate read three or four

[032-199]lines to himself, and said, “I must say the title of this novel does

[032-200]not seem to me a bad one, and I feel an inclination to read it all.” To

[032-201]which the landlord replied, “Then your reverence will do well to read

[032-202]it, for I can tell you that some guests who have read it here have been

[032-203]much pleased with it, and have begged it of me very earnestly; but I

[032-204]would not give it, meaning to return it to the person who forgot the

[032-205]valise, books, and papers here, for maybe he will return here some time

[032-206]or other; and though I know I shall miss the books, faith I mean to

[032-207]return them; for though I am an innkeeper, still I am a Christian.”

[032-208]“You are very right, friend,” said the curate; “but for all that, if

[032-209]the novel pleases me you must let me copy it.”

[032-210]“With all my heart,” replied the host.

[032-211]While they were talking Cardenio had taken up the novel and begun to

[032-212]read it, and forming the same opinion of it as the curate, he begged

[032-213]him to read it so that they might all hear it.

[032-214]“I would read it,” said the curate, “if the time would not be better

[032-215]spent in sleeping.”

[032-216]“It will be rest enough for me,” said Dorothea, “to while away the time

[032-217]by listening to some tale, for my spirits are not yet tranquil enough

[032-218]to let me sleep when it would be seasonable.”

[032-219]“Well then, in that case,” said the curate, “I will read it, if it were

[032-220]only out of curiosity; perhaps it may contain something pleasant.”

[032-221]Master Nicholas added his entreaties to the same effect, and Sancho

[032-222]too; seeing which, and considering that he would give pleasure to all,

[032-223]and receive it himself, the curate said, “Well then, attend to me

[032-224]everyone, for the novel begins thus.”
