# Ginés de Pasamonte

**Ginés de Pasamonte** is one of the most cunning and mobile minor characters in *Don Quixote*. First encountered as a galley slave in chains, he is freed by Don Quixote in the adventure of the galley slaves — only to later rob Sancho of his donkey, and still later to resurface in Part II as **Maese Pedro**, a puppeteer with a prophetic ape.

## The Galley Slave

Ginés makes his first appearance in chains, en route to the galleys. Unlike his fellow prisoners, he displays a sardonic self-awareness that immediately distinguishes him. When the commissary describes his crimes, Ginés corrects him with pedantic precision:

> "my family name is Pasamonte, not Parapilla as you say; let each one mind his own business and he will have enough to do"[022-182]

He is, by his own account, the author of his own autobiography — titled *The Life of Ginés de Pasamonte* — a metafictional joke that places him as a rival author within Cervantes's novel:

> "I am Ginés de Pasamonte, whose life is written by these fingers"[022-196]

When asked if the book is finished, his reply is a masterstroke of self-referential wit: "How can it be finished, when my life is not?"

## The Ingratitude

Don Quixote, inspired by a vision of knightly mercy, frees Ginés and the other galley slaves — only to be pelted with stones for his trouble. Ginés is the first to act:

> "Ginés de Pasamonte, who was the first to leap for joy at the freedom offered"[022-283]

The freed slaves immediately rob the knight and his squire, and Ginés later steals Sancho's donkey, Dapple. When Sancho discovers the theft, his grief is one of the novel's most genuinely moving moments — and Quixote's inability to remedy it exposes the limits of chivalric justice.

## Maese Pedro the Puppeteer

In Part II, Ginés resurfaces in a brilliant disguise: as **Maese Pedro**, a traveling puppeteer who travels with a "divining ape" that can supposedly answer questions about the past and present (but not the future). Don Quixote and Sancho encounter him at an inn, where Maese Pedro stages a puppet show about the rescue of Melisendra — a performance Don Quixote takes so literally that he attacks the puppets with his sword:

> "I remember well enough the Ginés de Pasamonte whom, with the other galley slaves, your worship set free"[079-013]

Ginés, recognizing Quixote, wisely keeps his distance — the criminal has more sense than the knight.

## Thematic Significance

Ginés de Pasamonte is the novel's supreme trickster figure. He is an author, an actor, a thief, a puppeteer — a man who creates and inhabits multiple identities. His autobiography, which he claims is better than *Lazarillo de Tormes*, makes him a competitor to Cervantes himself within the novel's universe. In a book obsessed with the relationship between life and narrative, Ginés embodies the claim that every life is a story — and every storyteller is, in some sense, a criminal.
