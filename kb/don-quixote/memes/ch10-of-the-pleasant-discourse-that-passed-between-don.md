OF THE PLEASANT DISCOURSE THAT PASSED BETWEEN DON QUIXOTE AND HIS

SQUIRE SANCHO PANZA

c10a.jpg (91K)


[010-001]Now by this time Sancho had risen, rather the worse for the handling of

[010-002]the friars’ muleteers, and stood watching the battle of his master, Don

[010-003]Quixote, and praying to God in his heart that it might be his will to

[010-004]grant him the victory, and that he might thereby win some island to

[010-005]make him governor of, as he had promised. Seeing, therefore, that the

[010-006]struggle was now over, and that his master was returning to mount

[010-007]Rocinante, he approached to hold the stirrup for him, and, before he

[010-008]could mount, he went on his knees before him, and taking his hand,

[010-009]kissed it saying, “May it please your worship, Señor Don Quixote, to

[010-010]give me the government of that island which has been won in this hard

[010-011]fight, for be it ever so big I feel myself in sufficient force to be

[010-012]able to govern it as much and as well as anyone in the world who has

[010-013]ever governed islands.”

[010-014]To which Don Quixote replied, “Thou must take notice, brother Sancho,

[010-015]that this adventure and those like it are not adventures of islands,

[010-016]but of cross-roads, in which nothing is got except a broken head or an

[010-017]ear the less: have patience, for adventures will present themselves

[010-018]from which I may make you, not only a governor, but something more.”

[010-019]Sancho gave him many thanks, and again kissing his hand and the skirt

[010-020]of his hauberk, helped him to mount Rocinante, and mounting his ass

[010-021]himself, proceeded to follow his master, who at a brisk pace, without

[010-022]taking leave, or saying anything further to the ladies belonging to the

[010-023]coach, turned into a wood that was hard by. Sancho followed him at his

[010-024]ass’s best trot, but Rocinante stepped out so that, seeing himself left

[010-025]behind, he was forced to call to his master to wait for him. Don

[010-026]Quixote did so, reining in Rocinante until his weary squire came up,

[010-027]who on reaching him said, “It seems to me, señor, it would be prudent

[010-028]in us to go and take refuge in some church, for, seeing how mauled he

[010-029]with whom you fought has been left, it will be no wonder if they give

[010-030]information of the affair to the Holy Brotherhood and arrest us, and,

[010-031]faith, if they do, before we come out of gaol we shall have to sweat

[010-032]for it.”

[010-033]“Peace,” said Don Quixote; “where hast thou ever seen or heard that a

[010-034]knight-errant has been arraigned before a court of justice, however

[010-035]many homicides he may have committed?”

[010-036]“I know nothing about omecils,” answered Sancho, “nor in my life have

[010-037]had anything to do with one; I only know that the Holy Brotherhood

[010-038]looks after those who fight in the fields, and in that other matter I

[010-039]do not meddle.”

[010-040]“Then thou needst have no uneasiness, my friend,” said Don Quixote,

[010-041]“for I will deliver thee out of the hands of the Chaldeans, much more

[010-042]out of those of the Brotherhood. But tell me, as thou livest, hast thou

[010-043]seen a more valiant knight than I in all the known world; hast thou

[010-044]read in history of any who has or had higher mettle in attack, more

[010-045]spirit in maintaining it, more dexterity in wounding or skill in

[010-046]overthrowing?”

[010-047]“The truth is,” answered Sancho, “that I have never read any history,

[010-048]for I can neither read nor write, but what I will venture to bet is

[010-049]that a more daring master than your worship I have never served in all

[010-050]the days of my life, and God grant that this daring be not paid for

[010-051]where I have said; what I beg of your worship is to dress your wound,

[010-052]for a great deal of blood flows from that ear, and I have here some

[010-053]lint and a little white ointment in the alforjas.”

[010-054]“All that might be well dispensed with,” said Don Quixote, “if I had

[010-055]remembered to make a vial of the balsam of Fierabras, for time and

[010-056]medicine are saved by one single drop.”

[010-057]“What vial and what balsam is that?” said Sancho Panza.

[010-058]“It is a balsam,” answered Don Quixote, “the receipt of which I have in

[010-059]my memory, with which one need have no fear of death, or dread dying of

[010-060]any wound; and so when I make it and give it to thee thou hast nothing

[010-061]to do when in some battle thou seest they have cut me in half through

[010-062]the middle of the body—as is wont to happen frequently—but neatly and

[010-063]with great nicety, ere the blood congeal, to place that portion of the

[010-064]body which shall have fallen to the ground upon the other half which

[010-065]remains in the saddle, taking care to fit it on evenly and exactly.

[010-066]Then thou shalt give me to drink but two drops of the balsam I have

[010-067]mentioned, and thou shalt see me become sounder than an apple.”

[010-068]“If that be so,” said Panza, “I renounce henceforth the government of

[010-069]the promised island, and desire nothing more in payment of my many and

[010-070]faithful services than that your worship give me the receipt of this

[010-071]supreme liquor, for I am persuaded it will be worth more than two reals

[010-072]an ounce anywhere, and I want no more to pass the rest of my life in

[010-073]ease and honour; but it remains to be told if it costs much to make

[010-074]it.”

[010-075]“With less than three reals, six quarts of it may be made,” said Don

[010-076]Quixote.

[010-077]“Sinner that I am!” said Sancho, “then why does your worship put off

[010-078]making it and teaching it to me?”

[010-079]“Peace, friend,” answered Don Quixote; “greater secrets I mean to teach

[010-080]thee and greater favours to bestow upon thee; and for the present let

[010-081]us see to the dressing, for my ear pains me more than I could wish.”

[010-082]Sancho took out some lint and ointment from the alforjas; but when Don

[010-083]Quixote came to see his helmet shattered, he was like to lose his

[010-084]senses, and clapping his hand upon his sword and raising his eyes to

[010-085]heaven, he said, “I swear by the Creator of all things and the four

[010-086]Gospels in their fullest extent, to do as the great Marquis of Mantua

[010-087]did when he swore to avenge the death of his nephew Baldwin (and that

[010-088]was not to eat bread from a table-cloth, nor embrace his wife, and

[010-089]other points which, though I cannot now call them to mind, I here grant

[010-090]as expressed) until I take complete vengeance upon him who has

[010-091]committed such an offence against me.”

[010-092]Hearing this, Sancho said to him, “Your worship should bear in mind,

[010-093]Señor Don Quixote, that if the knight has done what was commanded him

[010-094]in going to present himself before my lady Dulcinea del Toboso, he will

[010-095]have done all that he was bound to do, and does not deserve further

[010-096]punishment unless he commits some new offence.”

[010-097]“Thou hast said well and hit the point,” answered Don Quixote; and so I

[010-098]recall the oath in so far as relates to taking fresh vengeance on him,

[010-099]but I make and confirm it anew to lead the life I have said until such

[010-100]time as I take by force from some knight another helmet such as this

[010-101]and as good; and think not, Sancho, that I am raising smoke with straw

[010-102]in doing so, for I have one to imitate in the matter, since the very

[010-103]same thing to a hair happened in the case of Mambrino’s helmet, which

[010-104]cost Sacripante so dear.”

[010-105]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “let your worship send all such oaths to the

[010-106]devil, for they are very pernicious to salvation and prejudicial to the

[010-107]conscience; just tell me now, if for several days to come we fall in

[010-108]with no man armed with a helmet, what are we to do? Is the oath to be

[010-109]observed in spite of all the inconvenience and discomfort it will be to

[010-110]sleep in your clothes, and not to sleep in a house, and a thousand

[010-111]other mortifications contained in the oath of that old fool the Marquis

[010-112]of Mantua, which your worship is now wanting to revive? Let your

[010-113]worship observe that there are no men in armour travelling on any of

[010-114]these roads, nothing but carriers and carters, who not only do not wear

[010-115]helmets, but perhaps never heard tell of them all their lives.”

[010-116]“Thou art wrong there,” said Don Quixote, “for we shall not have been

[010-117]above two hours among these cross-roads before we see more men in

[010-118]armour than came to Albraca to win the fair Angelica.”

[010-119]“Enough,” said Sancho; “so be it then, and God grant us success, and

[010-120]that the time for winning that island which is costing me so dear may

[010-121]soon come, and then let me die.”

[010-122]“I have already told thee, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “not to give

[010-123]thyself any uneasiness on that score; for if an island should fail,

[010-124]there is the kingdom of Denmark, or of Sobradisa, which will fit thee

[010-125]as a ring fits the finger, and all the more that, being on _terra

[010-126]firma_, thou wilt all the better enjoy thyself. But let us leave that

[010-127]to its own time; see if thou hast anything for us to eat in those

[010-128]alforjas, because we must presently go in quest of some castle where we

[010-129]may lodge to-night and make the balsam I told thee of, for I swear to

[010-130]thee by God, this ear is giving me great pain.”

[010-131]“I have here an onion and a little cheese and a few scraps of bread,”

[010-132]said Sancho, “but they are not victuals fit for a valiant knight like

[010-133]your worship.”

[010-134]“How little thou knowest about it,” answered Don Quixote; “I would have

[010-135]thee to know, Sancho, that it is the glory of knights-errant to go

[010-136]without eating for a month, and even when they do eat, that it should

[010-137]be of what comes first to hand; and this would have been clear to thee

[010-138]hadst thou read as many histories as I have, for, though they are very

[010-139]many, among them all I have found no mention made of knights-errant

[010-140]eating, unless by accident or at some sumptuous banquets prepared for

[010-141]them, and the rest of the time they passed in dalliance. And though it

[010-142]is plain they could not do without eating and performing all the other

[010-143]natural functions, because, in fact, they were men like ourselves, it

[010-144]is plain too that, wandering as they did the most part of their lives

[010-145]through woods and wilds and without a cook, their most usual fare would

[010-146]be rustic viands such as those thou now offer me; so that, friend

[010-147]Sancho, let not that distress thee which pleases me, and do not seek to

[010-148]make a new world or pervert knight-errantry.”

[010-149]“Pardon me, your worship,” said Sancho, “for, as I cannot read or

[010-150]write, as I said just now, I neither know nor comprehend the rules of

[010-151]the profession of chivalry: henceforward I will stock the alforjas with

[010-152]every kind of dry fruit for your worship, as you are a knight; and for

[010-153]myself, as I am not one, I will furnish them with poultry and other

[010-154]things more substantial.”

[010-155]“I do not say, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “that it is imperative on

[010-156]knights-errant not to eat anything else but the fruits thou speakest

[010-157]of; only that their more usual diet must be those, and certain herbs

[010-158]they found in the fields which they knew and I know too.”

[010-159]“A good thing it is,” answered Sancho, “to know those herbs, for to my

[010-160]thinking it will be needful some day to put that knowledge into

[010-161]practice.”

[010-162]And here taking out what he said he had brought, the pair made their

[010-163]repast peaceably and sociably. But anxious to find quarters for the

[010-164]night, they with all despatch made an end of their poor dry fare,

[010-165]mounted at once, and made haste to reach some habitation before night

[010-166]set in; but daylight and the hope of succeeding in their object failed

[010-167]them close by the huts of some goatherds, so they determined to pass

[010-168]the night there, and it was as much to Sancho’s discontent not to have

[010-169]reached a house, as it was to his master’s satisfaction to sleep under

[010-170]the open heaven, for he fancied that each time this happened to him he

[010-171]performed an act of ownership that helped to prove his chivalry.
