OF WHAT HAPPENED SANCHO IN MAKING THE ROUND OF HIS ISLAND

[101-001]We left the great governor angered and irritated by that

[101-002]portrait-painting rogue of a farmer who, instructed by the majordomo,

[101-003]as the majordomo was by the duke, tried to practise upon him; he

[101-004]however, fool, boor, and clown as he was, held his own against them

[101-005]all, saying to those round him and to Doctor Pedro Recio, who as soon

[101-006]as the private business of the duke’s letter was disposed of had

[101-007]returned to the room, “Now I see plainly enough that judges and

[101-008]governors ought to be and must be made of brass not to feel the

[101-009]importunities of the applicants that at all times and all seasons

[101-010]insist on being heard, and having their business despatched, and their

[101-011]own affairs and no others attended to, come what may; and if the poor

[101-012]judge does not hear them and settle the matter—either because he cannot

[101-013]or because that is not the time set apart for hearing them—forthwith

[101-014]they abuse him, and run him down, and gnaw at his bones, and even pick

[101-015]holes in his pedigree. You silly, stupid applicant, don’t be in a

[101-016]hurry; wait for the proper time and season for doing business; don’t

[101-017]come at dinner-hour, or at bed-time; for judges are only flesh and

[101-018]blood, and must give to Nature what she naturally demands of them; all

[101-019]except myself, for in my case I give her nothing to eat, thanks to

[101-020]Señor Doctor Pedro Recio Tirteafuera here, who would have me die of

[101-021]hunger, and declares that death to be life; and the same sort of life

[101-022]may God give him and all his kind—I mean the bad doctors; for the good

[101-023]ones deserve palms and laurels.”

[101-024]All who knew Sancho Panza were astonished to hear him speak so

[101-025]elegantly, and did not know what to attribute it to unless it were that

[101-026]office and grave responsibility either smarten or stupefy men’s wits.

[101-027]At last Doctor Pedro Recio Agilers of Tirteafuera promised to let him

[101-028]have supper that night though it might be in contravention of all the

[101-029]aphorisms of Hippocrates. With this the governor was satisfied and

[101-030]looked forward to the approach of night and supper-time with great

[101-031]anxiety; and though time, to his mind, stood still and made no

[101-032]progress, nevertheless the hour he so longed for came, and they gave

[101-033]him a beef salad with onions and some boiled calves’ feet rather far

[101-034]gone. At this he fell to with greater relish than if they had given him

[101-035]francolins from Milan, pheasants from Rome, veal from Sorrento,

[101-036]partridges from Moron, or geese from Lavajos, and turning to the doctor

[101-037]at supper he said to him, “Look here, señor doctor, for the future

[101-038]don’t trouble yourself about giving me dainty things or choice dishes

[101-039]to eat, for it will be only taking my stomach off its hinges; it is

[101-040]accustomed to goat, cow, bacon, hung beef, turnips and onions; and if

[101-041]by any chance it is given these palace dishes, it receives them

[101-042]squeamishly, and sometimes with loathing. What the head-carver had best

[101-043]do is to serve me with what they call ollas podridas (and the rottener

[101-044]they are the better they smell); and he can put whatever he likes into

[101-045]them, so long as it is good to eat, and I’ll be obliged to him, and

[101-046]will requite him some day. But let nobody play pranks on me, for either

[101-047]we are or we are not; let us live and eat in peace and good-fellowship,

[101-048]for when God sends the dawn, he sends it for all. I mean to govern this

[101-049]island without giving up a right or taking a bribe; let everyone keep

[101-050]his eye open, and look out for the arrow; for I can tell them ‘the

[101-051]devil’s in Cantillana,’ and if they drive me to it they’ll see

[101-052]something that will astonish them. Nay! make yourself honey and the

[101-053]flies eat you.”

[101-054]“Of a truth, señor governor,” said the carver, “your worship is in the

[101-055]right of it in everything you have said; and I promise you in the name

[101-056]of all the inhabitants of this island that they will serve your worship

[101-057]with all zeal, affection, and good-will, for the mild kind of

[101-058]government you have given a sample of to begin with, leaves them no

[101-059]ground for doing or thinking anything to your worship’s disadvantage.”

[101-060]“That I believe,” said Sancho; “and they would be great fools if they

[101-061]did or thought otherwise; once more I say, see to my feeding and my

[101-062]Dapple’s for that is the great point and what is most to the purpose;

[101-063]and when the hour comes let us go the rounds, for it is my intention to

[101-064]purge this island of all manner of uncleanness and of all idle

[101-065]good-for-nothing vagabonds; for I would have you know that lazy idlers

[101-066]are the same thing in a State as the drones in a hive, that eat up the

[101-067]honey the industrious bees make. I mean to protect the husbandman, to

[101-068]preserve to the gentleman his privileges, to reward the virtuous, and

[101-069]above all to respect religion and honour its ministers. What say you to

[101-070]that, my friends? Is there anything in what I say, or am I talking to

[101-071]no purpose?”

[101-072]“There is so much in what your worship says, señor governor,” said the

[101-073]majordomo, “that I am filled with wonder when I see a man like your

[101-074]worship, entirely without learning (for I believe you have none at

[101-075]all), say such things, and so full of sound maxims and sage remarks,

[101-076]very different from what was expected of your worship’s intelligence by

[101-077]those who sent us or by us who came here. Every day we see something

[101-078]new in this world; jokes become realities, and the jokers find the

[101-079]tables turned upon them.”

[101-080]Night came, and with the permission of Doctor Pedro Recio, the governor

[101-081]had supper. They then got ready to go the rounds, and he started with

[101-082]the majordomo, the secretary, the head-carver, the chronicler charged

[101-083]with recording his deeds, and alguacils and notaries enough to form a

[101-084]fair-sized squadron. In the midst marched Sancho with his staff, as

[101-085]fine a sight as one could wish to see, and but a few streets of the

[101-086]town had been traversed when they heard a noise as of a clashing of

[101-087]swords. They hastened to the spot, and found that the combatants were

[101-088]but two, who seeing the authorities approaching stood still, and one of

[101-089]them exclaimed, “Help, in the name of God and the king! Are men to be

[101-090]allowed to rob in the middle of this town, and rush out and attack

[101-091]people in the very streets?”

[101-092]“Be calm, my good man,” said Sancho, “and tell me what the cause of

[101-093]this quarrel is; for I am the governor.”

[101-094]Said the other combatant, “Señor governor, I will tell you in a very

[101-095]few words. Your worship must know that this gentleman has just now won

[101-096]more than a thousand reals in that gambling house opposite, and God

[101-097]knows how. I was there, and gave more than one doubtful point in his

[101-098]favour, very much against what my conscience told me. He made off with

[101-099]his winnings, and when I made sure he was going to give me a crown or

[101-100]so at least by way of a present, as it is usual and customary to give

[101-101]men of quality of my sort who stand by to see fair or foul play, and

[101-102]back up swindles, and prevent quarrels, he pocketed his money and left

[101-103]the house. Indignant at this I followed him, and speaking to him fairly

[101-104]and civilly asked him to give me if it were only eight reals, for he

[101-105]knows I am an honest man and that I have neither profession nor

[101-106]property, for my parents never brought me up to any or left me any; but

[101-107]the rogue, who is a greater thief than Cacus and a greater sharper than

[101-108]Andradilla, would not give me more than four reals; so your worship may

[101-109]see how little shame and conscience he has. But by my faith if you had

[101-110]not come up I’d have made him disgorge his winnings, and he’d have

[101-111]learned what the range of the steel-yard was.”

[101-112]“What say you to this?” asked Sancho. The other replied that all his

[101-113]antagonist said was true, and that he did not choose to give him more

[101-114]than four reals because he very often gave him money; and that those

[101-115]who expected presents ought to be civil and take what is given them

[101-116]with a cheerful countenance, and not make any claim against winners

[101-117]unless they know them for certain to be sharpers and their winnings to

[101-118]be unfairly won; and that there could be no better proof that he

[101-119]himself was an honest man than his having refused to give anything; for

[101-120]sharpers always pay tribute to lookers-on who know them.

[101-121]“That is true,” said the majordomo; “let your worship consider what is

[101-122]to be done with these men.”

[101-123]“What is to be done,” said Sancho, “is this; you, the winner, be you

[101-124]good, bad, or indifferent, give this assailant of yours a hundred reals

[101-125]at once, and you must disburse thirty more for the poor prisoners; and

[101-126]you who have neither profession nor property, and hang about the island

[101-127]in idleness, take these hundred reals now, and some time of the day

[101-128]to-morrow quit the island under sentence of banishment for ten years,

[101-129]and under pain of completing it in another life if you violate the

[101-130]sentence, for I’ll hang you on a gibbet, or at least the hangman will

[101-131]by my orders; not a word from either of you, or I’ll make him feel my

[101-132]hand.”

[101-133]The one paid down the money and the other took it, and the latter

[101-134]quitted the island, while the other went home; and then the governor

[101-135]said, “Either I am not good for much, or I’ll get rid of these gambling

[101-136]houses, for it strikes me they are very mischievous.”

[101-137]“This one at least,” said one of the notaries, “your worship will not

[101-138]be able to get rid of, for a great man owns it, and what he loses every

[101-139]year is beyond all comparison more than what he makes by the cards. On

[101-140]the minor gambling houses your worship may exercise your power, and it

[101-141]is they that do most harm and shelter the most barefaced practices; for

[101-142]in the houses of lords and gentlemen of quality the notorious sharpers

[101-143]dare not attempt to play their tricks; and as the vice of gambling has

[101-144]become common, it is better that men should play in houses of repute

[101-145]than in some tradesman’s, where they catch an unlucky fellow in the

[101-146]small hours of the morning and skin him alive.”

[101-147]“I know already, notary, that there is a good deal to be said on that

[101-148]point,” said Sancho.

[101-149]And now a tipstaff came up with a young man in his grasp, and said,

[101-150]“Señor governor, this youth was coming towards us, and as soon as he

[101-151]saw the officers of justice he turned about and ran like a deer, a sure

[101-152]proof that he must be some evil-doer; I ran after him, and had it not

[101-153]been that he stumbled and fell, I should never have caught him.”

[101-154]“What did you run for, fellow?” said Sancho.

[101-155]To which the young man replied, “Señor, it was to avoid answering all

[101-156]the questions officers of justice put.”

[101-157]“What are you by trade?”

[101-158]“A weaver.”

[101-159]“And what do you weave?”

[101-160]“Lance heads, with your worship’s good leave.”

[101-161]“You’re facetious with me! You plume yourself on being a wag? Very

[101-162]good; and where were you going just now?”

[101-163]“To take the air, señor.”

[101-164]“And where does one take the air in this island?”

[101-165]“Where it blows.”

[101-166]“Good! your answers are very much to the point; you are a smart youth;

[101-167]but take notice that I am the air, and that I blow upon you a-stern,

[101-168]and send you to gaol. Ho there! lay hold of him and take him off; I’ll

[101-169]make him sleep there to-night without air.”

[101-170]“By God,” said the young man, “your worship will make me sleep in gaol

[101-171]just as soon as make me king.”

[101-172]“Why shan’t I make thee sleep in gaol?” said Sancho. “Have I not the

[101-173]power to arrest thee and release thee whenever I like?”

[101-174]“All the power your worship has,” said the young man, “won’t be able to

[101-175]make me sleep in gaol.”

[101-176]“How? not able!” said Sancho; “take him away at once where he’ll see

[101-177]his mistake with his own eyes, even if the gaoler is willing to exert

[101-178]his interested generosity on his behalf; for I’ll lay a penalty of two

[101-179]thousand ducats on him if he allows him to stir a step from the

[101-180]prison.”

[101-181]“That’s ridiculous,” said the young man; “the fact is, all the men on

[101-182]earth will not make me sleep in prison.”

[101-183]“Tell me, you devil,” said Sancho, “have you got any angel that will

[101-184]deliver you, and take off the irons I am going to order them to put

[101-185]upon you?”

[101-186]“Now, señor governor,” said the young man in a sprightly manner, “let

[101-187]us be reasonable and come to the point. Granted your worship may order

[101-188]me to be taken to prison, and to have irons and chains put on me, and

[101-189]to be shut up in a cell, and may lay heavy penalties on the gaoler if

[101-190]he lets me out, and that he obeys your orders; still, if I don’t choose

[101-191]to sleep, and choose to remain awake all night without closing an eye,

[101-192]will your worship with all your power be able to make me sleep if I

[101-193]don’t choose?”

[101-194]“No, truly,” said the secretary, “and the fellow has made his point.”

[101-195]“So then,” said Sancho, “it would be entirely of your own choice you

[101-196]would keep from sleeping; not in opposition to my will?”

[101-197]“No, señor,” said the youth, “certainly not.”

[101-198]“Well then, go, and God be with you,” said Sancho; “be off home to

[101-199]sleep, and God give you sound sleep, for I don’t want to rob you of it;

[101-200]but for the future, let me advise you don’t joke with the authorities,

[101-201]because you may come across someone who will bring down the joke on

[101-202]your own skull.”

[101-203]The young man went his way, and the governor continued his round, and

[101-204]shortly afterwards two tipstaffs came up with a man in custody, and

[101-205]said, “Señor governor, this person, who seems to be a man, is not so,

[101-206]but a woman, and not an ill-favoured one, in man’s clothes.” They

[101-207]raised two or three lanterns to her face, and by their light they

[101-208]distinguished the features of a woman to all appearance of the age of

[101-209]sixteen or a little more, with her hair gathered into a gold and green

[101-210]silk net, and fair as a thousand pearls. They scanned her from head to

[101-211]foot, and observed that she had on red silk stockings with garters of

[101-212]white taffety bordered with gold and pearl; her breeches were of green

[101-213]and gold stuff, and under an open jacket or jerkin of the same she wore

[101-214]a doublet of the finest white and gold cloth; her shoes were white and

[101-215]such as men wear; she carried no sword at her belt, but only a richly

[101-216]ornamented dagger, and on her fingers she had several handsome rings.

[101-217]In short, the girl seemed fair to look at in the eyes of all, and none

[101-218]of those who beheld her knew her, the people of the town said they

[101-219]could not imagine who she was, and those who were in on the secret of

[101-220]the jokes that were to be practised upon Sancho were the ones who were

[101-221]most surprised, for this incident or discovery had not been arranged by

[101-222]them; and they watched anxiously to see how the affair would end.

[101-223]Sancho was fascinated by the girl’s beauty, and he asked her who she

[101-224]was, where she was going, and what had induced her to dress herself in

[101-225]that garb. She with her eyes fixed on the ground answered in modest

[101-226]confusion, “I cannot tell you, señor, before so many people what it is

[101-227]of such consequence to me to have kept secret; one thing I wish to be

[101-228]known, that I am no thief or evildoer, but only an unhappy maiden whom

[101-229]the power of jealousy has led to break through the respect that is due

[101-230]to modesty.”

[101-231]Hearing this the majordomo said to Sancho, “Make the people stand back,

[101-232]señor governor, that this lady may say what she wishes with less

[101-233]embarrassment.”

[101-234]Sancho gave the order, and all except the majordomo, the head-carver,

[101-235]and the secretary fell back. Finding herself then in the presence of no

[101-236]more, the damsel went on to say, “I am the daughter, sirs, of Pedro

[101-237]Perez Mazorca, the wool-farmer of this town, who is in the habit of

[101-238]coming very often to my father’s house.”

[101-239]“That won’t do, señora,” said the majordomo; “for I know Pedro Perez

[101-240]very well, and I know he has no child at all, either son or daughter;

[101-241]and besides, though you say he is your father, you add then that he

[101-242]comes very often to your father’s house.”

[101-243]“I had already noticed that,” said Sancho.

[101-244]“I am confused just now, sirs,” said the damsel, “and I don’t know what

[101-245]I am saying; but the truth is that I am the daughter of Diego de la

[101-246]Llana, whom you must all know.”

[101-247]“Ay, that will do,” said the majordomo; “for I know Diego de la Llana,

[101-248]and know that he is a gentleman of position and a rich man, and that he

[101-249]has a son and a daughter, and that since he was left a widower nobody

[101-250]in all this town can speak of having seen his daughter’s face; for he

[101-251]keeps her so closely shut up that he does not give even the sun a

[101-252]chance of seeing her; and for all that report says she is extremely

[101-253]beautiful.”

[101-254]“It is true,” said the damsel, “and I am that daughter; whether report

[101-255]lies or not as to my beauty, you, sirs, will have decided by this time,

[101-256]as you have seen me;” and with this she began to weep bitterly.

[101-257]On seeing this the secretary leant over to the head-carver’s ear, and

[101-258]said to him in a low voice, “Something serious has no doubt happened

[101-259]this poor maiden, that she goes wandering from home in such a dress and

[101-260]at such an hour, and one of her rank too.” “There can be no doubt about

[101-261]it,” returned the carver, “and moreover her tears confirm your

[101-262]suspicion.” Sancho gave her the best comfort he could, and entreated

[101-263]her to tell them without any fear what had happened her, as they would

[101-264]all earnestly and by every means in their power endeavour to relieve

[101-265]her.

[101-266]“The fact is, sirs,” said she, “that my father has kept me shut up

[101-267]these ten years, for so long is it since the earth received my mother.

[101-268]Mass is said at home in a sumptuous chapel, and all this time I have

[101-269]seen but the sun in the heaven by day, and the moon and the stars by

[101-270]night; nor do I know what streets are like, or plazas, or churches, or

[101-271]even men, except my father and a brother I have, and Pedro Perez the

[101-272]wool-farmer; whom, because he came frequently to our house, I took it

[101-273]into my head to call my father, to avoid naming my own. This seclusion

[101-274]and the restrictions laid upon my going out, were it only to church,

[101-275]have been keeping me unhappy for many a day and month past; I longed to

[101-276]see the world, or at least the town where I was born, and it did not

[101-277]seem to me that this wish was inconsistent with the respect maidens of

[101-278]good quality should have for themselves. When I heard them talking of

[101-279]bull-fights taking place, and of javelin games, and of acting plays, I

[101-280]asked my brother, who is a year younger than myself, to tell me what

[101-281]sort of things these were, and many more that I had never seen; he

[101-282]explained them to me as well as he could, but the only effect was to

[101-283]kindle in me a still stronger desire to see them. At last, to cut short

[101-284]the story of my ruin, I begged and entreated my brother—O that I had

[101-285]never made such an entreaty—” And once more she gave way to a burst of

[101-286]weeping.

[101-287]“Proceed, señora,” said the majordomo, “and finish your story of what

[101-288]has happened to you, for your words and tears are keeping us all in

[101-289]suspense.”

[101-290]“I have but little more to say, though many a tear to shed,” said the

[101-291]damsel; “for ill-placed desires can only be paid for in some such way.”

[101-292]The maiden’s beauty had made a deep impression on the head-carver’s

[101-293]heart, and he again raised his lantern for another look at her, and

[101-294]thought they were not tears she was shedding, but seed-pearl or dew of

[101-295]the meadow, nay, he exalted them still higher, and made Oriental pearls

[101-296]of them, and fervently hoped her misfortune might not be so great a one

[101-297]as her tears and sobs seemed to indicate. The governor was losing

[101-298]patience at the length of time the girl was taking to tell her story,

[101-299]and told her not to keep them waiting any longer; for it was late, and

[101-300]there still remained a good deal of the town to be gone over.

[101-301]She, with broken sobs and half-suppressed sighs, went on to say, “My

[101-302]misfortune, my misadventure, is simply this, that I entreated my

[101-303]brother to dress me up as a man in a suit of his clothes, and take me

[101-304]some night, when our father was asleep, to see the whole town; he,

[101-305]overcome by my entreaties, consented, and dressing me in this suit and

[101-306]himself in clothes of mine that fitted him as if made for him (for he

[101-307]has not a hair on his chin, and might pass for a very beautiful young

[101-308]girl), to-night, about an hour ago, more or less, we left the house,

[101-309]and guided by our youthful and foolish impulse we made the circuit of

[101-310]the whole town, and then, as we were about to return home, we saw a

[101-311]great troop of people coming, and my brother said to me, ‘Sister, this

[101-312]must be the round, stir your feet and put wings to them, and follow me

[101-313]as fast as you can, lest they recognise us, for that would be a bad

[101-314]business for us;’ and so saying he turned about and began, I cannot say

[101-315]to run but to fly; in less than six paces I fell from fright, and then

[101-316]the officer of justice came up and carried me before your worships,

[101-317]where I find myself put to shame before all these people as whimsical

[101-318]and vicious.”

[101-319]“So then, señora,” said Sancho, “no other mishap has befallen you, nor

[101-320]was it jealousy that made you leave home, as you said at the beginning

[101-321]of your story?”

[101-322]“Nothing has happened me,” said she, “nor was it jealousy that brought

[101-323]me out, but merely a longing to see the world, which did not go beyond

[101-324]seeing the streets of this town.”

[101-325]The appearance of the tipstaffs with her brother in custody, whom one

[101-326]of them had overtaken as he ran away from his sister, now fully

[101-327]confirmed the truth of what the damsel said. He had nothing on but a

[101-328]rich petticoat and a short blue damask cloak with fine gold lace, and

[101-329]his head was uncovered and adorned only with its own hair, which looked

[101-330]like rings of gold, so bright and curly was it. The governor, the

[101-331]majordomo, and the carver went aside with him, and, unheard by his

[101-332]sister, asked him how he came to be in that dress, and he with no less

[101-333]shame and embarrassment told exactly the same story as his sister, to

[101-334]the great delight of the enamoured carver; the governor, however, said

[101-335]to them, “In truth, young lady and gentleman, this has been a very

[101-336]childish affair, and to explain your folly and rashness there was no

[101-337]necessity for all this delay and all these tears and sighs; for if you

[101-338]had said we are so-and-so, and we escaped from our father’s house in

[101-339]this way in order to ramble about, out of mere curiosity and with no

[101-340]other object, there would have been an end of the matter, and none of

[101-341]these little sobs and tears and all the rest of it.”

[101-342]“That is true,” said the damsel, “but you see the confusion I was in

[101-343]was so great it did not let me behave as I ought.”

[101-344]“No harm has been done,” said Sancho; “come, we will leave you at your

[101-345]father’s house; perhaps they will not have missed you; and another time

[101-346]don’t be so childish or eager to see the world; for a respectable

[101-347]damsel should have a broken leg and keep at home; and the woman and the

[101-348]hen by gadding about are soon lost; and she who is eager to see is also

[101-349]eager to be seen; I say no more.”

[101-350]The youth thanked the governor for his kind offer to take them home,

[101-351]and they directed their steps towards the house, which was not far off.

[101-352]On reaching it the youth threw a pebble up at a grating, and
