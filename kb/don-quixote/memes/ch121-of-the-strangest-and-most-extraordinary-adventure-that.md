OF THE STRANGEST AND MOST EXTRAORDINARY ADVENTURE THAT BEFELL DON

QUIXOTE IN THE WHOLE COURSE OF THIS GREAT HISTORY

p69a.jpg (141K)


[121-001]The horsemen dismounted, and, together with the men on foot, without a

[121-002]moment’s delay taking up Sancho and Don Quixote bodily, they carried

[121-003]them into the court, all round which near a hundred torches fixed in

[121-004]sockets were burning, besides above five hundred lamps in the

[121-005]corridors, so that in spite of the night, which was somewhat dark, the

[121-006]want of daylight could not be perceived. In the middle of the court was

[121-007]a catafalque, raised about two yards above the ground and covered

[121-008]completely by an immense canopy of black velvet, and on the steps all

[121-009]round it white wax tapers burned in more than a hundred silver

[121-010]candlesticks. Upon the catafalque was seen the dead body of a damsel so

[121-011]lovely that by her beauty she made death itself look beautiful. She lay

[121-012]with her head resting upon a cushion of brocade and crowned with a

[121-013]garland of sweet-smelling flowers of divers sorts, her hands crossed

[121-014]upon her bosom, and between them a branch of yellow palm of victory. On

[121-015]one side of the court was erected a stage, where upon two chairs were

[121-016]seated two persons who from having crowns on their heads and sceptres

[121-017]in their hands appeared to be kings of some sort, whether real or mock

[121-018]ones. By the side of this stage, which was reached by steps, were two

[121-019]other chairs on which the men carrying the prisoners seated Don Quixote

[121-020]and Sancho, all in silence, and by signs giving them to understand that

[121-021]they too were to be silent; which, however, they would have been

[121-022]without any signs, for their amazement at all they saw held them

[121-023]tongue-tied. And now two persons of distinction, who were at once

[121-024]recognised by Don Quixote as his hosts the duke and duchess, ascended

[121-025]the stage attended by a numerous suite, and seated themselves on two

[121-026]gorgeous chairs close to the two kings, as they seemed to be. Who would

[121-027]not have been amazed at this? Nor was this all, for Don Quixote had

[121-028]perceived that the dead body on the catafalque was that of the fair

[121-029]Altisidora. As the duke and duchess mounted the stage Don Quixote and

[121-030]Sancho rose and made them a profound obeisance, which they returned by

[121-031]bowing their heads slightly. At this moment an official crossed over,

[121-032]and approaching Sancho threw over him a robe of black buckram painted

[121-033]all over with flames of fire, and taking off his cap put upon his head

[121-034]a mitre such as those undergoing the sentence of the Holy Office wear;

[121-035]and whispered in his ear that he must not open his lips, or they would

[121-036]put a gag upon him, or take his life. Sancho surveyed himself from head

[121-037]to foot and saw himself all ablaze with flames; but as they did not

[121-038]burn him, he did not care two farthings for them. He took off the mitre

[121-039]and seeing it painted with devils he put it on again, saying to

[121-040]himself, “Well, so far those don’t burn me nor do these carry me off.”

[121-041]Don Quixote surveyed him too, and though fear had got the better of his

[121-042]faculties, he could not help smiling to see the figure Sancho

[121-043]presented. And now from underneath the catafalque, so it seemed, there

[121-044]rose a low sweet sound of flutes, which, coming unbroken by human voice

[121-045](for there silence itself kept silence), had a soft and languishing

[121-046]effect. Then, beside the pillow of what seemed to be the dead body,

[121-047]suddenly appeared a fair youth in a Roman habit, who, to the

[121-048]accompaniment of a harp which he himself played, sang in a sweet and

[121-049]clear voice these two stanzas:

[121-050]While fair Altisidora, who the sport

[121-051]Of cold Don Quixote’s cruelty hath been,

[121-052]Returns to life, and in this magic court

[121-053]The dames in sables come to grace the scene,

[121-054]And while her matrons all in seemly sort

[121-055]My lady robes in baize and bombazine,

[121-056]Her beauty and her sorrows will I sing

[121-057]With defter quill than touched the Thracian string.

[121-058]But not in life alone, methinks, to me

[121-059]Belongs the office; Lady, when my tongue

[121-060]Is cold in death, believe me, unto thee

[121-061]My voice shall raise its tributary song.

[121-062]My soul, from this strait prison-house set free,

[121-063]As o’er the Stygian lake it floats along,

[121-064]Thy praises singing still shall hold its way,

[121-065]And make the waters of oblivion stay.

[121-066]At this point one of the two that looked like kings exclaimed, “Enough,

[121-067]enough, divine singer! It would be an endless task to put before us now

[121-068]the death and the charms of the peerless Altisidora, not dead as the

[121-069]ignorant world imagines, but living in the voice of fame and in the

[121-070]penance which Sancho Panza, here present, has to undergo to restore her

[121-071]to the long-lost light. Do thou, therefore, O Rhadamanthus, who sittest

[121-072]in judgment with me in the murky caverns of Dis, as thou knowest all

[121-073]that the inscrutable fates have decreed touching the resuscitation of

[121-074]this damsel, announce and declare it at once, that the happiness we

[121-075]look forward to from her restoration be no longer deferred.”

[121-076]No sooner had Minos the fellow judge of Rhadamanthus said this, than

[121-077]Rhadamanthus rising up said:

[121-078]“Ho, officials of this house, high and low, great and small, make haste

[121-079]hither one and all, and print on Sancho’s face four-and-twenty smacks,

[121-080]and give him twelve pinches and six pin thrusts in the back and arms;

[121-081]for upon this ceremony depends the restoration of Altisidora.”

[121-082]On hearing this Sancho broke silence and cried out, “By all that’s

[121-083]good, I’ll as soon let my face be smacked or handled as turn Moor. Body

[121-084]o’ me! What has handling my face got to do with the resurrection of

[121-085]this damsel? ‘The old woman took kindly to the blits;’ they enchant

[121-086]Dulcinea, and whip me in order to disenchant her; Altisidora dies of

[121-087]ailments God was pleased to send her, and to bring her to life again

[121-088]they must give me four-and-twenty smacks, and prick holes in my body

[121-089]with pins, and raise weals on my arms with pinches! Try those jokes on

[121-090]a brother-in-law; ‘I’m an old dog, and “tus, tus” is no use with me.’”

[121-091]“Thou shalt die,” said Rhadamanthus in a loud voice; “relent, thou

[121-092]tiger; humble thyself, proud Nimrod; suffer and he silent, for no

[121-093]impossibilities are asked of thee; it is not for thee to inquire into

[121-094]the difficulties in this matter; smacked thou must be, pricked thou

[121-095]shalt see thyself, and with pinches thou must be made to howl. Ho, I

[121-096]say, officials, obey my orders; or by the word of an honest man, ye

[121-097]shall see what ye were born for.”

[121-098]At this some six duennas, advancing across the court, made their

[121-099]appearance in procession, one after the other, four of them with

[121-100]spectacles, and all with their right hands uplifted, showing four

[121-101]fingers of wrist to make their hands look longer, as is the fashion

[121-102]now-a-days. No sooner had Sancho caught sight of them than, bellowing

[121-103]like a bull, he exclaimed, “I might let myself be handled by all the

[121-104]world; but allow duennas to touch me—not a bit of it! Scratch my face,

[121-105]as my master was served in this very castle; run me through the body

[121-106]with burnished daggers; pinch my arms with red-hot pincers; I’ll bear

[121-107]all in patience to serve these gentlefolk; but I won’t let duennas

[121-108]touch me, though the devil should carry me off!”

[121-109]Here Don Quixote, too, broke silence, saying to Sancho, “Have patience,

[121-110]my son, and gratify these noble persons, and give all thanks to heaven

[121-111]that it has infused such virtue into thy person, that by its sufferings

[121-112]thou canst disenchant the enchanted and restore to life the dead.”

[121-113]The duennas were now close to Sancho, and he, having become more

[121-114]tractable and reasonable, settling himself well in his chair presented

[121-115]his face and beard to the first, who delivered him a smack very stoutly

[121-116]laid on, and then made him a low curtsey.

[121-117]“Less politeness and less paint, señora duenna,” said Sancho; “by God

[121-118]your hands smell of vinegar-wash.”

[121-119]In line, all the duennas smacked him and several others of the

[121-120]household pinched him; but what he could not stand was being pricked by

[121-121]the pins; and so, apparently out of patience, he started up out of his

[121-122]chair, and seizing a lighted torch that stood near him fell upon the

[121-123]duennas and the whole set of his tormentors, exclaiming, “Begone, ye

[121-124]ministers of hell; I’m not made of brass not to feel such

[121-125]out-of-the-way tortures.”

[121-126]At this instant Altisidora, who probably was tired of having been so

[121-127]long lying on her back, turned on her side; seeing which the bystanders

[121-128]cried out almost with one voice, “Altisidora is alive! Altisidora

[121-129]lives!”

[121-130]Rhadamanthus bade Sancho put away his wrath, as the object they had in

[121-131]view was now attained. When Don Quixote saw Altisidora move, he went on

[121-132]his knees to Sancho saying to him, “Now is the time, son of my bowels,

[121-133]not to call thee my squire, for thee to give thyself some of those

[121-134]lashes thou art bound to lay on for the disenchantment of Dulcinea.

[121-135]Now, I say, is the time when the virtue that is in thee is ripe, and

[121-136]endowed with efficacy to work the good that is looked for from thee.”

[121-137]To which Sancho made answer, “That’s trick upon trick, I think, and not

[121-138]honey upon pancakes; a nice thing it would be for a whipping to come

[121-139]now, on the top of pinches, smacks, and pin-proddings! You had better

[121-140]take a big stone and tie it round my neck, and pitch me into a well; I

[121-141]should not mind it much, if I’m to be always made the cow of the

[121-142]wedding for the cure of other people’s ailments. Leave me alone; or

[121-143]else by God I’ll fling the whole thing to the dogs, let come what may.”

[121-144]Altisidora had by this time sat up on the catafalque, and as she did so

[121-145]the clarions sounded, accompanied by the flutes, and the voices of all

[121-146]present exclaiming, “Long life to Altisidora! long life to Altisidora!”

[121-147]The duke and duchess and the kings Minos and Rhadamanthus stood up, and

[121-148]all, together with Don Quixote and Sancho, advanced to receive her and

[121-149]take her down from the catafalque; and she, making as though she were

[121-150]recovering from a swoon, bowed her head to the duke and duchess and to

[121-151]the kings, and looking sideways at Don Quixote, said to him, “God

[121-152]forgive thee, insensible knight, for through thy cruelty I have been,

[121-153]to me it seems, more than a thousand years in the other world; and to

[121-154]thee, the most compassionate upon earth, I render thanks for the life I

[121-155]am now in possession of. From this day forth, friend Sancho, count as

[121-156]thine six smocks of mine which I bestow upon thee, to make as many

[121-157]shirts for thyself, and if they are not all quite whole, at any rate

[121-158]they are all clean.”

[121-159]Sancho kissed her hands in gratitude, kneeling, and with the mitre in

[121-160]his hand. The duke bade them take it from him, and give him back his

[121-161]cap and doublet and remove the flaming robe. Sancho begged the duke to

[121-162]let them leave him the robe and mitre; as he wanted to take them home

[121-163]for a token and memento of that unexampled adventure. The duchess said

[121-164]they must leave them with him; for he knew already what a great friend

[121-165]of his she was. The duke then gave orders that the court should be

[121-166]cleared, and that all should retire to their chambers, and that Don

[121-167]Quixote and Sancho should be conducted to their old quarters.
