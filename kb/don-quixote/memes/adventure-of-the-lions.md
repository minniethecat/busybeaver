## The Encounter

In Part II, Don Quixote and Sancho Panza encounter a wagon carrying caged lions — a gift from the governor of Oran to the king. The wagon is escorted by a keeper who warns Don Quixote to keep his distance. The knight's response is characteristically defiant:

> "Your worship," said Sancho, "these are not giants or knights or enchanted folk, but lions in a cage, which the governor of Oran is sending to court as a present to his Majesty." (069-059)

Don Quixote dismisses Sancho's caution and orders the keeper to open the cage. The confrontation is set — a single aging knight against a wild lion — in what promises to be the novel's most dangerous encounter.

## The Anticlimax

The keeper, terrified, opens the cage. The lion stretches, yawns, surveys its surroundings, and then — turns around and lies back down with its hindquarters toward Don Quixote. There is no battle, no blood, no heroic death. The most anticipated adventure of Part II ends in anticlimax.

Yet Don Quixote interprets this as a victory. The lion, he declares, refused to fight because it recognized his superiority:

> "The lion has turned his back on me, and by that I understand that my courage is greater than his." (069-134)

## Knight of the Lions

From this episode, Don Quixote adopts a new title: the Knight of the Lions, replacing his earlier epithet, the Knight of the Sorrowful Face. The name change signals a shift in identity — from melancholy to pride, from suffering to mastery. The anticlimax, by the logic of chivalric interpretation, becomes a triumph.

The episode encapsulates the novel's treatment of heroism. Don Quixote displays genuine courage — he faces what he believes is mortal danger — but the universe refuses to cooperate. There is no heroic combat to validate his bravery. He must construct the meaning himself, and he does: the Knight of the Lions is born not from deed but from interpretation of a non-deed.
