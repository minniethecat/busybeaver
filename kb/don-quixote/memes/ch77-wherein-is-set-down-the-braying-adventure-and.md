[077-001]WHEREIN IS SET DOWN THE BRAYING ADVENTURE, AND THE DROLL ONE OF THE

[077-002]PUPPET-SHOWMAN, TOGETHER WITH THE MEMORABLE DIVINATIONS OF THE DIVINING

[077-003]Don Quixote’s bread would not bake, as the common saying is, until he

[077-004]had heard and learned the curious things promised by the man who

[077-005]carried the arms. He went to seek him where the innkeeper said he was

[077-006]and having found him, bade him say now at any rate what he had to say

[077-007]in answer to the question he had asked him on the road. “The tale of my

[077-008]wonders must be taken more leisurely and not standing,” said the man;

[077-009]“let me finish foddering my beast, good sir; and then I’ll tell you

[077-010]things that will astonish you.”

[077-011]“Don’t wait for that,” said Don Quixote; “I’ll help you in everything,”

[077-012]and so he did, sifting the barley for him and cleaning out the manger;

[077-013]a degree of humility which made the other feel bound to tell him with a

[077-014]good grace what he had asked; so seating himself on a bench, with Don

[077-015]Quixote beside him, and the cousin, the page, Sancho Panza, and the

[077-016]landlord, for a senate and an audience, he began his story in this way:

[077-017]“You must know that in a village four leagues and a half from this inn,

[077-018]it so happened that one of the regidors, by the tricks and roguery of a

[077-019]servant girl of his (it’s too long a tale to tell), lost an ass; and

[077-020]though he did all he possibly could to find it, it was all to no

[077-021]purpose. A fortnight might have gone by, so the story goes, since the

[077-022]ass had been missing, when, as the regidor who had lost it was standing

[077-023]in the plaza, another regidor of the same town said to him, ‘Pay me for

[077-024]good news, gossip; your ass has turned up.’ ‘That I will, and well,

[077-025]gossip,’ said the other; ‘but tell us, where has he turned up?’ ‘In the

[077-026]forest,’ said the finder; ‘I saw him this morning without pack-saddle

[077-027]or harness of any sort, and so lean that it went to one’s heart to see

[077-028]him. I tried to drive him before me and bring him to you, but he is

[077-029]already so wild and shy that when I went near him he made off into the

[077-030]thickest part of the forest. If you have a mind that we two should go

[077-031]back and look for him, let me put up this she-ass at my house and I’ll

[077-032]be back at once.’ ‘You will be doing me a great kindness,’ said the

[077-033]owner of the ass, ‘and I’ll try to pay it back in the same coin.’ It is

[077-034]with all these circumstances, and in the very same way I am telling it

[077-035]now, that those who know all about the matter tell the story. Well

[077-036]then, the two regidors set off on foot, arm in arm, for the forest, and

[077-037]coming to the place where they hoped to find the ass they could not

[077-038]find him, nor was he to be seen anywhere about, search as they might.

[077-039]Seeing, then, that there was no sign of him, the regidor who had seen

[077-040]him said to the other, ‘Look here, gossip; a plan has occurred to me,

[077-041]by which, beyond a doubt, we shall manage to discover the animal, even

[077-042]if he is stowed away in the bowels of the earth, not to say the forest.

[077-043]Here it is. I can bray to perfection, and if you can ever so little,

[077-044]the thing’s as good as done.’ ‘Ever so little did you say, gossip?’

[077-045]said the other; ‘by God, I’ll not give in to anybody, not even to the

[077-046]asses themselves.’ ‘We’ll soon see,’ said the second regidor, ‘for my

[077-047]plan is that you should go one side of the forest, and I the other, so

[077-048]as to go all round about it; and every now and then you will bray and I

[077-049]will bray; and it cannot be but that the ass will hear us, and answer

[077-050]us if he is in the forest.’ To which the owner of the ass replied,

[077-051]‘It’s an excellent plan, I declare, gossip, and worthy of your great

[077-052]genius;’ and the two separating as agreed, it so fell out that they

[077-053]brayed almost at the same moment, and each, deceived by the braying of

[077-054]the other, ran to look, fancying the ass had turned up at last. When

[077-055]they came in sight of one another, said the loser, ‘Is it possible,

[077-056]gossip, that it was not my ass that brayed?’ ‘No, it was I,’ said the

[077-057]other. ‘Well then, I can tell you, gossip,’ said the ass’s owner, ‘that

[077-058]between you and an ass there is not an atom of difference as far as

[077-059]braying goes, for I never in all my life saw or heard anything more

[077-060]natural.’ ‘Those praises and compliments belong to you more justly than

[077-061]to me, gossip,’ said the inventor of the plan; ‘for, by the God that

[077-062]made me, you might give a couple of brays odds to the best and most

[077-063]finished brayer in the world; the tone you have got is deep, your voice

[077-064]is well kept up as to time and pitch, and your finishing notes come

[077-065]thick and fast; in fact, I own myself beaten, and yield the palm to

[077-066]you, and give in to you in this rare accomplishment.’ ‘Well then,’ said

[077-067]the owner, ‘I’ll set a higher value on myself for the future, and

[077-068]consider that I know something, as I have an excellence of some sort;

[077-069]for though I always thought I brayed well, I never supposed I came up

[077-070]to the pitch of perfection you say.’ ‘And I say too,’ said the second,

[077-071]‘that there are rare gifts going to loss in the world, and that they

[077-072]are ill bestowed upon those who don’t know how to make use of them.’

[077-073]‘Ours,’ said the owner of the ass, ‘unless it is in cases like this we

[077-074]have now in hand, cannot be of any service to us, and even in this God

[077-075]grant they may be of some use.’ So saying they separated, and took to

[077-076]their braying once more, but every instant they were deceiving one

[077-077]another, and coming to meet one another again, until they arranged by

[077-078]way of countersign, so as to know that it was they and not the ass, to

[077-079]give two brays, one after the other. In this way, doubling the brays at

[077-080]every step, they made the complete circuit of the forest, but the lost

[077-081]ass never gave them an answer or even the sign of one. How could the

[077-082]poor ill-starred brute have answered, when, in the thickest part of the

[077-083]forest, they found him devoured by wolves? As soon as he saw him his

[077-084]owner said, ‘I was wondering he did not answer, for if he wasn’t dead

[077-085]he’d have brayed when he heard us, or he’d have been no ass; but for

[077-086]the sake of having heard you bray to such perfection, gossip, I count

[077-087]the trouble I have taken to look for him well bestowed, even though I

[077-088]have found him dead.’ ‘It’s in a good hand, gossip,’ said the other;

[077-089]‘if the abbot sings well, the acolyte is not much behind him.’ So they

[077-090]returned disconsolate and hoarse to their village, where they told

[077-091]their friends, neighbours, and acquaintances what had befallen them in

[077-092]their search for the ass, each crying up the other’s perfection in

[077-093]braying. The whole story came to be known and spread abroad through the

[077-094]villages of the neighbourhood; and the devil, who never sleeps, with

[077-095]his love for sowing dissensions and scattering discord everywhere,

[077-096]blowing mischief about and making quarrels out of nothing, contrived to

[077-097]make the people of the other towns fall to braying whenever they saw

[077-098]anyone from our village, as if to throw the braying of our regidors in

[077-099]our teeth. Then the boys took to it, which was the same thing for it as

[077-100]getting into the hands and mouths of all the devils of hell; and

[077-101]braying spread from one town to another in such a way that the men of

[077-102]the braying town are as easy to be known as blacks are to be known from

[077-103]whites, and the unlucky joke has gone so far that several times the

[077-104]scoffed have come out in arms and in a body to do battle with the

[077-105]scoffers, and neither king nor rook, fear nor shame, can mend matters.

[077-106]To-morrow or the day after, I believe, the men of my town, that is, of

[077-107]the braying town, are going to take the field against another village

[077-108]two leagues away from ours, one of those that persecute us most; and

[077-109]that we may turn out well prepared I have bought these lances and

[077-110]halberds you have seen. These are the curious things I told you I had

[077-111]to tell, and if you don’t think them so, I have got no others;” and

[077-112]with this the worthy fellow brought his story to a close.

[077-113]Just at this moment there came in at the gate of the inn a man entirely

[077-114]clad in chamois leather, hose, breeches, and doublet, who said in a

[077-115]loud voice, “Señor host, have you room? Here’s the divining ape and the

[077-116]show of the Release of Melisendra just coming.”

[077-117]“Ods body!” said the landlord, “why, it’s Master Pedro! We’re in for a

[077-118]grand night!” I forgot to mention that the said Master Pedro had his

[077-119]left eye and nearly half his cheek covered with a patch of green

[077-120]taffety, showing that something ailed all that side. “Your worship is

[077-121]welcome, Master Pedro,” continued the landlord; “but where are the ape

[077-122]and the show, for I don’t see them?” “They are close at hand,” said he

[077-123]in the chamois leather, “but I came on first to know if there was any

[077-124]room.” “I’d make the Duke of Alva himself clear out to make room for

[077-125]Master Pedro,” said the landlord; “bring in the ape and the show;

[077-126]there’s company in the inn to-night that will pay to see that and the

[077-127]cleverness of the ape.” “So be it by all means,” said the man with the

[077-128]patch; “I’ll lower the price, and be well satisfied if I only pay my

[077-129]expenses; and now I’ll go back and hurry on the cart with the ape and

[077-130]the show;” and with this he went out of the inn.

[077-131]Don Quixote at once asked the landlord what this Master Pedro was, and

[077-132]what was the show and what was the ape he had with him; which the

[077-133]landlord replied, “This is a famous puppet-showman, who for some time

[077-134]past has been going about this Mancha de Aragon, exhibiting a show of

[077-135]the release of Melisendra by the famous Don Gaiferos, one of the best

[077-136]and best-represented stories that have been seen in this part of the

[077-137]kingdom for many a year; he has also with him an ape with the most

[077-138]extraordinary gift ever seen in an ape or imagined in a human being;

[077-139]for if you ask him anything, he listens attentively to the question,

[077-140]and then jumps on his master’s shoulder, and pressing close to his ear

[077-141]tells him the answer which Master Pedro then delivers. He says a great

[077-142]deal more about things past than about things to come; and though he

[077-143]does not always hit the truth in every case, most times he is not far

[077-144]wrong, so that he makes us fancy he has got the devil in him. He gets

[077-145]two reals for every question if the ape answers; I mean if his master

[077-146]answers for him after he has whispered into his ear; and so it is

[077-147]believed that this same Master Pedro is very rich. He is a ‘gallant

[077-148]man’ as they say in Italy, and good company, and leads the finest life

[077-149]in the world; talks more than six, drinks more than a dozen, and all by

[077-150]his tongue, and his ape, and his show.”

[077-151]Master Pedro now came back, and in a cart followed the show and the

[077-152]ape—a big one, without a tail and with buttocks as bare as felt, but

[077-153]not vicious-looking. As soon as Don Quixote saw him, he asked him, “Can

[077-154]you tell me, sir fortune-teller, what fish do we catch, and how will it

[077-155]be with us? See, here are my two reals,” and he bade Sancho give them

[077-156]to Master Pedro; but he answered for the ape and said, “Señor, this

[077-157]animal does not give any answer or information touching things that are

[077-158]to come; of things past he knows something, and more or less of things

[077-159]present.”

[077-160]“Gad,” said Sancho, “I would not give a farthing to be told what’s past

[077-161]with me, for who knows that better than I do myself? And to pay for

[077-162]being told what I know would be mighty foolish. But as you know things

[077-163]present, here are my two reals, and tell me, most excellent sir ape,

[077-164]what is my wife Teresa Panza doing now, and what is she diverting

[077-165]herself with?”

[077-166]Master Pedro refused to take the money, saying, “I will not receive

[077-167]payment in advance or until the service has been first rendered;” and

[077-168]then with his right hand he gave a couple of slaps on his left

[077-169]shoulder, and with one spring the ape perched himself upon it, and

[077-170]putting his mouth to his master’s ear began chattering his teeth

[077-171]rapidly; and having kept this up as long as one would be saying a

[077-172]credo, with another spring he brought himself to the ground, and the

[077-173]same instant Master Pedro ran in great haste and fell upon his knees

[077-174]before Don Quixote, and embracing his legs exclaimed, “These legs do I

[077-175]embrace as I would embrace the two pillars of Hercules, O illustrious

[077-176]reviver of knight-errantry, so long consigned to oblivion! O never yet

[077-177]duly extolled knight, Don Quixote of La Mancha, courage of the

[077-178]faint-hearted, prop of the tottering, arm of the fallen, staff and

[077-179]counsel of all who are unfortunate!”

[077-187]jug with a broken spout that holds a good drop of wine, with which she

[077-188]solaces herself at her work.”

[077-189]“That I can well believe,” said Sancho. “She is a lucky one, and if it

[077-190]was not for her jealousy I would not change her for the giantess

[077-191]Andandona, who by my master’s account was a very clever and worthy

[077-192]woman; my Teresa is one of those that won’t let themselves want for

[077-193]anything, though their heirs may have to pay for it.”
