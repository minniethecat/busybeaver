WHICH TREATS OF THE CURIOUS DISCOURSE DON QUIXOTE DELIVERED ON ARMS AND

LETTERS

c38a.jpg (180K)


[038-001]Continuing his discourse Don Quixote said: “As we began in the

[038-002]student’s case with poverty and its accompaniments, let us see now if

[038-003]the soldier is richer, and we shall find that in poverty itself there

[038-004]is no one poorer; for he is dependent on his miserable pay, which comes

[038-005]late or never, or else on what he can plunder, seriously imperilling

[038-006]his life and conscience; and sometimes his nakedness will be so great

[038-007]that a slashed doublet serves him for uniform and shirt, and in the

[038-008]depth of winter he has to defend himself against the inclemency of the

[038-009]weather in the open field with nothing better than the breath of his

[038-010]mouth, which I need not say, coming from an empty place, must come out

[038-011]cold, contrary to the laws of nature. To be sure he looks forward to

[038-012]the approach of night to make up for all these discomforts on the bed

[038-013]that awaits him, which, unless by some fault of his, never sins by

[038-014]being over narrow, for he can easily measure out on the ground as he

[038-015]likes, and roll himself about in it to his heart’s content without any

[038-016]fear of the sheets slipping away from him. Then, after all this,

[038-017]suppose the day and hour for taking his degree in his calling to have

[038-018]come; suppose the day of battle to have arrived, when they invest him

[038-019]with the doctor’s cap made of lint, to mend some bullet-hole, perhaps,

[038-020]that has gone through his temples, or left him with a crippled arm or

[038-021]leg. Or if this does not happen, and merciful Heaven watches over him

[038-022]and keeps him safe and sound, it may be he will be in the same poverty

[038-023]he was in before, and he must go through more engagements and more

[038-024]battles, and come victorious out of all before he betters himself; but

[038-025]miracles of that sort are seldom seen. For tell me, sirs, if you have

[038-026]ever reflected upon it, by how much do those who have gained by war

[038-027]fall short of the number of those who have perished in it? No doubt you

[038-028]will reply that there can be no comparison, that the dead cannot be

[038-029]numbered, while the living who have been rewarded may be summed up with

[038-030]three figures. All which is the reverse in the case of men of letters;

[038-031]for by skirts, to say nothing of sleeves, they all find means of

[038-032]support; so that though the soldier has more to endure, his reward is

[038-033]much less. But against all this it may be urged that it is easier to

[038-034]reward two thousand soldiers, for the former may be remunerated by

[038-035]giving them places, which must perforce be conferred upon men of their

[038-036]calling, while the latter can only be recompensed out of the very

[038-037]property of the master they serve; but this impossibility only

[038-038]strengthens my argument.

[038-039]“Putting this, however, aside, for it is a puzzling question for which

[038-040]it is difficult to find a solution, let us return to the superiority of

[038-041]arms over letters, a matter still undecided, so many are the arguments

[038-042]put forward on each side; for besides those I have mentioned, letters

[038-043]say that without them arms cannot maintain themselves, for war, too,

[038-044]has its laws and is governed by them, and laws belong to the domain of

[038-045]letters and men of letters. To this arms make answer that without them

[038-046]laws cannot be maintained, for by arms states are defended, kingdoms

[038-047]preserved, cities protected, roads made safe, seas cleared of pirates;

[038-048]and, in short, if it were not for them, states, kingdoms, monarchies,

[038-049]cities, ways by sea and land would be exposed to the violence and

[038-050]confusion which war brings with it, so long as it lasts and is free to

[038-051]make use of its privileges and powers. And then it is plain that

[038-052]whatever costs most is valued and deserves to be valued most. To attain

[038-053]to eminence in letters costs a man time, watching, hunger, nakedness,

[038-054]headaches, indigestions, and other things of the sort, some of which I

[038-055]have already referred to. But for a man to come in the ordinary course

[038-056]of things to be a good soldier costs him all the student suffers, and

[038-057]in an incomparably higher degree, for at every step he runs the risk of

[038-058]losing his life. For what dread of want or poverty that can reach or

[038-059]harass the student can compare with what the soldier feels, who finds

[038-060]himself beleaguered in some stronghold mounting guard in some ravelin

[038-061]or cavalier, knows that the enemy is pushing a mine towards the post

[038-062]where he is stationed, and cannot under any circumstances retire or fly

[038-063]from the imminent danger that threatens him? All he can do is to inform

[038-064]his captain of what is going on so that he may try to remedy it by a

[038-065]counter-mine, and then stand his ground in fear and expectation of the

[038-066]moment when he will fly up to the clouds without wings and descend into

[038-067]the deep against his will. And if this seems a trifling risk, let us

[038-068]see whether it is equalled or surpassed by the encounter of two galleys

[038-069]stem to stem, in the midst of the open sea, locked and entangled one

[038-070]with the other, when the soldier has no more standing room than two

[038-071]feet of the plank of the spur; and yet, though he sees before him

[038-072]threatening him as many ministers of death as there are cannon of the

[038-073]foe pointed at him, not a lance length from his body, and sees too that

[038-074]with the first heedless step he will go down to visit the profundities

[038-075]of Neptune’s bosom, still with dauntless heart, urged on by honour that

[038-076]nerves him, he makes himself a target for all that musketry, and

[038-077]struggles to cross that narrow path to the enemy’s ship. And what is

[038-078]still more marvellous, no sooner has one gone down into the depths he

[038-079]will never rise from till the end of the world, than another takes his

[038-080]place; and if he too falls into the sea that waits for him like an

[038-081]enemy, another and another will succeed him without a moment’s pause

[038-082]between their deaths: courage and daring the greatest that all the

[038-083]chances of war can show. Happy the blest ages that knew not the dread

[038-084]fury of those devilish engines of artillery, whose inventor I am

[038-085]persuaded is in hell receiving the reward of his diabolical invention,

[038-086]by which he made it easy for a base and cowardly arm to take the life

[038-087]of a gallant gentleman; and that, when he knows not how or whence, in

[038-088]the height of the ardour and enthusiasm that fire and animate brave

[038-089]hearts, there should come some random bullet, discharged perhaps by one

[038-090]who fled in terror at the flash when he fired off his accursed machine,

[038-091]which in an instant puts an end to the projects and cuts off the life

[038-092]of one who deserved to live for ages to come. And thus when I reflect

[038-093]on this, I am almost tempted to say that in my heart I repent of having

[038-094]adopted this profession of knight-errant in so detestable an age as we

[038-095]live in now; for though no peril can make me fear, still it gives me

[038-096]some uneasiness to think that powder and lead may rob me of the

[038-097]opportunity of making myself famous and renowned throughout the known

[038-098]earth by the might of my arm and the edge of my sword. But Heaven’s

[038-099]will be done; if I succeed in my attempt I shall be all the more

[038-100]honoured, as I have faced greater dangers than the knights-errant of

[038-101]yore exposed themselves to.”

[038-102]All this lengthy discourse Don Quixote delivered while the others

[038-103]supped, forgetting to raise a morsel to his lips, though Sancho more

[038-104]than once told him to eat his supper, as he would have time enough

[038-105]afterwards to say all he wanted. It excited fresh pity in those who had

[038-106]heard him to see a man of apparently sound sense, and with rational

[038-107]views on every subject he discussed, so hopelessly wanting in all, when

[038-108]his wretched unlucky chivalry was in question. The curate told him he

[038-109]was quite right in all he had said in favour of arms, and that he

[038-110]himself, though a man of letters and a graduate, was of the same

[038-111]opinion.

[038-112]They finished their supper, the cloth was removed, and while the

[038-113]hostess, her daughter, and Maritornes were getting Don Quixote of La

[038-114]Mancha’s garret ready, in which it was arranged that the women were to

[038-115]be quartered by themselves for the night, Don Fernando begged the

[038-116]captive to tell them the story of his life, for it could not fail to be

[038-117]strange and interesting, to judge by the hints he had let fall on his

[038-118]arrival in company with Zoraida. To this the captive replied that he

[038-119]would very willingly yield to his request, only he feared his tale

[038-120]would not give them as much pleasure as he wished; nevertheless, not to

[038-121]be wanting in compliance, he would tell it. The curate and the others

[038-122]thanked him and added their entreaties, and he finding himself so

[038-123]pressed said there was no occasion to ask, where a command had such

[038-124]weight, and added, “If your worships will give me your attention you

[038-125]will hear a true story which, perhaps, fictitious ones constructed with

[038-126]ingenious and studied art cannot come up to.” These words made them

[038-127]settle themselves in their places and preserve a deep silence, and he

[038-128]seeing them waiting on his words in mute expectation, began thus in a

[038-129]pleasant quiet voice.
