# The Niece

**The Niece** (*la Sobrina*) is introduced in the novel's opening paragraph as one of the fixtures of Alonso Quijano's household: "a housekeeper past forty, a niece not yet twenty." Like [[the-housekeeper|the Housekeeper]], she is known only by her familial role, her identity entirely absorbed into her relationship to the man whose madness she cannot cure.

## Character Profile

The Niece is younger and somewhat more credulous than the Housekeeper — where the Housekeeper blames chivalric books with earthy pragmatism, the Niece is more prone to supernatural explanations. When Sancho Panza first departs with Don Quixote, she attributes his absence to darker forces:

> "it was not the devil," said the niece, "but a magician who came on a cloud"[007-054]

Her youth makes her less authoritative than the Housekeeper — she lacks the older woman's command of the household — but her emotional investment in her uncle's welfare is equally intense. She weeps, pleads, and argues with an intensity born of genuine love.

## The Library Burning

Like the Housekeeper, the Niece participates eagerly in the auto-da-fé of Don Quixote's library. When the curate and barber conduct their scrutiny, she feeds their determination with her own:

> "there is no reason for showing mercy to any of them," said the niece[006-015]

Her literalism about the books mirrors her uncle's literalism about chivalry:

> "Ah, señor!" said the niece, "your worship had better order these to be burned as well as the rest"[006-165]

## Desperate Opposition

In Part II, as Don Quixote prepares for his third sally, the Niece's arguments grow more desperate. She appeals to his vanity, his age, his station — anything that might keep him home:

> "remember that all this you say of knight-errantry is fable and fiction"[058-063]

When these arguments fail — as they always do — her despair is genuine. Cervantes never mocks the Niece's grief; he presents it as the reasonable response of a reasonable person to an unreasonable situation. The comedy belongs to Quixote; the pathos belongs to those who love him.

## Relationship with Sancho

The Niece's attitude toward [[sancho-panza|Sancho Panza]] evolves from suspicion to outright hostility. She blames him for enabling her uncle's adventures, calling him "detestable Sancho" when he arrives to visit Don Quixote before the third sally:

> "detestable Sancho," said the niece[054-014]

Yet her powerlessness is the same as the Housekeeper's: she can lock doors, hide keys, and weep — but she cannot prevent a knight-errant from seeking adventure.

## Thematic Significance

The Niece, along with the Housekeeper, represents the domestic world that Don Quixote's madness disrupts and that proves incapable of restraining it. She is the voice of ordinary affection — reasonable, protective, and utterly ineffective against the power of imagination.
