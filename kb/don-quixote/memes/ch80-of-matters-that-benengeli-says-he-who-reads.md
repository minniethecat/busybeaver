[080-001]OF MATTERS THAT BENENGELI SAYS HE WHO READS THEM WILL KNOW, IF HE READS

THEM WITH ATTENTION

p28a.jpg (111K)


[080-002]When the brave man flees, treachery is manifest and it is for wise men

[080-003]to reserve themselves for better occasions. This proved to be the case

[080-004]with Don Quixote, who, giving way before the fury of the townsfolk and

[080-005]the hostile intentions of the angry troop, took to flight and, without

[080-006]a thought of Sancho or the danger in which he was leaving him,

[080-007]retreated to such a distance as he thought made him safe. Sancho, lying

[080-008]across his ass, followed him, as has been said, and at length came up,

[080-009]having by this time recovered his senses, and on joining him let

[080-010]himself drop off Dapple at Rocinante’s feet, sore, bruised, and

[080-011]belaboured. Don Quixote dismounted to examine his wounds, but finding

[080-012]him whole from head to foot, he said to him, angrily enough, “In an

[080-013]evil hour didst thou take to braying, Sancho! Where hast thou learned

[080-014]that it is well done to mention the rope in the house of the man that

[080-015]has been hanged? To the music of brays what harmonies couldst thou

[080-016]expect to get but cudgels? Give thanks to God, Sancho, that they signed

[080-017]the cross on thee just now with a stick, and did not mark thee _per

[080-018]signum crucis_ with a cutlass.”

[080-019]“I’m not equal to answering,” said Sancho, “for I feel as if I was

[080-020]speaking through my shoulders; let us mount and get away from this;

[080-021]I’ll keep from braying, but not from saying that knights-errant fly and

[080-022]leave their good squires to be pounded like privet, or made meal of at

[080-023]the hands of their enemies.”

[080-024]“He does not fly who retires,” returned Don Quixote; “for I would have

[080-025]thee know, Sancho, that the valour which is not based upon a foundation

[080-026]of prudence is called rashness, and the exploits of the rash man are to

[080-027]be attributed rather to good fortune than to courage; and so I own that

[080-028]I retired, but not that I fled; and therein I have followed the example

[080-029]of many valiant men who have reserved themselves for better times; the

[080-030]histories are full of instances of this, but as it would not be any

[080-031]good to thee or pleasure to me, I will not recount them to thee now.”

[080-032]Sancho was by this time mounted with the help of Don Quixote, who then

[080-033]himself mounted Rocinante, and at a leisurely pace they proceeded to

[080-034]take shelter in a grove which was in sight about a quarter of a league

[080-035]off. Every now and then Sancho gave vent to deep sighs and dismal

[080-036]groans, and on Don Quixote asking him what caused such acute suffering,

[080-037]he replied that, from the end of his back-bone up to the nape of his

[080-038]neck, he was so sore that it nearly drove him out of his senses.

[080-039]“The cause of that soreness,” said Don Quixote, “will be, no doubt,

[080-040]that the staff wherewith they smote thee being a very long one, it

[080-041]caught thee all down the back, where all the parts that are sore are

[080-042]situated, and had it reached any further thou wouldst be sorer still.”

[080-043]“By God,” said Sancho, “your worship has relieved me of a great doubt,

[080-044]and cleared up the point for me in elegant style! Body o’ me! is the

[080-045]cause of my soreness such a mystery that there’s any need to tell me I

[080-046]am sore everywhere the staff hit me? If it was my ankles that pained me

[080-047]there might be something in going divining why they did, but it is not

[080-048]much to divine that I’m sore where they thrashed me. By my faith,

[080-049]master mine, the ills of others hang by a hair; every day I am

[080-050]discovering more and more how little I have to hope for from keeping

[080-051]company with your worship; for if this time you have allowed me to be

[080-052]drubbed, the next time, or a hundred times more, we’ll have the

[080-053]blanketings of the other day over again, and all the other pranks

[080-054]which, if they have fallen on my shoulders now, will be thrown in my

[080-055]teeth by-and-by. I would do a great deal better (if I was not an

[080-056]ignorant brute that will never do any good all my life), I would do a

[080-057]great deal better, I say, to go home to my wife and children and

[080-058]support them and bring them up on what God may please to give me,

[080-059]instead of following your worship along roads that lead nowhere and

[080-060]paths that are none at all, with little to drink and less to eat. And

[080-061]then when it comes to sleeping! Measure out seven feet on the earth,

[080-062]brother squire, and if that’s not enough for you, take as many more,

[080-063]for you may have it all your own way and stretch yourself to your

[080-064]heart’s content. Oh that I could see burnt and turned to ashes the

[080-065]first man that meddled with knight-errantry or at any rate the first

[080-066]who chose to be squire to such fools as all the knights-errant of past

[080-067]times must have been! Of those of the present day I say nothing,

[080-068]because, as your worship is one of them, I respect them, and because I

[080-069]know your worship knows a point more than the devil in all you say and

[080-070]think.”

[080-071]“I would lay a good wager with you, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that

[080-072]now that you are talking on without anyone to stop you, you don’t feel

[080-073]a pain in your whole body. Talk away, my son, say whatever comes into

[080-074]your head or mouth, for so long as you feel no pain, the irritation

[080-075]your impertinences give me will be a pleasure to me; and if you are so

[080-076]anxious to go home to your wife and children, God forbid that I should

[080-077]prevent you; you have money of mine; see how long it is since we left

[080-078]our village this third time, and how much you can and ought to earn

[080-079]every month, and pay yourself out of your own hand.”

[080-080]“When I worked for Tom Carrasco, the father of the bachelor Samson

[080-081]Carrasco that your worship knows,” replied Sancho, “I used to earn two

[080-082]ducats a month besides my food; I can’t tell what I can earn with your

[080-083]worship, though I know a knight-errant’s squire has harder times of it

[080-084]than he who works for a farmer; for after all, we who work for farmers,

[080-085]however much we toil all day, at the worst, at night, we have our olla

[080-086]supper and sleep in a bed, which I have not slept in since I have been

[080-087]in your worship’s service, if it wasn’t the short time we were in Don

[080-088]Diego de Miranda’s house, and the feast I had with the skimmings I took

[080-089]off Camacho’s pots, and what I ate, drank, and slept in Basilio’s

[080-090]house; all the rest of the time I have been sleeping on the hard ground

[080-091]under the open sky, exposed to what they call the inclemencies of

[080-092]heaven, keeping life in me with scraps of cheese and crusts of bread,

[080-093]and drinking water either from the brooks or from the springs we come

[080-094]to on these by-paths we travel.”

[080-095]“I own, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “that all thou sayest is true; how

[080-096]much, thinkest thou, ought I to give thee over and above what Tom

[080-097]Carrasco gave thee?”

[080-098]“I think,” said Sancho, “that if your worship was to add on two reals a

[080-099]month I’d consider myself well paid; that is, as far as the wages of my

[080-100]labour go; but to make up to me for your worship’s pledge and promise

[080-101]to me to give me the government of an island, it would be fair to add

[080-102]six reals more, making thirty in all.”

[080-103]“Very good,” said Don Quixote; “it is twenty-five days since we left

[080-104]our village, so reckon up, Sancho, according to the wages you have made

[080-105]out for yourself, and see how much I owe you in proportion, and pay

[080-106]yourself, as I said before, out of your own hand.”

[080-107]“O body o’ me!” said Sancho, “but your worship is very much out in that

[080-108]reckoning; for when it comes to the promise of the island we must count

[080-109]from the day your worship promised it to me to this present hour we are

[080-110]at now.”

[080-111]“Well, how long is it, Sancho, since I promised it to you?” said Don

[080-112]Quixote.

[080-113]“If I remember rightly,” said Sancho, “it must be over twenty years,

[080-114]three days more or less.”

[080-115]Don Quixote gave himself a great slap on the forehead and began to

[080-116]laugh heartily, and said he, “Why, I have not been wandering, either in

[080-117]the Sierra Morena or in the whole course of our sallies, but barely two

[080-118]months, and thou sayest, Sancho, that it is twenty years since I

[080-119]promised thee the island. I believe now thou wouldst have all the money

[080-120]thou hast of mine go in thy wages. If so, and if that be thy pleasure,

[080-121]I give it to thee now, once and for all, and much good may it do thee,

[080-122]for so long as I see myself rid of such a good-for-nothing squire I’ll

[080-123]be glad to be left a pauper without a rap. But tell me, thou perverter

[080-124]of the squirely rules of knight-errantry, where hast thou ever seen or

[080-125]read that any knight-errant’s squire made terms with his lord, ‘you

[080-126]must give me so much a month for serving you’? Plunge, scoundrel,

[080-127]rogue, monster—for such I take thee to be—plunge, I say, into the _mare

[080-128]magnum_ of their histories; and if thou shalt find that any squire ever

[080-129]said or thought what thou hast said now, I will let thee nail it on my

[080-130]forehead, and give me, over and above, four sound slaps in the face.

[080-131]Turn the rein, or the halter, of thy Dapple, and begone home; for one

[080-132]single step further thou shalt not make in my company. O bread

[080-133]thanklessly received! O promises ill-bestowed! O man more beast than

[080-134]human being! Now, when I was about to raise thee to such a position,

[080-135]that, in spite of thy wife, they would call thee ‘my lord,’ thou art

[080-136]leaving me? Thou art going now when I had a firm and fixed intention of

[080-137]making thee lord of the best island in the world? Well, as thou thyself

[080-138]hast said before now, honey is not for the mouth of the ass. Ass thou

[080-139]art, ass thou wilt be, and ass thou wilt end when the course of thy

[080-140]life is run; for I know it will come to its close before thou dost

[080-141]perceive or discern that thou art a beast.”

[080-142]Sancho regarded Don Quixote earnestly while he was giving him this

[080-143]rating, and was so touched by remorse that the tears came to his eyes,

[080-144]and in a piteous and broken voice he said to him, “Master mine, I

[080-145]confess that, to be a complete ass, all I want is a tail; if your

[080-146]worship will only fix one on to me, I’ll look on it as rightly placed,

[080-147]and I’ll serve you as an ass all the remaining days of my life. Forgive

[080-148]me and have pity on my folly, and remember I know but little, and, if I

[080-149]talk much, it’s more from infirmity than malice; but he who sins and

[080-150]mends commends himself to God.”

[080-151]“I should have been surprised, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “if thou

[080-152]hadst not introduced some bit of a proverb into thy speech. Well, well,

[080-153]I forgive thee, provided thou dost mend and not show thyself in future

[080-154]so fond of thine own interest, but try to be of good cheer and take

[080-155]heart, and encourage thyself to look forward to the fulfillment of my

[080-156]promises, which, by being delayed, does not become impossible.”

[080-157]Sancho said he would do so, and keep up his heart as best he could.

[080-158]They then entered the grove, and Don Quixote settled himself at the

[080-159]foot of an elm, and Sancho at that of a beech, for trees of this kind

[080-160]and others like them always have feet but no hands. Sancho passed the

[080-161]night in pain, for with the evening dews the blow of the staff made

[080-162]itself felt all the more. Don Quixote passed it in his never-failing

[080-163]meditations; but, for all that, they had some winks of sleep, and with

[080-164]the appearance of daylight they pursued their journey in quest of the

[080-165]banks of the famous Ebro, where that befell them which will be told in

[080-166]the following chapter.
