OF THE STRANGE ADVENTURE WHICH THE VALIANT DON QUIXOTE HAD WITH THE CAR

[063-001]OR CART OF “THE CORTES OF DEATH”

p11a.jpg (172K)


[063-002]Dejected beyond measure did Don Quixote pursue his journey, turning

[063-003]over in his mind the cruel trick the enchanters had played him in

[063-004]changing his lady Dulcinea into the vile shape of the village lass, nor

[063-005]could he think of any way of restoring her to her original form; and

[063-006]these reflections so absorbed him, that without being aware of it he

[063-007]let go Rocinante’s bridle, and he, perceiving the liberty that was

[063-008]granted him, stopped at every step to crop the fresh grass with which

[063-009]the plain abounded.

[063-010]Sancho recalled him from his reverie. “Melancholy, señor,” said he,

[063-011]“was made, not for beasts, but for men; but if men give way to it

[063-012]overmuch they turn to beasts; control yourself, your worship; be

[063-013]yourself again; gather up Rocinante’s reins; cheer up, rouse yourself

[063-014]and show that gallant spirit that knights-errant ought to have. What

[063-015]the devil is this? What weakness is this? Are we here or in France? The

[063-016]devil fly away with all the Dulcineas in the world; for the well-being

[063-017]of a single knight-errant is of more consequence than all the

[063-018]enchantments and transformations on earth.”

[063-019]“Hush, Sancho,” said Don Quixote in a weak and faint voice, “hush and

[063-020]utter no blasphemies against that enchanted lady; for I alone am to

[063-021]blame for her misfortune and hard fate; her calamity has come of the

[063-022]hatred the wicked bear me.”

[063-023]“So say I,” returned Sancho; “his heart rend in twain, I trow, who saw

[063-024]her once, to see her now.”

[063-025]“Thou mayest well say that, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “as thou

[063-026]sawest her in the full perfection of her beauty; for the enchantment

[063-027]does not go so far as to pervert thy vision or hide her loveliness from

[063-028]thee; against me alone and against my eyes is the strength of its venom

[063-029]directed. Nevertheless, there is one thing which has occurred to me,

[063-030]and that is that thou didst ill describe her beauty to me, for, as well

[063-031]as I recollect, thou saidst that her eyes were pearls; but eyes that

[063-032]are like pearls are rather the eyes of a sea-bream than of a lady, and

[063-033]I am persuaded that Dulcinea’s must be green emeralds, full and soft,

[063-034]with two rainbows for eyebrows; take away those pearls from her eyes

[063-035]and transfer them to her teeth; for beyond a doubt, Sancho, thou hast

[063-036]taken the one for the other, the eyes for the teeth.”

[063-037]“Very likely,” said Sancho; “for her beauty bewildered me as much as

[063-038]her ugliness did your worship; but let us leave it all to God, who

[063-039]alone knows what is to happen in this vale of tears, in this evil world

[063-040]of ours, where there is hardly a thing to be found without some mixture

[063-041]of wickedness, roguery, and rascality. But one thing, señor, troubles

[063-042]me more than all the rest, and that is thinking what is to be done when

[063-043]your worship conquers some giant, or some other knight, and orders him

[063-044]to go and present himself before the beauty of the lady Dulcinea. Where

[063-045]is this poor giant, or this poor wretch of a vanquished knight, to find

[063-046]her? I think I can see them wandering all over El Toboso, looking like

[063-047]noddies, and asking for my lady Dulcinea; and even if they meet her in

[063-048]the middle of the street they won’t know her any more than they would

[063-049]my father.”

[063-050]“Perhaps, Sancho,” returned Don Quixote, “the enchantment does not go

[063-051]so far as to deprive conquered and presented giants and knights of the

[063-052]power of recognising Dulcinea; we will try by experiment with one or

[063-053]two of the first I vanquish and send to her, whether they see her or

[063-054]not, by commanding them to return and give me an account of what

[063-055]happened to them in this respect.”

[063-056]“I declare, I think what your worship has proposed is excellent,” said

[063-057]Sancho; “and that by this plan we shall find out what we want to know;

[063-058]and if it be that it is only from your worship she is hidden, the

[063-059]misfortune will be more yours than hers; but so long as the lady

[063-060]Dulcinea is well and happy, we on our part will make the best of it,

[063-061]and get on as well as we can, seeking our adventures, and leaving Time

[063-062]to take his own course; for he is the best physician for these and

[063-063]greater ailments.”

[063-064]Don Quixote was about to reply to Sancho Panza, but he was prevented by

[063-065]a cart crossing the road full of the most diverse and strange

[063-066]personages and figures that could be imagined. He who led the mules and

[063-067]acted as carter was a hideous demon; the cart was open to the sky,

[063-068]without a tilt or cane roof, and the first figure that presented itself

[063-069]to Don Quixote’s eyes was that of Death itself with a human face; next

[063-070]to it was an angel with large painted wings, and at one side an

[063-071]emperor, with a crown, to all appearance of gold, on his head. At the

[063-072]feet of Death was the god called Cupid, without his bandage, but with

[063-073]his bow, quiver, and arrows; there was also a knight in full armour,

[063-074]except that he had no morion or helmet, but only a hat decked with

[063-075]plumes of divers colours; and along with these there were others with a

[063-076]variety of costumes and faces. All this, unexpectedly encountered, took

[063-077]Don Quixote somewhat aback, and struck terror into the heart of Sancho;

[063-078]but the next instant Don Quixote was glad of it, believing that some

[063-079]new perilous adventure was presenting itself to him, and under this

[063-080]impression, and with a spirit prepared to face any danger, he planted

[063-081]himself in front of the cart, and in a loud and menacing tone,

[063-082]exclaimed, “Carter, or coachman, or devil, or whatever thou art, tell

[063-083]me at once who thou art, whither thou art going, and who these folk are

[063-084]thou carriest in thy wagon, which looks more like Charon’s boat than an

[063-085]ordinary cart.”

[063-086]To which the devil, stopping the cart, answered quietly, “Señor, we are

[063-087]players of Angulo el Malo’s company; we have been acting the play of

[063-088]‘The Cortés of Death’ this morning, which is the octave of Corpus

[063-089]Christi, in a village behind that hill, and we have to act it this

[063-090]afternoon in that village which you can see from this; and as it is so

[063-091]near, and to save the trouble of undressing and dressing again, we go

[063-092]in the costumes in which we perform. That lad there appears as Death,

[063-093]that other as an angel, that woman, the manager’s wife, plays the

[063-094]queen, this one the soldier, that the emperor, and I the devil; and I

[063-095]am one of the principal characters of the play, for in this company I

[063-096]take the leading parts. If you want to know anything more about us, ask

[063-097]me and I will answer with the utmost exactitude, for as I am a devil I

[063-098]am up to everything.”

[063-099]“By the faith of a knight-errant,” replied Don Quixote, “when I saw

[063-100]this cart I fancied some great adventure was presenting itself to me;

[063-101]but I declare one must touch with the hand what appears to the eye, if

[063-102]illusions are to be avoided. God speed you, good people; keep your

[063-103]festival, and remember, if you demand of me ought wherein I can render

[063-104]you a service, I will do it gladly and willingly, for from a child I

[063-105]was fond of the play, and in my youth a keen lover of the actor’s art.”

[063-106]While they were talking, fate so willed it that one of the company in a

[063-107]mummers’ dress with a great number of bells, and armed with three blown

[063-108]ox-bladders at the end of a stick, joined them, and this merry-andrew

[063-109]approaching Don Quixote, began flourishing his stick and banging the

[063-110]ground with the bladders and cutting capers with great jingling of the

[063-111]bells, which untoward apparition so startled Rocinante that, in spite

[063-112]of Don Quixote’s efforts to hold him in, taking the bit between his

[063-113]teeth he set off across the plain with greater speed than the bones of

[063-114]his anatomy ever gave any promise of.

[063-115]Sancho, who thought his master was in danger of being thrown, jumped

[063-116]off Dapple, and ran in all haste to help him; but by the time he

[063-117]reached him he was already on the ground, and beside him was Rocinante,

[063-118]who had come down with his master, the usual end and upshot of

[063-119]Rocinante’s vivacity and high spirits. But the moment Sancho quitted

[063-120]his beast to go and help Don Quixote, the dancing devil with the

[063-121]bladders jumped up on Dapple, and beating him with them, more by the

[063-122]fright and the noise than by the pain of the blows, made him fly across

[063-123]the fields towards the village where they were going to hold their

[063-124]festival. Sancho witnessed Dapple’s career and his master’s fall, and

[063-125]did not know which of the two cases of need he should attend to first;

[063-126]but in the end, like a good squire and good servant, he let his love

[063-127]for his master prevail over his affection for his ass; though every

[063-128]time he saw the bladders rise in the air and come down on the hind

[063-129]quarters of his Dapple he felt the pains and terrors of death, and he

[063-130]would have rather had the blows fall on the apples of his own eyes than

[063-131]on the least hair of his ass’s tail. In this trouble and perplexity he

[063-132]came to where Don Quixote lay in a far sorrier plight than he liked,

[063-133]and having helped him to mount Rocinante, he said to him, “Señor, the

[063-134]devil has carried off my Dapple.”

[063-135]“What devil?” asked Don Quixote.

[063-136]“The one with the bladders,” said Sancho.

[063-137]“Then I will recover him,” said Don Quixote, “even if he be shut up

[063-138]with him in the deepest and darkest dungeons of hell. Follow me,

[063-139]Sancho, for the cart goes slowly, and with the mules of it I will make

[063-140]good the loss of Dapple.”

[063-141]“You need not take the trouble, señor,” said Sancho; “keep cool, for as

[063-142]I now see, the devil has let Dapple go and he is coming back to his old

[063-143]quarters;” and so it turned out, for, having come down with Dapple, in

[063-144]imitation of Don Quixote and Rocinante, the devil made off on foot to

[063-145]the town, and the ass came back to his master.

[063-146]“For all that,” said Don Quixote, “it will be well to visit the

[063-147]discourtesy of that devil upon some of those in the cart, even if it

[063-148]were the emperor himself.”

[063-149]“Don’t think of it, your worship,” returned Sancho; “take my advice and

[063-150]never meddle with actors, for they are a favoured class; I myself have

[063-151]known an actor taken up for two murders, and yet come off scot-free;

[063-152]remember that, as they are merry folk who give pleasure, everyone

[063-153]favours and protects them, and helps and makes much of them, above all

[063-154]when they are those of the royal companies and under patent, all or

[063-155]most of whom in dress and appearance look like princes.”

[063-156]“Still, for all that,” said Don Quixote, “the player devil must not go

[063-157]off boasting, even if the whole human race favours him.”

[063-158]So saying, he made for the cart, which was now very near the town,

[063-159]shouting out as he went, “Stay! halt! ye merry, jovial crew! I want to

[063-160]teach you how to treat asses and animals that serve the squires of

[063-161]knights-errant for steeds.”

[063-162]So loud were the shouts of Don Quixote, that those in the cart heard

[063-163]and understood them, and, guessing by the words what the speaker’s

[063-164]intention was, Death in an instant jumped out of the cart, and the

[063-165]emperor, the devil carter and the angel after him, nor did the queen or

[063-166]the god Cupid stay behind; and all armed themselves with stones and

[063-167]formed in line, prepared to receive Don Quixote on the points of their

[063-168]pebbles. Don Quixote, when he saw them drawn up in such a gallant array

[063-169]with uplifted arms ready for a mighty discharge of stones, checked

[063-170]Rocinante and began to consider in what way he could attack them with

[063-171]the least danger to himself. As he halted Sancho came up, and seeing

[063-172]him disposed to attack this well-ordered squadron, said to him, “It

[063-173]would be the height of madness to attempt such an enterprise; remember,

[063-174]señor, that against sops from the brook, and plenty of them, there is

[063-175]no defensive armour in the world, except to stow oneself away under a

[063-176]brass bell; and besides, one should remember that it is rashness, and

[063-177]not valour, for a single man to attack an army that has Death in it,

[063-178]and where emperors fight in person, with angels, good and bad, to help

[063-179]them; and if this reflection will not make you keep quiet, perhaps it

[063-180]will to know for certain that among all these, though they look like

[063-181]kings, princes, and emperors, there is not a single knight-errant.”

[063-182]“Now indeed thou hast hit the point, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “which

[063-183]may and should turn me from the resolution I had already formed. I

[063-184]cannot and must not draw sword, as I have many a time before told thee,

[063-185]against anyone who is not a dubbed knight; it is for thee, Sancho, if

[063-186]thou wilt, to take vengeance for the wrong done to thy Dapple; and I

[063-187]will help thee from here by shouts and salutary counsels.”

[063-188]“There is no occasion to take vengeance on anyone, señor,” replied

[063-189]Sancho; “for it is not the part of good Christians to revenge wrongs;

[063-190]and besides, I will arrange it with my ass to leave his grievance to my

[063-191]good-will and pleasure, and that is to live in peace as long as heaven

[063-192]grants me life.”

[063-193]“Well,” said Don Quixote, “if that be thy determination, good Sancho,

[063-194]sensible Sancho, Christian Sancho, honest Sancho, let us leave these

[063-195]phantoms alone and turn to the pursuit of better and worthier

[063-196]adventures; for, from what I see of this country, we cannot fail to

[063-197]find plenty of marvellous ones in it.”

[063-198]He at once wheeled about, Sancho ran to take possession of his Dapple,

[063-199]Death and his flying squadron returned to their cart and pursued their

[063-200]journey, and thus the dread adventure of the cart of Death ended

[063-201]happily, thanks to the advice Sancho gave his master; who had, the

[063-202]following day, a fresh adventure, of no less thrilling interest than

[063-203]the last, with an enamoured knight-errant.
