[103-001]OF THE PROGRESS OF SANCHO’S GOVERNMENT, AND OTHER SUCH ENTERTAINING

MATTERS

[103-002]Day came after the night of the governor’s round; a night which the

[103-003]head-carver passed without sleeping, so were his thoughts of the face

[103-004]and air and beauty of the disguised damsel, while the majordomo spent

[103-005]what was left of it in writing an account to his lord and lady of all

[103-006]Sancho said and did, being as much amazed at his sayings as at his

[103-007]doings, for there was a mixture of shrewdness and simplicity in all his

[103-008]words and deeds. The señor governor got up, and by Doctor Pedro Recio’s

[103-009]directions they made him break his fast on a little conserve and four

[103-010]sups of cold water, which Sancho would have readily exchanged for a

[103-011]piece of bread and a bunch of grapes; but seeing there was no help for

[103-012]it, he submitted with no little sorrow of heart and discomfort of

[103-013]stomach; Pedro Recio having persuaded him that light and delicate diet

[103-014]enlivened the wits, and that was what was most essential for persons

[103-015]placed in command and in responsible situations, where they have to

[103-016]employ not only the bodily powers but those of the mind also.

[103-017]By means of this sophistry Sancho was made to endure hunger, and hunger

[103-018]so keen that in his heart he cursed the government, and even him who

[103-019]had given it to him; however, with his hunger and his conserve he

[103-020]undertook to deliver judgments that day, and the first thing that came

[103-021]before him was a question that was submitted to him by a stranger, in

[103-022]the presence of the majordomo and the other attendants, and it was in

[103-023]these words: “Señor, a large river separated two districts of one and

[103-024]the same lordship—will your worship please to pay attention, for the

[103-025]case is an important and a rather knotty one? Well then, on this river

[103-026]there was a bridge, and at one end of it a gallows, and a sort of

[103-027]tribunal, where four judges commonly sat to administer the law which

[103-028]the lord of river, bridge and the lordship had enacted, and which was

[103-029]to this effect, ‘If anyone crosses by this bridge from one side to the

[103-030]other he shall declare on oath where he is going to and with what

[103-031]object; and if he swears truly, he shall be allowed to pass, but if

[103-032]falsely, he shall be put to death for it by hanging on the gallows

[103-033]erected there, without any remission.’ Though the law and its severe

[103-034]penalty were known, many persons crossed, but in their declarations it

[103-035]was easy to see at once they were telling the truth, and the judges let

[103-036]them pass free. It happened, however, that one man, when they came to

[103-037]take his declaration, swore and said that by the oath he took he was

[103-038]going to die upon that gallows that stood there, and nothing else. The

[103-039]judges held a consultation over the oath, and they said, ‘If we let

[103-040]this man pass free he has sworn falsely, and by the law he ought to

[103-041]die; but if we hang him, as he swore he was going to die on that

[103-042]gallows, and therefore swore the truth, by the same law he ought to go

[103-043]free.’ It is asked of your worship, señor governor, what are the judges

[103-044]to do with this man? For they are still in doubt and perplexity; and

[103-045]having heard of your worship’s acute and exalted intellect, they have

[103-046]sent me to entreat your worship on their behalf to give your opinion on

[103-047]this very intricate and puzzling case.”

[103-048]To this Sancho made answer, “Indeed those gentlemen the judges that

[103-049]send you to me might have spared themselves the trouble, for I have

[103-050]more of the obtuse than the acute in me; but repeat the case over

[103-051]again, so that I may understand it, and then perhaps I may be able to

[103-052]hit the point.”

[103-053]The querist repeated again and again what he had said before, and then

[103-054]Sancho said, “It seems to me I can set the matter right in a moment,

[103-055]and in this way; the man swears that he is going to die upon the

[103-056]gallows; but if he dies upon it, he has sworn the truth, and by the law

[103-057]enacted deserves to go free and pass over the bridge; but if they don’t

[103-058]hang him, then he has sworn falsely, and by the same law deserves to be

[103-059]hanged.”

[103-060]“It is as the señor governor says,” said the messenger; “and as regards

[103-061]a complete comprehension of the case, there is nothing left to desire

[103-062]or hesitate about.”

[103-063]“Well then I say,” said Sancho, “that of this man they should let pass

[103-064]the part that has sworn truly, and hang the part that has lied; and in

[103-065]this way the conditions of the passage will be fully complied with.”

[103-066]“But then, señor governor,” replied the querist, “the man will have to

[103-067]be divided into two parts; and if he is divided of course he will die;

[103-068]and so none of the requirements of the law will be carried out, and it

[103-069]is absolutely necessary to comply with it.”

[103-070]“Look here, my good sir,” said Sancho; “either I’m a numskull or else

[103-071]there is the same reason for this passenger dying as for his living and

[103-072]passing over the bridge; for if the truth saves him the falsehood

[103-073]equally condemns him; and that being the case it is my opinion you

[103-074]should say to the gentlemen who sent you to me that as the arguments

[103-075]for condemning him and for absolving him are exactly balanced, they

[103-076]should let him pass freely, as it is always more praiseworthy to do

[103-077]good than to do evil; this I would give signed with my name if I knew

[103-078]how to sign; and what I have said in this case is not out of my own

[103-079]head, but one of the many precepts my master Don Quixote gave me the

[103-080]night before I left to become governor of this island, that came into

[103-081]my mind, and it was this, that when there was any doubt about the

[103-082]justice of a case I should lean to mercy; and it is God’s will that I

[103-083]should recollect it now, for it fits this case as if it was made for

[103-084]it.”

[103-085]“That is true,” said the majordomo; “and I maintain that Lycurgus

[103-086]himself, who gave laws to the Lacedemonians, could not have pronounced

[103-087]a better decision than the great Panza has given; let the morning’s

[103-088]audience close with this, and I will see that the señor governor has

[103-089]dinner entirely to his liking.”

[103-090]“That’s all I ask for—fair play,” said Sancho; “give me my dinner, and

[103-091]then let it rain cases and questions on me, and I’ll despatch them in a

[103-092]twinkling.”

[103-093]The majordomo kept his word, for he felt it against his conscience to

[103-094]kill so wise a governor by hunger; particularly as he intended to have

[103-095]done with him that same night, playing off the last joke he was

[103-096]commissioned to practise upon him.

[103-097]It came to pass, then, that after he had dined that day, in opposition

[103-098]to the rules and aphorisms of Doctor Tirteafuera, as they were taking

[103-099]away the cloth there came a courier with a letter from Don Quixote for

[103-100]the governor. Sancho ordered the secretary to read it to himself, and

[103-101]if there was nothing in it that demanded secrecy to read it aloud. The

[103-102]secretary did so, and after he had skimmed the contents he said, “It

[103-103]may well be read aloud, for what Señor Don Quixote writes to your

[103-104]worship deserves to be printed or written in letters of gold, and it is

[103-105]as follows.”

[103-106]DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA’S LETTER TO SANCHO PANZA, GOVERNOR OF THE

[103-107]ISLAND OF BARATARIA.

[103-108]When I was expecting to hear of thy stupidities and blunders, friend

[103-109]Sancho, I have received intelligence of thy displays of good sense, for

[103-110]which I give special thanks to heaven that can raise the poor from the

[103-111]dunghill and of fools to make wise men. They tell me thou dost govern

[103-112]as if thou wert a man, and art a man as if thou wert a beast, so great

[103-113]is the humility wherewith thou dost comport thyself. But I would have

[103-114]thee bear in mind, Sancho, that very often it is fitting and necessary

[103-115]for the authority of office to resist the humility of the heart; for

[103-116]the seemly array of one who is invested with grave duties should be

[103-117]such as they require and not measured by what his own humble tastes may

[103-118]lead him to prefer. Dress well; a stick dressed up does not look like a

[103-119]stick; I do not say thou shouldst wear trinkets or fine raiment, or

[103-120]that being a judge thou shouldst dress like a soldier, but that thou

[103-121]shouldst array thyself in the apparel thy office requires, and that at

[103-122]the same time it be neat and handsome. To win the good-will of the

[103-123]people thou governest there are two things, among others, that thou

[103-124]must do; one is to be civil to all (this, however, I told thee before),

[103-125]and the other to take care that food be abundant, for there is nothing

[103-126]that vexes the heart of the poor more than hunger and high prices. Make

[103-127]not many proclamations; but those thou makest take care that they be

[103-128]good ones, and above all that they be observed and carried out; for

[103-129]proclamations that are not observed are the same as if they did not

[103-130]exist; nay, they encourage the idea that the prince who had the wisdom

[103-131]and authority to make them had not the power to enforce them; and laws

[103-132]that threaten and are not enforced come to be like the log, the king of

[103-133]the frogs, that frightened them at first, but that in time they

[103-134]despised and mounted upon. Be a father to virtue and a stepfather to

[103-135]vice. Be not always strict, nor yet always lenient, but observe a mean

[103-136]between these two extremes, for in that is the aim of wisdom. Visit the

[103-137]gaols, the slaughter-houses, and the market-places; for the presence of

[103-138]the governor is of great importance in such places; it comforts the

[103-139]prisoners who are in hopes of a speedy release, it is the bugbear of

[103-140]the butchers who have then to give just weight, and it is the terror of

[103-141]the market-women for the same reason. Let it not be seen that thou art

[103-142](even if perchance thou art, which I do not believe) covetous, a

[103-143]follower of women, or a glutton; for when the people and those that

[103-144]have dealings with thee become aware of thy special weakness they will

[103-145]bring their batteries to bear upon thee in that quarter, till they have

[103-146]brought thee down to the depths of perdition. Consider and reconsider,

[103-147]con and con over again the advices and the instructions I gave thee

[103-148]before thy departure hence to thy government, and thou wilt see that in

[103-149]them, if thou dost follow them, thou hast a help at hand that will

[103-150]lighten for thee the troubles and difficulties that beset governors at

[103-151]every step. Write to thy lord and lady and show thyself grateful to

[103-152]them, for ingratitude is the daughter of pride, and one of the greatest

[103-153]sins we know of; and he who is grateful to those who have been good to

[103-154]him shows that he will be so to God also who has bestowed and still

[103-155]bestows so many blessings upon him.

[103-156]My lady the duchess sent off a messenger with thy suit and another

[103-157]present to thy wife Teresa Panza; we expect the answer every

[103-158]moment. I have been a little indisposed through a certain

[103-159]scratching I came in for, not very much to the benefit of my nose;

[103-160]but it was nothing; for if there are enchanters who maltreat me,

[103-161]there are also some who defend me. Let me know if the majordomo who

[103-162]is with thee had any share in the Trifaldi performance, as thou

[103-163]didst suspect; and keep me informed of everything that happens

[103-164]thee, as the distance is so short; all the more as I am thinking of

[103-165]giving over very shortly this idle life I am now leading, for I was

[103-166]not born for it. A thing has occurred to me which I am inclined to

[103-167]think will put me out of favour with the duke and duchess; but

[103-168]though I am sorry for it I do not care, for after all I must obey

[103-169]my calling rather than their pleasure, in accordance with the

[103-170]common saying, _amicus Plato, sed magis amica veritas_. I quote

[103-171]this Latin to thee because I conclude that since thou hast been a

[103-172]governor thou wilt have learned it. Adieu; God keep thee from being

[103-173]an object of pity to anyone.

[103-174]Thy friend,

[103-175]DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA.

[103-176]Sancho listened to the letter with great attention, and it was praised

[103-177]and considered wise by all who heard it; he then rose up from table,

[103-178]and calling his secretary shut himself in with him in his own room, and

[103-179]without putting it off any longer set about answering his master Don

[103-180]Quixote at once; and he bade the secretary write down what he told him

[103-181]without adding or suppressing anything, which he did, and the answer

[103-182]was to the following effect.

[103-183]SANCHO PANZA’S LETTER TO DON QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA.

[103-184]The pressure of business is so great upon me that I have no time to

[103-185]scratch my head or even to cut my nails; and I have them so long—God

[103-186]send a remedy for it. I say this, master of my soul, that you may not

[103-187]be surprised if I have not until now sent you word of how I fare, well

[103-188]or ill, in this government, in which I am suffering more hunger than

[103-189]when we two were wandering through the woods and wastes.

[103-190]My lord the duke wrote to me the other day to warn me that certain

[103-191]spies had got into this island to kill me; but up to the present I

[103-192]have not found out any except a certain doctor who receives a

[103-193]salary in this town for killing all the governors that come here;

[103-194]he is called Doctor Pedro Recio, and is from Tirteafuera; so you

[103-195]see what a name he has to make me dread dying under his hands. This

[103-196]doctor says of himself that he does not cure diseases when there

[103-197]are any, but prevents them coming, and the medicines he uses are

[103-198]diet and more diet until he brings one down to bare bones; as if

[103-199]leanness was not worse than fever.

[103-200]In short he is killing me with hunger, and I am dying myself of

[103-201]vexation; for when I thought I was coming to this government to get

[103-202]my meat hot and my drink cool, and take my ease between holland

[103-203]sheets on feather beds, I find I have come to do penance as if I

[103-204]was a hermit; and as I don’t do it willingly I suspect that in the

[103-205]end the devil will carry me off.

[103-206]So far I have not handled any dues or taken any bribes, and I don’t

[103-207]know what to think of it; for here they tell me that the governors

[103-208]that come to this island, before entering it have plenty of money

[103-209]either given to them or lent to them by the people of the town, and

[103-210]that this is the usual custom not only here but with all who enter

[103-211]upon governments.

[103-212]Last night going the rounds I came upon a fair damsel in man’s

[103-213]clothes, and a brother of hers dressed as a woman; my head-carver

[103-214]has fallen in love with the girl, and has in his own mind chosen

[103-215]her for a wife, so he says, and I have chosen the youth for a

[103-216]son-in-law; to-day we are going to explain our intentions to the

[103-217]father of the pair, who is one Diego de la Llana, a gentleman and

[103-218]an old Christian as much as you please.

[103-219]I have visited the market-places, as your worship advises me, and

[103-220]yesterday I found a stall-keeper selling new hazel nuts and proved

[103-221]her to have mixed a bushel of old empty rotten nuts with a bushel

[103-222]of new; I confiscated the whole for the children of the

[103-223]charity-school, who will know how to distinguish them well enough,

[103-224]and I sentenced her not to come into the market-place for a

[103-225]fortnight; they told me I did bravely. I can tell your worship it

[103-226]is commonly said in this town that there are no people worse than

[103-227]the market-women, for they are all barefaced, unconscionable, and

[103-228]impudent, and I can well believe it from what I have seen of them

[103-229]in other towns.

[103-230]I am very glad my lady the duchess has written to my wife Teresa

[103-231]Panza and sent her the present your worship speaks of; and I will

[103-232]strive to show myself grateful when the time comes; kiss her hands

[103-233]for me, and tell her I say she has not thrown it into a sack with a

[103-234]hole in it, as she will see in the end. I should not like your

[103-235]worship to have any difference with my lord and lady; for if you

[103-236]fall out with them it is plain it must do me harm; and as you give

[103-237]me advice to be grateful it will not do for your worship not to be

[103-238]so yourself to those who have shown you such kindness, and by whom

[103-239]you have been treated so hospitably in their castle.

[103-240]That about the scratching I don’t understand; but I suppose it must

[103-241]be one of the ill-turns the wicked enchanters are always doing your

[103-242]worship; when we meet I shall know all about it. I wish I could

[103-243]send your worship something; but I don’t know what to send, unless

[103-244]it be some very curious clyster pipes, to work with bladders, that

[103-245]they make in this island; but if the office remains with me I’ll

[103-246]find out something to send, one way or another. If my wife Teresa

[103-247]Panza writes to me, pay the postage and send me the letter, for I

[103-248]have a very great desire to hear how my house and wife and children

[103-249]are going on. And so, may God deliver your worship from evil-minded

[103-250]enchanters, and bring me well and peacefully out of this

[103-251]government, which I doubt, for I expect to take leave of it and my

[103-252]life together, from the way Doctor Pedro Recio treats me.

[103-253]Your worship’s servant

[103-254]SANCHO PANZA THE GOVERNOR.

[103-255]The secretary sealed the letter, and immediately dismissed the courier;

[103-256]and those who were carrying on the joke against Sancho putting their

[103-257]heads together arranged how he was to be dismissed from the government.

[103-258]Sancho spent the afternoon in drawing up certain ordinances relating to

[103-259]the good government of what he fancied the island; and he ordained that

[103-260]there were to be no provision hucksters in the State, and that men

[103-261]might import wine into it from any place they pleased, provided they

[103-262]declared the quarter it came from, so that a price might be put upon it

[103-263]according to its quality, reputation, and the estimation it was held

[103-264]in; and he that watered his wine, or changed the name, was to forfeit

[103-265]his life for it. He reduced the prices of all manner of shoes, boots,

[103-266]and stockings, but of shoes in particular, as they seemed to him to run

[103-267]extravagantly high. He established a fixed rate for servants’ wages,

[103-268]which were becoming recklessly exorbitant. He laid extremely heavy

[103-269]penalties upon those who sang lewd or loose songs either by day or

[103-270]night. He decreed that no blind man should sing of any miracle in

[103-271]verse, unless he could produce authentic evidence that it was true, for

[103-272]it was his opinion that most of those the blind men sing are trumped

[103-273]up, to the detriment of the true ones. He established and created an

[103-274]alguacil of the poor, not to harass them, but to examine them and see

[103-275]whether they really were so; for many a sturdy thief or drunkard goes

[103-276]about under cover of a make-believe crippled limb or a sham sore. In a

[103-277]word, he made so many good rules that to this day they are preserved

[103-278]there, and are called _The constitutions of the great governor Sancho

[103-279]Panza_.
