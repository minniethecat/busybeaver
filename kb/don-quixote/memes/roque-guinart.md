# Roque Guinart

**Roque Guinart** is a Catalan bandit leader who appears in the closing chapters of Part II as Don Quixote and Sancho approach Barcelona. Based on the real-life figure **Perot Rocaguinarda** (1582–1635), a celebrated Catalan outlaw who was eventually pardoned by the Crown, Roque Guinart represents Cervantes's engagement with contemporary Spanish social realities — a figure of history intruding into fiction.

## Character Profile

Roque is introduced abruptly: Don Quixote and Sancho, traveling through Catalonia, are surrounded by armed men. Their leader is neither a common criminal nor a knight-errant but something in between — a bandit who operates by a code:

> "Roque Guinart at once perceived that Don Quixote was not in his right senses"[112-122]

Cervantes describes him as physically imposing and intellectually sharp. He commands a band of highwaymen who stop travelers for tribute, yet he maintains discipline among his followers and distributes plunder according to rules rather than whim. When Don Quixote's madness is revealed, Roque's response is not mockery but a kind of professional recognition — he understands men who live outside the law, and the knight-errant, in his delusion, is a fellow traveler.

## Justice Among Outlaws

Roque's banditry has a distinctly political dimension. He is not merely a thief but a figure of social rebellion — a Catalan guerrilla whose activities reflect the tensions between Catalonia and the Spanish Crown in Cervantes's time. His enforcement of internal discipline among his men mirrors the governance structures of legitimate authority:

> "Roque Guinart ordered them to be restored all that they had taken from Don Quixote"[112-264]

The episode most revealing of Roque's character involves Claudia Jerónima, a young woman who has murdered her lover in a fit of jealousy. Roque's response combines rough justice with human sympathy — he neither excuses her crime nor abandons her to despair. This episode marks him as more than a criminal: he is a source of order in a disordered world.

## Don Quixote's Stay

Don Quixote spends three days with Roque's band, and the encounter is notable for its mutual respect. Roque treats the knight with genuine courtesy, even as he recognizes his madness. The bandits, for their part, find Don Quixote entertaining but not contemptible — unlike the aristocrats at the ducal estate, these outlaws approach the knight without cruelty:

> "Long life to Roque Guinart, in spite of all the scoundrels that would work his ruin"[112-352]

This cry from Roque's men echoes the loyalty he commands — a loyalty based not on title or birth but on leadership earned through action.

## Historical Context

The real Perot Rocaguinarda was active in Catalonia during the early 17th century — precisely when Cervantes was writing Part II. By including a living contemporary figure, Cervantes blurs the boundary between fiction and reportage. Roque's presence in the novel also reflects the political realities of banditry in early modern Spain, where outlaws could be folk heroes, rebels, or common criminals depending on one's perspective.

## Thematic Significance

Roque Guinart serves as a final mirror for Don Quixote before the knight's defeat in Barcelona. Like Quixote, Roque lives by a personal code that places him outside conventional society. Unlike Quixote, Roque's code is effective — he achieves in reality what Don Quixote only dreams of. The encounter asks a question the novel never fully answers: is the difference between a knight-errant and a bandit merely a matter of social recognition?
