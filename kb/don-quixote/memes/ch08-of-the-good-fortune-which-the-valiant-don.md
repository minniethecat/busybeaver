OF THE GOOD FORTUNE WHICH THE VALIANT DON QUIXOTE HAD IN THE TERRIBLE

[008-001]AND UNDREAMT-OF ADVENTURE OF THE WINDMILLS, WITH OTHER OCCURRENCES

WORTHY TO BE FITLY RECORDED

c08a.jpg (142K)


[008-002]At this point they came in sight of thirty or forty windmills that

[008-003]there are on that plain, and as soon as Don Quixote saw them he said to

[008-004]his squire, “Fortune is arranging matters for us better than we could

[008-005]have shaped our desires ourselves, for look there, friend Sancho Panza,

[008-006]where thirty or more monstrous giants present themselves, all of whom I

[008-007]mean to engage in battle and slay, and with whose spoils we shall begin

[008-008]to make our fortunes; for this is righteous warfare, and it is God’s

[008-009]good service to sweep so evil a breed from off the face of the earth.”

[008-010]“What giants?” said Sancho Panza.

[008-011]“Those thou seest there,” answered his master, “with the long arms, and

[008-012]some have them nearly two leagues long.”

[008-013]“Look, your worship,” said Sancho; “what we see there are not giants

[008-014]but windmills, and what seem to be their arms are the sails that turned

[008-015]by the wind make the millstone go.”

[008-016]“It is easy to see,” replied Don Quixote, “that thou art not used to

[008-017]this business of adventures; those are giants; and if thou art afraid,

[008-018]away with thee out of this and betake thyself to prayer while I engage

[008-019]them in fierce and unequal combat.”

[008-020]So saying, he gave the spur to his steed Rocinante, heedless of the

[008-021]cries his squire Sancho sent after him, warning him that most certainly

[008-022]they were windmills and not giants he was going to attack. He, however,

[008-023]was so positive they were giants that he neither heard the cries of

[008-024]Sancho, nor perceived, near as he was, what they were, but made at them

[008-025]shouting, “Fly not, cowards and vile beings, for a single knight

[008-026]attacks you.”

[008-027]A slight breeze at this moment sprang up, and the great sails began to

[008-028]move, seeing which Don Quixote exclaimed, “Though ye flourish more arms

[008-029]than the giant Briareus, ye have to reckon with me.”

[008-030]So saying, and commending himself with all his heart to his lady

[008-031]Dulcinea, imploring her to support him in such a peril, with lance in

[008-032]rest and covered by his buckler, he charged at Rocinante’s fullest

[008-033]gallop and fell upon the first mill that stood in front of him; but as

[008-034]he drove his lance-point into the sail the wind whirled it round with

[008-035]such force that it shivered the lance to pieces, sweeping with it horse

[008-036]and rider, who went rolling over on the plain, in a sorry condition.

[008-037]Sancho hastened to his assistance as fast as his ass could go, and when

[008-038]he came up found him unable to move, with such a shock had Rocinante

[008-039]fallen with him.

c08b.jpg (358K)


[008-040]“God bless me!” said Sancho, “did I not tell your worship to mind what

[008-041]you were about, for they were only windmills? and no one could have

[008-042]made any mistake about it but one who had something of the same kind in

[008-043]his head.”

[008-044]“Hush, friend Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “the fortunes of war more

[008-045]than any other are liable to frequent fluctuations; and moreover I

[008-046]think, and it is the truth, that that same sage Friston who carried off

[008-047]my study and books, has turned these giants into mills in order to rob

[008-048]me of the glory of vanquishing them, such is the enmity he bears me;

[008-049]but in the end his wicked arts will avail but little against my good

[008-050]sword.”

c08c.jpg (301K)


[008-051]“God order it as he may,” said Sancho Panza, and helping him to rise

[008-052]got him up again on Rocinante, whose shoulder was half out; and then,

[008-053]discussing the late adventure, they followed the road to Puerto Lapice,

[008-054]for there, said Don Quixote, they could not fail to find adventures in

[008-055]abundance and variety, as it was a great thoroughfare. For all that, he

[008-056]was much grieved at the loss of his lance, and saying so to his squire,

[008-057]he added, “I remember having read how a Spanish knight, Diego Perez de

[008-058]Vargas by name, having broken his sword in battle, tore from an oak a

[008-059]ponderous bough or branch, and with it did such things that day, and

[008-060]pounded so many Moors, that he got the surname of Machuca, and he and

[008-061]his descendants from that day forth were called Vargas y Machuca. I

[008-062]mention this because from the first oak I see I mean to rend such

[008-063]another branch, large and stout like that, with which I am determined

[008-064]and resolved to do such deeds that thou mayest deem thyself very

[008-065]fortunate in being found worthy to come and see them, and be an

[008-066]eyewitness of things that will with difficulty be believed.”

[008-067]“Be that as God will,” said Sancho, “I believe it all as your worship

[008-068]says it; but straighten yourself a little, for you seem all on one

[008-069]side, may be from the shaking of the fall.”

[008-070]“That is the truth,” said Don Quixote, “and if I make no complaint of

[008-071]the pain it is because knights-errant are not permitted to complain of

[008-072]any wound, even though their bowels be coming out through it.”

[008-073]“If so,” said Sancho, “I have nothing to say; but God knows I would

[008-074]rather your worship complained when anything ailed you. For my part, I

[008-075]confess I must complain however small the ache may be; unless this rule

[008-076]about not complaining extends to the squires of knights-errant also.”

[008-077]Don Quixote could not help laughing at his squire’s simplicity, and he

[008-078]assured him he might complain whenever and however he chose, just as he

[008-079]liked, for, so far, he had never read of anything to the contrary in

[008-080]the order of knighthood.

[008-081]Sancho bade him remember it was dinner-time, to which his master

[008-082]answered that he wanted nothing himself just then, but that _he_ might

[008-083]eat when he had a mind. With this permission Sancho settled himself as

[008-084]comfortably as he could on his beast, and taking out of the alforjas

[008-085]what he had stowed away in them, he jogged along behind his master

[008-086]munching deliberately, and from time to time taking a pull at the bota

[008-087]with a relish that the thirstiest tapster in Malaga might have envied;

[008-088]and while he went on in this way, gulping down draught after draught,

[008-089]he never gave a thought to any of the promises his master had made him,

[008-090]nor did he rate it as hardship but rather as recreation going in quest

[008-091]of adventures, however dangerous they might be. Finally they passed the

[008-092]night among some trees, from one of which Don Quixote plucked a dry

[008-093]branch to serve him after a fashion as a lance, and fixed on it the

[008-094]head he had removed from the broken one. All that night Don Quixote lay

[008-095]awake thinking of his lady Dulcinea, in order to conform to what he had

[008-096]read in his books, how many a night in the forests and deserts knights

[008-097]used to lie sleepless supported by the memory of their mistresses. Not

[008-098]so did Sancho Panza spend it, for having his stomach full of something

[008-099]stronger than chicory water he made but one sleep of it, and, if his

[008-100]master had not called him, neither the rays of the sun beating on his

[008-101]face nor all the cheery notes of the birds welcoming the approach of

[008-102]day would have had power to waken him. On getting up he tried the bota

[008-103]and found it somewhat less full than the night before, which grieved

[008-104]his heart because they did not seem to be on the way to remedy the

[008-105]deficiency readily. Don Quixote did not care to break his fast, for, as

[008-106]has been already said, he confined himself to savoury recollections for

[008-107]nourishment.

[008-108]They returned to the road they had set out with, leading to Puerto

[008-109]Lapice, and at three in the afternoon they came in sight of it. “Here,

[008-110]brother Sancho Panza,” said Don Quixote when he saw it, “we may plunge

[008-111]our hands up to the elbows in what they call adventures; but observe,

[008-112]even shouldst thou see me in the greatest danger in the world, thou

[008-113]must not put a hand to thy sword in my defence, unless indeed thou

[008-114]perceivest that those who assail me are rabble or base folk; for in

[008-115]that case thou mayest very properly aid me; but if they be knights it

[008-116]is on no account permitted or allowed thee by the laws of knighthood to

[008-117]help me until thou hast been dubbed a knight.”

[008-118]“Most certainly, señor,” replied Sancho, “your worship shall be fully

[008-119]obeyed in this matter; all the more as of myself I am peaceful and no

[008-120]friend to mixing in strife and quarrels: it is true that as regards the

[008-121]defence of my own person I shall not give much heed to those laws, for

[008-122]laws human and divine allow each one to defend himself against any

[008-123]assailant whatever.”

[008-124]“That I grant,” said Don Quixote, “but in this matter of aiding me

[008-125]against knights thou must put a restraint upon thy natural

[008-126]impetuosity.”

[008-127]“I will do so, I promise you,” answered Sancho, “and will keep this

[008-128]precept as carefully as Sunday.”

[008-129]While they were thus talking there appeared on the road two friars of

[008-130]the order of St. Benedict, mounted on two dromedaries, for not less

[008-131]tall were the two mules they rode on. They wore travelling spectacles

[008-132]and carried sunshades; and behind them came a coach attended by four or

[008-133]five persons on horseback and two muleteers on foot. In the coach there

[008-134]was, as afterwards appeared, a Biscay lady on her way to Seville, where

[008-135]her husband was about to take passage for the Indies with an

[008-136]appointment of high honour. The friars, though going the same road,

[008-137]were not in her company; but the moment Don Quixote perceived them he

[008-138]said to his squire, “Either I am mistaken, or this is going to be the

[008-139]most famous adventure that has ever been seen, for those black bodies

[008-140]we see there must be, and doubtless are, magicians who are carrying off

[008-141]some stolen princess in that coach, and with all my might I must undo

[008-142]this wrong.”

[008-143]“This will be worse than the windmills,” said Sancho. “Look, señor;

[008-144]those are friars of St. Benedict, and the coach plainly belongs to some

[008-145]travellers: I tell you to mind well what you are about and don’t let

[008-146]the devil mislead you.”

[008-147]“I have told thee already, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote, “that on the

[008-148]subject of adventures thou knowest little. What I say is the truth, as

[008-149]thou shalt see presently.”

[008-150]So saying, he advanced and posted himself in the middle of the road

[008-151]along which the friars were coming, and as soon as he thought they had

[008-152]come near enough to hear what he said, he cried aloud, “Devilish and

[008-153]unnatural beings, release instantly the highborn princesses whom you

[008-154]are carrying off by force in this coach, else prepare to meet a speedy

[008-155]death as the just punishment of your evil deeds.”

[008-156]The friars drew rein and stood wondering at the appearance of Don

[008-157]Quixote as well as at his words, to which they replied, “Señor

[008-158]Caballero, we are not devilish or unnatural, but two brothers of St.

[008-159]Benedict following our road, nor do we know whether or not there are

[008-160]any captive princesses coming in this coach.”

[008-161]“No soft words with me, for I know you, lying rabble,” said Don

[008-162]Quixote, and without waiting for a reply he spurred Rocinante and with

[008-163]levelled lance charged the first friar with such fury and

[008-164]determination, that, if the friar had not flung himself off the mule,

[008-165]he would have brought him to the ground against his will, and sore

[008-166]wounded, if not killed outright. The second brother, seeing how his

[008-167]comrade was treated, drove his heels into his castle of a mule and made

[008-168]off across the country faster than the wind.

[008-169]Sancho Panza, when he saw the friar on the ground, dismounting briskly

[008-170]from his ass, rushed towards him and began to strip off his gown. At

[008-171]that instant the friars’ muleteers came up and asked what he was

[008-172]stripping him for. Sancho answered them that this fell to him lawfully

[008-173]as spoil of the battle which his lord Don Quixote had won. The

[008-174]muleteers, who had no idea of a joke and did not understand all this

[008-175]about battles and spoils, seeing that Don Quixote was some distance off

[008-176]talking to the travellers in the coach, fell upon Sancho, knocked him

[008-177]down, and leaving hardly a hair in his beard, belaboured him with kicks

[008-178]and left him stretched breathless and senseless on the ground; and

[008-179]without any more delay helped the friar to mount, who, trembling,

[008-180]terrified, and pale, as soon as he found himself in the saddle, spurred

[008-181]after his companion, who was standing at a distance looking on,

[008-182]watching the result of the onslaught; then, not caring to wait for the

[008-183]end of the affair just begun, they pursued their journey making more

[008-184]crosses than if they had the devil after them.

[008-185]Don Quixote was, as has been said, speaking to the lady in the coach:

[008-186]“Your beauty, lady mine,” said he, “may now dispose of your person as

[008-187]may be most in accordance with your pleasure, for the pride of your

[008-188]ravishers lies prostrate on the ground through this strong arm of mine;

[008-189]and lest you should be pining to know the name of your deliverer, know

[008-190]that I am called Don Quixote of La Mancha, knight-errant and

[008-191]adventurer, and captive to the peerless and beautiful lady Dulcinea del

[008-192]Toboso: and in return for the service you have received of me I ask no

[008-193]more than that you should return to El Toboso, and on my behalf present

[008-194]yourself before that lady and tell her what I have done to set you

[008-195]free.”

[008-196]One of the squires in attendance upon the coach, a Biscayan, was

[008-197]listening to all Don Quixote was saying, and, perceiving that he would

[008-198]not allow the coach to go on, but was saying it must return at once to

[008-199]El Toboso, he made at him, and seizing his lance addressed him in bad

[008-200]Castilian and worse Biscayan after his fashion, “Begone, caballero, and

[008-201]ill go with thee; by the God that made me, unless thou quittest coach,

[008-202]slayest thee as art here a Biscayan.”

[008-203]Don Quixote understood him quite well, and answered him very quietly,

[008-204]“If thou wert a knight, as thou art none, I should have already

[008-205]chastised thy folly and rashness, miserable creature.” To which the

[008-206]Biscayan returned, “I no gentleman!—I swear to God thou liest as I am

[008-207]Christian: if thou droppest lance and drawest sword, soon shalt thou

[008-208]see thou art carrying water to the cat: Biscayan on land, hidalgo at

[008-209]sea, hidalgo at the devil, and look, if thou sayest otherwise thou

[008-210]liest.”

[008-211]“‘“You will see presently,” said Agrajes,’” replied Don Quixote; and

[008-212]throwing his lance on the ground he drew his sword, braced his buckler

[008-213]on his arm, and attacked the Biscayan, bent upon taking his life.

[008-214]The Biscayan, when he saw him coming on, though he wished to dismount

[008-215]from his mule, in which, being one of those sorry ones let out for

[008-216]hire, he had no confidence, had no choice but to draw his sword; it was

[008-217]lucky for him, however, that he was near the coach, from which he was

[008-218]able to snatch a cushion that served him for a shield; and they went at

[008-219]one another as if they had been two mortal enemies. The others strove

[008-220]to make peace between them, but could not, for the Biscayan declared in

[008-221]his disjointed phrase that if they did not let him finish his battle he

[008-222]would kill his mistress and everyone that strove to prevent him. The

[008-223]lady in the coach, amazed and terrified at what she saw, ordered the

[008-224]coachman to draw aside a little, and set herself to watch this severe

[008-225]struggle, in the course of which the Biscayan smote Don Quixote a

[008-226]mighty stroke on the shoulder over the top of his buckler, which, given

[008-227]to one without armour, would have cleft him to the waist. Don Quixote,

[008-228]feeling the weight of this prodigious blow, cried aloud, saying, “O

[008-229]lady of my soul, Dulcinea, flower of beauty, come to the aid of this

[008-230]your knight, who, in fulfilling his obligations to your beauty, finds

[008-231]himself in this extreme peril.” To say this, to lift his sword, to

[008-232]shelter himself well behind his buckler, and to assail the Biscayan was

[008-233]the work of an instant, determined as he was to venture all upon a

[008-234]single blow. The Biscayan, seeing him come on in this way, was

[008-235]convinced of his courage by his spirited bearing, and resolved to

[008-236]follow his example, so he waited for him keeping well under cover of

[008-237]his cushion, being unable to execute any sort of manoeuvre with his

[008-238]mule, which, dead tired and never meant for this kind of game, could

[008-239]not stir a step.

[008-240]On, then, as aforesaid, came Don Quixote against the wary Biscayan,

[008-241]with uplifted sword and a firm intention of splitting him in half,

[008-242]while on his side the Biscayan waited for him sword in hand, and under

[008-243]the protection of his cushion; and all present stood trembling, waiting

[008-244]in suspense the result of blows such as threatened to fall, and the

[008-245]lady in the coach and the rest of her following were making a thousand

[008-246]vows and offerings to all the images and shrines of Spain, that God

[008-247]might deliver her squire and all of them from this great peril in which

[008-248]they found themselves. But it spoils all, that at this point and crisis

[008-249]the author of the history leaves this battle impending, giving as

[008-250]excuse that he could find nothing more written about these achievements

[008-251]of Don Quixote than what has been already set forth. It is true the

[008-252]second author of this work was unwilling to believe that a history so

[008-253]curious could have been allowed to fall under the sentence of oblivion,

[008-254]or that the wits of La Mancha could have been so undiscerning as not to

[008-255]preserve in their archives or registries some documents referring to

[008-256]this famous knight; and this being his persuasion, he did not despair

[008-257]of finding the conclusion of this pleasant history, which, heaven

[008-258]favouring him, he did find in a way that shall be related in the Second

[008-259]Part.
