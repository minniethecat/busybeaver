WHICH TREATS OF THE SHREWD CONVERSATION WHICH SANCHO PANZA HELD WITH

HIS MASTER DON QUIXOTE

c49a.jpg (181K)


[049-001]“Aha, I have caught you,” said Sancho; “this is what in my heart and

[049-002]soul I was longing to know. Come now, señor, can you deny what is

[049-003]commonly said around us, when a person is out of humour, ‘I don’t know

[049-004]what ails so-and-so, that he neither eats, nor drinks, nor sleeps, nor

[049-005]gives a proper answer to any question; one would think he was

[049-006]enchanted’? From which it is to be gathered that those who do not eat,

[049-007]or drink, or sleep, or do any of the natural acts I am speaking of—that

[049-008]such persons are enchanted; but not those that have the desire your

[049-009]worship has, and drink when drink is given them, and eat when there is

[049-010]anything to eat, and answer every question that is asked them.”

[049-011]“What thou sayest is true, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote; “but I have

[049-012]already told thee there are many sorts of enchantments, and it may be

[049-013]that in the course of time they have been changed one for another, and

[049-014]that now it may be the way with enchanted people to do all that I do,

[049-015]though they did not do so before; so it is vain to argue or draw

[049-016]inferences against the usage of the time. I know and feel that I am

[049-017]enchanted, and that is enough to ease my conscience; for it would weigh

[049-018]heavily on it if I thought that I was not enchanted, and that in a

[049-019]faint-hearted and cowardly way I allowed myself to lie in this cage,

[049-020]defrauding multitudes of the succour I might afford to those in need

[049-021]and distress, who at this very moment may be in sore want of my aid and

[049-022]protection.”

[049-023]“Still for all that,” replied Sancho, “I say that, for your greater and

[049-024]fuller satisfaction, it would be well if your worship were to try to

[049-025]get out of this prison (and I promise to do all in my power to help,

[049-026]and even to take you out of it), and see if you could once more mount

[049-027]your good Rocinante, who seems to be enchanted too, he is so melancholy

[049-028]and dejected; and then we might try our chance in looking for

[049-029]adventures again; and if we have no luck there will be time enough to

[049-030]go back to the cage; in which, on the faith of a good and loyal squire,

[049-031]I promise to shut myself up along with your worship, if so be you are

[049-032]so unfortunate, or I so stupid, as not to be able to carry out my

[049-033]plan.”

[049-034]“I am content to do as thou sayest, brother Sancho,” said Don Quixote,

[049-035]“and when thou seest an opportunity for effecting my release I will

[049-036]obey thee absolutely; but thou wilt see, Sancho, how mistaken thou art

[049-037]in thy conception of my misfortune.”

[049-038]The knight-errant and the ill-errant squire kept up their conversation

[049-039]till they reached the place where the curate, the canon, and the

[049-040]barber, who had already dismounted, were waiting for them. The carter

[049-041]at once unyoked the oxen and left them to roam at large about the

[049-042]pleasant green spot, the freshness of which seemed to invite, not

[049-043]enchanted people like Don Quixote, but wide-awake, sensible folk like

[049-044]his squire, who begged the curate to allow his master to leave the cage

[049-045]for a little; for if they did not let him out, the prison might not be

[049-046]as clean as the propriety of such a gentleman as his master required.

[049-047]The curate understood him, and said he would very gladly comply with

[049-048]his request, only that he feared his master, finding himself at

[049-049]liberty, would take to his old courses and make off where nobody could

[049-050]ever find him again.

[049-051]“I will answer for his not running away,” said Sancho.

[049-052]“And I also,” said the canon, “especially if he gives me his word as a

[049-053]knight not to leave us without our consent.”

[049-054]Don Quixote, who was listening to all this, said, “I give it;—moreover

[049-055]one who is enchanted as I am cannot do as he likes with himself; for he

[049-056]who had enchanted him could prevent his moving from one place for three

[049-057]ages, and if he attempted to escape would bring him back flying.”—And

[049-058]that being so, they might as well release him, particularly as it would

[049-059]be to the advantage of all; for, if they did not let him out, he

[049-060]protested he would be unable to avoid offending their nostrils unless

[049-061]they kept their distance.

[049-062]The canon took his hand, tied together as they both were, and on his

[049-063]word and promise they unbound him, and rejoiced beyond measure he was

[049-064]to find himself out of the cage. The first thing he did was to stretch

[049-065]himself all over, and then he went to where Rocinante was standing and

[049-066]giving him a couple of slaps on the haunches said, “I still trust in

[049-067]God and in his blessed mother, O flower and mirror of steeds, that we

[049-068]shall soon see ourselves, both of us, as we wish to be, thou with thy

[049-069]master on thy back, and I mounted upon thee, following the calling for

[049-070]which God sent me into the world.” And so saying, accompanied by

[049-071]Sancho, he withdrew to a retired spot, from which he came back much

[049-072]relieved and more eager than ever to put his squire’s scheme into

[049-073]execution.

[049-074]The canon gazed at him, wondering at the extraordinary nature of his

[049-075]madness, and that in all his remarks and replies he should show such

[049-076]excellent sense, and only lose his stirrups, as has been already said,

[049-077]when the subject of chivalry was broached. And so, moved by compassion,

[049-078]he said to him, as they all sat on the green grass awaiting the arrival

[049-079]of the provisions:

[049-080]“Is it possible, gentle sir, that the nauseous and idle reading of

[049-081]books of chivalry can have had such an effect on your worship as to

[049-082]upset your reason so that you fancy yourself enchanted, and the like,

[049-083]all as far from the truth as falsehood itself is? How can there be any

[049-084]human understanding that can persuade itself there ever was all that

[049-085]infinity of Amadises in the world, or all that multitude of famous

[049-086]knights, all those emperors of Trebizond, all those Felixmartes of

[049-087]Hircania, all those palfreys, and damsels-errant, and serpents, and

[049-088]monsters, and giants, and marvellous adventures, and enchantments of

[049-089]every kind, and battles, and prodigious encounters, splendid costumes,

[049-090]love-sick princesses, squires made counts, droll dwarfs, love letters,

[049-091]billings and cooings, swashbuckler women, and, in a word, all that

[049-092]nonsense the books of chivalry contain? For myself, I can only say that

[049-093]when I read them, so long as I do not stop to think that they are all

[049-094]lies and frivolity, they give me a certain amount of pleasure; but when

[049-095]I come to consider what they are, I fling the very best of them at the

[049-096]wall, and would fling it into the fire if there were one at hand, as

[049-097]richly deserving such punishment as cheats and impostors out of the

[049-098]range of ordinary toleration, and as founders of new sects and modes of

[049-099]life, and teachers that lead the ignorant public to believe and accept

[049-100]as truth all the folly they contain. And such is their audacity, they

[049-101]even dare to unsettle the wits of gentlemen of birth and intelligence,

[049-102]as is shown plainly by the way they have served your worship, when they

[049-103]have brought you to such a pass that you have to be shut up in a cage

[049-104]and carried on an ox-cart as one would carry a lion or a tiger from

[049-105]place to place to make money by showing it. Come, Señor Don Quixote,

[049-106]have some compassion for yourself, return to the bosom of common sense,

[049-107]and make use of the liberal share of it that heaven has been pleased to

[049-108]bestow upon you, employing your abundant gifts of mind in some other

[049-109]reading that may serve to benefit your conscience and add to your

[049-110]honour. And if, still led away by your natural bent, you desire to read

[049-111]books of achievements and of chivalry, read the Book of Judges in the

[049-112]Holy Scriptures, for there you will find grand reality, and deeds as

[049-113]true as they are heroic. Lusitania had a Viriatus, Rome a Cæsar,

[049-114]Carthage a Hannibal, Greece an Alexander, Castile a Count Fernan

[049-115]Gonzalez, Valencia a Cid, Andalusia a Gonzalo Fernandez, Estremadura a

[049-116]Diego García de Paredes, Jerez a Garci Perez de Vargas, Toledo a

[049-117]Garcilaso, Seville a Don Manuel de Leon, to read of whose valiant deeds

[049-118]will entertain and instruct the loftiest minds and fill them with

[049-119]delight and wonder. Here, Señor Don Quixote, will be reading worthy of

[049-120]your sound understanding; from which you will rise learned in history,

[049-121]in love with virtue, strengthened in goodness, improved in manners,

[049-122]brave without rashness, prudent without cowardice; and all to the

[049-123]honour of God, your own advantage and the glory of La Mancha, whence, I

[049-124]am informed, your worship derives your birth.”

[049-125]Don Quixote listened with the greatest attention to the canon’s words,

[049-126]and when he found he had finished, after regarding him for some time,

[049-127]he replied to him:

[049-128]“It appears to me, gentle sir, that your worship’s discourse is

[049-129]intended to persuade me that there never were any knights-errant in the

[049-130]world, and that all the books of chivalry are false, lying, mischievous

[049-131]and useless to the State, and that I have done wrong in reading them,

[049-132]and worse in believing them, and still worse in imitating them, when I

[049-133]undertook to follow the arduous calling of knight-errantry which they

[049-134]set forth; for you deny that there ever were Amadises of Gaul or of

[049-135]Greece, or any other of the knights of whom the books are full.”

[049-136]“It is all exactly as you state it,” said the canon; to which Don

[049-137]Quixote returned, “You also went on to say that books of this kind had

[049-138]done me much harm, inasmuch as they had upset my senses, and shut me up

[049-139]in a cage, and that it would be better for me to reform and change my

[049-140]studies, and read other truer books which would afford more pleasure

[049-141]and instruction.”

[049-142]“Just so,” said the canon.

[049-143]“Well then,” returned Don Quixote, “to my mind it is you who are the

[049-144]one that is out of his wits and enchanted, as you have ventured to

[049-145]utter such blasphemies against a thing so universally acknowledged and

[049-146]accepted as true that whoever denies it, as you do, deserves the same

[049-147]punishment which you say you inflict on the books that irritate you

[049-148]when you read them. For to try to persuade anybody that Amadis, and all

[049-149]the other knights-adventurers with whom the books are filled, never

[049-150]existed, would be like trying to persuade him that the sun does not

[049-151]yield light, or ice cold, or earth nourishment. What wit in the world

[049-152]can persuade another that the story of the Princess Floripes and Guy of

[049-153]Burgundy is not true, or that of Fierabras and the bridge of Mantible,

[049-154]which happened in the time of Charlemagne? For by all that is good it

[049-155]is as true as that it is daylight now; and if it be a lie, it must be a

[049-156]lie too that there was a Hector, or Achilles, or Trojan war, or Twelve

[049-157]Peers of France, or Arthur of England, who still lives changed into a

[049-158]raven, and is unceasingly looked for in his kingdom. One might just as

[049-159]well try to make out that the history of Guarino Mezquino, or of the

[049-160]quest of the Holy Grail, is false, or that the loves of Tristram and

[049-161]the Queen Yseult are apocryphal, as well as those of Guinevere and

[049-162]Lancelot, when there are persons who can almost remember having seen

[049-163]the Dame Quintañona, who was the best cupbearer in Great Britain. And

[049-164]so true is this, that I recollect a grandmother of mine on the father’s

[049-165]side, whenever she saw any dame in a venerable hood, used to say to me,

[049-166]‘Grandson, that one is like Dame Quintañona,’ from which I conclude

[049-167]that she must have known her, or at least had managed to see some

[049-168]portrait of her. Then who can deny that the story of Pierres and the

[049-169]fair Magalona is true, when even to this day may be seen in the king’s

[049-170]armoury the pin with which the valiant Pierres guided the wooden horse

[049-171]he rode through the air, and it is a trifle bigger than the pole of a

[049-172]cart? And alongside of the pin is Babieca’s saddle, and at Roncesvalles

[049-173]there is Roland’s horn, as large as a large beam; whence we may infer

[049-174]that there were Twelve Peers, and a Pierres, and a Cid, and other

[049-175]knights like them, of the sort people commonly call adventurers. Or

[049-176]perhaps I shall be told, too, that there was no such knight-errant as

[049-177]the valiant Lusitanian Juan de Merlo, who went to Burgundy and in the

[049-178]city of Arras fought with the famous lord of Charny, Mosen Pierres by

[049-179]name, and afterwards in the city of Basle with Mosen Enrique de

[049-180]Remesten, coming out of both encounters covered with fame and honour;

[049-181]or adventures and challenges achieved and delivered, also in Burgundy,

[049-182]by the valiant Spaniards Pedro Barba and Gutierre Quixada (of whose

[049-183]family I come in the direct male line), when they vanquished the sons

[049-184]of the Count of San Polo. I shall be told, too, that Don Fernando de

[049-185]Guevara did not go in quest of adventures to Germany, where he engaged

[049-186]in combat with Micer George, a knight of the house of the Duke of

[049-187]Austria. I shall be told that the jousts of Suero de Quiñones, him of

[049-188]the ‘Paso,’ and the emprise of Mosen Luis de Falces against the

[049-189]Castilian knight, Don Gonzalo de Guzman, were mere mockeries; as well

[049-190]as many other achievements of Christian knights of these and foreign

[049-191]realms, which are so authentic and true, that, I repeat, he who denies

[049-192]them must be totally wanting in reason and good sense.”

[049-193]The canon was amazed to hear the medley of truth and fiction Don

[049-194]Quixote uttered, and to see how well acquainted he was with everything

[049-195]relating or belonging to the achievements of his knight-errantry; so he

[049-196]said in reply:

[049-197]“I cannot deny, Señor Don Quixote, that there is some truth in what you

[049-198]say, especially as regards the Spanish knights-errant; and I am willing

[049-199]to grant too that the Twelve Peers of France existed, but I am not

[049-200]disposed to believe that they did all the things that the Archbishop

[049-201]Turpin relates of them. For the truth of the matter is they were

[049-202]knights chosen by the kings of France, and called ‘Peers’ because they

[049-203]were all equal in worth, rank and prowess (at least if they were not

[049-204]they ought to have been), and it was a kind of religious order like

[049-205]those of Santiago and Calatrava in the present day, in which it is

[049-206]assumed that those who take it are valiant knights of distinction and

[049-207]good birth; and just as we say now a Knight of St. John, or of

[049-208]Alcántara, they used to say then a Knight of the Twelve Peers, because

[049-209]twelve equals were chosen for that military order. That there was a

[049-210]Cid, as well as a Bernardo del Carpio, there can be no doubt; but that

[049-211]they did the deeds people say they did, I hold to be very doubtful. In

[049-212]that other matter of the pin of Count Pierres that you speak of, and

[049-213]say is near Babieca’s saddle in the Armoury, I confess my sin; for I am

[049-214]either so stupid or so short-sighted, that, though I have seen the

[049-215]saddle, I have never been able to see the pin, in spite of it being as

[049-216]big as your worship says it is.”

[049-217]“For all that it is there, without any manner of doubt,” said Don

[049-218]Quixote; “and more by token they say it is inclosed in a sheath of

[049-219]cowhide to keep it from rusting.”

[049-220]“All that may be,” replied the canon; “but, by the orders I have

[049-221]received, I do not remember seeing it. However, granting it is there,

[049-222]that is no reason why I am bound to believe the stories of all those

[049-223]Amadises and of all that multitude of knights they tell us about, nor

[049-224]is it reasonable that a man like your worship, so worthy, and with so

[049-225]many good qualities, and endowed with such a good understanding, should

[049-226]allow himself to be persuaded that such wild crazy things as are

[049-227]written in those absurd books of chivalry are really true.”
