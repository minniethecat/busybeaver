[058-001]OF WHAT TOOK PLACE BETWEEN DON QUIXOTE AND HIS NIECE AND HOUSEKEEPER;

ONE OF THE MOST IMPORTANT CHAPTERS IN THE WHOLE HISTORY

p06a.jpg (93K)


[058-002]While Sancho Panza and his wife, Teresa Cascajo, held the above

[058-003]irrelevant conversation, Don Quixote’s niece and housekeeper were not

[058-004]idle, for by a thousand signs they began to perceive that their uncle

[058-005]and master meant to give them the slip the third time, and once more

[058-006]betake himself to his, for them, ill-errant chivalry. They strove by

[058-007]all the means in their power to divert him from such an unlucky scheme;

[058-008]but it was all preaching in the desert and hammering cold iron.

[058-009]Nevertheless, among many other representations made to him, the

[058-010]housekeeper said to him, “In truth, master, if you do not keep still

[058-011]and stay quiet at home, and give over roaming mountains and valleys

[058-012]like a troubled spirit, looking for what they say are called

[058-013]adventures, but what I call misfortunes, I shall have to make complaint

[058-014]to God and the king with loud supplication to send some remedy.”

[058-015]To which Don Quixote replied, “What answer God will give to your

[058-016]complaints, housekeeper, I know not, nor what his Majesty will answer

[058-017]either; I only know that if I were king I should decline to answer the

[058-018]numberless silly petitions they present every day; for one of the

[058-019]greatest among the many troubles kings have is being obliged to listen

[058-020]to all and answer all, and therefore I should be sorry that any affairs

[058-021]of mine should worry him.”

[058-022]Whereupon the housekeeper said, “Tell us, señor, at his Majesty’s court

[058-023]are there no knights?”

[058-024]“There are,” replied Don Quixote, “and plenty of them; and it is right

[058-025]there should be, to set off the dignity of the prince, and for the

[058-026]greater glory of the king’s majesty.”

[058-027]“Then might not your worship,” said she, “be one of those that, without

[058-028]stirring a step, serve their king and lord in his court?”

[058-029]“Recollect, my friend,” said Don Quixote, “all knights cannot be

[058-030]courtiers, nor can all courtiers be knights-errant, nor need they be.

[058-031]There must be all sorts in the world; and though we may be all knights,

[058-032]there is a great difference between one and another; for the courtiers,

[058-033]without quitting their chambers, or the threshold of the court, range

[058-034]the world over by looking at a map, without its costing them a

[058-035]farthing, and without suffering heat or cold, hunger or thirst; but we,

[058-036]the true knights-errant, measure the whole earth with our own feet,

[058-037]exposed to the sun, to the cold, to the air, to the inclemencies of

[058-038]heaven, by day and night, on foot and on horseback; nor do we only know

[058-039]enemies in pictures, but in their own real shapes; and at all risks and

[058-040]on all occasions we attack them, without any regard to childish points

[058-041]or rules of single combat, whether one has or has not a shorter lance

[058-042]or sword, whether one carries relics or any secret contrivance about

[058-043]him, whether or not the sun is to be divided and portioned out, and

[058-044]other niceties of the sort that are observed in set combats of man to

[058-045]man, that you know nothing about, but I do. And you must know besides,

[058-046]that the true knight-errant, though he may see ten giants, that not

[058-047]only touch the clouds with their heads but pierce them, and that go,

[058-048]each of them, on two tall towers by way of legs, and whose arms are

[058-049]like the masts of mighty ships, and each eye like a great mill-wheel,

[058-050]and glowing brighter than a glass furnace, must not on any account be

[058-051]dismayed by them. On the contrary, he must attack and fall upon them

[058-052]with a gallant bearing and a fearless heart, and, if possible, vanquish

[058-053]and destroy them, even though they have for armour the shells of a

[058-054]certain fish, that they say are harder than diamonds, and in place of

[058-055]swords wield trenchant blades of Damascus steel, or clubs studded with

[058-056]spikes also of steel, such as I have more than once seen. All this I

[058-057]say, housekeeper, that you may see the difference there is between the

[058-058]one sort of knight and the other; and it would be well if there were no

[058-059]prince who did not set a higher value on this second, or more properly

[058-060]speaking first, kind of knights-errant; for, as we read in their

[058-061]histories, there have been some among them who have been the salvation,

[058-062]not merely of one kingdom, but of many.”

[058-063]“Ah, señor,” here exclaimed the niece, “remember that all this you are

[058-064]saying about knights-errant is fable and fiction; and their histories,

[058-065]if indeed they were not burned, would deserve, each of them, to have a

[058-066]sambenito put on it, or some mark by which it might be known as

[058-067]infamous and a corrupter of good manners.”

[058-068]“By the God that gives me life,” said Don Quixote, “if thou wert not my

[058-069]full niece, being daughter of my own sister, I would inflict a

[058-070]chastisement upon thee for the blasphemy thou hast uttered that all the

[058-071]world should ring with. What! can it be that a young hussy that hardly

[058-072]knows how to handle a dozen lace-bobbins dares to wag her tongue and

[058-073]criticise the histories of knights-errant? What would Señor Amadis say

[058-074]if he heard of such a thing? He, however, no doubt would forgive thee,

[058-075]for he was the most humble-minded and courteous knight of his time, and

[058-076]moreover a great protector of damsels; but some there are that might

[058-077]have heard thee, and it would not have been well for thee in that case;

[058-078]for they are not all courteous or mannerly; some are ill-conditioned

[058-079]scoundrels; nor is it everyone that calls himself a gentleman, that is

[058-080]so in all respects; some are gold, others pinchbeck, and all look like

[058-081]gentlemen, but not all can stand the touchstone of truth. There are men

[058-082]of low rank who strain themselves to bursting to pass for gentlemen,

[058-083]and high gentlemen who, one would fancy, were dying to pass for men of

[058-084]low rank; the former raise themselves by their ambition or by their

[058-085]virtues, the latter debase themselves by their lack of spirit or by

[058-086]their vices; and one has need of experience and discernment to

[058-087]distinguish these two kinds of gentlemen, so much alike in name and so

[058-088]different in conduct.”

[058-089]“God bless me!” said the niece, “that you should know so much,

[058-090]uncle—enough, if need be, to get up into a pulpit and go preach in the

[058-091]streets—and yet that you should fall into a delusion so great and a

[058-092]folly so manifest as to try to make yourself out vigorous when you are

[058-093]old, strong when you are sickly, able to put straight what is crooked

[058-094]when you yourself are bent by age, and, above all, a caballero when you

[058-095]are not one; for though gentlefolk may be so, poor men are nothing of

[058-096]the kind!”

[058-097]“There is a great deal of truth in what you say, niece,” returned Don

[058-098]Quixote, “and I could tell you somewhat about birth that would astonish

[058-099]you; but, not to mix up things human and divine, I refrain. Look you,

[058-100]my dears, all the lineages in the world (attend to what I am saying)

[058-101]can be reduced to four sorts, which are these: those that had humble

[058-102]beginnings, and went on spreading and extending themselves until they

[058-103]attained surpassing greatness; those that had great beginnings and

[058-104]maintained them, and still maintain and uphold the greatness of their

[058-105]origin; those, again, that from a great beginning have ended in a point

[058-106]like a pyramid, having reduced and lessened their original greatness

[058-107]till it has come to nought, like the point of a pyramid, which,

[058-108]relatively to its base or foundation, is nothing; and then there are

[058-109]those—and it is they that are the most numerous—that have had neither

[058-110]an illustrious beginning nor a remarkable mid-course, and so will have

[058-111]an end without a name, like an ordinary plebeian line. Of the first,

[058-112]those that had an humble origin and rose to the greatness they still

[058-113]preserve, the Ottoman house may serve as an example, which from an

[058-114]humble and lowly shepherd, its founder, has reached the height at which

[058-115]we now see it. For examples of the second sort of lineage, that began

[058-116]with greatness and maintains it still without adding to it, there are

[058-117]the many princes who have inherited the dignity, and maintain

[058-118]themselves in their inheritance, without increasing or diminishing it,

[058-119]keeping peacefully within the limits of their states. Of those that

[058-120]began great and ended in a point, there are thousands of examples, for

[058-121]all the Pharaohs and Ptolemies of Egypt, the Cæsars of Rome, and the

[058-122]whole herd (if I may apply such a word to them) of countless princes,

[058-123]monarchs, lords, Medes, Assyrians, Persians, Greeks, and barbarians,

[058-124]all these lineages and lordships have ended in a point and come to

[058-125]nothing, they themselves as well as their founders, for it would be

[058-126]impossible now to find one of their descendants, and, even should we

[058-127]find one, it would be in some lowly and humble condition. Of plebeian

[058-128]lineages I have nothing to say, save that they merely serve to swell

[058-129]the number of those that live, without any eminence to entitle them to

[058-130]any fame or praise beyond this. From all I have said I would have you

[058-131]gather, my poor innocents, that great is the confusion among lineages,

[058-132]and that only those are seen to be great and illustrious that show

[058-133]themselves so by the virtue, wealth, and generosity of their

[058-134]possessors. I have said virtue, wealth, and generosity, because a great

[058-135]man who is vicious will be a great example of vice, and a rich man who

[058-136]is not generous will be merely a miserly beggar; for the possessor of

[058-137]wealth is not made happy by possessing it, but by spending it, and not

[058-138]by spending as he pleases, but by knowing how to spend it well. The

[058-139]poor gentleman has no way of showing that he is a gentleman but by

[058-140]virtue, by being affable, well-bred, courteous, gentle-mannered, and

[058-141]kindly, not haughty, arrogant, or censorious, but above all by being

[058-142]charitable; for by two maravedis given with a cheerful heart to the

[058-143]poor, he will show himself as generous as he who distributes alms with

[058-144]bell-ringing, and no one that perceives him to be endowed with the

[058-145]virtues I have named, even though he know him not, will fail to

[058-146]recognise and set him down as one of good blood; and it would be

[058-147]strange were it not so; praise has ever been the reward of virtue, and

[058-148]those who are virtuous cannot fail to receive commendation. There are

[058-149]two roads, my daughters, by which men may reach wealth and honours; one

[058-150]is that of letters, the other that of arms. I have more of arms than of

[058-151]letters in my composition, and, judging by my inclination to arms, was

[058-152]born under the influence of the planet Mars. I am, therefore, in a

[058-153]measure constrained to follow that road, and by it I must travel in

[058-154]spite of all the world, and it will be labour in vain for you to urge

[058-155]me to resist what heaven wills, fate ordains, reason requires, and,

[058-156]above all, my own inclination favours; for knowing as I do the

[058-157]countless toils that are the accompaniments of knight-errantry, I know,

[058-158]too, the infinite blessings that are attained by it; I know that the

[058-159]path of virtue is very narrow, and the road of vice broad and spacious;

[058-160]I know their ends and goals are different, for the broad and easy road

[058-161]of vice ends in death, and the narrow and toilsome one of virtue in

[058-162]life, and not transitory life, but in that which has no end; I know, as

[058-163]our great Castilian poet says, that-

[058-164]It is by rugged paths like these they go

[058-165]That scale the heights of immortality,

[058-166]Unreached by those that falter here below.”

[058-167]“Woe is me!” exclaimed the niece, “my lord is a poet, too! He knows

[058-168]everything, and he can do everything; I will bet, if he chose to turn

[058-169]mason, he could make a house as easily as a cage.”

[058-170]“I can tell you, niece,” replied Don Quixote, “if these chivalrous

[058-171]thoughts did not engage all my faculties, there would be nothing that I

[058-172]could not do, nor any sort of knickknack that would not come from my

[058-173]hands, particularly cages and tooth-picks.”

[058-174]At this moment there came a knocking at the door, and when they asked

[058-175]who was there, Sancho Panza made answer that it was he. The instant the

[058-176]housekeeper knew who it was, she ran to hide herself so as not to see

[058-177]him; in such abhorrence did she hold him. The niece let him in, and his

[058-178]master Don Quixote came forward to receive him with open arms, and the

[058-179]pair shut themselves up in his room, where they had another

[058-180]conversation not inferior to the previous one.
