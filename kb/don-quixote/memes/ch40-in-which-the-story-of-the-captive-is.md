[040-001]IN WHICH THE STORY OF THE CAPTIVE IS CONTINUED.

SONNET

[040-002]“Blest souls, that, from this mortal husk set free,

[040-003]In guerdon of brave deeds beatified,

[040-004]Above this lowly orb of ours abide

[040-005]Made heirs of heaven and immortality,

[040-006]With noble rage and ardour glowing ye

[040-007]Your strength, while strength was yours, in battle plied,

[040-008]And with your own blood and the foeman’s dyed

[040-009]The sandy soil and the encircling sea.

[040-010]It was the ebbing life-blood first that failed

[040-011]The weary arms; the stout hearts never quailed.

[040-012]Though vanquished, yet ye earned the victor’s crown:

[040-013]Though mourned, yet still triumphant was your fall

[040-014]For there ye won, between the sword and wall,

[040-015]In Heaven glory and on earth renown.”

[040-016]“That is it exactly, according to my recollection,” said the captive.

[040-017]“Well then, that on the fort,” said the gentleman, “if my memory serves

[040-018]me, goes thus:

SONNET

[040-019]“Up from this wasted soil, this shattered shell,

[040-020]Whose walls and towers here in ruin lie,

[040-021]Three thousand soldier souls took wing on high,

[040-022]In the bright mansions of the blest to dwell.

[040-023]The onslaught of the foeman to repel

[040-024]By might of arm all vainly did they try,

[040-025]And when at length ’twas left them but to die,

[040-026]Wearied and few the last defenders fell.

[040-027]And this same arid soil hath ever been

[040-028]A haunt of countless mournful memories,

[040-029]As well in our day as in days of yore.

[040-030]But never yet to Heaven it sent, I ween,

[040-031]From its hard bosom purer souls than these,

[040-032]Or braver bodies on its surface bore.”

[040-033]The sonnets were not disliked, and the captive was rejoiced at the

[040-034]tidings they gave him of his comrade, and continuing his tale, he went

[040-035]on to say:

[040-036]The Goletta and the fort being thus in their hands, the Turks gave

[040-037]orders to dismantle the Goletta—for the fort was reduced to such a

[040-038]state that there was nothing left to level—and to do the work more

[040-039]quickly and easily they mined it in three places; but nowhere were they

[040-040]able to blow up the part which seemed to be the least strong, that is

[040-041]to say, the old walls, while all that remained standing of the new

[040-042]fortifications that the Fratin had made came to the ground with the

[040-043]greatest ease. Finally the fleet returned victorious and triumphant to

[040-044]Constantinople, and a few months later died my master, El Uchali,

[040-045]otherwise Uchali Fartax, which means in Turkish “the scabby renegade;”

[040-046]for that he was; it is the practice with the Turks to name people from

[040-047]some defect or virtue they may possess; the reason being that there are

[040-048]among them only four surnames belonging to families tracing their

[040-049]descent from the Ottoman house, and the others, as I have said, take

[040-050]their names and surnames either from bodily blemishes or moral

[040-051]qualities. This “scabby one” rowed at the oar as a slave of the Grand

[040-052]Signor’s for fourteen years, and when over thirty-four years of age, in

[040-053]resentment at having been struck by a Turk while at the oar, turned

[040-054]renegade and renounced his faith in order to be able to revenge

[040-055]himself; and such was his valour that, without owing his advancement to

[040-056]the base ways and means by which most favourites of the Grand Signor

[040-057]rise to power, he came to be king of Algiers, and afterwards

[040-058]general-on-sea, which is the third place of trust in the realm. He was

[040-059]a Calabrian by birth, and a worthy man morally, and he treated his

[040-060]slaves with great humanity. He had three thousand of them, and after

[040-061]his death they were divided, as he directed by his will, between the

[040-062]Grand Signor (who is heir of all who die and shares with the children

[040-063]of the deceased) and his renegades. I fell to the lot of a Venetian

[040-064]renegade who, when a cabin boy on board a ship, had been taken by

[040-065]Uchali and was so much beloved by him that he became one of his most

[040-066]favoured youths. He came to be the most cruel renegade I ever saw: his

[040-067]name was Hassan Aga, and he grew very rich and became king of Algiers.

[040-068]With him I went there from Constantinople, rather glad to be so near

[040-069]Spain, not that I intended to write to anyone about my unhappy lot, but

[040-070]to try if fortune would be kinder to me in Algiers than in

[040-071]Constantinople, where I had attempted in a thousand ways to escape

[040-072]without ever finding a favourable time or chance; but in Algiers I

[040-073]resolved to seek for other means of effecting the purpose I cherished

[040-074]so dearly; for the hope of obtaining my liberty never deserted me; and

[040-075]when in my plots and schemes and attempts the result did not answer my

[040-076]expectations, without giving way to despair I immediately began to look

[040-077]out for or conjure up some new hope to support me, however faint or

[040-078]feeble it might be.

[040-079]In this way I lived on immured in a building or prison called by the

[040-080]Turks a baño in which they confine the Christian captives, as well

[040-081]those that are the king’s as those belonging to private individuals,

[040-082]and also what they call those of the Almacen, which is as much as to

[040-083]say the slaves of the municipality, who serve the city in the public

[040-084]works and other employments; but captives of this kind recover their

[040-085]liberty with great difficulty, for, as they are public property and

[040-086]have no particular master, there is no one with whom to treat for their

[040-087]ransom, even though they may have the means. To these baños, as I have

[040-088]said, some private individuals of the town are in the habit of bringing

[040-089]their captives, especially when they are to be ransomed; because there

[040-090]they can keep them in safety and comfort until their ransom arrives.

[040-091]The king’s captives also, that are on ransom, do not go out to work

[040-092]with the rest of the crew, unless when their ransom is delayed; for

[040-093]then, to make them write for it more pressingly, they compel them to

[040-094]work and go for wood, which is no light labour.

[040-095]I, however, was one of those on ransom, for when it was discovered that

[040-096]I was a captain, although I declared my scanty means and want of

[040-097]fortune, nothing could dissuade them from including me among the

[040-098]gentlemen and those waiting to be ransomed. They put a chain on me,

[040-099]more as a mark of this than to keep me safe, and so I passed my life in

[040-100]that baño with several other gentlemen and persons of quality marked

[040-101]out as held to ransom; but though at times, or rather almost always, we

[040-102]suffered from hunger and scanty clothing, nothing distressed us so much

[040-103]as hearing and seeing at every turn the unexampled and unheard-of

[040-104]cruelties my master inflicted upon the Christians. Every day he hanged

[040-105]a man, impaled one, cut off the ears of another; and all with so little

[040-106]provocation, or so entirely without any, that the Turks acknowledged he

[040-107]did it merely for the sake of doing it, and because he was by nature

[040-108]murderously disposed towards the whole human race. The only one that

[040-109]fared at all well with him was a Spanish soldier, something de Saavedra

[040-110]by name, to whom he never gave a blow himself, or ordered a blow to be

[040-111]given, or addressed a hard word, although he had done things that will

[040-112]dwell in the memory of the people there for many a year, and all to

[040-113]recover his liberty; and for the least of the many things he did we all

[040-114]dreaded that he would be impaled, and he himself was in fear of it more

[040-115]than once; and only that time does not allow, I could tell you now

[040-116]something of what that soldier did, that would interest and astonish

[040-117]you much more than the narration of my own tale.

[040-118]To go on with my story; the courtyard of our prison was overlooked by

[040-119]the windows of the house belonging to a wealthy Moor of high position;

[040-120]and these, as is usual in Moorish houses, were rather loopholes than

[040-121]windows, and besides were covered with thick and close lattice-work. It

[040-122]so happened, then, that as I was one day on the terrace of our prison

[040-123]with three other comrades, trying, to pass away the time, how far we

[040-124]could leap with our chains, we being alone, for all the other

[040-125]Christians had gone out to work, I chanced to raise my eyes, and from

[040-126]one of these little closed windows I saw a reed appear with a cloth

[040-127]attached to the end of it, and it kept waving to and fro, and moving as

[040-128]if making signs to us to come and take it. We watched it, and one of

[040-129]those who were with me went and stood under the reed to see whether

[040-130]they would let it drop, or what they would do, but as he did so the

[040-131]reed was raised and moved from side to side, as if they meant to say

[040-132]“no” by a shake of the head. The Christian came back, and it was again

[040-133]lowered, making the same movements as before. Another of my comrades

[040-134]went, and with him the same happened as with the first, and then the

[040-135]third went forward, but with the same result as the first and second.

[040-136]Seeing this I did not like not to try my luck, and as soon as I came

[040-137]under the reed it was dropped and fell inside the baño at my feet. I

[040-138]hastened to untie the cloth, in which I perceived a knot, and in this

[040-139]were ten cianis, which are coins of base gold, current among the Moors,

[040-140]and each worth ten reals of our money.

[040-141]It is needless to say I rejoiced over this godsend, and my joy was not

[040-142]less than my wonder as I strove to imagine how this good fortune could

[040-143]have come to us, but to me specially; for the evident unwillingness to

[040-144]drop the reed for any but me showed that it was for me the favour was

[040-145]intended. I took my welcome money, broke the reed, and returned to the

[040-146]terrace, and looking up at the window, I saw a very white hand put out

[040-147]that opened and shut very quickly. From this we gathered or fancied

[040-148]that it must be some woman living in that house that had done us this

[040-149]kindness, and to show that we were grateful for it, we made salaams

[040-150]after the fashion of the Moors, bowing the head, bending the body, and

[040-151]crossing the arms on the breast. Shortly afterwards at the same window

[040-152]a small cross made of reeds was put out and immediately withdrawn. This

[040-153]sign led us to believe that some Christian woman was a captive in the

[040-154]house, and that it was she who had been so good to us; but the

[040-155]whiteness of the hand and the bracelets we had perceived made us

[040-156]dismiss that idea, though we thought it might be one of the Christian

[040-157]renegades whom their masters very often take as lawful wives, and

[040-158]gladly, for they prefer them to the women of their own nation. In all

[040-159]our conjectures we were wide of the truth; so from that time forward

[040-160]our sole occupation was watching and gazing at the window where the

[040-161]cross had appeared to us, as if it were our pole-star; but at least

[040-162]fifteen days passed without our seeing either it or the hand, or any

[040-163]other sign and though meanwhile we endeavoured with the utmost pains to

[040-164]ascertain who it was that lived in the house, and whether there were

[040-165]any Christian renegade in it, nobody could ever tell us anything more

[040-166]than that he who lived there was a rich Moor of high position, Hadji

[040-167]Morato by name, formerly alcaide of La Pata, an office of high dignity

[040-168]among them. But when we least thought it was going to rain any more

[040-169]cianis from that quarter, we saw the reed suddenly appear with another

[040-170]cloth tied in a larger knot attached to it, and this at a time when, as

[040-171]on the former occasion, the baño was deserted and unoccupied.

[040-172]We made trial as before, each of the same three going forward before I

[040-173]did; but the reed was delivered to none but me, and on my approach it

[040-174]was let drop. I untied the knot and I found forty Spanish gold crowns

[040-175]with a paper written in Arabic, and at the end of the writing there was

[040-176]a large cross drawn. I kissed the cross, took the crowns and returned

[040-177]to the terrace, and we all made our salaams; again the hand appeared, I

[040-178]made signs that I would read the paper, and then the window was closed.

[040-179]We were all puzzled, though filled with joy at what had taken place;

[040-180]and as none of us understood Arabic, great was our curiosity to know

[040-181]what the paper contained, and still greater the difficulty of finding

[040-182]someone to read it. At last I resolved to confide in a renegade, a

[040-183]native of Murcia, who professed a very great friendship for me, and had

[040-184]given pledges that bound him to keep any secret I might entrust to him;

[040-185]for it is the custom with some renegades, when they intend to return to

[040-186]Christian territory, to carry about them certificates from captives of

[040-187]mark testifying, in whatever form they can, that such and such a

[040-188]renegade is a worthy man who has always shown kindness to Christians,

[040-189]and is anxious to escape on the first opportunity that may present

[040-190]itself. Some obtain these testimonials with good intentions, others put

[040-191]them to a cunning use; for when they go to pillage on Christian

[040-192]territory, if they chance to be cast away, or taken prisoners, they

[040-193]produce their certificates and say that from these papers may be seen

[040-194]the object they came for, which was to remain on Christian ground, and

[040-195]that it was to this end they joined the Turks in their foray. In this

[040-196]way they escape the consequences of the first outburst and make their

[040-197]peace with the Church before it does them any harm, and then when they

[040-198]have the chance they return to Barbary to become what they were before.

[040-199]Others, however, there are who procure these papers and make use of

[040-200]them honestly, and remain on Christian soil. This friend of mine, then,

[040-201]was one of these renegades that I have described; he had certificates

[040-202]from all our comrades, in which we testified in his favour as strongly

[040-203]as we could; and if the Moors had found the papers they would have

[040-204]burned him alive.

[040-205]I knew that he understood Arabic very well, and could not only speak

[040-206]but also write it; but before I disclosed the whole matter to him, I

[040-207]asked him to read for me this paper which I had found by accident in a

[040-208]hole in my cell. He opened it and remained some time examining it and

[040-209]muttering to himself as he translated it. I asked him if he understood

[040-210]it, and he told me he did perfectly well, and that if I wished him to

[040-211]tell me its meaning word for word, I must give him pen and ink that he

[040-212]might do it more satisfactorily. We at once gave him what he required,

[040-213]and he set about translating it bit by bit, and when he had done he

[040-214]said:

[040-215]“All that is here in Spanish is what the Moorish paper contains, and

[040-216]you must bear in mind that when it says ‘Lela Marien’ it means ‘Our

[040-217]Lady the Virgin Mary.’”

[040-218]We read the paper and it ran thus:

[040-219]“When I was a child my father had a slave who taught me to pray the

[040-220]Christian prayer in my own language, and told me many things about Lela

[040-221]Marien. The Christian died, and I know that she did not go to the fire,

[040-222]but to Allah, because since then I have seen her twice, and she told me

[040-223]to go to the land of the Christians to see Lela Marien, who had great

[040-224]love for me. I know not how to go. I have seen many Christians, but

[040-225]except thyself none has seemed to me to be a gentleman. I am young and

[040-226]beautiful, and have plenty of money to take with me. See if thou canst

[040-227]contrive how we may go, and if thou wilt thou shalt be my husband

[040-228]there, and if thou wilt not it will not distress me, for Lela Marien

[040-229]will find me someone to marry me. I myself have written this: have a

[040-230]care to whom thou givest it to read: trust no Moor, for they are all

[040-231]perfidious. I am greatly troubled on this account, for I would not have

[040-232]thee confide in anyone, because if my father knew it he would at once

[040-233]fling me down a well and cover me with stones. I will put a thread to

[040-234]the reed; tie the answer to it, and if thou hast no one to write for

[040-235]thee in Arabic, tell it to me by signs, for Lela Marien will make me

[040-236]understand thee. She and Allah and this cross, which I often kiss as

[040-237]the captive bade me, protect thee.”

[040-238]Judge, sirs, whether we had reason for surprise and joy at the words of

[040-239]this paper; and both one and the other were so great, that the renegade

[040-240]perceived that the paper had not been found by chance, but had been in

[040-241]reality addressed to someone of us, and he begged us, if what he

[040-242]suspected were the truth, to trust him and tell him all, for he would

[040-243]risk his life for our freedom; and so saying he took out from his

[040-244]breast a metal crucifix, and with many tears swore by the God the image

[040-245]represented, in whom, sinful and wicked as he was, he truly and

[040-246]faithfully believed, to be loyal to us and keep secret whatever we

[040-247]chose to reveal to him; for he thought and almost foresaw that by means

[040-248]of her who had written that paper, he and all of us would obtain our

[040-249]liberty, and he himself obtain the object he so much desired, his

[040-250]restoration to the bosom of the Holy Mother Church, from which by his

[040-251]own sin and ignorance he was now severed like a corrupt limb. The

[040-252]renegade said this with so many tears and such signs of repentance,

[040-253]that with one consent we all agreed to tell him the whole truth of the

[040-254]matter, and so we gave him a full account of all, without hiding

[040-255]anything from him. We pointed out to him the window at which the reed

[040-256]appeared, and he by that means took note of the house, and resolved to

[040-257]ascertain with particular care who lived in it. We agreed also that it

[040-258]would be advisable to answer the Moorish lady’s letter, and the

[040-259]renegade without a moment’s delay took down the words I dictated to

[040-260]him, which were exactly what I shall tell you, for nothing of

[040-261]importance that took place in this affair has escaped my memory, or

[040-262]ever will while life lasts. This, then, was the answer returned to the

[040-263]Moorish lady:

[040-264]“The true Allah protect thee, Lady, and that blessed Marien who is the

[040-265]true mother of God, and who has put it into thy heart to go to the land

[040-266]of the Christians, because she loves thee. Entreat her that she be

[040-267]pleased to show thee how thou canst execute the command she gives thee,

[040-268]for she will, such is her goodness. On my own part, and on that of all

[040-269]these Christians who are with me, I promise to do all that we can for

[040-270]thee, even to death. Fail not to write to me and inform me what thou

[040-271]dost mean to do, and I will always answer thee; for the great Allah has

[040-272]given us a Christian captive who can speak and write thy language well,

[040-273]as thou mayest see by this paper; without fear, therefore, thou canst

[040-274]inform us of all thou wouldst. As to what thou sayest, that if thou

[040-275]dost reach the land of the Christians thou wilt be my wife, I give thee

[040-276]my promise upon it as a good Christian; and know that the Christians

[040-277]keep their promises better than the Moors. Allah and Marien his mother

[040-278]watch over thee, my Lady.”

[040-279]The paper being written and folded I waited two days until the baño was

[040-280]empty as before, and immediately repaired to the usual walk on the

[040-281]terrace to see if there were any sign of the reed, which was not long

[040-282]in making its appearance. As soon as I saw it, although I could not

[040-283]distinguish who put it out, I showed the paper as a sign to attach the

[040-284]thread, but it was already fixed to the reed, and to it I tied the

[040-285]paper; and shortly afterwards our star once more made its appearance

[040-286]with the white flag of peace, the little bundle. It was dropped, and I

[040-287]picked it up, and found in the cloth, in gold and silver coins of all

[040-288]sorts, more than fifty crowns, which fifty times more strengthened our

[040-289]joy and doubled our hope of gaining our liberty. That very night our

[040-290]renegade returned and said he had learned that the Moor we had been

[040-291]told of lived in that house, that his name was Hadji Morato, that he

[040-292]was enormously rich, that he had one only daughter the heiress of all

[040-293]his wealth, and that it was the general opinion throughout the city

[040-294]that she was the most beautiful woman in Barbary, and that several of

[040-295]the viceroys who came there had sought her for a wife, but that she had

[040-296]been always unwilling to marry; and he had learned, moreover, that she

[040-297]had a Christian slave who was now dead; all which agreed with the

[040-298]contents of the paper. We immediately took counsel with the renegade as

[040-299]to what means would have to be adopted in order to carry off the

[040-300]Moorish lady and bring us all to Christian territory; and in the end it

[040-301]was agreed that for the present we should wait for a second

[040-302]communication from Zoraida (for that was the name of her who now

[040-303]desires to be called Maria), because we saw clearly that she and no one

[040-304]else could find a way out of all these difficulties. When we had

[040-305]decided upon this the renegade told us not to be uneasy, for he would

[040-306]lose his life or restore us to liberty. For four days the baño was

[040-307]filled with people, for which reason the reed delayed its appearance

[040-308]for four days, but at the end of that time, when the baño was, as it

[040-309]generally was, empty, it appeared with the cloth so bulky that it

[040-310]promised a happy birth. Reed and cloth came down to me, and I found

[040-311]another paper and a hundred crowns in gold, without any other coin. The

[040-312]renegade was present, and in our cell we gave him the paper to read,

[040-313]which was to this effect:

[040-314]“I cannot think of a plan, señor, for our going to Spain, nor has Lela

[040-315]Marien shown me one, though I have asked her. All that can be done is

[040-316]for me to give you plenty of money in gold from this window. With it

[040-317]ransom yourself and your friends, and let one of you go to the land of

[040-318]the Christians, and there buy a vessel and come back for the others;

[040-319]and he will find me in my father’s garden, which is at the Babazon gate

[040-320]near the seashore, where I shall be all this summer with my father and

[040-321]my servants. You can carry me away from there by night without any

[040-322]danger, and bring me to the vessel. And remember thou art to be my

[040-323]husband, else I will pray to Marien to punish thee. If thou canst not

[040-324]trust anyone to go for the vessel, ransom thyself and do thou go, for I

[040-325]know thou wilt return more surely than any other, as thou art a

[040-326]gentleman and a Christian. Endeavour to make thyself acquainted with

[040-327]the garden; and when I see thee walking yonder I shall know that the

[040-328]baño is empty and I will give thee abundance of money. Allah protect

[040-329]thee, señor.”

[040-330]These were the words and contents of the second paper, and on hearing

[040-331]them, each declared himself willing to be the ransomed one, and

[040-332]promised to go and return with scrupulous good faith; and I too made

[040-333]the same offer; but to all this the renegade objected, saying that he

[040-334]would not on any account consent to one being set free before all went

[040-335]together, as experience had taught him how ill those who have been set

[040-336]free keep promises which they made in captivity; for captives of

[040-337]distinction frequently had recourse to this plan, paying the ransom of

[040-338]one who was to go to Valencia or Majorca with money to enable him to

[040-339]arm a bark and return for the others who had ransomed him, but who

[040-340]never came back; for recovered liberty and the dread of losing it again

[040-341]efface from the memory all the obligations in the world. And to prove

[040-342]the truth of what he said, he told us briefly what had happened to a

[040-343]certain Christian gentleman almost at that very time, the strangest

[040-344]case that had ever occurred even there, where astonishing and

[040-345]marvellous things are happening every instant. In short, he ended by

[040-346]saying that what could and ought to be done was to give the money

[040-347]intended for the ransom of one of us Christians to him, so that he

[040-348]might with it buy a vessel there in Algiers under the pretence of

[040-349]becoming a merchant and trader at Tetuan and along the coast; and when

[040-350]master of the vessel, it would be easy for him to hit on some way of

[040-351]getting us all out of the baño and putting us on board; especially if

[040-352]the Moorish lady gave, as she said, money enough to ransom all, because

[040-353]once free it would be the easiest thing in the world for us to embark

[040-354]even in open day; but the greatest difficulty was that the Moors do not

[040-355]allow any renegade to buy or own any craft, unless it be a large vessel

[040-356]for going on roving expeditions, because they are afraid that anyone

[040-357]who buys a small vessel, especially if he be a Spaniard, only wants it

[040-358]for the purpose of escaping to Christian territory. This however he

[040-359]could get over by arranging with a Tagarin Moor to go shares with him

[040-360]in the purchase of the vessel, and in the profit on the cargo; and
