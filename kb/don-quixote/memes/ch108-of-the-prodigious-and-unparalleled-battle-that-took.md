OF THE PRODIGIOUS AND UNPARALLELED BATTLE THAT TOOK PLACE BETWEEN DON

QUIXOTE OF LA MANCHA AND THE LACQUEY TOSILOS IN DEFENCE OF THE DAUGHTER

OF DOÑA RODRIGUEZ

p56a.jpg (158K)


[108-001]The duke and duchess had no reason to regret the joke that had been

[108-002]played upon Sancho Panza in giving him the government; especially as

[108-003]their majordomo returned the same day, and gave them a minute account

[108-004]of almost every word and deed that Sancho uttered or did during the

[108-005]time; and to wind up with, eloquently described to them the attack upon

[108-006]the island and Sancho’s fright and departure, with which they were not

[108-007]a little amused. After this the history goes on to say that the day

[108-008]fixed for the battle arrived, and that the duke, after having

[108-009]repeatedly instructed his lacquey Tosilos how to deal with Don Quixote

[108-010]so as to vanquish him without killing or wounding him, gave orders to

[108-011]have the heads removed from the lances, telling Don Quixote that

[108-012]Christian charity, on which he plumed himself, could not suffer the

[108-013]battle to be fought with so much risk and danger to life; and that he

[108-014]must be content with the offer of a battlefield on his territory

[108-015](though that was against the decree of the holy Council, which

[108-016]prohibits all challenges of the sort) and not push such an arduous

[108-017]venture to its extreme limits. Don Quixote bade his excellence arrange

[108-018]all matters connected with the affair as he pleased, as on his part he

[108-019]would obey him in everything. The dread day, then, having arrived, and

[108-020]the duke having ordered a spacious stand to be erected facing the court

[108-021]of the castle for the judges of the field and the appellant duennas,

[108-022]mother and daughter, vast crowds flocked from all the villages and

[108-023]hamlets of the neighbourhood to see the novel spectacle of the battle;

[108-024]nobody, dead or alive, in those parts having ever seen or heard of such

[108-025]a one.

[108-026]The first person to enter the field and the lists was the master of the

[108-027]ceremonies, who surveyed and paced the whole ground to see that there

[108-028]was nothing unfair and nothing concealed to make the combatants stumble

[108-029]or fall; then the duennas entered and seated themselves, enveloped in

[108-030]mantles covering their eyes, nay even their bosoms, and displaying no

[108-031]slight emotion as Don Quixote appeared in the lists. Shortly

[108-032]afterwards, accompanied by several trumpets and mounted on a powerful

[108-033]steed that threatened to crush the whole place, the great lacquey

[108-034]Tosilos made his appearance on one side of the courtyard with his visor

[108-035]down and stiffly cased in a suit of stout shining armour. The horse was

[108-036]a manifest Frieslander, broad-backed and flea-bitten, and with half a

[108-037]hundred of wool hanging to each of his fetlocks. The gallant combatant

[108-038]came well primed by his master the duke as to how he was to bear

[108-039]himself against the valiant Don Quixote of La Mancha; being warned that

[108-040]he must on no account slay him, but strive to shirk the first encounter

[108-041]so as to avoid the risk of killing him, as he was sure to do if he met

[108-042]him full tilt. He crossed the courtyard at a walk, and coming to where

[108-043]the duennas were placed stopped to look at her who demanded him for a

[108-044]husband; the marshal of the field summoned Don Quixote, who had already

[108-045]presented himself in the courtyard, and standing by the side of Tosilos

[108-046]he addressed the duennas, and asked them if they consented that Don

[108-047]Quixote of La Mancha should do battle for their right. They said they

[108-048]did, and that whatever he should do in that behalf they declared

[108-049]rightly done, final and valid. By this time the duke and duchess had

[108-050]taken their places in a gallery commanding the enclosure, which was

[108-051]filled to overflowing with a multitude of people eager to see this

[108-052]perilous and unparalleled encounter. The conditions of the combat were

[108-053]that if Don Quixote proved the victor his antagonist was to marry the

[108-054]daughter of Doña Rodriguez; but if he should be vanquished his opponent

[108-055]was released from the promise that was claimed against him and from all

[108-056]obligations to give satisfaction. The master of the ceremonies

[108-057]apportioned the sun to them, and stationed them, each on the spot where

[108-058]he was to stand. The drums beat, the sound of the trumpets filled the

[108-059]air, the earth trembled under foot, the hearts of the gazing crowd were

[108-060]full of anxiety, some hoping for a happy issue, some apprehensive of an

[108-061]untoward ending to the affair, and lastly, Don Quixote, commending

[108-062]himself with all his heart to God our Lord and to the lady Dulcinea del

[108-063]Toboso, stood waiting for them to give the necessary signal for the

[108-064]onset. Our lacquey, however, was thinking of something very different;

[108-065]he only thought of what I am now going to mention.

[108-066]It seems that as he stood contemplating his enemy she struck him as the

[108-067]most beautiful woman he had ever seen all his life; and the little

[108-068]blind boy whom in our streets they commonly call Love had no mind to

[108-069]let slip the chance of triumphing over a lacquey heart, and adding it

[108-070]to the list of his trophies; and so, stealing gently upon him unseen,

[108-071]he drove a dart two yards long into the poor lacquey’s left side and

[108-072]pierced his heart through and through; which he was able to do quite at

[108-073]his ease, for Love is invisible, and comes in and goes out as he likes,

[108-074]without anyone calling him to account for what he does. Well then, when

[108-075]they gave the signal for the onset our lacquey was in an ecstasy,

[108-076]musing upon the beauty of her whom he had already made mistress of his

[108-077]liberty, and so he paid no attention to the sound of the trumpet,

[108-078]unlike Don Quixote, who was off the instant he heard it, and, at the

[108-079]highest speed Rocinante was capable of, set out to meet his enemy, his

[108-080]good squire Sancho shouting lustily as he saw him start, “God guide

[108-081]thee, cream and flower of knights-errant! God give thee the victory,

[108-082]for thou hast the right on thy side!” But though Tosilos saw Don

[108-083]Quixote coming at him he never stirred a step from the spot where he

[108-084]was posted; and instead of doing so called loudly to the marshal of the

[108-085]field, to whom when he came up to see what he wanted he said, “Señor,

[108-086]is not this battle to decide whether I marry or do not marry that

[108-087]lady?” “Just so,” was the answer. “Well then,” said the lacquey, “I

[108-088]feel qualms of conscience, and I should lay a heavy burden upon it if I

[108-089]were to proceed any further with the combat; I therefore declare that I

[108-090]yield myself vanquished, and that I am willing to marry the lady at

[108-091]once.”

[108-092]The marshal of the field was lost in astonishment at the words of

[108-093]Tosilos; and as he was one of those who were privy to the arrangement

[108-094]of the affair he knew not what to say in reply. Don Quixote pulled up

[108-095]in mid career when he saw that his enemy was not coming on to the

[108-096]attack. The duke could not make out the reason why the battle did not

[108-097]go on; but the marshal of the field hastened to him to let him know

[108-098]what Tosilos said, and he was amazed and extremely angry at it. In the

[108-099]meantime Tosilos advanced to where Doña Rodriguez sat and said in a

[108-100]loud voice, “Señora, I am willing to marry your daughter, and I have no

[108-101]wish to obtain by strife and fighting what I can obtain in peace and

[108-102]without any risk to my life.”

[108-103]The valiant Don Quixote heard him, and said, “As that is the case I am

[108-104]released and absolved from my promise; let them marry by all means, and

[108-105]as ‘God our Lord has given her, may Saint Peter add his blessing.’”

[108-106]The duke had now descended to the courtyard of the castle, and going up

[108-107]to Tosilos he said to him, “Is it true, sir knight, that you yield

[108-108]yourself vanquished, and that moved by scruples of conscience you wish

[108-109]to marry this damsel?”

[108-110]“It is, señor,” replied Tosilos.

[108-111]“And he does well,” said Sancho, “for what thou hast to give to the

[108-112]mouse, give to the cat, and it will save thee all trouble.”

[108-113]Tosilos meanwhile was trying to unlace his helmet, and he begged them

[108-114]to come to his help at once, as his power of breathing was failing him,

[108-115]and he could not remain so long shut up in that confined space. They

[108-116]removed it in all haste, and his lacquey features were revealed to

[108-117]public gaze. At this sight Doña Rodriguez and her daughter raised a

[108-118]mighty outcry, exclaiming, “This is a trick! This is a trick! They have

[108-119]put Tosilos, my lord the duke’s lacquey, upon us in place of the real

[108-120]husband. The justice of God and the king against such trickery, not to

[108-121]say roguery!”

[108-122]“Do not distress yourselves, ladies,” said Don Quixote; “for this is no

[108-123]trickery or roguery; or if it is, it is not the duke who is at the

[108-124]bottom of it, but those wicked enchanters who persecute me, and who,

[108-125]jealous of my reaping the glory of this victory, have turned your

[108-126]husband’s features into those of this person, who you say is a lacquey

[108-127]of the duke’s; take my advice, and notwithstanding the malice of my

[108-128]enemies marry him, for beyond a doubt he is the one you wish for a

[108-129]husband.”

[108-130]When the duke heard this all his anger was near vanishing in a fit of

[108-131]laughter, and he said, “The things that happen to Señor Don Quixote are

[108-132]so extraordinary that I am ready to believe this lacquey of mine is not

[108-133]one; but let us adopt this plan and device; let us put off the marriage

[108-134]for, say, a fortnight, and let us keep this person about whom we are

[108-135]uncertain in close confinement, and perhaps in the course of that time

[108-136]he may return to his original shape; for the spite which the enchanters

[108-137]entertain against Señor Don Quixote cannot last so long, especially as

[108-138]it is of so little advantage to them to practise these deceptions and

[108-139]transformations.”

[108-140]“Oh, señor,” said Sancho, “those scoundrels are well used to changing

[108-141]whatever concerns my master from one thing into another. A knight that

[108-142]he overcame some time back, called the Knight of the Mirrors, they

[108-143]turned into the shape of the bachelor Samson Carrasco of our town and a

[108-144]great friend of ours; and my lady Dulcinea del Toboso they have turned

[108-145]into a common country wench; so I suspect this lacquey will have to

[108-146]live and die a lacquey all the days of his life.”

[108-147]Here the Rodriguez’s daughter exclaimed, “Let him be who he may, this

[108-148]man that claims me for a wife; I am thankful to him for the same, for I

[108-149]had rather be the lawful wife of a lacquey than the cheated mistress of

[108-150]a gentleman; though he who played me false is nothing of the kind.”

[108-151]To be brief, all the talk and all that had happened ended in Tosilos

[108-152]being shut up until it was seen how his transformation turned out. All

[108-153]hailed Don Quixote as victor, but the greater number were vexed and

[108-154]disappointed at finding that the combatants they had been so anxiously

[108-155]waiting for had not battered one another to pieces, just as the boys

[108-156]are disappointed when the man they are waiting to see hanged does not

[108-157]come out, because the prosecution or the court has pardoned him. The

[108-158]people dispersed, the duke and Don Quixote returned to the castle, they

[108-159]locked up Tosilos, Doña Rodriguez and her daughter remained perfectly

[108-160]contented when they saw that any way the affair must end in marriage,

[108-161]and Tosilos wanted nothing else.
