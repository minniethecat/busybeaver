WHEREIN IS SHOWN THE FURTHEST AND HIGHEST POINT WHICH THE UNEXAMPLED

[069-001]COURAGE OF DON QUIXOTE REACHED OR COULD REACH; TOGETHER WITH THE

HAPPILY ACHIEVED ADVENTURE OF THE LIONS

[069-002]The history tells that when Don Quixote called out to Sancho to bring

[069-003]him his helmet, Sancho was buying some curds the shepherds agreed to

[069-004]sell him, and flurried by the great haste his master was in did not

[069-005]know what to do with them or what to carry them in; so, not to lose

[069-006]them, for he had already paid for them, he thought it best to throw

[069-007]them into his master’s helmet, and acting on this bright idea he went

[069-008]to see what his master wanted with him. He, as he approached, exclaimed

[069-009]to him:

[069-010]“Give me that helmet, my friend, for either I know little of

[069-011]adventures, or what I observe yonder is one that will, and does, call

[069-012]upon me to arm myself.”

[069-013]He of the green gaban, on hearing this, looked in all directions, but

[069-014]could perceive nothing, except a cart coming towards them with two or

[069-015]three small flags, which led him to conclude it must be carrying

[069-016]treasure of the King’s, and he said so to Don Quixote. He, however,

[069-017]would not believe him, being always persuaded and convinced that all

[069-018]that happened to him must be adventures and still more adventures; so

[069-019]he replied to the gentleman, “He who is prepared has his battle half

[069-020]fought; nothing is lost by my preparing myself, for I know by

[069-021]experience that I have enemies, visible and invisible, and I know not

[069-022]when, or where, or at what moment, or in what shapes they will attack

[069-023]me;” and turning to Sancho, he called for his helmet; and Sancho, as he

[069-024]had no time to take out the curds, had to give it just as it was. Don

[069-025]Quixote took it, and without perceiving what was in it thrust it down

[069-026]in hot haste upon his head; but as the curds were pressed and squeezed

[069-027]the whey began to run all over his face and beard, whereat he was so

[069-028]startled that he cried out to Sancho:

[069-029]“Sancho, what’s this? I think my head is softening, or my brains are

[069-030]melting, or I am sweating from head to foot! If I am sweating it is not

[069-031]indeed from fear. I am convinced beyond a doubt that the adventure

[069-032]which is about to befall me is a terrible one. Give me something to

[069-033]wipe myself with, if thou hast it, for this profuse sweat is blinding

[069-034]me.”

[069-035]Sancho held his tongue, and gave him a cloth, and gave thanks to God at

[069-036]the same time that his master had not found out what was the matter.

[069-037]Don Quixote then wiped himself, and took off his helmet to see what it

[069-038]was that made his head feel so cool, and seeing all that white mash

[069-039]inside his helmet he put it to his nose, and as soon as he had smelt it

[069-040]he exclaimed:

[069-041]“By the life of my lady Dulcinea del Toboso, but it is curds thou hast

[069-042]put here, thou treacherous, impudent, ill-mannered squire!”

[069-043]To which, with great composure and pretended innocence, Sancho replied,

[069-044]“If they are curds let me have them, your worship, and I’ll eat them;

[069-045]but let the devil eat them, for it must have been he who put them

[069-046]there. I dare to dirty your helmet! You have guessed the offender

[069-047]finely! Faith, sir, by the light God gives me, it seems I must have

[069-048]enchanters too, that persecute me as a creature and limb of your

[069-049]worship, and they must have put that nastiness there in order to

[069-050]provoke your patience to anger, and make you baste my ribs as you are

[069-051]wont to do. Well, this time, indeed, they have missed their aim, for I

[069-052]trust to my master’s good sense to see that I have got no curds or

[069-053]milk, or anything of the sort; and that if I had it is in my stomach I

[069-054]would put it and not in the helmet.”

[069-055]“May be so,” said Don Quixote. All this the gentleman was observing,

[069-056]and with astonishment, more especially when, after having wiped himself

[069-057]clean, his head, face, beard, and helmet, Don Quixote put it on, and

[069-058]settling himself firmly in his stirrups, easing his sword in the

[069-059]scabbard, and grasping his lance, he cried, “Now, come who will, here

[069-060]am I, ready to try conclusions with Satan himself in person!”

[069-061]By this time the cart with the flags had come up, unattended by anyone

[069-062]except the carter on a mule, and a man sitting in front. Don Quixote

[069-063]planted himself before it and said, “Whither are you going, brothers?

[069-064]What cart is this? What have you got in it? What flags are those?”

[069-065]To this the carter replied, “The cart is mine; what is in it is a pair

[069-066]of wild caged lions, which the governor of Oran is sending to court as

[069-067]a present to his Majesty; and the flags are our lord the King’s, to

[069-068]show that what is here is his property.”

[069-069]“And are the lions large?” asked Don Quixote.

[069-070]“So large,” replied the man who sat at the door of the cart, “that

[069-071]larger, or as large, have never crossed from Africa to Spain; I am the

[069-072]keeper, and I have brought over others, but never any like these. They

[069-073]are male and female; the male is in that first cage and the female in

[069-074]the one behind, and they are hungry now, for they have eaten nothing

[069-075]to-day, so let your worship stand aside, for we must make haste to the

[069-076]place where we are to feed them.”

[069-077]Hereupon, smiling slightly, Don Quixote exclaimed, “Lion-whelps to me!

[069-078]to me whelps of lions, and at such a time! Then, by God! those

[069-079]gentlemen who send them here shall see if I am a man to be frightened

[069-080]by lions. Get down, my good fellow, and as you are the keeper open the

[069-081]cages, and turn me out those beasts, and in the midst of this plain I

[069-082]will let them know who Don Quixote of La Mancha is, in spite and in the

[069-083]teeth of the enchanters who send them to me.”

[069-084]“So, so,” said the gentleman to himself at this; “our worthy knight has

[069-085]shown of what sort he is; the curds, no doubt, have softened his skull

[069-086]and brought his brains to a head.”

[069-087]At this instant Sancho came up to him, saying, “Señor, for God’s sake

[069-088]do something to keep my master, Don Quixote, from tackling these lions;

[069-089]for if he does they’ll tear us all to pieces here.”

[069-090]“Is your master then so mad,” asked the gentleman, “that you believe

[069-091]and are afraid he will engage such fierce animals?”

[069-092]“He is not mad,” said Sancho, “but he is venturesome.”

[069-093]“I will prevent it,” said the gentleman; and going over to Don Quixote,

[069-094]who was insisting upon the keeper’s opening the cages, he said to him,

[069-095]“Sir knight, knights-errant should attempt adventures which encourage

[069-096]the hope of a successful issue, not those which entirely withhold it;

[069-097]for valour that trenches upon temerity savours rather of madness than

[069-098]of courage; moreover, these lions do not come to oppose you, nor do

[069-099]they dream of such a thing; they are going as presents to his Majesty,

[069-100]and it will not be right to stop them or delay their journey.”

[069-101]“Gentle sir,” replied Don Quixote, “you go and mind your tame partridge

[069-102]and your bold ferret, and leave everyone to manage his own business;

[069-103]this is mine, and I know whether these gentlemen the lions come to me

[069-104]or not;” and then turning to the keeper he exclaimed, “By all that’s

[069-105]good, sir scoundrel, if you don’t open the cages this very instant,

[069-106]I’ll pin you to the cart with this lance.”

[069-107]The carter, seeing the determination of this apparition in armour, said

[069-108]to him, “Please your worship, for charity’s sake, señor, let me unyoke

[069-109]the mules and place myself in safety along with them before the lions

[069-110]are turned out; for if they kill them on me I am ruined for life, for

[069-111]all I possess is this cart and mules.”

[069-112]“O man of little faith,” replied Don Quixote, “get down and unyoke; you

[069-113]will soon see that you are exerting yourself for nothing, and that you

[069-114]might have spared yourself the trouble.”

[069-115]The carter got down and with all speed unyoked the mules, and the

[069-116]keeper called out at the top of his voice, “I call all here to witness

[069-117]that against my will and under compulsion I open the cages and let the

[069-118]lions loose, and that I warn this gentleman that he will be accountable

[069-119]for all the harm and mischief which these beasts may do, and for my

[069-120]salary and dues as well. You, gentlemen, place yourselves in safety

[069-121]before I open, for I know they will do me no harm.”

[069-122]Once more the gentleman strove to persuade Don Quixote not to do such a

[069-123]mad thing, as it was tempting God to engage in such a piece of folly.

[069-124]To this, Don Quixote replied that he knew what he was about. The

[069-125]gentleman in return entreated him to reflect, for he knew he was under

[069-126]a delusion.

[069-127]“Well, señor,” answered Don Quixote, “if you do not like to be a

[069-128]spectator of this tragedy, as in your opinion it will be, spur your

[069-129]flea-bitten mare, and place yourself in safety.”

[069-130]Hearing this, Sancho with tears in his eyes entreated him to give up an

[069-131]enterprise compared with which the one of the windmills, and the awful

[069-132]one of the fulling mills, and, in fact, all the feats he had attempted

[069-133]in the whole course of his life, were cakes and fancy bread. “Look ye,

[069-134]señor,” said Sancho, “there’s no enchantment here, nor anything of the

[069-135]sort, for between the bars and chinks of the cage I have seen the paw

[069-136]of a real lion, and judging by that I reckon the lion such a paw could

[069-137]belong to must be bigger than a mountain.”

[069-138]“Fear at any rate,” replied Don Quixote, “will make him look bigger to

[069-139]thee than half the world. Retire, Sancho, and leave me; and if I die

[069-140]here thou knowest our old compact; thou wilt repair to Dulcinea—I say

[069-141]no more.” To these he added some further words that banished all hope

[069-142]of his giving up his insane project. He of the green gaban would have

[069-143]offered resistance, but he found himself ill-matched as to arms, and

[069-144]did not think it prudent to come to blows with a madman, for such Don

[069-145]Quixote now showed himself to be in every respect; and the latter,

[069-146]renewing his commands to the keeper and repeating his threats, gave

[069-147]warning to the gentleman to spur his mare, Sancho his Dapple, and the

[069-148]carter his mules, all striving to get away from the cart as far as they

[069-149]could before the lions broke loose. Sancho was weeping over his

[069-150]master’s death, for this time he firmly believed it was in store for

[069-151]him from the claws of the lions; and he cursed his fate and called it

[069-152]an unlucky hour when he thought of taking service with him again; but

[069-153]with all his tears and lamentations he did not forget to thrash Dapple

[069-154]so as to put a good space between himself and the cart. The keeper,

[069-155]seeing that the fugitives were now some distance off, once more

[069-156]entreated and warned him as before; but he replied that he heard him,

[069-157]and that he need not trouble himself with any further warnings or

[069-158]entreaties, as they would be fruitless, and bade him make haste.

[069-159]During the delay that occurred while the keeper was opening the first

[069-160]cage, Don Quixote was considering whether it would not be well to do

[069-161]battle on foot, instead of on horseback, and finally resolved to fight

[069-162]on foot, fearing that Rocinante might take fright at the sight of the

[069-163]lions; he therefore sprang off his horse, flung his lance aside, braced

[069-164]his buckler on his arm, and drawing his sword, advanced slowly with

[069-165]marvellous intrepidity and resolute courage, to plant himself in front

[069-166]of the cart, commending himself with all his heart to God and to his

[069-167]lady Dulcinea.

[069-168]It is to be observed, that on coming to this passage, the author of

[069-169]this veracious history breaks out into exclamations. “O doughty Don

[069-170]Quixote! high-mettled past extolling! Mirror, wherein all the heroes of

[069-171]the world may see themselves! Second modern Don Manuel de Leon, once

[069-172]the glory and honour of Spanish knighthood! In what words shall I

[069-173]describe this dread exploit, by what language shall I make it credible

[069-174]to ages to come, what eulogies are there unmeet for thee, though they

[069-175]be hyperboles piled on hyperboles! On foot, alone, undaunted,

[069-176]high-souled, with but a simple sword, and that no trenchant blade of

[069-177]the Perrillo brand, a shield, but no bright polished steel one, there

[069-178]stoodst thou, biding and awaiting the two fiercest lions that Africa’s

[069-179]forests ever bred! Thy own deeds be thy praise, valiant Manchegan, and

[069-180]here I leave them as they stand, wanting the words wherewith to glorify

[069-181]them!”

[069-182]Here the author’s outburst came to an end, and he proceeded to take up

[069-183]the thread of his story, saying that the keeper, seeing that Don

[069-184]Quixote had taken up his position, and that it was impossible for him

[069-185]to avoid letting out the male without incurring the enmity of the fiery

[069-186]and daring knight, flung open the doors of the first cage, containing,

[069-187]as has been said, the lion, which was now seen to be of enormous size,

[069-188]and grim and hideous mien. The first thing he did was to turn round in

[069-189]the cage in which he lay, and protrude his claws, and stretch himself

[069-190]thoroughly; he next opened his mouth, and yawned very leisurely, and

[069-191]with near two palms’ length of tongue that he had thrust forth, he

[069-192]licked the dust out of his eyes and washed his face; having done this,

[069-193]he put his head out of the cage and looked all round with eyes like

[069-194]glowing coals, a spectacle and demeanour to strike terror into temerity

[069-195]itself. Don Quixote merely observed him steadily, longing for him to

[069-196]leap from the cart and come to close quarters with him, when he hoped

[069-197]to hew him in pieces.

[069-198]So far did his unparalleled madness go; but the noble lion, more

[069-199]courteous than arrogant, not troubling himself about silly bravado,

[069-200]after having looked all round, as has been said, turned about and

[069-201]presented his hind-quarters to Don Quixote, and very coolly and

[069-202]tranquilly lay down again in the cage. Seeing this, Don Quixote ordered

[069-203]the keeper to take a stick to him and provoke him to make him come out.

[069-204]“That I won’t,” said the keeper; “for if I anger him, the first he’ll

[069-205]tear in pieces will be myself. Be satisfied, sir knight, with what you

[069-206]have done, which leaves nothing more to be said on the score of

[069-207]courage, and do not seek to tempt fortune a second time. The lion has

[069-208]the door open; he is free to come out or not to come out; but as he has

[069-209]not come out so far, he will not come out to-day. Your worship’s great

[069-210]courage has been fully manifested already; no brave champion, so it

[069-211]strikes me, is bound to do more than challenge his enemy and wait for

[069-212]him on the field; if his adversary does not come, on him lies the

[069-213]disgrace, and he who waits for him carries off the crown of victory.”

[069-214]“That is true,” said Don Quixote; “close the door, my friend, and let

[069-215]me have, in the best form thou canst, what thou hast seen me do, by way

[069-216]of certificate; to wit, that thou didst open for the lion, that I

[069-217]waited for him, that he did not come out, that I still waited for him,

[069-218]and that still he did not come out, and lay down again. I am not bound

[069-219]to do more; enchantments avaunt, and God uphold the right, the truth,

[069-220]and true chivalry! Close the door as I bade thee, while I make signals

[069-221]to the fugitives that have left us, that they may learn this exploit

[069-222]from thy lips.”

[069-223]The keeper obeyed, and Don Quixote, fixing on the point of his lance

[069-224]the cloth he had wiped his face with after the deluge of curds,

[069-225]proceeded to recall the others, who still continued to fly, looking

[069-226]back at every step, all in a body, the gentleman bringing up the rear.

[069-227]Sancho, however, happening to observe the signal of the white cloth,

[069-228]exclaimed, “May I die, if my master has not overcome the wild beasts,

[069-229]for he is calling to us.”

[069-230]They all stopped, and perceived that it was Don Quixote who was making

[069-231]signals, and shaking off their fears to some extent, they approached

[069-232]slowly until they were near enough to hear distinctly Don Quixote’s

[069-233]voice calling to them. They returned at length to the cart, and as they

[069-234]came up, Don Quixote said to the carter, “Put your mules to once more,

[069-235]brother, and continue your journey; and do thou, Sancho, give him two

[069-236]gold crowns for himself and the keeper, to compensate for the delay

[069-237]they have incurred through me.”

[069-238]“That will I give with all my heart,” said Sancho; “but what has become

[069-239]of the lions? Are they dead or alive?”

[069-240]The keeper, then, in full detail, and bit by bit, described the end of

[069-241]the contest, exalting to the best of his power and ability the valour

[069-242]of Don Quixote, at the sight of whom the lion quailed, and would not

[069-243]and dared not come out of the cage, although he had held the door open

[069-244]ever so long; and showing how, in consequence of his having represented

[069-245]to the knight that it was tempting God to provoke the lion in order to

[069-246]force him out, which he wished to have done, he very reluctantly, and

[069-247]altogether against his will, had allowed the door to be closed.

[069-248]“What dost thou think of this, Sancho?” said Don Quixote. “Are there

[069-249]any enchantments that can prevail against true valour? The enchanters

[069-250]may be able to rob me of good fortune, but of fortitude and courage

[069-251]they cannot.”

[069-252]Sancho paid the crowns, the carter put to, the keeper kissed Don

[069-253]Quixote’s hands for the bounty bestowed upon him, and promised to give

[069-254]an account of the valiant exploit to the King himself, as soon as he

[069-255]saw him at court.

[069-256]“Then,” said Don Quixote, “if his Majesty should happen to ask who

[069-257]performed it, you must say THE KNIGHT OF THE LIONS; for it is my desire

[069-258]that into this the name I have hitherto borne of Knight of the Rueful

[069-259]Countenance be from this time forward changed, altered, transformed,

[069-260]and turned; and in this I follow the ancient usage of knights-errant,

[069-261]who changed their names when they pleased, or when it suited their

[069-262]purpose.”

[069-263]The cart went its way, and Don Quixote, Sancho, and he of the green

[069-264]gaban went theirs. All this time, Don Diego de Miranda had not spoken a

[069-265]word, being entirely taken up with observing and noting all that Don

[069-266]Quixote did and said, and the opinion he formed was that he was a man

[069-267]of brains gone mad, and a madman on the verge of rationality. The first

[069-268]part of his history had not yet reached him, for, had he read it, the

[069-269]amazement with which his words and deeds filled him would have

[069-270]vanished, as he would then have understood the nature of his madness;

[069-271]but knowing nothing of it, he took him to be rational one moment, and

[069-272]crazy the next, for what he said was sensible, elegant, and well

[069-273]expressed, and what he did, absurd, rash, and foolish; and said he to

[069-274]himself, “What could be madder than putting on a helmet full of curds,

[069-275]and then persuading oneself that enchanters are softening one’s skull;

[069-276]or what could be greater rashness and folly than wanting to fight lions

[069-277]tooth and nail?”

[069-278]Don Quixote roused him from these reflections and this soliloquy by

[069-279]saying, “No doubt, Señor Don Diego de Miranda, you set me down in your

[069-280]mind as a fool and a madman, and it would be no wonder if you did, for

[069-281]my deeds do not argue anything else. But for all that, I would have you

[069-282]take notice that I am neither so mad nor so foolish as I must have

[069-283]seemed to you. A gallant knight shows to advantage bringing his lance

[069-284]to bear adroitly upon a fierce bull under the eyes of his sovereign, in

[069-285]the midst of a spacious plaza; a knight shows to advantage arrayed in

[069-286]glittering armour, pacing the lists before the ladies in some joyous

[069-287]tournament, and all those knights show to advantage that entertain,

[069-288]divert, and, if we may say so, honour the courts of their princes by

[069-289]warlike exercises, or what resemble them; but to greater advantage than

[069-290]all these does a knight-errant show when he traverses deserts,

[069-291]solitudes, cross-roads, forests, and mountains, in quest of perilous

[069-292]adventures, bent on bringing them to a happy and successful issue, all

[069-293]to win a glorious and lasting renown. To greater advantage, I maintain,

[069-294]does the knight-errant show bringing aid to some widow in some lonely

[069-295]waste, than the court knight dallying with some city damsel. All

[069-296]knights have their own special parts to play; let the courtier devote

[069-297]himself to the ladies, let him add lustre to his sovereign’s court by

[069-298]his liveries, let him entertain poor gentlemen with the sumptuous fare

[069-299]of his table, let him arrange joustings, marshal tournaments, and prove

[069-300]himself noble, generous, and magnificent, and above all a good

[069-301]Christian, and so doing he will fulfil the duties that are especially

[069-302]his; but let the knight-errant explore the corners of the earth and

[069-303]penetrate the most intricate labyrinths, at each step let him attempt

[069-304]impossibilities, on desolate heaths let him endure the burning rays of

[069-305]the midsummer sun, and the bitter inclemency of the winter winds and

[069-306]frosts; let no lions daunt him, no monsters terrify him, no dragons

[069-307]make him quail; for to seek these, to attack those, and to vanquish

[069-308]all, are in truth his main duties. I, then, as it has fallen to my lot

[069-309]to be a member of knight-errantry, cannot avoid attempting all that to

[069-310]me seems to come within the sphere of my duties; thus it was my bounden

[069-311]duty to attack those lions that I just now attacked, although I knew it

[069-312]to be the height of rashness; for I know well what valour is, that it

[069-313]is a virtue that occupies a place between two vicious extremes,

[069-314]cowardice and temerity; but it will be a lesser evil for him who is

[069-315]valiant to rise till he reaches the point of rashness, than to sink

[069-316]until he reaches the point of cowardice; for, as it is easier for the

[069-317]prodigal than for the miser to become generous, so it is easier for a

[069-318]rash man to prove truly valiant than for a coward to rise to true

[069-319]valour; and believe me, Señor Don Diego, in attempting adventures it is

[069-320]better to lose by a card too many than by a card too few; for to hear

[069-321]it said, ‘such a knight is rash and daring,’ sounds better than ‘such a

[069-322]knight is timid and cowardly.’”

[069-323]“I protest, Señor Don Quixote,” said Don Diego, “everything you have

[069-324]said and done is proved correct by the test of reason itself; and I

[069-325]believe, if the laws and ordinances of knight-errantry should be lost,

[069-326]they might be found in your worship’s breast as in their own proper

[069-327]depository and muniment-house; but let us make haste, and reach my

[069-328]village, where you shall take rest after your late exertions; for if

[069-329]they have not been of the body they have been of the spirit, and these

[069-330]sometimes tend to produce bodily fatigue.”

[069-331]“I take the invitation as a great favour and honour, Señor Don Diego,”

[069-332]replied Don Quixote; and pressing forward at a better pace than before,

[069-333]at about two in the afternoon they reached the village and house of Don

[069-334]Diego, or, as Don Quixote called him, “The Knight of the Green Gaban.”
