OF THE DELECTABLE DISCOURSE WHICH THE DUCHESS AND HER DAMSELS HELD WITH

[085-001]SANCHO PANZA, WELL WORTH READING AND NOTING

p33a.jpg (138K)


[085-002]The history records that Sancho did not sleep that afternoon, but in

[085-003]order to keep his word came, before he had well done dinner, to visit

[085-004]the duchess, who, finding enjoyment in listening to him, made him sit

[085-005]down beside her on a low seat, though Sancho, out of pure good

[085-006]breeding, wanted not to sit down; the duchess, however, told him he was

[085-007]to sit down as governor and talk as squire, as in both respects he was

[085-008]worthy of even the chair of the Cid Ruy Diaz the Campeador. Sancho

[085-009]shrugged his shoulders, obeyed, and sat down, and all the duchess’s

[085-010]damsels and duennas gathered round him, waiting in profound silence to

[085-011]hear what he would say. It was the duchess, however, who spoke first,

[085-012]saying:

[085-013]“Now that we are alone, and that there is nobody here to overhear us, I

[085-014]should be glad if the señor governor would relieve me of certain doubts

[085-015]I have, rising out of the history of the great Don Quixote that is now

[085-016]in print. One is: inasmuch as worthy Sancho never saw Dulcinea, I mean

[085-017]the lady Dulcinea del Toboso, nor took Don Quixote’s letter to her, for

[085-018]it was left in the memorandum book in the Sierra Morena, how did he

[085-019]dare to invent the answer and all that about finding her sifting wheat,

[085-020]the whole story being a deception and falsehood, and so much to the

[085-021]prejudice of the peerless Dulcinea’s good name, a thing that is not at

[085-022]all becoming the character and fidelity of a good squire?”

p33b.jpg (326K)


[085-023]At these words, Sancho, without uttering one in reply, got up from his

[085-024]chair, and with noiseless steps, with his body bent and his finger on

[085-025]his lips, went all round the room lifting up the hangings; and this

[085-026]done, he came back to his seat and said, “Now, señora, that I have seen

[085-027]that there is no one except the bystanders listening to us on the sly,

[085-028]I will answer what you have asked me, and all you may ask me, without

[085-029]fear or dread. And the first thing I have got to say is, that for my

[085-030]own part I hold my master Don Quixote to be stark mad, though sometimes

[085-031]he says things that, to my mind, and indeed everybody’s that listens to

[085-032]him, are so wise, and run in such a straight furrow, that Satan himself

[085-033]could not have said them better; but for all that, really, and beyond

[085-034]all question, it’s my firm belief he is cracked. Well, then, as this is

[085-035]clear to my mind, I can venture to make him believe things that have

[085-036]neither head nor tail, like that affair of the answer to the letter,

[085-037]and that other of six or eight days ago, which is not yet in history,

[085-038]that is to say, the affair of the enchantment of my lady Dulcinea; for

[085-039]I made him believe she is enchanted, though there’s no more truth in it

[085-040]than over the hills of Úbeda.”

[085-041]The duchess begged him to tell her about the enchantment or deception,

[085-042]so Sancho told the whole story exactly as it had happened, and his

[085-043]hearers were not a little amused by it; and then resuming, the duchess

[085-044]said, “In consequence of what worthy Sancho has told me, a doubt starts

[085-045]up in my mind, and there comes a kind of whisper to my ear that says,

[085-046]‘If Don Quixote be mad, crazy, and cracked, and Sancho Panza his squire

[085-047]knows it, and, notwithstanding, serves and follows him, and goes

[085-048]trusting to his empty promises, there can be no doubt he must be still

[085-049]madder and sillier than his master; and that being so, it will be cast

[085-050]in your teeth, señora duchess, if you give the said Sancho an island to

[085-051]govern; for how will he who does not know how to govern himself know

[085-052]how to govern others?’”

[085-053]“By God, señora,” said Sancho, “but that doubt comes timely; but your

[085-054]grace may say it out, and speak plainly, or as you like; for I know

[085-055]what you say is true, and if I were wise I should have left my master

[085-056]long ago; but this was my fate, this was my bad luck; I can’t help it,

[085-057]I must follow him; we’re from the same village, I’ve eaten his bread,

[085-058]I’m fond of him, I’m grateful, he gave me his ass-colts, and above all

[085-059]I’m faithful; so it’s quite impossible for anything to separate us,

[085-060]except the pickaxe and shovel. And if your highness does not like to

[085-061]give me the government you promised, God made me without it, and maybe

[085-062]your not giving it to me will be all the better for my conscience, for

[085-063]fool as I am I know the proverb ‘to her hurt the ant got wings,’ and it

[085-064]may be that Sancho the squire will get to heaven sooner than Sancho the

[085-065]governor. ‘They make as good bread here as in France,’ and ‘by night

[085-066]all cats are grey,’ and ‘a hard case enough his, who hasn’t broken his

[085-067]fast at two in the afternoon,’ and ‘there’s no stomach a hand’s breadth

[085-068]bigger than another,’ and the same can be filled ‘with straw or hay,’

[085-069]as the saying is, and ‘the little birds of the field have God for their

[085-070]purveyor and caterer,’ and ‘four yards of Cuenca frieze keep one warmer

[085-071]than four of Segovia broad-cloth,’ and ‘when we quit this world and are

[085-072]put underground the prince travels by as narrow a path as the

[085-073]journeyman,’ and ‘the Pope’s body does not take up more feet of earth

[085-074]than the sacristan’s,’ for all that the one is higher than the other;

[085-075]for when we go to our graves we all pack ourselves up and make

[085-076]ourselves small, or rather they pack us up and make us small in spite

[085-077]of us, and then—good night to us. And I say once more, if your ladyship

[085-078]does not like to give me the island because I’m a fool, like a wise man

[085-079]I will take care to give myself no trouble about it; I have heard say

[085-080]that ‘behind the cross there’s the devil,’ and that ‘all that glitters

[085-081]is not gold,’ and that from among the oxen, and the ploughs, and the

[085-082]yokes, Wamba the husbandman was taken to be made King of Spain, and

[085-083]from among brocades, and pleasures, and riches, Roderick was taken to

[085-084]be devoured by adders, if the verses of the old ballads don’t lie.”

[085-085]“To be sure they don’t lie!” exclaimed Doña Rodriguez, the duenna, who

[085-086]was one of the listeners. “Why, there’s a ballad that says they put

[085-087]King Rodrigo alive into a tomb full of toads, and adders, and lizards,

[085-088]and that two days afterwards the king, in a plaintive, feeble voice,

[085-089]cried out from within the tomb-

[085-090]They gnaw me now, they gnaw me now,

[085-091]There where I most did sin.

[085-092]And according to that the gentleman has good reason to say he would

[085-093]rather be a labouring man than a king, if vermin are to eat him.”

[085-094]The duchess could not help laughing at the simplicity of her duenna, or

[085-095]wondering at the language and proverbs of Sancho, to whom she said,

[085-096]“Worthy Sancho knows very well that when once a knight has made a

[085-097]promise he strives to keep it, though it should cost him his life. My

[085-098]lord and husband the duke, though not one of the errant sort, is none

[085-099]the less a knight for that reason, and will keep his word about the

[085-100]promised island, in spite of the envy and malice of the world. Let

[085-101]Sancho be of good cheer; for when he least expects it he will find

[085-102]himself seated on the throne of his island and seat of dignity, and

[085-103]will take possession of his government that he may discard it for

[085-104]another of three-bordered brocade. The charge I give him is to be

[085-105]careful how he governs his vassals, bearing in mind that they are all

[085-106]loyal and well-born.”

[085-107]“As to governing them well,” said Sancho, “there’s no need of charging

[085-108]me to do that, for I’m kind-hearted by nature, and full of compassion

[085-109]for the poor; there’s no stealing the loaf from him who kneads and

[085-110]bakes;’ and by my faith it won’t do to throw false dice with me; I am

[085-111]an old dog, and I know all about ‘tus, tus;’ I can be wide-awake if

[085-112]need be, and I don’t let clouds come before my eyes, for I know where

[085-113]the shoe pinches me; I say so, because with me the good will have

[085-114]support and protection, and the bad neither footing nor access. And it

[085-115]seems to me that, in governments, to make a beginning is everything;

[085-116]and maybe, after having been governor a fortnight, I’ll take kindly to

[085-117]the work and know more about it than the field labour I have been

[085-118]brought up to.”

[085-119]“You are right, Sancho,” said the duchess, “for no one is born ready

[085-120]taught, and the bishops are made out of men and not out of stones. But

[085-121]to return to the subject we were discussing just now, the enchantment

[085-122]of the lady Dulcinea, I look upon it as certain, and something more

[085-123]than evident, that Sancho’s idea of practising a deception upon his

[085-124]master, making him believe that the peasant girl was Dulcinea and that

[085-125]if he did not recognise her it must be because she was enchanted, was

[085-126]all a device of one of the enchanters that persecute Don Quixote. For

[085-127]in truth and earnest, I know from good authority that the coarse

[085-128]country wench who jumped up on the ass was and is Dulcinea del Toboso,

[085-129]and that worthy Sancho, though he fancies himself the deceiver, is the

[085-130]one that is deceived; and that there is no more reason to doubt the

[085-131]truth of this, than of anything else we never saw. Señor Sancho Panza

[085-132]must know that we too have enchanters here that are well disposed to

[085-133]us, and tell us what goes on in the world, plainly and distinctly,

[085-134]without subterfuge or deception; and believe me, Sancho, that agile

[085-135]country lass was and is Dulcinea del Toboso, who is as much enchanted

[085-136]as the mother that bore her; and when we least expect it, we shall see

[085-137]her in her own proper form, and then Sancho will be disabused of the

[085-138]error he is under at present.”

[085-139]“All that’s very possible,” said Sancho Panza; “and now I’m willing to

[085-140]believe what my master says about what he saw in the cave of

[085-141]Montesinos, where he says he saw the lady Dulcinea del Toboso in the

[085-142]very same dress and apparel that I said I had seen her in when I

[085-143]enchanted her all to please myself. It must be all exactly the other

[085-144]way, as your ladyship says; because it is impossible to suppose that

[085-145]out of my poor wit such a cunning trick could be concocted in a moment,

[085-146]nor do I think my master is so mad that by my weak and feeble

[085-147]persuasion he could be made to believe a thing so out of all reason.

[085-148]But, señora, your excellence must not therefore think me ill-disposed,

[085-149]for a dolt like me is not bound to see into the thoughts and plots of

[085-150]those vile enchanters. I invented all that to escape my master’s

[085-151]scolding, and not with any intention of hurting him; and if it has

[085-152]turned out differently, there is a God in heaven who judges our

[085-153]hearts.”

[085-154]“That is true,” said the duchess; “but tell me, Sancho, what is this

[085-155]you say about the cave of Montesinos, for I should like to know.”

[085-156]Sancho upon this related to her, word for word, what has been said

[085-157]already touching that adventure, and having heard it the duchess said,

[085-158]“From this occurrence it may be inferred that, as the great Don Quixote

[085-159]says he saw there the same country wench Sancho saw on the way from El

[085-160]Toboso, it is, no doubt, Dulcinea, and that there are some very active

[085-161]and exceedingly busy enchanters about.”

[085-162]“So I say,” said Sancho, “and if my lady Dulcinea is enchanted, so much

[085-163]the worse for her, and I’m not going to pick a quarrel with my master’s

[085-164]enemies, who seem to be many and spiteful. The truth is that the one I

[085-165]saw was a country wench, and I set her down to be a country wench; and

[085-166]if that was Dulcinea it must not be laid at my door, nor should I be

[085-167]called to answer for it or take the consequences. But they must go

[085-168]nagging at me at every step—‘Sancho said it, Sancho did it, Sancho

[085-169]here, Sancho there,’ as if Sancho was nobody at all, and not that same

[085-170]Sancho Panza that’s now going all over the world in books, so Samson

[085-171]Carrasco told me, and he’s at any rate one that’s a bachelor of

[085-172]Salamanca; and people of that sort can’t lie, except when the whim

[085-173]seizes them or they have some very good reason for it. So there’s no

[085-174]occasion for anybody to quarrel with me; and then I have a good

[085-175]character, and, as I have heard my master say, ‘a good name is better

[085-176]than great riches;’ let them only stick me into this government and

[085-177]they’ll see wonders, for one who has been a good squire will be a good

[085-178]governor.”

[085-179]“All worthy Sancho’s observations,” said the duchess, “are Catonian

[085-180]sentences, or at any rate out of the very heart of Michael Verino

[085-181]himself, who _florentibus occidit annis_. In fact, to speak in his own

[085-182]style, ‘under a bad cloak there’s often a good drinker.’”

[085-183]“Indeed, señora,” said Sancho, “I never yet drank out of wickedness;

[085-184]from thirst I have very likely, for I have nothing of the hypocrite in

[085-185]me; I drink when I’m inclined, or, if I’m not inclined, when they offer

[085-186]it to me, so as not to look either strait-laced or ill-bred; for when a

[085-187]friend drinks one’s health what heart can be so hard as not to return

[085-188]it? But if I put on my shoes I don’t dirty them; besides, squires to

[085-189]knights-errant mostly drink water, for they are always wandering among

[085-190]woods, forests and meadows, mountains and crags, without a drop of wine

[085-191]to be had if they gave their eyes for it.”

[085-192]“So I believe,” said the duchess; “and now let Sancho go and take his

[085-193]sleep, and we will talk by-and-by at greater length, and settle how he

[085-194]may soon go and stick himself into the government, as he says.”

[085-195]Sancho once more kissed the duchess’s hand, and entreated her to let

[085-196]good care be taken of his Dapple, for he was the light of his eyes.

[085-197]“What is Dapple?” said the duchess.

[085-198]“My ass,” said Sancho, “which, not to mention him by that name, I’m

[085-199]accustomed to call Dapple; I begged this lady duenna here to take care

[085-200]of him when I came into the castle, and she got as angry as if I had

[085-201]said she was ugly or old, though it ought to be more natural and proper

[085-202]for duennas to feed asses than to ornament chambers. God bless me! what

[085-203]a spite a gentleman of my village had against these ladies!”

[085-204]“He must have been some clown,” said Doña Rodriguez the duenna; “for if

[085-205]he had been a gentleman and well-born he would have exalted them higher

[085-206]than the horns of the moon.”

[085-207]“That will do,” said the duchess; “no more of this; hush, Doña

[085-208]Rodriguez, and let Señor Panza rest easy and leave the treatment of

[085-209]Dapple in my charge, for as he is a treasure of Sancho’s, I’ll put him

[085-210]on the apple of my eye.”

[085-211]“It will be enough for him to be in the stable,” said Sancho, “for

[085-212]neither he nor I are worthy to rest a moment in the apple of your

[085-213]highness’s eye, and I’d as soon stab myself as consent to it; for

[085-214]though my master says that in civilities it is better to lose by a card

[085-215]too many than a card too few, when it comes to civilities to asses we

[085-216]must mind what we are about and keep within due bounds.”

[085-217]“Take him to your government, Sancho,” said the duchess, “and there you

[085-218]will be able to make as much of him as you like, and even release him

[085-219]from work and pension him off.”

[085-220]“Don’t think, señora duchess, that you have said anything absurd,” said

[085-221]Sancho; “I have seen more than two asses go to governments, and for me

[085-222]to take mine with me would be nothing new.”

[085-223]Sancho’s words made the duchess laugh again and gave her fresh

[085-224]amusement, and dismissing him to sleep she went away to tell the duke

[085-225]the conversation she had had with him, and between them they plotted

[085-226]and arranged to play a joke upon Don Quixote that was to be a rare one

[085-227]and entirely in knight-errantry style, and in that same style they

[085-228]practised several upon him, so much in keeping and so clever that they

[085-229]form the best adventures this great history contains.
