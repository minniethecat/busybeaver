[106-001]The duke and duchess resolved that the challenge Don Quixote had, for

[106-002]the reason already mentioned, given their vassal, should be proceeded

[106-003]with; and as the young man was in Flanders, whither he had fled to

[106-004]escape having Doña Rodriguez for a mother-in-law, they arranged to

[106-005]substitute for him a Gascon lacquey, named Tosilos, first of all

[106-006]carefully instructing him in all he had to do. Two days later the duke

[106-007]told Don Quixote that in four days from that time his opponent would

[106-008]present himself on the field of battle armed as a knight, and would

[106-009]maintain that the damsel lied by half a beard, nay a whole beard, if

[106-010]she affirmed that he had given her a promise of marriage. Don Quixote

[106-011]was greatly pleased at the news, and promised himself to do wonders in

[106-012]the lists, and reckoned it rare good fortune that an opportunity should

[106-013]have offered for letting his noble hosts see what the might of his

[106-014]strong arm was capable of; and so in high spirits and satisfaction he

[106-015]awaited the expiration of the four days, which measured by his

[106-016]impatience seemed spinning themselves out into four hundred ages. Let

[106-017]us leave them to pass as we do other things, and go and bear Sancho

[106-018]company, as mounted on Dapple, half glad, half sad, he paced along on

[106-019]his road to join his master, in whose society he was happier than in

[106-020]being governor of all the islands in the world. Well then, it so

[106-021]happened that before he had gone a great way from the island of his

[106-022]government (and whether it was island, city, town, or village that he

[106-023]governed he never troubled himself to inquire) he saw coming along the

[106-024]road he was travelling six pilgrims with staves, foreigners of that

[106-025]sort that beg for alms singing; who as they drew near arranged

[106-026]themselves in a line and lifting up their voices all together began to

[106-027]sing in their own language something that Sancho could not understand,

[106-028]with the exception of one word which sounded plainly “alms,” from which

[106-029]he gathered that it was alms they asked for in their song; and being,

[106-030]as Cide Hamete says, remarkably charitable, he took out of his alforjas

[106-031]the half loaf and half cheese he had been provided with, and gave them

[106-032]to them, explaining to them by signs that he had nothing else to give

[106-033]them. They received them very gladly, but exclaimed, “Geld! Geld!”

[106-034]“I don’t understand what you want of me, good people,” said Sancho.

[106-035]On this one of them took a purse out of his bosom and showed it to

[106-036]Sancho, by which he comprehended they were asking for money, and

[106-037]putting his thumb to his throat and spreading his hand upwards he gave

[106-038]them to understand that he had not the sign of a coin about him, and

[106-039]urging Dapple forward he broke through them. But as he was passing, one

[106-040]of them who had been examining him very closely rushed towards him, and

[106-041]flinging his arms round him exclaimed in a loud voice and good Spanish,

[106-042]“God bless me! What’s this I see? Is it possible that I hold in my arms

[106-043]my dear friend, my good neighbour Sancho Panza? But there’s no doubt

[106-044]about it, for I’m not asleep, nor am I drunk just now.”

[106-045]Sancho was surprised to hear himself called by his name and find

[106-046]himself embraced by a foreign pilgrim, and after regarding him steadily

[106-047]without speaking he was still unable to recognise him; but the pilgrim

[106-048]perceiving his perplexity cried, “What! and is it possible, Sancho

[106-049]Panza, that thou dost not know thy neighbour Ricote, the Morisco

[106-050]shopkeeper of thy village?”

[106-051]Sancho upon this looking at him more carefully began to recall his

[106-052]features, and at last recognised him perfectly, and without getting off

[106-053]the ass threw his arms round his neck saying, “Who the devil could have

[106-054]known thee, Ricote, in this mummer’s dress thou art in? Tell me, who

[106-055]has frenchified thee, and how dost thou dare to return to Spain, where

[106-056]if they catch thee and recognise thee it will go hard enough with

[106-057]thee?”

[106-058]“If thou dost not betray me, Sancho,” said the pilgrim, “I am safe; for

[106-059]in this dress no one will recognise me; but let us turn aside out of

[106-060]the road into that grove there where my comrades are going to eat and

[106-061]rest, and thou shalt eat with them there, for they are very good

[106-062]fellows; I’ll have time enough to tell thee then all that has happened

[106-063]me since I left our village in obedience to his Majesty’s edict that

[106-064]threatened such severities against the unfortunate people of my nation,

[106-065]as thou hast heard.”

[106-066]Sancho complied, and Ricote having spoken to the other pilgrims they

[106-067]withdrew to the grove they saw, turning a considerable distance out of

[106-068]the road. They threw down their staves, took off their pilgrim’s cloaks

[106-069]and remained in their under-clothing; they were all good-looking young

[106-070]fellows, except Ricote, who was a man somewhat advanced in years. They

[106-071]carried alforjas all of them, and all apparently well filled, at least

[106-072]with things provocative of thirst, such as would summon it from two

[106-073]leagues off. They stretched themselves on the ground, and making a

[106-074]tablecloth of the grass they spread upon it bread, salt, knives,

[106-075]walnut, scraps of cheese, and well-picked ham-bones which if they were

[106-076]past gnawing were not past sucking. They also put down a black dainty

[106-077]called, they say, caviar, and made of the eggs of fish, a great

[106-078]thirst-wakener. Nor was there any lack of olives, dry, it is true, and

[106-079]without any seasoning, but for all that toothsome and pleasant. But

[106-080]what made the best show in the field of the banquet was half a dozen

[106-081]botas of wine, for each of them produced his own from his alforjas;

[106-082]even the good Ricote, who from a Morisco had transformed himself into a

[106-083]German or Dutchman, took out his, which in size might have vied with

[106-084]the five others. They then began to eat with very great relish and very

[106-085]leisurely, making the most of each morsel—very small ones of

[106-086]everything—they took up on the point of the knife; and then all at the

[106-087]same moment raised their arms and botas aloft, the mouths placed in

[106-088]their mouths, and all eyes fixed on heaven just as if they were taking

[106-089]aim at it; and in this attitude they remained ever so long, wagging

[106-090]their heads from side to side as if in acknowledgment of the pleasure

[106-091]they were enjoying while they decanted the bowels of the bottles into

[106-092]their own stomachs.

[106-093]Sancho beheld all, “and nothing gave him pain;” so far from that,

[106-094]acting on the proverb he knew so well, “when thou art at Rome do as

[106-095]thou seest,” he asked Ricote for his bota and took aim like the rest of

[106-096]them, and with not less enjoyment. Four times did the botas bear being

[106-097]uplifted, but the fifth it was all in vain, for they were drier and

[106-098]more sapless than a rush by that time, which made the jollity that had

[106-099]been kept up so far begin to flag.

[106-100]Every now and then someone of them would grasp Sancho’s right hand in

[106-101]his own saying, “Español y Tudesqui tuto uno: bon compaño;” and Sancho

[106-102]would answer, “Bon compaño, jur a Di!” and then go off into a fit of

[106-103]laughter that lasted an hour, without a thought for the moment of

[106-104]anything that had befallen him in his government; for cares have very

[106-105]little sway over us while we are eating and drinking. At length, the

[106-106]wine having come to an end with them, drowsiness began to come over

[106-107]them, and they dropped asleep on their very table and tablecloth.

[106-108]Ricote and Sancho alone remained awake, for they had eaten more and

[106-109]drunk less, and Ricote drawing Sancho aside, they seated themselves at

[106-110]the foot of a beech, leaving the pilgrims buried in sweet sleep; and

[106-111]without once falling into his own Morisco tongue Ricote spoke as

[106-112]follows in pure Castilian:

[106-113]“Thou knowest well, neighbour and friend Sancho Panza, how the

[106-114]proclamation or edict his Majesty commanded to be issued against those

[106-115]of my nation filled us all with terror and dismay; me at least it did,

[106-116]insomuch that I think before the time granted us for quitting Spain was

[106-117]out, the full force of the penalty had already fallen upon me and upon

[106-118]my children. I decided, then, and I think wisely (just like one who

[106-119]knows that at a certain date the house he lives in will be taken from

[106-120]him, and looks out beforehand for another to change into), I decided, I

[106-121]say, to leave the town myself, alone and without my family, and go to

[106-122]seek out some place to remove them to comfortably and not in the

[106-123]hurried way in which the others took their departure; for I saw very

[106-124]plainly, and so did all the older men among us, that the proclamations

[106-125]were not mere threats, as some said, but positive enactments which

[106-126]would be enforced at the appointed time; and what made me believe this

[106-127]was what I knew of the base and extravagant designs which our people

[106-128]harboured, designs of such a nature that I think it was a divine

[106-129]inspiration that moved his Majesty to carry out a resolution so

[106-130]spirited; not that we were all guilty, for some there were true and

[106-131]steadfast Christians; but they were so few that they could make no head

[106-132]against those who were not; and it was not prudent to cherish a viper

[106-133]in the bosom by having enemies in the house. In short it was with just

[106-134]cause that we were visited with the penalty of banishment, a mild and

[106-135]lenient one in the eyes of some, but to us the most terrible that could

[106-136]be inflicted upon us. Wherever we are we weep for Spain; for after all

[106-137]we were born there and it is our natural fatherland. Nowhere do we find

[106-138]the reception our unhappy condition needs; and in Barbary and all the

[106-139]parts of Africa where we counted upon being received, succoured, and

[106-140]welcomed, it is there they insult and ill-treat us most. We knew not

[106-141]our good fortune until we lost it; and such is the longing we almost

[106-142]all of us have to return to Spain, that most of those who like myself

[106-143]know the language, and there are many who do, come back to it and leave

[106-144]their wives and children forsaken yonder, so great is their love for

[106-145]it; and now I know by experience the meaning of the saying, sweet is

[106-146]the love of one’s country.

[106-147]“I left our village, as I said, and went to France, but though they

[106-148]gave us a kind reception there I was anxious to see all I could. I

[106-149]crossed into Italy, and reached Germany, and there it seemed to me we

[106-150]might live with more freedom, as the inhabitants do not pay any

[106-151]attention to trifling points; everyone lives as he likes, for in most

[106-152]parts they enjoy liberty of conscience. I took a house in a town near

[106-153]Augsburg, and then joined these pilgrims, who are in the habit of

[106-154]coming to Spain in great numbers every year to visit the shrines there,

[106-155]which they look upon as their Indies and a sure and certain source of

[106-156]gain. They travel nearly all over it, and there is no town out of which

[106-157]they do not go full up of meat and drink, as the saying is, and with a

[106-158]real, at least, in money, and they come off at the end of their travels

[106-159]with more than a hundred crowns saved, which, changed into gold, they

[106-160]smuggle out of the kingdom either in the hollow of their staves or in

[106-161]the patches of their pilgrim’s cloaks or by some device of their own,

[106-162]and carry to their own country in spite of the guards at the posts and

[106-163]passes where they are searched. Now my purpose is, Sancho, to carry

[106-164]away the treasure that I left buried, which, as it is outside the town,

[106-165]I shall be able to do without risk, and to write, or cross over from

[106-166]Valencia, to my daughter and wife, who I know are at Algiers, and find

[106-167]some means of bringing them to some French port and thence to Germany,

[106-168]there to await what it may be God’s will to do with us; for, after all,

[106-169]Sancho, I know well that Ricota my daughter and Francisca Ricota my

[106-170]wife are Catholic Christians, and though I am not so much so, still I

[106-171]am more of a Christian than a Moor, and it is always my prayer to God

[106-172]that he will open the eyes of my understanding and show me how I am to

[106-173]serve him; but what amazes me and I cannot understand is why my wife

[106-174]and daughter should have gone to Barbary rather than to France, where

[106-175]they could live as Christians.”

[106-176]To this Sancho replied, “Remember, Ricote, that may not have been open

[106-177]to them, for Juan Tiopieyo thy wife’s brother took them, and being a

[106-178]true Moor he went where he could go most easily; and another thing I

[106-179]can tell thee, it is my belief thou art going in vain to look for what

[106-180]thou hast left buried, for we heard they took from thy brother-in-law

[106-181]and thy wife a great quantity of pearls and money in gold which they

[106-182]brought to be passed.”

[106-183]“That may be,” said Ricote; “but I know they did not touch my hoard,

[106-184]for I did not tell them where it was, for fear of accidents; and so, if

[106-185]thou wilt come with me, Sancho, and help me to take it away and conceal

[106-186]it, I will give thee two hundred crowns wherewith thou mayest relieve

[106-187]thy necessities, and, as thou knowest, I know they are many.”

[106-188]“I would do it,” said Sancho; “but I am not at all covetous, for I gave

[106-189]up an office this morning in which, if I was, I might have made the

[106-190]walls of my house of gold and dined off silver plates before six months

[106-191]were over; and so for this reason, and because I feel I would be guilty

[106-192]of treason to my king if I helped his enemies, I would not go with thee

[106-193]if instead of promising me two hundred crowns thou wert to give me four

[106-194]hundred here in hand.”

[106-195]“And what office is this thou hast given up, Sancho?” asked Ricote.

[106-196]“I have given up being governor of an island,” said Sancho, “and such a

[106-197]one, faith, as you won’t find the like of easily.”

[106-198]“And where is this island?” said Ricote.

[106-199]“Where?” said Sancho; “two leagues from here, and it is called the

[106-200]island of Barataria.”

[106-201]“Nonsense! Sancho,” said Ricote; “islands are away out in the sea;

[106-202]there are no islands on the mainland.”

[106-203]“What? No islands!” said Sancho; “I tell thee, friend Ricote, I left it

[106-204]this morning, and yesterday I was governing there as I pleased like a

[106-205]sagittarius; but for all that I gave it up, for it seemed to me a

[106-206]dangerous office, a governor’s.”

[106-207]“And what hast thou gained by the government?” asked Ricote.

[106-208]“I have gained,” said Sancho, “the knowledge that I am no good for

[106-209]governing, unless it is a drove of cattle, and that the riches that are

[106-210]to be got by these governments are got at the cost of one’s rest and

[106-211]sleep, ay and even one’s food; for in islands the governors must eat

[106-212]little, especially if they have doctors to look after their health.”

[106-213]“I don’t understand thee, Sancho,” said Ricote; “but it seems to me all

[106-214]nonsense thou art talking. Who would give thee islands to govern? Is

[106-215]there any scarcity in the world of cleverer men than thou art for

[106-216]governors? Hold thy peace, Sancho, and come back to thy senses, and

[106-217]consider whether thou wilt come with me as I said to help me to take

[106-218]away treasure I left buried (for indeed it may be called a treasure, it

[106-219]is so large), and I will give thee wherewithal to keep thee, as I told

[106-220]thee.”

[106-221]“And I have told thee already, Ricote, that I will not,” said Sancho;

[106-222]“let it content thee that by me thou shalt not be betrayed, and go thy

[106-223]way in God’s name and let me go mine; for I know that well-gotten gain

[106-224]may be lost, but ill-gotten gain is lost, itself and its owner

[106-225]likewise.”

[106-226]“I will not press thee, Sancho,” said Ricote; “but tell me, wert thou

[106-227]in our village when my wife and daughter and brother-in-law left it?”

[106-228]“I was so,” said Sancho; “and I can tell thee thy daughter left it

[106-229]looking so lovely that all the village turned out to see her, and

[106-230]everybody said she was the fairest creature in the world. She wept as

[106-231]she went, and embraced all her friends and acquaintances and those who

[106-232]came out to see her, and she begged them all to commend her to God and

[106-233]Our Lady his mother, and this in such a touching way that it made me

[106-234]weep myself, though I’m not much given to tears commonly; and, faith,

[106-235]many a one would have liked to hide her, or go out and carry her off on

[106-236]the road; but the fear of going against the king’s command kept them

[106-237]back. The one who showed himself most moved was Don Pedro Gregorio, the

[106-238]rich young heir thou knowest of, and they say he was deep in love with

[106-239]her; and since she left he has not been seen in our village again, and

[106-240]we all suspect he has gone after her to steal her away, but so far

[106-241]nothing has been heard of it.”

[106-242]“I always had a suspicion that gentleman had a passion for my

[106-243]daughter,” said Ricote; “but as I felt sure of my Ricota’s virtue it

[106-244]gave me no uneasiness to know that he loved her; for thou must have

[106-245]heard it said, Sancho, that the Morisco women seldom or never engage in

[106-246]amours with the old Christians; and my daughter, who I fancy thought

[106-247]more of being a Christian than of lovemaking, would not trouble herself

[106-248]about the attentions of this heir.”

[106-249]“God grant it,” said Sancho, “for it would be a bad business for both

[106-250]of them; but now let me be off, friend Ricote, for I want to reach

[106-251]where my master Don Quixote is to-night.”

[106-252]“God be with thee, brother Sancho,” said Ricote; “my comrades are

[106-253]beginning to stir, and it is time, too, for us to continue our

[106-254]journey;” and then they both embraced, and Sancho mounted Dapple, and

[106-255]Ricote leant upon his staff, and so they parted.
