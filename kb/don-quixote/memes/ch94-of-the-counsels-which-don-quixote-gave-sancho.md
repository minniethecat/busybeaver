OF THE COUNSELS WHICH DON QUIXOTE GAVE SANCHO PANZA BEFORE HE SET OUT

[094-001]TO GOVERN THE ISLAND, TOGETHER WITH OTHER WELL-CONSIDERED MATTERS

p42a.jpg (120K)


[094-002]The duke and duchess were so well pleased with the successful and droll

[094-003]result of the adventure of the Distressed One, that they resolved to

[094-004]carry on the joke, seeing what a fit subject they had to deal with for

[094-005]making it all pass for reality. So having laid their plans and given

[094-006]instructions to their servants and vassals how to behave to Sancho in

[094-007]his government of the promised island, the next day, that following

[094-008]Clavileño’s flight, the duke told Sancho to prepare and get ready to go

[094-009]and be governor, for his islanders were already looking out for him as

[094-010]for the showers of May.

[094-011]Sancho made him an obeisance, and said, “Ever since I came down from

[094-012]heaven, and from the top of it beheld the earth, and saw how little it

[094-013]is, the great desire I had to be a governor has been partly cooled in

[094-014]me; for what is there grand in being ruler on a grain of mustard seed,

[094-015]or what dignity or authority in governing half a dozen men about as big

[094-016]as hazel nuts; for, so far as I could see, there were no more on the

[094-017]whole earth? If your lordship would be so good as to give me ever so

[094-018]small a bit of heaven, were it no more than half a league, I’d rather

[094-019]have it than the best island in the world.”

[094-020]“Recollect, Sancho,” said the duke, “I cannot give a bit of heaven, no

[094-021]not so much as the breadth of my nail, to anyone; rewards and favours

[094-022]of that sort are reserved for God alone. What I can give I give you,

[094-023]and that is a real, genuine island, compact, well proportioned, and

[094-024]uncommonly fertile and fruitful, where, if you know how to use your

[094-025]opportunities, you may, with the help of the world’s riches, gain those

[094-026]of heaven.”

[094-027]“Well then,” said Sancho, “let the island come; and I’ll try and be

[094-028]such a governor, that in spite of scoundrels I’ll go to heaven; and

[094-029]it’s not from any craving to quit my own humble condition or better

[094-030]myself, but from the desire I have to try what it tastes like to be a

[094-031]governor.”

[094-032]“If you once make trial of it, Sancho,” said the duke, “you’ll eat your

[094-033]fingers off after the government, so sweet a thing is it to command and

[094-034]be obeyed. Depend upon it when your master comes to be emperor (as he

[094-035]will beyond a doubt from the course his affairs are taking), it will be

[094-036]no easy matter to wrest the dignity from him, and he will be sore and

[094-037]sorry at heart to have been so long without becoming one.”

[094-038]“Señor,” said Sancho, “it is my belief it’s a good thing to be in

[094-039]command, if it’s only over a drove of cattle.”

[094-040]“May I be buried with you, Sancho,” said the duke, “but you know

[094-041]everything; I hope you will make as good a governor as your sagacity

[094-042]promises; and that is all I have to say; and now remember to-morrow is

[094-043]the day you must set out for the government of the island, and this

[094-044]evening they will provide you with the proper attire for you to wear,

[094-045]and all things requisite for your departure.”

[094-046]“Let them dress me as they like,” said Sancho; “however I’m dressed

[094-047]I’ll be Sancho Panza.”

[094-048]“That’s true,” said the duke; “but one’s dress must be suited to the

[094-049]office or rank one holds; for it would not do for a jurist to dress

[094-050]like a soldier, or a soldier like a priest. You, Sancho, shall go

[094-051]partly as a lawyer, partly as a captain, for, in the island I am giving

[094-052]you, arms are needed as much as letters, and letters as much as arms.”

[094-053]“Of letters I know but little,” said Sancho, “for I don’t even know the

[094-054]A B C; but it is enough for me to have the Christus in my memory to be

[094-055]a good governor. As for arms, I’ll handle those they give me till I

[094-056]drop, and then, God be my help!”

[094-057]“With so good a memory,” said the duke, “Sancho cannot go wrong in

[094-058]anything.”

[094-059]Here Don Quixote joined them; and learning what passed, and how soon

[094-060]Sancho was to go to his government, he with the duke’s permission took

[094-061]him by the hand, and retired to his room with him for the purpose of

[094-062]giving him advice as to how he was to demean himself in his office. As

[094-063]soon as they had entered the chamber he closed the door after him, and

[094-064]almost by force made Sancho sit down beside him, and in a quiet tone

[094-065]thus addressed him: “I give infinite thanks to heaven, friend Sancho,

[094-066]that, before I have met with any good luck, fortune has come forward to

[094-067]meet thee. I who counted upon my good fortune to discharge the

[094-068]recompense of thy services, find myself still waiting for advancement,

[094-069]while thou, before the time, and contrary to all reasonable

[094-070]expectation, seest thyself blessed in the fulfillment of thy desires.

[094-071]Some will bribe, beg, solicit, rise early, entreat, persist, without

[094-072]attaining the object of their suit; while another comes, and without

[094-073]knowing why or wherefore, finds himself invested with the place or

[094-074]office so many have sued for; and here it is that the common saying,

[094-075]‘There is good luck as well as bad luck in suits,’ applies. Thou, who,

[094-076]to my thinking, art beyond all doubt a dullard, without early rising or

[094-077]night watching or taking any trouble, with the mere breath of

[094-078]knight-errantry that has breathed upon thee, seest thyself without more

[094-079]ado governor of an island, as though it were a mere matter of course.

[094-080]This I say, Sancho, that thou attribute not the favour thou hast

[094-081]received to thine own merits, but give thanks to heaven that disposes

[094-082]matters beneficently, and secondly thanks to the great power the

[094-083]profession of knight-errantry contains in itself. With a heart, then,

[094-084]inclined to believe what I have said to thee, attend, my son, to thy

[094-085]Cato here who would counsel thee and be thy polestar and guide to

[094-086]direct and pilot thee to a safe haven out of this stormy sea wherein

[094-087]thou art about to ingulf thyself; for offices and great trusts are

[094-088]nothing else but a mighty gulf of troubles.

[094-089]“First of all, my son, thou must fear God, for in the fear of him is

[094-090]wisdom, and being wise thou canst not err in aught.

[094-091]“Secondly, thou must keep in view what thou art, striving to know

[094-092]thyself, the most difficult thing to know that the mind can imagine. If

[094-093]thou knowest thyself, it will follow thou wilt not puff thyself up like

[094-094]the frog that strove to make himself as large as the ox; if thou dost,

[094-095]the recollection of having kept pigs in thine own country will serve as

[094-096]the ugly feet for the wheel of thy folly.”

[094-097]“That’s the truth,” said Sancho; “but that was when I was a boy;

[094-098]afterwards when I was something more of a man it was geese I kept, not

[094-099]pigs. But to my thinking that has nothing to do with it; for all who

[094-100]are governors don’t come of a kingly stock.”

[094-101]“True,” said Don Quixote, “and for that reason those who are not of

[094-102]noble origin should take care that the dignity of the office they hold

[094-103]be accompanied by a gentle suavity, which wisely managed will save them

[094-104]from the sneers of malice that no station escapes.

[094-105]“Glory in thy humble birth, Sancho, and be not ashamed of saying thou

[094-106]art peasant-born; for when it is seen thou art not ashamed no one will

[094-107]set himself to put thee to the blush; and pride thyself rather upon

[094-108]being one of lowly virtue than a lofty sinner. Countless are they who,

[094-109]born of mean parentage, have risen to the highest dignities, pontifical

[094-110]and imperial, and of the truth of this I could give thee instances

[094-111]enough to weary thee.

[094-112]“Remember, Sancho, if thou make virtue thy aim, and take a pride in

[094-113]doing virtuous actions, thou wilt have no cause to envy those who have

[094-114]princely and lordly ones, for blood is an inheritance, but virtue an

[094-115]acquisition, and virtue has in itself alone a worth that blood does not

[094-116]possess.

[094-117]“This being so, if perchance anyone of thy kinsfolk should come to see

[094-118]thee when thou art in thine island, thou art not to repel or slight

[094-119]him, but on the contrary to welcome him, entertain him, and make much

[094-120]of him; for in so doing thou wilt be approved of heaven (which is not

[094-121]pleased that any should despise what it hath made), and wilt comply

[094-122]with the laws of well-ordered nature.

[094-123]“If thou carriest thy wife with thee (and it is not well for those that

[094-124]administer governments to be long without their wives), teach and

[094-125]instruct her, and strive to smooth down her natural roughness; for all

[094-126]that may be gained by a wise governor may be lost and wasted by a

[094-127]boorish stupid wife.

[094-128]“If perchance thou art left a widower—a thing which may happen—and in

[094-129]virtue of thy office seekest a consort of higher degree, choose not one

[094-130]to serve thee for a hook, or for a fishing-rod, or for the hood of thy

[094-131]‘won’t have it;’ for verily, I tell thee, for all the judge’s wife

[094-132]receives, the husband will be held accountable at the general calling

[094-133]to account; where he will have repay in death fourfold, items that in

[094-134]life he regarded as naught.

[094-135]“Never go by arbitrary law, which is so much favoured by ignorant men

[094-136]who plume themselves on cleverness.

[094-137]“Let the tears of the poor man find with thee more compassion, but not

[094-138]more justice, than the pleadings of the rich.

[094-139]“Strive to lay bare the truth, as well amid the promises and presents

[094-140]of the rich man, as amid the sobs and entreaties of the poor.

[094-141]“When equity may and should be brought into play, press not the utmost

[094-142]rigour of the law against the guilty; for the reputation of the stern

[094-143]judge stands not higher than that of the compassionate.

[094-144]“If perchance thou permittest the staff of justice to swerve, let it be

[094-145]not by the weight of a gift, but by that of mercy.

[094-146]“If it should happen to thee to give judgment in the cause of one who

[094-147]is thine enemy, turn thy thoughts away from thy injury and fix them on

[094-148]the justice of the case.

[094-149]“Let not thine own passion blind thee in another man’s cause; for the

[094-150]errors thou wilt thus commit will be most frequently irremediable; or

[094-151]if not, only to be remedied at the expense of thy good name and even of

[094-152]thy fortune.

[094-153]“If any handsome woman come to seek justice of thee, turn away thine

[094-154]eyes from her tears and thine ears from her lamentations, and consider

[094-155]deliberately the merits of her demand, if thou wouldst not have thy

[094-156]reason swept away by her weeping, and thy rectitude by her sighs.

[094-157]“Abuse not by word him whom thou hast to punish in deed, for the pain

[094-158]of punishment is enough for the unfortunate without the addition of

[094-159]thine objurgations.

[094-160]“Bear in mind that the culprit who comes under thy jurisdiction is but

[094-161]a miserable man subject to all the propensities of our depraved nature,

[094-162]and so far as may be in thy power show thyself lenient and forbearing;

[094-163]for though the attributes of God are all equal, to our eyes that of

[094-164]mercy is brighter and loftier than that of justice.

[094-165]“If thou followest these precepts and rules, Sancho, thy days will be

[094-166]long, thy fame eternal, thy reward abundant, thy felicity unutterable;

[094-167]thou wilt marry thy children as thou wouldst; they and thy

[094-168]grandchildren will bear titles; thou wilt live in peace and concord

[094-169]with all men; and, when life draws to a close, death will come to thee

[094-170]in calm and ripe old age, and the light and loving hands of thy

[094-171]great-grandchildren will close thine eyes.

[094-172]“What I have thus far addressed to thee are instructions for the

[094-173]adornment of thy mind; listen now to those which tend to that of the

[094-174]body.”
