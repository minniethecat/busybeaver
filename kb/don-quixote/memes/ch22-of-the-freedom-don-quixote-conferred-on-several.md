OF THE FREEDOM DON QUIXOTE CONFERRED ON SEVERAL UNFORTUNATES WHO

AGAINST THEIR WILL WERE BEING CARRIED WHERE THEY HAD NO WISH TO GO

c22a.jpg (178K)


[022-001]Cid Hamete Benengeli, the Arab and Manchegan author, relates in this

[022-002]most grave, high-sounding, minute, delightful, and original history

[022-003]that after the discussion between the famous Don Quixote of La Mancha

[022-004]and his squire Sancho Panza which is set down at the end of chapter

[022-005]twenty-one, Don Quixote raised his eyes and saw coming along the road

[022-006]he was following some dozen men on foot strung together by the neck,

[022-007]like beads, on a great iron chain, and all with manacles on their

[022-008]hands. With them there came also two men on horseback and two on foot;

[022-009]those on horseback with wheel-lock muskets, those on foot with javelins

[022-010]and swords, and as soon as Sancho saw them he said:

[022-011]“That is a chain of galley slaves, on the way to the galleys by force

[022-012]of the king’s orders.”

[022-013]“How by force?” asked Don Quixote; “is it possible that the king uses

[022-014]force against anyone?”

[022-015]“I do not say that,” answered Sancho, “but that these are people

[022-016]condemned for their crimes to serve by force in the king’s galleys.”

[022-017]“In fact,” replied Don Quixote, “however it may be, these people are

[022-018]going where they are taking them by force, and not of their own will.”

[022-019]“Just so,” said Sancho.

[022-020]“Then if so,” said Don Quixote, “here is a case for the exercise of my

[022-021]office, to put down force and to succour and help the wretched.”

[022-022]“Recollect, your worship,” said Sancho, “Justice, which is the king

[022-023]himself, is not using force or doing wrong to such persons, but

[022-024]punishing them for their crimes.”

[022-025]The chain of galley slaves had by this time come up, and Don Quixote in

[022-026]very courteous language asked those who were in custody of it to be

[022-027]good enough to tell him the reason or reasons for which they were

[022-028]conducting these people in this manner. One of the guards on horseback

[022-029]answered that they were galley slaves belonging to his majesty, that

[022-030]they were going to the galleys, and that was all that was to be said

[022-031]and all he had any business to know.

c22b.jpg (298K)


[022-032]“Nevertheless,” replied Don Quixote, “I should like to know from each

[022-033]of them separately the reason of his misfortune;” to this he added more

[022-034]to the same effect to induce them to tell him what he wanted so civilly

[022-035]that the other mounted guard said to him:

[022-036]“Though we have here the register and certificate of the sentence of

[022-037]every one of these wretches, this is no time to take them out or read

[022-038]them; come and ask themselves; they can tell if they choose, and they

[022-039]will, for these fellows take a pleasure in doing and talking about

[022-040]rascalities.”

[022-041]With this permission, which Don Quixote would have taken even had they

[022-042]not granted it, he approached the chain and asked the first for what

[022-043]offences he was now in such a sorry case.

[022-044]He made answer that it was for being a lover.

[022-045]“For that only?” replied Don Quixote; “why, if for being lovers they

[022-046]send people to the galleys I might have been rowing in them long ago.”

[022-047]“The love is not the sort your worship is thinking of,” said the galley

[022-048]slave; “mine was that I loved a washerwoman’s basket of clean linen so

[022-049]well, and held it so close in my embrace, that if the arm of the law

[022-050]had not forced it from me, I should never have let it go of my own will

[022-051]to this moment; I was caught in the act, there was no occasion for

[022-052]torture, the case was settled, they treated me to a hundred lashes on

[022-053]the back, and three years of gurapas besides, and that was the end of

[022-054]it.”

[022-055]“What are gurapas?” asked Don Quixote.

[022-056]“Gurapas are galleys,” answered the galley slave, who was a young man

[022-057]of about four-and-twenty, and said he was a native of Piedrahita.

[022-058]Don Quixote asked the same question of the second, who made no reply,

[022-059]so downcast and melancholy was he; but the first answered for him, and

[022-060]said, “He, sir, goes as a canary, I mean as a musician and a singer.”

[022-061]“What!” said Don Quixote, “for being musicians and singers are people

[022-062]sent to the galleys too?”

[022-063]“Yes, sir,” answered the galley slave, “for there is nothing worse than

[022-064]singing under suffering.”

[022-065]“On the contrary, I have heard say,” said Don Quixote, “that he who

[022-066]sings scares away his woes.”

[022-067]“Here it is the reverse,” said the galley slave; “for he who sings once

[022-068]weeps all his life.”

[022-069]“I do not understand it,” said Don Quixote; but one of the guards said

[022-070]to him, “Sir, to sing under suffering means with the _non sancta_

[022-071]fraternity to confess under torture; they put this sinner to the

[022-072]torture and he confessed his crime, which was being a cuatrero, that is

[022-073]a cattle-stealer, and on his confession they sentenced him to six years

[022-074]in the galleys, besides two hundred lashes that he has already had on

[022-075]the back; and he is always dejected and downcast because the other

[022-076]thieves that were left behind and that march here ill-treat, and snub,

[022-077]and jeer, and despise him for confessing and not having spirit enough

[022-078]to say nay; for, say they, ‘nay’ has no more letters in it than ‘yea,’

[022-079]and a culprit is well off when life or death with him depends on his

[022-080]own tongue and not on that of witnesses or evidence; and to my thinking

[022-081]they are not very far out.”

[022-082]“And I think so too,” answered Don Quixote; then passing on to the

[022-083]third he asked him what he had asked the others, and the man answered

[022-084]very readily and unconcernedly, “I am going for five years to their

[022-085]ladyships the gurapas for the want of ten ducats.”

[022-086]“I will give twenty with pleasure to get you out of that trouble,” said

[022-087]Don Quixote.

[022-088]“That,” said the galley slave, “is like a man having money at sea when

[022-089]he is dying of hunger and has no way of buying what he wants; I say so

[022-090]because if at the right time I had had those twenty ducats that your

[022-091]worship now offers me, I would have greased the notary’s pen and

[022-092]freshened up the attorney’s wit with them, so that to-day I should be

[022-093]in the middle of the plaza of the Zocodover at Toledo, and not on this

[022-094]road coupled like a greyhound. But God is great; patience—there, that’s

[022-095]enough of it.”

[022-096]Don Quixote passed on to the fourth, a man of venerable aspect with a

[022-097]white beard falling below his breast, who on hearing himself asked the

[022-098]reason of his being there began to weep without answering a word, but

[022-099]the fifth acted as his tongue and said, “This worthy man is going to

[022-100]the galleys for four years, after having gone the rounds in ceremony

[022-101]and on horseback.”

[022-102]“That means,” said Sancho Panza, “as I take it, to have been exposed to

[022-103]shame in public.”

[022-104]“Just so,” replied the galley slave, “and the offence for which they

[022-105]gave him that punishment was having been an ear-broker, nay

[022-106]body-broker; I mean, in short, that this gentleman goes as a pimp, and

[022-107]for having besides a certain touch of the sorcerer about him.”

[022-108]“If that touch had not been thrown in,” said Don Quixote, “he would not

[022-109]deserve, for mere pimping, to row in the galleys, but rather to command

[022-110]and be admiral of them; for the office of pimp is no ordinary one,

[022-111]being the office of persons of discretion, one very necessary in a

[022-112]well-ordered state, and only to be exercised by persons of good birth;

[022-113]nay, there ought to be an inspector and overseer of them, as in other

[022-114]offices, and recognised number, as with the brokers on change; in this

[022-115]way many of the evils would be avoided which are caused by this office

[022-116]and calling being in the hands of stupid and ignorant people, such as

[022-117]women more or less silly, and pages and jesters of little standing and

[022-118]experience, who on the most urgent occasions, and when ingenuity of

[022-119]contrivance is needed, let the crumbs freeze on the way to their

[022-120]mouths, and know not which is their right hand. I should like to go

[022-121]farther, and give reasons to show that it is advisable to choose those

[022-122]who are to hold so necessary an office in the state, but this is not

[022-123]the fit place for it; some day I will expound the matter to someone

[022-124]able to see to and rectify it; all I say now is, that the additional

[022-125]fact of his being a sorcerer has removed the sorrow it gave me to see

[022-126]these white hairs and this venerable countenance in so painful a

[022-127]position on account of his being a pimp; though I know well there are

[022-128]no sorceries in the world that can move or compel the will as some

[022-129]simple folk fancy, for our will is free, nor is there herb or charm

[022-130]that can force it. All that certain silly women and quacks do is to

[022-131]turn men mad with potions and poisons, pretending that they have power

[022-132]to cause love, for, as I say, it is an impossibility to compel the

[022-133]will.”

[022-134]“It is true,” said the good old man, “and indeed, sir, as far as the

[022-135]charge of sorcery goes I was not guilty; as to that of being a pimp I

[022-136]cannot deny it; but I never thought I was doing any harm by it, for my

[022-137]only object was that all the world should enjoy itself and live in

[022-138]peace and quiet, without quarrels or troubles; but my good intentions

[022-139]were unavailing to save me from going where I never expect to come back

[022-140]from, with this weight of years upon me and a urinary ailment that

[022-141]never gives me a moment’s ease;” and again he fell to weeping as

[022-142]before, and such compassion did Sancho feel for him that he took out a

[022-143]real of four from his bosom and gave it to him in alms.

[022-144]Don Quixote went on and asked another what his crime was, and the man

[022-145]answered with no less but rather much more sprightliness than the last

[022-146]one.

[022-147]“I am here because I carried the joke too far with a couple of cousins

[022-148]of mine, and with a couple of other cousins who were none of mine; in

[022-149]short, I carried the joke so far with them all that it ended in such a

[022-150]complicated increase of kindred that no accountant could make it clear:

[022-151]it was all proved against me, I got no favour, I had no money, I was

[022-152]near having my neck stretched, they sentenced me to the galleys for six

[022-153]years, I accepted my fate, it is the punishment of my fault; I am a

[022-154]young man; let life only last, and with that all will come right. If

[022-155]you, sir, have anything wherewith to help the poor, God will repay it

[022-156]to you in heaven, and we on earth will take care in our petitions to

[022-157]him to pray for the life and health of your worship, that they may be

[022-158]as long and as good as your amiable appearance deserves.”

[022-159]This one was in the dress of a student, and one of the guards said he

[022-160]was a great talker and a very elegant Latin scholar.

[022-161]Behind all these there came a man of thirty, a very personable fellow,

[022-162]except that when he looked, his eyes turned in a little one towards the

[022-163]other. He was bound differently from the rest, for he had to his leg a

[022-164]chain so long that it was wound all round his body, and two rings on

[022-165]his neck, one attached to the chain, the other to what they call a

[022-166]“keep-friend” or “friend’s foot,” from which hung two irons reaching to

[022-167]his waist with two manacles fixed to them in which his hands were

[022-168]secured by a big padlock, so that he could neither raise his hands to

[022-169]his mouth nor lower his head to his hands. Don Quixote asked why this

[022-170]man carried so many more chains than the others. The guard replied that

[022-171]it was because he alone had committed more crimes than all the rest put

[022-172]together, and was so daring and such a villain, that though they

[022-173]marched him in that fashion they did not feel sure of him, but were in

[022-174]dread of his making his escape.

[022-175]“What crimes can he have committed,” said Don Quixote, “if they have

[022-176]not deserved a heavier punishment than being sent to the galleys?”

[022-177]“He goes for ten years,” replied the guard, “which is the same thing as

[022-178]civil death, and all that need be said is that this good fellow is the

[022-179]famous Gines de Pasamonte, otherwise called Ginesillo de Parapilla.”

[022-180]“Gently, señor commissary,” said the galley slave at this, “let us have

[022-181]no fixing of names or surnames; my name is Gines, not Ginesillo, and my

[022-182]family name is Pasamonte, not Parapilla as you say; let each one mind

[022-183]his own business, and he will be doing enough.”

[022-184]“Speak with less impertinence, master thief of extra measure,” replied

[022-185]the commissary, “if you don’t want me to make you hold your tongue in

[022-186]spite of your teeth.”

[022-187]“It is easy to see,” returned the galley slave, “that man goes as God

[022-188]pleases, but someone shall know some day whether I am called Ginesillo

[022-189]de Parapilla or not.”

[022-190]“Don’t they call you so, you liar?” said the guard.

[022-191]“They do,” returned Gines, “but I will make them give over calling me

[022-192]so, or I will be shaved, where, I only say behind my teeth. If you,

[022-193]sir, have anything to give us, give it to us at once, and God speed

[022-194]you, for you are becoming tiresome with all this inquisitiveness about

[022-195]the lives of others; if you want to know about mine, let me tell you I

[022-196]am Gines de Pasamonte, whose life is written by these fingers.”

[022-197]“He says true,” said the commissary, “for he has himself written his

[022-198]story as grand as you please, and has left the book in the prison in

[022-199]pawn for two hundred reals.”

[022-200]“And I mean to take it out of pawn,” said Gines, “though it were in for

[022-201]two hundred ducats.”

[022-202]“Is it so good?” said Don Quixote.

[022-203]“So good is it,” replied Gines, “that a fig for ‘Lazarillo de Tormes,’

[022-204]and all of that kind that have been written, or shall be written

[022-205]compared with it: all I will say about it is that it deals with facts,

[022-206]and facts so neat and diverting that no lies could match them.”

[022-207]“And how is the book entitled?” asked Don Quixote.

[022-208]“The ‘Life of Gines de Pasamonte,’” replied the subject of it.

[022-209]“And is it finished?” asked Don Quixote.

[022-210]“How can it be finished,” said the other, “when my life is not yet

[022-211]finished? All that is written is from my birth down to the point when

[022-212]they sent me to the galleys this last time.”

[022-213]“Then you have been there before?” said Don Quixote.

[022-214]“In the service of God and the king I have been there for four years

[022-215]before now, and I know by this time what the biscuit and courbash are

[022-216]like,” replied Gines; “and it is no great grievance to me to go back to

[022-217]them, for there I shall have time to finish my book; I have still many

[022-218]things left to say, and in the galleys of Spain there is more than

[022-219]enough leisure; though I do not want much for what I have to write, for

[022-220]I have it by heart.”

[022-221]“You seem a clever fellow,” said Don Quixote.

[022-222]“And an unfortunate one,” replied Gines, “for misfortune always

[022-223]persecutes good wit.”

[022-224]“It persecutes rogues,” said the commissary.

[022-225]“I told you already to go gently, master commissary,” said Pasamonte;

[022-226]“their lordships yonder never gave you that staff to ill-treat us

[022-227]wretches here, but to conduct and take us where his majesty orders you;

[022-228]if not, by the life of—never mind—; it may be that some day the stains

[022-229]made in the inn will come out in the scouring; let everyone hold his

[022-230]tongue and behave well and speak better; and now let us march on, for

[022-231]we have had quite enough of this entertainment.”

[022-232]The commissary lifted his staff to strike Pasamonte in return for his

[022-233]threats, but Don Quixote came between them, and begged him not to

[022-234]ill-use him, as it was not too much to allow one who had his hands tied

[022-235]to have his tongue a trifle free; and turning to the whole chain of

[022-236]them he said:

[022-237]“From all you have told me, dear brethren, I make out clearly that

[022-238]though they have punished you for your faults, the punishments you are

[022-239]about to endure do not give you much pleasure, and that you go to them

[022-240]very much against the grain and against your will, and that perhaps

[022-241]this one’s want of courage under torture, that one’s want of money, the

[022-242]other’s want of advocacy, and lastly the perverted judgment of the

[022-243]judge may have been the cause of your ruin and of your failure to

[022-244]obtain the justice you had on your side. All which presents itself now

[022-245]to my mind, urging, persuading, and even compelling me to demonstrate

[022-246]in your case the purpose for which Heaven sent me into the world and

[022-247]caused me to make profession of the order of chivalry to which I

[022-248]belong, and the vow I took therein to give aid to those in need and

[022-249]under the oppression of the strong. But as I know that it is a mark of

[022-250]prudence not to do by foul means what may be done by fair, I will ask

[022-251]these gentlemen, the guards and commissary, to be so good as to release

[022-252]you and let you go in peace, as there will be no lack of others to

[022-253]serve the king under more favourable circumstances; for it seems to me

[022-254]a hard case to make slaves of those whom God and nature have made free.

[022-255]Moreover, sirs of the guard,” added Don Quixote, “these poor fellows

[022-256]have done nothing to you; let each answer for his own sins yonder;

[022-257]there is a God in Heaven who will not forget to punish the wicked or

[022-258]reward the good; and it is not fitting that honest men should be the

[022-259]instruments of punishment to others, they being therein no way

[022-260]concerned. This request I make thus gently and quietly, that, if you

[022-261]comply with it, I may have reason for thanking you; and, if you will

[022-262]not voluntarily, this lance and sword together with the might of my arm

[022-263]shall compel you to comply with it by force.”

[022-264]“Nice nonsense!” said the commissary; “a fine piece of pleasantry he

[022-265]has come out with at last! He wants us to let the king’s prisoners go,

[022-266]as if we had any authority to release them, or he to order us to do so!

[022-267]Go your way, sir, and good luck to you; put that basin straight that

[022-268]you’ve got on your head, and don’t go looking for three feet on a cat.”

[022-269]“’Tis you that are the cat, rat, and rascal,” replied Don Quixote, and

[022-270]acting on the word he fell upon him so suddenly that without giving him

[022-271]time to defend himself he brought him to the ground sorely wounded with

[022-272]a lance-thrust; and lucky it was for him that it was the one that had

[022-273]the musket. The other guards stood thunderstruck and amazed at this

[022-274]unexpected event, but recovering presence of mind, those on horseback

[022-275]seized their swords, and those on foot their javelins, and attacked Don

[022-276]Quixote, who was waiting for them with great calmness; and no doubt it

[022-277]would have gone badly with him if the galley slaves, seeing the chance

[022-278]before them of liberating themselves, had not effected it by contriving

[022-279]to break the chain on which they were strung. Such was the confusion,

[022-280]that the guards, now rushing at the galley slaves who were breaking

[022-281]loose, now to attack Don Quixote who was waiting for them, did nothing

[022-282]at all that was of any use. Sancho, on his part, gave a helping hand to

[022-283]release Gines de Pasamonte, who was the first to leap forth upon the

[022-284]plain free and unfettered, and who, attacking the prostrate commissary,

[022-285]took from him his sword and the musket, with which, aiming at one and

[022-286]levelling at another, he, without ever discharging it, drove every one

[022-287]of the guards off the field, for they took to flight, as well to escape

[022-288]Pasamonte’s musket, as the showers of stones the now released galley

[022-289]slaves were raining upon them. Sancho was greatly grieved at the

[022-290]affair, because he anticipated that those who had fled would report the

[022-291]matter to the Holy Brotherhood, who at the summons of the alarm-bell

[022-292]would at once sally forth in quest of the offenders; and he said so to

[022-293]his master, and entreated him to leave the place at once, and go into

[022-294]hiding in the sierra that was close by.

[022-295]“That is all very well,” said Don Quixote, “but I know what must be

[022-296]done now;” and calling together all the galley slaves, who were now

[022-297]running riot, and had stripped the commissary to the skin, he collected

[022-298]them round him to hear what he had to say, and addressed them as

[022-299]follows: “To be grateful for benefits received is the part of persons

[022-300]of good birth, and one of the sins most offensive to God is

[022-301]ingratitude; I say so because, sirs, ye have already seen by manifest

[022-302]proof the benefit ye have received of me; in return for which I desire,

[022-303]and it is my good pleasure that, laden with that chain which I have

[022-304]taken off your necks, ye at once set out and proceed to the city of El

[022-305]Toboso, and there present yourselves before the lady Dulcinea del

[022-306]Toboso, and say to her that her knight, he of the Rueful Countenance,

[022-307]sends to commend himself to her; and that ye recount to her in full

[022-308]detail all the particulars of this notable adventure, up to the

[022-309]recovery of your longed-for liberty; and this done ye may go where ye

[022-310]will, and good fortune attend you.”

[022-311]Gines de Pasamonte made answer for all, saying, “That which you, sir,

[022-312]our deliverer, demand of us, is of all impossibilities the most

[022-313]impossible to comply with, because we cannot go together along the

[022-314]roads, but only singly and separate, and each one his own way,

[022-315]endeavouring to hide ourselves in the bowels of the earth to escape the

[022-316]Holy Brotherhood, which, no doubt, will come out in search of us. What

[022-317]your worship may do, and fairly do, is to change this service and

[022-318]tribute as regards the lady Dulcinea del Toboso for a certain quantity

[022-319]of ave-marias and credos which we will say for your worship’s

[022-320]intention, and this is a condition that can be complied with by night

[022-321]as by day, running or resting, in peace or in war; but to imagine that

[022-322]we are going now to return to the flesh-pots of Egypt, I mean to take

[022-323]up our chain and set out for El Toboso, is to imagine that it is now

[022-324]night, though it is not yet ten in the morning, and to ask this of us

[022-325]is like asking pears of the elm tree.”

[022-326]“Then by all that’s good,” said Don Quixote (now stirred to wrath),

[022-327]“Don son of a bitch, Don Ginesillo de Paropillo, or whatever your name

[022-328]is, you will have to go yourself alone, with your tail between your

[022-329]legs and the whole chain on your back.”

[022-330]Pasamonte, who was anything but meek (being by this time thoroughly

[022-331]convinced that Don Quixote was not quite right in his head as he had

[022-332]committed such a vagary as to set them free), finding himself abused in

[022-333]this fashion, gave the wink to his companions, and falling back they

[022-334]began to shower stones on Don Quixote at such a rate that he was quite

[022-335]unable to protect himself with his buckler, and poor Rocinante no more

[022-336]heeded the spur than if he had been made of brass. Sancho planted

[022-337]himself behind his ass, and with him sheltered himself from the

[022-338]hailstorm that poured on both of them. Don Quixote was unable to shield

[022-339]himself so well but that more pebbles than I could count struck him

[022-340]full on the body with such force that they brought him to the ground;

[022-341]and the instant he fell the student pounced upon him, snatched the

[022-342]basin from his head, and with it struck three or four blows on his

[022-343]shoulders, and as many more on the ground, knocking it almost to

[022-344]pieces. They then stripped him of a jacket that he wore over his

[022-345]armour, and they would have stripped off his stockings if his greaves

[022-346]had not prevented them. From Sancho they took his coat, leaving him in

[022-347]his shirt-sleeves; and dividing among themselves the remaining spoils

[022-348]of the battle, they went each one his own way, more solicitous about

[022-349]keeping clear of the Holy Brotherhood they dreaded, than about

[022-350]burdening themselves with the chain, or going to present themselves
