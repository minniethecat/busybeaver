WHICH TREATS OF THE STRANGE THINGS THAT HAPPENED TO THE STOUT KNIGHT OF

[025-001]LA MANCHA IN THE SIERRA MORENA, AND OF HIS IMITATION OF THE PENANCE OF

BELTENEBROS

c25a.jpg (168K)


[025-002]Don Quixote took leave of the goatherd, and once more mounting

[025-003]Rocinante bade Sancho follow him, which he having no ass, did very

[025-004]discontentedly. They proceeded slowly, making their way into the most

[025-005]rugged part of the mountain, Sancho all the while dying to have a talk

[025-006]with his master, and longing for him to begin, so that there should be

[025-007]no breach of the injunction laid upon him; but unable to keep silence

[025-008]so long he said to him:

[025-009]“Señor Don Quixote, give me your worship’s blessing and dismissal, for

[025-010]I’d like to go home at once to my wife and children with whom I can at

[025-011]any rate talk and converse as much as I like; for to want me to go

[025-012]through these solitudes day and night and not speak to you when I have

[025-013]a mind is burying me alive. If luck would have it that animals spoke as

[025-014]they did in the days of Guisopete, it would not be so bad, because I

[025-015]could talk to Rocinante about whatever came into my head, and so put up

[025-016]with my ill-fortune; but it is a hard case, and not to be borne with

[025-017]patience, to go seeking adventures all one’s life and get nothing but

[025-018]kicks and blanketings, brickbats and punches, and with all this to have

[025-019]to sew up one’s mouth without daring to say what is in one’s heart,

[025-020]just as if one were dumb.”

[025-021]“I understand thee, Sancho,” replied Don Quixote; “thou art dying to

[025-022]have the interdict I placed upon thy tongue removed; consider it

[025-023]removed, and say what thou wilt while we are wandering in these

[025-024]mountains.”

[025-025]“So be it,” said Sancho; “let me speak now, for God knows what will

[025-026]happen by-and-by; and to take advantage of the permit at once, I ask,

[025-027]what made your worship stand up so for that Queen Majimasa, or whatever

[025-028]her name is, or what did it matter whether that abbot was a friend of

[025-029]hers or not? for if your worship had let that pass—and you were not a

[025-030]judge in the matter—it is my belief the madman would have gone on with

[025-031]his story, and the blow of the stone, and the kicks, and more than half

[025-032]a dozen cuffs would have been escaped.”

[025-033]“In faith, Sancho,” answered Don Quixote, “if thou knewest as I do what

[025-034]an honourable and illustrious lady Queen Madasima was, I know thou

[025-035]wouldst say I had great patience that I did not break in pieces the

[025-036]mouth that uttered such blasphemies, for a very great blasphemy it is

[025-037]to say or imagine that a queen has made free with a surgeon. The truth

[025-038]of the story is that that Master Elisabad whom the madman mentioned was

[025-039]a man of great prudence and sound judgment, and served as governor and

[025-040]physician to the queen, but to suppose that she was his mistress is

[025-041]nonsense deserving very severe punishment; and as a proof that Cardenio

[025-042]did not know what he was saying, remember when he said it he was out of

[025-043]his wits.”

[025-044]“That is what I say,” said Sancho; “there was no occasion for minding

[025-045]the words of a madman; for if good luck had not helped your worship,

[025-046]and he had sent that stone at your head instead of at your breast, a

[025-047]fine way we should have been in for standing up for my lady yonder, God

[025-048]confound her! And then, would not Cardenio have gone free as a madman?”

[025-049]“Against men in their senses or against madmen,” said Don Quixote,

[025-050]“every knight-errant is bound to stand up for the honour of women,

[025-051]whoever they may be, much more for queens of such high degree and

[025-052]dignity as Queen Madasima, for whom I have a particular regard on

[025-053]account of her amiable qualities; for, besides being extremely

[025-054]beautiful, she was very wise, and very patient under her misfortunes,

[025-055]of which she had many; and the counsel and society of the Master

[025-056]Elisabad were a great help and support to her in enduring her

[025-057]afflictions with wisdom and resignation; hence the ignorant and

[025-058]ill-disposed vulgar took occasion to say and think that she was his

[025-059]mistress; and they lie, I say it once more, and will lie two hundred

[025-060]times more, all who think and say so.”

[025-061]“I neither say nor think so,” said Sancho; “let them look to it; with

[025-062]their bread let them eat it; they have rendered account to God whether

[025-063]they misbehaved or not; I come from my vineyard, I know nothing; I am

[025-064]not fond of prying into other men’s lives; he who buys and lies feels

[025-065]it in his purse; moreover, naked was I born, naked I find myself, I

[025-066]neither lose nor gain; but if they did, what is that to me? many think

[025-067]there are flitches where there are no hooks; but who can put gates to

[025-068]the open plain? moreover they said of God—”

[025-069]“God bless me,” said Don Quixote, “what a set of absurdities thou art

[025-070]stringing together! What has what we are talking about got to do with

[025-071]the proverbs thou art threading one after the other? for God’s sake

[025-072]hold thy tongue, Sancho, and henceforward keep to prodding thy ass and

[025-073]don’t meddle in what does not concern thee; and understand with all thy

[025-074]five senses that everything I have done, am doing, or shall do, is well

[025-075]founded on reason and in conformity with the rules of chivalry, for I

[025-076]understand them better than all the world that profess them.”

[025-077]“Señor,” replied Sancho, “is it a good rule of chivalry that we should

[025-078]go astray through these mountains without path or road, looking for a

[025-079]madman who when he is found will perhaps take a fancy to finish what he

[025-080]began, not his story, but your worship’s head and my ribs, and end by

[025-081]breaking them altogether for us?”

c25b.jpg (330K)


[025-082]“Peace, I say again, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “for let me tell thee

[025-083]it is not so much the desire of finding that madman that leads me into

[025-084]these regions as that which I have of performing among them an

[025-085]achievement wherewith I shall win eternal name and fame throughout the

[025-086]known world; and it shall be such that I shall thereby set the seal on

[025-087]all that can make a knight-errant perfect and famous.”

[025-088]“And is it very perilous, this achievement?”

[025-089]“No,” replied he of the Rueful Countenance; “though it may be in the

[025-090]dice that we may throw deuce-ace instead of sixes; but all will depend

[025-091]on thy diligence.”

[025-092]“On my diligence!” said Sancho.

[025-093]“Yes,” said Don Quixote, “for if thou dost return soon from the place

[025-094]where I mean to send thee, my penance will be soon over, and my glory

[025-095]will soon begin. But as it is not right to keep thee any longer in

[025-096]suspense, waiting to see what comes of my words, I would have thee

[025-097]know, Sancho, that the famous Amadis of Gaul was one of the most

[025-098]perfect knights-errant—I am wrong to say he was one; he stood alone,

[025-099]the first, the only one, the lord of all that were in the world in his

[025-100]time. A fig for Don Belianis, and for all who say he equalled him in

[025-101]any respect, for, my oath upon it, they are deceiving themselves! I

[025-102]say, too, that when a painter desires to become famous in his art he

[025-103]endeavours to copy the originals of the rarest painters that he knows;

[025-104]and the same rule holds good for all the most important crafts and

[025-105]callings that serve to adorn a state; thus must he who would be

[025-106]esteemed prudent and patient imitate Ulysses, in whose person and

[025-107]labours Homer presents to us a lively picture of prudence and patience;

[025-108]as Virgil, too, shows us in the person of Æneas the virtue of a pious

[025-109]son and the sagacity of a brave and skilful captain; not representing

[025-110]or describing them as they were, but as they ought to be, so as to

[025-111]leave the example of their virtues to posterity. In the same way Amadis

[025-112]was the polestar, day-star, sun of valiant and devoted knights, whom

[025-113]all we who fight under the banner of love and chivalry are bound to

[025-114]imitate. This, then, being so, I consider, friend Sancho, that the

[025-115]knight-errant who shall imitate him most closely will come nearest to

[025-116]reaching the perfection of chivalry. Now one of the instances in which

[025-117]this knight most conspicuously showed his prudence, worth, valour,

[025-118]endurance, fortitude, and love, was when he withdrew, rejected by the

[025-119]Lady Oriana, to do penance upon the Peña Pobre, changing his name into

[025-120]that of Beltenebros, a name assuredly significant and appropriate to

[025-121]the life which he had voluntarily adopted. So, as it is easier for me

[025-122]to imitate him in this than in cleaving giants asunder, cutting off

[025-123]serpents’ heads, slaying dragons, routing armies, destroying fleets,

[025-124]and breaking enchantments, and as this place is so well suited for a

[025-125]similar purpose, I must not allow the opportunity to escape which now

[025-126]so conveniently offers me its forelock.”

[025-127]“What is it in reality,” said Sancho, “that your worship means to do in

[025-128]such an out-of-the-way place as this?”

[025-129]“Have I not told thee,” answered Don Quixote, “that I mean to imitate

[025-130]Amadis here, playing the victim of despair, the madman, the maniac, so

[025-131]as at the same time to imitate the valiant Don Roland, when at the

[025-132]fountain he had evidence of the fair Angelica having disgraced herself

[025-133]with Medoro and through grief thereat went mad, and plucked up trees,

[025-134]troubled the waters of the clear springs, slew shepherds, destroyed

[025-135]flocks, burned down huts, levelled houses, dragged mares after him, and

[025-136]perpetrated a hundred thousand other outrages worthy of everlasting

[025-137]renown and record? And though I have no intention of imitating Roland,

[025-138]or Orlando, or Rotolando (for he went by all these names), step by step

[025-139]in all the mad things he did, said, and thought, I will make a rough

[025-140]copy to the best of my power of all that seems to me most essential;

[025-141]but perhaps I shall content myself with the simple imitation of Amadis,

[025-142]who without giving way to any mischievous madness but merely to tears

[025-143]and sorrow, gained as much fame as the most famous.”

[025-144]“It seems to me,” said Sancho, “that the knights who behaved in this

[025-145]way had provocation and cause for those follies and penances; but what

[025-146]cause has your worship for going mad? What lady has rejected you, or

[025-147]what evidence have you found to prove that the lady Dulcinea del Toboso

[025-148]has been trifling with Moor or Christian?”

[025-149]“There is the point,” replied Don Quixote, “and that is the beauty of

[025-150]this business of mine; no thanks to a knight-errant for going mad when

[025-151]he has cause; the thing is to turn crazy without any provocation, and

[025-152]let my lady know, if I do this in the dry, what I would do in the

[025-153]moist; moreover I have abundant cause in the long separation I have

[025-154]endured from my lady till death, Dulcinea del Toboso; for as thou didst

[025-155]hear that shepherd Ambrosio say the other day, in absence all ills are

[025-156]felt and feared; and so, friend Sancho, waste no time in advising me

[025-157]against so rare, so happy, and so unheard-of an imitation; mad I am,

[025-158]and mad I must be until thou returnest with the answer to a letter that

[025-159]I mean to send by thee to my lady Dulcinea; and if it be such as my

[025-160]constancy deserves, my insanity and penance will come to an end; and if

[025-161]it be to the opposite effect, I shall become mad in earnest, and, being

[025-162]so, I shall suffer no more; thus in whatever way she may answer I shall

[025-163]escape from the struggle and affliction in which thou wilt leave me,

[025-164]enjoying in my senses the boon thou bearest me, or as a madman not

[025-165]feeling the evil thou bringest me. But tell me, Sancho, hast thou got

[025-166]Mambrino’s helmet safe? for I saw thee take it up from the ground when

[025-167]that ungrateful wretch tried to break it in pieces but could not, by

[025-168]which the fineness of its temper may be seen.”

[025-169]To which Sancho made answer, “By the living God, Sir Knight of the

[025-170]Rueful Countenance, I cannot endure or bear with patience some of the

[025-171]things that your worship says; and from them I begin to suspect that

[025-172]all you tell me about chivalry, and winning kingdoms and empires, and

[025-173]giving islands, and bestowing other rewards and dignities after the

[025-174]custom of knights-errant, must be all made up of wind and lies, and all

[025-175]pigments or figments, or whatever we may call them; for what would

[025-176]anyone think that heard your worship calling a barber’s basin

[025-177]Mambrino’s helmet without ever seeing the mistake all this time, but

[025-178]that one who says and maintains such things must have his brains

[025-179]addled? I have the basin in my sack all dinted, and I am taking it home

[025-180]to have it mended, to trim my beard in it, if, by God’s grace, I am

[025-181]allowed to see my wife and children some day or other.”

[025-182]“Look here, Sancho,” said Don Quixote, “by him thou didst swear by just

[025-183]now I swear thou hast the most limited understanding that any squire in

[025-184]the world has or ever had. Is it possible that all this time thou hast

[025-185]been going about with me thou hast never found out that all things

[025-186]belonging to knights-errant seem to be illusions and nonsense and

[025-187]ravings, and to go always by contraries? And not because it really is

[025-188]so, but because there is always a swarm of enchanters in attendance

[025-189]upon us that change and alter everything with us, and turn things as

[025-190]they please, and according as they are disposed to aid or destroy us;

[025-191]thus what seems to thee a barber’s basin seems to me Mambrino’s helmet,

[025-192]and to another it will seem something else; and rare foresight it was

[025-193]in the sage who is on my side to make what is really and truly

[025-194]Mambrino’s helmet seem a basin to everybody, for, being held in such

[025-195]estimation as it is, all the world would pursue me to rob me of it; but

[025-196]when they see it is only a barber’s basin they do not take the trouble

[025-197]to obtain it; as was plainly shown by him who tried to break it, and

[025-198]left it on the ground without taking it, for, by my faith, had he known

[025-199]it he would never have left it behind. Keep it safe, my friend, for

[025-200]just now I have no need of it; indeed, I shall have to take off all

[025-201]this armour and remain as naked as I was born, if I have a mind to

[025-202]follow Roland rather than Amadis in my penance.”

[025-203]Thus talking they reached the foot of a high mountain which stood like

[025-204]an isolated peak among the others that surrounded it. Past its base

[025-205]there flowed a gentle brook, all around it spread a meadow so green and

[025-206]luxuriant that it was a delight to the eyes to look upon it, and forest

[025-207]trees in abundance, and shrubs and flowers, added to the charms of the

[025-208]spot. Upon this place the Knight of the Rueful Countenance fixed his

[025-209]choice for the performance of his penance, and as he beheld it

[025-210]exclaimed in a loud voice as though he were out of his senses:

[025-211]“This is the place, oh, ye heavens, that I select and choose for

[025-212]bewailing the misfortune in which ye yourselves have plunged me: this

[025-213]is the spot where the overflowings of mine eyes shall swell the waters

[025-214]of yon little brook, and my deep and endless sighs shall stir

[025-215]unceasingly the leaves of these mountain trees, in testimony and token

[025-216]of the pain my persecuted heart is suffering. Oh, ye rural deities,

[025-217]whoever ye be that haunt this lone spot, give ear to the complaint of a

[025-218]wretched lover whom long absence and brooding jealousy have driven to

[025-219]bewail his fate among these wilds and complain of the hard heart of

[025-220]that fair and ungrateful one, the end and limit of all human beauty!

[025-221]Oh, ye wood nymphs and dryads, that dwell in the thickets of the

[025-222]forest, so may the nimble wanton satyrs by whom ye are vainly wooed

[025-223]never disturb your sweet repose, help me to lament my hard fate or at

[025-224]least weary not at listening to it! Oh, Dulcinea del Toboso, day of my

[025-225]night, glory of my pain, guide of my path, star of my fortune, so may

[025-226]Heaven grant thee in full all thou seekest of it, bethink thee of the

[025-227]place and condition to which absence from thee has brought me, and make

[025-228]that return in kindness that is due to my fidelity! Oh, lonely trees,

[025-229]that from this day forward shall bear me company in my solitude, give

[025-230]me some sign by the gentle movement of your boughs that my presence is

[025-231]not distasteful to you! Oh, thou, my squire, pleasant companion in my

[025-232]prosperous and adverse fortunes, fix well in thy memory what thou shalt

[025-233]see me do here, so that thou mayest relate and report it to the sole

[025-234]cause of all,” and so saying he dismounted from Rocinante, and in an

[025-235]instant relieved him of saddle and bridle, and giving him a slap on the

[025-236]croup, said, “He gives thee freedom who is bereft of it himself, oh

[025-237]steed as excellent in deed as thou art unfortunate in thy lot; begone

[025-238]where thou wilt, for thou bearest written on thy forehead that neither

[025-239]Astolfo’s hippogriff, nor the famed Frontino that cost Bradamante so

[025-240]dear, could equal thee in speed.”

[025-241]Seeing this Sancho said, “Good luck to him who has saved us the trouble

[025-242]of stripping the pack-saddle off Dapple! By my faith he would not have

[025-243]gone without a slap on the croup and something said in his praise;

[025-244]though if he were here I would not let anyone strip him, for there

[025-245]would be no occasion, as he had nothing of the lover or victim of

[025-246]despair about him, inasmuch as his master, which I was while it was

[025-247]God’s pleasure, was nothing of the sort; and indeed, Sir Knight of the

[025-248]Rueful Countenance, if my departure and your worship’s madness are to

[025-249]come off in earnest, it will be as well to saddle Rocinante again in

[025-250]order that he may supply the want of Dapple, because it will save me

[025-251]time in going and returning: for if I go on foot I don’t know when I

[025-252]shall get there or when I shall get back, as I am, in truth, a bad

[025-253]walker.”

[025-254]“I declare, Sancho,” returned Don Quixote, “it shall be as thou wilt,

[025-255]for thy plan does not seem to me a bad one, and three days hence thou

[025-256]wilt depart, for I wish thee to observe in the meantime what I do and

[025-257]say for her sake, that thou mayest be able to tell it.”

[025-258]“But what more have I to see besides what I have seen?” said Sancho.

[025-259]“Much thou knowest about it!” said Don Quixote. “I have now got to tear

[025-260]up my garments, to scatter about my armour, knock my head against these

[025-261]rocks, and more of the same sort of thing, which thou must witness.”

[025-262]“For the love of God,” said Sancho, “be careful, your worship, how you

[025-263]give yourself those knocks on the head, for you may come across such a

[025-264]rock, and in such a way, that the very first may put an end to the

[025-265]whole contrivance of this penance; and I should think, if indeed knocks

[025-266]on the head seem necessary to you, and this business cannot be done

[025-267]without them, you might be content—as the whole thing is feigned, and

[025-268]counterfeit, and in joke—you might be content, I say, with giving them

[025-269]to yourself in the water, or against something soft, like cotton; and

[025-270]leave it all to me; for I’ll tell my lady that your worship knocked

[025-271]your head against a point of rock harder than a diamond.”

[025-272]“I thank thee for thy good intentions, friend Sancho,” answered Don

[025-273]Quixote, “but I would have thee know that all these things I am doing

[025-274]are not in joke, but very much in earnest, for anything else would be a

[025-275]transgression of the ordinances of chivalry, which forbid us to tell

[025-276]any lie whatever under the penalties due to apostasy; and to do one

[025-277]thing instead of another is just the same as lying; so my knocks on the

[025-278]head must be real, solid, and valid, without anything sophisticated or

[025-279]fanciful about them, and it will be needful to leave me some lint to

[025-280]dress my wounds, since fortune has compelled us to do without the

[025-281]balsam we lost.”

[025-282]“It was worse losing the ass,” replied Sancho, “for with him lint and

[025-283]all were lost; but I beg of your worship not to remind me again of that

[025-284]accursed liquor, for my soul, not to say my stomach, turns at hearing

[025-285]the very name of it; and I beg of you, too, to reckon as past the three

[025-286]days you allowed me for seeing the mad things you do, for I take them

[025-287]as seen already and pronounced upon, and I will tell wonderful stories

[025-288]to my lady; so write the letter and send me off at once, for I long to

[025-289]return and take your worship out of this purgatory where I am leaving

[025-290]you.”

[025-291]“Purgatory dost thou call it, Sancho?” said Don Quixote, “rather call

[025-292]it hell, or even worse if there be anything worse.”

[025-293]“For one who is in hell,” said Sancho, “_nulla est retentio_, as I have

[025-294]heard say.”

[025-295]“I do not understand what _retentio_ means,” said Don Quixote.

[025-296]“_Retentio_,” answered Sancho, “means that whoever is in hell never

[025-297]comes nor can come out of it, which will be the opposite case with your

[025-298]worship or my legs will be idle, that is if I have spurs to enliven

[025-299]Rocinante: let me once get to El Toboso and into the presence of my

[025-300]lady Dulcinea, and I will tell her such things of the follies and

[025-301]madnesses (for it is all one) that your worship has done and is still

[025-302]doing, that I will manage to make her softer than a glove though I find

[025-303]her harder than a cork tree; and with her sweet and honeyed answer I

[025-304]will come back through the air like a witch, and take your worship out

[025-305]of this purgatory that seems to be hell but is not, as there is hope of

[025-306]getting out of it; which, as I have said, those in hell have not, and I

[025-307]believe your worship will not say anything to the contrary.”

[025-308]“That is true,” said he of the Rueful Countenance, “but how shall we

[025-309]manage to write the letter?”

[025-310]“And the ass-colt order too,” added Sancho.

[025-311]“All shall be included,” said Don Quixote; “and as there is no paper,

[025-312]it would be well done to write it on the leaves of trees, as the

[025-313]ancients did, or on tablets of wax; though that would be as hard to

[025-314]find just now as paper. But it has just occurred to me how it may be

[025-315]conveniently and even more than conveniently written, and that is in

[025-316]the notebook that belonged to Cardenio, and thou wilt take care to have

[025-317]it copied on paper, in a good hand, at the first village thou comest to

[025-318]where there is a schoolmaster, or if not, any sacristan will copy it;

[025-319]but see thou give it not to any notary to copy, for they write a law

[025-320]hand that Satan could not make out.”

[025-321]“But what is to be done about the signature?” said Sancho.

[025-322]“The letters of Amadis were never signed,” said Don Quixote.

[025-323]“That is all very well,” said Sancho, “but the order must needs be

[025-324]signed, and if it is copied they will say the signature is false, and I

[025-325]shall be left without ass-colts.”

[025-326]“The order shall go signed in the same book,” said Don Quixote, “and on

[025-327]seeing it my niece will make no difficulty about obeying it; as to the

[025-328]loveletter thou canst put by way of signature, ‘_Yours till death, the

[025-329]Knight of the Rueful Countenance._’ And it will be no great matter if

[025-330]it is in some other person’s hand, for as well as I recollect Dulcinea

[025-331]can neither read nor write, nor in the whole course of her life has she

[025-332]seen handwriting or letter of mine, for my love and hers have been

[025-333]always platonic, not going beyond a modest look, and even that so

[025-334]seldom that I can safely swear I have not seen her four times in all

[025-335]these twelve years I have been loving her more than the light of these

[025-336]eyes that the earth will one day devour; and perhaps even of those four

[025-337]times she has not once perceived that I was looking at her: such is the

[025-338]retirement and seclusion in which her father Lorenzo Corchuelo and her

[025-339]mother Aldonza Nogales have brought her up.”

[025-340]“So, so!” said Sancho; “Lorenzo Corchuelo’s daughter is the lady

[025-341]Dulcinea del Toboso, otherwise called Aldonza Lorenzo?”
