# The Housekeeper

**The Housekeeper** (*la Ama*) is introduced in the novel's very first sentence as one of the fixtures of Alonso Quijano's household, alongside "a niece not yet twenty." A woman past forty at the start of the narrative, she represents the domestic order that Don Quixote's madness disrupts — and that proves powerless to restrain him.

## Character Profile

The Housekeeper is never named in the novel; she is known only by her role. This anonymity is significant: she is defined entirely by her function within Don Quixote's household. She is practical, superstitious, and fiercely devoted to her master's welfare — but entirely unequipped to understand or address the nature of his affliction.

Her voice is characterized by earthy imprecation and comic exasperation:

> "plague on it!" cried the housekeeper at this: "did not my heart tell me which foot my master limped on?"[005-123]

This combination of folk wisdom and frustrated affection makes her one of the novel's most vivid minor characters.

## The Library Scrutiny

The Housekeeper's most memorable contribution to the narrative is her enthusiastic participation in the burning of Don Quixote's library. In Chapter 6, as the [[the-priest|Priest]] and [[master-nicholas|the Barber]] examine the books one by one, the Housekeeper acts as their energetic agent of destruction:

> "The simplicity of the housekeeper made the licentiate laugh, and he directed the barber to hand the books to him one by one"[006-011]

When the curate decides a book should be burned, the Housekeeper seizes it with relish:

> "here, into the yard with them," said the housekeeper[006-054]

The scene is both comic and dark: an illiterate woman destroying books she cannot read, convinced that their physical elimination will cure her master's madness. Her literalism — burning the books she cannot understand — mirrors Don Quixote's own literalism in taking chivalric romances as fact.

## Domestic Powerlessness

Throughout both parts of the novel, the Housekeeper's attempts to control Don Quixote prove consistently futile. She pleads, weeps, scolds, and conspires — but the knight invariably escapes. In Part II, she and [[the-niece|the Niece]] attempt to block Don Quixote's third sally with increasingly desperate arguments, but the bachelor [[sanson-carrasco|Samson Carrasco]] — ostensibly their ally — is already planning to follow the knight in disguise.

> "Take care, Sancho," said Samson; "honours change manners, and it may be that when I find myself a governor I won't know the mother that bore me"[056-136]

The Housekeeper's domestic authority is real — she runs the household, controls the larder, manages the finances — but it cannot extend to the one thing that matters: keeping her master safe from himself.

## Relationship with the Niece

The Housekeeper and the Niece function as a comic duo, their shared anxiety about Don Quixote manifesting in overlapping exclamations of distress. They are often mentioned together — "the niece and housekeeper" — as if they form a single unit of domestic opposition to chivalric adventure. Their powerlessness is the novel's quiet commentary on the limits of women's authority in a patriarchal household: they can manage daily life but cannot prevent the patriarch from destroying himself.

## Thematic Significance

The Housekeeper embodies the mundane reality that Don Quixote's imagination seeks to transcend. She is concerned with food, clothing, rest, and safety — all the things the knight-errant proudly disregards. Her failure to keep him home is, in Cervantes's scheme, both comic and tragic: the forces of ordinary life are no match for the power of imagination, for better and for worse.
