# Broughton Hall

Lord Herndon's country estate — referred to in the novel through various properties including Woughton Hall in Kent and the "tall, bleak-faced house in Eaton Square" (021-003) — is the setting for Sam Dodsworth's most concentrated encounter with the English aristocracy. The weekend party at Herndon's country house crystallizes everything Sam finds admirable and alienating about the Old World.

## Description

Lewis presents the English country house through Sam's American eyes: a world of ancient portraits, effortless manners, quiet assumptions of superiority, and landscape that has been tended for centuries rather than developed for profit. The estate is large, beautiful, and indifferent — like the aristocracy it houses, it does not need to try. Its very composure is its power.

The house is contrasted implicitly with Sam's Zenith world: where the Revelation Company building announces its importance with plate glass and marble, Herndon's estate simply assumes it. The difference in registers — one shouting, one murmuring — is the difference between American and English civilization as Lewis renders it.

## Significance in the Novel

The country house weekend is a turning point in Sam's European education. He finds the experience "too rich for my blood" (010-001), recognizing both the genuine appeal of the aristocratic world and its fundamental incompatibility with his own nature. Unlike Fran, who is intoxicated by titled acquaintances, Sam perceives the limitations of a class he can never join and does not truly wish to.

Lord Herndon himself, as diagnostician of the Dodsworth marriage, plays a crucial role in the novel's middle section. His country house is the stage on which his observations are made and his quiet, devastating verdict delivered.

## Key Events

**The weekend party (Chapter X)**: Sam's immersion in English country house society — the novel's fullest rendering of the Old World order and Sam's relationship to it.

**Lord Herndon's diagnosis**: Herndon observes the Dodsworths at close range and draws conclusions that will shape the novel's subsequent action.

## Characters Associated

Lord Herndon is the presiding figure — host, observer, diagnostician. Sam Dodsworth is the American visitor, both impressed and alienated. Fran Dodsworth finds in the country house the European world she has been seeking. Lockert appears as an intermediary between the American and English worlds.
