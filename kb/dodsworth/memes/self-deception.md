# Self-Deception

Fran Dodsworth's most dangerous quality is not her ambition but her gift for self-deception: the ability to believe, with absolute sincerity, that whatever she wants is what she deserves, and that whatever she does is justified by her superior sensibility.

## Definition

Self-deception in *Dodsworth* is presented with clinical precision. Fran does not lie to others so much as she inhabits her own lies so completely that they become, for her, the truth. When she pursues Lockert, it is because he represents "culture" and "experience"; when she pursues Kurt von Obersdorf, it is because she has discovered "real love" at last. The novel exposes each of these rationalizations without ever suggesting that Fran is consciously dishonest. She believes what she says — and that is precisely the problem.

Lewis's insight is that self-deception is not a moral failing so much as a psychological mechanism: a way of managing the gap between who one is and who one wishes to be. Fran cannot accept herself as the daughter of a German-American brewer from Zenith, so she constructs a self who is naturally at home among European aristocrats. The construction is fragile — Major Herndon's observation that she is "so typically American" (009-009) punctures it instantly — but Fran's genius is for repairing the damage so quickly that she never has to face the truth.

## Context in the Novel

Fran's self-deception structures the novel's marital drama. Each stage of the European journey provides her with new material for self-justification. In London, she is pursuing "culture"; in Paris, she is pursuing "beauty"; in Berlin, she is pursuing "love." The objects change but the mechanism remains constant: whatever Fran wants at any given moment is elevated into a principle.

Sam, who lacks the gift of self-deception, is bewildered by Fran's transformations. He takes her at her word — and her word keeps changing. His eventual recognition that Fran cannot be trusted not because she lies but because she does not know she is lying is one of the novel's most painful insights.

## Key Passages

> "I do so want us to be happy together! And now that you don't have to think about business, don't you think it might be nice to get acquainted with me? I'm really quite a nice person!" (007-021)

The perfect Fran sentence: self-deprecating, charming, and utterly manipulative, all while believing every word.

> "you're too American, Mrs. Dodsworth. ... You have the most charming and childish misconceptions about yourself." (009-009)

The outside observer who sees through the self-deception — and whose insight Fran will immediately forget.

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis's portrait of Fran's self-deception is remarkable for its avoidance of easy moralism. He does not condemn Fran — he anatomizes her. The novel understands that self-deception serves a function: it protects Fran from the unbearable recognition that her life has been a series of misdirected energies, that she has damaged the one person (Sam) who genuinely loved her, and that the European aristocracy she pursues will never accept her as an equal.

The tragedy is not that Fran is evil but that she is trapped — trapped by her own intelligence, which is sharp enough to recognize her dissatisfaction but not sharp enough to recognize its source. She is looking for the solution in all the wrong places, and her gift for self-deception ensures she will never stop looking.
