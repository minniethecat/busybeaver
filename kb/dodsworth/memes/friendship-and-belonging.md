# Friendship and Belonging

Beyond the Dodsworths' collapsing marriage lies another social world in the novel: the friendships, particularly male friendships, through which Sam seeks and sometimes finds the belonging that Fran cannot provide.

## Definition

Friendship in *Dodsworth* functions as both a counterpoint to and a commentary on the marriage plot. Sam's relationship with Tub Pearson, his Yale roommate and lifelong friend, represents a form of male intimacy that predates and outlasts his marriage — uncomplicated, dependable, grounded in shared history rather than performance. Tub's telegram wishing Sam "bon voyage" and mentioning his nephew at the American Embassy is a small gesture, but it carries the weight of decades of mutual regard.

In Europe, Sam seeks similar connection but finds it elusive. The shipboard friendships — with Lockert, the English adventurer, and the various passengers who populate the crossing — are intense but transient, the social equivalent of a vacation romance. The challenge the novel traces is Sam's need to find or build genuine community in a world where he is no longer defined by his role at the Revelation Motor Company.

## Context in the Novel

The theme emerges most clearly through Sam's loneliness. In London, despite being surrounded by people, he feels invisible: "here in this city of traditional, unsmiling stateliness, among these unhurried, well-dressed people who so thoroughly ignored him" (008-009). His American instinct to "get acquainted" — to introduce himself, to establish his credentials — finds no purchase in a society where introductions must be mediated and credentials are assumed rather than announced.

Sam's eventual relationship with Edith Cortright in Venice represents the novel's answer to the friendship-and-belonging question. Edith is neither a replacement wife nor a romantic fantasy; she is a companion — someone who shares Sam's appreciation for beauty, his impatience with pretense, and his capacity for quiet contentment. Their relationship suggests that belonging, in the end, may be found not in institutions (marriage, business, nation) but in the elective affinity of two people who recognize each other.

## Key Passages

> What delightful people! Sam glowed; what a pleasure to travel with them: Lockert, the stolidly loquacious English adventurer; the jolly and vulgar little Jewish millinery buyer from Denver ... Colonel Endersley, American military attaché at Constantinople; Sally O'Leary, the satiny movie actress. (005-100)

The shipboard community — temporary, diverse, exhilarating — and a preview of the expanded social world Europe offers.

> There was Tub Pearson's nephew at the American Embassy, and of course Hurd, the manager of the London Revelation agency. He belonged! (006-026)

The poignancy of Sam's need to belong, revealed in his relief at having even the thinnest social connections in a foreign country.

> anything to assure him that he was someone and meant something, here in this city of traditional, unsmiling stateliness, among these unhurried, well-dressed people who so thoroughly ignored him. (008-009)

The crisis of recognition: without his role, who sees him?

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis's treatment of friendship is, in its quiet way, one of the novel's most original features. The marriage plot is familiar; what is less familiar is the attention Lewis gives to the other relationships — with Tub, with Lockert, with Hurd at the London agency, with the European acquaintances who pass through Sam's journey — that together constitute a man's social world.

The novel suggests that American culture, for all its vaunted friendliness, does not provide men with adequate structures for intimacy outside of marriage. Sam's friendships are real but thin; they depend on shared activity (business, golf, the ship's smoking room) rather than shared vulnerability. Part of Sam's growth is learning to be vulnerable — to admit need, to accept help, to risk rejection — and this growth makes possible the deeper connection with Edith that the novel's ending celebrates.
