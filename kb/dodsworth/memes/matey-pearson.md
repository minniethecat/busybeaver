# Matey Pearson

Matey Pearson — Mrs. Thomas J. Pearson — is Tub's wife and, by extension, a representative of the Midwestern domestic world that Fran has definitively left behind. Her brief appearances in the novel serve as reminders of what Sam has lost and what Fran has rejected.

## Biographical Sketch

Matey is mentioned primarily through Fran's dismissive comments and Sam's affectionate recollections. Fran finds her "dreadfully uninteresting. And fat!" (025-052), refusing to travel "fifteen hours in a dirty train for the pleasure of acting as an unpaid Cook's guide to Mr. and Mrs. Tub Pearson!" (025-056).

Sam's perspective, characteristically, is more generous. Matey is part of the Zenith world he values — the world of old friends, comfortable dinners, and unpretentious kindness. Her exclusion from the Dodsworths' European life is one of the many ways Fran's ambition severs Sam from his own history.

## Role in the Novel

Matey functions as a symbol: she IS the Zenith that Fran despises, and Sam's loyalty to the Pearsons is his loyalty to his own origins. Fran's refusal to see the Pearsons in Europe is a small cruelty that reveals the larger pattern: her project of Europeanization requires the systematic rejection of everything and everyone associated with the American life she is fleeing.
