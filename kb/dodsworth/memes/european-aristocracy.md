# European Aristocracy

The European aristocracy — the titled, landed, and effortlessly superior class that the Dodsworths encounter throughout their journey — is the novel's most important social antagonist. It is the world Fran aspires to join and the world that Sam, after careful observation, decides he neither can nor wishes to belong to.

## Description

Lewis renders the aristocracy not as individuals — though individual aristocrats appear: Lord Herndon, Kurt von Obersdorf, the assorted counts and barons of Fran's Paris salon — but as a system. It is a world defined by inheritance rather than achievement, by effortless superiority rather than demonstrated competence, by the quiet assumption that one's place at the top of the social order is natural rather than earned.

Sam's response to this system is the novel's most sustained meditation on class. He is drawn to its beauty and repelled by its injustice; he respects its confidence and mistrusts its complacency. His final position — that the aristocracy is admirable but not for him — is one of the novel's key moral achievements.

## Role in the Novel

The aristocracy serves as the novel's counterweight to American democratic values. Where America measures worth by achievement, Europe measures it by birth. Where America is noisy, Europe is quiet. Where America is open, Europe is closed. The novel does not simply endorse one system over the other; it asks Sam to understand both and to choose, freely, what kind of life he wants.

Fran's pursuit of the aristocracy — culminating in her relationship with Kurt von Obersdorf — is the novel's case study in the costs of that pursuit. The aristocracy she seeks does not exist on terms she can meet, and her recognition of this fact is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.
