# Rome

Rome is one of the European cities visited by [[sam-dodsworth|Sam Dodsworth]] during his journey of self-discovery. It appears in the novel less as a richly described setting than as a symbolic waypoint: the city where Sam pauses alone, trying to persuade himself he is studying architecture, before fleeing the heat and his own solitude.

## Role in the Novel

Rome appears in the novel's final movement, after Sam's marriage to [[fran-dodsworth|Fran Dodsworth]] has effectively ended and he has begun traveling independently. Unlike [[paris|Paris]], which dominates the middle section of the novel as the center of Fran's social ambitions, or [[venice|Venice]], where Sam experiences one of his most poignant moments of solitude, Rome is treated with deliberate sketchiness. Sam "poked about Rome for a week, trying to persuade himself that he was studying architecture" (030-054). The half-heartedness of the verb — "poked about" — captures his state: a man going through the motions of tourism without any real conviction.

The city is mentioned earlier as a destination in the Dodsworths' imagined European itinerary. Fran, dreaming of escape from Zenith, envisions "Spring on the Italian Lakes! Motoring through the Tyrol!" — Rome is part of the glamorous European map she constructs as an alternative to her Midwestern life. But when Sam finally reaches Rome, Fran is no longer with him, and the glamour has dissolved into the mundane reality of a middle-aged man alone in a hot city.

## Key Scenes

**The week in Rome (Chapter XXX)**: Sam arrives in Rome alone, late in the novel, attempting to fill his days with cultural self-improvement. The architecture he studies is a pretext — what he is really confronting is the emptiness of a life without work, without Fran, and without the children who no longer need him. After a week, "it was hot, and he fled" (030-054). The brevity of the description mirrors the brevity of his engagement: Rome is a place he passes through, not a place that transforms him.

**The letter relay (Chapter XXV)**: A letter from [[tub-pearson|Tub Pearson]] follows Sam "from Paris to Rome to Berlin" (025-042), tracing his restless movement across the continent and underscoring his rootlessness.

## City in the Novel's Geography

| City | Narrative Function | Sam's Experience |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| [[zenith|Zenith]] | Origin point; the world Sam built | Belonging, then estrangement |
| [[paris|Paris]] | Fran's social theater; expatriate world | Disorientation, observation |
| [[venice|Venice]] | Solitude and reflection | Melancholy, self-confrontation |
| [[berlin|Berlin]] | The path not taken (with Fran) | Detachment |
| Rome | The final tourist stop | Fleeting, unengaged |

Rome occupies the strange position of being a world-historical city that barely registers on Sam's consciousness. This is not a failure of the novel's descriptive power but a deliberate choice: Lewis uses Rome's thinness as a setting to dramatize Sam's emotional exhaustion. A man who cannot be moved by Rome is a man who has lost the capacity to be moved.
