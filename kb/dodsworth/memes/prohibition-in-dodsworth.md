# Prohibition in Dodsworth

Prohibition — the constitutional ban on the manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcohol in the United States from 1920 to 1933 — is a recurring satirical target in *Dodsworth*. [[sinclair-lewis|Sinclair Lewis]] uses the drinking habits of his American characters to expose the gap between the nation's moral posturing and its actual behavior, a gap that [[sam-dodsworth|Sam Dodsworth]] increasingly recognizes as he experiences European freedom from American Puritanism.

## Prohibition as Social Criticism

Lewis deploys Prohibition as a shorthand for everything he finds hypocritical about American culture. The law is universally evaded — within half an hour of arriving in New York, Sam has a case of whisky delivered to his hotel room (016-033) — and yet Americans abroad cannot stop talking about it. The traveling American is caricatured as "certain to be talking about either Coolidge or Prohibition" (005-076), a national obsession that marks Americans as provincial even when they are trying to be cosmopolitan.

[[ross-ireland|Ross Ireland]], the journalist who serves as one of Lewis's authorial mouthpieces, delivers the novel's most sustained critique: "I'd be tickled to death to live in America *if*. If we got rid of Prohibition, so a man could get a glass of beer instead of being compelled to drink gin and hootch" (011-129). But he immediately clarifies that Prohibition is merely a symptom: "the whole thing, the fundamental thing, is a lot harder to express than that. Nothing like so simple as just Prohibition" (011-130). The real problem is a culture of surveillance and moral policing — "a lot of mental and moral policemen" who make genuine freedom impossible.

## Effects on American Life

Lewis catalogs Prohibition's unintended consequences with a sociologist's precision:

| Effect | Manifestation in the Novel | PN |
|--------|--------------------------|-----|
| Hypocrisy normalized | Whisky delivered to hotel rooms within half an hour | 016-033 |
| Drinking as obsession | Country club men talk too much about drinking too much | 019-017 |
| Quality replaced by quantity | Americans abroad drink gin and hootch instead of beer | 011-129 |
| Social performance | Tub Pearson drinking steadily for twelve hours as "not even par" for Prohibition America | 026-060 |
| Cultural marker | Americans abroad identified by their Prohibition obsession | 005-076 |

Sam, a moderate drinker by inclination, observes that "Prohibition had turned drinking from an agreeable, not very important accompaniment to gossip into a craze" (019-017). The men of the Zenith country club set "were jumpy about it, and as fascinated as a schoolboy peering at obscene posters." The law has infantilized American adults, turning a normal social pleasure into a furtive thrill.

## Europe as Counterpoint

The European sections of the novel implicitly critique Prohibition by depicting societies where alcohol is integrated into daily life without the hysteria that characterizes American drinking. In Berlin, a polite German woman asks Sam whether "since Prohibition it was difficult to get wine in America" (022-007) — a question that reveals how America appears to Europeans: as a nation that has chosen to make ordinary pleasures difficult.

The Captain's Dinner on the SS *Ultima* — where "even prohibitionists were betrayed into smiles" by free champagne — dramatizes the absurdity of a law that almost no one actually supports. The communal indulgence, with "toasts drunk from table to table," represents a temporary escape from American moral constraints, a foretaste of the European liberty that awaits.
