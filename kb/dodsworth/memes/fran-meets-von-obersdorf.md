# Fran Meets Kurt von Obersdorf

Fran Dodsworth's encounter with Kurt von Obersdorf in Berlin is the event that gives her European aspirations a specific object. After months of pursuing sophistication in London and Paris — collecting acquaintances, attending salons, cultivating the appearance of European cultivation — Fran meets a titled Austrian who seems to offer everything she has been seeking. The relationship that develops precipitates the novel's central crisis.

## The Meeting

Kurt von Obersdorf enters the novel in Berlin as the culmination of Fran's social trajectory. He is titled, cultivated, and genuinely drawn to Fran — the first European aristocrat who seems to see her as she wishes to be seen rather than as the American tourist she is. For Fran, who has spent two decades feeling misunderstood in Zenith and a year performing sophistication in Europe, von Obersdorf's attention is intoxicating.

Lewis renders the relationship with characteristic restraint: we see it through its effects on Fran and through Sam's increasingly clear-eyed observations rather than through romantic scene-setting. The restraint is deliberate — Lewis is less interested in the romance than in its consequences.

## Significance

The meeting with von Obersdorf is the novel's point of no return for Fran. Before Berlin, her European ambitions were diffuse — a general desire for sophistication, a vague rejection of American provincialism. After Berlin, they are specific: she wants this man, this title, this life. The specificity makes her ambition more dangerous and more legible to Sam, who can no longer pretend that Fran's dissatisfactions are temporary or superficial.

The relationship also exposes the limits of Fran's European project. Von Obersdorf may be charmed by her, but his world — the Austrian aristocracy — is no more likely to accept an American divorcée than the English aristocracy was. Fran's pursuit of Europe, Lewis suggests, is a pursuit of something that does not exist on terms she can meet.
