# The American Woman

Fran Dodsworth is not just a character; she is Lewis's anatomy of a particular type of American woman in the 1920s — intelligent, energetic, socially accomplished, and profoundly dissatisfied with the domestic role that her husband's success has assigned her.

## Definition

The "American woman" in *Dodsworth* is defined in contrast both to the European women Fran meets and to the previous generation of American wives. Fran is not a homemaker; she runs committees, manages social networks, and treats her clubs "like an Indiana politician" (002-030). She is not submissive; she is the active partner in the Dodsworth marriage, the one who proposes the European journey, the one who decides when the marriage is over. And she is not content with material comfort — she wants something more, something she cannot quite name, that she looks for in European aristocrats and Continental culture.

Lewis's treatment of Fran is notable for its mixture of sympathy and critique. He understands her boredom, her intelligence, her legitimate hunger for a life larger than Zenith can offer. But he also exposes the hollowness of her solution — the pursuit of titles and salon society — which reproduces the same emptiness in a different language.

## Context in the Novel

Fran's type is established early through her social activities: she is "interested in every aspect of these leagues except perhaps the purposes for which they had been founded" (002-030). This is Lewis at his satirical sharpest — Fran's energy is genuine, but it has no object worthy of it. She is a politician without a cause, an executive without a company.

The European journey reveals both her strengths and her fatal limitation. She navigates London society, charms aristocrats, and secures invitations that would be beyond most American tourists. But her goal — to become European, to shed her American identity — is impossible, and the attempt to achieve it destroys her marriage without bringing her the fulfillment she seeks. Major Herndon's observation that "you're too American, Mrs. Dodsworth" and that she is "so typically American" in her belief that she is European (009-009) is the novel's definitive judgment: you cannot escape what you are by pretending to be something else.

## Key Passages

> She was interested in every aspect of these leagues except perhaps the purposes for which they had been founded, and no Indiana politician was craftier at soaping enemies, advising friends, and building up a political machine to accomplish nothing in particular. (002-030)

The precise diagnosis: Fran's talents are real but misdirected.

> "I'm a very active female, Sam, and I want to do something besides sitting around Zenith." (002-037)

The legitimate complaint, spoken in Fran's characteristic voice — half plea, half demand.

> "you're too American, Mrs. Dodsworth. ... And you, who feel you're so European, you're so typically American! You have the most charming and childish misconceptions about yourself." (009-009)

The mirror held up: Fran's European ambition is itself the most American thing about her.

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis's portrait of Fran is one of the most complex treatments of an American wife in early twentieth-century fiction. She is not a villain — the novel grants her real grievances and real intelligence — but she is not a heroine either; her self-absorption and her treatment of Sam are genuinely damaging. She is, instead, a case study in what happens when a talented woman is given no outlet for her talents except social management, and then given the leisure to realize how empty that management has been.

The tragedy is that Fran's diagnosis of her situation is largely correct — Zenith IS provincial, her life IS narrow, she DOES deserve more — but her prescription is wrong. She looks for fulfillment in European titles rather than in genuine work, creativity, or self-knowledge. The novel suggests, implicitly, that the real solution to Fran's problem would be not a count but a career — but Lewis is too honest to pretend that option was readily available to a woman of Fran's generation and class.
