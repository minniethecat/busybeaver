# Sale of the Revelation Company

The sale of the Revelation Motor Company to the Unit Automotive Company is the novel's inciting event. It makes Sam Dodsworth wealthy but purposeless, transforms him from an industrialist into a traveler, and creates the conditions — free time, disposable income, existential restlessness — that make the European journey both possible and necessary.

## Background

Sam Dodsworth built the Revelation Motor Company from the ground up. The "immense glass and marble building on Constitution Avenue, North" (002-003) is the physical expression of a lifetime of work. When the novel opens, Sam is twenty years into his career, a respected figure in Zenith industry, and entirely defined by his role as the company's leader.

The Unit Automotive Company — "the imperial U.A.C., with its seven makes of motors, its body-building works, its billion dollars of capital" (002-002) — represents the forces of consolidation transforming American industry in the 1920s. Its absorption of the Revelation Company is not a hostile takeover but a recognition of value: the U.A.C. wants what Sam built, and it is willing to pay.

## The Transaction

Alec Kynance, president of the U.A.C., arrives in Zenith to finalize the transfer of holdings. The terms are generous: the Revelation will be positioned between the Chromecar and the Highroad, Sam's employees will be retained, and Sam himself will receive a substantial payout alongside a vague offer of a U.A.C. executive position. The deal is, by any business measure, a success.

But for Sam, the sale is an amputation. Without the company that has organized his days and defined his identity, he is "a man who didn't know what he wanted to do" (003-005). The novel makes clear that the European journey is not a reward for the sale but an attempt to fill the void it creates.

## Significance

The sale of the Revelation Company is the economic fact that makes the entire novel possible. Without it, Sam remains in Zenith, defined by work, his marriage continuing in its established pattern of accommodation and distance. The sale liberates him from the factory floor — and confronts him with the question of who he is without it.

The sale also introduces the novel's central theme: the relationship between work and identity. Sam is not a man who worked; he is a man constituted by work. When the work is removed, he must discover — or construct — a self that exists independently of it. The European journey is the laboratory in which this experiment is conducted.

## Key Characters

Sam Dodsworth sells the company he built. Alec Kynance, president of the U.A.C., represents the new order of American industrial consolidation. Tub Pearson, as Zenith banker, witnesses the transfer of his friend's life's work into corporate hands.

## Consequences

The sale leads directly to the European journey, the unraveling of the Dodsworth marriage, and Sam's eventual discovery of a life organized around companionship and attention rather than achievement and obligation. It is the beginning of everything the novel narrates.
