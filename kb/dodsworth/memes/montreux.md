# Montreux

Montreux, on the shores of Lake Geneva, is the destination Fran Dodsworth selects for her summer when she refuses to return to America with Sam. It is the novel's most significant unvisited place — the location that represents Fran's permanent election of Europe over America, and of a social life organized around titled acquaintances over a marriage organized around mutual accommodation.

## Description

Montreux appears in the novel only as an intention: Fran has "practically promised Renée de Pénable to take a villa with her near Montreux for the summer" (028-003). It is never described directly, existing only as a plan, a direction, a choice. This absence is itself significant — Montreux is the place where Fran imagines her European life will finally deliver what she has been pursuing, but the novel never follows her there.

Lewis's choice to name Montreux rather than a more famous Swiss resort is characteristic: it is a real place, modestly elegant, associated with the expatriate and literary culture that Fran aspires to join. Its very specificity — "near Montreux" rather than simply "Switzerland" — makes Fran's fantasy concrete in a way that a vaguer reference would not.

## Significance in the Novel

Montreux represents the fork in the road that the Dodsworth marriage does not survive. Sam is returning to America for his Yale reunion; Fran is choosing Montreux and Renée de Pénable. The decision is presented not as a dramatic confrontation but as a practical arrangement — which makes it more devastating. Fran can "understand that you need to go back," she tells Sam, but "I'm not going" (028-003).

The unvisited destination is a recurring motif in Lewis's novels: the place that is planned for, talked about, used as a social credential, but never actually inhabited in the narrative. Montreux is Fran's Europe in its purest form — the Europe of the imagination, uncontaminated by the disappointments that actual European residence has brought her.

## Key Events

**Fran's summer plans**: Rather than return to America, Fran commits to Montreux with Renée de Pénable — the decision that formalizes the Dodsworth separation.

## Characters Associated

Fran Dodsworth chooses Montreux over America, over Sam, over the life she has spent two decades escaping. Renée de Pénable is the friend with whom she will share the villa. Sam Dodsworth accepts the arrangement — and returns to America alone.
