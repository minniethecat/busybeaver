# Alec Kynance

Alec Kynance is the president of the Unit Automotive Company, the industrial conglomerate that absorbs Sam's Revelation Motor Company. He appears only briefly in the novel, but his actions set the entire plot in motion: by buying Sam's company, he strips Sam of the identity that has defined his adult life.

## Biographical Sketch

Kynance is the personification of the "booming industrial flood" (002-057) that is transforming American capitalism in the 1920s. He runs the U.A.C. — "the imperial U.A.C., with its seven makes of motors, its body-building works, its billion dollars of capital" — with the confidence of a man who understands that the future belongs to consolidation, not to individual craftsmanship.

He offers Sam a position as first vice president, with a salary of eighty-five thousand dollars and a share in the managers' pool. The offer is generous by any conventional measure — and it is precisely its generosity that makes it so devastating. Sam recognizes that he would be "nothing more than an office boy," unable to "make no daring decisions by himself" (002-057).

## Role in the Novel

Kynance is less a character than a force — the embodiment of the economic logic that is rendering men like Sam obsolete. He is not cruel or predatory; he is simply the agent of a process larger than any individual. His absorption of the Revelation Company is not personal — which is precisely what makes it so existentially threatening to Sam.

Kynance's offer to let Sam "take it easy and just think up mean ideas to shove over" (003-013) — including Sam's dream of "motorized touring-school for boys in summer" — reveals the ambiguity of his role. He is both destroyer and enabler: the man who takes away Sam's company and the man who offers him the resources to pursue new dreams. Sam's refusal of the offer is his assertion that he is not for sale — that Samuel Dodsworth is more than a line item on a corporate balance sheet.

## Relationships

Kynance's relationship with Sam is defined by the power asymmetry between them. Kynance holds all the cards — the capital, the market position, the future — and Sam knows it. Their interactions are cordial but charged: two men who understand that one of them is being absorbed by the other.

## Key Scenes

**The offer (Chapter II)**: Kynance presents Sam with the terms of the U.A.C. acquisition — generous, professional, and utterly devastating to Sam's sense of self.

**The counter-proposal (Chapter III)**: Kynance's suggestion that Sam could develop "motorized touring-schools" and "motor caravans with electric stoves" — the industrialist's vision of leisure, commodified and packaged for the masses.

## Character Analysis

Alec Kynance is a functional character — he exists to set the plot in motion — but Lewis gives him enough specificity to make him more than a plot device. He is not a villain; he is a businessman doing what businessmen do. The tragedy is not that Kynance is cruel but that the logic of capital is indifferent to the human lives it reorganizes. Sam's crisis is not caused by a bad man but by a system that has outgrown the kind of man Sam is.
