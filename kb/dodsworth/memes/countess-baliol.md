# Countess of Baliol

The Countess of Baliol is one of the titled Europeans who populate the novel's social scenes — a minor aristocrat whose brief appearance illustrates the world Fran is pursuing and Sam is observing with increasing detachment.

## Biographical Sketch

The Countess appears in a single, telling moment: "I saw you in the corner with the Countess of Baliol" (010-056). She is not developed beyond her title and her presence at a social gathering, but that is precisely the point. In the world Fran aspires to join, "Countess of Baliol" IS an identity — the title conveys everything one needs to know.

Sam's response to this world of titled strangers is characteristic: "Why the devil don't they wear their coronets? Honestly, Fran, this is too rich for my blood. I can stand meeting a couple hundred people at once, but not the entire British aristocracy" (010-056). His American impatience with inherited hierarchy is both a limitation (he cannot take the social game seriously) and a strength (he cannot be hurt by its exclusions the way Fran can).

## Role in the Novel

The Countess is a walk-on — a representative of the aristocratic world that Fran pursues. Her narrative function is minimal; her symbolic function is considerable. Every titled European in the novel is a test: can Fran gain entry to this world? And the answer, consistently, is that she can visit but never belong.
