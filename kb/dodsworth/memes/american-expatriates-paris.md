# American Expatriates in Paris

The American expatriate community in Paris — glimpsed through the De Pénable circle, the figures at Hurd's dinner, and the Americans Sam encounters throughout the European chapters — represents a possible future for the Dodsworths: the choice to remain in Europe, to become the Americans-abroad who "subscribe to the home paper, from Keokuk or New York or Pottsville" and whose "one great day each week is that of the arrival of the American mail" (026-002).

## Description

Lewis renders the expatriate community with the satirist's eye and the sociologist's precision. These Americans "insist that they cannot live in America, but, except for a tenth of them who have really become acclimated in Europe, they are so hungry for American news" that they read the Paris editions of the New York Herald and Chicago Tribune "solemnly, every word" (026-002). They "know quite as well as Sister Louisa, back home, when the Washington Avenue extension will be finished" (026-002).

The community is suspended between two worlds — no longer fully American, never fully European — and its members manage this suspension with varying degrees of success. Some, like Edith Cortright, have made genuine homes in Europe. Others, like Endicott Everett Atkins, have become caricatures of cultivation. Most, Lewis suggests, are simply Americans who have chosen to live somewhere else — a choice more practical than existential.

## Role in the Novel

The expatriate community provides the novel's gallery of possible futures for the Dodsworths. Fran aspires to join it; Sam observes it with wary curiosity; the novel's resolution — Sam with Edith Cortright at the Ercole estate — represents one version of what expatriate life can be: not flight from America but a genuine habitation of two worlds at once.
