# Kennepoose Canoe Club Dance

The dance at the Kennepoose Canoe Club on an August evening in 1903 is the novel's opening scene — the moment when twenty-eight-year-old [[sam-dodsworth|Sam Dodsworth]], driving his first automobile, arrives to court [[fran-dodsworth|Fran Voelker]]. It establishes the world Sam inhabits before the story's central crisis: a world of provincial prosperity, sentimental romance, and the innocent belief that Zenith is the center of civilization.

## The Setting

The scene is rendered as a consciously picturesque tableau. Lewis describes "the aristocracy of Zenith" dancing on the wide porch of the Canoe Club, "with its pillars of pine trunks, its bobbing Japanese lanterns." The young women wear "dance-frocks with wider sleeves" and their hair is "sensuously piled on little smiling heads." The moon is "moon-washed and spacious and proper for respectable romance." The narrator's tone hovers between affectionate nostalgia and gentle irony — the scene is "a sentimental chromo — crisping lake, lovers in canoes singing 'Nelly Was a Lady,' all very lugubrious and happy."

Three automobiles have already arrived — "for it was now 1903, the climax of civilization" — and a fourth is approaching, driven by Sam Dodsworth. The automobile, still a novelty bordering on a spectacle, marks Sam as a man of the future even as he participates in a scene of picturesque tradition.

## The Characters

Sam arrives as a "large and formidable young man, with a healthy brown mustache and a chaos of brown hair on a massive head." He is twenty-eight, assistant superintendent of the Zenith Locomotive Works, a Yale football veteran (class of 1896), and driving his first car — "none of your old-fashioned 'gasoline buggies,' with the engine under the seat. The engine bulked in front, under a proud hood over two feet long." He is proud, capable, and utterly at home in this world.

[[tub-pearson|Tub Pearson]], "round and short and jolly, class jester and class dandy at Yale," greets him in white kid gloves, already practicing the witty insouciance that will define their lifelong friendship. Tub's joke — "I've got a horse all ready to tow you back!" — captures the moment's technological threshold: the automobile is still unreliable enough to be a gamble, and driving one is an act of faith in the future.

## Narrative Function

The opening scene does more than establish atmosphere. It introduces the novel's central tensions in miniature: the clash between tradition and modernity, between provincial contentment and restless ambition, between the sentimental and the ironic. Sam Dodsworth at the Kennepoose dance is a man who believes in the world he inhabits — in Zenith, in the automobile, in Fran Voelker. The entire novel will be the story of what happens to that belief.

The scene's final image — Sam driving home at "twelve dizzy miles an hour" with "a feeling of power, of dominating the universe" — is an ironic premonition. The speed that exhilarates him will, over the decades to come, carry him far beyond the world of the Kennepoose Canoe Club, into a Europe where twelve miles an hour is not speed but stasis.
