# Nande Azeredo

Nande Azeredo is one of the international figures Sam encounters in Europe — a South American whose perspective offers a third term beyond the America-Europe binary that dominates the novel. His presence suggests that the world is larger than either continent, and that genuine cosmopolitanism may require leaving both behind.

## Biographical Sketch

Azeredo appears in the later European chapters, part of the international society of diplomats, expatriates, and wanderers that populates the novel's European scenes. He is South American — the novel is not more specific — and represents a genuinely international perspective: neither American nor European, neither provincial nor snobbish, but something else entirely.

His significance is less in any particular action than in his mere existence: he is proof that the America-Europe binary that structures so much of the novel's thinking is not exhaustive. There are other ways of being in the world, other modes of sophistication, other forms of belonging.

## Role in the Novel

Azeredo functions as a quiet alternative to the dominant cultural dynamic. Where Lockert represents the English perspective (ironic, self-aware, fundamentally European), Azeredo represents the genuinely cosmopolitan — someone who belongs to no single national tradition and therefore can appreciate each on its own terms.

His conversations with Sam are among the few in the novel that are not structured by the America-Europe opposition. They talk as two men of the world, each curious about the other's experience, neither feeling the need to assert superiority or apologize for origins.

## Key Scenes

**International encounters in Europe**: Azeredo's conversations with Sam offer a glimpse of what genuine cosmopolitanism might look like — not the anxious imitation of European manners but the relaxed confidence of someone who is at home everywhere because he is not defined by any single place.

## Character Analysis

Nande Azeredo is a minor character who gestures toward a possibility the novel does not fully explore: a cosmopolitanism that is neither American nor European, that does not require choosing between the factory and the cathedral, that finds its identity not in opposition but in curiosity. He is a reminder, in a novel about the difficulty of crossing cultural boundaries, that some people have already crossed them — and that crossing need not be traumatic.
