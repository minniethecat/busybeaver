# Zenith Chamber of Commerce

The Zenith Chamber of Commerce appears in *Dodsworth* as a four-sheet poster at Hurd's London dinner — a visual emblem of the civic boosterism that defines the Midwestern commercial world. It is one of the many institutional affiliations that constitute the American identity Sam carries with him to Europe.

## Description

Lewis renders the Chamber of Commerce indirectly, through its poster — a four-sheet advertisement for the Revelation car — and through the institutional landscape it represents. The Chamber is the organizational expression of Zenith's commercial confidence: the belief that business is civic virtue, that what is good for the Revelation Company is good for Zenith, and that what is good for Zenith is good for America.

That belief is presented in the novel without caricature. Lewis is critical of Midwestern boosterism — its complacency, its narrowness, its confusion of commercial success with cultural achievement — but he also respects its energy, its democratic openness, its willingness to build things. The Chamber of Commerce, like Zenith itself, is both admirable and limited.

## Role in the Novel

The Chamber's poster at Hurd's dinner is a small detail with large implications. Alongside the banners of Yale and Harvard and Winnemac, the shields of the Elks and the Rotarians and the Kiwanians, the Chamber of Commerce poster contributes to the dense texture of American institutional life that Hurd assembles in a Soho restaurant. It is the material culture of American identity — the flags, the banners, the posters, the shields — that Americans abroad surround themselves with to ward off European indifference.
