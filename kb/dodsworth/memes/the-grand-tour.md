# The Grand Tour

The historical practice of extended European travel as the culmination of a proper education — a tradition that Fran Dodsworth invokes to justify the couple's journey abroad, and that Lewis uses to structure his novel's inquiry into what travel can actually accomplish.

## Definition

The Grand Tour was an eighteenth- and nineteenth-century tradition in which young aristocrats (particularly British) spent months or years traveling through France, Italy, and Germany to absorb classical culture, acquire social polish, and establish Continental connections. By the 1920s, when *Dodsworth* is set, the tradition had democratized: middle-class Americans like the Dodsworths could now afford what had once been the privilege of lords.

But Lewis is interested in what happens when the Grand Tour is undertaken not by a twenty-year-old aristocrat but by a fifty-year-old industrialist. The traditional purpose — cultural education — collides with the reality of middle-aged identity: Sam is not a blank slate to be inscribed with European culture but a fully formed person whose American values are both a resource and a limitation.

## Context in the Novel

The Grand Tour structures the novel's geography. The Dodsworths follow something close to the classic itinerary: London for society, Paris for culture, Italy for beauty, Germany and Austria for romance. Each stop tests a different aspect of Sam's character. London tests his social confidence; Paris tests his aesthetic sensibilities; Venice tests his capacity for solitude and reflection.

Crucially, the tour transforms both travelers — but in opposite directions. Sam becomes more reflective, more self-aware, more capable of independence. Fran becomes more frantic, more dependent on external validation, more lost. The same journey that educates Sam into self-knowledge educates Fran into self-destruction.

## Critical Interpretation

The Grand Tour in *Dodsworth* is simultaneously a real journey and a metaphor for the examined life. Sam, who has spent twenty-five years absorbed in business, is belatedly undertaking the education in living that a different kind of upbringing might have given him. The novel suggests that this education is possible — that a fifty-year-old industrialist CAN learn to see Romanesque churches, can learn to sit alone in a Venetian café without feeling useless — but that it requires a willingness to be a beginner that most middle-aged people lack.

Fran's failure is instructive. She undertakes the Grand Tour not to learn but to confirm what she already believes: that Europe is superior, that Zenith is provincial, that she deserves a grander life. Travel, for her, is not exploration but validation. Sam, by contrast, travels with genuine curiosity. He does not know what he will find, and his openness — his "American" willingness to be impressed, to be wrong, to change his mind — is what allows the journey to change him.

The novel's title is itself a kind of answer: it is called *Dodsworth*, not *The Grand Tour* or *An American in Europe*. The journey matters, but what matters more is the person who undertakes it.
