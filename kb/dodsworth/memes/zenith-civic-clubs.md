# Zenith Civic Clubs

The civic and fraternal clubs of Zenith — the Elks, the Oddfellows, the Moose, the Woodmen, the Rotarians, the Kiwanians — form the social infrastructure of the Midwestern commercial world. Their banners, displayed at Hurd's London dinner alongside those of Yale and Harvard, are the visual evidence of a civic culture that Americans carry with them wherever they go.

## Description

Lewis names these clubs without describing them individually; their significance lies precisely in their interchangeability. The Elks and the Oddfellows and the Moose and the Woodmen — the list itself is the point, the accumulation of nearly identical organizations suggesting a world of cheerful, organized, slightly absurd male sociability.

The Rotarians and Kiwanians, more explicitly commercial in their orientation, represent the fusion of business and civic virtue that defines the Zenith ethos. To be a Rotarian is to declare oneself a responsible businessman, a pillar of the community, a man who takes his civic duties as seriously as his commercial ones — or at least performs them as publicly.

## Role in the Novel

The civic clubs appear most memorably at Hurd's dinner in Soho, where their shields and banners, collected heaven-knows-how by Hurd, form a dense texture of American institutional affiliation — a visual argument, assembled in a foreign restaurant, that Americans are somebody, belong somewhere, mean something. The clubs are part of the identity Sam carries to Europe: not something he thinks about, but something he is.

The clubs also represent the associational culture that Tocqueville identified as distinctively American — the tendency to form organizations, to join things, to express civic identity through membership. Lewis both satirizes and respects this culture: its earnestness may be comic, but its energy is genuine.
