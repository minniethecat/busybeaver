Endicott Everett Atkins is an American expatriate in Paris and a central figure in the social circle that [[Fran Dodsworth]] eagerly joins upon the Dodsworths' arrival in France. Reputed to resemble [[wikipedia:Henry James|Henry James]] in both appearance and manner, Atkins represents the kind of Europeanized American intellectual that Fran admires and [[Sam Dodsworth]] instinctively distrusts.

## Biographical Sketch

Atkins is an established figure in Paris expatriate society, living on the Rue des Petits-Champs where he hosts afternoon teas in a "dusky little studio" (014-025). His name—the triple-barreled "Endicott Everett Atkins"—becomes something of a running joke in the novel; Sam grumbles that it "sounds like a business college yell" (014-028). Physically, he is described as having "the massive and rather bald head, the portly dignity" of Henry James, and he speaks "in a measured voice" with "a small neat mustache" (014-032).

He is reputedly a writer of some kind, and he moves in circles that connect to literary figures—he knows anecdotes traced through E. V. Lucas back to Henry James (014-043). He is capable of genuine charm, particularly with women: "With men, Mr. Endicott Everett Atkins rarely let down, but with slim and glistening women he came near to being human" (014-041).

## Role in the Novel

Atkins functions as a satirical portrait of the American expatriate who has remade himself into a European aesthete. He and [[Renée de Pénable]] together "dominated" the Dodsworths' social life in Paris, drawing Fran into a world of tea parties, literary name-dropping, and affected sophistication (015-001).

> "I really can't tell you," Mr. Endicott Everett Atkins was moaning at Fran, "how very, very much I admire your wisdom in coming to Europe in a really leisured pilgrimage" (014-047).

For Sam, Atkins becomes a symbol of everything hollow about Fran's European aspirations. "He came to like the affabilities of Endicott Everett Atkins even less than the expensive sunset-hues of Madame de Pénable" (015-020). In a key comparison, Lewis notes that Sam felt more reverence in Notre-Dame cathedral than "was any Endicott Everett Atkins with his Adam's apple ecclesiastically throbbing" (015-026).

## Key Scenes

**The rue des Petits-Champs tea (Chapter 14)** — Fran and Sam attend Atkins's studio tea, where some thirty people gather around a refectory table with "a small teapot, a small plate of limp cakes, and an enormous bowl of punch" (014-034). Sam "never remembered any of them, save Endicott Everett Atkins"—the rest seemed "as indistinguishable as separate moths" (014-034). Atkins holds court, telling Fran anecdotes about Henry James and Swinburne, and convincing her that her European pilgrimage is culturally significant.

**The social takeover (Chapter 15)** — Atkins and Madame de Pénable effectively take over the Dodsworths' social calendar. The Dodsworths "ceased to be children exploring together, rather happy in their loneliness" (015-001) and became actors in someone else's social drama. This marks a turning point in Sam's alienation from Fran's European life.

## Character Analysis

Atkins is one of Lewis's most economical satirical creations. His triple-barreled name alone marks him as a type—the American who has accumulated European cultural signifiers the way a businessman accumulates capital. His resemblance to Henry James (the American writer who became a British subject) is thematically pointed: Atkins is a lesser, more pretentious version of that transatlantic transformation.

> For Sam, Atkins represents the substitution of manner for substance. Where Sam values "strength and endurance and wisdom" in Notre-Dame's architecture, Atkins offers only "his Adam's apple ecclesiastically throbbing"—the performance of culture without its soul.

Yet Lewis does not make Atkins merely a villain. His "benevolent" look and his "glossy baldness rose-hued in the red-shaded lamps" (014-038) suggest a certain pathos—he too is an American seeking a place to belong, even if his method is through affectation rather than authenticity.
