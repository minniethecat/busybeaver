# Departure from Zenith

The Dodsworths' departure from Zenith is the event that transforms the European journey from a plan into a fact. After the sale of the Revelation Company, after the weeks of packing and farewells, after "the artificially prolonged waving to friends on the dock endured" (005-001) — the departure is the point at which the familiar world recedes and the unknown asserts its claim.

## The Departure

Lewis renders the departure through Sam's consciousness: the messy details of preparation, the farewells to friends, the finality of leaving the city that has contained his entire adult life. For Sam, the departure is tinged with both excitement and loss — he genuinely wants to see Europe, but he genuinely belongs to Zenith in a way Fran never has.

For Fran, the departure is liberation. Zenith has been the cage she has spent two decades planning to escape. The moment the train pulls out — or the ship casts off — she is already, in her imagination, the European she will spend the rest of the novel trying to become.

## Significance

The departure from Zenith marks the boundary between the novel's two worlds: the American world of industry, sincerity, and provincial certainty, and the European world of culture, sophistication, and existential dislocation. Everything that follows — the Atlantic crossing, the London and Paris sojourns, the Berlin crisis, the Venetian resolution — traces its origin to this moment.

The departure is also the novel's first statement of its central question: can an American leave America? Can Sam Dodsworth, formed by Zenith industry and Yale loyalty and Midwestern virtue, become someone else — or must he remain, wherever he goes, the man Zenith made?
