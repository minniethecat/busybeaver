# Edith Cortright

Edith Cortright is the novel's quiet resolution: an American woman living in voluntary exile in Venice who becomes Sam's companion and, ultimately, the person with whom he can imagine a future. She represents not romantic fantasy but the possibility of mature love — love founded on mutual recognition rather than mutual need.

## Biographical Sketch

Edith Cortright is an American expatriate, probably in her late thirties or early forties, who has made a life for herself in Venice. The novel reveals little about her past — there is a suggestion of a failed marriage, of disappointments borne with dignity — but this reticence is itself characterization. Edith is not someone who explains herself; she is someone who has made peace with herself, and that peace is what she offers Sam.

She is described as having a quiet vitality, a talent for managing Venetian domestic life (the "surprisingly vigorous and outdoor Edith" (034-001)), and a capacity for attention that Fran entirely lacks. Where Fran is always performing, Edith is simply present.

## Role in the Novel

Edith appears relatively late in the novel — the first mention of her name comes well into the European journey — but her function is essential. She is the answer to the question the novel has been asking: what comes after the collapse of Sam's marriage? What kind of relationship is possible for a man who has been betrayed, who has lost his work, who has learned to be alone?

Edith offers not passion but companionship: "He had never so much as kissed her; only three or four times had he even patted her hand" (034-019). This restraint is presented not as deficiency but as maturity — the recognition that the deepest forms of intimacy do not require the gestures of romance. When Sam is away from her, he finds that "he missed her — had every moment some idea or observation he desired to share with her" (034-019). This is the novel's definition of love: not the hunger that consumes but the presence that sustains.

## Relationships

Edith's primary relationship in the novel is with Sam. Their connection develops slowly, almost imperceptibly, across the Venetian chapters. There is no courtship, no declaration, no dramatic climax — only the gradual mutual recognition of two people who have each, in their own way, learned to live without illusions.

Her relationship to Venice itself is also significant. She is not a tourist but a resident — someone who has made a home in a foreign place, who has learned to belong without being native. This capacity — to be at home anywhere, with anyone who truly sees you — is what she teaches Sam.

## Key Scenes

**Sam's discovery of the "outdoor Edith" (Chapter XXXIV)**: At the Ercole estate, Edith reveals a side of herself — vigorous, competent, at ease with Italian servants and rhythms — that Sam had not seen in Venice. She is not the fragile expatriate he imagined but a woman of substance and capability.

**The moment of recognition**: Sam realizes that "when she was away, he missed her — had every moment some idea or observation he desired to share with her" (034-019). This is his education in a different kind of love — less dramatic than what he felt for Fran, but more durable.

## Character Analysis

Edith Cortright is easy to underestimate because she does not demand attention. She is the novel's most adult character — someone who has processed her own disappointments and emerged not bitter but serene, not resigned but genuinely content. She offers Sam not excitement but peace, not passion but presence, and the novel presents this as the greater gift.

Lewis's portrayal of Edith is notable for what it refuses: she is not a prize to be won, not a reward for Sam's suffering, not a symbol of anything. She is a person — specific, irreducible, real — and her relationship with Sam is an achievement, not a plot point. In a novel about the difficulty of genuine connection, Edith represents the possibility that such connection, however late, is possible.
