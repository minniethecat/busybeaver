# Europe vs America

The structural opposition at the heart of *Dodsworth*: the encounter between American energy and European tradition. Sinclair Lewis uses Sam Dodsworth's journey to examine what each civilization values, what each lacks, and whether the gap can be bridged by a single human life.

## Definition

The Europe-vs-America theme in *Dodsworth* is not a simple travelogue contrast but a sustained inquiry into two different ways of organizing human experience. Europe represents history, hierarchy, aesthetic refinement, and social complexity; America represents industry, sincerity, democratic leveling, and a certain innocence. Lewis refuses to endorse either pole absolutely, instead tracing the damage and growth that the collision produces in his protagonist.

The binary is established early and explicitly — in the dismissive chauvinism of Sam's business associate who declares "Europe? Rats! Dead's a doornail! Place for women and long-haired artists" (003-016), and in Fran's countervailing hunger for the "Spring on the Italian Lakes! Motoring through the Tyrol! London in the Season!" (002-037). These two voices — the philistine and the aspirant — bracket Sam's own more tentative engagement with the question.

## Context in the Novel

The theme governs the novel's entire structure: the first section in Zenith establishes Sam's American identity; the transatlantic crossing is the liminal space where the two worlds first meet; and the European chapters — England, France, Italy, Germany — present a series of encounters that test and reshape that identity.

On the ship, the European Major Lockert and the American Sam Dodsworth circle each other with mutual curiosity. Lockert's observation that "the English are essentially more insufferable than the Americans" (005-085) is delivered with the ironic self-awareness that Sam, at this stage, cannot match. The Englishman can criticize his own nation in a way the American has not yet learned to do.

Sam's first morning in London crystallizes the theme: he is disoriented by the "traditional, unsmiling stateliness" of Pall Mall, relieved only by the sight of an American bank that looks "very busy and cheerful" — a fragment of home in an alien landscape. He buys five hats in one afternoon, beginning "the Europeanization which he wasn't going to begin" (008-016).

## Key Passages

> "Europe? Rats! Dead's a doornail! Place for women and long-haired artists. Dead! Only American loans that keep 'em from burying the corpse! All this art! More art in a good shiny spark-plug than in all the fat Venus de Mylos they ever turned out." (003-016)

> The English are essentially more insufferable than the Americans — by sitting back and quietly assuming that we're the noblest and rightest people on earth. And if any man or any nation has the courage or the magnificent egotism to do that long enough, almost everyone will accept it from him. (005-085)

> He entered the shop and came out with a new gray felt hat for town, a new brown one for the country, a bowler, a silk evening hat, and a cap, and he was proud of himself for having begun the Europeanization which he wasn't going to begin. (008-016)

> He was orthodox about American breakfasts, and nothing could have sent him more gloomily to his own decent Cream of Wheat than Fran's willingness to take a thing called a kipper. (008-010)

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis's treatment of the Europe-vs-America theme is more nuanced than it first appears. The novel is not a simple celebration of American virtue over European decadence, nor is it a Jamesian tragedy of American innocence crushed by Old World sophistication. Instead, Lewis uses the contrast to reveal what is incomplete in both civilizations — and, more importantly, what is incomplete in Sam himself.

The key insight is that Sam's American identity is both a strength and a limitation. His impatience with pretense, his belief in work as the measure of a man, his democratic refusal to be impressed by titles — these are real virtues. But they are also forms of blindness: to the pleasures of leisure, to the value of tradition, to the complexity of social life that cannot be reduced to business efficiency.

Europe, in turn, is shown to have its own pathologies: the "snobbishness" that Lockert himself diagnoses, the stifling weight of class, the aestheticism that masks moral vacancy. The novel's resolution suggests that the right response to the Europe-vs-America question is neither to choose a side nor to synthesize them, but to outgrow the question itself — to become, as Sam eventually does, a person for whom the opposition no longer defines the available options.
