# Pall Mall Walk

Sam Dodsworth's walk past the gentlemen's clubs of Pall Mall is the novel's most concentrated image of transatlantic alienation. Walking past "the aloof and white-faced clubs" (008-005), invisible to the "unhurried, well-dressed people who so thoroughly ignored him" (008-005), Sam confronts the question that will haunt the entire European journey: what is an American worth in a world that does not recognize American achievement?

## The Walk

The walk is rendered in a single paragraph that moves from external description to internal monologue with the fluidity characteristic of Lewis's late style. Sam sees the clubs, feels their indifference, and responds with a rush of defensive American self-assertion:

"Pictures? Why talk stupidly about pictures when he could talk intelligently about engines? Languages? If he had nothing to say, what was the good of saying it in three languages? Manners? These presumable dukes and dignitaries whom he was passing on Pall Mall might be able to enter a throne-room more loftily, but he didn't want to enter a throne-room. He'd rather awe Alec Kynance of the U.A.C. than anybody who'd only inherited the right to be called a king!" (008-006)

The passage is remarkable for what it reveals through what it denies. Sam's assertion that he does not care about European sophistication is so vehement that it betrays how much he does care — how deeply Pall Mall's indifference has wounded him.

## Significance

The Pall Mall walk is the moment when Sam's American identity becomes visible to himself as an identity — not a natural condition but a particular way of being in the world that can be threatened, defended, and ultimately complicated. The walk initiates the cultural self-consciousness that will develop through the rest of the novel, transforming Sam from a man who simply is American into a man who knows what being American means.
