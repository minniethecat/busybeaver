# Atlantic Ocean

The Atlantic Ocean in *Dodsworth* is more than a geographical fact — it is the physical threshold between two worlds, the space of suspension where American identities loosen and European possibilities begin to form. Sam Dodsworth crosses it twice, and each crossing marks a stage in his transformation.

## Description

Lewis renders the Atlantic through Sam's first encounter with the open sea: "the domination of the sea, the insignificance of the great ship and all mankind" (005-001). The ocean is "one universal gray except for a golden gash on the western horizon" (005-001), a vastness that reduces the thirty-two-thousand-ton Ultima to "a floating iron eggshell" (005-004).

For Sam, a man whose experience of water has been limited to lakes and New York ferries, the Atlantic is both threatening and liberating. "The wind filled his chest, exhilarated him" (005-001). The ocean strips away the roles that have defined him — industrialist, husband, Zenith citizen — and leaves him, for the first time in his adult life, accountable only to the elements.

## Significance in the Novel

The Atlantic functions as the novel's great pause — the six-day crossing during which nothing happens and everything changes. Away from the factory, the bank, the club, the familiar network of obligations, Sam begins to discover a self that exists independently of his social roles. The ocean is the novel's purest space of possibility.

The return crossing, when Sam sails for America alone while Fran plans her Montreux summer, reverses the pattern. The Atlantic is now a passage home rather than a passage away — but the home Sam returns to is no longer the one he left. The ocean has delivered him to a self that Zenith cannot contain.

## Key Events

**The eastward crossing (Chapters V–XII)**: Sam's first experience of the open sea — the awakening of a sensibility that will develop throughout the European journey.

**The westward return (Chapters XXVIII–XXIX)**: Sam crosses the Atlantic alone, returning for his Yale reunion — a different man than the one who sailed east.

## Characters Associated

Sam Dodsworth discovers the sea. Fran Dodsworth discovers shipboard society. The Atlantic itself is the novel's most impersonal and most transformative character.
