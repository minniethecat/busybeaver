# Sam Buys Five Hats

Sam Dodsworth's purchase of five hats in a single London afternoon is the novel's smallest and most revealing comic episode. It captures, in miniature, the American traveler's anxious negotiation with European sophistication — the desire to belong, the knowledge that one does not, and the compensatory overcorrection that reveals both.

## The Episode

Sam, disoriented by London and uncertain of his place in it, responds to his first full day in the city by buying five hats in a single afternoon — "beginning the Europeanization which he wasn't going to begin" (008-016). The hats are a small rebellion against his own self-image: the industrialist who does not care about appearances, the American who scorns European affectation. Five hats in one afternoon is the behavior of a man who cares very much indeed.

Lewis handles the episode with the lightest of touches — it occupies barely a sentence — but the hats recur throughout the London section as a running joke at Sam's expense. They are a reminder that Sam's resistance to European sophistication is not as secure as he believes, and that his American identity is more fragile — and more negotiable — than he admits.

## Significance

The five hats are the novel's most economical statement of its central theme: the American encounter with Europe changes the American, whether he intends it to or not. Sam buys the hats ironically, or semi-ironically, but he buys them — and in buying them, he begins a process of transformation that the European journey will carry far beyond haberdashery.

The episode also reveals Lewis's characteristic method: the largest themes expressed through the smallest details. A man buys five hats, and an entire cultural encounter is made visible.
