# Meeting Edith Cortright

Sam Dodsworth's encounter with Edith Cortright at the Ercole estate near Venice is the event that gives the novel its resolution. After the wreckage of his marriage — Berlin, the crisis, the acceptance that Fran has chosen a life without him — Sam meets a woman who offers not passion but presence, not romance but recognition. Their relationship, developed in the Venetian chapters, is the novel's argument that the deepest forms of intimacy are built on mutual appreciation rather than need.

## The Meeting

Edith Cortright is an American expatriate who has made a life in Venice on her own terms. She is cultivated without being pretentious, independent without being defensive, and — most important for Sam — capable of seeing him clearly. Unlike Fran, who has always measured Sam against an ideal he cannot meet, Edith accepts him as he is: a man of industry and sincerity, learning to pay attention to a world beyond the factory floor.

Their meeting at the Ercole estate is rendered without melodrama. There is no thunderbolt, no declaration — only conversation, shared meals, walks in the garden, the gradual accumulation of mutual regard. Lewis's restraint here is essential to the novel's argument: the deepest connections are built not in crisis but in quiet, not in passion but in daily attention.

## Significance

The meeting with Edith Cortright is the novel's answer to the Berlin crisis. Where Berlin represented the end of Sam's old life — defined by marriage, industry, and American provincialism — Venice represents the beginning of a new one, defined by companionship, attention, and cosmopolitan ease.

Edith is not a replacement for Fran; she is an alternative to everything Fran represented. The relationship Lewis imagines for Sam and Edith is not the beginning of another marriage but the discovery of a different kind of connection — one that does not consume the people who share it.
