# Major Clyde Lockert

Major Clyde Lockert is the first European character to engage Sam Dodsworth seriously, and he serves as the novel's bridge figure — a man comfortable in both the American world of practical achievement and the European world of ironic self-awareness. He is Lewis's demonstration that the transatlantic encounter need not be hostile.

## Biographical Sketch

Lockert is introduced on the Atlantic crossing, a veteran of the British War Mission who now grows cocoa in British Guiana. He is described as having a "stolidly loquacious" manner — by turns adventurous and domestic, worldly and provincial. He lives with a "bouncing old retired general" cousin in Kent when he is home, and his cousin "roars around on a shocking old motor bike — mustache and dignity flying in the morning breeze" (005-057).

Lockert is "insanely pro-American" — "am myself, except for your appalling ice water" — which makes him an ideal intermediary between Sam's American sincerity and European sophistication. He can mock American customs while genuinely admiring American achievements, a balance that the more reflexively anti-American Europeans in the novel cannot manage.

## Role in the Novel

Lockert's role is to introduce Sam — and the reader — to the pleasures and complexities of European social life without the hostility that characterizes Fran's later encounters. He is the friendly face of the Old World: curious about America, honest about England's limitations, and possessed of a self-deprecating irony that Sam finds both baffling and attractive.

It is Lockert who delivers some of the novel's sharpest observations about the transatlantic dynamic. His remark that "the English are essentially more insufferable than the Americans" (005-085) — because the English assume their superiority rather than asserting it — is one of Lewis's most quoted lines. And his question to Fran — "Why is it that you Americans, the nice ones, are so much more snobbish than the English?" (005-077) — cuts to the heart of the novel's critique of American cultural insecurity.

## Relationships

Lockert's relationship with Sam is one of genuine, if temporary, friendship. They bond over "labor turnover," night driving, and Bobby Jones's golf — the universal language of male camaraderie in the 1920s (005-058). Sam finds Lockert's company reassuring: proof that a European can be a "regular fellow" without ceasing to be European.

Lockert's relationship with Fran is more complicated. He is attracted to her, and she to him, but the flirtation is contained by Lockert's fundamental decency and Fran's awareness that a cocoa planter, however charming, is not the titled aristocrat she seeks.

## Key Scenes

**The shipboard introduction (Chapter V)**: "Pleased to meet you." (Like most Europeans, Lockert believed that all Americans of all classes always said "Pleased to meet you" (005-057)). The comic misunderstanding that opens a genuine connection.

**The transatlantic debate (Chapter V)**: Lockert's wide-ranging conversation with Sam and Fran — about America, England, snobbery, and self-knowledge — is the novel's first sustained engagement with its central themes, and Lockert is the novel's most articulate spokesman for the European perspective.

## Character Analysis

Lockert is a minor character who punches above his narrative weight. He appears primarily in the Atlantic crossing chapters, but his observations echo through the rest of the novel. He is Lewis's demonstration that the European perspective need not be hostile — that irony and self-awareness are not incompatible with genuine warmth — and his departure from the narrative leaves Sam without a European interlocutor he can trust.

Lockert is also, in his way, as representative a type as Sam: the post-imperial Englishman, at home everywhere and nowhere, charming but rootless, a man whose competence (growing cocoa in Guiana, serving in the War Mission) does not quite add up to a life. He is Sam's mirror — a man defined by what he does rather than who he is — and his friendship with Sam is the novel's brief, hopeful suggestion that the Atlantic can be crossed by more than ships.
