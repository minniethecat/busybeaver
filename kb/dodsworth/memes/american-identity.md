# American Identity

The central cultural tension in *Dodsworth*: the clash between American practicality, optimism, and industrial energy on one hand, and European tradition, sophistication, and social hierarchy on the other. Sam Dodsworth serves as Sinclair Lewis's vehicle for exploring what it means to be American when removed from American soil.

## Definition

American identity in *Dodsworth* is defined less by abstract patriotism than by a set of ingrained habits and assumptions: the belief in industry as a measure of worth, the democratic instinct that resists inherited hierarchy, the "yearning to become acquainted" that cuts across class lines, and an uneasy relationship with the cultural authority of Europe. Lewis presents these traits not as superiority but as the inescapable framework through which his protagonist experiences the world (002-021).

Sam embodies what Lewis calls "perfectly, the American Captain of Industry" — a type defined by Republican politics, high tariff, and a moderate Episcopal faith. But Lewis immediately complicates the stereotype: Sam is "none of the things which most Europeans and many Americans expect in a leader of American industry. He was not a Babbitt, not a Rotarian, not an Elk, not a deacon" (002-023). He is the American type, but with enough self-awareness to feel its limits.

## Context in the Novel

The theme emerges most sharply when Sam is extracted from Zenith — the Midwestern city that is the natural habitat of his American identity — and placed in the transatlantic world of steamers, London clubs, Paris salons, and Venetian palazzos. On the ship to England, Sam displays "the American yearning to become acquainted, to tell all about his achievements, not as boasting but to establish himself as a worthy fellow" (005-055). This impulse, natural and even charming in Zenith, becomes a liability among Europeans who read it as vulgarity.

The most explicit articulation of the theme comes not from Sam but from Major Clyde Herndon, the Englishman who tells Fran: "you're too American, Mrs. Dodsworth. Americans understand themselves less and are less understood by the world than any nation that's ever existed" (009-009). Herndon's speech — asserting that Americans excel at the things they're supposed to lack (lyric poetry, formal manners) and fail at the things they're supposed to master (fast motoring, pioneering) — is Lewis's sharpest ironic commentary on the transatlantic identity question.

Sam's journey is punctuated by moments of both patriotic defiance and self-conscious insecurity. When he first glimpses England from the ship, his imagination floods with "Mother England! Land of his ancestors" — a reverie shaped as much by *Ivanhoe* and *Oliver Twist* as by any real knowledge of the country. He is, Lewis suggests, an American schoolboy still, however many millions he has made (006-020).

## Key Passages

> Samuel Dodsworth was, perfectly, the American Captain of Industry, believing in the Republican Party, high tariff and, so long as they did not annoy him personally, in prohibition and the Episcopal Church. He was the president of the Revelation Motor Company; he was a millionaire, though decidedly not a multimillionaire; his large house was on Ridge Crest, the most fashionable street in Zenith; he had some taste in etchings; he did not split many infinitives; and he sometimes enjoyed Beethoven. He would certainly (so the observer assumed) produce excellent motor cars; he would make impressive speeches to the salesmen; but he would never love passionately, lose tragically, nor sit in contented idleness upon tropic shores. (002-021)

> To define what Sam Dodsworth was, at fifty, it is easiest to state what he was not. He was none of the things which most Europeans and many Americans expect in a leader of American industry. He was not a Babbitt, not a Rotarian, not an Elk, not a deacon. He rarely shouted, never slapped people on the back, and he had attended only six baseball games since 1900. He knew, and thoroughly, the Babbitts and baseball fans, but only in business. (002-023)

> "you're too American, Mrs. Dodsworth. Americans understand themselves less and are less understood by the world than any nation that's ever existed. You're excellent at all the things in which you're supposed to be lacking — lyric poetry, formal manners, lack of cupidity. And you're so timid and incompetent at the things in which you're supposed to excel — fast motoring, aviation, efficiency in business, pioneering" (009-009)

> He knew that in America, even in the Midwestern saneness of Zenith, where the factories and skyscrapers were not too far from the healing winds across the cornfields, an incredible anarchy had crept into the family life which, he believed, had been the foundation of American greatness. (007-060)

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis uses Sam Dodsworth to work through a paradox at the heart of early twentieth-century American identity. Sam's American virtues — his industry, his sincerity, his impatience with pretense — are genuine, and Lewis does not mock them. But those same virtues leave Sam vulnerable: his American faith in hard work and straightforward dealing cannot navigate the layered social codes of Europe, nor can it save his marriage from Fran's restless ambition.

The novel refuses both the easy chauvinism of "Europe? Rats! Dead's a doornail!" (003-016) and the reflexive self-hatred of Americans who "travel principally to get away from their own people" (005-073). Instead, Lewis traces a middle path: American identity is a real thing, with real strengths, but it is also a kind of innocence — an innocence that the European journey both shatters and, in the end, transforms into something wiser.

Sam's ultimate trajectory suggests that American identity, properly understood, is not something to be discarded or defended but something to be outgrown — or rather, integrated into a larger self that can hold both Zenith and the Grand Canal, both the assembly plant and the Romanesque church.
