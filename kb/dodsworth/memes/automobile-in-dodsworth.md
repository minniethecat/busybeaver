# The Automobile in Dodsworth

The automobile is the material and symbolic foundation of [[sam-dodsworth|Sam Dodsworth]]'s identity. He is a man who builds cars — first at the Zenith Locomotive Works, then as founder and president of the Revelation Motor Company — and the automobile represents everything he believes about American progress, craftsmanship, and his own worth. When the Revelation Company is absorbed by the Unit Automotive Company, Sam loses not merely a job but the organizing principle of his adult life.

## The Automobile as American Dream

The novel opens in 1903 with automobiles as novelties — "three guests had come in these newfangled automobiles, for it was now 1903, the climax of civilization. A fourth automobile was approaching, driven by Samuel Dodsworth." Tub Pearson jeers, "My dad says automobiles are nothing but a fad. Cost too much to run. In five years, he says, they'll disappear." Sam's reply — "I'll bet that some day automobiles'll run forty!" — marks him as a visionary and a gambler. He is not merely a manufacturer; he is a believer in the automobile as the vehicle of American modernity.

Sam's industrial philosophy is democratic: he "insisted that the largest profits would lie in selling automobiles as cheaply as possible to as many customers as possible" (001-067). This is not the logic of luxury but the logic of mass prosperity — the automobile as an instrument of social leveling, available to the many rather than the few.

## The Identity Crisis

Sam's existential crisis is triggered by the sale of the Revelation Company to [[alec-kynance|Alec Kynance]] and the U.A.C. The automobile is so thoroughly fused with his identity that without it, he becomes unmoored:

> "He was chained by every dollar he had made, every automobile he had manufactured — they meant a duty to his caste. He was chained by every hour he had worked — they had left him stiff, spiritually rheumatic." (017-031)

The metaphor is striking: the automobiles he built, which once represented freedom and motion, have become chains. The very hours of labor that gave his life structure have made him "spiritually rheumatic" — stiff, inflexible, unable to bend into a new shape.

## Motoring as European Experience

In Europe, the automobile takes on a different meaning. It becomes associated with leisure, tourism, and the freedom Sam is supposed to be enjoying — but cannot quite inhabit. Fran dreams of "Motoring through the Tyrol" as part of her European fantasy, but when Sam travels independently, the automobile recedes as a symbol. He travels by train, by ship, on foot — the man who built cars no longer needs to drive one to define himself.

| Phase | Automobile's Meaning | Sam's State |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| Zenith (1903–1920s) | Industrial ambition, democratic progress | Builder, creator, believer |
| After the Sale | Lost identity, chains of past labor | Adrift, obsolete |
| European Journey | Leisure, tourism (diminished importance) | Observer, tourist |

## The Automobile and American Exceptionalism

The automobile is also Lewis's vehicle for examining American exceptionalism. [[fran-dodsworth|Fran Dodsworth]] is described as "an automobile's headlight" by a European observer (022-023) — brilliant, forward-moving, but harsh and unsubtle. The metaphor cuts both ways: American energy illuminates but also blinds. Sam's cars are well-made, democratic, efficient — and yet they have consumed his life and left him, at fifty, with nothing to do.
