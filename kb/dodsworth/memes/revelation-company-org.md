# Revelation Motor Company (Organization)

The Revelation Motor Company — Sam Dodsworth's creation, his life's work, and the achievement that defined him — is the novel's central economic entity. As an organization, distinct from the glass-and-marble building that housed it, the Revelation Company represents the entrepreneurial capitalism that formed Sam's character and the American world he inhabits.

## Description

The Revelation Company is rendered through its position in the market: positioned between the Chromecar and the Highroad after the U.A.C. acquisition, "drop it three hundred below your price — two-door sedan at eleven-fifty" (002-004). It occupies a specific niche — quality, not luxury; reliable, not glamorous — that mirrors Sam's own character. The company is Sam made metal: solid, competent, slightly unimaginative, and genuinely valuable.

The organization's culture is suggested through its physical space: the "acre of little tables with typists and typists and typists, very busy, and clerks and clerks and clerks, with rattling papers" (002-003), the private offices with their "enormous desks in imitation of refectory tables" (002-003). It is an organization of productive energy and mild pretension — prosperous, earnest, slightly naive about its own importance.

## Role in the Novel

The Revelation Company is what Sam loses — and what he must learn to live without. Its absorption by the U.A.C. is the economic fact that makes the novel possible, but the company's significance extends beyond the plot. It represents a particular form of American life: the life organized around work, measured by achievement, defined by the enterprise one builds.

The novel's central question — who is Sam Dodsworth without his company? — is unanswerable within the company's terms. The European journey is, in part, the search for a vocabulary in which that question can be answered.
