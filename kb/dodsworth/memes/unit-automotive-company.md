# Unit Automotive Company (U.A.C.)

The Unit Automotive Company, universally referred to in the novel as "the U.A.C.," is the industrial giant whose absorption of Sam Dodsworth's Revelation Motor Company sets the entire plot in motion. Lewis presents the U.A.C. as the embodiment of 1920s American industrial consolidation — the force that makes men like Sam wealthy and purposeless in a single transaction.

## Description

Lewis renders the U.A.C. through its scale: "seven makes of motors, its body-building works, its billion dollars of capital" (002-002). It is the new order of American industry — the conglomerate that absorbs the individual entrepreneur, the corporation that makes the founder redundant. Its president, Alec Kynance, arrives in Zenith not as a predator but as a recognizer of value — the U.A.C. wants what Sam built, and it is willing to pay.

The company's vastness is both its power and its impersonality. It can offer Sam wealth; it cannot offer him purpose. The acquisition that makes him rich also makes him, for the first time in his adult life, unnecessary.

## Role in the Novel

The U.A.C. functions as the novel's economic engine. Without its acquisition of the Revelation Company, Sam remains in Zenith — defined by work, his marriage continuing in its established pattern. The U.A.C. liberates him from the factory floor and confronts him with the question that will define the rest of the novel: who is Sam Dodsworth without his company?

The U.A.C. also represents the larger forces transforming American life in the 1920s: consolidation, professionalization, the replacement of the founder-entrepreneur with the corporate manager. Sam's displacement is personal, but it is also historical — he belongs to an era of American capitalism that is passing.

## Key People

Alec Kynance, president of the U.A.C., is the face of the new order: corporate, efficient, impersonal. His interactions with Sam are cordial but transactional — he is buying an asset, not making a friend. Sam Dodsworth, briefly, becomes a U.A.C. executive before recognizing that the corporate life is not the life he wants.

## Significance

The U.A.C. is the economic fact that makes the novel possible. It is also a study in the relationship between work and identity: what does a man become when the work that defined him is taken away? The novel's answer — that he must discover a self that exists independently of his productive role — is the journey the U.A.C. inaugurates.
