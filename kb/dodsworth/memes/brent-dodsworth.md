# Brent Dodsworth

Brent Dodsworth is the son of [[sam-dodsworth|Sam Dodsworth]] and [[fran-dodsworth|Fran Dodsworth]], and the younger brother of [[emily-dodsworth|Emily Dodsworth]]. A junior at [[yale-university|Yale University]] when the novel begins, Brent embodies the confident, self-contained next generation whose independence leaves Sam with the painful recognition that his children no longer need him.

## Biographical Sketch

Born around 1909, Brent is the second child of Sam and Fran, raised in the Dodsworth home on Ridgecrest Avenue in [[zenith|Zenith]] (001-065). As a child, he joins Emily in their playful ritual with Sam — wailing "Not seriowsly, I trust" in imitation of their father's pronunciation (003-055). But where Emily was Sam's tomboy companion, Brent was "the earnest young Brent" — more serious, more reserved, less given to the easy intimacy his sister shared with their father (003-056).

By the time the novel opens, Brent is a junior at Yale, following in his father's collegiate footsteps. When Sam returns from Europe midway through the novel, Brent is finishing his examinations and preparing to graduate into a world of bond sales, yachting parties, and the self-assurance of the privileged young American male.

## Role in the Novel

Brent appears in only a few chapters, but his narrative function is precise: he is the second half of the generational equation that leaves Sam feeling obsolete. Along with Emily, Brent represents everything Sam has worked for — and everything that no longer requires Sam's presence. His polite but firm independence from his father is, in its way, as devastating to Sam as Fran's emotional abandonment.

When Brent cancels his planned fortnight in Zenith to join a "corking yachting party" in Nova Scotia, Sam composes his reply with "perfectly expressionless" face — "By all means go hope have splendid time" — then "sighed a little, and stood with his hands in his pockets, looking up and down the street, a man with nothing to do" (018-085). The moment crystallizes the novel's central emotional truth: the successful father is the father who is no longer needed.

## Key Scenes

**The New York dinner (Chapter XVII)**: The novel's most sustained portrait of Brent. He arrives from New Haven for a twelve-hour visit with his father, bringing dinner clothes (017-066). Over dinner at an imitation German restaurant — "beer mugs made in Pennsylvania; beams stained to look old" — Sam tries to initiate an intimate father-son conversation about drinking, gambling, and women (017-072). But he is chilled by Brent's self-confident demeanor: "the boy might think he was in Bad Taste" (017-073). Instead of the frank talk Sam imagined, he drones about European travel while Brent "was bright with compliments about Sam's knowledge of Europe" — the polite distance of the young toward the old (017-090).

Sam describes Brent as "like a young race horse, his pale face and high thin forehead almost too bred-down, too refined. But there was health and buoyancy in his humorous eyes" (017-056). The description captures both Sam's pride and his unease: Brent is a finer model than his father — more elegant, more polished — and belongs to a world Sam cannot enter.

**The cancelled visit (Chapter XVIII)**: After Brent telegraphs that he has been "Invited corking yachting party Nova Scotia mind if not return," Sam sends his reply and walks out of the Western Union office a diminished man (018-085).

**The career conversation (Chapter XVII)**: Sam tentatively proposes that Brent might join his parents in Europe after graduation rather than immediately starting work. Brent politely declines — he has already arranged a position selling bonds, with an architecture degree to follow. When Sam suggests that selling bonds might not satisfy a builder's instinct, Brent responds with "the modest confidence, the eager eyes, of a young poet announcing that he was going to write an epic" (017-083). The generational values are incommensurable: Sam built cars; Brent will sell financial instruments.

## Character Analysis

Brent is less fully realized than Emily — he appears in fewer scenes and speaks fewer memorable lines — but his symbolic weight in the novel is substantial. Where Emily represents the domestic competence that makes Sam superfluous, Brent represents the professional succession that makes him obsolete. Together they form the double verdict on Sam's life: he has raised children who do not need him, and that is both his greatest achievement and his deepest wound.

Lewis is careful not to make Brent unsympathetic. He is not cruel or dismissive; he is merely self-contained, self-directed, and wholly at ease in a world that Sam can observe but not inhabit. When Sam finally admits "that business did not need him... just as Brent did not need him, Emily did not need him" (018-108), the triple negation is the novel's most compressed statement of its existential theme.

> Key passages illuminating Brent's character:
>
> "Brent was like a young race horse, his pale face and high thin forehead almost too bred-down, too refined. But there was health and buoyancy in his humorous eyes, his shout of 'Hello, Dad! Swell to see you again. Good crossing?'" (017-056)
>
> "He wanted Brent's affection and respect more than that of anyone in the world. So, in parental fear, while he would have liked to expose his soul, he droned about Lord Herndon, Gioserro the aviator, the palace at Versailles." (017-073)

Brent Dodsworth is the future that Sam built but cannot live in — a young man whose very competence is an indictment of the generation that made him possible.
