Herr Dr. Biedner is a Berlin lawyer and a second cousin of [[Fran Dodsworth]] through her father [[Herman Voelker]]'s German family connections. Though a minor character in terms of page-time, his narrative importance is outsized: it is at Biedner's dinner party that Fran meets [[Kurt von Obersdorf]], the man who will become her second husband and precipitate the final dissolution of the Dodsworths' marriage.

## Biographical Sketch

Biedner is a successful Berlin *Rechtsanwalt* (lawyer) who lives in a comfortable flat "just off the Tiergarten" (022-001). Lewis describes him as "very Prussian, with close-cropped hair" (022-001), suggesting the disciplined, formal German professional class of the Weimar era. In the spring of 1927, Berlin was experiencing a period of relative prosperity, and "Herr Biedner had an excellent law practise, and his home was as thick with comfort as a coffeecake with sugar" (022-002).

His household includes his wife and a grandchild, and his flat is decorated with an impressive array of German domestic comforts: an arched hallway, a porcelain stove ("Sam was edified to discover that a porcelain stove really could heat a room"), and a piano—"the pianist of the family was not Frau Biedner or some unrevealed daughter but Herr Biedner himself" (022-003).

## Role in the Novel

Biedner's narrative function is that of the unwitting catalyst. His dinner party, intended as a gesture of family hospitality toward his American cousins, brings together Fran and Kurt von Obersdorf. With what Lewis dryly calls "apparent gratification, Biedner had introduced him as Count Obersdorf, taking Sam aside to explain that Kurt von Obersdorf was the present head of an old Hungarian family" (022-008).

The irony is rich: Biedner, the respectable family connection, inadvertently sets in motion the affair that will destroy his cousin's marriage. He is as unaware of the consequences as the Dodsworths themselves are at that moment. In this sense, Biedner is a classic Lewis character—a decent, ordinary person whose ordinary act of hospitality becomes the hinge on which a larger drama turns.

> The guests Biedner assembles—"half-dozen German businessmen and their wives"—speak English and are "so kind," but "they talked of things which meant nothing to Sam" (022-005). The cultural gap between German professional society and the American businessman is palpable.

## Key Scenes

**The dinner party (Chapter 22)** — Biedner hosts the dinner at his Tiergarten flat, assembling German businessmen and their wives to greet "his American cousins." The evening is culturally awkward for Sam, who understands little of the conversation, but it is momentous: Kurt von Obersdorf, seated beside Fran at dinner, begins the connection that will transform all their lives. Biedner proudly introduces Kurt and later vouches for his character, a recommendation that proves fateful.

## Character Analysis

Biedner is a background figure, but Lewis sketches him with characteristic precision. He is prosperous, cultured (he plays the piano himself), devoted to family (the grandchild is a fixture of the household), and generous in his hospitality. His Prussian formality masks genuine warmth—"the good Herr Rechtsanwalt Biedner" (022-001) is not merely a satirical tag but an accurate description.

> The Biedners represent a Germany that Sam can respect but never fully inhabit—stable, cultured, rooted in traditions he does not share. In vouching for Kurt, Biedner acts in good faith; the tragedy (for the Dodsworths) lies not in anyone's malice but in the ordinary operations of social life.
