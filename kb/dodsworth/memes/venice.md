# Venice

Venice is the novel's city of resolution — the place where Sam, alone and estranged from Fran, discovers that he can be at home in the world without being defined by work or marriage. It is here that he meets Edith Cortright and begins to imagine a future not organized around loss.

## Description

Lewis's Venice is rendered with an affection unusual in his prose: a city of canals and churches, of expatriate domesticity and Italian rhythms, of beauty that does not demand to be understood but simply rewards attention. The Ercole estate, with its "half a dozen villas" and "smiling Italian servants" (034-001), provides the setting for Sam's gradual recovery from the wreckage of his marriage.

Unlike Paris, which demands social performance, Venice permits solitude. Sam learns to sit alone in cafés, to look at buildings without needing to have an opinion about them, to let the city's beauty work on him without translating it into an achievement. This capacity — to be still, to pay attention, to be alone without being lonely — is the novel's definition of maturity.

## Significance in the Novel

Venice is the novel's answer to the question that has driven the entire plot: what comes after? After the company is sold, after the marriage collapses, after the European journey has stripped Sam of everything that defined him — what remains? Venice's answer is: beauty, companionship, the simple fact of being alive in a beautiful place with someone who sees you.

Edith Cortright, the American expatriate Sam meets in Venice, is essential to this answer. She offers not passion but presence, not romance but recognition. Their relationship, developed in the Venetian chapters, is the novel's argument that the deepest forms of intimacy are built not on need but on mutual appreciation — the capacity to share a life without consuming each other.

## Key Events

**Sam alone in Venice**: His discovery that solitude in a beautiful place is not punishment but possibility.

**Meeting Edith Cortright**: The relationship that becomes the novel's resolution — not a replacement for Fran but a different kind of connection entirely.

**The Ercole estate (Chapter XXXIV)**: Sam discovers a "surprisingly vigorous and outdoor Edith" (034-001) and begins to imagine a shared future.

## Characters Associated

Sam finds himself here; Edith Cortright offers the companionship he has been seeking. Venice itself — its beauty, its patience, its indifference to American achievement — is a character in its own right.
