# Modernity and Industry

The world of *Dodsworth* is shaped by the automobile — the machine that made Sam Dodsworth's fortune, transformed American life, and stands as the novel's central metaphor for the energies and anxieties of industrial modernity.

## Definition

Modernity in *Dodsworth* is experienced primarily through the lens of industrial capitalism: the rise of the automobile, the consolidation of small manufacturers into giant corporations, and the transformation of craftsmen-entrepreneurs into executives and "office boys." Sam Dodsworth is a transitional figure — a man who helped build the industrial age but finds himself displaced by its next phase.

The novel opens with the automobile as a symbol of exhilarating possibility: Sam drives his first car in 1903, "with a feeling of power, of dominating the universe, at twelve dizzy miles an hour." By 1925, that same industry has consolidated into the "imperial U.A.C., with its seven makes of motors, its body-building works, its billion dollars of capital" — a machine too large for any single human being to steer.

## Context in the Novel

The theme is established immediately through the sale of Sam's Revelation Motor Company to the Unit Automotive Company. This transaction, which makes Sam wealthier than ever, also strips him of his identity. He recognizes that at the U.A.C. he would be "nothing more than an office boy" who could "make no daring decisions by himself" (002-057).

Sam's European journey is made possible by industrial wealth, but it is also an attempt to find a self beyond that wealth. The question the novel poses — never quite answered — is whether the industrial virtues Sam embodies (efficiency, practicality, the "scientific efficiency" he brings to daily life) are transferable to the project of living well, or whether they become obstacles to the kind of self-knowledge that Europe demands.

The contrast between American and European attitudes toward industry is sharp. The American businessman who declares "More art in a good shiny spark-plug than in all the fat Venus de Mylos they ever turned out" (003-016) is crude but not entirely wrong — Lewis allows that there IS art in American industry, a kind of creative energy that Europe's museum-culture cannot match. The problem is not that Sam made motor cars; it is that making motor cars was the only thing he knew how to do.

He would certainly (so the observer assumed) produce excellent motor cars; he would make impressive speeches to the salesmen; but he would never love passionately, lose tragically, nor sit in contented idleness upon tropic shores. (002-021)

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis is both a chronicler and a critic of American industrial modernity. He understands the genuine achievement — the cars, the factories, the organized productivity that made America the economic wonder of the early twentieth century — but he also perceives the human cost. Sam Dodsworth is a man who has given his life to building machines and finds, at fifty, that machines cannot answer the questions he is now asking.

The novel's treatment of industry is connected to its treatment of masculinity. Sam's identity as a "captain of industry" is gendered: it is how he has understood what it means to be a man, a provider, a person of consequence. When the U.A.C. absorbs his company, it absorbs part of his manhood. The European journey is, among other things, a search for a new way of being male — one that can include art, leisure, and emotional vulnerability without shame.

The resolution is characteristically ambivalent. Sam does not reject industry — he remains proud of what he built — but he learns to live beyond it. His eventual decision to return to America (with Edith) is not a retreat into business but an embrace of a fuller life that includes work without being defined by it.
