# Paris Social Season

The Dodsworths' Paris sojourn is the novel's most sustained exploration of marital estrangement. In Paris, Fran's pursuit of European sophistication becomes the dominant fact of their daily life, and Sam's exclusion from that life becomes absolute. It is here, rather than in Berlin, that the Dodsworth marriage effectively ends — Berlin merely formalizes what Paris has already accomplished.

## The Social World

Fran's Paris is the Paris of salons, hotels, and fashionable circles — not the tourist's city of monuments and museums but the socialite's city of cultivated conversation and strategic acquaintance. Through Renée de Pénable, Fran gains access to a world of titled Europeans and intellectual Americans that represents everything she hoped Europe would deliver.

Sam, who does not speak French and cannot read the social codes Fran navigates with increasing fluency, is excluded from this world. "Between times, he sneaked off, like a small boy going to the circus, to low places" (028-001) — seeking out the American correspondent Ross Ireland, the one person in Paris who speaks his language in every sense.

## Key Figures

Renée de Pénable is Fran's patron and model — the European woman Fran aspires to become. Arnold Israel represents the cultivated European intellectual. Endicott Everett Atkins, with his "appalling, reproving, Christian Science sort of poise" (027-001), is the American-expatriate-turned-European who embodies everything Sam finds alienating about the Paris world. Ross Ireland, the Iowan correspondent who cannot learn any language "save Iowan" (028-002), is Sam's only comfort — an American who refuses to be Europeanized.

## Significance

Paris is where the novel's central marital dynamic reaches its most intense expression. Fran is thriving in a world that has no place for Sam; Sam is discovering that the world Fran inhabits is one he neither can nor wishes to join. The Paris social season transforms a marriage of accommodation into a marriage of parallel lives — and parallel lives, Lewis suggests, are no marriage at all.
