# Pall Mall

Pall Mall, the London thoroughfare famous for its gentlemen's clubs, appears in *Dodsworth* at the moment of Sam's sharpest alienation from European society. Walking past "the aloof and white-faced clubs of Pall Mall" (008-005), Sam confronts the reality of his situation: a man who was Someone in Zenith is, in London, invisible.

## Description

Lewis's Pall Mall is rendered through Sam's defensive perception: the clubs are "aloof and white-faced" (008-005), the passersby are "unhurried, well-dressed people who so thoroughly ignored him" (008-005). The architecture of exclusivity — buildings that refuse to explain themselves, that assume their own importance without argument — is the visual expression of the class system Sam cannot penetrate.

The street triggers one of the novel's most revealing passages of American self-assertion: "Pictures? Why talk stupidly about pictures when he could talk intelligently about engines? Languages? If he had nothing to say, what was the good of saying it in three languages? Manners? These presumable dukes and dignitaries whom he was passing on Pall Mall might be able to enter a throne-room more loftily, but he didn't want to enter a throne-room" (008-006). The defensiveness of this passage is its point — Sam is arguing with himself as much as with Pall Mall.

## Significance in the Novel

Pall Mall is the novel's most concentrated image of English social exclusivity. Unlike the country house weekend, where Sam is an invited guest, Pall Mall offers no welcome — it is a world that does not even notice him. His response — "he'd rather awe Alec Kynance of the U.A.C. than anybody who'd only inherited the right to be called a king" (008-006) — is both defiant and revealing. The terms of his self-worth have been set by American achievement, and in Pall Mall those terms have no currency.

The street also marks the first appearance of a pattern that will recur throughout the novel: Sam, excluded, finding reassurance in American institutions — in this case, the Guaranty Trust Company window, "looking very busy and cheerful behind the wide windows" (008-005).

## Key Events

**The Pall Mall walk (Chapter VIII)**: Sam, invisible among the English, seeks the reassurance of an American bank window — the novel's emblematic image of transatlantic dislocation.

## Characters Associated

Sam Dodsworth's solitary walk along Pall Mall is one of the novel's definitive images of his character: excluded, defensive, self-aware, and unexpectedly resilient.
