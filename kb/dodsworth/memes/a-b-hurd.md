A. B. Hurd is the manager of the London agency of the [[Revelation Automobile Company]] in Sinclair Lewis's novel *Dodsworth*. An American who had lived in England for six years at the time of the Dodsworths' first visit, Hurd embodies the tension between genuine American business competence and the social pretensions that characters like [[Fran Dodsworth]] find embarrassing.

## Biographical Sketch

Hurd is an American businessman posted to London to run the Revelation Motor Company's English operations. By the time [[Sam Dodsworth]] and Fran arrive in England on their European tour, Hurd has lived there for six years and has adopted what he believes to be English manners, though the narrative suggests he remains unmistakably American. He is married (Mrs. Hurd lives at Beaconsfield, outside London) and is professionally successful—Sam regards him as "a whirlwind at selling" (007-040).

Physically, Hurd is described as "a round-faced, horn-spectacled, heavy-voiced man who believed that he had become so English in manner and speech that no one could possibly take him for an American" (008-022). His given initials, A. B., are revealed when Sam invites him to lunch at the Savoy Grill (008-018).

## Role in the Novel

Hurd serves a dual narrative function. On one level, he is a competent business associate who demonstrates Sam's continued connection to the world of American industry during the Dodsworths' European travels. On another level, he functions as a social foil: Fran's disdain for Hurd reveals the depth of her social climbing and her rejection of the American business class from which she came.

> Fran protests when Sam mentions Hurd: "Oh, he—he's a terrible person—absolute roughneck—how you ever happened to send an American like Hurd over here when you might have had a nice Englishman as London agent" (007-039).

Sam, in contrast, defends Hurd warmly: "Now Hurd's a mighty good fellow! He's cocky, and I don't suppose he's read a book since he used to look at the lingerie ads in the Sears-Roebuck catalogue as a kid, but he's a whirlwind at selling" (007-040).

## Key Scenes

**First mention (Chapter 6)** — Sam feels a sense of belonging in London partly because "of course Hurd, the manager of the London Revelation agency" is there (006-011). Hurd represents a familiar touchstone of American business in an unfamiliar city.

**The Savoy Grill lunch (Chapter 8)** — Sam invites Hurd to lunch at the Savoy. Hurd proudly explains his philosophy of dealing with the English: "is not to bother 'em, like the Americans do, but let 'em alone when they want to be let alone" (008-027). Fran is "fairly amiable" (008-019) about meeting him, but her politeness masks condescension. She cannot help being "a little over-courteous" (008-021), treating him with the manner one might use toward a servant about to be dismissed.

**The abandoned follow-up** — After the lunch, Hurd's office is closed and his home address is not in the telephone book (007-046–047), and the promised second meeting never materializes in the narrative, suggesting the social gap between the Dodsworths' European circle and Hurd's business world proved unbridgeable.

## Character Analysis

Hurd is a minor but telling character in Lewis's satirical portrait of Americans abroad. He represents the type of American businessman who succeeds professionally overseas without ever truly assimilating—he "believed that he had become so English in manner and speech that no one could possibly take him for an American" (008-022) while remaining, to Fran and to the reader, unmistakably American. This self-deception parallels Fran's own pretensions to European sophistication, though Fran would never admit the comparison.

> Hurd's round face and horn spectacles mark him as a comic figure, but his commercial competence is real—he has kept the London agency running for six years. Lewis uses him to show that social graces and professional effectiveness are not always aligned.

Hurd also illuminates Sam's character. Sam's loyalty to Hurd—his insistence that "Hurd's a mighty good fellow"—demonstrates Sam's fundamental decency and his resistance to Fran's snobbery. Where Fran sees only a "roughneck," Sam sees a capable colleague and a fellow American.
