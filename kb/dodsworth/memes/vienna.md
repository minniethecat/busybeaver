# Vienna

Vienna is the ancestral city of [[kurt-von-obersdorf|Kurt von Obersdorf]] and, by extension, the symbolic capital of the European aristocracy that [[fran-dodsworth|Fran Dodsworth]] attempts to enter. It appears in the novel primarily through Kurt's stories and through the Viennese social world he represents, rather than through extended scenic description.

## Role in the Novel

Vienna functions as a cultural signifier rather than a physical setting. Kurt von Obersdorf, Fran's would-be aristocratic husband, is a product of Vienna's social training — what he calls "a small bit of the required training of the real European — German or Swiss or Dutch or whatefer" (023-053). This training, which includes linguistic fluency, social grace, and a deep historical consciousness, is precisely what Fran lacks and what she imagines marrying Kurt would confer upon her.

The city also appears as a node in the Dodsworths' European travels. The itinerary of their restless movement — "to Avignon... to San Sebastian and Madrid and Toledo and Seville... to Arles, Carcassonne, Marseilles, Monte Carlo" — traces a map of displacement in which Vienna is the promised destination that never quite materializes for Fran in the way she hopes. Kurt's mother, the formidable Baroness, embodies Viennese aristocratic values that ultimately find Fran unsuitable as a daughter-in-law.

## The Viennese Connection

Kurt's Vienna represents an Old World order that is simultaneously alluring and inaccessible to the American characters:

| Aspect | Zenith (America) | Vienna (Old World) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Social structure | Achievement-based; money talks | Birth-based; lineage matters |
| Cultural depth | Shallowness ("make-believe" clubs) | Centuries of tradition |
| Kurt's position | A tourist, a suitor | An heir, a Count |
| Fran's position | A wealthy American | An unsuitable foreigner |

Kurt's Viennese training includes a critique of American culture: "What most Europeans think of America! Because we were a pioneer nation, mostly busy with farming and cod-fishing and chewing tobacco, they believe we must be a race of savages" (023-060). His cosmopolitan perspective, shaped by Vienna, allows him to see America's virtues — but his mother's more traditional Viennese values cannot accept Fran.

## Key Scenes

**The Biedner connection (Chapter XXII)**: Kurt mentions "your cousins, the Biedners, they were so very kind to me when I first came from Vienna" (022-031), establishing the transatlantic network of German-speaking emigrants that connects Zenith to Central Europe.

**Kurt's stories of Vienna (Chapter XXIII)**: Kurt entertains the Dodsworths with "stories about the frivolous days of old Vienna" (022-096), painting a seductive picture of an aristocratic world of charm, leisure, and historical depth — a world Fran desperately wants to join but cannot.

**The friend from Vienna (Chapter XXV)**: Sam looks forward "only to a vague dinner with Kurt and a friend from Vienna" (025-001), a phrase that captures how the glamour of European social connections has worn thin for Sam by this point in the novel.
