# Home and Exile

Sam Dodsworth leaves Zenith as an exile from his own life — displaced from his company, estranged from his wife, uncertain of his future. His European journey is a search for a new home, literal and spiritual, and the novel's resolution asks whether home is a place you return to or a place you make.

## Definition

Home in *Dodsworth* is presented as both a geographical fact (Zenith, America) and a psychological condition (belonging, being known, having a role). Sam begins the novel securely at home in both senses: he is a prominent citizen of Zenith and a man comfortable in his own skin. The sale of the Revelation Company and the departure for Europe strip him of both forms of home simultaneously.

Exile is the condition of the European chapters. Sam is not a traveler — travelers have homes to return to — but an exile, someone whose home no longer exists in the form he left it. His wandering through London, Paris, and Venice is a search not for novelty but for a place where he can once again feel like Samuel Dodsworth rather than a tourist in his own life.

The novel's ending suggests that home, in the end, is neither a city nor a nation but a person. Sam finds home not in returning to Zenith but in finding Edith Cortright — an American like himself, living in voluntary exile in Venice, who offers the companionship that makes any place habitable.

## Context in the Novel

The theme is established in the novel's opening image: Sam, in his own house on Ridge Crest, already feeling the first stirrings of exile as his company is absorbed by the U.A.C. His departure from Zenith is not a liberation but an expulsion — he is being pushed out of the life he built by forces (industrial consolidation, Fran's ambition) he cannot control.

The European journey traces the arc from exile to the construction of a new home. Sam's relationship with Edith, which develops slowly across the later chapters, is the novel's answer to the home-and-exile question: home is where someone knows you, and exile is the condition of being unknown.

## Key Passages

> He was no longer Samuel Dodsworth, but merely part of a crowd vigorously pushing one another toward nowhere. (002-057)

Exile begins at home: the loss of the name that located him in the world.

> anything to assure him that he was someone and meant something, here in this city of traditional, unsmiling stateliness, among these unhurried, well-dressed people who so thoroughly ignored him. (008-009)

The exile's condition: to be unseen, to have no one who knows who you are.

## Critical Interpretation

The home-and-exile theme gives *Dodsworth* a resonance beyond its period. It is not just a novel about Americans in Europe; it is a novel about the experience of displacement that modernity makes available to everyone. Sam's loss of home — through corporate consolidation, marital breakdown, cultural dislocation — is a specifically twentieth-century version of an ancient human experience.

Lewis's resolution is characteristically American in its optimism: home can be rebuilt. Sam does not get Zenith back, and he does not get Fran back, but he gets something that matters more — a self that can make a home anywhere, with anyone who truly sees him.
