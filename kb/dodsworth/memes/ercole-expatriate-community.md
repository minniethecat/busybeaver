# Ercole Expatriate Community

The small expatriate community at the Ercole estate, near Venice, is the novel's model of an alternative society — a group of people who have chosen to live not for achievement or status but for beauty, companionship, and the quiet pleasures of daily existence. It is here that Sam Dodsworth discovers the life he has been seeking.

## Description

The Ercole community is rendered through the texture of its daily life: the villas set among gardens and olive groves, the Italian servants providing elegant but unobtrusive care, the residents passing their days in conversation and contemplation rather than commerce. "Half a dozen villas" with "smiling Italian servants" (034-001) — the description is minimal because the community's significance lies not in its physical details but in its values.

Those values represent a radical alternative to everything Sam has known. Where Zenith measures worth by factory output and committee membership, the Ercole community measures it by attention and appreciation. Where the De Pénable circle performs sophistication, the Ercole community simply inhabits it. The difference is the difference between Fran's Europe — anxious, aspirational, performative — and Edith's Europe: settled, genuine, at peace with itself.

## Role in the Novel

The Ercole community is the novel's positive vision — the life it offers as an alternative to the life Sam has led. It is not a repudiation of America (the community includes American expatriates alongside Europeans) but a transcendence of the values — productivity, status, restless ambition — that America has taught Sam to consider universal.

Edith Cortright is the community's exemplary member: an American who has made a life in Europe on her own terms, without losing her American self. Her relationship with Sam, developed within the community's rhythms, is the novel's argument that the deepest forms of belonging are built not on shared nationality but on shared attention.

## Key People

Edith Cortright presides as the community's spirit. Sam Dodsworth arrives as a visitor and discovers a home. The Italian servants and fellow residents form the quiet society that sustains the novel's resolution.
