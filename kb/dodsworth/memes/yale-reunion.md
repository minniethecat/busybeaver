# Yale Reunion

Sam Dodsworth's return to Yale for his thirtieth reunion is the novel's most significant American interlude — a mid-journey homecoming that offers him temporary refuge from the dislocations of Europe and a reminder of the self he was before the journey began. Alone, without Fran, Sam rediscovers the world that formed him.

## The Return

Sam sails back across the Atlantic alone — Fran has chosen Montreux with Renée de Pénable — and finds in the reunion everything he has been missing in Europe: familiarity, recognition, the effortless belonging of people who speak his language in every sense. The rituals of the reunion — the football game, the class dinner, the old songs — provide a comfort that European culture, for all its sophistication, has never offered.

Ross Ireland, the Iowan correspondent, accompanies Sam on the crossing, and his defiant Americanism — "English was 'enough to take anybody anywhere'" (028-002) — reinforces Sam's sense that he is returning to his own country, his own people, his own terms of value. The reunion is a homecoming to a self Sam recognizes and respects.

## Significance

The reunion serves a crucial structural function: it reminds Sam — and the reader — that the European journey is not a repudiation of America but a complication of it. Sam does not return to Yale to renounce Europe but to replenish himself, to draw on the resources of his American identity before returning to the challenges of his European life.

The reunion also marks Sam's first experience of being alone without being lonely. Without Fran, without the office, without the obligations that have defined him, Sam discovers that he can exist — and even enjoy himself — as an independent person. This discovery will prove essential in the novel's final movement.
