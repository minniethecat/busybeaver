# Atlantic Crossing

The Dodsworths' Atlantic crossing aboard the SS Ultima is the novel's threshold event — the six-day passage between the American life they are leaving and the European life they have not yet entered. Aboard the ship, the patterns that will define their journey are rehearsed in miniature: Fran collecting admirers, Sam observing from the margins, the marriage already showing its essential fractures.

## The Journey

The crossing begins with Sam's unease — "he felt lost in the round of ocean, one universal gray except for a golden gash on the western horizon" (005-001) — and develops into exhilaration. The ship, "the most sure and impressive mechanism he had ever seen" (005-004), awakens in Sam a capacity for wonder that his industrial life has suppressed. He tramps the decks, explores the engine spaces, stands at the rail watching "the long straight lines of the decking planks, rigid as bars of music" (005-012).

Fran, meanwhile, discovers the shipboard society that will become her element in Europe. Within three days she has "a dozen men to 'play with,' to dance and argue with, and race with around the deck" (012-001). Major Lockert emerges as her particular companion, "proprietorially about her, always" (012-002) — the first of the titled European admirers who will populate the Dodsworths' journey.

## Key Moments

**Sam at the rail (Chapter V)**: His first experience of the open sea — fear giving way to exhilaration, the beginning of a sensibility beyond the factory floor.

**The gala dance**: Under Japanese lanterns on the starboard deck, Sam remembers the Kennepoose Canoe Club dance where he first met Fran — the memory connecting his present estrangement to his fullest moment of belonging.

**Fran and Lockert**: The shipboard flirtation that establishes the pattern of Fran's European relationships — intense, proprietary, ultimately dismissible.

## Significance

The Atlantic crossing is the novel's first sustained exploration of the theme that will dominate its middle section: the American in Europe. Aboard the Ultima, Sam experiences his first dislocation from the world that has defined him — and his first discovery that solitude, properly understood, is not loneliness but possibility.
