# Hurd's London Dinner

Hurd's dinner at the Dindonneau Restaurant in Soho is the novel's most sustained comic set piece — a gathering of American expatriates in London that Lewis renders with both affection and devastating accuracy. The dinner is a miniature of the American-European encounter: earnest, overdecorated, and deeply uncertain of its own dignity.

## The Dinner

The private room features a horseshoe table with seats for thirty. "Along the table little American flags were set in pots of forget-me-nots. Behind the chairman's table was a portrait of President Coolidge, draped with red, white and blue bunting" (021-005). Heaven knows where Hurd collected them all, but "about the wall... were shields and banners of Yale and Harvard and the University of Winnemac, of the Elks, the Oddfellows, the Moose, the Woodmen, of the Rotarians, the Kiwanians, and the Zenith Chamber of Commerce, with a four-sheet poster of the Revelation car" (021-005).

The accumulation of American institutional paraphernalia in a Soho restaurant is Lewis's visual argument about the character of American identity abroad: it is assembled from fragments, asserted rather than assumed, and slightly ridiculous — but also, in its sincerity, genuinely moving. Sam, surrounded by the banners of the world he has left, feels both at home and absurdly displaced.

## Significance

Hurd's dinner captures the particular condition of the American expatriate: culturally insecure, socially ambitious, and permanently homesick in ways that express themselves as patriotic display. The dinner's excess — the flags, the banners, the Coolidge portrait — is a defense against the European indifference that Sam has been feeling since his Pall Mall walk.

For Sam, the dinner is a moment of recognition. These are his people — not the titled Europeans Fran pursues, but the engineers and managers and Rotarians who built the America he belongs to. The recognition is both comforting and limiting: it reminds him of who he is, and of the world he may be outgrowing.
