# Herman Voelker

Herman Voelker, Fran's father, appears only in the novel's opening genealogy — but his influence shapes everything that follows. He is the German-American brewer whose wealth made Fran's aspirations possible and whose ethnicity she spends her life trying to transcend.

## Biographical Sketch

Herman Voelker "had brewed his way into millions and respectability" (001-018). His house was "almost the largest in Zenith," boasting "the greatest amount of turrets, colored glass windows, and lace curtains." He was a leader among the German-Americans who were "supplanting the New Englanders throughout the state as controllers of finance and merchandising."

Lewis's description of Herman as a "beef-colored burgher" who "should have fathered anything so poised and luminous as Fran" (001-018) establishes the central fact of Fran's psychology: she is the daughter of an immigrant brewer who has become, through education and marriage, something her father could not have imagined. Her entire European project is, in a sense, an attempt to complete the assimilation Herman began — to move from German-American respectability to genuine cosmopolitan sophistication.

## Role in the Novel

Herman never appears directly, but his shadow falls across the entire novel. Fran's hunger for European aristocracy is, at root, a flight from Herman Voelker's world — the world of beer and turrets and lace curtains, of ethnic aspiration and provincial success. She wants to be not the daughter of a brewer but the companion of counts.

The irony Lewis exposes is that Fran's flight from her father reproduces his ambition in a different key. Herman wanted to be accepted by the New Englanders of Zenith; Fran wants to be accepted by the aristocrats of Europe. The mechanism is identical; only the target has changed.
