# Leisure and Purpose

The problem that Sam Dodsworth never anticipated: what does a man do with himself when he no longer has to do anything? The novel examines leisure not as a reward but as a crisis — the sudden absence of the work that gave life its shape and meaning.

## Definition

Leisure in *Dodsworth* is not the pleasant interlude of a vacation but the terrifying emptiness that opens when work is permanently removed. Sam's initial response to Fran's proposal of a year in Europe — "I think we'd get pretty tired of Europe, living around in hotels" (002-038) — is not philistinism but premonition. He senses, correctly, that a life organized around pleasure and tourism will not sustain him.

The novel treats leisure as a specifically American problem. The Protestant work ethic, which made Sam's success possible, provides no guidance for what comes after success. Sam has been so thoroughly shaped by the demands of industry — the "scientific efficiency" (002-018) he brought to every aspect of life — that he has no resources for the unprogrammed life. Europe offers him beauty, history, culture — and he genuinely comes to appreciate these things — but appreciation is not the same as purpose.

## Context in the Novel

The leisure problem is dramatized in the novel's pacing. The early chapters in Zenith are dense with activity: meetings, decisions, the machinery of business. The European chapters, by contrast, are filled with waiting — in hotel lobbies, at cafés, on steamer decks. Sam is always waiting for Fran, waiting for the next destination, waiting for something to happen that will restore his sense of agency.

His eventual resolution is not a return to full-time work but the discovery that purpose can be found in smaller things: in genuine companionship (with Edith), in aesthetic appreciation (the churches, the landscapes), in the simple satisfaction of paying attention to the world. This is a quieter kind of purpose than running a factory, but the novel presents it as more sustainable — and more suited to the second half of life.

## Key Passages

> "Well, it would be kind of nice to get away from the grind. I'd like to look over the Rolls-Royce and Mercedes plants. And see Paris and the Alps. But a year — That's a long time. I think we'd get pretty tired of Europe, living around in hotels." (002-038)

Sam's instinctive recognition that leisure, unorganized by purpose, will become exhausting.

> He had no longer the dignity of a craftsman. He made nothing; he meant nothing. (002-057)

The existential core: without productive work, Sam experiences himself as nonexistent.

## Critical Interpretation

Lewis's treatment of leisure is a critique of the American inability to imagine a good life beyond work. Sam is not lazy — he is lost. The culture that produced him provided an excellent education in how to make things and no education at all in how to enjoy them. His European journey is, in part, a remedial course in the art of living — and the novel suggests, cautiously, that it is possible to learn this art even at fifty.

The resolution is deliberately modest. Sam does not become a connoisseur or an intellectual; he becomes a man who can sit in a Venetian café without feeling useless. For Lewis, this is genuine achievement: the transformation of leisure from an absence (of work) into a presence (of attention, appreciation, connection).
