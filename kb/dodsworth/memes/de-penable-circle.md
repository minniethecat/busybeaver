# De Pénable Circle

The social circle orbiting Renée de Pénable in Paris represents everything Fran Dodsworth hoped Europe would deliver: sophistication, titled acquaintances, intellectual conversation, the performance of cultivation that passes for cultivation itself. For Sam, it represents everything about Europe that excludes him — a world whose language he does not speak, whose codes he cannot read, whose values he neither shares nor respects.

## Description

Lewis renders the De Pénable circle through Sam's alienated perspective. The circle gathers in salons and restaurants, its members — Endicott Everett Atkins with his "appalling, reproving, Christian Science sort of poise" (027-001), Billy Dawson the "airiest and most objectionable" of the gentlemen valets, Arnold Israel the cultivated European intellectual — performing sophistication with an assurance that Sam finds both impressive and hollow.

The circle's defining characteristic is its impenetrability to the outsider. Sam attends its gatherings — the restaurants "reeking with cigarette smoke and expensive perfume and smart scandal" (028-001) — but he is never of them. He slips away, "like a small boy going to the circus, to low places" (028-001), seeking out Ross Ireland, the one American in Paris who refuses to be Europeanized.

## Role in the Novel

The De Pénable circle is the social world Fran has been seeking since she first proposed the European journey. It is here that her ambition finds its fullest expression — and here that Sam's exclusion becomes absolute. The circle is not hostile to Sam; it simply has no place for him, which is worse.

Renée de Pénable herself becomes Fran's patron and model: the European woman Fran aspires to become. Their plan to share a villa near Montreux for the summer represents Fran's permanent election of this world over the American life she has spent two decades despising.

## Key People

Renée de Pénable presides. Endicott Everett Atkins provides the intellectual veneer. Billy Dawson, "the airiest and most objectionable of the De Pénable's gentlemen valets" (028-001), exemplifies the circle's younger members. Fran Dodsworth aspires to belong. Sam Dodsworth does not.
