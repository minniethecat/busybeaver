# Virtue

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, **virtue** (*aretē*) is the sole good and the necessary and sufficient condition for happiness (*eudaimonia*). Virtue is not one quality among many but the perfection of human nature — the disposition of the rational soul to live in accordance with nature and reason. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the orthodox Stoic position that virtue is its own reward: "virtue promises good fortune and happiness and freedom from perturbation" (004-009).

The Stoics recognized four cardinal virtues: wisdom (*sophia*), justice (*dikaiosynē*), courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*), and temperance (*sōphrosynē*). These are not separate qualities but aspects of a single unified virtue — the knowledge of what is good, bad, and indifferent.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] emphasizes virtue as a practical achievement, not merely theoretical understanding. "What is the product of virtue? A mind free from perturbation, tranquil, steadfast" (004-015). He repeatedly warns against those who can discourse about virtue but fail to embody it in their actions and judgments.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], virtue is identical with the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]] (*[[appearances|phantasiai]]*). The virtuous person is one who assents only to adequate [[appearances|impressions]] and never to false ones: "in assent and suspension of assent, that you be not deceived" (004-029). This connection between virtue and the discipline of assent is a distinctive emphasis of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching.

[[progress|Progress]] toward virtue (*prokopē*) is possible through continual [[exercise|practice]], but [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns against complacency: "virtue is progress towards each of these things" (004-011). One must examine one's judgments daily and test them against the standard of nature.

## Key Passages

- **Virtue as the sole good**: "Now if virtue promises good fortune and happiness and freedom from perturbation, certainly progress towards virtue is progress towards each of these things" (004-009, 004-011).
- **The product of virtue**: "What is the product of virtue? A mind free from perturbation, tranquil, steadfast" (004-015).
- **Virtue and assent**: "See that you commit no error; in assent and suspension of assent, that you be not deceived" (004-028, 004-029).

## Related Concepts

- **[[nature|Nature]]** (*[[nature|physis]]*) — Virtue is living according to nature; the virtuous person understands and accepts the natural order.
- **[[reason|Reason]]** (*logos*) — Virtue is the perfection of the rational faculty; to be virtuous is to reason correctly about what is good and bad.
- **[[wisdom|Wisdom]]** (*sophia*) — The knowledge of what is truly good, bad, and indifferent; the crown of the virtues.
- **[[freedom|Freedom]]** (*[[freedom|eleutheria]]*) — The condition of the virtuous soul, which cannot be constrained by externals.
