[004-001]He who is making progress, having learned from

[004-002]philosophers that [[desire]] means the desire of [[good]] things, and aversion means

[004-003]aversion from bad things; having learned too that [[happiness]] and tranquillity

[004-004]are not attainable by man otherwise than by not failing to obtain what he

[004-005]desires, and not falling into that which he would avoid; such a man takes from

[004-006]himself desire altogether and confers it, but he employs his aversion only on

[004-007]things which are dependent on his will. For if he attempts to avoid anything

[004-008]independent of his will, he knows that sometimes he will fall in with something

[004-009]which he wishes to avoid, and he will be unhappy. Now if [[virtue]] promises good

[004-010]fortune and tranquillity and happiness, certainly also the progress towards

[004-011]virtue is progress towards each of these things. For it is always true that to

[004-012]whatever point the perfecting of anything leads us, progress is an approach

[004-013]towards this point.

[004-014]How then do we admit that virtue is such as I have said, and yet seek progress

[004-015]in other things and make a display of it? What is the product of virtue?

[004-016]Tranquillity. Who then makes improvement? Is it he who has read many books of

[004-017][[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]? But does virtue consist in having understood Chrysippus? If this is

[004-018]so, progress is clearly nothing else than knowing a great deal of Chrysippus.

[004-019]But now we admit that virtue produces one thing, and we declare that

[004-020]approaching near to it is another thing, namely, progress or improvement. Such

[004-021]a person, says one, is already able to read Chrysippus by himself. Indeed, sir,

[004-022]you are making great progress. What kind of progress? But why do you mock the

[004-023]man? Why do you draw him away from the perception of his own misfortunes? Will

[004-024]you not show him the effect of virtue that he may learn where to look for

[004-025]improvement? Seek it there, wretch, where your work lies. And where is your

[004-026]work? In desire and in aversion, that you may not be disappointed in your

[004-027]desire, and that you may not fall into that which you would avoid; in your

[004-028]pursuit and avoiding, that you commit no error; in [[assent]] and suspension of

[004-029]assent, that you be not deceived. The first things, and the most necessary are

[004-030]those which I have named. But if with trembling and lamentation you seek not to

[004-031]fall into that which you avoid, tell me how you are improving.

[004-032]Do you then show me your improvement in these things? If I were talking to an

[004-033]athlete, I should say, Show me your shoulders; and then he might say, Here are

[004-034]my Halteres. You and your Halteres look to that. I should reply, I wish to see

[004-035]the effect of the Halteres. So, when you say: Take the treatise on the active

[004-036]powers ([Greek: hormea]), and see how I have studied it, I reply: Slave, I am

[004-037]not inquiring about this, but how you exercise pursuit and avoidance, desire

[004-038]and aversion, how you design and purpose and prepare yourself, whether

[004-039]conformably to [[nature]] or not. If conformably, give me evidence of it, and I

[004-040]will say that you are making progress; but if not conformably, be gone, and not

[004-041]only expound your books, but write such books yourself; and what will you gain

[004-042]by it? Do you not know that the whole book costs only five denarii? Does then

[004-043]the expounder seem to be worth more than five denarii? Never then look for the

[004-044]matter itself in one place, and progress towards it in another. Where then is

[004-045]progress? If any of you, withdrawing himself from [[externals]], turns to his own

[004-046]will ([Greek: proairesis]) to exercise it and to improve it by labor, so as to

[004-047]make it conformable to nature, elevated, free, unrestrained, unimpeded,

[004-048]faithful, modest; and if he has learned that he who desires or avoids the

[004-049]things which are not in his power can neither be faithful nor free, but of

[004-050]necessity he must change with them and be tossed about with them as in a

[004-051]tempest, and of necessity must subject himself to others who have the power to

[004-052]procure or prevent what lie desires or would avoid; finally, when he rises in

[004-053]the morning, if he observes and keeps these rules, bathes as a man of fidelity,

[004-054]eats as a modest man; in like manner, if in every matter that occurs he works

[004-055]out his chief principles ([Greek: ta proaegoumena]) as the runner does with

[004-056]reference to running, and the trainer of the voice with reference to the

[004-057]voice—this is the man who truly makes progress, and this is the man who has not

[004-058]travelled in vain. But if he has strained his efforts to the practice of

[004-059]reading books, and labors only at this, and has travelled for this, I tell him

[004-060]to return home immediately, and not to neglect his affairs there; for this for

[004-061]which he has travelled is nothing. But the other thing is something, to study

[004-062]how a man can rid his life of lamentation and groaning, and saying, Woe to me,

[004-063]and wretched that I am, and to rid it also of misfortune and disappointment,

[004-064]and to learn what death is, and exile, and prison, and poison, that he may be

[004-065]able to say when he is in fetters, Dear Crito, if it is the will of the gods

[004-066]that it be so, let it be so; and not to say, Wretched am I, an old man: have I

[004-067]kept my gray hairs for this? Who is it that speaks thus? Do you think that I

[004-068]shall name some man of no repute and of low condition? Does not Priam say this?

[004-069]Does not Oedipus say this? Nay, all kings say it! For what else is tragedy than

[004-070]the perturbations ([Greek: pathae]) of men who value externals exhibited in

[004-071]this kind of poetry? But if a man must learn by fiction that no external things

[004-072]which are independent of the will concern us, for my part I should like this

[004-073]fiction, by the aid of which I should live happily and undisturbed. But you

[004-074]must consider for yourselves what you wish.

[004-075]What then does Chrysippus teach us? The reply is, to know that these things are

[004-076]not false, from which happiness comes and tranquillity arises. Take my books,

[004-077]and you will learn how true and conformable to nature are the things which make

[004-078]me free from perturbations. O great good fortune! O the great benefactor who

[004-079]points out the way! To Triptolemus all men have erected temples and altars,

[004-080]because he gave us food by cultivation; but to him who discovered truth and

[004-081]brought it to light and communicated it to all, not the truth which shows us

[004-082]how to live, but how to live well, who of you for this [[reason]] has built an

[004-083]altar, or a temple, or has dedicated a statue, or who worships God for this?

[004-084]Because the gods have given the vine, or wheat, we sacrifice to them; but

[004-085]because they have produced in the human mind that fruit by which they designed

[004-086]to show us the truth which relates to happiness, shall we not thank God for

[004-087]this?
