[040-001]There are three things (topics, [Greek: topoi]) in

[040-002]which a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and [[good]]. The first

[040-003]concerns the desires and the aversions, that a man may not fail to get what he

[040-004]desires, and that he may not fall into that which he does not [[desire]]. The

[040-005]second concerns the movements towards an object and the movements from an

[040-006]object, and generally in doing what a man ought to do, that he may act

[040-007]according to order, to [[reason]], and not carelessly. The third thing concerns

[040-008]freedom from deception and rashness in [[judgment]], and generally it concerns the

[040-009]assents ([Greek: sugchatatheseis]). Of these topics the chief and the most

[040-010]urgent is that which relates to the affects ([Greek: ta pathae] perturbations);

[040-011]for an affect is produced in no other way than by a failing to obtain that

[040-012]which a man desires or falling into that which a man would wish to avoid. This

[040-013]is that which brings in perturbations, disorders, bad fortune, misfortunes,

[040-014]sorrows, lamentations, and envy; that which makes men envious and jealous; and

[040-015]by these causes we are unable even to listen to the precepts of reason. The

[040-016]second topic concerns the duties of a man; for I ought not to be free from

[040-017]affects ([Greek: apathae]) like a statue, but I ought to maintain the relations

[040-018]([Greek: scheseis]) natural and acquired, as a pious man, as a son, as a

[040-019]father, as a citizen.

[040-020]The third topic is that which immediately concerns those who are making

[040-021]proficiency, that which concerns the security of the other two, so that not

[040-022]even in sleep any appearance unexamined may surprise us, nor in intoxication,

[040-023]nor in melancholy. This, it may be said, is above our power. But the present

[040-024]philosophers neglecting the first topic and the second (the affects and

[040-025]duties), employ themselves on the third, using sophistical arguments ([Greek:

[040-026]metapiptontas]), making conclusions from questioning, employing hypotheses,

[040-027]lying. For a man must, it is said, when employed on these matters, take care

[040-028]that he is not deceived. Who must? The wise and good man. This then is all that

[040-029]is wanting to you. Have you successfully worked out the rest? Are you free from

[040-030]deception in the matter of money? If you see a beautiful girl do you resist the

[040-031]appearance? If your neighbor obtains an estate by will, are you not vexed? Now

[040-032]is there nothing else wanting to you except unchangeable firmness of mind

[040-033]([Greek: ametaptosia])? Wretch, you hear these very things with fear and

[040-034]anxiety that some person may despise you, and with inquiries about what any

[040-035]person may say about you. And if a man come and tell you that in a certain

[040-036]conversation in which the question was, Who is the best philosopher, a man who

[040-037]was present said that a certain person was the chief philosopher, your little

[040-038]soul which was only a finger’s length stretches out to two cubits. But if

[040-039]another who is present says, You are mistaken; it is not worth while to listen

[040-040]to a certain person, for what does he know? he has only the first principles,

[040-041]and no more? then you are confounded, you grow pale, you cry out immediately, I

[040-042]will show him who I am, that I am a great philosopher. It is seen by these very

[040-043]things: why do you wish to show it by others? Do you not know that Diogenes

[040-044]pointed out one of the sophists in this way by stretching out his middle

[040-045]finger? And then when the man was wild with rage, This, he said, is the certain

[040-046]person: I have pointed him out to you. For a man is not shown by the finger, as

[040-047]a stone or a piece of wood; but when any person shows the man’s principles,

[040-048]then he shows him as a man.

[040-049]Let us look at your principles also. For is it not plain that you value not at

[040-050]all your own will ([Greek: proairesis]), but you look externally to things

[040-051]which are independent of your will? For instance, what will a certain person

[040-052]say? and what will people think of you? Will you be considered a man of

[040-053]learning; have you read [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] or Antipater? for if you have read

[040-054]Archedamus also, you have every thing (that you can desire). Why you are still

[040-055]uneasy lest you should not show us who you are? Would you let me tell you what

[040-056]manner of man you have shown us that you are? You have exhibited yourself to us

[040-057]as a mean fellow, querulous, passionate, cowardly, finding fault with

[040-058]everything, blaming everybody, never quiet, vain: this is what you have

[040-059]exhibited to us. Go away now and read Archedamus; then if a mouse should leap

[040-060]down and make a noise, you are a dead man. For such a death awaits you as it

[040-061]did—what was the man’s name—Crinis; and he too was proud, because he understood

[040-062]Archedamus. Wretch, will you not dismiss these things that do not concern you

[040-063]at all? These things are suitable to those who are able to learn them without

[040-064]perturbation, to those who can say: “I am not subject to anger, to grief, to

[040-065]envy: I am not hindered, I am not restrained. What remains for me? I have

[040-066]leisure, I am tranquil: let us see how we must deal with sophistical arguments;

[040-067]let us see how when a man has accepted an hypothesis he shall not be led away

[040-068]to any thing absurd.” To them such things belong. To those who are happy it is

[040-069]appropriate to light a fire, to dine; if they choose, both to sing and to

[040-070]dance. But when the vessel is sinking, you come to me and hoist the sails.
