# Reason

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, **reason** (*logos*) is both the [[divinity|divine]] principle that orders the cosmos and the human faculty by which we understand and align ourselves with that order. Reason is what distinguishes humans from animals: "we have in common with the animals the use of appearances, and reason is that which distinguishes us" (003-007).

For the Stoics, reason is not merely instrumental — a tool for calculating means to independently given ends. Rather, reason is itself the source of value: what reason judges to be good is good, and what it judges to be bad is bad. The proper functioning of reason *is* virtue.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] grounds his entire teaching in the supremacy of the rational faculty. Reason [[solitude|alone]] can examine itself: it is "the only faculty that we have received which examines itself, what it is, and what power it has" (Discourse 1). This self-reflexive character of reason gives it unique authority over all other human capacities.

Throughout the Discourses, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that acting "consistent with reason" (002-005) is the standard for all moral action. When reason judges an action to be appropriate, that judgment [[solitude|alone]] suffices; no external authority or consequence can override it.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] particularly emphasizes the practical application of reason. It is not enough to understand Stoic doctrines intellectually — one must use reason to examine one's [[appearances|impressions]] in real time: "Who of you for this reason has built an altar? Who has given thanks to God for this?" (004-082). Reason must become a lived [[exercise|practice]].

## Key Passages

- **Reason as distinguishing faculty**: "We have in common with the animals the use of appearances, and reason is that which distinguishes us" (003-007).
- **Consistency with reason**: "Consistent with reason. To hang yourself is not consistent with reason" (002-005).
- **Reason as self-examining**: The rational faculty is unique in its ability to "examine itself, what it is, and what power it has."

## Related Concepts

- **[[nature|Nature]]** (*[[nature|physis]]*) — Reason and nature are ultimately one; to live according to nature is to follow reason.
- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — [[virtue|Virtue]] is the perfection of reason in its practical application.
- **[[assent|Assent]]** (*synkatathesis*) — The act by which reason accepts or rejects an impression as true.
- **[[prohairesis|Prohairesis]]** (moral purpose) — The rational faculty in its role as the seat of choice.
