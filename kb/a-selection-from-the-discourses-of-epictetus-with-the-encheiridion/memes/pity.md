## Definition

Pity (*eleos*) is classified in Stoic ethics as a passion — a form of [[grief|distress]] at another person's apparent [[fortune|misfortune]]. The Stoics argued that pity, despite its reputation as a benevolent emotion, is actually based on a philosophical error: the belief that external circumstances (poverty, illness, exile, death) constitute genuine evils. For the Stoic, since only vice is truly evil and only virtue is truly good, there is no rational basis for pity. What appears compassionate is in fact a shared delusion about the nature of good and evil.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats pity with particular sharpness, devoting substantial attention to it in his Discourses. He does not merely dismiss pity as philosophically incorrect — he actively resists being pitied by others, seeing their pity as an insult to his moral autonomy. "Show them that I do not require pity" (062-004) is his characteristic response to those who would pity his poverty or physical condition.

His [[disputation|argument]] proceeds in two steps. First, the person who pities another has, by that very act, conceded that externals are genuine goods and evils — a fundamental philosophical mistake. Second, the person pitied should refuse the role of victim. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that the real question is not whether one is pitied but whether one *deserves* pity, and the Stoic who has placed his good in the ruling faculty emphatically does not (062-005, 062-006).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the point by distinguishing proper from improper objects of compassion. If one must pity, he says, pity those who are truly unfortunate — those enslaved to their passions, those who do not know where good and evil lie (062-007). To pity a person for poverty or physical limitation is to insult what is genuinely admirable in them. He groups pity with [[jealousy|envy]], fear, and resentment as passions that must be eliminated through philosophical [[education|training]] (053-011, 057-008).

He also makes a more subtle point: those who pity themselves for their circumstances have doubly erred — first in valuing externals, and second in abdicating their capacity to bear hardship virtuously. The proper response to [[fortune|misfortune]] is not self-pity but to "pity the blind and the lame" only if they are morally blind, not if they are physically so (018-024, 018-025).

## Key Passages

- (012-026) — Even when someone suffers, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] withholds automatic pity: the wise person asks whether the suffering is from genuine evil.
- (018-024) — "If it is possible, rather pity" — but only for moral, not physical affliction.
- (018-025) — Pity the blind and lame only as we pity those blind to virtue.
- (053-011) — "Nor resentment or [[jealousy|envy]] nor pity" — the Stoic student must eliminate all three.
- (057-008) — Pity listed among the passions of one who "chooses to live in [[grief|sorrow]], fear, [[jealousy|envy]]."
- (062-003) — "Is it in your power to prevent those who pity you?" — the crucial question.
- (062-004) — "Show them that I do not require pity."
- (062-005) — The question is whether one *deserves* pity — the Stoic does not.
- (062-008) — "They pity me for my poverty, for not possessing honorable offices" — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] critiques the misdirection of pity.
