# Fever

**Fever** (Greek *pyretos*) serves throughout [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses as the [[example|paradigm]]atic test of Stoic character. As a bodily indifferent — neither good nor evil in itself — fever reveals whether a person's philosophical principles are genuine convictions or mere classroom ornaments. The question is never whether one will have a fever, but whether one will bear it well when it comes.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s use of fever is remarkably concrete. At [[rome|Rome]], he reminds his students, there was literally an altar to Fever (012-020), testifying to how seriously the ancients took this affliction. For the Stoic, fever is not a [[divinity|god]] to be placated but an opportunity to exercise virtue.

## Epictetus's Treatment

Fever appears in the Discourses as the representative bodily trial. In Discourse 46, the most extended treatment, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] confronts a student who fears the onset of fever and the loss of all philosophical convictions with it. "Now is the time for the fever," he declares. "Let it be borne well. Now is the time for the thirst. Let it be borne well" (046-027).

The point is that fever does not touch the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*) unless one surrenders it. "What hinders you when you have a fever from having your ruling faculty conformable to nature?" (046-035). Just as one does not read when walking, one does not walk well when feverish — but one can bear a fever well, and that is what belongs to a human being: "If you bear a fever well, you have all that belongs to a man" (046-040).

The metaphor is medical throughout. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] compares the onset of philosophical panic at fever to patients who, as soon as the fever begins, "lose and forget all" their former resolutions (046-013). The true philosopher, by contrast, maintains the same relationship to fever as to any other indifferent — treating it as material for virtue, not as an evil to be feared.

Fever also serves as a general symbol for bodily vulnerability. The body is "the slave of fever, of gout, of ophthalmia, of dysentery" (053-098), and the Stoic must accept that fevers, robbers, and tyrants are among the things that may overpower the body but never the will (054-053). In the scale of human suffering, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] notes grimly that while some causes of death kill quickly, "a fever is often a year about it" (023-047), underscoring the [[endurance|patience]] demanded of the Stoic sufferer.

## Key Passages

- The altar at [[rome|Rome]]: "There is at [[rome|Rome]] an altar to Fever" (012-020), evidence of how fever was worshipped as a divinity.
- The trial: "Must another have a fever, another sail on the sea, another die?" (022-050), listing fever among the universal human trials.
- The countenance: "Nothing else changes the countenance like a man who is in a fever" (028-046), on fever's power to alter even the philosopher's appearance.
- The time for fever: "Now is the time for the fever. Let it be borne well" (046-027).
- The ruling faculty preserved: "What hinders you when you have a fever from having your ruling faculty conformable to nature?" (046-035).
- Bearing fever well: "If you bear a fever well, you have all that belongs to a man" (046-040).
- The body as slave: "Do you not know that it is the slave of fever, of gout, ophthalmia, dysentery?" (053-098).
