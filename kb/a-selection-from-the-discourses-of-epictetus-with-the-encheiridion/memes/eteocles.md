# Eteocles

## Biography

Eteocles is one of the two sons of Oedipus and a king of Thebes, whose war with his brother [[polynices|Polynices]] over the throne is one of the darkest stories of Greek legend, dramatized in Aeschylus's *Seven Against Thebes* and Euripides's *Phoenissae*. In the Discourses he never appears [[solitude|alone]]: he is always half of a pair, "Eteocles and [[polynices|Polynices]]," the two brothers who destroyed each other over royal power.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is not interested in the narrative of the Theban war for its own sake. He reaches for the brothers because they present, in the most extreme form imaginable, his central claim about the causes of human conflict. Here are two men bound by the closest natural tie — sons of the same father, brothers — who nonetheless become mortal enemies.

If even brotherhood cannot hold them together, the cause of their enmity cannot lie in their external circumstances or in any real injury done by one to the other. It must lie, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues, in something internal: a shared but false opinion about what is good and evil.

Eteocles is thus, in the text, a standing proof that enmity is manufactured by judgment, and that the same false valuation which drives strangers apart will drive brothers to kill each other.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] twice makes the brothers the illustration of his thesis that "no man either hurts another or does good to another" (061-099); rather it is a man's own opinion that "overturns him; this is fighting, this is civil discord, this is war" (061-101).

The specific opinion is named: "That which made Eteocles and [[polynices|Polynices]] enemies was nothing else than this opinion which they had about royal power" (061-102), their opinion "about exile, that the one is the extreme of evils, the other the greatest good" (061-104).

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] repeats the lesson in compressed form. Because men revile whatever they think is the cause of harm, "a father is reviled by his son" who is denied "the things which are considered to be good" (099-019); "it was this which made [[polynices|Polynices]] and Eteocles enemies, the opinion that royal power was a good" (099-021).

## References in the Discourses

- **Enmity from opinion**: what made Eteocles and [[polynices|Polynices]] enemies was their opinion about royal power (061-102) and exile, "the one is the extreme of evils, the other the greatest good" (061-104).
- **The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] restatement**: the brothers made enemies by "the opinion that royal power was a good" (099-021).
