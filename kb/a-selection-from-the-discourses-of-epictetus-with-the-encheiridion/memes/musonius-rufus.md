# Musonius Rufus

## Biography

Gaius Musonius Rufus (c. 30 – c. 100 CE) was a Roman Stoic philosopher of the first century, remembered as one of the most influential teachers of his age. He came from Volsinii in Etruria and belonged to the equestrian order, but devoted his life to philosophy rather than to public office.

Under the emperors of his day he was more than once banished from [[rome|Rome]] for his outspokenness, a [[necessity|fate]] common to Stoic teachers whose moral independence made them suspect to those in power. In later antiquity he was remembered as a kind of Roman [[socrates|Socrates]], valued less for written treatises than for the moral force of his [[education|instruction]].

The biographical note prefixed to this selection records the single most consequential fact of his career for the history of [[stoicism|Stoicism]]: that the young slave [[epictetus|Epictetus]] was "sent or permitted... to attend the lectures of C. Musonius Rufus, an eminent Stoic philosopher" (P02-014 – P02-016).

It was in Musonius's school that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] received his philosophical formation, and much of the method preserved in the Discourses — the insistence on turning doctrine into daily [[exercise|practice]] — descends from this teacher. Like [[socrates|Socrates]] before him, Musonius wrote nothing that survives intact; his teaching is known chiefly through the record left by pupils, of whom [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is the most famous.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Musonius Rufus was [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own teacher, and this is the reason his name is preserved in the introductory notice to the Discourses (P02-015). [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s characteristic concern — that philosophy is worthless unless it changes how one lives — reflects the practical [[stoicism|Stoicism]] for which Musonius was famous.

The one saying of Musonius reproduced in the text itself shows this temper. When the emperor Galba was murdered and a bystander asked whether the world was really governed by [[providence|Providence]], Musonius answered by refusing the challenge:

> A person said to Rufus when Galba was murdered: Is the world now governed by Providence? But Rufus replied: Did I ever incidentally form an argument from Galba that the world is governed by Providence? (050-042)

The reply — that no single political catastrophe can overturn a conclusion never founded on political fortune in the first place — is exactly the discipline of judgment that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges throughout the Discourses.

## References in the Discourses

- **The biographical note**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is sent "to attend the lectures of C. Musonius Rufus, an eminent Stoic philosopher" (P02-014 – P02-016), the fact that fixes Musonius as his teacher.

- **The Galba exchange**: Musonius's answer, when asked after Galba's murder whether the world is governed by [[providence|Providence]], that no [[disputation|argument]] for [[providence|Providence]] was ever built on Galba in the first place (050-042 – 050-044).
