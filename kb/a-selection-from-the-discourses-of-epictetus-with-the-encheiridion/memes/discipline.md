# Three Disciplines

## Definition

The **Three Disciplines** (*treis topoi*) are [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s systematic program of moral [[education|training]], covering the entire field of Stoic [[exercise|practice]]. The three are: the discipline of **desire** (*[[desire|orexis]]*), governing what we should want and avoid; the discipline of **action** (*hormē*), governing how we should act toward others; and the discipline of **assent** (*synkatathesis*), governing how we should judge [[appearances|impressions]].

This tripartite scheme is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s distinctive contribution to Stoic pedagogy. It organizes the entire Stoic curriculum into a progressive sequence, beginning with the most fundamental (desire) and advancing to the most subtle (assent).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents the three disciplines as the core of his teaching throughout the Discourses. The discipline of desire concerns our relationship to the [[divinity|gods]] and to nature: we must learn to desire only what is within our power and to accept everything else as providentially ordered.

The discipline of action concerns our relationship to other human beings: we must learn to act appropriately (*kathēkon*) in every social role — as child, parent, citizen, friend. "Remember that you are an actor in a drama," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] writes.

The discipline of assent concerns our relationship to our own mind: we must learn to examine every impression before granting or withholding assent. This is the most advanced discipline, presupposing [[power|mastery]] of the first two.

## Key Passages

- **The three fields of study**: "There are three fields of study in which the one who would be noble and good must be trained" (Discourse 3).
- **[[desire|Desire]] and aversion**: The first discipline concerns "desires and aversions, so that you may not fail to get what you desire."
- **[[action|Action]] and duties**: The second concerns "impulses to act and not to act, and generally appropriate actions."
- **[[assent|Assent]] and judgment**: The third concerns "freedom from deception and rash judgment."

## Related Concepts

- **[[desire|Desire]]** (*[[desire|orexis]]*) — The subject matter of the first discipline; must be directed only at what is within our power.
- **[[assent|Assent]]** (*synkatathesis*) — The subject matter of the third discipline; the most refined form of Stoic [[exercise|practice]].
- **[[impression|Impression]]** (*phantasia*) — What must be examined before assent in the third discipline.
- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — The three disciplines are the path to virtue.
