## Definition

Constancy (or firmness) is the Stoic virtue of unshakable [[firmness|steadfastness]] — the capacity to maintain one's moral judgments and character even under extreme external pressure.

It is the quality of the will that remains stable when confronted with threats, pain, loss, or the temptations of pleasure and power.

Constancy is not stubbornness or insensitivity; it is the natural consequence of having placed one's good entirely in what depends on the will, so that external events cannot disturb one's inner equilibrium.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes Discourse 19 to [[firmness|constancy]] ("On Constancy or [[firmness|Firmness]]"), and the theme pervades his teaching. His treatment is distinctive in its psychological directness.

The foundation of [[firmness|constancy]] is the distinction between the will and its materials. "The being of the good is a certain will; the being of the bad is a certain kind of will. What, then, are externals? Materials for the will" (019-001–019-004).

If one's good is located entirely in right judgment, then no external force can touch it. The tyrant who threatens chains, prison, or death threatens only the body — and the body is not the self (019-010–019-019).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] illustrates with [[socrates|Socrates]], who when condemned to death, did not lose his [[firmness|constancy]]: "[[anytus|Anytus]] and Melitus can kill me, but they cannot hurt me" (019-044). The true self — the ruling faculty — cannot be harmed by anything external.

The same principle is expressed in the law of nature: "Let the stronger always be superior to the weaker" (019-029). Ten men are stronger at putting someone in chains, but the one with right opinions is stronger where it matters (019-032–019-035).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] closes with a challenge: the world needs not more subtle [[disputation|argument]]s but "a man who shall apply them, one who by his acts shall bear testimony to his words" (019-070–019-073). Constancy is the quality that makes such testimony possible.

## Key Passages

> The being of the good is a certain will; the being of the bad is a certain kind of will. What, then, are externals? Materials for the will. (019-001–019-004)

> If I feel that all these things do not concern me, he does not threaten me at all; but if I fear any of them, it is I whom he threatens. (019-015)

> Nothing else can conquer will except the will itself. (019-027)

> Anytus and Melitus can kill me, but they cannot hurt me. (019-044, quoting Socrates)

> What then is the thing which is wanted? A man who shall apply them, one who by his acts shall bear testimony to his words. (019-070–019-071)
