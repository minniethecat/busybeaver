## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, pleasure (*hēdonē*) is classified as a passion (*pathos*) — an irrational movement of the soul that arises from false judgments about what is good and evil. Unlike the Epicureans, who made pleasure the goal of life (*telos*), the Stoics regarded pleasure as at best a byproduct of virtuous activity and at worst a tyrant that enslaves the ruling faculty. Pleasure is an indifferent (*adiaphoron*): it is neither good nor evil in itself, but the pursuit of pleasure as an end is vicious.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is deeply suspicious of pleasure. He recognizes its power over human beings and treats it as a test of philosophical commitment. "Is then pleasure anything secure? No. Take it and throw it out of the scale" (027-064). The image of the scale is characteristic: the student must weigh pleasure against virtue and find pleasure wanting every time.

His most sustained treatment of pleasure appears in Discourse 043, where he distinguishes between bodily pleasure and the pleasure of the soul. "Is then the pleasure of the soul a thing within the will?" he asks, probing whether even mental pleasures are under our [[power|control]] (043-019). The answer is nuanced: the pleasure of the soul cannot depend on itself [[solitude|alone]] — it depends on obtaining certain objects — and therefore it too is not fully within our power. "It remains then that the pleasure of the soul is in the pleasure of the body" (043-031), a striking conclusion that challenges any attempt to spiritualize pleasure away from its bodily roots.

Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not counsel a joyless life. The Stoic's joy comes from virtue itself, from acting in accordance with nature, and from the exercise of the ruling faculty. This is not *hēdonē* in the ordinary sense but a deeper satisfaction — what the later Stoics called *chara* (joy), one of the good passions (*eupatheiai*). "With pleasure you will abstain from things which have a persuasive power" (043-035), he says — the pleasure of self-[[power|mastery]] outweighs the pleasure of indulgence.

His sharpest warning appears in Discourse 019: "[[death|Death]] is, and life and pleasure and pain; for if he comes with pleasure, he will soon turn everything upside down" (019-083). Pleasure is not merely a distraction — it is a force that can overturn all one's philosophical progress. The student must be on guard against it as against an enemy, not because pleasure is evil in itself, but because pleasure's seductive power is so great that it regularly defeats the untrained soul.

## Key Passages

- (015-018) — Pleasure and poverty are both discussed as things people mistakenly pursue or flee.
- (019-083) — "If he comes with pleasure, he will soon turn everything upside down." Pleasure as a dangerous visitor.
- (027-064) — "Is then pleasure anything secure? No. Take it and throw it out of the scale."
- (027-068) — "Elated over present pleasure? See that you do not say that."
- (036-001) — Even the pleasure of [[books|reading]] must be examined — is it for virtue or for entertainment?
- (043-019) — "Is then the pleasure of the soul a thing within the will?" A penetrating epistemological question.
- (043-031) — "It remains then that the pleasure of the soul is in the pleasure of the body."
- (043-035) — "With pleasure you will abstain from things which have a persuasive power."
