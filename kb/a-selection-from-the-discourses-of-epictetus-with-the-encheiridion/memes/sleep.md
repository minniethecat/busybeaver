## Definition

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s practical philosophy, sleep serves a dual role: it is both a natural human need and a potent metaphor for the state of spiritual torpor that the philosopher must overcome. Just as physical sleep suspends waking consciousness, moral "sleep" represents a lapse in the continuous attention (*prosochē*) that Stoic [[exercise|practice]] demands. The transition between waking and sleeping — the moment when [[appearances|impressions]] are no longer examined — becomes in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s hands a test-case for the discipline of assent, revealing whether one's philosophical principles are sufficiently internalized to govern even the semi-conscious mind.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats sleep as a site of philosophical vulnerability. He warns that "even in sleep" an unexamined impression may slip past the guard of reason (040-022). This startling claim extends the discipline of assent — normally understood as a waking activity — into the twilight state between consciousness and unconsciousness. The implication is that the true philosopher's vigilance is so thorough that it permeates even sleep, a standard that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] acknowledges as aspirational.

The most famous passage on sleep in the Epictetan corpus is the Pythagorean evening examination, quoted in the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]* (attributed in the text to the *Golden Verses*): "Let sleep not come upon thy languid eyes / Ere each has daily been completely conned / 'What wrong have I committed, what done, what duty left undone?'" (046-005 through the following lines). This [[exercise|practice]] — reviewing one's day before sleep — transforms the moments before rest from a passive transition into an active philosophical exercise.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also draws on the simple hardship of sleeplessness to make a Stoic point: "when things should fail you, you are sleepless by night" (056-007). [[anxiety|Anxiety]] that prevents sleep reveals an attachment to externals; the person who has truly internalized Stoic principles sleeps soundly regardless of circumstances. Elsewhere, he refers to the "sleepless nights" (097-030) of those who "endure toil" — not as a complaint but as evidence of commitment to philosophical [[education|training]].

## Stoic Context

The evening examination was a [[exercise|practice]] shared across several philosophical schools. The Pythagoreans originated the formal [[exercise|practice]], and it was adopted by the Stoics as a key spiritual exercise. Seneca describes his own evening review in *De Ira*, and Marcus Aurelius opens Book 2 of his *Meditations* with reflections written upon waking, emphasizing the continuity of philosophical awareness across the sleep-wake boundary. The broader Stoic theme of vigilance — that philosophy is a continuous activity, not an intermittent one — finds in sleep its most challenging frontier: the philosopher proves their commitment by maintaining the discipline of assent even as consciousness dims.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that "even in sleep" an unexamined impression may pass undetected, extending the discipline of assent to the semi-conscious state (040-022).
- He acknowledges that "we are forthwith caught half asleep" by untrained [[appearances|impressions]] (041-027), diagnosing moral inattention through the metaphor of drowsiness.
- The Pythagorean evening examination is quoted as a daily [[exercise|practice]]: "Let sleep not come upon thy languid eyes" (046-005), reviewing wrongs committed and duties neglected.
- He observes that those who cling to externals "are sleepless by night" when those things fail (056-007), linking insomnia to misplaced attachment.
- The "sleepless nights" (097-030) of the philosophical trainee are cited as evidence of commitment to the Stoic path.
