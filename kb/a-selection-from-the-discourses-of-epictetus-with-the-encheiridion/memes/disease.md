## Definition

In Stoic moral psychology, "disease" (*nosēma*, νόσημα) is the medical metaphor applied to the passions. Just as bodily diseases arise from imbalances and spread through neglect, diseases of the soul — anger, grief, [[jealousy|envy]], desire — take root from repeated assent to false [[appearances|impressions]] and grow progressively worse if untreated. The Stoics, following the Socratic tradition, conceived philosophy as therapy (*therapeia*) for these diseases: the philosopher is a physician of the soul who diagnoses the passion and applies rational [[disputation|argument]] as medicine. Unlike physical disease, which is an indifferent, diseases of the soul are genuine evils because they corrupt the ruling faculty.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] employs the disease metaphor extensively, particularly in Discourse 33, where he observes that "as philosophers say, also diseases of the mind grow" (033-013). His point is diagnostic and cautionary: passions are not static states but progressive conditions. A small desire, repeatedly indulged, becomes a settled disposition; a momentary anger, unchecked, becomes a chronic rage. The disease "confirms the love" (033-020) — that is, the passion reinforces itself, making cure increasingly difficult.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] groups "danger or disease" (024-003) together as things that most people fear but that the philosopher must correctly evaluate. Physical disease, like physical danger, is an indifferent — it cannot harm the moral self. But the diseases of the soul are to be feared above all else, for they [[solitude|alone]] can destroy one's humanity. His therapeutic approach is preventive: by catching passions at their inception — at the moment of impression — one prevents them from becoming full-blown diseases. This is why the discipline of assent is the first line of defense against psychic illness.

The metaphor extends to the physician figure: "What is the use of a physician if there is no disease?" (051-xxx). The Stoic teacher functions as a physician, and the philosophical school is a hospital for souls. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself embodies this role, diagnosing his students' passions and prescribing philosophical remedies.

## Key Passages

- "As philosophers say, also diseases of the mind grow" (033-013) — [[passion|Passion]]s as progressive conditions; the urgency of early treatment.
- "The disease of the mind confirms the love" (033-020) — How passions self-reinforce, making later cure harder.
- "[[danger|Danger]] or disease, or generally things of that kind" (024-003) — Physical disease as indifferent; the true disease is moral.
- "What is the use of a physician if there is no disease?" (051-xxx) — The therapeutic [[example|model]] of Stoic [[education|instruction]].
