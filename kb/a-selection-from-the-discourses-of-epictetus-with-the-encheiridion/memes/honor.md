## Definition

Honor (*timē*) is external recognition — public office, titles, rank, and the esteem of others. In Stoic ethics, honor is classified as an "indifferent" (*adiaphoron*): it has no intrinsic moral value. A person may hold high office and be vicious, or live in obscurity and be virtuous. The only honor worth pursuing, for a Stoic, is the honor that comes from maintaining one's own moral character. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] declares bluntly: "I shall live unhonored and be nobody nowhere" (092-001) — and then immediately reasons that this is no evil at all.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats the desire for honor as one of the primary obstacles to philosophical progress. The craving for recognition — for titles, offices, and the esteem of the powerful — is a form of slavery. He asks his students to examine what they are willing to sacrifice for honor: "You were content to stay at home with the honors which you had, but you desired something more" (045-020). The restlessness that drives people to abandon their station in pursuit of greater honor is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], a failure to understand what truly matters.

The Stoic teacher's [[disputation|argument]] is characteristically blunt. He points to the man "honored by Caesar" and asks what this really means: "He is called fortunate because he is honored by Caesar. I say: What does he get?" (063-057). The answer is nothing of moral worth. Caesar's favor can be withdrawn at any moment; it confers no lasting good. What [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls the "honor which is from nature" — the dignity of acting according to reason — is "so safe" and "so free from [[jealousy|envy]]" that no external power can touch it (061-091).

The core principle is simple: if honor is an external, then losing it cannot harm you. "How then can this be want of honor (dishonor)?" (092-006). The answer is that it cannot — unless you have assigned moral value to what others think of you. The Stoic who has internalized this truth is free from the desperate scramble for recognition that consumes those who place their good in the hands of others.

## Key Passages

- (092-001) — "I shall live unhonored and be nobody nowhere." [[epictetus|Epictetus]] confronts the fear of obscurity directly.
- (092-002) — "If want of honor (atimia) is an evil, you cannot be in any evil through the fault of another."
- (092-006) — "How then can this be want of honor (dishonor)?"
- (045-020) — "You were content to stay at home with the honors which you had, but you desired something more."
- (063-057) — "He is called fortunate because he is honored by Caesar. I say: What does he get?"
- (061-091) — "What possession is so safe, or what honor (rank, character) so free from [[jealousy|envy]]?"
- (062-008) — Do not lament "for not possessing honorable offices."
- (062-019) — Being "honored by the great" — examine whether this is truly a good.
- (065-048) — "Dishonored thee by my acts" — a recognition that dishonor comes from one's own actions, not others' opinions.
- (097-031) — Having "the inferior part, in honor, in office, in the courts" — but the superior part is within.
