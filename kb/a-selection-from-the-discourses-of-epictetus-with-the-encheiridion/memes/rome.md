# Rome

**Rome** was the capital of the Roman Empire and the center of political, economic, and intellectual life during [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s lifetime (1st–2nd century CE). It is the city where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] spent his early life as a slave, studied philosophy under [[Musonius Rufus]], and later taught as a free man.

His experience of Rome — its extremes of wealth and power, its imperial court, its slaves and masters — profoundly shaped his teaching on [[freedom]] and the indifference of [[externals]].

## Location

Rome is situated on the Tiber River in the region of Latium, on the western coast of central Italy. By the 1st century CE, it was the largest city in the ancient world, with a population estimated at over one million inhabitants. As the seat of imperial power, it drew philosophers, rhetoricians, and teachers from across the Greek-speaking world — including [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own teacher, [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]].

The city was also, for Stoic philosophers, a theater of moral danger and opportunity. "Terrible is death, terrible is exile," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] summarizes the common attitude toward the risks of living in Rome (015-011). His philosophical task was to teach his Roman students that these things are not terrible — that the only true danger is a disordered will.

## Historical Context

Rome's political significance during [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s lifetime is inseparable from his biography. He lived under a succession of emperors — [[nero|Nero]], Vespasian, Titus, Domitian, Nerva, Trajan, and possibly Hadrian — spanning the most turbulent and the most stable periods of the early Empire.

As a slave in the household of Epaphroditus, a freedman of [[nero|Nero]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] had direct exposure to the imperial court. This experience gave his teaching on the slavery of desire a concrete foundation. Having seen the most powerful people in the world enslaved by their own passions, he could speak with unique authority about the nature of true freedom.

The philosopher ban of Domitian in 89–93 CE was the decisive rupture (P02-036). "After the expulsion of the philosophers from Rome by Domitian, he retired to [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]" (P02-037). This forced exile, while disruptive, proved providential. At [[Nicopolis]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] developed the distinctive teaching style that Arrian would preserve.

## Significance in the Discourses

Rome appears throughout the *Discourses* as both a real place and a symbol. As a real place, it is the city [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s students inhabit or visit — "We are now sending a scout to Rome" (015-007) — with all its attendant anxieties about lawsuits, political advancement, and social status.

In Discourse 45, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] questions a student who is going to Rome on account of a lawsuit. "Tell me what you will do in Rome, whether you will succeed or fail" (045-005). The exchange reveals [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s method: he uses the concrete ambition — going to Rome to become governor (045-019) — to expose the student's deeper confusion about what is truly good.

Rome also serves as a symbol of the world of [[externals]] — the realm where reputation, wealth, and power seem to matter. "Though we are often in Rome, though such a crowd meet us" (048-006), the philosopher remains inwardly indifferent. The true Stoic lives in Rome as if he did not live in Rome — present in the world but not mastered by it.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s advice to students living in Rome is characteristically direct. "If you be in Rome, do not imagine what the life in Rome might be elsewhere, but think only of the life in Rome" (054-147). The command is to accept one's circumstances fully rather than fantasizing about alternatives.
