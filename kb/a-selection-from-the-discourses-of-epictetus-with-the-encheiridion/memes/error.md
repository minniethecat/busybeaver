## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, error (Greek: *hamartēma*, ἁμάρτημα) is a mistaken action or judgment rooted in ignorance of what is truly good or bad. Unlike the common notion of error as mere inaccuracy, the Stoic concept carries moral weight: every error is a failure to correctly apply reason to [[appearances|impressions]]. However, [[stoicism|Stoicism]] distinguishes error from vice (*[[vice|kakia]]*): error results from incorrect understanding, while vice is a settled disposition of the soul. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] teaches that all human error is ultimately correctable through philosophical [[education|training]], because no one errs willingly — all error stems from being mistaken about where one's true good lies.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats error as a universal human condition arising from misplaced assent. In the Discourses, he repeatedly identifies error with "living in deception" and contrasts it with the philosophical life of correct judgment. A distinctive Epictetan emphasis is that error is avoidable through the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]]: by pausing between impression and assent, one can test whether a thing is truly good, bad, or indifferent before acting.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks pointedly: "Who then chooses to live in error? No man. Who chooses to live in deception, to be impetuous, unjust, intemperate, querulous, mean?" (057-005). The answer is no one — yet people do err because they are mistaken about what constitutes their good. Error, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is not a sin but a cognitive failure that philosophy is designed to remedy. He locates the source of error in the failure to properly apply the discipline of assent: avoiding error requires correctly distinguishing between knowledge (*epistēmē*), opinion (*doxa*), and error itself (023-002).

The therapeutic dimension is central: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges his students to "escape at least a few errors" (067-040) through steady [[exercise|practice]], treating error reduction as a measurable sign of moral progress. He observes that "in the case of most errors, for this reason chiefly they occur — because we do not apply our preconceptions to particular cases" (034-016), highlighting his methodological approach to error prevention through the correct application of preconceptions.

## Key Passages

- "Who then chooses to live in error? ... No one then who lives in error is free." (020-070–071) — Error as slavery; freedom requires correct knowledge.
- "[[knowledge|Knowledge]] or opinion or error" (023-002) — The epistemological triad distinguishing certainty, probability, and mistake.
- "In the case of most errors ... they occur because we do not apply our preconceptions to particular cases" (034-016) — The methodological source of error.
- "Escape at least a few errors" (067-040) — Error reduction as the measure of philosophical progress.
- "All errors and [[fortune|misfortune]]s that have come to the human race" (037-035) — Error as the root of human suffering across history.
