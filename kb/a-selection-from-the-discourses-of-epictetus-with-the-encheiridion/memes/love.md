## Definition

Love in Stoic philosophy is not a single unified concept but encompasses several distinct Greek terms. *Erōs* denotes passionate desire or romantic attachment, while *philia* refers to affection, friendship, and familial bonds. The Stoics held that love, properly understood, is an impulse of the rational soul directed toward what is genuinely good. When love attaches to externals — persons, possessions, or reputation — it becomes a passion (*pathos*) that disturbs the mind. When love is directed by reason toward virtue and aligned with nature, it is a proper affection (*eupatheia*) consistent with the life of wisdom.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] discusses love with remarkable psychological acuity, diagnosing the common experience of romantic love as a form of self-imposed slavery. He frames the lover's predicament bluntly: "Is any man then master of another man? ... How is it then that you said 'the woman whom I love'? 'Yes, her whom you love.' Is she then your slave?" (014-021). The lover who claims to be mastered by passion has, in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s analysis, made a false judgment about the beloved's relationship to his own happiness.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the central problem with passionate love is its externality: the beloved is not under the lover's [[power|control]], yet the lover has staked his contentment on that person. He notes that lovers excuse their condition by saying "I am not my own master; I am in love" (014-059) — offering passion as a defense for intemperance, just as others plead illness or poverty. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] rejects this excuse, insisting that love, like all other impulses, falls under the governance of the rational faculty (*prohairesis*).

The proper exercise of love, in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s view, depends on the capacity for correct discrimination. He argues that "he who has been instructed, as the matter requires, can also know how to love; but he who cannot distinguish a good man from a rogue ... cannot possess the power of loving" (035-007 through 035-009). Genuine love is therefore an achievement of philosophical [[education|training]], not a spontaneous emotion. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends this analysis to familial love — "is a man a father, a child, or beloved?" (035-012) — suggesting that all forms of human attachment must be integrated with the recognition that externals are ultimately indifferent.

He also notes the human inclination toward love and community: "man is an animal that loves contemplation" (019-075) and humans possess a natural "desire of community and mutual love" (048-019). This reflects the Stoic doctrine of *oikeiōsis* — the natural affection that rational beings feel for one another — which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats as a foundation for ethical life, provided it is governed by sound judgment.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] interrogates the logic of romantic [[power|mastery]]: the lover claims the beloved is "the woman whom I love" yet cannot [[power|control]] her — exposing the contradiction at the heart of passionate attachment (014-021 through 014-059).
- He identifies the love of money as a diagnostic case: "the increase of the mind confirms the love of money" (033-020), showing that attachment to wealth follows the same psychological pattern as attachment to persons.
- The intemperate man "alleges love as an excuse" for his behavior (034-019), treating passion as a justification rather than recognizing it as a failure of judgment.
- Love is presented as a skill requiring philosophical [[education|training]]: only one who can distinguish good from bad can "know how to love" (035-007 through 035-009).
- He acknowledges love's vicissitudes — the lover who "hates, spurns, curses" (035-013) the same person he once adored — illustrating how passions oscillate when founded on externals.
- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] invokes the natural human "desire of community and mutual love" (048-019) as a positive foundation for social affection grounded in reason.
- He questions whether one must "love a little reputation" (053-012), warning against moderating rather than eliminating attachment to external validation.
