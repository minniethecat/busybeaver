# Nicopolis

## Historical Context

**Nicopolis** ("City of Victory") was founded by the emperor Augustus to commemorate his victory at the Battle of Actium (31 BCE). Located on the western coast of Greece in the region of Epirus, Nicopolis was a substantial Roman colony with Greek cultural institutions.

## Epictetus's School

After the philosopher ban of 93 CE forced him from [[rome|Rome]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] chose Nicopolis as the site for his philosophical school. Here he taught until his death around 135 CE, attracting students from across the Roman world. It was in Nicopolis that Arrian, a young Roman of equestrian rank, attended [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s lectures and recorded the teachings that survive as the Discourses and [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]].

## Significance

Nicopolis became, through [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s presence, one of the most important centers of Stoic philosophy in the late 1st and early 2nd centuries CE. The school he established there carried forward the tradition of [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]] and represented the mature phase of Roman [[stoicism|Stoicism]] before the era of Marcus Aurelius.
