# Flattery

## Definition

**Flattery** (*kolakeia*) is, in Stoic moral philosophy, the vice of winning favour through insincere praise or attentiveness — courting the powerful by saying what pleases them rather than what is true.

It is a species of moral slavery: the flatterer trades his own judgment and freedom for another person's goodwill, treating externals such as approval, position, and invitations as if they were goods worth buying at the price of his [[modesty|self-respect]].

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats flattery as a symptom of misplaced desire — of valuing another's favour over one's own moral character. He sketches the type in unflattering terms: the man who gets on by flattering "is free from [[modesty|shame]], and is watchful" (052-004), alert to every [[fortune|chance]] to please.

The point is not that the courtier fails to win worldly advantages — he may win them — but that he pays for them with the only thing that is truly his own.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] frames the exchange as a literal transaction. When someone offers you a supper or a favour in return for your [[obedience|compliance]], you are being asked to "sell[] it for praise (flattery)," to "sell[] it for [[mask|persona]]l attention" (093-018). [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s rule is bracingly clear: "Give then the price, if it is for your interest, for which it is sold.

But if you wish both not to give the price and to obtain the things, you are insatiable and silly" (093-018). And even when you refuse the bargain you are not the poorer: "You have... the not flattering of him, whom you did not choose to flatter" (093-022). Integrity is itself the reward.

The root of flattery is fear of those whose favour we seek. Once a person has grasped what is truly his own, the motive collapses: "Remembering this, whom will you still flatter or fear?" (016-028). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] also diagnoses a religious form of the vice — bargaining with seers for favourable predictions: "This is the reason why we flatter the [[divinity|divine]]rs" (024-019).

In every case the cure is the same: withdraw value from the external and place it where it belongs, in one's own moral choice.

## Key Passages

- **The [[modesty|shame]]less flatterer**: "he flatters, is free from [[modesty|shame]], and is watchful" (052-004).

- **Flattery as a sale**: "sells it for praise (flattery), he sells it for [[mask|persona]]l attention. Give then the price, if it is for your interest" (093-018).

- **The reward of refusal**: "You have... the not flattering of him, whom you did not choose to flatter" (093-022).

- **The fear behind flattery**: "Remembering this, whom will you still flatter or fear?" (016-028).

- **Flattering the [[divinity|divine]]rs**: "This is the reason why we flatter the [[divinity|divine]]rs" (024-019).

## Related Concepts

- **[[freedom|Freedom]]** (*[[freedom|eleutheria]]*) — Flattery is its opposite: the voluntary surrender of independence for favour.

- **[[externals|Externals]]** — The approval and advantages the flatterer pursues are "indifferent" externals, not true goods.

- **Self-conceit** — A related distortion of judgment about one's own worth and the opinions of others.
