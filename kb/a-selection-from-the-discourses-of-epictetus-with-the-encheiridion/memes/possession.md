## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, possessions (*ktēsis*) — meaning property, belongings, and material goods — are classified among the externals (*ta ektos*), things outside the faculty of choice. They belong to the category of indifferents (*adiaphora*): neither good nor evil in themselves, they can be used well or badly depending on the moral character of the possessor. The Stoic doctrine holds that possessions are not required for happiness (*eudaimonia*), nor does their loss constitute genuine harm. Where the majority of humanity sees possessions as goods to be pursued and protected, the Stoic sees them as merely the material of virtuous or vicious action.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to the theme of possessions repeatedly, often with sharp, memorable formulations. His central teaching is that attachment to possessions is itself a form of slavery. "If I admire my poor body, I have given myself away as a slave; if I admire my poor possessions, I also make myself a slave" (016-024). The logic is straightforward: whatever you value above your moral integrity becomes your master.

He grounds this teaching in the fundamental distinction between what is "ours" and what is "not ours," listing possessions alongside body, family, and reputation as externals: "the body, the parts of the body, possessions, parents, brothers, children, country" (014-028). These things are entrusted to us by nature, not owned by us in any morally significant sense.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also uses possession as a test of character. When people boast of their possessions, he redirects attention to what truly matters: "In possessions? Look at Myro, look at Ophellius" (053-053) — implying that wealth and property are no measure of human worth. The truly excellent person is not the one with many possessions but the one who possesses virtue, for "what makes a man beautiful? Is it not the possession of the excellence of a man?" (039-022). By analogizing moral excellence to a possession, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] subverts the ordinary valuation of property: the only possession worth caring about is virtue.

## Stoic Context

The classification of possessions as indifferents traces back to the founders of [[stoicism|Stoicism]]. [[zeno|Zeno of Citium]] taught that only virtue is good and only vice is evil; everything else — wealth, property, health, reputation — is indifferent. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] refined this by distinguishing "preferred" indifferents (which have some natural value, like adequate resources) from "dispreferred" indifferents (like poverty). Seneca, himself a wealthy man, wrestled explicitly with the question of possessions in *De Vita Beata*, arguing that the sage can possess wealth without being possessed by it. [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teacher, advocated voluntary simplicity — owning only what is necessary — as a philosophical discipline. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] carries this tradition forward with an especially sharp focus on the psychology of attachment: the problem is not having things but being enslaved to them.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that attachment to possessions constitutes self-enslavement: "if I admire my poor possessions, I also make myself a slave" (016-024).
- Possessions are catalogued among the externals beyond the will's governance (014-028), (007-005).
- He rejects property as a measure of worth, asking "In possessions? Look at Myro, look at Ophellius" (053-053).
- The concept of true "possession" is redirected toward virtue: "what makes a man beautiful? Is it not the possession of the excellence of a man?" (039-022).
- Material possessions are described as things "about which are not in your power" (057-077), reinforcing that happiness does not require "the full possession of the things which are desired" (057-174).
