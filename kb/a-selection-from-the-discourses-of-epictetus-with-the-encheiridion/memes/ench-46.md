[114-001]On no occasion call yourself a philosopher, and do not speak much among the

[114-002]uninstructed about theorems (philosophical rules, precepts); but do that which

[114-003]follows from them. For example, at a banquet do not say how a man ought to eat,

[114-004]but eat as you ought to eat. For remember that in this way [[socrates|Socrates]] also

[114-005]altogether avoided ostentation. Persons used to come to him and ask to be

[114-006]recommended by him to philosophers, and he used to take them to philosophers,

[114-007]so easily did he submit to being overlooked. Accordingly, if any conversation

[114-008]should arise among uninstructed persons about any theorem, generally be silent;

[114-009]for there is great danger that you will immediately vomit up what you have not

[114-010]digested. And when a man shall say to you that you know nothing, and you are

[114-011]not vexed, then be sure that you have begun the work (of philosophy). For even

[114-012]sheep do not vomit up their grass and show to the shepherds how much they have

[114-013]eaten; but when they have internally digested the pasture, they produce

[114-014]externally wool and milk. Do you also show not your theorems to the

[114-015]uninstructed, but show the acts which come from their digestion.
