## Definition

Habit (*ethos* in Greek, ἦθος) is a settled disposition formed by repeated actions over time. In Stoic moral psychology, habits are not merely behavioral routines but the very substance of character: a person is, in large measure, the sum of their habitual responses.

The Stoics understood that virtue itself must become habitual — it is not enough to know the right thing; one must train oneself to do it automatically, without hesitation. This is why the daily [[exercise|practice]] of philosophical exercises (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*) is central to the Stoic way of life.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] gives habit a central role in his practical ethics. He insists that philosophical understanding is insufficient without the corresponding [[education|training]] of habits. "Every habit and faculty is formed and strengthened by the corresponding actions: the habit of walking by walking, the habit of running by running" (033-001–033-003).

This principle applies equally to moral qualities. If you wish to be patient, you must [[exercise|practice]] [[endurance|patience]]; if you wish to avoid anger, you must repeatedly refrain from angry responses. The soul, like the body, grows strong or weak through exercise: "If you would not be of an angry temper, do not feed the habit: throw nothing on it which will increase it" (033-026).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s approach is strikingly practical. He recognizes that bad habits cannot simply be eliminated by an act of will — they must be replaced by contrary habits. "To the habit of anger we must oppose the habit of mildness; to sophisms, the habit of attention" (017-014).

He also warns that habits, once formed, have a momentum of their own. An old habit "in a manner throws fuel upon the fire" of passion (033-012). The time to form right habits is now — before wrong ones become entrenched. This is why he urges his students to begin philosophical [[exercise|practice]] immediately, not postpone it to some future date.

## Key Passages

> Every habit and faculty is formed and strengthened by the corresponding actions: the habit of walking by walking, the habit of running by running. If you would be a reader, read; if a writer, write. (033-001–033-003)

> Would you make anything a habit, do it; if you would not make it a habit, do not do it. (033-008)

> If it is habit which annoys us, we must try to find means to oppose to one habit the contrary habit. (017-009–017-014)
