# Freedom

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, and particularly in the teachings of [[epictetus|Epictetus]], **freedom** (*eleutheria*) is not a political or external condition but an inner state — the power of the rational faculty to assent or withhold assent independently of external circumstances. Freedom is the natural condition of a person who understands what is "up to us" and what is not, and who restricts their desires to the former [[solitude|alone]].

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] defines freedom succinctly: "Is freedom anything else than the power of living as we choose?" (020-069). This power, however, is not license to do whatever one pleases in the external world, but the capacity to maintain one's moral purpose (*prohairesis*) in accordance with nature, regardless of external pressures.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes freedom one of the central themes of his teaching. Throughout the Discourses, he returns repeatedly to the question of what constitutes genuine freedom and how it can be attained.

Freedom, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is incompatible with desire for externals. A person who desires wealth, reputation, or even bodily safety has placed their happiness in something outside their [[power|control]], and thereby made themselves a slave. "What then, is freedom madness? Certainly not; for madness and freedom do not consist together. But I would have you know that freedom is a noble and valuable thing" (008-025).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies the free person as one who has mastered the discipline of desire: "For what purpose then have I received these things? To use them. How long? So long as the owner permits. But if they are not my own, if the owner demands them back, I will give them up" (057-120). The free person treats all externals as borrowed goods, returning them without [[grief|distress]] when nature or circumstance demands.

The path to freedom lies through rigorous self-examination and the continual [[exercise|practice]] of distinguishing [[appearances|impressions]] from reality. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that freedom is achieved not by changing external conditions but by transforming one's judgments: "You have never sought [[firmness|constancy]], freedom from perturbation, and peace; you have never been associated with any person for this purpose" (056-032).

## Key Passages

- **The nature of freedom**: "Is freedom anything else than the power of living as we choose?" (020-069). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] poses this rhetorical question to establish that freedom means self-determination according to rational choice.
- **Freedom and externals**: "Know that freedom is a noble and valuable thing. But to desire things not in our power is to be enslaved" (008-025, 008-033). Freedom requires restricting desire to what is within our [[power|control]].
- **Freedom from perturbation**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] teaches that the free person acts "with firmness and freedom from perturbations because they understand what is and is not their own" (022-015).
- **Freedom as the goal of philosophy**: The purpose of philosophical [[education|training]] "is for kingship, for freedom, for happiness, for freedom from perturbation" (033-052).

## Related Concepts

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — Freedom is the result of virtuous living; only the virtuous person is truly free.
- **[[prohairesis|Prohairesis]]** (moral purpose) — The faculty of choice that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies as the seat of freedom. Freedom is the proper functioning of prohairesis.
- **[[externals|Externals]]** (*ta aprohaireta*) — Things outside our [[power|control]]. Attachment to externals is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the cause of all slavery.
- **[[nature|Nature]]** (*[[nature|physis]]*) — Freedom consists in living according to nature, which means accepting the [[divinity|divine]] order of the cosmos.
