## Definition

Action (Greek: *hormē*, impulse toward action; *praxis*, deed) in Stoic ethics belongs to the second of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s three disciplines — the discipline of action (*to hormētikon*). The Stoics held that all actions proceed from judgments (*[[doctrine|dogma]]ta*): "opinions are the causes of all actions" (045-012). A correct action (*kathēkon*) is one that accords with nature and is performed from a virtuous disposition. The Stoic sage performs all actions with reservation (*hupexairesis*), recognizing that outcomes depend on externals while the moral quality of the action depends solely on the agent's will. This doctrine places action at the center of Stoic moral psychology: character is not a static possession but is continuously constituted through the actions one performs.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops a dynamic theory of action in which deeds and character mutually reinforce each other. He argues that "modest actions preserve the modest man, and the actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man" (025-024 through 025-025). Conversely, "contrary actions strengthen contrary characters" (025-026). This is not a truism but a substantive psychological claim: each action leaves a trace on the soul, fortifying the disposition that produced it. The person who acts modestly becomes more modest; the person who acts [[modesty|shame]]lessly entrenches [[modesty|shame]]lessness.

The practical implication is that moral [[education|training]] (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*) must extend to action, not merely to intellectual understanding. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that philosophy has failed if it remains theoretical: "What is the use of these things, if it is only for the purpose of learning what is said, and not for acting?" (060-031 through 060-036). The worth of philosophical study is measured not by the doctrines one can recite but by the quality of one's deeds.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] locates the source of all actions in the ruling faculty. "Whose movements to action" are hindered when the will is impeded (057-002). Since action proceeds from choice (*prohairesis*), no external force can compel a wrong action — the agent always retains the power of assent. He illustrates this with the striking image of the theatre: the philosopher attends to the drama of life "free from distraction" (019-077), responding to events with deliberate action rather than impulsive reaction.

The distinction between actions that depend on us and those that do not underlies [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire teaching on action. "No man is damaged by an action which is not his own" (018-064) — moral harm can only come from one's own choices. This provides both a ground of invulnerability and a summons to responsibility: every action matters because it constructs the self.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] declares that "opinions are the causes of all actions" (045-012), grounding action in judgment.
- The dynamic theory of character: "modest actions preserve the modest man" and "contrary actions strengthen contrary characters" (025-024 through 025-026).
- He warns against the separation of theory and [[exercise|practice]]: the purpose of philosophy is "that we may act well" (060-037), not merely that we may speak well.
- Actions are likened to habits: the soul "is increased by the corresponding actions" just as the body is "increased by the corresponding actions" (033-002).
- [[philosophy|Philosophy]] applied to daily life: "Before each daily action thou hast scanned" (046-006) — a verse [[epictetus|Epictetus]] quotes approvingly to illustrate the premeditation of deeds.
- No external action can harm the agent: "no man is damaged by an action which is not his own" (018-064).
- The contrast between "the place of a sheep" and "a good action" (059-004) — [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s vivid image for the dignity of human action when governed by reason.
