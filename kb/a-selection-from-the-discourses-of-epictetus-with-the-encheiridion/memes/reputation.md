## Definition

Reputation (*doxa*) is one of the classic Stoic "indifferents" (*adiaphora*) — something that is neither good nor evil in itself and lies outside the ruling faculty's [[power|control]]. In the Stoic framework, what others think of you cannot affect your moral character; only your own judgments and choices can do that. Reputation may be a "preferred indifferent" in some contexts, but [[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats it with particular contempt, regarding the desire for reputation as one of the most persistent and corrosive sources of mental slavery.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] delivers one of his most memorable lines on reputation: "Reputation is the noise of madmen" (015-017). This pithy dismissal captures his entire attitude toward public opinion. What others say about you is noise — it has no substance, no truth-value that matters, and no power over the soul unless the soul grants it power. Those who chase reputation are, by implication, listening to the noise of madmen and mistaking it for music.

He includes reputation in the classic triad of indifferents: "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation" (025-039). But reputation occupies a special place in his teaching because it is the indifferent that most directly threatens the student of philosophy. The desire for reputation makes people "lovers of reputation" (*philodoxoi*), a term he uses with scorn. "If he reads and writes for reputation, I say that he is a lover of reputation" (060-063). The act itself — [[books|reading]], writing, philosophizing — is indifferent; the motive determines its moral character.

In Discourse 045, he paints a vivid picture of the person who has achieved reputation and then lost it: "Where are they now who then seemed your equal?" (045-030). Reputation is fleeting; those who build their identity on it build on sand. The same people who praised you yesterday will condemn you tomorrow, and neither the praise nor the condemnation touches what you truly are.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] reinforces the point with characteristic brevity: "In our power are opinion, movement toward a thing, desire, aversion; not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices" (069-004). Reputation belongs firmly in the second list — not in our power, and therefore not a proper object of desire or aversion. To care about reputation is to care about something that is not yours, which is the definition of a slave in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s psychology.

His advice is not to cultivate a good reputation but to become indifferent to reputation altogether. "Take my property, take my reputation, take those who are about me" (019-022) — the Stoic's response to threatened loss is not anxiety but readiness. What cannot be taken — the moral purpose — is the only thing worth guarding.

## Key Passages

- (010-008) — Reputation listed alongside land and health as a thing people mistakenly cling to.
- (015-017) — "Reputation is the noise of madmen." [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s sharpest dismissal of public opinion.
- (019-022) — "Take my property, take my reputation, take those who are about me." The Stoic's readiness to lose all externals.
- (025-039) — "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation." The classic triad.
- (045-030) — "Where are they now who then seemed your equal?" Reputation's impermanence.
- (053-012) — "You must not love a little reputation." The subtle danger of even small attachments to reputation.
- (060-063) — "If he reads and writes for reputation, I say that he is a lover of reputation."
- (069-004) — "Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices." The dichotomy of [[power|control]].
