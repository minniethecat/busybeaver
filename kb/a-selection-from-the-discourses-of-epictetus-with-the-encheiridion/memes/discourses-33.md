[033-001]Every habit and faculty is

[033-002]maintained and increased by the corresponding actions: the habit of walking by

[033-003]walking, the habit of running by running. If you would be a [[good]] reader, read;

[033-004]if a writer, write. But when you shall not have read for thirty days in

[033-005]succession, but have done something else, you will know the consequence. In the

[033-006]same way, if you shall have lain down ten days, get up and attempt to make a

[033-007]long walk, and you will see how your legs are weakened. Generally then if you

[033-008]would make anything a habit, do it; if you would not make it a habit, do not do

[033-009]it, but accustom yourself to do something else in place of it.

[033-010]So it is with respect to the affections of the soul: when you have been angry,

[033-011]you must know that not only has this evil befallen you, but that you have also

[033-012]increased the habit, and in a manner thrown fuel upon fire.

[033-013]In this manner certainly, as philosophers say, also diseases of the mind grow

[033-014]up. For when you have once desired money, if [[reason]] be applied to lead to a

[033-015]perception of the evil, the [[desire]] is stopped, and the ruling faculty of our

[033-016]mind is restored to the original authority. But if you apply no means of cure,

[033-017]it no longer returns to the same state, but being again excited by the

[033-018]corresponding appearance, it is inflamed to desire quicker than before: and

[033-019]when this takes place continually, it is henceforth hardened (made callous),

[033-020]and the disease of the mind confirms the love of money. For he who has had a

[033-021]fever, and has been relieved from it, is not in the same state that he was

[033-022]before, unless he has been completely cured. Something of the kind happens also

[033-023]in diseases of the soul. Certain traces and blisters are left in it, and unless

[033-024]a man shall completely efface them, when he is again lashed on the same places,

[033-025]the lash will produce not blisters (weals) but sores. If then you wish not to

[033-026]be of an angry temper, do not feed the habit: throw nothing on it which will

[033-027]increase it: at first keep quiet, and count the days on which you have not been

[033-028]angry. I used to be in [[passion]] every day; now every second day; then every

[033-029]third, then every fourth. But if you have intermitted thirty days, make a

[033-030]sacrifice to God. For the habit at first begins to be weakened, and then is

[033-031]completely destroyed. “I have not been vexed to-day, nor the day after, nor yet

[033-032]on any succeeding day during two or three months; but I took care when some

[033-033]exciting things happened.” Be assured that you are in a good way.

[033-034]How then shall this be done? Be willing at length to be approved by yourself,

[033-035]be willing to appear beautiful to God, desire to be in purity with your own

[033-036]pure self and with God. Then when any such appearance visits you, Plato says,

[033-037]Have recourse to expiations, go a suppliant to the temples of the averting

[033-038]deities. It is even sufficient if you resort to the society of noble and just

[033-039]men, and compare yourself with them, whether you find one who is living or

[033-040]dead.

[033-041]But in the first place, be not hurried away by the rapidity of the appearance,

[033-042]but say, Appearances, wait for me a little; let me see who you are, and what

[033-043]you are about; let me put you to the test. And then do not allow the appearance

[033-044]to lead you on and draw lively pictures of the things which will follow; for if

[033-045]you do, it will carry you off wherever it pleases. But rather bring in to

[033-046]oppose it some other beautiful and noble appearance, and cast out this base

[033-047]appearance. And if you are accustomed to be exercised in this way, you will see

[033-048]what shoulders, what sinews, what strength you have. But now it is only

[033-049]trifling words, and nothing more.

[033-050]This is the true athlete, the man who exercises himself against such

[033-051]appearances. Stay, wretch, do not be carried away. Great is the combat, divine

[033-052]is the work; it is for kingship, for freedom, for [[happiness]], for freedom from

[033-053]perturbation. Remember God; call on him as a helper and protector, as men at

[033-054]sea call on the Dioscuri in a storm. For what is a greater storm than that

[033-055]which comes from appearances which are violent and drive away the reason? For

[033-056]the storm itself, what else is it but an appearance? For take away the fear of

[033-057]death, and suppose as many thunders and lightnings as you please, and you will

[033-058]know what calm and serenity there is in the ruling faculty. But if you have

[033-059]once been defeated and say that you will conquer hereafter, and then say the

[033-060]same again, be assured that you will at last be in so wretched a condition and

[033-061]so weak that you will not even know afterwards that you are doing wrong, but

[033-062]you will even begin to make apologies (defences) for your wrong-doing, and then

[033-063]you will confirm the saying of Hesiod to be true,

[033-064]With constant ills the dilatory strives.
