# Magnanimity

**Magnanimity** (*megalopsychia*, literally "greatness of soul") is the Stoic virtue by which a person recognizes the resources of [[reason]] already within and thereby rises above the apparent blows of fortune. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], magnanimity is not a special endowment reserved for the few but a capacity given by [[nature]] to every human being who will properly use it.

The source of magnanimity is the recognition that external events — poverty, exile, death — cannot touch the ruling faculty (*[[prohairesis]]*) unless we permit it. Once a person truly grasps this, no external circumstance can make him small.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, magnanimity is the disposition of soul that faces great fortune and great [[fortune|misfortune]] with equal steadiness. It is closely allied to courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*) and endurance (*[[endurance|karteria]]*), but distinct in its emphasis on the inner source of strength: magnanimity flows from the knowledge that the self is inviolable, not from the effort of steeling oneself against difficulty.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] connects magnanimity directly to the gifts bestowed by [[nature]]: "Have you not received greatness of soul? Have you not received manliness? Have you not received endurance?" (006-050). The rhetorical form of his questioning is significant — he does not urge his students to *acquire* greatness of soul, but reminds them that they already possess it and have merely forgotten (006-051).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats magnanimity not as an aspirational ideal but as a birthright that must be claimed through the proper use of reason. In Discourse 6, "On [[providence|Providence]]," he confronts his students with a sharp rebuke: they complain about the difficulties of life while ignoring the powers they have already been given.

The key insight of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment is that magnanimity is the default condition of a rational being who sees things as they are. When a person complains or finds fault with circumstances, they demonstrate "meanness of spirit" — the opposite of magnanimity (006-070).

Meanness is not a lack of ability but a failure of recognition: the person does not see what [[nature]] has already provided (006-071).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] ties magnanimity to the discipline of [[desire]]: the person of great soul desires only what is within the will's power, and therefore can never be frustrated. The person of mean spirit desires [[externals]] — wealth, reputation, safety — and therefore lives in perpetual anxiety.

This same principle appears in the treatment of [[courage]]: bearing things bravely is "a thing within the power of the will: it is a good" (044-010). The magnanimous person and the courageous person draw from the same inner source.

## Key Passages

> Have you not received greatness of soul? Have you not received manliness? Have you not received endurance? And why do I trouble myself about anything that can happen if I possess greatness of soul? (006-050)

> Yet I will show to you that you have powers and means for greatness of soul and manliness; but what powers you have for finding fault and making accusations, do you show me. (006-072)

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] rebukes those who, "through meanness of spirit, betaking yourselves to fault-finding and making charges against God" (006-070), fail to acknowledge the greatness already within them.

The magnanimous disposition manifests itself in action as courage: bearing difficulty bravely is "a thing within the power of the will: it is a good. If we train ourselves in this manner, we shall make progress" (044-010).
