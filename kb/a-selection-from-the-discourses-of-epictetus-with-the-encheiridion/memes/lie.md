## Definition

In Stoic ethics, lying (*pseudos*) is not merely a social wrong but a corruption of the rational faculty itself. To lie is to knowingly assent to a false impression, and since assent is the sole act within the power of the will (*proairesis*), lying constitutes a direct corruption of the self. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the Socratic principle that every soul naturally assents to truth and recoils from falsehood (041-005); the liar therefore damages the very faculty that distinguishes humans from animals. Lying is classified as a vice — a disturbance of the ruling principle — and is incompatible with the Stoic ideal of the sage who never forms false opinions.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats [[truth|truthfulness]] as fundamental to the philosophical life and falsehood as a primary symptom of the untrained mind. He teaches that assent must be guarded: "be not deceived" is among his first [[education|instruction]]s to students (004-029). The person who assents to falsehood is "deceived" and suffers for it: "show her plainly that she is deceived, and she will not do it" (018-020). He insists that no one can compel another to assent to falsehood — "who can compel you to assent to that which is false? No man" (053-104). The liar's predicament is self-inflicted: the false judgment, not the external consequence, is the evil. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] distinguishes between the mere statement of falsehood (error) and the knowing embrace of it (lying), locating the moral gravity in the assent. In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], falsehood is treated under the third topic (*topos*) of assent and certainty (119-007).

## Stoic Context

The Stoic epistemology of truth and falsehood is rooted in the concept of the "cognitive impression" (*phantasia kataleptike*) — a clear and distinct presentation that compels assent. Zeno compared the open hand (impression) to the closed fist (assent) to illustrate the two-stage process. Lying is therefore understood as a failure at the point of assent. The broader Socratic background — that no one knowingly does wrong — makes lying particularly pernicious: it is not ignorance but a willful disorder of the rational faculty. Seneca and Marcus Aurelius both treat [[truth|truthfulness]] as a primary virtue of the Stoic, and falsehood as incompatible with the cosmic reason (*logos*) that governs the universe.
