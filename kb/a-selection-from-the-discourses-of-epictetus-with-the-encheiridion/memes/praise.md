## Definition

Praise (*epainos*) in Stoic ethics is classified as an external indifferent — something neither good nor evil in itself, whose value depends entirely on how it is pursued and received. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws a sharp distinction between the praise of the multitude, which is worthless and often corrupting, and the praise that consists in a well-reasoned approval of virtuous action. To hunger for the crowd's applause is to enslave oneself to opinion; to be indifferent to it is freedom.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes sustained attention to the psychology of praise-seeking, especially in the Discourse "To the [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]]ians" (031). He observes that most people go through life without ever asking what praise actually is or what kind of praise is worth having. "When did he hear from any one what praise is, what blame is, what the nature of each is, what kind of praise should be sought, or what kind of blame should be shunned?" (031-017–018). The unphilosophical person simply reaches for praise as a child reaches for a sweet, without reflection.

The central error, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is to value the praise of the crowd. "He knows not what a crowd is or what the praise of a crowd is" (028-030). The crowd's approval is fickle, ignorant, and based on externals — appearance, wealth, rhetorical performance — rather than on moral character. The person who has trained himself in the Stoic discipline, by contrast, "has not been undisciplined with respect to praise and blame" (031-016). He neither craves the one nor fears the other.

The danger of praise-seeking is that it makes a person a slave to others' opinions. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the striking image of a man who "sells it for praise (flattery), he sells it for [[mask|persona]]l attention" (093-018) — "it" being one's moral integrity, one's prohairesis. To exchange one's ruling faculty for the cheap coin of applause is the worst bargain a philosopher can make. When the actor on stage "has been praised, he leaves the theatre puffed up" (031-027) — a vivid warning against internalizing the audience's reaction.

Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not condemn all praise. He speaks approvingly of praising [[providence|Providence]] when one recognizes the [[divinity|divine]] order (006-002), and he notes that "we praise, we censure" (032-006) as part of our natural moral functioning. The key is that praise should be directed toward what is truly admirable — virtue, justice, moderation (039-026) — not toward externals.

## Key Passages

- (031-016–018) — The undisciplined person has never been trained with respect to praise and blame; does not know what praise is or what kind should be sought.
- (028-030–031) — "He knows not what a crowd is or what the praise of a crowd is" — the crowd's approval is worthless.
- (093-018) — "He sells it for praise (flattery), he sells it for [[mask|persona]]l attention" — the slave-market of opinion.
- (031-027) — The actor "has been praised, he leaves the theatre puffed up" — a warning against internalizing applause.
- (032-006) — "On these matters we praise, we censure" — praise has a legitimate role when directed at real goods.
- (039-025–026) — "Do you praise the moderate or the immoderate?" — praise should track virtue, not externals.
- (006-002) — "Praise [[providence|Providence]], if a man possesses" understanding of the [[divinity|divine]] order — true praise is directed at the cosmic order.
