# Temperance

**Temperance** (*sōphrosynē*) is one of the four cardinal virtues of Stoic ethics, alongside [[wisdom]], [[courage]], and [[justice]].

The Stoics held that these four are aspects of a single unified [[virtue]] — the knowledge of what is good, bad, and indifferent (004-009). Temperance is the virtue concerned with the governance of desire.

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s distinctive treatment, temperance is not a negative restraint but a positive power — the faculty already present to resist the pull of attractive appearances.

"If virtue promises good fortune, tranquillity, and happiness, certainly also the progress towards virtue is progress towards each of these things" (004-011). The temperate person is not one who has no desires, but one whose desires are correctly aligned with nature.

## Definition

In the Stoic tradition, temperance is the virtue of self-[[power|mastery]] with respect to pleasure and desire. It is not abstinence for its own sake, but the rational governance of impulse.

The Stoic analysis distinguishes temperance from mere [[abstinence|continence]]: [[abstinence|continence]] is the struggle against desire, while temperance is the settled state in which desire no longer pulls against reason.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] locates temperance within the discipline of [[desire]]: it is the power one turns to when confronted with an appearance that promises pleasure. The key insight is that this power is not something to be acquired — it is already given, and must simply be exercised.

The goal of Stoic [[education|training]] is to reach the state where "the appearances will not carry you along with them" (078-005).

## Epictetus's Treatment

The primary passage on temperance in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching appears in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], where he presents it as one of three powers available to every person facing an appearance:

"On the occasion of every accident that befalls you, remember to turn to yourself and inquire what power you have for turning it to use. If you see a fair man or a fair woman, you will find that the power to resist is temperance. If labor be presented to you, you will find that it is endurance. If it be abusive words, you will find it to be [[endurance|patience]]" (078-003–078-005).

This passage reveals several distinctive features of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s approach. First, temperance is a power already given, to be deployed at the moment of need, not a character trait developed over years.

Second, temperance is paired with endurance and [[endurance|patience]] as the three practical responses to the three classes of appearances. Third, the exercise of temperance is immediate and situational: when the attractive appearance arises, ask yourself what power you have.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not discuss temperance as an abstract virtue or analyze its subdivisions. He gives the student a usable rule, consistent with the broader Stoic view that virtue is progress (004-011). Temperance is not an all-or-nothing attainment but something one progresses toward with each act of [[abstinence|self-control]].

## Key Passages

> If you see a fair man or a fair woman, you will find that the power to resist is temperance. (078-003)

> If you have been thus formed to the proper habit, the appearances will not carry you along with them. (078-005)

> If virtue promises good fortune, tranquillity, and happiness, certainly also the progress towards virtue is progress towards each of these things. (004-011)
