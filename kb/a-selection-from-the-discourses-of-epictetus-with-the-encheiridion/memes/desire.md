# Desire

**Desire** (*orexis*) is the starting point of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire philosophical program. As the first of his three disciplines — the discipline of desire — it governs our fundamental orientation toward what we pursue and what we avoid.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], "the beginning of philosophy is the perception of the disagreement of men with one another" about what is truly good and bad (004-002). Until we recognize that our desires are misdirected toward [[externals]], reputation, and the body, we cannot begin to live as philosophers. The [[power|mastery]] of desire is therefore the gateway to [[freedom]] and the foundation of the [[good]] life.

## Definition

In Stoic psychology, **desire** (*orexis*) is the impulse (*hormē*) directed toward a perceived future good. It is distinct from **impulse** proper (which initiates action in the present) and from **aversion** (*[[aversion|ekklisis]]*), which is the corresponding negative impulse away from a perceived evil. Together, desire and aversion form the pair that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] places at the center of his first discipline.

The core Stoic insight — and [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most radical teaching — is that desire itself is not the problem. The problem is desiring what is not within our power (*eph' hēmin*). To desire only what is up to us — right [[judgment]], [[virtue|virtuous]] action, a will conformable to [[nature]] — is to be free.

> In desire and in aversion, that you may not fail in your desire, and that you may not fall into that which you would avoid. (004-026)

The discipline of desire teaches that we must "desire each thing as it happens." We accept whatever events [[nature]] ordains, not because we are passive, but because the only genuine good is a rightly directed will.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] organizes his entire pedagogical method around the management of desire. The three disciplines — desire, action, and assent — follow a developmental sequence. Desire comes first because it is the root of our emotional disturbances.

He teaches that desire must be eliminated for things outside our [[power|control]] — wealth, health, reputation — and redirected toward moral excellence. "He who desires or avoids the things which are not in his power can neither be faithful nor free" (004-048).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] vividly describes the psychology of misdirected desire: "When you have once desired money, if reason be applied to bring you to the perception of the evil, the desire is stopped, and the ruling faculty is restored to its authority" (033-014).

But if reason is not applied, "it is inflamed to desire quicker than before" (033-018). The image is of a fire that reason must extinguish before it spreads. Until we apply rational examination to our impulses, each indulgence of a misdirected desire makes the next one stronger.

The goal is *apatheia* — not the absence of all feeling, but freedom from the turbulent passions (*pathē*) that arise from desiring what is not ours. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] speaks of "desire which shall have no hindrance" (012-004): a desire so perfectly aligned with nature that it can never be frustrated.

## Stoic Context

The Stoic theory of desire is rooted in the broader Stoic psychology of the passions, developed most systematically by [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]. In the orthodox Stoic view, the passions are false judgments about what is good and bad.

Desire (*epithumia* in the standard four-passion schema) is one of the four primary passions, alongside fear (*phobos*), pleasure (*hēdonē*), and [[grief|distress]] (*lupē*). Each passion involves a judgment that something is good or bad when in fact it is indifferent (*adiaphoron*).

Zeno defined passion as an "excessive impulse" (*pleonazousa hormē*) that overrides reason. The Stoic sage (*sophos*) is free from all passions because the sage never judges anything except virtue to be good or anything except vice to be bad. This is the theoretical ideal behind [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s practical teaching.

What distinguishes [[epictetus|Epictetus]] from earlier Stoics is his pedagogical focus. Where [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] wrote technical treatises on the logic of the passions, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] speaks directly to the struggling student: "You still desire small stones and a beautiful statue" (031-081).

The discipline of desire also draws on the Cynic tradition that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] admired. [[diogenes|Diogenes of Sinope]] [[exercise|practice]]d radical simplification of desire — owning nothing, wanting nothing, and thereby achieving complete freedom. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] moderates this extremism for his Roman students, but the ideal of desire reduced to its natural minimum remains powerful.

## Key Passages

- Philosophers define desire as "the desire of good things" (004-002).

- The discipline: "In desire and in aversion, that you may not fail" (004-026).

- Misdirected desire destroys freedom: "can neither be faithful nor free" (004-048).

- [[reason|Reason]] applied to desire: "the desire is stopped, and the ruling faculty is restored" (033-014).

- Unchecked desire: "inflamed to desire quicker than before" (033-018).

- The ideal: "desire which shall have no hindrance" (012-004).

- Natural desire: "I am satisfied if I desire and avoid conformably to nature" (013-003).

- Diagnosis: "This man's desire and aversion are disordered" (028-045).
