## Definition

Friendship, in Stoic philosophy, is not merely affection or social connection — it is a moral relationship grounded in shared understanding of what is truly good. True friendship requires that both parties place their good in the will, not in externals.

If one friend values externals (wealth, reputation, comfort) above virtue, the friendship will fracture the moment those externals conflict with the friend's interest. As [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues, only the wise — those who correctly distinguish good from bad — are capable of genuine love and friendship (035-009).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes Discourse 35 entirely to friendship, offering one of the most rigorous and psychologically penetrating analyses of the subject in ancient philosophy. His central [[disputation|argument]] is that friendship cannot be built on anything other than the correct placement of one's interest.

The [[disputation|argument]] begins from human nature: every person is attached to their own interest above all things. "Every animal is attached to nothing so much as to its own interests. Whatever then appears to it an impediment to this interest, whether this be a brother, or a father, or a child, or beloved, or lover, it hates, spurns, curses" (035-010–035-013). This is not cynical — it is descriptive. The question is where one places one's interest.

If a man places his interest in his flesh, or in externals like wealth and reputation, then the moment a friend becomes an impediment to these, the friendship collapses. "Where the I and the Mine are placed, to that place of necessity the animal inclines" (035-022–035-023). But if one places one's interest in the will — in maintaining moral character and right judgment — then one's own good never conflicts with another's.

"If then I am there where my will is, then only shall I be a friend such as I ought to be, and son, and father; for this will be my interest, to maintain the character of fidelity, of modesty, of [[endurance|patience]], of abstinence, of active cooperation, of keeping my relations" (035-025–035-028).

The practical test [[epictetus|Epictetus]] poses is devastating: would you die for a friend? Would you share their danger? The Stoic answer is that doing so is not a sacrifice if your good is in your will — dying with fidelity is better than living without it. "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (035-032).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also diagnoses what destroys friendships: desire, pleasure, and the competition for externals. He notes that even marriage cannot survive when partners compete for advantage: "What is the thing which broke asunder the friendship between husband and wife, that small couch of which you speak?" (035-072–035-073).

## Key Passages

> To love, then, is only in the power of the wise. (035-009)

> Every animal is attached to nothing so much as to its own interests. Whatever then appears to it an impediment to this interest, whether this be a brother, or a father, or a child, or beloved, or lover, it hates, spurns, curses. (035-010–035-013)

> Where there is fidelity, there is friendship; where there is friendship, there is fidelity. (035-062)
