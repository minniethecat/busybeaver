## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, madness (*mania*) is not primarily a clinical condition but a moral and cognitive state in which a person abandons reason and acts contrary to nature. The Stoics held that all non-sages are in a sense "mad" because they lack perfect knowledge of what is truly good and evil. However, clinical madness represents an extreme form of this irrationality — a wholesale loss of the rational faculty's governance over [[appearances|impressions]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] distinguishes true madness from mere error: the former severs a person from the possibility of philosophical correction, while the latter remains remediable through rational discourse.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses madness in several crucial passages that illuminate his understanding of freedom and reason. When discussing whether freedom might itself be a form of madness — since it involves rejecting external constraints that others accept — he emphatically denies the equivalence: "What then, is freedom madness? Certainly not; for madness and freedom are never associated" (008-022). This clarifies that far from being akin to madness, true freedom is the perfection of rationality.

In another passage, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the "tone of madness" as a kind of energy or vehemence that arises when a person is wholly governed by passion rather than reason (030-044). The mad person has lost the capacity to examine [[appearances|impressions]] and instead reacts impulsively. He also notes that philosophical discourse "cures" people of their "madness" (027-055), using the medical metaphor common to Hellenistic philosophy: just as a physician treats bodily disease, the philosopher treats the soul's madness through [[disputation|argument]] and [[education|training]].

Perhaps most revealing is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s linking of madness with despair: a person "affected by some madness or despair" (063-014) ceases to care about consequences — a state that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] views as the abandonment of one's rational nature. This connection underscores that for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], madness is not merely cognitive error but a collapse of the will's commitment to living according to reason.

## Stoic Context

The Stoic view of madness builds on the Socratic paradox that no one does wrong willingly — all wrongdoing stems from ignorance, and in that sense all vice is a form of madness. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] wrote extensively on the passions as diseases of the soul, and the medical analogy runs throughout Stoic ethics: the passions are pathologies that require therapeutic treatment. Seneca devoted portions of his *Moral Letters* to the theme of human madness, arguing that society collectively suffers from irrationality while mistaking it for sanity. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] applies this tradition with characteristic practical focus, treating madness as something the philosophical life specifically prevents and cures.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] rejects any association between freedom and madness: "freedom and madness are never associated" (008-022).
- He invokes the therapeutic metaphor, where philosophical discourse "cures" the madness of those who hold false opinions (027-055).
- The "tone of madness" is characterized as a disordered vehemence incompatible with rational deliberation (030-044).
- Madness and despair are linked as states in which a person no longer cares about consequences, marking the collapse of rational self-governance (063-014).
