## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, the body (*sōma*) is classified as an external (*ta ektos*) and an indifferent (*adiaphoron*). It is not within the ruling faculty's (*hēgemonikon*) direct [[power|control]] and therefore cannot be a genuine good or evil. The body belongs to the same category as property, reputation, and office — things that fortune can give and take away. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the orthodox Stoic position: the body is a "preferred indifferent" (*proēgmenon*) worth caring for, but its health or illness has no bearing on virtue or happiness.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself was weak in body and lame from an early age (P02-011). His [[mask|persona]]l experience of physical limitation gives his teaching on the body a distinctive authenticity. Rather than resenting his condition, he treats it as proof that the body is not the self: "You will cut my leg, you will banish me. Can you cut my neck too? You cannot touch my moral purpose" (001-028).

He repeatedly describes the body as a fetter or bond that ties the soul to the material world. "We are fettered by these bonds, the body, I mean, and its possessions" (007-005). The body makes demands — food, shelter, protection — and through these demands fortune gains power over us. "How does [[fortune|Fortune]] have power over us? By means of the body" (007-018). Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not counsel neglect. "I shall not neglect my body," he insists, "nor shall I be a [[croesus|Croesus]]" — the body should be cared for as one cares for a tool, not as one cares for what is truly one's own (002-018).

In Discourse 008, he develops a vivid analogy: God has given each person a body, possessions, and companions as gifts, like a master entrusting property to a steward. The steward uses these things but does not mistake them for his own. We should "take care of them as things which are not our own, as travelers take care of an inn" (008-038, 008-039). The body's claims must be met, but they must not govern the ruling faculty's judgments.

When death approaches, the Stoic must be ready to "depart without flinching" (007-025). The body's destruction is not an evil; only the corruption of moral character is. "The body and the possessions of the body — these we have considered as things of no value" (007-025). This radical detachment from the body is not a denial of its practical importance but a refusal to let its [[necessity|fate]] determine the soul's [[tranquillity|serenity]].

## Key Passages

- (001-028) — "My poor body, you mean. I will cut your head off. I am a free man. You cannot touch my moral purpose."
- (002-018) — I shall not neglect my body; nor shall I be a [[croesus|Croesus]]. The body is a tool, not the self.
- (007-005) — "We are fettered by these bonds, the body, I mean, and its possessions."
- (007-018) — [[fortune|Fortune]] has power over us "by means of the body."
- (007-025) — "We have considered as things of no value the body and the possessions of the body."
- (008-038) — God has given each of us a body, parts of the body, possessions, and companions — as things entrusted, not owned.
- (011-038) — Even when the body is hated, it is not the true self that is affected.
