# Anxiety

**Anxiety** (Greek *tarachē*, translated by Long as "solicitude" or perturbation) is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], a symptom of misplaced valuation. A person is anxious precisely to the extent that they invest their well-being in things outside their [[power|control]] — reputation, the body, the will of a superior, the opinion of a crowd. The Stoic therapy of anxiety consists not in managing symptoms but in withdrawing attachment from every external.

Discourse 28, "That We Ought Not to Be Angry with Men, and What Are the Small and the Great Things Among Men," is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most concentrated treatment of the topic, though the theme runs throughout the *Discourses*.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] opens Discourse 28 with a diagnostic question: "When I see a man anxious, I say, What does this man want?" (028-021). The answer is always the same: he wants something not in his power. The anxiety is not the problem — it is a symptom revealing a deeper error in the person's judgment about what is good.

The lute player illustrates the mechanism. When singing [[solitude|alone]], he has no anxiety. "But when he enters the theatre, he is anxious, even if he has a good voice and plays well" (028-024). The change is not in the performance but in the presence of an audience whose approval he desires. Anxiety enters precisely where desire has attached itself to something external and uncertain.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the diagnosis to every domain of life. "Are we anxious about our poor body, about our little property, about the will of Caesar; but not anxious about things internal?" (028-040). The irony is that people invest anxiety in what they cannot [[power|control]] — externals — while neglecting the one thing they can [[power|control]]: their own judgments and character.

The solution is not to learn relaxation techniques but to redirect concern to its proper object. "Where is room left for anxiety?" (028-039) — [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s rhetorical question implies that for the person who has mastered the discipline of desire, who wants only what nature provides and fears only moral failure, anxiety simply has no foothold.

The broader Stoic framework is visible in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s repeated question: "What does it matter to you?" The anxious person has forgotten the distinction between what is his (*prohairesis*) and what is not. Anxiety is the emotional cost of forgetting this boundary. "Why then are you anxious about that which belongs to another?" (028-065).

## Key Passages

- The diagnostic opening: "When I see a man anxious, I say, What does this man want?" (028-021).
- The lute player's anxiety: "when he enters the theatre, he is anxious, even if he has a good voice" (028-024).
- Where anxiety has no room: "where is room left for anxiety? But we are anxious about our poor body" (028-039).
- Misplaced concern: "Are we anxious about not forming a false opinion? No" (028-041).
- The philosopher without anxiety: "he was not anxious" when meeting a king (028-050).
- "Neither does he know what anxiety is" — the true philosopher (031-025).
