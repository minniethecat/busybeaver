[017-001]Appearances are to us in four ways. For either things appear as they are;

[017-002]or they are not, and do not even appear to be; or they are, and do not appear

[017-003]to be; or they are not, and yet appear to be. Further, in all these cases to

[017-004]form a right [[judgment]] (to hit the mark) is the office of an educated man. But

[017-005]whatever it is that annoys (troubles) us, to that we ought to apply a remedy.

[017-006]If the sophisms of Pyrrho and of the Academics are what annoys (troubles), we

[017-007]must apply the remedy to them. If it is the persuasion of appearances, by which

[017-008]some things appear to be [[good]], when they are not good, let us seek a remedy for

[017-009]this. If it is habit which annoys us, we must try to seek aid against habit.

[017-010]What aid, then, can we find against habit? The contrary habit. You hear the

[017-011]ignorant say: “That unfortunate person is dead; his father and mother are

[017-012]overpowered with sorrow; he was cut off by an untimely death and in a foreign

[017-013]land.” Hear the contrary way of speaking. Tear yourself from these expressions;

[017-014]oppose to one habit the contrary habit; to sophistry oppose [[reason]], and the

[017-015]exercise and [[discipline]] of reason; against persuasive (deceitful) appearances

[017-016]we ought to have manifest præcognitions ([Greek: prolaepseis]), cleared of all

[017-017]impurities and ready to hand.

[017-018]When death appears an evil, we ought to have this rule in readiness, that it is

[017-019]fit to avoid evil things, and that death is a necessary thing. For what shall I

[017-020]do, and where shall I escape it? Suppose that I am not Sarpedon, the son of

[017-021][[zeus|Zeus]], nor able to speak in this noble way. I will go and I am resolved either

[017-022]to behave bravely myself or to give to another the opportunity of doing so; if

[017-023]I cannot succeed in doing anything myself, I will not grudge another the doing

[017-024]of something noble. Suppose that it is above our power to act thus; is it not

[017-025]in our power to reason thus? Tell me where I can escape death; discover for me

[017-026]the country, show me the men to whom I must go, whom death does not visit.

[017-027]Discover to me a charm against death. If I have not one, what do you wish me to

[017-028]do? I cannot escape from death. Shall I not escape from the fear of death, but

[017-029]shall I die lamenting and trembling? For the origin of perturbation is this, to

[017-030]wish for something, and that this should not happen. Therefore if I am able to

[017-031]change [[externals]] according to my wish, I change them; but if I cannot, I am

[017-032]ready to tear out the eyes of him who hinders me. For the [[nature]] of man is not

[017-033]to endure to be deprived of the good, and not to endure the falling into the

[017-034]evil. Then at last, when I am neither able to change circumstances nor to tear

[017-035]out the eyes of him who hinders me, I sit down and groan, and abuse whom I can,

[017-036]Zeus and the rest of the gods. For if they do not care for me, what are they to

[017-037]me? Yes, but you will be an impious man. In what respect, then, will it be

[017-038]worse for me than it is now? To sum up, remember that unless piety and your

[017-039]interest be in the same thing, piety cannot be maintained in any man. Do not

[017-040]these things seem necessary (true)?
