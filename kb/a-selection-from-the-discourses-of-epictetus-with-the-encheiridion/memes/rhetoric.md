# Rhetoric

**Rhetoric** (*rhētorikē*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses functions primarily as a negative example — the [[example|paradigm]]atic false education that cultivates external polish while neglecting the inner discipline of the will. The rhetorician is the man who has learned to speak beautifully but not to live well, who dresses his hair and his sentences with equal care while his ruling faculty remains untrained.

"When you were become a youth and attended the rhetoricians, and yourself practised rhetoric, what did you imagine that you were deficient in?" (045-027). [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s question cuts to the heart of his critique: rhetoric promises competence but delivers only ornament; it fills the student with confidence while leaving him entirely ignorant of the one thing needful.

## Definition

In the educational landscape of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s [[rome|Rome]], rhetoric was the standard higher education — the art of persuasive speaking that prepared young men for public life and legal advocacy. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not deny rhetoric's utility for its proper sphere; what he condemns is the confusion of rhetorical [[education|training]] with philosophical education. Rhetoric teaches *how to speak*; philosophy teaches *how to live*. The tragedy is that students mistake the former for the latter.

Rhetoric in the Discourses overlaps significantly with [[sophist|sophistry]] (*sophisteia*), and [[epictetus|Epictetus]] often treats them together. Both represent the corruption of reason into mere verbal display — the use of the rational faculty for impression management rather than self-government.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses rhetoric through several characteristic episodes and [[disputation|argument]]s in the Discourses.

**The young rhetorician in fine dress**: Discourse 39, titled "Of Finery in Dress," presents the most vivid critique. "A certain young man, a rhetorician, came to see [[epictetus|Epictetus]], with his hair dressed more carefully than was usual and his attire in an ornamental style" (039-001). Rather than rebuking the youth directly, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] engages him in a Socratic examination of beauty: a dog is beautiful when it excels according to a dog's nature, a horse according to a horse's nature — what then makes a *man* beautiful? The answer is human excellence — justice, moderation, temperance. The rhetorician's careful coiffure is revealed as a category error: he has adorned his body when he should have cultivated his soul.

**Rhetoric as a station in life**: In Discourse 50, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] lists rhetoric as one station among many through which a person might pass — "first a grammarian, then a philosopher, then a rhetorician" (050-015). The implication is that rhetoric is not inherently worthless, but it is a station that can be occupied with or without philosophical foundation. The danger lies in stopping at rhetoric, mistaking verbal facility for wisdom.

**The retrospective question**: In Discourse 45, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] confronts a man who seeks philosophical advice about public office. He asks him to review his life: "when you were become a youth and attended the rhetoricians, and yourself practised rhetoric, what did you imagine that you were deficient in?" (045-027). The question exposes the false confidence that rhetorical [[education|training]] instills — the student who has learned to argue any side of any question believes he lacks nothing, when in fact he lacks everything that matters.

**Rhetoric vs. logic**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] distinguishes carefully between rhetoric and logic. [[logic|Logic]] (*logikē*) is necessary — it is the tool by which we distinguish truth from falsehood. In Discourse 38 ("That [[logic|Logic]] Is Necessary"), [[epictetus|Epictetus]] demonstrates that even the person who denies logic's necessity must use logical [[disputation|argument]] to do so. Rhetoric, by contrast, is the art of persuasion without regard to truth — a skill that can serve either virtue or vice, and that easily becomes an end in itself.

## Key Passages

> A certain young man, a rhetorician, came to see Epictetus, with his hair dressed more carefully than was usual and his attire in an ornamental style. (039-001)

> And do you then, if you wish to be beautiful, young man, labor at this, the acquisition of human excellence. (039-023)

> When you were become a youth and attended the rhetoricians, and yourself practised rhetoric, what did you imagine that you were deficient in? (045-027)

> First a grammarian, then a philosopher, then a rhetorician, then a procurator — and none of these permanent. (050-015)
