[029-001]When a certain Roman entered with his son and listened to one reading,

[029-002][[epictetus|Epictetus]] said, This is the method of instruction; and he stopped. When the

[029-003]Roman asked him to go on, Epictetus said, Every art when it is taught causes

[029-004]labor to him who is unacquainted with it and is unskilled in it, and indeed the

[029-005]things which proceed from the arts immediately show their use in the purpose

[029-006]for which they were made; and most of them contain something attractive and

[029-007]pleasing. For indeed to be present and to observe how a shoemaker learns is not

[029-008]a pleasant thing; but the shoe is useful and also not disagreeable to look at.

[029-009]And the [[discipline]] of a smith when he is learning is very disagreeable to one

[029-010]who chances to be present and is a stranger to the art: but the work shows the

[029-011]use of the art. But you will see this much more in music; for if you are

[029-012]present while a person is learning, the discipline will appear most

[029-013]disagreeable; and yet the results of music are pleasing and delightful to those

[029-014]who know nothing of music. And here we conceive the work of a philosopher to be

[029-015]something of this kind: he must adapt his wish ([Greek: boulaesin]) to what is

[029-016]going on, so that neither any of the things which are taking place shall take

[029-017]place contrary to our wish, nor any of the things which do not take place shall

[029-018]not take place when we wish that they should. From this the result is to those

[029-019]who have so arranged the work of philosophy, not to fail in the [[desire]], nor to

[029-020]fall in with that which they would avoid; without uneasiness, without fear,

[029-021]without perturbation to pass through life themselves, together with their

[029-022]associates maintaining the relations both natural and acquired, as the relation

[029-023]of son, of father, of brother, of citizen, of man, of wife, of neighbor, of

[029-024]fellow-traveller, of ruler, of ruled. The work of a philosopher we conceive to

[029-025]be something like this. It remains next to inquire how this must be

[029-026]accomplished.

[029-027]We see then that the carpenter ([Greek: techton]) when he has learned certain

[029-028]things becomes a carpenter; the pilot by learning certain things becomes a

[029-029]pilot. May it not then in philosophy also not be sufficient to wish to be wise

[029-030]and [[good]], and that there is also a necessity to learn certain things? We

[029-031]inquire then what these things are. The philosophers say that we ought first to

[029-032]learn that there is a God and that he provides for all things; also that it is

[029-033]not possible to conceal from him our acts, or even our intentions and thoughts.

[029-034]The next thing is to learn what is the [[nature]] of the gods; for such as they are

[029-035]discovered to be, he, who would please and obey them, must try with all his

[029-036]power to be like them. If the divine is faithful, man also must be faithful; if

[029-037]it is free, man also must be free; if beneficent, man also must be beneficent;

[029-038]if magnanimous, man also must be magnanimous; as being then an imitator of God

[029-039]he must do and say everything consistently with this fact.
