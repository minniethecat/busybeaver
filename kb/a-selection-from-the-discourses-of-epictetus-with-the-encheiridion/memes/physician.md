## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, the physician (Greek: *iatros*, ἰατρός) is a central metaphor for the philosopher's role as healer of the soul. Just as the physician diagnoses and treats diseases of the body through art (*technē*) and reason, the philosopher-physician diagnoses and treats diseases of the soul — the passions — through philosophical [[disputation|argument]] and ethical [[education|training]]. This medical analogy runs throughout the Stoic tradition from Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] onward, reflecting the Stoic conviction that the passions are not mere feelings but cognitive errors that can be corrected through rational therapy. The metaphor also reinforces the Stoic distinction between the body (the physician's proper domain, an external) and the soul (the domain that truly belongs to the person).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] deploys the physician metaphor with characteristic directness, often using it to teach the proper attitude toward externals and the limits of what others can do to us. In Discourse 46, his most concentrated treatment of the theme, he instructs his students on how to bear sickness: "when the physician comes in, not to be frightened at what he says; nor if he says you are doing well, to be overjoyed" (046-043–044). The physician can advise about the body, but cannot touch the ruling faculty — "he cannot prevent you from bearing thirst well" (046-030). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the metaphor provocatively: "Do you not value a physician, as you do a shoemaker when he is measuring your foot?" (046-052). The point is not to disparage the physician but to put the body in its proper place — as an external that is "not yours, but by nature dead" (046-054). The true self, the *prohairesis*, remains beyond the physician's reach.

Elsewhere, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the physician com[[alexander-of-troy|paris]]on diagnostically: "When you see a man pale, as the physician says, judging from the complexion" (028-043), one discerns the presence of internal disorder. In Discourse 30, he chides those who complain of spiritual sickness yet refuse to "seek the physician" (030-010), calling their condition not "tone" but "atony" — a deficiency in right tension. The metaphor thus serves both to affirm the philosopher's therapeutic function and to remind students that healing the soul, unlike healing the body, is always within their power, provided they apply the correct judgments.

## Key Passages

- "When the physician comes in, not to be frightened at what he says; nor if he says you are doing well, to be overjoyed" (046-043–044) — The proper Stoic demeanor toward medical judgment.
- "The physician will hinder you from drinking; but he cannot prevent you from bearing thirst well" (046-029–030) — The limit of the physician's power over what is truly yours.
- "Do you not value a physician, as you do a shoemaker when he is measuring your foot?" (046-052) — Placing the physician in proper perspective relative to the soul.
- "Seek the physician; this is not tone, but atony" (030-010) — The failure to seek philosophical therapy as spiritual weakness.
- "When you see a man pale, as the physician says, judging from the complexion" (028-043) — The physician's diagnostic art as a [[example|model]] for discerning spiritual disorder.
