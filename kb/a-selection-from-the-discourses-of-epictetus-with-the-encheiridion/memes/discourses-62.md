[062-001]I am grieved, a man says, at being

[062-002]pitied. Whether then is the fact of your being pitied a thing which concerns

[062-003]you or those who pity you? Well, is it in your power to stop this pity? It is

[062-004]in my power, if I show them that I do not require pity. And whether then are

[062-005]you in the condition of not deserving (requiring) pity, or are you not in that

[062-006]condition? I think that I am not; but these persons do not pity me, for the

[062-007]things for which, if they ought to pity me, it would be proper, I mean, for my

[062-008]faults; but they pity me for my poverty, for not possessing honorable offices,

[062-009]for diseases and deaths and other such things. Whether then are you prepared to

[062-010]convince the many, that not one of these things is an evil, but that it is

[062-011]possible for a man who is poor and has no office ([Greek: anarchonti)] and

[062-012]enjoys no honor to be happy; or to show yourself to them as rich and in power?

[062-013]For the second of these things belong to a man who is boastful, silly, and [[good]]

[062-014]for nothing. And consider by what means the pretence must be supported. It will

[062-015]be necessary for you to hire slaves and to possess a few silver vessels, and to

[062-016]exhibit them in public, if it is possible, though they are often the same, and

[062-017]to attempt to conceal the fact that they are the same, and to have splendid

[062-018]garments, and all other things for display, and to show that you are a man

[062-019]honored by the great, and to try to sup at their houses, or to be supposed to

[062-020]sup there, and as to your person to employ some mean arts, that you may appear

[062-021]to be more handsome and nobler than you are. These things you must contrive, if

[062-022]you choose to go by the second path in order not to be pitied. But the first

[062-023]way is both impracticable and long, to attempt the very thing which [[zeus|Zeus]] has

[062-024]not been able to do, to convince all men what things are good and bad. Is this

[062-025]power given to you? This only is given to you, to convince yourself; and you

[062-026]have not convinced yourself. Then I ask you, do you attempt to persuade other

[062-027]men? and who has lived so long with you as you with yourself? and who has so

[062-028]much power of convincing you as you have of convincing yourself; and who is

[062-029]better disposed and nearer to you than you are to yourself? How then have you

[062-030]not yet convinced yourself in order to learn? At present are not things upside

[062-031]down? Is this what you have been earnest about doing, to learn to be free from

[062-032]grief and free from disturbance, and not to be humbled (abject), and to be

[062-033]free? Have you not heard then that there is only one way which leads to this

[062-034]end, to give up (dismiss) the things which do not depend on the will, to

[062-035]withdraw from them, and to admit that they belong to others? For another man

[062-036]then to have an opinion about you, of what kind is it? It is a thing

[062-037]independent of the will—Then is it nothing to you? It is nothing. When then you

[062-038]are still vexed at this and disturbed, do you think that you are convinced

[062-039]about good and evil?
