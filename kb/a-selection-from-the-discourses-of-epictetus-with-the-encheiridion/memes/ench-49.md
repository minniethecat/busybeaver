[117-001]When a man is proud because he can understand and explain the writings of

[117-002][[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], say to yourself, If Chrysippus had not written obscurely, this man

[117-003]would have had nothing to be proud of. But what is it that I wish? To

[117-004]understand [[nature]] and to follow it. I inquire therefore who is the interpreter?

[117-005]and when I have heard that it is Chrysippus, I come to him (the interpreter).

[117-006]But I do not understand what is written, and therefore I seek the interpreter.

[117-007]And so far there is yet nothing to be proud of. But when I shall have found the

[117-008]interpreter, the thing that remains is to use the precepts (the lessons). This

[117-009]itself is the only thing to be proud of. But if I shall admire the exposition,

[117-010]what else have I been made unless a grammarian instead of a philosopher? except

[117-011]in one thing, that I am explaining Chrysippus instead of Homer. When, then, any

[117-012]man says to me, Read Chrysippus to me, I rather blush, when I cannot show my

[117-013]acts like to and consistent with his words.
