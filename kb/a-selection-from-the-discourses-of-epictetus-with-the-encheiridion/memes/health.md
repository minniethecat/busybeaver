## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, health (*hugieia*) is one of the classic "preferred indifferents" (*proēgmena adiaphora*). It is naturally worth pursuing, contributes to a flourishing physical life, and is generally in accordance with nature. Yet health is not a moral good — it does not make its possessor virtuous, nor does its absence make one vicious. Like wealth and reputation, health belongs to the domain of things outside the ruling faculty's [[power|control]]. The Stoic values health and takes reasonable steps to maintain it, but does not stake happiness on its presence.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses health as a recurring example of an indifferent in his teachings. He asks his students pointedly: "Is not health then a good thing?" (014-031). The correct answer, for a Stoic, is no — health is a preferred thing, but not a good. Only virtue is good. The distinction is crucial because it determines where one places one's desire. If health is believed to be a good, then its loss will cause [[grief|distress]]; if it is recognized as an indifferent, its loss is bearable.

The threefold classification appears in Discourse 025: "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation" (025-039). This formula recurs throughout the Discourses as a shorthand for everything that is not virtue or vice. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] pairs health with wealth and reputation so consistently that the triad becomes a teaching device — a way of reminding the student that even the most universally desired things are still externals.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is not dismissive of health. He acknowledges that a healthy body, like an athletic body, possesses a kind of fitness that is admirable (030-008). But he insists on the proper hierarchy: health is to the body what virtue is to the soul. The body's health is worth maintaining, but the soul's health — moral character — is the only thing that can truly be called good.

His sharpest teaching on health comes when he confronts the student with death: "I will die, if you compel me. — Of health. — I will die, if you compel me" (030-045). The willingness to lose even health, if virtue requires it, is the test of whether one has truly internalized the doctrine of indifferents. A Stoic maintains health when possible, surrenders it when necessary, and in neither case confuses it with the good.

## Key Passages

- (010-008) — Health listed alongside land and reputation as things people mistakenly cling to.
- (014-031) — "Is not health then a good thing, and sickness an evil?" The Stoic's answer tests their understanding.
- (025-039) — "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation." The classic triad.
- (025-047) — The body's healthy appearance — sinews, flesh, bones, blood, healthy breath — is distinguished from the moral self.
- (030-008) — Health in the body compared to the proper condition of the athletic body; the analogy extends to the soul.
- (030-045) — "Of health. — I will die, if you compel me." The Stoic's readiness to surrender health.
- (032-023) — Even those who use the words "healthy" and "unhealthy" do not consistently apply the preconception.
