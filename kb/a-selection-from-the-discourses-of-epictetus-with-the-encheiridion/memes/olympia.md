# Olympia

**Olympia** was the most important religious sanctuary in ancient Greece, dedicated to [[zeus|Zeus]] and site of the Olympic Games. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Olympia serves as his most vivid metaphor for the philosophical life. Just as an athlete must submit to rigorous [[education|training]] to compete at Olympia, so the philosopher must undergo the discipline of examining [[appearances|impressions]] and res[[education|training]] [[desire]].

## Location

Olympia is located in the region of Elis, in the western Peloponnese of Greece. The sanctuary complex included the Temple of [[zeus|Zeus]] — which housed Phidias's colossal gold-and-ivory statue of the [[divinity|god]], one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world — as well as the Temple of Hera, the stadium, and numerous treasuries and altars.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] mentions the journey to Olympia as an example of a difficult undertaking that people willingly accept for the sake of its reward. "You take a journey to Olympia to see the work of Phidias, and all of you think it a [[fortune|misfortune]] to die without having seen such things" (006-038). If people will travel such distances for art, why not for virtue?

## Historical Context

The Olympic Games were held every four years from 776 BCE to 393 CE, drawing competitors and spectators from across the Greek world. The Games were not merely athletic but deeply religious — a festival in honor of [[zeus|Zeus]], accompanied by sacrifices, processions, and artistic competitions.

By [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s time, the Games had been celebrated for over eight centuries. They were one of the few institutions that connected the Roman imperial period to the archaic Greek past. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws on this cultural prestige to elevate the philosophical life: if Olympic athletes endure so much for a crown of wild olive, how much more should the philosopher endure for the crown of [[virtue]]?

The athletic metaphor had deep roots in Greek philosophy. The Cynics, whom [[epictetus|Epictetus]] admired, had long compared philosophical [[education|training]] to athletic [[education|training]]. But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops the Olympia analogy with unprecedented vividness and frequency, making it a signature feature of his pedagogical style.

## Significance in the Discourses

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses Olympia as his master metaphor for the demanding nature of philosophical [[education|training]]. The Olympic athlete's regimen — strict diet, punishing exercise, [[obedience|submission]] to the trainer's commands — parallels the philosopher's discipline of desire and assent.

"Do you wish to conquer at Olympia?" [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks. "Then you must observe the [[precept|rules]], exercise regularly, accustom yourself to heat and cold, abstain from wine and pleasant foods." The candidate who balks at these requirements is told: "Then do not run" (006-038). The same logic applies to philosophy: if you want the crown, you must accept the [[education|training]].

The prize at Olympia is temporary — a wreath of wild olive. The prize of philosophy is permanent: a will aligned with [[nature]], a mind free from passion (*apatheia*). "At Olympia you wish for nothing more, but are content to have witnessed the spectacle" (054-083). How much more should the philosopher be content with the spectacle of a well-ordered cosmos?

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also uses the statue of [[zeus|Zeus]] at Olympia as a teaching point about human dignity. "Does the [[zeus|Zeus]] at Olympia lift up his brow? No, his look is fixed as becomes one who is ready to say: It cannot be otherwise" (024-077). The statue [[example|model]]s the philosophical attitude: serene acceptance of the cosmic order.
