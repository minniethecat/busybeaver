# Ignorance

**Ignorance** (*agnoia*) in Stoic ethics is not a mere lack of information but the fundamental cause of all moral error. Following [[socrates|Socrates]], the Stoics held that no one does wrong willingly — all wrongdoing arises from ignorance of what is truly good and bad. If a person genuinely knew that virtue is the sole good, they would act virtuously without fail.

This is the doctrine of Stoic intellectualism: vice is ignorance, virtue is knowledge. The stakes could not be higher. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] traces the great historical catastrophes — the wars of [[athens|Athens]] and Sparta — to this single root cause. "It was through this ignorance that the Athenians and the [[lacedaemon|Lacedaemon]]ians" fought and suffered (035-032).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherits the Socratic intellectualist position and sharpens it through the lens of the discipline of assent. Ignorance, in his framework, is not merely not knowing a fact — it is systematically assenting to false appearances while failing to recognize their falsity.

The core mechanism is this: every person pursues what appears good and avoids what appears bad. When someone does wrong, they have mistaken a merely apparent good for a real one. "On account of his ignorance, on account of his being mistaken in things" (035-081). The error is cognitive, not motivational — the will is not evil but misinformed.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] traces this to a universal human condition. "Ever since the human race existed, all errors and [[fortune|misfortune]]s have arisen through this ignorance" (037-036). This is both a diagnosis and a prescription: if ignorance is the cause, then philosophy — the correct understanding of what is truly good — is the cure.

The practical consequence is that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats moral errors not as occasions for blame but as opportunities for [[education|instruction]]. The person who errs is like someone who misses the target because they cannot see it clearly. The philosopher's task is to correct the vision, not to punish the blind.

This intellectualism also explains [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s hostility to the Academic Skeptics, who denied that certain knowledge is possible. If knowledge is the cure for moral error, then skepticism — the claim that nothing can be known — is not merely an intellectual error but a moral catastrophe. It blocks the only path to virtue.

## Key Passages

- Ignorance as the cause of historical catastrophe: "It was through this ignorance that the Athenians and the [[lacedaemon|Lacedaemon]]ians..." (035-032).
- The ignorant man's error: "on account of his ignorance, on account of his being mistaken in things" (035-081).
- All error traced to ignorance: "ever since the human race existed, all errors and [[fortune|misfortune]]s have arisen through this ignorance" (037-035, 037-036).
