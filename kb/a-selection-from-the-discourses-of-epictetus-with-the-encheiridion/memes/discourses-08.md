[008-001]With respect to gods, there are some who say that a divine

[008-002]being does not exist; others say that it exists, but is inactive and careless,

[008-003]and takes no forethought about anything; a third class say that such a being

[008-004]exists and exercises forethought, but only about great things and heavenly

[008-005]things, and about nothing on the earth; a fourth class say that a divine being

[008-006]exercises forethought both about things on the earth and heavenly things, but

[008-007]in a general way only, and not about things severally. There is a fifth class

[008-008]to whom Ulysses and [[socrates|Socrates]] belong, who say:

[008-009]I move not without thy knowledge.—Iliad, x., 278.

[008-010]Before all other things then it is necessary to inquire about each of these

[008-011]opinions, whether it is affirmed truly or not truly. For if there are no gods,

[008-012]how is it our proper end to follow them? And if they exist, but take no care of

[008-013]anything, in this case also how will it be right to follow them? But if indeed

[008-014]they do exist and look after things, still if there is nothing communicated

[008-015]from them to men, nor in fact to myself, how even so is it right (to follow

[008-016]them)? The wise and [[good]] man then, after considering all these things, submits

[008-017]his own mind to him who administers the whole, as good citizens do to the law

[008-018]of the state. He who is receiving instruction ought to come to be instructed

[008-019]with this intention, How shall I follow the gods in all things, how shall I be

[008-020]contented with the divine administration, and how can I become free? For he is

[008-021]free to whom everything happens according to his will, and whom no man can

[008-022]hinder. What then, is freedom madness? Certainly not; for madness and freedom

[008-023]do not consist. But, you say, I would have everything result just as I like,

[008-024]and in whatever way I like. You are mad, you are beside yourself. Do you not

[008-025]know that freedom is a noble and valuable thing? But for me inconsiderately to

[008-026]wish for things to happen as I inconsiderately like, this appears to be not

[008-027]only not noble, but even most base. For how do we proceed in the matter of

[008-028]writing? Do I wish to write the name of Dion as I choose? No, but I am taught

[008-029]to choose to write it as it ought to be written. And how with respect to music?

[008-030]In the same manner. And what universally in every art or science? Just the

[008-031]same. If it were not so, it would be of no value to know anything, if knowledge

[008-032]were adapted to every man’s whim. Is it then in this alone, in this which is

[008-033]the greatest and the chief thing, I mean freedom, that I am permitted to will

[008-034]inconsiderately? By no means; but to be instructed is this, to learn to wish

[008-035]that everything may happen as it does. And how do things happen? As the

[008-036]disposer has disposed them? And he has appointed summer and winter, and

[008-037]abundance and scarcity, and [[virtue]] and vice, and all such opposites for the

[008-038]harmony of the whole; and to each of us he has given a body, and parts of the

[008-039]body, and possessions, and companions.

[008-040]What then remains, or what method is discovered of holding commerce with them?

[008-041]Is there such a method by which they shall do what seems fit to them, and we

[008-042]not the less shall be in a mood which is conformable to [[nature]]? But you are

[008-043]unwilling to endure, and are discontented; and if you are alone, you call it

[008-044]solitude; and if you are with men, you call them knaves and robbers; and you

[008-045]find fault with your own parents and children, and brothers and neighbors. But

[008-046]you ought when you are alone to call this condition by the name of tranquillity

[008-047]and freedom, and to think yourself like to the gods; and when you are with

[008-048]many, you ought not to call it crowd, nor trouble, nor uneasiness, but festival

[008-049]and assembly, and so accept all contentedly.

[008-050]What then is the punishment of those who do not accept? It is to be what they

[008-051]are. Is any person dissatisfied with being alone? Let him be alone. Is a man

[008-052]dissatisfied with his parents? Let him be a bad son, and lament. Is he

[008-053]dissatisfied with his children? Let him be a bad father. Cast him into prison.

[008-054]What prison? Where he is already, for he is there against his will; and where a

[008-055]man is against his will, there he is in prison. So Socrates was not in prison,

[008-056]for he was there willingly. Must my leg then be lamed? Wretch, do you then on

[008-057]account of one poor leg find fault with the world? Will you not willingly

[008-058]surrender it for the whole? Will you not withdraw from it? Will you not gladly

[008-059]part with it to him who gave it? And will you be vexed and discontented with

[008-060]the things established by [[zeus|Zeus]], which he, with the Moirae (fates) who were

[008-061]present and spinning the thread of your generation, defined and put in order?

[008-062]Know you not how small a part you are compared with the whole. I mean with

[008-063]respect to the body, for as to intelligence you are not inferior to the gods

[008-064]nor less; for the magnitude of intelligence is not measured by length nor yet

[008-065]by height, but by thoughts.
