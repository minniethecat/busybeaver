## Definition

Envy (*[[jealousy|phthonos]]*) is classified in Stoic ethics as a passion (*pathos*) — a disordered and excessive impulse contrary to reason. Specifically, [[jealousy|envy]] is a form of [[grief|distress]] felt at another person's possession of goods that one believes one lacks. Like all passions in the Stoic framework, [[jealousy|envy]] results from an erroneous judgment: the belief that external goods (wealth, status, reputation) are genuinely valuable and that their possession by another constitutes a harm to oneself. The Stoic remedy is to correct the underlying judgment by recognizing that only virtue is good and only vice is evil.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses [[jealousy|envy]] as a symptom of a deeper failure to understand what is truly in our power. He observes that [[jealousy|envy]] arises when we focus on things outside our sphere of choice — wealth, honors, the favor of powerful men — and neglect the one thing that is genuinely ours: the right use of [[appearances|impressions]]. The person who arrives "full of [[jealousy|envy]], and humbled" because he failed to secure some external advantage has fundamentally misunderstood where value lies (034-038).

In the Discourse on the three topics of philosophy, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies [[jealousy|envy]] as one of the passions produced by erroneous judgment about what is good and evil (040-014). The envious person is enslaved — hindered, constrained, unable to act freely — because his happiness depends on factors beyond his [[power|control]]. By contrast, the person who understands that good is solely in the ruling faculty is free from [[jealousy|envy]]: "I am not hindered, I am not constrained" (040-065).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also treats [[jealousy|envy]] alongside pity as passions that the Stoic-in-[[education|training]] must eliminate. Both arise from the same root error — mistaking externals for goods — and both must be overcome through the discipline of desire (053-011, 057-008). The cure for [[jealousy|envy]] is the recognition that another's external prosperity cannot diminish one's own capacity for virtue, which is the only genuine good (087-005).

## Key Passages

- (034-038) — "You come full of [[jealousy|envy]], and humbled, because you bring nothing from home."
- (040-014) — Envy listed among the [[grief|sorrow]]s and [[grief|lamentation]]s produced by false judgments about good and evil.
- (040-065) — The person free from [[jealousy|envy]] says: "I am not hindered, I am not constrained."
- (053-011) — Neither resentment, [[jealousy|envy]], nor pity should find a place in the Stoic student.
- (057-008) — "Who chooses to live in [[grief|sorrow]], fear, [[jealousy|envy]], pity, desiring and failing in his desires?"
- (087-005) — When we recognize that good is in our power, "neither [[jealousy|envy]] nor jealousy will have a place."
