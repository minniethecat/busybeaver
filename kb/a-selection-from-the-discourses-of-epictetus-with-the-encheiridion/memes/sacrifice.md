## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, sacrifice (Greek: *thysia*, θυσία) is radically reinterpreted from its conventional religious sense. While traditional Greco-Roman religion understood sacrifice as the offering of animals, libations, or first-fruits at altars, the Stoics internalized the concept: true sacrifice is not the burning of incense or the slaughter of victims but the offering of a rightly disposed mind to God. The rational soul's alignment with the [[divinity|divine]] order — expressed through correct judgments, virtuous actions, and grateful acceptance of [[necessity|fate]] — constitutes the only sacrifice worthy of the deity. This redefinition reflects the Stoic conviction that God is rational (*logos*) and therefore values what is rational in humans: not material gifts but the excellence of the *hēgemonikon*.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] transforms the notion of sacrifice into a tool for teaching the proper relationship between humans and the [[divinity|divine]]. In Discourse 4, he observes that "because the [[divinity|gods]] have given the vine, or wheat, we sacrifice to them" (004-084), acknowledging the conventional [[exercise|practice]] of gratitude-offerings. But his deeper teaching consistently redirects the student from external ritual to internal disposition. In Discourse 33, he urges his students to "sacrifice to God" (033-030) not through elaborate ceremonies but through the right use of [[appearances|impressions]] and the cultivation of virtue. The habit of correct judgment, he suggests, is itself the truest form of worship.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also uses sacrifice to expose the hypocrisy of conventional piety. In Discourse 12, he notes sardonically that "he offers a sacrifice on the occasion" (012-043) — the occasion being the acquisition of a desired external. This is not true piety but a transaction with the [[divinity|gods]], treating them as dispensers of indifferents. Similarly, in Discourse 24, he frames the decision-making process itself as a kind of sacrificial act: "Let us sacrifice on the occasion" (024-020) of considering an inheritance, meaning: let us submit the question to [[divinity|divine]] reason rather than acting from greed. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the culmination of this redefinition is found in the ideal of making "libations and to sacrifice and to offer first-fruits according to the custom of our fathers" (099-027) — not superstitiously, but "purely" — that is, with the understanding that the true offering is the soul's conformity to nature.

## Key Passages

- "Because the [[divinity|gods]] have given the vine, or wheat, we sacrifice to them" (004-084) — Gratitude as the root of sacrificial [[exercise|practice]], acknowledged but transcended.
- "He offers a sacrifice on the occasion. Now who ever sacrificed for having had good desires?" (012-043) — The hypocrisy of sacrificing for externals while neglecting the inner offering.
- "Let us sacrifice on the occasion" (024-020) — Decision-making itself as a sacrificial act of submitting to [[divinity|divine]] reason.
- "Sacrifice to God. For the habit at first is a great thing" (033-030) — Cultivating the inner disposition of piety through [[exercise|practice]].
- "To make libations and to sacrifice and to offer first-fruits according to the custom of our fathers, purely" (099-027) — The culmination: external ritual performed with pure inner understanding.
