# Nero

## Biography

Nero (37–68 CE) was Roman emperor from 54 to 68 CE, the last of the Julio-Claudian line and, in the moral memory of antiquity, a byword for tyranny and vice. He stands at the very edge of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own story.

The biographical note records that [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s master, Epaphroditus, was "a profligate freedman of the Emperor Nero" (P02-007 – P02-008), a man close enough to the throne that he "accompanied Nero when he fled from [[rome|Rome]] before his enemies, and... aided the miserable tyrant in killing himself" (P02-032 – P02-033).

The biographer even remarks, with heavy irony, that only the "debaucheries of Nero would have desired to possess such a slave" as the crippled [[epictetus|Epictetus]] (P02-030).

Beyond this biographical proximity, Nero functions in the Discourses less as a historical individual than as a moral symbol. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reaches for his name whenever he needs an image of the powerful man who is nonetheless wretched, or of the corrupt stamp that a citizen of the world must refuse.

Where an older tradition pointed to Sardanapalus as the emblem of luxurious ruin, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] pairs Nero with him as a Roman equivalent close to his own memory (053-062). The name thus does double duty in the text: it marks the real world into which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] was born a slave, and it serves as shorthand for everything a Stoic education is meant to render harmless.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Nero is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s standing example that political power does not confer happiness. Arguing that the good does not lie in royal power, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] writes that if it did, "Nero would have been happy, and Sardanapalus. But neither was [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] happy, though he was a better man than Sardanapalus and Nero" (053-061 – 053-063). The throne, on this view, buys nothing that matters.

Nero also supplies [[epictetus|Epictetus]] with one of his sharpest images for judging character. Just as a coin is tested by its stamp, a man is known by the impress of his ruling principle:

> What is the stamp on this sestertius? The stamp of Trajan. Present it. It is the stamp of Nero. Throw it [away]. (061-057 – 061-058)

To bear "Nero's stamp" (061-063) is to be passionate, faithless, and unfit for the fellowship of good men; such a coin is to be rejected whatever its face value. Even the "hot baths of Nero" (031-075) serve elsewhere as a passing example of the external goods that are not in our power.

## References in the Discourses

- **[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s biographical setting**: his master was a freedman of Nero (P02-007 – P02-008); Nero's court is the backdrop of his enslavement (P02-030 – P02-035).

- **[[power|Power]] without happiness**: were the good in royal power, "Nero would have been happy" — but he was not (053-061 – 053-063).

- **The corrupt stamp**: a man bearing "Nero's stamp" is to be thrown back like false coin (061-057 – 061-063).

- **[[externals|Externals]]**: "the hot baths of Nero" as an example of things outside our power (031-075).
