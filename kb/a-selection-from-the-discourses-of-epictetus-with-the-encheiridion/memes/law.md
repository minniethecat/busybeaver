## Definition

Law (*nomos*) in Stoic thought operates on two distinct levels. There are the "wretched laws of dead men" (009-015) — the conventional statutes of particular states, which are contingent and often unjust. And there is the [[divinity|divine]] law — the rational order of the cosmos itself, which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls "the law of nature and of God" (019-048). For the Stoic, ethical [[duties|obligation]] derives not from human legislation but from this universal law, which is identical with right reason and with the will of God. To live according to nature is to obey this law.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws a stark contrast between human law and [[divinity|divine]] law, and he uses this contrast to reorient the student's entire ethical framework. The ordinary person obeys human laws out of fear of punishment; the philosopher obeys the [[divinity|divine]] law out of understanding and [[piety|reverence]].

This [[divinity|divine]] law is not an arbitrary command but the very structure of reality. "God has fixed this law, and says, 'If you would have anything good, take it from yourself'" (019-007). The law is that genuine goods are internal — within the province of the ruling faculty — and that externals are indifferents. To grasp this law is to understand why virtue is sufficient for happiness and why no external event can harm a good person.

The law has a prescriptive force that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents as absolute. "Law is everything to me" (057-152) — this is the voice of the philosopher who has internalized the cosmic order so completely that he needs nothing else. The laws "which have been sent from there" (059-035) — from the [[divinity|divine]] realm — are the subject matter that the philosopher must be "an expositor" of (059-036), interpreting and applying them to life.

The metaphor of citizenship is central: "as good citizens do to the law" (008-017), so the philosopher obeys the [[divinity|divine]] administration. The law is not a constraint on freedom but its condition — just as a citizen of a well-governed state is free precisely by obeying just laws, so the Stoic is free by aligning his will with the [[divinity|divine]] law. To remember "the law and to have it before his eyes" (031-062) is a core spiritual [[exercise|practice]].

The practical application is uncompromising. When someone sets before you "the law of the state" (054-138), give it what it demands — but recognize that this is a lower law. The higher law is what you must "not transgress" (118-016), and you must treat it as "a law which must not be transgressed" — the inviolable standard of right action.

## Key Passages

- (019-007) — "God has fixed this law, and says, 'If you would have anything good, take it from yourself.'"
- (019-048) — "This is the law of nature and of God" — the [[divinity|divine]] law is the law of nature.
- (057-152) — "Law is everything to me, and nothing else" — the philosopher's total commitment.
- (059-035–036) — "Laws which have been sent from there" — the philosopher as expositor of [[divinity|divine]] law.
- (009-015–016) — The "wretched laws of dead men" contrasted with "the laws of the [[divinity|gods]]."
- (031-062) — "To remember the law and to have it before his eyes" — the [[exercise|practice]] of keeping the law present.
- (008-017) — "As good citizens do to the law" — obedience to law as the [[example|model]] of philosophical conduct.
- (118-016) — That which you hold to be best should be "a law which must not be transgressed."
