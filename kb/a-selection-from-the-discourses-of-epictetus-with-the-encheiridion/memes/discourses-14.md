[014-001]Præcognitions are common to all men, and præcognition is not

[014-002]contradictory to præcognition. For who of us does not assume that Good is

[014-003]useful and eligible, and in all circumstances that we ought to follow and

[014-004]pursue it? And who of us does not assume that Justice is beautiful and

[014-005]becoming? When then does the contradiction arise? It arises in the adaptation

[014-006]of the præcognitions to the particular cases. When one man says, “He has done

[014-007]well; he is a brave man,” and another says, “Not so; but he has acted

[014-008]foolishly,” then the disputes arise among men. This is the dispute among the

[014-009]Jews and the Syrians and the Egyptians and the Romans; not whether holiness

[014-010]should be preferred to all things and in all cases should be pursued, but

[014-011]whether it is holy to eat pig’s flesh or not holy. You will find this dispute

[014-012]also between Agamemnon and Achilles; for call them forth. What do you say,

[014-013]Agamemnon? ought not that to be done which is proper and right? “Certainly.”

[014-014]Well, what do you say, Achilles? do you not admit that what is [[good]] ought to be

[014-015]done? “I do most certainly.” Adapt your præcognitions then to the present

[014-016]matter. Here the dispute begins. Agamemnon says, “I ought not to give up

[014-017]Chryseis to her father.” Achilles says, “You ought.” It is certain that one of

[014-018]the two makes a wrong adaptation of the præcognition of “ought” or “duty.”

[014-019]Further, Agamemnon says, “Then if I ought to restore Chryseis, it is fit that I

[014-020]take his prize from some of you.” Achilles replies, “Would you then take her

[014-021]whom I love?” “Yes, her whom you love.” “Must I then be the only man who goes

[014-022]without a prize? and must I be the only man who has no prize?” Thus the dispute

[014-023]begins.

[014-024]What then is education? Education is the learning how to adapt the natural

[014-025]præcognitions to the particular things conformably to [[nature]]; and then to

[014-026]distinguish that of things some are in our power, but others are not. In our

[014-027]power are will and all acts which depend on the will; things not in our power

[014-028]are the body, the parts of the body, possessions, parents, brothers, children,

[014-029]country, and, generally, all with whom we live in society. In what then should

[014-030]we place the good? To what kind of things ([Greek: ousia]) shall we adapt it?

[014-031]To the things which are in our power? Is not health then a good thing, and

[014-032]soundness of limb, and life, and are not children and parents and country? Who

[014-033]will tolerate you if you deny this?

[014-034]Let us then transfer the notion of good to these things. Is it possible, then,

[014-035]when a man sustains damage and does not obtain good things, that he can be

[014-036]happy? It is not possible. And can he maintain towards society a proper

[014-037]behavior? He can not. For I am naturally formed to look after my own interest.

[014-038]If it is my interest to have an estate in land, it is my interest also to take

[014-039]it from my neighbor. If it is my interest to have a garment, it is my interest

[014-040]also to steal it from the bath. This is the origin of wars, civil commotions,

[014-041]tyrannies, conspiracies. And how shall I be still able to maintain my duty

[014-042]towards [[zeus|Zeus]]? For if I sustain damage and am unlucky, he takes no care of me.

[014-043]And what is he to me if he cannot help me? And further, what is he to me if he

[014-044]allows me to be in the condition in which I am? I now begin to hate him. Why

[014-045]then do we build temples, why setup statues to Zeus, as well as to evil demons,

[014-046]such as to Fever; and how is Zeus the Saviour, and how the giver of rain, and

[014-047]the giver of fruits? And in truth if we place the nature of Good in any such

[014-048]things, all this follows.

[014-049]What should we do then? This is the inquiry of the true philosopher who is in

[014-050]labor. Now I do not see what the good is nor the bad. Am I not mad? Yes. But

[014-051]suppose that I place the good somewhere among the things which depend on the

[014-052]will; all will laugh at me. There will come some greyhead wearing many gold

[014-053]rings on his fingers, and he will shake his head and say: “Hear, my child. It

[014-054]is right that you should philosophize; but you ought to have some brains also;

[014-055]all this that you are doing is silly. You learn the syllogism from

[014-056]philosophers; but you know how to act better than philosophers do.” Man why

[014-057]then do you blame me, if I know? What shall I say to this slave? If I am

[014-058]silent, he will burst. I must speak in this way: “Excuse me, as you would

[014-059]excuse lovers; I am not my own master; I am mad.”
