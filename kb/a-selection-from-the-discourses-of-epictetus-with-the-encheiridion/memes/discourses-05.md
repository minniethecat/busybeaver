[005-001]If a man, said [[epictetus|Epictetus]], opposes evident truths, it is

[005-002]not easy to find arguments by which we shall make him change his opinion. But

[005-003]this does not arise either from the man’s strength or the teacher’s weakness;

[005-004]for when the man, though he has been confuted, is hardened like a stone, how

[005-005]shall we then be able to deal with him by argument?

[005-006]Now there are two kinds of hardening, one of the understanding, the other of

[005-007]the sense of shame, when a man is resolved not to [[assent]] to what is manifest

[005-008]nor to desist from contradictions. Most of us are afraid of mortification of

[005-009]the body, and would contrive all means to avoid such a thing, but we care not

[005-010]about the soul’s mortification. And indeed with regard to the soul, if a man be

[005-011]in such a state as not to apprehend anything, or understand at all, we think

[005-012]that he is in a bad condition; but if the sense of shame and modesty are

[005-013]deadened, this we call even power (or strength).
