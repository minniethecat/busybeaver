# Measure

**Measure** (*metron*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s ethics is the rational standard against which all acts, duties, and judgments must be calibrated. To live philosophically is to abandon "mere seeming" as one's measure and to adopt instead the objective standard of nature and reason.

The concept is pervasive in the *Discourses* and the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]*, appearing in contexts ranging from moral judgment to social duty to the proper assessment of possessions. In every case, the core lesson is the same: without a correct measure, one cannot act rightly.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents measure as the antidote to the common human error of judging by appearances. "Whoever then clearly remembers this, that to man the measure of every act is the appearance" (018-027) — the key insight is that while the appearance (*phantasia*) presents itself, the wise person does not mistake the appearance for the reality. Those who lack the correct measure "are satisfied with the bare appearance" (027-049) and consequently misuse it, leading to error.

The metaphor of scales and measurement instruments appears vividly in Discourse 56. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] demands: "show me the scales or the medimnus" — the measuring vessel by which grain was weighed — and asks "how long will you go on measuring the dust?" (056-048, 056-049). The point is that without a calibrated instrument, measurement is pointless; in ethics, the calibrated instrument is the rational faculty trained in Stoic principles.

In the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]*, the principle of measure is applied to social relations. "[[duties|Duties]] are universally measured by relations" (098-001). One's [[duties|obligation]]s to a father differ from those to a brother, a neighbor, or a fellow citizen. The correct measure of duty is determined by the specific relation (*schēsis*) in which one stands to another. To "discover your duty from the relation of a neighbor, from that of a citizen, of a general" (098-009) is to apply the Stoic measure to practical ethics.

The concept also governs [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on property and desire. "The measure of possession is to each man the body, as the foot is of the shoe" (107-001). If one maintains this measure — desiring only what is proper to one's nature — one preserves tranquility. But "if you pass beyond it, you must thenceforth be borne as down a cataract" (107-003). Measure here becomes the boundary between freedom and enslavement to externals.

## Key Passages

- The measure of every act: "to man the measure of every act is the appearance" — but the wise person judges appearances by the correct standard (018-027).
- Misusing the measure: "those who use mere 'seeming' as a measure, and misuse it; so that for the future they employ it no longer" (027-056).
- The scales demand: "show me the scales or the medimnus; or how long will you go on measuring the dust?" (056-048, 056-049).
- [[duties|Duties]] measured by relations: "[[duties|Duties]] are universally measured by relations. Is a man a father? The precept is to take care of him" (098-001).
- The measure of possession: "The measure of possession is to each man the body, as the foot is of the shoe" (107-001).
