# Truth

**Truth** (*alētheia*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses functions on two interconnected levels. Epistemologically, truth is correct judgment about what is good, evil, and indifferent — the recognition that externals are not in our power and that virtue [[solitude|alone]] is the true good. Morally, truth is the virtue of veracity — the refusal to deceive oneself or others about the nature of things, and the courage to speak plainly about what one knows and does not know.

"Where truth is and where nature is, there is caution: where truth is, there is courage where nature is" (021-034). This dense formulation reveals that for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], truth is not merely a property of propositions but the ground of the ethical life itself.

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, truth belongs primarily to the discipline of assent — the third of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s three topics. It is the condition of the ruling faculty when it assents only to adequate [[appearances|impressions]] (*[[appearances|phantasiai]] katalēptikai*) and withholds assent from the inadequate. The Stoic sage is defined in part by the inability to be deceived — not because he knows everything, but because he never assents prematurely.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends this epistemic standard into the moral domain. The person who speaks truth is one whose words correspond both to reality and to his own best judgment. The liar, by contrast, is not merely factually incorrect but in a state of moral corruption — he has subordinated his rational faculty to fear, desire, or the opinion of others.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats truth across several distinct contexts in the Discourses.

**Truth as the gift of philosophy**: In Discourse 4, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] contrasts the gratitude shown to those who discovered agriculture with the neglect of those who discovered philosophical truth. "To him who discovered truth and brought it to light and communicated it to all, not the truth which shows us how to live, but how to live well, who of you for this reason has built an altar, or a temple?" (004-080). The truth that philosophy reveals is not abstract or theoretical — it is the truth about happiness, about what is genuinely good and evil. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]'s books, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] says, reveal "how true and conformable to nature are the things which make me free from perturbations" (004-081).

**Truth as the ground of ethical action**: Discourse 21 contains one of the most compressed statements in the Discourses: "Where is the nature of evil and good? It is where truth is: where truth is and where nature is, there is caution: where truth is, there is courage where nature is" (021-033, 021-034). The point is that once one has correctly identified what is good (virtue) and what is evil (vice), both caution and courage follow naturally — caution toward moral evil, courage toward the externals that are neither good nor evil.

**Truth-telling as moral [[exercise|practice]]**: In Discourse 28, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges the frank acknowledgment of one's actual power. "Speak the truth, then, unhappy man, and do not boast, nor claim to be any other than an athlete" (028-081). The context is a man who boasts that he cannot be killed by anyone, and [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s response is: anyone can kill your body; speak the truth about what is in your power and what is not. Truth-telling here is not a social nicety but a discipline of self-knowledge — the [[exercise|practice]] of stripping away delusion about one's own capacities.

**The human affinity for truth**: In Discourse 18, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes a natural human orientation toward truth: "no one is willingly deprived of the truth, as [[plato|Plato]] says; but every man is unwillingly deprived of the truth" (018-010). This [[plato|Plato]]nic principle — that the soul naturally seeks truth and errs only through ignorance or passion — underpins [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s conviction that philosophical education is a matter of removing impediments rather than implanting new faculties.
