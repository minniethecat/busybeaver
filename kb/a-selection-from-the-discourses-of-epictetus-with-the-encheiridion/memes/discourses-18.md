[018-001]What is the cause of assenting to anything? The fact that it

[018-002]appears to be true. It is not possible then to [[assent]] to that which appears not

[018-003]to be true. Why? Because this is the [[nature]] of the understanding, to incline to

[018-004]the true, to be dissatisfied with the false, and in matters uncertain to

[018-005]withhold assent. What is the proof of this? Imagine (persuade yourself), if you

[018-006]can, that it is now night. It is not possible. Take away your persuasion that

[018-007]it is day. It is not possible. Persuade yourself or take away your persuasion

[018-008]that the stars are even in number. It is impossible. When then any man assents

[018-009]to that which is false, be assured that he did not intend to assent to it as

[018-010]false, for every soul is unwillingly deprived of the truth, as Plato says; but

[018-011]the falsity seemed to him to be true. Well, in acts what have we of the like

[018-012]kind as we have here truth or falsehood? We have the fit and the not fit (duty

[018-013]and not duty), the profitable and the unprofitable, that which is suitable to a

[018-014]person and that which is not, and whatever is like these. Can then a man think

[018-015]that a thing is useful to him and not choose it? He cannot. How says Medea?

[018-016]“’Tis true I know what evil I shall do,

[018-017]But [[passion]] overpowers the better counsel.”

[018-018]She thought that to indulge her passion and take vengeance on her husband was

[018-019]more profitable than to spare her children. It was so; but she was deceived.

[018-020]Show her plainly that she is deceived, and she will not do it; but so long as

[018-021]you do not show it, what can she follow except that which appears to herself

[018-022](her opinion)? Nothing else. Why then are you angry with the unhappy woman that

[018-023]she has been bewildered about the most important things, and is become a viper

[018-024]instead of a human creature? And why not, if it is possible, rather pity, as we

[018-025]pity the blind and the lame, so those who are blinded and maimed in the

[018-026]faculties which are supreme?

[018-027]Whoever then clearly remembers this, that to man the measure of every act is

[018-028]the appearance (the opinion), whether the thing appears [[good]] or bad. If good,

[018-029]he is free from blame; if bad, himself suffers the penalty, for it is

[018-030]impossible that he who is deceived can be one person, and he who suffers

[018-031]another person—whoever remembers this will not be angry with any man, will not

[018-032]be vexed at any man, will not revile or blame any man, nor hate, nor quarrel

[018-033]with any man.

[018-034]So then all these great and dreadful deeds have this origin, in the appearance

[018-035](opinion)? Yes, this origin and no other. The Iliad is nothing else than

[018-036]appearance and the use of appearances. It appeared to Alexander to carry off

[018-037]the wife of Menelaus. It appeared to Helene to follow him. If then it had

[018-038]appeared to Menelaus to feel that it was a gain to be deprived of such a wife,

[018-039]what would have happened? Not only would the Iliad have been lost, but the

[018-040]Odyssey also. On so small a matter then did such great things depend? But what

[018-041]do you mean by such great things? Wars and civil commotions, and the

[018-042]destruction of many men and cities. And what great matter is this? Is it

[018-043]nothing? But what great matter is the death of many oxen, and many sheep, and

[018-044]many nests of swallows or storks being burnt or destroyed? Are these things

[018-045]then like those? Very like. Bodies of men are destroyed, and the bodies of oxen

[018-046]and sheep; the dwellings of men are burnt, and the nests of storks. What is

[018-047]there in this great or dreadful? Or show me what is the difference between a

[018-048]man’s house and a stork’s nest, as far as each is a dwelling; except that man

[018-049]builds his little houses of beams and tiles and bricks, and the stork builds

[018-050]them of sticks and mud. Are a stork and a man then like things? What say you?

[018-051]In body they are very much alike.

[018-052]Does a man then differ in no respect from a stork? Don’t suppose that I say so;

[018-053]but there is no difference in these matters (which I have mentioned). In what

[018-054]then is the difference? Seek and you will find that there is a difference in

[018-055]another matter. See whether it is not in a man the understanding of what he

[018-056]does, see if it is not in social community, in fidelity, in modesty, in

[018-057]steadfastness, in intelligence. Where then is the great good and evil in men?

[018-058]It is where the difference is. If the difference is preserved and remains

[018-059]fenced round, and neither modesty is destroyed, nor fidelity, nor intelligence,

[018-060]then the man also is preserved; but if any of these things is destroyed and

[018-061]stormed like a city, then the man too perishes: and in this consist the great

[018-062]things. Alexander, you say, sustained great damage then when the Hellenes

[018-063]invaded and when they ravaged Troy, and when his brothers perished. By no

[018-064]means; for no man is damaged by an action which is not his own; but what

[018-065]happened at that time was only the destruction of stork’s nests. Now the ruin

[018-066]of Alexander was when he lost the character of modesty, fidelity, regard to

[018-067]hospitality, and to decency. When was Achilles ruined? Was it when Patroclus

[018-068]died? Not so. But it happened when he began to be angry, when he wept for a

[018-069]girl, when he forgot that he was at Troy not to get mistresses, but to fight.

[018-070]These things are the ruin of men, this is being besieged, this is the

[018-071]destruction of cities, when right opinions are destroyed, when they are

[018-072]corrupted.
