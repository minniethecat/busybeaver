## Definition

Philosophy, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is not a theoretical enterprise but the art of living rightly. Its beginning is not in books or [[disputation|argument]]s but in self-awareness: the recognition that one does not know how to live, and the earnest desire to learn.

"The beginning of philosophy, to him at least who enters on it in the right way and by the door, is a consciousness of his own weakness and inability about necessary things" (027-001–027-003). Philosophy begins where [[self-conceit|self-deception]] ends. It is, fundamentally, the discipline of bringing one's preconceptions into alignment with reality and one's actions into alignment with right judgment.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s understanding of philosophy is distinctive in its radical practicality. He has little [[endurance|patience]] for philosophy as intellectual display or subtle [[disputation|argument]] for its own sake. Philosophy is the work of a lifetime, and its test is in how one lives, not in what one says.

He locates the beginning of philosophy in the recognition of disagreement among human beings about the most important matters: "The beginning of philosophy is a perception of the disagreement among men" (027-036–027-037). We all agree on the names — good, evil, just, unjust — but we disagree violently on their application. Philosophy is the investigation that resolves this disagreement by grounding moral language in reality.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] defines philosophy's task in terms of his signature distinction between what is and is not in our power. "The work of philosophy is this: not to fail in the desire, nor to fall into that which we would avoid" (029-019). That is, philosophy's purpose is to train desire and aversion so that they attach only to what is within the will's [[power|control]].

This has direct consequences for how philosophy should be studied. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] criticizes those who read philosophical texts without applying them: "What then is the thing which is wanted? A man who shall apply them, one who by his acts shall bear testimony to his words" (019-070–019-071). The measure of a philosopher is not [[rhetoric|eloquence]] but character: does this person respond to insult without anger? To loss without grief? To temptation without capitulation?

He is similarly sharp about the relationship between the three branches of Stoic philosophy — logic, physics, and ethics. While all are necessary, ethics is primary and logic is instrumental. "The end is to follow the [[divinity|gods]]; the work of philosophy is this: not to fail in the desire, nor to fall into that which we would avoid" (029-019). [[logic|Logic]], grammar, and [[disputation|argument]]ation are tools, not ends.

Perhaps most provocatively, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] suggests that philosophy is wasted if it does not produce a certain kind of person: one who is free from fear, steady in judgment, gentle in bearing, and useful to others. "Show me a Stoic, if you have one" (029-040–029-043) — this is his challenge to his students and to philosophy itself.
