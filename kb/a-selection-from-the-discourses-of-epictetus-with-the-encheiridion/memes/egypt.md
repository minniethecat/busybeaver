# Egypt

**Egypt** appears in the Discourses not as a described locale but as one member of a recurring list of nations — Jews, Syrians, Egyptians, Romans — through which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes a point about [[preconceptions|preconceptions]] and moral disagreement. The Egyptians stand for a distinct set of customs whose divergence from others exposes the difference between a shared principle and its contested application.

## Location

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] gives no geographic account of Egypt; he names it only through its people, the "Egyptians," set beside other nations of the eastern Mediterranean and [[rome|Roman]] world (014-009, 027-046). In the text it functions as a name for a distinct national custom rather than as a place on a map.

## Significance in the Discourses

Egypt figures in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s analysis of how disputes arise. All men share the preconception that [[piety|holiness]] is to be pursued; contradiction enters only "in the adaptation of the præcognitions to the particular cases" (014-005, 014-006). "This is the dispute among the Jews and the Syrians and the Egyptians and the Romans," he says — not whether [[piety|holiness]] "should be preferred to all things," but "whether it is holy to eat pig's flesh or not holy" (014-008, 014-009, 014-011). The Egyptians thus illustrate that peoples quarrel over application, not over first principles.

The Egyptians also appear in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s [[disputation|argument]] against making [[custom|custom]] the measure of truth. To the claim that a thing is right because it "seems" right to oneself, he replies: "How more to you than those which seem right to the Syrians? why more than what seem right to the Egyptians?" (027-045, 027-046). What merely seems so to each man or nation "is not sufficient for determining" what is; as with weights and measures, there must be a rule superior to bare appearance (027-047, 027-048, 027-050). National custom — Egyptian or any other — cannot be that standard.

Finally, "Egyptian" serves among the labels [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses to distinguish truly being something from only acting the part. Just as a man is "called a Jew, or a Syrian, or an" Egyptian, so one is what he professes only when he has genuinely absorbed its doctrine and does not merely "act as one" (025-055, 025-056, 025-057).

## Historical Context

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s day Egypt was a province of the [[rome|Roman]] empire, and "the Egyptians" would have been familiar to his audience as one of the distinct peoples of the eastern Mediterranean, each with its own religious customs — hence its usefulness as a stock example of national difference in matters of [[piety|holiness]] and diet.
