[010-001]When a man was consulting him how he should persuade

[010-002]his brother to cease being angry with him, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] replied: Philosophy does

[010-003]not propose to secure for a man any external thing. If it did (or if it were

[010-004]not, as I say), philosophy would be allowing something which is not within its

[010-005]province. For as the carpenter’s material is wood, and that of the statuary is

[010-006]copper, so the matter of the art of living is each man’s life. When then is my

[010-007]brother’s? That again belongs to his own art; but with respect to yours, it is

[010-008]one of the external things, like a piece of land, like health, like reputation.

[010-009]But Philosophy promises none of these. In every circumstance I will maintain,

[010-010]she says, the governing part conformable to [[nature]]. Whose governing part? His

[010-011]in whom I am, she says.

[010-012]How then shall my brother cease to be angry with me? Bring him to me and I will

[010-013]tell him. But I have nothing to say to you about his anger.

[010-014]When the man who was consulting him said, I seek to know this, How, even if my

[010-015]brother is not reconciled to me, shall I maintain myself in a state conformable

[010-016]to nature? Nothing great, said Epictetus, is produced suddenly, since not even

[010-017]the grape or the fig is. If you say to me now that you want a fig, I will

[010-018]answer to you that it requires time: let it flower first, then put forth fruit,

[010-019]and then ripen. Is then the fruit of a fig-tree not perfected suddenly and in

[010-020]one hour, and would you possess the fruit of a man’s mind in so short a time

[010-021]and so easily? Do not expect it, even if I tell you.
