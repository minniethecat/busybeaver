[061-001]The wise and [[good]] man neither himself

[061-002]fights with any person, nor does he allow another, so far as he can prevent it.

[061-003]And an example of this as well as of all other things is proposed to us in the

[061-004]life of [[socrates|Socrates]], who not only himself on all occasions avoided fights

[061-005](quarrels), but would not allow even others to quarrel. See in Xenophon’s

[061-006]Symposium how many quarrels he settled, how further he endured Thrasymachus and

[061-007]Polus and Callicles; how he tolerated his wife, and how he tolerated his son

[061-008]who attempted to confute him and to cavil with him. For he remembered well that

[061-009]no man has in his power another man’s ruling principle. He wished therefore for

[061-010]nothing else than that which was his own. And what is this? Not that this or

[061-011]that man may act according to [[nature]], for that is a thing which belongs to

[061-012]another; but that while others are doing their own acts, as they choose, he may

[061-013]nevertheless be in a condition conformable to nature and live in it, only doing

[061-014]what is his own to the end that others also may be in a state conformable to

[061-015]nature. For this is the object always set before him by the wise and good man.

[061-016]Is it to be commander (a prætor) of an army? No; but if it is permitted him,

[061-017]his object is in this matter to maintain his own ruling principle. Is it to

[061-018]marry? No; but if marriage is allowed to him, in this matter his object is to

[061-019]maintain himself in a condition conformable to nature. But if he would have his

[061-020]son not to do wrong or his wife, he would have what belongs to another not to

[061-021]belong to another: and to be instructed is this, to learn what things are a

[061-022]man’s own and what belongs to another.

[061-023]How then is there left any place for fighting (quarrelling) to a man who has

[061-024]this opinion (which he ought to have)? Is he surprised at any thing which

[061-025]happens, and does it appear new to him? Does he not expect that which comes

[061-026]from the bad to be worse and more grievous than that what actually befalls him?

[061-027]And does he not reckon as pure gain whatever they (the bad) may do which falls

[061-028]short of extreme wickedness? Such a person has reviled you. Great thanks to him

[061-029]for not having struck you. But he has struck me also. Great thanks that he did

[061-030]not wound you. But he wounded me also. Great thanks that he did not kill you.

[061-031]For when did he learn or in what school that man is a tame animal, that men

[061-032]love one another, that an act of injustice is a great harm to him who does it.

[061-033]Since then he has not learned this and is not convinced of it, why shall he not

[061-034]follow that which seems to be for his own interest? Your neighbor has thrown

[061-035]stones. Have you then done anything wrong? But the things in the house have

[061-036]been broken. Are you then a utensil? No; but a free power of will. What then is

[061-037]given to you (to do) in answer to this? If you are like a wolf, you must bite

[061-038]in return, and throw more stones. But, if you consider what is proper for a

[061-039]man, examine your storehouse, see with what faculties you came into the world.

[061-040]Have you the disposition of a wild beast, have you the disposition of revenge

[061-041]for an injury? When is a horse wretched? When he is deprived of his natural

[061-042]faculties, not when he cannot crow like a cock, but when he cannot run. When is

[061-043]a dog wretched? Not when he cannot fly, but when he cannot track his game. Is

[061-044]then a man also unhappy in this way, not because he cannot strangle lions or

[061-045]embrace statues, for he did not come into the world in the possession of

[061-046]certain powers from nature for this purpose, but because he has lost his

[061-047]probity and his fidelity? People ought to meet and lament such a man for the

[061-048]misfortunes into which he has fallen; not indeed to lament because a man has

[061-049]been born or has died, but because it has happened to him in his lifetime to

[061-050]have lost the things which are his own, not that which he received from his

[061-051]father, not his land and house, and his inn, and his slaves; for not one of

[061-052]these things is a man’s own, but all belong to others, are servile, and subject

[061-053]to account ([Greek: hupeithuna]), at different times given to different persons

[061-054]by those who have them in their power: but I mean the things which belong to

[061-055]him as a man, the marks (stamps) in his mind with which he came into the world,

[061-056]such as we seek also on coins, and if we find them we approve of the coins, and

[061-057]if we do not find the marks we reject them. What is the stamp on this

[061-058]sestertius? The stamp of Trajan. Present it. It is the stamp of Nero. Throw it

[061-059]away; it cannot be accepted, it is counterfeit. So also in this case: What is

[061-060]the stamp of his opinions? It is gentleness, a sociable disposition, a tolerant

[061-061]temper, a disposition to mutual affections. Produce these qualities. I accept

[061-062]them: I consider this man a citizen, I accept him as a neighbor, a companion in

[061-063]my voyages. Only see that he has not Nero’s stamp. Is he passionate, is he full

[061-064]of resentment, is he fault-finding? If the whim seizes him, does he break the

[061-065]heads of those who come in his way? (If so), why then did you say that he is a

[061-066]man? Is everything judged (determined) by the bare form? If that is so, say

[061-067]that the form in wax is an apple and has the smell and the taste of an apple.

[061-068]But the external figure is not enough: neither then is the nose enough and the

[061-069]eyes to make the man, but he must have the opinions of a man. Here is a man who

[061-070]does not listen to [[reason]], who does not know when he is refuted: he is an ass;

[061-071]in another man the sense of shame is become dead: he is good for nothing, he is

[061-072]anything rather than a man. This man seeks whom he may meet and kick or bite,

[061-073]so that he is not even a sheep or an ass, but a kind of wild beast.

[061-074]What then? would you have me to be despised?—By whom? by those who know you?

[061-075]and how shall those who know you despise a man who is gentle and modest?

[061-076]Perhaps you mean by those who do not know you? What is that to you? For no

[061-077]other artisan cares for the opinion of those who know not his art. But they

[061-078]will be more hostile to me for this reason. Why do you say “me”? Can any man

[061-079]injure your will, or prevent you from using in a natural way the appearances

[061-080]which are presented to you? In no way can he. Why then are you still disturbed

[061-081]and why do you choose to show yourself afraid? And why do you not come forth

[061-082]and proclaim that you are at peace with all men whatever they may do, and laugh

[061-083]at those chiefly who think that they can harm you? These slaves, you can say,

[061-084]know not either who I am, nor where lies my good or my evil, because they have

[061-085]no access to the things which are mine.

[061-086]In this way also those who occupy a strong city mock the besiegers (and say):

[061-087]What trouble these men are now taking for nothing; our wall is secure, we have

[061-088]food for a very long time, and all other resources. These are the things which

[061-089]make a city strong and impregnable; but nothing else than his opinions makes a

[061-090]man’s soul impregnable. For what wall is so strong, or what body is so hard, or

[061-091]what possession is so safe, or what honor (rank, character) so free from

[061-092]assault (as a man’s opinions)? All (other) things everywhere are perishable,

[061-093]easily taken by assault, and if any man in any way is attached to them, he must

[061-094]be disturbed, except what is bad, he must fear, lament, find his desires

[061-095]disappointed, and fall into things which he would avoid. Then do we not choose

[061-096]to make secure the only means of safety which are offered to us, and do we not

[061-097]choose to withdraw ourselves from that which is perishable and servile and to

[061-098]labor at the things which are imperishable and by nature free; and do we not

[061-099]remember that no man either hurts another or does good to another, but that a

[061-100]man’s opinions about each thing, is that which hurts him, is that which

[061-101]overturns him; this is fighting, this is civil discord, this is war? That which

[061-102]made Eteocles and Polynices enemies was nothing else than this opinion which

[061-103]they had about royal power, their opinion about exile, that the one is the

[061-104]extreme of evils, the other the greatest good. Now this is the nature of every

[061-105]man to seek the good, to avoid the bad; to consider him who deprives us of the

[061-106]one and involves us in the other an enemy and treacherous, even if he be a

[061-107]brother, or a son, or a father. For nothing is more akin to us than the good;

[061-108]therefore, if these things ([[externals]]) are good and evil, neither is a father a

[061-109]friend to sons, nor a brother to a brother, but all the world is everywhere

[061-110]full of enemies, treacherous men, and sycophants. But if the will ([Greek:

[061-111]proairesis], the purpose, the intention) being what it ought to be, is the only

[061-112]good; and if the will being such as it ought not to be, is the only evil, where

[061-113]is there any strife, where is there reviling? about what? about the things

[061-114]which do not concern us? and strife with whom? with the ignorant, the unhappy,

[061-115]with those who are deceived about the chief things?

[061-116]Remembering this Socrates managed his own house and endured a very ill-tempered

[061-117]wife and a foolish (ungrateful?) son.
