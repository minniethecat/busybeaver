## Definition

Contentment, in Stoic thought, is not passive resignation but the active disposition of accepting whatever happens as the work of a providential nature. It is the state of one who has aligned their will with the cosmic order and therefore finds nothing to complain about.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] expresses this through the ideal of being "content with what you see daily" (031-078) and accepting "contentedly, and with equanimity" whatever the [[divinity|divine]] administration brings (009-003). Contentment is the emotional signature of correct judgment: when one truly believes that externals are indifferent and that God's order is good, contentment follows naturally.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats contentment as a direct consequence of the central Stoic disciplines. If you limit desire to what is within your power, and if you accept what comes from outside as the will of God, then contentment is the natural result.

The link between contentment and the discipline of desire is explicit: "Do you not… offer thanksgiving for the things which you have? Do you not know that you are naturally free, and if you are content with these, what else do you want?" (021-005). Contentment is not something one must strive to feel; it is the absence of the [[impulse|striving]] that creates discontent. When desire is correctly limited, contentment simply is.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes that discontent arises from a failure of perspective. We compare ourselves to others, we want more than we need, we treat our present circumstances as if they were permanent deprivations rather than temporary assignments. "Are you not content with what you see daily? Have you anything better than the sun, than the moon, than the stars?" (031-078). The natural world, reason, the capacity for virtue — these are gifts of immense value that we overlook while fixating on trivial lacks.

The Stoic response to discontent is not to acquire more but to want less — or, more precisely, to want only what is already within one's [[power|control]]. "Why are you insatiable? Why are you not content? Why do you contract your stomach?" (057-105). The restlessness of desire is self-perpetuating: each satisfaction only fuels the next craving. The Stoic alternative is to step off this wheel entirely by placing one's good where it cannot be lost.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] connects contentment with piety. To be content is to accept the [[divinity|divine]] administration without complaint. "Accept all contentedly" (008-059) — this is not [[obedience|submission]] to blind [[necessity|fate]] but trust in a rational cosmic order. The content person is not passive; they are actively aligned with nature in a way that makes complaint irrational.
