## Definition

Wealth (*ploutos*) is one of the classic Stoic "indifferents" (*adiaphora*) — something that is neither good nor evil in itself. In the Stoic system, wealth is classified as a "preferred indifferent" (*proēgmenon*): it has natural value and is rational to pursue, but its possession contributes nothing to virtue, and its loss cannot diminish moral character. The only true wealth, for a Stoic, is the possession of virtue and wisdom.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats wealth as a [[example|paradigm]]atic external — something that appears desirable but exercises a hidden tyranny over those who pursue it. He asks bluntly: "Is it life or death, poverty or wealth?" (024-013). The rhetorical question forces the student to recognize that these alternatives are not the point — the point is what use one makes of whatever circumstances fortune provides.

The triad of classic indifferents appears again with wealth at the head: "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation" (025-039). [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s repetition of this formula throughout the Discourses drills the point home: these are the things most people spend their lives pursuing, and yet none of them can deliver what they promise. Wealth cannot make a person master of themselves. "For wealth does not do it, nor consulship" — only philosophy can provide true [[power|mastery]] (057-037).

In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that power and wealth become objects of desire precisely because people aim at them rather than at virtue. "[[power|Power]] and wealth, perhaps you too aim at these very things" (069-020, 069-021). The danger is not in possessing wealth but in aiming at it — in making it the target of one's desire rather than a side-effect of virtuous activity. When wealth is the goal, the soul is enslaved to fortune; when virtue is the goal, wealth or poverty become matters of indifference.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not condemn wealth itself. A wealthy person can be a Stoic; what matters is the internal disposition. The test is simple: "If you aim at wealth, you will be some time a slave" (083-007). The wealthy Stoic holds wealth loosely, uses it virtuously, and would surrender it without [[grief|distress]] if circumstances demanded.

## Key Passages

- (024-013) — "Is it life or death, poverty or wealth?" The alternatives are not what matters; virtue is.
- (025-039) — "The indifferent are wealth, health, reputation." The classic triad.
- (057-037) — "For wealth does not do it, nor consulship" — external goods cannot make one master of oneself.
- (069-020) — [[power|Power]] and wealth, perhaps you too aim at these very things.
- (069-021) — "These very things (power and wealth) because you aim at them" become sources of slavery.
- (083-007) — "If you aim at wealth, you will be some time a slave."
