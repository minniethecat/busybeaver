[022-001]Things themselves (materials) are

[022-002]indifferent; but the use of them is not indifferent. How then shall a man

[022-003]preserve firmness and tranquillity, and at the same time be careful and neither

[022-004]rash nor negligent? If he imitates those who play at dice. The counters are

[022-005]indifferent; the dice are indifferent. How do I know what the cast will be? But

[022-006]to use carefully and dexterously the cast of the dice, this is my business.

[022-007]Thus then in life also the chief business is this: distinguish and separate

[022-008]things, and say: Externals are not in my power: will is in my power. Where

[022-009]shall I seek the [[good]] and the bad? Within, in the things which are my own. But

[022-010]in what does not belong to you call nothing either good or bad, or profit or

[022-011]damage or anything of the kind.

[022-012]What then? Should we use such things carelessly? In no way: for this on the

[022-013]other hand is bad for the faculty of the will, and consequently against [[nature]];

[022-014]but we should act carefully because the use is not indifferent, and we should

[022-015]also act with firmness and freedom from perturbations because the material is

[022-016]indifferent. For where the material is not indifferent, there no man can hinder

[022-017]me or compel me. Where I can be hindered and compelled, the obtaining of those

[022-018]things is not in my power, nor is it good or bad; but the use is either bad or

[022-019]good, and the use is in my power. But it is difficult to mingle and to bring

[022-020]together these two things—the carefulness of him who is affected by the matter

[022-021](or things about him), and the firmness of him who has no regard for it; but it

[022-022]is not impossible: and if it is, [[happiness]] is impossible. But we should act as

[022-023]we do in the case of a voyage. What can I do? I can choose the master of the

[022-024]ship, the sailors, the day, the opportunity. Then comes a storm. What more have

[022-025]I to care for? for my part is done. The business belongs to another, the

[022-026]master. But the ship is sinking—what then have I to do? I do the only thing

[022-027]that I can, not to be drowned full of fear, nor screaming nor blaming God, but

[022-028]knowing that what has been produced must also perish: for I am not an immortal

[022-029]being, but a man, a part of the whole, as an hour is a part of the day: I must

[022-030]be present like the hour, and past like the hour. What difference then does it

[022-031]make to me how I pass away, whether by being suffocated or by a fever, for I

[022-032]must pass through some such means.

[022-033]How then is it said that some external things are according to nature and

[022-034]others contrary to nature? It is said as it might be said if we were separated

[022-035]from union (or society): for to the foot I shall say that it is according to

[022-036]nature for it to be clean; but if you take it as a foot and as a thing not

[022-037]detached (independent), it will befit it both to step into the mud and tread on

[022-038]thorns, and sometimes to be cut off for the good of the whole body; otherwise

[022-039]it is no longer a foot. We should think in some such way about ourselves also.

[022-040]What are you? A man. If you consider yourself as detached from other men, it is

[022-041]according to nature to live to old age, to be rich, to be healthy. But if you

[022-042]consider yourself as a man and a part of a certain whole, it is for the sake of

[022-043]that whole that at one time you should be sick, at another time take a voyage

[022-044]and run into danger, and at another time be in want, and in some cases die

[022-045]prematurely. Why then are you troubled? Do you not know, that as a foot is no

[022-046]longer a foot if it is detached from the body, so you are no longer a man if

[022-047]you are separated from other men. For what is a man? A part of a state, of that

[022-048]first which consists of gods and of men; then of that which is called next to

[022-049]it, which is a small image of the universal state. What then must I be brought

[022-050]to trial; must another have a fever, another sail on the sea, another die, and

[022-051]another be condemned? Yes, for it is impossible in such a universe of things,

[022-052]among so many living together, that such things should not happen, some to one

[022-053]and others to others. It is your duty then since you are come here, to say what

[022-054]you ought, to arrange these things as it is fit. Then some one says, “I shall

[022-055]charge you with doing me wrong.” Much good may it do you: I have done my part;

[022-056]but whether you also have done yours, you must look to that; for there is some

[022-057]danger of this too, that it may escape your notice.
