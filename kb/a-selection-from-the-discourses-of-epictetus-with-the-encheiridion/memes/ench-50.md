[118-001]Whatever things (rules) are proposed to you (for the conduct of life) abide by

[118-002]them, as if they were laws, as if you would be guilty of impiety if you

[118-003]transgressed any of them. And whatever any man shall say about you, do not

[118-004]attend to it; for this is no affair of yours. How long will you then still

[118-005]defer thinking yourself worthy of the best things, and in no matter

[118-006]transgressing the distinctive [[reason]]? Have you accepted the theorems (rules),

[118-007]which it was your duty to agree to, and have you agreed to them? what teacher

[118-008]then do you still expect that you defer to him the correction of yourself? You

[118-009]are no longer a youth, but already a full-grown man. If, then, you are

[118-010]negligent and slothful, and are continually making procrastination after

[118-011]procrastination, and proposal (intention) after proposal, and fixing day after

[118-012]day, after which you will attend to yourself, you will not know that you are

[118-013]not making improvement, but you will continue ignorant (uninstructed) both

[118-014]while you live and till you die. Immediately then think it right to live as a

[118-015]full-grown man, and one who is making proficiency, and let everything which

[118-016]appears to you to be the best be to you a law which must not be transgressed.

[118-017]And if anything laborious or pleasant or glorious or inglorious be presented to

[118-018]you, remember that now is the contest, now are the Olympic games, and they

[118-019]cannot be deferred; and that it depends on one defeat and one giving way that

[118-020]progress is either lost or maintained. [[socrates|Socrates]] in this way became perfect, in

[118-021]all things improving himself, attending to nothing except to reason. But you,

[118-022]though you are not yet a Socrates, ought to live as one who wishes to be a

[118-023]Socrates.
