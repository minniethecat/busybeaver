# Doctrine

**Doctrine** (*dogma*) is a settled conviction or principle that constitutes the foundation of one's ethical life in Epictetan [[stoicism|Stoicism]]. Unlike casual opinion or speculative theory, a doctrine is a judgment that has been deliberately examined, tested against reason, and internalized to the point that it reliably governs conduct. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the beginning of philosophy itself lies in scrutinizing one's doctrines: a person must first become aware that their existing convictions are inconsistent or harmful before any genuine progress is possible. The Stoic teacher's task is not to transmit new information but to help the student examine and reform their *dogmata*, replacing false or conflicting doctrines with those consistent with nature and reason.

## Definition

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s usage, *dogma* denotes a practical conviction that bridges the gap between abstract knowledge and lived behavior. It is not merely a proposition one professes but a principle one actually lives by. A doctrine, once formed and settled in the mind, determines how a person responds to [[appearances|impressions]], makes choices, and endures circumstances. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] distinguishes sharply between holding a doctrine as intellectual furniture and possessing it as an active, operative guide: the Stoic is defined not by what doctrines he can recite but by what doctrines his actions reveal (003-001). The formation of sound doctrines is therefore the central project of moral education, and the measure of philosophical attainment is the consistency between one's professed convictions and one's daily conduct.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats doctrine as the invisible architecture of character. In his view, every action springs from some underlying conviction about what is good, what is evil, or what is appropriate. When a person acts dishonestly, the fault lies not in the action [[solitude|alone]] but in the doctrine that made dishonesty seem advantageous — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes that if dishonesty appears profitable, "then the doctrine of [[epicurus|Epicurus]] becomes strong" (035-029), meaning that a false ethical premise, once accepted, inevitably produces vicious conduct. The remedy is not to scold the behavior but to interrogate and overturn the doctrine that produced it.

The biographer notes that a person who has received Stoic doctrine must internalize it for their own use, "that they may be able to assent to this doctrine as they ought" (P02-029). This framing is characteristic of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s practical approach: doctrine is not ornamental but instrumental. It must equip the student to face [[appearances|impressions]], desires, and adversities with the correct judgments already in place. The alternative — having doctrines that sit unused while one lives by contrary impulses — is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the mark of the unphilosophical life.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists on the internal consistency of doctrines. He exhorts his students to "seek for doctrines which are consistent with what you profess" (043-034), recognizing that a mind divided against itself cannot achieve tranquility. A person who professes Stoic principles while harboring Epicurean assumptions about pleasure, or who affirms the insignificance of externals while being enslaved to reputation, lives in contradiction. The path of progress consists in systematically rooting out such inconsistencies, bringing every operative conviction into alignment with the core Stoic teaching that virtue [[solitude|alone]] is good.

## Key Passages

> The beginning of philosophy, at least for those who enter upon it in the proper way and by the front door, is a consciousness of one's own weakness and incapacity with regard to necessary things. (003-001)

> Plato's doctrine that every mind is deprived of the truth involuntarily teaches you nothing else than this. (035-083)

> Seek for doctrines which are consistent with what you profess. (043-034)
