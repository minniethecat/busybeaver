## Definition

Shame (*aischynē*) is analyzed in Stoic ethics as a passion — a form of fear or [[grief|distress]] arising from the perception of having done something dishonorable in the eyes of others. The Stoics carefully distinguished [[modesty|shame]] from modesty (*[[modesty|aidōs]]*), which they regarded as a healthy and virtuous disposition. [[modesty|Modesty]] is the internal sense of what is fitting and honorable, rooted in one's own moral judgment. Shame, by contrast, is concern with the opinions of others — it is a fear of external disapproval, and therefore, like all passions based on externals, a disturbance to be overcome.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes considerable attention to distinguishing proper from improper [[modesty|shame]]. He repeatedly invokes the "sense of [[modesty|shame]]" as a virtuous quality that keeps a person from base actions: "the sense of [[modesty|shame]], when a man is resolved not to do what is unseemly" (005-007). This is *[[modesty|aidōs]]* — the internal moral compass that prevents wrongdoing before it occurs. It is one of the qualities that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] most admires in a student of philosophy.

But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is equally sharp about what should NOT cause [[modesty|shame]]. "Virtuous [[modesty|shame]] is your own; who can take it from you?" (016-012). The point is that genuine [[modesty|shame]] — the kind worth having — concerns only one's own moral failures, not external circumstances or the opinions of others. To be a[[modesty|shame]]d of poverty, physical deformity, or low social status is to have mistaken indifferents for evils.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is particularly critical of philosophers who display [[modesty|shame]] about things that are philosophically irrelevant. "It is [[modesty|shame]]ful for philosophers thus to be frightened and disturbed" (019-081). The philosopher who trembles at the prospect of exile, death, or poverty has failed to internalize the very doctrines he professes. True [[modesty|shame]] should attach only to moral failure — to assenting to false [[appearances|impressions]], to valuing externals above virtue.

He also distinguishes between the [[modesty|shame]]less person (one who has lost *[[modesty|aidōs]]*) and the person who is free from false [[modesty|shame]] (one who has overcome *aischynē*). The former is morally defective; the latter is philosophically advanced. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] aims to produce students who are "[[modesty|shame]]less" in the second sense — indifferent to the opinions of the ignorant — while preserving the inner moral sensitivity of *[[modesty|aidōs]]*.

## Key Passages

- (005-007) — "The sense of [[modesty|shame]], when a man is resolved not to do what is unseemly" — positive *[[modesty|aidōs]]*.
- (005-012) — "If the sense of [[modesty|shame]] and modesty are destroyed" — the danger of losing moral sensitivity.
- (016-012) — "Virtuous [[modesty|shame]] is your own; who can take it from you?" — only moral [[modesty|shame]] is genuine.
- (019-076) — "But it is [[modesty|shame]]ful to contemplate these things" — distinguishing proper from improper objects of [[modesty|shame]].
- (019-081) — "It is [[modesty|shame]]ful for philosophers thus to be frightened" — critique of philosophers who feel false [[modesty|shame]].
- (020-045) — "Not death is evil, but a [[modesty|shame]]ful death" — [[modesty|shame]] properly attaches only to the manner of dying, not death itself.
- (024-039) — "You are not a[[modesty|shame]]d of thinking such things" when God sees all — the theological dimension of [[modesty|shame]].
