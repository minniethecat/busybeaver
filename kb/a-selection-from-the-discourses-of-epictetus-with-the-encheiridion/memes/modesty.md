# Modesty

**Modesty** (*aidōs*) is the sense of shame before what is base — an inward check against ignoble speech and action. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching, modesty is not bashfulness or social anxiety but a natural faculty given to every human being, a "protection" (053-017) that guards character from corruption. Alongside [[fidelity]], it is the virtue [[epictetus|Epictetus]] invokes most often when describing what the philosopher must preserve at all costs.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] explicitly gives the Greek term *aidōs* at one point (049-019), confirming that the concept he has in mind is the classical virtue of self-respecting shame — the capacity to feel disgrace at what is genuinely disgraceful. This is distinct from mere embarrassment before others' opinions; it is an inner sensitivity to moral ugliness.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, modesty is best understood as the rational person's natural aversion to what is morally ugly (*aischron*). Just as courage is the knowledge of what is truly fearful and what is not, modesty is the knowledge of what is truly shameful and what is not. The modest person is not one who fears others' judgments indiscriminately, but one who is sensitive specifically to actions that degrade the self.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] connects modesty to the discipline of action: it governs how we carry ourselves in society. "If you choose to be modest and faithful, who shall not allow you to be so?" (021-007). The point is that modesty, like all virtues, is within our power — it depends only on our judgments and choices, not on external circumstances.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats modesty in four recurring patterns across the Discourses.

**As a natural endowment**: In Discourse 5, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] lists modesty among the things that nature has given us to recognise what is appropriate: "If the sense of shame and modesty are anything, they are certainly things that belong to us by nature" (005-012). Modesty is not acquired through [[education|training]]; it is present from the start and needs only to be exercised, not stifled.

**As protection**: In Discourse 53, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the striking image of modesty as armour: "One who is to do these things must use modesty as his protection; if he does not, he will be exposed" (053-017). The idea is that modesty shields a person from moral harm in the same way that caution shields from physical harm.

**Paired with fidelity**: Throughout the Discourses, modesty and [[fidelity]] appear as a pair of complementary social virtues. "[[fidelity|Fidelity]] and modesty and a sure use of appearances have no mean or ignoble character" (003-012). The two are so closely linked that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] can use them almost interchangeably as shorthand for the entire sphere of social virtue. Both are destroyed by the same cause — valuing externals over character.

**As a possession beyond price**: In Discourse 59, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] contrasts modesty with political office. "He possesses a prætorship, I possess modesty. But I do not make a display of where I possess modesty" (059-024). The point is that modesty is a greater possession than any office, precisely because it cannot be lost through external events — only through one's own choices.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns explicitly that modesty can be lost. "If you shall lose modesty, is the damage small?" (026-046). The question is rhetorical: the loss of modesty is the loss of the self as a moral being. And once lost, it is not easily recovered — like fidelity, it is preserved by actions consistent with it and destroyed by actions contrary to it.

## Key Passages

> Fidelity and modesty and a sure use of appearances have no mean or ignoble character. (003-012)

> Is there not modesty ([Greek: aidos]), fidelity? (049-019)

> One who is to do these things must use modesty as his protection. (053-017)

> To preserve is no small matter, but it is modesty and fidelity and constancy, and a mind free from perturbation. (059-018)

> He possesses a prætorship, I possess modesty. But I do not make a display of where I possess modesty. (059-024)
