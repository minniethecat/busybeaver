## Definition

The **Three Topics** (Greek: τρεῖς τόποι, *treis topoi*) are [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s tripartite framework for philosophical exercise in Discourse 40. They define three fields: (1) desire and aversion, (2) impulse and action, and (3) assent and judgment.

They are [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own formulation, distinct from Pierre Hadot's later "[[discipline|Three Disciplines]]," which was inspired by but simplifies the original framework.

> "There are three things (topics, τόποι) in which a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and good." (040-001)

## Epictetus's Treatment

**First Topic — [[desire|Desire]]s and [[aversion|Aversion]]s** (040-003): That a man may not fail to get what he desires and not fall into what he wishes to avoid. This concerns the passions, for every perturbation arises from frustrated desire. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls it "the chief and most urgent" topic (040-009).

**Second Topic — [[impulse|Impulse]] and [[action|Action]]** (040-005): Concerning movements toward and away from objects, and doing what one ought according to order and reason. The goal is not freedom from all affect, but maintaining natural and acquired relations (040-016).

**Third Topic — [[assent|Assent]] and [[judgment|Judgment]]** (040-007): [[freedom|Freedom]] from deception and rashness in judgment. This "concerns the security of the other two." It is "above our power" for beginners, yet many neglect the first two topics for this one (040-023).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists on the proper order: first master desire, then duties, then logical refinement. He mocks those studying logic while still disturbed by money and reputation (040-030).

## Stoic Context

The Three Topics reflect the Stoic division of philosophy into physics, ethics, and logic — recast into a practical framework. The first topic corresponds to physics, the second to ethics, and the third to logic.

Hadot's "[[discipline|Three Disciplines]]" framework derives from but simplifies [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s original, which emphasizes [[education|training]] order and the priority of the first topic as "chief and most urgent."

## Key Passages

Discourse 40 (040-001) presents the primary statement of the Three Topics — their content, the order of [[education|training]], and the primacy of the first topic.

Discourse 40 (040-003) defines the first topic: desires and aversions, [[power|control]]ling the passions that prevent one from listening to reason.

Discourse 40 (040-016) explains the second topic: duties and maintaining relations, not becoming an unfeeling statue.

Discourse 40 (040-020) describes the third topic as security for the first two, with a critique of philosophers who invert the priority.

[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] §1 (069-001) provides the power/not-in-power distinction that grounds the entire Three Topics framework.
