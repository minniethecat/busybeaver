# Confidence

**Confidence** (*tharsos*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching is not blind boldness but the rational assurance that comes from understanding what truly matters. It forms one half of what seems to be a paradox: the Stoic is instructed to act with both confidence and caution simultaneously. "[[caution|Caution]] seems to be in a manner contrary to confidence, and contraries are in no way consistent" (020-004).

The paradox is resolved by distinguishing the domain of each attitude. We should be confident about things independent of the will — [[externals]], health, reputation, death — because none of these can harm our true self. We should be cautious about things within the will — our [[judgment|judgments]], [[desire|desires]], and [[assent]] — because only here can genuine harm occur.

## Definition

In Stoic usage, confidence (*tharsos*) is the opposite of fear and is closely allied with courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*). But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] redefines it in terms of his fundamental distinction between what is within our power (*eph' hēmin*) and what is not.

Confidence properly belongs to the domain of externals: "If things independent of the will and not in our power are nothing to us, with respect to these we must employ confidence" (020-018). Since health, wealth, reputation, and even life and death are neither good nor bad in themselves, we can face them with complete fearlessness. Nothing real is at stake.

[[caution|Caution]], however, belongs to the inner domain: "If the bad consists in the bad exercise of the will, caution ought only to be used where things are dependent on the will" (020-016). The only genuine evil is a corrupted [[judgment]] or a vicious choice. Here we must be watchful and careful, because the health of the soul is at stake.

The result is what [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls being "confident because of our caution" (020-019). Because we are so careful about the one thing that truly matters — our moral character — we can be utterly fearless about everything else.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes the entirety of Discourse 20 to this theme, titled "Of the Things Which Are in Our [[power|Power]]." He recognizes that the doctrine appears paradoxical and asks his students to "examine as well as we can, if it is true that it is possible to do everything both with caution and with confidence" (020-003).

His [[disputation|argument]] proceeds from his established principle: "The nature of good is in the use of [[appearances]], and the nature of evil likewise, and that things independent of our will do not admit either the nature of evil or of good" (020-010). From this, the distribution of confidence and caution follows logically.

The practical implication is a complete reorientation of emotional life. The Stoic student learns to face illness, poverty, exile, and death with [[tranquillity|serenity]] — not because these things are easy, but because they are, in the Stoic sense, nothing. Meanwhile, the same student becomes exquisitely sensitive to the slightest moral error, the smallest failure of [[reason]] or justice. This inversion of ordinary attitudes is the hallmark of Stoic [[education|training]].

## Key Passages

- The paradox: how can caution and confidence, which seem contrary, be consistent? (020-004).
- "Where things are not dependent on the will, there you should employ confidence" (020-013).
- "[[caution|Caution]] ought only to be used where things are dependent on the will" (020-016).
- "By employing caution towards things which are really bad, it results that we shall be confident" (020-019).
- The nature of good is in the use of appearances — this is the premise that resolves the paradox (020-010).
