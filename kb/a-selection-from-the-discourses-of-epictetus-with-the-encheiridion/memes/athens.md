# Athens

**Athens**, the cultural and philosophical center of the Greek world, appears in the Discourses less as a place to admire than as a test of the philosopher's attachments. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses longing for Athens and its Acropolis as a case of the homesickness a Stoic must master, and he recalls Athens as the city whose citizens put [[socrates|Socrates]] to death.

## Location

Athens lies in Attica, in eastern Greece, and was in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s time the ancient seat of Greek learning, crowned by the citadel of the Acropolis. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] alludes to it through that famous rock and through its citizens, the "Athenians," rather than through any geographic description of his own (031-077, 019-036).

## Significance in the Discourses

Athens serves [[epictetus|Epictetus]] chiefly as an example of misplaced [[desire|desire]] — the attachment to a particular place that turns to grief when one is kept from it. He mocks the exile who laments, "When then shall I see Athens again and the Acropolis?" and answers: "Wretch, are you not content with what you see daily? Have you anything better or greater to see than the sun, the moon, the stars, the whole earth, the sea?" (031-077, 031-078, 031-079). To pine for "small stones and a beautiful rock" while ignoring the order of the cosmos is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], to make a tragedy out of common things (031-081, 031-076).

The same lesson recurs in his teaching on [[exile|exile]]. One should live wholly in one's present circumstances: "if you be in [[rome|Rome]], do not imagine what the life in Athens is, but think only of the life in [[rome|Rome]]" (054-146, 054-147). Athens here is the imagined elsewhere whose pull the disciplined mind refuses.

Athens is also the city that condemned [[socrates|Socrates]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reframes the complaint that "[[socrates|Socrates]] should have been so treated by the Athenians," insisting that it was only "the poor body of [[socrates|Socrates]]" that was dragged to prison and given hemlock, not the man himself (019-036, 019-038, 019-040). Elsewhere the Athenians appear, beside the [[lacedaemon|Lacedaemon]]ians, as an example of nations that "quarrelled" through ignorance of how to apply their moral preconceptions (035-032).

## Historical Context

By the Roman imperial period Athens remained the prestige center of Greek philosophy and rhetoric, drawing students from across the empire even as political power lay with [[rome|Rome]]. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Greek-speaking audience it was the natural symbol of everything cultured and desirable — which is precisely why he chooses it as the object of longing that the philosopher must learn to relinquish.
