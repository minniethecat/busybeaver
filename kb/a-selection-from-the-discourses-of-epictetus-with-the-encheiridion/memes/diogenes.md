# Diogenes of Sinope

## Biography

Diogenes of Sinope (c. 412–323 BCE) was the most famous of the Cynic philosophers. He rejected conventional standards of wealth, comfort, and social [[moderation|propriety]], living in a storage jar in [[athens|Athens]] and reducing his possessions to the bare minimum. His [[modesty|shame]]lessness (*anaideia*) and commitment to living according to nature made him a legendary figure.

Diogenes's encounter with [[alexander-the-great|Alexander the Great]] is among the most famous anecdotes in the history of philosophy: when Alexander offered to grant Diogenes any request, the philosopher replied, "Stand out of my sunlight."

## Role in Epictetus

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] regards Diogenes as second only to [[socrates|Socrates]] among the [[example|exemplar]]s of the philosophical life. Where [[socrates|Socrates]] represents the perfection of reason and self-examination, Diogenes represents radical independence from externals — the ability to be happy with nothing that is not within one's power.

"Diogenes was free," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] tells his students. "How so? Not because he was born of free parents — he was not — but because he had cast off the things that make men slaves." Diogenes demonstrated that freedom is a matter of inner disposition, not external circumstances.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes considerable attention to Diogenes in the Discourses, using him to illustrate key Stoic themes: the indifference of externals, the sufficiency of virtue for happiness, and the invulnerability of the sage to fortune.
