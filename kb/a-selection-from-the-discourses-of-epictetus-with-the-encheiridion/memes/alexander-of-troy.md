# Alexander of Troy

*In the Discourses the name "Alexander" denotes two different men. This page treats the Trojan prince — Paris, son of Priam — whom [[epictetus|Epictetus]] always calls Alexander. For the Macedonian king to whom the Cynic Diogenes spoke, see [[alexander-the-great]].*

## Biography

Alexander is the Homeric name [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses for Paris, the Trojan prince whose carrying-off of Helen, the wife of [[menelaus]], began the Trojan War. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] never tells the story for its own sake; he draws the figure straight from Homer, treating the *Iliad* as a worked example rather than an epic to admire.

"The Iliad," he says, "is nothing else than appearance and the use of appearances" (018-035), and Alexander stands at the head of the chain of appearances that produced the war — "It appeared to Alexander to carry off the wife of [[menelaus|Menelaus]]" (018-036), just as "it appeared to Helene to follow him" (018-037).

The point [[epictetus|Epictetus]] extracts is that the whole catastrophe hung on a private opinion. Had it "appeared to [[menelaus|Menelaus]] to feel that it was a gain to be deprived of such a wife" (018-038), then "not only would the Iliad have been lost, but the Odyssey also" (018-039 to 018-040). Alexander's crime, in this [[books|reading]], was to treat a woman — an external — as a good worth seizing.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is equally precise about when Alexander was actually ruined. It was not, he insists, "when the Hellenes invaded and when they ravaged Troy, and when his brothers perished" (018-062 to 018-063) — "for no man is damaged by an action which is not his own" (018-064).

"The ruin of Alexander was when he lost the character of modesty, fidelity, regard to hospitality, and to [[moderation|decency]]" (018-066). The sack of the city destroyed only "stork's nests" (018-065); the man himself had perished earlier, in the act of wronging his host.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Alexander serves [[epictetus|Epictetus]] as a double [[example|exemplum]] of the doctrine that men are moved by their [[opinion|opinions]], not by things. In Discourse 18 he is the first mover in a sequence of judgments — his, Helen's, and [[menelaus|Menelaus]]'s — any one of which, differently made, would have unmade the war (018-036 to 018-038).

What looks like the greatest of heroic events reduces, on inspection, to people acting on [[appearances]] about what was worth having.

He returns in Discourse 35, on the fragility of friendship. "Alexander was the guest of [[menelaus|Menelaus]], and if any man had seen their friendly disposition, he would not have believed any one who said that they were not friends" (035-035 to 035-036).

That guest-friendship held only until "there was cast between them (as between dogs) a bit of meat, a handsome woman, and about her war arose" (035-038 to 035-039). For [[epictetus|Epictetus]] a bond resting on a shared [[externals|external]] is no bond at all: the moment the disputed thing appears, host and guest become enemies.

Alexander is thus a standing proof that [[friendship]] built on anything but agreement about the [[good]] cannot survive.

## References in the Discourses

- **The Iliad as a chain of appearances**: it "appeared to Alexander to carry off the wife of [[menelaus|Menelaus]]," and the war turned on that opinion and [[menelaus|Menelaus]]'s answering one (018-035, 018-036, 018-038).

- **Ruined by his own act, not by the war**: Alexander was destroyed not when Troy fell but "when he lost the character of modesty, fidelity, regard to hospitality, and to [[moderation|decency]]" (018-064, 018-066).

- **Guest-friendship dissolved by an external**: host and guest seemed unshakeable friends until "a handsome woman" was cast between them and "war arose" (035-035, 035-038).
