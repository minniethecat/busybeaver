# Obedience

**Obedience** in [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is not submission to human authority but the willing alignment of one's own will with the [[divinity|divine]] order. It is the practical expression of [[piety]] — the recognition that we are parts of a larger whole and that our good lies in conforming to the will of that whole rather than in pursuing our own isolated desires. "I must always obey the law in every thing" (054-138).

This is a paradoxical virtue: by obeying God and nature, the Stoic becomes free. Obedience to externals — to tyrants, to passions, to the opinions of others — is slavery. But obedience to the [[divinity|divine]] will, which is identical with reason and nature, is the path to genuine freedom. The person who obeys God obeys only what their own reason, properly exercised, would command.

## Definition

In Stoic theology, God (*[[zeus|Zeus]]*) is not an arbitrary commander but the rational principle that orders the universe. To obey God is therefore to obey reason itself. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] can say both "I must obey God" and "I must obey the law" without contradiction, because the [[divinity|divine]] will and the rational law of nature are the same thing.

The key distinction is between willing and unwilling obedience. "Conscious that you are obeying God, that not in word, but in deed you are showing your obedience" (054-149) — this is the Stoic ideal: obedience that is not resentful compliance but glad participation. The person who obeys unwillingly is a slave; the person who obeys willingly is free, because they have made God's will their own.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats obedience in two main contexts: the cosmic and the [[mask|persona]]l.

**Obedience to God**: In Discourse 54, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops the theme of obedience most fully. It comes in the context of the famous passage where he declares his willingness to accept whatever God sends — disease, poverty, death — because "I know what the Deity has given me" (054-140). The logic is: God has given me reason and the capacity to choose; if I use these gifts to recognise and accept God's will, I am living according to nature. To do otherwise would be to "fight against God" (054-039), and such disobedience brings its own punishment — internal discord, anxiety, and the loss of inner peace.

**Obedience to the master**: In Discourse 21, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the master–slave relationship as an analogy for the soul's relation to externals. "You must go up and down in obedience to the will of your master" (021-046). The master here is not a human owner but whatever external thing one has placed above character — pleasure, reputation, wealth. The person who desires these things must obey them. But the person who desires only virtue obeys only reason, and is therefore truly free.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also warns against the wrong kind of obedience — "obedience to your inclinations" (067-021). This is the opposite of Stoic obedience: following impulse rather than reason, serving the body rather than the ruling faculty. The distinction is crucial: not all obedience is virtuous; only obedience to what is rational and [[divinity|divine]] qualifies.

## Key Passages

> You must go up and down in obedience to the will of your master. (021-046)

> It is no small thing to be fighting against God and of this disobedience not only will the children of my children suffer, but I myself shall suffer now. (054-039)

> Give it me then, for I must always obey the law in every thing. (054-138)

> Conscious that you are obeying God, that not in word, but in deed you are showing your obedience to the will of the Deity. (054-149)
