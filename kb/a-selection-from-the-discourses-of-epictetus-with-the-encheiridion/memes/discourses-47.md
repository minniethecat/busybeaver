[047-001]We ought not to make our exercises consist in means contrary to

[047-002][[nature]] and adapted to cause admiration, for if we do so, we who call ourselves

[047-003]philosophers, shall not differ at all from jugglers. For it is difficult even

[047-004]to walk on a rope; and not only difficult, but it is also dangerous. Ought we

[047-005]for this [[reason]] to practice walking on a rope, or setting up a palm-tree, or

[047-006]embracing statues? By no means. Every thing which is difficult and dangerous is

[047-007]not suitable for practice; but that is suitable which conduces to the working

[047-008]out of that which is proposed to us. And what is that which is proposed to us

[047-009]as a thing to be worked out? To live with [[desire]] and aversion (avoidance of

[047-010]certain things) free from restraint. And what is this? Neither to be

[047-011]disappointed in that which you desire, nor to fall into anything which you

[047-012]would avoid. Towards this object then exercise (practice) ought to tend. For

[047-013]since it is not possible to have your desire not disappointed and your aversion

[047-014]free from falling into that which you would avoid, without great and constant

[047-015]practice, you must know that if you allow your desire and aversion to turn to

[047-016]things which are not within the power of the will, you will neither have your

[047-017]desire capable of attaining your object, nor your aversion free from the power

[047-018]of avoiding that which you would avoid. And since strong habit leads

[047-019](prevails), and we are accustomed to employ desire and aversion only to things

[047-020]which are not within the power of our will, we ought to oppose to this habit a

[047-021]contrary habit, and where there is great slipperiness in the appearances, there

[047-022]to oppose the habit of exercise. Then at last, if occasion presents itself, for

[047-023]the purpose of trying yourself at a proper time you will descend into the arena

[047-024]to know if appearances overpower you as they did formerly. But at first fly far

[047-025]from that which is stronger than yourself; the contest is unequal between a

[047-026]charming young girl and a beginner in philosophy. The earthen pitcher, as the

[047-027]saying is, and the rock do not agree.
