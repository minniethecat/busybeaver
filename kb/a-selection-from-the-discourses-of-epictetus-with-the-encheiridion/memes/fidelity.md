# Fidelity

**Fidelity** (*pistis*) is the virtue of being faithful — keeping one's word, honouring commitments, and maintaining the trust others have placed in one. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching, fidelity is not a social convention or a pragmatic calculation but a fundamental expression of human nature: to destroy fidelity is to destroy the person. It is one of a cluster of social virtues — alongside [[modesty]], [[constancy]], and [[justice]] — that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to repeatedly as the outward sign of inner moral health.

Fidelity appears in [[epictetus|Epictetus]] most often in the context of friendship and social community. Where there is fidelity and modesty, he says, there is friendship; where these are absent, the word "friend" is empty (035-062). The faithful person is one whose inner state is consistent with their outer actions — a coherence that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents as the natural condition of the rational being.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, fidelity belongs to the genus of justice — the virtue concerned with giving each person their due. To keep faith is to render to another what one has promised or implied. But fidelity in [[epictetus|Epictetus]] goes deeper than contractual [[duties|obligation]]. It is a quality of character (*prohairesis*) that expresses itself in every action: "the actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man, and the contrary actions destroy him" (025-025).

The Stoic foundation of fidelity is the recognition that we are social beings by nature. To be unfaithful is to act against nature — to sever the bond that connects us to other rational beings. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks: "When have you not done harm to yourself? When has the loss of fidelity, modesty, or intelligence not been immediate?" (018-059).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats fidelity as one of the essential qualities of character (*prohairesis*), and he deploys it in three distinct contexts.

**As a [[mask|persona]]l virtue**: Fidelity is what preserves the person. In Discourse 25, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] states the general principle: just as "modest actions preserve the modest man," so "actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man, and the contrary actions destroy him" (025-025). This is not a metaphor — the loss of fidelity is the destruction of the trustworthy person, leaving only the external shell.

**As the foundation of friendship**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues that friendship is impossible without fidelity. "For where else is friendship than where there is fidelity, and modesty, where there is a giving up of things which are good" (035-062). A friend who cannot be trusted is not a friend at all — the word has been emptied of meaning.

**As a mark of the philosopher**: In Discourse 68, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the philosopher as one who has earned a "pledge of fidelity" through consistent behaviour (068-015). The faithful and modest person is contrasted with "a man who thinks that his own actions [[solitude|alone]] are good or bad, and the actions of others nothing" (068-038) — again linking fidelity to the core Stoic distinction between what is up to us and what is not.

Fidelity frequently appears paired with [[modesty]] (*[[modesty|aidōs]]*) as a pair of complementary social virtues. In Discourse 3, they are listed together as things that "have no mean or ignoble character" (003-012). In Discourse 59, fidelity, modesty, and [[firmness|constancy]] are described as "no small matter" — things worth preserving at any cost (059-018).

## Key Passages

> Fidelity and modesty and a sure use of appearances have no mean or ignoble character. (003-012)

> The actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man, and the contrary actions destroy him. (025-025)

> When was Alexander worst ruined? When he lost the character of modesty, fidelity, regard to friends. (018-066)

> For where else is friendship than where there is fidelity, and modesty, where there is a giving up of things which are good? (035-062)

> To preserve is no small matter, but it is modesty and fidelity and constancy, and a mind free from perturbation. (059-018)
