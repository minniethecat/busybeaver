[054-001]Let not that which in another is contrary to [[nature]] be an evil to you;

[054-002]for you are not formed by nature to be depressed with others nor to be unhappy

[054-003]with others, but to be happy with them. If a man is unhappy, remember that his

[054-004]unhappiness is his own fault; for God has made all men to be happy, to be free

[054-005]from perturbations. For this purpose he has given means to them, some things to

[054-006]each person as his own, and other things not as his own; some things subject to

[054-007]hindrance and compulsion and deprivation; and these things are not a man’s own;

[054-008]but the things which are not subject to hindrances, are his own; and the nature

[054-009]of [[good]] and evil, as it was fit to be done by him who takes care of us and

[054-010]protects us like a father, he has made our own. But you say, I have parted from

[054-011]a certain person, and he is grieved. Why did he consider as his own that which

[054-012]belongs to another? why, when he looked on you and was rejoiced, did he not

[054-013]also reckon that you are a mortal, that it is natural for you to part from him

[054-014]for a foreign country? Therefore he suffers the consequences of his own folly.

[054-015]But why do you or for what purpose bewail yourself? Is it that you also have

[054-016]not thought of these things? but like poor women who are good for nothing, you

[054-017]have enjoyed all things in which you took pleasure, as if you would always

[054-018]enjoy them, both places and men and conversation; and now you sit and weep

[054-019]because you do not see the same persons and do not live in the same places.

[054-020]Indeed you deserve this, to be more wretched than crows and ravens who have the

[054-021]power of flying where they please and changing their nests for others, and

[054-022]crossing the seas without lamenting or regretting their former condition. Yes,

[054-023]but this happens to them because they are irrational creatures. Was [[reason]] then

[054-024]given to us by the gods for the purpose of unhappiness and misery, that we may

[054-025]pass our lives in wretchedness and lamentation? Must all persons be immortal

[054-026]and must no man go abroad, and must we ourselves not go abroad, but remain

[054-027]rooted like plants; and if any of our familiar friends goes abroad, must we sit

[054-028]and weep; and on the contrary, when he returns, must we dance and clap our

[054-029]hands like children?

[054-030]But my mother laments when she does not see me. Why has she not learned these

[054-031]principles? and I do not say this, that we should not take care that she may

[054-032]not lament, but I say that we ought not to [[desire]] in every way what is not our

[054-033]own. And the sorrow of another is another’s sorrow; but my sorrow is my own. I

[054-034]then will stop my own sorrow by every means, for it is in my power; and the

[054-035]sorrow of another I will endeavor to stop as far as I can; but I will not

[054-036]attempt to do it by every means; for if I do, I shall be fighting against God,

[054-037]I shall be opposing [[zeus|Zeus]] and shall be placing myself against him in the

[054-038]administration of the universe; and the reward (the punishment) of this

[054-039]fighting against God and of this disobedience not only will the children of my

[054-040]children pay, but I also shall myself, both by day and by night, startled by

[054-041]dreams, perturbed, trembling at every piece of news, and having my tranquillity

[054-042]depending on the letters of others. Some person has arrived from [[rome|Rome]]. I only

[054-043]hope there is no harm. But what harm can happen to you, where you are not? From

[054-044]Hellas (Greece) some one is come; I hope that there is no harm. In this way

[054-045]every place may be the cause of misfortune to you. Is it not enough for you to

[054-046]be unfortunate there where you are, and must you be so even beyond sea, and by

[054-047]the report of letters? Is this the way in which your affairs are in a state of

[054-048]security? Well then suppose that my friends have died in the places which are

[054-049]far from me. What else have they suffered than that which is the condition of

[054-050]mortals? Or how are you desirous at the same time to live to old age, and at

[054-051]the same time not to see the death of any person whom you love? Know you not

[054-052]that in the course of a long time many and various kinds of things must happen;

[054-053]that a fever shall overpower one, a robber another, and a third a tyrant? Such

[054-054]is the condition of things around us, such are those who live with us in the

[054-055]world; cold and heat, and unsuitable ways of living, and journeys by land, and

[054-056]voyages by sea, and winds, and various circumstances which surround us, destroy

[054-057]one man, and banish another, and throw one upon an embassy and another into an

[054-058]army. Sit down then in a flutter at all these things, lamenting, unhappy,

[054-059]unfortunate, dependent on another, and dependent not on one or two, but on ten

[054-060]thousands upon ten thousands.

[054-061]Did you hear this when you were with the philosophers? did you learn this? do

[054-062]you not know that human life is a warfare? that one man must keep watch,

[054-063]another must go out as a spy, and a third must fight? and it is not possible

[054-064]that all should be in one place, nor is it better that it should be so. But you

[054-065]neglecting to do the commands of the general complain when anything more hard

[054-066]than usual is imposed on you, and you do not observe what you make the army

[054-067]become as far as it is in your power; that if all imitate you, no man will dig

[054-068]a trench, no man will put a rampart round, nor keep watch, nor expose himself

[054-069]to danger, but will appear to be useless for the purposes of an army. Again, in

[054-070]a vessel if you go as a sailor, keep to one place and stick to it. And if you

[054-071]are ordered to climb the mast, refuse; if to run to the head of the ship,

[054-072]refuse; and what master of a ship will endure you? and will he not pitch you

[054-073]overboard as a useless thing, an impediment only and bad example to the other

[054-074]sailors? And so it is here also: every man’s life is a kind of warfare, and it

[054-075]is long and diversified. You must observe the duty of a soldier and do every

[054-076]thing at the nod of the general; if it is possible, divining what his wishes

[054-077]are; for there is no resemblance between that general and this, neither in

[054-078]strength nor in superiority of character. Know you not that a good man does

[054-079]nothing for the sake of appearance, but for the sake of doing right? What

[054-080]advantage is it then to him to have done right? And what advantage is it to a

[054-081]man who writes the name of Dion to write it as he ought? The advantage is to

[054-082]have written it. Is there no reward then? Do you seek a reward for a good man

[054-083]greater than doing what is good and just? At [[olympia|Olympia]] you wish for nothing more,

[054-084]but it seems to you enough to be crowned at the games. Does it seem to you so

[054-085]small and worthless a thing to be good and happy? For these purposes being

[054-086]introduced by the gods into this city (the world), and it being now your duty

[054-087]to undertake the work of a man, do you still want nurses also and a mamma, and

[054-088]do foolish women by their weeping move you and make you effeminate? Will you

[054-089]thus never cease to be a foolish child? know you not that he who does the acts

[054-090]of a child, the older he is, the more ridiculous he is?

[054-091]So in this matter also: if you kiss your own child, or your brother or friend,

[054-092]never give full license to the appearance ([Greek: phantasian]), and allow not

[054-093]your pleasure to go as far as it chooses; but check it, and curb it as those

[054-094]who stand behind men in their triumphs and remind them that they are mortal. Do

[054-095]you also remind yourself in like manner, that he whom you love is mortal, and

[054-096]that what you love is nothing of your own; it has been given to you for the

[054-097]present, not that it should not be taken from you, nor has it been given to you

[054-098]for all time, but as a fig is given to you or a bunch of grapes at the

[054-099]appointed season of the year. But if you wish for these things in winter, you

[054-100]are a fool. So if you wish for your son or friend when it is not allowed to

[054-101]you, you must know that you are wishing for a fig in winter. For such as winter

[054-102]is to a fig, such is every event which happens from the universe to the things

[054-103]which are taken away according to its nature. And further, at the times when

[054-104]you are delighted with a thing, place before yourself the contrary appearances.

[054-105]What harm is it while you are kissing your child to say with a lisping voice:

[054-106]To-morrow you will die; and to a friend also: To-morrow you will go away or I

[054-107]shall, and never shall we see one another again? But these are words of bad

[054-108]omen—and some incantations also are of bad omen; but because they are useful, I

[054-109]don’t care for this; only let them be useful. But do you call things to be of

[054-110]bad omen except those which are significant of some evil? Cowardice is a word

[054-111]of bad omen, and meanness of spirit, and sorrow, and grief, and shamelessness.

[054-112]These words are of bad omen; and yet we ought not to hesitate to utter them in

[054-113]order to protect ourselves against the things. Do you tell me that a name which

[054-114]is significant of any natural thing is of evil omen? say that even for the ears

[054-115]of corn to be reaped is of bad omen, for it signifies the destruction of the

[054-116]ears, but not of the world. Say that the falling of the leaves also is of bad

[054-117]omen, and for the dried fig to take the place of the green fig, and for raisins

[054-118]to be made from the grapes. For all these things are changes from a former

[054-119]state into other states; not a destruction, but a certain fixed economy and

[054-120]administration. Such is going away from home and a small change: such is death,

[054-121]a greater change, not from the state which now is to that which is not, but to

[054-122]that which is not now. Shall I then no longer exist? You will not exist, but

[054-123]you will be something else, of which the world now has need; for you also came

[054-124]into existence not when you chose, but when the world had need of you.

[054-125]Let these thoughts be ready to hand by night and by day; these you should

[054-126]write, these you should read; about these you should talk to yourself and to

[054-127]others. Ask a man: Can you help me at all for this purpose? and further, go to

[054-128]another and to another. Then if anything that is said be contrary to your wish,

[054-129]this reflection first will immediately relieve you, that it is not unexpected.

[054-130]For it is a great thing in all cases to say: I knew that I begot a son who is

[054-131]mortal. For so you also will say: I knew that I am mortal, I knew that I may

[054-132]leave my home, I knew that I may be ejected from it, I knew that I may be led

[054-133]to prison. Then if you turn round and look to yourself, and seek the place from

[054-134]which comes that which has happened, you will forthwith recollect that it comes

[054-135]from the place of things which are out of the power of the will, and of things

[054-136]which are not my own. What then is it to me? Then, you will ask, and this is

[054-137]the chief thing: And who is it that sent it? The leader, or the general, the

[054-138]state, the law of the state. Give it me then, for I must always obey the law in

[054-139]everything. Then, when the appearance (of things) pains you, for it is not in

[054-140]your power to prevent this, contend against it by the aid of reason, conquer

[054-141]it: do not allow it to gain strength nor to lead you to the consequences by

[054-142]raising images such as it pleases and as it pleases. If you be in Gyara, do not

[054-143]imagine the mode of living at Rome, and how many pleasures there were for him

[054-144]who lived there and how many there would be for him who returned to Rome; but

[054-145]fix your mind on this matter, how a man who lives in Gyara ought to live in

[054-146]Gyara like a man of courage. And if you be in Rome, do not imagine what the

[054-147]life in Athens is, but think only of the life in Rome.

[054-148]Then in the place of all other delights substitute this, that of being

[054-149]conscious that you are obeying God, that not in word, but in deed you are

[054-150]performing the acts of a wise and good man. For what a thing it is for a man to

[054-151]be able to say to himself: Now whatever the rest may say in solemn manner in

[054-152]the schools and may be judged to be saying in a way contrary to common opinion

[054-153](or in a strange way), this I am doing; and they are sitting and are

[054-154]discoursing of my virtues and inquiring about me and praising me; and of this

[054-155]Zeus has willed that I shall receive from myself a demonstration, and shall

[054-156]myself know if he has a soldier such as he ought to have, a citizen such as he

[054-157]ought to have, and if he has chosen to produce me to the rest of mankind as a

[054-158]witness of the things which are independent of the will: See that you fear

[054-159]without reason, that you foolishly desire what you do desire; seek not the good

[054-160]in things external; seek it in yourselves: if you do not, you will not find it.

[054-161]For this purpose he leads me at one time hither, at another time sends me

[054-162]thither, shows me to men as poor, without authority, and sick; sends me to

[054-163]Gyara, leads me into prison, not because he hates me—far from him be such a

[054-164]meaning, for who hates the best of his servants? nor yet because he cares not

[054-165]for me, for he does not neglect any even of the smallest things; but he does

[054-166]this for the purpose of exercising me and making use of me as a witness to

[054-167]others. Being appointed to such a service, do I still care about the place in

[054-168]which I am, or with whom I am, or what men say about me? and do I not entirely

[054-169]direct my thoughts to God and to his instructions and commands?

[054-170]Having these things (or thoughts) always in hand, and exercising them by

[054-171]yourself, and keeping them in readiness, you will never be in want of one to

[054-172]comfort you and strengthen you. For it is not shameful to be without something

[054-173]to eat, but not to have reason sufficient for keeping away fear and sorrow. But

[054-174]if once you have gained exemption from sorrow and fear, will there any longer

[054-175]be a tyrant for you, or a tyrant’s guard, or attendants on Cæsar? Or shall any

[054-176]appointment to offices at court cause you pain, or shall those who sacrifice in

[054-177]the Capitol on the occasion of being named to certain functions, cause pain to

[054-178]you who have received so great authority from Zeus? Only do not make a proud

[054-179]display of it, nor boast of it; but show it by your acts; and if no man

[054-180]perceives it, be satisfied that you are yourself in a healthy state and happy.
