# Duties

**Duties** (*kathēkonta*, "appropriate acts") are the actions that befit a rational being living according to [[nature]]. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]] and the Stoics, duty is not an external imposition but a function of our natural relations — to our parents, siblings, neighbors, fellow citizens, and ultimately to all humanity. "Duties are universally measured by relations" (098-001).

The Stoic doctrine of duty grounds ethics not in arbitrary commands but in the rational structure of human relationships. To understand what you ought to do, examine who you are and how you stand in relation to others.

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, a *kathēkon* (plural *kathēkonta*) is an action that can be rationally justified — an act for which a reasonable defence can be given. The term was introduced by [[zeno|Zeno of Citium]], the founder of [[stoicism|Stoicism]], who derived it from the Greek phrase *kata tinas hēkein* ("to come down upon certain persons"), suggesting that duty descends upon us from our place in the natural order.

Duties are distinguished from virtuous actions (*katorthōmata*) in orthodox [[stoicism|Stoicism]]: a duty is what a rational person would ordinarily do, while a perfectly virtuous action is done from the right disposition with complete understanding. [[epictetus|Epictetus]], however, focuses on the practical dimension: duties are discovered by contemplating our relations (*scheseis*).

The relational framework means that no one occupies a single role. A man may simultaneously be a father, a son, a brother, a citizen, and a member of the human community. Each relation generates distinct duties, and part of [[virtue]] is learning to navigate between them without conflict.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents duty as the concrete expression of living according to nature. The man who asks "What is my duty?" need not consult oracles or [[divinity|divine]]rs, for "have I not within me a [[divinity|divine]]r who has told me the nature of good and of evil?" (024-006). The knowledge of duty is accessible to reason without supernatural aid.

His treatment is characteristically practical. "Is a man a father? The precept is to take care of him, to yield to him in all things, to submit when he is reproachful, when he inflicts blows" (098-002). The duty holds even when the father is bad: "Were you then by nature made akin to a good father? No; but to a father" (098-004).

This reveals a crucial Stoic distinction: duties are determined by the relation, not by the other person's character. A brother who wrongs you does not dissolve the relation of brotherhood, and therefore does not abolish the duties that arise from it. "Maintain then your own position towards him, and do not examine what he is doing, but what you must do that your will shall be conformable to nature" (098-005).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also emphasizes that duty sometimes requires sacrifice. "If then I must expose myself to danger for a friend, and if it is my duty even to die for him, what need have I then for divination?" (024-004). The Stoic conception of duty encompasses the willingness to give up externals — even life — for the sake of acting rightly.

## Key Passages

- "Duties are universally measured by relations" — the foundational principle (098-001).
- A father's duty holds regardless of whether the father is good or bad (098-004).
- "Maintain your own position towards him" — duty depends on your role, not the other's merit (098-005).
- Duty to a friend may require exposing oneself to danger, even death (024-004).
- Duty, like truth, compels rational assent — "Can a man think that a thing is useful to him and not choose it?" (018-014).
