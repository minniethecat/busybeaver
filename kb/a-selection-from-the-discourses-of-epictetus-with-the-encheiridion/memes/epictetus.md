# Epictetus

Epictetus is the most important practical ethicist of the Stoic tradition. His teachings center on the radical distinction between what is within our power (*eph' hēmin*) and what is not — a doctrine that opens both the *Discourses* and the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]*.

For Epictetus, the goal of philosophy is not theoretical knowledge but the transformation of [[desire]], action, and judgment. His concept of [[prohairesis]] — the rational faculty of choice — is his most distinctive contribution to Stoic thought and the foundation of his teaching on [[freedom]].

## Biography

Epictetus was born around 50 CE in Hierapolis, Phrygia (modern-day Pamukkale, Turkey). "It is said that he was a native of Hierapolis, in Phrygia. The date of his birth is unknown. The only recorded fact of his early life is that he was a slave" (P02-001, P02-006). He served Epaphroditus, a freedman of the emperor [[nero|Nero]], in [[rome|Rome]].

While still enslaved, Epictetus studied philosophy under [[Musonius Rufus]], one of the leading Stoic teachers of the era. This was a formative period. [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]] was known for his practical, ethically focused approach to [[stoicism|Stoicism]] — an emphasis that would shape Epictetus's own distinctive teaching style.

The biographical note records that Epictetus "was weak in body and lame from an early age" (P02-011). One tradition holds that his lameness resulted from mistreatment during his enslavement; another suggests it was from illness. Either way, this physical vulnerability became part of his philosophical [[mask|persona]] — his students saw a man whose body was constrained but whose mind was entirely free.

Epictetus eventually gained his freedom and taught philosophy in [[rome|Rome]] until approximately 93 CE. That year, the emperor Domitian banished all philosophers from the city. "Epictetus opened a school or lecture room at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]" (P02-038), a city in northwestern Greece near the site of the Battle of Actium.

At [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]], Epictetus established what became the most influential Stoic school of his generation. Students traveled from across the empire to study with him. Among them was [[Arrian]] (Lucius Flavius Arrianus), a young Roman who would later become a distinguished historian and imperial administrator.

Epictetus "was never married, as we learn from Lucian" (P02-040), though late in life he is said to have adopted a child who would otherwise have been exposed. He lived simply and died around 135 CE, leaving no written works of his own. Everything we have comes through Arrian's transcriptions.

## Philosophical Significance

Epictetus represents the mature, practical development of late Stoic thought. Unlike earlier Stoics who wrote extensively on logic and physics, Epictetus's surviving works concentrate almost exclusively on ethics — on the question of how to live.

His most distinctive contributions include three interrelated doctrines. First, the absolute centrality of **[[prohairesis]]** (moral purpose, the faculty of choice) as the seat of human identity, freedom, and responsibility. "My prohairesis not even [[zeus|Zeus]] himself can overpower" (001-027) — this claim is the cornerstone of Epictetan philosophy.

Second, the doctrine of the **[[discipline|three disciplines]]** — desire, action, and assent — as the organizing framework for Stoic [[exercise|practice]]. Each discipline addresses a different domain of the moral life: our relationship to the [[divinity|gods]] (desire), to other people (action), and to our own judgments (assent).

Third, the distinction between what is within our power and what is not, which opens both the *Discourses* and the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]*. This is not merely a theoretical classification but a practical tool for achieving tranquility: if you only desire what is within your power, you can never be frustrated.

Epictetus exerted profound influence on later philosophy. The Roman emperor [[Marcus Aurelius]] repeatedly cites or echoes Epictetus in his *Meditations*. In the modern era, Epictetan themes have shaped cognitive-behavioral therapy, resilience psychology, and leadership philosophy. His influence extends well beyond academic philosophy into practical domains of human welfare.

## Key Ideas

- **The Dichotomy of Control**: "Of things that exist, some are in our power, and some are not in our power" ([[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] §1).

- **[[prohairesis|Prohairesis]]**: The moral purpose or faculty of choice that constitutes the true self. Even [[zeus|Zeus]] cannot overpower it (001-027).

- **The [[discipline|Three Disciplines]]**: [[desire|Desire]] (*[[desire|orexis]]*), action (*hormē*), and assent (*synkatathesis*) as the framework for Stoic [[exercise|practice]].

- **Inner [[freedom|Freedom]]**: "[[zeus|Zeus]] has set me free; do you think that he intended his own son to be enslaved?" (012-028). True freedom is independence from externals through [[power|mastery]] of desire.

- **[[philosophy|Philosophy]] as [[exercise|practice]]**: Epictetus constantly insists that philosophy is not about clever speech but about transforming one's character and desires.

## References in the Discourses

Epictetus appears throughout the biographical note (P02-001–P02-071), which records the known facts of his life. In the body of the *Discourses*, he is the speaker of every discourse. His students address him with [[piety|reverence]] — "Frequently I desired to hear you" (037-001) — and he responds with the sharp, vivid moral exhortation that defines his teaching style.

- Biographical note: P02-001, P02-006, P02-011, P02-019, P02-031, P02-038, P02-040
- Epictetus on his own role: "a philosopher's duty" to serve others (P02-043)
- His school at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]: students came to hear him discourse on freedom, desire, and the good life
