## Definition

Change (Greek: *metabolē*) in Stoic philosophy refers to the continuous transformation of all things according to the rational principle (*logos*) that governs the cosmos. In Stoic physics, change is not random but providentially ordered: all matter is permeated by *pneuma* (creative fire), which directs the eternal cycle of generation and dissolution. Because everything external to the ruling faculty is subject to change, the Stoic ethic demands that the wise person cultivate an inner [[firmness|constancy]] that is independent of shifting circumstances. The sage's mind, unlike the external world, is "unchangeable" (*ametabolos*) in its commitment to virtue.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws a sharp line between two domains: the external world, which is inherently mutable, and the faculty of choice (*prohairesis*), which [[solitude|alone]] can achieve [[firmness|stability]]. He observes that a person who stakes their happiness on externals must, "of necessity, change with them and be tossed about as the waves of circumstance demand" (004-050). The image of being tossed by waves captures the psychological in[[firmness|stability]] that results from investing one's well-being in what is inherently unstable.

The recognition that one cannot [[power|control]] external circumstances is central to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s therapy. He acknowledges frank human limitation: "When I am neither able to change circumstances nor to tear out the eyes of a man who obstructs me, I sit and groan" (017-034). The appropriate response is not to futilely resist change but to adjust one's own judgments: the task is to "change externals according to my wish" (017-031) — a wish properly limited to what is within one's power.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses the question of changing one's mind — a topic of considerable ethical weight in a philosophical school that prizes [[firmness|constancy]]. He allows that changing one's resolution can be rational: "Change your mind, if you think fit, and do not persist in your first opinion" (030-021). The Stoic is not stubborn but responsive to reason. What matters is not fixedness of opinion but firmness of character. The wise person "will not change his mind" (035-077) on matters of principle because his principles are correct, but he is open to revision on matters where new evidence or reasoning warrants it.

The goal, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is "unchangeable firmness of mind" (040-032) — not rigidity of opinion but [[firmness|constancy]] of moral purpose. This is contrasted with the feverish person, who changes color and trembles (028-046), illustrating how bodily changes can signal — but not cause — psychological disturbance. The philosophical life aims at a [[firmness|stability]] that is immune to the flux of circumstances.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that one who stakes happiness on externals "must change with them and be tossed about" (004-050), capturing the in[[firmness|stability]] of misplaced attachment.
- He poses the frank question of agency: "am I able to change circumstances?" (017-031 through 017-034), answering that the only legitimate object of change is one's own judgment.
- The [[disputation|debate]] over [[firmness|constancy]] versus stubbornness: "Change your mind, if you think fit, and do not persist" (030-021), distinguishing rational flexibility from fickleness.
- Some are "with difficulty persuaded to change his mind" (030-032), while others adopt determinations "and not to change it" (030-044) — the value of change depends on whether it serves reason or passion.
- The person in a fever changes color and trembles (028-046) — a physical image of the psychological in[[firmness|stability]] that philosophy aims to overcome.
- The ideal: "unchangeable firmness of mind" (040-032) as the Stoic goal, contrasted with everything external that is in constant flux.
- The wise person "will not change his mind" (035-077) on matters of principle, because correct principles are stable.
