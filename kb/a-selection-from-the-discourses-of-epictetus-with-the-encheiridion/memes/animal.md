# Animal

**Animal** in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is not a topic of zoology but a central concept in Stoic anthropology — the category against which human nature is defined and elevated. Man is described repeatedly as "the rational animal" (*zōon logikon*), a being who shares body and instinct with irrational animals but possesses reason (*logos*) and intelligence in common with the [[divinity|gods]]. This dual kinship is the foundation of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire ethical project.

"If we observe, we shall find that the animal man is pained by nothing so much as by that which is irrational; and, on the contrary, attracted to nothing so much as to that which is rational" (002-007). The distinctive nature of the rational animal is the key to understanding both human excellence and human misery.

## Definition

In Stoic physics and anthropology, animals (*zōa*) are living beings endowed with soul (*psychē*) and the faculty of impression (*phantasia*). Irrational animals use appearances for survival — eating, drinking, reproduction — but do not understand them. The rational animal [[solitude|alone]] possesses the capacity to examine, interpret, and assent to or dissent from appearances. This distinction is the basis of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s repeated insistence that man must not "begin and end where irrational animals do" (006-033).

The concept has an explicitly theological dimension: God "had need of irrational animals to make use of appearances, but of us to understand the use of appearances" (006-017). Man is introduced into the world as "a spectator of God and of his works; and not only a spectator of them, but an interpreter" (006-024).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the concept of animal in three interconnected ways throughout the Discourses.

**The rational animal as the defining anthropology**: In Discourse 2, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] establishes that the rational animal is "pained by nothing so much as by that which is irrational" (002-007). This is not a peripheral observation but the very foundation of his ethics — what harms a human being is not pain, exile, or death, but acting against reason. The animal nature of man means he is subject to bodily needs and passions, but the *rational* nature means he can transcend them through correct judgment.

**The dual kinship**: In Discourse 3, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents the most concentrated statement of his anthropology: "since these two things are mingled in the generation of man, body in common with the animals, and reason and intelligence in common with the [[divinity|gods]], many incline to this kinship, which is miserable and mortal; and some few to that which is [[divinity|divine]] and happy" (003-007). The moral life is a choice of which kinship to cultivate — to live as a beast (wolf, lion, fox) or to live as befits a child of [[zeus|Zeus]].

**Men who become animals**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the animal metaphor to describe moral degradation. Those who incline to the flesh "become like wolves, faithless and treacherous and mischievous; some become like lions, savage and bestial and untamed; but the greater part of us become foxes, and other worse animals" (003-020). A slanderer is "a fox, or some other more wretched and meaner animal" (003-021). This is not poetic ornament but a precise philosophical claim: when reason is subordinated to impulse, the human being literally becomes animal-like, forfeiting the unique dignity of the rational creature.

**[[providence|Providence]] and the ends of animals**: Discourse 6 provides the most systematic treatment. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] explains that God "constitutes every animal, one to be eaten, another to serve for agriculture, another to supply cheese, and another for some like use; for which purposes what need is there to understand appearances?" (006-028). But man's constitution is different — he possesses the intellectual faculty, and "unless there be the due exercise of the understanding, he will never attain his proper end" (006-026). The animal serves; man contemplates and interprets.

## Key Passages

> Since these two things are mingled in the generation of man, body in common with the animals, and reason and intelligence in common with the gods, many incline to this kinship, which is miserable and mortal; and some few to that which is divine and happy. (003-007)

> God had need of irrational animals to make use of appearances, but of us to understand the use of appearances. (006-017)

> God has introduced man to be a spectator of God and of his works; and not only a spectator of them, but an interpreter. For this reason it is shameful for man to begin and to end where irrational animals do. (006-024, 006-033)

> What else is a slanderer and malignant man than a fox, or some other more wretched and meaner animal? See then and take care that you do not become some one of these miserable things. (003-021)
