[043-001]When the

[043-002]administrator came to visit him, and the man was an Epicurean, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] said,

[043-003]It is proper for us who are not philosophers to inquire of you who are

[043-004]philosophers, as those who come to a strange city inquire of the citizens and

[043-005]those who are acquainted with it, what is the best thing in the world, in order

[043-006]that we also after inquiry may go in quest of that which is best and look at

[043-007]it, as strangers do with the things in cities. For that there are three things

[043-008]which relate to man—soul, body, and things external, scarcely any man denies.

[043-009]It remains for you philosophers to answer what is the best. What shall we say

[043-010]to men? Is the flesh the best? and was it for this that Maximus sailed as far

[043-011]as Cassiope in winter (or bad weather) with his son, and accompanied him that

[043-012]he might be gratified in the flesh? When the man said that it was not, and

[043-013]added, Far be that from him. Is it not fit then, Epictetus said, to be actively

[043-014]employed about the best? It is certainly of all things the most fit. What then

[043-015]do we possess which is better than the flesh? The soul, he replied. And the

[043-016][[good]] things of the best, are they better, or the good things of the worse? The

[043-017]good things of the best. And are the good things of the best within the power

[043-018]of the will or not within the power of the will? They are within the power of

[043-019]the will. Is then the pleasure of the soul a thing within the power of the

[043-020]will? It is, he replied. And on what shall this pleasure depend? On itself? But

[043-021]that cannot be conceived; for there must first exist a certain substance or

[043-022][[nature]] ([Greek: ousia]) of good, by obtaining which we shall have pleasure in

[043-023]the soul. He assented to this also. On what then shall we depend for this

[043-024]pleasure of the soul? for if it shall depend on things of the soul, the

[043-025]substance (nature) of the good is discovered; for good cannot be one thing, and

[043-026]that at which we are rationally delighted another thing; nor if that which

[043-027]precedes is not good, can that which comes after be good, for in order that the

[043-028]thing which comes after may be good, that which precedes must be good. But you

[043-029]would not affirm this, if you are in your right mind, for you would then say

[043-030]what is inconsistent both with Epicurus and the rest of your doctrines. It

[043-031]remains then that the pleasure of the soul is in the pleasure from things of

[043-032]the body; and again that those bodily things must be the things which precede

[043-033]and the substance (nature) of the good.

[043-034]Seek for doctrines which are consistent with what I say, and by making them

[043-035]your guide you will with pleasure abstain from things which have such

[043-036]persuasive power to lead us and overpower us. But if to the persuasive power of

[043-037]these things, we also devise such a philosophy as this which helps to push us

[043-038]on towards them and strengthens us to this end, what will be the consequence?

[043-039]In a piece of toreutic art which is the best part? the silver or the

[043-040]workmanship? The substance of the hand is the flesh; but the work of the hand

[043-041]is the principal part (that which precedes and leads the rest). The duties then

[043-042]are also three: those which are directed towards the existence of a thing;

[043-043]those which are directed towards its existence in a particular kind; and third,

[043-044]the chief or leading things themselves. So also in man we ought not to value

[043-045]the material, the poor flesh, but the principal (leading things, [Greek: ta

[043-046]proaegoumena]). What are these? Engaging in public business, marrying,

[043-047]begetting children, venerating God, taking care of parents, and generally,

[043-048]having desires, aversions ([Greek: echchlinein]), pursuits of things and

[043-049]avoidances, in the way in which we ought to do these things, and according to

[043-050]our nature. And how are we constituted by nature? Free, noble, modest; for what

[043-051]other animal blushes? what other is capable of receiving the appearance (the

[043-052][[impression]]) of shame? and we are so constituted by nature as to subject

[043-053]pleasure to these things, as a minister, a servant, in order that it may call

[043-054]forth our activity, in order that it may keep us constant in acts which are

[043-055]conformable to nature.
