# Wrestler

The **wrestler** (*palaistēs*) is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most developed athletic metaphor for the Stoic trainee. Unlike the gladiator who fights with weapons or the runner who merely races, the wrestler engages in close bodily contest that demands strength, skill, and the ability to respond to an opponent's every move. This makes wrestling the perfect analogue for moral [[education|training]], where the opponent is not another person but the [[appearances|impressions]] (*[[appearances|phantasiai]]*) that must be grappled with and thrown.

The metaphor has deep roots in Greek culture, where wrestling (*palē*) was the foundational combat sport and the wrestling school (*palaistra*) was a center of both physical and philosophical education.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] deploys the wrestler metaphor most powerfully in Discourse 15, where he describes God as a trainer of wrestlers who has matched each person with a "rough and strong" antagonist — not a soft Persian king's son, but a true opponent worthy of Olympic [[education|training]] (015-003). The difficulty of life is not evidence of [[divinity|divine]] neglect but of [[divinity|divine]] pedagogy. A good trainer does not give his athlete easy opponents.

In Discourse 39, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] sharpens the point about proportionality. "Does that which makes a pancratiast beautiful make a wrestler to be not good, and a runner to be most ridiculous?" (039-016). Each athlete is judged by the standard of his own discipline. Wrestling requires one set of qualities, running another. So too in philosophy: one person's contest is with anger, another's with grief, another's with the fear of poverty. Each must be judged by the relevant standard, not by the standards of a different contest.

The most concentrated treatment comes in Discourse 50, where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] advises the aspiring philosopher to assess his natural capacity honestly. "If you are a wrestler, look at your shoulders, your thighs, your loins. For one man is naturally fitted for one thing, another for another" (050-025). Not everyone is built for every contest. Self-knowledge — knowing what kind of athlete one is — precedes effective [[education|training]].

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also uses the wrestler to distinguish genuine philosophy from mere role-play. [[child|Child]]ren "at one time play at wrestlers, another at gladiators, then as trumpeters, then as tragedians" (097-015). The adult Stoic does not play at wrestling; he wrestles in earnest with his own character.

## Key Passages

- God as trainer: "God, like a trainer of wrestlers, has matched you with a rough and strong antagonist" (015-003).
- Proportional judgment: "Does that which makes a pancratiast beautiful make a wrestler to be not good?" (039-016).
- Self-assessment: "If you are a wrestler, look at your shoulders, your thighs, your loins" (050-025).
- Earnest combat: "The combat before us is not in wrestling and the pancration" (055-006), distinguishing physical from moral contest.
- [[child|Child]]'s play: "[[child|Child]]ren, who at one time play at wrestlers, another at gladiators" (097-015).
- Know your capacity: "Do you wish to be a pentathlete or a wrestler? Look at your arms, your thighs" (097-026).
