# Moderation

**Moderation** — in Greek, *to metrion* (due measure) or the quality expressed by *sōphrosynē* in its outward aspect — is the Stoic virtue of self-restraint in conduct, speech, and manner. Where [[temperance]] is the inner power to resist desire, moderation is its visible expression: the person who does nothing in excess, who maintains propriety and gentleness in all interactions, whose outward life reflects an inwardly ordered soul.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], moderation is distinctive among the virtues in one respect: it is lost not by external forces but solely by our own fault. And once lost, it is not easily recovered — the lapse of moderation becomes a habit of immoderation.

## Definition

In the Stoic ethical framework, moderation is closely related to the cardinal virtue of temperance but occupies a somewhat different register. [[temperance|Temperance]] governs desire directly; moderation governs the outward expression of character — speech, demeanor, and conduct — ensuring that all actions bear the stamp of self-possession. The moderate person does not simply [[power|control]] his impulses; he conducts himself with a visible decency and gentleness that others recognize.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the Greek term *chtastolaen* (translated "moderation" by Long) in Discourse 26, pairing it with modesty and gentleness as qualities whose loss constitutes genuine damage to the self — unlike the loss of external goods, which are neither good nor evil.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses moderation most directly in Discourse 26, where he asks a penetrating question: "If you had lost the art of grammar or music, would you think the loss of it a damage? And if you shall lose modesty, moderation, and gentleness, do you think the loss nothing?" (026-047). His point is that we grieve over the loss of externals — property, reputation, technical skills — while remaining indifferent to the loss of the very qualities that make us human. The loss of moderation is a genuine harm because it corrupts the ruling faculty itself.

In Discourse 67, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] connects the loss of moderation to the lapse of [[attention]] (*prosochē*). When the mind is let loose, "it is no longer in your power to recall it, either to propriety, or to modesty, or to moderation; but you do everything that comes into your mind in obedience to your inclinations" (067-020). The sequence is significant: inattention leads to immoderation, and immoderation becomes habitual — "a habit of not attending is formed in you; then a habit of deferring your attention."

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also associates moderation with [[gentleness]] and a sociable disposition. In Discourse 35, he describes the person who has made progress as one who "will be kind to him, gentle, ready to pardon" (035-080). Moderation is thus not a cold, rigid [[abstinence|self-control]] but a warm and humane quality — the gentleness that arises when the soul is not at war with itself.

## Key Passages

> If you shall lose modesty, moderation, and gentleness, do you think the loss nothing? And yet the things first mentioned are lost by some cause external and independent of the will, and the second by our own fault. (026-047)

> Do you not see that when you have let your mind loose, it is no longer in your power to recall it, either to propriety, or to modesty, or to moderation; but you do everything that comes into your mind in obedience to your inclinations. (067-020)
