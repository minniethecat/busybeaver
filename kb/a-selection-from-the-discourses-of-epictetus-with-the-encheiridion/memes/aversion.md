# Aversion

**Aversion** (*ekklisis*) is, alongside [[desire]] (*[[desire|orexis]]*), one of the two primary movements of the human will in Stoic psychology. It is the impulse to turn away from, to avoid, or to reject something that appears bad. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s system, mastering aversion — restricting it to what truly lies within one's power — is the first and most fundamental step in moral progress.

The distinction between desire and aversion structures the whole of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s practical ethics. "The first things, and the most necessary are those which I have named, the passions" (004-029), and at the head of those passions stands the pair of desire and aversion. To get aversion right is to secure the foundation of the moral life; to misdirect it is to guarantee misery.

## Definition

In the Stoic theory of action, every impulse (*hormē*) is either a movement toward something (desire, *[[desire|orexis]]*) or away from something (aversion, *ekklisis*). Both are judgments about what is good and bad. The sage desires only virtue and the things that contribute to it; he feels aversion only toward vice and the things that lead to it. All externals — health, wealth, reputation, and their opposites — are neither proper objects of desire nor of aversion, because they are indifferents (*adiaphora*).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the orthodox Stoic position but sharpens it into a practical discipline. For the *proficiens* (the one making progress), the task is to suspend desire for externals entirely and to redirect aversion away from external [[fortune|misfortune]]s and onto the only true evil: the corruption of one's own moral character. "He employs his aversion only on things which are dependent on his will" (004-006).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats aversion as the companion and counterpart of desire throughout the Discourses. His teaching can be organized around three themes.

**Aversion belongs to the first of the [[discipline|Three Disciplines]]**. In the famous passage that outlines the three areas of Stoic [[exercise|practice]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] declares: "The first concerns the desires and the aversions, that a man may not fail to get what he desires, and may not fall into that which he would avoid" (040-003). This discipline — the discipline of desire — is the foundation; until desire and aversion are correctly ordered, the disciplines of action and assent cannot function properly.

**Aversion must be limited to what depends on the will**. The central Epictetan insight about aversion is that misdirected aversion causes unhappiness. "If he attempts to avoid anything independent of his will, he knows that sometimes he will fall in with something which he wishes to avoid, and he will be unhappy" (004-007). The path to freedom, therefore, is to restrict aversion to moral evil [[solitude|alone]]: "an aversion which never falls on that which is not in our power" (024-085).

**[[desire|Desire]] and aversion are the first markers of progress**. When [[epictetus|Epictetus]] wants to assess whether a student is making genuine philosophical progress, he does not ask about [[books|book-learning]] or clever [[disputation|argument]]s. "Where then is progress? ... In desire and in aversion, that you may not be disappointed in your desire, and that you may not fall into that which you would avoid" (004-026). Someone who still directs aversion toward poverty, exile, or death — however learned in doctrine — has not yet begun the philosophical life.

**The persistent coupling with desire**. Throughout the Discourses, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] almost never mentions aversion without desire. The two form an inseparable pair: "To live with desire and aversion" (047-009) is the human condition; to discipline them is the philosophical task. "If you allow your desire and aversion to turn to externals" (047-015), the result is disturbance; but "accustomed to employ desire and aversion only to things in your power" (047-019), the result is freedom.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] opens with this pairing as its first principle: "Some things are in our power, others are not" (069-001). The practical corollary is immediate — desire and aversion are to be applied only to what is in our power.

## Key Passages

> The first things, and the most necessary are those which I have named, the passions. The second are those which concern the desires and the aversions, that a man may not fail to get what he desires, and may not fall into that which he would avoid. (040-003)

> He employs his aversion only on things which are dependent on his will. For if he attempts to avoid anything independent of his will, he knows that sometimes he will fall in with something which he wishes to avoid, and he will be unhappy. (004-006–007)

> Where then is progress? ... In desire and in aversion, that you may not be disappointed in your desire, and that you may not fall into that which you would avoid. (004-026)
