[011-001]Ought not

[011-002]then this robber and this adulterer to be destroyed? By no means say so, but

[011-003]speak rather in this way: This man who has been mistaken and deceived about the

[011-004]most important things, and blinded, not in the faculty of vision which

[011-005]distinguishes white and black, but in the faculty which distinguishes [[good]] and

[011-006]bad, should we not destroy him? If you speak thus you will see how inhuman this

[011-007]is which you say, and that it is just as if you would say, Ought we not to

[011-008]destroy this blind and deaf man? But if the greatest harm is the privation of

[011-009]the greatest things, and the greatest thing in every man is the will or choice

[011-010]such as it ought to be, and a man is deprived of this will, why are you also

[011-011]angry with him? Man, you ought not to be affected contrary to [[nature]] by the bad

[011-012]things of another. Pity him rather; drop this readiness to be offended and to

[011-013]hate, and these words which the many utter: “These accursed and odious

[011-014]fellows.” How have you been made so wise at once? and how are you so peevish?

[011-015]Why then are we angry? Is it because we value so much the things of which these

[011-016]men rob us? Do not admire your clothes, and then you will not be angry with the

[011-017]thief. Consider this matter thus: you have fine clothes; your neighbor has not;

[011-018]you have a window; you wish to air the clothes. The thief does not know wherein

[011-019]man’s good consists, but he thinks that it consist in having fine clothes, the

[011-020]very thing which you also think. Must he not then come and take them away? When

[011-021]you show a cake to greedy persons, and swallow it all yourself, do you expect

[011-022]them not to snatch it from you? Do not provoke them; do not have a window; do

[011-023]not air your clothes. I also lately had an iron lamp placed by the side of my

[011-024]household gods; hearing a noise at the door, I ran down, and found that the

[011-025]lamp had been carried off. I reflected that he who had taken the lamp had done

[011-026]nothing strange. What then? To-morrow, I said, you will find an earthen lamp;

[011-027]for a man only loses that which he has. I have lost my garment. The [[reason]] is

[011-028]that you had a garment. I have a pain in my head. Have you any pain in your

[011-029]horns? Why then are you troubled? For we only lose those things, we have only

[011-030]pains about those things, which we possess.

[011-031]But the tyrant will chain—what? The leg. He will take away—what? The neck. What

[011-032]then will he not chain and not take away? The will. This is why the ancients

[011-033]taught the maxim, Know thyself. Therefore we ought to exercise ourselves in

[011-034]small things, and beginning with them to proceed to the greater. I have pain in

[011-035]the head. Do not say, Alas! I have pain in the ear. Do not say alas! And I do

[011-036]not say that you are not allowed to groan, but do not groan inwardly; and if

[011-037]your slave is slow in bringing a bandage, do not cry out and torment yourself,

[011-038]and say, Every body hates me; for who would not hate such a man? For the

[011-039]future, relying on these opinions, walk about upright, free; not trusting to

[011-040]the size of your body, as an athlete, for a man ought not to be invincible in

[011-041]the way that an ass is.
