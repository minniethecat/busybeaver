[027-001]The beginning of philosophy, to him at

[027-002]least who enters on it in the right way and by the door is a consciousness of

[027-003]his own weakness and inability about necessary things; for we come into the

[027-004]world with no natural notion of a right-angled triangle, or of a diesis (a

[027-005]quarter tone), or of a half-tone; but we learn each of these things by a

[027-006]certain transmission according to art; and for this [[reason]] those who do not

[027-007]know them do not think that they know them. But as to [[good]] and evil, and

[027-008]beautiful and ugly, and becoming and unbecoming, and [[happiness]] and misfortune,

[027-009]and proper and improper, and what we ought to do and what we ought not to do,

[027-010]who ever came into the world without having an innate idea of them? Wherefore

[027-011]we all use these names, and we endeavor to fit the preconceptions to the

[027-012]several cases (things) thus: he has done well; he has not done well; he has

[027-013]done as he ought, not as he ought; he has been unfortunate, he has been

[027-014]fortunate; he is unjust, he is just; who does not use these names? who among us

[027-015]defers the use of them till he has learned them, as he defers the use of the

[027-016]words about lines (geometrical figures) or sounds? And the cause of this is

[027-017]that we come into the world already taught as it were by [[nature]] some things on

[027-018]this matter ([Greek: topon]), and proceeding from these we have added to them

[027-019]self-conceit ([Greek: oiaesin]). For why, a man says, do I not know the

[027-020]beautiful and the ugly? Have I not the notion of it? You have. Do I not adapt

[027-021]it to particulars? You do. Do I not then adapt it properly? In that lies the

[027-022]whole question; and conceit is added here; for beginning from these things

[027-023]which are admitted men proceed to that which is matter of dispute by means of

[027-024]unsuitable adaptation; for if they possessed this power of adaptation in

[027-025]addition to those things, what would hinder them from being perfect? But now

[027-026]since you think that you properly adapt the preconceptions to the particulars,

[027-027]tell me whence you derive this (assume that you do so). Because I think so. But

[027-028]it does not seem so to another, and he thinks that he also makes a proper

[027-029]adaptation; or does he not think so? He does think so. Is it possible then that

[027-030]both of you can properly apply the preconceptions to things about which you

[027-031]have contrary opinions? It is not possible. Can you then show us anything

[027-032]better towards adapting the preconceptions beyond your thinking that you do?

[027-033]Does the madman do any other things than the things which seem to him right? Is

[027-034]then this criterion sufficient for him also? It is not sufficient. Come then to

[027-035]something which is superior to seeming ([Greek: tou dochein]). What is this?

[027-036]Observe, this is the beginning of philosophy, a perception of the disagreement

[027-037]of men with one another, and an inquiry into the cause of the disagreement, and

[027-038]a condemnation and distrust of that which only “seems,” and a certain

[027-039]investigation of that which “seems” whether it “seems” rightly, and a discovery

[027-040]of some rule ([Greek: chanonos]), as we have discovered a balance in the

[027-041]determination of weights, and a carpenter’s rule (or square) in the case of

[027-042]straight and crooked things.—This is the beginning of philosophy. Must we say

[027-043]that all things are right which seem so to all? And how is it possible that

[027-044]contradictions can be right?—Not all then, but all which seem to us to be

[027-045]right.—How more to you than those which seem right to the Syrians? why more

[027-046]than what seem right to the Egyptians? why more than what seems right to me or

[027-047]to any other man? Not at all more. What then “seems” to every man is not

[027-048]sufficient for determining what “is”; for neither in the case of weights nor

[027-049]measures are we satisfied with the bare appearance, but in each case we have

[027-050]discovered a certain rule. In this matter then is there no rule superior to

[027-051]what “seems”? And how is it possible that the most necessary things among men

[027-052]should have no sign (mark), and be incapable of being discovered? There is then

[027-053]some rule. And why then do we not seek the rule and discover it, and afterwards

[027-054]use it without varying from it, not even stretching out the finger without it?

[027-055]For this, I think, is that which when it is discovered cures of their madness

[027-056]those who use mere “seeming” as a measure, and misuse it; so that for the

[027-057]future proceeding from certain things (principles) known and made clear we may

[027-058]use in the case of particular things the preconceptions which are distinctly

[027-059]fixed.

[027-060]What is the matter presented to us about which we are inquiring? Pleasure (for

[027-061]example). Subject it to the rule, throw it into the balance. Ought the good to

[027-062]be such a thing that it is fit that we have confidence in it? Yes. And in which

[027-063]we ought to confide? It ought to be. Is it fit to trust to anything which is

[027-064]insecure? No. Is then pleasure anything secure? No. Take it then and throw it

[027-065]out of the scale, and drive it far away from the place of good things. But if

[027-066]you are not sharp-sighted, and one balance is not enough for you, bring

[027-067]another. Is it fit to be elated over what is good? Yes. Is it proper then to be

[027-068]elated over present pleasure? See that you do not say that it is proper; but if

[027-069]you do, I shall then not think you worthy even of the balance. Thus things are

[027-070]tested and weighed when the rules are ready. And to philosophize is this, to

[027-071]examine and confirm the rules; and then to use them when they are known is the

[027-072]act of a wise and good man.
