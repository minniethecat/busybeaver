# Euphrates

## Biography

Euphrates was a Stoic philosopher of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own generation, remembered in antiquity for the beauty of his speech. Within the Discourses, however, he is present only as a name and a reputation. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] twice invokes him as the very type of the impressive speaker, yet tells us nothing of his life, his city, or his doctrines.

Both mentions make the same point in nearly the same words. In Discourse 50, describing people who take up philosophy rashly, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] writes of "some persons having seen a philosopher and having heard one speak like Euphrates—and yet who can speak like him?—wish to be philosophers themselves" (050-021 to 050-023).

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] preserves the parallel version: "some who have seen a philosopher and having heard one speak, as Euphrates speaks—and who can speak as he does?—they wish to be philosophers themselves also" (097-022 to 097-023).

The admiring aside — "who can speak like him?" — is the only judgment [[epictetus|Epictetus]] passes on the man, and it is unmistakable praise of his [[rhetoric|eloquence]] (050-022, 097-023). Everything else the reader might wish to know about Euphrates lies outside what the text will supply.

That the same illustration recurs in both the Discourses and the condensed [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] shows how firmly [[epictetus|Epictetus]] attached the name of Euphrates to a single lesson: that a fine voice is no measure of whether one should take up philosophy (050-022, 097-023).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Euphrates serves as a cautionary emblem rather than an authority. The danger he represents lies not in himself but in the effect of his [[rhetoric|eloquence]] on careless listeners, who mistake admiration for a genuine calling.

In both passages the naming of Euphrates comes in the middle of a warning against undertaking philosophy "at hazard and with a cold desire" (050-020 to 050-021), or "carelessly and with cold desire" (097-021 to 097-022). Hearing a fine speaker is exactly the wrong reason to begin.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s remedy is deliberation. "Man, consider first what the matter is," he urges, "then your own nature also, what it is able to bear" (050-024 to 050-025). The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] echoes it: "first of all consider what kind of thing it is; and then examine your own nature, if you are able to sustain the character" (097-024 to 097-025).

[[philosophy|Philosophy]], on this teaching, is not an eloquent performance to be copied but a discipline whose demands must be weighed against one's own [[nature]] before it is undertaken. Euphrates' brilliance, admirable in itself, becomes a snare only when it provokes imitation grounded in [[desire]] rather than considered choice.

## References in the Discourses

- **Discourse 50**: people who hear "one speak like Euphrates—and yet who can speak like him?—wish to be philosophers themselves" without first weighing the task (050-022).
- **[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] (ench-29)**: the parallel passage, warning against imitation prompted by how "Euphrates speaks" (097-023).
