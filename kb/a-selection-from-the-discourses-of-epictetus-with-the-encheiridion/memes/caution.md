## Definition

Caution (*eulabeia* in Greek, εὐλάβεια) in Stoic ethics is the rational circumspection exercised by the wise person regarding matters that lie within the sphere of moral choice. Unlike ordinary fear — which is directed at externals such as death, poverty, or disgrace — Stoic caution is directed inward, at the state of one's own will.

This distinction is crucial: caution about externals is a passion (fear), while caution about one's moral character is a virtue. The Stoic is cautious about doing wrong, but confident when facing the apparent threats of the external world.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes Discourse 20 ("That From [[reason|Reason]] We Derive Our Notions of Good and [[evil|Evil]]") to the proper relationship between caution and confidence. He observes a paradox: most people invert the correct relationship, exercising caution where they should have confidence, and confidence where they should have caution.

"We ought to employ caution and confidence in the same things," yet most people "employ caution where there is danger in the will, and confidence where there is danger in externals" (020-004–020-007).

His resolution is precise and practical. Where the bad consists in a faulty exercise of the will — that is, in vice — there we should employ caution. Where the bad consists in things outside the will — that is, in death, exile, pain — there we should employ confidence. "If the bad consists in the bad exercise of the will, caution ought only to be used where things are dependent on the will" (020-015–020-016).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that misapplied caution leads to moral deformities. "We convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness" when we direct our caution at external things rather than at our own moral choices (020-036).

In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], he gives a condensed version of this teaching: "Where good is, there is caution; where truth is, there is confidence" (021-034).

## Key Passages

> We must employ caution and confidence in the same things. But we employ caution where there is danger in the will, and confidence where there is danger in externals. (020-004–020-007)

> If the bad consists in the bad exercise of the will, caution ought only to be used where things are dependent on the will. (020-015–020-016)

> How then do we act? We convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness. (020-036)
