## Definition

In Stoic ethics, danger (*peristasis*) is redefined from its conventional meaning. While ordinary usage treats danger as the threat of physical harm, loss, or death, [[stoicism|Stoicism]] teaches that the only genuine danger is moral harm — the corruption of one's character, the loss of virtue, the failure to act justly. Physical dangers, including death, are classified as "indifferents" (*adiaphora*): they are neither good nor bad in themselves, and therefore not truly dangerous. This reframing is central to the Stoic project of eliminating fear: once one understands that only vice can harm the self, no external circumstance can present real danger.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops the Stoic doctrine of danger with characteristic vividness. In Discourse 20, he contrasts the philosopher's attitude toward danger with the ordinary person's: while most people are "struck with terror" (020-033) by apparent threats, the philosopher recognizes that "no one then who lives in error is free" (020-070) — the real danger is not death or pain but living in moral error.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] emphasizes that confidence in the face of danger is a defining mark of philosophical progress. He challenges his students: do you approach trials "as if there were no danger?" (020-028). The answer is not recklessness but a clear-eyed recognition that what most call danger cannot touch the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*). Physical threats harm the body, not the self.

He also warns against the inconsistency of being brave in one sphere and fearful in another: some men "run into danger" (022-044) on the battlefield yet tremble before social disapproval. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the test of danger is universal — the Stoic should face every apparent threat, from death to disgrace, with the same equipoise, knowing that the only real danger lies in betraying one's own moral principles.

## Key Passages

- "[[confidence|Confidence]], as if there were no danger?" (020-028) — The philosophical attitude toward apparent threats.
- "We are struck with terror. Therefore, as we may expect..." (020-033) — How ordinary people react to perceived danger; the starting point for philosophical correction.
- "At another time be in want of confidence, and run into danger" (022-044) — Critiquing inconsistent behavior toward danger.
- "Any trial (danger) of this kind? It is for this..." (020-086) — Danger as a test (*peira*) of character.
