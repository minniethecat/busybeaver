## Definition

Sorrow (Greek: *lupē*) is one of the four primary Stoic passions — an irrational contraction of the soul in response to a perceived present evil. In the Stoic taxonomy of passions, [[grief|sorrow]] belongs to the genus of [[grief|distress]], alongside [[jealousy|envy]], jealousy, and grief. It arises from a false judgment that something genuinely bad has occurred, when in fact no external event can harm the moral character of the wise person. The Stoics held that the sage (*sophos*) is free from all passions, including [[grief|sorrow]], because the sage judges only virtue to be good and only vice to be evil.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats [[grief|sorrow]] as fundamentally incompatible with human freedom and flourishing. In his view, the person who experiences [[grief|sorrow]] has made a cognitive error: they have placed their happiness in externals — things outside the faculty of choice (*prohairesis*). He poses the question starkly: "Do you wish to live in [[grief|sorrow]]? Do you wish to live in perturbation?" (020-072) — framing [[grief|sorrow]] not as an inevitable human feeling but as a consequence of mistaken judgments about value.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the elimination of [[grief|sorrow]] is coterminous with the attainment of freedom. He insists that "whoever is delivered from [[grief|sorrow]]s and fears and perturbations, this man is also free" (020-074). This therapeutic approach distinguishes his thought: [[grief|sorrow]] is not suppressed but dissolved through philosophical reframing. When one recognizes that the source of [[grief|distress]] lies in one's own judgments rather than in events, [[grief|sorrow]] loses its grip.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also addresses the social dimension of [[grief|sorrow]], distinguishing between one's own [[grief|sorrow]] and that of another. He counsels: "I will stop my own [[grief|sorrow]] by every means, for it is in my power; but the [[grief|sorrow]] of another I will endeavor to stop by persuasion" (054-034). This balanced view acknowledges the appropriateness of helping others while maintaining that one's own emotional state remains under the governance of reason.

## Stoic Context

In the broader Stoic tradition, [[grief|sorrow]] (*lupē*) was classified by [[zeno|Zeno of Citium]] and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] as one of the four generic passions, alongside fear (*phobos*), desire (*epithumia*), and pleasure (*hēdonē*). The early Stoics developed a detailed taxonomy of [[grief|sorrow]] subtypes, including pity, [[jealousy|envy]], jealousy, grief, and vexation. Seneca, in his *Consolationes*, applied the Stoic therapy of [[grief|sorrow]] to bereavement, arguing that excessive grief violates both reason and nature. Marcus Aurelius frequently reminded himself that [[grief|sorrow]] over external losses mistakes the indifferent for the harmful.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s contribution to this tradition lies in his relentless focus on the practical discipline of assent: [[grief|sorrow]] is prevented not by suppressing emotion but by refusing to assent to the impression that an external loss constitutes genuine harm. His emphasis on the individual's power over their own judgments — "it is in my power" (054-034) — reflects the core Epictetan doctrine that the will is the sole locus of moral responsibility.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks whether a person "wishes to live in [[grief|sorrow]]" and "in perturbation," asserting that whoever is "in a state of fear or [[grief|sorrow]] or perturbation is not free" (020-072 through 020-074).
- He notes the absurdity of those who experience "their [[grief|sorrow]] if they receive a small cake" (031-057), illustrating how trivial the objects of [[grief|distress]] often are.
- In the catalogue of passions at 054-111, [[grief|sorrow]] is listed among "meanness of spirit, and [[grief|sorrow]], and grief, and [[modesty|shame]]lessness" as perturbations that the philosopher must overcome.
- He promises that the philosophical life delivers "exemption from [[grief|sorrow]] and fear" (054-174), tying [[grief|sorrow]]'s removal to the central Stoic project of attaining tranquillity.
- The question at 057-008—"Who chooses to live in [[grief|sorrow]], fear, [[jealousy|envy]], pity?"—presupposes that no rational person would, and that eliminating these passions is within the power of philosophical [[education|training]].
