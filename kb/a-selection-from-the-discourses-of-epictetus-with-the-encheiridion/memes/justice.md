# Justice

**Justice** (*dikaiosynē*) is the Stoic cardinal virtue concerned with relations among rational beings — the disposition to give each person what is properly theirs. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], justice is not primarily a matter of law courts and punishments but of the proper use of the ruling faculty in all social transactions.

Every human being, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] teaches, possesses an innate [[preconceptions|preconception]] of justice: "Who of us does not assume that Justice is beautiful and becoming?" (014-004). This natural notion is shared universally. The difficulty lies not in knowing *that* justice is good but in applying that knowledge correctly to particular cases.

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, justice is the knowledge of distribution — the rational understanding of what is owed to each person according to their nature and relationship to us. It is inseparable from the other cardinal virtues: wisdom knows what is just, courage defends what is just, and temperance refuses what is unjust.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] grounds justice in the universal community of rational beings. All humans share in reason (*[[reason|logos]]*), and this shared [[nature]] creates mutual [[duties|obligation]]s. To act unjustly toward another person is therefore to act against one's own nature — it is a form of self-harm.

This is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most distinctive contribution to the Stoic theory of justice: the wrongdoer does not primarily harm his victim but himself. "Since that man has hurt himself by doing an unjust act to me, shall I not hurt myself by doing some unjust act to him?" (026-056–026-057). The [[disputation|argument]] is not that retaliation is morally wrong (though it is) but that it is *irrational*: it adds one injury to another and benefits no one.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops his account of justice through the framework of [[preconceptions]]. In Discourse 14, he observes that all peoples — Jews, Syrians, [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, Romans — agree that justice and [[piety|holiness]] are to be pursued. Their disagreements arise only "in the adaptation of the preconceptions to the particular cases" (014-005). Justice, like other fundamental ethical concepts, is universally intuited but misapplied because of deficient reasoning.

The most concentrated treatment appears in Discourse 26 ("On the Infallible Criterion"), where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses a question of apparent injustice. His response is characteristic: the harm of an unjust act falls on the doer, not the receiver. The receiver suffers what is external — property, body, reputation — none of which are true goods. The doer corrupts what is internal — the will — which is the only true evil. Thus "he who has been deceived or he who has done an unjust act neither suffers" in the only sense that matters (026-055).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also connects justice to [[divinity|divine]] providence. "The law of God is most powerful and most just, which is this: Let the stronger always be superior to the weaker" (019-028–019-029). But the "stronger," in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s providential scheme, is not the physically powerful but the morally wise. Ten men may overpower one, but they cannot make their action just.
