## Definition

Preparation (Greek: *paraskeuē*) in Stoic ethics denotes the state of readiness that the philosopher cultivates through [[education|training]] (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*). It is not a passive condition of waiting but an active disposition achieved through the premeditation of future events (*praemeditatio malorum*) and the rehearsal of correct judgments. The prepared person is not surprised by [[fortune|misfortune]], not because they possess foreknowledge of events, but because they have already judged that no external event can harm their moral character. Preparation is thus the practical expression of the Stoic doctrine of indifferents: if nothing except virtue is genuinely good, one can be prepared for anything.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats preparation as an essential component of the philosophical life, one of the topics over which the faculty of choice exercises its governance. In a programmatic passage, he lists the objects of the ruling faculty's supervision: "desire, aversion, preparation, design (purpose), assent" (066-022). Preparation ranks alongside desire and assent as a fundamental category of moral activity — it is not ancillary to philosophy but central to it.

The method of preparation, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is the anticipatory rehearsal of challenges. He advises the student to "consider both the nature of each event and your own preparation and power" (023-007) before acting. This dual assessment — of the external situation and of one's internal resources — is the essence of Stoic deliberation. One does not simply hope for the best; one prepares for the worst, having already judged that the worst cannot touch what is truly one's own.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] emphasizes that preparation must be completed before crisis strikes. He urges his students to make "a preparation against events which may happen" (046-016) — the future is uncertain, but one's response to it can be determined in advance through the discipline of judgment. The person who waits until disaster arrives to decide how to respond has already lost the contest; the person who has prepared their judgments in advance meets the same disaster with equanimity.

The [[exercise|practice]] of preparation culminates in a state of self-possession that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes as having "maintained that which is my own" (021-019). He elaborates: this requires "possible preparation" (021-024) — preparation within the limits of human capacity, not the impossible standard of perfect prescience. The philosopher prepares for what can reasonably be anticipated and, for the rest, trusts in the rational order of the cosmos.

"When you have made this preparation, and have practised this discipline" (057-067), [[epictetus|Epictetus]] promises, the student will possess a freedom that external events cannot touch. Preparation is the bridge between philosophical theory and lived invulnerability — the means by which Stoic doctrine becomes Stoic character.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] lists preparation among the five domains of the ruling faculty: "desire, aversion, preparation, design (purpose), assent" (066-022).
- The method of dual assessment: "consider both the nature of each event and your own preparation and power" (023-007).
- Preparation as self-defense: "I have maintained that which is my own" (021-019) through "possible preparation" (021-024).
- The anticipatory discipline: "a preparation against events which may happen" (046-016) — premeditation as [[education|training]].
- The promise of completion: "When you have made this preparation, and have practised this discipline" (057-067) — preparation is the path to freedom from perturbation.
