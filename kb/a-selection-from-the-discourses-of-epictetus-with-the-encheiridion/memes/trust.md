## Definition

Trust (Greek: *[[fidelity|pistis]]*) in Stoic ethics denotes a confident reliance grounded in correct judgment. The Stoics distinguished between proper trust — placed in that which is genuinely reliable, namely the [[divinity|divine]] order of nature and the virtuous character of the wise — and misplaced trust in externals, which are fundamentally unstable. Trust is not a passive feeling but an active disposition of the rational mind: to trust something is to judge it worthy of confidence. For the Stoic, the only ultimately trustworthy entity is the rational faculty itself, governed by correct principles.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] approaches trust through a characteristic reversal of common assumptions. He observes that people readily entrust themselves to ships and horses without hesitation, yet are reluctant to entrust their moral welfare to philosophy. "When you are in a ship, do you trust yourself to the helmsman? When you are in a chariot, do you trust to the driver? And how is this?" (012-007 through 012-009). His point is that humans routinely place practical trust in skilled practitioners, yet fail to apply the same logic to the care of the soul.

The core of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on trust is that it must be earned through demonstrated character — and that character is revealed through actions, not words. He poses a searching question: "Is it fit to trust to anything which is unstable?" (027-063). Since externals are by nature unstable and outside our [[power|control]], trusting in them is irrational. The only stable foundation for trust is the virtuous disposition of the rational mind.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends this analysis to self-trust. He argues that the [[divinity|divine]] has "entrusted you to yourself" (024-063), making each person the steward of their own moral character. This entrustment carries both dignity and responsibility: to betray oneself through vicious action is to violate a sacred trust. The faithful man "preserves" himself through right action, while the faithless man is "strengthened" in his [[modesty|shame]]lessness by each betrayal (025-025 through 025-027).

He also addresses inter[[mask|persona]]l trust. In Discourse 068, he observes that "it is the opinion that we can safely trust him who has already told us his own affairs" (068-010), analyzing the social dynamics of confidence and disclosure. Trust between persons depends on demonstrated integrity, not mere profession. The ruling principle of a bad man "cannot be trusted" (035-042) because it is governed by unstable passions rather than fixed principles.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] compares the trust we place in helmsmen and chariot drivers to the trust we ought to place in philosophy (012-007 through 012-009).
- He frames self-governance as a [[divinity|divine]] entrustment: God "entrusted you to yourself" (024-063), making each person responsible for their own moral welfare.
- The faithful man is "preserved" by fidelity, while the faithless man is "strengthened" in his faithlessness (025-025 through 025-027) — a dynamic account of how character is reinforced by action.
- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] questions whether it is "fit to trust to anything which is unstable" (027-063), identifying trust in externals as a category error.
- The ruling principle of a bad man "cannot be trusted; it is insecure, inconstant" (035-042) — [[fidelity|trustworthiness]] is a function of philosophical character.
- He pairs arrogance (*tuphos*) with distrust (049-006 through 049-008): arrogance overestimates one's own judgment, while distrust refuses to credit anything beyond it. Both are errors of the rational faculty.
- He analyzes the conditions of safe disclosure: "we can safely trust him who has already told us his own affairs" (068-010), linking trust to reciprocal vulnerability.
