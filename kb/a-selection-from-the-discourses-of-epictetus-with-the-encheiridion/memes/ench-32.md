[100-001]When you have recourse to divination, remember that you do not know how it will

[100-002]turn out, but that you are come to inquire from the diviner. But of what kind

[100-003]it is, you know when you come, if indeed you are a philosopher. For if it is

[100-004]any of the things which are not in our power, it is absolutely necessary that

[100-005]it must be neither [[good]] nor bad. Do not then bring to the diviner [[desire]] or

[100-006]aversion ([Greek: echchlinein]): if you do, you will approach him with fear.

[100-007]But having determined in your mind that everything which shall turn out

[100-008](result) is indifferent, and does not concern you, and whatever it may be, for

[100-009]it will be in your power to use it well, and no man will hinder this, come then

[100-010]with confidence to the gods as your advisers. And then when any advice shall

[100-011]have been given, remember whom you have taken as advisers, and whom you will

[100-012]have neglected, if you do not obey them. And go to divination, as [[socrates|Socrates]] said

[100-013]that you ought, about those matters in which all the inquiry has reference to

[100-014]the result, and in which means are not given either by [[reason]] nor by any other

[100-015]art for knowing the thing which is the subject of the inquiry. Wherefore when

[100-016]we ought to share a friend’s danger, or that of our country, you must not

[100-017]consult the diviner whether you ought to share it. For even if the diviner

[100-018]shall tell you that the signs of the victims are unlucky, it is plain that this

[100-019]is a token of death, or mutilation of part of the body, or of exile. But reason

[100-020]prevails, that even with these risks, we should share the dangers of our

[100-021]friend, and of our country. Therefore attend to the greater diviner, the

[100-022]Pythian god, who ejected from the temple him who did not assist his friend,

[100-023]when he was being murdered.
