# Grief

**Grief** (*lupē*) in the Stoic classification is the passion of distress at something present that is judged to be evil. Unlike [[fear]], which is directed at an anticipated evil, "grief from that which is present" (057-084). Together with fear, desire, and pleasure, grief constitutes one of the four genera of passion (*pathē*) that the Stoic sage claims to be free of — not through insensibility, but through the correction of the underlying judgments.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment of grief is characteristically practical. He does not offer consolation in the ordinary sense; he offers re[[education|training]]. If grief is the product of a false judgment about the value of an external, the cure is not time but philosophy — the deliberate reclassification of what one counts as genuinely good and evil.

## Definition

In Stoic psychology, grief is defined as a "fresh opinion that something present is an evil" (the standard Stoic definition from Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]). It is distinguished from bodily pain, which is a sensation, not a passion. Grief requires a judgment: I have lost something good, and this is terrible. If the judgment is corrected — either by recognizing that what was lost was not genuinely good, or by recognizing that virtue remains intact — the grief dissipates.

Subspecies of grief in the Stoic catalogue include pity, [[jealousy|envy]], jealousy, sorrow, anxiety, and lamentation. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] frequently groups grief with "perturbation" (*tarachē*) and "disturbance," emphasizing that the goal of philosophy is to be "free from grief and free from disturbance" (062-032).

## Epictetus's Treatment

**Grief and opinion**: In a striking passage, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] lists grief alongside cowardice, meanness of spirit, and [[modesty|shame]]lessness as words "of bad omen" — not because they should not be uttered, but because they signify real evils of the soul (054-111). Grief is not a [[fortune|misfortune]] that happens to a person; it is a condition of the person's ruling faculty, and therefore something for which they bear responsibility.

**The sage's claim**: In Discourse 40, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the ideal philosophical student as one who can say, "I am not subject to anger, to grief, to [[jealousy|envy]]: I am not hindered, I am not restrained" (040-064). This is the Stoic claim to *apatheia* — not the absence of feeling, but the absence of passion grounded in false judgment.

**Grief versus hunger**: In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes the priority stark: "It is better to die of hunger and so to be released from grief and fear than to live in abundance with perturbation" (080-003). The point is not to endorse suicide but to establish the correct hierarchy of value — inner freedom outweighs bodily comfort.

## Key Passages

> I am not subject to anger, to grief, to envy: I am not hindered, I am not restrained. (040-064)

> What is grief to you? For fear comes from what you expect, but grief from that which is present. (057-084)

> To learn to be free from grief and free from disturbance, and not to be humbled, and to be free. (062-032)

> It is better to die of hunger and so to be released from grief and fear than to live in abundance with perturbation. (080-003)
