# Gladiator

The **gladiator** (*monomachos*) appears in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses as one stage in a distinctive progression of athletic metaphors for the philosophical life. The sequence — wrestler, gladiator, philosopher, rhetorician — traces an ascent from physical contest to moral and intellectual [[power|mastery]].

Unlike the modern association of gladiators with brutal spectacle, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the figure to emphasize disciplined [[education|training]] under real pressure. The gladiator fights with actual weapons against a genuine opponent, not with [[exercise|practice]] swords in a safe gymnasium. This realism is what makes the metaphor apt for Stoic moral combat.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The gladiator metaphor appears most prominently in Discourse 50, where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] lists the typical career of a young man with more ambition than judgment: "as wrestlers, then as gladiators, then blow a trumpet, then act a tragedy" (050-013). The implied criticism is that the undisciplined flit from one role to another without mastering any. True philosophical [[education|training]] requires sustained commitment to a single pursuit.

The same progression recurs in Discourse 51, where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] imagines a conversation about "gladiators, about horses, about athletes" (051-010) — the idle chatter of those who have not yet turned their attention to what truly matters. The gladiator, like the horse or athlete, is a topic of distraction for the unphilosophical.

In Discourse 97, the progression appears again in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s description of those who play at different roles like children: "at one time an athlete, at another a gladiator, then as trumpeters, then as tragedians" (097-016). The adult philosopher, by contrast, has fixed his role and does not swap costumes. The gladiator represents a stage of seriousness — one who fights with real weapons — but still falls short of the philosopher who fights with reason [[solitude|alone]].

The common subjects of conversation betray one's attachments. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] notes that "the common subjects, not about gladiators, nor horse-races, nor athletes, nor about eating and drinking" (101-005) are the mark of those who have turned toward philosophy, while those still entranced by externals talk endlessly of spectacles.

## Key Passages

- The progression: "as wrestlers, then as gladiators, then blow a trumpet, then act a tragedy" (050-013).
- The philosopher's progression: "wrestler, then a gladiator, then a philosopher, then a rhetorician" (050-015).
- Idle conversation: "if a man speaks about gladiators, about horses, about athletes" (051-010).
- Playing at roles: "at one time an athlete, at another a gladiator, then as trumpeters, then as tragedians" (097-016).
- The common subjects: "not about gladiators, nor horse-races, nor athletes" (101-005).
