## Definition

In Stoic psychology, impulse (*hormē*) is the rational soul's movement toward an object judged to be appropriate or away from an object judged to be inappropriate. It is one of the fundamental "acts of the soul" that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies: "the acts of the soul are movement towards an object or movement from it, desire, aversion, preparation, purpose, and assent" (069-002).

Impulse is the second of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s [[three-topics|Three Topics]] — the domain concerned with appropriate action (*kathēkon*). While the first topic addresses desire and aversion (what one should want or avoid), the second topic governs how one moves in the world: one's impulses must be properly directed, measured, and aligned with nature. The person making progress in [[stoicism|Stoicism]] learns to bring their impulses into harmony with rational judgment about what is fitting.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] gives impulse a central role in his pedagogical system. In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], he presents the [[three-topics|Three Topics]] as the curriculum of Stoic progress: the first concerns desires and aversions, the second concerns "movement towards a thing (*hormae*) and movement from it, and generally in doing what is fitting" (069-002), and the third concerns assent and freedom from deception.

The critical point for [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is that impulse must follow judgment, not precede it. The untrained person feels an impulse and then rationalizes it; the Stoic in [[education|training]] examines the impression that elicits impulse and withholds movement until the impression has been properly tested. This is why the discipline of assent (the third topic) logically precedes the discipline of impulse: one must judge correctly before one can move correctly.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that impulses must be "moderate": the philosopher should exhibit "moderate movement towards everything; whether he is considered foolish or wise he does not care" (116-013). The moderation of impulse is a sign that one no longer values externals excessively — the intensity of one's striving is proportional to the value one assigns to its object.

## Stoic Context

The concept of *hormē* has deep roots in Stoic philosophy. The early Stoics, especially [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], developed a sophisticated psychology in which impulse is the rational faculty's function of initiating motion. Crucially, for the Stoics, impulse is always rational — there is no irrational "appetitive" part of the soul as in [[plato|Plato]]'s tripartite [[example|model]]. What appears as irrational impulse is actually an error of reason: a hastily assented-to impression that something is good or bad.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s organization of the [[three-topics|Three Topics]] around desire, impulse, and assent is his own pedagogical innovation, but it maps onto the traditional Stoic division of ethics into the study of goods and evils (desire), appropriate actions (impulse), and the virtues of the rational faculty (assent). His emphasis on moderation of impulse reflects the Stoic ideal of *apatheia* — not absence of feeling but properly measured response.
