[019-001]The being ([[nature]]) of the [[good]] is a certain will;

[019-002]the being of the bad is a certain kind of will. What, then, are [[externals]]?

[019-003]Materials for the will, about which the will being conversant shall obtain its

[019-004]own good or evil. How shall it obtain the good? If it does not admire

[019-005](over-value) the materials; for the opinions about the materials, if the

[019-006]opinions are right, make the will good: but perverse and distorted opinions

[019-007]make the will bad. God has fixed this law, and says, “If you would have

[019-008]anything good, receive it from yourself.” You say, No, but I will have it from

[019-009]another. Do not so: but receive it from yourself. Therefore when the tyrant

[019-010]threatens and calls me, I say, Whom do you threaten? If he says, I will put you

[019-011]in chains, I say, You threaten my hands and my feet. If he says, I will cut off

[019-012]your head, I reply, You threaten my head. If he says, I will throw you into

[019-013]prison, I say, You threaten the whole of this poor body. If he threatens me

[019-014]with banishment, I say the same. Does he then not threaten you at all? If I

[019-015]feel that all these things do not concern me, he does not threaten me at all;

[019-016]but if I fear any of them, it is I whom he threatens. Whom then do I fear? the

[019-017]master of what? The master of things which are in my own power? There is no

[019-018]such master. Do I fear the master of things which are not in my power? And what

[019-019]are these things to me?

[019-020]Do you philosophers then teach us to despise kings? I hope not. Who among us

[019-021]teaches to claim against them the power over things which they possess? Take my

[019-022]poor body, take my property, take my reputation, take those who are about me.

[019-023]If I advise any persons to claim these things, they may truly accuse me. Yes,

[019-024]but I intend to command your opinions also. And who has given you this power?

[019-025]How can you conquer the opinion of another man? By applying terror to it, he

[019-026]replies, I will conquer it. Do you not know that opinion conquers itself, and

[019-027]is not conquered by another? But nothing else can conquer will except the will

[019-028]itself. For this [[reason]] too the law of God is most powerful and most just,

[019-029]which is this: Let the stronger always be superior to the weaker. Ten are

[019-030]stronger than one. For what? For putting in chains, for killing, for dragging

[019-031]whither they choose, for taking away what a man has. The ten therefore conquer

[019-032]the one in this in which they are stronger. In what then are the ten weaker? If

[019-033]the one possesses right opinions and the others do not. Well then, can the ten

[019-034]conquer in this matter? How is it possible? If we were placed in the scales,

[019-035]must not the heavier draw down the scale in which it is.

[019-036]How strange then that [[socrates|Socrates]] should have been so treated by the Athenians.

[019-037]Slave, why do you say Socrates? Speak of the thing as it is: how strange that

[019-038]the poor body of Socrates should have been carried off and dragged to prison by

[019-039]stronger men, and that anyone should have given hemlock to the poor body of

[019-040]Socrates, and that it should breathe out the life. Do these things seem

[019-041]strange, do they seem unjust, do you on account of these things blame God? Had

[019-042]Socrates then no equivalent for these things? Where then for him was the nature

[019-043]of good? Whom shall we listen to, you or him? And what does Socrates say?

[019-044]“Anytus and Melitus can kill me, but they cannot hurt me.” And further, he

[019-045]says, “If it so pleases God, so let it be.”

[019-046]But show me that he who has the inferior principles overpowers him who is

[019-047]superior in principles. You will never show this, nor come near showing it; for

[019-048]this is the law of nature and of God that the superior shall always overpower

[019-049]the inferior. In what? In that in which it is superior. One body is stronger

[019-050]than another: many are stronger than one: the thief is stronger than he who is

[019-051]not a thief. This is the reason why I also lost my lamp, because in wakefulness

[019-052]the thief was superior to me. But the man bought the lamp at this price: for a

[019-053]lamp he became a thief, a faithless fellow, and like a wild beast. This seemed

[019-054]to him a good bargain. Be it so. But a man has seized me by the cloak, and is

[019-055]drawing me to the public place: then others bawl out, Philosopher, what has

[019-056]been the use of your opinions? see, you are dragged to prison, you are going to

[019-057]be beheaded. And what system of philosophy ([Greek: eisagogaen)] could I have

[019-058]made so that, if a stronger man should have laid hold of my cloak, I should not

[019-059]be dragged off; that if ten men should have laid hold of me and cast me into

[019-060]prison, I should not be cast in? Have I learned nothing else then? I have

[019-061]learned to see that everything which happens, if it be independent of my will,

[019-062]is nothing to me. I may ask, if you have not gained by this. Why then do you

[019-063]seek advantage in anything else than in that in which you have learned that

[019-064]advantage is?

[019-065]Will you not leave the small arguments ([Greek: logaria]) about these matters

[019-066]to others, to lazy fellows, that they may sit in a corner and receive their

[019-067]sorry pay, or grumble that no one gives them anything; and will you not come

[019-068]forward and make use of what you have learned? For it is not these small

[019-069]arguments that are wanted now; the writings of the Stoics are full of them.

[019-070]What then is the thing which is wanted? A man who shall apply them, one who by

[019-071]his acts shall bear testimony to his words. Assume, I intreat you, this

[019-072]character, that we may no longer use in the schools the examples of the

[019-073]ancients, but may have some example of our own.

[019-074]To whom then does the contemplation of these matters (philosophical inquiries)

[019-075]belong? To him who has leisure, for man is an animal that loves contemplation.

[019-076]But it is shameful to contemplate these things as runaway slaves do; we should

[019-077]sit, as in a theatre, free from distraction, and listen at one time to the

[019-078]tragic actor, at another time to the lute-player; and not do as slaves do. As

[019-079]soon as the slave has taken his station he praises the actor and at the same

[019-080]time looks round; then if any one calls out his master’s name, the slave is

[019-081]immediately frightened and disturbed. It is shameful for philosophers thus to

[019-082]contemplate the works of nature. For what is a master? Man is not the master of

[019-083]man; but death is, and life and pleasure and pain; for if he comes without

[019-084]these things, bring Cæsar to me and you will see how firm I am. But when he

[019-085]shall come with these things, thundering and lightning, and when I am afraid of

[019-086]them, what do I do then except to recognize my master like the runaway slave?

[019-087]But so long as I have any respite from these terrors, as a runaway slave stands

[019-088]in the theatre, so do I. I bathe, I drink, I sing; but all this I do with

[019-089]terror and uneasiness. But if I shall release myself from my masters, that is

[019-090]from those things by means of which masters are formidable, what further

[019-091]trouble have I, what master have I still?

[019-092]What then, ought we to publish these things to all men? No, but we ought to

[019-093]accommodate ourselves to the ignorant ([Greek: tois idiotais]) and to say:

[019-094]“This man recommends to me that which he thinks good for himself. I excuse

[019-095]him.” For Socrates also excused the jailer who had the charge of him in prison

[019-096]and was weeping when Socrates was going to drink the poison, and said, “How

[019-097]generously he laments over us.” Does he then say to the jailer that for this

[019-098]reason we have sent away the women? No, but he says it to his friends who were

[019-099]able to hear (understand) it; and he treats the jailer as a child.
