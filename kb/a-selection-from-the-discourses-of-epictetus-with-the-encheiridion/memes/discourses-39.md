[039-001]A certain young man, a rhetorician, came to see [[epictetus|Epictetus]],

[039-002]with his hair dressed more carefully than was usual and his attire in an

[039-003]ornamental style; whereupon Epictetus said, Tell me if you do not think that

[039-004]some dogs are beautiful and some horses, and so of all other animals. I do

[039-005]think so, the youth replied. Are not then some men also beautiful and others

[039-006]ugly? Certainly. Do we then for the same [[reason]] call each of them in the same

[039-007]kind beautiful, or each beautiful for something peculiar? And you will judge of

[039-008]this matter thus. Since we see a dog naturally formed for one thing, and a

[039-009]horse for another, and for another still, as an example, a nightingale, we may

[039-010]generally and not improperly declare each of them to be beautiful then when it

[039-011]is most excellent according to its [[nature]]; but since the nature of each is

[039-012]different, each of them seems to me to be beautiful in a different way. Is it

[039-013]not so? He admitted that it was. That then which makes a dog beautiful, makes a

[039-014]horse ugly; and that which makes a horse beautiful, makes a dog ugly, if it is

[039-015]true that their natures are different. It seems to be so. For I think that what

[039-016]makes a Pancratiast beautiful, makes a wrestler to be not [[good]], and a runner to

[039-017]be most ridiculous; and he who is beautiful for the Pentathlon, is very ugly

[039-018]for wrestling. It is so, said he. What then makes a man beautiful? Is it that

[039-019]which in its kind makes both a dog and a horse beautiful? It is, he said. What

[039-020]then makes a dog beautiful? The possession of the excellence of a dog. And what

[039-021]makes a horse beautiful? The possession of the excellence of a horse. What then

[039-022]makes a man beautiful? Is it not the possession of the excellence of a man? And

[039-023]do you then, if you wish to be beautiful, young man, labor at this, the

[039-024]acquisition of human excellence? But what is this? Observe whom you yourself

[039-025]praise, when you praise many persons without partiality: do you praise the just

[039-026]or the unjust? The just. Whether do you praise the moderate or the immoderate?

[039-027]The moderate. And the temperate or the intemperate? The temperate. If then you

[039-028]make yourself such a person, you will know that you will make yourself

[039-029]beautiful; but so long as you neglect these things, you must be ugly ([Greek:

[039-030]aischron]), even though you contrive all you can to appear beautiful.
