# Divinity

**Divinity** in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is not an abstract theological proposition but a lived conviction that shapes every aspect of ethical [[exercise|practice]]. The divine (*theion*) is the intelligent, benevolent order that governs the cosmos, and the human being's highest calling is to align the individual will with this cosmic order. "How shall I follow the gods in all things, how shall I be contented with the divine administration, and how can I become free?" (008-019). These three questions — obedience, contentment, freedom — are for [[epictetus|Epictetus]] a single question, because freedom consists precisely in willing what the divine administration wills.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherits the Stoic identification of God with [[nature|Nature]], [[reason|Reason]], and [[providence|Providence]], but he gives the doctrine a distinctively [[mask|persona]]l and hortatory inflection. The divine is not remote; it is present in every circumstance as the material for virtue.

## Definition

In Stoic physics, divinity is the active principle (*to poioun*) that pervades and orders passive matter. It is identical with universal [[reason|Reason]] (*logos*), [[fate|Fate]] (*heimarmenē*), and [[providence|Providence]] (*pronoia*). The divine is corporeal (a refined fire or *pneuma*), immanent rather than transcendent, and thoroughly rational.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not dwell on the physics of divinity. His concern is ethical: if the cosmos is administered by a rational and benevolent intelligence, then every event is an expression of that intelligence, and the appropriate response is not complaint but gratitude and conformity. The person who "blames the gods" for what happens has failed to understand both the nature of divinity and their own place in the cosmic order (006-061).

## Epictetus's Treatment

**The divine as father**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] opens Discourse 3 with the claim that "God is the father both of men and of gods" (003-003). This kinship (*syngeneia*) between human beings and the divine is the foundation of human dignity. If a Roman felt [[arrogance|pride]] at being adopted by Caesar, how much more should a person feel at being a child of [[zeus|Zeus]]? The implication is that anyone who thinks meanly of themselves has forgotten their divine lineage.

**Five views of the gods**: In Discourse 8, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] surveys five philosophical positions on divinity — from atheism through Epicurean indifference to full Stoic providence — and insists that one must first settle the question of the gods before determining how to live. "For if there are no gods, how is it our proper end to follow them?" (008-011). The practical conclusion is that the gods exist, exercise particular providence, and communicate through the rational faculty they have given to human beings.

**Likeness to the divine**: The ethical ideal is likeness to god (*homoiōsis theōi*). "If the divine is faithful, man also must be faithful; if the divine is free, man also must be free" (029-036). The virtues are not arbitrary [[precept|rules]] but imitations of the divine character.

## Key Passages

> How shall I follow the gods in all things, how shall I be contented with the divine administration, and how can I become free? (008-019)

> If the divine is faithful, man also must be faithful; if the divine is free, man also must be free. (029-036)

> To think yourself like to the gods; and when you are with many, you ought not to call it crowd, nor trouble, nor uneasiness, but festival and assembly. (008-047)

> You are not inferior to the gods nor less; for the magnitude of intelligence is not measured by length nor yet by height, but by thoughts. (008-063)
