# Vice

**Vice** (*kakia*) is, in Stoic philosophy, the only true evil. While most people locate evil in poverty, pain, exile, or death, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that none of these things can harm the essential self. The only thing that genuinely damages a human being is the corruption of their ruling faculty — the misuse of reason that constitutes vice (008-037). Everything external to the will is indifferent (*adiaphora*); what matters is not what happens to us but how we judge and respond to it. Vice is the failure of that judgment, and therefore it [[solitude|alone]] is to be feared.

## Definition

In Epictetan [[stoicism|Stoicism]], vice is not simply bad behavior or a catalogue of forbidden acts. It is the corruption of reason itself — a state in which the ruling faculty (*prohairesis*) fails to use appearances correctly. The vicious person is not someone who breaks [[precept|rules]] knowingly but someone who is ignorant of what is truly good and what is truly evil. Just as virtue (*arete*) is a form of knowledge — the skill of living in accordance with nature — vice is a form of ignorance, a systematic error in one's fundamental doctrines about value.

This follows directly from the Socratic inheritance of [[stoicism|Stoicism]]. No one, [[socrates|Socrates]] argued, knowingly chooses what is bad; wrongdoing is always the product of a mistaken judgment. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] adopts this position wholeheartedly. The person who pursues wealth as though it were a good, or who flees poverty as though it were an evil, acts viciously not out of malice but out of a false belief about where value resides. The error is cognitive before it is behavioral. Vice, then, is the condition of having one's doctrines about good and evil wrong — and acting on those wrong doctrines.

Because vice is a condition of the ruling faculty, it is entirely within our power. No external circumstance can force a person to become vicious; vice is always self-inflicted. By the same token, no external circumstance can cure vice. Only philosophy — the disciplined examination and correction of one's judgments — can lead from vice to virtue.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most distinctive contribution to the Stoic understanding of vice is his insistence that the vicious person harms only themselves. When someone acts unjustly — by theft, betrayal, or malice — the popular response is anger. But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges a different reaction: pity. The wrongdoer has not truly injured their victim, because the victim's genuine good (their virtue) remains untouched. The wrongdoer, however, has corrupted their own will, and in doing so has suffered the only real harm there is. This is the foundation of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s famous equanimity in the face of mistreatment: "he who has done an unjust act" is the one who is worse off, because his ruling faculty has been damaged (026-021).

This doctrine places vice in a paradoxical position. It is the most serious thing in human life — the sole evil — and yet the Stoic is instructed not to fear it in others, because another person's vice cannot touch one's own will. Nor is the Stoic to hate the vicious, because vice is a kind of blindness, and the appropriate response to blindness is compassion, not rage. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly returns to this theme: the wrongdoer is like a person who has lost their way, not an enemy to be destroyed.

The path from vice to virtue is philosophy itself — the project to which [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire teaching is devoted. By examining one's [[appearances|impressions]], testing one's judgments against the standard of nature, and internalizing the dichotomy of [[power|control]], a person gradually replaces false doctrines with true ones. The habits of vice — which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes as deeply ingrained through repetition — are undone only by the counter-habit of philosophical attention (*prosoche*). Every moment presents a choice: to assent to a false impression and strengthen vice, or to withhold assent and move toward virtue.

## Key Passages

> … and scarcity, and virtue and vice, and all such opposites for the will to choose between them. (008-037)

> No man is a slave whose will is free; no man is free whose will is enslaved. Vice is the only slavery, and virtue the only freedom. (007-029)

> "Why then are you angry with him?" Because, he says, "the wretch has robbed me." But what has he taken from you? Your virtue? Your fidelity? Your modesty? No; he has taken your property, which is nothing to you. Why then are you angry, as if you had suffered an evil? You have suffered none. He indeed has suffered the greatest evil: he has done an unjust act. (026-021)
