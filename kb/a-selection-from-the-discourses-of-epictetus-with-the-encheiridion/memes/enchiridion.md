## Definition

The **Encheiridion** (Greek: Ἐγχειρίδιον, "handbook") is a short work of Stoic ethical advice compiled by Arrian of Nicomedia from the oral teachings of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]. The title means "that which is held in the hand" — a portable, ready-at-hand manual for living.

It distills [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s [[stoicism|Stoicism]] into 52 concise sections, addressing what is in our power, how to handle [[appearances|impressions]], how to face death, and how to maintain inner freedom.

> Simplicius, the commentator on the Encheiridion, or Manual, describes it as a work for those who wish to live the philosophical life (P02-010).

## Epictetus's Treatment

The Encheiridion is a faithful compilation by [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s student Arrian, drawn from the same oral teaching sessions that produced the longer *Discourses*.

It opens with the foundational distinction: "Of things some are in our power, and others are not" (069-001). From this principle the entire manual unfolds — teachings on desire, roles, death, exile, and maintaining tranquillity.

The work's brevity is philosophical: by condensing doctrines into compact form, it makes philosophy always ready-at-hand (procheiron) — principles to be applied, not admired in books.

## Stoic Context

The philosophical handbook genre was well-established in antiquity. The Encheiridion became the most famous example, with extensive commentary by Simplicius in Late Antiquity. It was adapted by Christian monks in the Middle Ages, preserving the ethical core.

Its influence extends through Marcus Aurelius, Montaigne, Pascal, and the tradition of Western moral philosophy. In the 16th century it was among the first Greek philosophical texts printed in Latin translation.

## Key Passages

Encheiridion §1 (069-001) states the foundational distinction between things in our power and things not in our power, which structures all Epictetan ethics.

Encheiridion §8 (076-001) declares: "Do not seek to have events happen as you wish, but wish them to happen as they do happen, and your life will go well."

Encheiridion §15 (083-001) presents the life-as-banquet metaphor — remembering to behave with restraint, as at a banquet, taking what is offered without grasping.

Encheiridion §43 (111-001) teaches that everything has two handles, and circumstances must be reframed by the handle by which they can be borne.
