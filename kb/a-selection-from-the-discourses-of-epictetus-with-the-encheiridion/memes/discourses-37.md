[037-001]A certain person said to him ([[epictetus|Epictetus]]): Frequently I desired to hear you

[037-002]and came to you, and you never gave me any answer; and now, if it is possible,

[037-003]I entreat you to say something to me. Do you think, said Epictetus, that as

[037-004]there is an art in anything else, so there is also an art in speaking, and that

[037-005]he who has the art, will speak skilfully, and he who has not, will speak

[037-006]unskilfully?—I do think so.—He then who by speaking receives benefit himself,

[037-007]and is able to benefit others, will speak skilfully; but he who is rather

[037-008]damaged by speaking and does damage to others, will he be unskilled in this art

[037-009]of speaking? And you may find that some are damaged and others benefited by

[037-010]speaking. And are all who hear benefited by what they hear? Or will you find

[037-011]that among them also some are benefited and some damaged? There are both among

[037-012]these also, he said. In this case also then those who hear skilfully are

[037-013]benefited, and those who hear unskilfully are damaged? He admitted this. Is

[037-014]there then a skill in hearing also, as there is in speaking? It seems so. If

[037-015]you choose, consider the matter in this way also. The practice of music, to

[037-016]whom does it belong? To a musician. And the proper making of a statue, to whom

[037-017]do you think that it belongs? To a statuary. And the looking at a statue

[037-018]skilfully, does this appear to you to require the aid of no art? This also

[037-019]requires the aid of art. Then if speaking properly is the business of the

[037-020]skilful man, do you see that to hear also with benefit is the business of the

[037-021]skilful man? Now as to speaking and hearing perfectly, and usefully, let us for

[037-022]the present, if you please, say no more, for both of us are a long way from

[037-023]everything of the kind. But I think that every man will allow this, that he who

[037-024]is going to hear philosophers requires some amount of practice in hearing. Is

[037-025]it not so?

[037-026]Why then do you say nothing to me? I can only say this to you, that he who

[037-027]knows not who he is, and for what purpose he exists, and what is this world,

[037-028]and with whom he is associated, and what things are the [[good]] and the bad, and

[037-029]the beautiful and the ugly, and who neither understands discourse nor

[037-030]demonstration, nor what is true nor what is false, and who is not able to

[037-031]distinguish them, will neither [[desire]] according to [[nature]] nor turn away nor

[037-032]move towards, nor intend (to act), nor [[assent]], nor dissent, nor suspend his

[037-033][[judgment]]: to say all in a few words, he will go about dumb and blind, thinking

[037-034]that he is somebody, but being nobody. Is this so now for the first time? Is it

[037-035]not the fact that ever since the human race existed, all errors and misfortunes

[037-036]have arisen through this ignorance?

[037-037]This is all that I have to say to you; and I say even this not willingly. Why?

[037-038]Because you have not roused me. For what must I look to in order to be roused,

[037-039]as men who are expert in riding are roused by generous horses? Must I look to

[037-040]your body? You treat it disgracefully. To your dress? That is luxurious. To

[037-041]your behavior, to your look? That is the same as nothing. When you would listen

[037-042]to a philosopher, do not say to him, You tell me nothing; but only show

[037-043]yourself worthy of hearing or fit for hearing; and you will see how you will

[037-044]move the speaker.
