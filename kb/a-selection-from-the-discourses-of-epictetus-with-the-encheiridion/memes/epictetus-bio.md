[P02-001]Very little is known of the life of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]. It is said that he was a native

[P02-002]of Hierapolis in Phrygia, a town between the Maeander and a branch of the

[P02-003]Maeander named the Lycus. Hierapolis is mentioned in the epistle of Paul to the

[P02-004]people of Colossae (Coloss. iv., 13); from which it has been concluded that

[P02-005]there was a Christian church in Hierapolis in the time of the apostle. The date

[P02-006]of the birth of Epictetus is unknown. The only recorded fact of his early life

[P02-007]is that he was a slave in [[rome|Rome]], and his master was Epaphroditus, a profligate

[P02-008]freedman of the Emperor Nero. There is a story that the master broke his

[P02-009]slave’s leg by torturing him; but it is better to trust to the evidence of

[P02-010]Simplicius, the commentator on the Encheiridion, or Manual, who says that

[P02-011]Epictetus was weak in body and lame from an early age. It is not said how he

[P02-012]became a slave; but it has been asserted in modern times that the parents sold

[P02-013]the child. I have not, however, found any authority for this statement.

[P02-014]It may be supposed that the young slave showed intelligence, for his master

[P02-015]sent or permitted him to attend the lectures of C. Musonius Rufus, an eminent

[P02-016]Stoic philosopher. It may seem strange that such a master should have wished to

[P02-017]have his slave made into a philosopher; but Garnier, the author of a “Mémoire

[P02-018]sur les Ouvrages d’Epictète,” explains this matter very well in a communication

[P02-019]to Schweighaeuser. Garnier says: “Epictetus, born at Hierapolis of Phrygia of

[P02-020]poor parents, was indebted apparently for the advantages of a [[good]] education to

[P02-021]the whim, which was common at the end of the Republic and under the first

[P02-022]emperors, among the great of Rome to reckon among their numerous slaves

[P02-023]grammarians, poets, rhetoricians, and philosophers, in the same way as rich

[P02-024]financiers in these later ages have been led to form at a great cost rich and

[P02-025]numerous libraries. This supposition is the only one which can explain to us

[P02-026]how a wretched child, born as poor as Irus, had received a good education, and

[P02-027]how a rigid Stoic was the slave of Epaphroditus, one of the officers of the

[P02-028]imperial guard. For we cannot suspect that it was through predilection for the

[P02-029]Stoic doctrine, and for his own use, that the confidant and the minister of the

[P02-030]debaucheries of Nero would have desired to possess such a slave.”

[P02-031]Some writers assume that Epictetus was manumitted by his master, but I can find

[P02-032]no evidence for this statement. Epaphroditus accompanied Nero when he fled from

[P02-033]Rome before his enemies, and he aided the miserable tyrant in killing himself.

[P02-034]Domitian (Sueton., Domit. 14), afterwards put Epaphroditus to death for this

[P02-035]service to Nero. We may conclude that Epictetus in some way obtained his

[P02-036]freedom, and that he began to teach at Rome; but after the expulsion of the

[P02-037]philosophers from Rome by Domitian, A.D. 89, he retired to [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]] in Epirus,

[P02-038]a city built by Augustus to commemorate the victory at Actium. Epictetus opened

[P02-039]a school or lecture room at Nicopolis, where he taught till he was an old man.

[P02-040]The time of his death is unknown. Epictetus was never married, as we learn from

[P02-041]Lucian (Demonax, c. 55, torn, ii., ed. Hemsterh., p. 393). When Epictetus was

[P02-042]finding fault with Demonax, and advising him to take a wife and beget children,

[P02-043]for this also, as Epictetus said, was a philosopher’s duty, to leave in place

[P02-044]of himself another in the universe, Demonax refuted the doctrine by answering:

[P02-045]Give me then, Epictetus, one of your own daughters. Simplicius says (Comment.,

[P02-046]c. 46, p. 432, ed. Schweigh.) that Epictetus lived alone a long time. At last

[P02-047]he took a woman into his house as a nurse for a child, which one of Epictetus’

[P02-048]friends was going to expose on account of his poverty, but Epictetus took the

[P02-049]child and brought it up.

[P02-050]Epictetus wrote nothing; and all that we have under his name was written

[P02-051]Photius (Biblioth., 58) mentions among Arrian’s works “Conversations with

[P02-052]Epictetus,” [Greek: Homiliai Epichtaeton], in twelve books. Upton thinks that

[P02-053]this work is only another name for the Discourses, and that Photius has made

[P02-054]the mistake of taking the Conversations to be a different work from the

[P02-055]Discourses. Yet Photius has enumerated eight books of the Discourses and twelve

[P02-056]books of the Conversations. Schweighaeuser observes that Photius had not seen

[P02-057]these works of Arrian on Epictetus, for so he concludes from the brief notice

[P02-058]of these works by Photius. The fact is that Photius does not say that he had

[P02-059]read these books, as he generally does when he is speaking of the books which

[P02-060]he enumerates in his Bibliotheca. The conclusion is that we are not certain

[P02-061]that there was a work of Arrian entitled “The Conversations of Epictetus.”

[P02-062]Upton remarks in a note on iii., 23 (p. 184, Trans.), that “there are many

[P02-063]passages in these dissertations which are ambiguous or rather confused on

[P02-064]account of the small questions, and because the matter is not expanded by

[P02-065]oratorical copiousness, not to mention other causes.” The discourses of

[P02-066]Epictetus, it is supposed, were spoken extempore, and so one thing after

[P02-067]another would come into the thoughts of the speaker (Wolf). Schweighaeuser also

[P02-068]observes in a note (ii., 336 of his edition) that the connection of the

[P02-069]discourse is sometimes obscure through the omission of some words which are

[P02-070]necessary to indicate the connection of the thoughts. The reader then will find

[P02-071]that he cannot always understand Epictetus, if he does not read him very

[P02-072]carefully, and some passages more than once. He must also think and reflect, or

[P02-073]he will miss the meaning. I do not say that the book is worth all this trouble.

[P02-074]Every man must judge for himself. But I should not have translated the book, if

[P02-075]I had not thought it worth study; and I think that all books of this kind

[P02-076]require careful reading, if they are worth reading at all.

[P02-077]G.L.
