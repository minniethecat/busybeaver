## Definition

In Epictetan [[stoicism|Stoicism]], slavery (*douleia*) has a dual meaning. In the literal sense, it refers to the legal institution of chattel slavery, which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] knew firsthand — he was born a slave in Hierapolis, served in [[rome|Rome]] under Epaphroditus, and was freed later in life. In the philosophical sense, which is his primary concern, slavery denotes the inner condition of a person whose will is subject to externals: one who fears poverty, craves reputation, or grieves at loss is a "slave" regardless of legal status. Conversely, the person whose ruling principle is aligned with nature is genuinely free, even if physically enslaved.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on slavery is uniquely forceful because it is rooted in autobiography. The biographical note records that he was a slave in [[rome|Rome]] whose master was Epaphroditus, a freedman of [[nero|Nero]] (P02-007). An anecdote relates that Epaphroditus once twisted his slave's leg with a torture device; [[epictetus|Epictetus]] [[tranquillity|calm]]ly observed, "You will break it," and when it broke, said only, "Did I not tell you so?" (P02-009). This [[mask|persona]]l history of suffering gives unique authority to his central philosophical claim: that true slavery is moral rather than physical. Throughout the Discourses, he deploys the figure of the "runaway slave" as a metaphor for the person who knows philosophical truth but flees from practicing it (019-076, 019-086). He warns that when one seeks external goods, "you are a slave yourself" (009-008). The person who wishes to be both free and slave to externals is self-contradictory (021-027).

## Stoic Context

The Stoic paradox that only the wise person is free and everyone else is a slave originates with Zeno and was elaborated by [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]. Seneca, like [[epictetus|Epictetus]], wrote extensively on the theme, notably in Letter 47 where he urges humane treatment of slaves on the grounds that master and slave share a common human nature. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] stands apart, however, in speaking from direct experience of enslavement. His language of "inner slavery" parallels the Cynic-Stoic tradition of Diogenes, who declared himself free even when captured by pirates. The Epictetan emphasis on the will (*proairesis*) elevates the inner freedom/slavery distinction to the center of practical ethics.
