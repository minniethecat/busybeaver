[057-001]He is free who lives as he wishes to live; who is neither

[057-002]subject to compulsion nor to hindrance, nor to force; whose movements to action

[057-003]([Greek: hormai]) are not impeded, whose desires attain their purpose, and who

[057-004]does not fall into that which he would avoid ([Greek: echchliseis

[057-005]aperiptotoi]). Who then chooses to live in error? No man. Who chooses to live

[057-006]deceived, liable to mistake, unjust, unrestrained, discontented, mean? No man.

[057-007]Not one then of the bad lives as he wishes; nor is he then free. And who

[057-008]chooses to live in sorrow, fear, envy, pity, desiring and failing in his

[057-009]desires, attempting to avoid something and falling into it? Not one. Do we then

[057-010]find any of the bad free from sorrow, free from fear, who does not fall into

[057-011]that which he would avoid, and does not obtain that which he wishes? Not one;

[057-012]nor then do we find any bad man free.

[057-013]Further, then, answer me this question, also: does freedom seem to you to be

[057-014]something great and noble and valuable? How should it not seem so? Is it

[057-015]possible then when a man obtains anything so great and valuable and noble to be

[057-016]mean? It is not possible. When then you see any man subject to another or

[057-017]flattering him contrary to his own opinion, confidently affirm that this man

[057-018]also is not free; and not only if he do this for a bit of supper, but also if

[057-019]he does it for a government (province) or a consulship; and call these men

[057-020]little slaves who for the sake of little matters do these things, and those who

[057-021]do so for the sake of great things call great slaves, as they deserve to be.

[057-022]This is admitted also. Do you think that freedom is a thing independent and

[057-023]self-governing? Certainly. Whomsoever then it is in the power of another to

[057-024]hinder and compel, declare that he is not free. And do not look, I entreat you,

[057-025]after his grandfathers and great-grandfathers, or inquire about his being

[057-026]bought or sold, but if you hear him saying from his heart and with feeling,

[057-027]“Master,” even if the twelve fasces precede him (as consul), call him a slave.

[057-028]And if you hear him say, “Wretch that I am, how much I suffer,” call him a

[057-029]slave. If, finally, you see him lamenting, complaining, unhappy, call him a

[057-030]slave, though he wears a praetexta. If, then, he is doing nothing of this kind

[057-031]do not yet say that he is free, but learn his opinions, whether they are

[057-032]subject to compulsion, or may produce hindrance, or to bad fortune, and if you

[057-033]find him such, call him a slave who has a holiday in the Saturnalia; say that

[057-034]his master is from home; he will return soon, and you will know what he

[057-035]suffers.

[057-036]What then is that which makes a man free from hindrance and makes him his own

[057-037]master? For wealth does not do it, nor consulship, nor provincial government,

[057-038]nor royal power; but something else must be discovered. What then is that which

[057-039]when we write makes us free from hindrance and unimpeded? The knowledge of the

[057-040]art of writing. What then is it in playing the lute? The science of playing the

[057-041]lute. Therefore in life also it is the science of life. You have then heard in

[057-042]a general way; but examine the thing also in the several parts. Is it possible

[057-043]that he who desires any of the things which depend on others can be free from

[057-044]hindrance? No. Is it possible for him to be unimpeded? No. Therefore he cannot

[057-045]be free. Consider then, whether we have nothing which is in our own power only,

[057-046]or whether we have all things, or whether some things are in our own power, and

[057-047]others in the power of others. What do you mean? When you wish the body to be

[057-048]entire (sound) is it in your power or not? It is not in my power. When you wish

[057-049]it to be healthy? Neither is this in my power. When you wish it to be handsome?

[057-050]Nor is this. Life or death? Neither is this in my power. Your body then is

[057-051]another’s, subject to every man who is stronger than yourself. It is. But your

[057-052]estate is it in your power to have it when you please, and as long as you

[057-053]please, and such as you please? No. And your slaves? No. And your clothes? No.

[057-054]And your house? No. And your horses? Not one of these things. And if you wish

[057-055]by all means your children to live, or your wife, or your brother, or your

[057-056]friends, is it in your power? This also is not in my power.

[057-057]Whether then have you nothing which is in your own power, which depends on

[057-058]yourself only and cannot be taken from you, or have you anything of the kind? I

[057-059]know not. Look at the thing then thus, and examine it. Is any man able to make

[057-060]you [[assent]] to that which is false? No man. In the matter of assent then you are

[057-061]free from hindrance and obstruction. Granted. Well; and can a man force you to

[057-062][[desire]] to move towards that to which you do not choose? He can, for when he

[057-063]threatens me with death or bonds he compels me to desire to move towards it. If

[057-064]then you despise death and bonds, do you still pay any regard to him? No. Is

[057-065]then the despising of death an act of your own or is it not yours? It is my

[057-066]act.

[057-067]When you have made this preparation, and have practised this [[discipline]], to

[057-068]distinguish that which belongs to another from that which is your own, the

[057-069]things which are subject to hindrance from those which are not, to consider the

[057-070]things free from hindrance to concern yourself, and those which are not free

[057-071]not to concern yourself, to keep your desire steadily fixed to the things which

[057-072]do concern yourself, and turned from the things which do not concern yourself;

[057-073]do you still fear any man? No one. For about what will you be afraid? [[About]] the

[057-074]things which are your own, in which consists the [[nature]] of [[good]] and evil? and

[057-075]who has power over these things? who can take them away? who can impede them?

[057-076]No man can, no more than he can impede God. But will you be afraid about your

[057-077]body and your possessions, about things which are not yours, about things which

[057-078]in no way concern you? and what else have you been studying from the beginning

[057-079]than to distinguish between your own and not your own, the things which are in

[057-080]your power and not in your power, the things subject to hindrance and not

[057-081]subject? and why have you come to the philosophers? was it that you may

[057-082]nevertheless be unfortunate and unhappy? You will then in this way, as I have

[057-083]supposed you to have done, be without fear and disturbance. And what is grief

[057-084]to you? for fear comes from what you expect, but grief from that which is

[057-085]present. But what further will you desire? For of the things which are within

[057-086]the power of the will, as being good and present, you have a proper and

[057-087]regulated desire; but of the things which are not in the power of the will you

[057-088]do not desire any one, and so you do not allow any place to that which is

[057-089]irrational, and impatient, and above measure hasty.

[057-090]Then after receiving everything from another and even yourself, are you angry

[057-091]and do you blame the giver if he takes anything from you? Who are you, and for

[057-092]what purpose did you come into the world? Did not he (God) introduce you here,

[057-093]did he not show you the light, did he not give you fellow-workers, and

[057-094]perceptions and [[reason]]? and as whom did he introduce you here? did he not

[057-095]introduce you as subject to death, and as one to live on the earth with a

[057-096]little flesh, and to observe his administration, and to join with him in the

[057-097]spectacle and the festival for a short time? Will you not then, as long as you

[057-098]have been permitted, after seeing the spectacle and the solemnity, when he

[057-099]leads you out, go with adoration of him and thanks for what you have heard and

[057-100]seen? No; but I would still enjoy the feast. The initiated too would wish to be

[057-101]longer in the initiation; and perhaps also those at [[olympia|Olympia]] to see other

[057-102]athletes. But the solemnity is ended; go away like a grateful and modest man;

[057-103]make room for others; others also must be born, as you were, and, being born,

[057-104]they must have a place, and houses, and necessary things. And if the first do

[057-105]not retire, what remains? Why are you insatiable? Why are you not content? why

[057-106]do you contract the world? Yes, but I would have my little children with me and

[057-107]my wife. What, are they yours? do they not belong to the giver, and to him who

[057-108]made you? then will you not give up what belongs to others? will you not give

[057-109]way to him who is superior? Why then did he introduce me into the world on

[057-110]these conditions? And if the conditions do not suit you, depart. He has no need

[057-111]of a spectator who is not satisfied. He wants those who join in the festival,

[057-112]those who take part in the chorus, that they may rather applaud, admire, and

[057-113]celebrate with hymns the solemnity. But those who can bear no trouble, and the

[057-114]cowardly, he will not unwillingly see absent from the great assembly ([Greek:

[057-115]panaeguris]) for they did not when they were present behave as they ought to do

[057-116]at a festival nor fill up their place properly, but they lamented, found fault

[057-117]with the deity, fortune, their companions; not seeing both what they had, and

[057-118]their own powers, which they received for contrary purposes, the powers of

[057-119]magnanimity, of a generous mind, manly spirit, and what we are now inquiring

[057-120]about, freedom. For what purpose then have I received these things? To use

[057-121]them. How long? So long as he who has lent them chooses. What if they are

[057-122]necessary to me? Do not attach yourself to them and they will not be necessary;

[057-123]do not say to yourself that they are necessary, and then they are not

[057-124]necessary.

[057-125]You then, a man may say, are you free? I wish, by the gods, and pray to be

[057-126]free; but I am not yet able to face my masters, I still value my poor body, I

[057-127]value greatly the preservation of it entire, though I do not possess it entire.

[057-128]But I can point out to you a free man, that you may no longer seek an example.

[057-129]Diogenes was free. How was he free? Not because he was born of free parents,

[057-130]but because he was himself free, because he had cast off all the handles of

[057-131]slavery, and it was not possible for any man to approach him, nor had any man

[057-132]the means of laying hold of him to enslave him. He had everything easily

[057-133]loosed, everything only hanging to him. If you laid hold of his property, he

[057-134]would have rather let it go and be yours, than he would have followed you for

[057-135]it; if you had laid hold of his leg, he would have let go his leg; if of all

[057-136]his body, all his poor body; his intimates, friends, country, just the same.

[057-137]For he knew from whence he had them, and from whom, and on what conditions. His

[057-138]true parents indeed, the gods, and his real country he would never have

[057-139]deserted, nor would he have yielded to any man in obedience to them and to

[057-140]their orders, nor would any man have died for his country more readily. For he

[057-141]was not used to inquire when he should be considered to have done anything on

[057-142]behalf of the whole of things (the universe, or all the world), but he

[057-143]remembered that everything which is done comes from thence and is done on

[057-144]behalf of that country and is commanded by him who administers it. Therefore

[057-145]see what Diogenes himself says and writes: “For this reason,” he says,

[057-146]“Diogenes, it is in your power to speak both with the King of the Persians and

[057-147]with Archidamus the King of the Lacedaemonians, as you please.” Was it because

[057-148]he was born of free parents? I suppose all the Athenians and all the

[057-149]Lacedaemonians, because they were born of slaves, could not talk with them

[057-150](these kings) as they wished, but feared and paid court to them. Why then does

[057-151]he say that it is in his power? Because I do not consider the poor body to be

[057-152]my own, because I want nothing, because law is everything to me, and nothing

[057-153]else is. These were the things which permitted him to be free.

[057-154]Think of these things, these opinions, these words; look to these examples, if

[057-155]you would be free, if you desire the thing according to its worth. And what is

[057-156]the wonder if you buy so great a thing at the price of things so many and so

[057-157]great? For the sake of this which is called liberty, some hang themselves,

[057-158]others throw themselves down precipices, and sometimes even whole cities have

[057-159]perished; and will you not for the sake of the true and unassailable and secure

[057-160]liberty give back to God when he demands them the things which he has given?

[057-161]Will you not, as Plato says, study not to die only, but also to endure torture,

[057-162]and exile, and scourging, and, in a word, to give up all which is not your own?

[057-163]If you will not, you will be a slave among slaves, even if you be ten thousand

[057-164]times a consul; and if you make your way up to the palace (Cæsar’s residence),

[057-165]you will no less be a slave; and you will feel that perhaps philosophers utter

[057-166]words which are contrary to common opinion (paradoxes), as Cleanthes also said,

[057-167]but not words contrary to reason. For you will know by experience that the

[057-168]words are true, and that there is no profit from the things which are valued

[057-169]and eagerly sought to those who have obtained them; and to those who have not

[057-170]yet obtained them there is an imagination ([Greek: phantasia]), that when these

[057-171]things are come, all that is good will come with them; then, when they are

[057-172]come, the feverish feeling is the same, the tossing to and fro is the same, the

[057-173]satiety, the desire of things, which are not present; for freedom is acquired

[057-174]not by the full possession of the things which are desired, but by removing the

[057-175]desire. And that you may know that this is true, as you have labored for those

[057-176]things, so transfer your labor to these: be vigilant for the purpose of

[057-177]acquiring an opinion which will make you free; pay court to a philosopher

[057-178]instead of to a rich old man; be seen about a philosopher’s doors; you will not

[057-179]disgrace yourself by being seen; you will not go away empty nor without profit,

[057-180]if you go to the philosopher as you ought, and if not (if you do not succeed),

[057-181]try at least; the trial (attempt) is not disgraceful.
