[012-001]If a man possesses any superiority, or thinks

[012-002]that he does when he does not, such a man, if he is uninstructed, will of

[012-003]necessity be puffed up through it. For instance, the tyrant says, I am master

[012-004]of all! And what can you do for me? Can you give me [[desire]] which shall have no

[012-005]hindrance? How can you? Have you the infallible power of avoiding what you

[012-006]would avoid? Have you the power of moving towards an object without error? And

[012-007]how do you possess this power? Come, when you are in a ship, do you trust to

[012-008]yourself or to the helmsman? And when you are in a chariot, to whom do you

[012-009]trust but to the driver? And how is it in all other arts? Just the same. In

[012-010]what, then, lies your power? All men pay respect to me. Well, I also pay

[012-011]respect to my platter, and I wash it and wipe it; and for the sake of my

[012-012]oil-flask, I drive a peg into the wall. Well, then, are these things superior

[012-013]to me? No, but they supply some of my wants, and for this [[reason]] I take care of

[012-014]them. Well, do I not attend to my ass? Do I not wash his feet? Do I not clean

[012-015]him? Do you not know that every man has regard to himself, and to you just the

[012-016]same as he has regard to his ass? For who has regard to you as a man? Show me.

[012-017]Who wishes to become like you? Who imitates you, as he imitates [[socrates|Socrates]]? But I

[012-018]can cut off your head. You say right. I had forgotten that I must have regard

[012-019]to you, as I would to a fever and the bile, and raise an altar to you, as there

[012-020]is at [[rome|Rome]] an altar to fever.

[012-021]What is it then that disturbs and terrifies the multitude? Is it the tyrant and

[012-022]his guards? (By no means.) I hope that it is not so. It is not possible that

[012-023]what is by [[nature]] free can be disturbed by anything else, or hindered by any

[012-024]other thing than by itself. But it is a man’s own opinions which disturb him.

[012-025]For when the tyrant says to a man, I will chain your leg, he who values his leg

[012-026]says, Do not; have pity. But he who values his own will says, If it appears

[012-027]more advantageous to you, chain it. Do you not care? I do not care. I will show

[012-028]you that I am master. You cannot do that. [[zeus|Zeus]] has set me free; do you think

[012-029]that he intended to allow his own son to be enslaved? But you are master of my

[012-030]carcase; take it. So when you approach me, you have no regard to me? No, but I

[012-031]have regard to myself; and if you wish me to say that I have regard to you

[012-032]also, I tell you that I have the same regard to you that I have to my pipkin.

[012-033]What then? When absurd notions about things independent of our will, as if they

[012-034]were [[good]] and (or) bad, lie at the bottom of our opinions, we must of necessity

[012-035]pay regard to tyrants: for I wish that men would pay regard to tyrants only,

[012-036]and not also to the bedchamber men. How is it that the man becomes all at once

[012-037]wise, when Cæsar has made him superintendent of the close stool? How is it that

[012-038]we say immediately, Felicion spoke sensibly to me? I wish he were ejected from

[012-039]the bedchamber, that he might again appear to you to be a fool.

[012-040]Has a man been exalted to the tribuneship? All who meet him offer their

[012-041]congratulations; one kisses his eyes, another the neck, and the slaves kiss his

[012-042]hands. He goes to his house, he finds torches lighted. He ascends the Capitol;

[012-043]he offers a sacrifice on the occasion. Now who ever sacrificed for having had

[012-044]good desires? for having acted conformably to nature? For in fact we thank the

[012-045]gods for those things in which we place our good.

[012-046]A person was talking to me to-day about the priesthood of Augustus. I say to

[012-047]him: Man, let the thing alone; you will spend much for no purpose. But he

[012-048]replies, Those who draw up agreements will write my name. Do you then stand by

[012-049]those who read them, and say to such persons, It is I whose name is written

[012-050]there? And if you can now be present on ail such occasions, what will you do

[012-051]when you are dead? My name will remain. Write it on a stone, and it will

[012-052]remain. But come, what remembrance of you will there be beyond [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]? But I

[012-053]shall wear a crown of gold. If you desire a crown at all, take a crown of roses

[012-054]and put it on, for it will be more elegant in appearance.
