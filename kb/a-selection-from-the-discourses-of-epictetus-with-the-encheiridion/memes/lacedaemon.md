# Lacedaemon

**Lacedaemon** — the city-state of Sparta in the southern Peloponnese — appears in the Discourses chiefly as an emblem of trained endurance. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] points to the Spartans' capacity to bear pain once they judge it reasonable, and to their king as a figure before whom even a Cynic like [[diogenes|Diogenes]] refused to abase himself.

## Location

Lacedaemon is the classical name for Sparta and the surrounding territory of Laconia, in the southern Peloponnese of Greece. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] refers to its people as the "Lacedaemonians" rather than to the city itself, treating them as a byword for a particular hardness of character (002-004, 035-032).

## Significance in the Discourses

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most striking use of Lacedaemon is as evidence that physical suffering is not intolerable in itself. Arguing that blows are not naturally intolerable, he observes that the "Lacedaemonians endure whipping when they have learned that whipping is consistent with reason" (002-003, 002-004). The Spartan flogging becomes a Stoic lesson: it is [[opinion|opinion]], not the blow, that determines whether pain can be borne, for the man who reasons rightly is pained by nothing so much as by what he judges irrational (002-007).

The Lacedaemonians also serve as an example of how ignorance breeds conflict. "It was through this ignorance that the Athenians and the Lacedaemonians quarrelled, and the Thebans with both" (035-032, 035-033) — nations fall to war not over whether honesty is good but over its application to particular cases.

Finally, the Spartan king Archidamus figures in a lesson on true [[freedom|freedom]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reports that [[diogenes|Diogenes]] claimed it was in his power "to speak both with the King of the Persians and with Archidamus the King of the Lacedaemonians, as you please" (057-146, 057-147). This freedom sprang not from noble birth but from wanting nothing and holding law to be everything (057-151, 057-152); before those same kings, ordinary Athenians and Lacedaemonians "feared and paid court to them" (057-149, 057-150).

## Historical Context

Sparta was, with [[athens|Athens]], one of the two dominant powers of classical Greece, famed for a militarized society and a severe system of civic discipline. By [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s day it survived as a storied name from the Greek past rather than as a great power — which is how it functions in the Discourses: a familiar shorthand for endurance and austerity that his audience would recognize at once.
