# Education

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s [[stoicism|Stoicism]], **education** (*paideia*) is radically redefined. It is not the transmission of information, the [[power|mastery]] of books, or the acquisition of rhetorical skill. It is the practical art of learning to live according to nature — specifically, learning "how to adapt the natural præcognitions to the particular things conformably to nature" (014-024). Education, in this sense, is indistinguishable from moral progress; it is the discipline by which a human being becomes what nature intends.

This redefinition is one of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most characteristic moves. Throughout the Discourses, he contrasts true philosophical education with the false education offered by the schools of rhetoric and the lecture-halls of professional philosophers. The latter produces clever speakers; the former produces good human beings.

## Definition

The key text is Discourse 14, where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] poses the question directly: "What then is education? Education is the learning how to adapt the natural præcognitions to the particular things conformably to nature; and then to distinguish that of things some are in our power, but others are not" (014-024–026). The definition has two parts. First, education is the application of innate moral concepts (*prolēpseis*) — such as the notion that good is to be pursued and evil avoided — to the concrete situations of life. Second, it is the recognition of the fundamental division between what depends on the will and what does not. Both parts are practical, not theoretical: they are learned by doing, not by [[books|reading]].

**The præcognitions (*prolēpseis*)**. All human beings, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] holds, are born with innate preconceptions of good and evil, just and unjust, noble and base. These are not yet knowledge; they are the raw materials that education shapes. The uneducated person applies these preconceptions clumsily, calling "good" whatever happens to please the senses; the educated person learns to apply them correctly, recognizing that only virtue is good and only vice is evil.

**The distinction of what is in our power**. The second component of education is the cornerstone of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire system: the distinction between *ta eph' hēmin* (things in our power) and *ta ouk eph' hēmin* (things not in our power). To be educated is to have internalized this distinction so thoroughly that one never confuses the two categories in [[exercise|practice]].

## Epictetus's Treatment

**The biographical dimension**. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own life embodies his doctrine of education. Born a slave in Hierapolis, he received a philosophical education under [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]] while still a slave. The biographical note in the Discourses records that [[epictetus|Epictetus]], "as Irus, had received a good education" (P02-026) — an education that equipped him not with social status but with inner freedom. His teaching career in [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]] after his emancipation was devoted to offering this same education to others.

**Education and the philosophical schools**. Throughout the Discourses, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] critiques those who mistake formal schooling for education. To explain [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]'s syllogisms is not to be educated; to demonstrate self-[[power|mastery]] in the face of temptation is education. "The first things, and the most necessary are those which I have named" (004-029) — the passions, desire and aversion, pursuit and [[aversion|avoidance]] — and education that skips these for logical subtleties is a fraud.

**Education as a continuous [[exercise|practice]]**. Education is not completed in a school or at a particular age. It is the work of a lifetime: every encounter with an impression (*phantasia*) is an opportunity to exercise the distinction between what is and is not in one's power. "You can celebrate a festival daily, to-day because you have behaved well in one act, and to-morrow because you have behaved well in another" (060-078). Each act of correct judgment is both the fruit of education and a further step in it.
