## Definition

School (*scholē*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses carries a double meaning. In its literal sense, it refers to the lecture room at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]] where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] taught (P02-039) — the physical space of philosophical [[education|instruction]]. But more importantly, "school" functions as a metaphor for the entire enterprise of philosophical [[education|training]]: a protected environment where the student [[exercise|practice]]s judgment, desire, and action before being tested in the un[[power|control]]led conditions of life. The school is the gymnasium of the soul.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is both an inhabitant of the school and its fiercest critic. He taught in a school — "a school or lecture room at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]" (P02-039) — and his Discourses are records of that teaching. Yet he repeatedly warns against the danger of becoming a mere "school-philosopher," someone who handles [[disputation|argument]]s skillfully within the classroom but collapses under the pressure of real life.

The school's proper function is preparation, not permanent residence. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the vivid image of going "from the school into active life" (024-042), and he worries that students may be "roused a little only in the school" (041-028) — stirred by the teacher's words but unchanged once they leave. The school is a place to "learn history and understand" (034-032) — but learning is not the end; it is the means to living differently.

What is actually taught in the school? [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is scathing about the curriculum of his rivals: "What else did you learn in the school? Syllogisms and" logical puzzles (028-073). The true philosophical school teaches not logical tricks but the art of living — how to use [[appearances|impressions]], how to manage desire, how to fulfill one's roles. The student who has only learned syllogisms "out of the schools" (031-004) has wasted his time.

The school is also a place of shared [[exercise|practice]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] speaks of "our minds in the school" (051-030) being shaped by the community of fellow-students. The school provides not only a teacher but companions in [[education|training]] — others who share the same project and can offer correction and encouragement. "Withdraw then yourselves far" (051-030) from corrupting influences, and enter the school as a refuge for the soul.

Yet the school's ultimate test is outside its walls. "In the schools the examples of" (019-072) heroes and sages are used — but the point is to become such a person, not merely to study them. The school that produces only clever talkers rather than virtuous actors "may be judged" (054-152) a failure. The true measure of a school is not what its students say but how they live when no teacher is watching.

## Key Passages

- (P02-039) — "A school or lecture room at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]]" — the physical setting of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching.
- (024-042) — "From the school into active life" — the transition from [[education|training]] to testing.
- (041-028) — "Only, if ever, in the school we are roused a little" — the danger of school-only philosophy.
- (028-073) — "What else did you learn in the school? Syllogisms and" — critique of mere logical [[education|training]].
- (031-004) — "Out of the schools" — the contrast between classroom and life.
- (034-032) — "Go to my school to learn history and understand" — the school as place of learning.
- (051-030) — "Our minds in the school. Withdraw then yourselves far" — the school as refuge.
- (019-072) — "In the schools the examples of" — the use of exempla in philosophical education.
- (054-152) — "The schools and may be judged" — the school measured by its products.
