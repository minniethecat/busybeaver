# Courage

**Courage** (*andreia*) is one of the four cardinal Stoic virtues, the disposition to face what appears terrible with the knowledge that no [[externals|external]] thing — not pain, not poverty, not exile, not death — is truly an evil. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], courage is not the absence of fear but the correct judgment about what is to be feared.

The Stoic analysis of courage begins from the recognition that fear is always directed at something: we are afraid *of* a particular thing. Courage is not a brute effort of will that overcomes fear; it is the rational insight that the feared object cannot harm what is truly ours — the ruling faculty (*[[prohairesis]]*).

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, courage is the virtue concerned with the management of fear and confidence. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes this in Discourse 20, where he explains that caution (*eulabeia*) and confidence (*[[confidence|tharsos]]*) are both rational dispositions, but they must be directed at the right objects: confidence toward what is within the will's power, caution toward what lies outside it (020-033).

When confidence and caution are misdirected, courage collapses into its distortions. "We convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness, which are full of fear and confusion" (020-036). The coward is not someone who merely feels fear — everyone feels fear — but someone who has made the cognitive error of treating an external thing as if it could genuinely harm the self.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats courage primarily through its opposite, cowardice, employing vivid examples to [[modesty|shame]] his students out of their false fears. In Discourse 56, he opens with a direct challenge: "Are you not a[[modesty|shame]]d at being more cowardly and more mean than fugitive slaves?" (056-001). The fugitive slave, he observes, leaves behind his master and trusts in his own resources; the Stoic student who fears poverty or exile trusts in nothing but his possessions.

The scout analogy in Discourse 15 is equally pointed: "No man sends a cowardly scout, who, if he only hears a noise and sees a shadow anywhere, comes running back in terror and reports that the enemy is near" (015-008). The Stoic is a scout sent into life by [[zeus|Zeus]], and to return in terror at the first sign of difficulty is a betrayal of that trust.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also ties courage to the discipline of [[assent]]. In Discourse 44, he teaches that [[education|training]] in bearing things bravely is "a thing within the power of the will: it is a good" (044-010). The act of refusing to assent to the appearance that something terrible is happening — this is the essence of Stoic courage.

The ultimate cowardice, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is not the fear of death but the fear of losing externals: "He will not unwillingly see absent from the great [[festival|assembly]]" (057-114). The person whose happiness depends on being seen at the festival will cling to life and reputation at any cost.

## Key Passages

> No man sends a cowardly scout, who, if he only hears a noise and sees a shadow anywhere, comes running back in terror. (015-008)

> We convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness, which are full of fear and confusion. (020-036)

> Are you not ashamed at being more cowardly and more mean than fugitive slaves? (056-001)

> That is a thing within the power of the will: it is a good. If we train ourselves in this manner, we shall make progress. (044-010–044-011)
