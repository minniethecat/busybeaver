[068-001]When a man has seemed

[068-002]to us to have talked with simplicity (candor) about his own affairs, how is it

[068-003]that at last we are ourselves also induced to discover to him our own secrets

[068-004]and we think this to be candid behavior? In the first place, because it seems

[068-005]unfair for a man to have listened to the affairs of his neighbor, and not to

[068-006]communicate to him also in turn our own affairs; next, because we think that we

[068-007]shall not present to them the appearance of candid men when we are silent about

[068-008]our own affairs. Indeed, men are often accustomed to say, I have told you all

[068-009]my affairs, will you tell me nothing of your own? where is this done? Besides,

[068-010]we have also this opinion that we can safely trust him who has already told us

[068-011]his own affairs; for the notion rises in our mind that this man could never

[068-012]divulge our affairs because he would be cautious that we also should not

[068-013]divulge his. In this way also the incautious are caught by the soldiers at

[068-014][[rome|Rome]]. A soldier sits by you in a common dress and begins to speak ill of Cæsar;

[068-015]then you, as if you had received a pledge of his fidelity by his having begun

[068-016]the abuse, utter yourself also what you think, and then you are carried off in

[068-017]chains.

[068-018]Something of this kind happens to us also generally. Now as this man has

[068-019]confidently intrusted his affairs to me, shall I also do so to any man whom I

[068-020]meet? (No), for when I have heard, I keep silence, if I am of such a

[068-021]disposition; but he goes forth and tells all men what he has heard. Then, if I

[068-022]hear what has been done, if I be a man like him, I resolve to be revenged, I

[068-023]divulge what he has told me; I both disturb others, and am disturbed myself.

[068-024]But if I remember that one man does not injure another, and that every man’s

[068-025]acts injure and profit him, I secure this, that I do not anything like him, but

[068-026]still I suffer what I do suffer through my own silly talk.

[068-027]True, but it is unfair when you have heard the secrets of your neighbor for you

[068-028]in your turn to communicate nothing to him. Did I ask you for your secrets, my

[068-029]man? did you communicate your affairs on certain terms, that you should in

[068-030]return hear mine also? If you are a babbler and think that all who meet you are

[068-031]friends, do you wish me also to be like you? But why, if you did well in

[068-032]intrusting your affairs to me, and it is not well for me to intrust mine to

[068-033]you, do you wish me to be so rash? It is just the same as if I had a cask which

[068-034]is water-tight, and you one with a hole in it, and you should come and deposit

[068-035]with me your wine that I might put it into my cask, and then should complain

[068-036]that I also did not intrust my wine to you, for you have a cask with a hole in

[068-037]it. How then is there any equality here? You intrusted your affairs to a man

[068-038]who is faithful and modest, to a man who thinks that his own actions alone are

[068-039]injurious and (or) useful, and that nothing external is. Would you have me

[068-040]intrust mine to you, a man who has dishonored his own faculty of will, and who

[068-041]wishes to gain some small bit of money or some office or promotion in the court

[068-042](emperor’s palace), even if you should be going to murder your own children,

[068-043]like Medea? Where (in what) is this equality (fairness)? But show yourself to

[068-044]me to be faithful, modest, and steady; show me that you have friendly opinions;

[068-045]show that your cask has no hole in it; and you will see how I shall not wait

[068-046]for you to trust me with your own affairs, but I myself shall come to you and

[068-047]ask you to hear mine. For who does not choose to make use of a [[good]] vessel? Who

[068-048]does not value a benevolent and faithful adviser? Who will not willingly

[068-049]receive a man who is ready to bear a share, as we may say, of the difficulty of

[068-050]his circumstances, and by this very act to ease the burden, by taking a part of

[068-051]it.
