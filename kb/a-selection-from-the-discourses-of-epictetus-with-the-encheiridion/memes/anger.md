## Definition

Anger (*orgē*) is classified in Stoic ethics as a passion — specifically, a form of desire (*epithumia*) directed at revenge or retaliation for a perceived wrong. The Stoics held that anger, like all passions, is based on a false judgment: the belief that another person's action has genuinely harmed us. Since only our own ruling faculty can harm us (by assenting to false judgments), and another person's actions are external indifferents, there is never a rational basis for anger. The Stoic sage is entirely free from anger, achieving this not through suppression but through the philosophical correction of the judgments that give rise to it.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses anger through his characteristic framework of [[appearances|impressions]] and assent. An angry person has assented to the impression that they have been wronged — a judgment that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] systematically dismantles. If someone insults or harms you, the insult is merely their own erroneous judgment; it cannot touch your moral self unless you allow it to. "Abusive words the abusive man, anger the man of an angry temper" (025-028) — the fault lies with the one who displays the vice, not with the recipient.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s therapeutic approach to anger emphasizes rapid intervention at the level of [[appearances|impressions]]. Before anger can crystallize, the student must catch the impression and refuse assent. The key is to remember, in the moment, that the other person's action is an external — indifferent to one's own virtue — and that anger only compounds the harm by damaging one's own ruling faculty.

He also situates anger within the broader Stoic framework of passions. Anger is one of the perturbations that the discipline of desire aims to eliminate. The goal is not to moderate anger but to extirpate it entirely, replacing it with the [[tranquillity|calm]] rationality proper to a human being. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] would have agreed with Seneca's judgment that anger is a form of "temporary madness," and his discourses consistently present the angry person as someone who has lost their grip on the fundamental distinction between what is and is not in their power.

## Key Passages

- (010-013) — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is asked to speak about anger, indicating its importance as a topic of moral [[education|instruction]].
- (025-028) — "Anger the man of an angry temper" — the anger belongs to the angry person, not to you.
- (020-028) — The danger of anger is that it causes us to lose confidence and act irrationally.
- (024-041) — Even the anger of God is referenced, placing human anger in cosmic perspective.
