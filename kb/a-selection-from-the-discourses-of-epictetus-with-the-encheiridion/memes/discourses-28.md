[028-001]What things a man must learn in order to be able

[028-002]to apply the art of disputation, has been accurately shown by our philosophers

[028-003](the Stoics); but with respect to the proper use of the things, we are entirely

[028-004]without practice. Only give to any of us, whom you please, an illiterate man to

[028-005]discuss with, and he cannot discover how to deal with the man. But when he has

[028-006]moved the man a little, if he answers beside the purpose, he does not know how

[028-007]to treat him, but he then either abuses or ridicules him, and says, He is an

[028-008]illiterate man; it is not possible to do anything with him. Now a guide, when

[028-009]he has found a man out of the road, leads him into the right way; he does not

[028-010]ridicule or abuse him and then leave him. Do you also show the illiterate man

[028-011]the truth, and you will see that he follows. But so long as you do not show him

[028-012]the truth, do not ridicule him, but rather feel your own incapacity.

[028-013]Now this was the first and chief peculiarity of [[socrates|Socrates]], never to be irritated

[028-014]in argument, never to utter anything abusive, anything insulting, but to bear

[028-015]with abusive persons and to put an end to the quarrel. If you would know what

[028-016]great power he had in this way, read the Symposium of Xenophon, and you will

[028-017]see how many quarrels he put an end to. Hence with [[good]] [[reason]] in the poets

[028-018]also this power is most highly praised:

[028-019]Quickly with skill he settles great disputes.

[028-020]Hesiod, Theogony, v. 87.

[028-021]ON ANXIETY (SOLICITUDE).—When I see a man anxious, I say, What does this man

[028-022]want? If he did not want something which is not in his power, how could he be

[028-023]anxious? For this reason a lute player when he is singing by himself has no

[028-024]anxiety, but when he enters the theatre, he is anxious, even if he has a good

[028-025]voice and plays well on the lute; for he not only wishes to sing well, but also

[028-026]to obtain applause: but this is not in his power. Accordingly, where he has

[028-027]skill, there he has confidence. Bring any single person who knows nothing of

[028-028]music, and the musician does not care for him. But in the matter where a man

[028-029]knows nothing and has not been practised, there he is anxious. What matter is

[028-030]this? He knows not what a crowd is or what the praise of a crowd is. However,

[028-031]he has learned to strike the lowest chord and the highest; but what the praise

[028-032]of the many is, and what power it has in life, he neither knows nor has he

[028-033]thought about it. Hence he must of necessity tremble and grow pale. Is any man

[028-034]then afraid about things which are not evils? No. Is he afraid about things

[028-035]which are evils, but still so far within his power that they may not happen?

[028-036]Certainly he is not. If then the things which are independent of the will are

[028-037]neither good nor bad, and all things which do depend on the will are within our

[028-038]power, and no man can either take them from us or give them to us, if we do not

[028-039]choose, where is room left for anxiety? But we are anxious about our poor body,

[028-040]our little property, about the will of Cæsar; but not anxious about things

[028-041]internal. Are we anxious about not forming a false opinion? No, for this is in

[028-042]my power. [[About]] not exerting our movements contrary to [[nature]]? No, not even

[028-043]about this. When then you see a man pale, as the physician says, judging from

[028-044]the complexion, this man’s spleen is disordered, that man’s liver; so also say,

[028-045]this man’s [[desire]] and aversion are disordered, he is not in the right way, he

[028-046]is in a fever. For nothing else changes the color, or causes trembling or

[028-047]chattering of the teeth, or causes a man to

[028-048]Sink in his knees and shift from foot to foot.

[028-049]Iliad, xiii., 281.

[028-050]For this reason, when Zeno was going to meet Antigonus, he was not anxious, for

[028-051]Antigonus had no power over any of the things which Zeno admired; and Zeno did

[028-052]not care for those things over which Antigonus had power. But Antigonus was

[028-053]anxious when he was going to meet Zeno, for he wished to please Zeno; but this

[028-054]was a thing external (out of his power). But Zeno did not want to please

[028-055]Antigonus; for no man who is skilled in any art wishes to please one who has no

[028-056]such skill.

[028-057]Should I try to please you? Why? I suppose, you know the measure by which one

[028-058]man is estimated by another. Have you taken pains to learn what is a good man

[028-059]and what is a bad man, and how a man becomes one or the other? Why then are you

[028-060]not good yourself? How, he replies, am I not good? Because no good man laments

[028-061]or groans or weeps, no good man is pale and trembles, or says, How will he

[028-062]receive me, how will he listen to me? Slave, just as it pleases him. Why do you

[028-063]care about what belongs to others? Is it now his fault if he receives badly

[028-064]what proceeds from you? Certainly. And is it possible that a fault should be

[028-065]one man’s, and the evil in another? No. Why then are you anxious about that

[028-066]which belongs to others? Your question is reasonable; but I am anxious how I

[028-067]shall speak to him. Cannot you then speak to him as you choose? But I fear that

[028-068]I may be disconcerted? If you are going to write the name of Dion, are you

[028-069]afraid that you would be disconcerted? By no means. Why? is it not because you

[028-070]have practised writing the name? Certainly. Well, if you were going to read the

[028-071]name, would you not feel the same? and why? Because every art has a certain

[028-072]strength and confidence in the things which belong to it. Have you then not

[028-073]practised speaking? and what else did you learn in the school? Syllogisms and

[028-074]sophistical propositions? For what purpose? was it not for the purpose of

[028-075]discoursing skilfully? and is not discoursing skilfully the same as discoursing

[028-076]seasonably and cautiously and with intelligence, and also without making

[028-077]mistakes and without hindrance, and besides all this with confidence? Yes. When

[028-078]then you are mounted on a horse and go into a plain, are you anxious at being

[028-079]matched against a man who is on foot, and anxious in a matter in which you are

[028-080]practised, and he is not? Yes, but that person (to whom I am going to speak)

[028-081]has power to kill me. Speak the truth, then, unhappy man, and do not brag, nor

[028-082]claim to be a philosopher, nor refuse to acknowledge your masters, but so long

[028-083]as you present this handle in your body, follow every man who is stronger than

[028-084]yourself. Socrates used to practice speaking, he who talked as he did to the

[028-085]tyrants, to the dicasts (judges), he who talked in his prison. Diogenes had

[028-086]practised speaking, he who spoke as he did to Alexander, to the pirates, to the

[028-087]person who bought him. These men were confident in the things which they

[028-088]practised. But do you walk off to your own affairs and never leave them: go and

[028-089]sit in a corner, and weave syllogisms, and propose them to another. There is

[028-090]not in you the man who can rule a state.
