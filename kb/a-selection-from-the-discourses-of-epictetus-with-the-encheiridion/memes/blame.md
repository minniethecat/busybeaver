## Definition

Blame (*mempsis*) in Stoic ethics is the act of assigning moral fault to another person — or to the [[divinity|gods]], or to fortune. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the refusal to blame anyone is a defining mark of philosophical progress. The person who blames others has failed to understand the Stoic doctrine that all wrongdoing results from ignorance: people err because they mistake what is truly good, not because they are willfully evil. To blame is to reveal that one's own judgment is still undisciplined.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes the prohibition of blame a central pillar of his practical ethics. The student of philosophy must "never blame him to another, nor to say or do anything like a man who has been injured" (026-025). The reason is not mere politeness but a deep philosophical conviction: blame presupposes that someone has harmed you, but no one can harm your moral character — the only thing that matters — without your consent.

The diagnosis is precise. When someone behaves badly, the untrained person blames the wrongdoer. The person making progress blames himself. The fully trained person blames neither himself nor another (see [[progress|Progress]]). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes that "we accuse, we blame, we judge" (032-007) — the unphilosophical mind is constantly assigning fault. But the philosopher, understanding that every person acts according to what appears to them to be good, finds no grounds for blame. "Whom do you blame for an act which is not his own?" (056-022). If error is ignorance, blame is incoherent.

This doctrine has a theological dimension as well. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that one must "not blame God or man" (046-041, 053-008). To blame the [[divinity|gods]] is to rebel against the cosmic order — to suppose that the universe is badly administered. The person who blames God "for these things" (019-041) has failed to grasp that the [[divinity|divine]] administration assigns to each person the circumstances most conducive to their moral development. The proper Stoic response to any event is not "Why me?" but "What use can I make of this?"

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the principle to the most ordinary interactions. "If you go and blame your brother, I say to you: You have forgotten who you are" (026-037). The person who blames has forgotten that they are a philosopher, a being whose task is to use [[appearances|impressions]] rightly, not to police the behavior of others. The freedom from blame is, ultimately, a form of [[mask|persona]]l liberation: when you stop blaming others, you stop being disturbed by their actions.

## Key Passages

- (026-025) — "Never to blame him to another, nor to say or do anything like a man who has been injured."
- (032-007) — "We accuse, we blame, we judge" — the unphilosophical mind constantly assigns fault.
- (046-041) — "To bear a fever well? Not to blame God or man; not to be disturbed."
- (053-008) — "You must not blame God or man; you must take" responsibility for your own judgments.
- (056-022) — "Whom do you blame for an act which is not his own?" — if error is ignorance, blame is incoherent.
- (031-016) — "With respect to praise and blame he has not been disciplined" — blame-impulses reflect a failure of [[education|training]].
- (031-018) — "What kind of blame should be shunned?" — the very question assumes blame can sometimes be appropriate.
- (026-037) — "If you go and blame your brother, I say to you: You have forgotten who you are."
- (019-041) — "Do you on account of these things blame God?" — the theological dimension of blame.
- (014-057) — "Then do you blame me, if I know?" — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] dramatizes the futility of blame.
