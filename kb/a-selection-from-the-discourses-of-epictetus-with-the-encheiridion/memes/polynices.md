# Polynices

## Biography

Polynices is a son of Oedipus and brother of [[eteocles|Eteocles]], driven into exile from Thebes and returning at the head of an army to reclaim the throne — the war that ended with the two brothers killing each other, told in Aeschylus's *Seven Against Thebes* and Euripides's *Phoenissae*. In the Discourses he is never named on his own; [[epictetus|Epictetus]] always cites him together with [[eteocles|Eteocles]].

Polynices is one half of the [[example|exemplar]]y pair of brothers-turned-enemies. What draws [[epictetus|Epictetus]] to him is exactly what draws him to [[eteocles|Eteocles]]: the two present the most violent possible test of his claim that conflict is generated from within.

Polynices adds one further element to the picture — exile. Where his brother held the throne, Polynices had lost it, and [[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes the pair carry a symmetrical error: the one treats exile as "the extreme of evils," the other treats royal power as "the greatest good."

Because both brothers place their good in an external thing, and each sees the other as the one who deprives him of it, they can only regard each other as enemies, "even if he be a brother."

Polynices therefore stands, in the text, for the exile's half of a false valuation — proof that a man who locates the good outside his own will is set at war even with his closest kin.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] embeds Polynices in his general law of conflict: "no man either hurts another or does good to another" (061-099); it is a man's own opinion that "overturns him; this is fighting, this is civil discord, this is war" (061-101).

The brothers become enemies through "this opinion which they had about royal power" (061-102), their valuation "that the one is the extreme of evils, the other the greatest good" (061-104); and so it is "the nature of every man to seek the good, to avoid the bad," and to treat as an enemy whoever seems to deprive him, "even if he be a brother" (061-107).

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] states the same cause plainly: "it was this which made Polynices and [[eteocles|Eteocles]] enemies, the opinion that royal power was a good" (099-021).

## References in the Discourses

- **Enmity from opinion**: Polynices and [[eteocles|Eteocles]] made enemies not by real harm but by opinion — the enemy is whoever deprives one of the good, "even if he be a brother" (061-107).
- **The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] restatement**: the brothers made enemies by "the opinion that royal power was a good" (099-021); the son reviles the father who denies him "the things which are considered to be good" (099-019).
