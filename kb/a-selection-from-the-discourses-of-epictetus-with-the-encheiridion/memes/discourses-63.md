[063-001]What makes the tyrant formidable? The guards, you say,

[063-002]and their swords, and the men of the bedchamber, and those who exclude them who

[063-003]would enter. Why then if you bring a boy (child) to the tyrant when he is with

[063-004]his guards, is he not afraid; or is it because the child does not understand

[063-005]these things? If then any man does understand what guards are and that they

[063-006]have swords, and comes to the tyrant for this very purpose because he wishes to

[063-007]die on account of some circumstance and seeks to die easily by the hand of

[063-008]another, is he afraid of the guards? No, for he wishes for the thing which

[063-009]makes the guards formidable. If then any man neither wishing to die nor to live

[063-010]by all means, but only as it may be permitted, approaches the tyrant what

[063-011]hinders him from approaching the tyrant without fear? Nothing. If then a man

[063-012]has the same opinion about his property as the man whom I have instanced has

[063-013]about his body; and also about his children and his wife, and in a word is so

[063-014]affected by some madness or despair that he cares not whether he possesses them

[063-015]or not, but like children who are playing with shells (quarrel) about the play,

[063-016]but do not trouble themselves about the shells, so he too has set no value on

[063-017]the materials (things), but values the pleasure that he has with them and the

[063-018]occupation, what tyrant is then formidable to him, or what guards or what

[063-019]swords?

[063-020]What hinders a man, who has clearly separated (comprehended) these things, from

[063-021]living with a light heart and bearing easily the reins, quietly expecting

[063-022]everything which can happen, and enduring that which has already happened?

[063-023]Would you have me to bear poverty? Come and you will know what poverty is when

[063-024]it has found one who can act well the part of a poor man. Would you have me to

[063-025]possess power? Let me have power, and also the trouble of it. Well, banishment?

[063-026]Wherever I shall go, there it will be well with me; for here also where I am,

[063-027]it was not because of the place that it was well with me, but because of my

[063-028]opinions which I shall carry off with me, for neither can any man deprive me of

[063-029]them; but my opinions alone are mine and they cannot be taken from me, and I am

[063-030]satisfied while I have them, wherever I may be and whatever I am doing. But now

[063-031]it is time to die. Why do you say to die? Make no tragedy show of the thing,

[063-032]but speak of it as it is. It is now time for the matter (of the body) to be

[063-033]resolved into the things out of which it was composed. And what is the

[063-034]formidable thing here? what is going to perish of the things which are in the

[063-035]universe? what new thing or wondrous is going to happen? Is it for this [[reason]]

[063-036]that a tyrant is formidable? Is it for this reason that the guards appear to

[063-037]have swords which are large and sharp? Say this to others; but I have

[063-038]considered about all these things; no man has power over me. I have been made

[063-039]free; I know his commands, no man can now lead me as a slave. I have a proper

[063-040]person to assert my freedom; I have proper judges. (I say) are you not the

[063-041]master of my body? What then is that to me? Are you not the master of my

[063-042]property? What then is that to me? Are you not the master of my exile or of my

[063-043]chains? Well, from all these things and all the poor body itself I depart at

[063-044]your bidding, when you please. Make trial of your power, and you will know how

[063-045]far it reaches.

[063-046]Whom then can I still fear? Those who are over the bedchamber? Lest they should

[063-047]do, what? Shut me out? If they find that I wish to enter, let them shut me out.

[063-048]Why then do you go to the doors? Because I think it befits me, while the play

[063-049](sport) lasts, to join in it. How then are you not shut out? Because unless

[063-050]some one allows me to go in, I do not choose to go in, but am always content

[063-051]with that which happens; for I think that what God chooses is better than what

[063-052]I choose. I will attach myself as a minister and follower to him; I have the

[063-053]same movements (pursuits) as he has, I have the same desires; in a word, I have

[063-054]the same will ([Greek: sunthelo]). There is no shutting out for me, but for

[063-055]those who would force their way in. Why then do not I force my way in? Because

[063-056]I know that nothing [[good]] is distributed within to those who enter. But when I

[063-057]hear any man called fortunate because he is honored by Cæsar, I say what does

[063-058]he happen to get? A province (the government of a province). Does he also

[063-059]obtain an opinion such as he ought? The office of a Prefect. Does he also

[063-060]obtain the power of using his office well? Why do I still strive to enter

[063-061](Cæsar’s chamber)? A man scatters dried figs and nuts: the children seize them,

[063-062]and fight with one another; men do not, for they think them to be a small

[063-063]matter. But if a man should throw about shells, even the children do not seize

[063-064]them. Provinces are distributed: let children look to that. Money is

[063-065]distributed; let children look to that. Prætorships, consulships, are

[063-066]distributed; let children scramble for them, let them be shut out, beaten, kiss

[063-067]the hands of the giver, of the slaves: but to me these are only dried figs and

[063-068]nuts. What then? If you fail to get them, while Cæsar is scattering them about,

[063-069]do not be troubled; if a dried fig come into your lap, take it and eat it; for

[063-070]so far you may value even a fig. But if I shall stoop down and turn another

[063-071]over, or be turned over by another, and shall flatter those who have got into

[063-072](Cæsar’s) chamber, neither is a dried fig worth the trouble, nor anything else

[063-073]of the things which are not good, which the philosophers have persuaded me not

[063-074]to think good.
