[041-001]The material for the wise and [[good]] man is his

[041-002]own ruling faculty: and the body is the material for the physician and the

[041-003]aliptes (the man who oils persons); the land is the matter for the husbandman.

[041-004]The business of the wise and good man is to use appearances conformably to

[041-005][[nature]]: and as it is the nature of every soul to [[assent]] to the truth, to

[041-006]dissent from the false, and to remain in suspense as to that which is

[041-007]uncertain; so it is its nature to be moved towards the [[desire]] for the good, and

[041-008]to aversion from the evil; and with respect to that which is neither good nor

[041-009]bad it feels indifferent. For as the money-changer (banker) is not allowed to

[041-010]reject Cæsar’s coin, nor the seller of herbs, but if you show the coin, whether

[041-011]he chooses or not, he must give up what is sold for the coin; so it is also in

[041-012]the matter of the soul. When the good appears, it immediately attracts to

[041-013]itself; the evil repels from itself. But the soul will never reject the

[041-014]manifest appearance of the good, any more than persons will reject Cæsar’s

[041-015]coin. On this principle depends every movement both of man and God.

[041-016]Against (or with respect to) this kind of thing chiefly a man should exercise

[041-017]himself. As soon as you go out in the morning, examine every man whom you see,

[041-018]every man whom you hear; answer as to a question, What have you seen? A

[041-019]handsome man or woman? Apply the rule. Is this independent of the will, or

[041-020]dependent? Independent. Take it away. What have you seen? A man lamenting over

[041-021]the death of a child. Apply the rule. Death is a thing independent of the will.

[041-022]Take it away. Has the proconsul met you? Apply the rule. What kind of a thing

[041-023]is a proconsul’s office? Independent of the will or dependent on it?

[041-024]Independent. Take this away also; it does not stand examination; cast it away;

[041-025]it is nothing to you.

[041-026]If we practised this and exercised ourselves in it daily from morning to night,

[041-027]something indeed would be done. But now we are forthwith caught half asleep by

[041-028]every appearance, and it is only, if ever, that in the school we are roused a

[041-029]little. Then when we go out, if we see a man lamenting, we say, He is undone.

[041-030]If we see a consul, we say, He is happy. If we see an exiled man, we say, He is

[041-031]miserable. If we see a poor man, we say, He is wretched; he has nothing to eat.

[041-032]We ought then to eradicate these bad opinions, and to this end we should direct

[041-033]all our efforts. For what is weeping and lamenting? Opinion. What is bad

[041-034]fortune? Opinion. What is civil sedition, what is divided opinion, what is

[041-035]blame, what is accusation, what is impiety, what is trifling? All these things

[041-036]are opinions, and nothing more, and opinions about things independent of the

[041-037]will, as if they were good and bad. Let a man transfer these opinions to things

[041-038]dependent on the will, and I engage for him that he will be firm and constant,

[041-039]whatever may be the state of things around him. Such as is a dish of water,

[041-040]such is the soul. Such as is the ray of light which falls on the water, such

[041-041]are the appearances. When the water is moved, the ray also seems to be moved,

[041-042]yet it is not moved. And when then a man is seized with giddiness, it is not

[041-043]the arts and the virtues which are confounded, but the spirit (the nervous

[041-044]power) on which they are impressed; but if the spirit be restored to its

[041-045]settled state, those things also are restored.
