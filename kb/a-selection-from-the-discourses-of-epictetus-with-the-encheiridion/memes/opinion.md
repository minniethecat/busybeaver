## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, an opinion (*[[doctrine|dogma]]* in Greek, δόγμα) is a settled judgment or belief about the value of things — whether something is good, evil, or indifferent. Unlike a fleeting impression (*phantasia*), an opinion is a proposition to which the mind has given its assent.

The Stoics held that all human passions and disturbances arise not from external events themselves but from the opinions we hold about them. This is the foundation of Stoic cognitive therapy: change one's opinions, and one changes one's emotional life.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] places the concept of opinion at the very center of his ethical teaching. The famous opening of the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]'s first principle rests on it: "It is not things themselves that disturb men, but their opinions about things" (012-024).

This insight structures [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire approach to moral [[education|training]]. When a student reports being troubled by poverty, exile, or the fear of death, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not dispute the external facts — he examines the underlying opinion. "Why then are you troubled? Because you have an opinion" (018-022).

In his analysis, all emotional disturbance can be traced to specific erroneous opinions. The tyrant frightens us because we hold the opinion that he has power over what is truly ours. But once we understand that the will [[solitude|alone]] is in our power, the opinion that externals can harm us loses its grip: "We are disturbed, we are confounded, we are ruined; that is, our opinions" (016-035).

The Stoic education (*[[education|paideia]]*) is fundamentally a process of replacing false opinions with true ones. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents the philosopher as one who has learned to examine his opinions critically, testing them against the standard of reason: "In a word, it is the nature of every soul to assent to the truth, to dissent from the false" (041-005).

## Key Passages

> It is not things themselves that disturb men, but their opinions about things. For example, death is nothing terrible; for if it were, it would have appeared so to Socrates. But the opinion about death — that it is terrible — that is the terrible thing. (012-024–012-025)

> What then disturbs men's minds? An opinion. For when a man goes away leaving his friend, what disturbs him? His opinion. Why then are you troubled? Because you have an opinion. (018-022)
