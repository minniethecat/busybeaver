# Appearances

**Appearances** (*phantasiai*, also translated as "impressions" or "representations") are the way things present themselves to the mind. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]] and the Stoics, the entire ethical life turns on how we handle appearances — whether we accept them uncritically or subject them to rational examination. "The [[nature]] of [[good]] is in the use of appearances" (020-010).

Every appearance carries a propositional content: it presents something as true or false, [[good]] or evil, to be pursued or avoided. The disciplined mind does not simply react to how things appear, but pauses to test the appearance against its [[preconceptions]] before giving or withholding [[assent]].

## Definition

In Stoic psychology, an appearance (*phantasia*) is an impression on the soul (*tupōsis en psychē*), originally compared by Zeno to the impression of a seal on wax. Later Stoics refined the concept: an appearance is an alteration of the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*) that presents some state of affairs to consciousness.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] classifies appearances into four kinds: "Either things appear as they are; or they are not, and do not even appear to be; or they are, and do not appear to be; or they are not, and yet appear to be" (017-001). The task of the educated person — the philosopher — is to form a right [[judgment]] in each case. This fourfold classification reveals that the problem is not simply distinguishing truth from falsehood, but handling all the ways in which reality and appearance can diverge.

Crucially, the Stoics held that we are not compelled by appearances. Between the appearance and our response stands the faculty of [[assent]], which can always pause and examine before reacting. This gap — the "space of reasons," as contemporary philosophers call it — is the arena of human [[freedom]].

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] builds his entire practical philosophy around the management of appearances. The first discipline — desire — has its work in the realm of appearance: learning not to be carried away by how things seem but to test every impression against the standard of what is truly good.

He teaches that appearances gain their power over us through habit. "When you have once desired money, if [[reason]] be applied to lead to a perception of the evil, the [[desire]] is stopped" (033-014). But if reason is not applied, the appearance of money as good "is inflamed to desire quicker than before" (033-018). Each uncritical acceptance of a deceptive appearance strengthens the habit, until the mind is hardened and the "disease of the mind confirms the love of money" (033-020).

The remedy [[epictetus|Epictetus]] prescribes is twofold. First, we must have clear preconceptions ready to hand — standards by which to test appearances (017-016). Second, we must practise opposing one habit with another: when appearances present death as an evil, we must have ready the counter-thought that death is a necessary and natural thing (017-018).

## Key Passages

- Appearances come in four ways: true, false, hidden, and deceptive — the philosopher's task is right judgment in each case (017-001).
- "The nature of good is in the use of appearances" — the foundation of Epictetan ethics (020-010).
- [[reason|Reason]] applied to desire "stops" it, but without reason it is "inflamed to desire quicker than before" (033-014, 033-018).
- Clear preconceptions are the remedy against persuasive but deceptive appearances (017-016).
