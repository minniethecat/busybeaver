[045-001]When a certain

[045-002]person came to him, who was going up to [[rome|Rome]] on account of a suit which had

[045-003]regard to his rank, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] inquired the [[reason]] of his going to Rome, and the

[045-004]man then asked what he thought about the matter. Epictetus replied: If you ask

[045-005]me what you will do in Rome, whether you will succeed or fail, I have no rule

[045-006]([Greek: theoraema]) about this. But if you ask me how you will fare, I can

[045-007]tell you: if you have right opinions ([Greek: dogmata]), you will fare well; if

[045-008]they are false, you will fare ill. For to every man the cause of his acting is

[045-009]opinion. For what is the reason why you desired to be elected governor of the

[045-010]Cnossians? Your opinion. What is the reason that you are now going up to Rome?

[045-011]Your opinion. And going in winter, and with danger and expense? I must go. What

[045-012]tells you this? Your opinion. Then if opinions are the causes of all actions,

[045-013]and a man has bad opinions, such as the cause may be, such also is the effect!

[045-014]Have we then all sound opinions, both you and your adversary? And how do you

[045-015]differ? But have you sounder opinions than your adversary? Why? You think so.

[045-016]And so does he think that his opinions are better; and so do madmen. This is a

[045-017]bad criterion. But show to me that you have made some inquiry into your

[045-018]opinions and have taken some pains about them. And as now you are sailing to

[045-019]Rome in order to become governor of the Cnossians, and you are not content to

[045-020]stay at home with the honors which you had, but you [[desire]] something greater

[045-021]and more conspicuous, so when did you ever make a voyage for the purpose of

[045-022]examining your own opinions, and casting them out, if you have any that are

[045-023]bad? Whom have you approached for this purpose? What time have you fixed for

[045-024]it? What age? Go over the times of your life by yourself, if you are ashamed of

[045-025]me (knowing the fact) when you were a boy, did you examine your own opinions?

[045-026]and did you not then, as you do all things now, do as you did do? and when you

[045-027]were become a youth and attended the rhetoricians, and yourself practised

[045-028]rhetoric, what did you imagine that you were deficient in? And when you were a

[045-029]young man and engaged in public matters, and pleaded causes yourself, and were

[045-030]gaining reputation, who then seemed your equal? And when would you have

[045-031]submitted to any man examining and showing that your opinions are bad? What

[045-032]then do you wish me to say to you? Help me in this matter. I have no theorem

[045-033](rule) for this. Nor have you, if you came to me for this purpose, come to me

[045-034]as a philosopher, but as to a seller of vegetables or a shoemaker. For what

[045-035]purpose then have philosophers theorems? For this purpose, that whatever may

[045-036]happen, our ruling faculty may be and continue to be conformable to [[nature]].

[045-037]Does this seem to you a small thing? No; but the greatest. What then? does it

[045-038]need only a short time? and is it possible to seize it as you pass by? If you

[045-039]can, seize it.

[045-040]Then you will say, I met with Epictetus as I should meet with a stone or a

[045-041]statue: for you saw me and nothing more. But he meets with a man as a man, who

[045-042]learns his opinions, and in his turn shows his own. Learn my opinions: show me

[045-043]yours; and then say that you have visited me. Let us examine one another: if I

[045-044]have any bad opinion, take it away; if you have any, show it. This is the

[045-045]meaning of meeting with a philosopher. Not so (you say): but this is only a

[045-046]passing visit, and while we are hiring the vessel, we can also see Epictetus.

[045-047]Let us see what he says. Then you go away and say: Epictetus was nothing; he

[045-048]used solecisms and spoke in a barbarous way. For of what else do you come as

[045-049]judges? Well, but a man may say to me, if I attend to such matters (as you do),

[045-050]I shall have no land as you have none; I shall have no silver cups as you have

[045-051]none, nor fine beasts as you have none. In answer to tins it is perhaps

[045-052]sufficient to say: I have no need of such things; but if you possess many

[045-053]things you have need of others: whether you choose or not, you are poorer than

[045-054]I am. What then have I need of? Of that which you have not? of firmness, of a

[045-055]mind which is conformable to nature, of being free from perturbation.
