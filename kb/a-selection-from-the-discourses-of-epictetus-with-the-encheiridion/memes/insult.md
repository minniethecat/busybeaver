# Insult

An **insult**, in the Stoic ethics of [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is a speech-act that another person intends as harmful but which has no power to harm the moral character of the recipient unless that recipient judges it to be an injury. The treatment of insult in the Discourses and [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] is one of the clearest applications of the fundamental Stoic principle that "it is not things that disturb men, but the opinions about things" (069-001).

To be immune to insult is not to be insensitive; it is to understand correctly where harm actually resides. The body can be struck, property can be taken, reputation can be attacked — but none of these is the self. The self is the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*), and it can only be harmed by its own false judgments.

## Definition

In ordinary usage, an insult is language or conduct intended to wound another's dignity or self-esteem. For the Stoic, however, the crucial question is: what is actually wounded? If one's sense of worth is attached to externals — reputation, the body, social standing — then an insult can reach its target. But if one's worth is placed solely in virtue, which no external agent can touch, then the insult falls on nothing. "Remember that it is not he who reviles you or strikes you, who insults you, but it is your opinion about these things as being insulting" (088-001).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses insult across three distinct passages, each adding a layer to the Stoic response.

**The Socratic [[example|model]]: treating insult as irrelevant**. In Discourse 16, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] recounts the [[exercise|practice]] of [[socrates|Socrates]] when someone attempted to provoke him: "Strip him. What do you mean by him? Lay hold of his garment, strip it off. I have insulted you. Much good may it do you" (016-041). The remark is Socratic irony turned into a spiritual exercise. The insulter believes he has harmed [[socrates|Socrates]]; [[socrates|Socrates]], knowing that the body is not the self, treats the act as a meaningless gesture. The garment can be taken, but the man cannot be touched.

**[[endurance|Endurance]] as the philosophical response**. In Discourse 28, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] holds up the capacity to bear insult as a distinguishing mark of the philosopher: "not to return anything abusive, anything insulting, but to bear" (028-014). The verb "to bear" (*pherein*) is central to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s vocabulary of endurance. The philosopher does not retaliate, does not even feel the impulse to retaliate, because he sees the insult for what it is — a product of the insulter's own ignorance and disturbance.

**The role of opinion in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]**. The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] returns to the theme with a definitive formulation: "Remember that it is not he who reviles you or strikes you, who insults you, but it is your opinion about these things as being insulting" (088-001). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] then offers practical advice for the moment of provocation: "Therefore especially try not to be carried away by the appearance. For if you once gain time and delay, you will more easily master yourself" (088-004). The discipline is cognitive — one interrupts the automatic judgment of injury and replaces it with a Stoic principle.

## Key Passages

> Strip him. What do you mean by him? Lay hold of his garment, strip it off. I have insulted you. Much good may it do you. (016-041)

> Not to return anything abusive, anything insulting, but to bear. (028-014)

> Remember that it is not he who reviles you or strikes you, who insults you, but it is your opinion about these things as being insulting. When then a man irritates you, you must know that it is your own opinion which has irritated you. (088-001–003)
