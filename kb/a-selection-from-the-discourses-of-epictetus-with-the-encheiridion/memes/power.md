# Power

**Power** (*dynamis*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is the central concept around which the entire ethical edifice is built. The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] opens with the distinction that defines the Stoic life: "Of things some are in our power, others are not. In our power are opinion, desire, aversion, and in a word, whatever are our own acts; not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices, and in a word, whatever are not our own acts." This is not merely a classification; it is a declaration of where genuine power resides.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], most people are powerless precisely because they seek power in the wrong place. The tyrant who can imprison a body cannot touch the will; the slave in chains who has trained his judgment is freer than the emperor who has not. "My will not even [[zeus|Zeus]] himself can overpower" (001-027). True power is cognitive — the capacity to judge correctly and to desire only what is appropriate.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, power (*dynamis*, *to eph' hēmin*) denotes the sphere of the voluntary — those mental acts that depend solely on the rational faculty and cannot be hindered by any external force. These include judgment (*hypolēpsis*), desire (*[[desire|orexis]]*), aversion (*[[aversion|ekklisis]]*), and impulse (*hormē*). Everything external to this sphere — health, wealth, reputation, the actions of others, even one's own body — is classified as "not in our power" (*ouk eph' hēmin*).

The distinction is exhaustive and exclusive. Nothing is partly in our power and partly not; either a thing depends entirely on our will or it depends on factors beyond our control. The practical consequence is that all emotional disturbance arises from treating something not in our power as if it were.

## Epictetus's Treatment

**Power and the tyrant**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly stages confrontations with tyrants to demonstrate the nature of real power. A tyrant says, "I am master of all." The philosopher replies: "Can you give me desire which shall have no hindrance? Have you the infallible power of avoiding what you would avoid?" (012-005). The tyrant's power is merely over bodies and property — things that are indifferent. Over the one thing that matters — the ruling faculty — he has no power whatsoever.

**Power as capacity for use**: In Discourse 6, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents a theological [[disputation|argument]]. [[animal|Animal]]s are "adapted only to use"; human beings have "also the power of understanding the use" (006-054). This capacity to reflect on and choose how to employ externals is the distinctive human power, and exercising it well is the human function (*ergon*).

**The unassailable will**: The strongest statement comes early in Discourse 1: "You may fetter my leg, but my will not even [[zeus|Zeus]] himself can overpower" (001-027). This is not bravado but a logical consequence of the Stoic ontology of mind. The will (*prohairesis*) is by nature free, because it judges only itself; no external cause can compel a judgment.

**Power and education**: In a dense passage, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] defines education itself as learning "that of things some are in our power, but others are not" (012-034). All philosophical [[education|instruction]] reduces to this distinction. To be educated is to know where to invest effort; to be uneducated is to waste effort on what cannot be controlled.

## Key Passages

> I will not, for this is in my power. But I will put you in chains. Man, what are you talking about? Me, in chains? You may fetter my leg, but my will not even Zeus himself can overpower. (001-025)

> Permit us to show them that they have no power over any man. (007-019)

> He who desires or avoids the things which are not in his power can neither be faithful nor free. (004-049)

> Education is the learning...to distinguish that of things some are in our power, but others are not. (012-034)
