## Definition

Self-conceit (Greek: **oiēsis**, οἴησις) is the conviction that one already possesses knowledge or virtue that one in fact lacks. It is not mere vanity but a specific cognitive error: the false belief that one has correctly applied one's natural preconceptions to particulars.

Self-conceit is the primary obstacle to beginning philosophy, because a person who thinks they already know will not undertake the effort to learn.

> "What is the first business of him who philosophizes? To throw away self-conceit (οἴησις). For it is impossible for a man to begin to learn that which he thinks that he knows." (032-002)

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies self-conceit as what humans add to their natural preconceptions about good and bad, beautiful and ugly (027-017). We all possess these innate notions, but we add the false belief that we already know how to apply them correctly.

The first business of philosophy is "to throw away self-conceit" (032-002). People come to philosophers praising and blaming, judging honorable and dishonorable — all on unexamined assumptions about what these words mean (032-005).

The problem is not that we lack natural preconceptions. Every person has a notion of health, justice, and the good. But having a preconception differs from knowing how to apply it correctly to particular cases (032-018).

## Stoic Context

The Stoic conception of oiēsis builds on the Socratic tradition of knowing one's own ignorance. It is not merely an intellectual failing but a moral one — it blocks moral progress because the self-conceited person cannot receive correction.

Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] emphasized recognizing one's own ignorance as the foundation of virtue. The term appears in Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*, where the emperor guards against the self-conceit that comes with power.

## Key Passages

Discourse 27 (027-017) explains how self-conceit arises: humans add conceit to their natural preconceptions. The problem lies in unsuitable application, not the preconceptions themselves.

Discourse 32 (032-001) opens with the declaration that throwing away self-conceit is the first task of philosophy. It is impossible to learn what one thinks one already knows.

Discourse 32 (032-015) notes that preconceptions are natural, but adapting them correctly is difficult — like people using medical terms before Hippocrates without proper understanding.
