# Purity

**Purity** (*katharotēs*) in Stoic thought is the condition of a soul that holds correct opinions about what is [[good]] and what is evil. It has nothing to do with ritual washing or bodily cleanliness. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], as for the earlier Stoic tradition, purity is entirely a matter of the ruling faculty's judgments.

The [[divinity|gods]] themselves are the [[example|model]] of purity — "by their nature pure and free from corruption" (066-011) — and the human task is to approximate this [[divinity|divine]] condition through the discipline of right [[reason]].

## Definition

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] offers the clearest Stoic definition of purity in Discourse 66: "the impurity of the soul is the soul's bad opinions; and the purification of the soul is the planting in it of proper opinions; and the soul is pure which has proper opinions" (066-025–066-026).

This is a radical redefinition of purity. In the popular religion of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s time, purity was a matter of avoiding pollution — touching corpses, eating forbidden foods, entering temples in a state of ritual uncleanness. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] dismisses this external conception entirely. The only pollution that matters is the pollution of false [[judgment]].

The Stoic theory of purity rests on the doctrine that the soul's acts — [[desire]], aversion, preparation, purpose, and [[assent]] — can be either clean or filthy depending on the opinions that guide them (066-022–066-023).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes Discourse 66 entirely to this theme, arguing that true purity is achieved through philosophical discipline, not through external observances. Human nature, being "mixed of such materials," cannot be "altogether pure" (066-014); but [[reason]] is applied "as far as it is possible" to make human nature love purity.

The connection to the three disciplines is explicit: the purification of desire means desiring only what is within the will's power; the purification of impulse means acting in accordance with one's roles; the purification of assent means refusing to accept any impression without examination.

In Discourse 53, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the metaphor to the philosopher's vocation: "In the first place then you must make your ruling faculty pure, and this mode of life also" (053-028). One cannot supervise the moral condition of others — the Cynic's task — without first having purified one's own ruling faculty.

The discourse on purity also connects to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s theology: one approaches the [[divinity|gods]] through purity of reason, not through external rites. "Since the [[divinity|gods]] by their nature are pure and free from corruption, so far as men approach them by reason, so far do they cling to purity" (066-011–066-012).

## Key Passages

> Since the gods by their nature are pure and free from corruption, so far as men approach them by reason, so far do they cling to purity and to a love of purity. (066-011–066-012)

> The impurity of the soul is the soul's bad opinions; and the purification of the soul is the planting in it of proper opinions; and the soul is pure which has proper opinions, for the soul alone in her own acts is free from perturbation and pollution. (066-025–066-027)

> In the first place then you must make your ruling faculty pure, and this mode of life also. (053-028)
