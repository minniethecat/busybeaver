## Definition

Pain (*ponos*) is one of the classic Stoic "indifferents" (*adiaphora*) — a physical sensation that is neither good nor evil in itself. In the Stoic system, pain is classified as a "dispreferred indifferent" (*apoproēgmenon*): it is naturally aversive and rational to avoid when possible, but it cannot harm the moral character, and its endurance can be an opportunity for the exercise of virtue. What troubles human beings, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists, is not pain itself but the judgment that pain is an evil.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats pain as a testing-ground for Stoic [[exercise|practice]]. He famously says: "What is pain? A mask. Turn it, examine it, and it does not bite" (020-059). The soldier of philosophy must face pain and not flinch — this is the mark of someone who has internalized Stoic principles. When pain appears, the proper response is not [[grief|lamentation]] ("Alas!") but a [[tranquillity|calm]] assessment of the impression.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s approach is strikingly practical. He addresses common pains — headache, earache — and teaches that the disciplined student should not say "Alas! I have a pain in my head" or "Alas! I have a pain in the ear" (011-034, 011-035). The exclamation itself reinforces the false judgment that pain is an evil. Instead, one should accept the sensation without adding the layer of [[grief|distress]] that comes from judgment.

The Stoic teacher acknowledges that the "animal man" is "pained by nothing so much as by that which is irrational" (002-007), but the philosophical man distinguishes between the raw sensation and the judgment about it. The fear of pain, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues, is often more formidable than pain itself: "For death or pain is not formidable, but the fear of pain or death" (020-042, 020-043). When pain arrives, the philosopher asks: "What has this spy said about pain?" (015-017) — treating the impression as information to be examined, not a command to be obeyed.

## Key Passages

- (020-059) — "What is pain? A mask. Turn it, examine it, and it does not bite."
- (002-007) — The animal man is "pained by nothing so much as by that which is irrational."
- (020-042) — "For death or pain is not formidable, but the fear of pain or death."
- (020-043) — The fear of pain is what troubles us, not the sensation itself.
- (011-034) — "I have a pain in the head. Have you any pain in the head? Do not say 'Alas!'"
- (011-035) — "I have a pain in the ear. Do not say 'Alas!'"
- (015-017) — "What has this spy said about pain?" — examining the impression.
- (045-018) — "You have opinions and have taken some pains about them."
- (046-022) — On each "painful thing" occurring, the student is to recall that preparation was made for this moment.
- (054-139) — "When the appearance (of things) pains you" — it is not the thing but the appearance that causes disturbance.
