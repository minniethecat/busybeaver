# Voyage

The **voyage** (*plous*) is one of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most versatile metaphors for human life. Drawing on the Mediterranean experience of sea travel — with its harbors, storms, steersmen, and disembarkations — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the voyage to illuminate the Stoic's relationship to external circumstances, [[divinity|divine]] providence, and the moment of death.

The metaphor's power lies in its realism. A sea voyage is not within the passenger's [[power|control]]: the steersman chooses the course, the winds determine the speed, and storms may alter the plan without warning. The passenger's only genuine choice is how to respond — to cooperate with the voyage or to struggle uselessly against it.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The most developed version of the metaphor appears in Discourse 22, where [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the Stoic art of managing desire: "As we do in the case of a voyage" (022-023). When one boards a ship, one does not cling to the shore or fixate on a single harbor. One surveys the possible destinations, recognizes that storms may force a different landing, and accepts the steersman's judgment.

The question "What can I do?" is answered with precision: "I can choose the steersman, the sailors, the day, the moment" (022-023). But once the ship has sailed, the passenger's role is acceptance. A storm arises — one does not curse the steersman or leap overboard. One sits [[tranquillity|calm]]ly, recognizing that the safety of the ship is the steersman's concern, not the passenger's.

In Discourse 75, the metaphor takes on its eschatological dimension: "As on a voyage when the vessel has reached a harbor, if you go out to get water, you may pick up a shell or a bulb by the way, but you must keep your mind fixed on the ship" (075-001). The harbor is death or the [[divinity|divine]] destination; the shells and bulbs are the pleasant externals one may gather along the way; but the mind must never lose sight of the ship and the impending call of the steersman.

The voyage appears alongside other trials — "voyages by sea, and winds, and various accidents" (054-056) — as one of the indifferent circumstances that test Stoic resolve. And in Discourse 45, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reproaches those who make voyages for trivial purposes but never undertake the inward voyage of self-examination: "When did you ever make a voyage for the purpose of examining your own opinions?" (045-021).

## Key Passages

- The art of voyaging: "As we do in the case of a voyage. What can I do? I can choose the steersman, the sailors, the day, the moment" (022-023).
- Trials of the sea: "Must another have a fever, another sail on the sea, another die?" (022-050), listing the voyage among universal trials.
- The self-examination voyage: "When did you ever make a voyage for the purpose of examining your own opinions?" (045-021).
- Winds and accidents: "Voyages by sea, and winds, and various accidents" (054-056).
- The shell-gatherer's distraction: "As on a voyage when the vessel has reached a harbor..." (075-001).
