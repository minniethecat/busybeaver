# Wisdom

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, **wisdom** (*sophia*) is the cardinal virtue that consists in the knowledge of what is truly good, evil, and indifferent. Together with justice (*dikaiosynē*), courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*), and temperance (*sōphrosynē*), it is one of the four virtues into which the single, unified virtue is analyzed.

Wisdom is not mere cleverness or learning but the practical knowledge that governs correct assent, desire, and action — the knowledge possessed fully only by the ideal **sage** (*sophos*).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] rarely invokes "wisdom" as an abstract noun; instead he speaks constantly of the "wise and good man" (*spoudaios*), the person who has attained this knowledge. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]] the wise man's distinctive mark is that he works upon the right material with the right art:

> "The material for the wise and good man is his own ruling faculty: and the body is the material for the physician... The business of the wise and good man is to use appearances conformably to nature." (041-001, 041-004)

This "business" is precise. Because every soul by nature "assent[s] to the truth, dissent[s] from the false, and remain[s] in suspense as to that which is uncertain" (041-004), wisdom is the correct performance of these very acts of the mind. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] grounds wisdom in the three disciplines of [[education|training]]: "a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and good.

The first concerns the desires and the aversions... The second concerns the movements towards an object" (040-002). Wisdom is thus inseparable from [[exercise|practice]] (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*).

Because wisdom perfects the whole person, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] can make the striking claim that even love is its prerogative: "To love, then, is only in the power of the wise. For universally... every animal is attached to nothing so much as to its own interests" (035-009) — only the wise can love without betraying their own good.

Finally, he warns that mere aspiration falls short: as a pilot needs more than the wish to sail, so "in philosophy also [it may] not be sufficient to wish to be wise" (029-029).

## Key Passages

- **The wise man's material and business**: "The material for the wise and good man is his own ruling faculty... The business of the wise and good man is to use appearances conformably to nature" (041-001, 041-004).

- **The disciplines of the wise man**: "a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and good. The first concerns the desires and the aversions" (040-002).

- **[[love|Love]] belongs to the wise [[solitude|alone]]**: "To love, then, is only in the power of the wise" (035-009).

- **Wishing is not enough**: "not be sufficient to wish to be wise" (029-029).

## Related Concepts

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — Wisdom is the leading cardinal virtue, the knowledge that unifies the other three.

- **[[sage|Sage]]** (*sophos*) — The ideal possessor of perfect wisdom.

- **[[reason|Reason]]** (*logos*) — The faculty wisdom perfects; the wise man reasons correctly about good and evil.

- **[[temperance|Temperance]]** / **[[courage|Courage]]** / **[[justice|Justice]]** — The remaining cardinal virtues, aspects of the one wisdom.
