## Definition

In Stoic physics, [[necessity|fate]] (*heimarmenē*) is the unbreakable chain of causation through which all events in the cosmos unfold according to [[divinity|divine]] reason (*logos*). It is not a blind or arbitrary force but the expression of a providential intelligence that orders everything for the best. The Stoics identified [[necessity|fate]] with [[zeus|Zeus]] himself — or more precisely, with the rational principle that pervades and governs the universe.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherits this doctrine but inflects it in a distinctively practical direction. For him, the philosophical significance of [[necessity|fate]] lies not in cosmological speculation but in its implications for human freedom and emotional well-being. If one truly understands that everything happens according to a [[divinity|divine]] plan, one can face any circumstance — even death — with equanimity. The key is to align one's own will with the universal will: to want things to happen exactly as they do happen.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats [[necessity|fate]] primarily through the lens of willing acceptance. He quotes with approval Cleanthes' famous prayer: "Lead me, O [[zeus|Zeus]], and thou too Destiny, to whatever place I am by you assigned" (120-002; cf. 036-083). This act of voluntary consent transforms the person from a passive victim of circumstance into an active participant in the cosmic order. The Stoic does not merely endure [[necessity|fate]] — they embrace it.

The distinction is crucial. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not counsel passive resignation. Rather, he insists that the sphere of choice (*prohairesis*) remains free even within the causal [[necessity|determinism]] of [[necessity|fate]]. One cannot [[power|control]] whether it is "[[necessity|fate]]d" to become ill, to lose wealth, or to die — but one can [[power|control]] how one responds to these events. "If I knew that it was [[necessity|fate]]d for me to be sick, I would even wish to be sick; for the foot also, if it had understanding, would wish to be covered with mud" (023-024). The part must will what the whole requires.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also connects [[necessity|fate]] to his theological vision of [[zeus|Zeus]] as a wise and benevolent ruler. The things established by "[[zeus|Zeus]], which he, with the Moirae ([[necessity|fate]]s) who were present and were assisting him" (008-060) are not arbitrary decrees but the necessary conditions of a well-ordered cosmos. To fight against [[necessity|fate]] is to fight against God — a futile and impious enterprise that only generates suffering.

## Stoic Context

The doctrine of [[necessity|fate]] (*heimarmenē*) was a cornerstone of Stoic physics from the school's founding. Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] both defended a strong causal [[necessity|determinism]]: every event has a prior cause, and the entire sequence of causes forms an unbreakable chain. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] in particular worked to reconcile [[necessity|determinism]] with moral responsibility through his doctrine of "co-[[necessity|fate]]d" events and the distinction between external and internal causes.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] simplifies this complex metaphysical apparatus for his pedagogical purposes. He assumes the Stoic physics of [[necessity|fate]] as background but focuses on the ethical response: what matters is not whether [[necessity|fate]] exists (it does) but whether one assents to it willingly or resists it futilely. This practical focus reflects his Roman context, where [[stoicism|Stoicism]] was primarily a guide to life rather than a system of speculative philosophy.
