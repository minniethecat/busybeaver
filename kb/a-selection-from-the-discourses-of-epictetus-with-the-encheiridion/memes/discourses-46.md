[046-001]When the need of each opinion comes,

[046-002]we ought to have it in readiness: on the occasion of breakfast, such opinions

[046-003]as relate to breakfast; in the bath, those that concern the bath; in bed, those

[046-004]that concern bed.

[046-005]Let sleep not come upon thy languid eyes

[046-006]Before each daily action thou hast scann’d;

[046-007]What’s done amiss, what done, what left undone;

[046-008]From first to last examine all, and then

[046-009]Blame what is wrong, in what is right rejoice.

[046-010]And we ought to retain these verses in such way that we may use them, not that

[046-011]we may utter them aloud, as when we exclaim, “Paean Apollo.” Again in fever we

[046-012]should have ready such opinions as concern a fever; and we ought not, as soon

[046-013]as the fever begins, to lose and forget all. A man who has a fever may say: If

[046-014]I philosophize any longer, may I be hanged: wherever I go, I must take care of

[046-015]the poor body, that a fever may not come. But what is philosophizing? Is it not

[046-016]a preparation against events which may happen? Do you not understand that you

[046-017]are saying something of this kind? “If I shall still prepare myself to bear

[046-018]with patience what happens, may I be hanged.” But this is just as if a man

[046-019]after receiving blows should give up the Pancratium. In the Pancratium it is in

[046-020]our power to desist and not to receive blows.

[046-021]But in the other matter if we give up philosophy, what shall we gain? What then

[046-022]should a man say on the occasion of each painful thing? It was for this that I

[046-023]exercised myself, for this I disciplined myself. God says to you: Give me a

[046-024]proof that you have duly practised athletics, that you have eaten what you

[046-025]ought, that you have been exercised, that you have obeyed the aliptes (the

[046-026]oiler and rubber). Then do you show yourself weak when the time for action

[046-027]comes? Now is the time for the fever. Let it be borne well. Now is the time for

[046-028]thirst, bear it well. Now is the time for hunger, bear it well. Is it not in

[046-029]your power? Who shall hinder you? The physician will hinder you from drinking;

[046-030]but he cannot prevent you from bearing thirst well: and he will hinder you from

[046-031]eating; but he cannot prevent you from bearing hunger well.

[046-032]But I cannot attend to my philosophical studies. And for what purpose do you

[046-033]follow them? Slave, is it not that you may be happy, that you may be constant,

[046-034]is it not that you may be in a state conformable to [[nature]] and live so? What

[046-035]hinders you when you have a fever from having your ruling faculty conformable

[046-036]to nature? Here is the proof of the thing, here is the test of the philosopher.

[046-037]For this also is a part of life, like walking, like sailing, like journeying by

[046-038]land, so also is fever. Do you read when you are walking? No. Nor do you when

[046-039]you have a fever. But if you walk about well, you have all that belongs to a

[046-040]man who walks. If you bear a fever well, you have all that belongs to a man in

[046-041]a fever. What is it to bear a fever well? Not to blame God or man; not to be

[046-042]afflicted at that which happens, to expect death well and nobly, to do what

[046-043]must be done: when the physician comes in, not to be frightened at what he

[046-044]says; nor if he says you are doing well, to be overjoyed. For what [[good]] has he

[046-045]told you? and when you were in health, what good was that to you? And even if

[046-046]he says you are in a bad way, do not despond. For what is it to be ill? is it

[046-047]that you are near the severance of the soul and the body? what harm is there in

[046-048]this? If you are not near now, will you not afterwards be near? Is the world

[046-049]going to be turned upside down when you are dead? Why then do you flatter the

[046-050]physician? Why do you say if you please, master, I shall be well? Why do you

[046-051]give him an opportunity of raising his eyebrows (being proud; or showing his

[046-052]importance)? Do you not value a physician, as you do a shoemaker when he is

[046-053]measuring your foot, or a carpenter when he is building your house, and so

[046-054]treat the physician as to the body which is not yours, but by nature dead? He

[046-055]who has a fever has an opportunity of doing this: if he does these things, he

[046-056]has what belongs to him. For it is not the business of a philosopher to look

[046-057]after these [[externals]], neither his wine nor his oil nor his poor body, but his

[046-058]own ruling power. But as to externals how must he act? so far as not to be

[046-059]careless about them. Where then is there [[reason]] for fear? where is there then

[046-060]still reason for anger, and of fear about what belongs to others, about things

[046-061]which are of no value? For we ought to have these two principles in readiness,

[046-062]that except the will nothing is good nor bad; and that we ought not to lead

[046-063]events, but to follow them. My brother ought not to have behaved thus to me.

[046-064]No, but he will see to that; and, however he may behave, I will conduct myself

[046-065]towards him as I ought. For this is my own business; that belongs to another:

[046-066]no man can prevent this, the other thing can be hindered.
