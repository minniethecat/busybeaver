# Tranquillity

**Tranquillity** (*ataraxia*, literally "freedom from disturbance") is the calm, untroubled state of mind that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies as the natural product of [[virtue]]. "What is the product of virtue? Tranquillity" (004-016). It is not a separate achievement or an additional quality to be pursued alongside virtue — it is virtue's own fruit, the subjective experience of a soul in harmony with [[nature]].

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], tranquillity is both the goal and the test of philosophical progress. The person who has read many books but remains disturbed by events has made no real improvement. The person who faces [[fortune|misfortune]] with serene acceptance has made genuine progress, regardless of their learning.

## Definition

In Greek philosophy, *ataraxia* (from *a-* "not" + *tarassein* "to disturb") was prized by multiple schools — the Epicureans and Skeptics as well as the Stoics — though each defined the path to it differently. For the Stoics, tranquillity is not the result of withdrawing from the world or moderating one's desires, but of redirecting desire entirely toward what is within our power.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] directly links tranquillity to the discipline of [[desire]]: the person who desires only what is within the will can never be disappointed, and the person who never meets disappointment cannot be disturbed. "[[happiness|Happiness]] and tranquillity are not attainable by man otherwise than by not failing in obtaining what we desire and by not falling into that which we would avoid" (004-003).

This makes tranquillity a diagnostic tool. If you are disturbed, examine your desires: somewhere, you are desiring something not within your power, or avoiding something you cannot escape. The disturbance is not caused by the external event but by your [[judgment]] about it.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is characteristically blunt about what tranquillity is not. It is not the result of [[books|book-learning]]. "Does virtue consist in having understood [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]? If this is so, progress is clearly nothing else than knowing a great deal of [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]" (004-017). Yet the student who can read [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] fluently but still grieves, still fears, still desires what is not his — that student has made no progress at all.

The true test of progress is not in the library but in life. "Seek it there, wretch, where your work lies. And where is your work? In [[desire]] and in aversion, that you may not be disappointed in your desire" (004-025). Tranquillity is won or lost in the moment-by-moment management of impulse, not in the accumulation of doctrines.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also connects tranquillity to the acceptance of [[nature]]'s dispensations. The person who desires each thing to happen as it does happen — who has surrendered the demand that the world conform to private preference — achieves a tranquillity that no external event can disrupt. This is not passivity but the active alignment of the will with the rational order of the universe.

## Key Passages

- "What is the product of virtue? Tranquillity" — the definitive Epictetan statement (004-016).
- Tranquillity requires not failing in desire and not falling into what we would avoid (004-003).
- [[progress|Progress]] toward virtue is progress toward happiness and tranquillity (004-010).
- Book-learning [[solitude|alone]] does not produce tranquillity — "does virtue consist in having understood [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]?" (004-017).
- The work of tranquillity lies "in desire and in aversion" (004-025).
