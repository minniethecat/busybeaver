# Menelaus

## Biography

Menelaus is the Spartan king of Homer's *Iliad*, husband of the Helen whose departure with Alexander (Paris) is the traditional cause of the Trojan War. As with [[achilles]] and [[agamemnon]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws him not from history but from the poem, and uses him as a worked example rather than a hero to admire.

His most pointed appearance comes in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s account of how opinion governs conduct. Having argued that "all these great and dreadful deeds have this origin, in the appearance (opinion)" (018-034), he declares that "the Iliad is nothing else than appearance and the use of appearances" (018-035 to 018-036).

The whole war, on this [[books|reading]], turned on a chain of appearances: "It appeared to Alexander to carry off the wife of Menelaus" (018-036 to 018-037), and "it appeared to Helene to follow him" (018-037).

Menelaus's own opinion is the hinge on which the epic swings. Had it "appeared to Menelaus to feel that it was a gain to be deprived of such a wife" (018-038), then "not only would the Iliad have been lost, but the Odyssey also" (018-039 to 018-040).

He appears a second time in Discourse 35, on the fragility of friendship. Menelaus had been host to Alexander, and "if any man had seen their friendly disposition, he would not have believed any one who said that they were not friends" (035-036 to 035-037).

That appearance held only until a disputed external — "a handsome woman" cast between them "as between dogs" (035-038) — turned guest-friends into enemies, and "war arose" (035-039).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] never treats Menelaus as a victim of fortune. In both passages the king illustrates the Stoic doctrine that men are moved by their [[appearances]], not by things: what set the Iliad and Odyssey in motion was a set of opinions about what was worth having and losing (018-035).

The counter-factual is the lesson. Had Menelaus judged the loss of Helen to be no real loss, the war would never have happened (018-038). His grief and outrage were responses not to an event but to an [[opinion]] about that event.

The friendship passage carries the same teaching into human bonds. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s point is that a friendship resting on a shared external is no friendship: a "handsome woman" was cast between the two guest-friends, "and about her war arose" (035-038 to 035-039).

Menelaus and Alexander appeared to be friends, but the appearance dissolved the moment an [[externals|external]] came between them, because their attachment was to the thing and not to the [[good]]. Only those who agree about what is truly good, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] implies, can be steady friends.

## References in the Discourses

- **The Iliad as "appearance and the use of appearances"**: the abduction of Helen and the war it caused are traced to a chain of opinions, with Menelaus's judgment the decisive one (018-035, 018-037, 018-038).
- **Guest-friendship undone by an external**: Menelaus and Alexander seemed unshakeable friends until "a handsome woman" was cast between them and "war arose" (035-036, 035-038).
