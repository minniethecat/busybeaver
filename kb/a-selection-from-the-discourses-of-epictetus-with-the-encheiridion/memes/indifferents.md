## Definition

*Adiaphora* (indifferents) is the Stoic technical term for everything that is neither morally good nor morally evil. In the Stoic ethical system, only virtue is good, only vice is evil, and everything else — health, wealth, reputation, life, death, pleasure, pain — falls into the category of indifferents. This classification is foundational to Stoic ethics: it is what allows the Stoic to maintain equanimity in the face of fortune's reversals, since nothing that is "indifferent" can touch the moral self.

Crucially, "indifferent" does not mean "unimportant." The Stoics further divided indifferents into "preferred" (*proēgmena*), "dispreferred" (*apoproēgmena*), and "absolute" indifferents. Preferred indifferents (health, wealth, good reputation) are naturally worth pursuing, but their possession or loss has no bearing on virtue or happiness.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes the doctrine of indifferents the practical cornerstone of his teaching. He repeatedly confronts his students with the question: "Are not these things indifferent and nothing to us?" (007-013). The answer is always that externals are indifferent in themselves — it is the *use* of them that carries moral weight. This is one of his most characteristic moves: the material is indifferent, but the use of the material is not (022-002, 022-005).

This distinction between the material and its use runs through the Discourses. Life itself is indifferent, but how one lives is not (023-003). Dice are indifferent; how one plays the game is what matters (022-005). The hypothetical proposition is indifferent; the judgment about it is either knowledge or ignorance (023-001). In each case, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] redirects attention from the thing itself to the ruling faculty's handling of it.

He also warns against a common misinterpretation: the doctrine of indifferents is not an invitation to negligence. "When you are told that these things also are indifferent, do not become negligent" (023-004). The point is not to stop caring but to care about the right things — to invest concern in the use of [[appearances|impressions]] rather than in the [[appearances|impressions]] themselves.

The classic Stoic taxonomy appears in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s summary: "The good then are virtues, and the things which participate in virtues; and the bad are vices, and the things which participate in vice; and the indifferent are wealth, health, reputation" (025-037). This threefold classification underpins every ethical judgment in the Discourses.

## Key Passages

- (007-013) — "Are not these things indifferent and nothing to us; and is not death no evil?"
- (022-002) — The material is indifferent, but the use of the material is not indifferent.
- (022-005) — "The dice are indifferent. How do I know what is going to happen?"
- (022-014) — [[exercise|Exercise]] care precisely because the use is not indifferent.
- (023-003) — "Thus life is indifferent: the use is not indifferent."
- (023-004) — "When you are told that these things also are indifferent, do not become negligent."
- (025-037) — The tripartite division: good (virtues), bad (vices), and indifferent (wealth, health, reputation).
