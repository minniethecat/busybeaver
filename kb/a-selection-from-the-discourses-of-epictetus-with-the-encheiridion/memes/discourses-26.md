[026-001]Consider who you are. In the

[026-002]first place, you are a man; and this is one who has nothing superior to the

[026-003]faculty of the will, but all other things subjected to it; and the faculty

[026-004]itself he possesses unenslaved and free from subjection. Consider then from

[026-005]what things you have been separated by [[reason]]. You have been separated from

[026-006]wild beasts; you have been separated from domestic animals ([Greek: probaton]).

[026-007]Further, you are a citizen of the world, and a part of it, not one of the

[026-008]subservient (serving), but one of the principal (ruling) parts, for you are

[026-009]capable of comprehending the divine administration and of considering the

[026-010]connection of things. What then does the character of a citizen promise

[026-011](profess)? To hold nothing as profitable to himself; to deliberate about

[026-012]nothing as if he were detached from the community, but to act as the hand or

[026-013]foot would do, if they had reason and understood the constitution of [[nature]],

[026-014]for they would never put themselves in motion nor [[desire]] anything otherwise

[026-015]than with reference to the whole. Therefore, the philosophers say well, that if

[026-016]the [[good]] man had foreknowledge of what would happen, he would co-operate

[026-017]towards his own sickness and death and mutilation, since he knows that these

[026-018]things are assigned to him according to the universal arrangement, and that the

[026-019]whole is superior to the part, and the state to the citizen. But now because we

[026-020]do not know the future, it is our duty to stick to the things which are in

[026-021]their nature more suitable for our choice, for we were made among other things

[026-022]for this.

[026-023]After this, remember that you are a son. What does this character promise? To

[026-024]consider that everything which is the son’s belongs to the father, to obey him

[026-025]in all things, never to blame him to another, nor to say or do anything which

[026-026]does him injury, to yield to him in all things and give way, co-operating with

[026-027]him as far as you can. After this know that you are a brother also, and that to

[026-028]this character it is due to make concessions; to be easily persuaded, to speak

[026-029]good of your brother, never to claim in opposition to him any of the things

[026-030]which are independent of the will, but readily to give them up, that you may

[026-031]have the larger share in what is dependent on the will. For see what a thing it

[026-032]is, in place of a lettuce, if it should so happen, or a seat, to gain for

[026-033]yourself goodness of disposition. How great is the advantage.

[026-034]Next to this, if you are a senator of any state, remember that you are a

[026-035]senator; if a youth, that you are a youth; if an old man, that you are an old

[026-036]man; for each of such names, if it comes to be examined, marks out the proper

[026-037]duties. But if you go and blame your brother, I say to you, You have forgotten

[026-038]who you are and what is your name. In the next place, if you were a smith and

[026-039]made a wrong use of the hammer, you would have forgotten the smith; and if you

[026-040]have forgotten the brother and instead of a brother have become an enemy, would

[026-041]you appear not to have changed one thing for another in that case? And if

[026-042]instead of a man, who is a tame animal and social, you are become a mischievous

[026-043]wild beast, treacherous, and biting, have you lost nothing? But (I suppose) you

[026-044]must lose a bit of money that you may suffer damage? And does the loss of

[026-045]nothing else do a man damage? If you had lost the art of grammar or music,

[026-046]would you think the loss of it a damage? and if you shall lose modesty,

[026-047]moderation ([Greek: chtastolaen]) and gentleness, do you think the loss

[026-048]nothing? And yet the things first mentioned are lost by some cause external and

[026-049]independent of the will, and the second by our own fault; and as to the first

[026-050]neither to have them nor to lose them is shameful; but as to the second, not to

[026-051]have them and to lose them is shameful and matter of reproach and a misfortune.

[026-052]What then? shall I not hurt him who has hurt me? In the first place consider

[026-053]what hurt ([Greek: blabae]) is, and remember what you have heard from the

[026-054]philosophers. For if the good consists in the will (purpose, intention, [Greek:

[026-055]proaireeis]), and the evil also in the will, see if what you say is not this:

[026-056]What then, since that man has hurt himself by doing an unjust act to me, shall

[026-057]I not hurt myself by doing some unjust act to him? Why do we not imagine to

[026-058]ourselves (mentally think of) something of this kind? But where there is any

[026-059]detriment to the body or to our possession, there is harm there; and where the

[026-060]same thing happens to the faculty of the will, there is (you suppose) no harm;

[026-061]for he who has been deceived or he who has done an unjust act neither suffers

[026-062]in the head nor in the eye nor in the hip, nor does he lose his estate; and we

[026-063]wish for nothing else than (security to) these things. But whether we shall

[026-064]have the will modest and faithful or shameless and faithless, we care not the

[026-065]least, except only in the school so far as a few words are concerned. Therefore

[026-066]our proficiency is limited to these few words; but beyond them it does not

[026-067]exist even in the slightest degree.
