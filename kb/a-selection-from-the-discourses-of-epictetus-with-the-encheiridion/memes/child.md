## Definition

In Epictetan discourse, the child (*paidion*) functions both as a literal referent — children in families, the raising of offspring — and as a philosophical metaphor for the untrained person. A child, in the Epictetan sense, is one who lacks knowledge and is therefore frightened by false appearances, just as children are terrified by masks (020-050). The child represents the pre-philosophical condition: impulsive, governed by appearances, unable to distinguish genuine goods from illusory ones. The goal of Stoic education is to outgrow this childish condition and attain the reasoned [[firmness|stability]] of the adult philosopher.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the child metaphor to explain why ordinary people suffer: "What is a child? Want of knowledge. For when a child knows, he is no better than we are" (020-053). He illustrates the mechanism of fear by comparing adults' terror at externals to children's fear of masks — the cause is ignorance, not the thing itself (020-050). When addressing family [[duties|obligation]]s, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] acknowledges genuine duties: the death of a child is a natural event that should be met with the rule "death is a thing indifferent" (041-021). He teaches that the wise person fulfills the role of parent — "marry, to beget children, to fill the usual magistracies" (036-064) — but without attaching the will to outcomes. He also records the founding story of his own school: a child was brought to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s house to be nursed and raised, showing his commitment to practical benevolence (P02-047, P02-049).

## Stoic Context

The Stoic tradition distinguishes between the natural parental impulse (*oikeiōsis*) and the rational management of that impulse. Seneca and [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]] both wrote on marriage and child-rearing as duties of the Stoic sage. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows this tradition but adds his characteristic emphasis: one must play the role of parent well, but recognize that the child, like all externals, is "not up to us." The child metaphor also bridges to the Socratic tradition — [[socrates|Socrates]] compared himself to a midwife helping others give birth to knowledge, implying that most people are in a childish state of ignorance.
