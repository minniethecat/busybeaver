## Definition

Custom (*ethos*, also rendered as habit or accustomed [[exercise|practice]]) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching denotes the ingrained patterns of thought, desire, and action that shape a person's character. Custom can be either a prison or a path: the unphilosophical person is enslaved by unexamined custom, while the philosopher deliberately cultivates new customs — new habits of judgment — that align the soul with nature. The Stoic [[education|training]] is, at its core, a project of re-customization.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is acutely aware of the power of custom. Human beings are creatures of habit, and our customary ways of responding to [[appearances|impressions]] are so deep that they feel like nature itself. "We are accustomed to employ desire and aversion" (047-019) in ways that violate the fundamental Stoic distinction between what is in our power and what is not. The unphilosophical person has been "accustomed to do contrary things" (025-032) — to pursue externals as if they were goods, to flee indifferents as if they were evils.

The remedy is not a single act of will but a sustained [[exercise|practice]] of counter-habituation. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly uses the language of accustoming: "accustom yourself to do something else" (033-009), "if you are accustomed to be exercised in this way" (033-047). The verb is active and deliberate — one does not simply wait for new customs to form; one deliberately trains oneself, as an athlete trains the body. "If a man accustoms himself to write" (025-022) certain letters, his hand will form them automatically — so too with moral habits.

The challenge is that custom has a physical gravity. "On whom we were accustomed to repose, we say that we are ruined" (048-005) — when a customary support is removed, the untrained person collapses. The philosopher must anticipate this, building strength through deliberate [[exercise|practice]] before the test comes. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] speaks of students "accustomed while you were studying philosophy" (056-026) to certain ways of thinking — but the school is only the gymnasium; the real test is in life.

The goal is not to eliminate all custom — that is impossible — but to replace bad customs with good ones. The Stoic is "accustomed to reckon" (060-044) not by externals but by the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]]. The person who has fully internalized the philosophical [[education|training]] is "accustomed to speak" (066-006) of actions with accurate moral language. Custom, properly directed, becomes the stable foundation of virtue rather than an obstacle to it.

## Key Passages

- (047-019) — "We are accustomed to employ desire and aversion" wrongly — custom as the source of error.
- (025-032) — "Accustomed to do contrary things" — the contradictory customs of the untrained person.
- (033-009) — "Accustom yourself to do something else" — deliberate counter-habituation as philosophical [[exercise|practice]].
- (033-047) — "If you are accustomed to be exercised in this way" — [[education|training]] as the formation of new customs.
- (048-005) — "On whom we were accustomed to repose" — the vulnerability created by dependency-customs.
- (056-026) — "Accustomed while you were studying philosophy" — school customs vs. life customs.
- (060-044) — "Not what so far we have been accustomed to reckon" — the shift in what counts as valuable.
- (066-006) — "Are accustomed to speak of the act with" moral language — custom shapes speech and thought.
