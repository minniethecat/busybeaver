# Fortune

**Fortune** (*tychē*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is not a [[divinity|god]]dess who distributes blessings and curses, but a name for the external circumstances that lie outside the sphere of the rational will. What the world calls "good fortune" — wealth, health, reputation, favorable events — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] classifies as indifferent (*adiaphora*). What the world calls "misfortune" — poverty, illness, exile, death — is likewise indifferent. The only genuine good fortune is the possession of a well-ordered [[judgment]], and the only genuine misfortune is its corruption.

This revaluation of fortune is central to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s therapeutic project. If one believes that externals determine happiness, one is necessarily anxious, envious, and servile. If one transfers the notion of fortune from circumstances to character, one becomes free.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, fortune is exhaustively classified under "things not in our power" (*ta ouk eph' hēmin*). The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] opens with this distinction: some things are up to us — judgment, desire, aversion — and some are not — body, property, reputation, office. Fortune names the latter category considered as a source of apparent benefit or harm.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the orthodox Stoic position: fortune is neither good nor bad, because it can be used well or ill. A tyrant's power is fortune to the tyrant; to the philosopher it is an occasion for [[courage]]. An illness is misfortune to one who has not trained his judgment; to the student of philosophy it is material for [[endurance]]. Fortune is indifferent; the use of fortune is everything.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats fortune in several registers across the Discourses.

**Fortune as opinion**: In a characteristic passage, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reduces the emotional weight of fortune to a cognitive error. "What is bad fortune? [[opinion|Opinion]]. What is civil sedition, what is divided opinion, what is blame... All these things are opinions, and nothing more" (041-034). The diagnosis is radical: misfortune is not an event but a judgment about an event.

**The combat for good fortune**: In Discourse 55, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses an athletic metaphor. "The combat is for good fortune and happiness themselves" (055-009). Unlike the Olympic games, however, the contest of philosophy can be renewed at any moment — one need not wait four years. The implication is that good fortune is available now, to anyone who corrects their judgment.

**Fortune as companion of ignorance**: In a passage on freedom, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes those who "lamented, found fault with the deity, fortune, their companions; not seeing both what they had, and their own powers" (057-117). Fortune here is a scapegoat — those who blame fortune reveal only that they have not yet understood where the true good lies.

## Key Passages
