## Definition

Cynicism was a Greek philosophical school founded by [[diogenes|Diogenes of Sinope]] in the 4th century BCE. Its core teaching was that virtue is the only good, and that conventional goods — wealth, reputation, pleasure, even physical comfort — are not merely indifferent but positive impediments to the philosophical life. The Cynic [[exercise|practice]]d radical simplicity, living without possessions, family, or social standing, in open defiance of convention.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats Cynicism not as a historical curiosity but as the most demanding form of the philosophical life — a [[divinity|divine]] calling that few are equipped to undertake. His Discourse 53 ("[[About]] Cynicism") is one of the most detailed ancient accounts of what the Cynic vocation entailed.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment of Cynicism is remarkable for its combination of admiration and caution. He does not recommend Cynicism to his students; he warns them away from it unless they are called by God. "He who without God attempts so great a matter is hateful to God and has no other purpose than to act indecently in public" (053-004–053-006).

The Cynic, in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s account, must first master the basic Stoic disciplines before even considering the Cynic life: "You must take away desire altogether, you must transfer [[aversion|avoidance]] only to the things which are within the power of the will; you must not feel anger nor resentment nor [[jealousy|envy]] nor pity" (053-008–053-011). The Cynic's asceticism is not the starting point but the culmination — it is what becomes possible when one has fully internalized the distinction between the will and externals.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most distinctive contribution is his conception of the Cynic as a [[divinity|divine]] messenger and spy. "In fact a Cynic is a spy of the things which are good for men and which are evil" (053-042). The Cynic lives without walls, without concealment, and without private life — not from eccentricity, but because his entire existence is a testimony. His poverty, his endurance, his freedom from passion are all public proofs that the philosophical life is possible.

But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is clear-eyed about the cost. The Cynic must be prepared to be beaten, reviled, and rejected — and to respond with [[endurance|patience]] and love. The Cynic "must be beaten like an ass, and when he is beaten must love those who beat him" (053-038–053-039). This is not masochism but the logical extension of the principle that externals cannot harm one's true self.

The Cynic's role in the Stoic imagination is thus aspirational: the Cynic is what every Stoic would become if they followed the principles to their ultimate conclusion. Most of us, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] implies, are not ready — and that is not a failure, but an honest assessment.
