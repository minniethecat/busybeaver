[048-001]Solitude is a

[048-002]certain condition of a helpless man. For because a man is alone, he is not for

[048-003]that [[reason]] also solitary; just as though a man is among numbers, he is not

[048-004]therefore not solitary. When then we have lost either a brother, or a son, or a

[048-005]friend on whom we were accustomed to repose, we say that we are left solitary,

[048-006]though we are often in [[rome|Rome]], though such a crowd meet us, though so many live

[048-007]in the same place, and sometimes we have a great number of slaves. For the man

[048-008]who is solitary, as it is conceived, is considered to be a helpless person and

[048-009]exposed to those who wish to harm him. For this reason when we travel, then

[048-010]especially do we say that we are lonely when we fall among robbers, for it is

[048-011]not the sight of a human creature which removes us from solitude, but the sight

[048-012]of one who is faithful and modest and helpful to us. For if being alone is

[048-013]enough to make solitude, you may say that even [[zeus|Zeus]] is solitary in the

[048-014]conflagration and bewails himself saying, Unhappy that I am who have neither

[048-015]Hera, nor Athena, nor Apollo, nor brother, nor son, nor descendant, nor

[048-016]kinsman. This is what some say that he does when he is alone at the

[048-017]conflagration. For they do not understand how a man passes his life when he is

[048-018]alone, because they set out from a certain natural principle, from the natural

[048-019][[desire]] of community and mutual love and from the pleasure of conversation among

[048-020]men. But none the less a man ought to be prepared in a manner for this also

[048-021](being alone), to be able to be sufficient for himself and to be his own

[048-022]companion. For as Zeus dwells with himself, and is tranquil by himself, and

[048-023]thinks of his own administration and of its [[nature]], and is employed in thoughts

[048-024]suitable to himself; so ought we also to be able to talk with ourselves, not to

[048-025]feel the want of others also, not to be unprovided with the means of passing

[048-026]our time; to observe the divine administration, and the relation of ourselves

[048-027]to everything else; to consider how we formerly were affected towards things

[048-028]that happened and how at present; what are still the things which give us pain;

[048-029]how these also can be cured and how removed; if any things require improvement,

[048-030]to improve them according to reason.

[048-031]Well then, if some man should come upon me when I am alone and murder me? Fool,

[048-032]not murder You, but your poor body.

[048-033]What kind of solitude then remains? what want? why do we make ourselves worse

[048-034]than children; and what do children do when they are left alone? They take up

[048-035]shells and ashes, and they build something, then pull it down, and build

[048-036]something else, and so they never want the means of passing the time. Shall I

[048-037]then, if you sail away, sit down and weep, because I have been left alone and

[048-038]solitary? Shall I then have no shells, no ashes? But children do what they do

[048-039]through want of thought (or deficiency in knowledge), and we through knowledge

[048-040]are unhappy.

[048-041]Every great power (faculty) is dangerous to beginners. You must then bear such

[048-042]things as you are able, but conformably to nature: but not ... Practise

[048-043]sometimes a way of living like a person out of health that you may at some time

[048-044]live like a man in health.
