[024-001]Through an unreasonable regard to divination

[024-002]many of us omit many duties. For what more can the diviner see than death or

[024-003]danger or disease, or generally things of that kind? If then I must expose

[024-004]myself to danger for a friend, and if it is my duty even to die for him, what

[024-005]need have I then for divination? Have I not within me a diviner who has told me

[024-006]the [[nature]] of [[good]] and of evil, and has explained to me the signs (or marks) of

[024-007]both? What need have I then to consult the viscera of victims or the flight of

[024-008]birds, and why do I submit when he says, It is for your interest? For does he

[024-009]know what is for my interest, does he know what is good; and as he has learned

[024-010]the signs of the viscera, has he also learned the signs of good and evil? For

[024-011]if he knows the signs of these, he knows the signs both of the beautiful and of

[024-012]the ugly, and of the just and of the unjust. Do you tell me, man, what is the

[024-013]thing which is signified for me: is it life or death, poverty or wealth? But

[024-014]whether these things are for my interest or whether they are not, I do not

[024-015]intend to ask you. Why don’t you give your opinion on matters of grammar, and

[024-016]why do you give it here about things on which we are all in error and disputing

[024-017]with one another?

[024-018]What then leads us to frequent use of divination? Cowardice, the dread of what

[024-019]will happen. This is the [[reason]] why we flatter the diviners. Pray, master,

[024-020]shall I succeed to the property of my father? Let us see: let us sacrifice on

[024-021]the occasion. Yes, master, as fortune chooses. When he has said, You shall

[024-022]succeed to the inheritance, we thank him as if we received the inheritance from

[024-023]him. The consequence is that they play upon us.

[024-024]Will you not then seek the nature of good in the rational animal? for if it is

[024-025]not there, you will not choose to say that it exists in any other thing (plant

[024-026]or animal). What then? are not plants and animals also the works of God? They

[024-027]are; but they are not superior things, nor yet parts of the gods. But you are a

[024-028]superior thing; you are a portion separated from the Deity; you have in

[024-029]yourself a certain portion of him. Why then are you ignorant of your own noble

[024-030]descent? Why do you not know whence you came? will you not remember when you

[024-031]are eating who you are who eat and whom you feed? When you are in social

[024-032]intercourse, when you are exercising yourself, when you are engaged in

[024-033]discussion, know you not that you are nourishing a god, that you are exercising

[024-034]a god? Wretch, you are carrying about a god with you, and you know it not. Do

[024-035]you think that I mean some god of silver or of gold, and external? You carry

[024-036]him within yourself, and you perceive not that you are polluting him by impure

[024-037]thoughts and dirty deeds. And if an image of God were present, you would not

[024-038]dare to do any of the things which you are doing; but when God himself is

[024-039]present within and sees all and hears all, you are not ashamed of thinking such

[024-040]things and doing such things, ignorant as you are of your own nature and

[024-041]subject to the anger of God. Then why do we fear when we are sending a young

[024-042]man from the school into active life, lest he should do anything improperly,

[024-043]eat improperly, have improper intercourse with women; and lest the rags in

[024-044]which he is wrapped should debase him, lest fine garments should make him

[024-045]proud. This youth (if he acts thus) does not know his own God; he knows not

[024-046]with whom he sets out (into the world). But can we endure when he says, “I wish

[024-047]I had you (God) with me.” Have you not God with you? and do you seek for any

[024-048]other when you have him? or will God tell you anything else than this? If you

[024-049]were a statue of Phidias, either Athena or [[zeus|Zeus]], you would think both of

[024-050]yourself and of the artist, and if you had any understanding (power of

[024-051]perception) you would try to do nothing unworthy of him who made you or of

[024-052]yourself, and try not to appear in an unbecoming dress (attitude) to those who

[024-053]look upon you. But now because Zeus has made you, for this reason do you care

[024-054]not how you shall appear? And yet is the artist (in the one case) like the

[024-055]artist in the other? or the work in the one case like the other? And what work

[024-056]of an artist, for instance, has in itself the faculties, which the artist shows

[024-057]in making it? Is it not marble or bronze, or gold or ivory? and the Athena of

[024-058]Phidias, when she has once extended the hand and received in it the figure of

[024-059]Victory, stands in that attitude for ever. But the works of God have power of

[024-060]motion, they breathe, they have the faculty of using the appearances of things

[024-061]and the power of examining them. Being the work of such an artist do you

[024-062]dishonor him? And what shall I say, not only that he made you, but also

[024-063]entrusted you to yourself and made you a deposit to yourself? Will you not

[024-064]think of this too, but do you also dishonor your guardianship? But if God had

[024-065]entrusted an orphan to you, would you thus neglect him? He has delivered

[024-066]yourself to your own care, and says: “I had no one fitter to entrust him to

[024-067]than yourself; keep him for me such as he is by nature, modest, faithful,

[024-068]erect, unterrified, free from [[passion]] and perturbation.” And then you do not

[024-069]keep him such.

[024-070]But some will say, Whence has this fellow got the arrogance which he displays

[024-071]and these supercilious looks? I have not yet so much gravity as befits a

[024-072]philosopher; for I do not yet feel confidence in what I have learned and in

[024-073]what I have assented to. I still fear my own weakness. Let me get confidence

[024-074]and then you shall see a countenance such as I ought to have and an attitude

[024-075]such as I ought to have; then I will show to you the statue, when it is

[024-076]perfected, when it is polished. What do you expect? a supercilious countenance?

[024-077]Does the Zeus at [[olympia|Olympia]] lift up his brow? No, his look is fixed as becomes him

[024-078]who is ready to say:

[024-079]Irrevocable is my word and shall not fail.—Iliad, i., 526.

[024-080]Such will I show myself to you, faithful, modest, noble, free from

[024-081]perturbation. What, and immortal, too, except from old age, and from sickness?

[024-082]No, but dying as becomes a god, sickening as becomes a god. This power I

[024-083]possess; this I can do. But the rest I do not possess, nor can I do. I will

[024-084]show the nerves (strength) of a philosopher. What nerves are these? A [[desire]]

[024-085]never disappointed, an aversion which never falls on that which it would avoid,

[024-086]a proper pursuit ([Greek: hormaen]), a diligent purpose, an [[assent]] which is not

[024-087]rash. These you shall see.
