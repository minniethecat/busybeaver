[060-001]Remember that not

[060-002]only the [[desire]] of power and of riches makes us mean and subject to others, but

[060-003]even the desire of tranquillity, and of leisure, and of travelling abroad, and

[060-004]of learning. For, to speak plainly, whatever the external thing may be, the

[060-005]value which we set upon it places us in subjection to others. What then is the

[060-006]difference between desiring to be a senator or not desiring to be one; what is

[060-007]the difference between desiring power or being content with a private station;

[060-008]what is the difference between saying, I am unhappy, I have nothing to do, but

[060-009]I am bound to my books as a corpse; or saying, I am unhappy, I have no leisure

[060-010]for reading? For as salutations and power are things external and independent

[060-011]of the will, so is a book. For what purpose do you choose to read? Tell me. For

[060-012]if you only direct your purpose to being amused or learning something, you are

[060-013]a silly fellow and incapable of enduring labor. But if you refer reading to the

[060-014]proper end, what else is this than a tranquil and happy life ([Greek: eusoia])?

[060-015]But if reading does not secure for you a happy and tranquil life, what is the

[060-016]use of it? But it does secure this, the man replies, and for this [[reason]] I am

[060-017]vexed that I am deprived of it.—And what is this tranquil and happy life, which

[060-018]any man can impede, I do not say Cæsar or Cæsar’s friend, but a crow, a piper,

[060-019]a fever, and thirty thousand other things? But a tranquil and happy life

[060-020]contains nothing so sure as continuity and freedom from obstacle. Now I am

[060-021]called to do something: I will go then with the purpose of observing the

[060-022]measures (rules) which I must keep, of acting with modesty, steadiness, without

[060-023]desire and aversion to things external; and then that I may attend to men, what

[060-024]they say, how they are moved; and this not with any bad disposition, or that I

[060-025]may have something to blame or to ridicule; but I turn to myself, and ask if I

[060-026]also commit the same faults. How then shall I cease to commit them? Formerly I

[060-027]also acted wrong, but now I do not: thanks to God.

[060-028]What then is the reason of this? The reason is that we have never read for this

[060-029]purpose, we have never written for this purpose, so that we may in our actions

[060-030]use in a way conformable to [[nature]] the appearances presented to us; but we

[060-031]terminate in this, in learning what is said, and in being able to expound it to

[060-032]another, in resolving a syllogism, and in handling the hypothetical syllogism.

[060-033]For this reason where our study (purpose) is, there alone is the impediment.

[060-034]Would you have by all means the things which are not in your power? Be

[060-035]prevented then, be hindered, fail in your purpose. But if we read what is

[060-036]written about action (efforts, [Greek: hormae]), not that we may see what is

[060-037]said about action, but that we may act well; if we read what is said about

[060-038]desire and aversion (avoiding things), in order that we may neither fail in our

[060-039]desires, nor fall into that which we try to avoid; if we read what is said

[060-040]about duty (officium), in order that remembering the relations (of things to

[060-041]one another) we may do nothing irrationally nor contrary to these relations; we

[060-042]should not be vexed, in being hindered as to our readings, but we should be

[060-043]satisfied with doing the acts which are conformable (to the relations), and we

[060-044]should be reckoning not what so far we have been accustomed to reckon: To-day I

[060-045]have read so many verses, I have written so many; but (we should say), To-day I

[060-046]have employed my action as it is taught by the philosophers; I have not

[060-047]employed my desire; I have used avoidance ([Greek: echchlisei]) only with

[060-048]respect to things which are within the power of my will; I have not been afraid

[060-049]of such a person, I have not been prevailed upon by the entreaties of another;

[060-050]I have exercised my patience, my abstinence, my co-operation with others; and

[060-051]so we should thank God for what we ought to thank him.

[060-052]There is only one way to [[happiness]], and let this rule be ready both in the

[060-053]morning and during the day and by night: the rule is not to look towards things

[060-054]which are out of the power of our will, to think that nothing is our own, to

[060-055]give up all things to the Divinity, to Fortune; to make them the

[060-056]superintendents of these things, whom [[zeus|Zeus]] also has made so; for a man to

[060-057]observe that only which is his own, that which cannot be hindered; and when we

[060-058]read, to refer our reading to this only, and our writing and our listening. For

[060-059]this reason I cannot call the man industrious, if I hear this only, that he

[060-060]reads and writes; and even if a man adds that he reads all night, I cannot say

[060-061]so, if he knows not to what he should refer his reading. For neither do you say

[060-062]that a man is industrious if he keeps awake for a girl, nor do I. But if he

[060-063]does it (reads and writes) for reputation, I say that he is a lover of

[060-064]reputation. And if he does it for money, I say that he is a lover of money, not

[060-065]a lover of labor; and if he does it through love of learning, I say that he is

[060-066]a lover of learning. But if he refers his labor to his own ruling power that he

[060-067]may keep it in a state conformable to nature and pass his life in that state,

[060-068]then only do I say that he is industrious. For never commend a man on account

[060-069]of these things which are common to all, but on account of his opinions

[060-070](principles); for these are the things which belong to each man, which make his

[060-071]actions bad or [[good]]. Remembering these rules, rejoice in that which is present,

[060-072]and be content with the things which come in season. If you see anything which

[060-073]you have learned and inquired about occurring to you in your course of life (or

[060-074]opportunely applied by you to the acts of life), be delighted at it. If you

[060-075]have laid aside or have lessened bad disposition and a habit of reviling; if

[060-076]you have done so with rash temper, obscene words, hastiness, sluggishness; if

[060-077]you are not moved by what you formerly were, and not in the same way as you

[060-078]once were, you can celebrate a festival daily, to-day because you have behaved

[060-079]well in one act, and to-morrow because you have behaved well in another. How

[060-080]much greater is this a reason for making sacrifices than a consulship or the

[060-081]government of a province? These things come to you from yourself and from the

[060-082]gods. Remember this, who gives these things and to whom, and for what purpose.

[060-083]If you cherish yourself in these thoughts, do you still think that it makes any

[060-084]difference where you shall be happy, where you shall please God? Are not the

[060-085]gods equally distant from all places? Do they not see from all places alike

[060-086]that which is going on?
