[051-001]If a

[051-002]man has frequent intercourse with others either for talk, or drinking together,

[051-003]or generally for social purposes, he must either become like them, or change

[051-004]them to his own fashion. For if a man places a piece of quenched charcoal close

[051-005]to a piece that is burning, either the quenched charcoal will quench the other,

[051-006]or the burning charcoal will light that which is quenched. Since then the

[051-007]danger is so great, we must cautiously enter into such intimacies with those of

[051-008]the common sort, and remember that it is impossible that a man can keep company

[051-009]with one who is covered with soot without being partaker of the soot himself.

[051-010]For what will you do if a man speaks about gladiators, about horses, about

[051-011]athletes, or what is worse about men? Such a person is bad, such a person is

[051-012][[good]]; this was well done, this was done badly. Further, if he scoff, or

[051-013]ridicule, or show an ill-natured disposition? Is any man among us prepared like

[051-014]a lute-player when he takes a lute, so that as soon as he has touched the

[051-015]strings, he discovers which are discordant, and tunes the instrument? Such a

[051-016]power as [[socrates|Socrates]] had who in all his social intercourse could lead his

[051-017]companions to his own purpose? How should you have this power? It is therefore

[051-018]a necessary consequence that you are carried about by the common kind of

[051-019]people.

[051-020]Why then are they more powerful than you? Because they utter these useless

[051-021]words from their real opinions; but you utter your elegant words only from your

[051-022]lips; for this [[reason]] they are without strength and dead, and it is nauseous to

[051-023]listen to your exhortations and your miserable [[virtue]], which is talked of

[051-024]everywhere (up and down). In this way the vulgar have the advantage over you;

[051-025]for every opinion ([Greek: dogma]) is strong and invincible. Until then the

[051-026]good ([Greek: chompsai]) sentiments ([Greek: hupolaepseis]) are fixed in you,

[051-027]and you shall have acquired a certain power for your security, I advise you to

[051-028]be careful in your association with common persons; if you are not, every day

[051-029]like wax in the sun there will be melted away whatever you inscribe on your

[051-030]minds in the school. Withdraw then yourselves far from the sun so long as you

[051-031]have these waxen sentiments. For this reason also philosophers advise men to

[051-032]leave their native country, because ancient habits distract them and do not

[051-033]allow a beginning to be made of a different habit; nor can we tolerate those

[051-034]who meet us and say: See such a one is now a philosopher, who was once so and

[051-035]so. Thus also physicians send those who have lingering diseases to a different

[051-036]country and a different air; and they do right. Do you also introduce other

[051-037]habits than those which you have; fix you opinions and exercise yourselves in

[051-038]them. But you do not so; you go hence to a spectacle, to a show of gladiators,

[051-039]to a place of exercise ([Greek: chuston]), to a circus; then you come back

[051-040]hither, and again from this place you go to those places, and still the same

[051-041]persons. And there is no pleasing (good) habit, nor attention, nor care about

[051-042]self and observation of this kind. How shall I use the appearances presented to

[051-043]me? according to [[nature]], or contrary to nature? how do I answer to them? as I

[051-044]ought, or as I ought not? Do I say to those things which are independent of the

[051-045]will, that they do not concern me? For if you are not yet in this state, fly

[051-046]from your former habits, fly from the common sort, if you intend ever to begin

[051-047]to be something.
