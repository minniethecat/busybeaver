# Epicurus

## Biography

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) was the Greek philosopher who founded the Epicurean school, the chief philosophical rival to [[stoicism|Stoicism]] in the Hellenistic world.

He does not appear in the Discourses as a biographical individual, with a life or death to be narrated. He enters the text only as a name attached to a body of doctrine — "the opinions of Epicurus" (025-052), "the doctrine of Epicurus" (035-029) — which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] sets up in order to argue against.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Epicurus is the standing representative of the position that the good is bodily pleasure and that virtue is either an illusion or a mere matter of opinion. He is used as a foil: a coherent, well-known alternative to the Stoic account of the good, precisely so that the Stoic account can be defined by contrast.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] never treats him with the [[mask|persona]]l scorn he reserves for a [[nero|Nero]]; the quarrel is doctrinal, not moral. What is at stake, in his handling, is whether honesty and fidelity are real goods rooted in the nature of a rational being, or whether — as he takes the Epicurean to hold — there is "no honesty" except what "opinion holds to be honest" (035-030).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes "the doctrine of Epicurus" the test case for what happens when a person locates the self in one place and honesty in another.

If a man puts his will in externals rather than in maintaining "the character of fidelity, of modesty, of [[endurance|patience]]" (035-027), then "the doctrine of Epicurus becomes strong" (035-029), asserting "either that there is no honesty or it is that which opinion holds to be honest" (035-030).

The Epicurean conclusion, on this view, is what one is forced to accept once virtue has been detached from the rational will.

He also uses Epicurus to expose the pretender. Rebuking a student who can expound doctrines he does not live by, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] says he might "explain according to the [[precept|rules]] of art" (025-051) "the opinions of Epicurus" — perhaps in "a more useful manner than Epicurus himself" — and then asks, "Why then do you call yourself a Stoic?" (025-053).

To master Epicurus's words while claiming the Stoic name is to be a man "inclining to two sides."

## Key Ideas or Contributions

As [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reports it, the Epicurean doctrine identifies the good with bodily pleasure: "the pleasure of the soul is in the pleasure from things of the body" (043-031), and those bodily things are "the substance (nature) of the good" (043-033).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presses this as internally inconsistent — one who grounds all good in the body says "what is inconsistent both with Epicurus and the rest of your doctrines" (043-030) — and treats the whole position as the natural home of anyone who denies that honesty is good in itself (035-030).

## References in the Discourses

- **The pretended Stoic**: one who explains "the opinions of Epicurus" better than he lives the Stoic life (025-052); "Why then do you call yourself a Stoic?" (025-053).

- **Honesty denied**: detach the self from virtue and "the doctrine of Epicurus becomes strong" (035-029), asserting there is no honesty but what opinion holds (035-030).

- **[[pleasure|Pleasure]] as the good**: the Epicurean grounding of good in bodily pleasure (043-031), which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls inconsistent (043-030).
