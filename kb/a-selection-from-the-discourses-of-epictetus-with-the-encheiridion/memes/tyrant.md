## Definition

The tyrant (*tyrannos*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is more than a political figure — he is a philosophical archetype, a symbol of everything that appears fearsome but has power only over externals. The tyrant threatens chains, exile, and death. But he cannot touch the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*), the only thing that truly belongs to a person. The tyrant's power is real but limited: "The tyrant will chain — what? The leg. He will take away — what? The neck" (011-031). The will remains free.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The tyrant appears throughout the Discourses as a pedagogical device — the ultimate test of whether a student has internalized Stoic principles. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly stages the encounter between the philosopher and the tyrant to demonstrate that external power is powerless against genuine moral freedom.

The core [[disputation|argument]] is simple and repeated in many forms: "The tyrant says, 'I am master of all.' Of what? Of what can you be master?" (012-003). The answer is that the tyrant can master bodies, property, and even life — but never the will. "When the tyrant says to a man, 'I will chain your leg,' he who values his leg says, 'Do not; have mercy.' But he who values his own will says, 'Chain it, if it seems better to you'" (012-025). This is the acid test of Stoic freedom: can you face the worst the tyrant can do and remain unmoved?

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the tyrant to expose a deeper philosophical truth: it is not the tyrant who makes people afraid but their own judgments. "What makes the tyrant formidable? The guards, you say" (063-001). But the real answer is opinion — the belief that what the tyrant threatens is an evil. When a small child is brought before the tyrant, the child does not fear him because the child has not yet formed the judgment that death or chains are terrible (063-003). The Stoic's task is to recover that clarity through reason, not innocence.

The historical context deepens the urgency: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself lived under the emperor Domitian, who banished philosophers from [[rome|Rome]]. The reference to "the miserable tyrant" who Metius Rufus aided "in killing himself" (P02-033) is a glancing but pointed allusion to the reality of imperial power. Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s response is not political revolt but philosophical independence: the tyrant's power is real, but the philosopher's freedom is more real.

## Key Passages

- (011-031) — "But the tyrant will chain — what? The leg. He will take away — what? The neck." The body but never the will.
- (012-003) — "The tyrant says, 'I am master of all.' Of what? Of what can you be master?"
- (012-025) — "When the tyrant says to a man, 'I will chain your leg'" — the Stoic says "Chain it."
- (019-009) — "Therefore when the tyrant" threatens, examine what he can actually harm.
- (020-084) — "A girl or a boy, or some tyrant or some friend of the tyrant" — all external objects of fear.
- (063-001) — "What makes the tyrant formidable? The guards, you say" — but it is opinion, not guards.
- (063-003) — "Bring a boy (child) to the tyrant when he is with" — the child does not fear because he lacks the judgment of evil.
- (P02-033) — Metius Rufus "aided the miserable tyrant in killing himself" — a historical reference.
- (023-044) — Tyrant listed among externals — with "the sea, or a tile" — all equally indifferent.
- (053-099) — The Cynic must be prepared to face "the dysentery, the tyrant, the fire, the iron."
