# Distrust

## Definition

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s moral vocabulary, **distrust** carries two related senses. In the first, it names a passion to be eradicated: the defeatist opinion that one *cannot* be happy given one's circumstances — a despairing distrust of one's own capacity for the good.

In the second, it names a discipline to be cultivated: a healthy distrust of mere *appearances* and of external things, which is "the beginning of philosophy." The two senses are united by a single question — what may I safely trust? — and [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s answer is always the same: trust only your own correct judgment, distrust everything that merely "seems."

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies distrust (in its vicious sense) as one of the two diseases he most wants to cure. Together with arrogance, it blocks all progress:

> "You must root out of men these two things, arrogance (pride) and distrust. Arrogance then is the opinion that you want nothing... but distrust is the opinion that you cannot be happy when so many circumstances surround you." (049-006, 049-008)

The two are mirror images: arrogance overrates the self, distrust underrates it, and both wrongly locate happiness in external circumstances rather than in moral choice. [[arrogance|Arrogance]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] notes, "is removed by confutation; and [[socrates|Socrates]] was the first who practised this" (049-006) — dialectic cures the proud by exposing what they in fact lack.

Yet distrust has a virtuous twin. Turned upon *appearances* rather than upon the self, it becomes the founding act of philosophy:

> "...a condemnation and distrust of that which only 'seems,' and a certain investigation of that which 'seems' whether it 'seems' rightly... This is the beginning of philosophy." (027-038)

This is the discipline of assent: refusing to grant that an impression is true until it has been tested, "as we have discovered a balance in the determination of weights, and a carpenter's rule (or square) in the case of straight and crooked things" (027-038).

Finally, in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges self-distrust precisely *about externals*: if you "seem to some to be a person of importance, distrust yourself. For you should know that it is not easy both to keep your will in a condition conformable to nature and (to secure) external things" (081-003).

The remedy for vicious distrust of the self is thus a virtuous distrust of the world's rewards.

## Key Passages

- **The two diseases**: "You must root out of men these two things, arrogance ([[arrogance|pride]]) and distrust... distrust is the opinion that you cannot be happy when so many circumstances surround you" (049-006, 049-008).

- **Distrust of appearances is philosophy's beginning**: "a condemnation and distrust of that which only 'seems'... This is the beginning of philosophy" (027-038).

- **A rule for testing [[appearances|impressions]]**: "as we have discovered a balance in the determination of weights, and a carpenter's rule (or square)" (027-038).

- **Distrust yourself about externals**: "distrust yourself. For you should know that it is not easy both to keep your will in a condition conformable to nature and (to secure) external things" (081-003).

## Related Concepts

- **[[arrogance|Arrogance]]** — The companion disease; arrogance overrates the self while distrust underrates it (049-006).

- **Self-conceit** — A neighbouring distortion of self-judgment.

- **[[externals|Externals]]** — The circumstances that distrust wrongly fears and rightly questions.

- **[[assent|Assent]]** — The discipline that gives virtuous distrust of appearances its technical form.
