# Stoicism

## Definition

**Stoicism** is a Hellenistic philosophical school founded around 300 BCE by [[zeno|Zeno of Citium]], who taught in the Stoa Poikile ("[[pain|Pain]]ted Porch") of [[athens|Athens]]. At its core, Stoicism teaches that virtue (*aretē*) [[solitude|alone]] is genuinely good, that vice [[solitude|alone]] is genuinely evil, and that all external things — health, wealth, reputation — are "indifferent" to moral happiness.

The Stoic sage achieves tranquillity not by reshaping the world but by correctly ordering his own judgments and desires in agreement with nature (*[[nature|physis]]*) and reason (*logos*).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is one of the three great surviving voices of later (Roman) Stoicism, alongside Seneca and Marcus Aurelius. The biographical note records that, even as a slave, he was permitted to attend the lectures of "C. [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]], an eminent Stoic philosopher" (P02-016), and he is described as "a rigid Stoic" in the household of Epaphroditus (P02-027).

His teaching stands squarely within the Stoic tradition, and he freely refers to the school's foundational literature: "the writings of the Stoics are full of them" — that is, of the very [[disputation|argument]]s presently needed (019-069).

What is distinctive in [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is his insistence that the name "Stoic" must be earned in [[exercise|practice]], not merely claimed. In a celebrated passage he turns on a man who wears the philosopher's cloak but lacks the corresponding character:

> "Why then do you call yourself a Stoic? Why do you deceive the many? ... when we see a man inclining to two sides, we are accustomed to say, This man is not a Jew, but he acts as one." (025-053)

Theory without [[exercise|practice]] is a form of imposture. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] concedes that the Stoics possess the correct doctrines about what is truly good and evil (028-003), yet he charges that "with respect to the proper use of the things, we are entirely without [[exercise|practice]]" (028-003).

His entire pedagogy — the disciplines of desire, action, and assent — is designed to close the gap between knowing the Stoic doctrines and actually living them.

## Key Passages

- **[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Stoic formation**: he attended the lectures of "C. [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]], an eminent Stoic philosopher" (P02-016); see also the description of him as "a rigid Stoic" (P02-027).

- **The Stoic literary tradition**: "the writings of the Stoics are full of them" (019-069).

- **The imposture of the name only**: "Why then do you call yourself a Stoic? Why do you deceive the many?" (025-053).

- **Right theory, no [[exercise|practice]]**: "with respect to the proper use of the things, we are entirely without [[exercise|practice]]" (028-003).

## Related Concepts

- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — The sole good in Stoic ethics and the unifying goal of the school.

- **[[nature|Nature]]** (*[[nature|physis]]*) — The standard by which the Stoic lives; "living in agreement with nature."

- **[[prohairesis|Prohairesis]]** — Moral choice or volition, the faculty [[epictetus|Epictetus]] places at the centre of Stoic [[exercise|practice]].

- **[[sage|Sage]]** (*sophos*) — The ideal Stoic who possesses perfect virtue; rarely if ever attained.
