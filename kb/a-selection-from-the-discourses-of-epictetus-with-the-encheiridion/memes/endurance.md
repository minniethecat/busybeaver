# Endurance

**Endurance** (*karteria*) is the power to bear hardship and pain without being disturbed. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching, endurance is not a skill to be laboriously acquired but a faculty already given — one of the inner resources that every human being possesses by nature and that simply needs to be exercised. It belongs to the discipline of [[desire]], governing our response to things that appear painful or difficult.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] pairs endurance with [[temperance]] and patience as the three powers available for turning every accident to use (078-003–078-005). Where temperance faces pleasure and patience faces insult, endurance faces pain and labor. Together they form a complete practical armoury against every class of disturbing appearance.

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, endurance is the virtue concerned with bearing things that appear hard to bear. It is closely related to courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*) but distinct: courage faces the fear of what might happen, while endurance faces the actual experience of what is happening. The courageous person does not shrink from danger; the enduring person does not crumble under pain.

The Stoic analysis of endurance rests on the same foundation as all the virtues: the judgment that externals are neither good nor evil. If pain is not an evil, then enduring it is not a [[fortune|misfortune]] but an opportunity to exercise virtue. "Shall I not use the power for the purposes for which I received it?" (006-051).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats endurance in two complementary registers: as a [[divinity|divine]] gift and as a private discipline. In Discourse 6, he places endurance among the faculties given by God: "Have you not received greatness of soul? Have you not received manliness? Have you not received endurance? And why do I trouble myself about anything that can happen if I possess greatness of soul?" (006-049–006-051). Endurance is presented as part of a larger endowment — alongside magnanimity and courage — that renders every external event manageable.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] passage on the three powers is the most memorable formulation. "If labor be presented to you, you will find that it is endurance" (078-004). The verb "find" (*heurēseis*) is significant: endurance is not constructed but discovered, already present, like a tool one had forgotten was in one's possession.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] is careful to distinguish genuine endurance from its counterfeit — endurance performed for display. In [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] §XLVII, he warns: "If you ever wish to exercise yourself in labor and endurance, do it for yourself, and not for others. Do not embrace statues" (115-005). The reference to "embracing statues" is a jab at the Cynic [[exercise|practice]] of hugging cold statues in winter as a public demonstration of austerity. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that endurance is an inner discipline; the moment it becomes performance, it ceases to be virtue and becomes [[self-conceit|vanity]].

He also connects endurance to the broader theme of frugality and self-sufficiency. In the same passage, he advises: "consider first how much more frugal the poor are than we, and how much more enduring of labor" (115-005). The poor, who endure hardship by necessity, put to [[modesty|shame]] the philosopher who makes a show of voluntary hardship.
