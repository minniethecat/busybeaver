## Definition

Death (*thanatos* in Greek, θάνατος) is the permanent cessation of the body's vital functions and, in Stoic physics, the separation of the soul from the body. Like all corporeal events, death is classified by the Stoics as an "indifferent" (*adiaphoron*) — neither good nor evil in itself.

Yet within the class of indifferents, death occupies a special position. It is a "dispreferred" indifferent, something one would naturally avoid, but which a virtuous person can face with complete equanimity. The Stoic's goal is not to deny death's natural undesirability, but to recognize that dying virtuously is infinitely more important than not dying at all.

## Epictetus's Treatment

Death is the most frequently invoked "terrible thing" in the Discourses, serving as [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s ultimate test case for Stoic principles. If the philosopher's teachings cannot stand up to the prospect of death, they are worth nothing. "Is not death no evil? And are we not in a state of alliance with it?" (007-014).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] consistently presents death as the extreme case of an external event that cannot harm a person's moral character. A tyrant may threaten death, but "death is a necessary thing" — it comes to all humans eventually, and the only question is whether one faces it well or poorly (017-019).

He dismantles the fear of death by tracing it to erroneous opinion. "When death appears an evil, we ought to have this rule ready: that it is fit to avoid evil things, and that death is a necessary thing" (017-018-017-019). The apparent contradiction — that we must avoid what is inevitable — dissolves once we recognize that death is not truly an evil.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most pointed teaching on death appears in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]: "Let death and exile and every other thing which appears dreadful be daily before your eyes; but most of all death: and you will never think of anything mean, nor will you desire anything extravagantly" (089-001). The regular contemplation of mortality (*memento mori*) is not morbid — it is liberating.
