## Definition

In Stoic ethics, poverty is classified as an "indifferent" (*adiaphoron*) — an external condition that is neither intrinsically good nor evil. What matters is not whether one is poor or wealthy, but how one judges and responds to material circumstances. The Stoic sage is indifferent to poverty, recognizing that virtue and vice reside solely in the ruling faculty's judgments, not in external possessions. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself lived in extreme poverty for much of his life, making his teaching on this subject particularly authoritative.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats poverty as a [[example|paradigm]]atic test of Stoic principles. He uses it repeatedly to illustrate the core doctrine that externals cannot harm the moral self. Poverty, he insists, is not the real enemy — the *opinion* about poverty is what causes [[grief|distress]] (052-023). A person who fears poverty has misunderstood where good and evil lie.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] goes further by framing poverty as something a Stoic should be prepared to accept willingly, even to embrace as an opportunity to demonstrate virtue. "Would you have me to bear poverty? Come and you will know what poverty is when a good actor takes the part" (063-023). He challenges the conventional view that poverty is a [[fortune|misfortune]] deserving pity. Those who pity the poor are themselves mistaken about the nature of good and evil (062-008).

The Discourses depict poverty as one extreme of fortune's wheel, paired with wealth. When fortune offers either, the Stoic remains unmoved: "Is it life or death, poverty or wealth?" — the question is irrelevant to virtue (024-013). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] even holds up Diogenes the Cynic, who lived in utter destitution, as a [[example|model]] of freedom, arguing that poverty voluntarily accepted is a form of liberation (015-012, 015-018).

## Key Passages

- (015-012) — "Terrible is poverty; fly, my friends, the enemy is coming." [[epictetus|Epictetus]] ironically cites the common view before refuting it.
- (024-013) — What does fortune offer? "Is it life or death, poverty or wealth?" — all indifferent to the virtuous person.
- (052-023) — It is not poverty itself that troubles us, but the opinion about poverty.
- (062-008) — "They pity me for my poverty" — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] critiques those who pity external conditions.
- (063-023) — "Would you have me to bear poverty? Come and you will know what poverty is."
- (070-008) — If you fear poverty, you will be unhappy — the diagnosis of a misplaced value judgment.
