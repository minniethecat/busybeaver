# Banquet

The **banquet** (*symposion*) is one of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most memorable metaphors for the conduct of life. Drawn from the social institution of the Greco-Roman dinner party, the metaphor instructs the Stoic to partake of life's goods with detachment, politeness, and readiness to leave when the hour comes.

The banquet metaphor appears most famously in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], where it serves as a compact summary of the Stoic attitude toward externals: enjoy what passes before you, but do not clutch at it, and do not complain when it is time to go.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The locus classicus is [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] §15: "Remember that in life you ought to behave as at a banquet" (083-001). A dish is carried round and comes to you; stretch out your hand and take a share politely. It passes by; do not stop it. It has not yet reached you; do not send your desire forward, but wait until it reaches you. In the same way, toward children, toward a wife, toward public office, toward wealth — take your portion when it arrives, and let it pass when it passes.

The metaphor contains a complete ethics of detachment. The key virtue is knowing when to partake and when to refrain. The unphilosophical person is the greedy guest who seizes dishes before they arrive and clings to them when they depart. The Stoic is the polite diner who takes a moderate share of what is offered and does not complain when the course changes.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the metaphor to its theological conclusion: if you behave in this way, "you will be not only a fellow banqueter with the [[divinity|gods]], but also a partner in their rule" (083-009). The cosmos itself is a [[divinity|divine]] banquet, and the Stoic who accepts his portion gratefully dines with [[zeus|Zeus]].

The banquet also serves as a test of character in social settings. In Discourse 93, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] discusses the irritation of being seated below another at a banquet (093-001) — a minor social slight that reveals whether one truly considers externals indifferent. The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] elsewhere advises avoiding the banquets of strangers (101-014), not from puritanism but because their conversation revolves around gladiators and horse-races rather than philosophy.

## Key Passages

- The central metaphor: "Remember that in life you ought to behave as at a banquet" (083-001).
- Fellow banqueter with the [[divinity|gods]]: "you will be not only a fellow banqueter with the [[divinity|gods]], but also a partner in their rule" (083-009).
- The test of seating: "Has a man been preferred before you at a banquet, or in being saluted?" (093-001).
- Avoiding distraction: "Avoid banquets which are given by strangers" (101-014).
- Behavior at banquets: "At a banquet do not say how a man ought to eat, but eat as you ought" (114-003).
