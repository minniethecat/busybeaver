# Abstinence

**Abstinence** (*enkrateia*) is the disciplined refusal of pleasure or comfort — the capacity to say no to what the body desires in order to affirm the sovereignty of the rational will. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching, abstinence is not a lifelong renunciation but an exercise (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*), a [[exercise|practice]] one undertakes to test and strengthen the ruling faculty. It appears in the Discourses as one among several practical powers — alongside [[endurance]] and [[patience]] — that turn external circumstances into occasions for virtue.

Unlike the Cynic ideal of permanent austerity, [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s abstinence is provisional and purposeful. One abstains not because pleasure is evil, but because the ability to abstain proves that one is not a slave to pleasure. "I have exercised my [[endurance|patience]], my abstinence, my co-operation with others; and on this account I am more prepared for every difficulty" (060-050).

## Definition

In Stoic ethics, abstinence is classified under the virtue of [[temperance]] (*sōphrosynē*), the rational management of desire. Where temperance is the settled disposition to desire only what is appropriate, abstinence is the active [[exercise|practice]] of refusing what could be desired — a deliberate exercise that strengthens the disposition.

The Stoic foundation of abstinence is the same as that of all practical virtues: the judgment that externals are indifferent. If rich food, sexual pleasure, and comfort are neither good nor evil, then abstaining from them is neither deprivation nor virtue in itself — it is simply [[education|training]]. The value lies in what the [[education|training]] produces: a character that can face any external circumstance without being [[power|control]]led by it.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats abstinence in three distinct registers across the Discourses and [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]].

**As a listed virtue**: In Discourse 35, abstinence appears in a catalogue of moral qualities that together define the good person: "fidelity, modesty, [[endurance|patience]], abstinence, active co-operation" (035-027). The list is significant because it places abstinence alongside the social virtues — it is not an isolated ascetic [[exercise|practice]] but part of a complete ethical life. The abstinent person is also co-operative, faithful, and patient; the virtues reinforce each other.

**As [[education|training]]**: The most detailed treatment comes in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]. "As to pleasure with women, abstain as far as you can before marriage" (101-022). The qualification "as far as you can" is characteristically Epictetan — he does not demand impossible perfection but urges steady, progressive effort. The same passage continues: "But abstain entirely from shouts and laughter at sports" (101-033), extending abstinence from bodily pleasure to the restraint of emotional indulgence in public spectacle.

**As a demonstration of freedom**: In Discourse 60, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] frames abstinence retrospectively as a source of confidence. "I have exercised my [[endurance|patience]], my abstinence, my co-operation with others" (060-050). The speaker here is looking back on a life of [[exercise|practice]], and the result is not [[arrogance|pride]] but preparedness — the settled assurance that one has the inner resources to face whatever comes.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also provides a concrete example in Discourse 30, where he visits a philosopher on the third day of a voluntary fast: "it was the third day of his abstinence from food, and I went to inquire" (030-014). The fact that he mentions this without either praise or criticism is itself instructive — abstinence is a tool, neither praiseworthy nor blameworthy in itself; what matters is the purpose it serves.

## Key Passages

> Fidelity, modesty, patience, abstinence, active co-operation — in these things let there be nothing of display. (035-027)

> I have exercised my patience, my abstinence, my co-operation with others; and on this account I am more prepared for every difficulty. (060-050)

> As to pleasure with women, abstain as far as you can before marriage. (101-022)

> But abstain entirely from shouts and laughter at sports. (101-033)
