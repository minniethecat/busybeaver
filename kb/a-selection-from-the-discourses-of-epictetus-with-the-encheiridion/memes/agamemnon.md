# Agamemnon

## Biography

Agamemnon is the king who leads the Greek expedition against Troy in Homer's *Iliad*. Like the other Homeric figures in the Discourses, he appears in [[epictetus|Epictetus]] not as a historical individual but as a case study — a name "called forth" to be examined for what it teaches about judgment, power, and happiness.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws on two moments in the poem. The first is Agamemnon's quarrel with [[achilles|Achilles]] at the opening of the *Iliad*, over whether he must give up the captive Chryseis and, if so, take another's prize in her place. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reads this not as a clash of tempers but as a clash of misapplied preconceptions.

The second is the sleepless night of the tenth book, where the commander of the whole host lies awake in anguish while lesser men sleep. This image lets [[epictetus|Epictetus]] make Agamemnon the counter-example to a common assumption: that supreme power brings happiness.

Placed beside [[nero|Nero]] and Sardanapalus as emblems of rulers who were wretched, Agamemnon is granted the concession that he was morally "a better man" than they — and yet no happier for his crown. In the text he stands, therefore, both as a figure of the internal error that generates conflict and as proof that royal power is no guarantee of the good.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

In the quarrel with [[achilles|Achilles]], Agamemnon supplies half of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s demonstration that disputes arise from the misuse of preconceptions. Both agree that what is right "ought to be done"; then "Agamemnon says, 'I ought not to give up Chryseis'" (014-016), and, pressing further, that if he must restore her "it is fit that I take his prize from some of you" (014-020).

One of the two, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists, "makes a wrong adaptation of the præcognition" of duty (014-018).

His second role is to sever power from happiness. Arguing that the good is not in royal power, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] writes that "neither was Agamemnon happy" (053-062), "though he was a better man than Sardanapalus and [[nero|Nero]]" (053-063); while others snore, the king "much from his head he tore his rooted hair" (053-065, citing *Iliad* x. 15). The throne buys anguish, not the good.

## References in the Discourses

- **The quarrel with [[achilles|Achilles]]**: Agamemnon's claim over Chryseis (014-016) as a case of misapplied preconceptions of duty (014-018).
- **[[power|Power]] without happiness**: "neither was Agamemnon happy" (053-062), though better than [[nero|Nero]] and Sardanapalus, but lay awake tearing his hair (053-065).
