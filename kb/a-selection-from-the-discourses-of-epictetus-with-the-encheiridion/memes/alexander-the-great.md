# Alexander the Great

*In the Discourses the name "Alexander" is used of two different men. This page treats the Macedonian king, the conqueror to whom the Cynic Diogenes spoke. For the Trojan prince (Paris), whom [[epictetus|Epictetus]] also calls Alexander, see [[alexander-of-troy]].*

## Biography

Alexander the Great, the Macedonian king whose conquests carried Greek arms across Asia, enters the Discourses only twice, and in both places as an example rather than a hero. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is not concerned with his campaigns; he uses the most powerful man of the old world to make two separate points about the mind.

In the first, Alexander is the figure of worldly power before whom a trained man feels no fear. Setting [[diogenes]] beside [[socrates]] as men who had "practised speaking," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] recalls that Diogenes "spoke as he did to Alexander, to the pirates, to the person who bought him" (028-086).

The king, the pirates, and the slave-dealer are ranked together as so many external threats. The Cynic, "confident in the things which he practised" (028-087), addressed the conqueror no differently than he addressed his captors.

In the second, Alexander is a warning about passion. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes that when men make their own interest their [[divinity|god]], and the [[divinity|gods]] "appear to us to be an impediment to this, we [[insult|abuse]] them and throw down their statues and burn their temples" (035-015 to 035-016).

His instance is the king himself: Alexander "ordered the temples of Aesculapius to be burned when his dear friend died" (035-016 to 035-017). [[grief|Grief]] for a friend, left uncorrected, turned the conqueror into a man at war with heaven.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Alexander matters precisely because his greatness is external. His command of cities and armies is the very kind of thing the Stoic files under "not in our power," and so it gives him no hold over a man whose good lies within.

[[diogenes]] speaks to the king exactly as he speaks to pirates and slave-buyers, because to the philosopher [[power]] over the body is one more indifferent thing (028-086). The king can kill or enslave, but he cannot touch what the Cynic values.

The temple-burning shows the reverse case — the great man mastered by an [[externals|external]]. When his dear friend died, Alexander met the loss not as something outside his will but as an injury to avenge, and in that [[grief]] he ordered the temples of the [[divinity|gods]] to be burned (035-016) — a [[passion]] violent enough to turn against heaven itself.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] places the episode inside a general law: whoever loves his own interest above all will "hate, spurn, curse" whatever seems to obstruct it, "a brother, or a father, or a child" — or a [[divinity|god]] (035-012 to 035-015). Alexander is the extreme illustration — the most powerful man on earth, undone from within by an attachment he could not govern.

## References in the Discourses

- **Diogenes's fearless speech**: the Cynic "spoke as he did to Alexander, to the pirates, to the person who bought him," ranking the king among mere external threats (028-086).
- **Impiety from grief**: making his own interest supreme, Alexander "ordered the temples of Aesculapius to be burned when his dear friend died" (035-016).
