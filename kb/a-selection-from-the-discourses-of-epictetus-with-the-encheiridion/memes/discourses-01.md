[001-001]Of all the faculties

[001-002](except that which I shall soon mention), you will find not one which is

[001-003]capable of contemplating itself, and, consequently, not capable either of

[001-004]approving or disapproving. How far does the grammatic art possess the

[001-005]contemplating power? As far as forming a [[judgment]] about what is written and

[001-006]spoken. And how far music? As far as judging about melody. Does either of them

[001-007]then contemplate itself? By no means. But when you must write something to your

[001-008]friend, grammar will tell you what words you should write; but whether you

[001-009]should write or not, grammar will not tell you. And so it is with music as to

[001-010]musical sounds; but whether you should sing at the present time and play on the

[001-011]lute, or do neither, music will not tell you. What faculty then will tell you?

[001-012]That which contemplates both itself and all other things. And what is this

[001-013]faculty? The rational faculty; for this is the only faculty that we have

[001-014]received which examines itself, what it is, and what power it has, and what is

[001-015]the value of this gift, and examines all other faculties: for what else is

[001-016]there which tells us that golden things are beautiful, for they do not say so

[001-017]themselves? Evidently it is the faculty which is capable of judging of

[001-018]appearances. What else judges of music, grammar, and the other faculties,

[001-019]proves their uses, and points out the occasions for using them? Nothing else.

[001-020]What then should a man have in readiness in such circumstances? What else than

[001-021]this? What is mine, and what is not mine; and what is permitted to me, and what

[001-022]is not permitted to me. I must die. Must I then die lamenting? I must be put in

[001-023]chains. Must I then also lament? I must go into exile. Does any man then hinder

[001-024]me from going with smiles and cheerfulness and contentment? Tell me the secret

[001-025]which you possess. I will not, for this is in my power. But I will put you in

[001-026]chains. Man, what are you talking about? Me, in chains? You may fetter my leg,

[001-027]but my will not even [[zeus|Zeus]] himself can overpower. I will throw you into prison.

[001-028]My poor body, you mean. I will cut your head off. When then have I told you

[001-029]that my head alone cannot be cut off? These are the things which philosophers

[001-030]should meditate on, which they should write daily, in which they should

[001-031]exercise themselves.

[001-032]What then did Agrippinus say? He said, “I am not a hindrance to myself.” When

[001-033]it was reported to him that his trial was going on in the Senate, he said: “I

[001-034]hope it may turn out well; but it is the fifth hour of the day”—this was the

[001-035]time when he was used to exercise himself and then take the cold bath,—“let us

[001-036]go and take our exercise.” After he had taken his exercise, one comes and tells

[001-037]him, “You have been condemned.” “To banishment,” he replies, “or to death?” “To

[001-038]banishment.” “What about my property?” “It is not taken from you.” “Let us go

[001-039]to Aricia then,” he said, “and dine.”
