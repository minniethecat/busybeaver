[034-001]Some things men readily confess, and other things they do

[034-002]not. No one then will confess that he is a fool or without understanding; but

[034-003]quite the contrary you will hear all men saying, I wish that I had fortune

[034-004]equal to my understanding. But men readily confess that they are timid, and

[034-005]they say: I am rather timid, I confess; but as to other respects you will not

[034-006]find me to be foolish. A man will not readily confess that he is intemperate;

[034-007]and that he is unjust, he will not confess at all. He will by no means confess

[034-008]that he is envious or a busybody. Most men will confess that they are

[034-009]compassionate. What then is the [[reason]]?

[034-010]The chief thing (the ruling thing) is inconsistency and confusion in the things

[034-011]which relate to [[good]] and evil. But different men have different reasons; and

[034-012]generally what they imagine to be base, they do not confess at all. But they

[034-013]suppose timidity to be a characteristic of a good disposition, and compassion

[034-014]also; but silliness to be the absolute characteristic of a slave. And they do

[034-015]not at all admit (confess) the things which are offences against society. But

[034-016]in the case of most errors for this reason chiefly they are induced to confess

[034-017]them, because they imagine that there is something involuntary in them as in

[034-018]timidity and compassion; and if a man confess that he is in any respect

[034-019]intemperate, he alleges love (or [[passion]]) as an excuse for what is involuntary.

[034-020]But men do not imagine injustice to be at all involuntary. There is also in

[034-021]jealousy, as they suppose, something involuntary; and for this reason they

[034-022]confess to jealousy also.

[034-023]Living then among such men, who are so confused, so ignorant of what they say,

[034-024]and of the evils which they have or have not, and why they have them, or how

[034-025]they shall be relieved of them, I think it is worth the trouble for a man to

[034-026]watch constantly (and to ask) whether I also am one of them, what imagination I

[034-027]have about myself, how I conduct myself, whether I conduct myself as a prudent

[034-028]man, whether I conduct myself as a temperate man, whether I ever say this, that

[034-029]I have been taught to be prepared for everything that may happen. Have I the

[034-030]consciousness, which a man who knows nothing ought to have, that I know

[034-031]nothing? Do I go to my teacher as men go to oracles, prepared to obey? or do I

[034-032]like a snivelling boy go to my school to learn history and understand the books

[034-033]which I did not understand before, and, if it should happen so, to explain them

[034-034]also to others? Man, you have had a fight in the house with a poor slave, you

[034-035]have turned the family upside down, you have frightened the neighbors, and you

[034-036]come to me as if you were a wise man, and you take your seat and judge how I

[034-037]have explained some word, and how I have babbled whatever came into my head.

[034-038]You come full of envy, and humbled, because you bring nothing from home; and

[034-039]you sit during the discussion thinking of nothing else than how your father is

[034-040]disposed towards you and your brother. What are they saying about me there? now

[034-041]they think that I am improving, and are saying, He will return with all

[034-042]knowledge. I wish I could learn everything before I return; but much labor is

[034-043]necessary, and no one sends me anything, and the baths at [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]] are dirty;

[034-044]everything is bad at home, and bad here.
