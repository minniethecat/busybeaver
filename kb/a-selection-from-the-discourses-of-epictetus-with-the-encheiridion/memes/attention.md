## Definition

Attention (Greek: *prosoche*) is the fundamental Stoic spiritual exercise of maintaining continuous, vigilant awareness over one's inner life — judgments, desires, impulses, and actions.

It is not mere concentration but a moral and philosophical attentiveness: the constant application of Stoic principles to every impression that arises.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats attention as the prerequisite for all moral progress; without it, philosophical doctrines remain inert knowledge that never transforms character.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes an entire Discourse (Discourse 67, "On Attention") to this concept, treating it as the practical linchpin of the Stoic way of life.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], attention must be unremitting — not something one turns on for philosophical study and then sets aside. He insists that there is no part of life exempted from the demand for attention (067-013): whether playing, singing, or engaging in any ordinary activity, one must do so with attention.

The core danger is the formation of a *habit of inattention*. Once the mind wanders from its philosophical watch, it becomes progressively harder to recall (067-018–067-021).

The procrastination of attention — "Tomorrow I will begin to attend" — is [[self-conceit|self-deception]], for the deferral itself entrenches the very habit one claims to be preparing to break (067-040–067-047).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] connects attention directly to the three disciplines: attention to desires ensures they remain directed at what is within the will's power; attention to impulses ensures action befits one's roles; attention to judgments guards against rash acceptance of misleading [[appearances|impressions]] (067-022–067-036).

## Key Passages

> When you have remitted your attention for a short time, do not imagine this, that you will recover it when you choose; but let this thought be present to you, that in consequence of the fault committed today your affairs must be in a worse condition for all that follows. (067-001)

> Is there any part of life excepted, to which attention does not extend? For will you do it (anything in life) worse by using attention, and better by not attending at all? (067-013)

> We must be content if by never remitting this attention we shall escape at least a few errors. (067-039)

> If it is good to use attention tomorrow, how much better is it to do so today? (067-044)
