## Definition

Knowledge (Greek: *epistēmē*) in Stoic epistemology denotes a cognition that is firm, systematic, and immune to revision by contrary [[appearances|impressions]]. The Stoics distinguished knowledge sharply from opinion (*doxa*) and error: knowledge is the secure grasp (*katalēpsis*) of a true proposition, held with such certainty that it cannot be overturned by [[disputation|argument]]. In the Stoic taxonomy, knowledge belongs to the sage [[solitude|alone]] — the ordinary person operates with opinion, which is inherently unstable. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherits this framework but adapts it to his practical, therapeutic purposes: knowledge is less a theoretical achievement than the lived certainty that externals are indifferent and that virtue [[solitude|alone]] is good.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats knowledge as fundamentally practical. He asks pointedly: "What is the value of knowing anything, if knowledge is not applied?" (008-031). The question is not rhetorical but diagnostic — it exposes the gap between intellectual acquaintance and lived conviction. A person who "knows" that death is not evil but still fears death does not, in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s sense, possess knowledge at all. Knowledge must penetrate to the level of action and emotional response.

The developmental dimension of knowledge is central to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching. He compares the ordinary person to a child: "What is a child? Want of knowledge. For when a child knows these things, he is not inferior to us" (020-053). Knowledge is what distinguishes the mature from the immature, the philosopher from the untrained. But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not treat knowledge as an all-or-nothing possession — it is acquired incrementally through the [[exercise|practice]] of philosophy.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies the subject matter of genuine knowledge with the fundamental Stoic distinction between what is and is not within our power. He asks: "What is it which is free from hindrance and unimpeded? The knowledge of the art of living" (057-039). The "art of living" (*technē peri bion*) is the practical knowledge that distinguishes the philosopher — not a body of doctrines but a skilled capacity for navigating life's challenges.

He also addresses the obstacles to knowledge. "Deficiency in knowledge" (048-039) is a universal human condition, and "through knowledge we are made" (048-039) — knowledge is constitutive of our rational nature. The aspiration to knowledge is natural, but it is easily corrupted into intellectual [[self-conceit|vanity]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] mocks the ambition to universal erudition: "I wish I could learn everything" (034-042) — the desire for knowledge for its own sake, detached from moral improvement, is a form of spiritual disease.

The distinction between knowledge, opinion, and error structures [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s epistemology. When confronted with an impression, the task is to "test it" — to determine whether it is "knowledge or opinion or error" (023-002). This testing is the discipline of assent in its cognitive dimension: one must withhold assent from what is uncertain and grant it only to what is clearly true.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] challenges: "What is the value of knowing anything, if knowledge is not applied?" (008-031), subordinating theoretical to practical knowledge.
- The child as a figure of ignorance: "What is a child? Want of knowledge" (020-053) — knowledge is the mark of maturity.
- The taxonomy of cognition: any impression must be evaluated as "knowledge or opinion or error" (023-002).
- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] exposes the fantasy of omniscience: "I wish I could learn everything" (034-042) — the intemperate desire for knowledge is itself a passion.
- "Deficiency in knowledge" is the human condition, and "through knowledge we are made" (048-039) — knowledge as constitutive of rational nature.
- The art of living: "What is it which is free from hindrance and unimpeded? The knowledge of the art of living" (057-039).
