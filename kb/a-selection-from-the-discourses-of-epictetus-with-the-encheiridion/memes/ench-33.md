[101-001]Immediately prescribe some character and some form to yourself, which you shall

[101-002]observe both when you are alone and when you meet with men.

[101-003]And let silence be the general rule, or let only what is necessary be said, and

[101-004]in few words. And rarely, and when the occasion calls, we shall say something;

[101-005]but about none of the common subjects, not about gladiators, nor horse-races,

[101-006]nor about athletes, nor about eating or drinking, which are the usual subjects;

[101-007]and especially not about men, as blaming them or praising them, or comparing

[101-008]them. If then you are able, bring over by your conversation, the conversation

[101-009]of your associates, to that which is proper; but if you should happen to be

[101-010]confined to the company of strangers, be silent.

[101-011]Let not your laughter be much, nor on many occasions, nor excessive.

[101-012]Refuse altogether to take an oath, if it is possible; if it is not, refuse as

[101-013]far as you are able.

[101-014]Avoid banquets which are given by strangers and by ignorant persons. But if

[101-015]ever there is occasion to join in them, let your attention be carefully fixed,

[101-016]that you slip not into the manners of the vulgar (the uninstructed). For you

[101-017]must know, that if your companion be impure, he also who keeps company with him

[101-018]must become impure, though he should happen to be pure.

[101-019]Take (apply) the things which relate to the body as far as the bare use, as

[101-020]food, drink, clothing, house, and slaves; but exclude everything which is for

[101-021]show or luxury.

[101-022]As to pleasure with women, abstain as far as you can before marriage; but if

[101-023]you do indulge in it, do it in the way which is conformable to custom. Do not

[101-024]however be disagreeable to those who indulge in these pleasures, or reprove

[101-025]them; and do not often boast that you do not indulge in them yourself.

[101-026]If a man has reported to you, that a certain person speaks ill of you, do not

[101-027]make any defence (answer) to what has been told you; but reply, The man did not

[101-028]know the rest of my faults, for he would not have mentioned these only.

[101-029]It is not necessary to go to the theatres often: but if there is ever a proper

[101-030]occasion for going, do not show yourself as being a partisan of any man except

[101-031]yourself, that is, [[desire]] only that to be done which is done, and for him only

[101-032]to gain the prize who gains the prize; for in this way you will meet with no

[101-033]hindrance. But abstain entirely from shouts and laughter at any (thing or

[101-034]person), or violent emotions. And when you are come away, do not talk much

[101-035]about what has passed on the stage, except about that which may lead to your

[101-036]own improvement. For it is plain, if you do talk much, that you admired the

[101-037]spectacle (more than you ought).

[101-038]Do not go to the hearing of certain persons’ recitations, nor visit them

[101-039]readily. But if you do attend, observe gravity and sedateness, and also avoid

[101-040]making yourself disagreeable.

[101-041]When you are going to meet with any person, and particularly one of those who

[101-042]are considered to be in a superior condition, place before yourself what

[101-043][[socrates|Socrates]] or Zeno would have done in such circumstances, and you will have no

[101-044]difficulty in making a proper use of the occasion.

[101-045]When you are going to any of those who are in great power, place before

[101-046]yourself that you will not find the man at home, that you will be excluded,

[101-047]that the door will not be opened to you, that the man will not care about you.

[101-048]And if with all this it is your duty to visit him, bear what happens, and never

[101-049]say to yourself that it was not worth the trouble. For this is silly, and marks

[101-050]the character of a man who is offended by [[externals]].

[101-051]In company take care not to speak much and excessively about your own acts or

[101-052]dangers; for as it is pleasant to you to make mention of your own dangers, it

[101-053]is not so pleasant to others to hear what has happened to you. Take care also

[101-054]not to provoke laughter; for this is a slippery way towards vulgar habits, and

[101-055]is also adapted to diminish the respect of your neighbors. It is a dangerous

[101-056]habit also to approach obscene talk. When then, anything of this kind happens,

[101-057]if there is a [[good]] opportunity, rebuke the man who has proceeded to this talk;

[101-058]but if there is not an opportunity, by your silence at least, and blushing and

[101-059]expression of dissatisfaction by your countenance, show plainly that you are

[101-060]displeased at such talk.
