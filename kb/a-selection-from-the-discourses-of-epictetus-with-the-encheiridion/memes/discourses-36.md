[036-001]Every man will read a book with more pleasure or even

[036-002]with more ease, if it is written in fairer characters. Therefore every man will

[036-003]also listen more readily to what is spoken, if it is signified by appropriate

[036-004]and becoming words. We must not say then that there is no faculty of

[036-005]expression: for this affirmation is the characteristic of an impious and also

[036-006]of a timid man. Of an impious man, because he undervalues the gifts which come

[036-007]from God, just as if he would take away the commodity of the power of vision,

[036-008]or hearing, or of seeing. Has then God given you eyes to no purpose? and to no

[036-009]purpose has he infused into them a spirit so strong and of such skilful

[036-010]contrivance as to reach a long way and to fashion the forms of things which are

[036-011]seen? What messenger is so swift and vigilant? And to no purpose has he made

[036-012]the interjacent atmosphere so efficacious and elastic that the vision

[036-013]penetrates through the atmosphere which is in a manner moved? And to no purpose

[036-014]has he made light, without the presence of which there would be no use in any

[036-015]other thing?

[036-016]Man, be neither ungrateful for these gifts nor yet forget the things which are

[036-017]superior to them. But indeed for the power of seeing and hearing, and indeed

[036-018]for life itself, and for the things which contribute to support it, for the

[036-019]fruits which are dry, and for wine and oil give thanks to God: but remember

[036-020]that he has given you something else better than all these, I mean the power of

[036-021]using them, proving them, and estimating the value of each. For what is that

[036-022]which gives information about each of these powers, what each of them is worth?

[036-023]Is it each faculty itself? Did you ever hear the faculty of vision saying

[036-024]anything about itself? or the faculty of hearing? or wheat, or barley, or a

[036-025]horse, or a dog? No; but they are appointed as ministers and slaves to serve

[036-026]the faculty which has the power of making use of the appearances of things. And

[036-027]if you inquire what is the value of each thing, of whom do you inquire? who

[036-028]answers you? How then can any other faculty be more powerful than this, which

[036-029]uses the rest as ministers and itself proves each and pronounces about them?

[036-030]for which of them knows what itself is, and what is its own value? which of

[036-031]them knows when it ought to employ itself and when not? what faculty is it

[036-032]which opens and closes the eyes, and turns them away from objects to which it

[036-033]ought not to apply them and does apply them to other objects? Is it the faculty

[036-034]of vision? No, but it is the faculty of the will. What is that faculty which

[036-035]closes and opens the ears? what is that by which they are curious and

[036-036]inquisitive, or on the contrary unmoved by what is said? is it the faculty of

[036-037]hearing? It is no other than the faculty of the will. Will this faculty then,

[036-038]seeing that it is amidst all the other faculties which are blind and dumb and

[036-039]unable to see anything else except the very acts for which they are appointed

[036-040]in order to minister to this (faculty) and serve it, but this faculty alone

[036-041]sees sharp and sees what is the value of each of the rest; will this faculty

[036-042]declare to us that anything else is the best, or that itself is? And what else

[036-043]does the eye do when it is opened than see? But whether we ought to look on the

[036-044]wife of a certain person, and in what manner, who tells us? The faculty of the

[036-045]will. And whether we ought to believe what is said or not to believe it, and if

[036-046]we do believe, whether we ought to be moved by it or not, who tells us? Is it

[036-047]not the faculty of the will?

[036-048]But if you ask me what then is the most excellent of all things, what must I

[036-049]say? I cannot say the power of speaking, but the power of the will, when it is

[036-050]right ([Greek: orthae]). For it is this which uses the other (the power of

[036-051]speaking), and all the other faculties both small and great. For when this

[036-052]faculty of the will is set right, a man who is not [[good]] becomes good: but when

[036-053]it fails, a man becomes bad. It is through this that we are unfortunate, that

[036-054]we are fortunate, that we blame one another, are pleased with one another. In a

[036-055]word, it is this which if we neglect it makes unhappiness, and if we carefully

[036-056]look after it, makes [[happiness]].

[036-057]What then is usually done? Men generally act as a traveller would do on his way

[036-058]to his own country, when he enters a good inn, and being pleased with it should

[036-059]remain there. Man, you have forgotten your purpose: you were not travelling to

[036-060]this inn, but you were passing through it. But this is a pleasant inn. And how

[036-061]many other inns are pleasant? and how many meadows are pleasant? yet only for

[036-062]passing through. But your purpose is this, to return to your country, to

[036-063]relieve your kinsmen of anxiety, to discharge the duties of a citizen, to

[036-064]marry, to beget children, to fill the usual magistracies. For you are not come

[036-065]to select more pleasant places, but to live in these where you were born and of

[036-066]which you were made a citizen. Something of the kind takes place in the matter

[036-067]which we are considering. Since by the aid of speech and such communication as

[036-068]you receive here you must advance to perfection, and purge your will and

[036-069]correct the faculty which makes use of the appearances of things; and since it

[036-070]is necessary also for the teaching (delivery) of theorems to be effected by a

[036-071]certain mode of expression and with a certain variety and sharpness, some

[036-072]persons captivated by these very things abide in them, one captivated by the

[036-073]expression, another by syllogisms, another again by sophisms, and still another

[036-074]by some other inn ([Greek: paudocheiou]) of the kind; and there they stay and

[036-075]waste away as they were among sirens.

[036-076]Man, your purpose (business) was to make yourself capable of using conformably

[036-077]to [[nature]] the appearances presented to you, in your desires not to be

[036-078]frustrated, in your aversion from things not to fall into that which you would

[036-079]avoid, never to have no luck (as one may say), nor ever to have bad luck, to be

[036-080]free, not hindered, not compelled, conforming yourself to the administration of

[036-081][[zeus|Zeus]], obeying it, well satisfied with this, blaming no one, charging no one

[036-082]with fault, able from your whole soul to utter these verses:

[036-083]Lead me, O Zeus, and thou too Destiny.
