[021-001]Consider, you who are going into

[021-002]court, what you wish to maintain and what you wish to succeed in. For if you

[021-003]wish to maintain a will conformable to [[nature]], you have every security, every

[021-004]facility, you have no troubles. For if you wish to maintain what is in your own

[021-005]power and is naturally free, and if you are content with these, what else do

[021-006]you care for? For who is the master of such things? Who can take them away? If

[021-007]you choose to be modest and faithful, who shall not allow you to be so? If you

[021-008]choose not to be restrained or compelled, who shall compel you to [[desire]] what

[021-009]you think that you ought not to desire? who shall compel you to avoid what you

[021-010]do not think fit to avoid? But what do you say? The judge will determine

[021-011]against you something that appears formidable; but that you should also suffer

[021-012]in trying to avoid it, how can he do that? When then the pursuit of objects and

[021-013]the avoiding of them are in your power, what else do you care for? Let this be

[021-014]your preface, this your narrative, this your confirmation, this your victory,

[021-015]this your peroration, this your applause (or the approbation which you will

[021-016]receive).

[021-017]Therefore [[socrates|Socrates]] said to one who was reminding him to prepare for his trial,

[021-018]Do you not think then that I have been preparing for it all my life? By what

[021-019]kind of preparation? I have maintained that which was in my own power. How

[021-020]then? I have never done anything unjust either in my private or in my public

[021-021]life.

[021-022]But if you wish to maintain [[externals]] also, your poor body, your little

[021-023]property, and your little estimation, I advise you to make from this moment all

[021-024]possible preparation, and then consider both the nature of your judge and your

[021-025]adversary. If it is necessary to embrace his knees, embrace his knees; if to

[021-026]weep, weep; if to groan, groan. For when you have subjected to externals what

[021-027]is your own, then be a slave and do not resist, and do not sometimes choose to

[021-028]be a slave, and sometimes not choose, but with all your mind be one or the

[021-029]other, either free or a slave, either instructed or uninstructed, either a

[021-030]well-bred cock or a mean one, either endure to be beaten until you die or yield

[021-031]at once; and let it not happen to you to receive many stripes and then to

[021-032]yield. But if these things are base, determine immediately. Where is the nature

[021-033]of evil and [[good]]? It is where truth is: where truth is and where nature is,

[021-034]there is caution: where truth is, there is courage where nature is.

[021-035]For this [[reason]] also it is ridiculous to say, Suggest something to me (tell me

[021-036]what to do). What should I suggest to you? Well, form my mind so as to

[021-037]accommodate itself to any event. Why that is just the same as if a man who is

[021-038]ignorant of letters should say, Tell me what to write when any name is proposed

[021-039]to me. For if I should tell him to write Dion, and then another should come and

[021-040]propose to him not the name of Dion but that of Theon, what will be done? what

[021-041]will he write? But if you have practised writing, you are also prepared to

[021-042]write (or to do) anything that is required. If you are not, what can I now

[021-043]suggest? For if circumstances require something else, what will you say, or

[021-044]what will you do? Remember then this general precept and you will need no

[021-045]suggestion. But if you gape after externals, you must of necessity ramble up

[021-046]and down in obedience to the will of your master. And who is the master? He who

[021-047]has the power over the things which you seek to gain or try to avoid.
