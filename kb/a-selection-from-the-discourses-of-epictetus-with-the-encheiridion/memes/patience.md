## Definition

Patience (*patientia*, also *hypomonē*) is the virtue of bearing difficulties, delays, and provocations without disturbance of the ruling faculty. In Stoic ethics, [[endurance|patience]] is not passive resignation but an active exercise of correct judgment: the patient person understands that external hardships cannot harm the moral self, and therefore faces them with equanimity. Patience is closely allied to endurance (*[[endurance|karteria]]*) and courage (*[[courage|andreia]]*), but distinctively emphasizes the temporal dimension — the capacity to persist [[tranquillity|calm]]ly through prolonged difficulty rather than reacting impulsively.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not treat [[endurance|patience]] as a stand[[solitude|alone]] topic in a single dedicated discourse, but the virtue permeates his entire teaching. His central exhortation — to bear whatever fortune assigns with dignity and without complaint — is, in essence, a call to [[endurance|patience]]. Every time he tells his students to accept poverty, exile, illness, or death without [[grief|distress]], he is [[education|training]] them in [[endurance|patience]].

The Discourses present [[endurance|patience]] as a corollary of the fundamental distinction between what is in our power and what is not. When we genuinely internalize that external events are indifferent, [[endurance|patience]] follows naturally: why would we be impatient about something that cannot harm us? Im[[endurance|patience]], in this framework, is a symptom of misplaced value — it reveals that we are treating an indifferent as though it were a genuine good or evil.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also frames [[endurance|patience]] as a form of strength. The impatient person is weak — they are disturbed by trifles, enslaved to circumstances, and unable to sustain a rational course of action. The patient person, by contrast, has the steadiness that comes from anchoring their happiness in the one thing that cannot be taken away. This [[endurance|patience]] is not a separate virtue but an expression of the unified Stoic virtue of wisdom applied to temporal endurance.

Patience is tested particularly in social contexts — bearing with difficult people, tolerating insults, persisting through misunderstanding. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s advice in these situations consistently points back to the same principle: the other person's error is their own harm, not yours. Patience toward others is thus an extension of the same wisdom that produces [[endurance|patience]] toward circumstances.
