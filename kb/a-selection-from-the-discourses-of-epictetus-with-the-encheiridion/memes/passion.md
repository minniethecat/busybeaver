# Passion

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, a **passion** (*pathos*) is an excessive and irrational impulse that disturbs the soul's natural tranquility. Unlike the modern sense of "passion" as strong enthusiasm, the Stoic *pathos* is always pathological — a disease of the soul caused by false judgments about the value of externals.

The Stoics classified passions under four main genera: desire (*epithymia*), fear (*phobos*), pleasure (*hēdonē*), and [[grief|distress]] (*lypē*). All arise from assenting to the false impression that something external is either good (prompting desire) or bad (prompting fear).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]], like other Stoics, regards the elimination of passions as central to the philosophical life. The sage (*sophos*) is free from passion (*apatheia*) — not because the sage lacks emotions altogether, but because all emotional responses are proportionate to reality and aligned with reason.

Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is acutely aware of the difficulty: "But passion overpowers the better counsel" (018-017). He acknowledges that even those who understand Stoic doctrine can be overwhelmed by passion in the moment. His response is not to condemn human weakness but to emphasize the need for continuous [[exercise|practice]] (*[[exercise|askēsis]]*).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] illustrates the destructive power of passion through vivid examples: "the thought that to indulge her passion and take vengeance on her husband was more endurable than to endure the wrong" (018-018). Passions, once assented to, drive a person to actions contrary to their own best judgment.

The Stoic ideal is to stand "erect, unterrified, free from passion and perturbation" (024-068). This is achieved not by suppressing feelings but by correcting the underlying judgments that give rise to them.

## Key Passages

- **Passion overpowers counsel**: "But passion overpowers the better counsel" (018-017).
- **Passion and vengeance**: "The thought that to indulge her passion and take vengeance on her husband was more endurable" (018-018).
- **[[freedom|Freedom]] from passion**: The sage stands "erect, unterrified, free from passion and perturbation" (024-068).
- **Violent passion**: Those who act "passionately and violently" have lost the capacity for rational judgment (025-011).

## Related Concepts

- **[[reason|Reason]]** (*logos*) — The antidote to passion; passions are literally failures of reason.
- **[[virtue|Virtue]]** (*aretē*) — [[virtue|Virtue]] is incompatible with passion; the virtuous person is *apatheia* (free from destructive emotions).
- **[[assent|Assent]]** (*synkatathesis*) — Passions arise from giving assent to false value judgments about externals.
- **[[desire|Desire]]** (*[[desire|orexis]]*) — The passion of reaching toward a perceived future good; must be disciplined to target only what is within our power.
