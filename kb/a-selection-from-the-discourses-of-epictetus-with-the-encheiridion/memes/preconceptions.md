# Preconceptions

**Preconceptions** (*prolēpseis*, also translated as "præcognitions") are the innate, natural notions that all human beings possess about fundamental ethical concepts — good and evil, beautiful and ugly, just and unjust, proper and improper. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], these are the raw materials of philosophy. We do not need to be taught these concepts from scratch, because [[nature]] has equipped every person with them (027-007).

The challenge of philosophy is not acquiring preconceptions, but learning to apply them correctly to particular cases. Disagreement among people does not arise because we lack the notions of justice and goodness; it arises because we misapply them.

## Definition

In Stoic epistemology, preconceptions (*prolēpseis*) are universal, naturally-formed concepts that arise from repeated experience and serve as the starting point for all reasoning. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] explains: "Who ever came into the world without having an innate idea of them? Wherefore we all use these names, and we endeavor to fit the preconceptions to the several cases" (027-010).

Preconceptions are distinct from technical knowledge. Nobody is born knowing geometry or music — those are learned through [[education|instruction]]. But every person comes into the world with a natural notion of what is [[good]], what is [[modesty|shame]]ful, what is just. This is why, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes, even the uneducated use moral language confidently, without waiting to be taught its meaning (027-014).

The Stoic doctrine of preconceptions was originally developed by [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] and the early Stoa as a response to [[plato|Plato]]'s theory of recollection. But where [[plato|Plato]] located our grasp of universals in a prenatal vision of the Forms, the Stoics grounded preconceptions in the accumulated experience of the human community, shaped by the rational [[nature]] we all share.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes preconceptions central to his account of philosophical progress. The beginning of philosophy, he teaches, is not learning new concepts but becoming conscious of the inadequacy of our existing application of them.

The fundamental error, according to [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is self-conceit (*oiēsis*) — the false belief that we already know how to apply our preconceptions correctly. "It is impossible for a man to begin to learn that which he thinks that he knows" (032-003). The first task of the philosopher is therefore to "throw away self-conceit" and recognize how badly we have been adapting our natural notions to particular cases.

This is vividly illustrated in the disputes he describes: Jews and Syrians, [[egypt|Egypt]]ians and Romans all agree that [[piety|holiness]] should be pursued — but they disagree violently about whether eating pig's flesh is holy (014-009). The preconception is common; the application divides them.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also connects preconceptions to the discipline of [[assent]]. We must have "manifest præcognitions, cleared of all impurities and ready to hand" so that we can properly evaluate the [[appearances]] (*[[appearances|phantasiai]]*) that present themselves to us (017-016).

## Key Passages

- All humans possess innate preconceptions of good, evil, beauty, and justice — "who ever came into the world without having an innate idea of them?" (027-010).
- Philosophical disagreement arises from the misapplication of shared preconceptions, not from their absence (014-005).
- The first task of philosophy is to "throw away self-conceit" — the false belief that we already know (032-003).
- Clear preconceptions are necessary for evaluating the appearances that confront us (017-016).
- Even the rhetorician Theopompus, who criticized [[plato|Plato]] for defining everything, relied on natural notions and preconceptions without knowing it (032-019).
