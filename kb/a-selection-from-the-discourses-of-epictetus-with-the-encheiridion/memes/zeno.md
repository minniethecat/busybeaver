# Zeno of Citium

## Biography

Zeno of Citium (c. 334 – c. 262 BCE) was the founder of the Stoic school of philosophy. Born in Citium on Cyprus, he came to [[athens|Athens]] as a merchant and turned to philosophy after a shipwreck, studying first with the Cynic Crates before developing his own system.

He taught in the Stoa Poikile — the "[[pain|Pain]]ted Porch" — from which [[stoicism|Stoicism]] takes its name.

His system embraced logic, physics, and ethics as a single connected whole, and it was as an ethical guide above all that it reached [[epictetus|Epictetus]]. Though none of his writings survive intact, the doctrines he established were elaborated by his successors, above all [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], into the philosophy that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherited and taught.

In the Discourses Zeno appears not as a doctrine to be expounded but as a person to be imitated. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] twice sets him beside [[socrates|Socrates]] as the standard against which conduct is to be measured (101-043), treating the founder of his school as a living example of the composure that Stoic [[education|training]] is meant to produce.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] assumes his hearers already know who Zeno is; the founder needs no introduction, only invocation as a name that settles how a Stoic ought to behave. The anecdote he preserves concerns Zeno's meeting with Antigonus — the Macedonian king who is reported to have admired the philosopher and sought his company (028-050).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

As the founder of [[stoicism|Stoicism]], Zeno stands at the head of the tradition [[epictetus|Epictetus]] represents, and [[epictetus|Epictetus]] honors him by ranking him with [[socrates|Socrates]] as a pattern for imitation. Advising the student who must face powerful men, he writes: "place before yourself what [[socrates|Socrates]] or Zeno would have done in such circumstances, and you will have no [trouble]" (101-042 – 101-043).

The fullest treatment turns on the difference between the man who fears externals and the man who does not:

> For this reason, when Zeno was going to meet Antigonus, he was not anxious, for Antigonus had no power over any of the things which Zeno admired; and Zeno did not care for those things over which Antigonus had power. But Antigonus was anxious when he was going to meet Zeno, for he wished to please Zeno. (028-050 – 028-053)

The reversal is the whole Stoic lesson: the king, who commands armies, is the anxious party, because he wants something — the philosopher's regard — that lies outside his [[power|control]]; the philosopher is [[tranquillity|calm]] because he admires only what is his own.

## References in the Discourses

- **Zeno and Antigonus**: the philosopher unanxious before the king, because the king held power over nothing Zeno valued, while the king wished to please the philosopher (028-050 – 028-054).
- **A [[example|model]] for imitation**: "place before yourself what [[socrates|Socrates]] or Zeno would have done" when confronting the powerful (101-042 – 101-043).
