# Socrates

## Biography

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher who transformed the course of Western philosophy by shifting its focus from cosmology to ethics. He left no writings of his own; his teachings are known through the works of his students, principally [[plato|Plato]] and [[xenophon|Xenophon]].

Socrates was condemned to death by the Athenian democracy on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth. He accepted the death penalty [[tranquillity|calm]]ly, drinking hemlock rather than fleeing into exile — an act that made him the [[example|paradigm]]atic example of philosophical integrity in the face of injustice.

## Role in Epictetus

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] invokes Socrates more frequently than any other historical figure, presenting him as the closest approximation to the Stoic sage. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Socrates exemplifies every virtue that a philosopher should cultivate: courage in the face of death, equanimity under injustice, and unwavering commitment to rational examination.

"Socrates," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] tells his students, "became perfect by attending to everything and neglecting nothing" (Discourse 3). The secret of Socrates's achievement was not extraordinary talent but extraordinary diligence in self-examination.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] particularly admires Socrates's ability to maintain his composure under extreme provocation. When struck or insulted, Socrates did not retaliate but responded with reason: "If a donkey kicks you, do you kick it back?" This equanimity — the refusal to be disturbed by externals — embodies the Stoic ideal.

## Key Passages

- **Socrates as [[example|exemplar]]**: "Socrates became perfect by attending to everything and neglecting nothing."
- **Socrates and death**: Socrates faced death with "cheerfulness and contentment," demonstrating that the sage cannot be harmed.
- **Socrates and insults**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly cites Socrates's equanimity when insulted or struck.
