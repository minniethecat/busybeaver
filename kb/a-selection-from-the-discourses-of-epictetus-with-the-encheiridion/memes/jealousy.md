## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, jealousy (*phthonos*) is classified as a species of the passion of [[grief|distress]] (*lupē*) — a painful irrational contraction of the soul at another person's apparent good fortune. Unlike emulation (*zēlos*), which is a rational [[impulse|striving]] toward virtue, jealousy is rooted in the mistaken belief that another's possession of an external good diminishes one's own share of what is genuinely valuable.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] emphasizes that jealousy — like all passions — is not an involuntary force but a judgment. People often excuse it "as they suppose, something involuntary; and for this reason they confess to jealousy also" (034-021–022). But in Stoic psychology, every passion rests on an act of assent; jealousy persists only because the jealous person continues to assent to the judgment that another's externals are genuine goods worth envying.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats jealousy as one of the signature disturbances of the untrained person — a clear symptom that one has not yet internalized the fundamental Stoic distinction between what is in our power and what is not. He lists jealousy alongside [[grief|sorrow]], fear, envy, and pity as the emotional states that characterize a life lived "in a manner worthy of tragedy" (057-008).

The root cause, in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s analysis, is the failure to locate the good within the sphere of choice (*prohairesis*). When a person believes that wealth, reputation, or pleasure are genuine goods, they will inevitably experience jealousy toward those who possess more of these things. The cure is not to acquire more externals but to reorient desire entirely: "if the good is in our power, neither envy nor jealousy will have a place in us" (087-005).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also connects jealousy to a deeper spiritual poverty. When a student comes to philosophy "full of envy, and humbled, because you bring nothing from home" (034-038), he diagnoses a failure to recognize what one already possesses — the rational faculty, kinship with the [[divinity|divine]], and the capacity for virtue that no external circumstance can diminish.

## Stoic Context

The Stoic tradition classified jealousy (*phthonos*) among the passions of [[grief|distress]], distinct from hatred and spite. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] and the early Stoics defined it as "pain at another's having what one desires for oneself." Seneca, in *De Ira* and the *Epistles*, similarly treats envy as a particularly corrosive passion because it feeds on com[[alexander-of-troy|paris]]on and is never satisfied — the envious person suffers doubly, first from their own perceived lack and then from another's perceived abundance.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s distinctive contribution is the directness of his remedy: he does not offer gradual techniques for moderating jealousy but insists on its total elimination through the radical reorientation of the ruling faculty. Jealousy simply cannot coexist with the recognition that nothing external is either good or evil. This absolutism reflects [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s broader pedagogical strategy of treating passions not as manageable impulses but as errors of judgment to be eradicated.

## Key Passages

> You come full of envy, and humbled, because you bring nothing from home; and during the discussion you sit thinking of nothing else but how your father is disposed toward you, and your brother. (034-038)

> That which makes men envious and jealous; and by these passions they cannot even listen to reason. (040-014)

> Who chooses to live in sorrow, fear, envy, pity, desiring and failing in his desires, avoiding and falling into that which he would avoid? (057-008)

> If the good is in our power, neither envy nor jealousy will have a place in us. (087-005)
