## Definition

Character (*[[mask|prosōpon]]*, also *ēthos*) in Stoic ethics refers to the moral quality of a person — the settled disposition from which actions flow. It is not a fixed inheritance but something continuously shaped by judgments and actions. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the metaphor of a dramatic role: each person is assigned a "character" by the [[divinity|divine]] director, and the task is to play that part well. "What does the character of a citizen promise? To consider nothing as useful for himself which is not useful for the whole" (026-010). Every social role — citizen, son, brother, councilor — carries a "character" that defines its proper duties.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes character (*[[mask|prosōpon]]*) one of the central concepts of his ethics. He teaches that every action either strengthens or weakens one's character. "By acting in one way we strengthen contrary characters" (025-026) — if you act angrily, you strengthen the character of an angry person; if you act patiently, you strengthen the character of a patient person. This is a dynamic, cumulative view of moral development: character is not something you *have* but something you *do*, over and over, until it becomes who you are.

The concept is closely tied to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s doctrine of roles. Each person has multiple roles — as a human being, as a citizen, as a son or daughter, as a professional — and each role carries a "character" that defines its proper conduct. "You are a son. What does this character promise? To consider that everything of yours belongs to your father" (026-023, 026-028). The point is not blind obedience but understanding what each role requires and fulfilling it according to reason.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that a person who loses their moral character through bad judgment has suffered the only real loss. When Alexander lost "the character of modesty, fidelity, regard to kinship" (018-066), he lost everything that mattered, regardless of his worldly conquests. The consistent theme is that maintaining one's character is the supreme task: "It is my interest to maintain the character of" whatever role one has been given (035-026), and "you will always maintain your character such as it ought to be" (059-007) — if you keep your judgments aligned with nature.

## Key Passages

- (025-006) — "You have not fulfilled the character of a man" — the most fundamental role.
- (025-026) — "By acting in one way we strengthen contrary characters" — the dynamic nature of moral formation.
- (026-010) — "What does the character of a citizen promise?" — role-based ethics.
- (026-023) — "You are a son. What does this character promise?"
- (026-028) — To this character "it is due to make concessions."
- (035-026) — "It is my interest, to maintain the character of" one's assigned role.
- (036-002) — "If it is written in fairer characters" — every man will act better if his character is well-formed.
- (053-021) — "The character of a Cynic, of a man who lives" according to the harshest discipline.
- (059-007) — "You will always maintain your character such as it ought to be" through right judgment.
- (067-028) — "Direct our duties towards the character (nature)" — character and nature are aligned in proper conduct.
- (067-034) — "Maintain our own character. But wherever you have deviated" — return to it promptly.
