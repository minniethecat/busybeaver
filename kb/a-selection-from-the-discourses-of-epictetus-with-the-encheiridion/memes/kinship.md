## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, kinship (Greek: *syngeneia*, συγγένεια) is the natural relation or affinity that connects human beings to the [[divinity|divine]]. The Stoics recognized two kinds of kinship: a base kinship with the flesh that humans share with animals, and an elevated kinship with the [[divinity|gods]] through the possession of reason (*logos*) and intelligence. This doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] kinship is foundational to Stoic ethics, grounding the call to live according to nature and to recognize one's own dignity as a rational being. Unlike the colloquial sense of family relation, Stoic kinship is a metaphysical and moral concept: it is not about blood ties but about the shared rational nature that makes humans akin to God.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] makes the doctrine of kinship a central pillar of his moral teaching. In Discourse 3, he develops the theme at length: since humans are "sprung from God in an especial manner" (003-001–002), possessing reason and intelligence in common with the [[divinity|gods]], the recognition of this [[divinity|divine]] kinship should transform one's self-conception entirely. No one who truly grasped that they are the child of God, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues, would harbor "ignoble or mean thoughts" about themselves (003-004). He draws a sharp contrast with the alternative: "Through this kinship with the flesh, some of us inclining to it become like wolves, faithless and treacherous and mischievous; some become like lions, savage and bestial and untamed" (003-017–019). The choice between these two kinships — the [[divinity|divine]] and the fleshly — is, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the fundamental moral choice.

In Discourse 7, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presses the consequences further: those who have "recognized their kinship to God" (007-004) understand that they are not merely citizens of a particular city but "citizens of the world" (*kosmopolitai*). This recognition reorients desire and aversion: the person who knows their [[divinity|divine]] kinship will not be enslaved to externals, because they understand that their true good lies in the perfection of reason, not in the fortunes of the body. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s use of kinship is thus tightly linked to his doctrines of freedom, moral progress, and the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]].

## Key Passages

- "If a man should be able to assent to this doctrine as he ought, that we are all sprung from God in an especial manner, and that God is the father both of men and of [[divinity|gods]], I suppose that he would never have any ignoble or mean thoughts about himself" (003-001–004) — The foundational statement of [[divinity|divine]] kinship.
- "Through this kinship with the flesh, some of us inclining to it become like wolves... some become like lions... but the greater part of us become foxes" (003-017–020) — The degrading alternative to recognizing [[divinity|divine]] kinship.
- "[[body|Body]] in common with the animals, and reason and intelligence in common with the [[divinity|gods]], many incline to this kinship, which is miserable and mortal; and some few to that which is [[divinity|divine]] and happy" (003-007–009) — The dual kinship and the moral contrast.
- "When they have recognized their kinship to God, and that we are citizens of the world" (007-004) — Kinship as the basis of cosmopolitanism.
