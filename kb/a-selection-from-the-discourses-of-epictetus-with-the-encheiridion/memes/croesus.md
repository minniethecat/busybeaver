# Croesus

## Biography

Croesus was the last king of Lydia, whose name became in antiquity a byword for immense wealth — the standard against which any great fortune was measured. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses him in exactly that proverbial sense, as a fixed point of maximal riches, and he does so twice, to opposite ends.

In Discourse 2, arguing that no one should abandon self-cultivation merely because perfection is out of reach, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] sets Croesus at the summit of wealth as Milo of Croton stands at the summit of strength: "I shall never be a Milo, and yet I do not neglect my body; nor shall I be a Croesus, and yet I do not neglect my property" (002-017 to 002-018).

In Discourse 53 the same figure serves the opposite emphasis — not the limit of wealth but the proof that wealth does not give happiness. Seeking happiness in possessions, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] says, is to seek it where it is not: "if you do not believe me, look at Croesus: look at those who are now rich, with what [[grief|lamentation]]s their life is filled" (053-054 to 053-055).

Between the two passages, Croesus never becomes a biography. He is a measure — of how much a man may possess, and of how little that possession is worth.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Croesus belongs to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment of [[wealth]] as an "external" — something outside the will, and therefore neither good nor evil in itself. The two mentions display the two halves of that doctrine.

The first shows the right attitude toward the pursuit of externals. One need not despair of self-improvement just because one will never be a Croesus in riches or a Milo in strength; "nor, in a word, do we neglect looking after anything because we despair of reaching the highest degree" (002-019 to 002-020).

The second denies that reaching such extremes brings [[happiness]]. Croesus, richest of men, is offered as living evidence that possessions do not contain what men seek: the rich pass their lives "with what [[grief|lamentation]]s" (053-055).

[[happiness|Happiness]], on [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s account, is therefore not to be sought "without" (053-052), in the body or in [[possession|possessions]], but in the faculty of will. Croesus is the perfect counter-example — a man who reached the summit of fortune and found no [[tranquillity]] there.

## References in the Discourses

- **Discourse 2**: Croesus as the unattainable summit of wealth — no reason to neglect one's property, or oneself, for falling short of him (002-018).
- **Discourse 53**: Croesus as proof that riches do not hold happiness — "look at Croesus... with what [[grief|lamentation]]s their life is filled" (053-054).
