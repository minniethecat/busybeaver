## Definition

Logic, in the Stoic system, is one of the three branches of philosophy alongside ethics and physics.

It encompasses not only formal reasoning and [[disputation|argument]]ation but also epistemology — the theory of knowledge, the nature of [[appearances|impressions]], and the criteria by which true judgments are distinguished from false ones.

For the Stoics, logic is not an end in itself but an indispensable tool: without it, one cannot reliably distinguish truth from error in any domain, including ethics.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] addresses the value of logic in Discourse 38, "That Logic Is Necessary," through a characteristically practical demonstration.

When a listener challenges him to prove that logic is necessary, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks: "How then will you know if I am cheating you by my [[disputation|argument]]?" (038-004). The man falls silent, having already conceded the point — for to judge whether an [[disputation|argument]] is valid or fallacious is itself an act of logical reasoning.

The [[disputation|argument]] is elegantly reflexive: the very attempt to dispute the necessity of logic necessarily employs logic. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not present logic as an academic luxury but as a protective faculty.

Just as one cannot safely handle money without distinguishing genuine coin from counterfeit, one cannot navigate [[appearances|impressions]] without knowing how to test them for truth.

This treatment aligns with [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s emphasis on the discipline of assent — examining every impression before accepting it. Logic is the tool by which this examination is performed.

## Stoic Context

In the orthodox Stoic curriculum established by Zeno and developed by [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], logic was treated as the first of the three parts of philosophy.

The Stoic concept of the *phantasia kataleptike* (cognitive impression) — an impression so clear and distinct that it compels assent — was a central epistemological doctrine.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]], while far less technical than [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], preserves the practical core of this tradition: logic matters because right living depends on right judgment.

## Key Passages

> When one of those who were present said, Persuade me that logic is necessary, he replied, Do you wish me to prove this to you? The answer was, Yes. Then I must use a demonstrative form of speech. (038-001–038-003)

> Do you see that you yourself are admitting that logic is necessary, if without it you cannot know so much as this, whether logic is necessary or not necessary? (038-006–038-007)
