## Definition

Security (*asphaleia*) in Stoic ethics is not the outward safety of possessions, bodily health, or social standing — it is the inner [[firmness|stability]] and freedom from anxiety that results from correctly identifying what is in one's power. When a person places his good solely in the ruling faculty — in right judgment, virtuous choice, and the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]] — he achieves a security that no external event can threaten. This security is the experiential fruit of Stoic [[exercise|practice]] and one of the most attractive promises of the philosophy.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents security as the natural consequence of living according to the fundamental distinction between what is and is not in our power. When a person's desires and aversions are aligned with nature, security follows necessarily. "If you follow nature, you have every security, every ease" (021-003). Conversely, the person who pursues externals lives in perpetual insecurity, because the objects of his desire are always vulnerable to fortune.

He develops the metaphor of building on a foundation. A person who "lays a rotten and unstable foundation" — that is, one who grounds his happiness in external goods — can never achieve security (030-024). The only firm foundation is the recognition that the ruling faculty [[solitude|alone]] is under our [[power|control]]. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the discipline of desire, the first of the three topics, as "that which concerns the security of the other two" (040-021), meaning that until desire is properly trained, no other moral progress is secure.

Security, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is also a social and ethical achievement. He criticizes those who seek security through power, wealth, or the protection of powerful friends, pointing out that such security is always conditional and can be withdrawn (054-048, 051-027). True security comes from having nothing that can be taken away by another — and only virtue meets this criterion. The person who has "protected this with security" is the one who has placed his good beyond the reach of fortune (056-040, 056-043).

He also links security to careful action. When we "take care not to damage our own ruling faculty," we "undertake every act with more security" (106-002, 106-003). The connection is practical: by guarding the mind against false judgments, we act from a place of [[firmness|stability]] rather than impulse.

## Key Passages

- (021-003) — "If you follow nature, you have every security, every ease."
- (026-063) — The person who wishes for nothing beyond what is in his power has security.
- (030-024) — Building on a rotten and unstable foundation — the insecurity of valuing externals.
- (040-021) — The discipline of desire concerns "the security of the other two" — it is foundational.
- (051-027) — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] advises against seeking security through external power.
- (054-048) — "Security? Well then suppose that my friend..." — exposing the illusion of external security.
- (056-040) — "[[About]] security in these things you have been deceived."
- (106-003) — Guarding the ruling faculty lets us "undertake this act with more security."
