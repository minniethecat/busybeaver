## Definition

In Stoic ethics, evil (*kakon*) is strictly defined as moral evil — that which corrupts the ruling principle (*hēgemonikon*) and impairs virtue. Unlike the common usage that labels death, poverty, sickness, or pain as "evils," the Stoic position, following [[socrates|Socrates]], holds that nothing external to the will can be genuinely evil. Evil resides solely in false judgments, vicious choices, and the corruption of the rational faculty. Everything outside the sphere of moral choice — health, wealth, reputation, even life itself — is classified as "indifferent" (*adiaphoron*), neither good nor evil in itself.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] places the nature of good and evil at the very center of his teaching. He repeatedly insists that the beginner in philosophy must first learn "the nature of good and evil" before anything else (024-006). For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the root of all human misery is the mistaken belief that externals contain good or evil: "When death appears an evil, we ought to have this rule in readiness" (017-018). His therapy consists in relocating good and evil from the external world into the will (*proairesis*): "the nature of good and evil is in the use of appearances" (020-011). He teaches that if a person places evil in the will, they will never blame others or be vexed at any man (018-032). The philosophical life is therefore a continuous [[exercise|practice]] of refusing to label any external event as evil, and instead examining one's own judgments.

## Stoic Context

The Epictetan doctrine of evil builds on the foundational Stoic principle, inherited from [[socrates|Socrates]], that no one does evil willingly — all wrongdoing stems from ignorance of the true good. Earlier Stoics like Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] classified all things into good (virtue and its participants), evil (vice and its participants), and indifferent (everything else). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] sharpens this doctrine with his emphasis on the will (*proairesis*) as the exclusive locus of moral value. His contemporary Seneca similarly taught that "death is not an evil," while Marcus Aurelius would later echo that "the evil that men do does me no harm unless I judge it so." [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is distinctive in making the good/evil distinction the first topic (*topos*) of his threefold practical program, alongside desire and assent.
