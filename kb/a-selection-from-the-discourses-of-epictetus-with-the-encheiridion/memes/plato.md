# Plato

## Biography

Plato (c. 428 – c. 348 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher, pupil of [[socrates|Socrates]] and founder of the Academy, whose dialogues shaped the whole subsequent course of Western thought.

Though he belonged to a different school from the Stoics — the Academy rather than the Porch — his authority was so established that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] quotes him repeatedly as a settled source, introducing his views with the simple formula "as Plato says" (018-010; 057-161).

That [[epictetus|Epictetus]] can cite him without [[disputation|argument]], expecting the name [[solitude|alone]] to carry weight, shows how far Plato's authority reached beyond the boundaries of his own school.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not expound Plato's system; he draws on particular Platonic doctrines and sayings to reinforce Stoic points. Two themes recur. The first is a claim about the will and knowledge — that error is never chosen for its own sake, since "every soul is unwillingly deprived of the truth" (018-010).

The second is the philosopher's discipline of facing death and pain, which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] takes from Plato's portrait of the examined life (057-161).

He also preserves a report, through the rhetorician Theopompus, of the criticism that Plato was over-fond of definitions (032-014 – 032-015) — a reminder that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] read him critically as well as reverently.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Plato serves [[epictetus|Epictetus]] as an external authority whose conclusions converge with Stoic teaching. The doctrine he cites most insistently is that no one is willingly deprived of the truth:

> every soul is unwillingly deprived of the truth, as Plato says; but the falsity seemed to him to be true. (018-009 – 018-011)

He returns to the same principle later, urging the philosopher to be gentle with those who err, "being well convinced of Plato's doctrine that every mind is deprived of truth unwillingly" (035-082 – 035-083). From this it follows that wrongdoers are to be pitied and corrected rather than hated — a thoroughly Stoic conclusion drawn from a Platonic premise.

Plato also supplies [[epictetus|Epictetus]] with a [[example|model]] of the philosopher's readiness to suffer. Pressing his students to give back to God whatever has been lent, he asks: "Will you not, as Plato says, study not to die only, but also to endure torture, and exile, and scourging, and, in a word, to give up all which is not your own?" (057-160 – 057-162).

And in the discipline of purity, he echoes Plato's counsel to have recourse to the temples when a base appearance visits the mind (033-035 – 033-037).

## References in the Discourses

- **The will and truth**: "every soul is unwillingly deprived of the truth, as Plato says" (018-009 – 018-011), repeated as "Plato's doctrine that every mind is deprived of truth unwillingly" (035-082 – 035-083).

- **Studying to endure**: "as Plato says, study not to die only, but also to endure torture, and exile, and scourging" (057-160 – 057-162).

- **On purity**: Plato's counsel to seek the temples when a base appearance arises (033-035 – 033-037).

- **A critical note**: Theopompus's charge that Plato wished "everything to be defined" (032-014 – 032-016).
