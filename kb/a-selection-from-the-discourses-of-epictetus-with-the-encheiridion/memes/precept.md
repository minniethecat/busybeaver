# Precept

**Precept** (Greek: *theōrēma*, plural *theōrēmata*) is, in Epictetan [[stoicism|Stoicism]], a practical rule or theorem derived from philosophical doctrines (*[[doctrine|dogma]]ta*) that guides conduct in particular situations. Precepts form the bridge between abstract principle and concrete action: where doctrines establish what is good, precepts tell you what to do right now. The *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]* (Handbook) itself is essentially a collection of precepts — condensed, actionable rules distilled from the *Discourses* for immediate use in life. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists that precepts are not objects of study but tools for living; their value lies entirely in their application.

## Definition

In the Stoic curriculum, a distinction is drawn between two kinds of philosophical knowledge. *Dogmata* (doctrines) are the foundational convictions: universal principles about the nature of the good, the structure of the cosmos, and the place of human choice within it. *Theōrēmata* (precepts or theorems) are the specific rules derived from these doctrines for application in the concrete circumstances of daily life. A doctrine states that only virtue is good and only vice is evil; a precept says, "When someone insults you, remember that it is not the insult that harms you but your judgment of it." Precepts are therefore the application layer of philosophy — they translate metaphysical and ethical first principles into [[education|instruction]]s for conduct. Without doctrines, precepts are rootless rules without justification; without precepts, doctrines remain inert theory that never touches the texture of lived experience.

[[doctrine|Doctrine]]s are few and universal; precepts are many and situation-specific. The same doctrine — that the only true good is a correct use of [[appearances|impressions]] — generates countless precepts addressing particular cases: how to bear illness, how to respond to slander, how to treat a parent, how to face death. This is why the Stoic life requires both deep study of principles and continuous [[exercise|practice]] in their application.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats precepts as essential but secondary. They are essential because no one can apply a universal principle without knowing what it demands in the concrete case. A student who has grasped the doctrine that the will is free by nature still needs the precept "Remember that you are an actor in a drama, of such a part as the playwright chooses" (017-031) in order to live that freedom when assigned a difficult role. Precepts bridge the gap between knowing and doing.

Yet precepts are secondary because they are useless — even dangerous — without the doctrines that give them meaning. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] warns that a person who collects precepts without understanding the principles behind them becomes like a man who has memorized a physician's prescriptions without knowing medicine: he may apply the wrong rule to the wrong case, or follow the letter while violating the spirit. Precepts are not a checklist to be ticked off but the expression of a transformed understanding. As he puts it, one must not merely recite theorems (philosophical rules, precepts) but "do that which follows from them" (114-002). The test of a precept is not whether you can repeat it but whether your life conforms to it.

The *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]* is the most concentrated expression of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on precepts. Each of its fifty-two chapters is effectively a single precept — a short, memorable rule for handling a particular class of impression or situation. Chapter 1, for example, gives the precept that we must distinguish what is in our power from what is not; Chapter 5 gives the precept that it is not things that disturb us but our judgments about them; Chapter 8 gives the precept to wish for things to happen as they do, not as you wish. The form is deliberate: the *[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]* is meant to be carried in the mind, recalled in the moment of need, and put into [[exercise|practice]] immediately. Its brevity is a feature, not a defect — a short rule that is used is worth more than an elaborate doctrine that is merely admired.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also teaches that precepts require readiness. A person who is "unable even to listen to the precepts of reason" (040-015) is one whose passions have so clouded his judgment that the rational rule cannot gain entry. The capacity to hear a precept and act on it is itself a fruit of prior [[education|training]]. This is why the pedagogic order matters: first learn to govern your desires and aversions, then train yourself in appropriate action, and only then approach the subtler precepts of the advanced Stoic curriculum. A precept addressed to a soul in the grip of anger or grief is like seed thrown on stone — it cannot take root.

## Key Passages

> Remember then this general precept and you will need no other: whenever you are troubled by any impression, apply this rule — "You are an impression and not at all the thing you appear to be." Then examine it and test it by the rules you have, and first and chiefly by this: whether it concerns things that are in our power or things that are not in our power. (021-044)

> But what follows from these theorems? That which is most excellent, to apply them in act: do not merely repeat the theorems, but do that which follows from them. (114-002)

> The precept is to take care of a father, to yield to him in all things, to submit when he reviles you, when he strikes you. "But he is a bad father." Were you then by nature made akin to a good father? No, but to a father. (098-002)

> And now that which remains is to use the precepts. This is the one thing necessary. (117-008)
