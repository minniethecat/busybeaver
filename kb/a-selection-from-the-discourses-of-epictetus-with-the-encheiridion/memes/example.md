# Example

**Example** (*paradeigma*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses is a central pedagogical tool — the use of concrete, historical or mythical figures as patterns for moral imitation. Unlike abstract doctrinal [[education|instruction]], examples engage the student's admiration and [[modesty|shame]], demonstrating that the philosophical life is not a theoretical construct but a lived reality. "That you may no longer seek an example," [[epictetus|Epictetus]] declares to his students, pointing to the figures who embody Stoic principles (057-128).

Examples serve a distinctive function in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching: they answer the student's implicit question, "Has anyone actually lived this way?" By presenting [[socrates|Socrates]], Diogenes, Agrippinus, and others, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] proves that virtue is not beyond human capacity.

## Definition

In Stoic moral education, an example (*paradeigma*) is a person whose life demonstrates a philosophical principle in action. Examples differ from precepts in that they show rather than tell — they make virtue visible. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses examples drawn from three sources: the philosophical tradition ([[socrates|Socrates]], Diogenes, Zeno), contemporary Roman life (Agrippinus, Helvidius Priscus), and mythic history (Heracles, Odysseus).

The example has a specific psychological effect: it produces *[[modesty|aidōs]]* — the sense of [[modesty|shame]] or respectful awe that arises when one compares oneself to a superior model. This is not destructive [[modesty|shame]] but productive self-awareness; the example shows the student both what is possible and how far he has to go.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses examples throughout the Discourses in several characteristic ways.

**Contemporary examples**: In Discourse 19, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] expresses a preference for recent examples over ancient ones. "We may no longer use in the schools the examples of the ancients, but may have some example of our own" (019-072, 019-073). This reveals a key pedagogical principle: examples are most effective when the student can verify that a real person — not a legendary hero — achieved moral excellence. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] supplies such examples: Agrippinus, who exercised [[tranquillity|calm]]ly while awaiting the Senate's verdict (001-035); Helvidius Priscus, who told Vespasian that it was the emperor's role to put him to death and his own role to speak the truth.

**[[socrates|Socrates]] as the supreme example**: [[socrates|Socrates]] appears throughout the Discourses as the paradigmatic philosopher. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] invokes him not as a distant authority but as a living model: "What would [[socrates|Socrates]] have done?" becomes the test for every difficult situation. In Discourse 28, [[socrates|Socrates]]'s [[tranquillity|calm]] acceptance of death is presented as the standard for all human conduct.

**Natural examples**: In Discourse 39, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses examples from nature — a dog, a horse, a nightingale — to illustrate that beauty is relative to nature. "Since we see a dog naturally formed for one thing, and a horse for another, and for another still, as an example, a nightingale, we may generally declare each of them to be beautiful then when it is most excellent according to its nature" (039-009). The natural examples prepare the ground for the moral point: a human being is beautiful when he excels according to *human* nature, which is rational and virtuous.

**Examples as moral suasion**: In Discourse 57, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] urges his students: "Look to these examples, if you would be free" (057-154). The examples are not optional ornaments to the teaching — they are the teaching. To "look to" an example is to engage in the imaginative exercise of placing oneself alongside the model and measuring the distance.
