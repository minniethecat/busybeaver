## Definition

Fear, in Stoic psychology, is one of the four primary passions — an irrational contraction of the soul in response to the appearance of a future evil. It arises from the error of attaching value to things outside one's [[power|control]].

The Stoic diagnosis is precise: we fear only because we have placed our good in things that can be taken from us. The remedy is not the removal of external threats but the correction of the judgment that makes those threats seem harmful. "If I feel that all these things do not concern me, he does not threaten me at all; but if I fear any of them, it is I whom he threatens" (019-015).

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats fear as the direct consequence of misidentifying where good and evil lie. His analysis appears most concentratedly in Discourse 20 ("That [[confidence|Confidence]] Is Not Inconsistent with [[caution|Caution]]") and Discourse 19 ("On the [[freedom|Freedom]] of the Will").

The core [[disputation|argument]] is that fear and confidence are properly directed at different objects. "Where things are not dependent on the will, there you should employ confidence, but where they are dependent on the will, there you should employ caution" (020-013–020-014). The reason is that things independent of the will — death, exile, pain, infamy — cannot touch what is truly good; they are indifferent. One should be confident in facing them. But things dependent on the will — right choice, just action, faithful speech — are where the real good and evil lie; there, caution is appropriate.

Human beings, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] observes, systematically reverse this: "In what cases do we fear? In things which are independent of the will. In what cases on the contrary do we behave with confidence, as if there were no danger? In things dependent on the will" (020-026–020-029). This inversion is the root of moral and psychological disorder.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses a vivid analogy: we are like deer who, fleeing the huntsman's feathers (a harmless scare), run into the nets (real destruction), "perishing by confounding things which are objects of fear with things that they ought not to fear" (020-023–020-026).

The practical path to overcoming fear is progressive. When fear of death arises, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not offer consolation — he offers examination: "Is death something that can harm me? No. Then why do I fear it?" The goal is to place one's good so firmly in the will that even death becomes indifferent (017-028). The man who has laid aside fear becomes a light and free being: "How much lighter you have become when you have laid aside fear" (015-025).

## Key Passages

> In what cases do we fear? In things which are independent of the will. In what cases on the contrary do we behave with confidence? In things dependent on the will. (020-026–020-029)

> If I feel that all these things do not concern me, he does not threaten me at all; but if I fear any of them, it is I whom he threatens. (019-015)

> We convert natural confidence into audacity, desperation, rashness, shamelessness; and we convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness. (020-034–020-036)

> Shall I not escape from the fear of death? (017-028)

> How much lighter you become when you have laid aside fear. (015-025)
