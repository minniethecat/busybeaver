[035-001]What a man applies himself to earnestly, that he naturally

[035-002]loves. Do men then apply themselves earnestly to the things which are bad? By

[035-003]no means. Well, do they apply themselves to things which in no way concern

[035-004]themselves? Not to these either. It remains then that they employ themselves

[035-005]earnestly only about things which are [[good]]; and if they are earnestly employed

[035-006]about things, they love such things also. Whoever then understands what is good

[035-007]can also know how to love; but he who cannot distinguish good from bad, and

[035-008]things which are neither good nor bad from both, how can he possess the power

[035-009]of loving? To love, then, is only in the power of the wise.

[035-010]For universally, be not deceived, every animal is attached to nothing so much

[035-011]as to its own interests. Whatever then appears to it an impediment to this

[035-012]interest, whether this be a brother, or a father, or a child, or beloved, or

[035-013]lover, it hates, spurns, curses; for its [[nature]] is to love nothing so much as

[035-014]its own interests: this is father, and brother, and kinsman, and country, and

[035-015]God. When then the gods appear to us to be an impediment to this, we abuse them

[035-016]and throw down their statues and burn their temples, as Alexander ordered the

[035-017]temples of Aesculapius to be burned when his dear friend died.

[035-018]For this [[reason]], if a man put in the same place his interest, sanctity,

[035-019]goodness, and country, and parents, and friends, all these are secured: but if

[035-020]he puts in one place his interest, in another his friends, and his country and

[035-021]his kinsmen and justice itself, all these give way, being borne down by the

[035-022]weight of interest. For where the I and the Mine are placed, to that place of

[035-023]necessity the animal inclines; if in the flesh, there is the ruling power; if

[035-024]in the will, it is there; and if it is in [[externals]], it is there. If then I am

[035-025]there where my will is, then only shall I be a friend such as I ought to be,

[035-026]and son, and father; for this will be my interest, to maintain the character of

[035-027]fidelity, of modesty, of patience, of abstinence, of active co-operation, of

[035-028]observing my relations (towards all). But if I put myself in one place, and

[035-029]honesty in another, then the doctrine of Epicurus becomes strong, which asserts

[035-030]either that there is no honesty or it is that which opinion holds to be honest

[035-031](virtuous).

[035-032]It was through this ignorance that the Athenians and the Lacedaemonians

[035-033]quarrelled, and the Thebans with both; and the great king quarrelled with

[035-034]Hellas, and the Macedonians with both: and the Romans with the Getae. And still

[035-035]earlier the Trojan war happened for these reasons. Alexander was the guest of

[035-036]Menelaus, and if any man had seen their friendly disposition, he would not have

[035-037]believed any one who said that they were not friends. But there was cast

[035-038]between them (as between dogs) a bit of meat, a handsome woman, and about her

[035-039]war arose. And now when you see brothers to be friends appearing to have one

[035-040]mind, do not conclude from this anything about their friendship, not even if

[035-041]they swear it and say that it is impossible for them to be separated from one

[035-042]another. For the ruling principle of a bad man cannot be trusted; it is

[035-043]insecure, has no certain rule by which it is directed, and is overpowered at

[035-044]different times by different appearances. But examine, not what other men

[035-045]examine, if they are born of the same parents and brought up together, and

[035-046]under the same pedagogue; but examine this only, wherein they place their

[035-047]interest, whether in externals or in the will. If in externals, do not name

[035-048]them friends, no more than name them trustworthy or constant, or brave or free;

[035-049]do not name them even men, if you have any [[judgment]]. For that is not a

[035-050]principle of human nature which makes them bite one another, and abuse one

[035-051]another, and occupy deserted places or public places, as if they were

[035-052]mountains, and in the courts of justice display the acts of robbers; nor yet

[035-053]that which makes them intemperate and adulterers and corrupters, nor that which

[035-054]makes them do whatever else men do against one another through this one opinion

[035-055]only, that of placing themselves and their interests in the things which are

[035-056]not within the power of their will. But if you hear that in truth these men

[035-057]think the good to be only there, where will is, and where there is a right use

[035-058]of appearances, no longer trouble yourself whether they are father or son, or

[035-059]brothers, or have associated a long time and are companions, but when you have

[035-060]ascertained this only, confidently declare that they are friends, as you

[035-061]declare that they are faithful, that they are just. For where else is

[035-062]friendship than where there is fidelity, and modesty, where there is a

[035-063]communion of honest things and of nothing else.

[035-064]But you may say, Such a one treated me with regard so long; and did he not love

[035-065]me? How do you know, slave, if he did not regard you in the same way as he

[035-066]wipes his shoes with a sponge, or as he takes care of his beast? How do you

[035-067]know, when you have ceased to be useful as a vessel, he will not throw you away

[035-068]like a broken platter? But this woman is my wife, and we have lived together so

[035-069]long. And how long did Eriphyle live with Amphiaraus, and was the mother of

[035-070]children and of many? But a necklace came between them: and what is a necklace?

[035-071]It is the opinion about such things. That was the bestial principle, that was

[035-072]the thing which broke asunder the friendship between husband and wife, that

[035-073]which did not allow the woman to be a wife nor the mother to be a mother. And

[035-074]let every man among you who has seriously resolved either to be a friend

[035-075]himself or to have another for his friend, cut out these opinions, hate them,

[035-076]drive them from his soul. And thus first of all he will not reproach himself,

[035-077]he will not be at variance with himself, he will not change his mind, he will

[035-078]not torture himself. In the next place, to another also, who is like himself,

[035-079]he will be altogether and completely a friend. But he will bear with the man

[035-080]who is unlike himself, he will be kind to him, gentle, ready to pardon on

[035-081]account of his ignorance, on account of his being mistaken in things of the

[035-082]greatest importance; but he will be harsh to no man, being well convinced of

[035-083]Plato’s doctrine that every mind is deprived of truth unwillingly. If you

[035-084]cannot do this, yet you can do in all other respects as friends do, drink

[035-085]together, and lodge together, and sail together, and you may be born of the

[035-086]same parents, for snakes also are: but neither will they be friends, nor you,

[035-087]so long as you retain these bestial and cursed opinions.
