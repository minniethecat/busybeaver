[031-001]Where is the

[031-002][[good]]? In the will. Where is the evil? In the will. Where is neither of them? In

[031-003]those things which are independent of the will. Well then? Does any one among

[031-004]us think of these lessons out of the schools? Does any one meditate (strive) by

[031-005]himself to give an answer to things as in the case of questions?—Is it

[031-006]day?—Yes.—Is it night?—No.—Well, is the number of stars even?—I cannot

[031-007]say.—When money is shown (offered) to you, have you studied to make the proper

[031-008]answer, that money is not a good thing? Have you practised yourself in these

[031-009]answers, or only against sophisms? Why do you wonder then if in the cases which

[031-010]you have studied, in those you have improved; but in those which you have not

[031-011]studied, in those you remain the same? When the rhetorician knows that he has

[031-012]written well, that he has committed to memory what he has written, and brings

[031-013]an agreeable voice, why is he still anxious? Because he is not satisfied with

[031-014]having studied. What then does he want? To be praised by the audience? For the

[031-015]purpose then of being able to practise declamation he has been disciplined; but

[031-016]with respect to praise and blame he has not been disciplined. For when did he

[031-017]hear from any one what praise is, what blame is, what the [[nature]] of each is,

[031-018]what kind of praise should be sought, or what kind of blame should be shunned?

[031-019]And when did he practise this [[discipline]] which follows these words (things)?

[031-020]Why then do you still wonder, if in the matters which a man has learned, there

[031-021]he surpasses others, and in those in which he has not been disciplined, there

[031-022]he is the same with the many. So the lute player knows how to play, sings well,

[031-023]and has a fine dress, and yet he trembles when he enters on the stage; for

[031-024]these matters he understands, but he does not know what a crowd is, nor the

[031-025]shouts of a crowd, nor what ridicule is. Neither does he know what anxiety is,

[031-026]whether it is our work or the work of another, whether it is possible to stop

[031-027]it or not. For this [[reason]] if he has been praised, he leaves the theatre puffed

[031-028]up, but if he has been ridiculed, the swollen bladder has been punctured and

[031-029]subsides.

[031-030]This is the case also with ourselves. What do we admire? Externals. [[About]] what

[031-031]things are we busy? Externals. And have we any doubt then why we fear or why we

[031-032]are anxious? What then happens when we think the things, which are coming on

[031-033]us, to be evils? It is not in our power not to be afraid, it is not in our

[031-034]power not to be anxious. Then we say, Lord God, how shall I not be anxious?

[031-035]Fool, have you not hands, did not God make them for you? Sit down now and pray

[031-036]that your nose may not run. Wipe yourself rather and do not blame him. Well

[031-037]then, has he given to you nothing in the present case? Has he not given to you

[031-038]endurance? Has he not given to you magnanimity? Has he not given to you

[031-039]manliness? When you have such hands do you still look for one who shall wipe

[031-040]your nose? But we neither study these things nor care for them. Give me a man

[031-041]who cares how he shall do anything, not for the obtaining of a thing, but who

[031-042]cares about his own energy. What man, when he is walking about, cares for his

[031-043]own energy? Who, when he is deliberating, cares about his own deliberation, and

[031-044]not about obtaining that about which he deliberates? And if he succeeds, he is

[031-045]elated and says, How well we have deliberated; did I not tell you, brother,

[031-046]that it is impossible, when we have thought about anything, that it should not

[031-047]turn out thus? But if the thing should turn out otherwise, the wretched man is

[031-048]humbled; he knows not even what to say about what has taken place. Who among us

[031-049]for the sake of this matter has consulted a seer? Who among us as to his

[031-050]actions has not slept in indifference? Who? Give (name) to me one that I may

[031-051]see the man whom I have long been looking for, who is truly noble and

[031-052]ingenuous, whether young or old; name him.

[031-053]What then are the things which are heavy on us and disturb us? What else than

[031-054]opinions? What else than opinions lies heavy upon him who goes away and leaves

[031-055]his companions and friends and places and habits of life? Now little children,

[031-056]for instance, when they cry on the nurse leaving them for a short time, forget

[031-057]their sorrow if they receive a small cake. Do you choose then that we should

[031-058]compare you to little children? No, by [[zeus|Zeus]], for I do not wish to be pacified

[031-059]by a small cake, but by right opinions. And what are these? Such as a man ought

[031-060]to study all day, and not to be affected by anything that is not his own,

[031-061]neither by companion nor place nor gymnasia, and not even by his own body, but

[031-062]to remember the law and to have it before his eyes. And what is the divine law?

[031-063]To keep a man’s own, not to claim that which belongs to others, but to use what

[031-064]is given, and when it is not given, not to [[desire]] it; and when a thing is taken

[031-065]away, to give it up readily and immediately, and to be thankful for the time

[031-066]that a man has had the use of it, if you would not cry for your nurse and

[031-067]mamma. For what matter does it make by what thing a man is subdued, and on what

[031-068]he depends? In what respect are you better than he who cries for a girl, if you

[031-069]grieve for a little gymnasium, and little porticos, and young men, and such

[031-070]places of amusement? Another comes and laments that he shall no longer drink

[031-071]the water of Dirce. Is the Marcian water worse than that of Dirce? But I was

[031-072]used to the water of Dirce. And you in turn will be used to the other. Then if

[031-073]you become attached to this also, cry for this too, and try to make a verse

[031-074]like the verse of Euripides,

[031-075]The hot baths of Nero and the Marcian water.

[031-076]See how tragedy is made when common things happen to silly men.

[031-077]When then shall I see Athens again and the Acropolis? Wretch, are you not

[031-078]content with what you see daily? Have you anything better or greater to see

[031-079]than the sun, the moon, the stars, the whole earth, the sea? But if indeed you

[031-080]comprehend Him who administers the whole, and carry him about in yourself, do

[031-081]you still desire small stones and a beautiful rock?
