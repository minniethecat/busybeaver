# Xenophon

## Biography

Xenophon (c. 430 – c. 354 BCE) was an Athenian soldier, historian, and one of the companions of [[socrates|Socrates]]. A pupil of [[socrates|Socrates]] in his youth, he later led the surviving Greek mercenaries of the failed expedition of Cyrus the Younger back to the sea, an exploit he recorded himself in the Anabasis.

For the Stoics, however, his lasting importance lay in his Socratic memoirs. Alongside that famous account of the march of the Ten Thousand, he wrote a group of "Socratic" works — the Memorabilia, the Apology, and the Symposium — that preserve, beside [[plato|Plato]]'s dialogues, a portrait of [[socrates|Socrates]]'s conversation and character.

It is in this last capacity, as a witness to [[socrates|Socrates]], that he enters the Discourses.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not discuss Xenophon's life or his military writings; he cites him strictly as a documentary source. When he wants to prove a claim about [[socrates|Socrates]]'s conduct, he sends the reader directly to Xenophon's book, treating the Symposium as reliable evidence for how the philosopher actually behaved (028-016).

He names Xenophon twice, both times as a book to be consulted rather than a thinker to be weighed. The reference is characteristic of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s method: rather than merely assert [[socrates|Socrates]]'s virtues, he points to the record in which they can be checked (061-005).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Xenophon's value is that he documents [[socrates|Socrates]] — the supreme [[example|exemplar]] of the philosophical life. Arguing that the trained man should never quarrel but should bear with abusive persons and put an end to strife, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] appeals to Xenophon's account for proof:

> If you would know what great power he had in this way, read the Symposium of Xenophon, and you will see how many quarrels he put an end to. (028-015 – 028-016)

He makes the same appeal a second time in praising the life of [[socrates|Socrates]], "who not only himself on all occasions avoided fights (quarrels), but would not allow even others to quarrel": "See in Xenophon's Symposium how many quarrels he settled, how further he endured Thrasymachus" (061-004 – 061-006).

In both places Xenophon is the guarantor of a claim about [[socrates|Socrates]] that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] wants his students to imitate.

## References in the Discourses

- **The Symposium as evidence**: "read the Symposium of Xenophon, and you will see how many quarrels he put an end to" (028-014 – 028-016).
- **Witness to [[socrates|Socrates]]'s forbearance**: "See in Xenophon's Symposium how many quarrels he settled, how further he endured Thrasymachus" (061-004 – 061-006).
