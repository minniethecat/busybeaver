## Definition

Hope (Greek: *elpis*) in Stoic philosophy is the expectation of a future good — a forward-looking impulse (*hormē*) that, like desire, belongs to the faculty of choice. The Stoics classified hope as a species of desire, and like all desires, it is subject to the discipline of assent: one must carefully examine whether the object of hope is genuinely good (virtue) or merely an indifferent (health, wealth, reputation). Hope detached from reason becomes a source of perturbation, as the hoped-for outcome may fail to materialize. Properly directed, hope is aligned with the rational acceptance of whatever providence brings.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats hope with characteristic suspicion, recognizing it as a form of attachment to future externals. His analysis begins from a simple observation: people habitually hope that things "may turn out well" (001-034), yet this very hoping reveals that they have placed their good in something outside their [[power|control]]. The philosophical alternative is not despair but a reorientation of hope toward what is truly one's own — the proper use of [[appearances|impressions]] and the cultivation of virtue.

He exposes the common structure of anxious hope through a domestic image: "In Greece some one is come; I hope that there is no harm. In this manner, every place and every occasion is the cause of disquiet" (054-043 through 054-044). The person who hopes for harmlessness from external arrivals is perpetually unsettled, because every new circumstance triggers the same cycle of hope and fear. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s remedy is to transfer hope from external outcomes to internal dispositions.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] opens with the foundational distinction that structures [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire treatment of hope: "The profession (hope) of obtaining that which you desire; and the profession (hope) in aversion" (070-001 through 070-002). Hope is here rendered as a "profession" — a declared commitment — that governs both pursuit and [[aversion|avoidance]]. The discipline of hope therefore consists in restricting one's professions to what lies within the sphere of choice. To hope for an external is to set oneself up for disappointment; to hope for virtue is to aim at what is always attainable.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also raises the more radical question of whether hope itself should be abandoned. He asks: "Is it from this day forward that we resolve to hope for nothing from ourselves?" (056-027). The question is deliberately provocative — its target is not all hope but the false hope that looks to externals for fulfillment. The genuine Stoic places "all their hopes in this" (108-004): the cultivation of the rational faculty and the alignment of the will with nature.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies the common structure of wishful thinking: hoping things "may turn out well" (001-034) reveals attachment to externals.
- He disclaims any pretension to superiority: "I hope not. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is not superior to [[socrates|Socrates]]" (002-016) — a rare [[mask|persona]]l note that situates hope within the modesty of the philosophical aspirant.
- He rhetorically denies that philosophy "teaches us to despise kings? I hope not" (019-020), distinguishing Stoic indifference from arrogant contempt.
- The anxious-hope cycle: "In Greece some one is come; I hope that there is no harm" (054-043 through 054-044) — every new event becomes a trigger for disquiet.
- The radical question: "Is it from this day forward that we resolve to hope for nothing from ourselves?" (056-027) — challenging the listener to abandon false hope.
- The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]'s opening defines the "profession (hope) of obtaining" and the "profession (hope) in aversion" (070-001 through 070-002) as the two domains requiring philosophical discipline.
- The ultimate reorientation: to "place all their hopes in this" (108-004) — meaning the cultivation of the rational self — is the mark of the philosopher.
