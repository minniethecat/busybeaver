## Definition

Progress (*prokope*) is the Stoic concept of moral and philosophical advancement toward virtue and the excellent life.

Crucially, Stoic progress is not measured by intellectual acquisition — how many books one has read or how well one can expound [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] — but by the observable transformation of one's character.

The test is whether one's desires have become disciplined, whether one's aversions are directed only at things within one's power, and whether one faces external events with equanimity.

Progress is the gradual approach toward the ideal of the [[sage|Sage]], understood not as a single dramatic conversion but as daily, incremental work on the ruling faculty.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment of progress in Discourse 4 ("Of Progress or Improvement") is among his most distinctive teachings. He relentlessly attacks the assumption that philosophical progress consists in mastering texts or performing intellectual displays.

"What is the product of virtue? [[tranquillity|Tranquillity]]. Who then makes improvement? Is it he who has read many books of [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]?" (004-015–004-018). The answer is no: one can read all of [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] and remain morally unchanged.

True progress, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], is located in the transformation of one's fundamental orientation. The person making progress has withdrawn desire from externals entirely and directed aversion only at what depends on the will (004-006–004-007).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] tells the story of a man proudly announcing he can read [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] by himself — "Indeed, sir, you are making great progress. What kind of progress?" (004-022). The sarcasm is pointed: [[books|reading]] a text unaided is no measure of moral advancement.

The genuine test is whether one has learned to rid life of [[grief|lamentation]], groaning, and the cry of "Wretched that I am" (004-062–004-064).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] locates the field of progress precisely in the three disciplines: "Where is your work? In desire and in aversion... in your pursuit and avoiding... in assent and suspension of assent, that you be not deceived" (004-025–004-029).

Progress is not one thing and virtue another; progress is the approach toward virtue, measured by the same criteria (004-010–004-013). He who rises in the morning and applies his principles in every action — bathing as one of fidelity, eating as one of modesty — "this is the man who truly makes progress" (004-057).

## Key Passages

> He who is making progress, having learned from philosophers that desire means the desire of good things... such a man takes from himself desire altogether and defers it, but he employs his aversion only on things which are dependent on his will. (004-001–004-007)

> How then do we admit that virtue is such as I have said, and yet seek progress in other things and make a display of it? (004-014)

> Never then look for the matter itself in one place, and progress towards it in another. (004-044)

> This is the man who truly makes progress, and this is the man who has not travelled in vain. (004-057)
