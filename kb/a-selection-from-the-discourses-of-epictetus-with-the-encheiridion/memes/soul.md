## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, the soul (*psychē*) is a corporeal substance — a portion of the [[divinity|divine]] breath (*pneuma*) that pervades and animates the living body. The soul is not an immaterial spirit in the [[plato|Plato]]nic sense but a physical entity in a state of tension (*tonos*), responsible for perception, impulse, assent, and reason. The soul's highest part is the ruling principle (*hēgemonikon*), located in the heart, which processes [[appearances|impressions]] and issues judgments. At death, the individual soul separates from the body and, according to orthodox [[stoicism|Stoicism]], either dissolves back into the cosmic whole or (for the wise) persists for a limited duration.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats the soul primarily through the lens of moral psychology rather than physics. He speaks of "greatness of soul" (*[[magnanimity|megalopsychia]]*) as a quality to be cultivated (006-050, 006-052), and of a "sickly soul" that recoils from the philosophical cure (030-051). He describes the soul's natural operation: "it is the nature of every soul to assent to the truth, to dissent from the false" (041-005), and "the soul will never reject the good when it clearly appears" (041-013). He compares the soul's receptivity to a ray of light (041-040). [[disease|Disease]] of the soul, like disease of the body, leaves "traces and blisters" that careful philosophical [[exercise|practice]] must heal (033-023). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws a sharp distinction between soul, body, and externals as the three domains of human concern (043-008), teaching that the pleasure of the soul — not the body — is what the philosopher pursues (043-019, 043-024). He urges his students to face the "severance of the soul and the body" (046-047) with equanimity, since death is no evil.

## Stoic Context

The Stoic theory of soul builds on the physical doctrines of Zeno and [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]. Unlike [[plato|Plato]]'s tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite), the Stoic soul is a unified entity with the ruling principle at its center — all impulses and emotions are ultimately judgments of the *hēgemonikon*. The soul's *tonos* determines moral character: proper tension yields virtue, slackness yields vice. Cleanthes compared the soul's relation to the body to an octopus's tentacles extending throughout. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the physical details are downplayed in favor of practical moral psychology: the soul is the arena where the struggle for virtue takes place.
