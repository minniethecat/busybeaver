# Chrysippus

## Biography

Chrysippus of Soli (c. 279–206 BCE) was the third head of the Stoic school in [[athens|Athens]], succeeding Cleanthes. He is credited with systematizing Stoic doctrine and defending it against the attacks of the Academic skeptics. It was said of him: "If there had been no Chrysippus, there would have been no Stoa."

Chrysippus was a prolific writer, said to have authored over 700 works. None of his writings survive complete; our knowledge of his thought comes from fragments and testimonia preserved by later authors, including [[epictetus|Epictetus]].

## Role in Epictetus

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] cites Chrysippus as the preeminent authority on Stoic doctrine, frequently invoking him to settle technical philosophical questions. "What does Chrysippus say?" is a refrain that appears throughout the Discourses.

Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s attitude toward Chrysippus is complex. While he respects Chrysippus's intellectual achievements, he warns against treating the study of Chrysippus's works as an end in itself. "What is the product of virtue?" he asks rhetorically. "Does virtue consist in having understood Chrysippus?" (004-017). The point is not to become a scholar of Chrysippus but to become a good person — and for that, Chrysippus's writings are means, not ends.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] particularly values Chrysippus's role in making Stoic philosophy accessible and defensible. Chrysippus took the insights of Zeno and Cleanthes and gave them the systematic form that later Stoics, including [[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself, would build upon.

## Key Passages

- **Chrysippus as authority**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly invokes "What does Chrysippus say?" to settle questions.
- **[[virtue|Virtue]] vs. scholarship**: "Does virtue consist in having understood Chrysippus? No" (004-017).
- **Chrysippus as systematizer**: The one who gave [[stoicism|Stoicism]] its definitive form.
