## Definition

Faith (Greek: *[[fidelity|pistis]]*) in Stoic usage carries a broader meaning than its modern English religious connotations. It encompasses fidelity, [[fidelity|trustworthiness]], good faith, and moral [[firmness|constancy]] — the quality of being *pistos* (faithful, reliable). In Stoic theology, faith also refers to the conviction that the cosmos is governed by [[divinity|divine]] reason (*logos*) and that all events unfold according to a providential order. This dual sense — [[fidelity|faithfulness]] as a moral virtue and faith as trust in the [[divinity|divine]] — is unified in the Stoic ideal of the sage, whose unwavering character reflects the [[firmness|constancy]] of cosmic reason itself.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats [[fidelity|faithfulness]] (*[[fidelity|pistis]]*) as a defining characteristic of the philosophical life, repeatedly pairing it with modesty (*[[modesty|aidōs]]*) as the twin markers of moral character. He asks pointedly: "If you choose to be modest and faithful, who shall not allow you to be so?" (021-007) — affirming that [[fidelity|faithfulness]], as a quality of the will, is entirely within one's power, regardless of external circumstances.

The dynamic relationship between action and character is central to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s treatment. He argues that "the actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man, and the contrary actions destroy him" (025-025). This is not mere tautology but a substantive claim about moral psychology: each faithful act reinforces the disposition of [[fidelity|faithfulness]], while each betrayal entrenches faithlessness. Similarly, "the actions of fidelity preserve" the faithful person "as fire consumes wood" (025-027) — a vivid image of how character is built and destroyed through action.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] grounds human [[fidelity|faithfulness]] in [[divinity|divine]] [[fidelity|faithfulness]]. He argues from analogy: "If the [[divinity|divine]] is faithful, man also must be faithful" (029-036). The [[firmness|constancy]] of the [[divinity|gods]] provides the template for human moral [[firmness|constancy]]. To be faithless is not merely to violate a social convention but to contradict the nature of the rational being, which is fashioned in the image of the [[divinity|divine]].

The portrait of the faithful person recurs throughout the Discourses as [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s ideal. The philosopher shows himself "faithful, modest, noble, free from perturbation" (024-080). The faithful person is "by nature modest, faithful" (024-067) — suggesting that [[fidelity|faithfulness]] is not an acquired refinement but a return to one's natural condition. Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] also acknowledges the opposite: "wolves, faithless and treacherous and mischievous" (003-018) represent the degradation of character that results from abandoning fidelity to reason.

## Key Passages

- [[epictetus|Epictetus]] poses the rhetorical question: "If you choose to be modest and faithful, who shall not allow you to be so?" (021-007), affirming the inviolability of moral choice.
- The faithful person is described "by nature" as "modest, faithful" (024-067) and resolves to show himself "faithful, modest, noble, free from perturbation" (024-080).
- The actions-fidelity dynamic: "The actions of fidelity preserve the faithful man" (025-025), just as "faithlessness strengthens the faithless man" (025-027).
- The will to be faithful: "have the will modest and faithful or [[modesty|shame]]less and faithless" (026-064) — the choice is presented as binary and entirely within one's power.
- Divine [[example|exemplar]]: "If the [[divinity|divine]] is faithful, man also must be faithful" (029-036), grounding human virtue in cosmic order.
- Hypocrisy exposed: people "declare that they are faithful, that they are just" (035-061) while their actions reveal the opposite — character is demonstrated, not professed.
- The faithful person is contrasted with the faithless as "a man who is faithful and modest, to a man who" is not (068-038).
