# Promise

**Promise** (*epangelia*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s vocabulary is a technical term drawn from Stoic teleology. Every natural kind has a distinctive "promise" or undertaking — the function it was designed by nature to fulfill. For a human being, the promise is to live as a rational and moral agent, not as a wild [[animal|beast]] driven by impulse or a sheep governed by appetite.

The concept bridges [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s physics (the nature of each kind of being) with his ethics (the [[duties|obligation]]s that follow from being human). To "fulfill the promise of a man's nature" is the very definition of the philosophical life.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] develops the concept of promise most fully in Discourse 25, where he asks: "What is a man? A rational and mortal being" (025-003). Because human nature is defined by the rational faculty, fulfilling the human promise means acting according to reason and refraining from behavior that degrades us to the level of animals.

The [[disputation|argument]] proceeds by negative example. When we act gluttonously or lewdly, we descend to the level of sheep. When we act contentiously, harmfully, and violently, we descend to wild [[animal|beasts]] (025-010). In either case, "you have not fulfilled your promise" (025-006). The promise is not an external [[duties|obligation]] but the internal standard of what it means to be human.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] extends the logic analogically. Just as a conjunctive proposition is preserved when it fulfills what its nature promises, and flutes, lyres, horses, and dogs are each preserved when they "severally keep their promise" (025-019), so too a human being is preserved — made whole and flourishing — by corresponding acts: "modest actions preserve the modest man, and immodest actions destroy him" (025-024).

The concept also appears in Discourse 10, "What [[philosophy|Philosophy]] Promises." There [[epictetus|Epictetus]] clarifies that philosophy does not promise externals — wealth, health, reputation — but rather "in every circumstance I will maintain the ruling faculty in a condition conformable to nature" (010-009). [[philosophy|Philosophy]]'s promise is to deliver the very thing that human nature itself promises: a life governed by reason.

The citizen and son also have their respective promises. "What does the character of a citizen promise?" (026-010). "What does this character promise?" (026-023). These nested promises — human, citizen, son — reflect the Stoic doctrine of appropriate acts (*kathēkonta*), where each role brings its own normative claims.

## Key Passages

- [[virtue|Virtue]]'s promise: "Now if virtue promises good fortune and happiness and freedom from perturbation" (004-009).
- [[philosophy|Philosophy]]'s promise: "[[philosophy|Philosophy]] promises none of these. In every circumstance I will maintain the ruling faculty conformable to nature" (010-009).
- The definitional passage: "only, to fulfil the promise of a man's nature. For what is a man? A rational and mortal being" (025-002).
- Failure to fulfill: "you have not fulfilled your promise. See that you do nothing like a sheep; but if you do, in this case also the man is lost" (025-006).
- The preservative logic: "When are flutes, a lyre, a horse, a dog, preserved? When they severally keep their promise" (025-019).
- Role-based promise: "What does the character of a citizen promise?" (026-010).
