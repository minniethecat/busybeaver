# Medea

## Biography

Medea is the sorceress of Greek tragedy who, betrayed by her husband Jason, takes vengeance by killing their children. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] knows her chiefly through the celebrated verses of Euripides, and he uses her — as he uses the Homeric figures — not as a character to condemn but as a clinical case in the psychology of action.

His central use of her comes in Discourse 18, where he is arguing that no one chooses what he does not believe to be useful: "Can then a man think that a thing is useful to him and not choose it? He cannot" (018-014 to 018-015).

To prove it he cites Medea's own words: "'Tis true I know what evil I shall do, but passion overpowers the better counsel" (018-016 to 018-017). His gloss is precise: she thought "to indulge her passion and take vengeance on her husband was more profitable than to spare her children. It was so; but she was deceived" (018-018 to 018-019).

From this he draws a conclusion about how she should be met. So long as her false [[opinion]] stands, "what can she follow except that which appears to herself?" (018-021 to 018-022). Show her plainly that she is deceived, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] says, "and she will not do it" (018-020).

Her crime is therefore an error of judgment, not sheer [[vice|wickedness]] — she "has been bewildered about the most important things, and is become a viper" (018-023). She reappears in Discourse 68 as shorthand for the corrupted will, one willing to "murder your own children, like Medea" (068-042 to 068-043).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

Medea is one of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most important illustrations of the Socratic-Stoic thesis that all wrongdoing is involuntary — that no one errs willingly, but only through a mistaken [[judgment]] about what is good.

Even the child-murderer acts on what appears to her the more profitable course (018-018); her tragedy is that "[[passion]] overpowers the better counsel" (018-017), so that a false appearance is followed as if it were true.

This shapes [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s counsel about anger and blame. Because Medea does her evil only under the spell of a deception, the fitting response is not fury but the pity "as we pity the blind and the lame" (018-024 to 018-025) — those "maimed in the faculties which are supreme" (018-025 to 018-026).

Her second appearance sharpens the point: in Discourse 68 she stands for anyone who has "dishonored his own faculty of will" (068-040) and would sacrifice what is nearest for "some small bit of money or some office" (068-041). Of such a man [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks, "where (in what) is this equality (fairness)?" (068-043).

## References in the Discourses

- **"[[passion|Passion]] overpowers the better counsel"**: even a monstrous act is chosen as the apparently profitable one; she does it because "she was deceived" (018-016, 018-018).
- **Response to her error**: she is to be shown her deception, not hated (018-020, 018-023).
- **Shorthand for the corrupted will**: one ready to "murder your own children" like Medea (068-042).
