# Mask

The **tragic mask** — *prosōpon* in Greek — is a Socratic metaphor that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] adopts and develops into one of his most striking teaching devices. In Greek theatre, actors wore exaggerated masks that were terrifying or pitiable at a distance but empty and harmless when examined up close. By analogy, the things that most frighten human beings — [[death]], [[pain]], exile, poverty — are masks. They appear formidable because of ignorance, but when turned over and examined by reason, they reveal themselves to be hollow.

The metaphor is explicitly attributed to [[socrates|Socrates]] by [[epictetus|Epictetus]]: "These things [[socrates|Socrates]] properly used to call tragic masks" (020-049). It belongs to the discipline of assent — the [[exercise|practice]] of interrogating appearances before granting them the power to disturb.

## Definition

In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s usage, a mask is any appearance (*phantasia*) that presents itself as terrible or desirable but whose power over us depends on our failure to examine it. The mask works through ignorance: "For what is a child? [[ignorance|Ignorance]]. What is a child? Want of knowledge" (020-052). A child is frightened by a theatrical mask because the child does not know what lies behind it. Adults, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] suggests, are in precisely the same position with respect to the apparent evils of life — they are frightened because they have not yet acquired the philosophical knowledge that would neutralize the fear.

The remedy is not courage in the conventional sense — steeling oneself against fear — but examination. "Turn it and examine it" (020-054) is the repeated refrain. [[reason|Reason]] is the act of turning the mask over and seeing that there is nothing behind it that can harm the moral self.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents the mask metaphor in a single sustained passage in Discourse 20, but the passage is among the most concentrated and rhetorically powerful in the entire Discourses. He proceeds through a sequence of apparent evils, applying the same treatment to each.

**[[death|Death]] is a mask**. "What is death? A tragic mask. Turn it and examine it. See, it does not bite" (020-054). The reasoning is strictly physical: "The poor body must be separated from the spirit either now or later as it was separated from it before. Why then are you troubled?" (020-055). [[death|Death]] is a natural event, the dissolution of a temporary compound. It cannot be an evil because it is inevitable and universal — part of the completion of "the period of the universe" (020-058).

**[[pain|Pain]] is a mask**. "What is pain? A mask. Turn it and examine it" (020-059). [[pain|Pain]] is a rough motion of the flesh, nothing more — "The poor flesh is moved roughly, then on the contrary smoothly" (020-060). If the flesh cannot bear it, the door stands open (a reference to suicide as a rational exit). If it can bear it, one bears it. In neither case is the moral self — the ruling faculty — affected.

**The Socratic pedigree**. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] traces the mask metaphor to [[socrates|Socrates]], who used it to combat what [[epictetus|Epictetus]] calls "rashness, and indifference" (020-049) — the two errors of the untrained mind. Rashness leaps to judgment without examination; indifference fails to distinguish what truly matters from what does not. The mask metaphor corrects both: it demands examination (turning the mask) and it teaches discrimination (the hollowness behind).

## Key Passages

> These things Socrates properly used to call tragic masks; for as to children masks appear terrible and fearful from inexperience, we also are affected in like manner by events for no other reason than children are by masks. (020-049–052)

> What is death? A tragic mask. Turn it and examine it. See, it does not bite. (020-054)

> What is pain? A mask. Turn it and examine it. The poor flesh is moved roughly, then on the contrary smoothly. (020-059–060)
