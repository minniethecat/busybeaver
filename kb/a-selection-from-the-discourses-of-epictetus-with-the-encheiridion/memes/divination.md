## Definition

Divination is the [[exercise|practice]] of consulting supernatural signs — the viscera of sacrificed animals, the flight of birds, oracles, and similar omens — to gain knowledge of future events.

In the Greco-Roman world, divination was a widely accepted religious and civic [[exercise|practice]]. The Stoics, unusually among philosophical schools, gave qualified endorsement to divination as consistent with their doctrine of providence and cosmic interconnectedness.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]], however, takes a distinctive approach: rather than debating the validity of divination, he questions its psychological motivation, arguing that frequent recourse to [[divinity|divine]]rs reveals a failure of philosophical understanding.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats divination in Discourse 24 ("How We Ought to Use Divination") and again in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] (§XXXII). His approach is notably different from that of earlier Stoics.

He does not deny divination may reveal truths about the future; rather, he argues the need for divination arises from a moral failing: "What then leads us to frequent use of divination? Cowardice, the dread of what will happen" (024-018).

The core of his [[disputation|argument]] is that a genuine philosopher already possesses everything necessary for right decision. "Have I not within me a [[divinity|divine]]r who has told me the nature of good and of evil?" (024-005–024-006).

The inner "[[divinity|divine]]r" is reason itself, trained by Stoic philosophy to distinguish what is truly good (virtue) from what is merely preferred or dispreferred. If one genuinely knows that externals — death, poverty, exile — are neither good nor evil, then no [[divinity|divine]]r's prediction can affect one's moral choice.

In the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] gives practical counsel: when one must consult divination, go with the attitude of [[socrates|Socrates]] — prepared to follow advice only about things indifferent, never to compromise virtue on the basis of an omen (100-012).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also notes the irony that people consult [[divinity|divine]]rs most eagerly about precisely what matters least from a Stoic perspective: inheritance, business success, and other externals. This pattern reveals how far they are from understanding where the true good lies (024-020–024-023).

## Key Passages

> Through an unreasonable regard to divination many of us omit many duties. For what more can the diviner see than death or danger or disease? If then I must expose myself to danger for a friend, and if it is my duty even to die for him, what need have I then for divination? (024-001–024-005)

> Have I not within me a diviner who has told me the nature of good and of evil, and has explained to me the signs of both? (024-005–024-006)

> What then leads us to frequent use of divination? Cowardice, the dread of what will happen. (024-018)

> When you have recourse to divination, remember that you do not know what the event will be; but if you are a philosopher, you know, when you come, of what nature it is. (100-001)
