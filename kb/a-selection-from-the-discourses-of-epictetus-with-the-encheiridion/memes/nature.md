# Nature

**Nature** (*physis*) is the central normative concept of Stoic philosophy. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], to "live according to nature" (*homologoumenōs tē physei zēn*) is the very definition of the philosophical life — the standard by which every [[desire]], [[judgment]], and action must be measured.

Nature is not merely the physical world. It is the intelligent design of [[god]], identical with reason (*logos*) and [[necessity|fate]] (*heimarmenē*). Understanding nature is therefore the foundation of [[freedom]]: the person who aligns their will with the cosmic order cannot be hindered by anything external.

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, **Nature** (*physis*) operates simultaneously as a descriptive and a normative principle. Descriptively, it is the rational organizing power that structures all things — "the nature and constitution of each thing" (006-022). Normatively, it is the standard against which actions and judgments are evaluated: is a given impulse "conformably to nature or not?" (004-039).

The Stoics identified Nature with God (*[[zeus|theos]]*), universal reason (*logos*), and [[necessity|fate]] (*heimarmenē*). These are not separate entities but different aspects of a single [[divinity|divine]] rational order.

Because human beings are rational by nature, to live according to nature is to live according to reason — to bring one's individual ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*) into alignment with the cosmic intelligence that governs all things.

> The being (nature) of the good is a certain will; the being of the bad is a certain kind of will. (019-001)

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] deploys the concept of nature as his ultimate court of appeal throughout the *Discourses*. Whenever he needs to establish the goodness or badness of something, he asks whether it accords with nature: "Is it conformably to nature or not? If conformably, give yourself evidence that it is conformable" (004-039).

The practical goal of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s entire teaching program is to "make the ruling faculty conformable to nature, elevated, free, unrestrained, unhindered" (004-047). This language of conformity — *kata physin* — recurs throughout the text, linking the inner work of philosophical self-examination with the outer reality of the [[divinity|divine]] cosmic order.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] grounds his ethics in the specific nature of each kind of being. A horse has one nature, a human being another; virtue for a human being consists in fulfilling the distinctive potential of human nature, which is rationality.

He constantly reminds his students that "the nature of man is not" to be hindered or enslaved (017-032). Our essential nature as rational beings means that external events cannot touch our true self.

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the person who lives according to nature achieves complete security. "If you maintain a will conformable to nature, you have every security, every facility, you have no trouble" (021-003). This is not passive resignation but active alignment — a continuous [[exercise|practice]] of examining one's [[appearances|impressions]] and desires against the standard of natural reason.

## Stoic Context

The concept of "living according to nature" originates with [[zeno|Zeno of Citium]], the founder of [[stoicism|Stoicism]]. Zeno formulated the *telos* (end or goal) of life as *homologoumenōs tē physei zēn* — "living in agreement with nature."

Cleanthes refined this formula, adding "living in agreement with nature" to Zeno's original "living in agreement." His emphasis was that the standard is external and objective, not merely internal consistency.

[[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], the third head of the Stoa and the great systematizer of Stoic doctrine, further elaborated the doctrine. He argued that individual human nature is a fragment (*apospasma*) of universal nature, so that acting according to one's own rational nature necessarily aligns with the cosmic order.

This Stoic conception contrasts sharply with Epicurean naturalism. Where the Stoics saw nature as rationally ordered by [[divinity|divine]] providence, the Epicureans saw it as the result of atomic collisions without purpose. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] inherits and sharpens the orthodox Stoic position: nature is providential, and understanding this providence is essential to the philosophical life.

The Roman Stoics — Seneca, [[musonius-rufus|Musonius Rufus]], and Marcus Aurelius — each developed the concept in their own way. But [[epictetus|Epictetus]] is distinctive in making the alignment with nature the organizing principle of his entire pedagogical method, structured around the three disciplines of desire, action, and assent.

## Key Passages

- Conformity to nature as criterion: "Is it conformably to nature or not?" (004-039).

- The goal of philosophical [[education|training]]: make the ruling faculty "conformable to nature" (004-047).

- Each thing has its own "nature and constitution" (006-022).

- Rational faculty marks nature's limit: "nature ends where the rational faculty ends" (006-034).

- Human nature's freedom: "the nature of man is not" to be hindered (017-032).

- [[security|Security]] through nature: "maintain a will conformable to nature" (021-003).

- The good defined: "The being (nature) of the good is a certain will" (019-001).

- Contemplation: "contemplate the works of nature" (019-082).
