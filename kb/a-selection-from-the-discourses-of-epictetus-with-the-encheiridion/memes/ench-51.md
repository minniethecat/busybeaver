[119-001]The first and most necessary place (part, [Greek: topos]) in philosophy is the

[119-002]use of theorems (precepts, [Greek: theoraemata]), for instance, that we must

[119-003]not lie; the second part is that of demonstrations, for instance, How is it

[119-004]proved that we ought not to lie? The third is that which is confirmatory of

[119-005]these two, and explanatory, for example, How is this a demonstration? For what

[119-006]is demonstration, what is consequence, what is contradiction, what is truth,

[119-007]what is falsehood? The third part (topic) is necessary on account of the

[119-008]second, and the second on account of the first; but the most necessary and that

[119-009]on which we ought to rest is the first. But we do the contrary. For we spend

[119-010]our time on the third topic, and all our earnestness is about it; but we

[119-011]entirely neglect the first. Therefore we lie; but the demonstration that we

[119-012]ought not to lie we have ready to hand.
