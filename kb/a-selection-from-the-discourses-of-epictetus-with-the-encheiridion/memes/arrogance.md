## Definition

In the Stoic framework of [[epictetus|Epictetus]], arrogance (*huperēphania*) is more than mere pride — it is the deeply held opinion that one is self-sufficient, complete, and in need of nothing from others or from philosophical correction. This vice blocks moral progress because the arrogant person believes they have already arrived at wisdom and therefore cannot learn (049-007).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] pairs arrogance with distrust as the two fundamental obstacles that must be "rooted out" of human beings before any philosophical [[education|training]] can succeed (049-006). Whereas distrust prevents a person from accepting truth from others, arrogance prevents a person from recognizing their own deficiency — together they form a closed loop that keeps the untrained person trapped in ignorance.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats arrogance as a practical problem of the philosophical classroom rather than an abstract vice. He observes that students who have made some small progress in Stoic study are especially susceptible: having learned to analyze syllogisms or to read [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], they become "proud of that which is the act of an ass" (049-022) — mistaking mere [[books|book-learning]] for genuine philosophical attainment.

The remedy [[epictetus|Epictetus]] prescribes is *confutation* (*elenchus*), following the Socratic method. "Arrogance is removed by confutation; and [[socrates|Socrates]] was the first" to [[exercise|practice]] this art systematically (049-009). By exposing a person's inconsistencies through questioning, the teacher demonstrates that the student does not in fact know what they thought they knew — puncturing the illusion of self-sufficiency that sustains arrogance.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also grounds his treatment of arrogance in the Stoic doctrine of kinship with the [[divinity|divine]]. If a person truly understood that they are "the son of [[zeus|Zeus]]," they would never display the arrogance that makes them insufferable to others (003-005). True self-knowledge — recognizing both one's [[divinity|divine]] lineage and one's present ignorance — is the natural antidote to arrogant presumption.

## Stoic Context

Arrogance sits within the Stoic taxonomy of passions as a species of excessive desire or elation. The broader Stoic tradition, from Zeno onward, classified the passions into four genera — desire, fear, pleasure, and [[grief|distress]] — with arrogance falling under the genus of pleasure (*hēdonē*) as a form of irrational elation at the false appearance of one's own excellence.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s distinctive contribution is his practical, pedagogical focus. While earlier Stoics analyzed arrogance within their systematic psychology, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats it primarily as a classroom problem: the student who has read a few philosophical texts and now believes they are wise. His pairing of arrogance with distrust (049-006) is original and reflects his experience as a teacher working with Roman students of varying sincerity and aptitude.

## Key Passages

> You must root out of men these two things, arrogance (pride) and distrust. Arrogance then is the opinion that you want nothing (are deficient in nothing); but distrust is the opinion that you cannot be happy when so many circumstances surround you. Arrogance is removed by confutation; and Socrates was the first who practiced this. (049-006–009)

> If you had comprehended that you are the son of Zeus, you would have known that no one could endure your arrogance. (003-005)

> Whence has this fellow got the arrogance which he displays? (024-070)
