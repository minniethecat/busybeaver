# Achilles

## Biography

Achilles is the foremost warrior of the Greek host at Troy in Homer's *Iliad*, and it is from that poem, not from history, that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] draws him. In the Discourses he is never a person with a lifespan to record; he is a worked example, a figure [[epictetus|Epictetus]] can "call them forth" and cross-examine to make a philosophical point (014-012).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to Achilles for two distinct lessons, both concerning the inner sources of conflict and ruin. The first is the great quarrel with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] at the opening of the *Iliad*, over the captive women Chryseis and Briseis — the dispute [[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats as a textbook case of two men who agree on a principle but misapply it.

The second is Achilles' own downfall, which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] deliberately relocates: not the death of Patroclus, nor the fall of Troy, but the moment his judgment was corrupted by passion. In both uses, Achilles serves the same doctrine — that what destroys a man is his own opinion and conduct, not the things that happen to him.

He belongs, in the text, alongside [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and the other Homeric figures whom [[epictetus|Epictetus]] reads as living illustrations of Stoic ethics rather than as heroes to be admired.

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the quarrel with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] to show how disputes arise from the misuse of "præcognitions" — the natural preconceptions of "ought" and "duty." Both men grant that "what is good ought to be done" (014-014); the trouble begins when they adapt that preconception to the particular case.

[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] says, "I ought not to give up Chryseis" (014-016); Achilles says, "You ought" (014-017); and [[epictetus|Epictetus]] comments that "one of the two makes a wrong adaptation of the præcognition" (014-018). The Homeric feud thus becomes a lesson in why education means learning to fit general preconceptions to particular facts.

His second use is sharper. Asking when Achilles was truly ruined, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] rejects the obvious answer: "Was it when Patroclus died? Not so" (018-067).

It happened instead "when he began to be angry, when he wept for a girl" (018-068), "when he forgot that he was at Troy not to get mistresses, but to fight" (018-069). Ruin comes "when right opinions are destroyed" (018-071), not from any external blow.

## References in the Discourses

- **The quarrel with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]**: the dispute over Chryseis as a case of misapplied preconceptions of duty — "one of the two makes a wrong adaptation of the præcognition" (014-018).
- **The ruin of Achilles**: not Patroclus's death but anger and corrupted judgment destroyed him (018-068); ruin is "when right opinions are destroyed" (018-071).
