## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, despair (Greek: *apognōsis*, ἀπόγνωσις) is not merely the absence of hope but an active passion — a form of irrational contraction (*systolē*) of the soul in the face of a perceived evil that appears insurmountable. As a passion (*pathos*), despair belongs to the class of [[grief|distress]] (*lypē*) and represents a failure of reason: it is the judgment that one's situation is hopeless and that no good can be attained. The Stoics viewed despair as particularly dangerous because, unlike ordinary fear which still acknowledges a possible good to be protected, despair abandons the pursuit of virtue entirely — it is a surrender of the ruling faculty (*hēgemonikon*) to false appearances.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats despair as a condition that falls outside the proper Stoic attitude toward moral progress. In Discourse 2, he warns against "despair of reaching the highest degree" (002-020) as a temptation that afflicts those who compare themselves unfavorably to idealized sages. His characteristic response is to redirect attention from the distant summit to the immediate step: one need not be a sage to begin practicing philosophy today. The despairing thought — "I can never attain that" — is itself an error of judgment, treating a preferred indifferent (being a perfect sage) as though it were a true good necessary for happiness.

In Discourse 63, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes a more extreme form of despair: being "affected by some madness or despair that he cares not whether he possesses" (063-014) his property, children, or wife. Here despair produces a kind of indifference that mimics Stoic detachment but is rooted in passion rather than reason. The true Stoic values externals at their proper worth (as indifferents) while maintaining inner freedom; the despairing person simply ceases to care, which is not virtue but a form of madness. [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s therapy for despair follows his general method: examine the impression, test whether the feared outcome is truly an evil, and recognize that the only genuine good — the proper use of the will — is never beyond reach.

## Key Passages

- "Despair of reaching the highest degree" (002-020) — The temptation to abandon moral progress because the ideal seems too distant.
- "Affected by some madness or despair that he cares not whether he possesses them or not" (063-014) — Despair as false indifference, distinct from genuine Stoic detachment.
- "Is it not in your power? Who shall hinder you?" (046-029) — [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s characteristic rebuttal to the despairing thought that circumstances block one's good.
