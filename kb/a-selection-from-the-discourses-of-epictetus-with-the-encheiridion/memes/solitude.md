# Solitude

**Solitude** (*erēmia*) is the condition of being alone without being helpless. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the common understanding of solitude as a [[fortune|misfortune]] — a state of deprivation and vulnerability — is a fundamental mistake. True solitude is not the absence of other people but the presence of inner resources: a mind furnished with right opinions and a character capable of being its own companion.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] devotes an entire discourse (Discourse 48) to correcting the popular dread of being alone. The man who fears solitude, he argues, has mistaken the source of his security. He has placed it in other people rather than in his own ruling faculty.

## Definition

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] opens Discourse 48 with a striking redefinition: "Solitude is a certain condition of a helpless man. For because a man is alone, he is not for that reason also solitary" (048-001).

The key distinction is between the physical fact of being alone and the psychological condition of helplessness. A person can be physically among a crowd and yet be "solitary" in the relevant sense if he lacks inner resources; conversely, a person can be physically alone and yet not be solitary if his mind is well-ordered (048-002).

The Stoic ideal of solitude finds its highest expression in the analogy with [[zeus|Zeus]]: "For as [[zeus|Zeus]] dwells with himself, and is tranquil by himself, and thinks of his own administration and of its nature, and is employed in thoughts suitable to himself; thus ought we also to be able to talk with ourselves, and not to need the aid of others" (048-021).

The [[divinity|divine]] [[example|model]] of self-sufficient contemplation shows that solitude is not a privation but a perfection.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] diagnoses the fear of solitude as a failure to understand human nature. People set out from "a certain natural principle, from the natural desire of community and mutual love and from the pleasure of conversation among men" (048-018), and they conclude that being alone is therefore a deprivation.

But this reasoning is incomplete: it ignores the fact that a wise person carries community within, through the possession of reason.

The remedy [[epictetus|Epictetus]] proposes is not to avoid being alone but to develop the inner life so that solitude becomes welcome. "Being alone, to be able to be sufficient for himself and to be his own companion" (048-021) — this is the mark of the Stoic student who has made progress.

This self-sufficiency is the same quality that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] celebrates throughout the Discourses as the defining mark of the philosopher. When he discusses the man who is "discontented with being alone," he prescribes not more company but better use of the ruling faculty: the discontented man should "call this condition by the name of tranquillity" (008-046).

Solitude, reframed in this way, becomes not a deprivation but an opportunity for peace.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] also addresses the practical fear that arises in solitude — the fear of being attacked when no one is near to help: "Well then, if some man should come upon me when I am alone and murder me? Fool, not murder You, but your poor body" (048-031).

The Stoic who has internalized the distinction between what is his (the will) and what is not his (the body) cannot be threatened even in the most isolated circumstances.

## Key Passages

> Solitude is a certain condition of a helpless man. For because a man is alone, he is not for that reason also solitary; just as though a man is among numbers, he is not for that reason not solitary. (048-001)

> For if being alone is enough to make solitude, you may say that even Zeus is solitary in the conflagration. (048-012)

> Being alone, to be able to be sufficient for himself and to be his own companion. For as Zeus dwells with himself, and is tranquil by himself... thus ought we also to be able to talk with ourselves. (048-021)

> Well then, if some man should come upon me when I am alone and murder me? Fool, not murder You, but your poor body. (048-031)

The same principle of self-sufficiency informs [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s broader teaching: if you are alone and discontented, "call this condition by the name of tranquillity" — solitude is not a deprivation but an opportunity for peace (008-046).
