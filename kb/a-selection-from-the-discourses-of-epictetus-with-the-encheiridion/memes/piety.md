# Piety

**Piety** (*eusebeia*) is the Stoic virtue of right relationship with the [[divinity|divine]]. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], piety is not merely a matter of ritual observance but the fundamental orientation of the will toward the cosmic order that [[zeus|Zeus]] administers. To be pious is to think rightly about the [[divinity|gods]], to desire what they ordain, and to accept every event as the work of a benevolent intelligence.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to the theme of piety throughout the Discourses and the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], treating it not as a separate compartment of life but as the unifying principle that connects right opinion, right desire, and right action. His most concentrated treatment appears in [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] §XXXI, which may be read as a summary of Stoic theology in practical form.

## Definition

In the Stoic framework, piety is the knowledge of how to honor the [[divinity|gods]]. It belongs to the field of ethics but draws upon physics — the Stoic understanding of the cosmos as a living, rational, providentially ordered whole. Piety is therefore inseparable from correct opinions about the nature of the universe: one cannot be pious while believing that the [[divinity|gods]] are neglectful, unjust, or non-existent.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] adds a distinctive practical emphasis: piety requires that one's interest and the [[divinity|gods]]' interest coincide. "Unless piety and your interest be in the same thing, piety cannot be maintained in any man" (017-038–017-039). This is a radical claim — it means that piety is impossible for someone who secretly wishes that events had gone otherwise.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on piety spans multiple Discourses and reaches its fullest expression in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]. In Discourse 6, he identifies impiety as the consequence of "meanness of spirit" (006-062) — the failure to recognize that one has been equipped by God with the faculties needed to face every difficulty. The person who complains about events is, in effect, accusing the [[divinity|gods]] of poor administration.

In Discourse 17, he makes the striking claim that piety and self-interest must align: "To sum up, remember that unless piety and your interest be in the same thing, piety cannot be maintained in any man" (017-038–017-039). This is not a cynical reduction of piety to self-interest but a profound assertion that the cosmic order is genuinely good for each individual — the [[divinity|gods]]' will and the individual's true good never conflict.

In Discourse 41, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] groups impiety with other passions as fundamentally a matter of opinion: "What is impiety, what is trifling? All these things are opinions, and nothing more" (041-035). The implication is that impiety, like all vice, is a cognitive error — a false judgment about what is good and bad.

The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] provides the most complete statement. In §XXXI, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] sets out the five points of true piety: have right opinions about the [[divinity|gods]] (that they exist and administer all things well); obey them; yield to every event voluntarily as the work of the wisest intelligence; never blame the [[divinity|gods]]; and never accuse them of neglect (099-001–099-005). He adds that the person who "takes care to desire as he ought and to avoid as he ought" is thereby practicing piety (099-025). Traditional observances — libations, sacrifices, first-fruits — are to be performed "according to the custom of your fathers," but always with the understanding that the true offering is a rightly disposed will.

In §L, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to piety in the context of [[precept|rules]] for living: "Whatever things are proposed to you for the conduct of life, abide by them, as if they were laws, as if you would be guilty of impiety if you transgressed any of them" (118-002). The [[precept|rules]] of philosophy are themselves sacred, and to violate them knowingly is an act of impiety.

## Key Passages

> As to piety towards the gods you must know that this is the chief thing, to have right opinions about them, to think that they exist, and that they administer the All well and justly; and you must fix yourself in this principle, to obey them, and to yield to them in everything which happens, and voluntarily to follow it as being accomplished by the wisest intelligence. (099-001–099-003)

> Unless piety and your interest be in the same thing, piety cannot be maintained in any man. (017-038–017-039)

> What is impiety, what is trifling? All these things are opinions, and nothing more, and opinions about things independent of the will. (041-035)

> Whatever things are proposed to you for the conduct of life, abide by them, as if they were laws, as if you would be guilty of impiety if you transgressed any of them. (118-002)

> Consequently he who takes care to desire as he ought and to avoid as he ought, at the same time also takes care to be pious. (099-025)
