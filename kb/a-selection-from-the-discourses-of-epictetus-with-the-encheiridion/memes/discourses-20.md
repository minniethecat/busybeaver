[020-001]The opinion of the

[020-002]philosophers perhaps seem to some to be a paradox; but still let us examine as

[020-003]well as we can, if it is true that it is possible to do everything both with

[020-004]caution and with confidence. For caution seems to be in a manner contrary to

[020-005]confidence, and contraries are in no way consistent. That which seems to many

[020-006]to be a paradox in the matter under consideration in my opinion is of this

[020-007]kind; if we asserted that we ought to employ caution and confidence in the same

[020-008]things, men might justly accuse us of bringing together things which cannot be

[020-009]united. But now where is the difficulty in what is said? for if these things

[020-010]are true, which have been often said and often proved, that the [[nature]] of [[good]]

[020-011]is in the use of appearances, and the nature of evil likewise, and that things

[020-012]independent of our will do not admit either the nature of evil or of good, what

[020-013]paradox do the philosophers assert if they say that where things are not

[020-014]dependent on the will, there you should employ confidence, but where they are

[020-015]dependent on the will, there you should employ caution? For if the bad consists

[020-016]in the bad exercise of the will, caution ought only to be used where things are

[020-017]dependent on the will. But if things independent of the will and not in our

[020-018]power are nothing to us, with respect to these we must employ confidence; and

[020-019]thus we shall both be cautious and confident, and indeed confident because of

[020-020]our caution. For by employing caution towards things which are really bad, it

[020-021]will result that we shall have confidence with respect to things which are not

[020-022]so.

[020-023]We are then in the condition of deer; when they flee from the huntsmen’s

[020-024]feathers in fright, whither do they turn and in what do they seek refuge as

[020-025]safe? They turn to the nets, and thus they perish by confounding things which

[020-026]are objects of fear with things that they ought not to fear. Thus we also act:

[020-027]in what cases do we fear? In things which are independent of the will. In what

[020-028]cases on the contrary do we behave with confidence, as if there were no danger?

[020-029]In things dependent on the will. To be deceived then, or to act rashly, or

[020-030]shamelessly, or with base [[desire]] to seek something, does not concern us at all,

[020-031]if we only hit the mark in things which are independent of our will. But where

[020-032]there is death or exile or pain or infamy, there we attempt to run away, there

[020-033]we are struck with terror. Therefore, as we may expect it to happen with those

[020-034]who err in the greatest matters, we convert natural confidence (that is,

[020-035]according to nature) into audacity, desperation, rashness, shamelessness; and

[020-036]we convert natural caution and modesty into cowardice and meanness, which are

[020-037]full of fear and confusion. For if a man should transfer caution to those

[020-038]things in which the will may be exercised and the acts of the will, he will

[020-039]immediately by willing to be cautious have also the power of avoiding what he

[020-040]chooses; but if he transfer it to the things which are not in his power and

[020-041]will, and attempt to avoid the things which are in the power of others, he will

[020-042]of necessity fear, he will be unstable, he will be disturbed; for death or pain

[020-043]is not formidable, but the fear of pain or death. For this [[reason]] we commend

[020-044]the poet, who said:

[020-045]“Not death is evil, but a shameful death.”

[020-046]Confidence (courage) then ought to be employed against death, and caution

[020-047]against the fear of death. But now we do the contrary, and employ against death

[020-048]the attempt to escape; and to our opinion about it we employ carelessness,

[020-049]rashness, and indifference. These things [[socrates|Socrates]] properly used to call tragic

[020-050]masks; for as to children masks appear terrible and fearful from inexperience,

[020-051]we also are affected in like manner by events (the things which happen in life)

[020-052]for no other reason than children are by masks. For what is a child? Ignorance.

[020-053]What is a child? Want of knowledge. For when a child knows these things, he is

[020-054]in no way inferior to us. What is death? A tragic mask. Turn it and examine it.

[020-055]See, it does not bite. The poor body must be separated from the spirit either

[020-056]now or later as it was separated from it before. Why then are you troubled if

[020-057]it be separated now? for if it is not separated now, it will be separated

[020-058]afterwards. Why? That the period of the universe may be completed, for it has

[020-059]need of the present, and of the future, and of the past. What is pain? A mask.

[020-060]Turn it and examine it. The poor flesh is moved roughly, then on the contrary

[020-061]smoothly. If this does not satisfy (please) you, the door is open; if it does,

[020-062]bear (with things). For the door ought to be open for all occasions; and so we

[020-063]have no trouble.

[020-064]What then is the fruit of these opinions? It is that which ought to be the most

[020-065]noble and the most becoming to those who are really educated, release from

[020-066]perturbation, release from fear. [[freedom|Freedom]]. For in these matters we must not

[020-067]believe the many, who say that free persons only ought to be educated, but we

[020-068]should rather believe the philosophers who say that the educated only are free.

[020-069]How is this? In this manner: Is freedom anything else than the power of living

[020-070]as we choose? Nothing else. Tell me then, ye men, do you wish to live in error?

[020-071]We do not. No one then who lives in error is free. Do you wish to live in fear?

[020-072]Do you wish to live in sorrow? Do you wish to live in perturbation? By no

[020-073]means. No one then who is in a state of fear or sorrow or perturbation is free;

[020-074]but whoever is delivered from sorrows and fears and perturbations, he is at the

[020-075]same time also delivered from servitude. How then can we continue to believe

[020-076]you, most dear legislators, when you say, We only allow free persons to be

[020-077]educated? For philosophers say we allow none to be free except the educated;

[020-078]that is, God does not allow it. When then a man has turned round before the

[020-079]prætor his own slave, has he done nothing? He has done something. What? He has

[020-080]turned round his own slave before the prætor. Has he done nothing more? Yes: he

[020-081]is also bound to pay for him the tax called the twentieth. Well then, is not

[020-082]the man who has gone through this ceremony become free? No more than he is

[020-083]become free from perturbations. Have you who are able to turn round (free)

[020-084]others no master? is not money your master, or a girl or a boy, or some tyrant

[020-085]or some friend of the tyrant? Why do you trouble then when you are going off to

[020-086]any trial (danger) of this kind? It is for this reason that I often say, study

[020-087]and hold in readiness these principles by which you may determine what those

[020-088]things are with reference to which you ought to be cautious, courageous in that

[020-089]which does not depend on your will, cautious in that which does depend on it.
