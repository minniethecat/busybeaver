# Necessity

**Necessity** (*anankē*) in [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses names the inescapable causal order of the universe. In Stoic physics, every event follows necessarily from antecedent causes, and this chain of necessity is identical with [[fate|Fate]] (*heimarmenē*), [[providence|Providence]] (*pronoia*), and the will of [[zeus|Zeus]]. To the untrained mind, necessity appears as a constraint on freedom. To the Stoic student, it is the very condition of freedom: once one understands what must be, one can redirect effort from the impossible to the possible.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not argue for determinism as a speculative thesis. He presents it as a fact of experience with immediate ethical consequences. "Since then it is of necessity that every man uses everything according to the opinion which he has about it" (003-010). Our judgments compel our actions; the question is whether those judgments are correct.

## Definition

In Stoic philosophy, necessity refers to the causal determinism according to which every event has a cause, every cause produces its effect without exception, and the entire sequence of causes forms a single, rational, [[divinity|divine]] order. The Stoics identified necessity with [[fate|Fate]] and with the rational principle (*logos*) that pervades the cosmos.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] focuses on the practical dimension: the things that are "not in our power" are governed by necessity. The body will age and die of necessity; other people will act according to their own judgments of necessity; fortune will rise and fall of necessity. [[wisdom|Wisdom]] consists in recognizing this domain of necessity and ceasing to invest desire or aversion in it.

## Epictetus's Treatment

**Necessity and opinion**: The opening of Discourse 3 states the principle directly: "it is of necessity that every man uses everything according to the opinion which he has about it" (003-010). This is a psychological necessity — we cannot act contrary to our considered judgments. The therapeutic implication is that to change behavior, one must change the underlying opinions.

**Necessity and in[[firmness|stability]]**: In Discourse 4, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] argues that anyone who desires things not in their power "of necessity must change with them and be tossed about with them as in a tempest" (004-050). The in[[firmness|stability]] of the unphilosophical life is not a moral failing but a structural necessity — if you tie your well-being to externals, you must be unstable, because externals are inherently unstable.

**Noble yielding**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] quotes with approval the Euripidean maxim, "Whoso nobly yields unto necessity, we hold him wise, and skilled in things [[divinity|divine]]" (120-005). The word "nobly" (*kalōs*) is crucial: the sage does not merely submit to necessity but embraces it as the expression of [[divinity|divine]] will. This transforms necessity from a prison into a path.

## Key Passages

> Since then it is of necessity that every man uses everything according to the opinion which he has about it. (003-010)

> He who desires or avoids the things which are not in his power can neither be faithful nor free, but of necessity he must change with them and be tossed about with them as in a tempest. (004-050)

> Whoso nobly yields unto necessity, we hold him wise, and skilled in things divine. (120-005)
