# Exercise

**Exercise** (*askēsis*) is the systematic practical [[education|training]] by which a person transforms Stoic principles from intellectual knowledge into lived character. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s Discourses, exercise is not physical [[education|training]] but spiritual and moral discipline — the deliberate, repeated practice of correct judgments about what is good, what is evil, and what is indifferent. "These are the things which philosophers should meditate on, which they should write daily, in which they should exercise themselves" (001-031).

Unlike modern notions of exercise as optional self-improvement, Epictetan exercise is foundational: without it, philosophy remains mere words. The true athlete, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] says, is "the man who exercises himself against such appearances" (033-050) — against the [[appearances|impressions]] that threaten to carry away the ruling faculty.

## Definition

In Epictetan [[stoicism|Stoicism]], exercise (*askēsis*) refers to the intentional, repeated practice of applying philosophical principles to the [[appearances|impressions]] (*[[appearances|phantasiai]]*) that arise in daily life. It is one component of the broader concept of [[discipline|discipline]] (*askēsis* in its wider sense), but more specifically denotes the active, moment-by-moment effort to judge appearances correctly — to assent only to what is true, to desire only what is good, and to avert only what is evil.

Exercise operates within [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s scheme of three topics (*topoi*): the discipline of desire and aversion, the discipline of impulse and action, and the discipline of assent (040-002). The first of these is "the chief and the most urgent," because failure in desire produces the passions (*pathē*) — [[grief|sorrow]], [[jealousy|envy]], fear — that render a person incapable of hearing reason at all.

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats exercise across several distinct registers in the Discourses, each illuminating a different dimension of the practice.

**Exercise as the mark of the philosopher**: At the outset of the Discourses, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents the daily meditation of the philosopher as an exercise: "These are the things which philosophers should meditate on, which they should write daily, in which they should exercise themselves" (001-031). The philosopher is distinguished not by his books or [[disputation|argument]]s but by what he practices. He illustrates this with the example of Agrippinus, who, upon hearing that his trial was proceeding in the Senate, [[tranquillity|calm]]ly noted the hour and said: "it was the time when he was used to exercise himself and then take the cold bath — let us go and take our exercise" (001-035). After exercising, he learned of his condemnation to banishment and simply said, "Let us go to Aricia then, and dine" (001-036). The exercise of the body here mirrors the exercise of the soul — both are maintained without perturbation.

**The three topics of exercise**: Discourse 40 is the most systematic treatment. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] identifies "three things in which a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and good" (040-002): first, the management of desires and aversions so that one never fails to get what one desires or falls into what one would avoid; second, the management of impulses and actions so that one acts "according to order, to reason, and not carelessly"; third, the management of assent so that one is free from "deception and rashness in judgment" (040-002). He criticizes contemporary philosophers who neglect the first two topics — the actual [[education|training]] of character — and devote themselves exclusively to the third, resolving syllogisms while remaining slaves to anger, [[jealousy|envy]], and the fear of others' opinions.

**Exercise as habituation against appearances**: In Discourse 33, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] describes the person who is "accustomed to be exercised" as one who can pause before an appearance, test it, and oppose it with a "beautiful and noble appearance" (033-047). "This is the true athlete," he declares, "the man who exercises himself against such appearances. Stay, wretch, do not be carried away. Great is the combat, [[divinity|divine]] is the work; it is for kingship, for freedom, for happiness, for freedom from perturbation" (033-050). The athletic metaphor is not ornamental — [[epictetus|Epictetus]] means that moral exercise requires the same daily discipline, the same willingness to endure discomfort, and the same refusal to quit that physical [[education|training]] demands.

**Exercise as the criterion of progress**: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] repeatedly insists that exercise, not study, is the true measure of philosophical progress. In Discourse 4, he challenges the student who boasts of having read [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]: "I am not inquiring about this, but how you exercise pursuit and [[aversion|avoidance]], desire and aversion, how you design and purpose and prepare yourself, whether conformably to nature or not" (004-037). [[progress|Progress]] is found not in books but in the daily practice of turning the will (*proairesis*) toward conformity with nature, "to exercise it and to improve it by labor, so as to make it conformable to nature, elevated, free, unrestrained, unimpeded, faithful, modest" (004-046).

**Exercise as daily mindfulness**: In Discourse 41, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] prescribes a concrete daily practice: "As soon as you go out in the morning, examine every man whom you see, every man whom you hear; answer as to a question, What have you seen? A handsome man or woman? Apply the rule. Is this independent of the will, or dependent? Independent. Take it away" (041-004). He laments that "if we practised this and exercised ourselves in it daily from morning to night, something indeed would be done. But now we are forthwith caught half asleep by every appearance" (041-012).

## Key Passages

> There are three things in which a man ought to exercise himself who would be wise and good. The first concerns the desires and the aversions... The second concerns the movements towards an object and the movements from an object... The third thing concerns freedom from deception and rashness in judgment. (040-002)

> This is the true athlete, the man who exercises himself against such appearances. Stay, wretch, do not be carried away. Great is the combat, divine is the work; it is for kingship, for freedom, for happiness, for freedom from perturbation. (033-050)

> I am not inquiring about this, but how you exercise pursuit and avoidance, desire and aversion, how you design and purpose and prepare yourself, whether conformably to nature or not. (004-037)

> If we practised this and exercised ourselves in it daily from morning to night, something indeed would be done. But now we are forthwith caught half asleep by every appearance. (041-012)
