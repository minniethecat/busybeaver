[003-001]If a man should be able to [[assent]] to this doctrine as he ought,

[003-002]that we are all sprung from God in an especial manner, and that God is the

[003-003]father both of men and of gods, I suppose that he would never have any ignoble

[003-004]or mean thoughts about himself. But if Cæsar (the emperor) should adopt you, no

[003-005]one could endure your arrogance; and if you know that you are the son of [[zeus|Zeus]],

[003-006]will you not be elated? Yet we do not so; but since these two things are

[003-007]mingled in the generation of man, body in common with the animals, and [[reason]]

[003-008]and intelligence in common with the gods, many incline to this kinship, which

[003-009]is miserable and mortal; and some few to that which is divine and happy. Since

[003-010]then it is of necessity that every man uses everything according to the opinion

[003-011]which he has about it, those, the few, who think that they are formed for

[003-012]fidelity and modesty and a sure use of appearances have no mean or ignoble

[003-013]thoughts about themselves; but with the many it is quite the contrary. For they

[003-014]say, What am I? A poor, miserable man, with my wretched bit of flesh. Wretched,

[003-015]indeed; but you possess something better than your bit of flesh. Why then do

[003-016]you neglect that which is better, and why do you attach yourself to this?

[003-017]Through this kinship with the flesh, some of us inclining to it become like

[003-018]wolves, faithless and treacherous and mischievous; some become like lions,

[003-019]savage and bestial and untamed; but the greater part of us become foxes, and

[003-020]other worse animals. For what else is a slanderer and malignant man than a fox,

[003-021]or some other more wretched and meaner animal? See then and take care that you

[003-022]do not become some one of these miserable things.
