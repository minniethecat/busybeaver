# Books

**Books** occupy a distinctive and deliberately ambivalent place in Epictetan philosophy. They are valuable instruments of philosophical [[education|training]] — repositories of [[disputation|argument]]s, demonstrations, and the recorded wisdom of earlier thinkers — but they are never to be mistaken for philosophy itself. For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], the Stoic art of living is a practical discipline measured by character and conduct, not by the number of volumes one has read or the elegance with which one can expound them. His own life embodies this teaching: [[epictetus|Epictetus]] wrote nothing, and the Discourses that bear his name were compiled by his pupil Arrian, who recorded his oral teachings in (originally) twelve books, of which eight were known to the Byzantine scholar Photius (P02-052)(P02-055). The surviving four books of the Discourses, together with the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], represent what remains of a much larger body of oral [[education|instruction]] that Arrian considered worthy of preservation — not as literature, but as a practical aid to the philosophical life (P02-075).

## Definition

Within the Epictetan framework, books are preparatory tools — external aids that serve the philosophical life without constituting it. They belong to the category of things not in our power, indifferent in themselves but capable of being used well or badly depending on the disposition of the reader. A book well used strengthens the rational faculty and equips the student to live according to nature; a book made into an object of [[self-conceit|vanity]] or a refuge from the difficulty of [[exercise|practice]] becomes an obstacle to genuine progress. The Stoic student reads not to accumulate knowledge but to transform character, and books are judged entirely by whether they serve this end. The question [[epictetus|Epictetus]] puts to every reader is not "How many books have you read?" but "What kind of person have you become through your reading?"

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] returns to the subject of books repeatedly across the Discourses, and his treatment is consistently cautionary. In Discourse 4, which contains his most concentrated [[disputation|discussion]] of the topic, he confronts a student who has become absorbed in the study of [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]'s treatises and has lost sight of why one reads philosophy at all. The point of reading [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]], [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists, is not to master [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] but to master oneself — to learn how to align one's will with nature. The person who reads philosophical books without applying their teachings is like a patient who reads prescriptions attentively but never takes the medicine. The knowledge sits in the intellect but never reaches the governing faculty, where it must do its work. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] categorizes philosophical study into three topics — the application of [[precept|theorems]] to conduct, the understanding of demonstrations, and the analysis of the demonstrations themselves — and he laments that students spend all their energy on the third while neglecting the first, so that they can prove that lying is wrong but continue to lie nonetheless (004-016).

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] presses this critique further by turning it against those who take [[arrogance|pride]] in their scholarship. He observes with characteristic sharpness that many students are content merely to "expound your books, but write such books yourself" — that is, to become commentators on commentaries, building a career out of interpreting texts rather than living the life those texts describe (004-041). This is the peculiar danger of books as a class of externals: because they deal in ideas, they create the illusion of philosophical progress while allowing the reader to avoid the genuine labor of self-examination and moral effort that philosophy demands. The scholar who can analyze [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]]'s syllogisms but cannot [[power|control]] his temper or desires has missed the point entirely. True knowledge of the Stoic doctrines is demonstrated not in the lecture hall but in the marketplace, the bedroom, and the tribunal — wherever character is tested by circumstance.

Yet [[epictetus|Epictetus]] does not reject books outright, and his own teaching depends on the existence of philosophical writings. Books are the proper vehicles for the study of logic, which [[epictetus|Epictetus]] regards as a necessary instrument for distinguishing truth from falsehood and for testing the [[appearances|impressions]] that assail the mind. He acknowledges that reading and studying the treatises of the Stoic masters is part of the curriculum — but always as a means, never as an end. The study of history and the interpretation of difficult texts have their place in preparing the mind, but only insofar as they sharpen the rational faculty for its true work (034-032). The test of having read well is not the ability to recite doctrine but the condition of one's character: whether one is more patient, less anxious, more just in dealings with others. The books that produce this transformation are well used; those that merely enlarge the library or sharpen the tongue are wasted. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] drives the point home by imagining a student who defends himself by saying, "Take my books, and you will see how conformable my pursuits are to reason" — a defense that [[epictetus|Epictetus]] dismisses as that of a man who deserves to be condemned, for he substitutes the reading of [[disputation|argument]]s for the living of them (004-076).

## Key Passages

> The first and most necessary topic in philosophy is that of the use of theorems, for instance, not to lie; the second is that of demonstrations, for instance, How is it proved that we ought not to lie; the third is that which gives confirmation and articulation to the other two, for instance, How is this a demonstration? For what is a demonstration, what is consequence, what is contradiction, what is truth, what is falsehood? The third topic is necessary because of the second, and the second because of the first; but the most necessary and the one on which we ought to rest is the first. We, however, do the contrary: we spend our time on the third topic, and all our zeal is about it, while we utterly neglect the first. Therefore we lie indeed, but we have the demonstration that we ought not to lie always ready to hand. (004-016)

> Why, then, do you read? Tell me. If you only read for the sake of entertainment or in order to learn something, you are foolish and miserable. But if you refer it to what you ought, what is this except a happy life? But if reading does not procure for you a happy life, of what use is it? "But it does procure it," he says, "and for this reason I am vexed with those who have not supplied me with the means of this knowledge." What kind of knowledge? Is it not the knowledge of good and evil? But have you derived no advantage from this? No, he says, for I am still subject to anger and sorrow and fear. (004-041)

> Take my books, and you will see how conformable my pursuits are to reason. This is the defence of a man who deserves to be condemned. How long will you delay to become good? You have read these things, you say. What have you read? You have read paltry arguments: you ought to have read something else, something that leads to action, not to disputation. (004-076)
