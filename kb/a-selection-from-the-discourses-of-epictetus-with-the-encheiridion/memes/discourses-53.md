[053-001]When one of his pupils inquired of [[epictetus|Epictetus]], and he was a

[053-002]person who appeared to be inclined to Cynicism, what kind of person a Cynic

[053-003]ought to be, and what was the notion ([Greek: prolaepsis]) of the thing, we

[053-004]will inquire, said Epictetus, at leisure; but I have so much to say to you that

[053-005]he who without God attempts so great a matter, is hateful to God, and has no

[053-006]other purpose than to act indecently in public.

[053-007]In the first place, in the things which relate to yourself, you must not be in

[053-008]any respect like what you do now; you must not blame God or man; you must take

[053-009]away [[desire]] altogether, you must transfer avoidance ([Greek: echchlisis]) only

[053-010]to the things which are within the power of the will; you must not feel anger

[053-011]nor resentment or envy nor pity; a girl must not appear handsome to you, nor

[053-012]must you love a little reputation, nor be pleased with a boy or a cake. For you

[053-013]ought to know that the rest of men throw walls around them and houses and

[053-014]darkness when they do any such things, and they have many means of concealment.

[053-015]A man shuts the door, he sets somebody before the chamber; if a person comes,

[053-016]say that he is out, he is not at leisure. But the Cynic instead of all these

[053-017]things must use modesty as his protection; if he does not, he will be indecent

[053-018]in his nakedness and under the open sky. This is his house, his door; this is

[053-019]the slave before his bedchamber; this is his darkness. For he ought not to wish

[053-020]to hide anything that he does; and if he does, he is gone, he has lost the

[053-021]character of a Cynic, of a man who lives under the open sky, of a free man; he

[053-022]has begun to fear some external thing, he has begun to have need of

[053-023]concealment, nor can he get concealment when he chooses. For where shall he

[053-024]hide himself and how? And if by chance this public instructor shall be

[053-025]detected, this pædagogue, what kind of things will he be compelled to suffer?

[053-026]when then a man fears these things, is it possible for him to be bold with his

[053-027]whole soul to superintend men? It cannot be: it is impossible.

[053-028]In the first place then you must make your ruling faculty pure, and this mode

[053-029]of life also. Now (you should say), to me the matter to work on is my

[053-030]understanding, as wood is to the carpenter, as hides to the shoemaker; and my

[053-031]business is the right use of appearances. But the body is nothing to me: the

[053-032]parts of it are nothing to me. Death? Let it come when it chooses, either death

[053-033]of the whole or of a part. Fly, you say. And whither; can any man eject me out

[053-034]of the world? He cannot. But wherever I go, there is the sun, there is the

[053-035]moon, there are the stars, dreams, omens, and the conversation ([Greek:

[053-036]omilia]) with gods.

[053-037]Then, if he is thus prepared, the true Cynic cannot be satisfied with this; but

[053-038]he must know that he is sent a messenger from [[zeus|Zeus]] to men about [[good]] and bad

[053-039]things, to show them that they have wandered and are seeking the substance of

[053-040]good and evil where it is not, but where it is, they never think; and that he

[053-041]is a spy, as Diogenes was carried off to Philip after the battle of Chaeroneia

[053-042]as a spy. For in fact a Cynic is a spy of the things which are good for men and

[053-043]which are evil, and it is his duty to examine carefully and to come and report

[053-044]truly, and not to be struck with terror so as to point out as enemies those who

[053-045]are not enemies, nor in any other way to be perturbed by appearances nor

[053-046]confounded.

[053-047]It is his duty then to be able with a loud voice, if the occasion should arise,

[053-048]and appearing on the tragic stage to say like [[socrates|Socrates]]: Men, whither are you

[053-049]hurrying, what are you doing, wretches? like blind people you are wandering up

[053-050]and down; you are going by another road, and have left the true road; you seek

[053-051]for prosperity and [[happiness]] where they are not, and if another shows you where

[053-052]they are, you do not believe him. Why do you seek it without? In the body? It

[053-053]is not there. If you doubt, look at Myro, look at Ophellius. In possessions? It

[053-054]is not there. But if you do not believe me, look at Croesus: look at those who

[053-055]are now rich, with what lamentations their life is filled. In power? It is not

[053-056]there. If it is, those must be happy who have been twice and thrice consuls;

[053-057]but they are not. Whom shall we believe in these matters? You who from without

[053-058]see their affairs and are dazzled by an appearance, or the men themselves? What

[053-059]do they say? Hear them when they groan, when they grieve, when on account of

[053-060]these very consulships and glory and splendor they think that they are more

[053-061]wretched and in greater danger. Is it in royal power? It is not: if it were,

[053-062]Nero would have been happy, and Sardanapalus. But neither was Agamemnon happy,

[053-063]though he was a better man than Sardanapalus and Nero; but while others are

[053-064]snoring, what is he doing?

[053-065]Much from his head he tore his rooted hair:

[053-066]Iliad, x., 15.

[053-067]and what does he say himself?

[053-068]“I am perplexed,” he says, “and

[053-069]Disturb’d I am,” and “my heart out of my bosom

[053-070]Is leaping.”

[053-071]Iliad, x., 91.

[053-072]Wretch, which of your affairs goes badly? Your possessions? No. Your body? No.

[053-073]But you are rich in gold and copper. What then is the matter with you? That

[053-074]part of you, whatever it is, has been neglected by you and is corrupted, the

[053-075]part with which we desire, with which we avoid, with which we move towards and

[053-076]move from things. How neglected? He knows not the [[nature]] of good for which he

[053-077]is made by nature and the nature of evil; and what is his own, and what belongs

[053-078]to another; and when anything that belongs to others goes badly, he says, Woe

[053-079]to me, for the Hellenes are in danger. Wretched is his ruling faculty, and

[053-080]alone neglected and uncared for. The Hellenes are going to die destroyed by the

[053-081]Trojans. And if the Trojans do not kill them, will they not die? Yes; but not

[053-082]all at once. What difference then does it make? For if death is an evil,

[053-083]whether men die altogether, or if they die singly, it is equally an evil. Is

[053-084]anything else then going to happen than the separation of the soul and the

[053-085]body? Nothing. And if the Hellenes perish, is the door closed, and is it not in

[053-086]your power to die? It is. Why then do you lament (and say), Oh, you are a king

[053-087]and have the sceptre of Zeus? An unhappy king does not exist more than an

[053-088]unhappy god. What then art thou? In truth a shepherd: for you weep as shepherds

[053-089]do, when a wolf has carried off one of their sheep: and these who are governed

[053-090]by you are sheep. And why did you come hither? Was your desire in any danger?

[053-091]was your aversion ([Greek: echchlisis])? was your movement (pursuits)? was your

[053-092]avoidance of things? He replies, No; but the wife of my brother was carried

[053-093]off. Was it not then a great gain to be deprived of an adulterous wife? Shall

[053-094]we be despised then by the Trojans? What kind of people are the Trojans, wise

[053-095]or foolish? If they are wise, why do you fight with them? If they are fools,

[053-096]why do you care about them?

[053-097]Do you possess the body then free or is it in servile condition? We do not

[053-098]know. Do you not know that it is the slave of fever, of gout, ophthalmia,

[053-099]dysentery, of a tyrant, of fire, of iron, of everything which is stronger? Yes,

[053-100]it is a slave. How then is it possible that anything which belongs to the body

[053-101]can be free from hindrance? and how is a thing great or valuable which is

[053-102]naturally dead, or earth, or mud? Well then, do you possess nothing which is

[053-103]free? Perhaps nothing. And who is able to compel you to [[assent]] to that which

[053-104]appears false? No man. And who can compel you not to assent to that which

[053-105]appears true? No man. By this then you see that there is something in you

[053-106]naturally free. But to desire or to be averse from, or to move towards an

[053-107]object or to move from it, or to prepare yourself, or to propose to do

[053-108]anything, which of you can do this, unless he has received an [[impression]] of the

[053-109]appearance of that which is profitable or a duty? No man. You have then in

[053-110]these things also something which is not hindered and is free. Wretched men,

[053-111]work out this, take care of this, seek for good here.
