# Crito

## Biography

Crito is the Athenian friend and companion of [[socrates]], familiar from [[plato|Plato]]'s dialogue that bears his name, and [[epictetus|Epictetus]] invokes him only in that role — as the man to whom [[socrates|Socrates]] speaks in the face of death. He is never described or characterized in the Discourses; he functions purely as the addressee of [[socrates|Socrates]]'s formula of [[obedience|submission]] to the [[divinity|divine]] will.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] twice puts on [[socrates|Socrates]]'s lips, spoken to Crito, the same resignation: "Dear Crito, if it is the will of the [[divinity|gods]] that it be so, let it be so" (004-065), and again, among the maxims to be kept ready to hand, "O Crito, if so it pleases the [[divinity|gods]], so let it be" (120-007).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Crito is the human occasion for one of his favourite sayings. The point is not Crito himself but the posture [[socrates|Socrates]] takes toward him: even "in fetters," awaiting [[death]], the philosopher greets his sentence not with "Wretched am I, an old man" (004-066) but with acceptance of what the [[divinity|gods]] have willed.

The maxim reaches its full form in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]], where [[socrates|Socrates]] adds, still addressing Crito, that "[[anytus|Anytus]] and Melitus are able indeed to kill me, but they cannot harm me" (120-007 to 120-008) — the killers can end his life but cannot touch his [[good]].

Crito thus stands, in the text, at the threshold between a man and his death, hearing the words in which [[obedience|submission]] to [[providence]] is most compactly expressed.

## References in the Discourses

- **[[socrates|Socrates]]'s resignation in fetters**: "Dear Crito, if it is the will of the [[divinity|gods]] that it be so, let it be so" (004-065).

- **The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]'s closing maxim**: "O Crito, if so it pleases the [[divinity|gods]], so let it be; [[anytus|Anytus]] and Melitus are able indeed to kill me, but they cannot harm me" (120-007).
