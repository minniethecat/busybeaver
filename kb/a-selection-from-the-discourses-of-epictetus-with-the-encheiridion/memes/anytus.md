# Anytus

## Biography

Anytus was one of the accusers who brought [[socrates|Socrates]] to trial at [[athens|Athens]] and secured his death sentence. In [[epictetus|Epictetus]] he never appears [[solitude|alone]]: he is always paired with Melitus, and the two stand for the whole class of enemies who can destroy the body but reach no further.

The pairing is [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s own shorthand rather than a full account of the trial. He assumes his reader already knows the story of the condemnation, and needs only the two names to summon the whole scene of [[socrates|Socrates]] standing before his judges.

The Discourses give the famous saying in the middle of an [[disputation|argument]] that the good man suffers no real harm from those who wrong him. Asked what [[socrates|Socrates]] says, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] answers in [[socrates|Socrates]]' own words: "Anytus and Melitus can kill me, but they cannot hurt me" (019-044).

The line is set directly against the claim that a man "who has the inferior principles" might overpower "him who is superior in principles" (019-046 to 019-047) — a thing, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] insists, that can never be shown. Anytus, for all his legal power, could take [[socrates|Socrates]]' life but not his good.

The same words return as the very last quotation of the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]. Among the three sayings [[epictetus|Epictetus]] has Arrian set at the close of the [[enchiridion|manual]] is [[socrates|Socrates]]' word to [[crito|Crito]]: "Anytus and Melitus are able indeed to kill me, but they cannot harm me" (120-007 to 120-008).

## Relationship to Epictetus / Stoicism

For [[epictetus|Epictetus]], Anytus is less a person than a demonstration. The whole force of the example lies in the gap between what Anytus could do and what he could not: he could "kill" [[socrates|Socrates]] but could not "hurt" him (019-044), because a man's good lies in his [[prohairesis|will]], which no enemy can touch.

This is why the saying anchors [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s teaching on [[freedom]] and on what can truly injure a man. External powers — courts, accusers, tyrants — command the body and its circumstances but have no jurisdiction over judgment and character.

To be harmed, in the strict sense, is to be made worse in one's own will, and that no [[tyrant]] can do to another; only the self can. Anytus is the standing proof: the accuser prevailed in the court and still could not touch the thing that mattered.

That [[epictetus|Epictetus]], through Arrian, chose this saying to end the [[enchiridion|manual]] marks how central it is. The [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] closes not with a rule but with the image of [[socrates|Socrates]] unharmed by his killers — the practical proof that "they cannot harm me" (120-008) as the last word of Stoic [[education|training]].

## References in the Discourses

- **Discourse 19**: [[socrates|Socrates]]' saying that "Anytus and Melitus can kill me, but they cannot hurt me," set against the impossibility of the worse overpowering the better (019-044).
- **[[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] (ench-52)**: the [[enchiridion|manual]]'s closing quotation — Anytus and Melitus "are able indeed to kill me, but they cannot harm me" (120-008).
