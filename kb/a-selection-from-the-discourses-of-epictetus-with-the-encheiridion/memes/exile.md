## Definition

Exile (*exsilium* in Latin, φυγή *phygē* in Greek) is the forced removal of a person from their native city or country, a punishment commonly employed in the ancient world against political opponents and philosophical dissenters.

In Stoic philosophy, exile is classified among the "dispreferred indifferents" (*adiaphora*) — circumstances one would naturally prefer to avoid, but which cannot affect the moral character of the wise person. For the Stoic, the only true evil is vice, and the only true good is virtue. Exile, being external to the will, is neither good nor evil in itself.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] himself lived this doctrine: he was exiled from [[rome|Rome]] around 93 CE when Domitian banished all philosophers from Italy, and spent the remainder of his life teaching in [[nicopolis|Nicopolis]].

## Epictetus's Treatment

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] treats exile as a [[example|paradigm]]atic test case for the Stoic doctrine of indifferents. Throughout the Discourses, he pairs it repeatedly with death, pain, and poverty as one of the "terrible" things that ordinary people fear but the philosopher has learned to regard with equanimity.

His key move is to locate the true harm not in exile itself but in one's opinion about it. "But it is a man's own opinions which disturb him" (012-024). If one believes exile is an evil, one will be miserable; if one understands it as an external indifferent, one can remain free regardless of physical location.

[[epictetus|Epictetus]] sharpens the point by asking: can a tyrant send your moral character into exile? He famously asks in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]: "Let death and exile and every other thing which appears dreadful be daily before your eyes; but most of all death: and you will never think of anything mean, nor will you desire anything extravagantly" (089-001).

The philosopher, he argues, carries his true home within: "Are you not the master of my exile or of my...?" (063-042). The implication is that moral choice (*prohairesis*) is always one's own, regardless of geographical circumstances.

## Key Passages

> What then is that to me? Are you not the master of my exile or of my...? (063-042)

> I must go into exile. Does any man then hinder me from going with a smile, and cheerful, and content? (001-023)

> Let death and exile and every other thing which appears dreadful be daily before your eyes; but most of all death: and you will never think of anything mean, nor will you desire anything extravagantly. (089-001)

> There is death or exile or pain or infamy, there we attempt to run away, there we are struck with terror. (020-032-020-033)
