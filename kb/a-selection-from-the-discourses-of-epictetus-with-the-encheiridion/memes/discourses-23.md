[023-001]The hypothetical proposition is indifferent: the [[judgment]]

[023-002]about it is not indifferent, but it is either knowledge or opinion or error.

[023-003]Thus life is indifferent: the use is not indifferent. When any man then tells

[023-004]you that these things also are indifferent, do not become negligent; and when a

[023-005]man invites you to be careful (about such things), do not become abject and

[023-006]struck with admiration of material things. And it is [[good]] for you to know your

[023-007]own preparation and power, that in those matters where you have not been

[023-008]prepared, you may keep quiet, and not be vexed, if others have the advantage

[023-009]over you. For you too in syllogisms will claim to have the advantage over them;

[023-010]and if others should be vexed at this, you will console them by saying, “I have

[023-011]learned them, and you have not.” Thus also where there is need of any practice,

[023-012]seek not that which is acquired from the need (of such practice), but yield in

[023-013]that matter to those who have had practice, and be yourself content with

[023-014]firmness of mind.

[023-015]Go and salute a certain person. How? Not meanly. But I have been shut out, for

[023-016]I have not learned to make my way through the window; and when I have found the

[023-017]door shut, I must either come back or enter through the window. But still speak

[023-018]to him. In what way? Not meanly. But suppose that you have not got what you

[023-019]wanted. Was this your business, and not his? Why then do you claim that which

[023-020]belongs to another? Always remember what is your own, and what belongs to

[023-021]another; and you will not be disturbed. [[chrysippus|Chrysippus]] therefore said well, So long

[023-022]as future things are uncertain, I always cling to those which are more adapted

[023-023]to the conservation of that which is according to [[nature]]; for God himself has

[023-024]given me the faculty of such choice. But if I knew that it was fated (in the

[023-025]order of things) for me to be sick, I would even move towards it; for the foot

[023-026]also, if it had intelligence, would move to go into the mud. For why are ears

[023-027]of corn produced? Is it not that they may become dry? And do they not become

[023-028]dry that they may be reaped? for they are not separated from communion with

[023-029]other things. If then they had perception, ought they to wish never to be

[023-030]reaped? But this is a curse upon ears of corn to be never reaped. So we must

[023-031]know that in the case of men too it is a curse not to die, just the same as not

[023-032]to be ripened and not to be reaped. But since we must be reaped, and we also

[023-033]know that we are reaped, we are vexed at it; for we neither know what we are

[023-034]nor have we studied what belongs to man, as those who have studied horses know

[023-035]what belongs to horses. But Chrysantas when he was going to strike the enemy

[023-036]checked himself when he heard the trumpet sounding a retreat: so it seemed

[023-037]better to him to obey the general’s command than to follow his own inclination.

[023-038]But not one of us chooses, even when necessity summons, readily to obey it, but

[023-039]weeping and groaning we suffer what we do suffer, and we call them

[023-040]“circumstances.” What kind of circumstances, man? If you give the name of

[023-041]circumstances to the things which are around you, all things are circumstances;

[023-042]but if you call hardships by this name, what hardship is there in the dying of

[023-043]that which has been produced? But that which destroys is either a sword, or a

[023-044]wheel, or the sea, or a tile, or a tyrant. Why do you care about the way of

[023-045]going down to Hades? All ways are equal. But if you will listen to the truth,

[023-046]the way which the tyrant sends you is shorter. A tyrant never killed a man in

[023-047]six months: but a fever is often a year about it. All these things are only

[023-048]sound and the noise of empty names.
