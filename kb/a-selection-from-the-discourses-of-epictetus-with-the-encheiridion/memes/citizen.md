## Definition

Citizen (*politēs*) in Stoic ethics designates a person's membership in a political community — but with a crucial expansion. While the ordinary sense refers to membership in a particular city or state, [[epictetus|Epictetus]] follows the Stoic tradition of cosmopolitanism: every rational being is fundamentally a "citizen of the world" (*kosmopolitēs*), a part of the cosmic city governed by [[divinity|divine]] reason. Local citizenship is contingent and secondary; cosmic citizenship is essential and inalienable.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The declaration is direct and uncompromising: "You are a citizen of the world, and a part of it" (026-007). This is not a metaphor but a metaphysical claim. The cosmos is a single city — the city of God — and every rational being is by nature a fellow-citizen with every other rational being, and with the [[divinity|gods]] themselves.

From this cosmic citizenship flow specific duties. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks: "What then does the character of a citizen promise?" (026-010). The answer is that the citizen must consider no private advantage in isolation from the whole, must never act as a detached part, and must deliberate as one who understands that his own good cannot be separated from the good of the whole. The citizen "of the same city, both the great and the small" (030-028) must recognize his kinship with every other citizen.

The duties of a citizen extend to every role one occupies: "the character of a son, of father, of brother, of citizen, of man, of wife, of neighbor" (029-023). [[epictetus|Epictetus]] presents these as nested identities, each with its own [[duties|obligation]]s, all grounded in the fundamental fact of cosmic citizenship. To "discharge the duties of a citizen" (036-063) is not optional — it is what one was made for. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] asks the student to consider "for what purpose you were made a citizen" (036-066), suggesting that citizenship is not an accident of birth but a telos, a purpose built into one's nature.

The practical implication is a radical reorientation of loyalty. "As good citizens do to the law" (008-017) — just as the citizen of a city obeys its laws, so the cosmic citizen obeys the [[divinity|divine]] law. The ordinary person asks "What is the custom of my city?" but the philosopher asks "What is the law of the cosmos?" [[epictetus|Epictetus]] uses the image of inquiring of the "citizens and rulers" of a strange city to learn its ways (043-004) — the philosopher, arriving in the cosmos, inquires of its [[divinity|divine]] administrator.

## Key Passages

- (026-007) — "You are a citizen of the world, and a part of it, not one of the subservient parts, but one of the ruling parts."
- (026-010) — "What then does the character of a citizen promise?" — the duties of cosmic citizenship.
- (029-023) — The nested identities: "son, father, brother, citizen, man, wife, neighbor" — each with its proper work.
- (036-063) — "To discharge the duties of a citizen" — citizenship as a set of [[duties|obligation]]s.
- (036-066) — "For what purpose you were made a citizen" — citizenship as telos.
- (008-017) — "As good citizens do to the law" — the citizen's obedience as [[example|model]] for philosophical conduct.
- (030-028) — "Citizen of the same city, both the great and the small" — kinship with all rational beings.
- (043-004) — "In a strange city inquire of the citizens" — the philosopher's orientation to the cosmic city.
