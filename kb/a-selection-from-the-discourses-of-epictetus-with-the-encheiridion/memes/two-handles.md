## Definition

"Everything has two handles" is one of [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s most famous and distinctive metaphors, appearing in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]] (§XLIII). The image is simple: every circumstance, relationship, or event presents itself with two "handles" — one by which it can be borne (or carried, or managed) and one by which it cannot. The handle by which a thing can be borne is the one aligned with reason, virtue, and the recognition of what is truly under one's [[power|control]]. The other handle — the one that makes the thing unbearable — is the handle of complaint, of seeing oneself as wronged, of demanding that externals conform to one's will. The art of living, for [[epictetus|Epictetus]], consists in learning to grasp every situation by its bearable handle.

## Epictetus's Treatment

The two-handles teaching appears in its canonical form in the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]]. The example [[epictetus|Epictetus]] gives is characteristically concrete: if a brother wrongs you, do not take hold of the situation by the handle of "he wronged me" — that handle makes the situation unbearable. Instead, take hold by the handle of "he is my brother, he was brought up with me" — that handle makes the situation bearable (111-001). The choice of handle is always available, but it requires philosophical presence of mind to select the right one.

The metaphor extends beyond family relations. [[epictetus|Epictetus]] applies it to the body and external circumstances generally: "as you present this handle in your body, follow every man" (028-083) warns against the wrong handle of physical vulnerability. And those who have "cast off all the handles" (057-130) of attachment to externals achieve true freedom — they have nothing by which fortune can seize them.

The two-handles teaching is closely related to [[epictetus|Epictetus]]'s doctrine of [[appearances|impressions]] and judgment. Every impression presents itself for evaluation; the "handle" one grasps is the judgment one makes. To grasp the wrong handle is to assent to a false impression — to treat an indifferent as a good or an evil. To grasp the right handle is to judge correctly, in accordance with nature.

## Key Passages

- "Everything has two handles, the one by which it may be borne, the other by which it cannot" (111-001) — The canonical formulation from the [[enchiridion|Encheiridion]].
- "As you present this handle in your body, follow every man" (028-083) — Warning against the wrong handle of physical vulnerability.
- "He had cast off all the handles of..." (057-130) — The sage who has removed all external handles by which fortune might seize him.
