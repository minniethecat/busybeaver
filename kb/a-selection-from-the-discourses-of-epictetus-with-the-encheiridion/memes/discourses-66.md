[066-001]Some persons raise a question whether the social

[066-002]feeling is contained in the [[nature]] of man; and yet I think that these same

[066-003]persons would have no doubt that love of purity is certainly contained in it,

[066-004]and that if man is distinguished from other animals by anything, he is

[066-005]distinguished by this. When then we see any other animal cleaning itself, we

[066-006]are accustomed to speak of the act with surprise, and to add that the animal is

[066-007]acting like a man; and on the other hand, if a man blames an animal for being

[066-008]dirty, straightway, as if we were making an excuse for it, we say that of

[066-009]course the animal is not a human creature. So we suppose that there is

[066-010]something superior in man, and that we first receive it from the gods. For

[066-011]since the gods by their nature are pure and free from corruption, so far as men

[066-012]approach them by [[reason]], so far do they cling to purity and to a love (habit)

[066-013]of purity. But since it is impossible that man’s nature ([Greek: ousia]) can be

[066-014]altogether pure, being mixed (composed) of such materials, reason is applied,

[066-015]as far as it is possible, and reason endeavors to make human nature love

[066-016]purity.

[066-017]The first then and highest purity is that which is in the soul; and we say the

[066-018]same of impurity. Now you could not discover the impurity of the soul as you

[066-019]could discover that of the body; but as to the soul, what else could you find

[066-020]in it than that which makes it filthy in respect to the acts which are her own?

[066-021]Now the acts of the soul are movement towards an object or movement from it,

[066-022][[desire]], aversion, preparation, design (purpose), [[assent]]. What then is it which

[066-023]in these acts makes the soul filthy and impure? Nothing else than her own bad

[066-024]judgments ([Greek: chrimata]). Consequently the impurity of the soul is the

[066-025]soul’s bad opinions; and the purification of the soul is the planting in it of

[066-026]proper opinions; and the soul is pure which has proper opinions, for the soul

[066-027]alone in her own acts is free from perturbation and pollution.

[066-028]For we ought not even by the appearance of the body to deter the multitude from

[066-029]philosophy; but as in other things, a philosopher should show himself cheerful

[066-030]and tranquil, so also he should in the things that relate to the body. See, ye

[066-031]men, that I have nothing, that I want nothing; see how I am without a house,

[066-032]and without a city, and an exile, if it happens to be so, and without a hearth

[066-033]I live more free from trouble and more happily than all of noble birth and than

[066-034]the rich. But look at my poor body also and observe that it is not injured by

[066-035]my hard way of living. But if a man says this to me, who has the appearance

[066-036](dress) and face of a condemned man, what god shall persuade me to approach

[066-037]philosophy, if it makes men such persons? Far from it; I would not choose to do

[066-038]so, even if I were going to become a wise man. I indeed would rather that a

[066-039]young man, who is making his first movements towards philosophy, should come to

[066-040]me with his hair carefully trimmed than with it dirty and rough, for there is

[066-041]seen in him a certain notion (appearance) of beauty and a desire of (attempt

[066-042]at) that which is becoming; and where he supposes it to be, there also he

[066-043]strives that it shall be. It is only necessary to show him (what it is), and to

[066-044]say: Young man, you seek beauty, and you do well; you must know then that it

[066-045](is produced) grows in that part of you where you have the rational faculty;

[066-046]seek it there where you have the movements towards and movements from things,

[066-047]where you have the desires towards and the aversion from things; for this is

[066-048]what you have in yourself of a superior kind; but the poor body is naturally

[066-049]only earth; why do you labor about it to no purpose? if you shall learn nothing

[066-050]else, you will learn from time that the body is nothing. But if a man comes to

[066-051]me daubed with filth, dirty, with a moustache down to his knees, what can I say

[066-052]to him, by what kind of resemblance can I lead him on? For about what has he

[066-053]busied himself which resembles beauty, that I may be able to change him and

[066-054]say, Beauty is not in this, but in that? Would you have me to tell him, that

[066-055]beauty consists not in being daubed with muck, but that it lies in the rational

[066-056]part? Has he any desire of beauty? has he any form of it in his mind? Go and

[066-057]talk to a hog, and tell him not to roll in the mud.
