# Technique

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, technique (*tecnica*) is sharply distinguished from art proper: it belongs to the **practical, economic sphere** of externalization, not to the aesthetic sphere of intuition-expression.

## Definition

Technique is the practical knowledge of how to produce physical artifacts that serve as stimuli for aesthetic reproduction. The painter's brushwork, the poet's versification, the composer's instrumentation — all are technique. They are means to an end (communication), not the aesthetic act itself. Croce's chapter on "Externalization and Technique" (ch15-externalization-technique) establishes that the production of the physical art object is a practical, not aesthetic, activity.

## In Croce's System

### Technique as Practical Activity

Technique belongs to the economic sphere — the pursuit of individual ends through efficient means. The artist qua artist does not need technique; the artist qua communicator does. Raphæl's genius was his forming power; his technique was the skill by which he produced canvases that enable others to reproduce his intuitions.

### The Danger of Technicism

Croce warns against the confusion of technique with art. When technique is mistaken for the essence of art, art degenerates into virtuosity — the empty display of skill without genuine expressive content. Academic art, with its fixed rules and prescriptions, is the institutional form of this confusion.

## Key Distinctions

**Technique vs Expression.** Expression is the inner forming of the image; technique is the outer means of physical production. Expression is necessary and constitutive of art; technique is contingent and instrumental to communication.

**Technique vs Genius.** Genius is the power of forming intuitions; technique is the skill of externalizing them. One can be a genius with poor technique (a visionary who cannot execute) and a technician with no genius (a skilled craftsman who produces nothing genuinely expressive).

**Technique vs Style.** Style is the characteristic quality of an artist's expressions — a matter of intuition. Technique is the means by which those expressions are physically realized. Style is personal and spiritual; technique is learnable and transferable.

## Historical Context

The separation of art from craft is a modern development. In the **Renaissance**, the artist was still largely a craftsman, and *disegno* (drawing/design) was both a technical skill and an intellectual activity. **G. E. Lessing**'s *Laocoön* established medium-specific technical analysis. Croce radicalizes the art-craft distinction by making technique entirely external to the aesthetic act — a consequence of his intuition-expression identity thesis.
