[029-001] *Æsthetic mysticism in the opponents of [[idealism|Idealism]].*

[029-002] Nothing, perhaps, shows more clearly how well this imaginative

[029-003] conception of art suited the spirit of the times (not only a particular

[029-004] fashion in philosophy, but the psychological conditions expressed

[029-005] by the [[romanticism|Romantic movement]]) than the fact that the adversaries of

[029-006] the systems of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]], [[karl-solger|Solger]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] either agreed with this

[029-007] conception in general or, while believing themselves to be departing

[029-008] widely from it, actually returned to it involuntarily.

[029-009] *[[arthur-schopenhauer|A. Schopenhauer]].*

[029-010] Everybody knows with what lack, shall we say, of *phlegma

[029-011] philosophicum* Arthur Schopenhauer fought against Schelling, Hegel

[029-012] and all the "charlatans" and "professors" who had divided amongst

[029-013] themselves the heritage of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]. But what was the artistic theory

[029-014] accepted and developed by Schopenhauer?

[029-015] *Ideas as the object of art.*

[029-016] His theory, like Hegel's own, turns upon the distinction between

[029-017] the concept which is abstraction and the concept which is concrete,

[029-018] or Idea; although Schopenhauer's Ideas are by himself likened to

[029-019] Plato's, and in the particular form in which he presents them more

[029-020] nearly resemble those of Schelling than the Idea of Hegel. They have

[029-021] something in common with intellectual concepts, for like them they

[029-022] are unities representing a plurality of real things: but "the concept

[029-023] is abstract and discursive, entirely indeterminate in its sphere,

[029-024] rigorously precise within its own limits only; the intellect suffices

[029-025] to conceive and understand it, speech expresses it without need for

[029-026] other intermediary, and its own definition exhausts its whole nature;

[029-027] the idea, on the contrary (which may be defined clearly as the adequate

[029-028] representative of the concept) is absolutely intuitive, and although

[029-029] it represents an infinite number of individual things, it is not for

[029-030] that any the less determined in all its aspects. The individual, as

[029-031] individual, cannot know it; in order to conceive it he must strip

[029-032] himself of all will, of all individuality, and raise himself to the

[029-033] state of a pure knowing subject. The idea, therefore, is attained

[029-034] by genius only, or by one who finds himself in a genial disposition

[029-035] attained by that elevation of his cognitive powers inspired usually

[029-036] by genius." "The idea is unity become plurality by means of space

[029-037] and time, forms of one intuitive apperception; the concept, on the

[029-038] contrary, is unity extracted from plurality by means of abstraction,

[029-039] which is the procedure of our intellect: the concept may be described

[029-040] as *unitas post yewi* the idea, *unitas ante rem.*"[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_1_432) Schopenhauer is

[029-041] in the habit of calling ideas the genera of things; but on one occasion

[029-042] he remarks that ideas are of species, not genera; that genera are

[029-043] simply concepts, and that there are natural species, but only logical

[029-044] genera.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_2_433) This psychological illusion as to the existence of ideas for

[029-045] types originates (as we find elsewhere in Schopenhauer) in the habit

[029-046] of converting the empirical classifications of the natural sciences

[029-047] into living realities. "Do you wish to see ideas?" he asks; "look at

[029-048] the clouds which scud across the sky; look at a brooklet leaping over

[029-049] rocks; look at the crystallization of hoar-frost on a window-pane

[029-050] with its designs of trees and flowers. The shapes of the clouds, the

[029-051] ripples of the gushing brook, the configurations of the crystals exist

[029-052] for us individual observers, in themselves they are indifferent. The

[029-053] clouds in themselves are elastic vapour; the brook is an incompressible

[029-054] fluid, mobile, transparent, amorphous, the ice obeys the laws of

[029-055] crystallization: and in these determinations their ideas consist."[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_3_434)

[029-056] All these are the immediate objectification of will in its various

[029-057] degrees; and it is these, not their pale copies in real things,

[029-058] that art delineates; whence Plato was right in one sense and wrong

[029-059] in another, and is justified and condemned by Schopenhauer exactly

[029-060] in the same way as by Plotinus of old, as well as by Schopenhauer's

[029-061] worst enemy, the modern Schelling.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_4_435) In consequence, each art has

[029-062] a special category of ideas for its own dominion. Architecture, and

[029-063] in some cases hydraulics, facilitate the clear intuition of those

[029-064] ideas which constitute the lower degrees of objectification—weight,

[029-065] cohesion, resistance, hardness, the general properties of stone and

[029-066] some combinations of light; gardening and (most curious association)

[029-067] landscape painting represent the ideas of vegetable nature; sculpture

[029-068] and animal painting those of zoology; historical painting and the

[029-069] higher forms of sculpture that of the human body; poetry the very idea

[029-070] of man himself.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_5_436) As for music, that (let him who can justify the

[029-071] logical discontinuity) is outside the hierarchy of the other arts.

[029-072] We have seen how Schelling considered it to be representative of the

[029-073] very rhythm of the universe;[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_6_437) differing but slightly from this,

[029-074] Schopenhauer affirms that music does not express ideas but, parallel

[029-075] with ideas, Will itself. The analogies between music and the world,

[029-076] between the fundamental bass and crude matter, between the scale and

[029-077] the series of species, between melody and conscious will, led him to

[029-078] the conclusion that music was not, as Leibniz thought, an arithmetic

[029-079] but a metaphysic: *exercitium metaphysices occultum nescientis se

[029-080] philosophari animi*.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_7_438)

[029-081] *Æsthetic catharsis.*

[029-082] To Schopenhauer, no less than his idealistic predecessors, art

[029-083] beatifies; it is the flower of life; he who contemplates art is no

[029-084] longer an individual but a pure knowing subject, at liberty, free from

[029-085] desire, from pain, from time.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_8_439)

[029-086] *Signs of a better theory in Schopenhauer.*

[029-087] Schopenhauer's system no doubt contains here and there premonitions

[029-088] of a better and more profound treatment of art. Schopenhauer, who was

[029-089] capable on occasion of clear and keen analysis, constantly insists

[029-090] that the forms of space and time must not be applied to the idea

[029-091] or to artistic contemplation, which admits of the general form of

[029-092] representation only.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_9_440) From this he might have inferred that art, so

[029-093] far from being a superior and extraordinary level of consciousness, is

[029-094] actually its most immediate level, namely that which in its primitive

[029-095] simplicity precedes even common perception with its reference of

[029-096] objects to a position in the spatial and temporal series. To free

[029-097] oneself from common perception and to live in imagination does not mean

[029-098] rising to a Platonic contemplation of the ideas, but descending once

[029-099] more into the region of immediate intuition, becoming children again,

[029-100] as [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] had seen. On the other hand Schopenhauer had begun to examine

[029-101] the categories of Kant with an unprejudiced eye; he was not satisfied

[029-102] with the two forms of intuition, and wished to add to them a third,

[029-103] causality.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_10_441) In conclusion, we note that, like his predecessors, he

[029-104] makes a comparison between art and history, with this difference and

[029-105] advantage over the idealist authors of the philosophy of history, that

[029-106] for him history was irreducible to concepts; it was contemplation of

[029-107] the individual, and therefore not science. Had he persevered in his

[029-108] comparison between art and history, he would have arrived at a better

[029-109] solution than that at which he stopped; that is to say, that the matter

[029-110] of history is the particular in its particularity and contingency,

[029-111] while that of art is that which is, and is always identical.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_11_442) But

[029-112] instead of pursuing these happy ideas Schopenhauer preferred to play

[029-113] variations on the themes fashionable in his day.

[029-114] *[[j-f-herbart|J. F. Herbart]].*

[029-115] Most astounding of all is the fact that a dry intellectualist,

[029-116] the avowed enemy of idealism, of dialectic and of speculative

[029-117] constructions, head of the school calling itself realistic or the

[029-118] school of exact philosophy, Johann Friedrich Herbart, when he

[029-119] turns his attention to Æsthetic, turns mystic too, though in a

[029-120] slightly different way. How weightily he speaks when expounding his

[029-121] philosophical method! Æsthetic must not bear the blame of the faults

[029-122] into which metaphysic has fallen; we must make it an independent study,

[029-123] and detach it from all hypothesis about the universe. Nor must it be

[029-124] confounded with psychology or asked to describe the emotions awakened

[029-125] by the content of works of art, such as the pathetic or the comic,

[029-126] sadness or joy; its duty is to determine the essential character of

[029-127] art and beauty. In the analysis of particular cases of beauty and

[029-128] in registering what they reveal lies the way of salvation. These

[029-129] proposals and promises have misled numbers of people as to the nature

[029-130] of Herbart's Æsthetic. But *ce sont là jeux de princes*; by paying

[029-131] attention we shall see what Herbart meant by analysis of particular

[029-132] case; and how he held himself aloof from metaphysics.

[029-133] *Pure [[beauty|Beauty]] and relations of form.*

[029-134] Beauty, for him, consisted in relations: relations of tone, colour,

[029-135] line, thought and will; experience must decide which of these relations

[029-136] are beautiful, and æsthetic science consists solely in enumerating the

[029-137] fundamental concepts (*Musterbegriffe)* in which are summarized the

[029-138] particular cases of beauty. But these relations, Herbart thought, were

[029-139] not like physiological facts; they could not be empirically observed,

[029-140] *e.g.* in a psycho-physical laboratory. To correct this error it is

[029-141] only necessary to observe that these relations include not only tones,

[029-142] lines and colours, but also thoughts and will, and that they extend to

[029-143] moral facts no less than to objects of external intuition. He declares

[029-144] explicitly "No true beauty is sensible, although it frequently happens

[029-145] that sense-impressions precede and follow the intuition of beauty."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_12_443)

[029-146] There is a profound distinction between the beautiful and the pleasant;

[029-147] for the pleasant needs no representation, while the beautiful consists

[029-148] in representation of relations, followed immediately in consciousness

[029-149] by a judgment, an appendix (*Zusatz)* which expresses unqualified

[029-150] approbation ("*es gefällt!*"). And while the pleasant and the

[029-151] unpleasant "in the progress of culture gradually become transient and

[029-152] unimportant, Beauty stands out more and more as something permanent and

[029-153] possessed of undeniable value."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_13_444) The [[taste|judgment of taste]] is universal,

[029-154] eternal, immutable: "the complete representation (*vollendete

[029-155] [[representation|Vorstellung]]*) of the same relations is always followed by the same

[029-156] judgment; just as the same cause always produces the same effect.

[029-157] This happens at all times and in all circumstances, conditions and

[029-158] complications, which gives to the particularity of certain cases the

[029-159] appearance of a universal rule. Granted that the elements of a relation

[029-160] are universal concepts, it is plain that although in judging we think

[029-161] only of the content of these concepts, the judgment must have a sphere

[029-162] as large as that common to the two concepts."[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_14_445) Herbart considers

[029-163] æsthetic judgements as a general class comprising ethical judgements as

[029-164] a subdivision: "amongst other beauties is to be distinguished morality,

[029-165] as a thing not only of value in itself but as actually determining the

[029-166] unconditioned value of persons"; within morality in the narrowest sense

[029-167] is distinguished in turn justice.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_15_446) The five ethica ideas guiding

[029-168] moral life (internal liberty, perfection, benevolence, equity and

[029-169] justice) are five æsthetic ideas or rather æsthetic concepts applied to

[029-170] relations of will.

[029-171] *[[art|Art]] as sum of content and form.*

[029-172] Herbart looks on art as a complex fact, the combination of an

[029-173] extra-æsthetic element, content, which may have logical or

[029-174] psychological or any other kind of value, and a purely æsthetic

[029-175] element, form, which is an application of the fundamental æsthetic

[029-176] concepts. Man looks for that which is diverting, instructive, moving,

[029-177] majestic, ridiculous; and "all these are mingled with the beautiful

[029-178] in order to procure favour and interest for the work. The beautiful

[029-179] thus assumes various complexions, and becomes graceful, magnificent,

[029-180] tragic, or comic; it can become all these because the æsthetic

[029-181] judgement, in itself calmly serene, tolerates the company of the most

[029-182] diverse excitations of the soul which are no part of itself."[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_16_447) But

[029-183] all these things have nothing to do with beauty. In order to discover

[029-184] the objectively beautiful or ugly, one must make abstraction from

[029-185] every predicate concerning the content. "In order to recognize the

[029-186] objectively beautiful or ugly in poetry, one must show the difference

[029-187] between this and that thought, and the discussion will concern itself

[029-188] with thoughts; to recognize it in sculpture, one must show the

[029-189] difference between this and that outline, and the discussion will turn

[029-190] upon outlines; to recognize it in music, one should show the difference

[029-191] between this and that tone, and the discussion will turn upon tones.

[029-192] Now, such predicates as 'magnificent, charming, graceful' and so

[029-193] forth contain nothing whatever about tones, outlines or thoughts, and

[029-194] therefore tell us nothing about the objectively beautiful in poetry,

[029-195] sculpture, or music; indeed they rather lead us to believe in the

[029-196] existence of an objective beauty to which thought, outline, or tone are

[029-197] equally accidental, which may be approached by receiving impressions

[029-198] from poetry, sculpture, music and so forth, obliterating the object

[029-199] and giving oneself up to the pure emotion of mind."[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_17_448) Very different

[029-200] is the æsthetic judgement, the "cold judgement of the connoisseur"

[029-201] who considers exclusively form, *i.e.* objectively pleasant formal

[029-202] relations. This abstraction from the content in order to contemplate

[029-203] pure form is the catharsis produced by art. [[content|Content]] is transitory,

[029-204] relative, subject to moral law and liable to moral judgement: form is

[029-205] permanent, absolute, free.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_18_449) Concrete art may be the sum of two or

[029-206] more values; but the æsthetic fact is form alone.

[029-207] *Herbart and Kantian thought.*

[029-208] The reader who goes behind appearances and discounts diversities of

[029-209] terminology will not fail to observe the close similarity of the

[029-210] æsthetic doctrine of Herbart to that of Kant. In Herbart we again

[029-211] find the distinction between free and adherent beauty, and between

[029-212] form and the sensuous stimulus (*Reiz)* attached to form: we find an

[029-213] affirmation of the existence of pure beauty, the object of necessary

[029-214] and universal, but not discursive, judgements; lastly, we find a

[029-215] certain connexion between beauty and morality, between Æsthetic and

[029-216] Ethics. In these matters Herbart is perhaps the most faithful follower

[029-217] and propagator of the thought of Kant, whose doctrine contains the germ

[029-218] of his own. In one passage he describes himself as "a Kantian, but of

[029-219] the year 1828"; and he is quite right, even in pointing out the exact

[029-220] difference in date. Amidst the errors and uncertainties of his æsthetic

[029-221] thought, Kant is rich in suggestion and scatters fertile seed; he

[029-222] belongs to a period when philosophy was still young and impressionable.

[029-223] Herbart, coming later, is dry and one-sided; he takes whatever is

[029-224] false in Kant's doctrine and hardens it into a system. If they had

[029-225] done little else, the Romanticists and idealists had at least united

[029-226] the theory of beauty to that of art, and destroyed the rhetorical

[029-227] and mechanical view; and they had brought into relief (frequently

[029-228] exaggerating, doubtless) various important characteristics of artistic

[029-229] activity. Herbart re-states the mechanical view, restores the duality,

[029-230] and presents a capricious, narrow, barren mysticism, devoid of all

[029-231] breath of artistic feeling.
