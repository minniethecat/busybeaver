# Epic

The traditional distinction of epic from [[lyric]] and dramatic poetry; Croce systematically rejects this tripartite division as an empirical pseudo-concept that fails to capture the true nature of artistic expression.

## Definition

Croce treats "epic" as one member of the long-standing tripartite division of poetry into lyric, epic, and dramatic kinds. In the *Æsthetic*, he lists "cruel, lyrical, epic, dramatic, chivalrous, idyllic" among the many adjectival classifications that critics apply to works of art (005-114). For Croce, these labels belong to the realm of empirical [[classification]], not philosophical understanding.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to the Theory of Artistic Kinds

Croce's treatment of epic is inseparable from his general critique of the classification of the arts. The distinction between subjective and objective poetry — "lyric from epic" — rests, in his view, on a false dichotomy, since all artistic expression is at once subjective intuition and objective form (005-149, 005-151).

### Key Distinction: Epic vs. History

One of Croce's more nuanced discussions concerns the boundary between epic and historical writing. He observes that "later we distinguish epic from what we call history," noting that early epic served functions later taken over by historiography (025-488). The primitive epic arose not from a distinct aesthetic intention but from the natural impulse to depict: "Natural man depicts himself" spontaneously, without genre consciousness (025-479).

## Historical Context

### The Classical and Renaissance Tradition

The tripartite division into epic, lyric, and dramatic was inherited from Greek poetics and systematised by Renaissance and Neoclassical critics. Aristotle's *Poetics* treated epic alongside tragedy, and later theorists elaborated rules for each genre. Croce traces how this classificatory impulse produced treatises on "the laws of epic or of tragedy, of historical painting" — all of which he considers philosophically empty (005-158).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation lies in refusing to treat the epic/lyric/dramatic distinction as a real division within art. For him, every genuine artistic expression is lyrical in essence — an intuition-expression — and the labelling of some works as "epic" is merely a practical convenience for librarians and booksellers, not a philosophical truth about art. This position follows directly from his identification of intuition with expression: if every expression is individual and unique, no genuine classification of expressions is possible.

## Criticism and Debate

> "All the books dealing with classifications and discussions of the limits of the arts could be burned without any loss whatever." — Croce, *Æsthetic* (016-124)
