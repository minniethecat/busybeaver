# Signification

In Croce's aesthetic system, *signification* refers to the meaning or semantic content borne by aesthetic expressions and artistic forms. Croce treats signification primarily as a **quantitative** rather than qualitative distinction — it differentiates degrees of artistic achievement without establishing separate categories of creative faculty.

## Definition

Croce employs the term "signification" in several interrelated senses throughout the *Æsthetic*. In its most general usage, signification denotes the semantic dimension of expression — what an artistic form *means* or *communicates* beyond its immediate sensible appearance. This usage traces back to Baumgarten's tripartite division of aesthetic theory into heuristic (invention of beautiful thoughts), methodic (aesthetic method), and **semiotic** (the beautiful signification of thoughts) (025-230).

Croce also applies the term in a more specific, critical sense when discussing artistic genius. He argues that the distinction between genius and ordinary talent possesses "more than a quantitative signification," rejecting the Romantic notion of genius as a qualitatively distinct faculty (003-086). In this context, signification serves to mark a difference of *degree* rather than *kind*.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Expression and Intuition

For Croce, signification is not an independent aesthetic category but is **absorbed into expression**. The signifying function of art — its capacity to convey meaning — is inseparable from the intuitive-expressive act itself. When Baumgarten's followers divided aesthetics into heuristic, methodic, and semiotic branches, they were artificially separating what Croce considers a unified spiritual process (025-230).

The aesthetic employment of the term must be distinguished from other domains. Croce observes that "the term signification is employed in one of the following significations" — as equivalent to expression in general, as the conceptual content sometimes associated with aesthetic intuition, or as the practical end to which an artistic form may be put (010-098). Only the first usage is properly aesthetic; the others belong respectively to logic and to economic action.

### Key Distinctions

Croce carefully distinguishes the *aesthetic* signification of terms from their *practical* or *logical* significations. In his discussion of aesthetic feelings, he catalogues "various significations of the word feeling" (011-001), demonstrating how the same term operates differently across the four forms of spiritual activity: intuition, concept, economic action, and moral action. The aesthetic signification is always tied to **individual expression**, never to abstract concepts or practical ends.

A related distinction concerns the relationship between signification and communication. Croce treats the signification of aesthetic forms as **immanent** to the work itself, not as an external message to be decoded. An artistic form "has a completely aesthetic signification" only when it is apprehended as pure intuition, not as a vehicle for conceptual or practical content (014-152).

> The term "signification" is employed in one of the following significations: as equivalent to expression in general, as the conceptual content sometimes associated with aesthetic intuition, or as the practical end to which an artistic form may be put. Croce insists that only the first is properly aesthetic. (010-098, 010-161)

## Historical Context

Baumgarten's original tripartite scheme — heuristic, methodic, semiotic — established signification as a formal branch of aesthetic theory in the eighteenth century. The "beautiful signification of thoughts" (semiotic) formed the third part of his system, occupying two and a half volumes subdivided into sense-apprehension, sensitive faculties, and semiotic proper (025-230).

Later thinkers, particularly in the Neo-Kantian tradition, developed the concept of "transcendental signification" (027-093), attempting to ground aesthetic meaning in a priori conditions of sensibility. Croce's treatment of signification represents a **simplification** of this tradition. By absorbing the semiotic function into expression itself, he eliminates the need for a separate theory of aesthetic signs — a move consistent with his broader project of dissolving distinctions that multiply aesthetic categories unnecessarily.
