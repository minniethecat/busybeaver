# Communication

In Croce's aesthetic system, communication is a practical activity that may or may not follow the aesthetic fact. It is categorically distinct from expression and belongs to the sphere of utility and social action, not to art proper.

## Definition

Communication is "the practical activity" of transmitting an aesthetic expression to other minds (016-188). Croce insists that "communication may or may not follow the aesthetic fact" (016-188). Art as art — as intuition-expression — is complete before any act of communication takes place. The artist may choose to externalize and communicate, but this choice is practical, not aesthetic.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Expression

The core of Croce's position is the radical separation of expression from communication. Expression is the theoretical act of forming an intuition in the spirit; communication is the practical act of transmitting it. "Communication and judgement alike inconceivable" without prior expression (017-094). This reverses the common assumption that art-making is essentially communicative.

### Key Distinctions

1. **Expression vs. Communication** — Expression is internal and constitutive of the aesthetic fact. Communication is external and incidental to it. A work of art is fully art before it is shared.

2. **Communication vs. Externalization** — Externalization is the physical embodiment of expression; communication is the social transmission of that embodiment. One may externalize without communicating, and communicate non-aesthetic facts.

3. **Aesthetic vs. Practical Value** — "If by art be understood the externalization of art, then utility and morality" become entangled with aesthetic judgment (016-189). Croce insists on maintaining the autonomy of the aesthetic by distinguishing it from the practical act of communication.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Throughout the tradition, art has been understood as essentially communicative. Aristotle's *Poetics* treats tragedy as producing an effect (*katharsis*) in an audience. Rhetorical theory from Cicero through the Renaissance placed communication at the center of artistic production. Romantic theorists like Schleiermacher emphasized the hermeneutic act of understanding across individuals.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's radical claim is that communication is not the aim of art. Art aims at expression; whether that expression is then communicated is a separate practical question. This allows Croce to maintain the purity of the aesthetic sphere against utilitarianism and moralism: art does not exist to communicate a message, to instruct, or to persuade.

## Criticism and Debate

> We live in communication with other men" (017-305), and Croce acknowledges this social reality. But critics argue that by making communication incidental to art, Croce cannot account for the deeply social nature of artistic production and reception.

The language-based critique: if art is expression, and expression is inseparable from language, and language is fundamentally communicative, then Croce's separation of expression from communication collapses. Croce's response is that language itself, in his system, is identical with expression, not communication — "linguistic" is synonymous with "expressive."
