[013-001] *Pseudo-æsthetic concepts, and the æsthetic of the

[013-002] sympathetic.*

[013-003] The doctrine of the sympathetic (very often animated and seconded in

[013-004] this by the capricious metaphysical and mystical Æsthetic, and by that

[013-005] blind traditionalism which assumes an intimate connection between

[013-006] things fortuitously treated together by the same authors in the same

[013-007] books), has introduced and rendered familiar in systems of Æsthetic a

[013-008] series of concepts a rapid mention of which suffices to justify our

[013-009] resolute expulsion of them from our own treatise.

[013-010] Their catalogue is long, not to say interminable: *tragic, comic,

[013-011] sublime, pathetic, moving, sad, ridiculous, melancholy, tragi-comic,

[013-012] humorous, majestic, dignified, serious, grave, imposing, noble,

[013-013] decorous, graceful, attractive, piquant, coquettish, idyllic, elegiac,

[013-014] cheerful, violent, ingenuous, cruel, base, horrible, disgusting,

[013-015] dreadful, nauseating;* the fist can be increased at will.

[013-016] Since that doctrine took the sympathetic as its special object, it was

[013-017] naturally unable to neglect any of the varieties of the sympathetic,

[013-018] any of the mixtures or gradations by means of which, starting from

[013-019] the sympathetic in its loftiest and most intense manifestation, its

[013-020] contrary, the antipathetic and repugnant, is finally reached. And

[013-021] since the sympathetic content was held to be the *beautiful* and

[013-022] the antipathetic the *ugly,* the varieties (tragic, comic, sublime,

[013-023] pathetic, etc.) constituted for that conception of Æsthetic the shades

[013-024] and gradations intervening between [[beauty|the beautiful]] and the ugly.

[013-025] *Criticism of the theory of the ugly in art and of the

[013-026] overcoming of it.*

[013-027] Having enumerated and defined as well as it could, the chief of these

[013-028] varieties, the Æsthetic of the sympathetic set itself the problem

[013-029] of the place to be assigned to the *ugly in art.* This problem is

[013-030] without meaning for us, who do not recognize any ugliness save the

[013-031] anti-æsthetic or inexpressive, which can never form *part* of the

[013-032] æsthetic fact, being, on the contrary, its *antithesis.* But in the

[013-033] doctrine which we are here criticizing the positing and discussion

[013-034] of that problem meant neither more nor less than the necessity of

[013-035] reconciling in some way the false and defective idea of art from which

[013-036] it started—art reduced to the representation of the pleasurable—with

[013-037] real art, which occupies a far wider field. Hence the artificial

[013-038] attempt to settle what examples of the *ugly* (antipathetic) could be

[013-039] admitted in artistic representation, and for what reasons, and in what

[013-040] ways.

[013-041] The answer was: that the ugly is admissible, only when it can be

[013-042] *overcome*; an unconquerable ugliness, such as the *disgusting* or the

[013-043] *nauseating,* being altogether excluded. Further, that the duty of

[013-044] the ugly, when admitted in art, is to contribute towards heightening

[013-045] the effect of the beautiful (sympathetic), by producing a series of

[013-046] contrasts, from which the pleasurable may issue more efficacious and

[013-047] joy-giving. It is, indeed, a common observation that pleasure is more

[013-048] vividly felt when preceded by abstinence and suffering. Thus the ugly

[013-049] in art was looked upon as adapted for the service of the beautiful, a

[013-050] stimulant and condiment of æsthetic pleasure.

[013-051] That special refinement of hedonistic theory which used to be pompously

[013-052] called the doctrine of the *overcoming of the ugly* falls with the

[013-053] Æsthetic of the sympathetic, and with it the enumeration and definition

[013-054] of the concepts mentioned above, which show themselves to be completely

[013-055] foreign to Æsthetic. For Æsthetic does not recognize the sympathetic or

[013-056] the antipathetic or their varieties, but only the spiritual activity of

[013-057] representation.

[013-058] *Pseudo-æsthetic concepts belong to Psychology.*

[013-059] Nevertheless, the important place which, as we have said, those

[013-060] concepts have hitherto occupied in æsthetic treatises makes it

[013-061] advisable to supply a rather more complete explanation as to their

[013-062] nature. What shall be their lot? Excluded from Æsthetic, in what other

[013-063] part of Philosophy will they be received?

[013-064] In truth, nowhere; for all those concepts are without philosophical

[013-065] value. They are nothing but a series of classes, which can be fashioned

[013-066] in the most various ways and multiplied at pleasure, to which it is

[013-067] sought to reduce the infinite complications and shadings of the values

[013-068] and disvalues of life. Of these classes, some have an especially

[013-069] positive significance, like the beautiful, [[sublime|the sublime]], the majestic,

[013-070] the solemn, the serious, the weighty, the noble, the elevated; others

[013-071] a significance chiefly negative, like the ugly, the painful, the

[013-072] horrible, the dreadful, the tremendous, the monstrous, the insipid, the

[013-073] extravagant; finally in others a mixed significance prevails, such as

[013-074] the comic, the tender, the melancholy, the humorous, the tragi-comic.

[013-075] The complications are infinite, because the individuations are

[013-076] infinite; hence it is not possible to construct the concepts, save in

[013-077] the arbitrary and approximate manner proper to the natural sciences,

[013-078] satisfied with making the best classification they can of that reality

[013-079] which they can neither exhaust by enumeration, nor understand and

[013-080] conquer speculatively. And since *Psychology* is the naturalistic

[013-081] science which undertakes to construct types and schemes of the

[013-082] spiritual life of man (a science whose merely empirical and descriptive

[013-083] character becomes more evident day by day), these concepts do not

[013-084] belong to Æsthetic, nor to Philosophy in general, but must simply be

[013-085] handed over to Psychology.

[013-086] *Impossibility of rigorous definitions of them.*

[013-087] The case of those concepts is that of all other psychological

[013-088] constructions: no rigorous definitions of them are possible; and

[013-089] consequently they cannot be deduced from one another nor be connected

[013-090] in a system, though this has often been attempted, with great waste

[013-091] of time and without obtaining thereby any useful results. Nor can it

[013-092] be claimed as possible to obtain empirical definitions, universally

[013-093] acceptable as precise and true in the place of those philosophical

[013-094] definitions recognized as impossible. For no single definition of a

[013-095] single fact can be given, but there are innumerable definitions of it,

[013-096] according to the cases and the purposes for which they are made; and

[013-097] it is clear that if there were only one which had the value of truth

[013-098] it would no longer be an empirical, but a rigorous and philosophical

[013-099] definition. And as a matter of fact whenever one of the terms to which

[013-100] we have referred has been employed (or indeed any other belonging to

[013-101] the same class), a new definition of it has been given at the same

[013-102] time, expressed or understood. Each one of those definitions differed

[013-103] somehow from the others, in some particular, however minute, and in its

[013-104] implied reference to some individual fact or other, which thus became a

[013-105] special object of attention and was raised to the position of a general

[013-106] type. Thus it is that not one of such definitions satisfies either the

[013-107] hearer or the constructor of it. For a moment later he finds himself

[013-108] before a new instance to which he recognizes that his definition is

[013-109] more or less insufficient, ill-adapted, and in need of retouching. So

[013-110] we must leave writers and speakers free to define the sublime or the

[013-111] comic, the tragic or the humorous, on every occasion as they please and

[013-112] as may suit the end they have in view. And if an empirical definition

[013-113] of universal validity be demanded, we can but submit this one:—The

[013-114] sublime (or comic, tragic, humorous, etc.) is *everything* that is or

[013-115] shall be so *called* by those who have employed or shall employ these

[013-116] *words.*

[013-117] *Examples: definitions of the sublime, the comic, the

[013-118] humorous.*

[013-119] What is the sublime? The unexpected affirmation of an overwhelming

[013-120] moral force: that is one definition. But the other definition is

[013-121] equally good, which recognizes the sublime also where the force which

[013-122] affirms itself is certainly overwhelming, but immoral and destructive.

[013-123] Both remain vague and lack precision, until applied to a concrete

[013-124] case, to an example which makes clear what is meant by "overwhelming,"

[013-125] and what by unexpected. They are quantitative concepts, but falsely

[013-126] quantitative, since there is no way of measuring them; they are at

[013-127] bottom metaphors, emphatic phrases, or logical tautologies. The

[013-128] humorous will be laughter amid tears, bitter laughter, the sudden

[013-129] spring from the comic to the tragic and from the tragic to the comic,

[013-130] the romantic comic, the opposite of the sublime, war declared against

[013-131] every attempt at insincerity, compassion ashamed to weep, a laugh,

[013-132] not at the fact, but at the ideal itself; and what you will beside,

[013-133] according as it is wished to get a view of the physiognomy of this or

[013-134] that poet, of this or that poem, which, in its uniqueness, is its own

[013-135] definition, and though momentary and circumscribed, is alone adequate.

[013-136] The comic has been defined as the displeasure arising from the

[013-137] perception of a deformity immediately followed by a greater pleasure

[013-138] arising from the relaxation of our psychical forces, strained in

[013-139] expectation of a perception looked upon as important. While listening

[013-140] to a narrative, which might, for example, be a description of the

[013-141] magnificently heroic purpose of some individual, we anticipate in

[013-142] imagination the occurrence of a magnificent and heroic action, and we

[013-143] prepare for its reception by concentrating our psychic forces. All of

[013-144] a sudden, however, instead of the magnificent and heroic action, which

[013-145] the preliminaries and the tone of the narrative had led us to expect,

[013-146] there is an unexpected change to a small, mean, foolish action, which

[013-147] does not satisfy to our expectation. We have been deceived, and the

[013-148] recognition of the deceit brings with it an instant of displeasure. But

[013-149] this instant is as it were conquered by that which immediately follows:

[013-150] we are able to relax our strained attention, to free ourselves from

[013-151] the provision of accumulated psychic energy henceforth superfluous, to

[013-152] feel ourselves light and well. This is the pleasure of the comic, with

[013-153] its physiological equivalent of laughter. If the unpleasant fact that

[013-154] has appeared should painfully affect our interests, there would not

[013-155] be pleasure, laughter would be at once suffocated, the psychic energy

[013-156] would be strained and overstrained by other more weighty perceptions.

[013-157] If on the other hand such more weighty perceptions do not appear, if

[013-158] the whole loss be limited to a slight deception of our foresight,

[013-159] then the feeling of our psychic wealth that ensues affords ample

[013-160] compensation for this very slight disappointment. Such, expressed in

[013-161] a few words, is one of the most accurate modern definitions of the

[013-162] comic. It boasts of containing in itself, justified or corrected and

[013-163] verified, the manifold attempts to define the comic, from [[greece|Hellenic]]

[013-164] antiquity to our own day, from Plato's definition in the *Philebus,*

[013-165] and from Aristotle's, which is more explicit, and looks upon the comic

[013-166] as an *ugliness without pain,* to that of Hobbes, who replaced it in

[013-167] the feeling of *individual superiority*; of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], who saw in it the

[013-168] *relaxation of a tension*; or from the other proposals of those for

[013-169] whom it was *the conflict between great and small, between the finite

[013-170] and the infinite* and so on. But on close observation, the analysis

[013-171] and definition above given, although in appearance most elaborate

[013-172] and precise, yet enunciates characteristics which are applicable,

[013-173] not only to the comic, but to every spiritual process; such as the

[013-174] succession of painful and pleasing moments and the satisfaction

[013-175] arising from the consciousness of strength and of its free expansion.

[013-176] The differentiation is here given by quantitative determinations

[013-177] whose limits cannot be laid down. They therefore remain vague words,

[013-178] possessing some degree of meaning from their reference to this or that

[013-179] particular comic fact, and from the psychic disposition of qualities of

[013-180] the speaker. If such definitions be taken too seriously, there happens

[013-181] to them what Jean Paul Richter said of all the definitions of the

[013-182] comic: namely, that their sole merit is *to be themselves comic* and to

[013-183] produce in reality the fact which they vainly try to fix logically. And

[013-184] who will ever logically determine the dividing line between the comic

[013-185] and the non-comic, between laughter and smiles, between smiling and

[013-186] gravity, or cut the ever varying continuum into which life melts into

[013-187] clearly divided parts?

[013-188] *Relation between these concepts and æsthetic concepts.*

[013-189] The facts, classified as far as possible in these psychological

[013-190] concepts, bear no relation to the artistic fact, beyond the general

[013-191] one, that all of them, in so far as they constitute the material of

[013-192] life, can become the object of artistic representation; and the other,

[013-193] an accidental relation, that æsthetic facts also may sometimes enter

[013-194] the processes described, such as the impression of the sublime aroused

[013-195] by the work of a Titanic artist, such as Dante or Shakespeare, and of

[013-196] the comic produced by the attempts of a dauber or scribbler.

[013-197] But here too the process is external to the æsthetic fact, to which

[013-198] is linked only the feeling of æsthetic value and disvalue, of the

[013-199] beautiful and of the ugly. Dante's Farinata is æsthetically beautiful

[013-200] and nothing but beautiful: if the force of will of that personage seem

[013-201] also sublime, or the expression that Dante gives him seem, by reason of

[013-202] his great genius, sublime in comparison with that of a less energetic

[013-203] poet, these are things altogether outside æsthetic consideration. We

[013-204] repeat again that this last pays attention always and only to the

[013-205] adequateness of the expression, that is to say, to beauty.
