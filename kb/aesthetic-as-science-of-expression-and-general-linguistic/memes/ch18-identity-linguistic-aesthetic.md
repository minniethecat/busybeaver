[018-001] This brief exposition of the method by which is obtained the

[018-002] reintegration of the original conditions in which the [[art|work of art]]

[018-003] was produced, and consequently reproduction and judgement are made

[018-004] possible, shows how important is the function fulfilled by historical

[018-005] research in relation to artistic and literary works which is what is

[018-006] usually called *historical criticism* or method in literature and art.

[018-007] *Historical criticism in literature and art. Its

[018-008] importance.*

[018-009] Without tradition and historical criticism the enjoyment of all or

[018-010] nearly all the works of art produced by humanity would be irrevocably

[018-011] lost: we should be little more than animals, immersed in the present

[018-012] alone, or in the most recent past. It is fatuous to despise and laugh

[018-013] at one who reconstitutes an authentic text, explains the sense of

[018-014] forgotten words and customs, investigates the conditions in which an

[018-015] artist lived, and accomplishes all those labours which revive the

[018-016] qualities and the original colouring of works of art.

[018-017] Sometimes a depreciatory or negative judgement is passed upon

[018-018] historical research because of the presumed or proved inability of such

[018-019] researches, in many cases, to give us a true understanding of works

[018-020] of art. But it must be observed, in the first place, that historical

[018-021] research does not only fulfil the task of helping to reproduce and

[018-022] judge artistic works: the biography of a writer or of an artist, for

[018-023] example, and the study of the customs of a period, have an interest of

[018-024] their own, that is to say, extraneous to the history of art, but not to

[018-025] other forms of historiography. If allusion be made to those researches

[018-026] which do not appear to have interest of any kind, nor to fulfil any

[018-027] purpose, it must be replied that the historical student must often

[018-028] reconcile himself to the useful but inglorious function of a collector

[018-029] of facts. These facts remain for the time being formless, incoherent

[018-030] and meaningless, but they are preserves or mines for the historian of

[018-031] the future and for whosoever may afterwards want them for any purpose.

[018-032] In the same way in a library, books which nobody asks for are placed

[018-033] on the shelves and catalogued, because they may be asked for at some

[018-034] time or other. Certainly, just as an intelligent librarian gives the

[018-035] preference to the acquisition and cataloguing of those books which he

[018-036] foresees may be of more or better service, so intelligent students

[018-037] possess an instinct as to what is or may more probably be of use among

[018-038] the material of facts which they are examining; while others less

[018-039] well endowed, less intelligent or more hasty in producing, accumulate

[018-040] useless rubbish, refuse and sweepings, and lose themselves in details

[018-041] and petty discussions. But this appertains to the economy of research,

[018-042] and does not concern us. It concerns at most the master who selects the

[018-043] subjects, the publisher who pays for the printing, and the critic who

[018-044] is called upon to praise or to blame the research workers.

[018-045] On the other hand, it is clear that historical research directed to

[018-046] illuminate a work of art does not alone suffice to bring it to birth

[018-047] in our spirit and place us in a position to judge it, but presupposes

[018-048] taste, that is to say, an alert and cultivated imagination. The

[018-049] greatest historical erudition may accompany a gross or otherwise

[018-050] defective taste, a slow imagination, or, as they say, a cold hard heart

[018-051] closed to art. Which is the lesser evil, great erudition with defective

[018-052] taste, or natural taste and much ignorance? The question has often been

[018-053] asked, and perhaps it will be best to deny that it has any meaning,

[018-054] because one cannot tell which of two evils is the less, or what exactly

[018-055] that means. The merely learned man never succeeds in entering into

[018-056] direct communion with great spirits; he keeps wandering for ever about

[018-057] the outer courts, the staircases and antechambers of their palaces; but

[018-058] the gifted ignoramus either passes by masterpieces to him inaccessible,

[018-059] or instead of understanding works of art as they really are, invents

[018-060] others with his [[imagination|fancy]]. Now, the labour of the former may at least serve

[018-061] to enlighten others; but the genius of the latter remains altogether

[018-062] sterile in relation to knowledge. How then can we in a certain respect

[018-063] fail to prefer the conscientious learned man to the inconclusive though

[018-064] gifted man, who is not really gifted, if he resign himself and in so

[018-065] far as he resigns himself, to his inconclusiveness?

[018-066] *Literary and artistic history. Its distinction from

[018-067] historical criticism and from the æsthetic judgement.*

[018-068] We must accurately distinguish *the history of art and literature*

[018-069] from those historical labours where works of art are used, but for

[018-070] extraneous purposes (such as biography, civil, religious and political

[018-071] history, etc.), and also from historical erudition directed to the

[018-072] preparation of the æsthetic synthesis of reproduction.

[018-073] The difference of the first two is obvious. The history of art and

[018-074] literature has the works of art themselves as its principal subject;

[018-075] those other labours invoke and interrogate works of art, but only

[018-076] as witnesses from whom to discover the truth of facts which are not

[018-077] æsthetic. The second difference to which we have referred may seem less

[018-078] profound. It is, however, very great. Erudition directed to illuminate

[018-079] the understanding of works of art aims simply at calling into existence

[018-080] a certain internal fact, an æsthetic reproduction. Artistic and

[018-081] literary history, on the other hand, does not appear until after such

[018-082] reproduction has been obtained. It implies, therefore, a further stage

[018-083] of labour.

[018-084] Like all other history, its object is to record precisely such facts

[018-085] as have really taken place, in this case artistic and literary facts.

[018-086] A man who, after having acquired the requisite historical erudition,

[018-087] reproduces in himself and tastes a work of art, may remain simply

[018-088] a man of taste, or at the most express his own feeling with an

[018-089] exclamation of praise or condemnation. This does not suffice for

[018-090] the making of a historian of literature and art. Something else is

[018-091] needed, namely, that a new mental operation succeed in him the simple

[018-092] reproduction. This new operation is in its turn an expression: the

[018-093] expression of the reproduction; the historical description, exposition

[018-094] or representation. There is this difference, then, between the man of

[018-095] taste and the historian: the first merely reproduces in his spirit the

[018-096] work of art; the second, after having reproduced it, represents it

[018-097] historically, or applies those categories by which, as we know, history

[018-098] is differentiated from pure art. Artistic and literary history is

[018-099] therefore *a historical work of art founded upon one or more works of

[018-100] art.*

[018-101] The name "artistic" or "literary" critic is used in various senses:

[018-102] sometimes it is applied to the scholar who devotes his services to

[018-103] literature; sometimes to the historian who reveals the works of art of

[018-104] the past in their reality; more often to both. By critic is sometimes

[018-105] understood in a more restricted sense he who judges and describes

[018-106] contemporary literary works, and by historian, he who treats of those

[018-107] less recent. These are linguistic uses and empirical distinctions,

[018-108] which may be neglected; because the true difference lies between

[018-109] *the scholar, the man of taste* and *the historian of art.* These

[018-110] words designate three successive stages of work, each one independent

[018-111] relatively to the one that follows, but not to that which precedes. As

[018-112] we have seen, a man may be a mere scholar, and possess little capacity

[018-113] for understanding works of art; he may even both be learned and possess

[018-114] taste, yet be unable to portray them by writing a page of artistic and

[018-115] literary history. But the true and complete historian, while containing

[018-116] in himself both the scholar and the man of taste as necessary

[018-117] pre-requisites, must add to their qualities the gift of historical

[018-118] comprehension and representation.

[018-119] *The method of artistic and literary history.*

[018-120] The theory of artistic and literary historical method presents problems

[018-121] and difficulties, some common to the theory of historical method in

[018-122] general, others peculiar to it, because derived from the concept of art

[018-123] itself.

[018-124] *Criticism of the problem of the origin of art.*

[018-125] History is commonly divided into human history, natural history, and

[018-126] the mixture of both. Without! examining here the question of the

[018-127] solidity of this distinction, it is clear that artistic and literary

[018-128] history belongs in any case to the first, since it concerns a spiritual

[018-129] activity, that is to say, an activity proper to man. And since this

[018-130] activity is its subject, the absurdity of propounding the historical

[018-131] problem of the *origin* of *art* becomes at once evident. We should

[018-132] note that by this formula many different things have in turn been

[018-133] included on many different occasions. *Origin* has often meant *nature*

[018-134] or *character* of the artistic fact, in which case an attempt was

[018-135] made to deal with a real scientific or philosophic problem, the very

[018-136] problem in fact which our treatise has attempted to solve. At other

[018-137] times, by origin has been understood the *ideal genesis,* the search

[018-138] for the reason of art, the deduction of the artistic fact from a first

[018-139] principle containing in itself both spirit and nature. This is also

[018-140] a philosophical problem, complementary to the preceding, coinciding

[018-141] indeed with it, although it has sometimes been strangely interpreted

[018-142] and solved by means of an arbitrary and semi-imaginary metaphysic.

[018-143] But when the object was to discover further exactly in what way the

[018-144] artistic function was *historically formed,* the result has been the

[018-145] absurdity which we have mentioned. If expression be the first form of

[018-146] consciousness, how can we look for the historical origin of what is not

[018-147] a product of nature and is presupposed by human history? How can we

[018-148] assign a historical genesis to a thing which is a category by means of

[018-149] which all historical processes and facts are understood? The absurdity

[018-150] has arisen from the comparison with human institutions, which have been

[018-151] formed in the course of history, and have disappeared or may disappear

[018-152] in its course. Between the æsthetic fact and a human institution

[018-153] (such as monogamic marriage or the fief) there exists a difference

[018-154] comparable with that between simple and compound bodies in chemistry.

[018-155] It is impossible to indicate the formation of the former, otherwise

[018-156] they would not be simple, and if this be discovered, they cease to be

[018-157] simple and become compound.

[018-158] The problem of the origin of art, historically understood, is only

[018-159] justified when it is proposed to investigate, not the formation of

[018-160] the artistic category, but where and when art has appeared for the

[018-161] first time (appeared, that is to say, in a striking manner), at what

[018-162] point or in what region of the globe and at what point or epoch of its

[018-163] history; when, that is to say, not the origin of art, but its earliest

[018-164] or primitive history is the object of research. This problem forms

[018-165] one with that of the appearance of human civilization on the earth.

[018-166] Data for its solution are certainly wanting, but there yet remains

[018-167] the abstract possibility of a solution, and certainly tentative and

[018-168] hypothetical solutions abound.

[018-169] *The criterion of progress and history.*

[018-170] Every representation of human history has the concept of *progress* as

[018-171] foundation. But by progress must not be understood the imaginary *law

[018-172] of progress* which is supposed to lead the generations of man with

[018-173] irresistible force to some unknown destiny, according to a providential

[018-174] plan which we can divine and then understand logically. A supposed law

[018-175] of this sort is the negation of history itself, of that accidentality,

[018-176] that empiricity, that contingency, which distinguish concrete fact

[018-177] from abstraction. And for the same reason, progress has nothing to do

[018-178] with the so-called law of *evolution,* which, if it mean that reality

[018-179] evolves (and it is only reality in so far as it evolves or becomes),

[018-180] cannot be called a law, and if it be given as a law, becomes identical

[018-181] with the law of progress in the sense just described. The progress

[018-182] of which we speak here is nothing but *the very concept of human

[018-183] activity,* which, working upon the material supplied to it by nature,

[018-184] conquers its obstacles and bends it to its own ends.

[018-185] Such conception of progress, that is to say, of human activity

[018-186] applied to a given material, is the *point of view* of the historian

[018-187] of humanity. No one but a mere collector of unrelated facts, a mere

[018-188] antiquary or inconsequent annalist, can put together the smallest

[018-189] narrative of human doings unless he have a determined point of

[018-190] view, that is to say, a personal conviction of his own regarding the

[018-191] facts whose history he has undertaken to relate. No one can start

[018-192] from the confused and discordant mass of crude facts and arrive at

[018-193] the historical work of art save by means of this apperception, which

[018-194] makes it possible to carve a definite representation in that rough and

[018-195] formless mass. The historian of a practical action should know what is

[018-196] economy and what is morality; the historian of mathematics, what is

[018-197] mathematics; the historian of botany, what is botany; the historian

[018-198] of philosophy, what is philosophy. If he does not really know these

[018-199] things, he must at least have the illusion of knowing them; otherwise

[018-200] he will not even be able to delude himself into believing that he is

[018-201] writing history.

[018-202] We cannot here expand the demonstration of the necessity and

[018-203] inevitability of this subjective criterion in every narrative of human

[018-204] affairs (which is compatible with the utmost objectivity, impartiality

[018-205] and scrupulousness in dealing with data of fact and indeed forms a

[018-206] constitutive element in these virtues), in every narrative of human

[018-207] doings and happenings. It suffices to read any book of history to

[018-208] discover at once the point of view of the author, if he be a historian

[018-209] worthy of the name and know his own business. There are liberal and

[018-210] reactionary, rationalist and catholic historians, who deal with

[018-211] political or social history; for the history of philosophy there

[018-212] are metaphysical, empirical, sceptical, idealist and spiritualist

[018-213] historians. Purely historical historians do not and cannot exist.

[018-214] Were Thucydides and Polybius, Livy and Tacitus, Machiavelli and

[018-215] Guicciardini, Giannone and Voltaire, wholly without moral and political

[018-216] views; and, in our time, was Guizot or Thiers, Macaulay or Balbo,

[018-217] Ranke or Mommsen? And in the history of philosophy, from [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], who

[018-218] was the first to raise it to a great height, to Ritter, Zeller,

[018-219] Cousin, Lewes and our Spaventa, was there one who did not possess his

[018-220] conception of progress and his criterion of judgement? Is there one

[018-221] single work of any value on the history of Æsthetic which has not

[018-222] been written from this or that point of view, with this or that bias

[018-223] (Hegelian or Herbartian), from a sensationalist or from an eclectic

[018-224] or some other point of view? If the historian is to escape from the

[018-225] inevitable necessity of taking a side, he must become a political or

[018-226] scientific eunuch; and history is not an occupation for eunuchs. Such

[018-227] would at most be of use in compiling those great tomes of not useless

[018-228] erudition, *elumbis atque fracta,* which are called, not without

[018-229] reason, monkish.

[018-230] If, then, a concept of progress, a point of view, a criterion, be

[018-231] inevitable, the best to be done is not to try and escape from it,

[018-232] but to obtain the best possible. Every one tends to this end when he

[018-233] forms his own convictions, seriously and laboriously. Historians who

[018-234] profess to wish to interrogate the facts without adding anything of

[018-235] their own to them are not to be trusted. This is at best the result

[018-236] of ingenuousness and illusion on their part: they will always add

[018-237] something of their own, if they be truly historians, even without

[018-238] knowing it, or they will only believe that they have avoided doing

[018-239] so because they have conveyed it only by hints, which is the most

[018-240] insinuating, penetrative and effective of methods.

[018-241] *Non-existence of a single line of progress in artistic and

[018-242] literary history.*

[018-243] Artistic and literary history cannot dispense with the criterion of

[018-244] progress any more easily than other history. We cannot show what a

[018-245] given work of art is, save by proceeding from a conception of art, in

[018-246] order to fix the artistic problem which the author of such work of art

[018-247] had to solve, and by determining whether or no he has solved it, or by

[018-248] how much and in what way he has failed to do so. But it is important

[018-249] to note that the criterion of progress assumes a different form in

[018-250] artistic and literary history to that which it assumes (or is believed

[018-251] to assume) in the history of science.

[018-252] It is customary to represent the whole history of knowledge by one

[018-253] single line of progress and regress. Science is the universal, and

[018-254] its problems are arranged in one single vast system or comprehensive

[018-255] problem. All thinkers labour upon the same problem as to the nature of

[018-256] reality and of knowledge: contemplative Indians and Greek philosophers,

[018-257] Christians and Mohammedans, bare heads and turbaned heads, wigged heads

[018-258] and college-capped heads (as Heine said); and future generations will

[018-259] weary themselves with it, as ours has done. It would take too long to

[018-260] inquire here if this be true or not of science. But it is certainly

[018-261] not true of art; art is intuition, and intuition is individuality, and

[018-262] individuality does not repeat itself. To conceive of the history of the

[018-263] artistic production of the human race as developed along a single line

[018-264] of progress and regress would therefore be altogether erroneous.

[018-265] At the most, and working to some extent with generalizations and

[018-266] abstractions, it may be asserted that the history of æsthetic

[018-267] productions shows progressive cycles, but each cycle with its own

[018-268] problem and each progressive only in respect to that problem. When many

[018-269] are at work in a general way upon the same subject, without succeeding

[018-270] in giving to it the suitable form, yet drawing always more near to

[018-271] it, there is said to be progress, and when appears the man who gives

[018-272] it definite form, the cycle is said to be complete, and progress is

[018-273] ended. A typical example of this would here be the progress in the

[018-274] elaboration of the mode of using the subject-matter of chivalry, during

[018-275] the [[renaissance|Italian Renaissance]], from Pulci to Ariosto (using this as an

[018-276] example and excusing excessive simplification). Nothing but repetition

[018-277] and imitation, diminution or exaggeration, a spoiling of what had

[018-278] already been done, in short decadence could be the result of employing

[018-279] that same material after Ariosto. The epigoni of Ariosto prove this.

[018-280] Progress begins with the beginning of a new cycle. Cervantes, with

[018-281] his more open and conscious irony, is an instance of this. In what

[018-282] did the general decadence of Italian literature at the end of the

[018-283] sixteenth century consist? Simply in having nothing more to say and in

[018-284] repeating and exaggerating motives already discovered. If the Italians

[018-285] of this period had even been able to express their own decadence, they

[018-286] would not have been altogether failures, but would have anticipated

[018-287] the literary movement of the Risorgimento. Where the matter is not

[018-288] the same, a progressive cycle does not exist. Shakespeare does not

[018-289] represent an advance on Dante, nor Goethe upon Shakespeare. Dante,

[018-290] however, represents an advance on the visionaries of the Middle Ages,

[018-291] Shakespeare on the Elizabethan dramatists, Goethe, with *Werther* and

[018-292] the first part of *Faust,* on the writers of the *Sturm und Drang*

[018-293] period. This mode of presenting the history of poetry and art contains,

[018-294] however, as we have remarked, something of the abstract, of the merely

[018-295] practical, and is without strict philosophical value. Not only is the

[018-296] art of savages not inferior, as art, to that of civilized peoples,

[018-297] if it be correlative to the impressions of the savage; but every

[018-298] individual, indeed every moment of the spiritual life of an individual,

[018-299] has its artistic world; none of these worlds can be compared with any

[018-300] other in respect of artistic value.

[018-301] *Errors committed against this law.*

[018-302] Many have sinned and continue to sin against this special form of the

[018-303] criterion of progress in artistic and literary history. Some, for

[018-304] instance, talk of the infancy of Italian art in Giotto, and of its

[018-305] maturity in Raphæl or in Titian; as though Giotto were not complete

[018-306] and absolutely perfect, granted the material of feeling with which his

[018-307] mind was furnished. He was certainly incapable of drawing a figure

[018-308] like Raphæl, or of colouring it like Titian; but was Raphæl or Titian

[018-309] capable of creating the *Marriage of Saint Francis with Poverty* or

[018-310] the *Death of Saint Francis*? The spirit of Giotto had not felt the

[018-311] attraction of the body beautiful, which the Renaissance studied and

[018-312] raised to a place of honour; the spirits of Raphæl and of Titian were

[018-313] no longer interested in certain movements of ardour and of tenderness

[018-314] with which the man of the fourteenth century was in love. How, then,

[018-315] can a comparison be made, where there is no comparative term?

[018-316] The celebrated divisions of the history of art into an oriental period,

[018-317] representing a lack of equilibrium between idea and form, the latter

[018-318] dominating, a classical representing an equilibrium between idea and

[018-319] form, a romantic representing a new lack of equilibrium between idea

[018-320] and form, the former dominating, suffer from the same defect. The same

[018-321] is true of the division into oriental art, representing imperfection

[018-322] of form; classical, perfection of form; romantic or modern, perfection

[018-323] of content and of form. Thus classic and romantic have also received,

[018-324] among their many other meanings, that of progressive or regressive

[018-325] periods, in respect to the realization of some alleged artistic ideal

[018-326] of all humanity.

[018-327] *Other meanings of the word "progress" in respect to

[018-328] Æsthetic.*

[018-329] There is no such thing, then, as an *æsthetic* progress of humanity.

[018-330] However, by æsthetic progress is sometimes meant, not what the two

[018-331] words coupled together really signify, but the ever-increasing

[018-332] accumulation of our historical knowledge, which makes us able to

[018-333] sympathize with all the artistic products of all peoples and of all

[018-334] times, or, as they say, makes our taste more catholic. The difference

[018-335] appears very great if the eighteenth century, so incapable of escaping

[018-336] from itself, be compared with our own time, which enjoys alike Greek

[018-337] and Roman art, now better understood, Byzantine, mediæval, Arabic and

[018-338] Renaissance art, the art of the Cinquecento, baroque art, and the art

[018-339] of the eighteenth century. Egyptian, Babylonian, Etruscan, and even

[018-340] prehistoric art are more profoundly studied every day. Certainly,

[018-341] the difference between the savage and civilized man does not lie in

[018-342] the human faculties. The savage has speech, intellect, religion and

[018-343] morality in common with civilized man, and is a complete man. The only

[018-344] difference lies in this, that civilized man penetrates and dominates

[018-345] a larger portion of the universe with his theoretic and practical

[018-346] activity. We cannot claim to be more spiritually alert than, for

[018-347] example, the contemporaries of Pericles; but no one can deny that we

[018-348] are richer than they—rich with their riches and with those of how many

[018-349] other peoples and generations besides our own?

[018-350] By æsthetic progress is also meant, in another sense, which is also

[018-351] improper, the greater abundance of artistic intuitions and the smaller

[018-352] number of imperfect or inferior works which one epoch produces in

[018-353] respect to another. Thus it may be said that there was æsthetic

[018-354] progress, an artistic awakening in [[italy|Italy]], at the end of the thirteenth

[018-355] or of the fifteenth century.

[018-356] Finally, æsthetic progress is talked of in a third sense, with an eye

[018-357] to the refinement and complications of soul-states exhibited in the

[018-358] works of art of the most civilized peoples, as compared with those of

[018-359] less civilized peoples, barbarians and savages. But in this case the

[018-360] progress is of the comprehensive psycho-social conditions, not of the

[018-361] artistic activity, to which the material is indifferent.

[018-362] These are the most important points to note concerning the method of

[018-363] artistic and literary history.
