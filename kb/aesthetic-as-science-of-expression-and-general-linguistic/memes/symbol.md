# Symbol

In Croce's aesthetics, the concept of the symbol occupies a critical boundary: when understood as an inseparable aspect of artistic intuition, it is synonymous with art itself; when treated as a separable sign standing for a distinct meaning, it collapses into intellectualist allegory and must be rejected.

## Definition

Croce addresses the symbol directly in his chapter on analogous errors in aesthetic theory. He observes that "the *symbol* has sometimes been given as the essence of art" (005-070).

His resolution is twofold. If the symbol is conceived as inseparable from the artistic intuition, "it is a synonym for the intuition itself, which always has an ideal character" (005-071). In this sense, the symbol is not an additional layer of meaning but the very fabric of expressive form.

If, however, the symbol is conceived as separable -- a sign distinct from the thing signified -- then "we fall back again into the intellectualist error: the so-called symbol is the exposition of an abstract concept, an *allegory*; it is science, or art aping science" (005-077).

This decisive distinction frames the entire Crocean treatment: art cannot be split into a sensible shell and an intelligible kernel.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

The symbol, in Croce's analysis, touches all four levels of spiritual activity -- intuition, concept, economic will, and moral will -- but in a negative sense. When the symbol is taken as a separable signifier-signified relation, it drags the aesthetic into the logical sphere, confusing the first grade of cognition (intuition) with the second (concept).

The proper place of the symbolic is thus within the aesthetic itself: "in art all is symbolical, because all is ideal" (005-074). The ideality of art means that every expression, simply by being expression, is symbolic in the only legitimate sense.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp line between the symbol and related categories that are often conflated with it. The aesthetic symptom or sign (such as the expression of emotions studied by Darwin) belongs to naturalistic semiotics, not to aesthetics.

"There is nothing in common between the science of spiritual expression and a *Semiotic,* whether it be medical, meteorological, political, physiognomic, or chiromantic" (014-050). The symbol in the true aesthetic sense is always spiritual expression, never a mere natural sign.

The distinction between realistic and symbolic art is equally problematic. Croce demonstrates that the same terms are used with contradictory meanings:

> Some will say of two pictures -- one without inspiration, in which the author has copied natural objects without intelligence; the other inspired, but without close relation to existing objects -- that the first is *realistic,* the second *symbolic.* Others, on the contrary, utter the word *realistic* before a picture strongly felt representing a scene of ordinary life, while they apply that of *symbolic* to another picture that is but a cold allegory. (010-115)

Croce's point is that "realistic" and "symbolic" are empty categories that "reveal their philosophical nullity when the attempt is made to develop them in precise definitions" (010-065). The only meaningful distinction in art is between successful expression and failed expression.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The concept of the symbol has deep roots in aesthetic theory. Kant, by "making beauty consist in a symbolism of morality" (038-1643), treated the symbol as a bridge between the sensible and the intelligible -- a position Croce implicitly rejects as intellectualist.

The Hegelian tradition, through Vischer and others, treated symbolic art as a distinct historical phase (Oriental art being the symbolic period), a classification Croce also finds untenable because it substitutes empirical categories for genuine aesthetic judgment.

The symbolic forms "so often mentioned in relation to painting and sculpture" (010-058) are for Croce merely rhetorical categories that resist precise definition.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's contribution is to dissolve the symbol as a separate aesthetic category. Rather than arguing for a particular theory of the symbol, he shows that the concept has no stable theoretical ground: it either collapses into intuition (where it is redundant) or into allegory (where it is an error).

The realistic-symbolic distinction and similar binaries reveal "their philosophical nullity when the attempt is made to develop them in precise definitions, because they either grasp the void or fall into the absurd" (010-065).

Where earlier theorists had seen a spectrum from symbol to allegory to concept, Croce sees a simple binary: all genuine art is symbolic in the sense of being ideal expression, and anything that claims to be symbolic in the sense of veiling a separate intellectual content is not art but a pseudo-aesthetic hybrid.

## Criticism and Debate

Croce's reduction of the symbol to a synonym for intuition is powerful but controversial. By denying that art can bear conceptual meaning in a symbolically structured way, he risks flattening the richness of allegorical and symbolic traditions -- from Dante's allegorical levels to the symbolic systems of Renaissance painting. His own concession about allegory is telling:

> But we must also be just toward the allegorical. Sometimes it is altogether harmless. Given the *Gerusalemme liberata,* the allegory was imagined afterwards; given the *Adone* of Marino, the poet of the lascivious afterwards insinuated that it was written to show how "immoderate indulgence ends in pain"; given a statue of a beautiful woman, the sculptor can attach a label to the statue saying that it represents *Clemency* or *Goodness.* This allegory that arrives attached to a finished work *post festum* does not change the work of art. (005-079)

This admits allegory as an external addition, but leaves unresolved the status of works where symbolic meaning is internal to the artistic conception from the start. The Crocean framework would require such works to be judged on their expressive success alone, with the symbolic content treated as part of the intuition rather than as a separable intellectual layer.

Croce also notes that the complete process of aesthetic production can itself be "symbolized in four stages: *a,* impressions; *b,* expression or spiritual aesthetic synthesis; *c,* hedonistic accompaniment; *d,* translation of the aesthetic fact into physical phenomena" (014-058).

Expression alone, for Croce, is the truly aesthetic moment -- a further demonstration that the symbol, when properly understood, refers not to a dual structure of sign and meaning but to the unitary act of expressive synthesis.
