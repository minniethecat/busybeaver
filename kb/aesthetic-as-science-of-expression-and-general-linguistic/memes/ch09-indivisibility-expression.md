[009-001] In this summary sketch that we have given of the entire philosophy of

[009-002] the spirit in its fundamental moments, the spirit is thus conceived

[009-003] as consisting of four moments or degrees, disposed in such a way that

[009-004] the theoretical activity is to the practical as the first theoretical

[009-005] degree is to the second theoretical, and the first practical degree to

[009-006] the second practical. The four moments imply one another regressively

[009-007] by their concreteness. The concept cannot exist without expression, the

[009-008] useful without both and morality without the three preceding degrees.

[009-009] If the æsthetic fact is in a certain sense alone independent while

[009-010] the others are more or less dependent, then the logical is the least

[009-011] dependent and the moral will the most. Moral intention acts on given

[009-012] theoretic bases, with which it cannot dispense, unless we are willing

[009-013] to accept that absurd procedure known to the Jesuits as *direction of

[009-014] intention,* in which people pretend to themselves not to know what they

[009-015] know only too well.

[009-016] *The forms of genius.*

[009-017] *The system of the spirit.*

[009-018] If the forms of human activity are four, four also are the forms of

[009-019] *genius.* Men endowed with genius in art, in science, and in moral

[009-020] will or heroes, have always been recognized. But the genius of pure

[009-021] economicity has met with repugnance. It is not altogether without

[009-022] reason that a category of bad geniuses or of *geniuses of evil* has

[009-023] been created. The practical, merely economic genius, which is not

[009-024] directed to a rational end, cannot but excite an admiration mingled

[009-025] with alarm. To dispute as to whether the word "genius" should be

[009-026] applied only to creators of æsthetic expression or also to men of

[009-027] scientific research and of action would be a mere question of words. To

[009-028] observe, on the other hand, that "genius," of whatever kind it be, is

[009-029] always a quantitative conception and an empirical distinction, would be

[009-030] to repeat what has already been explained as regards [[genius|artistic genius]].

[009-031] *Non-existence of a fifth form of activity. Law;

[009-032] sociability.*

[009-033] A fifth form of spiritual activity does not exist. It would be easy to

[009-034] show how all the other forms either do not possess the character of

[009-035] activity, or are verbal variants of the activities already examined, or

[009-036] are complex and derivative facts, in which the various activities are

[009-037] mingled, and are filled with particular and contingent contents.

[009-038] The *juridical* fact, for example, considered as what is called

[009-039] objective law, is derived both from the economic and from the logical

[009-040] activities. Law is a rule, a formula (whether oral or written matters

[009-041] little here) in which is fixed an economic relation willed by an

[009-042] individual or by a community, and this economic side at once unites it

[009-043] with and distinguishes it from moral activity. Take another example.

[009-044] Sociology (among the many meanings the word bears in our times) is

[009-045] sometimes conceived as the study of an original element, which is

[009-046] called *sociability.* Now what is it that distinguishes sociability,

[009-047] or the relations which are developed in a meeting of men, and not in a

[009-048] meeting of sub-human beings, if it be not just the various spiritual

[009-049] activities which exist among the former and which are supposed not to

[009-050] exist, or to exist only in a rudimentary degree, among the latter?

[009-051] Sociability, then, far from being an original, simple, irreducible

[009-052] conception, is very complex and complicated. A proof of this would

[009-053] be the impossibility, generally recognized, of enunciating a single

[009-054] law which could be described as purely sociological. Those that are

[009-055] improperly so called are shown to be either empirical historical

[009-056] observations, or spiritual laws, that is to say judgements into which

[009-057] the conceptions of the spiritual activities are translated, when

[009-058] they are not simply empty and indeterminate generalities, like the

[009-059] so-called law of evolution. Sometimes, too, nothing more is understood

[009-060] by "sociability" than "social rule," and so law; thus confounding

[009-061] sociology with the science or theory of law itself. Law, sociability,

[009-062] and similar concepts, are to be dealt with in a mode analogous to that

[009-063] employed by us in the consideration and analysis of historicity and

[009-064] technique.

[009-065] *Religion.*

[009-066] It may seem that *religious* activity should be judged otherwise.

[009-067] But religion is nothing but knowledge, and does not differ from its

[009-068] other forms and sub-forms. For it is in turn either the expression of

[009-069] practical aspirations and ideals (religious ideals), or historical

[009-070] narrative (legend), or conceptual science (dogma).

[009-071] It can therefore be maintained with equal truth either that religion

[009-072] is destroyed by the progress of human knowledge, or that it is always

[009-073] present there. Their religion was the whole intellectual patrimony of

[009-074] primitive peoples: our intellectual patrimony is our religion. The

[009-075] content has been changed, bettered, refined, and it will change and

[009-076] become better and more refined in the future also; but its form is

[009-077] always the same. We do not know what use could be made of religion by

[009-078] those who wish to preserve it side by side with the theoretic activity

[009-079] of man, with his art, with his criticism and with his philosophy. It

[009-080] is impossible to preserve an imperfect and inferior kind of knowledge,

[009-081] such as religion, side by side with what has surpassed and disproved

[009-082] it. Catholicism, which is always consistent, will not tolerate a

[009-083] Science, a History, an Ethics, in contradiction to its views and

[009-084] doctrines. The rationalists are less coherent: they are disposed to

[009-085] allow a little space in their souls for a religion in contradiction

[009-086] with their whole theoretic world.

[009-087] The religious affectations and weaknesses prevalent among the

[009-088] rationalists of our time have their origin in the superstitious worship

[009-089] so recklessly lavished upon the natural sciences. We know ourselves

[009-090] and their chief representatives admit that these sciences are all

[009-091] surrounded by *limits.* Science having been wrongly identified with

[009-092] the so-called natural sciences, it could be foreseen that the remainder

[009-093] would be sought in religion; that remainder with which the human

[009-094] spirit cannot dispense. We are therefore indebted to materialism, to

[009-095] positivism, to naturalism for this unhealthy and often disingenuous

[009-096] recrudescence of religious exaltation, which belongs to the hospital,

[009-097] when it does not belong to the politician.

[009-098] *Metaphysic.*

[009-099] Philosophy removes from religion all reason for existing, because it

[009-100] substitutes itself for religion. As the science of the spirit, it

[009-101] looks upon religion as a phenomenon, a transitory historical fact, a

[009-102] psychic condition that can be surpassed. Philosophy shares the domain

[009-103] of knowledge with the natural sciences, with history and with art.

[009-104] To the first it leaves enumeration, measurement and classification;

[009-105] to the second, the chronicling of what has individually happened; to

[009-106] the third, the individually possible. There is nothing left to allot

[009-107] to religion. For the same reason, philosophy, as the science of the

[009-108] spirit, cannot be philosophy of the intuitive datum; nor, as has

[009-109] been seen, *philosophy of history,* nor *philosophy of nature*; and

[009-110] therefore there cannot be a philosophical science of what is not form

[009-111] and universal, but material and particular. This amounts to affirming

[009-112] the impossibility of *Metaphysic.*

[009-113] The methodology or logic of history has supplanted the philosophy

[009-114] of history; an epistemology of the concepts employed in the natural

[009-115] sciences succeeded the Philosophy of Nature. What philosophy can

[009-116] study of history is its mode of construction (intuition, perception,

[009-117] document, probability, etc.); of the natural sciences the forms of the

[009-118] concepts which constitute them (space, time, motion, number, types,

[009-119] classes, etc.). Philosophy as metaphysic in the sense above described

[009-120] would, on the other hand, claim to compete with history and with the

[009-121] natural sciences, which alone are legitimate and effective in their

[009-122] field. Such a challenge could do nothing but reveal the incompetence

[009-123] of those who made it. In this sense we are *anti-metaphysicans,*

[009-124] while declaring ourselves to be *ultra-metaphysicians,* when the

[009-125] word is used to claim and to affirm the office of philosophy as

[009-126] self-consciousness of the spirit, distinguished from the merely

[009-127] empirical and classificatory office of the natural sciences.

[009-128] *Mental imagination and the intuitive intellect.*

[009-129] Metaphysic has been obliged to assert the existence of a specific

[009-130] spiritual activity producing it, in order to maintain itself side

[009-131] by side with the sciences of the spirit. This activity, called in

[009-132] antiquity *mental or superior imagination,* and more often in modern

[009-133] times *intuitive intellect or intellectual intuition,* was held to

[009-134] unite the characters of imagination and intellect in an altogether

[009-135] special form. It was supposed to provide the means of passing by

[009-136] deduction or dialectic from the infinite to the finite, from form to

[009-137] matter, from the concept to the intuition, from science to history,

[009-138] acting by a method which was held to penetrate both the universal and

[009-139] the particular, the abstract and the concrete, intuition and intellect.

[009-140] A faculty marvellous indeed and most valuable to possess; but we, who

[009-141] do not possess it, have no means of establishing its existence.

[009-142] *Mystical Æsthetic.*

[009-143] Intellectual intuition has sometimes been considered to be the true

[009-144] æsthetic activity. At others a no less marvellous æsthetic activity

[009-145] has been placed beside, below, or above it, a faculty altogether

[009-146] different from simple intuition. The glories of this faculty have been

[009-147] celebrated, and the production of art attributed to it, or at least

[009-148] of certain groups of artistic production, arbitrarily chosen. [[art|Art]],

[009-149] religion and philosophy have seemed in turn to be one only, or three

[009-150] distinct faculties of the spirit, sometimes one, sometimes another of

[009-151] them being supreme in the dignity shared by all.

[009-152] It is impossible to enumerate all the various attitudes assumed or

[009-153] capable of being assumed by this conception of Æsthetic, which we will

[009-154] call *mystical.* We are here in the kingdom, not of the science of

[009-155] imagination, but of imagination itself, which creates its world out

[009-156] of varying elements drawn from impressions and feelings. Suffice it

[009-157] to mention that this mysterious faculty has been conceived, sometimes

[009-158] as practical, sometimes as a mean between the theoretic and the

[009-159] practical, at others again as a theoretic form side by side with

[009-160] philosophy and religion.

[009-161] *Mortality and immortality of art.*

[009-162] The immortality of art has sometimes been deduced from this last

[009-163] conception, as belonging with its sisters to the sphere of absolute

[009-164] spirit. At other times, on the other hand, when religion has been

[009-165] looked upon as mortal and as dissolved in philosophy, then has been

[009-166] proclaimed the mortality, even the death, actual or at least imminent,

[009-167] of art. This question has no meaning for us, because, seeing that the

[009-168] function of art is a necessary degree of the spirit, to ask if art can

[009-169] be eliminated is the same as to ask if sensation or intelligence can be

[009-170] eliminated. But Metaphysic, in the above sense, transplanting itself

[009-171] into an arbitrary world, is not to be criticized in its particulars,

[009-172] any more than we can criticize the botany of the garden of Alcina or

[009-173] the navigation of the voyage of Astolfo. Criticism can only exist when

[009-174] we refuse to join in the game; that is to say, when we reject the very

[009-175] possibility of Metaphysic, always in the sense above indicated.

[009-176] There is therefore no intellectual intuition in philosophy, as there

[009-177] is no surrogate or equivalent of it in art, or any other mode by which

[009-178] this imaginary function may be called and represented. There does not

[009-179] exist (if we may repeat ourselves) a fifth degree, a fifth or supreme

[009-180] faculty, theoretic or practical-theoretic, imaginative-intellectual, or

[009-181] intellectual-imaginative, or however otherwise it may be attempted to

[009-182] conceive such a faculty.
