# Emotion

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic theory, emotion (*emozione*) occupies an ambiguous position: it is the raw affective matter that intuition forms into expression, yet it is not itself an autonomous category of spiritual activity. Emotion belongs to the pre-intuitive, passive domain of sensation and impression — necessary as the material of art, but in itself below the threshold of aesthetic knowledge.

## Definition

Croce classifies emotion within the undifferentiated flux of psychic content that stands beneath intuition. In his taxonomy, what lies beyond the reach of formed expression "are only impressions, sensations, feelings, impulses, emotions, or whatever else one may term what still falls short of the spirit and is not assimilated by man" (002-337). Emotion is therefore matter awaiting form — the content (*contenuto*) that the aesthetic act elaborates into expression. When this content remains unelaborated, Croce calls it "emotionality not aesthetically elaborated" (003-113), emphasizing that emotion achieves spiritual reality only through the formative work of intuition-expression.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Intuition and Expression

Emotion serves as the indispensable material of the aesthetic act, yet it is wholly dependent on intuition for its spiritual existence. The expressive activity "is not added to the fact of the impressions, but these latter are formed and elaborated by it" (003-119–003-120). Emotion without expression is not incomplete art — it is not art at all, nor any kind of knowledge. It is the lower limit posited by the mind to account for the givenness of its content.

This dependence also runs in the other direction: intuition requires emotional matter as that which it forms. Without the passive, affective given, spiritual activity would lack all concreteness and individuality. Matter differentiates one intuition from another; form is constant spiritual activity, while matter — including emotion — is what changes (002-167–002-169).

### Key Distinctions

**Emotion in Art vs. Emotion in Life.** Croce draws a sharp boundary between natural emotional expression and aesthetic expression:

> But there is, in fact, an abyss between a man who is the prey of anger with all its natural manifestations and another man who expresses it aesthetically; between the appearance, the cries and contortions of some one grieving at the loss of a dear one and the words or song with which the same individual portrays his suffering at another time; between the grimace of emotion and the gesture of the actor. (014-043–014-048)

Darwin's study of the expression of the emotions in man and animals, Croce argues, does not belong to aesthetics at all, because naturalistic expression lacks "expression in the spiritual sense" (014-049–014-054). A grimace is not a gesture; the latter is formed, the former merely emitted.

**Emotion vs. Feeling vs. Passion.** Croce inherits and refines the vocabulary of his eighteenth-century predecessors. Home, for instance, confined his analysis to "simple feelings (emotions, not passions)" — those unaccompanied by desires, occupying a middle position between sense-impressions and intellectual ideas (026-103–026-104). For Croce, feeling (*sentimento*) is the broader category; emotion (*emozione*) is the more intense, less differentiated affective state; passion (*passione*) belongs to the practical sphere and is intertwined with volition and desire.

**Aesthetic Emotion vs. Psychological Emotion.** Croce insists that aesthetics must not be "confounded with psychology or asked to describe the emotions awakened by the content of works of art, such as the pathetic or the comic, sadness or joy" (029-124–029-125). The task of aesthetics is to determine the essential character of art and beauty, not to catalogue subjective emotional responses. This distinction separates Croce's philosophical aesthetics from the psychological aesthetics of his time, which sought to explain art by tracing the emotional effects it produced in spectators.

## Historical Context

The concept of emotion entered aesthetic theory through several converging channels. In eighteenth-century France and England, literary theorists increasingly "emphasiz[ed] the concept of 'emotion' in their theories of literature" (022-216), treating feeling and sentiment as the authentic source of poetic value. Figures such as Cartaut de la Villate and Trublet identified poetry with the expression of sentiment, while English critics developed a rich vocabulary of taste, feeling, and emotion (022-212–022-216).

Vico anticipated aspects of this development by asserting that poetry's chief aim was to arouse "strong emotion" in the popular understanding (024-387). Yet Vico's deeper contribution, which Croce endorses, was to recognize that poetry operates through imaginative universals rather than abstract concepts — a view that subordinates emotion to the broader cognitive function of art.

In Croce's own intellectual milieu, Herbart's school had defined aesthetic "content" as "the emotional and intellectual element which falls outside beauty" (030-023), treating emotion as extraneous to the formal essence of the aesthetic. Croce synthesizes these traditions by acknowledging emotion as the material of art while insisting that the aesthetic fact is form and nothing but form. Emotion is what the artist forms, not what the spectator feels.
