# Sophism

In Croce's *Æsthetic*, sophism denotes a specific logical fallacy that confuses the nature of historical understanding, and by extension, aesthetic judgment. It is closely related to the concept of philosophism, which Croce identifies as a characteristic vice of exaggerated Romantic thought.

The critique of sophism runs through Croce's entire methodology, grounding his insistence on the autonomy of aesthetic intuition against conceptual encroachment.

## Definition

Croce introduces sophism in the Author's Preface to expose a widespread error in philosophical method. He describes it as a fallacy that "consists in admitting" a false equivalence between distinct modes of cognition [004-173].

The term thereby obscures the true nature of the subject under examination. It serves as a diagnostic tool for identifying arguments that appear plausible but rest on concealed category errors.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Historical Knowledge

The sophism Croce targets in the Preface concerns the conflation of historical narrative with scientific explanation. He warns against the sophism that treats history as though it were a branch of natural science [004-171].

The error is one of method: subjecting the individual and unrepeatable to the same methods of generalization and prediction. This directly threatens the autonomy of aesthetic intuition, which for Croce is fundamentally historical and individual.

### Philosophism as a Species of Sophism

In his discussion of Romanticism, Croce introduces the term philosophism to designate a particular form of sophism — the tendency to replace artistic expression with philosophical abstraction [034-049]. Philosophism represents "exaggerated Romanticism" in which the artist or critic substitutes conceptual reasoning for the intuitive activity that properly constitutes art [034-050].

> The sophism consists in admitting that history can be reduced to general concepts, thereby denying the individuality that is the very essence of both historical knowledge and artistic intuition. [004-173]

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The critique of sophism has ancient roots in Plato's and Aristotle's rejection of sophistic rhetoric, which privileged persuasion over truth. In modern philosophy, Kant's critique of paralogisms and Hegel's analysis of the sophistry of the Understanding provided precedents.

Croce inherits this tradition but applies it specifically to aesthetic theory, targeting the conflation of art with philosophy.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's distinctive contribution lies in linking sophism to the broader framework of his aesthetic system. By identifying philosophism as a historically specific form of sophism rooted in Romantic aesthetics, Croce provides both a diagnostic category and a prescriptive remedy: the rigorous separation of intuition from concept, expression from communication.
