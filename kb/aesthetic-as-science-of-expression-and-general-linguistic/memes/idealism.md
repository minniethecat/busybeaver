# Idealism

In the history of aesthetics, idealism (*idealismo*) names a family of theories that subordinate art to philosophical truth — the view that beauty is the sensuous appearance of the Idea. Benedetto Croce both inherits and radically transforms this tradition.

## Definition

Aesthetic idealism, as Croce analyzes it in his historical survey, is the doctrine that art expresses metaphysical truth through sensuous form. Its classic formulation is **Hegel**'s: "Art is the sensuous appearance of the Idea." Croce treats this as a partial truth — art does involve the forming of spiritual content — but rejects its subordination of intuition to concept. The aesthetic sphere is autonomous; it does not "point to" philosophical truth any more than philosophy "points to" art.

## In Croce's System

### Idealism as Partial Insight

"Æsthetic idealism" (ch27-aesthetic-idealism) recognizes that art is a spiritual activity, not mere sensation or entertainment. This is its lasting contribution. Idealism correctly denies that beauty is a physical property of objects and insists that aesthetic experience involves the activity of spirit. But it errs in making this activity a vehicle for conceptual truth rather than an autonomous form of cognition.

### Croce's Correction

Croce's own system is a form of idealism — he holds that all reality is spirit and its activities. But Crocean idealism is a **philosophy of distincts**, not a philosophy of reduction. Each form of spirit (intuition, concept, economic, ethical) is autonomous and irreducible. Aesthetic idealism in the Hegelian sense conflates intuition with concept; Croce's idealism of distincts preserves their autonomy.

## Key Distinctions

**Idealism vs Crocean Idealism.** Both hold that art is a spiritual activity. Hegelian idealism sees art as a stage toward philosophy; Crocean idealism sees art as a permanent and autonomous form of spirit. The difference is between a hierarchy (art → religion → philosophy) and a circle of distinct yet equally necessary activities.

## Historical Context

Aesthetic idealism has ancient roots in **Plato**'s theory of beauty as a Form, but its modern form begins with the German Idealists. **F. W. J. Schelling** made art the organon of philosophy; **G. W. F. Hegel** systematized art as the first stage of Absolute Spirit. "F. Schiller" and "the aesthetic sphere as play" (P02-233) developed Kant's aesthetics in an idealist direction. Croce's history traces idealism's evolution and its contest with other traditions: "metaphysical idealism and Baumgartenism" (P02-242), "mysticism in the opponents of idealism — A. Schopenhauer" (P02-246).
