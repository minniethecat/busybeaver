# Grace

In Croce's aesthetic, grace is not a fundamental category but one of the many empirical "modifications" that aestheticians have historically attributed to beauty. Croce treats it as a psychological descriptor of aesthetic reception, not a constituent of aesthetic science.

## Definition

Grace appears in Croce's catalogue of aesthetic pseudo-concepts: the "long, not to say interminable" list that includes "decorous, graceful, attractive, piquant, coquettish, idyllic, elegiac" (013-013). For Croce, these terms describe the psychological effects of artworks, not properties of the aesthetic fact itself. They belong to the empirical psychology of reception, not to the philosophy of art.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Beauty

Croce, following Herbart, holds that predicates like "graceful" tell us nothing about the objectively beautiful. "Such predicates as 'magnificent, charming, graceful' and so forth contain nothing whatever about tones, outlines or thoughts, and therefore tell us nothing about the objectively beautiful in poetry, sculpture, or music" (029-192, 029-194). Grace is a "complexion" that beauty assumes when mingled with extra-aesthetic content (029-179).

### Key Distinctions

1. **Empirical vs. Philosophical** — Grace, like the sublime, comic, and tragic, is an empirical concept derived from psychological observation. It is not a logical category of the spirit. Croce's method demands that such empirical descriptions be excluded from a rigorous aesthetic science.

2. **Form vs. Content** — Grace describes the emotional effect produced by content, not the formal quality of expression. Herbart's insight, which Croce endorses, is that to judge the beautiful, "one must make abstraction from every predicate concerning the content" (029-185).

3. **Grace and the Sublime** — In the dialectical tradition, grace and the sublime are often paired as complementary modifications: Ast spoke of "a feminine, negative element which is the graceful" contrasted with "a masculine, positive element, which is the sublime" (032-365). Jouffroy similarly held that "the sublime and the graceful" are the two species of beauty (033-043).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The concept of grace has a long history in aesthetic theory. Hogarth's "line of grace" and Winckelmann's ideal of "noble simplicity and quiet grandeur" both placed grace at the center of aesthetic value. Schiller wrote dissertations on "the graceful" alongside the sublime, tragic, and pathetic (032-335). For eighteenth-century aesthetics, grace represented the harmonious reconciliation of freedom and necessity in beautiful form.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of grace is deflationary. He absorbs it into his broader critique of aesthetic pseudo-concepts: the entire catalogue of modifications — tragic, comic, sublime, graceful — are empirical descriptions of psychological response, not structural features of the aesthetic fact. This is not to deny that some works are graceful, but to insist that "graceful" describes an effect, not a constitutive aesthetic property.

## Criticism and Debate

> All these things have nothing to do with beauty. In order to discover the objectively beautiful or ugly, one must make abstraction from every predicate concerning the content (029-183, 029-185).

Critics argue that Croce's elimination of grace and other modifications impoverishes aesthetic discourse. If "graceful" is merely psychological, then the entire vocabulary of aesthetic description — elegant, clumsy, fluid, stiff — is relegated to irrelevance. Defenders of Croce respond that this is precisely his point: these terms describe our *response* to art, not art itself, and confusing the two is the fundamental error of psychologistic aesthetics.
