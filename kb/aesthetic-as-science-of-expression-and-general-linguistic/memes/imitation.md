# Imitation

Imitation (*mimesis* in Greek, *imitazione* in Italian) is one of the oldest and most influential concepts in aesthetic theory — the doctrine that art is essentially the imitation of nature. In Croce's aesthetic system, imitation is critically examined: in one sense it is reinterpreted as a legitimate form of spiritual knowledge (representation or intuition); in another, it is rejected as a mechanical and passive duplication that has nothing to do with the artistic process.

## Definition

Croce distinguishes two fundamentally different meanings of the proposition "art is imitation of nature." In the first, scientifically legitimate sense, imitation is understood as **representation or intuition of nature** — a form of knowledge and a spiritual activity:

> One of the scientifically legitimate meanings occurs when "imitation" is understood as representation or intuition of nature, a form of knowledge. And when the phrase is used with this intention, and in order to emphasize the spiritual character of the process, another proposition becomes legitimate also: namely, that art is the *idealization* or *idealizing* imitation of nature. (003-145–003-146)

In the second sense, however, imitation as **mechanical reproduction** — the attempt to produce duplicates of natural objects — is emphatically false:

> But if by imitation of nature be understood that art gives mechanical reproductions, more or less perfect duplicates of natural objects, in the presence of which is renewed the same tumult of impressions as that caused by natural objects, then the proposition is evidently false. The coloured waxen effigies that imitate the life, before which we stand astonished in the museums where such things are shown, do not give æsthetic intuitions. (003-147–003-148)

| Meaning | Croce's Verdict | Domain |
|---------|----------------|--------|
| Imitation as representation/intuition | Legitimate — a form of spiritual knowledge | Aesthetics proper |
| Imitation as idealization | Derivatively legitimate — but reverses the true order of artistic creation | Misplaced emphasis |
| Imitation as mechanical reproduction | False — confuses physical fact with spiritual act | Physics, not aesthetics |
| Illusionism / verism | False — "illusion and hallucination have nothing to do with the calm domain of artistic intuition" | Pseudo-art |

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Imitation theory, when properly understood, belongs to the domain of **intuition** — the first and foundational tier of Croce's fourfold spiritual activity. The artist who "imitates" nature in the legitimate sense is not passively copying but actively forming an expressive representation. This act of forming is identical with [[Intuition]] itself: it is theoretical, individual knowledge (003-143).

The confusion between imitation-as-representation and imitation-as-duplication is, in Croce's framework, a confusion between **Æsthetic** and **Physics** — between the spiritual act of expression and the external, material facts that may (or may not) accompany it. This is one instance of the broader error that Croce identifies as the conflation of the aesthetic fact with the physical fact (ch03-art-philosophy).

### Key Distinctions

**Imitation vs Expression.** Croce inverts the traditional priority: the artist does not proceed from external reality toward an ideal; rather, the artist proceeds from impression to expression — the ideal is prior, and nature is the instrument:

> The artist does not proceed from external reality, in order to modify it by approximating it to the ideal; he goes from the impression of external nature to expression, that is to say, his ideal, and from this passes to the natural fact, which he employs as instrument of reproduction of the ideal fact. (003-... )

**Imitation vs Representation.** Croce sometimes treats "imitation" in its legitimate sense as equivalent to representation — but representation itself is already subsumed under intuition. The distinction between "representing the real" and "imagining the possible" is, for Croce, a distinction introduced by the intellect after the fact; intuition simply forms images without classifying their ontological status.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The theory of imitation originates in Greek antiquity. **Plato**, in the *Republic*, famously condemned art as "imitation of an imitation" — the painter copies a bed that is itself a copy of the ideal Form, standing at three removes from truth (019-...). **Aristotle**, in the *Poetics*, rehabilitated mimesis by treating it as a natural human instinct and a form of cognitive activity:

> Aristotle that mimesis, being proper to man by nature, is contemplation or theoretic activity; although he sometimes seems to forget this (as when he confuses imitation with the case of boys, who acquire their first knowledge by following an example)... (019-...)

During the **Renaissance**, the imitation of nature became a governing principle of artistic theory, often paired with the imitation of classical models. In the **seventeenth and eighteenth centuries**, French academic theory codified the doctrine of *imitation de la belle nature* — the imitation of "beautiful nature" rather than nature as it empirically appears — which already begins to expose the instability of the concept: if only beautiful nature is to be imitated, then some principle of selection beyond mere imitation is already at work.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's decisive contribution is not simply to reject imitation theory but to **reinterpret it** within his own system of spiritual activity. Rather than dismissing the tradition outright, he distinguishes between the scientifically legitimate meaning (imitation as representation, i.e., intuition) and the illegitimate meaning (imitation as mechanical reproduction). This allows him to preserve the ancient insight — that art has a cognitive dimension — while eliminating the confusion that has dogged aesthetic theory for two millennia.

Moreover, Croce's inversion — "nature imitates the artist, and obeys him" — radicalizes the idealist turn begun by Kant and Hegel. The artist is not a passive mirror but an active spirit that imposes expressive form upon the formless matter of sensation.

## Criticism and Debate

> The imitation theory, even in its "idealizing" form, rests on the assumption that the external world provides a norm to which art must conform. Croce's critique goes deeper than simply denying this — he argues that the very notion of a "nature" existing independently of the forming activity of spirit is incoherent within his idealist framework. Nature for Croce is not an independent reality but the "practical construction of the human spirit." Art, as pure intuition-expression, is logically prior to any distinction between real and ideal, natural and artificial.

Critics of Croce's position, however, point out that it risks dissolving the specific historical and material conditions under which art is actually produced — the "models," sketches, and studies that Leonardo and others used were not merely instruments of an already-formed ideal but active participants in the shaping of the final work.
