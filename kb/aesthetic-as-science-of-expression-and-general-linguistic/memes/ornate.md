# Ornate

The category of the ornate is one of the oldest and most persistent in rhetorical and aesthetic theory. It designates a form of expression that is elaborated, decorated, or embellished beyond plain statement. Croce subjects this category to a characteristic critique: the ornate, he argues, is either identical with expression itself or it is nothing at all.

## Definition

The ornate is classically defined as expression that adds something — ornament, decoration, figure — to simple or plain expression. Croce presents this definition in order to dismantle it: the distinction between "simple and ornate, proper and metaphorical" is one of the "rhetorical categories" that have been "introduced into the theory of art" without philosophical justification (010-062).

Croce's objection is logical: "If the ornate consisted of a plus, in what would this plus consist?" (038-279). Since expression is a synthesis of content and form, there is no space between the simple and the ornate into which an "addition" can be inserted. Either the ornate is already present in the original expression, or it is a separate expression added on — in which case it is not ornament but a new aesthetic fact.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

The concept of the ornate belongs, in Croce's classification, to the sphere of rhetoric — the practical doctrine of persuasion and effect. Rhetoric deals with the external manipulation of expressive means for practical ends; aesthetics deals with the internal law of expression itself. To treat the ornate as an aesthetic category is therefore to confuse two distinct orders of spiritual activity.

Croce identifies the "doctrine of ornate form" as a pathology of rhetorical theory (038-079, 038-204). The attempt to classify "many classes of the ornate" — figures of speech, tropes, schemes — rests on the assumption that expression can be decomposed into a base element and an ornamental addition (038-255). This assumption, Croce argues, cannot survive philosophical scrutiny.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a fundamental distinction between the ornate as an aesthetic concept and the ornate as a pseudo-concept. The former would have to identify a specific mode of expression distinct from simple expression; the latter merely registers a vague impression of elaboration that dissolves under analysis.

The distinction between "ornate expression distinct from simple expression" is, for Croce, "altogether inadmissible" (019-025). What we call ornate is simply expression that uses certain linguistic resources — metaphor, periphrasis, amplification — but these resources are not added to an underlying simple statement; they are the expression itself. "There are no two modes of expression, an ornate and a simple one" (010-137); there is only expression, which is always determinate and always complete.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The classification of ornate and plain style has ancient roots in Greek and Roman rhetoric. Theophrastus distinguished three styles — plain, middle, and grand — and the *Rhetorica ad Herennium* developed an elaborate taxonomy of figures. This tradition was transmitted through the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, becoming a standard part of rhetorical education.

In Croce's own intellectual context, the concept of the ornate was associated with the Baroque and with various theories of literary decoration. Croce's teacher, Francesco De Sanctis, had already begun the work of dissolving rhetorical categories into historical description — a project Croce carries forward in his aesthetic theory.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's critique of the ornate is part of his broader campaign against the "rhetorical categories" that have been mistaken for aesthetic concepts. By showing that the distinction between plain and ornate cannot be maintained as a real distinction within expression, Croce clears the ground for an aesthetic theory based on the identity of intuition and expression rather than on the classification of stylistic modes.

The positive result of this critique is the recognition that what we call "ornate" and "plain" are not two kinds of expression but two moments in the history of taste. "The plain and the ornate" are historical descriptions, not aesthetic categories (038-468). The proper philosophical question is not whether a work is ornate or plain, but whether it succeeds or fails as expression.

## Criticism and Debate

> The ornate form which is deprived of imagination is not ornate form at all; it is empty mechanism (038-489).

Critics have argued that Croce's elimination of the ornate as an aesthetic category goes too far. Even if the distinction between plain and ornate cannot be drawn with philosophical precision, it seems to capture something real about the phenomenology of artistic experience. Some works announce themselves as elaborated, others as restrained; this difference, even if historically conditioned, is part of what the work expresses.

Croce's response would be that the phenomenology of reception is not a reliable guide to aesthetic theory. The impression of elaboration or restraint belongs to the psychology of the audience, not to the structure of the work. The aesthetic fact is expression; everything else — including the impression of ornateness — is practical reaction.
