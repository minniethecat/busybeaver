# Ideal Beauty

Ideal beauty (*beau idéal*, *ideale Schönheit*) is the doctrine that true beauty consists not in any particular sensible object but in an ideal type — a perfected form achieved by abstracting from the imperfections of actual things and combining their best features. Croce criticises this doctrine as a confusion between the æsthetic and the conceptual: beauty belongs to individual expression, not to abstract types (026-326).

## Definition

The doctrine of ideal beauty holds that the artist, rather than copying nature as it appears, should "correct" nature by reference to an ideal — a mental image of perfection. "Theorists of ideal beauty" (026-349) from Winckelmann onward taught that the artist must select the best parts from many particulars and combine them into a whole more beautiful than any actual thing. Croce sees this as a theory of artistic production masquerading as a theory of beauty.

## In Croce's System

### The Confusion of Type and Expression

For Croce, beauty is successful expression — the perfect adequacy of the expressive act to the intuition it embodies. It is therefore always individual. The ideal beauty doctrine treats beauty as a universal type, abstracted from individuals — which, in Croce's system, makes it a concept, not an intuition. "The concept of ideal beauty" (026-326) is thus a category error: it treats the æsthetic as though it were logical.

### Dissent from the Doctrine

Croce notes with approval that "dissent from this doctrine of ideal beauty was not lacking" (026-365). Artists and critics have always recognised that the living particular resists reduction to an ideal type. The academic tradition that made ideal beauty the standard of artistic value was, in Croce's view, a pedagogic convenience elevated to a theoretical principle.

### Ideal Beauty and Moral Beauty

In the broader history, ideal beauty was often conflated with moral beauty. Croce traces how "the moral, the last being the true ideal beauty" (033-028) became a common view — partly through Platonic influence — making beauty the sensuous appearance of the good. Croce firmly separates the æsthetic from the moral: beauty is expression, not virtue made visible.

## Historical Context

### Winckelmann and Neoclassicism

Winckelmann's "noble simplicity and quiet grandeur" made ideal beauty the cornerstone of neoclassical aesthetics. The doctrine "ventured to deny the view that ideal beauty is the prerogative of a few forms" (026-401), claiming universal validity. Against this, Croce argues that the fear of "disturbing ideal beauty" (026-403) led to an art that suppressed precisely what makes art alive — individual expressiveness.

### David and the Academic Tradition

The painter David, "in his turn a critic of ideal beauty" (033-014), represents the internal critique within the classical tradition itself. By David's time, ideal beauty had hardened into academic formula — a set of rules for drawing the human figure — and artists who worked from life began to challenge its authority.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to replace the ideal beauty doctrine with a theory of expressive success. Rather than asking whether a work conforms to an ideal type, Croce asks whether it adequately expresses the intuition that generated it. This shifts the criterion of beauty from the abstract to the individual, from the conceptual to the æsthetic.

## Criticism and Debate

> The ideal beauty doctrine, for all its vulnerability to Croce's critique, addressed a real question: why do some works seem to transcend their time and place? Croce's expressionism can explain why a work is adequate to its originating intuition but struggles to explain why some adequate expressions are more compelling than others (026-350).
>
> Later aestheticians have suggested that "ideal" in the classical sense names not a conceptual abstraction but a normativity internal to artistic practice — a standard that artists themselves recognise. Croce's dualism of intuition and concept may oversimplify this.
