# Tradition

Tradition occupies an ambivalent position in Croce's aesthetics. On one hand, tradition is the indispensable vehicle of historical understanding — the accumulated labour of interpretation without which the art of the past would be mute. On the other hand, tradition can degenerate into "blind traditionalism which assumes an intimate character of necessity" (013-005), stifling the spontaneity that is the life of art.

## Definition

Croce defines tradition functionally: "the product of this historical labour is tradition, with the help of which the spirit preserves the human works of the past" (017-274). Tradition is not a mystical force or an external authority; it is the concrete, cumulative result of successive acts of interpretation — "the execution of a piece of music" or the reading of a poem across generations (017-198).

Tradition exists because interpretation is never given once and for all. Each generation must appropriate the art of the past anew, and in doing so, it relies upon — and adds to — the tradition of interpretation. "Without tradition and historical criticism the understanding of works of art becomes uncertain and confused" (018-009).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Tradition belongs to the sphere of historical knowledge — the theoretic activity of the spirit that apprehends the individual and the concrete. It is not itself an act of aesthetic production (which belongs to intuition-expression) but a condition that makes the reception and understanding of past aesthetic productions possible.

Croce is careful to distinguish tradition from the creative act itself. Artistic creation "reappeared by spontaneous generation" or is transmitted "by tradition" (021-003), but the two must not be confused. Tradition preserves the results of past expression; it does not generate new expression. The artist draws upon tradition not as a source of rules but as a storehouse of achieved forms that the new intuition may transform.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp distinction between tradition as living interpretation and tradition as dead formalism. "Where the tradition is broken, interpretation is arbitrary; where it is intact, interpretation is guided" (017-279). But this guidance must remain fluid — the interpreter's task is to "link up again broken traditions" (017-291), not to preserve them as fixed codes.

The danger Croce identifies is "blind traditionalism" — the treatment of traditional forms as binding norms rather than as historical documents. This error is the aesthetic counterpart of the logical error that treats the traditional Logic of the schools as if it were "permanent" and unrevisable (006-216).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The problem of tradition has been central to aesthetic theory since antiquity. The classical doctrine of *imitatio* treated tradition as a repository of models to be emulated. The Romantics, by contrast, celebrated originality and spontaneity, often treating tradition as a constraint to be overthrown.

Croce's treatment of tradition draws upon "the best German tradition" of aesthetic thought (P03-119), which combined historical erudition with philosophical analysis. He acknowledges the contribution of scholars "whose æsthetic traditions" have shaped Italian thought (001-040), while insisting that tradition must always be tested against philosophical truth.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's distinctive contribution is to integrate tradition into his general theory of expression without granting it normative authority over artistic production. Tradition is a historical fact, not an aesthetic criterion. The question "Is this work traditional?" is historical; the question "Is this work beautiful?" is aesthetic. The two must not be conflated.

This position allows Croce to maintain the Romantic emphasis on spontaneity and originality while acknowledging the real role that tradition plays in the transmission and interpretation of art. "Tradition or historical criticism" does not determine whether a work succeeds as expression, but it determines whether we can understand what the work expresses (018-009).

## Criticism and Debate

> The mystical tradition is refreshed and transformed by contact with the new philosophy, but it does not thereby become that philosophy (021-134).

Critics have questioned whether Croce's treatment of tradition adequately accounts for the normative force that traditions actually exert in artistic practice. If tradition is merely a "help" to interpretation, why do artists so often experience it as a burden or a constraint? Croce's answer is that this experience of constraint is precisely the sign that tradition has degenerated into blind traditionalism — the substitution of historical precedent for aesthetic judgment.

A related criticism concerns the apparent circularity of Croce's account: tradition enables interpretation, but interpretation constitutes tradition. Croce would accept this circularity as the mark of a genuine dialectical relation, not a logical defect.
