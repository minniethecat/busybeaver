# Wit

In Croce's historical survey of aesthetic ideas, wit (*ingegno*) emerges as a key seventeenth-century term that designates a faculty of inventive or artistic association distinct from pure intellect — a precursor to the modern concept of genius and a term intimately intertwined with taste, imagination, and feeling.

## Definition

Croce treats wit not as a fully-formed concept in his own systematic philosophy, but rather as a historical symptom — a word whose career in the seventeenth century reveals the growing awareness of an autonomous aesthetic realm. Wit, in Croce's account, "differed somewhat from intellect" (022-009). It had its roots in the ancient rhetorical tradition, where it was conceived as a "suave and facile mode of knowledge, as opposed to the severity of Dialectic; an 'Antistrophe to Dialectic'" (022-011). In the hands of seventeenth-century theorists, wit came to designate the faculty that "seeks to find and create the beautiful and the efficacious" (022-027), and was eventually elevated to the status of the strictly inventive or artistic faculty — what would later be called "genius" (022-040).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Aesthetic Concepts

For Croce, wit belongs to the constellation of pre-scientific terms — along with taste, imagination, and feeling — that seventeenth- and eighteenth-century writers used to grope toward the concept of aesthetic autonomy without yet possessing a rigorous theoretical framework. These terms tended to fuse into one another: "imagination was often identified with wit, wit with taste, taste with feeling, and feeling with first apprehensions or imagination" (022-218). Croce's own judgment is characteristically sharp:

> [T]aste, wit, imagination, feeling, and so on, instead of new concepts scientifically grasped, were simply new words corresponding to vague impressions: at most they were problems, not concepts: apprehensions of ground still to be conquered, not yet annexed and brought into subjection. (022-233)

### Wit and Intellect

The central distinction Croce examines is that between wit and intellect. Seventeenth-century theorists such as Pellegrini and Tesauro "never fail to point out that intellectual truth lies at the root of wit" (022-244). Trevisano defined wit as "an internal virtue of the soul which invents methods for expressing and executing its own concepts" (022-247), while Muratori called it "that virtue and active force with which the intellect is able to assemble, unite and discover the similarities, relations and reasons of things" (022-254). In every case, wit, after having been distinguished from intellect, eventually becomes a part or manifestation of it — an oscillation that Croce sees as characteristic of the pre-critical period. Alexander Pope captured this tension in verse:

> For wit and judgement often are at strife,
> Though meant each other's aid like man and wife. (022-261)

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The origins of the concept of wit lie in classical rhetoric. Aristotle "in his Rhetoric writes at considerable length of wit" (032-278), and Cicero's *De oratore* and Quintilian's *Institutions* each devote sections to wit and the comic (032-279). This line of thinking was revived in the seventeenth century by theorists such as Matteo Pellegrini, who dedicated a treatise (*Argutezza*, 1639) to the subject, and Emmanuele Tesauro, whose *Cannochiale Aristotelico* (1654) explored wit across verbal, symbolic, and figurative domains (022-032).

The Spaniard Baltasar Gracian (1642) gave the concept its most influential formulation, making wit the strictly inventive or artistic faculty — synonymous with genius (022-040). Mario Pagano later wrote: "Wit may be taken as equivalent to the *genie* of the French, a word now commonly used in Italy" (022-043). In England, Locke adopted the distinction between wit and judgement, defining wit as that which "combines ideas with pleasing variety, discovering their similarities and relations and thus grouping them into beautiful pictures" (023-067).

### Croce's Analysis

Croce's distinctive contribution is to see wit not as a stable concept but as a symptom — a word that indicates "the obscure world of wit, taste, imagination, feeling and the *je ne sais quoi*" (023-003) that Cartesian intellectualism could not accommodate. Leibniz's philosophy, with its law of continuity, "found ample room for shelter" for wit and its cognates (023-098). Yet even within this more hospitable framework, the concept remained unstable. Croce argues that Baumgarten's school, while giving wit a place in the taxonomy of aesthetic faculties — listing it among the modes of "beauty of sense-apprehension (aesthetic richness, grandeur, verisimilitude, vivacity, certainty, sensitive life and wit)" (025-232) — never succeeded in freeing it from intellectualistic assumptions.

For Vico, wit was "the father of all invention," referring to imagination and memory (024-289), yet Vico himself was not always consistent about how far "sensation," "memory," "imagination," and "wit" were synonymous or different (024-404).

## Criticism and Debate

Croce's ultimate verdict on wit is that it belongs to a class of terms that mark an unresolved problem rather than a solution. Without a clear theory of intuition and expression — the foundation of his own aesthetic system — every attempt to define wit falls into the same trap:

> [T]he pathless labyrinth of the "probable" which is and is not false, of the wit which is and is not intellect, of the taste which is and is not intellectual judgement, of the imagination and feeling which are and are not sensibility and material pleasure. (023-328)

Only when aesthetic activity is recognized as a distinct, autonomous form of spiritual activity — intuitive knowledge independent of intellectual knowledge — does the unstable oscillation between wit and intellect, taste and judgement, resolve itself. In this sense, Croce treats the history of the concept of wit as a prehistory of his own aesthetic theory: a long series of failed attempts to express a truth whose proper formulation awaited a rigorous philosophy of expression.
