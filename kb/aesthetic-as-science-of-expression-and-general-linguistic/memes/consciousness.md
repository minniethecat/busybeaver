# Consciousness

The faculty or state through which aesthetic intuitions are objectified and rendered knowable; in Croce's philosophy of spirit, consciousness ranges from immediate sensory awareness to the reflective self-consciousness that distinguishes artistic genius from mere feeling (P02-196, 009-126).

## Definition

Croce treats consciousness as the medium in which intuitions take form and become accessible to reflective thought. Intuitions are "objectified in consciousness" (002-115), meaning that the raw material of sensation becomes knowable only through the act of conscious intuition. What is not raised to consciousness remains at the level of formless impression. This view ties consciousness intimately to the aesthetic moment: aesthetic activity is the primary form of conscious knowing, prior to conceptual thought.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Within the fourfold structure of spirit, consciousness operates at multiple levels. At the theoretical level, sensory consciousness constitutes the domain of aesthetics — "the science of sensory consciousness" (P02-196) — distinct from intellectual or conceptual knowledge. At the practical level, moral consciousness emerges as a higher grade of awareness, wherein the individual becomes aware of the contradiction between what is and what ought to be (008-111, 008-126). Croce describes self-consciousness as the highest integration: "the self-consciousness of the spirit" (009-126), the point at which spirit grasps itself as the unity of all its distinct forms.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws several critical distinctions regarding consciousness. He separates **artistic consciousness** from both feeling and religion: artistic production requires raising feeling to the level of conscious expression, yet it is not reducible to religious or conceptual consciousness (P02-257). He further distinguishes **reflective consciousness** from the primary aesthetic intuition: the former is "the superadded consciousness of the historian or critic" (003-104), a secondary act of judgment applied to the primary aesthetic fact. The "distinctive consciousness of the beautiful and of the ugly" (011-189) develops gradually, becoming a refined faculty through repeated aesthetic experience. What is repugnant to "our aesthetic consciousness" (015-010) is perceived as ugly — a direct, pre-conceptual judgment.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The concept of consciousness in aesthetics traces through German Idealism, where Kant distinguished empirical from transcendental apperception, and Hegel developed the phenomenology of spirit as the progressive self-consciousness of Geist. Late nineteenth-century psychological aesthetics — represented by figures such as Fechner — treated consciousness empirically, as a measurable domain of sensory experience, a position Croce explicitly critiqued.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's distinctive contribution was to situate consciousness within his systematic philosophy of spirit, rejecting both the empirical-psychological reduction of consciousness and the transcendental-metaphysical inflation of it. For Croce, consciousness is neither a passive receptor (empiricism) nor a purely logical self-relation (Hegelianism), but the active, expressive medium of spirit's self-realization. Artistic genius, for Croce, "is the reflective consciousness" (003-103) — not an inexplicable gift but the capacity to bring intuition to full conscious clarity.

## Criticism and Debate

> The claim that all aesthetic activity is conscious has drawn criticism from those who emphasize the role of the unconscious or pre-conscious in artistic creation. Croce's own response is that "those who claim unconsciousness as the essence of art confuse art with mere sensation or physiological process" (003-098). The debate touches the fundamental Crocean commitment to the cognitive character of art.
