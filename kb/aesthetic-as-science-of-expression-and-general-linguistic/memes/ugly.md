# Ugly

In Croce's aesthetic theory, the ugly (*il brutto*) is not a positive quality or a contrary aesthetic value opposed to beauty. It is a privation — an expression that has failed to fully realize its formative aim. Beauty and ugliness are not symmetrical opposites but success and failure of the same activity: intuition-expression.

## Definition

Croce defines the ugly as "unsuccessful expression" (011-167). Where beauty succeeds in adequately forming intuitive content into coherent expression, the ugly marks a breakdown in this process. The ugly is expression that has not achieved its own proper end. The core aesthetic fact is expression, and this fact admits of degrees — it can be more or less successful, and failure is what we designate as ugliness.

The ugly is not the absence of expression entirely, for such a thing would not enter the aesthetic sphere at all. As Croce puts it, "if the ugly were complete, that is to say, without any element of beauty, it would for that very reason cease to be ugly, because it would be without the contradiction in which is the reason of its existence" (011-185). Ugliness is always relative: a partial failure against a partial success.

## In Croce's System

### Beauty as Unity, Ugly as Multiplicity

Croce adopts and transforms a traditional philosophical pairing. "Beauty is unity, the ugly as multiplicity" (011-169). A successful expression integrates diverse intuitive elements into a coherent whole; an unsuccessful one leaves them in unresolved tension. The ugly work of art is one in which the formative impulse has not achieved synthesis — the parts remain disjunct, the expression incomplete.

### Degrees of Ugliness

Ugliness admits of degrees. Croce speaks of "the rather ugly (or almost beautiful) to the extremely ugly" (011-182). This gradation confirms the non-absolute character of the distinction: the aesthetic fact is always a mixture, and what we classify as ugly is simply that in which the unsuccessful element predominates.

### The Unsuccessful in Art

When artists succeed, their works are beautiful; "when they are unsuccessful, ugly, false, bad" (011-146). The ugly in art is therefore a contingent matter of execution, not a separate aesthetic category. It belongs to the artist's failure, not to a realm of negative aesthetic value existing independently.

## Historical Context

The problem of the ugly has occupied aesthetic thought since antiquity. Plato banished ugliness from the ideal realm; Aristotle accommodated it within the theory of *mimesis*, arguing that skillful imitation could make even the ugly pleasurable. In the nineteenth century, **Solger**, **Weisse**, and **Rosenkranz** developed elaborate theories of the ugly in art, treating it as a positive aesthetic principle with its own laws and justification (P02-280). Croce's contribution is to dissolve rather than solve the problem: the ugly is not a category of aesthetic value but a measure of expressive failure. It needs no special theory because it is nothing other than incomplete beauty.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's reduction of the ugly to expressive failure has been challenged by those who see ugliness as a genuine aesthetic value in modernist and avant-garde art. If certain works deliberately cultivate dissonance and fragmentation as expressive means, are they merely "unsuccessful"? Croce's system would answer that such works, if they achieve their intended expression, are beautiful regardless of their content — the ugly is always an unintended failure, never a stylistic programme. The debate turns on whether expression can be judged by formal adequacy alone, or must also account for the quality of what is expressed.
