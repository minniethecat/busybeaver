# Mythology

Mythology, as treated in Croce's Aesthetic, denotes the philosophical interpretation of myth as a spontaneous mode of imaginative knowledge rather than a collection of arbitrary fictions. In surveying the history of aesthetic thought, Croce traces how the concept of mythology underwent radical transformation -- from a dismissible body of ancient fables to a serious philosophical category intimately linked with the origin of poetry, language, and art.

## Definition

Croce treats mythology not as a fixed canon of stories but as a category of aesthetic speculation. Through his exposition of Vico, Croce presents mythology as a spontaneous expression of primitive consciousness: "an entirely new insight into mythology: it is no longer an arbitrary calculated invention, but a spontaneous vision of truth as it presented itself to the spirit of primitive man" (024-141). This definition displaces the Enlightenment view of myth as deliberate fabrication, recasting it as a necessary phase in the development of the human spirit. In the idealist tradition culminating in Schelling, mythology becomes a philosophical demand: the Absolute, having been severed from poetic intuition by the progress of reflection, must be reunited with it through a new mythology born of the new philosophy (028-313).

## In Croce's System

### Mythology and Art

In Croce's historical survey, mythology occupies a transitional position between pure intuition and conceptual thought. Vico's *Scienza Nuova* demonstrates that poetry and mythology are originally identical -- both arise from the imaginative faculty that precedes logical abstraction (024-348). The Romantic and idealist thinkers, particularly Schelling, radicalize this insight by declaring that mythology is not merely a precursor to art but its essential condition. In Schelling's system, art represents the Absolute in the form of ideas, and those ideas, considered from the standpoint of reality, are themselves gods; thus every genuine artistic idea participates in a mythological framework. The individual forms of art, being representatives of the infinite, "are called Ideas. Considered from the point of view of reality, Ideas are gods" (028-358).

### Key Distinctions

Croce, through his exposition of Schelling, draws a crucial distinction between mythology and allegory. In allegory, "the particular signifies only the universal," whereas in mythology the particular "is already itself the universal" (028-387). This distinction is foundational: it explains why allegorical readings of Homer succeed -- the poems already embody their universal content in concrete mythical form. It also illuminates why a purely allegorical approach to art, as found in certain strands of medieval and rationalist aesthetics, misses the essential nature of aesthetic expression.

A second distinction separates mythology from religion. While both engage the transcendent, mythology in the aesthetic tradition is a product of the imagination -- a faculty Croce distinguishes sharply from pure intellect. Mythology and art share a common origin in intuitive expression, whereas religion in its doctrinal form belongs to the sphere of conceptual thought. As Croce notes, the boundary between these domains is fluid in the idealist program: "The line between mythology and art is as shadowy as that between art and philosophy" (028-394).

> Mythology is proclaimed a necessary condition of all art: mythology which is not allegory, for in the latter the particular signifies only the universal, while the former is already itself the universal; which explains how easy it is to allegorize, and how fascinating are such poems as those of Homer which lend themselves to such interpretations. Christian, as well as Hellenic, art has its mythology: Christ; the persons of the Trinity; the Virgin mother of God. (028-387--028-392)

## Historical Context

The philosophical elevation of mythology from antiquarian curiosity to aesthetic principle begins with Giambattista Vico. In the *Scienza Nuova*, Vico discovered that the earliest peoples thought not in concepts but in "poetic characters" -- imaginative universals that constitute the substance of myth. Croce reports that Vico, in his *Autobiography*, "boasts of having discovered other principles of poetry than those found by Greeks and Latins and all others from those times down to the present day; on these are founded other views on mythology" (024-225). Vico's larger project of a "system of civilization, of the Republic, of laws, of poetry, of history, in a word, of humanity at large" was founded upon this discovery of the creative imagination, encompassing mythology alongside language, writing, and symbolic representation (024-348).

The German idealist tradition transformed Vico's historical insight into a metaphysical program. Schelling's philosophy culminates in the prophecy that philosophy will one day return to poetry, "and from the new philosophy a new mythology will arise" (028-313). This mythological turn was not without its critics: Croce notes that some philosophers "speculated on language and mythology," with Schelling himself "considering them to be the products of a pre-human consciousness" (031-037). Later, in the hands of epigoni, the mythological principle degenerated into what Croce dismissively terms an "artificial mythology" -- a hollow schematism that reduced aesthetic categories to personified abstractions, composed "without the least imagination or literary skill" (032-455).

Yet the legacy of mythological thinking in aesthetics is profound. By demonstrating that myth is not falsehood but a distinctive mode of truth -- imaginative rather than logical, concrete rather than abstract -- Vico and the idealists secured for mythology a permanent place in the philosophy of art.
