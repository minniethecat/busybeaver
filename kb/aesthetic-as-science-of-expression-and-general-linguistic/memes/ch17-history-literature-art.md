[017-001] *Æsthetic judgement. Its identity with æsthetic

[017-002] reproduction.*

[017-003] When the entire æsthetic and externalizing process has been completed,

[017-004] when a beautiful expression has been produced and it has been fixed

[017-005] in a definite physical material, what is meant by *judging ill To

[017-006] reproduce it in oneself,* answer the critics of art, almost with one

[017-007] voice. Very good. Let us try thoroughly to understand this fact, and

[017-008] with that object in view, let us represent it schematically.

[017-009] The individual A is seeking the expression of an impression which

[017-010] he feels or anticipates, but has not yet expressed. See him trying

[017-011] various words and phrases which may give the sought-for expression,

[017-012] that expression which must exist, but which he does not possess. He

[017-013] tries the combination *m,* but rejects it as unsuitable, inexpressive,

[017-014] incomplete, ugly: he tries the combination *n,* with a like result.

[017-015] *He does not see at all, or does not see clearly.* The expression

[017-016] still eludes him. After other vain attempts, during which he sometimes

[017-017] approaches, sometimes retreats from the mark at which he aims, all of a

[017-018] sudden (almost as though formed spontaneously of itself) he forms the

[017-019] sought-for expression, and *lux facta est.* He enjoys for an instant

[017-020] æsthetic pleasure or the pleasure of [[beauty|the beautiful]]. The ugly, with

[017-021] its correlative displeasure, was the æsthetic activity which had not

[017-022] succeeded in conquering the obstacle; the beautiful is the expressive

[017-023] activity which now displays itself triumphant.

[017-024] We have taken this example from the domain of speech, as being nearer

[017-025] and more accessible, and because we all talk, though we do not all draw

[017-026] or paint. Now if another individual, whom we shall call B, is to judge

[017-027] that expression and decide whether it be beautiful or ugly, he *must of

[017-028] necessity place himself at A's point of view,* and go through the whole

[017-029] process again, with the help of the physical sign supplied to him by A.

[017-030] If A has seen clearly, then B (who has placed himself at A's point of

[017-031] view) will also see clearly and will see this expression as beautiful.

[017-032] If A has not seen clearly, then B also will not see clearly, and will

[017-033] find the expression more or less ugly, *just as A did.*

[017-034] *Impossibility of divergences.*

[017-035] It may be observed that we have not taken into consideration two other

[017-036] cases: that of A having a clear and B an obscure vision; and that of A

[017-037] having an obscure and B a clear vision. Strictly speaking, these two

[017-038] cases are *impossible.*

[017-039] Expressive activity, just because it is activity, is not caprice, but

[017-040] spiritual necessity; it cannot solve a definite æsthetic problem save

[017-041] in one way, which is the right way. It will be objected to this plain

[017-042] statement that works which seem beautiful to the artists are afterwards

[017-043] found to be ugly by the critics; while other works with which the

[017-044] artists were discontented and held to be imperfect or failures are, on

[017-045] the contrary, held to be beautiful and perfect by the critics. But in

[017-046] this case, one of the two is wrong: either the critics or the artists,

[017-047] sometimes the artists, at other times the critics. Indeed, the producer

[017-048] of an expression does not always fully realize what is happening in

[017-049] his soul. Haste, vanity, want of reflexion, theoretic prejudices,

[017-050] make people say, and others sometimes almost believe, that works of

[017-051] ours are beautiful, which, if we really looked into ourselves, we

[017-052] should see to be ugly, as they are in reality. Thus poor Don Quixote,

[017-053] when he had reattached to his helmet as well as he could the vizor of

[017-054] cardboard—the vizor that had showed itself to possess but the feeblest

[017-055] force of resistance at the first encounter,—took good care not to test

[017-056] it again with a well-delivered sword-thrust, but simply declared and

[017-057] maintained it to be (says the author) *por celada finisima de encaxe.*

[017-058] And in other cases, the same reasons, or opposite but analogous

[017-059] ones, trouble the consciousness of the artist, and cause him to value

[017-060] badly what he has successfully produced, or to strive to undo! and do

[017-061] again for the worse what he has done well in artistic spontaneity.

[017-062] An instance of this is Tasso and his passage from the *Gerusalemme

[017-063] liberata* to the *Gerusalemme conquistata.* In the same way, haste,

[017-064] laziness, want of reflexion, theoretic prejudices, personal sympathies

[017-065] or animosities, and other motives of a similar sort, sometimes cause

[017-066] the critics to proclaim ugly what is beautiful, and beautiful what is

[017-067] ugly. Were they to eliminate such disturbing elements, they would feel

[017-068] the [[art|work of art]] as it really is, and would not leave it to posterity,

[017-069] that more diligent and more dispassionate judge, to award the palm, or

[017-070] to do that justice which they have refused.

[017-071] *Identity of taste and genius.*

[017-072] It is clear from the preceding theorem that the activity of judgement

[017-073] which criticizes and recognizes the beautiful is identical with

[017-074] what produces it. The only difference lies in the diversity of

[017-075] circumstances, since in the one case it is a question of æsthetic

[017-076] production, in the other of reproduction. The activity which judges is

[017-077] called *taste*; the productive activity is called *genius*: genius and

[017-078] taste are therefore substantially *identical.*

[017-079] The common remark that the critic should possess something of the

[017-080] genius of the artist and that the artist should possess taste, gives

[017-081] a glimpse of this identity; or the remark that there exists an active

[017-082] (productive) and a passive (reproductive) taste. But it is also

[017-083] negated in other equally common remarks, as when people speak of taste

[017-084] without genius, or of genius without taste. These last observations

[017-085] are meaningless, unless they allude to quantitative or psychological

[017-086] differences, those being called geniuses without taste who produce

[017-087] works of art, inspired in their chief parts and neglected or defective

[017-088] in their secondary parts, and men of taste without genius, those

[017-089] who, while they succeed in obtaining certain isolated or secondary

[017-090] merits, do not possess sufficient power for a great artistic

[017-091] synthesis. Analogous explanations can easily be given of other similar

[017-092] expressions. But to posit a substantial difference between genius and

[017-093] taste, between artistic production and reproduction, would render both

[017-094] communication and judgement alike inconceivable. How could we judge

[017-095] what remained external to us? How could that which is produced by a

[017-096] given activity be judged by a *different* activity? The critic may

[017-097] be a small genius, the artist a great one; the former may have the

[017-098] strength of ten, the latter of a hundred; the former, in order to reach

[017-099] a certain height, will have need of the assistance of the other; but

[017-100] the nature of both must remain the same. To judge Dante, we must raise

[017-101] ourselves to his level: let it be well understood that empirically we

[017-102] are not Dante, nor Dante we; but in that moment of contemplation and

[017-103] judgement, our spirit is one with that of the poet, and in that moment

[017-104] we and he are one thing. In this identity alone resides the possibility

[017-105] that our little souls can echo great souls, and grow great with them in

[017-106] the universality of the spirit.

[017-107] *Analogy with other activities.*

[017-108] Let us remark in passing that what has been said of the æsthetic

[017-109] judgement holds good equally for every other activity and for every

[017-110] other judgement; and that scientific, economic, and ethical criticism

[017-111] is effected in a like manner. To limit ourselves to this last, only if

[017-112] we place ourselves ideally in the same conditions in which he found

[017-113] himself who took a given resolution, can we form a judgement as to

[017-114] whether his decision were moral or immoral. An action would otherwise

[017-115] remain incomprehensible and therefore impossible to judge. A homicide

[017-116] may be a rascal or a hero: if this be, within limits, indifferent

[017-117] as regards the defence of society, which condemns both to the same

[017-118] punishment, it is not indifferent to one who wishes to distinguish and

[017-119] judge from the moral point of view, and we therefore cannot dispense

[017-120] with reconstructing the individual psychology of the homicide, in order

[017-121] to determine the true nature of his deed, not merely in its legal,

[017-122] but also in its moral aspect. In Ethics, a moral taste or tact is

[017-123] sometimes mentioned, answering to what is generally called the moral

[017-124] consciousness, that is to say, to the activity of the good will itself.

[017-125] *Criticism of æsthetic absolutism (intellectualism) and

[017-126] relativism.*

[017-127] The explanation above given of æsthetic judgement or reproduction both

[017-128] agrees with and condemns the absolutists and relativists, those who

[017-129] affirm and those who deny the absoluteness of taste.

[017-130] In affirming that the beautiful can be judged, the absolutists are

[017-131] right; but the theory on which they found their affirmation is not

[017-132] tenable, because they conceive of the beautiful, that is, æsthetic

[017-133] value, as something placed outside the æsthetic activity, as a concept

[017-134] or a model which an artist realizes in his work, and of which the

[017-135] critic avails himself afterwards in judging the work itself. These

[017-136] concepts and models have no existence in art, for when proclaiming

[017-137] that every art can be judged only in itself and that it has its model

[017-138] in itself, they implicitly denied the existence of objective models of

[017-139] beauty, whether these are intellectual concepts, or ideas suspended in

[017-140] a metaphysical heaven.

[017-141] In proclaiming this, their-adversaries, the relativists, are perfectly

[017-142] right, and effect an advance upon them. However, the initial

[017-143] rationality of their thesis in its turn becomes converted into a false

[017-144] theory. Repeating the ancient adage that there is no accounting for

[017-145] tastes, they believe that æsthetic expression is of the same nature as

[017-146] the pleasant and the unpleasant, which every one feels in his own way,

[017-147] and about which there is no dispute. But we know that the pleasant and

[017-148] the unpleasant are utilitarian, practical facts. Thus the relativists

[017-149] deny the specific character of the æsthetic fact, and again confound

[017-150] expression with impression, the theoretic with the practical.

[017-151] The true solution lies in rejecting alike relativism or psychologism

[017-152] and false absolutism; and in recognizing that the criterion of taste is

[017-153] absolute, but absolute in a different way from that of the intellect,

[017-154] which expresses itself in ratiocination. The criterion of taste is

[017-155] absolute, with the intuitive absoluteness of the imagination. Thus any

[017-156] act of expressive activity, which is so really, is to be recognized

[017-157] as beautiful, and any fact as ugly in which expressive activity and

[017-158] passivity are found engaged with one another in an unfinished struggle.

[017-159] *Criticism of relative relativism.*

[017-160] Between absolutists and relativists is a third class, which may be

[017-161] called that of the relative relativists. These affirm the existence of

[017-162] absolute values in other fields, such as Logic and Ethic, but deny it

[017-163] in the field of Æsthetic. To dispute about science or morals seems to

[017-164] them to be rational and justifiable, because science depends upon the

[017-165] universal, common to all men, and morality upon duty, which is also

[017-166] a law of human nature; but how dispute about art, which depends upon

[017-167] imagination? Not only, however, is the imaginative activity universal

[017-168] and no less inherent in human nature than the logical concept and

[017-169] practical duty; but there is a preliminary objection to the thesis in

[017-170] question. If the absoluteness of the imagination be denied, we must

[017-171] also deny intellectual or conceptual truth and implicitly morality.

[017-172] Does not morality presuppose logical distinctions? How could these be

[017-173] known, otherwise than in expressions and words, that is to say, in

[017-174] imaginative form? If the absoluteness of the imagination were removed,

[017-175] the life of the spirit would tremble to its foundations. One individual

[017-176] would no longer understand another, nor indeed his own self of a moment

[017-177] before, which is already another individual considered a moment after.

[017-178] *Objection founded on the variation of the stimulus and of

[017-179] psychic disposition.*

[017-180] Nevertheless, variety of judgements is an indubitable fact. Men

[017-181] disagree as to logical, ethical, and economical valuations; and they

[017-182] disagree equally or even more as to the æsthetic. If certain reasons

[017-183] recorded by us above, such as haste, prejudices, passions, etc., may

[017-184] lessen the importance of this disagreement, they do not on that account

[017-185] annul it. When speaking of the stimuli of reproduction we have added a

[017-186] caution, for we said that reproduction takes place, *if all the other

[017-187] conditions remain equal.* Do they remain equal? Does the hypothesis

[017-188] correspond to reality?

[017-189] It would appear not. In order to reproduce an impression several times

[017-190] by means of a suitable physical stimulus it is necessary that this

[017-191] stimulus be not changed, and that the organism remain in the same

[017-192] psychical conditions as those in which was experienced the impression

[017-193] that it is desired to reproduce. Now it is a fact that the physical

[017-194] stimulus is continually changing, and in like manner the psychological

[017-195] conditions.

[017-196] Oil-paintings grow dark, frescoes fade, statues lose noses, hands

[017-197] and legs, architecture becomes totally or partially a ruin, the

[017-198] tradition of the execution of a piece of music is lost, the text of a

[017-199] poem is corrupted by bad copyists or bad printing. These are obvious

[017-200] instances of I the changes which daily occur in objects or physical

[017-201] stimuli. As regards psychological conditions, we will not dwell upon

[017-202] the cases of deafness or blindness, that is to say, upon the loss of

[017-203] entire orders of psychical impressions; these cases are secondary and

[017-204] of less importance compared with the fundamental, daily, inevitable

[017-205] and perpetual changes of the society around us and of the internal

[017-206] conditions of our individual life. The phonetic manifestations or

[017-207] words and verses of Dante's *Commedia* must produce a very different

[017-208] impression on an Italian citizen engaged in the politics of the

[017-209] third Rome, from that experienced by a well-informed and intimate

[017-210] contemporary of the poet. The Madonna of Cimabue is still in the Church

[017-211] of Santa Maria Novella; but does she speak to the visitor of to-day as

[017-212] to the Florentines of the thirteenth century? Even though she were not

[017-213] also darkened by time, must we not suppose that the impression which

[017-214] she now produces is altogether different from that of former times? And

[017-215] even in the case of the same individual poet, will a poem composed by

[017-216] him in youth make the same impression upon him when he re-reads it in

[017-217] his old age, with psychic conditions altogether changed?

[017-218] *Criticism of the distinction of signs into natural and

[017-219] conventional.*

[017-220] It is true that certain æstheticians have attempted a distinction

[017-221] between stimuli and stimuli, between *natural* and *conventional*

[017-222] signs. The former are held to have a constant effect upon all; the

[017-223] latter only upon a limited circle. In their belief, signs employed

[017-224] in painting are natural, those used in poetry conventional. But the

[017-225] difference between them is at the most only one of degree. It has

[017-226] often been said that painting is a language understood by all, while

[017-227] with poetry it is otherwise. Here, for example, Leonardo found one

[017-228] of the prerogatives of his art, "which hath not need of interpreters

[017-229] of different tongues as have letters," and it pleases man and beast.

[017-230] He relates the anecdote of that portrait of the father of a family

[017-231] "which the little grandchildren were wont to caress while they were

[017-232] still in swaddling-clothes, and the dogs and cats of the house in like

[017-233] manner." But other anecdotes, such as those of the savages who took the

[017-234] portrait of a soldier for a boat, or considered the portrait of a man

[017-235] on horseback to be furnished with only one leg, are apt to shake one's

[017-236] faith in the understanding of painting by sucklings, dogs and cats.

[017-237] Fortunately, no arduous researches are necessary to convince oneself

[017-238] that pictures, poetry and all works of art only produce effects upon

[017-239] souls prepared to receive them. Natural signs do not exist; because

[017-240] all are equally conventional, or, to speak with greater exactness,

[017-241] *historically conditioned.*

[017-242] *The surmounting of variety.*

[017-243] Granting this, how are we to succeed in causing the expression to be

[017-244] reproduced by means of the physical object? How obtain the same effect,

[017-245] when the conditions are no longer the same? Would it not, rather, seem

[017-246] necessary to conclude that expressions cannot be reproduced, despite

[017-247] the physical instruments made for the purpose, and that what is called

[017-248] reproduction consists in ever new expressions? Such would indeed be the

[017-249] conclusion if the varieties of physical and psychical conditions were

[017-250] intrinsically insurmountable. But since the insuperability has none

[017-251] of the characteristics of necessity we must on the contrary conclude

[017-252] that reproduction always occurs when we can replace ourselves in the

[017-253] conditions in which the stimulus (physical beauty) was produced.

[017-254] Not only can we replace ourselves in these conditions as an abstract

[017-255] possibility, but as a matter of fact we do so continually. Individual

[017-256] life, which is communion with ourselves (with our past), and social

[017-257] life, which is communion with our like, would not otherwise be

[017-258] possible.

[017-259] *Restorations and historical interpretation.*

[017-260] As regards the physical object, palæographers and philologists,

[017-261] who *restore* to texts their original physiognomy, *restorers* of

[017-262] pictures and of statues and other industrious toilers strive precisely

[017-263] to preserve or to restore to the physical object all its primitive

[017-264] energy. These efforts are certainly not always successful, or are

[017-265] not completely successful, for it is never or hardly ever possible

[017-266] to obtain a restoration complete in its smallest details. But the

[017-267] insurmountable is here only present accidentally and must not lead us

[017-268] to overlook the successes which actually are achieved.

[017-269] *Historical interpretation* labours for its part to reintegrate in

[017-270] us the psychological conditions which have changed in the course of

[017-271] history. It revives the dead, completes the fragmentary, and enables us

[017-272] to see a work of art (a physical object) as its author saw it in the

[017-273] moment of production.

[017-274] A condition of this historical labour is tradition, with the help of

[017-275] which it is possible to collect the scattered rays and concentrate them

[017-276] in one focus. With the help of memory we surround the physical stimulus

[017-277] with all the facts among which it arose; and thus we enable it to act

[017-278] upon us as it acted upon him who produced it.

[017-279] Where the tradition is broken, interpretation is arrested; in this

[017-280] case, the products of the past remain silent for us. Thus the

[017-281] expressions contained in the Etruscan or Mexican inscriptions are

[017-282] unattainable; thus we still hear discussions among ethnographers as

[017-283] to whether certain products of the art of savages are pictures or

[017-284] writings; thus archæologists and prehistorians are not always able

[017-285] to establish with certainty whether the figures found on the pottery

[017-286] of a certain region, and on other instruments employed, are of a

[017-287] religious or profane nature. But the arrest of interpretation, as that

[017-288] of restoration, is never a definitely insurmountable barrier; and the

[017-289] daily discoveries of new historical sources and of new methods of

[017-290] better exploiting the old, which we may hope to see ever improving,

[017-291] link up again broken traditions.

[017-292] We do not wish to deny that erroneous historical interpretation

[017-293] sometimes produces what may be called *palimpsests,* new expressions

[017-294] imposed upon the ancient, artistic fancies instead of historical

[017-295] reproductions. The so-called "fascination of the past" depends in part

[017-296] upon these expressions of ours, which we weave upon the historical.

[017-297] Thus has been discovered in Greek plastic art the calm and serene

[017-298] intuition of life of those peoples, who nevertheless felt the universal

[017-299] sorrow so poignantly; thus "the terror of the year 1000" has recently

[017-300] been discerned on the faces of the Byzantine saints, a terror which is

[017-301] a misunderstanding, or an artificial legend invented later by men of

[017-302] learning. But *historical criticism* tends precisely to circumscribe

[017-303] fancies and to establish exactly the point of view from which we must

[017-304] look.

[017-305] By means of the above process we live in communication with other men

[017-306] of the present and of the past; and we must not conclude because we

[017-307] sometimes, and indeed often, meet with an unknown or an ill-known,

[017-308] that therefore, when we believe we are engaged in a dialogue, we are

[017-309] always speaking a monologue; or that we are unable even to repeat the

[017-310] monologue which we formerly held with ourselves.
