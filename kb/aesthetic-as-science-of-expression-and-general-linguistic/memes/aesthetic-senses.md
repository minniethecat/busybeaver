# Æsthetic Senses

The theory of æsthetic senses is a recurring doctrine in the history of aesthetics — and a primary target of Croce's critique — that identifies certain sensory modalities (typically sight and hearing) as uniquely qualified to apprehend beauty, while relegating others (touch, taste, smell) to the domain of mere bodily pleasure.

## Definition

The theory of æsthetic senses holds that some senses are intrinsically "superior" or "æsthetic" because their objects admit of form, harmony, and disinterested contemplation, whereas other senses are "inferior" or "practical" because their objects are bound up with bodily appetite and utility. Croce summarizes the claim: "That æsthetic senses or superior senses exist and that the lower senses are incapable of æsthetic enjoyment is a very ancient theory" (038-1396–1397). Typical formulations list sight and hearing as æsthetic senses and touch, taste, and smell as non-æsthetic.

## Croce's Critique

### The Argument from Indivisibility

Croce devotes a sustained discussion to the theory of æsthetic senses in Chapter III of the Æsthetic. His fundamental objection is that aesthetic experience is a spiritual activity, not a physiological one. "The theory of the æsthetic senses has also arisen from the failure to distinguish the æsthetic fact from the physiological fact" (003-196–197). The senses are physical instruments; they furnish the raw material of impressions, but they do not "enter *directly* into the æsthetic fact" (003-222). The æsthetic fact is the spiritual synthesis — intuition-expression — which forms impressions into an organic whole regardless of which sense organ transmitted them.

To ask which senses are æsthetic is to misunderstand the nature of art: "To ask, in fact, what the æsthetic senses are, implies asking what æsthetic impressions are — a question that has no meaning, because no impression is æsthetic in itself; it becomes æsthetic only when elaborated by the spirit" (003-202).

### Unity and Indivisibility of Art

Croce insists on "the unity and indivisibility of the æsthetic fact" (P02-022). The division of the arts by sensory medium — painting for sight, music for hearing — is a merely empirical classification, not a philosophical distinction. The true medium of art is expression itself, which is spiritual and indivisible. Whatever the physical vehicle, the æsthetic fact is one.

### The Herderian Triad

In his historical survey, Croce notes that the theory was systematized by Herder and his successors, who established "the triad of æsthetic senses thus established by Herder (sight, hearing, touch)" (038-1441). The inclusion of touch in Herder's triad (as the sense of plastic form) shows the instability of the distinction itself — criteria that admit touch one century exclude it the next. Later formulations by Schleiermacher and others reduced the æsthetic senses to "free organs and no less spiritual" (038-1468), further muddying the boundary between the physiological and the spiritual.

## Historical Development

The distinction between higher and lower senses has ancient roots. Greek philosophy already ranked sight as the noblest sense (the etymological connection between *eidos* and *idea*). In the Enlightenment, the Abbé Du Bos argued that painting and music engage the mind more directly than the literary arts do, precisely because sight and hearing are the most "intellectual" senses. Baumgarten's foundation of aesthetics as a science of sensible cognition implicitly privileged certain senses as capable of clear-but-confused perception.

The theory reached its most elaborate form in the nineteenth century, when German aestheticians built systematic hierarchies of the senses. Herder's triad, Hegel's prioritization of sight and hearing (the "theoretical senses"), and Schopenhauer's ranking of the arts by their distance from the will all participated in this tradition.

## Key Distinctions

**Physiological vs. Spiritual.** Croce's critique turns on his fundamental distinction between the physiological organ and the spiritual activity. The senses belong to the organism; the æsthetic fact belongs to the spirit. Confusing the two is the "spectacle of a confusion of the æsthetic fact with the processes of the senses" (005-036).

**Theory of Art vs. Classification of Arts.** The theory of æsthetic senses belongs to the empirical classification of the arts, not to the philosophy of art. Croce accepts such classifications as practically useful — some arts do use sight, others hearing — but denies that they illuminate the essence of the æsthetic.

**Medium vs. Message.** The sensory medium through which a work of art is perceived is external to the work's aesthetic essence. A poem recited and a poem read are the same æsthetic fact, despite involving different sensory channels. The physical stimulus is a *conditio sine qua non*, not a constituent of the aesthetic experience.

## Criticism and Debate

Critics of Croce's position argue that his thoroughgoing idealism leads him to undervalue the material specificity of the arts. The particular sensory qualities of a medium — the timbre of a violin, the texture of oil paint, the rhythm of spoken verse — are not indifferent vehicles of an immaterial content but are integral to the work's meaning. Phenomenological aesthetics, in the tradition of Merleau-Ponty, insists that perception is always embodied and that the distinction between "spiritual" intuition and "physiological" sensation is untenable.

More recent work in neuroaesthetics has reopened the question of whether certain sensory pathways are privileged in aesthetic processing. Mirror-neuron research, studies of cross-modal perception, and investigations of the "reward circuit" activated by artworks all suggest that the question of æsthetic senses, stripped of its idealist framework, may admit of empirically testable hypotheses.
