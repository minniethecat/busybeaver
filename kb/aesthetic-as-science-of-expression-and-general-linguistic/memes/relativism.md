# Relativism

In Croce's aesthetic, relativism is the doctrine that denies any absolute criterion of taste, reducing aesthetic judgment to individual psychology, cultural convention, or physiological stimulus-response. Croce diagnoses relativism as a confusion of the theoretic with the practical, and defends an alternative conception of aesthetic absoluteness that is intuitive rather than rational.

## Definition

Aesthetic relativism holds that judgments of beauty and ugliness are relative — varying with individuals, epochs, races, or cultures — and that no universally valid standard of taste exists. In Croce's analysis, the relativist "denies the specific character of the æsthetic fact, and again confounds expression with impression, the theoretic with the practical" (017-150). The relativist position treats the aesthetic judgment as if it were a report of private feeling rather than a cognitive act.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Relativism, for Croce, arises from a failure to distinguish between the theoretic and practical activities of Spirit. When taste is treated as a matter of pleasure — a practical, economic phenomenon — it becomes relative by definition, since pleasure varies between individuals. But Croce insists that aesthetic judgment belongs to the theoretic sphere: it is a recognition of successful or unsuccessful expression, which admits of an absolute criterion "absolute in a different way from that of the intellect, which expresses itself in ratiocination" (017-153).

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a crucial distinction between **relativism and psychologism**. Both reduce aesthetic value to psychological states, but relativism does so by appealing to variation across persons or cultures, while psychologism appeals to mental processes within the individual. Croce rejects both as species of the same error: "The true solution lies in rejecting alike relativism or psychologism and false absolutism" (017-151).

A second distinction is between **relative relativism and absolute relativism**. Croce notes that some relativists attempt to moderate their position by allowing partial or provisional standards — what he calls "relative relativism" — but this, he argues, is an unstable halfway position that collapses back into full relativism under pressure (017-158).

The absolute criterion Croce proposes is not the rational absolute of logical demonstration but "the intuitive absoluteness of the imagination" (017-155). A genuine expression is beautiful not because it conforms to a rule but because the act of expression has fully succeeded in fusing impression with form. Ugliness, conversely, is "any fact in which expressive activity and passivity are found engaged with one another in an unfinished struggle" (017-157).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The problem of aesthetic relativism has ancient roots. The Sophists' claim that "man is the measure of all things" was already recognized by Plato as entailing a relativism about beauty. In the modern period, Hume's essay "Of the Standard of Taste" grappled with the apparent variability of taste while attempting to salvage a norm grounded in the consensus of qualified judges. Kant's *Critique of Judgment* addressed the problem through the notion of subjective universality — the claim that aesthetic judgments, while subjective, lay claim to universal assent.

In Croce's own intellectual milieu, the rise of experimental psychology and anthropology lent new empirical weight to relativist arguments. The discovery of radical variation in aesthetic preferences across cultures appeared to undermine any claim to a universal standard. It is this psychologistic and anthropological relativism, rather than the philosophical skepticism of the ancients, that Croce primarily engages.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to dissolve the problem of relativism not by proposing yet another absolute standard but by redefining what aesthetic absoluteness means. For Croce, the criterion of taste is not a rule or principle that can be stated in propositional form; it is the immediate, intuitive recognition that an expression has succeeded — that form and matter have achieved unity. This is absolute in the sense that it is not subject to empirical variation, but it is not "absolute" in the intellectualist sense of being demonstrable by argument. "Thus any act of expressive activity, which is so really, is to be recognized as beautiful" (017-155).

A criticism of aesthetic relativism, Croce observes, "cannot be based upon" empirical evidence alone, because the relativist has already conceded the empirical variation that the absolutist would need to explain away (038-1629). The only effective response is to show that the relativist has misdescribed the nature of aesthetic experience itself.

## Criticism and Debate

> "The true solution lies in rejecting alike relativism or psychologism and false absolutism; and in recognizing that the criterion of taste is absolute, but absolute in a different way from that of the intellect, which expresses itself in ratiocination." [017-151]

Critics have argued that Croce's appeal to an "intuitive absoluteness" is either vacuous — saying nothing more than that successful expression is successful — or secretly reintroduces a normative standard under the guise of description. If the criterion of taste is simply the fact of successful expression, how do we independently identify which expressions are successful without already applying a standard? Croce's response, implicit in his system, is that the question mistakes the nature of intuition: we do not *apply* a standard to expression; we *recognize* expressive success in the very act of intuiting it.
