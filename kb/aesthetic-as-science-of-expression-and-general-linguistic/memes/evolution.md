# Evolution (Aesthetic Evolutionism)

In the latter half of the nineteenth century, the ground lost by idealistic metaphysic was occupied by a new intellectual force: evolutionary theory. The prestige of Darwinian biology encouraged the conviction that all phenomena — including art and beauty — could be explained by tracing their origins in natural history. Herbert Spencer, the most ambitious positivist of his day, offered an evolutionary aesthetics whose principal thesis was disarmingly simple: the useful becomes beautiful when it ceases to be useful. Croce treats this entire enterprise as a category mistake — the substitution of natural for philosophical science, a "confused" metaphysic that fails to ask what art is by seeking only to explain how it came to be.

## Definition

Croce identifies evolutionary aesthetics as a form of "positivistic and evolutionary metaphysic," describing it as "a confused substitution of natural for philosophical sciences, and a hotch-potch of materialistic and idealistic, mechanical and theological theories, the whole crowned with scepticism and agnosticism" (036-003). The evolutionist defines art not by its intrinsic character as a spiritual activity but by its supposed emergence from pre-aesthetic biological functions — play, utility, sexual selection, or the pleasure of sensory stimulation.

Spencer's central claim, advanced in his essays on *The Useful and the Beautiful* (1852–1854) and *The Philosophy of Style*, was that aesthetic feelings originate in the surplus energy that remains after vital needs are satisfied. The beautiful is the formerly useful that has outlived its practical function: a ruined castle is ugly for habitation but beautiful for a picnic. Croce notes that Spencer "identifies the principle of evolution from the useful to the beautiful as contrast" (036-030), an explanation that, for Croce, explains nothing — it merely redescribes the phenomenon without penetrating its aesthetic character.

## In Croce's System

### Evolutionism as Metaphysical Confusion

Croce's critique of evolutionary aesthetics is twofold. First, it commits the genetic fallacy: it confuses the origin of a phenomenon with its nature. Even if art did arise from play or utility in the remote past of the species, that tells us nothing about what art is as a distinctive form of spiritual activity. The evolution of kinds, Croce argues, is an empirical observation about biological succession, not a philosophical account of spiritual categories. When aestheticians claimed to "portray the evolution of the artistic spirit" by tracing "the evolution of kinds" (005-184), they mistook a classificatory schema for a philosophical explanation.

Second, evolutionary aesthetics is historically uninformed in a way that is philosophically disabling. Spencer, Croce notes with evident disdain, "whilst discussing Æsthetic, actually did not know that he was dealing with problems for all, or almost all, of which solutions had been already proposed and discussed" (036-030). His ignorance of the tradition — he could not recall Schiller's name when discussing the relation of art and play — is not incidental but symptomatic: evolutionary aesthetics imagines itself to be making a fresh start from scientific first principles, but it is merely repeating, in a cruder key, questions that philosophy had already posed with greater precision.

### Key Distinctions

The principal distinction is between **evolutionary explanation and philosophical understanding**. The evolutionist asks "how did art arise?" — a question of natural history. The philosopher asks "what is art?" — a question of spiritual taxonomy. Croce insists that the second question is logically prior: one cannot trace the evolution of something without first knowing what that something is. The so-called "law of evolution" is, in Croce's terse verdict, among those "empty and indeterminate generalities" (009-059) that pass for explanation only because they are never subjected to philosophical scrutiny.

A second distinction opposes **evolutionism to Croce's philosophy of spirit**. In Croce's system, art is intuition-expression — a primary, autonomous, and eternal form of spiritual activity. It does not "evolve" from lower to higher forms; it is always and everywhere the same creative act, differing only in the empirical materials with which it works. To speak of art as having an evolutionary history is to confuse the spiritual category with its historical instances.

## Historical Context

Evolutionary aesthetics flourished in the intellectual climate that followed the publication of Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859). Spencer's *Principles of Psychology* (1855) and his later essays on aesthetics applied the evolutionary framework to art with an ambition that, Croce concedes, would have won "a low position amongst the early crude attempts at æsthetic speculation" had they been written two centuries earlier; "in the nineteenth century, one knows not how to judge them" (036-030). Grant Allen, Helmholtz, and other physiologists of aesthetic experience extended the program by investigating the sensory and neurological correlates of aesthetic pleasure, a line of inquiry that Croce treats as a legitimate empirical study that becomes illegitimate only when it claims to be a complete theory of art.

Later representatives of the tendency included Ernst Grosse, who attempted to found a "Science of Art" on sociological principles, and the Italian scholar Venturi, who "has tried to bring evolutionism into fashion" (036-030) in art history. Croce treats these efforts not as individual failures but as the predictable outcome of a method that confuses the natural with the spiritual.

## Criticism and Debate

> The ground lost by idealistic metaphysic was conquered in the latter half of the nineteenth century by positivistic and evolutionary metaphysic, a confused substitution of natural for philosophical sciences. Characteristic of this trend of opinion was its contempt of history, especially the history of philosophy; which prevented its ever making that contact with the unbroken and age-long efforts of thinkers without which it is idle to hope for fertile work and true progress. (036-003)
