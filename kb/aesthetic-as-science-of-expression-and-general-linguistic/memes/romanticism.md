# Romanticism

Romanticism (late 18th–mid 19th century) was the cultural movement that elevated art to the highest expression of human spirit. Croce both inherits Romanticism's valuation of aesthetic activity and critiques its tendency toward mystification.

## Significance for Æsthetic

Romanticism transformed the conception of the artist. From **Kant**'s theory of genius as "the talent that gives the rule to art" (P02-359) through **Schiller**'s aesthetic education to **Schelling**'s art as "organon of philosophy" (P02-351), Romanticism made art the privileged site of spiritual activity. The artist became a quasi-divine creator, and the work of art became a revelation of truth.

**Friedrich Schlegel** and the Jena circle developed the concept of *Romantische Ironie* (Romantic irony) — the artist's self-conscious transcendence of their own creation — which **Karl Solger** systematized philosophically (P02-239).

Croce's relationship to Romanticism is ambivalent. He affirms the Romantic insistence on the autonomy and dignity of art but criticizes the Romantic tendency to mystify the creative process as unconscious, inspired, or transcendent. For Croce, artistic creation is a fully conscious, rational activity of spirit.
