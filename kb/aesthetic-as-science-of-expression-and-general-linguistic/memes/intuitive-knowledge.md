# Intuitive Knowledge

Intuitive knowledge (*conoscenza intuitiva*) is the foundational form of knowledge in Croce's epistemological system — cognition through images rather than concepts. It is identified with art, expression, and the æsthetic fact, constituting the first of the two theoretic forms of spirit, prior to and independent of intellectual (logical) knowledge.

## Definition

Knowledge, Croce asserts, "has two forms: it is either intuitive knowledge or logical knowledge; knowledge obtained through the imagination or knowledge obtained through the intellect" (002-002). Intuitive knowledge is the grasp of individual things — this person, this landscape, this color — through the formation of images. It does not ask whether things are real or unreal, true or false: those are questions for intellectual knowledge. "Intuitive knowledge is expressive knowledge," Croce writes at the chapter's close; "independent and autonomous in respect to intellectual function" (002-344).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Intuitive knowledge occupies the first position among the four forms of spirit — intuition → concept → economic → moral. It is theoretic (not practical), individual (not universal), and autonomous. Without it, no other form can operate: "constant appeal is made to intuitive knowledge" wherever thought moves from the general to the concrete (002-008). The intellectual requires the intuitive as its material.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes intuitive knowledge sharply from intellectual knowledge. Intuition concerns the individual; the concept concerns the universal. "The identity of art and intuitive knowledge" (003-004) is the cornerstone of the entire Æsthetic: art is not a special region of experience but the primary mode of cognition itself.

The distinction from perception is equally critical. Intuitive knowledge is not passive reception of sense-data but active formation: "What can intuitive knowledge be," Croce asks, "without expression?" (002-026). Pure sensation, unformed by expression, does not rise to the level of knowledge at all.

### The Image

The unit of intuitive knowledge is the image (*immagine*) or representation (*rappresentazione*). The image is not a copy of reality but a constructive synthesis — form imposed on the formless matter of sensation. "The presentation or image and our intuitive knowledge" are one and the same (002-216).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The distinction between intuitive and intellectual cognition runs from Aristotle through Scholastic philosophy to Kant. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, Kant distinguishes intuitions from concepts but makes intuitions dependent on concepts for knowledge. Croce radicalizes this: intuitive knowledge is complete and autonomous before any concept arrives (P02-026).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's originality lies in three claims. First, intuitive knowledge is not a lower or confused form of intellectual knowledge — it is fully adequate on its own terms. Second, intuition is identical with expression; there is no unexpressed intuition awaiting words or forms. Third, art is simply intuitive knowledge raised to its highest clarity and perfection. These three theses upend both the rationalist denigration of art and the Romantic exaltation of ineffable genius (004-131).

## Historical Development

Croce's theory evolves across his career. In the *Æsthetic* (1902), intuitive knowledge is presented as the primary theoretic form; in later works, particularly the *Logic* and *Philosophy of the Practical*, the interweaving of intuition with the other spiritual forms is elaborated. "The intuitive knowledge of the discursive" (021-116) becomes a topic of special interest, as does the "perception of the individual" (021-117) that intuitive knowledge makes possible.
