# Intuition

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, intuition (*intuizione*) is the foundational form of spiritual activity — an immediate, non-conceptual mode of knowledge that is identical with expression and forms the essence of all art.

## Definition

Croce defines intuition as **expressive knowledge** — knowledge that is entirely independent of the intellect and consists in the forming of images or representations (002-342, 002-344). Unlike conceptual thought, which relates particulars to universals, intuition apprehends the individual directly: it is "the undifferentiated unity of the perception of the real and of the simple image of the possible" (002-060).

The core principle of Crocean aesthetics is that **intuition and expression are identical** — the one cannot be separated from the other:

> Feelings or impressions, then, pass by means of words from the obscure region of the soul into the clarity of the contemplative spirit. It is impossible to distinguish intuition from expression in this cognitive process. The one appears with the other at the same instant, because they are not two, but one. (002-261–002-264)

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Intuition occupies the first tier in Croce's fourfold division of spiritual activity:

1. **Intuition** (theoretical, individual) — art and aesthetics
2. **Concept** (theoretical, universal) — logic and philosophy
3. **Economic** (practical, individual) — utility and desire
4. **Ethical** (practical, universal) — morality

Each form is autonomous and irreducible. Intuition is logically prior to concept: "to admit the independence of intuition as regards concept does not suffice to give a true and precise idea of intuition" (002-074). Aesthetic activity operates below the threshold of logical judgment, producing images rather than propositions.

### Key Distinctions

**Intuition vs Sensation.** Intuition is not passive sensation but an active, formative process. Sensation is "formless matter, which the spirit can never apprehend in itself as simple matter" (002-152). Intuition gives form to sensation, transforming raw impression into coherent image. While some have "asserted that intuition is sensation, but not so much simple sensation as association of sensations" (002-195–002-196), Croce insists that intuition is a distinct cognitive act — the imposition of form upon formless psychic material.

**Intuition vs Perception vs Representation.** Croce collapses the traditional distinction between perception (of the real) and representation (of the imaginary). Both are intuitions: "Certainly perception is intuition: the perceptions of the room in which I am writing... are all intuitions. But the image that is now passing through my brain... is also an intuition" (002-081, 002-087). The distinction between real and unreal belongs to the intellect, not to intuition.

**The Expression Thesis.** "He who separates intuition from expression never succeeds in reuniting them" (002-239–002-240). Every genuine intuition is already expressed — the artist does not first intuit a Madonna and then learn to paint her; the intuition exists only as and through its expression (002-255–002-264).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The modern concept of intuition in aesthetics traces to **Giambattista Vico**, whom Croce regarded as "the inventor of aesthetic science" (P02-200). Vico opposed Cartesian deductive logic and recognized poetry as a form of autonomous, pre-logical knowledge — anticipating Croce's theory of intuition.

**Immanuel Kant** provided the other major precursor. In the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant distinguished aesthetic judgment from conceptual knowledge, and his doctrine of the "forms of intuition" — space and time as the a priori conditions of sensory experience — directly influenced Croce's framework (P02-225, 002-108). However, Croce departed from Kant by rejecting the thing-in-itself and making intuition the primary, self-sufficient mode of spiritual activity rather than a preliminary stage requiring conceptual completion.

**Alexander Baumgarten**, who coined the term "aesthetic" in the 18th century, conceived it as the science of "inferior knowledge" — a lower, sensuous counterpart to logic (P02-195). Croce rejected this intellectualist subordination of intuition to concept.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's decisive innovation was the **identity thesis** — the claim that intuition = expression = art. He radicalized Vico's insight into a complete philosophical system, arguing that all cognitive activity begins with the formation of expressive images. This unified aesthetics, linguistics, and the philosophy of art under a single principle. By identifying intuition with expression, Croce also dissolved the traditional distinction between artistic inspiration and technical execution: "If these thoughts seem to vanish or to become few and meagre in the act of expressing them, the reason is that they did not exist" (002-273–002-274).

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's identity thesis has been challenged on the grounds that it confuses the psychological process of artistic creation with its material product. Critics argue that the painter who "sees" a composition mentally has not yet produced a work of art — the canvas, paint, and technique are not reducible to intuitive expression. Croce's response is that without the forming activity, there is simply no image to speak of: the mental image exists only insofar as it is expressed (002-269–002-272).
