# Realism

In Croce's aesthetic, realism is the doctrine that art's truth consists in its fidelity to an external, objective reality independent of the mind — a position that Croce diagnoses as the symmetrical opposite of idealism, both of which err by introducing an external criterion of truth into a domain where the only relevant distinction is between successful and unsuccessful expression (030-035).

## Definition

Realism, in its aesthetic sense, is the theory that the value and truth of art lie in its accurate representation of the external world — whether of nature, society, or psychological reality. Croce identifies two principal forms: naive realism, which treats art as a direct copy of sensory appearances, and programmatic realism, which elevates fidelity to reality into a critical and polemical standard. Both forms, in Croce's analysis, make the same fundamental mistake: they judge art not by the success of its expression but by its correspondence to something outside itself. "The Æsthetic of idealism and that of realism" are, for Croce, twin errors generated by the same misunderstanding (030-035). Realism "could not avoid touching" upon the question of artistic truth, but it answered that question in the wrong terms — by appeal to external reference rather than internal coherence (021-109).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Realism, like mimesis before it, represents a *defective deviation* in Croce's taxonomy — one that denies or diminishes the autonomy of aesthetic intuition by subordinating it to a standard derived from the practical or intellectual spheres. The realist, Croce argues, confuses the theoretical activity of intuition (which produces its own object) with the practical activity of accurate observation (which measures against an external standard). This is particularly clear in the positivist versions of realism that flourished in the later nineteenth century, which "underlie the realism of" impressionist and naturalist aesthetics (037-202).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **realism vs. idealism** not as genuine alternatives but as two sides of the same coin. Between "idealism, realism, idealism-realism" — the proliferation of hybrid terms reveals the conceptual exhaustion of both positions (030-044). Neither can define art without invoking an extra-aesthetic standard: the realist appeals to external nature, the idealist to transcendent form. Second, Croce distinguishes **realism vs. verisimilitude**, a more sophisticated version of the mimetic principle that demands not exact copying but the *appearance* of truth. Even this refined form, however, retains the fundamental error of measuring art against an external norm. Third, Croce distinguishes **realism vs. naturalism**: naturalism is the scientific or positivist form of realism, which "underlie the realism of" an aesthetic that treats art as a document of social and biological conditions (037-202).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The roots of aesthetic realism lie in Aristotle's theory of mimesis, but realism as a distinct and self-conscious doctrine emerges in the nineteenth century, particularly in France. The realist novel — Balzac, Flaubert, the Goncourt brothers — asserted the artist's obligation to represent contemporary life with documentary fidelity. The parallel movement in painting, from Courbet through the early Impressionists, rejected academic idealism in favor of direct observation of the visible world. In aesthetic theory, realism found its philosophical articulation in figures such as Taine, who treated art as the product of race, milieu, and moment — a reduction that Croce singles out for particular criticism (037-012). The positivist and evolutionist aesthetics of the late nineteenth century carried the realist program to its logical conclusion, treating art as a natural phenomenon to be explained by scientific laws.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of realism is genealogical rather than refutative. He does not argue that realism is false in its particular claims but that it is incoherent as a theory of art: it cannot state what it is *for* without surreptitiously invoking aesthetic criteria that realism itself cannot justify. His key innovation is to show that realism and idealism are not genuine alternatives but moments in a dialectic that can only be resolved by abandoning both in favor of the concept of expression. "Idealism, or realism-idealism, as he called it" — the attempt to synthesize the two positions, associated with figures like Schiller and Schelling, merely exposes the inadequacy of the opposition itself (035-228).

## Criticism and Debate

> "Between idealism and realism, the Æsthetic of idealism and that of realism" — Croce's formulation has been criticized for collapsing a real and productive distinction into a false dichotomy (030-035). Marxist and materialist aestheticians have argued that Croce's dismissal of realism is an evasion of art's social and historical referentiality. Defenders respond that Croce does not deny art's relation to reality; he denies that this relation — rather than the expressive act itself — constitutes the criterion of artistic value.
