# Classification

In Croce's aesthetic theory, classification refers to the empirical practice of dividing works of art into distinct kinds, genres, and categories — a practice he rejects as philosophically groundless. The classification of the arts is, for Croce, one of the central errors of traditional aesthetics.

## Definition

Croce defines classification as the attempt to group individual intuition-expressions under general headings — lyric, epic, dramatic, tragic, comic, and so forth. He writes that "a classification of intuition-expressions is certainly permissible" as a practical convenience, but it has no cognitive or philosophical value (010-026). The error lies in mistaking this practical grouping for a real division in the nature of art.

## In Croce's System

### The Absurdity of Artistic Classification

Croce's most forceful statement on the subject appears in his chapter on the classification of the arts: "the classification of the arts is absurd. If the arts are without limits, they cannot be delimited; if they are not species of a genus, they cannot be classified" (016-122). The argument follows from his fundamental premise that every artistic expression is individual and unique — where there are no real species, there can be no real classification. He goes so far as to declare that "all the books dealing with classifications and discussions of the limits of the arts could be burned without any loss whatever" (016-124).

### Universality and Empirical Classification

Croce distinguishes between the universality of true philosophy and "the empirical classification of the individual arts," which he associates with the part dealing with the "theory of the limits of the arts" (019-114). The classifications proposed by theorists — painting vs. sculpture, poetry vs. prose, tragic vs. comic — are empirical generalisations drawn from observing the external, physical aspect of works, not from any insight into the essence of artistic expression.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors: The Classification Tradition

The impulse to classify the arts has deep roots in Western aesthetics. Aristotle distinguished epic from tragedy; Lessing drew boundaries between poetry and painting in the *Laocoön*; Hegel arranged the arts in a historical-systematic hierarchy. Croce surveys these efforts and finds them all wanting, noting the "limits and classifications of the arts in later philosophy" as a persistent but misguided preoccupation (P02-350). He singles out Lotze's "attack on classifications" as a partial anticipation of his own position, though he finds Lotze still trapped in "contradictions" (P02-353).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is not merely to critique particular classifications but to deny the very possibility of a valid classification of the arts. For him, the individuality and originality of every genuine expression means that no two works can be subsumed under the same concept. Classifications serve only the practical purposes of memory, pedagogy, and bibliography.

## Criticism and Debate

> "If the arts are without limits, they cannot be delimited; if they are not species of a genus, they cannot be classified." — Croce, *Æsthetic* (016-122)

Croce's position has been criticised as overly dismissive of the heuristic value of genre concepts. Later aestheticians have argued that genre expectations play a real role in both artistic production and audience reception, even if genre boundaries are fluid.
