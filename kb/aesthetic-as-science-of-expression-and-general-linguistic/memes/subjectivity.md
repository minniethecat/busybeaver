# Subjectivity

In Croce's *Æsthetic*, subjectivity is treated not as a threat to artistic truth but as a constitutive element of certain literary modes. Croce engages with the concept in his historical survey of aesthetic thought and in his analysis of poetic genres, positioning subjectivity in relation to the lyrical essence of art.

## Definition

Croce notes that a key figure in modern aesthetics "had been the first to oppose the subjectivity of" certain philosophical approaches, marking a turning point in the recognition of subjective factors in aesthetic experience [028-022]. In the Bibliographical Appendix, Croce examines the relationship between subjectivity and poetic form, asking how the Elegy and the Idyl should be classified "if subjectivity be placed in" relation to their formal characteristics [038-922]. He further distinguishes between "the object and subjectivity in the poet, didactic poetry" drawing on both elements [038-924].

## In Croce's System

### Subjectivity and Lyricism

For Croce, all art is fundamentally lyrical — an expression of the individual intuition. This means that subjectivity is not an optional feature of certain genres but the ground of artistic creation itself. The artist's subjective spirit is what gives form to intuitive content; without it, there would be no expression and therefore no art.

### The Objective-Subjective Polarity

Croce does not treat the objective and the subjective as mutually exclusive categories. Rather, they are poles of a unified expressive act. The object enters art only through subjective intuition; subjectivity realizes itself only through the formation of objective content [038-924]. This dialectical view allows Croce to discuss didactic poetry, elegy, and idyl without reducing them to either pure subjectivity or pure objectivity.

> The distinction between the object and subjectivity in the poet is not a separation but a polarity — the object is always subjectively intuited, and subjectivity always expresses itself through objective form. [038-924]

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Kant's Copernican revolution in philosophy placed subjectivity at the center of epistemological inquiry, influencing all subsequent aesthetic theory. Hegel historicized subjectivity within the dialectic of spirit, while the Romantics celebrated subjective feeling as the source of artistic creation. Schopenhauer and Nietzsche each developed distinct accounts of subjectivity in relation to aesthetic experience.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's distinctive contribution is to anchor subjectivity in the expressive act itself rather than in a transcendental subject or empirical psychology. By identifying art with intuition-expression, Croce makes subjectivity inseparable from the creative process while avoiding the metaphysical excesses of Romantic subjectivism. The artist's personality is present in the work not as autobiographical content but as the formative energy of expression.
