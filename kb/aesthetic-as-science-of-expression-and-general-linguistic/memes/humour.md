# Humour

In Croce's aesthetic system, humour (*l'umorismo*) is not an autonomous aesthetic category with a stable philosophical definition but one of the empirical varieties of expression -- a pseudo-concept belonging to psychology rather than to the philosophy of art. Nevertheless, Croce devotes sustained attention to the concept, tracing its migration from English into Continental thought during the eighteenth century and surveying the various attempts by German and Italian theorists to define it. Humour occupies a borderland between the comic and the tragic, characterized by a reflective self-consciousness that distinguishes it from mere wit.

## Definition

Croce treats humour as one of the "pseudo-aesthetic concepts" that cannot be given a rigorous philosophical definition, since it belongs to the domain of empirical psychology rather than the pure philosophy of spirit. In his survey of such concepts, he observes that humour carries a mixed significance, neither purely positive like the beautiful and the sublime nor purely negative like the ugly and the horrible; instead it shares this intermediate character with the comic, the tender, the melancholy, and the tragi-comic (013-074). Because such individuations are infinite, Croce argues, no single definition can do more than describe the physiognomy of a particular poet or poem (013-133).

In a passage that itself constitutes a miniature anthology of definitions, Croce writes:

> The humorous will be laughter amid tears, bitter laughter, the sudden spring from the comic to the tragic and from the tragic to the comic, the romantic comic, the opposite of the sublime, war declared against every attempt at insincerity, compassion ashamed to weep, a laugh, not at the fact, but at the ideal itself; and what you will beside, according as it is wished to get a view of the physiognomy of this or that poet, of this or that poem, which, in its uniqueness, is its own definition. (013-128--013-134)

This enumeration is not offered as a settled theory but as a demonstration that all such definitions are "metaphors, emphatic phrases, or logical tautologies" (013-127), useful only as ad hoc descriptions for particular cases.

## In Croce's System

### Humour and the Comic

Within Croce's framework, humour is understood as a modification or inflection of the comic, not a category on equal footing with it. The comic itself is merely one of the countless empirical varieties of the beautiful, and humour represents a particular shading within that variety. In the history of German aesthetics after Kant, theorists repeatedly attempted to systematize these varieties: Zeising, for instance, classified the humorous as one of the three primary modifications of beauty (alongside the sublime and the attractive), corresponding to the colour green in his chromatic analogy, and further subdivided it into the quaint, the capricious, and the melancholy (032-338--032-339). Croce recounts such schemes not to endorse them but to illustrate the futility of converting empirical observations into metaphysical hierarchies.

### Key Distinctions

The most important conceptual distinction Croce records is between English humour and the German *Laune* (caprice, whim). When the word "humour" reached the Continent from England, its original meaning was simply "temperament," and sometimes "spirit" or "wit" -- evidenced by the Italian phrase *belli umori* and the seventeenth-century Roman Academy of the *Umoristi* (032-300--032-303). In 1767, Lessing distinguished humour from *Laune*, a distinction maintained by Herder in 1769 against Riedel, who had conflated the two terms (032-308--032-310). This linguistic boundary proved influential: it marked off English humour as something richer and more reflective than mere capriciousness, carrying connotations of sympathetic insight that the German term lacked.

A further distinction separates humour from irony. Whereas irony -- particularly in its Romantic formulation by Solger and the Schlegels -- posits the artist's detached superiority over his material, humour as described by Jean Paul Richter involves a form of self-transcendence. Richter "discoursed at great length on wit and humour, described by him as the romantic comic, or the sublime reversed (*umgekehrte Erhabene*)" (032-338--032-339). In this formulation, humour is the sublime turned inward, an elevation achieved not through overwhelming magnitude but through a reflective turn that exposes the disproportion between the ideal and the real from within.

In the mid-nineteenth century, the Italian critic Francesco De Sanctis offered his own formulation: in 1856 he "attempted a definition of humour as 'an artistic form having for signification the destruction of limit, with consciousness of such destruction'" (034-069--034-071). This definition, more abstract than Richter's, emphasizes the cognitive dimension of humour -- it is not merely the breaking of boundaries but the simultaneous awareness that the boundary has been broken, a form of meta-comic consciousness.

## Historical Context

The Continental reception of English humour constitutes one of the finer threads in Croce's history of aesthetics. He identifies Voltaire as the key mediator who introduced the concept into France, writing in 1761 that "the English have a term to signify that pleasantry, that true comic, that gaiety, that urbanity, those sallies which escape a man without his being aware of them; and they render this idea by the word *humour*" (032-304--032-307). From France the concept passed into German aesthetic discourse, where it was taken up by Lessing, Herder, Richter, and eventually the idealist systems of Hegel and his followers.

Hegel incorporated humour into his tripartite scheme of art history in a distinctive way. Where Roman art (with its satire) marked the dissolution of classical art, Hegel "found in the subjective humour of Cervantes and Ariosto the dissolution of romantic art" (038-1786--038-1787). In this grand-historical scheme, humour signals the end of a cultural epoch: it is the form art takes when it can no longer sustain the unselfconscious unity of form and content and turns instead to ironic self-reflection. Cervantes and Ariosto thus become, for Hegel, the terminal figures of the romantic phase, their subjective humour the harbinger of art's sublation into philosophy.

Croce himself contributed to the scholarly literature on humour. In the Bibliographical Appendix he notes his own study, *Dei varî significanti della parola umorismo e del suo uso nella critica letteraria*, published in the *Journal of Comparative Literature* (New York, 1903) and reprinted in *Problemi di estetica*, alongside Baldensperger's *Les Definitions de l'humour* (Paris, 1907) as key reference works on the subject (038-2357--038-2361).
