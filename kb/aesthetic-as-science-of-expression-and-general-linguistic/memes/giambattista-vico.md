# Giambattista Vico

Giambattista Vico (1668–1744) was an Italian philosopher whose *Scienza Nuova* (New Science) anticipated many themes of modern aesthetics. Croce regarded Vico as the true founder of aesthetic science — "Vico as inventor of aesthetic science" (P02-200).

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Born in Naples, Vico was largely self-educated. He studied law, rhetoric, and classical literature, eventually becoming professor of rhetoric at the University of Naples in 1699 — a post he held for most of his career.

### Career and Major Works

Vico's major work, *Principi di Scienza Nuova* (Principles of a New Science, 1725), proposed a revolutionary theory of historical knowledge. Vico argued that we can truly know only what we have made — history and culture, not nature. This principle (*verum ipsum factum*) made him the first philosopher to treat poetry, myth, and language as autonomous forms of knowledge rather than defective versions of rational thought.

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Poetry as Autonomous Knowledge**: Vico opposed "Cartesianism and imagination" (P02-191), arguing that poetry is not failed philosophy but a distinct and valid mode of knowing — "Vico opposed to all former theories of poetry" (P02-202). The first poets were not confused philosophers but creators of a pre-logical symbolic world
- **Imagination and Intellect**: Vico separated imagination from intellect, recognizing that "poetry and history" are autonomous (P02-201). This distinction directly anticipates Croce's separation of intuition from concept
- **Vico's Judgements**: His "judgements of the grammarians" (P02-203) critiqued the intellectualist tradition that read ancient poetry through modern rational categories
- **The Influence of Vico**: Croce traces how Vico influenced Italian writers including A. Conti and others (P02-209), establishing an Italian tradition of aesthetic thought independent of German Idealism

## Criticism and Reception

> Croce writes that Vico was "opposed to all former theories of poetry" (P02-202) and that in Vico, for the first time, we find "Æsthetic in the Scienza Nuova" (P02-205). Croce credits Vico with anticipating the core thesis of his own *Aesthetic*: the autonomy of intuitive knowledge from conceptual thought. However, Vico's insights remained embedded in a theological and historical framework that Croce's idealist system secularizes and systematizes.
