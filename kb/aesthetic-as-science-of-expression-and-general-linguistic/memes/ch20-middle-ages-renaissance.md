[020-001] *Point of view of this history of Æsthetic.*

[020-002] The question whether Æsthetic is to be considered as an ancient or a

[020-003] modern science has on several occasions been a matter of controversy;

[020-004] whether, that is to say, it arose for the first time in the eighteenth

[020-005] century, or had previously arisen in the Græco-Roman world. This is

[020-006] a question, not only of facts, but of criteria, as is easily to be

[020-007] understood: whether one answers it in this way or that depends upon

[020-008] one's idea of that science, an idea afterwards adopted as a standard or

[020-009] criterion.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_1_1)

[020-010] Our view is that Æsthetic is the *science of the expressive*

[020-011] (representative or imaginative) *activity.* In our opinion, therefore,

[020-012] it does not appear until a precise concept is formulated of

[020-013] imagination, representation or expression, or in whatever other manner

[020-014] we prefer to name that attitude of the spirit, which is theoretical but

[020-015] not intellectual, a producer of knowledge, but of the individual, not

[020-016] of the universal. Outside this point of view, we for our part are not

[020-017] able to discover anything but deviations and errors.

[020-018] These deviations can lead in various directions. Following the

[020-019] distinctions and terminology of an eminent Italian philosopher[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_2_2) in

[020-020] an analogous case, we shall be inclined to say that they arise either

[020-021] from *excess* or from *defect.* The deviation from defect would be

[020-022] that which denies the existence of a special æsthetic and imaginative

[020-023] activity, or, which amounts to the same thing, denies its autonomy,

[020-024] and thus mutilates the reality of the spirit. Deviation by excess is

[020-025] that which substitutes for it or imposes upon it another activity,

[020-026] altogether undiscoverable in the experience of the interior life, a

[020-027] mysterious activity which does not really exist. Both these deviations,

[020-028] as can be deduced from the theoretical part of this work, take

[020-029] various forms. The first, that due to defect, may be: (*a*) *purely

[020-030] hedonistic,* in so far as it considers and accepts art as a simple fact

[020-031] of sensuous pleasure; (*b*) *rigoristic-hedonistic,* in so far as,

[020-032] looking upon it in the same way, it declares it to be irreconcilable

[020-033] with the highest life of man; (*c*) *hedonistic-moralistic* or

[020-034] *pedagogic,* in so far as it consents to a compromise, and while still

[020-035] considering art to be a fact of sense, declares that it need not be

[020-036] harmful, indeed that it may render some service to morality, provided

[020-037] always that it is submissive and obedient.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_3_3) The forms of the second

[020-038] deviation (which we shall call "mystical") are not determinable *a

[020-039] priori,* for they belong to feeling and imagination in their infinite

[020-040] variety and shades of meaning.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_4_4)

[020-041] *Mistaken tendencies, and attempts towards an Æsthetic, in

[020-042] Græco-Roman antiquity.*

[020-043] The Græco-Roman world presents all these fundamental forms of

[020-044] deviation: pure hedonism, moralism or pedagogism, mysticism, and

[020-045] together with them the most solemn and celebrated rigoristic negation

[020-046] of art which has ever been made. It also exhibits attempts at the

[020-047] theory of expression or pure imagination; but nothing more than

[020-048] approaches and attempts. Hence, since we must now take sides in the

[020-049] controversy as to whether Æsthetic is an ancient or modern science,

[020-050] we cannot but place ourselves upon the side of those who affirm its

[020-051] modernity.

[020-052] A rapid glance at the theories of antiquity will suffice to justify

[020-053] what we have said. We say rapid, because to enter into minute

[020-054] particulars, collecting all the scattered observations of ancient

[020-055] writers upon art, would be to do again what has been done many times

[020-056] and sometimes very well. Further, those ideas, propositions and

[020-057] theories have passed into the common patrimony of knowledge, together

[020-058] with what else remains of the classical world. It is therefore more

[020-059] advisable here than in any other part of this history merely to

[020-060] indicate the general lines of development.

[020-061] Origin of the æsthetic problem in [[greece|Greece]].

[020-062] [[art|Art]], the artistic faculty, only became a philosophical problem in

[020-063] Greece after the sophistical movement and as a consequence of the

[020-064] Socratic dialectic. The historians of literature generally point to

[020-065] the origins of Greek Æsthetic in the first appearance of criticism

[020-066] and reflection upon poetical works, painting and sculpture; in the

[020-067] judgements pronounced on the occasion of poetical competitions, in

[020-068] the observations that were made as to the methods of the different

[020-069] artists, in the analogies between painting and poetry as expressed in

[020-070] the sayings attributed to Simonides and Sophocles; or, finally, in the

[020-071] appearance of that word which served to group together the various

[020-072] arts and to indicate in a certain way their relationship—the word

[020-073] mimesis or mimetic (μίμησις)—which oscillates between the meaning of

[020-074] "imitation" and that of "representation." Others make the origin of

[020-075] Æsthetic go back to the polemics which were conducted by the first

[020-076] naturalistic and moralistic philosophers against the tales, fantasies

[020-077] and morals of poets, and to the interpretations of the hidden meaning

[020-078] (υπόνοια), or, as the moderns call it, allegory, employed to defend the

[020-079] good name of Homer and of the other poets; finally, to the *ancient

[020-080] quarrel* between philosophy and poetry, as Plato was afterwards to call

[020-081] it.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_5_5) But, to tell the truth, none of these reflections, observations

[020-082] and arguments implied a true and proper philosophical discussion of

[020-083] the nature of art. Nor was the sophistical movement favourable to its

[020-084] appearance. For although attention was at that time certainly given to

[020-085] internal psychical facts, yet these were conceived as mere phenomena

[020-086] of opinion and feeling, of pleasure and pain, of illusion, whim or

[020-087] caprice. And where there is no true and no false, no good and no evil,

[020-088] there can be no question of beautiful and ugly, nor of a difference

[020-089] between the true and [[beauty|the beautiful]] or between the beautiful and the

[020-090] good. The most one has in that case is the general problem of the

[020-091] irrational and the rational, but not that of the nature of art, which

[020-092] assumes the difference between rational and irrational, material

[020-093] and spiritual, mere fact and value, to have been already stated and

[020-094] grasped. If, then, the sophistical period was the necessary antecedent

[020-095] to the discoveries of Socrates, the æsthetic problem could only arise

[020-096] after Socrates. And it did indeed arise with Plato, author of the

[020-097] first, or indeed of the only really great negation of art of which

[020-098] there remains documentary proof in the history of ideas.

[020-099] *Plato's rigoristic negation.*

[020-100] Is art, mimesis, a rational or an irrational fact? Does it belong to

[020-101] the noble region of the soul, where philosophy and virtue are found,

[020-102] or does it dwell in that base lower sphere, with sensuality and crude

[020-103] passionality? This is the question asked by Plato,[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_6_6) who thus states

[020-104] the problem of Æsthetic for the first time. The sophist Gorgias was

[020-105] able to note, with his sceptical acuteness, that tragic representation

[020-106] is a deception, which (strangely enough) turns out to the honour

[020-107] both of him who deceives and of him who is deceived, in which it is

[020-108] shameful not to know how to deceive oneself and not to let oneself be

[020-109] deceived.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_7_7) With that remark he could rest content. That was for him

[020-110] a fact like another. But Plato, the philosopher, was bound to solve

[020-111] the problem: if it were a deception, then down with tragedy and the

[020-112] rest of mimetic productions: down with them among the other things to

[020-113] be despised, among the animal qualities of man. But if it were not

[020-114] deception, what was it? What place did art occupy among the lofty

[020-115] activities of philosophy and of good action?

[020-116] The answer that he gave is well known. Mimetic does not realize the

[020-117] ideas, that is to say the truth of things, but reproduces natural or

[020-118] artificial things, which are pale shadows of them; it is a diminution

[020-119] of a diminution, a third-hand work. Art, then, does not belong to the

[020-120] lofty and rational region of the soul (του λογιστικοϋ ἐν ψυχή) but to

[020-121] the sensual; it is not a strengthening but a corruption of the mind

[020-122] (λώβη τής διάνοιας); it can serve only sensual pleasure, which troubles

[020-123] and obscures. For this reason, mimetic, poetry and poets, must be

[020-124] excluded from the perfect Republic.

[020-125] Plato is the most consistent example of those who do not succeed in

[020-126] discovering any other form of knowledge but the intellectual. It was

[020-127] correctly observed by him that imitation stops at natural things,

[020-128] at the [[representation|image]] (το φάντασμα), and does not reach the concept, logical

[020-129] truth (άλήθεια), of which poets and painters are altogether ignorant.

[020-130] But his error consisted in believing that there is no other form of

[020-131] truth below the intellectual; that there is nothing but sensuality and

[020-132] passionality outside or prior to the intellect, that which discovers

[020-133] the ideas. Certainly, the fine æsthetic sense of Plato did not echo

[020-134] that depreciatory judgement of art; he himself declared that he would

[020-135] have been very glad to have been shown how to justify art and to place

[020-136] it among the forms of the spirit. But since none was able to give him

[020-137] this assistance, and since art with its *appearance* that yet lacks

[020-138] *reality* was repugnant to his ethical consciousness, and reason

[020-139] compelled him (ό λόγος ήρει) to banish it and place it with its peers,

[020-140] he resolutely obeyed his conscience and his reason.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_8_8)

[020-141] *Æsthetic hedonism and moralism.*

[020-142] Others were not troubled with these scruples, and although art was

[020-143] always looked upon as a mere thing of pleasure among the later

[020-144] hedonistic schools of various sorts, among rhetoricians and worldly

[020-145] people the duty of combating or of abolishing it was not felt.

[020-146] Nevertheless, this opposite extreme was also not calculated to meet

[020-147] with the endorsement of public opinion, for the latter, if tender

[020-148] towards art, is no less tender towards rationality and morality. For

[020-149] this reason both rationalists and moralists, compelled to recognize

[020-150] the force of such a condemnation as Plato's, sought for a compromise,

[020-151] a half measure. Away with the sensual and with art: certainly. But

[020-152] can we expel the sensual and the pleasurable without more ado? Can

[020-153] fragile human nature nourish itself exclusively with the strong food of

[020-154] philosophy and morality? Can we obtain observance of the true and of

[020-155] the good from the young and from the people, without allowing them at

[020-156] the same time some amusement? And has not man himself always something

[020-157] of the child, has he not always something of the people in him, is

[020-158] he not to be treated with the same precautions? Is there not a risk

[020-159] that the over-bent bow will break?—These considerations prepared the

[020-160] way for the justification of art, for they showed that if it were not

[020-161] rational in itself, it could on the other hand serve a rational end.

[020-162] Hence the search for the *external end* of art, which takes the place

[020-163] of the search for the essence or *internal end*. When art had been

[020-164] lowered to the level of a simple pleasurable illusion, an inebriation

[020-165] of the senses, it was necessary to subordinate the practical action

[020-166] of producing such an illusion and inebriation, like any other action,

[020-167] to the moral end. Art, being deprived of any dignity of its own,

[020-168] was obliged to assume a reflected or secondhand dignity. Thus the

[020-169] moralistic and pedagogic theory was constructed upon a hedonistic

[020-170] basis. The artist, who, for the pure hedonist, was comparable to

[020-171] a *hetaira,* became for the moralist a *pedagogue.* Hetaira and

[020-172] pedagogue, these are the symbols of the two conceptions of art that

[020-173] were disseminated in antiquity, and the second was grafted upon the

[020-174] first.

[020-175] Even before Plato's peremptory negation had directed thought to this

[020-176] way of issue, the literary criticism of Aristophanes was already full

[020-177] of the pedagogic idea: "What schoolmasters are to children, poets

[020-178] are to young men" (τοΐς ήβώσιν δὲ ποιηταί), he says in a celebrated

[020-179] verse[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_9_9) But we can find traces of it in Plato himself (in the

[020-180] dialogues in which he seems to withdraw from the too rigid conclusions

[020-181] of the *Republic)* and in Aristotle, both in the *Politics,* where he

[020-182] determines the use of music in education, and perhaps in the *Poetics,*

[020-183] where he speaks obscurely of a tragical *catharsis*; although as

[020-184] regards this latter, it is not to be altogether denied that he may

[020-185] have had a sort of glimpse of the modern idea of the liberating power

[020-186] of art.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_10_10) Later on, the pedagogic theory takes a form that was much

[020-187] affected by the Stoics. Strabo develops and defends this at great

[020-188] length, in the introduction to his geographical work, where he combats

[020-189] Eratosthenes, who has made poetry consist in mere pleasure without any

[020-190] notion of teaching. Strabo, on the contrary, maintained the opinion of

[020-191] the ancients, that it was "a first philosophy (φιλοσοφίαν τινα πρωτήν),

[020-192] which educated young men for life, and created customs, affections and

[020-193] actions, by means of pleasure." Therefore, he said, poetry has always

[020-194] been a part of education; one cannot be a good poet unless one is a

[020-195] good man (άνδρα άγαθόν). Legislators and founders of cities were the

[020-196] first to employ fables to admonish and to terrify: then this duty,

[020-197] which must be performed for women and children and even for adults,

[020-198] passed to the poets. We caress and dominate the multitude with fiction

[020-199] and with falsehood.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_11_11) "The poets tell many lies" (πολλά ψεύδονται

[020-200] άοιδοί) is a hemistich recorded by Plutarch, who describes minutely in

[020-201] one of his lesser works how the poets should be read to youths.[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_12_12)

[020-202] For him too poetry is a preparation for philosophy; it is a disguised

[020-203] philosophy, and therefore delights us in the same way as do fish and

[020-204] meat at feasts, so prepared as not to seem to be fish and meat; it is

[020-205] philosophy softened with fables, like the vine that grows close to the

[020-206] mandragora, and produces a wine that is the giver of sweet slumbers.

[020-207] It is not possible to pass from dense darkness to sunlight; one should

[020-208] first accustom the eyes to moderate light. Philosophers, in order to

[020-209] exhort and instruct, take their examples from true things; poets aim

[020-210] at a like result, when they create fictions and fables.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_13_13) Lucretius,

[020-211] in Roman literature, gives us the well-known comparison of the boys for

[020-212] whom the doctors "*prius or as pocula circum Contingunt mellis dulci

[020-213] flavoque liquore,*" in order to administer the bitter wormwood.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_14_14)

[020-214] Horace, in certain verses of the Epistle to the Pisones which have

[020-215] become proverbial (perhaps his source for them was the Greek of

[020-216] Neoptolemus of Paros?), offers both views (that of art as courtesan and

[020-217] of art as pedagogue) in his "*Aut prodesse volunt aut deledare poetae

[020-218] ... omne tulit punctum qui miscuit utile dulci.*"[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_15_15)

[020-219] Thus looked at, the office of the poet was confounded with that of the

[020-220] orator, for he too was a practical man aiming at practical effects;

[020-221] hence there arose discussions as to whether Virgil was to be considered

[020-222] as a poet or as an orator ("*Virgilius poeta an orator?*"). To both was

[020-223] assigned the triple end of *delectare, movere, docere*; in any case

[020-224] this tripartition was very empirical, for we clearly perceive that

[020-225] the *delectare* is here a means-and the *docere* a simple part of the

[020-226] *movere*: to move in the direction of the good, and therefore, among

[020-227] other goods, towards that of instruction. In like manner, it was said

[020-228] of the orator and poet (recording the meretricious basis of their task,

[020-229] and with a metaphor significant in its *naïveté*) that they were bound

[020-230] to avail themselves of the *allurements* (*lenocinium*) of form.

[020-231] *Mystical æsthetic in antiquity.*

[020-232] The mystical view, which considers art as a special mode of

[020-233] self-beatification, of entering into relation with the Absolute, with

[020-234] the Summum Bonum, with the ultimate root of things, appeared only

[020-235] in late antiquity, almost at the entrance to the Middle Ages. Its

[020-236] representative is the founder of the neo-Platonic school, Plotinus.

[020-237] It is strange that Plato should be usually selected as the founder

[020-238] and head of this æsthetic tendency, and that for this very reason to

[020-239] him should be attributed the honour of being the father of Æsthetic.

[020-240] But how could he, who had expounded with such great limpidity and

[020-241] clearness the reasons for which he was not able to accord to art a

[020-242] high place among the activities of the spirit, be credited with having

[020-243] accorded to it one of the highest places, equal, if not superior, to

[020-244] philosophy itself? This misunderstanding has evidently arisen out of

[020-245] the enthusiastic effusions about the Beautiful that we read in the

[020-246] *Gorgias,* the *Philebus,* the *Phædrus,* the *Symposium,* and other

[020-247] Platonic dialogues. It is well to dissipate it by declaring that the

[020-248] *Beauty* of which Plato discourses has nothing to do with art or with

[020-249] *artistic beauty.*

[020-250] *Investigations as to the Beautiful.*

[020-251] The search for the meaning and scientific content of the word

[020-252] "beautiful" could not but early attract the attention of the subtle

[020-253] and elegant Greek dialecticians. Indeed, we find Socrates engaged

[020-254] in discussing this question in one of the discourses that have been

[020-255] preserved for us by Xenophon; and we find him disposed to stop for

[020-256] the moment at the conclusion that the beautiful is *that which is

[020-257] convenient and which answers to the end desired,* or at the other

[020-258] conclusion that it is *that which one loves*[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_16_16) Plato too examines

[020-259] this sort of problem and proposes various sorts of solutions or

[020-260] attempts at solutions of it. He sometimes speaks of a beauty that

[020-261] dwells not only in bodies, but also in laws, in actions, in the

[020-262] sciences; sometimes he seems to conjoin and almost to identify it

[020-263] with the true, the good and the divine; now he returns to the view of

[020-264] Socrates and confuses it with the useful; now he distinguishes between

[020-265] a beautiful in itself (καλά καθ' αυτά) and a relatively beautiful (πρός

[020-266] τι καλά); or he makes true beauty consist in pure pleasure (ήδονη

[020-267] καθαρά), free from all shadow of pain; or he places it in measure and

[020-268] proportion (μετριότης καί ξνμμετρία); or talks of colours and sounds

[020-269] as possessing a beauty in themselves.[17] It was impossible to find

[020-270] an independent dominion for the beautiful, if the artistic or mimetic

[020-271] activity were deserted. This explains his wandering among so many

[020-272] different conceptions, among which it is just possible to say that the

[020-273] identification of the Beautiful with the Good prevails. Nothing better

[020-274] describes this uncertainty than the dialogue of the *Hippias maior*

[020-275] (which, if it be not Plato's, is Platonic). He here wishes to find

[020-276] out not what things are beautiful things, but what the beautiful is;

[020-277] that is to say, what it is that makes beautiful, not only a beautiful

[020-278] virgin, but also a beautiful mare, a beautiful lyre, a beautiful pot

[020-279] with two graceful ears of clay. Hippias and Socrates himself propose

[020-280] in turn the most various solutions; but the latter ends by confuting

[020-281] them all. "That which makes things beautiful is the gold that is added

[020-282] to them by way of ornament." No: gold only embellishes where it is

[020-283] *fitting* (πρέπων): for instance, a pot should have a wooden rather

[020-284] than a golden handle. "That is beautiful which cannot seem ugly to any

[020-285] one." But it is not a question of *seeming*: the question is to define

[020-286] what the beautiful is, whether it seems so or not. It is the *fitting*

[020-287] which makes things seem to be beautiful. But in that case, the fitting

[020-288] (which makes them *appear,* not *be)* is one thing, and the beautiful

[020-289] another. "The beautiful is what leads to the end, that is to say, the

[020-290] *useful* (χρήσιμον)." But if that were so, then evil would also be

[020-291] beautiful, because the useful leads also to the evil. "The beautiful

[020-292] is the *helpful,* that which leads to the good (ωφέλιμον)." But in

[020-293] this case, the good would not be beautiful nor the beautiful good; for

[020-294] the cause is not the effect, and the effect is not the cause. "The

[020-295] beautiful is that which delights the sight and hearing." But this fails

[020-296] to persuade for three reasons: firstly, because beautiful studies and

[020-297] laws are beautiful, which have nothing to do with the eye or with the

[020-298] ear; secondly, because we cannot discover a reason for limiting the

[020-299] beautiful to those senses, while excluding the pleasure of eating and

[020-300] smelling, and the extremely vivid pleasures of sex; thirdly, because,

[020-301] if the foundation of the beautiful were *visibility,* it would not be

[020-302] *audibility,* and if it were audibility it would not be visibility;

[020-303] hence that which constitutes the beautiful cannot dwell in either

[020-304] of the two qualities. And the question which has been repeated so

[020-305] insistently in the course of the dialogue: *what is the beautiful?* (τί

[020-306] εστι το καλόν;) remains unanswered.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_18_17)

[020-307] Later writers also conducted inquiries into the beautiful, and we

[020-308] possess the titles of several treatises upon the theme, which have

[020-309] been lost. Aristotle shows himself changeable and uncertain upon the

[020-310] point. In the scanty references which he makes to it, he at one time

[020-311] confounds the beautiful with the good, defining it as that which is

[020-312] both good and pleasing;[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_19_18) at another he notes that the good consists

[020-313] of action (εν πράξει) and the beautiful also in things that are

[020-314] immoveable (εν τοΐς άκινήτοις), drawing from this the argument that

[020-315] mathematics should be studied in order to determine its characters,

[020-316] order, symmetry and limit;[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_20_19) sometimes he places it in bigness and

[020-317] in order (εν μεγεθει καί τάξει);[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_21_20) at others he was led to look upon

[020-318] it as something apparently indefinable.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_22_21) Antiquity also established

[020-319] canons of beautiful things, such as that attributed to Polycletus on

[020-320] the proportions of the human body. And Cicero said of the beauty of

[020-321] bodies that they were "*quaedam apta figura membrorum cum coloris quadam

[020-322] suavitate.*"[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_23_22) All these affirmations, even when they are not mere

[020-323] empirical observations, or verbal glosses and substitutions, meet with

[020-324] unsurmountable obstacles.

[020-325] *Distinction between the theory of Art and the theory of the

[020-326] Beautiful.*

[020-327] In any case, not only is the conception of the beautiful, taken as

[020-328] a whole, identified with art in none of them; but sometimes art and

[020-329] beauty, mimesis and pleasing or displeasing material of mimesis, are

[020-330] clearly distinguished. Aristotle notes in his *Poetics* that it pleases

[020-331] us to see the most faithful images of things that are repugnant to

[020-332] us in reality, such, for instance, as the most contemptible forms of

[020-333] animals, or corpses (τάς εικόνας τάς μάλιστα ήκριβωμενας χαίρομεν

[020-334] θεωρουντες).[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_24_23) Plutarch demonstrates at length that works of art

[020-335] please us not as beautiful but as *resembling* (ούχ ως καλόν, άλλ,'

[020-336] ως ομοιον); he affirms that if the artist beautified things that are

[020-337] ugly in nature he would be offending against fitness and resemblance

[020-338] (το πρεπον και το eίκός); and he proclaims the principle that *the

[020-339] beautiful is one thing and beautiful imitation another* (oύ yaρ εστι

[020-340] ταυτό, το καλον και καλως τι μιμεισθαι). Paintings of horrible events

[020-341] are pleasing, such as *Medea slaying her sons* by Timomachus, *Orestes

[020-342] the matricide* by Theon, and the *Pretended madness of Ulysses* by

[020-343] Parrhasius; and if the grunting of a pig, the grating of a machine,

[020-344] the noise of the winds and the tumult of the sea are unpleasing, they

[020-345] pleased on the contrary in the case of Parmenon, who imitated the pig

[020-346] perfectly, and in Theodorus, who was not less expert in rendering the

[020-347] grating of machines.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_25_24) If the ancients had really wanted to place

[020-348] the beautiful and art in relation, a secondary and partial connexion

[020-349] of the two conceptions was to hand in the shape of the category of the

[020-350] *relatively* as distinguished from the *absolutely* beautiful. But

[020-351] where the word *καλόν* or *pulchrum* is applied to artistic productions

[020-352] in the writings of literary critics, it does not seem to be more than a

[020-353] linguistic usage, as we find, for instance, in the case of Plutarch's

[020-354] *beautiful* imitation, or also in the terminology of the rhetoricians,

[020-355] who sometimes called elegance and adornment of discourse *beauty* of

[020-356] elocution (το τής φράσεως κάλλος).

[020-357] *Fusion of the two by Plotinus.*

[020-358] It is only with Plotinus that the two divided territories are united

[020-359] and *the beautiful and art are fused into a single concept,* not by

[020-360] means of a beneficial absorption of the *equivocal* Platonic conception

[020-361] of beauty into the *unequivocal* conception of art, but by absorption

[020-362] of the clear into the confused, of *imitative art* in the so-called

[020-363] *beautiful.* And thus we reach an altogether new view: the beautiful

[020-364] and art are now both alike melted into a mystical passion and elevation

[020-365] of the spirit.

[020-366] Beauty, observes Plotinus, resides chiefly in things visible; but it

[020-367] is also to be found in things audible, such as verbal and musical

[020-368] compositions, and it is not lacking in things supersensible, such as

[020-369] works, offices, actions, habits, sciences and virtues. What is it

[020-370] that makes beautiful sensible and supersensible things alike? Not, he

[020-371] answers, the symmetry of their parts among themselves, and with the

[020-372] whole (συμμετρία των μερών προς αλληλα και προς το ολον) and their

[020-373] colour (ενχροια), according to one of the definitions most in vogue,

[020-374] which we have quoted above in the words of Cicero; because there are

[020-375] proportions in things ugly, and there are things that are simply

[020-376] beautiful without any relation of proportion: beauty, then, is one

[020-377] thing and symmetry another.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-8.htm.html#Footnote_26_25) The beautiful is what we welcome as

[020-378] akin to our own nature; the ugly is what repels us as our opposite,

[020-379] and the affinity of beautiful things with our souls that perceive them

[020-380] has its origin in the Idea, which produces both. That is beautiful

[020-381] which is *formed*; the ugly is what is *unformed,* that is to say,

[020-382] something which is capable of receiving form, but does not receive it

[020-383] or is not entirely dominated by it. A beautiful body is such, because

[020-384] of its communion (κοινωνία) with the Divine; beauty is the Divine, the

[020-385] Idea, shining through; and matter is beautiful, not in itself, but only

[020-386] when it is illuminated by the Idea. Light and fire, which are nearest

[020-387] to this state, shed beauty upon visible things, as the most spiritual

[020-388] among bodies. But the soul must purify itself, in order to perceive the

[020-389] beautiful, and make the power of the Idea that lies in it efficacious.

[020-390] Moderation, strength, prudence, and every other virtue, what else are

[020-391] they, according to the oracle, but *purification*? Thus there opens

[020-392] another eye in the soul, beside that of sensible beauty, which permits

[020-393] it to contemplate divine Beauty coincident with the Good, which is the

[020-394] supreme condition of beatitude.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_27_26) Art enters into such contemplation,

[020-395] because beauty, in things made by man, comes from the mind. Compare two

[020-396] blocks of stone, the one placed beside the other: one rough and crude,

[020-397] the other reduced to the statue of a god or of a man, for example of

[020-398] a Grace or of a Muse, or of a human being of such a shape, as art has

[020-399] collected from many particular beauties. The beauty of a block of this

[020-400] shape does not consist in its being of stone, but in the form that

[020-401] art has been able to give to it (παρά του ειδους o ενηκεν η τέχνη);

[020-402] and when the form is fully impressed upon it, the thing of art is more

[020-403] beautiful than any other natural thing. Hence he who despised the arts

[020-404] (Plato), because they imitated nature, was wrong; whereas the truth

[020-405] is, in the first place, that nature itself imitates the idea, and then

[020-406] that the arts do not simply limit themselves to imitating what the eyes

[020-407] see, but go back to those reasons or ideas from which nature itself is

[020-408] derived (ώς ούχ απλώς το όρώμενον μεμούνται, αλλ' άνατρέχουσιν επι τούς

[020-409] λόγους έξ ων η φύσις). Art therefore does not belong to nature, but

[020-410] adds beauty where it is wanting in nature: Phidias did not represent

[020-411] Jove because he had seen him, but such as he would appear if he wished

[020-412] to reveal himself to mortal eyes.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_28_27) The beauty of natural things

[020-413] is the archetype existing in the soul, the sole source of natural

[020-414] beauty.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_29_28)

[020-415] *The scientific tendency. Aristotle.*

[020-416] This affirmation of Plotinus and of neo-Platonism is the first

[020-417] true and proper affirmation of mystical Æsthetic, destined to such

[020-418] high fortunes in modern times, especially in the first half of the

[020-419] nineteenth century. But the attempts at a true Æsthetic, excluding

[020-420] certain luminous but incidental observations to be found even in

[020-421] Plato: for instance, that the poet should weave fables, not arguments

[020-422] (μύθους άλλ' ού λόγους),[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_30_29) go back to Aristotle and are altogether

[020-423] independent of his few and feeble speculations as to the beautiful.

[020-424] Aristotle by no means agreed with the Platonic condemnation; he felt

[020-425] (as indeed Plato himself had suspected) that such a result could not

[020-426] be altogether true, and that some aspect of the problem must have been

[020-427] neglected. When in his turn he attempted to find a solution, he found

[020-428] himself in more advantageous conditions than his great predecessor,

[020-429] since he had already overcome the obstacle that arose from the Platonic

[020-430] doctrine of ideas, a hypostasis of concepts and abstractions. The ideas

[020-431] were for him simply concepts, and reality presented itself in a far

[020-432] more lively manner, not as a diminution of ideas, but as a synthesis of

[020-433] matter and form, it was thus much more easy for him to recognize the

[020-434] rationality of mimesis in his general philosophical doctrine and to

[020-435] assign to it its right place; and indeed it seems generally clear to

[020-436] Aristotle that mimesis, being proper to man by nature, is contemplation

[020-437] or theoretic activity; although he sometimes seems to forget this (as

[020-438] when he confuses imitation with the case of boys, who acquire their

[020-439] first knowledge by following an example[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_31_30)), and although his system,

[020-440] which admits practical sciences and poietic activities (distinguished

[020-441] from the practical as leaving a material object behind them), disturbed

[020-442] the firm and constant consideration of artistic mimesis and poetry as

[020-443] a theoretical activity. But if it is a theoretical activity, by what

[020-444] characteristic is poetry distinguished both from *scientific* knowledge

[020-445] and from *historical* knowledge? This is the way Aristotle states the

[020-446] problem concerning the nature of art, and this is the true and only

[020-447] way of stating it. Even we moderns ask ourselves in what way art is

[020-448] distinguished from history and from science, and what this artistic

[020-449] form can be, which has the ideality of science and the concreteness

[020-450] and individuality of history. Poetry, answers Aristotle, differs from

[020-451] history, because, while the latter draws things that have happened

[020-452] (τα γενόμενα), poetry draws things that may possibly happen (οια αν

[020-453] γένοιτο), and differs from science, because, although it regards the

[020-454] universal and not the particular (τα καθ' εκαστον) like history,

[020-455] it does not regard it in the same way as science, but in a certain

[020-456] measure, which the philosopher indicates by the word *rather* (μαλλον

[020-457] τα καθόλου). The point then is to establish the precise meaning of

[020-458] the *possible,* the *rather* and the *historical particular.* But no

[020-459] sooner does Aristotle attempt to determine the meaning of these words,

[020-460] than he falls into contradictions and fallacies. That *universal* of

[020-461] poetry, which is the *possible,* seems to identify itself for him with

[020-462] the probable or the necessary (τα *κατά το είκος η το άναγκαΐον*),

[020-463] and the particular of history is not explained at all, except by

[020-464] giving instances: "that which Alcibiades did and what happened to

[020-465] him."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_32_31) Aristotle, in fact, after having made so good a beginning

[020-466] in the discovery of the purely imaginative, proper to poetry, remains

[020-467] half-way, perplexed and uncertain. Thus he sometimes makes the truth

[020-468] of imitation consist in a certain learning and syllogizing that takes

[020-469] place when we look at imitations, by which we recognize that "this is

[020-470] that," that a copy answers to the original;[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_33_32) or, worse, he loses the

[020-471] grains of truth that he has found and forgets that poetry has for its

[020-472] content the possible, admitting, not only that it may also depict the

[020-473] *impossible* (το αδύνατον), and even the *absurd* (το άτοπον), seeing

[020-474] that both are *credible* and that they do not injure the end of art,

[020-475] but even that we must prefer impossible probabilities to incredible

[020-476] possibilities.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_34_33) Art, since it has to do even with the impossible

[020-477] and absurd, will not therefore have in it anything of the rational,

[020-478] but in accordance with the Platonic theory it will be an imitation of

[020-479] the appearance in which empty sense indulges itself; that is to say, a

[020-480] thing of pleasure. Aristotle does not attain to this result, because

[020-481] he does not attain to any clear and precise result in this part of the

[020-482] subject, but it is one of the results that can be deduced from what he

[020-483] has said, or that, at any rate he is not able to exclude. This means

[020-484] that he did not fulfil his tacitly assumed task, and that although

[020-485] he re-examined the problem with marvellous acuteness after Plato, he

[020-486] failed truly to rid himself of the Platonic definition, by substituting

[020-487] a firmly-established one of his own.

[020-488] *The concepts of imitation and of imagination after A

[020-489] ristotle. Philostratus.*

[020-490] But the field of investigation toward which Aristotle had turned was

[020-491] generally neglected in antiquity: the very *Poetics* of Aristotle does

[020-492] not seem to have been widely known or influential. Ancient psychology

[020-493] knew [[imagination|fancy]] or imagination as a faculty midway between sense and

[020-494] intellect, but always as conservative and reproductive of sensuous

[020-495] impressions or conveying conceptions to the senses, never properly

[020-496] as a productive autonomous activity. That faculty was rarely and with

[020-497] little result placed in relation with the problem of art. Several

[020-498] historians of Æsthetic attach singular importance to certain passages

[020-499] in the *Life of Apollonius of Tyana* by the elder Philostratus, in

[020-500] which they believe that they discover a correction of the theory of

[020-501] *mimesis* and the first affirmation in history of the conception of

[020-502] *imaginative creation.* Phidias and Praxiteles (says the extract in

[020-503] question) did not need to go to heaven to see the gods, in order to

[020-504] be able to depict them in their works, as would have been necessary

[020-505] according to the theory of imitation. Imagination, without any need

[020-506] of models, made them able to do what they did: imagination, which is

[020-507] a wiser agent than simple imitation (φαντασία ... σοφωτόρα μιμήσεως

[020-508] δημιουργός), and gives form, like the other, not only to what has been

[020-509] seen, but also to what has never been seen, imagining it on the basis

[020-510] of existing things and in that way creating Jupiters and Minervas.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_35_34)

[020-511] However, the imagination of which Philostratus speaks here is not

[020-512] something different from the Aristotelian mimesis, which, as has been

[020-513] noted, was concerned not only with real things but also and chiefly

[020-514] with possible things. And had not Socrates observed (in the dialogue

[020-515] with the painter Parrhasius, preserved for us by Xenophon) that

[020-516] painters work by collecting what they need to form their figures from

[020-517] several bodies (εκ πολλων συνάγοντες τα εξ εκάστου καλλιστα)?[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_36_35) And

[020-518] was not the anecdote of Zeuxis, who was supposed to have taken the

[020-519] best of five Crotonian maidens in order to paint his Helen, and other

[020-520] anecdotes of a like sort, sufficiently widespread in antiquity? And

[020-521] had not Cicero eloquently explained, some years before Philostratus,

[020-522] how Phidias, when he was carving Jupiter, did not copy anything real,

[020-523] but kept his looks fixed upon "*species pulcritudinis eximia quaedam,*"

[020-524] which he had in his soul and which directed his art and his hand?[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_37_36)

[020-525] Nor can it be said that Philostratus opened the way to Plotinus,

[020-526] for whom the superior or intellectual imagination (νοητή), or eye of

[020-527] supersensible beauty, when it is not a new designation for beautiful

[020-528] imitation, is mystical intuition.

[020-529] The vagueness of the concept of mimesis reached its apex in those

[020-530] writers who gave it as a general title to any sort of work that had

[020-531] nature for its object, employing the Aristotelian phrase to affirm

[020-532] that "*omnis ars naturae imitatio est,*"[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_38_37) or saying, like the

[020-533] painter Eupompus when he blamed his servile imitators, that "*natura

[020-534] est imitanda, non artifex.*"[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_39_38) And those who wished to escape this

[020-535] vagueness did not know how to do so, save by conceiving the activity of

[020-536] imitation as the practical producer of duplicates of natural objects, a

[020-537] prejudice bora in the bosom of the pictorial and plastic arts, against

[020-538] which Philostratus perhaps intended to argue, in common with the other

[020-539] advocates of imagination.

[020-540] *Speculations on language.*

[020-541] The speculations upon language had a close connexion with those upon

[020-542] the nature of art begun by the sophists, for whom it became a matter

[020-543] for wonder that sounds could signify colours or things inaudible; that

[020-544] is to say, *speech* presented itself as a *problem.*[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_40_39) It was then

[020-545] discussed whether language was by nature (φύσει or by convention νόμω).

[020-546] By nature was sometimes understood mental necessity, and by convention

[020-547] what we should call a merely natural fact, psychological mechanism or

[020-548] sensationalism. In that sense of the terms, language would have been

[020-549] better called φύσει than νόμω. But at other times the distinction led

[020-550] to the question whether language answers to objective or logical truth

[020-551] and to the real relations between things (όρθότης των ονομάτων); and

[020-552] in this case, those would seem to be nearer the truth who proclaimed

[020-553] it to be conventional or arbitrary in respect to logical truth: νόμω

[020-554] or θέσει, and not φύσει Two different questions were consequently

[020-555] being treated together, and both were confusedly and equivocally

[020-556] discussed. They find their monument in the obscure *Cratylus* of

[020-557] Plato, which seems to fluctuate between different solutions. Nor did

[020-558] the later affirmation that the word is a sign (σημείον) of the thought

[020-559] solve anything, for it still remained to be shown in what way the sign

[020-560] was to be understood, whether φύσει or νόμω. Aristotle, who looked

[020-561] upon words as imitations (μιμηματα), in the same way as poetry,[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_41_40)

[020-562] made an observation of first-rate importance: in addition to the

[020-563] *enunciative* propositions, which express the (logically) true or

[020-564] false, there are others which do not express either the (logically)

[020-565] true or false, as for example the expressions of aspirations and of

[020-566] desires (εύχή), which therefore belong, not to logical exposition, but

[020-567] to poetical and rhetorical exposition.[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_42_41) And in another place we

[020-568] find him affirming in opposition to Bryson (who had said that a base

[020-569] thing remained such with whatever word it were designated) that base

[020-570] things can be expressed both with words that place them beneath the

[020-571] eye in all their crudity, and with other words which surround them

[020-572] with a veil.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_43_42) All this might have led to the separation of the

[020-573] linguistic faculty from the properly logical, and to its consideration

[020-574] in union with the poetical and artistic faculty; but here too the

[020-575] attempt stopped half-way. The Aristotelian logic assumed a verbal and

[020-576] formalistic character, which became more and more accentuated as time

[020-577] went on and formed an obstacle to the distinction between the two

[020-578] theoretical forms. Nevertheless, Epicurus asserted that the diversity

[020-579] of names designating the same thing with various peoples was due,

[020-580] not to convention and caprice, but to the fact that the impressions

[020-581] produced by things were different in each one of them.[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_44_43) And the

[020-582] Stoics, although they connected language with thought (διάνοια) and

[020-583] not with imagination, seem to have had a suspicion of the non-logical

[020-584] nature of language, for they interposed between thought and sound a

[020-585] *certain something* which was indicated in Greek by the word λεκτόν,

[020-586] and by the words *effatum* or *dicibile* in Latin. But we are not sure

[020-587] what they really meant, and whether that vague concept were intended

[020-588] by them to distinguish the linguistic representation from the abstract

[020-589] concept (which would bring them into touch with the modern view), or

[020-590] the meaning of sound in general.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_45_44)

[020-591] We cannot collect any other germ of truth from the ancient writers.

[020-592] A philosophical Grammar, like a philosophical Poetics, remained

[020-593] unattainable in antiquity.
