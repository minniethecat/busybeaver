[038-001] *Result of the history of Æsthetic.*

[038-002] We have reached the end of our history. Having passed in review the

[038-003] travail and doubt through which the discovery of the æsthetic concept

[038-004] was achieved, the vicissitudes first of neglect, then of revival and

[038-005] rediscovery to which it was exposed, the various oscillations and

[038-006] failures in its exact determination, the resurrection, triumphant and

[038-007] overwhelming, of ancient errors supposed to be dead and buried; we

[038-008] may now conclude, without appearing to assert anything unproven, that

[038-009] of Æsthetic in the proper sense of the word we have seen very little,

[038-010] even including the last two centuries' active research. Exceptional

[038-011] intellects have hit the mark and have supported their views with

[038-012] energy, with logic, and with consciousness of what they were doing.

[038-013] It would no doubt be possible to extract many true affirmations

[038-014] leading to the same point of view from the works of non-philosophical

[038-015] writers, art-critics and artists, from commonly received opinions and

[038-016] proverbial sayings; such a collection would show that this handful

[038-017] of philosophers does not stand alone, but is surrounded by a throng

[038-018] of supporters and is in perfect agreement with the general mind and

[038-019] universal common sense. But if [[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]] was right in saying that the

[038-020] rhythm of philosophy is to diverge from common opinion in order to

[038-021] return with redoubled vigour, it is evident that such divergence is

[038-022] necessary, and constitutes the growth of science, which is science

[038-023] itself. During this tedious process Æsthetic made mistakes which were

[038-024] at once deviations from the truth and attempts to reach it: such were

[038-025] the hedonism of the sophists and rhetoricians of antiquity and of the

[038-026] sensationalists of the eighteenth and second half of the nineteenth

[038-027] century; the moralistic hedonism of Aristophanes, of the Stoics, of

[038-028] the Roman eclectics, of the mediæval and [[renaissance|Renaissance]] writers; the

[038-029] ascetic and logical hedonism of Plato and the Fathers of the Church,

[038-030] of some mediæval and even some quite modern rigorists; and finally,

[038-031] the æsthetic mysticism which first appeared in Plotinus and reappeared

[038-032] again and again until its last and great triumph in the classical

[038-033] period of German philosophy. In the midst of these variously erroneous

[038-034] tendencies, ploughing the field of thought in every direction, a

[038-035] tenuous golden rivulet seems to flow, formed by the acute empiricism

[038-036] of Aristotle, the forceful penetration of [[giambattista-vico|Vico]], the analytical work

[038-037] of [[friedrich-schleiermacher|Schleiermacher]], Humboldt, [[francesco-de-sanctis|De Sanctis]] and others who echoed them

[038-038] with weaker voice. This series of thinkers suffices to remind us that

[038-039] æsthetic science no longer remains to be discovered; but at the same

[038-040] time the fact that they are so few and so often despised, ignored or

[038-041] controverted, proves that it is in its infancy.

[038-042] *History of science and history of the scientific criticism

[038-043] of particular errors.*

[038-044] The birth of a science is like that of a living being: its later

[038-045] development consists, like every life, in fighting the difficulties

[038-046] and errors, general and particular, which lurk in its path on every

[038-047] side. The forms of error are numerous in the extreme and mingle with

[038-048] each other and with the truth in complications equally numerous:

[038-049] root out one, another appears in its stead; the uprooted ones also

[038-050] reappear, though never in the same shape. Hence the necessity for

[038-051] perpetual scientific criticism and the impossibility of repose or

[038-052] finality in a science and of an end to further discussion. The errors

[038-053] which may be described as general, negations of the concept of art

[038-054] itself, have been touched on from time to time in the course of this

[038-055] History; whence it may be gathered a simple affirmation of the truth

[038-056] has not always been accompanied by any considerable recapture of enemy

[038-057] territory. As to what we have called particular errors, it is clear

[038-058] that when freed from confusing admixture of other forms and divested

[038-059] of fanciful expression, they reduce themselves to three heads, under

[038-060] which they have already been criticized in the first or theoretical

[038-061] part of this work. That is to say, errors may be directed (*a*) against

[038-062] the characteristic quality of the æsthetic fact; (*b*) against the

[038-063] specific; (*c*) against the generic: they may involve denial of the

[038-064] character of intuition, of theoretic contemplation, or of spiritual

[038-065] activity, which together constitute the æsthetic fact. Among the errors

[038-066] which fall into these three categories we are now to sketch in outline

[038-067] the history of those which have had, or have to-day, the greatest

[038-068] importance. Rather than a history it will be a historical essay,

[038-069] sufficient to show that, even in the criticism of individual errors,

[038-070] æsthetic science is in its infancy. If among these errors some appear

[038-071] to be decadent and nearly forgotten, they are not dead; they have not

[038-072] accomplished a legal demise at the hands of scientific criticism.

[038-073] Oblivion or instinctive rejection is not the same thing as scientific

[038-074] denial.

#### I

##### RHETORIC: OR THE THEORY OF ORNATE FORM

[038-075] *[[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] in the ancient sense.*

[038-076] Proceeding according to rank in importance, we inevitably head the list

[038-077] of theories for examination with the theory of Rhetoric, or Ornate [[form|Form]].

[038-078] It will not be superfluous to observe that the meaning given in

[038-079] modern times to the word Rhetoric, namely, the doctrine of ornate

[038-080] form, differs from that which it had for the ancients. Rhetoric in

[038-081] the modern sense is above all a theory of elocution, while elocution

[038-082] (λέξις, φράσις, ἑρμηνεία, elocutio) was but one portion, and not

[038-083] the principal one, of ancient Rhetoric. Taken as a whole, it consisted

[038-084] strictly of a manual or *vade-mecum* for advocates and politicians;

[038-085] it concerned itself with the two or the three "styles" (judicial,

[038-086] deliberative, demonstrative), and gave advice or furnished models to

[038-087] those striving to produce certain effects by means of speech.

[038-088] No definition of the art is more accurate than that given by its

[038-089] inventors the earliest Sicilian rhetoricians, scholars of Empedocles

[038-090] (Corax, Tisias, Gorgias): Rhetoric is the creator of persuasion

[038-091] (πειθος δημιουργός). It devoted itself to showing the method of

[038-092] using language so as to create a certain belief, a certain state of

[038-093] mind, in the hearer; hence the phrase "making the weaker case stronger"

[038-094] (τὸ τὸν ἥττω λόgον κρείττω ποιεῖν); the "increase or diminution

[038-095] according to circumstances" (*eloquentia in augendo minuendoque

[038-096] consistit*); the advice of Gorgias to "turn a thing to a jest if the

[038-097] adversary takes it seriously, or to a serious matter if he takes it as

[038-098] a jest,"[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_1_742) and many similar well-known maxims.

[038-099] *Criticism from moral point of view.*

[038-100] He who acts in this manner is not only æsthetically accomplished,

[038-101] as saying beautifully that which he wishes to say; he is also and

[038-102] especially a practical man with a practical end in view. As a practical

[038-103] man, however, he cannot evade moral responsibility for his actions;

[038-104] this point was fastened upon by Plato's polemic against Rhetoric, that

[038-105] is to say against fluent political charlatans and unscrupulous lawyers

[038-106] and journalists. Plato was quite right to condemn Rhetoric (when

[038-107] dissociated from a good purpose) as blameworthy and discreditable,

[038-108] directed to arouse the passions, a diet ruinous to health, a paint

[038-109] disastrous to beauty. Even had Rhetoric allied herself to Ethics,

[038-110] becoming a true guide of the soul (ψυχαγωγία τις διὰ τῶν λόγον);

[038-111] had Plato's criticism been directed solely against her abusers

[038-112] (everything being liable to abuse save virtue itself, says Aristotle);

[038-113] had Rhetoric been purified, producing such an orator as Cicero desired,

[038-114] *non ex rhetorum officinis sed ex academiae spatiis*[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_2_743) and imposing on

[038-115] him, with Quintilian, the duty of being *vir bonus dicendi peritus*;[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_3_744)

[038-116] yet the unalterable fact remains that Rhetoric can never be considered

[038-117] a regular science, being formed of a congeries of widely dissimilar

[038-118] cognitions.

[038-119] *Accumulation without system.*

[038-120] It included descriptions of passions and affections, comparisons of

[038-121] political and judicial institutions, theories of the abbreviated

[038-122] syllogism or enthymeme and of proof leading to a probable conclusion,

[038-123] pedagogic and popular exposition, literary elocution, declamation and

[038-124] mimicry, mnemonic, and so forth.

[038-125] *Its fortunes in the Middle Ages and Renaissance.*

[038-126] The rich and heterogeneous content of this ancient Rhetoric (which

[038-127] reached its highest development in the hands of Hermagoras of Temnos

[038-128] in the second century B.C.) gradually diminished in volume with the

[038-129] decadence of the ancient world and the change in political conditions.

[038-130] This is not the place to dwell on its fortunes in the Middle Ages or

[038-131] its partial replacement by formularies and *Artes dictandi* (and later

[038-132] by treatises upon the art of preaching), or to quote the reasons given

[038-133] by such writers as Patrizzi and Tassoni for its disappearance from the

[038-134] world of their day;[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_4_745) such history would be well worth writing, but

[038-135] would be out of place here. We will merely state that whilst conditions

[038-136] were at work on every side corroding this complex of cognitions, Louis

[038-137] Vives, Peter Ramus and Patrizzi himself were busy criticizing it from

[038-138] the point of view of systematic science.

[038-139] *Criticisms by Vives, Ramus and Patrizzi.*

[038-140] Vives emphasized the confused methods of the ancient treatise-writers,

[038-141] who embraced *omnia,* united eloquence with morality, and insisted that

[038-142] the orator must be *vir bonus.* He rejected four-fifths of ancient

[038-143] Rhetoric as extraneous: namely, memory, which is necessary in all arts;

[038-144] invention, which is the matter of each individual art; recitation,

[038-145] which is external; and disposition, which belongs to invention. He

[038-146] retained elocution only, not that which treats of *quid dicendum,* but

[038-147] of *quem ad modum,* extending it beyond the three styles or kinds to

[038-148] include history, apologue, epistles, novels and poetry.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_5_746) Antiquity

[038-149] furnishes us with few and faint attempts at such extension; now and

[038-150] then a Rhetorician ventures to suggest that the γένος ίστορικόν and

[038-151] ἐπιστολικόν be included in Rhetoric, and even (in spite of opposition)

[038-152] "infinite" questions, that is to say merely theoretical questions

[038-153] with no practical application, which amounts to a scientific or

[038-154] philosophical genus;[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_6_747) others agreed with Cicero[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_7_748) that when one had

[038-155] mastered the most difficult of all arts, forensic eloquence, all else

[038-156] seemed child's-play (*ludus est homini non hebeti* ...). Ramus and

[038-157] his pupil Omer Talon reproached Aristotle, Cicero and Quintilian with

[038-158] having confused Dialectic and Rhetoric; and they assigned invention

[038-159] and disposition to the former, agreeing with Vives that "elocution"

[038-160] alone should be allowed to Rhetoric.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_8_749) Patrizzi, on the other hand,

[038-161] refused the name of science to either, recognizing them as simple

[038-162] faculties, containing no individual matter (not even the three genera),

[038-163] and differentiating them only by attaching the term Dialectic to the

[038-164] dialogue form and proof of the necessary, and Rhetoric to connected

[038-165] discourse directed to persuasion in matters of opinion. Patrizzi

[038-166] observes that "conjoined speech" is used by historians, poets and

[038-167] philosophers, no less than by orators; and thus approaches the view of

[038-168] Vives.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_9_750)

[038-169] *Survival into modern times.*

[038-170] In spite of these opinions the body of rhetorical doctrine continued

[038-171] to flourish in the schools. Patrizzi was forgotten; if Ramus and Vives

[038-172] had some followers (such as Francisco Sanchez and Keckermann), they

[038-173] were generally held up to odium by the traditionalists. In the end,

[038-174] Rhetoric found a supporter in philosophy when Campanella made the

[038-175] following declaration in his *Rational Philosophy*: "*quodammodo Magiae

[038-176] portiuncula, quae affectus animi moderator et per ipsos voluntatem ciet

[038-177] ad quaecumque vult sequenda vel fugienda.*"[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_10_751) [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]] owed to it

[038-178] his tripartition of Æsthetic into heuristic, methodology and semeiotic

[038-179] (invention, disposition and elocution), adopted later by Meier. Among

[038-180] Meier's numerous works is a little book entitled *Theoretic Doctrine

[038-181] of Emotional Disturbances in General*,[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_11_752) considered by him to be a

[038-182] psychological introduction to æsthetic doctrine. On the other hand,

[038-183] [[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]] in his *Critique of Judgment* observes that eloquence, in

[038-184] the sense of *ars oratoria* or art of persuasion by means of beautiful

[038-185] appearance and dialectical form, must be distinguished from beautiful

[038-186] speaking (*Wohlredenheit)*; and that the art of oratory, playing upon

[038-187] the weakness of men to gain its own ends, "is worthy of no esteem"

[038-188] (*gar keiner Achtungwürdig)*[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_12_753) But in the schools it flourished in

[038-189] many celebrated compilations, including one by the [[france|French]] Jesuit Father

[038-190] Dominique de Colonne, which was in use until some few decades ago. Even

[038-191] to-day, in so-called Literary Institutions, we come across survivals of

[038-192] ancient Rhetoric, notably in chapters devoted to the art of oratory;

[038-193] and fresh manuals on judicial or sacred eloquence (Ortloff, Whately,

[038-194] etc.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_13_754)) are actually appearing, though rarely, to-day. Still,

[038-195] Rhetoric in the ancient sense may be said to have disappeared from the

[038-196] system of the sciences; to-day no philosopher would dream of following

[038-197] Campanella in dedicating a special section of rational philosophy to

[038-198] Rhetoric.

[038-199] *Modern signification of Rhetoric. Theory of literary form.*

[038-200] In compensation for this process, the theory of elocution and beautiful

[038-201] speech has been in modern times progressively emphasized and thrown

[038-202] into scientific form. But the idea of such a science is ancient, as we

[038-203] have seen; and equally ancient is the style of exposition, consisting

[038-204] in the doctrine of a double form and the concept of ornate form.

[038-205] *Concept of ornament.*

[038-206] The concept of "ornament" must have occurred spontaneously to the mind

[038-207] as soon as attention was directed to the values of speech by listening

[038-208] to poets reciting[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_14_755) or to oratorical contests in public gatherings.

[038-209] It must very early have been thought that the difference between

[038-210] good speaking and bad, or between that which gave more pleasure and

[038-211] that which gave less, between grave or solemn, and commonplace or

[038-212] colloquial, consisted in something additional superimposed upon the

[038-213] canvas of ordinary speech like an embroidery by a skilful orator. These

[038-214] considerations led the Græco-Roman rhetoricians to adopt the practice,

[038-215] like the Indians, who arrived at the distinction independently, to

[038-216] distinguish the bare (ψιλή) or purely grammatical form from another

[038-217] form containing an addition which they called ornament, κόσμος: *ornatum

[038-218] est* (Quintilian will serve, as typical of all the rest) quod perspicuo

[038-219] ac probabili plus est.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_15_756)

[038-220] The notion of ornament as something added on from outside forms the

[038-221] basis of the theory which Aristotle, the philosopher of Rhetoric, gave

[038-222] of the queen of ornaments, Metaphor. According to him the high pleasure

[038-223] aroused by metaphor arises from the collocation of different terms

[038-224] and the discovery of relations between species and genera, producing

[038-225] "learning and knowledge by means of the genus" (μαθησιν καi γνῶσιν

[038-226] διὰ τοῦ γένους), and that easy learning which is the greatest of human

[038-227] pleasures,[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_16_757) which amounts to saying that metaphor adds to the

[038-228] concept under consideration a group of minor incidental cognitions, as

[038-229] a kind of diversion and relief and pleasant instruction for the mind.

[038-230] *Classes of ornament.*

[038-231] Ornaments were divided and subdivided in a number of different ways.

[038-232] Aristotle (and previously Isocrates, rather differently) classified

[038-233] the ornaments which diversify bare or nude form, under the heads of

[038-234] dialect forms, substitutions and epithets, prolongations, truncations

[038-235] and abbreviations of words, and other departures from common usage,

[038-236] and, finally, rhythm and harmony. Substitutions were of four classes:

[038-237] species for genus; genus for species; species for species; and

[038-238] proportionate.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_17_758) After Aristotle, elocution was especially studied

[038-239] by Theophrastus and Demetrius Phalereus; these rhetoricians and their

[038-240] followers further solidified the classification of ornament by

[038-241] distinguishing tropes from figures (σχήματα) and dividing figures

[038-242] into figures of speech (scheimata τῆς λέχεως) and of thought

[038-243] (τῆς διανοίας), figures of speech into grammatical and rhetorical,

[038-244] and figures of thought into pathetic and ethic. Substitutions were

[038-245] divided into fourteen principal forms, metaphor, synecdoche, metonymy,

[038-246] antonomasia, onomatopeia, catachresis, metalepsis, epithet, allegory,

[038-247] enigma, irony, periphrase, hyperbaton and hyperbole; each divided

[038-248] into subspecies and contrasted with its relative vice. Figures of

[038-249] speech amounted to a score or so (repetition, anaphora, antistrophe,

[038-250] climax, asyndeton, assonance, etc.); figures of thought to about the

[038-251] same number (interrogation, prosopopœia, ætiopœia, hypotyposis,

[038-252] commotion, simulation, exclamation, apostrophe, aposiopesis, etc.).

[038-253] If these divisions have any value as aids to memory in relation to

[038-254] particular literary forms, considered rationally they are simply

[038-255] capricious, as is evidenced by the fact that many classes of the ornate

[038-256] appear now under the heading of tropes, now of figures; sometimes

[038-257] under figures of speech, then as those of thought, no reason for the

[038-258] alteration is given except the arbitrary caprice of an individual

[038-259] rhetorician which so decrees and disposes. And since one function

[038-260] which may be fulfilled by the rhetorical categories is to point

[038-261] out the divergence between two ways of expressing the same thing,

[038-262] one of which is arbitrarily selected as "proper,"[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_18_759) it is easy

[038-263] to see why the ancients defined metaphor as "*verbi vel sermonis a

[038-264] propria significatione in aliam cum virtute mutatio,*" and figure as

[038-265] "*conformatio quaedam orationis remota a communi et primum se offerenti

[038-266] ratione.*"[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_19_760)

[038-267] *The concept of the Fitting.*

[038-268] So far as we know, antiquity raised no revolt against the theory of

[038-269] ornament or of double form. We do sometimes hear Cicero, Quintilian,

[038-270] Seneca and others saying, *Ipsae res verba rapiunt, Pectus est quod

[038-271] disertos facit et vis mentis, Rem tene, verba sequentur, Curam

[038-272] verborum rerum volo esse sollicitudinem,* or *Nulla est verborum nisi

[038-273] rei cohaerentium virtus.* But these maxims did not bear the weighty

[038-274] meanings which we moderns might attach to them; they were perhaps in

[038-275] contradiction with the theory of ornament, but as the contradiction was

[038-276] unheeded, it was ineffective: they were the protests of common sense,

[038-277] powerless to combat the fallacies of school doctrine. Moreover, the

[038-278] latter was fitted with a safety-valve, a sage contrivance to disguise

[038-279] its inherent absurdity. If the ornate consisted of a *plus,* in what

[038-280] degree should it be used? if it gave pleasure, must we not conclude

[038-281] that the more it were used, the greater the pleasure derived? would its

[038-282] extravagant use be attended by extravagant pleasure? Herein was peril:

[038-283] instinctively the rhetoricians hastened to the defence, snatching

[038-284] up the first weapon that came to hand, namely, the fitting (πρέπον)

[038-285] Ornament must be used carefully; neither too much too little; *in medio

[038-286] virtus*; as much as is fitting (ἀλλά πρέπον). Aristotle recommends

[038-287] a style seasoned with "a certain dose" (δεῑ ἃρα κεκρᾶσθαί πως

[038-288] τούτοις.) for ornament should be a condiment, not a food (ἤδυσμα, οὐκ

[038-289] ἒδεσμα). [[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_20_761) The fitting was a concept quite inconsistent with that of

[038-290] ornament; it was a rival, and enemy, destined to destroy it. Fitting to

[038-291] what? to expression of course; but that which is fitting to expression

[038-292] cannot be called an ornament, an external addition; it coincides with

[038-293] expression itself. But the rhetoricians contented themselves with

[038-294] maintaining peaceful relations between the ornate and the fitting,

[038-295] without troubling to mediate them through a third concept. The

[038-296] pseudo-Longinus alone in answer to an observation of his predecessor

[038-297] Cæcilius that more than two or three metaphors must not be used in

[038-298] the same place, remarked that a larger number ought to be used where

[038-299] passion (τὰ πάθη) rushes headlong like a torrent, carrying with it as

[038-300] necessaries (ὡς ἀναγκαῑον) a multitude of such substitutions.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_21_762)

[038-301] *The theory of ornament in the Middle Ages and Renaissance.*

[038-302] Preserved in the compilations of later antiquity (such as the works of

[038-303] Donatus and Priscian and the celebrated allegorical tract of Marcianus

[038-304] Capella), and in the compendia of Bede, Rhabanus Maurus and others,

[038-305] the theory of ornament passed to the Middle Ages. Throughout this

[038-306] period Rhetoric, Grammar and Logic continued to form the *trivium* of

[038-307] the schools. The theory was to some extent favoured in mediæval times

[038-308] by the fact that writers and scholars made use of a dead language;

[038-309] this helped to reinforce the idea that beautiful form was not a

[038-310] spontaneous thing but consisted in an addition or embroidery. Under the

[038-311] Renaissance the theory continued to flourish and was revived by study

[038-312] of the best classical sources; to the works of Cicero were added the

[038-313] *Institutiones* of Quintilian and the *Rhetoric* of Aristotle, with the

[038-314] host of minor Latin and Greek rhetoricians, amongst whom was Hermogenes

[038-315] with his celebrated *Ideas,* brought into fashion by Giulio Camillo.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_22_763)

[038-316] Even those writers who dared to criticize the organism of ancient

[038-317] Rhetoric left the theory of ornament unassailed. Vives lamented

[038-318] over the "exaggerated subtlety of the Greeks" which had multiplied

[038-319] distinctions to infinity in this matter without diffusing light,[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_23_764)

[038-320] but he never took up a definite stand against the theory of ornament.

[038-321] Patrizzi was dissatisfied with the insufficient definition of

[038-322] ornament given by the ancients; but he asserted the existence of

[038-323] ornaments and metaphors as well as seven different modes of "conjoined

[038-324] speech,"—narrative, proof, amplification, diminution, ornament with

[038-325] its contrary, elevation and depression.[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_24_765) The school of Ramus

[038-326] continued to entrust Rhetoric with the "embellishment" of thought.

[038-327] Owing to the vast extension and intensification of life and literature

[038-328] in the sixteenth century, it would be easy to quote phrases, as we have

[038-329] done from ancient authors, asserting the strict dependence of speech

[038-330] upon the things it wishes to express, and lively attacks on pedants

[038-331] and pedantic forms and rules for beautiful speech. But what would be

[038-332] the use? The theory of ornament was always in the background, tacitly

[038-333] admitted as indisputable by all. Juan de Valdés, for instance, makes

[038-334] the following confession of stylistic faith: "*Escribo como hablo;

[038-335] solamente tengo cuidado de usar de vocablos que sinifiquen bien lo

[038-336] que quiero decir, y dígolo cuanto más llanamente me es posible,

[038-337] porqué, á mi parecer, en ninguna lengua está bien la afectación.*"

[038-338] But Valdés also says that beautiful language consists "*en que digais

[038-339] lo que quereis con las menos palabras que pudiéredes, de tal manera

[038-340] que ... no se pueda quitar ninguna sin ofender á la sentencia, ó

[038-341] al encarescimiento, ó á la elegancia.*"[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_25_766) Here it seems that

[038-342] amplification and elegance are conceived as extraneous to the meaning

[038-343] or content.—A gleam of truth is visible in Montaigne, who, confronted

[038-344] by the laboured categories into which rhetoricians divide ornament,

[038-345] observes: "*Oyez dire Métonymie, Métaphore, Allégorie et aultres tels

[038-346] noms de la Grammaire; semble il pas qu'on signifie quelque forme de

[038-347] langage rare et pellegrin? Ce sont tiltres qui touchent le babil de

[038-348] vostre chambrière.*"[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_26_767) That is to say, they are anything but language

[038-349] remote from the *primum se offerens ratio.*

[038-350] Reductio ad absurdum in the seventeenth century.

[038-351] The impossibility of upholding the theory of ornament was first noticed

[038-352] during the decadence of Italian literature in the seventeenth century,

[038-353] when literary production became but a play of empty forms, and the

[038-354] convenient, long violated in practice, was abandoned and forgotten even

[038-355] in theory, and came to be looked on as a limit arbitrarily imposed on

[038-356] the fundamental principle of ornamentation. The opponents of that style

[038-357] loaded with conceits which is known as "secentismo" from its prevalence

[038-358] in the seventeenth century (Matteo Pellegrini, Orsi and others) felt

[038-359] the viciousness of the literary production of their day; they were

[038-360] aware that decadence was due to the fact that literature was no longer

[038-361] the serious expression of a content; but they were embarrassed by the

[038-362] reasoning of the champions of bad taste, who were able to demonstrate

[038-363] that the whole business conformed in every particular with the literary

[038-364] theory of ornament, the common ground of both parties. In vain did the

[038-365] former appeal to the "convenient," the "moderate," the "avoidance of

[038-366] affectation," to ornament as "condiment, not food," and all the other

[038-367] weapons which had sufficed in times when healthy literary production

[038-368] and sound æsthetic taste had automatically corrected faulty theory:

[038-369] the other party replied, there was no reason to be sparing in use

[038-370] of ornament when it lay in masses ready to hand, or to avoid an

[038-371] ostentatious display of wit when one had an inexhaustible supply.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_27_768)

[038-372] *Polemic concerning the theory of ornament.*

[038-373] The same reaction against the abuse of ornament, against "Spanish and

[038-374] Italian conceits" (whose supporters had been Gracian in Spain and

[038-375] Tesauro in [[italy|Italy]]), took place in France. "... *Laissez à l'Italie

[038-376] De tous ces faux brillants l'éclatante folie"; "Ce que l'on conçoit

[038-377] bien s'énonce clairement. Et les mots, pour le dire, arrivent

[038-378] aisément.*"[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_28_769) Among the sharpest critics of conceits was the Jesuit

[038-379] Bouhours, already quoted, author of the *Manière de bien penser dans

[038-380] les œuvres d'esprit.* The rhetorical forms were the subject of warm

[038-381] controversy. Orsi, on national grounds the opponent of Bouhours (1703),

[038-382] asserted that all the ornamental devices of wit rested on a middle

[038-383] term and could be reduced to a rhetorical syllogism, and that wit

[038-384] consists of a truth which appears false or a falsehood which appears

[038-385] true.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_29_770) If this controversy produced no great scientific result at

[038-386] the time, at least it prepared the mind for greater liberty; and, as

[038-387] we have remarked elsewhere,[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_30_771) it may have influenced Vico, who, in

[038-388] framing his new concept of poetical imagination, recognized that it

[038-389] necessitated a wholesale reconstruction of the theory of rhetoric

[038-390] and the conclusion that its figures and tropes are not "caprices of

[038-391] pleasure" but "necessities of the human mind."[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_31_772)

[038-392] *Du Marsais and metaphor.*

[038-393] We find the theory of rhetorical ornament jealously kept intact by

[038-394] Baumgarten and Meier, while in France it was as vigorously assailed

[038-395] by César Chesneau du Marsais, who published in 1730 a treatise on

[038-396] *Tropes* (the seventh part of his *General Grammar*)[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_32_773) wherein he

[038-397] develops, on the subject of metaphor, the observation already made by

[038-398] Montaigne: indeed he was perhaps inspired by Montaigne, although he

[038-399] does not mention his name. Du Marsais remarks that it is said that

[038-400] figures are modes of speech and turns of expression removed from the

[038-401] ordinary and common; which is an empty phrase, as good as saying "the

[038-402] figured differs from the non-figured and figures are figures and not

[038-403] non-figures." On the other hand it is wholly untrue that figures

[038-404] are removed from ordinary speech, for "nothing is more natural,

[038-405] ordinary and common than figures: more figures of speech are used

[038-406] in the town square on a market-day than in many days of academical

[038-407] discussion"; and no speech, however short, can be composed entirely of

[038-408] non-figurative expressions. And Du Marsais gives instances of quite

[038-409] obvious and spontaneous expressions in which Rhetoric cannot refuse

[038-410] to recognize the figures of apostrophe, congeries, interrogation,

[038-411] ellipsis, prosopopœia: "The apostles were persecuted and suffered

[038-412] their persecutions with patience. What can be more natural than

[038-413] the description given by St. Paul? *Maledicimur et benedicimus;

[038-414] persecutionem patimur et sustinemus; blasphemamur et obsecramus.*

[038-415] Yet the apostle makes use of a fine figure of antithesis; cursing is

[038-416] the opposite to blessing; persecution to endurance; blasphemy to

[038-417] prayer." But further, the very language of the figure is figured,

[038-418] since it is a metaphor.—But after such acute observations, Du Marsais

[038-419] ends by himself becoming confused and defines figures as "manners of

[038-420] speech differing from others in a particular modification by which it

[038-421] is possible to reduce each one to a species apart, and give a more

[038-422] lively, noble or pleasing effect than can be gained by a manner of

[038-423] speech expressing the same content of thought without such particular

[038-424] modification."[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_33_774)

[038-425] *Psychological interpretation.*

[038-426] But the psychological interpretation of figures of speech, the first

[038-427] stage towards their æsthetic criticism, was not allowed to drop here.

[038-428] In his *Elements of Criticism,* Home says that he had long questioned

[038-429] whether that part of Rhetoric concerning figures might not be reduced

[038-430] to rational principles, and had finally discovered that figures consist

[038-431] in the passional element;[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_34_775) he set himself therefore to analyse

[038-432] prosopopœia, apostrophe and hyperbole in the light of the passional

[038-433] faculty. From Du Marsais and Home is derived everything of value in the

[038-434] *Lectures on Rhetoric and belles lettres* of Hugh Blair, professor

[038-435] at Edinburgh University from 1759 onwards;[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_35_776) published in book form,

[038-436] these lectures had an immense vogue in all the schools of Europe

[038-437] including those of Italy, and replaced advantageously, by their "reason

[038-438] and good sense," works of a much cruder type. Blair defined figures

[038-439] in general as "language suggested by imagination or passion."[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_36_777)

[038-440] Similar ideas were promulgated in France by Marmontel in his *Elements

[038-441] of Literature*.[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_37_778) In Italy Cesarotti was contrasting the logical

[038-442] element or "cypher-terms" of language with the rhetorical element or

[038-443] "figure-terms," and rational eloquence with imaginative eloquence.[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_38_779)

[038-444] Beccaria, though a shrewd psychological analyst, held to the view of

[038-445] literary style as "accessory ideas or feelings added to the principal

[038-446] in any discourse"; that is, he failed to free himself from the

[038-447] distinction between the intellectual form intended for the expression

[038-448] of the principal ideas, and the literary form, modifying the first by

[038-449] the addition of accessory ideas.[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_39_780) In [[germany|Germany]] an effort was made by

[038-450] Herder to interpret tropes and metaphors as Vico had done, that is to

[038-451] say as essential to primitive language and poetry.

[038-452] *[[romanticism|Romanticism]] and Rhetoric. Present day.*

[038-453] Romanticism was the ruin of the theory of ornament, and caused it

[038-454] practically to be thrown on the scrap-heap, but it cannot be said

[038-455] to have gone under for good or to have been superseded by a new and

[038-456] accurately stated theory. The chief philosophers of Æsthetic (not

[038-457] only Kant, who as we know remained in bondage to the mechanical and

[038-458] ornamental theory; not only Herder, whose knowledge of art seems to

[038-459] have been confined to a little music and a great deal of rhetoric;

[038-460] but such romantic philosophers as [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]], [[karl-solger|Solger]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]) still

[038-461] retained the sections devoted to metaphor, trope and allegory for

[038-462] tradition's sake, without severe scrutiny. Italian Romanticism with

[038-463] Manzoni at its head destroyed the belief in beautiful and elegant

[038-464] words, and dealt a blow at Rhetoric: but was it killed by the stroke?

[038-465] Apparently not, judging by the concessions unconsciously made by the

[038-466] scholastic treatise-writer Ruggero Bonghi, whose *Critical Letters*

[038-467] assert the existence of two styles or forms, which at bottom are

[038-468] nothing else than the plain and the ornate.[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_40_781) German schools of

[038-469] philology have pretty generally accepted the stylistic theory of

[038-470] Gröber, who divides style into logical (objective) and affective

[038-471] (subjective):[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_41_782) an ancient error masked by terminology borrowed from

[038-472] the psychological philosophy in fashion at modern universities. In

[038-473] the same spirit a recent writer rechristens the rhetorical doctrine

[038-474] of tropes and figures by the title "Doctrine of the Forms of Æsthetic

[038-475] Apperception," and divides them into the four categories (the ancient

[038-476] wealth of categories reduced to a paltry four!) of personification,

[038-477] metaphor, antithesis, and symbol.[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_42_783) Biese has devoted an entire

[038-478] book to metaphor; but one searches it in vain for a serious æsthetic

[038-479] analysis of this category.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_43_784)

[038-480] The best scientific criticism of the theory of ornament is found

[038-481] scattered throughout the writings of De Sanctis, who when lecturing on

[038-482] rhetoric preached what he called anti-rhetoric.[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_44_785) But even here the

[038-483] criticism is not conducted from a strictly systematic point of view. It

[038-484] seems to us that the true criticism should be deduced negatively from

[038-485] the very nature of æsthetic activity, which does not lend itself to

[038-486] partition; there is no such thing as activity type *a* or type *b,* nor

[038-487] can the same concept be expressed now in one way, now in another. Such

[038-488] is the only way of abolishing the double monster of bare form which

[038-489] is, no one knows how, deprived of imagination, and ornate form which

[038-490] contains, no one knows how, an addition on the side of imagination.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-26.htm.html#Footnote_45_786)

#### II

##### HISTORY OF THE ARTISTIC AND LITERARY KINDS

[038-567] *The kinds in antiquity. Aristotle.*

[038-568] The theory of artistic and literary kinds and of the laws or rules

[038-569] proper to each separate kind has almost always followed the fortunes of

[038-570] the rhetorical theory.

[038-571] Traces of the threefold division into epic, lyric and dramatic are

[038-572] found in Plato; and Aristophanes gives an example of criticism

[038-573] according to the canon of the kinds, particularly that of tragedy.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_1_787)

[038-574] But the most conspicuous theoretical treatment of the kinds bequeathed

[038-575] us by antiquity is precisely the doctrine of Tragedy which forms a

[038-576] large part of the Aristotelian fragment known as the Poetics. Aristotle

[038-577] defines such a composition as an imitation of a serious and complete

[038-578] action, having size, in language adorned in accordance with the

[038-579] requirements of the different parts, its exposition to be by action and

[038-580] not by narration, and using pity or terror as means to free or purify

[038-581] us from these same passions;[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_2_788) he gives minute details as to the six

[038-582] parts of which it is composed, especially the plot and the tragic

[038-583] character. It has been often said, ever since the days of Vincenzo

[038-584] Maggio in the sixteenth century, that Aristotle treated of the nature

[038-585] of poetry, or particular forms of poetry, without claiming to give

[038-586] precepts. But Piccolomini answered that "all these things and other

[038-587] similar ones are shown or asserted with no other purpose but that we

[038-588] may see in what way their precepts and laws must be obeyed and carried

[038-589] out," just as, to make a hammer or saw, one begins by describing

[038-590] the parts of which they are composed.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_3_789) The error of which we take

[038-591] Aristotle as representative lies in transmuting abstractions and

[038-592] empirical partitions into rational concepts: this was almost inevitable

[038-593] at the beginnings of æsthetic reflexion, and the Sanskrit theory of

[038-594] poetry employed the same method independently when, for example, it

[038-595] defines and legislates for ten principal and eighteen secondary styles

[038-596] of drama; forty-eight varieties of hero; and we know not how many kinds

[038-597] of heroines.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_4_790)

[038-598] *In the Middle Ages and Renaissance*.

[038-599] After Aristotle, the theory of poetic kinds does not seem to have been

[038-600] completely or elaborately developed in antiquity. The Middle Ages may

[038-601] be said to have expressed the doctrine in treatises of the kind known

[038-602] as "rhythmic arts" or "methods of composition." When the Aristotelian

[038-603] fragment was first noticed, it is curious to see the way in which

[038-604] the paraphrase of Averroes distorted the theory of kinds. Averroes

[038-605] conceives tragedy as the art of praise, comedy as that of blame,

[038-606] which amounts to identifying the former with panegyric, the latter

[038-607] with satire; and he believes the *peripeteia* to be the same thing as

[038-608] antithesis, or the artifice of beginning the description of a thing by

[038-609] describing its opposite.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_5_791) This distortion demonstrates afresh the

[038-610] merely historical character of these kinds and their unintelligibility

[038-611] by the methods of pure logic to a thinker living in times and under

[038-612] customs different from those of the [[greece|Hellenic]] world. The Renaissance

[038-613] seized upon Aristotle's text, partly expounded it, partly distorted it

[038-614] and partly thought it out afresh, and thus succeeded in establishing

[038-615] a long list of kinds and sub-kinds rigidly defined and subjected to

[038-616] inexorable laws. Controversy now began over the correct understanding

[038-617] of the unities of epic or dramatic poetry; over the moral quality and

[038-618] social standing proper to the characters in this kind of poem and in

[038-619] that; over the nature of the plot, and whether it includes passions and

[038-620] thoughts, and whether lyrics should or should not be received as true

[038-621] poetry; whether the material of tragedy should be historical; whether

[038-622] the dialogue of comedy may be in prose; whether a happy ending may

[038-623] be allowed in tragedy; whether the tragic character may be a perfect

[038-624] gentleman; what kind and number of episodes is admissible in the poem,

[038-625] and how they should be incorporated in the main plot; and so on.

[038-626] Great anguish was caused by the mysterious rule of catharsis found in

[038-627] black and white in Aristotle's text, and Segni naïvely predicted that

[038-628] tragic poetry would be revived in its perfect spectacular entirety

[038-629] for the sake of experiencing the effect spoken of by Aristotle, that

[038-630] "purgation" which causes "the birth of tranquillity in the soul and of

[038-631] freedom of all perturbation."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_6_792)

[038-632] *The doctrine of the three unities.*

[038-633] Amongst the many undertakings brought to a glorious end by the critics

[038-634] and treatise-writers of the sixteenth century, the best known is the

[038-635] establishment of the three unities of time, place and action. One

[038-636] cannot indeed see why they are called unities, for in strictness they

[038-637] could at most be spoken of as shortness of time, straitness of space

[038-638] and limitation of tragic subjects to a certain class of action. It is

[038-639] well known that Aristotle prescribed unity of action only, and reminded

[038-640] his hearers that theatrical custom alone imposed on the action a

[038-641] time-limit of one day. On this last point the critics of the sixteenth

[038-642] century accorded six, eight, or twelve hours according to individual

[038-643] taste or humour: some of them (amongst them Segni) allowed twenty-four

[038-644] hours, including the night as particularly propitious to assassinations

[038-645] and the other acts of violence which usually form the plot of

[038-646] tragedies; others extended the limit to thirty-six or forty-eight

[038-647] hours. The last, and most curious, unity, that of place, was slowly

[038-648] developed by Castelvetro, Riccoboni and Scaliger until the Frenchman

[038-649] Jean de la Taille joined it as a third to the existing two in 1572, and

[038-650] in 1598 Angelo Ingegneri finally formulated it more explicitly.

[038-651] *Poetics of the kinds and rules. Scaliger.*

[038-652] The Italian treatises were widely read and regarded as authoritative

[038-653] all over Europe, and awakened the first effort towards a learned theory

[038-654] of poetry in France, Spain. [[england|England]] and Germany. A good representative

[038-655] of his class is Julius Cæsar Scaliger, who has been considered, with

[038-656] some exaggeration, as the true founder of French pseudo-classicism or

[038-657] neo-classicism; as one who (it has been said) "laid the first stone of

[038-658] the classical Bastille." But if he was neither the first nor the only

[038-659] one, he certainly helped greatly to reduce "to a system of doctrines

[038-660] the principal consequences of the sovranty of Reason in works of

[038-661] literature," with his minute distinctions and classifications of kinds,

[038-662] the insurmountable barriers he erected between them, and his distrust

[038-663] of free inspiration and imagination.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_7_793) Scaliger numbers among his

[038-664] descendants (beside Daniel Heinsius) d'Aubignac, Rapin, Dacier and

[038-665] other tyrants of French literature and drama: Boileau turned the rules

[038-666] of neo-classicism into neat verses.

[038-667] *[[g-e-lessing|Lessing]].*

[038-668] It has been noticed that Lessing entered the same field; his opposition

[038-669] to the French rules (which was an opposition of rule to rule, in which

[038-670] he had been forestalled by Italian writers, for example by Calepio in

[038-671] 1732) is anything but radical. Lessing maintained that Corneille and

[038-672] other authors had misinterpreted Aristotle, to whose laws even the

[038-673] Shakespearian drama could be shown to conform;[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_8_794) but on the other

[038-674] hand he strongly opposed the abolition of all rules and those who

[038-675] shouted "genius, genius," placing genius above the law and saying that

[038-676] genius makes the law. For the very reason that genius is law, replied

[038-677] Lessing, laws have their value and can be determined: negation of them

[038-678] would entail the confinement of genius to its first trial flights,

[038-679] making example or practice useless.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_9_795)

[038-680] *Compromises and extensions.*

[038-681] But the "kinds" and their "limits" could be maintained for centuries

[038-682] solely by means of infinitely subtle interpretations, analogical

[038-683] extensions and more or less concealed compromises. The Italian

[038-684] Renaissance critics, while working at their Poetics in the style of

[038-685] Aristotle, found themselves confronted with chivalric poetry, and had

[038-686] to make the best of it; this they did by assigning it to a kind of poem

[038-687] not foreseen by antiquity (Giraldi Cintio).[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_10_796) Here and there indeed

[038-688] a rigorist was heard protesting that romances were in no way different

[038-689] from heroic poetry, and were only "badly written heroics" (Salviati).

[038-690] And since it was impossible to deny a place in Italian literature to

[038-691] Dante's poem, Iacopo Mazzoni, in his *Defence of Dante,* overhauled

[038-692] once more the categories of Poetics in order to find a niche for the

[038-693] sacred poem.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_11_797) Farces made their appearance at this time, and Cecchi

[038-694] (1585) declares "Farce is a third novelty, occupying a place between

[038-695] tragedy and comedy ..."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_12_798) The *Pastor fido* of Guarini was published,

[038-696] neither tragedy nor comedy, but tragicomedy; and discovering no heading

[038-697] among the kinds deduced from moral or civil philosophy suitable for

[038-698] the intruder, Jason de Nores proceeded to rule it out of existence;

[038-699] Guarini made a valiant defence and claimed special protection for his

[038-700] beloved *Pastor* under a third, or mixed, style, representative of real

[038-701] life.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_13_799) Another rigorist, Fioretti (Udeno Nisieli) proclaimed the

[038-702] poem "a poetic monster, so huge and deformed that centaurs, hippogriffs

[038-703] and chimæras are comparatively graceful and charming ..., fit to bring

[038-704] a blush to the cheek of the muse, a disgrace to poetry, a mixture of

[038-705] ingredients in themselves discordant, inimical and incompatible";[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_14_800)

[038-706] but will this bluster drive the delicious *Pastor fido* from the hands

[038-707] of lovers of poetry? The same thing occurred in the case of Marino's

[038-708] *Adone,* described by Chapelain as "a poem of peace" for want of

[038-709] a better definition, though other supporters called it "a new form

[038-710] of epic poem";[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_15_801) and the same thing happened again in the case of

[038-711] the comedy of art and musical drama. Corneille, who had called down

[038-712] a furious tempest from Scudéry and the Academicians on the head of

[038-713] his *Cid,* remarked in his discourse on Tragedy, though basing his

[038-714] position on that of Aristotle, that there was necessity for "*quelque

[038-715] modération, quelque favorable interprétation,... pour n'être pas

[038-716] obligés de condamner beaucoup de poèmes, que nous avons vu réussir sur

[038-717] nos théâtres." "Il est aisé de nous accommoder avec Aristote*..."[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_16_802)

[038-718] he says in another place: a piece of literary hypocrisy which startles

[038-719] by its verbal resemblance to "*les accommodements avec le Ciel*" of

[038-720] the Tartuffian ethics. The following century saw the accepted kinds

[038-721] augmented by "bourgeois tragedy" and pathetic comedy, nicknamed

[038-722] "lachrymose" by its enemies; de Chassiron[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_17_803) attacked, and Diderot,

[038-723] Gellert and Lessing[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_18_804) defended the new arrival. In this way the

[038-724] schematism of the kinds continued to suffer violence and to cut a very

[038-725] poor figure; nevertheless, in spite of adversity, it made every effort

[038-726] to retain power even at the sacrifice of dignity: just as an absolute

[038-727] king turns constitutional by force of circumstance, and chooses the

[038-728] lesser evil of squaring his divine right with the will of the nation.

[038-729] *Rebellion against rules in general.*

[038-730] This retention of power would have been more difficult had any success

[038-731] attended the attempts at rebellion against all laws, against law in

[038-732] general, which broke out in varying degrees at the end of the sixteenth

[038-733] century. Pietro Aretino made mock of the most sacred precepts: in a

[038-734] prologue to one of his comedies he remarks derisively, "If you see more

[038-735] than five characters on the stage at once, do not laugh; for chains

[038-736] which would fasten water-mills to the river could not hold the fools of

[038-737] to-day."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_19_805)

[038-738] *G. Bruno. Guarini.*

[038-739] A philosopher, Giordano Bruno, entered the lists against the

[038-740] "regulators of poetry": rules, said he, are derived from poetry: "there

[038-741] are as many genera and species of true rules as there are genera and

[038-742] species of true poets"; such an individualization of kinds dealt them

[038-743] a deathblow. "How then" (asks the interlocutory opponent) "shall

[038-744] veritable poets be recognized?" "By their singing of verse" (answers

[038-745] Bruno); "of that which, being sung, either delights or instructs, or

[038-746] delights and instructs at the same time."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_20_806) In much the same way

[038-747] Guarini defended his *Pastor fido* in 1588, declaring "the world is the

[038-748] judge of poets; against its sentence there is no appeal."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_21_807)

[038-749] *Spanish critics.*

[038-750] Amongst European countries, Spain was perhaps the sturdiest in her

[038-751] resistance to the pedantic theories of the writers of treatises;

[038-752] Spain was the land of freedom in criticism from Vives to Feijóo, from

[038-753] the sixteenth to the middle of the eighteenth century when decadence

[038-754] of the old Spanish spirit allowed Luzán, with others, to introduce

[038-755] neo-classical poetry of Italian and French origin.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_22_808) That rules

[038-756] must change with the times and with actual conditions; that modern

[038-757] literature demands modern poetics; that work carried out contrary to

[038-758] established rule does not signify that it is contrary to all rule

[038-759] or unwilling to submit itself to a higher law; that nature should

[038-760] give, not receive, laws; that the laws of the three unities are

[038-761] as ridiculous as it would be to forbid a painter to paint a large

[038-762] landscape in a small picture; that the pleasure, taste, approbation of

[038-763] readers and spectators are the deciding element in the long run; that

[038-764] notwithstanding the laws of counterpoint, the ear is the true judge of

[038-765] music; these affirmations and many like them are frequent in Spanish

[038-766] criticism of the period. One critic, Francisco de la Barreda (1622),

[038-767] went so far as to compassionate the strong wits of Italy bound by fear

[038-768] and cowardice (*temerosos y acobardados*) to rules that hampered them

[038-769] on every side;[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_23_809) he may have been thinking of Tasso, a memorable case

[038-770] of such degradation. Lope de Vega wavered between neglect of rules in

[038-771] practice, and obsequious acceptance of them in theory, alleging in

[038-772] excuse for his conduct that he was forced to yield to the demands of

[038-773] the public who paid money to see his plays; he said, "when I write my

[038-774] comedies, I lock and double-lock the door against the precept-mongers,

[038-775] that they may not rise up and bear witness against me"; "Art (that is,

[038-776] Poetics) speaks truth which is contradicted by the vulgar ignorant";

[038-777] "may the rules forgive us when we are induced to violate them."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_24_810) But

[038-778] a contemporary admirer of Lope's work writes of him that "*en muchas

[038-779] partes de sus escritos dice que el no guardar el arte antiguo lo hace

[038-780] por conformarse con el gusto de la plebe ... dicelo por su natural

[038-781] modestia, y porqué no atribuya la malicia ignorante à arrogancia lo que

[038-782] es politica perfeccion.*"[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_25_811)

[038-783] *G. B. Marino.*

[038-784] Giambattista Marino also protested "I assert that I have a more

[038-785] thorough knowledge of the rules than have all the pedants in the

[038-786] world; but the only true rule is to know how to break the rules at the

[038-787] right place and time, and to conform with the custom and taste of the

[038-788] day."[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_26_812) The drama of Spain, the comedy of art, and other literary

[038-789] novelties of the seventeenth century caused Minturno, Castelvetro and

[038-790] other rigid treatise-writers of the preceding century to be looked at

[038-791] with contemptuous pity as "antiquaries"; this may be seen in Andrea

[038-792] Perucci (1699), the theorist of improvised comedy.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_27_813) Pallavicino

[038-793] criticized the writers on "the disciplines of beautiful speech" on

[038-794] the ground that they "generally base their precepts on observing by

[038-795] experience what things in writers give pleasure, rather than pointing

[038-796] out what would naturally conform to the particular affections and

[038-797] instincts implanted by the Creator in the souls of men."[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_28_814)

[038-798] *G. V. Gravina.*

[038-799] A note of distrust towards the fixed kinds may be heard in the

[038-800] *Discorso sull' Endimione* (1691), wherein Gravina severely blames

[038-801] the "ambitious and miserly precepts" of rhetoricians, and makes the

[038-802] penetrating comment: "No work can see the fight without finding itself

[038-803] confronted by a tribunal of critics specially convened to examine it,

[038-804] and questioned firstly as to its name and nature. Next begins the

[038-805] action which lawyers call prejudicial, and controversy arises as to

[038-806] its status, whether it is a poem, a romance, a tragedy, a comedy, or

[038-807] another of the prescribed kinds. And if the said work have ignored the

[038-808] slightest precept ... they decree forthwith its exile and perpetual

[038-809] banishment. And yet, however they recast and expand their aphorisms,

[038-810] they will never be able to include all the different kinds that can

[038-811] be freshly created by the varied and ceaseless motion of human wit.

[038-812] For this reason I cannot see why we should not free ourselves from

[038-813] this insolent curb on the soaring grandeur of our imaginations, and

[038-814] allow them to follow an open road amongst those immeasurable spaces

[038-815] they are fitted to explore." He remarks on the work of Guidi which

[038-816] forms the subject of his discourse, "I know not whether it be tragedy,

[038-817] comedy, tragicomedy, or anything else invented by rhetoricians. It is a

[038-818] representation of the loves of Endymion and Diana. If those terms have

[038-819] sufficient breadth of extension, they will comprehend this work; if

[038-820] they have not, let another be framed (a power which may be granted to

[038-821] any one in so unimportant a matter); if no such term can be invented,

[038-822] let us not, for want of a word, deprive ourselves of a thing so

[038-823] beautiful."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_29_815) These remarks have quite a modern ring, but Gravina can

[038-824] hardly have thought out their implications very deeply, for later on he

[038-825] wrote a special treatise on the rules of the tragic kind.[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_30_816) Antonio

[038-826] Conti too declared at times his antagonism towards the rules, but he

[038-827] referred to the Aristotelian rules only.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_31_817)

[038-828] *Fr. Montani.*

[038-829] More courage was displayed by Count Francesco Montani of Pesaro in the

[038-830] polemic roused by Orsi's book against Bouhours; in 1705 he wrote: "I

[038-831] know that there are immutable and eternal rules, founded on such sound

[038-832] good sense and solid reason as will remain unshaken as long as mankind

[038-833] lives. But these rules, whose incorruptibility gives them authority to

[038-834] guide our spirits to the end of time, are rare enough to be counted

[038-835] with the nose, and it seems to me somewhat arbitrary to claim to

[038-836] test and regulate our new works by old laws now wholly abrogated and

[038-837] annulled."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_32_818)

[038-838] *Critics of the eighteenth century.*

[038-839] In France the rigorism of Boileau was followed by the rebellion of Du

[038-840] Bos, who unhesitatingly declared that "men will always prefer poetry

[038-841] which moves them to that composed according to rule,"[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_33_819) and the like

[038-842] heresies. In 1730, De la Motte made war against the unities of time

[038-843] and place, asserting as the most general, and even superior to that of

[038-844] action, the unity of interest.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_34_820) Batteux tended to make free with

[038-845] the rules; and Voltaire, though he opposed De la Motte and declared

[038-846] the three unities to be the "three great laws of good sense," uttered

[038-847] some bold sentiments in his *Essay on Epic Poetry,* and it was he who

[038-848] remarked that "*tous les genres sont bons hors le genre ennuyeux,*"

[038-849] and that the best kind is "*celui qui est le mieux traité.*" Diderot

[038-850] was in certain respects a forerunner of Romanticism, and with him must

[038-851] be mentioned Friedrich Melchior Grimm, who was influenced by him. A

[038-852] breath of liberty was wafted into Italy by Metastasio, Bettinelli,

[038-853] Baretti and Cesarotti: in 1766 Buonafede notes in his *Epistola della

[038-854] libertà poetica* that when erudite persons "define epic poetry, or

[038-855] comedy, or odes, they ought to frame as many definitions as there

[038-856] are compositions and authors."[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_35_821) In Germany the first to rise in

[038-857] rebellion against the rules (opposing Gottsched and his disciples)

[038-858] were the representatives of the Swiss school.[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_36_822) In England, after

[038-859] examining the definitions by which critics endeavoured to distinguish

[038-860] epic poetry from other compositions, Home wrote, "It affords no little

[038-861] diversion to watch so many profound critics hunting after that which

[038-862] does not exist. They presuppose—without shadow of proof—that there

[038-863] exists a precise criterion by which to distinguish epic poetry from

[038-864] all other kinds of composition. But literary compositions melt one

[038-865] into another like colours: and if in their stronger shades it is easy

[038-866] to recognize them, they are susceptible of such variety and of so many

[038-867] different forms that it is impossible to say where one ends and another

[038-868] begins."[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_37_823)

[038-869] *Romanticism and the "strict kinds": Berchet, V. Hugo.*

[038-870] Literary thought between the late eighteenth and the first decades of

[038-871] the nineteenth century, that is to say from" the period of genius"

[038-872] to that of romanticism properly so called, rose in rebellion against

[038-873] separate individual rules and against all rules as such. But to

[038-874] describe the battles fought, and their more important episodes; to

[038-875] recount the names of captains victorious or discomfited, or to deplore

[038-876] the excesses committed by the conquerors, is no part of our present

[038-877] task. Upon the ruins of the strict kinds, the "*genres tranchés*"

[038-878] beloved by Napoleon[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_38_824) (a Romanticist in the art of war, but a

[038-879] Classicist in poetry), flourished the drama, the romance and every

[038-880] other mixed kind: upon the ruins of the three unities, flourished the

[038-881] unity of *ensemble.* Italy made her protest against rules of style in

[038-882] Berchet's famous *Lettera semiseria di Grisostomo* (1816); and France

[038-883] made hers somewhat later in Victor Hugo's preface to *Cromwell* (1827).

[038-884] Henceforth men discussed not the kinds, but Art. What is the unity of

[038-885] *ensemble* but the demand of art itself, which is always an *ensemble,*

[038-886] a synthesis? What else is the principle, introduced by August Wilhelm

[038-887] Schlegel and adopted by Manzoni and other Italian romanticists, to the

[038-888] effect that form of component parts must be "organic not mechanical,

[038-889] resulting from the nature of the subject and its interior development

[038-890] ... not from the impress of an external and extraneous stamp"?[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_39_825)

[038-891] *Their persistence in philosophical theories.*

[038-892] But it would be quite wrong to suppose that this victory over the

[038-893] rhetoric of kinds was either the cause or the consequence of a final

[038-894] victory over its philosophical presuppositions. In pure theory, none

[038-895] of the critics above named wholly abandoned the kinds and the rules.

[038-896] Berchet admitted four elementary forms, that is four fundamental

[038-897] kinds, in poetry; lyrical, didactic, epic and dramatic, claiming for

[038-898] the poet only the right of "uniting and fusing together the elementary

[038-899] forms in a thousand fashions."[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_40_826) Manzoni's only real quarrel was

[038-900] with those rules "founded on special facts instead of on general

[038-901] principles; on the authority of rhetoricians instead of reason."[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_41_827)

[038-902] Even De Sanctis was satisfied with a concept somewhat vague, though

[038-903] true enough at bottom: "the most important rules are not those capable

[038-904] of being applied to every content, but those which draw their force *ex

[038-905] visceribus caussæ,* from the very heart of the content itself."[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_42_828)

[038-906] Even more diverting than the spectacle which had delighted Home, is

[038-907] the sight of German philosophy according the honour of a dialectical

[038-908] deduction to the empirical classification of kinds. We shall give two

[038-909] examples, each representing one extreme end of the chain:

[038-910] *Fr. Schelling.*

[038-911] Schelling at the beginning of the century (1803), and Hartmann at

[038-912] the end (1890). One section of Schelling's *Philosophy of Art* is

[038-913] devoted to "the construction of individual poetic kinds"; in it he

[038-914] remarks that were he to follow the historical order, Epic would come

[038-915] first; whereas in the scientific order the Lyric occupies the first

[038-916] place: indeed, if poetry is the representation of the infinite in

[038-917] the finite, the Lyric, in which difference prevails (the finite, the

[038-918] subject), is its first moment, corresponding with the first power of

[038-919] the ideal series, reflexion, knowledge, consciousness, whereas Epic

[038-920] corresponds with the second power, action.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_43_829) From Epic, which is *par

[038-921] excellence* the objective kind (as being the identity of subjective and

[038-922] objective), derive the Elegy and the Idyl if subjectivity be placed in

[038-923] the object and objectivity in the poet: if objectivity be placed in

[038-924] the object and subjectivity in the poet, didactic poetry results.[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_44_830)

[038-925] To these differentiations of the Epic, Schelling adds the romantic or

[038-926] modern Epic, the poem of chivalry; the novel; and the experiments in

[038-927] an epic of ordinary life such as the *Luisa* of Voss and the *Hermann

[038-928] and Dorothea* of Goethe; and, co-ordinate with all the foregoing, the

[038-929] *Comedia* of Dante, "an epic kind in itself" (*eine epische Gattung

[038-930] für sich*). Finally, from the union on a higher plane of Lyric with

[038-931] Epic, liberty with necessity, arises the third form, the Drama, the

[038-932] reconciliation of antitheses in a totality, "supreme incarnation of the

[038-933] essence and the in-itself of all art."[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_45_831)

[038-934] *E. von Hartmann.*

[038-935] In Hartmann's *Philosophy of the Beautiful,* poetry is divided into

[038-936] spoken poetry and read poetry. The former is subdivided into Epic,

[038-937] Lyric and Dramatic, with further subdivisions of Epic into plastic

[038-938] Epic, or strictly epic Epic, and pictorial or lyrical Epic; of Lyric

[038-939] into epical Lyric, lyrical Lyric and dramatic Lyric; of Dramatic into

[038-940] lyrical Drama, epic Drama and dramatic Drama. Read poetry (*Lese

[038-941] poesie*) is again subdivided into predominantly epical, lyrical or

[038-942] dramatic form with tertiary partitions of the affecting, the comic, the

[038-943] tragic and humorous; and into poems "to be read at a sitting" (like the

[038-944] short story) or to be taken up again and again (like the novel).[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_46_832)

[038-945] *The kinds in the schools.*

[038-946] Without these highly philosophical trivialities the divisions of kinds

[038-947] still wander through the books called *Institutions of Literature,*

[038-948] written by philologists and men of letters, and the ordinary

[038-949] school-books of Italy, France and Germany; and psychologists and

[038-950] philosophers still persist in writing about the Æsthetic of the tragic,

[038-951] of the comic and of the humorous.[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_47_833) The objectivity of literary kinds

[038-952] is frankly maintained by Ferdinand Brunetière, who looks on literary

[038-953] history as "the evolution of kinds,"[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_48_834) and gives sharply defined form

[038-954] to a superstition which, seldom confessed so truthfully or applied so

[038-955] rigorously, survives to contaminate modern literary history.[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-27.htm.html#Footnote_49_835)

#### III

##### THE THEORY OF THE LIMITS OF THE ARTS

[038-1028] To Lessing must be ascribed the merit and the sole glory of having

[038-1029] discovered that every art has its special character and inviolable

[038-1030] limits. But his merit lies not in his own theory, which, in itself,

[038-1031] is scarcely tenable,[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_1_836) but in having, though by an error, aroused

[038-1032] discussion of a highly important æsthetical point till then wholly

[038-1033] overlooked. After some slight notice from Du Bos and Batteux, some

[038-1034] preparation of the field by Diderot[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_2_837) and Mendelssohn,[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_3_838) and

[038-1035] long disquisitions by Meier and other Wolffians upon natural and

[038-1036] conventional symbols,[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_4_839) Lessing was the first to raise clearly the

[038-1037] question of the value attaching to the distinction between the various

[038-1038] arts. Antiquity, the Middle Ages and the Renaissance had enumerated

[038-1039] the arts according to denominations of current phraseology, and had

[038-1040] composed numbers of technical hand-books distinguishing major and

[038-1041] minor arts; but in Aristoxenus or Vitruvius, Marchetto da Padova or

[038-1042] Cennino Cennini, Leonardo da Vinci or Leon Battista Alberti, Palladio

[038-1043] or Scamozzi, it would be vain to look for the problem proposed by

[038-1044] Lessing, for the spirit of these technical treatise-writers is entirely

[038-1045] different. Some rudiments of the question may be detected in the

[038-1046] comparisons made, and the questions of precedence raised, between

[038-1047] poetry and painting or painting and sculpture, to be found now and

[038-1048] then in stray paragraphs of their books (Leonardo da Vinci pressed

[038-1049] the claims of painting, Michæl Angelo those of sculpture): the theme

[038-1050] eventually became a favourite one for academic discussion, and was not

[038-1051] despised by Galileo himself.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_5_840)

[038-1052] *The limits of the arts in Lessing. Arts of space and arts

[038-1053] of time.*

[038-1054] Lessing was induced to raise the question in the attempt to controvert

[038-1055] the strange views of Spence concerning the close union between painting

[038-1056] and poetry among the ancients, and of Count Caylus, who held that

[038-1057] the excellence of a poem must be judged by the number of subjects

[038-1058] it offers to the brush of the painter. He was further instigated by

[038-1059] the comparisons between poetry and painting upon which were commonly

[038-1060] founded the most ridiculous rules for tragedy: the maxim *Ut pictura

[038-1061] poësis,* whose original motive was to emphasize the representative or

[038-1062] imaginative character of poetry, and the community of nature among the

[038-1063] arts, had been converted by superficial interpretation into a defence

[038-1064] of the most vicious intellectualistic and realistic prejudices. Lessing

[038-1065] argued in this wise: "If painting in its imitations employs precisely

[038-1066] a medium or symbol different from that of poetry (the former employing

[038-1067] spatial forms and colours, the latter temporal articulated sounds),

[038-1068] since the symbol must certainly be in close relation with that

[038-1069] which is signified, coexistent symbols can only express coexistent

[038-1070] objects or parts of objects, and consecutive symbols can only express

[038-1071] consecutive objects or parts of objects. Objects mutually coexistent,

[038-1072] or having mutually coexistent parts, are called bodies. Bodies, then,

[038-1073] through their quality of visibility, are the true objects of painting.

[038-1074] Objects successively consecutive amongst themselves, or whose parts

[038-1075] are consecutive, are called in general actions. Actions, then, are

[038-1076] the suitable objects of poetry." Painting, undoubtedly, may represent

[038-1077] action, but only by means of bodies which indicate it; and poetry may

[038-1078] represent bodies, but only by indicating them by means of actions.

[038-1079] When a poet using language, *i.e.* arbitrary symbols, sets himself

[038-1080] to describe bodies, he is no longer a poet but a prose-writer, since

[038-1081] a true poet only describes bodies by the effect they produce on the

[038-1082] soul.[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_6_841) Retouching and developing this distinction, Lessing described

[038-1083] action or movement in a picture as an addition made by the imagination

[038-1084] of the beholder; so true is this, says he, that animals perceive

[038-1085] nothing save immobility in a picture. He further studied the various

[038-1086] unions of arbitrary with natural symbols, such as that of poetry with

[038-1087] music (in which the former is subordinate to the latter), of music

[038-1088] with dancing, of poetry with dancing, and of music and poetry with

[038-1089] dancing (union of arbitrary consecutive audible symbols with natural

[038-1090] visible symbols): of the pantomime of antiquity (union of arbitrary

[038-1091] consecutive visible symbols with natural consecutive visible symbols):

[038-1092] of the language of the dumb (the only art that employs arbitrary

[038-1093] consecutive visible symbols): and, lastly, of imperfect unions, such

[038-1094] as that of painting with poetry. If not every use to which language is

[038-1095] put is poetic, Lessing holds that not every use of natural coexistent

[038-1096] signs is pictorial: painting, like language, has its prose. Prosaic

[038-1097] painters are those who represent consecutive objects notwithstanding

[038-1098] the character of coexistence in their signs, allegorical painters

[038-1099] those who make arbitrary use of natural signs, and those who pretend

[038-1100] to represent the invisible or the audible by means of the visible.

[038-1101] Desirous of preserving the naturalness of symbolism, Lessing ended by

[038-1102] condemning the custom of painting objects on a diminished scale, and

[038-1103] concludes: "I think that the aim of an art should be that only to which

[038-1104] it is specially adapted, not that which can be performed equally well

[038-1105] by other arts. I find in Plutarch a comparison which illustrates this

[038-1106] admirably: he who would split wood with a key and open the door with an

[038-1107] axe not only spoils both utensils but deprives himself of the unity of

[038-1108] each alike."[7]

[038-1109] *Limits and classifications of the arts in later

[038-1110] philosophy.*

[038-1111] The principle of limitations or of the specific character of individual

[038-1112] arts, as laid down by Lessing, occupied the attention of philosophers

[038-1113] in later days, who, without discussing the principle itself, employed

[038-1114] it in classifying the arts and arranging them in series.

[038-1115] *Herder and Kant.*

[038-1116] Herder here and there continued Lessing's examination in his fragment

[038-1117] on *Plastic* (1769);[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_8_842) Heydenreich wrote a treatise (1790) on the

[038-1118] limits of the six arts (music, dance, figurative arts gardening,

[038-1119] poetry and representative art), and criticized the *clavecin oculaire*

[038-1120] of Father Castel, a contrivance for the combination of colours which

[038-1121] should act in the same way as the series of musical notes in harmony

[038-1122] and melody,[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_9_843) Kant appealed to the analogy of a speaking man, and

[038-1123] classified the arts according to speech, gesture and tone as arts of

[038-1124] speech, figurative arts, and arts producing a mere play of sensations

[038-1125] (mimicry and colouring).[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_10_844)

[038-1126] *Schelling.*

[038-1127] Schelling differentiated the artistic identity according as it

[038-1128] consisted in the infusion of the infinite into the finite, or of the

[038-1129] finite into the infinite (ideal art or real art): into poetry and art

[038-1130] proper. Under the heading of real arts he included the figurative arts,

[038-1131] music, painting, plastic (which comprehended architecture, bas-relief

[038-1132] and sculpture): in the ideal series were the three corresponding forms

[038-1133] of poetry, lyrical, epical and dramatic.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_11_845)

[038-1134] *Solger.*

[038-1135] With a similar method, Solger placed poetry, the universal art,

[038-1136] side by side with art strictly so called, which is either symbolical

[038-1137] (sculpture) or allegorical (painting), and, in either case, is a union

[038-1138] of concepts and bodies: if you take corporality without concept, you

[038-1139] have architecture; if concept without matter, music.[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_12_846) Hegel makes

[038-1140] poetry the bond of union between the two extremes of figurative art and

[038-1141] of music.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_13_847)

[038-1142] *[[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]].*

[038-1143] We have already seen how Schopenhauer destroyed the accepted

[038-1144] limitations of art and built them up again, following the order of

[038-1145] the ideas which they represent.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_14_848) [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]] clung to Lessing's two

[038-1146] groups, simultaneous arts and successive arts, and defined the former

[038-1147] as "permitting themselves to be inspected from every side," the latter

[038-1148] as "rejecting complete investigation and remaining in semi-darkness":

[038-1149] in the first group he placed architecture, plastic, church music and

[038-1150] classical poetry; in the second ornamental gardening, painting, secular

[038-1151] music and romantic poetry.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_15_849)

[038-1152] *Herbart.*

[038-1153] Herbart was implacable against those who look in one art for the

[038-1154] perfections of another; who "look on music as a sort of painting,

[038-1155] painting as poetry, poetry as an elevated plastic and plastic as

[038-1156] a species of æsthetic philosophy,"[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_16_850) while admitting that a

[038-1157] concrete work of art, such as a picture, may contain elements of the

[038-1158] picturesque, the poetic and other kinds, held together by the skill of

[038-1159] the artist.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_17_851)

[038-1160] *Weisse. Zeising.*

[038-1161] Weisse divided the arts into three triads, intended to recall the

[038-1162] nine Muses.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_18_852) Zeising invented-a cross-division into figurative

[038-1163] arts (architecture, sculpture, painting), musical arts (instrumental

[038-1164] music, song, poetry), and arts of mimicry (dance, musical mimicry,

[038-1165] representative art), and into macrocosmic arts (architecture,

[038-1166] instrumental music, dance), microcosmic arts (sculpture, song, musical

[038-1167] mimicry) and historical arts (painting, poetry and representative

[038-1168] art).[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_19_853)

[038-1169] *Vischer.*

[038-1170] Vischer classified them according to the three forms of

[038-1171] imagination (figurative, sensuous and poetic), into objective arts

[038-1172] (architecture, plastic and painting), a subjective art (music) and an

[038-1173] objective-subjective art[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_20_854) (poetry). Gerber proposed to recognize a

[038-1174] special "art of language" (*Sprachkunst*), distinguishable alike from

[038-1175] prose and poetry and consisting in the expression of simple movements

[038-1176] of the soul. Such an art would correspond with plastic in the following

[038-1177] scheme: arts of the eye—(*a)* architecture, (*b*) plastic, (c)

[038-1178] painting; arts of the ear—(*a)* prose, (*b)* the art of language, (c)

[038-1179] poetry.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_21_855)

[038-1180] *M. Schasler.*

[038-1181] The two most recent systems of classification are furnished by Schasler

[038-1182] and Hartmann, who have also submitted the schemes of their predecessors

[038-1183] to searching criticism. Schasler[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_22_856) arranges the arts in two groups,

[038-1184] adopting the criterion of simultaneity and succession: the arts of

[038-1185] simultaneity are architecture, plastic and painting; of succession,

[038-1186] music, mimicry and poetry. He says that by following the series in

[038-1187] the order indicated, it will be seen that simultaneity, originally

[038-1188] predominant, yields place to succession, which predominates in the

[038-1189] second group and subordinates without wholly displacing the other.

[038-1190] Parallel with this, another division is evolved, deduced from the

[038-1191] relation between the ideal and material elements in each separate art,

[038-1192] between movement and repose; which begins with architecture "materially

[038-1193] the heaviest, spiritually the lightest of all the arts," and ends

[038-1194] with poetry, in which the opposite relation is observed. Curious

[038-1195] analogies are established by this method between the first and second

[038-1196] group of arts: between architecture and music; between plastic and

[038-1197] mimicry; between painting in its three forms of landscape, *genre* and

[038-1198] historical, and poetry in its three forms of lyric (declamatory), epic

[038-1199] (rhapsodic) and drama (representative).

[038-1200] *E. v. Hartmann.*

[038-1201] Hartmann[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_23_857) divides the arts into arts of perception and arts of

[038-1202] imagination: the former tripartite into spatial or visual (plastic

[038-1203] and painting), temporal or auditory (instrumental music, linguistic

[038-1204] mimicry, expressive song) and temporal-spatial or mimic (pantomime,

[038-1205] mimic dances, art of the actor, art of the opera-singer); the second

[038-1206] contains but one single species, which is poetry. Architecture,

[038-1207] decoration, gardening, cosmetic and prosewriting are excluded from this

[038-1208] system of classification and lumped together as non-free arts.

[038-1209] *The supreme art. Richard Wagner.*

[038-1210] Parallel with this search for a classification of the arts, the same

[038-1211] philosophers were led into the quest of the supreme art. Some favoured

[038-1212] poetry, others music or sculpture; others again claimed the supremacy

[038-1213] for combined arts, especially for Opera, according to the theory of

[038-1214] it already advanced in the eighteenth century[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_24_858) and maintained

[038-1215] and developed in our day by Richard Wagner.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_25_859) One of the latest

[038-1216] philosophers to raise the question "whether single arts, or arts in

[038-1217] combination, had the greater value," concluded that single arts as such

[038-1218] possess their own perfection, yet the perfection of united arts is

[038-1219] still greater, notwithstanding the compromises and mutual concessions

[038-1220] enforced upon them by their union; that single arts, from another

[038-1221] point of view, have the greater value; and lastly, that both single

[038-1222] and combined arts are necessary to the realisation of the concept of

[038-1223] art.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_26_860)

[038-1224] *Lotze's attack on classifications.*

[038-1225] The capriciousness, emptiness and childishness of such problems

[038-1226] and their solutions must have excited feelings of impatience and

[038-1227] disgust, but we rarely find a doubt thrown on their validity. One such

[038-1228] dissentient is Lotze when he writes: "It is difficult to see the use

[038-1229] of such attempts. Knowledge of the nature and laws of individual arts

[038-1230] is but little increased by indication of the systematic place allotted

[038-1231] to each." He further observed that in real life the arts are variously

[038-1232] conjoined, forming themselves into no systematic series, while in

[038-1233] the world of thought an immense variety of orders can be created; he

[038-1234] therefore selected one of these possible orders, not because it was

[038-1235] the sole legitimate one, but because it was convenient (*bequem*). His

[038-1236] series begins with music, "the art of free beauty, determined only by

[038-1237] the laws of its matter, not by conditions imposed by a given task of

[038-1238] purpose or of imitation"; followed by architecture, "which no longer

[038-1239] plays freely with forms, but subjects them to the service of an end";

[038-1240] and then by sculpture, painting and poetry, excluding minor arts which

[038-1241] cannot be co-ordinated with the others, since they are incapable of

[038-1242] expressing with any approach to completeness the totality of the

[038-1243] spiritual life.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_27_861) A recent French critic, Basch, opens his treatise

[038-1244] with the following excellent remarks: "Is it necessary to show there is

[038-1245] no such thing as an absolute art, differentiating itself later by means

[038-1246] of one knows not what immanent laws? What exists is the particular

[038-1247] forms of art, or rather artists who have striven to translate, as best

[038-1248] they can, according to the material means at their command, the song of

[038-1249] the ideal in their souls." But later on he thinks it possible to effect

[038-1250] a division of the arts by starting "from the artist, instead of the

[038-1251] art in itself," by proceeding "according to the three great types of

[038-1252] [[imagination|fancy]], visual, motor and auditory"; and as for the debated point of the

[038-1253] supreme art, he thinks it must be settled in favour of music.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_28_862)

[038-1254] Schasler is not altogether wrong in his spirited counterattack on

[038-1255] Lotze's criticism; he protests against the principle of indifference

[038-1256] and convenience, and remarks that "the classification of the arts

[038-1257] must be regarded as the real touchstone, the real differential test

[038-1258] of the scientific value of an æsthetic system; for on this point all

[038-1259] theoretical questions are concentrated and crowd together to find a

[038-1260] concrete solution."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_29_863)

[038-1261] *Contradictions in Lotze.*

[038-1262] The principle of convenience may be excellent as applied to the

[038-1263] approximative grouping of botanical or zoological classifications, but

[038-1264] it has no place in philosophy; and as Lotze, in common with Schasler

[038-1265] and other æstheticians, conformed to Lessing's principle of the

[038-1266] constancy, limits and peculiar nature of each art, and therefore held

[038-1267] that the concepts of the individual arts were speculative and not

[038-1268] empirical concepts, he could not evade the duty of fixing the mutual

[038-1269] relations of these concepts, arranging them in series, subordinating

[038-1270] and co-ordinating them, and arriving at each of them either deductively

[038-1271] or dialectically. He ought, in order to get definitely rid of these

[038-1272] barren attempts at classification and at discovering the supreme

[038-1273] art, to have criticized and dissolved Lessing's principle itself: to

[038-1274] keep the principle and deny the need for a classification, as Lotze

[038-1275] did, was obviously inconsistent. But not a single æsthetician has

[038-1276] ever re-examined or investigated the scientific foundation of the

[038-1277] distinctions enunciated by Lessing in his fluent and elegant prose; no

[038-1278] one has probed to the bottom the truth which was illumined by Aristotle

[038-1279] in a single lightning-flash, when he refused to allow an extrinsic

[038-1280] difference, that of metre, as the real distinction between prose and

[038-1281] poetry:[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_30_864) no one, that is to say, save perhaps Schleiermacher, who at

[038-1282] least called attention to the difficulties of the current doctrine.

[038-1283] *Doubts in Schleiermacher.*

[038-1284] He proposed to start from the general concept of art and prove by

[038-1285] deduction the necessity of all its forms; and after finding two sides

[038-1286] to artistic activity, the objective consciousness (*gegenständliche)*

[038-1287] and the immediate consciousness (*unmittelbare)*, and observing that

[038-1288] art stands wholly neither in the one nor in the other and that the

[038-1289] immediate consciousness or representation (*[[representation|Vorstellung]])* gives rise to

[038-1290] mimicry and music, while the objective consciousness or image (*Bild*)

[038-1291] gives rise to the figurative arts, he then, proceeding to analyse a

[038-1292] painting, found the two forms of consciousness to be in this case

[038-1293] inseparable, and remarks: "Here we arrive at the precise opposite:

[038-1294] searching for distinction, we find unity." Nor did the traditional

[038-1295] division of the arts into simultaneous and successive seem to him

[038-1296] very solid, for "when looked at attentively, it evaporates entirely";

[038-1297] in architecture or gardening, contemplation is successive, while in

[038-1298] the arts labelled as successive, such as poetry, the chief thing

[038-1299] is coexistence and grouping: "from whichever side we look at it,

[038-1300] the difference is but secondary and the antithesis between the two

[038-1301] orders of art merely means that every contemplation, like every act of

[038-1302] production, is always successive, but, in thinking out the relation of

[038-1303] the two sides in a work of art, both seem indispensable: coexistence

[038-1304] (*Zugleichsein*) and successive existence (*das Successivsein*)." In

[038-1305] another passage he observes: "The reality of art as external appearance

[038-1306] is conditioned by the mode, depending on our physical and corporeal

[038-1307] organism, in which the internal is externalised: movements, forms,

[038-1308] words.... That which is common to all arts is not the external, which

[038-1309] is rather the element of diversification." When these observations

[038-1310] are compared with the sharp distinction he himself drew between art

[038-1311] and technique, it would be easy to deduce that he held the partitions

[038-1312] of the arts and the concepts of the particular arts to be devoid

[038-1313] of æsthetic value. But Schleiermacher does not draw this logical

[038-1314] inference, he wavers and hesitates: he recognizes the inseparability

[038-1315] of the subjective and objective, musical and figurative, elements in

[038-1316] poetry, yet he struggles to discover the definitions and limits of

[038-1317] the individual arts; sometimes he dreams of a union of the various

[038-1318] arts from which a complete art would spring; and when composing the

[038-1319] syllabus of his lectures on Æsthetic, he arranged the arts into arts

[038-1320] of accompaniment (mimicry and music), figurative arts (architecture,

[038-1321] gardening, painting, sculpture) and poetry.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-28.htm.html#Footnote_31_865) Nebulous, vague,

[038-1322] contradictory as this may be, Schleiermacher had the acumen to distrust

[038-1323] the soundness of Lessing's theory and to inquire by what right

[038-1324] particular arts are singled out from art in general.

#### IV

##### OTHER PARTICULAR DOCTRINES

[038-1364] *The æsthetic theory of Natural [[beauty|Beauty]].*

[038-1365] I. Schleiermacher also rejected the concept of Natural Beauty, giving

[038-1366] Hegel greater praise than he deserved in the matter, because Hegel's

[038-1367] denial of this concept was, as we have seen, more verbal than real.

[038-1368] At all events, Schleiermacher's radical denial of the existence of a

[038-1369] natural beauty external to and independent of the human mind marked

[038-1370] a victory over a serious error, and appears to us imperfect and

[038-1371] one-sided only so far as it seems to exclude those æsthetic facts of

[038-1372] imagination which are attached to objects given in nature.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_1_866) Important

[038-1373] contributions towards the correction of this imperfect and one-sided

[038-1374] element were supplied by the historical and psychological study of the

[038-1375] "feeling for nature," promoted successfully by Alexander Humboldt in

[038-1376] his dissertation to be found in the second volume of *Cosmos*,[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_2_867) and

[038-1377] continued by Laprade, Biese, and others in our own time.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_3_868) In his

[038-1378] criticism of his own *Ästhetik,* Vischer completes the passage from

[038-1379] the metaphysical construction of beauty in nature to the psychological

[038-1380] interpretation of it, and recognizes the necessity of suppressing

[038-1381] the section devoted to Natural Beauty in his first æsthetic system,

[038-1382] and incorporating it with the doctrine of imagination: he says that

[038-1383] such treatments do not belong to æsthetic science, being a medley

[038-1384] of zoology, sentiment, fantasy and humour, worthy of development in

[038-1385] monographs in the style of the poet G. G. Fischer's on the life of

[038-1386] birds, or Bratranek's on the æsthetic of the vegetable world.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_4_869)

[038-1387] Hartmann, as heir of the old metaphysics, reproaches Vischer for

[038-1388] this exclusion, and maintains that, in addition to the beauty of

[038-1389] imagination introduced by man into natural things (*hineingelegte

[038-1390] Schönheit*), there exist a formal and a substantial beauty in nature,

[038-1391] coinciding with realisation of the immanent ends or ideas of nature.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_5_870)

[038-1392] But the way chosen ultimately by Vischer is the only one by which

[038-1393] Schleiermacher's thesis can be successfully developed so as to show

[038-1394] the precise meaning which may be given to the assertion of (æsthetic)

[038-1395] beauty in nature.

[038-1396] *The theory of æsthetic senses.*

[038-1397] II. That æsthetic senses or superior senses exist and that beauty

[038-1398] attaches to certain senses only, not to all, is a very old opinion. We

[038-1399] have seen already[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_6_871) that Socrates, in the *Hippias maior,* mentions

[038-1400] the doctrine of beauty as "that which pleases hearing and sight" (τὸ

[038-1401] καlὸν eστὶ τὸ δι' ἀκοῆs τε καὶ ὃψεως ήδύ): and he adds, it seems

[038-1402] impossible to deny that we take pleasure in looking at handsome men

[038-1403] and fine ornaments, pictures and statues with our eyes, and hearing

[038-1404] beautiful songs or beautiful voices, music, speeches and conversations

[038-1405] with our ears. Nevertheless Socrates himself in the same dialogue

[038-1406] confutes this theory by perfectly valid arguments, amongst which is

[038-1407] that, besides the difficulty arising from the fact that beautiful

[038-1408] things may be found outside the range of the sensible impressions of

[038-1409] eye and ear, there is no reason for creating a special class for the

[038-1410] pleasure arising from impressions on these two senses, to the exclusion

[038-1411] of others. He also states the more subtle and philosophical objection

[038-1412] that that which is pleasing to the sight is not so to the hearing, and

[038-1413] *vice versa*; whence it follows that the ground of beauty must not be

[038-1414] sought in visibility or audibility, but in something differing from

[038-1415] either and common to both.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_7_872)

[038-1416] The problem was never again, perhaps, attacked with such acumen and

[038-1417] seriousness as in this ancient dialogue. In the eighteenth century

[038-1418] Home remarked that beauty depended on sight, and that impressions

[038-1419] received by the other senses might be agreeable but were not

[038-1420] beautiful, and distinguished sight and hearing as superior to those

[038-1421] of touch, taste and smell, the latter being merely bodily in nature

[038-1422] and without the spiritual refinement of the other two. He held these

[038-1423] to produce pleasures superior to organic pleasures though inferior to

[038-1424] intellectual; decorous pleasures, that is to say; elevated, sweet,

[038-1425] moderately exhilarating; as far removed from the turbulence of the

[038-1426] passions as from the languor of indolence, and intended to refresh

[038-1427] and soothe the spirit.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_8_873) Following suggestions of Diderot, Rousseau

[038-1428] and Berkeley, Herder drew attention to the importance of the sense of

[038-1429] touch (*Gefühl*) in plastic art: of this "third sense, which perhaps

[038-1430] deserves to be investigated first of all, and is unjustly relegated to

[038-1431] a place amongst the grosser senses." Certainly "touch knows nothing of

[038-1432] surface or colour," but "sight, for its part, knows nothing of forms

[038-1433] and configurations." Thus "touch cannot be so gross a sense as it is

[038-1434] reputed, if it is the very organ by which we sensate all other bodies,

[038-1435] and rules over a vast kingdom of subtle and complex concepts. As the

[038-1436] surface stands to the body, so does sight stand in respect of touch,

[038-1437] and it is merely a colloquial abbreviation to speak of seeing bodies as

[038-1438] surfaces and to suppose that we see with our eyes that which we have

[038-1439] gradually learnt in infancy simply by the sense of touch." Every beauty

[038-1440] of form or corporeity is a concept not visible, but palpable.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_9_874) From

[038-1441] the triad of æsthetic senses thus established by Herder (sight for

[038-1442] painting; hearing for music; touch for sculpture), Hegel returned to

[038-1443] the customary dyad, saying that "the sensory part of art has reference

[038-1444] only to the two theoretic senses of sight and hearing"; that smell,

[038-1445] taste and touch must be excluded from artistic pleasures, since they

[038-1446] are connected with matter as such and the immediate sensible quality it

[038-1447] may possess (smell with material volatilization; taste with material

[038-1448] solution of objects; and touch with hot, cold, smooth and so forth);

[038-1449] and that hence they can claim no concern with the objects of art,

[038-1450] which are obliged to keep themselves in real independence, rejecting

[038-1451] all relation with the merely sensory. That which pleases these senses

[038-1452] is not the beautiful of art.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_10_875)

[038-1453] It was Schleiermacher once more who recognized the impossibility of

[038-1454] disposing of the matter in this summary fashion. He refused to admit

[038-1455] the distinction between confused senses and clear senses, and asserted

[038-1456] that the superiority of sight and hearing over the other senses lay in

[038-1457] the fact that the others "are not capable of any free activity, and

[038-1458] indeed represent the maximum of passivity, whereas sight and hearing

[038-1459] are capable of an activity proceeding from within, and are able to

[038-1460] produce forms and notes without having received impressions from

[038-1461] outside"; were eye and ear merely means of perception, there would

[038-1462] be no visual or auditory arts, but they also operate as a function

[038-1463] of voluntary movements which supply a content to the dominion of the

[038-1464] senses. From another standpoint, however, Schleiermacher thinks that

[038-1465] "the difference seems to be one rather of degree or quantity, and a

[038-1466] minimum of independence must be recognized as existing in the other

[038-1467] senses as well."[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_11_876) Vischer remains faithful to the traditional "two

[038-1468] æsthetic senses," "free organs and no less spiritual than sensuous,"

[038-1469] which "have no reference to the material composition of the object,"

[038-1470] but allow this "to subsist as a whole and work upon them."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_12_877) Köstlin

[038-1471] was of opinion that the inferior senses offer "nothing intuitible

[038-1472] separate from themselves, and are only modifications of ourselves, but

[038-1473] taste, smell and touch are not devoid of all æsthetic importance, since

[038-1474] they assist the superior senses; without touch an image could not be

[038-1475] recognized by the eye as being hard, resistant or rough; without smell

[038-1476] certain images could not be represented as sweet or scented."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_13_878)

[038-1477] We cannot go into a detailed account of all doctrines connected with

[038-1478] sensationalistic principles,[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_14_879) for all the senses are naturally

[038-1479] accepted as æsthetic by the sensationalists, who use "æsthetic"

[038-1480] interchangeably with" hedonistic": it will suffice if we recall the

[038-1481] "learned" Kralik, who was ridiculed by Tolstoy for his theory of the

[038-1482] five arts of taste, smell, touch, hearing and sight.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_15_880) The few

[038-1483] quotations already given show the embarrassing difficulty caused

[038-1484] by the use of the word "æsthetic" as a qualification of "sense,"

[038-1485] compelling writers to invent absurd distinctions between various groups

[038-1486] of senses, or to recognize all senses as being æsthetic, thus giving

[038-1487] æsthetic value to every sensory impression, as such. No way out of

[038-1488] this labyrinth can be found save by asserting the impossibility of

[038-1489] effecting a union between such wholly disparate orders of ideas as the

[038-1490] concept of the representative form of the spirit and that of particular

[038-1491] physiological organs or a particular matter of sense-impressions.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_16_881)

[038-1492] *The theory of kinds of style*.

[038-1493] III. A variety of the error of literary kinds is to be found in

[038-1494] the theory of modes, forms or kinds of style (χαρακτῆρες τῆς

[038-1495] φράσεως), considered by the ancients as consisting of three forms,

[038-1496] [[sublime|the sublime]], the medium and the tenuous, a tripartition due, it would

[038-1497] seem, to Antisthenes,[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_17_882) modified later into *subtile, robustum*

[038-1498] and *floridum,* or amplified into a fourfold division, or designated

[038-1499] by adjectives of historic origin as in the Attic, Asiatic or Rhodian

[038-1500] styles. The Middle Ages preserved the tradition of a tripartite

[038-1501] division, sometimes giving it a curious interpretation, to the effect

[038-1502] that the sublime style treats of kings, princes and barons (*e.g.* the

[038-1503] *Aeneid*); the mediocre, of middle-class people (*e.g. Georgies)*; the

[038-1504] humble, of the lowest class (*e.g. Bucolics;*) and the three styles

[038-1505] were for this reason also called tragic, elegiac and comic.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_18_883) It

[038-1506] is a well-known fact that kinds in style have never ceased to afford

[038-1507] matter for discussion in rhetorical text-books down to modern times;

[038-1508] for instance, we find Blair distinguishing styles by such epithets

[038-1509] as the diffuse, the concise, the nervous, the daring, the soft, the

[038-1510] elegant, the flowery, etc. In 1818 the Italian Melchiorre Delfico, in

[038-1511] his book on *The Beautiful,* energetically criticized the "endless

[038-1512] division of styles," or the superstition "that there could be so many

[038-1513] kinds of style"; saying that "style is either good or bad," and adding

[038-1514] that it is not possible "it should exist as a preconceived idea in the

[038-1515] artist's mind," but that "it should be the consequence of the principal

[038-1516] idea, *i.e.* that conception which determines the invention and the

[038-1517] composition."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_19_884)

[038-1518] *The theory of grammatical forms or parts of speech.*

[038-1519] IV. The same error reappears in the philosophy of language, as the

[038-1520] theory of grammatical forms or parts of speech,[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_20_885) first created by

[038-1521] the sophists (Protagoras is credited with having first distinguished

[038-1522] the gender of nouns), adopted by the philosophers, notably by Aristotle

[038-1523] and the Stoics (the former was acquainted with two or three parts of

[038-1524] speech, the latter with four or five), developed and elaborated by the

[038-1525] Alexandrian grammarians in the famous and endless controversy between

[038-1526] the analogists and the anomalists. The analogists (Aristarchus) aimed

[038-1527] at introducing logical order and regularity into linguistic facts,

[038-1528] and described as deviations all such as seemed to them irreducible

[038-1529] to logical form. These they called pleonasm, ellipsis, enallage,

[038-1530] parallage, and metalepsis. The violence thus wrought by the analogists

[038-1531] upon spoken and written language was such that (as Quintilian tells

[038-1532] us) some one wittily (*non invenuste*) remarked that it appeared to

[038-1533] be one thing to talk Latin and quite another to talk grammar (*aliud

[038-1534] esse latine*, *aliud grammatice loqui*).[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_21_886) The anomalists must be

[038-1535] credited with restoring to language its free imaginative movement: the

[038-1536] Stoic Chrysippus composed a treatise to prove that one thing (one same

[038-1537] concept) may be expressed by different sounds, and one and the same

[038-1538] sound may express different concepts (*similes res dissimilibus verbis

[038-1539] et similibus dissimiles esse vocabulis notatas.*) Another anomalist

[038-1540] was the celebrated grammarian Apollonius Dyscolus, who rejected

[038-1541] the metalepsis, the schemes, and the other artifices by which the

[038-1542] analogists tried to explain facts which did not fit their categories,

[038-1543] and pointed out that the use of one word for another, or one part of

[038-1544] speech for another, is not a grammatical figure, but a blunder, a thing

[038-1545] hardly to be attributed to a poet such as Homer. The upshot of the

[038-1546] dispute between anomalists and analogists was the science of Grammar

[038-1547] (τεχνη γραμματική), as handed down by the ancients to the modern

[038-1548] world, which is justly considered as a sort of compromise between the

[038-1549] two opposed parties because, if the schemes of inflection (κανόνες)

[038-1550] satisfy the demands of the analogists, their variety satisfies those

[038-1551] of the anomalists; hence the original definition of Grammar as theory

[038-1552] of analogy was changed subsequently to "theory of analogy and anomaly"

[038-1553] (ὁμοίον τε καὶ ἀνoμoίου θεωρία). The concept of correct usage, with

[038-1554] which Varro hoped to settle the controversy, fell into the trap (common

[038-1555] to compromises), merely stating the contradiction in set terms, like

[038-1556] the "convenient ornament" of Rhetoric or the kinds accorded a "certain

[038-1557] licence" in the literature of precept. If language follows usage (that

[038-1558] is to say, the imagination), it does not follow reason (or logic); if

[038-1559] it follows reason, it does not follow usage. When the analogists upheld

[038-1560] logic as supreme at least inside the individual kinds and sub-kinds,

[038-1561] the anomalists hastened to show that even this was not the case. Varro

[038-1562] himself was forced to confess that "this part of the subject really is

[038-1563] very difficult" (*hic locus maxime lubricus est*).[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_22_887)

[038-1564] In the Middle Ages grammar was cultivated to the point of superstition.

[038-1565] Divine inspiration was found lurking in the eight parts of speech

[038-1566] because "*octavus numerus frequenter in divinis scripturis sacratis

[038-1567] invenitur,*" and in the three persons of verbal conjugation, created

[038-1568] simply "*ut quod in Trinitatis fide credimus, in eloquiis inesse

[038-1569] videatur.*"[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_23_888) Grammarians of the Renaissance and later recommenced

[038-1570] the study of linguistic problems and worked to death ellipsis,

[038-1571] pleonasm, licence, anomaly and exception; only in comparatively recent

[038-1572] times has Linguistic begun to question the very validity of the concept

[038-1573] of parts of speech (Pott, Paul and others).[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_24_889) If they still survive,

[038-1574] the reason may lie in the facts that empirical, practical grammar

[038-1575] cannot do without them; that their venerable antiquity disguises their

[038-1576] illegitimate and shady origin; and that energetic opposition has been

[038-1577] worn down by the fatigue of an endless war.

[038-1578] *Theory of æsthetic criticism.*

[038-1579] V. The relativity of taste is a sensationalistic theory which denies

[038-1580] a spiritual value to art. But it is rarely maintained by writers in

[038-1581] the ingenuous categorical garb of the old adage: *De gustibus non

[038-1582] est disputandum* (concerning which it would be useful to enquire

[038-1583] when the saying was born, and what it fust meant: whether, too, the

[038-1584] word *gustibus* referred solely to impressions of the palate, and

[038-1585] was only later extended to include æsthetic impressions); as though

[038-1586] sensationalists, as if dimly conscious of the higher nature of art,

[038-1587] have never been able to resign themselves to the complete relativity

[038-1588] of taste. Their torments in the matter really move one to pity. "Is

[038-1589] there," Batteux asks, "such a thing as [[taste|good taste]], and is it the only

[038-1590] good taste? In what does it consist? Upon what depend? Does it depend

[038-1591] upon the object itself or the genius at work upon it? Are there, or are

[038-1592] there not, rules? Is wit alone, or heart alone, the organ of taste, or

[038-1593] both together? How many questions have been raised on this familiar

[038-1594] often-treated subject, how many obscure and involved answers have

[038-1595] been given!"[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_25_890) This perplexity is shared by Home. Tastes, he says,

[038-1596] must not be disputed; neither those of the palate nor those of other

[038-1597] senses. A remark which seems highly reasonable from one point of view;

[038-1598] but, from another, somewhat exaggerated. But yet how can one dispute

[038-1599] it? how can one maintain that what actually pleases a man ought not

[038-1600] to please him? The proposition then must be true. But now no man of

[038-1601] taste will assent to it. We speak of good taste and bad taste; are all

[038-1602] criticisms which turn upon this distinction to be considered absurd?

[038-1603] have these everyday expressions no meaning? Home ends by asserting

[038-1604] a common standard of taste, deduced from the necessity of a common

[038-1605] life for mankind or, as he says, from a "final cause"; for without

[038-1606] uniformity of taste, who would trouble to produce works of art, build

[038-1607] elegant and costly edifices, or lay out beautiful gardens and so forth?

[038-1608] He does not fail to draw attention to a second final cause; that of

[038-1609] the advisability of attracting citizens to public shows and uniting

[038-1610] those whom class-differences and diversity of occupation tend to keep

[038-1611] apart. But how shall a standard of taste be established? This is a new

[038-1612] perplexity, which one cannot think to be escaped by observing that, as

[038-1613] in framing moral rules we seek the counsel of the most honourable of

[038-1614] educated men, not of savages; so to determine the standard of taste

[038-1615] we should have recourse to the few who are not worn out by degrading

[038-1616] bodily labour, not corrupted in taste, and not rendered effeminate

[038-1617] by pleasure, who have received the gift of good taste from nature,

[038-1618] and have brought it to perfection by the education and practice of a

[038-1619] lifetime: if, notwithstanding, controversies arise, then reference

[038-1620] must be made to the principles of Criticism as set forth by Home

[038-1621] himself in his own book.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_26_891) Similar contradictions and vicious circles

[038-1622] reappear in David Hume's *Essay on Taste,* where Hume tries in vain

[038-1623] to define the distinctive characteristics of the man of taste whose

[038-1624] judgement must be law, and, while asserting the uniformity of the

[038-1625] general principles of taste as founded in human nature, and warning

[038-1626] the reader against giving undue weight to individual perversions and

[038-1627] ignorances, at the same time asserts that divergences in taste may be

[038-1628] irreconcilable, insuperable, and yet blameless.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_27_892)

[038-1629] But a criticism of æsthetic relativism cannot be based upon the

[038-1630] opposite doctrine which, by its affirmation of absoluteness, resolves

[038-1631] taste into concepts and logical inferences. The eighteenth century

[038-1632] offers examples of this mistake in Muratori, one of the first to

[038-1633] maintain the existence of a rule of taste and a universal beauty

[038-1634] whose rules are furnished by Poetics;[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_28_893) in André, who said that

[038-1635] "the beauty in a work of art is not that which pleases at the first

[038-1636] glance of fancy through certain individual dispositions of the mental

[038-1637] faculties or bodily organs, but that which has a right to please the

[038-1638] reason and reflexion by its own inherent excellence or rightness and,

[038-1639] if the expression be allowed, by its intrinsic agreeableness";[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_29_894)

[038-1640] in Voltaire, who recognized a "universal taste" which was

[038-1641] "intellectual";[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_30_895) and in very many others. This intellectualistic

[038-1642] error, no less than the sensationalistic, was attacked by Kant; but

[038-1643] even Kant, by making beauty consist in a symbolism of morality, failed

[038-1644] to grasp the concept of an imaginative absoluteness of taste.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_31_896)

[038-1645] Succeeding generations of philosophers met the difficulty by passing it

[038-1646] over in silence.

[038-1647] Nevertheless, this criterion of an imaginative absoluteness, the idea

[038-1648] that in order to judge works of art one must place oneself at the

[038-1649] artist's point of view at the moment of production, and that to judge

[038-1650] is to reproduce, gathered weight little by little from the beginning

[038-1651] of the eighteenth century, when its first appearance is seen in the

[038-1652] work of the Italian Francesco Montani already quoted (1705), and by

[038-1653] the English poet Alexander Pope in his *Essay on Criticism.* ("A

[038-1654] perfect judge will read each work of wit With the same spirit that its

[038-1655] author writ."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_32_897)) A few years later Antonio Conti recognized part of

[038-1656] the truth in the *règle du premier aspect* advised by Terrasson as

[038-1657] a test for judging poetry, while noting it to be more applicable to

[038-1658] modern than to ancient works: "*quand on n'a pas l'esprit prévenu,

[038-1659] et que d'ailleurs on l'a assez pénétrant, on peut voir tout d'un

[038-1660] coup si un poète a bien imité son objet; car, comme on connaît

[038-1661] l'original, c'est-à-dire les hommes et les mœurs de son siècle,

[038-1662] on peut aisément lui confronter la copie, c'est-à-dire la poésie qui

[038-1663] les imite.*" In judging ancient writers something more is necessary:

[038-1664] "*cette règle du premier aspect n'est presque d'aucun usage dans

[038-1665] l'examen de l'ancienne poésie, dont on ne peut pas juger qu'après

[038-1666] avoir longtemps réfléchi sur la religion des anciens, sur leurs lois,

[038-1667] leur mœurs, sur leurs manières de combattre et d'haranguer, etc.

[038-1668] Les beautés d'un poème, indépendantes de toutes ces circonstances

[038-1669] individuelles, sont très rares, et les grands peintres les ont toujours

[038-1670] évitées avec soin, car ils voulaient peindre la nature et non pas

[038-1671] leurs idées;*"[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_33_898) the necessary criterion, therefore, is to be found

[038-1672] in history. The end of the same century saw the concept of congenial

[038-1673] reproduction sufficiently defined by Heydenreich: "A philosophical

[038-1674] critic of art must himself be possessed of genius for art; reason

[038-1675] exacts this qualification and grants no dispensation, just as she will

[038-1676] refuse to appoint a blind man as judge of colours. The critic must

[038-1677] not pretend to be able to feel the attraction of beauty by means of

[038-1678] syllogisms (*Vernunftschlüsse*); beauty must manifest itself to feeling

[038-1679] with irresistible self-evidence and, attracted by its fascination,

[038-1680] reason must find no time to linger over the why and wherefore; the

[038-1681] effect, with its delightful and unexpected possession and domination

[038-1682] of the whole being, should suffocate at birth any inquiry into origins

[038-1683] or causes. But this state of fanatical admiration cannot last long;

[038-1684] reason must inevitably recover consciousness of itself and direct

[038-1685] its attention upon the state in which it was during the enjoyment

[038-1686] of beauty and upon its present memories of that state...."[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_34_899) This

[038-1687] was the wholesomely impressionistic theory which prevailed among the

[038-1688] Romanticists and was accepted even by De Sanctis.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_35_900) Still there

[038-1689] was even then no definite theory of criticism, which demanded as its

[038-1690] condition of existence a precise concept of art and of the relations

[038-1691] of the work of art with its historical antecedents.[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_36_901) The very

[038-1692] possibility of æsthetic criticism was questioned in the second half

[038-1693] of the nineteenth century, when taste was relegated to a place amongst

[038-1694] the facts of individual caprice, and a so-called historical criticism

[038-1695] was proclaimed the sole scientific criticism and expounded in works of

[038-1696] irrelevant learning or buried beneath the preconceptions of positivists

[038-1697] and materialists. Those who reacted against such extremalism and

[038-1698] materialism generally made the mistake of supporting themselves by a

[038-1699] kind of intellectualistic dogmatism[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_37_902) or an empty æstheticism.[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_38_903)

[038-1700] *Distinction between taste and genius.*

[038-1701] VI. We have seen that in the seventeenth century, when the words

[038-1702] "taste" and "genius" or "wit" were in fashion, the facts they

[038-1703] designated were sometimes interchanged amongst themselves and came to

[038-1704] be considered as one single fact, while sometimes each was conceived

[038-1705] as distinct in itself, genius being the faculty of production, and

[038-1706] taste the faculty of judgement, taste being further subdivided into

[038-1707] the sterile and the fertile: a terminology adopted by Muratori[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_39_904) in

[038-1708] Italy and Ulrich König[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_40_905) in Germany. Batteux said, "*le goût juge

[038-1709] des productions du génie*"[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_41_906); and Kant speaks of defective works

[038-1710] having genius without taste or taste without genius, and of others in

[038-1711] which taste alone suffices;[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_42_907) now we find him distinguishing the two

[038-1712] concepts as the judging and producing faculties, now he speaks of them

[038-1713] as a single faculty existing in various degrees. An inherent difference

[038-1714] between taste and genius was accepted by later writers on Æsthetic and

[038-1715] assumed its most rigid form in the hands of Herbart and his followers.

[038-1716] *Concept of artistic and literary history.*

[038-1717] VII. The evolutionary theory of art made its appearance towards the

[038-1718] end of the eighteenth century. This was the time when the distinction

[038-1719] between classical and romantic art was first made; a classification

[038-1720] later augmented by an introductory section on Oriental art, owing to

[038-1721] the increase of knowledge concerning the pre-Hellenic world. Towards

[038-1722] the end of his life Goethe told his friend Eckermann that the concepts

[038-1723] of classical and romantic had been formed by himself and Schiller, for

[038-1724] he himself had upheld the objective method in poetry, whilst Schiller,

[038-1725] in order to champion the subjective form to which he inclined, had

[038-1726] written the essay *On Naïve and Sentimental Poetry*, in which the word

[038-1727] naïve (*naiv*) expresses the style later called classical and the

[038-1728] word sentimental (*sentimentalisch)* that later called romantic. "The

[038-1729] Schlegels," continues Goethe, "seized upon these ideas and disseminated

[038-1730] them, so that to-day everyone uses them and speaks of classical and

[038-1731] romantic, things perfectly unknown fifty years ago"[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_43_908) (Goethe was

[038-1732] speaking in 1831). Schiller's essay bears the imprint of Rousseau's

[038-1733] influence and is dated 1795-6.[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_44_909) It contains such statements as this:

[038-1734] "Poets are above all things the preservers of nature; and when they

[038-1735] cannot be so entirely, and have tried upon themselves the destructive

[038-1736] force of arbitrary and artificial forms or have fought against such

[038-1737] forms, they stand up to bear witness on her behalf. Poets, therefore,

[038-1738] either are nature or, having lost her, seek her. Hence arise two wholly

[038-1739] distinct kinds of poetic composition, exhausting between them the whole

[038-1740] field of poetry; all poets who are worthy of the name must belong,

[038-1741] according to the times and conditions in which they flourish, either

[038-1742] to the category of naïve or to that of sentimental poets." Schiller

[038-1743] recognized three kinds of sentimental poetry: satirical, elegiac and

[038-1744] idyllic; he defined a satirical poet as one "who takes as his object

[038-1745] the desertion of nature and the contrast of the real with the ideal."

[038-1746] The weak point of this division is the concept of two distinct kinds

[038-1747] of poetry, the reduction of the infinite forms in which poetry appears

[038-1748] to individuals, to two kinds. If one of these two kinds be taken the

[038-1749] perfect and the other as the imperfect kind, the mistake is made of

[038-1750] converting imperfection into a kind or species, the negative into a

[038-1751] positive. Wilhelm von Humboldt pointed out to his friend that if

[038-1752] form is the essence of art, there cannot be a kind of poetry, such

[038-1753] as the sentimental or romantic is supposed to be, in which matter

[038-1754] preponderates over form, for that would constitute a pseudo-art, not

[038-1755] a separate kind of art.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_45_910) Schiller attached no historical meaning

[038-1756] to his classification, in fact he declared explicitly that in using

[038-1757] the words "ancient" and "modern" as equivalent to "ingenuous" and

[038-1758] "sentimental" he did not mean to deny that some "ancient" poets, in his

[038-1759] sense of the word, could be found among contemporary writers; the two

[038-1760] characters might even be united in the same poet or the same poetical

[038-1761] work, as (to give Schiller's own example) in *Werther*[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_46_911) The first to

[038-1762] assign a historical meaning to the division were Friedrich and Wilhelm

[038-1763] von Schlegel; the former in an early work of 1795, the latter in his

[038-1764] celebrated lectures on literary history given at Berlin in 1801-4. But

[038-1765] the two senses, systematic and historical, were variously alternated

[038-1766] and mixed by literary men and critics, and other distinctions were

[038-1767] added; "classical" was sometimes used to describe poetry of a frigid

[038-1768] and imitative style, while "romantic" poetry was the inspired; in some

[038-1769] countries the word "romantic" came to mean a political reactionary, in

[038-1770] Italy it stood for "liberal"; and so forth. In 1815, when Friedrich

[038-1771] Schlegel spoke of ancient Persian romantic poems, or when in our times

[038-1772] attention is called to the romanticism of the Greek, Latin or French

[038-1773] classics, the historical signification is lost in the theoretical, the

[038-1774] sense originally intended by Schiller.

[038-1775] But the historical sense was prevalent in German idealism, which

[038-1776] inclined towards the construction of a universal history, including

[038-1777] that of literature and art, upon a scheme of ideal evolution. Schelling

[038-1778] made a sharp division between pagan and Christian art; the second

[038-1779] being held an advance upon the former which was the lowest step.[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_47_912)

[038-1780] Hegel accepted this division and introduced a final regress by

[038-1781] dividing the history of art into three periods: symbolic (Oriental)

[038-1782] art, classical (Hellenic) and romantic (modern). Just as he conceived

[038-1783] Roman art (with its introduction of satire and other kinds indicative

[038-1784] of a failure to maintain harmony between form and content) as the

[038-1785] dissolution of classical art, a thought suggested by Schiller, so

[038-1786] he found in the subjective humour of Cervantes and Ariosto[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_48_913) the

[038-1787] dissolution of romantic art; and he regarded this series as completing

[038-1788] the possibilities of art, though some interpreters think that by a

[038-1789] self-contradiction he admitted the possibility of a fourth period, an

[038-1790] art of the modern or future world. Indeed amongst his disciples we

[038-1791] find Weiss rejecting the Oriental period in order to save the triadic

[038-1792] division, and placing as third the modern period, synthesis of the

[038-1793] ancient and the mediæval:[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_49_914) Vischer too inclines to recognize a

[038-1794] modern or progressive period.[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_50_915)

[038-1795] These arbitrary constructions reappear in the works of positivist

[038-1796] metaphysicians in the shape of an evolutionary or progressive history

[038-1797] of art. Spencer dreamed of writing some sort of treatise on the

[038-1798] subject, and in the published programme of his system (1860) we read

[038-1799] that the third volume of his *Principles of Sociology* was to contain

[038-1800] amongst other things a chapter on æsthetic progress "with the gradual

[038-1801] differentiation of fine arts from primitive institutions and from each

[038-1802] other, with their increasing variety in development, their progress in

[038-1803] reality of expression and superiority of end." No grief need be felt

[038-1804] that the chapter was left unwritten when we remember the samples of it

[038-1805] preserved in the *Principles of Psychology* and already reviewed in

[038-1806] these pages.[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_51_916)

[038-1807] The strong historical sense of our own day is leading us further and

[038-1808] further away from the evolutionary or abstractly progressive theories

[038-1809] which falsify the free and original movement of art. Fiedler remarked

[038-1810] not without justice that unity and progress cannot be introduced

[038-1811] into a history of art, and that the works of artists must be judged

[038-1812] discretely as so many fragments of the life of the universe.[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_52_917)

[038-1813] In recent times a remarkable student of the history of figurative

[038-1814] art, Venturi, has tried to bring evolutionism into fashion, and has

[038-1815] illustrated it in a *History of the Madonna,* in which the presentment

[038-1816] of the Virgin is conceived as an organism which is born, grows, attains

[038-1817] perfection, grows old and dies! Others have claimed for artistic

[038-1818] history its true character, intolerant of outward curb and rule,

[038-1819] drawing her ever-varied productions from the well-head of the infinite

[038-1820] Spirit.[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-29.htm.html#Footnote_53_918)

[038-1821] *Conclusion.*

[038-1822] These hurried notes may suffice to show in how narrow a circle has

[038-1823] hitherto moved the scientific criticism of the errors we have called

[038-1824] "particular." Æsthetic needs to be surrounded and nourished by a

[038-1825] watchful and vigorous critical literature drawing its life from her and

[038-1826] forming in turn her safeguard and strength.

#### BIBLIOGRAPHICAL APPENDIX

[038-1902] The first attempt at a history of Æsthetic is the work of J. Roller

[038-1903] (see above, p. 248) mentioned by [[robert-zimmermann|Zimmermann]] (*Gesch. d. Ästh.* pref.,

[038-1904] p. v) as being so exceedingly rare that he had never been able to see

[038-1905] a copy of the book. We ourselves have had the good fortune to find the

[038-1906] book in the Royal Library of Munich in Bavaria, by the help of our

[038-1907] friend Dr. Arturo Farinelli of Innsbruck University, and to obtain the

[038-1908] loan of it. It bears the title *Entwurf* | *zur* | *Geschichte und

[038-1909] Literatur* | *der Æsthetik* | *von Baumgarten auf die* | *neueste

[038-1910] Zeit.* | *Herausgegeben* | *von* | *J. Koller*. | *Regensburg* | in

[038-1911] der Montag und Weissischen Buchhandlung | 1799 (pp. viii-107, small

[038-1912] 8vo); in the preface the author declares his intention of supplying

[038-1913] young men attending Lectures on the Criticism of Taste and the Theory

[038-1914] of the Fine Arts in the German Universities with a "lucid summary of

[038-1915] the origin and later progress of these studies," premising that he will

[038-1916] treat of general theories only and that his judgements are frequently

[038-1917] derived from reviews in literary periodicals. The introduction (§§

[038-1918] 1-7) treats of æsthetic theories from antiquity down to the beginning

[038-1919] of the eighteenth century; Koller observes that "the names and form

[038-1920] of a general Theory of Fine Art and Criticism of Taste were unknown

[038-1921] to the ancients, whose imperfect ethical theory prevented their

[038-1922] producing anything in this field." He dedicates § 5 to the Italians,

[038-1923] "who have produced little in theory"; indeed the only Italian books

[038-1924] mentioned are the *Entusiasmo* of Bettinelli and the small work of

[038-1925] Jagemann, *Saggio di buon gusto nelle belle arti ove si spiegano gli

[038-1926] elementi dell' estetica,* di Fr. Gaud. Jagemann, Regente agostiniano,

[038-1927] In Firenze, MDCCLXXI, Presso Luigi Bastianelli e compagni; 60 pp.

[038-1928] (concerning this, see B. Croce, *Problemi di estetica,* pp. 387-390).

[038-1929] The section on the History and Literature of Æsthetic begins with the

[038-1930] oft-quoted passage from Bülffinger ("*Vellem existerent,* etc.") and

[038-1931] passes at once to Baumgarten: "the theoretical epoch owes its existence

[038-1932] undeniably to Baumgarten; to him belongs the inalienable merit of

[038-1933] having first conceived an Æsthetic founded on principles of reason and

[038-1934] wholly developed, and of having tried to put it into practice by the

[038-1935] means offered him by his own philosophy." Immediately after this, Meier

[038-1936] is mentioned, followed by the titles, accompanied by brief extracts

[038-1937] and remarks—a sort of *catalogue raisonné*—of many German books on

[038-1938] Æsthetic from those of K. W. Müller (1759) to one by Ramier (1799),

[038-1939] mixed with various French and English writings under the dates of their

[038-1940] German translations. Special emphasis is laid on Kant (pp. 64-74), with

[038-1941] the remark that, prior to the appearance of the *Critique of Judgment,*

[038-1942] æstheticians were divided into sceptics, dogmatics and empiricists: the

[038-1943] most powerful intellects of the nation inclined towards empiricism, so

[038-1944] much so that had Kant himself "been asked by what literature he had

[038-1945] been most strongly influenced in the development of his own thought,

[038-1946] he would certainly have named the acute empirical writers of England,

[038-1947] France and Germany"; but "by no pre-Kantian method had it been possible

[038-1948] to establish an agreement (*eine Einhelligkeit*) between men upon

[038-1949] matters of taste." The last pages call attention to the revival of

[038-1950] interest in æsthetic studies, which nobody would now dare call a waste

[038-1951] of time as in former days. "May Jacobi, Schiller and Mehmel soon enrich

[038-1952] literature by publication of their theories!" (p. 104).

[038-1953] The rarity of Koller's book has led us to notice it at some length.

[038-1954] Apart from this the first general history of Æsthetic worthy the

[038-1955] name is that written by Robert Zimmermann, *Geschichte der Ästhetik

[038-1956] als philosophischer Wissenschaft,* Vienna, 1858. It is divided into

[038-1957] four books: "the first of these contains the history of philosophical

[038-1958] concepts concerning the beautiful and art from the Greeks down to the

[038-1959] constitution of Æsthetic as a philosophical science through the labours

[038-1960] of Baumgarten"; the second runs from Baumgarten down to the reform of

[038-1961] Æsthetic brought about by the *Critique of Judgment*; the third, from

[038-1962] Kant to the Æsthetic of idealism; the fourth, from the beginnings of

[038-1963] idealistic Æsthetic down to the author's own day (1798-1858). The work

[038-1964] is on Herbartian lines, and is remarkable for solid research and lucid

[038-1965] exposition, although the erroneous point of view and neglect of all

[038-1966] æsthetic movement other than Græco-Roman or German are grave defects;

[038-1967] besides, it is now sixty years out of date.

[038-1968] Less solid and more compilatory in nature, whilst retaining all the

[038-1969] defects of the foregoing, is the history by Max Schasler, *Kritische

[038-1970] Geschichte der Ästhetik,* Berlin, 1872, divided into three books

[038-1971] treating of ancient Æsthetic and that of the eighteenth and nineteenth

[038-1972] centuries. The author belongs to the Hegelian school and conceives his

[038-1973] history as a propædeutic to theory, "in order, that is, to attain a

[038-1974] supreme principle for the construction of a new system"; he schematizes

[038-1975] the material of facts for each period into three grades of Æsthetic of

[038-1976] sensation (*Empfindungsurtheil,*) of intellect (*Verstandsurtheil*) and

[038-1977] of reason (*Vernunfturtheil.*)

[038-1978] English literature has Bernard Bosanquet's *History of Æsthetics,*

[038-1979] London, 1892; a sober and well-arranged work, written from an eclectic

[038-1980] point of view between the Æsthetic of content and the Æsthetic of

[038-1981] form. The author, however, is wrong in believing he has passed over

[038-1982] "no writer of the first rank"; he has passed over not only writers but

[038-1983] some important movements of ideas, and in general he shows insufficient

[038-1984] knowledge of the literature of the Latin races. Another general

[038-1985] history of Æsthetic in English is the first volume of *The Philosophy

[038-1986] of the Beautiful, being Outlines of the History of Æsthetics,* by

[038-1987] William Knight, London, Murray, 1895: it consists mainly of a rich

[038-1988] collection of extracts and abridgements of ancient and modern books

[038-1989] treating of Æsthetic. In this respect the most noteworthy chapters

[038-1990] are those on Holland, Great Britain and America (10-13); the second

[038-1991] volume, published in 1898, has in an appendix, pp. 251-281, notices

[038-1992] upon Æsthetic in Russia and Denmark. Another recent publication is

[038-1993] George Saintsbury's *A History of Criticism and Literary Taste in

[038-1994] Europe from the Earliest Times to the Present Day*; vol. i., Edinburgh

[038-1995] and London, 1900, concerning classical and mediæval criticism; vol.

[038-1996] ii., 1902, criticism from the Renaissance to end of the eighteenth

[038-1997] century: vol. iii., 1904, modern criticism. The writer of this History,

[038-1998] equally skilled in literature and innocent of philosophy, has thought

[038-1999] it possible to exclude æsthetic science in the strict sense, "the

[038-2000] more transcendental Æsthetic, those ambitious theories of Beauty and

[038-2001] artistic pleasure in general which seem so noble and fascinating until

[038-2002] we discover them to be but cloud-appearances of Juno," and to limit his

[038-2003] treatise to "lofty Rhetoric and Poetic, to the theory and practice of

[038-2004] Criticism and literary taste" (book i. ch. I). Thus is produced a book

[038-2005] instructive in many ways but wholly deficient in method and definite

[038-2006] object. What is lofty Rhetoric and Poetic, the theory of Criticism and

[038-2007] literary taste, if not Æsthetic pure and simple? how can the history of

[038-2008] these be composed without due notice of metaphysical Æsthetic and other

[038-2009] manifestations whose interaction and development are the fabric of

[038-2010] history itself? Perhaps Saintsbury hoped to be able to write a History

[038-2011] of Criticism as distinct from that of Æsthetic; if that be the case,

[038-2012] he has been unsuccessful in writing either one or the other. Cf. *La

[038-2013] Critica,* ii. (1904), pp. 59-63.

[038-2014] The generosity of the Hungarian Academy of Science has enabled us to

[038-2015] handle the History of Æsthetic (*Az Æsthetika története*) of Bela

[038-2016] Janosi, Budapesth, 1899-1901, in three volumes; the first volume treats

[038-2017] the Æsthetic of Greece; the second, of Æsthetic from the Middle Ages to

[038-2018] Baumgarten; the third, from Baumgarten to the present day. For us it is

[038-2019] a book sealed with seven seals, save for reviews which have appeared in

[038-2020] the *Deutsche Litteraturzeitung* of Berlin, August 25, 1900, July 12,

[038-2021] 1902, and May 2, 1903.

[038-2022] Amongst Latin countries, France has no special history of Æsthetic,

[038-2023] for this title cannot be given to the portion of the second volume

[038-2024] (pp. 311-570) of the work by Ch. Levêque, *La Science du beau*

[038-2025] (Paris, 1862), under the heading *Examen des principaux systèmes

[038-2026] d'esthétique anciens et modernes,* where eight chapters are devoted

[038-2027] to an exposition of the theories of Plato, Aristotle, Plotinus and

[038-2028] St. Augustine, Hutcheson, André and Baumgarten, Reid, Kant, Schelling

[038-2029] and Hegel. Spain, on the other hand, possesses the work of Marcelino

[038-2030] Menendez y Pelayo, *Historia de las idéas estéticas en España,* 2nd

[038-2031] ed., Madrid, 1890-1901 (5 vols., variously distributed amongst the

[038-2032] 1st ed., 1883-1891, and the 2nd), which is not restricted, as the

[038-2033] title suggests, to Spain alone or to Æsthetic alone but, as the author

[038-2034] observes in his preface (i. pp. xx-xxi), includes the metaphysical

[038-2035] disquisitions on the beautiful, the speculations of mystics on the

[038-2036] beauty of God and on love; the theories of art scattered through

[038-2037] the pages of philosophers; the æsthetic considerations found in

[038-2038] treatises upon individual arts (Poetics and Rhetoric, works on

[038-2039] painting, architecture, etc.); and, finally, ideas enunciated by

[038-2040] artists concerning their own particular arts. This work is of capital

[038-2041] importance on everything to do with Spanish authors, and also in its

[038-2042] general part contains good treatments of matters generally passed over

[038-2043] by historians. Menendez y Pelayo inclines to [[idealism|metaphysical idealism]],

[038-2044] yet seems not disinclined to welcome elements from other systems, even

[038-2045] empirical theories: in our opinion this vagueness has an unfortunate

[038-2046] effect on the work as a whole. Some years ago Professor V. Spinazzola

[038-2047] announced the forthcoming publication of a course of lectures given

[038-2048] by Francesco de Sanctis in Naples in 1845 on *Storia della critica da

[038-2049] Aristotele ad Hegel.* For the history of Æsthetic in Italy cf. Alfredo

[038-2050] Rolla, *Storia delle idee estetiche in Italia,* Turin, 1904; on which

[038-2051] see Croce, *Problemi di estetica,* pp. 401-415.

[038-2053] generally stand at the beginning of treatises on Æsthetic; the most

[038-2054] important occur in the volumes of Solger, Hegel and Schleiermacher. A

[038-2055] general history of Æsthetic, from the rigorous point of view of the

[038-2056] principle of [[expression|Expression]], has not been attempted before the present work.
