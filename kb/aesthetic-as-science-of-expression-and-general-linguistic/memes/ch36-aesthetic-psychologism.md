[036-001] *Positivism and Evolutionism.*

[036-002] The ground lost by idealistic metaphysic was conquered in the latter

[036-003] half of the nineteenth century by positivistic and evolutionary

[036-004] metaphysic, a confused substitution of natural for philosophical

[036-005] sciences, and a hotch-potch of materialistic and idealistic, mechanical

[036-006] and theological theories, the whole crowned with scepticism and

[036-007] agnosticism. Characteristic of this trend of opinion was its contempt

[036-008] of history, especially the history of philosophy; which prevented its

[036-009] ever making that contact with the unbroken and age-long efforts of

[036-010] thinkers without which it is idle to hope for fertile work and true

[036-011] progress.

[036-012] [Sidenote*Æsthetic of H. Spencer.*]

[036-013] Spencer (the greatest positivist of his day), whilst discussing

[036-014] Æsthetic, actually did not know that he was dealing with problems for

[036-015] all, or almost all, of which solutions had been already proposed and

[036-016] discussed. At the beginning of his essay on the *Philosophy of Style,*

[036-017] he remarks innocently: "I believe nobody has ever sketched a general

[036-018] theory of the art of writing" (in 1852!); and in his *Principles of

[036-019] Psychology* (1855), touching the æsthetic feelings he remarks that

[036-020] he has some recollection of observations concerning the relation of

[036-021] art and play made "by some German author whose name I cannot recall"

[036-022] ([[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]]!). Had his pages on Æsthetic been written in the seventeenth

[036-023] century, they would have won a low position amongst the early crude

[036-024] attempts at æsthetic speculation; in the nineteenth century, one knows

[036-025] not how to judge them. In his essay on *The Useful and the Beautiful*

[036-026] (1852-1854), he shows how the useful becomes beautiful when it ceases

[036-027] to be useful, illustrating this by a ruined castle useless for the

[036-028] purposes of modern life, but a suitable scene for picnic parties and

[036-029] a good subject for a picture to hang on a parlour wall; which leads

[036-030] him to identify the principle of evolution from the useful to the

[036-031] beautiful as contrast. In another essay on the *[[beauty|Beauty]] of the Human

[036-032] Face* (1852) he explains this beauty as a sign and effect of moral

[036-033] goodness; in that on *Grace* (1852) he considers the sentiment of the

[036-034] graceful as sympathy for power in conjunction with agility. In the

[036-035] *Origin of Architectural Styles* (1852-1854) he discovers the beauty of

[036-036] architecture as consisting in uniformity and symmetry, an idea which

[036-037] is aroused in a man looking at the bodily equilibrium of the higher

[036-038] animals or, as in Gothic architecture, by analogy with the vegetable

[036-039] kingdom; in his essay on *Style,* he places the cause of stylistic

[036-040] beauty in economy of effort; in his *Origin and Function of Music*

[036-041] (1857) he theorizes on music as the natural language of the passions,

[036-042] adapted to increase sympathy between men.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_1_643) In his *Principles of

[036-043] Psychology,* he maintains that the æsthetic feelings arise from the

[036-044] overflow of exuberant energy in the organism, and distinguishes

[036-045] various degrees of them, from simple sensation to that accompanied

[036-046] by representative elements, and so on until perception is reached,

[036-047] with more complex elements of representation, then emotion, and, last

[036-048] of all, that state of consciousness which transcends sensation and

[036-049] perception. The most perfect form of æsthetic feeling is attained

[036-050] by the coincidence of the three orders of pleasures, a coincidence

[036-051] produced by the full action of their respective faculties with the

[036-052] least possible subtraction due to the painful effect of excessive

[036-053] activity. But it is very rarely that we experience æsthetic excitement

[036-054] of this kind and strength; almost all works of art are imperfect

[036-055] because they contain a mixture of artistic with anti-artistic effects;

[036-056] now the technique is unsatisfactory, now the emotion is of a low

[036-057] order. These works of art which are universally admired, are found

[036-058] when measured by this criterion to deserve a lower place than that

[036-059] accorded them by popular taste. "Beginning with the Greek epic and

[036-060] the representations of analogous legends given by their sculptors,

[036-061] tending to excite egoistic or ego-altruistic sentiments, and passing

[036-062] through the literature of the Middle Ages, equally impregnated with

[036-063] inferior sentiments, then through the works of the old masters, whose

[036-064] ideas and sentiments seldom compensate for the displeasing effect they

[036-065] inflict on our senses overrefined in study of appearances; and coming

[036-066] at last to the vaunted works of modern art, excellent for technical

[036-067] execution in many cases but deplorable for the emotions they arouse

[036-068] and express, such as Gérôme's battle-pieces, alternately sensual and

[036-069] sanguinary;—they are all far off indeed from the qualities deemed

[036-070] desirable, from the artistic forms corresponding to the highest forms

[036-071] of æsthetic feeling."[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_2_644) These last critical denunciations, like the

[036-072] theories noticed above, are mere substitutions of one word for another;

[036-073] "facility" for "grace"; "economy" for "beauty," and so on. Indeed,

[036-074] when one tries to define the exact philosophical position of Spencer,

[036-075] one can only possibly say that he wavers between sensationalism and

[036-076] moralism, and is never for a moment conscious of art as art.

[036-077] *Physiologists of Æsthetic. Grant Allen, Helmholtz, and

[036-078] others.*

[036-079] The same oscillation is noticeable in other [[england|English]] writers such as

[036-080] Sully and Bain, in whom, however, we find more familiarity with works

[036-081] of art.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_3_645) In his numerous essays and in *Physiological Æsthetics*

[036-082] (1877), Grant Allen collected a great many records of physiological

[036-083] experiments, all of which may be of supreme value to physiology, for

[036-084] aught we know to the contrary, but most assuredly are worthless from

[036-085] the point of view of Æsthetic. He keeps to the distinction between

[036-086] necessary or vital activity and the superfluous or that of play, and

[036-087] defines æsthetic pleasure as "the subjective concomitant of the normal

[036-088] sum of activity, not connected directly with the vital functions, in

[036-089] the terminal peripheric organs of the cerebrospinal nervous system."[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_4_646)

[036-090] Physiological processes considered as causes of pleasure in art are

[036-091] presented under other aspects by later investigators, who assert that

[036-092] such pleasure arises not only "from the activity of the visual organs

[036-093] and the muscular systems associated with them, but also from the

[036-094] participation of some of the more important functions of the organism,

[036-095] as for instance breathing, circulation of the blood, equilibrium and

[036-096] internal muscular accommodation." [[art|Art]], then, indubitably originated

[036-097] in "a prehistoric man who was habitually a deep-breather, having

[036-098] no call to rearrange his natural habits when scratching lines on

[036-099] bones or in mud and taking pains to draw them regularly spaced."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_5_647)

[036-100] Physical-Æsthetic researches were pursued in [[germany|Germany]] by Helmholtz,

[036-101] Brücke and Stumpf,[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_6_648) who generally confined themselves to the narrower

[036-102] field of optics and acoustics, giving descriptions of the physical

[036-103] processes of [[technique|artistic technique]] and the conditions to which pleasurable

[036-104] visual and auditive impressions must conform, without claiming to merge

[036-105] Æsthetic in Physics, but even pointing out the divergences between

[036-106] them. Degenerate Herbartians hastened to disguise in physiological

[036-107] terms the metaphysical forms and relations of which their master had

[036-108] spoken, and to coquet with the hedonism of the naturalists.

[036-109] *Method of the natural sciences in Æsthetic.*

[036-110] The superstitious cult of natural sciences was often accompanied (as is

[036-111] frequently the fate of superstition) by a sort of hypocrisy. Chemical,

[036-112] physical and physiological laboratories became Sybilline grottoes,

[036-113] resounding with the questions of credulous inquirers concerning the

[036-114] profoundest problems of the human spirit; and many of those who were

[036-115] really conducting their inquiries on inherently philosophic principles

[036-116] pretended or deluded themselves into believing that they followed the

[036-117] Method of Natural Science. A proof of this illusion or pretence is

[036-118] Hippolyte Taine's *Philosophy of Art*[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_7_649)

[036-119] *H. Taine's Æsthetic.*

[036-120] "If by studying the art of various peoples and various epochs," says

[036-121] Taine, "we could define the nature and establish the conditions of

[036-122] the existence of each art, we should have arrived at a complete

[036-123] explanation of the fine arts and of art in general, *i.e.* at what

[036-124] is called an Æsthetic." A historical Æsthetic, not a dogmatic, which

[036-125] fixes characters and indicates laws "like Botany, and studies with

[036-126] equal attention orange and ivy, pine and birch; indeed it is a sort of

[036-127] botanical science applied to the works of man instead of to plants";

[036-128] an Æsthetic which shall follow "the general movement which tends

[036-129] daily more and more to join the moral to the natural sciences and by

[036-130] extending to the former the principles, the safeguards and the rules of

[036-131] the latter, enables both to attain the same security and maintain the

[036-132] same progress."[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_8_650) The naturalistic prelude is followed by definitions

[036-133] and doctrines indistinguishable from those offered by philosophers

[036-134] whose infallibility is not guaranteed by scientific methods, indeed,

[036-135] from those of the wildest of such philosophers. For, says Taine, art

[036-136] is imitation, an imitation so carried out as to render sensible the

[036-137] essential character of objects; the essential character being "a

[036-138] quality from which all other qualities, or many others, are derived and

[036-139] follow unalterably from it." The essential character of a lion, for

[036-140] example, is to be "a great carnivore"; this determines the formation of

[036-141] all its limbs; the essential character of Holland is to be "a country

[036-142] formed by alluvial soil." This is why art is not restricted to objects

[036-143] existing in reality, but is able, as in architecture or in music, to

[036-144] represent essential characters without natural objects to correspond.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_9_651)

[036-145] *Taine's metaphysic and moralism.*

[036-146] Now, in what do these essential characters, this carnivorosity and this

[036-147] alluviality differ, save perhaps in extravagance of example, from the

[036-148] "types" and "ideas" which intellectualiste or metaphysical Æsthetic

[036-149] had always considered as the proper content of art? Taine himself

[036-150] clears away every doubt in the matter by explicitly stating that "this

[036-151] character is what philosophers call the 'essence of things,' in virtue

[036-152] of which they affirm that the aim and end of art is to make manifest

[036-153] the essence of things"; he adds that, for his part, he "refuses to

[036-154] make use of the word 'essence' as being a technical term":[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_10_652) of the

[036-155] word itself, maybe; not of the concept for which it stands. There are

[036-156] two ways (says Taine, for all the world as though he were a [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]])

[036-157] leading to the higher life of man, to contemplation: the way of

[036-158] science and the way of art: "the former investigates the causes and

[036-159] fundamental laws of reality, and expresses them in exact formulæ and

[036-160] abstract terms: the latter makes manifest these causes and laws, not in

[036-161] dry definitions inaccessible to the vulgar, and intelligible only to

[036-162] the select few, but in a sensible manner, appealing not merely to the

[036-163] reason but to the heart and senses of the most commonplace man; it has

[036-164] the power of being both elevated and popular, of manifesting what is

[036-165] most noble and elevated, and of manifesting it to every one."[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_11_653)

[036-166] For Taine, as for the Hegelian æstheticians, works of art are arranged

[036-167] in a scale of values; so that, having begun by condemning as absurd

[036-168] every judgement of taste (every one to his taste[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_12_654)), he ends by

[036-169] asserting that "personal taste has no value whatever," and that some

[036-170] common measure should be abstracted and set up as a standard of

[036-171] progress and retrogression, ornamentation and degeneracy; a standard

[036-172] by which to approve and disapprove, praise and blame.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_13_655) The scale of

[036-173] values set up by him is twofold or threefold, in the first instance

[036-174] it turns on the degree of importance of the character, *i.e.* the

[036-175] greater or less generality in idea, and the degree of beneficent effect

[036-176] (*degré de bienfaisance*), *i.e.* the greater or less moral value of

[036-177] the representation (two grades which are aspects of one single quality,

[036-178] viz. power, considered first for its own sake and then in its connexion

[036-179] with others): in the second instance upon the degree of convergence of

[036-180] effects, *i.e.* the fulness of expression, the harmony between idea

[036-181] and form.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_14_656) This intellectualistic, moralistic, rhetorical doctrine

[036-182] is interrupted now and then by the usual naturalistic protests: "We

[036-183] shall, according to our custom, study this question in the manner of

[036-184] the natural scientist; that is to say methodically, by analysis; hoping

[036-185] to raise not merely a song of praise, but a code of laws," etc.;[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_15_657) as

[036-186] though that sufficed to alter the substance of the method adopted and

[036-187] the doctrine expounded. Taine finally gave himself over to dialectical

[036-188] treatments and solutions, and asserted that in the primitive period

[036-189] of Italian art, in the pictures of Giotto, we have soul without body

[036-190] (thesis); under the [[renaissance|Renaissance]], in Verrocchio's pictures, body without

[036-191] soul (antithesis); in the sixteenth century, in Raphæl, there is

[036-192] harmony of expression and anatomy, soul and body (synthesis).[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_16_658)

[036-193] *G. T. Feckner. Inductive Æsthetic.*

[036-194] The same protests and similar methods are to be found in the works of

[036-195] Gustav Theodor Fechner. In his *Introduction to Æsthetic* (1876),

[036-196] Fechner claims to "abandon the attempt at conceptual determination

[036-197] of the objective essence of beauty," since he desires to compose not

[036-198] a metaphysical Æsthetic from above (*von oben*), but an inductive

[036-199] Æsthetic from below (*von unten)* and to achieve clearness, not

[036-200] [[sublime|sublimity]]; metaphysical Æsthetic should bear the same relation to

[036-201] inductive, as the Philosophy of Nature to Physics.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_17_659) Proceeding

[036-202] on inductive lines, he discovers a long series of æsthetic laws or

[036-203] principles: the æsthetic threshold; assistance or increment; unity in

[036-204] variety; absence of contradictions; clarity; association; contrast;

[036-205] consequence; conciliation; the correct mean; economic use; persistency;

[036-206] change; measure; and so on without end. This chaos of concepts he

[036-207] expounds with a chapter apiece, pleased and proud to show himself so

[036-208] highly scientific and so wholly inconclusive.

[036-209] *Experiments.*

[036-210] Next he describes the experiments he can recommend to his readers.

[036-211] They are of this type. Take ten rectangular pieces of white cardboard

[036-212] of fairly equal area (say ten square inches), but with sides variously

[036-213] proportioned from a ratio of 1:1 to one of 2:5, including the ratio

[036-214] of the golden section, 21:34; mix all these together on a black table

[036-215] and collect persons of every kind and character, but all belonging

[036-216] to the educated classes, and applying the method of choice ask these

[036-217] people first to free their minds of all questions as to a particular

[036-218] use and then to pick out the pieces of cardboard which give them the

[036-219] highest sensation of pleasure and those which inspire them with the

[036-220] strongest feelings of disgust; the answers to be most carefully noted,

[036-221] keeping male and female subjects apart, and tabulated. Then see what

[036-222] follows. Fechner admits that the chosen cardboard-pickers often made

[036-223] reservations when questioned by himself, not knowing (very naturally)

[036-224] how to tell whether they liked a shape or disliked it without referring

[036-225] it to a definite use; sometimes they refused point-blank to make any

[036-226] selection at all; and they almost always seemed vague and perplexed in

[036-227] mind and generally, when submitted to a second test, answered in a way

[036-228] totally different from the first. Still, we all know that errors cancel

[036-229] out; and anyhow the tabulations showed that the highest sensations

[036-230] of delight were aroused not by the square, but by rectangular forms

[036-231] most nearly approaching the square, an enthusiastic rush being made

[036-232] for the proportion 21:34.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_18_660) This method of selection received an

[036-233] extraordinarily felicitous definition; it was known as "an average of

[036-234] arbitrary judgements by an arbitrary number of persons arbitrarily

[036-235] selected."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_19_661) Fechner also informs us (always in tabular form) of the

[036-236] result of a statistical inquiry of his own, by means of countless heaps

[036-237] of catalogues and gallery-guides, as to the dimensions and shapes of

[036-238] pictures in relation to the subjects they depict.[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_20_662)

[036-239] *Trivial nature of his ideas on Beauty and Art.*

[036-240] Nevertheless, when he tries to tell us what beauty is, he falls back

[036-241] on using—whether well or ill—the old speculative method, which he

[036-242] prefaces with the remark that for him the concept of beauty is "merely

[036-243] an expedient in conformity with linguistic usage for indicating

[036-244] briefly the link which unites the prevailing conditions of immediate

[036-245] pleasure."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_21_663) He distinguishes three meanings of the word "beauty":

[036-246] first, in a broad sense, the pleasing in general: secondly, in a

[036-247] narrow sense, a higher pleasure, but still sensuous: thirdly, in the

[036-248] narrowest sense, true beauty, which "not only pleases, but has the

[036-249] right of pleasing, possesses value in pleasing"; in it are united the

[036-250] concepts of beauty (the pleasing) and of goodness.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_22_664) Beauty, in fact,

[036-251] is that which must please objectively and as such it corresponds with

[036-252] the good of action. "The Good," says Fechner, "is like a serious man,

[036-253] the capable organiser of his whole domestic life, sagaciously weighing

[036-254] the present and future, setting himself to extract the greatest benefit

[036-255] from both. Beauty is his florid spouse, careful of the present and

[036-256] mindful of her husband's wishes. The Pleasing is the baby, all senses

[036-257] and play: the Useful is the servant who puts his hands at his master's

[036-258] disposal and is given bread solely in accordance with his deserts.

[036-259] Truth, lastly, is the preacher and teacher to the household; preacher

[036-260] in matters of faith, teacher in those of learning: he gives an eye to

[036-261] the Good and a helping hand to the Useful, and holds up a looking-glass

[036-262] to Beauty."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_23_665) When speaking of art, he sums up all essential laws or

[036-263] rules into the following: (1) art chooses a valuable or, at any rate,

[036-264] an interesting, idea for representation: (2) it expresses the idea in

[036-265] sensible material in the manner most suitable to its contents: (3) from

[036-266] amongst the various means at its disposal, it selects those which in

[036-267] themselves are more pleasing than the others: (4) the same procedure

[036-268] is observed in all particulars: (5) in the event of conflict between

[036-269] these rules, one is made to give way to another in such a way that the

[036-270] greatest possible pleasure and that of highest value is attained (*das

[036-271] grösstmögliche und werthvollste Gefallen*).[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_24_666) But why should Fechner,

[036-272] who had this eudemonistic theory of beauty and art (as he calls it) all

[036-273] ready made in advance,[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_25_667) take the trouble to enumerate principles

[036-274] and laws and conduct experiments and tabulate statistics wholly

[036-275] incapable of illustrating or proving it? One is tempted to believe

[036-276] that these pseudo-scientific operations were to him, and still are to

[036-277] his followers, a pastime or hobby neither more nor less important than

[036-278] playing Patience or collecting stamps.

[036-279] *Ernst Grosse. Speculative Æsthetic and the science of art.*

[036-280] Another example of the superstitious cult of the natural sciences is to

[036-281] be found in Professor Ernst Grosse's *Origins of Art.*[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_26_668) Contemner of

[036-282] all philosophical research into art, which he dismisses under the title

[036-283] of "Speculative Æsthetic," Grosse invokes a Science of art (*Kunst

[036-284] wissenschaft)* whose mission is to dig out all the laws lying hidden

[036-285] in the mass of historical facts collected to date. It is his opinion

[036-286] that all ethnographic and prehistoric material should be united to

[036-287] historical matter proper, there being no possibility, according to him,

[036-288] of framing general laws when study is restricted to the art of cultured

[036-289] peoples "just as a theory of generation must necessarily be imperfect

[036-290] if founded exclusively on the form of that function predominant among

[036-291] mammals."[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_27_669) But immediately after his declaration of abhorrence for

[036-292] philosophy, and of faith in scientific methods, Grosse finds himself

[036-293] in the same difficulty as Taine and Fechner. Indeed, there is no

[036-294] escape; in order to examine the artistic productions of primitive and

[036-295] savage peoples, a start must be made from some sort of concept of

[036-296] art. All the scientific metaphors, all the verbal emollients employed

[036-297] by Grosse cannot hide the nature of the plan he is forced to adopt,

[036-298] or its striking resemblance to the despised speculative Æsthetic.

[036-299] "As a traveller who desires to explore an unknown land must provide

[036-300] himself with a general outline of the country and have some knowledge

[036-301] of the direction in which his path should lie, if he does not wish to

[036-302] lose his way entirely; so we, before beginning our enquiry, need a

[036-303] general preliminary orientation concerning the essence of the phenomena

[036-304] (*über das Wesen der Erscheinungen*) about to engage our attention."

[036-305] Most certainly "we may count upon having an exact and exhaustive

[036-306] answer, at earliest, when our enquiry is finished; and it is not yet

[036-307] begun. That characteristic which we seek to determine at the outset

[036-308] ... may be most radically modified by the time we reach the end:"

[036-309] there is no question, fie on the suggestion! of imitating the old

[036-310] æstheticians: the only question is how "to give a definition which may

[036-311] serve as provisional scaffolding, to be broken away on completion of

[036-312] the edifice."[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_28_670) Words, words, words: the mite of general ideas and

[036-313] artistic laws to be found in his book has been quarried by Grosse not

[036-314] from study of the reports brought back by travellers in savage lands,

[036-315] but from speculation on the forms of the spirit; and (inevitably) his

[036-316] interpretation of the former is reached by the light thrown on it by

[036-317] the latter. In his final definition, Grosse concludes by considering

[036-318] art as an activity which in its development or as its result, possesses

[036-319] immediate feeling-value (*Gefühlswerth*), and is an end to itself;

[036-320] practical and æsthetic activity are in direct mutual opposition between

[036-321] which as a middle term lies the activity of play, which like the

[036-322] practical activity has its end outside itself, but, like the æsthetic,

[036-323] finds its enjoyment not in its external end, which is more or less

[036-324] insignificant, but in its own activity.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_29_671) At the end of his book he

[036-325] remarks that the artistic activity of primitive peoples is hardly ever

[036-326] unaccompanied by the practical; and that art began by being social and

[036-327] became individual only in civilized times.[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_30_672)

[036-328] The Æsthetics of Taine and Grosse have also been described by the

[036-329] epithet sociological.

[036-330] *Sociological Æsthetic.*

[036-331] But since no one knows what the science of Sociology is, we must deal

[036-332] with the sociological superstition as we dealt with the naturalistic;

[036-333] that is to say, by skipping the preface with its proposals that

[036-334] can never be carried out, and seeing what it is that the objective

[036-335] necessities of the case have forced the author to assert, and which of

[036-336] the possible alternative views he accepts, or between what selection of

[036-337] them his allegiance wavers. During this examination we shall ignore the

[036-338] fairly common case of an author who while pretending to construct an

[036-339] Æsthetic simply compiles a list of facts connected with the history of

[036-340] art or civilization.

[036-341] *Proudhon.*

[036-342] Some social reformers of our day, like Proudhon, have revived the

[036-343] condemnations of Plato, or the mitigated moralism of antiquity and

[036-344] the Middle Ages. Proudhon denied the formula Art for Art's sake; he

[036-345] looked on art as a mere purveyor of sensuous pleasure, something which

[036-346] must be subordinated to legal and economical ends; poetry, sculpture,

[036-347] painting, music, romance, history, comedy, tragedy had for him no aim

[036-348] save exhortation to virtue and dissuasion from vice.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_31_673)

[036-349] *J. M. Guyau.*

[036-350] Development of social sympathy is the whole duty of art in the

[036-351] estimation of J. M. Guyau, who became famous as the founder of Social

[036-352] Æsthetic and was, according to certain [[france|French]] critics, inaugurator

[036-353] of the third epoch in the history of Æsthetic, the first being the

[036-354] æsthetic of the ideal (Plato), the second that of perception ([[immanuel-kant|Kant]]),

[036-355] and the third that of "Social Sympathy" (Guyau). In his *Problems

[036-356] of Contemporary Æsthetic* (1884) Guyau combats the theory of play,

[036-357] and substitutes that of Life; in a posthumous publication *Art in

[036-358] Its Sociological Aspect* (1889) he explains more clearly that the

[036-359] life of which he speaks is social life.[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_32_674) If the beautiful be the

[036-360] intellectually pleasing, certainly it cannot be identified with the

[036-361] useful which is only searching for what is pleasing; but the useful

[036-362] (says Guyau, in the belief that he is correcting both Kant and

[036-363] the evolutionists) does not always exclude the beautiful, of which

[036-364] indeed it often forms the lowest degree. The study of art is embraced

[036-365] partly,[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_33_675) not wholly, by Sociology: for art fulfils two ends, firstly

[036-366] and primarily that of provoking pleasant sensations (of colour, sound,

[036-367] etc.) and in this sense finds itself in the presence of practically

[036-368] incontestable scientific laws which connect Æsthetic with the physics

[036-369] (optics, acoustics, etc.), mathematics, physiology and psychophysics.

[036-370] Sculpture, in fact, rests especially on anatomy and physiology:

[036-371] painting on anatomy, physiology and optics: architecture on optics

[036-372] (golden section, etc.): music on physiology and acoustics: poetry on

[036-373] metrics, whose most general laws are acoustical and physiological. The

[036-374] second function of art is to produce the phenomena of "psychological

[036-375] induction," which bring to a head ideas and sentiments of most

[036-376] complex nature (sympathy with personages represented, interest, pity,

[036-377] indignation, etc.), in short all the social feelings, which constitute

[036-378] it "the expression of life." Whence are derived the two tendencies

[036-379] recognised in art; one inclining towards harmony, consonance, and

[036-380] everything delightful to ear and eye: the other towards the transfusion

[036-381] of life into the domain of art. [[genius|Genius]], true genius is destined to

[036-382] preserve the balance of the two tendencies: decadents and degenerates

[036-383] deprive art of its social sympathetic aim by setting æsthetic sympathy

[036-384] at war against human sympathy.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_34_676) Translating all this into familiar

[036-385] terms, we may say that Guy au asserts one purely hedonistic art, above

[036-386] which he superimposes another art, also hedonistic, but serviceable to

[036-387] the cause of morality.

[036-388] *M. Nordau.*

[036-389] The same polemic against decadents, degenerates and individualists

[036-390] is carried on by another writer, Max Nordau, who gives art the task

[036-391] of re-establishing the wholeness of life amongst the fragmentary

[036-392] specialisation characteristic of industrial society; he asserts that

[036-393] art for art's sake, art as the simple expression of internal states or

[036-394] the objectification of the artist's feelings, no doubt exists, but is

[036-395] merely "the art of Quaternary man, the art of the cave-dweller."[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_35_677)

[036-396] *Naturalism. C. Lombroso.*

[036-397] Naturalistic is the best term with which to qualify the Æsthetic

[036-398] derived from that identification of genius with degeneracy which made

[036-399] the fortune of Lombroso and his school. This identification derives

[036-400] its chief strength from the following piece of reasoning. Great mental

[036-401] efforts, total absorption in one dominating thought, often bring about

[036-402] physiological disorders in the bodily organism and weakness or atrophy

[036-403] of various vital functions. But such derangements come under the

[036-404] head of the pathological concept of illness, degeneration, madness.

[036-405] Therefore genius is identical with illness, degeneration and madness.

[036-406] A syllogism from particular to general, in which case, according to

[036-407] traditional Logic, *non est consequentia.* But with sociologists such

[036-408] as Nordau, Lombroso and company, we almost overstep the line separating

[036-409] respectable error from that grosser form which we call a blunder.

[036-410] A mere confusion between scientific analysis and historical inquiry

[036-411] or description is visible in the works of certain sociologists and

[036-412] anthropologists. Thus one of them, Carl Bücher, in studying the life of

[036-413] primitive peoples, asserts that poetry, music and work were originally

[036-414] fused in one single act; that poetry and music were used to regulate

[036-415] the rhythms of labour.[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_36_678) This may be historically true or false,

[036-416] important or no: it has nothing whatever to do with æsthetic science.

[036-417] In the same way Andrew Lang maintains that the doctrine concerning the

[036-418] origin of art as disinterested expression of the mimetic faculty finds

[036-419] no confirmation from what we know of primitive art, which is decorative

[036-420] rather than expressive:[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_37_679) as though primitive art, which is a mere

[036-421] fact awaiting interpretation, could ever be converted into a criterion

[036-422] for the interpretation of art in general.

[036-423] *Decline of Linguistic.*

[036-424] The same vague naturalism exercised a baneful influence on Linguistic,

[036-425] which of late years has been wholly lacking in such profound research

[036-426] as that inaugurated by Humboldt and followed up by Steinthal. But

[036-427] Steinthal never succeeded in founding a school. Max Müller, popular

[036-428] and inaccurate, maintained the indivisibility of speech and thought,

[036-429] confounding, or at least not distinguishing, æsthetic and logical

[036-430] thought; although at one time he had noted that the formation of

[036-431] names had a closer connexion with wit, in the sense of Locke, than

[036-432] with judgement. He maintained, moreover, that the science of language

[036-433] is not a historical but a natural science, because language is not

[036-434] the invention of man: the dilemma of "historical" and "natural" was

[036-435] canvassed and resolved over and over again with little result.[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_38_680)

[036-436] Another philologist, Whitney, attacked the "miraculous" theory of

[036-437] Müller and denied that thought is indivisible from speech: "The

[036-438] deaf-mute does not speak, but he can think," he observes; "thought is

[036-439] not function of the acoustic nerve." By this means Whitney relapsed

[036-440] into the ancient doctrine that speech is a symbol or means of

[036-441] expression, of human thought, subject to the will, the result of a

[036-442] synthesis of faculties and of a capacity for intelligent adaptation of

[036-443] means to end.[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_39_681)

[036-444] *Signs of revival. H. Paul.*

[036-445] Philosophical spirit reappeared in Paul's *Principles of the History

[036-446] of Language* (1880),[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_40_682) though the author's efforts to defend himself

[036-447] from the terrifying accusation of being a philosopher led him to hunt

[036-448] out a fresh title to replace the scandalous "Philosophy of Language."

[036-449] But if Paul is vague about the relation of Logic to Grammar, he must

[036-450] be given every credit for identifying, as Humboldt had already done,

[036-451] the question of the origin of language with that of its nature; and

[036-452] reasserting that language is created afresh whenever we speak. He

[036-453] must also be given credit for having conclusively criticized the

[036-454] Ethnopsychology (*Völkerpsychologie*) of Steinthal and Lazarus, showing

[036-455] that there is no such thing as collective psyche and that there can be

[036-456] no language other than of the individual.

[036-457] *The linguistic of Wundt.*

[036-458] Wundt[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_41_683) on the other hand attached the study of language, mythology

[036-459] and customs to this non-existent science of Ethnopsychology; in his

[036-460] latest work, on this very subject of language,[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_42_684) he foolishly echoes

[036-461] Whitney's gibes and denounces as a "miracle theory" (*Wundertheorie*)

[036-462] that glorious doctrine inaugurated by Herder and Humboldt, whom he

[036-463] accuses of "mystical obscurity" (*mystiche Dunkel*): he observes that

[036-464] this view may have had some justification before the principle of

[036-465] evolution had reached its triumphant application to organic nature in

[036-466] general and to man in particular. He has not the faintest notion of

[036-467] the function of imagination, or of the true relation between thought

[036-468] and expression; he finds no substantial difference between expression

[036-469] in the naturalistic, and expression in the spiritual and linguistic

[036-470] sense; he considers language as a special highly developed form of the

[036-471] vital psychophysical manifestations and of the expressive movements

[036-472] of animals. Out of these facts language is developed by imperceptible

[036-473] gradations; so that, beyond the general concept of expressive movement

[036-474] (*Ausdrucksbewegung*) "there is no specific mark by which language can

[036-475] be distinguished in any but an arbitrary manner."[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_43_685) The philosophy

[036-476] of Wundt betrays its own weakness by showing its inability to master

[036-477] the problem of language and art. In his *Ethics* æsthetic facts are

[036-478] presented as a complex of logical and ethical elements; the existence

[036-479] of æsthetic as a special normative science is denied, not for the good

[036-480] and sufficient reason that there are no such things as "normative

[036-481] sciences," but because this special science is said by him to be

[036-482] absorbed by the two sciences of Logic and Ethics,[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-24.htm.html#Footnote_44_686) which amounts to

[036-483] denying the existence of Æsthetic and the originality of art.
