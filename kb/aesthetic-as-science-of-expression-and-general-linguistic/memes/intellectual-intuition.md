# Intellectual Intuition

Intellectual intuition (*intellektuelle Anschauung*) names a supposed faculty by which the mind directly grasps reality through pure thought, without the mediation of sense or discursive reasoning. In Croce's Æsthetic, it stands as a paradigmatic pseudo-concept — something that "was held to be" a genuine power of the mind but dissolves under analysis (009-133). Croce systematically denies intellectual intuition any place in philosophy, in æsthetic theory, or in the account of spiritual activity.

## Definition

The term "intellectual intuition" belongs to the vocabulary of German Idealism, where it designated a mode of cognition higher than both sensory intuition and conceptual reasoning — a direct intellectual grasp of the absolute. Croce rejects this outright: "There is therefore no intellectual intuition in philosophy, as there is none in art" (009-176). What his predecessors called intellectual intuition is either ordinary intuition (the æsthetic moment) or conceptual thought — never a third, higher faculty.

## In Croce's System

### The Rejection of a Third Cognitive Faculty

Croce's philosophy of spirit recognises only two theoretical activities: intuition (the æsthetic) and conception (the logical). Intellectual intuition would be a third — and for Croce, "an intellectual intuition or apperception" (028-568) is simply not given in experience. The desire to posit such a faculty arises from confusing the distinctness of intuition with the universality of the concept, trying to amalgamate what cannot be amalgamated.

### Kant's Limiting Concept

Kant occupies an ambivalent position in this history. "Intellectual intuition, which for Kant was a limiting concept" (028-382), served to mark the boundary of human cognition: we cannot intuit intellectually, but a divine intellect might. Croce treats this Kantian caution as correct in its negative moment — Kant did not claim to possess intellectual intuition — but notes that the post-Kantians erased the limit that Kant had drawn.

### The German Metaphysicians' Error

For the post-Kantian idealists, intellectual intuition became the organ of metaphysical knowledge. Croce groups this with "the intellectual intuition of German metaphysicians" (034-097) as a prime example of philosophy overstepping its bounds. The claim to intuit the absolute intellectually is, for Croce, a confusion between the æsthetic (which intuits particulars) and the logical (which thinks universals) — trying to do both at once and achieving neither.

## Historical Context

### Origins in German Idealism

The concept of intellectual intuition entered modern philosophy through Fichte, Schelling, and Hegel, who each posited some form of direct intellectual access to reality beyond the Kantian bounds of possible experience. Fichte's "intellectual intuition of the self," Schelling's "intellectual intuition of the absolute," and Hegel's speculative dialectic all treated intellectual intuition as the philosopher's proper method.

### Croce's Critique in Context

Croce's rejection belongs to his broader campaign against "historicism and intellectualism in æsthetic" (see [[historicism]] and [[intellectualism]]). By the late nineteenth century, intellectual intuition had been criticised from multiple directions — empiricist, positivist, and neo-Kantian. Croce's contribution is to show that intellectual intuition is not merely empirically unsupported but conceptually incoherent: it asks intuition to do the work of the concept and the concept to do the work of intuition (035-305).

### The Mysterious Faculties

In his historical survey, Croce groups intellectual intuition with "the other mysterious faculties" (037-145) that aestheticians have invoked to explain what resists explanation. Like [[sympathy]], [[taste]], and the [[je-ne-sais-quoi]], intellectual intuition is a name for a gap in understanding, not a genuine capacity of mind.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's dismissal of intellectual intuition has been questioned by scholars who see in late Schelling and in twentieth-century phenomenology a more defensible version of the idea — one that does not conflate intuition with concept but describes a distinct mode of intellectual receptivity (009-170).
>
> The Kantian "limiting concept" that Croce endorses may itself understate the role of intellectual intuition in Kant's own account of aesthetic judgment, where the free play of imagination and understanding has a quasi-intuitive character that resists Croce's sharp intuition-concept dichotomy.
