# Verisimilitude

Verisimilitude occupies a modest but significant place in Croce's aesthetic system. Unlike the grand metaphysical concepts — beauty, expression, intuition — verisimilitude belongs to the practical sphere of historical judgement and, in aesthetics, to the subordinate domain of sensory richness rather than to the core of artistic truth. Croce treats verisimilitude not as a constitutive principle of art but as a regulative principle of criticism: when certainty is unavailable, probability must suffice. This modesty is itself a philosophical position — a refusal to inflate the probable into the necessary.

## Definition

Croce introduces verisimilitude in the context of historical method. When the distinction between real and unreal intuitions becomes too fine to discern, "we must either renounce for the time being at least the knowledge of what really happened (and this we often do), or we must fall back upon conjecture, verisimilitude, probability. The principle of verisimilitude and of probability in fact dominates all historical criticism" (004-223–224). Verisimilitude, in this sense, is not a defect of historical knowledge but its operative principle: "examination of sources and authorities is devoted to establishing the most credible evidence. And what is the most credible evidence, save that of the best observers, that is, of those who best remember and (be it understood) have not wished to falsify, nor had interest in falsifying the truth of things?" (004-223).

The definition is deceptively simple. Croce is not asserting that history is merely probable; he is distinguishing the kind of certainty proper to history — the certainty of memory and testimony — from the kind proper to science — the certainty of analysis and demonstration. Verisimilitude is the form that truth takes when it is mediated by human testimony rather than deduced from axioms.

## In Croce's System

### Verisimilitude in Aesthetics vs. History

In the aesthetic domain, verisimilitude appears in a different — and for Croce, subordinate — guise. In his survey of Baumgarten's school, Croce notes that Meier's *Principles of all the Beautiful Sciences* classified "beauty of sense-apprehension" into a catalogue of qualities including "æsthetic richness, grandeur, verisimilitude, vivacity, certainty, sensitive life and wit" (025-232). Here, verisimilitude is one property among many in the heuristic branch of the Baumgartenian system — a quality of beautiful thought rather than a structural feature of expression.

Croce's implicit criticism is that this catalogue approach mistakes attributes of reception for constituents of expression. Whether a work is "verisimilar" — whether it conforms to our expectations of how things are likely to be — tells us nothing about whether it succeeds as expression. A fantastical poem may be perfectly expressive, while a meticulously verisimilar representation may be aesthetically dead. The error is characteristic of the rationalist aesthetic: it substitutes classification for understanding.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp distinction between **verisimilitude as historical probability** and **verisimilitude as aesthetic quality**. The first is a legitimate principle of critical method; the second is an extrinsic criterion that has no bearing on artistic truth. When we judge a work of art "probable" or "improbable," we are applying a standard derived from our experience of the actual world — but art creates its own world, and its coherence is internal, not referential. Croce makes this point explicitly in his critique of the probabilists, noting that "by probability used really to be meant the artistic" verisimilitude of a coherent imaginative world, not conformity to statistical likelihood (005-012) — but art creates its own world, and its coherence is internal, not referential.

A second distinction opposes **verisimilitude to certainty**. "The certainty of history differs from that of science. It is the certainty of memory and of authority, not that of analysis and demonstration" (004-223). Historical certainty is not inferior to scientific certainty; it is simply different in kind. Croce's point has an aesthetic corollary: the certainty of artistic intuition is neither the certainty of logic nor the probability of empirical judgement. It is the certainty of successful expression — the immediate recognition that form has been given to content.

## Criticism and Debate

> The certainty of history differs from that of science. It is the certainty of memory and of authority, not that of analysis and demonstration. To speak of historical induction or demonstration is to make a metaphorical use of these expressions, which bear a quite different meaning in history to that which they bear in science. The conviction of the historian is the undemonstrable conviction of the juryman, who has heard the witnesses, listened attentively to the case, and prayed Heaven to inspire him. (004-223)

## Historical Context

The concept of verisimilitude has a long history in Western poetics, from Aristotle's distinction between the historian who relates "what happened" and the poet who relates "what might happen" (*Poetics* 1451b), through the neo-classical doctrine of the *vraisemblable*, to the probabilistic revolution in eighteenth-century epistemology. The French classical tradition, from Corneille to Boileau, made *vraisemblance* a central principle of dramatic art: the poet need not tell the truth, but must tell what is plausible. This doctrine was both a constraint on artistic freedom and a recognition that art must carry conviction.

Croce's treatment of verisimilitude departs from this tradition by relocating the concept from poetics to historical method. In a dedicated section on the "Criticism of probability and of naturalism" (005-005), Croce argues that the concept of probability, when applied to art, has been systematically misunderstood: "By probability used really to be meant the artistic" coherence of the representation rather than its conformity to empirical likelihood (005-012). By making verisimilitude the principle of historical criticism rather than of artistic creation, he frees art from the demand for probability while preserving the concept's utility in the domain where it properly belongs — the evaluation of testimony. This move is characteristic of Croce's larger project: the emancipation of art from all extrinsic norms, whether moral, cognitive, or probabilistic.
