# Metaphor

In Croce's aesthetic system, metaphor is not an ornament added to language but a fundamental feature of expression itself. The concept of metaphor is dissolved into the broader theory of expression.

## Definition

The classical definition of metaphor is "a word used in place of the proper word" (010-069). But Croce questions this definition: if metaphor is "the improper word," what makes a word proper or improper (010-074)? The distinction between proper and metaphorical language cannot be maintained at the level of expression.

Croce connects metaphor to the "metaphorical and mythological activity" of the human spirit (002-176). Metaphor is not a deviation from normal language but is continuous with the expressive activity itself. "To make a metaphorical use" of words is simply to express (004-235).

## In Croce's System

### Metaphor as Expression

Croce treats metaphor not as a figure of speech but as a mode of expression. The distinction between "simple and ornate, proper and metaphorical" language belongs to rhetoric, not to aesthetics (010-062). Rhetoric classifies "the fourteen forms of metaphor, the figures of word and sentence" as external ornaments, but for Croce, there are no ornaments — there is only expression (010-063).

### Key Distinctions

1. **Metaphor vs. proper speech** — An empirical, not a philosophical distinction. All language is in some sense metaphorical because all language is expression, and expression is always individual and creative.

2. **Metaphor as ornament** — The rhetorical tradition treats metaphor as decoration. Croce rejects this: metaphor is not something added to expression but an aspect of expression itself.

3. **Metaphor and myth** — Croce connects metaphor to "the metaphorical and mythological activity" (002-176). Myth is extended metaphor; metaphor is condensed myth. Both are forms of the expressive activity that characterizes the aesthetic spirit.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Aristotle defined metaphor as "the application of an alien name," establishing the classical tradition that treats metaphor as a deviation from literal speech. The rhetorical tradition classified metaphors into kinds and prescribed rules for their use. This tradition treats metaphor as a technique — something that can be learned and applied.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of metaphor is part of his broader dissolution of rhetoric. If all expression is individual and creative, there is no general theory of metaphor. Each metaphor is a unique expressive act, and the classification of metaphors into kinds is an empirical exercise that tells us nothing about the nature of art. "Neither such metaphysical nor such metaphorical meaning is ours" (007-013) — Croce rejects both the metaphysical elevation and the rhetorical reduction of metaphor.

## Criticism and Debate

> The definition of metaphor as of a word used in place of the proper word has been the foundation of rhetorical theory for millennia (010-069).

Critics have argued that Croce's dissolution of metaphor as a category ignores the distinctive cognitive work that metaphor performs. Lakoff and Johnson, for example, argue that metaphor is fundamental to thought, not just language. Croce's response might be that this cognitive work is precisely what he means by expression — metaphor is not a special case but the general case.
