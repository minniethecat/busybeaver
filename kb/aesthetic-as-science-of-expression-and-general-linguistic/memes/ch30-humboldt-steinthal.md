[030-001] *Æsthetic of content and Æsthetic of form: meaning of the

[030-002] contrast.*

[030-003] We have now reached a point when we are able to give ourselves an exact

[030-004] account of the signification and importance of the celebrated war

[030-005] waged for over a century in [[germany|Germany]] between the Æsthetic of content

[030-006] (*Gehaltsästhetik)* and the Æsthetic of form (*[[form|Form]]ästhetik*); a war

[030-007] which gave birth to vast works on the history of Æsthetic undertaken

[030-008] from one or other point of view, and sprang from [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]]'s opposition

[030-009] to the idealism of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]], [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], and their contemporaries and

[030-010] followers. "Form" and "[[content|Content]]" are among the most equivocal words

[030-011] in the whole philosophical vocabulary, particularly in Æsthetic;

[030-012] sometimes, indeed, what one calls form, others call content. The

[030-013] Herbartians were specially given to quoting in their own defence

[030-014] [[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]]'s dictum, that the secret of art consists in "cancelling

[030-015] content by form." But what is there in common between Schiller's

[030-016] concept of "form," which placed the æsthetic activity side by side

[030-017] with the moral and intellectual, and Herbart's "form," which does not

[030-018] penetrate or enliven, but clothes and adorns a content? Hegel, on

[030-019] the other hand, often gives the name "form" to what Schiller would

[030-020] call "matter" (*Stoff*), that is, the sensible matter which it is

[030-021] the business of spiritual energy to dominate. Hegel's "content" is

[030-022] the idea, the metaphysical truth, the constituent element of beauty:

[030-023] Herbart's "content" is the emotional and intellectual element which

[030-024] falls outside beauty. The Æsthetic of "form" in [[italy|Italy]] is an æsthetic of

[030-025] expressive activity; the form is neither a clothing nor a metaphysical

[030-026] idea nor sensible matter, but a representative or imaginative faculty

[030-027] with the power of framing impressions; yet there have been attempts

[030-028] to confute this Italian æsthetic formalism with the same arguments

[030-029] that are used against German æsthetic formalism, a totally different

[030-030] thing in every respect. And so forth. Having given a plain account of

[030-031] the thoughts of the post-Kantian æstheticians, we shall be able to

[030-032] appreciate their opponents without seeking light from their obscure

[030-033] terminology or allowing ourselves to be misled by the banners they

[030-034] wave. The antithesis between the Æsthetic of content and that of

[030-035] form, the Æsthetic of idealism and that of realism, the Æsthetic of

[030-036] Schelling, [[karl-solger|Solger]], Hegel and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] and that of Herbart, will

[030-037] appear in its true light, as the lamily quarrel between two conceptions

[030-038] of art united by a common mysticism, although one is destined almost to

[030-039] meet with truth during its long journey, while the other wanders ever

[030-040] further away.

[030-041] The first half of the nineteenth century was for Germany a period of

[030-042] many fine-sounding philosophical formulæ: subjectivism, objectivism,

[030-043] subjective—objectivism; abstract, concrete, abstract-concrete;

[030-044] idealism, realism, idealism—realism; between pantheism and theism

[030-045] Krause inserted his pan-en-theism. In the midst of this uproar, in

[030-046] which the second-rate men shouted down the first-rate and made good

[030-047] their claim to their only true property, namely words, it is not

[030-048] surprising that a few modest clear thinkers, philosophers who preferred

[030-049] to think about realities, should have the worst of it and remain

[030-050] unheard and unnoticed, lost among the roaring crowd or labelled with a

[030-051] false ticket.

[030-052] *[[friedrich-schleiermacher|Friedrich Schleiermacher]].*

[030-053] This, at least, seems to have been the lot of Friedrich Schleiermacher,

[030-054] whose æsthetic doctrine is amongst the least known although it is

[030-055] perhaps the most noteworthy of the day.

[030-056] *Wrong judgements concerning him.*

[030-057] Schleiermacher delivered his first lectures on Æsthetic at Berlin

[030-058] University in 1819, and from that date he began to study the subject

[030-059] seriously with a view to writing a book on it. He repeated his

[030-060] lectures on two occasions, in 1825 and 1832-1833; but his death,

[030-061] which occurred in the following year, prevented him from carrying

[030-062] out his plan, and all we know of his thoughts on Æsthetic comes from

[030-063] his lectures, as collected by his pupils and published in 1842.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_1_450) A

[030-064] Herbartian historian of Æsthetic, [[robert-zimmermann|Zimmermann]], attacks the posthumous

[030-065] work of Schleiermacher with real ferocity; after twenty pages of

[030-066] invective and sarcasm he concludes by asking, how could his pupils

[030-067] so dishonour their great master by publishing such a mass of waste

[030-068] paper, "all play upon words, sophistical conceits and dialectical

[030-069] subtleties"?[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_2_451) Nor was the idealistic historian Hartmann much more

[030-070] benevolent when he describes the work as "a confused mess in which,

[030-071] among much that is merely trivial, many half-truths and exaggerations,

[030-072] one can detect a few acute observations"; and says that, in order

[030-073] to make bearable "such unctuous afternoon sermons delivered by a

[030-074] preacher in his dotage," it must be shortened by three-quarters; and

[030-075] that, "as regards fundamental principles," it is simply useless,

[030-076] offering no innovations upon concrete idealism as presented by Hegel

[030-077] and others; and that, in any case, it seems impossible "to attach it

[030-078] to any line of thought except the Hegelian, to which Schleiermacher's

[030-079] contribution is only of second-rate importance." He further observes

[030-080] that Schleiermacher was primarily a theologian, and in philosophy more

[030-081] or less an amateur.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_3_452) Now it cannot be denied that Schleiermacher's

[030-082] doctrine has reached us in a hazy form, by no means free from

[030-083] uncertainties and contradictions; and, which is more important,

[030-084] it is here and there affected for the worse by the influence of

[030-085] contemporary metaphysics. But, side by side with these defects, what

[030-086] excellent method, really scientific and philosophical; what a number of

[030-087] cornerstones well and truly laid; what wealth of new truths, and of

[030-088] difficulties and problems not suspected or discussed before his day!

[030-089] *Schleiermacher contrasted with his predecessors.*

[030-090] Schleiermacher considered Æsthetic as an essentially modern line

[030-091] of thought, and drew a sharp distinction between the *Poetics* of

[030-092] Aristotle, which never shakes itself free from the empirical standpoint

[030-093] of the maker of rules, and what [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]] tried to do in the

[030-094] eighteenth century. He praised [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] for having been the first truly

[030-095] to include Æsthetic among the philosophical sciences, and recognized

[030-096] that in Hegel [[art|artistic activity]] had attained the highest elevation by

[030-097] being brought into connexion and almost into equality with religion

[030-098] and philosophy. But he was not satisfied either with the followers of

[030-099] Baumgarten when they degenerated into the absurd attempt to construct

[030-100] a science or theory of sensuous pleasure, or with the Kantian point of

[030-101] view which made its principal aim the consideration of taste; or with

[030-102] the philosophy of Fichte, in which art became a means of education; or

[030-103] with the more widely received opinion which placed at the centre of

[030-104] Æsthetic the vague and equivocal concept of [[beauty|Beauty]]. Schiller pleased

[030-105] him by having called attention to the moment of artistic spontaneity or

[030-106] productiveness, and he praised Schelling for having laid stress on the

[030-107] importance of the figurative arts, which lend themselves less easily

[030-108] than poetry to facile and illusory moralistic interpretations.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_4_453)

[030-109] Having with the utmost clearness excluded from Æsthetic the study of

[030-110] practical rules as empirical, and therefore irreducible to a science,

[030-111] he assigned to Æsthetic the task of determining the proper position of

[030-112] artistic activity in the scheme of ethics.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_5_454)

[030-113] *Place assigned to Æsthetic in his Ethics.*

[030-114] To avoid falling into error over this terminology, we must call to

[030-115] mind that the philosophy of Schleiermacher followed the ancient

[030-116] traditions in its tripartite division into Dialectic, Ethics and

[030-117] Physics. Dialectic corresponds with ontology; Physics embraces all

[030-118] the sciences of natural facts; Ethics includes the study of all free

[030-119] activities of mankind (language, thought, art, religion and morality).

[030-120] Ethics represented to him not only the science of morality but what

[030-121] others name Psychology or, better still, the Science or Philosophy of

[030-122] the Spirit. This explanation once given, Schleiermacher's point of

[030-123] departure seems to be the only one just and permissible, and we shall

[030-124] not be surprised when he talks of will, of voluntary acts and so on,

[030-125] where others would have simply spoken of activity or spiritual energy;

[030-126] he even endows such expressions with a broader meaning than that

[030-127] conferred upon them by practical philosophy.

[030-128] *Æsthetic activity as immanent and individual.*

[030-129] A double distinction may be made amongst human activities. In the

[030-130] first place, there are activities which we presume to be constituted

[030-131] in the same manner in all men (such as the logical activity) and are

[030-132] called activities of identity; and others whose diversity is presumed,

[030-133] which are called activities of difference or individual activities.

[030-134] Secondly, there are activities which exhaust themselves in the

[030-135] internal life, and others which actualize themselves in the external

[030-136] world: immanent activities and practical activities. To which of the

[030-137] two classes in each of the two orders does artistic activity belong?

[030-138] There can be no doubt of its different modes of development, if not

[030-139] actually in each individual person, at least in different peoples and

[030-140] nations; therefore it belongs properly to activities of difference or

[030-141] individual activities.[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_6_455) As for the other distinction, it is true

[030-142] that art does realize itself in the external world, but this fact is

[030-143] something superadded ("*ein später Hinzukommendes*") "which stands to

[030-144] the internal fact as the communication of thought by means of speech

[030-145] or writing stands to thought itself": art's true work is the internal

[030-146] [[representation|image]] ("*das innere Bild ist das eigentliche Kunstwerk*"). Exceptions

[030-147] to this might be adduced, such as mimicry; but they would be apparent

[030-148] only. Between a really angry man and the actor who plays the part of

[030-149] an angry man on the stage there is this difference: in the second case

[030-150] anger appears as controlled and therefore beautiful; that is, the

[030-151] internal image is in the actor's soul interposed between the fact of

[030-152] passion and its physical manifestation.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-18.htm.html#Footnote_7_456) Artistic activity "belongs

[030-153] to those human activities in which we presuppose the individual in its

[030-154] differentiation; it belongs equally to those activities developing

[030-155] essentially within themselves and not completing themselves in any

[030-156] external world. Art, therefore, is an immanent activity in which we

[030-157] presuppose differentiation." Internal, not practical: individual, not

[030-158] universal or logical.

[030-159] *Artistic truth and intellectual truth.*

[030-160] But if art be one form of thought, there must be one form of thought

[030-161] in which identity is presupposed, and another in which difference is

[030-162] presupposed. We do not look for truth in poetry; or, rather, we do look

[030-163] for truth, but for one that is totally different from that objective

[030-164] truth to which there must correspond some being, either universal or

[030-165] individual (scientific and historical truth). "When a character in a

[030-166] poem is said to be devoid of truth, a slur is cast on the given poem;

[030-167] but if the character is said to be a pure invention, corresponding with

[030-168] no reality, that is quite a different matter." The truth of a poetic

[030-169] character consists in the coherence with which a single person's divers

[030-170] modes of thinking and acting are represented: even in portraits it is

[030-171] not an exact correspondence with an objective reality that makes the

[030-172] thing a work of art. From art and poetry "springs no iota of knowledge"

[030-173] (*das Geringste vom Wissen*); "it expresses but the truth of the single

[030-174] consciousness." There are then "productions of thought and of sensible

[030-175] intuitions, opposed to the other productions because they do not

[030-176] presuppose identity, and they express the singular as such."[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_8_457)

[030-177] *Difference of artistic consciousness from feeling and

[030-178] religion.*

[030-179] The domain of art is immediate self-consciousness (*unmittelbare

[030-180] Selbstbewusstsein*), which must be carefully distinguished from the

[030-181] thought or concept of the ego or of the determinate ego. This latter is

[030-182] the consciousness of identity in the diversity of moments; immediate

[030-183] self-consciousness is "diversity itself, of which one must be aware,

[030-184] since life in its entirety is but the development of consciousness." In

[030-185] this domain art has often been confused with two facts which accompany

[030-186] it: sensuous consciousness (the feeling of pleasure and pain), and

[030-187] religion. A double confusion, of which the sensationalists fall into

[030-188] the first half and Hegel into the second; Schleiermacher clears it up

[030-189] by proving that art is free productivity, whereas sensuous pleasure and

[030-190] religious feeling, however different in other ways, are both determined

[030-191] by an objective fact (*äussere Sein*).[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_9_458)

[030-192] *Dreams and art: inspiration and deliberation.*

[030-193] The better to understand this free productivity, we must further

[030-194] circumscribe the domain of immediate consciousness. In this we can

[030-195] find nothing more helpful than comparing it with the images produced

[030-196] by dreams. The artist has his own dreams: he dreams with open eyes,

[030-197] and from among the thick-thronging images of this dream-state those

[030-198] having sufficient energy alone become works of art, the rest remaining

[030-199] a mere background from which the others stand out. All the essential

[030-200] elements of art are found in the dream-state, which is the production

[030-201] of free thoughts and sensuous intuitions consisting of mere images.

[030-202] Certainly something is lacking in dreams, and they differ from art not

[030-203] only in their absence of technique, which has already been excluded as

[030-204] irrelevant to art, but in another way, viz. that a dream is a chaotic

[030-205] fact, without stability, order, connexion or measure. But when some

[030-206] sort of order is introduced into the chaos the difference at once

[030-207] disappears, and the likeness to art merges in identity. This internal

[030-208] activity which introduces order and measure, fixes and determines the

[030-209] image, is that which distinguishes art from a dream or transforms a

[030-210] dream into art. It often involves struggle, labour, the obligation to

[030-211] stem the involuntary flood of internal images; in a word, it means

[030-212] reflexion or deliberation. But the dream and the cessation of dreaming

[030-213] are equally indispensable elements of art. There must be production of

[030-214] thoughts and images and, together with such production, there must

[030-215] be measure, determination and unity, "otherwise each image would be

[030-216] confused with its neighbour and have no definiteness." The instant of

[030-217] inspiration (*Begeisterung*) is as essential as that of deliberation

[030-218] (*Besonnenheit)*.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_10_459)

[030-219] *Art and the typical.*

[030-220] But in order to arrive at artistic truth it is also I necessary (here

[030-221] Schleiermacher's thought becomes less clear and accurate) that the

[030-222] singular be accompanied by consciousness of the species; consciousness

[030-223] of the self as individual man is impossible without consciousness of

[030-224] mankind; nor is a single object true unless referred to its universal.

[030-225] In a pictured landscape "every tree must possess natural truth, that

[030-226] is to say, it must be contemplated as a specimen of a given kind;

[030-227] similarly, the whole complex of natural and individual life must have

[030-228] effective truth of nature and constitute a single harmony. Just because

[030-229] in art we do not strive after the production of individual figures

[030-230] in themselves and for themselves, but their internal truth as well,

[030-231] we commonly assign to them a high place as being a free realization

[030-232] of that in which all cognition has its value, that is to say, in the

[030-233] principle that all forms of being are inherent in the human spirit.

[030-234] If this principle fails, truth is no longer possible; scepticism only

[030-235] remains." The productions of art are the ideal or typical figures

[030-236] which real nature would create were it not impeded by external

[030-237] influences.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_11_460) "The artist creates a figure on the basis of a general

[030-238] scheme, rejecting whatever may hinder or impede the play of the living

[030-239] forces of reality; such a production, founded on a general scheme, is

[030-240] what we call the Ideal."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_12_461)

[030-241] In spite of all these determinations, Schleiermacher did not apparently

[030-242] intend to limit the artist's scope. He remarks, "When an artist

[030-243] represents something really given, whether portrait, landscape or

[030-244] single human figure, he renounces the freedom of productivity and

[030-245] adheres to the real."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_13_462) There is a twofold tendency at work in the

[030-246] artist: towards perfection of type, and towards representation of

[030-247] natural reality. An artist must not fall into the abstractness of

[030-248] the type or into the unmeaningness of empirical reality.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_14_463) If in

[030-249] flower-painting it is necessary to bring out the specific type, a much

[030-250] more complete individualisation is demanded when representing man,

[030-251] owing to the lofty position which he occupies.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_15_464) Representation

[030-252] of the ideal in the real does not exclude "an infinite variety,

[030-253] such as is found in actual reality." "For instance, the human face

[030-254] wavers between the ideal and caricature, in its moral conformation

[030-255] no less than in its physical. Every human face contains elements of

[030-256] disfigurement (*Verbildung,*) but it has also something by which it

[030-257] is a determinate modification of human nature; this does not appear

[030-258] openly, but a practised eye can seize it and ideally complete the face

[030-259] in question."[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_16_465) Schleiermacher is keenly aware of the difficulties

[030-260] and perplexities of' such problems as the question whether there exists

[030-261] one or many ideals of the human face.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_17_466) He observes that the two

[030-262] views which strive for mastery in the field of poetry may be extended

[030-263] to art as a whole. Some assert that poetry and art should represent the

[030-264] perfect, the ideal, that which would have been produced by nature, had

[030-265] she not been prevented by mechanical forces; others reject the ideal as

[030-266] incapable of realisation and prefer that the artist should depict man

[030-267] as he really is, with those perturbing elements which in reality belong

[030-268] to him no less than his ideal qualities. Each view is a half-truth:

[030-269] it is the duty of art to represent the ideal as well as the real, the

[030-270] subjective as well as the objective.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_18_467) The comic element, that is the

[030-271] unideal and the faulty ideal, is included in the circle of art.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_19_468)

[030-272] *Independence of art.*

[030-273] In respect to morality, art is free just as philosophical speculation

[030-274] is free: its essence excludes practical and moral effects. This leads

[030-275] to the proposition that "there is no difference between various

[030-276] works of art, except in so far as they can be compared in respect

[030-277] of artistic perfection" (*Vollkommenheit in der Kunst.*) "Given an

[030-278] artistic object perfect of its kind, it has an absolute value which

[030-279] cannot be increased or diminished by anything else. If motions of

[030-280] the will could truly be described as consequences of works of art, a

[030-281] different standard of values would apply to works of art: and since

[030-282] the objects which an artist may depict are not all equally adapted to

[030-283] influence volition, a scale of values would exist which did not depend

[030-284] on artistic perfection." Nor must we confound the judgement passed

[030-285] upon the varied and complex personality of the artist himself with the

[030-286] strictly æsthetic judgement passed upon his work. "In this respect

[030-287] the biggest, most complicated canvas is on a level with the smallest

[030-288] arabesque, the longest poem with the shortest: the value of a work of

[030-289] art depends on the perfect manner in which the external corresponds to

[030-290] the internal."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_20_469)

[030-291] Schleiermacher rejects the doctrine of Schiller because in his opinion

[030-292] it makes art a sort of game or pastime in contrast to the serious

[030-293] affairs of life: a view, he says, for business men to whom their

[030-294] business is the only serious thing. Artistic activity is universally

[030-295] human, a man devoid of it is inconceivable; although, of course, there

[030-296] are in this respect great differences betwixt man and man, running from

[030-297] the mere desire to enjoy art to real taste, and from this again to

[030-298] productive genius.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_21_470)

[030-299] *Art and language.*

[030-300] The artist makes use of instruments which, by their nature, are framed

[030-301] not for the individual but for the universal; of this kind is language.

[030-302] But it is the business of poetry to extract the individual from

[030-303] language which is universal without giving to its productions the form

[030-304] of the antithesis between individual and universal which is proper to

[030-305] science. Of the two elements of language, the musical and the logical,

[030-306] the poet claims the first for his own ends and constrains the other

[030-307] to awaken individual images. In comparison with pure science as in

[030-308] comparison with the individual image, there is something irrational

[030-309] about language: but the tendencies of speculation and of poetry are

[030-310] always contrary, even in their use of language; the former tends to

[030-311] make language approximate to mathematical formulæ; the latter to

[030-312] imagery (*Bild*)[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_22_471)

[030-313] *Schleiermacher's defects.*

[030-314] Leaving out many details which will be touched on in their

[030-315] proper places, the foregoing is a fair summary of the heads of

[030-316] Schleiermacher's æsthetic thought. Adding up the accounts of the

[030-317] whole statement of views, on the side of error and oversight we

[030-318] find: first, ideas or types are not wholly excluded, in spite

[030-319] of all Schleiermacher's care and anxiety to safeguard artistic

[030-320] individualisation and to make the ideas and types superfluous.

[030-321] Secondly, there is still, undefeated and unexpelled, a certain residue

[030-322] of abstract formalism, visible at various points of his theories.[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_23_472)

[030-323] Thirdly, the definition of art as an activity of mere difference may be

[030-324] diluted but is not destroyed by making art a difference of complexes

[030-325] of individuals, a national difference. A closer reflexion on the

[030-326] history of art, a recognition of the possibility of appreciating the

[030-327] art of various nations and various times, a more patient investigation

[030-328] into the moment of artistic reproduction, even an examination of the

[030-329] relation between science and art, would have led Schleiermacher to

[030-330] treat this difference as empirical and surmountable, still holding

[030-331] firmly to the distinctive character (individual as opposed to

[030-332] universal) he assigned to art in comparison with science. Fourthly, he

[030-333] did not recognize the identity of æsthetic activity with linguistic,

[030-334] and failed to make it the basis of all other theoretic activity. It

[030-335] would seem, moreover, that Schleiermacher had no clear ideas concerning

[030-336] that artistic element which enters into the constitution of historic

[030-337] narrative and is indispensable as the concrete form of science; or

[030-338] concerning language, taken not as a complex of abstract means of

[030-339] expression but as expressive activity.

[030-340] *Schleiermacher's services to Æsthetic.*

[030-341] These defects and uncertainties may perhaps be attributable in part

[030-342] to the fact that his thoughts on æsthetic have reached us in an

[030-343] inchoate form, very far from a mature development. But if on the other

[030-344] hand we wish to cast up the sum of his very striking merits, it will

[030-345] suffice to run over the list of accusations heaped upon him by the two

[030-346] historians before mentioned, Zimmermann and Hartmann. Schleiermacher

[030-347] has denuded Æsthetic of its imperative character; he recognizes in it a

[030-348] form of thought differing from logical thought; he gives this science

[030-349] a non-metaphysical and merely anthropological character; he denies

[030-350] the concept of beauty, substituting that of artistic perfection, and

[030-351] actually affirms the æsthetic equivalence of small and great works of

[030-352] art, so long as each is perfect in its own sphere; he considers the

[030-353] æsthetic fact as pure human productivity: and so on and so forth. All

[030-354] these criticisms are meant for blame and are really praise; for what

[030-355] is blame to the mind of a Zimmermann or a Hartmann, is to ours praise.

[030-356] In the metaphysical orgy of his day, in the perpetual building and

[030-357] pulling down of more or less arbitrary systems, Schleiermacher the

[030-358] theologian, with philosophic acumen, fixed his eye upon what was really

[030-359] characteristic of the æsthetic fact and succeeded in defining its

[030-360] properties and connexions; when he failed to see clearly and wandered

[030-361] from the track, he never abandoned analysis for fantastic caprice.

[030-362] By his discovery that the obscure region of immediate consciousness

[030-363] is also that of the æsthetic fact, he seems to bid his distracted

[030-364] contemporaries listen to the old adage: *Hic Rhodus, hic salta.*
