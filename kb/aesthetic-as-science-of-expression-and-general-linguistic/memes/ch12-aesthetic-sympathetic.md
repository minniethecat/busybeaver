[012-001] As we are opposed to hedonism in general, that is to say, to the theory

[012-002] based upon the pleasure and pain intrinsic to the economic activity and

[012-003] accompanying every other form of activity, which, confounding container

[012-004] and content, fails to recognize any process but the hedonistic; so we

[012-005] are opposed to æsthetic hedonism in particular, which looks at any rate

[012-006] upon the æsthetic, if not also upon all other activities, as a simple

[012-007] fact of feeling, and confounds the pleasurable expression, which is the

[012-008] beautiful, with the simply pleasurable and all its other species.

[012-009] *Criticism of [[beauty|the beautiful]] as that which pleases the higher

[012-010] senses.*

[012-011] The æsthetic-hedonistic point of view has been presented in several

[012-012] forms. One of the most ancient conceives the beautiful as that which

[012-013] pleases sight and hearing, that is to say, the so-called *higher

[012-014] senses.* When analysis of æsthetic facts first began, it was, indeed,

[012-015] difficult to avoid the false belief that a picture and a piece of

[012-016] music are impressions of sight or hearing and correctly to interpret

[012-017] the obvious remark that the blind man does not enjoy the picture, nor

[012-018] the deaf man the music. To show, as we have shown, that the æsthetic

[012-019] fact does not depend upon the nature of the impressions, but that all

[012-020] sensible impressions can be raised to æsthetic expression and that

[012-021] none need of necessity be so raised, is an idea which presents itself

[012-022] only when all other doctrinal constructions of this problem have been

[012-023] tried. Any one who holds that the æsthetic fact is something pleasing

[012-024] to the eyes or to the hearing, has no line of defence against him who

[012-025] consistently proceeds to identify the beautiful with the pleasurable in

[012-026] general, and includes in Æsthetic cooking, or (as some positivists have

[012-027] called it) the viscerally beautiful.

[012-028] *Criticism of the theory of play.*

[012-029] The theory of *play* is another form of æsthetic hedonism. The concept

[012-030] of play has sometimes helped towards the realization of the activistic

[012-031] character of the expressive fact: man (it has been said) is not really

[012-032] man, save when he begins to play (that is to say, when he frees himself

[012-033] from natural and mechanical causality and works spiritually); and his

[012-034] first game is art. But since the word "play" also means that pleasure

[012-035] which arises from the expenditure of the exuberant energy of the

[012-036] organism (which is a practical fact), the consequence of this theory

[012-037] has been that every game has been called an æsthetic fact, or that the

[012-038] æsthetic function has been called a game, because like science and

[012-039] everything else, it may form part of a game. Morality alone cannot

[012-040] ever be caused by the will to play (for it will never consent to such

[012-041] an origin), but on the contrary itself dominates and regulates the act

[012-042] itself of playing.

[012-043] *Criticism of the theories of sexuality and of triumph.*

[012-044] Finally, some have tried to deduce the pleasure of art from the echo

[012-045] of that of the sexual organs. And some of the most recent æstheticians

[012-046] confidently find the genesis of the æsthetic fact in the pleasure of

[012-047] *conquering* and in that of *triumphing,* or, as others add, in the

[012-048] wish of the male to conquer the female. This theory is seasoned with

[012-049] much anecdotal erudition, heaven knows of what degree of credibility,

[012-050] as to the customs of savage peoples. But there was really no need for

[012-051] such assistance, since in ordinary life one often meets poets who adorn

[012-052] themselves with their poetry, like cocks raising their crests, or

[012-053] turkeys spreading out their tails. But any one who does this, in so far

[012-054] as he does it, is not a poet but a poor fool, in fact, a poor fool of

[012-055] a cock or turkey, and the desire for the victorious conquest of women

[012-056] has nothing to do with the fact of art. It would be just as correct to

[012-057] look upon poetry as *economic,* because there once were court poets

[012-058] and salaried poets, and there are poets now who find in the sale of

[012-059] their verses an aid to life if not a complete living. This deduction

[012-060] and definition has not failed to attract some zealous neophytes in

[012-061] historical materialism.

[012-062] *Criticism of the Æsthetic of the sympathetic. Meaning in it

[012-063] of content and form.*

[012-064] Another less vulgar current of thought considers Æsthetic as the

[012-065] science of the *sympathetic,* as that with which we sympathize,

[012-066] which attracts, rejoices, arouses pleasure and admiration. But the

[012-067] sympathetic is nothing but the [[representation|image]] or representation of what pleases.

[012-068] And as such it is a complex fact, resulting from a constant element,

[012-069] the æsthetic element of representation, and a variable element, the

[012-070] pleasing in its infinite forms, arising from all the various classes of

[012-071] values.

[012-072] In ordinary language, there is sometimes a feeling of repugnance at

[012-073] calling an expression "beautiful," unless it is an expression of the

[012-074] sympathetic. Hence the continual conflicts between the point of view

[012-075] of the æsthetician or art critic and that of the ordinary person,

[012-076] who cannot succeed in persuading himself that the image of pain and

[012-077] baseness can be beautiful or at least that it has as much right to be

[012-078] beautiful as the pleasing and the good.

[012-079] The conflict could be put an end to by distinguishing two different

[012-080] sciences, one of expression and the other of the sympathetic, if the

[012-081] latter could be the object of a special science; that is to say, if

[012-082] it were not, as has been shown, a complex and equivocal concept. If

[012-083] predominance be given to the expressive fact, it enters Æsthetic as

[012-084] science of expression; if to the pleasurable content, we fall back

[012-085] to the study of facts essentially hedonistic (utilitarian), however

[012-086] complicated they may appear. The particular origin of the doctrine

[012-087] which conceives the relation between form and content as the sum of two

[012-088] values is also to be sought in the doctrine of the sympathetic.

[012-089] *Æsthetic hedonism and moralism.*

[012-090] In all the doctrines just now discussed, art is considered as a merely

[012-091] hedonistic thing. But æsthetic hedonism cannot be maintained, save by

[012-092] uniting it with a general philosophical hedonism, which does not admit

[012-093] any other form of value. Hardly has this hedonistic conception of art

[012-094] been received by philosophers who admit one or more spiritual values,

[012-095] truth or morality, when the following question must necessarily be

[012-096] asked: What must be done with art? To what use should it be put? Should

[012-097] a free course be allowed to the pleasures it procures? And if so, to

[012-098] what extent? The question of the *end of art,* which in the Æsthetic of

[012-099] expression is inconceivable, has a clear significance in the Æsthetic

[012-100] of the Sympathetic and demands a solution.

[012-101] *The rigoristic negation, and the pedagogic justification of

[012-102] art.*

[012-103] Now it is evident that such solution can have but two forms, one

[012-104] altogether negative, the other of a restrictive nature. The first,

[012-105] which we shall call *rigoristic* or *ascetic,* appears several times,

[012-106] although not frequently, in the history of ideas. It looks upon art

[012-107] as an inebriation of the senses and therefore as not only useless but

[012-108] harmful. According to this theory, then, we must exert all our strength

[012-109] to liberate the human soul from its disturbing influence. The other

[012-110] solution, which we shall call *pedagogic* or *moralistic-utilitarian,*

[012-111] admits art, but only in so far as it co-operates with the end of

[012-112] morality; in so far as it assists with innocent pleasure the work

[012-113] of him who points the way to the true and the good; in so far as it

[012-114] anoints the edge of the cup of wisdom and morality with sweet honey.

[012-115] It is well to observe that it would be an error to divide this second

[012-116] view into intellectualistic and moralistic-utilitarian, according as to

[012-117] whether be assigned to art the end of leading to the true or to what

[012-118] is practically good. The educational task which is imposed upon it,

[012-119] precisely because it is an end which is sought after and advised, is no

[012-120] longer merely a theoretical fact, but a theoretical fact already become

[012-121] the ground for practical action; it is not, therefore, intellectualism,

[012-122] but pedagogism and practicism. Nor would it be more exact to subdivide

[012-123] the pedagogic view into pure utilitarian and moralistic-utilitarian;

[012-124] because those who admit only the satisfaction of the individual

[012-125] (the desire of the individual), precisely because they are absolute

[012-126] hedonists, have no motive for seeking an ulterior justification for

[012-127] art.

[012-128] But to enunciate these theories at the point to which we have attained

[012-129] is to confute them. We prefer to restrict ourselves to observing that

[012-130] in the pedagogic theory of art is to be found another of the reasons

[012-131] why the claim has erroneously been made that the content of art should

[012-132] be *chosen* with a view to certain practical effects.

[012-133] *Criticism of pure beauty.*

[012-134] The thesis that art consists of *pure beauty* has often been brought

[012-135] forward against hedonistic and pedagogic Æsthetic, and eagerly taken

[012-136] up by artists: "Heaven places all our joy in *pure beauty,* and the

[012-137] Verse is everything." If by this be understood that art is not to be

[012-138] confounded with sensual pleasure (utilitarian practicism), nor with

[012-139] the exercise of morality, then our Æsthetic also must be permitted to

[012-140] adorn itself with the title of *Æsthetic of pure beauty.* But if (as is

[012-141] often the case) something mystical and transcendent be meant by this,

[012-142] something unknown to our poor human world, or something spiritual and

[012-143] beatific, but not expressive, we must reply that while applauding the

[012-144] conception of a beauty *free from all that is not the spiritual form of

[012-145] expression,* we are unable to conceive a beauty superior to this and

[012-146] still less that it should be *purified of expression,* or severed from

[012-147] itself.
