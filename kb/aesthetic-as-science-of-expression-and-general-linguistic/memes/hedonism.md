# Hedonism

Aesthetic hedonism (*edonismo estetico*) is the theory that art's essence is the production of pleasure. Benedetto Croce subjects this view to sustained critique, arguing that it confuses the theoretical activity of intuition with the practical activity of seeking satisfaction.

## Definition

Aesthetic hedonism holds that beauty is what pleases and ugliness is what displeases. In Croce's assessment, this is the most common and persistent error in the philosophy of art — appealing to common sense but fundamentally confused. Pleasure and pain belong to the practical sphere (economic activity); beauty and ugliness belong to the theoretical sphere (intuitive activity). To say a painting is beautiful is not to say it causes pleasure but to say its expression succeeds (P02-096).

## In Croce's System

### The Category Error

Croce identifies hedonism as a category error: "Æsthetic hedonism and moralism" (P02-096) conflates the aesthetic with the practical. The pleasure we feel from a beautiful work is a consequence of successful intuition, not its essence. We enjoy the work because we have intuited it well — not the reverse.

### Hedonism and Sensationalism

Aesthetic hedonism is closely allied with sensationalism — the view that art consists in agreeable sensations. "Eclecticism and sensationalism" (P02-217) traces how 18th-century British aesthetics (Burke, Home) reduced the sublime and beautiful to physiological responses. Croce's "Criticism of aesthetic hedonism" (ch11-criticism-aesthetic-hedonism) systematically dismantles these theories.

## Key Distinctions

**Pleasure vs Beauty.** Pleasure is a psychological event; beauty is an aesthetic quality. To collapse the distinction is to eliminate the autonomy of art: if art's purpose is pleasure, then art is simply one source of pleasure among many, and there is nothing specifically artistic about it.

**Hedonism vs Eudaemonism.** Some theories treat art as contributing to happiness or well-being rather than mere pleasure. Croce's critique applies equally: art is a form of knowledge, not a means to any practical end, however elevated.

## Historical Context

Hedonistic theories of art appear throughout history. **Aristotle**'s *Poetics* includes *katharsis* — the purgation of emotions through pleasure. **Edmund Burke**'s *Philosophical Enquiry* grounded the sublime and beautiful in pleasure and pain. **H. Home** (Lord Kames) and the Scottish Enlightenment developed a sensationalist aesthetics (P02-217). Croce's critique of this tradition is fundamental to his defense of aesthetic autonomy.
