# Sensationalism

In Croce's history of æsthetic theory, sensationalism names the empiricist tendency to reduce artistic experience to the production of pleasurable sensations.

It is a confusion that Croce diagnoses as the failure to distinguish æsthetic intuition from mere sense-impression.

The critique of sensationalism runs throughout the historical portions of the *Æsthetic* and is one of the most persistent polemical threads in the work.

## Definition

Croce identifies sensationalism as the view that beauty consists in the capacity of an object to produce agreeable sensations in the perceiver — "an old-fashioned expression of sensationalism" [006-041].

> "Imagination and sensationalism: the corrective of imagination." [022-335]

This reductive empiricism treats art as a stimulus producing a response, thereby collapsing the æsthetic (which is theoretic and cognitive) into the economic (which concerns pleasure and utility).

For Croce, "the poet should aim only at" expression, not at calculated sensory effect [022-358]; to confuse the two is to mistake the vehicle for the content.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Sensationalism, in Croce's analysis, is fundamentally a category error.

It assigns æsthetic experience to the domain of the agreeable — which belongs to economic volition — rather than to æsthetic intuition.

The pleasure taken in art is real but derivative; it follows from successful expression, not from the sensory properties of the medium.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp line between **imagination** and **sensationalism**.

Imagination is the synthetic-productive faculty that creates the æsthetic image; sensationalism is the passive-receptive registering of sensory stimuli.

The eighteenth-century sensationalists "upheld sensation as the sole criterion of beauty" [022-410], a doctrine Croce regards as philosophically primitive because it cannot account for the unity, individuality, and cognitive character of the work of art.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The Abbé Du Bos, in his *Réflexions critiques sur la poésie et sur la peinture* (1719), gave sensationalism its classic eighteenth-century formulation: art is judged by the pleasure it gives.

Condillac's *Traité des sensations* (1754) provided the philosophical underpinning, deriving all mental operations from transformed sensations.

In Germany, E. Platner [026-128] and others "passed from physiological sensationalism to moralism," [026-131] attempting to bridge the gap between bodily sensation and æsthetic judgment.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce traces the lineage of sensationalist error through the full span of modern æsthetics.

Burke and Home in the British tradition [P02-217], the French "tinged with sensationalism" [023-078], even Kant "began by tending towards sensationalism" [027-240] before his critical turn.

Throughout the nineteenth century, Croce observes sensationalism resurgent in positivism and psychologism — Condillac's doctrine "had a great vogue in Italy" [033-087] and Kirchmann represented a late "low plains of positivism and sensationalism" [037-460].

Croce's contribution is not a new refutation but a systematic diagnosis: sensationalism misidentifies which activity of Spirit is in play.

## Criticism and Debate

One can question whether Croce's dismissal of sensationalism is overly sweeping.

His idealism leads him to treat sensory pleasure as categorically non-æsthetic, yet the materiality of art — color, texture, sound — undeniably contributes to the experience of works.

Later æstheticians (Dewey, Merleau-Ponty) have argued that sensation and meaning are interwoven in embodied experience rather than cleanly separable into discrete spiritual grades.

Croce's refutation of sensationalism is philosophically elegant but may underestimate the role of the sensorium in æsthetic experience.
