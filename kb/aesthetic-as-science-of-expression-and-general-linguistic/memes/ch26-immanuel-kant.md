[026-001] *Other writers of the eighteenth century: Batteux.*

[026-002] A great medley of heterogeneous ideas is noticeable among other writers

[026-003] on Æsthetic during the same period. In 1746 appeared a little volume

[026-004] by Abbé Batteux bearing the attractive title of *The Fine Arts reduced

[026-005] to a Single Principle,* in which the author attempted a unification

[026-006] of all the different rules laid down by the writers of treatises. All

[026-007] such rules (says Batteux) are branches emerging from one trunk; he who

[026-008] possesses the simple principle will be able to deduce the rules one by

[026-009] one without entangling himself in their mass, which can but involve him

[026-010] in endless coils. The author had passed in review the *Ars Poetica* of

[026-011] Horace and that of Boileau, and the works of Rollin, Dacier, le Bossu

[026-012] and d'Aubignac; but had found real help only in Aristotle's principle

[026-013] of imitation, which he thought could be easily and strikingly applied

[026-014] to poetry, painting, music and the art of gesture. But suddenly the

[026-015] Aristotelian principle of imitation yields place to a wholly new

[026-016] rendering, namely the "imitation of natural *beauty.*" The business

[026-017] of art is to "select the most beautiful parts of nature in order to

[026-018] frame them into an exquisite whole which shall be more beautiful than

[026-019] nature's self, without ceasing to be natural." Now, what may this

[026-020] greater perfection, this beautiful nature, be? On one occasion Batteux

[026-021] identifies it with truth: but "with the truth which may be; with

[026-022] beauty-truth, which is represented as though it really existed with all

[026-023] the perfections it could possibly receive," recalling one example from

[026-024] the ancients in the Helen of Zeuxis, and one from the moderns in the

[026-025] *Misanthrope* of Molière. In another place he explains that beautiful

[026-026] nature, *"tum ipsius (obiecti) naturæ, tum nostræ convenit," i.e.* that

[026-027] it has the closest connexion with our own perfection, our advantage

[026-028] and our interest, and is, at the same time, perfect in itself. The

[026-029] aim of imitation is "to please, to move, to soften, in one word, to

[026-030] delight"; so beautiful nature must be interesting and furnished with

[026-031] unity, variety, symmetry and proportion. Embarrassed by the question

[026-032] of artistic imitation of things naturally ugly or objectionable,

[026-033] Batteux falls back on saying, as Castelvetro had said before him, that

[026-034] displeasing objects please when imitated, since imitation, being always

[026-035] imperfect, in comparison with the reality, cannot excite the horror and

[026-036] disgust aroused by the latter. From pleasure he deduces the other aim

[026-037] of utility: if the aim of poetry be to give pleasure, and "pleasure

[026-038] by moving the passions, then in order to give a perfect and enduring

[026-039] pleasure it ought to rouse such passions only as it is well to excite,

[026-040] not those inimical to goodness."[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_1_317)

[026-041] *The [[england|English]]: W. Hogarth.*

[026-042] It is difficult to string together a more insubstantial mass of

[026-043] contradictions. But Batteux is rivalled and outdone by the English

[026-044] philosophers or rather scribblers on Æsthetic or rather on things in

[026-045] general which sometimes accidentally include æsthetic facts. Happening

[026-046] to find in Lomazzo some words attributed to Michæl Angelo on the beauty

[026-047] of shapes, Hogarth the artist took into his head the idea that the

[026-048] figurative arts can be regulated by a special principle which can be

[026-049] expressed in a particular fine.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_2_318) Filled with this discovery, in 1745

[026-050] he designed a frontispiece for a volume of his engravings; it depicted

[026-051] a painter's palette scored across with an undulating line and the words

[026-052] *The Line of [[beauty|Beauty]].* Public curiosity was immediately aroused by this

[026-053] hieroglyphic, to be satisfied a little later by the publication of

[026-054] his book *The Analysis of Beauty* (1753).[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_3_319) In this he combated the

[026-055] mistake of judging pictures either by the subject or the excellence of

[026-056] the imitation instead of by their form, which is the true essential

[026-057] of art and is composed "of symmetry, variety, uniformity, simplicity,

[026-058] intricacy and quantity; all things which co-operate in the production

[026-059] of beauty, correcting and restraining each other as required."[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_4_320)

[026-060] But immediately afterwards Hogarth proclaims that there must also be

[026-061] correspondence and agreement with the thing copied; for "regularity,

[026-062] uniformity and symmetry give pleasure in so far only as they serve

[026-063] to give the illusion of faithful correspondence."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_5_321) Further on, the

[026-064] reader learns that "amongst the immense variety of undulating lines

[026-065] which may be conceived, there is but one which truly merits the name of

[026-066] the Line of Beauty, and this is a precisely serpentine line which may

[026-067] be called the Line of Grace."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_6_322) Again, we are told that intricacy of

[026-068] lines is beautiful because "the active mind likes to be engaged," and

[026-069] the eye delights in being "guided in a sort of hunt."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_7_323) A straight

[026-070] line has no beauty, and the pig, the bear, the spider and the toad are

[026-071] ugly because devoid of undulating lines.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_8_324) The ancients showed much

[026-072] judgement in the management and grouping of lines, "varying from the

[026-073] precise line of grace only on those occasions when the character or

[026-074] action demanded."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_9_325)

[026-075] *[[edmund-burke|E. Burke]].*

[026-076] With similar indecision Edmund Burke wavers between the principle

[026-077] of imitation and other heterogeneous or imaginary principles in his

[026-078] book, *An Enquiry into the Origin of our Ideas of the [[sublime|Sublime]] and the

[026-079] Beautiful* (1756). He observes, "Natural properties contained in an

[026-080] object give pleasure or displeasure to the imagination: beyond this,

[026-081] however, imagination may delight in the likeness of a copy to its

[026-082] original"; he asserts that from "these two reasons" arises the whole

[026-083] pleasure of imagination.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_10_326)

[026-084] Without dwelling further on the second, he proceeds to a lengthy

[026-085] discussion of the natural qualities which should be found in an object

[026-086] of sensible beauty: "Firstly, comparative smallness; secondly, smooth

[026-087] surface; thirdly, variety in disposition of the parts; fourthly, that

[026-088] it have no angularity, all lines fusing one in another; fifthly, a

[026-089] structure of great delicacy betraying no signs of violence; sixthly,

[026-090] vivid colouring without glare or harshness; seventhly, if it have any

[026-091] glaring colour, let it be different from the background." These are the

[026-092] properties of beauty working in harmony with nature and least liable to

[026-093] suffer from caprice and differences of taste.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_11_327)

[026-094] *H. Home.*

[026-095] These books of Hogarth and Burke are generally described as classical;

[026-096] if so, they belong to the type of classic that fails to convince. To

[026-097] a somewhat higher type belongs the *Elements of Criticism* (1761)

[026-098] of Henry Home, Lord Kaimes, who seeks "the true principles of the

[026-099] fine arts" with the object of converting criticism into "a rational

[026-100] science," and to this end chooses "the upward path of facts and

[026-101] experiments." Home confines himself to feelings derived from objects

[026-102] of sight and hearing, which, in so far as unaccompanied by desires,

[026-103] are more truly described as simple feelings (emotions, not passions).

[026-104] These occupy a middle position between mere sense-impressions and

[026-105] intellectual or moral ideas, and are therefore akin to both; and it is

[026-106] from these that the pleasures of beauty are derived. Beauty is divided

[026-107] into beauty of relation and intrinsic beauty.[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_12_328) Of the latter, Home's

[026-108] only account is that regularity, simplicity, uniformity, proportion,

[026-109] order and other pleasing qualities have been "so disposed by the Author

[026-110] of nature in order to increase our happiness here on earth which, as

[026-111] is clearly shown in numberless instances, is not foreign to his care."

[026-112] This notion is confirmed when he reflects that "our taste for such

[026-113] details is not accidental, but uniform and universal, being a very

[026-114] part of our nature"; adding that "regularity, uniformity, order and

[026-115] simplicity help to facilitate perception and make it possible for us

[026-116] to form clearer conception of objects than it would be possible to

[026-117] gain by the most earnest attention were such qualities not present."

[026-118] Proportions are often combined with a view to utility, "as we see that

[026-119] the best proportioned amongst animals are also the strongest; but there

[026-120] are also many examples in which this conjunction does not hold good";

[026-121] wherefore the wisest plan "is to rest content with the final cause just

[026-122] mentioned: that of the increase of our happiness intended by the Author

[026-123] of nature."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_13_329) In his *Essay on [[taste|Taste]]* (1758) and on *[[genius|Genius]]* (1774)

[026-124] Alexander Gérard employs by turns, according to the various forms of

[026-125] art, the principles of association, of direct pleasure, of expression,

[026-126] and even of moral sense: the same kind of explanation reappears in

[026-127] another *Essay on Taste* by Alison (1792).

[026-128] *Eclecticism and sensationalism. E. Platner.*

[026-129] It is impossible to classify works of such calibre, almost wholly

[026-130] lacking as they are in scientific method; on each page their writers

[026-131] pass from physiological sensationalism to moralism; from the imitation

[026-132] of nature to mysticism and transcendent finalism without the slightest

[026-133] sense of incongruity. It would be absurd to take them seriously; in

[026-134] comparison it is almost refreshing to come across a frank hedonist

[026-135] in the German, Ernst Plainer, who interpreted Hogarth's inquiry into

[026-136] lines after a fashion of his own and was unable to see anything in

[026-137] æsthetic facts except a reverberation of sexual pleasure. Where can we

[026-138] find a beauty, he asks, that is not derived from the female figure,

[026-139] the centre of all beauty? Undulating lines are beautiful because

[026-140] found in a woman's body; beautiful are all movements distinctively

[026-141] feminine; beautiful the tones of music melting one into another;

[026-142] beautiful the poem where one thought embraces another with tenderness

[026-143] and facility.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_14_330) Condillac's sensationalism had already shown

[026-144] itself wholly incapable of understanding æsthetic productivity; the

[026-145] associationism especially promoted by the work of Hume fared no better.

[026-146] *Fr. Hemsterhuis.*

[026-147] The Dutchman Hemsterhuis considered beauty as a phenomenon born of

[026-148] the meeting between sensibility, which gives multiplicity, and the

[026-149] internal sense, which tends to unity; hence the beautiful is "that

[026-150] which exhibits the greatest number of ideas in the shortest time." Man,

[026-151] to whom it is not permitted to attain ultimate unity, finds in beauty

[026-152] an approximate unity which gives him a pleasure somewhat analogous

[026-153] with the joy of love. This theory of Hemsterhuis, in which elements of

[026-154] mysticism and sensationalism mingle with glimpses of truth, developed

[026-155] later into the sentimentalism of Jacobi, for whom the totality of Truth

[026-156] and Goodness and even the Supersensible itself are sensibly present to

[026-157] the soul in the form of beauty.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_15_331)

[026-158] *[[neo-platonism|Neo-Platonism]] and mysticism. [[j-j-winckelmann|Winckelmann]].*

[026-159] Platonism or, more accurately, neo-Platonism was revived by the creator

[026-160] of the history of figurative art, Winckelmann (1764). Contemplation

[026-161] of the masterpieces of antique plastic art, and the impression of

[026-162] superhuman loftiness and divine indifference which they create all

[026-163] the more irresistibly because we cannot reawaken the life they once

[026-164] possessed or understand their real significance, led Winckelmann, and

[026-165] others with him, to the conception of a Beauty which, descending from

[026-166] the seventh heaven of the divine Idea, embodied itself in works of this

[026-167] description. [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]]'s follower Mendelssohn had denied the enjoyment

[026-168] of beauty to God: the neo-Platonist Winckelmann gave it back to him and

[026-169] lodged it in his bosom.

[026-170] *Beauty and lack of significance.*

[026-171] "Wise men who have meditated upon the causes of universal Beauty,

[026-172] seeking her amongst created things and trying to gain the contemplation

[026-173] of Supreme Beauty, have placed it in the perfect harmony of creatures

[026-174] with their ends and of their parts with one another. But as this is

[026-175] equivalent to perfection, which man is incapable of attaining, our

[026-176] concept of universal beauty remains indeterminate, and arises by means

[026-177] of particular cognitions which, when accurately collected and fitted

[026-178] together, give us the highest idea we can attain of human beauty,

[026-179] which we elevate in proportion as we raise it above matter. But,

[026-180] again, since the Creator deals out perfection to all his creatures

[026-181] in the proportion that befits them, and since every concept rests

[026-182] on some cause which must be sought outside the concept itself, the

[026-183] cause of Beauty which is to be found in every created thing cannot

[026-184] be sought in anything outside these created things. For this reason,

[026-185] and because our cognitions are comparative concepts, whereas Beauty

[026-186] cannot be compared with anything higher, it is difficult to attain a

[026-187] distinct and universal cognition of Beauty."[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_16_332) The only way out of

[026-188] this difficulty and others like it is the recognition that "supreme

[026-189] beauty resides in God": "the concept of human beauty becomes the more

[026-190] perfect in proportion as it can be thought more in conformity and

[026-191] agreement with supreme Being, which is distinguished from matter by

[026-192] its own unity and indivisibility. This conception of Beauty is as a

[026-193] spirit which, freed by fire from the prison of matter, strives to

[026-194] conjure up a creature in the likeness of the first reasonable creature

[026-195] formed by the divine intelligence. The forms of such an [[representation|image]] are

[026-196] simple and continuous and within this unity they are varied and for

[026-197] that very reason harmonious."[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_17_333) 2 To these characteristics is added

[026-198] "lack of significance" (*Unbezeichnung*), since supreme beauty cannot

[026-199] be described with points or fines different from those which alone

[026-200] can constitute that beauty; its form "is not peculiar to this or that

[026-201] determinate person, neither does it express any state of feeling or

[026-202] sensation of passion, things which disturb unity and overcloud beauty."

[026-203] Winckelmann concludes: "We look upon Beauty as a purest water drawn

[026-204] from the centre of the spring; the less taste it has the higher it is

[026-205] esteemed because free from all impurities."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_18_334)

[026-206] To perceive pure beauty, a special faculty is required, which certainly

[026-207] is not sense, but may perhaps be intellect or even, as Winckelmann

[026-208] says, "a fine internal sense" free from all intentions or passions

[026-209] of instinct, inclination or pleasure. Having asserted beauty to be

[026-210] something supersensible, it is not surprising that Winckelmann should

[026-211] wish, if not wholly to exclude colour, at least to reduce it to a

[026-212] minimum, and treat it not as a constitutive element in beauty but as

[026-213] secondary and ancillary.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_19_335) True beauty is given in form: by which he

[026-214] means line and surface, forgetting that these are only apprehended by

[026-215] the senses, and could not be seen without being in some way coloured.

[026-216] *Winckelmann's contradictions and compromises.*

[026-217] When error refuses to retire, hermit-like, to the narrow cell of a

[026-218] brief aphorism, it finds itself condemned to self-contradiction in

[026-219] order to live at all in the world of concrete facts and problems.

[026-220] Although composed with a view to stating a theory, the work of

[026-221] Winckelmann always led him among concrete historical facts clamouring

[026-222] to be brought into relation with his formally stated idea of supreme

[026-223] beauty. In his admission of line-drawing and his further admission, on

[026-224] a lower plane, of colour, we have two compromises already; to which

[026-225] a third is added in his principle of [[expression|Expression]]. "Since human nature

[026-226] has no state intermediate between pain and pleasure" and as living

[026-227] creature without such feelings is inconceivable, "the human figure must

[026-228] be represented in a condition of action and passion, which artists

[026-229] call expression." Hence Winckelmann, after dealing with Beauty, goes

[026-230] on to treat of Expression.[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_20_336) He then found himself obliged to effect

[026-231] a fourth compromise between the single constant supreme beauty and

[026-232] individual beauties; for while he preferred the male to the female body

[026-233] as a completer embodiment of perfect beauty, he could not shut his eyes

[026-234] to the obvious fact that we know and admire beautiful women's bodies

[026-235] and even beautiful animals' bodies.

[026-236] *A. R. Mengs.*

[026-237] Friend and, in a sense, collaborator of Winckelmann was Raphæl Mengs

[026-238] the artist, no less eager than his archæological fellow-countryman to

[026-239] understand the nature of that beauty which the one studied as a critic

[026-240] while the other produced it as a painter. Remarking, writes Mengs,

[026-241] that of the two chief duties of a painter, the imitation of appearances

[026-242] and the selection of the most beautiful objects, much has been written

[026-243] on the former, while the latter "has scarcely been touched by the

[026-244] modems, who would have been ignorant of the art of drawing were it

[026-245] not for the statues of ancient [[greece|Greece]]";[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_21_337) pondering this, "I read,

[026-246] asked and looked at everything likely to throw light on the subject,

[026-247] but never was I satisfied; either they spoke of beautiful things or

[026-248] of qualities which are the attributes of beauty, or they pretended to

[026-249] explain, as the saying is, the obscure by the more obscure, or even

[026-250] confused the beautiful with the pleasing: so that finally I determined

[026-251] to search for the nature of beauty on my own account."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_22_338) One of his

[026-252] works on this subject was published during his lifetime by the advice

[026-253] and assistance of Winckelmann (1761); many others appeared posthumously

[026-254] (1780), all were reprinted several times and translated into several

[026-255] languages. In his *Dreams of Beauty* he says, "I have been sailing

[026-256] a long time on a vast sea seeking the understanding of beauty, and

[026-257] still I am far from any shore and in great doubt how to shape my

[026-258] course: gazing around, my sight is confounded by the immensity of the

[026-259] subject."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_23_339) In truth it seems as though Mengs never arrived at a

[026-260] formula satisfactory to himself, although he conformed more or less to

[026-261] Winckelmann's doctrine that "beauty consists in material perfection

[026-262] according to our ideas; and since God alone is perfect, beauty is

[026-263] divine"; it is the "visible idea of perfection" and stands in the same

[026-264] relation to it as does a visible to a mathematical point. Our ideas

[026-265] proceed from the purposes which the Creator has willed to fulfil in

[026-266] various things; hence the multiplicity of beauties. In general, Mengs

[026-267] finds the types of things in natural species: *e.g.* "a stone, of

[026-268] which we have the idea that it should be uniform in colour"; which"

[026-269] is called ugly if it happen to be spotted"; or a child "would be

[026-270] ugly if he were like a man of mature age, just as a man is ugly when

[026-271] shaped like a woman, and a woman when she is like a man." He adds

[026-272] surprisingly, "As among stones there is but one perfect species, the

[026-273] diamond; among metals, gold; and among animated creatures, man only; so

[026-274] there is difference and distinction in every order, and very rarely is

[026-275] there perfection."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_24_340) In his *Dreams of Beauty* he considers beauty

[026-276] as "a middle disposition, including perfection on the one hand and

[026-277] the pleasing on the other"; in reality it is a third thing, differing

[026-278] from perfection and the pleasing, and deserving a special name for

[026-279] itself.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_25_341) The art of painting arises from four sources: beauty,

[026-280] significant or expressive character, the pleasing united to harmony,

[026-281] and colouring. Mengs finds the first amongst the ancients, the second

[026-282] in Raphæl, the third in Correggio and the fourth in Titian.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_26_342) From

[026-283] this empirical studio-gossip he rouses himself to exclaim, "The force

[026-284] of beauty so transports me that I will tell thee, reader, what I

[026-285] feel. All nature is beautiful, and so is virtue; beautiful are forms

[026-286] and proportions; beautiful are appearances and beautiful the causes

[026-287] thereof; more beautiful is reason, most beautiful of all is the great

[026-288] first cause."[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_27_343)

[026-289] *[[g-e-lessing|G. E. Lessing]].*

[026-290] An attenuated, that is to say, a less metaphysical, echo of

[026-291] Winckelmann's theory is found in Lessing (1766), who infused a new

[026-292] spirit into the literature and social life of the [[germany|Germany]] of his time.

[026-293] According to Lessing the aim of art is "delight"; and since delight is

[026-294] a "superfluous thing" it seems reasonable that the legislator should

[026-295] not allow to art that liberty which is indispensable to science in

[026-296] her search for truth, the soul's necessity. For the Greeks painting

[026-297] was what by its nature it ought to be, "the imitation of beautiful

[026-298] bodies." "Its (Hellenic) cultivator represented nothing but the

[026-299] beautiful: common beauty of a low grade served him as an accidental

[026-300] subject, an exercise, a diversion. The attractiveness of his work

[026-301] must depend simply and solely on the perfection of his subject: he

[026-302] was far too true an artist to wish his audience to content itself

[026-303] with the barren pleasure arising from mere resemblance or from the

[026-304] inspection of skilful workmanship: nothing in his art was dearer to

[026-305] him, nothing seemed more noble, than the end at which it aimed."[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_28_344)

[026-306] Pictorial representation must exclude everything unpleasing or ugly;

[026-307] "painting as imitation may express ugliness: painting as a [[art|fine art]]

[026-308] will refuse to do so: all visible objects belong to art taken under

[026-309] the former title: the latter may claim only such objects as awaken

[026-310] pleasing sensations." If, on the contrary, ugliness may be represented

[026-311] by the poet, the reason is this: poetic description "conveys a less

[026-312] displeasing sense of bodily malformation which, in the end, almost

[026-313] loses its character as such; unable to use it for itself, the poet

[026-314] uses it as a means to provoke certain mixed feelings (the ridiculous,

[026-315] the terrible), in which we are content to remain, in the absence of

[026-316] any purely pleasant feelings."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_29_345) In his *Dramaturgie* (1767) Lessing

[026-317] takes his stand upon the Aristotelian *Poetics*: it is well known that

[026-318] not only did he approve of rules in general but he believed those

[026-319] laid down by Aristotle to be as incontrovertible as the theorems of

[026-320] Euclid. His polemic against [[france|French]] writers and critics is waged in the

[026-321] name of probability, not to be confounded with historical accuracy.

[026-322] He understood the universal as a sort of average of what appears in

[026-323] individuals, and catharsis as a conversion of passions into virtuous

[026-324] dispositions, asserting it as beyond doubt that the aim of all

[026-325] poetry is to inspire a love for virtue.[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_30_346) He follows the example

[026-326] of Winckelmann in introducing the concept of ideal beauty into the

[026-327] doctrine of figurative art: "expression of corporeal beauty is the aim

[026-328] of painting: therefore supreme beauty of body is the supreme aim of

[026-329] art. But this supreme beauty of body is found in man only, and for

[026-330] him it exists only through the ideal. This ideal may be found among

[026-331] the brute creation in inferior degree; but is entirely absent from

[026-332] vegetable or inanimate nature." Landscape and flower painters are not

[026-333] really artists because "they imitate beauties possessed of no ideal:

[026-334] whereby they work by eye and hand alone, genius having little or no

[026-335] part in their compositions." Nevertheless, Lessing prefers a landscape

[026-336] painter to "the painter of historic pieces who, instead of making

[026-337] beauty his aim, merely depicts a crowd in order to show his cunning in

[026-338] simple expression, not in expression subordinate to beauty."[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_31_347) The

[026-339] ideal of bodily beauty then consists "chiefly in the ideal of form,

[026-340] but also in that of texture of the flesh, and in that of permanent

[026-341] expression. Mere colouring and transitory expression have no ideal

[026-342] since nature herself has placed no indelible seal upon them."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_32_348) At

[026-343] the bottom of his heart Lessing dislikes colour; and when he finds

[026-344] the pen-sketches of painters showing "a life, a freedom, a brilliancy

[026-345] never to be found in their painted pictures," he asks himself "whether

[026-346] the most marvellous colouring can compensate so heavy a loss," and

[026-347] whether it is not to be wished "that painting in oils had never been

[026-348] invented"?[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_33_349)

[026-349] *Theorists of ideal beauty.*

[026-350] Ideal beauty, that curious alliance between God and the subtle outline

[026-351] traced with pen or graver, that cold academical mysticism, came into

[026-352] fashion. In [[italy|Italy]] (the home of Winckelmann and Mengs, who published

[026-353] many of their works in Italian) it was much discussed by artists,

[026-354] antiquaries and connoisseurs. The architect Francesco Milizia professed

[026-355] himself a follower of "the principles of Sulzer and Mengs";[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_34_350)

[026-356] the Spaniard d'Azara, living in Italy, edited and annotated Mengs,

[026-357] adding his own definition of beauty: "The union of the perfect and

[026-358] the pleasing made visible";[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_35_351) another Spaniard, Arteaga, one of

[026-359] the many Jesuit refugees in Italy, wrote a treatise on *Ideal Beauty*

[026-360] (1789);[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_36_352) the Englishman Daniel Webb on coming to Rome and making

[026-361] the acquaintance of Mengs seized upon the ideas he heard him express

[026-362] on beauty, collected them and actually published them in a book

[026-363] anticipating Mengs' own.[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_37_353)

[026-364] *G. Spalletti and the characteristic.*

[026-365] The first voice of dissent from this doctrine of ideal beauty was

[026-366] raised in 1764 by a small circle of Italians who asserted the

[026-367] characteristic to be the principle of art. As such appears to

[026-368] be the necessary interpretation of the little *Essay on Beauty*

[026-369] written by Guiseppe Spalletti in the form of a letter to Mengs,

[026-370] with whom Spalletti had discussed the subject "in the solitudes of

[026-371] Grottaferrata," and who had urged him to put all his thoughts in

[026-372] writing.[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_38_354) Its polemical character, though not openly asserted, is

[026-373] discernible in every page. "Truth in general, conscientiously rendered

[026-374] by the artist, is the object of Beauty in general. When the soul finds

[026-375] those characteristics which wholly converge upon the matter which the

[026-376] work of art claims to represent, it judges that work beautiful. The

[026-377] same is true of the works of nature: if the soul perceives a man of

[026-378] fine proportions having the face of a lovely woman, which causes it to

[026-379] doubt whether the object before it be man or woman, it esteems that man

[026-380] ugly rather than the reverse, through deficiency of the characteristic

[026-381] of truth; if this can be said of natural Beauty, how much more can

[026-382] it be said of the Beauty of art." The pleasure given by Beauty is

[026-383] intellectual, that is to say, it is the pleasure of apprehending

[026-384] truth: when confronted by ugly things represented characteristically,

[026-385] man "delights in having increased his cognitions": Beauty, "with its

[026-386] property of supplying to the soul likeness, order, proportion, harmony

[026-387] and variety, provides it with an immense field for the construction

[026-388] of innumerable syllogisms, and by reasoning in this manner it will

[026-389] take pleasure in itself, in the object which arouses such pleasure, and

[026-390] in the feeling of its own perfection." Finally, the beautiful may be

[026-391] defined as "the inherent modification of the object under observation

[026-392] which presents it in the inevitably characteristic manner in which it

[026-393] is bound to appear."[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_39_355) In contrast to the fallacious profundity of

[026-394] Winckelmann and Mengs we welcome the sound good sense of this obscure

[026-395] Spalletti, upholder of the Aristotelian position against the revived

[026-396] neo-Platonism of the æstheticians.

[026-397] *Beauty and the characteristic: Hirt, Meyer, Goethe.*

[026-398] Many years went by before a similar rebellion arose in Germany; at

[026-399] length in 1797 the art-historian Ludwig Hirt, basing his case on

[026-400] ancient works of art which depicted all things, even things utterly

[026-401] vulgar and ugly, ventured to deny the view that ideal beauty is the

[026-402] principle of art, and that expression has only a secondary place, above

[026-403] which it must not rise for fear of disturbing ideal beauty. For the

[026-404] ideal he substituted the characteristic, as a principle to be applied

[026-405] equally to gods, heroes or animals. Character is "that individuality by

[026-406] which form, movement, signs, physiognomy and expression, local colour,

[026-407] fight, shade and chiaroscuro are distinguished and represented in the

[026-408] manner demanded by the object."[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_40_356) Another historian of art, Heinrich

[026-409] Meyer, who started from the position of Winckelmann and went on by

[026-410] adopting a series of compromises, finally asserting an ideal of trees

[026-411] and landscape side by side with the ideal of man and various other

[026-412] animals, tried to find an intermediate position between this doctrine

[026-413] and Hirt's, in the course of controversy with the latter. And Wolfgang

[026-414] von Goethe, forgetful of his youthful days when he chanted the praises

[026-415] of Gothic architecture, returning home from an Italian tour impregnated

[026-416] with Greece and Rome in 1798, also sought a middle term between Beauty

[026-417] and Expression; dwelling on the thought of certain characteristic

[026-418] contents which should supply the artist with forms of beauty to be by

[026-419] him remodelled and developed into complete beauty. The characteristic

[026-420] was thus the mere point of departure, and beauty was simply the result

[026-421] of the artist's elaboration: "we must start from the characteristic"

[026-422] (says he) "in order to attain the beautiful."[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-15.htm.html#Footnote_41_357)
