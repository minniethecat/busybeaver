# Ornament

In Croce's aesthetic system, ornament is the concept of a decorative addition that is falsely supposed to beautify expression from without; Croce argues that what is genuinely aesthetic in any work of art is never ornamental but constitutive, and that the category of ornament arises from the confusion of expression with extraneous embellishment (010-083, 038-217).

## Definition

The concept of ornament, Croce observes, "must have occurred spontaneously to the human mind" as soon as reflection upon art began (038-205). Ornament is classically understood as an addition — "they called ornament, κόσμος: *ornatum*" — something appended to the bare structure of a work to enhance its beauty (038-217). Croce traces this notion from its origins in Greek and Roman rhetoric, where ornament was one of the virtues of style, through its medieval systematization and its Renaissance elaboration into detailed taxonomies of "Classes of ornament" (P02-334, 038-230). But Croce rejects ornament as an aesthetic category: what is truly beautiful in art is never an addition but an integral element of expression.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

In the economy of spirit, ornament belongs to the sphere of practical activity — specifically, to the techniques of externalization. "It may be asked how an ornament can be joined to expression," Croce writes; the answer is that it cannot, because expression is an indivisible synthesis. What appears to be ornament is either (a) a genuine but unrecognized element of the expression itself, or (b) a practical addition that serves extra-aesthetic purposes such as display, ritual, or social distinction. Croce's treatment of ornament thus parallels his critique of technique: both concepts mistake the external instruments of art's physical realization for constituents of its aesthetic substance (010-079).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **ornament vs. expression** as the central conceptual opposition: "ornament" cannot be "joined to expression" because expression is a complete, indivisible synthesis — nothing can be added to it from without without destroying its integrity (010-083). Second, **ornament vs. form**: the formalist tradition that treats beauty as residing in the arrangement of ornamental elements inverts the true relation; form is the intuitive synthesis itself, not a pattern imposed upon content. Third, Croce distinguishes the **historical concept of ornament** — a genuine category in the history of rhetoric and art theory — from its **critical invalidity**: the concept has descriptive value for understanding how past theorists thought about art, but no normative value for understanding what art is (038-220).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The theory of ornament has a long and elaborate history. In classical rhetoric, ornament (*ornatus*) was one of the four virtues of style, and treatises catalogued the figures and tropes that "diversify bare or nude" speech (038-233). Cicero and Quintilian provided detailed classifications. In "the theory of ornament in the Middle Ages," these classical models were adapted to Christian rhetoric and art (P02-334). The Renaissance witnessed an "academic" debate "concerning the theory of ornament," with figures like Du Marsais contributing to its systematization (P02-336). In the visual arts, "the old mechanical, ornamental, pedagogic" approach to drawing treated ornament as a repertoire of fixed patterns to be copied (025-332). Vico identified metaphor as "the queen of ornaments" — the supreme figure that transforms language (038-222).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to dissolve the category of ornament entirely. Where the entire rhetorical tradition had treated ornament as a legitimate though secondary aspect of style, Croce argues that it is "a useless ornament, not the free instrument of writing" — a superfluity that the genuine artist discards (014-277). He cites with approval the observation that "gold only embellishes when it embellishes without appearing to do so," transforming this into the stronger claim that authentic aesthetic value never resides in the "ornament" as such but always in the total expression (020-282). By relocating ornamental elements to the practical sphere of externalization, Croce eliminates the conceptual space for a distinct aesthetic theory of ornament while preserving the historical study of ornamental traditions as a branch of cultural history.

## Criticism and Debate

> The danger of the ornament-concept is that it reduces the work of art "to an idle ornament, a covering (Hülle) presented" to a content that is presumed already complete — a dualism that Croce's expression-identity thesis decisively rejects (028-550).

Critics have argued that Croce's rejection of ornament is too sweeping: ornamental traditions in Islamic art, Gothic manuscript illumination, and Rococo decoration seem to constitute genuine aesthetic achievements that resist reduction to mere practical externalization. If ornament can be the primary vehicle of aesthetic value rather than an addition to it, Croce's identity of intuition and expression may need qualification. Defenders respond that Croce does not deny the beauty of ornamental traditions; he denies only that their beauty is *ornamental* in the dualistic sense — what is beautiful in them is precisely their expressive unity.
