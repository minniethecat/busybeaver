# Herbartian School

The Herbartian School was a philosophical movement that developed from the thought of **Johann Friedrich Herbart** (1776–1841), who held the chair of philosophy at Königsberg (Kant's former position) and later at Göttingen. In aesthetics, the Herbartians represented a distinctive current that rejected both Hegelian speculative idealism and the rising tide of positivism, emphasizing instead the formal, relational character of aesthetic value.

## Overview

Herbart occupies an unusual position in the history of aesthetics. A "dry intellectualist, the avowed enemy of idealism, of dialectic and of speculative constructions" (028-... ), Herbart nevertheless became, when addressing aesthetic questions, what Croce describes as a mystic — albeit a mystic of form rather than of content. His central aesthetic doctrine is that beauty consists in **relations of form**, not in the expression of feeling or the sensuous appearance of the Idea:

> Herbart conceived beauty as consisting in relations; and these relations, in so far as they please, are judged by taste with an immediate and involuntary judgment, which is not logical but aesthetic. (028-... )

This formalist approach made aesthetics for the Herbartians a science of **pure beauty** — beauty considered independently of any content, emotion, or metaphysical meaning. A musical chord, a geometric proportion, a color harmony — these elementary aesthetic facts, in which the relational character of beauty is most clearly visible — became the paradigmatic objects of aesthetic inquiry.

## Key Members

| Member | Role | Contribution |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **J. F. Herbart** (1776–1841) | Founder | Theory of pure beauty as formal relations; anti-idealist method |
| **Robert Zimmermann** (1824–1898) | Leading systematizer | *Allgemeine Ästhetik als Formwissenschaft* (1865) — aesthetics as formal science |
| **Johann Friedrich Droysen** (1808–1884) | Historian | Applied Herbartian principles to historical methodology |
| **Wilhelm Drobisch** (1802–1896) | Logician | Extended Herbartian logic to aesthetic judgment |
| **O. Hostinský** (1847–1910) | Czech aesthetician | Formalist theory of music |

**Robert Zimmermann** was the most important aesthetic theorist of the later Herbartian school. His *General Aesthetic as Formal Science* (1865) attempted to construct a complete aesthetic system on purely formal principles, categorizing all aesthetic phenomena in terms of fundamental relations: harmony, proportion, rhythm, and balance. Zimmermann engaged in a famous polemic with the Hegelian **F. T. Vischer**, with Vischer representing the aesthetics of content (*Gehaltsästhetik*) and Zimmermann the aesthetics of form (*Formästhetik*).

The Herbartians also developed a distinctive theory of artistic judgment. Following Herbart's psychological theory of "apperceptive masses" — the idea that new perceptions are assimilated to existing mental structures — they argued that aesthetic judgment is immediate and involuntary. When a formal relation "pleases," it does so because it facilitates the harmonious functioning of our apperceptive apparatus, not because it satisfies a desire or communicates a truth.

## Contributions to Æsthetic

The Herbartian school's principal contributions to aesthetic theory were:

1. **Formalism.** The insistence that aesthetic value resides in form, not content, anticipated important developments in twentieth-century art theory and criticism. The Herbartians were among the first to argue systematically that "what" a work represents is aesthetically irrelevant — only "how" it is formed matters.

2. **Anti-speculative method.** The Herbartian commitment to "exact philosophy" — philosophy that proceeds from clearly defined concepts rather than speculative flights — represented an important methodological alternative to Hegelianism, even if Croce judges its execution to have fallen short.

3. **Bridge to psychological aesthetics.** Although the Herbartians rejected the reduction of aesthetics to physiology (as attempted by the positivists), their psychological theory of aesthetic judgment as immediate and involuntary opened a path that later psychological aesthetics would follow.

Croce's evaluation is characteristically dialectical: he praises Herbart's opposition to Hegelian excess while criticizing the Herbartian reduction of expression to formal relations. In Croce's system, the aesthetic fact is expression — which always has both form and content in indivisible unity. The Herbartian attempt to isolate pure form was, in Croce's terms, another instance of the confusion between the physical fact (the externalized object with its measurable relations) and the aesthetic fact (the spiritual act of expression-intuition).
