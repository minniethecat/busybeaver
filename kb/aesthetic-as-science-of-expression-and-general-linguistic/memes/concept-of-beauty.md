# Concept of Beauty

The "concept of beauty" (*Begriff des Schönen*) is, in Croce's Æsthetic, a term used critically to designate the rationalist error of subsuming beauty under a general definition. Rather than recognising beauty as the quality of individual expression, the Wolffian school and its heirs treated beauty as though it were a concept to be analysed — an approach Croce regards as fundamentally mistaken (027-036).

## Definition

For the rationalist aestheticians, particularly "the school of Wolff," the concept of beauty meant a definable universal — a set of necessary and sufficient conditions under which an object could be called beautiful (027-036). Croce rejects this approach entirely. Beauty is not a concept but a quality of successful expression: it cannot be defined in advance because each expressive act is individual and unrepeatable. The search for "the concept of beauty" is therefore a search for what does not exist.

## In Croce's System

### Beauty as Expression, Not Concept

In Croce's system, the æsthetic is the domain of intuition — the knowledge of particulars. The concept belongs to the logical sphere — the knowledge of universals. To speak of a "concept of beauty" is to confuse these two distinct theoretical activities. Beauty is the successful expression of an intuition; it is always *this* beauty, of *this* work, not an instance of a general kind (030-350).

### The Wolffian Error

The rationalist tradition stemming from Christian Wolff treated aesthetics as a science of sensible cognition — a *gnoseologia inferior*. Within this framework, beauty was the perfection of sensible representation, and the concept of beauty was a legitimate object of philosophical analysis. Croce sees this as a fundamental mislocation: the Wolffians took a logical approach to what is essentially an æsthetic phenomenon.

### Véron's Rejection

Croce notes with sympathy that Eugène Véron expressed "his hatred for the concept of beauty" (037-209), seeing it as an academic imposition on the living reality of art. Véron's rejection, while less systematic than Croce's, points in the same direction: beauty is not a definition but a quality of individual works.

## Historical Context

### Rationalist Aesthetics

The concept of beauty was central to eighteenth-century German aesthetics. Baumgarten's *Aesthetica* (1750) defined beauty as the perfection of sensible cognition, and the subsequent tradition — Meier, Mendelssohn, the early Kant — worked within this conceptualist framework. "The concept of beauty as a definable universal" (027-036) was the shared premise of the entire rationalist school.

### From Concept to Judgement

Kant's *Critique of Judgement* marked a turning point: beauty became a matter of reflective judgement, not of determinate concepts. But even Kant retained the language of the "concept of beauty" in his analytic of the beautiful. Croce goes further: he "utterly denies the concept of beauty" (032-196) altogether, dissolving the question into the theory of expression.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is not merely to reject one definition of beauty but to reject the very project of defining beauty conceptually. "Substituting that of artistic expression for the concept of beauty" (030-350) means replacing the question "What is beauty?" with the question "How does expression succeed?" The former is a logical question misapplied; the latter is a genuinely æsthetic one.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's elimination of the concept of beauty has been challenged as too sweeping. Even if beauty is always individual, the capacity to recognise and compare beauties seems to involve general terms that function much like concepts — "grace," "sublimity," "elegance" — without which aesthetic discourse would be mute (032-050).
>
> The remark that "the concept of beauty is merely a convenient abbreviation" (036-242) may concede more than Croce intends: if the concept of beauty is convenient, it is not merely an error but a functional element of aesthetic practice.
