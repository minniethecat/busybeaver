[010-001] RHETORIC

[010-002] *The characters of art.*

[010-003] It is customary to give long catalogues of the *characters* of art.

[010-004] Having reached this point of the treatise, after having studied

[010-005] art as spiritual activity, as theoretic activity, and as special

[010-006] theoretic activity (intuitive), we are able to discover that those

[010-007] varied and numerous determinations of characters, where they refer

[010-008] to anything real, do nothing but represent what we have already met

[010-009] with as genera, species and individuality of the æsthetic form. To the

[010-010] generic are reducible, as we have already observed, the characters, or

[010-011] rather, the verbal variants of *unity,* and of *unity* in *variety,*

[010-012] of *simplicity,* or *originality,* and so on; to the specific,

[010-013] the characters of *truth,* of *sincerity,* and the like; to the

[010-014] individual, the characters of *life,* of *vivacity,* of *animation,* of

[010-015] *concreteness,* of *individuality,* of *characteristicality*. The words

[010-016] may change again, but they will not contribute anything scientifically

[010-017] new. The analysis of expression as such is completely effected in the

[010-018] results expounded above.

[010-019] *Non-existence of modes of expression.*

[010-020] It might, on the other hand, be asked at this point if there be *modes*

[010-021] or *degrees* of expression; if, having distinguished two degrees of

[010-022] activity of the spirit, each of which is subdivided into two other

[010-023] degrees, one of these, the intuitive-expressive, is not in its turn

[010-024] subdivided into two or more intuitive modes, into a first, second or

[010-025] third degree of expression. But this further division is impossible;

[010-026] a classification of intuition-expressions is certainly permissible,

[010-027] but is not philosophical: individual expressive facts are so many

[010-028] individuals, not one of which is interchangeable with another, save

[010-029] in its common quality of expression. To employ the language of the

[010-030] schools: expression is a species which cannot function in its turn

[010-031] as a genus. Impressions or contents vary; every content differs from

[010-032] every other content, because nothing repeats itself in life; and

[010-033] the irreducible variety of the forms of expression corresponds to

[010-034] the continual variation of the contents, the æsthetic synthesis of

[010-035] impressions.

[010-036] *Impossibility of translations.*

[010-037] A corollary of this is the impossibility of *translations,* in so

[010-038] far as they pretend to effect the re-moulding of one expression into

[010-039] another, like a liquid poured from a vase of a certain shape into a

[010-040] vase of another shape. We can elaborate logically what we have already

[010-041] elaborated in æsthetic form only; but we cannot reduce what has already

[010-042] possessed its æsthetic form to another form also æsthetic. Indeed,

[010-043] every translation either diminishes and spoils, or it creates a new

[010-044] expression, by putting the former back into the crucible and mingling

[010-045] it with the personal impressions of the so-called translator. In the

[010-046] former case, the expression always remains one, that of the original,

[010-047] the translation being more or less deficient, that is to say, not

[010-048] properly expression: in the other case, there would certainly be two

[010-049] expressions, but with two different contents. "Faithful ugliness or

[010-050] faithless beauty" is a proverb that well expresses the dilemma with

[010-051] which every translator is faced. Un-æsthetic translations, such as

[010-052] those that are word for word, or paraphrastic, are to be looked upon as

[010-053] simple commentaries upon the original.

[010-054] *Criticism of the rhetorical categories.*

[010-055] The illegitimate division of expressions into various grades is known

[010-056] in literature by the name of doctrine of *ornament* or of *rhetorical

[010-057] categories.* But similar attempts at distinctions in other artistic

[010-058] groups are not wanting: suffice it to recall the *realistic* and

[010-059] *symbolic* forms, so often mentioned in relation to painting and

[010-060] sculpture.

[010-061] *Realistic* and *symbolic, objective* and *subjective, classical*

[010-062] and *romantic, simple* and *ornate, proper* and *metaphorical,* the

[010-063] fourteen forms of metaphor, the figures of *word* and *sentence,

[010-064] pleonasm, ellipse, inversion, repetition, synonyms* and *homonyms,*

[010-065] these and all other determinations of modes or degrees of expression

[010-066] reveal their philosophical nullity when the attempt is made to develop

[010-067] them in precise definitions, because they either grasp the void or

[010-068] fall into the absurd. A typical example of this is the very common

[010-069] definition of metaphor as of *another word used in place of the proper

[010-070] word.* Now why give oneself this trouble? Why substitute the improper

[010-071] for the proper word? Why take the worse and longer road when you know

[010-072] the shorter and better road? Perhaps, as is commonly said, because the

[010-073] proper word is in certain cases not so *expressive* as the so-called

[010-074] improper word or metaphor? But if this be so the metaphor is exactly

[010-075] the proper word in that case, and the so-called "proper" word, if

[010-076] it were used, would be *inexpressive* and therefore most improper.

[010-077] Similar observations of elementary good sense can be made regarding the

[010-078] other categories, as, for example, the general one of the *ornate.*

[010-079] Here for instance it may be asked how an ornament can be joined to

[010-080] expression. Externally? In that case it is always separated from the

[010-081] expression. Internally? In that case, either it does not assist the

[010-082] expression and mars it; or it does form part of it and is not an

[010-083] ornament, but a constituent element of the expression, indivisible and

[010-084] indistinguishable in its unity.

[010-085] It is needless to say how much harm has been done by rhetorical

[010-086] distinctions. [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] has often been declaimed against, but although

[010-087] there has been rebellion against its consequences, its principles

[010-088] have, at the same time, been carefully preserved (perhaps in order to

[010-089] show proof of philosophic consistency). In literature the rhetorical

[010-090] categories have contributed, if not to make dominant, at least to

[010-091] justify theoretically, that particular kind of *bad writing* which is

[010-092] called *fine writing* or writing according to rhetoric.

[010-093] *Use of these categories as synonyms of the æsthetic fact.*

[010-094] The terms above mentioned would never have gone beyond the schools,

[010-095] where we all of us learned them (only we never found an opportunity

[010-096] of using them in strictly æsthetic discussions, or at most of doing

[010-097] so jocosely and with a comic intention), were it not that they can

[010-098] sometimes be employed in one of the following significations: as

[010-099] *verbal variants* of the æsthetic concept; as indications of the

[010-100] *anti-æsthetic,* or, finally (and this is their most important use), no

[010-101] longer in the service of art and æsthetic, but of *science* and *logic.*

[010-102] *Empirical sense of the rhetorical categories.*

[010-103] *First.* Expressions considered directly or positively are

[010-104] not divisible into classes, but some are successful, others

[010-105] half-successful, others failures. There are perfect and imperfect,

[010-106] successful and unsuccessful expressions. The words recorded, and others

[010-107] of the same sort, may therefore sometimes indicate the successful

[010-108] expression, and the various forms of the failures. But they do this in

[010-109] the most inconstant and capricious manner, so much so that the same

[010-110] word serves sometimes to proclaim the perfect, sometimes to condemn the

[010-111] imperfect.

[010-112] For example, some will say of two pictures—one without inspiration, in

[010-113] which the author has copied natural objects without intelligence; the

[010-114] other inspired, but without close relation to existing objects—that

[010-115] the first is *realistic,* the second *symbolic.* Others, on the

[010-116] contrary, utter the word *realistic* before a picture strongly felt

[010-117] representing a scene of ordinary life, while they apply that of

[010-118] *symbolic* to another picture that is but a cold allegory. It is

[010-119] evident that in the first case symbolic means artistic and realistic

[010-120] inartistic, while in the second, realistic is synonymous with artistic

[010-121] and symbolic with inartistic. What wonder, then, that some hotly

[010-122] maintain the true art form is the symbolic, and that the realistic is

[010-123] inartistic; others, that the realistic is artistic and the symbolic

[010-124] inartistic? We cannot but grant that both are right, since each uses

[010-125] the same words in such a different sense.

[010-126] The great disputes about *classicism* and *romanticism* were frequently

[010-127] based upon such equivocations. Sometimes the former was understood

[010-128] as the artistically perfect, and the second as lacking balance and

[010-129] imperfect; at others "classic" meant cold and artificial, "romantic"

[010-130] pure, warm, powerful, truly expressive. Thus it was always possible

[010-131] reasonably to take the side of the classic against the romantic, or of

[010-132] the romantic against the classic.

[010-133] The same thing happens as regards the word *style.* Sometimes it is

[010-134] said that every writer must have style. Here style is synonymous with

[010-135] form of expression. At others the form of a code of laws or of a

[010-136] mathematical work is said to be without style. Here the error is again

[010-137] committed of admitting diverse modes of expression, an ornate and a

[010-138] naked form, because, if style is form, the code and the mathematical

[010-139] treatise must also be asserted, strictly speaking, to have each its

[010-140] style. At other times, one hears the critics blaming some one for

[010-141] "having too much style" or for "writing a style." Here it is clear

[010-142] that style signifies, not the form, nor a mode of it, but improper and

[010-143] pretentious expression, a form of the inartistic.

[010-144] *Their use to indicate various æsthetic imperfections.*

[010-145] *Second.* The second not altogether meaningless use of these words

[010-146] and distinctions is to be found when we hear in the examination of a

[010-147] literal composition such remarks as these: here is a pleonasm, here an

[010-148] ellipse, there a metaphor, here again a synonym or an ambiguity. The

[010-149] meaning is: Here is an error consisting of using a larger number of

[010-150] words than necessary (pleonasm); here, on the other hand, the error

[010-151] arises from too few having been used (ellipse), here from the use of

[010-152] an unsuitable word (metaphor), here of two words which seem to say

[010-153] two different things, but really say the same thing (synonym); here,

[010-154] on the contrary, of one word which seems to express the same thing,

[010-155] whereas it says two different things (ambiguity). This depreciatory

[010-156] and pathological use of the terms is, however, less common than the

[010-157] preceding.

[010-158] *Their use in a sense transcending æsthetic, in the

[010-159] service of science.*

[010-160] *Thirdly* and finally, when rhetorical terminology possesses no

[010-161] æsthetic signification similar or analogous to those passed in review,

[010-162] and yet one feels that it is not void of meaning and designates

[010-163] something that deserves to be noted, this means that it is used in

[010-164] the service of logic and of science. Granted that a concept used by

[010-165] a writer in a scientific sense is designated by a definite term, it

[010-166] is natural that other terms found in use by that writer on which he

[010-167] incidentally employs himself to signify the same thought, become *in

[010-168] respect to* the vocabulary fixed upon by him as true, metaphors,

[010-169] synecdoches, synonyms, elliptical forms and the like. We ourselves in

[010-170] the course of this treatise have several times made use of, and intend

[010-171] again to make use of such language, in order to make clear the sense of

[010-172] the words we employ, or may find employed. But this proceeding, which

[010-173] is of value in discussions pertaining to the criticism of science and

[010-174] philosophy, has none whatever in literary and artistic criticism. There

[010-175] are words and metaphors proper to science: the same concept may be

[010-176] psychologically formed in various circumstances and therefore differ in

[010-177] its intuitional expression. When the scientific terminology of a given

[010-178] writer has been established and one of these modes fixed as correct,

[010-179] then all other uses of it become improper or tropical. But in the

[010-180] æsthetic fact there are none but proper words: the same intuition can

[010-181] be expressed in one way only, precisely because it is intuition and not

[010-182] concept.

[010-183] *Rhetoric in the schools.*

[010-184] Some, while admitting the æsthetic non-existence of the rhetorical

[010-185] categories, yet make a reservation as to their utility and the service

[010-186] they are supposed to render, especially in schools of literature. We

[010-187] confess that we fail to understand how error and confusion can educate

[010-188] the mind to logical distinction, or aid the teaching of a science

[010-189] which they disturb and obscure. Perhaps what is meant is that such

[010-190] distinctions, as empirical classes, can aid memory and learning, as was

[010-191] admitted above for literary and artistic kinds. To this there is no

[010-192] objection. There is certainly another purpose for which the rhetorical

[010-193] categories should continue to appear in schools: to be criticized

[010-194] there. The errors of the past must not be forgotten and no more said,

[010-195] and truths cannot be kept alive save by making them combat errors.

[010-196] Unless an account of the rhetorical categories be given, accompanied

[010-197] by a criticism of them, there is a risk of their springing up again,

[010-198] and it may be said that they are already springing up among certain

[010-199] philologists as the latest *psychological* discoveries.

[010-200] *The resemblances of expressions.*

[010-201] It might seem that we thus wished to deny all bond of resemblance

[010-202] between different expressions and works of art. Resemblances

[010-203] exist, and by means of them, works of art can be arranged in this

[010-204] or that group. But they are likenesses such as are observed among

[010-205] individuals, and can never be rendered with abstract determinations.

[010-206] That is to say, it would be incorrect to apply identification,

[010-207] subordination, co-ordination and the other relations of concepts to

[010-208] these resemblances, which consist wholly of what is called a *family

[010-209] likeness,* derived from the historical conditions in which the various

[010-210] works have appeared and from relationship of soul among the artists.

[010-211] *The relative possibility of translations.*

[010-212] It is in these resemblances that lies the *relative* possibility of

[010-213] translations; not as reproductions of the same original expressions

[010-214] (which it would be vain to attempt), but as productions of *similar*

[010-215] expressions more or less nearly resembling the originals. The

[010-216] translation called good is an approximation which has original value as

[010-217] a [[art|work of art]] and can stand by itself.
