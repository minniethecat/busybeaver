# Artistic Production

Artistic production (*produzione artistica*) in Croce's Æsthetic denotes the practical activity by which an inner intuition-expression is translated into a physical, publicly accessible object — a painting, a manuscript, a performance. Crucially, Croce separates this external, technical process from the æsthetic act proper: expression is complete in the mind of the artist; production is its subsequent communication.

## Definition

Croce draws a sharp line between the æsthetic and the practical dimensions of art-making. Expression — the formation of an intuition into a coherent image — belongs to theoretic activity and occurs entirely within the spirit. Artistic production, by contrast, is a practical activity: it involves physical materials, technical skill, and utilitarian choices. "Artistic production is a doing" — an act of will, not of intuition (007-172).

## In Croce's System

### Production vs. Expression

The distinction between expression and production is fundamental to Croce's system. "Choice does to artistic production" what it cannot do to expression proper (007-172): the artist chooses materials, techniques, and means of dissemination, but does not choose what to express — expression is the necessary outcome of intuition. This separation allows Croce to maintain art's autonomy while acknowledging the material conditions of art-making.

### Externalization

Externalization (*estrinsecazione*) is Croce's term for the process by which inner expression becomes outer object. "Certain groups of artistic production, arbitrarily chosen" (009-148) — painting, sculpture, music — are classifications of externalization techniques, not of æsthetic forms. The æsthetic fact is unitary; the diversity of arts is a practical diversity.

### Taste and Reproduction

Artistic production involves a secondary relation to taste. "Taste, between artistic production and reproduction, would render" the work communicable (017-093). Production is the bridge between the artist's private intuition and the audience's re-creation of that intuition through the physical object. The object — the painting, the poem on the page — is not the art; it is the stimulus that enables others to reproduce the art in their own spirits.

## Historical Context

### Classical and Renaissance Views

Traditional aesthetics treated artistic production as the central mystery: how does the artist make the beautiful object? From Aristotle's *technē* through Renaissance workshop theory to the Romantic cult of genius, production was the focus. Croce inverts this: the mystery is not how the object is made but how intuition becomes expression. Production is demystified into technique.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's separation of production from expression is one of his most controversial moves. "The artistic production of the human race as developed" through history (018-263) is the history of techniques and conventions, not of art as such. Art history studies production; aesthetics studies expression. This divorce — and the apparent demotion of materiality — has been the target of sustained criticism from Marxist, phenomenological, and materialist aestheticians.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's sharp production/expression distinction has been widely challenged. The material turn in aesthetics argues that the medium is not a neutral vehicle but constitutive of meaning: a sculptor's intuition is shaped by marble, a poet's by the sonnet form. Croce's retort — that the artist intuits the work complete before touching material — seems empirically doubtful for many artistic practices.
>
> "The artistic productions of certain peoples" (019-205) being treated historically rather than aesthetically also raises the question of whether Croce's framework can account for traditions in which technique and expression are inseparable — calligraphy, ceramics, architecture.

## Historical Development

In later works, Croce softens the production/expression dichotomy somewhat. The concept of "technique" is granted a "metaphorical sense" in which it can refer to the inner organization of expression. But the core thesis remains: art is not making, and making is not art. The "artistic productions of primitive and" traditional cultures (036-294) can be studied archaeologically and anthropologically, but their æsthetic value is independent of their production history.
