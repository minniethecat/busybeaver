# Finality

Finality — or purposiveness (*Zweckmässigkeit*) — is a concept Croce inherits from Kant's *Critique of Judgment* and subjects to the same demystifying critique he applies to the entire Kantian aesthetic. For Kant, the judgement of taste is distinguished by the form of finality without the representation of an end: the beautiful object appears as if it were designed for a purpose, yet no determinate purpose can be specified. Croce regards this formulation as a brilliant symptom of Kant's inability to recognize aesthetic activity as a distinct form of spiritual life. Finality, for Croce, is one of those "phantasms" that arise when philosophy senses the existence of an autonomous aesthetic sphere but lacks the conceptual apparatus to name it.

## Definition

Croce's most concentrated discussion of finality appears in his chapter on Kant. He presents Kant's definition — "That is beautiful which has the form of finality without the representation of an end" (027-257) — as one of two formulaic attempts to circumscribe the aesthetic region, the other being universal pleasure. Croce's verdict is blunt:

> What is this mysterious sphere? What this disinterested pleasure we experience in pure colours and tones, in flowers, and even in adherent beauty when we make abstraction from the concept to which it adheres? Our answer is: there is no such sphere; it does not exist; the examples given are instances either of pleasure in general or of facts of artistic expression. (027-257)

Croce pairs finality with universal pleasure as twin expressions of Kant's theoretical impasse: "'Necessary and universal pleasure' and 'finality without the idea of an end' are the organized expression in words of this contradiction" (027-293). The contradiction, for Croce, is that Kant correctly sensed the autonomy of the aesthetic but lacked the concept of intuition-expression that would have allowed him to articulate it without recourse to teleological mysticism.

## In Croce's System

### The Pseudo-Problem of Finality

The concept of finality arises, in Croce's reconstruction, from a confusion between the aesthetic and the practical. When we say that a work of art exhibits finality — that its parts cohere as if designed — we are describing, in confused teleological language, the fact that the work succeeds as expression. The coherence of a poem or painting is not the result of a purpose external to it; it is simply the achieved unity of intuition and form.

Kant's error, Croce argues, was to treat this achieved unity as a problem requiring explanation. By starting from the assumption that aesthetic pleasure must be "disinterested" — detached from both sensory gratification and conceptual determination — Kant found himself needing to explain what kind of purposiveness could remain when all determinate purposes had been excluded. The answer, "finality without end," is logically coherent but phenomenologically empty. There is no such experience; there are only successful and unsuccessful expressions.

### Key Distinctions

The critical distinction Croce draws is between **finality as aesthetic coherence** and **finality as teleological explanation**. The former is simply the internal unity of a successful intuition-expression, which requires no special concept to describe. The latter is a metaphysical importation — the vestige, Croce suggests, of eighteenth-century neo-Platonism. Kant, "who so emphatically criticizes the sensationalists and the intellectualists, does not show the same severity towards the neo-Platonic line of thought whose revival we remarked in the eighteenth century. Winckelmann in particular exercised strong influence over his mind" (027-257).

A second distinction opposes **finality in art** to **finality in science**. Croce writes in the historical appendix that the impossibility of "finality in a science and of an end to further discussion" (038-052) is a necessary consequence of the endless variety of error. In the aesthetic sphere, by contrast, the question of finality does not arise at all — not because aesthetic inquiry is interminable, but because art is not the kind of thing that admits of teleological description. Art does not have an end; it is an end in itself as the successful expression of intuition.

Croce also notes, in a biographical context, that "there is no finality about problems: the solution of one leads to the posing of another, and so on. Man is the maker of life, and his spirit ever proceeds from a lower to a higher perfection" (P03-126). This remark, from the translator's note, captures the Crocean attitude toward all philosophical system-building: the pursuit of finality is itself a misunderstanding of the spirit's nature as perpetual self-transcendence.

## Historical Context

The concept of finality entered modern aesthetics through Kant's *Critique of Judgment* (1790), where it served as the bridge between the theoretical and practical domains of the critical philosophy. Kant's four moments of the judgement of taste — quality (disinterestedness), quantity (universality without concept), relation (finality without end), and modality (necessity without proof) — gave the concept of finality its canonical formulation. The third moment was the pivot: by describing beauty as if it exhibited purposiveness, Kant linked the aesthetic to the teleological judgement that governs our understanding of organisms, creating a systematic connection between aesthetics and biology.

Schiller developed Kant's concept of finality into a theory of aesthetic play, while Schelling and Hegel subsumed it into their metaphysical systems of absolute spirit. The neo-Kantian revival of the late nineteenth century returned to finality as a problem in its own right, but Croce regarded this return as a regression — an attempt to solve with epistemological subtlety a problem that could only be dissolved by recognizing the autonomous nature of aesthetic activity. Croce's translator, Douglas Ainslie, captures the spirit of the Crocean position in his remark that "there is no finality about problems" (P03-126): the philosopher who seeks a final word on beauty has mistaken the nature of philosophical inquiry itself.
