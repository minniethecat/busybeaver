# Psychologism

Psychologism in aesthetics names the tendency, pervasive in nineteenth-century German thought, to explain the aesthetic fact by reference to psychological processes — feelings, associations, empathic projections — rather than by reference to the sui generis character of expression itself. For Croce, psychologism is not merely a methodological error but a category mistake: it treats the aesthetic as a species of psychological fact when it is, in reality, a primary activity of the spirit, irreducible to any of the natural sciences. The history of psychologism is the history of the failure to recognize the autonomy of art.

## Definition

Croce's most direct definition of psychologism comes in the context of his critique of aesthetic relativism. He writes that relativists "believe that æsthetic expression is of the same nature as the pleasant and the unpleasant, which every one feels in his own way, and about which there is no dispute. But we know that the pleasant and the unpleasant are utilitarian, practical facts. Thus the relativists deny the specific character of the æsthetic fact, and again confound expression with impression, the theoretic with the practical." Against this, Croce insists that "the true solution lies in rejecting alike relativism or psychologism and false absolutism; and in recognizing that the criterion of taste is absolute, but absolute in a different way from that of the intellect, which expresses itself in ratiocination" (017-151).

Psychologism, in this formulation, is the twin of relativism: both deny the specific, autonomous character of aesthetic activity. Relativism does so by reducing taste to subjective preference; psychologism does so by reducing expression to a causal process amenable to empirical investigation. Both fail to grasp that the aesthetic is a form of theoretical activity — knowledge of the individual through intuition — and not a species of practical or natural fact.

## In Croce's System

### The Psychological Reduction and Its Varieties

Croce traces psychologism through two principal channels in German aesthetics. The first is the sensationalist tradition, represented by Kirchmann, who "clung to æsthetic sensationalism and psychologism" and promoted "a so-called realism" in his *Æsthetic on a Realistic Basis* (037-011). Kirchmann's theory defined the aesthetic fact as "an image (*Bild*) of a real; an animated (*seelenvolles*) image, purified and strengthened, that is, idealized" (037-011). Croce's criticism is that Kirchmann "not having grasped the nature of æsthetic objectification, takes the trouble to draw up a new psychological category of ideal or apparent feelings, arising from artistic images and being attenuations of the feelings of real life" (037-011). The psychological category is invented to explain a phenomenon — aesthetic experience — that cannot be explained in psychological terms because it is not a psychological phenomenon.

The second channel is more surprising: the evolution of the German idealists themselves into adherents of psychologism. Croce notes that "to the evolution or involution of the Herbartians into physiologists of æsthetic pleasure corresponds a similar evolution or involution of the idealists into adherents of psychologism. The first place must be given to the veteran Theodor Vischer" (037-028). Vischer, who had been one of the most ambitious systematic aestheticians in the Hegelian tradition, "in a criticism of his own work pronounced Æsthetic to be 'the union of mimics and harmonics' (*vereinte Mimik und Harmonik*), and Beauty the 'harmony of the universe,' never actually realized because realized only at infinity, so that when we think to seize it in the Beautiful, we are under an illusion: a transcendent illusion, which is the very essence of the æsthetic fact" (037-028).

The trajectory from Hegelian metaphysics to Vischer's psychologized aesthetic is, for Croce, a cautionary tale. When idealist metaphysics collapses under its own weight, it does not give way to a properly philosophical aesthetics but to a psychologism that is metaphysics in disguise — the "harmony of the universe" translated into the "transcendent illusion" of individual experience.

### Key Distinctions

The governing distinction is between **psychologism and the philosophy of spirit**. Croce's own system posits four distinct forms of spiritual activity: intuition (the aesthetic), concept (the logical), economic volition, and ethical volition. Psychologism errs by collapsing the first two into the third — by treating aesthetic activity as a causal process rather than as a mode of knowledge. "The criterion of taste is absolute, with the intuitive absoluteness of the imagination. Thus any act of expressive activity, which is so really, is to be recognized as beautiful" (017-151).

A subsidiary distinction opposes **psychologism to proper philosophical aesthetics**. The psychological approach — measuring responses, cataloguing associations, analyzing empathic projection — may yield interesting data about how humans respond to art, but it cannot answer the question of what art is. That question belongs to philosophy, and its answer must be grounded in the systematic study of the spirit's activities, not in empirical psychology. Vischer's son Robert coined the term *Einfühlung* (empathy) to describe the psychological mechanism of aesthetic experience (037-028), but for Croce, *Einfühlung* explains nothing; it merely redescribes the phenomenon in psychological language.

## Historical Context

Psychologism flourished in the nineteenth century as part of the broader naturalization of philosophy that followed the collapse of German Idealism. The "back to Kant" movement of the 1860s, the rise of experimental psychology in Fechner and Wundt, and the general prestige of the natural sciences all contributed to the belief that aesthetic questions could be answered by empirical investigation of mental processes. Fechner's *Vorschule der Aesthetik* (1876) attempted to establish an "aesthetics from below" grounded in experimental data rather than philosophical speculation.

Croce's anti-psychologism aligns him with the phenomenological tradition that would later be developed by Husserl and Ingarden, though Croce's philosophy of spirit is fundamentally different from Husserlian phenomenology. What they share is the conviction that the aesthetic cannot be reduced to the psychological — that the work of art is not a stimulus but a meaning, and that the experience of art is not a causal effect but an act of understanding.

The Italian reception of German psychologism was shaped by Croce's own polemics in *La Critica* and the *Aesthetic*. By diagnosing psychologism as a category mistake, Croce cleared the ground for an aesthetics grounded in the philosophy of spirit — an aesthetics that would take intuition-expression, not psychological process, as its foundational concept.
