# Formalism

Formalism in aesthetics is the doctrine that beauty consists in certain abstract relations of form — proportion, symmetry, unity in variety — independently of the content that is formed. Croce's engagement with formalism is both historical and systematic: he traces its development through German and Italian aesthetics, and he subjects its premises to philosophical critique.

## Definition

Croce defines formalism not as a single school but as a recurring tendency in aesthetic theory: the attempt to isolate "form" as a separate principle of beauty, distinct from the matter that is formed. In its most rigorous version — the Herbartian formalism of Robert Zimmermann — beauty is reduced to "relations of form" that can be studied mathematically, entirely abstracted from the content that bears those relations.

Croce's term for this tendency is "abstract formalism," and he finds it "visible at various points" in the history of aesthetics (030-322). It is abstract because it takes one moment of the aesthetic synthesis — the formal moment — and treats it as if it were the whole, forgetting that form without content is an empty concept.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

In Croce's classification of the forms of spiritual activity, formalism represents a confusion between the theoretic activity of intuition-expression and the logical activity of conceptual abstraction. By treating aesthetic form as an abstract relation, the formalist substitutes a concept for an intuition — a logical product for an aesthetic one. Beauty is not a concept that can be defined by formal relations; it is the character of successful expression.

Croce distinguishes Herbartian formalism from other varieties of aesthetic error. "The Herbartian formalism was little studied and less understood" in its original context, yet it proved influential as a counterweight to the excesses of Romantic aesthetics (032-122). The formalism of Herbart "placed in the heaven of aesthetic ideas" relations that properly belong to the psychology of perception (032-237).

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp distinction between two kinds of formalism:

1. **Herbartian (abstract) formalism** — the attempt to define beauty in terms of abstract formal relations, independently of content. This is the "rigid formalism" of Zimmermann, which Croce regards as a "scientific dish in which a little formalism and a great deal of empiricism are mixed" (035-167, 035-171).

2. **Conciliatory formalism** — the attempt to combine formalist principles with the insights of other schools, such as Hegelian idealism. Croce describes an "Æsthetic in which formalism prevailed" that attempted to mediate between Hegel and Herbart (035-185). This conciliatory approach, while more moderate, ultimately fails for the same reason: it treats form as separable from content.

Croce himself maintains that expression is always the synthesis of form and content — a position that is neither formalist (prioritizing form) nor contentist (prioritizing content) but dialectical.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The roots of aesthetic formalism extend to antiquity. Pythagoras's theory of musical harmony as mathematical proportion, Plato's treatment of geometric form in the *Philebus*, and Aristotle's analysis of the formal cause all contributed elements that later coalesced into formalist doctrine.

In modern philosophy, the Leibnizian-Wolffian tradition — with its emphasis on perfection as unity in variety — provided a rationalist foundation for formalist aesthetics. Baumgarten, the founder of aesthetics as a philosophical discipline, incorporated formalist elements into his theory of sensible perfection. But the most rigorous development of formalism came from Johann Friedrich Herbart (1776–1841), who sought to apply mathematical precision to aesthetic relations.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of formalism is developed in the context of his critique of Italian aesthetic thought. In his chapter on "The Æsthetic of Form and the Æsthetic of Content" (chapter 30 of Part II), Croce subjects "Italian æsthetic formalism" to the same critique he applies to its German counterpart, arguing that "a totally different" error underlies both (030-028, 030-029). The Renaissance "reaction against formalism and scholastic philosophy" had already pointed toward a more concrete approach to aesthetic experience (024-317).

Croce's innovation is to treat formalism not merely as an error to be refuted but as a dialectical moment in the development of aesthetic theory. The formalist emphasis on relations captures something real — the fact that aesthetic expression has structure — but it mistakes the part for the whole. Croce's theory of expression preserves what is true in formalism (the structured character of aesthetic intuition) while correcting what is false (the abstraction of form from content).

## Criticism and Debate

> The formalism of Herbart placed in the heaven of aesthetic ideas relations that properly belong to the psychology of perception (032-237).

The debate between formalism and its critics continued well beyond Croce's time. In the twentieth century, formalist movements in art criticism (Clive Bell, Roger Fry) and literary theory (Russian Formalism, New Criticism) revived the idea that aesthetic value resides in form rather than content. These movements, while not directly Herbartian, represent the same tendency that Croce identified: the attempt to isolate formal relations as the distinctive object of aesthetic attention.

From a Crocean perspective, these later formalisms commit the same error as their nineteenth-century predecessors: they treat as separate what is indivisible in the aesthetic fact. Expression is neither form nor content but the synthesis of both.
