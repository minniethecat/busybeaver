# Probability

In Croce's aesthetic theory, probability (or verisimilitude) is the pseudo-criterion by which art is judged according to its conformity with empirical likelihood rather than its internal intuitive coherence; Croce devotes sustained critical attention to this concept, arguing that it confuses the logical standard of factual correspondence with the properly aesthetic standard of expressive unity (005-005, 005-010).

## Definition

Probability, as an aesthetic criterion, holds that a work of art should represent events or characters that are "likely" or "true to life." Croce identifies this concept as a persistent error in aesthetic theory, traceable to Aristotle's Poetics, where the philosopher asserted that poetry deals with what *could* happen according to probability or necessity. For Croce, this imports a logical standard — probability is a form of inductive reasoning about facts — into the domain of intuition, where the only relevant question is whether the expression is coherent and vivid, not whether its content corresponds to statistical likelihood (005-012, 005-020).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Probability belongs properly to the sphere of conceptual thought and practical action, not to aesthetic intuition. When we judge that a character's action is "improbable," we are applying an empirical generalization derived from past experience — a logical operation that has no bearing on the aesthetic quality of the representation (004-223). Croce insists that "the principle of verisimilitude and of probability in fact dominates all naturalistic criticism" (004-224), revealing its roots in the confusion between art and natural science. Within the fourfold structure of spirit, probability is a tool of the economic and conceptual moments — it helps us navigate the practical world and organize empirical knowledge — but it is irrelevant to the intuitive moment that constitutes art.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a sharp line between **aesthetic coherence** and **empirical probability**. The former concerns the internal unity of an expression — whether its elements cohere into a single, vivid intuition. The latter concerns the external correspondence between a representation and the statistical regularities of experience. A fantastical tale may be aesthetically perfect while being empirically impossible; a documentary account may be highly probable while being aesthetically inert (005-030). Croce also distinguishes probability from **historical truth**: the historian seeks what actually happened, but the artist seeks only what is vividly intuited. When critics censure a work like *Jerusalem Delivered* "by criticism based on probability" (005-030), they commit the error of treating poetry as failed history.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The criterion of probability in art originates with Aristotle's distinction in the *Poetics* between history (what happened) and poetry (what could happen according to probability or necessity). This Aristotelian doctrine was transmitted through Renaissance neo-classicism, where the unities of time, place, and action were defended on grounds of verisimilitude. French neo-classical critics such as Boileau elevated *vraisemblance* to a central critical principle. The naturalists of the nineteenth century, particularly Zola, revived probability as a scientific criterion: art should represent reality with the fidelity of a laboratory experiment.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's critique of probability is distinctive in its systematic grounding. Rather than merely arguing that probability is too restrictive a standard (the Romantic defense of imaginative freedom), Croce argues that it is a *category mistake* — the application of a logical criterion to an aesthetic object. His chapter "Criticism of Probability and of Naturalism" (005-005) treats probability and naturalism as two faces of the same error: both substitute an extra-aesthetic standard (empirical likelihood, natural causality) for the properly aesthetic standard of expressive unity. "By probability is really meant the artistic coherence of the representation" (005-012), Croce argues — critics invoke probability when they should be evaluating the internal logic of the intuition itself.

## Criticism and Debate

> "Why should art proceed according to probability? What do you mean by talking of probability in a domain where the only law is the coherence of the image?" (021-282, 022-124). Croce's rhetorical questions crystallize his view that probability-talk in aesthetics is a persistent conceptual confusion.

Defenders of probability as an aesthetic criterion argue that Croce conflates two distinct senses: *external* probability (correspondence to empirical regularities) and *internal* probability (coherence within the imagined world). Croce's response, implicit in his system, is that "internal probability" is simply another name for aesthetic coherence — and that retaining the language of probability only perpetuates the confusion it purports to resolve.
