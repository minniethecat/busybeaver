[021-001] *Middle Ages, Mysticism, Ideas on [[beauty|the beautiful]].*

[021-002] Almost all the developments of ancient Æsthetic were continued by

[021-003] tradition or reappeared by spontaneous generation in the course of the

[021-004] Middle Ages. Neo-Platonic mysticism continued, entrusted to the care

[021-005] of the pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite (*De cœlesti hierarchia,

[021-006] De ecclesiastica hierarchia, De divinis nominibus,* etc.), to the

[021-007] translations of these works made by John Scotus Eriugena, and to the

[021-008] divulgations of the Spanish Jews (Avicebron). The Christian God took

[021-009] the place of the Summum Bonum or Idea: God, wisdom, goodness, supreme

[021-010] beauty, source of beautiful things in nature, which are a ladder to

[021-011] the contemplation of the Creator. But these speculations continued to

[021-012] recede further and further from the consideration of art, with which

[021-013] Plotinus had connected them; and the empty definitions of the beautiful

[021-014] by Cicero and other ancient writers were often repeated. Saint

[021-015] Augustine defined beauty in general as unity (*omnis pulchritudinis

[021-016] forma unitas est,*) and that of the body as *congruentia partium cum

[021-017] quadam colons suavitate,* and the old distinction between something

[021-018] that is beautiful in itself and relative beauty reappeared in a book

[021-019] of his, which has been lost, entitled *De pulchro et apto;* the very

[021-020] name shows that he reasserted the old distinction between the beautiful

[021-021] in itself and the relatively beautiful, *quoniam apte accommodaretur

[021-022] alicui.* Elsewhere he notes that an [[representation|image]] is called beautiful *si

[021-023] perfecte implei illud cujus imago est, et coaequatur ei.*[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_1_45)

[021-024] Thomas Aquinas varied but little from him in positing three requisites

[021-025] for beauty: integrity or perfection, due proportion, and clearness;

[021-026] following Aristotle, he distinguished the beautiful from the good,

[021-027] defining the first as that which pleases in the mere contemplation of

[021-028] it (*pulcrum ... id cujus ipsa apprehensio placet*); he referred to

[021-029] the beauty that even base things possess if well imitated, and applied

[021-030] the doctrine of imitation to the beauty of the Second Person of the

[021-031] Trinity (*in quantum est imago expressa Patris*).[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_2_46) If it were wished

[021-032] to discover references to the hedonistic conception of art, it would

[021-033] be possible to do this, with a little goodwill, in some of the sayings

[021-034] of jongleurs and troubadours. Æsthetic rigorism, the total negation

[021-035] of art for religion or for divine and human science, shows itself in

[021-036] Tertullian and among certain Fathers of the Church, at the entrance to

[021-037] the Middle Ages; at their conclusion, in a certain crude scholastic

[021-038] spirit, for example in Cecco d' Ascoli, who proclaimed against Dante:

[021-039] "I leave trifles behind me and return to the *true*; fables are always

[021-040] unpleasing to me," and later, in the reactionary Savonarola. But the

[021-041] narcotic theory of pedagogic or moralistic art prevailed over every

[021-042] other. It had contributed to send to sleep the æsthetic doubts and

[021-043] inquiries of the ancients, and was well suited to a period of relative

[021-044] decadence of culture. This was all the more the case, seeing that it

[021-045] accorded well with the moral and religious ideas of the Middle Ages,

[021-046] and afforded a justification not only for the new art of Christian

[021-047] inspiration, but also for the surviving works of classical and pagan

[021-048] art.

[021-049] *The pedagogic theory of art in the Middle Ages.*

[021-050] The allegorical interpretation was again a means of salvation for these

[021-051] last. The *De continentia Virgiliana* of Fulgentius (sixth century)

[021-052] is a curious monument to this fact. This work made Virgil compatible

[021-053] with the Middle Ages and opened his way to that great reputation which

[021-054] he was destined to attain, as the "gentle sage who knew all things."

[021-055] Even John of Salisbury says of the Roman poet, that "*sub imagine

[021-056] fabularum totius philosophiae exprimit veritatem.*"[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_3_47) The process of

[021-057] interpretation became fixed in the doctrine of the *four meanings,*

[021-058] literal, allegorical, moral and anagogic, which Dante afterwards

[021-059] transferred to vernacular poetry. It would be easy to accumulate

[021-060] quotations from mediæval writers, repeating in all keys the theory

[021-061] that art inculcates the truths of morality and of faith and constrains

[021-062] hearts to Christian piety, beginning with those well-known verses of

[021-063] Theodulf: "*In quorum dictis* (that is to say, in the utterances of the

[021-064] poets) *quamquam sint frivola multa, Plurima sub falso tegmine vera

[021-065] latent,*" and so on, until we reach the doctrines and opinions of our

[021-066] own great men, Dante and Boccaccio. For Dante, poetry "*nihil aliud est

[021-067] quam fictio rhethorica in musicaque posita.*"[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_4_48) The poet should have

[021-068] a "reasoning" in his verses "under a cloak of figure or of rhetorical

[021-069] colour"; and it would be a shameful thing for him, if, "when asked,

[021-070] he were not able to divest his words of such a garment, in such a way

[021-071] as to show that they possessed a true meaning."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_5_49) Readers sometimes

[021-072] stop at the external vesture alone, and this indeed suffices for

[021-073] those who, like the vulgar, do not succeed in penetrating the hidden

[021-074] meaning. Poetry will say to the vulgar, which does not understand "its

[021-075] argument," what a song of Dante's says at its conclusion, "At least

[021-076] behold how *beautiful* I am": if you are not able to obtain instruction

[021-077] from me, at least enjoy me as a pleasing thing. Many, indeed, "their

[021-078] beauty more than their goodness will delight," in poems, unless they

[021-079] are assisted by commentaries in the nature of the *Convivio,* "a light

[021-080] which will allow every shade of meaning to reach them."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_6_50) Poetry was

[021-081] the "gay science," "*un fingimiento*" (as the Spanish poet the Marquis

[021-082] of Santillana wrote) "*de cosas utiles, cubiertas ó veladas con muy

[021-083] fermosa cobertura, compuestas, distinguidas é scandidas, por cierto

[021-084] cuento, pessoé medida.*"[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_7_51)

[021-085] It would not then be correct to say that the Middle Ages simply

[021-086] identified art with theology and with philosophy. Indeed it sharply

[021-087] distinguished the one from the other, defining art and poetry, like

[021-088] Dante, with the words *fictio rhethorica*, "figure" and "rhetorical

[021-089] colour," "cloak," "beauty," or like Santillana with those of

[021-090] *fingimiento* or *fermosa cobertura.* This pleasing falsity was

[021-091] justified from the practical point of view, very much in the same way

[021-092] as sexual union and love were justified and sanctified in matrimony.

[021-093] This did not exclude, indeed it implied, that the perfect state was

[021-094] certainly celibacy—that is to say, pure science, free from admixture

[021-095] of art.

[021-096] *Hints of an Æsthetic in scholastic philosophy.*

[021-097] The only tendency that had no true and proper representatives was

[021-098] the sound scientific tendency. The *Poetics* of Aristotle itself was

[021-099] hardly known or rather it was ill-known, from the Latin translation

[021-100] that a German of the name of Hermann made, not earlier than 1256, of

[021-101] the paraphrase or commentary of Averroes. Perhaps the best of the

[021-102] mediæval investigations into language is that supplied by Dante's *De

[021-103] vulgari eloquentia,* where the word is, however, still looked upon as

[021-104] a sign ("*rationale signum et sensuale ... natura sensuale quidem,

[021-105] in quantum sonus est, rationale vero in quantum aliquid significare

[021-106] videtur ad piacitum*").[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_8_52) The study of the expressive, æsthetic,

[021-107] linguistic faculty would, however, have found an appropriate occasion

[021-108] and a point of departure in the secular debate between nominalism and

[021-109] realism, which could not avoid touching to some extent the relations

[021-110] between the word and the flesh, thought and language. Duns Scotus wrote

[021-111] a treatise *De modis significandi seu* (the addition is due perhaps to

[021-112] the editors) *grammatica speculativa*.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_9_53) Abelard had defined sensation

[021-113] as *confusa conceptio,* and *imaginatio* as a faculty that preserved

[021-114] sensations; the intellect renders discursive what is intuitive in the

[021-115] preceding stage, and we have finally the perfection of knowledge in

[021-116] the [[intuition|intuitive knowledge]] of the discursive. We find the same importance

[021-117] attached to intuitive knowledge, perception, of the individual or

[021-118] *species specialissima,* in Duns Scotus, together with the progressive

[021-119] denominations of the different sorts of knowledge as *confusæ,

[021-120] indistinctæ* and *distinctæ.* We shall see this terminology reappear,

[021-121] big with consequences, at the very commencement of modern Æsthetic.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_10_54)

[021-122] *[[renaissance|Renaissance]]. Philography and philosophical and empirical

[021-123] inquiries concerning the beautiful.*

[021-124] It may be said that the literary and artistic doctrines and opinions

[021-125] of the Middle Ages have, with few exceptions, a value rather for the

[021-126] history of culture than for the general history of science. The like

[021-127] observation holds good of the Renaissance, for here, too, the circle of

[021-128] the ideas of antiquity was not overstepped. Culture increases; original

[021-129] sources are studied; the ancient writers are translated and commented

[021-130] upon; many treatises are written and henceforth printed upon poetry

[021-131] and the arts, grammars, rhetorics, dialogues, and dissertations upon

[021-132] the beautiful: the proportions have increased, the world has become

[021-133] bigger; but truly original ideas do not yet show themselves in the

[021-134] domain of æsthetic science. The mystical tradition is refreshed and

[021-135] strengthened by the renewed cult of Plato: Marsilio Ficino, Pico della

[021-136] Mirandola, Cattani, Leon Battista Alberti, in the fifteenth century,

[021-137] and Pietro Bembo, Mario Equicola, Castiglione, Nobili, Betussi, and

[021-138] very many others in the following century, wrote upon the Beautiful

[021-139] and upon Love. Among the most noteworthy productions of the sort, a

[021-140] crossing of the mediæval and classical currents, is the book of the

[021-141] *Dialogues of Love* (1535), composed in Italian by the Spanish Jew

[021-142] Leo, and translated into all the cultured languages of the time.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_11_55)

[021-143] The three parts into which it is divided treat of the nature and

[021-144] essence, of the universality, and of the origin of love; and it is

[021-145] demonstrated that every beautiful thing is good, but not every good

[021-146] thing is beautiful; that beauty is a grace which dilates the soul and

[021-147] moves it to love, and that knowledge of lesser beauties leads to that

[021-148] of higher spiritual beauties. The author gave the name of "Philography"

[021-149] to these and similar affirmations and effusions of which the book is

[021-150] composed. Equicola's[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_12_56) work is also interesting, because it contains

[021-151] historical accounts of those who wrote upon the subject before he did

[021-152] so himself. The same intuition was versified and sighed forth by the

[021-153] Petrarchists in their sonnets and ballads, while others, rebellious and

[021-154] mocking, derided it in comedies, verses in *terza rima* and parodies of

[021-155] all sorts. Some mathematicians, reincarnations of Pythagoras, set to

[021-156] work to determine beauty by exact relations: for instance Leonardo's

[021-157] friend, Luca Paciolo, in the *De divina proportione* (1509), in which

[021-158] he laid down the pretended æsthetic law of the golden section.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_13_57) And

[021-159] side by side with these new Pythagoreans were those who revived the

[021-160] canon of Polycletus as to the beauty of the human body, especially

[021-161] of the female body, such as Firenzuola, Franco, Luigini, and Dolce.

[021-162] Michæl Angelo fixed an empirical canon for painting in general, when

[021-163] he stated that the means of giving movement and grace to figures[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_14_58)

[021-164] consisted in the observance of a certain arithmetical relation. Others,

[021-165] such as Fulvio Pellegrino Morato, investigated the symbolism or meaning

[021-166] of colours. The Platonists generally placed beauty in the soul, the

[021-167] Aristotelians rather in the physical qualities. The Averroist, Agostino

[021-168] Nifo, amid much chatter and many inconclusive remarks, demonstrated

[021-169] the existence of the beautiful in nature by describing the supremely

[021-170] beautiful body of Joan of Aragon, Princess of Tagliacozzo, to whom the

[021-171] book is dedicated.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_15_59) Torquato Tasso, in the "Mintumo,"[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_16_60) imitated

[021-172] the uncertainties of the *Hippias* of Plato, not without making a free

[021-173] use of the speculations of Plotinus. A chapter of the *Poetica* of

[021-174] Campanella possesses greater importance, where he describes the good as

[021-175] *signum boni* and the ugly as *signum mali,* understanding by good the

[021-176] three prime forces of Power, Wisdom and Love. Although Campanella was

[021-177] still tied to the Platonic idea of the beautiful, the conception of a

[021-178] sign or symbol, here introduced by him, represents progress. By this

[021-179] means he succeeded in perceiving that material things or external facts

[021-180] are neither beautiful nor ugly in themselves. "Mandricard called the

[021-181] wounds in the bodies of his friends the Moors beautiful, for they were

[021-182] large and gave evidence of the great strength of Roland who dealt them;

[021-183] Saint Augustine called the gashes and the dislocations in the body of

[021-184] Saint Vincent beautiful, because they were evidence of his endurance,

[021-185] but they were on the other hand ugly in so far as they were signs of

[021-186] the cruelty of the tyrant Dacianus and of his executioners. It is

[021-187] beautiful to die fighting, said Virgil, for it is the sign of a strong

[021-188] soul. The pet dog of his mistress will seem beautiful to the lover, and

[021-189] doctors call even urine and fæces beautiful, when they indicate health.

[021-190] Everything is both beautiful and ugly" (*quapropter nihil est quod non

[021-191] sit pulcrum simul et turpe*).[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_17_61) In such observations as these we have

[021-192] not a mere state of mystical exaltation, but to some extent a movement

[021-193] in the direction of analysis.

[021-194] *The pedagogic theory of art and the Poetics of Aristotle.*

[021-195] Nothing better serves to demonstrate that the Renaissance did not pass

[021-196] beyond the confines of ancient æsthetic thought than the fact that

[021-197] notwithstanding the renewed acquaintance with the thought of Aristotle,

[021-198] the pedagogic theory of art not only persisted and triumphed, but was

[021-199] transplanted bodily into the text of Aristotle, where its interpreters

[021-200] read it with a certainty that we have to make efforts to achieve.

[021-201] Certainly, a Robortelli (1548) or a Castelvetro (1570) stopped short

[021-202] at the simple, purely hedonistic solution, giving simple pleasure as

[021-203] the end of art: poetry, says Castelvetro, "was discovered solely

[021-204] for the purpose of delighting and of recreating ... the souls of the

[021-205] rude multitude and of the common people."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_18_62) And here and there

[021-206] some were able to free themselves from both the pleasure theory and

[021-207] that of the didactic end; but the majority, such as Segni, Maggi,

[021-208] Vettori,[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_19_63) were for the *docere delectando.* Scaliger (1561) declared

[021-209] that mimesis or imitation was "*finis medius ad illum ultimum qui est

[021-210] docendi cum delectatione,*" and believing himself to be altogether in

[021-211] agreement with Aristotle as to this, he continued, "*docet affectus

[021-212] poeta per actiones, ut bonos amplectamur atque imitemur ad agendum,

[021-213] malos aspernemur ad abstinendum.*"[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_20_64) Piccolomini (1575) observed

[021-214] that "It must not be thought that so many excellent poets and artists,

[021-215] ancient and modern, would have devoted such care and diligence to this

[021-216] most noble study, had they not known and believed that in so doing

[021-217] they were aiding human life," and if "they had not thought that we

[021-218] were to be instructed, directed, and well established by it."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_21_65) The

[021-219] "truth preserved in soft verses, which attracts and persuades the most

[021-220] reluctant" (Tasso),[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_22_66) with the comparison from Lucretius attached,

[021-221] is the conception that even Campanella repeats. Poetry is for him

[021-222] "*Rhetorica quaedam figurata, quasi magica, quae exempla ministrat ad

[021-223] suadendum bonum et dissuadendum malum delectabiliter iis qui simplici

[021-224] verum et bonum audire nolunt, aut non possunt aut nesciunt.*"[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_23_67) Thus

[021-225] returned the comparison of poetry with oratory; according to Segni

[021-226] they only differ because the first occupies a more lofty situation:

[021-227] "for since imitation representing itself in act by means of poetry, in

[021-228] mighty, chosen words, in metaphors, images, and indeed the whole of

[021-229] figured speech, which is to be found more in poetry than in the art

[021-230] of oratory, the metrical qualities that are also required in verse,

[021-231] the subjects of which it treats, which have something of the great and

[021-232] delightful, make it appear most beautiful and worthy of being held all

[021-233] the greater marvel."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_24_68) "Three most noble arts" (wrote Tassoni in

[021-234] 1620, and he repeated common opinion), "History, Poetics, and Oratory,

[021-235] come under the heading of Politics and depend upon it; the first of

[021-236] these has reference to the instruction of princes and gentlemen, the

[021-237] second of the people, the third of those who give counsel in public

[021-238] trials or defend private ones that come up for judgment."[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_25_69)

[021-239] According to these views, the tragical catharsis was regarded as

[021-240] designed in general to demonstrate the instability of fortune, or to

[021-241] terrify by example, or to proclaim the triumph of justice, or to render

[021-242] the spectators insensible to the strokes of fortune, owing to their

[021-243] familiarity with suffering. The pedagogic theory, thus renewed and

[021-244] sustained by the authority of the ancients, was popularized in [[france|France]],

[021-245] Spain, [[england|England]] and [[germany|Germany]], together with all the Italian poetic

[021-246] doctrines of the Renaissance. The French writers of the period of Louis

[021-247] XIV. are altogether penetrated with it. "*Cette science agréable qui

[021-248] mêle la gravité des préceptes avec la douceur du langage*," is what La

[021-249] Ménardière calls poetry (1640), in the same way as Le Bossu (1675), for

[021-250] whom "*le premier but du poète est d'instruire*,"[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_26_70) as Homer taught,

[021-251] when he wrote two interesting didactic manuals relating to military and

[021-252] political events: the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey.*

[021-253] *The "Poetics of the Renaissance."*

[021-254] This pedagogic theory has therefore been reasonably described by all

[021-255] the modern critics in concert, as if by antonomasia, as the *Poetics

[021-256] of the Renaissance.* It must, however, always be understood that it

[021-257] did not appear for the first time in the fifteenth or sixteenth

[021-258] century, but that it was prevalent and generally accepted at that

[021-259] time. It may even be remarked, as has already been acutely done,[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_27_71)

[021-260] that the Renaissance naturally did not distinguish the didactic kind

[021-261] of poetry from the other kinds, since for it every kind of poetry was

[021-262] didactic. But the Renaissance was not a real Renaissance, save when

[021-263] and where it continued the interrupted spiritual work of antiquity,

[021-264] and in this sense it would perhaps be more just to describe as its

[021-265] Poetics, or rather, as the important element in its Poetics, not the

[021-266] repetition of the pedagogic theory of antiquity and of the Middle Ages,

[021-267] but the resumption, which also took place, of the discussions upon

[021-268] the possible, the probable (*verisimile*, εικός) of Aristotle, on the

[021-269] reasons of Plato's condemnation and on the procedure of the artist who

[021-270] creates by imagining.

[021-271] *Dispute concerning the universal and the probable in art.*

[021-272] It is in such discussions that is to be found the true contribution of

[021-273] that epoch, not to learning, but to the formation of the science of

[021-274] Æsthetic. The ground was prepared and enriched through the work of the

[021-275] interpreters and commentators of Aristotle and of the new writers on

[021-276] Poetics, especially the Italians, and it was also enriched with some

[021-277] seed that was destined to sprout and to become a vigorous plant in

[021-278] the future. The study of Plato also contributed not a little to call

[021-279] attention to the function of the idea, or of the universal, in poetry.

[021-280] What meaning was to be attached to the statement that poetry should aim

[021-281] at the universal and history at the particular? What was the meaning of

[021-282] the proposition that poetry should proceed according to *probability*?

[021-283] What could that *certain idea* consist of, which Raphæl said that he

[021-284] followed in his painting?

[021-285] *Fracastoro.*

[021-286] Girolamo Fracastoro was among the first to ask himself this question

[021-287] seriously, in the dialogue *Naugerius, sive De poetica* (1555). He

[021-288] disdainfully rejected the thesis that the end of poetry is pleasure:

[021-289] far be from us, he exclaimed, so bad an opinion of the poets, who the

[021-290] ancients said were the inventors of all the good arts. Nor did the

[021-291] end of instruction seem to him to be acceptable, which is the task,

[021-292] not of poetry, but of other faculties, such as geography, history,

[021-293] agronomy, philosophy. The poet's task is to represent or to imitate,

[021-294] and he differs from the historian, not in the matter, but in the manner

[021-295] of representation. The others imitate the particular, the poet the

[021-296] universal: the others are like the painters of portraits, the poet

[021-297] produces things as he contemplates the universal and most beautiful

[021-298] idea of them: the others say only what they need to say for their

[021-299] purposes, the poet that he may say everything beautifully and fully.

[021-300] But the beauty of a poem must always be understood as relative to

[021-301] the class of subject of which it treats; it is the most beautiful

[021-302] in this class, not the supremely beautiful: one must be careful to

[021-303] guard against the equivocal or double meaning of this word "beauty"

[021-304] (*æquivocatio illius verbi*). A poet never utters what is false or

[021-305] expresses what does not exist, for his words inevitably harmonize in

[021-306] appearance or signification either with the opinions of men or with the

[021-307] universal. Nor can we accept the Platonic axiom that the poet has no

[021-308] knowledge of the things of which he treats; he does know them, but in

[021-309] his own poet's manner.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_28_72)

[021-310] *L. Castelvetro.*

[021-311] While Fracastoro strives to elaborate the important passage in

[021-312] Aristotle touching the universal of poetry, and though somewhat

[021-313] vague in his treatment, keeps fairly close to the mark; Castelvetro,

[021-314] on the contrary, judges the Aristotelian fragment with the freedom

[021-315] and superior knowledge of the true critic. He recognizes that the

[021-316] *Poetics* is merely a notebook recording certain principles and

[021-317] methods of compiling the art, not the art fully compiled. He remarks,

[021-318] moreover, not without logical acumen, that Aristotle having adopted

[021-319] the criterion of probability or of that "which presents an appearance

[021-320] of historic truth," should have applied his theory in the first

[021-321] case to history, not to poetry; for history being a "narrative

[021-322] according to truth of memorable human actions," and poetry a narrative

[021-323] according to probability of events which might possibly occur, the

[021-324] second cannot receive "all its radiance" from the first. Nor does it

[021-325] escape him that Aristotle describes two different things by the one

[021-326] word "imitation": (*a*) "following the example of another," which is

[021-327] "acting in exactly the same way as another without knowing the reason

[021-328] of such action": and (*b*) the imitation "demanded by poetry," which

[021-329] "does things in a manner totally different from that in which they

[021-330] have been done hitherto and proposes a new example for imitation."

[021-331] Nevertheless Castelvetro cannot extricate himself from the confusion

[021-332] between the imaginary and the historical; for he himself says "the

[021-333] realm of the former is generally that of certainty," but "the field

[021-334] of certainty is often crossed with bars of uncertainty just as the

[021-335] field of uncertainty is often crossed with bars of certainty." Also

[021-336] what can be said of this curious interpretation of the Aristotelian

[021-337] theory of pleasure experienced in the imitation of ugly models, that

[021-338] such pleasure is based on the fact that since an imitation is always

[021-339] imperfect, it is incapable of exciting the disgust and fear which would

[021-340] arise from the contemplation of real ugliness? And what of his remark

[021-341] that the characteristics of painting and poetry are so diverse as to

[021-342] be in opposition one to the other; imitation of objects giving rise

[021-343] to great pleasure in the former art and as great displeasure in the

[021-344] latter? And so on in numberless cases of bold but scarcely felicitous

[021-345] subtleties.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_29_73)

[021-346] *Piccolomini and Pinciano.*

[021-347] In opposition to Robortelli, who asserted the identity of the probable

[021-348] and the false, Piccolomini held that the probable (*verisimile*) is

[021-349] inherently neither false nor true, only by accident becoming one or

[021-350] other.[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_30_74) Of the same mind is the Spaniard Alfonso Lopez Pinciano

[021-351] (1596), who says the scope of poetry "*no es la mentira, que seria

[021-352] coincider con la sophística, ni la historia que seria tomar la materia

[021-353] al histórico; y no siendo historia porque toca fabúlas ni mentira

[021-354] porque toca historia, tiene por objeto el verisimil, que todo lo

[021-355] abraza. De aqui resulta que es un arte superior á la metaphysica,

[021-356] porqué comprende mucho mas, y se extiende a lo que es y á lo que no

[021-357] es.*"[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_31_75) What may lie behind this notion of probability is still

[021-358] indefinite and impenetrable.

[021-359] *Fr. Patrizzi* (*Patricius*).

[021-360] Moved by a wish to place poetry on a foundation other than the

[021-361] probable, Francesco Patrizzi, the anti-Aristotelian, composed his

[021-362] *Poetica* between 1555 and 1586 in refutation of all Aristotle's main

[021-363] doctrines. Patrizzi notes that the word "imitation" is given many

[021-364] meanings by the Greek philosopher, who uses it now to denote a single

[021-365] word, now to describe a tragedy; at times it stands for a figure of

[021-366] speech, at others for a fiction: whence he draws the logical conclusion

[021-367] (from which, however, he shrinks alarmed) "that all philosophic and

[021-368] other kinds of writing and speaking are poetry, since they are made

[021-369] of words which themselves are imitations." He observes further that,

[021-370] according to Aristotle, it is impossible to distinguish between poetry

[021-371] and history (since both are imitations), or to prove that verse is not

[021-372] essential to poetry, or that history, science and art are unsuitable

[021-373] material for it; since Aristotle in several passages says that poetry

[021-374] may comprise "fable, actual occurrences, belief of others, duty,

[021-375] the best, necessity, the possible, the probable, the credible, the

[021-376] incredible, the suitable" as well as "all things worldly." After these

[021-377] objections, some sound, others sophistical, Patrizzi comes to the

[021-378] conclusion that "there is no truth in the dogma that poetry is wholly

[021-379] imitation; and even if it be imitation at all, it belongs not to poets

[021-380] alone, nor is it mere imitation of any kind, but something else not

[021-381] mentioned by Aristotle nor pointed out by any one else, nor yet borne

[021-382] into the mind of man. The discovery may possibly be made in course of

[021-383] time, or some one may hit upon the truth and bring it to light"; but

[021-384] up to the present "such discovery has not been made."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-9.htm.html#Footnote_32_76)

[021-385] Yet these confessions of ignorance, these endeavours, though vain, to

[021-386] escape from the Aristotelian circle of ideas, and the great literary

[021-387] controversies of the sixteenth century concerning the concept of poetic

[021-388] truth and the probable had their use in that they stimulated interest

[021-389] by directing attention to a mystery still unsolved. Thought had once

[021-390] more begun to move upon the æsthetic problem, and this time it was not

[021-391] destined to be broken off or to lose itself.
