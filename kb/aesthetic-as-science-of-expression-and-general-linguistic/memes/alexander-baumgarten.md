# Alexander Baumgarten

Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten (1714–1762) was a German Enlightenment philosopher who **coined the term "aesthetic"** (*aesthetica*) and established it as a distinct philosophical discipline. Croce discusses Baumgarten as the founder of modern aesthetics, while critiquing his intellectualist framework (P02-195–P02-197).

## Biography

Baumgarten studied at Halle under Christian Wolff and became professor at Frankfurt an der Oder. His *Aesthetica* (1750–1758) was the first work to use the term "aesthetics" in its modern sense, though the term appears earlier in his *Meditationes Philosophicae* (1735).

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Æsthetic as Science of Sensible Knowledge**: Baumgarten defined aesthetics as the science of sensible cognition (*scientia cognitionis sensitivae*) — the "inferior" counterpart to logic, which governs intellectual cognition (P02-195)
- **Beauty as Perfection of Sensible Knowledge**: For Baumgarten, beauty is the perfection of sensible cognition — the clarity, richness, and coherence of sensory representations. This Leibnizian-Wolffian framework treats beauty as a lower form of truth
- **Intellectualism of Baumgarten**: Croce's main critique is the "intellectualism of Baumgarten" (P02-197) — the subordination of aesthetic knowledge to logical knowledge. For Croce, intuition is autonomous, not a lesser version of conceptual thought
