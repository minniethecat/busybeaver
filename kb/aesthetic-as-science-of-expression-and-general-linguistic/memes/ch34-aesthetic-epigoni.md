[034-001] *F. de Sanctis: development of his thought.*

[034-002] On the other hand, the autonomy of art found a strong supporter in

[034-003] [[italy|Italy]] in the critical work of Francesco de Sanctis, who held private

[034-004] classes in literature at Naples from 1838 to 1848, taught at Turin and

[034-005] Zürich from 1852 to 1860 and in 1870 became professor in the University

[034-006] of Naples. He expressed his doctrines in critical essays, in monographs

[034-007] on Italian writers and in his classic *History of Italian Literature.*

[034-008] Receiving his first elements of old Italian culture in Puoti's

[034-009] school, his natural bent! towards speculation led him to investigate

[034-010] grammatical and rhetorical doctrines with the view of reducing them

[034-011] to a system; but he soon began to criticize and to grow out of this

[034-012] phase. He pronounced Fortunio, Alunno, Accarisio and Corso "empirics";

[034-013] he had a slightly better opinion of Bembo, Varchi, Castelvetro and

[034-014] Salviati, who introduced "method" into grammar, a process completed

[034-015] subsequently by Buonmattei, Corticelli and Bartoli; and he proclaimed

[034-016] Francisco Sanchez, author of the *Minerva,* "the Descartes of

[034-017] grammarians." From these his admiration spread to the [[france|French]] writers of

[034-018] the eighteenth century and the philosophical grammars of; Du Marsais,

[034-019] Beauzée, Condillac and Gérard; following in their wake and pursuing the

[034-020] ideal of Leibniz, he conceived a "logical grammar"; in this effort,

[034-021] however, he soon began to recognize the impossibility of reducing the

[034-022] differences of languages to fixed logical principles., If he found

[034-023] the French theorists admirable in their ability to reconstitute the

[034-024] simple and primitive forms; from "I love" to "I am loving," something

[034-025] disquieted him; "Such decomposition of 'I love' into 'I am loving'"

[034-026] (said he) "deadens the word by depriving it of the movement proceeding

[034-027] from active will."[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_1_586) In the same way he read and criticized the

[034-028] writers of treatises on [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] and Poetics from sixteenth-century

[034-029] men such as Castelvetro and Torquato Tasso (whom he dared to describe

[034-030] as an "indifferent critic," to the great scandal of Neapolitan men

[034-031] of letters) to Muratori and Gravina, "more acute than accurate"; and

[034-032] eighteenth-century Italians, Bettinelli, Algarotti and Cesarotti.

[034-033] Coldly rational rules found no favour with him: he urged the young to

[034-034] confront literary works boldly and freely absorb impressions, the only

[034-035] possible foundation for taste.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_2_587)

[034-036] *Influence of Hegelism.*

[034-037] Philosophical study had not been abandoned and had not even fallen

[034-038] into entire decadence in Southern Italy; in these days of renewed

[034-039] interest in philosophy the theories on [[beauty|Beauty]] from over the Alps and

[034-040] the new ideas of Gioberti and other Italians[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_3_588) aroused enthusiastic

[034-041] discussion. [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] was read again, and Bénard's French translation of

[034-042] [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s *Æsthetic* appeared and was canvassed in Naples volume by

[034-043] volume (the first in 1840, the second in 1843, and the rest between

[034-044] 1848 and 1852). In its desire for new intellectual food Italian youth

[034-045] set itself to learn German: [[francesco-de-sanctis|De Sanctis]] himself had to translate the

[034-046] greater *Logic* of Hegel and Rosenkranz's *History of Literature*

[034-047] in the dungeon of the Bourbon prison where he was incarcerated on

[034-048] account of his liberal opinions. The new critical tendency was named

[034-049] "philosophism" to distinguish it from the old grammatical criticism

[034-050] and from the vague, incoherent, exaggerated [[romanticism|Romanticism]]. Philosophism

[034-051] attracted De Sanctis; to show how deeply he was imbued with the

[034-052] Hegelian spirit a tale was told that, having devoured the first volumes

[034-053] of Bénard's translation, he guessed the contents of the remaining

[034-054] volumes and, before they could appear, was expounding them publicly in

[034-055] his classroom.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_4_589)

[034-056] His first writings show traces of [[idealism|metaphysical idealism]] and Hegelism;

[034-057] and they still linger here and there in the terminology of his later

[034-058] works. In a lecture prior to 1848 he placed the safety of criticism in

[034-059] the philosophic school which, in works of literature, fixed its eyes

[034-060] upon "that absolute part ... that uncertain idea which moves within the

[034-061] mind of great writers, till it appears abroad clothed in fine raiment

[034-062] only less beautiful than itself."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_5_590) In a preface to [[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]]'s plays

[034-063] (1850) he wrote, "The Idea is not thought, nor is poetry reason in

[034-064] song, as a poet of our time is pleased to assert; the idea is at once

[034-065] necessity and freedom, reason and passion, and its perfect form in

[034-066] drama is action."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_6_591) Elsewhere he calls attention to the death of faith

[034-067] and poetry, absorbed by the development of philosophy: a thesis, he

[034-068] remarked some years later, "imposed on our generation by Hegel with his

[034-069] omnipotent thought."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_7_592) In 1856 he attempted a definition of humour as

[034-070] "an artistic form having for signification the destruction of limit,

[034-071] with consciousness of such destruction."[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_8_593) Not to dwell too long on

[034-072] other particulars, in the distinction to which De Sanctis always held

[034-073] firm throughout his critical work, that between Fancy and [[imagination|Imagination]],

[034-074] the latter considered as the true and only faculty of poetry, arises

[034-075] undoubtedly from suggestions of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]] and Hegel (*Einbildungskraft,

[034-076] Phantasie)*; from the same philosophers come the phrases "prosaic

[034-077] content," "prosaic world," sometimes used by him.

[034-078] *Unconscious criticism of Hegelism.*

[034-079] For De Sanctis the Hegelian Æsthetic was but a lever wherewith to

[034-080] lift himself clear of the discussions and views of the old Italian

[034-081] schools. A fresh, clear spirit such as his could not escape the

[034-082] arbitrary shackles of grammarians and rhetoricians only to fall into

[034-083] those of metaphysicians, the torturers of art. He absorbed the vital

[034-084] part of Hegel's teaching and re-expressed the Hegelian theories in

[034-085] correct or somewhat attenuated interpretations; but he only maintained

[034-086] with hesitation, and in the end openly rebelled against, all that was

[034-087] artificial, formalistic and pedantic in Hegel.

[034-088] The following examples of such reductions and attenuations show how

[034-089] substantial and radical was the change he effected. "Faith has vanished

[034-090] and poetry is dead" (he wrote in 1856, echoing Hegel); "or it were

[034-091] better to say" (here is De Sanctis' own correction) "faith and poetry

[034-092] are immortal: what has disappeared is but one particular mode of their

[034-093] being. To-day faith springs from conviction and poetry is the spark

[034-094] struck from meditation; they are not dead, they are transformed."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_9_594)

[034-095] Certainly he distinguished between imagination and fancy; but for

[034-096] him imagination was never the mystic faculty of transcendental

[034-097] apperception, the intellectual intuition of German metaphysicians,

[034-098] but simply the poet's faculty of synthesis and creation, contrasting

[034-099] with fancy as the faculty of collecting particulars and materials in

[034-100] a somewhat mechanical fashion.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_10_595) When students of Vico and Hegel

[034-101] understood and expounded their master's theories as emphasizing the

[034-102] importance of concepts in art, De Sanctis replied, "The concept does

[034-103] not exist in art, nature or history: the poet works unconsciously and

[034-104] sees no concept but only form, in which he is involved and well-nigh

[034-105] lost. If the philosopher, by means of abstraction, can extract the

[034-106] concept thence and contemplate it in all its purity, he acts in a way

[034-107] entirely contrary to that of art, nature and history." He warned his

[034-108] hearers not to misunderstand Vico, who, when he extracts concepts and

[034-109] exemplary types from the Homeric poems, is not writing as an art critic

[034-110] but as a historian of civilization: Achilles is artistically Achilles,

[034-111] not strength or any other abstraction.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_11_596) Thus his polemic is directed

[034-112] in the first instance against misunderstanding what he called the true

[034-113] Hegelian thought, which was in fact usually a correction made upon

[034-114] Hegel more or less consciously by himself. He was able to boast in

[034-115] his latter years that even at the time when all Naples went wild over

[034-116] Hegel, "at the time when Hegel was master of the field," he had always

[034-117] "made certain reservations and refused to accept his apriorism, his

[034-118] triad or his formulæ."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_12_597)

[034-119] *Criticisms of German Æsthetic.*

[034-120] De Sanctis also took up an independent attitude towards the other

[034-121] German æstheticians. The views of Wilhelm Schlegel, very advanced

[034-122] for the day in which they had been promulgated, seemed to him to

[034-123] have been already superseded. In 1856 he wrote that Schlegel strives

[034-124] to "transcend ordinary criticism, which leads a humdrum existence

[034-125] among phraseology, versification and elocution, but loses its way

[034-126] and never comes face to face with art: whereas Schlegel throws

[034-127] himself headlong into the probable, the decorous and the moral; into

[034-128] everything save art."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_13_598) Thrown by the hazards of life into German

[034-129] territory, he found himself at the Zürich Polytechnic, and found among

[034-130] his colleagues (only imagine such a thing!) Theodor Vischer. What

[034-131] opinion can he have formed of the ponderous Hegelian scholastic who

[034-132] emerged dusty and panting from the systematic labours so well known to

[034-133] us, and smiled disdainfully at the poetry and music of the decadent

[034-134] Italian race? De Sanctis writes, "I went there with my opinions and

[034-135] my prejudices and ridiculed their ridicule. Richard Wagner seemed to

[034-136] me a corrupter of music, and nothing could be more inæsthetic than

[034-137] the Æsthetic of Vischer."[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_14_599) His desire to correct the distorted

[034-138] views of Vischer, Adolf Wagner, Valentin Schmidt and other German

[034-139] critics and philosophers led him to undertake in 1858-59 a course of

[034-140] lectures before an international audience at Zürich upon Ariosto

[034-141] and Petrarch, the two Italian poets worst maltreated by these judges

[034-142] because hardest to reduce to philosophical allegory. He sketched a

[034-143] typical German critic and contrasted him with a French one, each with

[034-144] his own characteristic defects. "The Frenchman does not indulge in

[034-145] theories; he goes straight to the subject: his argument palpitates with

[034-146] warmth of impression and sagacity of observation: he never leaves the

[034-147] concrete: he estimates the quality of the talent and the work, studying

[034-148] the man in order to understand the writer." He makes the mistake of

[034-149] substituting reflexion on the psychology of the author and history of

[034-150] his time for reflexion upon art. "Quite otherwise is your German: be a

[034-151] thing never so plain, he makes it his business to manipulate, distort

[034-152] and embroil: he accumulates a mass of darkness from whose centre rays

[034-153] of dazzling light now and again shoot forth: truth is there at bottom,

[034-154] in grievous pangs of parturition. Confronted with a [[art|work of art]], he

[034-155] labours to fasten down and fix the quality which is most evanescent

[034-156] and impalpable. While nobody is more given to talk of life and the

[034-157] world of the living, nobody on earth takes more pains to decompose and

[034-158] disembody it in generalities: as consequence of this last process (last

[034-159] in appearance, that is to say; in reality preconceived and *a priori*),

[034-160] he is able to fit you the same boot on every foot and the same coat on

[034-161] every back." "The German school is dominated by metaphysic, the French

[034-162] by history."[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_15_600) [[About]] this time (1858) a Piedmontese review published

[034-163] his exhaustive critical survey of the philosophy of [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]],[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_16_601)

[034-164] which was then beginning to attract disciples among his friends

[034-165] and companions in exile in Switzerland; the criticism provoked the

[034-166] philosopher himself to confess that "this Italian" had "absorbed him

[034-167] *in succum et sanguinem.*"[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_17_602) What value did De Sanctis attach to

[034-168] all Schopenhauer's subtleties concerning art? Having fully stated his

[034-169] doctrine of ideas, he contents himself with the merest reference to the

[034-170] third book "wherein is found an exaggerated theory of Æsthetic."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_18_603)

[034-171] *Final rebellion against metaphysical Æsthetic.*

[034-172] This moderate resistance and opposition to the partisans of the

[034-173] concept and to the romantic Italian mystics and moralists (he directed

[034-174] criticisms equally against Manzoni, Mazzini, Tommaseo and Cantù[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_19_604))

[034-175] turned to open rebellion in one of his critical writings on Petrarch

[034-176] (1868) in which this false tendency is characterized with biting

[034-177] sarcasm. "According to this school" (he says, meaning the school of

[034-178] Hegel and Gioberti), "according to this school the real and living is

[034-179] art only in so far as it surpasses its form and reveals its concept or

[034-180] the pure idea. The beautiful is the manifestation of the idea. Art is

[034-181] the ideal, a particular idea. Under the gaze of the artist the body

[034-182] becomes subtilized until it is nothing but the shadow of the soul, a

[034-183] beautiful veil. The world of poetry is peopled with phantasms; and

[034-184] the poet, eternal dreamer, with the eyes of one slightly intoxicated

[034-185] sees bodies float unsteadily around him and change their shapes. Nor

[034-186] do bodies merely become attenuated into forms and phantasms; these

[034-187] forms and phantasms themselves become free manifestations of every

[034-188] idea and every concept. The theory of the ideal has been driven to

[034-189] its last victorious limit, to the destruction of the very phantasms

[034-190] themselves, to concept as concept, form becoming a mere accessory."

[034-191] "Thus the vague, the undecided, the undulating, the vaporous, the

[034-192] celestial, the ærial, the veiled, the angelic, have now a high position

[034-193] among artistic forms: whilst criticism revels in the beautiful,

[034-194] the ideal, the infinite, genius, the concept, the idea, truth, the

[034-195] superintelligible, the supersensible, the being and the existent, and

[034-196] many more generalities cast into barbarous formulæ just like those

[034-197] of the scholastics from whose influence we had so much difficulty in

[034-198] escaping." All these things, instead of determining the character of

[034-199] art, do nothing; save illustrate the contrary of art: its feebleness

[034-200] and impotence, preventing it from slaying abstractions and laying hold

[034-201] of life. If beauty and the ideal have actually the meaning given them

[034-202] by these philosophers "the essence of art is neither the beautiful nor

[034-203] the ideal, but the living, the form; the ugly too belongs to art since

[034-204] ugliness lives also in nature; outside the domain of art lies nothing

[034-205] but the formless and the deformed. Thais in Malebolge is more living

[034-206] and poetical than Beatrice, who is pure allegory representing abstract

[034-207] combinations. The Beautiful? Tell me of anything as beautiful as Iago,

[034-208] a form uprisen from the profundity of real life; so rich, so concrete;

[034-209] in every part, in each finest gradation, one of the most beautiful

[034-210] creations in the world of poetry." If in the course of "wrangling

[034-211] about the idea or the concept or real, moral, or intellectual beauty,

[034-212] and confusing philosophical or moral truths with æsthetic" you choose

[034-213] to call "a great part of the poetic world ugly, granting it a permit

[034-214] merely that it may act as contrast, antagonist or foil to beauty,

[034-215] accepting Mephistopheles as a foil to Faust, or Iago as foil to

[034-216] Othello," you are imitating "those good folk who thought, *in illo

[034-217] tempore,* that the stars shone in the firmament in order to give light

[034-218] to this earth."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_20_605)

[034-219] *De Sanctis own theory*

[034-220] The æsthetic theory of De Sanctis himself arises entirely from the

[034-221] criticism of the highest manifestations of European æsthetic as known

[034-222] to him. Its nature is revealed by the contrast. "If you desire a statue

[034-223] in the vestibule of art," says he, "let it be that of [[form|Form]]; gaze upon

[034-224] this, question this, begin with this. Before form is attained, that

[034-225] exists which existed before the creation: chaos. Chaos is no doubt a

[034-226] respectable thing, with a most interesting history: science has not yet

[034-227] uttered its last word about this pre-world of fermenting elements. Art

[034-228] also has its pre-world: art also has its geology, born but yesterday

[034-229] and as yet scarcely stretched, a science *sui generis,* which is

[034-230] neither Criticism nor Æsthetic. Æsthetic appears when form appears,

[034-231] in which this pre-world is sunk, fused, forgotten and lost. Form is

[034-232] itself as the individual is himself; and no theory is so destructive

[034-233] to art as the continual harping upon the beautiful as manifestation,

[034-234] clothing, light, or veil of truth or the idea. The æsthetic world

[034-235] is not appearance, it is substance; to it indeed belongs everything

[034-236] substantial and living: its criterion, its *raison d'être,* lies

[034-237] nowhere save in this motto: I live."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_21_606)

[034-238] *The concept of form.*

[034-239] For De Sanctis, form did not mean form "in the pedantic sense attached

[034-240] to it until the end of the eighteenth century," that is to say, that

[034-241] which first strikes a superficial observer, the words, the period, the

[034-242] sense, the individual [[representation|image]];[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_22_607) or form in the Herbartian sense, the

[034-243] metaphysical hypostatization of the former. "Form is not *a Priori,* it

[034-244] is not something existing of itself and distinct from the content as

[034-245] though it were a kind of ornament or vesture or appearance or adjunct

[034-246] of the content: it is generated by the content acting in the mind of

[034-247] the artist: such as the content is, such is the form."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_23_608) Between

[034-248] form and content there is at the same time identity and diversity. In

[034-249] a work of art the content, which had been lying in a chaotic state in

[034-250] the mind of the artist, appears "not as it was originally, but as it

[034-251] has become; the whole of it, with its own value, its own importance,

[034-252] its own natural beauty enriched, not weakened, by the process."

[034-253] Therefore content is essential for the production of concrete form;

[034-254] but the abstract quality of the content does not determine that of

[034-255] artistic form." If the content, though beautiful and important, remain

[034-256] inoperative or lifeless or waste within the mind of the artist, if it

[034-257] have not sufficient generative power and reveal itself in the form as

[034-258] weak or false or vitiated, why trouble to sing its praises? In such

[034-259] cases the content may be important in itself, but as literature or

[034-260] art it is worthless. On the other hand the content may be immoral,

[034-261] absurd, false or frivolous: but if at certain times or in certain

[034-262] circumstances it has worked powerfully on in the brain of the artist,

[034-263] and taken form, such content is immortal. The gods of Homer are dead;

[034-264] the *Iliad* remains. Italy may die and, with her, every memory of Guelf

[034-265] and Ghibelline; the *Divina Commedia* will remain. The content is

[034-266] subject to all the hazards of history; it is born and it dies; the form

[034-267] is immortal."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_24_609) He held firmly to the independence of art, without

[034-268] which there can be no Æsthetic; but he objected to the exaggeration of

[034-269] the formula of art for art's sake in that it tended to the separation

[034-270] of the artist from life, to the mutilation of the content and to the

[034-271] conversion of art into a proof of mere cleverness.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_25_610)

[034-272] *De Sanctis as art-critic.*

[034-273] For De Sanctis, the concept of form was identical with that of

[034-274] imagination, the faculty of expression or representation, artistic

[034-275] vision. So much must be said by any one anxious to express clearly

[034-276] the direction which his thought was taking. But De Sanctis himself

[034-277] never succeeded in defining his own theory with scientific exactitude;

[034-278] and his æsthetic ideas remained the mere sketch of a system never

[034-279] properly interrelated and deduced. The speculative tendency shared his

[034-280] attention with many other lively interests, the desire to understand

[034-281] the concrete, to enjoy art and rewrite its actual history, to plunge

[034-282] into practical and political life; so that by turns he was professor,

[034-283] conspirator, journalist and statesman. "My mind inclines to the

[034-284] concrete," he was wont to say. He philosophized just so much as was

[034-285] necessary to the acquisition of a point of view in problems of art,

[034-286] history and life; and, having procured light for his intellect, found

[034-287] his bearings, derived some satisfaction from the consciousness of his

[034-288] own activity, he plunged as quickly as possible into the particular and

[034-289] the determinate. To immense power of seizing the truth in the highest

[034-290] general principles was joined a no less intense abhorrence for the

[034-291] pale region of ideas in which the philosopher takes an almost ascetic

[034-292] delight. As critic and historian of literature he is unrivalled. Those

[034-293] who have compared him with [[g-e-lessing|Lessing]], Macaulay, Sainte-Beuve or Taine are

[034-294] making rhetorical comparisons.

[034-295] Gustave Flaubert wrote to George Sand: "In your last letter you speak

[034-296] of criticism, and say you expect it soon to disappear. I think, on the

[034-297] contrary, that it is just appearing over the horizon. Criticism to-day

[034-298] is the exact opposite of what it was, but that is all. In the days of

[034-299] Laharpe the critic was a grammarian; to-day he is a historian like

[034-300] Sainte-Beuve and Taine. When will he be an artist, a mere artist, but a

[034-301] real artist? Do you know a critic who interests himself whole-heartedly

[034-302] in the work itself? They analyse with the greatest delicacy the

[034-303] historical surroundings of the work and the causes which produced

[034-304] it: but the underlying poetry and its causes? the composition? the

[034-305] style? the author's own point of view? Never. Such a critic must have

[034-306] great imagination and a great goodness of heart; I mean an ever-ready

[034-307] faculty of enthusiasm; and then, taste; but this last is so rare, even

[034-308] among the best, that it is never mentioned nowadays."[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_26_611) Flaubert's

[034-309] ideal has been worthily reached by one critic only (that is to say,

[034-310] amongst critics who have given themselves to the interpretation of

[034-311] great writers and entire periods of literature) and that one is De

[034-312] Sanctis.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-22.htm.html#Footnote_27_612) No literature of any country possesses so perfect a mirror

[034-313] as that possessed by Italy in the *History* and the other critical

[034-314] essays of Francesco de Sanctis.

[034-315] *De Sanctis as philosopher.*

[034-316] But the philosopher of art, the æsthetician in De Sanctis is less

[034-317] great than the critic and historian of literature. The critic is

[034-318] primary, the philosopher a mere accessory. The æsthetic observations

[034-319] scattered in aphorisms up and down his essays and monographs take

[034-320] various colours from various occasions, and are expressed in uncertain

[034-321] and often metaphorical language; this has led to his being accused of

[034-322] contradictions and inexactitudes which had no existence in his inmost

[034-323] thought and whose very appearance vanishes as soon as one takes into

[034-324] account the particular cases with which he was dealing. But form,

[034-325] forms, content, the living, the beautiful, natural beauty, ugliness,

[034-326] fancy, feeling, imagination, the real, the ideal, and all the other

[034-327] terms which he used with varying signification, demand a science both

[034-328] on which to rest and from which to derive. Meditation on these words

[034-329] stirs up doubts and problems on every side and reveals everywhere gaps

[034-330] and discontinuities. Compared with the few philosophical æstheticians,

[034-331] De Sanctis seems wanting in analysis, in order and in system, and

[034-332] vague in his definitions. But these defects are outweighed by the

[034-333] contact he establishes between the reader and real concrete works of

[034-334] art, and by the feeling for truth which never leaves him. He has, too,

[034-335] the attraction possessed by those writers who lead one on to suspect

[034-336] and to divine new treasures in store beyond what they themselves
