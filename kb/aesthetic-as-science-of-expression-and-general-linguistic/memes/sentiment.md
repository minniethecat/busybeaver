# Sentiment

In Croce's system, sentiment occupies an intermediate position between the raw material of practical emotion and the completed form of aesthetic intuition: it is "at the most a confused presentiment of a thought beyond yet to come" — the affective impulse that seeks and finds its clarification in expression (008-103).

## Definition

Sentiment, in eighteenth-century aesthetic theory, designated a mode of judgment intermediate between sensory perception and rational cognition — the faculty by which we apprehend beauty, sublimity, and other aesthetic qualities. Croce's treatment of sentiment is both historical and systematic. Historically, he traces the concept through the British and French theorists of taste — Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume, Burke, Dubos — who elevated sentiment to the status of a distinct mental faculty. Systematically, Croce argues that sentiment is not a separate form of spirit but a transitional state: practical passion on its way to becoming aesthetic intuition. "The sentimental" is never an autonomous category in Croce's scheme; it is always either practical feeling insufficiently transformed, or aesthetic intuition insufficiently recognized as such.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Sentiment straddles the boundary between the practical and the theoretical in Croce's fourfold structure. As a *passion* — love, hatred, desire — sentiment belongs to the economic sphere of individual volition or the ethical sphere of moral feeling. As an *aesthetic quality* — the tragic, the sublime, the sentimental proper — it is the residue of practical emotion within the contemplative form of intuition. Croce's crucial move is to deny sentiment any independent cognitive status: it is not a "sixth sense" or a special faculty but a name for the felt transition from practical to theoretical activity. "Intuition, reflexion and presentiment" — Croce lists these together to show that presentiment (the affective anticipation of form) has no place among the primary forms of spirit (032-030).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **sentiment vs. intuition**: sentiment is confused and practical, intuition is clear and theoretical; sentiment pushes toward action, intuition contemplates. "The tragic, the sentimental, the ingenuous, the sublime" — these are not distinct aesthetic categories for Croce but names for different admixtures of practical sentiment within the aesthetic form (032-334). Second, **sentiment vs. taste**: the eighteenth-century theory of taste treated sentiment as a special cognitive faculty that apprehends beauty directly. Croce rejects the special-faculty model: "is a mixture of fancy, sentiment and reason" — and a mixed faculty is no faculty at all (033-031). Third, **sentiment vs. moral sense**: Shaftesbury and his followers "considered it a sentiment always willing to" the good — but this moralization of sentiment confuses the ethical and aesthetic spheres (022-291). Croce insists that the aesthetic transformation of sentiment is neutral with respect to moral value.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The career of sentiment as an aesthetic concept begins with the British moralists — particularly Shaftesbury, who treated the "moral sense" and the "sense of beauty" as analogous faculties of immediate, non-rational apprehension. Francis Hutcheson systematized this intuition into a full theory of the "internal sense," capable of perceiving "uniformity amidst variety" without the mediation of concepts. In France, the Abbé Dubos argued that "ceux qui sont accoutumés à juger par le sentiment" possess a direct, unmediated access to aesthetic value that reason cannot supply (022-188). The Abbé Trublet summed up the position: "C'est un principe être une expression de sentiment" — aesthetic judgment is, at bottom, the expression of feeling, not the application of rules (022-215). In Germany, the sentimentalist tradition culminated in Jacobi's philosophy of feeling (*Gefühlsphilosophie*), which Croce treats as "the sentimentalism of Jacobi" — a surrender of philosophical rigor to emotional immediacy (026-155).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of sentiment is diagnostic. He does not deny the existence of aesthetic feeling; he denies that feeling can serve as the *foundation* of aesthetic theory. The eighteenth-century theorists, in Croce's view, were right to insist on the immediacy and non-conceptual character of aesthetic experience but wrong to identify this immediacy with a special faculty of sentiment. The correct analysis, Croce argues, recognizes that what the theorists of taste called "sentiment" is simply the felt quality of successful intuition — the pleasure that accompanies the free exercise of the expressive faculty. By relocating sentiment from the faculty of judgment to the quality of experience, Croce dissolves the problem that the eighteenth century could not solve: how a feeling can be at once subjective and universally valid.

## Criticism and Debate

> "A sentiment always willing to" recognize beauty — the formulation of seventeenth-century Italian critic Trevisano (022-291) captures the ambiguity that Croce seeks to resolve: sentiment cannot be both a passive receptivity and an active judgment. Croce's resolution — that sentiment is the affective quality of successful intuition — has been criticized for reducing the rich phenomenology of aesthetic emotion to a single, undifferentiated pleasure in form.

The question remains whether Croce's system can adequately distinguish among the qualitatively distinct emotional responses — terror, pity, laughter, wonder — that characterize different kinds of aesthetic experience. Are these all simply "the pleasure of successful expression"? If so, Croce's theory may be too impoverished to serve as a descriptive aesthetics.
