# Pedagogic Theory of Art

The pedagogic (or moralistic-utilitarian) theory of art is the doctrine that art finds its justification only in so far as it serves the practical end of moral instruction and edification. In Croce's system, it represents one of the fundamental deviations from a proper understanding of art, arising from the confusion of the aesthetic (theoretical) sphere with the practical (ethical) sphere.

## Definition

Croce identifies the pedagogic or moralistic-utilitarian theory as one of two possible solutions (the other being the rigoristic or ascetic) to the problem of reconciling the pleasure that art affords with the demands of morality. The rigoristic solution condemns art outright as a harmful inebriation of the senses; the pedagogic solution, by contrast, admits art "only in so far as it co-operates with the end of morality; in so far as it assists with innocent pleasure the work of him who points the way to the true and the good" (012-110). This makes art a subordinate instrument — the honey on the rim of the cup of wisdom and morality.

Croce further observes that it is a mistake to subdivide this view into intellectualistic and moralistic-utilitarian branches, arguing that "the educational task which is imposed upon it, precisely because it is an end which is sought after and advised, is no longer merely a theoretical fact, but a theoretical fact already become the ground for practical action; it is not, therefore, intellectualism, but pedagogism and practicism" (012-123). The pedagogic theory is a single, unified error: the confusion of the aesthetic with the practical.

He also classifies it as one of the forms of deviation from true aesthetic theory, specifically the "hedonistic-moralistic" or "pedagogic" deviation, which "consents to a compromise, and while still considering art to be a fact of sense, declares that it need not be harmful, indeed that it may render some service to morality, provided always that it is submissive and obedient" (020-034).

## In Croce's System

In Croce's philosophy of the spirit, human activity is divided into four distinct spheres: the theoretical (intuition and concept) and the practical (economic and ethical). Art belongs exclusively to the first theoretical sphere, as intuition-expression. The pedagogic theory violates this fundamental architecture by demanding that art — a theoretical activity — be judged by practical (ethical) criteria. This is what Croce calls a confusion of the spheres: art is made to serve an external end instead of being recognized as autonomous.

The theory is intimately linked with hedonism, because both share the premise that art is a matter of mere sensuous pleasure. The difference is that hedonism accepts this as sufficient, while the moralistic-pedagogic view attempts to redeem art by subordinating that pleasure to a moral purpose. As Croce explains, "the moralistic and pedagogic theory was constructed upon a hedonistic basis. The artist, who, for the pure hedonist, was comparable to a *hetaira*, became for the moralist a *pedagogue*" (020-169). This grafting of moral purpose onto hedonistic premises is the core of the pedagogic fallacy.

Furthermore, Croce identifies that the pedagogic theory is one of the reasons for the erroneous claim that the content of art must be *chosen* with a view to certain practical effects (012-130). For Croce, content in art is not selected for its moral utility; it is consubstantial with the expressive form that the artist intuits.

## Historical Context

Croce traces the pedagogic theory through the entire history of aesthetic thought. In Græco-Roman antiquity, the idea was already present. He cites Aristophanes' celebrated verse: "What schoolmasters are to children, poets are to young men" (020-177). Traces appear in Plato's more moderate dialogues and in Aristotle's *Politics* on music education, and perhaps even in the *Poetics* with the concept of tragic catharsis, though Croce allows that Aristotle may have glimpsed "the modern idea of the liberating power of art" (020-169). The Stoics, especially Strabo, developed the theory at length, maintaining that poetry was "a first philosophy which educated young men for life."

In the Middle Ages, the "narcotic theory of pedagogic or moralistic art prevailed over every other" (021-041). It suited the moral and religious temper of the age, providing a justification both for the new art of Christian inspiration and for the surviving works of classical pagan art, which were salvaged through allegorical interpretation (021-049). Figures like Fulgentius, John of Salisbury, and Dante all partook of this tradition, treating poetry as a vehicle for moral and religious truth concealed beneath a veil of fable.

The Renaissance did not escape the pedagogic framework. Despite the renewed acquaintance with Aristotle, "the pedagogic theory of art not only persisted and triumphed, but was transplanted bodily into the text of Aristotle" (021-194, 021-198). Commentators such as Scaliger, Segni, Maggi, Vettori, and Piccolomini all affirmed the *docere delectando* (to teach with delight) as the end of poetry. Scaliger declared that imitation was "finis medius ad illum ultimum qui est docendi cum delectatione." This conception was then exported to France, Spain, England, and Germany, permeating the literary doctrines of the seventeenth century. La Menardiere and Le Bossu in France continued the tradition, the latter writing that "le premier but du poete est d'instruire."

Even in later thinkers such as Vico and Meier, traces of the pedagogic theory persist. Croce notes that when Vico asserts that "the chief aim of poetry" is to "teach the ignorant vulgar to act virtuously," this "cannot be taken as a return to the pedagogic and heteronomous theory," but must be understood in the context of Vico's historical hypothesis of a wholly poetical epoch. Meier, a disciple of Baumgarten, likewise lapses into the "old mechanical, ornamental, pedagogic theory of poetry."

## Criticism and Debate

> "But to enunciate these theories at the point to which we have attained is to confute them. We prefer to restrict ourselves to observing that in the pedagogic theory of art is to be found another of the reasons why the claim has erroneously been made that the content of art should be *chosen* with a view to certain practical effects." (012-130)

Croce's fundamental criticism is that the pedagogic theory misunderstands the nature of art itself. Art is intuition-expression, autonomous and self-sufficient; it belongs to the theoretical sphere and has no need of justification by practical results. To demand that art serve a moral end is to treat it as a means rather than an end, which is to destroy its very essence. The theory of "pure beauty" — art free from all that is not the spiritual form of expression — has often been invoked against the pedagogic and hedonistic aesthetic (012-135). Croce aligns with this position, though he rejects any mystical or transcendent interpretation of "pure beauty" that would sever it from expression.

The pedagogic theory thus stands alongside hedonism and rigorism as one of the three great errors in aesthetic philosophy — a confusion between the theoretical activity of art and the practical activity of morality.
