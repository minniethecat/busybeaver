[002-001] *Intuitive knowledge.*

[002-002] Knowledge has two forms: it is either *intuitive* knowledge or

[002-003] *logical* knowledge; knowledge obtained through the *imagination*

[002-004] or knowledge obtained through the *intellect*; knowledge of the

[002-005] *individual* or knowledge of the *universal*; of *individual things* or

[002-006] of the *relations* between them: it is, in fact, productive either of

[002-007] *images* or of *concepts.*

[002-008] In ordinary life, constant appeal is made to [[intuition|intuitive knowledge]]. It is

[002-009] said that we cannot give definitions of certain truths; that they are

[002-010] not demonstrable by syllogisms; that they must be learnt intuitively.

[002-011] The politician finds fault with the abstract reasoner, who possesses no

[002-012] lively intuition of actual conditions; the educational theorist insists

[002-013] upon the necessity of developing the intuitive faculty in the pupil

[002-014] before everything else; the critic in judging a [[art|work of art]] makes it

[002-015] a point of honour to set aside theory and abstractions, and to judge

[002-016] it by direct intuition; the practical man professes to live rather by

[002-017] intuition than by reason.

[002-018] But this ample acknowledgment granted to intuitive knowledge

[002-019] in ordinary life, does not correspond to an equal and adequate

[002-020] acknowledgment in the field of theory and of philosophy. There exists a

[002-021] very ancient science of intellectual knowledge, admitted by all without

[002-022] discussion, namely, Logic; but a science of intuitive knowledge is

[002-023] timidly and with difficulty asserted by but a few. Logical knowledge

[002-024] has appropriated the lion's share; and if she does not slay and devour

[002-025] her companion outright, yet yields to her but grudgingly the humble

[002-026] place of maid-servant or doorkeeper.—What can intuitive knowledge be

[002-027] without the light of intellectual knowledge? It is a servant without

[002-028] a master; and though a master find a servant useful, the master is a

[002-029] necessity to the servant, since he enables him to gain his livelihood.

[002-030] Intuition is blind; intellect lends her eyes.

[002-031] *Its independence with respect to intellectual knowledge.*

[002-032] Now, the first point to be firmly fixed in the mind is that intuitive

[002-033] knowledge has no need of a master, nor to lean upon any one; she does

[002-034] not need to borrow the eyes of others, for she has excellent eyes

[002-035] of her own. Doubtless it is possible to find concepts mingled with

[002-036] intuitions. But in many other intuitions there is no trace of such

[002-037] a mixture, which proves that it is not necessary. The impression of

[002-038] a moonlight scene by a painter; the outline of a country drawn by a

[002-039] cartographer; a musical motive, tender or energetic; the words of

[002-040] a sighing lyric, or those with which we ask, command and lament in

[002-041] ordinary life, may well all be intuitive facts without a shadow of

[002-042] intellectual relation. But, think what one may of these instances,

[002-043] and admitting further the contention that the greater part of the

[002-044] intuitions of civilized man are impregnated with concepts, there yet

[002-045] remains to be observed something more important and more conclusive.

[002-046] Those concepts which are found mingled and fused with the intuitions

[002-047] are no longer concepts, in so far as they are really mingled and

[002-048] fused, for they have lost all independence and autonomy. They have

[002-049] been concepts, but have now become simple elements of intuition.

[002-050] The philosophical maxims placed in the mouth of a personage of

[002-051] tragedy or of comedy, perform there the function, not of concepts,

[002-052] but of characteristics of such personage; in the same way as the

[002-053] red in a painted face does not there represent the red colour of

[002-054] the physicists, but is a characteristic element of the portrait.

[002-055] The whole is that which determines the quality of the parts. A work

[002-056] of art may be full of philosophical concepts; it may contain them

[002-057] in greater abundance and they may there be even more profound than

[002-058] in a philosophical dissertation, which in its turn may be rich to

[002-059] overflowing with descriptions and intuitions. But notwithstanding all

[002-060] these concepts the total effect of the work of art is an intuition;

[002-061] and notwithstanding all those intuitions, the total effect of the

[002-062] philosophical dissertation is a concept. The *Promessi Sposi* contains

[002-063] copious ethical observations and distinctions, but does not for that

[002-064] reason lose as a whole its character of simple story or intuition. In

[002-065] like manner the anecdotes and satirical effusions to be found in the

[002-066] works of a philosopher like [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] do not deprive those works

[002-067] of their character of intellectual treatises. The difference between

[002-068] a scientific work and a work of art, that is, between an intellectual

[002-069] fact and an intuitive fact, lies in the difference of the total effect

[002-070] aimed at by their respective authors. This it is that determines and

[002-071] rules over the several parts of each not these parts separated and

[002-072] considered abstractly in themselves.

[002-073] *Intuition and perception.*

[002-074] But to admit the independence of intuition as regards concept does

[002-075] not suffice to give a true and precise idea of intuition. Another

[002-076] error arises among those who recognize this, or who at any rate do not

[002-077] explicitly make intuition dependent upon the intellect, to obscure

[002-078] and confuse the real nature of intuition. By intuition is frequently

[002-079] understood *perception,* or the knowledge of actual reality, the

[002-080] apprehension of something as *real.*

[002-081] Certainly perception is intuition: the perceptions of the room in

[002-082] which I am writing, of the ink-bottle and paper that are before me,

[002-083] of the pen I am using, of the objects that I touch and make use of as

[002-084] instruments of my person, which, if it write, therefore exists;—these

[002-085] are all intuitions. But the [[representation|image]] that is now passing through my brain

[002-086] of a me writing in another room, in another town, with different paper,

[002-087] pen and ink, is also an intuition. This means that the distinction

[002-088] between reality and non-reality is extraneous, secondary, to the true

[002-089] nature of intuition. If we imagine a human mind having intuitions

[002-090] for the first time, it would seem that it could have intuitions of

[002-091] actual reality only, that is to say, that it could have perceptions

[002-092] of nothing but the real. But since knowledge of reality is based upon

[002-093] the distinction between real images and unreal images, and since this

[002-094] distinction does not at the first moment exist, these intuitions

[002-095] would in truth not be intuitions either of the real or of the unreal,

[002-096] not perceptions, but pure intuitions. Where all is real, nothing is

[002-097] real. The child, with its difficulty of distinguishing true from

[002-098] false, history from fable, which are all one to childhood, can furnish

[002-099] us with a sort of very vague and only remotely approximate idea of

[002-100] this ingenuous state. Intuition is the undifferentiated unity of the

[002-101] perception of the real and of the simple image of the possible. In our

[002-102] intuitions we do not oppose ourselves as empirical beings to external

[002-103] reality, but we simply objectify our impressions, whatever they be.

[002-104] *Intuition and the concepts of space and time.*

[002-105] Those, therefore, who look upon intuition as sensation formed and

[002-106] arranged simply according to the categories of space and time, would

[002-107] seem to approximate more nearly to the truth. Space and time (they

[002-108] say) are the forms of intuition; to have an intuition is to place

[002-109] it in space and in temporal sequence. Intuitive activity would then

[002-110] consist in this double and concurrent function of spatiality and

[002-111] temporality. But for these two categories must be repeated what was

[002-112] said of intellectual distinctions, when found mingled with intuitions.

[002-113] We have intuitions without space and without time: the colour of a

[002-114] sky, the colour of a feeling, a cry of pain and an effort of will,

[002-115] objectified in consciousness: these are intuitions which we possess,

[002-116] and with their making space and time have nothing to do. In some

[002-117] intuitions, spatiality may be found without temporality, in others,

[002-118] *vice versa*; and even where both are found, they are perceived by

[002-119] later reflexion: they can be fused with the intuition in like manner

[002-120] with all its other elements: that is, they are in it *materialiter*

[002-121] and not *formaliter,* as ingredients and not as arrangement. Who,

[002-122] without an act of reflexion which for a moment breaks in upon his

[002-123] contemplation, can think of space while looking at a drawing or a

[002-124] view? Who is conscious of temporal sequence while listening to a story

[002-125] or a piece of music without breaking into it with a similar act of

[002-126] reflexion? What intuition reveals in a work of art is not space and

[002-127] time, but *character, individual physiognomy.* The view here maintained

[002-128] is confirmed in several quarters of modern philosophy. Space and time,

[002-129] far from being simple and primitive functions, are nowadays conceived

[002-130] as intellectual constructions of great complexity. And further, even

[002-131] in some of those who do not altogether deny to space and time the

[002-132] quality of formative principles, categories and functions, one observes

[002-133] an effort to unite them and to regard them in a different manner from

[002-134] that in which these categories are generally conceived. Some limit

[002-135] intuition to the sole category of spatiality, maintaining that even

[002-136] time can only be intuited in terms of space. Others abandon the three

[002-137] dimensions of space as not philosophically necessary, and conceive the

[002-138] function of spatiality as void of all particular spatial determination.

[002-139] But what could such a spatial function be, a simple arrangement that

[002-140] should arrange even time? It represents, surely, all that criticism

[002-141] and refutation have left standing—the bare demand for the affirmation

[002-142] of some intuitive activity in general. And is not this activity

[002-143] truly determined, when one single function is attributed to it, not

[002-144] spatializing nor temporalizing, but characterizing? Or rather, when it

[002-145] is conceived as itself a category or function which gives us knowledge

[002-146] of things in their concreteness and individuality?

[002-147] *Intuition and sensation.*

[002-148] Having thus freed intuitive knowledge from any suggestion of

[002-149] intellectualism and from every later and external addition, we must

[002-150] now explain it and determine its limits from another side and defend

[002-151] it from a different kind of invasion and confusion. On the hither side

[002-152] of the lower limit is sensation, formless matter, which the spirit can

[002-153] never apprehend in itself as simple matter. This it can only possess

[002-154] with form and in form, but postulates the notion of it as a mere limit.

[002-155] Matter, in its abstraction, is mechanism, passivity; it is what the

[002-156] spirit of man suffers, but does not produce. Without it no human

[002-157] knowledge or activity is possible; but mere matter produces animality,

[002-158] whatever is brutal and impulsive in man, not the spiritual dominion,

[002-159] which is humanity. How often we strive to understand clearly what is

[002-160] passing within us! We do catch a glimpse of something, but this does

[002-161] not appear to the mind as objectified and formed. It is in such moments

[002-162] as these that we best perceive the profound difference between matter

[002-163] and form. These are not two acts of ours, opposed to one another; but

[002-164] the one is outside us and assaults and sweeps us off our feet, while

[002-165] the other inside us tends to absorb and identify itself with that

[002-166] which is outside. Matter, clothed and conquered by form, produces

[002-167] concrete form. It is the matter, the content, which differentiates one

[002-168] of our intuitions from another: the form is constant: it is spiritual

[002-169] activity, while matter is changeable. Without matter spiritual

[002-170] activity would not forsake its abstractness to become concrete and

[002-171] real activity, this or that spiritual content, this or that definite

[002-172] intuition.

[002-173] It is a curious fact, characteristic of our times, that this very form,

[002-174] this very activity of the spirit, which is essentially ourselves, is

[002-175] so often ignored or denied. Some confound the spiritual activity of

[002-176] man with the metaphorical and mythological activity of what is called

[002-177] nature, which is mechanism and has no resemblance to human activity,

[002-178] save when we imagine, with Æsop, that "*arbores loquuntur non tantum

[002-179] ferae.*" Some affirm that they have never observed in themselves this

[002-180] "miraculous" activity, as though there were no difference, or only

[002-181] one of quantity, between sweating and thinking, feeling cold and the

[002-182] energy of the will. Others, certainly with greater reason, would

[002-183] unify activity and mechanism in a more general concept, though they

[002-184] are specifically distinct. Let us, however, refrain for the moment

[002-185] from examining if such a final unification be possible, and in what

[002-186] sense, but admitting that the attempt may be made, it is clear that

[002-187] to unify two concepts in a third implies to begin with the admission

[002-188] of a difference between the two first. Here it is this difference that

[002-189] concerns us and we set it in relief.

[002-190] *Intuition and association.*

[002-191] Intuition has sometimes been confused with simple sensation. But since

[002-192] this confusion ends by being offensive to common sense, it has more

[002-193] frequently been attenuated or concealed with a phraseology apparently

[002-194] designed at once to confuse and to distinguish them. Thus, it has

[002-195] been asserted that intuition is sensation, but not so much simple

[002-196] sensation as *association* of sensations. Here a double meaning is

[002-197] concealed in the word "association." Association is understood, either

[002-198] as memory, mnemonic association, conscious recollection, and in that

[002-199] case the claim to unite in memory elements which are not intuited,

[002-200] distinguished, possessed in some way by the spirit and produced by

[002-201] consciousness, seems inconceivable: or it is understood as association

[002-202] of unconscious elements, in which case we remain in the world of

[002-203] sensation and of nature. But if with certain associationists we speak

[002-204] of an association which is neither memory nor flux of sensations, but

[002-205] a *productive* association (formative, constructive, distinguishing);

[002-206] then our contention is admitted and only its name is denied to it.

[002-207] For productive association is no longer association in the sense

[002-208] of the sensationalists, but *synthesis,* that is to say, spiritual

[002-209] activity. Synthesis may be called association; but with the concept of

[002-210] productivity is already posited the distinction between passivity and

[002-211] activity, between sensation and intuition.

[002-212] *Intuition and representation.*

[002-213] Other psychologists are disposed to distinguish from sensation

[002-214] something which is sensation no longer, but is not yet intellectual

[002-215] concept: the *representation* or *image.* What is the difference

[002-216] between their representation or image and our intuitive knowledge?

[002-217] Everything and nothing: for "representation" is a very equivocal word.

[002-218] If by representation be understood something cut off and standing

[002-219] out from the psychic basis of the sensations, then representation is

[002-220] intuition. If, on the other hand, it be conceived as complex sensation

[002-221] we are back once more in crude sensation, which does not vary in

[002-222] quality according to its richness or poverty, or according to whether

[002-223] the organism in which it appears is rudimentary or highly developed

[002-224] and full of traces of past sensations. Nor is the ambiguity remedied

[002-225] by defining representation as a psychic product of secondary degree

[002-226] in relation to sensation, defined as occupying the first place. What

[002-227] does secondary degree mean here? Does it mean a qualitative, formal

[002-228] difference? If so, representation is an elaboration of sensation

[002-229] and therefore intuition. Or does it mean greater complexity and

[002-230] complication, a quantitative, material difference? In that case

[002-231] intuition is once more confused with simple sensation.

[002-232] *Intuition and expression.*

[002-233] And yet there is a sure method of distinguishing true intuition, true

[002-234] representation, from that which is inferior to it: the spiritual fact

[002-235] from the mechanical, passive, natural fact. Every true intuition or

[002-236] representation is also *expression.* That which does not objectify

[002-237] itself in expression is not intuition or representation, but sensation

[002-238] and mere natural fact. The spirit only intuites in making, forming,

[002-239] expressing. He who separates intuition from expression never succeeds

[002-240] in reuniting them.

[002-241] Intuitive activity *possesses intuitions to the extent that it

[002-242] expresses them.* Should this proposition sound paradoxical, that is

[002-243] partly because, as a general rule, a too restricted meaning is given to

[002-244] the word "expression." It is generally restricted to what are called

[002-245] verbal expressions alone. But there exist also non-verbal expressions,

[002-246] such as those of line, colour and sound, and to all of these must

[002-247] be extended our affirmation, which embraces therefore every sort of

[002-248] manifestation of the man, as orator, musician, painter, or anything

[002-249] else. But be it pictorial, or verbal, or musical, or in whatever other

[002-250] form it appear, to no intuition can expression in one of its forms be

[002-251] wanting; it is, in fact, an inseparable part of intuition. How can we

[002-252] really possess an intuition of a geometrical figure, unless we possess

[002-253] so accurate an image of it as to be able to trace it immediately upon

[002-254] paper or on the blackboard?

[002-255] How can we really have an intuition of the contour of a region, for

[002-256] example of the island of Sicily, if we are not able to draw it as

[002-257] it is in all its meanderings? Every one can experience the internal

[002-258] illumination which follows upon his success in formulating to

[002-259] himself his impressions and feelings, but only so far as he is able

[002-260] to formulate them. Feelings or impressions, then, pass by means of

[002-261] words from the obscure region of the soul into the clarity of the

[002-262] contemplative spirit. It is impossible to distinguish intuition from

[002-263] expression in this cognitive process. The one appears with the other at

[002-264] the same instant, because they are not two, but one.

[002-265] *Illusion as to their difference.*

[002-266] The principal reason which makes our view appear paradoxical as we

[002-267] maintain it, is the illusion or prejudice that we possess a more

[002-268] complete intuition of reality than we really do. One often hears people

[002-269] say that they have many great thoughts in their minds, but that they

[002-270] are not able to express them. But if they really had them, they would

[002-271] have coined them into just so many beautiful, sounding words, and thus

[002-272] have expressed them. If these thoughts seem to vanish or to become few

[002-273] and meagre in the act of expressing them, the reason is that they did

[002-274] not exist or really were few and meagre. People think that all of us

[002-275] ordinary men imagine and intuite countries, figures and scenes like

[002-276] painters, and bodies like sculptors; save that painters and sculptors

[002-277] know how to paint and carve such images, while we bear them unexpressed

[002-278] in our souls. They believe that any one could have imagined a Madonna

[002-279] of Raphæl; but that Raphæl was Raphæl owing to his technical ability

[002-280] in putting the Madonna upon canvas. Nothing can be more false than

[002-281] this view. The world which as a rule we intuite is a small thing. It

[002-282] consists of little expressions, which gradually become greater and

[002-283] wider with the increasing spiritual concentration of certain moments.

[002-284] They are the words we say to ourselves, our silent judgments: "Here

[002-285] is a man, here is a horse, this is heavy, this is sharp, this pleases

[002-286] me," etc. It is a medley of light and colour, with no greater pictorial

[002-287] value than would be expressed by a haphazard splash of colours, from

[002-288] among which one could barely make out a few special, distinctive

[002-289] traits. This and nothing else is what we possess in our ordinary life;

[002-290] this is the basis of our ordinary action. It is the index of a book.

[002-291] The labels tied to things (it has been said) take the place of the

[002-292] things themselves. This index and these labels (themselves expressions)

[002-293] suffice for small needs and small actions. From time to time we pass

[002-294] from the index to the book, from the label to the thing, or from the

[002-295] slight to the greater intuitions, and from these to the greatest and

[002-296] most lofty. This passage is sometimes far from easy. It has been

[002-297] observed by those who have best studied the psychology of artists that

[002-298] when, after having given a rapid glance at any one, they attempt to

[002-299] obtain a real intuition of him, in order, for example, to paint his

[002-300] portrait, then this ordinary vision, that seemed so precise, so lively,

[002-301] reveals itself as little better than nothing. What remains is found to

[002-302] be at the most some superficial trait, which would not even suffice for

[002-303] a caricature. The person to be painted stands before the artist like a

[002-304] world to discover. Michæl Angelo said, "One paints, not with the hands,

[002-305] but with the brain." Leonardo shocked the prior of the Convent of the

[002-306] Graces by standing for days together gazing at the "Last Supper,"

[002-307] without touching it with the brush. He remarked of this attitude: "The

[002-308] minds of men of lofty genius are most active in invention when they are

[002-309] doing the least external work." The painter is a painter, because he

[002-310] sees what others only feel or catch a glimpse of, but do not see. We

[002-311] think we see a smile, but in reality we have only a vague impression

[002-312] of it, we do not perceive all the characteristic traits of which it

[002-313] is the sum, as the painter discovers them after he has worked upon

[002-314] them and is thus able to fix them on the canvas. We do not intuitively

[002-315] possess more even of our intimate friend, who is with us every day

[002-316] and at all hours, than at most certain traits of physiognomy which

[002-317] enable us to distinguish him from others. The illusion is less easy as

[002-318] regards musical expression; because it would seem strange to every

[002-319] one to say that the composer had added or attached notes to a motive

[002-320] which was already in the mind of him who is not the composer; as if

[002-321] Beethoven's Ninth Symphony were not his own intuition and his intuition

[002-322] the Ninth Symphony. Now, just as one who is deluded as to the amount

[002-323] of his material wealth is confuted by arithmetic, which states its

[002-324] exact amount, so he who nourishes delusions as to the wealth of his

[002-325] own thoughts and images is brought back to reality, when he is obliged

[002-326] to cross the *Pons Asinorum* of expression. Let us say to the former,

[002-327] count; to the latter, speak; or, here is a pencil, draw, express

[002-328] yourself.

[002-329] Each of us, as a matter of fact, has in him a little of the poet, of

[002-330] the sculptor, of the musician, of the painter, of the prose writer:

[002-331] but how little, as compared with those who bear those names, just

[002-332] because they possess the most universal dispositions and energies

[002-333] of human nature in so lofty a degree! How little too does a painter

[002-334] possess of the intuitions of a poet! And how little does one painter

[002-335] possess those of another painter! Nevertheless, that little is all

[002-336] our actual patrimony of intuitions or representations. Beyond these

[002-337] are only impressions, sensations, feelings, impulses, emotions, or

[002-338] whatever else one may term what still falls short of the spirit and is

[002-339] not assimilated by man; something postulated for the convenience of

[002-340] exposition, while actually non-existent, since to exist also is a fact

[002-341] of the spirit.

[002-342] *Identity of intuition and expression.*

[002-343] We may thus add this to the various verbal descriptions of intuition,

[002-344] noted at the beginning: intuitive knowledge is [[expression|expressive knowledge]].

[002-345] Independent and autonomous in respect to intellectual function;

[002-346] indifferent to later empirical discriminations, to reality and to

[002-347] unreality, to formations and apperceptions of space and time, which are

[002-348] also later: intuition or representation is distinguished as *form* from

[002-349] what is felt and suffered, from the flux or wave of sensation, or from

[002-350] psychic matter; and this form, this taking possession, is expression.

[002-351] To intuite is to express; and nothing else (nothing more, but nothing

[002-352] less) than *to express.*
