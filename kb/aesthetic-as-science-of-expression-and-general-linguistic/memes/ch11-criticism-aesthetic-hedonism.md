[011-001] *Various significations of the word feeling.*

[011-002] Passing to the study of more complex concepts, where the æsthetic

[011-003] activity is to be considered in conjunction with other orders of facts,

[011-004] and showing the mode of their union or complication, we find ourselves

[011-005] first face to face with the concept of *feeling* and with those

[011-006] feelings that are called *æsthetic.*

[011-007] The word "feeling" is one of the richest in meanings in philosophic

[011-008] terminology. We have already had occasion to meet with it once, among

[011-009] those used to designate the spirit in its passivity, the matter or

[011-010] content of art, and so as synonym of *impressions.* Once again (and

[011-011] then the meaning was altogether different), we have met with it as

[011-012] designating the *non-logical* and *non-historical* character of the

[011-013] æsthetic fact, that is to say, pure intuition, a form of truth which

[011-014] defines no concept and affirms no fact.

[011-015] *Feeling as activity.*

[011-016] But here it is not regarded in either of these two meanings, nor in

[011-017] the others which have also been conferred upon it to designate other

[011-018] *cognitive* forms of the spirit, but only in that where feeling is

[011-019] understood as a special activity, of non-cognitive nature, having its

[011-020] two poles, positive and negative, in *pleasure* and *pain.*

[011-021] This activity has always greatly embarrassed philosophers, who have

[011-022] therefore attempted either to deny it as activity, or to attribute it

[011-023] to *nature,* excluding it from the spirit. But both these solutions

[011-024] bristle with difficulties of such a kind as to prove them finally

[011-025] unacceptable to any one who examines them with care. For what could

[011-026] a non-spiritual activity ever be, an *activity of nature,* when we

[011-027] have no other knowledge of activity save as spirituality, nor of

[011-028] spirituality save as activity? Nature is in this case, by definition,

[011-029] the merely passive, inert, mechanical, material. On the other hand,

[011-030] the negation of the character of activity to feeling is energetically

[011-031] disproved by those very poles of pleasure and of pain which appear in

[011-032] it and manifest activity in its concreteness, or, so to say, quivering.

[011-033] *Identification of feeling with economic activity.*

[011-034] This critical conclusion should place us especially in the greatest

[011-035] embarrassment, for in the sketch of the system of the spirit given

[011-036] above we have left no room for the new activity of which we are now

[011-037] obliged to recognize the existence. But the activity of feeling, if

[011-038] it is activity, is not new. It has already had its place assigned to

[011-039] it in the system that we have sketched, where, however, it has been

[011-040] given another name, *economic* activity. What is called the activity of

[011-041] feeling is nothing but that more elementary and fundamental practical

[011-042] activity which we have distinguished from the ethical activity and made

[011-043] to consist of the appetition and volition for some individual end,

[011-044] apart from any moral determination.

[011-045] If feeling has been sometimes considered to be an organic or natural

[011-046] activity, this has happened just because it does not coincide either

[011-047] with logical, æsthetic or ethical activity. Looked at from the

[011-048] standpoint of those three (which were the only ones admitted), it

[011-049] has seemed to lie *outside* the true and real spirit, spirit in its

[011-050] aristocracy, and to be almost a determination of nature, or of the

[011-051] soul in so far as it is nature. From this too results the truth of

[011-052] another thesis, often maintained, that the æsthetic activity, like the

[011-053] ethical and intellectual activities, is not feeling. This thesis is

[011-054] inexpugnable, when feeling has already been understood implicitly and

[011-055] unconsciously as economic volition.

[011-056] *Criticism of hedonism.*

[011-057] The view refuted in this thesis is known as *hedonism.* This consists

[011-058] in reducing all the various forms of the spirit to one, which thus also

[011-059] loses its own distinctive character and becomes something obscure

[011-060] and mysterious, like "the night in which all cows are black." Having

[011-061] brought about this reduction and mutilation, the hedonists naturally do

[011-062] not succeed in seeing anything else in any activity but pleasure and

[011-063] pain. They find no substantial difference between the pleasure of art

[011-064] and that of easy digestion, between the pleasure of a good action and

[011-065] that of breathing the fresh air with wide-expanded lungs.

[011-066] *Feeling as a concomitant of every form of activity.*

[011-067] But if the activity of feeling in the sense here defined must not be

[011-068] substituted for all the other forms of spiritual activity, we have not

[011-069] said that it cannot *accompany* them. Indeed it accompanies them of

[011-070] necessity, because they are all in close relation both with one another

[011-071] and with the elementary volitional form. Therefore each of them has for

[011-072] concomitants individual volitions and volitional pleasures and pains,

[011-073] known as feeling. But we must not confound a concomitant with the

[011-074] principal fact, and substitute the one for the other. The discovery of

[011-075] a truth, or the fulfilment of a moral duty, produces in us a joy which

[011-076] makes vibrate our whole being, which, by attaining the aim of those

[011-077] forms of spiritual activity, attains at the same time that to which

[011-078] it was *practically* tending, as its end. Nevertheless, *economic*

[011-079] or *hedonistic* satisfaction, *ethical* satisfaction, *æsthetic*

[011-080] satisfaction, *intellectual* satisfaction, though thus united, remain

[011-081] always distinct.

[011-082] A question often asked is thus answered at the same time, one which

[011-083] has correctly seemed to be a matter of life or death for æsthetic

[011-084] science, namely, whether feeling and pleasure precede or follow, are

[011-085] cause or effect of the æsthetic fact. We must widen this question to

[011-086] include the relation between the various spiritual forms, and answer

[011-087] it by maintaining that one cannot talk of cause and effect and of a

[011-088] chronological before and after in the unity of the spirit.

[011-089] And once the relation above expounded is established, all necessity for

[011-090] inquiry as to the nature of æsthetic, moral, intellectual and even what

[011-091] was sometimes called economic feelings, must disappear. In this last

[011-092] case, it is clear that it is a question, not of two terms, but of one,

[011-093] and inquiry as to economic feeling must be the same as that relating to

[011-094] economic activity. But in the other cases also, we must attend, not to

[011-095] the substantive, but to the adjective: the æsthetic, moral and logical

[011-096] character will explain the colouring of the feelings as æsthetic, moral

[011-097] and intellectual, whereas feeling, studied alone, will never explain

[011-098] those refractions and colorations.

[011-099] *Meaning of certain ordinary distinctions of feelings.*

[011-100] A further consequence is, that we no longer need retain the well-known

[011-101] distinctions between values or feelings *of value,* and feelings

[011-102] that are merely hedonistic and *without value*; *disinterested*

[011-103] and *interested* feelings, *objective* feelings and feelings not

[011-104] *objective* but simply *subjective* feelings of *approbation* and of

[011-105] *mere pleasure* (cf. the distinction of *Gefallen* and *Vergnügen*

[011-106] in German). Those distinctions were used to save the three spiritual

[011-107] forms, which were recognized as the triad of the *True,* the *Good*

[011-108] and the *Beautiful,* from confusion with the fourth form, still

[011-109] unknown, and therefore insidious in its indeterminateness and mother

[011-110] of scandals. For us this triad has completed its task, because we are

[011-111] capable of reaching the distinction far more directly, by receiving

[011-112] also the selfish, subjective, merely pleasurable feelings among the

[011-113] respectable forms of the spirit; and where formerly antitheses were

[011-114] conceived (by ourselves and others), between value and feelings, as

[011-115] between spirituality and naturality, henceforth we see nothing but

[011-116] differences between value and value.

[011-117] *Value and disvalue: the contraries and their union.*

[011-118] As has already been said, feeling or the economic activity presents

[011-119] itself as divided into two poles, positive and negative, pleasure

[011-120] and pain, which we can now translate into useful and disuseful (or

[011-121] hurtful). This bipartition has already been noted above, as a mark of

[011-122] the activistic character of feeling, and one which is to be found in

[011-123] all forms of activity. If each of these is *value,* each has opposed

[011-124] to it *antivalue* or *disvalue.* Absence of value is not sufficient to

[011-125] cause dis value, but activity and passivity must be struggling between

[011-126] themselves, without the one getting the better of the other; hence

[011-127] the contradiction and disvalue of the activity that is embarrassed,

[011-128] impeded, or interrupted. Value is activity that unfolds itself freely:

[011-129] disvalue is its contrary.

[011-130] We will content ourselves with this definition of the two terms,

[011-131] without entering into the problem of the relation between value and

[011-132] disvalue, that is, the problem of contraries (that is to say, whether

[011-133] they are to be thought of dualistically, as two beings or two orders

[011-134] of beings, like Ormuzd and Ahriman, angels and devils, enemies to one

[011-135] another; or as a unity, which is also contrariety). This definition

[011-136] of the two terms will be sufficient for our purpose, which is to make

[011-137] clear the nature of æsthetic activity, and at this particular point one

[011-138] of the most obscure and disputed concepts of Æsthetic: the concept of

[011-139] the *Beautiful.*

[011-140] *The Beautiful as the value of expression, or expression

[011-141] without qualification.*

[011-142] Æsthetic, intellectual, economic and ethical values and disvalues

[011-143] are variously denominated in current speech: *beautiful, true, good,

[011-144] useful, expedient, just, right* and so on—thus designating the free

[011-145] development of spiritual activity, action, scientific research,

[011-146] artistic production, when they are successful; *ugly, false, bad,

[011-147] useless, inexpedient, unjust,* wrong designating embarrassed activity,

[011-148] the product that is a failure. In linguistic usage, these denominations

[011-149] are being continually shifted from one order of facts to another.

[011-150] *Beautiful,* for instance, is said not only of a successful expression,

[011-151] but also of a scientific truth, of an action successfully achieved,

[011-152] and of a moral action: thus we talk of an *intellectual beauty,* of a

[011-153] *beautiful action,* of a *moral beauty.* The attempt to keep up with

[011-154] these infinitely varying usages leads into a trackless labyrinth of

[011-155] verbalism in which many philosophers and students of art have lost

[011-156] their way. For this reason we have thought it best studiously to avoid

[011-157] the use of the word "beautiful" to indicate successful expression in

[011-158] its positive value. But after all the explanations that we have given,

[011-159] all danger of misunderstanding being now dissipated, and since on the

[011-160] other hand we cannot fail to recognize that the prevailing tendency,

[011-161] both in current speech and in philosophy, is to limit the meaning of

[011-162] the word "beautiful" precisely to the æsthetic value, it seems now both

[011-163] permissible and advisable to define beauty as *successful expression,*

[011-164] or rather, as *expression* and nothing more, because expression when it

[011-165] is not successful is not expression.

[011-166] *The ugly, and the elements of beauty which compose it.*

[011-167] Consequently, the ugly is unsuccessful expression. The paradox is true,

[011-168] for works of art that are failures, that [[beauty|the beautiful]] presents itself

[011-169] as *unity,* the ugly as *multiplicity.* Hence we hear of *merits* in

[011-170] relation to works of art that are more or less failures, that is to

[011-171] say, of *those parts of them that are beautiful,* which is not the case

[011-172] with perfect works. It is in fact impossible to enumerate the merits or

[011-173] to point out what parts of the latter are beautiful, because being a

[011-174] complete fusion they have but one value. Life circulates in the whole

[011-175] organism: it is not withdrawn into the several parts.

[011-176] *Illusion that there exist expressions neither beautiful nor

[011-177] ugly.*

[011-178] Unsuccessful works may have merit in various degrees, even the

[011-179] greatest. The beautiful does not possess degrees, for there is no

[011-180] conceiving a more beautiful, that is, an expressive that is more

[011-181] expressive, an adequate that is more than adequate. Ugliness, on the

[011-182] other hand, does possess degrees, from the rather ugly (or almost

[011-183] beautiful) to the extremely ugly. But if the ugly were *complete,*

[011-184] that is to say, without any element of beauty, it would for that very

[011-185] reason cease to be ugly, because it would be without the contradiction

[011-186] in which is the reason of its existence. The disvalue would become

[011-187] non-value; activity would give place to passivity, with which it is not

[011-188] at war, save when activity is really present to oppose it.

[011-189] And because the distinctive consciousness of the beautiful and of the

[011-190] ugly is based on the conflicts and contradictions in which æsthetic

[011-191] activity is developed, it is evident that this consciousness becomes

[011-192] attenuated to the point of disappearing altogether, as we descend from

[011-193] the more complicated to the more simple and to the simplest instances

[011-194] of expression. Hence the illusion that there are expressions neither

[011-195] beautiful nor ugly, those which are obtained without sensible effort

[011-196] and appear easy and natural being considered such.

[011-197] *True æsthetic feelings and concomitant or accidental

[011-198] feelings.*

[011-199] The whole mystery of the *beautiful* and the *ugly* is reduced to

[011-200] these henceforth most easy definitions. Should any one object that

[011-201] there exist perfect æsthetic expressions before which no pleasure is

[011-202] felt, and others, perhaps even failures, which give him the greatest

[011-203] pleasure, we must recommend him to concentrate his attention in the

[011-204] æsthetic fact, upon that which is truly æsthetic pleasure. Æsthetic

[011-205] pleasure is sometimes reinforced or rather complicated by pleasures

[011-206] arising from extraneous facts, which are only accidentally found united

[011-207] with it. The poet or any other artist affords an instance of purely

[011-208] æsthetic pleasure at the moment when he sees (or intuites) his work

[011-209] for the first time; that is to say, when his impressions take form and

[011-210] his countenance is irradiated with the divine joy of the creator. On

[011-211] the other hand, a mixed pleasure is experienced by one who goes to the

[011-212] theatre, after a day's work, to witness a comedy: when the pleasure of

[011-213] rest and amusement, or that of laughingly snatching a nail from his

[011-214] coffin, accompanies the moment of true æsthetic pleasure in the art

[011-215] of the dramatist and actors. The same may be said of the artist who

[011-216] looks upon his labour with pleasure when it is finished, experiencing,

[011-217] in addition to the æsthetic pleasure, that very different one which

[011-218] arises from the thought of self-complacency satisfied, or even of the

[011-219] economic gain which will come to him from his work. Instances could be

[011-220] multiplied.

[011-221] *Criticism of apparent feelings.*

[011-222] A category of *apparent* æsthetic feelings has been formed in modern

[011-223] Æsthetic, not arising from the form, that is to say, from the works of

[011-224] art as such, but from their content. It has been remarked that artistic

[011-225] representations arouse pleasure and pain in their infinite shades

[011-226] of variety. We tremble with anxiety, we rejoice, we fear, we laugh,

[011-227] we weep, we desire, with the personages of a drama or of a romance,

[011-228] with the figures in a picture and with the melody of music. But these

[011-229] feelings are not such as would be aroused by the real fact outside

[011-230] art; or rather, they are the same in quality, but are quantitatively

[011-231] an attenuation of real things. Æsthetic and *apparent* pleasure and

[011-232] pain show themselves to be light, shallow, mobile. We have no need to

[011-233] treat here of these *apparent feelings,* for the good reason that we

[011-234] have already amply discussed them; indeed, we have hitherto treated

[011-235] of nothing but them. What are these apparent or manifested feelings,

[011-236] but feelings objectified, intuited, expressed? And it is natural that

[011-237] they do not trouble and afflict us as passionately as those of real

[011-238] life, because those were matter, these are form and activity; those

[011-239] true and proper feelings, these intuitions and expressions. The formula

[011-240] of *apparent feelings* is therefore for us nothing but a tautology,

[011-241] through which we can run the pen without scruple.
