[003-001] *Corollaries and explanations.*

[003-002] Before proceeding further, it may be well to draw certain consequences

[003-003] from what has been established and to add some explanations.

[003-004] *Identity of art and [[intuition|intuitive knowledge]].*

[003-005] We have frankly identified intuitive or [[expression|expressive knowledge]] with the

[003-006] æsthetic or artistic fact, taking works of art as examples of intuitive

[003-007] knowledge and attributing to them the characteristics of intuition, and

[003-008] *vice versa.* But our identification is combated by a view held even by

[003-009] many philosophers, who consider art to be an intuition of an altogether

[003-010] special sort. "Let us admit" (they say) "that art is intuition; but

[003-011] intuition is not always art: artistic intuition is a distinct species

[003-012] differing from intuition in general by something *more*."

[003-013] *No specific difference.*

[003-014] But no one has ever been able to indicate of what this something more

[003-015] consists. It has sometimes been thought that art is not a simple

[003-016] intuition, but an intuition of an intuition, in the same way as the

[003-017] concept of science has been defined, not as the ordinary concept,

[003-018] but as the concept of a concept. Thus man would attain to art by

[003-019] objectifying, not his sensations, as happens with ordinary intuition,

[003-020] but intuition itself. But this process of raising to a second power

[003-021] does not exist; and the comparison of it with the ordinary and

[003-022] scientific concept does not prove what is intended, for the good

[003-023] reason that it is not true that the scientific concept is the concept

[003-024] of a concept. If this comparison proves anything, it proves just the

[003-025] opposite. The ordinary concept, if it be really a concept and not a

[003-026] simple representation, is a perfect concept, however poor and limited.

[003-027] Science substitutes concepts for representations; for those concepts

[003-028] that are poor and limited it substitutes others, larger and more

[003-029] comprehensive; it is ever discovering new relations. But its method

[003-030] does not differ from that by which is formed the smallest universal

[003-031] in the brain of the humblest of men. What is generally called *par

[003-032] excellence* art, collects intuitions that are wider and more complex

[003-033] than those which we generally experience, but these intuitions are

[003-034] always of sensations and impressions.

[003-035] [[art|Art]] is expression of impressions, not expression of expression.

[003-036] *No difference of intensity.*

[003-037] For the same reason, it cannot be asserted that the intuition, which is

[003-038] generally called artistic, differs from ordinary intuition as intensive

[003-039] intuition. This would be the case if it were to operate differently on

[003-040] the same matter. But since the artistic function is extended to wider

[003-041] fields, yet does not differ in method from ordinary intuition, the

[003-042] difference between them is not intensive but extensive. The intuition

[003-043] of the simplest popular love-song, which says the same thing, or very

[003-044] nearly, as any declaration of love that issues at every moment from the

[003-045] lips of thousands of ordinary men, may be intensively perfect in its

[003-046] poor simplicity, although it be extensively so much more limited than

[003-047] the complex intuition of a love-song by Leopardi.

[003-048] *The difference is extensive and empirical.*

[003-049] The whole difference, then, is quantitative, and as such is indifferent

[003-050] to philosophy, *scientia qualitatum.* Certain men have a greater

[003-051] aptitude, a more frequent inclination fully to express certain

[003-052] complex states of the soul. These men are known in ordinary language

[003-053] as artists. Some very complicated and difficult expressions are not

[003-054] often achieved, and these are called works of art. The limits of

[003-055] the expression-intuitions that are called art, as opposed to those

[003-056] that are vulgarly called non-art, are empirical and impossible to

[003-057] define. If an epigram be art, why not a simple word? If a story, why

[003-058] not the news-jottings of the journalist? If a landscape, why not a

[003-059] topographical sketch? The teacher of philosophy in Molière's comedy was

[003-060] right: "whenever we speak, we create prose." But there will always be

[003-061] scholars like Monsieur Jourdain, astonished at having spoken prose for

[003-062] forty years without knowing it, who will have difficulty in persuading

[003-063] themselves that when they call their servant John to bring their

[003-064] slippers, they have spoken nothing less than—prose.

[003-065] We must hold firmly to our identification, because among the principal

[003-066] reasons which have prevented Æsthetic, the science of art, from

[003-067] revealing the true nature of art, its real roots in human nature,

[003-068] has been its separation from the general spiritual life, the having

[003-069] made of it a sort of special function or aristocratic club. No one

[003-070] is astonished when he learns from physiology that every cell is an

[003-071] organism and every organism a cell or synthesis of cells. No one is

[003-072] astonished at finding in a lofty mountain the same chemical elements

[003-073] that compose a small stone fragment. There is not one physiology of

[003-074] small animals and one of large animals; nor is there a special chemical

[003-075] theory of stones as distinct from mountains. In the same way, there is

[003-076] not a science of lesser intuition as distinct from a science of greater

[003-077] intuition, nor one of ordinary intuition as distinct from artistic

[003-078] intuition. There is but one Æsthetic, the science of intuitive or

[003-079] expressive knowledge, which is the æsthetic or artistic fact. And this

[003-080] Æsthetic is the true analogue of Logic, which includes, as facts of the

[003-081] same nature, the formation of the smallest and most ordinary concept

[003-082] and the most complicated scientific and philosophical system.

[003-083] *Artistic genius.*

[003-084] Nor can we admit that the word *genius* or [[genius|artistic genius]], as

[003-085] distinct from the non-genius of the ordinary man, possesses more than

[003-086] a quantitative signification. Great artists are said to reveal us to

[003-087] ourselves. But how could this be possible, unless there were identity

[003-088] of nature between their imagination and ours, and unless the difference

[003-089] were only one of quantity? It were better to change *poeta nascitur*

[003-090] into *homo nascitur poeta*: some men are born great poets, some small.

[003-091] The cult of the genius with all its attendant superstitions has arisen

[003-092] from this quantitative difference having been taken as a difference of

[003-093] quality. It has been forgotten that genius is not something that has

[003-094] fallen from heaven, but humanity itself. The man of genius who poses or

[003-095] is represented as remote from humanity finds his punishment in becoming

[003-096] or appearing somewhat ridiculous. Examples of this are the *genius* of

[003-097] the romantic period and the *superman* of our time.

[003-098] But it is well to note here, that those who claim unconsciousness as

[003-099] the chief quality of an artistic genius, hurl him from an eminence

[003-100] far above humanity to a position far below it. Intuitive or artistic

[003-101] genius, like every form of human activity, is always conscious;

[003-102] otherwise it would be blind mechanism. The only thing that can be

[003-103] wanting to artistic genius is the *reflective* consciousness, the

[003-104] superadded consciousness of the historian or critic, which is not

[003-105] essential to it.

[003-106] *[[content|Content]] and form in Æsthetic.*

[003-107] The relation between matter and form, or between *content* and

[003-108] *form,* as is generally said, is one of the most disputed questions

[003-109] in Æsthetic. Does the æsthetic fact consist of content alone, or of

[003-110] form alone, or of both together? This question has taken on various

[003-111] meanings, which we shall mention, each in its place. But when these

[003-112] words are taken as signifying what we have above defined, and matter is

[003-113] understood as emotionality not æsthetically elaborated, or impressions,

[003-114] and form as intellectual activity and expression, then our view cannot

[003-115] be in doubt. We must, that is to say, reject both the thesis that makes

[003-116] the æsthetic fact to consist of the content alone (that is, the simple

[003-117] impressions), and the thesis which makes it to consist of a junction

[003-118] between form and content, that is, of impressions plus expressions.

[003-119] In the æsthetic fact, expressive activity is not added to the fact of

[003-120] the impressions, but these latter are formed and elaborated by it. The

[003-121] impressions reappear as it were in expression, like water put into a

[003-122] filter, which reappears the same and yet different on the other side.

[003-123] The æsthetic fact, therefore, is form, and nothing but form.

[003-124] From this was inferred not that the content is something superfluous

[003-125] (it is, on the contrary, the necessary point of departure for the

[003-126] expressive fact); but that *there is no passage* from the qualities of

[003-127] the content to those of the form. It has sometimes been thought that

[003-128] the content, in order to be æsthetic, that is to say, transformable

[003-129] into form, should possess some determined or determinable qualities.

[003-130] But were that so, then form and content, expression and impression,

[003-131] would be the same thing. It is true that the content is that which

[003-132] is convertible into form, but it has no determinable qualities until

[003-133] this transformation takes place. We know nothing about it. It does not

[003-134] become æsthetic content before, but only after it has been actually

[003-135] transformed. The æsthetic content has also been defined as the

[003-136] *interesting.* That is not an untrue statement; it is merely void of

[003-137] meaning. Interesting to what? To the expressive activity? Certainly

[003-138] the expressive activity would not have raised the content to the

[003-139] dignity of form, had it not been interested in it. Being interested is

[003-140] precisely the raising of the content to the dignity of form. But the

[003-141] word "interesting" has also been employed in another and a illegitimate

[003-142] sense, which we shall explain further on.

[003-143] *Criticism of the imitation of nature and of the artistic

[003-144] illusion.*

[003-145] The proposition that art is *imitation of nature* has also several

[003-146] meanings. Sometimes truths have been expressed or at least shadowed

[003-147] forth in these words, sometimes errors have been promulgated. More

[003-148] frequently, no definite thought has been expressed at all. One of

[003-149] the scientifically legitimate meanings occurs when "imitation" is

[003-150] understood as representation or intuition of nature, a form of

[003-151] knowledge. And when the phrase is used with this intention, and in

[003-152] order to emphasize the spiritual character of the process, another

[003-153] proposition becomes legitimate also: namely, that art is the

[003-154] *idealization* or *idealizing* imitation of nature. But if by imitation

[003-155] of nature be understood that art gives mechanical reproductions,

[003-156] more or less perfect duplicates of natural objects, in the presence

[003-157] of which is renewed the same tumult of impressions as that caused

[003-158] by natural objects, then the proposition is evidently false. The

[003-159] coloured waxen effigies that imitate the life, before which we stand

[003-160] astonished in the museums where such things are shown, do not give

[003-161] æsthetic intuitions. Illusion and hallucination have nothing to do

[003-162] with the calm domain of artistic intuition. But on the other hand if

[003-163] an artist paint the interior of a wax-work museum, or if an actor

[003-164] give a burlesque portrait of a man-statue on the stage, we have work

[003-165] of the spirit and artistic intuition. Finally, if photography have in

[003-166] it anything artistic, it will be to the extent that it transmits the

[003-167] intuition of the photographer, his point of view, the pose and grouping

[003-168] which he has striven to attain. And if photography be not quite an art,

[003-169] that is precisely because the element of nature in it remains more or

[003-170] less unconquered and ineradicable. Do we ever, indeed, feel complete

[003-171] satisfaction before even the best of photographs? Would not an artist

[003-172] vary and touch up much or little, remove or add something to all of

[003-173] them?

[003-174] *Criticism of art conceived as a fact of feeling, not a

[003-175] theoretical fact. Æsthetic appearance, and feeling.*

[003-176] The statements repeated so often, that art is not knowledge, that

[003-177] it does not tell the truth, that it does not belong to the world of

[003-178] theory, but to the world of feeling, and so forth, arise from the

[003-179] failure to realize exactly the theoretic character of simple intuition.

[003-180] This simple intuition is quite distinct from intellectual knowledge,

[003-181] as it is distinct from perception of the real; and the statements

[003-182] quoted above arise from the belief that only intellectual cognition is

[003-183] knowledge. We have seen that intuition is knowledge, free from concepts

[003-184] and more simple than the so-called perception of the real. Therefore

[003-185] art is knowledge, form; it does not belong to the world of feeling or

[003-186] to psychic matter. The reason why so many æstheticians have so often

[003-187] insisted that art is *appearance* (*Schein*), is precisely that they

[003-188] have felt the necessity of distinguishing it from the more complex fact

[003-189] of perception, by maintaining its pure intuitiveness. And if for the

[003-190] same reason it has been claimed that art is *feeling* the reason is

[003-191] the same. For if the concept as content of art, and historical reality

[003-192] as such, be excluded from the sphere of art, there remains no other

[003-193] content than reality apprehended in all its ingenuousness and immediacy

[003-194] in the vital impulse, in its *feeling,* that is to say again, pure

[003-195] intuition.

[003-196] *Criticism of the theory of æsthetic senses.*

[003-197] The theory of the *æsthetic senses* has also arisen from the failure to

[003-198] establish, or from having lost to view, the character of expression as

[003-199] distinct from impression, of form as distinct from matter.

[003-200] This theory can be reduced to the error just indicated of wishing to

[003-201] find a passage from the qualities of the content to those of the form.

[003-202] To ask, in fact, what the æsthetic senses are, implies asking what

[003-203] sensible impressions are able to enter into æsthetic expressions, and

[003-204] which must of necessity do so. To this we must at once reply, that

[003-205] all impressions can enter into æsthetic expressions or formations,

[003-206] but that none are bound to do so of necessity. Dante raised to the

[003-207] dignity of form not only the "sweet colour of the oriental sapphire"

[003-208] (visual impressions), but also tactual or thermic impressions, such as

[003-209] the "dense air" and the "fresh rivulets" which "parch the more" the

[003-210] throat of the thirsty. The belief that a picture yields only visual

[003-211] impressions is a curious illusion. The bloom on a cheek, the warmth of

[003-212] a youthful body, the sweetness and freshness of a fruit, the edge of a

[003-213] sharp knife, are not these, too, impressions obtainable from a picture?

[003-214] Are they visual? What would a picture mean to an imaginary man, lacking

[003-215] all or many of his senses, who should in an instant acquire the organ

[003-216] of sight alone? The picture we are looking at and believe we see only

[003-217] with our eyes would seem to his eyes to be little more than an artist's

[003-218] paint-smeared palette.

[003-219] Some who hold firmly to the æsthetic character of certain groups of

[003-220] impressions (for example, the visual and auditive), and exclude others,

[003-221] are nevertheless ready to admit that if visual and auditive impressions

[003-222] enter *directly* into the æsthetic fact, those of the other senses

[003-223] also enter into it, but only as *associated.* But this distinction is

[003-224] altogether arbitrary. Æsthetic expression is synthesis, in which it

[003-225] is impossible to distinguish direct and indirect. All impressions are

[003-226] placed by it on a level, in so far as they are æstheticized. A man who

[003-227] absorbs the subject of a picture or poem does not have it before him as

[003-228] a series of impressions, some of which have prerogatives and precedence

[003-229] over the others. He knows nothing as to what has happened prior to

[003-230] having absorbed it, just as, on the other hand, distinctions made after

[003-231] reflexion have nothing whatever to do with art as such.

[003-232] The theory of the æsthetic senses has also been presented in another

[003-233] way; as an attempt to establish what physiological organs are necessary

[003-234] for the æsthetic fact. The physiological organ or apparatus is nothing

[003-235] but a group of cells, constituted and disposed in a particular manner;

[003-236] that is to say, it is a merely physical and natural fact or concept.

[003-237] But expression does not know physiological facts. Expression has its

[003-238] point of departure in the impressions, and the physiological path

[003-239] by which these have found their way to the mind is to it altogether

[003-240] indifferent. One way or another comes to the same thing: it suffices

[003-241] that they should be impressions.

[003-242] It is true that the want of given organs, that is, of certain groups of

[003-243] cells, prevents the formation of certain impressions (when these are

[003-244] not otherwise obtained through a kind of organic compensation). The

[003-245] man born blind cannot intuite and express light. But the impressions

[003-246] are not conditioned solely by the organ, but also by the stimuli which

[003-247] operate upon the organ. One who has never had the impression of the sea

[003-248] will never be able to express it, in the same way as one who has never

[003-249] had the impression of the life of high society or of the political

[003-250] arena will never express either. This, however, does not prove the

[003-251] dependence of the expressive function on the stimulus or on the

[003-252] organ. It merely repeats what we know already: expression presupposes

[003-253] impression, and particular expressions particular impressions. For the

[003-254] rest, every impression excludes other impressions during the moment in

[003-255] which it dominates; and so does every expression.

[003-256] *Unity and indivisibility of the work of art.*

[003-257] Another corollary of the conception of expression as activity is the

[003-258] *indivisibility* of the work of art. Every expression is a single

[003-259] expression. Activity is a fusion of the impressions in an organic

[003-260] whole. A desire to express this has always prompted the affirmation

[003-261] that the work of art should have *unity,* or, what amounts to the same

[003-262] thing, *unity in variety.* Expression is a synthesis of the various, or

[003-263] multiple, in the one.

[003-264] The fact that we divide a work of art into parts, a poem into scenes,

[003-265] episodes, similes, sentences, or a picture into single figures and

[003-266] objects, background, foreground, etc., may seem opposed to this

[003-267] affirmation. But such division annihilates the work, as dividing the

[003-268] organism into heart, brain, nerves, muscles and so on, turns the living

[003-269] being into a corpse. It is true that there exist organisms in which

[003-270] division gives rise to other living beings, but in such a case we must

[003-271] conclude, maintaining the analogy between the organism and the work of

[003-272] art, that in the latter case too there are numerous germs of life each

[003-273] ready to grow, in a moment, into a single complete expression.

[003-274] It may be said that expression sometimes arises from other expressions.

[003-275] There are simple and there are *compound* expressions. One must surely

[003-276] admit some difference between the *eureka,* with which Archimedes

[003-277] expressed all his joy at his discovery, and the expressive act (indeed

[003-278] all the five acts) of a regular tragedy.—Not in the least: expression

[003-279] always arises directly from impressions. He who conceives a tragedy

[003-280] puts into a crucible a great quantity, so to say, of impressions:

[003-281] expressions themselves, conceived on other occasions, are fused

[003-282] together with the new in a single mass, in the same way as we can

[003-283] cast into a melting furnace formless pieces of bronze and choicest

[003-284] statuettes. Those choicest statuettes must be melted just like the

[003-285] pieces of bronze, before there can be a new statue. The old expressions

[003-286] must descend again to the level of impressions, in order to be

[003-287] synthesized in a new single expression.

[003-288] *Art as liberator.*

[003-289] By elaborating his impressions, man *frees* himself from them. By

[003-290] objectifying them, he removes them from him and makes himself their

[003-291] superior. The liberating and purifying function of art is another

[003-292] aspect and another formula of its character as activity. Activity is

[003-293] the deliverer, just because it drives away passivity.

[003-294] This also explains why it is usual to attribute to artists both the

[003-295] maximum of sensibility or *passion*, and the maximum of insensibility

[003-296] or Olympian *serenity.* The two characters are compatible, for they do

[003-297] not refer to the same object. The sensibility or passion relates to

[003-298] the rich material which the artist absorbs into his psychic organism;

[003-299] the insensibility or serenity to the form with which he subdues and

[003-300] dominates the tumult of the sensations and passions.
