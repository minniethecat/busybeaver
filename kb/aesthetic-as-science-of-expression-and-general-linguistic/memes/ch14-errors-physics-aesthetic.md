[014-001] *Æsthetic activity and physical concepts.*

[014-002] Æsthetic activity, distinct from the practical activity, is always

[014-003] accompanied by it in its manifestations. Hence its utilitarian or

[014-004] hedonistic side, and the pleasure and pain which are, as it were, the

[014-005] practical echo of æsthetic value and disvalue, of [[beauty|the beautiful]] and of

[014-006] the ugly. But this practical side of the æsthetic activity has in its

[014-007] turn a *physical* or *psycho-physical* accompaniment, which consists of

[014-008] sounds, tones, movements, combinations of lines and colours, and so on.

[014-009] Does it *really* possess this side, or does it only seem to possess it,

[014-010] through the construction which we put on it in physical science, and

[014-011] the useful and arbitrary methods which we have already several times

[014-012] set in relief as proper to the empirical and abstract sciences? Our

[014-013] reply cannot be doubtful, that is, it must affirm to the second of the

[014-014] two hypotheses.

[014-015] However, it will be better to leave this point in suspense, since it

[014-016] is not at present necessary to press this line of inquiry further. The

[014-017] mere mention suffices to secure our speaking (for reasons of simplicity

[014-018] and adhesion to ordinary language) of the physical element as something

[014-019] objective and existing, against leading to hasty conclusions as to the

[014-020] concepts of spirit and nature and their relation.

[014-021] *[[expression|Expression]] in the æsthetic sense, and expression in the

[014-022] naturalistic sense.*

[014-023] It is important, on the other hand, to make clear that as the existence

[014-024] of the hedonistic side in every spiritual activity has given rise

[014-025] to the confusion between the æsthetic activity and the useful or

[014-026] pleasurable, so the existence of, or rather the possibility of

[014-027] constructing, this physical side, has caused the confusion between

[014-028] *æsthetic* expression and expression *in a naturalistic sense*; that

[014-029] is to say, between a spiritual fact and a mechanical and passive fact

[014-030] (not to say, between a concrete reality and an abstraction or fiction).

[014-031] In common speech, sometimes it is the words of the poet that are

[014-032] called *expressions,* the notes of the musician, or the figures of the

[014-033] painter; sometimes the blush which generally accompanies the feeling of

[014-034] shame, the pallor often due to fear, the grinding of the teeth proper

[014-035] to violent anger, the shining of the eyes and certain movements of the

[014-036] muscles of the mouth, which manifest cheerfulness. We also say that a

[014-037] certain degree of heat is the *expression* of fever, that the falling

[014-038] of the barometer is the *expression* of rain, and even that the height

[014-039] of the exchange *expresses* the depreciation of the paper currency of a

[014-040] State, or social discontent the approach of a revolution. One can well

[014-041] imagine what sort of scientific results would be attained by allowing

[014-042] oneself to be governed by verbal usage and classing together facts so

[014-043] widely different. But there is, in fact, an abyss between a man who

[014-044] is the prey of anger with all its natural manifestations and another

[014-045] man who expresses it æsthetically; between the appearance, the cries

[014-046] and contortions of some one grieving at the loss of a dear one and the

[014-047] words or song with which the same individual portrays his suffering

[014-048] at another time; between the grimace of emotion and the gesture of

[014-049] the actor. Darwin's book on the expression of the emotions in man and

[014-050] animals does not belong to Æsthetic; because there is nothing in common

[014-051] between the science of spiritual expression and a *Semiotic,* whether

[014-052] it be medical, meteorological, political, physiognomic, or chiromantic.

[014-053] Expression in the naturalistic sense simply lacks *expression in the

[014-054] spiritual sense,* that is to say, the very character of activity and

[014-055] of spirituality, and therefore the bipartition into the poles of

[014-056] beauty and of ugliness. It is nothing but a relation between cause

[014-057] and effect, fixed by the abstract intellect. The complete process

[014-058] of æsthetic production can be symbolized in four stages, which are:

[014-059] *a,* impressions; *b,* expression or spiritual æsthetic synthesis;

[014-060] *c,* hedonistic accompaniment, or pleasure of the beautiful (æsthetic

[014-061] pleasure); *d,* translation of the æsthetic fact into physical

[014-062] phenomena (sounds, tones, movements, combinations of lines and colours,

[014-063] etc.). Any one can see that the capital point, the only one that

[014-064] is properly speaking æsthetic and truly real, is in *b,* which is

[014-065] lacking to the merely naturalistic manifestation or construction also

[014-066] metaphorically called expression.

[014-067] The expressive process is exhausted when these four stages have been

[014-068] passed through. It begins again with new impressions, a new æsthetic

[014-069] synthesis, and the accompaniments that belong to it.

[014-070] *Representations and memory.*

[014-071] Expressions or representations follow one another, the one drives out

[014-072] the other. Certainly, this passing away, this being driven out, is

[014-073] not a perishing, it is not total elimination: nothing that is born

[014-074] dies with that complete death which would be identical with never

[014-075] having been born. If all things pass away, nothing can die. Even the

[014-076] representations that we have forgotten persist somehow in our spirit,

[014-077] for without this we could not explain acquired habits and capacities.

[014-078] Indeed the strength of life lies in this apparent forgetting: one

[014-079] forgets what has been absorbed and what life has superseded.

[014-080] But other representations are also powerful elements in the present

[014-081] processes of our spirit; and it is incumbent upon us not to forget

[014-082] them, or to be capable of recalling them when they are wanted. The

[014-083] will is always vigilant in this work of preservation, which aims at

[014-084] preserving (we may say) the greater, the more fundamental part of all

[014-085] our riches. But its vigilance does not always suffice. Memory, as we

[014-086] say, abandons or betrays us in different ways. For this very reason,

[014-087] the human spirit devises expedients which succour the weakness of

[014-088] memory and are its *aids.*

[014-089] *The production of aids to memory.*

[014-090] How these aids are possible we have been informed from what has been

[014-091] said. Expressions or representations are *also* practical facts, which

[014-092] are also called physical in so far as physics classifies and reduces

[014-093] them to types. Now it is clear that if we can succeed in making those

[014-094] practical or physical facts somehow permanent, it will always be

[014-095] possible (all other conditions remaining equal) on perceiving them to

[014-096] reproduce in ourselves the already produced expression or intuition.

[014-097] If that be called the object or physical stimulus in which the

[014-098] practical concomitant acts, or (to use physical terms) in which the

[014-099] movements have been isolated and made in some sort permanent, and

[014-100] if that object or stimulus be designated by the letter *e*; the

[014-101] process of reproduction will take place in the following order: *e,*

[014-102] the physical stimulus; *d-b,* perception of physical facts (sounds,

[014-103] tones, mimetic, combinations of lines and colours, etc.), which is

[014-104] together the æsthetic synthesis, already produced; *c*, the hedonistic

[014-105] accompaniment, which is also reproduced.

[014-106] And what else are those combinations of words called poetry, prose,

[014-107] poems, novels, romances, tragedies or comedies, but *physical

[014-108] stimulants of reproduction* (the stage *e*); what else are those

[014-109] combinations of sound called operas, symphonies, sonatas; or

[014-110] those combinations of lines and colours called pictures, statues,

[014-111] architecture? The spiritual energy of memory, with the assistance of

[014-112] the physical facts above mentioned, makes possible the preservation and

[014-113] the reproduction of the intuitions produced by man. The physiological

[014-114] organism and with it the memory become weakened; the monuments of art

[014-115] are destroyed, and lo, all that æsthetic wealth, the fruit of the

[014-116] labours of many generations, diminishes and rapidly disappears.

[014-117] *Physical beauty.*

[014-118] Monuments of art, the stimulants of æsthetic reproduction, are called

[014-119] *beautiful things* or *physical beauty.* This combination of words

[014-120] constitutes a verbal paradox, for the beautiful is not a physical

[014-121] fact; it does not belong to things, but to the activity of man, to

[014-122] spiritual energy. But it is now clear through what transferences and

[014-123] associations, physical things and facts which are simply aids to the

[014-124] reproduction of the beautiful are finally called elliptically beautiful

[014-125] things and physical beauty. And now that we have explained this

[014-126] elliptical usage, we shall ourselves employ it without hesitation.

[014-127] *[[content|Content]] and form: another meaning.*

[014-128] The intervention of "physical beauty" serves to explain another meaning

[014-129] of the words "*content*" and "*form,*" as used by æstheticians. Some

[014-130] call "content" the internal fact or expression (for us, on the other

[014-131] hand, form), and "form" the marble, the colours, the rhythm, the sounds

[014-132] (for us the antithesis of form); thus looking upon the physical fact

[014-133] as the form, which may or may not be joined to the content. It also

[014-134] serves to explain another aspect of what is called æsthetic "ugliness."

[014-135] Somebody who has nothing definite to express may try to conceal his

[014-136] internal emptiness in a flood of words, in sounding verse, in deafening

[014-137] polyphony, in painting that dazzles the eye, or by heaping together

[014-138] great architectural masses which arrest and astonish us without

[014-139] conveying anything whatever. Ugliness, then, is the capricious, the

[014-140] charlatanesque; and, in reality, if practical caprice did not intervene

[014-141] in the theoretic function, there might be absence of beauty, but never

[014-142] the real presence of something deserving the adjective "ugly."

[014-143] *Natural and artificial beauty.*

[014-144] Physical beauty is usually divided into *natural* and *artificial*

[014-145] beauty. Thus we reach one of the facts which have given the greatest

[014-146] trouble to thinkers: *natural beauty.* These words often designate

[014-147] facts of merely practical pleasure. Any one who calls a landscape

[014-148] beautiful where the eye rests upon verdure, where the body moves

[014-149] briskly and the warm sun envelops and caresses the limbs, does not

[014-150] speak of anything æsthetic. But it is nevertheless indubitable that

[014-151] on other occasions the adjective "beautiful," applied to objects and

[014-152] scenes existing in nature, has a completely æsthetic signification.

[014-153] It has been observed that in order to enjoy natural objects

[014-154] æsthetically, we must abstract from their external and historical

[014-155] reality, and separate their simple semblance or appearance from

[014-156] existence; that if we contemplate a landscape with our head between

[014-157] our legs, so as to cancel our wonted relations with it, the landscape

[014-158] appears to us to be an ideal spectacle; that nature is beautiful

[014-159] only for him who contemplates her *with the eye of the artist*; that

[014-160] zoologists and botanists do not recognize *beautiful* animals and

[014-161] flowers; that natural beauty is *discovered* (and examples of discovery

[014-162] are the points of view, pointed out by men of taste and imagination,

[014-163] to which more or less æsthetic travellers and excursionists afterwards

[014-164] have recourse in pilgrimage, whence a kind of collective *suggestion)*;

[014-165] that, without the *aid of the imagination,* no part of nature is

[014-166] beautiful, and that with such aid the same natural object or fact

[014-167] is, according to the disposition of the soul, now expressive, now

[014-168] insignificant, now expressive of one definite thing, now of another,

[014-169] sad or glad, sublime or ridiculous, sweet or laughable; finally, that

[014-170] a *natural beauty* which an artist would not *to some extent correct,

[014-171] does not exist.*

[014-172] All these observations are just, and fully confirm the fact that

[014-173] natural beauty is simply a *stimulus* to æsthetic reproduction,

[014-174] which presupposes previous production. Without the previous æsthetic

[014-175] intuitions of the imagination, nature cannot awaken any at all. As

[014-176] regards natural beauty, man is like the mythical Narcissus at the

[014-177] fountain. Leopardi said that natural beauty is "rare, scattered, and

[014-178] fugitive": it is imperfect, equivocal, variable. Each refers the

[014-179] natural fact to the expression in his mind. One artist is thrown into

[014-180] transports by a smiling landscape, another by a rag-shop, another by

[014-181] the pretty face of a young girl, another by the squalid countenance

[014-182] of an old rascal. Perhaps the first will say that the rag-shop and

[014-183] the ugly face of the old rascal are *repulsive*; the second, that the

[014-184] smiling landscape and the face of the young girl are *insipid.* They

[014-185] may dispute for ever; but they will never agree, save when they are

[014-186] supplied with a sufficient dose of æsthetic knowledge to enable them to

[014-187] recognize that both are right. *Artificial* beauty, created by man,

[014-188] supplies an aid that is far more ductile and efficacious.

[014-189] *Mixed beauty.*

[014-190] In addition to these two classes, æstheticians also sometimes talk in

[014-191] their treatises of a *mixed* beauty. A mixture of what? Precisely of

[014-192] natural and artificial. Whoever fixes and externalizes, operates with

[014-193] natural data which he does not create but combines and transforms.

[014-194] In this sense, every artificial product is a mixture of nature and

[014-195] artifice; and there would be no occasion to speak of a mixed beauty,

[014-196] as of a special category. But it sometimes happens that combinations

[014-197] already given in nature can be used a great deal more than in others;

[014-198] as, for instance, when we design a beautiful garden and include in our

[014-199] design groups of trees or ponds already in place. On other occasions

[014-200] externalization is limited by the impossibility of producing certain

[014-201] effects artificially. Thus we can mix colouring matters, but we cannot

[014-202] create a powerful voice or a face and figure appropriate to this or

[014-203] that character in a play. We must therefore seek them among already

[014-204] existing things, and make use of them when found. When, therefore, we

[014-205] employ a great number of combinations already existing in nature, such

[014-206] as we should not be able to produce artificially if they did not exist,

[014-207] the resulting fact is called *mixed* beauty.

[014-208] *Writings.*

[014-209] We must distinguish from artificial beauty those instruments of

[014-210] reproduction called *writings,* such as alphabets, musical notes,

[014-211] hieroglyphics, and all pseudolanguages, from the language of flowers

[014-212] and flags to the language of patches (so much in vogue in the society

[014-213] of the eighteenth century). Writings are not physical facts which

[014-214] arouse directly impressions answering to æsthetic expressions; they

[014-215] are simple *indications* of what must be done in order to produce such

[014-216] physical facts. A series of graphic signs serves to remind us of the

[014-217] movements which we must execute with our vocal apparatus in order to

[014-218] emit certain definite sounds. If, through practice, we become able

[014-219] to hear the words without opening our mouths and (what is much more

[014-220] difficult) to hear the sounds by running the eye along the stave, all

[014-221] this does not alter in any way the nature of the writings, which are

[014-222] altogether different from direct physical beauty. No one calls the

[014-223] book which contains the *Divine Comedy,* or the score which contains

[014-224] *Don Giovanni,* beautiful in the same sense in which the block of

[014-225] marble which contains Michæl Angelo's *Moses,* or the piece of coloured

[014-226] wood which contains the *Transfiguration,* is metaphorically called

[014-227] beautiful. Both serve the reproduction of the beautiful, but the former

[014-228] by a far longer and more indirect route than the latter.

[014-229] *Free and non-free beauty.*

[014-230] Another division of the beautiful, still found in treatises, is that

[014-231] into *free and not free.* By not-free beauties have been understood

[014-232] those objects which have to serve a double purpose, extra-æsthetic

[014-233] and æsthetic (stimulants of intuitions); and since it seems that the

[014-234] first purpose sets limits and barriers in the way of the second, the

[014-235] resulting beautiful object has been considered as not-free beauty.

[014-236] Architectural works are especially cited; and just for this reason,

[014-237] architecture has often been excluded from the number of what are called

[014-238] the fine arts. A temple must above all things be for the use of a

[014-239] cult; a house must contain all the rooms needed for the convenience

[014-240] of life, and they must be arranged with a view to this convenience; a

[014-241] fortress must be a construction capable of resisting the attacks of

[014-242] given armies and the blows of given instruments of war. It is therefore

[014-243] concluded that the architect's field is restricted: he may *embellish*

[014-244] to some extent the temple, the house, the fortress; but he is bound by

[014-245] the *object* of those edifices, and he can only manifest that part of

[014-246] his vision of beauty which does not impair their extra-æsthetic but

[014-247] fundamental objects.

[014-248] Other examples are taken from what is called art applied to industry.

[014-249] Plates, glasses, knives, guns and combs can be made beautiful; but it

[014-250] is held that their beauty must not be pushed so far as to prevent our

[014-251] eating from the plate, drinking from the glass, cutting with the knife,

[014-252] firing off the gun, or combing one's hair with the comb. The same is

[014-253] said of the art of typography: a book should be beautiful, but not to

[014-254] the extent of being difficult or impossible to read.

[014-255] *Criticism of non-free beauty.*

[014-256] In respect of all this we must observe in the first place that the

[014-257] extrinsic purpose is not necessarily, precisely because it is such,

[014-258] a limit or impediment to the other purpose of being a stimulus to

[014-259] æsthetic reproduction. It is therefore quite false to maintain that

[014-260] architecture, for example, is by its nature imperfect and not free,

[014-261] since it must also obey other practical purposes; in fact, the mere

[014-262] presence of fine works of architecture is enough to dispel any such

[014-263] illusion.

[014-264] In the second place, not only are the two purposes not necessarily

[014-265] contradictory, but we must add that the artist always has the means

[014-266] of preventing this contradiction from arising. How? by simply making

[014-267] the *destination* of the object which serves a practical end enter

[014-268] as material into his æsthetic intuition and externalization. He will

[014-269] not need to add anything to the object, in order to make it the

[014-270] instrument of æsthetic intuitions: it will be so, if perfectly adapted

[014-271] to its practical purpose. Rustic dwellings and palaces, churches and

[014-272] barracks, swords and ploughs, are beautiful, not in so far as they are

[014-273] embellished and adorned, but in so far as they express their end. A

[014-274] garment is only beautiful because it is exactly suitable to a given

[014-275] person in given conditions. The sword bound to the side of the warrior

[014-276] Rinaldo by the amorous Armida was not beautiful: "so adorned that it

[014-277] may seem a useless ornament, not the free instrument of war," or it

[014-278] was beautiful, if you will, but to the eyes and imagination of the

[014-279] sorceress, who liked to see her lover equipped in that effeminate way.

[014-280] The æsthetic activity can always agree with the practical, because

[014-281] expression is truth.

[014-282] It cannot however be denied that æsthetic contemplation sometimes

[014-283] hinders practical usage. For instance, it is a quite common experience

[014-284] to find certain new objects seem so well adapted to their purpose,

[014-285] and therefore so beautiful, that people occasionally feel scruples in

[014-286] maltreating them by passing from their contemplation to their use. It

[014-287] was for this reason that King Frederick William of Prussia showed

[014-288] such repugnance to sending his magnificent grenadiers, so well adapted

[014-289] to war, into the mud and fire of battle, while his less æsthetic son,

[014-290] Frederick the Great, obtained from them excellent service.

[014-291] *Stimulants of production.*

[014-292] It might be objected to the explanation of the physically beautiful

[014-293] as a simple aid to the reproduction of the internally beautiful, or

[014-294] expressions, that the artist creates his expressions by painting or

[014-295] by sculpturing, by writing or by composing, and that therefore the

[014-296] physically beautiful, instead of following, sometimes precedes the

[014-297] æsthetically beautiful. This would be a somewhat superficial mode of

[014-298] understanding the procedure of the artist, who never in reality makes

[014-299] a stroke with his brush without having previously seen it with his

[014-300] imagination; and if he has not yet seen it, he will make the stroke,

[014-301] not in order to externalize his expression (which does not yet exist),

[014-302] but as a kind of experiment and in order to have a point of departure

[014-303] for further meditation and internal concentration. The physical

[014-304] point of departure is not the physically beautiful instrument of

[014-305] reproduction, but a means that may be called *pedagogic,* like retiring

[014-306] into solitude, or the many other expedients frequently very strange,

[014-307] adopted by artists and scientists, who vary in these according to their

[014-308] various idiosyncrasies. The old æsthetician [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]] advised poets

[014-309] seeking inspiration to ride on horseback, to drink wine in moderation,

[014-310] and (provided they were chaste) to look at beautiful women.
