# Literary Kinds

The theory of artistic and literary kinds (*generi letterari*) is, in Croce's Æsthetic, a fundamental error: the attempt to classify works of art into fixed genres — tragedy, comedy, epic, lyric — and then to judge them by their conformity to these categories. Croce rejects this approach entirely, arguing that each work of art is an irreducibly individual expression governed by its own internal logic, not by an external rule.

## Definition

"The theory of artistic and literary kinds," Croce writes, "still has vogue in literary criticism" despite its conceptual incoherence (005-095). A "kind" is neither a universal concept (it cannot define art's essence) nor an empirical generalization (it claims normative force over individual works). It is what Croce calls a pseudo-concept — a hybrid that borrows the form of conceptual thought without its substance.

## In Croce's System

### The Pseudo-Concept

The theory of kinds belongs to the error of *intellectualism* in æsthetics: the imposition of conceptual categories upon what is, by its nature, intuitive and individual. Artistic and literary kinds are "erroneous doctrines" (005-154) that treat art as a species within a genus, when art is precisely that which resists generic classification. Each work gives itself its own law.

### Consequences of the Error

From the theory of literary kinds "derive those erroneous judgments" (005-135) that dominate academic criticism: the demand that a tragedy observe the unities, that an epic contain certain episodes, that a lyric avoid didacticism. These judgments mistake historical conventions for aesthetic necessities. "The condemnation of artistic and literary kinds is found" in the recognition that no rule precedes the work (005-186).

### Varieties of the Error

Croce traces multiple forms this error takes. "A variety of the error of literary kinds is to be found in" theories that classify by subject matter (historical novel, nature poetry), by technique (sonnet, fresco), or by effect (comic, sublime) (038-1493). None captures what makes a work art — its success as expression. "The objectivity of literary kinds" (038-951) is an illusion created by the inertia of critical practice.

## Historical Context

### The Classical Tradition

The theory of kinds originates in antiquity: Aristotle's *Poetics* distinguished tragedy from comedy and epic; Horace's *Ars Poetica* prescribed forms and decorum; Renaissance criticism elaborated these into a system of rules. "For the history of the literary kinds in antiquity see the works" of the major critics (038-2487). This tradition, Croce argues, conflates description with prescription, taking the successful practices of Greek dramatists as binding laws for all subsequent art.

### Modern Persistence

Despite Romanticism's rejection of neoclassical rules, the theory of kinds persists in subtler forms. "The theory of artistic and literary kinds, already criticized in our Æsthetic" (019-196), reappears whenever critics speak of "the novel form" or "the lyric mode" as if these were essences rather than historical conveniences. The "attempt to attach æsthetic value" to generic membership (015-045) remains a permanent temptation.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's rejection of kinds is more radical than previous critiques. It is not merely that the classical rules are too rigid; it is that any generic classification of art is conceptually incoherent. Art is the domain of the individual-intuitive; kinds belong to the universal-conceptual. To classify art by kind is to treat it as something other than art — a document, a social phenomenon, a technical product, but not an expression.

## Historical Development

Croce's critique of literary kinds appears early in the *Æsthetic* (Chapter V) and remains stable throughout his career. Later works extend the argument to related errors: the theory of styles, the classification of the arts by medium (poetry vs. painting vs. music), and the notion of artistic "laws." In each case, the error is the same: substituting an external category for the internal logic of expression.
