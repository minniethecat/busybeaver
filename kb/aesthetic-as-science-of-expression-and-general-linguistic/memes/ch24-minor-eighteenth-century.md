[024-001] *[[giambattista-vico|Vico]] as inventor of æsthetic science.*

[024-002] The real revolutionary who by putting aside the concept of probability

[024-003] and conceiving imagination in a novel manner actually discovered the

[024-004] true nature of poetry and art and, so to speak, invented the science of

[024-005] Æsthetic, was the Italian Giambattista Vico.

[024-006] Ten years prior to the publication in [[germany|Germany]] of [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]]'s first

[024-007] treatise, there had appeared in Naples (1725) the first *Scienza

[024-008] nuova,* which developed ideas on the nature of poetry outlined in

[024-009] a former work (1721), *De constantia iurisprudentis,* outcome of

[024-010] "twenty-five years' continuous and harsh meditation."[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_1_198) In 1730 Vico

[024-011] republished it with fresh developments which gave rise to two special

[024-012] books (*Della sapienza poetica* and *Della discoperta del vero Omero*)

[024-013] in the second *Scienza Nuova.* Nor did he ever tire of repeating his

[024-014] views and forcing them upon the attention of his hostile contemporaries

[024-015] at every opportunity, seizing such occasion even in prefaces and

[024-016] letters, poems on the occasion of weddings or funerals, and in such

[024-017] press notices as fell to his duty as public censor of literature.

[024-018] And what were these ideas? Neither more nor less, we may say, than

[024-019] the solution of the problem stated by Plato, attacked but not solved

[024-020] by Aristotle, and again vainly attacked during the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] and

[024-021] afterwards: is poetry rational or irrational, spiritual or brutal?

[024-022] and, if spiritual, what is its special nature and what distinguishes

[024-023] it from history and science?

[024-024] As we know, Plato confined it within the baser part of the soul, the

[024-025] animal spirits. Vico re-elevates it and makes of it a period in the

[024-026] history of humanity: and since history for him means an ideal history

[024-027] whose periods consist not of contingent facts but of forms of the

[024-028] spirit, he makes it a moment in the ideal history of the spirit, a form

[024-029] of consciousness. Poetry precedes intellect, but follows sense; through

[024-030] confusing it with the latter, Plato failed to grasp the position it

[024-031] should really occupy and banished it from his Republic. "Men at first

[024-032] feel without being aware; next they become aware with a perturbed and

[024-033] agitated soul; finally they reflect with an undisturbed mind. This

[024-034] Aphorism is the Principle of poetical sentences which are formed by the

[024-035] sense of passions and affections; differing thereby from philosophical

[024-036] sentences which are formed by reflexion through ratiocination; whence

[024-037] the latter approach more nearly to truth the more they rise towards

[024-038] the universal, while the former have more of certainty the more they

[024-039] approach the individual."[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_2_199) An imaginative phase of consciousness, but

[024-040] one possessed of positive value.

[024-041] *Poetry and Philosophy: imagination and intellect.*

[024-042] The imaginative phase is altogether independent and autonomous with

[024-043] respect to the intellectual, which is not only incapable of endowing

[024-044] it with any fresh perfection but can only destroy it. "The studies of

[024-045] Metaphysics and Poetry are in natural opposition one to the other;

[024-046] for the former purges the mind of childish prejudice and the latter

[024-047] immerses and drowns it in the same: the former offers resistance to

[024-048] the judgement of the senses, while the latter makes this its chief

[024-049] rule: the former debilitates, the latter strengthens, imagination: the

[024-050] former prides itself in not turning spirit into body, the latter does

[024-051] its utmost to give a body to spirit: hence the thoughts of the former

[024-052] must necessarily be abstract, while the concepts of the latter show

[024-053] best when most clothed with matter: to sum up, the former strives that

[024-054] the learned may know the truth of things stripped of all passion: the

[024-055] latter that the vulgar may act truly by means of intense excitement

[024-056] of the senses, without which stimulant they assuredly would not act

[024-057] at all. Hence from all time, in all languages known to man, never has

[024-058] there been a strong man equally great as metaphysician and poet: such a

[024-059] poet as Homer, father and prince of poetry."[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_3_200) Poets are the senses,

[024-060] philosophers the intellect, of mankind.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_4_201) [[imagination|Imagination]] is "stronger in

[024-061] proportion as reason is weaker."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_5_202)

[024-062] No doubt "reflexion" may be put in verse; but it does not become poetry

[024-063] thereby. "Abstract sentences belong to philosophers, since they contain

[024-064] universals; and reflexions concerning such passions are made by poets

[024-065] who are false and frigid."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_6_203) Those poets "who sing of the beauty and

[024-066] virtue of ladies by reflexion ... are philosophers arguing in verses

[024-067] or in love-rhymes."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_7_204) One set of ideas belongs to philosophers,

[024-068] another to poets: these latter are identical with those of painters,

[024-069] from which "they differ only in colours and words."[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_8_205) Great poets are

[024-070] born not in epochs of reflexion but in those of imagination, generally

[024-071] called barbarous: Homer, in the barbarism of antiquity: Dante in that

[024-072] of the Middle Ages, the "second barbarism of [[italy|Italy]]."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_9_206) Those who have

[024-073] chosen to read philosophic reason into the verse of the great father

[024-074] of Greek poetry have transferred the character of a later age into

[024-075] an earlier, since the era of poets precedes that of philosophers and

[024-076] countries in infancy were sublime poets. Poetic locutions arose before

[024-077] prose, "by the necessity of nature" not "by caprice of pleasure";

[024-078] fables or imaginative universals were conceived before reasoned, *i.e.*

[024-079] philosophical universals.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_10_207)

[024-080] With these observations Vico justified and at the same time corrected

[024-081] the opinion of Plato in the *Republic,* denying to Homer wisdom, every

[024-082] kind of wisdom; the legislative of Lycurgus and Solon, the philosophic

[024-083] of Thales, Anacharsis and Pythagoras, the strategic of military

[024-084] commanders.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_11_208) To Homer (he says) belongs wisdom, undoubtedly, but

[024-085] poetic wisdom only: the Homeric images and comparisons derived from

[024-086] wild beasts and the elements of savage nature are incomparable; but

[024-087] "such success does not spring from talent imbued with domesticity and

[024-088] civilized with any philosophy."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_12_209)

[024-089] When anybody takes to writing poetry in an era of reflexion, it is

[024-090] because he is returning to childhood and "putting his mind in fetters";

[024-091] no longer reflecting with his intellect, he follows imagination

[024-092] and loses himself in the particular. If a true poet dallies with

[024-093] philosophical ideas, it is not "that he may assimilate them and dismiss

[024-094] imagination," but merely "that he may have them in front of him, to

[024-095] examine as though on a stage or public platform."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_13_210) The New Comedy

[024-096] which made its appearance after Socrates is undeniably impregnated with

[024-097] philosophic ideas, with intellectual universals, with "intelligible

[024-098] kinds of human conduct"; but its authors were poets in so far only as

[024-099] they knew how to transform logic into imagination and their ideas into

[024-100] portraits.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_14_211)

[024-101] *Poetry and History.*

[024-102] The dividing line between art and science, imagination and intellect,

[024-103] is here very strongly drawn: the two distinct activities are repeatedly

[024-104] contrasted with a sharpness that leaves no room for confusion. The

[024-105] line of demarcation between poetry and history is hardly less firm.

[024-106] While not quoting Aristotle's passage, Vico implicitly shows why

[024-107] poetry seemed to Aristotle more philosophical than history, and at

[024-108] the same time he dispels the erroneous opinion that history concerns

[024-109] the particular and poetry the universal. Poetry joins hands with

[024-110] science not because it consists in the contemplation of concepts but

[024-111] because, like science, it is ideal. The most beautiful poetic story

[024-112] must be "wholly ideal": "by means of idea, the poet breathes reality

[024-113] into things otherwise unreal; masters of poetry claim that their art

[024-114] must be wholly compact of imagination, like a painter of the ideal,

[024-115] not imitative like a portrait-painter: whence, from their likeness to

[024-116] God the Creator, poets and painters alike are called divine."[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_15_212) And

[024-117] against those who blame poets for telling stories which, they say, are

[024-118] untrue, Vico protests: "The best stories are those approximating most

[024-119] nearly to ideal truth, the eternal truth of God: it is immeasurably

[024-120] more certain than the truth of historians who often bring into play

[024-121] caprice, necessity or fortune; but such a Captain as, for instance,

[024-122] Tasso's Godfrey is the type of a captain of all times, of all nations,

[024-123] and so are all personages of poetry, whatever difference there may be

[024-124] in sex, age, temperament, custom, nation, republic, grade, condition

[024-125] or fortune; they are nothing save the eternal properties of the human

[024-126] soul, rationally discussed by politicians, economists and moral

[024-127] philosophers, and painted as portraits by the poet."[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_16_213) Referring

[024-128] to an observation made by Castelvetro, and approving it in part, to

[024-129] the effect that if poetry is a presentiment of the possible it should

[024-130] be preceded by history, imitation of the real, yet finding himself

[024-131] confronted by the difficulty that, nevertheless, poets invariably

[024-132] precede historians, Vico solves the problem by identifying history

[024-133] with poetry: primitive history was poetry, its plot was narration of

[024-134] fact, and Homer was the first historian; or rather "he was a heroic

[024-135] character amongst Greek men, in so far as they poetically narrated

[024-136] their own history."[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_17_214) Poetry and history, therefore, are originally

[024-137] identical; or rather, undifferentiated. "But inasmuch as it is not

[024-138] possible to give false ideas, since falsity arises from an embroiled

[024-139] combination of ideas, so is it impossible to give a tradition, however

[024-140] fabulous, that has not had, at the beginning, a basis of truth."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_18_215)

[024-141] Hence we gain an entirely new insight into mythology: it is no longer

[024-142] an arbitrary calculated invention, but a spontaneous vision of truth

[024-143] as it presented itself to the spirit of primitive man. Poetry gives an

[024-144] imaginative vision; science or philosophy intelligible truth; history

[024-145] the consciousness of certitude.

[024-146] *Poetry and language.*

[024-147] Language and poetry are, in Vico's estimation, substantially the same.

[024-148] In refuting the "vulgar error of grammarians" who maintain the priority

[024-149] of the birth of prose over that of verse, he finds "within the origin

[024-150] of Poetry, so far as it has been herein discovered," the "origin of

[024-151] languages and the origin of letters."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_19_216) This discovery was made by

[024-152] Vico after "toil as disagreeable and overwhelming as we should undergo

[024-153] had we to strip off our own nature and enter into that of the primæval

[024-154] men of Hobbes, Grotius, or Puffendorf; creatures possessing no language

[024-155] at all, by whom were created the languages of the ancient world."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_20_217)

[024-156] But his painful labour was richly repaid by his refutation of the

[024-157] erroneous theory that languages sprang from convention or, as he said,

[024-158] "signified at will," whereas it is evident that "from their natural

[024-159] origin words must have had natural meanings; this is plainly seen

[024-160] in common Latin ... wherein almost all words have arisen by natural

[024-161] necessity, either from natural properties or from their sensible

[024-162] effects; and in general, metaphor forms the bulk of language in the

[024-163] case of every people."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_21_218) This argument strikes a blow at another

[024-164] common error of the grammarians, "that the language of prose writers

[024-165] is correct, that of poets incorrect."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_22_219) The poetic tropes grouped

[024-166] under the heading of metonymy seem to Vico to be "born of the nature

[024-167] of primitive peoples, not of capricious selection by men skilled in

[024-168] poetic art";[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_23_220) stories told "by means of similitudes, imagery and

[024-169] comparisons," result "from lack of the genera and species required to

[024-170] define things with propriety," and "are therefore, by reason of natural

[024-171] necessities, common to entire peoples."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_24_221) The earliest languages

[024-172] must have consisted of "dumb gestures and objects which had natural

[024-173] connexions with the ideas to be expressed."[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_25_222) He observes very

[024-174] acutely that to these figurate languages belong not only hieroglyphics

[024-175] but the emblems, knightly bearings, devices and blazons which he calls

[024-176] "mediæval hieroglyphics."[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_26_223) In the barbarous Middle Ages "Italy was

[024-177] forced to fall back on the mute language ... of the earliest gentile

[024-178] nations in which men, before discovering articulate speech, were

[024-179] obliged like mutes to use actions or objects having natural connexions

[024-180] with the ideas, which at that time must have been exceedingly sensuous,

[024-181] of the things which they wished to signify; such expressions, clad in

[024-182] almost vocal words, must have had all the lively expressiveness of

[024-183] poetic diction." [[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_27_224) Hence arise three kinds or phases of language:

[024-184] dumb show, the language of the gods; heraldic language, or that of the

[024-185] heroes; and spoken language. Vico also looked forward to a universal

[024-186] system of etymology, a "dictionary of mental words common to all

[024-187] nations."

[024-188] *Inductive and formalistic*

[024-189] A man with ideas of this sort about imagination, language and poetry

[024-190] could not say he was satisfied with formalistic and verbal Logic,

[024-191] whether Aristotelian or scholastic. The human mind (says Vico) "makes

[024-192] use of intellect when from things which it feels by sense it gathers

[024-193] something that does not fall under sense: this is the true meaning

[024-194] of the Latin *intelligere*."[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_28_225) In a rapid outline of the history

[024-195] of Logic, Vico wrote: "Aristotle came and taught the syllogism, a

[024-196] method more suited to expound universals in their particulars than to

[024-197] unite particulars by the discovery of universals: then came Zeno with

[024-198] his sorites, which corresponds with modern philosophic methods and

[024-199] refines, without sharpening, the wits; and no advantage whatever was

[024-200] reaped from either by mankind at large. With great reason, therefore,

[024-201] does Verulam, equally eminent as politician and philosopher, propound,

[024-202] commend and illustrate induction in his Organum: he is followed by the

[024-203] [[england|English]] with excellent results to experimental philosophy."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_29_226) From

[024-204] this source is derived his criticism of mathematics, which have always,

[024-205] but especially in his day, been considered as the type of perfect

[024-206] science.

[024-207] Vico opposed to all formal theories of poetry.

[024-208] In all this, Vico is not only a thorough revolutionary, but is quite

[024-209] conscious of being so: he knows himself to be in opposition to all

[024-210] previous theories on the subject. He says that his new principles of

[024-211] poetry "are wholly opposed to, and not merely different from, all which

[024-212] have been imagined from the time of Plato and his disciple Aristotle

[024-213] to Patrizzi, Scaliger and Castelvetro among the moderns; poetry is now

[024-214] discovered to have been the first language used by all nations alike,

[024-215] even the Hebrew."[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_30_227) In another passage he says that by his theories

[024-216] "is overthrown all that has ever been said of the origin of poetry,

[024-217] beginning from Plato and Aristotle, right down to our own Patrizzi,

[024-218] Scaliger and Castelvetro; and it is found that poetry arising through

[024-219] defect of human ratiocination is as sublime as any which owes its

[024-220] being to the later rise of philosophy and the arts of composition and

[024-221] criticism; indeed, that these later sources never gave rise to any

[024-222] poetry that could equal, far less surpass it."[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_31_228) In the Autobiography

[024-223] he boasts of having discovered "other principles of poetry than those

[024-224] found by Greeks and Latins and all others from those times down to the

[024-225] present day; on these are founded other views on mythology."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_32_229)

[024-226] These ancient principles of poetry "laid down first by Plato and

[024-227] confirmed by Aristotle" had been the anticipation or prejudice which

[024-228] had misled all writers on poetic reason (among whom he cites Jacopo

[024-229] Mazzoni). Statements "even of most serious philosophers such as

[024-230] Patrizzi and others" upon the origin of song and verse are so inept

[024-231] that he "blushes even to mention them."[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_33_230) It is curious to see him

[024-232] annotating the *Ars Poetica* of Horace, with a view to finding some

[024-233] plausible sense in it by applying the principles of the *Scienza

[024-234] nuova*.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_34_231)

[024-235] It is probable that he was familiar with the writings of Muratori

[024-236] among contemporaries, for he quotes him by name, and of Gravina, who

[024-237] was a personal acquaintance; but if he read the *Perfetta Poesia* and

[024-238] the *Forza della fantasia* he could not have been satisfied by the

[024-239] treatment meted out to the faculty of imagination, so highly valued

[024-240] and respected by himself; and if Gravina influenced him at all it must

[024-241] have been by provoking him to contradiction. In this latter (if not

[024-242] directly in such [[france|French]] writers as Le Bossu) he may have met with the

[024-243] fallacy of regarding Homer as a repository of wisdom, a fallacy which

[024-244] he combated with vigour and pertinacity. In his estimation, among the

[024-245] gravest faults of the Cartesians was their inability to appreciate the

[024-246] world of imagination and poetry. Of his own times he complained they

[024-247] were "benumbed by analytical methods and by a philosophy which sought

[024-248] to deaden every faculty of soul which reached it through the body,

[024-249] especially that of imagination, now held to be mother of all human

[024-250] error": times "of a wisdom which freezes the generous soul of the best

[024-251] poetry," and prevents all understanding of it.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_35_232)

[024-252] *Judgments of grammarians and linguists who preceded him*

[024-253] It is just the same with the theory of language. "The manner of birth

[024-254] and the nature of languages has been the cause of much painful toil

[024-255] and meditation: nor, from the *Cratylus* of Plato, in which in our

[024-256] other works we have falsely delighted and believed" (he alludes to

[024-257] the doctrine followed by him in his own first book, *De antiquissima

[024-258] Italorum sapientia*), "down to Wolfgang Latius, Julius Cæsar Scaliger,

[024-259] Francisco Sanchez and others, can we find anything to satisfy our

[024-260] understanding; so much that in discussing matters of this kind Signor

[024-261] Giovanni Clerico says there is nothing in philology involved in such a

[024-262] maze of doubt and difficulty."[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_36_233) The chief grammarian-philosophers do

[024-263] not escape criticism. Grammar, says he, lays down rules for speaking

[024-264] correctly: Logic for speaking truly; "and since in the order of

[024-265] nature we must speak truly before learning to speak correctly, Giulio

[024-266] Cesare della Scala, followed by the best grammarians, employs all his

[024-267] magnificent energy to reason to the causes of the Latin language from

[024-268] the principles of logic. But his great design ended in failure for this

[024-269] reason, that he attached himself to the logical principles of a single

[024-270] philosopher, namely Aristotle, whose principles are too universal to

[024-271] explain the almost infinite particulars which naturally beset him who

[024-272] would reason concerning a language. Whence it happened that Francisco

[024-273] Sanchez, who followed him with admirable zeal, attempting in his

[024-274] *Minerva* to explain the innumerable particles which are found in

[024-275] Latin by his famous principle of ellipsis, and trying thereby, though

[024-276] without success, to vindicate the logical principles of Aristotle, fell

[024-277] into the most cumbrous clumsinesses among an almost innumerable host

[024-278] of Latin phrases whereby he meant to make good the slight and subtle

[024-279] omissions employed by Latin in expressing its meaning."[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_37_234) The origin

[024-280] of parts of speech and syntax is wholly different from that assigned

[024-281] to them by folk who fancied that "the people who invented language

[024-282] must first have gone to school to Aristotle."[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_38_235) The same criticism

[024-283] undoubtedly must have extended to the logico-grammarians of Port-Royal,

[024-284] for Vico remarked that the Logic of Arnauld was built "on the same plan

[024-285] as that of Aristotle."[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_39_236)

[024-286] *Influence of seventeenth century writers on Vico.*

[024-287] It may well be granted that Vico was more in sympathy with the

[024-288] seventeenth-century rhetoricians, in whom we have detected a

[024-289] premonition of æsthetic science. For Vico, as for them, wit (referring

[024-290] to imagination and memory) was "the father of all invention": judgement

[024-291] concerning poetry was for him a "judgement of the senses," a phrase

[024-292] equivalent to "taste" or "[[taste|good taste]]," expressions never used by him

[024-293] in this connexion. There is no doubt he was familiar with the writers

[024-294] of treatises on wit and conceits, for, in a dry rhetorical manual

[024-295] written for the use of his school (in which one looks in vain for a

[024-296] shadow of his own personal ideas), he quotes Paolo Beni, Pellegrini,

[024-297] Pallavicino and the Marquis Orsi.[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_40_237) He highly esteems Pallavicini's

[024-298] treatise on *Style* and has knowledge of the book *Del bene* by the

[024-299] same author;[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_41_238) perhaps too his mind was not unaffected by the flash

[024-300] of genius which had enabled the Jesuit for one instant to perceive that

[024-301] poetry consists of "first apprehensions." He does not name Tesauro, but

[024-302] there is no doubt he knew him; indeed the *Scienza nuova* includes a

[024-303] section, besides that on poetry, upon "blazons," "knightly bearings,"

[024-304] "military banners," "medals," and so forth, precisely similar in

[024-305] method to that of Tesauro when he treats of" figurate conceits" in

[024-306] his *Cannochiale aristotelico*.[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_42_239) For Tesauro such conceits are

[024-307] merely metaphorical ingenuities, like any other; for Vico they are

[024-308] wholly the work of imagination, for imagination expresses itself not

[024-309] in words only, but in the "mute language" of lines and colours. He

[024-310] knew something also of Leibniz; the great German and Newton were by

[024-311] him described as" the greatest wits of the time"[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_43_240); but he seems to

[024-312] have remained in complete ignorance of the æsthetic attempts of the

[024-313] Leibnitian school in Germany. His "Logic of poetry" was a discovery

[024-314] independent of, and earlier than, Bülffinger's Organon of the inferior

[024-315] faculties, the *Gnoseologia inferior* of Baumgarten, and the *Logik

[024-316] der Einbildungskraft* of Breitinger. In truth, Vico belongs on one

[024-317] side to the vast Renaissance reaction against formalism and scholastic

[024-318] verbalism, which, beginning with the reaffirmation of experience and

[024-319] sensation (Telesio, Campanella, Galileo, Bacon), was bound to go on by

[024-320] reasserting the function of imagination in individual and social life:

[024-321] on the other side he is a precursor of [[romanticism|Romanticism]].

[024-322] *Æsthetic in the "Scienza nuova."*

[024-323] The importance of Vico's new poetic theory in his thought as a whole

[024-324] as well as in the organism of his *Scienza nuova* has never been fully

[024-325] appreciated, and the Neapolitan philosopher is still commonly regarded

[024-326] as the inventor of the Philosophy of History. If by such a science is

[024-327] meant the attempt to deduce concrete history by ratiocination and to

[024-328] treat epochs and events as if they were concepts, the only result of

[024-329] Vico's efforts to solve the problem could have been failure; and the

[024-330] same is true of his many successors. The fact is that his philosophy of

[024-331] history, his ideal history, his *Scienza nuova d' intorno alia comune

[024-332] natura delle nazioni,* does not concern the concrete empirical history

[024-333] which unfolds itself in time: it is not history, it is a science of the

[024-334] ideal, a Philosophy of the Spirit. That Vico made many discoveries in

[024-335] history proper which have been to a great extent confirmed by modern

[024-336] criticism (*e.g.* on the development of the Greek epic and the nature

[024-337] and genesis of feudal society in antiquity and in the Middle Ages)

[024-338] certainly deserves all emphasis; but this side of his work must be kept

[024-339] distinctly apart from the other, strictly philosophical, side. And

[024-340] if the philosophical part is a doctrine expounding the ideal moments

[024-341] of the spirit, or in his own words "the modifications of our human

[024-342] mind," of these moments or modifications Vico undertakes especially

[024-343] to define and fully describe not the logical, ethical and economic

[024-344] moments (though on these too he throws much fight), but precisely the

[024-345] imaginative or poetic. The larger portion of the second *Scienza nuova*

[024-346] hinges on the discovery of the creative imagination, including the "new

[024-347] principles of Poetry," the observations on the nature of language,

[024-348] mythology, writing, symbolic figures and so forth. All his "system

[024-349] of civilization, of the Republic, of laws, of poetry, of history, in

[024-350] a word, of humanity at large" is founded upon this discovery, which

[024-351] constitutes the novel point of view at which Vico places himself. The

[024-352] author himself observes that his second book, dedicated to Poetic

[024-353] Wisdom, "wherein is made a discovery totally opposed to Verulam's,"

[024-354] forms "nearly the whole body of the work"; but the first and third

[024-355] books also deal almost exclusively with works of the imagination. It

[024-356] might be maintained, therefore, that Vico's "New Science" was really

[024-357] just Æsthetic; or at least the Philosophy of the Spirit with special

[024-358] emphasis upon the Philosophy of the Æsthetic Spirit.

[024-359] *Vico's mistakes.*

[024-360] Among so many luminous points, or rather in such a general blaze of

[024-361] light, there are yet dark nooks in his mind; corners that remain in

[024-362] shadow. By not maintaining a rigid distinction between concrete history

[024-363] and the philosophy of the spirit, Vico allowed himself to suggest

[024-364] historical periods which do not correspond with the real periods, but

[024-365] are rather allegories, the mythological expression of his philosophy

[024-366] of the spirit. From the same source arises the multiplicity of those

[024-367] periods (usually three in number) which Vico finds in the history of

[024-368] civilization in general, in poetry and language and practically every

[024-369] subject. "The first peoples, who were the children of the human race,

[024-370] founded first the world of the arts: next, after a long interval, the

[024-371] philosophers, who were therefore the aged among nations, founded the

[024-372] world of the sciences: with which humanity attained completion."[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_44_241)

[024-373] Historically, understood in an approximate sense, this scheme of

[024-374] evolution has some truth; but only an approximate truth. In consequence

[024-375] of the same confusion of history and philosophy he denied primitive

[024-376] peoples any kind of intellectual logic, and conceived not only their

[024-377] physics, cosmology, astronomy and geography as poetic in character, but

[024-378] their morals, their economy and their politics as well. But not only

[024-379] has there never been a period in concrete human history entirely poetic

[024-380] and ignorant of all abstraction or power of reasoning, but such a state

[024-381] cannot even be conceived. Morals, politics, physics, all presuppose

[024-382] intellectual work, however imperfect they may be. The ideal priority of

[024-383] poetry cannot be materialized into a historical period of civilization.

[024-384] Linked with this error is another into which Vico often falls when

[024-385] he asserts that "the chief aim of poetry" is to "teach the ignorant

[024-386] vulgar to act virtuously" and to "invent fables adapted with the

[024-387] popular understanding capable of producing strong emotion."[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-13.htm.html#Footnote_45_242)

[024-388] Having regard to the clear explanations he himself gave of the

[024-389] inessentiality of abstractions and intellectual artifice in poetry;

[024-390] when we remember that for him poetry makes her own rules for herself

[024-391] without consulting anybody, and that he clearly established the

[024-392] peculiar theoretical nature of the imagination, such a proposition

[024-393] cannot be taken as a return to the pedagogic and heteronomous theory

[024-394] of poetry which in substance he had left far behind: therefore,

[024-395] without doubt, it follows from his historical hypothesis of a wholly

[024-396] poetical epoch of civilization, in which education, science and

[024-397] morality were administered by poets. Another consequence is that

[024-398] "imaginative universals" are apparently sometimes understood by him as

[024-399] imperfect universals (empirical or representative concepts as they were

[024-400] subsequently called); although, on the other hand, individualization

[024-401] is so marked in them and their unphilosophical nature so accentuated

[024-402] that their interpretation as purely imaginative forms may be taken

[024-403] as normal. In conclusion, we remark that fundamental terms are not

[024-404] always used by Vico in the same sense: it is not always clear how

[024-405] far "sensation," "memory," "imagination," "wit" are synonymous

[024-406] or different. Sometimes "sensation" seems outside the spirit, at

[024-407] others one of its chief moments; poets are sometimes the organ of

[024-408] "imagination," sometimes the "sensation" of humanity; and imagination

[024-409] is described as "dilated memory." These are the aberrations of a

[024-410] thought so virgin and original that it was not easy to regulate.

[024-411] *Progress still to be achieved.*

[024-412] To sever the Philosophy of the Spirit from History, the modifications

[024-413] of the human mind from the historic vicissitudes of peoples, and

[024-414] Æsthetic from Homeric civilization, and by continuing Vico's analyses

[024-415] to determine more clearly the truths he uttered, the distinctions he

[024-416] drew and the identities he divined; in short, to purge Æsthetic of the

[024-417] remains of ancient [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] and Poetics as well as from some over-hasty

[024-418] schematisms imposed upon her by the author of her being: such is the

[024-419] field of labour, such the progress still to be achieved after the

[024-420] discovery of the autonomy of the æsthetic world due to the genius of

[024-421] Giambattista Vico.
