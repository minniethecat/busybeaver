# Didactic

The didactic theory of art — the view that poetry and the arts exist primarily to convey moral or intellectual instruction — is one of the oldest and most tenacious doctrines in æsthetic theory.

Croce subjects it to a decisive critique: art is a form of knowledge, but it is intuitive knowledge, not conceptual instruction.

To make didactic purpose primary is to mistake art for philosophy in verse.

## Definition

Croce identifies the didactic theory as the confusion of art's cognitive character with the delivery of lessons.

Art does convey knowledge — but it is the knowledge of individual things, of intuited particulars, not of universal concepts or moral precepts.

> "All didactic poetry is nothing more than poetry, when it is poetry." [025-545]

The didactic view, by contrast, "illustrates for didactic purposes only" [001-021], treating the æsthetic image as a vehicle for an intellectual content that could be stated independently in propositional form.

For Croce, this gets the relation exactly backward: in genuine art, the content is inseparable from its expression.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

In Croce's fourfold scheme, the didactic theory commits the error of collapsing æsthetic intuition (first grade) into conceptual thought (second grade).

Art knows the individual; philosophy knows the universal.

Didactic poetry, regarded by Croce as a hybrid, "is nothing more than poetry" [025-545] only insofar as it succeeds as expression; its didactic intention is æsthetically irrelevant or even harmful.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes between **art that teaches** and **teaching that uses art**.

The former is a contradiction in terms: art as art has no practical end, didactic or otherwise.

The latter — using aesthetically pleasing forms to convey instruction — is a legitimate practical activity, but it is not æsthetic production proper.

The Renaissance writers who "did not distinguish the didactic kind" [021-262] from other poetic genres were reflecting an Aristotelian classification scheme that Croce regards as pre-æsthetic.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Horace's *Ars Poetica* enshrined the formula *prodesse et delectare* (to profit and to delight), which dominated European poetics for nearly two millennia.

The Renaissance inherited this framework, treating didactic poetry as one of the canonical genres alongside epic, lyric, and dramatic.

In Germany, "all didactic poetry" was placed within the hierarchy of literary kinds [025-545], and even major theorists like the Schlegels struggled to assign it a coherent place.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's contribution is to cut through the genre-classification problem by denying the didactic any genuinely æsthetic status.

The "didactic manuals relating to military" matters [021-251] may be useful documents, but they are not art.

Even when the content is philosophical, "didactic poetry" remains an unstable compound [038-897].

Croce's contemporary, Giovanni Gentile, went further, proposing "to substitute didactic poetry for drama as the highest" form [028-529] — a view Croce firmly opposed.

## Criticism and Debate

The austerity of Croce's position has drawn objections.

If all practical intention is æsthetically irrelevant, what of Dante's *Divine Comedy*, which is explicitly didactic in its moral-theological architecture?

Croce's answer — that Dante is a poet when he intuits and expresses, a theologian when he instructs — has struck many readers as artificially separating what is unified in the experience of the poem.

The didactic impulse may be more deeply woven into the fabric of literary art than Croce's sharp intuition/concept distinction allows.
