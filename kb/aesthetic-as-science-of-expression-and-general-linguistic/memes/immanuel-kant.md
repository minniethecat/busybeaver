# Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German Enlightenment philosopher whose critical philosophy transformed aesthetics by establishing the autonomy of aesthetic judgment. Croce engages deeply with Kant's legacy, both inheriting and departing from his framework (P02-225).

## Biography

### Early Life

Kant spent his entire life in Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad), where he studied philosophy, mathematics, and natural science at the University of Königsberg. His early work focused on natural philosophy before his "critical turn" in the 1780s.

### Career and Major Works

Kant's critical trilogy established his philosophical system: *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781), *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788), and **Critique of Judgment** (1790). The third *Critique* is the foundational text of modern philosophical aesthetics. In it, Kant distinguished the beautiful from the agreeable and the good, grounding beauty in the free play of imagination and understanding rather than in concepts or desires (P02-225–P02-227).

### Kant and Croce

Croce discusses Kant extensively in his historical survey, noting "Kant and Vico — Identity of the Beautiful and the Artistic Genius in Kant's system" (P02-225). Croce acknowledges Kant's contribution — the autonomy of the aesthetic — but criticizes Kant's residual intellectualism. Kant's "forms of intuition" (space and time) suggest that intuition depends on a priori conceptual structures, which Croce rejects: intuition is autonomous, not organized by categories (002-108).

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Autonomy of Aesthetic Judgment**: Kant established that aesthetic judgment is disinterested — neither cognitive (it makes no claim about what the object is) nor practical (it makes no claim about what the object is good for) (P02-225–P02-229)
- **Forms of Intuition**: Kant's doctrine that space and time are a priori forms of intuition directly influenced Croce's own theory, though Croce transformed Kant's transcendental idealism into a philosophy of spirit (002-108–002-110)
- **Theory of Genius**: Kant defined genius as "the talent that gives the rule to art" — a natural gift through which nature provides the rule — establishing the modern concept of artistic creativity (P02-359)
- **The Sublime**: Kant's distinction between the mathematical and dynamical sublime was a major contribution, though Croce ultimately dissolves the sublime as a distinct category

## Criticism and Reception

> Croce writes that Kant's aesthetics contains "mystical features" (P02-229) — a residual attachment to the thing-in-itself that prevents Kant from fully identifying beauty with the expressive act. "The relations between Schiller and Kant — The aesthetic sphere as play" (P02-233) traces how Kant's immediate successors attempted to overcome these limitations.
