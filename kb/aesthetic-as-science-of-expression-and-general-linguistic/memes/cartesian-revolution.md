# Cartesian Revolution

The Cartesian Revolution refers to the transformation of philosophy initiated by **René Descartes** (1596–1650) that established rationalism as the dominant framework of modern thought. Croce discusses "Cartesianism and imagination" (P02-191) as the intellectualist challenge that **Giambattista Vico** directly opposed.

## Significance for Æsthetic

Descartes' *Discourse on Method* (1637) established clear and distinct ideas as the standard of knowledge, relegating imagination, sensation, and poetry to the realm of the confused and the uncertain. Cartesianism treated intuitive and artistic knowledge as a lower, pre-rational form of cognition — precisely the intellectualist hierarchy that Croce's aesthetics systematically overturns.

**Vico**'s *Scienza Nuova* was directed against Cartesian rationalism: "Vico opposed to all former theories of poetry" (P02-202). Vico argued that poetry is not failed philosophy but an autonomous, pre-logical mode of knowing — the "first wisdom" of humanity. Croce inherits and systematizes Vico's anti-Cartesianism, making the autonomy of intuition from concept the cornerstone of his aesthetic philosophy.

The French aesthetic tradition after Descartes — **Crousaz**, **André**, **Batteux** — continued the Cartesian separation of reason and imagination while gradually recognizing the specificity of aesthetic experience, preparing the ground for Baumgarten's disciplinary innovation (P02-191).
