# Intellectualism

In Croce's *Æsthetic*, intellectualism (*intellettualismo*) names the perennial philosophical error of reducing aesthetic experience to conceptual or logical activity. It is, for Croce, the "chief enemy" of a genuine science of aesthetics — the tendency to treat art as a disguised form of philosophy, science, or rational cognition.

## Definition

Croce identifies intellectualism as the systematic confusion of intuition with concept. "Historical intellectualism," he writes, "has opened the way to the manifold attempts to reduce the aesthetic to the logical" (006-007). The intellectualist position, in its various forms, denies the autonomy of art by treating it as an inferior or confused mode of thought — as philosophy in images, or truth wrapped in fiction.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Intellectualism is not a form of spirit but a philosophical error: the attempt to collapse the first degree of theoretical activity (intuition) into the second (logic):

| Position | What Art Is | Error |
|----------|------------|-------|
| Intellectualism | Confused philosophy | Collapses intuition into concept |
| Croce's position | Autonomous intuition | Preserves the distinction |
| Sensualism | Pleasant sensation | Collapses intuition into sensation |

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes between several varieties of intellectualism. "Æsthetic absolutism (intellectualism)" (P02-135) is the view that beauty is an absolute, conceptual property — an error Croce combats with his relativism of expression. A related but distinct error identifies art with allegory or didactic content.

> "With Cartesian intellectualism is to be found in Boileau" (023-013) — Croce traces the intellectualist tradition through French classicism, where art was judged by its conformity to rational rules rather than by the authenticity of its expression. "We find the same invincible intellectualism in the speculations on" (023-158) beauty throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

Croce contrasts intellectualism with his own position: intuition is not only distinct from but prior to conceptual thought. Art can exist without philosophy, and indeed philosophy always presupposes the expressive-intuitive activity that is art. "It is not, therefore, intellectualism" (012-121) to recognize that art has a cognitive dimension — so long as that dimension is understood as intuitive rather than logical.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Intellectualism in aesthetics can be traced to Plato's treatment of poetry as imitation of imitation, to the medieval doctrine of art as allegory, and to the rationalist aesthetics of Baumgarten (who coined "aesthetics" as the science of "confused cognition"). The tradition reached its apogee in Hegel, for whom art is "the sensuous appearing of the Idea."

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's entire aesthetics can be read as a sustained polemic against "intellectualism and every later and external" (002-149) reduction of art to concept. His innovation is to give intuition positive content — not as a defective form of thought but as a complete and autonomous spiritual activity. This allows him to critique intellectualism not as exaggeration but as a category error: art and philosophy are different spiritual functions, not higher and lower versions of the same thing.

## Criticism and Debate

> Defenders of Hegelian and cognitivist aesthetics have responded that Croce's anti-intellectualism goes too far in severing art from truth and knowledge. If art has no cognitive content whatsoever, they charge, it becomes mere subjective feeling — a charge Croce deflects by insisting on the cognitive value of intuitive knowledge, which is knowledge of the individual, not the universal, but knowledge nonetheless.
