# Sensation

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, sensation (*sensazione*) is the **lower limit of knowledge** — formless psychic matter that the spirit can never apprehend in itself but can only possess through the formative act of intuition.

## Definition

Sensation is "formless matter, which the spirit can never apprehend in itself as simple matter" (002-152). It is the passive, pre-cognitive given — the raw impress of the external world upon consciousness — that has not yet been shaped into an image. Sensation without intuition is below the threshold of knowledge: we do not know sensation; we know only the intuitions that give form to sensation.

## In Croce's System

### Sensation as the Lower Limit

In Croce's epistemology, every act of knowing involves a matter-form relation. The matter is sensation; the form is intuition (space, time, and the expressive act). Just as concepts give form to intuitions at the higher theoretical level, intuitions give form to sensations at the foundational level. Sensation is that which is given; intuition is the act that forms it.

### Intuition and Sensation

Croce devotes a section to "Intuition and sensation" (002-147) precisely because the two have been persistently confused. Some philosophers "look upon intuition as sensation formed and arranged" (002-105) — which Croce partly accepts, since intuition does form sensation. But the key point is that the forming is not an arrangement of sensations but a transformation of sensation into something categorially different: an image, an expression.

## Key Distinctions

**Sensation vs Intuition.** Sensation is passive, formless, pre-cognitive; intuition is active, formative, cognitive. "Intuition has sometimes been confused with simple sensation" (002-191), but Croce insists that sensation is merely the occasion for intuition, not its content. The content of intuition is the expression itself.

**Sensation vs Perception.** Sensation is the raw sensory input; perception is an intuition that refers to a real object. Both are intuitions insofar as they are formed, but sensation is prior to this forming. "Certainly perception is intuition" (002-081) — but sensation is not perception, because sensation has no object.

**Sensation vs Feeling.** Feeling (*sentimento*) is sensation that has been taken up into the aesthetic sphere. Croce distinguishes mere organic sensation (pleasure/pain as bodily states) from aesthetic feeling (the felt quality of an expression). The former is pre-aesthetic; the latter is an aspect of intuition.

## Historical Context

The concept of sensation has been central to philosophy since **Aristotle**'s *De Anima*. **Empiricists** like Locke and Hume made sensation the foundation of all knowledge, while **Kant** argued that sensations require a priori forms (space, time, categories) to become experience. Croce follows Kant in denying that sensation alone constitutes knowledge but departs from Kant by locating the formative act in intuition-expression rather than in transcendental categories (002-108).

## Criticism and Debate

> Critics have questioned whether "formless matter" is a coherent concept — can we meaningfully speak of something that by definition cannot be known? Croce acknowledges that sensation is a "limit concept," not an object of experience. We infer its existence from the fact that intuition is always the forming of something, even if that "something" is never encountered in its unformed state. This Kantian-style transcendental argument has been both defended and challenged.
