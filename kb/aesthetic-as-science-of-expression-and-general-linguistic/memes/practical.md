# Practical Activity

In Croce's philosophy of spirit, practical activity is the volitional form of spiritual life — the domain of will, action, and practical production — standing alongside theoretical activity (intuition and concept) as one of the two fundamental divisions of the human spirit.

## Definition

For Croce, the practical form or activity is the will, understood not in a metaphysical sense (as the ground of the universe) nor in a metaphorical sense (as the energy of spirit in general), but in the ordinary sense of that activity of the spirit which differs from theoretical contemplation and is productive not of knowledge but of actions (P02-049). Action is genuinely action only insofar as it is voluntary; the will to resist, to reject, or to refrain from acting is equally action in this scientific sense. The practical activity is thus the sphere of volition, decision, and doing, as opposed to the sphere of knowing and contemplating. Crucially, Croce insists that practical activity always presupposes theoretical activity: a knowing independent of the will is thinkable, but will independent of knowing is unthinkable — "blind will is not will; true will has eyes" (P02-057).

## In Croce's System

### Place in the Fourfold Division

Croce's system of the spirit is structured by two fundamental dichotomies: *theoretical* versus *practical*, and within each, *individual* versus *universal*. This yields a fourfold division:

| Form | Individual | Universal |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Theoretical** | Intuition (Aesthetic) | Concept (Logic) |
| **Practical** | Economic Activity | Ethical Activity |

The practical activity occupies the lower (individual) and higher (universal) degrees of the volitional half of the spirit. As Croce writes, the practical activity is also divided into a first and second degree, the second implying the first: "The first practical degree is the simply *useful* or *economical* activity; the second the *moral* activity" (P01-013). He draws a parallel with the theoretical domain: "Economy is, as it were, the Æsthetic of practical life; Morality its Logic" (P02-060).

### Relation to the Aesthetic

The relationship between practical activity and aesthetic activity is one of Croce's most crucial and carefully defended positions. He argues that the aesthetic fact is altogether completed in the expressive elaboration of impressions — the practical moment enters only at the stage of *externalization*, that is, the volitional decision to preserve and communicate the inner expression to others. "We cannot will or not will our æsthetic vision: we can however will or not will to externalize it, or rather, to preserve and communicate to others, or not, the externalization produced" (P02-125). This means that art as inner intuition is theoretically prior to and independent of practical will; the practical activity of externalization (technique, production of physical beauty) is a subsequent, distinct moment.

Croce expressly condemns any theory that "annexes the æsthetic activity to the practical, or introduces the laws of the second into the first" (P02-050). The practical element that such theories point to is real, but it lies *outside* the aesthetic fact proper — it accompanies it without being identical to it. This is the principle known as the "practical innocence of art": the theme or content of art cannot be practically or morally judged, because the artistic act is theoretical (intuitive), not volitional.

### The Two Forms: Economic and Ethical

The practical domain is itself structured by a double degree, mirroring the two degrees of the theoretical domain. The first and lower degree is economic activity (the useful, the merely individual volition), while the second and higher degree is ethical activity (the moral, the universal volition). The economic cannot be absorbed into the ethical any more than intuition can be absorbed into the concept: each is a distinct form with its own autonomy.

Economic activity is the will of an individual end, the pursuit of what is useful for the individual agent. Croce insists that the economic has been misunderstood by philosophers who confused it with the technical (which is actually a form of knowledge at the service of action) or with the egoistical (which is a moral judgment, not a descriptive category). "It is possible to will economically without willing morally," he writes, and one can "conduct oneself with perfect economic coherence, while remaining outside the moral law" (P02-083). The economic is thus a legitimate, autonomous form of the spirit, not a deficient mode of the moral.

Ethical activity (the moral will) is the second and higher degree, which presupposes economic willing but transcends it toward the universal. The relation is one of implication, not opposition: abstract moralism that denies the economic is as erroneous as economism that denies the moral. Both are integral moments of practical spirit.

## Key Distinctions

**Practical vs. Theoretical.** This is the fundamental distinction of Croce's system. The theoretical form appropriates the universe through knowledge (intuition and concept); the practical form changes the universe through will and action. The theoretical is the basis of the practical: "Man understands things with the theoretical form, with the practical form he changes them; with the one he appropriates the universe, with the other he creates it" (P02-057). But they stand in a relation of double degree: theoretical activity can exist without being followed by practical activity, but practical activity cannot exist without preceding theoretical activity.

**Economic vs. Ethical.** Within the practical, these two degrees are distinct but not opposed. The economic is the will of the individual end; the ethical is the will of the universal end. The ethical presupposes the economic: one cannot will the universal without first being capable of coherent individual volition. This is a distinction of form, not of content — the same action can be both economically coherent and morally good, but it is judged according to two distinct criteria.

**Practical Judgments vs. Theoretical Judgments.** Croce criticizes the notion of "practical judgments" or "judgments of value" as a separate category interpolated between theory and practice. He argues that such judgments are not a third moment between knowing and doing; rather, "a good or useful action is an action willed" — the judgment follows from the will, not the will from the judgment (P02-057). The idea that we desire things because we know them to be good is an illusion: "We do not desire things because we know them to be good or useful; but we know them to be good and useful, because we desire them" (P02-063).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The distinction between theoretical and practical activity has deep roots in Western philosophy, from Aristotle's division of the sciences into theoretical, practical, and productive, through Kant's separation of pure and practical reason. The Kantian framework, in particular, strongly influenced Croce's Italian idealist predecessors. However, Croce found that previous systems had not given adequate recognition to the economic as an autonomous form of the spirit. The useful or economic had been "left to wander about in the prolegomena to treatises on political economy, often vague and but little developed" (P02-060), confused with technique on one side and egoism on the other.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's great innovation was to give the economic activity its proper place in the system of the spirit as a distinct, autonomous form co-equal with intuition, concept, and morality. By establishing the fourfold division (intuition, concept, economic, ethical) and the principle of distincts (the theory of distinct moments of the spirit that are not in opposition but in a relation of implication), he resolved what he saw as Hegel's confusion of opposites with distincts. He argued that earlier philosophers had conflated the economic with the technical (a form of knowledge) or with the egoistical (a moral category), thereby failing to recognize the economic as a sui generis form of practical volition. This reconceptualization laid the groundwork for his famous — and controversial — claim that art is "practically innocent," a claim that depends entirely on the rigorous separation of the theoretical from the practical.

## Criticism and Debate

Croce's sharp separation of theoretical (especially aesthetic) activity from practical activity has drawn sustained criticism. Critics argue that art is always embedded in practical life — that the artist's choices, the work's social context, and its effects on audiences are irreducibly practical. Some contend that by denying art any practical or moral dimension, Croce strips it of its seriousness as a human endeavor. Others object that the economic activity, by being elevated to an autonomous spiritual form, receives a philosophical justification that risks equating all forms of practical rationality with mere utility.

Croce himself was aware of the apparent paradox, especially the objection that "men of action, practical men *par excellence*, are the least disposed to contemplate and to theorize." His response is that such objections rest on merely empirical and quantitative distinctions, not philosophical ones:

> "Certainly, the practical man has no need of a philosophical system in order to act; nor is his action determined or retarded by theoretical doubts about abstract points. But he cannot act without knowing the concrete situation; he cannot take a step without having a perception, without having an intuition of the actual conditions, without knowing, in a practical manner, the circumstances upon which he must act and the means of action. Practical action is preceded by knowledge." (P02-057)

The debate continues to resonate in contemporary aesthetics and philosophy of action, where the relation between knowledge, will, and value remains a central concern.
