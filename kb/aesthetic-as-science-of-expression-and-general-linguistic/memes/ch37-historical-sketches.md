[037-001] *Neo-criticism and empiricism.*

[037-002] The neo-critical or neo-Kantian movement was powerless to make headway

[037-003] against hedonistic, psychological and moralistic views of the æsthetic

[037-004] fact, although it made every effort to save the concept of spirit from

[037-005] the invading rush of naturalism and materialism.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_1_687) [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] bequeathed to

[037-006] neo-criticism his own failure to understand creative imagination, and

[037-007] the neo-Kantians do not seem to have had the faintest notion of any

[037-008] form of cognition other than the intellectual.

[037-009] *Kirchmann.*

[037-010] Amongst German philosophers of any renown who clung to æsthetic

[037-011] sensationalism and psychologism was Kirchmann, promoter of a so-called

[037-012] realism, and author of *Æsthetic on a Realistic Basis* (1868).[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_2_688)

[037-013] In his doctrine the æsthetic fact is an [[representation|image]] (*Bild*) of a real;

[037-014] an animated (*seelenvolles*) image, purified and strengthened, that

[037-015] is, idealized, and divided into the image of pleasure, which is the

[037-016] beautiful, and that of pain, which is the ugly. [[beauty|Beauty]] admits of a

[037-017] threefold series of varieties or modifications, being determined

[037-018] according to the content as sublime, comic, tragic, etc.; according

[037-019] to the image, as beauty of nature or of art; and according to the

[037-020] idealization as idealistic or naturalistic, formal or spiritual,

[037-021] symbolical or classical. Not having grasped the nature of æsthetic

[037-022] objectification, Kirchmann takes the trouble to draw up a new

[037-023] psychological category of ideal or apparent feelings, arising from

[037-024] artistic images and being attenuations of the feelings of real life.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_3_689)

[037-025] *Metaphysic translated into Psychology. Vischer.*

[037-026] To the evolution or involution of the Herbartians into physiologists

[037-027] of æsthetic pleasure corresponds a similar evolution or involution of

[037-028] the idealists into adherents of psychologism. The first place must be

[037-029] given to the veteran Theodor Vischer, who in a criticism of his own

[037-030] work pronounced Æsthetic to be "the union of mimics and harmonics"

[037-031] (*vereinte Mimik und Harmonik*), and Beauty the "harmony of the

[037-032] universe," never actually realized because realized only at infinity,

[037-033] so that when we think to seize it in the Beautiful, we are under an

[037-034] illusion: a transcendent illusion, which is the very essence of the

[037-035] æsthetic fact.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_4_690) His son Robert Yischer coined the word *Einfühlung*

[037-036] to express the life with which man endows natural objects by means of

[037-037] the æsthetic process.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_5_691) Volkelt, when treating of the *Symbol*[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_6_692) and

[037-038] joining symbolism to pantheism, opposed associationism and favoured a

[037-039] natural teleology immanent in Beauty.

[037-040] *Siebeck.*

[037-041] The Herbartian Siebeck (1875) abandoned the formalistic theory and

[037-042] tried to explain the fact of beauty by the concept of the appearance of

[037-043] personality.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_7_693) He distinguishes between objects which please by their

[037-044] content alone (sensuous pleasures), those which please by form alone

[037-045] (moral facts), and those which please by the connexion of content with

[037-046] form (organic and æsthetic facts). In organic facts the form is not

[037-047] outside the content, but is the expression of the reciprocal action and

[037-048] conjunction of the constitutive elements: whereas in æsthetic facts

[037-049] the form is outside the content, and as it were its mere surface; not

[037-050] a means to the end, but an end in itself. Æsthetic intuition is a

[037-051] relation between the sensible and the spiritual, matter and spirit,

[037-052] and is thus form regarded as the appearance of personality. Æsthetic

[037-053] pleasure arises from the spirit's consciousness of discovering itself

[037-054] in the sensible. Siebeck borrows the theory of modifications of the

[037-055] beautiful from the metaphysical idealists, who held that only in such

[037-056] modifications can beauty be found in the concrete, just as humanity can

[037-057] only exist as a man of determinate race and nationality. The sublime is

[037-058] that species of beauty wherein the formal moment of circumscription is

[037-059] lost, and is therefore the unlimited, which is a kind of extensive or

[037-060] intensive infinity; the tragic arises when the harmony is not given but

[037-061] is the result of conflict and development; the comic is a relation of

[037-062] the small to the great; and so on. These traces of idealism, together

[037-063] with his firm hold on the Kantian and Herbartian absoluteness of the

[037-064] judgement of taste, make it impossible to regard Siebeck's Æsthetic as

[037-065] purely psychological and empirical and wholly devoid of philosophical

[037-066] elements.

[037-067] *M. Diez.*

[037-068] It is the same with Diez, who, in his *Theory of Feeling as Foundation

[037-069] of Æsthetic* (1892),[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_8_694) tries to explain the [[art|artistic activity]] as

[037-070] a return to the ideal of feeling (*Ideal des fühlenden Geistes*),

[037-071] parallel with science (ideal of thought), morality (ideal of will)

[037-072] and religion (ideal of personality). But whatever is this so-called

[037-073] feeling? is it the empirical feeling of the psychologists, irreducible

[037-074] to an ideal, or the mystic faculty of communication and conjunction

[037-075] with the Infinite and the Absolute? the absurd "pleasure-value" of

[037-076] Fechner, or the "judgement" of Kant? One is inclined to say that

[037-077] these writers, and others like them, still under the influence of

[037-078] metaphysical views, lack the courage of their opinions: they feel

[037-079] themselves to be in an atmosphere of hostility and speak under

[037-080] reservations or compromises. The psychologist Jodi asserts the

[037-081] existence of elementary æsthetic feelings, as discovered by [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]],

[037-082] and defines them as "immediate excitations not resting upon associative

[037-083] or reproductive activity or on the [[imagination|fancy]]," although "in ultimate

[037-084] analysis they must be reduced to the same principles."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_9_695)

[037-085] *Psychological tendency. Teodor Lipps.*

[037-086] The purely psychological and associationistic tendency becomes clearly

[037-087] defined in Professor Teodor Lipps and his school. Lipps criticizes

[037-088] and rejects a whole series of æsthetic theories: (*a*) of play;

[037-089] (*b*) of pleasure; (*c*) of art as recognition of real life, even

[037-090] if displeasing; (*d*) of emotion and passional excitation; (*e*)

[037-091] syncretism, attributing to art beside the primary purpose of play and

[037-092] pleasure the further ends of recognition of life, in its reality,

[037-093] revelation of individuality, commotion, freedom from a weight, or free

[037-094] play of the imagination. His theory differs little at bottom from that

[037-095] of Jouffroy, for in his thesis he assumes artistic beauty to be the

[037-096] sympathetic. "The object of sympathy is our objectified ego, transposed

[037-097] into others and therefore discovered in them. We feel ourselves in

[037-098] others and we feel others in ourselves. In others, or by means of them,

[037-099] we feel ourselves happy, free, enlarged, elevated, or the contrary

[037-100] of all these. The æsthetic feeling of sympathy is not a mere mode of

[037-101] æsthetic enjoyment, it is that enjoyment itself. All æsthetic enjoyment

[037-102] is founded, in the last analysis, singly and wholly upon sympathy; even

[037-103] that caused by geometrical, architectonic, tectonic, ceramic, etc.,

[037-104] lines and forms." "Whenever in a work of art we find a personality (not

[037-105] a defect of the man, but something positively human) which harmonizes

[037-106] with and awakes an echo in the possibilities and tendencies of our

[037-107] own life and vital activities: whenever we find positive, objective

[037-108] humanity, pure and free from all real interests lying outside the work

[037-109] of art, as art only can reproduce it and æsthetic contemplation alone

[037-110] can demand; the harmony, the resonance, fills us with joy. The value

[037-111] of personality is ethical value: outside it there is no possibility

[037-112] or determination of ethical character. All artistic and in general

[037-113] æsthetic enjoyment is, therefore, the enjoyment of something which has

[037-114] ethical value (*eines ethische Werthvollen*); not as element of a

[037-115] complex, but as object of æsthetic intuition."[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_10_696)

[037-116] The æsthetic fact is thus deprived of all its own value and allowed

[037-117] merely a reflexion from the value of morality.

[037-118] Without lingering over Lipps's pupils (such as Stern and others[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_11_697))

[037-119] and writers of similar tendency (such as Biese, with his theory

[037-120] of anthropomorphism and universal metaphor;[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_12_698) or Konrad Lange,

[037-121] who propounds a thesis that art is conscious self-deception),[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_13_699)

[037-122] we will call attention to Professor Karl Groos (1892), who comes

[037-123] within measurable distance of the concept of æsthetic activity as

[037-124] a theoretic value.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_14_700) Between the two poles of consciousness,

[037-125] sensibility and intellect, are several intermediate grades, amongst

[037-126] which lies intuition or fancy, whose product, the image or appearance

[037-127] (*Schein*), is midway between sensation and concept. The image is

[037-128] full like sensation, but regulated like the concept; it has neither

[037-129] the inexhaustible richness of the former, or the barren nudity of the

[037-130] latter. Of the nature of image or appearance is the æsthetic fact;

[037-131] which is distinguished from the simple, ordinary image not by its

[037-132] quality, but by its intensity alone: the æsthetic image is merely a

[037-133] simple image occupying the summit of consciousness. Representations

[037-134] pass through consciousness like a crowd of people hurrying over a

[037-135] bridge, each bent on his own business; but when a passer-by halts on

[037-136] the bridge and looks at the scene, then is it holiday, then arises the

[037-137] æsthetic fact. This is therefore not passivity but activity; according

[037-138] to the formula adopted by Groos it is internal imitation (*innere

[037-139] Nachahnung*).[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_15_701) It may be objected against the theory that every

[037-140] image, so far as it is an image at all, must occupy the summit of

[037-141] consciousness if only for an instant; and that the mere image is either

[037-142] the product of an activity just as is the æsthetic image, or it is not

[037-143] a real image at all. It may also be objected that the definition of the

[037-144] image as something sharing in the nature of sensation and concept may

[037-145] lead back to intellectual intuition and the other mysterious faculties

[037-146] of the metaphysical school, for which Groos professes abhorrence. His

[037-147] division of the æsthetic fact into form and content is even less happy.

[037-148] He recognizes four classes of content: associative (in the strict

[037-149] sense), symbolic, typical, individual:[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_16_702) and into his inquiries

[037-150] he introduces, quite unnecessarily, the concepts of infusion of

[037-151] personality and of play. In connexion with the latter he remarks that

[037-152] "internal imitation is the noblest game of man,"[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_17_703) and adds that "the

[037-153] concept of play applies fully to contemplation, but not to æsthetic

[037-154] production, save in the case of primitive peoples."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_18_704)

[037-155] *The modifications of the Beautiful in Groos and Lipps.*

[037-156] Groos does however free himself from the "modifications of Beauty,"

[037-157] because, æsthetic activity having been identified with internal

[037-158] imitation, it is clear that whatever is not internal imitation is

[037-159] excluded from that activity as something different. "All Beauty

[037-160] (beauty understood in the sense of 'sympathetic') belongs to the

[037-161] æsthetic activity, but not every æsthetic fact is beautiful." Beauty,

[037-162] then, is the representation of the sensuously pleasant; ugliness, the

[037-163] representation of the unpleasant; [[sublime|the sublime]], that of a mighty thing

[037-164] (*Gewaltiges*) in a simple form; the comic, that of an inferiority

[037-165] which arouses in us a pleasing sense of our own superiority. And so

[037-166] forth.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_19_705) With great good sense Groos holds up to derision the office

[037-167] assigned to the ugly by Schasler and Hartmann with their superficial

[037-168] dialectic. To say that an ellipse contains an element of ugliness

[037-169] in comparison with the circle because it is symmetrical about its

[037-170] two axes only and not about infinite diameters is like saying "wine

[037-171] has a relatively unpleasant taste because in it is lacking (*ist

[037-172] aufgehoben*) the pleasant taste of beer."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_20_706) Lipps too, in his

[037-173] writings upon Æsthetic, recognizes that the comic (of which he gives an

[037-174] accurate psychological analysis)[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_21_707) has in itself no æsthetic value;

[037-175] but his moralistic views lead him to outline a theory of it not unlike

[037-176] that of the overcoming of the ugly; he explains it as a process leading

[037-177] to a higher æsthetic value (*i.e.* sympathy).[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_22_708)

[037-178] *E. Viron and the double form of Æsthetic.*

[037-179] Work such as that of Groos and, occasionally, of Lipps is of some

[037-180] value towards the elimination of errors, as well as confining æsthetic

[037-181] research to the field of internal analysis. Merit of the same kind

[037-182] belongs to the work of a Frenchman, Véron,[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_23_709) who controverts the

[037-183] Absolute Beauty of academical Æsthetic and, after accusing Taine of

[037-184] confounding Art with Science and Æsthetic with Logic, remarks that if

[037-185] it be the duty of art to make manifest the essence of things, their

[037-186] one dominating quality, then "the greatest artists would be those who

[037-187] have best succeeded in exhibiting this essence ... and the greatest

[037-188] works would resemble each other more closely than any others and would

[037-189] clearly demonstrate their common identity, whereas the exact opposite

[037-190] happens."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_24_710) But one looks in vain for scientific method in Véron; a

[037-191] precursor of Guyau,[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_25_711) he asserts that art is at bottom two different

[037-192] things; there are two arts: one decorative, whose end is beauty, that

[037-193] is to say the pleasure of eye and ear resulting from determinate

[037-194] dispositions of fines, forms, colours, sounds, rhythms, movements,

[037-195] fight and shade, without necessary interventions of ideas and feelings,

[037-196] and capable of being studied by Optics and Acoustics: the other,

[037-197] expressive, which gives "the agitated expression of human personality."

[037-198] He considers that decorative art prevails in the ancient world, and

[037-199] expressive art in the modern.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_26_712)

[037-200] We cannot here examine in detail the æsthetic theories of artists

[037-201] and men of letters; the scientific and historicist prejudices, the

[037-202] theory of experiment and human document, which underlie the realism of

[037-203] Zola, or the moralism which underlies the problem-art of Ibsen and the

[037-204] Scandinavian school. Gustave Flaubert wrote of art profoundly, better

[037-205] perhaps than any other Frenchman has ever written, not in special

[037-206] treatises but throughout his letters, which were published after his

[037-207] death.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_27_713)

[037-208] *L. Tolstoy.*

[037-209] Under the influence of Véron and his hatred for the concept of beauty,

[037-210] Leo Tolstoy wrote his book on art,[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_28_714) which, according to the great

[037-211] Russian artist, communicates feelings in the same way in which words

[037-212] communicate thoughts. The meaning of this theory is made clear by the

[037-213] parallel he drew between Art and Science, and his conclusion that "the

[037-214] mission of art is to render sensible and capable of assimilation that

[037-215] which could not be assimilated under the form of argumentation";

[037-216] and that "true science examines truths considered as important for a

[037-217] certain society at a given epoch and fixes them in the consciousness

[037-218] of man, whereas art transports them from the domain of knowledge to

[037-219] that of feeling."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_29_715) There is therefore no such thing as art for art's

[037-220] sake, any more than science for science' sake. Every human function

[037-221] should be directed to increase morality and to suppress violence.

[037-222] This amounts to saying that nearly all art, from the beginning of the

[037-223] world, is false. Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes, Dante,

[037-224] Tasso, Milton, Shakespeare, Raphæl, Michæl Angelo, Bach, Beethoven are

[037-225] (according to Tolstoy) "artificial reputations created by critics."[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_30_716)

[037-226] *F. Nietzsche.*

[037-227] Amongst artists rather than amongst philosophers must be reckoned

[037-228] Friedrich Nietzsche, whom we should wrong (as we said of Ruskin) by

[037-229] trying to expound his æsthetic doctrines in scientific language and

[037-230] then holding them up to the facile criticism which, so translated,

[037-231] they would draw upon themselves. In none of his books, not even in

[037-232] his first, *The Birth of Tragedy,*[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_31_717) in spite of the title, does he

[037-233] offer us a real theory of art; what appears to be theory is the mere

[037-234] expression of the author's feelings and tendencies. He shows a kind

[037-235] of anxiety concerning the value and aim of art and the problem of its

[037-236] inferiority or superiority to science and philosophy, a state of mind

[037-237] characteristic of the Romantic period of which Nietzsche was, in many

[037-238] respects, a belated but magnificent representative. To [[romanticism|Romanticism]], as

[037-239] well as to [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], belong the elements of thought which issued in

[037-240] the distinction between Apollinesque art (that of serene contemplation,

[037-241] to which belong the epic and sculpture) and Dionysiac art (the art of

[037-242] agitation and tumult, such as music and the drama). The thought is

[037-243] vague and does not bear criticism; but it is supported by a flight of

[037-244] inspiration which lifts the mind to a spiritual region seldom if ever

[037-245] reached again in the second half of the nineteenth century.

[037-246] *An æsthetician of music: E. Hanslick.*

[037-247] The most notable æsthetic students of that time were perhaps a group

[037-248] of persons engaged in constructing theories of particular arts.

[037-249] And since—as we have seen[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_32_718)—philosophical laws or theories of

[037-250] individual arts are inconceivable, it was inevitable that the ideas

[037-251] presented by such thinkers should be (as indeed they are) nothing more

[037-252] than general æsthetic conclusions. First may be mentioned the acute

[037-253] Bohemian critic Eduard Hanslick, who published his work *On Musical

[037-254] Beauty* in 1854; it was often reprinted and was translated into various

[037-255] languages.[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_33_719) Hanslick waged war against Richard Wagner and in general

[037-256] against the pretension of finding concepts, feelings and other definite

[037-257] contents in music. "In the most insignificant musical works, where the

[037-258] most powerful microscope can discover nothing, we are now asked to

[037-259] recognize a *Night Before the Battle,* a *Summer Night in Norway,* a

[037-260] *Longing for the Sea,* or some such absurdity, should the cover have

[037-261] the audacity to affirm that this is the subject of the piece."[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_34_720) With

[037-262] equal vivacity he protests against the sentimental hearers who, instead

[037-263] of enjoying the work of art, set themselves to extract pathological

[037-264] effects of passionate excitement and practical activity. If it be true

[037-265] that Greek music produced effects of this kind, "if it needed but a

[037-266] few Phrygian strains to animate troops with courage in the face of

[037-267] the enemy, or a melody in the Dorian mode to ensure the fidelity of

[037-268] a wife whose husband was far away, then the loss of Greek music is

[037-269] a melancholy thing for generals and husbands; but æstheticians and

[037-270] composers need not regret it."[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_35_721) "If every senseless *Requiem,* every

[037-271] noisy funeral march, every wailing *Adagio* had the power of depressing

[037-272] us, who could put up with existence under such conditions? But let a

[037-273] real musical work confront us, clear-eyed and glowing with beauty, and

[037-274] we feel ourselves enslaved by its invincible fascination even if its

[037-275] material is all the sorrows of the age."[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_36_722)

[037-276] *Hanslick's concept of form.*

[037-277] Hanslick maintained that the sole aim of music is form, musical

[037-278] beauty. This affirmation won him the goodwill of the Herbartians, who

[037-279] hastened to welcome such a vigorous and unexpected ally; by way of

[037-280] returning the compliment, Hanslick felt obliged in later editions of

[037-281] his work to mention Herbart himself and his faithful disciple Robert

[037-282] [[robert-zimmermann|Zimmermann]] who had given (so he said) "full development to the great

[037-283] æsthetic principle of [[form|Form]]."[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_37_723) The praises of the Herbartians and the

[037-284] courteous declarations of Hanslick both arose from a misunderstanding:

[037-285] for the words "beauty" and "form" have one meaning for the former and

[037-286] quite another for the latter. Hanslick never thought that symmetry,

[037-287] purely acoustical relations and pleasures of the ear constituted

[037-288] musical beauty;[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_38_724) mathematics, he held, are utterly useless to

[037-289] musical Æsthetic.[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_39_725) Musical beauty is spiritual and significative: it

[037-290] has thoughts, undoubtedly; but those thoughts are musical. "Sonorous

[037-291] forms are not empty, but perfectly filled; they cannot be compared

[037-292] with simple lines delimiting a space; they are the spirit assuming body

[037-293] and extracting from itself the stuff of its own incarnation. Rather

[037-294] than an arabesque, music is a picture; but a picture whose subject

[037-295] can neither be expressed in words nor enclosed in precise concept.

[037-296] There are in music both meaning and connexion, but these are of a

[037-297] specifically musical nature; music is a language we understand and

[037-298] speak, but which it is not possible to translate."[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_40_726) Hanslick asserts

[037-299] that though music does not portray the quality of feelings, it does

[037-300] portray their dynamic aspect or tone: if not the substantives, then

[037-301] the adjectives: it depicts not "murmuring tenderness" or "impetuous

[037-302] courage," but the "murmuring" and the "impetuous."[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_41_727) The backbone of

[037-303] the book is the denial that form and content can ever be separated in

[037-304] music. "In music there can be no content in opposition to the form,

[037-305] since there can be no form outside the content." "Take a motive, the

[037-306] first that comes into your head; what is its content, what its form?

[037-307] where does this begin, and that end? ... What do you wish to call

[037-308] content? The sounds? Very well: but they have already received a form.

[037-309] What will you call form? Also the sounds? but they are form already

[037-310] filled; form supplied with content."[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_42_728) Such observations denote

[037-311] acute penetration of the nature of art, though not scientifically

[037-312] formulated or framed into a system. Hanslick thought he was dealing

[037-313] with peculiarities of music,[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_43_729) instead of with the universal and

[037-314] constitutive character of every form of art, and this prevented him

[037-315] from taking larger views.

[037-316] *Æstheticians of the figurative arts. C. Fiedler.*

[037-317] Another specialist æsthetician is Conrad Fiedler, author of many

[037-318] essays on the figurative arts, the most important being his *Origin

[037-319] of Artistic Activity* (1887).[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_44_730) No one, perhaps, has better or

[037-320] more eloquently emphasized the activistic character of art, which

[037-321] he compares with language. "Art begins exactly where intuition

[037-322] (perception) ends. The artist is not differentiated from other people

[037-323] by any special perceptive attitude enabling him to perceive more or

[037-324] with greater intensity, or endowing his eye with any special power

[037-325] of selecting, collecting, transforming, ennobling or illuminating;

[037-326] but rather by his peculiar gift of being able to pass immediately

[037-327] from perception to intuitive expression; his relation with nature is

[037-328] not perceptive, but expressive." "A man standing passively at gaze

[037-329] may well imagine himself in possession of the visible world as an

[037-330] immense, rich, varied whole: the entire absence of fatigue with which

[037-331] he traverses the infinite mass of visual impressions, the rapidity

[037-332] with which representations dart across his consciousness, convince

[037-333] him that he stands in the midst of an immense visible world, although

[037-334] he may quite well be unable at any one instant to represent it to

[037-335] himself as a whole. But this world, so great, so rich, so immeasurable,

[037-336] disappears the moment art seeks to become its master. The very first

[037-337] effort to emerge from this twilight and arrive at clear vision

[037-338] restricts the circle of things to be seen. Artistic activity may be

[037-339] conceived as continuation of that concentration by which consciousness

[037-340] makes the first step towards clear vision, which it reaches only by

[037-341] self-limitation." Spiritual process and bodily process are here an

[037-342] indivisible whole, which is expression.

[037-343] *[[intuition|Intuition]] and [[expression|Expression]].*

[037-344] "This activity, simply because it is spiritual, must consist of forms

[037-345] wholly determinate, tangible, sensibly demonstrative." Art is not in

[037-346] a state of subjection to science. Like the man of science, the artist

[037-347] desires to escape from the natural perceptive state and to make the

[037-348] world his own; but there are regions to which we can penetrate not

[037-349] by the forms of thought and science but only through art. Art is,

[037-350] strictly speaking, not imitation of nature; for what is nature save

[037-351] this confused mass of perceptions and representations, whose real

[037-352] poverty has been demonstrated already? In another sense, however, art

[037-353] may be called imitation of nature inasmuch as its aim is not to expound

[037-354] concepts or to arouse emotions, that is to create values of intellect

[037-355] and feeling. Art does create both these values, if you like to say so;

[037-356] but only in one quite peculiar quality, which consists in complete

[037-357] visibility (*Sichtbarkeit*). Here we have the same sane conception, the

[037-358] same lively comprehension of the true nature of art which we found in

[037-359] Hanslick, only expressed in a more rigorous and philosophical manner.

[037-360] With Fiedler is connected his friend Adolf Hildebrand, who brought into

[037-361] high relief the activistic, or architectonic as opposed to imitative,

[037-362] character of art, illustrating his theoretical discussions especially

[037-363] from sculpture, the art which he himself followed.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_45_731)

[037-364] *Narrow limits of these theories.*

[037-365] What we chiefly miss in Fiedler and others of the same tendency is the

[037-366] conception of the æsthetic fact not as something exceptional, produced

[037-367] by exceptionally gifted men, but as a ceaseless activity of man as

[037-368] such; for man possesses the world, so far as he does possess it, only

[037-369] in the form of representation-expressions, and only knows in so far as

[037-370] he creates.[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_46_732) Nor are these writers justified in treating language

[037-371] as parallel with art, or art with language; for comparisons are drawn

[037-372] between things at least partially different, whereas art and language

[037-373] are identical.

[037-374] *H. Bergson.*

[037-375] The same criticism can be made in the case of the [[france|French]] philosopher

[037-376] Bergson, who in his book on *Laughter*[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_47_733) states a theory of art very

[037-377] similar to that of Fiedler and makes the same mistake of conceiving the

[037-378] artistic faculty as something distinct and exceptional in comparison

[037-379] with the language of everyday use. In ordinary life, says Bergson, the

[037-380] individuality of things escapes us; we see only as much of them as

[037-381] our practical needs demand. Language helps this simplification; since

[037-382] all names, proper names excepted, are names of kinds or classes. Now

[037-383] and then, however, nature, as if in a fit of absence of mind, creates

[037-384] souls of a more divisible and detached kind (artists), who discover

[037-385] and reveal the riches hidden under the colourless signs and labels

[037-386] of everyday life, and help others (non-artists) to catch a glimpse of

[037-387] what they themselves see, employing for this purpose colours, forms,

[037-388] rhythmic connexions of words, and those rhythms of life and breath even

[037-389] more intimate to man, the sounds and notes of music.

[037-390] *Attempts to return to [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]]. C. Hermann.*

[037-391] A healthy return to Baumgarten, a revival and correction of the old

[037-392] philosopher's theories in the light of later discoveries, might

[037-393] perhaps have given Æsthetic some assistance, after the collapse of

[037-394] the old idealistic metaphysic, towards thinking the concept of art

[037-395] in its universality and discovering its identity with pure and true

[037-396] intuitive knowledge. But Conrad Hermann, who preached the return to

[037-397] Baumgarten[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_48_734) in 1876, did bad service to what might have been a good

[037-398] cause. According to him Æsthetic and Logic are normative sciences;

[037-399] but Logic does not contain, as does Æsthetic, "a definite category

[037-400] of external objects exclusively and specifically adequate to the

[037-401] faculty of thought"; and on the other hand "the products and results

[037-402] of scientific thought are not so external and sensibly intuitive as

[037-403] those of artistic invention." Logic and Æsthetic alike refer not to the

[037-404] empirical thinking and feeling of the soul, but to pure and absolute

[037-405] sensation and thought. Art constructs a representation standing midway

[037-406] between the individual and the universal. Beauty expresses specific

[037-407] perfection, the essential or, so to speak, the rightful (*seinsollend*)

[037-408] character of things. Form is "the external sensible limit, or mode of

[037-409] appearance of a thing, in opposition to the kernel of the thing itself

[037-410] and to its essential and substantial content." [[content|Content]] and form are

[037-411] both æsthetic, and the æsthetic interest concerns the entirety of the

[037-412] beautiful object. The artistic activity has no special organ such as

[037-413] thought possesses in speech. The æsthetician, like the lexicographer,

[037-414] has the task of compiling a dictionary of tones and colours and of

[037-415] the different meanings which may possibly be attached to them.[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_49_735)

[037-416] We can see that Hermann accepted side by side the most inconsistent

[037-417] propositions. He welcomes even the æsthetic law of the golden section,

[037-418] and applies it to tragedy; the longer segment of the Une is the tragic

[037-419] hero; the punishment which overtakes him (the entire line) exceeds his

[037-420] crime in the same proportion in which he oversteps the common measure

[037-421] (the shorter segment of the line).[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_50_736) It reads almost like a joke.

[037-422] Without direct reference to Baumgarten, a proposal that Æsthetic be

[037-423] reformed and treated as the "science of intuitive knowledge" was made

[037-424] in a miserable little work by one Willy Nef (1898),[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_51_737) who makes the

[037-425] dumb animals share his "intuitive knowledge," in which he distinguishes

[037-426] a formal side (intuition) and a material side or content (knowledge),

[037-427] and considers the everyday relations between men, their games and their

[037-428] art, as belonging to intuitive knowledge.

[037-429] *Eclecticism. B. Bosanquet.*

[037-430] The [[england|English]] historian of Æsthetic, Bosanquet (1892) tried to find

[037-431] a reconciliation between content and form in unity of expression.

[037-432] "Beauty," says Bosanquet in the Introduction to his *History,* "is

[037-433] that which has characteristic and individual expressiveness for

[037-434] sensuous perception or imagination, subject to the conditions of

[037-435] general or abstract expressiveness by the same means." In another

[037-436] passage he observes: "The difficulty of real Æsthetic is to show how

[037-437] the combination of decorative forms in characteristic representations,

[037-438] by intensifying the essential character immanent in them from the

[037-439] beginning, subordinates them to a central signification which stands

[037-440] to their complex combination as their abstract signification stands to

[037-441] each one of them taken singly."[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_52_738) But the problem, as propounded in a

[037-442] way suggested by the antithesis between the two schools (contentism and

[037-443] formalism) of German Æsthetic, is in our opinion insoluble.

[037-444] *Æsthetic of expression: present state.*

[037-445] [[francesco-de-sanctis|De Sanctis]] founded no school of æsthetic science in [[italy|Italy]]. His thought

[037-446] was quickly misunderstood and mutilated by those who presumed to

[037-447] correct it, and, in fact, only returned to the outworn rhetorical

[037-448] conception of art as consisting of a little content and a little

[037-449] form. Only within the last ten years has there been a renewal of

[037-450] philosophical studies, arising out of discussions concerning the nature

[037-451] of history[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_53_739) and the relation in which it stands to art and science,

[037-452] and nourished by the controversy excited by the publication of De

[037-453] Sanctis' posthumous works.[[54]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_54_740) The same problem of the relation between

[037-454] history and science, and their difference or antithesis, reappeared

[037-455] also in [[germany|Germany]], but without being put in its true connexion with the

[037-456] problem of Æsthetic.[[55]](280320132259829826_54618-h-25.htm.html#Footnote_55_741) These inquiries and discussions, and the

[037-457] revival of a Linguistic impregnated by philosophy in the work of Paul

[037-458] and some others, appear to us to offer much more favourable ground for

[037-459] the scientific development of Æsthetic than can be found on the summits

[037-460] of mysticism or the low plains of positivism and sensationalism.
