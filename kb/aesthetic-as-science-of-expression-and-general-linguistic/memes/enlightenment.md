# Enlightenment

The Enlightenment (18th century) was the intellectual movement that established aesthetics as an autonomous philosophical discipline. Croce's *History of Æsthetic* treats the Enlightenment as the period when aesthetics achieved self-consciousness as a distinct branch of philosophy.

## Significance for Æsthetic

**Alexander Baumgarten** coined the term "aesthetic" in 1735, defining it as the science of sensible cognition — the sister discipline to logic. Though Croce critiques Baumgarten's intellectualism, the act of naming aesthetics was itself a decisive philosophical event (P02-195).

**Immanuel Kant**'s *Critique of Judgment* (1790) established the autonomy of aesthetic judgment — the claim that beauty is neither cognitive (it makes no claim about what the object is) nor practical (it makes no claim about what the object is good for) but reflective, grounded in the free play of imagination and understanding (P02-225–P02-229).

The British Enlightenment — **Hume**, **Burke**, **Home** — developed an empirical aesthetics grounded in psychology rather than metaphysics, which Croce critiques as "eclecticism and sensationalism" (P02-217) while acknowledging its contribution to the analysis of aesthetic experience.
