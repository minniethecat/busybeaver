# Sublime

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, the sublime (*sublime*) is not an autonomous aesthetic category co-equal with the beautiful but a **psychological variety of beauty** — of interest to empirical criticism but not to philosophical aesthetics.

## Definition

Croce treats the sublime as a traditional category that dissolves under philosophical analysis. Since beauty is successful expression, there is no room for a second, independent aesthetic quality. What has been called "sublime" is simply beauty of a certain character — beauty that involves vastness, power, or transcendence. The philosophical structure (successful expression) is the same; only the psychological content differs.

## In Croce's System

### Sublimity as Pseudo-Category

The sublime-ugly-beautiful triad was central to 18th-century aesthetics, but Croce's identity of beauty with successful expression eliminates the need for additional categories. A "sublime" landscape is simply one whose expression succeeds in a particular way — involving vastness, awe, or limitlessness. The category is empirical, not philosophical.

### The Pseudo-Concept

Croce classifies the sublime as a "pseudo-concept" — a term that appears to name a universal but actually refers to a class of particulars grouped by family resemblance. Philosophical aesthetics deals with the universal (beauty = successful expression); empirical criticism deals with varieties like the sublime, the graceful, the tragic, and the comic.

## Key Distinctions

**Sublime vs Beautiful (Traditional).** In traditional aesthetics (Burke, Kant), the beautiful pleases through form and harmony; the sublime pleases through formlessness and overwhelming magnitude. Croce rejects this distinction as philosophically groundless — both are successful expressions, differing only in their empirical content.

**Sublime vs Tragic.** The tragic is a literary genre, not an aesthetic quality. Croce treats genre distinctions as empirical classifications useful for criticism but irrelevant to philosophical aesthetics.

## Historical Context

The sublime was a central concept in 18th-century aesthetics. **Edmund Burke**'s *Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757) grounded the sublime in terror and astonishment. **Immanuel Kant**'s *Critique of Judgment* distinguished the mathematical sublime (overwhelming magnitude) from the dynamical sublime (overwhelming power). Croce's dissolution of the sublime as a distinct category reflects his broader project of simplifying the conceptual apparatus of aesthetics to its philosophical essentials (P02-088, P02-089).
