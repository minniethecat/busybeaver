[035-001] *Revival of Herbartian Æsthetic.*

[035-002] When the cry "Away with metaphysic!" was raised in [[germany|Germany]], and a

[035-003] furious reaction began against the kind of Walpurgis-night to which

[035-004] the later Hegelians had reduced the life of science and history, the

[035-005] disciples of [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]] came to the front and seemed to ask, with an

[035-006] insinuating air: "What is all this? a rebellion against [[idealism|Idealism]] and

[035-007] Metaphysic? why, it is exactly what Herbart wished and undertook all by

[035-008] himself half a century ago! Here we stand, his legitimate descendants,

[035-009] and we offer you our services as allies. We shall not find it hard to

[035-010] agree. Our Metaphysic accords with the atomic theory, our Psychology

[035-011] with mechanism, and our Ethics and Æsthetic with hedonism." Herbart

[035-012] himself (had he not died in 1841) would most likely have spumed these

[035-013] disciples of his who pandered to popularity, cheapened metaphysics and

[035-014] gave naturalistic interpretations to his reals, his representations,

[035-015] his ideas, and all his highest conceptions.

[035-016] With the school thus coming into fashion, the Herbartian Æsthetic

[035-017] too tried to put on flesh and acquire a pleasing plumpness so as not

[035-018] to cut too miserable a figure beside the well-nourished *corpora* of

[035-019] science launched upon the world by idealists. The feeding-up process

[035-020] was accomplished by [[robert-zimmermann|Robert Zimmermann]], professor of philosophy at

[035-021] Prague and later at Vienna, who, after years of laborious effort and

[035-022] an introductory sample in the shape of an ample history of Æsthetic

[035-023] (1858), at length produced his *General Æsthetic as Science of [[form|Form]]*

[035-024] in 1865.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_1_613)

[035-025] *Robert Zimmermann.*

[035-026] This formalistic Æsthetic, born under bad auspices, is a curious example

[035-027] of servile fidelity in externals combined with internal infidelity.

[035-028] Starting from unity, or rather from subordination of Ethics and

[035-029] Æsthetic to a general Æsthetic defined as "a science which treats of

[035-030] the modes by which any given content may acquire the right to arouse

[035-031] approval or disapproval" (thereby differing from Metaphysic, science

[035-032] of the real, and from Logic, science of right thinking), Zimmermann

[035-033] places such modes in form, that is to say, in the reciprocal relation

[035-034] of elements. A simple mathematical point in space, a simple impression

[035-035] of hearing or sight, a simple note, is in fact neither pleasing nor

[035-036] displeasing: music shows that the judgement of beauty or ugliness

[035-037] always depends on the relation between two notes at least. Now these

[035-038] relations, *i.e.* forms universally pleasing, cannot be empirically

[035-039] collected by induction; they must be developed by deduction. By

[035-040] the deductive method it can be demonstrated that the elements of

[035-041] an [[representation|image]], which in themselves are representations, may enter into

[035-042] relations either according to their force (quantity), or according to

[035-043] their nature (quality); whence we have two groups—æsthetic forms of

[035-044] quantity, and æsthetic forms of quality. According to the first, the

[035-045] strong (large) is pleasing in comparison with the weak (small), and

[035-046] these latter are displeasing when set beside the former; according

[035-047] to the other form, that pleases which is substantially identical in

[035-048] quality (the harmonious), and that displeases which is on the whole

[035-049] diverse (the discordant).

[035-050] But the substantial identity must not be pushed to the point of

[035-051] absolute identity, for in that case the harmony itself would cease to

[035-052] be. From harmonious form is deduced the pleasure of the characteristic

[035-053] or expression; for what is the characteristic but a relation of

[035-054] prevalent identity between the thing itself and its model? But while

[035-055] similarity prevailing in the distinction produces accord (*Einklang*),

[035-056] qualitative disharmony is as such disagreeable, and demands a

[035-057] resolution. (It is easy to detect the sleight of hand with which

[035-058] Zimmermann first slips the characteristic into the relations of pure

[035-059] form, thereby entirely altering Herbart's original thought; and how, by

[035-060] a second trick, he here introduces into pure beauty the variations and

[035-061] modifications of [[beauty|the beautiful]], by the help of the despised Hegelian

[035-062] dialectic.) If such resolution is effected by the skilful substitution

[035-063] of something other than the unpleasant image, we shall certainly have

[035-064] removed the cause of offence and established quietude (not accord:

[035-065] *Eintracht, nicht Einklang*), but we shall have gained the mere form

[035-066] of correctness: it is better, then, to supersede this by means of the

[035-067] true image so as to reach the form of compensation (*Ausgleichung*);

[035-068] and, when the true image is also pleasing in itself, the final form

[035-069] of definitive compensation (*abschliessende Ausgleich,*) with which

[035-070] we exhaust the series of possible forms. And, in conclusion, what is

[035-071] Beauty? It is a conjunction of all these forms: a model (*Vorbild*)

[035-072] which has grandeur, plenitude, order, accord, correctness, definitive

[035-073] compensation; all this appears in a copy (*Nachbild*) in the form of

[035-074] the characteristic.

[035-075] Putting on one side the artificial connexion Zimmermann makes between

[035-076] [[sublime|the sublime]], the comic, the tragic, the ironic, the humorous and

[035-077] the æsthetic forms, notice must be taken (so that we may recognize

[035-078] into which of the seven heavens he is wafting us) that these general

[035-079] æsthetic forms concern art equally with nature and morality, whose

[035-080] individual spheres are differentiated solely by the application of the

[035-081] general æsthetic forms to particular contents. These forms, applied to

[035-082] nature, give us natural beauty, the cosmos; applied to representation,

[035-083] beauty of wit (*Schöngeist*) or imagination; applied to feeling,

[035-084] the beautiful soul (*schöne Seele*) or taste; applied to the will,

[035-085] character or virtue. On one side, then, is natural beauty, on the other

[035-086] human beauty, in which (latter), on one hand, we have the beauty of

[035-087] representation, that is to say æsthetic fact in the strict sense (art);

[035-088] on the other, we have the beauty of will, or morality; and between the

[035-089] two, lastly, we have taste, common to Ethics and Æsthetic. Æsthetic in

[035-090] the narrow sense, as the theory of beautiful representation, determines

[035-091] the beauty of representations, divided into the three classes of

[035-092] the beauty of temporal and spatial connexion (figurative arts); the

[035-093] beauty of sensitive representation (music); and the beauty of thoughts

[035-094] (poetry). This tripartition of beauty into figurative, musical and

[035-095] poetical brings to a conclusion theoretical Æsthetic, the only section

[035-096] developed by Zimmermann.

[035-097] *Vischer versus Zimmermann.*

[035-098] Zimmermann's work was a polemic against the principal representative

[035-099] of Hegelian Æsthetic, Vischer, who had little difficulty in defending

[035-100] his own position and counter-attacking that of his assailant. He

[035-101] held Zimmermann up to ridicule, for example, in connexion with his

[035-102] view of symbolism. Zimmermann defined a symbol as the object "round

[035-103] which beautiful forms adhere." A painter depicts a fox simply for the

[035-104] sake of painting a part of animal nature. Nothing of the sort: this

[035-105] is a symbol, because the painter "makes use of fines and colours to

[035-106] express things other than fines and colours." "You think I'm a fox,"

[035-107] says the animal in the picture, "but you make a great mistake: I'm a

[035-108] clothes-peg: I'm an appearance created by the painter with gradations

[035-109] of grey, white, yellow and red." Even easier was it to make game of

[035-110] Zimmermann's enthusiastic praises of the æsthetic quality of the sense

[035-111] of touch. It was a pity, the latter had written, that the pleasures of

[035-112] this sense were so difficult to attain; since "to touch the back of the

[035-113] Resting Hercules and the sinuous limbs of the Venus of Melos or the

[035-114] Barberini Faun would give to the hand a delight comparable only with

[035-115] that felt by the ear when listening to the majestic fugues of Bach or

[035-116] the suave melodies of Mozart." Vischer does not seem to be far wrong in

[035-117] declaring formalistic Æsthetic to be "a grotesque union of mysticism

[035-118] and mathematics."[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_2_614)

[035-119] *Hermann Lotze.*

[035-120] The works of Zimmermann seem to have given satisfaction to nobody

[035-121] save himself. Even Lotze, by no means an adversary of Herbartianism,

[035-122] blames him severely in his *History of Æsthetic in Germany* (1868) and

[035-123] other writings. Still, Lotze was unable to offer any better substitute

[035-124] for æsthetic formalism than of a variant of the old idealism. "Can

[035-125] any one persuade us," he wrote in criticism of the formalists,

[035-126] "that a spiritual discord expressed by a corresponding discord in

[035-127] external appearances may have a value equal to that of the harmonious

[035-128] expression of a harmonious content solely because, in both cases,

[035-129] the formal relation of accord is respected? Can any one persuade us

[035-130] that the human form is pleasing solely for its formal stereometric

[035-131] relations, irrespective of the spiritual life by which it is animated?

[035-132] In empirical reality the three domains of laws, facts and values

[035-133] invariably appear as divided; and although they are united in the

[035-134] Highest Good, in Goodness in itself, in the living Love of a Personal

[035-135] God, in the Ought which is the basis of Being, our reason is unable to

[035-136] attain or to know such union. Beauty alone can reveal it to us: it is

[035-137] in close connexion with the Good and the Holy and reproduces the rhythm

[035-138] of the divine ordinance and the moral government of the universe.

[035-139] Æsthetic fact is neither intuition nor concept; it is idea, which

[035-140] presents the essential of an object in the form of an end referred to

[035-141] the ultimate end. [[art|Art]], like beauty, must include the world of values

[035-142] in the world of forms."[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_3_615) The war between the Æsthetic of content and

[035-143] that of form, having Zimmermann, Vischer and Lotze as protagonists,

[035-144] reached its culminating point between 1860 and 1870.

[035-145] *Efforts to reconcile Æsthetic of form and Æsthetic of

[035-146] content.*

[035-147] Several people were in favour of a reconciliation. But the

[035-148] reconciliations they offered were not the right one, which was at

[035-149] least glimpsed by a certain young Johann Schmidt, who in his thesis

[035-150] for doctorate observed (1875) that, with all respect for Zimmermann

[035-151] and Lotze, it seemed to him they were both wrong in confusing the

[035-152] various meanings of the word "beauty," and discussed such an absurdity

[035-153] as a beauty or ugliness of natural objects, that is to say, of things

[035-154] external to the spirit; that Lotze, following [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], added the second

[035-155] absurdity of an intuitive concept or conceptual intuition: lastly,

[035-156] that neither of them grasped the fact that the æsthetic problem does

[035-157] not turn upon the beauty or ugliness of the abstract content or of

[035-158] form understood as a system of mathematical relations, but with the

[035-159] beauty or ugliness of representation. Form undoubtedly must exist, but

[035-160] "concrete form, full of content."[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_4_616) These utterances of Schmidt met

[035-161] with a hostile reception: it is easy (he was told in reply) to identify

[035-162] beauty with artistic perfection, but the whole crux of the matter lies

[035-163] in finding whether, beside this perfection, there exists another beauty

[035-164] dependent on a supreme cosmic or metaphysical principle: otherwise one

[035-165] is guilty of a naïve *petitio principii*.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_5_617) It was thought better,

[035-166] therefore, to seek other modes of reconciliation, which consisted

[035-167] in cooking up an appetizing dish in which a little formalism and a

[035-168] little contentism were mixed to taste, the latter as a rule giving the

[035-169] predominant flavour.

[035-170] Some Herbartians were found in the ranks of the mediating or

[035-171] conciliatory party. Hardly had Zimmermann's rigid formalism appeared,

[035-172] when Nahlowsky jumped up to protest that it had never entered the

[035-173] master's head to exclude content from Æsthetic;[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_6_618) but even the ablest

[035-174] of the school, men such as Volkmann and Lazarus, chose a middle

[035-175] course.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_7_619) In the opposite camp Carrière,[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_8_620) and even Vischer himself

[035-176] (in a criticism of his own old *Æsthetic*), began to concede a larger

[035-177] part to the consideration of form; thus for Vischer beauty became

[035-178] "life appearing harmoniously," which when it appears in space is called

[035-179] form, and must always possess form, *i.e.* limitation (*Begrenzung* )

[035-180] in space and time, measure, regularity, symmetry, proportion, propriety

[035-181] (these characters constituting its quantitative moments) and harmony

[035-182] (qualitative moment), which includes variety and contrast and is

[035-183] therefore the most important characteristic.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_9_621)

[035-184] *K. Köstlin.*

[035-185] A conciliatory Æsthetic in which formalism prevailed was attempted

[035-186] by Karl Köstlin, a professor at Tübingen and formerly collaborator in

[035-187] the musical section of the works of Vischer. Köstlin[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_10_622) had been

[035-188] influenced by [[friedrich-schleiermacher|Schleiermacher]], Hegel, Vischer and Herbart, but, truth

[035-189] to tell, does not seem to have perfectly understood the teaching

[035-190] of any one of his predecessors. According to him, the æsthetic

[035-191] object presented three requirements: richness and variety of imagery

[035-192] (*anregende Gestaltenfülle*), interesting content and beautiful form.

[035-193] Under the first we recognize, with no little difficulty, a distorted

[035-194] reflexion of Schleiermacher's "inspiration" (*Begeisterung*).

[035-195] Interesting content he defined as that which concerns man; that which

[035-196] he knows or does not know; that which he loves or hates (it is thus

[035-197] always relative to the individual and the conditions in which he

[035-198] exists); and he asserted that interest of content is joined to value

[035-199] of form, that is, he conceived content as a second value, the same

[035-200] of which we have heard Herbart speak. He also agreed with Herbart

[035-201] that form is absolute, and that its general character is determined

[035-202] as being easily perceptible by intuition (*anschaulich*), and by its

[035-203] power of giving satisfaction, pleasure and delight, in fact, as being

[035-204] beautiful. Its particular characteristics for Köstlin were, according

[035-205] to quantity, circumscription, simplicity (*Einheitlichkeit*), extensive

[035-206] and intensive size, and equilibrium (*Gleichmass*); according to

[035-207] quality, determination (*Bestimmtheit*), unity (*Einheit*), importance

[035-208] (*Bedeutung*) extensive and intensive, and harmony. But when Köstlin

[035-209] sets himself to the empirical verification of his categories, he falls

[035-210] into hopeless confusion. Greatness is pleasing, but so is smallness;

[035-211] unity is pleasing, but so is variety; regularity is pleasing, but so,

[035-212] confound it, is irregularity: uncertainties and contradictions at every

[035-213] step; he was aware of them and made no effort to conceal them; but they

[035-214] should have convinced him that the abstraction of "beautiful form,"

[035-215] whose qualities and quantities he had so laboriously collected, is a

[035-216] ghostly shape without body, since that alone gives æsthetic pleasure

[035-217] which fulfils an expressive function. But having illustrated the three

[035-218] demands of the æsthetic object, Köstlin wasted all his remaining breath

[035-219] in constructing a kingdom of intuitive imagination in the manner of

[035-220] Vischer, *i.e.* beauty of organic and inorganic nature; of civil life;

[035-221] of morality; of religion; of science; of games; of conversations; of

[035-222] feasts and banquets; and lastly of history, reviewing and passing

[035-223] æsthetic comment on its three periods, patriarchal, heroic and

[035-224] historical.

[035-225] *Æsthetic of content. M. Schasler.*

[035-226] Schasler, who had written as vast a history on Æsthetic as Zimmermann's

[035-227] own, found a starting-point for a movement toward formalism in absolute

[035-228] idealism, or realism-idealism, as he called it. He began by defining

[035-229] Æsthetic as "the science of the beautiful and of art" (a single

[035-230] science ill defined as having two different objects), and proceeded

[035-231] to justify his unmethodical definition by saying that beauty does not

[035-232] exist in art alone, nor does art concern itself solely with beauty. The

[035-233] sphere of Æsthetic he defines as that of intuition (*Anschauung*) in

[035-234] which knowledge assumes a practical character and will a theoretical:

[035-235] the sphere of indivisible unity and absolute reconciliation of the

[035-236] theoretical and practical spirit, in which in a certain sense the

[035-237] highest human activities are developed. Beauty is the ideal, but the

[035-238] concrete ideal; this is why there is no ideal of a human body in

[035-239] abstraction from sex, no ideal of a mammal in general, but only of such

[035-240] and such species, as of horse or dog, and then only of determinate

[035-241] kind of horse or dog. Thus by descending from the more to the less

[035-242] abstract genus Schasler vainly attempted to reach the concrete, which

[035-243] inevitably escaped his grasp. In art we pass from the typical, which

[035-244] is natural beauty, to the characteristic, which is the typical of

[035-245] human feeling; hence we can frame the ideal of an old woman, a beggar

[035-246] or a ruffian. The characteristic of art is in closer relationship

[035-247] to the ugly than to the beautiful in nature. On this head (passing

[035-248] over the remainder, which is on familiar lines) it is well to notice

[035-249] that Schasler has a bias towards that version of the romaunt of Sir

[035-250] Purebeauty which ascribes the birth of the "modifications of Beauty" to

[035-251] the influence of the Ugly.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_11_623) "Although," he writes, "the thought may

[035-252] disturb our minds, it must not be forgotten that were there no world

[035-253] of ugliness there could be no world of beauty; for it is only when

[035-254] the Ugly stirs up empty abstract Beauty, that it begins to combat the

[035-255] enemy and thus to produce concrete Beauty."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_12_624) He even succeeded in

[035-256] converting Vischer himself, the chief supporter of the other version:

[035-257] "Formerly I had been accustomed to think in the old-fashioned Hegelian

[035-258] style," Vischer confesses, "that unrest, fermentation and strife dwelt

[035-259] in the essence of Beauty; that the Idea prevails and thrusts the

[035-260] image forth into the infinite; so arises the Sublime; that the image,

[035-261] offended in its finitude, makes war on the Idea; whence arises the

[035-262] Comic; this finished the struggle; Beauty returned to itself from the

[035-263] conflict of the two moments, and was created." But now, he continues,

[035-264] "I must acknowledge that Schasler is right, and so are his predecessors

[035-265] Weisse and Ruge: the Ugly has a hand in the matter; this is the

[035-266] principle of movement, the ferment of differentiation: without such

[035-267] leaven we never reach the special forms of Beauty, for each single one

[035-268] presupposes' the Ugly."[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_13_625)

[035-269] *Ed. von Hartmann.*

[035-270] Closely allied to that of Schasler is the Æsthetic of Eduard von

[035-271] Hartmann (1890), preceded by a historical treatise on *German Æsthetic

[035-272] since [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]*[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_14_626) wherein with meticulous, critical and polemical study

[035-273] he upholds the definition of Beauty as "the appearance of the Idea"

[035-274] (*das Scheinen der Idee*). Inasmuch as he insisted on appearance

[035-275] (*Schein*) as the necessary characteristic of Beauty, Hartmann held

[035-276] himself justified in naming his Æsthetic the "Æsthetic of Concrete

[035-277] Idealism," and in ranging himself alongside Hegel, Trahndorff,

[035-278] Schleiermacher, Deutinger, Oersted, Vischer, Meising, Carrière

[035-279] and Schasler, against the abstract idealism of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]], [[karl-solger|Solger]],

[035-280] [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], Krause, Weisse and Lotze, all of whom, by placing

[035-281] beauty in the supersensible idea, overlooked the sensory element and

[035-282] reduced it to the rank of a mere accessory.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_15_627) By his insistence on

[035-283] the idea as the other indispensable and determining element, Hartmann

[035-284] proclaimed himself as opposed to the Herbartian formalism. Beauty is

[035-285] truth; neither historical, scientific nor reflective, but metaphysical

[035-286] or idealistic, the very truth of Philosophy: "in proportion as Beauty

[035-287] is in opposition to every science and to realistic truth, so much

[035-288] nearer is it to Philosophy and metaphysical truth": "Beauty, with its

[035-289] own peculiar efficacy, remains the prophet of idealistic truth in an

[035-290] unbelieving age that abhors Metaphysic and recognizes no value in

[035-291] anything but realistic truth." Æsthetic truth, which leaps immediately

[035-292] from subjective appearance to ideal essence, is lacking in the control

[035-293] and method possessed by philosophical truth; in compensation, however,

[035-294] she possesses the fascinating power of conviction, the sole property of

[035-295] sensible intuition, and unattainable by gradual or reflected mediation.

[035-296] The higher Philosophy soars, the less does it need the gradual passage

[035-297] through the world of the senses and of science, and the slighter

[035-298] becomes the distance separating Philosophy and Art. The latter, for

[035-299] its part, will be well advised to start on its journey towards the

[035-300] ideal world as Bædeker's handbooks counsel the intending traveller,

[035-301] "with as little luggage as possible"; "not overloading herself with a

[035-302] weight which paralyses the wings and is made up of unnecessary and

[035-303] indifferent trifles,"[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_16_628) Logical character, the microcosmic idea,

[035-304] the unconscious are immanent in beauty; by means of the unconscious,

[035-305] intellectual intuition operates in it,[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_17_629) and, from its being rooted

[035-306] in the unconscious, it is a Mystery.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_18_630)

[035-307] *Hartmann and the theory of Modifications.*

[035-308] In his employment of the exciting or reactionary influence of the

[035-309] Ugly, Hartmann exceeded Schasler himself. Lowest among the degrees of

[035-310] Beauty, indeed forming the lower limit of æsthetic fact, lies sensuous

[035-311] pleasure, which is unconscious formal beauty; its first true degree

[035-312] is formal beauty of the first order, or the mathematically pleasing

[035-313] (unity, variety, symmetry, proportion, the golden section, etc.); its

[035-314] second degree is formal beauty of the second order, the dynamically

[035-315] pleasing; its third is formal beauty of the third order, the passive

[035-316] teleological, as in the case of utensils or machinery. Indeed it may

[035-317] here be noted that among machines and utensils, on a level with jars,

[035-318] plates and cups, Hartmann placed language: it is a dead thing, said

[035-319] he; receiving the appearances of life (*Scheinleben*)[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_19_631) only at the

[035-320] very instant of utterance. Language a "dead thing," an "utensil" for

[035-321] the philosopher of the Unconscious, in the land of Humboldt, with a

[035-322] Steinthal still living! There follow, as formal beauty of the fourth

[035-323] order, the active teleological or living, and as formal beauty of the

[035-324] fifth order, conformity to species (*das Gattungsmässige)*: lastly

[035-325] and above all, since the individual idea is superior to the specific,

[035-326] is beauty concrete beauty or the microcosmic individual, which is no

[035-327] longer formal, but beauty of content. As is to be expected, the passage

[035-328] from lower to Higher degrees is made by means of the Ugly: nobody has

[035-329] laboured like Hartmann to recount in detail the services rendered by

[035-330] Ugliness to Beauty. From ugliness, in the form of the destruction of

[035-331] the beauty of equality, arises symmetry: from ugliness in the case of

[035-332] the circle arises the ellipse; the beauty of a waterfall tumbling over

[035-333] rocks is caused by the mathematically ugly; destruction, that is to

[035-334] say, of a fall in a parabolic curve; beauty of spiritual expression is

[035-335] achieved through the introduction of an ugliness relative to fleshly

[035-336] perfection. Beauty of a higher degree is founded on ugliness at a

[035-337] lower degree. When the highest degree is reached, that of individual

[035-338] beauty beyond which there can be nothing, even then elemental ugliness

[035-339] continues its work of beneficent irritation. The later phases thus

[035-340] produced are well known to us as the famous Modifications of the

[035-341] Beautiful: in this section also, nobody is so copious or detailed as

[035-342] Hartmann. He certainly does admit, side by side with simple or pure

[035-343] beauty, certain modifications free from conflict, such as the sublime

[035-344] or graceful; but the more important modifications can arise only

[035-345] through conflict. There are four cases, because the resolution must

[035-346] be either immanent, logical, transcendent or combined: immanent in

[035-347] the idyllic, the melancholy, the sad, the cheerful, the moving, the

[035-348] elegiac; logical in the comic in all its varieties; transcendent in the

[035-349] tragic; combined in the humorous with the tragi-comic and its other

[035-350] varieties. When none of these resolutions is possible, there arises

[035-351] ugliness; when an ugliness of content is expressed by an ugliness of

[035-352] form, we have the maximum of ugliness, the real æsthetic devil.

[035-353] *Metaphysical Æsthetic in [[france|France]]. C. Levêque.*

[035-354] Hartmann is the last considerable representative of the old æsthetic

[035-355] school in Germany; he inspires terror by the mass of his literary

[035-356] production, like many others of the school, who seem to accept it as

[035-357] a dogma that art cannot be dealt with except in several volumes a

[035-358] thousand pages long. Those who are not afraid of giants and are able

[035-359] to attack this sort of Æsthetic, will find it a fat good-humoured

[035-360] Magog full of vulgar prejudices, and so constituted that, despite his

[035-361] apparent strength, a little blow will kill him.

[035-362] In other countries metaphysical Æsthetic had few followers. In France

[035-363] the celebrated competition of the Academy of Moral and Political

[035-364] Sciences in 1857 crowned with their approval and presented to the

[035-365] world the *Science of Beauty* by Levêque;[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_20_632) of which nobody now

[035-366] thinks or speaks, only remembering the author (who attitudinized

[035-367] as a disciple of Plato) by his eight characteristics of Beauty,

[035-368] derived by him from examination of a lily. The eight characteristics

[035-369] were as follows:—sufficient size of form, unity, variety, harmony,

[035-370] proportion, normal vivacity of colour, grace and propriety; ultimately

[035-371] reducible to two, size and order. As supplementary proof of the truth

[035-372] of his theory, Levêque applied it to three beautiful things: a child

[035-373] playing with its mother, a symphony of Beethoven and the life of a

[035-374] philosopher (Socrates). Really, it is somewhat difficult (says one of

[035-375] his fellow-spiritualists, venturing to comment on this doctrine though

[035-376] speaking with the utmost deference) to imagine what may be the normal

[035-377] vivacity of colour in the life of a philosopher.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_21_633) Translations and

[035-378] explanatory articles by Charles Bénard[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_22_634) and books by various writers

[035-379] belonging to French Switzerland (Töpffer, Pictet, Cherbuliez) were not

[035-380] successful in popularizing the German systems of Æsthetic in France.

[035-381] *In [[england|England]]. J. Ruskin.*

[035-382] England showed even less disposition to interest herself, although

[035-383] John Ruskin may have some claim to be considered a metaphysical

[035-384] æsthetician with a distinctive national stamp. But it is difficult

[035-385] to treat of Ruskin in a history of science, for his temperament was

[035-386] wholly opposed to the scientific. His disposition was that of the

[035-387] artist, impressionable, excitable, voluble, rich in feeling; a dogmatic

[035-388] tone and the appearance of theoretical form veil, in his exquisite

[035-389] and enthusiastic pages, a texture of dreams and fancies. The reader

[035-390] who recalls those pages will regard as irreverent any detailed and

[035-391] prosaic review of Ruskin's æsthetic thought, which must inevitably

[035-392] reveal its poverty and incoherence. Suffice it to say that, following

[035-393] a finalistic, mystical intuition of nature, he considered beauty as a

[035-394] revelation of divine intentions, the seal "God sets on his works, even

[035-395] upon the smallest." For him the faculty which perceives the beautiful

[035-396] is neither intellect nor sensibility, but a particular feeling which

[035-397] he names the theoretic faculty. Natural beauty, which reveals itself

[035-398] to a pure heart when contemplating any object untouched and unspoiled

[035-399] by the hand of man, asserts itself for this reason as immeasurably

[035-400] superior to any work of art. Ruskin was too hasty in analysis to

[035-401] understand the complicated psychological and æsthetic process which

[035-402] went on in his mind when he was moved to an artist's ecstasy by

[035-403] contemplating some humble natural object such as a bird's nest or a

[035-404] flowing rivulet.[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_23_635)

[035-405] *Æsthetic in [[italy|Italy]].*

[035-406] In Italy the Abate Tornasi wrote a half-Hegelian, half-Catholic

[035-407] Æsthetic, wherein the beautiful is identified with the second person of

[035-408] the Trinity, the Word made man;[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_24_636) by this means he hoped to raise a

[035-409] bank of opposition against the liberal criticism of [[francesco-de-sanctis|De Sanctis]], whom he

[035-410] considered, from the sublime height of his own philosophy, as "a subtle

[035-411] grammarian." Combined Giobertian and German, especially Hegelian,

[035-412] influence produced several works of secondary importance; De Meis

[035-413] developed at length the thesis of the death of Art in the historical

[035-414] world.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_25_637) Somewhat later Gallo also treated Æsthetic from the Hegelian

[035-415] point of view,[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_26_638) and others repeated, nearly word for word, the

[035-416] doctrines of Schasler and Hartmann on the overcoming of the Ugly.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_27_639)

[035-417] *Antonio Tari and his lectures.*

[035-418] The only genuine Italian teacher of metaphysical Æsthetic according

[035-419] to the Germans was Antonio Tari, who lectured on this very subject

[035-420] in Naples University from 1861 to 1884. He had a meticulous and

[035-421] superstitiously minute knowledge of everything that issued from German

[035-422] printing-presses, and was the author of an *Ideal Æsthetic* as well

[035-423] as essays on style, taste, serious work and play (*Spiel,*) music and

[035-424] architecture, wherein he tried to keep the mean between the idealism

[035-425] of Hegel and the formalism of Herbart:[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_28_640) his lectures on Æsthetic

[035-426] attracted huge throngs and were one of the regular sights in the noisy,

[035-427] crowded Neapolitan university. Tari divided his treatment under three

[035-428] heads, Æsthesinomy, Æsthesigraphy and Æsthesipraxis, corresponding to

[035-429] the Metaphysic of the beautiful, to the doctrine of beauty in nature,

[035-430] and to that of beauty in art; like the German idealists, he defined the

[035-431] æsthetic sphere as intermediate between the theoretical and practical:

[035-432] he says emphatically that "in the world of spirit the temperate zone

[035-433] is equidistant from the glacial, peopled by the Esquimaux of thought,

[035-434] and from the torrid, peopled by the giants of action." He pulled Beauty

[035-435] from her throne, substituting in her stead the Æsthetic, of which

[035-436] Beauty is but an initial moment, the simple "beginning of æsthetic

[035-437] life, eternal mortality, flower and fruit in one," whose successive

[035-438] moments are represented by the Sublime, the Comic, the Humorous, and

[035-439] the Dramatic.

[035-440] *Æsthesigraphy.*

[035-441] But the most attractive part of Tari's lectures was that devoted

[035-442] to Æsthesigraphy, subdivided into Cosmography, Physiography and

[035-443] Psychography, in the course of which he frequently quoted Vischer with

[035-444] great devotion; "the great Vischer" as he called him, in imitation of

[035-445] whom he constructed his own "æsthetic physics," brightening it with

[035-446] much varied erudition and enlivening it with quaint comparisons. Is

[035-447] he speaking of beauty in inorganic nature—water, for example? He

[035-448] says in his fanciful manner, "When water ripples in the sunshine, in

[035-449] that act it has its smile; it has its frown in the breaking wave, its

[035-450] caprice in the fountain, its majestic fury in the foam." Is he speaking

[035-451] of geological configuration? "The vale, cradle perchance of the

[035-452] human race, is idyllic; the plain, monotonous but fat, is didactic."

[035-453] Of metals? "Gold is born great; iron, the apotheosis of human toil,

[035-454] achieves greatness; the former boasts of its cradle when it does not

[035-455] bring it to dishonour; the latter causes it to be forgotten." He looked

[035-456] on vegetable life as a dream, repeating Herder's fine saying that the

[035-457] plant is "the new-born babe that hangs sucking upon the breast of

[035-458] mother nature." He divided vegetables into three types: foliaceous,

[035-459] ramified and umbelliferous: "the foliaceous type," he says, "attains

[035-460] gigantic proportions in the tropics, where the queen of monocotyledons,

[035-461] the Palm-tree, represents despotism, the human scourge of those desert

[035-462] regions. Of that solitary pinnacle, all crown, the negro may well be

[035-463] identified as the reptile that crawls round its base." Amongst flowers,

[035-464] the carnation is "symbol of betrayal, by reason of the variegation of

[035-465] its colours and its deeply-dissected petals"; the celebrated comparison

[035-466] by Ariosto of a rose with a young girl is permissible only when the

[035-467] flower is still in bud, because "when it has unfolded its petals,

[035-468] disdaining the protection of thorns, displaying itself in all the pomp

[035-469] of its full colour, and boldly asking to be plucked by any hand, then

[035-470] it is woman, all woman, to call it by no harsher name, giving pleasure

[035-471] without feeling it, simulating love by its perfume and modesty by the

[035-472] crimson of its petals." He searches for and comments upon analogies

[035-473] between certain fruits and certain flowers; between the strawberry, for

[035-474] instance, and the violet; between the orange and the rose; he admired

[035-475] "the luxuriant spirals and the delicate architecture of a bunch of

[035-476] grapes": the mandarin-orange reminded him of the nobleman *qui s'est

[035-477] donné la peine de naître*; the fig, on the contrary, was the great

[035-478] country bumpkin, "rough, rude, but profitable." In the animal kingdom,

[035-479] the spider symbolized primitive isolation; the bee, monasticism; the

[035-480] ant, republicanism. He noted, with Michelet, that the spider is a

[035-481] living paralogism; it cannot feed itself without its web, and it cannot

[035-482] spin its web without feeding. Fish he condemns as un-æsthetic: "they

[035-483] are of stupid appearance with their wide—open eyes and incessant

[035-484] gaping, which makes them look voraciously gluttonous." Not so with

[035-485] amphibians, for which he entertains a sympathy: the frog and the

[035-486] crocodile, "alpha and omega of the family, start from the comical, or

[035-487] even the scurrilous, and attain the sublimity of the horrid." Birds

[035-488] are especially æsthetic by nature, "possessing the three most genial

[035-489] attributes of a living being: love, song, and flight"; moreover, they

[035-490] present contrasts and antitheses: "opposite to the eagle, queen of the

[035-491] skies, stands the swan, the mild king of the marshes; the libertine

[035-492] vainglorious cock has its contrast in the humble uxorious turtle-dove;

[035-493] the magnificent peacock is balanced by the rude and rustic turkey."

[035-494] Amongst mammals, nature compensates for defects of pure beauty by

[035-495] dramatic value; if they cannot throw their song into the air, they

[035-496] have the rudiments of speech; if they have no variegated, myriad-hued

[035-497] plumage, they have dark, heavily-marked colouring, instinct with life;

[035-498] if they cannot fly, they have many other modes of powerful progression;

[035-499] and, the higher they go, the more do they attain individuality in

[035-500] appearance and life. "The epic of animal life is comedy in the donkey,

[035-501] *iniquae mentis asellus*; idyl in the great wild beasts; downright

[035-502] tragedy in the Kaffir bull, that cloven-hoofed Codrus, who gives

[035-503] himself voluntarily to the lion in order to save the herd." As amongst

[035-504] birds, so amongst beasts attractive contrasts are to be made:—the lamb

[035-505] and the kid seem to typify Jesus and the devil; dog and cat, abnegation

[035-506] and egoism; hare and fox, the foolish simpleton and crafty villain.

[035-507] Many quaint and subtle observations does Tari let fall on human beauty

[035-508] and the relative beauty of the sexes, allowing the female to have

[035-509] charm, not beauty: "bodily beauty is poise, and woman's body is so

[035-510] ill-poised that she falls easily when running; made for child-bearing,

[035-511] she has knock-kneed legs, adapted to support the large pelvis; her

[035-512] shoulders have a curve compensating the convexity of the chest." He

[035-513] describes the various parts of the body: "curly hair expresses physical

[035-514] force; straight hair, moral"; "blue, napoleonic eyes have sometimes

[035-515] a depth like the sea; green eyes have a melancholy fascination; grey

[035-516] eyes are wanting in individuality; black eyes are the most intensely

[035-517] individual"; "a lovely mouth has been best described by Heine; two lips

[035-518] evenly matched; to lovers the mouth will rather seem a shell whose

[035-519] pearl is the kiss."[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_29_641)

[035-520] How could we better take a smiling leave of metaphysical Æsthetic in

[035-521] the German manner than by recording this quaint vernacular version

[035-522] of it made by Tari, that kindly little old man, "the last jovial

[035-523] high-priest of an arbitrary and confused Æsthetic"?[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-23.htm.html#Footnote_30_642)
