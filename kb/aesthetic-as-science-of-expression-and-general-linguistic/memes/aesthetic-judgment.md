# Aesthetic Judgment

In Croce's aesthetic system, aesthetic judgment (*giudizio estetico*) is not a separate cognitive faculty but the critical moment of taste — the capacity to recognize and evaluate beauty in works of art. Croce identifies aesthetic judgment with taste, and taste with genius, dismantling the traditional opposition between the creator and the critic.

## Definition

Aesthetic judgment is the act of recognizing whether an expression succeeds in adequately forming its intuitive content. The judgment does not apply external rules; it re-produces the artist's own expressive act within the spectator's spirit. As Croce argues in the chapter on taste and genius, "taste and genius are substantially identical" (017-077). The faculty that produces art and the faculty that judges it are one and the same, differing only in circumstance: production versus reproduction.

The judgment of taste is universal in aspiration: it claims validity for all spirits, not merely for the individual making the judgment. As Kant recognized, "the judgment of taste is universal" (029-153), though Croce criticizes Kant for retaining a residual subjectivism that his own system overcomes.

## In Croce's System

### Taste as Reproductive Activity

Croce distinguishes productive activity (genius) from reproductive activity (taste), but insists on their underlying identity. "What is called taste; the productive activity is called genius" (017-077). Both are intuition-expression: the artist expresses a formed intuition; the spectator re-expresses it by re-creating the same intuition within their own spirit. The good critic must be capable of the same spiritual act as the artist, even if unable to originate it independently.

### Identity of Taste and Genius

The productive and reproductive activities are "substantially identical" (017-078). Croce dismisses the common distinction between artists "with genius without taste" and critics "with taste without genius" as merely a quantitative difference. "Genius without taste" describes artists who excel in primary inspiration but fail in secondary execution; "taste without genius" describes critics capable of recognizing great art without producing it (017-086–017-088). The essential spiritual faculty is the same.

### Against Absolutism of Taste

Croce rejects the view that taste provides absolute, rule-governed judgments. Taste does not apply pre-existing canons; it re-creates each work afresh. Those who "deny the absoluteness of taste" (017-122) err in the opposite direction, reducing judgment to mere personal preference. The truth lies between: aesthetic judgment is universal in intention but achieved only through successful re-creation.

## Historical Context

The problem of aesthetic judgment has a long pre-history. **Plato** treated beauty as an objective Form; **Aristotle** gave practical canons in the *Poetics*. The eighteenth century shifted focus from objective properties to subjective experience. **Kant**'s *Critique of Judgment* (1790) established the landmark formulation: aesthetic judgment is subjective yet claims universal validity — disinterested pleasure, purposiveness without purpose. **Baumgarten** had earlier coined "aesthetics" as the science of sensible cognition. Croce inherits this tradition but radicalizes it: judgment is not inference from rules but the re-productive intuition of expression itself.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's identification of taste with genius has been questioned: if every successful aesthetic judgment requires re-creating the artist's intuition, how can we verify that two spirits share the same intuition? Croce's answer appeals to the universality of spirit — intuition is pre-individual, and successful expression is recognizable by all who attune themselves to it. The practical test is the judgment's capacity to illuminate the work for others.
