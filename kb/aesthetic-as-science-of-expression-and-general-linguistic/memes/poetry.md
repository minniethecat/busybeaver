# Poetry

In Croce's aesthetic system, poetry occupies a position that is both central and paradoxical. Poetry is not one literary kind among others; it is identical with art itself.

## Definition

The distinction between poetry and prose "cannot be justified" as a philosophical distinction (004-123, 004-132). Poetry is not a particular use of language but the aesthetic fact itself — expression in its purity. "There is poetry without prose, but not prose without poetry" (004-136). Prose is a derivative form; poetry is primary.

This identification means that every successful expression is poetry, regardless of its medium or genre. "Poetry is the language of feeling, prose the language of the intellect" — but since "the intellect is also feeling," the distinction collapses (004-127, 004-129).

## In Croce's System

### The Identity of Poetry and Art

Croce's identification of poetry with expression has radical implications. The combinations of words "called poetry, prose, poems" are all physical stimuli for aesthetic reproduction (014-106). What makes something poetry is not its verse form or its subject matter but its success as expression.

### Key Distinctions

1. **Poetry vs. prose** — An empirical, not a philosophical distinction. Both are expression; the difference lies in the content and conventions, not in the form of spirit.

2. **Poetry vs. versification** — Verse is a technique, poetry is expression. One can write verse without poetry (bad verse) and poetry without verse (poetic prose).

3. **Poetry as primary** — "There is poetry without prose, but not prose without poetry" (004-136). Expression is fundamentally poetic; prose is poetry adapted to practical purposes.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The quarrel between philosophy and poetry is ancient, "as Plato was afterwards to carry to its extreme consequences" (020-080). Plato banished poets from his ideal republic because he saw poetry as opposed to truth. Aristotle defended poetry as more philosophical than history.

Eratosthenes "made poetry consist in mere pleasure," claiming "poetry has always aimed at pleasure, not at instruction" (020-189, 020-193). This hedonistic theory is one Croce rejects: poetry is not pleasure but expression.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce dissolves the ancient quarrel by identifying poetry with expression itself. Poetry is not opposed to truth or philosophy; it is a form of theoretic activity — intuition-expression — that is prior to conceptual thought. The poet creates the individual image; the philosopher elaborates the universal concept. Both are necessary moments of the spirit.

## Criticism and Debate

> The distinction between poetry and prose has been one of the most persistent confusions in literary theory (004-123).

Critics have argued that Croce's identification of poetry with expression is too broad — it erases the specific formal features that distinguish poetry from other modes of discourse. If all expression is poetry, the term loses its descriptive value. Croce's response is that the term's descriptive value is precisely what he wants to lose: poetry is not a kind of writing but the essence of art.
