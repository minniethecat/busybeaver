# Characteristic

The characteristic emerged in late eighteenth-century aesthetics as a principled dissent from the doctrine of ideal beauty. Where Winckelmann and Mengs held that the artist should depict not nature as it is but nature as it ought to be — a perfected, idealized form — the theorists of the characteristic insisted that art's true principle lies in fidelity to the individual character of its subject. Croce, in his historical survey, presents this debate as a pivotal moment in the long struggle between neo-Platonic idealism and Aristotelian particularity in aesthetic theory.

## Definition

The characteristic, in its original eighteenth-century formulation, is the principle that artistic representation should capture the distinctive, individuating traits of its object rather than conforming it to a pre-established norm of beauty. Guiseppe Spalletti, the first articulate proponent, defined the beautiful as "the inherent modification of the object under observation which presents it in the inevitably characteristic manner in which it is bound to appear" (026-364). On this view, the pleasure afforded by beauty is intellectual — it is the pleasure of apprehending truth through the convergence of characteristics proper to the object represented.

For the art-historian Ludwig Hirt, who revived the position in Germany in 1797, character is "that individuality by which form, movement, signs, physiognomy and expression, local colour, fight, shade and chiaroscuro are distinguished and represented in the manner demanded by the object" (026-364). The characteristic thus becomes a principle of fidelity that applies equally to gods, heroes, and animals — it makes no distinction between the beautiful and the ugly, only between the truthfully rendered and the falsified.

## In Croce's System

### The Aristotelian Revolt against Neo-Platonism

Croce interprets the characteristic debate as a recurrence of the ancient tension between Aristotelian and neo-Platonic aesthetics. The Winckelmann-Mengs doctrine of ideal beauty — "the union of the perfect and the pleasing made visible" (026-364) — represents neo-Platonism revived: Beauty is conceived as a transcendent form imperfectly instantiated in particular things. Spalletti's characteristic, by contrast, recovers the Aristotelian insight that artistic imitation concerns the individual in its concrete truth, not the abstract universal.

Croce's own aesthetics of intuition-expression aligns with the spirit of the characteristic, though he would reject its intellectualist framing. For Croce, art does not represent a truth external to it — it is the formation of intuitive knowledge. Yet Spalletti's insistence that even ugly things "represented characteristically" give pleasure because the spectator "delights in having increased his cognitions" (026-364) anticipates Croce's own argument that aesthetic value resides in successful expression, not in the pre-existing beauty of the subject matter.

### Key Distinctions

The primary distinction is between **the characteristic and ideal beauty**. Ideal beauty selects and perfects; the characteristic receives and renders. The former asks "what should this be?"; the latter asks "what is this, in its individual truth?" A painting of an old woman, to borrow an illustration Croce cites from Conti, may be "highly beautiful, if depicted as having suffered all the dilapidations of age" (025-075) — not despite, but because of, its fidelity to the characteristic.

A second distinction opposes **the characteristic to abstract perfection**. When an artist depicts "a man of fine proportions having the face of a lovely woman," Spalletti argues that the spectator "esteems that man ugly rather than the reverse, through deficiency of the characteristic of truth" (026-364). The defect is not in any individual feature but in the failure of the whole to cohere as *this* individual. Beauty is the felt coherence of characteristic traits, not the sum of independently attractive features.

## Historical Context

Spalletti's *Essay on Beauty* (1764) was composed as a letter to Mengs, with whom he had discussed the subject "in the solitudes of Grottaferrata" (026-364). Its polemical character, though not openly asserted, is discernible in every page. Croce welcomes "the sound good sense of this obscure Spalletti, upholder of the Aristotelian position against the revived neo-Platonism of the æstheticians" (026-364).

In Germany, the characteristic debate took a different turn. Hirt's radical position — that expression and character, not ideal beauty, are the principle of art — provoked a search for compromise. Heinrich Meyer, starting from Winckelmann, arrived at a series of intermediate positions. Goethe, "forgetful of his youthful days when he chanted the praises of Gothic architecture" (026-364), proposed a synthesis: the characteristic is the "mere point of departure" from which the artist's elaboration produces beauty. "We must start from the characteristic in order to attain the beautiful" (026-364).

## Criticism and Debate

> The first voice of dissent from this doctrine of ideal beauty was raised in 1764 by a small circle of Italians who asserted the characteristic to be the principle of art. In contrast to the fallacious profundity of Winckelmann and Mengs we welcome the sound good sense of this obscure Spalletti. (026-364)
