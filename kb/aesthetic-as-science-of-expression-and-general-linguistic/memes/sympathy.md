# Sympathy

In Croce's aesthetic theory, sympathy (*simpatia*) is a feeling that accompanies aesthetic experience — the emotional resonance between spectator and artwork — but it is not the defining characteristic of art. Croce critically examines theories that reduce art to the arousal of sympathy, most notably Tolstoy's, and argues that sympathy is a derivative psychological effect rather than the essence of aesthetic activity.

## Definition

Sympathy, in the aesthetic context, is the emotional concord between the spectator and the represented content: "the object of sympathy is our objectified ego, transported into the object contemplated" (037-096). It arises when we recognize ourselves in the feelings depicted by art. However, Croce insists that "the aesthetic feeling of sympathy is not a mere mode of" the aesthetic act itself (037-100) — it is a secondary elaboration, not the primary intuition-expression that constitutes art.

## In Croce's System

### Sympathy and the Theory of Art

Croce addresses sympathy most extensively in his critique of Tolstoy's theory. Tolstoy argued that art's purpose is to transmit feeling from artist to audience, awakening sympathy and thereby promoting moral community. Croce rejects this: "Development of social sympathy is the whole duty of art" in Tolstoy's view (036-350), but this confuses art's effects with its essence. Art involves a "complex nature" of reactions, including "sympathy with personages represented" (036-376), but none of these effects constitute its defining activity.

### Sympathy Versus Aesthetic Pleasure

Croce distinguishes aesthetic pleasure from sympathetic feeling. Sympathy is a psychological reaction to *what* is represented (the content); aesthetic pleasure is satisfaction in *how* it is expressed (the form). To base art on sympathy is to make artistic value dependent on the spectator's emotional disposition toward fictional characters — which varies unpredictably between individuals and cultures.

### Grace and Sympathy

In the analysis of grace, sympathy appears as a component: "graceful as sympathy for power in conjunction with benevolence" (036-034). The graceful object evokes sympathy because it suggests power held in reserve, accessible but not threatening. Yet even here, sympathy accompanies rather than constitutes the aesthetic fact.

## Historical Context

The association of art with sympathy has a long lineage. **Aristotle**'s concept of *katharsis* involves sympathetic identification with tragic heroes. Eighteenth-century moral sense philosophers (**Hutcheson**, **Hume**, **Adam Smith**) made sympathy central to both ethics and aesthetics. **Tolstoy**'s *What Is Art?* (1898) represents the most ambitious attempt to ground art entirely in sympathetic contagion. Croce engages this tradition to distinguish his own position: art is expression, not infection.

## Criticism and Debate

> The sharp separation between aesthetic pleasure and sympathy may underestimate how deeply emotional resonance shapes our experience of narrative art. A tragedy that leaves us cold has failed, regardless of its formal virtues. Croce might reply that "leaving us cold" indicates a failure of expression — the artist has not successfully formed the intuition, so the spectator cannot re-create it. The feeling of sympathy is a symptom of successful expression, not its cause.
