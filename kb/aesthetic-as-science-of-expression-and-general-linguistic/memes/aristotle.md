# Aristotle

**Aristotle** (384--322 BCE), the Greek philosopher from Stagira, was a student of **[[Plato]]** and tutor to Alexander the Great, and his *Poetics* represents the first systematic attempt in Western thought to define the nature of art, poetry, and **[[Imitation]]**. Croce's *Aesthetic* treats Aristotle as the originator of the "scientific tendency" in aesthetic enquiry, one who posed the fundamental question -- how art differs from science and from history -- even though, in Croce's judgment, he failed to reach a definitive answer (020-415). Aristotle's reflections on **[[Beauty]]**, **[[mimesis]]**, and catharsis permeate the entire subsequent history of Western aesthetics, from the Renaissance to the present day.

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Aristotle was born in 384 BCE in Stagira, a Greek colony on the Chalcidice peninsula. His father, Nicomachus, served as court physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon, which gave Aristotle early exposure to the natural sciences and empirical observation. At the age of seventeen, he moved to Athens to study at **[[Plato]]**'s Academy, where he remained for nearly twenty years until Plato's death in 347 BCE. Although deeply influenced by Platonic thought, Aristotle gradually developed a philosophical method that diverged sharply from his teacher's doctrine of transcendent Ideas, grounding knowledge instead in the empirical study of the natural world (020-424). This tension between Platonic and Aristotelian approaches would later define much of Western aesthetic theory.

### Career and Major Works

After leaving Athens, Aristotle spent several years in Asia Minor and on the island of Lesbos, conducting biological research before being summoned to the Macedonian court by King Philip II to tutor the young Alexander. Upon returning to Athens in 335 BCE, he founded his own school, the Lyceum (the Peripatetic School), where he produced the bulk of his written corpus. His works on aesthetics and poetics include the seminal *Poetics*, the *Rhetoric*, and several passages of the *Politics* and the *Nicomachean Ethics* that touch on beauty and art (020-181). The *Poetics*, though extant only in a fragmentary form, became the most influential text in the history of Western criticism, particularly through its definitions of tragedy, mimesis, and catharsis.

In the *Poetics*, Aristotle defines tragedy as "an imitation of a serious and complete action, having size, in language adorned in accordance with the requirements of the different parts, its exposition to be by action and not by narration, and using pity or terror as means to free or purify us from these same passions" (020-330). This definition -- the first systematic account of a literary genre in Western philosophy -- would dominate European poetic theory for more than two millennia.

### Later Years and Legacy

Following the death of Alexander the Great in 323 BCE, anti-Macedonian sentiment in Athens forced Aristotle to flee to Chalcis, where he died the following year at the age of sixty-two. His school, the Lyceum, continued under his successor Theophrastus, but his writings were lost to the Western world for several centuries, preserved mainly in the Arab world before being reintroduced to Europe through Latin translations in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The *Poetics* itself was hardly known in late antiquity and the early Middle Ages (020-491); it was only during the Renaissance that it achieved its extraordinary influence, becoming the cornerstone of poetic theory across Europe.

Croce observes that although Aristotle re-examined the aesthetic problem "with marvellous acuteness after Plato," he "failed truly to rid himself of the Platonic definition, by substituting a firmly-established one of his own" (020-480). Nevertheless, subsequent thinkers -- from the Italian humanists of the sixteenth century to the German idealists of the nineteenth -- repeatedly returned to Aristotle's formulations as the starting point for their own investigations.

## Contributions to Aesthetic

### Mimesis and the Nature of Art

Aristotle's greatest contribution to aesthetic theory lies in his reformulation of **[[mimesis]]**. Unlike Plato, who condemned mimesis as a mere copy of a copy, Aristotle argued that imitation is natural to human beings and constitutes a form of contemplative or theoretic activity (020-436). He felt that the Platonic condemnation of art "could not be altogether true, and that some aspect of the problem must have been neglected" (020-424). By grounding mimesis in human nature rather than in metaphysical deficiency, Aristotle opened the way for a positive valuation of artistic creation.

Croce attributes to Aristotle the insight that mimesis, "being proper to man by nature, is contemplation or theoretic activity" (020-436). This recognition of the cognitive dimension of art stands as one of the foundational moves in Western aesthetics. However, Croce also notes that Aristotle's system, which "admits practical sciences and poietic activities (distinguished from the practical as leaving a material object behind them), disturbed the firm and constant consideration of artistic mimesis and poetry as a theoretical activity" (020-442).

### The Distinction between Poetry, History, and Science

Aristotle posed the fundamental question of aesthetics in terms that Croce considers "the true and only way of stating it" (020-445): by what characteristic is poetry distinguished from scientific knowledge and from historical knowledge? His answer -- that poetry deals with the universal and the possible, while history deals with the particular and the actual -- established a framework that remains central to philosophical aesthetics to this day.

> "Poetry, answers Aristotle, differs from history, because, while the latter draws things that have happened, poetry draws things that may possibly happen, and differs from science, because, although it regards the universal and not the particular like history, it does not regard it in the same way as science, but in a certain measure, which the philosopher indicates by the word 'rather.'" (020-450)

Yet Croce is sharply critical of Aristotle's execution: "no sooner does Aristotle attempt to determine the meaning of these words, than he falls into contradictions and fallacies" (020-459). The universal of poetry, which Aristotle identifies with the possible, "seems to identify itself for him with the probable or the necessary" (020-459), creating a conceptual confusion that Croce argues prevents Aristotle from reaching a clear and consistent theory of art.

### The Concept of Catharsis

Aristotle's theory of **catharsis** -- the purgation of pity and fear through tragic representation -- has been one of the most debated concepts in the history of aesthetics. During the Renaissance, it was frequently interpreted in pedagogical terms: tragedy was thought to demonstrate the instability of fortune or to terrify spectators into moral virtue (020-181). Croce's work emphasizes that this pedagogical reading, sustained by the authority of Aristotle's name, "was popularized in France, Spain, England and Germany, together with all the Italian poetic doctrines of the Renaissance" (021-194).

### The Beautiful and Its Relation to Art

Aristotle's scattered remarks on the beautiful show him "changeable and uncertain upon the point" (020-309). He sometimes confounds the beautiful with the good, defining it as "that which is both good and pleasing"; at other times he places beauty in "bigness and in order," in symmetry, or in the immutable structures studied by mathematics (020-309). Croce notes that these affirmations, however influential, "meet with unsurmountable obstacles" (020-329) and never achieve a clear integration with Aristotle's theory of mimesis.

Nevertheless, Aristotle also made the important observation that we take pleasure in viewing faithful imitations of things that would be repugnant in reality, such as the forms of contemptible animals or corpses (020-330). This insight -- that aesthetic pleasure is distinct from the practical pleasure we take in the objects themselves -- marks an early recognition of what modern philosophers would call the aesthetic attitude.

## Later Influence

### Renaissance and the Poetics

The Renaissance witnessed an explosion of commentary on Aristotle's *Poetics*, with Italian humanists such as Robortelli, Castelvetro, and Scaliger producing extensive interpretations. However, Croce argues that these commentators frequently "transplanted bodily into the text of Aristotle" the pedagogical theory of art that had dominated medieval thought (021-194). Scaliger declared that mimesis was "*finis medius ad illum ultimum qui est docendi cum delectatione*" (021-194), believing himself to be in full agreement with Aristotle.

The disputed question of the universal and the probable in art, which runs through Renaissance poetics from Fracastoro to Patrizzi, derives directly from Aristotle's unresolved formulations. Croce's history traces the persistence of these debates through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, demonstrating the extraordinary longevity of Aristotelian categories in European thought.

### Influence on German Idealism

Aristotle's aesthetic concepts were taken up and transformed by the German idealists, particularly **[[G. W. F. Hegel]]** and **[[Friedrich Schiller]]**, as well as by **[[Arthur Schopenhauer]]** and **[[Friedrich Schleiermacher]]**. Hegel's theory of art as the "sensible appearance of the Idea" reworks, in a metaphysical register, Aristotle's insight that art partakes of the universal. Schiller's theory of aesthetic education as the reconciliation of sense and reason similarly echoes Aristotelian themes. Croce's own aesthetic -- which identifies art with intuition and expression -- can be seen as an attempt to resolve precisely the difficulties that Aristotle first formulated but left unresolved (020-480).

## Criticism and Reception

Croce's evaluation of Aristotle is characteristically ambivalent: he credits him with posing the aesthetic question in its correct form, but judges that he failed to provide a satisfactory answer.

> "Aristotle, in fact, after having made so good a beginning in the discovery of the purely imaginative, proper to poetry, remains half-way, perplexed and uncertain. Thus he sometimes makes the truth of imitation consist in a certain learning and syllogizing that takes place when we look at imitations... or, worse, he loses the grains of truth that he has found." (020-465)

This assessment reflects Croce's broader view that a genuine science of aesthetics -- one that recognizes the autonomy of the expressive activity -- did not emerge until the modern era. Nevertheless, Croce acknowledges that Aristotle's formulations, for all their incompleteness, constitute the indispensable starting point for any serious philosophical reflection on art.

> "The attempts at a true Aesthetic, excluding certain luminous but incidental observations to be found even in Plato, go back to Aristotle and are altogether independent of his few and feeble speculations as to the beautiful." (020-422)

The quotation captures Croce's distinctive stance: he dismisses Aristotle's theory of beauty as "few and feeble speculations," yet locates the origin of scientific aesthetics precisely in Aristotle's attempt to define art as a distinctive form of knowledge -- an attempt that failed in its particulars but set the agenda for two thousand years of subsequent inquiry.
