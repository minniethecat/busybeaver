# Naturalism

In Croce's critical taxonomy of aesthetic errors, naturalism is the doctrine that art should imitate nature, represent external reality faithfully, or be judged by standards of probability and verisimilitude. Croce rejects naturalism as a fundamental confusion between aesthetic expression and logical or practical concerns.

## Definition

Naturalism is the theory that aesthetic value consists in the faithful reproduction of natural or historical reality. Croce treats it under the heading "Criticism of probability and of naturalism" (005-005) as one of the primary errors that arise from mistaking the nature of artistic expression. "Verism and naturalism also have afforded the" clearest examples of the imitation fallacy (005-035).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to the Imitation Theory

Naturalism is a species of the broader imitation theory. While the general imitation theory holds that art imitates nature, naturalism adds the demand for fidelity and probability. Croce's critique is that art neither imitates nor reproduces reality — it *expresses* intuitions. The demand for naturalistic accuracy confuses the aesthetic fact with logical judgment about external correspondence.

### Key Distinctions

1. **Naturalism vs. Realism** — While both terms refer to fidelity to external reality, naturalism in Croce's usage specifically denotes the theoretical error of making such fidelity the criterion of aesthetic value. Realism as an artistic movement may produce genuine expressions despite its theoretical commitments.

2. **Naturalism vs. Expression** — Naturalism demands correspondence to an external model; expression demands only internal coherence of the intuition. "The same vague naturalism exercised a baneful influence" precisely because it substituted external standards for internal aesthetic judgment (036-424).

3. **Probability vs. Aesthetic Truth** — The demand for probability in art ("censuring of *Jerusalem Delivered* based upon the history of the Crusades") treats art as if it were history or science (005-031).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The imitation theory originates with Plato and Aristotle, but naturalism as a specific doctrine gained force in the nineteenth century with the rise of positivism and scientific materialism. Croce sees naturalism as the aesthetic counterpart of positivistic philosophy: "positivism, to naturalism for this unhealthy and often" degrading conception of art (009-095).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's critique of naturalism is more radical than earlier idealist critiques because it is grounded in his identification of art with intuition-expression. If art is internal expression, the question of whether it "corresponds" to external nature is simply irrelevant. The aesthetic fact is autonomous.

## Criticism and Debate

> Crass naturalism consists of a sort of eclecticism that picks and chooses from external reality without the unifying power of genuine aesthetic intuition (019-136).

Defenders of naturalism argue that Croce's dismissal is too sweeping — that the demand for fidelity to nature has historically been a productive constraint on artistic creation, not merely a theoretical error. Croce's response is that whatever is aesthetically valuable in naturalistic works comes from their expressive quality, not from their naturalism.
