# Sincerity

The demand that art be sincere appears in nearly every tradition of aesthetic thought, from Plato's suspicion of poetic falsehoods to Romantic celebrations of authentic feeling. Croce subjects this demand to a searching critique, concluding that sincerity, properly understood, belongs to the practical order of moral life, not to the theoretical order of aesthetic expression.

## Definition

Croce addresses sincerity most directly in his "Criticism of the concept of sincerity in art" (007-216). He observes that "sincerity imposed as a duty upon the artist" arises from a confusion between two distinct meanings of the term (007-217). In its first sense, sincerity refers to the moral quality of the artist as a person — the obligation not to deceive. In its second, looser sense, it refers to the correspondence between the artist's internal state and its external expression.

Croce's analysis shows that neither sense provides an aesthetic criterion. Moral sincerity is a practical virtue, not an artistic one; a wicked person may produce great art, and a saintly person may produce none. The second sense collapses into the concept of expression itself: "For by sincerity may be meant, in the first place, the immediate and spontaneous correspondence between the intuition and its expression" (007-219). But this correspondence is precisely what defines successful expression — to add "sincerity" adds nothing.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Sincerity illustrates the crucial Crocean distinction between the **practical** and the **theoretic**. The artist as a moral agent may be sincere or insincere, just as she may be generous or mean, courageous or cowardly. These practical qualities bear on her life as a person but not on her work as an artist. A sonnet is not made better by the poet's honesty in daily life, nor worse by her duplicity.

When critics praise a work for its sincerity, what they typically mean is that the expression appears unforced and coherent — that the intuition and its expression form an indivisible unity. "If by sincerity we mean the fullness and truth of the expression," Croce notes, "we are saying nothing other than that the expression is successful" (007-227). The term "sincerity" is thus either a moral judgment irrelevant to art or a redundant restatement of aesthetic success.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes three senses of sincerity:

1. **Moral sincerity**: the ethical obligation not to deceive — relevant to the artist as a person, irrelevant to the artwork as expression.
2. **Expressive sincerity**: the correspondence between inner state and outer form — identical with successful expression, and therefore not a separate criterion.
3. **Rhetorical sincerity**: the appearance of earnestness in the work itself — a technical effect achieved through expressive skill, not evidence of the artist's actual feelings.

"Every attempt at insincerity," Croce observes, produces a failure of expression; "compassion ashamed to weep" cannot find adequate artistic form for the very feeling it tries to conceal (013-131). The remedy is not moral exhortation to sincerity but aesthetic attention to expressive coherence.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The demand for sincerity in art gained particular force in the Romantic period, when the doctrine of self-expression elevated the artist's authentic feeling to the supreme artistic value. Wordsworth's definition of poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings" made sincerity the measure of poetic worth. Ruskin and Carlyle extended this moral-aesthetic fusion, judging art by the perceived honesty of its creator.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to dismantle the Romantic fusion of ethics and aesthetics without denying the importance of either. By relocating sincerity to the practical sphere, Croce preserves its moral force while preventing it from functioning as a pseudo-aesthetic criterion. This allows him to acknowledge that artists, like all persons, have moral obligations — while insisting that the fulfillment of these obligations neither guarantees nor precludes artistic achievement.

## Criticism and Debate

> Finally, sincerity imposed as a duty upon the artist is either a moral duty irrelevant to art or a redundant name for successful expression (007-217, 007-219).

Critics have argued that Croce's separation of ethics and aesthetics, while philosophically rigorous, neglects the ways in which moral qualities can become *internal* to artistic evaluation. When we condemn propaganda art as insincere, we are not merely noting a moral failing; we are identifying a defect in the work's aesthetic coherence. The insincerity has deformed the expression itself.

Croce's defender might reply that this objection only reinforces his point: the defect is in the expression, not in the moral character of the propagandist. A skilled propagandist who genuinely believed her message would produce the same artistic result as one who cynically manipulated her audience — the aesthetic judgment turns on the expression, not the intention behind it.
