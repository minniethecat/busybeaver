[032-001] *Minor æstheticians in the metaphysical school.*

[032-002] When we turn from the pages of methodical and serious thinkers such as

[032-003] [[friedrich-schleiermacher|Schleiermacher]], Humboldt and Steinthal, we are filled with distaste

[032-004] by the books written in enormous quantities during the first half of

[032-005] the nineteenth century by disciples of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]. We are

[032-006] fatigued and almost disgusted as we pass from this illuminating and

[032-007] scientific study to something which oscillates between vapid fancies

[032-008] and charlatanism; between the vanity of empty formulæ and the attempt,

[032-009] not always free from dishonesty, to employ them in order to amaze and

[032-010] overwhelm the reader or student.

[032-011] *Krause, Trahndorff, Weisse and others.*

[032-012] Why should we encumber a general History of Æsthetic (which ought,

[032-013] certainly, to take account of aberrations from the truth, but only in

[032-014] so far as they indicate the general trend of contemporary thought) with

[032-015] the theories of such men as Krause, Trahndorff, Weisse, Deutinger,

[032-016] Oersted, Zeising, Eckardt and the crowd of manipulators of manuals and

[032-017] systems? The only one who obtained a hearing outside his native [[germany|Germany]]

[032-018] was Krause, who was imported into Spain; we are justified, therefore,

[032-019] in leaving them to the memory or forgetfulness of their compatriots.

[032-020] For Krause,[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_1_496) the humanitarian, the freethinker, the theosophist,

[032-021] everything is organism, everything is beauty; beauty is organism, and

[032-022] organism is beauty: Essence, that is to say God, is one, free and

[032-023] entire; one, free and entire is [[beauty|Beauty]]. There is but one artist, God;

[032-024] but one art, the divine. The beauty of finite things is the Divinity,

[032-025] or rather the likeness of Divinity manifested in the finite. Beauty

[032-026] brings into play reason, intellect and imagination in a mode conforming

[032-027] to their laws, and awakens disinterested pleasure and inclination in

[032-028] the soul. Trahndorff,[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_2_497) describing the various degrees by which the

[032-029] individual seeks to grasp the essence or form of the universe (the

[032-030] degrees of feeling, intuition, reflexion and presentiment), and noting

[032-031] the insufficiency of simple theoretical knowledge till supplemented

[032-032] by the Will, the Will which is power (*Können*), in its three degrees

[032-033] of Aspiration, Faith and Love, places the Beautiful in the highest

[032-034] grade, in Love: it would seem, therefore, that Beauty is Love which

[032-035] comprehends itself. Christian Weisse[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_3_498) attempted, like Trahndorff, to

[032-036] reconcile the God of Christianity with the Hegelian philosophy: in his

[032-037] estimation the æsthetic Idea is superior to the logical, and leads to

[032-038] religion, to God; the idea of beauty, existing outside the sensible

[032-039] universe, is the reality of the concept of beauty, and, as the idea of

[032-040] divinity is absolute Love, so must that of Beauty be found truly in

[032-041] Love. The same reconciliation was attempted by the Catholic theologian

[032-042] Deutinger;[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_4_499) beauty, for him, is born of power (*Können*), an activity

[032-043] parallel with those of the knowledge of truth and the doing of good

[032-044] but (differing in this from knowledge, which is receptive) realizing

[032-045] itself in an outward movement from within, mastering the world of

[032-046] matter and imprinting upon it the seal of personality. An internal

[032-047] ideal intuition, the Idea: an external shapable matter: the power of

[032-048] interpenetrating internal with external, invisible with visible, ideal

[032-049] with real: such is Beauty. Oersted[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_5_500) (the celebrated Danish naturalist

[032-050] whose works were translated into German and gained him a considerable

[032-051] reputation in Germany) defines beauty as the objective Idea in the

[032-052] moment of subjective contemplation: the Idea expressed in things in so

[032-053] far as it reveals itself to intuition. Zeising[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_6_501) turned his attention

[032-054] partly to exploration of the mysteries of the golden section, and

[032-055] partly to speculations on Beauty, which he considered as one of the

[032-056] three forms of the Idea; first, the Idea which expresses itself in

[032-057] object and subject; secondly, the Idea as intuition; and thirdly, the

[032-058] Absolute which appears in the world and is conceived intuitively by

[032-059] the spirit. Eckardt,[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_7_502) intent on creating a theistic Æsthetic which

[032-060] should avoid the one-sided transcendence of deism on the one hand and

[032-061] the one-sided immanence of pantheism on the other, maintained that its

[032-062] principles must be sought not in the feelings of the contemplator, not

[032-063] in works of art, not in the idea of the beautiful, not in the concept

[032-064] of art, but in the creative spirit of the artist, the original fount

[032-065] of beauty; and since a creative artist cannot be conceived except as

[032-066] derived from the highest [[genius|creative genius]] which is God, Eckardt invokes

[032-067] aid from a psychology of God (*eine Psychologie des Weltkünstlers*).

[032-068] *Fried. Theodor Vischer.*

[032-069] If quantity is as important as quality, we must devote some space to

[032-070] Friedrich Theodor Vischer, the bulkiest of all German æstheticians,

[032-071] indeed the German æsthetician *par excellence*: after publishing a book

[032-072] on *The [[sublime|Sublime]] and the Comic, a contribution to the Philosophy of

[032-073] the Beautiful*,[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_8_503) in 1837, he produced four huge tomes on *Æsthetic

[032-074] as Science of the Beautiful* between 1846 and 1857,[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_9_504) where, in

[032-075] hundreds of paragraphs and long observations and sub-observations, is

[032-076] massed a stupendous amount of æsthetic material, of matter foreign to

[032-077] Æsthetic, and of subjects taken haphazard from the whole thinkable

[032-078] universe. Vischer's work is divided into three parts: a Metaphysic of

[032-079] the Beautiful, which investigates the concept of Beauty in itself, no

[032-080] matter where and how it is realized: a treatise on concrete Beauty,

[032-081] which inquires into the two one-sided modes of realization, Beauty

[032-082] of nature and Beauty of imagination, one lacking subjective, the

[032-083] other lacking objective, existence: lastly, a theory of the arts,

[032-084] which studies the synthesis in art of the two artistic moments, the

[032-085] physical and psychical, the objective and subjective. It is easy to

[032-086] sum up Vischer's concept of æsthetic activity; it is Hegel's concept,

[032-087] debased. For Vischer, Beauty belongs neither to the theoretical nor to

[032-088] the practical activity, but is placed in a serene sphere, superior to

[032-089] these antitheses; that is to say in the sphere of absolute Spirit, in

[032-090] company with Religion and Philosophy;[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_10_505) but, in contradistinction to

[032-091] Hegel, Vischer assigns the first place in this sphere to Religion, the

[032-092] second to [[art|Art]], and the third to Philosophy. Much ingenuity was devoted

[032-093] in those days to moving these words about like pieces on a chess-board;

[032-094] it has been observed that of the six possible combinations of the

[032-095] three terms Art, Religion and Philosophy, four were actually adopted:

[032-096] by Schelling, *P.R.A.*; by Hegel, *A.R.P.*; by Weisse, *P.A.R.*; and

[032-097] by Vischer, *R.A.P*.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_11_506) But Vischer himself[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_12_507) states that Wirth,

[032-098] author of a *System of Ethics*,[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_13_508) opted for the fifth combination,

[032-099] *R.P.A.,* which leaves us but the sixth, *A.P.R.,* unclaimed, unless

[032-100] (as is not improbable) some unrecognized genius seized upon it and made

[032-101] it the text of his system. Beauty, therefore, as the second form of

[032-102] the absolute Spirit, is the realization of the Idea, not as abstract

[032-103] concept but as union of concept and reality; and the Idea determines

[032-104] itself as species (*Gattung*), and every idea of a species, even on the

[032-105] lowest degree, is beautiful as being an integral part in the totality

[032-106] of Ideas; although the higher the degree of the idea the greater is

[032-107] its beauty.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_14_509) Highest of all degrees is that of human personality:

[032-108] "in this spiritual world the Idea attains its true significance; the

[032-109] name of idea is given to the great moral motive powers to which the

[032-110] concept of species may also be applied in the sense that they stand

[032-111] to their restricted spheres in the same relation in which the genus

[032-112] stands to its species and individuals." At the head of all is the Idea

[032-113] of morality: "the world of moral and autonomous ends is destined to

[032-114] furnish the most important, the most worthy content of the Beautiful";

[032-115] with the warning, however, that Beauty, in actualizing this world

[032-116] through intuition, excludes art having a moral tendency.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_15_510) So Vischer

[032-117] proceeds now to degrade Hegel's Idea to the simple class-concept,

[032-118] now to couple it with the idea of the Good; now, in accord with the

[032-119] teaching of his master, to make it different from, yet superior to,

[032-120] intellect and morality.

[032-121] *Other tendencies.*

[032-122] From the first, the Herbartian formalism was little studied and less

[032-123] followed: two writers, Griepenkerl in 1827 and Bobrik in 1834, made

[032-124] some attempt to develop and apply the cursory notes with which [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]]

[032-125] contented himself.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_16_511) Schleiermacher's lectures, even before their

[032-126] appearance in book form, had served as basis for a series of elegant

[032-127] dissertations by Erich Ritter (1840)[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_17_512) (better known as a historian

[032-128] of philosophy); his work is of little value, for instead of dwelling

[032-129] on the important points of the master's doctrine Ritter brings into

[032-130] prominence secondary matters relating to sociability and the æsthetic

[032-131] fife. A penetrating critic of German Æsthetic from [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]] to the

[032-132] post-Kantian school was Wilhelm Theodor Danzel, who lived about this

[032-133] time and very properly rebelled against the claim to find "thought" in

[032-134] works of art: "Artistic thought:" he writes; "unhappy phrase, which

[032-135] helped to condemn an entire epoch to the Sisyphean labour of trying to

[032-136] reduce art to intellectual and rational thinking! The thought of a

[032-137] work of art is nothing save that which is contemplated in a definite

[032-138] way; it is not represented, as is commonly asserted, in a work of art,

[032-139] it is the work of art itself. Artistic thought can never be expressed

[032-140] by concepts and words."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_18_513) By his early death Danzel ended the hopes

[032-141] he raised by his original views on the science and history of Æsthetic.

[032-142] *Theory of the Beautiful in nature, and that of the

[032-143] Modifications of Beauty.*

[032-144] The post-Hegelian metaphysical Æsthetic is chiefly noteworthy for

[032-145] the fuller development of two theories or, to speak more accurately,

[032-146] of two very curious combinations of arbitrary assertion and fanciful

[032-147] caprice: the so-called theory of Natural Beauty, and the theory of

[032-148] Modifications of the Beautiful. Neither of the two had any intimate

[032-149] or necessary connexion with this philosophical movement, to which

[032-150] they are rather linked by historical or psychological causes; by the

[032-151] relationship between facts of pleasure and pain and the inclination

[032-152] towards mysticism; by the confusion arising from the really æsthetic

[032-153] (imaginative) quality of some representations wrongly described as

[032-154] observation of natural beauties; or by the scholastic and literary

[032-155] tradition of discussing these cases of pleasure and pain and

[032-156] extra-æsthetic natural beauties in books devoted to the discussion

[032-157] of art.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_19_514) These metaphysicians were sometimes rather grotesque

[032-158] and remind one of the story told of Paisiello, that in the fury of

[032-159] composition he set even the stage directions of his libretto to music;

[032-160] bitten with the rage for construction and dialectic, they did not spare

[032-161] even the indexes of chaotic old books, but seized on them as suitable

[032-162] material for a dialectical exercise.

[032-163] *Development of the first theory. Herder.*

[032-164] Beginning with the theory of Natural Beauty, observations on beautiful

[032-165] natural objects are found among the inquiries of the ancient

[032-166] philosophers on beauty, and especially among the mystical effusions

[032-167] of neo-Platonists and their followers in the Middle Ages and the

[032-168] [[renaissance|Renaissance]].[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_20_515) Less frequently such questions were introduced into

[032-169] treatises on Poetics: Tesauro (1654) is among the first who, in his

[032-170] *Cannochiale aristotelico,* discusses not only the conceits of men, but

[032-171] also of God, the angels, nature and animals; and somewhat later (1707)

[032-172] Muratori speaks of "the beauty of matter," of which examples are "the

[032-173] gods, a flower, the sun, a rivulet."[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_21_516) Observations on that which

[032-174] is outside art and is merely natural, are made by Crousaz, by André,

[032-175] and especially by those authors of the eighteenth century who wrote

[032-176] on Beauty and Art in an empirical and gallant style.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_22_517) It was the

[032-177] influence of these persons that led [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], as we have seen, to sever the

[032-178] theory of beauty from that of art, specially connecting free beauty

[032-179] with objects of nature and those productions of man which reproduce

[032-180] natural beauties.[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_23_518) When the adversary of Kant's theory of Æsthetic,

[032-181] Herder (1800), in his sketch of an ethical system united spirit and

[032-182] nature, pleasure and value, feeling and intellect, he inevitably made

[032-183] much of natural beauty, and affirmed that everything in nature has its

[032-184] own beauty, the expression of its own greatest content, and that this

[032-185] accounts for the ascending scale of beautiful objects: beginning with.

[032-186] outlines, colours and tones, light and sound, and proceeding by way of

[032-187] flowers, water and sea, to birds, terrestrial animals, and man himself.

[032-188] For instance "a bird is the sum of the properties and perfections of

[032-189] its element, a representation of its potency, a creature of light, song

[032-190] and air"; amongst terrestrial animals, the ugliest are those resembling

[032-191] man, as the melancholy moping monkey; the most beautiful, those of

[032-192] perfect build, well proportioned, noble, free in action; those which

[032-193] express sweetness; those, in fine, which live in harmony and happiness,

[032-194] endowed with a perfection of their own, harmless to man.[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_24_519)

[032-195] *Schelling, [[karl-solger|Solger]], Hegel.*

[032-196] Schelling, on the contrary, utterly, denies the concept of beauty

[032-197] in nature, and considers that such beauty is purely accidental and

[032-198] that art alone supplies the norm by which it can be discovered and

[032-199] judged.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_25_520) Solger also excludes natural beauty;[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_26_521) so does Hegel,

[032-200] who distinguishes himself not by denying it but by proceeding with

[032-201] the utmost inconsequence to deal at length with the beautiful in

[032-202] nature. It is in fact not clear whether he means that really no beauty

[032-203] exists in nature and that man introduces it in his vision of things,

[032-204] or whether natural beauty really exists though inferior in degree

[032-205] to the beauty of art. "The beauty of art," he says," stands higher

[032-206] than that of nature; it is beauty born and reborn by the work of the

[032-207] spirit, and spirit alone is truth and reality; hence beauty is truly

[032-208] beauty only when it participates in spirit and is produced therefrom.

[032-209] Taken in this sense, the beauty of nature appears as a mere reflexion

[032-210] of the beauty appertaining to spirit, as an imperfect and incomplete

[032-211] mode, which substantially is contained within the spirit itself." In

[032-212] confirmation, he adds that nobody has attempted a systematic exposition

[032-213] of natural beauties, whereas there actually is, from the point of view

[032-214] of the utility of natural objects, a *materia medica*[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_27_522) But the

[032-215] second chapter of the first part of his Æsthetic is devoted precisely

[032-216] to natural Beauty on the ground that, in order to grasp the idea of

[032-217] artistic beauty in its entirety, three stages must be traversed: beauty

[032-218] in general, natural beauty (whose defects show the necessity for art),

[032-219] and, lastly, the Idea; "the first existence of the Idea is nature,

[032-220] and its first beauty is natural beauty." This beauty, which is beauty

[032-221] for us and not for itself, has several phases, from that in which the

[032-222] concept is immersed in matter to the point of disappearing, such as

[032-223] physical facts and isolated mechanisms, to that higher phase in which

[032-224] physical facts are united in systems (*e.g.* the solar system); but

[032-225] the Idea first reaches a true and real existence in organic facts, in

[032-226] the living creature. And even the living creature is liable to the

[032-227] distinction between beautiful and ugly; for example, among animals,

[032-228] the sloth, trailing itself laboriously and incapable of animation or

[032-229] activity, displeases us by its apathetic somnolence; nor can beauty be

[032-230] found in amphibians or in many kinds of fish, or in crocodiles, or

[032-231] toads, as well as in many insects and especially in those equivocal

[032-232] creatures which express a transition from one i class to another, such

[032-233] as the ornithorhyncus, a mixture of bird and beast.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_28_523) These samples

[032-234] may suffice to show the general trend of Hegel's doctrine of natural

[032-235] beauty; elsewhere he discusses the external beauty of abstract form,

[032-236] regularity, symmetry, harmony, etc., which are; precisely the concepts

[032-237] which the formalism of Herbart placed in the heaven of the Ideas of the

[032-238] Beautiful.

[032-239] *Schleiermacher.*

[032-240] Schleiermacher, who praised Hegel for his attempt to exclude natural

[032-241] beauty from his Æsthetic, excluded it from his own not verbally but

[032-242] actually, by confining his attention to the artistic perfection of

[032-243] the internal [[representation|image]] formed by the energy of the human spirit.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_29_524) But

[032-244] the so-called Feeling for Nature which came in with [[romanticism|Romanticism]], and

[032-245] the *Cosmos* and other descriptive works of Humboldt,[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_30_525) directed

[032-246] attention increasingly to the impressions awakened by natural facts.

[032-247] *Alexander Humboldt.*

[032-248] This led to the compilation of those systematic lists of natural

[032-249] beauties whose impossibility had been proclaimed by Hegel, though he

[032-250] himself had furnished an example of them; amongst others, Bratranek

[032-251] published an *Æsthetic of the Vegetable World.*[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_31_526)

[032-252] *Vischer's "Æsthetic Physics."*

[032-253] The best-known and most widely circulated treatment of the subject was

[032-254] contained in this very work of Vischer's; who following Hegel's example

[032-255] devoted a section of his *Æsthetic,* as we have seen, to the objective

[032-256] existence of Beauty, *i.e.* to the Beauty of nature, and entitled it by

[032-257] the perhaps new and certainly characteristic name of Æsthetic Physics

[032-258] (*ästhetische Physik*). This Æsthetic Physics comprised the beauty of

[032-259] inorganic nature (light, heat, air, water, earth); organic nature, with

[032-260] its four vegetable types and its animals vertebrate and invertebrate;

[032-261] and beauty of human beings, divided into generic and historic. The

[032-262] generic was subdivided into sections on the beauty of general forms

[032-263] (age, sex, conditions, love, marriage, family); of special forms

[032-264] (races, peoples, culture, political life); and of individual forms

[032-265] (temperament and character). Historical beauty included that of

[032-266] ancient history (Oriental, Greek, Roman), of Mediæval or Germanic, and

[032-267] of modern times; because, according to Vischer, it was the duty of

[032-268] Æsthetic to cast a glance over universal history before summing up the

[032-269] different degrees of the beautiful according to the varying phases of

[032-270] the struggle for freedom against nature.[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_32_527)

[032-271] *The Theory of the Modifications of Beauty. From antiquity

[032-272] to the eighteenth century.*

[032-273] As regards the Modifications of Beauty, it should be remembered that

[032-274] the ancient manuals of Poetics, and more frequently those of [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]],

[032-275] contained more or less scientific definitions of psychological states

[032-276] and facts; Aristotle attempted in his *Poetics* to determine the nature

[032-277] of a tragic action or personality, and sketched a definition of the

[032-278] comic; in his Rhetoric he writes at considerable length of wit;[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_33_528)

[032-279] sections of the *De oratore* of Cicero and the *Institutions* of

[032-280] Quintilian[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_34_529) are devoted to wit and the comic; the lofty style was

[032-281] the subject of a lost treatise of Cæcilius, which anticipated that

[032-282] attributed to Longinus, whose title was translated in modern times

[032-283] as *De sublimitate* or *On the Sublime.* Following the example of

[032-284] the ancients, this kind of medley was perpetuated by writers of the

[032-285] sixteenth and seventeenth centuries; whole treatises on the comic are

[032-286] incorporated in, for instance, the *Argutezza* of Matteo Pellegrini

[032-287] (1639) and the *Cannochiale* of Tesauro. La Bruyère treated of the

[032-288] sublime[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_35_530) and Boileau by his translation gave a fresh vogue to

[032-289] Longinus: the following century saw [[edmund-burke|Burke]] inquiring into the origin

[032-290] of our ideas of the beautiful and the sublime, and deriving the

[032-291] former from the instinct for sociability, the latter from that of

[032-292] self-preservation; he also tried to define ugliness, grace, elegance

[032-293] and extraordinary beauty; Home, in his celebrated *Elements of

[032-294] Criticism,* discussed grandeur, sublimity, the ridiculous, wit,

[032-295] dignity and grace: Mendelssohn discussed sublimity, dignity and

[032-296] grace in fine art, and described some of these facts as due to mixed

[032-297] feelings, in which he was followed by [[g-e-lessing|Lessing]][[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_36_531) and others: Sulzer

[032-298] welcomed all these various concepts into his æsthetic encyclopædia

[032-299] and collected round them an elaborate bibliography. A new and curious

[032-300] meaning of the word humour reached the continent from [[england|England]] at this

[032-301] time. Its original meaning was simply "temperament," and sometimes

[032-302] "spirit," or "wit" ("*belli umori*" in [[italy|Italy]]; in the seventeenth

[032-303] century there was in Rome an Academy of *Umoristi*). Voltaire

[032-304] introduced it into [[france|France]] and wrote in 1761, "*Les Anglais ont un terme

[032-305] pour signifier cette plaisanterie, ce vrai comique, cette gaieté, cette

[032-306] urbanité, ces saillies, qui échappent à un homme sans qu'il s'en doute;

[032-307] et ils rendent cette idée par le mot* humour ...";[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_37_532) in 1767 Lessing

[032-308] distinguishes humour from the German *Laune* (caprice, whim),[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_38_533) a

[032-309] distinction maintained by Herder in 1769 in opposition to Riedel who

[032-310] had confused the terms.[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_39_534)

[032-311] *Kant and the post-Kantians.*

[032-312] Accustomed to find all these subjects treated in the same book,

[032-313] philosophers at first theorized about them all without attempting to

[032-314] link them up together by introducing an artificial logical connexion.

[032-315] Kant, who had already in imitation of Burke written in 1764 a

[032-316] dissertation on the beautiful and the sublime, ingenuously remarked

[032-317] in the course of his lectures on Logic in 1771 that the beautiful and

[032-318] the æsthetic are not identical, because "the sublime also belongs to

[032-319] Æsthetic";[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_40_535) and in his *Critique of Judgment,* while treating of

[032-320] the comic in a mere digression (a magnificent piece of psychological

[032-321] analysis)[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_41_536) places side by side with and as if on an equality with

[032-322] the "Analytic of Beauty," an "Analytic of the Sublime."[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_42_537) We may

[032-323] note in passing that, before the publication of the third Critique,

[032-324] Heydenreich arrived at the same doctrine of the sublime which is

[032-325] contained in Kant's book.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_43_538) Did Kant ever think of uniting the

[032-326] beautiful and the sublime and deducing them from a single concept?

[032-327] Apparently not. By his declaration that the principle of beauty must

[032-328] be sought outside ourselves, and that of the sublime within us, he

[032-329] tacitly assumes that the two objects are wholly disparate. In 1805 Ast,

[032-330] a follower of Schelling, declared the necessity of overcoming what he

[032-331] called the Kantian dualism of the beautiful and the sublime:[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_44_539) others

[032-332] reproached Kant with having treated the comic by the psychological, not

[032-333] the metaphysical, method. [[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]] wrote a series of dissertations on

[032-334] the tragic, the sentimental, the ingenuous, the sublime, the pathetic,

[032-335] the trivial, the low, the dignified and the graceful, and their

[032-336] varieties, the fascinating, the majestic, the grave, and the solemn.

[032-337] Another artist, Jean Paul Richter, discoursed at great length on wit

[032-338] and humour, described by him as the romantic comic, or the sublime

[032-339] reversed (*umgekehrte Erhabene)*.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_45_540)

[032-340] Herbart, in virtue of his formalistic principle, asserts that all

[032-341] these concepts are irrelevant to Æsthetic; he attributes them to the

[032-342] work of art, not to pure beauty;[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_46_541) Schleiermacher comes to the same

[032-343] conclusion, but for much better reasons, as a result of his sane

[032-344] conception of art. Amongst other things he observes: "It is usual

[032-345] to describe the beautiful and the sublime as two kinds of artistic

[032-346] perfection; and so accustomed have we grown to the union of these

[032-347] two concepts that we must make an effort to convince ourselves how

[032-348] very far they are from being co-ordinate or from together exhausting

[032-349] the concept of artistic perfection"; he regrets that even the best

[032-350] æstheticians should give rhetorical descriptions of them instead of

[032-351] demonstrating them. "The thing," says he, "is not right and just" (*hat

[032-352] keine Richtigkeit*), and he proceeds to exclude the whole subject from

[032-353] his Æsthetic,[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_47_542) as he had done previously in the case of natural

[032-354] beauty. Other philosophers, however, clung persistently to their search

[032-355] for a connexion between these various concepts, and called in dialectic

[032-356] to help them. The habit of applying dialectic to empirical concepts

[032-357] affected everybody at that time; even the great enemy of dialectic,

[032-358] Herbart, showed the cloven hoof, when in order to explain the union of

[032-359] different æsthetic ideas in the beautiful he appealed to the formula

[032-360] "they lose regularity in order to regain it."[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_48_543) Schelling asserted

[032-361] that the sublime is the infinite in the finite, and the beautiful the

[032-362] finite in the infinite, adding that the absolutely sublime includes the

[032-363] beautiful, and the beautiful the sublime;[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_49_544) and Ast, whom we have

[032-364] mentioned already, spoke of a masculine, positive element, which is the

[032-365] sublime, and a feminine, negative element which is the graceful and

[032-366] pleasing: between which there is a contrast and a struggle.

[032-367] *Culmination of the development.*

[032-368] These exercises in dialectical system-building developed and increased

[032-369] till about the middle of the nineteenth century they assumed two

[032-370] distinct forms whose history must here be shortly outlined.

[032-371] *Double form of the theory. The overcoming of the ugly.

[032-372] Solger, Weisse and others.*

[032-373] The first form may be called the Overcoming of the Ugly. This theory

[032-374] conceives the comic, the sublime, the tragic, the humorous, and so

[032-375] forth, as so many engagements in the war between the Ugly and the

[032-376] Beautiful, wherein the latter was invariably victorious, and arose by

[032-377] means of this war to more and more lofty and complex manifestations.

[032-378] The second form of the theory may be described as the Passage from

[032-379] Abstract to Concrete; it held that Beauty cannot emerge from the

[032-380] abstract, cannot become this or that concrete beauty, except by

[032-381] particularizing itself in the comic, tragic, sublime, humorous, or

[032-382] some other modification. The first form was already well developed in

[032-383] Solgei, an adherent of the romantic theory of Irony: but historically

[032-384] it presupposes the æsthetic theory of the Ugly, first sketched by

[032-385] Friedrich Schlegel in 1797. We have already noted that Schlegel

[032-386] considered the characteristic or interesting, not the beautiful, to

[032-387] be the principle of modern art; hence the importance attached by him

[032-388] to the piquant, the striking (*frappant*), the daring, the cruel, the

[032-389] ugly.[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_50_545) Solger found here the basis for his dialectic; amongst other

[032-390] things he maintains that the finite, earthly element may be dissolved

[032-391] and absorbed in the divine, which constitutes the tragic: or else the

[032-392] divine element may be entirely corrupted by the earthly, producing the

[032-393] comic.[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_51_546) These methods of Solger were followed by Weisse (1830), and

[032-394] by Ruge (1837); for the former, ugliness is "the immediate existence of

[032-395] beauty" which is overcome in the sublime and the comic; for the latter,

[032-396] the effort to achieve the Idea, or the Idea searching for itself,

[032-397] generates the sublime; when the Idea loses instead of discovering

[032-398] itself, ugliness is produced; when the Idea rediscovers itself and

[032-399] rises out of ugliness to new life, the comic.[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_52_547) A whole treatise

[032-400] entitled *The Æsthetic of the Ugly*[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_53_548) was published by Rosenkranz in

[032-401] 1853, presenting this concept as intermediate between the beautiful

[032-402] and the comic, and tracing it from its first origin to that "sort

[032-403] of perfection" it attains in the satanic. Passing from the common

[032-404] (*Gemeine)* which is the petty, the weak, the low, and the sub-species

[032-405] of the low, viz. the usual, the casual, the arbitrary and the crude,

[032-406] Rosenkranz goes on to describe the repugnant, trisected into the

[032-407] awkward, the dead and empty, and the horrible: thus he proceeds from

[032-408] tripartition to tripartition, dividing the horrible into the absurd,

[032-409] the nauseating and the wicked: the wicked into criminal, spectral and

[032-410] diabolical: the diabolical into demoniac, magical and satanic. He

[032-411] opposes the childish notion that ugliness acts as a foil to beauty

[032-412] in art, and justifies its introduction by the necessity for art to

[032-413] represent the entire appearance of the Idea; on the other hand he

[032-414] admits that the ugly is not on the same level as the beautiful, for,

[032-415] if the beautiful can stand by itself alone, the other cannot do so and

[032-416] must always be reflected by and in the beautiful.[[54]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_54_549)

[032-417] *Passage from abstract to concrete: Vischer.*

[032-418] The second form prevailed with Vischer. The following extract will

[032-419] serve as an illustration of his manner: "The Idea arouses itself from

[032-420] the tranquil unity in which it was fused with the appearance and

[032-421] pushes onward, affirming, in face of its own finitude, its infinity";

[032-422] this rebellion and transcendence is the sublime. "But Beauty demands

[032-423] full satisfaction for this disruption of its harmony: the violated

[032-424] right of the image must be reasserted: this can be accomplished only

[032-425] by means of a fresh contradiction, that is to say by the negative

[032-426] position now taken up by the image towards the Idea by rejecting all

[032-427] interpenetration with it and by affirming its own separate existence

[032-428] as the whole"; this second moment is the comic, negation of a

[032-429] negation.[[55]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_55_550) The same process is further enriched and complicated by

[032-430] Zeising, who compares the modifications of Beauty to the refraction of

[032-431] colours: the three primary modifications, the sublime, the attractive

[032-432] and the humorous, correspond with the primary colours violet, orange

[032-433] and green; the three secondary, pure beauty, comic and tragic, to

[032-434] the colours red, yellow and blue. Each of these six modifications

[032-435] (exactly like the degrees of the Ugly in Rosenkranz) branches out, like

[032-436] fireworks, into three rays: pure beauty into the decorous, noble and

[032-437] pleasing: the attractive into graceful, interesting and piquant: the

[032-438] comic into buffoonery, the diverting and burlesque: the humorous into

[032-439] the quaint, capricious and melancholy: the tragic into the moving,

[032-440] pathetic and demoniac: the sublime into the glorious, majestic and

[032-441] imposing.[[56]](280320132259829826_54618-h-20.htm.html#Footnote_56_551)

[032-442] *The Legend of Sir Purebeauty.*

[032-443] All the works of this period on Æsthetic are filled in this way

[032-444] with the *gest, chanson* or romaunt of the knight Sir Purebeauty

[032-445] (*Reinschon)* and his extraordinary adventures, recounted in two

[032-446] conflicting versions. According to one story, Sir Purebeauty is

[032-447] constrained to abandon his beloved leisure by the Mephistophelean

[032-448] devices of the temptress Ugliness, who leads him into countless

[032-449] dangers from which he invariably emerges victorious; his victories and

[032-450] successes (his Marengo, Austerlitz and Jena) are called the Sublime,

[032-451] the Comic, the Humorous and so forth. The other story tells how the

[032-452] knight, bored by his life of loneliness, sallies forth purposely to

[032-453] seek adversaries and occasions for fighting; he is always vanquished,

[032-454] but even in his overthrow *ferum victorem capit,* he transforms

[032-455] and irradiates the enemy. Beyond this artificial mythology, this

[032-456] legend composed without the least imagination or literary skill,

[032-457] this miserably dull tale, it is vain to look for anything whatever

[032-458] in the much elaborated theory of German æstheticians known as the

[032-459] Modifications of Beauty.
