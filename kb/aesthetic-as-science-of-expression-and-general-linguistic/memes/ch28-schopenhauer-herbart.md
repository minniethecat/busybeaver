[028-001] *The "Critique of Judgment" and [[idealism|metaphysical idealism]].*

[028-002] It is well known that [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]] held the *Critique of Judgment* to be

[028-003] the most important of the three Kantian *Critiques,* and that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]

[028-004] together with the great majority of the followers of metaphysical

[028-005] idealism had a special affection for the book. According to them the

[028-006] third *Critique* was the attempt to bridge the gulf, to resolve the

[028-007] antitheses between liberty and necessity, teleology and mechanism,

[028-008] spirit and nature: it was the correction [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] was preparing for

[028-009] himself, the concrete vision which dispelled the last traces of his

[028-010] abstract subjectivism.

[028-011] *[[friedrich-schiller|F. Schiller]].*

[028-012] The same admiration and an opinion even more favourable were extended

[028-013] by them to Friedrich Schiller, the first to elaborate that part

[028-014] of Kant's philosophy and to study the third sphere which united

[028-015] sensibility to reason. "It was the artistic sense dwelling in his

[028-016] also profoundly philosophical mind," says Hegel, "which, against the

[028-017] abstract infinity of Kant's thought, against his living for duty,

[028-018] against his conception of nature and reality, and of sense and feeling

[028-019] as utterly hostile to intellect, asserted the necessity and enunciated

[028-020] the principle of totality and reconciliation, even before it had been

[028-021] recognized by professed philosophers: to Schiller must be allowed the

[028-022] great merit of having been the first to oppose the subjectivity of

[028-023] Kant, and of having dared try to go beyond it."[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_1_382)

[028-024] *Relations between Schiller and Kant.*

[028-025] Discussion has raged around the true relation between Schiller and

[028-026] Kant, and it has lately been maintained that his Æsthetic was not, as

[028-027] would seem to be the case, derived from Kant, but from the pandynamism

[028-028] which, starting from Leibniz, had propagated itself in [[germany|Germany]] through

[028-029] Creuzens, Ploucket and Reimarus down to Herder, who had conceived

[028-030] a wholly animated nature.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_2_383) There can be no doubt that Schiller

[028-031] shared Herder's conception, as may be seen from the theosophical tone

[028-032] of the fragment of correspondence between Julius and Raphæl and in

[028-033] other writings. It cannot be denied, however, that whatever personal

[028-034] feelings Kant may have had towards Herder, or Herder towards his

[028-035] former teacher (against whose *Critique of Judgment* he published his

[028-036] *Kaligone,* as he had replied to the *Critique of Pure Reason* with his

[028-037] *Metacritica*), when Kant in a somewhat dubious manner made the first

[028-038] step towards a reconciliation, the breach was at all events partially

[028-039] healed. The dispute is therefore of small importance: we shall find it

[028-040] more useful to observe that Schiller introduced an important correction

[028-041] of Kant's views when he obliterated every trace of the double theory

[028-042] of art and [[beauty|the beautiful]], giving no weight to the distinction drawn

[028-043] between pure and adherent beauty, and finally abandoning the mechanical

[028-044] conception of art as consisting in beauty joined to the intellectual

[028-045] concept. It was certainly his own experience of active artistic work

[028-046] that led him to this simplification.

[028-047] *The æsthetic sphere as the sphere of Play.*

[028-048] Schiller defined the æsthetic sphere as the sphere of play (*Spiel*);

[028-049] the unfortunate term, suggested to him partly by some phrases of Kant,

[028-050] partly, perhaps, by an article on card-games by one Weisshuhn which he

[028-051] published in his review *The Hours* (*Die Horen*),[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_3_384) has given rise

[028-052] to the belief that he anticipated certain modern doctrines of artistic

[028-053] activity as the overflow of exuberant spirits, analogous with the play

[028-054] of children and animals. Schiller did not fail to warn his readers

[028-055] against such a mistaken interpretation (to which, however, he lent

[028-056] himself) when he begged them not to think of "games in real life,

[028-057] which are usually concerned with wholly material things," nor yet of

[028-058] the idle dreaming of the imagination left to itself.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_4_385) The activity

[028-059] of the play of which he treated held the mean between the material

[028-060] activity of the senses, of nature, of animal instinct or passion as

[028-061] it is called, and the formal activity of intellect and morality. The

[028-062] man who plays, *i.e.* contemplates nature æsthetically and produces

[028-063] art, sees all natural objects as animated; in such a phantasmagoria

[028-064] mere natural necessity gives place to the free determination of the

[028-065] faculties; spirit appears as spontaneously reconciled with nature,

[028-066] form with matter. Beauty is life, the living form (*lebende Gestalt)*;

[028-067] not life in the physiological sense, since beauty does not extend

[028-068] throughout all physiological life, nor is it restricted to that alone:

[028-069] marble when worked by an artist may have a living form; and a man,

[028-070] although possessed of life and form, need not be a living form.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_5_386)

[028-071] Wherefore art must conquer nature with form: "in an artistic work of

[028-072] true beauty the content ought to be nil, the form everything: by form

[028-073] man is influenced in his entirety; by content in his separate faculties

[028-074] only. The true secret of great artists is that they cancel matter

[028-075] through form (*den Stoff durch die [[form|Form]] vertilgt)*; the more imposing,

[028-076] overwhelming or seductive the matter is in itself, the greater its

[028-077] obstinacy in striving to emphasize its own particular effect, the more

[028-078] the spectator inclines to lose himself immediately in the matter, so

[028-079] much the more triumphant is the art which brings it into subjection

[028-080] and enforces its own sovereign power. The mind of hearer or spectator

[028-081] should remain perfectly free and calm; from the magic circle of art

[028-082] it should issue as pure and perfect as when it left the hands of the

[028-083] Creator. The most frivolous object should be treated in such a manner

[028-084] as to enable us to pass at once to the most serious matters; and the

[028-085] most serious in such a way that we may pass from them to the lightest

[028-086] game." There is a [[art|fine art]] of passion; a passionate fine art would be

[028-087] a contradiction in terms.[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_6_387) "So long as man in his early physical

[028-088] state passively absorbs the world of senses and simply feels it, he is

[028-089] one with it; and precisely because he merely is a world there is for

[028-090] him as yet no world at all. Only when in his æsthetic state he places

[028-091] the world outside himself and contemplates it, does he detach his

[028-092] personality from the rest; then a world appears to him, since he is no

[028-093] longer one with the world."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_7_388)

[028-094] *Æsthetic education.*

[028-095] Schiller ascribed high educational value to art thus conceived as at

[028-096] once sensible and rational, material and formal. Not that it teaches

[028-097] moral precepts or excites to good actions; if it acted thus, or when

[028-098] it acted thus, it would at once cease, as we have seen, to be art.

[028-099] Determination in whatsoever direction, to the good or the bad, to

[028-100] pleasure or to duty, destroys the character of the æsthetic sphere,

[028-101] which is rather indeterminism. By means of art man frees himself from

[028-102] the yoke of the senses; but before putting himself spontaneously under

[028-103] that of reason and duty, he takes as it were a little breathing-space

[028-104] by staying in a region of indifference and serene contemplation. "While

[028-105] having no claim to promote exclusively any special human faculty, the

[028-106] æsthetic condition is favourable to each and all without favouritism;

[028-107] and the reason why it favours none in particular is that it is the

[028-108] foundation of the possibility of all alike. Every other exercise gives

[028-109] some inclination to the soul, and therefore presupposes a special

[028-110] limit; æsthetic activity alone is unlimited." This indifference, which

[028-111] if not yet pure form is not pure matter, confers its educational value

[028-112] on art; it opens a way to morality, not by preaching and persuading,

[028-113] that is to say, determining, but by making determination possible.

[028-114] Such is the fundamental concept of his celebrated *Letters on the

[028-115] Æsthetic Education of Man* (1795), in which Schiller took his cue from

[028-116] the conditions of his times and from the necessity of finding a middle

[028-117] way between supine acquiescence in tyranny and savage rebellion as

[028-118] exemplified by the revolution then raging in [[france|France]].

[028-119] *Vagueness and lack of precision in Schiller's Æsthetic.*

[028-120] The defects of Schiller's æsthetic doctrine are its lack of precision

[028-121] and its generality. Who has given a better description of certain

[028-122] aspects of art, the catharsis produced by artistic activity, the

[028-123] serenity and calm resulting from the domination over natural

[028-124] impressions? Equally just is his remark that art, although wholly

[028-125] independent of morality, is in some way connected with it. But what

[028-126] precisely this connexion may be, or what the exact nature of æsthetic

[028-127] activity, Schiller does not succeed in explaining. Conceiving the

[028-128] moral and intellectual as the only formal activities (*Formtrieb)* and

[028-129] denying as a convinced anti-sensationalist in opposition to [[edmund-burke|Burke]] and

[028-130] philosophers of his type that art can belong to the passionate and

[028-131] sensuous nature (*Stofftrieb*), he cut himself off from the means of

[028-132] recognizing the general category to which artistic activity belongs.

[028-133] His own concept of the formal is too narrow: too narrow, also, his

[028-134] concept of the cognitive activity, in which he is able to see the

[028-135] logical or intellectual form, but not that of the imagination. What

[028-136] for him was this art he describes as an activity neither formal nor

[028-137] material, neither cognitive nor moral? Was it for him, as for Kant,

[028-138] an activity of feeling, a play of several faculties at once? It would

[028-139] seem so, since Schiller distinguishes four points of view or relations

[028-140] of man with things: the physical, in which these affect our senses:

[028-141] the logical, in which they excite knowledge: the moral, in which they

[028-142] appear to us as an object of rational volition: and the æsthetic

[028-143] "in which they refer to our powers in entirety without becoming the

[028-144] determinate object of any one faculty." For example, a man is pleased

[028-145] æsthetically when his feeling depends in no way on the pleasure of

[028-146] the senses and when he is not conscious of thinking about any law or

[028-147] end.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_8_389) We look in vain for any more conclusive reply.

[028-148] It must not be overlooked that Schiller delivered a course of lectures

[028-149] on Æsthetic in Jena University in 1792, and that his writings on the

[028-150] subject intended for reviews were couched in a popular style: no

[028-151] less popular, in his own opinion, was the style of the book quoted

[028-152] above, which grew out of a series of letters actually sent to his

[028-153] patron the Duke of Holstein-Augustenburg. But the great work to be

[028-154] entitled *Rallias,* which he intended writing upon Æsthetic, was never

[028-155] completed; the only fragments which have reached us are contained in

[028-156] the correspondence with Körner (1793-1794). From the discussions between

[028-157] the two friends we gather that Körner was not satisfied with Schiller's

[028-158] formula and desired something objective, something more precise, a

[028-159] positive characteristic of the beautiful: and one day Schiller told him

[028-160] that he had definitely discovered such a characteristic. But what it

[028-161] was that he had discovered we do not know; no mention of it occurs in

[028-162] any further document, and we are left in doubt as to whether we have

[028-163] lost an integral part of his thought or merely the momentary illusion

[028-164] of a discovery.

[028-165] *Schiller's caution and the rashness of the Romanticists.*

[028-166] The uncertainty and vagueness of Schiller's theory seem almost a merit

[028-167] in contrast with that which followed. He had constituted himself

[028-168] guardian of the teaching of Kant and refused to abandon the realm of

[028-169] criticism; faithful disciple of his master, he conceived the third

[028-170] sphere not as real but as an ideal, a concept not constitutive but

[028-171] regulative, an imperative. "From transcendental motives, reason here

[028-172] demands that communion be established between formal and material

[028-173] activity; that is to say, there must be an activity of play, since the

[028-174] concept of humanity can be complete only by the union of reality with

[028-175] form, the accidental with the necessary, passivity with liberty. This

[028-176] demand must be made because reason, in conformity with her essence,

[028-177] aims at perfection and at sweeping away all obstacles; and every

[028-178] exclusive operation of one or other activity leaves humanity incomplete

[028-179] and confined within limits."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_9_390) Schiller's thought, as it appears in

[028-180] his correspondence with Körner, has been well represented as follows:"

[028-181] The union of sensibility with liberty in the Beautiful, which does

[028-182] not actually take place but is supposed to do so, suggests to man

[028-183] an intuition of the union of these elements within himself: a union

[028-184] which does not take place actually but ought to do so."[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_10_391) The times

[028-185] which followed had no such nice scruples. Kant had given new vigour

[028-186] to the production of works on æsthetic, and, as in the days following

[028-187] [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]], every new year saw a number of new treatises. It was the

[028-188] fashion. "Nothing swarms like æstheticians" (wrote Jean Paul Richter

[028-189] in 1804 when preparing his own book on the subject for publication):

[028-190] "it is rare for a youth who has paid his fees for a course of lectures

[028-191] on Æsthetic not to produce a book on some point of the science in the

[028-192] hope that the public may refund him his expenses by buying his book:

[028-193] some there are indeed who pay their professor's fees out of their

[028-194] author's royalties."[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_11_392) It was hoped, not unreasonably, that the

[028-195] exploration of the obscure region of æsthetic might throw some light

[028-196] on metaphysics, and the procedure of artists seemed to offer a good

[028-197] example to philosophers seeking to create a world for themselves:

[028-198] so philosophy modelled itself upon art and, as though to render the

[028-199] transition easier, the concept of art was brought as close as possible

[028-200] to that of philosophy. [[romanticism|Romanticism]], gaining vogue daily, was a renewal

[028-201] or continuation of that "age of genius" in which the youth of Goethe

[028-202] and Schiller had been passed; and as the period of *Sturm und Drang*

[028-203] had zealously worshipped the genius who breaks all rules and oversteps

[028-204] all limitations, so did Romanticism hail the domination of a faculty

[028-205] called Fancy, or more frequently [[imagination|Imagination]], to which were attributed

[028-206] the most diverse characteristics and the most miraculous effects.

[028-207] *Ideas on Art: J. P. Richter.*

[028-208] The Romantic theorists, artists themselves for the most part, abounded

[028-209] in truthful and subtle observations concerning artistic procedure. Jean

[028-210] Paul Richter makes many excellent remarks about productive imagination,

[028-211] which he distinguishes clearly from the reproductive and asserts to be

[028-212] shared by all men as soon as they are able to say "This is beautiful";

[028-213] for "how could a genius be acclaimed or even tolerated for a single

[028-214] month, not to mention thousands of centuries, by the common herd, if

[028-215] he had not a strong connecting-link of relationship with the herd?" He

[028-216] also describes how imagination is variously divided among individuals:

[028-217] as simple talent, as passive or feminine genius, and in the highest

[028-218] degree as the active or masculine genius, formed by reflexion and

[028-219] instinct, in which "all faculties flourish simultaneously and fancy

[028-220] is no isolated flower, but the goddess Flora herself who, in order

[028-221] to produce new combinations, crosses with each other those blossoms

[028-222] whose conjunction is fertile, and is, so to speak, a faculty full of

[028-223] faculties."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_12_393) This latter sentence betrays a tendency on Richter's

[028-224] part to exaggerate the functions of imagination and to construct upon

[028-225] it a kind of mythology.

[028-226] *Romantic Æsthetic and idealistic Æsthetic.*

[028-227] Contemporary systems of philosophy are partly impregnated with, and

[028-228] partly the source of, such mythologies: the Romantic conception of

[028-229] art may be said to have found its most complete expression in German

[028-230] idealism, where this attained its most coherent and systematic form.

[028-231] *J. G. Fichte.*

[028-232] It did not attain this form with Fichte, the first great pupil of Kant;

[028-233] for though Fichte regarded imagination as the activity which creates

[028-234] the universe, effects the synthesis of the ego and the non-ego, posits

[028-235] the object and therefore precedes consciousness, he does not connect it

[028-236] with art.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_13_394) In his æsthetic notions Fichte is influenced by Schiller,

[028-237] with the addition of a moralism imposed upon him by the general

[028-238] character of his system; hence the ethical sphere, midway between the

[028-239] cognitive and the æsthetic, becomes from his point of view a mere

[028-240] appurtenance of morality, as being the representation of, and hence

[028-241] reverence for, the moral ideal.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_14_395) His subjective idealism eventually

[028-242] produced an æsthetic doctrine through the work of Friedrich Schlegel

[028-243] and Ludwig Tieck; the doctrine of Irony as the basis of art.

[028-244] *Irony: Schlegel, Tieck, Novalis.*

[028-245] The ego which created the universe can also destroy it; the universe is

[028-246] an empty appearance at which the only true reality, the ego, can smile,

[028-247] holding itself aloof, like an artist or a creative god, from creatures

[028-248] of its own which it does not take seriously.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_15_396) Friedrich Schlegel

[028-249] described art as a perpetual parody of itself and a "transcendental

[028-250] farce." Tieck defined irony as "a power which allows the poet to

[028-251] dominate the matter which he handles." Another Romantic Fichtian,

[028-252] Novalis, dreamed of a magical idealism, an art of creation by the

[028-253] instantaneous act of the ego and of realizing our dreams.

[028-254] *F. Schelling.*

[028-255] But it is only to the *System of Transcendental Idealism* (1800) of

[028-256] Schelling, to his *Bruno* (1802), to his celebrated course of lectures

[028-257] on the *Philosophy of Art* given at Jena in 1802-1803 (repeated at

[028-258] Würzburg, and distributed subsequently in manuscript notes all over

[028-259] Germany), to the no less celebrated lecture on the *Relation between

[028-260] the Figurative Arts and Nature* (1807), as well as to other works

[028-261] of this eloquent and enthusiastic philosopher that we owe the first

[028-262] great philosophical affirmation of Romanticism, and of a renewed and

[028-263] conscious neo-Platonism in Æsthetic.

[028-264] *Beauty and character.*

[028-265] Like all the other idealistic philosophers, Schelling held firmly to

[028-266] the fusion of the theories of art and the beautiful already effected

[028-267] by Schiller. From this point of view it is interesting to note his

[028-268] explanation of the condemnation of art by Plato: this condemnation,

[028-269] says Schelling, was directed against the art of his time, the natural

[028-270] and realistic art of antiquity in general, with its character of

[028-271] finitude: Plato could not have uttered such a condemnation (as we

[028-272] moderns are unable to utter it) if he had known Christian art, whose

[028-273] characteristic is infinity.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_16_397) The pure abstract beauty of [[j-j-winckelmann|Winckelmann]]

[028-274] is not enough; no less inadequate, false and negative is that concept

[028-275] of the characteristic which would try to make art something dead, hard

[028-276] and ugly by imposing upon it the limitations of the individual. Art is

[028-277] beauty and characteristic in one; characteristic beauty, character from

[028-278] which beauty is evolved, according to Goethe's saying; it is therefore

[028-279] not the individual but the living concept of the individual. When the

[028-280] artist's eye recognizes the creative idea of the individual and draws

[028-281] it forth, he transforms the individual into a world in itself, into a

[028-282] species (*Gattung*), an eternal idea (*Urbild*), and fears no more the

[028-283] limitation or hardness which is the condition of life: characteristic

[028-284] beauty is that plenitude of form which kills form; it does not inflame

[028-285] passion, it regulates it, like the banks of a river which are filled

[028-286] but not overflowed by the waters.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_17_398) In all of this we feel the

[028-287] influence of Schiller, with something added which Schiller could never

[028-288] have expressed.

[028-289] *Art and Philosophy.*

[028-290] Indeed, whilst gratefully acknowledging the excellent contributions to

[028-291] the theory of art made by the writers who succeeded Kant, Schelling

[028-292] laments that in none of them can he find exact scientific method

[028-293] (*Wissenschaftlichkeit*),[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_18_399) The true point of departure in his

[028-294] theory is in the philosophy of nature, *i.e.* in that criticism of

[028-295] the teleological judgement which Kant places directly after that of

[028-296] the æsthetic judgement in his third *Critique.* Teleology is the

[028-297] union of theoretical and practical philosophy; but the system would

[028-298] be incomplete but for the possibility of demonstrating in the subject

[028-299] itself, in the ego, the identity of the two worlds, theoretical and

[028-300] practical; an activity which has, and at the same time has not,

[028-301] consciousness; unconscious as nature, conscious as spirit. This

[028-302] activity is precisely the æsthetic activity: "the general organ of

[028-303] philosophy, keystone of the whole edifice."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_19_400) There are but two ways

[028-304] open to one who is desirous of escaping from common realities: poetry,

[028-305] which transports into the ideal world; and philosophy which annihilates

[028-306] the real world.[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_20_401) Strictly speaking, "there is but one sole absolute

[028-307] work of art; it may exist in various exemplars, but in itself it is

[028-308] one, although it may not yet possess existence in its original form."

[028-309] True art is not the impression of one moment, but the representation

[028-310] of infinite life;[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_21_402) it is transcendental intuition become objective,

[028-311] and is therefore not only the organ but the document of philosophy.

[028-312] A time will come when philosophy will return to poetry, from which

[028-313] she has detached herself; and from the new philosophy a new mythology

[028-314] will arise.[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_22_403) The Absolute is thus the object of art as well as of

[028-315] philosophy (as Schelling insists elsewhere in greater detail): the

[028-316] first represents it in idea (*Urbild*), the second in its reflexion

[028-317] (*Gegenbild)*: "philosophy portrays ideas, not realities: so is it with

[028-318] art: those same ideas of which real things, as philosophy demonstrates,

[028-319] are imperfect copies, themselves appear in the objective arts as

[028-320] ideas, *i.e.* in all their perfection, and represent the intellectual

[028-321] world in the world of reflexion."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_23_404) Music is the "very ideal rhythm

[028-322] of Nature and the Universe, which by means of this art makes itself

[028-323] felt in the derivative world"; perfect creations of statuary are "the

[028-324] very ideas of organic nature represented objectively"; the Homeric

[028-325] epic, "the very identity constituting the foundation of history in the

[028-326] Absolute."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_24_405) But while philosophy gives an immediate representation

[028-327] of the Divine, of absolute Identity, art can but give the immediate

[028-328] representation of Indifference; and "since the degree of perfection

[028-329] or reality in a thing becomes higher in proportion as it approaches

[028-330] nearer to the absolute Idea and the fulness of infinite affirmation

[028-331] and in proportion as it comprehends within itself other powers, it is

[028-332] clear that art, above everything else, is in closest relation with

[028-333] philosophy, from which it is distinguished merely by the character

[028-334] of its specification: in everything else it may be considered as the

[028-335] highest power in the ideal world."[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_25_406) To the three powers of the real

[028-336] and ideal world correspond in a rising scale the three ideas of Truth,

[028-337] Goodness and Beauty. Beauty is neither the mere universal (truth),

[028-338] nor mere reality (action), but the perfect interpenetration of both:

[028-339] "beauty exists when the particular (the real) is so adequate to its

[028-340] concept that the latter, as infinite, enters the finite and presents

[028-341] itself to our contemplation in concrete form. With the appearance of

[028-342] the concept, the real becomes truly similar and equal to the idea,

[028-343] wherein the universal and the particular find their absolute identity.

[028-344] Without ceasing to be rational, the rational becomes at the same time

[028-345] apparent and sensible."[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_26_407) But as above the three powers is poised

[028-346] God, their point of union, so Philosophy stands supreme over the three

[028-347] ideas; concerning itself not with truth or morality or even beauty

[028-348] alone, but with that which belongs to all the three in common, deduced

[028-349] from one common source. If philosophy assumes the character of science

[028-350] and truth, while yet remaining superior to truth, this is made possible

[028-351] by the fact that science and truth are its formal determination;

[028-352] "philosophy is science in the sense that truth, goodness and beauty,

[028-353] *i.e.* science, virtue and art, interpenetrate each other; therefore

[028-354] it is also not science but is that which is common to science, virtue

[028-355] and art." This interpenetration distinguishes philosophy from all other

[028-356] sciences; for instance, if mathematics can dispense with morality and

[028-357] beauty, philosophy cannot do so.[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_27_408)

[028-358] *Ideas and the gods. Art and mythology.*

[028-359] In Beauty are contained truth and goodness, necessity and liberty. When

[028-360] beauty appears to be in conflict with truth, the truth in question

[028-361] is a finite truth with which beauty ought not to agree, because, as

[028-362] we have seen, the art of naturalism and of the merely characteristic

[028-363] is a false art.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_28_409) The individual forms of art, being in themselves

[028-364] representatives of the infinite and the universe, are called Ideas.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_29_410)

[028-365] Considered from the point of view of reality, Ideas are gods; their

[028-366] essence, their "in-itself," is in fact equivalent to God; every idea

[028-367] is an idea so far as it is God in a particular form; every idea,

[028-368] therefore, is equal to God, but to a particular god. Characteristic of

[028-369] all the gods is pure limitation and indivisible absoluteness: Minerva

[028-370] is the idea of wisdom united with strength, but she is lacking in

[028-371] womanly tenderness; Juno is power without wisdom and without the sweet

[028-372] attraction of love, for which she is forced to borrow the cestus of

[028-373] Venus; Venus again has not the weighty wisdom of Minerva. What would

[028-374] become of these ideas if deprived of their limitations? They would

[028-375] cease to be objects of Imagination.[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_30_411) Imagination is a faculty which

[028-376] has no connexion with pure intellect or with reason (*Vernunft*) and is

[028-377] distinct from fancy (*Einbildungskraft*) which collects and arranges

[028-378] the products of art, whereas imagination intuits them, forms them out

[028-379] of itself, represents them. Imagination is to fancy as intellectual

[028-380] intuition is to reason: it is therefore the intellectual intuition of

[028-381] art.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_31_412) "Reason" no longer suffices in a philosophy such as this:

[028-382] intellectual intuition, which for Kant was a limiting concept, is now

[028-383] asserted as really existing: intellect sinks to a subordinate place:

[028-384] even the genuine imagination which operates in art is overshadowed

[028-385] by this new-fangled Imagination, twin with intellectual [[intuition|Intuition]],

[028-386] who sometimes changes places with this sister of hers. Mythology

[028-387] is proclaimed a necessary condition of all art: mythology which is

[028-388] not allegory, for in the latter the particular signifies only the

[028-389] universal, while the former is already itself the universal; which

[028-390] explains how easy it is to allegorize, and how fascinating are such

[028-391] poems as those of Homer which lend themselves to such interpretations.

[028-392] Christian, as well as [[greece|Hellenic]], art has its mythology: Christ; the

[028-393] persons of the Trinity; the Virgin mother of God.[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_32_413) The fine between

[028-394] mythology and art is as shadowy as that between art and philosophy.

[028-395] *K. W. [[karl-solger|Solger]].*

[028-396] The year 1815 saw the publication of Solger's principal work, *Erwin,*

[028-397] a long philosophical dialogue on the beautiful; subsequently in

[028-398] 1819 he gave a course of lectures on Æsthetic which were published

[028-399] posthumously. He was one of those who found but a glimpse of truth

[028-400] in Kant and held the post-Kantians in very slight estimation,

[028-401] particularly Fichte; in Schelling, who begins from the original unity

[028-402] of the subjective and the objective, he detects for the first time a

[028-403] speculative principle not adequately developed, since Schelling had

[028-404] never triumphed dialectically over the difficulties of intellectual

[028-405] intuition.[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_33_414)

[028-406] *Fancy and Imagination.*

[028-407] Solger was one of those who conceived of Imagination as totally

[028-408] distinct from Fancy: fancy (says he) belongs to common cognition

[028-409] and is none other than "the human consciousness, in so far as it

[028-410] continues, in temporal succession, infinitely reasserting an original

[028-411] intuition"; it presupposes the distinctions between common cognition,

[028-412] abstraction and judgement, concept and representation, amongst which

[028-413] "it acts as mediator by giving to the general concept the form of

[028-414] individual representation; and to the latter the form of a general

[028-415] concept; in this manner it has its being among the antitheses of the

[028-416] ordinary understanding." Imagination is totally different; proceeding

[028-417] "from the original unity of the antitheses in the Idea, it acts so

[028-418] that the elements in opposition, separated as they are from the idea,

[028-419] find themselves united in the reality; by its means we are capable

[028-420] of apprehending objects higher than those of common cognition and of

[028-421] recognizing in them the idea itself as real: also, in art, it is the

[028-422] faculty of transforming the idea into reality." It presents itself

[028-423] in three modes or degrees: as Imagination of the Imagination, which

[028-424] conceives the whole as idea, and activity as nothing more than the

[028-425] development of the idea in reality; as Sensibility of the Imagination,

[028-426] in so far as it expresses the life of the idea in the real and reduces

[028-427] the one to the other; lastly (and here we have the highest grade of

[028-428] artistic activity, corresponding with Dialectic in philosophy) as

[028-429] Intellect of the Imagination or artistic Dialectic, conceiving idea and

[028-430] reality in such a way that one passes over into the other, that is to

[028-431] say, into reality. Other divisions and subdivisions are made on which

[028-432] it is not necessary to dwell. Imagination is said to produce the Irony

[028-433] essential to true art: this is the Irony of Tieck and Novalis, of whom

[028-434] Solger is in a sense a follower.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_34_415)

[028-435] *Art, practice and religion.*

[028-436] Solger joins Schelling in placing beauty in the region of the Idea,

[028-437] inaccessible to common consciousness. It is distinct from the idea

[028-438] of Truth, because instead of dissolving the appearances of common

[028-439] consciousness after the manner of truth, art accomplishes the

[028-440] miracle of making appearance dissolve itself while still remaining

[028-441] appearance; artistic thought, therefore, is practical, not theoretical.

[028-442] Furthermore, it is distinct from the idea of Goodness, with which

[028-443] at first sight it would seem to be closely related, because in the

[028-444] case of Goodness the union of ideal with real, of the simple with the

[028-445] multiple, of the infinite with the finite, is not real and complete,

[028-446] but remains ideal, a mere ought-to-be. It is related more closely

[028-447] to Religion, which thinks the Idea as the abyss of life where our

[028-448] individual conscience must lose itself in order to become "essential"

[028-449] (*wesentlich*), while in beauty and art the Idea manifests itself by

[028-450] gathering into itself the world of distinctions between universal

[028-451] and particular and placing itself in their place. Artistic activity

[028-452] is more than theoretical, it is of a practical nature, but realized

[028-453] and perfected; art, therefore, belongs not to theoretical philosophy

[028-454] (as Kant thought, according to Solger), but to practical. Necessarily

[028-455] attached on one side to infinity, it cannot have common nature as its

[028-456] object; for example, art is absent from a portrait, and the ancients

[028-457] showed their discrimination in selecting gods and heroes for objects

[028-458] in sculpture since every deity—even in limited and particular

[028-459] form—always signifies a determinate modification of the Idea.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_35_416)

[028-460] * G. W. F. Hegel*.

[028-461] The same concept of art appears in the philosophy of Hegel, whatever

[028-462] may be the minor differences which he felt to separate himself from his

[028-463] predecessors. Little concerned as we are with the shades and varieties

[028-464] of mystical Æsthetic exhibited by each of these thinkers, we are

[028-465] chiefly concerned to lay bare the substantial underlying identity,

[028-466] the mysticism of arbitrarism which gives them their historic place in

[028-467] Æsthetic.

[028-468] *Art in the sphere of absolute spirit.*

[028-469] Opening the *Phenomenology* and the *Philosophy of Spirit,* one need

[028-470] not expect to find any discussion of art in the analysis of the forms

[028-471] of the theoretical Spirit, among definitions of sensibility and

[028-472] intuition, language and symbolism, and various grades of imagination

[028-473] and thought. Hegel places Art in the sphere of absolute Spirit,

[028-474] together with Religion and Philosophy,[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_36_417) and in this he regards

[028-475] Kant, Schiller, Schelling and Solger as his precursors, for like them

[028-476] he strongly denies that art has the function of representing the

[028-477] abstract concept, but not that it represents the concrete concept

[028-478] or Idea. Hegel's whole philosophy consists in the affirmation of a

[028-479] concrete concept, unknown to ordinary or scientific thought. "Indeed,"

[028-480] says he, "no concept has in our day been more mishandled than the

[028-481] concept in itself and for itself; for by concept is generally meant

[028-482] the abstract determinateness or one-sidedness of representation and

[028-483] intellectualistic thought, with which it is naturally impossible to

[028-484] think either the entirety of truth or concrete beauty."[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_37_418) To the

[028-485] realm of the concrete concept belongs art, as one of the three forms

[028-486] wherein the freedom of the spirit is achieved; it is the first form,

[028-487] namely that of immediate, sensible, objective knowledge (the second is

[028-488] religion, a representative consciousness *plus* worship, an element

[028-489] extraneous to mere art: the third is philosophy, free thought of the

[028-490] absolute spirit).[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_38_419)

[028-491] *Beauty as sensible appearance of the Idea.*

[028-492] Beauty and truth are at the same time one yet distinct. "Truth is

[028-493] Idea as Idea, according to its being-in-itself and its universal

[028-494] principle, and so far as it is thought as such. There is no sensible

[028-495] or material existence in Truth; thought contemplates therein nothing

[028-496] but universal idea. But the Idea must also realize itself externally

[028-497] and attain an actual and determinate existence. Truth also as such

[028-498] has existence; but when in its determinate external existence it is

[028-499] immediately for consciousness, and the concept remains immediately one

[028-500] with the external appearance, the Idea is not only true but beautiful.

[028-501] In this way Beauty may be defined as the sensible appearance of the

[028-502] Idea."[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_39_420) The Idea is the content of art: its sensible and imaginative

[028-503] configuration; its form: two elements which must interpenetrate and

[028-504] form a whole, hence the necessity that a content destined to become

[028-505] a work of art should show itself capable of such transformation;

[028-506] otherwise we have but an imperfect union of poetic form with prosaic

[028-507] and incongruous content.[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_40_421) An ideal content must gleam through the

[028-508] sensible form; the form is spiritualized by this ideal light;[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_41_422)

[028-509] artistic imagination does not work in the same way as the passive

[028-510] or receptive fancy, it does not stop at the appearances of sensible

[028-511] reality but searches for the internal truth and rationality of the

[028-512] real. "The rationality of the object selected by him should not be

[028-513] alone in awakening the consciousness of the artist: he should have

[028-514] well meditated upon the essential and the true in all their extension

[028-515] and profundity, for without reflexion a man cannot become conscious

[028-516] of that which is within himself, and all great works of art show

[028-517] that their material has been thought again and again from every

[028-518] side. No successful work of art can issue from light and careless

[028-519] imagination."[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_42_423) It is a delusion to fancy that poet and painter need

[028-520] nothing beyond intuitions: "a true poet must reflect and meditate

[028-521] before and during the execution of his poem."[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_43_424) But it is always

[028-522] understood that the thought of the poet does not take the form of

[028-523] abstraction.

[028-524] *Æsthetic in metaphysical idealism and Baumgartenism.*

[028-525] Some critics[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_44_425) affirm that the æsthetic movement from Schelling to

[028-526] Hegel is a revived Baumgartenism on the ground that this movement

[028-527] regarded art as a mediator of philosophical concepts; they mention

[028-528] the fact that a follower of Schelling, one Ast, was moved by the trend

[028-529] of his system to substitute didactic poetry for drama as the highest

[028-530] form of art.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_45_426) Putting aside some isolated and accidental deviations,

[028-531] there is no truth in this affirmation: these philosophers are hostile

[028-532] to intellectualistic and moralistic views, frequently entering upon

[028-533] definite and explicit polemic against them. Schelling wrote: "Æsthetic

[028-534] production is in its origin an absolutely free production.... This

[028-535] independence on any extraneous purpose constitutes the sanctity and

[028-536] purity of art, enabling it to repel all connexion with mere pleasure, a

[028-537] connexion which is a mark of barbarism, or with utility, which cannot

[028-538] be demanded of art save at times when the loftiest form of the human

[028-539] spirit is found in utilitarian discoveries. The same reasons forbid an

[028-540] alliance with morality and hold even science at arm's length, although

[028-541] nearest by reason of her disinterestedness; having her aim, however,

[028-542] outside herself, she must restrict herself definitely to serve as means

[028-543] to something higher than herself: the arts."[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_46_427) Hegel says, "Art

[028-544] contains no universal as such." "If the aim of instruction is treated

[028-545] as an aim, so that the nature of the content represented appears for

[028-546] itself directly, as an abstract proposition, prosaic reflexion, or

[028-547] general theory, and is not merely contained indirectly and implicitly

[028-548] in the concrete artistic form, the result of such a separation is to

[028-549] reduce the sensible and imaginative form, the true constituent of a

[028-550] work of art, to an idle ornament, a covering (*Hülle)* presented simply

[028-551] as a covering, an appearance maintained as mere appearance. The very

[028-552] nature of the work of art is thus completely altered, for a work of art

[028-553] must not present to intuition a content in its universality, but this

[028-554] universal individualized and converted into a sensible individual."[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_47_428)

[028-555] It is a bad sign, he adds, when an artist sets himself about his work

[028-556] from a motive of abstract ideas instead of that of the fulness of

[028-557] life (*Überfülle des Lebens*).[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_48_429) The aim of art lies in itself, in

[028-558] presentation of truth in a sensible form; any other aim is altogether

[028-559] extraneous.[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_49_430) It would not be hard to prove, certainly, that by

[028-560] separating art from pure representation and imagination and making it

[028-561] in some sense the vehicle of the concept, the universal, the infinite,

[028-562] these philosophers were facing in the direction of the road opened by

[028-563] Baumgarten. But to prove this would mean accepting as a presupposition

[028-564] the dilemma that if art be not pure imagination, it must be sensuous

[028-565] and subordinate to reason; and it is just this presupposition and

[028-566] dilemma that the metaphysical idealists denied. The road they tried to

[028-567] follow was to conceive a faculty which should be neither imagination

[028-568] nor intellect but should partake of both; an intellectual intuition or

[028-569] intuitive intellect, a mental imagination after the fashion of Plotinus.

[028-570] *Mortality and decay of art in Hegel's system.*

[028-571] In a greater degree than any of his predecessors Hegel emphasized the

[028-572] cognitive character of art. But this very merit brought him into a

[028-573] difficulty more easily avoided by the rest. Art being placed in the

[028-574] sphere of absolute Spirit, in company with Religion and Philosophy,

[028-575] how will she be able to hold her own in such powerful and aggressive

[028-576] company, especially in that of Philosophy, which in the Hegelian

[028-577] system stands at the summit of all spiritual evolution? If Art and

[028-578] Religion fulfilled functions other than the knowledge of the Absolute,

[028-579] they would be inferior levels of the Spirit, but yet necessary and

[028-580] indispensable. But if they have in view the same end as Philosophy

[028-581] and are allowed to compete with it, what value can they retain? None

[028-582] whatever; or, at the very most, they may have that sort of value which

[028-583] attaches to transitory historical phases in the life of humanity. The

[028-584] principles of Hegel's system are at bottom rationalistic and hostile to

[028-585] religion, and hostile no less to art. A strange and painful consequence

[028-586] for a man like Hegel, endowed with a warmly æsthetic spirit and a

[028-587] fervid lover of the arts; almost a repetition of the hard fate endured

[028-588] by Plato. But as the Greek philosopher, in obedience to the presumed

[028-589] command of religion, did not hesitate to condemn the mimetic art and

[028-590] the Homeric poetry he loved, so the German refused to evade the logical

[028-591] exigencies of his system and proclaimed the mortality, nay, the very

[028-592] death, of art. "We have assigned," he says, "a very high place to

[028-593] art: but it must be recollected that neither in content nor in form

[028-594] can art be considered the most perfect means of bringing before the

[028-595] consciousness of the mind its true interests. Precisely by reason of

[028-596] its form, art is limited to a particular content. Only a definite

[028-597] circle or grade of truth can be made visible in a work of art; that

[028-598] is to say, such truth as may be transfused into the sensible and

[028-599] adequately presented in that form, as were the Greek gods. But there

[028-600] is a deeper conception of truth, by which it is not so intimately

[028-601] allied to the sensible as to permit of its being received or expressed

[028-602] suitably in material fashion. To this class belongs the Christian

[028-603] conception of truth; and, furthermore, the spirit of our modern world,

[028-604] more especially that of our religion and our mental evolution, seems to

[028-605] have passed the point at which art is the best road to the apprehension

[028-606] of the Absolute. The peculiar character of artistic production no

[028-607] longer satisfies our highest aspirations.... Thought and reflexion

[028-608] have superseded fine art." Many reasons have been adduced in order to

[028-609] account for the moribund condition of modern art; in especial, the

[028-610] prevalence of material and political interests; the true reason, says

[028-611] Hegel, consists of the inferiority in grade of art in comparison with

[028-612] pure thought. "Art in its highest form is and for us must remain a

[028-613] thing of the past"; and just because the thing has vanished, one can

[028-614] reason about it philosophically.[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-17.htm.html#Footnote_50_431) The Æsthetic of Hegel is thus a

[028-615] funeral oration: he passes in review the successive forms of art, shows

[028-616] the progressive steps of internal consumption and lays the whole in

[028-617] its grave, leaving Philosophy to write its epitaph.

[028-618] Romanticism and metaphysical idealism had elevated art to such a

[028-619] fantastic height among the clouds that at last they were obliged to

[028-620] admit that it was so far away as to be absolutely useless.
