# Enthusiasm (Poetic)

In the aesthetic psychology of the eighteenth century, enthusiasm names the peculiar state of heightened emotion and imaginative transport from which poetry was thought to arise. It is a concept with ancient roots — Plato's *furor poeticus*, the divine madness that seizes the poet — but in the hands of eighteenth-century Italian theorists it became the object of empirical description rather than metaphysical speculation. Croce treats these enquiries with a characteristic mixture of historical appreciation and philosophical reserve: the psychology of enthusiasm captures something real about the experience of creation, but it leaves the aesthetic problem — what is poetry? — untouched.

## Definition

Saverio Bettinelli, the most prominent theorist of enthusiasm, offered the most detailed empirical account in his book *Del Entusiasmo* (1769). Paradisi, writing to Bettinelli as the work was being prepared, expressed the hope that it would prove "a metaphysical history of enthusiasm which shall outweigh all those Poetics which are only fit to be burned" (025-191). What Bettinelli actually produced was less revolutionary: Croce reports that his book "contains little beyond vivacious and eloquent empirical observations concerning the psychology of poets" (025-196).

Bettinelli's signal contribution was a taxonomy of poetic enthusiasm in six degrees: "elevation, vision, rapidity, novelty and surprise, passion and transfusion" (025-196). Each degree marks an intensification of the poet's emotional and imaginative engagement — from the initial lifting of the mind above mundane concerns (elevation) to the complete absorption and communication of the poetic state to the reader (transfusion). The scheme is descriptive, not explanatory; it catalogues the phenomenology of poetic creation without offering a philosophical account of what makes a poem a poem.

## In Croce's System

### Enthusiasm as Empirical Psychology

Croce's treatment of enthusiasm is consistent with his broader distinction between empirical psychology and aesthetic philosophy. The psychology of the poet — what Bettinelli calls enthusiasm — is a legitimate object of empirical study: one can observe and classify the states of mind that accompany artistic creation. But these states do not constitute the aesthetic fact. The aesthetic fact is the successful expression — the achieved intuition — and this is neither caused by enthusiasm nor measurable by its intensity.

Croce makes this point sharply in his discussion of a later variant of the enthusiasm theory. He notes "a mysticism without conviction or enthusiasm, almost in spite of itself" (027-312), suggesting that enthusiasm, far from being the cause of artistic achievement, may be entirely absent from works of genuine spiritual depth. The quiet lucidity of a Mozart or a Jane Austen is no less artistic than the volcanic transport of a Byron — which is to say that enthusiasm is an aesthetic variable, not an aesthetic criterion.

### Key Distinctions

The principal distinction is between **enthusiasm and expression**. Enthusiasm is an affective state of the artist; expression is the cognitive act of forming an intuition. An artist may be filled with enthusiasm and produce nothing of value; another may work in a state of calm and produce a masterpiece. The aesthetic value of a work is a property of the expression, not of the psychological conditions under which it was produced.

A second distinction opposes **the eighteenth-century enthusiasm theorists to Croce's anti-psychological aesthetics**. Bettinelli, Pagano, and their contemporaries treated the psychology of the artist as the key to understanding art. Croce reverses this priority: art is understood through its products — the intuitions that are expressed — not through the mental states of its producers. The "faculty of enthusiasm" to which some eighteenth-century theorists appealed is, for Croce, a pseudo-faculty: a name for something we do not understand, not an explanation of anything.

## Historical Context

The concept of poetic enthusiasm descends from Plato's *Ion*, where Socrates describes the poet as possessed by divine inspiration — a passive vehicle for the god's message, knowing not what he says. This Platonic model was revived in the Renaissance by Marsilio Ficino and others, who gave it a Neoplatonic inflection: the *furor poeticus* becomes the soul's ascent toward divine Beauty.

The eighteenth-century theorists whom Croce surveys — Bettinelli, Mario Pagano, the circle around Paradisi — secularize this tradition. Enthusiasm is no longer divine possession but a natural, analysable psychological state. Pagano, for instance, "grotesquely combines some ideas from Vico with the current sensationalism" (025-196), treating enthusiasm as a phase in the historical development of art from primitive expression to refined beauty. This naturalization of enthusiasm prepared the ground for Romantic theories of genius and imagination, which Croce regards as equally empirical and equally inadequate as philosophical accounts of art.
