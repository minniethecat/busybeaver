# Classicism

In Croce's aesthetic system, classicism is not a permanent philosophical category but a historical and empirical classification — one pole of the "classic versus romantic" dichotomy that has structured European art criticism since the Renaissance. Croce treats this distinction as useful for historical description but philosophically empty: both classic and romantic are valid only as they succeed in the universal act of intuition-expression.

## Definition

The term "classic" has carried shifting meanings across centuries. At times it signified formal perfection and equilibrium; at others, "cold and artificial" (010-129). Croce observes that "the great disputes about classicism and romanticism were frequently imperfect" (010-126), each side claiming exclusive validity for what was, in fact, a contingent historical preference. The classical ideal — equilibrium of form, restraint, fidelity to established models — represents one mode of expression among many, not a normative standard.

## In Croce's System

### Critique of Normative Classicism

Croce denies that classicism has any philosophical claim to superiority. The distinction between classic and romantic belongs to empirical art criticism, not to aesthetic science. "Classical represents an equilibrium" while "romantic represents an excess of content over form" (018-318–018-323) — but both can produce successful expressions. The error of classicism as a doctrine is to mistake one historical style for the eternal law of art.

### The Classic-Romantic Polarity

The polarity arose historically but hardened into a pseudo-philosophical opposition. Artworks "divided into classic and romantic, or into oriental, classic, and romantic" (016-137–016-138) reflect the classifying instinct of critics, not essential differences in artistic activity. Croce's philosophy dissolves the distinction: what matters is whether the work achieves adequate expression, not which historical label it wears.

### Neo-Classicism as Regression

The neo-classical movement of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries represents, for Croce, the degeneration of a living classical tradition into academic rule-making. Boileau, "the true founder of French pseudo-classicism" (038-656), or "neo-classicism" as it was later called (038-657), codified the practice of the ancients into inflexible precepts. The result was a poetry of "neat verses" (038-666) without living intuition — form without formed content.

## Historical Context

The classical ideal originates in **Aristotle**'s *Poetics*, which Renaissance theorists elevated from descriptive analysis to prescriptive rulebook. French classicism of the seventeenth century (Corneille, Racine) achieved powerful art within these constraints, but the eighteenth-century neo-classicism of **Boileau** and his followers reduced classicism to a mechanical doctrine. The **Romantic** reaction of the nineteenth century rejected these rules in favor of expressive freedom — a battle that, Croce argues, was fought on the wrong terrain, since both sides confused historical taste with philosophical truth.

## Criticism and Debate

> The Crocean dissolution of the classic-romantic distinction has been criticized as too sweeping. If all adequate expression is equally art, does the distinction between Sophocles and Shelley lose all philosophical significance? Croce's reply is that historical taxonomies remain useful for the empirical study of art history; they simply cannot ground normative prescriptions.
