# Convention

The distinction between the natural and the conventional runs through the history of aesthetics and the philosophy of language. Croce engages with this distinction critically, arguing that it rests on a misunderstanding of the nature of expression. What is called "convention" is not a separate species of sign but a disguised form of the same expressive activity.

## Definition

Convention, in the context of Croce's philosophy, denotes the idea that certain signs, words, or artistic forms are established by arbitrary human agreement rather than by nature. Croce traces the origin of this distinction to the ancient debate over whether language was "by nature (φύσει) or by convention (νόμῳ)" (020-545). By nature the Greeks understood "natural and rational necessity," by convention "caprice, arbitrariness, or utility" (020-546).

Croce's definitive judgment is that from the point of view of expression, "all are equally conventional, or, to speak with greater precision, nothing is conventional" in the sense the term is usually understood (017-240).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

The concept of convention appears in Croce's system primarily as a negative foil — a doctrine to be refuted. When philosophers claim that certain signs or forms are "conventions," they implicitly invoke a standard of naturalness against which conventions are measured. Croce's critique is that this standard is illusory: "But if there are to be conventions, something must exist which is no convention, but is itself the author of conventions" (004-301). This "something" — the expressive activity itself — is what Croce identifies as the true foundation.

Croce places the convention-nature opposition in the context of his four-fold spiritual activity. The error of conventionalism is to treat theoretic activity (intuition-expression) as if it were practical activity (arbitrary choice). Expression is not a tool selected by the will; it is the necessary form of theoretic knowledge.

### Key Distinctions

Croce makes a critical distinction between convention as a practical expedient and convention as a theory of expression. Natural scientists and mathematicians may treat their symbols as "expedients, or conventions" (004-295), and this is pragmatically defensible. But when philosophers of language extend this doctrine to claim that "truth and morality are conventions" and that convention itself is "the creator of truth" (004-298, 004-299), they commit a fundamental error: "But if there are to be conventions, something must exist which is no convention, but is itself the author of conventions" (004-301).

The distinction between "natural" and "conventional" signs in art — for instance, the claim that the signs used in painting are natural while those used in poetry are conventional (017-221, 017-224) — is, for Croce, a false dichotomy. Every sign, insofar as it is an expression, is equally natural and equally non-conventional.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The natural-versus-conventional distinction has ancient roots. The Greek Sophists debated whether language was φύσει (by nature) or νόμῳ (by convention), with Plato's *Cratylus* providing the classic treatment. This ancient opposition was inherited by modern philosophy and became entangled with Enlightenment debates about the origin of language.

In Croce's time, "the theory of association or convention" had made a significant appearance in aesthetic theory (019-126). According to this view, artistic forms and meanings are conventions established by historical association. Croce considers this doctrine "altogether erroneous" (019-139) because it confuses the genetic question of how a form came to be accepted with the philosophical question of what expression is.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to dissolve the opposition between natural and conventional by relocating both within the theory of expression. What appears to be "convention" is simply expression whose practical origin has been forgotten. The true philosophical question is not whether a sign is natural or conventional, but whether it succeeds or fails as expression.

This move parallels Croce's treatment of the distinction between "natural" and "conventional" in language: "The surmounting of variety" that linguistic convention achieves is itself an act of expression, not an arbitrary imposition (P02-138).

## Criticism and Debate

> Those who make conventions of truth and morality, and a convention even of the Supreme Being, know the fate of convention. It is that of being abandoned by him who made it (004-300).

Croce's dissolution of the convention-nature distinction has been both praised and criticized. Supporters see it as a powerful unification that eliminates a persistent false opposition in aesthetic theory. Critics argue that it does not adequately account for the social dimension of conventions — the fact that conventions are shared, learned, and historically transmitted in ways that purely expressive acts are not. If all signs are equally expressive, what explains the specific role that shared conventions play in enabling communication and the transmission of artistic traditions?
