[015-001] We must mention a series of fallacious scientific doctrines which have

[015-002] arisen from the failure to understand the purely external relation

[015-003] between the æsthetic fact or artistic vision and the physical fact

[015-004] or instrument which aids in its reproduction, together with brief

[015-005] criticisms of them deduced from what has already been said.

[015-006] *Criticism of æsthetic associationism.*

[015-007] That form of associationism which identifies the æsthetic fact with the

[015-008] *association* of two images finds support in such lack of apprehension.

[015-009] By what path has it been possible to arrive at such an error, so

[015-010] repugnant to our æsthetic consciousness, which is a consciousness of

[015-011] perfect unity, never of duality? Precisely because the physical and

[015-012] æsthetic facts have been considered separately, as two distinct images,

[015-013] which enter the spirit, the one drawn in by the other, first one and

[015-014] then the other. A picture has been divided into the [[representation|image]] of the

[015-015] *picture* and the image of the *meaning* of the picture; a poem, into

[015-016] the image of the *words* and the image of the *meaning* of the words.

[015-017] But this dualism of images is non-existent: the physical fact does not

[015-018] enter the spirit as an image, but causes the reproduction of the image

[015-019] (the only image, which is the æsthetic fact), in so far as it blindly

[015-020] stimulates the psychic organism and produces the impression which

[015-021] answers to the æsthetic expression already produced.

[015-022] The efforts of the associationists (the usurpers of to-day in the field

[015-023] of Æsthetic) to emerge from the difficulty, and to reaffirm in some way

[015-024] the unity which has been destroyed by their principle of association,

[015-025] are highly instructive. Some maintain that the image recalled is

[015-026] unconscious; others, leaving unconsciousness alone, hold that, on the

[015-027] contrary, it is vague, vaporous, confused, thus reducing the *force* of

[015-028] the æsthetic fact to the *weakness* of bad memory. But the dilemma is

[015-029] inexorable: either keep association and give up unity, or keep unity

[015-030] and give up association. No third way out of the difficulty exists.

[015-031] *Criticism of æsthetic physics.*

[015-032] From the failure to analyse so-called natural beauty thoroughly and to

[015-033] recognize that it is simply an incident of æsthetic reproduction, and

[015-034] from having looked upon it, on the contrary, as given in nature, is

[015-035] derived all that portion of treatises upon Æsthetic entitled *[[beauty|Beauty]]

[015-036] of Nature* or *Æsthetic Physics*; sometimes even subdivided, save

[015-037] the mark, into æsthetic Mineralogy, Botany and Zoology. We do not

[015-038] wish to deny that such treatises contain many just observations, and

[015-039] are sometimes themselves works of art, in so far as they represent

[015-040] beautifully the imaginings and fancies or impressions of their authors.

[015-041] But we must affirm it to be scientifically false to ask oneself if

[015-042] the dog be beautiful and the ornithorhynchus ugly, the lily beautiful

[015-043] and the artichoke ugly. Indeed, the error is here double. On the one

[015-044] hand, æsthetic Physics falls back into the equivocation of the theory

[015-045] of artistic and literary kinds, of attempting to attach æsthetic

[015-046] determinations to the abstractions of our intellect; on the other, it

[015-047] fails to recognize, as we said, the true formation of so-called natural

[015-048] beauty, a formation which excludes even the possibility of the question

[015-049] as to whether some given individual animal, flower or man be beautiful

[015-050] or ugly. What is not produced by the æsthetic spirit, or cannot be

[015-051] referred to it, is neither beautiful nor ugly. The æsthetic process

[015-052] arises from the ideal connexions in which natural objects are placed.

[015-053] *Criticism of the theory of the beauty of the human body.*

[015-054] The double error can be exemplified by the question as to the *Beauty

[015-055] of the human body,* upon which whole volumes have been written. Here

[015-056] we must before everything turn those who discuss this subject from the

[015-057] abstract toward the concrete, by asking: "What do you mean by the

[015-058] human body, that of the male, the female, or the hermaphrodite?" Let

[015-059] us assume that they reply by dividing the inquiry into two distinct

[015-060] inquiries, as to male and female beauty (there really are writers

[015-061] who seriously discuss whether man or woman is the more beautiful);

[015-062] and let us continue: "Masculine or feminine beauty; but of what race

[015-063] of men—the white, the yellow or the black, or any others that may

[015-064] exist, according to the division you prefer?" Let us assume that they

[015-065] limit themselves to the white race, and drive home the argument: "To

[015-066] what sub-species of the white race?" And when we have restricted them

[015-067] gradually to one corner of the white world, going, let us say, from

[015-068] the Italian to the Tuscan, the Siennese, the Porta Camollia quarter,

[015-069] we will proceed: "Very good; but at what age of the human body, and in

[015-070] what condition and stage—that of the newborn babe, of the child, of

[015-071] the boy, of the adolescent, of the man of middle age, and so on? and of

[015-072] him who is at rest or of him who is at work, or of him who is occupied

[015-073] like Paul Potter's bull, or the Ganymede of Rembrandt?"

[015-074] Having thus arrived, by successive reductions, at the individual

[015-075] *omnimode determinatum,* or rather at "this man here," pointed out with

[015-076] the finger, it will be easy to expose the other error, by recalling

[015-077] what we have said about the natural fact, which is now beautiful, now

[015-078] ugly, according to the point of view and to what is passing in the soul

[015-079] of the artist. If even the Gulf of Naples have its detractors, and if

[015-080] there be artists who declare it inexpressive, preferring the "gloomy

[015-081] firs," the "clouds and perpetual north winds," of northern seas; is it

[015-082] really possible that such relativity does not exist for the human body,

[015-083] source of the most varied suggestions?

[015-084] *Criticism of the beauty of geometrical figures.*

[015-085] The question of the *beauty of geometrical figures* is connected with

[015-086] æsthetic Physics. But if by geometrical figures be understood the

[015-087] concepts of geometry (the concepts of the triangle, the square, the

[015-088] cone), these are neither beautiful nor ugly, just because they are

[015-089] concepts. If, on the other hand, by such figures be understood bodies

[015-090] which possess definite geometrical forms, they will be beautiful

[015-091] or ugly, like every natural fact, according to the ideal connexions

[015-092] in which they are placed. Some hold that those geometrical figures

[015-093] are beautiful which point upwards, since they give the suggestion

[015-094] of firmness and of power. We do not deny that this may be so. But

[015-095] it must not be denied on the other hand that those also may possess

[015-096] beauty which give the impression of instability and weakness, where

[015-097] they represent just the insecure and the feeble; and that in these

[015-098] last cases the firmness of the straight fine and the lightness of the

[015-099] cone or of the equilateral triangle would seem to be on the contrary

[015-100] elements of ugliness.

[015-101] Certainly, such questions as to the beauty of nature and the beauty

[015-102] of geometry, like others analogous as to the historically beautiful

[015-103] and human beauty, seem less absurd in the Æsthetic of the sympathetic,

[015-104] which really means by the words "æsthetic beauty" the representation

[015-105] of the pleasing. But the claim to determine scientifically what are

[015-106] sympathetic contents and what are irremediably antipathetic is none the

[015-107] less erroneous, even in the sphere of that doctrine and after laying

[015-108] down those premises. One can only answer such questions by repeating

[015-109] with an infinitely long postscript the *Sunt quos* of the first ode of

[015-110] the first book of Horace, and the *Havvi chi* of Leopardi's letter to

[015-111] Carlo Pepoli. To each man his beautiful (= sympathetic), as to each man

[015-112] his fair one. Philography is not science.

[015-113] *Criticism of another aspect of the imitation of nature.*

[015-114] The artist sometimes has naturally existing facts before him, in

[015-115] producing the artificial instrument, or physically beautiful. These are

[015-116] called his *models*: bodies, stuffs, flowers and so on. Let us run over

[015-117] the sketches, studies and notes of artists: Leonardo noted down in his

[015-118] pocket-book, when he was working on the Last Supper: "Giovannina, weird

[015-119] face, is at St. Catherine's, at the Hospital; Cristofano di Castiglione

[015-120] is at the Pietà, he has a fine head; Christ, Giovan Conte, of Cardinal

[015-121] Mortaro's suite." And so on. From this comes the illusion that the

[015-122] artist *imitates nature,* when it would perhaps be more exact to say

[015-123] that nature imitates the artist, and obeys him. The illusion that

[015-124] *art imitates nature* has sometimes found ground and support in this

[015-125] illusion, as also in its variant, more easily maintained, which makes

[015-126] of art the *idealizer of nature.* This last theory presents the process

[015-127] out of its true order, which indeed is not merely upset but actually

[015-128] inverted; for the artist does not proceed from external reality, in

[015-129] order to modify it by approximating it to the ideal; he goes from

[015-130] the impression of external nature to expression, that is to say, his

[015-131] ideal, and from this passes to the natural fact, which he employs as

[015-132] instrument of reproduction of the ideal fact.

[015-133] *Criticism of the theory of the elementary forms of the

[015-134] beautiful.*

[015-135] Another consequence of the confusion between the æsthetic fact and the

[015-136] physical fact is the theory of the *elementary forms of the beautiful.*

[015-137] If expression, if the beautiful, be indivisible, the physical fact on

[015-138] the contrary, in which it externalizes itself, can easily be divided

[015-139] and subdivided: for example, a painted surface, into lines and colours,

[015-140] groups and curves of lines, kinds of colours, and so on; a poem, into

[015-141] strophes, verses, feet, syllables; a piece of prose, into chapters,

[015-142] paragraphs, headings, periods, phrases, words and so on. The parts

[015-143] thus obtained are not æsthetic facts, but smaller physical facts,

[015-144] arbitrarily divided. If this path were followed and the confusion

[015-145] persisted in, we should end by concluding that the true elementary

[015-146] forms of the beautiful are *atoms.*

[015-147] The æsthetic law, several times promulgated, that beauty must

[015-148] have *bulk,* could be invoked against the atoms. It cannot be the

[015-149] imperceptibility of the too small, or the inapprehensibility of the

[015-150] too large. But a greatness determined by perceptibility, not by

[015-151] measurement, implies a concept widely different from the mathematical.

[015-152] Indeed, what is called imperceptible and inapprehensible does not

[015-153] produce an impression, because it is not a real fact, but a concept:

[015-154] the demand for bulk in the beautiful is thus reduced to the actual

[015-155] presence of the physical fact, which serves for the reproduction of the

[015-156] beautiful.

[015-157] *Criticism of the search for the objective conditions of the

[015-158] beautiful.*

[015-159] Continuing the search for the *physical laws* or for the *objective

[015-160] conditions of the beautiful,* it has been asked: To what physical facts

[015-161] does the beautiful correspond? To what the ugly? To what unions of

[015-162] tones, colours, sizes, mathematically determinable? Such inquiries are

[015-163] as if in Political Economy one were to seek for the laws of exchange

[015-164] in the physical nature of the objects exchanged. The persistent

[015-165] fruitlessness of the attempt should have given rise before long to some

[015-166] suspicion of its vanity. In our times, especially, necessity for an

[015-167] *inductive* Æsthetic has been often proclaimed, of an Æsthetic starting

[015-168] *from below,* proceeding like natural science and not jumping to its

[015-169] conclusions. Inductive? But Æsthetic has always been both inductive and

[015-170] deductive, like every philosophical science; induction and deduction

[015-171] cannot be separated, nor can they separately avail to characterize

[015-172] a true science. But the word "induction" was not pronounced here by

[015-173] chance. The intention was to imply that the æsthetic fact is really

[015-174] nothing but a physical fact, to be studied by the methods proper to the

[015-175] physical and natural sciences.

[015-176] With such a presupposition and in such a faith did inductive Æsthetic

[015-177] or Æsthetic *from below* (what pride in this modesty!) begin its

[015-178] labours. It conscientiously began by making a collection of *beautiful

[015-179] things,* for example, a great number of envelopes of various shapes and

[015-180] sizes, and asked which of these give the impression of beauty and which

[015-181] of ugliness. As was to be expected, the inductive æstheticians speedily

[015-182] found themselves in a difficulty, for the same objects that appeared

[015-183] ugly in one aspect appeared beautiful in another. A coarse yellow

[015-184] envelope, which would be extremely ugly for the purpose of enclosing

[015-185] a love-letter, is just what is wanted for a writ served by process on

[015-186] stamped paper, which in its turn would look very bad, or seem at any

[015-187] rate an irony, enclosed in a square envelope of [[england|English]] paper. Such

[015-188] considerations of simple common sense should have sufficed to convince

[015-189] inductive æstheticians that the beautiful has no physical existence,

[015-190] and cause them to desist from their vain and ridiculous quest. But no:

[015-191] they had recourse to an expedient, as to which we should hardly like to

[015-192] say how far it belongs to the strict method of natural science. They

[015-193] sent their envelopes round and opened a *referendum,* trying to settle

[015-194] in what beauty or ugliness consists by the votes of the majority.

[015-195] *The Astrology of Æsthetic.*

[015-196] We will not waste time over this subject, lest we should seem to be

[015-197] turning ourselves into tellers of comic tales rather than expositors of

[015-198] æsthetic science and of its problems. It is a matter of fact that the

[015-199] inductive æstheticians have not yet discovered *one single law.*

[015-200] He who despairs of doctors is apt to abandon himself to charlatans.

[015-201] This has befallen those who have believed in the naturalistic laws of

[015-202] the beautiful. Artists sometimes adopt empirical canons, such as that

[015-203] of the proportions of the human body, or of the golden section, that

[015-204] is to say, of a line divided into two parts in such a manner that the

[015-205] less is to the greater as is the greater to the whole line (*be : ac

[015-206] = ac : ab*). Such canons easily become their superstitions, and they

[015-207] attribute to them the success of their works. Thus Michæl Angelo left

[015-208] as a precept to his disciple Marco del Pino da Siena that "he should

[015-209] always make a pyramidal serpentine figure multiplied by one two and

[015-210] three," a precept which did not enable Marco da Siena to emerge from

[015-211] that mediocrity which we can yet observe in many of his paintings that

[015-212] exist here in Naples. Others took Michæl Angelo's words as authority

[015-213] for the precept that serpentine undulating lines were the true *lines

[015-214] of beauty.* Whole volumes have been composed on these laws of beauty,

[015-215] on the golden section and on the undulating and serpentine lines. These

[015-216] should in our opinion be looked upon as the *astrology of Æsthetic.*
