# Pure Beauty

In the history of aesthetics, the concept of pure beauty designates a beauty free of all admixture — beauty that pleases without reference to any concept, purpose, or individual character. Croce subjects this concept to sustained criticism, arguing that it is a philosophical abstraction that cannot correspond to any real aesthetic experience.

## Definition

Pure beauty is the thesis "that art consists of pure beauty" (012-134) — beauty unadulterated by conceptual content, moral purpose, utility, or sensuous charm. In its Kantian formulation as *pulchritudo vaga* (free beauty), it is the beauty that pleases without presupposing a concept of what the object ought to be, as opposed to *pulchritudo adhaerens* (dependent beauty) which presupposes such a concept. Croce notes that Kant "held that art is not pure beauty wholly detached from the concept" (027-103), reserving pure beauty for natural objects (flowers, crystals, bird plumage) and ornamental designs, while admitting that artistic beauty involves conceptual content.

## Croce's Critique

### The Impossibility of Content-Free Beauty

Croce's central objection to pure beauty is that it is ontologically impossible within his system. Every æsthetic fact is intuition-expression — the form given to a particular content. To speak of beauty without content is to speak of form without anything to form, which is a contradiction. Pure beauty is "abstract beauty, which does not exist, or which exists only as an abstraction of the intellect" (012-133). The so-called pure beauty of geometrical figures, arabesques, and musical patterns is not content-free; it is rather the expression of a particular kind of content — the feeling of rhythm, balance, and proportion — and this content is as concrete and individual as any other.

### The Herbartian Formalism

The most systematic attempt to establish a science of pure beauty was the Herbartian school, which sought to reduce aesthetics to formal relations: symmetry, proportion, harmony of lines and colours. Croce acknowledges that the Herbartians "here introduce into pure beauty the variations and qualifications that make it art" (035-060), thereby undermining their own thesis. What begins as an austere formalism must constantly smuggle in historical, psychological, and individual elements to account for actual works of art.

### The Kantian Legacy and Its Difficulties

Croce's treatment of Kant is nuanced. He recognizes that Kant's distinction between free and dependent beauty was an important analytical tool, but argues that it ultimately fails: "According to Kant, art is not pure beauty wholly detached from the concept" (027-103). The very act of making art involves concepts, intentions, and purposes — the artist intends to make a *poem*, not merely a beautiful sound; a *portrait*, not merely a pleasing arrangement of colours. Free beauty cannot account for the fact that art is always *about* something.

## Historical Development

The concept of pure beauty traces a long arc from Plato's Form of Beauty (the Beautiful itself, beyond particular beautiful things) through medieval theories of divine radiance to eighteenth-century formalism. Schiller's "Heaven places all our joy in pure beauty" (012-136) captures the Romantic elevation of beauty to a transcendent principle. But it was in the nineteenth century, particularly in the Herbartian school, that the concept received its most rigorous formulation as a formal science. Robert Zimmermann, the leading Herbartian æsthetician, gave the movement its title with his *Æsthetic of Pure Beauty* (*Æsthetik der reinen Form*), to which Croce refers as "the title of Æsthetic of pure beauty" (012-140).

## Key Distinctions

**Pure Beauty vs. Artistic Beauty.** Pure beauty is a philosophical abstraction; artistic beauty is the concrete quality of individual works. Croce's entire anti-formalist polemic aims to show that the latter cannot be reduced to the former.

**Form vs. Content.** The pure-beauty thesis assumes that form can be isolated from content — that the beauty of a line, a chord, or a proportion can be considered independently of what it expresses. Croce's identity of intuition and expression entails that form is always the form-of-a-content, never form *per se*.

**Abstraction vs. Concreteness.** Pure beauty is an intellectual abstraction, a concept produced by the logical faculty. As such it belongs to the second degree of theoretical activity, not to the first (æsthetic). The æsthetic fact is always concrete and individual.

## In the System of the Arts

Croce notes that formalist theories have particular difficulty with the literary arts, where pure beauty is hardest to isolate. Poetry, drama, and narrative are inescapably tied to meanings, characters, and ideas. The Herbartians attempted to locate pure beauty in the "decorous, noble and comic" (032-436), but this only multiplies the forms of pure beauty and reintroduces conceptual content through the back door.

## Criticism and Debate

Contemporary aestheticians have largely abandoned the pure-beauty thesis in its strict Herbartian form, but the underlying intuition — that some instances of beauty seem independent of conceptual content — persists. The beauty of sunset colours, musical intervals, and abstract patterns continues to challenge theories that tie aesthetic value too tightly to semantic content.

Within Croce scholarship, the question is whether his critique of pure beauty succeeds on its own terms or whether it depends on premises (the identity of intuition and expression, the absolute individuality of the æsthetic fact) that are themselves open to challenge. If intuition-expression always involves individual content, then pure beauty is indeed a chimera; but if there are forms of aesthetic experience that do not fit the intuition-expression model, the concept of pure beauty may yet have a defensible domain.
