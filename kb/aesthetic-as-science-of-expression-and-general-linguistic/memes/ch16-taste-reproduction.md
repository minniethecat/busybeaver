[016-001] *The practical activity of externalization.*

[016-002] The fact of the production of physical beauty implies, as has already

[016-003] been remarked, a vigilant will, which persists in not allowing certain

[016-004] visions, intuitions or representations to be lost. Such a will must be

[016-005] able to act with the utmost rapidity and as it were instinctively, and

[016-006] may also need long and laborious deliberations. In any case, thus and

[016-007] thus only does the practical activity enter into relations with the

[016-008] æsthetic, that is to say, no longer as its simple accompaniment, but as

[016-009] a really distinct moment of it. We cannot will or not will our æsthetic

[016-010] vision: we can however will or not will to externalize it, or rather,

[016-011] to preserve and communicate to others, or not, the externalization

[016-012] produced.

[016-013] *The technique of externalization.*

[016-014] This volitional fact of externalization is preceded by a complex of

[016-015] various kinds of knowledge. These are known as *technique,* like all

[016-016] knowledge which precedes a practical activity. Thus we talk of an

[016-017] *[[technique|artistic technique]]* in the same metaphorical and elliptic manner that

[016-018] we talk of the physically beautiful, that is to say (in more precise

[016-019] language), *knowledge at the service of the practical activity directed

[016-020] to producing stimuli to æsthetic reproduction.* In place of employing

[016-021] so lengthy a phrase, we shall here avail ourselves of ordinary

[016-022] terminology, whose meaning we now understand.

[016-023] The possibility of this technical knowledge, at the service of artistic

[016-024] reproduction, is what has led minds astray to imagine the existence

[016-025] of an æsthetic technique of internal expression, which is tantamount

[016-026] to saying, a doctrine of the *means of internal expression,* a thing

[016-027] that is altogether inconceivable. And we know well the reason of its

[016-028] inconceivability; expression, considered in itself, is a primary

[016-029] theoretic activity, and as such precedes practice and intellectual

[016-030] knowledge which illumines practice and is independent alike of both.

[016-031] It aids for its part to illumine practice, but is not illuminated by

[016-032] it. [[expression|Expression]] does not possess *means,* because it has not an *end*;

[016-033] it has intuitions of things, but it does not will and is therefore

[016-034] unanalysable into the abstract components of volition, means and end.

[016-035] Sometimes a certain writer is said to have invented a new technique

[016-036] of fiction or of drama, or a painter is said to have discovered a new

[016-037] technique of distributing light. The word is used here at hazard;

[016-038] because the so-called *new technique* is really *that romance itself,

[016-039] or that new picture* itself and nothing else. The distribution of

[016-040] light belongs to the vision of the picture itself; as the technique

[016-041] of a dramatist is his dramatic conception itself. On other occasions,

[016-042] the word "technique" is used to designate certain merits or defects

[016-043] in a work that is a failure; and it is euphemistically said that the

[016-044] conception is bad but the technique good, or that the conception is

[016-045] good but the technique bad.

[016-046] On the other hand, when we talk of the different ways of painting

[016-047] in oils, or of etching, or of sculpturing in alabaster, then the

[016-048] word "technique" is in its place; but in such a case the adjective

[016-049] "artistic" is used metaphorically. And if a dramatic technique in the

[016-050] æsthetic sense be impossible, a theatrical technique of processes of

[016-051] externalization of certain particular æsthetic works is not impossible.

[016-052] When, for instance, women were introduced on the stage in [[italy|Italy]] in the

[016-053] second half of the sixteenth century, in place of men dressed as women,

[016-054] this was a true and real discovery in theatrical technique; such too

[016-055] was the perfecting in the following century of machines for the rapid

[016-056] changing of scenery by the impresarios of Venice.

[016-057] The collection of technical knowledge at the service of artists

[016-058] desirous of externalizing their expressions, can be divided into

[016-059] groups, which may be entitled *theories of the arts.* Thus arises

[016-060] a theory of Architecture, comprising mechanical laws, information

[016-061] relating to the weight or resistance of the materials of construction

[016-062] or of fortification, manuals relating to the method of mixing lime or

[016-063] stucco; a theory of Sculpture, containing advice as to the instruments

[016-064] to be used for sculpturing the various sorts of stone, for obtaining

[016-065] a successful mixture of bronze, for working with the chisel, for

[016-066] the accurate casting of the clay or plaster model, for keeping clay

[016-067] damp; a theory of Painting, on the various techniques of tempera,

[016-068] of oil-painting, of water-colour, of pastel, on the proportions of

[016-069] the human body, on the laws of perspective; a theory of Oratory,

[016-070] with precepts as to the method of producing, of exercising and of

[016-071] strengthening the voice, of attitude in impersonation and gesture; a

[016-072] theory of Music, on the combinations and fusions of tones and sounds;

[016-073] and so on. Such collections of precepts abound in all literatures.

[016-074] And since it is impossible to say what is useful and what useless to

[016-075] know, books of this sort become very often a sort of encyclopædias or

[016-076] *catalogues of desiderata.* Vitruvius, in his treatise on Architecture,

[016-077] claims for the architect a knowledge of letters, of drawing, of

[016-078] geometry, of arithmetic, of optic, of history, of natural and moral

[016-079] philosophy, of jurisprudence, of medicine, of astrology, of music, and

[016-080] so on. Everything is worth knowing: learn the art and have done with it.

[016-081] *Technical theories of the different arts.*

[016-082] It should be evident that such empirical collections are not reducible

[016-083] to science. They are composed of notions, taken from various sciences

[016-084] and disciplines, and their philosophical and scientific principles

[016-085] are to be found in the latter. To propose to construct a scientific

[016-086] theory of the different arts would be to wish to reduce to the single

[016-087] and homogeneous what is by nature multiple and heterogeneous; to wish

[016-088] to destroy the existence as a collection of what was put together

[016-089] precisely to form a collection. Were we to try to give scientific

[016-090] form to the manuals of the architect, the painter, or the musician,

[016-091] it is clear that nothing would remain in our hands but the general

[016-092] principles of Mechanics, Optics, or Acoustics. And if we were to

[016-093] extract and isolate what may be scattered among them of properly

[016-094] artistic observations, to make of them a scientific system, then the

[016-095] sphere of the individual art would be abandoned and that of Æsthetic

[016-096] entered, for Æsthetic is always general Æsthetic, or rather it cannot

[016-097] be divided into general and special. This last case (that is, the

[016-098] attempt to furnish a technique which ends in composing an Æsthetic)

[016-099] arises when men possessing strong scientific instincts and a natural

[016-100] tendency to philosophy set themselves to work to produce such theories

[016-101] and technical manuals.

[016-102] *Criticism of æsthetic theories of particular arts*.

[016-103] But the confusion between Physics and Æsthetic has attained to its

[016-104] highest degree, when æsthetic theories of particular arts are imagined,

[016-105] to answer such questions as: What are the *limits* of each art? What

[016-106] can be represented with colours, and what with sounds? What with simple

[016-107] monochromatic lines and what with touches of various colours? What with

[016-108] tones, and what with metres and rhythms? What are the limits between

[016-109] the figurative and the auditive arts, between painting and sculpture,

[016-110] poetry and music?

[016-111] This, translated into scientific language, is tantamount to asking:

[016-112] What is the connexion between Acoustics and æsthetic expression? What

[016-113] between the latter and Optics?—and the like. Now, if *there is no

[016-114] passage* from the physical fact to the æsthetic, how could there be

[016-115] from the æsthetic to particular groups of physical facts, such as the

[016-116] phenomena of Optics or of Acoustics?

[016-117] *Criticism of the classification of the arts.*

[016-118] The so-called *arts* have no æsthetic limits, because, in order to

[016-119] have them, they would need to have also æsthetic existence in their

[016-120] particularity; and we have demonstrated the altogether empirical

[016-121] genesis of those partitions. Consequently, any attempt at an æsthetic

[016-122] classification of the arts is absurd. If they be without limits,

[016-123] they are not exactly determinable, and consequently cannot be

[016-124] philosophically classified. All the books dealing with classifications

[016-125] and systems of the arts could be burned without any loss whatever. (We

[016-126] say this with the utmost respect to the writers who have expended

[016-127] their labours upon them.)

[016-128] The impossibility of such systematizations finds something like a proof

[016-129] in the strange attempts made to carry it out. The first and most common

[016-130] partition is that into arts of *hearing, sight,* and *imagination*;

[016-131] as if eyes, ears, and imagination were on the same level and could be

[016-132] deduced from the same logical variable as *fundamentum divisionis.*

[016-133] Others have proposed the division into arts of *space* and arts of

[016-134] *time,* arts of *rest*; and *movement*; as if the concepts of space,

[016-135] time, rest and motion could determine special æsthetic forms and

[016-136] possess anything in common with art as such. Finally, others have

[016-137] amused themselves by dividing them into *classic* and *romantic,*

[016-138] or into *oriental, classic,* and *romantic,* thereby conferring the

[016-139] value of scientific concepts upon simple historical denominations, or

[016-140] falling into those rhetorical partitions of expressive forms, already

[016-141] criticized above; or into arts *that can only be seen from one side,*

[016-142] like painting, and arts *that can be seen from all sides,* like

[016-143] sculpture—and similar extravagances, which hold good neither in heaven

[016-144] nor on earth.

[016-145] The theory of the limits of the arts was perhaps at the time when

[016-146] it was put forward a beneficial critical reaction against those who

[016-147] believed in the possibility of remodelling one expression into another,

[016-148] as the *Iliad* or *Paradise Lost* into a series of paintings, and

[016-149] indeed held a poem to be of greater or lesser value according as it

[016-150] could or could not be translated into pictures by a painter. But if the

[016-151] rebellion were reasonable and resulted in victory, this does not mean

[016-152] that the arguments employed and the systems constructed for the purpose

[016-153] were sound.

[016-154] *Criticism of the theory of the union of the arts.*

[016-155] Another theory which is a corollary to that of the arts and their

[016-156] limits, falls with them; that of the *union of the arts.* Given

[016-157] particular arts, distinct and limited, it was asked: Which is the most

[016-158] *powerful*? Do we not obtain *more powerful* effects by *uniting*

[016-159] several? We know nothing of this: we know only that in each particular

[016-160] case certain given artistic intuitions have need of definite physical

[016-161] means for their reproduction and other artistic intuitions of other

[016-162] means. We can obtain the effect of certain plays by simply reading

[016-163] them; others need declamation and scenic display: there are some

[016-164] artistic intuitions which need for their full externalization words,

[016-165] song, musical instruments, colours, statuary, architecture, actors;

[016-166] while others are quite complete in a slight outline made with the

[016-167] pen, or a few strokes of the pencil. But it is false to suppose that

[016-168] declamation and scenic effects and all the other things together that

[016-169] we have mentioned are *more powerful* than a simple reading or a simple

[016-170] outline of pen or pencil; because each of those facts or groups of

[016-171] facts has, so to say, a different purpose, and the power of the means

[016-172] cannot be compared when the purposes are different.

[016-173] *Relation of the activity of externalization to utility and

[016-174] morality.*

[016-175] Finally, it is only from the point of view of a clear and rigorous

[016-176] distinction between the true and proper æsthetic activity and the

[016-177] practical activity of externalization that we can solve the complicated

[016-178] and confused questions as to the relations between *art and utility*

[016-179] and *art and morality.*

[016-180] We have demonstrated above that art as art is independent both of

[016-181] utility and of morality, as also of all practical value. Without this

[016-182] independence, it would not be possible to speak of an intrinsic value

[016-183] of art, nor indeed to conceive an æsthetic science, which demands the

[016-184] autonomy of the æsthetic fact as its necessary condition.

[016-185] But it would be erroneous to maintain that this independence of the

[016-186] vision or intuition or *internal expression* of the artist should

[016-187] be simply extended to the practical activity of externalization and

[016-188] communication which may or may not follow the æsthetic fact. If by art

[016-189] be understood the externalization of art, then utility and morality

[016-190] have a perfect right to enter into it; that is to say, the right to be

[016-191] master in one's own house.

[016-192] Indeed we do not externalize and fix all the many expressions and

[016-193] intuitions which we form in our spirit; we do not declare our every

[016-194] thought in a loud voice, or write it down, or print, or draw, or

[016-195] paint, or expose it to the public. We *select* from the crowd of

[016-196] intuitions which are formed or at least sketched within us; and the

[016-197] selection is ruled by the criteria of the economic disposition of life

[016-198] and of its moral direction. Therefore, when we have fixed an intuition,

[016-199] we have still to decide whether or no we should communicate it to

[016-200] others, and to whom, and when, and how; all which deliberations come

[016-201] equally under the utilitarian and ethical criterion.

[016-202] Thus we find the concepts of *selection,* of the *interesting,* of

[016-203] *morality,* of an *educational end,* of *popularity,* etc., to some

[016-204] extent justified, although these can in no way be justified when

[016-205] imposed upon art as art, and we have ourselves rejected them in pure

[016-206] Æsthetic. Error always contains an element of truth. He who formulated

[016-207] those erroneous æsthetic propositions in reality had his eye on

[016-208] practical facts, which attach themselves externally to the æsthetic

[016-209] fact and belong to economic and moral fife.

[016-210] It is well to advocate yet greater freedom in making known the means

[016-211] of æsthetic reproduction; we are of the same opinion, and leave

[016-212] projects for legislation and for legal action against immoral art,

[016-213] to hypocrites, to the ingenuous and to wasters of time. But the

[016-214] proclamation of this freedom, and the fixing of its limits, how

[016-215] wide soever they be, is always the task of morality. And it would

[016-216] in any case be out of place to invoke that highest principle, that

[016-217] *fundamentum æsthetices,* which is the independence of art, to deduce

[016-218] from it the guiltlessness of the artist who calculates like an

[016-219] immoral speculator upon the unhealthy tastes of his readers in the

[016-220] externalization of his imaginings, or the freedom of hawkers to sell

[016-221] obscene statuettes in the public squares. This last case is the affair

[016-222] of the police, as the first must be brought before the tribunal of

[016-223] the moral consciousness. The æsthetic judgement on the [[art|work of art]]

[016-224] has nothing to do with the morality of the artist as a practical man,

[016-225] or with the provisions to be taken that the things of art may not be

[016-226] diverted to evil ends alien to her nature, which is pure theoretic

[016-227] contemplation.
