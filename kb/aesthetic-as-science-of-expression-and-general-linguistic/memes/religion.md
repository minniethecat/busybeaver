# Religion

In Croce's philosophy of spirit, religion is a transitional and incomplete form of theoretical knowledge: it is philosophy still clothed in myth and imagination, destined to be superseded by the pure concept but retaining a permanent office as the matrix from which philosophy continually arises (009-067, 009-099).

## Definition

Croce defines religion as "nothing but knowledge" — but knowledge in an immature, mythologized form (009-067). Religion shares with art the form of intuitive, individual expression, and with philosophy the aspiration to universal truth; but it achieves neither the pure expressiveness of art nor the pure conceptuality of philosophy. It is, in Croce's striking formulation, "a phenomenon, a transitory historical fact" in the life of spirit — necessary at certain stages, but destined to be overcome as spirit rises to philosophical self-consciousness (009-101). "Philosophy removes from religion all reason for existing, because it substitutes itself for religion" (009-099).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Religion occupies an unstable position in Croce's fourfold system, poised between intuition (art) and concept (philosophy) in the theoretical sphere. It is not a distinct fifth form of spirit but a hybrid: religion borrows the imaginative form of art and the universal aspiration of philosophy without fully achieving either. "The savage has speech, intellect, religion" — all three as undifferentiated modes of addressing reality, before the critical separation of spheres (018-342). In developed spiritual life, religion persists as a residue: that "remainder with which the human spirit cannot dispense" but which philosophy continually works to clarify and dissolve (009-093). Croce's position is thus not atheistic negation but dialectical supersession (*Aufhebung*).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **religion vs. philosophy** sharply: "with equal truth" one may say "that religion is destroyed" or "that religion is preserved" in philosophy — destroyed in its mythological form, preserved in its rational content (009-071). Second, **religion vs. art**: both employ imaginative form, but art seeks only individual expression whereas religion makes universal claims through that form. Art "can and does exist without philosophy" or religion; religion cannot exist without art's expressive means (P03-087). Third, Croce distinguishes **historical religion** (the concrete spiritual patrimony of peoples — "their religion was the whole intellectual patrimony") from **systematic religion** (theological doctrine), treating the former as a genuine phase of spiritual development and the latter as a doomed attempt to arrest that development in fixed formulae (009-073, 009-074).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The relation between art and religion has been a central theme in Western aesthetics. Hegel, whose philosophy Croce engages extensively, treated art, religion, and philosophy as three forms of absolute spirit, with art giving way to religion and religion to philosophy in the dialectical unfolding of self-consciousness. The Romantic tradition — from Schleiermacher to Novalis — elevated religion as the supreme form of spiritual experience and often conflated aesthetic with religious feeling. In Italy, Vico had already traced the origins of poetry and myth to a primitive religious consciousness, a view that deeply influenced Croce's own genetic account. G. W. F. Hegel's treatment of "Art in the sphere of practice and religion" provided the immediate foil for Croce's reclassification (P02-240).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of religion is distinctive in its refusal to grant it an autonomous place in the system of spirit while simultaneously acknowledging its historical necessity and permanent recurrence. Unlike Hegel, who made religion a distinct moment of absolute spirit, Croce dissolves the category into its components — imaginative form (art) and conceptual content (philosophy) — and treats the compound as inherently unstable. "Philosophy looks upon religion as a phenomenon" (009-101), but this looking is not dismissive: it recognizes that "our intellectual patrimony is our religion" (009-074) — the entire historical accumulation of spiritual achievement that each generation inherits and transforms. Croce's innovation is to treat the art-religion-philosophy relation not as a linear progression but as a permanent dialectic in which religion is continuously generated from the imaginative life and continuously dissolved by critical thought.

## Criticism and Debate

> "Religion has been sometimes hostile to art and philosophy, and sometimes philosophy and religion have seemed inseparable" (009-160, 009-164). Croce's dialectical account captures this oscillation but may underestimate religion's capacity for self-critical renewal independent of philosophy.

Critics have argued that Croce's reduction of religion to "mythologized philosophy" fails to do justice to the experiential and practical dimensions of religious life. If religion is fundamentally a mode of practice — a form of life — rather than a form of theory, then its subordination to philosophy within the theoretical sphere may be a category error. The debate continues in contemporary aesthetics, where the relation between art and the sacred remains a live question.
