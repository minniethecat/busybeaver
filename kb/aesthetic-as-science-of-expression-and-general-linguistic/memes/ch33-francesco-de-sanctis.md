[033-001] NINETEENTH CENTURY

[033-002] *Æsthetic movement in [[france|France]]: Cousin, Jouffroy.*

[033-003] In the last quarter of the eighteenth century and the first half of

[033-004] the nineteenth century German thought, notwithstanding the glaring

[033-005] errors which vitiated it, and were soon to bring about a violent and

[033-006] indeed exaggerated reaction, must on the whole be awarded the foremost

[033-007] place in the general history of European thought as well as in the

[033-008] individual study of Æsthetic, the contemporary philosophy of other

[033-009] countries standing on an inferior level of the second and third degree.

[033-010] France still lay under the dominion of the sensationalism of Condillac

[033-011] and, at the opening of the century, was quite incapable of grasping

[033-012] the spiritual activity of art. A faint gleam of [[j-j-winckelmann|Winckelmann]]'s abstract

[033-013] spiritualism just appears in the theories of Quatremère de Quincy, who,

[033-014] in criticism of Émeric-David (in his turn a critic of ideal beauty and

[033-015] an adherent of the imitation of nature),[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_1_552) maintained that the arts

[033-016] of design have pure beauty, devoid of individual character, as their

[033-017] objective; they depict man and not; men.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_2_553) Some sensationalists, such

[033-018] as Bonstetten, vainly endeavoured to trace the peculiar processes

[033-019] of imagination in life and in art.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_3_554) Followers of the orthodox

[033-020] spiritualism of the French universities date the beginning of a new

[033-021] era, and the foundation of Æsthetic in France, to 1818, the year when

[033-022] Victor Cousin first delivered at the Sorbonne his lectures on the

[033-023] True, the Beautiful and the Good, which later formed his book with the

[033-024] same name, frequently reprinted.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_4_555) These lectures of Cousin are but

[033-025] poor stuff, although some scraps of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] are to be found in them here

[033-026] and there; he denies the identity of [[beauty|the beautiful]] with the pleasant

[033-027] or useful, and substitutes the affirmation of a threefold beauty,

[033-028] physical, intellectual and moral, the last being the true ideal beauty,

[033-029] having its foundations in God; he says that art expresses ideal Beauty,

[033-030] the infinite, God, that genius is the power of creation, and that taste

[033-031] is a mixture of [[imagination|fancy]], sentiment and reason.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_5_556) Academic phrases all

[033-032] of them; pompous and void and, for that very reason, well received. Of

[033-033] much greater value were the lectures on Æsthetic delivered by Théodore

[033-034] Jouffroy in 1822, before a small audience, and published posthumously

[033-035] in 1843.[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_6_557) Jouffroy allowed a beauty of expression, to be found alike

[033-036] in art and nature: a beauty of imitation, consisting in the perfect

[033-037] accuracy with which a model is reproduced: a beauty of idealisation,

[033-038] which reproduces the model, accentuating a particular quality in

[033-039] order to give it greater significance: and, finally, a beauty of the

[033-040] invisible or of content, reducible to force (physical, sensible,

[033-041] intellectual, moral), which, as force, awakens sympathy. Ugliness is

[033-042] the negation of this sympathetic beauty; its species or modifications

[033-043] are [[sublime|the sublime]] and the graceful. One sees that Jouffroy did not

[033-044] succeed in isolating the strictly æsthetic fact in his analysis and

[033-045] gave, instead of a scientific system, little beyond explanations of the

[033-046] use of words. He could not see or understand that expression, imitation

[033-047] and idealization are identical with each other and with artistic

[033-048] activity. Moreover he had many curious ideas, chiefly concerning

[033-049] expression. He said that if we were to see a drunkard with all the

[033-050] most disgusting symptoms of intoxication on a road where there was also

[033-051] an unhewn rock, we should be pleased by the drunken man, since he had

[033-052] expression, and not by the rock, since it had none. Beside Jouffroy,

[033-053] whose theories, crude and immature though they be, reveal an inquiring

[033-054] mind, it is hardly worth while to cite Lamennais,[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_7_558) who like Cousin

[033-055] regarded art as the manifestation of the infinite through the finite,

[033-056] of the absolute through the relative. French [[romanticism|Romanticism]] in de Bonald,

[033-057] de Barante and Mme. de Staël had defined literature as "the expression

[033-058] of society," had honoured, under German influence, the characteristic

[033-059] and the grotesque,[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_8_559) and had proclaimed the independence of art by

[033-060] means of the formula "art for art's sake"; but these vague affirmations

[033-061] or aphorisms did not supersede, philosophically speaking, the old

[033-062] doctrine of the "imitation of nature."

[033-063] *[[england|English]] Æsthetic.*

[033-064] In England associationistic psychology still flourished (and has

[033-065] continued to flourish uninterruptedly), unable to emancipate itself

[033-066] wholly from sensationalism or to understand imagination. Dugald

[033-067] Stewart[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_9_560) had recourse to the wretched expedient of establishing

[033-068] two forms of association: one of accidental associations, the other

[033-069] of associations innate in human nature and therefore common to all

[033-070] mankind. England did not escape German influence, as appears, for

[033-071] example, in Coleridge, to whom we owe a saner concept of poetry and

[033-072] the difference between it and science[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_10_561) (in collaboration with

[033-073] the poet Wordsworth), and in Carlyle, who placed intellect lower

[033-074] than imagination, "organ of the Divine." The most noteworthy English

[033-075] æsthetic essay of this period is the *Defence of Poetry* by Shelley

[033-076] (1821),[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_11_562) containing profound, if not very systematic, views on the

[033-077] distinctions between reason and imagination, prose and poetry; on

[033-078] primitive language and the faculty of poetic objectification which

[033-079] enshrines and preserves "the record of the best and happiest moments of

[033-080] the happiest and best minds."

[033-081] *Italian Æsthetic.*

[033-082] In [[italy|Italy]], where neither Parini nor Foscolo[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_12_563) had been able to shake

[033-083] off the fetters of the old doctrines (although the latter, in his later

[033-084] writings, was in several ways an innovator in literary criticism), many

[033-085] treatises and essays on Æsthetic were published during the earlier

[033-086] decades of the century, the greater part showing the influence of

[033-087] Condillac's sensationalism, which had a great vogue in Italy. Such

[033-088] authors as Delfico, Malaspina, Cicognara, Talia, Pasquali, Visconti

[033-089] and Bonacci belong more exclusively to the special, or rather, the

[033-090] anecdotal, history of Italian philosophy. Now and then, however,

[033-091] one comes across remarks that are not wholly contemptible, as in

[033-092] Melchiorre Delfico (1818) who, after wandering aimlessly hither and

[033-093] thither, fixes on the principle of expression, observing, "If it

[033-094] were possible to establish that expression is always an element in

[033-095] the beautiful, it would be a legitimate inference to regard it as

[033-096] the real characteristic of beauty, *i.e.* a condition without which

[033-097] the beautiful could not exist, and the pleasing modification which

[033-098] arouses the sentiment of beauty could not take place in us"; he tries

[033-099] to develop this principle by asserting that all other characters

[033-100] (order, harmony, proportion, symmetry, simplicity, unity and variety)

[033-101] have significance only by their subordination to the principle of

[033-102] expression.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_13_564) In opposition to Malaspina's definition of beauty

[033-103] as "pleasure born of a representation"; and in opposition to the

[033-104] then fashionable threefold division of beauty into sensible, moral

[033-105] and intellectual, a critic of Malaspina observed that if beauty be

[033-106] representation, it is inconceivable that there should be intellectual

[033-107] beauty, which would be intelligible but not presentable.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_14_565) Nor must

[033-108] Pasquale Balestrieri be forgotten; he was a student of medicine who

[033-109] in 1847 tried to construct an Æsthetic of an exact or mathematical

[033-110] kind, with neither better nor worse result than many famous authors in

[033-111] other countries. He noticed, while turning his algebraical expressions

[033-112] into numerals, that such general formulæ "fulfil their object with an

[033-113] infinite number of systems of different ciphers"; and that in art there

[033-114] is an element "not arbitrary, but unknown."[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_15_566) Works by German authors

[033-115] were frequently translated at this time, some of them, for example

[033-116] the writings of the two Schlegels, being reprinted several times; the

[033-117] *Æsthetic* of Bouterweck, deriving from Kant and [[friedrich-schiller|Schiller]],[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_16_567) was read

[033-118] and discussed; Colecchi gave an excellent statement of the æsthetic

[033-119] doctrines of Kant;[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_17_568) and in 1831 a certain Lichtenthal adapted the

[033-120] *Æsthetic* of Franz Ficker[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_18_569) to the use of Italian readers; later the

[033-121] same book was fully translated by another hand; some of [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]]'s

[033-122] writings were translated, *e.g.* his discourses on the relation between

[033-123] figurative art and nature.

[033-124] *Rosmini and Gioberti.*

[033-125] It must be admitted that in Italy Æsthetic received but inadequate

[033-126] treatment in the revival of philosophical speculation effected by

[033-127] the work of Galluppi, Rosmini and Gioberti. It is treated in a

[033-128] merely incidental and popular manner by the first named.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_19_570) Rosmini

[033-129] devotes a section of his philosophical system to the deontological

[033-130] sciences, which "treat of the perfection of being, and the method of

[033-131] acquiring or producing such perfection or losing it"; among these

[033-132] sciences is that of "beauty in the universal" under the name of

[033-133] Callology, of which a special part is Æsthetic, the science of "beauty

[033-134] in the sensible," establishing the "archetypes of beings."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_20_571) In

[033-135] his longest literary work, considered by him as his Æsthetic,[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_21_572)

[033-136] his essay on *The Idyl*,[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_22_573) Rosmini declares the aim of art to be

[033-137] neither imitation of nature nor direct intuition of the archetypes,

[033-138] but the reduction of natural things to their archetypes, which are

[033-139] arranged in a hierarchy of three ideals, natural, intellectual and

[033-140] moral. Gioberti[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_23_574) is clearly under the influence of German idealism,

[033-141] especially of Schelling's; for him the beautiful is "the individual

[033-142] union of an intelligible type with an imaginative element called into

[033-143] being by fancy"; the phantasm gives material, while the intelligible

[033-144] type (concept) gives form, in the Aristotelian sense,[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_24_575) and since the

[033-145] ideal element predominates over the sensible or fantastic, art is a

[033-146] propædeutic to the true and the good. Gioberti is of opinion that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]

[033-147] was wrong in detaching natural beauty from Æsthetic, for perfect beauty

[033-148] of nature is "the full correspondence of sensible reality with the Idea

[033-149] which informs and represents it," and as such "makes its appearance

[033-150] in the sensible universe during the second period of the primordial

[033-151] age described in detail by Moses in the six days of creation"; it is

[033-152] only through original sin that imperfection and ugliness arose in

[033-153] nature.[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_25_576) [[art|Art]] is nothing but a supplement to natural beauty, whose

[033-154] decadence it presupposes, and thus art is at once record and prophecy,

[033-155] referring to the first and last ages of the world. The Last Judgement

[033-156] will reintroduce perfect beauty: "organic restitution, by empowering

[033-157] the faculties to contemplate the intelligible in the sensible, and by

[033-158] refining their capabilities, will greatly intensify and purify æsthetic

[033-159] enjoyment. The contemplation of perfect beauty will be the beatitude of

[033-160] imagination, of which Christ gave an ineffable foretaste by appearing

[033-161] to his disciples visibly transfigured and shining with celestial

[033-162] radiance."[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_26_577) Gioberti agrees with Schelling's division of art into

[033-163] pagan and Christian, a "heterodox beauty" (Oriental and Græco-Italian

[033-164] art), imperfect when compared with "orthodox beauty"; and between the

[033-165] two, a "semi-orthodox" beauty,[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_27_578) transitional to Christian art; he

[033-166] also attempted a doctrine of modifications of the beautiful, wherein he

[033-167] held the sublime to be creator of the beautiful. Beauty is the relative

[033-168] intelligibility of created things apprehended by fancy: the sublime

[033-169] is the absolute intelligibility of time, space and infinite power as

[033-170] presented to itself by the faculty of imagination: "The ideal formula:

[033-171] the Being creates the Existing, translated into æsthetic language,

[033-172] gives the following formula: by means of the dynamical sublime Being

[033-173] creates the beautiful; and by means of the mathematical sublime

[033-174] contains it: this shows the ontological and psychological connexions of

[033-175] Æsthetic in First Science." Ugliness enters into the beautiful either

[033-176] as relief and counterpoise, or to open a way to the comic, or to depict

[033-177] the struggle between good and evil. The Christian ideal of artistic

[033-178] beauty is the figure of the God-Man, absolute union of the two forms

[033-179] of beauty, the sublime and the beautiful, a transfigured and divinely

[033-180] illuminated expression of man.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_28_579) However carefully we sift the

[033-181] thoughts of Gioberti from their mythological Judaico-Christian husk, we

[033-182] find nothing of the least value to science.

[033-183] *Italian Romantics. Dependence of Art.*

[033-184] On the other hand, if Italian literature of the day chose to revive

[033-185] and refurbish certain antiquated critical ideas, a much wider field

[033-186] was opened by social and political upheavals which tended to make

[033-187] use of literature as a practical instrument for spreading abroad the

[033-188] truths of history, science, religion and morality. In 1816 Giovanni

[033-189] Berchet wrote that "poetry ... is intended to improve the habits of

[033-190] man and satisfy the cravings of his imagination and heart, since the

[033-191] tendency towards poetry, like every other desire, awakens in us moral

[033-192] needs";[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_29_580) and Ermes Visconti in his *Conciliatore* of 1818 says that

[033-193] æsthetic aims must be subordinated "to the improvement of mankind and

[033-194] public and private weal, the eminent aim of all studies." Manzoni,

[033-195] who subsequently took to philosophizing on art on the principles of

[033-196] Rosmini, declared in his letter on Romanticism (1823) that "poetry

[033-197] or literature in general should have utility as its objective, truth

[033-198] as its subject and interest as its means";[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_30_581) and though noticing

[033-199] the vagueness of the concept of truth in poetry, he inclined always

[033-200] (as is seen also in his discourse on the historical novel) to its

[033-201] identification with historical and scientific truth.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_31_582) Pietro

[033-202] Maroncelli proposed as a substitute for the classic formula of art,

[033-203] "founded on imitation of the real and having pleasure as its object,"

[033-204] a formula of art as "founded on inspiration, having the beautiful as

[033-205] means and good as end"; this doctrine he baptized "cormentalism,"

[033-206] contrasting it with the doctrine of art for art's sake found in the

[033-207] writings of August Wilhelm Schlegel and Victor Hugo.[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_32_583) Tommaseo

[033-208] defined beauty as "the union of many truths in one concept" effected

[033-209] by the power of feeling.[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_33_584) Giuseppe Mazzini, too, always conceived

[033-210] literature as the mediator of the universal idea or intellectual

[033-211] concept.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-21.htm.html#Footnote_34_585) Attempting to restore serious content to a literature

[033-212] grown weak and frivolous, the Italian Romantics found themselves forced

[033-213] on the theoretical side, by a natural reaction, into constant and

[033-214] perpetual opposition to every tendency of thought likely to affirm the

[033-215] independence of art.
