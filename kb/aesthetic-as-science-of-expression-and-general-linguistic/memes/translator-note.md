[P03-001] I can lay no claim to having discovered an America, but I do claim to

[P03-002] have discovered a Columbus. His name is Benedetto Croce, and he dwells

[P03-003] on the shores of the Mediterranean, at Naples, city of the antique

[P03-004] Parthenope.

[P03-005] It was at Naples, in the winter of 1907, that I first saw the

[P03-006] Philosopher of Æsthetic. Benedetto Croce, although born in the Abruzzi,

[P03-007] Province of Aquila (1866), is essentially a Neapolitan, and rarely

[P03-008] remains long absent from the city, on the shore of that magical sea

[P03-009] where once Ulysses sailed, and where sometimes yet (near Amalfi) we

[P03-010] may hear the Syrens sing their song. But more wonderful than the song

[P03-011] of any Syren seems to me the Theory of Æsthetic as the Science of

[P03-012] [[expression|Expression]], and that is why I have overcome the obstacles that stood

[P03-013] between me and the giving of this theory, which in my belief is the

[P03-014] truth, to the [[england|English]]-speaking world.

[P03-015] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-016] The solution of the problem of Æsthetic is not in the gift of the Muses.

[P03-017] This Philosophy of the Spirit is symptomatic of the happy reaction of

[P03-018] the twentieth century against the crude materialism of the second half

[P03-019] of the nineteenth. It is the spirit which gives to the [[art|work of art]] its

[P03-020] value, not this or that method of arrangement, this or that tint or

[P03-021] cadence, which can always be copied by skilful plagiarists: not so

[P03-022] the *spirit* of the creator. In England we hear too much of (natural)

[P03-023] science, which has usurped the very name of Philosophy. The natural

[P03-024] sciences are very well in their place, but discoveries such as aviation

[P03-025] are of infinitely less importance to the race than the smallest

[P03-026] addition to the philosophy of the spirit. Empirical science, with the

[P03-027] collusion of positivism, has stolen the cloak of philosophy and must be

[P03-028] made to give it back.

[P03-029] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-030] Yet though severe, the editor of *La Critica* is uncompromisingly just,

[P03-031] and would never allow personal dislike or jealousy, or any extrinsic

[P03-032] consideration, to stand in the way of fair treatment to the writer

[P03-033] concerned. Many superficial English critics might benefit considerably

[P03-034] by attention to this quality in one who is in other respects also so

[P03-035] immeasurably their superior. A good instance of this impartiality is

[P03-036] his critique of [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], with whose system he is in complete

[P03-037] disagreement, yet affords him full credit for what of truth is

[P03-038] contained in his voluminous writings.

[P03-039] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-040] This thoroughness it is which gives such importance to the literary

[P03-041] and philosophical criticisms of *La Critica.* Croce's method is always

[P03-042] historical, and his object in approaching any work of art is to

[P03-043] classify the spirit of its author, as expressed in that work. There

[P03-044] are, he maintains, but two things to be considered in criticizing a

[P03-045] book. These are, *firstly,* what is its *peculiarity,* in what way is

[P03-046] it singular, how is it differentiated from other works? *Secondly,*

[P03-047] what is its degree of *purity*?—That is, to what extent has its author

[P03-048] kept himself free from all considerations alien to the perfection of

[P03-049] the work as an expression, as a lyrical intuition? With the answering

[P03-050] of these questions Croce is satisfied. He does not care to know if the

[P03-051] author keep a motor-car, like Mæterlinck; or prefer to walk on Putney

[P03-052] Heath, like Swinburne. This amounts to saying that all works of art

[P03-053] must be judged by their own standard. How far has the author succeeded

[P03-054] in doing what he intended?

[P03-055] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-056] As regards Croce's general philosophical position, it is important to

[P03-057] understand that he is *not* a Hegelian, in the sense of being a close

[P03-058] follower of that philosopher. One of his last works is that in which

[P03-059] he deals in a masterly manner with the philosophy of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]. The title

[P03-060] may be translated, "What is living and what is dead of the philosophy

[P03-061] of Hegel." Here he explains to us the Hegelian system more clearly

[P03-062] than that wondrous edifice was ever before explained, and we realize

[P03-063] at the same time that Croce is quite as independent of Hegel as of

[P03-064] [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], of [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] as of Spinoza. Of course he has made use of the best of

[P03-065] Hegel, just as every thinker makes use of his predecessors and is in

[P03-066] his turn made use of by those that follow him. But it is incorrect

[P03-067] to accuse of Hegelianism the author of an anti-hegelian *Æsthetic,*

[P03-068] of a *Logic* where Hegel is only half accepted, and of a *Philosophy

[P03-069] of the Practical* which contains hardly a trace of Hegel. I give an

[P03-070] instance. If the great conquest of Hegel be the dialectic of opposites,

[P03-071] his great mistake lies in the confusion of opposites with things

[P03-072] which are distinct but not opposite. If, says Croce, we take as an

[P03-073] example the application of the Hegelian triad that formulates becoming

[P03-074] (affirmation, negation and synthesis), we find it applicable for those

[P03-075] opposites which are true and false, good and evil, being and not-being,

[P03-076] but *not applicable* to things which are distinct but not opposite,

[P03-077] such as art and philosophy, beauty and truth, the useful and the moral.

[P03-078] These confusions led Hegel to talk of the death of art, to conceive as

[P03-079] possible a Philosophy of History, and to the application of the natural

[P03-080] sciences to the absurd task of constructing a Philosophy of Nature.

[P03-081] Croce has cleared away these difficulties by showing that if from the

[P03-082] meeting of opposites must arise a superior synthesis, such a synthesis

[P03-083] cannot arise from things which are distinct *but not opposite,* since

[P03-084] the former are connected together as superior and inferior, and the

[P03-085] inferior can exist without the superior, but *not vice versa.* Thus we

[P03-086] see how philosophy cannot exist without art, while art, occupying the

[P03-087] lower place, can and does exist without philosophy. This brief example

[P03-088] reveals Croce's independence in dealing with Hegelian problems.

[P03-089] I know of no philosopher more generous than Croce in praise and

[P03-090] elucidation of other workers in the same field, past and present. For

[P03-091] instance, and apart from Hegel, *Kant* has to thank him for drawing

[P03-092] attention to the marvellous excellence of the *Critique of Judgment,*

[P03-093] generally neglected in favour of the Critiques of *Pure Reason and of

[P03-094] Practical Judgment*; *[[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]]* for drawing the attention of the

[P03-095] world to his obscure name and for reprinting his Latin thesis in which

[P03-096] the word *Æsthetic* occurs for the first time; and *[[friedrich-schleiermacher|Schleiermacher]]* for

[P03-097] the tributes paid to his neglected genius in the History of Æsthetic.

[P03-098] *La Critica,* too, is full of generous appreciation of contemporaries

[P03-099] by Croce and by that profound thinker, Gentile.

[P03-100] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-101] There can be no doubt of the great value of Croce's work as an

[P03-102] *educative influence,* and if we are to judge of a philosophical system

[P03-103] by its action on others, then we must place the *Philosophy of the

[P03-104] Spirit* very high. It may be said with perfect truth that since the

[P03-105] death of the poet Carducci there has been no influence in [[italy|Italy]] to

[P03-106] compare with that of Benedetto Croce.

[P03-107] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-108] Of the popularity that his system and teaching have already attained

[P03-109] we may judge by the fact that the *Æsthetic,* despite the difficulty of

[P03-110] the subject, is already in its third edition in Italy, where, owing to

[P03-111] its influence, philosophy sells better than fiction; while the [[france|French]]

[P03-112] and Germans, not to mention the Czechs, have long had translations

[P03-113] of the earlier editions. His *Logic* is on the point of appearing

[P03-114] in its second edition, and I have no doubt that the *Philosophy of

[P03-115] the Practical* will eventually equal these works in popularity. *The

[P03-116] importance and value of Italian thought have been too long neglected

[P03-117] in Great Britain.* Where, as in Benedetto Croce, we get the clarity

[P03-118] of vision of the Latin, joined to the thoroughness and erudition of

[P03-119] the best German tradition, we have a combination of rare power and

[P03-120] effectiveness, which can by no means be neglected.

[P03-121] The philosopher feels that he has a great mission, which is nothing

[P03-122] less than the leading back of thought to belief in the spirit, deserted

[P03-123] by so many for crude empiricism and positivism. His view of philosophy

[P03-124] is that it sums up all the higher human activities, including religion,

[P03-125] and that in proper hands it is able to solve any problem. But there is

[P03-126] no finality about problems: the solution of one leads to the posing

[P03-127] of another, and so on. Man is the maker of life, and his spirit ever

[P03-128] proceeds from a lower to a higher perfection.

[P03-129] . . . . . . . . . .

[P03-130] I believe that Croce will one day be recognized as one of the very

[P03-131] few great teachers of humanity. At present he is not appreciated at

[P03-132] nearly his full value. One rises from a study of his philosophy with

[P03-133] a sense of having been all the time as it were in personal touch with

[P03-134] the truth, which is very far from the case after the perusal of certain

[P03-135] other philosophies.

[P03-136] Secure in his strength, Croce will often introduce a joke or some

[P03-137] amusing illustration from contemporary life, in the midst of a most

[P03-138] profound and serious argument. This spirit of mirth is a sign of

[P03-139] superiority. He who is not sure of himself can spare no energy for

[P03-140] the making of mirth. Croce loves to laugh at his enemies and with

[P03-141] his friends. So the philosopher of Naples sits by the blue gulf

[P03-142] and explains the universe to those who have ears to hear. "One can

[P03-143] philosophize anywhere," he says—but he remains significantly at Naples.

[P03-144] Thus I conclude these brief remarks upon the author of the *Æsthetic,*

[P03-145] confident that those who give time and attention to its study will be

[P03-146] grateful for having placed in their hands this pearl of great price

[P03-147] from the diadem of the antique Parthenope.

[P03-148] DOUGLAS AINSLIE.

[P03-149] THE ATHENÆUM, PALL MALL,

[P03-150] *May* 1909.
