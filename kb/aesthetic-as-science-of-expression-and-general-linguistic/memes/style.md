# Style

In Croce's aesthetic system, style is not an external quality added to a work of art but is identical with form itself — the individual expressive act that constitutes the aesthetic fact.

## Definition

Style, properly understood, is synonymous with form. Croce identifies style with the expressive act: "if style is form, the code and the mathematical work is said to be without style" only insofar as the aesthetic quality of the expression is being judged (010-138).

The common saying "the style is the man" is true only "so far as he is expression" — that is, style is the man *qua* style, not the man in his totality (007-201).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Expression

Style occupies a central place in Croce's identification of intuition, expression, and art. Since every genuine intuition is also expression, and every expression possesses its own individual character, style is simply the individuating aspect of the expressive act. There is no expression without style, just as there is no form without content.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes three senses in which the word "style" is used:

1. **Style as form** — the correct sense. "Here style is synonymous with form" (010-134). Every successful expression has style because style *is* the form taken by the intuition.

2. **Style as ornament** — an erroneous sense. When critics blame someone for "having too much style" or "writing a style," they treat style as an improper and external addition to content (010-141, 010-142). This confusion arises from the false separation of form and content.

3. **"Style is the man"** — a saying that Croce partially endorses but refines: the man is present in style only insofar as he expresses himself aesthetically, not in his entire practical and moral personality (007-195, 007-200).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The identification of style with the individual character of expression has roots in classical rhetoric, but Croce's treatment is more radical. Unlike rhetorical theories that classify kinds of style (grand, middle, plain), Croce rejects the "theory of kinds of style" entirely (P02-357). For Croce, such classifications mistake external physical characteristics for the internal aesthetic fact.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's key innovation is to dissolve style as a separate aesthetic category. By identifying style with form and form with expression, he makes style constitutive of the aesthetic act rather than an attribute that can be added or removed. A work "without style" is, in Croce's system, simply a failed expression — not a work that lacks ornament.

## Criticism and Debate

> The same thing happens as regards the word style. Sometimes it is said that every writer must have style. Here style is synonymous with form (010-133, 010-134).

Critics have argued that Croce's identification of style with form collapses a useful distinction between the individual manner of an artist and the structural features of the work. However, within Croce's idealist framework, this collapse is intentional: style *is* the form, not a property of it.
