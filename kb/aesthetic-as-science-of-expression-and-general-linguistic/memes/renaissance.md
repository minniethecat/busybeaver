# Renaissance

The Renaissance (14th–16th centuries) was the cultural movement that revived classical antiquity and established the intellectual conditions for modern aesthetics. Croce traces the Renaissance contribution through figures like **Leonardo da Vinci**, **Michelangelo**, and the Italian humanists who elevated the artist from craftsman to creator.

## Significance for Æsthetic

The Renaissance transformed the status of art and artists. The medieval artisan worked within guilds and produced objects for use or worship; the Renaissance artist claimed the dignity of a liberal art. *Disegno* (drawing/design) was recognized as an intellectual activity, not merely a manual skill. **Giorgio Vasari**'s *Lives of the Artists* (1550) established the artist as a historical figure worthy of biography and the history of art as a narrative of progress.

The Renaissance also revived Platonic aesthetics, particularly through **Marsilio Ficino**'s translations and commentaries. The Neoplatonic theory of beauty as divine radiance, mediated through Ficino's Christian Platonism, shaped aesthetic discourse through the 17th century.
