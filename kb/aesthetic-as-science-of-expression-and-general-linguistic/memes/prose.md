# Prose

In Croce's aesthetic system, prose is not the opposite of poetry but a secondary and derivative form of expression. The distinction between poetry and prose is empirical, not philosophical.

## Definition

Croce follows the ancient insight that "whenever we speak, we create prose" — a statement attributed to Molière's character who is "astonished at having spoken prose" without knowing it (003-060, 003-061, 003-064). Prose is simply expression in its ordinary, unheightened form.

The key claim is that "poetry is the language of feeling, prose the language of the intellect" — but "since the intellect is also feeling," all prose "has its poetical side" (004-127, 004-129). There is no pure prose, just as there is no expression devoid of aesthetic quality.

## In Croce's System

### The Derivative Nature of Prose

Croce's most striking claim about prose is that "there is poetry without prose, but not prose without poetry" (004-136). Poetry is the primary form of expression; prose is poetry that has been adapted to practical and intellectual purposes.

### Key Distinctions

1. **Prose as technique** — Breaking text "into chapters, paragraphs, sentences" is a practical activity that serves the reproduction of expression (015-141). It is technique, not art.

2. **Prose and science** — Prose is the language of conceptual thought. But "false knowledge in excellent prose" may be "æsthetically most true" (006-195).

3. **Prose and expression** — Prose is not the absence of expression but expression in the service of practical ends. The "language of prose writers" has its own aesthetic quality (024-164).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The birth of prose has been a subject of speculation since antiquity. Vico placed "the birth of prose over that of verse" as a later development in the history of civilization (024-149). Humboldt "distinguishes poetry and prose" as fundamentally different uses of language (031-145).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to collapse the distinction. Prose is not a different kind of expression but expression in a different context. The difference between a scientific treatise and a sonnet is not in the spiritual activity but in the content and purpose of that expression.

## Criticism and Debate

> There is poetry without prose, but not prose without poetry (004-136).

If prose cannot exist without poetry, then the distinction between them is one of degree, not kind. This is Croce's central claim: prose is poetry adapted to practical ends.
