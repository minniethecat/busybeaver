# Parts of Speech

In Croce's philosophy of language, the traditional theory of parts of speech — the division of words into nouns, verbs, adjectives, and other grammatical categories — is a prime example of the confusion between logic and æsthetic, between concept and intuition. Croce subjects this theory to a radical critique that follows directly from his identification of language with expression.

## Definition

The parts of speech (grammatical kinds or grammatical forms) are the categories into which traditional grammar divides words: noun, verb, adjective, adverb, pronoun, preposition, conjunction, and interjection. These categories are presented as if they described real distinctions in the nature of words — as if a word *is* a noun or a verb in the same way that an animal *is* a mammal or a reptile. Croce argues that this is an illusion: the parts of speech are logical abstractions superimposed upon the living reality of linguistic expression.

## Croce's Critique

### The Identity of Grammar and Logic

Croce's radical thesis is that "the theory of the parts of speech is really identical with the theory of the categories of logic" (019-195). Noun corresponds to substance, verb to action or process, adjective to quality — the grammatical kinds are simply the logical categories in thin disguise. This follows from Croce's broader identity thesis: language is not a separate faculty but is identical with æsthetic expression. Since the æsthetic fact is pre-logical (it is pure intuition, not concept), the superimposition of logical categories upon language is a category mistake.

The problem is not merely terminological. "The theory of the parts of speech has caused the analogous error of the theory of the arts" (019-209) — just as grammarians divide language into fixed word-classes, so theorists of art divide artistic expression into fixed genres (tragedy, comedy, lyric, epic). Both errors stem from the same source: the substitution of abstract classification for the concrete individuality of the æsthetic fact.

### The Empirical Origin of Grammatical Kinds

Croce does not deny that the parts of speech have a practical origin. They arise, he acknowledges, from the observation of regularities in languages — and specifically in the classical languages (Greek and Latin) whose grammar furnished the original model. But these regularities are empirical generalizations, not necessary structures of language. "Even the abstractions of the parts of speech are in the last analysis empirical concepts" (019-268) — they summarize tendencies observed in particular languages at particular times, not universal laws of linguistic expression.

Evidence for this comes from the instability of the categories themselves. Different languages distribute grammatical functions differently; even within a single language, the same word can function now as noun, now as verb, now as adjective. The boundary between the parts of speech is fluid because it is not grounded in the nature of expression but in the practice of classification.

### The Special Case of the Interjection

Croce notes with characteristic irony that the interjection — the primitive cry of feeling — has been a persistent embarrassment to the theory of parts of speech. Some grammarians classify it as a part of speech; others exclude it on the grounds that it is not truly linguistic. Croce points out that "interjections are not parts of speech but are speech itself in its primordial form" (023-167). The interjection reveals what the theory of parts of speech conceals: that language is first of all expression, and that grammatical classification is a secondary, logical operation upon the primary æsthetic fact.

## Historical Development

In his bibliographical appendix, Croce traces "the history of grammar and parts of speech" (038-2534) through the Western tradition. The theory of eight parts of speech, canonized by Dionysius Thrax and transmitted through the Latin grammarians (Donatus, Priscian), dominated European linguistics until the nineteenth century. Croce notes that even after comparative philology had revealed the diversity of linguistic structures, "the old theory of the eight parts of speech was found lurking" (038-1565) in new guises.

The most significant modern development was the work of the Humboldtian tradition and the comparative grammarians (Pott, Paul, and others), who "treated the theory of parts of speech with greater philosophical depth" (038-1573) by connecting grammatical categories to psychological processes rather than logical ones. But Croce argues that this psychological turn, while an improvement, still misses the point: language is neither logical classification nor psychological process but æsthetic expression.

## Key Distinctions

**Words vs. Propositions.** Croce's theory implies that the true unit of language is not the word but the expressive proposition — the complete intuition-expression. Words are abstractions from propositions; the parts of speech are abstractions from words. Each level of abstraction takes us further from the living reality of language.

**Grammar vs. Art.** "The study of parts of speech and syntax is wholly different from the study of expression" (024-280). Grammar is a practical discipline — it formulates rules for the correct use of language. Art is expression, which has no rules in the grammatical sense. The grammarian classifies; the artist expresses.

**Linguistic Kinds vs. Artistic Kinds.** Croce explicitly parallels "the theory of grammatical kinds or parts of speech" (038-1518) with the theory of literary genres. Both are "empirical classifications, useful for practical purposes but without philosophical validity" (038-1520). Just as there are no real kinds of art (tragedy, comedy, epic are practical labels), there are no real kinds of words.

## Implications for Aesthetics

The critique of parts of speech is not a marginal philological digression in Croce's system but a direct consequence of his core doctrines: the identity of intuition and expression, the identity of language and art, and the absolute individuality of the æsthetic fact. If language is art — if every speech act is an æsthetic creation — then the logical analysis of language into fixed categories is as misguided as the logical analysis of a painting into fixed symbolic elements. In both cases, the abstracting intellect dissects what the æsthetic spirit has created as an indivisible whole.

## Criticism and Debate

Linguists have been almost uniformly hostile to Croce's theory of language. Modern linguistics, from Saussurean structuralism through Chomskyan generativism to cognitive linguistics, has developed sophisticated accounts of grammatical categories as psychologically real structures, not merely empirical labels. The discovery of universal grammatical constraints across unrelated languages challenges Croce's claim that parts of speech are merely conventional.

Defenders of Croce note that his target is not the empirical study of grammar but the philosophical claim that grammatical categories reveal the essence of language. One can accept the scientific validity of modern linguistics while maintaining, with Croce, that language as expression is logically prior to language as structure — that the creative, æsthetic dimension of speech is not reducible to grammatical analysis.
