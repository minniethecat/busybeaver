# Psychology

The empirical science of mental processes which, in Croce's system, is sharply distinguished from the philosophical science of spirit — psychology describes facts, whereas Æsthetic elucidates the necessary forms of spiritual activity (004-060, 004-061).

## Definition

Croce defines psychology as a naturalistic discipline that collects and classifies empirical data about mental life, but cannot ground the normative distinctions between true and false, beautiful and ugly. Psychology "would constrain us on the contrary to deny the existence of any qualitative difference between sensations" if taken as the foundation of Æsthetic (004-063). It belongs to the practical order of human activity, not to the theoretic sphere where intuition and logic reside (006-010).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

In the fourfold division of spirit — intuition, concept, economic action, ethical action — psychology occupies no autonomous place. Croce treats it as a hybrid discipline that confuses the practical with the theoretical. The Herbartian school attempted to base aesthetics on psychological laws of association and mental mechanics, an approach Croce considered a category error (031-237). Herbart's followers "divorced psychology from logic as a normative science," but Croce insists the divorce cannot hold: normative distinctions are philosophical, not empirical (031-239).

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a firm line between psychology and philosophical Æsthetic. Psychology studies how artists actually think and feel — "those who have best studied the psychology of artists" may describe creative processes (002-297) — but it cannot explain what makes an intuition beautiful or a concept true. Ancient psychology, as Croce notes, already treated the imagination as a faculty of the soul, yet failed to distinguish the aesthetic from the merely psychological (020-492). The "psychology of poets, 'poetic enthusiasm,'" belongs to biography, not to the science of expression (025-196).

> The confusion of Æsthetic with psychology is one of the most persistent errors in the history of the discipline. Croce writes that Æsthetic is constantly "confounded with psychology or asked to describe the emotions" of the artist — a demand as misguided as asking logic to describe the brain states of the reasoner (029-124).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The associationistic psychology that flourished in England — from Locke through Hartley to the Mills — treated mental life as a chain of atomistic impressions linked by contiguity and resemblance. Croce observes that this tradition "still flourished (and has not yet entirely ceased to flourish)" in modern thought, but it cannot account for the synthetic unity of the aesthetic intuition (033-064). In Germany, the Herbartian school developed an elaborate psychological aesthetics based on formal relations and mental mechanics, which Croce criticizes for reducing the spirit to a "psychology of God" — a theological surrogate that disguises its empirical inadequacy (032-067).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's decisive move is to remove psychology entirely from the foundations of Æsthetic. He replaces the psychological question "How does the mind produce art?" with the philosophical question "What is the logical place of art within the life of the spirit?" This is not a rejection of psychological facts but a recognition that they belong to a different order of inquiry — "substituting reflexion on the psychology of the author and history of culture" for genuine aesthetic judgment is a methodological error (034-149). Ethnopsychology (Völkerpsychologie), the late nineteenth-century attempt by Steinthal and Lazarus to explain art through collective mental development, receives particular scorn from Croce as a "non-existent science" (036-459).

## Criticism and Debate

> The rigor with which Croce separates psychology from philosophy has been questioned by later aestheticians who argue that empirical psychology — particularly the psychology of perception and emotion — provides essential data for any complete theory of art. Croce's response, implicit in the Æsthetic, is that such data may enrich the natural history of art but can never determine its philosophical definition (038-2187).
