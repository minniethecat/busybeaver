# Pantheism

Pantheism enters Croce's history of aesthetics not as a position he endorses but as a recurring temptation he diagnoses. The impulse to identify Beauty with the divine — to see in art the immanence of the absolute in the finite — runs through German aesthetics from Schelling to Volkelt, producing formulations of great rhetorical power but, in Croce's view, of negligible philosophical clarity. Pantheism in aesthetics is the sign of a category mistake: the confusion of aesthetic expression with religious intuition, of the making of form with the contemplation of the divine.

## Definition

Croce introduces the concept of pantheism obliquely, through description rather than definition. In surveying the philosophical landscape of early nineteenth-century Germany, he notes with characteristic irony the proliferation of abstract formulae: "The first half of the nineteenth century was for Germany a period of many fine-sounding philosophical formulæ: subjectivism, objectivism, subjective—objectivism; abstract, concrete, abstract-concrete; idealism, realism, idealism—realism; between pantheism and theism Krause inserted his pan-en-theism" (030-044). Pantheism here appears as one term in a conceptual bazaar, a label whose proliferation signals not intellectual fertility but conceptual inflation.

The more specific aesthetic meaning emerges in Croce's discussion of the attempts to construct a theistic aesthetics. Eckardt, "intent on creating a theistic Æsthetic which should avoid the one-sided transcendence of deism on the one hand and the one-sided immanence of pantheism on the other, maintained that its principles must be sought not in the feelings of the contemplator, not in works of art, not in the idea of the beautiful, not in the concept of art, but in the creative spirit of the artist, the original fount of beauty" (032-061). Eckardt's solution — to invoke "a psychology of God (*eine Psychologie des Weltkünstlers*)" (032-061) — illustrates for Croce the absurdity to which pantheistic aesthetics leads: unable to locate beauty in the human act of expression, it projects the creative act onto the deity.

## In Croce's System

### Pantheism as Category Mistake

The pantheistic impulse, in Croce's analysis, derives from a legitimate intuition — that art participates in something larger than individual psychology — that is expressed in the wrong philosophical idiom. The artist does indeed give form to the universal through the particular, but this universality is the universality of human spiritual activity, not the universality of the divine. Pantheism errs by converting a philosophical insight about the nature of spirit into a metaphysical claim about the nature of reality.

The error reaches its most extreme form in Theodor Vischer, whom Croce describes as "the bulkiest of all German æstheticians." Vischer's system, elaborated across four volumes, culminated in the claim that "Beauty the 'harmony of the universe,' never actually realized because realized only at infinity, so that when we think to seize it in the Beautiful, we are under an illusion: a transcendent illusion, which is the very essence of the æsthetic fact" (037-028). For Croce, this is pantheism in its terminal phase — Beauty so thoroughly identified with the cosmos that it ceases to be an aesthetic concept at all.

### Key Distinctions

The principal distinction is between **pantheism and Croce's philosophy of spirit**. In Croce's system, art is intuition-expression — a primary, autonomous activity of the human spirit. It neither needs nor admits of theological grounding. The beauty of a poem is not a reflection of divine beauty; it is the achieved form of an individual intuition. To describe this achievement in pantheistic language is to obscure its specifically aesthetic character.

A second distinction opposes **pantheism to naturalism**. The naturalistic tendency in aesthetics, represented by the Herbartians and their physiological reductionism, treats aesthetic pleasure as a natural fact to be investigated by empirical methods. Pantheism appears, in this context, as naturalism's metaphysical mirror image: both reduce art to something other than itself, the one by making it a natural process, the other by making it a divine epiphany. Croce's position cuts between both reductions: art is spiritual activity, neither natural nor supernatural.

The later development of aesthetic pantheism is captured in Croce's brief but pointed discussion of Volkelt, who "when treating of the Symbol and joining symbolism to pantheism, opposed associationism and favoured a natural teleology immanent in Beauty" (037-038). The phrase "natural teleology immanent in Beauty" condenses the pantheistic position: Beauty is not a human achievement but a cosmic tendency, and art is the medium through which this tendency becomes conscious of itself. For Croce, this is poetry in the guise of philosophy.

## Criticism and Debate

> In the midst of this uproar, in which the second-rate men shouted down the first-rate and made good their claim to their only true property, namely words, it is not surprising that a few modest clear thinkers, philosophers who preferred to think about realities, should have the worst of it and remain unheard and unnoticed, lost among the roaring crowd or labelled with a false ticket. (030-044)

## Historical Context

Aesthetic pantheism flourished in the wake of German Idealism, particularly in the systems that followed Schelling's identification of art as the organ of philosophy. Schelling's *System of Transcendental Idealism* (1800) had declared that "art is the only true and eternal organ and document of philosophy" — a formulation that, while not pantheistic in the strict sense, opened the door to the divinization of art. Hegel's more sober system, which subordinated art to religion and philosophy in the dialectic of absolute spirit, represented a partial correction, but the pantheistic impulse survived in the epigoni and in the diffuse romantic sensibility of the nineteenth century.

Croce's Italian perspective gives him critical distance from this German tradition. The Italian aesthetic tradition, from Vico through De Sanctis, had consistently grounded art in human creativity rather than in cosmic process. Croce's anti-pantheism is thus not merely a philosophical preference but a recovery of the Vichian insight that poetry is made by humans, for humans, and is intelligible in purely human terms.
