# Intellect

The faculty of conceptual knowledge that elaborates the data of intuition into logical relations and universal concepts; in Croce's system, intellect is the second theoretical form of spirit, dependent upon yet distinct from the aesthetic-intuitive moment (002-004, P02-026).

## Definition

Croce defines intellect as the organ of conceptual knowledge — knowledge of the universal, the relational, and the abstract — in contrast to intuition, which grasps the particular and the concrete. "Knowledge obtained through the intellect" (002-004) constitutes the domain of logic and science, whereas aesthetic knowledge proceeds through the direct formation of images. Croce insists on both the inseparability and the distinction: intellectual knowledge cannot operate "without the light of intellectual knowledge" supplied by intuition, yet intuition retains its "independence with respect to intellectual knowledge" (002-031).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

In Croce's fourfold philosophy of spirit, intellect occupies the second position within the theoretical sphere. The sequence runs: (1) aesthetic intuition — knowledge of the particular through images; (2) intellectual conception — knowledge of the universal through concepts. Croce's famous dictum — "Intuition is blind; intellect lends her eyes" (002-030) — captures the dependency: intuition provides the concrete content without which concepts are empty, but intellect alone supplies the relational structure that makes systematic knowledge possible. Yet Croce warns against reducing intuition to an intellectual function, calling this error "intellectualism" (P02-135).

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a firm line between **intellectual truth** and **artistic truth**. The former seeks correspondence between concept and reality in logical terms; the latter is the expressive adequacy of the image to the intuition. They operate by different criteria: "Artistic truth and intellectual truth" differ fundamentally, and confusing the two — judging art by intellectual standards — constitutes a category error (P02-256). Croce also distinguishes **wit** from intellect: wit and intellectual judgment belong to different orders, with wit pertaining to the rapid association and recombination of images rather than to conceptual reasoning (P02-184, P02-185). Poetry and history, for Croce, are products of imagination, not intellect (P02-201), though both may engage intellectual elements after the primary aesthetic act.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The distinction between intuitive and intellectual cognition has deep roots in Western philosophy, from Plato's division of knowledge into opinion (doxa) and understanding (noesis), through Kant's separation of sensibility and understanding, to Hegel's dialectical integration of the two. Croce drew particularly on Vico's earlier critique of Cartesian rationalism and on the German idealist tradition's treatment of intellectual intuition.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's contribution was to give the aesthetic-intuitive moment not merely equal standing with the intellectual, but logical priority. Against the dominant intellectualism of nineteenth-century philosophy — which treated art as an inferior or confused form of conceptual thought — Croce argued that intuition is the foundational cognitive act, without which intellect has no material to work upon. Language, for Croce, is not an intellectual construction but "activity" (P02-265), a form of expression co-extensive with the aesthetic itself.

## Criticism and Debate

> The Crocean separation of intuition and intellect has been criticized as overly schematic, particularly by later hermeneutic philosophers who see understanding as always already conceptually mediated. Within Croce's own framework, however, the distinction is necessary to protect the autonomy of art from the encroachment of philosophy and science — a position grounded in Croce's insistence that intuition retains its "independence with respect to intellectual knowledge" (002-031).
