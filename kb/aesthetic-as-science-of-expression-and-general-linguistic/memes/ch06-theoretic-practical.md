[006-001] The better to confirm these criticisms, it will be useful to cast a

[006-002] rapid glance over analogous and opposite errors, due to ignorance as

[006-003] to the true nature of art and its relation to history and to science.

[006-004] These errors have injured alike the theory of history and that of

[006-005] science, Historic (or Historiology) and Logic.

[006-006] *Criticism of the philosophy of history.*

[006-007] Historical intellectualism has opened the way to the many attempts,

[006-008] made especially during the last two centuries and continued to-day, to

[006-009] discover *a philosophy of history,* an *ideal history,* a *sociology,*

[006-010] a *historical psychology,* or whatever else a science may be called,

[006-011] whose object is to extract from history concepts and universal laws.

[006-012] What must these laws, these universals be? Historical laws and

[006-013] historical concepts? In that case, an elementary acquaintance with

[006-014] the theory of knowledge suffices to make clear the absurdity of the

[006-015] attempt. When such expressions as a *historical law,* a *historical

[006-016] concept* are not simply metaphors colloquially employed, they are truly

[006-017] contradictory terms: the adjective is as unsuitable to the substantive

[006-018] as in the expressions "qualitative quantity" or "pluralistic monism."

[006-019] History implies concreteness and individuality, law and concept

[006-020] mean abstractness and universality. But if the attempt to extract

[006-021] *historical* laws and concepts from history be abandoned, and it be

[006-022] merely desired to draw from it laws and concepts, the attempt is

[006-023] certainly not frivolous; but the science thus obtained will be, not

[006-024] a philosophy of history, but rather, according to circumstances,

[006-025] either philosophy in its various forms of Ethics, Logic, etc., or

[006-026] empirical science with its infinite divisions and subdivisions. The

[006-027] search is in fact either for those philosophical concepts which, as

[006-028] already remarked, are the basis of every historical construction and

[006-029] differentiate perception from intuition, historical intuition from pure

[006-030] intuition, history from art; or already formed historical intuitions

[006-031] are collected and arranged in types and classes, which is exactly the

[006-032] method of the natural sciences. Great thinkers have sometimes donned

[006-033] the ill-fitting cloak of the philosophy of history, and notwithstanding

[006-034] the covering, they have attained philosophical truths of the greatest

[006-035] magnitude. The cloak discarded, the truth has remained. Modern

[006-036] sociologists are rather to be blamed, not so much for the illusion

[006-037] in which they are involved when they talk of an impossible science

[006-038] of sociology, as for the infecundity which almost always accompanies

[006-039] their illusion. It matters little that Æsthetic should be called

[006-040] "sociological Æsthetic," or Logic, "sociological Logic." The grave evil

[006-041] is that such Æsthetic is an old-fashioned expression of sensationalism,

[006-042] such Logic verbal and incoherent. The philosophical movement to which

[006-043] we have referred has however borne two good fruits in relation to

[006-044] history. First of all, a keener desire has arisen for a theory of

[006-045] history, that is, a theory of the nature and the limits of history, a

[006-046] theory which, in conformity with the analysis made above, cannot obtain

[006-047] satisfaction save in a general science of intuition, in an Æsthetic, in

[006-048] which the theory of history would form a special chapter, distinguished

[006-049] by the insertion of universal functions. Furthermore, concrete truths

[006-050] relating to historical events have often been expressed beneath the

[006-051] false and presumptuous cloak of a philosophy of history; rules and

[006-052] warnings have been formulated, empirical no doubt, yet by no means

[006-053] useless to students and critics. It does not seem possible to deny this

[006-054] utility even to the most recent of philosophies of history, known as

[006-055] historical materialism, which has thrown a very vivid light upon many

[006-056] sides of social life formerly neglected or ill understood.

[006-057] *Æsthetic intrusions into Logic.*

[006-058] The principle of authority, of the *ipse dixit*, is an intrusion

[006-059] by historicity into the domains of science and philosophy which

[006-060] has dominated the schools and substitutes for introspection

[006-061] and philosophical analysis this or that evidence, document, or

[006-062] authoritative statement, with which history certainly cannot dispense.

[006-063] But Logic, the science of thought and of intellectual knowledge, has

[006-064] suffered the most grave and destructive of all disturbances and errors

[006-065] through an imperfect understanding of the æsthetic fact. How could it

[006-066] be otherwise, if logical activity come after and contain in itself

[006-067] æsthetic activity? An inexact Æsthetic must of necessity drag after it

[006-068] an inexact Logic.

[006-069] Whoever opens a logical treatise, from the *Organon* of Aristotle

[006-070] to the modern works on the subject, must agree that all contain a

[006-071] haphazard mixture of verbal facts and facts of thought, of grammatical

[006-072] forms and of conceptual forms, of Æsthetic and of Logic. Not that

[006-073] attempts have been wanting to escape from verbal expression and to

[006-074] seize thought in its true nature. Aristotelian logic itself did not

[006-075] become mere syllogistic and verbalism without some hesitation and

[006-076] indecision. The problem proper to logic was often touched upon in

[006-077] their disputes by the nominalists, realists and conceptualists of the

[006-078] Middle Ages. With Galileo and with Bacon, the natural sciences gave an

[006-079] honourable place to induction. [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] combated formalist and mathematical

[006-080] logic in favour of inventive methods. [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] called attention to the *a

[006-081] priori* synthesis. Absolute idealism despised the Aristotelian Logic.

[006-082] The followers of [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]], though still loyal to Aristotle, emphasized

[006-083] those judgements which they called narrative and which have a character

[006-084] altogether differing from that of other logical judgements. Finally,

[006-085] the linguists insisted upon the irrationality of the word, in relation

[006-086] to the concept. But a conscious, sure and radical movement of reform

[006-087] can find no basis or point of departure, save in the science of

[006-088] Æsthetic.

[006-089] *Logic in its essence.*

[006-090] In a Logic suitably reformed on this basis, this truth must first and

[006-091] foremost be proclaimed, and all its consequences deduced: the logical

[006-092] fact, *the only logical fact,* is *the concept,* the universal, the

[006-093] spirit that forms, and in so far as it forms, the universal. And if

[006-094] by induction be understood, as sometimes it has been, the formation

[006-095] of universals, and by deduction their verbal development, then it is

[006-096] clear that true Logic can be nothing but inductive Logic. But since by

[006-097] the word "deduction" has been more frequently understood the special

[006-098] processes of mathematics, and the word "induction" those of the natural

[006-099] sciences, it will be best to avoid both words and say that true Logic

[006-100] is Logic of the concept. The Logic of the concept, while employing

[006-101] a method which is both induction and deduction, will employ neither

[006-102] exclusively, that is, it will employ the speculative method which is

[006-103] intrinsic to it.

[006-104] The concept, the universal, considered abstractly in itself, is

[006-105] *inexpressible.* No word is proper to it. So true is this, that the

[006-106] logical concept remains always the same, notwithstanding the variation

[006-107] of verbal forms. In respect to the concept, expression is a simple

[006-108] *sign* or *indication.* There must be an expression, it cannot be

[006-109] absent; but what it is to be, this or that, is determined by the

[006-110] historical and psychological conditions of the individual who is

[006-111] speaking. The quality of the expression is not deducible from the

[006-112] nature of the concept. There does not exist a true (logical) sense of

[006-113] words. The true sense of words is that which is conferred upon them on

[006-114] each occasion by the person forming a concept.

[006-115] *Distinction between logical and non-logical judgements.*

[006-116] This being so, the only truly logical (that is, æsthetico-logical)

[006-117] propositions, the only rigorously logical judgements, must be those

[006-118] whose proper and sole content is the determination of a concept. These

[006-119] propositions or judgements are *definitions.* Science itself is nothing

[006-120] but a collection of definitions, unified in a supreme definition; a

[006-121] system of concepts, or highest concept.

[006-122] It is therefore necessary (at least as a preliminary) to exclude

[006-123] from Logic all those propositions which do not affirm universals.

[006-124] Narrative judgements, not less than those termed non-enunciative by

[006-125] Aristotle, such as the expression of desires, are not properly logical

[006-126] judgements. They are either purely æsthetic propositions or historical

[006-127] propositions. "Peter is passing; it is raining to-day; I am sleepy; I

[006-128] want to read": these and an infinity of propositions of the same kind

[006-129] are nothing but either a mere enclosing in words the impression of

[006-130] the fact that Peter is passing, of the falling rain, of my organism

[006-131] inclining to sleep, and of my will directed to reading, or an

[006-132] existential affirmation concerning those facts. They are expressions of

[006-133] the real or of the unreal, historical-imaginative or pure-imaginative;

[006-134] they are certainly not definitions of universals.

[006-135] *Syllogistic.*

[006-136] This exclusion cannot meet with great difficulties. It is already

[006-137] almost an accomplished fact, and the only thing required is to render

[006-138] it explicit, decisive and coherent. But what is to be done with

[006-139] all that part of human thought called *syllogistic,* consisting of

[006-140] judgements and reasonings based upon concepts? What is syllogistic?

[006-141] Is it to be looked down upon with contempt, as something useless, as

[006-142] has so often been done by the humanists in their reaction against

[006-143] scholasticism, by absolute idealism, by the enthusiastic admiration of

[006-144] our times for the methods of observation and experiment of the natural

[006-145] sciences?—Syllogistic, reasonings *forma,* is not the discovery of

[006-146] truth; it is the art of expounding, debating, disputing with oneself

[006-147] and others. Proceeding from concepts already formed, from facts already

[006-148] observed, and appealing to the persistence of the true or of thought

[006-149] (such is the meaning of the laws of identity and contradiction), it

[006-150] infers consequences from those data, that is, it re-states what has

[006-151] already been discovered. Therefore, if it be an *idem per idem* from

[006-152] the point of view of invention, it is most efficacious in teaching and

[006-153] in exposition. To reduce affirmations to a syllogistic form is a way of

[006-154] controlling one's own thought and of criticizing the thought of others.

[006-155] It is easy to laugh at syllogizers, but, if syllogistic has been born

[006-156] and persists, it must have good reasons of its own. Satire on it can

[006-157] concern only its abuses, such as the attempt to prove syllogistically

[006-158] questions of fact, observation and intuition, or the neglect of

[006-159] profound meditation and unprejudiced investigation of problems, in

[006-160] favour of syllogistic externality. And if so-called *mathematical

[006-161] Logic* can sometimes aid us in our attempt to remember with ease,

[006-162] rapidly to control the results of our own thought, let us welcome this

[006-163] form of syllogistic also, anticipated by Leibnitz among others and

[006-164] again attempted by some in our own days.

[006-165] But precisely because syllogistic is the art of exposition and debate,

[006-166] its theory cannot hold the first place in a philosophical Logic, thus

[006-167] usurping that belonging to the doctrine of the concept, which is

[006-168] the central and dominating doctrine, to which everything logical in

[006-169] syllogistic is reducible, without leaving a residuum (relations of

[006-170] concepts, subordination, co-ordination, identification and so on). Nor

[006-171] must it ever be forgotten that concept and (logical) judgement and

[006-172] syllogism are not in the same line. The first alone is the logical

[006-173] fact, the second and third are the forms in which the first manifests

[006-174] itself. These, in so far as they are forms, can only be examined

[006-175] æsthetically (grammatically), and in so far as they possess logical

[006-176] content, only by ignoring the forms themselves and passing to the

[006-177] doctrine of the concept.

[006-178] *Logical falsehood and æsthetic truth.*

[006-179] This confirms the truth of the ordinary remark to the effect that

[006-180] he who reasons ill, also speaks and writes ill, that exact logical

[006-181] analysis is the basis of good expression. This truth is a tautology,

[006-182] for to reason well is in fact to express oneself well, because the

[006-183] expression is the intuitive possession of one's own logical thought.

[006-184] The principle of contradiction itself is at bottom nothing but

[006-185] the æsthetic principle of coherence. It may be maintained that it

[006-186] is possible to write and to speak exceedingly well, as it is also

[006-187] possible to reason well though starting from erroneous concepts; that

[006-188] some, though lacking the acuteness that makes a great discoverer,

[006-189] are nevertheless exceedingly lucid writers; because to write well

[006-190] depends upon having a clear intuition of one's own thought, even if

[006-191] it be erroneous; not of its scientific, but of its æsthetic truth,

[006-192] which indeed is the same thing as writing well. A philosopher like

[006-193] [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] can imagine that art is a representation of the Platonic

[006-194] ideas. This doctrine is scientifically false, yet he may develop this

[006-195] false knowledge in excellent prose, æsthetically most true. But we

[006-196] have already replied to these objections, when observing that at that

[006-197] precise point where a speaker or a writer enunciates an ill-thought

[006-198] concept, he is at the same time a bad speaker and a bad writer,

[006-199] although he may afterwards recover himself in the many other parts

[006-200] of his thought which contain true propositions not connected with

[006-201] the preceding error, and therefore lucid expressions following upon

[006-202] confused expressions.

[006-203] *Reformed logic.*

[006-204] All researches as to the forms of judgements and of syllogisms, their

[006-205] conversions and their various relations, which still encumber treatises

[006-206] on Logic, are therefore destined to diminish, to be transformed, to be

[006-207] converted into something else. The doctrine of the concept and of the

[006-208] organism of concepts, of definition, of system, of philosophy and the

[006-209] various sciences, and the like, will occupy the field and alone will

[006-210] constitute true and proper Logic.

[006-211] Those who first had some suspicion of the intimate connexion between

[006-212] Æsthetic and Logic and conceived Æsthetic as a *Logic of sensible

[006-213] knowledge* were peculiarly addicted to applying logical categories to

[006-214] the new knowledge, talking of *æsthetic concepts, æsthetic judgements,

[006-215] æsthetic syllogisms,* and so on. We who are less superstitious as

[006-216] regards the permanence of the traditional Logic of the schools,

[006-217] and better informed as to the nature of Æsthetic, do not recommend

[006-218] the application of Logic to Æsthetic, but the liberation of Logic

[006-219] from æsthetic forms. These have given rise to non-existent forms or

[006-220] categories of Logic, due to the adoption of altogether arbitrary and

[006-221] ill-considered distinctions.

[006-222] Logic thus reformed will still be *formal* Logic; it will study the

[006-223] true form or activity of thought, the concept, excluding individual

[006-224] and particular concepts. The old Logic is ill called formal; it would

[006-225] be better to call it *verbal* or *formalistic.* Formal Logic will drive

[006-226] out formalistic Logic. To attain this object, it will not be necessary

[006-227] to have recourse, as some have done, to a real or material Logic,

[006-228] which is no longer a science of thought, but thought itself in action;

[006-229] not only a Logic, but the whole of Philosophy, in which Logic is also

[006-230] included. The science of thought (Logic) is that of the concept, as

[006-231] that of imagination (Æsthetic) is that of expression. The well-being

[006-232] of both sciences lies in exactly carrying out in every particular the

[006-233] distinction between the two domains.
