# Representation

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, representation (*rappresentazione*) is a term whose ambiguity reveals the very confusions that Croce's system is designed to resolve. It straddles the boundary between intuition and concept.

## Definition

"'Representation' is a very equivocal word" (002-217) — Croce's assessment captures the problem. In common usage, representation can mean either a mental image (intuition) or a general idea (concept). Some philosophers treat representation as the generic form of all mental content, subsuming both images and concepts. Croce argues that this conflation obscures the fundamental distinction between the two forms of theoretical knowledge.

Within Croce's own system, representation is sometimes used as a synonym for intuition — "Intuition and representation" (P02-011, 002-212) — but the term is ultimately supplanted by the more precise "intuition" and "expression."

## In Croce's System

### Representation as Intuition

When Croce uses representation positively, he identifies it with intuition: "the representation or image" (002-215) is the product of the aesthetic activity. "What is the difference between their representation or image and our intuitive knowledge? Everything and nothing" (002-216–002-217). The "nothing" is because both are intuitions; the "everything" is because the artistic representation is a fully achieved expression, while ordinary representations may be partial or confused.

### The Ambiguity Problem

The term representation is prone to ambiguity because it suggests a "standing-for" relation — a representation is always a representation *of* something. This invites the intellectualist error of treating the image as a sign that points to a concept. Croce's insistence that intuition is autonomous and self-sufficient eliminates the need for representation as a separate category: an intuition is not a representation of something else; it is itself the primary cognitive act.

## Key Distinctions

**Representation vs Intuition.** In Croce's mature system, intuition is the preferred term because it emphasizes the active, forming character of the act. Representation suggests passivity (something represented *to* consciousness) and referentiality (representation *of* something beyond itself).

**Representation vs Concept.** The traditional philosophical use of representation as covering both images and abstract ideas conflates what Croce separates. A concept represents a universal; an intuition is an individual image. The word representation blurs this distinction.

**Representation vs Expression.** Representation is a pre-Crocean term for mental imagery; expression is Croce's term for the formed, determinate image. Every expression is a representation in the older sense, but not every representation (as traditionally understood) is an expression in Croce's sense — because some purported representations are confused, unformed, or merely physiological.

## Historical Context

The concept of representation has a long history from **Aristotle**'s *phantasia* through **Descartes**' "ideas" to **Kant**'s *Vorstellung*. German Idealism made representation (*Vorstellung*) a central category. Croce's contribution to this tradition is to split representation into two distinct acts — intuition and concept — and to eliminate the ambiguous middle term. This clarifies the structure of knowledge at the cost of abandoning a familiar philosophical vocabulary.
