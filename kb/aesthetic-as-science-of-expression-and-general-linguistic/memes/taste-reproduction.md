# Taste and Reproduction

Taste and reproduction are paired concepts in Croce's aesthetic theory. Taste is the critical or receptive side of the aesthetic activity; reproduction is the process by which an aesthetic fact is re-created in another mind through physical stimuli. Croce's key claim is that taste and genius — criticism and production — are substantially identical.

## Definition

Croce defines taste as the capacity to judge the beautiful, and reproduction as the process by which the beautiful is re-awakened through external means. The two are intimately connected: taste is the activity of judgment, and reproduction is the process of re-experiencing the judged object. Together they form the receptive side of the aesthetic fact.

The crucial identity that Croce establishes is that "taste and genius are therefore substantially identical" (017-078). The activity that produces art and the activity that judges it are the same theoretic activity — the only difference lies in the circumstances of their exercise.

## In Croce's System

### The Identity of Taste and Genius

Croce's argument for the identity of taste and genius proceeds from the unity of the spirit:

1. **Production is judgment**. The artist, in creating, is constantly judging — selecting, refining, rejecting. This is already taste at work (017-071).

2. **Judgment is reproduction**. The critic, in judging, must reproduce the artist's intuition within his own spirit. "The productive activity is called genius; the receptive activity is called taste" — but both are the same spiritual function (017-077).

3. **Genius without taste is impossible**. When people speak of "geniuses without taste who produce works of genius in their primary parts and something of inferior quality in their secondary parts," they are describing a failure of sustained expression, not the absence of taste (017-086, 017-088).

### The Process of Reproduction

Reproduction is the process by which an aesthetic fact is re-created. Physical objects serve as "stimulants of æsthetic reproduction" (014-108). The process follows a definite sequence: "the reproduction of the intuitions produced by the artist" is set in motion by physical stimuli (014-113), and these stimuli — "the stimulants of æsthetic reproduction — are called beautiful things" (014-118, 014-124).

The distinction between natural and artificial beauty is, for Croce, a distinction in the means of reproduction: "Both serve the reproduction of the beautiful, but the former are given by nature, the latter produced by man" (014-227).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes the reproductive from the productive moment without separating them. There is no "passive (reproductive) taste" that is genuinely distinct from active genius — these are "empirical distinctions" that have no philosophical basis (017-082). The only real distinction is between the successful reproduction (which is taste) and the unsuccessful one (which is the absence of taste).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The problem of taste dominated eighteenth-century aesthetics. From Addison and Hume to Kant, philosophers sought to establish the grounds of aesthetic judgment. "What are we doing when we judge a work of art beautiful?" became the central question. The saying that "good taste always recognized" what was beautiful became a commonplace, but the philosophical foundations remained elusive (005-188, 005-189).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to dissolve the problem of taste into the problem of expression. There is no special faculty of taste, just as there is no special faculty of genius. Both are the single theoretic activity of the spirit, viewed now from the side of production, now from the side of reproduction. This move eliminates the need for a separate "faculty of judgment" and integrates criticism into the unified life of the spirit.

## Criticism and Debate

> The identity of taste and genius has been one of the most contested of Croce's claims in aesthetics (017-071).

Critics have argued that Croce's identity thesis blurs a genuine distinction: the artist produces, the critic judges, and these are different activities with different criteria of success. A great critic need not be a great artist, and vice versa. Croce's response is that the distinction is practical and empirical, not philosophical — at the level of spiritual activity, both are intuition-expression.
