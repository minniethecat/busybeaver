# Truth

In Croce's aesthetic philosophy, truth (*verità*) is not a single undifferentiated concept but must be distinguished into two forms: artistic truth and intellectual truth. This distinction is fundamental to Croce's defence of art's cognitive autonomy from philosophy and science.

## Definition

Croce draws a sharp line between "artistic truth and intellectual truth" (P02-256). Artistic truth is the truth of expression — the adequacy of an image to the feeling it embodies. Intellectual truth, by contrast, is the truth of propositions — the correspondence of a judgment to reality. As Croce puts it, a work of art "does not tell the truth" in the logical sense, "it does not belong to the world of fact" (003-177); yet it possesses its own kind of truth, which is the sincerity and completeness of expression.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Truth divides along the fundamental theoretical/practical and intuitive/logical axes in Croce's system:

| Form of Spirit | Type of Truth | Criterion |
|---------------|---------------|-----------|
| Intuition (aesthetic) | Artistic truth | Expressive adequacy |
| Logic (concept) | Intellectual truth | Logical correspondence |
| Economic (useful) | Practical success | Utility |
| Moral (good) | Ethical rightness | Universal law |

### Key Distinctions

Croce insists that "beauty and truth" are distinct values corresponding to distinct spiritual activities (P03-077). However, he also acknowledges that "it may be said with perfect truth that since the" dawn of philosophy the relationship between the two has been a central problem (P03-104). The key distinction is that artistic truth is individual (this image, this intuition), while intellectual truth is universal (this concept, this relation).

> "Space and time are not forms of intuition, but of conceptual construction" — Croce argues in his critique of Kant (002-107). Artistic truth does not require correspondence to spatio-temporal reality; it operates in the pure space of imagination. This is why a poem can be "true" even if it describes events that never happened.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The relation between poetry and truth was a central problem for Plato, who banished poets from his Republic for telling lies about the gods. Aristotle's *Poetics* offered the classical counterpoint: poetry is more philosophical than history because it deals with universals. Hegel's *Lectures on Æsthetics* developed the identity-in-difference of art, religion, and philosophy as three forms of absolute spirit.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce generalizes the Aristotelian defence into a full system: art has its own truth entirely independent of logical verification. Aesthetic truth is internal — it is the successful expression of an intuition, not the accurate representation of an external object. This allows Croce to defend the "truth" of non-representational and lyrical art, which would fail by any correspondence criterion yet succeed as authentic expressions.

## Criticism and Debate

> The concept of "artistic truth" has been criticized as either trivial (everything successfully expressed is "true") or an equivocation on the word "truth." Croce's distinction responds that artistic and intellectual truth are analogical, not univocal — they share the form of "adequacy" (of expression to feeling, of judgment to fact) but differ in their material domains.
