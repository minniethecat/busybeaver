# Speculation

The intellectual activity by which the spirit seeks conceptual truth, which Croce positions as the defining work of the second grade of theoretic activity — distinct from and irreducible to the intuitive-expressive operation of the first grade. Speculation is not a method among others but the native mode of the concept itself: it is the spirit thinking universally (006-102, 004-151).

## Definition

Croce identifies speculation with the life of the concept — the domain in which the spirit rises above individual intuitions to grasp universals. "The whole speculative life of man is spent in passing from one to the other and back again" — that is, oscillating between intuition (the knowledge of particulars) and concept (the knowledge of universals), the two and only two forms of theoretic cognition (004-151). The Logic of the concept, Croce insists, "will employ the speculative method which is intrinsic to it" — a method that is neither exclusively inductive nor exclusively deductive, but the proper movement of thought as it distinguishes and relates concepts (006-102).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Speculation belongs to the second degree of theoretic activity — conceptual thought — and must be rigorously distinguished from the first degree, aesthetic intuition. Croce accuses the Hegelian tradition of collapsing this distinction: "Absolute idealism despised the Aristotelian Logic" and with it the boundary between intuition and concept, allowing speculative thought to overrun the autonomy of art. For Croce, the speculative life presupposes intuition — there can be no concepts without prior intuitions — but intuition does not require speculation to be complete (006-081).

In the practical sphere, art and speculation share a formal independence from moral constraint: "In respect to morality, art is free just as philosophical speculation is free: its essence excludes practical and moral effects" (030-273). Both are theoretic activities, and neither can be judged by the standards of the practical spirit. Yet their resemblance ends there. "The tendencies of speculation and of poetry are always contrary, even in their use of language; the former tends to make language approximate to mathematical formulae; the latter to imagery" (030-309). Speculation seeks the abstract and universal; poetry, the individual and image-bound — and each bends language to its own ends.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes philosophical speculation from two inferior forms of intellectual activity. The first is empiricism — the mere collection and classification of data without the grasp of universal concepts. Grosse's project of a sociological aesthetics, for instance, "cannot hide the nature of the plan he is forced to adopt, or its striking resemblance to the despised speculative Aesthetic" — he claims to proceed empirically, but the general ideas he deploys are "quarried not from study of the reports brought back by travellers in savage lands, but from speculation on the forms of the spirit" (036-298, 036-315). The second is the "old speculative method" of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — pre-critical speculation that constructs systems without examining the conditions of knowledge itself (036-241).

Within genuine speculation, Croce further distinguishes the speculative principle from its adequate development. Schelling, for instance, "detects for the first time a speculative principle not adequately developed, since Schelling had never triumphed dialectically over the difficulties of intellectual intuition" (028-403). A speculative principle is a genuine insight into the structure of the spirit; its development requires the patient working-out of consequences — a task that falls to the true speculative genius.

> "The whole speculative life of man is spent in passing from one to the other and back again" (004-151). Expression and concept exhaust the cognitive spirit: there is no third form of theoretic knowledge, and speculation is simply the name for the spirit's activity when it thinks in universals rather than intuitions.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The tradition of aesthetic speculation begins with Greek antiquity, where "the speculations upon language had a close connexion with those upon the nature of art begun by the sophists" (020-541). Language presented itself as a problem — how can sounds signify colours or inaudible things? — and this linguistic wonder fed directly into the earliest philosophical accounts of artistic making.

In the medieval period, "speculations continued to recede further and further from the consideration of art" — Plotinus had connected beauty to the contemplation of the divine, but his successors reduced aesthetic speculation to "the empty definitions of the beautiful by Cicero and other ancient writers" (021-011, 021-013). The eighteenth century saw a revival: Antonio Conti "kept himself at the highest level of aesthetic speculation in contemporary Europe (Vico always excepted)" (025-139), and "Linguistic speculation at the beginning of the nineteenth century" — particularly the work of Wilhelm von Humboldt — opened new pathways by treating language as activity rather than product (031-011).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's contribution is to assign speculation its proper location within the philosophy of spirit. Before Croce, "speculative Aesthetic" was often a term of abuse — a label applied by naturalists and positivists to any aesthetic theory that proceeded from philosophical principles rather than empirical observation. Croce accepts the label but transforms its meaning: speculative aesthetics is not a rival to empirical aesthetics but its necessary foundation. The revival of philosophical speculation in nineteenth-century Italy — the work of Galluppi, Rosmini, and Gioberti — gave "inadequate treatment" to aesthetics precisely because it failed to recognize the autonomy of the intuitive moment (033-126).

Francesco De Sanctis embodies the tension between speculative rigour and concrete engagement: "his natural bent towards speculation led him to investigate grammatical and rhetorical doctrines with the view of reducing them to a system," yet "the speculative tendency shared his attention with many other lively interests" — the desire to understand the concrete, to enjoy art and rewrite its history, to plunge into practical and political life (034-009, 034-279). His aesthetic ideas "remained the mere sketch of a system never properly interrelated and deduced" — a cautionary instance of speculation left incomplete (034-279).
