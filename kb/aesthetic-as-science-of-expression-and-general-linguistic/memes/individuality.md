# Individuality

The defining character of aesthetic intuition in Croce's philosophy: every genuine intuition is an individual, unrepeatable formation that cannot be subsumed under a universal concept or classified without loss of its essential nature (018-261, 018-262, 010-009).

## Definition

For Croce, individuality is not a secondary property of aesthetic expression but its essence. "Intuition is individuality" (018-261), and because each intuition is a concrete, individual whole, "individuality does not repeat itself" (018-262). This principle grounds Croce's separation of art from science: where science seeks the universal and the repeatable, art produces the irreducibly singular. The individuality of the aesthetic form stands opposed to the "genera and species" (010-009) of logical classification.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Individuality operates at the aesthetic level of spirit — the domain of the particular — in contrast to the intellectual level, which deals with universals. History, for Croce, "implies concreteness and individuality" (006-019), sharing with art a focus on the singular event, whereas "law and concept" abstract from individuality to reach the universal. The practical forms of spirit (economic and moral) also engage individuals, but in the mode of action rather than expression.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes individuality from mere **particularity**. A particular is simply an instance of a kind; an individual, in Croce's sense, is irreducibly unique and cannot be placed under a universal without remainder. The "individuality of speech" (019-213) illustrates this: every genuine linguistic expression is individual, and the classification of expressions into parts of speech, grammatical categories, and stylistic types is a practical convenience that falsifies the aesthetic reality. Croce also distinguishes the individuality of art from the **universality** sought by philosophy: what "cannot attain to that individuality" (004-190) remains at the level of abstraction, outside the properly aesthetic. The individuality of expression is what makes art "incapable of being reduced to universality" (004-265).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The philosophical problem of individuality traces to Aristotle's distinction between primary substance (the individual) and secondary substance (the species). In modern philosophy, Leibniz's principle of the identity of indiscernibles and Kant's treatment of aesthetic judgment as singular ("this rose is beautiful") laid groundwork for Croce's position. Vico's emphasis on the concrete and the particular in historical understanding was a direct influence.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce made individuality the central criterion of the aesthetic, more radically than any predecessor. By identifying intuition, expression, and art, and grounding all three in individuality, Croce produced a system in which the uniqueness of each artwork is not an accident but the very definition of the aesthetic. This has practical consequences: the impossibility of translation (as opposed to re-creation), the rejection of artistic genres as aesthetic categories, and the denial of linear progress in art history all follow from the principle of individuality.

## Criticism and Debate

> The radical individualism of Croce's aesthetics has been criticized for making comparative aesthetic judgment difficult: if each work is absolutely individual, on what basis can we judge one to be better than another? Croce's answer lies in his conception of taste and the "distinctive consciousness of the beautiful" (011-189), which develops through repeated aesthetic experience and can judge the individual without reducing it to a universal rule.
