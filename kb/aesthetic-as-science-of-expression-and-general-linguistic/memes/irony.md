# Irony

In Croce's *Æsthetic*, irony appears in two distinct guises: as a formal rhetorical device classified among the figures of speech, and as Romantic irony — a broader aesthetic attitude theorized by the German Romantics. Croce treats both with the analytical clarity of his expressionist framework, situating irony within the general theory of artistic form.

## Definition

Croce lists irony among the rhetorical figures in the Bibliographical Appendix, alongside enigma, periphrase, and hyperbaton — formal devices that belong to the technical classification of expression [038-247]. He also notes irony's more ambitious form in the work of certain writers, describing "his more open and conscious irony" as a deliberate artistic stance [018-281]. The German Romantic theorist Tieck provided the most influential definition: irony is "a power which allows the poet to rise above his material" [028-250].

## In Croce's System

### Irony as Rhetorical Form

Within Croce's taxonomy of expression, irony as a rhetorical figure is a particular mode of indirection — "enclosed in a square envelope" [015-187]. It does not constitute a distinct aesthetic category but is simply one of the many formal resources available to expression. For Croce, the rhetorical classification of irony belongs to the empirical study of literary technique, not to aesthetic philosophy proper.

### Romantic Irony and Artistic Detachment

Romantic irony represents a more ambitious claim: that the artist can achieve a privileged vantage point through ironic detachment from the created work. Croce is characteristically skeptical of claims that elevate any particular rhetorical mode to a metaphysical principle. For him, the power of irony is the power of expression itself — the artist's capacity to give form to intuitive content, not a special transcendence of form.

> Tieck defined irony as a power which allows the poet to rise above his material — but this rising is nothing other than the freedom of expression itself, the artist's capacity to shape rather than be shaped. [028-250]

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Socratic irony, as practiced by Socrates in the Platonic dialogues, established the philosophical paradigm: the speaker feigns ignorance to expose the interlocutor's confusion. In modern aesthetics, Friedrich Schlegel and the Jena Romantics transformed irony from a rhetorical strategy into a metaphysical principle of artistic creation, influencing Tieck, Solger, and later Kierkegaard.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of irony is notable for its restraint. Rather than engage in the metaphysical debates surrounding Romantic irony, he classifies it within the general theory of expression. This deflationary approach reflects his broader conviction that aesthetic philosophy should not multiply entities beyond necessity — irony is expression, and expression is art.
