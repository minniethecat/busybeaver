[025-001] *The influence of [[giambattista-vico|Vico]].*

[025-002] This step in advance had no immediate effect. The pages in the *Scienza

[025-003] nuova* devoted to æsthetic doctrine were actually the least read of any

[025-004] in that marvellous book. Not that Vico exercised no influence at all;

[025-005] we shall see that several Italian authors both of his own time and of

[025-006] the generation immediately following show traces of his æsthetic ideas;

[025-007] but these traces are all external and material and therefore sterile.

[025-008] Outside [[italy|Italy]] the *Scienza nuova* (already announced by a compatriot

[025-009] in 1726 in the *Acta* of Leipzig with the graceful comment that *magis

[025-010] indulget ingenio quam veritati* and the pleasing information that *ab

[025-011] ipsis Italis taedio magis quam applausu excipitur*)[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_1_243) was mentioned

[025-012] toward the end of the century, as is well known, by Herder, Goethe,

[025-013] and some few others.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_2_244) In connection with poetry, especially with the

[025-014] Homeric question, Vico's book was quoted by Friedrich August Wolf, to

[025-015] whom it had been recommended by Cesarotti[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_3_245) after the publication of

[025-016] the *Prolegomena ad Homerum* (1795), but without any suspicion of the

[025-017] importance of its general doctrine of poetry, of which the Homeric

[025-018] hypothesis was a mere application. Wolf (1807) imagined himself in the

[025-019] presence of a talented forerunner in an isolated problem, instead of

[025-020] a man of intellectual stature towering above any philologist, however

[025-021] great.

[025-022] *Italian writers: Conti.*

[025-023] Neither by reliance on the works of Vico, who founded no real school,

[025-024] nor, it must be added, by any independent effort along new lines,

[025-025] did thought succeed in maintaining or improving upon the position

[025-026] already attained. A notable attempt to establish a philosophical

[025-027] theory of poetry and the arts was made by the Venetian A. Conti, who

[025-028] left numerous sketches for essays on imagination, the faculties of the

[025-029] soul, poetic imitation and similar subjects, designed for inclusion

[025-030] in a large treatise on the Beautiful and [[art|Art]]. Conti had started by

[025-031] professing ideas very like those of Du Bos, affirming that the poet

[025-032] must "put everything in images"; that taste is as indefinable as

[025-033] feeling, and that there are persons without taste just as there are

[025-034] blind and deaf persons; he also wrote polemical tracts against the

[025-035] Cartesians. Later he abandoned his sensationalistic or sentimentalist

[025-036] theories,[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_4_246) and, inquiring into the nature of poetry, declared

[025-037] himself ill-satisfied with Castelvetro, Patrizzi, and even Gravina.

[025-038] "Had Castelvetro," he observes, "who writes so subtly of Aristotle's

[025-039] *Poetics,* given two or three chapters to a philosophical explanation

[025-040] of the idea of imitation, he would have solved many questions raised

[025-041] but not clearly answered by himself concerning poetic theories. In his

[025-042] *Poetica* and in his controversy against Torquato Tasso, Patrizzi never

[025-043] succeeded in clearly defining the philosophical idea of imitation; he

[025-044] collected much useful information about the history of poetry, but

[025-045] wilfully lost the Platonic doctrine by allowing it to mingle with the

[025-046] historical detail instead of gathering it up without sophistry into a

[025-047] single point, when it would have appeared in a very different guise.

[025-048] The *Ragion poetica* of Gravina shadows forth a sort of philosophical

[025-049] idea of imitation; but so wholly engrossed is he in deducing therefrom

[025-050] rules for lyrical, dramatical and epic poetry, and illustrating each

[025-051] with examples from the most celebrated poets, Greek, Latin and Italian,

[025-052] that he is too busy to question the sufficiency of the fertile idea he

[025-053] has propounded."[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_5_247) A close follower of contemporary European thought,

[025-054] Conti was familiar with Hutcheson, whose theories he vigorously

[025-055] repudiated, observing, "Why this multiplication of faculties?" The

[025-056] soul is one, and for scholastic convenience only has been divided into

[025-057] three faculties: sense, imagination, intellect; the first "concerns

[025-058] herself with objects present before her; imagination with those afar

[025-059] into which memory gradually merges: but the object of sense and

[025-060] imagination is always particular; it is only the mind, the intellect,

[025-061] the spirit, that by comparing particulars apprehends the universal."

[025-062] "Before introducing a new sense for the pleasure of beauty" Hutcheson

[025-063] should have "assigned limits to these three faculties of cognition and

[025-064] demonstrated that the pleasure occasioned by beauty does not arise from

[025-065] the three pleasures of these three faculties, or from intellectual

[025-066] pleasure alone, to which they all reduce, if the functions of the

[025-067] soul be carefully analysed." Thus it would appear that the mistake

[025-068] of the Scotchman[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_6_248) arose from his habit of separating pleasure from

[025-069] the cognitive faculties, placing the former apart in a special empty

[025-070] "sense of beauty."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_7_249) On the other hand, when rewriting the history of

[025-071] the opinions of various critics upon the Aristotelian doctrine of the

[025-072] universal in poetry, Conti gave much weight to the dialogue *Naugerius

[025-073] seu De poëtica* of Fracastoro;[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_8_250) for an instant he seems on the

[025-074] point of grasping the essence of the poetic universal and identifying

[025-075] it with the characteristic, which makes us call even horrible things

[025-076] wholly beautiful. "In all his journeys Balzac never saw a beautiful

[025-077] old woman: in the poetic or picturesque sense an old woman is highly

[025-078] beautiful, if depicted as having suffered all the dilapidations of

[025-079] age": immediately after, however, he identifies the characteristic with

[025-080] Wolff's concept of perfection: "It does not differ from being, nor does

[025-081] being differ from the truth which the schoolmen call transcendental

[025-082] and which is the object of all arts and all sciences; we call it the

[025-083] object of poetry when by means of imaginary presentations it ravishes

[025-084] the intellect and moves the wall, transporting both these faculties

[025-085] into the ideal and archetypal world of which, following S. Augustine,

[025-086] Father Malebranche discourses at length in his *Recherche de la

[025-087] vérité*."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_9_251) In the same way Fracastoro's universal gives place to the

[025-088] universal of science: "Owing to the infinity of their determinations

[025-089] all we can know of particulars is their common properties, which

[025-090] is merely another manner of saying that we have no science save of

[025-091] universal. Thus it is precisely the same if we say the object of

[025-092] poetry is science or the universal; which is the doctrine of Navagero,

[025-093] following Aristotle."[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_10_252) The "imaginative universals of Signor Vico"

[025-094] (with whom he had interchanged some letters) opened no new views for

[025-095] him: he notes that Signor Vico "talks a great deal about them" and

[025-096] "holds that the most uncivilized men, having framed them not from any

[025-097] wish to please or serve others, but from the necessity of expressing

[025-098] their feelings as nature taught them, spoke in poetical language the

[025-099] elements of a theology, a physics, and an ethics wholly poetical."

[025-100] Conti excuses himself from immediate examination of "this critical

[025-101] question" and only opines that "it can be shown in many ways that

[025-102] these imaginative universals are the material or object of poetry,

[025-103] in so far as they contain within them sciences or things considered

[025-104] in themselves"[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_11_253)—a conclusion diametrically opposed to that which

[025-105] "Signor Vico" meant to express. Conti is next obliged to ask himself

[025-106] how it is possible that poetry's object should be not the true but the

[025-107] probable, when the universal of poetry is the same as that of science.

[025-108] He answers by coming down to the commonplace level of a [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]]:

[025-109] "When sciences receive a particular colouring, we pass from the true to

[025-110] the probable." Imitation means giving the impression of truth; that is

[025-111] done by selecting a few of its features only; and this is the procedure

[025-112] in which the probable just consists. If you wish to describe the

[025-113] rainbow poetically, a great part of the Newtonian optics must be thrown

[025-114] overboard; thus "many circumstances of mathematical demonstration" will

[025-115] be neglected in poetical descriptions, and the rest, which is utilized,

[025-116] will form the probable or that particular "which awakens the universal

[025-117] idea, slumbering in the minds of the learned." The great art of poetry

[025-118] consists "in selection of the [[representation|image]] containing the greatest number of

[025-119] points of universal doctrine which, by being inserted in the example,

[025-120] may so colour the precept that I may find it without seeking it, or

[025-121] recognize it through its connexion with events described."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_12_254) Hence

[025-122] poetry cannot be content with imitation; allegory too is needed: "in

[025-123] ancient poetry one thing is read and another is meant." Here follows

[025-124] the inevitable instance of the Homeric poems, in which Conti certainly

[025-125] finds elements which cannot be reduced to instruction and allegory and

[025-126] therefore to some extent deserve the Platonic condemnation.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_13_255) He

[025-127] recognizes a species of imagination differing from passive sensibility,

[025-128] "which Father Malebranche calls active imagination, and Plato the art

[025-129] of imagery; it comprises all that is meant by wit, sagacity, judgement

[025-130] and [[taste|good taste]], which teach a poet to use or not to use at a given time

[025-131] or place the rules and licences of art, and to control the extravagance

[025-132] of his imagery."[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_14_256) On the question of literary taste he follows

[025-133] the opinion of Trevisano and decides that it consists in "setting in

[025-134] mutual harmony, that is to say restraining within limits, the soul's

[025-135] cognitive faculties, memory, imagination and intellect, allowing none

[025-136] to overwhelm another."[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_15_257)

[025-137] *Quadrio and Zanotti.*

[025-138] By assiduous travail of thought and perpetual search for the best,

[025-139] Conti kept himself at the highest level of æsthetic speculation in

[025-140] contemporary Europe (Vico always excepted); at the same level as

[025-141] Baumgarten in [[germany|Germany]]. We pass rapidly over other Italian writers

[025-142] such as Quadrio (1739), author of the first great encyclopædia of

[025-143] universal literature, in which he defines poetry as "the science of

[025-144] things human and divine, presented in pictures to the populace, and

[025-145] written in words connected by measure";[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_16_258) and Francesco Maria Zanotti

[025-146] (1768), who describes poetry as "the art of versification in order to

[025-147] give pleasure":[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_17_259) the first is worthy of a mediæval anthologist,

[025-148] the second of a no less mediæval composer of handbooks on rhythm and

[025-149] methods of composition. The only serious student of æsthetic was

[025-150] Melchior Cesarotti.

[025-151] *Cesarotti*

[025-152] Cesarotti called attention to popular and primitive poetry: he

[025-153] translated Ossian and illustrated the text with dissertations; he

[025-154] unearthed antique Spanish poems and even the folk-songs of Mexico and

[025-155] Lapland; he studied Hebrew poetry; he dedicated the greater part of

[025-156] his life to the Homeric poems, examining all the theories of critics

[025-157] past and present, encountering Vico in this connexion and discussing

[025-158] his views. Besides this, he debated the origin of poetry, the pleasure

[025-159] given by tragedy, taste, [[beauty|the beautiful]], eloquence, style, in short

[025-160] every problem belonging to æsthetics which had been raised up to his

[025-161] time.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_18_260) One seems to catch an echo of Vico as one listens to his

[025-162] words on La Motte: "He had logic, but knew not that the logic of

[025-163] poetry differs somewhat from ordinary logic: he was a man of great

[025-164] talent, but he recognized talent only, and was incapable of feeling the

[025-165] immeasurable distance between judicious prose and poetry: the real

[025-166] Homer with his attractive faults will always be more beloved than his

[025-167] reformed Homer with his cold, affected virtue."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_19_261) Cesarotti purposed

[025-168] (1762) bringing out a great theoretico-historical book in whose first

[025-169] part "we shall suppose the non-existence of poetry and poetic art and

[025-170] try to trace by what path a man of illuminated reason can have reached

[025-171] the idea of the possibility of such an art and how he can have attained

[025-172] perfection by these means: every one will be able to see poetry growing

[025-173] up under his eyes, so to speak, and attest the truth of theory by

[025-174] the testimony of his own personal feelings."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_20_262) Although celebrated

[025-175] throughout Italy in his day as one who "with the most pure torch of

[025-176] philosophy has thrown beams of light into the darkest recesses of

[025-177] poetry and eloquence,"[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_21_263) it does not appear that the distinguished

[025-178] scholar, the pleasing and desultory philosopher, offered any profound

[025-179] or original solutions. In 1797 he defined poetry as "the art of

[025-180] representing and perfecting nature by means of picturesque, animated,

[025-181] imaginative and harmonious discourse."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_22_264)

[025-182] *Bettinelli and Pagano.*

[025-183] The fashion of the day in philosophy made men impatient of the ideas

[025-184] found in writers of treatises of former times. Arteaga praises

[025-185] Cesarotti for "that fine tact, that impartial criticism, that

[025-186] logical spirit derived not from the trickling streamlets of Sperone,

[025-187] Castelvetro, Casa and Bembo, but from the profound and inexhaustible

[025-188] springs of Montesquieu, Hume, Voltaire, d'Alembert, Sulzer, and

[025-189] writers of like temper."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_23_265) Writing to Saverio Bettinelli, who was

[025-190] preparing a work on *Enthusiasm,* Paradisi hoped it would prove "a

[025-191] metaphysical history of enthusiasm which shall outweigh all those

[025-192] Poetics which are only fit to be burned," and would "make waste paper

[025-193] of Castelvetro, the 'Mintumo,' and that stupid creature, Quadrio."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_24_266)

[025-194] In spite of these aspirations Bettinelli's book (1769) contains little

[025-195] beyond vivacious and eloquent empirical observations concerning the

[025-196] psychology of poets, "poetic enthusiasm," to which he assigns six

[025-197] degrees, namely, elevation, vision, rapidity, novelty and surprise,

[025-198] passion and transfusion. Equally empirical was Mario Pagano in his two

[025-199] fragments, *Gusto e le belle arti* and *Origine e natura della poesia*

[025-200] (1783-1785), in which he grotesquely combines some ideas from Vico with

[025-201] the current sensationalism. Theoretico-imaginative form and sensuous

[025-202] pleasure are presented by him as two historical periods of art. "In

[025-203] their cradle the fine arts are directed towards making a true imitation

[025-204] of nature rather than towards loveliness. Their first steps are towards

[025-205] expression rather than charm.... In the most ancient poetry, even in

[025-206] the ballads of barbarous ages, there lives a most compelling pathos:

[025-207] passions are expressed naturally, even the sound of the words is

[025-208] alive with the expression of the things described." But "the period

[025-209] of perfection is reached at the moment when exact imitation of nature

[025-210] is coupled with complete beauty, accord and harmony," when "the taste

[025-211] is refined and society reaches its most complete form of culture."

[025-212] Fine arts "precede by a short time the dawn of philosophy, that is

[025-213] to say, the time of the most intense perfection of society"; indeed,

[025-214] certain modes of art, such as tragedy, must necessarily come later

[025-215] than philosophy whose aid must be invoked to further "the purgation of

[025-216] manners."[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_25_267)

[025-217] *German disciples of Baumgarten. G. F. Meier.*

[025-218] The compatriots and successors of Baumgarten, like those of Vico,

[025-219] did little by way of understanding or improving upon his work. An

[025-220] enthusiastic admirer and disciple of Baumgarten who had attended his

[025-221] lectures at Frankfort-on-the-Oder, Georg Friedrich Meier, came forward

[025-222] in 1746 to defend the *Meditationes* against the attacks of Quistorp to

[025-223] whom the master had deigned no reply;[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_26_268) already in 1748, prior to

[025-224] the publication of the *Æsthetic,* he had published the first volume

[025-225] of his *Principles of all the Beautiful Sciences*,[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_27_269) followed in

[025-226] 1749 and 1750 by the second and third volumes. This book, which is

[025-227] a complete exposition of Baumgarten's theory, is divided, according

[025-228] to the master's method, into three parts: invention of beautiful

[025-229] thoughts (heuristic), æsthetic method (methodic), and the beautiful

[025-230] signification of thoughts (semiotic); the first of these (occupying

[025-231] two and a half volumes) is subdivided into three sections: beauty

[025-232] of sense-apprehension (æsthetic richness, grandeur, verisimilitude,

[025-233] vivacity, certainty, sensitive life and wit), sensitive faculties

[025-234] (attention, abstraction, senses, imagination, subtlety, acumen,

[025-235] memory, poetic power, taste, foresight, conjecture, signification and

[025-236] the minor appetitive faculties), and the diverse kinds of beautiful

[025-237] thought (æsthetic concepts, judgements, and syllogisms). Elsewhere

[025-238] than in this book, which was reprinted many times (in 1757 an epitome

[025-239] was issued[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_28_270)), Meier discusses Æsthetic in several of his numerous

[025-240] works, especially in a little tract, *Considerations on the First

[025-241] Principles of all Fine Arts and Sciences*.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_29_271) Who was more tenderly

[025-242] inclined than he towards the science so recently born and baptized? He

[025-243] was ardent in her defence against those who denied both her possibility

[025-244] and her utility, and against those who admitted these yet complained,

[025-245] not unreasonably, that she was substantially the same as that which in

[025-246] former days had been treated as Poetics and [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]]. He parried this

[025-247] accusation, of which he recognized the partial truth, by asserting

[025-248] that it was impossible for one writer to have perfect knowledge of all

[025-249] the arts: another of his excuses was to the effect that Æsthetic was

[025-250] a science too young to show the perfection reached by other sciences

[025-251] after the cultivation of centuries; in one place he says he has no

[025-252] intention of arguing "with those enemies of Æsthetic who will not or

[025-253] cannot see the true nature and aim of this science, but have built for

[025-254] themselves in its place a deformed and miserable image against which,

[025-255] when they fight, they fight against themselves." With philosophic

[025-256] resignation he concludes that the same fate is in store for Æsthetic as

[025-257] for every science: "At first when almost unknown they encounter enemies

[025-258] and detractors who ridicule them through ignorance and prejudice;

[025-259] but later they meet persons of intellect who, by working at them

[025-260] conjointly, carry them on to their proper perfection."[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_30_272)

[025-261] *Confusions of Meier.*

[025-262] Students of the new science flocked to Halle University to hear Meier

[025-263] lecture on Æsthetic whose "chief author" or "inventor" (*Haupturheber,

[025-264] Erfinder*), as Meier never tired of repeating, was "Herr Professor

[025-265] Baumgarten"; at the same time warning them that his own *Anfangsgründe*

[025-266] were no mere transcription of Baumgarten's lectures.[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_31_273) Still, while

[025-267] recognizing the great gifts of Meier as publicity-agent, the facility,

[025-268] clarity and wealth of his eloquence, and his shrewdness in polemic,

[025-269] one cannot altogether deny the justice of the remark upon "Professor

[025-270] Baumgarten of Frankfort and his ape (*Affe)* Professor Meier of

[025-271] Halle."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_32_274) Every defect of Baumgarten's Æsthetic reappears accentuated

[025-272] in Meier; the limits of the inferior cognitive faculties, alleged as

[025-273] the domain of poetry and the arts, are laid down by him most strangely.

[025-274] It is curious to note how, for example, he interprets the difference

[025-275] between the confused (æsthetical) and the distinct (logical), and the

[025-276] proposition that beauty disappears when made the object of distinct

[025-277] thought. "The cheeks of a beautiful girl whereon bloom the roses of

[025-278] youth are lovely so long as they are looked at with the naked eye. But

[025-279] let them be examined with a magnifying glass. Where is their beauty?

[025-280] One can hardly believe that such a disgusting surface, scaly, all

[025-281] mounts and hollows, the pores full of dirt, with hairs sprouting here

[025-282] and there, can be the seat of that amorous attraction which subdues

[025-283] the heart."[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_33_275) That is described as "æsthetically false" whose truth

[025-284] the inferior faculty is unable to grasp: for example, the theory that

[025-285] bodies are composed of monads.[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_34_276) Once they have become intelligible

[025-286] to these faculties, general concepts possess great æsthetic richness,

[025-287] since they include infinite consequences and particular cases.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_35_277)

[025-288] Æsthetic also comprehends those things which cannot be thought

[025-289] distinctly or, if so thought, might be capable of upsetting philosophic

[025-290] gravity: a kiss may be an excellent subject for a poet; but whatever

[025-291] would be thought of a philosopher who sought to demonstrate its

[025-292] necessity by the mathematical method?[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_36_278) Moreover, Meier includes the

[025-293] whole theory of observation and experiment in Æsthetic, to which this

[025-294] theory belongs, he says, by right of its connexion with the senses,[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_37_279)

[025-295] and also the whole theory of the appetitive faculties, because

[025-296] "æsthetic requires not only a fine wit but a noble heart as well."[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_38_280)

[025-297] He comes near truth sometimes, when, for example, he observes that

[025-298] the logical form presupposes the æsthetic and that our first concepts

[025-299] are sensitive, later becoming distinct by the help of logic;[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_39_281)

[025-300] and when he condemns allegory as "among the most decadent forms of

[025-301] beautiful thinking."[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_40_282) But, on the other hand, he thinks that logical

[025-302] distinctions and definitions, although not necessarily sought after

[025-303] by genius, are very useful in poetry; they are even indispensable as

[025-304] regulators of beautiful thinking and make up, as it were, the skeleton

[025-305] of the body poetic: great care, however, must be taken not to judge

[025-306] æsthetical general concepts, *notiones æstheticæ universales,* with

[025-307] the rigorous exactitude demanded by philosophical. And since such

[025-308] concepts, taken singly, may be likened to unstrung jewels, they must be

[025-309] connected by the string of æsthetic judgement and syllogism, the theory

[025-310] of which is identical with that presented by Logic, setting aside that

[025-311] part which is of little or no use to genius, but belongs exclusively

[025-312] to the philosopher.[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_41_283) In his *Considerations* of 1757 Meier, having

[025-313] combated the principle of imitation (which appeared to him at once too

[025-314] broad, since science and morals are also imitations of nature, and too

[025-315] narrow, since art does not imitate natural objects solely nor should

[025-316] it imitate them all, for the immoral must be excluded), reaffirmed the

[025-317] thesis that the æsthetic principle consists in the "greatest possible

[025-318] beauty of sense-perception."[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_42_284) He upheld this by condemning as

[025-319] erroneous the belief that this sense-perception is wholly sensuous

[025-320] and confused, without any gleam of distinctness or rationality. The

[025-321] perception of sweet, bitter, red, etc., is wholly sensuous; but there

[025-322] is another perception which is both sensuous and intellectual, confused

[025-323] and distinct, in which both faculties, the higher and the lower,

[025-324] collaborate. When intellectuality prevails in this consciousness,

[025-325] then we have science: when sensibility, then we have poetry. "From

[025-326] our explanation it will be gathered that the inferior cognitive

[025-327] faculties must collect all the material of a poem, and all its parts.

[025-328] Intellect and judgement, on the other hand, watch and ensure that these

[025-329] materials are placed side by side in such a way that in their connexion

[025-330] distinction and order may be observed."[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_43_285) Here a plunge into

[025-331] sensationalism, there a fugitive glimpse of truth: most often, and in

[025-332] conclusion, an adherence to the old mechanical, ornamental, pedagogic

[025-333] theory of poetry: this is the impression left on us by the æsthetic

[025-334] writings of Meier.

[025-335] *M. Mendelssohn and other followers of Baumgarten. Vogue of

[025-336] Æsthetic.*

[025-337] Another disciple of Baumgarten, Mendelssohn, conceiving beauty

[025-338] as "indistinct image of a perfection," deduced that God can have

[025-339] no perception of beauty, as this is merely a phenomenon of human

[025-340] imperfection. According to him a primary form of pleasure is that

[025-341] of the senses, arising from "the bettered state of our bodily

[025-342] constitution"; a secondary form is the æsthetic fact of sensible

[025-343] beauty, that is to say, unity in variety; a third form is perfection,

[025-344] or harmony in variety.[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_44_286) He too repudiates Hutcheson's *deus ex

[025-345] machina,* the sense of beauty. Sensible beauty, perfection such as

[025-346] can be apprehended by the senses, is independent of the fact that

[025-347] the object represented is beautiful or ugly, good or bad by nature;

[025-348] it suffices that it leaves us not indifferent: whence Mendelssohn

[025-349] agrees with Baumgarten's definition, "a poem is a discourse sensibly

[025-350] perfect."[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_45_287) Elias Schlegel (1742) conceived art as imitation, not

[025-351] so servile as to seem a copy, but having similarity rather than

[025-352] identity with nature: he considered the duty of poetry was first to

[025-353] please and only afterwards to instruct.[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_46_288) Treatises on Æsthetic,

[025-354] university lectures or slender volumes for use of the public, *Theories

[025-355] of the Fine Arts and Letters, Manuals, Sketches, Texts, Principles,

[025-356] Introductions, Lectures, Essays,* and *Considerations on Taste* poured

[025-357] down thick and fast on Germany during the second half of the eighteenth

[025-358] century. There are at least thirty full or complete treatises and many

[025-359] dozens of minor tracts or fragments. After the Protestant universities,

[025-360] the Catholic took up the new science, which was taught by Riedel at

[025-361] Vienna, Herwigh at Würzburg, Ladrone at Mainz, Jacobi at Freiburg,

[025-362] and by others at Ingolstadt after the expulsion of the Jesuits.[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_47_289) A

[025-363] pretty little volume on the *First Principles of the Fine Arts*[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_48_290) was

[025-364] written (1790) for Catholic schools by the notorious Franciscan friar

[025-365] Eulogius Schneider, who, after being unfrocked, terrorised Strasburg in

[025-366] the days of the Convention, and met his end under the guillotine. The

[025-367] frenzied output of these German *Æsthetics* resembles that of *Poetics*

[025-368] in Italy in the sixteenth century, after the rise to popularity of

[025-369] Aristotle's treatise. Between 1771 and 1774 the Swiss Sulzer brought

[025-370] out his great æsthetic encyclopædia, *The General Theory of the Fine

[025-371] Arts,* in alphabetical order, with historical notes upon each article,

[025-372] which were greatly enlarged in the second edition of 1792, edited by a

[025-373] retired Prussian captain, von Blankenburg.[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_49_291) In 1799, one J. Roller

[025-374] published a first *Sketch of the History of Æsthetic,*[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_50_292) in which he

[025-375] observes not unjustly, "Patriotic youth will be pleased to recognize

[025-376] that Germany has produced more literature on this subject than any

[025-377] other country."[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_51_293)

[025-378] *Eberhard and Eschenburg.*

[025-379] Confining ourselves to bare mention of the works of Riedel (1767),

[025-380] Faber (1767), Schütz (1776-1778), Schubart (1777-1781), Westenrieder

[025-381] (1777), Szerdahel (1779), König (1784), Gang (1785), Meiners (1787),

[025-382] Schott (1789), Moritz (1788),[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_52_294) we will select from the crowd the

[025-383] *Theory of Fine Arts and Letters* (1783) of Johann August Eberhard,

[025-384] successor to Meier in the Chair at Halle,[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_53_295) and the *Sketch of a

[025-385] Theory and Literature of Letters* (1783) by Johann Joachim Eschenburg,

[025-386] one of the most popular books of the day for students.[[54]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_54_296) Both

[025-387] these authors are followers of Baumgarten, with inclinations towards

[025-388] sensationalism; amongst other things Eberhard considered the beautiful

[025-389] as "that which pleases the most distinct senses," that is to say, of

[025-390] sight and hearing.

[025-391] *J. G. Sulzer.*

[025-392] A word must be accorded to Sulzer, in whom we find the most curious

[025-393] alternation of new and old, the romantic influence of the new Swiss

[025-394] school and the utilitarianism and intellectualism of his day. He

[025-395] asserts that beauty exists wherever unity, variety and order are found:

[025-396] the work of an artist is strictly in the form, in lively expression

[025-397] (*lebhafte Darstellung*): the material is irrelevant to art, but

[025-398] the duty of every reasonable and sensible man is to make judicious

[025-399] selection. The beauty which is used to clothe the good as well as the

[025-400] bad is not the ineffable, celestial Beauty, offspring of the alliance

[025-401] between the beautiful, the good and the perfect, which awakens more

[025-402] than mere pleasure, a veritable joy which ravishes and beatifies our

[025-403] soul. Such is the human face when, by filling the eye of the beholder

[025-404] with the pleasure of form arising from the variety, proportion and

[025-405] order of the features, it proceeds to arouse the imagination and

[025-406] intellect by its suggestion of interior perfection; of the same

[025-407] nature is the statue of a great man carved by Phidias, or a patriotic

[025-408] oration by Cicero. If truth lie outside art and belong to philosophy,

[025-409] the most noble use to which art may be put is to make us feel the

[025-410] important truths which lend her strength and energy, not to mention

[025-411] that truth itself enters into art in the shape of truthful imitation

[025-412] or representation. Sulzer also repeats (and he is not the last) that

[025-413] orators, historians and poets are intermediaries between speculative

[025-414] philosophy and the people.[[55]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_55_297)

[025-415] *K. H. Heydenreich.*

[025-416] Karl Heinrich Heydenreich returns to a sounder tradition when he

[025-417] defines art (1790) as "a representation of a determinate state of

[025-418] sensibility," and observes that man, as a cognitive being, is impelled

[025-419] to enlarge the sphere of his cognitions and impart his discoveries to

[025-420] his fellows, while as a sensitive being he is impelled to represent

[025-421] and communicate his sensations; whence arise science and art. But

[025-422] Heydenreich does not clearly grasp the cognitive character of art; for

[025-423] in his opinion sensations become objects of artistic representation

[025-424] either because they are pleasing or, when not pleasing, because they

[025-425] are useful to further the moral aims of man as a social being; the

[025-426] objects of sensibility which enter into art must be possessed of

[025-427] intrinsic excellence and value and bear reference not to a single

[025-428] individual but to the individual as a rational being: hence the

[025-429] objectivity and necessity of taste. Like Baumgarten and Meier, he

[025-430] divides Æsthetic into three parts: a doctrine of *inventio,* another of

[025-431] *methodica,* a third of the *ars significandi*.[[56]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_56_298)

[025-432] J. G. Herder.

[025-433] Another disciple of Baumgarten is J. G. Herder, who had an unbounded

[025-434] admiration for the old Berlin master, whom he calls "the Aristotle

[025-435] of his day," and defends him warmly against those who think fit to

[025-436] describe him as a "stupid and obtuse syllogizer" (1769). On the

[025-437] other hand he had slight esteem for subsequent Æsthetic, for example

[025-438] Meier's work, which he stigmatized accurately enough as "in part a

[025-439] re-mastication of Logic, in part a patchwork of metaphorical terms,

[025-440] comparisons and examples." "O Æsthetic!" he cries with emphasis,

[025-441] "O Æsthetic! the most fertile, the most beautiful and by far the

[025-442] most novel of all abstract sciences, in what cavern of the Muses is

[025-443] sleeping the youth of my philosophic nation destined to bring thee

[025-444] to perfection?"[[57]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_57_299) He denied Baumgarten's claim to have established

[025-445] an *Ars pulchre cogitandi* instead of limiting himself to a simple

[025-446] *Scientia de pulchro et pulchris philosophice cogitans,* and ridiculed

[025-447] the scruple which held Æsthetic to be unworthy of the dignity of

[025-448] Philosophy.[[58]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_58_300) To compensate for this, however, he accepted the

[025-449] fundamental definition cf poetry as *oratio sensitiva perfecta*:

[025-450] gem of definitions (says he), the best that has ever been invented,

[025-451] that penetrates to the heart of the matter, touches the true poetic

[025-452] principles and opens the most extended view over the entire philosophy

[025-453] of the beautiful, "coupling poetry with her sisters, the fine

[025-454] arts."[[59]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_59_301) Like Cesarotti the Italian, but with much less vivacity and

[025-455] brilliance, Herder the German had studied primitive poetry, Ossian and

[025-456] the songs of ancient peoples, Shakespeare (1773), popular love-songs

[025-457] (1778), the spirit of Hebrew poetry (1782), and oriental poetry; these

[025-458] studies powerfully impressed upon his mind the sensitive nature of

[025-459] poetry. His friend Hamann (1762) had written these memorable words,

[025-460] which read like an extract from one of Vico's aphorisms: "Poetry is

[025-461] the mother-tongue of mankind: in the same way that the garden is older

[025-462] than the ploughed field, painting than writing, song than declamation,

[025-463] barter than trade. The repose of our most ancient progenitors was a

[025-464] slumber deeper than ours; their motion a tumultuous dance. They spent

[025-465] seven days in the silence of thought or of stupor; and opened their

[025-466] mouths to pronounce winged words. Their speech was sensation and

[025-467] passion, and they understood nothing but images. Of images is composed

[025-468] all the treasure of human knowledge and felicity."[[60]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_60_302) Although

[025-469] Herder, who knew and admired Vico,[[61]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_61_303) does not mention him by name

[025-470] when treating of language and poetry, one might suppose him to be

[025-471] influenced by the great Neapolitan at least in the final consolidation

[025-472] of his theories; but, on the contrary, the authors whom he chiefly

[025-473] quotes in this connexion are Du Bos, Goguet and Condillac, and observes

[025-474] "the first beginnings of human speech in tone, gesture, expression of

[025-475] sensations and thoughts by means of images and signs, can only have

[025-476] been a kind of crude poetry, and so it is among every savage nation

[025-477] in the world." Not a speech with punctuation and a sense of syllable,

[025-478] like ours, learning as we do to read and write, but an unsyllabled

[025-479] melody which gave birth to the primitive epic. "Natural man depicts

[025-480] what he sees and as he sees it, alive, powerful, monstrous; in order

[025-481] or disorder, as he sees and hears, so he reproduces. Not alone did

[025-482] barbarous tongues thus arrange their images, but Greek and Latin do

[025-483] the same. As the senses offered material, so the poets utilized it;

[025-484] especially in Homer we see how closely nature is followed in images

[025-485] which glow and fade perpetually and inimitably. He describes things

[025-486] and events line by line, scene by scene; and, in the same way, he

[025-487] paints men in their very bodies, actually as they speak and move."

[025-488] Later we distinguish epic from what we call history; because the former

[025-489] "not only describes what has happened but describes the event in its

[025-490] entirety, showing how it occurred in the only possible way, having

[025-491] regard to surrounding circumstance of body and spirit": this is the

[025-492] reason of the more philosophical character of poetry. As for pleasure,

[025-493] no doubt we do find poetry pleasant; but the idea that the poet's

[025-494] motive is merely to excite pleasure cannot be condemned too strongly.

[025-495] "Homer's gods were as essential and indispensable to the poet's world

[025-496] as the forces of motion are to the world of matter. Without the

[025-497] deliberations and activities of Olympus, none of the necessary events

[025-498] which happen on this earth could take place. Homer's magic island in

[025-499] the western sea belongs to the map of his hero's wanderings by the same

[025-500] necessity which placed it on the map of the world: it was necessary

[025-501] to the plan of his poem. It is the same with the severe Dante and his

[025-502] circles of Hell and Heaven." Art is formative: she disciplines, orders

[025-503] and governs the imagination and every faculty of man: not only did she

[025-504] generate history, "but, earlier yet, she created gods and heroes and

[025-505] purified the uncouth imaginations and fables of peoples with their

[025-506] Titans, monsters and Gorgons, reducing to limit and law the riotous

[025-507] imagination of ignorant men which knows no bounds or rule."[[62]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_62_304)

[025-508] Notwithstanding these intuitions, so like those of Vico early in the

[025-509] same century, Herder as a philosopher is inferior to his Italian

[025-510] predecessor, and in point of fact does not rise superior to Baumgarten.

[025-511] By application of Leibniz' law of continuity, he too arrived at the

[025-512] opinion that the pleasing, the true, the beautiful and the good are

[025-513] degrees of one single activity. For instance, sensible pleasure" is

[025-514] a participation in the true and the good, so far as the senses may

[025-515] comprehend them; the feeling of pleasure and pain is no other than the

[025-516] feeling of the true and the good, that is to say, the consciousness

[025-517] that the aim of our organism, the conservation of our well-being and

[025-518] the avoidance of our hurt, has been attained."[[63]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_63_305) Fine arts and

[025-519] letters are all instructive (*bildend*): hence the terms *humaniora,*

[025-520] the Greek *καλόν,* the Latin *pulchrum,* the *gentle* arts of days

[025-521] of chivalry, *les belles lettres et les beaux arts* of the [[france|French]]. A

[025-522] group of them (gymnastic, dance, etc.) educates the body; a second

[025-523] group (painting, plastic, music) educates the nobler senses of man,

[025-524] the eye, the ear, the hand and tongue; a third (poetry) touches the

[025-525] intellect, the imagination and the reason: a fourth group governs human

[025-526] tendencies and inclinations.[[64]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_64_306) Herder disapproved of the facile

[025-527] theorists of art who began straight away with a definition of beauty,

[025-528] a complex and involved concept. He held that the theory of fine arts

[025-529] should be subdivided into three theories, each to be built up from

[025-530] the foundations, the theory of sight, of hearing and of touch, that

[025-531] is to say of painting, music and sculpture, *i.e.* into æsthetical

[025-532] Optics, æsthetical Acoustics and æsthetical Physiology. "Fairly well

[025-533] elaborated in the psychological and subjective aspects, Æsthetic

[025-534] is sadly undeveloped in all that belongs to the object and to the

[025-535] sensation of beauty, without which there can never be a fertile theory

[025-536] of the Beautiful capable of influencing all the arts."[[65]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_65_307) Taste is not

[025-537] "a fundamental faculty of the soul but a habitual application of our

[025-538] judgement (intellectual judgement) to objects of beauty"; an acquired

[025-539] facility of the intellect (of which Herder outlines the genesis).[[66]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_66_308)

[025-540] The poet is poet not only in his imagination but in his intellect.

[025-541] In 1782 he writes: "The barbarous name Æsthetic of recent invention

[025-542] indicates nothing beyond a section of Logic: that which we call taste

[025-543] is neither more nor less than a quick and rapid judgement which does

[025-544] not exclude truth and profundity, but rather presupposes and promotes

[025-545] them. All didactic poetry is nothing more than philosophy rendered

[025-546] sensible: the fable as exposition of a general doctrine is truth in

[025-547] act, in activity.... When expounded and applied to human affairs,

[025-548] Philosophy is not only a fine art in herself (*schöne Wissenschaft,*)

[025-549] but the mother of Beauty: it is only through her that Rhetoric and

[025-550] Poetry can ever be educational, useful, or in the truest sense

[025-551] pleasant."[[67]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_67_309)

[025-552] *Philosophy of language.*

[025-553] Herder and Hamann deserve our gratitude for having brought a current of

[025-554] fresh air into the study of the philosophy of language. The lead given

[025-555] by the Port-Royal authors had been followed since the beginning of

[025-556] the century by many writers of logical or general grammars. According

[025-557] to the French Encyclopædia, "*La grammaire générale est la science

[025-558] raisonnée des principes immuables et généraux de la parole prononcée

[025-559] ou écrite dans toutes les langues*,"[[68]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_68_310) and d'Alembert spoke of

[025-560] grammarians of invention and grammarians of memory, assigning to the

[025-561] former the duty of studying the metaphysics of grammar.[[69]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_69_311) General

[025-562] grammars had been written by Du Marsais, De Beauzée, and Condillac

[025-563] in France; Harris in [[england|England]]; and many others.[[70]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_70_312) But what was the

[025-564] relation between general grammar and particular grammars? If logic be

[025-565] one, how comes it that languages are many? Is the variety of tongues

[025-566] but a deviation on their part from one single model? And, if there be

[025-567] no such deviation or error, what is the explanation of the fact? What

[025-568] is language, and how was it born? If language be external to thought,

[025-569] how can thought exist if not in language? "*Si les hommes*," says

[025-570] Jean-Jacques Rousseau, "*ont eu besoin de la parole pour apprendre

[025-571] à penser, ils ont eu bien plus besoin encore de savoir penser pour

[025-572] trouver l'art de la parole*"; appalled at the difficulty, he declares

[025-573] his conviction "*de l'impossibilité presque démontrée que les langues

[025-574] aient pu naître et s'établir par des moyens purement humains.*"[[71]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_71_313)

[025-575] Such questions became fashionable; books on the origin and formation

[025-576] of language were written by de Brosses (1765) and Court de Gébelin

[025-577] (1776) in France, by Monboddo (1774) in England, Süssmilch (1766) and

[025-578] Tiedemann in Germany, and Cesarotti (1785) in Italy, and by others

[025-579] who had some slight acquaintance with Vico, but profited little by

[025-580] it.[[72]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_72_314) None of the above-named writers was able to free himself of

[025-581] the notion that speech was either natural and mechanical, or else a

[025-582] symbol attached to thought: whereas in fact it was impossible to solve

[025-583] the difficulties under which they were labouring except by dropping

[025-584] the notion of a sign or symbol and attaining the conception of the

[025-585] active and expressive imagination, verbal imagination, language as

[025-586] the expression not of intellect but of intuition. An approach towards

[025-587] this explanation was made by Herder in a brilliant and imaginative

[025-588] thesis in 1770 upon this subject of the origin of language, chosen

[025-589] for discussion by the Berlin Academy. In it he says that language is

[025-590] the reflexion or consciousness (*Besonnenheit*) of man. "Man shows

[025-591] reflexion when he puts forth freely such force of mind as enables him

[025-592] to make selection from amongst the crowd of sensations by which he is

[025-593] assailed: from the ocean of the senses, so to speak, to select a single

[025-594] wave and consciously to watch it. He shows reflexion when, amidst the

[025-595] thronging chaos of images which pass before him as in a dream, he can

[025-596] in a waking moment collect himself and fasten his attention upon a

[025-597] single image, examine it calmly and clearly, and separate it from its

[025-598] neighbours. Once again, man shows reflexion when he is able not merely

[025-599] to grasp vividly and clearly all the properties of an image, but also

[025-600] to recognize one or more of its distinctive properties." The language

[025-601] of man "does not depend on the organization of the mouth, for even he

[025-602] who is dumb from birth has, if he reflects, a language; it is not a

[025-603] cry of the senses, since it resides in a reflective creature, not in a

[025-604] breathing machine; it is not an affair of imitation, since imitation

[025-605] of nature is a means, and we are here trying to explain the end: much

[025-606] less is it an arbitrary convention; a savage in the depths of the

[025-607] forest would have had to create a language for himself even though he

[025-608] never used it. Language is an understanding of the soul with herself,

[025-609] necessary just in so far as man is man."[[73]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_73_315) Here language begins to

[025-610] show itself no longer as purely mechanical or as something derived

[025-611] from arbitrary choice and invention, but as a creative activity and a

[025-612] primary affirmation of the activity of the human mind. Herder's essay

[025-613] may not state such a view unequivocally, but it points forward to such

[025-614] a conclusion in a striking way for which its author has not received

[025-615] the credit he deserves. Hamann, in reviewing his friend's theories,

[025-616] agreed with him in denying the origin of language by invention or

[025-617] arbitrary choice; while dwelling also on the liberty of man, he

[025-618] regarded language as something which man could only have learned by

[025-619] means of a mystical *communicatio idiomatum* from God.[[74]](280320132259829826_54618-h-14.htm.html#Footnote_74_316) That, too,

[025-620] was one way of recognizing that the mystery of language is not to be

[025-621] solved except by placing it in the forefront of the problem of the

[025-622] spirit.
