# Normative Science

In Croce's philosophy, the term "normative" designates any science or discipline that claims to prescribe what ought to be, rather than simply describe what is. Croce argues that aesthetic and linguistic phenomena are intrinsically creative and cannot be subjected to normative regulation.

## Definition

Croce discusses normative sciences in general as disciplines "which regulate" — that is, which set out rules for correct practice (007-091). The paradigm case is normative grammar: the attempt to prescribe correct linguistic usage. Croce rejects the very possibility of such a science, arguing that the creative, expressive nature of language makes any fixed system of rules inapplicable.

## In Croce's System

### The Impossibility of Normative Grammar

Croce's most sustained argument against normativity in aesthetics appears in his discussion of language. He declares "the impossibility of a normative grammar" as a direct consequence of the identity of language and expression (019-244). "The error lies in the conception of a (normative) Grammar, establishing rules of speaking well" — for if language is individual expression, there can be no general standard of correctness external to the particular act of expression (019-251). Even those who recognise "the impossibility of a normative grammar" in theory often relapse into prescribing rules in practice (019-255). The most a grammar can be is "precisely a description" of linguistic facts, not a legislator of usage (019-262).

### Normative Aesthetics

The same argument applies to aesthetics. Croce denies "the possibility of æsthetic as a special normative science," not because aesthetics is not a science, but because "there are no such things as normative sciences" in the strict sense at all (036-479, 036-480). The rules of art — the unities of time and place, the prescriptions for genre, the canons of proportion — are empirical generalisations that have been illegitimately elevated to the status of laws.

## Historical Context

### The Normative Tradition

The normative approach to art and language has ancient roots. Aristotle's *Poetics* was read for centuries as a rulebook for tragedy. Horace's *Ars Poetica* explicitly prescribed norms for poetic composition. In the eighteenth century, normative poetics reached its apogee in the French Academy's codification of literary rules. Croce traces how "psychology was separated from logic as a normative science" in the post-Kantian tradition, and how the normative impulse persisted even in otherwise descriptive approaches (031-239).

In the German idealist tradition, both "Æsthetic and Logic" were treated "as normative sciences" (037-398). Croce's rejection of normativity is thus a rejection of a tradition stretching from Aristotle through Wolff and Baumgarten to the nineteenth century.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to apply consistently to aesthetics the principle that creative activity cannot be governed by external rules. Just as there is no normative grammar that can teach one to speak well — good speech arises from clear thought and expressive mastery, not from rule-following — there is no normative aesthetics that can teach one to make art. The only "rule" of art is the internal necessity of the individual intuition-expression.

## Criticism and Debate

> "The error lies in the conception of a (normative) Grammar, establishing rules of speaking well. But a (normative) grammar be precisely a description." — Croce, *Æsthetic* (019-255)

Critics have argued that Croce conflates two distinct senses of "normative": prescriptive rules imposed from without, and immanent norms that arise from within a practice. Later aesthetic theory has attempted to recover a notion of artistic normativity that does not reduce to external prescription, recognising that traditions, conventions, and shared standards play a real role in artistic production and evaluation.
