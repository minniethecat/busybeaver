# Empirical

A term Croce uses critically to designate distinctions and sciences that operate on quantitative or extensive grounds rather than on the qualitative, philosophical grasp of the essence of aesthetic activity; empirical approaches, for Croce, can never penetrate to the nature of art (P02-018, 003-048, 006-026).

## Definition

Croce contrasts the empirical with the properly philosophical throughout his aesthetics. An "empirical distinction" is one based on external, extensive, or quantitative criteria rather than on the inner, qualitative nature of the phenomenon. For Croce, aesthetic science must be philosophical — that is, it must grasp the essence of art as expression — and not empirical, which merely classifies outward features. The difference between art and non-art is "extensive and empirical" (003-048) when framed in terms of degree rather than kind, and such formulations fail to capture the real distinction.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Empirical thinking belongs, for Croce, to the practical rather than the theoretical sphere: it serves the purposes of classification, pedagogy, and convenience, but yields no genuine knowledge of essence. The "altogether empirical distinction between an internal and an external world" (004-090) is a practical fiction, not a philosophical truth. Empirical science, with "its infinite divisions" (006-026), fragments the unity of spirit into arbitrary categories that obscure rather than illuminate. Croce does not deny the practical utility of empirical classifications, but he insists they must not be mistaken for philosophical understanding.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes sharply between **empirical science** and **philosophical science**. The former operates by enumeration and induction, collecting data without grasping the principle that makes the data intelligible. The latter proceeds from the concept, understanding phenomena through their essential nature. In aesthetics, "empirical and impossible to define" (003-056) are the various sub-genres and classifications that critics vulgarly apply to art — they are empirical because they lack a philosophical ground. Croce also distinguishes the empirical from the **speculative**: "philosophical and empirical" (P02-174) treatments of art differ not in degree but in kind.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Croce's critique of empiricism draws on the German idealist tradition, particularly Hegel's critique of the Understanding (Verstand) as operating through fixed, external distinctions. It also reflects the nineteenth-century reaction against positivism in aesthetics, where figures like Fechner attempted to build an "empirical aesthetics" (Ästhetik von unten) based on experimental psychology.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's critique of the empirical is more radical than that of many of his contemporaries. Where some sought to combine philosophical and empirical approaches to aesthetics, Croce insists that empirical methods are constitutively incapable of reaching the aesthetic essence. The various empirical distinctions between artistic genres, styles, and techniques are "indifferent to later empirical discriminations" (002-346) — they do not affect the philosophical understanding of art as expression. This position flows from Croce's identification of intuition and expression: if every genuine intuition is an individual expression, then no empirical classification can capture its nature.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's blanket rejection of empirical aesthetics has drawn criticism from later scholars who argue that empirical methods, from psychology to computational analysis, can contribute genuine insights into aesthetic experience. Croce would respond that such methods study the conditions or effects of art, not art itself — the difference between art and non-art, when framed in merely extensive terms, remains "extensive and empirical" (003-048) and fails to reach the philosophical essence.
