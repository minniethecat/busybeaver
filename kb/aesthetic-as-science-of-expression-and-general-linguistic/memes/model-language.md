# Model Language

The model language (*lingua modello*) is, in Croce's Æsthetic, a philosophical illusion — the idea that there exists or could exist a single, perfect language that would serve as the standard for all expression. Croce treats the search for a model language as a symptom of the intellectualist error: the attempt to reduce the endless variety of individual expression to a fixed conceptual scheme (019-325).

## Definition

"The search for a model language, or for a method of reducing linguistic usage to unity" (019-325), is how Croce characterises a recurring ambition in the history of thought about language. Whether it takes the form of a quest for the original Adamic language, a universal characteristic in the Leibnizian manner, or a logically perfect idiom, the model language project assumes that expression can be standardised — an assumption Croce flatly rejects.

## In Croce's System

### Expression Is Always Individual

Croce's philosophy of language rests on the identity of intuition and expression. Every genuine expression is the unique articulation of a unique intuition; there can be no duplicate, no synonym, no alternative formulation that says "the same thing" in different words. The model language project misunderstands this from the ground up. "To seek the model language, then, is to seek the immobile in a world where everything moves" (019-334).

### The Unity of Art and Language

Croce's subtitle — "Æsthetic as science of expression and general linguistic" — signals that for him, linguistics is aesthetics, and aesthetics is linguistics. Both study expression, and expression is always individual. A universal language would be a contradiction in terms: a language that is not the expression of anyone in particular, a linguistic form without æsthetic content.

### The Question Dismissed

In his summary of æsthetic concerns, Croce records "the dismissal of the question of the model language, or of the unity of the arts" (019-354) as a settled conclusion. The quest for a model language is a "false problem" (*falso problema*) — not because it is too difficult to solve, but because it is framed in terms that make solution impossible. Language is not a tool that can be perfected but an activity that is always already perfect as expression.

## Historical Context

### The Search for a Universal Language

The dream of a perfect language stretches from Dante's *De vulgari eloquentia* through Leibniz's *characteristica universalis* to the logical positivism of the early twentieth century. "Æsthetic judgement and the model language — Conclusion" (P02-157) identifies this as a persistent theme: the desire for a medium of thought that would transcend the ambiguities of natural language.

### Eighteenth-Century Language Theory

The eighteenth century saw a flowering of speculation about the origin and perfection of language — Condillac, Rousseau, Herder, and Hamann all contributed to a debate that took the model language as either an ideal to be pursued or a lost paradise to be lamented. Croce's dismissal cuts through this tradition by denying the premise that language can be evaluated against an external standard.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's "dismissal of the question of the model language" (019-354) is an application of his broader expressionism. Just as there is no ideal beauty, no concept of beauty, no intellectual intuition — so there is no model language. Each of these rejected concepts rests on the same error: substituting the conceptual universal for the æsthetic particular.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's dismissal of the model language may underestimate the pragmatic value of standardisation in actual linguistic practice. The existence of standard written languages, technical terminologies, and formal notations shows that "model languages" — in a weaker, non-metaphysical sense — do function and serve genuine communicative purposes (019-340).
>
> Contemporary linguistics and philosophy of language have moved beyond both Croce's expressionism and the rationalist model-language project, finding in the social and conventional nature of language a middle ground that Croce's individualist framework makes invisible.
