# Empiricism

In Croce's aesthetic, empiricism names the philosophical tendency to ground all knowledge in sensory experience, rejecting a priori principles and metaphysical speculation.

Croce acknowledges the contributions of empiricist aesthetics — particularly its critical response to rationalist dogmatism — yet judges it insufficient, because aesthetic intuition is not reducible to sensory data.

## Definition

Empiricism, in the aesthetic domain, is the view that aesthetic facts are to be explained by reference to experience — whether sensory, psychological, or historical — rather than by deduction from metaphysical first principles. Croce treats this tendency under the heading of "Neo-criticism and empiricism" (037-001), associating it with the broader positivist movement.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Empiricism and metaphysics represent, for Croce, the twin errors between which aesthetic theory must steer. Both fail to grasp the autonomy of the aesthetic fact, because both import standards from outside the aesthetic sphere.

The empiricist treats aesthetic intuition as if it were sense perception — as if the beautiful were a quality of objects rather than a success of expression. This confusion is evident in the psychological aesthetics surveyed under "Æsthetic psychologism and other recent tendencies" (P02-314).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **empiricism from positivism**. Positivism is empiricism hardened into a metaphysical doctrine — the claim that only empirical knowledge is genuine. Croce's critique of positivism is more severe, because positivism elevates a methodological preference into an ontological principle.

A second distinction concerns **British empiricism vs. German Neo-criticism**. Croce notes that "the most powerful intellects of the nation inclined towards empiricism" in England (038-1943).

The neo-Kantian movement "was powerless to make headway against hedonistic, psychological and moralistic views of the æsthetic fact, although it made every effort to save the concept of spirit from the invading rush of naturalism and materialism" (037-003).

In his historical survey, Croce identifies an "acute empiricism" running like a "tenuous golden rivulet" through the tradition — from Aristotle, through Vico, to Schleiermacher, Humboldt, and De Sanctis (038-035). This is not empiricism as a doctrine but as a scholarly disposition: the willingness to examine actual works rather than merely speculating about Beauty.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

British empiricism furnished foundational texts of modern aesthetics. Burke attempted to derive aesthetic categories from sensory causes; Hume brought skeptical empiricism to bear on aesthetic judgment. In the nineteenth century, Spencer, Bain, and Grant Allen developed evolutionary accounts of aesthetic experience.

In Germany, the Neo-critical movement represented by Kirchmann attempted to translate metaphysics into empirical psychology. Prior to Kant's *Critique of Judgment*, "æstheticians were divided into sceptics, dogmatics and empiricists" (038-1942).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of empiricism is notable for its balanced acknowledgment. He recognizes that the empirical tradition provided a necessary corrective to metaphysical excess and enriched the descriptive resources of aesthetic theory.

Where Croce innovates is in arguing that empiricism cannot provide a philosophy of art — because it lacks the resources to account for the unity, autonomy, and spirituality of the aesthetic fact.

His own position has been characterized "by so many for crude empiricism and positivism" (P03-123), but this is a misunderstanding: his philosophy is neither empirical nor metaphysical in the traditional senses.

## Criticism and Debate

> "The neo-critical or neo-Kantian movement was powerless to make headway against hedonistic, psychological and moralistic views of the æsthetic fact, although it made every effort to save the concept of spirit from the invading rush of naturalism and materialism." [037-003]

Critics have argued that Croce's dismissal of empirical methods is too sweeping. The cognitive science of art and the empirical sociology of taste have produced genuine knowledge that Croce's system has no resources to assimilate. Whether these findings are relevant to the philosophy of art — as opposed to its psychology — remains a live question.
