# Comic

In Croce's aesthetic theory, the comic (*il comico*) is not a distinct aesthetic category with its own essence but a particular modification of the general aesthetic fact — one of many varieties of expression that cannot be rigidly defined. The comic belongs to the family of pseudo-concepts that critics have attempted to elevate into permanent philosophical kinds, with only limited success.

## Definition

The comic has been defined in countless ways throughout history. Croce surveys these attempts and finds them all insufficient: "The comic has been defined as the displacement of the ordinary, the unexpected, the incongruous" (013-136). Each definition captures some instances of the comic but fails to cover others. The "manifold attempts to define the comic, from Hellenic times to our own" (013-163) have produced only partial descriptions, never a philosophical definition.

Croce's conclusion is that the comic, like the sublime, the tragic, and the tender, is not a philosophical category but a psychological one. It "boasts of containing in itself all the others" (013-162) — a claim that reveals the futility of trying to make one variety of aesthetic experience the master category for all others.

## In Croce's System

### The Pseudo-Concept Critique

Croce classifies the comic alongside the sublime, the tragic, and the humorous as aesthetic pseudo-concepts. These terms are useful in empirical criticism for describing the emotional tonality of particular works, but they have no place in philosophical aesthetics. "The definitions of the sublime, the comic, the humorous" (013-114) all suffer from the same defect: they attempt to freeze a fluid spectrum of aesthetic experience into fixed categories.

### The Continuum of Feeling

The varieties of aesthetic feeling — "tragic, comic, pathetic, ridiculous, melancholy, tragi-comic" (013-010) — form a continuous spectrum, not a set of discrete kinds. "Spring from the comic to the tragic and from the tragic to the comic" (013-129) is possible because these are not ontological divisions but descriptive conveniences. The same work may shift from comic to tragic within a single scene.

### The Pleasure of the Comic

Croce acknowledges the psychological reality of comic pleasure: "This is the pleasure of the comic" (013-152) — the sudden perception of incongruity resolved. But this pleasure is not unique to art; it occurs in everyday life when expectations are subverted. The philosophical error is to make this psychological phenomenon the defining essence of a class of artworks.

## Historical Context

The analysis of the comic begins with **Aristotle**'s lost second book of the *Poetics*, which promised a theory of comedy. **Hellenistic** and **Roman** rhetoricians developed theories of the ridiculous. In the modern era, **Hobbes** explained laughter as "sudden glory" arising from superiority; **Kant** defined laughter as "an affection arising from the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing"; **Schopenhauer** located the comic in the incongruity between concept and percept. Croce synthesizes these traditions by dissolving the question: the comic is not a kind of art but a modification of the one aesthetic act.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's dissolution of the comic into the general aesthetic act may leave unanswered the question of why the comic *feels* so different from the tragic or the sublime. If all are modifications of the same underlying act of expression, what accounts for the qualitative distinctness of comic pleasure? Croce would likely respond that the difference is psychological and empirical — a matter for the science of psychology, not the philosophy of art.
