# Literature

The collective term for written artistic works, which in Croce's system carries no philosophical weight — literature is an empirical classification, not a spiritual category, and literary history must be kept rigorously separate from the act of æsthetic judgement (018-006, 018-068).

## Definition

Croce refuses to grant "literature" any standing as a distinct form of spiritual activity. The word designates a collection of expressive facts grouped for practical convenience, much as "the figurative arts" groups painting and sculpture. Literature is not a category of the spirit but a label of the library. The doctrine of literary kinds — tragedy, comedy, epic, lyric — is "the illegitimate division of expressions into various grades," known in literary study as the doctrine of rhetorical categories (010-056). These categories are "phantoms," empty abstractions that historians have mistaken for real entities (005-182).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Literature belongs entirely to the domain of intuition-expression, the first moment of theoretic activity. It shares its spiritual nature with every other form of art — a poem, a symphony, and a painting are all, philosophically, identically expressions. What distinguishes "literature" from "music" is not a difference in spiritual kind but a difference in the physical means of externalization: words versus sounds (016-073). Croce is particularly severe on attempts to build a science of literary kinds: "Such collections of precepts abound in all literatures," yet they never capture the living individuality of any genuine work (016-073).

### Key Distinctions

The distinction between the history of literature and literary criticism is fundamental to Croce's method. Historical criticism in literature — the reconstruction of the conditions under which a work was produced, the verification of sources, the establishment of chronology — is indispensable: "without tradition and historical criticism the enjoyment of all or nearly all the works of art produced by humanity would be irrevocably lost" (018-009). But this historical labour must never be confused with æsthetic judgement. We must "accurately distinguish the history of art and literature from the æsthetic judgement" — the former reconstructs facts, the latter evaluates expressions (018-068).

> "The history of art and literature has the works of art themselves as its principal subject; but these works, when viewed historically, are not expressions to be judged but facts to be explained" (018-074). The literary historian traces influences, documents reception, establishes chronologies; the critic judges beauty and ugliness. To conflate the two is the cardinal sin of literary scholarship.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The tradition of literary classification runs from Aristotle's *Poetics* through the Renaissance codifiers to the neo-classical rule-makers. Croce observes that "historians of art and literature" became "so much fascinated with these ideas of kinds, that they claimed to write the history, not of individual and real literary and artistic works, but of those empty phantoms, their kinds" (005-181). They portrayed "not the evolution of the artistic spirit, but the evolution of kinds" — an enterprise Croce dismisses as fundamentally misguided (005-184). The rhetorical tradition, from Cicero through Quintilian to the Renaissance, elaborated ever-finer grids of tropes and figures, all of which Croce dismisses as pseudo-science.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's radical simplification is to deny that literature has any internal structure whatsoever from the standpoint of the philosophy of spirit. Literary works are intuitions-expressions; their grouping by genre, period, or language is a matter of empirical convenience, not conceptual necessity. This does not mean literary history is worthless — on the contrary, Croce himself was a practicing literary historian — but it means that literary history can never ground an æsthetic theory. The critic who judges a work by its conformity to a genre has confused the empirical with the philosophical, substituting "reflexion on the psychology of the author and history of culture" for genuine æsthetic judgement (034-149).

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's rejection of genre has been both influential and controversial. Later theorists, from the Russian Formalists to Northrop Frye, have argued that genre is not an external rule imposed on works but an internal logic that works themselves generate and transform. The genre, on this view, is not a phantom but a living tradition — a set of expectations that the artist adopts, modifies, or subverts. Croce's response would be that the identification of such patterns is a legitimate empirical exercise but tells us nothing about the essence of art.
