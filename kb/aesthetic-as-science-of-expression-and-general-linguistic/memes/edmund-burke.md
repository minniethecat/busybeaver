# Edmund Burke

Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was a British statesman and philosopher whose *Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757) established an empirical, sensationalist aesthetics. Croce discusses Burke in the context of "eclecticism and sensationalism" in 18th-century British thought (P02-217).

## Biography

Born in Dublin and educated at Trinity College, Burke moved to London, where he became a leading Whig politician and prolific writer. His aesthetic treatise, published when he was 27, was his only dedicated contribution to the philosophy of art.

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Empirical Aesthetics**: Burke grounded the sublime and beautiful in physiological and psychological responses rather than in metaphysical or rational principles. The sublime arises from terror and astonishment moderated by distance; the beautiful from pleasure associated with sociability and love
- **The Sublime versus the Beautiful**: Burke's key distinction — the sublime (vast, obscure, powerful, terrifying) as fundamentally different from the beautiful (small, smooth, delicate, lovable) — structured aesthetic discourse for generations, though Croce dissolves this distinction (P02-217)
- **Sensationalism and Eclecticism**: Croce identifies Burke as a leading representative of the sensationalist tradition that reduces aesthetic quality to psychological effects, a framework Croce systematically critiques
