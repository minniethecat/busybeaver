# Allegory

In Croce's aesthetic, allegory is the paradigmatic case of a failed aesthetic mode — the attempt to make art serve conceptual thought by attaching abstract meanings to concrete images, which Croce diagnoses as "science, or art aping science" rather than genuine artistic intuition (005-078, P02-037).

## Definition

Allegory, for Croce, is not a legitimate genre of art but a confusion between two distinct spiritual activities: the intuitive (aesthetic) and the conceptual (logical). When a poet constructs an allegory — as Tasso did in *Gerusalemme Liberata*, where characters represent abstract virtues — they are not producing an authentic intuition but rather "an abstract concept" dressed in sensuous clothing (005-078). The allegorical image is not intuited for its own sake but serves as a sign pointing to something beyond itself. Croce's critique is radical: "that allegory that arrives at the end of a long poetic work" (005-085) is not a supplement to the poetry but a negation of it, subordinating the living intuition to a dead intellectual schema.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Allegory occupies an unstable position at the boundary between intuition and concept. In the fourfold structure of spirit, the production of abstract concepts belongs to the logical moment, while the production of expressive images belongs to the aesthetic moment. Allegory attempts to fuse these into a single act, but the fusion is inherently unstable: "in the latter the particular is a mere sign of the universal" (028-388). The allegorical image is degraded to a mere vehicle; its sensuous particularity — which is the very substance of aesthetic intuition — becomes incidental to the intellectual content it conveys. This is why Croce judges allegory to be "cold" (010-118): it lacks the living warmth of genuine intuition.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes allegory sharply from **symbol**. While symbol and allegory are often conflated in critical discourse — Croce notes the "criticism of the symbol and of the allegory" (005-068) as a single topic — they represent opposite tendencies. The symbol, in Croce's analysis, is a genuine aesthetic achievement: the concrete image that, without ceasing to be concrete, resonates with universal significance. Allegory, by contrast, starts from the abstract concept and fabricates an image to illustrate it. Croce also separates allegory from **metaphor**: the metaphor is a spontaneous, intuitive identification of two distinct images, while allegory is a calculated, intellectual construction. Finally, Croce distinguishes allegorical interpretation — the medieval practice of reading Christian doctrine into pagan texts — from allegorical composition: both substitute the conceptual for the intuitive, but the former is a critical error, the latter an artistic one.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Allegory has a long and prestigious history in Western aesthetics. In antiquity, the allegorical interpretation of Homer — reading the gods as personified natural forces or moral qualities — was already practiced by the Sophists and Stoics. In the Middle Ages, allegory was elevated to a theological method: Dante's *Divine Comedy* was read on four levels, the allegorical being one. The Renaissance saw "enthusiastic partisans of allegory" (023-015) who defended poetry's moral worth by claiming that its pleasing fictions concealed philosophical truths. In German aesthetics, thinkers such as Winckelmann continued to defend allegory as a noble mode of artistic expression — "he could not be content with imitation; allegory too is needed" (025-122).

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's critique of allegory is more radical than any before him. Earlier critics — including Goethe and the Romantics — had distinguished "good" symbol from "bad" allegory, preserving a space for legitimate symbolic art while rejecting mechanical allegory. Croce goes further: he condemns allegory as "among the most decadent forms of artistic expression" (025-300) and treats the entire allegorical tradition as a sustained confusion between art and philosophy. His innovation is to diagnose allegory not as a defective *kind* of art but as a *non-artistic* act — an intellectual operation that mistakenly presents itself as an aesthetic one. The "allegory of intellect" (031-032) that reduces philosophical positions to "a fantastic allegory, as diabolic" (031-039) is not bad philosophy presented as art; it is philosophy that has mistaken itself for art, and art that has been sacrificed to philosophy.

## Criticism and Debate

> "That picture which is but a cold allegory — it is not art, because it does not arise from a single, vivid intuition but from the meeting of an abstract concept with an arbitrary image" (010-118). Croce's dismissal of allegory as "cold" has been challenged by defenders who point to the enduring power of allegorical works from Dante to Kafka.

Critics argue that Croce's absolute opposition between intuition and concept is too rigid: many works that Croce would call allegorical possess undeniable aesthetic power, suggesting that conceptual content can be fused with intuitive form in ways that Croce's system cannot accommodate. Defenders of Croce respond that such works succeed *despite* their allegorical intentions, not because of them — that the living intuitions in Dante or Kafka overwhelm the allegorical schema, and it is those intuitions, not the schema, that constitute their aesthetic value.
