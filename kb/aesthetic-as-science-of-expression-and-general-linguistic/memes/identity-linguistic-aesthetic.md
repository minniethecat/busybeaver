# Identity of Linguistic and Æsthetic

The identity of linguistic and aesthetic is one of Croce's most radical and far-reaching theses. It asserts that the science of language and the science of art are not merely related but fundamentally identical: both study expression, and expression is one.

## Definition

Croce states the thesis in its strongest form: "Identity of linguistic and Æsthetic" (019-076). Linguistics is not a special science alongside aesthetics; it is aesthetics itself, considered from a particular angle. "If, on the other hand, linguistic were a special science, it would have to be distinguished from æsthetic by possessing a special and irreducible form of the spirit to rest upon" — but it has no such form (019-093).

The consequence is that "the æsthetic formulation of linguistic problems" is the only correct formulation. All properly linguistic questions are aesthetic questions, and all aesthetic questions have linguistic implications (019-097).

## In Croce's System

### The Unity of Expression

The identity thesis follows directly from Croce's identification of intuition with expression. If every genuine intuition is also expression, and if language is the general faculty of expression, then aesthetics (the science of intuition-expression) and linguistics (the science of language) share the same object. There is no expression that is not linguistic, and there is no language that is not expressive.

### Key Distinctions

Croce's identity thesis requires several negative corollaries:

1. **Against linguistic associationism**. "A variant of linguistic associationism is the imitation theory" — the claim that language is learned through imitation. For Croce, this mistakes the physical stimulus for the spiritual reality (019-130).

2. **Against grammatical categories**. The division of language into parts of speech — "noun or substantive" — destroys "the sole linguistic reality, which is the sentence" (019-193, 019-200). Grammar is a technique, not a science.

3. **Against elementary linguistic facts**. Those "who go in search of elementary linguistic facts, decorating with that name physical with linguistic or expressive facts," are looking in the wrong place (019-288, 019-297). "The simplest linguistic facts" are always already sentences — complete expressions (019-300).

### The Sentence as Linguistic Reality

The sentence, not the word, is the fundamental unit of language. Words are abstractions from sentences, and sentences are complete expressions. This is why linguistics cannot be a natural science that decomposes language into elements — each expression is an individual and indivisible whole.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The relationship between language and art had been explored by the German tradition, particularly Humboldt and Steinthal. Humboldt recognized the creative character of language, and Steinthal developed "the linguistic function independent of logic," treating language as a distinct spiritual activity (P02-266). However, neither fully identified linguistics with aesthetics.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to make the identity thesis absolute. Where Humboldt saw language as a bridge between logic and art, and Steinthal saw it as a third spiritual function, Croce collapses the distinction entirely. "The philosophy of language and the philosophy of art are the same thing" — not because language is a kind of art, but because both are expression, and expression is one.

The decline of linguistics in the late nineteenth century — "the decline of linguistic" that Croce observes — is, in his view, a consequence of linguistics losing its philosophical foundation and becoming a merely empirical science of classifications (P02-311). The "signs of revival" in the work of Hermann Paul and the "linguistic of Wundt" were attempts to restore this foundation, but only the identification with aesthetics could provide it (P02-312).

## Criticism and Debate

> The identity thesis has been described as Croce's most provocative claim about language — and his most vulnerable (019-076).

Critics have argued that Croce's identity thesis conflates two distinct domains. Language has communicative, logical, and social functions that are not reducible to aesthetic expression. A scientific paper and a sonnet are both expressions, but treating them as instances of the same spiritual activity obscures more than it reveals. Croce's response is that the communicative function is secondary: expression is primary, and communication is merely the "technique of linguistic" — a practical activity that presupposes expression (019-262).
