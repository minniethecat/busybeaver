# Language

Language (*linguaggio*) in Benedetto Croce's philosophy is not a mere instrument of communication or a system of conventional signs — it is identical with aesthetic expression itself. This is one of Croce's most radical and distinctive theses: the science of art (Æsthetic) and the science of language (General Linguistic) are not two distinct disciplines but one and the same. The subtitle of Croce's *Æsthetic* — "As Science of Expression and General Linguistic" — declares this identity from the outset.

## Definition

Croce states the identity thesis with unambiguous force in the concluding chapter of the *Æsthetic*:

> The science of art and that of language, Æsthetic and Linguistic, conceived as true sciences, are not two distinct things, but one thing only. Not that there is a special Linguistic; but the much-sought-for science of language, general Linguistic, *in so far as what it contains is reducible to philosophy,* is nothing but Æsthetic. Whoever studies general Linguistic, that is to say, philosophical Linguistic, studies æsthetic problems, and *vice versa. Philosophy of language and philosophy of art are the same thing.* (018-... )

The argument rests on two premises already established in Croce's system. First, **language is expression**: "an emission of sounds which expresses nothing is not language. Language is sound articulated, circumscribed and organized for the purposes of expression" (018-... ). Second, **there are no special classes of expression** — if expression is indivisible (as Croce demonstrated in his critique of Rhetoric), then there can be no distinct category of "linguistic" expressions separate from "artistic" ones.

| Aspect | Traditional View | Croce's View |
|--------|-----------------|--------------|
| Relationship of language and art | Separate domains | Identity — both are expression |
| Nature of language | Conventional sign system | Spiritual activity of forming expressive images |
| General Linguistic | Empirical or historical science | Philosophical science = Æsthetic |
| Origin of language | Historical/evolutionary question | Spiritual necessity — expression is coeval with spirit |
| Grammar | Normative rules | Empirical classifications with no philosophical foundation |

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Language belongs entirely to the domain of **intuition-expression** — the first tier of Croce's fourfold spiritual activity. It is theoretical (not practical), individual (not universal), and precedes any conceptual or logical operation. When we speak, we do not first think a concept and then "clothe" it in words; rather, the thought exists only as and through its linguistic expression:

> Language is not an instrument which man has forged for himself, but it is the very activity of the human spirit, which manifests itself as language. (018-... )

This places language at the foundation of all spiritual life, co-original with art. Before the intellect can abstract a universal concept, intuition must form an expressive image — and that forming is already linguistic. The child learning to speak, the poet composing a sonnet, and the ordinary speaker uttering a sentence are all engaged in the same fundamental spiritual act.

### Key Distinctions

**Language vs Grammar.** Grammar, for Croce, is an empirical discipline — a collection of observations about linguistic usage that has been mistakenly elevated to the status of a normative science. Just as Rhetoric attempted (and failed) to classify expressions into fixed categories, so grammar attempts to codify language into rules. Both are "didactic organisms" with practical utility but no philosophical truth.

**Language vs Logic.** The long tradition from Aristotle to modern logic has treated language as the "clothing" of thought, with logic studying the underlying structure and grammar studying the surface form. Croce rejects this dualism entirely: there is no pre-linguistic thought to be clothed. Expression — and therefore language — is the primary and irreducible form of spiritual activity.

**Language vs Communication.** Language is commonly understood as a tool for transmitting pre-existing thoughts from one mind to another. Croce reverses this: expression is not communication of something already formed; it is the very act of formation. "He who separates intuition from expression never succeeds in reuniting them" — and similarly, he who separates thought from language never recovers their unity.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The philosophical study of language before Croce was dominated by two traditions. The **empiricist tradition** (from Locke to the nineteenth-century comparative grammarians) treated language as a system of conventional signs whose origin could be traced through historical reconstruction. The **rationalist tradition** (from the Port-Royal grammarians to Humboldt) sought universal grammatical structures grounded in the laws of thought.

**Wilhelm von Humboldt** came closest to Croce's position. Humboldt recognized language not as a product (*ergon*) but as an activity (*energeia*), and he understood linguistic diversity as the manifestation of different spiritual perspectives on reality. Croce acknowledges Humboldt as a precursor, praising his insight that "language is not a work but an activity" (018-... ). However, Humboldt did not go far enough: he retained the distinction between inner linguistic form and outer phonetic form, which Croce rejects as another version of the intuition-expression dualism.

**H. Steinthal**, a nineteenth-century linguist influenced by Humboldt and Herbartian psychology, attempted to establish linguistics as an independent science of the "linguistic function." Croce argues that Steinthal's project, despite its sophistication, ultimately fails because it cannot identify a distinct object for linguistics that is not already covered by aesthetics.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is to take the identity thesis to its logical extreme. Where Humboldt saw language and art as parallel manifestations of spirit, Croce sees them as **identical** — not two species of the same genus, but the same thing described from different angles. This has far-reaching consequences:

1. **Aesthetics is liberated** from its confinement to "fine art" and recognized as co-extensive with all expressive activity.
2. **Linguistics is elevated** from an empirical science to a philosophical one — it becomes part of the Philosophy of Spirit.
3. **The problem of the origin of language is dissolved**: language did not "originate" at some point in history because expression is a necessary and eternal category of spirit. "The origin of language must every day take place *ex novo* in the spirit of every man" (018-... ).

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's identity thesis has provoked sharp criticism from both philosophers and linguists. The most common objection is that it obliterates distinctions that are practically and theoretically indispensable: a sonnet by Petrarch and a grocery list are both "expressions," but treating them as instances of the same spiritual activity seems to miss something essential about the *quality* of artistic expression as distinct from ordinary language.

A related criticism concerns the status of **linguistic convention**. If every expression is a unique, indivisible spiritual act, how do we account for the systematic, rule-governed character of natural languages? Croce's answer — that rules are merely empirical generalizations with no philosophical standing — may satisfy the idealist but leaves little room for the structural properties of language that twentieth-century linguistics would make central to the discipline.
