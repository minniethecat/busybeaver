[022-001] *New words and new observations in the seventeenth century*

[022-002] Interest in æsthetic investigation increased rapidly in the early years

[022-003] of the following century, owing either to the popularity acquired by

[022-004] certain new words or to the novel meanings given to words already

[022-005] familiar, which emphasized new aspects of artistic production and

[022-006] criticism, complicating the problem and rendering it thereby more

[022-007] puzzling and attractive. For example: wit, taste, imagination or [[imagination|fancy]],

[022-008] feeling, and several others, which must be examined rather closely.

[022-009] Wit (*ingegno*) differed somewhat from intellect. Free use of the word

[022-010] arose, if we mistake not, from its convenience in [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] as conceived

[022-011] by antiquity; that is to say, a suave and facile mode of knowledge, as

[022-012] opposed to the severity of Dialectic; an "Antistrophe to Dialectic,"

[022-013] which substituted for reasons of actual fact those of probability or

[022-014] fancy; enthymemes for syllogisms, examples for inductions; so much

[022-015] so that Zeno the Stoic figured Dialectic with her fist clenched and

[022-016] Rhetoric with her hand open. The empty style of the decadent Italian

[022-017] authors in the seventeenth century found its complete justification

[022-018] in this theory of rhetoric; their prose and verse, Marinesque and

[022-019] Achillinesque, professed to exhibit not the true but the striking,

[022-020] subtly conceited, curious or nice. The word wit, *ingegno,* was now

[022-021] repeated much more frequently than in the preceding century; wit

[022-022] was hailed as presiding genius of Rhetoric; its "vivacities" were

[022-023] lauded to the skies; "*belli ingegni*" was a phrase seized upon by

[022-024] the [[france|French]], who rendered it as "*esprit*" or "*beaux esprits*."[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_1_77)

[022-025] One of the most noteworthy commentators on these matters (although

[022-026] opposed to the literary excesses of the times), Matteo Pellegrini

[022-027] of Bologna (1650), defines wit as "that part of the soul which in

[022-028] a certain way practises, aims, and seeks to find and create the

[022-029] beautiful and the efficacious";[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_2_78) he considers the work of "wit" to

[022-030] be the "conceits" and "subtleties" noted by him in a previous pamphlet

[022-031] (1639).[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_3_79) Emmanuele Tesauro also descants at considerable length

[022-032] in his *Cannochiale Aristotelico* (1654) upon wit and subtleties,

[022-033] not alone "verbal" and "lapidary" conceits, but also "symbolic" and

[022-034] "figurative" (statues, stories, devices, satires, hieroglyphs, mosaics,

[022-035] emblems, insignia, sceptres), and even "animated agents" (pantomimes,

[022-036] play-scenes, masques and dances): all things which may be grouped under

[022-037] "polite quibbling" or rhetoric as distinct from "dialectic."

[022-038] Amongst such treatises, product of their age, one written by the

[022-039] Spaniard Baltasar Gracian (1642) became celebrated throughout

[022-040] Europe.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_4_80) Wit became in his hands the strictly inventive or artistic

[022-041] faculty, "genius"; *génie,* "genius" were now used as synonyms of

[022-042] wit, *ingegno* and *esprit.* In the following century Mario Pagano[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_5_81)

[022-043] wrote: "Wit may be taken as equivalent to the *génie* of the French, a

[022-044] word now commonly used in [[italy|Italy]]." To return to the seventeenth century,

[022-045] Bouhours, a Jesuit writer of dialogues on the *Manière de bien penser

[022-046] dans les ouvrages d'esprit* (1687), says that "'heart' and 'wit' are

[022-047] greatly in fashion just now, nothing else is spoken of in polite

[022-048] conversation, and all discourse is at last brought round to *l'esprit

[022-049] et le cœur.*"[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_6_82)

[022-050] *[[taste|Taste]].*

[022-051] The word *taste* or *good taste* was equally widespread and

[022-052] fashionable, signifying the faculty of judgement brought to bear

[022-053] on [[beauty|the beautiful]], distinct to some extent from intellectual power,

[022-054] and sometimes divided into active and passive, so that it was usual

[022-055] to speak of one kind of taste as "productive" or "fertile" (thus

[022-056] coinciding with "wit"), and of another as "sterile."

[022-057] *Various meanings of the word taste.*

[022-058] From the rough notes which we possess as to the history of the concept

[022-059] of taste, several meanings of the word, not all of equal importance

[022-060] as indications of the development of ideas, detach themselves in a

[022-061] somewhat confused manner. "Taste," meaning "pleasure" or "delight," was

[022-062] an old-established word in Italy and Spain, as is shown in such phrases

[022-063] as "to have a taste for, to be to one's taste"; when Lope di Vega

[022-064] and other Spaniards speak continually of the drama of their country

[022-065] as seeking to please the popular taste ("*deleita el gusto*"; "*para

[022-066] darle gusto*") they mean only the "pleasure" of the populace. In Italy

[022-067] there was a very ancient use of the word in the metaphorical sense

[022-068] of "judgement," either literary, scientific, or artistic; numberless

[022-069] examples of this use occur in writers of the sixteenth century

[022-070] (Ariosto, Varchi, Michæl Angelo, Tasso). To take but one of these: the

[022-071] lines in *Orlando Furioso* where it is said of the Emperor Augustus,

[022-072] "*L' aver avuto in poesia buon gusto La proscrizione iniqua gli

[022-073] perdona,*" "For having had good taste in poetry he shall be forgiven

[022-074] his iniquitous proscriptions"; or the remark of Ludovico Dolce that'

[022-075] some person "had such exquisite taste, he sang no verses save those of

[022-076] Catullus and Calvus."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_7_83) The word "taste," in the sense of a special

[022-077] faculty or attitude of mind, appears to have been used for the first

[022-078] time in Spain in the middle of the seventeenth century by Gracian,[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_8_84)

[022-079] the moralist and political writer already quoted. It is evidently to

[022-080] him that the Italian author Trevisano alludes in a preface to a book by

[022-081] Muratori (1708) when he speaks of "Spaniards, above all others cunning

[022-082] in metaphor," who express themselves in "that eloquent and laconic

[022-083] phrase, good taste"; touching further on taste and genius he quotes,

[022-084] "that ingenious Spaniard," Gracian,[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_9_85) who gave the word the sense of

[022-085] "practical wit," enabling one to perceive the "true signification" of

[022-086] things; his "man of good taste" becomes in our language "a man of tact"

[022-087] in the affairs of life.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_10_86)

[022-088] The transference of the word to the domain of æsthetic seems to have

[022-089] taken place in France during the last quarter of the century. "*Il y

[022-090] a dans l'art un point de perfection, comme de bonté ou de maturité

[022-091] dans la nature: celui qui le sent et qui l'aime a le goût parfait;

[022-092] celui qui ne le sent pas, et qui aime au deçà ou au delà, a le goût

[022-093] défectueux. Il y a donc un bon et un mauvais goût, et l'on dispute des

[022-094] goûts avec fondement,*" writes La Bruyère[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_11_87) (1688). As attributes

[022-095] or variants of taste it was usual to mention *delicacy* and *variety*

[022-096] or *variability.* Bearing its fresh critical—literary content,

[022-097] but not freed from the encumbrance of its earlier practical and

[022-098] moral significance, the word spread from France into other European

[022-099] countries. Thomasius introduced it into [[germany|Germany]] in 1687;[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_12_88) and in

[022-100] [[england|England]] it becomes "good taste." In Italy it appears as early as 1696

[022-101] as title of a large book written by Camillo Ettori, the Jesuit, *Il

[022-102] buon gusto ne' componimenti rettorici*.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_13_89) The preface notes: "The

[022-103] expression 'good taste,' proper to those who rightly distinguish good

[022-104] from bad flavour in foods, is now in general use and claimed by every

[022-105] one as a title in connexion with literature and the humanities"; it

[022-106] reappears in 1708 at the beginning of Muratori's[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_14_90) book already

[022-107] quoted: Trevisano treats of it philosophically: Salvini discusses it

[022-108] in his note upon the *Perfetta Poesia* of Muratori above mentioned,

[022-109] where the subject of good taste occupies several pages,[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_15_91) and finally

[022-110] it gives its name to the Academy of Good Taste founded at Palermo in

[022-111] 1718. [[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_16_92) Scholars of the day who took up the discussion of the theme,

[022-112] recollecting some passages scattered throughout the ancient classics,

[022-113] placed the new concept in relation with the "*tacitus quidam sensus

[022-114] sine ulla ratione et arte*" of Cicero; and with the "*indicium*" which

[022-115] "*nec magis arte traditur quam gustus aut odor*" of Quintilian.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_17_93)

[022-116] More particularly Montfaucon de Villars (1671)[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_18_94) wrote a book on

[022-117] "Delicacy"; Ettori strove to find some definition more satisfactory

[022-118] than those current at the time (*e.g.* "it is the finest invention

[022-119] of wit, the flower of wit and extract of beauty's self," and similar

[022-120] conceits);[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_19_95) Orsi made it the subject of his *Considerazioni* written

[022-121] in reply to Bouhours' book.

[022-122] *Fancy or Imagination.*

[022-123] In Italy in the seventeenth century we find imagination or fancy

[022-124] placed on a pinnacle. What do you mean by talking of probability

[022-125] and historical truth (asks Cardinal Sforza Pallavicino in 1644), of

[022-126] false or true in connexion with poetry; which deals not with fiction,

[022-127] fact or historical probability but with primary apprehensions which

[022-128] assert neither truth nor falsehood? Following this line of argument,

[022-129] imagination takes the place of that probable, neither true nor false,

[022-130] advocated by some commentators of Aristotle; a theory strongly

[022-131] criticized by Pallavicino, here agreeing with Piccolomini, whom however

[022-132] he does not name, and in opposition to Castelvetro whom he explicitly

[022-133] mentions. He who goes to the play (continues Pallavicino) knows quite

[022-134] well that the scenes acted on the stage are not real; although he has

[022-135] no belief in them yet they please him greatly. For "if poetry desired

[022-136] to be mistaken for truth, the end she had in view would be a he, by

[022-137] the laws of nature and of God doomed inevitably to perish: for a lie

[022-138] is nothing but an untruth uttered in the hope that it may be mistaken

[022-139] for truth. How then should an art so tainted be allowed to flourish in

[022-140] the best-regulated republics? How should it be commended and used by

[022-141] the very writers of Holy Scripture?" *Ut pictura poësis*: poetry is

[022-142] like painting, which is a "diligent imitation" aiming at a close copy

[022-143] of the features, colours, acts, nay, even the hidden motives, of the

[022-144] objects it represents: and it "does not pretend that fiction is truth."

[022-145] The sole aim of poetic tales is "to adorn our understanding with

[022-146] imagery, that is to say, with sumptuous, novel, marvellous and splendid

[022-147] appearances. And this is known to diffuse so useful an influence on

[022-148] mankind that humanity insists on rewarding poets with praise more

[022-149] glorious than is bestowed on any other men; their books are protected

[022-150] from the ravages of time with greater solicitude than is shown to

[022-151] scientific treatises or productions of any other art; in the end the

[022-152] names of poets are crowned with adoring veneration. See how the world

[022-153] thirsts for beautiful first apprehensions, although these are neither

[022-154] laden with science nor are they vehicles of truth."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_20_96)

[022-155] Sixty years later these ideas, although expressed by a Cardinal, seemed

[022-156] all too daring to Muratori, who could not bring himself to allow poets

[022-157] so much latitude, or to enfranchize them from their obligations to the

[022-158] probable. Nevertheless Muratori allows a large space to imagination,

[022-159] "an inferior apprehensive faculty" which, without caring whether

[022-160] things be false or true, confines itself to apprehending them, and

[022-161] "represents" the truth merely, leaving the task of "cognition" to the

[022-162] "superior apprehensive faculty" or intellect.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_21_97) Even the stony heart

[022-163] of Gravina yields to the charm of imagination: he admits it occupies

[022-164] a considerable place in the realm of poetry and suffers his own arid

[022-165] prose to describe it as "a sorceress, but beneficent," "a delirium

[022-166] which cures madness."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_22_98)

[022-167] Earlier than either of these, Ettori commended it to the good

[022-168] rhetorician, "who in order that he may awaken images" must "familiarize

[022-169] himself with whatever is subject to bodily feeling" and "encounter

[022-170] the genius of imagination, which is a sensuous faculty," to these

[022-171] ends using "species rather than genera (since the latter, being more

[022-172] universal than the former, are less sensible), individuals rather than

[022-173] species, effects than causes, the number of the greater rather than the

[022-174] number of the less."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_23_99)

[022-175] As far back as 1578 the Spaniard Huarte had maintained that eloquence

[022-176] is the product of imagination rather than of intellect or reason.[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_24_100)

[022-177] In England Bacon (1605) ascribed science to intellect, history to

[022-178] memory and poetry to imagination or fancy:[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_25_101) Hobbes inquired into

[022-179] the procedure of poetry:[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_26_102) Addison (1712) devoted several numbers

[022-180] of his *Spectator* to analysis of the "pleasures of imagination."[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_27_103)

[022-181] Somewhat later, the importance of imagination was felt in Germany,

[022-182] where it found advocates in Bodmer, Breitinger and other writers of the

[022-183] Swiss school, who owed much to the influence of the Italians (Muratori,

[022-184] Gravina, Calepio) and the English: acting in their turn as teachers of

[022-185] Klopstock and the new German critical school.[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_28_104)

[022-186] *Feeling.*

[022-187] It was at this same period that opposition became clearly marked

[022-188] between those accustomed "*à juger par le sentiment*" and those used

[022-189] to "*raisonner par principes*."[29] The Frenchman, Du Bos, author of

[022-190] *Réflexions critiques sur la poésie et la peinture* (1719), upholds the

[022-191] theory of feeling; according to him art is simply a self-abandonment

[022-192] "*aux impressions que les objets étrangers font sur nous,*" setting

[022-193] aside all reflective labour. He laughs at those philosophers who

[022-194] deny the force of imagination, and Malebranche's eloquent discourse

[022-195] founded on this denial draws from Du Bos the remark, "*c'est à notre

[022-196] imagination qu'il parle contre l'abus de l'imagination.*" He refuses

[022-197] to see any intellectual nucleus in the productions of the arts, saying

[022-198] that art consists not in instruction but in style: nor is he too

[022-199] respectful towards the probable: he says he finds himself unable to

[022-200] set limits between it and the marvellous, and leaves to "born poets"

[022-201] the task of thus miraculously uniting opposites. For Du Bos there is

[022-202] no criterion of art save feeling, which he calls a "*sixième sens,*"

[022-203] against which dispute is vain since in such matters popular opinion

[022-204] invariably wins the day over the dogmatic pronouncements of artists

[022-205] and men of letters: all the ingenious conceits of the greatest

[022-206] metaphysicians, though unimpeachable in themselves, will not in the

[022-207] slightest degree diminish the lustre of poetry or despoil it of one

[022-208] single attraction. Attempts to discredit Ariosto and Tasso in the eyes

[022-209] of Italians were as vain as those made against the *Cid* in France.

[022-210] Other people's arguments can never persuade us of the contrary of

[022-211] what we feel.[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_29_105) These notions were adopted by many French writers:

[022-212] for example Cartaut de la Villate[30] observes, "*Le grand talent

[022-213] d'un écrivain qui veut plaire, est de tourner ses réflexions en

[022-214] sentiments*;" and Trublet, "*C'est un principe sûr, que la poésie doit

[022-215] être une expression de sentiment.*"[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_30_106) Nor were the English slow in

[022-216] emphasizing the concept of "emotion" in their theories of literature.

[022-217] *Tendency to unite these terms.*

[022-218] In the writings of this period *imagination* was often identified with

[022-219] *wit, wit* with *taste, taste* with *feeling,* and *feeling* with

[022-220] first apprehensions or *imagination*;[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_31_107) we have already noted that

[022-221] taste is sometimes critical and sometimes productive: this fusion,

[022-222] identification and subordination of terms apparently distinct shows how

[022-223] they gravitate round one single concept.

[022-224] *Difficulties and contradictions in their definition.*

[022-225] A German critic, one of the very few who have sought to penetrate

[022-226] the darkness surrounding the origins of modern Æsthetic, considers

[022-227] the concept of taste (which we owe, he thinks, to Gracian) "the

[022-228] most important æsthetic doctrine which remained for modern times to

[022-229] discover."[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_32_108) But without going so far as to say that taste is the

[022-230] chief doctrine of the science, and the foundation of all the rest,

[022-231] instead of only a particular doctrine, and without recapitulating what

[022-232] we have already said of Gracian's relation to the theory of taste,

[022-233] it is well to repeat that taste, wit, imagination, feeling, and so

[022-234] on, instead of new concepts scientifically grasped, were simply new

[022-235] words corresponding to vague impressions: at most they were problems,

[022-236] not concepts: apprehensions of ground still to be conquered, not yet

[022-237] annexed and brought into subjection. It must not be forgotten that the

[022-238] very men who made use of these terms could scarcely grope after the

[022-239] ideas they suggested without falling back into the old traditions, the

[022-240] only ones on which they had an intellectual grasp. To them the new

[022-241] words were shades, not bodies: when they tried to embrace them their

[022-242] arms returned empty to their own breasts.

[022-243] *Wit and intellect.*

[022-244] Certainly wit differs to a certain extent from intellect. Yet

[022-245] Pellegrini and Tesauro, with other writers of treatises, never fail to

[022-246] point out that intellectual truth lies at the root of wit. Trevisano

[022-247] defines it as "an internal virtue of the soul which invents methods

[022-248] for expressing and executing its own concepts: it is recognizable now

[022-249] in the arrangement of things we invent, now in the clear expression

[022-250] of them: sometimes in cunning reconciliations of matters seemingly

[022-251] opposed, sometimes in tracing analogies but faintly discernible." To

[022-252] sum up, one must not "allow the actions of wit to go unaccompanied

[022-253] by those of intellect," or even by those of practical morality.[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_33_109)

[022-254] More ingenuously Muratori says, "Wit is that virtue and active force

[022-255] with which the intellect is able to assemble, unite and discover the

[022-256] similarities, relations and reasons of things."[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_34_110) In this manner wit,

[022-257] after having been distinguished from intellect, eventually becomes a

[022-258] part or a manifestation of it. By a somewhat different path the same

[022-259] conclusion is reached by Alexander Pope when he counsels that wit be

[022-260] reined in like a mettlesome horse, and observes:

[022-261] For wit and judgement often are at strife,

[022-262] Though meant each other's aid like man and wife.[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_35_111)

[022-263] *Taste and intellectual judgement.*

[022-264] Similar vicissitudes befell the word "taste," outcome of a metaphor

[022-265] (as was noted by [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]) whose effect was to stand in opposition

[022-266] to intellectualistic principles, as if to say that the judgement

[022-267] governing the choice of food destined solely for the delectation

[022-268] of the palate is of the same nature as that which decides opinions

[022-269] in matters of art.[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_36_112) Nevertheless, the very definition of this

[022-270] anti-intellectualistic concept contained a reference to intellect and

[022-271] reason; the implicit comparison with the palate was ultimately taken

[022-272] as signifying an anticipation of reflexion: as Voltaire wrote in the

[022-273] following century: "*De même que la sensation du palais anticipe

[022-274] la réflexion.*"[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_37_113) Intellect and reason glimmer through all the

[022-275] definitions of taste belonging to this period. Mme. Dacier wrote in

[022-276] 1684, "*Une harmonie, un accord de l'esprit et de la raison.*"[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_38_114)

[022-277] "*Une raison éclairée qui, d'intelligence avec le cœur, fait

[022-278] toujours un juste choix parmi des choses opposées ou semblables,*"

[022-279] wrote the author of *Entretiens galants.*[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_39_115) According to another

[022-280] writer quoted by Bonhours, "taste" is "a natural feeling implanted in

[022-281] the soul, independent of any science that can possibly be acquired"; it

[022-282] is practically "an instinct of right reason."[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_40_116) The same Bouhours,

[022-283] whilst deprecating this interpretation of one metaphor by another,

[022-284] says, "Taste is more nearly allied to judgement than wit."[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_41_117) The

[022-285] Italian Ettori thinks that it may generally be described as "judgement

[022-286] regulated by art,"[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_42_118) and Baruffaldi (1710) identifies it with

[022-287] "discernment" reduced from theory to practice.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_43_119) De Crousaz (1715)

[022-288] observes: "*Le bon goût nous fait d'abord estimer par sentiment ce que

[022-289] la raison aurait approuvé, après qu'elle se serait donné le temps de

[022-290] l'examiner assez pour en juger par des justes idées.*"[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-10.htm.html#Footnote_44_120) And somewhat

[022-291] prior to him Trevisano considered it "a sentiment always willing to

[022-292] conform to whatsoever reason accepts," and in conjunction with divine

[022-293] grace, a powerful help to man in revealing the true and good, no longer

[022-294] able to circulate freely among mankind owing to original sin. For

[022-295] König (1727) in Germany taste was "a power of the intellect, product

[022-296] of a healthy mind and acute judgement which makes one able to feel

[022-297] the true, good and beautiful"; and for Bodmer in 1736 (after lengthy

[022-298] correspondence on the subject with his Italian friend Calepio) "a

[022-299] practised reflexion, prompt and penetrating into the smallest details,

[022-300] by which intellect is able to distinguish the true from the false, the

[022-301] perfect from the imperfect." Calepio and Bodmer were opponents of pure

[022-302] feeling, and made a distinction between "taste" and "good taste."[45]

[022-303] Traversing the same intellectualistic path, Muratori speaks of "good

[022-304] taste" in "erudition" and others of "good taste in philosophy."

[022-305] *The "je ne sais quoi."*

[022-306] Perhaps those authors were wise who preferred to remain vague and to

[022-307] identify taste with an indefinable Something, a *je ne sais quoi*; a

[022-308] *nescio quid*: a new expression which expressed nothing new, but at

[022-309] least called attention to the problem. Bouhours (1671) discusses it at

[022-310] length: "*Les Italiens, qui font mystère de tout, emploient en toutes

[022-311] rencontres leur* non so che: *on ne voit rien de plus commune dans

[022-312] leurs poètes,*" and quotes Tasso and others in confirmation.[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_45_121) A

[022-313] note upon it is found in Salvini: "This 'good taste' has but recently

[022-314] come to the front; it seems a vague term applicable to nothing

[022-315] particular, and is equivalent to the *non so che,* to a happy or

[022-316] successful turn of wit."[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_46_122) Father Feijóo, who wrote on the *Razón

[022-317] del gusto* and on *El no se qué* (1733), says very wisely: "*En muchas

[022-318] producciones no solo de la naturaleza, sino del arte, y aun mas del

[022-319] arte que de la naturaleza, encuentran los hombres, fuera di aquellas

[022-320] perfecciones sujetes á su comprehension racional, otro genero de primor

[022-321] misterioso que, lisonjeando el gusto, atormenta el entendemento. Los

[022-322] sentidos le palpan, pero no le puede dissipar la razon, y así, al

[022-323] querer explicarle, no se encuentran voces ni conceptos que cuadren

[022-324] á su idea, y salimos del paso con decir que hay un non se qué, que

[022-325] agrada, que enamora que hechiza, sin que pueda encontrarse revelacion

[022-326] mas clara da este natural misterio.*"[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_47_123) And President Montesquieu:

[022-327] "*Il y a quelquefois dans les personnes ou dans les choses un charme

[022-328] invisible, une grâce naturelle, qu'on n'a pu définir, et qu'on a été

[022-329] forcé d'appeler le je ne sais quoi. Il me semble que c'est un effet

[022-330] principalement fondé sur la surprise.*"[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_48_124) Some writers rebelled

[022-331] against the subterfuge of the *je ne sais quoi,* saying, rightly

[022-332] enough, that it was a confession of ignorance: but they knew not how to

[022-333] escape that ignorance without falling into confusion between taste and

[022-334] intellectual judgement.

[022-335] *Imagination and sensationalism. The corrective of

[022-336] Imagination.*

[022-337] If the attempt to define "wit" and "taste" usually resulted in

[022-338] intellectualism, it was easy to transform imagination and feeling into

[022-339] sensationalistic doctrines. We have seen how earnestly Pallavicino

[022-340] insisted on the non-intellectuality of the fantasies and inventions

[022-341] of the imagination. "Nothing presents itself to the admirer of the

[022-342] beautiful (he writes) to enable him to verify his cognition and satisfy

[022-343] himself that the object recognized is or is not that for which he takes

[022-344] it; if either by vision or by strong apprehension he is led to think

[022-345] it actually present by an act of judgement, his taste for beauty as

[022-346] beauty does not arise from such act of judgement, but from the vision

[022-347] or lively apprehension which might remain in ourselves even when the

[022-348] deception of belief was corrected"; just as happens when we are drowsy

[022-349] and know ourselves to be but half awake, yet are unwilling to tear

[022-350] ourselves from sweet dreams. For Pallavicino imagination cannot err; he

[022-351] assimilates it wholly to the sensations, which are incapable of truth

[022-352] or falsity. And if imaginative knowledge pleases, it is not because

[022-353] it holds a special truth (imaginative truth), but because it creates

[022-354] objects which "though false are pleasing": the painter makes not

[022-355] likenesses but images which, all resemblance apart, are pleasing to the

[022-356] sight: the poet awakens apprehensions "sumptuous, novel, marvellous,

[022-357] splendid."[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_49_125) His opinion coincides, if we mistake not, with Marino's

[022-358] sensationalism: "The poet should aim only at the marvellous ... he who

[022-359] cannot amaze his hearers is not worth a straw":[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_50_126) he applauds the

[022-360] oft-repeated dictum of "Gabriel Chiabrera, that Pindar of Savona, that

[022-361] poetry should cause the eyebrows to arch themselves."[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_51_127) But in the

[022-362] *Treatise upon Style* written later (1646) he repents of his youthful

[022-363] achievement and appears willing to return to the pedagogic theory:

[022-364] "And forasmuch as I theorized concerning poetry in the basest manner,

[022-365] treating it solely as a minister of that delight which the mind enjoys

[022-366] in the less noble operation of imagination or apprehension arising

[022-367] from imagination; and, therefore, in consequence I somewhat relaxed the

[022-368] strings which bind it to the probable: I now wish to demonstrate that

[022-369] poetry has other functions more exalted and fruitful, while remaining

[022-370] in strict servitude to the probable: which office is to guide our

[022-371] minds in the noble exercise of judgement; thus it becomes the nurse of

[022-372] philosophy which it nourishes with sweet milk."[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_52_128) The Jesuit Ettori,

[022-373] while inculcating the use of imagination and recommending orators to go

[022-374] to school with the "actors," points out that imagination should fulfil

[022-375] the simple office of "interpreter" between intellect and truth, never

[022-376] assuming dominion, otherwise the orator would be treating his audience

[022-377] or readers "not as men, to whom intellect is proper, but as beasts whom

[022-378] imagination satisfies."[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_53_129)

[022-379] The conception of imagination as purely sensuous shows strongly in

[022-380] Muratori, who is so convinced that the faculty, if left to itself,

[022-381] would deteriorate into a riot of dreams and intoxication, that he links

[022-382] it to intellect as to "an authoritative friend" who shall influence

[022-383] the choice and combination of images.[[54]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_54_130) The problem of the nature of

[022-384] imagination had strong attraction for Muratori, and, while traducing

[022-385] and vilifying, he returns to it again in his *Della forza della

[022-386] fantasia umana*;[[55]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_55_131) describing it as a material faculty essentially

[022-387] different from the mental or spiritual, and denying it the validity of

[022-388] knowledge. Although he had observed that the aim of poetry is distinct

[022-389] from that of science, in that the latter seeks to "know," and the

[022-390] former to "represent" truth,[[56]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_56_132) he persisted in counting Poetry as an

[022-391] "art of delectation" subordinate to Moral Philosophy, of whom she was

[022-392] one of the three servants or ministers.[[57]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_57_133) Very similarly Gravina held

[022-393] that along with novelty and delight in the marvellous, poetry should

[022-394] endow the mind of the vulgar with "truth and universal cognitions."[[58]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_58_134)

[022-395] Outside Italy the same movement was going on. Bacon, although he

[022-396] assigned poetry to imagination, yet considered it as something

[022-397] intermediary between history and science, approximating epic to

[022-398] history and the most lofty style, the parabolic, to science: ("*poēsis

[022-399] parabolica inter reliquas eminet".*) Elsewhere he calls poetry

[022-400] *somnium* or declares absolutely that "*scientias fere non parit,*" and

[022-401] that "*pro lusu potius ingenii quam pro scientia est habenda*": music,

[022-402] painting and sculpture are voluptuous arts.[[59]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_59_135) Addison identified the

[022-403] pleasures of the imagination with those produced by visible objects or

[022-404] the ideas to which they give rise: such pleasures are not so strong as

[022-405] those of the senses nor so refined as those of the intellect: he groups

[022-406] together the pleasures experienced respectively in comparing imitations

[022-407] with the objects imitated, and in sharpening by this means the faculty

[022-408] of observation.[[60]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_60_136)

[022-409] [Sidenote *Feeling and Sensationalism.*]

[022-410] The sensationalism of Du Bos and other upholders of feeling appears

[022-411] very clearly. For Du Bos art is a pastime whose pleasantness consists

[022-412] in the fact that it occupies the mind without fatigue, and has

[022-413] affinities with the pleasure provoked by gladiatorial contests,

[022-414] bullfights and tourneys.[[61]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_61_137)

[022-415] For these reasons, whilst noting the importance, in the prehistory

[022-416] of Æsthetic, of these new words and the new views they express; and

[022-417] while recognizing their value as a ferment in the discussion of the

[022-418] æsthetic problem, taken up by thinkers of the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] at the point

[022-419] at which it had been left by the ancients; we yet cannot discern in

[022-420] their apparition the true origin of our science. By these words and the

[022-421] discussions they aroused, the æsthetic fact clamoured even louder and

[022-422] more insistently for its own philosophical justification; but this it

[022-423] was not yet to attain either by this means or by any other.
