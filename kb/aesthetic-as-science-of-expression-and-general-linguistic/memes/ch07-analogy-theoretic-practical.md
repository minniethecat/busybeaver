[007-001] The intuitive and intellectual forms contain between them, as we have

[007-002] said, the whole theoretic domain of the spirit. But it is not possible

[007-003] to know them thoroughly, nor to criticize another series of erroneous

[007-004] æsthetic theories, without first establishing clearly the relations of

[007-005] the theoretic spirit with the *practical* spirit.

[007-006] *The will.*

[007-007] The practical form or activity is the *will.* We do not here employ

[007-008] this word in the sense of some philosophical systems, where the will

[007-009] is the foundation of the universe, the ground of things and the true

[007-010] reality. Nor do we employ it in the wide sense of other systems,

[007-011] which understand by will the energy of the spirit, spirit or activity

[007-012] in general, making of every act of the human spirit an act of will.

[007-013] Neither such metaphysical nor such metaphorical meaning is ours. For

[007-014] us, the will is, as generally understood, that activity of the spirit

[007-015] which differs from the merely theoretical contemplation of things,

[007-016] and is productive, not of knowledge, but of actions. Action is really

[007-017] action, in so far as it is voluntary. It is not necessary to remark

[007-018] that in the will to do, we include, in the scientific sense, also what

[007-019] is usually called not-doing: the will to resist, to reject, the will of

[007-020] a Prometheus, which also is action.

[007-021] *The will as an ulterior stage in respect to knowledge.*

[007-022] Man understands things with the theoretical form, with the practical

[007-023] form he changes them; with the one he appropriates the universe, with

[007-024] the other he creates it. But the first form is the basis of the second;

[007-025] and the relation of *double degree,* which we have already found

[007-026] existing between æsthetic and logical activity, is repeated between

[007-027] these two on a larger scale. A knowing independent of the will is

[007-028] thinkable, at least in a certain sense; will independent of knowing is

[007-029] unthinkable. Blind will is not will; true will has eyes.

[007-030] How can we will, without having before us historical intuitions

[007-031] (perceptions) of objects, and knowledge of (logical) relations, which

[007-032] enlightens us as to the nature of those objects? How can we really

[007-033] will, if we do not know the world which surrounds us or how to change

[007-034] things by acting upon them?

[007-035] *Objections and explanations.*

[007-036] It has been objected that men of action, practical men *par

[007-037] excellence,* are the least disposed to contemplate and to theorize:

[007-038] their energy is not delayed in contemplation, it rushes at once into

[007-039] will. And conversely, that contemplative men, philosophers, are

[007-040] often very mediocre in practical matters, weak willed, and therefore

[007-041] neglected and thrust aside in the tumult of life. It is easy to

[007-042] see that these distinctions are merely empirical and quantitative.

[007-043] Certainly, the practical man has no need of a philosophical system in

[007-044] order to act, but in the spheres where he does act, he starts from

[007-045] intuitions and concepts which are perfectly clear to him. Otherwise the

[007-046] most ordinary actions could not be willed. It would not be possible

[007-047] to will to feed oneself, for instance, without knowledge of the food,

[007-048] and of the link of cause and effect between certain movements and

[007-049] certain satisfactions. Rising gradually to the more complex forms

[007-050] of action, for example to the political, how could we will anything

[007-051] politically good or bad without knowing the real conditions of society,

[007-052] and consequently the means and expedients to be adopted? When the

[007-053] practical man feels himself in the dark about one or more of these

[007-054] points, or when he is seized with doubt, action either does not begin

[007-055] or stops. It is then that the theoretical moment, which in the rapid

[007-056] succession of human actions is hardly noticed and rapidly forgotten,

[007-057] becomes important and occupies consciousness for a longer time. And

[007-058] if this moment be prolonged, then the practical man may become a

[007-059] Hamlet, divided between desire for action and his deficient theoretical

[007-060] clarity as regards the situation and the means to be employed. And if

[007-061] he develop a taste for contemplation and discovery, and leave willing

[007-062] and acting, to a greater or less extent, to others, there is formed in

[007-063] him the calm disposition of the artist, of the man of science, or of

[007-064] the philosopher, who in practice are sometimes incompetent or downright

[007-065] immoral. These observations are all obvious. Their exactitude cannot be

[007-066] denied. Let us, however, repeat that they are founded on quantitative

[007-067] distinctions and do not disprove but confirm the fact that an action,

[007-068] however slight it be, cannot really be an action, that is, an action

[007-069] that is willed, unless it be preceded by the cognitive activity.

[007-070] *Criticism of practical judgements or judgements of value.*

[007-071] Some psychologists, on the other hand, place before practical action

[007-072] an altogether special class of judgements, which they call *practical*

[007-073] judgements or *judgements of value.* They say that in order to resolve

[007-074] on performing an action there must have been a judgement to the

[007-075] effect: "this action is useful, this action is good." And at first

[007-076] sight this seems to have the testimony of consciousness on its side.

[007-077] But closer observation and analysis of greater subtlety reveal that

[007-078] such judgements follow instead of preceding the affirmation of the

[007-079] will, and are nothing but the expression of the volition already

[007-080] exercised. A good or useful action is an action willed. It will always

[007-081] be impossible to distil a single drop of usefulness or goodness from

[007-082] the objective study of things. We do not desire things because we know

[007-083] them to be good or useful; but we know them to be good and useful,

[007-084] because we desire them. Here too, the rapidity with which the facts

[007-085] of consciousness follow one another has given rise to an illusion.

[007-086] Practical action is preceded by knowledge, but not by practical

[007-087] knowledge, or rather, knowledge of the practical: to obtain this, we

[007-088] must first have practical action. The third moment, therefore, of

[007-089] practical judgements, or judgements of value, is altogether imaginary.

[007-090] It does not come between the two moments or degrees of theory and

[007-091] practice. For the rest, normative sciences in general, which regulate

[007-092] or command, discover and indicate values to the practical activity,

[007-093] do not exist; indeed none exist for any sort of activity, since every

[007-094] science presupposes that activity to be already realized and developed,

[007-095] which it afterwards takes as its object.

[007-096] *Exclusion of the practical from the æsthetic.*

[007-097] These distinctions established, we must condemn as erroneous every

[007-098] theory which annexes the æsthetic activity to the practical, or

[007-099] introduces the laws of the second into the first. That science is

[007-100] theory and art practice has been many times affirmed. Those who make

[007-101] this statement, and look upon the æsthetic fact as a practical fact,

[007-102] do not do so capriciously or because they are groping in the void; but

[007-103] because they have their eye on something which is really practical. But

[007-104] the practical which they aim is not Æsthetic, nor within Æsthetic; it

[007-105] is *outside and beside it*; and although often found united, they are

[007-106] not united necessarily or by the bond of identity of nature.

[007-107] The æsthetic fact is altogether completed in the expressive elaboration

[007-108] of impressions. When we have achieved the word within us, conceived

[007-109] definitely and vividly a figure or a statue, or found a musical motive,

[007-110] expression is born and is complete; there is no need for anything else.

[007-111] If after this we should open our mouths-*will* to open them to speak,

[007-112] or our throats to sing, that is to say, utter by word of mouth and

[007-113] audible melody what we have completely said or sung to ourselves; or

[007-114] if we should stretch out*—will* to stretch out our hands to touch the

[007-115] notes of the piano, or to take up the brush and chisel, thus making

[007-116] on a large scale movements which we have already made in little and

[007-117] rapidly, in a material in which we leave more or less durable traces;

[007-118] this is all an addition, a fact which obeys quite different laws from

[007-119] the former, with which we are not concerned for the moment, although

[007-120] we recognize henceforth that this second movement is a production

[007-121] of things, a *practical* fact, or fact of *will*. It is usual to

[007-122] distinguish the internal from the external [[art|work of art]]: the terminology

[007-123] seems to us infelicitous, for the work of art (the æsthetic work) is

[007-124] always *internal*; and what is called *external* is no longer a work of

[007-125] art. Others distinguish between *æsthetic* fact and *artistic* fact,

[007-126] meaning by the second the external or practical stage, which may follow

[007-127] and generally does follow the first. But in this case, it is simply a

[007-128] question of a linguistic usage, doubtless permissible, though perhaps

[007-129] not advisable.

[007-130] *Criticism of the theory of the end of art and of the choice

[007-131] of content.*

[007-132] For the same reasons the search for the *end of art* is ridiculous,

[007-133] when it is understood of art as art. And since to fix an end is to

[007-134] choose, the theory that the content of art must be *selected* is

[007-135] another form of the same error. A selection among impressions and

[007-136] sensations implies that these are already expressions, otherwise how

[007-137] could a selection be made among the continuous and indistinct? To

[007-138] choose is to will: to will this and not to will that: and this and that

[007-139] must be before us, expressed. Practice follows, it does not precede

[007-140] theory; expression is free inspiration.

[007-141] The true artist, in fact, finds himself big with his theme, he knows

[007-142] not how; he feels the moment of birth drawing near, but he cannot will

[007-143] it or not will it. If he were to wish to act in opposition to his

[007-144] inspiration, to make an arbitrary choice, if, born Anacreon, he should

[007-145] wish to sing of Atreus and of Alcides, his lyre would warn him of

[007-146] his mistake, sounding only of Venus and of Love, notwithstanding his

[007-147] efforts to the contrary.

[007-148] *Practical innocence of art.*

[007-149] The theme or content cannot, therefore, be practically or morally

[007-150] charged with epithets of praise or blame. When critics of art remark

[007-151] that a theme is *badly selected,* in cases where that observation has

[007-152] a just foundation, it is a question of blaming, not the selection of

[007-153] the theme (which would be absurd), but the manner in which the artist

[007-154] has treated it, the failure of the expression due to the contradictions

[007-155] which it contains. And when the same critics object to the theme or

[007-156] content of works which they proclaim to be artistically perfect as

[007-157] being unworthy of art and blameworthy; if these expressions really are

[007-158] perfect, there is nothing to be done but to advise the critics to

[007-159] leave the artists in peace, for they can only derive inspiration from

[007-160] what has moved their soul. They should rather direct their attention

[007-161] towards effecting changes in surrounding nature and society, that

[007-162] such impressions and states of soul should not recur. If ugliness

[007-163] were to vanish from the world, if universal virtue and felicity were

[007-164] established there, perhaps artists would no longer represent perverse

[007-165] or pessimistic feelings, but calm, innocent and joyous feelings,

[007-166] Arcadians of a real Arcady. But so long as ugliness and turpitude

[007-167] exist in nature and impose themselves upon the artist, to prevent

[007-168] the expression of these things also is impossible; and when it has

[007-169] arisen, *factum infectum fieri nequit.* We speak thus entirely from the

[007-170] æsthetic point of view, and of pure criticism of art.

[007-171] We are not concerned to estimate the damage which the criticism of

[007-172] "choice" does to artistic production, with the prejudices which it

[007-173] produces or maintains among the artists themselves, and with the

[007-174] conflict to which it gives rise between artistic impulse and critical

[007-175] demands. It is true that sometimes it seems also to do some good, by

[007-176] aiding artists to discover themselves, that is, their own impressions

[007-177] and their own inspiration, and to acquire consciousness of the task

[007-178] which is, as it were, imposed upon them by the historical moment in

[007-179] which they live, and by their individual temperament. In these cases,

[007-180] criticism of "choice," while believing that it generates, merely

[007-181] recognizes and aids the expressions which are already being formed.

[007-182] It believes itself to be the mother, where, at most, it is only the

[007-183] midwife.

[007-184] *The independence of art.*

[007-185] The impossibility of choice of content completes the theorem of the

[007-186] *independence of art,* and is also the only legitimate meaning of the

[007-187] expression: *art for art's sake.* Art is independent both of science

[007-188] and of the useful and the moral. There should be no fear lest frivolous

[007-189] or cold art should thus be justified, since what is truly frivolous

[007-190] or cold is so because it has not been raised to expression; or in

[007-191] other words, frivolity and frigidity come always from the form of the

[007-192] æsthetic treatment, from failure to grasp a content, not from the

[007-193] material qualities of the content itself.

[007-194] *Criticism of the saying: the style is the man*

[007-195] The saying: *the style is the man*, can also not be completely

[007-196] criticized, save by starting from the distinction between the theoretic

[007-197] and the practical, and from the theoretic character of the æsthetic

[007-198] activity. Man is not simply knowledge and contemplation: he is will,

[007-199] which contains the cognitive moment in itself. Hence the saying is

[007-200] either altogether void, as when it is taken to mean that the style is

[007-201] the man *qua* style—is the man, that is, but only so far as he is

[007-202] expressive activity; or it is erroneous, as when the attempt is made

[007-203] to deduce what a man has done and willed from what he has seen and

[007-204] expressed, thereby asserting that there is a logical connexion between

[007-205] knowing and willing. Many legends in the biographies of artists have

[007-206] sprung from this erroneous identification, since it seemed impossible

[007-207] that a man who gives expression to generous feelings should not be a

[007-208] noble and generous man in practical life; or that the dramatist whose

[007-209] plays are full of stabbing, should not himself have done a little

[007-210] stabbing in real life. Artists protest vainly: "*Lasciva est nobis

[007-211] pagina, vita proba.*" They are merely taxed in addition with lying

[007-212] and hypocrisy. How far more prudent you were, poor women of Verona,

[007-213] when you founded your belief that Dante had really descended to hell

[007-214] upon his blackened countenance! Yours was at any rate a historical

[007-215] conjecture.

[007-216] *Criticism of the concept of sincerity in art.*

[007-217] Finally, *sincerity* imposed as a duty upon the artist (a law of ethics

[007-218] also said to be a law of æsthetic) rests upon another double meaning.

[007-219] For by sincerity may be meant, in the first place, the moral duty not

[007-220] to deceive one's neighbour; and in that case it is foreign to the

[007-221] artist. For indeed he deceives no one, since he gives form to what

[007-222] is already in his soul. He would only deceive if he were to betray

[007-223] his duty as an artist by failing to execute his task in its essential

[007-224] nature. If lies and deceit are in his soul, then the form which he

[007-225] gives to these things cannot be deceit or lies, precisely because it

[007-226] is æsthetic. If the artist be a charlatan, a liar, or a miscreant,

[007-227] he purifies his other self by reflecting it in art. If by sincerity

[007-228] be meant, in the second place, fulness and truth of expression, it is

[007-229] clear that this second sense has no relation to the ethical concept.

[007-230] The law, called both ethical and æsthetic, reveals itself here as

[007-231] nothing but a word used both by Ethics and Æsthetic.
