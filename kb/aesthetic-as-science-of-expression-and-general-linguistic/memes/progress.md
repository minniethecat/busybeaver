# Progress

The concept employed by Croce to describe historical development in art and thought, understood not as a single linear advance but as a criterion of judgment operating through progressive cycles, each with its own internal standard (018-171, 018-172, 018-267).

## Definition

Croce treats progress as the foundational concept of human history, yet he sharply criticizes the naive conception of progress as a unilinear march toward improvement. "Human history has the concept of progress as its foundation" (018-170), but "by progress must not be understood the imaginary law of progress which is supposed to lead things toward an ever-better state" (018-172). For Croce, progress is a criterion of historical interpretation — a point of view from which the historian judges — rather than an intrinsic property of events.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Progress functions within Croce's historicist framework as the critical principle linking aesthetic judgment to historical understanding. If a concept of progress is "a point of view, a criterion" (018-230), then historical narrative — including the history of art — is always governed by a standard internal to the domain under study. Croce explicitly denies "the existence of a single line of progress in artistic and literary history" (018-241), arguing that such a line cannot be drawn "any more easily than other historians" can draw one for other domains (018-244).

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws a crucial distinction between **progress in science** and **progress in art**. "Science is the universal" (018-253): in scientific knowledge, later theories genuinely supersede earlier ones, making linear progress meaningful. In art, by contrast, each work is an individual, unrepeatable intuition: "intuition is individuality, and individuality does not repeat itself" (018-261, 018-262). Therefore art history shows not linear progress and regress but "progressive cycles" (018-267), each cycle possessing its own internal standard of excellence. The criterion of progress "assumes a different form" (018-249) depending on whether it is applied to the universal (science, philosophy) or the particular (art).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The idea of historical progress was central to Enlightenment thought, from Condorcet's perfectibility to Hegel's dialectical unfolding of Spirit. Nineteenth-century positivism, represented by figures such as Comte and Spencer, treated progress as a scientific law of social evolution. In aesthetics, evolutionary accounts of art (from primitive to classical to decadent) were common.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of progress is part of his broader critique of positivism and his defense of the autonomy of art. By denying a single line of progress in art, Croce protects individual works from being judged by extrinsic standards. A Greek tragedy is not "less advanced" than a Shakespearean one; each is complete within its own expressive cycle. This position flows from Croce's core commitment to the individuality of intuition: if every genuine artistic intuition is unique, then no work can be superseded by a later one in the manner of a scientific theory.

## Criticism and Debate

> The cyclic conception of progress has been challenged by Marxist critics who see in Croce's denial of linear progress a refusal to engage with the material conditions of artistic production. For Croce, however, the "law of progress in the sense just described" (018-181) — as material or technological advance — has "nothing to do" (018-177) with the properly aesthetic question of expressive value.
