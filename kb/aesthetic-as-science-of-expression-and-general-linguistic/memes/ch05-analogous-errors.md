[005-001] These relations between intuitive or æsthetic knowledge and the other

[005-002] fundamental or derivative forms of knowledge having been definitely

[005-003] established, we are now in a position to reveal the errors of a series

[005-004] of theories which have been, or are, presented as theories of Æsthetic.

[005-005] *Criticism of probability and of naturalism.*

[005-006] From the confusion between the demands of art in general and the

[005-007] particular demands of history has resulted the theory (which has lost

[005-008] ground to-day, but was once dominant) of the *probable* as the object

[005-009] of art. As is generally the case with erroneous propositions, the

[005-010] meaning of those who employed and employ the concept of probability

[005-011] has no doubt often been much more reasonable than their definition

[005-012] of the word. By probability used really to be meant the artistic

[005-013] *coherence* of the representation, that is to say, its completeness

[005-014] and effectiveness, its actual presence. If "probable" be translated

[005-015] "coherent," a very just meaning will often be found in the discussions,

[005-016] examples, and judgements of the critics who employ this word. An

[005-017] improbable personage, an improbable ending to a comedy, are really

[005-018] badly-drawn personages, badly-arranged endings, happenings without

[005-019] artistic motive. It has been said with reason that even fairies and

[005-020] sprites must have probability, that is to say, be really sprites and

[005-021] fairies, coherent artistic intuitions. Sometimes the word "possible"

[005-022] has been used instead of "probable." As we have already remarked in

[005-023] passing, this word possible is synonymous with the imaginable or

[005-024] intuitible. Everything truly, that is to say coherently, imagined, is

[005-025] possible. But also, by a good many critics and theorists, the probable

[005-026] was taken to mean the historically credible, or that historical truth

[005-027] which is not demonstrable but conjecturable, not true but probable.

[005-028] This was the character which these theorists sought to impose upon art.

[005-029] Who does not remember how great a part was played in literary history

[005-030] by criticism based on probability, for example, censure of *Jerusalem

[005-031] Delivered,* based upon the history of the Crusades, or of the Homeric

[005-032] poems, upon the probable customs of emperors and kings? Sometimes too

[005-033] the æsthetic reproduction of historical reality has been imposed upon

[005-034] art. This is another of the erroneous forms taken by the theory of the

[005-035] *imitation of nature.* Verism and naturalism also have afforded the

[005-036] spectacle of a confusion of the æsthetic fact with the processes of the

[005-037] natural sciences, by aiming at some sort of *experimental* drama or

[005-038] romance.

[005-039] *Criticism of ideas in art, of theses in art and of the

[005-040] typical.*

[005-041] Confusions between the methods of art and those of the philosophic

[005-042] sciences have been far more frequent. Thus it has often been held to

[005-043] be the task of art to expound concepts, to unite an intelligible with

[005-044] a sensible, to represent *ideas* or *universals*; putting art in the

[005-045] place of science, that is, confusing the artistic function in general

[005-046] with the particular case in which it becomes æsthetico-logical.

[005-047] The theory of art as supporting *theses,* of art considered as an

[005-048] individual representation exemplifying scientific laws, can be proved

[005-049] false in like manner. The example, as example, stands for the thing

[005-050] exemplified, and is thus an exposition of the universal, that is to

[005-051] say, a form of science, more or less popular or vulgarizing.

[005-052] The same may be said of the æsthetic theory of the *typical,* when

[005-053] by type is understood, as it frequently is, the abstraction or the

[005-054] concept, and it is affirmed that art should make the *species* shine

[005-055] in the *individual.* If individual be here understood by typical, we

[005-056] have here too a merely verbal variation. To typify would signify, in

[005-057] this case, to characterize; that is, to determine and to represent

[005-058] the individual. Don Quixote is a type; but of what is he a type, save

[005-059] of all Don Quixotes? A type, so to speak, of himself. Certainly he

[005-060] is not a type of abstract concepts, such as the loss of the sense of

[005-061] reality, or of the love of glory. An infinite number of personages

[005-062] can be thought of under these concepts, who are not Don Quixotes. In

[005-063] other words, we find our own impressions fully determined and realized

[005-064] in the expression of a poet (for example in a poetical personage). We

[005-065] call that expression typical, which we might call simply æsthetic. Thus

[005-066] poetical or artistic universals have sometimes been spoken of, only to

[005-067] show that the artistic product is altogether spiritual and ideal.

[005-068] *Criticism of the symbol and of the allegory.*

[005-069] Continuing to correct these errors, or to clear up misunderstandings,

[005-070] we shall also remark that the *symbol* has sometimes been given as the

[005-071] essence of art. Now, if the symbol be conceived as inseparable from the

[005-072] artistic intuition, it is a synonym for the intuition itself, which

[005-073] always has an ideal character. There is no double bottom to art, but

[005-074] one only; in art all is symbolical, because all is ideal. But if the

[005-075] symbol be conceived as separable—if the symbol can be on one side,

[005-076] and on the other the thing symbolized, we fall back again into the

[005-077] intellectualist error: the so-called symbol is the exposition of an

[005-078] abstract concept, an *allegory*; it is science, or art aping science.

[005-079] But we must also be just toward the allegorical. Sometimes it is

[005-080] altogether harmless. Given the *Gerusalemme liberata,* the allegory

[005-081] was imagined afterwards; given the *A done* of Marino, the poet of

[005-082] the lascivious afterwards insinuated that it was written to show how

[005-083] "immoderate indulgence ends in pain"; given a statue of a beautiful

[005-084] woman, the sculptor can attach a label to the statue saying that

[005-085] it represents *Clemency* or *Goodness.* This allegory that arrives

[005-086] attached to a finished work *post festum* does not change the work of

[005-087] art. What then is it? It is an expression externally *added* to another

[005-088] expression. A little page of prose is added to the *Gerusalemme,*

[005-089] expressing another thought of the poet; a verse or a strophe is added

[005-090] to the *Adone,* expressing what the poet would like to make a part

[005-091] of his public believe; to the statue nothing but the single word:

[005-092] *Clemency* or *Goodness.*

[005-093] *Criticism of the theory of artistic and literary kinds.*

[005-094] But the greatest triumph of the intellectualist error lies in the

[005-095] theory of artistic and literary kinds, which still has vogue in

[005-096] literary treatises and disturbs the critics and the historians of art.

[005-097] Let us observe its genesis.

[005-098] The human mind can pass from the æsthetic to the logical, just because

[005-099] the former is a first step in respect to the latter. It can destroy

[005-100] expression, that is, the thought of the individual, by thinking of the

[005-101] universal. It can gather up expressive facts into logical relations.

[005-102] We have already shown that this operation becomes in its turn concrete

[005-103] in an expression, but this does not mean that the first expressions

[005-104] have not been destroyed. They have yielded their place to the new

[005-105] æsthetico-logical expressions. When we are on the second step, we have

[005-106] left the first.

[005-107] One who enters a picture-gallery, or who reads a series of poems,

[005-108] having looked and read, may go further: he may seek out the nature and

[005-109] the relations of the things there expressed. Thus those pictures and

[005-110] compositions, each of which is an individual inexpressible in logical

[005-111] terms, are gradually resolved into universals and abstractions, such

[005-112] as *costumes, landscapes, portraits, domestic life, battles, animals,

[005-113] flowers, fruit, seascapes, lakes, deserts; tragic, comic, pathetic,

[005-114] cruel, lyrical, epic, dramatic, chivalrous, idyllic facts,* and the

[005-115] like. They are often also resolved into merely quantitative categories,

[005-116] such as *miniature, picture, statuette, group, madrigal, ballad,

[005-117] sonnet, sonnet-sequence, poetry, poem, story, romance,* and the like.

[005-118] When we think the concept *domestic life,* or *chivalry,* or *idyll,*

[005-119] or *cruelty,* or one of the quantitative concepts mentioned above, the

[005-120] individual expressive fact from which we started has been abandoned.

[005-121] From æsthetes that we were, we have changed into logicians; from

[005-122] contemplators of expression, into reasoners. Certainly no objection

[005-123] can be made to such a process. In what other way could science arise,

[005-124] which, if it have æsthetic expressions presupposed in it, must yet go

[005-125] beyond them in order to fulfil its function? The logical or scientific

[005-126] form, as such, excludes the æsthetic form. He who begins to think

[005-127] scientifically has already ceased to contemplate æsthetically; although

[005-128] his thought assumes of necessity in its turn an æsthetic form, as has

[005-129] already been said, and as it would be superfluous to repeat.

[005-130] Error begins when we try to deduce the expression from the concept, and

[005-131] to find in what takes its place the laws of the thing whose place is

[005-132] taken; when the difference between the second and the first step has

[005-133] not been observed, and when, in consequence, we declare that we are

[005-134] standing on the first step, when we are really standing on the second.

[005-135] This error is known as the *theory of artistic and literary kinds.*

[005-136] "What is the *æsthetic* form of domestic life, of chivalry, of

[005-137] the idyll, of cruelty, and so forth? How should these contents be

[005-138] *represented*?" Such is the absurd problem implied in the theory of

[005-139] artistic and literary classes, when it has been shorn of excrescences

[005-140] and reduced to a simple formula. It is in this that consists all

[005-141] search after laws or rules of classes. Domestic life, chivalry, idyll,

[005-142] cruelty and the like, are not impressions, but concepts. They are not

[005-143] contents, but logical-æsthetic forms. You cannot express the form,

[005-144] for it is already itself expression. For what are the words cruelty,

[005-145] idyll, chivalry, domestic life, and so on, but the expression of those

[005-146] concepts?

[005-147] Even the most refined of such distinctions, which possess the most

[005-148] philosophic appearance, do not resist criticism; as when works of art

[005-149] are divided into subjective and objective kinds, into lyric and epic,

[005-150] into works of feeling and decorative works. In æsthetic analysis it is

[005-151] impossible to separate subjective from objective, lyric from epic, the

[005-152] [[representation|image]] of feeling from that of things.

[005-153] *Errors derived from this theory in judgements on art.*

[005-154] From the theory of artistic and literary kinds derive those erroneous

[005-155] modes of judgement and of criticism, thanks to which, instead of asking

[005-156] before a [[art|work of art]] if it be expressive and what it expresses, whether

[005-157] it speak or stammer or is altogether silent, they ask if it obey the

[005-158] *laws* of epic or of tragedy, of historical painting or of landscape.

[005-159] While making a verbal pretence of agreeing, or yielding a feigned

[005-160] obedience, artists have, however, really always disregarded these *laws

[005-161] of the kinds.* Every true work of art has violated some established

[005-162] kind and upset the ideas of the critics, who have thus been obliged to

[005-163] broaden the kinds, until finally even the broadened kind has proved too

[005-164] narrow, owing to the appearance of new works of art, naturally followed

[005-165] by new scandals, new upsettings and—new broadenings.

[005-166] To the same theory are due the prejudices, owing to which at one time

[005-167] (is it really passed?) people used to lament that [[italy|Italy]] had no tragedy

[005-168] (until one arose who bestowed such a wreath, which alone of adornments

[005-169] was wanting to her glorious locks), nor [[france|France]] the epic poem (until the

[005-170] *Henriade,* which slaked the thirsty throats of the critics). Eulogies

[005-171] accorded to the inventors of new kinds are connected with these

[005-172] prejudices, so much so, that in the seventeenth century the invention

[005-173] of the *mock-heroic* poem seemed an important event, and the honour of

[005-174] it was disputed, as though it were the discovery of America. But the

[005-175] works adorned with this name (the *Secchia rapita* and the *Scherno

[005-176] degli Dei*) were still-born, because their authors (a slight drawback)

[005-177] had nothing new or original to say. Mediocrities racked their brains to

[005-178] invent new kinds artificially. The *piscatorial* eclogue was added to

[005-179] the *pastoral,* and finally the *military* eclogue. The *Aminta* was

[005-180] dipped and became the *Alceo.* Finally, there have been historians of

[005-181] art and literature, so much fascinated with these ideas of kinds, that

[005-182] they claimed to write the history, not of individual and real literary

[005-183] and artistic works, but of those empty phantoms, their kinds. They have

[005-184] claimed to portray, not the evolution of the *artistic spirit,* but the

[005-185] *evolution of kinds.*

[005-186] The philosophical condemnation of artistic and literary kinds is found

[005-187] in the formulation and demonstration of what artistic activity has

[005-188] always done and [[taste|good taste]] always recognized. What are we to do if good

[005-189] taste and the real fact, when reduced to formulas, sometimes assume

[005-190] the air of paradoxes?

[005-191] *Empirical sense of the divisions of kinds.*

[005-192] It is not scientifically incorrect to talk of tragedies, comedies,

[005-193] dramas, romances, pictures of everyday life, battle-pieces, landscapes,

[005-194] seascapes, poems, versicles, lyrics, and the like, if it be only with

[005-195] a view to be understood, and to draw attention to certain groups

[005-196] of works, in general and approximately, to which, for one reason

[005-197] or another, it is desired to draw attention. To employ *words* and

[005-198] *phrases* is not to establish *laws* and *definitions.* The mistake

[005-199] only arises when the weight of a scientific definition is given to a

[005-200] word, when we ingenuously let ourselves be caught in the meshes of

[005-201] that phraseology. Pray permit me a comparison. The books in a library

[005-202] must be arranged in one way or another. This used generally to be done

[005-203] by a rough classification of subjects (among which the categories of

[005-204] miscellaneous and eccentric were not wanting); they are now generally

[005-205] arranged by sizes or by publishers. Who can deny the necessity and

[005-206] the utility of such arrangements? But what should we say if some one

[005-207] began seriously to seek out the literary laws of miscellanies and of

[005-208] eccentricities, of the Aldines or Bodonis, of shelf A or shelf B,

[005-209] that is to say, of those altogether arbitrary groupings whose sole

[005-210] object was their practical utility. Yet should any one attempt such

[005-211] an undertaking, he would be doing neither more nor less than those do

[005-212] who seek out the *æsthetic laws* which must in their belief control

[005-213] literary and artistic kinds.
