# Arthur Schopenhauer

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was a German post-Kantian philosopher whose pessimistic metaphysics of the will placed art in a unique position — as a temporary liberation from the blind striving that constitutes reality. Croce discusses Schopenhauer as a representative of "mysticism in the opponents of idealism" (P02-246).

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in Danzig (now Gdańsk, Poland) to a wealthy merchant family, Schopenhauer studied at Göttingen and Berlin, where he attended Fichte's lectures. His doctoral dissertation, *On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason* (1813), established his philosophical foundations.

### Career and Major Works

Schopenhauer's masterwork, *The World as Will and Representation* (1818), was initially ignored — it sold few copies and went out of print — but gained recognition in the 1850s. His philosophy centers on the concept of the Will (*Wille*) — a blind, insatiable striving that constitutes the inner nature of all things. The intellect is merely the Will's instrument; only in aesthetic contemplation and ascetic renunciation does the individual escape, temporarily, the tyranny of desire.

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Art as Liberation from Will**: For Schopenhauer, aesthetic contemplation is the one state in which the subject becomes a "pure, will-less subject of knowledge," freed from the demands of desire. Music, as the most direct expression of the Will itself, is the highest art
- **Platonic Ideas in Art**: Schopenhauer held that art presents not individual things but Platonic Ideas — the eternal archetypes of which particular things are imperfect copies. This reintroduces a metaphysical foundation for beauty that Croce rejects (P02-246)
- **Ideas as Objects of Art**: Croce discusses Schopenhauer's "Ideas" (P02-246) — the metaphysical objects that art contemplates — as an important but ultimately mistaken attempt to give art a metaphysical status without subordinating it to the concept
- **Pessimism and Aesthetics**: Schopenhauer's association of art with temporary escape from suffering represents a psychological theory of art's value rather than a philosophical theory of art's nature

## Criticism and Reception

> Croce classifies Schopenhauer among the "mystical" opponents of idealism (P02-246), whose aesthetics attempts to transcend the concept-intuition distinction through metaphysical speculation. For Croce, Schopenhauer's theory of art as contemplation of Ideas conflates intuition with a quasi-conceptual universal and fails to recognize the full autonomy of the aesthetic act.
