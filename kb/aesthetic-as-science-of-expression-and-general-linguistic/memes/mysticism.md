# Mysticism

In Croce's aesthetic system, mysticism denotes the tendency to conflate aesthetic activity with religious or transcendent experience, dissolving art into an ineffable union with the absolute; Croce treats this as a category error that obscures the autonomous, cognitive character of art (P02-246, 029-001).

## Definition

Mysticism in aesthetics is the view that art's highest function is to reveal a supersensible reality beyond rational comprehension — a position that Croce identifies throughout the history of aesthetic thought, from Neo-Platonism through German Idealism. Rather than recognizing art as a distinct form of theoretical knowledge (intuition-expression), mysticism "confuses the aesthetic with the religious, subordinating the clarity of intuition to the vagueness of transcendent feeling" (026-132). Croce argues that this confusion undermines both aesthetics and religion by denying each its proper sphere within the system of spirit.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Within the fourfold structure of spirit — intuition, concept, economic action, ethical action — mysticism belongs to none of the four forms but represents a *failure to distinguish* among them. Croce locates mysticism's origin in the attempt to elevate aesthetic intuition beyond its proper theoretical domain into the sphere of the absolute, thereby bypassing conceptual thought and practical action alike (020-044). This conflation is particularly dangerous for aesthetics because it denies art its cognitive dignity: if art is merely a vehicle for mystical transcendence, it ceases to be a form of knowledge.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **aesthetic mysticism** from both **religious consciousness** and **philosophical idealism**. Religion, in Croce's system, is an incomplete form of philosophy — mythologized concepts awaiting rational refinement — but it is not identical with mysticism, which abandons conceptual articulation altogether in favor of ineffable union (027-240). Similarly, Croce separates mysticism from genuine idealism: whereas idealism treats art as a moment in the dialectical self-realization of spirit, mysticism "dissolves the determinate content of art into an indeterminate absolute, robbing it of its concrete, individual character" (027-311). The key distinction is that mysticism lacks the critical, discriminating function that defines both philosophy and art.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The mystical tendency in aesthetics originates with Neo-Platonism, particularly in Plotinus, who conceived of beauty as the radiance of the One shining through material form. This tradition "continued through the Middle Ages, entrusted to the custodians of Neo-Platonic mysticism" (021-004). In the Renaissance, figures such as the Dutch philosopher Frans Hemsterhuis carried forward the Neo-Platonic fusion of aesthetics and mysticism (P02-218). German Idealism, especially in its later phases, provided fertile ground for mystical interpretations of art: Schelling's philosophy of identity and Schleiermacher's religion of feeling both blurred the boundary Croce insists upon between art and transcendence.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of mysticism is decisively critical. Where earlier philosophers — including Kant, who "began by tending toward mysticism" (027-240) in his treatment of the sublime — allowed mystical elements into aesthetic theory, Croce insists on the strict autonomy of each form of spirit. His innovation is not merely to criticize mysticism but to diagnose it as the predictable consequence of failing to distinguish intuition from concept, and both from practical activity. Croce characterizes the outcome of aesthetic mysticism as "a capricious, narrow, barren mysticism, devoid of all critical power" (029-230). The historical chapter "Æsthetic Mysticism in the Opponents of Idealism" (029-001) systematically traces how anti-idealist thinkers fell into the very mysticism they purported to oppose, revealing mysticism as a recurring structural temptation rather than a specific doctrinal position.

## Criticism and Debate

> "A mysticism without conviction or enthusiasm, an academic posture rather than a living spiritual attitude — this is the graver danger, that cold academical mysticism which came into vogue" (026-351, 027-312). Croce's critique extends beyond doctrinal mysticism to the aesthetic posture that valorizes obscurity for its own sake.

Critics have argued that Croce's anti-mysticism goes too far, reducing the numinous dimension of aesthetic experience to mere conceptual confusion. Defenders respond that Croce does not deny the emotional power of art; he insists only that this power operates through determinate intuition, not through the dissolution of form. The question remains whether Croce's system can adequately account for the experience of the sublime, which seems to press against the limits of determinate expression.
