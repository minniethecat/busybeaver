# Naples

**Naples** (Italian: *Napoli*; Neapolitan: *Napule*) is a city on the western coast of southern Italy, situated on the Gulf of Naples. In the history of aesthetics, Naples holds a singular importance as the home of [[Benedetto Croce]], the residence of [[Giambattista Vico]], and the site of the [[Accademia Pontaniana]] where the foundations of expressionist aesthetic theory were first articulated. The city has been a vital center of philosophical and artistic production from the 17th century to the present.

## Significance in Æsthetic History

Naples occupies a distinctive position in the history of aesthetics as the birthplace of two of the most revolutionary theories of art and expression: Vico's *Scienza Nuova* and Croce's *Filosofia dello Spirito*. Both thinkers grounded their systems in the expressive character of human consciousness, and both did their essential work within the intellectual and cultural milieu of Naples.

### Cradle of the Science of Expression

The city is indelibly associated with the birth of aesthetics as a science of expression. In the winter of 1907, Douglas Ainslie visited Naples to meet Croce, whom he would later introduce to the English-speaking world as the "discoverer" of a modern philosophical continent:

> I can lay no claim to having discovered an America, but I do claim to have discovered a Columbus. His name is Benedetto Croce, and he dwells on the shores of the Mediterranean, at Naples, city of the antique Parthenope. (P03-001–P03-004)

Croce's philosophy, which holds that art is nothing but intuition-expression and that intuition is identical with expression, was developed in Naples and presented first at the Accademia Pontaniana in 1900 (P03-005, 001-005).

> So the philosopher of Naples sits by the blue gulf and explains the universe to those who have ears to hear. "One can philosophize anywhere," he says—but he remains significantly at Naples. (P03-141–P03-143)

### Birthplace of Vichian Philosophy

A century and a half before Croce, another Neapolitan thinker had already transformed European thought. Giambattista Vico published his *Scienza Nuova* in Naples in 1725, ten years before Baumgarten's first treatise on aesthetics in Germany (024-006–024-007). Vico discovered the true nature of poetry and art by recognizing imagination as a distinct and legitimate mode of cognition, making him, in Croce's assessment, the true inventor of the science of aesthetics (024-005).

## Associated Figures

### Benedetto Croce (1866–1952)

Croce is the central philosophical figure of modern Naples. Although born in the Abruzzi, he was "essentially a Neapolitan" and rarely remained long absent from the city (P03-005). His major work, the *Æsthetic as Science of Expression and General Linguistic*, was first presented as a memoir at the Accademia Pontaniana of Naples on February 18 and May 6, 1900 (001-005). Croce later edited the works of other Italian philosophers, including a reprint of Baumgarten's treatise published in Naples (023-490). His journal *La Critica*, which he founded and edited in Naples, became the leading organ of Italian philosophical culture.

### Giambattista Vico (1668–1744)

Vico was the first to recognize that poetry and art are not inferior forms of rational knowledge but rather an autonomous mode of spiritual activity. His *Scienza Nuova* appeared in Naples in 1725, establishing the foundation for what later thinkers—including Croce, [[Herbartian School]], and [[Hegelian Movement]]—would take up under the name of aesthetics. Vico's revolutionary insight was that "poetry precedes intellect, but follows sense" (024-025–024-030), and that language and poetry are substantially the same (P02-203–P02-205).

### Other Neapolitan Thinkers

Naples was also the home of a rich tradition of 17th-century moral philosophy that anticipated modern aesthetics. Works such as Michelangelo Fardella's *Del bene* (Naples, 1681) and later expositions of *Il buon gusto* (Naples, 1839) demonstrate the continuity of Neapolitan aesthetic reflection across two centuries (022-460, 022-465).

## Institutions and Academies

### Accademia Pontaniana

Founded in the 15th century, the Accademia Pontaniana of Naples is one of the oldest learned societies in Europe. It was before this academy that Croce delivered his epochal memoir "Fundamental Theses of an Æsthetic as Science of Expression and General Linguistic" in 1900 (001-005). The academy had earlier hosted the Neapolitan review *Flegrea*, in which Croce first published the historical chapters of his work under the title "Giambattista Vico, First Discoverer of Æsthetic Science" (001-012).

### Neapolitan Philosophical Milieu

The city supported a dense network of publishers, journals, and cultural institutions that sustained philosophical debate. Vico served as public censor of literature in Naples (024-017), and Croce directed the review *La Critica* from his Neapolitan study. The city's artistic heritage, including the many paintings of Marco da Siena and other artists that "exist here in Naples" (015-212), provided an ever-present context for aesthetic theorizing.

### The Gulf of Naples as Aesthetic Trope

The natural beauty of the Gulf of Naples itself became a subject of aesthetic reflection within Croce's work. Croce uses the Gulf as a case study for the relativity of natural beauty: "If even the Gulf of Naples have its detractors, and if there be artists who declare it inexpressive, preferring the 'gloomy firs,' the 'clouds and perpetual north winds,' of northern seas; is it really possible that such relativity does not exist for the human body, source of the most varied suggestions?" (015-079–015-080).
