# Plotinus

**Plotinus** (204--270 CE), the founder of the neo-Platonic school, is the pivotal figure in ancient aesthetic mysticism, the philosopher who for the first time fused the concepts of **[[Beauty]]** and art into a single spiritual doctrine. In Croce's history of aesthetics, Plotinus marks the decisive turn away from the rational analysis of **[[Imitation]]** toward a mystical conception in which the beautiful and art are "both alike melted into a mystical passion and elevation of the spirit" (020-358). His influence radiates through the entire later tradition of **[[Neo-Platonism]]**, from **[[Saint Augustine]]** and medieval mysticism to the German idealists of the nineteenth century.

## Biography

### Life and Historical Context

Plotinus was born in Lycopolis, Egypt, in 204 CE, at a time of profound transition in the Greco-Roman world. He studied philosophy in Alexandria under Ammonius Saccas, from whom he absorbed the Platonic tradition that he would transform into a comprehensive metaphysical system. Around 244 CE, Plotinus moved to Rome, where he established his own school and attracted a circle of distinguished followers that included the physician Eustochius and the future biographer Porphyry. He attempted, with the support of the Emperor Gallienus, to found a city based on Platonic principles -- a project that ultimately failed (020-236). He died in 270 CE at the villa of his friend Zethus in Campania.

Croce places Plotinus at "the entrance to the Middle Ages" (020-236), identifying his mystical aesthetic as a late-antique phenomenon that bridges the classical and medieval worlds. It was "only with Plotinus," Croce writes, that "the two divided territories are united and the beautiful and art are fused into a single concept" (020-357).

### Major Works and the Enneads

Plotinus's teachings were collected and published after his death by his student Porphyry in six groups of nine treatises each, known as the *Enneads*. The treatises on beauty and art are concentrated in *Ennead* I.6 (*On Beauty*) and V.8 (*On the Intelligible Beauty*). The *Enneads* present a hierarchical metaphysics in which all reality emanates from the One (the Good), passing through Intellect (Nous) and Soul to the material world. Within this framework, beauty is not a property of material objects but the radiance of the divine shining through the sensible world.

## Contributions to Aesthetic

### The Fusion of Beauty and Art

Plotinus's single most important contribution to aesthetic theory is his unification of the two strands of ancient thought that had previously run separately: the investigation into the Beautiful and the theory of art as imitation. The Greeks had maintained a clear distinction between these domains; "art and beauty, mimesis and pleasing or displeasing material of mimesis, are clearly distinguished" even by Aristotle (020-329). Plotinus dissolves this distinction entirely.

> "It is only with Plotinus that the two divided territories are united and the beautiful and art are fused into a single concept, not by means of a beneficial absorption of the equivocal Platonic conception of beauty into the unequivocal conception of art, but by absorption of the clear into the confused, of imitative art in the so-called beautiful." (020-358)

The result is an "altogether new view" in which aesthetic experience becomes a form of spiritual ascent. This fusion, Croce argues, is the first "true and proper affirmation of mystical Aesthetic, destined to such high fortunes in modern times, especially in the first half of the nineteenth century" (020-416).

### Beauty as the Formative Principle

Plotinus's theory of beauty is a direct challenge to the classical definition, standard since Cicero, that identified beauty with the symmetry of parts and pleasing colour. Plotinus raises a series of objections that Croce records with evident admiration:

> "Beauty, observes Plotinus, resides chiefly in things visible; but it is also to be found in things audible, such as verbal and musical compositions, and it is not lacking in things supersensible, such as works, offices, actions, habits, sciences and virtues. What is it that makes beautiful sensible and supersensible things alike? Not, he answers, the symmetry of their parts among themselves, and with the whole and their colour, according to one of the definitions most in vogue... because there are proportions in things ugly, and there are things that are simply beautiful without any relation of proportion: beauty, then, is one thing and symmetry another." (020-366)

Plotinus substitutes for the formal definition an ontological one: "That is beautiful which is formed; the ugly is what is unformed" (020-366). Beauty is the visible manifestation of the Idea, the divine Form, shining through matter. Matter is beautiful "not in itself, but only when it is illuminated by the Idea" (020-366). This position represents a complete inversion of Plato's aesthetic: where Plato had condemned art as a copy of a copy, Plotinus elevates art to the rank of a revelatory activity that goes beyond nature.

### Art, Nature, and the Idea

Plotinus's most striking argument, and the one that Croce treats as the center of his aesthetic, concerns the relationship between art and nature. Reversing the Platonic hierarchy, Plotinus argues that art does not merely imitate nature; rather, nature itself imitates the Idea, and art goes back to the same Ideas from which nature derives:

> "Art therefore does not belong to nature, but adds beauty where it is wanting in nature: Phidias did not represent Jove because he had seen him, but such as he would appear if he wished to reveal himself to mortal eyes. The beauty of natural things is the archetype existing in the soul, the sole source of natural beauty." (020-366)

This famous argument about Phidias -- that the sculptor could portray Zeus despite never having seen him, because the artist has access to the intelligible Form -- anticipates the Renaissance theory of imagination and the Romantic doctrine of artistic creation. Philostratus had earlier made a similar claim about imagination being "a wiser agent than simple imitation" (020-525), but Croce insists that "it cannot be said that Philostratus opened the way to Plotinus, for whom the superior or intellectual imagination, or eye of supersensible beauty, when it is not a new designation for beautiful imitation, is mystical intuition" (020-525).

### Intellectual Intuition and Mystical Vision

For Plotinus, the perception of beauty is inseparable from the purification of the soul. The soul "must purify itself, in order to perceive the beautiful, and make the power of the Idea that lies in it efficacious" (020-366). Through this purification, "there opens another eye in the soul, beside that of sensible beauty, which permits it to contemplate divine Beauty coincident with the Good, which is the supreme condition of beatitude" (020-366).

This conception of an "intellectual intuition" or "mental imagination" (028-569) became a crucial resource for later philosophers who sought to overcome the dualism of sense and intellect. The German idealists, particularly **[[F. W. J. Schelling]]** and **[[G. W. F. Hegel]]**, attempted "to conceive a faculty which should be neither imagination nor intellect but should partake of both; an intellectual intuition or intuitive intellect, a mental imagination after the fashion of Plotinus" (028-569).

## Influence and Reception

### Ancient and Medieval Transmission

Plotinus's fusion of beauty and art was transmitted to the medieval world through the writings of the pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, John Scotus Eriugena, and the neo-Platonic tradition of the Arabic philosophers. Croce observes that in the Middle Ages, "the Christian God took the place of the Summum Bonum or Idea: God, wisdom, goodness, supreme beauty, source of beautiful things in nature, which are a ladder to the contemplation of the Creator" (020-357). But these speculations "continued to recede further and further from the consideration of art, with which Plotinus had connected them; and the empty definitions of the beautiful by Cicero and other ancient writers were often repeated" (020-357).

### Renaissance Revival

During the Renaissance, Plotinian ideas were revived by philosophers and poets seeking to defend the dignity of art. Torquato Tasso made "free use of the speculations of Plotinus" (021-173) in his dialogues on art, while Campanella developed a semiotic theory of beauty as the sign of the good that bears traces of Plotinian influence. The **[[Renaissance]]** discourse on the imagination, from Ficino's neo-Platonic academy to the later theorists of *concetto* and *invenzione*, was deeply shaped by Plotinus's arguments about the artist's access to intelligible realities.

### Modern Influence and German Idealism

The most spectacular revival of Plotinian aesthetic came with German idealism. Croce identifies Plotinus as the fountainhead of "the aesthetic mysticism which first appeared in Plotinus and reappeared again and again until its last and great triumph in the classical period of German philosophy" (038-031). **[[Arthur Schopenhauer]]** and **[[F. W. J. Schelling]]** explicitly drew on Plotinian concepts: Schopenhauer's theory of aesthetic contemplation as a liberation from the Will, and Schelling's conception of art as the organon of philosophy, both depend on the Plotinian notion of intellectual intuition.

**[[Friedrich Schleiermacher]]**, too, in his aesthetic lectures, treats the beautiful in terms that recall Plotinus: "in the same way as by Plotinus of old, as well as by Schopenhauer's worst enemy, the modern Schelling" (029-060). The theme of the death of art in Hegel's system -- the famous thesis that art has been superseded by philosophy -- is itself a transformation of the Plotinian ascent from the sensible to the intelligible.

### Croce's Assessment

Croce's posture toward Plotinus is complex. On the one hand, he recognizes the historical importance of Plotinus's fusion of beauty and art: it represents the first clear emergence of the mystical tendency that would dominate European aesthetics from the Romantic period onward. On the other hand, Croce's own philosophy -- which insists on the autonomy of art as intuitive knowledge -- is fundamentally opposed to the mystical dissolution of art into religious ecstasy. For Croce, Plotinus represents the "deviation by excess" (020-038): he substitutes for the genuine expressive activity a "mysterious activity which does not really exist" (020-038). The result is not a true science of aesthetics but a spiritual intoxication that ultimately obscures the real nature of art.

Nevertheless, Croce acknowledges the permanence of Plotinus's contribution. The arguments about form and matter, about the artist's access to the Idea, and about the kinship between beauty and the soul, have proven to be among the most fertile ideas in the history of aesthetic thought. The mystical tradition that Plotinus inaugurated remains a living force, and the questions he posed -- about the relationship between sensible beauty and intelligible form, about art as a mode of spiritual ascent -- continue to challenge aesthetic theory to this day.
