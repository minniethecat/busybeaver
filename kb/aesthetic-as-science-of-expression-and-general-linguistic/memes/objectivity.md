# Objectivity

Objectivity in Croce's aesthetics is a concept he approaches with suspicion, for it has been used to justify the reification of aesthetic categories — literary genres, fixed rules of taste, canons of beauty — that his theory of expression dissolves. Yet Croce does not reject objectivity outright; he distinguishes between a spurious objectivity grounded in the hypostatization of abstract kinds and a genuine objectivity rooted in the universal human capacity for intuition-expression. The historian, like the aesthetician, must operate with a subjective criterion, but this subjectivity, correctly understood, is compatible with — indeed constitutive of — the highest rigour.

## Definition

Croce's position on aesthetic objectivity emerges most clearly through his treatment of literary kinds. In the historical section of his *Aesthetic*, he locates the problem in German Idealist aesthetics, where Schelling classified poetry into lyric, epic, and dramatic forms based on the interplay of subjectivity and objectivity. Croce quotes Schelling's scheme with evident irony: "From Epic, which is *par excellence* the objective kind (as being the identity of subjective and objective), derive the Elegy and the Idyl if subjectivity be placed in the object and objectivity in the poet: if objectivity be placed in the object and subjectivity in the poet, didactic poetry results" (038-923). The arbitrary permutations expose the emptiness of the framework: objectivity here is a mere placeholder, a term of art whose meaning shifts with each application.

Croce's more direct engagement with the problem of aesthetic objectivity comes through his discussion of historical method. He argues that "the necessity and inevitability of this subjective criterion in every narrative of human affairs (which is compatible with the utmost objectivity, impartiality and scrupulousness in dealing with data of fact and indeed forms a constitutive element in these virtues)" (018-204). The historian cannot be a "political or scientific eunuch"; a point of view is not a distortion but a condition of intelligibility.

## In Croce's System

### Objectivity of Taste and Its Critique

The concept of "objectivity of taste" was introduced into German aesthetics by Heydenreich, a follower of Baumgarten. Croce notes that in Heydenreich's theory, "the objects of sensibility which enter into art must be possessed of intrinsic excellence and value and bear reference not to a single individual but to the individual as a rational being: hence the objectivity and necessity of taste" (025-429). This formulation, which links objectivity to rationality and universality, represents the eighteenth-century attempt to rescue aesthetic judgement from mere subjective preference. But for Croce, Heydenreich's approach suffers from the same flaw as Baumgarten's: it treats aesthetic knowledge as a confused form of conceptual knowledge rather than as a sui generis activity of the spirit.

The deeper problem is that Heydenreich "does not clearly grasp the cognitive character of art" (025-429). By grounding objectivity in the "individual as a rational being" rather than in the expressive act itself, Heydenreich imports a logical criterion — universality — into a domain where it does not belong. The true universality of art is not the universality of the concept but the universality of the human capacity for intuition-expression.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws several critical distinctions. The first is between **objectivity as intersubjective validity** and **objectivity as conformity to a rule**. The former, which Croce accepts, means that a genuine aesthetic judgement — the recognition of an expression as successful — is not merely personal preference but is grounded in the universal human capacity for intuition. "The criterion of taste is absolute, but absolute in a different way from that of the intellect, which expresses itself in ratiocination. The criterion of taste is absolute, with the intuitive absoluteness of the imagination" (017-151).

The second distinction is between **objectivity in history** and **objectivity in science**. Historical objectivity does not require the elimination of the historian's perspective; on the contrary, "purely historical historians do not and cannot exist" (018-204). The historian's criterion of judgement is not an obstacle to truth but its enabling condition — a position that aligns Croce with the tradition running from Vico to contemporary hermeneutics.

The third distinction concerns **literary kinds** as pseudo-objective entities. Croce devotes sustained criticism to Ferdinand Brunetière, who "frankly maintained" the objectivity of literary kinds, treating literary history as "the evolution of kinds" (038-951). For Croce, this is "a superstition which, seldom confessed so truthfully or applied so rigorously, survives to contaminate modern literary history" (038-951). Literary kinds are not natural kinds with objective existence; they are retrospective abstractions from individual works of expression.

## Criticism and Debate

> Purely historical historians do not and cannot exist. Were Thucydides and Polybius, Livy and Tacitus, Machiavelli and Guicciardini, wholly without moral and political views; and, in our time, was Guizot or Thiers, Macaulay or Balbo, Ranke or Mommsen? Is there one single work of any value on the history of Æsthetic which has not been written from this or that point of view, with this or that bias? If the historian is to escape from the inevitable necessity of taking a side, he must become a political or scientific eunuch; and history is not an occupation for eunuchs. (018-204)

## Historical Context

The demand for objectivity in aesthetics has two principal sources. The first is the rationalist tradition stemming from Leibniz and Wolff, which sought to ground aesthetic judgement in logical principles — clarity, perfection, the conformity of the manifold to a unifying concept. Baumgarten's *cognitio sensitiva* was an attempt to give aesthetic knowledge objective standing without reducing it to logic. The second is the Kantian project of establishing the universality and necessity of judgements of taste through the "subjective universality" of the *sensus communis*. Kant's solution, though more sophisticated than the rationalist one, still tethered aesthetic objectivity to a cognitive framework that Croce rejects.

Croce's own solution is radically different. By identifying art with intuition-expression — a primary, pre-logical activity of the spirit — he relocates the problem of objectivity. Aesthetic judgement is not about matching a work to external criteria; it is about recognizing whether expression has succeeded in giving form to intuition. This recognition is, in principle, universally available because the capacity for intuition-expression is universal. The objectivity of taste is thus grounded in the anthropology of the spirit, not in logic.
