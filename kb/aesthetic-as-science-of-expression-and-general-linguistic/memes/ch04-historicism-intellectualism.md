[004-001] *Inseparability of intellectual from [[intuition|intuitive knowledge]].*

[004-002] The two forms of knowledge, æsthetic and intellectual or conceptual,

[004-003] are indeed different, but this does not altogether amount to separation

[004-004] and disjunction, as of two forces each pulling in its own direction.

[004-005] If we have shown that the æsthetic form is altogether independent of

[004-006] the intellectual and suffices to itself without external support, we

[004-007] have not said that the intellectual can stand without the æsthetic. To

[004-008] describe the independence as *reciprocal* would not be true.

[004-009] What is knowledge by concepts? It is knowledge of the relations of

[004-010] things, and things are intuitions. Concepts are not possible without

[004-011] intuitions, just as intuition is itself impossible without the matter

[004-012] of impressions. Intuitions are: this river, this lake, this brook,

[004-013] this rain, this glass of water; the concept is: water, not this or

[004-014] that appearance and particular example of water, but water in general,

[004-015] in whatever time or place it be realized; the material of infinite

[004-016] intuitions, but of one single constant concept.

[004-017] But the concept, the universal, if it be no longer intuition in one

[004-018] respect, is intuition in another respect, and cannot fail of being

[004-019] intuition. The man who thinks has impressions and emotions, in so far

[004-020] as he thinks. His impression and emotion will be not love or hate,

[004-021] not the passion of the man who is not a philosopher, not hate or love

[004-022] for certain objects and individuals, but *the effort of his thought

[004-023] itself,* with the pain and the joy, the love and the hate joined to it.

[004-024] This effort cannot but assume an intuitive form, in becoming objective

[004-025] to the spirit. To speak is not to think logically; but to *think

[004-026] logically* is also to *speak.*

[004-027] *Criticism of the negations of this thesis.*

[004-028] That thought cannot exist without speech, is a truth generally

[004-029] admitted. The negations of this thesis are all founded on equivocations

[004-030] and errors.

[004-031] The first of the equivocations is that of those who observe that one

[004-032] can likewise think with geometrical figures, algebraical numbers,

[004-033] ideographic signs, without any word, even pronounced silently and

[004-034] almost insensibly within one; that there are languages in which the

[004-035] word, the phonetic sign, expresses nothing, unless the written sign

[004-036] also be examined, and so on. But when we said "speak," we intended

[004-037] to employ a synecdoche, by which was to be understood "expression"

[004-038] in general, for we have already remarked that expression is not only

[004-039] so-called verbal expression. It may or may not be true that certain

[004-040] concepts may be thought without phonetic manifestations. But the very

[004-041] examples adduced to show this also prove that those concepts never

[004-042] exist without expressions.

[004-043] Others point out that animals, or certain animals, think and reason

[004-044] without speaking. Now as to how, whether, and what animals think,

[004-045] whether they be rudimentary men, like savages who refuse to be

[004-046] civilized, rather than physiological machines, as the old spiritualists

[004-047] maintained, are questions that do not concern us here. When the

[004-048] philosopher talks of animal, brutal, impulsive, instinctive nature

[004-049] and the like, he does not base himself on such conjectures as to

[004-050] dogs or cats, lions or ants; but upon observations of what is called

[004-051] animal and brutal in man: of the animal side or basis of what we feel

[004-052] in ourselves. If individual animals, dogs or cats, lions or ants,

[004-053] possess something of the activity of man, so much the better, or so

[004-054] much the worse, for them. This means that in respect to them also we

[004-055] must talk, not of "nature" as a whole, but of its animal basis, as

[004-056] being perhaps larger and stronger in them than the animal basis of

[004-057] man. And if we suppose that animals think and form concepts, what kind

[004-058] of conjecture would justify the assertion that they do so without

[004-059] corresponding expressions? Analogy with man, knowledge of the spirit,

[004-060] human psychology, the instrument of all our conjectures as to animal

[004-061] psychology, would constrain us on the contrary to suppose that if they

[004-062] think in any way, they also somehow speak.

[004-063] Another objection is derived from human psychology, and indeed literary

[004-064] psychology, to the effect that the concept can exist without the word,

[004-065] for it is certainly true that we all know books *well thought and

[004-066] ill written*: that is to say, a thought which remains *beyond* the

[004-067] expression, or *notwithstanding* faulty expression. But when we talk of

[004-068] books well thought and ill written, we cannot mean anything but that in

[004-069] such books are parts, pages, periods or propositions well thought and

[004-070] well written, and other parts (perhaps the least important) ill thought

[004-071] and ill written, not really thought and so not really expressed. Where

[004-072] [[giambattista-vico|Vico]]'s *Scienza nuova* is really ill written, it is also ill thought.

[004-073] If we pass from the consideration of big books to a short sentence, the

[004-074] error or inaccuracy of such a contention will leap to the eyes. How

[004-075] could a single sentence be clearly thought and confusedly written?

[004-076] All that can be admitted is that sometimes we possess thoughts

[004-077] (concepts) in an intuitive form, which is an abbreviated or rather

[004-078] peculiar expression, sufficient for us, but not sufficient to

[004-079] communicate it easily to any other given person or persons. Hence it

[004-080] is incorrect to say that we have the thought without the expression;

[004-081] whereas we should rather say that we have, indeed, the expression, but

[004-082] in such a form that it is not easy to communicate it to others. This,

[004-083] however, is a very variable, relative fact. There are always those who

[004-084] catch our thought on the wing, prefer it in this abbreviated form,

[004-085] and would be wearied by the greater development of it required by

[004-086] others. In other words, the thought considered abstractly and logically

[004-087] will be the same; but æsthetically we are dealing with two different

[004-088] intuition-expressions, into which different psychological elements

[004-089] enter. The same argument suffices to destroy, that is, to interpret

[004-090] correctly, the altogether empirical distinctior between an *internal*

[004-091] and an *external* language.

[004-092] *[[art|Art]] and science.*

[004-093] The most lofty manifestations, the summits of intellectual and of

[004-094] intuitive knowledge shining from afar, are called, as we know, Art and

[004-095] Science. Art and Science, then, are different and yet linked together;

[004-096] they meet on one side, which is the æsthetic side. Every scientific

[004-097] work is also a work of art. The æsthetic side may remain little noticed

[004-098] when our mind is altogether taken up with the effort to understand the

[004-099] thought of the man of science and to examine its truth. But it is no

[004-100] longer unnoticed when we pass from the activity of understanding to

[004-101] that of contemplation and see that thought either develop itself before

[004-102] us, limpid, exact, well-shaped, without superfluous or insufficient

[004-103] words, with appropriate rhythm and intonation; or confused, broken,

[004-104] embarrassed, tentative. Great thinkers are sometimes called great

[004-105] writers, while other equally great thinkers remain more or less

[004-106] fragmentary writers even if their fragments have the scientific value

[004-107] of harmonious, coherent, and perfect works.

[004-108] We pardon thinkers and men of science their literary mediocrity. The

[004-109] fragments, the flashes, console us for the whole, because it is far

[004-110] easier to recover the well-arranged composition from the fragmentary

[004-111] work of genius, to liberate the flame latent in the spark, than to

[004-112] achieve the discovery of genius. But how can we pardon mediocre

[004-113] expression in pure artists? "*Mediocribus esse poetis non di, non

[004-114] homines, non concessere columnae*" The poet or painter who lacks

[004-115] form, lacks everything, because he lacks *himself.* Poetical material

[004-116] permeates the souls of all: the expression alone, that is to say,

[004-117] the form, makes the poet. And here appears the truth of the view

[004-118] which denies all content to art, just the intellectual concept being

[004-119] understood as content. In this sense, when we take "content" as equal

[004-120] to "concept" it is most true, not only that art does not consist of

[004-121] content, but also that *it has no content.*

[004-122] *[[content|Content]] and form: another meaning. Prose and poetry.*

[004-123] The distinction between *poetry and prose* also cannot be justified,

[004-124] save as that between art and science. It was seen in antiquity that

[004-125] such distinction could not be founded on external elements, such as

[004-126] rhythm and metre, or on rhymed or unrhymed form; that it was, on the

[004-127] contrary, altogether internal. Poetry is the language of feeling, prose

[004-128] of the intellect; but since the intellect is also feeling, in its

[004-129] concreteness and reality, all prose has its poetical side.

[004-130] *The relation of first and second degree.*

[004-131] The relation between intuitive knowledge or expression and intellectual

[004-132] knowledge or concept, between art and science, poetry and prose, cannot

[004-133] be otherwise defined than by saying that it is one of *double degree.*

[004-134] The first degree is the expression, the second the concept: the first

[004-135] can stand without the second, but the second cannot stand without the

[004-136] first. There is poetry without prose, but not prose without poetry.

[004-137] [[expression|Expression]], indeed, is the first affirmation of human activity. Poetry

[004-138] is "the mother tongue of the human race"; the first men "were by nature

[004-139] sublime poets." We assert this in another way, when we observe that

[004-140] the passage from soul to spirit, from animal to human activity, is

[004-141] effected by means of language. And this should be said of intuition

[004-142] or expression in general. But to us it appears somewhat inaccurate to

[004-143] define language or expression as an *intermediate* link between nature

[004-144] and humanity, as though it were a mixture of both. Where humanity

[004-145] appears, the other has already disappeared; the man who expresses

[004-146] himself, certainly emerges from the state of nature, but he really does

[004-147] emerge: he does not stand half within and half without, as the use of

[004-148] the phrase "intermediate link" would imply.

[004-149] *Non-existence of other forms of knowledge.*

[004-150] The cognitive spirit has no form other than these two. Expression and

[004-151] concept exhaust it completely. The whole speculative life of man is

[004-152] spent in passing from one to the other and back again.

[004-153] *Historicity. Its identity with and difference from art.*

[004-154] *Historicity* is incorrectly held to be a third theoretical form.

[004-155] Historicity is not form, but content: as form, it is nothing but

[004-156] intuition or æsthetic fact. History does not seek for laws nor form

[004-157] concepts; it employs neither induction nor deduction; it is directed

[004-158] *ad narrandum, non ad demonstrandum*; it does not construct universals

[004-159] and abstractions, but posits intuitions. The this and here, the

[004-160] *individuum omnimode determinatum,* is its domain, as it is the domain

[004-161] of art. History, therefore, is included in the universal concept of art.

[004-162] As against this doctrine, in view of the impossibility of conceiving

[004-163] a third mode of knowledge, objections have been brought forward which

[004-164] would lead to the affiliation of history to intellectual or scientific

[004-165] knowledge. The greater portion of these objections is animated by the

[004-166] prejudice that in refusing to history the character of conceptual

[004-167] science something of its value and dignity has been taken from it. This

[004-168] really arises from a false idea of art, conceived not as an essential

[004-169] theoretic function, but as an amusement, a superfluity, a frivolity.

[004-170] Without reopening a long debate, which so far as we are concerned

[004-171] is finally closed, we will mention here one sophism which has been

[004-172] and still is widely repeated. Its purpose is to show the logical and

[004-173] scientific nature of history. The sophism consists in admitting that

[004-174] historical knowledge has for its object the individual; but not the

[004-175] representation, it is added, but rather the concept of the individual.

[004-176] From this it is argued that history is also a logical or scientific

[004-177] form of knowledge. History, in fact, is supposed to work out the

[004-178] concept of a personage such as Charlemagne or Napoleon; of an epoch,

[004-179] like the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] or the Reformation; of an event, such as the

[004-180] [[france|French]] Revolution and the Unification of [[italy|Italy]]. This it is held to do

[004-181] in the same way as Geometry works out the concepts of spatial forms, or

[004-182] Æsthetic that of expression. But all this is untrue. History cannot do

[004-183] otherwise than *represent* Napoleon and Charlemagne, the Renaissance

[004-184] and the Reformation, the French Revolution and the Unification of Italy

[004-185] as individual facts with their individual physiognomy: that is, in the

[004-186] sense in which logicians use the word "represent" when they say that

[004-187] one cannot have a concept of the individual, but only a representation.

[004-188] The so-called concept of the individual is always a universal or

[004-189] general concept, full of characteristics, supremely full, if you like,

[004-190] but however full it be, incapable of attaining to that individuality to

[004-191] which historical knowledge, as æsthetic knowledge, alone attains.

[004-192] To show how the content of history comes to be distinguished from

[004-193] that of art in the narrow sense, we must recall what has already

[004-194] been observed as to the ideal character of the intuition or first

[004-195] perception, in which all is real and therefore nothing is real. Only

[004-196] at a later stage does the spirit form the concepts of external and

[004-197] internal, of what has happened and what is desired, of object and

[004-198] subject, and the like: only at this later stage, that is, does it

[004-199] distinguish historical from non-historical intuition, the *real* from

[004-200] the *unreal,* real imagination from pure imagination. Even internal

[004-201] facts, what is desired and imagined, castles in the air, and countries

[004-202] of Cockaigne, have their reality, and the soul, too, has its history.

[004-203] His illusions form part of the biography of every individual as real

[004-204] facts. But the history of an individual soul is history, because

[004-205] the distinction between the real and the unreal is always active

[004-206] in it, even when the illusions themselves are the real. But these

[004-207] distinctive concepts do not appear in history like the concepts of

[004-208] science, but rather like those that we have seen dissolved and melted

[004-209] in the æsthetic intuitions, although in history they stand out in a

[004-210] manner altogether special to themselves. History does not construct

[004-211] the concepts of the real and unreal, but makes use of them. History,

[004-212] in fact, is not the theory of history. Mere conceptual analysis is

[004-213] of no use in ascertaining whether an event in our lives was real or

[004-214] imaginary. We must mentally reproduce the intuitions in the most

[004-215] complete form, as they were at the moment of production. Historicity

[004-216] is distinguished in the concrete from pure imagination as any one

[004-217] intuition is distinguished from any other: in memory.

[004-218] *Historical criticism.*

[004-219] Where this is not possible, where the delicate and fleeting shades

[004-220] between the real and unreal intuitions are so slight as to mingle

[004-221] the one with the other, we must either renounce for the time being at

[004-222] least the knowledge of what really happened (and this we often do), or

[004-223] we must fall back upon conjecture, verisimilitude, probability. The

[004-224] principle of verisimilitude and of probability in fact dominates all

[004-225] historical criticism. Examination of sources and authorities is devoted

[004-226] to establishing the most credible evidence. And what is the most

[004-227] credible evidence, save that of the best observers, that is, of those

[004-228] who best remember and (be it understood) have not wished to falsify,

[004-229] nor had interest in falsifying the truth of things?

[004-230] *Historical scepticism.*

[004-231] From this it follows that intellectualistic scepticism finds it easy

[004-232] to deny the certainty of any history, for the certainty of history

[004-233] differs from that of science. It is the certainty of memory and

[004-234] of authority, not that of analysis and demonstration. To speak of

[004-235] historical induction or demonstration is to make a metaphorical use of

[004-236] these expressions, which bear a quite different meaning in history to

[004-237] that which they bear in science. The conviction of the historian is the

[004-238] undemonstrable conviction of the juryman, who has heard the witnesses,

[004-239] listened attentively to the case, and prayed Heaven to inspire him.

[004-240] Sometimes, without doubt, he is mistaken, but the mistakes are in a

[004-241] negligible minority compared with the occasions when he grasps the

[004-242] truth. That is why good sense is right against the intellectualists in

[004-243] believing in history, which is not a "fable agreed upon," but what the

[004-244] individual and humanity remember of their past. We strive to enlarge

[004-245] and to render as precise as possible this record, which in some places

[004-246] is dim, in others very clear. We cannot do without it, such as it is,

[004-247] and taken as a whole it is rich in truth. Only in a spirit of paradox

[004-248] can one doubt that there ever was a [[greece|Greece]] or a Rome, an Alexander or a

[004-249] Cæsar, a feudal Europe overthrown by a series of revolutions, that on

[004-250] the 1st of November 1517 the theses of Luther were fixed to the door of

[004-251] the church at Wittemberg, or that the Bastile was taken by the people

[004-252] of Paris on the 14th of July 1789.

[004-253] "What proof hast thou of all this?" asks the sophist, ironically.

[004-254] Humanity replies: "I remember it."

[004-255] *Philosophy as perfect science. The so-called natural

[004-256] sciences, and their limits.*

[004-257] The world of what has happened, of the concrete, of historical fact,

[004-258] is the world called real, natural, including in this definition both

[004-259] the reality called physical and that called spiritual and human. All

[004-260] this world is intuition; historical intuition, if it be shown as it

[004-261] realistically is; imaginary or artistic intuition in the narrow sense,

[004-262] if presented in the aspect of the possible, that is to say, of the

[004-263] imaginable.

[004-264] Science, true science, which is not intuition but concept, not

[004-265] individuality but universality, cannot be anything but science of the

[004-266] spirit, that is, of what reality has of universal: Philosophy. If

[004-267] natural *sciences* be spoken of, apart from philosophy, we must observe

[004-268] that these are not perfect sciences: they are aggregates of cognitions,

[004-269] arbitrarily abstracted and fixed. The so-called natural sciences

[004-270] indeed themselves recognize that they are surrounded by limitations,

[004-271] and these limitations are nothing but historical and intuitive data.

[004-272] They calculate, measure, establish equalities and uniformities,

[004-273] create classes and types, formulate laws, show in their own way how

[004-274] one fact arises out of other facts; but while doing this they are

[004-275] constantly running into facts known intuitively and historically.

[004-276] Even geometry now states that it rests altogether on hypotheses,

[004-277] since threedimensional or Euclidean space is but one of the possible

[004-278] spaces, selected for purposes of study because more convenient. What

[004-279] is true in the natural sciences is either philosophy or historical

[004-280] fact. What of properly naturalistic they contain, is abstraction and

[004-281] caprice. When the natural sciences wish to become perfect sciences,

[004-282] they must leave their circle and enter philosophy. They do this when

[004-283] they posit concepts which are anything but naturalistic, such as those

[004-284] of the unextended atom, of ether or vibration, of vital force, of

[004-285] non-intuitional space, and the like. These are true and proper attempts

[004-286] at philosophy, when they are not mere words void of meaning. The

[004-287] concepts of natural science are, without doubt, most useful; but one

[004-288] cannot obtain from them that *system* which belongs only to the spirit.

[004-289] These historical and intuitive data which cannot be eliminated from the

[004-290] natural sciences furthermore explain not only how, with the advance

[004-291] of knowledge, what was once believed to be true sinks gradually to

[004-292] the level of mythological belief and fantastic illusion, but also how

[004-293] among natural scientists some are to be found who call everything in

[004-294] their sciences upon which reasoning is founded *mythical facts, verbal

[004-295] expedients,* or *conventions.* Natural scientists and mathematicians

[004-296] who approach the study of the energies of the spirit without

[004-297] preparation, are apt to carry thither such mental habits and to speak

[004-298] in philosophy of such and such conventions as "decreed by man." They

[004-299] make conventions of truth and morality, and a supreme convention of

[004-300] the Spirit itself! But if there are to be conventions, something must

[004-301] exist which is no convention, but is itself the author of conventions.

[004-302] This is the spiritual activity of man. The limitation of the natural

[004-303] sciences postulates the illimitability of philosophy.

[004-304] *The phenomenon and the noumenon.*

[004-305] These explications have firmly established that the pure or fundamental

[004-306] forms of knowledge are two: the intuition and the concept—Art, and

[004-307] Science or Philosophy. With these are to be included History, which

[004-308] is, as it were, the product of intuition placed in contact with the

[004-309] concept, that is, of art receiving in itself philosophic distinctions,

[004-310] while remaining concrete and individual. All other forms (natural

[004-311] sciences and mathematics) are impure, being mingled with extraneous

[004-312] elements of practical origin. Intuition gives us the world, the

[004-313] phenomenon; the concept gives us the noumenon, the Spirit.
