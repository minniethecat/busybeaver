# Drama

In Croce's aesthetic system, drama is not a distinct spiritual category but one of the many external forms — alongside lyric, epic, painting, and music — through which the single act of intuition-expression may be physically externalized (016-036, 016-041).

## Definition

Croce rejects the traditional division of poetry into dramatic, lyric, and epic as a substantive aesthetic classification. These are "empirical concepts" that describe external technique, not internal spiritual distinctions. A dramatist's "dramatic conception" is, philosophically, no different from any other aesthetic intuition (016-041). The term "dramatic" refers to the manner of physical externalization — the use of dialogue, action, and theatrical performance — not to a distinct species of expression. "If a dramatic technique in the larger sense" is taken as the essence of the art, one has confused the means of communication with the expressive act itself (016-049).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Drama, like all art forms, belongs to the first moment of spirit: aesthetic intuition. The fact that a drama is performed by actors, involves plot and character, and unfolds in time does not alter its fundamental nature as expression. The dramatist who creates character through "the personages of a drama or of a romance" is performing the same spiritual act as the lyric poet who expresses a single moment of feeling (011-227). Croce notes that critics "speak continually of the drama of their country" as if national drama were an aesthetic category, when in fact it is a sociological or historical grouping (022-064).

### Key Distinctions

The central distinction is between the aesthetic intuition (which is indivisible) and the physical technique (which is divisible into genres). Aristotle's rules for tragedy — unity of time, place, and action — belong to technique, not to aesthetics. Croce points out that "rules for lyrical, dramatical and epic poetry" are empirical generalizations from practice, never normative laws (025-050). The practical conditions of theatrical production — the stage, the audience, the actors — are external to the work of art as expression. "At some sort of experimental drama" the technique may be novel, but the aesthetic act remains intuition-expression (005-037).

> The history of drama criticism is a history of confusing technique with art. The unities of "epic or dramatic poetry," the "moral quality of the characters," the division into acts and scenes — all these are practical considerations that do not touch the essence of drama as expression (038-617).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Aristotle's Poetics established the foundational framework for dramatic theory in the West, defining tragedy in terms of plot, character, and catharsis. In the French classical tradition, Boileau "turned the rules" of drama into rigid prescriptions, codifying the three unities as aesthetic law (038-665). The Renaissance recovery of Aristotle generated an extensive literature on "the division into epic, lyric and dramatic" poetry (038-571) and produced no fewer than "forty-eight varieties of heroic drama" (038-596) — a proliferation of pseudo-categories that Croce regards as evidence of the bankruptcy of genre theory.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's dissolution of drama as an aesthetic category follows from his general argument against the division of art into genres. "Shakespeare on the Elizabethan dramatists, Goethe, with Werther and Faust" — these comparisons are historical and psychological, not aesthetic (018-291). The substitution of "didactic poetry for drama as the highest form" in certain critical traditions merely replaces one arbitrary hierarchy with another (028-529). Croce's innovation is to treat drama not as a kind of art but as a set of practical conventions for the physical externalization of the single act of expression. "Drama is action," as one tradition has it, but for Croce action belongs to the practical, not the aesthetic, order (034-066).

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's rejection of genre distinctions — including the lyric/dramatic/epic triad — has been sharply contested. Critics argue that the medium in which an artist works is not merely "external technique" but actively shapes the expressive possibilities and thus the aesthetic achievement itself. The experience of drama is not reducible to lyric expression plus theatrical apparatus; the theatrical apparatus is constitutive of the aesthetic experience. Croce's response would likely point to bad dramas that obey all the rules and great dramas that break them — evidence, he would argue, that the rules are not the art.
