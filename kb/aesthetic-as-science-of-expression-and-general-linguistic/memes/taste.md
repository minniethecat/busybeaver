# Taste

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, taste (*gusto*) is not a passive faculty of appreciation separate from artistic creation but **identical with genius** — the receptive aspect of the same intuitive-expressive activity.

## Definition

"Identity of taste and genius" (017-071) is Croce's radical thesis: taste is not a lesser, merely appreciative faculty but the same aesthetic activity that produces art, operating in a receptive mode. When we appreciate a work of art, we re-create it in our own spirit — we repeat, in however diminished a form, the artist's original act of intuition-expression. "Identity of taste — Analogy with other activities" (P02-133–P02-134) extends this: taste is to genius as understanding is to reasoning.

This identity resolves the ancient paradox of how taste can be both subjective (each person feels their own pleasure) and universal (we argue about taste as if there were right answers). If taste is the re-creation of the artist's expression, then differences in taste are differences in the success of that re-creation.

## In Croce's System

### Taste as Reproductive Intuition

Croce distinguishes productive from reproductive intuition. The artist produces an original intuition-expression; the spectator reproduces it. This reproduction is not passive reception but active re-formation — the spectator must intuit the work for it to exist as an aesthetic object. "The productive activity is called genius; the reproductive activity is called taste" (017-077). Both are intuition.

### Taste and Criticism

If taste is identical with genius, then aesthetic criticism is not the application of external standards but the re-creation of the work in the critic's spirit, followed by conceptual reflection on that re-creation. Taste provides the material for critical judgment; critical judgment articulates what taste has intuited. Croce thus bridges the gap between impressionistic criticism (pure taste) and dogmatic criticism (pure rules).

## Key Distinctions

**Taste vs Pleasure.** Taste is a cognitive act (intuition); pleasure is a practical fact (feeling). You may take pleasure in a work you have poor taste for (misunderstanding it), and you may have excellent taste for a work that gives you no pleasure (understanding it perfectly while finding its content disagreeable).

**Taste vs Fashion.** Taste is the individual's genuine intuitive response; fashion is socially conformist preference. "Good taste always recognized" (005-188) refers to authentic intuitive judgment, not to what is currently fashionable.

**Taste vs Rules.** Taste is autonomous — it does not apply pre-existing rules to works of art. The rules of art are abstractions from past acts of taste; they can guide but never substitute for the living act of intuition.

## Historical Context

The concept of taste emerged in the 18th century as aesthetics separated from metaphysics. **David Hume**'s "Of the Standard of Taste" attempted to reconcile subjective pleasure with objective standards. **Immanuel Kant**'s "judgment of taste" in the *Critique of Judgment* identified taste as the faculty for judging the beautiful, grounded in the free play of imagination and understanding — but Kant distinguished taste from genius, making taste the judgment of beauty and genius the production of beautiful art (029-149, 029-153). Croce's "Identity of taste and genius" (017-071) unifies what Kant separated.
